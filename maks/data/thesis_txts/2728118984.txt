Abstract
Cerebral Palsy is a brain injuryôÙcaused disorder. This injury usually occurs during early
fetal brain development. It is nonôÙprogressive, but it can cause secondary conditions over
time. The focus of this thesis is the ankle of children between 6 and 10 years, who are
affected by the disease on a mild level. They often have a hard time with the proper
movements for walking because of muscle spasticity.
The more severe cases are often treated surgically, but nonsurgical methods getting more
attention nowadays. The field of nonsurgical methods in the ankle treatment affected by
Cerebral Palsy is quite new, since just a few studies deal with it. In this thesis, the develôÙ
opment of a 3DôÙprinted feet training device for rehabilitation is introduced. The device has
been connected to another studentãs serious game, which was developed for the same
purpose. Afterwards, testing was conducted and feedback was collected. The results
were promising, since the system was well accepted. Suggestions for future developôÙ
ment were stated at the end of the report.

1.1
Problem and Motivation
Cerebral Palsy (CP) is the most common childhood physical disability. It is a brain damageôÙ
caused neurological disorder, which is nonôÙprogressive. [1] One of the most common
complications of CP is muscle spasticity, which often affects the ankle. It often ends up in
footôÙdrop, resulting that they cannot walk properly. This project is addressing this problem
by making a 3DôÙprinted ankle rehabilitation device. 3D printing is an additive manufacôÙ
turing method, which allows the production of several variations in a relatively short time
and low cost. [2]
Nonsurgical treatment methods are becoming more preferred, but they still hold unexôÙ
plored areas. [3] The aim of this research is to broaden the knowledge about feet training
devices and discover the possibilities provided by Serious Games. The final prototype has
been planned through several stages. It has been tested, and the results were evaluated.
This report will lead the reader through a researchôÙbased design process.
1.2
Previous Work at DTU
The background inspiration is coming from the ReHyb project at DTU, which is developing
hybrid upperôÙlimb exoskeleton for rehabilitation based on hybrid neuroprosthesis. [4] The
system collects data and creates a digital twin during the rehabilitation, which interfaces
with the advanced cognitive system, and it plans and controls the exoskeleton based on
it, with the respect on the userãs health status and capabilities. The main inspiration was
taken from the thesis of Hans Kristian Bech Hansen, who was working on his project
User adaptable upperôÙlimp exoskeleton for stroke victim rehabilitation in 2023. [5] In this
project, a similar 3DôÙprinted device will be adjusted and reôÙframed for the ankle.
1.3
Goal and Research Question
The main goal of the research is to develop a 3DôÙprinted device, which is paired with
a digital game to help with the rehabilitation exercises. The focus is on the training of
the muscles in the ankle responsible for walking. A certain target group will be defined to
ensure inclusivity for every participant in that specific age group. Furthermore, the system
should be meant to be for daily home use.
Therefore, the main research question is the following:
How can a 3DôÙprinted exoskeleton be designed to train the ankle and help to strengthen
the muscles responsible for walking in the ankle of children with cerebral palsy?

2.1
About Cerebral Palsy
Cerebral Palsy (CP) is a disorder caused by brain injury, usually during early fetal brain
development. The incidence of CP is 2ôÙ3 per 1000 live births, and it is characterised by
abnormal posture, movement, abnormal tone and muscle spasticity. The earliest age of
the disorder being recognised is two years. Although CP is nonôÙprogressive, it can cause
various secondary conditions over time, affecting functional abilities. The motor disorôÙ
ders of CP are often associated with cognitive disorders. Prematurity and low birthweight
are the highest risk factors for CP, but multiple other factors can potentially increase the
chance of the disorder for infants. At 2 to 5 years old, abnormal neuromotor findings tend
to resolve within the first few years. Therefore, CP is reported more often in the early ages.
On the other hand, half of the children who develop CP have nonôÙidentified risk factors.
And CP can also result from injury to the brain after the neonatal period and before five
years of age. The most common causes are traumatic brain injuries, nearôÙdrowning and
meningitis. [6]
Symptoms caused by CP are often treated surgically [7], but a trend favouring nonsurgical
treatment methods is observable. Although convincing evidence supporting any nonsurôÙ
gical treatment options has yet to be found, it is essential to continue the research of
the possibilities. [8] Among the several trials of mobility rehabilitation, functional therapy
gained improvements. Functional therapy aims at everyday tasks and daily functioning,
such as walking, climbing stairs and clothing. At the end of both revised trials, the imôÙ
provement of gross motor function was observed, and the parents positively perceived
the interventions. [9, 10] Intensive training focused on selected activities also resulted in
improved basic motor abilities. [11]
This thesis focuses on improving walking ability. Therefore, the ankle training device
should focus on the muscles related to ankle movement and walking. Since spasticity of
the gastrosoleus muscle often causes the development of reduced passive dorsiflexion
of the ankle, and often results in footôÙdrop (involuntary plantarflexion), the training should
focus on the muscles responsible for the dorsiflexion. [12] In this case, dorsiflexion in
the ankle is the backward bending of the foot, while the toes are drawn backwards to the
shins.[13] If the gastrosoleus muscle is too spastic, performing dorsiflexion can be hard
or even impossible.
There are several classification levels for CP. In this thesis, the focus will be on the Gross
Motor Function Classification System (GMFCS), since it is more reliable compared to
other classifications [14], validated [15], and the rehabilitation device will be developed

2.2
3D Printing as Prototyping Method
3D printing is a rapid prototyping method which creates threeôÙdimensional objects with
additive manufacturing technology from a digital file. Compared to other prototyping methôÙ
ods, some of the hugest advantages are the low material price, the fast production, no
need for special or expensive tools, and the printers can produce complex shapes. [17]
Although 3D printing is very convenient, it also has disadvantages. If a structure has
angles over 45ô¯, supports are required for the print. They are often hard to access and
remove, leaving a rough surface on the product. [18] Supports are easily breakable thin
plastic structures added to the model during printing to underpin the midôÙair particles or
structures with more than 45ô¯. Another disadvantage could be the size limitations due
to the build plate dimensions. The size of a build plate always depends on the printerãs
brand and is usually fixed.
Figure 2.1: Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM) [17]
3D printers available in DTU Skylab were used during the prototyping phase. Two brands
are available: Original Prusa i3 MK3S+ 3D printer (later Prusa) and Ultimaker 3 Extended
(later Ultimaker). Therefore during the model preparation, the build volume of these printôÙ
ers should be considered as overall dimensions, which are the followings: 25û21û21
cm for the Prusa and 30x21.5x21.5 cm for the Ultimaker. Both printers work with Fused
Deposition Modeling (FDM), which means a hot nozzle melts the material, in this case,
Polylactic Acid (PLA) plastic, and extrudes it on the build plate, as seen in Figure 2.1. [17]
PLA is a vegetableôÙbased, hard and tough, easyôÙtoôÙprint and lowôÙwarping material, usuôÙ
ally made from cornstarch or sugarcane. It is a biodegradable thermoplastic with lowôÙ
temperature resistance. Over 60 ô¯C, the parts start to lose mechanical strength. ThereôÙ
fore, postôÙprocessing of the material is hard, and water usage is suggested to keep the
frictionôÙcaused warmth lower, for example, during sanding. PLA is an affordable and verôÙ
satile plastic suitable for printing larger and tinier objects. [19, 20]

2.3
Existing Devices
3D printing in rehabilitation is widely spread and often associated with Serious Games.
Serious Games are game interfaces meant to adapt to the userãs needs and focus on
training for activities of daily living. This type of game should adapt to the improvements
individuals achieve. Their main target is training, learning or rehabilitation, and not only
fun. [21, 22] During the play, data can be collected and analysed to create a Digital Twin
of the person playing. A Digital Twin helps the system make decisions based on the
patientãs current state, behaviour, and needs. This way, motivation and challenges can
be provided to the user during gaming. [23]
3DôÙprinted ankle orthoses are supporting people with CP already. 3D printing technology
provides the possibility of customisation and maximising comfort. These devices can
provide support during everyday tasks and assist with stretching during rehabilitation.
[24] Exoskeletons in the area of hand and wrist rehabilitation are more spread than ankle
training devices. Still, curiosity has already led some researchers to look into this field. For
example, the Rutgers Ankle CP system aims to strengthen the ankle of children with CP.
The training session of 12 weeks (3 sessions each week) improved strength and motor
control.
The device has a snowboard foot binding on the top connected with a 1 kg aluminium
adapter to the robot and has two different rehabilitation games: Airplane and Breakout
3D. In the Airplane game, the patient (7 years old boy) had to pass through frames with
an airplane, while in the Breakout 3D, the ankle controlled a paddle to bounce a ball to
destroy bricks. The second game was only focusing on dorsiflexion and plantarflexion.
[25] The IntelliStrech rehabilitation robot is another device providing stretching and active
playing modes. In the active playing mode, the participant should control a video game
with the ankle. The device offered assistance and a predetermined range of motion with
torque limits to ensure safety. This device contains a builtôÙin screen and chair. ImproveôÙ
ments were demonstrated after the rehabilitation period. [26] Both devices introduced
are bigger and not optional for home use, but presented improvements after use for a
specific period. Another research was also conducted with the second device introduced.
This research compared homeôÙbased and laboratoryôÙbased training among two groups of
children. There were no significant differences between the outcomes of the two groups,
which means homeôÙbased rehabilitation is feasible. [27]

cards. [30] The relevant Ability Prompt Cards that should be considered during the design
process are the followings:
ãÂ Unable to think: The stress caused by the desire for achievement can cause overôÙ
load during device use. Thus, putting on and removing the device should be as
simple and safe as possible. Possible solutions could be the use of Velcro fastenôÙ
ers or a slipperôÙlike design.

Modularity in Design
Modularity means the standardisation of the parts, and it can provide easy and fast proôÙ
duction, customisation and variety, and an adaptable assembly. Modularity provides a
sustainable solution and flexibility of changes that can be performed on the device. It
means only the broken part should be changed instead of the whole product, and upôÙ

Modularity provides the possibility of having the different functions separated. This way,

Modularity also addresses the life cycle design of the product with all the aboveôÙmentioned
properties. Functionality, commonality, and life cycle similarity should be considered while
creating the modules. The planning of the modules should mainly focus on the functionôÙ

well because the connections have to be able to take on the stress caused by use. FurôÙ

The different modules can be created by mapping a system of functions the product should

structure. The functions not belonging to this system should be shown as inôÙ and outflows.

2.8
Research Focus
The main focus of the research is to create a 3DôÙprinted ankle training device for children
with Cerebral Palsy to train them with Serious Games, while reaching improvements in
mobility. Dorsiflexion is an essential movement for everyday life because the absence of
it directly affects the ability to walk. The training focus was on the muscles responsible
for this specific movement, and the minimum age of the target group will be 6 years old
due to the reasons mentioned in Section 2.1. The maximum age was 10 because this is
the common maximum age limit for all the chosen anthropometric data in Section 2.6.
GMFCS Levels I and II was the target because children on those levels have difficulties
but no problems with balance, sitting and walking alone. However, a seated position is
suggested to prevent balance problems from occurring during playing. This way, no harm
was secured due to fewer limitations in the capabilities of the participants. On Level III
children walk using a handheld mobility device, which means they do not have free hands
to put the device on. Since a seated position is required for the training, participants on
Level III would need a seat belt for pelvic alignment and balance, so it was better to leave
this option for eventual extensions.
The device was planned for home use to ensure availability and the necessary materials
are available for an average family in Denmark. The families do not have to own a 3D
printer, since printer shops are available, so they only need to send the digital models
there.
Furthermore, the research focused on the final prototypeãs properties, such as the conveôÙ
nience of wearing it, the user experience of using it with Serious Games, the appearance
of wear in the material after use and possible future improvements.

Problems and Improvements During the Testing
During the continuous testing, some problems with the physical device occurred. They
got solved before the testing with the children and the woman so that they could have the
best experience of the final outcome, these projects can provide. After a while the plastic
shaft started to creak on the plastic stand. It was probably occurring because of the
friction between them. Slight signs of wear started to appear on the shaft and the stand.
It did not affect the deviceãs function, but summer bike chain lube was used on the shafts
to prevent further wear and creaky sound. After lubricating the parts, the problem fully
ceased. Another problem was the overheat of the Raspberry Pi, which caused problems
in the information flow between the digital game and the device. A larger scaled grid was
implemented into the cover, as seen in Section 3.1. This grid prevented the overheat,
and the trouble did not appear again. The last issue during the continuous testing was
a loud snap sound and a slight feeling of a hit when the pedal returned and touched the
minimum stick. First, a piece of rubber mat was glued on the bottom of the pedal, but
it just reduced the effects and did not eliminate them. Therefore, the softer half of the
Velcro was glued on the bottom of the pedal due to availability. The hit and the sound
disappeared finally. All the problems that came up during the first round were addressed
and successfully solved.
In the second round of testing, only three problems came up. One of them was, that the
device was still slipping because of the different floor types and dust, despite the the rubber
mat on the bottom. A bigger rubber mat was laid underneath the device and the chair the
participant was sitting on as a solution in the final testing setup. The participantãs body
weight secured the stability of the bigger mat on the floor, and slipping was prevented due
to the rubberôÙonôÙrubber connection between the device and the floor. The second problem
was the heel part, which got broken during the transportation of the device. Because of
this, the leg holder parts were not tested on the participants, since they could not be
attached. Luckily, this damage did not affect any other vital functions. The third problem
was the lack of common language between some participants and the experimenters.
Therefore, the questionnaire got translated into Danish, and a new person, who speaks
it, was asked to interpret. This can cause misunderstandings both during the testing and
during the filling of the questionnaires. A new person, who does not know the plans of
the testing, and the work of the device as well as the experimenters, may have some
misleading translation. From the experience of the testing and the filled questionnaire, it
can be stated, that the language barriers did not affect the quality of the results.

5.6
Limitations
During this project, several limitations occurred. The main restrictions were traceable from
3D printing. In addition to the limitations of the material properties and the technology,
DTU Skylab had a limited number of 3D printers. This means, most of the time it was
very hard to get one available printer. The experimentation of the parts often required
the printing parallel in several printers, to keep the necessary efficiency. Often unknown
people stopped the prints and started theirs, or fail occurred during the printing process,
for example, the printers ran out of filament. For a certain time period, most of the 3D
printers were out of filament. Because of this, the prototyping stretched out for a long

a lot of students from the university, the settings were often wrong, or the build plate was
dirty or damaged, and the plastic didnãt stick to it. It often led to damaged, and deformed
parts. In these cases, at the moment of realisation, the printing had to be restarted, but
usually there were no available printers.
Problems often occurred with the electrical parts as well. In those cases, the device had
to be disassembled, and all the electronics checked. This usually led to the wear of the
plastic connections. The magnetic encoder is a really sensitive sensor in the device, and
small changes in the distance between the magnet and the encoder often affected the
functioning of the game system.
The last limitation was the lack of connections to find participants for the testing. Several
conversations ran to a dead end because no answer came, or there were no available
common time slots for the participants and the experimenters. Therefore, the device was
finally tested with children without the disorder, and one person, who has the disorder, but

got finished and tested in time, and valuable data was collected for evaluation.
5.7
Future improvements
By restarting the Double Diamond design method this report could serve as the basis

information and possibilities to move on with. The participants of the test clearly expressed
that the final device was oversized. This opens new challenges of resizing the product
without compromising the strength and stability of it.
Metal shafts and the implementation of bearings could improve the strength and data
quality generated by the magnetic encoder. They could provide smoother operation and
prevent wear. These could be commercial and easily accessible parts, and they would
definitely lengthen the lifetime and improve the quality of the device. The bearings and the
metal shafts were planned to take the stress occurring during the rotational movements.
The deviceãs material has been chosen as PLA because that was the available material
at the university. However, it is highly recommended to research other available materials
on the market, which are less brittle, has higher temperature resistance, and are easier to
postôÙprocess. Better quality and more reliable electrical components could be researched
to prevent continuous disassembling and reôÙassembling, so the wear in the connecting
components can be prevented.
Improvements can take place in the field of inclusivity and modularity as well. ModularityôÙ

further improved. InclusivityôÙwise, the partially or not fulfilled points in Section 5.4 should

Conclusion
In this project, a 3DôÙprinted ankle training device for children with CP was successfully
developed. The device can be manufactured by using a Fused Deposition Modeling 3D
printer. The electronics are commercially available products. The field of ankle training
devices for children with Cerebral Palsy is quite new, with few existing studies.
After research and interviews, the target group has been defined: children between the
ages of 6 and 10 with Cerebral Palsy who are either on Level I or Level II of the Gross Motor
Function Classification System. The focus of the project is to improve the dorsiflexion of
this specific target group. The project has been conducted through the Double Diamond
Design Process and addresses inclusivity, availability and modularity. The final prototype
was made after several conceptualisation steps: ideation, pretotype, first prototype, and
final prototype. The final prototype was tested with a Serious Game developed parallel to
it. The gameãs mechanism was that colourful dots were coming towards the users, and
they must lift their toes to destroy them.
During the development of the final prototype, a function diagram was created to define
the possible modules. Modules were meant to provide generalised components and a
simplified assembly. Furthermore, modularization provides the possibility of upgrades and
easier reparations. Implementing the modules in the product architecture was successful,
and the final device was assembled. The final device stands from two pedals, one for
each foot, weight boxes, and covered spaces to securely accommodate the electronics.
Graphics were implemented on the outside as a preparation for the testing. The graphics
aimed to predict the digital game theme for the participants and make the device look
more friendly. After a working setup was conducted, tests were performed.
The information gathering happened in two ways. One was through interviews with proôÙ
fessionals, and the other was questionnaires after the testing of the device. The data was
evaluated, and conclusions were drawn. Next to the generally positive feedback, possible
improvements were suggested. The preôÙdefined points of inclusivity and availability were
revised and evaluated. There were fully, partially and not fulfilled points as well.
The amount of time and plastic used for the final version was calculated. The 3D printed
components of the device stand from 51 pieces which take 9.7 days to print continuously
with one printer, and take up 2.556 kg of plastic. The chosen material is PLA, which is an
environmentally friendly, disposable, vegetableôÙbased plastic.
Overall, wideôÙrange research was conducted to develop a rehabilitation device, and exôÙ
perimentation was carried out to broaden the knowledge about the training for rehabilitaôÙ
tion purposes. This project highly encourages future work for further improvements.

Meeting notes with Tonie Uldahl Jansen
ãÂ What do you think? Is it better to use the device with or without shoes?
With shoes and no orthoses because not every child has joints in their orthoses.
ãÂ Children sometimes get frightened of bandages and devices like this. Do you know
any good tricks that can help build trust in them?
Use graphics on the device.
Further notes:
ãÂ The device should have a length to fix the leg/shin into it.
ãÂ Velcros should be attached: one under the knee and one close to the ankle joint.
ãÂ Support the leg from the sides so it does not move. It is fine with plastic, it can be
strong enough.
ãÂ Something should be at the heel to stop the children from sliding out the feet backôÙ
wards.
ãÂ It should be with straightened knees at the end to teach them to walk because when
we are walking, dorsiflexion happens with an already straightened leg. As they
straighten their leg, the exercise gets harder because of the muscle spasticity.
ãÂ By the time, the pedalãs angle should be set so they are straightening their leg. It is
easier with a bent knee, so they should start there. With time, changing the angle
and making them straighten their leg can be challenging and very useful from the
rehabilitation perspective.
ãÂ There is no need for weights on the feet during the training. Their spasticity provides
enough resistance.
ãÂ For those with good balance, standing position to use body weight can be beneficial.
On the other hand, dorsiflexion during walking happens with no body weight on the
leg.
ãÂ The axis of the rotation should be in the same line as the ankle, underneath the heel,
not at the end of the foot.
