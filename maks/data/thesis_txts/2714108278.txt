Abstract 
Satellite remote sensing of the ocean has growing importance in the light of climate change and 
increased pressure on ecosystem services. Ocean colour is identified as an essential climate 
variable and with modern satellite data it is possible to analyse time spans of several decades 
covering vast areas. In this study, ocean colour in the subarctic and arctic North Atlantic from 2001 
to 2020 was analysed for patterns and changes based on monthly data provided by the Ocean 
Colour Climate Change Initiative. Six wave bands of remote sensing reflectance data were studied 
without further assumptions or algorithms applied: 412 nm, 443 nm, 490 nm, 510 nm, 560 nm, 
665 nm. Geographical patterns and general trends were investigated using visualizations and 
temporal means. It was found that wave bands between 412 nm and 510 nm were significantly 
correlated with their neighboring wave bands respectively, while also resembling their 
geographical distribution of intensity patterns. Focusing on reflectance impacted by organic 
material and Chlorophyll-a, pixelwise regressions of Rrs(412 nm) and Rrs(665 nm) were used to 
indicate regions of change, which then were defined as subareas. Subareas were subsampled and 
analysed for trends with linear regressions over monthly means. Changes appeared in large patches 
in the outer wave bands, 412 nm and 665 nm. Significant increases in Rrs(665 nm) were found in 
Eastern Fram Strait West of Spitsbergen, in the Greenland Sea, the Eastern Labrador Sea and 
Iceland Basin, accompanied with decreases in Rrs(412 nm), supporting an interpretation as 
increased phytoplankton abundance. In contrast, a decrease in Rrs(665 nm) was found in northern 
Rockall area, accompanied by an increase in Rrs(412 nm), pointing towards a decrease in the local 
phytoplankton abundance. Moreover, a strong decrease in reflectance of Rrs(412 nm) was found 
in Baffin Bay, suggesting an increased allochthonous input of organic material which might be 
due to melting permafrost in the Canadian Arctic. A preliminary unconstrained qualitative analysis 
using PCA (principal component analysis) revealed that it is possible to distinguish between 
different phytoplankton groups, which might shed more light on the nature of the changes observed 
and impact on carbon cycling in the region. All combined, the analysis reveals regional hot spots 
of change that warrant further study. The extracted trends and changes found need to be verified 
with in situ water column measurements, and potential linkages to hydrography and fisheries 
investigated.

1. Introduction 
Satellite based measurements have in the past few decades enhanced ocean observations into a new era. 
In the globally changing climate, monitoring becomes essential for an understanding of new conditions, 
especially in remote regions where it can otherwise be difficult to gather data with good temporal and 
spatial coverage. In the rapidly warming Arctic, sea ice is melting (Kwok et al., 2009; Lindsay & 
Schweiger, 2015), stratification is changing (Farmer et al., 2021; Fu et al., 2020; Polyakov et al., 2018), 
large scale circulation is slowing down (Rahmstorf et al., 2015; Sévellec et al., 2017), and increasing 
glacial meltwater runoff is influencing marine ecosystems (Bamber et al., 2018; Hopwood et al., 2020) 
Also, the input of organic material to surface layers changes due to runoff from land (Stedmon et al., 
2011; Stedmon et al., 2015). Altogether, this can potentially alter the plankton community structure, 
change the carbon flow in the ecosystem, and propagate into higher trophic levels with potential knock-
on effects on fisheries in the region (Hátún et al., 2009; Richardson & Schoeman, 2004). 
The colour of the ocean acts as an essential climate variable as it represents the combined effects 
of phytoplankton, coloured dissolved organic material, as well as suspended materials interacting with 
light. The remote monitoring of ocean colour can contribute with a better understanding of all ocean 
colour derived parameters such as changed phytoplankton dynamics as well as indications of global 
warming induced changes in hydrogeography.  
The overall aim of this project was to use satellite ocean colour data from the past 20 years to 
reveal regions of changing bio-optical properties in subarctic and arctic North Atlantic waters. The region 
has an important role in global climate as a node in deep-water ocean circulation and in carbon 
sequestration. Ocean colour measurements can potentially reveal changes in phytoplankton and organic 
matter distribution and phenology, as the surface water of the region responds to climate change.  
This is done by i) A literature study of ocean colour and reported changes thereof. ii) A thorough 
data processing of satellite data recorded over a period of 20 years. iii) Identification and visualization 
of regional changes over time in ocean colour around Greenland.

2. Ocean Colour Theory 
2.2 From optical constituents to remote sensing reflectance spectra  
The colour of the ocean is a product of sunlight and optically active constituents in the water (OACs). 
The major OACs are phytoplankton, coloured dissolved organic material (CDOM), and sediments, which 
all contribute to the inherent optical properties of seawater (IOPs). When sunlight meets the surface of 
the ocean a large part of it interacts with the OACs, where some is absorbed, some is scattered, and the 
remainder is directly reflected.  
The absorbance intensity of the OACs vary with wavelength and can be illustrated in absorbance spectra 
as by (Lee et al., 2014) (Figure 1). Water itself absorbs strongest in the red end (>600 nm). Phytoplankton 
is a major contributor to light absorption due to their photosynthetic pigments. The abundant pigment 
chlorophyll-a (Chl-a) absorbs mainly at red and blue visible wavelengths, showing two absorbance 
maxima, one around 440 nm, and the second around 675 nm (Figure 1), while largely reflecting green 
light, so that its presence tones the ocean green. Once phytoplankton dies off and turns into detritus, it 
absorbs strongest in the blue end of the spectra, where also CDOM (also known as gelbstoff) contributes 
largely to the absorption of light at ultraviolet and blue wavelengths. Sediments absorb light in the blue 
end as well, with a similar spectrum as CDOM. At the same time, sediments also scatter light which 
generally is equal through all wave lengths.  
Light which is not absorbed will in the end leave the water again and can be measured by 
spectrophotometers. To create comparability between differently illuminated areas of sea surface, the 
water leaving radiance is set as ratio to the downwelling irradiance just above the sea, which gives the 
measure of remote sensing reflectance (Rrs). The Rrs of a water mass will vary across wavelengths and 
can just like the absorbance be illustrated as spectrum over the visible range of light.

2.3 From reflectance spectra to underlying properties 
Rrs can be used to derive bio-optical properties of the surface layer of waters, giving information about 
the underlying OACs. The Rrs can give indices about whether the water has high or low concentrations 
of absorbing and scattering OACs. In coastal areas, resuspended sediments and runoff may cause 
strong absorption and scattering, toning the water brown or green, while open ocean areas with little 
Chl-a may show as areas with little absorption and appear as blue (Figure 2, a + b).  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The spectral Rrs distributed over the visible range of light can be inversely or directly 
proportional with the underlying OAC concentration, depending on the water body studied. For high 
latitude waters in the North Atlantic, namely west of Spitsbergen, a study showed that the intensity of 
Rrs signals for wavelengths at around 400 to 550 nm was inversely proportional with Chl-a (Figure 2 c) 
(Konik et al., 2020). In that range, a high reflectance indicated a low Chl-a concentration in the water. 
In contrast to this, at wave lengths between 650 and 700 nm, the reflectance was directly proportional 
with Chl-a, so that a high reflectance indicated high concentrations of Chl-a.  
This relationship between OACs and the measurement of ocean colour is limited to the upper 
10-20 m surface layers of the water body, while subsurface blooms and under-ice blooms cannot be 
accounted for with this measure (Ardyna et al., 2013; Arrigo et al., 2012). Technical limitations to 
satellite measurements are primarily cloud cover and sun glint, while daylight also is a prerequisite for 
ocean colour monitoring.

3. Study Area 
3.1 Range of the area and provinces 
The area of interest covers a vast area with diverse oceanographic conditions (Figure 3). It starts in the 
south with the Atlantic Ocean, covering the Arctic and going all the way north to polar waters. It 
reaches from the Canadian east coast in the west (78 °W) to the European shelf in the east (0 °E), and 
from 57 ° N to 72 ° N. Thereby, the area stretches over several marine biogeographical provinces, 
including the boreal polar province, the Atlantic Arctic, and the Atlantic sub-Arctic (Longhurst, 2007). 
Coastal areas around Norway and Great Britain were trimmed away to minimize coastal influences.

3.2 Major Basins and currents in the North Atlantic 
The Atlantic Meridional Overturning Circulation (AMOC) which transports water masses throughout 
the entire Atlantic controls the hydrogeography of the study area (Figure 3). On the southern boundary 
of the area, the North Atlantic Drift (NAD) transports warm and salty Atlantic Ocean waters northwards. 
As a major current, the North Atlantic Current (NAC) flows between Iceland and Great Britain (GB), 
touching the Rockall area northwest of GB. The NAC feeds several smaller branches along the way. One 
branch travels westward south of Iceland and north of the Iceland Basin to form the Irminger Current 
(IC). Another branch travels between Iceland and the Faroe Islands. Along the path of the NAC, smaller 
branches turn westward into the highly dynamic area of the Nordic Seas and the Greenland Sea. Along 
the Norwegian coast, one part follows the shelf and turns eastward into the Barents Sea. The major part 
continues northward, going west of Spitsbergen where it forms the West Spitsbergen Current (WSC). 
While a minor part turns southwards into the Greenland Sea, the dominating part enters the Arctic Ocean 
through the major gateway into the Arctic Ocean, the Fram Strait.  
In contrast to this, an Arctic outflow of cold and relatively fresh water leaves the Arctic Ocean 
through Fram Strait and travels southwards, bound along the shelf break, forming the East Greenland 
Current (EGC). With small portions going into the dynamic area of the Greenland and Nordic Sees the 
way continues southwards, and wraps around the southern tip of Greenland, Cape Farewell (Buch, 2000). 
With more Atlantic influence as well as freshwater runoff from the Greenland Ice Sheet, it further follows 
the Greenlandic coast, now travelling northwards as the West Greenland Current (WGC) (Rysgaard et 
al., 2020). The WGC dissolves as the way continues northwards through Davis Strait and into Baffin 
Bay. Here, polar waters from the Arctic Ocean enter through Nares Strait and through the Canadian 
Arctic Archipelago (CAA). In northern Baffin Bay, the North Water Polynya is the largest polynya in 
the Arctic, which is largely ice free all throughout the year due to wind and tides. It creates a warm 
microclimate and serves as rest and retreat for several marine mammals as well as whales.  
The waters merging in Baffin Bay travel southwards again as the Baffin Current (BC) and further 
south as the Labrador Current (LC). Along the way, terrestrial impact increases, as water leaving the 
CAA carries runoff and meltwater. The water which warms along its path continues south to eventually 
meet the Atlantic again.

4. Literature study 
The global ocean is subject to climate change and warming due to anthropogenic forcing (Barnett et al., 
2001). Especially in the Arctic, substantial changes happen in a fast pace and models show an Arctic 
Amplification (Serreze et al., 2009). Temperature is increasing (Straneo & Heimbach, 2013), sea ice 
decreases in volume and coverage, and the ice-free season expands (Kwok et al., 2009, Lindsay & 
Schweiger, 2015). The melting of the Greenland Ice Sheet (GrIS) adds freshwater supply to the subpolar 
and polar North Atlantic (Bamber et al., 2018), while melting of permafrost adds freshwater and a surplus 
of terrestrial originating organic material to the Arctic Ocean (Stedmon et al., 2011). Moreover, a general 
accumulation of freshwater occurs due to changes in precipitation and runoff (Haine et al., 2015). As the 
Arctic overall is warming, the subpolar North Atlantic experiences oceanographic changes. The decline 
in sea ice in the Arctic together with large scale climate change induces a cooling of the area which in 
turn leads to a weakening of the AMOC (Rahmstorf et al., 2015; Sévellec et al., 2017). Simultaneously, 
a northward intrusion of subtropical Atlantic waters into subpolar regions results in a warming and 
salinification of the area (Desbruyères et al., 2021; Sarafanov et al., 2008). These counteracting changes 
between the North Atlantic and the Arctic Ocean assign a special interest to the corridor between the two 
areas. In the face of climate change, the interest in using ocean colour as indicator for change is growing 
(Dutkiewicz et al., 2019; Groom et al., 2019; Sathyendranath et al., 2019). Climate change impacts 
optical constituents, and at the same time, ocean colour can reveal oceanographic changes in currents 
and the like. Both phytoplankton and CDOM play major roles in the global carbon cycle, and, moreover, 
they are the fundament for higher trophic levels (Richardson & Schoeman, 2004), and, hereby, go all the 
way to commercially harvested stocks. Changes can be observed already, where oceanic fronts move and 
prey availability initiates a move of larger fish species (Mackenzie et al., 2014). The need for ecosystem-
based management approaches is growing and this includes the use of ocean colour as indicator for 
productive areas and for tracking oceanographic changes (Chassot et al., 2007; Schmidt et al., 2019; 
Stuart et al., 2011). Therefore, ocean colour has been recognized as an essential climate variable (ECV) 
by the Global Climate Observing System and is followed closely by the Ocean Colour Climate Change 
Initiative (OC-CCI) under the European Space Agency (ESA).  
 
In the meanwhile, the technics to analyse and interpret ocean colour data has come a long way, 
and long timeseries with inter-calibration of sensors are available. While shipborne ocean colour data 
often is gathered parallel to in situ sampled for verification, satellite borne data is the most abundant data 
source. In the past three decades, satellite imagery of ocean colour has improved substantially, both in 
terms of sensors and coverage, and this enables an analysis of ocean colour at a time scale close to 
climatic time scales.  
 
Studies often use algorithms to derive measures of CDOM or chlorophyll-a (Chl-a) as proxy for 
phytoplankton based on the remote sensing reflectance satellite data. More recently, these algorithms are 
under growing critics due to overlap in absorbance range of the different OACs and a risk for mistaking 
one for the other (Arrigo & van Dijken, 2015; Gonçalves-Araujo et al., 2018; Lewis & Arrigo, 2020). 
Further, studies so far have often focused on the Arctic Ocean or regional parts of the North Atlantic 
(Arrigo et al., 2017; Arrigo & van Dijken, 2015; Frajka-Williams & Rhines, 2010; Marchese et al., 2017; 
Martinez et al., 2011), while a comprehensive study of recent ocean colour change in the Subarctic North 
Atlantic in its entirety is missing. What can be found in the literature at hand, are plenty of studies about

phytoplankton and CDOM in parts of the region, giving indications for patterns in ocean colour and

Reported changes in CDOM are mostly in the allochthonous pool, where the melting of 
permafrost induces an increase of terrestrial born material in riverine outflow to the Arctic Ocean. The 
Arctic Ocean receives 11 % of the freshwater volume while only making up for 1 % of the global ocean 
(Shiklomanov, 1993). The bio-optics of the Arctic Ocean are dominated by CDOM as the major non-
water optical component (Gonçalves-Araujo et al., 2018) and this is in turn closely linked to freshwater 
(Granskog et al., 2012). Generally, the outflow of the Arctic Ocean is fresher than the water it meets in 
the North Atlantic (Dodd et al., 2012), and the changes to the input to the Arctic Ocean, might 
consequently also appear in the outflow through Fram Strait and the Canadian Archipelago. Yet, it has 
not been clearly measured in the field, and the question remains how the surplus in freshwater will move 
on and whether it will leave the Arctic Ocean through the Fram Strait (Rabe et al., 2013). The study of 
ocean colour in the outflow regions in the past 20 years might add to the list of indices to enhance the 
understanding of that question.  
 
In addition to the allochthonous CDOM pool, in the open ocean most of the CDOM is 
autochthonous as it is linked to phytoplankton (Romera-Castillo et al., 2010). Phytoplankton in the study 
area falls into two biomes, where the Arctic biome exhibits one annual bloom, namely the summer bloom, 
while the subpolar and mid-latitudinal biomes have the potential to exhibit two blooms per year, which 
are a spring bloom and a summer or fall bloom. Globally it has been found that the integrated net primary 
production (NPP) decreases, which was found through analysing satellite data on a decadal time scale 
(Behrenfeld et al., 2006). Looking more closely, the global warming has opposing effects depending on 
the limiting factors at different latitudes which also plays into a more flexible understanding of 
boundaries between ecological provinces (Reygondeau et al., 2013). 
 
In low latitudes, where production generally is nutrient limited, the shallowing of the mixed layer 
depth (MLD) reduces nutrient flux and thereby the NPP. In high latitudes, where vertical mixing is 
predominant, a stratification allows for a stable mixed layer with sufficient nutrients and light which 
results in an increased NPP (Doney, 2006). In the meanwhile, different studies on this topic do not match 
into one dominating trend. A study of ocean colour data from 1998 to 2012 found an increase of NPP in 
the Arctic Ocean, but a decrease in outflowing regions (Arrigo & van Dijken, 2015), recommending an 
extension of the study region southwards to allow for a better understanding of the dynamics. In contrast 
to this, another study predicts an increase in NPP in the North Atlantic using an ecosystem model 
(Sarmiento et al., 2004) which is supported by a study using both another study on ocean colour data and 
a coupled ecosystem-ocean circulation model, finding an increase in phytoplankton productivity in the 
subarctic Atlantic (Harrison et al., 2013). 
 
The ambiguity in the listed studies illuminates some of the challenges when working with ocean 
colour data. The binning of subareas with diverging trends (Arrigo & van Dijken, 2015) might be one 
reason for the disagreement, while another factor is varying data quality, dependent on different 
generations of satellite sensors as well as interpretation algorithms. The importance of a regionally 
adjusted analysis of a recent time series becomes evident here.  
 
Apart from the abundance and productivity of the phytoplankton changing, the phenology of the 
phytoplankton bloom and occurrence is under change. In the polar region the decrease of sea ice and the

increased glacial meltwater runoff allows for a widening of the growing season (Oliver et al., 2018). This 
was amongst others found by Kahru et al., (2011) who found that 11 % of the Arctic Ocean phytoplankton 
blooms occur up to 50 days earlier, while they occur later in the subpolar North Atlantic. The sea ice 
melts earlier, which allows for light penetration and earlier solar induced warming. Phytoplankton 
blooms occur earlier and are followed by a mismatch of the zooplankton with potential consequences for 
the marine food web (Hansen et al., 2003; Møller & Nielsen, 2020). The novel phenology of 
phytoplankton provokes a rearrangement in community structure. The heterogeneity of the study area 
encompasses different directions of change which is reflected in literature. While some authors point at 
a northwards expansion of a subarctic plankton community (Glen Harrison et al., 2013; Neukermans et 
al., 2018; Oziel et al., 2020), others argue for a change in size distribution with a benefit for smaller 
phytoplankton groups (Marinov et al., 2010) and not a northward elongation of the present community 
(Kalenitchenko et al., 2019).  
 
Different phytoplankton types have different ecosystem services, and, thus, a change in 
community structure may have far reaching effects in both carbon sequestration, as well as cascading 
effects such as fisheries and the conservation of marine mammals. Diatoms are the dominating groups in 
the biological carbon pump as their size results in high sinking rates. Smaller groups would have less 
potential for sinking and are more likely to be recycled in the upper water layers. As a result, changes in 
phytoplankton composition may have strong effects on the global climate system, as the biological 
carbon pump plays an important role (IPCC, 2013). In situ studies of species composition are very limited 
due to the remoteness of the area. Therefore, the remote identification of phytoplankton functional types 
based on satellite data experiences growing interest in research. Recent studies test new approaches in 
using ocean colour data for studying the functional types of phytoplankton. A non-parametric approach 
of multivariate statistics has been used, applying an empirical orthogonal function analysis on ocean 
colour remote sensing reflectance data to identify phytoplankton functional types (Bracher et al., 2015). 
A review of the situation around determining phytoplankton diversity from ocean colour measurements, 
showed major deficiencies such as mismatch between in situ data, model predictions, and remotely 
sensed data, but also the restricted applicability of algorithms for the use of phytoplankton diversity 
analyses (Bracher et al., 2017). While the authors point at potential solutions it becomes clear the that 
the field is still in its fledgling stage.

5. Methods and Technical Background 
5.1 Data 
The ocean colour data used for this project was the remote sensing reflectance product version 5.0 
provided by the Ocean Colour Climate Change Initiative (OC-CCI) based under the European Space 
Agency (ESA) (Sathyendranath et al., 2019). It was chosen to work with remote sensing reflectance data 
rather than a specific ocean colour product such as Chl-a values, to allow for an analysis without 
algorithms, assumptions, and a priori introduced bias towards the interpretation of the signals in different 
wave bands. 
 
The product provided by ESA was merged from five different satellite components: Aqua-
MODIS (Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer on Aqua), MERIS (Medium Resolution 
Imaging Spectrometer on ENVISAT), SeaWiFS (Sea-viewing Wide Field-of-view Sensor on Orb-View-
2), OLCI (Ocean and Land Colour Instrument on Sentinel-3), and VIIRS (Visible Infrared Imaging 
Radiometer Suite). To be join the different wavebands and latitudinal coverages to one product, OC-CCI 
uses MERIS as reference sensor, and data from the other satellites was band-shifted and bias-corrected 
to match the MERIS configurations. In the OC-CCI product, six different wavebands are represented: 
412 nm, 443 nm 490 nm 510 nm, 560 nm 665 nm, with a resolution of 4 km per pixel. Limitation of 
satellite data were amongst others cloud cover which was filtered for in the OC-CCI process, which also 
included binning, flagging and atmospheric corrections. 
 
5.2 Time span 
The time frame used in this product was from 2001 to 2020 to obtain the most recent time series possible 
while ensuring a good data coverage. The five satellites contributed differently to the time series. 
SeaWiFS contributed from 2001 to 2011, MERIS from 2002 to 2012, MODIS from 2002 to 2020, VIIRS 
from 2012 to 2020, and OLCI from 2016 to 2010 (Figure 4). The observations of all sensors are summed 
in the OC-CCI product which is reflected in the total number of observations which increases over the 
time span from 2001 to 2020.  
 
5.3 Choice of season 
There was a strong seasonal variation in the area of interest due to sea ice coverage hindering detection 
of light reflection from the sea surface, as well as daylight which is a prerequisite for the collection of 
optical data. This resulted in a varying number of observations throughout the year (Figure 5). Generally, 
the number of observations was lowest in January and largest in July. For this study it was chosen to 
include May, June, July, August, and September, to make sure that sea ice coverage had retreated and 
the ocean colour of the area of interest had become largely visible.

5.4 Retrieving the raw data 
The data retrieval started with net-cdf files provided by the OC-CCI. Due to the large file size of about 
three gigabyte, cloud computing was used to gather all files. All further data processing was done in 
MATLAB (versions R2020a, R2020b, R2021a, R20222a) and Microsoft Excel. Initial data investigation 
included plotting and summary statistics, where outliers were determined and managed by excluding the 
upper 90th percentile of the data set. The area selection was refined by excluding coastal areas.  
 
5.5 Trend analysis 
For all pixels, pixelwise regressions were done, using first order linear regressions, and evaluated for 
significance with a Pearson correlation test. Due to small numbers of measurements for some pixels, the 
likelihood of finding significant regressions was high. To elevate the confidence, larger groupings of 
pixels with similar trends were gathered as subareas and defined as rectangular sub-selections of pixels. 
Once the definition of subareas was done, monthly averages were calculated, and a trend analysis was 
done on the averages with regression analysis tools and significant testing in Microsoft Excel.

6.2 Correlations between wave bands 
A correlation analysis between Rrs at difference wavelengths was performed (Figure 7). Due to the large

6.3 Overall patterns in RRS412 for each month and all years 
When averaged over all months and years, the pattern of the Rrs(412 nm) showed highest values in Baffin 
Bay and southeast of Cape Farewell as well as in a minor patch in Iceland Sea, with up to 0.005 sr-1 
(Figure 8). The lowest values of about 0.003 sr-1 were found in Nares Strait, south of Disko Bay, in 
southeast Labrador Sea, as well as in Fram Strait, around Iceland and along the Greenland-Scotland 
Ridge. The map of the overall standard deviation (SD) indicated where there was largest variability in 
Rrs(412 nm), which was focused mainly in Labrador, Irminger, Greenland and Iceland Seas as well as in 
the Rockall area.  
 
The maps of monthly means showed the lowest Rrs(412 nm) in May for West Greenland (Figure 
8) after which reflectance increased until August and decreased again in September. In contrast, 
Greenland Sea was characterized by two minima, in June and in September, while having the highest 
values in July. Irminger Sea showed two peaks, in May and July respectively, while Iceland Basin and 
Rockall area were highest in June. The Iceland Faroe Ridge was clearly highest in May and decreased 
continuously from then. 
 
The overall trend in monthly averages for the entire study area showed a decreasing trend (Figure 
8 and Figure 9). From 2001 to 2007 a stable pattern with some annual variation could be seen. From 
2008 to 2011 a minor decrease was seen, and from 2012 and onwards a persistent decrease was found 
(Figure 8). There was a pronounced seasonality with an increase of the Rrs(412 nm) values over early 
summer and a decrease after the peak in August.  
 
6.3.1 Temporal trends in RRS412 
Pixelwise regressions revealed significantly decreasing trends in Rrs(412 nm) (p<0.01) for large parts of 
the study area (-0.0002 sr-1 yr-1) which was largely driven by August and September (Figure 9). The 
decrease was generally most pronounced in three area assemblages: Irminger Sea and Iceland Basin, 
Baffin Bay and Davis Strait, Greenland Sea and Nordic Seas. The eastern Greenland Sea showed a 
significant reduction over the time series, which was visible mostly in July and August. Decreases in 
Irminger Sea could be seen mostly in May, June and September, while reductions in Rrs(412 nm) in 
Iceland Basin where most prevalent in July, August and September. Changes in Labrador Sea were in 
May and July, while Baffin Bay decreased most in August and September. All decreases had low 
explained variance (R2 < 0.2). In contrast, there was an increase of 0.0002 sr-1 yr-1 in the Rockall area 
and in a smaller patch in Greenland Sea. This increase was largely due to higher Rrs(412 nm) in June and 
July and for these months, the regressions had an R2 of ~0.6 (Figure 9).

quite diverse hydrogeographic characteristics and hold different water masses. The generally low R2 
values indicated that the correlation to time was not linear, and altogether a look into how the decreases

7. Discussion 
7.1 General findings 
It was found that wave band signals were highly correlated when close to each other. 412, 443, 490 and 
510 nm were each similar in patterns to their neighbouring wave bands, where 412 and 443 nm, as well 
as 490 and 510 nm could be presented interchangeably for overall patterns. Rrs(560 nm) and Rrs(665 nm) 
had to be seen for themselves as their variable distribution as well as their geographical patterns deviated 
from the remaining wave bands. Generally, geographical high intensity patterns in Rrs(665 nm) were 
accompanied by high standard deviations which indicated high variability and potentially changes in the 
relevant areas. This wave band is expected to primarily represent phytoplankton abundance.  
 
The same pattern of high intensity and high variability was also present while much less 
pronounced for 412 nm. Rrs(412 nm) was interpreted as indicator for organic material (from both CDOM 
and phytoplankton), and the patterns found fitted with that interpretation. Rrs(443 nm) resembled the 
patterns of Rrs(412 nm), largely, underlining the overlap in optical properties for phytoplankton and other 
organic material. The intermediate wave bands of 490 and 510 nm could not as clearly be attributed to 
optically active constituents while their geographical patterns still looked similar to their neighbouring 
wave bands. Therefore, they might also be associated to phytoplankton.  
 
In contrast to the other four wave bands, 560 nm is generally the wavelength with least absorbance 
in seawater and is therefore in other studies often used to normalize towards an underlying baseline 
signal. Interestingly, the patterns for Rrs(560 nm) found here showed high intensity in phytoplankton 
bloom areas, and moreover, the regressions indicated a change in areas where Rrs(665 nm) also changed 
significantly, namely in eastern Labrador Sea and in Rockall area. Therefore, the use of Rrs(560 nm) as 
a baseline signal is thought to introduce a skewing into some areas, while not affecting others. This 
matches the discourse found in recent literature, reconsidering the applicability of standard normalization 
algorithms in the high latitudinal Atlantic (Gonçalves-Araujo et al., 2018). This highlights a need for 
regionally adapted algorithms and feeds into the improvement of new analysis strategies, such as 
variability based machine learning approaches (Bracher et al., 2015).

and Rrs(665 nm), and are summarised in Table 1. In short, phytoplankton and associated organic material 
seemed to increase West of Spitsbergen, in Iceland Basin, Labrador Sea East, and Greenland Sea, while 
it decreased in Rockall area. No major changes could be found for phytoplankton in Baffin Bay and 
Irminger Sea, while Rrs(412 nm) decreased and indicated an increase of organic material. In the 
following, each subarea will be compared to literature in order to discuss and evaluate on the findings.

7.2.1 West of Spitsbergen and Greenland Sea 
The area West of Spitsbergen showed a quadrupling of Rrs(665 nm) for June and July which indicates a 
strong increase in the summer bloom in this area. September also showed a significant increase of about 
doubling, which may indicate the beginning of a fall bloom. Rrs(412 nm) showed a halving and a 
transition towards continued low reflectance levels after a minimum in June. This matches the indications 
of a beginning fall bloom, and the low reflectance signals indicate high levels of absorbance of 
phytoplankton associated organic material, such as detritus.  
 
The indices for an increased phytoplankton bloom during summer can be supported by studies 
which found a similar trend for 1991-2011 (Cherkasheva et al., 2014; Nöthig et al., 2015), while 
simultaneously identifying a transition towards smaller phytoplankton groups, namely from diatoms to 
flagellates. Generally, the bio-optical properties of the eastern Fram Strait region west of Spitsbergen is 
dominated by phytoplankton as found by Kowalczuk et al., (2019). They analysed water samples and 
found that 676 nm was the best suited wave band as optical proxy for studying Chl-a in this high 
latitudinal setting, which supports the interpretation of the Rrs(665 nm) data in the study at hand.  
 
In addition, another study found that the amount of Chl-a did not change from 2009-2017 when 
integrated for the upper water layer from 0 to 100 m depth (Engel et al., 2019). Together with the results 
discussed here, this supports a change in the phytoplankton composition while the amount of Chl-a may 
stay the same. Moreover, the study by Engel et al. (2019) highlights that the concentrations of dissolved 
organic carbon (DOC) could be correlated with temperature and salinity, showing that the outflow of the 
Central Arctic adds DOC to this region, while the concentrations of particulate organic carbon (POC)

could be linked to the abundance of phytoplankton. This underlines the caution which is needed when 
interpreting remote sensing reflectance in the wave lengths strongly impacted by organic material from 
both phytoplankton and external sources. The interpretation of Rrs therefore always has to be combined 
with knowledge about the regional hydrogeographic conditions.  
 
The adjacent subarea of the one in West Spitsbergen was a patch in Greenland Sea which showed 
a somewhat similar pattern to West of Spitsbergen with increases in Rrs(665 nm) and a tendency towards 
an earlier spring bloom, moving from June into May, while also developing a novel fall bloom. Unique 
for the area in Greenland Sea, it was outstanding for Rrs(412 nm) that July and August deviated from 
May, June, and September. Over the 20-year period this created a split between summer and the shoulder 
months which can be interpreted as a general increase in seasonality. This is highly interesting, as both 
the area West of Spitsbergen as well as the area in Greenland Sea hereby show a development from being 
commonly perceived as a high latitudinal system with one bloom per year, towards an ecosystem with 
two seasonal blooms.  
 
Generally, this fits well with the Atlantification and warming trends in the region. Earlier studies 
found that a bloom in central Greenland Sea developed in May and peaked in June for data from 1993-
1995 (Rey et al., 2011). More recent studies investigated the relationship between the phytoplankton 
spring bloom and the sea ice export from the Arctic Ocean, which moves through this subarea, and found 
that few years of larger than average sea ice export had earlier phytoplankton blooms, while other years 
with high sea ice export had no or negative phytoplankton anomalies (Mayot et al., 2020). Together with 
the ambiguity in different studies from the literature review, this underlines that the development in 
Greenland Sea does not show a persistent trend. Despite this, the study at hand points at an earlier bloom 
and the initiation of a fall bloom in September which is supported by a similar finding in a study from 
2014 (Ardyna et al.).  
 
7.2.2 Labrador Sea East 
The area in eastern Labrador Sea showed an increase in Rrs(665 nm) in all months but May, and a 
decrease in Rrs(412 nm) with lowest values in July. Together, this pointed towards an increase in the 
phytoplankton bloom intensity with a peak in July which could be seen as an inverse peak in the seasonal 
shape of Rrs(412 nm). Since the two wave bands matched well in seasonality, the  absorbance in the band 
of 412 nm is likely to be due to phytoplankton associated organic material. Trying to put the findings 
into perspective, the multifarious hydrogeography comes into the spotlight. The Labrador Sea is an area 
with a salinity gradient decreasing from the entrance in southeast towards the basin end and Davis Strait 
in northwest. Arctic surface waters and Atlantic intermediate waters flow into the basin and meet with 
polar waters from the north, so that the area has at least two biogeographical regions (Frajka-Williams & 
Rhines, 2010) with several local features such as a hotspot of Rrs(665 nm) off of Sisimiut (see Figure 
12). Several studies have shown that Greenlandic runoff may enhance phytoplankton blooms (Frajka-
Williams & Rhines, 2010; Oliver et al., 2018), which is well in line with the results of this study. Apart 
from that, Labrador Sea remains a very complex area with storms and deep convection during winter. A 
thorough monitoring and studying is needed to further understand the processes going on here and to 
follow how the changing climate might affect the biosphere in this region. Until further, the results found

in this study point towards an increase in phytoplankton blooms and may have potential effects for higher 
trophic levels.  
 
7.2.3 Iceland Basin and Irminger Sea 
In contrast to the areas of high phytoplankton abundance are the two subareas in Iceland Basin and 
Irminger Sea. Iceland Basin showed a large seasonal spread in Rrs(665 nm) with an increase of the 
reflectance signal in summer. Parallel to this, the Rrs(412 nm) values showed a decrease, which was 
significant for August and September. The seasonal shapes of Rrs(412 nm) and Rrs(665 nm) could not be 
matched, and while the Rrs(412 nm) decrease indicated an increase in organic material, the phytoplankton 
related Rrs(665 nm) did not increase accordingly. No geographically matching literature was found, while 
a study of an adjacent similar area showed the central Iceland Basin as a low chlorophyll area (Moore et 
al., 2005). Further, an iron limitation might limit the growth during summer (Nielsdóttir et al., 2009). 
The source of increased Rrs(412 nm) remains unclear, while the idea is near that for this region, this wave 
band might be more influenced by phytoplankton associated organic material, since no adjacent source 
for allochthonous organic material was present.  
The neighbouring subarea of Irminger Sea showed similarities with Iceland Basin. Meanwhile, the slight 
increase in Rrs(665 nm) was less spread out between the years, and the increase was only significant for 
September rather than June as it was in Iceland Basin.  
 
Further, the results for Irminger Sea indicated high levels of organic material in May and June 
with low Rrs(412 nm) signals, as well as a general decrease in Rrs(412 nm). In the meanwhile, Rrs(665 
nm) signals indicated only a minor phytoplankton bloom in June. In comparison with literature this fits 
well, as one study found the bloom in central Irminger Sea to usually be in May and June (Holliday et 
al., 2006). Generally, the area is considered a low Chl-a area due to its relative fresh and cold waters. 
Interestingly, the onset of the bloom could not be explained by simple thermal stratification only 
(Holliday et al., 2006). A modelling study highlighted that apart from hydrography and solar induced 
thermal stratification, the seasonality in physical and meteorological forcing of the specific area was part 
of the phytoplankton bloom control (Waniek & Holliday, 2006). This may also explain the large 
interannual variability in the data at hand, together with no clear seasonal bloom shape. In addition, the 
central Irminger Sea is surrounded by highly dynamic waters, especially towards the shelf break along 
the east coast of Greenland, where horizontal mixing takes place, when polar, sub-polar and sub-tropical 
waters meet. The front may move slightly from year to year and thereby contribute to the variability 
within Rrs data.  
 
7.2.4 Baffin Bay 
Another iteration of little change in Rrs(665 nm) and a decrease in the signal of Rrs(412 nm) could be 
found in the subarea in Baffin Bay. This region showed very little change in Rrs(665 nm) with a 
significant increase in September which could point towards an increase in a fall bloom system. Data 
coverage in May was very limited due to ice cover in some years, and an increase for the May was not 
significant. However, Rrs(412 nm) showed significant decreases in all months with the seasonal shape 
staying the same. This indicates increased absorbance by organic material, and since signal in Rrs(665

nm) had no significant changes in most months, the source of the organic material is likely to also be 
allochthonous.  
 
Although little is known about the freshwater fluxes and the permafrost loss in the Canadian 
Arctic (Lantuit et al., 2012; Spence & Burke, 2008), the signs of more allochthonous organic material 
are in line with general understanding of an increased input of terrestrial runoff throughout the Arctic 
(Stedmon et al., 2011; Stedmon et al., 2015). Generally, Baffin Bay is described as a biological 
productive area with eutrophic characteristics while exhibiting a decline in Chl-a (1998-2017) and 
primary production (Ardyna et al., 2011; Bélanger et al., 2013; Marchese et al., 2017). More specifically, 
one study attributes the decrease to a smaller cell size and, thereby, a shift in the phytoplankton 
community, going from nanoplankton towards picoplankton (Blais et al., 2017). Together with the 
increase in organic material absorbance, this draws the picture of an area in transition towards a new 
environmental state which future monitoring must follow closely to understand the environmental 
consequences.  
 
7.2.5. Rockall area 
In sharp contrast to all other subareas, the Rockall area, northwest of Scotland, exhibits clear signs of a 
decrease in phytoplankton production, as Rrs(665 nm) decreased and Rrs(412 nm) increased (apart from 
May) with a curious shift in that regime from 2013 and onwards. 
From 2001 to 2013, Rrs(665 nm) showed persistent decreases in all months, while from 2013 an onwards, 
May and June started to increase, while the remaining months continued to decrease. Simultaneously, in 
Rrs(412 nm) the trend showed an increased divergence amongst the months. May decreased significantly, 
pointing at more organic material, while the remaining months increased, hereof June and July 
significantly, pointing towards less organic material. This indicates a temporal shift in the phytoplankton 
bloom, moving towards May and away from June and July, while keeping in mind that the initial spring 
bloom in this region may be outside the seasonal scope of this study as it is on the southernmost edge of 
entire study area.  
 
The literature study showed that recent published data for this subarea is scarce. Nevertheless, 
the initial decrease in phytoplankton could be supported by findings of a decline in primary production 
from 2000 to 2006 in the Celtic Seas, including the Rockall area (ICES, 2022). In contrast to that, the 
regime shift from 2013 an onward could not be found in literature within the scope of this study which 
indicates a novel and yet under-researched development in the area.  
 
Generally, other studies characterize the area in terms of phytoplankton groups with a transition 
towards picophytoplankton and a current domination by flagellates, while diatoms also contribute (ICES, 
2022; Moore et al., 2005). Reconstructed absorption spectra underline that Rrs(412 nm) is inseparably 
linked with not only organic material but also phytoplankton associated absorbance, foremost the 
photosynthetic pigments.  
 
The reasons for the changes in the phytoplankton and organic material abundance are not trivially 
seen. However, large scale circulation deserves increased attention in this area. Studies show that the 
strength of the subpolar gyre system dominates the productivity of the region. When the subpolar gyre 
index is strong, the phytoplankton is low, while an inflated gyre promotes biological production (Hátún

blue whiting and pilot whales, illustrating the far-reaching impacts of the circulation pattern.  
 
7.3. Fisheries redistribution 
These highly impacting variations in the phytoplankton abundance together with trends indicating a 
phytoplankton community under change, set the future of fisheries northwest of Great Britain up for 
evaluation. As off now, highly productive fisheries can be found in the Rockall area. If the productivity 
persistently decreases in the Celtic waters, the fisheries yield may decrease and cause socioeconomic 
knock-on effects. At the same time, the phytoplankton productivity increases in the Eastern Fram Strait 
west of Spitsbergen and in the Labrador Sea, which may provoke a new assemblage of the fishing fleet 
between countries with fishing quota with potential consequences for the socioeconomic system built on 
this industry.  
 
One example already seen for a change in fish distribution and the connected fishery is the 
Atlantic mackerel (Scomber scombrus) fisheries around Iceland. Mackerel was no target species in 
Iceland waters and started as bycatch fisheries, but soon developed into a regular fishery due to new and 
sufficient abundance which could be linked to warming waters (Astthorsson et al., 2012). Moreover, 
explorative fishing for Atlantic mackerel further north, in east Greenlandic waters, found not only 
mackerel, but also bluefin tuna (Thunnus thynnus) (Mackenzie et al., 2014). This highlights that the effect 
of warming waters did not stop with one species, but also impacted the next trophic levels of predators 
migrating with their prey. Altogether, this emphasizes the picture of ecosystems under rapid change.  
 
8. Outlook and Perspectives 
The changes in phytoplankton in both phenology, abundance and community composition have to be 
studied further and, here, the phytoplankton functional types may serve with an additional understanding 
of the changing Sub-Arctic and Arctic North Atlantic. A recent approach to separate between 
phytoplankton groups is a variability-based data analysis approach such as a principal component 
analysis (PCA) which is generally similar to empirical orthogonal functions(EOF). Within the temporal 
and content wise scope of this study it was not possible to fully analyse and interpret a PCA. Nevertheless, 
a trial run has been done and resulted in six principal components (PCs). Most interestingly, the loadings 
of each component, which represent the spectral composition of the leading variability parameters, do 
highly resemble the EOFs of a published study by Bracher et al., (2015) (Figure 29). In their study the 
authors aimed to identify EOFs as certain phytoplankton groups by comparing to pigment analysis of 
water samples. For areas with major changes in the Rrs trends, an analysis of the PCs might give indices 
on how the phytoplankton community is changing and from that, the management of ecosystems and 
fisheries can be improved to further secure a sustainable use of ecosystem services.

9. Conclusion 
This study points out regions under change and may lead towards new sample sites and a more 
economical way of analysing ocean colour data in high latitudinal areas. For this study, remote sensing 
reflectance was analysed statistically without dependency on algorithms or other assumptions about the 
wave bands. The pixelwise regressions were an economic way of scanning for areas of change as no 
prior definition of areas of interest was needed. The grouping of significantly changing pixels into 
subareas gave confidence to the trend analysis and allowed to focus any further analysis on those 
interesting regions. 
 
It was found that the chosen subareas within the study region change differently on the overall 
scape. Despite this, when the change found was similar in two subareas, the driving factors were different 
and seemed to be dominated by hydrogeography. The results support the idea of the Atlantification of 
Arctic North Atlantic. All the way north to Fram Strait, the ocean colour indicated a change from an 
Arctic single bloom system towards a two-bloom system building up. In contrast to this, at the same 
latitude west of Greenland, in Baffin Bay, the results did not indicate clear phytoplankton trends. 
However, a strong increase in signals of organic material could be found which might be an expression 
of permafrost degradation in Northern Canada.  
 
The interpretation of the seen changes can now be taken to new studies and asks for  comparison 
with oceanographic data and observed changes in fishing sites. The hotspots are shown in the analysis 
for the changing subarctic and arctic North Atlantic. A combination with a variability lead analysis 
including all six original wave bands may add to the understanding of phytoplankton community changes 
and further study of the principal component analysis is suggested.

Ratios to 560 
In the region of about 560 nm the water column is most transparent. This is often used as a way to 
normalize the changes occurring in other regions, removing effects of scattering on RRS. The signal is 560 
nm is recognized as having the least contribution of phytoplankton and CDOM, and can as such make up for 
a baseline signal  
 
Baseline changes for 560 
It was found that the intensity of the signal at 560 nm has significant changes. It was found that the 
changes in different areas occur at different months. In May, spotted pixels increased in Nares Strait, Baffin 
Bay and the northern Labrador Sea, as well as in Fram Strait, Greenland Sea, west of Spitsbergen and north 
of Iceland. Decreases in May could be seen in the Nordic Seas and in the Rockall area.  
In June, the increases were more enhanced in Nares Strait, the southern Labrador Sea and very pronounced 
area in West of Spitsbergen. In addition, a new decrease was identified along the southeast coast of 
Greenland. In July, the increase in Labrador Sea wrapped along the shelf southwest off of Cape Farewell. 
The increase in West Spitsbergen area was smaller in area, while being followed by a decrease in western 
Greenland Sea. In Iceland Sea a new patch of an increase was seen, while another phase of decrease was 
seen in July. 
In August, the decrease in Rockall area expanded, and the Labrador Sea increase expanded too. In addition, 
Baffin Bay was now characterized by a few pixels showing a significant decrease. In the final month, 
September, a widespread but scattered increase could be seen in Davis Strait, Labrador Sea, Nordic Seas, 
Greenland Sea and Fram Strait, while a decrease could still be seen in Rockall area and Baffin Bay. 
 
When the monthly averages for subareas were overlaid each other to follow the development in 
seasonality without taking significant regressions into account, it could be seen, that for Baffin Bay, a 
decrease in most months and a shallowed seasonality was seen. In Greenland Sea, an increase in May, 
June, and September, while a decrease in July and August, made up for a sharper seasonal peak in June. In 
West Spitsbergen, a clear increase in June and July enhanced a seasonal peak. In Irminger Sea a decrease 
could be seen, while Iceland Basin showed a small increase. Rockall area was characterized by a decrease 
which was most pronounced in July and August which resulted in a sharpened seasonal peak in June, where 
before the peak in June were followed by a slower decrease in July and August.  
In Labrador Sea an increase could be seen, which was more pronounced in the eastern part of the Labrador 
Sea with enhanced seasonal variation. Finally, in the western part of Irminger Sea, a decrease could be 
seen.  
 
Overall, the signal of 560 nm was object to change in all subareas, both in significant regressions as well as 
in seasonality trends. Especially the West Spitsbergen area, the Baffin Bay, the Rockall area and the 
Labrador Sea East area showed strong changes in deviating directions, where the West Spitsbergen and 
Labrador Sea showed increase, Rockall areas and Baffin Bay showed decreases.

Remarks on the ratio analysis 
Since the signal of 560 nm itself is changing over the study period, with different directions 
(increase/decrease) and in different areas, the effect of normalizing with 560 nm is different for 
different regions and subareas.  
When dividing by the averages of 560 nm for each month and each area it can be seen that the 
magnitude of the different months is affected differently. The ranking of the intensity of the signle 
months is changed and the interpretation of the seasonality of the waveband at hand is challenged.  
Not only is that the case, but also do the pixelwise regressions of 560 nm over time indicate changes 
in areas such as Labrador Sea, West Spitsbergen area and Rockall area. These areas undergo 
changes in the wavebands linked to the major optical constituents, foremost the waveband 665 nm 
indicating chlorophyll and such phytoplankton. The reason could be scattering. But this means that 
a normalization with 560 cannot be done for the entire study region, but needs local/regional 
adjustment in order to further interpret the results.
