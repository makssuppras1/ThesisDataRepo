Abstract
Class-D ampliﬁers are very eﬃcient but are far more sensitive to disturbances at
the supply than class A and class A/B ampliﬁers are. This is a common problem
in hearing aids, since signal ﬁdelity is extremely important, and since it is costly to
ﬁlter the supply in a compact
This work investigate several solutions for such problems, and proposes the use
of pulse-width modulation (PWM) feedback to compensate the disturbances by
correcting the duty cycle of each pulse in the PWM signal.
The low power requirement for hearing aid applications sets up an interesting
challenge for this work, and gives rise to a fully diﬀerential solution. The pro-
posed solution is implemented in a 65 nm process and simulated in order to verify
performance.
The system uses 200 µA and the output signal has an signal-to-noise ratio (SNR)
of 105 dB and increases the power supply rejection ratio (PSRR) of the class-D
ampliﬁer with 15 dB. This means that the ampliﬁer becomes less aﬀected by noise.
The system has a switching frequency of 250 kHz.

Introduction
This project is composed of a literature study, a suggested solution and the work
on designing and implementing the solution, while this report documents the work
done.
GN ReSound has implemented hardware in their products that creates high
frequency (above the audible range) disturbances in the battery supply. In this project
the eﬀects of these disturbances on the hearing aid’s performance are investigated.
The consequences of these disturbances can be audible, and degrades the system
SNR. It goes to show, that the current system is not durable, and causes the hearing
aid designers to worry and carefully consider changes made to the system.
Besides quality of sound, the most critical aspects of a hearing aid’s performance
is the battery life-time, which is related to the hardware power consumption. Class-D
ampliﬁers have a high power eﬃciency over a large modulation index [1] and [2],
which is why Class-D ampliﬁers always are used in hearing aids. A depiction of the
hearing aid system is illustrated by ﬁgure 1.1, where the signal vi(t) is the analog
signal generated by an external transducer, vi[n] is the sampled digital version of
vi(t), this signal is processed by a nonlinear digital signal processor (DSP) algorithm
and produces the discrete signal r[n], which is encoded by a delta-sigma modulator
(∆ΣM) into a single bit pulse-density modulation (PDM) signal called y[n], this
signal is then converted into a continues time signal by some buﬀer, that drives
the H-bridge with the signal y(t). An unregulated class-D power stage can achieve
very good performance if the supply rails are well regulated [3], but large changes in
current drawn from the supply will cause disturbances (Vdist) in the ampliﬁer output
(Vo), this is especially true when the ampliﬁer is supplied directly from a zink-air
battery, which has a larger output impedance than lithium-ion batteries, typically in
the order of 10 Ω.

Another critical aspect of a hearing aid’s performance is the the system SNR. A
low SNR makes the apparatus useless for helping the auditory system, especially
for hearing disabled. It is interesting to investigate the possibility to implement a
system that maintain a high signal ﬁdelity, even when the unregulated supply is
disturbing the ampliﬁer, while consuming as little power as possible.
Since the hearing aid must ﬁt in an ear, large components take up critical space
from the device. This limits the available area for the implementing this project,
and illiminates the possibility for external components.
The report will put a large focus on proposing a well considered solution, and carry
out the theory for designing and implementing such system, while also documenting
the performance using simulations.

2
Problem
The purpose of this master’s thesis projects is to investigate various solutions to
increase the PSRR for a class-D output stage in a hearing aid, and suggest a CMOS
integrated circuit (IC) low power solution for linearization of the class-D power stage
in a hearing aid.
The signal controlling a class-D stage is a pulse width modulated (PWM) signal.
Supply disturbances are modulated directly into the output signal resulting in noise
and distortion in the output signal.
The disturbances should be suppressed using feed-back. An analog system, or a
combined analog and digital system, for suppression of noise and distortion should be
suggested. A model of the system should be developed in Matlab or Cadence VHDL-
AMS. The model can contain behavioral blocks but it should contain transistor
implementation for critical blocks.
This work should resolve the questions:
• Can the output stage become less susceptible to disturbances using very little
power, very little area and while adding very little noise to the system.

3.1
Topology study
Based on research three common solutions have been found. Each has advantages
and disadvantages that will be summarized in section 3.4. All of the solutions use
feedback for compensation, and their loop gain is an important aspect, because a
large loop gain gives rise to a good PSRR. Two of the proposed solutions operate in
the analog domain, while the third compensates in the digital domain.
3.1.1
Pulse-width modulation (PWM) feedback
The disturbances introduced through the supply voltage, will be introduced via the
H-bridge. The H-bridge input signal is a well regulated digital signal.
Comparing the input PWM signal with the disturbed output signal gives the error
signal. The pulse-width modulation-feedback topology utilizes this, by integrating
the error and correcting the pulse-width over time in such a manner, that the average
energy in a pulse, is the same regardless of the disturbance. This technique is widely
utilized in the literature [4]–[8].
Figures 3.1 and 3.2 show this principle with a ﬁrst order integrator and negative
feedback. The integrated error signal (ho(t)) is quantized by a comparator, because
the H-bridge must be controlled by a logic signal (y(t)). This comparator inherently
has a well-deﬁned hysteresis window, that control the corrected output pulse-width
window, and ensure that the output PWM signal’s frequency is the same as the
input PWM signal. The corrected output PWM pulses become compensated in
pulse-width because of the integrated error. The compensated PWM pulses with
supply noise (vo(t)) does therefore contain the same energy in each period as the
PWM input signal (vi(t)). This is easily seen from the low pass output signal (the
audible signal vo,LP(t)), which is intended to be DC.

3.1.2
Self-oscillating (SO) ampliﬁer
SO ampliﬁers are becoming very popular in audio applications mainly because they
have inherently high loop gain, and thereby can suppress total harmonic distortion
(THD) and noise in the output stage [9].
The SO ampliﬁer topology is very similar to the PWM feedback. The error signal
is integrated to compensate for errors over time. The integrated error is quantized.
This is illustrated by ﬁgure 3.31.
The SO ampliﬁer’s PWM output signal’s (VPWM(t)) frequency is not intended
to be controlled by the input PWM signal, hence the name self-oscillating. This sets
the SO ampliﬁer topology apart from the PWM feedback topology, even though the
systems look the same.
 
Figure 3.3: General SO ampliﬁer model [10].
The SO can be designed to be free running, or be controlled by an external carrier,
Vext(t). The free running system do not get exposed to noise and jitter coupled in
from an external carrier signal. The drawback of the free running system is that the
switching frequency varies strongly with the input signal and drops to zero near the
full scale points [10].
Using a low jitter oscillator, requires an external crystal oscillator, since integrated
RC-oscillators does not have a high Q. So this solution is not optimal for low total
harmonic distortion + noise (THD + N), given by equation (3.1).
Using an internal oscillator could be an option since the system already has low noise
clocks. A free running SO with a triangular carrier (sliding) signal experience less
carrier related distortion and produces THD ﬁgures that are comparable to the state
of the art [11], but variable switching frequency is undesired in a hearing aid. Free
running oscillators are therefore undesirable.

3.5
Subconclusion
Based on the pros and cons summarized in section 3.4 a topology is chosen, by ruling
out the rest. As mentioned in section 3.1, the SO ampliﬁer and PWM feedback
topologies are similar. The main diﬀerence is that the SO ampliﬁer can achieve
larger loop gain, and thereby a higher PSRR, but only by increasing the switching
frequency. This is not advantageous because it leads to larger switching losses, while
the PSRR easily can be increased for PWM feedback solutions, by increasing the
ﬁlter order, as shown by ﬁgure 3.13.
The calculated FOM for the suggested ADC is lower than the state of the
current art mentioned by [14]. It is therefore not feasible to to meet the requirement
speciﬁcation of section 2.2 using DSPC. It must be taken into consideration that ADC
converters become more and more eﬃcient over time. This conclusion is therefore
strongly dependent on the evolution of data converters, which might be an obvious
choice in the future.
It is not an easy task to map the speciﬁcation ﬁgures that can be obtained using
a PWM feedback solutions, based on the literature alone, since the applications for
the reported solutions [4]–[8] are less concerned with power consumption than the
speciﬁcations for this work. It is therefore concluded that a deeper investigation of
the PWM feedback solution is required, in order to get a better understanding of
the PWM feedback solution. This will be carried out in chapter 4.

Future work
This section highlights important topics that must be investigated further.
8.1
Hysteretic comparetor
In this project the hysteretic comparator was implemented using an ideal model. A
topology must be chosen and implemented.
8.2
Simulations
Process corners has not been considered in any of these simulations, due to a limited
amount of time. These must be considered and their eﬀects evaluated.
Especially ”slow” and ”fast” corners are expected to cause the system penalty
since it can aﬀect the integrator gain.
8.3
Layout
A layout of the system must be carried out for this design. Output referred oﬀset
must be minimized in this design, since it leads to oﬀset, which wastes power at DC
and heat up the receiving speaker, which is not AC coupled. Since the system has a
closed loop gain of 1, input related oﬀset will be equal to output related oﬀset. Oﬀset
in the input stage is therefore not critical. The system is therefore not sensitive to
mismatch in the layout. Though, it is recommended to match both input transistors
in the op-amp and the input resistors in the active RC-integrator, using a common
centroid layout as described in [15].
The design is sensitive to parasitic eﬀects from extra input resistance and extra
feedback capacitance in the active RC-integrator, and must therefore be veriﬁed with
parasitic extractions, to ensure loop gain and large signal behaviour.
8.4
Loop ﬁlter order
The system was implemented using a simple ﬁrst order loop ﬁlter, due to limitations
in time. The ﬁlter order can easily be extrapolated to achieve higher PSRR, without
changing the current design. The order of the loop ﬁlter inﬂuence the PSRR of the
closed loop system. For this reason eﬀects of using higher order loop ﬁlters should
be investigated. It is suggested to investegate and compare performance with loop
ﬁlters like the one suggested by [7] as illustrated by ﬁgure 3.9.

9
Conclusion
A literature study has been made in order to map the state of the art solutions within
the problem of this project. Three solutions proposed in literature has been discussed
here, and one has been implemented, namely the PWM feedback solution proposed
through the literature [4]–[8]. This topology is very similar to those proposed in [9],
[11]. This similarities and diﬀerences are discussed in this work.
A fully diﬀerential version of the PWM feedback solution has been proposed
by this work. The solution proposed by this work, has been analysed, modelled,
implemented and veriﬁed through this work.
The design has been implemented in TSMC65N, which is a process that could be
used for modern hearing aids. Minor blocks still need implementation on a transistor
level, and a layout must be designed for the system, in order to simulate with parasitic
extraction and estimate the required die area (Adie). It has been explained that the
design is not sensitive to bad layout penalties like mismatch, but it is suggested to
use common centroid for critical components, as also discussed in this work.
The design has been thoroughly veriﬁed by simulation, using Cadence 6.0.
It has been shown, that the output can become less susceptible to disturbances
using only 200 µA. The PSRR has been increased by 15.38 dB, and it has been shown
that this ﬁgure can be increased by expanding the loop ﬁlter order in section 4.4 or
by increasing the open loop DC gain of the designed op-amp.
Alternative solutions have been suggested and visited, through this work. It
has been suggested to use gmC-integratorﬁlters for the loop, in order to avoid noisy
input resistors. This suggestion has been discarded because the input range of
gmC-integratorﬁlters is not large enough for this application.
It has been suggested to investigate the use of a fully diﬀerential class A/B
ampliﬁer, in order to lower the power consumption for the RC-integrator.
A model describing the loop gain of non-linear ﬁrst order PWM ﬁlters has been
derived in this work. The model can be used for ﬁlters reported in [4]–[9], [11]. This
model works under the assumption that an ideal integrator is used. The model is
based on the math derived by [10].
A linearized model of the H-bridge is derived in this work and is used to make
an illustrative approach to ﬁnd the connection between high frequency disturbances
and the spectral noise in the audio band.
speciﬁcations conclusion: In this section the results from chapter 6 are summarized
and discussed with respect to the requirements in section 2.2. The system has been
shown to work over a voltage range of 900 mV to 1.30 mV, and a temperature range
of −10 ◦C to 55 ◦C. The SNR is guaranteed to be 105.45 dB assuming that the input
signal from the ∆ΣM is 109 dB. The required current has been estimated to be up
to 200 µA.
The op-amp designed for the RC-integrator was not designed with the speciﬁed
open loop gain (AOL). It has been shown that this aﬀects the systems ability to
suppress spectral disturbances. The PSRR was increased by 15 dB for this design.
