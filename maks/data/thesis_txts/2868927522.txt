Abstract
CD8+ T cells are essential components of antiviral immunity, eliminating virus-infected
cells through antigen-specific recognition and mediation of cytotoxic effector functions. A
deeper understanding of their activation, functionality, and cytotoxic potential is crucial
for advancing immunological research and therapeutic strategies. Therefore, this study
aimed to establish an in vitro killing assay to assess the cytotoxicity of peptide-specific
CD8+ T cells, while also investigating their functionality and activation profiles through
complementary analyses. Peptide-specific CD8+ T cells were successfully expanded
from donor peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs), demonstrating that even low-
frequency T cell populations could undergo substantial proliferation under suitable condi-
tions. These expanded CD8+ T cells were characterized using peptide-MHC multimers to
quantify antigen-specific populations. Their functionality and ability to become activated
upon antigenic stimulation were confirmed through intracellular cytokine staining (ICS)
and activation-induced marker (AIM) assays. Furthermore, a killing assay was estab-
lished and optimized to assess the cytotoxic capabilities of the peptide-expanded CD8+
T cells. While the cells demonstrated target cell killing, the level of reproducibility var-
ied, highlighting the need for further assay optimization. In summary, this study provides
insights into peptide-specific CD8+ T cell responses and offers a framework for refining
future killing assays to minimize variability.

Introduction
The manifestation of novel infectious diseases in humans can constitute a great threat to
public health. Especially new pathogenic viruses pose a risk due to their high transmis-
sion rates, their ability to evolve rapidly, and the limited availability of effective treatments.
With the number of different viruses reported in humans increasing every year, this risk
is now more apparent than ever. The majority of the new viral strains detected are of
zoonotic origin [1].
A recent exemplification of the extent to which a virus can overwhelm healthcare systems
and cause excess mortality was the outbreak of severe acute respiratory syndrome coro-
navirus 2 (SARS-CoV-2) in 2019. This RNA virus originated in bats, eventually spreading
to humans, and was the cause of the global coronavirus disease 19 (COVID-19) pan-
demic. Characterized by rapid and uncontrollable spread through airborne transmission,
the COVID-19 pandemic lasted 3 years and caused ∼7 million deaths [2]. While some
patients remain asymptomatic, common symptoms of the disease include fever, cough-
ing, and other cold-like symptoms. In severe cases, infection with COVID-19 can lead
to pneumonia, respiratory- and organ failure [3]. Age, sex, genetics, obesity, and pre-
existing medical conditions all contribute to a higher risk of a more severe disease course
[4]. Though no longer classified as a global pandemic, some questions about COVID-19
still remain unanswered, e.g., concerning long-term effects [5].
Studies have emphasized the importance of a functional CD8+ T cell response for clinical
outcomes in COVID-19 patients. Generally, asymptomatic individuals and patients with
mild disease are characterized by being able to mount a strong CD8+ T cell response [6].
The essential role of functional CD8+ T cell responses for viral clearance is, however, not
limited to SARS-CoV-2 infections but also applies to other viral infections. By recogniz-
ing infected cells, CD8+ T cells can eliminate these in a highly specific manner through
several mechanisms. Importantly, CD8+ T cells also can differentiate into memory cells
that provide protection against subsequent infections with the same pathogen. This has
been proven to be more robust and ensure longer-lasting protection compared to anti-
body responses [7]. Understanding CD8+ T cell responses towards specific antigens is
therefore crucial for expanding current knowledge on T cell biology and shaping future
therapeutic approaches against viral infections.
The T cell Antigens and Immunogenicity group aims to identify novel T cell antigens and
characterize immune responses towards these. Most of the work surrounding viral infec-
tion applies SARS-CoV-2 as a model to study CD8+ T cell immunity using functional and
phenotypic assessments. Incorporating a method for cytotoxic assessment of CD8+ T
cells in the group’s current methodological framework could add a valuable dimension to
the ongoing research. It could offer a more rigorous evaluation of T cell effector functions
1
==PAGE:10==
and complement existing approaches in the study of viral infections and beyond.
The overall aim of this project is to establish a method to evaluate the cytotoxicity of CD8+
T cells, hereby broadening the experimental repertoire of the group. Using CD8+ T cells
expanded from healthy donor peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMCs), the project
seeks to assess their ability to recognize and eliminate peptide-pulsed target cells. This
will be done through killing assays conducted on real-time monitoring machinery. The
project also encompasses functional assessment of the aforementioned CD8+ T cells
along with continuous optimization of the killing assay.
5.1
The immune system
The immune system consists of an intricate network of cells, molecules, organs, tissues,
and pathways set in place to protect the body against infectious agents, all while shield-
ing the body’s own cells. Infection, e.g., by bacteria, viruses, archaea, fungi, or parasites,
can be eliminated with innate and adaptive immunity [8].
The innate immune system is the first line of defense encountered by pathogens after
a successful entry into the body. It is characterized by being quick, non-specific, and
involving cells like macrophages, dendritic cells (DCs), natural killer (NK) cells, mast
cells, basophils, neutrophils, and eosinophils. These cells eliminate infections through
the secretion of antimicrobial components, mediation of cell lysis, or phagocytosis. Ad-
ditionally, some cells of the innate immune system, like macrophages, neutrophils, and
DCs, express pattern-recognition receptors (PRRs) that recognize pathogen-associated
molecular patterns (PAMPs) on pathogens. When PAMPs are detected, pro-inflammatory
cytokine and chemokine production is initiated. This activates NK cells to exert their ef-
fector functions and also serves as an activating signal for cells of the adaptive immune
system [8].
Although rapid, the innate immunity lacks the specificity needed to eliminate many patho-
gens. Here, adaptive immunity, which is characterized by being highly specific, will be ini-
tiated. The adaptive immune response involves B and T cells, which, through their B cell
receptor (BCR) or T cell receptor (TCR), respectively, can recognize a specific antigen.
BCRs and TCRs are highly diverse in terms of specificity, hereby shaping a large reper-
toire of B and T cells equipped for fighting infection. Upon antigen recognition through
their BCR, B cells will differentiate into plasma cells which secrete antigen-specific anti-
bodies, i.e., the humoral immunity. The antibodies can mediate opsonization, neutraliza-
tion, and activation of the complement system. The T cells make up the cell-mediated
immunity. Contrary to B cells, T cells rely on antigen presenting cells (APCs) like DCs
to present antigen fragments, i.e., epitopes, to them on major histocompatibility complex
(MHC) molecules on their cell surface. Upon recognition, the T cells will proliferate and
differentiate into either cytotoxic T cells (CD8+) or helper T cells (CD4+). CD8+ T cells
2
==PAGE:11==
will then carry out their effector functions through cytokine production and killing of the
infected cells. In contrast, activated CD4+ T cells act by activating other immune cells
through the secretion of cytokines. It typically takes a week from antigen recognition un-
til an adaptive immune response is mounted [8]. Another essential part of the adaptive
immunity is the ability to form immunological memory. After an immune response, some
B cells and T cells will differentiate into memory cells. Upon re-infection with the same
pathogen as previously encountered, these will be re-activated [8].
Having both a functioning innate and adaptive immune system is crucial for defending
the body against infection. The two are interconnected and complement each other to
mount a fast and strong response against harmful agents.
This study is centered around CD8+ T cells, which will be further explored in the following
sections.
5.2
Antigen uptake and presentation
T cells rely on APCs like macrophages, DCs, and B cells to detect antigens as they
cannot recognize antigens directly. APCs process the antigens into smaller fragments
and present them on MHC molecules on their cell surface for T cells to recognize.
MHC molecules can be divided into MHC class I, which is expressed on most cells and
presents intracellular antigen fragments, and MHC class II, which is expressed on APCs,
such as DCs, and presents extracellular peptides [8]. As this project is centered around
viral infection, i.e., intracellular pathogens and CD8+ T cell responses, the following sec-
tions will focus on MHC-I molecules.
5.3
MHC-I
Upon infection, viruses replicate inside the infected cells leading to the production of viral
antigens which can be presented by MHC-I molecules. As with other cytosolic proteins
that are repeatedly produced and degraded, these get ubiquitinated and hereby tagged
for degradation by the proteasome. This leads to the generation of smaller peptides that
can be used for antigen presentation [8].
Antigens originating from pathogens that do not infect DCs can still be processed and
presented on their MHC-I molecules through a process known as cross-presentation.
This is important in some viral infections as it still allows for a CD8+ T cell response to
be mounted. In such cases, exogenous antigens from infected or dying cells or soluble
viral proteins are taken up and processed by the DCs before being loaded onto MHC-I
molecules [8, 9].
3
==PAGE:12==
Transporters associated with antigen processing (TAP) proteins mediate the transport
of peptides from the cytosol of APCs to the endoplasmic reticulum (ER). Specifically,
TAP-1 dimerizes with TAP-2 to form a complex which is nested into the ER membrane
and allows peptide passage in an ATP-dependent manner. The TAP complex can trans-
port peptides of up to 16 amino acids into the ER, which includes the 8-10 amino acid
MHC-I-binding peptides. In the ER, MHC-I molecules are assembled and bound to the
chaperone proteins calnexin, calreticulum, tapasin, and ERp57 until the TAP-mediated
peptide transport has been completed. The chaperone proteins ensure that peptides
of low affinity are not loaded onto the MHC-I molecules, and these are instead replaced
with higher affinity peptides. Peptides with high affinity will result in the folding of the MHC
molecules into a correct conformation that allows them to transport to the cell surface [8].
The MHC-I peptide loading and presentation process is illustrated in Figure 1.

intracellular proteins (1) are digested by the proteasome (2) to create smaller peptide fragments
(3). TAP proteins transport the peptides into the ER (4) where complexation with MHC-I can occur

In humans, the MHC genes are referred to as human leukocyte antigen (HLA) genes, and
accordingly, MHC-I corresponds to HLA-A, HLA-B, and HLA-C. Each person can carry
up to two alleles of each HLA class I variant. In this study there has been an emphasis on
specific HLA-A and HLA-B alleles. Throughout this report, they will be referred to based
on standardized nomenclature: HLA-[gene]*[allele]:[protein] (e.g., HLA-A*01:01) [8, 10].

5.4
T cells
A central component of the adaptive immune system are T cells which together with B
cells make up the cellular immunity. T cells can broadly be divided into either CD4+
or CD8+ T cells. CD4+ T cells, also known as helper T cells, recognize antigens pre-
sented to them on MHC-II molecules and function by interacting with and activating other
immune cells upon detection of a pathogen. Contrary, CD8+ T cells, also known as cyto-
toxic T cells, can recognize antigens presented on MHC-I molecules and upon activation
kill the infected cells. Specifically, CD8+ T cells target infected cells and induce apoptosis
by secreting granules containing cytotoxic proteins such as perforin and granzymes [8].
5.4.1
T cell development
T cells develop in the thymus from bone marrow-derived lymphoid progenitors and un-
dergo several developmental stages before maturation. Initially, they are characterized
as double negative (DN) thymocytes due to the lack of CD4 and CD8 expression. During
the DN stage, thymocytes will commit to the T cell lineage, and the majority of them will
differentiate into αβ T cells, the T cells this project concerns. Alternatively, they will dif-
ferentiate into γδ T cells but these will not be further described here. The TCR consists
of an α and a β chain which are made up of variable (V), diversity (D), joining (J), and
constant (C) gene segments. After T cell lineage commitment, TCR development is initi-
ated in DN thymocytes by rearrangement of the β chain. Briefly, the Dβ and Jβ segments
are rearranged followed by the joining of Vβ to DJβ. If the β chain is not productive, this
process will be repeated. The newly formed β chain will be expressed on the cell surface
together with a placeholder α chain, leading to the proliferation and expression of CD4
and CD8. Hereby, the thymocytes enter the double positive (DP) stage. The α chain is
then rearranged by joining Vα to Jα. As the α chain does not consist of any D segments
it allows for many more attempts at a successful α chain rearrangement than for the β
chain. After successful production of a functional α chain it is paired with the β chain
and the DP thymocytes will undergo positive selection to ensure that they are capable
of recognizing and interacting with peptide:MHC (pMHC) complexes. Thymic epithelial
cells will present self antigens on both MHC-I and MHC-II molecules, and only thymo-
cytes capable of recognizing the self antigens will survive and reach the single positive
(SP) stage where the thymocyte is either CD4+ or CD8+. Lastly, the SP thymocytes will
undergo negative selection, i.e., the central tolerance, which functions to eliminate self-
reactive T cells (T cells with high affinity to self-antigen will undergo apoptosis whereas
those with low-affinity will survive) [8, 11].
T cells that have survived both positive and negative selection will enter the circulation
and later secondary lymphoid organs as naive T cells. These remain of low functionality
until they encounter their antigen, become activated and differentiate into effector T cells

==PAGE:14==
which can exert their effector functions at the site of infection [8].
5.4.2
T cell activation
Together with other signals, the TCR makes up a complex that is necessary for T cell
activation. Apart from the TCR, the complex also includes CD3 made up of a γ, δ, and
two ϵ chains, as well as two ζ chains. The γ, δ, and ϵ chains have one intracellular im-
munoreceptor tyrosine-based activation motif (ITAM), whereas the ζ chains have three.
These get phosphorylated upon pMHC:TCR binding and initiate a downstream signal-
ing cascade leading to the production of activating signals, or cytokines. In the case of
CD8+ T cells, the CD8 co-receptor is also part of the complex as it associates with the
pMHC. Collectively, this process is referred to as signal 1 needed for T cell activation, as
illustrated in Figure 2. The second signal needed for T cell activation is a co-stimulatory
signal provided by the binding of CD28 on T cells to B7.1 or B7.2 on APCs. This induces
T cell proliferation and cytokine production. The third and last signal needed for T cell
activation is cytokine signaling from the APC, which promotes the T cell to differentiate
and expand [8, 12]. Cytokines involved in T cell activation, growth, and differentiation
include interleukin 2 (IL-2), IL-15, and interferon-α/β (IFN-α/β) [13, 14].
Figure 2: Three signals are needed for T cell activation: pMHC:TCR interaction, co-stimulatory
signaling and cytokine signaling. Created using biorender.com.
As previously mentioned, CD8+ T cells will mediate apoptosis of the infected cells upon
activation by releasing cytotoxic proteins. In addition, they secrete the pro-inflammatory
cytokines tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α) and IFN-γ. If the infected cells express TNF-α
receptors, this cytokine will induce them to undergo apoptosis. IFN-γ can activate APCs
and has antiviral effects [8].
6
==PAGE:15==
Most of the effector T cells will undergo apoptosis after clearance of the infection. The re-
maining cells can differentiate into memory T cells which can mount a faster and stronger
immune response upon re-infection. Memory T cells can be separated into different sub-
sets based on their expression of CD197 (also known as C-C chemokine receptor type
7 (CCR7)) and CD45RA. CD45RA helps regulate TCR signaling, and CCR7 is involved
in T cell homing to the lymphoid tissues [8]. Central memory T cells (TCM) are CD45RA-
and CCR7+, effector memory T cells (TEM) are CD45RA- and CCR7- and terminally dif-
ferentiated effector memory T cells (TEMRA) are CD45RA+ and CCR7-. For reference,
naive T cells are CD45RA+ and CCR7+ [15].
5.4.2.1

The ability to detect T cells at different stages of differentiation or activation is a valuable
research tool. Cell surface expression profiles of T cells allow this as they are dynamic
and highly dependent on environmental cues. This is exploited in different cellular assays,
e.g., the activation-induced marker (AIM) assay. In this study, different T cell activation
markers are used to characterize CD8+ T cells responsive towards peptide-stimulation,
and these are briefly described in Table 1.

5.5
Viral infection
Viruses are intracellular pathogens that rely on host cells to survive and replicate. They
are composed of RNA or DNA encapsulated by a protein capsid and, in some cases,
further coated by a viral envelope. In brief, viruses infect cells by attaching to host cell
surface proteins, leading to their internalization by one of several pathways, which will
not be further elaborated here. If the virus consists of an envelope, it is fused with the
cell membrane during this time, exposing the capsid. Depending on the type of virus,
it will relocate to the appropriate site for replication which is either in the cytoplasm or
the nucleus. In this process, the capsid is disassembled exposing the RNA or DNA for
protein synthesis and replication of the viral genome. Subsequently, the newly formed
proteins and genome will assemble into new virus particles by RNA/DNA encapsulation
into a capsid and exit the host through cell lysis. If the virus is enveloped, it will exit by
fusing out of the host cell membrane, creating the viral envelope before or during budding
from the membrane. The released viruses can infect new cells and repeat this cycle [23,
24].
As previously mentioned, the innate and adaptive immune system are highly interlinked.
Upon viral infection, the innate immunity will kick in when recognizing viral components
like proteins through PRRs. This leads to the production of IFN-α, IFN-β, TNF-α and IL-
12 which act pro-inflammatory, activating innate immune cells like NK cells and macro-
phages for killing. NK cells are especially important for limiting the magnitude of the
infection and keeping the viral titers at a constant level until the adaptive immunity can
take over [8, 25]. The adaptive immune system will be fully activated around 7 days post-
infection. T cells are especially crucial contributors as viruses are intracellular pathogens
and hereby not detectable by antibodies. During viral replication inside the host cell,
antigens are processed and presented on MHC-I molecules as described in section 5.3.
These can be recognized by CD8+ T cells, which will exert their effector functions and kill
the infected cells, thereby clearing the infection [8]. Type 1 T helper cells and the humoral
immune response are also involved in the defense against viral infection, however these
are outside the scope of this project.
This study mainly concerns CD8+ T cell responses towards SARS-CoV-2 peptides but
also includes responses to Cytomegalovirus (CMV), Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) and In-
fluenza virus (FLU) peptides described below.
5.5.1
SARS-CoV-2
SARS-CoV-2 is part of the Coronaviridae family and can infect both humans and an-
imals. In fact, SARS-CoV-2 and viruses with high similarity to it, like SARS-CoV and
Middle East respiratory syndrome coronavirus (MERS-CoV), have all originated in an-
imals and later been transmitted to humans. The disease caused by SARS-CoV-2 is
8
==PAGE:17==
referred to as COVID-19 reflecting its outbreak in December of 2019.
Originating in
bats, the disease was transmitted to humans causing a rapid spread that evolved into
a global pandemic. COVID-19 caused over 6.8 million deaths due to factors like lack of
available treatment and prevention, the disease being highly infectious and the virus hav-
ing great immune evasion capabilities. The emergence of new virus variants (including
Alpha, Beta, Gamma, Delta, Epsilon, Zeta, Eta, Theta, Iota, Kappa, Lambda, Mu and
Omicron) also complicated the efforts to restrain the virus and combat these challenges.
The way of transmission is through particles released from the respiratory tract of an in-
fected individual, e.g., when coughing or sneezing. Common symptoms include regular
cold symptoms like fever and coughing, whereas patients with severe cases can experi-
ence shortness of breath, an impaired or failing respiratory system, as well as injuries to
the heart, kidneys, and liver. Children and younger individuals generally have milder (or
asymptomatic) disease courses than adults and the elderly [2, 26].
Like the rest of the members of the Coronaviridae family, SARS-CoV-2 is an enveloped
RNA virus. The RNA encodes the membrane (M), nucleocapsid (N), envelope (E) and
spike (S) proteins that make up its structure as well as 16 non-structural proteins essen-
tial for replication and transcription. Lastly, the RNA also codes for several accessory
proteins. The spike protein is present on the virus surface in a trimeric state and consists
of the S1 and S2 subunits. During pathogenesis, the S1 subunit will bind to angiotensin-
converting enzyme 2 (ACE2) on the host cell surface leading to S1/S2 cleavage. The
exposed S2 subunit is activated and facilitates virus to host fusion [27]. This allows the
viral RNA to enter the host cell cytosol where the translation of the non-structural proteins
takes place. In the ER, RNA replication as well as the translation of structural and acces-
sory proteins take place. From here the N, S, E and M proteins bud out in the ER:Golgi
apparatus space encapsulating the RNA to generate new virus particles. These are re-
leased from the host cell by exocytosis and can continue to infect more cells [28].
CD8+ T cells play an important role in clearing SARS-CoV-2 infections. Several stud-
ies have reported that the profile and numbers of these cytotoxic T cells differ between
patients with mild and severe disease [6]. The studies showed that patients with mild
disease had reduced humoral responses to the infection but could induce a strong and
functional CD8+ T cell response, as determined by their ability to produce granzyme
B and perforin. In contrast, patients with moderate or severe disease showed stronger
humoral responses but had a low frequency of CD8+ T cells, of which few generated
cytotoxic molecules. It was proposed that the lack of robust B cell responses in patients
with mild disease could be a result of the strong CD8+ T cell response limiting the spread
of infection and eliminating the infected cells early in the infection. This would not allow
for prolonged antigen presentation which germinal centers of B cells require. CD8+ T
cells of patients with mild disease also showed cell surface markers compatible with a
TEM phenotype indicating that they have undergone differentiation and activation. In in-
9
==PAGE:18==
dividuals with severe disease, the CD8+ T cells had phenotypes indicative of exhaustion
[6].
As described above, SARS-CoV-2 consists of different proteins that can serve as anti-
gens, be presented to CD8+ T cells on MHC-I molecules, and serve as the basis for
an immune response. Studies concerning which proteins can elicit CD8+ T cells of the
highest magnitude show conflicting results. While some find that all proteins create T cell
responses of equal magnitudes, others show that the spike protein response predom-
inates, especially in patients with mild disease and following vaccination [29, 30]. Still
poorly understood, further research is needed to reach conclusive results. Interestingly,
some of these studies also detected CD8+ T cells reactive against SARS-CoV-2 in un-
exposed subjects. This is likely indicative of cross-reactivity from previous infection with
viruses from the Coronaviridae family, given their high genomic similarity [31].
5.5.2
Other
Viral responses against peptides from other viruses are also covered in this study though
to a lesser extent. These include CMV, EBV and FLU. Since these viral infections are
common in humans, they can also serve as positive controls in the experiments de-
scribed later in the report.
Human CMV is a DNA virus belonging to the Herpesviridae family. It is highly preva-
lent in humans and most people remain asymptomatic through the infection while others
can experience cold-like symptoms. Once infected, the virus persists in the body for life,
though in a latent state [32].
EBV is also a DNA virus that belongs to the Herpesviridae family and is widespread. It is
estimated that up to 95 % of adults have been infected and subsequently remain asymp-
tomatically infected due to its evolving mechanisms to avoid the immune responses of
the host. Most people get infected during childhood and experience mild symptoms.
EBV has, however, also been shown to contribute to the development of diseases like
mononucleosis and cancer [33].
Influenza viruses belong to the Orthomyxoviridae family and are RNA viruses that infect
the respiratory system. Originating in birds, Influenza A which is relevant for this study,
can be transmitted to humans through sources like infected poultry. Mild cases are as-
sociated with cold-like symptoms, whereas severe cases can include acute pulmonary
hemorrhage and pneumonia. Since the 1900s, there have been several influenza pan-
demics [34, 35].

6
Aim and objectives
CD8+ T cells are important mediators of anti-viral immunity and several studies have
demonstrated that robust CD8+ T cell responses are linked to milder disease [6]. It is
crucial to map the cytotoxicity and functionality of these cytotoxic T cells in response
to viral infection as it can advance our understanding of their role in infection settings
and pave the way for new therapeutic strategies. To investigate such aspects of T cell-
mediated immunity, robust in vitro systems are required.
The main aim of this master’s thesis is to develop and optimize an in vitro killing assay to
assess the cytotoxicity of peptide-specific CD8+ T cells. The T cells used will primarily be
specific towards SARS-CoV-2 peptides, but peptides derived from other viruses will also
be utilized. Functional analyses will be conducted in parallel to provide a more compre-
hensive understanding of the activity and activation of the cells. Ultimately, this research
seeks to establish an experimental setup to simulate and study the natural cellular im-
mune response against viral infections. To achieve this aim, the following objectives have
been set:
• Expand peptide-specific CD8+ T cells from healthy donor PBMCs.
• Perform functional evaluation of the expanded CD8+ T cells using intracellular cy-
tokine staining (ICS) and activation-induced marker (AIM) assays.
• Establish and optimize a killing assay for cytotoxicity assessment of the expanded
CD8+ T cells using the Incucyte® S3 platform.

Materials and Methods
7.1
Patient samples and cell lines
All buffy coats used in this study were obtained from the blood bank at Copenhagen Uni-
versity Hospital, Rigshospitalet, from healthy donors. A comprehensive list is available
in Appendix A1.1. PBMCs were isolated by centrifugation using Leucosep tubes (Fisher
Scientific, #227290) and Lymphoprep™density gradient medium (StemCell, #7861) ac-
cording to the manufacturers’ instructions. They were stored at -196 °C in fetal bovine
serum (FBS) (Gibco, #10500-064) supplemented with 10% Dimethylsulfoxide (DMSO)
(Sigma Aldrich, #D2650).
The HLA-A*01:01 HLA-B*35:01 EBV-transformed lymphoblastoid cell line BM36.1 was
used as a target cell line throughout this study together with the T2 lymphoma-derived
cell line expressing HLA-A*02:01.
7.2
Peptides
Peptides were purchased Pepscan (Lelystad, Netherlands). Prior to use, they were dis-
solved in DMSO (Sigma Aldrich, #D2650) to reach a 10 mM concentration. An exhaustive
list of all peptides used during this thesis is provided in Appendix A1.2.
7.3
Cell culture
Cells were thawed using RPMI 1640 medium (Gibco, #61870010) with 10% FBS (Gibco,
#10500-064), washed twice with centrifugation at 1500 rpm for 5 minutes. For culturing,
cells were resuspended in X-VIVO™15 media (Lonza, #02060Q) with 5% human serum
(Sigma Aldrich, #H3667). Cytokines were added after day 3 at a final concentration of
100 IU/mL IL-2 and 50 ng/mL IL-15. They were cultured at 37°C with 5% CO2 and pas-
saged every 2-3 days. Throughout the culturing period, the cells were kept at a density
of 0.5 × 106 −2 × 106 cells/ml. The NucleoCounter® NC-200™(ChemoMetec, Denmark)
and Via1-Cassette™technology (ChemoMetec, Denmark) were used for cell counting.
The target cell lines BM36.1 and T2 were expanded prior to the initiation of killing assays.
During this time, these cells were cultured in RPMI 1640 medium (Gibco, #61870010)
with 10% FBS (Gibco, #10500-064).
7.4
Expansion of peptide-specific T cells
The protocol for expansion of peptide-specific T cells was adapted from Grant & Gras
(2022) [36]. Cells were washed with X-VIVO™15 media (Lonza, #02060Q), centrifuged
12
==PAGE:21==
at 1500 rpm for 5 minutes, and counted using the NucleoCounter® NC-200™(ChemoMe-
tec, Denmark) and Via1-Cassette™. For each peptide-specific expansion, 1/3 of the cells
(stimulators) were pulsed with 10 µM peptide and incubated for 90 minutes at 37°C with
5% CO2. During this time, the remaining 2/3 cells (responders) were rested at the same
condition. After incubation, the stimulators were washed twice with X-VIVO™15 media
(Lonza, #02060Q) and centrifuged at 1500 rpm for 5 minutes. Lastly, the stimulators were
resuspended in X-VIVO™15 media (Lonza, #02060Q) with 5% human serum (Sigma
Aldrich, #H3667) and added to the responder cells. The cells were cultured as described
above for 10-13 days. Frequency testing was performed at days 0 and 13 using pMHC
multimers and flow cytometry.
7.5
Tetramers
MHC-I monomers were produced on-site by laboratory personnel. Briefly, Escherichia
coli was used to express HLA-A*01:01, HLA-A*02:01, HLA-A*11:01, HLA-A*24:02, HLA-
B*15:01 and HLA-B*35:01 heavy chains and β2m light chain. These were refolded with
UV-sensitive ligands to stabilize the pMHC complexes. Biotinylation and purification steps
were performed prior to storage at -80°C. These pMHC complexes with the placeholder
UV-sensitive ligand are referred to as monomers throughout this work.
Monomers were diluted to 200 µg/ml and peptides were diluted to 400 µM in phosphate
buffered saline (PBS). Each monomer was mixed with its corresponding peptide in a
1:1 ratio and placed at room temperature under 366 nm UV light exposure for 60 min-
utes. After the peptide exchange, the pMHCs were centrifuged at 3300g, 4°C, for 5
minutes. The supernatant was split into two, and each volume was mixed with a strepta-
vidin fluorophore. Streptavidin conjugates used in this project included PE (BioLegend,
#405204), APC (BioLegend, #405243), PE-Cy7 (BioLegend, #405206), BV421 (BD Hori-
zon, #563259), BUV395 (BD Horizon, #564176), BV605 (BD Horizon, #563260) and
BUV737 (BD Horizon, #612775). For BV- and BUV-colours, 18.04 µl streptavidin con-
jugates were added for every 100 µl pMHC, and for the remaining colours 9.02 µl were
added for every 100 µl pMHC. After a 30 minute incubation period on ice, D-Biotin (In-
vitrogen, #B20656) was added to a final concentration of 25 µM. The two colour panels
were incubated for another 20 minutes on ice before being mixed together. The tetramers
stored at -20°C in 0.5% bovine serum albumin (BSA) and 5% glycerol.

Empty loadable tetramers
Empty loadable tetramers (ELTs) were produced as plug-and-play alternatives to tra-
ditional tetramers for T cell staining. The monomers used were Y84C mutants enabling
peptide exchange without UV light. The monomer was diluted to 50 µg/ml in PBS and dis-
tributed equally into two tubes. The streptavidin conjugates, two for each ELT pair, were
also diluted in PBS to reach a concentration of 0.009 mg/ml at the same total volume as

the monomer. Streptavidin conjugates used to create ELTs in this project included PE
(BioLegend, #405204) and APC (BioLegend, #405243). The fluorophores were added to

T cell staining with pMHCs
0.5 × 106 −2 × 106 cells were used for each T cell staining. Initially, the cells were washed

1500 rpm for 5 minutes at 4°C. The supernatant was slinged and dasatinib (LC Labora-

µl Brilliant stain buffer plus (BD Biosciences, #566385). The tetramers were thawed and
centrifuged at 3300g for 5 minutes at 4°C before adding 1 µl of each specificity to the
desired wells and topping up with FACS buffer. The cells were incubated for 15 minutes
at 37°C with the tetramers after which the antibody panel was added to the cells. In this

Incucyte® killing assay
Killing assays were performed using the Incucyte® S3 Live-Cell Analysis System (Sarto-
rius, Germany). BM36.1 and T2 TAP-deficient cell lines were used as target cells and
pulsed with peptide as described in section 7.8. Expanded CD8+ T cells with the same
specificity were used as effector cells, and these were rested in RPMI 1640 medium
(Gibco, #61870010) with 10% FBS (Gibco, #10500-064) for 120 minutes prior to initiating

assay was run using the non-adherent cell-by-cell program taking 4-9 images through the
red channel every 1-4 hour(s) for 24-72 hours. For negative controls, effector cells were
added to non-peptide pulsed target cells, and as a positive control, 10% detergent was
added to peptide-pulsed target cells. All conditions were run in replicates (n = 4).
After completion of the killing assay, the Incucyte® S3 Live-Cell Analysis System Software
was used to display and analyze the images taken. Parameters were adjusted to ensure
proper masking and identification of target and effector cells, respectively. Lastly, the data
was exported and processed as described in section 7.11.

Results and Discussion
This project aims to establish a killing assay to evaluate T cell cytotoxicity. T cells of
known specificity were therefore expanded from donor PBMCs using viral peptides. The
peptide-specific cells were functionally assessed and subsequently used to optimize and
conduct killing assays against target cells presenting their cognate antigens.
8.1
Peptide-specific T cell expansions
8.1.1
HLA-A*01:01
Peptide-specific CD8+ T cell frequencies were measured prior to and immediately after
expansion using flow cytometry. The donor BC-447 (HLA-A*01:01) was expanded with
the three SARS-CoV-2 peptides LTD, TPS, and TTD, and the pre-expansion frequencies
are displayed in Figure 3.A. While initial frequencies of LTD- and TPS-specific CD8+ T
cells were 3.57E-3%, there were substantially more TTD-specific CD8+ T cells with a
frequency approaching 0.1%. If the cells have a naive phenotype, the low frequencies
would be consistent with the PBMCs being collected before the COVID-19 pandemic
and, as such, not having encountered the particular peptides and mounted a response
against them before. Contrary, a memory phenotype would indicate cross-reactivity from
infection with other viruses. Given the presence of T cells with specificity against all three
peptides it was expected that all these would be expandable.

Functional assessment of peptide-expanded CD8+ T cells
Some peptide-specific CD8+ T cell expansions from the donors BC-447 and BC-446
were selected to undergo functional assessment. The selection was based on similar
peptide-specific CD8+ T cell frequencies and HLA-typing profiles. Intracellular cytokine
stainings and AIM assays were conducted for the assessment. These analyses also
served as a way to determine the suitability of the cells to engage in killing assays.
8.2.1
Intracellular cytokine staining
The peptide-specific CD8+ T cell expansions utilized a fraction of donor PBMCs as APCs
to activate the remaining cells and induce proliferation. An alternative approach to antigen
presentation involves using dedicated target cells designed for studying T cell responses.
Examples include the TAP-deficient BM36.1 and T2 cell lines, which lack endogenous
peptide presentation, resulting in few and empty HLA molecules on their cell surface.
BM36.1 is an EBV-transformed lymphoblastoid cell line that expresses HLA-A*01:01 and
HLA-B*35:01 [40], while T2 is a lymphoma-derived cell line expressing HLA-A*02:01
[41]. When pulsed with peptides containing HLA-A*01:01 or HLA-A*02:01 binding mo-
tifs, these cells form stabilized pMHC complexes, enabling antigen presentation to T cells.
Throughout this report, peptide-pulsed BM36.1 and T2 cells are referred to as target cells.
ICS experiments were performed to evaluate the functionality of the peptide-expanded
CD8+ T cells, with some assays utilizing BM36.1 and T2 target cells as APCs. The ca-
pacity of IFN-γ and TNF-α cytokine production in expanded cells was compared between
peptide-pulsed PBMCs and target cells, as seen in Figure 6.A. Target cells consistently
induced higher cytokine production across all peptide-specific CD8+ T cell expansions
tested. For the BC-447 TTD- and LTD-expansions, cytokine production was approxi-
mately twice as high in the target cell condition, whereas BC-446 V13-expanded CD8+ T
cells exhibited a four-fold increase. The greater antigen presentation capability of target
cells compared to PBMCs is likely due to the fact that only a small fraction of PBMCs
(approximately 15% [42]) function as APCs, whereas all TAP-deficient cells are capable
of antigen presentation. Based on this finding, subsequent ICS experiments were con-
ducted using target cells as APCs to ensure consistent and efficient antigen presentation.
This approach also prevents endogenous antigen presentation from influencing and in-
terfering with cytokine production.

increase in cytokine production.

All peptide-expanded CD8+ T cells in the ICS experiments are functional as they exhib-

expected to possess cytotoxic capabilities and effectively mediate the killing of peptide-

8.2.2
Activation-induced marker assay
AIM assays were conducted on the four types of peptide-specific CD8+ T cells to eval-
uate their activation profiles in response to antigenic stimulation. This analysis relied on
the principle that a CD69 and CD137 double positive population is the truly activated T
cell population, as their expression promotes tissue retention and co-stimulatory signal-
ing [43]. Figure 7.A illustrates the distribution of CD69+CD137+ double-positive CD8+ T
cells across the tested conditions. For the BC-446 donor, V13-expanded CD8+ T cells ex-
hibited a higher activation level (23%) compared to YLQ-expanded CD8+ T cells (14%).
This suggests that V13-expanded CD8+ T cells are more readily activated by peptide-
pulsed target cells. BC-447 expansions generally displayed lower activation levels than
those of BC-446, with TTD-expanded CD8+ T cells at 12% activation and LTD-expanded
CD8+ T cells at 6%. Interestingly, this donor-based difference in CD69+CD137+ expres-
sion contrasts with what was observed for IFN-γ and TNF-α secretion.

Panel B of Figure 7 presents the activation marker profile for each of the four expan-
sions, normalized to their corresponding negative control. Since multiple AIM assays

BC-446 V13-expanded CD8+ T cells exhibit a strong activation profile, with notable up-

activation and proliferation, while the increased expression of CD39 and CD279 may indi-

T cell activation has been shown to induce TCR internalization [44], which can be quan-
tified by a decrease in tetramer frequency pre- and post-activation. This was therefore
investigated in conjunction with the AIM assays, comparing tetramer frequencies of the
peptide-expanded CD8+ T cells 24 hours after antigen exposure to the negative controls.
Tetramer frequencies are compared in Figure 8. For BC-446 YLQ- and V13-expanded
CD8+ T cells, a reduction in frequency is observed, consistent with the expected activa-
tion based on the AIM assay data. In contrast, tetramer frequencies do not reduce but
increase for BC-447 TTD- and LTD-expanded CD8+ T cells. This suggests the prolifera-
tion of the tetramer+ cells rather than activation-mediated TCR internalization.
Collectively, the AIM assay results suggest activation for all four peptide expansions at 24
hours post-stimulation with antigen. However, the degree of activation varies with BC-446
peptide-expanded CD8+ T cells showing more pronounced activation marker expression
than BC-447 expansions. This is backed up by the changes seen in tetramer frequencies
after exposure to peptide-pulsed target cells compared to the negative controls.
8.3
Evaluation of the cytotoxic capabilities of peptide-expanded CD8+
T cells
8.3.1
Development and optimization of a killing assay
The peptide-expanded CD8+ T cells described in the previous sections were used to
establish, optimize, and validate killing assays to determine their cytotoxic capabilities.
Using the Incucyte® S3 Live-Cell Analysis System, the initial aim was to determine a
suitable cell number for future assays. To achieve this, a titration was performed using
BC-447 TTD-expanded CD8+ T cells co-cultured with TTD-pulsed BM36.1 target cells.
25
==PAGE:34==
Previous work in the research group showed that a 1:1 effector-to-target cell ratio was
optimal for similar assays. Based on this, the titration included conditions with 5000,
10000, 20000, and 40000 effector and target cells, respectively. As depicted in Figure
9.A, increasing cell numbers led to a steeper and more sustained decline in fluorescence
signal from the target cells, indicating greater killing. In particular, the 20000 and 40000
cell conditions show distinct killing trends. This can be attributed to the fact that higher
cell numbers increase interactions between effector and target cells compared to lower
cell densities when using the same vessel. In the 5000-cell condition, fluorescence levels
remain stable throughout the assay, while the other conditions do not plateau until later.
Supporting the idea of limited killing at lower cell densities, live-cell images in Appendix
A4.1 show that the cells are spaced far apart in the 5000-cell condition, whereas they
are tightly packed together in conditions with higher cell numbers. To ensure sufficient
proximity between effector and target cells for effective interaction, and to account for the
capabilities of the Incucyte® analysis software, the 20000-cell condition was determined
to be optimal and was used for subsequent killing assays.
In the 20000 cell condition in Figure 9.A it is apparent that a longer plateau phase follows
the initial decline in target cell fluorescence intensity. For resource and time-saving pur-
poses, subsequent killing assays were limited to 24 hours instead of the 72 hours used
in the initial assay.
Figure 9: Determination of the optimal (A) cell numbers and (B) target cells for killing assays
assessing the cytotoxicity of peptide-expanded CD8+ T cells.
To quantify specific killing in the killing assays, the fluorescence of peptide-pulsed target
cells co-cultured with peptide-expanded CD8+ T cells was subtracted from and normal-
ized to that of the negative control using the formula ([A −B]/A, where A = negative con-
trol and B = sample). This calculation provided the relative proportion of specific killing
by the effector cells, enabling direct comparisons between expansions and killing assays.

==PAGE:35==
Selecting appropriate target cells is crucial for a successful killing assay setup. To as-
sess this, a killing assay was conducted to evaluate the suitability of peptide-pulsed,
non-expanded PBMCs and peptide-pulsed TAP-deficient T2 cells as target cells. In both
conditions, BC-446 YLQ-expanded CD8+ T cells served as effector cells. It was observed
that peptide-expanded CD8+ T cells initially induced slightly higher specific PBMC killing
compared to T2 cell killing, as shown in Figure 9.B. However, while PBMC-specific killing
plateaued early at a relatively low level, T2 cell killing increased rapidly over time. This
killing peaked at around 10 hours before declining. T2-specific killing remained higher
than PBMC-specific killing for most of the assay duration.
Based on the findings above and supported by the ICS results, greater T cell activation
and cytotoxicity were observed when TAP-deficient cells were used as target cells. This
is likely due to the considerably lower presence of APCs in PBMCs compared to TAP-
deficient target cells. Additionally, since the PBMC-based setup mimics the one used for
peptide-specific CD8+ T cell expansion, it suggests that during the 24-hour killing assay,
peptide-pulsed PBMCs may induce T cell priming rather than being targeted for killing.
As a result, all subsequent killing assays were conducted using the TAP-deficient cell
lines BM36.1 and T2 as target cells.

8.3.2
Cytotoxicity assessment
Killing assays were conducted under optimized conditions using TAP-deficient target cells
at a 1:1 target-to-effector cell ratio over 24 hours to determine the cytotoxic capabili-
ties of peptide-expanded CD8+ T cells. During this study, the cytotoxicity of the follow-
ing peptide expansions was examined: BC-447 TTD-expanded (HLA-A*01:01), BC-447
LTD-expanded (HLA-A*01:01), BC-446 V13-expanded (HLA-A*02:01), and BC-446 YLQ-
expanded (HLA-A*02:01).
Figure 11.A illustrates the fluorescence of peptide-pulsed T2 cells when co-cultured BC-
446 V13- and YLQ-expanded cells. Both exhibit a steady decline in fluorescence over
time, indicating target cell killing. However, YLQ-pulsed target cells show a more rapid
and pronounced decline, stabilizing earlier than V13-pulsed target cells, which display
more variable fluorescence levels after the initial decline. Examining specific killing in
Figure 11.B, the killing profiles of the effector cells share similar patterns but differ in
magnitude. For BC-446 YLQ-expanded CD8+ T cells, specific killing gradually increases
over the first 10 hours before stabilizing at a slightly lower level. A similar trend is ob-
served for BC-446 V13-expanded CD8+ T cells. However, these initially display negative
specific killing values, reaching a peak at 20 hours. Throughout the assay, BC-446 YLQ-
expanded CD8+ T cells exhibit higher cytotoxicity and a more uniform killing response.

8.3.3
Reproducibility
While the BC-447 TTD-expanded CD8+ T cells exhibited the fastest and most pronounced
killing among all the expanded cells tested in Figure 11, these results were not consis-
tently reproducible. Attempts to replicate these findings, available in Appendix A4.2.1,
show that only one of the three repetitions of the TTD killing assay closely resembled the
data presented in Figure 11.C-D. The other attempts resulted in high fluctuations in flu-
orescence levels, with no clear trend of sustained killing or even target cell proliferation.
The observed fluctuations in fluorescence may be due to variability in T cell performance
over time or potential T cell exhaustion. An unpublished study conducted by the T cell
Antigens and Immunogenicity research group has revealed that TTD-specific CD8+ T
cells often possess compromised TCRs in severe disease, leading to functional impair-
ments. While the ICS results in this project showed that 72% of TTD-expanded CD8+ T
cells were capable of producing IFN-γ and TNF-α, the overall functionality of the cells is
not solely dependent on these cytokines. In the AIM assay, it was observed that only 12%
of TTD-expanded CD8+ T cells were CD69+ CD137+ (indicating true activation), though
individual measurements varied considerably. Therefore, the lack of killing in some TTD-
expanded CD8+ T cell assays may be attributed to impaired functionality.
As seen in Appendix A4.2.2, repeated killing assays with BC-447 LTD-expanded CD8+ T
cells exhibited target cell fluorescence patterns similar to those observed in the initial as-
say in Figure 11.A. Like with the TTD-expanded cells, this lack of pronounced killing could
be due to an inability to sustain killing activity over time, T cell exhaustion, or reduced
target cell engagement. In contrast, while Figure 11.D shows an initial specific killing pro-
portion of 35% that steadily decreases to 7%, subsequent killing assays exhibited more
stable values ranging from 0-10% throughout the duration of the assay. These lower spe-
cific killing proportions may indicate that the cytotoxic capabilities of LTD-expanded CD8+
T cells are not sufficiently potent, potentially leading to an equilibrium between target cell
killing and proliferation.
Repetitions of the killing assay with V13-expanded CD8+ T cells show a consistent de-
cline in fluorescence throughout the assay duration. Although some repetitions exhibited
fluctuations in fluorescence levels, the overall decrease in target cell fluorescence was
maintained, indicating greater reproducibility compared to the killing assays with TTD-
and LTD-expanded effector cells. Since fluorescence levels do not increase to the same
extent as in the LTD-expanded CD8+ T cell assays, this suggests that target cell killing

exceeds proliferation. However, when the killing assay was repeated, the specific killing
curves displayed a different pattern compared to the initial assay. Unlike the parabola-
shaped curve seen in Figure 11.B, the specific killing curves in Appendix A4.2.3 either
increase linearly or remain stable throughout the assay. Furthermore, while negative val-
ues were also observed in the initial assay, it peaked at 15% specific killing, whereas the
repetitions showed lower maximum values between 0-10%. This indicates that fluores-
cence measurements of V13-expanded CD8+ T cells alone are not fully representative
of specific killing. This discrepancy can be attributed to target cells in the negative con-
trols exhibiting fluorescence levels and trends comparable to those of the peptide-pulsed
counterparts.
Cytotoxicity testing of YLQ-expanded CD8+ T cells was only repeated once as seen in
Appendix A4.2.4. Although the fluorescence signal fluctuated more in the repeated as-
say, it followed the same pattern as the initial killing assay. However, specific killing was
lower, with values ranging between 0-10% and peaking only at the end of the assay. This
contrasts with the killing assay in Figure 11, where specific killing peaked earlier at 30%.
It also contradicts the results from the repeated antigenic challenge killing assay in Figure
10 which demonstrated pronounced target cell killing by BC-446 YLQ-expanded CD8+ T
cells. These findings indicate limited consistency in specific killing, similar to what was
observed for other peptide expansions. Previous results from this project suggest that
YLQ-expanded CD8+ T cells are functional, as TCR internalization and CD69/CD137
expression were observed. In addition, while both the ICS and AIM assays indicated
superior functionality of V13-expanded CD8+ T cells compared to YLQ-expanded CD8+
T cells (based on cytokine production and the fraction of CD69+CD137+ cells) the killing
assays suggest the opposite regarding cytotoxicity.
A noteworthy observation from the killing assays is the substantial variation in initial flu-
orescence levels of the target cells, despite consistent cell numbers and stain concen-
tration across experiments. While this may not affect the overall fluorescence decline
caused by killing, it can impact the specific killing plots. Since the tested conditions are
normalized to their negative control, discrepancies in starting fluorescence levels can
lead to over- or under-estimation of specific killing proportions, making it challenging
to accurately compare killing efficiency across experiments. To address this potential
source of variation, a suggested approach is to generate stable target cell lines virally
transduced to express fluorescent proteins such as green fluorescent protein (GFP) or
mCherry. This would eliminate the need for a separate staining step before each killing
assay, thereby reducing variability. Such an approach was explored during this project
using mCherry-expressing T2 target cells. However, flow cytometry analysis revealed
low to no mCherry expression, so these cells were not included in the killing assays pre-
sented here.

As previously mentioned, sources of experimental variability may include T cell exhaus-
tion, compromised TCRs in TTD-expanded CD8+ T cells, artifact buildup, limited target-
effector cell interactions due to target cell aggregation, and technical inconsistencies.
Therefore, the killing assay should undergo further optimization and standardization to
improve reproducibility. Additionally, the cytotoxicity of peptide-expanded T cells should
be assessed using alternative assays to further investigate the potential sources of varia-
tion. These additional assays would help determine whether biological factors contribute
to variability or if the experimental setup lacks the robustness needed to accurately cap-
ture T cell cytotoxicity.

Conclusion
This study aimed to develop and optimize an in vitro killing assay to assess the cyto-
toxicity of peptide-specific CD8+ T cells. To achieve this, PMBCs from several donors
with different HLA profiles were used to expand CD8+ T cells specific to selected viral
peptides. Functional evaluation of the peptide-specific CD8+ T cells was performed to
complement the findings from the killing assays and provide a comprehensive evaluation
of the T cell activity.
Peptide-specific CD8+ T cell expansions were achieved, increasing the frequency of
antigen-specific T cells across most donors. Even pre-existing T cell populations with
initially low frequencies could undergo substantial expansion, yielding high expansion
factors and emphasizing the robustness of the protocol.
Functional assessment using intracellular cytokine stainings and activation induced mar-
ker assays confirmed the ability of the expanded cells to become activated upon pep-
tide stimulation. While all expansions exhibited a degree of cytokine production, there
were considerable differences among them. Activation profiles of the peptide-expanded
CD8+ T cells suggested distinct activation dynamics and phenotypes between expan-
sions. Among the expansions, some exhibited signs of T cell exhaustion, potentially
impacting their cytotoxic activity.
The cytotoxic capabilities of the peptide-expanded CD8+ T cells were evaluated using
a killing assay optimized for both cell number and target cell selection. While the killing
assays confirmed that the peptide-expanded CD8+ T cells were capable of target cell
killing, the efficiency and reproducibility varied across different expansions. The BC-447
TTD-expanded CD8+ T cells initially exhibited the highest killing efficiency, but this re-
sult was not consistently reproducible, possibly due to functional impairment or T cell
exhaustion. Others, such as BC-446 YLQ-expanded CD8+ T cells, demonstrated more
consistent killing profiles, though at lower magnitudes. These findings suggest that while
the established killing assay can capture the cytotoxicity of T cells, further optimization is
needed to ensure reproducibility and reliability.
Despite the aforementioned challenges, this study can serve as a foundation for future
research into T cell functionality and cytotoxicity in response to peptide stimulation. The
observed variability in T cell expansion, activation, and cytotoxic abilities underscores
the complexity of immune responses. Future work should, therefore, focus on further
optimizing the killing assay to address sources of variability.

Future perspectives
Several aspects need to be addressed to enhance the killing assay and improve its appli-
cability for evaluating T cell cytotoxicity. Further optimization is required to increase both
the reproducibility and sensitivity of the assay. Suggestions include reducing staining
variability with stable target cell lines expressing mCherry or GFP, which would ensure
more consistent fluorescence measurements. Incorporating fluorescently labeled apop-
tosis markers into the killing assay could also enhance its sensitivity, providing a more
precise assessment of cytotoxic activity. Additionally, using larger well plates may im-
prove assay robustness by accommodating higher cell numbers in each well.
The killing assay setup could be further evaluated by testing its ability to capture killing
events of peptide-expanded CD8+ T cells across a broader range of HLA types. In this
study, additional peptide-specific expansions were performed using PBMCs with the al-
leles HLA-A*11:01, HLA-A*24:01, HLA-B*15:01 and HLA-B*35:01, and these could be
used as effector cells in future killing assays. BM36.1 cells could still serve as target cells
by being transduced with HLA molecules matching those of the effector cells. This would
also allow for a more direct comparison with the previously conducted killing assays.
A more detailed characterization of the peptide-expanded CD8+ T cells is also necessary
to fully understand their cytotoxic potential. This can be achieved by generating compre-
hensive chemokine and cytokine profiles, offering deeper insights into the activation state
and potential exhaustion of the cells.
