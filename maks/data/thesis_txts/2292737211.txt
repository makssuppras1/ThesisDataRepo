Abstract: 
What influence does the height difference between the train carriage and the walkway 
combined with the width of the walkway have on the total evacuation time for trains in 
tunnels.  
The aim of this report is, on the basis of computer simulations, to analyze and combine test 
results from several different experiments on: 
1. Flow, speed and density of different widths of raised walkways. 
2. Door capacity of train doors with different vertical descent height (0.0-1.2m) 
Last present a conclusion on the correlation between the height difference and width of the 
walkway. 
 
Keywords: 
High difference, train carriage, walkway, tunnel evacuation.

1 Introduction 
 
Underground transportation systems:  
The bigger cities, all around the world, are establishing or expanding their underground 
transportation systems these days. These underground transportations systems will always be 
based on a thoroughly calculated cost benefit analysis.  
An important part of this analysis will always be the escape scenarios and thereby the escape 
routes. The escape routes in tunnels will always take up valuable and extremely costly space. 
Therefore, escape routes have to be carefully dimensioned to the transport system. Several 
reports and experiments have shed light on how the width of the walkway effects the speed of 
the evacuation. Further reports and experiments have shed light on how the vertical height 
difference between the train carriage and the walkway affects the speed of the evacuation. 
This report will combine and further investigate the results of previous experiments and create 
an overall picture of which conditions that interact the best and create the most desirable 
parameters. 
The construction of new underground transportation systems will almost always have a 
relation to an existing transportation system. The assessment of keeping these as two separate 
systems or to join them will be based on several different parameters. When connecting a new 
tunnel system to an existing system it will always be a big concern whether you can use the 
existing trains on the new expansion or use the existing tunnels with the new trains. It is 
common with older trains that there is a rather large vertical difference between the train-
floor and the surfaces of train platform/walkway.  
From the perspective of this report new underground transportation can be subdivided into 3 
main groups: 
1. National and international tunnels which are connected to the European railway system.  
These tunnels have to be built by the AEIF1 regulations and their specific codes. These tunnels 
will not benefit from the results of this report since the geometry of their escape routes is 
determined by the technical specifications of the AEIF code (0.7-1.2m walkway width 
depending on the length of the tunnel)2. 
 
2. New national underground transportations systems that are not connected to any other rail 
systems.  
 
3. Expansions of existing older national underground rail systems. 
 
This report will have relevance for group 2 and 3 since these have the possibility to shape their 
escape walkways to their specific criteria.

1.1 Evacuation Scenarios 
 
Evacuation from an underground transportation system is often very complex with regards to 
space, directions, density and heat. The space you can evacuate to is often a narrow walkway, 
which is often a single lane walkway to a safe place. Direction is often limited to one way 
which is the opposite of the smoke. The population density in the train carriage during rush 
hour can be very high. Heat and smoke build op can be very fast and intense.  
There are several different ways to construct tunnels and therefore several different ways and 
principals of evacuation. The evacuation scenarios are often a direct consequence of which 
construction solution you choose or the other way around. The most common evacuation 
principals are listed below.

It is important to understand that this model does not represent all rail systems that exist but 
are based on a fictive layout of a tunnel and train. The trainset consists of three train carriages, 
each train carriage has 3 doors, and each door is 2m wide. The train contains a total of 300 
people, 100 in each carriage. 
 
The variables tested in this report are the height difference between the train carriage floor 
and walkway in relation to the walkway width. The height difference on the train carriage floor 
and walkway determines the disembarking speed / descent rate. The walkway width 
determines the walk speed and density of the walkway.

2 Background 
 
A basic source of information and understanding of accidents in tunnels can be done through 
analyzing the accidents which have already happened in the past.  
The following accidents have been studied in relation with execution of this report. 
King’s Cross, 1987.3 
Fire on escalators of the multi storey underground-station King’s-cross. 
The fire occurred in the station but because of the complexity of the station passengers had a 
hard time locating the exits. 
 
Zürich, 1991.3 
Fire on train in the Zürich metro. Arson set fire on the train when leaving Stadelhofen station 
and entering the Hirschengraben tunnel.  
 
Baku, 1995.3 
Fire on train in the Baku metro. An electrical failure caused the fire. The train stopped in the 
tunnel and is to this day the worst railway tunnel fire measured in casualties.  
 
Kaprun, 2000.3 
Fire on funicular-train/ski-lift in the tunnel to the Kitzsteinhorn glacier. The fire started in a 
retrofitted heater fan. Communication to the driver was not possible because there was no 
intercom in the train. The steep incline (45°) contributed to creating a chimney effect and the 
majority of the passengers chose to move upwards and died of smoke inhalation. 
 
Daegu, 2003.3 
Fire on a train in the Daegu metropolitan subway, ignited a train coming up behind it. The 
second train was disconnected of electricity and could therefore not open the doors so the 
passengers could not escape 
 
Rinkeby, 2005.3 
Fire on a train in the Stockholm subway, on the station of Rinkeby. A fire emerged in the 
undercarriage of the train. The train driver was informed and told the passengers to leave the 
train. Luckily, the train was at a stop at the Rinkeby station when the fire was discovered and 
the driver could therefore initiate a rapid evacuation.

2.1 Walkway width 
 
J. Ahlfont and F. V. Lundströms results form the basis of my calculations and simulations with 
regards to the walkway input. 
Tunnelutrymning 
Effekten av gångbanans bredd på förflytningshastighet vid utrymning i en spårtunnel. 
av Jenny Ahlfont og Frida Vermina Lundström. 
Tunnel evacuation 
The effect of the walkway width on movement time when evacuating in railway tunnels. 
By Jenny Ahlfont og Frida Vermina Lundström. 
In 2012 J. Ahlfont and F. V. Lundström. performed a series of experiments. The aim was to 
determine the walking speed, flow and density of people evacuating on different widths of 
raised walkways. 
The experiments were performed at Lund University in a controlled environment, where 
people knew that they participated in an experiment. The population consisted of university 
students from 18-27 years of age. 
The Walkway was 2,3m wide, 18m long and raised 1,3m. The width of the walkway could be 
adjusted with a moveable wall which was 2.4m high. In each end of the walkway were 
staircases, 1,5m wide. The largest tested walkway width was 1.35m, so in that way the stairs 
were always wider than the walkways and didn’t decrease the flow on the walkway. 
 
The walkway was constructed of aluminum pillars and beams with a plywood deck. There were 
mounted 7 cameras at the line of 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11 and 13m. The speed and density were 
registered from camera 1 to 5 (1 to 9m) to make sure the stairs didn’t affect the results.

2.2 Vertical distance between the carriage and walkway 
 
In 2003 A. Norén and J. Winér. performed a series of observations. One of the targets was to 
estimate how the train-door flow depends on the vertical distance/descent. 
Modelling Crowd Evacuation from Road and Train Tunnels - Data and design for faster 
evacuations. 
By Anders Norén and Joel Winér. 
A. Norén and J. Winérs results, form the basis of my calculations and simulations with regards 
to the traindoor flow/ vertical descent input. 
 
A. Norén and J. Winér gather data in two different ways. 
1. through live observation of existing stations with different vertical descent height. 
(Stockholm, Utrecht, Schiphol) 
As scientific work the observations have their strengths and weaknesses. Participants 
represent a good normal distribution of the daily train passengers, but the environment and 
conditions do not give an accurate picture of an evacuation in a tunnel. 
 
2. By summing up the results from previous performed external exercises. 
(Best exercise and Stockholm exercise) 
These two exercises have each been carried out by NS / Nederlandse Spoorwegen / Dutch 
railways (Best exercise) and SJ / Svenska Jernbaneverket / Swedish railways (Stockholm 
exercise). The purposes of the two exercises were very different. 
The Best exercise was to observe and educate emergency personnel. 
The Stockholm exercise was to observe the relatively large descent height (1.2 m) and its 
influence on the evacuation time.

3 Method 
 
Using the simulation program STEPS (grid based flow model) and hand calculations, this report 
will analyze and combine results from previous experiments and reports. In an attempt to shed 
some light on how the width of the walkway and the vertical distances between the train 
carriage and the walkway interact when evacuating in a railway tunnel. 
The previous section describes what independent experiments have found respectively on 
walkway width and vertical height difference. Results from these previously performed 
experiments are combined even though they have no relation in the individual experiments. 
The evacuation will only take place in one direction. This will result in a longer evacuation time 
due to queue buildup on the walkway and in the train, if the walkway is too narrow for people 
to pas when other passengers are jumping/exiting the train carriages. 
 
Evacuation in one direction is one of the most likely scenarios because of smoke ventilation in 
the opposite direction. Basically, it depends on whether the train is longer or shorter than the 
distance to the nearest tunnel exit.  
This case scenario is very representative of many of the modern, short, Driverless subway / 
metro trains.  
The focus of this model will only be on the movement time. Detection, alerting and response 
time will not be a part of the simulation. 
Disabled and persons with reduced mobility are not included in this simulation. The reason for 
this is that there is very little scientific material and measurements on what impact disabled 
persons have on the evacuation on raised walkways. 
It is most likely that there may reside disabled or handicapped people on the train in an 
accident, therefore, this report does not create a complete picture of a potential evacuation.

Manipulating the walkway grid: 
 
Fig. 1 Manipulating the walkway grid, ex. 0.6m walkway width. 
 
Steps will not let you define anything but squared grids. This is limited in the way that when 
you define a grid to a surface/plane you can only enter one single value to define the grid. 
Under normal circumstances it makes perfect sense and it is always recommendable to make a 
square grid. The primary reason for this is that people need to move at the same speed in both 
the x and y direction of the plane. 
In section 2.1, graf 2 shows how the walkway density is determined by the walkway width.  
When the walkway becomes narrower than the grid, the grid has to be manipulated. This 
means that you actually have to overcompensate for the lack of width by over-sizing the grid. 
 
Example 0.6m wide walkway as shown in fig. 1: 
Max available symmetric grid 0.6m * 0.6m.  
Tested4 walkway density =1.2pers/m2  0.833m2/pers  0.913m*0.913m grid. 
However there is only 0.6m’s of width available, therefore, the right grid size is  
0.833m2/pers / 0.6m = 1.368m 
0.6m * 1.388m = 0.833m2/pers

5 Error analyses  
 
Disabled and persons with reduced mobility are not included in this simulation. The reason for 
this is that there is very little scientific material and measurements on what impact disabled 
persons have on the evacuation on raised walkways. The measurements will also greatly 
depend on the co-passengers that are expected to help them. If the situation becomes 
sufficiently threatening, it is questionable whether people will have an equal tendency to help, 
and this factor is difficult to simulate. 
It is most likely that there may reside disabled or handicapped people on an everyday train in 
an accident. 
The longer the train, the more queuing will occur and the longer it will take. The train is a 
fictional train which is relatively short. However, the train design is very similar to modern 
driverless subway trains. 
The external data that simulations on the vertical distances of 0.7 and 0.3 m is based on, is 
carried out with 2-3 steps on a stairway at a regular platform / station and not in a vertical 
jump. The experiments are based on observations that (Winér, 2003) are done on stations with 
ordinary disembarking from trains at the platform / station. 
In the Lund 2012 experiments with different walkway widths, the height of walkway was not 
changed along the width. We therefore have no data on what influence the height of the 
walkway has on how close people will walk to the edge or how fast people will walk on the 
walkway corresponding to a specific height of the walkway. 
 
5.1 STEPS model 
In the Lund 2012 experiments with different walkway widths, the only test subjects that were 
used were young students. This does not give a correct picture of a normal population. It is 
most likely that these young people create a higher walk speed and thus resulting in a better 
flow and possibly a higher density. 
It should be emphasized that these simulations are based on the results of several different 
experiments and thus do not give a 100% accurate insight into an actual trial. These 
experiments have not interacted with each other in real life. For example, the Lund 2012 
experiments that form the flow/speed and density on the walkway sre measured based on a 
"flowmodel". The door exit time is found by observing regular stations with every day 
disembarking from the train to the platform, there is thus no data on how wide the area they 
descend on is. 
 
As mentioned earlier in 3.1.1 you can only make a symmetrical grid in the simulations program 
STEPS. This is advisable because it simulates an even speed, flow and density in all directions. 
This however enables you to differentiate between forward and sideways density. In relation

to this simulation it creates some problems in the context of density in narrow passages. One 
example is that when the walkway width is 1,05m the density is 0.9 pers/m2. This results in a 
grid size of 1054 x 1.054m. The grid size is bigger than the walkway and thereby people are not 
able to overtake and everyone walks in a straight line behind each other. This is not an 
accurate picture of the reality, as this leads to the fact that the only real difference between 
0.6-1.05m width is the walking speed. In reality you would have a floating transition from 
single lane to double lanes in the spectra of 0,9 - 1,2m walkway width. This contributes to very 
straight results that is largely controlled by the doors capacity and the disembarking speed. 
Furthermore, it also gives an inaccurate picture of formation of queuing. When you are only 
able to define one density on the plane, it means that there won’t emerge tight queuing 
because the density is defined by the flow density. 
The difference in the disembarking speed for the vertical jump height of 0.7 and 0.3 is very 
small, only 0.07 pers/s. This seems unlikely and can be caused by a divergence in the tests 
which are used as input data for this model.  
 
Evacuation in reduced visibility (smoke). 
 
It would have been more scientifically correct to create two planes on the walkway; one in the 
length of the train carriages with reduced speed caused by reduced visibility, and one from the 
end of the last train carriage to the tunnel exit with the input speed.5 Karl Fridolf and others 
have made several experiments where they investigate the movement speed and exit choice 
of people in smoke-filled rail tunnels. These experiments are not focused on the width of the 
walkway, but you could interpolate between these experiments. The experiments were 
however conducted under very different circumstances. 
 
5.2 Manual calculations 
The manual calculations for the single lane models are very straight forward and simplified. In 
real life, there will probably be more people who overtake or bypass descending persons and 
thus the flow will be increased significantly. On the other hand, one can also fear that people 
will become impatient by waiting in a long queue and maybe even in a smoky environment 
where they will be tempted to escape the wrong way and be exposed to high smoke exposure 
like in the Kaprun disaster.

6 Conclusion  
 
6.1 Walkway width 
 
The fastest evacuation occurs as soon as the possibility to move in two lanes emerges. In this 
report/simulation the two lanes occur at widths from 1.2 to 1.35 m. This is based on the 
parameters of the walkway grid and density of 1.054m. In real life you will see a smoother 
transition from one to two lanes in the range between 0.9 - 1.2m of width. 
As soon as the two lanes occur, the flow of the walkway is increased significantly. The flow 
goes from being limited to the capacity of the last door of the last train carriage to include the 
capacity of the free lane. Furthermore, it is desirable to have two lanes if you have to evacuate 
people in wheelchairs or people with reduced mobility. 
 
6.2 Vertical distance between the carriage and walkway 
 
For the sake of the disabled and people in wheelchairs it is not an acceptable solution to have 
a vertical difference of more than 2cm. You cannot expect the fellow passengers to be able to 
carry people in wheelchairs or people with reduced mobility down onto the walkway. 
However, it should be noted that it is interesting to observe the results from the model with 
two lanes. With two lanes it is no longer the doors that limit the flow, the 8 doors which feeds 
the inner lane have no problem keeping up with the flow on the lanes. In fact there is only a 
difference of 20-25 sec. on the total evacuation time from 0.0m to 1.2m vertical height 
difference. It is necessary to note that this time may change drastically if the total length of the 
train is increased. 
 
6.3 Combined Vertical distance and walkway width 
 
There is no doubt that the most desirable case scenario is to have as small a height difference, 
combined with a walkway as wide as possible, but this is often a question of money during the 
construction phase. There should at least be a minimum of two lanes on the walkway. This 
corresponds to a minimum width of 1.2m. Standard handicap regulations prescribe 0,7m as 
the minimum accessible width of people in wheelchairs and 0,02m as the maximum height 
difference. This should be combined with the minimum width of a regular walking path 0,5-
0.7m. All in all, it is recommendable to design a walkway with at least 1.2-1.35m of width, and 
a height difference that doesn’t prevent wheelchair users from independently leaving the train 
0,0-0,002m. 
If the distance to the nearest tunnel exit is smaller than the length of the train it would be 
more acceptable to make a one lane walkway.

7 Future research  
 
How do disabled and handicapped people affect the total evacuation on various walkway 
widths? 
 
The data that this report is based on for Vertical Jump from train carriage to the walkway is 
partially based on observations at normal stations not in a tunnel and not onto a narrow 
walkway.  
 
How do people react when they have to evacuate to a 0.8-0.6m vide walkway. Will they stay 
on the walkway or will they jump on to the tracks? This is interesting in the context of disabled 
and handicapped people that will slow down the evacuation and create queuing, when the 
walkway is limited to one lane. 
 
Is it possible to use the tracks as escape route or will "railway ties" be an obstacle?

10.1 Single lane walkway 
 
Example 1. 
Walkway width = 0.6m 
Vertical distances = 1.2m. 
Walking speed = 1.33 m/s 
Walkway density = 1.2 pers/m2 
Door capacity = 0.5 pers/s 
 
1. Phase from 0 sek. To the walkway is fully occupied. 
 
((55m * 0.6m) * 1.2 pers/m2) / (0.5 pers/s * 8 doors) = 40 pers / 4 pers/s = 10 s 
 
Note: within the 10 sek. I takes to fill up the walkway, 5 persons (10 s * 0.5 per/s) will have 
exited the front door. 
 
2. Phase from fully occupied Walkway to all tree train carriages is emptied one by one 
through the front door of each carriage. 
 
45 pers. – 300 pers. = 255 pers.  
255 / 0.5 pers/s = 510 s. 
 
3. Phase as the last person leaves the last train carriage, the person furthest from the 
walkway exit walks the entire length of the walkway to the exit. 
 
100 m / 1.33 m/s = 75 s 
 
Total: 10 + 510 + 75 = 595 s.
