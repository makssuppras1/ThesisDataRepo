Abstract
In the modern world, we have a lot more news sources and ways to consume
news than ever before. We can consume news and share it with all our friends.
This can both be a blessing and a curse. If the news source is credible and true
it can be an easy way to stay up to date, however this can also lead to the spread
of false news leading to an ill informed public. In this thesis we are interested
in the dynamics of the spreading in a social system. In this case how topics are
spread through a social network.
To investigate this we have look at a collection of tweets from the Malaysian
Airlines ﬂight 17 crash. We have looked at some statistics of this data set, and
build a network over the tweets. Afterwards, we have classiﬁed the tweets with
latent Dirichlet allocation, and investigated how these topics spread through the
network.
Finally we have concluded that there is some evidence towards the topics spread-
ing though the system, but more ﬁne tuning needs to be done.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Spreading of information is an important tool in the modern world. As we use
online media more and more people are increasingly getting their information
from diﬀerent sources. People today might only get their news from Facebook
or Twitter, and not a from a single reputable source, which was the case a few
years back. Because of the multitude of news sources, today it is easier to spread
a particular message. This can both be a blessing and a curse, as new people
are given a new platform to communicate, but this communication can have
negative eﬀects if used for malicious purposes. Finding out how information
spreads is an important detail to think about going forward.
In this project we want to investigate large scale dynamics of spreading processes
in social systems. To do this we have looked at a collection of tweets surrounding
the Malaysian Airlines ﬂight 17 crash. As there was a lot of alternative theories
being spread about this event, we want to see if we can catch the spread of
information in a social system. The spreading of processes in a network is not
something new, but it has not been studied on this data set before, and that is
what makes this project interesting.
In this project we will start of by describing some basic statistics of the data,
like looking at the number of tweets over time. We then build a network over
the tweets, again looking at some characteristics of this network before looking
at the LDA model, to ﬁgure out what was talk about during the event. Finally

1.1
Background on the MH17
The data set is about the Malaysian Airlines ﬂight 17, MH17, crash that hap-
pened on the 17th of July 2014. The ﬂight was a scheduled ﬂight from Ams-
terdam at 10:13(UTC) to Kuala Lumpur International Airport supposed to be
arriving 12 hours later. As the ﬂight was crossing Ukrainian airspace the plane
crashed outside of Hrabove, a town near Torez in the Eastern Ukrainian oblast
(province) of Donetsk at around 13:20 UTC. [Mal]
The crash had a high fatality count, all 283 passengers and 15 crew members,
a total of 298 people, all died. Most of the people on board, 193 people, were
Dutch nationals while the next largest nationality on board was Malaysian with
43 citizens. As a result the Netherlands "started a criminal investigation on
suspicion of murder, war crimes and intentionally downing an airliner" [Par14]
and Ukraine’s National Bureau of Air Accidents Investigation asked the Dutch
Safety Board to co-ordinate an investigating into how the ﬂight was shot down.
[Par14]
The Dutch Saftey Board, DSB, released a preliminary report on the 9th of
September 2014 and concluded that the cause of the crash was not due to
technical faults or by the crew, and concluded that the crash was probably
caused by structural damage caused by a "large number of high-energy objects
that penetrated the aircraft from outside" [Jou14]. The full report was released
on the 13th of October and concluded that the plane was brought down by a
warhead ﬁred by a Buk surface-to-air missile system that struck the left-hand
side of the cockpit, and that the missile was ﬁred from somewhere in eastern
Ukraine [BBC15].
Lets take a moment to look at what was going on in Ukraine leading up to
the crash. During the same period as the crash Ukraine was going through a
prolonged crisis, which started on the 21st of November 2013 when the then
president decided to suspend an agreement with the European Union which
would have established an economic and political association between the two in
favour of an association with Russia. This sparked mass protests and led to the
Ukrainian revolution, or Euromaidan revolution, which then turned into violent
clashes between Ukrainian security forces and protesters before the Ukrainian
president was ousted and ﬂed to Russia on the 22 of February 2014 and a new

This in turn caused pro-Russian unrest in eastern and southern Ukraine, areas
of Ukraine that supported the ousted president, and are largely Russophone

the 14th of July 2014 by pro-Russian forces.
The joint criminal investigation team, JIT, into the crash was led by the Nether-
lands and also included Australia, Belgium, Malaysia and Ukraine CITE and
was concluded on the 28th of September 2016. The investigation concluded that
the missile that hit the aircraft came from a Buk missile system that came from
the Russian Federation, furthermore, the missile had been transported from
Russia on the day of the crash and returned back to Russian the next day. The
JIT also concluded that the missile had been ﬁred from the rebel held area near
the village of Pervomaiskyi. After the report came out Russia denied that the
Buk system came from Russia and did not accept the ﬁndings of the report.
[BBC14]
In the aftermath of the crash Russia has provided a number of diﬀerent reasons
for the MH17 crash. Four days after the crash the Russian defence ministry
released images that suggested that it was a Ukrainian jet or Buk missile that
caused the crash, in June 2015 a Russian investigation said they had a key

Russia’s ever changing story has been a subject in diﬀerent analyses.
The diﬀerence of narrative between the Russian news and Ukrainian has been a

likely to think that the Ukrainian military was responsible for the downing of
the plane. They also found that people who deﬁnitely or mostly trust Ukrainian
TV news blamed the Russians or Donbass militants more than the people who
do not trust this source, and those who watch Russian TV news are more likely
to be reluctant to give out blame. All this goes to show that who we trust and
where we get our news from has an important impact on how we perceive world
events.
In the paper Russian State Narrative in the Digital Age by Sarah Oats [Oat14],
she analyzes the narrative of the MH17 crash brought forth by the Russian
state-run TV channel Vremya compared to how the objective Western media,
in this case the BBC online, presented the crash using the Russian state-run
media as a proxy for the views of the Russian government. She argues that
the Russian news propaganda had to change its narrative as international news
sources are readily available and oﬀer an alternative to the Russian government’s
view of the news. She presents methods used by the Russian state to discredit
the West’s count of the news by using tactics like; not acknowledging Rus-
sia’s involvement in the Ukrainian conﬂict, discrediting information because it
comes from social media, failing to report why these events are happening and
only presenting personal tragic stories, promoting alternative theories, scare-
mongering, and framing the news in a way that makes Russia the victim of
American imperialism. [Oat14]
Russia is very good at framing the news to their advantage, not just on TV but
also through their famed so-called troll army [Ben16]. Matt Sienkiewicz argues
in his paper Open Buk [Sie15] that the Ukrainian government combated the

2.1
Twitter Data
Our data set is collected from Twitter. Twitter is a social media service where
a user can share thoughts restricted to 140 characters called tweets. Twitter’s
stated mission is "To give everyone the power to create and share ideas and
information instantly, without barriers"[Twi16]. Twitter allows registered users
to post tweets, while everyone else (registered and non-registered) can read their
tweets. According to their website Twitter has 313 million active users with a
billion unique monthly visits (As of June 30, 2016). An important way commu-
nications happen on Twitter are through hashtags (#), which are keywords that
let users categorize their tweets so that they can communicate their thoughts on
that topic to other users. It is also possible to reply to other users and re-tweet
(embed other users tweet on your own feed), as a way to converse with other
and your own followers. Data from Twitter can be collected through the Twitter
API, which contains data like: tweet id, user id, time stamp, tweet text, ect.
[Twi16]
The data set we are working on is a collection of tweets collected from the 3rd of
January 2014 to the 8th of December 2016 and is about the MH17 plane crash.
The tweets are collected in a *.txt-ﬁle which has the ﬁelds:

To ﬁgure out which languages are in the data set, we have used the Python
package langdetect [Shu10]. This package is a direct port of Google’s language
detection library written in Java and ported to Python. This package uses Naive
Bayes to classify documents into languages, the way that this works is that we
want to update the posterior probability of a category, in this case a language,
by:

Where Ck is the k’th language and x1, ..., xn are features in the documents. The

characters from text written in non-Latin texts, and ﬁnally it removes Latin

Chapter 3
Network
To analyze the spread in the data set, we now build a network. A network
is a way of representing a relationship between two actors. In a network we
typically have vertices, also called nodes, that are connected by a links, or edge.
Networks can be undirected or directed, a directed graph means that there is a
one way relationship from one node to another, while in an undirected network
the relationship goes both ways. Networks can be used to model diﬀerent things,
we can the to model the internet, or we could use them to model an electrical
grid.
Another use for networks is to model social interactions.
In a social
network the nodes would be the users and, depending on what we want to
model, an edge is formed if the person has interaction with another user in
some form. In our case we want to create a network where the Twitter users are
the nodes, and they are linked together if they have had a written interaction
with each other.
To build this network we add a directed edge between two nodes if a user has
replied to another user’s tweet, this means that each edge is a speciﬁc tweet.
Because of the way we create the network, we will not use all the data points
in the ﬁle only the tweets that have a corresponding reply user id. The number
of tweets with a reply user id is 48141 which is 5.12% of the total data and is
spread out between 29290 users which is 6.5% of all the users. These users are
the vertices and the tweets between them are the edges that we will base our
network on. This network has a few problems, where the biggest problem is

Topic Modeling
Now that we have looked into some basic statistics and the network of the data,
it is time to take a deeper look at what users are talking about. Reading through
a large number of tweets is a time consuming and tedious task, luckily we can
use machine learning and natural language processing methods to extract topics
from a document. Topic modeling are statistical models that try to learn the
topics of a collection of documents, and for this project we have chosen to use
the latent Dirichlet allocation model which was proposed by Blei, Ng and Jordan
in 2003[BNJL03].
In this chapter we will start oﬀby describing the theory behind the topic model
latent Dirichlet allocation, which is a probabilistic topic model. Next, we take a
look at model selection to try to ﬁnd out which parameters create a model that
ﬁts best to our data set. We use some diﬀerent methods to evaluate the model
including; AIC, BIC, loglikelihood and perplexity. Finally we will use the model
and look at an example and apply the model to our collection of tweets.

4.1
Theory
In this section we will describe the theory behind the latent Dirichlet alloca-
tion model, or LDA. LDA is a generative probabilistic model that can be used
in general as a statistical model to describe how observable discreet data are
grouped when described by some unobservable data. [GS04] In this case, we
want to use LDA to ﬁnd unobservable topics from a corpus of text. As men-
tioned above the LDA was ﬁrst formulated as a topic model by Blei, Ng and
Jordan in 2003 and independently by Pritchard, Stephens and Donnelly in 2000,
as a model to assign individuals to population groups from genetics. The model
is built upon the probabilistic latent semantic index, pLSI, which itself is an ex-
tension of the latent semantic index, LSI. The LSI, is a dimensionality reduction
method that tries to ﬁnd a a linear subspace in the tf-idf space that captures
the most variance through singular value decomposition , however, instead of
an algebraic method we would like to formulate the problem with a probabilis-
tic model. This led to the formulation of the pLSI model which uses mixture
models to sample each word in a document. The pLSI model was incomplete as
a probabilistic model, as it did not model probabilistic at the document level,
this is the problem that was solved by the LDA model.[BNJL03]
When we are talking about topic modeling we usually have three important
terms to consider: corpus, documents and words. Let us start by quickly deﬁning
these terms:
• Word: This is our primary data and the only data that is truly observable.
This data is discrete and is indexed by {1, ..., V }, where V is the number
of words.
• Document: This is a collection of N words deﬁned as w = (w1, w2, ..., wN).
This makes w represent documents in a bag of words representation.
• Corpus: This is the collection of M documents which can be deﬁned as:
D = {w1, w2, ..., wM}
As topic modeling is a classiﬁcation problem, we want to ﬁnd a model that
classiﬁes each document to some topic. Before talking about the LDA model,
let us take a step back and talk in general about classiﬁcation models and
generative models. [BNJL03]

model for the posterior probability, p(Ck|x), and during the decision stage we

infer the prior p(Ck), and ﬁnally we can use Bayes’ theorem , given by:

4.2.1
Akaike Information Criterion
One method to measure how well a model ﬁts the data is the Akaike information
criterion, AIC. AIC measures the trade oﬀbetween the goodness of ﬁt, or how
well the data ﬁts the observations, of the model and the complexity of the model,
how many parameters are used in the model. The AIC value of a model is given
by the formula:
AIC = 2k −2 ln(L)
(4.11)
Here we assume that we have some model, where k is the number of estimated
parameters, and L is the maximized value of the likelihood function. As we
==PAGE:46==
40

can see, the AIC value depends on two things, the number of parameters and
the likelihood function.
The ﬁrst part of the term in equation 4.11, 2k, is
an increasing function that discourages overﬁtting as increasing the number of
parameters usually leads to a better ﬁt, as seen in ﬁgure 4.2. The second part
uses the likelihood function to estimate the goodness of the ﬁt. The two parts
work together by penalizing the value when adding more parameters as the
likelihood increases because of overﬁtting.
4.2.2
Bayesian Information Criterion
Another measure that we consider is the Bayesian information criterion, or BIC.
This criterion is closely related to the AIC model we just looked at. The BIC
criterion is deﬁned as:
BIC = ln(n)k −2 ln(L)
(4.12)
In BIC L and k are the maximized values of the likeihood function and the
number of free parameters to be estimated, just as in AIC, and n is the number
of observations in the data.
This looks a lot like AIC, however, in general BIC penalizes parameters more
strongly than AIC.

Looking at these ﬁgures are all well and good, however it is not very scientiﬁc

5.1 Permutation Test
59
the topics just tweets that have been assigned at random. We want to quantify
how the topics relate to each other in the network, and to quantify this we need
to start by making some assumptions; ﬁrstly we assume that users that have
tweeted in the same topic must be closer together, and secondly we assume that
these users must cluster together. To quantify our assumption we need to create
a statistical test, and here we will use a permutation test.
To start oﬀwith we need to talk about our hypothesis for our statistical test.
In this test we need to formulate a null hypothesis and an alternate hypothesis;
our alternative hypothesis is the assumption that we hope is true, while our null
hypothesis, H0, is a statement that chance is responsible for the result. What we
hope to ﬁnd when running our test, is that we have a sample that is so unlikely
that we can reject the null hypothesis, in other words we expect the sample is
generated from a random process, and we can reject this hypothesis if we ﬁnd
that the sample is unlikely to have been generated from a random sample. To
ﬁnd the distribution of this random process we use a permutation test.
A permutation test is a statistical test to test the statistical signiﬁcance of
the null hypothesis.
This is calculated by calculating all values of the test
statistic when rearranging all the labels on the observed data points. When this
is done we can calculate how many times our permutation test conﬁrms our
null-hypothesis, and use this p-value to see whether our sample is statistically
signiﬁcant.
So let us formulate the what we want to test for our problem. In our problem
we want to perform two permutations test to test two diﬀerent null hypothesis.
The ﬁrst null hypothesis is for our shortest path assumption as is formulated
as:
H0: The average distance between shortest paths in a topic is generated from
the random distribution over the average distance between nodes
We want to ﬁnd statistical signiﬁcance for our assumption that the average
shortest path between users in the same topic are smaller than if the topics
were assigned at random, so need the opposite of this as our null hypothesis.
We also will reject the null hypothesis if our observation is signiﬁcantly larger,
this is called a two tailed test, and we reject our null hypothesis if it is under
2.5% at each end. Next we need a null hypothesis to test our assumption for
the average clustering in a topic, we deﬁne the null hypothesis as:
H0: The average clustering between nodes in a topic is generated from the ran-
dom distribution over the average clustering between nodes
Here the alternative hypotheses is that the average clustering in the topics are

tion test to test these hypothesis. For these two hypotheses we can think of the
mean of the permutation test as having a p-value of 0.5 and we reject the null
hypothesis if we get a p-value that is signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from 0.5, as this is a
two-tailed test we reject if the p-value is under 0.025. For the permutation test
for our shortest path assumption, we start by creating 1000 random networks
where we randomly assign the same number of nodes to each topic as there are
in each of our found topics. For the shortest path test, we compute the shortest
path for each node pair in the topics and ﬁnd the average over these. This
means that we get 1000 average shortest paths for each topic. For the clustering
assumption we take the subgraph of the randomly assigned nodes and compute
the average clustering of these nodes.
Again we get 1000 average clustering
values that we can use as our distribution. After calculating this we can look at
some results.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Having look at all this what have we learned? With the permutation test we
have seen that there is some evidence toward our assumption that topics spread
in the network. This can more clearly be seen in year 2015 than in other years.
Sending some more time trying to ﬁnd a better number of topics for the diﬀerent
years, probably would help improve the results, as it seems that we are ﬁnding
some form of structure in the information.
Through this project we have look into a lot of diﬀerent statistics, starting with
some basic statistics. Here we found that the number of tweets are correlated
with when news come out about this event, this is logical as people want to
discuss what is going on right now. We also found out the data set is mostly
made up of English tweets and that most of the tweets that have location come
from either the Netherlands or Malaysia.
Next we build a directed network over the tweets, where an edge was formed if
a user had replied to another user’s tweet. We narrowed our focus to the largest
connected component, and found that most of the users only tweeted once or
recived one tweet in the data set. We then split our analysis to look at the
diﬀerent years. In the ﬁrst year, 2014, we had the most number of active users
with 9447 active users. From the in and out degree distribution of the nodes
in 2014 we seem to see that a lot of reporting is going on, and not as much
discussion as in 2015 and 2016. The years 2015 and 2016 seem more like each
==PAGE:74==

other in that they have a positive correlation between in and out degrees and
have users that appear often according to diﬀerent measures.
We have also described the topic model, latent Dirichlet allocation and found
the 11 topics for the corpus over the diﬀerent years. Finding that the model
usually can distinguish between languages, and ﬁnding topics in English that
are clearly distinguishable from each other. Finally we have transferred these
topics over to the network, and seen that there is some statistical evidence for
that the information spreads. These ﬁndings are promising, however they need
more work to be useful.
