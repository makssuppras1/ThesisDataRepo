Abstract
Severe fire accidents in concrete tunnels have shown the importance of the development
of research in the area of structural fire safety design. Especially, the research of hydro-
gen vehicles are important, since the effect of a hydrogen fire in a tunnel is not fully
understood.
The aim of this thesis is to analyse the thermal and structural response of a reinforced
concrete slab exposed to a hydrogen fire. A finite element model is developed in the
software program Abaqus. The main focus is on developing the thermal model based
on different data input from a fire model. The issues regarding model coupling between
fire model and thermal model are highlighted.
The thermal model of the concrete slab is a simplified 2d beam that represents the cross
section of the slab. The thermal model is verified with an analytical solution and a fire
curve, where the temperature distribution is already known. The hydrogen fire and the
material properties of the concrete slab is then applied to the thermal model.
The maximum temperature over time is investigated and sensitivity analyses of impor-
tant parameters are obtained for a local part of the slab. The temperature distribution
along the the entire cross section of the slab is investigated, and no reduction of steel
strength is seen.
The risk of explosive spalling of the concrete surface layer is also investigated. A spalling
model is developed by removing a layer of concrete from the thermal model based on
an explosive spalling criteria.The temperature distribution of the spalling model is in-
vestigated, and no reduction of steel strength is seen. Only a small layer of concrete is
reduced in strength.
The allowable design yield strength of the steel is investigated in order to determine,
if the steel strength may be reduced when exposed to the hydrogen fire. From these
investigations, it is seen that there is no reduction in the load bearing capacity of the
concrete slab when exposed to the hydrogen fire.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Structural fire safety is an important part of designing structures especially in tunnels
where the combination of flammable vehicles and limited space can result in severe
accidents. Fire accidents from vehicles has can occur inside tunnels, car parks and other
large structures. Since concrete is commonly used in tunnels and other large structures, it
has been subjected to a large amount of research in relation to fire. With new structural
designs and an increase in new hydrogen and electrical vehicles, more research needs to
be conducted on structural fire safety of tunnels.
A fire accident inside a tunnel can cause both great damage to the concrete structure
and endanger human lives. This happened in 1999 where a fire broke out inside the
Mont Blanc tunnel. The damages after the fire can be found in Figure 1.1. Another fire
accident happened in the Great Bealt tunnel in 1994 which caused severe damage to the
concrete structure. In Figure 1.2 it is seen that the fire caused explosive spalling of the
concrete where most of the cover layer had spalled away.
Figure 1.1. The Mont Blanc tunnel after a
fire accident in Italy/France 1999 [29].
Figure 1.2. Spalling due to fire in the Great
Bealt tunnel in Denmark 1994 [38].
Explosive spalling is a phenomenon that might happen during fire where the concrete
surface layer is heated. Under the right circumstances, this can lead to the concrete sur-
face layer exploding away, which is referred to as spalling or explosive spalling. Spalling
can result in a reduced load bearing capacity or completely destroy an entire cross-
section of a construction [22]. Depending on how dense the concrete is and its moisture

content, spalling might occur. In research it has been found, that dense concrete and
a high moisture content can cause spalling as well as thermal and external stresses on
the structure [22]. In circular tunnel walls, the thermal curvature is hindered by the
geometry which causes thermal stresses. Further research is still needed on explosive
spalling since a complete theory on the subject is missing. Numerical modeling can be
used to model explosive spalling and extend the knowledge on whether a construction
will be susceptible to spalling or not.
The flame from a hydrogen vehicle has a much higher heat release rate than a gasoline
passenger vehicle. This means that the gas temperature also becomes much higher. If a
hydrogen vehicle suddenly catches fire inside a tunnel, the concrete at the ceiling could
be exposed to local elevated temperatures over 1500°C [28]. These high temperatures
can cause both spalling and reduced load bearing capacity of the concrete construction.
In a study on explosive spalling by Deeny et al. [14], a finite element analysis was used
to model spalling of a simply supported reinforced concrete beam.
A heat transfer
analysis of the cross section is performed using Abaqus. Two models, abrupt spalling
and progressive spalling, are investigated. In the first model, the spalling is modelled by
removing all the elements making up the bottom concrete cover. In the second model,
the spalling is modelled by only removing single layers of elements at a time. The results
indicated that spalling invariable triggers an early failure of the beam when it is exposed
to the standard fire.
A study on risks and implications of traﬀic incident in tunnels involving hydrogen fuel
cell vehicles was made by LaFleur et al. [28]. A scenario, where the hydrogen from
a hydrogen fuel cell vehicles ignites is investigated. When the hydrogen ignites, it is
most likely a jet flame from the pressure relief device release due to a hydrocarbon
fire. A Sandia-developed code called Sierra was used to perform a computational fluid
dynamics simulation, a heat transfer simulation, and a solid mechanics analysis of the
structural members. The scenario was modelled in two different tunnels (CANA and Ted
Wiliams). The CANA Tunnel has a concrete roof where it was of interest to determine
the possibility o explosive spalling due to rapid temperature increase on the concrete
roof. The Ted Williams Tunnel have concrete panels supported by steel hangers which
are attached to the tunnel roof with bolts anchored in epoxy. Besides the concern for
spalling, the steel hangers were also investigated. The worst-case scenarios were seen
when the ventilation is not operating. Both the CANA and Ted Williams Tunnel results
show that the thermal conditions may result in localized spalling in the area where the
hydrogen jet flame impinges the ceiling.
Since it can be expensive in time and cost to conduct experimental research on fires in
tunnels, numerical modeling is often used in research. In numerical modeling, the model
is validated analytically or with experimental results in order to obtain reliable models
that can predict the concretes behaviour at elevated temperatures. The Finite Element
modeling (FEM) is commonly used as a numerical modeling method.

1.2
Aim and Method
The aim of this project is to investigate the thermal and structural response of a re-
inforced concrete slab in a tunnel exposed to a hydrogen fire flame from a hydrogen
vehicle.
The main analysis of the concrete slab will be focused on a 2D thermal model made
in Abaqus.
Quantities from a Computational Fluid Dynamic model (CFD) will be
transferred to thermal model of the concrete slab. The quantities include the surface
temperature and the heat flux data. This data will be transferred from the CFD model
to the thermal model and analysed in different thermal models in order to assess if
the data input simulates the hydrogen fire suﬀiciently. From the thermal model, the
temperature distribution of the slab will be outputted and the load-bearing capacity
will be assessed in order to analyse whether the hydrogen fire reduces the load bearing
capacity of the slab.
The risk of explosive spalling of the outer concrete surface layer will also be investigated.
The influence of the explosive spalling on the temperature distribution will be analysed
in the thermal model. Based on the results of the thermal model, it will be assessed
whether a mechanical model is required to model the mechanical response.

Theoretical background
2.1
Fire curves
The first step to assess in structural fire safety design is the fire model. The goal is to
find a model that can predict the gas temperature in a fire compartment. Depending
on the design approach, different fire models can be used.
The fire models include
computational fluid dynamics (CFD) models, the standard fire and parametric fires.
The standard fire represents a fully developed fire, and does not take the cooling phase
into account, whereas a parametric fire and CFD model does. Therefore, when it is
needed to have an more accurate representation of an fire, the parametric fire or CFD
model is typically used. The output of a fire model is a time-temperature curve which
can be inputted in a thermal model to calculate the heat distribution inside a structural
member exposed to fire.
According to EN 1991-1-2 [4] the standard fire ISO 834 is usually the most relevant
design fire in a fire compartment. The ISO 834 curve is used for structural members
exposed to fire, and it is expressed as:
θg = 20 + 345 log10(8t + 1)
(2.1)
Where θg is the gas temperature and t is the time [min]. Another fire model without
a cooling phase is is the hydrocarbon curve which is used to model the burning of
hydrocarbon-based materials i.e. small petroleum fire from car fuel tanks.
θg = 1080(1 −0.325e−0.167t −0.675e−2.5t) + 20
(2.2)
Where t is the time [min].
A third fire curve is the RWS curve from the Netherlands which have been verified with
full scale tests. It simulates a worst case scenario where a 50 m3 fuel, oil or petrol tanker
is on fire. This kind of fire can cause a fire load of 300 MW for the duration of 120
minutes.

2.2.1
Heat Transfer
An important output of the thermal model of a structure is the temperature profile.The
temperature profile is obtained from the heat transfer model. The heat transfer model
consists of three mechanisms: conduction, convection and radiation. Theses mechanism
are presented in this chapter based on [11].
2.2.1.1
Conduction
Conduction occurs when molecules that are in contact, transfer heat energy from one to
another by means of vibration. This can occur in solids, fluids and gasses. It is easier
to transfer heat through tightly packed molecules. Therefore it is important to consider
the thermal conductivity of a solid structure i.e. concrete structure, since he molecules
are more tightly packed. The thermal conduction can be formulated as steady-state
or transient-state one dimensional heat transfer. For the steady-state condition, it is
assumed that the temperature does not change with time, which results in a constant
heat flow. For the transient state analysis, it is assumed that the temperature is time
dependent, which results in a transient heat flow. The heat transfer for the steady-state
and transient-condition is investigated further in Appendix A and B where the heat flux
is calculated analytically and numerically for a steel beam.

elevated temperatures
In this chapter, the behaviour of concrete and steel during fire exposure is investigated.
When concrete structures are exposed to high temperatures the strength of both the
concrete and steel is reduced. However the two materials are affected differently since
the steel is protected by the concrete layer and concrete is a heterogeneous material.
Furthermore, during fire exposure the phenomenon of explosive spalling of concrete may
occur.
Explosive spalling is also investigated, since its occurrence imposes a risk of
reduction in the load-bearing capacity of the concrete structure.
2.3.1
Concrete
The deterioration of concrete when exposed to fire is described in this section. This is
also described by Hertz [23]. The constituents of concrete are aggregates and cement
paste. The aggregates are made of sand, gravel and stones, and the cement paste which is
porous and hydrated is made of hydrated calcium and calcium hydroxide. The moisture
in concrete is situated in the concrete pores as free water and in the cement paste as
chemically bound water.
In the beginning of a fire exposure, the free water starts to evaporate. When the con-
crete temperatures becomes higher than 150 ◦C, the chemically bound water from the
hydrated calcium silica is released [18]. This process has a local peak at 270 ◦C. As a
consequence of this development, the cement paste starts to shrink, and the aggregates
start to expand. This behaviour at 200 ◦C is shown in Figure 2.6a, which causes tensile
stresses in the cement paste. At 200 ◦C the concrete is still intact, and the hydrated
calcium silica can still be rebuild. If the fire is stopped before the concrete temperature
reaches 300 ◦C, the concrete absorbs moisture from the air, which rebuilds the chemical
bonds. When the concrete temperature reaches 300 ◦C, micro cracks starts to appear in
the concrete. This behaviour is shown in Figure 2.6b. Due to the cracks, the strength
of the concrete becomes permanently reduced at 300 ◦C and above [23]. The thermal
conductivity, E-modulus, tensile strength and compressive strength of the concrete is
reduced.

2.3.3
Explosive Spalling
Explosive spalling is a phenomenon that can occur in concrete structures exposed to
fire. It can cause serious damage to the concrete structure, reduction in load capacity
and in worst case risk human lives. There exist several types of spalling and they can
generally be classified into three types: Aggregate spalling, corner spalling and explosive
spalling [14]. Aggregate spalling involves small pieces of concrete flying off the surface
due to aggregate failure. Aggregate spalling happens close to the surface and only causes
superficial damage. Corner spalling is a phenomenon where larger corner pieces fall of
the concrete. This usually occurs at a later stage into the fire when the concrete has
been considerably weakened. The temperature difference between the reinforcement and
concrete causes a difference in deformation. This leads to tensile cracks which eventually
leads to corner pieces falling of. The explosive spalling usually occurs at beginning of
the fire [22]. During explosive spalling, large pieces of the concrete surface layer explodes
when the concrete structure is exposed to high and rapidly rising temperatures. The
concrete pieces breaks off at high velocities which can be cause a threat to the stability
of the structure and its surroundings.
Explosive spalling has the biggest impact on the concrete structure since this type of
spalling causes the most serious loss of material. Therefore it is explosive spalling that
is of focus in this thesis. In this section, the causes of explosive spalling, and the factors
that influence explosive spalling are presented. This is also explained by So [37]. Some
experimental studies on explosive spalling are discussed in order to obtain quantitative
data which is used to make an explosive spalling criteria.
2.3.3.1
Causes of explosive spalling
The causes of explosive spalling have been classified into three categories: Build up of
pore pressure, thermal stresses, and combined high pore pressure with thermal stresses.
This is also explained by So [37].
Build up of pore pressure
This theory is also explained by Meyer-Ottens [30] and Shorter and Harmathy [36].
Concrete has a certain amount of moisture content. There is free water located in the
capillary pores of the concrete as well as bound water in the cement paste.
When
the concrete surface is heated up, the moisture is forced both out and further into the
concrete due to the temperature gradient. This results in three moisture zones as shown
in Figure 2.11. The water inside the pores starts to evaporate at a certain depth from
the concrete surface, and three moisture zones occurs. The first zone closest to the
concrete surface is a dry zone. The second zone is an evaporation intermediate zone,
and the third zone is a moisture saturated zone. The water vapor condenses again in
the moisture saturated zone which acts as an moisture clog so where the vapor can not
==PAGE:34==
26
2 Theoretical background
penetrate. It is in the depth between the the evaporation intermediate zone and the
moisture saturated zone that the capillary pore water transitions from liquid to vapor
which can cause very high pore pressures to build up [30].
Figure 2.11. Principle of the three moisture zones in ordinary concrete and high performance
concrete [37].
The high pore pressure can be relieved by the water vapour escaping the pores or by
explosive spalling. The ability of the water vapour to escape the pores is dependent on
the density of the concrete. The density of the concrete dictates the permeability of the
concrete i.e. concrete with high density has a low permeability. The maximum pore
pressure build up is dependent on the permeability of the concrete. If the water vapor
can not escape the concrete matrix, then explosive spalling might occur. This depends
on the maximum reached pore pressure. This theory of build up of pore pressure suggests
that explosive spalling occurs, when the pore pressure exceeds the tensile strength of
the capillary pores [41].
Thermal stresses
Within concrete exposed to fire, tensile stresses can occur, if the concrete surface is ex-
posed to rapid heating rates. This theory is also explained by Saito [33] and Dougill [15].
When the concrete is exposed to the elevated temperatures, temperature gradients occur
within the concrete from the surface and toward the inner sections. The temperature
gradient causes tensile and compressive stresses within the concrete. Other factors that
can cause an increase in stresses is hindered expansion, loads and heating rates. This
theory of thermal stresses suggest that when the compressive stresses close to the heated
surface exceeds the compressive strength of the concrete then explosive spalling occurs.
Dougill has explained that explosive spalling do not always occur due to compression fail-
ure because concrete is not a linear elastic material. Also the theory of thermal stresses
do not explain the fact that explosive spalling do not occur in traditional concrete unless
the moisture content is above 3-4 % by weight [22].

This theory combines the mechanism of high pore pressure and thermal stresses to
explain the cause of explosive spalling.
The theory is proposed by Zhukov [42] and
Connolly [12]. A sketch of the mechanism is shown in Figure 2.12.
Figure 2.12. Explosive spalling of concrete due to combined thermal stresses and pore pressure.
[37].
Zhukov [42] has proposed that the stresses that develops within the concrete exposed to
elevated temperatures are superimposed upon each other. He classifies the stresses as:
load-induced stresses, thermal stress and pore pressure stresses. It is the summation of
these stresses that should be compared to the strength of the concrete. Since explosive
spalling may be due to this combined theory, and the many different factors influencing
it may have opposing effects on the mechanism of explosive spalling, not one single set
of conditions can explain when explosive spalling occurs Khoury and Anderberg [27].
2.3.3.2
Factors influencing explosive spalling
Some of the known factors influencing explosive spalling is presented in this section. The
factors have been classified into three types: Material-related factors, mechanical factors
and heating. This is also explained by So [37].
==PAGE:36==
28
2 Theoretical background
Material-related factors
• The moisture content of concrete has an significant influence on the occurrence
of explosive spalling. The build up of pore pressure due to elevated temperatures
becomes higher for concrete with high moisture content, and this increases the risk
of explosive spalling.
• The concrete permeability also influences the risk of explosive spalling. In high
density concrete, the permeability is low and the ability to release the pore pressure
becomes smaller. As explained in the theory of build up of pore pressure, if the
permeability is too low the pore pressure can not be released and explosive spalling
occurs. Even though the permeability in high strength concrete usually is lower,
the tensile strength is higher. These are two counteracting influences which can
cancel each other out [27].
• The compressive strength also has a high influence on the occurrence of explosive
spalling. Since concrete with a high strength grade has a lower permeability the
risk of explosive spalling is increased.
Mechanical factors
• The load levels of a concrete structure influences the risk of explosive spalling.
A high load level causes higher compressive stresses because of the restraint to
expansion. If the heating rate is high enough, the compressive stresses build up.
This increases the risk of explosive spalling, if the stresses can not be relieved
otherwise quickly enough.
• The cross section geometry also influence the risk of explosive spalling. In example,
a cross section with rounded corners and suﬀicient reinforcement cover and spacing
lowers the risk.
• Thermal expansion is also a factor that influences the occurrence of explosive
spalling. In a concrete structure with fixed ends the thermal expansion induces
thermal stresses in the concrete which increases the risk of explosive spalling.
Heating characteristics
• Another important factor influencing the occurrence of spalling is the heating rate
of the fire. Higher heating rates usually increases the risk of explosive spalling.
• Higher temperature gradients along the thickness of the concrete member also
increases the risk of explosive spalling. The temperature gradient is related to the
heating rate where higher heating rates induce higher temperature gradients which
induce thermal stresses. As explained in the theory of thermal stresses, they can
cause explosive spalling.

• The absolute temperature of the concrete also influences the occurrence of explosive
spalling. However different experiments on explosive spalling show different results
on the absolute temperature at which explosive spalling occurs.
The absolute
temperature can not be immediate determined since the risk of explosive spalling
is influenced by many other factors.
2.3.3.3
Spalling according to EN-1992-1-2
According to EN 1992-1-2[6], explosive spalling should be avoided when designing struc-
tural concrete members or the influence of explosive spalling on the performance require-
ments should be taken into account. In regards to preventive measures, Eurocode states
that the occurrence of explosive is unlikely, when the moisture content of the concrete
is less than 3 % by weight. If the moisture content is higher, Eurocode recommends
that the moisture content, type of aggregates, permeability of concrete and heating rate
should be assessed more accurate. Eurocode also states that when using tabulated data
to design a normal weight concrete member, the moisture content criteria is valid if
the cover layer is 70 mm or more. Eurocode also states for slabs and tensile members
with a moisture content higher than 3 % that the explosive spalling can be assessed by
assuming local loss of cover to one reinforcing bar or bundle of bars. After which the
reduced load-bearing capacity should be checked. In the danish national annex for EN
1992-1-2 DK NA [31] the recommended value of 3 % moisture content is also chosen if
the concrete is at most densified with 6 % of the cement weight of particles that are
smaller than the cement grains.
Regarding the concrete strength grades, EN 1992-1-2[6] states that the moisture content
criteria applies to concrete grades C55/67 to C80/95, if the maximum content of silica
fume is less than 6 % by weight of the cement. For concrete grades 80/95 < C ≤90/105
Eurocode states that at lest one of four methods (method A, B, C and D) should be
applied. Method A involves adding a reinforcement mesh with a nominal cover of 15 mm
where the wires should have a diameter ≥2 mm with a pitch leq 50 x 50 mm. In this
method it is also required that the main reinforcement has a nominal cover ≥40 mm.
Method B involves demonstrating by testing that no spalling of the concrete occurs under
fire exposure. Method C involves applying protective layers to the concrete surface where
it has been demonstrated that no spalling occurs under fire exposure. Method D involves
adding fibres to the concrete mix. More than 2 kg/m3 of monofilament propylene fibres
should be added. According to the danish national annex for EN 1992-1-2 DK NA [31],
method B and C can be applied.
2.3.3.4
Literature review on explosive spalling
Even though studies have investigated the causes and influencing factors of explosive
spalling, more research is needed in order to establish a model that can predict, if explo-

sive spalling will occur during a fire. The focus of this thesis is therefore to investigate
some studies on explosive spalling to obtain an explosive spalling criterion. This criterion
is then used to analyse the thermal model of the hydrogen fire in order to investigate
whether or not there is a risk of explosive spalling.
According to Hertz [22], traditional concrete will not spall with less than 3 % moisture
content by wight, and there is only a limited risk of spalling of traditional concrete with
a moisture content of 3-4 % by weight.
According to Harmathy [17], spalling is unlikely to occur, if the permeability of the
concrete is greater than about 5 · 10−11cm2.
This criteria is deduced based on the
moisture clog theory described by Shorter and Harmathy [35].
Heating rates of 20-30 ◦C/min are found in the literature to be significant for spalling
Khoury [26]. These heating rates are typical for fires and explosive spalling typically
occurs in the first 20-30 minutes into the fire. However Ali et al. [9] investigated the
occurrence of explosive spalling in normal reinforced concrete slabs exposed to a convec-
tional fire curve (BS476) and a severe hydrocarbon fire curve. The slabs were loaded
with 65 % of it design load and was only heated from the bottom side. For the BS476
and severe hydrocarbon fire curve the first occurrence of spalling was seen after 15 min-
utes and 2 minutes, respectively. Therefore, under the right conditions and with a high
heating rate explosive spalling can occur within the first minutes of the fire.
Akhtaruzzaman and Sullivan [8] exposed 50 x 75 x 840 mm long beams to heating and
found that explosive spalling occurred for a moderate heating rate of 10 ◦C/min.
However Hertz [20] found that explosive spalling of very dense concrete (density = 2660
kg/m3 can occur at a very low heating rate of 1 ◦C/min. The specimens consisted of
dense concrete cylinders (100 x 200 mm) with different compressive strengths ranging
from 60 N/mm2 to 180 N/mm2. The specimens consisted of silica-fume admixture and
had a moisture content of 3.2 %. Therefore lower heating rates can cause explosive
spalling if the density of the concrete is increased.
Shirley et al. [34] did an experimental study on concrete slabs exposed to standard fire
ISO 834. The slabs were relatively small in dimension (900 x 900 x 104 mm) with 90
days old high strength concrete with 80 % relative humidity. Even though the heating
rate of the ISO 834 is relatively high, spalling did not occur in the specimens which
might be due to the relatively small specimen size. Therefore spalling does not always
occur in high strength concrete even though the heating rate and moisture content is
high.
Jumpannen [25] tested quartzite aggregates cylindrical concrete specimens (diameter:
80 mm, length: 40 mm) at elevated temperatures and found that no spalling occurred
at 2 ◦/min. A normal strength concrete (density = 2390 kg/m3, cylinder strength = 36
MPa) and three high strength concretes (density = 2537-2610 kg/m3, cylinder strength
= 115.7-132.3 MPa) were tested. When the specimens were exposed to a heating rate of
32 ◦C/min, slight spalling occurred in some cases. This was seen both for loaded and non-
loaded specimens. Jumpannen [25] also tested larger specimens of 100 x 100 x 400 mm

prism where it was found that for all specimens with high strength concrete heated under
load, explosive spalling occurred. The loading conditions and specimen size therefore
affect whether explosive spalling occurs during high heating rates. Jumpannen [25] also
tested three ultra high strength concretes (density = 2082-2757 kg/m3, compressive
strength = 190-242 MPa). The specimens were exposed to the ISO 834 curve. In all
ultra high strength specimens explosive spalling occurred at heating rates > 1 ◦C/min
after 7 minutes.
In an experimental study done by Connolly [12], three different fires with different heat-
ing rates were tested on concrete slabs.
The three fires included: BS476 fire curve,
radiant heat with flux 100 kW/m2 and a more severe direct flame. The slabs had a
thickness of 30 mm and the highest heating rate was 50 ◦C/min. For the unrestrained
slabs, no explosive spalling occurred whereas for the slabs load with 30 N/mm2 spalling
did occur in some cases. The study showed that an increase in heating rate from 80 to
140 kW/m2 of the loaded slabs resulted in and increase in the probability of spalling
from 0.25 to 0.5. This also demonstrates, that loading conditions have an influence on
the occurrence of spalling and that an increase in heating rate increases the probability
of explosive spalling.
According to Hertz [20] explosion of slow-heated specimens of dense concrete occurs,
when the temperature of the exploding layer is in the range of 350-400 ◦C. Hertz [22]
states that spalling often occurs near the critical point of steam at 374 ◦C because
beyond this point a pore cannot contain liquid and vapour at the same time, and the
pressure increases dramatically. However, it is found that fiber reinforced concrete can
delay the explosive spalling to a temperature about 450 ◦C [22].
The specimens that exploded in the experiment done by Hertz [21], had a surface tem-
perature of at least 350 ◦. The concrete cylinders with lesser strength first exploded at
a surface temperature of at least 400 ◦.
Critical concrete surface temperatures of 375-425 ◦C have been cited by Akhtaruzzaman
and Sullivan [8] at which point, if spalling do occur, the spalling will begin at this
temperature.
Jumpannen [25] has tested ultra high strength concrete with different heating rates
and found, that the concrete surface temperature always was in the range of 300 to
350 ◦C, when spalling occurred.
The difference in Akhtaruzzaman and Sullivan [8],
Jumpannen [25] and Hertz [21] results can probably be explained by the difference in
concrete strength and specimen sizes. It seems that when explosive spalling occurs for
high strength concrete, the surface temperature when the spalling starts, is lower than
for normal weight concrete.
Explosive spalling is influenced by many factors, but when it does occur, the concrete
temperature is within a consistent temperature range based on the previous studies
discusses above. In example, whether the heating rate is high or low, if explosive spalling
occurs, it seems to happen when the concrete temperature is around the critical point
of steam (374 ◦C).

2 Theoretical background
2.3.3.5
Explosive spalling criteria
Based on the previous studies presented in Section 2.3.3.4 the following criteria are
used to indicate if there is risk of explosive spalling in a concrete slab. Three criteria
has been formulated which include heating rate, moisture content and density of the
concrete. Explosive spalling is a complex phenomenon influenced by a combination of
several parameters including these criteria. Since the combination of factors leading
to explosive spalling is not fully understood yet, it must be assumed that there is a
risk of spalling, if any of the influencing factors are present. Therefore, if any of these
criteria presented in this section are met, there might be a risk of explosive spalling in
the concrete slab.
The formulation of the heating rate criteria is based on Khoury [26] where heating rates of
at least 20-30 ◦C/min are considered to increase the risk of spalling. Other studies have
shown the occurrence of spalling at smaller heating rates Akhtaruzzaman and Sullivan
[8], Hertz [20] but that may be due to very dense concrete specimens. The formulation
of the moisture content criterion is based on Hertz [22] and EN 1992-1-2 1992-1-2 [6]. It
is well known that a high moisture content increases the risk of occurrence of spalling.
The limit is chosen as 3 % moisture content by weight of concrete.
The formulation of the density criterion is diﬀicult to quantify since explosive spalling
can occur in concretes even in normal density concretes (density = 2400 kg/m3) as shown
by Jumpannen [25]. Explosive spalling occurred in a ultra high strength specimen with a
density of 2082 kg/m3. Therefore the occurrence of spalling is dependent on the concrete
strength. Both Hertz [20] and Jumpannen [25] tested high strength specimens with a
range of densities from 2537-2660 kg/m3 where explosive spalling occurred. Therefore,
for high strength concrete, the density criterion is chosen as 2500 kg/m3. In EN 1992-
1-1 [5] there is no definition of high strength concrete but most formulas change at for
concrete strengths geq C50/60. As already mentioned, concrete with high permeability,
which usually has a lower density, is more likely to spall for permeability’s above 5 ·
10−11cm2.
From the discussion above the following explosive spalling criteria is formulated:
• Heating rate: For heating rates above 20 ◦C/min, there is risk of spalling
• Moisture content: For concrete with a moisture content above 3 % by weight,
there is risk of spalling
• Density: For high strength concrete (concrete grade ≥C50/60), with a density
above 2500 kg/m3 or a permeability greater than 5 · 10−11cm2, there is risk of
spalling
The criteria will be used to analyse if there is risk of spalling of the concrete slab. If
there is a risk, then based on the studies on the absolute temperature during spalling

Finite element modelling
Finite element modelling (FEM) is a widely used numerical method which solves complex
problems in engineering designs. In a given design situation where an analytical solution
cannot be obtained, a numerical solution can be obtained with FEM. Basically, FEM
is used to solve field problems, which includes stress analysis and heat transfer of solids
and structures. In FEM, an object is discretized into an number of elements. Each
element has a number of nodes which link all the elements.
The procedure of FEM is shown in Figure 3.1. First an appropriate mathematical model
must be chosen to represent the physical problem. In order to idealize a physical problem,
certain assumptions must be made about the object [10]. These assumption are shown
in Figure 3.1.

The choice of geometry, material properties, boundary and loading conditions will influ-
ence the amount of information transferred from the physical problem to the mathemati-
cal model. An important aspect of FEM is that the solution only solves the mathematical
model and not the physical problem [10]. It is the designer’s responsibility to choose a
model which is suﬀicient in accuracy but also yields the response at least cost. When a
proper mathematical model is chosen, the model is discretized into elements. The choice
of finite elements, and mesh density are important factors in FEM since they influence
the accuracy of the solution of the model significantly. An example on adopted assump-
tions for structural elements are the Timoshenko or Euler-Bernoulli theory which is used
for beam elements.
When a solution to the model has been found, the accuracy of the model must be assessed,
since the FEM is a numerical procedure. This is done with an accuracy criteria, where
the model is assumed suﬀicient if the criteria is met. However, if the accuracy criteria is
not met, the solution parameters must be modified i.e. refined meshes. When the mesh
has been refined and a new solution found the model is assessed again until the accuracy
criteria is met. If the solution to the model is not suﬀicient even with refined solution
parameters the mathematical model must be improved.

An element is assigned to a specific family depending on the geometry type. Overall
the elements can be divided into two groups - structural elements and continuum ele-
ments. In a stress/displacement analysis both the structural and continuum elements
can be applied. In a thermal analysis only the continuum elements can be applied expect
for the shell element (structural element) which can also be applied. The structural ele-
ments include membrane, truss, beam, frame, elbow, and shell elements. The continuum
elements include solid continuum, and infinite elements.
The elements also have different dimensionality. For example, the beam and truss ele-
ments are one-dimensional, the plate, shell and membrane elements are two-dimensional
and the solid continuum elements are three-dimensional. The geometry type of the ele-
ment influences the number of nodes and degrees of freedom which is explained in the
following.
3.1.2
Degrees of freedom
Each element consists of a number of nodes which connects each element. Each node
has a number of degree of freedom assigned to it. The degrees of freedom (DOF) are fun-
damental variables which are calculated during the finite element analysis. A stress/dis-
placement analysis include translation and rotation DOF, and a heat transfer analysis
include the temperature DOF. There are three translation DOF. One in the x-direction,
one in the y-direction and one in the z-direction. The same is true for the temperature
DOF. There are also three rotation DOF. One about the x-direction, one about the
y-direction and one about the z-direction. It is important to choose the correct element
for an specific analysis, since it determines the behaviour of the elements. For a coupled
thermal-stress analysis both the translation, rotation and temperature DOF are needed.
In example the shell element can be applied in a coupled thermal-stress analysis.
3.1.3
Interpolation
The displacement (or any other DOF) at any point in an element is obtained by interpo-
lation. The interpolating is obtained from the nodal displacements. Depending on the
number of nodes in the element, the interpolation order is determined. In Figure 3.3
examples on different elements are shown. Element (a) has 8 nodes which are placed
at the corners. For elements with nodes only placed at the corners, the interpolation
is linear between the nodes in each direction. Element (b) has 20 nodes where 16 are
placed at the corners, and 4 are placed at the midside. For elements with nodes placed
at the midside the interpolation is quadratic between the nodes.

• Heating rate: From Figure 4.15 the maximum heating rate of the concrete surface

• Density: The concrete grade of the slab is C35/45, and the density is 2300 kg/m3.
The permeability of the concrete is not known. Since the concrete strength is below

It is only the heating criteria that is met, however as discussed in Section 2.3.3 the

be noted that the parameters influencing the occurrence of explosive spalling needs to be

4.6
Structural response
4.6.1
Model coupling
The modelling of concrete structures exposed to fire is typically divided into three models:
a fire model, a thermal model and a mechanical model. The transferring of physical
quantities from the CFD model to the thermal model entails some practical issues. First
the data from the CFD model must be converted into a readable format. Both the surface
temperature, heat flux and adiabatic surface temperature has to be applied in a specific
way so that the thermal model reads the data correctly. The thermal model presented
in this thesis was quite extensive to develop, since the data input had to be applied
manually. A second issue in model coupling of the CFD model and thermal model is
that the spatial and temporal discretization usually are different in the two models [32].
This issue had to be resolved in this project, since the time-temperature curves from the
CFD model was given for spatial coordinates with different spacing.
Paajanen et al. [32] has developed a coupling tool called FDS2FEM that addresses the
issue transferring data between the two models. The coupling tool transform the thermal
boundary conditions from the CFD model to an output of resulting data that can be
read by the thermal-mechanical model. Basically, FDS2FEM calculates the data input
for a FEM node as a distance-weighted average of the CFD node, automatically [32].
The analyst therefore has to input the results of the CFD model to the FDS2FEM
model which calculates the time-dependent boundary condition files. The analyst then
extract these files and has to input them to FEM model. The transferring of data is
only one-way from the CFD model to the FEM model. The mechanical response can
therefore not affect the fire dynamics. However, this is not an issue for models with
minor deformations.
If the hydrogen fire had caused a significant reduction the steel strength it would have
been relevant to analyze the mechanical response in Abaqus. In order to fully analyze the
behavior of the concrete slabs when exposed to fire, the thermal model should be coupled
with the mechanical model. In Abaqus, the temperature results from the thermal model
can be transferred to the mechanical model but a common issue the difference in spatial
discretization between the two models.
The thermal model usually requires a much
finer mesh size than the mechanical model. This results in more nodal temperature
points in the thermal model which needs to be transferred to a mechanical model with
fewer nodal temperature points. In order to do this, multiple nodal temperature point
must be averaged into a single nodal temperature point. Therefore some temperature
information may be lost in the coupling between the thermal and mechanical model.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
The aim of this project was to investigate the thermal and structural response of a
reinforced concrete slab exposed to a hydrogen fire. The FEM method was used in order
to analyse the temperature distribution of the concrete slab. A 2D thermal model of a
beam was developed in Abaqus in order to simulate the cross section of the slab. Thus,
the stresses ans strains were only considered in the plane. Explosive spalling of the
concrete slab was also investigated. The explosive spalling was modelled in the thermal
model to investigate its influence on the structural response of the slab.
At first, a thermal model was developed to analyse the 1-dimensional heat transfer in the
slab. The thermal model was exposed to the standard fire in order to verify the results
of the thermal model, since the analytical solution to the standard fire is known. The
thermal model was compared to the temperature distribution curves of a slab in EN 1992-
1-2 [6]. It was found that the thermal model could predict the concrete temperatures of
the concrete slab exposed to the standard fire.
Secondly, a local part of the slab exposed to the hydrogen fire was analysed. Only one
time-temperature curve was applied uniformly to the model. This was done in order
to investigate the maximum temperature development after the fire had burned out
and to conduct sensitivity analyses of important factors influencing the model. It was
found that the concrete temperature in the 3 cm depth an above do not exceed 200
◦C. The hydrogen fire duration is 279 s, and it was found that the temperature do
not increase significantly after the hydrogen fire is burned out. From the sensitivity
analyses it was found that the temperature response was significantly changed when the
thermal conductivity was changed. Regarding the sensitivity of the specific heat, the
temperature response was not changed significantly when the specific heat was changed.
It was also found from the local thermal model that the minimum time increment criteria
required to run the analysis in Abaqus was not met for coarser meshes. This resulted in
significantly small mesh of 0.5 mm.
Afterwards, the final thermal model of the entire concrete slab exposed to the hydrogen
fire was modelled. It was found that the application of the data from the CFD model was
extensive since every time-boundary condition curve had to be applied manually, and the
discretization of the CFD model and thermal model were different. Since the heat from
the hydrogen fire only penetrated the concrete until a depth of 5-10 cm an investigation
of the required thermal model depth was conducted. The model was minimize to a depth
of 5 cm which minimized the computational time significantly.
An investigation of the three different data inputs from the CFD model was also con-

temperature and the heat flux. The gas surface temperature (GST) was chosen for the

model were found and discussed. It was mainly the difference in discretization and the
transferring of data between the two models that were a challenge.
Finally, when the thermal model was complete, it was found from the temperature
distribution of the concrete slab, that the strength of the reinforcement steel was not
reduced by the hydrogen fire. The hydrogen fire only damaged the concrete at 0-1 cm
depth. Thus, there was no need to develop a mechanical model of the concrete slab.
The risk of explosive spalling of the concrete slab was also investigated. First an explosive
spalling criteria was formulated based on a literature review of the influencing factors
on explosive spalling. From the criteria it was found that there was a risk of explosive
spalling. The explosive spalling was modelled in the thermal model by removing a layer
of concrete defined by a temperature limit. The concrete with temperatures above 375
◦C was removed. This resulted in 1.5 cm of the surface layer being removed from the
model. The temperature distribution was analysed in the new spalling model, and it was
found that the hydrogen fire would damage the first 0-1.5 cm depth layer of concrete. It
was also found that explosive spalling did not reduce the load bearing capacity of the
slab, since the temperatures at the depth of the steel (5.5 cm depth) remained at 20 ◦C.
The load bearing capacity of the slab was investigated analytically in order to determined
whether the hydrogen fire would reduce the steel strength. It was found that the yield
strain of the steel exceeded 2.0 % which meant that the steel strength first would be
reduced at temperatures above 400 ◦C. From the temperature distribution of the spalling
model it was found that the concrete temperature only exceeded 400 ◦C in the first 0-1
cm depth layer. The load bearing capacity of the slab was therefore not reduced after
the occurrence of explosive spalling.
From the explosive spalling model it can be generalized that the cover layer of a slab
exposed to the hydrogen fire in this project should be greater than 3.5 cm, if the 0.2 %
steel strength is considered for the reinforcement. However this presumes that explosive
spalling occurs. The study of the concrete slab in this project is only limited to the
hydrogen fire, and the possible fire spread between vehicles in a tunnel has not been
considered.
