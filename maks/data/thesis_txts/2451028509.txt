ABSTRACT
Antimicrobial resistance poses major threat to the health care system world wide. Combi-
nation therapy where more than one antimicrobial agent is used to treat patients suffering
from bacterial infections. The advantages of combination therapy include broader spec-
trum of coverage, the theoretical possibility of minimizing mutation frequency and the
potential synergistic interaction between two agents. In theory the combination of two
antimicrobial agents should lower the incidence of selecting resistant mutants compared
to monotherapy. However, combination therapy has gotten renewed attention in the fight
against resistance evolution. The aim of this project was to investigate how mono- and
combination therapy effected the evolution of resistance in P. aeruginosa.
Monotherapy with ceftazidime and tobramycin and a combination of the two were used to
select P. aeruginosa mutants. The phenotypic MIC profile observed were determined using
E-test and relative gene expressions were determine using RT-qPCR.
This study demonstrated that combination therapy did not prevent nor reduce the selec-
tion of mutants. The data showed that the mutation frequency resulting in resistance
to ceftazidime and tobramycin simultaneously is similar to the mutation frequencies of
monotherapy.
The reduced susceptibility towards ceftazidime in ceftazidiem-selected
strains was attributed to up-regulation of ampC. The experiments performed during this
study were not sufficient enough to be able to identify the resistance mechanisms selected
on tobramycin or the combination of tobramycin and ceftazidime. A statistical signifi-
cance difference (P<0.05) in mexX expression was observed between tobramycin-selected
strains and the wild type. Despite not being able to identify the resistance mechanism se-
lected using combination therapy and that the susceptibilities were below the MIC break
points, it is with great importance to emphasize that the combination-selected strains did
not only show reduced susceptibility towards ceftazidime and tobramycin, but also towards
meropenem. This indicates that the selected mechanisms resulting in a broad spectrum of
resistance, compromises the resistance prevention argument for combination therapy in
treatment of P. aeruginosa.

RESUME
Antimikrobiel resistens udgør en stor trussel mod sundhedssystemet verden over. Kom-
bination terapi, med mere end et antimikrobielt præparat bruges til at behandle patien-
ter, der lider fra bakterielle infektioner. Fordelene ved kombinationsbehandling omfatter
bredere dækning, den teoretiske mulighed for at minimere mutationsfrekvensen og poten-
tiel synergistisk interaktion mellem to præparater. I teorien bør kombinationen af to an-
timikrobielle præparater nedsætte forekomsten af resistente mutanter sammenlignet med
monoterapi. Kombinationsterapi har fået fornyet opmærksomhed i kampen mod antibi-
otika resistens. Formålet med dette projekt var at undersøge, hvordan mono- og kombina-
tionsterapy påvirkede selektionen af resistens i P. aeruginosa.
Monoterapi med ceftazidime og tobramycin, og en kombination af de to var brugt til at
selektere P. aeruginosa mutanter. Den observerede fænotypiske MIC-profil blev bestemt
med brug af E-test og relative genuttryk blev bestemt ved brug af RT-qPCR.
Dette studie viste, at kombinationsterapy ikke forhindrede eller reducerede selektion af
mutanter. Resultaterne viste, at mutationsfrekvensen for mutanter selekteret ved hjælp af
monoterapy var sammenlignelig med mutationsfrekvensen for kombinationsterapy. Den
reducerede overfølsomhed over for ceftazidime i ceftazidime-selekterede stammer skyldtes
opregulering af ampC De eksperimenter, der blev udført under i dette studie var ikke
tilstrækkelig nok til at identificere de selekterede resistensmekanismer fra monoterapy med
tobramycin og kombinationsterapy. En statistisk signifikant forskel (P <0,05) af mexX ek-
spression blev observeret mellem tobramycin-selekterede mutanter og vildtypen. Selvom
dette studie ikke var i stand til at identificere resistensmekanismen for kombinations-
selekterede mutanter og at følsomheden var under MIC-break point, er det vigtigt at un-
derstrege, at de kombinations-selekterede stammer havde en nedsat følsomhed for ikke
kun ceftazidime og tobramycin, men også overfår meropenem. Dette indikerer, at de se-
lekterede mekanismer giver et bredt spektrum af resistans.

1 INTRODUCTION
Antibiotics are an invaluable resource in human health, and since the discovery of peni-
cillin by Sir Alexander Fleming in 1928 millions of live have been saved [1]. Penicillin
was first introduced to patients in the 1940s and played a key role in the treatment of in-
fectious diseases during World War II. Since then the threat of infectious diseases were
significantly lowered which was due to many novel antibiotic classes reaching the market
and subsequently class modifications have been introduced and applied to already existing
agents.
However, infectious diseases have yet again become a threat to the health care system world-
wide [1]. It has been estimated that 175,000 out of 2 million patients in the EU who catch
a hospital-acquired infections die every year [2]. On a worldwide scale approximately
700.000 people die every year because of antibiotic resistance infections and this number
is estimated to increase tremendously by 2050 to around 10 million if new and more proac-
tive solutions are not found [2, 3].
Several reasons account for this fact. First of all antibiotic resistance is an increasing con-
cern, as the bacteria started to show resistance very shortly after the introduction of a new
class of antibiotic, these classes and the time frame for resistance development is sum-
marised in figure 1.1. This led to very few antibiotics being introduced between the 1960’s
and 2000 (figure 1.1 and 1.2).
Figure 1.1 – Timeline of antibiotic deployment and discovery of antibiotic resis-
tance. Between 1962 and 2000 very few antibiotics have been introduced. Above the
timeline are the deployments of the antibiotics and beneath the timeline are the obser-
vation of antibiotic resistances [4, 5].

along side the number of companies researching antibiotics [2]. All these facts highlight

1.1 Antibiotics
An antibiotic can be defined as a ”substance that destroys or inhibits the growth of other mi-
croorganisms and is used in the treatment of external or internal infections” [8]. Antibiotics
are selective for specific microbe processes and must importantly they have a low toxicity
for human when administered at the right concentration.
Antibiotics can be classified either as a bactericidal and bacteriostatic compound. Bacte-
ricidal compounds will kill the bacteria, whereas bacteriostatic compounds merely stop
or slows down the growth of bacteria. For patients, whose immune system is intact, bac-
teriostatic compounds are very effective in treating the infection, whereas patients with a
compromised immune system obtains a greater effect by using bactericidal compounds [9].
Antibiotics are natural produced compounds. Some fungi are able to produce antibiotics
e.g. Penicillium chrysogenum, as the name indicates, produced penicillin. Antibacterials
are also compounds able to kill or inhibit the growth of bacteria. However they are syn-
thetic produced meaning there are produced by chemical synthesis.
Page 2 of 64
==PAGE:13==
Bacterial susceptibility and antimicrobial efficacy can be measured with a simple growth
test where the minimal inhibitory concentration (MIC) is determined. The MIC is the
concentration at which the bacteria fail to grow. The lower the concentration is the more
potent the antibiotic is as an inhibitor. There are different methods to measure the MIC
value including microbroth dilution method and the Epsilometer test (E-test), which is
further described in section 3.3.
To distinguish between susceptible and resistant strains MIC breakpoints are determined
by the European Committee on Antimicrobial Susceptibility testing (EUCAST) [10]. If
the MIC value is less or equal to the susceptibility breakpoint the bacteria is considered
susceptible to the antibiotic. If the MIC is greater than this value the bacteria is considered
resistant to the antibiotic. The breakpoint values for caftazidime, tobramycin, meropenem
and ciprofloxacin in P. aeruginosa can be found in table 1.1.

1.1.1 Beta-lactams
Beta-lactams are a broad spectrum antibiotic effective against both gram-negative and
gram-positive bacteria. General for this class of antibiotics, is that they all contain the
characteristic beta-lactam ring in their structure. It is a four-membered ring with three
carbon and one nitrogen atom bound to one oxygen atom as seen in figure 1.3 [12].
Figure 1.3 – The chemical structure of ceftazidime (A) and meropenem (B) [14, 15].
The structure shows the characteristic beta-lactam ring containing a four-membered
ring with three carbon and one nitrogen atom bound to one oxygen atom.
Page 4 of 64
==PAGE:15==
Beta-lactams are bactericidal and work by inhibiting the synthesis of the peptidoglycan
layer of the cell wall. This layer consist of linked sugars named N-acetylglucosamine (NAG)
and N-acetylmuramic acid (NAM) and attached to NAM is a side chain of 3-5 amino acids.
These side chains are cross-linked by PBPs in the final step called transpeptidation [16].
Because beta-lactams are analogues to the terminal amino acid, they bind exactly at the
active side of PBPs.

1.1.3 Fluoroquinolones
Fluoroquinolones are effective against both gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria.
They all contain a fluorine atom in their chemical structure as seen in figure 1.7. Fluoro-
quinolones work by interfering with DNA replication. They have two related but distinct
targets: DNA gyrase and topoisomerase IV, which are very similar in their structure.
[20, 21]. They do however have two different functions in the cell.
Figure 1.7 – The chemical structure of ciprofloxacin and belongs to the class of
flouroquinolons [22].
DNA gyrase is a tetramere, consisting of three subunits; one subunit A and two subunits B.
They are encoded by gyrA and gyrB [21]. DNA gyrase is an enzyme responsible for intro-
ducing negative super coiling into double-stranded DNA (dsDNA): a process dependent
on ATP. Negative supercoiling favors strand separation and DNA replication [23]. DNA
gyrase works by breaking and joining two DNA strands simultaneously (see figure 1.8 A)
[23] .
Topoisomerase IV consist of four subunits: two encoded by parC and two encoded by parE
genes [21]. Topoisomerase IV also works by breaking and joining DNA strands, however,
the enzyme binds to DNA crossovers rather than wrapping DNA [21]. It is involved in
chromosome segregation: the decatenation events required during the replication of cir-
cular DNA (see figure 1.8 B) [21, 24].
Ciprofloxacin is an example of a fluoroquinolone (figure 1.7). It binds to the complex of
DNA and DNA gyrase. The result of the binding of the fluoroquinolone to the target is

1.2 Evolution of antibiotic resistance
The evolution of antibiotic resistance is essential when discussing and researching
antibiotics and clinical therapies used in treating patients with bacterial infections. It is
important to understand how pressure from antibiotic use can select different types of
resistance mechanisms mechanisms displaying different susceptibility phenotypes. Also
of importance in the understanding of how these mechanisms occur as well as how they
are shared between neighbouring bacteria.
Page 8 of 64
==PAGE:19==
Antibiotic resistance can be classified as either: adaptive, intrinsic or acquired resistance
[25]. The adaptive resistance is ”a temporary increase in the ability of a bacterium to sur-
vive an antibiotic pressure due to alterations in gene and/or protein expression as a result of
an exposure to an environmental trigger […] and usually reverts upon the removal of the
inducing condition.” [25]. This type of resistance is found in Pseudomonas aeruginosa and
is e.g. seen as resistance towards aminoglycosides. The resistance is mainly mediated by
the MexXY–OprM efflux pump that is rapidly upregulated in bacteria surviving the first
exposure to aminoglycosides and is downregulated when bacteria are no longer in contact
with the drug [26].
Intrinsic resistance is when an entire bacterial species is resistant towards an antibiotic
without any additionally genetic alterations [25, 27, 28]. An example is the bacteria my-
coplasma, which is always resistant towards beta-lactams, due to lack of peptidoglycan layer
in the cell wall. Another example is P. aeruginosa, which shows a very low susceptibility
towards hydrophobic antibiotics such as macrolides. Macrolides struggle to penetrate the
outer membrane of this species [27].
The last type, acquired resistance, is when a susceptible strain becomes resistance by acquir-
ing new genetic material. Bacteria are able to share resistance genes with other bacteria
by horizontal gene transfer (HGT) such as conjugation, transduction or transformation.
Conjugation is when one bacteria shares genetic material with another bacteria directly
by cell-to-cell contact. The two cells form a tunnel like formation between the two cells.
Another way is transduction where DNA is introduced into the cell by a virus such as a
bacteriophage. This type of HGT differ form conjugation because the cells do not need
to be in contact with each other in order to transfer the DNA, due to the bacteriophage.
The last type is transformation where the cell can take up exogenous genetic material from
the environment around it. The genetic material is transferred into the cell through the
cell wall. However the cell needs to be in a competent state to be able to take up e.g DNA
from the surroundings. Bacteria can also acquire resistance through spontaneous muta-
tions during growth. Spontaneous mutations are assumed to be stochastic, meaning that
the rate of beneficial mutations occur at the same frequency as those that are neutral or
disadvantageous. When bacteria are under selective pressure, only strains with beneficial
mutations will survive, because they have an advantage over strains with neutral or unfa-
vorable mutations.
There are four main resistance mechanisms: reduced uptake, drug inactivation, modifi-
cation of drug target/altered target interactions or increased efflux. The mechanisms are
further elaborated in section 1.3.

1.3.2.1 Reduced drug uptake
In order of antibiotics to be effective against P. aeruginosa they need to be able to reach
their targets [18]. Therefore reduced permeability of the outer membrane will work as a
resistance mechanism. It will restrict the uptake of antibiotics, which otherwise could be
transported through the porin channels. The low permeability will reduce the rate, hence
lowering the antibiotic concentration at its target. In P. aeruginosa it is often observed
with mutational loss or alterations of the OprD2 porin, resulting in resistance towards car-
bapenems [33].
1.3.2.2 Increased efflux
Another resistance mechanism often found in P. aeruginosa is increased efflux of the drug.
The efflux pumps in P. aeruginosa consist of three components: an energy-dependent
transporter, located in the cytoplasmic membrane, an outer membrane porin and a linker

where mexB is the the energy-pendent transporter, mexA is the linker proteins which
couples the two membrane components together and oprM is the outer membrane porin.
When the efflux pump is in its functioning state the linker proteins pulls the transporter
close together to the outer membrane porin allowing it to pump out compounds from the
cytoplasm, the cytoplasmic membrane or the periplasm (see figure 1.11 B) [27].

1.3.2.3 Modification of drug target
Bacteria can also become resistant when mutations cause conformational changes in the
drug target. This means that the antibiotic will no longer be able to bind to the target,
however, the target maintains its vital role in the cell metabolism [18]. A very common
resistance mechanism seen in P. aeruginosa towards fluoroquinolone is mutations in
the gyrA gene, which encode the A subunit of the target enzyme DNA gyrase. Another
example is resistance towards aminoglycoside, where mutations results in structural
changes of the 30S subunit of the ribosome. Furthermore it can also be seen in alterations
of the PBPs, making P. aeruginosa resistant towards beta-lactams [18].
Page 13 of 64
==PAGE:24==
1.3.2.4 Inactivation of drugs
Resistance towards beta-lactams is often due to drug inactivation by enzymes. P. aerugi-
nosa produce an enzyme called AmpC, a beta-lactamase, which is able to cleave the beta-
lactam ring making the antibiotic inactive. In the wild type strain of P. aeruginosa AmpC
are expressed at low levels. Some beta-lactams, such as cefoxitin or imipenem, are ampC
inducers. This means that when they are present ampC is produced at high levels confer-
ring intrinsic resistance to the antibiotic [41]. The exact mechanism responsible for the
induction of the expression of AmpC in the presence of these antibiotics is still not fully
understood [41].
Mutations in different genes can also account for up-regulation of ampC expression. Genes
such as AmpG: an inner membrane protein, AmpD: a cytosolic amidase, AmpR: a tran-
scription factor that can lead to this upregulation of AmpC. They are all part of the normal
cell wall recycling process, where peptidoglycan degradation products, such as MurNac-
peptides (tri or tetra peptides) are recycled [18, 27]. Mutations in another gene called
dacB has also shown high expression of AmpC. The gene encodes the nonessential PBP4
and mutations can cause inactivation of the protein, hence up-regulating the expression of
AmpC [18, 36, 41]. Another example is seen in aminoglycoside resistant mutants, where
the strains produce enzymes that are able to transfer acetyl, phosphate or adenylyl groups
to the antibiotic, hence inactivating it [18].
1.4 Fitness cost of mutations
The fitness of a bacteria is its ability to replicate and survive in a competitive environment.
When a bacteria become resistant to e.g. antibiotics it is often accompanied by a fitness
cost. It makes the strain less fit when the antibiotic is removed and it reproduces slower
compared to the wild type often due to a lower growth rate. This means that if the bacte-
ria is not under selective pressure from antibiotics, the susceptible strain (wild type) will
eventually outgrow the resistant one (figure 1.12 A). This can take days, months or even
years - it all depends on the relative fitness difference.

1.5 Combination therapy as a strategy
As resistance towards antibiotics continues to be a problem new strategies are needed to
combat infectious diaseases. One strategy used to treat e.g. cystic fibrosis patients suffer-
ing from P. aeruginosa infections is combination therapy. With combination therapy two
or more drugs are administered together and they might affect each other. They can be
either additive (or indifference), antagonistic or synergistic. Two drugs having an additive
response means that the effect in the combination is the same as the drug effect when used
alone [31, 44]. When two drugs have an antagonistic response the combination of the drug
is less effective when comparing to using the drugs independently [31]. Two drugs can also
be synergistic which is when the combination of the two compounds exhibits a stronger
antibiotic effect than the sum of the drugs used alone [31, 44]. This effect can also result
in lowering the concentration needed to treat the bacterial infections in the patients. An
example of two antibiotics being synergistic is the combination of beta-lactams and amino-
glycosides which have shown synergy in in vitro and animals models [44]. Beta-lactams
will disturb the cell wall of the bacteria making it easier for the aminoglycoside to enter the
cell [44]. Two antibiotics effect on each other can be investigated through e.g. a checker-
board assay or by combination testing with E-test. In both assays the idea is to observed
how the two antibiotics behave when the other is present. The checkerboard assay is fur-
ther elaborated in section 3.4.

centration (FIC) index [31]. This index can be determined using MIC measurements as

Besides the synergistic effects on the drug efficacy, combination therapy does also have
the potential to slow down the evolution of resistance mechanisms by potentially lower
the mutation frequency [31]. Assuming that the probability of spontaneous mutations,
resulting in resistance to antibiotic A and B, are 1x10−6 and 1x10−7, respectively and that
mutations happen independently, then the probability of mutations resulting in resistance
to A+B are the product of the two rates: 1x10−13 [5].
Even though combination therapy has a lot of positive effect, the strategy has had some
renewed attention. Studies shows that not all combinations will lower the mutation fre-
quency when comparing mono- with combination therapy [36]. Furthermore combina-
tion therapy can select for another phenotype, which has a common resistance mecha-
nisms for both antibiotics [36]. This phenotype can have a fitness advantage, but not nec-
essarily a survival advantage over phenotypes selected by monotherapy. In addition the
new phenotype can result in resistance not only to antibiotic A and B, but also to other
antibiotics [36].

1.6 Methods and techniques exploring antibiotic resistance
1.6.1 Fluctuation test
In 1943 Luria and Delbrück showed that mutations in bacteria occur spontaneously and
are not directed by the environment e.g. antibiotic pressure [46]. The experiment (known
as the fluctuation test) was performed by inoculation of twenty over-night (ON) cultures
with an E. coli strain sensitive to the bacteriophage T1.
Figure 1.13 – Luria and Delbrück’s fluctuation test [Natacha Faxø, 2018]. The fluctu-
ation shows the difference in late mutations and early mutations, where late mutations
result in few or non colony forming units (CFU) and early mutations result in many
CFU (also called a jackpot culture) [47].
Equal volumes of the cultures was plated onto agar plates containing T1 phage. After in-
cubation, the resistance colonies were counted. Luria and Delbrück argued that if the
presence of the phage was needed to trigger resistance, then the distribution of mutant
colonies should demonstrate a Poisson distribution [28]. By exterminating the colonies
on the plates, they found that the number of colonies fluctuated (hence the name of the
test). This meant that the mutations responsible for the resistance must have been present
in the culture before the strain came in contact with the phage. The fluctuated number of
colonies was a result of when the mutation had occurred during cell growth. As seen in
figure 1.13 early mutations result in a high number of colonies and is also called a jackpot
culture. On the contrary late mutations resulted in few or no colonies. The fluctuation test
can be used to determine the mutation rate (µ) and the mutation frequency. Mutation rate
is an estimate of the probability of a mutation occurring per cell division [28]. Mutation
rate is difficult to estimates and require the number of mutational events/culture (m). In
this study focus has been on mutation frequency.
Page 18 of 64
==PAGE:29==
Mutation frequency is much easier to determine. It describes the proportion of resistant
bacteria present in a given culture [28]. Mutation frequency can simply be determined as
the number of resistant colonies divided by the total number of colonies and is the method
used in this project.
1.6.2 Epsilometer test
The E-test is a simple growth test used to determine the MIC value of a specific antibiotic
towards a bacterial strain. ON culture is added to an agar plate and the e-test strip is placed
in the middle. The antibiotic will diffuse into the plate at different concentrations and
prevent bacterial growth. The plate is incubated ON and a symmetrical inhibition ellipse
is produced and the MIC value can be read (See figure 1.14) [48].
Figure 1.14 – E-test for MIC determination. E-test strip showing code for antibiotic,
inhibition ellipse, bacterial growth, MIC (µg/mL) and predefined gradients [49].
1.6.3 Real-time quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR)
Real-time quantitative polymerase chain reaction (RT-qPCR) is a method used to monitor
the amplification of specific DNA molecules during PCR in real-time, so not just at the end
point. First cells are harvest and RNA is extracted from the cells and transcribed into cDNA
by the enzyme Reverse Transciptase. RNA is more unstable than DNA and the amount
of RNA will decrease resulting in a wrong picture of the gene-expression. The cDNA is
used as the template for the qPCR, which consist of the same three acquisition steeps as
PCR: denaturation, annealing and extension. In the step of denaturation the dsDNA is
separated by increasing the temperature to e.g. 95○C[50]. The primers bind to the DNA
in the annealing step where the temperature is lowered to e.g. 55-65○C. This temperature
can vary a lot depending on the primers and a gradient PCR can be used to determine the
optimal temperature for the specific primers. In the last step, extension, the DNA template
Page 19 of 64
==PAGE:30==
is replicated by the DNA polymerase. These three steps are repeated a fixed number of
cycles [50]. For every cycle the amount of DNA will theoretically be doubled. However
with a normal PCR it is not possible to determine the amount of the starting material. With
qPCR starting material can be determined, by the use of standard curves, and it also makes
it possible to determine if specific genes are upregulated [51, 50]. The qPCR differs from
PCR by the used of fluoresces compounds, that the qPCR machine is able to detect. There
are two types of fluorescent compounds that are often used in qPCR[50]. The first one is
called SYBR Green and it works by binding to dsDNA and only when bound to dsDNA
will it be able to fluorescent [50, 51, 52]. The second one is called Taqman and it works by
binding in the middle of amplification area. Taqman consists of a fluorescent end and an
anti fluorescent end called a quencher. When the DNA polymerase reaches the Taqman it
will separate the two ends causing it to fluorescent.
With both SYBR Green and Taqman the increasing amount of DNA will be proportional
with the fluorescent signal, which means that the PCR reaction can be followed in real time
hence the name [50, 51, 52].
When analyzing the data from RT-qPCR can be analyzed by either absolute quantification
or relative quantification. Absolute quantification makes is possible to determine the exact
number of target DNA molecules by comparison with DNA standards using a calibration
curve with known concentration. In this project relative quantification has been used. This
method is based on having a internal reference gene to determine the fold-changes in the
expression target genes.

To verify that the primers only produce one amplicon a normal PCR on a agarose gel can
be run. If the primers produce more than one amplicon it will result in more than one

The ∆∆Ct-method
The Ct can also be used to compare the gene expression of a specific gene in the wild type
with a mutant. The results are expressed as a fold-change and are called relative quantifi-
cation [50, 53]. To do this a reference gene is needed (also called a housekeeping gene)
Page 22 of 64
==PAGE:33==
to be able to normalize the Ct values [52]. This method is only used when duing relative
quantification. A way to calculate the relative gene expression is the Livak method also
known as the ∆∆Ct-method. The fold-change can be calculated by first normalizing the
Ct to the reference gene[53]:
∆Ct(Wild type) = Ct(Gene of interest) −Ct(Reference gene)
∆Ct(Mutant) = Ct(Gene of interest) −Ct(Reference gene)
(1.4)
The next step is to normalize the ∆Ct to the wild type[53]:
∆∆Ct = ∆Ct(Mutant) −∆Ct(Wild type)
(1.5)
The last step is calculating the fold change in expression [53]:
Fold change = 2−∆∆Ct
(1.6)
For this project the ∆∆Ct-method is used to do relative quantification in order to
calculate the relative gene expression of specific genes.

2 AIM OF STUDY
With antibiotic resistance threatening the heath care system world wide, strategies to com-
bat bacterial infections and lower resistance evolution is of high importance.
Combination therapy, where more than one antimicrobial agent is used, are one strategy
proposed to overcome the battle of antibiotic resistance. The advantages of combination
therapy include broader spectrum of coverage, the theoretical possibility of minimizing
mutation frequency and the potential synergistic interaction between two agents. How-
ever, combination therapy has gotten renewed attention in the fight against resistance evo-
lution. Not all combination will lower the evolution of resistance and there is a possibility
for combination therapy to select broad-spectrum resistant mechanisms.
The aim of this project is to determine if combination therapy of tobramycin and cef-
tazidime can lower the mutation frequency compared to monotherapy in P. aeruginosa,
as P. aeruginosa is one of the most prevalent bacterial strains found in patients suffering
from cystic fibrosis. Furthermore this project is going to investigate which resistance mech-
anism are developed during mono- and combination therapy.
Initially, P. aeruginosa mutants are selected using tobramycin, ceftazidime and a combina-
tion of the two. MIC determinations are carried out for the mutants using E-tests. Based on
those results more specific resistance mechanisms are proposed and investigated through
RT-qPCR. Furthermore mutation frequency for mono- and combination therapy is also
determined.

4 RESULTS
4.1 MIC determination of PA01 (wt)
Initially the MIC value for the wild type PA01 towards tobramycin, ceftazidime,
ciprofloxacin and meropenem were determined using E-test. The E-test showed a MIC
value of 0.38 µg/ml for tobramycin, 1 µg/ml for ceftazidime, 0.094 µg/ml for ciprofloxacin
and 0.38 µg/ml for meropenem.
4.2 FIC index determination by checkerboard assay
The checkerboard assay was conducted prior to this project and was used to calculate the
FIC index and to determine the effect of the combination of tobramycin and ceftazidime.
The median FIC index was calculated as 0.75. Based on the interpretation in table 1.5
the interaction between ceftazidime and tobramycin was additive. The individual MIC
values for tobramycin and ceftazidime were also determined by the checkerboard assay
and were 0.5 µg/ml and 1 µg/ml, respectively. The MIC values were consistent with the
ones determined by E-test in section 4.1.
4.3 Mutant selection
Based on the MIC values determined by E-test and FIC index calculated from the checker-
board three selection approaches were decided. For the two monotherapy selections 4x
MIC was used, hence mutants were selected on 1.5 µg/ml tobramycin and 4 µg/ml cef-
tazidime. The concentrations were chosen in order to ensure selection of resistant mutants.
It was not possible to select mutants on concentrations with 4xMIC for the combination
therapy. The concentration of 0.5 µg/ml tobramycin + 0.5µg/ml ceftazidime was therefore
chosen.
Twenty mutants were selected on 4 µg/ml CAZ, 18 mutants were selected on 1.5 µg/ml
TM and 19 mutants were selected 0.5 µg/ml CAZ + 0.5 µg/ml TM. A complete list of the
selected strains can be found in annex A.1.

5 DISCUSSION
Infectious diseases still posses a great risk to the health care systems worldwide due to an-
tibiotic resistance and since few new antibiotics reach the market. Having effective treat-
ment strategies are of high importance in order to minimize the evolution and spread of
antibiotic resistance.
Combination therapy is often chosen to treat e.g. cystic fibrosis patients suffering from
bacterial infections commonly caused by the pathogen P. aeruginosa [30]. Combination
therapy of ceftazidime and tobramycin is considered the standard treatment [58].
Based on the analysis done by Tamma et al., studies comparing monotherapy with combi-
nation therapy focus on the clinical effects and toxicities [44]. This means that the stud-
ies are not designed to study the evolution of antibiotic resistance as the main focus [44].
Determining the effect of combination therapy on the antibiotic resistance evolution com-
pared with monotherapy is important from both a evolutionary and medical perspective
[36]. Combination therapy has been suggested to potentially lower the rate of resistance
selection. Not many studies have been made on combination therapy and the data sup-
porting the benefits of combination therapy are still conflicting [36, 44].
A study done by Vestergaard et al,. investigated how combination therapy affected the
resistant evolution in P. aeruginosa compared with monotherapy [36]. The study mainly
focuses on the combination of ceftazidime and ciprofloxacin and the data showed that
the combination selected broad-spectrum resistance mechanisms possible caused by up-
regulation of the mexAB-oprM efflux pump, which had broadest spectrum of substrate
specificity.
In this study mutation frequencies of resistance and the spectrum of resistance mecha-
nisms were investigated. Mutants were selected using either ceftazidime, tobramycin or a
combination of the two.
5.1 Mutation frequencies are not lowered by combination
therapy
The mutation frequencies showed that using a combination of ceftazidime and to-
bramycin could neither prevent nor reduce the selection for resistant mutants compared
to monotherapy with either of the two antibiotics. In fact, the mutation frequencies for
tobramycin-selected mutants (6.04 ⋅10−9) and ceftazidime-selected mutants (1.24 ⋅10−8)
were similar to the mutation frequency for combination selected strains (6.75 ⋅10−9).
Page 44 of 64
==PAGE:55==
A study done by Vestergaard et al., (2016) showed similar results when using a combina-
tion of ceftazidime and ciprofloaxacin to select resistant P. aeruginosa mutants. Similarly,
this combination could not prevent or reduce the selection for resistant mutants compared
to monotherapy [36].
Even though the potential of lowering the selection of resistant mutants has been a strong
argument for using combination therapy, the data in this study does not support that
[31, 44].
5.2 Ceftazidime-selected strains showed up-regulation of
ampC
Ceftazidime-selected strains showed substantial difference in susceptibility towards cef-
tazidime with a median reduction of 12-fold (figure 4.1). As mentioned before reduced
susceptibility towards ceftazidime can be a result of ampC up-regulation [36, 41, 59]. An
up-regulation of more than a 100-fold was observed for the six ceftazidime-selected strains
investigated (figure 4.8), which was a significant difference (P<0.01) compared to the wild
type. The mutation causing up-regulation of ampC was not identified in any of the strains.
However, three of the strains showed similar up-regulation as the reconstructed strain
MV148. This could indicate mutations in dacB, which previously have been identified
by Vestergaard et al., (2016) in ceftazidime-selected strains [36].
Since the MIC values for ceftazidime-selected strains showed a large deviation between
8µg/ml and 256µg/ml it would have been interesting to investigate with a statistical anal-
ysis how the MIC values correlated with up-regulation of ampC. Of the six strains inves-
tigated for ampC up-regulation NFA19 showed the phenotype with highest reduction in
ceftazidime susceptibility (MIC of 256µg/ml). NFA10 was also the the strain which had
the highest up-regulation of ampC with more than 600-fold. This could indicate a clear cor-
relation between ceftazidime susceptibility and ampC expression. On the contrary, both
NFA14 and NFA20 had a phenotype with a MIC value towards ceftazidime of 64 µg/ml,
but they showed an up-regulation of ampC 321-fold and 138-fold, respectively. These data
might indicate that a correlation between MIC values and ampC expression is not neces-
sarily present.. The difference in ampC expression could also be due to different mutations
resulting in the up-regulation.
Page 45 of 64
==PAGE:56==
5.3 Tobramycin-selected strains showed a signiﬁcant differ-
ence in mexX up-regulation
Tobramycin-selected strains showed substantial difference in susceptibility towards to-
bramycin with a median reduction of 5-fold (figure 4.2). In addition susceptibility towards
ceftazidime was also effected for three strains as well as 4-fold reduction in meropenem
susceptibility for one strain. Mutations in the mexZ gene causing up-regulation of the
mexXY operon have been observed in aminoglycoside resistant P. aeruginosa mutants
[60, 61, 62]. The mexXY-oprM efflux pump has a much narrower substrate profile com-
pared to mexAB-oprM [35]. The mexXY-oprM efflux pump is able to pump both to-
bramycin and ciprofloxacin out of the bacteria, but no reduced susceptibility was observed
towards ciprofloxacin for the tobramycin-selected strains (figure 4.2). This could indicate
that the mutants did not up-regulate mexX. The t-test performed did show a significant dif-
ference (P<0.05) in mexX expression between the wild type and the tobramycin-selected
strains, however, the maximum difference was only 4-fold, questioning the biological sig-
nificance of the data. In addition tho this the fold-changes of tobramycin susceptibility in
figure 4.2 suggest that more than one resistance mechanism could have been selected.
It has not been possible to identify the resistance mechanism selected during monotherapy
with tobramycin. Even though the data shows a significant difference in mexX expression
it is not enough to conclude that up-regulation of the MexXY-oprM efflux pump is re-
sponsible for the phenotypic profile observed. Since tobramycin-selected strains not only
showed increase in resistance towards tobramycin it is also important to stress that using
monotherapy with only tobramycin might also select for broader spectrum of resistance.
In addition to this it is worth to mention that none of the selected strains were above the
MIC break point of 4µg/ml for tobramycin. Based on the EUCAST break points all mu-
tants are not considered resistant. However
Previous studies have reported mutations in rplY, galU and⁄or nuoG genes in laboratory
strains, reducing the susceptibility towards aminoglycosides [63]. The gene rplY encodes
for the ribosomal protein L25 [64, 65]. A suppression of this gene results in both moderate
up-regulation of the efflux system MexXY–OprM and hypersusceptibility to beta-lactam
antibiotics [66]. Since the tobramycin-selected strains did not show any hypersusceptibil-
ity toward ceftazidime, the data does not indicate mutations in rplY.
The gene galU encodes the enzyme UTP-glucose-1-phosphate uridylyltransferase [67],
which is involved in the synthesis of Lipopolysaccharides (LPS). Mutations can cause
changes in the structure for LPS affecting the binding of aminoglycosides to LPS [68]. At
last the gene nuoG encodes NADH-quinone oxidoreductase subunit G [69], which func-
tions as an electron transporter. Studies have shown that mutation in nuoG are likely to
Page 46 of 64
==PAGE:57==
impair electron transport [70] and aminoglycosides need a proton motive force to be trans-
ported across the inner membranes.
5.4 Combination therapy results in selection of resistance
mechanisms conferring broad-spectrum resistance
Even though none of the selected mutants had MIC above the breakpoint above the MIC
break point, figure 4.4 shows that the strains did not only show reduced susceptibility
towards ceftazidime and tobramycin, but also towards meropenem. A significant differ-
ence between meropenem susceptibility for tobramycin-selected strains and combination-
selected strains (P<0.001) and between ceftazidime-selected strains and combination-
selected strains (P<0.001) were found. This supports the claim that combination therapy
can select for broader spectrum of resistance compared to monotherapy.
Broad-spectrum resistance in P. aeruginosa has been associated with up-regulation of the
broad-spectrum efflux pump mexAB-oprM [36, 71]. The up-regulation of the pump can be
due to mutations in the repressor gene mexR [36]. In this study, up-regulation of mexA was
investigated and a significant difference (P<0.05) was found. However, the fold-changes
were a maximum of 3-fold. When comparing with Vestergaard et al., (2016) mutants se-
lected on a combination of ceftazidime and ciprofloxacin showed both a lower susceptibil-
ity towards meropenem and a higher expression of mexA [36]. Not all of their mutants
showed up-regulation of mexA, indicating at least one additional mechanism conferring
broad-spectrum resistance was selected, but they were not able to identify the causative
mechanism.
A study done by Masuda et al., (2002) showed that only the efflux pump mexXY-oprM
was able to pump aminoglycosides out of the bacteria [39], indicating that the resistance
mechanism selected on the combination of ceftazidime and tobramycin is not due to up-
regulation of mexA.
Based on the assays preformed in this study it was not possible to determine the resistance
mechanism responsible for the phenotypic profile observed in the combination-selected
mutants.
5.5 Clinical relevance of this study
The goal of treating any infectious disease is to remove the bacteria from the patient, pre-
venting the patient from infecting others and to prevent the spreading and evolution of
antibiotic resistance. Even though in vitro experiments can not always be directly corre-
Page 47 of 64
==PAGE:58==
lated to clinical settings, some general aspects relating to antibiotic evolution can be applied
from this study.
The ideal scenario for combination therapy would be to select two antibiotics that do not
have common resistance mechanisms. An idea could be to select two antibiotics that are
not substrates of the same efllux pump. This was investigated by Lister et al., (2006) and
the study showed that a combination of levofloaxin and imipenem was able to prevent
the selection of mutants [40]. Ceftazidime and tobramycin are not substrates of the same
efllux pump according to Masuda et al., (2002), however mutations were still selected in
this study [39]. This also means that in order for combination therapy to reduce the evo-
Page 48 of 64
==PAGE:59==
lution of resistance, the drug concentrations attainable in patients most be kept above the
concentration at which mutants can be selected. Despite the fact that the combination of
ceftazidime and tobramycin shows greater effect than using ceftazidime alone in the treat-
ment of lower respiratory tract infections [58], it cannot be assumed that broad-spectrum
resistance mechanisms are not selected in clinical settings with the same frequency. This
highlights the importance of investigating the correlation between mutation frequencies
and resistance mechanisms observed in vitro and in clinical isolates.

6 CONCLUSION
The effect of antibiotics in treating infectious deceases are constantly affected by the
pathogens’ ability to develop resistance. This means that the understanding of the an-
tibiotic resistance evolution is of great importance. As combination therapy has been a
strategy suggested to lower the evolution of resistance, the strategy has been compared
to monotherapy in this study. The aim of this study was to investigate how combination
therapy affected the mutation frequency and spectrum of resistance mechanisms selected
compared to monotherapy.
In conclusion this work demonstrated that combination therapy did not prevent nor reduce
the selection of mutants. The data showed that the mutation frequency conferring resis-
tance to ceftazidime and tobramycin simultaneously is similar to the mutation frequencies
of monotherapy.
The mutants selected on ceftazidime showed reduction in susceptibility towards cef-
tazidime and this was most likely caused by up-regulation of the expression of ampC, an
enzyme able to cleave the active ring of beta-lactams making them inactive.
The experiments performed during this study were not sufficient enough to be able to iden-
tify the resistance mechanisms selected on tobramycin or combination of tobramycin and
ceftazidime. Even though the mutants selected on tobramycin did not show phenotypes
above the MIC break point, a 5.5-fold median reduction in tobramycin susceptibility was
still observed. A statistically significant difference (P<0.05) in mexX expression was also
observed, however, whether up-regulation of the mexXY-oprM was responsible for the
reduced susceptibility is questionable as reduced susceptibility towards ciprofloxacin was
not observed.
Despite not being able to identify the resistance mechanism selected using combination
therapy and that the susceptibilities were below the MIC break points, it is very important
to emphasize that the combination-selected strains did not only show reduced suscepti-
bility towards ceftazidime and tobramycin, but also towards meropenem. This indicates
that the selected mechanisms confer a broad spectrum of resistance, compromises the re-
sistance prevention argument for combination therapy in treatment of P. aeruginosa. The
effects of combination therapy on resistance evolution needs to be further investigated in
vivo in order to be able to determine the correlation between laboratory experiments and
clinical realities.
Page 50 of 64
==PAGE:61==
Unfortunately, overcoming antibiotic evolution does not seem to be in the near future.
Especially because companies are pulling out of antibiotic research which can have fatal
consequences to the health care system if we are no longer able to treat infectious diseases.
Therefore, the importance of antibiotic resistance research cannot be stressed enough.
7 FUTURE WORK
In order to be able to identify the resistant mechanisms responsible for the phenotypic MIC
profiles observed whole genome sequencing could be applied. Identification of exact mu-
tations would make it easier do determine the resistance mechanism. To verify the that the
identified mutation was in fact responsible for the resistance observed allelic replacement
could be applied.
