Abstract
The focus on drinking water softening in Denmark has recently increased, due to con­
sumer interest in reducing the inconveniences of hard water, such as calcium carbonate
scaling. A few utility companies currently supply softened water, and since 2016, domestic
water softeners have been legal in Denmark. Contrary to centralised softening, there are
limited regulations on domestic water softening. Domestic softeners must be certified for
drinking water use and installed by an authorised plumber, but there are no requirements
for water quality control. This can result in water, that does not comply with the para­
metric values for drinking water. The aim of this thesis is to investigate the water quality
from decentralised systems for drinking water softening in Denmark. Available domestic
water softeners were identified through a desktop review, which revealed that there are
currently many models from different manufacturers on the Danish market. Though the
size, capacity, and features varied on the reviewed water softeners, they all used strong
acid cation exchange for softening. Consumer perception of water hardness and drinking
water softening was investigated through available utility user surveys, which showed that
consumers are generally bothered by the hardness of their water, and are interested in
centralised drinking water softening. The project participants were overall satisfied with
their softening units, and they were less bothered by lime scaling after installing a soft­
ener, compared to before. The water quality was investigated through fieldwork, and wa­
ter samples were collected from 14 households and three institutions with decentralised
strong acid cation exchange water softeners. The concentration of magnesium and cal­
cium in the softened water decreased compared to the non­softened water and resulted
in a hardness of 0.4­21.7◦dH in the softened water. The sodium concentration increased
in the softened water and the parametric value for drinking water was exceeded at two
locations. The metal concentrations generally increased in the softened water compared
to the non­softened water. Zinc and copper showed the largest increase, but only lead
exceeded the drinking water parametric value in two locations. The Calcium Carbonate
Precipitation Potential (CCPP) for closed systems at 10◦C indicated lime aggressive wa­
ter at 10 locations. No E. coli was found in the water samples, but coliform bacteria were
found at two locations. The coliforms likely originated from the inlet water, and there was
no indication of the softening unit supporting the growth of coliforms. The bacterial con­
centration varied up to 2,000 CFU/mL in the softened water, and below 50 CFU/mL in the
non­softened water. The softened water exceeded the drinking water parametric value of
200 CFU/mL in five locations.
Overall, the results showed that domestic ion exchange water softeners alter the water
quality. The concentration of calcium, magnesium, and sodium changed as intended, but
the softeners also increased the concentrations of metals and bacteria in the water. The
softened water exceeded the drinking water parametric values in 47% of the locations.

Introduction
Drinking water that is free from harmful contaminants and safe for consumption is es­
sential for sustaining human life. Clean drinking water is also included in the United Na­
tions Sustainability Goals, which emphasises the importance of safe drinking water for the
health and well­being of all humans (UN, 2022). In Denmark, the quality of drinking water
is ensured through the legislative framework for drinking water, which is described in sev­
eral laws and acts. This includes Drikkevandsbekendsgørelsen, (2022), which describes
the responsibilities of the water suppliers including drinking water parametric values and
test frequencies.
In Denmark, drinking water is abstracted from groundwater (DANVA, 2022). The com­
position of groundwater depends on the geology, which in Denmark varies from sandy
aquifers in the west to chalk aquifers dominating in the east. This results in naturally
occurring mineral­rich drinking water in the eastern part of Denmark (Wodschow et al.,
2018), where the drinking water is also considered hard. Water hardness is caused by
multivalent cations with dissolved calcium and magnesium as the most important ions
(Van der Bruggen et al., 2009). Due to high concentrations of calcium and magnesium,
hard water can cause lime scaling e.g., in appliances and water heaters, which can de­
crease the efficiency and service life (Brink et al., 2004). Lime scaling also increases the
need for cleaning as well as the use of soap and detergent (Abeliotis et al., 2015).
To mitigate the negative impacts of hard water, water softening can be implemented, which
includes all technologies that reduce the concentration of ions causing hardness (Jensen
& Brinck, 2014). Softening can either be implemented centrally at the waterworks or as
decentralised treatment at private households and businesses. In some European coun­
tries, such as the Netherlands, drinking water has been softened since the 1980s (Hofman
et al., 2007). Today softening is used at utilities in e.g., USA, Germany, Sweden, Belgium,
and France (Tang et al., 2021b), and decentralised softening is also widely implemented
(Tang et al., 2019b). Previously, softening was primarily used as a way of limiting the
release of copper and lead from the supply system to the water, but nowadays consumer
convenience is paramount as softening reduces lime scaling (Hofman et al., 2007).
In Denmark, water softening has gained significant interest in recent years. In 2017 the
first Danish utility added softening to their treatment (Svingkær, 2017), and other utilities
have since followed. At the waterworks, the water treatment and water quality are contin­
uously monitored by professionals (Drikkevandsbekendtgørelsen, 2022). As the quality
criteria for softened water are the same as for non­softened water, the potentially negative
impacts from the changed water composition are minimised. Central softening is currently
limited to utilities located in the eastern part of Denmark due to the water hardness there.
As an example, HOFOR, which supplies water to the capital area, has softening at three
of their waterworks and plan to implement softening at the remaining waterworks by 2029
(HOFOR, 2022). Other utilities currently consider whether to implement central softening
due to consumer interest, however, softening is still a very debated subject.
In 2016 a legislative change in Denmark made domestic water softeners legal and more
admissible (Tang et al., 2021a). This means that if private households are dissatisfied
with the water hardness supplied by the utility companies, they may choose to install a
domestic water softener to reduce the negative effects of hard water (Tang et al., 2021b).
Strong acid cation exchange is the most commonly used technology for domestic water

softeners (Van der Bruggen et al., 2009; Tang et al., 2021a). During this process, calcium
and magnesium ions are removed by cation exchange with sodium ions (Tang et al.,
2021b).
Even though domestic ion exchange softeners must be certified for drinking water use in
Denmark, there are no requirements for maintenance checks or water quality tests once
the unit is installed in the household. Contrary to centralised softening, the processes
and parametric values are not monitored, and there are no criteria for quality control.
Hence, decreased water quality due to incorrect installation or poor maintenance might
go unnoticed. Due to the lack of quality monitoring and overall regulation, domestic ion
exchange softeners may constitute a risk to consumers. These potential risks include
increased bacterial concentrations due to microbial growth in the unit, a non­compliant
sodium concentration and corrosive water, which can result in metal leaching.
Previous studies on ion exchange softeners have focused on metal corrosion and leaching
(Sorg et al., 1999), microbial growth (Flemming, 1987; Parsons, 2000), and impact on
water quality indicators (Tang et al., 2021b). However, these studies were not conducted
on already in­use installations in private households. No studies currently exist on the
overall water quality produced by domestic ion exchanger softeners. This leaves the
impacts of in­use domestic water softeners on drinking water quality unknown.
1.1
Aim and objectives
The overall aim of this thesis is to investigate the water quality from decentralised systems
for drinking water softening in Denmark.
The research questions of this project are as follows:
• What is the consumer perception of water hardness and drinking water softening?
• Which domestic softening units are available in Denmark, and how do they work?
• How is drinking water quality affected by domestic water softeners?
• To what extent does drinking water from domestic water softeners comply with the
Danish drinking water legislation?

2.1
Legislation on drinking water
2.1.1
International legislation
The regulation and legislation on drinking water vary tremendously from country to coun­
try. Even within the European Union (EU), the legislation can be very different in some
aspects. However, all EU member states need to comply with the EU Drinking Water
Directive when designing laws and regulations related to drinking water (Directive (EU)
2020/2184, 2020), which means that some quality parameters will be the same or simi­
lar. In the Drinking Water Directive, a lot of different parameters are regulated. The EU
member states can choose to adhere directly to the advised value, or they can choose
a stricter value. The member states can also regulate additional parameters not men­
tioned in the Drinking Water Directive (Directive (EU) 2020/2184, 2020). Globally, the
World Health Organisation (WHO) is often the organisation that countries turn to when
they need recommendations on how to define the regulations on drinking water.
The current EU Drinking Water Directive does not include any guideline values for cal­
cium and magnesium in drinking water. Therefore, only a few countries in the EU have
legislation targeting these compounds. Some countries have light regulations or tech­
nical recommendations on the magnesium and calcium concentrations, while others do
not mention it at all (Kozisek, 2020). WHO does not currently have any guideline values
or recommendations for magnesium and calcium concentrations as they argue a lack of
evidence or insufficient data (WHO, 2010).
The common European directive for drinking water quality, issued in 1980, had quan­
titative requirements for calcium and magnesium. The recommended guideline levels
were 100 mg/L for calcium and 30 mg/L for magnesium with a maximum admissible con­
centration of 50 mg/L for magnesium. For softened water, a minimum concentration of
60 mg/L calcium or equivalent cations for total hardness was required (Council Directive
80/778/EEC, 1980). When the revised Directive was issued in 1998, these parameters
were excluded (Council Directive 98/83/EC, 1998), and have not been re­implemented
since.
In the Drinking Water Directive, there are no specific guidelines or regulations regard­
ing drinking water softening, as long as the quality criteria are still met. However, it is
suggested that minimum concentrations of calcium and magnesium are considered when
water is softened or demineralised (Directive (EU) 2020/2184, 2020).
WHO has a threshold of 200 mg/L sodium in drinking water, based on taste (WHO, 2022),
which is in alignment with the EU Drinking Water Directive (Directive (EU) 2020/2184, 2020).
Only two countries within the EU comment on the health aspects of increased sodium in­
take due to softening. Ireland states in educational recommendations, that an increase in
sodium levels might be unsuitable for at­risk groups and infants. Luxembourg states that
only hot water should be softened as increased sodium concentration in drinking water is
not healthy (Kozisek, 2020).

2.2
Geology of aquifers
All drinking water in Denmark is abstracted from groundwater (DANVA, 2022). Due to
the spatial variations in geology, the groundwater composition varies across the coun­
try. Drinking water is abstracted from chalk or limestone aquifers in approximately half
of Denmark, but mostly in the eastern and northern parts. Water from these aquifers is
usually more alkaline with a pH above 7. The water is typically also harder. In the remain­
ing parts of Denmark, drinking water is generally abstracted from sand and gravel layers,
which results in softer water (Sørensen, 2014).

salt (Benham et al., 2013). Fluoride is also found in groundwater but is a less common
solute. The concentration of fluoride depends on aquifer geology, where sandy aquifers
usually contain lower concentrations, while chalk aquifers typically contain more, due to
mineral fluorite deposits (Arvin et al., 2018). The concentration of the four major cations,
calcium, magnesium, sodium, and potassium are mainly determined by three processes:
The carbonate system, the solubility of minerals, and cation exchange (Wodschow et al.,
2018).
2.4
Microbiology in groundwater
Microorganisms such as bacteria, viruses, and protozoa naturally occur in groundwater.
However, an aquifer is a challenging environment for microorganisms due to the low level
of nutrients, low temperatures, and lack of light. Usually, microorganisms are concen­
trated on the matrix, while the concentration is low in the water phase. However, at the
waterworks, the conditions change e.g., oxygenation and increased temperatures, and
this allows for increased microbial growth. Microorganisms naturally occurring in ground­
water do generally not cause diseases in humans, however, pathogens can enter the

2.5

in the water. These often originate from sedimentary rocks, runoff, and seepage. Calcium
and magnesium are most abundant, leaving the other ions to play a minor role. Total

2.6
Scaling and precipitation
Scale or limescale is a hard deposit usually consisting of calcium carbonate, mostly in
the form of calcite. Scaling occurs when cations in the water react with anions such as
carbonate and sulphate and hereby form a precipitate. The primary inverse solubility salts
causing scaling are the carbonates and sulphates of calcium and magnesium, but barium
salts, phosphate and silicate scaling can also be significant in some areas (MacAdam &
Parsons, 2004).

Hard water can cause scale deposition, which is affected by several factors including tem­
perature, flow velocity, pH, and alkalinity (MacAdam & Parsons, 2004). Scale deposition
can occur in the water distribution system, but it can also occur in appliances with heat
transfer, due to the lower solubility of calcium carbonate at higher temperatures (Brink
et al., 2004). Here insoluble carbonates form, reducing the efficiency of the appliances
by coating the surfaces (Van der Bruggen et al., 2009). Once attached, scaling will be
subjected to ageing, which can increase the strength of the scaling by dehydration and
recrystallization (MacAdam & Parsons, 2004).
The development of scaling is affected by reaction kinetics, supersaturation, water com­
position, adhesion, and erosion (Brink et al., 2004; MacAdam & Parsons, 2004). Scaling
will not occur without supersaturation. During the distribution of drinking water, the water
is usually saturated with calcium carbonate to prevent lime aggressive water. So when
the water is heated to a boil it will become supersaturated and hereby prone to scaling
(Brink et al., 2004). The calcium carbonate precipitation reaction kinetics are coupled
with supersaturation in the sense that, if the reaction rate is slow, scaling might be less
noticeable despite high supersaturation, whereas fast reaction rates might result in ex­
cessive scaling even though the supersaturation is moderate. Other factors influencing
the reaction kinetics are temperature and availability of calcium carbonate surfaces (Brink
et al., 2004). Available surfaces can be both a wall, e.g., of a water heater, or particles
in the water, which can function as heterogeneous nuclei. Calcium carbonate will then
precipitate on the heated wall, due to high local temperatures, but will only cause severe
scaling, if calcium carbonate adheres without erosion caused by high water flows (Brink et
al., 2004). Surface roughness also has an impact on scale formation and rate, though as

soon as scaling is already present, the effect of the surface finish diminishes (MacAdam
& Parsons, 2004). E.g., scaling is believed to be less likely in plastic pipes compared to

If consumers chemically clean their equipment due to scaling, it can decrease the lifetime
of the appliances (Brink et al., 2004). Furthermore, scale buildup can reduce the pressure

energy consumption. Even though scaling can potentially repair or clog leaks in water
pipelines and systems (Richards et al., 2018), scaling is one of the biggest nuisances for
consumers regarding the use of drinking water.
Some parameters can affect the precipitation by acting as natural inhibiting agents on
scaling, e.g., impurities such as metal ions or organic molecules. Chemical inhibitors are
also a way of minimising the scaling potential. Processes reducing the level of supersat­
uration, e.g., softening or temperature decrease, can furthermore result in reduced scale
formation (MacAdam & Parsons, 2004).

The practice in the Netherlands is to measure MCCP, as this has been found to most
accurately describe the scaling, while CCPP is used to explain the measurements. How­
ever, if no water sample and test setup are available, CCPP has proven to derive the best
scaling predictions (Brink et al., 2004). Due to substantial variation in MCCP results and
reproducibility issues (Afshar et al., 2021), CCPP is the preferred method in this thesis.
CCPP can predict the potential for scaling and dissolution in drinking water and can be
used as a corrosion indicator (Tang et al., 2020). It is defined as the theoretical amount
of calcium carbonate that can dissolve or precipitate from water to reach equilibrium
with solid phase CaCO3 (Rossum & Merrill, 1983). In reality, equilibrium might not be
achieved, due to short residence times, meaning that CCPP represents the theoretical
maximum potential, and can hence be used as a tool for estimating risks concerning
precipitation and dissolution (Tang et al., 2020). When calculating CCPP, equilibrium
is assumed with solid calcite, which is the most stable of the three forms of crystalline
calcium carbonate that exist (Tang et al., 2020). The three forms have different crystal
systems and include calcite, aragonite, and vaterite. However, at higher temperatures ev­
idence suggest that aragonite formation is favoured over calcite (Martínez Moya & Boluda
Botella, 2021). Aragonite has a higher density and mineral hardness compared to calcite
(Richards et al., 2018).
When CCPP is used as an indicator of corrosion, the system is traditionally closed as
corrosion is most relevant in pipes. Whereas when scaling is predicted, the system is
usually open and exchanges CO2 with the atmosphere, as scaling is most relevant on
open surfaces e.g., in kitchens, bathrooms, and appliances. This also ensures the most
conservative values for dissolution and scaling as CCPP will increase in open systems
compared to closed (Tang et al., 2020). This is ascribed to the calcium carbonate buffer
system, which dictates that when the water is in contact with the CO2 in the air the equi­
librium will shift and more calcium carbonate will precipitate. A recent study has shown
that CCPP calculated for open systems strongly correlates with hardness measurements,
while CCPP for closed systems does not correlate well with hardness (Afshar et al., 2021).
Such results suggest that CCPP is not necessarily better than hardness when predicting
precipitation in open systems.
The heating of the water must also be considered when predicting lime scaling, as more
scaling occurs at higher temperatures. CCPP is therefore usually calculated for 90◦C wa­
ter for scaling predictions (Table 2.4). A temperature of 90◦C is commonly used as proper
equilibrium calculations can be made, based on the validity of the solubility constant for
CaCO3 at this temperature. 90◦C is also a temperature usually met during heating. The
introduction of higher temperatures increases the CCPP, which in practice would cause
more scaling (Tang et al., 2020). The temperature is therefore an important parame­
ter to incorporate along with complexation, ionic activity, and ionic strength (Brink et al.,
2004; Tang et al., 2020). Even though open systems should be used when calculating
calcite precipitation, closed system calculations are believed to represent more accurate
predictions of scaling based on observed effects (Brink et al., 2004). Table 2.5 contains
recommendations for closed system CCPP values according to their surmised level of

2.9
Drinking water and human health
2.9.1
Sodium
Sodium affects human health and plays an important role in nutrition. It maintains the
water balance in the body, can affect muscle contraction and nerves, and contribute to
electrolytes. Too much sodium can have harmful effects such as disruption of blood or
cell chemistry and excess consumption may contribute to hypertension (Hudak, 2001).
Though no conclusions have been drawn between hypertension and the sodium concen­
tration found in drinking water, it might be a contributing factor (WHO, 2022). Even though
sodium is not regulated as a primary contaminant, a total daily intake of 500 to 1,500 mg
is recommended (Benham et al., 2013). The current recommendation from the U.S. EPA
of 30 to 60 mg/L sodium in drinking water, is purely based on taste. However, 20 mg/L is
advised for individuals on a sodium­restricted diet (Office of Water (Environmental Protec­
tion Agency U.S.), 2018). If the concentration of sodium in the water is above 100 mg/L,
drinking water can be responsible for a substantial amount of the total intake. Therefore
the concentration in water may be important to recognise, especially for infants (Kozisek,
2020).
2.9.2
Calcium
Calcium has been associated with many different health aspects e.g., kidney stones,
sialolithiasis, and osteoporosis (Vannucci et al., 2018; Schroder et al., 2015; Sulaiman
et al., 2020; Michael & Somani, 2022), with both positive and negative impacts. One of
the more pronounced is bone health as the body stores in the order of 1,200 g calcium
with around 99% in bones and teeth (WHO, 2010). Here it functions as a key structural
element and can reduce fracture risk by increasing bone mass (Vannucci et al., 2018). A
recent study found a beneficial role of high contents of calcium and bicarbonate in drinking
water against coronary artery disease (Dore et al., 2021). The taste threshold for calcium
ions in drinking water is in the range of 100 to 300 mg/L (WHO, 2010).
2.9.3
Magnesium
Magnesium is the fourth most abundant cation in the body, with a total body store in
the order of 25 g, where around 60% is found in bones (WHO, 2010). Low magnesium
levels are associated with many different health aspects, including increased vascular
reactions, decreased insulin sensitivity, type 2 diabetes mellitus, coronary heart disease,
and metabolic syndrome (WHO, 2010).
2.9.4
Combined effects of calcium and magnesium
Calcium and magnesium in drinking water also prevent the absorption of some toxic el­
ements, such as lead and cadmium, and reduce the harmful effects, either by a reaction
resulting in a non­absorbable compound or by competition for binding sites (Rosborg et
al., 2019). Both minerals are primarily sourced through food with a recommended daily
intake of about 200 to 400 mg magnesium and 1,000 mg calcium. The typical contribution
from water varies but is around 5 to 20% of the daily intake depending on e.g., dietary
habits and country of origin (WHO, 2010).

3
Background: Water softening
Water softening is the process of removing hardness ions from the water, which can re­
duce scaling and improve laundry and general washing characteristics. Scholz (2006)
advocates that softening seldom is used at municipal waterworks if the water hardness is
below 150 mg CaCO3/L, which corresponds to 8.4◦dH. Though, if softening is applied, a
reasonable target would be 80 mg CaCO3/L corresponding to 4.5◦dH. Centralised soften­
ing of drinking water is used on a large scale in the Netherlands, with scaling problems as
one of the main drivers (Brink et al., 2004), along with limiting the release of copper and
lead from the supply system (Hofman et al., 2007). Central softening is also implemented
in Denmark, Belgium, France, Germany, Sweden, and USA using various technologies
(Tang et al., 2021b).
3.1
Water softening technologies
Water softening can be implemented as centralised softening at the waterworks or as
decentralised or domestic softening at industries and households. Softening technolo­
gies with complex processes that require special facilities are only suitable for centralised
softening, while more simple and less space demanding systems can be implemented
as domestic water softeners also. The existing technologies can be divided into three
categories based on their softening mechanism: Precipitation, ion exchange, and mem­
brane separation. Physical water conditioning is not a type of softening, but it is also
included here as these methods aim to reduce scaling without reducing the water hard­
ness. All technologies described in the following can be used for centralised softening,
except for physical water conditioning devices, which are only suitable for decentralised
treatment. Strong acid cation exchange and reverse osmosis can be implemented as
both centralised and decentralised softening (Tang et al., 2019b).
3.1.1
Precipitation softening
Softening processes which remove hardness by precipitating hardness ions as solid min­
erals include lime/soda­ash softening and pellet softening (Tang et al., 2019b). During
lime/soda­ash softening, lime and soda­ash are added to the water and using precipi­
tation and flocculation, calcium and magnesium are removed from the water as sludge
(Figure 3.1) (De Zuane, 1996).

hydroxide, is added to the water (Figure 3.2). Calcium crystallises as calcium carbonate

3.1.2
Ion exchange softening
Ion exchange includes strong acid cation exchange and weak acid cation exchange. Dur­
ing these processes, cations in the water are exchanged with cations in the negatively
charged ion exchange material, called the resin. In strong acid cation exchange, water
flows through the resin, where calcium and magnesium in the water are exchanged with
sodium on the resin (Figure 3.3) (Jensen & Brinck, 2014). This exchange is possible due
to the higher retention strength of calcium and magnesium compared to sodium. The
retention strength of cations varies according to the following order (Jensen & Brinck,
2014):
Al3+ > Ca2+ > Mg2+ > K+ > Na+
When the resin is saturated with calcium and magnesium, regeneration is needed, which
is the process of recharging the resin with sodium. Regeneration occurs when the resin
is flushed with a sodium chloride brine, which produces wastewater with calcium, mag­
nesium, and chloride (Jensen & Brinck, 2014). The parameters affected by ion exchange
include calcium, magnesium, sodium, and possibly potassium (Wist et al., 2009). Accord­
ing to MacAdam & Parsons (2004), ion exchange is the most common technology used
to reduce scaling both for domestic and industrial applications.
In weak acid cation exchange the hardness ions are exchanged with hydrogen ions, and
CO2 is used for regeneration (Höll & Hagen, 2002). Carbon Dioxide Regenerated Ion
Exchange (CARIX) is a specific type of ion exchange, which combines weak acid ion ex­
change, using carbonic acid, with anion exchange, using hydrogen carbonate, in a mixed
bed column (Figure 3.4) (Feuerstein et al., 1985 cited in Jensen & Brinck, 2014 p. 515).
For regeneration, carbon dioxide is used, which is collected so that the wastewater only
contains the compounds removed from the water. However, the feasible softening depth
using CARIX is limited, as the frequency of regeneration and thus the water use, will
increase with increasing softening depth (Tang et al., 2021b). Besides affecting the con­
centration of hardness ions, CARIX can also change the concentrations of bicarbonate,
sulfate, and nitrate (Höll & Hagen, 2002).

3.1.3
Membrane separation for softening
Membrane separation covers technologies such as nanofiltration and reverse osmosis.
These technologies can affect the concentration of almost all minerals in the water (Fritz­
mann et al., 2007). During nanofiltration, water is filtered through a semi­permeable mem­
brane, which removes divalent ions but passes monovalent ions (Figure 3.5) (Miljøstyrelsen,
2022). Using this technology, almost all calcium and magnesium ions are removed, and
the final hardness of the water is controlled by mixing with bypassed water (Hofman et
al., 2007). Though it is an energy intensive process, nanofiltration is suitable for smaller
waterworks (Miljøstyrelsen, 2022).
In reverse osmosis, the water is also forced across a selectively permeable membrane,
but the membrane pore size is smaller than for nanofiltration and higher pressure is thus
required. This process removes both divalent and monovalent ions, which makes it suit­
able for desalination (Jensen, 2014). Using reverse osmosis, the water hardness can be
reduced to almost zero due to the high rejection, and the final water hardness is therefore
controlled by mixing with bypassed water (Tang et al., 2021b).

3.1.4
Physical water conditioning
Devices for physical water conditioning do not reduce the hardness of the water but re­
duce lime scaling by altering how minerals precipitate in the water (Georgiou et al., 2018).
The technologies include electronic, electrostatic, and electrolytic devices as well as mag­
nets and electromagnets. However, no studies show how these devices affect the use of
e.g., descaling agents and soap (Tang et al., 2019b). Furthermore, the effects of using
anti­scale magnetic treatment have been reported to vary widely (MacAdam & Parsons,
2004), and some researchers find the method dubious (Richards et al., 2018). Chemical
limescale inhibitors, which reduce or delay the formation of scales also exist (MacAdam
& Parsons, 2004), however, these are not used in drinking water in Denmark.
3.2
Comparison of centralised and decentralised softening
The environmental and financial advantages and disadvantages of softening are of in­
terest when considering whether to soften the water and when deciding on whether to
prioritise centralised or decentralised softening.
A life cycle assessment of the carbon footprint of softening showed, that different as­
pects of centralised and decentralised softening contribute positively and negatively to
the carbon footprint. Beneficial processes include the increased lifespan of appliances,
fewer cleaning agents, and decreased electricity use. Adverse processes include the
softening process and production of the softener. The study also found that domestic
ion exchange softeners had the largest carbon footprint, compared to centralised pellet
softening, nanofiltration, and ion exchange, which all had negative carbon footprints, and
thus a positive environmental impact (Beeftink et al., 2021).
Another study found, that decentralised softening has a water use of 2.5 to 4.5% com­
pared to 3.6% for centralised pellet softening, and 5.3% for centralised CARIX, which are
somewhat comparable water consumptions (Tang et al., 2019b). A disadvantage of de­
centralised softening units is, that water loss can affect the wastewater. Decentralised
softening with ion exchangers leads to wastewater with high concentrations of chloride,
which is discharged during regeneration (Section 3.1.2). This can cause problems with
wastewater treatment if such wastewater is discharged from a majority of households.
Implementing centralised softening instead of decentralised would minimize this risk if
another softening technology than ion exchange is used (Bakshi et al., 2021). Further­
more, in terms of cost, centralised softening systems performed generally better, and
decentralised ion exchange was more than 10 times as costly as centralised pellet or
CARIX softening (Tang et al., 2019b)
A Danish report from 2017 found that centralised drinking water softening is socioeconom­
ically feasible in a large part of Denmark. However, in the parts of Jutland where the water
hardness is below 12◦dH, it is unknown whether centralised softening is feasible. The

largest benefits of softening were seen in private households, due to the longer service
life of appliances, less energy consumption, and less use of cleaning agents (Rambøll,
2017). This indicates that the expected positive benefits of central softening depend on
whether the consumers change their habits according to the decreased water hardness.
This is also consistent with previous findings, which showed that centralised softening

Overall, based on financial and environmental advantages and disadvantages, central

compared to decentralised softening. However, domestic softening could be considered
in areas where centralised softening is not feasible and thus not implemented, or if house­
holds desire a lower water hardness than that supplied by the utilities.
3.3
Observed effects of hardness reductions
The observed effects of softening compared to the expected effects have been studied. A
Danish study from 2019, examined the effects of centralised softening in Brøndby, where
hardness was reduced from 22 to 11◦dH, and CCPP15 was reduced from 242 to 88 mg/L
CaCO3 for a closed system (Godskesen et al., 2019). The observed effects consisted of
less scaling, increased efficiency of heat exchangers, decreased salt use in private ion
exchangers, and possibly decreased consumption of detergent and most other cleaning
and descaling agents. A link between a reduction in hardness and a reduction in con­
sumer annoyances, such as scaling, was also documented. The study also found that
though scaling decreased in electric kettles and coffee makers, the energy consumption
was unchanged, which was contrary to previous assumptions. This indicates that pre­
vious reports might have slightly overestimated the energy savings caused by softening
(Section 3.2).
Another study found a difference in the scaling from centrally softened and non­softened
water. Along with a difference in colour, the precipitation from the softened water also
adhered more to heating elements compared to the precipitation from the non­softened
water (Afshar et al., 2021). This indicates that softening not only affects the amount of
scaling but can also alter the scaling type.
3.4
Human health and water softening
Magnesium and calcium are essential to human health and inadequate intake can result
in adverse health consequences (WHO, 2010) (Section 2.9). Some of the most discussed
health effects of varying mineral content in drinking water are described in the following.
3.4.1
Cardiovascular disease
The effects of water hardness on health have frequently been studied due to the link to
cardiovascular disease (Kozisek, 2014). Many studies have investigated the effect of
water hardness on cardiovascular diseases and mortality and reported an inverse rela­
tionship between them (Rapant et al., 2021). Especially for increased magnesium con­
centrations, where benefits were observed to occur at concentrations of around 10 mg/L
or more (WHO, 2010). However, the topic is still very much debated and the literature
is divided. Mixed results were found in a recent Danish study, investigating the connec­
tion between magnesium in drinking water and cardiovascular mortality. On one hand,
a correlation between low concentrations of magnesium and increased mortality due to
acute myocardial infarction was seen. On the other hand, low concentrations were also
associated with decreased cardiovascular mortality and mortality due to stroke (Theisen
et al., 2022).

3.4.2
Atopic Eczema
Other studies have suggested that exposure to hard water can be a risk factor exacerbat­
ing eczema. However, the exact association is not known. A Danish study found that a
5◦dH increase in hardness led to a 5% increased relative risk of eczema in infants. The
overall attributable risk to the population of hard water on eczema was found to be 2%
(Engebretsen et al., 2017). Another study found no direct association between hard water
and an increased risk of eczema in infants. Instead, they found evidence of an interaction
between filaggrin gene mutations and water hardness affecting eczema (Jabbar­Lopez
et al., 2020). Thomas et al. (2011) suggested an indirect explanation as increased soap
usage, due to hard water, can result in residues on clothes or skin, which can lead to con­
tact irritation and eczema. Furthermore, a chemical irritant effect from magnesium and
calcium salts also provides a possible explanation.
3.4.3
Caries
Caries is another well­documented health aspect, that is affected by drinking water. Mul­
tiple Danish studies have been conducted on the effect of e.g., fluoride and calcium con­
centrations (Bruvo et al., 2008; Kirkeskov et al., 2010; Arvin et al., 2018). Drinking water
quality influences caries, and the protective effect of fluoride and calcium against dental
caries is important (Arvin et al., 2018). There is typically much more calcium in drinking
water compared to fluoride, however, fluoride is responsible for the biggest protection
effect considering the amounts available in drinking water compared to calcium. But if
the fluoride concentration is low, around 0.1 to 0.3 mg/L, calcium can play an important
role, especially if the concentration of calcium is high, around 100 mg/L. The combined
effect matters, as water without these ions strongly promotes dental caries (Arvin et al.,
2018). Note that too high concentrations of fluoride can also have a negative impact on
human health e.g., skeletal and dental fluorosis (Naik et al., 2021). The adequate daily
intake of fluoride for adults is 3 mg and the tolerable upper intake level is 10 mg (Insti­
tute of Medicine (US) Standing Committee on the Scientific Evaluation of Dietary Refer­
ence Intakes, 1997). Other factors besides drinking water also affect caries, and in recent
decades the overall dental health care has improved in Denmark. This suggests that other
caries prevention measures are important for the population’s dental health (Rygaard &
Albrechtsen, 2012).
3.5
Previous studies
The effect of ion exchange softening on water quality has previously been studied under
somewhat controlled conditions. In the following, studies on microbial growth in ion ex­
change resins are presented, along with a study on corrosion and metal leaching due to
softened water.
3.5.1
Microbiology in domestic ion exchange softeners
Ion exchange resins used for water treatment can support microbial growth, which in­
creases the bacterial concentration in the effluent water (Parsons, 2000). Varying bac­
terial compositions have been found in the resins, including pathogenic bacteria. The
main source of bacterial contamination in ion exchangers is the influent water, but con­
tamination can also originate from the production process or the surrounding environment
(Flemming, 1987). Continuous operation of ion exchange units was found to reduce bac­
terial growth, while infrequent regeneration could lead to increased growth. It has been
suggested that microorganisms can use the ion exchange resin as a carbon source, how­
ever, others assume that the microbial growth is caused by the resin’s filtering effect,
which captures both bacteria and nutrients from the influent water (Flemming, 1987). In
one study on microbial growth, an ion exchange softener was installed in a domestic prop­
18

==PAGE:31==
erty. It was found that the softener increased the total viable count from an average of
300 CFU/mL in the influent, to 1,330 CFU/mL in the effluent water. A stagnation period
of 20 days did not significantly increase the total viable count. Furthermore, regenera­
tion was found, at one point, to decrease the bacterial concentration in the effluent water
from 1,500 to 800 CFU/mL at 22◦C. Two mechanisms were suggested for the decrease
in bacteria due to regeneration: Removal of biofilm or removal of accumulated bacteria.
The study also showed that the majority of the microbial population was environmental
and not enteric or pathogenic (Parsons, 2000).
3.5.2
Corrosion and domestic ion exchange softeners
A previous study examined the effect of ion exchange softening on the corrosion of pipes
and metal leaching into the water, using a corrosion control pipe loop system. Both treated
tap water and untreated groundwater were used as influent water to the softener. The
results showed no consistent pattern in metal leaching due to softening, when analysing
the lead and copper concentrations in the water samples. No conclusions were made on
zinc leaching due to high variability in zinc concentrations and poor reproducibility (Sorg
et al., 1999).

In the following the different methods and equipment used to obtain the results in this

guides for domestic ion exchange softeners, water sampling at households, laboratory
analysis of water samples, and calculation and validation of data.

Reviews
Two different reviews were conducted. The first was based on user surveys provided
by utility companies, highlighting consumer preferences and wishes with a focus on wa­

decentralised ion exchange models, legal requirements, operations and settings, and cur­

4.3
Water sampling
The water in the households and institutions was sampled to investigate microbial con­

pH, oxygen concentration, and temperature were also measured on­site for each sam­

Water samples were taken from a tap which supplied softened water, usually the kitchen

system. With a flush time of 5 minutes, the resin in the softener was assumed to be

using a tissue soaked in 70% v/v ethanol (Figure 4.2). The tap was then turned fully on

5.2.1
Legal requirements
Water softeners, and other building materials, in contact with drinking water, must be
certified to be legal in Denmark. There are four certificates, which comply with the regula­
tions: The Danish GDV approval, the German DVGW certificate, the Dutch KIWA Water
Mark, and the Swedish RISE approval (Sikkerhedsstyrelsen, 2019). The reviewed mod­
els from BWT, Grünbeck, and North Star have the DVGW certificate, while the models
from Watasoftana have the RISE approval. AQUA Danmark sells models with either the
DVGW certificate or the RISE approval (Table 5.5). Note that the AQUA Danmark Slim
Soft models are sold by AQUA Danmark, but share the RISE approval with the Watasof­
tana Smartflow models, where Watasoftana is stated as the holder. Thus, these models
are assumed to be very similar. The AQUA Danmark Leyco Soft models and the North
Star models are only DVGW certified for houses with 6 to 8 families or more, correspond­
ing to 20 persons or more. However, as shown in Table 5.5, the manufacturers state that
the units are suitable for single­family houses, which is not consistent with the DVGW
certification.
In Denmark, ion exchangers must be installed by an authorised plumber. For the re­
viewed models, this is stated in all installation guides, except for the manuals for the
AQUA Danmark Slim Soft models. Furthermore, a controllable check valve must also
be implemented by an authorised plumber upstream of the device, to prevent backflow
into the distribution system (Dansk Standard, 2002). This is mentioned in all installation
guides for the reviewed models, except for the manuals for the Grünbeck models. For
BWT Perla and BWT Perla Home, a controllable check valve is mentioned as an option
depending on local conditions, however, it is noted that installations must comply with cur­

from the softener and the drain (Dansk Standard, 2002). This prevents potential backflow
from the drain to the softening unit. This precaution is stated in all installation manuals,
except for the AQUA Danmark Slim Soft models and the Watasoftana models.
5.2.2
Composition of domestic ion exchange units
Though the size, capacity, and features vary, the reviewed water softeners are all based
on the same technology using strong acid cation exchange Section 3.1.2). The water soft­
eners have a tank with sodium chloride brine and one or two columns with ion exchange
resin. Systems with one resin column will supply non­softened water during regeneration,
while systems with two resin columns will switch columns when regeneration is needed,
and thus continuously supply softened water. The size of the resin does not affect the
unit’s ability to remove hardness ions from the water, but smaller resins do require more
frequent regeneration, as the capacity to exchange ions is smaller. During regenera­
tion, brine from the tank is used to flush the resin, and the wastewater, which contains
high concentrations of calcium, magnesium, and chloride, is led to the sewer. The brine
is produced by regularly adding sodium chloride tabs to the brine tank (Figure 5.1). As
the ion­exchanged water will have a hardness close to zero, the final water hardness is
determined by mixing the ion­exchanged water with bypassed water.
Figure 5.1: Example of an open brine tank with visible sodium chloride tabs.
5.2.3
Settings on softening units
When installing a water softener, two settings, determine the water hardness: The fre­
quency of regeneration and the mixing with bypassed water. The frequency of regenera­
tion depends on the inlet water hardness and the water consumption. How this is adjusted
on the softener varies on the reviewed models. The mixing with bypassed water, which
determines the final water hardness, is for all models adjusted by a mixing valve, which
controls the distribution of ion­exchanged and bypassed water. The setting of the mixing
valve is determined iteratively by measuring the hardness of the mixed water, with a kit
for determining hardness, and manually adjusting the mixing valve until the desired water
hardness is achieved. This means that if there are changes to the hardness of the water
delivered by the utility company, the settings on the softener must manually be changed
to adapt to the new hardness.
Overall, a final water hardness between 3 and 8◦dH is recommended in the manuals,
except for North Star where no recommendation on hardness is given. The achievable
softening depth also depends on the sodium concentration in the water delivered by the
32

utility company. During ion exchange, 8.2 mg sodium/L is added to the water per 1◦dH
removed. Using the sodium concentration in the inlet water, and the maximum allowed
sodium concentration of 175 mg/L in Denmark (Table 2.2), the maximum softening depth
can be calculated. This limit to the softening depth is stated in the installation guides for
all models, except for the Watasoftana models and the AQUA Danmark Soft Slim models.
Some installation guides also include a calculation example.
5.2.4
Market in Denmark
The total number of domestic ion exchange water softeners in Denmark is estimated to be
80,000 to 100,000 (Jørgensen & Lund, 2022). Based on the current interest in softened
water (Section 5.1), this number is likely to increase in the future. Consumer comfort is
the main reason for purchasing a domestic water softener, but the increased service life
of appliances is also considered (Jørgensen & Lund, 2022). This is consistent with the
findings in the HOFOR user survey (Wilkes, 2022). The current market leader of domestic
water softeners in Denmark is BWT. They estimate that 25% of the domestic BWT soften­
ers have service agreements (Jørgensen & Lund, 2022), while AQUA Danmark estimates
that 50% of their units have service agreements (Kocak, 2022).

5.5.1
Water hardness
Strong acid cation exchange softening aims to reduce the concentration of calcium and
magnesium in the water, while the sodium concentration increases as a result (Jensen
& Brinck, 2014). The concentrations of calcium, magnesium, and sodium in the samples
are shown in Figure 5.7. The concentration of cations changed as anticipated when com­
paring the water before and after softening (Figure 5.7a). Despite sampling from locations
with different water suppliers, the calcium concentrations in the non­softened water had
only small variations between 100 and 120 mg/L. The softened flush samples had larger
variations between 1 and 110 mg/L calcium. The magnesium concentration also gener­
ally decreased after softening, but the variations in concentrations were smaller, as all
samples contained 1 to 28 mg/L magnesium. So, even though the non­softened calcium
concentrations were comparable, the concentrations in the softened water had larger vari­
ations, due to varying softener settings. The settings can vary due to preferences or due
to variations in possible softening depth based on the sodium concentration in the inlet
water.
The concentrations of calcium, magnesium, and sodium found on the identity line are from
the same sample location and indicate that the water was not softened (Figure 5.7a). The
reason could be, that we took the sample during regeneration, where the softener supplies
non­softened water. As sampling did not take place in the room where the softener was
located, it was not possible for us to observe any signs of regeneration. However, it is
more likely due to the settings on the softener. When comparing the softened immediate
and flush samples, the cation concentrations vary slightly, however, there is no clear trend
in these variations (Figure 5.7b). A possible reason for the variations can be, changes
to the water composition in stagnant water, e.g., precipitation or dissolution of calcium
carbonate or other minerals.

Sodium
An apparent increase in sodium concentrations was visible in the flushed softened wa­
ter compared to the non­softened water (Figure 5.7a). The concentration in the softened
flush samples varied a lot from 49 to 177 mg/L, compared to the somewhat similar con­
centrations in the non­softened samples with concentrations from 12 to 43 mg/L. The
increase in sodium concentration in the softened water depends on the softening depth
and can be calculated based on the reduction in calcium and magnesium concentrations.
In some cases, we measured slightly more than anticipated based on the calculations, in
other cases, we measured slightly less, see Appendix F for calculations. This indicates
that other ions are also part of the ion exchange in the resin and that they too are affected
by the softening process. Potassium could be a candidate, as we saw an overall slight
decrease in the concentrations in the softened samples compared to the non­softened
samples (Figure G.1 in Appendix G). This is also consistent with the higher retention
strength of potassium compared to sodium (Section 3.1.2).
Overall, the sodium concentrations in the softened samples were found in a large range
from 47 to 190 mg/L (Figure 5.7b). When comparing the sodium in the softened immediate
and flush samples, the concentrations were very similar but with some variations. No clear
trend was seen, as concentrations both increased and decreased. Two of the softened
samples exceeded the drinking water parametric value for sodium of 175 mg/L (Table 2.1),
with concentrations of 176 and 190 mg/L. The samples were a softened flush sample
and a softened immediate sample. In both cases, the sodium concentration in the other
softened sample was below the parametric value.

were observed in the immediate samples, and in general, the concentrations of copper

5.5.4
Calcium carbonate dissolution and precipitation
The CCPP was calculated for all samples for open and closed systems at temperatures
10, 60, and 90◦C, see Table I.1 in Appendix I for all CCPP results. The CCPP calculated
for closed systems was lower than for open systems and CCPP increased with temper­
ature (Figure 5.12). For all locations, the CCPP decreased after softening, though the
decrease varied. Small variations between the CCPP for the softened immediate and
flush samples were also observed, but no overall tendencies were deducted (Figure I.1
in Appendix I).
Figure 5.12: CCPP for the non­softened flush samples and the softened flush samples (N = 8).
The identity line is represented by the dashed line.
Positive CCPP values indicate that calcium carbonate precipitates. Negative CCPP val­
ues indicate that the water is lime aggressive, which is not allowed according to Danish
legalisation (Drikkevandsbekendtgørelsen, 2022). A positive CCPP value below 60 mg/L
CaCO3 may lead to insignificant precipitation, which does not cause visible scaling, while
a value above 120 mg/L CaCO3 may lead to excessive precipitation (Table 2.5). Note
that these values are based on 90◦C closed system calculations, which have shown the
best correlation with observed scaling (Brink et al., 2004).
Figure 5.12 shows that lime aggressive water occurred in all scenarios except for CCPP60,open
and CCPP90,open. Values above 120 mg/L CaCO3 were found in two non­softened and
one softened sample in CCPP90,closed. In 16 additional samples, including the remaining
non­softened samples, the CCPP90,closed were above 60 mg/L CaCO3. To avoid both
lime aggressive water and excessive scaling, CCPP should be between 0 and 120 mg/L
CaCO3 in all scenarios. However, this only occurred in four softened samples from two
different locations. When omitting CCPP90,open, 19 samples had a value in this range.
However, only three samples were between 0 and 60 mg/L CaCO3. This indicates that
it is challenging to avoid both excessive lime scale and lime aggressive water when do­
mestically softening the water. In the two households that reported hard deposits after
softening the water (Section 5.3.2), CCPP were in all scenarios below 70 mg/L CaCO3.
Negative values were found for CCPP10,closed and CCPP60,closed. Thus, CCPP is not
able to explain the observed deposits.
In the following, only results for CCPP10,closed and CCPP90,open will be presented, as
these represent the most conservative estimates for calcium carbonate dissolution and
precipitation, respectively.

Recommendations for final hardness after domestic softening are usually expressed as
water hardness in ◦dH, which can be measured at the household by a simple kit. The
CCPP is not considered, as it requires more knowledge of the water composition as well
as calculation software. The results showed, that there is a linear correlation between
hardness and both CCPP10, closed and CCPP90, open, but the correlation is strongest for
CCPP90, open (Figure 5.13). The reason is, that in open systems at high temperatures,
the concentration of calcium and magnesium controls the precipitation, and a large frac­
tion of the calcium content will precipitate. As the hardness depends on the calcium and
magnesium concentration, CCPP and hardness are correlated. This is in agreement with
previous findings (Afshar et al., 2021). None of the non­softened samples had negative
values for CCPP10,closed, (Figure 5.13a). In 10 locations, the softened samples had neg­
ative CCPP values, meaning that the water was lime aggressive. A lower limit around
5◦dH is often suggested, when domestically softening the water, but the results show,
that out of the 20 softened samples with lime aggressive water, including both immedi­
ate and flush samples, 11 samples had a hardness above 5◦dH. This illustrates that a
hardness of 5◦dH did not prevent lime aggressive water in several households.

Even though the CCPP only estimates the concentration of calcium carbonate that can

The CCPP results showed that the softened water was lime aggressive in closed systems
in several households. Moreover, the CCPP indicates that excessive scaling in some
households can occur despite of softening the water, but also that CCPP is not necessarily
consistent with the observed scaling. Several households with a hardness of 5◦dH or
higher had negative CCPP values. This indicates that hardness is not a sufficient measure
to determine whether the softened water is lime aggressive. The results suggest that the
SI could be used to estimate whether the water is lime aggressive, as there is a strong
correlation with CCPP at 10◦C in closed systems. However, based on our results, SI
values below 0.25, instead of 0, indicate lime aggressive water.

Bacteria
There were no E. coli in any of the samples, but coliform bacteria were found at two differ­
ent households (Table E.12 in Appendix E). In one household, only the flushed softened
sample had 1 coliform bacteria per 100 mL. To verify, that this was not caused by a lab­
oratory error, we repeated the test on the sample the next day, which confirmed that the
result was not an error. As only the softened flush sample was positive for coliforms,
and not the softened immediate sample, we contemplated whether the presence of co­
liforms could be caused by contamination of the sampling flask or contamination during
sampling. However, as the CFU in both softened samples was low, this was unlikely.
Another reason could be alterations to the distribution system near the household, which
could have caused minor contamination with e.g., soil or surface water. As there was no
access to non­softened water in the household, it was not possible to further analyse the
origin of the coliforms. At the second household, all samples were positive for coliform
bacteria, including the non­softened flush sample (Figure 5.15). The concentrations in
the samples varied from 4 to 6 coliform bacteria per 100 mL. The presence of coliforms
in all samples indicated, that the contamination originated from the house inlet water and

5.5.7
Overall water quality
The parametric values for drinking water were exceeded for sodium, lead, and bacte­
ria (Table 5.10). The concentrations of chloride, nitrate, sulphate, fluoride, iron, nickel,
copper, zinc, and chromium complied with the parametric values in all samples. The ten­
dencies of increasing concentrations of sodium and decreasing concentrations of calcium
and magnesium were consistent with the technology of ion exchange softeners, as they
are developed to alter the concentration of these compounds (Tang et al., 2019b). Overall,
the metal concentrations increased in the softened water. The same was true for bacteria,
which was the parameter leading to the most and largest exceedances. Bacteria concen­
trations were exceeding the parametric value in 6 (35%) of the locations (Table 5.10). This
is despite the fact, that almost all sampled softening units had the DVGW certification, al­
lowing them to be in contact with drinking water.
Out of the 17 sampled locations, 8 (47%) had softened water, which exceeded the drinking
water parametric values in round 1 (Table 5.10). In most locations with exceeding con­
centrations, only one parameter was exceeded while the remaining analysed compounds
complied. Only two locations had exceeding concentrations of more than one parameter.
One location had exceeding concentrations of both sodium and bacteria, and the other
had exceeding concentrations of both lead and bacteria. This indicates that the exceeding
parameters are not linked, and that non­compliance of a parameter occurs individually of
the other parameters.

For the sake of the consumers’ health, it is important to comply with the drinking water
parametric values. In some samples, bacterial concentrations were significantly above
the parametric value, however, we were not able to evaluate how this potentially affects
human health. The parametric values for metals are often based on health concerns but
also include some precautions. Lead specifically can affect the nervous system, liver,
kidney, and blood circulation, especially in young children (Li et al., 2022). In excessive
amounts, sodium can disrupt blood or cell chemistry (Hudak, 2001). However, the sodium
exceedances seen in our samples were still below the EU quality criteria of 200 mg/L.
In general, the observed decrease in calcium and magnesium concentrations in the soft­
ened water might pose a health risk to consumers drinking the water. Even though no
parametric values exist for calcium or magnesium, 30 mg/L has been suggested as a min­
imum value for calcium (Kozisek, 2020). In 6 households and two institutions the calcium,
concentrations were below this level, where two of the households use non­softened wa­
ter for drinking. A decrease in the calcium concentration can have an impact on e.g.,
caries and bone health (Arvin et al., 2018; Vannucci et al., 2018). For magnesium, 10
mg/L has been suggested as a minimum value (Kozisek, 2020). In two institutions and
12 households magnesium concentrations were below this level in the softened water,
where three of the households use non­softened water for drinking. A low magnesium
concentration is associated with e.g., increased risk of some types of cardiovascular dis­
eases, metabolic syndrome, and decreased insulin sensitivity (Theisen et al., 2022; WHO,
2010). A combination of less magnesium and calcium in the drinking water, and an in­
creased concentration of lead, might result in increased absorption of lead in the body,
which can have various negative health effects (Rosborg et al., 2019).
The results from sampling round 2 showed, that the water composition can vary, which
makes impacts on health difficult to estimate. Furthermore, as we only lean on literature
when evaluating the health risks, we were not able to analyse how the observed water
quality affects human health.
5.6
Recommendations for the consumers
Based on the findings and observations during the work with this thesis, the following rec­
ommendations are made to minimise potential negative effects when acquiring a domestic
strong acid cation exchange water softener.
When a domestic water softener is installed, it is important to comply with the current
drinking water legislation, as the consumer will be responsible for the water quality. The
consumer must choose a softener that is certified for drinking water use in Denmark,
Decentralised softening of drinking water in Denmark
51
==PAGE:64==
and ensure that an authorised plumber correctly installs the unit. Furthermore, to ensure
compliance with the parametric value for sodium, the maximum softening depth must
be calculated, based on the sodium concentration in the non­softened inlet water. As
the softener can negatively impact the water quality, the consumer should consider in­
stalling a tap with non­softened water for drinking and cooking. If this is not possible, we
recommend letting the water run for a while before using it for drinking and cooking, as
the concentration of bacteria and metals then decreases in the water. Previous studies
showed that regeneration decreased the bacterial concentration in the water (Flemming,
1987; Parsons, 2000), so regeneration should occur frequently. The regeneration fre­
quency is dependent on the resin volume and water consumption, hence smaller resins
need more frequent regeneration compared to larger resin volumes. Note that acquiring
a softener will not eliminate all scaling and that some scaling will occur if the water is
not lime aggressive. Setting the hardness to zero is not advised, as it can result in lime
aggressive water, which is not allowed and can lead to corrosion of boilers, heating ele­
ments, pipes, and faucets. Ideally, professionals should regularly test the water quality to
ensure compliance with the drinking water parametric values.
Based on the results in this thesis, sodium, lead, and bacteria are the most important
parameters to test. To determine if the water is lime aggressive, the aggressive carbon
dioxide should be measured, alternatively CCPP can be calculated, but this requires more
analysed parameters and calculation software. However, as testing is not a requirement
and because professional water analyses are expensive, we expect consumers to be
unlikely to test the water quality. Therefore legislative changes might be necessary to
protect the consumers.

6
Perspectives and future work
During this thesis, we learned, that when a project depends on cooperation with private
households, the logistic part including planning and communication was much more time­
consuming than anticipated.
The results of this thesis emphasise the need for further and more extensive studies on
water quality from domestic ion exchangers. For future work, it would be relevant to sam­
ple water from softening units located in different parts of Denmark with varying water
compositions. This would also increase the sample size, which would contribute to deter­
mining the cause of variations in our results.
Non­softened samples were not available in all households. Even though some owners
offered to turn off their softening unit or bypass it, in order for us to sample the inlet water,
we chose to decline these offers to avoid causing changes to their water. For future
work, samples could be taken at a neighbouring house, when non­softened water is not
available in a household. This would allow for comparisons between non­softened and
softened water at all locations.
We used a lot of time and energy acquiring different analysis results necessary to calculate
the CCPP of the samples. However, as we learned that the CCPP was mostly useful
for estimating whether the water was lime aggressive or not, we would for future work
recommend directly measuring the aggressive carbon dioxide. If the CCPP is used, it
would be relevant to make a sensitivity analysis, primarily to determine the sensitivity of
compounds with concentrations below the detection limit, and parameters measured in
the field, as these can have some uncertainties.
Throughout our work, we noticed a lack of guidelines and control of domestic ion exchange
softeners. We, therefore, recommend that control of the water quality is implemented to
ensure compliance with the parametric values for drinking water. Further studies are
needed to determine a suitable control frequency. This also raises the question of who
is responsible for testing the softened water. It can be discussed whether it is the house­
holds, manufacturers, utilities, or municipalities. Other legislative changes could include
requirements for additional certification of softening units on the market, as well as on­
going quality control and testing of in­use units. To reinforce quality control, it might be
necessary to also register the location of all softening units in use.
To increase knowledge in this field of research, we suggest that future studies focus on
what determines the water quality produced by the softeners. This should include how
bacterial growth is affected by normal use over time, regeneration, cleaning, and disinfec­
tion. For this, we suggest frequent sampling during a short period, as well as continuous
sampling over a longer period of time. Furthermore, the influence of water consumption,
salt type, pipe and faucet materials, and stagnant water should be investigated more thor­
oughly. Mapping of the bacteria found in the softened water would also be a relevant focus
for future work. Finally, non­softened samples should be prioritised for direct comparison
with the softened samples. Non­softened immediate samples could also be considered
for comparison with softened immediate samples on e.g., concentrations of metals and
bacteria.

7
Conclusion
In this thesis, consumer preferences for drinking water softening and available domestic
softeners were investigated, and we found that:
• Utility user surveys from Fors, HOFOR, Solrød Vandværk, and VandCenter Syd
showed that consumers are bothered by the water hardness, and are generally
interested in centralised softening of drinking water.
• The current number of domestic ion exchange water softeners in Denmark is esti­
mated to be 80,000­100,000. For the reviewed models from AQUA Danmark, BWT,
Grünbeck, North Star, and Watasoftana the final water hardness is determined by
mixing softened water with bypassed water. The mixing is determined by a man­
ually adjusted mixing valve. Most installation guides recommend a final hardness
between 3­8◦dH.
The water quality produced by domestic strong acid cation exchange water softeners was
examined at 14 households and three institutions, and we can conclude that:
• The participating households generally went from being very bothered by limescale
prior to installing a softener, to overall not being bothered after installation. The
satisfaction level with both the softener and the softened water varied from neutral
to very satisfied.
• The hardness decreased in the softened water compared to the non­softened water.
The hardness in the softened water varied from 0.4­21.7◦dH. Hardness below 5◦dH
was measured at seven locations.
• At two locations the sodium concentration in the softened water exceeded the para­
metric value for drinking water with concentrations of 176 and 190 mg/L.
• The concentrations of iron, copper, zinc, chromium, and lead overall increased in
the softened water compared to the non­softened water. The largest increase was
seen for copper and zinc in the immediate softened samples. At two locations the
drinking water parametric value for lead was exceeded in the softened immediate
samples with concentrations of 8.9 and 6.6 µg/L.
• The CCPP for a closed system at 10◦C showed lime aggressive water in 20 soft­
ened samples corresponding to 10 locations. In 11 of these samples, the measured
hardness was above 5◦dH.
• E. coli was not found in any sample. Coliform bacteria were found at two locations.
In both cases, the coliforms likely originated from the supplied water. Domestic water
softeners were not indicated to affect the concentration of coliforms in the softened
water.
• The concentration of bacteria varied greatly, up to 2,000 CFU/mL in the softened
water. In the non­softened water, the concentration was below 50 CFU/mL. Overall,
the bacterial concentration was elevated in the softened immediate samples, with
five locations exceeding the parametric value for drinking water. At one location, the
parametric value was also exceeded in the softened flush sample.
Overall, strong acid cation exchange water softeners do impact the water quality, and the
softened water exceeded the parametric values for drinking water in 47% of the locations.

C
Laboratory analysis procedure
Ion­Chromatography (IC)
No pretreatment of the samples was necessary before the IC analysis. The anions in­
cluded in the analysis were chloride, bromide, fluoride, nitrate, phosphate, and sulfate.
All ions had a quantification limit of 0.5 mg/L. An anion­exchange column equipped with
a guard column (IonPac AS22, 4 x 250 mm, Ionpac AS22 Guard column, 4 x 50 mm,
Thermo Scientific) was used under isocratic conditions with a 4.5 mM bicarbonate/1.4
mM carbonate eluent system. The flow was kept constant at 1.2 mL/min and the column
and detector compartment at 20◦C. A 200 µL sample loop was used, with an injection vol­
ume of up to 100 µL. To remove background conductivity from eluent and sample counter
ions, the eluent was converted to a weakly dissociated form by the suppressor (Dionex
AERS 500 for Carbonate, 4 mm, ThermoScientific), which was operated at 41 mA in auto
suppression recycle mode with a water source as the regenerant. Detection was achieved
by a conductivity detector (ICS­5000 Conductivity detector, ThermoScientific). The total
analysis time was 13 minutes per sample. Different anion calibration standards were used
during the analysis.
Inductively Coupled Plasma ­ Optical Emission Spectrometry (ICP­OES)
Immediately before analysis with the ICP­OES, the subsamples were acidified to 2%
HNO3 and 0.5% HCl. The ICP­OES used was an Avio 200, PerkinElmer with Yttrium as
internal standard and the detector operating in Radial mode. Detection was achieved at
317.933 nm for calcium, 766.490 nm for potassium, 589.592 nm for sodium, and 285.213
nm for magnesium. The instrument was equipped with a baffled, cyclonic spray chamber
and a Micromist nebulizer.
Inductively Coupled Plasma ­ Mass Spectrometry (ICP­MS)
Before analysis with the ICP­MS, the subsamples were acidified to 2% HNO3 and 0.5%
HCl. The used ICP­MS was a 7700x, Agilent Technologies. The internal standards Yttrium
and Scandium were added directly to the samples. The instrument was equipped with a
platinum tipped skimmer and sample cones, a double pass Scott spray chamber operating
at 2◦C and a Micromist nebulizer. All elements were analysed in helium collision mode
with a flow of 5 mL/min.
