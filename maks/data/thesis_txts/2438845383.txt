Abstract 
 
Mobile traffic is growing exponentially. To keep up with this demand, major technology evolutions 
on mobile communication systems occur every decade.  
This thesis starts by giving a brief overview on the radio access technologies, main architecture 
characteristics, and capabilities of the systems currently used by network providers. However, 
they will not be able to accommodate the traffic volume expect to hit as early as 2025. 
A fifth-generation system, 5G, promises to bring the necessary technological features to not only 
accommodate the increased traffic, but to introduce new disruptive services. This work goes 
through the main use cases enabled by 5G technology, their tight technical requirements, and 
how these requirements can be met by 5G’s technology. 
This work also offers considerations on backhaul dimensioning based on throughput and latency 
requirements for the deployment of small cells in an ultra-dense outdoor environment. 
It presents an analysis and discussion of results from experiments performed on a real-life 
operational LTE network. And finally, how conclusions on a future 5G backhaul network are 
derived from the experiments.

1. Introduction 
 
Digital traffic is rapidly increasing. This trend can be explained by how users’ behavior towards digital 
devices is changing and how digital services are appearing to fulfill users’ needs. Or is it the other way 
around? Digital services are shaping the way users use their digital devices? That is a good question itself, 
but out of the scope for this work. 
  
 
Figure 1. Data transfer projections to 2020. (Source: Bell Labs) 
The figure above correlates the appearance of these services (and its individual usage trend), to the overall 
exponential increase in digital traffic. All these numbers can be argued, as difference sources offer their 
own data and estimates. However, the trend is undeniable and it shows that digital traffic is increasing 
exponentially over the years. 
Mobile traffic, in particular, has seen a very similar growth rate, to the point that it will soon surpass fixed 
traffic in overall volume. This is reported by every network operator around the world.  
As a consequence mobile communication systems are constantly susceptible for upgrades. However, 
major evolutions come around every decade. It came in 1992 with the Global System for Mobile 
Communications (GMS), then in 2001 the Universal Mobile Telecommunications System (UMTS) arrived. 
The last major evolution happened in 2011 with the introduction of the Long-Term Evolution (LTE). 
Following this trend, and to deal with the exponential growth in traffic demand, the early 2020s should 
yet again introduce an evolved system, and that will be the New Radio (NR). 
NR, as a business enabler, is pretty much a reality, with business already selling related services and/or 
promises. But truth is that it is still in research and standardization phase. So how exactly those numbers 
and trends will become a reality is still a magic trick being rehearsed by engineers.

2. Background 
 
Most communications now a days are made wireless, going away from fixed wired connections. Wireless 
connections enable relative mobility and flexibility for users, while also improve the communication 
systems’ efficiency [1]. 
There are two main distinct areas in mobile communication systems: 
• 
Core Network  
Considered the brains of the system. It involves the networking and processing of all 
tasks that enable the exchange of information.  
 
• 
Access  Network  
Deals with establishing wireless channels of communication between mobile users and 
the core network. This wireless medium is known as the air interface, where signals are 
sent over radio waves, and hence the name Radio Access Network (RAN). 
 
 
 
 
Figure 2. Access network and core network. (Source: [1]) 
Over the years, efforts were spent on improving end to end communications by dealing with these two 
main parts individually. The earlier systems focused on improving architecture and networking protocols, 
as well as radio access for users. More recent developments have shown the most developments on the 
users’ radio access and resource allocation. 
There are many considerations to make when deploying a mobile communication network. However, 
these are the elementary requirements: 
• 
Coverage   
Geographical region where users can be served by the network. If a user wants to 
==PAGE:15==
4 | P a g e  
 
communicate in a certain area, it must be within a range that allows it to send and 
receive radio signals. Characteristic that enables users to be mobile. 
 
• 
Capacity    
The maximum amount of data that can be delivered on a coverage area. Capacity is 
shared among users in the same coverage area, therefore a minimum amount needs 
to be provided to guarantee that each user’s needs are fulfilled. 
 
Mobile network operators have the complex task of providing coverage and capacity over huge areas. 
That is mostly accomplished by dividing the coverage area into sectorized cells. Achievable capacities are 
related to the radio frequencies used and the number of channels allocated. A higher number of users 
translates into less available capacity per user, since more users require more channels. Neighboring cells 
using different frequency bands help avoiding interference between them, as well as improving an 
efficient use of available spectrum, which is a scarce resource for radio communication. 
 
 
Figure 3. Sectorized cells using different frequency bands. (Source: [2]) 
Other important parameters to consider are throughput and latency, as these indicate how the quality 
of a service (QoS) is delivered. 
Throughput 
Throughput is the actual amount of data sent on a user connection. In a best-case scenario, throughput 
will be equal to capacity on a link. Throughputs are influenced by several factors. For example, the number 
of users sharing a channel. The channel conditions between a user and a base station, this is known as the 
air interface. And how users access a channel, this is given by access and modulation schemes, and 
network resources allocation. 
Latency  
 
It is the time to transfer one bit through the system from one end to the other. It can be described by 
the following equation:

Backhaul Network 
 
There is also an important part of mobile systems that must not be ignored. It is the subnetwork 
responsible for connecting and transporting signals between the access and core networks. This link is 
known as the backhaul network.  
Different terms are used to call this linking network. It may be known in the telecommunications’ industry 
as the Transport or Transmission network. Using these different terms is not an issue in this thesis, but 
the academic term backhaul is the one preferred for the rest of the paper. 
Terminology is the least important of the undefinitions regarding the backhaul. It is a big grey area to 
determine where exactly the backhaul network starts and where it ends. It specially overlaps with the 
access network because of their dependency. For this thesis, the backhaul is considered to be the stretch 
from a Site to a network backbone (core) node.  
 
 
 
Figure 4. Mobile backhaul network. (Source: [3]) 
Backhaul must never be the bottleneck has it would affect the quality of service delivered to the users, 
and undermine the expensive RAN deployment. Therefore, careful planning considerations over its 
capacity requirements must be considered. For this reason, backhaul planning is heavily connected to RAN 
requirements. So, backhaul planning is made based on extensive RAN considerations. In other words, 
when there is a RAN technology development, backhaul must be re-evaluated accordingly, as it will be 
shown throughout this paper.  
 
2.1 Current mobile communication systems (2G, 3G, 4G) 
 
Network operators currently use different generations of mobile systems. Although they follow the same 
general goals to offer coverage and capacity, they differ in the technologies and methods used for access 
==PAGE:17==
6 | P a g e  
 
and core architecture. For this reason, coverage range and capacity offered by them is not the same. Also, 
the services offered by each system can be quite distinct and with different requirements, reason why 
these systems are still deployed today and overlap in a lot of geographical areas. 
 
2.1.1 GSM 
 
Introduced in 1991, the GSM, also known as the second-generation (2G) digital cellular networks, replaced 
the first generation (1G) analog cellular networks. It was designed as a circuit-switched network optimized 
for full-duplex voice telephony.  
Radio Access 
Frequency-division duplexing is used as the access scheme in GSM. For multiplexing, GSM uses a 
combination of frequency division multiple access (FDMA) and time division multiple access (TDMA), 
where the smallest resource element is one time slot out of 8, and one frequency slot of 200 kHz. This 
leads to max bit rates of 13 kbps per connection. 
Architecture 
GSM architecture allows for authenticated (SIM card) user with a mobile station (MS) to access the system 
through a base station (BTS) and use voice call services and short message services (SMS). BTS serves 
several MSs within its coverage, and is therefore the entity responsible for transmitting and receiving data 
resources to and from MSs. BCS in its turn control the different BTSs in a region serving as gateway 
between the base station subsystem (BSS) and the network switching system (NSS), where the users get 
authenticated, calls are handled. Operations support system (OSS) is responsible for monitoring network 
performance, helping for a better management.

2.1.2 UMTS 
 
Although later enabled, GSM was not designed for data services, so its performance with packets data is 
not good. So 3G aimed to improve the quality of data services by adding a dedicated packet switched 
system. 
Radio Access 
UMTS separates Downlink streams from Uplink streams by FDD or TDD access schemes. For multiplexing, 
it uses wide-code division multiple access (WCDMA) where users (UE) use the same frequencies but are 
differentiated by spreading codes. This system improves interference issues and allows now for peak bit 
rates of 384 kbps. 
 
Architecture 
In UMTS, synchronization and power control becomes more necessary. So, Universal Terrestrial Radio 
Access (UTRAN) substitutes the BCS in GSM, and manages all the base stations (Node Bs (NBs)).

2.1.3 LTE Advanced 
 
With the growth of internet based services and computing power of mobile devices, it was natural that 
these services become more available for mobile devices. What this meant from a network perspective is 
that new technical requirements were then necessary to accompany the mobile usage trend: 
 
• 
Higher Capacity  
 
Unlike its preceding technologies, Long Term Evolution Advanced (LTE-A) uses an 
exclusive packet switched system. It uses OFDMA (Orthogonal Frequency Division 
Multiple Access) for downlink and Single-Carrier FDMA (SC-FDMA) for uplink [5]. 
 
• 
Higher Speed   
Simplified core network that is scalable to peak bitrates of more than 100 Mbps. 
 
Figure 6. UMTS architecture. (Source: [4]) 
==PAGE:20==
9 | P a g e  
 
• 
Lower latency   
Moved all of its radio functionalities to a single evolved Node B (eNB) enabling latencies 
of around 25ms. 
 
• 
Better coverage  
 
Relays appear as one of the most prominent enablers for improving the mean and cell-
edge user experience [7] 
 
Some other key driving forces behind the transition from the UMTS based mobile system to the LTE-A are 
to improve the user access fairness, and improve QoS satisfaction for all users [3].  
Radio Access 
LTE-A uses two separate access methods for the Downlink (DL) and Uplink (UL). For the downlink, it uses 
Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiple Access (OFDMA) which is multiple narrow 15 kHz BW orthogonal 
subcarriers modulated by different data symbols. Each data symbol last for a relatively long duration. It 
allows for efficient scheduling of users in the downlink. 
For the uplink, SC-FDMA is preferred over OFDMA due to OFDMA’s high Peak-to-Average Power Ratio 
(PAPR). As we know, UEs are devices powered by small batteries where power savings are key, in contrast 
to Base Stations which are a lot more sensitive to power issues. So, SC-FDMA allows for a reduction on 
the PAPR and increase the efficiency of the UE power amplifier and consequently have a savings effect on 
the device’s battery life. This is done by reducing the time for each data symbol, resulting in shorter 
transmitting times per symbol.

LTE-A uses an evolved UTRAN (E-UTRAN) that consists only of eNBs capable of executing all radio 
functionalities. eNBs do not need a controller entity like the previous systems because it uses the X2 
interface that allows direct communication between eNBs. There is also the S1 interface that consists of

management and storage UE data. Service gateway (S-GW) is the gateway between the LTE system and 
the other systems. Packet data network gateway (P-GW) is connects the LTE systems to the internet. 
Home subscriber server (HSS) stores information about the users in the network. 
 
2.2 Challenges with the current systems 
 
The fast development of personal computing devices opened door for new services and ways to interact 
with the world. Entertainment, public and even emergency services are depending more and more on 
internet connections (different requirements). Therefore, technological advances on the mobile networks 
side must happen as well. It must handle the exponential increase in the number of devices, as well as, its 
consequence increase in traffic. LTE networks provided significant capacity improvements through flexible 
multiple access technologies and multi-antenna and interference mitigation techniques [2]. But this 
improvements are not sufficient to handle traffic expected for 2025, unless new techniques are made 
available. They also lack the capability to handle a huge number of devices simultaneously, providing 
extremely low latencies and extremely high reliability. Issues will also arise when different requirements 
are necessary simultaneously on the same physical space, being that current systems do not have the 
capability flexibly allocate different resources over the same infrastructure. Mobility is a main concern as 
users increase their mobility patterns.

Summary 
 
Mobile communication systems aim to offer enough coverage and capacity so that its users can be mobile 
while using services. These systems are divided in RANs, core networks, and a backhaul network that is 
the link between the two. Mobile communication systems currently in use have different technologies, 
access schemes and architectures as building blocks. Thus, deliver different performance aimed at 
different services. GSM was a circuit switched system that aimed at delivering voice and messaging 
services. UMTS uses both circuit-switched and packet-switched systems, offering better data services. LTE 
id a packet-switched system only, that aimed to improve capacity, speed, latency and coverage 
requirements. However, none of these systems will be able to handle traffic demands expected as early 
as 2025.

3. New Radio 
 
New Radio (NR), commercially known as the 5th generation (5G) of mobile communication systems, 
promises to revolutionize the world of communications. 5G is expected to considerably increase 
broadband capacity and deliver higher data rates with much lower latencies for increased power savings. 
This will be accomplished by leveraging higher frequency bands for radio access, densification of cell

3.1 Use Cases 
 
5G is already bringing a lot of excitement for a big range of industries due to its potential for creating

Broadband access everywhere  
Provide access to broadband service everywhere, including the more challenging situations in terms of

Higher user mobility 
 
Provide consistent and reliable service while moving at different speeds, whether it is walking, traveling

content or interact in social media without interruptions. Another scenario are the vehicles itself

Density

Capacity

populated are or event, such as 30.000 devices in a stadium.

3.3.2 Physical layer 
 
A big improvement in individual user throughput comes from new or improved techniques in providing 
physical access to the network. Also,  
Massive MIMO and Beamforming 
Multiple-input multiple-output (MIMO) is a technique already in use by LTE systems. It uses multiple 
number of antennas in transmitters and receivers to provide better throughput. Technically, it exploits 
large antenna arrays to spatially multiplex many terminals and thus achieving greater spectral efficiency. 
It operates at the sub-6 GHz spectrum. Massive MIMO, although made possible already in sub-6 GHz 
frequencies, its performances considerably increase due to the high spectrum availability in the mmW 
range because efficient multiplexing requires sufficiently different channels for different terminals [12]. 
Some demonstrations have shown impressive spectral efficiency values of 145 bps/Hz. 
mmWs suffer from much more attenuation because of their physical characteristics. This leads to way 
small coverage range. However, the smaller wavelength enables usage of large antenna arrays supporting 
strong beamforming gains that compensate for the higher path loss and thus allow for a deployment of 
cellular access networks in dense urban outdoor scenarios [13]. Beamforming will solve issues regarding 
path loss and possible blockage by the varying environment around a transmitter and a receiver. 
==PAGE:29==
18 | P a g e  
 
Beamforming is already used in a certain extent in LTE-A technology. It only uses it in data channels, while 
5G will require it to be used in control channels as well. Therefore, it is essential that NR RAN is planned 
in a beam-centric design [14].   
Access scheme and resource allocation 
Previous generations exploited orthogonality properties in time, code or frequency domains to avoid inter 
symbol interference. OFDMA will again be the method for modulation, preferably combined with Time 
Domain Duplex (TDD) with a flexible time slot assignment for dynamicity on traffic demands. Chip design 
technology advances also may enable the use of Full Duplex in 5G systems, which would contribute to 
spectral efficiency by allowing uplink and downlink transmission to occur at the same time and the same 
frequency band without inter symbol interference. 
Non-orthogonal multiple access (NOMA) is also being considered as a possible solution to address the 
requirements for high spectral efficiency and massive connectivity. NOMA introduces some controllable 
interferences to realize overloading at the expense of slightly increased receiver complexity [15]. 
Another access scheme explored by 5G is space division multiplexing (SDMA). Beamforming allows for 
dedicated spatial slots to be created. This way users could be further serviced using the same spectrum in 
each spatial sector. 
Resource allocation is still a research challenge as NR needs to allocate resources at a faster rate and in a 
more dynamic and flexible way. However, to achieve that’s it must reduce physical resource blocks and 
increase cooperation between neighbor cells [16]. 
 
3.3.3 Small cells 
 
It has been explained that the use of mmWs mean higher path loss, which leads to smaller coverage 
distances. Therefore, deployment of a high number of base stations with smaller radius of coverage will 
need to be planned. This type of deployment brings great advantages as well as complex challenges. A 
dedicated section on this topic can be found further on this thesis, in chapter 4.  
 
3.3.4 Architecture   
 
A goal for 5G’s architecture design will be to exploit one unique infrastructure, while running a flexible 
logical network. That way it will be able to provide the different set of parameters required by the 
different use cases simultaneously. A major change in paradigm will be the architecture moving away from 
the traditional cellular centric network to a more user-centric one. This architecture must also for easy 
management, reduction in energy consumption and costs of operation. 
 
Network virtualization and cloudification 
 
==PAGE:30==
19 | P a g e  
 
The key enabler for such architecture will be the exploitation of the virtualization trends and techniques. 
Decoupling network functions from the hardware by using the programmability offered by software 
defined networks (SDN) and pooling processing enabled by network function virtualization (NFV) are some 
of the aspects to be leveraged in the next generation network. 
Exploiting virtualization and cloud processing will have a great impact on the energy efficiency goals 
envisioned in its requirements.  
This virtualization will allow for a three-layer architecture (infrastructure resource, business enabler, 
business application). The E2E management and orchestration entity is the contact point to translate use 
cases into actual network functions and slices [9]. 
 
Network slicing 
 
5G intends to provide only the necessary traffic treatment to provide specific resources required for the 
requested use case. Doing so, it avoids unnecessary network functionality and resources to be wasted. 
This will be enabled by a flexible division of the logical network, creating what is called “network slicing”. 
Network slicing is the key enabler for an efficient use of the network by offering different use cases to run 
simultaneously on the same physical network infrastructure.

To achieve such architecture, there needs to be distributed architecture that clearly separates the control 
plane (C-plane) from the user plane (U-plane). While distribution offers a lot of flexibility, it also brings 
many complexities. Therefore, a right balance between a distributed and centralized architecture would 
be optimal.

It is widely recognized that 5G will bring a unimaginable number of benefits and possibilities in a that 
has not been seen in the transition between the previous generations. Those advantages come with 
many costs and complex considerations. 
Co-existence with other RATs 
If 5G performs that much better and enables a lot more services over current mobile technologies,

The fifth generation of mobile communication systems, NR, will open possibilities for new use cases that

4 Ultra-dense networks 
 
In rural and suburban areas, it is possible to handle increased traffic by densifying the existing networks 
with the deployment of macro sites.  
In a 5G reality, ultra-dense networks (UDNs) will result from the high number of small cells deployment in 
a region, so that user-centric networks become a reality. 
 
Figure 14. Cellular network vs user-centric network. (Source: [17]) 
4.1 Small Cells 
 
Even though many of the technologies that will serve as foundation to enable 5G are still under 
consideration, it is very much agreed that small cells will be a building block in next generation networks. 
They have already been at use in 3G and 4G networks but will evolve to fit 5G requirements. This process 
will require lot more planning considerations from network operators, as there are several aspects that 
need to be considered. 
 
“Small cell” is an umbrella term for a base station that englobes any type of cell that ranges from a few 
meters up to a few hundred meters [18]: 
 
• 
Femtocell 
Distance range varies up to a few tens of meters. They are currently used to cover homes and 
small offices. Wireless fidelity (WiFi) is an example of such cell. Can currently serve around 8 
users in residential hardware, and up to 32 in an enterprise one. 
 
• 
Picocell 
Coverage range varies up to 100 meters. Use cases for this type of small cell are large offices, 
stadiums or other outdoor public areas. Can serve between 64-128 users. 
 
• 
Microcell 
==PAGE:33==
22 | P a g e  
 
Coverage range varies up to a few hundred meters. They are used in large and densely 
populated areas. They cover coverage gaps and capacity insufficiencies left by traditional 
macro BSs. Can serve between 128-2568 users. 
 
To put those ranges into perspective, a standard base station (BS) can range up to several kilometers. 
Reason why these small cells are mainly used for indoor coverage improvements in areas where 
attenuation from standard BS greatly affect performance. They have proven to be a good solution for that 
as they require a much lower investment costs. Spectral efficiency is another aspect that is greatly 
improved by deploying smaller cells. This due to the fact that frequencies are then reused between 
adjacent cells. For these reasons they are now considered also for outdoor solutions. 
 
4.2 Densification of networks 
 
 
Densification of networks occurs when a defined area covered by a BS, requires the deployment of further 
BSs to improve performance. It is normally quantified with the number of BS per squared kilometer.  
Network densification is a technique already developed in current networks to improve performance. 3G 
networks have a deployment density of around 4-5 BS/𝑘𝑚2. In LTE-A, microcell deployment density 
reaches 8-10 BS/𝑘𝑚2 [19].  
 
4.3 5G UDNs 
 
There are different drivers for making UDNs a requirement for 5G networks. 
 
High number of devices 
 
5G calls for a higher deployment of BSs because the number of devices to be served is expected to increase 
to a million per square kilometer. Device density numbers like those result in unfeasible cell loads to be 
handled by standard base stations. So, there is the need to shrink cell sizes so that the number of devices 
served per cell also decreases. In other words, cell loads decrease as the number of users per cell 
decreases. That way, better performances are achieved in an area as the number of smaller cells increases.  
 
mmWs 
 
Another big driver for network densification in 5G is the use of mmW frequencies for wireless 
communication. As seen in the previous chapter, mmW poor propagation characteristics result in high 
attenuations over long distances. This issue is minimized with shrinking cell sizes. 
mmW’s maximum propagation distances can be estimated to 100 meters. Therefore, it is safe to assume 
that maximum radius for small cells must to be within that distance.  
 
Small cells

With that maximum distance in mind, as well as 5G performance requirements, the network density 
expected to satisfy them is estimated to be around 40-50 BS/𝑘𝑚2 [19].  
This is applied in areas where there is a high density of devices, with very high traffic demands. Some of 
these small cells are already deployed now a days to serve mostly as indoor access point (AP) solutions, 
and do not contain full BS processing capabilities.  
APs in UDNs may also be relay stations, distributed remote radio heads (RRH) or even UEs itself that can 
act as an AP [1]. 5G technology will enable similar solutions and concepts for seamless outdoor coverage.  
The high number and diversity of APs works in the favor of flexible allocation of network resources through 
network slicing.

Virtual cells 
 
UDNs will allow for a better user mobility management, where the concept of “virtual cells” is applied. In 
a virtual cell scenario, the control plane combines several APs to make up a larger virtual cell. This leads 
to a considerable reduction of handovers that otherwise would happen a lot more frequently with small 
cells. It improves system efficiency while proving a better user experience. 
 
4.3.1 C-RAN 
 
The trend in 5G is to provide a more uniform quality of service across all the network. RAN topologies will 
help accomplish that goal. There is also the goal to virtualize the network by removing processing 
capabilities from certain elements and perform their functions in a cloud. 
A centralized RAN (C-RAN) combines both objectives in a topology that is comprised in a Central Unit (CU) 
and a Distributed Unit (DU). A comparison between C-RAN and the traditional topology is illustrated

The main difference comprises in removing the processing capabilities from the small BSs and allowing

4.2 Challenges 
 
Different UDNs scenarios will carry different challenges when considering their deployment. The follow 
are the main common ones. 
 
4.2.1 Interference 
 
Intercell interference 
One obvious challenge is severe intercell interference. A centralized architecture where 
resources are carefully scheduled may be the solution for this issue. This type of architecture may 
allow for easy coordination between the interfering cells. Also, the reuse of LTE-A concept of 
Inter-Cell Interference Coordination (ICIC) should be applied for interference mitigation. 
 
==PAGE:36==
25 | P a g e  
 
Intracell interference 
Besides intercell interference, intracell interference would be an issue as well. That is because besides the 
much larger number of devices, there would be several APs in simultaneous communication. In certain 
areas, signal reflections may increase the risk for that intercell interference. 
 
4.2.2 Synchronization 
 
Tight synchronization will be necessary to orchestrate all the UDN nodes and with that accomplish 
efficient in resource allocation, virtualization and interference mitigation.  
 
4.3.3 Management 
 
The deployment of small cells in a densely manner brings great challenges on how to manage its 
complexity. 5G’s trend is to minimize management by having autonomous processes.  
Self-organizing networks (SON) is an enabler for BSs auto-configuration and optimization. Some functions 
enable by SON are self-configuration of PCIs, self-allocation of neighbor relation list, load-balance 
optimization, mobility optimization, tilt optimization, self-energy saving and failure detection and 
analysis. 
 
Mobility 
Even though virtual cells would allow for a better user mobility management, user mobility is nevertheless 
a topic for research in small cells. Coordinated Multi-Point (CoMP) already in use in LTE’s eNBs is a 
technique that can be improved and reutilized in 5G networks. 
 
4.3.4 Backhaul 
 
Finally, a huge challenge with these kinds of deployment is finding the best solution for data transmission 
from all the small BS and APs deployed. Cells will show different traffic patterns and capacity demands. 
How to do the aggregation of all varying traffic from all the smaller cells is a complex task that requires 
many considerations. For instance, the type of transmission links to be used is one of the main topics for 
research. The next chapter introduces some of the main points to consider. 
 
Summary 
 
Network densification is already introduced in current networks to improve performance. 3G networks 
densification is at 4-5 BS/𝑘𝑚2, while that number in 4G is at 8-10 BS/𝑘𝑚2.  
5G performance will require the shrinking of cell sizes to maximum radius of up to 100 meters. This will 
call for the need of ultra-dense networks that deploy around 40-50 BS/𝑘𝑚2. C-RAN exploits a central 
processing location to dynamically allocate resources to APs, while saving on power consumption. The 
high number and diversity of APs will help slicing the network for a flexible resource allocation. However, 
many challenges arise with these kind of dense deployments, such as severe interference, 
synchronization, management and finding the best backhaul method.

5 Backhaul 
 
The previous chapter showed how small cells and their ultra-dense deployment will be a building block to 
achieve 5G’s promised performances. However, this type of cell deployment poses a big challenge as to 
how to the transport of the high data volume from the RAN to the fixed network. 
5.1 Capacity considerations 
 
RAN networks are the most expensive part of a communication network. For this reason, dimensioning 
backhaul capacity appropriately is an essential task to avoid bottlenecks and minimize costs.  
5.1.1 Estimating cell traffic 
 
Cell load dictates how PRBs are allocated to active users served by a cell, this is measured in percentage 
values. In consequence, the traffic on a cell is given by how a cell is loaded. Cell load is mostly influenced 
by these: 
 
• 
User profiles 
User profiles are the behavior and tendencies of users to use network resources. It influences 
cell load because the number of UEs served by a cell vary due to this behaviors and tendencies. 
A cell will show higher percentages of load as the number of simultaneous users increase. 
 
• 
Air interface 
Air interface indicates the wireless channel conditions between the UE and the BS. As seen in 
section 2.1.3, for different channel conditions different modulation schemes are applied, 
which as a direct influence on peak and average throughputs for the cell.  
 
• 
Control Traffic 
It is considered negligible in LTE because of the huge difference in proportion by user and 
control traffic volume. Control traffic will substantially increase in 5G compared to LTE due to 
the higher number of differentiated services. So, its weighted the percentage might be 
considerable for some use cases such as ultra-high reliability services, and therefore influence 
cell loads. 
5.1.2 Cell traffic aggregation  
 
How to aggregate traffic from different cells in a UDN is an important task for backhaul dimensioning.  
A safe way to do this is by over-provisioning. In other words, after estimating the cell traffic for each cell, 
all peak volume is added and that would give the capacity required for the backhaul of that region. 
However, this is clearly not an efficient way to do it in terms of costs as peak values only occur for short 
instances when compared to the longer duration of average traffic volume. 
A better strategy would then be to exploit traffic demand variances between cells so total backhaul 
capacity is optimized. Statistical multiplexing takes in consideration the different loads experienced by 
==PAGE:38==
27 | P a g e  
 
different cells at distinct times. It allows for backhaul capacity resources to be estimated and shared in an 
efficient way. 
 
5.2 Latency considerations 
 
Ultra-low latency is one of the main requirements for a lot of the use cases coming with 5G networks. It 
will require for packets to move from the user to application servers within a few milliseconds. Therefore, 
it is essential to design a backhaul network that makes these latencies achievable. Besides the topology, 
another important point to consider are the type of transmission links as their physical characteristics, 
protocols and network equipment may have an important influence on the end-to-end latencies 
experienced.  
 
5.3 Transmission topology 
 
5.3.1 Current method 
 
Backhaul for RANs in current networks is done in a combination of wired and wireless transmissions in a 
tree physical layer topology. BSs are connected directly to the network or they are connected through a 
“hop” based chain, where the traffic on a site is aggregated to the traffic of the next one until it reaches 
the network access node. 
 
 
Figure 17. Transmission tree topology. (Source: Telia Danmark) 
 
==PAGE:39==
28 | P a g e  
 
Endsites  
BSs located the furthest from the network. These always contain RATs to serve the users. 
Hubsite   
They aggregate data from one or several different endsites in a region (cluster). It is the last hop to an 
access node (one hop connection). The number of aggregated endsites depends on the number of slots 
available on the aggregating RAN equipment. And on the backhaul link capacity connecting to the access 
node. As more BSs are aggregated, the greater the backhaul link capacity should be. Exception make is 
aggregating BSs peak at different times and statistical multiplexing is applied. In most cases contain RATs 
but not necessarily. 
 
Relay sites  
These sites are used when there is no LOS between an endsite and a hubsite, in the case backhaul is done 
by a wireless link. Or if distances greater than wireless links can sucessfully propagate. They are not used 
to aggregate, but simply to relay signals, increasing the number hops in a desired end to end connection.  
 
Access node (AN) 
It aggregates the traffic from endsites, hubsites and relay sites. As hubsites, the way traffic is aggregating 
is of importance for a successful backhaul link. 
 
5.3.2 Centralized vs Distributed 
 
Considering some aspects already seen, in particular section 4.3.1 on C-RAN, centralized backhaul would 
exploit traffic patterns efficiently. Would help and satisfying other 5G requirements such as energy savings 
by pooling processing capabilities. A centralized topology advocates for an easier implementation of 
CoMP and ICIC which are essential in UDNs. However, it would require high backhaul capabilities. 
On the other hand, a distributed architecture turns deployment of small cells easier although interference 
and mobility management would require APs to have processing capabilities, and thus being more cost 
inefficient. Self-backhauling between the small BSs would be necessary. 
It is clear then, that the idea backhaul would be a hybrid combination between the two, where APs 
processing are done in a central aggregation site, but would be able to cooperate with other APs for self-
backhauling when necessary. 
 
5.4 Transmission links 
 
The type of transmission links to be used for backhauling is critical when trying to satisfy use case 
requirements. Capacity wise, if not chosen properly, the backhaul becomes the bottleneck and thus 
undermining the operation of the whole system. Transmission rates, protocols and achievable distances 
can vary depending on the type of link. Currently, network operators use a combination of wireless and 
wired solutions, as the figure below exemplifies: 
 
5.3.1 Wired backhaul  
 
==PAGE:40==
29 | P a g e  
 
Several wired transmission link types are used for communication purposes, for example copper and  
optical fibers. Optical fibers offer large bandwidths, immunity to electrical noise and very low attenuation, 
making them a better option than copper. 
Based on capacity and latency requirements required by 5G technology Dense Wavelength Division 
Multiplexing (DWDM) is currently the best optical fiber solution. DWDM allows for several signals to be 
multiplexed into a single fiber using different wavelengths, having a dedicated channel for both DL and UL 
on that same fiber. DWDM is especially appropriate for 5G for the fact that it has large aggregation 
capabilities. This serves the need to aggregate high number of small BS deployment in UDNs. It can offer 
up to 8 Tbps (even more with certain techniques) in total capacity divided by 88 channels in a rack [21]. 
Latency requirements are also met as it sustains transmission delays of 100 µs per 20km [22]. On the other 
hand, the penetration of fiber points of presence (PoPs) in dense urban areas is a huge challenge, both in 
feasibility and financial cost. This leads to the consideration of other solutions. 
 
5.3.2 Wireless backhaul  
 
Ideally, fiber is the appropriate transmission type to carry 5G traffic, due to its large bandwidth availability 
as well as its low latency requirements. However, there are some constraints that lead to the 
consideration of wireless transmission links, such as cost and easiness of deployment in city environments.  
Wireless links are currently used for backhauling because they are a cheap and easy to deploy solution. 
That is proven by the fact that they are used in about 55% of connections [3]. Some network operators 
show an even higher percentage number reaching around 80%. The main reason is financial cost. Wireless 
transmission is normally fully owned while wired transmission is in a big part leased.  
The issue with wireless transmission links, when thinking of its use for 5G backhaul networks, is that they 
use point to point microwave radio (MWR). MWR do not have the characteristics required to efficiently 
do 5G data transmission. MWR has a maximum capacity of 1 Gbps, which is insufficient for 5G backhaul. 
 
Like in radio access where the mmW is being explored, mmWs are also being considered for point to point 
high capacity transmission. mmW spectrum would allow for 1 GHz bandwidths making it possible to 
achieve bit rates of 100 Gbps [23].  
As seen before, mmW suffer from high attenuation which makes long transmissions a challenge. Also, 
radio links are influenced by atmospheric conditions which have great influence on link budgets. 
Nevertheless, path loss is compensated by extreme beamforming, enabling mmW radio links to be a 
possible solution for short and mid distances. Combined with its much cheaper costs and easy of 
deployment, it has the potential of being the best solution for communication between small BSs in a UDN 
environment up to a PoP 
 
Considering the pros and cons from these transmission types as well as topology needs, a hybrid topology 
is ideal. In this topology, a combination of C-RAN and self-backhauling would maximize performance. 
mmW links would then be used for the fronthaul and communication between small BSs and a PoP, this 
could be a traditional macro BS. This would limit the number of hops on a chain, and would also allow for 
new links on its turn. And DWDM would be used for the backhaul from PoPs to aggregation points where 
pool processing is executed.

Summary 
 
Dimensioning the backhaul network is essential to avoid bottlenecks that undermine performances on 
the RAN. Correct dimensioning can be done by estimating traffic for one cell and using statistical 
multiplexing to aggregate cell traffic in a region in an efficient way. Choosing the right topology is an 
important consideration to maximize 5G performances. There are different transmission link types that 
carry pros and cons in a cost-performance wat. DWDM links are ideal for 5G, however hard to be 
implemented for UDN needs in city scenarios, being then used for backhauling aggregation from 
aggregation sites. Therefore, mmW radio links are positioned to be a good solution for UDN fronthaul and 
small BSs self-backhauling.

6 Experiments 
 
This chapter presents 3 experiments on an operational LTE network owned by Telia Danmark. Experiments 
were envisioned to help addressing several defined analysis goals concerning latency and throughput 
performance, mobility challenges and feasibility of different transmission link types. The overall goal of 
this chapter is using the analysis on the LTE network to make reasoned assumptions for 5G backhauling.  
Previous chapters introduced the main considerations when designing a backhaul network. Throughput 
and latency are the essential requirements in today’s 4G networks as well as future 5G networks. They 
have substantial influence on the quality of the services delivered, and how users perceive them. For this 
reason, these two parameters were chosen to be examined in a backhaul context. For simplicity reasons, 
the term “performance” maybe be used to refer to “throughput and latency” in some instances. 
There are 2 basic methods to do the examinations: 
• 
Computer  simulations  
Controlled environment scenarios can be conveniently modelled to replicate real-life 
network behavior. Advantages are that detailed analysis of a big number of parameters can 
be made without financial costs other than the purchase of appropriate software. 
Parameters can be easily changed based on what needs to be analyzed. The biggest 
challenges are having to learn how to properly work with the software tool, which can require 
a great amount of time. But more important, defining and achieving an acceptable level of 
fidelity with a real-network scenario can be a huge issue, which can possibly lead to 
inaccurate results and conclusions.  
 
• 
Experiments 
These could be done in a controlled lab environment, where only a fraction or prototype of 
a network would be represented. Or measurements could be performed on a real-life 
network. The two involve a great amount of financial expenses, time and effort to have it 
operational. Network parameters cannot be easily tweaked (if feasible at all), making it 
challenging to derive detailed conclusions on specific parameters’ influence on the results. 
However, the biggest advantage is that results are 100% accurate to real world dynamics and 
therefore exclude the need for reservations in its analysis. 
 
Development of a real-life network would be unfeasible for the scope of this thesis. Courteously, Telia 
Denmark has made it possible to access their LTE network infrastructure and software tools associated 
with it. So, based on the pros and cons given in the points above, and for availability of real-life network 
resources, experiments on a real-life network was chosen as the preferred method. At the end, the 
objective of any academic work, especially in the engineering field, is to have it translated into a real-
world application. 
Import terms to define for these experiments are: 
 
==PAGE:43==
32 | P a g e  
 
• 
Throughput (Tput) 
Number of bits sent or received per second. 
 
• 
Latency 
End-to-end latency is considered. This will be derived from the round-trip time. 
 
• 
Cell load 
Cell load is a key performance indicator (KPI) that can be measured in a network’s perspective 
as PRBs per transmission time interval (TTI). 
 
• 
Backhaul 
For the scope of these experiments, backhaul is a term defining the transmission link from a 
RAN site to an MPLS aggregation node 
 
6.1 Experimental objectives 
 
Mean user throughput is a key QoS metric in cellular data networks 
The following are the expected outcomes from the experiments: 
a) Study the correlation between throughput and latency 
 
It would be crucial to know what the tradeoffs are between throughput and latency when 
planning a backhaul. If there is a negative correlation, it would mean that planning resources for 
one parameter would impact negatively the other parameter. These constraints would not exist 
if the correlation is positive, or just nonexistent. 
 
b) Analyze the influence of different cell loads on performance for a single UE 
 
It has been showed in section 2.1.3 that PRBs have a big influence on throughput performance. 
Section 5.1.1 emphasized cell load important in backhaul dimensioning. The goal is observing how 
the availability of PRBs translates into throughput measured by the UE. 
 
c) Analyze performance for a UE based on handover frequency 
5G networks will consist of smaller cells. It is therefore expected that the number of handovers 
increases. With that in mind, a conclusion on performance based on the number of handovers 
would be the goal. 
 
d) Determine how the type of backhaul link influences performance  
 
As seen in chapter 5, radio and fiber links yield different channels for signal propagation. 
Therefore, it is of great importance to observe how throughput and latency perform depending 
on what type of link is used for the backhaul. 
==PAGE:44==
33 | P a g e  
 
 
e) Determine whether radio link is a viable solution to address performance requirements 
 
Small cells will be densely deployed in 5G networks. Radio link backhaul to a macro base station 
is a strongly considered solution. For that, viability in terms of throughput and latency 
performance needs to be attested. 
 
f) Analyze performance based on the number of transmission chain hops 
 
An important consideration when planning a backhaul the number of hops in a transmission link. 
The goal is analyzing is increasing the number of hops has a direct effect on performance.  
 
 
6.2 Experimental process 
 
After having the objectives defined, an experimental process was developed. It should be a simple uniform 
measuring process that allows for data collection and posteriori analysis. So, the idea was to perform the 
measurements from a single user equipment (UE) towards a specific mobile site in the network, like the 
figure bellow illustrates: 
 
Figure 19. Experimental set up. (Source: [1]) 
User Equipment 
The hardware had to be a movable and capable of accessing LTE networks for the flexibility of performing 
measurements under different set of scenarios. Ideally it would be a device available in the market for the 
public user, so that results reflect what they normally perceive.  
For those reasons, and courtesy of Professor Henrik Christiansen, the hardware chosen was a Samsung 
Galaxy Note 10.1 2014 edition [25].  
Furthermore, the hardware device needed to be equipped with a tool that allowed for the measurement 
of the parameters to be studied, collect the associated data and allow that data to be exported for 
analysis. That tool had to be LTE accessible, energy efficient and easy to use solution. Therefore, the 
QualiPoc application software [26] was installed as the measurement tool. 
==PAGE:45==
34 | P a g e  
 
 
The process consisted in the following 5 steps: 
 
1st step – UE Preparation 
Before a measurement began, the UE prepared a “test” in order to measure only the relevant parameters 
for the experiments. UE was forced to connect to LTE networks only. LTE bands were also forced to a 
specific value depending on the site to be measured. More info about the LTE frequency band is given 
later in this section. 
 
2nd step – Choosing the parameters to be measured 
The software tool used has many features and parameters that were not relevant towards the defined 
goals. For this reason, only the necessary parameters were enabled. The choice of parameters and its 
specifications were made based on the basic requirements for delivery of common services sought by 
users, according to chapter 1 of this thesis. These were the chosen parameters: 
Downlink, Uplink and Browsing Tput 
As seen in section 2.1.3, in LTE, Downlink and Uplink use different access and modulation schemes. 
Therefore, their throughputs need to be considered separately. So, there will be a Downlink Tput and an 
Uplink Tput measurement. There will also be a browsing Tput which has influence on users’ experience 
when surfing the web. 
For Downlink Tput the following website was used: ipv4.download.thinkbroad.com:8080, where a 1MB 
file could be downloaded. DL Throughput is then measured based on the average bit rate of the process. 
For Uplink Tput the following website was used: ipv4.upload.thinkbroad.com:8080, where a 1MB file 
could be uploaded. UL Throughput is then measured based on the average bit rate of the process. 
For Browsing Tput the following website was used: http://www.netplan.dk. This website was chosen 
because it is not a picture heavy website, therefore it would not require a long loading time. 
Round-Trip Time 
It is the time a packet takes to leave a UE, go through the network up to a destination application server 
and return to UE. It is an important parameter to measure because from it latency values can be derived. 
Google Public DNS at the Internet Packet (IP) Address 8.8.8.8 was used, this is mapped to the nearest 
operational server. Number of pings chosen was 3, with a one second interval in between them and a 
packet size of 32 bytes. RTT is then the mean of these 3 pings. Due to some unknowns, such as

Video streaming response time and Jerkiness 
The goal with choosing measurement parameters is to have a basic indication on a streaming service. The 
tool recorded the response time for the 1st picture of the streamed video. In video transmission, Jerkiness 
describes the perception of individual still images in a motion picture. It is a combination of frame 
dropping, freeze and reduced number of frames. It is given in a percentage value [27]. In other words, it 
evaluates video quality. 
In order to make this measurement short, the video used is a 640x360 in resolution, with 10 seconds of 
display duration. The following website was used for the stream: youtube.com/OHU80BsabxQ. 
 
3rd step – UE placement 
UE positioning was the critical step for these experiments. As seen in section 2.1.3 channel conditions 
influence the modulation scheme used, having a direct impact on throughput performance. Therefore, it 
was first necessary to make sure the UE was within the range of the correct cell. For that, Telia’s resources 
provided the Physical Cell ID (PCI) which could then be counter checked with the PCI indicated in the UE.  
Finally, UE had to be positioned to achieve either good or excellent channel conditions, according to 
figure[9]. 
 
4th step – Perform the measurement 
Start the job. Each job ran five cycles of measurements, creating 5 different data points for each parameter 
measured.  
 
5th step – Data treatment and analysis 
Export the data measured to an excel sheet and perform the analysis of the data. This step was the most 
time-consuming part of the whole process by far. More than 75% of the time was spent on this task 
because the data had to be exported manually from the software interface to an excel sheet.

6.4 Results and discussion 
 
This section presents the results after processing the data from the experiments conducted as explained 
in the previous section. For better conclusions, some results are further compared with specific data from 
the network infrastructure side, provided by Telia. The results are presented mainly in two ways: 
 
• 
Mean Values  
 
One of the approaches to analyze and compare the measured data was doing so in a time 
perspective. Mean values allowed for a summary overview of the large datasets. It gave 
several single values that allowed for an easy visualization of the measured data. Results could 
then be compared on a time perspective. A downside is that a lot of information got discarded 
in the averaging process. For example, peak and minimum values (data range), and data 
variance. 
 
• 
Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF)  
 
The datasets resulting from the experiments carried a huge range of variation and levels of 
randomness. CDFs gave the actual spread of the datasets in a probabilistic plot. It allowed for 
a visualization of the entire range of the dataset involving a specific instance in a scenario.  
Different instances could then be visualized in a common plot for a performance comparison, 
where the expected value (0.5 probability value) would be the baseline for that comparison.

6.5 Main conclusions 
 
Main conclusions are expressed in relation to the experimental objectives. 
a) Study the correlation between throughput and latency 
 
Although in some graphs throughput and latency seemed to have an inverse relationship, in some 
instances they showed the opposite behavior. 
Other circumstantial evidence was the fact that time of the day had great influence on throughput 
but not as much on RTT.  
 
b) Analyze the effect of different cell loads on throughput and latency 
 
It had been seen that when the cell load increases, throughput performance decreases and vice-
versa, leading to the conclusion that throughput is directly influenced by the cell load. The results 
for RTT lead to the conclusion that, unlike for throughput, cell loads do influence RTT performance 
but in a lower factor.  
 
c) Analyze performance based on user mobility  
 
Handovers frequencies have a clear effect on performance. Measurement performed do not allow 
for a conclusion on a positive or negative effect because that is also dependent on each cell’s 
specific load. However, it is possible to conclude on the important that the mobility management 
entity will have on a 5G system performance. 
Mobility speeds showed to have an influence on streaming quality, this can be explained by the 
huge variations in the channel conditions. This lead to the conclusion that travelling speeds have 
a negative impact on QoS. Also, these results attest for the complex challenges in providing QoS 
in mobility scenarios. 
 
d) Observe differences in performance between radio and fiber links for backhaul  
 
Having a site connected with fiber does not always guarantee lower RTT compared to radio. 
Results on experiment 3, scenario 1, showed that the lowest mean RTT values were observed on 
the third hop which was connected by radio link. However, the lowest mean value including both 
scenarios was observed on a site with fiber backhaul.  
Overall, RTT results between the two types of links were very similar, therefore the decision on 
the type of link deployment should not be done based on this factor. 
Results on throughput did not allow for a clear interpretation. A more accurate analysis of 
throughput should include cell loads. Therefore, the effect of the different type of links on 
throughput was inconclusive. 
 
e) Determine whether radio link is a viable solution to address latency requirements

Based on the findings also applied to answer the previous objective, radio link has been measured 
to be a viable solution regarding latency, and inconclusive when it comes to throughput. These 
findings only apply for good climate conditions. 
 
f) Analyze throughput and latency based on the number of transmission link hops 
 
Once again, no conclusion can be derived from the throughput analysis without considering cell 
loads. On the other hand, it is easy to conclude from these results that the number of hops do not

6.6 Experimental limitations 
 
The experiments described above derived from a conceptual consideration. Finding some of the 
conceptual scenarios on the real-life network proved to be a challenging task. For experiment 1 and 3, 
channel conditions are constantly changing, so it was a huge challenge to always maintain channel 
conditions at a good level. Despite that, measurements fell within desired ranges for most of the time.  
Experiment 3 aimed to give insights on fiber and radio link performance. Therefore, for the sake of fairness 
and accuracy, similar transmission chains should be considered for each fiber and radio links, however it 
is not possible to find transmission chains exclusively on fiber. That is because, in Telia’s network, when a 
site is backhauled through fiber, it is done so directly to an aggregation node. An extra point for fairness 
would have been using link with similar traffic loads, as it influences 𝐷𝑒𝑙𝑎𝑦𝑃𝑟𝑜𝑡𝑜𝑐𝑜𝑙.  
Another limitation was encountered when trying to find the best scenario to answer objective f) 
concerning performance based on the number of hops. In this experiment, the more hops the better for 
a more accurate analysis. Telia’s network has transmission chains with a higher number of hops than what 
experiment 3 presents. Unfortunately, these transmission chains are in rural areas making accessibility 
for measurements unfeasible for the purpose of this thesis.  
Also, for an accurate analysis on radio link influence on throughput, ideally a chain with similar cell loads 
would be the best scenario. Though, no transmission chains with that prerequisite was found in the 
vicinities of where tests would take place.  
Radio link performance is affected by climate conditions, particularly rain. An ideal basis for a thorough 
assessment on radio link performance would have been conducting these experiments in different climate 
conditions. However, it has not rained during the period these measurements took place. 
Mobile devices have a huge impact on the performance of services, mostly due to its varying antenna 
performances [1]. So, it is possible that different mobile device could have measured different values than 
those presented. 
It is a noticeable issue how low measured values for throughputs in all experiments is regarding the 
theoretical values for LTE technology. Nevertheless, the scope of the thesis does not lay on the 
quantitative values, but rather on the behavior associated with them.  
Another issue that could be considered for future work is the fact that the chosen servers are out of the 
controlled network. Random testing times may yield different results due to not being able to know traffic 
loads at servers. On the other hand, this a real-world issue that network operators must deal with.

7 Conclusion 
 
Mobile communication systems aim to offer enough coverage and capacity so that its users can be mobile 
while using services. These systems are divided among RANs, core networks, and a backhaul network that 
does the linking between the other two. The systems currently in use have different technologies, access 
schemes and architectures as building blocks. Thus, deliver different performance aimed at different 
services. GSM was a circuit switched system that aimed at delivering voice and messaging services. UMTS 
uses both circuit-switched and packet-switched systems, offering better data services. LTE is a packet-
switched system only, that aimed to improve capacity, speed, latency and coverage requirements. 
However, none of these systems will be able to handle traffic demands expected as early as 2025. 
 
The fifth generation of mobile communication systems, NR, will open possibilities for new use cases that 
require distinct capabilities from the network. This is enabled by the exploration of the mmW spectrum, 
new physical layer techniques for network’s access and resource allocation, and the deployment of a high 
number of smaller cells. Architecture will also evolve to enable the use of network functions virtualization 
and cloudification. Consequently, 5G will be made of several logical networks running over a unique 
infrastructure. Although 5G will exponentially improve network capabilities, these will come at a 
complexity cost that require detailed considerations. 
 
Network densification is already introduced in current networks to improve performance. 3G networks 
densification is at 4-5 BS/𝑘𝑚2, while that number in 4G is at 8-10 BS/𝑘𝑚2.  
5G performance will require the shrinking of cell sizes to maximum radius of up to 100 meters. This will 
call for the need of ultra-dense networks that deploy around 40-50 BS/𝑘𝑚2. C-RAN exploits a central 
processing location to dynamically allocate resources to APs, while saving on power consumption. The 
high number and diversity of APs will help slicing the network for a flexible resource allocation. However, 
many challenges arise with these kind of dense deployments, such as severe interference, 
synchronization, management and finding the best backhaul method. 
 
Dimensioning the backhaul network is essential to avoid bottlenecks that undermine performances on 
the RAN. Correct dimensioning can be done by estimating traffic for one cell and using statistical 
multiplexing to aggregate cell traffic in a region in an efficient way. Choosing the right topology is an 
important consideration to maximize 5G performances. There are different transmission link types that 
carry pros and cons in a cost-performance wat. DWDM links are ideal for 5G, however hard to be 
implemented for UDN needs in city scenarios, being then used for backhauling aggregation from 
aggregation sites. Therefore, mmW radio links are positioned to be a good solution for UDN fronthaul and 
small BSs self-backhauling. 
 
The experiments conducted showed that although fiber transmission links are desired, radio links are a 
viable solution for 5G deployment due to cost and deployment feasibility. 
First thing learned was that latency and throughput appeared to have no direct correlation. This means 
that it is not easy to know what the effect of planning the network regarding one parameter would have 
over the other, turning this task quite complex.  
==PAGE:72==
61 | P a g e  
 
Imagine now that these are only two of the many parameters considered to serve as foundation for the 
several use cases offered by 5G. This will call for a future network ability to pool resources accordingly to 
a scenario, and not just attempt to deliver them concurrently when not required. 
The experiments also showed that QoS is very dependent on cell loads. However, it is possible to observe 
and to a certain degree predict cell load patterns. These patterns should then be exploited to optimize 
cell usage. Using the right resources only when required will allow for performance, energy and cost 
efficiency.  
 
Smaller cells mean higher number of handovers for moving users, which has been proven in this work to 
result in poorer performances. A challenge brought by UDNs  is how to dimension the backhaul and 
whether it should be done by radio or fiber transmission links.  
In terms of latency performance, it has been shown that there is not a significant difference between the 
two methods. Therefore, deployment considerations should be based on other factors such involving 
other requirements, deployment area and financial costs.  
The possibility to do transmission chains in several hops to a macro BS or fiber PoP, make them an 
attractive solution for a city scenario where fiber may not be a possibility everywhere. 
 
The issue then turns on how to bring the 1000-fold expected traffic from a macro BS to the core network 
in an efficient way. Besides the increased traffic, this next generation backhaul network will need to 
address the network need to flexibly allocate its resources while making it easy to manage, reduce energy 
consumption and operational costs.  
C-RAN is the most viable solution to accomplish these on a network that will, either way, require 
automation and virtualization for a good number of its functions, as it is the case of a 5G network. C-RAN 
allow for the exploitation of cell load patterns measured to optimize the network operational efficiency. 
 
7.1 Future Work 
 
Future work would improve the experimental process by mitigating the issues mentioned in the 
experimental limitations section first. Different UE devices would be used to perform the experiments. 
Servers inside the Telia’s network domain would be used instead, so that exact delays could be taken into 
account.  
Cell loads would be studied for every cell involved in the experiments, with the respective account for the 
load at the time of the measurements. 
To address the fast-changing channel conditions issue, experiment 1 could be performed under the 
controlled environment provided by a lab. And then compare results so inconsistencies could be 
accounted. 
For experiment 2, a different x-axis for figure [24] would help drawing better conclusions. For example, 
plotting throughput over time, and seeing how it changes by the time a handover happens. Also, compare 
results with the cell loads on the path followed would allow for would be a good implementation. 
For experiment 3, use chains with similar traffic loads. 
Use an automated way to export the data from the UE. 
All measurements were done under ideal weather conditions for radio propagation. Future work would 
be to make measurements under rain conditions.

include a study on other technical considerations regarding future 5G networks.
