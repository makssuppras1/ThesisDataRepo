Abstract
The Danish parliament agreed in 2012, to to work toward a fossil free energy
supply in 2050. The goal is 40-70% of the total energy supply will be based
on electricity. Therefore electrical heat pumps are expected to play a impor-
tant role in the future energy system. The aim of this thesis is to investigate
how an 5,5MW two-stage ammonia heat pump for district heating, using low
temperature sources, is aected by the seasonal changes. In order to simulate
the heat pump performance response to a range of changes in external (envi-
ronmental) and internal parameters a numerical model has been implemented.
The simulation input parameters comprise a change in source medium between
seawater and sewage, source temperature, forward temperature, heat capacity,
annual operation time, electricity spot price. Based on a specied design point
the response of coecient of performance (COP), heating cost, and exergy
destruction rate and eciency were estimated by parametric analysis. The
COP range for the heating season (October-April) is found to be 2,95-3,65
with a mean of 3,21 for seawater and 3,18-3,71 with a mean of 3,40 for sewage
water. The forward temperature is found to have the largest eect on COP,
with higher temperature yielding lower eciency. The COP was found to be
at its highest in October with values of 3,54 for seawater and 3,60 sewage
water, corresponding heat costs of 494 and 491 DKK/MWh. The heat pump
is expected to have 1300-2000 annual operating hours (AOH) in the heating
season, AOH is found to have negative correlation with heat cost. From exergy
analysis it was found that the two high-stage compressors were responsible for
a combined 54% of the total irreversibilities. The performance of the heat
pump can be increased by lowering the load, however this is not economically
viable. Looking forward actions should be taken towards understanding the
interdependencies of the individual components. It is proposed that advanced
exergy analysis is undertaken in order to better understand the irreversibility
interdependence.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Introduction
The focus on phasing out fossil fuel based power plants has been on the agenda
for many years.
By introducing renewable energy sources such as biomass
and wind energy, the carbon footprint can be reduced drastically. The focus
on phasing out fossil fuel based power plants has been on the agenda for
many years. By introducing renewable energy sources such as biomass and
wind energy, the carbon footprint can be reduced drastically.
In 2012 the
Danish parliament agreed on working towards that all the energy supply in
2050 is generated by renewable energy sources. Biomass and wind are therefore
expected to play a major role in the Danish energy sector in the future. A goal
is that 40-70% of the energy system will be based on electricity, compared to
20% in 2010.
Calculations from the Danish commission on climate change have shown that
the biomass resources have to be prioritized in the transport sector as the
local production is limited. By placing the biomass resources in the transport
sector, Denmark could be self sustainable with the production of biomass [1].
As a consequence of this is, electricity and heat has to be produced by other
renewable sources, such as wind and solar power. Therefore electrical driven
heat pumps are expected to play a important role in the development of the
future district heating system.
In the context of this, the project SVAF (Experimental development of elec-
tric heat pumps in the Greater Copenhagen DH system) has been established
as a development program for large scale heat pumps (50-100MW). As the
technology for large scale heat pumps is immature, two test heat pumps of
5MW will be installed for demonstration and investigations of how heat pumps
can be integrated in a future smart energy system. The rst heat pump is
a geothermal hybrid compression-absorption heat pump, using a mixture of
ammonia and water as refrigerant, and will placed at the already existing

1.2
Problem statement
The aim of this study is to develop a steady state numerical model, in EES
(Engineering Equation Solver), of an ammonia heat pump based on the spec-
ications given for the 5MW test heat pump for sea- and sewage water in the
SVAF project. The model will be used to analyse how the heat pumps per-
formance and heat cost is aected by the seasonal environmental variations
in a district heating network in the Greater Copenhagen Area. With exergy
analysis, the model will be used to locate where actions should be taken to
improve the overall performance.
1.3
Readers guide
The report consists of 8 chapters:
Chapter 1 introduces the motivation and problem statement for the thesis.
Chapter 2 presents the basics of a heat pump, and gives a brief introduction
to previous experiences with similar heat pumps.
Chapter 3 introduces the heat pump technologies, and how they are con-
trolled.
Chapter 4 outlines how the components are described mathematically, and
how heat pump is evaluated.
Chapter 5 describes how the numerical model can be used in EES and
presents the verication of the model and limiting parameters of the model.
Chapter 6 presents the results for the seasonal simulations and an analysis
of specic operation conditions.
Chapter 7 discusses the results in the context of the assumptions and lim-
itations of the model, and how the results can be used as part of the SVAF
project, and gives suggestions for future work.
Chapter 8 concludes on the problem statement.

2.3
Heat sources
From the SVAF projects phase 1, the focus has been on testing heat sources
with sucient energy potential to supply the Greater Copenhagen Area (GCA)
district heating network. Here it was decided test two facilities - one based on
geothermal energy as a high temperature source. The second facility, which is

ature sources. When the heat is extracted from the source, the temperature of
the source decreases. The SVAF heat pump is expected to operate at source
temperatures down to 2◦C [9].
The potential of the low temperature sources will be discussed below.
2.3.1

Sea water can be considered a nearly inexhaustible heat source, and given
most large cities lie out by the sea, it can be seen as a great potential source
for district heating heat pumps.

[8]. However temperatures below 0◦C are observed. The low temperatures
may give some restriction to the evaporators, as ice may build up in e.g a
traditional plate evaporator.

with COP of 3 could in total have a heating capacity of 12MW, with a cooling
of 3◦C of the waste water.
As the temperature of the sewage water is higher then for the seawater, it is
expected to reach a higher COP, when using the sewage water water as heat
source.

Exergy
The exergy analysis is a method which objectively accounts for both the quality
and quantity of the energy. When treating energy balances with the rst law
of thermodynamics, the energy is conserved from one state to another. Exergy
is a qualitative way to describe the irreversibilities of a system, and thus a
manageable application of the second law of thermodynamics [32].
The value of exergy is the maximum theoretical work that can be obtained
in a system that interacts with the environment. All systems operate within
surroundings of some kind. Here the environment specically refers to the im-
mediate surroundings of the system. The environment is chosen such that the
systems surroundings are at a dead state, meaning that it is not aected by
the systems process. In this study, the surroundings are dened from the tem-
perature of the coldest media acting on the system, being either the ambient
air or the source.
The exergy, E, of a system can be divided into four components: physical
exergy EPH, kinetic exergy EKN, potential exergy EPT and chemical exergy
ECH.
E = EPH + EKN + EPT + ECH

it on a unit of mass basis, hence the specic exergy becomes

Chapter 7
Discussion
Based on the design of the SVAF heat pump a numerical model was suc-
cessfully implemented in EES. With limited data available and no standing
decision on the heat pump control strategy, several assumptions were made.
The compressors in the system are oil cooled, and as no compressor polynomi-
als were available that could give the exact heat loss, the computation of the
compressors heat loss was modelled assuming constant inlet oil temperatures
and ow. The compressor heat loss was estimated from the design point, such
that that the compressor discharge temperature could be found.
The compressors were modelled with variable isentropic and volumetric e-
ciencies.
Previous studies have shown that the eect of variable isentropic
eciency has an impact on the COP, as the temperature lift varies [26]. The
volumetric eciency determines the size and displacement volume of the com-
pressor and thereby the has an eect on the compressor speed. Ommen et al.
showed that the net present value is highly sensitive for relative changes of
the isentropic eciency [37], resulting in variation of the heat cost. The isen-
tropic and volumetric eciencies for the low- and high stages were given for
the design point. Assuming that these were the maximum eciencies, simpli-
ed equations were implemented to accommodate for the changes in operating
conditions. The built in volume ratio of the compressors was optimized for the
design point and kept constant throughout the simulations.
From the capacity simulations, the compressor speeds got below their minimum
rated limit. The compressor speeds should not be taken as the nite speeds of
the real heat pump, however the change in speeds is more important to focus
on, as the compressor speeds in the model were based on estimations such that
they could operate in a wide range of conditions. To get the exact compressor
speeds in the model, compressor polynomials should have been implemented.
The heat exchangers were modelled with UA-values calculated from the AMTD,
which gave a wider operation range of the EES model compared to using the
LMTD (appendix A.3). By using the AMTD the overall heat transfer is un-

in temperature in and outlet temperatures increases, the error increases. One
of the initiatives to compensate for this was to calculate the UA-value for each
phase of the refrigerant separately, to minimize the overall temperature dif-
ferences. This was also done, to be able to match the connections of the grid
on the sink side. Further to minimize the error the heat exchangers could be
discretized [38], this was however out of the scope of this work.
To compensate for the changes in heat transfer due to changes in operation
conditions, the UA-value was set to vary with the heat exchangers load with
reference to the design load, based on the equation given in [31]. This method
does not take into consideration what type of heat exchanger is used, and
does not consider the exact changes in the heat transfer coecient due to the
changes in operating conditions in for example the phase transition region in
the two-phase region of the refrigerant. However the method used was found
to be suitable for the purpose of this thesis.
Imbalances in the mass ow on the sink side were observed when the forward
temperature was getting above 86◦C (gure 5.13a. These were found to be
caused by the oil cooler on heat pump 1, which turned negative. A solution to
solve this was unfortunately not found, however a boundary was implemented
such that in case of negative mass ows and pinch temperatures, the results
would not be considered.
The heat pumps performance varies with the external temperature conditions.
From the sensitivity analysis the dependency of COP was claried (gure 6.2).
The greatest eect on the COP was found to be the district heating forward-
and return temperature followed by the source temperature. With an increase
of the source and forward temperatures of 10%, the source temperature in-
creases the COP with the 0,5%, and the increase in forward temperature de-
creases the COP with 3,5%, ending up with a net change in COP of -3%points.
The corresponding change in heat cost is a increase of 1.5%points. Even if the
COP is less sensitive to the relative change in source temperature, a strong
correlation was found for between the two, with R2 being above 0,97 for the
seawater conguration. However it was found to be somewhat less accurate
for the sewage water condition with R2 ranging from 0,91 to 0,96.
With an average COP of 3,21 in the heating season, the heat pump was able to
have a heat production cost of 520DKK/MWh th with 1650 annual operating
hours at a spot price of 235DKK/MWh el on seawater, and with sewage water
the COP is 3,40 at a cost of 505DKK/MWh th. In Clausen et. al, 2014 [10,
page 57], the heat cost of a heat pump with a COP of 3, is expected to
be able to produce heat at 380DKK/MWh th.
This is however based on a
annual operation time of 5000 hours. In section 6.4.3 and 6.5.3 it was shown
that by increasing the amount of operating hours, the heat price is reduced.
By running the heat pump model for 5000 hours, the heat cost is reduced to
366DKK/MWh th and 351DKK/MWh th for sea- and sewage water respectively.

In the simulations with the with constant spot price in section 6.4.4 and 6.5.4
it was seen that preferred operation hours in the heating season were clustered
on the warmest months, April, October and November. However this would re-
quire a constant price for the electricity. As the heat pumps integration to the
power market will be addressed with the commercial spot price, the variation
in the spot price must be taken into consideration. The inuence of the shifting
spot price is seen, as the cheapest hours were more spread out trough the heat-
ing season (gure 6.30a and 6.32a). For the sea water condition a heat price of
503DKK/MWh th was calculated while it was 485DKK/MWh th for the sewage
water conguration at spot prices of 286DKK/MWh el and 233DKK/MWh el in
the right operating conditions (for 1650 annual operating hours). Thus strate-
gies for locating these in these conditions should be developed to minimize
cost. The eect of start-up and stop sequences should also be considered when
aiming to operate at low spot prices.
The economic evaluation of the heat pumps seasonal variation was based on
the net present value being zero within its lifetime of 20 years. It was found
that the factors with most inuence on the heat price were the heat load,
annual operation time and the investment of the heat pump. The uctuation
in heat price can be traced back to the COP, as it is directly linked to the
compressor work. However by optimising the systems COP at all costs may
not be the most economically sensible choice. In gure 6.9 it was seen that,
at low forward temperatures (bellow 80◦C), the capacity should be lowered to
about 80% or lower depending on the conditions. However by lowering the
capacity the heat price increases. Instead of lowering the capacity, the COP
should be optimized in other ways. When increasing the heat capacity, the
COP got worse, however the heat cost went down. As the economy is based
on the heat pump running at 100% capacity, by increasing the load more heat
will be produced, resulting in a lower heat price. By increasing the load on the
heat pump the wear on the equipment will also be increased. In the simulations
the intermediate pressure was calculated with equation 4.26. It was shown that
the COP could be optimized by controlling the intermediate pressure in the
intercoolers. Another way of optimizing the COP performance is to balance
the load distribution between the two heat pumps, this was not considered in
the model because of the lacking decision on a heat pump control strategy.
Other ways to minimize the heat cost are to look at operation strategy of the
heat pump. First of all the investment should be kept as low as possible as
it is of great inuence. However if investing in state of the art technology it
comes at a cost. As the heat pump is capable of running on both sea- and
sewage water, changing between the two sources for reaching an optimum COP
is also a possibility, as the seawater conguration gives an overall higher COP
when the two sources have equal temperatures. Another solution could be to
to run the evaporators in parallel, if the volume ow allows it, decreasing the
temperature lift of heat pump 1. This would in the condition given in table
2.3 give COP of 3.33, compared to 3,27 with the conguration analysed in the

Looking at the specic components it was shown in the exergy analysis that the
high stage compressors were responsible for 53,4% of the total exergy destruc-
tion in the system. One of the reasons to this is the fact that the refrigerant
is ammonia, which imposes high discharge temperatures at high pressures,
combined with the low isentropic eciency of the compressor. Thus the large
amount of oil cooling is needed. The exergy destruction in the intercooler of
heat pump 1 was found to account for 9% of the total destruction, compared
to 1,4% of the intercooler of heat pump 2. The source for the large destruction
ratios can not be claried by the conventional exergy analysis, as interdepen-
dencies of the exergy destruction exist. The conventional exergy analysis is
thus only used to pinpoint the location and magnitude irreversibilities. To un-
derstand source of the irreversibilities advanced exergy analysis must be used.
Advanced exergy analysis provides information about the interdependencies
between the components, and reveals the potential of improvement for each
component, compared to state of the art technology [39]. Further a thermoeco-
nomical analysis could be conducted to quantify the cost of the irreversibilities,
as an economical incentive to improve the cycle. This is however left for future
work on the project.
The conguration of the heat exchanger network was changed during the work
of the thesis, where a desuperheater was connected on the discharge line of
each of the two low-stage compressors. The information about the update of
the conguration did not arrive in time to be implemented in the model, due to
the time limit of the work. Future studies on the heat pump should therefore
include these updates.
The ow in the heat exchanger network was controlled by mass and energy
balances alone. In the real heat pump control valves are placed on the sink side
such that the ow can be directed to the dierent heat exchangers to optimize
the performance. This was however not considered in the project, and should
be implemented for future ow and optimization analysis.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
From the developed heat pump model annual performance and heat costs could
be estimated. Since there was limited data available for the heat pump, sev-
eral assumptions were made e.g. no compressor polynomials and no decision on
how the heat pump is controlled. Despite this, a simulation tool was produced,
in which it is possible to vary the appropriate parameters, and analyse their
eect on the model result, and in turn to optimize the heat pump. Bound-
ary conditions were implemented to avoid negative pinch temperatures, which
could occur when too high forward temperatures were encountered due to the
complex heat exchanger grid on the sink side.
From the sensitivity analysis it was found that the parameters with the strongest
impact on the COP were the forward- and return temperatures, followed by
the source temperature. Even if the variation in source temperature was not
the parameter with greatest impact on the COP, they were found to have a
strong correlation in the seasonal analysis. From the the sensitivity analysis on
heat price the annual operation time and heat load were found to have largest
impact followed by the expected lifetime and capital investment.
From the simulation of annual variations an average COP of 3,21 and 3,40
were found for the sea- and sewage water condition respectively in the heat-
ing season (October to April). For the sea water condition the minimum and
maximum COP were 2,92 and 3,65 respectively. The calculated heat cost was
520DKK/MWh in average in the heating season for the sea water congura-
tion, and 505DKK/MWh for the sewage water conguration.
From the exergy analysis it was found that the high-stage compressors are
responsible for a combined 53,4% of the total exergy destruction, followed by
the intercooler on heat pump 1, which accounts for 9% of the destruction. The
condenser of heat pump 1 was also found to have a signicant destruction of
6,5%. The systems total exergy eciency was found to be 59%.

A.1
Comparison of key gures of EES model
and GEA's budget quotation
The systems heat exchanger temperatures and capacity are given in the GEA
"Budget Quotation" [7]. The EES model was unable to nd an equilibrium
when using the UA-values, calculated from the GEA budget quotation, hence
the UA-values used in the system, were calculated on basis of the estimated
pinch temperatures given in table 5.1. The pinch temperatures were chosen
such that the temperature settings and mass ows in the heat exchanger system
match the settings given in the budget quotation.
The UA-values used in the model was were calculated from the chosen pinch
temperatures in table 5.1, to match the temperature and mass ows in the
heat exchanger network at the design point given in the budget quotation
by GEA [7]. In table A.1 a comparison of the calculated UA-values in EES
and the UA-values from the budget quotation is given, when calculated with
the LMTD and AMTD method. Generally a higher deviation is found when
calculating the UA-values with the LMTD method. In total the deviation is
-12,6% and -28,6% on the source and sin side respectively, compared to -11,5%
and -20,8% when using the AMTD method.
By observing the transport of heat through the heat exchangers, it can be seen
that the greatest deviations are seen for the oil coolers and desuperheaters (ta-
ble A.1), which are aected by the heat loss of the compressors. The deviation
is specially clear on heat pump 1, and may be caused by that no compressor
polynomials were available, to calculate the actual heat loss of the compres-
sors. However the total heat in the system only varies with -1,1% and 0% on
the source and sink side. The deviations of the evaporators are caused by the
load distribution of the heat pumps. The load distribution in the EES model
is found by the mass and energy balances of the system in the design point.
