Preface
This report is made to discover the behavior of an induction machine outside the nominal
operational region in relation to the use in an electric vehicle as traction motor.
The project is a Master’s Thesis at the Technical University of Denmark under the guid-
ance of supervisor Nenad Mijatovic.
A great thanks to Nenad Mijatovic for inspiration and support throughout the project
and giving valuable feedback.
Abstract
Vehicles with internal combustion engines have multi speed gearboxes with typically 5 to
8 diﬀerent gear ratios to increase the constant power speed Region (CPSR) on the wheels.
It makes it possible to climb up steep slopes at low speeds and drive very fast when the
road is ﬂat, without having an oversized engine.
Electric Machines (EM) have a large CPSR, but they still have a limit.
The design
requirement of weight and space limitations of traction machines indirectly set a lower
speed limit of the CPSR.
The upper limit is caused by the total leakage inductance, which dominates the voltage
drop across the machine windings at high speeds.
With a higher voltage across the machine windings the CPSR can be increased.
By using an open-end winding induction machine and by adding a second 2-level voltage
source converter (VSC), the voltage can be increased and thereby the CPSR, without
increasing the stress of the winding insulation.
The second converter only needs to deliver reactive power, but this enables the main
converter to provide more active power.
For this reason, a study has been conducted to investigate several ways to implement
a dual converter control to increase the CPSR, and a comparison of the results with tra-
ditional single converter control has been made.
The study relies on the theory of induction machines from books and papers and sim-
ulations in Simulink. Field Orientation Control (FOC) is used as a bench mark for the
control strategy and is compared with Model Predicted Control (MPC), which is a new
innovative way of controlling electrical machines.

Introduction
Background
The number of EVs increases heavily these years due to political motivations and the
general motivation to save the climate with new green technologies.
The main drive components in EVs are the battery, converter, motor and gearbox. This
report will focus on the converter and motor.
Motors which are also called Electrical Machines (EM), as they can both serve as a
motor and as a generator, have been known for more than 100 years since the inventors,
Thomas Edison and Nikola Tesla, developed the DC-machine and AC-induction machine,
respectively.
But not until approximately 100 years later the 3-phase converter was developed, and in
the 1980’s and 1990’s it becomes more widely used in the industry to increase the eﬃciency.
Nowadays, the converter is required in EVs to convert the DC-voltages from the bat-
tery to AC-voltages for the EM. The eﬃciency is maybe even more in focus than earlier,
as better eﬃciency leads to longer battery range.
Permanent Magnet assisted Synchronous Reluctance Machines (PMSRM) are used in al-
most every modern EV, which typically has a few percent better eﬃciency than IMs.
However, in some oﬀ-highway vehicles the change from hydraulic to electric conversion
has more attention than gaining the last percent of eﬃciency.
Some reasons could be:
That the total cost price is typically less sensitive to the battery costs compared to road
legal EVs.
The change from hydraulic to electric conversion can in some cases more than half the
energy consumption.
The electriﬁcation enables the vehicle to drive fully electric, which has a strong brand
value, no matter the overall eﬃciency.
Oﬀ-highway vehicles ﬁrst of all need a replacement of traditional hydraulics.
==PAGE:9==
Motivation
2
Motivation
As IMs are easily accessible, widespread and have a lower cost price compared to new
technology, the IM has some advantages over the PMSRM.
Furthermore, operation above nominal speed for IMs does not lead to danger due to the
lack of permanent magnets, but PSSRMs have permanent magnets which can cause high
back-induced voltages if a converter fail occurs above nominal speed.
The drawback of IMs is its large consumption of reactive power, which limits the CPSR
range due to the relatively high leakage inductance.
However, reactive power is easily obtained by increasing the voltage.
By increasing the voltage, extra reactive power is achieved to increase the CPSR. The
terminal voltage is limited by the insulation of the windings.
Therefore a new solution is purposed, where a second converter is connected to the other
end of the windings, instead of a star or a delta connection in the typical IM. In that way
the reactive voltage can be more than double as high as normal.
The second converter only supplies the IM with reactive energy, and therefore it only
needs to be connected to a capacitor, which can be ﬂoating, to stabilize the DC-voltage.
In order to cover the losses in the ﬂoating converter as well as in the IM, a small amount
of active power is needed. This could be achieved by a voltage control that draws active
power from the main converter through the IM to the ﬂoating reactive converter.
==PAGE:10==
Project Description / Problem Statement
3
Project Description / Problem Statement
In this project, optimal control of IMs using split phase topology will be investigated.
This is not a typical way of controlling IMs even though they are very common in both
industrial use and for transport use.
Traction machines, in particular, beneﬁt from the ability to operate at high-speed range
with constant power. Normally the leakage inductance of electrical machines limits the
CPSR but that is where the split phase topology could have an advantage and potentially
reduce the requirements for a gearbox.
Topics, which will be addressed during the project, are as follows:
• Understand the control schemes
• Established control schemes: Vector control (FOC), Direct Torque Control (DTC)*.
• Emerging control schemes: Model Predictive Control (MPC)
• Development of IM Simulink model with standard 2level VSI and FOC** as a
benchmark.
• Development of split phase IM Simulink model with standard 2x 2level VSI and
FOC**.
• Development of split phase IM Simulink model with standard 2x 2level VSI and
MPC**.
• Comparison of performance for control schemes and hardware requirements.
Comments:
*DTC will on be brieﬂy described, as FOC is chosen as benchmark for the comparison of
result.
**Original speed control was included in the project description, but as the simulation
execution time could be heavily reduced by only focusing on the current control, this has
been left out.
Limitations
As mentioned earlier, due to the limit of time, this report will focus only on the current
control, not on the speed control.
The MPC will be a simple one sample head prediction and not two sample ahead.
The control sample time of the FOC is set to the same value as the MPC (40us), due to
limitations of the IM-model in the simulation program Simulink (Simscape components),
although the typical control sampling time is lower for FOC in typical applications (1-
4ms).
Knowledge Accumulated by Others
Diﬀerent papers listed in the references section have made dual converter schemes with
both FOC and MPC control. These have been used as inspiration for this report.

With this DT model it is possible to predict the future behavior of a SCIM. For one
sample ahead predictions, it is only necessary to predict the stator currents, because the
rotor ﬂux will not change in the approximated discrete model within one sample.
In real applications using model predicted control (MPC), two sample ahead predictions
are needed, as the ﬁrst sample time step is used for the processor to update the inputs,
to calculate the future behavior and to update the output stage. The next sample time
step is the one where the impact from the output stage should change the process values
to the predicted values.
dq-system
For applications with Field Oriented Control (FOC) a rotating reference frame called
the synchronous dq-system is preferred instead of the stationary abc- or αβ-system. As
the stator currents in an IM is in synchronism with the speed of the magnetic ﬁeld of
the rotor, it is convenient to handle them in a synchronous dq-reference frame which
are aligned with the rotor ﬁeld. By this method all AC-machines can be handled like a
separate excited DC-machine. The synchronous reference frame has two axis which are
90◦shifted: d and q. Where the d-axis is aligned with the rotor ﬁeld.
The stator current in the d-axis (ids) is the ﬁeld producing current that magnetizes the
rotor. A ﬁctive current called magnetization current (imr, can be used to express the
actual ﬁeld strength of the rotor. This current follows ids with a ﬁrst order lag, due to
the inductance of the rotor and is: imr = ids −idr. Where idr is the d-current ﬂoating in
rotor.
The stator current in the q-axis (iqs) is the torque producing current. The electrical torque
(Te) is:

FOC of IM
Field Oriented Control (FOC) is a way to control an AC-machine like a DC-machine.
It converts the alternating stator currents from the stationary reference frame to DC
signals in the synchronous reference frame by a clark- and park-transformation, which are
easier to handle by classic PI-controllers.
The output from the PI-controls is then converted back from DC to AC by a reverse clark-
park-transformation to the output stage. The output stage makes the output voltage
vector impact the alternating stator currents as shown in Figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3 – Closed loop indirect FOC-control scheme of an SCIM, [5]
Measurements
The necessary input parameters for a FOC are:
• Stator currents
• DC-voltage
• Rotor speed (for closed loop)
The measurements of the stator currents are either done much more frequent than the
switching frequency and then ﬁltered, or done with the same frequency as the switching
frequency but in a speciﬁc time of the switching period to eliminate the distortion from
the switching and to pick up only the fundamental current.
In some cases it is only required to measure two phase currents, as the last phase current
is equal to the sum of the two others.
The measurement of the DC capacitor voltage is required to calculate the output voltage
==PAGE:19==

12
vectors from the output stage.
The rotor speed is needed for closed-loop operation and is either measured with a encoder
or a resolver. The encoder generates a number of pulses for each turn. A resolver is
a kind of rotational transformer that is supplied with a 5-10kHz sinusoidal reference
signal, and the resolver generates two output signals that are 90◦shifted from each other.
The absolute rotor position angle can be calculated based on the amplitude and the
polarisation of the two feedback signals.

DTC of IM
Direct torque control also uses the forward clark park transformation as FOC but does
not have any PI-controllers or reverse clark park transformation.
Instead DTC contains two hysteresis controllers that determine whether the ids and iqs
are within a deﬁned threshold of the reference value.
If not, the reference values are sent to a look-up table which decides what is the optimal
switching state to reach the desired reference value.
The look-up table contains the eight switching combinations, and each of them will have
a little or big, positive or negative eﬀect on the reference currents.
So if the ids is very low, and the iqs is a bit too high, the most optimal switching state is
the one that generates a big increase in the ids and a little decrease in iqs.
This topic will not be investigated deeper in this report.

MPC of RL-load

MPC of RL-load
Goal of MPC
Model Predictive Control (MPC) is a way to control state variables and to predict how
to reach them based on a model of the physical system.
Finite Control Set Model Predictive Control (FCS-MPC) is a way to control several
coupled state variables by given each of them a reference value and a weight that will
count in the selection of the optimal output.
Finite Control Set Model Predictive Control Predictive Current Control (PCS-MPC-PCC)
refers to the control of the currents. The behavior of an EM is a result of ids and iqs,
so the goal for the PCS-MPC-PCC is to keep the ids and iqs as close as possible to the
desired reference values. The higher the sampling frequency 1/Tsc for the MPC is, the
faster the response and the smaller the ripple current is.
However, the switching of the output voltages is done by a converter which is not lossless.
Every switching is associated with a switching loss.
To keep the switching frequency (fsw) within an acceptable limit, fsw is added to the list
of objectives, and should be kept to a constant value between 1kHz and 4kHz, to have a
good compromise between machine losses and converter losses.
Another advantage to keep the switching frequency at a constant level is to have a con-
stant noise from the machine, as a varying high frequent noise can be quite annoying for
the surroundings, if people are close to the sound.

In this section four setups and results of simulations will be shown.
To make a fair

conditions, which means equal sample time, control sample time, switching frequency, etc.
Below the common conditions are shown:
• Control sample time (Tsc) for all simulations is set to 5E-5s (20kHz), based on the
knowledge that converters with FOC and a control sample frequency fsc of 8kHz
is already achievable. This satisﬁes the requirement of an MPC to have a high fsw
and is still in the neighbourhood of a normal fsc for FOC.
• Simulation sample time is set to 5E-6s, as the IM-model begins to be inaccurate at
lower sample time. The drawback was a very long simulation execution time.
• The switching frequency (fsw) is set to 4kHz for FOC. For MPC the weighting factor
for (fsw) is adjusted to fsw ≈4kHz, although fsw is changing a lot for MPC during
speed changes.
• The simulations start with a magnetization sequence in order to reach nominal
magnetization before speed changes. This sequence is also used for pre-charging the
ﬂoating converter capacitor in the DVSC simulations.
• Torque reference is set to 2 pu, which is typical for IM during a acceleration period
of under 1 minute. The torque is applied after λdr = λdr,rated.
• The rotational speed is forced from 0 to 5 pu, after the torque is applied.

measurements are delayed by a single Ts in order to make the simulation converge.

the conversion from CT to DT.
• Predictions of the MPCs are only done for (k+1) due to simplicity reasons but also
because the simulation tool works diﬀerently to a physical controller, as it does the
calculations inﬁnitely fast, in contrast to a real controller which needs one control
sample period to calculate the future behavior.
• The FOC is set to space-vector-modulation and the output voltage is set very near
to the voltage limit, which means that slightly over-modulation can occur, as vds

• Saturation of the machine is neglected. However, λdr =< λdr,rated and Te =< 2

Test results of SVSC-MPC
Figure 3.25 shows the performance of a SVSC-MPC. The overall goal of an MPC is to
have a very high sampling frequency, so that it is only the selection of the optimal voltage
vector that decides when a change in the output stage is required.
This means that fsw is not constant. On the other hand, it cannot make faster changes
than the sample time.
In this case, it results in more oscillations of the currents, compared to FOC, as FOC
is able to produce voltage outputs with pulse widths much smaller than the control sam-
ple time.
Although i∗
qs is zero from 0 - 0.25s, it still oscillates with the same amplitude as ids.
This is because the output voltage vector is chosen on a compromise of the error in ids
and iqs and prioritizes freely among all seven possible voltage vectors.
In contrast to MPC, the voltage angle is more stable for FOC within a sample period, as
FOC uses space-vector-modulation. This is the reason why iqs does not oscillate for FOC
between 0 - 0.25s.
As τσ ≈10ms and the sample time of both FOC and MPC is set to 0.05ms, it is not
possible to see a diﬀerence in the step response of iqs at 0.3s.
Figure 3.25 C) shows the selection of the optimal voltage vector. It is actually possible
to see the rotor ﬂux angle rotate from Sopt. At 0.35 it only rotates slowly and begins to
rotate faster and faster in the rate of the rotor speed.
In the same period the switching frequency drops from almost 4kHz to only 160Hz at
0.62s. That is because the converter enters the breakdown torque region, where the IM
requires more voltage to achieve the requested currents than the converter is able to pro-
duce. The optimal voltage vector therefore changes with the same rotational speed as the
rotor in order to achieve the highest possible voltage: 160Hz = 3.2 pu.

Hardware requirements

Hardware requirements
In relation to EVs several factors have to be taken into account when it comes to the
drive line. The maximum power requirement is one of them.
It is shown that an additional converter can increase the power, not only by increasing the
CPSR, but also by increasing the nominal speed. But some extra components are required:
• A ﬂoating additional converter
• Some capacitance to supply the ﬂoating converter
• Measurement of the ﬂoating capacitor voltage
• Extra regulators in the software
These components are related to some costs but on the other hand it can also replace
other components and thereby maybe save some costs. The increment of the overall power
makes it possible to use a smaller IM, and thereby increasing the utilization and maybe
the overall eﬃciency of the drive line.
The increased CPSR could reduce the gearbox requirement of a 2-speed gearbox to a
single speed gearbox, as a second gear is no longer required.
The second converter is maybe also easier to implement, if it is optional compared to a
large gearbox or an EM, which could lead to a reduction in production costs.
Software requirements
An additional converter increases the computational burden, as additional regulators,
values, and measurements have to be calculated.
But the most determining factor is whether FOC or MPC is used, as MPC both requires
more calculations and requires a high sample frequency to compete with the performance
of an FOC in lower speeds.
On the other hand an MPC increases the power by approx. 10 −20%, which can lead to
cost reductions.
==PAGE:59==
52
5
Conclusion
An additional ﬂoating converter coupled to the other end of an open winded IM can
increase the CPSR but also increase the overall maximum power by increasing the nominal
speed of the machine without increasing the source voltage or increase the stress of the
machine windings.
The additional converter only has to supply the IM with reactive power, and thereby only
a capacitor is required to stabilize the DC-voltage of the ﬂoating converter.
To cover the losses and maintain a constant DC-voltage, a voltage regulator can control
the active power between the main converter and ﬂoating converter to charge the ﬂoating
capacitor. No DC/DC-converter is required.
It is also shown that MPC improves the performance at higher speeds. The drawback
is the requirement of computational power and more current and torque ripple at low
speeds.
Future work
The calculations of the reference values could be improved, as the main focus has been
on the high speed performance, not on the low speed performance.
This means that the ﬁeld weakening regulation of the ﬁrst ﬁeld weakening region could
be improved.
The control of imr in combination with iqs could also be improved at lower speeds, to
increase the maximum torque capability.
