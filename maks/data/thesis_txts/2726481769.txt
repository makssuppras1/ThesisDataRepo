Abstract: 
This thesis explores how top management and their decision-making create an attentional 
perspective during business model innovation in an early-stage startup. The thesis 
investigates how different aspect of the decision-making process affect each member of the 
top management to shed light on an under-researched part of the decision-making theory.  
The thesis applied a processual look at the decision-making process using a longitudinal 
mixed-method case study as the focus of the research. The primary data collected was 
through semi-structured interviews and supported by quantitative data in the form of 
quantitative analysis of overlapping themes and task-based attentional perspective 
calculations. The thesis found that creating an attentional perspective is an ongoing process 
in which each member of top management explores their own terrain and creates their own 
view. This view integrates learnings from past experiences and biases, which will be 
combined together with the other top management member to create an alignment. In the 
decision scenario, top management will make use of estimations and guesses and relate 
that to them achieving their target to determine what decision to make. Further, the 
specific pattern is dependent from decision type to decision type. The findings broaden the 
scope of the current research by showing individual top management members have unique 
learning processes that influence top management alignment and that the decision-making 
process is influenced by the company's stage of business model innovation and the type of 
decision required.

1. Introduction 
Startups are crucial in bringing about societal change and ensuring responsible growth 
(Jurgens, 2022). In the OECD countries startups account for about 20% of employment and 
they create almost half of the new jobs (OECD, 2022). Early-stage companies face a myriad 
of challenges as they strive to establish a strong foundation and achieve sustainable growth. 
Central to their success is the process of decision-making (Christensen, Bartmann, and Van 
Bever, 2016). The managers in startups are further differently affected by bias & heuristics 
than managers in large situations and therefore have different decision-making patterns 
(Barney & Busenitz, 1997). Business model innovation serves as an important element for 
startups, as they seek to design, refine, and adapt their core business models to meet 
market demands and seize new opportunities. The decisions made during this phase can 
have profound implications for their long-term viability and competitiveness (Christen et al. 
2016). Exploring the facets of decision-making in this context is not only academically 
intriguing but also offers valuable insights for practitioners  
1.1 Problem introduction and research question: 
There is already a lot of literature on the topic of business model innovation and what 
influences companies, both big and small, when they undertake it. The prior work draws 
focus on a multitude of different areas of research such as business models, attention, 
cognition, and decision-making theory. We know that business model innovation is a 
sequential process that goes through predictable stages of its journey (Smulders, 2014). We 
also know that many companies fail or succeed to varying degrees. This happens because of 
the many conditions affecting the business such as market conditions and conditions 
influencing the decision-making process of the firm such as the top management. The 
literature has already established that what the firm put its attention on matters a lot for 
the success of the final business model and the business model innovation process in 
general (Christensen et al. 2016). It’s important to differentiate between what the firm says 
it will focus on and what it focuses on as there quite often is a discrepancy between that (Li-
Ying, Van Oorschot, and Rosati, 2023). The reason for the discrepancy is because of the 
Founder’s experience, or lack thereof, and the uncertainty in which the firm operates (Li-
Ying et al. 2023). 
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
It is most often top management heavily influences what the firm focuses on as they 
determine the attentional perspective of the rest of the company. We do however also 
know that they have different attentional perspectives that are dependent on their own 
characteristics like biases, experience, position, etc. (Cho & Hambrik, 2006). The literature 
does however have a static view of founder characteristics and does not consider the 
learning opportunities founders have whilst during business model innovation. It does also 
not take into account the possibility of the top-management team sharing and learning from 
each other and how that influences the decision-making process. The question is then in an 
exploratory process such as BMI, how does top management continuously align their 
learnings to form an attentional perspective. This leads to the following research question: 
“How does top management team create a shared top-management attentional perspective 
during business model innovation in an early-stage startup.” 
The research question is to be answered through a mixed-method case study spanning 7 
weeks using different methodologies for data collection. The methods of qualitative analysis 
and its results are described later in the thesis. The findings in the analysis will be 
conceptualized into a Framework to help in practical use. This framework and its 
implications will be discussed in the discussion section. 
1.2  Thesis overview 
This thesis is structured into six chapters, which are comprised of three main parts, an in-
depth review of the literature and an empirical study. The first chapter introduced the 
research topic and its relevance and the research question. The second chapter will provide 
a literature review focusing on theories within business model innovation, attention, search, 
top management, and decision-making theory. It ends with a theoretical framework that 
functions as the baseline for the analysis. The third chapter will show the methodology of 
the thesis describing a mixed-methods case study, data collection, coding, and coding 
analysis. The fourth chapter will present the findings of the data structured around four key 
themes. Learning, decision, cognition, and attention.  The fifth chapter will discuss the 
findings; where they agree with the existing literature, where they enrich the literature, and 
provide an updated theoretical framework. Afterward, the discussion provides managerial

implications and potential avenues for future research. Finally, this thesis will summarize 
the thesis and conclude the presented work. 
2. Business model innovation and top management’s decision 
making 
The purpose of this chapter is to provide an overview of the existing literature on the topic 
based on the investigation of the researcher.  Firstly, to set the scene an overview of 
business models and business model innovation will be gone through. Secondly, the focus 
will be on highlighting attentional theory and how that relates to business model 
innovation. Thirdly, search theory will be examined and shown how that is affected by 
attention. Fourthly, it will be gone through how top management’s cognitive capabilities are 
affected by and how they make decisions. Lastly, a theoretical framework that underlines 
the theoretical lens on which this thesis will be based will be presented. 
2.1 Business model & business model innovation: 
Business models are a core function of how business work. The research defines them as 
Systems of ‘interdependent organizational activities centered on a focal firm’ (Zott and 
Amit, 2010: 217) that are ‘made up of components, linkages between components, and 
dynamics. There are multiple definitions of exactly what those components are as illustrated 
by Christensen, 2016 that uses: Value proposition, Resources, Profit formula, and Processes 
as the components. These four components are congruent with each other. Another way of 
understanding a business model is simply as the configuration of a machine, where all parts 
are connected in some way with a unique inner logic to create and capture value 
(Casadesus, Masanell and Ricart, 2010; Li-Ying et al. 2023).   
 
A business model is however very rarely static, and can/will change over time. When a 
company changes its business model, they are engaging in business model innovation. Foss 
and Saebi (2017) define business model innovation as “designed, novel, and nontrivial 
changes to the key elements of a firm’s BM and/or the architecture linking these elements”. 
This means that whenever a company decides to change one of the key elements, for 
example, the value proposition, they undergo business model innovation.  As a well-
functioning business model is claimed to be a core performance enabler, BMI as a specific 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
innovation type is considered essential for an organization's success (Futterer, Schmidt, and 
Heidenreich. 2018). Business models by their very nature are designed not to change, and 
they become less flexible and more resistant to change as they develop over time. 
Therefore, the better the business model is at performing its assigned task, the harder it will 
be to change. This is due to the interdependencies from processes that emerged as a 
natural and collective response to recurrent activities (Christensen et al. 2016).  
 
There are many reasons for undertaking business model innovation. These can both be 
internal such as a change in strategy and dynamic capability, and they can be external like 
changes in competition, stakeholder demands, and technology (Foss & Saebi, 2017). 
Business model innovation does however not always happen as a reaction to change. 
Sometimes the business model innovation happens despite no outside change and is 
instead the reason for the exogenous change (Martins, Rindova, and Greenbaum. 2015).  
 
There are many different things that affect the process and success of business model 
innovation. One of them is the size of the company where the literature focused on both 
early-stage startups and more established companies. Depending on its size, the company 
may engage differently in business model innovation and receive different outcomes. This 
can be illustrated by that early-stage startups might be more flexible and therefore easier to 
engage in business model innovation, but more established companies can receive large 
performance effects from BMI (Foss & Saebi, 2017). For start-ups, any act of 
entrepreneurship means the choice of a BM, while in established firms the exercise of 
entrepreneurial judgment results in changes in the BM’s components or architecture (Foss 
& Saebi, 2017). This means that because startups often don’t have a business model in place 
or they have a singular business model, they are per definition engaging in business model 
innovation.  
 
Another factor affecting the success of business model innovation is market timing. Time is 
not necessarily linear, it's relative and context-dependent. Axelson & Bjurström (2019) 
introduces Managed timing as a way of thinking about market timing and business model 
success. Managed timing is the conscious choice to manage business model evolution in 
cases when only time and actual customer engagement can dissolve uncertainty. When 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
evolving a new business model in a largely unknown context, prior knowledge of conditions 
such as customer understanding and relationship maturity might no longer be true and 
companies therefore can’t rely on them anymore. Additionally, prior experience of how long 
it takes for these elements to emerge may also be different and the company needs to 
relearn it. The ideal speed of development is therefore not necessarily about being fastest, 
but instead finding the right time, which differs for each company. 
 
To succeed in business model innovation, it is important to understand that it is the process 
that for the majority drives failure or success and that business models develop over time 
through predictable stages (Christensen et al. 2016). Christensen et al. 2016 define three 
stages of business model innovation: Creation, Sustaining innovation, and Efficiency. 
Creation relates primarily to the creation of the value proposition and the needed 
resources. Sustaining innovation relates to the processes created in the ‘Creation’ stage. 
Lastly, at the efficiency stage, the firm makes incremental innovations to improve efficiency 
(Christensen et al. 2016).   
 
Another way to look at the process of business model innovation is through Smulders’s 
(2014) perspective of the IDER model. The IDER model consists of four stages: initiating, 
Designing, engineering, and realization. Initiating is the front end of product development 
like market research and/or ethnographic studies. Designing is about the development of 
concepts of the new product/service. Engineering which happens and the associated 
development of the necessary manufacturing process and tools. Smulders (2014) agrees 
with Christensen et al. (2016) that business model innovation happens in predictable stages, 
and the IDER model, therefore, has a theoretically correct sequencing. The IDER process 
however sees the process as more cyclical in nature and the company engaging in 
innovation will therefore also keep revisiting the previous stages and not have an 
attentional focus on only one stage. The IDER describes where the attentional focus lies 
over time of the business model innovation journey (Smulders, 2014). In real life, it is 
however not easy to uphold the theoretical correct attentional focus as illustrated by Li-Ying 
et al. (2023). Instead of progressing through IDER somewhat chronologically, the sequence 
and amount of attention allocated to each stage changes according to the founders and the 
environment in which the startup operates (Li-Ying et al. 2023).

2.2. Attention 
There is a multitude of ways that research looks upon a firm’s business model innovation: 
Rational positioning view, Evolutionary view, Cognitive view (Martins et al. 2015), and 
Attention-based view (Ocasio, 2011). The rational positing view sees firms and their 
business models as purposefully designed systems that reflect rational managerial choices. 
The evolutionary views see the development of a business model as a response to specific 
problems and/or opportunities focusing on strategic change being driven by trial rather than 
forethought. In the cognitive view, the business models stand as cognitive structures 
providing a theory of how to set boundaries to the firm of how to create value, and how to 
organize its internal structure and governance (Martins et al. 2015). The attentional-based 
view sees organizations as systems of distributed attentional processing (Ocasio, 2011). This 
thesis will primarily use the cognitive and attentional-based view of business model 
development as they fit better for understanding and explaining how top managers create a 
shared attentional perspective.  
 
2.2.1. Types of Attention 
To understand and to be able to work with the attentional-based view, it’s important to 
know what attention is.  
Attention is a cognitive process that shapes the way we perceive and respond to 
information in our environment. There are three different types of attention: Selected 
attention, executive attention, and vigilance (Ocasio, 2011). Selected attention sees 
attention as a process where individuals focus their information processing on a specific set 
of stimuli at a specific moment in time. Because of information processing capacity and 
information overload, humans have to choose a range of stimuli to attend to, and some to 
leave out. Executive attention guides cognition and action when there is no predetermined 
schema to achieve goals or task demands (Parasurman 2000; Ocasio 2011). This is further 
enriched by that point out that the same happens when there is a conflict between the 
goals (Fernandez-Duque et al., 2002; Ocasio 2011). The last variation of attention, vigilance, 
sees attention as the process where individuals maintain concentration on a particular 
stimulus, such as a specific signal or if there is a change happening. During periods of 
sustained attention, attention toward a stimulus is high, on the other hand, when sustained 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
attention can no longer be maintained, attention is low (Ocasio, 2011). Importantly, 
attention is not just an individual cognitive process. It is also shaped by social factors such as 
organizational culture, power dynamics, and social networks (Ocasio, 2011) 
 
Using the different attention modes of the brain, Ocasio (2011) builds upon prior research 
within neuroscience to classify three different types of attention: attentional perspective, 
attentional engagement, and attentional selection. Attentional perspective is the “top-down 
cognitive (and motivational) structures that generate heightened awareness and focus over 
time” (Ocasio, 2011, p. 3). It can be understood as the cognitive frame for how individuals 
process information. It can affect how individuals perceive and interpret information and 
their subsequent actions and decisions. Attentional engagement is defined as the process of 
intentional, sustained allocation of cognitive resources to a task or stimulus. Attentional 
selection is the process of the passive or intentional attentional process that results in 
focusing attention on and attending to a chosen stimuli or task and which stimuli or tasks to 
ignore. Li-Ying et al. (2023) further enriches the discussion on selected attention by 
differentiating between selective attention and action attention: “Selective attention refers 
to the attention allocated to what has been prioritized as the result of rational decision-
making, while action attention is the attention actually paid to concrete actions that are 
emerging and considered necessary and urgent” (p. 6). There is a difference between what 
founders rationally have agreed to focus on and what they actually focus their attention on 
subconsciously.  Further, Li-Ying et al. (2023) sargues that the reason for a messy business 
model innovation lies in the misallocation of attention. 
 
2.2.1. Attention and top management 
While all aspects of attention previously mentioned are important, the attentional 
perspective is important to understand as it is the same as the dominating strategy of the 
firm when looking at a business model through an attention-based view (Ocasio, 2011). That 
means the attentional perspective determines, at least in part, what the employees of the 
firm are working on and therefore how the company progresses through the business model 
innovation journey 
This is also pointed out more generally that attention also determines decision-makers’ 
actions and shapes subsequent organizational actions (Ocasio, 1997). The problem is that a 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
decision-maker has a finite amount of attention to focus on a problem (Simon, 1947; Li-Ying 
et al. 2023). This leads to managers only being able to attend to a narrow set of issues and 
answers. Furthermore, only those issues that the managers attend to will influence 
decisions and lead to organizational moves (Ocasio, 1997). As the issue that managers can 
attend to is limited, it is important for top management to find the right information search 
in the right place and the for the right thing to make the right organization moves for the 
business model innovation.  
2.3. Search 
When managers make decisions in their environment, a lot of them are highly affected by 
the knowledge they have gained leading up to the decision. This is pointed out by 
Raghunathan (1999) who highlight that the quality of information obtained by managers has 
a significant impact on their ability to make accurate decisions. Li, & Maggitti, Patrick & Smith 
& Tesluk & Katila (2012) enrich that and point out that attention risk being wasted if much of 
what is encountered during the search is irrelevant or redundant or if the relevant 
knowledge is not used for innovation. 
2.3.1 Top management and search 
Top management all have their own characteristics and knowledge from prior experience, 
but the way that managers gain new knowledge and information is through search (Li et al. 
2012). To clarify, search is the controlled and proactive process of attending to, examining, 
and evaluating new knowledge and information (Li et. al, 2012).  When managers search, 
they allocate attention to certain aspects of their environment and ignore others. When 
talking about search, there are two different cognitive areas that affect the firm’s 
innovation: Search selection and search intensity (Li et al. 2012). Search selection focuses on 
the location managers select to focus their attention during the search, and search intensity 
refers to the cognitive effort, cognitive capacity, and persistence that managers use when 
searching. These concepts are not interdependent as failure to identify new information and 
knowledge in one location may prompt simultaneous effort to continue searching. 
2.3.2 Factors affecting search 
Building upon prior search and selective attention literature (Katila & Ahuja, 2002; Martin & 
Mitchell, 1998; Rosenkopf & Almeida, 2003; Li et al. 2012), Li et al. (2012) points out three 
different factors affecting search selection and the knowledge gained from the search: 
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
“terrain unfamiliarity”,” “terrain distance,” and “terrain source diversity”. Specifically, the 
more unfamiliar, distant, and diverse the sources of information being looked at, the more 
likely to contain novel, salient, and vivid information for the searcher. They are also more 
likely to capture searchers’ attention. Furthermore, new knowledge is also more likely to 
make the searcher detect insights. In addition to capturing the searcher’s attention better, 
unfamiliar, distant, and diverse terrains are also more likely to contain new information, 
which can help managers update their knowledge base and gain insights into the detection, 
development, and deployment of new products 
Effortful and persistent searches enable top management to consider more alternatives 
while also providing them with the additional capacity to interpret elements of their terrain 
along with their own knowledge. By utilizing this approach, individuals can categorize and 
recategorize new information and knowledge and infer connections between different 
information. Furthermore, it also allows them to combine new information and knowledge 
in different ways, and thereby reach a deeper comprehension of the information’s 
usefulness. Further, searchers’ interpretations of new information significantly affect the 
creative outcomes (Li et al. 2012).  Another reason why It’s important to search for new 
information is that organizations risk being myopic in their search behavior, and focus too 
much on existing knowledge, and failing to explore new sources of knowledge (Levinthal & 
March, 1993).  
Having described search, how managers search, and what affects the search, it is needed to 
pinpoint how that intertwines with business model innovation and creating a shared 
attentional perspective. To do this, the next part will concern how top management search 
and affect attention and how it intertwines during business model innovation. 
2.4. Top management 
2.4.1. Top management and business model innovation 
The reason for the deviation in the IDER model can be found in how top management 
allocation of attention. More specifically, as pointed out by Li-Ying et al. (2023), the reason 
can be found in the misallocation of attention which can result in failure or a high cost of 
BMI. Li-Ying et al (2023) further illustrate that reason for the misallocation of attention is 
the founders’ experience and the uncertainty in which the company operates. To 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
understand why the founders’ experience and the uncertainty in which the startup operates 
are key to explaining deviation in the IDER model, it Is necessary to understand how the 
concepts of attention and cognition affect a firm and the individuals within. 
Understanding top management is as important as they are responsible for the firm’s key 
strategic decisions. In particular, the top management team is directly involved in strategic 
decisions regarding innovation (Li et al. 2012). Furthermore, attentional orientation can be 
seen as a result of the top management team, what they focus on, and what they tell other 
people to focus on. A top management team does however also consist of multiple 
individuals with their own attention patterns. This pattern is a result of their collective 
experiences, the distribution of influence among them, and their processes for sharing 
information and making decisions. In addition to their collective experiences and current 
impacts such as distribution influence, the top management is also affected by their own 
executive characteristics like background, function, biases, etc. which all serve to filter and 
distort the abundant information that confronts the individual members of the top 
management team (Ocasio 1997; Cho & Hambrik 2006). In the end, some attentional 
orientations will prevail amongst top management, and others will be secondary or even 
nonexistent (Cho & Hambrik, 2006). 
2.4.2. Top management’s learning process 
As pointed out, by Li-Ying al. (2023) building upon Levinthal & March (1981), business model 
innovation is an adaptive organizational learning process. This learning process takes place 
through the interplay of experiential learning and experimental learning. Experimental 
learning is more trial and error based where teams try out new things and adjust their 
actions without changing or questioning fundamental goals, assumptions, and meanings. 
Experiential learning on the other hand involves teams and individuals questioning and 
challenging the fundamental goals of the business based on the feedforward from 
experiential learning. This process is also called double-loop learning (Argyris 1976; Li-Ying 
et al. 2023).  
The business model innovation as pointed out earlier is a process that is sequential in nature 
where managers search for information and allocate attention to create organizational 
moves. This plays out in the organizational learning process where managers search in one 
area by allocating attention and experimenting, learn from that, and search in another area. 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
Managers in business model innovation are subject to failure traps and success traps where 
they follow the initial success full path even though it may not be right and stray away from 
potential paths because of initial. This then leads to continued investment and search in 
that failure (Levinthal & March 1993; Li-Ying et al. 2023). Thus, what top management 
decides to focus on is affected by where they see the most initial success and not 
necessarily, what might be the right path.  
 
While initial success affects what top management focuses on, that process is also affected 
by prior actions and decisions that led to the specific options being explored. What decisions 
and actions are taken does however also depend on a lot of things, such as search and 
attention, and also humans’ inherent limitations. To address this, the next part of the 
literature review will explore research on how people cognitively make decisions, what 
affects this, and what the limitations are. 
 
2.5. Decision-making process 
2.5.1. Limited rationality 
When people make decisions, they are unable to act fully rational, instead, they are 
following mental shortcuts and have limited rationality. Scholars have long considered how 
people think and the process of our thinking. There have been defined generally two major 
areas of research on rationality: unbounded rationality and bounded rationality. Unbounded 
rationality says that the correct decision is the one that maximizes one’s own subjective 
expected utility. The issue that scholars found was that the person would have to consider 
all possible consequences of their action to know that, which the human mind is simply 
incapable of. As a result of the critique, another close thought stream arose, optimization 
under constraints where a person had to stop searching for further information once the 
cost of searching increased the benefit gained (Anderson & Miles, 1989; Gigerenzer & Todd, 
1999). The issue with that approach is that the calculation would require even more 
computational ability than unbounded rationality (Vriend, 1996; Gigerenzer & Todd, 1999). 
Most recent research has focused on bounded rationality where individuals are not always 
able to make completely rational decisions because of various factors such as cognitive 
limitations, time constraints, limited information, and emotional biases. Instead, humans 
==PAGE:22==
21 
 
leverage heuristics, which can be conceptualized as mental shortcuts to simplify complex 
decision-making tasks (Gigerenzer, Todd, 1999). Gigerenzer & Todd (1999) more specifically 
focused on fast and frugal heuristics that are characterized by being simple, fast, and frugal, 
meaning requiring little information and cognitive effort. The good thing about fast and 
frugal heuristics is that they can be used even in the face of incomplete or uncertain 
information. An example of fast and frugal heuristics is satisficing where an individual 
chooses a lower threshold for success and stops searching once an option that is good 
enough has been found.  
 
2.5.2. System 1 & System 2 
Another way of looking at how people make decisions is by separating human thinking into 
system 1 and system 2 (Shleifer, 2012).  System 1 thinking refers to automatic, intuitive, and 
fast thinking that operates mostly outside of our conscious thinking, while System 2 thinking 
refers to more deliberate, analytical, and effortful thinking that involves conscious cognitive 
processes. System 2 is a slave to our system 1 where system 1 sends suggestions to system 2 
which then turns them into beliefs. System 1 is the default option and system 2 has to be 
activated. Additionally, system 2 is characterized by that humans have limited attention and 
have the risk of cognitive overload. Furthermore, system 2 is also affected by the law of 
least effort where it will take the action that seems to require the least amount of effort 
(Shleifer, 2012). Gigerenzer and Todd (1999) can be seen as related to System 1 as they both 
emphasize quick and efficient decision-making. The difference lies in that system 1 is often 
associated with cognitive bias and errors (Shleifer, 2012) while fast and frugal heuristics are 
seen more as an adaptive toolbox (Gigerenzer & Todd, 1999). 
While Gigerenzer and Todd (1999) and Kahneman look at how individuals make decisions 
March's (March & Heath, 1994) perspective is focused more on the organizational context 
of decision-making, and how the limitations of the decision-making environment can shape 
the choices that decision-makers make. March & Heath (1994) argues that decision-making 
processes in organizations are often constrained by time, information, and cognitive 
capacity limitations, which may result in bounded decision-making. In addition, March's 
ideas about decision-making in organizations can be related to Kahneman's concept of 
System 1 and System 2 thinking, in that the limitations of the decision-making environment 
==PAGE:23==
22 
 
can lead decision-makers to rely more heavily on System 1 thinking, which is more 
automatic and intuitive, and less on System 2 thinking, which is more analytical and 
deliberate. This can lead to biases and errors in decision-making, similar to those associated 
with System 1 thinking in Kahneman's framework. 
March also has a different view of rationality compared to Gigerenzer and Todd who 
focuses on bounded rationality. Instead, people are limited rational which means that 
humans are not capable of fully rational decision-making. People want to appear rational 
even though they are not. It is assumed that decisions are based on one’s preference and 
the expected value of the actions. Instead, people follow a logic of appropriateness where 
instead of asking themselves about alternatives and their consequences, they ask: Who am 
I, what situation am I in, and how do I believe a person in my situation should act (March & 
Heath, 1994). 
2.5.3. Bias & heuristics 
Biases and heuristics often operate within system 1 as they are cognitive shortcuts and 
patterns of thinking. It is important to note that they can also influence System 2 thinking as 
it is still susceptible to biases and can rely on heuristics to simplify complex decision-making 
tasks. Building upon attention determines the firm focus and top management’s 
characteristics including their biases determine attention, it is important to understand 
what type of biases entrepreneurs. Thomas (2018) synthesizes decades of research on 
cognitive bias in research and illustrates the key determinants for biases to occur: Personal 
factors, environment & organization. Thomas (2018) additionally also points out that there 
are generally three categories where cognitive biases affect entrepreneurs: Venture 
formation, performance, and decision making. As this paper is focusing on the decision-
making process, the takeaways will also be focused on cognitive bias related to decision-
making. 
Thomas 2018 describes multiple biases that affect decision-makers and entrepreneurs with 
overoptimism and overconfidence being the two most prevalent and researched. 
Overoptimism concerns viewing a risky environment too optimistically, and overconfidence 
concerns overestimating one’s ability to make correct predictions (Busenitz and Barney 
1997; Thomas 2018). Overoptimism affects behavior and mood while overconfidence 
affects thinking. One way that overconfidence can be noticed in entrepreneurs is by how 
==PAGE:24==
23 
 
they may treat their assumptions as facts at least with a higher probability than what is 
actually true. While there are some obvious negative effects of these biases such as 
entrepreneurs being more likely to make the decision to expand, despite negative 
marketplace feedback (McCarthy, 1993; Thomas 2018) overestimating potential profit 
(Camerer and Lovallo 1999; Thomas 2018), increase in the risk of non-survival 
(Gudmundsson and Lechner 2013; Thomas 2018) they also help entrepreneurs find more 
opportunities and develop more pioneering solutions (Thomas, 2018).  
Another bias that entrepreneurs can suffer from is the illusion of control, which leads 
entrepreneurs to only consider a limited number of alternatives in their decision-making 
process (Carr and Blettner, 2010; Thomas 2018) This leads to less comprehensive decisions 
that lead to lower decision-making quality. Furthermore, they found that additional 
contextual factors such as time stress and prior experience magnify these negative effects 
(Carr and Blettner, 2010; Thomas, 2018). The additional context factors such as time and 
stress might be more potent in early-stage startups where they are under a lot of time 
pressure from employees and investors to keep the business running. 
 
Another bias that might affect entrepreneurs’ decision-making is the escalation of 
commitment bias. While, as Thomas (2018) points out, it’s a not well-researched topic there 
is research that indicates that entrepreneurs are more likely to justify their past decisions 
and make subsequent biased decisions (Staw, 1976; Thomas, 2018).  
Further, entrepreneurs are also affected by the law of small numbers. This means that 
Entrepreneurs have a larger tendency to use limited information and non-sample data to 
generalize findings and draw firm conclusions and thereby affecting their decision-making 
(Tversky and Kahneman 1971; Thomas, 2018). This finding however could also just be a 
factor of the limited amount of information available to entrepreneurship (Busenitz and 
Barney 1997; Thomas, 2018) 
  
2.5.3. Decision-making techniques 
Other than heuristics and the cognitive decision-making process, there are other decision-
making tools that can be leveraged. One of them is how the company determines its goals. 
These perspectives are characterized as effectuation and causation. Effectuation has a 
resource perspective and takes the means at hand as the starting point from which different 
==PAGE:25==
24 
 
possible effects can be achieved. Causation, on the other hand, is goal-orientated, where 
the desired effect is then achieved through the right selection of means (Futterer et al. 
2018). In other words, does the company plan ahead based on the goal they want to 
achieve, or does the company see what resources it has available and then create the plans.  
There are two ways this happens: Analogical reasoning and conceptual combination 
(Martins et al. 2015).  Analogical reasoning refers to the use of the knowledge contained in 
the schema about one domain (termed ‘source’) to interpret information in another domain 
(termed ‘target’). More simply, you use existing knowledge and see if it can be transferred 
directly to another knowledge domain. Conceptual combination on the other hand is when 
you combine a target concept with a modifier concept to create a new concept It preserves 
core similarities with the target concept, but it generates essentially a new concept through 
the integration of attributes derived from the modifier concept. In the case of business 
model innovation, the conceptual combination allows managers to be more flexible with 
comparing and matching different dimensions of the business model to better suit their 
target concept (Martins et al. 2015) 
 
2.6. Theoretical Framework:  
Combining the cognitive view of the business model with the attentional view, this paper 
sees the strategy and output of the firm as a result of the attentional resource allocation, 
while attention is a result of the mental processes in how the firm processes information. 
Top management plays a key role in shaping the attentional focus of the company, but 
managers themselves, like any other, are limited in their rationality. They are affected by 
their own biases, take mental shortcuts, and are unable to have all the necessary 
information and alternatives in their head. When a company undergoes business model 
innovation, top management’s experience and the uncertainty in which the firm operates 
are the biggest determiners of its success or failure. What top management determines to 
focus on is done through their search, both their own search, but also through their 
subordinates and experiments where they fail, succeed and learn from research. Combining 
the search with the inherent cognitive human limitations and biases, the top management 
members’ own experience, and success traps, each member of the top management team 
most likely has their own perspective. These varying views end up creating one dominant 
==PAGE:26==
25 
 
attentional perspective. Knowing this, the question is then, how does the top management 
team create a shared top-management attentional perspective during business model 
innovation?

3.2.1 Case study pitfalls: 
There are two common pitfalls that arise when doing a case study: making the findings 
generalize and producing a credible case study (Yin, Robert, 2014). To make a case study 
credible, there are three general areas that need to be satisfactory: Validity, Reliability, and 
trustworthiness. Maybe find a source to define the three criteria. To build trustworthiness, 
the key is to “convince readers that you actually did the fieldwork reported in your case 
study – that is, being “really and fully present – physically, cognitively and emotionally – In 
the scenes of action under study” (Yin, 2014, p.4).  
Ways to be better at trustworthiness: engage in thick description, meaning highly 
descriptive accounts of events and enable the reader the better appreciate and gain a 
deeper understanding of the social conditions being studied. Another way is to develop a 
tool, a case study protocol containing the framework and decisions for what to record and 
what not to record (Yin 2014).  
To strengthen the reliability of the case study it’s important to make the data collection as 
transparent as possible (Yin, 2014). The case study protocol therefore also helps with 
building reliability as it helps promote transparency in the data collection (Yin, 2014). In 
addition, striving towards triangulating findings is important to boost reliability. 
Triangulation is when “the evidence from several different sources converges on the same 
finding, increasing the reliability of the finding” (Yin, 2014 p. 5). To achieve triangulation, 
data has been collected through different data sources such as in-depth interviews, archival

3.3 Coding and Analysis 
3.3.1 Coding the Interviews 
The data collection and analysis stages in this study were done at the same time. To ensure 
that the uncovered and developing themes ground in the original data and theory, the 
researcher reread the previous stages of the coding process before doing further analysis.  
Coding interviews are an important part of the qualitative research process, as it allows 
researchers to identify themes and patterns in the data. By coding the data, researchers can 
identify similarities and differences in the responses of participants, which can be used to 
develop a deeper understanding of the research topic. Furthermore, coding interviews allow 
for a more detailed analysis of the data. By breaking down data into smaller parts, 
researchers can better identify specific ideas, concepts, and themes that otherwise might 
have been overlooked in a more general analysis (Miles, Huberman, & Saldana, 2013). 
Coding can be understood as working with materials in order to generate concepts, identify 
relevant fragments of the material, and allocate those into categories. Afterward, the 
material can be interpreted to learn what is understood and explain what the coding has 
==PAGE:33==
32 
 
revealed about the material, the links among the fragments, and how they tie into the 
contextual conditions (Flick, 2014) 
To follow this and have the best possibility for coding, all the interviews done has been 
recorded. All interviews were then transcribed afterward whereas some were auto-
transcribed and some were not due to technical difficulties. Some grammatical errors, 
stuttering, and repetitions have been removed from the transcription for readability. 
Additionally, some spoken language has been rewritten so that it became clear in the 
transcript to avoid confusion for readers. In general, the transcription follows rather closely 
the spoken language for precision and to not reduce meaning. 
The coding process started with the researcher highlighting relevant paragraphs and 
phrases to gain a preliminary understanding of the interview data and help further research 
by narrowing down the potential areas of interest in the interviews. The initial codes were 
built on the IDER framework proposed by Smulders (2014) and used by other researchers 
such as Li-Ying et al. (2023). The coding aims to help understand what part of the business 
model process the decisions were taken in. The selected codes were: Ideation, Designing, 
Engineering, and Realization. The initialization phase consists of brainstorming, initial 
market research, assessment of technologies, and competitor analysis. In the designing 
phase, the focus is on developing a clear value proposition for customers as well as 
designing the actual product.  In the engineering phase, the focus is on engineering and 
creating the embodiment of the product. This also includes creating the necessary internal 
business processes and external connections with partners and suppliers if needed to build 
the product. In the fourth stage, realization, the firm increases existing processes and starts 
to focus on monetizing the product. The selection of code was based on what stage of 
business model innovation was affected by the interviewee’s response. The second level of 
codes was primarily based on the previously established theoretical framework consisting of 
attention, cognition, learning, and decision. Attention encompasses all attentional and focus 
points and how attention is allocated. Cognition relates to the human limitations of 
cognitive powers such as systems 1 & 2, limited rationality, and heuristics. Learning relates 
to how top management receives information to build their decisions on.  The decision 
coding relates to decision techniques pointed out in the literature review and other aspects 
related to how they prioritize their actions. Further, based on the initial highlighting session

the researcher has added an additional g into micro and macro decisions based on the CEO’s 
own categorization; Micro decisions are the everyday decisions and adjustments to the

Given the amount of existing theory on business model innovation and decisions but the

4.2.2 How did the decision to move change customers happen? 
As described earlier, the CEO was already predominantly focusing on larger clients in Europe

Starting with looking at how the CEO understands the situation, he focuses on thinking and

from a revenue-first approach and tried to predict which client would actually be giving 
them the revenue. Trying to make decisions based on prediction aligns very well with how 
they made decisions in general and for the major pivot. This leads to the question of how he 
determines which clients would be giving them revenue. The way he did that was by looking 
at which clients he thought would gain the most from using their product and then 
comparing.

The quote illustrates two important things. Firstly, he tried to tie the product usage to how

4.2.3 Macro and micro decisions 
An interesting perspective on the decisions they take was brought up by the CEO himself

Here the CEO mentions that the macro decision is the overall decision about what the

4.3 Cognition 
This section of the result is focused on highlighting the different processes that affect and 
can help explain how top management made decisions. The section will start by looking into 
the roles, responsibilities, and authority within the top management team. Then it will look 
at how they determine if a decision has been good or bad and how that affects their further 
decision-making process.  
Another aspect of the decision-making process and what might affect the outcomes of the 
decisions is the official roles that each of the top managers has in the company. In the case 
of the company, both the CEO and the COO are part of the top management, but there is a 
difference in authority. This difference in authority primarily stems from the fact the CEO is 
also the founder of the company while the COO is not a part of the founding team. This 
means that not only is there a difference in terms of CEO vs. COO, but there is also a 
difference in the equity each of them has which can affect the decision-making hierarchy. 
This decision hierarchy is illustrated by the COO when asked about how he says his opinions 
to the CEO: “And then we have a discussion but if it turns out that he still thinks other when 
he heard my opinion, then we have to go that way.” (Interview 2). In the quote, the COO 
points out that even if they have a discussion where they add their own opinions on the 
specific target, then ultimately the CEO has the final say on the matter. So for the COO, a lot 
of the decision-making interactions are about making the CEO understand his reasoning. 
This focus on making his reasoning clear can be seen when the COO talks about how he 
provides his input to the CEO: 
But I am trying to say it in a quite structured way so he gets my input in a quite 
concrete way that it's easy to understand: why I'm pushing for something. What is 
the reason I'm thinking what I'm thinking (Interview 2).

From the quote, it can be seen how much importance the COO puts on providing 
information regarding his reasoning. This is done not only by the arguments he provides but 
also by the way he does it to make sure that it’s understandable for the CEO. Interestingly 
when asked the same question, the CEO did not mention anything regarding what happens 
in case of disagreements. This indicates that the CEO might not have the same thoughts 
about the disagreements which might stem from the inherent difference in the power

It has been found that the attentional perspective of the top management determined, to

too different on overlapping issues it can create clashes in their respective prior

they received from relevant stakeholders that related to their roles. This was based on the

some degree replace the learnings they previously had.

uncertainty in the environment where the company exists, top management tried to predict

5.1. Where do the results fit with existing literature 
The discussion of this thesis will start by introducing how the CEO and COO of the company 
learned and received information for their decisions. This section will start by focusing on 
how they were the literature fits within the cognitive theory. Afterward, it will be 
highlighted how their decision-making process fits first with causation and effectuation and

5.1.1 Where do the results fit with biases 
Many of the biases mentioned in the theoretical framework can be argued to also be 
present in the decision-making amongst top management in the company. Specifically, 
when examining the decision to prioritize larger clients over smaller ones, it becomes 
apparent that the COO relies heavily on a limited subset of feedback from potential clients. 
An interesting aspect of the decision is how quickly the COO makes this decision based on 
feedback from just a few potential clients. This tendency to make decisions based on a small 
subset of data aligns well with what Tversky and Kahneman (1971; Thomas, 2018) calls the 
"law of small numbers," where decisions are made from limited information and non-
sample data to generalize findings and draw firm conclusions from. This tendency of 
adhering to the bias of the law of small numbers might also help explain why the top 
management spend so little time searching in their decision-making process. Both the CEO 
and COO said that they normally spend less than one day searching for information before 
making a decision. Here the law of small numbers might give an indication as to why they 
==PAGE:73==
72 
 
feel like this amount of data is enough to make a decision. It might however also just be a 
factor of the limited amount of information available when engaging in entrepreneurship 
(Busenitz and Barney 1997; Thomas, 2018). On the other hand, while the COO thinks that 
they do have a culture of ‘pushing’ for things to be done quickly, he also said as highlighted 
in section 4.2.1, that he sometimes feels like they maybe should spend more time searching 
before making a decision.  
This finding also resonates with the research by Li-Ying et al. (2023), who found that success 
traps are one of the factors that affect decision-making in entrepreneurship. Something that 
deviates slightly from how Li-Ying et al. (2023) sees the decision process is that while 
success traps played a big part in the decision, another key reason why they decided to 
change client focus was based on the clients’ predicted value to the business. This can be 
demonstrated by looking at section 5.1.2 where the COO chooses to focus on the larger 
clients despite also seeing success with smaller clients in the Nordics. They took an active 
choice between two terrains with success and chose to follow the one where they predicted 
the largest revenue potential. Interestingly, top management decided to switch target 
customers, based on the feedback from the market and the ‘rational’ weighing of the 
customers’ value to the company, despite that they had a set target of 100 clients live by a 
specific date. This happened despite them knowing the sales cycle is longer for larger 
clients.   
 Furthermore, as pointed out in section 5.3.2 where the COO talks about the reasons for 
deciding to focus on large clients and that it was based on the positive feedback they got 
and the predicted value, but when they didn’t have good results for a week or two, he 
downplayed they result as just a bad week. This downplaying of the bad feedback after the 
decision has been taken is indicative of the escalation of commitment bias where people try 
to justify their past decisions and make subsequent biased decisions (Martins et al. 2018). 
Considering that overconfidence is so prevalent amongst entrepreneurs, it can be 
considered risky to choose where to focus as Camerer and Lovallo (1999; Thomas, 2018) has 
linked overconfidence to overestimating potential profit. This is therefore an inherent risk 
for the company to make decisions based on the wrong grounds as top management is 
comparing a small client with low potential revenue and an overestimation of the potential 
profit of the big clients.

5.1.2. Causation and Effectuation 
Looking at the way the top management makes decisions, a common theme is that the top 
management starts with a target they are trying to achieve and then use this as the starting 
point upon which they center their decision around. This can be seen both in the meeting 
notes where the CEO uses a specific date as a go-live date and then goes back from there to 
create the next steps and in how they talk around making decisions related to their target of 
100 live clients. This relates quite well to one of the two techniques pointed out by Futterer 
et al. (2018) as causation as the management starts from a goal orientation and then selects 
the means to achieve that goal.  
Another part of their decision that aligns with causation can be seen in how they try to 
predict the benefit they gain from selling to one customer compared to another as 
causation is a step-by-step approach to deal with uncertainty by using deliberate planning 
activities focused on predicting outcomes. These activities namely include calculating 
expected returns (Futterer et al. 2018) which can be argued is what predicting the worth of 
each customer is. Further, the behavior in the decision meeting where top management 
planned out all the actions necessary to achieve the agreed target is indicative of planning, 
which is another characteristic of causation. As pointed out by Flutterer et. al (2018) 
causation is best used when uncertainty is low while effectuation provides better company 
performance when uncertainty is low. The question is if it is still causation when the targets 
are not set directly by the top management, but instead placed upon them like the case 
with the 100 targets directly tied to getting funding.  
5.1.3 Analogical reasoning & conceptual combination: 
COO tries to use his own experiences, which can be assumed to be somewhat similar to 
one’s existing knowledge, as the first way of making a decision and what he means. This can 
be seen in how he thinks about moving from small clients to focusing on big clients. His 
existing knowledge almost entirely consists of ‘slow-moving ships’ where he is not sure if 
they are an attractive customer, primarily because of the slow-selling cycle. When he was 
then presented with the decision on whether to focus on big clients in Asia or not, he 
initially made use of analogical reasoning and judges the attractiveness of the big clients in 
Asia by his previous knowledge, namely the feedback from big clients in the Nordics. 
==PAGE:75==
74 
 
Interestingly, the CEO also makes use of analogical reasoning to come up with a different 
decision compared to the COO. Like the COO, the CEO also makes use of his previous 
knowledge to base the decision, but the CEO is pushing for a different decision than the 
COO. This indicates that there while they are both engaging in the same roles, being sales, 
and getting feedback from clients to base their decision on, there is something that is 
different. This difference could therefore be argued to lie in their existing knowledge.  
An interesting point if that is the case, why do they have a difference in existing knowledge? 
As both the CEO and COO pointed out, they had daily feedback meetings discussing what 
feedback they received. Additionally, they had also pointed out that they had additional 
consultants providing them with feedback. So, they were both aware of their respective 
learnings and had additional learnings that supported a specific view, yet the COO was not 
convinced until he had the learnings for himself. This implies that the COO would never be 
truly convinced before he had the same learning opportunity as the CEO.

5.2.1 Where do the findings enrich the theoretical framework: 
In the case of the company, the COO and CEO are gaining different learnings while engaging 
in similar activities in different "terrains", borrowing the term from Li et al. (2012), with the 
COO operating in the Nordics and the CEO operating in Southeast Asia. As the result 
highlighted, this led to the top management having different learnings and assumptions 
around how big clients acted and their potential. Furthering this discrepancy in learning is 
that the COO had success with smaller clients in the Nordics while the CEO had no success 
with small or medium clients in Southeast Asia.  
The result section also pointed out that the top management engaged in multiple feedback 
mechanisms like frequent meetings and used additional consultants to provide feedback 
from potential clients through their sales meetings. Even though they had all these potential 
alignment situations, the alignment in the top management team did not happen before the 
==PAGE:76==

COO experienced the learnings directly from the source by engaging in sales talks with

by one top management member, even if they come from another member of the top 
management team. One way this could look at is that this divergence in perception arose 
because each top management member focused their attention on different aspects and 
experienced different truths based on their respective search areas. While this might hold 
true in one sense in that they learned different things from their respective search areas, it 
does not explain why it was so hard to convince the COO to switch client focus.  
Another perspective on the divergence in perception could be to look at how the top 
management learns from their findings and how those learnings are processed. As 
highlighted by Li-Ying et al. (2023)'s paper, experimental and experiential learning is an 
important aspect to consider in the decision-making process during business model 
innovation. Experimental learning involves trial-and-error experiments, where individuals 
and teams try out new practices, identify inconsistencies from established mental rules, and 
adjust their actions accordingly. Experiential learning involves individuals and teams 
consciously questioning and challenging their fundamental goals, assumptions, and meaning 
based on the insights gained through their experiences. 
Looking at how the COO and CEO learned, it could be argued that they are both engaging in 
experimental learning in their own terrains. They tried out different sales pitches and got 
feedback from potential clients, adjusted their sales pitch, and refocused on the clients 
providing them with positive feedback. This experimental learning will, as indicated by Li-
Ying et al. (2023) become experiences, which in turn provide feedback to guide new 
experimentation. The persistent view on this decision could also be attributed to 
confirmation bias, which is the tendency of individuals to favor information that aligns with 
their existing beliefs or values. Once these beliefs are affirmed, they become resistant to 
change or dislodging. This could help explain that the success traps the COO has followed 
through his experimentation has lodged his view on big clients being slow to a degree that it 
it’s very hard to change.  
Building upon the divergence in perception related to the terrain of search, this indicates 
that experiential learning does not necessarily occur simultaneously for all top management 
members. Instead, each individual must personally experience and undergo the learning 
==PAGE:77==

process. This is exemplified by the COO initially not fully believing that the CEO was correct 
and that his own assumptions about big clients being slow were correct. The COO had to 
experience it himself by attending meetings with the big clients, engaging in conversations

convinced the CEO also was correct. 
This could indicate that the ‘terrain’ or environment where the search, and thereby learning, 
occurs and how it compares to previous experiences can significantly influence the 
accumulation of learnings and how they are perceived and used. The results can therefore 
be argued to provide new insight into the relationship between search, learning, and the 
decision-making process of top management. 
This relationship suggests that there is a need for alignment and shared understanding

5.2 Limitations: 
After discussing theories and concepts in this thesis, it is essential to discuss the limitations 
relating to methodological choices, the chosen data, and the findings of the results will be 
presented: 
5.2.1. Limitations on methodology: 
A key limitation relating to the generalizability of the findings is due to the study being a 
single case study. The unique characteristics, circumstances, and contextual factors of the 
case may restrict the generalizability of the results to other settings. The findings and 
conclusions derived from this study may therefore not be applicable to other contexts or 
populations. To address this limitation, future research could incorporate multiple case 
studies or employ a comparative case study design. By including multiple cases, researchers 
can identify commonalities and differences, thereby enhancing the external validity and 
generalizability of the findings. 
Other limitations relate to the assumptions being made in relation when coding the data. 
One of the assumptions relates to the coding of the attentional perspective of the 
employees and top management in the Company. Here it was assumed that each task has 
the same amount of importance in determining the attentional perspective focus. This 
might however not be true as some tasks most likely would occupy more of the employees’ 
focus and time during the week. The findings should still be considered valid as they were 
said by the employees themselves without any minimum or maximum criteria in place 
which means they only said tasks that occupied some focus. This leads to another 
assumption and a slight limitation which is that it was up to each employee to determine 
==PAGE:83==
82 
 
what tasks are ‘worthy’ of being mentioned. As it was up to each employee to do this, there 
might be some inconsistencies in how they determined each task from week to week, yet 
they themselves are still the best indicator of what they actually did, so the results are still 
considered valid. 
Another limitation in the methodology that affects the interpretation of the results is the 
results related to the quantification of the coding of the theoretical framework and the ÍDER 
framework. More specifically, when looking for overlaps between the theoretical framework 
and IDER framework I assume that where they are most mentioned is where they are 
almost making decisions. The limitation primarily lies in that it is not certain that more 
mentions equal more importance. Each coding has been given the same inherent value even 
though, as was the case for the coding related to measuring the company’s attentional 
perspective, some tasks most likely would occupy more of the top management’s cognitive 
attention than what the same value might be able to provide. This limitation of detail is 
however also considered not invalidating as the results are still proving a pattern so the few 
inconsistencies that might occur would not affect the overall findings.  
The limitation is that the coding was done without any third party approving the coding to 
ensure that they are consistent. This limitation concerns primarily the use of the same 
coding framework, IDER, and the use of two different types of text; One is the interviews 
while the other is tasks.  Yet as they have been coding using the same underlying framework 
and the researcher has provided coding clarifications, the deviation is considered 
nonconsequential. To increase the reliability of the findings, the interviews have been 
recorded and transcribed. Further, a list of codes can be seen in Appendix 3 and a 
description of when each code has been used has been provided which supports the 
reliability of data as any other scholar should be able to demonstrate similar findings. 
5.2.2. Limitations relating to chosen data 
Another limitation is that most of the data used is based on interviews held with top 
management. While the researcher tried to place those meetings shortly after decisions 
were taken, it was not possible to do so immediately after. This was due to, amongst other 
reasons, cancellations from the top management members. The meetings were however 
held within 1-2 weeks after the decision has been made public with the regular employees. 
As the data was collected post the actual decisions, there is the risk of getting what people 
==PAGE:84==
83 
 
think they did instead of what they were actually doing. There are also limitations with 
people having to recollect and think back upon what made them do x and y where they 
often don’t create a fully accurate picture. In addition, as March points out, people tend to 
think that they themselves make rational decisions and want to appear as if they do so, 
even if they don’t make rational decisions (March & Heath, 1994). This in itself might have 
created a distorted view of the actual decision process when interviewing the top 
management retrospectively. 
In relation to that, there is also an additional limitation with the lack of actual decision 
meetings attended. The reason for the lack of decision meetings attended was the 
cancellations of one decision meeting and the fact that they were in Southeast Asia. This 
limitation especially has an effect when trying to describe decision-making, in which theory 
such as heuristics and system 1 & system 2 plays a significant role and the cognitive aspects 
of decision-making are hard to identify. Following March and the logic of bounded 
rationality as well, humans don’t remember how they are actually making decisions and are 
unable to know the exact mental shortcuts taken (March & Heath, 1994). As there was an 
actual meeting observed, and the findings were consistent with the findings from the 
interviews, the results from the interviews are still considered relevant and valid for the 
purpose of this paper. A limitation related to the meeting notes is that they were not 
transcribed and were only written down in note form, with the researcher having to write 
down what he considered to be of importance. This happened due to an unforeseen 
meeting change where a separate meeting morphed into the scheduled prioritization 
meeting. This notetaking most likely led to some loss of information, but as the essence has 
been written down and the findings were consistent with the other data sources, the 
findings are considered valid. Another limitation related to the data is that there haven’t 
been held interviews with employees but they were still measured for their attentional 
focus. This might limit the findings by not being able to triangulate them as the researcher 
has done with the top management. As the employee findings are only a very small part of 
the findings and not part of the scope of the study, this limitation is considered to be 
inconsequential.  
Another limitation is that most of the findings that relate to the macro decisions are from 
outside the observation time and are therefore not a part of the attentional observations 
==PAGE:85==
84 
 
which limits the findings relating to those. Following that, the researcher was quite focused 
on macro decisions but has only either observed or taken notes of micro decisions. This was 
due to no macro decision being taken whilst the company was under observation and the 
researcher was therefore unable to observe it. Further, macro and micro decisions were not 
originally a part of the thoughts behind the study, but due to the exploratory nature of the 
study, they have been included in the findings by reason of their perceived importance in 
the decision-making pattern. 
As shown in the result section, the difference in authority might have affected why they 
ended up choosing big clients in Southeast Asia is that it was the CEO who pushed for that. 
As the COO said in the results, his role is sometimes to also just follow along with what the 
CEO wants, because the CEO has the final say. Additionally, the power dynamic might also 
be affected by the fact that the CEO is also the founder, whilst the COO is ‘just’ an early 
employee. It is however beyond the scope of the study to address the question of how the 
power dynamic affects the decision-making. 
The last limitation is that the researcher has been employed in the company for 
approximately 2.5 years and he is therefore at risk of subconsciously infusing some of his 
own knowledge into his interpretations of the results without the results demanding that 
interpretation. It is hard to say that none of the researcher’s prior experiences has affected 
the findings but to reduce researcher bias, the researcher has asked the interviewees if the 
findings are an accurate representation of their experience. Additionally, triangulation 
methods have been incorporated through multiple data sources and perspectives which 
provide a more comprehensive and balanced understanding of the case. 
5.3. Implications for Theory and Practice 
The findings of this thesis include some practical recommendations for theory and 
practitioners. This research’s findings contribute to three streams of literature on the topics 
of the decision-making process, search and attention, and phases of business model 
innovation and decision patterns. Firstly, this thesis extends research on the nuances of the 
decision process in early-stage startups by arguing that top management members can have 
different learnings and experiences which in turn affects the decision-making process. 
Secondly, it argues that different decisions types have different decision-making patterns 
==PAGE:86==
85 
 
during business model innovation. Thirdly, it indicates that there might be a correlation 
between what phase of business model innovation the company is engaging in and which 
type of decision and learning they engage in. 
Consequently, this thesis provides beneficial insights for practitioners. It is however difficult 
to give concrete and actual recommendations as this thesis' merely' observed the decision-
making of a single company and hasn’t tried anything in real life. Instead, it has shed light on 
the importance of having awareness about how important it is to get the same learnings to 
achieve alignment. Different managers being aware of how and what they’re learning might 
affect their actions or directions to prioritize. This might make them more open to 
interacting with the same stakeholders or having meetings together while also fostering 
self-awareness about their own decision-making style. They can reflect on their tendencies, 
biases, and preferences, which can enhance their ability to make more informed and 
effective decisions. This might be especially true when there is an overlap in roles and 
responsibilities among the managers. 
Secondly, the findings have illustrated that there are different types of decisions. This 
awareness enables top management in startups engaging in business model innovation to 
know about the different types of decisions that they might have to be involved in and how 
they have different decision patterns. Knowing this, managers might better understand the 
context in which decisions are made which allows them to consider the specific constraints, 
uncertainties, and trade-offs of their decisions. 
Thirdly, it has been analyzed that the phase of business model innovation they engage in 
might affect their subsequent actions through learning and prioritization. This might make 
managers aware of not only the importance of knowing what phase of business model 
innovation they are engaging in but also the importance of knowing how that inadvertently 
might affect their future decisions. By understanding the impact of their actions on 
subsequent decisions, managers can focus their attention on tasks that align with the 
organization's targets and create the greatest value. 
5.4. Further research 
This research highlights some nuances in current research that can be important for future 
research. The exploratory nature of this thesis provides several possibilities for future 
==PAGE:87==
86 
 
investigation and research. Firstly, this thesis has taken a processual look at the decision-
making of an early-stage startup engaging in business model innovation and found that 
there are different levels to decisions that the startup engages in. The findings provided an 
indication that it’s different with macro decisions being more conscious and taking longer 
time than micro decisions. It also indicated the macro decision search is more proactive 
could also involve employees. This thesis did however unfortunately not observe both types 
of decisions being taken. Consequently, potential avenue for future research could be to 
observe if there is a different pattern in decision-making depending on the type of decision 
top management engages, with it being micro or macro in this thesis. Moreover, it could be 
interesting to research more in-depth the employee’s role in search and learning and how 
that affects the top management’s learning. 
Another avenue for future research could be to look more deeply into the relationship 
between the business model innovation phases the company engages in and how that 
affects its decision-making. This thesis found a correlation between which phases the 
company and top management primarily engaged in and where they learned and made 
decisions. The company is primarily engaging in tasks related to the realization phase and 
bases most of its learnings and decisions on the sales meeting they get. Future research 
could therefore look into if this decision-making pattern and see if it differs for an early-
stage startup engaging in other phases of business model innovation. This could enable 
more generalizability of the findings and provide a better view on if learning amongst top 
management is different from phase to phase. 
Thirdly there could be done more research more exactly how top management come to an 
agreement. This thesis ended up focusing a lot on the learning and searching aspects of top 
management decision-making due to the empirical data collected which was affected by the 
top management working remotely most of the time, limiting the observation options of the 
research. While it did shed some light on the actual decision-making process after the 
search with top management engaging in prediction and target-based prioritization, the 
thesis was unable to clearly identify how the decision happened at the moment. This could 
potentially be researched by having more interviews focused specifically on the decision 
shortly after a decision was taken and more in-depth observations of the actual decision 
situation.  
==PAGE:88==
87 
 
Fourthly, the newly proposed frameworks of the findings could be analyzed in future 
research with further qualitative work that could confirm or refute the proposed framework 
for how top management creates an attentional perspective. Additionally, since the 
framework has only been researched in the context of the company, this framework could 
be better generalized by observing more startups and doing cross-comparison.  
6. Conclusion 
This thesis aimed to investigate how “top management creates an attentional perspective 
during business model innovation in early-stage startups”. To achieve this, a longitudinal 
case study was conducted, utilizing a mixed methods approach that involved qualitative 
interviews and quantitative analysis of overlapping themes and task-based attentional 
perspective calculations. The findings showed that the creation of an attentional perspective 
is a continuous process involving each top management member searching within their own 
terrain, combining learnings with previous experiences and biases, and ultimately making 
decisions collectively to create a new attentional perspective. The findings enrich the 
current research by not only illustrating that each top management member has their own 
learning process affecting top management alignment but also that the decision-making 
process is affected by the phase of business model innovation that the company is in and 
what type of decision that needs to be taken. With this understanding, practitioners should 
be better able to recognize how their actions influence their subsequent decisions and 
thereby make better and more informed decisions.
