Abstract
Antibiotic resistance poses a major global health challenge, caused by the overuse of an­
tibiotics. As conventional treatments become less effective, interest has grown in other
treatment methods such as nanoparticles. These systems enhance biofilm penetration
and minimize systemic toxicity, with pH­responsive nanoparticles enabling targeted, on­
demand release of antibiotics. This study explored the formulation and characterization of
pH­responsive nanoparticles for targeted drug delivery using microfluidics. The focus was
particularly on the encapsulation of the polar and hydrophilic antibiotic ciprofloxacin HCl
in Eudragit EPO­based nanoparticles. Four different microfluidic strategies were utilized
and evaluated by varying the difference in nanoparticle size and encapsulation efficiency.
The One­Factor­at­a­Time approach was used followed by statistical ANOVA analysis,
to investigate the influence of the individual parameters; flow rates, polymer concentra­
tion, and drug concentration, on nanoparticle size, polydispersity index, encapsulation
efficiency, and zeta potential. Acetone was the preferred solvent, producing nanoparti­
cles with acceptable sizes below 150 nm and PDI below 0.2, although the encapsula­
tion efficiency remained below 25%, for all four methods. Attempts to improve encap­
sulation through polymer and drug concentration optimization or advanced chip designs
showed limited success. Substitution with the reference polymer PLGA and antibiotic
tobramycin showed similar results. The testing of alternative APIs, paracetamol, and in­
domethacin, showed an encapsulation efficiency of 42 to 64%, demonstrating that the high
aqueous solubility and ionic character of ciprofloxacin HCl, and poor compatibility with Eu­
dragit EPO hindered encapsulation. The pH­sensitive release behavior was confirmed for
paracetamol­loaded nanoparticles, supporting Eudragit EPO and microfluidic potential for
site­specific delivery in acidic microenvironments such as biofilms. This work highlights
the importance of drug–polymer compatibility in microfluidic nanoparticle systems and
suggests that future strategies may benefit from polymer modifications or alternative drug
candidates.

Introduction
Today, there is an emergence and spread of bacterial resistance which represents one
of the most pressing global health challenges. Antibiotics was once highly effective treat­
ments for bacterial infections, but are increasingly losing their efficacy due to misuse and
incomplete treatment. This has caused natural process of resistance development, re­
sulting in the selection of multidrug­resistant bacterial strains that are difficult to treat with
standard therapies. This has lead to infections that were once easily curable now require
prolonged treatment, carry a higher risk of complications, and place a growing burden on
healthcare systems.
An example hereof is chronic rhinosinusitis (CRS). CRS is a persistent inflammatory con­
dition of the paranasal sinuses and nasal passages, as seen in Figure 1.1. It typically
lasts 12 weeks despite medical treatment and affects approximately 8.7 to 13% of the
global population [1, 2].
Figure 1.1: Schematic illustration of sinus regions before and after inflammation. Inflammatory
processes cause swelling and blockage of the sinuses, which may lead to increased mucus pro­
duction, supporting biofilm formation
One of the key factors in the persistence of CRS is the formation of bacterial biofilms, from
bacteria as S. aureus and P. aeruginosa. The biofilm protects pathogens from antibiotics
and host defenses [3, 4, 5]. Current standard treatment often relies on administered oral
antibiotics, which are limited by several pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic barriers,
undergoing first­pass metabolism. As a result of this, antibiotics are often unable to reach
therapeutic concentrations at the infection site within the sinuses [6]. Furthermore, the
need for high systemic doses to overcome this barrier can result in off­target toxicities.
Due to the limitation of conventional treatments, there is growing interest in localized con­
trolled drug delivery systems such as nanoparticles. These approaches aim to improve
biofilm penetration and reduce systemic toxicity [7, 8]. Some development methods in­
clude solvent­emulsion evaporation, nanoprecipitation, and double emulsion (W/O/W),
while advanced techniques like microfluidics offer precise control over particle size with im­
proved encapsulation efficiency. These methods support the development of pH­responsive
nanoparticles (NPs), working as drug delivery carriers for on­demand release of active
pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) such as ciprofloxacin, tobramycin, and colistin, which
are effective against bacterial biofilms [9, 10].

1.1
Bacterial biofilm
Bacterial biofilms are complex microbial communities in which microorganisms can ad­
here to various tissue surfaces in the body. When microorganisms are transported by ei­
ther convection, brownian motion, or sedimentation, to attach to mucosal epithelial tissues,
such as the sinus [11]. Biofilm formation begins when planktonic bacteria adhere to sur­
faces through weak reversible interactions like van der Waals forces, electrostatic forces,
and hydrophobic interactions. Properties of the surface such as roughness, charge, and
hydrophobicity play a key role in promoting or hindering attachment [12]. In the irreversible
adhesion phase, bacterial surface molecules establish stronger interactions such as hy­
drophobic, hydrogen, covalent, ionic and dipole–dipole bonds, stabilizing cell attachment
and interconnections [13]. In the third stage of biofilm development, bacteria produce
extracellular polymeric substances (EPS), which support adhesion, cohesion, and protec­
tion, as seen from Figure 1.2 [14]. During the maturation stage, bacteria multiply within the
EPS matrix, forming structured, three­dimensional colonies with altered gene expression
that supports further matrix production. It contains water channels to distribute nutrients
and remove waste. Dispersal marks the final stage, where cells leave the biofilm to colo­
nize new sites [14].
1.1.1
EPS and Biofilm
The EPS matrix consists of exopolysaccharides for stability; lipids, polysaccharides, and
biosurfactants for scaffolding, adhesion, and cohesion; proteins and enzymes for nutrient
uptake and resistance; extracellular DNA for structure and gene transfer; and c­di­GMP,
which regulates EPS production and biofilm attachment. Water maintains hydration, pre­
venting desiccation, as seen from Figure 1.2 [15].
Figure 1.2: Stages of biofilm development. The biofilm life cycle transitions from reversible phases
to irreversible phases. During maturation, a structured matrix of polysaccharides, proteins, en­
zymes, lipids, and extracellular DNA forms, creating nutrient gradients and acidic microenviron­
ments (e.g., pH 5.5) that support persistent cells. Inspiration taken from Biorender (Gaia Lugano)
and Crouzet et al. (2014) [16]
The components in the EPS create a dense, hydrated mesh that can trap or slow down
molecules trying to diffuse through it. The typical pore sizes in bacterial biofilms vary but

are often in the range of 5–200 nanometers (nm), depending on the species, maturity of
the biofilm and local EPS composition [17].
1.1.2
pH conditions inside biofilms
The bacterial biofilms create highly specialized microenvironments that differ significantly
from their surrounding extracellular fluids. One of the most critical features of these mi­
croenvironments is the localized pH variation, often characterized by acidic regions within
the biofilm. This acidification is primarily caused by bacterial metabolic activity, such as fer­
mentation under limited oxygen availability, which produces acidic byproducts and CO2.
The dense and heterogeneous structure of biofilms limits diffusion, allowing metabolic
byproducts to accumulate and decrease the local pH, reaching values as low as pH 5.0­
5.5, even when the surrounding environment remains neutral (pH 7.4), as seen from
Figure 1.2 [18, 19].
1.1.3
Challenges and Current treatment limitations
Treating chronic biofilm­related infections like CRS is challenging due to bacterial resis­
tance mechanisms within biofilms. The protective EPS matrix and the upregulation of mul­
tidrug efflux pumps reduce intracellular antibiotic concentrations [20, 21]. Another function
is quorum sensing (QS), which is a bacterial communication system that coordinates gene
expression based on population density. QS promotes EPS production and coordinates
resistance mechanisms such as efflux pump activation and antibiotic­degrading enzymes
[22, 23]. The bacterial biofilm also consist of persister cells, a dormant subpopulation
tolerant to antibiotics due to metabolic inactivity. These cells survive treatment and can
repopulate the biofilm, leading to relapse [24].
These things are among others, along first­pass metabolism and poor penetration of
biofilms, meaning antibiotics often fail to reach effective concentrations within biofilms.
Therefore, high concentrations of antibiotic is needed, requiring concentrations up to
1,000 times higher than those effective against planktonic cells [25, 26]. High systemic
doses are often required to achieve therapeutic levels at the infection site. This increases
the risk of off­target effects such as nephrotoxicity and ototoxicity [6, 9, 27]. To overcome
these limitations, there is a growing interest in localized and controlled drug delivery sys­
tems like nanoparticles. These aim to deliver high concentrations of antibiotics directly
to the infection site, maintaining therapeutic levels in biofilm environments, improving ef­
ficacy while minimizing systemic toxicity. These NPs respond to environmental triggers,
such as acidic pH or enzymatic activity within biofilms, enabling on­demand release. Such
advanced delivery strategies offer a promising path forward in managing persistent infec­
tions like CRS [6, 9, 27].
1.2
Nanoparticle drug delievery
NPs for drug delievery are in which nanoscale materials are used as diagnostic tools or
to deliver therapeutic agents to specific target sites in a controlled manner. These can
come in many different forms, each for different drug­release purposes.
1.2.1
Types of Nanoparticles
The different types of NPs comes with many advantages such as enhance drug solubility,
target specific tissues or cells, reducing systemic toxicity, protecting drugs, and many
more. Some of these different NPs include the ones as seen from Table 1.1.

As seen from Table 1.1 all have different advantages. In the context of this thesis, the
aim is to overcome key challenges such as mucosal penetration, localized retention, an­
tibiotic resistance, and sustained drug release, without major toxicities. This rule out poly­
meric NPs such as dendrimers, metallic NPs, and inorganic NPs, all of which influence
cytotoxicity. Lipid­based and hybrid NPs, offer excellent biocompatibility and effectively
deliver hydrophilic antibiotics in nasal applications. Thus, pH­responsive polymeric NPs,
such as Eudragit EPO and E100, are suitable for the penetration barriers and the acid mi­
croenvironment of biofilms. These polymers release their antimicrobial cargo under acidic
conditions, improving the accumulation and activity of drugs at the infection site through
controlled and pH­responsive release mechanisms [28].
1.2.2
Advantages of polymeric nanoparticles
Polymeric NPs offer a versatile and powerful platform for drug delivery. Their advan­
tages include first and foremost enhancing bioavailability by improving the solubility and
absorption of poorly soluble drugs. In addition, it enables the possibility of encapsulat­
ing hydrophobic and hydrophilic drugs [26]. The encapsulation protects sensitive drugs
from degradation, acting as a decoy that travels the API directly to the biofilm. Once at
the biofilm, the NPs penetratate biofilms because of their nanoscale size, which allows
them to diffuse into the EPS and overcome one of the major barriers to conventional
therapies. Inside the EPS, pH dissolvable NPs would result in controlled and sustained
drug release, releasing therapeutic agents over extended periods, improving efficacy and
reducing dosing frequency. This helping in reducing toxicity with localized delivery that
minimizes systemic exposure and reduces the risk of off­target effects, such as nephro­
toxicity or ototoxicity often associated with antibiotics like tobramycin [27, 29]
In order for all this, the NPs needs to be chemical stable. Chemically, the zeta potential
plays a key role in ensuring NP stability. The zeta potential is the electrostatic poten­
tial, which reflects the surface charge of the NP. It represents the electric potential at the
boundary layer (slipping plane) between the NP surface and the surrounding liquid, as
seen from Figure 1.3. It is measured in millivolts (mV) [30].
4
==PAGE:15==
Figure 1.3: Illustration of the electrical double layer surrounding a nanoparticle and the concept
of zeta potential. The surface layer contains firmly adsorbed ions, followed by the stem layer,
while the slipping plane defines the boundary between the moving and stationary fluid. The zeta­
potential is measured at this slipping plane and reflects the potential that influences colloidal sta­
bility.
An absolute zeta potential greater than ±30 mV indicate a high colloidal stability. They
exhibit strong electrostatic repulsion, which helps to maintain the stability of the dispersion
and prevents aggregation [30]. In the context of drug delivery, positively charged (+25
to +40 mV) NPs can strongly interact with negatively charged bacterial membranes and
biofilms, improving adhesion and penetration [31, 32]. Surface coatings such as polyvinyl
alcohol (PVA) and other cationic polymers and surfactants, are often applied to tune NPs
zeta potential and enhance stability and functionality. Controlling the zeta potential is
essential for optimizing NPs performance and will be touched more in Section 1.3.3 [33].
1.3
pH­Responsive Polymers and Surfactants in
Nanoparticle Drug Delivery
By understanding the pH­related environment in bacterial biofilms, this has led to the de­
velopment of pH­responsive drug delivery systems [28]. pH­responsive NPs respond to
environmental pH changes by dissolution or conformational transitions that trigger the
release of encapsulated therapeutic agents. They have been designed to exploit pH gra­
dients in biological environments, such as the acidic pH of the stomach (pH 1–3), the
mildly acidic tumor microenvironment (pH 6.5), or the bacterial biofilm (pH 5.5), which
makes them suitable for the targeted delivery of antibiotics [9, 10].
pH­responsive NPs display burst release behavior when dissolved in acidic enviroment.
This resulting in rapidly releasing their API once the pH drops below a specific threshold,
such as within a bacterial biofilm (pH 5.5). Examples of pH­sensitive polymer is Eudragit
E100 and Eudragit EPO, which dissolves below pH 5.5. Thereby, enabling high local drug
concentration at the site of infection or disease [9, 10].
1.3.1
Eudragit E100 & Eudragit EPO
Eudragit EPO and Eudragit E100, as seen in Figure 1.4. These are a cationic methacry­
late copolymer that is soluble below pH 5.5 and forms an insoluble matrix at neutral or
alkaline pH. This makes it an ideal candidate for delivery systems that protect acid­labile
drugs or enable their release in acidic compartments, such as a bacterial biofilm [34]. Eu­
5
==PAGE:16==
dragit EPO and E100 are primarily made from methacrylate­based compounds (methacry­
late, methyl methacrylate, and butyl methacrylate). Due to the dimethylamino groups in
their structure, the polymers respond to changes in pH. These groups are weak bases
that become positively charged in acidic environments. At pH values below 5.5, they take
on a charge, making the polymer water­soluble. However, in neutral or basic conditions,
they lose this charge and become water­insoluble. This smart behavior means the the
polymers stay intact in the (pH 7.4) water formulations but dissolve when they reach more
acidic environments within biofilms [35, 36].

PVA is a synthetic polymer that primarily helps with NP formation, improves mechanical

It attaches to the surface of forming particles, which reduces interfacial tension between

Chitosan is a natural cationic polysaccharide. Its primary amino groups become proto­
7
==PAGE:18==
nated in acidic to neutral pH. This gives the polymer a positive surface charge, which

One important factor in choosing a surfactant is the hydrophilic­lipophilic balance (HLB).
Surfactants are molecules with both hydrophilic and lipophilic parts. The HLB value shows
the balance between these two parts, indicating whether the surfactant is more soluble
in water or oil. It is a numerical value ranging from 0 to 20. This value helps predict how
a surfactant will behave in a mixture, especially when forming emulsions, as shown in
Appendix A.11 [47].
Surfactants with low HLB values are more lipophilic and are typically used to form water­
in­oil (W/O) emulsions. Surfactants with high HLB values are more hydrophilic and are
typically used to form oil­in­water (O/W) emulsions. PVA is not formally assigned HLB
but act like a high­HLB stabilizer. Pluronic 127 and Poloxamer 407 has an HLB around
22 and provides strong steric stabilization. PVP is also highly hydrophilic, while chitosan,
though not on the HLB scale, is a cationic polymer used for coating and mucoadhesion.
Together, these support stable O/W or W/O/W emulsions [48, 49]
1.3.4
Buffers and Buffer Capacity
Besides requiring stability for the NPs, the ability to dissolve and obtain them in the right
media is important. A big influence of this, is the buffer capacity. Buffer capacity is a
measure of how well a solution resists changes in pH when an acid or base is added.
This is a critical property for maintaning pH stability during drug delivery, dissolution, or
release. A buffer with high capacity meaning it can neutralize more acid or base without
big shifts in the pH. Buffer capacity depends on several factors, including the concentration
of the buffering agents and the pKa of the buffer relative to the target pH range. Citric
acid buffers has pKa values of 3.13, 4.76, and 6.4, and is commonly used in formulations
targeting acidic environments, such as pH­sensitive NP release. In contrast, phosphate
buffer, with a pKa around 7.2, is widely used for physiological pH applications, such as
maintaining stability of neutral drug formulations. This means that selecting a buffer with
an appropriate capacity is essential in controlled release systems and pH­sensitive drug
carriers, where the surrounding environment may influence release kinetics of API.

1.5.6
Other Measurement Methods
Other measurement methods can be used to characterize and obtain knowledge about
the NPs after formation with microfluidics, these include µDiss, Dynamic Light Scattering
(DLS), and nanodrop.
µDiss is a small­volume, high­throughput dissolution platform designed to evaluate drug
release from nanocarriers under sink or non­sink conditions. It is especially suitable for
nanoparticles, where precise control of sample volume and mixing is crucial. µDiss en­
ables kinetic release studies using less than 10 mL of medium and works with UV/Vis
plate readers for time­point measurements. This feature is particularly helpful for study­
ing acid­labile or pH­triggered formulations [81].
Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS) is commonly used to characterize nanoparticles, measur­
ing hydrodynamic diameter, polydispersity index (PDI), and zeta potential. The technique
relies on observing the time­dependent changes in the intensity of scattered light caused
by the Brownian motion of particles in suspension. For pharmaceutical nanoparticles,

1.5.7
Sink Condition
In drug release studies, maintaining sink conditions is essential to accurately evaluate
the performance of controlled release systems such as NPs. Sink conditions is the state
in which the concentration of the drug in the release medium remains well below its sat­
uration solubility. To make sure that dissolution or diffusion is not restricted by solubility
limits, this approach imitates physiological environments. In these environments, the drug
is consistently absorbed or cleared. This allows the release to show the formulation’s true
performance instead of being limited by drug buildup in the medium. To maintain sink
conditions, a large volume of release medium relative to the drug dose is used, or alterna­
tively, the system can be refreshed either through medium replacement or flow­through
setups.
In experiments, where sampling is required to monitor drug release over time, small vol­
umes of medium at predefined intervals. To keep the total volume and sink conditions,
each removed sample is usually replaced with the same amount of fresh medium. How­
ever, this process causes dilution, which needs to be considered to calculate the total
amount of drug released accurately. This can be done using the following correction for­
mula.:

Results and Discussion
3.1
Generel effects of nanoparticles
Before the microfluidic system and ciprofloxacin HCl encapsulation was performed, sev­
eral chemical and physical properties of the polymers and NPs were evaluated. This in­
clude; effect of centrifugation, evaporation, solubility, storage condition, and temperature
change of NPs. Initially, the solubility of both Eudragit E100 and EPO was investigated in
buffer (pH 5.5) in order to find which polymer was most suitable for microfluidic purposes.
Upon dispersing Eudragit EPO and E100 in pH­adjusted phosphate buffer (pH < 5.5), no
dissolution was observed (see Appendix A.1). As stated in Section 1.3.4 ”Buffers”, the
buffering capacity of phosphate peaks near its pKa at 6.8. Therefore, it is more suitable
near neutral pH (7.2–7.4) and was not ideal for acidic conditions below pH 5.5. This
aligned with the known, Silvia Chuy & Leonard N. Bell (2006), who showed that factors
such as added solutes changed the apparent pKa of the buffers and altered their pH [84].
This means that adding the polymer far from the peak of phosphate buffer capacity has
limitations, since the pH changed easily because it exhibited a weak buffering capacity
below pH 5.8 because of its pKa.
Instead, in citrate buffer, as shown in Appendix A.1, dissolution was observed for both
polymers. This was due to its stronger buffer capacity under acidic conditions, as stated
in Section 1.3.4 ”Buffers”. Furthermore, in Appendix A.1, both Eudragit EPO and Eudragit
E100 were soluble in acetone, demonstrating the compatibility of the polymer with organic
solvent, which was important for the precipitation of NPs for the microfluidic system. Al­
though both polymers were commonly used, Eudragit EPO was preferred because it was
easier to work with in powder form than in pellets. Acetone served as the organic solvent
to dissolve Eudragit EPO in the microfluidic system unless stated otherwise. PBS at pH
7.4 was used for control studies, while citrate buffers at pH less than 5.5 were used for
NP dissolution.
Before assessing the impact of various methods and parameters, such as flow rate and the
concentration of Eudragit EPO and ciprofloxacin HCl, preliminary characterization studies
took place. This helped to evaluate the stability of the polymer and surfactant before using
any formulation methods. The preformulated NPs followed the setup described in Section

3.3
pH variation of Eudragit EPO
The role of pH variation was critical in understanding the behavior of pH­responsive poly­
meric NPs, such as those formulated with Eudragit EPO. As suggested by Tollemeto et
al. (2024), pH­sensitive polymers can undergo significant conformational, ionic, or hy­
drophilic/hydrophobic shifts depending on their surrounding environment. Such pH gradi­
ents can trigger a functional response from the polymeric matrix, leading to the alteration
of surface charge, permeability, and drug release profiles [90]. At low pH, protonation of
Eudragit EPO increases surface charge and induces polymer dissolution, resulting in burst
release of ciprofloxacin HCl. The positive charge may also exert a bacteriostatic effect
by interacting with negatively charged bacterial membranes. This aligned with Tollemeto
et al. (2024), who showed that ionizable groups on the polymer backbone influence drug
release and stability. Eudragit EPO was tested for solubility at the target pH (<5.5), en­
abling the investigation of its pH­triggered delivery efficiency. To evaluate this behavior,
dynamic light scattering (DLS) analysis was conducted following 24­hour incubation of
NPs at different pH conditions, as shown in Figure 3.3. All formulations shared the same
composition: 1% w/v Eudragit EPO, 10 µg/mL ciprofloxacin HCl, 1% w/v PVA, and a flow
rate ratio of 100:10.

Development
Four distinct microfluidic strategies were employed for the preparation of Eudragit EPO
NPs loaded with ciprofloxacin HCl. Each method reflected a different strategy in phase
arrangement, chip design, or reagent combination, with the aim of optimizing particle
size, PDI, encapsulation efficiency, and zeta potential. The flow rates were estimated

from the calibration done prior as seen in Appendix A.14. For all methods, an OFAT

3.4.1
Method 1: Solvent Displacement Using a Micro Mixer Chip
The first method followed as described in section 2.4 ”Method 1”. This approach combined
API and PVA in the aqueous outer phase and Eudragit EPO in acetone as the inner phase,
using a 125 µm micro mixer chip to enhance shear and mixing. As stated in Section 3.4
”Microfluidic Formulation Strategies for Nanoparticle Development”, this approach follows
OFAT with ANOVA.
Statistical analysis using ANOVA revealed a significant influence of flow rate on particle
size (p = 0.047), indicating that increasing the oil­to­aqueous flow ratio resulted in smaller
particle sizes, as shown in Figure 3.4. Higher ratios, meaning more water compared to
acetone, consistently produced smaller NPs. Lower ratios led to larger particles. This
suggested that increased shear and quicker jetting of the aqueous phase into the organic
stream likely encouraged faster supersaturation, improved nucleation rates, and short­
ened particle growth time, which resulted in smaller, more uniform NPs. This trend was
consistent in multiple experiments in at least triplicates (n≥3).
Figure 3.4: Effect of water:solvent (acetone) flow ratio on nanoparticle size using Method 1. Mean
particle size (± SD) is plotted against increasing water­to­solvent flow ratios. A general decreas­
ing trend in particle size is observed as the aqueous­to­organic phase ratio increases, suggesting
enhanced nanoprecipitation efficiency and improved polymer solidification at higher dilution con­
ditions. The shaded area represents the 95% confidence interval of the linear regression.
The increased acetone flow rate led to larger particle sizes, as shown in Figure 3.4 and
Appendix A.10, with particle sizes decreasing from over 250 nm in low aqueous to organic
flow ratios to below 150 nm at higher ratios. The higher organic solvent content probably
decreased the interfacial tension gradient between the two phases. This change resulted

adjustment of the water:solvent ratio was an effective strategy to control NP size [91].

concentration on NP size. A mild but not statistically significant trend (p=0.075) was ob­

in particle size with higher polymer content, probably due to increased viscosity and poly­

ganic phase prior to emulsification, with the aim of investigating whether the drug–polymer
preassociation influenced NP formation, encapsulation efficiency (EE%), size and stabil­
ity. The transition from Method 1 to Method 2 also involved replacing the microfluidic chip.
Method 1 used a 125 µm micromixer chip suitable for simple emulsions, but lacked the
42
==PAGE:53==
structural capacity to generate stable W/O/W emulsions, as reflected by the poor encap­
sulation results in Section 3.4.1. Therefore, Method 2 utilized a 3D flow­focusing chip
(100 µm etch depth) with a porous design optimized for double emulsion generation. This
setup allowed for better laminar flow, phase separation, and droplet monodispersity, con­

the formulation variables were evaluated using OFAT and analyzed using ANOVA.

hydrophobic, cationic polymer with tertiary amines (pKa 7.0–7.3). Their differences in po­
larity, solubility, and ionization at both physiological and acidic pH made it hard to dissolve
them together. The solvent was needed to sufficiently dissolve both components while
preventing premature precipitation or phase separation. Poor solubilization could weaken
NP formation, reduce stability, or lower encapsulation efficiency. Therefore, solvent com­
patibility was vital for successful co­encapsulation and stable emulsification.

aqueous stream within the chip, limiting droplet coalescence and promoting the formation
of compact, uniform droplets. These smaller primary droplets were carried into the second
emulsification stage, where they ultimately form smaller NPs.
Statistical analysis showed that the Eudragit EPO flow rate had no significant effect on
NP size (p = 0.834), likely due to sufficient polymer already present for stabilization. This
suggested that beyond a certain threshold, changes in Eudragit EPO flow have minimal
impact on interfacial dynamics or particle morphology. In this two­chip system, droplet

took place in pH­buffered systems, this acts as a controlled model to mimic the acidic
conditions in biofilms. Thus, the observed pH­triggered release behavior supports the po­
tential use of Eudragit EPO NPs for delivering drugs aimed at biofilms. This may improve
local drug accumulation and reduce systemic exposure and off­target effects.
3.7
Interpretation and Limitations
The microfluidic method allowed for the creation of nanoparticles that are acceptable in
size, measuring less than 130 nm, and have low polydispersity indices of less than 0.2.
This method may perform better than traditional bulk methods in precision and repro­
55
==PAGE:66==
ducibility. However, some formulations showed larger standard deviations, which sug­
gests variability under certain conditions. Both the 3D flow­focusing and micromixer chips
demonstrated highly reproducible droplet generation, with the 3D flow­focusing chip pro­
viding better control over droplet and NP size. Solvent selection was critical for the organic
phase, and acetone produced smaller and more uniform particles than ethanol or aqueous
mixtures, likely due to its better solubility of Eudragit EPO and rapid interfacial diffusion.
1% PVA w/v helped maintain the stability of Eudragit EPO, with recommended concentra­
tions of 1% w/v Eudragit EPO, 100 µg/mL API, flow rate ratio of approximately 10:1, with
sizes below 130nm and PDI 0.2.
Furthermore, the NPs exhibited a clear pH­responsive behavior with minimal release at
pH 7.4 and significantly enhanced release at pH 5.5. This confirmed the functionality of Eu­
dragit EPO as a pH­sensitive carrier for site­specific drug delivery. Thus, EE% was lower
than hoped for hydrophilic drugs, tobramycin and ciprofloxacin HCl, the reason for this
was likely due to poor partitioning and weak interaction with Eudragit EPO, with drug diffu­
sion into the external aqueous phase during emulsification, or surface adsorption losses,
before full polymer precipitation. Thus, for hydrophobic, poorly water­soluble, and anionic
drugs, paracetamol and indomethacin, showed better compatibility with the organic phase
and polymer matrix, resulting in EE% around 64.1 ± 2.3% and 42.2 ± 2.4%, respectively.
Potential scientific limitations of this study include drug loss during centrifugation or wash­
ing steps, particularly if the drug was loosely adsorbed to the NP surface. Additionally,
nonspecific adsorption of the drug to microfluidic tubing or device surfaces may have con­
tributed to reduced recovery. Furthermore, the use of low drug concentrations may have
approached the detection limit of the UV–Vis assay, leading to increased variability or
potential underestimation of encapsulation efficiency (EE%).
In this study, the OFAT approach was employed to investigate the influence of individual
formulation and process parameters on NP characteristics. Compared to DoE, OFAT has
notable limitations. While DoE enables simultaneous evaluation of multiple variables and
their interactions, OFAT does not account for interdependent effects between factors. DoE
offers a statistically robust and efficient framework, by allowing predictive modeling and
optimization of outcomes with fewer total experiments. However, DoE is less suited for
highly unstable or poorly understood systems, such as microfluidic NP formation, where
initial conditions may be unpredictable, and outcomes difficult to reproduce. At the time of
this study, limited literature existed on microfluidic encapsulation of ciprofloxacin HCl, and
as shown later, EE% was generally low. Therefore, OFAT was preferred for its straightfor­
ward interpretation in early­stage screening. By systematically varying individual param­
eters, such as flow rate, polymer concentration, and drug loading, while keeping others
constant. OFAT provided a clear and intuitive framework for isolating the effect of each
variable on NP size and EE%.

4
Conclusion
This study explored NPs formulated using Eudragit EPO and prepared via microfluidics
for the encapsulation of ciprofloxacin HCl, showing pH­responsive drug release. The aim
was to achieve controlled size in range of 90­130 nm, narrow PDI below 0.2, zeta poten­
tial above 25 mV and efficient encapsulation efficiency. Using an One­Factor­At­a­Time
approach four different methods were evaluated and investigated by the effects of key
formulation parameters including polymer concentration, drug concentration, surfactant
type/concentration, solvent system and flow rate.
The results demonstrated that while microfluidics enabled reproducible production of NPs
with acceptable sizes below 150 nm and low PDI below 0.2, encapsulation efficiency
remained below 25%. This was particularly for hydrophilic and zwitterionic drugs like
ciprofloxacin HCl and tobramycin. These compounds shared high aqueous solubility and
ionic charge states, which likely contributed to poor compatibility with the hydrophobic
polymer matrix, rapid diffusion during emulsification, and low drug entrapment.
In contrast, hydrophobic APIs such as paracetamol and indomethacin showed signifi­
cantly higher EE% between 42 to 66%, when formulated under similar conditions, con­
firming that drug–polymer compatibility plays a critical role in microfluidic encapsulation
success. Method 3 showed promise for these drugs with following conditions; 1% w/v
Eudragit EPO, 100 µg/mL paracetamol or indomethacin, and 1% w/v PVA, with flow rate
100:10 (aqueous:solvent). This resulted in sizes below 150 nm and PDI below 0.2.
A release study demonstrated a clear pH­dependent release profile of Eudragit EPO, with
enhanced drug release of paracetamol observed under mildly acidic conditions (pH 5.5)
compared to neutral conditions (pH 7.4). This supports the pH­responsive behavior of
Eudragit EPO, making it particularly suitable for targeting acidic microenvironments such
as bacterial biofilms.
Different surfactants concentrations from PVA, Poloxamer 420, Pluronic 127, and, or chi­
tosan, showed no affect on the zeta potential. These results suggested that the stabi­
lizing role of the surfactant was likely steric rather than electrostatic. Instead, increas­
ing ciprofloxacin HCl concentration led to a moderate increase in zeta potential reaching
values above 25 mV. This was likely due to its cationic nature at acidic to neutral pH,
contributing additional surface charge to the NPs and enhancing electrostatic stability.
Overall, microfluidic strategies proved effective in controlling particle size, their suitability
for encapsulating polar, hydrophilic antibiotics in Eudragit EPO is limited.

Future Aspects
For further goal and purpose of this project, future work could focus on different things.
Firstly, based on the EE% of ciprofloxacin HCl and paracetamol, the drug­polymer in­
teractions played a huge role. Future work could include exploring chemically modified
Eudragit derivatives or alternative polymers to enhance drug–polymer compatibility, espe­
cially for polar or charged drugs. Otherwise, it could be possible to incorporate other APIs,
especially other antibiotics, with varying physicochemical properties to systematically map
polymer–drug compatibility trends. With these changes, new setup of flow control could
be tested for more stable encapsulation either by using co­solvation or a W/O/W emulsion.
Once these adjustment has been mad, long terms future aspect could include a shift
toward full Design of Experiments approaches, to improve process optimization and pa­
rameter interaction insights. Moreover, extended release studies under simulated biofilm
or infection­like conditions, to simulate bacterial biofilm, test pH­triggered delivery, antimi­
crobial efficacy, and cytotoxicity in biofilm models. In the end, scale­Up and translation
strategies for continuous, scalable nanoparticle production using parallelized microfluidic
devices or droplet­based reactors. These findings contribute to the broader understanding
of formulation design for targeted drug delivery using pH­responsive polymeric nanopar­
ticles.
