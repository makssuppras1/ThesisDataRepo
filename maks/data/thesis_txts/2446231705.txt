Abstract
The western fjords of the Svalbard archipelago have in recent years met an increased
input of Atlantic water carried north by the West Spitsbergen Current. The marine
microbial communities in the Arctic waters are nely tuned to the extreme conditions,
and a change in dominance of water masses is predicted to have large implications on
the community composition of these organisms in the Arctic region.
The aim of this study was to investigate dierences in microbial eukaryote communi-
ties in Arctic versus Atlantic water masses, by comparing diversity and composition to
water characteristics at various sampling stations. Water characteristics were obtained
through CTD measurements of the water column and included salinity, temperature
and uorescence. Additionally, chlorophyll a biomass was calculated from uorescence
reads on extracted GF/F lters. The community composition was studied through Illu-
mina next generation sequencing (NGS) of the V4 variable region of the small subunit
(SSU) 18S rRNA gene, using eukaryote specic primers. Two independent datasets
were investigated and compared, collected respectively on one cruise during July 2013
(Micro Fun) and on ve cruises during June-August 2018 (CruiseScience).
Comparison of the identied taxa in the two datasets revealed the abundance of sev-
eral species common for both years. These included two species of the dinoagellate
Gyrodinium as well as an unidentied pelagophyte, which appeared to be in a full
bloom stage in 2013. The abundance of the Arctic Micromonas polaris in the Arctic
water inuenced Erik Eriksen Strait supported previous observations on this species
thriving in cold temperature waters.
No clear patterns were found between water mass and community diversity and compo-
sition, due to the incompleteness of both datasets. Nevertheless, the study underlined
the importance and relevance of this type of studies, seeing the large potential on both
spatial and temporal scale of continuous sampling throughout an entire summer.

Preface
When initiating this project, I had no idea about the challenges I would be facing.
Like most people writing their thesis, I presume.
I had an ambitious plan: I wanted to include laboratory work and next generation se-
quencing with a partner university in my time schedule, counting precisely ve months.
Luckily, the eld work had already been carried out. After a month in the laboratory,
the libraries were sent o for Illumina sequencing. The sequencing unfortunately took
much longer than expected, and when we nally received the data, it turned out to
be of extremely poor quality. The majority of January and February was therefore
spent in the attempt to process the data using several dierent pipelines. Due to
the poor quality, the vast majority of the sequences were weeded out, and a total
of only 18 OTUs was at best recovered from the dataset. Based on the assumption
that a denoising-based pipeline would improve the recovery of usable data, this was
also attempted, but without success. Ultimately, an alternative dataset similar to my
original dataset was included in the project. From this, it was possible to carry out
an analysis similar to what was intended on the original data.
To reect the actual process of my work and the challenges along the way, this report
is structured in such way that the focus is put on the original data until the processing
failure. Only after this, the alternative dataset is introduced and included. Additional
weight was put on details of the bioinformatics, since this was a large part of the
work for this project. Since the desired ASVs were not obtained, though, the partial
results of the data processing were included in the methods part in stead of the results.
Despite the challenges, working with this dataset has been a steep learning curve, and
has probably taught me more about bioinformatics than a "perfect" dataset could.
Given the time available, I tried to the best of my ability to demonstrate the positive
outcome of my project and the potential of continuing working with the samples from
2018. In the light of this, I plan to continue working on these samples during the next
months together with Anna, even though my thesis has come to an end. New libraries
will be prepared from the samples and sent to a dierent Illumina sequencing facility,
hopefully producing sequence data of better quality.
This thesis project was carried out in connection with the CruiseScience (2018) project,
in corporation with Hurtigruten AS. The secondary dataset obtained for community
analysis was originally part of the MicroFun (2015) project.

1. Introduction
The Svalbard archipelago is located in the high Arctic, where extreme light and
whether conditions dominate. Positioned between 76 and 81° North, half way between
the northernmost tip of Norway and the North Pole, Svalbard lies in the entrance from
the North Atlantic and the Barents Sea to the Arctic Ocean. Darkness dominates from
November to mid February, and the sun is above the horizon 24 hours a day from April
to August. Due to its location in the pathway connecting the Atlantic Ocean and the
Arctic Ocean, Svalbard is however considerably milder, wetter and cloudier than other
regions at the same latitude (Hanssen-Bauer et al., 2019). The Arctic has in recent
years been subject to increasing attention due to observed changes in the climate, and
sea ice coverage in both the Arctic Ocean itself and in fjords in the Arctic is declining
(MET Norway, 2005-2019).
Changes in the physical environment in the Arctic during the past decades have been
proven repeatedly, and since 2004 the waters around Svalbard have received an in-
creased amount of heat from the North Atlantic (Walczowski et al., 2006). Nowhere
else in the Arctic have changes happened as fast as they currently are on Svalbard
(Isaksen et al., 2016), and according to Nordli et al. (2014), the temperature increase
on Svalbard during the past 3-4 decades is the largest increase of anywhere in Europe.
1.1. The waters around Svalbard
The fjords around Svalbard are dominated by Arctic Water (ArW), which is a rela-
tively cold and fresh water mass (table 1.1). ArW consists partly of Local Water (LW)
and Winter Cooled Water (WCW) created locally within the fjords by inow of cold
fresh melt water from snow and ice, and partly of water carried south from the Arctic
Ocean by the East Spitsbergen Current (ESC) (Saloranta et al., 2001). South-west of
the southern tip of Spitsbergen, the Spitsbergen Polar Current (SPC) leads the ArW
water north once again along the west coast of Spitsbergen (gure 1.1). As it runs
along the coast and into the fjords, this cold and relatively fresh polar water functions
as a barrier shielding o other water masses.
The West Spitsbergen Current (WSC) reaches the Svalbard archipelago from the south,
as a branch of the North Atlantic Current (NAC). Meeting the Polar Front on its way
north, Atlantic Water (AW) runs northward and thus parallelly with ArW from the
SPC. AW is shielded o by ArW and much of the water stays on the outer side of the
West Spitsbergen Shelf, but the WSC does periodically succeed in passing through the
barrier of ArW, feeding the western fjords of Svalbard with warmer and more saline
water (Cottier et al., 2005).

1.2. Arctic marine ecosystems
As part of the increased attention on the Arctic, marine ecosystems have been sub-
ject to greater scientic interest in recent years (e.g. Søreide et al., 2010; Meier et
al., 2014). The Arctic marine ecosystems are above all characterized by seasonality
(Leu, J. Søreide, et al. (2011), Marquardt et al. (2016)), and are nely adjusted to
the extreme variation in light and weather conditions between seasons. As primary
producers and as the very base of the food web, marine microbial organisms are an
important part of the Arctic ecosystem. Primary production uctuates largely over
the year; in fact the primary production can be several orders of magnitude dierent
between high and low production periods (Leu, J. Søreide, et al., 2011). Nutrients
are generally abundant in the cold ArW, but the light and temperature conditions
shape harsh living circumstances for organisms inhabiting the fjords.
The pelagic
spring bloom around Svalbard typically occurs between April and June, and some of
the primary factors inuencing the bloom include light availability, stratication of
the water column and the timing of the sea-ice break-up. Factors like these are all
interconnected; light availability in the water column highly depends on ice and snow
cover (e.g. Leu, Mundy, et al. (2015)), and stratication in part depends on mixing
of the water column, also inuenced by the ice cover. An increase in melt water run
o from glaciers will likewise inuence stratication.
In the western fjords of Svalbard, sea ice conditions are especially controlled by the
input of AW, and according to the mismatch-hypothesis (Cushing, 1990), changes in

1.2.1. Marine microbial eukaryotes
Microbial eukaryotes can be dened as all microscopic organisms with the genetic ma-
terial enclosed in a nucleus. In the marine environment, these are plankton and include
micro algae, but also non-autotrophic protists (Poulin et al., 2011). In the Arctic ma-
rine ecosystems, microbial eukaryotes are either pelagic communities adapted to live
in the upper water column, or sympagic communities (i.e. sea ice associated).
Microbial communities have been widely studied, and their role is considered of crit-
ical importance for ecosystem in all environments. In the marine setting, the most
important roles include primary production, controlling blooms as predators, and they
play a large role in mechanisms such as vertical ux and the microbial loop as well

Many functions and roles of these organisms in the Arctic are still not fully understood,

1.3. Molecular methods & bioinformatics
As a central requirement for building and interpreting possible scenarios for the future
Arctic ecosystems, a thorough understanding of the current situation is vital. Tra-
ditional methods for identication of organisms in a sample involve the use of light
microscopy for counting and distinguishing individuals. This method is rst of all
is time consuming, and often also involves high uncertainties; organisms in dierent
life stages may vary in size and morphology, and samples may contain fragments of
organisms that are dicult to identify morphologically. Also, dierent people might
see and assess in dierent ways, and correct species identication can thus be dicult.
When it comes to microbial organisms, a more signicant issue is the matter of size.
Many species are simply too small to distinguish in light microscopes, and even the

==PAGE:20==

use of electron microscopes may not be sucient to distinct between morphologically
indistinct species (e.g. Bik et al. (2012), Pedrós-Alió et al. (2015)).
Modern methods include molecular tools, which use genetic material to assess the or-
ganisms present in the sample. By looking at extracted DNA from ltered water, it is
possible to conduct more accurate and less uncertain assessments of community com-
positions. Rare species can moreover be hard to point out using traditional methods,
since only small volumes of water are investigated. Molecular tools can thus be useful
for looking at species of low abundance as well.
Molecular methods have faced an extreme development during the past two decades
(Pe£nikar et al., 2014), and methods are now so quick and precise that millions of
sequences can be sequenced in a short period of time. Using the older electrophoresis-
based Sanger sequencing method (Sanger et al., 1977), sequencing of the human
genome (Collins et al. (2003), initiated in 1988) took almost 15 years to carry out,
and had a cost of nearly 3 billion USD. It however lay ground for the emergence of
high-throughput sequencing, and today the entire human genome can be sequenced
in a single sequencing experiment (Reuter et al., 2015). Large-scale sequencing is to-
day available for the average researcher, and the so-called next-generation sequencing
(NGS) is transforming today's biology (e.g. Creer et al. (2016), Reuter et al. (2015)).
1.3.1. Marker genes & metabarcoding
Analysing environmental samples for assessment of species composition, a marker gene
is usually chosen. A marker gene is a short DNA sequence with a known location in
the genome. For a genetic marker to be useful for species identication, the same gene
has to be represented in a large number of organisms, but has to vary enough between
the species to allow distinction. It thus has to have a high interspecic variation, but
a low intraspecic variation. A species' version of this marker gene is the species'
barcode.
A genetic marker that is often used for eukaryotic species identication is the 18S rRNA
gene (or 18S rDNA) in the small ribosomal subunit (SSU); thus the ribosomal DNA
from which the 18S rRNA is being transcribed. As part of the 18S, the hypervariable
V4 region is often used as the marker gene to distinguish between species (Nickrent
et al., 1991). Unfortunately, there is no single marker gene that can be used universally
for all organisms, but the 18S V4 has proven to be a good marker gene for broader
assessment of microbial eukaryote community composition.
Metabarcoding is the term used for simultaneous barcoding of several organisms in
environmental samples, combining the idea behind marker gene barcoding with high-
throughput sequencing, so that large numbers of sequences are barcoded at the same
time. The leading NGS platform on the market is Illumina® (Van Dijk et al., 2014),
which has proven to have one of the highest eciencies of sequencing environmental
samples (Reuter et al., 2015).The advantages of metabarcoding are many, as the tech-
nology gives a new perspective on time consumption, counts, eciency and accuracy,
but also standardization and automatization (Van Dijk et al., 2014). Many of those
are challenges that apply to the traditional microscopy methods.
Metabarcoding is only semi-quantitative, since the sequences are amplied by poly-

merase chain reaction (PCR). Also, it is important to note that there is a large variation
in the number of rRNA gene copies from one species to another, which inuences the
amount of DNA found per species in a sample (Bik et al., 2012). Although it cannot
be used to dene exact numbers of each sequence, it does however give an indication of
the relative abundance between the dierent sequences in the sample and thus species
at the sample location.
The major limitation to metabarcoding is the dependency on a reference library. To
assign taxonomy to a dataset, the outputted sequences are usually compared with
sequences in a reference database, or blasted; derived from the Basic Local Alignment
Search Tool (BLAST, Altschul et al. (1990)). In the blast search, each sequence is com-
pared to previously found sequences of known taxonomy and is on basis of that given a
taxonomic assignment. Metabarcoding is therefore highly dependent on accurate and
updated reference databases. Open-source libraries such as the widely used nucleotide
database at the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI-NT) are very
useful due to the amount of sequences in the library, but are also at risk of contain-
ing uncertain information, since anyone can upload results to the database. Also, no
quality check is carried out. Curated databases also exist, e.g. the Protist Ribosomal
Reference database PR2 (Guillou et al., 2012). These can be very useful when looking
at specic taxonomic groups or habitats. For Arctic species, a useful database is the
curated CLJ database by (Lovejoy et al., 2016), which contains reference sequences
of marine and freshwater communities of bacteria, archaea and microbial eukaryotes.
The disadvantage of using curated reference databases is however the limitation in
reference sequences, which increases the probability of having non-matching sequences
in the dataset. For this reason, it can be advantageous to match sequences with several
datasets and pick out the best hits.
1.3.2. OTUs versus ASVs
Receiving a raw sequence le from e.g. Illumina sequencing, the rst challenge is the
quantities; several millions of sequences are often outputted through NGS. One of the
most common ways to deal with the quantities has been to cluster the data into op-
erational taxonomic units (OTU) (Callahan et al., 2017). This method clusters the
sequence reads that dier by less than a xed dissimilarity threshold (usually 97 or
98 %), eventually boiling all reads down to a number of representative OTUs. The
output is a table containing number of reads of each OTU in each sample, along with
a list of one representative sequence per OTU. These sequences can subsequently be
blasted against a reference database and thereby identied.
Traditional OTU "picking" can be divided into three dierent approaches; de novo,
closed-reference and open-reference picking (Bik et al., 2012). De novo OTU picking
matches the sequences within the dataset only within a given threshold, while closed-
reference OTU picking uses an external database and maps the unknown sequences
to this.
The sequences that do not match any reference sequences are discarded.
Open-reference OTU picking combines the two former by matching the sequences to
a reference database like closed-reference, but keeps the sequences that do not match
with the database, attempting to cluster these according to a threshold, like de novo.
7
==PAGE:22==
1 Introduction
Closed-reference OTU picking can be advantageous, since it allows comparison with
other studies as it does not produce OTUs based on internal similarities, but may also
weed out novel and potentially important sequences.
A more recent approach groups sequences into amplicon sequence variants (ASVs).
ASVs build on the de novo concept, and is a higher resolution analogue of OTUs, as it
records the amount of times each exact amplicon sequence variant is observed in each
sample. The method is based on the expectation that biological sequences are likely
to be repeatedly observed, and applies an algorithm that takes sequencing errors into
account (Callahan et al., 2017).
The ASVs that are produces can be directly compared to ASVs from other studies,
and this method of grouping sequences increases the sensitivity and ultimately the
reliability of the assigned taxonomies. Also, ASVs that cannot be taxonomically iden-
tied, are also kept.

2.4.3. Statistical analysis in R
The OTU table received from the Micro Fun project was analysed in R with the pur-
pose of investigating the diversity and species composition in relation to water masses.
vegan was the main package used (Oksanen et al., 2013). Other important packages
included seqinr (Charif et al., 2017), devtools (Wickham et al., 2018), bioformat
(McMurdie PJ, 2019), heatmaply (Galili et al., 2017), gplots (Warnes et al., 2009)
and dendextend (Galili, 2015).
The physical conditions at the depth of the samples (5, 15 and 35m) were investi-
gated though temperature-salinity (T-S) plots. The denitions of the various water
masses (table 1.1) were drawn onto the plots for to determine the water masses each
sample was taken from.
For comparison, T-S plots were also produced for the 31
Hurtigruten & Helmer Hanssen stations where CTD data was available. In order to
investigate if there was a dierence in water masses between east and west, the plot
was repainted according to the position of each sampling station.
Since the temperature and salinity at each point only informed about the conditions
at the specic depth of sampling, these data were not necessarily representative for the
whole water column and might not give a correct picture of the dominating water mass
at the station. Therefore, T-S plots of the entire water column were produced to asses
the dominating water masses. The proles of salinity, temperature and uorescence
with pressure (as proxy of depth) at each station was also plotted.
Prior to any further work with the new OTU table, all samples were rareed (subsam-
pled) using the rarrefy function. This was done to enable comparison of relative
read abundances between the samples. Thus, a subsample of a specied size was ran-
domly picked from each sample in the dataset. The preferred subsample size would be
the largest possible, and therefore the sample with lowest total amount of reads de-
ned the size of the subsample. rowSums was used for printing the sums of reads for
each sample, and the size of the subsample was chosen to 10,357, based on the sample
from Isfjorden (ISA15m). The samples were rareed to this value, and the rarefaction
curves were plotted with rarecurve to investigate whether the subsample size was
large enough to represent the original sample; i.e. whether more reads would add more
OTUs to each sample.
Alpha diversity (i.e. within-site diversity site) was investigated by looking at rich-
ness and evenness. Richness was addressed by counting the number of OTUs in each
sample. The results were plotted in a barchart, where the richness of the whole dataset
was compared to the subsampled dataset. To visualize the evenness of the datasets,
rank abundance plots showing the number of reads for each OTU were made. In the
plots, OTUs are shown in decreasing abundance (rank). Using the Shannon diversity
index (Shannon, 1948), the Pielous index J' (Pielou, 1966) was calculated and shown
along with richness.
Beta diversity (i.e. diversity between sites) was investigated to assess dierences
in diversity between the samples. The samples were compared in a Bray-Curtis dis-
similarity matrix (Bray et al., 1957) using vegdist, method = "bray".
The
23
==PAGE:38==
2 Materials & methods
Bray-Curtis approach was specically chosen, as it takes abundances into account and
not only presence/absence of each species in the samples. The Bray-Curtis dissimilar-
ity was calculated for the whole dataset as well, and the ability of the subsample to
represent the entire sample was investigated by plotting the correlation. The correla-
tion was further investigated in a Mantel test (Mantel, 1967) using mantel (Mantel
r value ranging from -1 (strong negative correlation) to 1 (strong positive correlation)).
A non-metric multidimensional scaling (NMDS, Kruskal (1964)) plot was produced
using metaMDS, performing Bray-Curtis transformation. Normalized environmental
factors (z-scores) were added to the plot as potential explanatory variables. These
included temperature, salinity and chlorophyll a content. The stress was calculated
and noted to assess how well the original dissimilarities were represented in reduced
dimensions (two).
The bioenv function was used to test the normalized environmental data against
the Bray-Curtis dissimilarity matrix to assess which variable was most likely to be
explanatory. Subsequently, a permutational multivariate analysis of variance (PER-
MANOVA) was carried out using adonis in order to assess the statistical signicance
of the variables.
To investigate the sample stations further, a dendrogram using the unweighted pair
group method with arithmetic mean (UPGMA, Sokal (1958)) method was produced
based on hierarchical clustering using hclust. The dendrogram was based on all
OTUs in the dataset.
Taxonomy was assigned to the 25 most abundant OTUs by a blast search (Altschul
et al., 1990) of the representative sequence for each OTU against three reference
databases: PR2 (Guillou et al., 2012), CLJ (Lovejoy et al., 2016) and NCBI-NT (ac-
cessed March 6, 2019). The most specic (i.e. species if denoted, otherwise genus/fam-
ily/etc) was chosen among the three blast results for each OTU, providing the E-value
was identical (the E-value was mostly 0). Finally, if the taxonomic assignment of an
OTU diered between the databases and there was no dierence in specication level
or E-value, hits from the curated databases (CLJ and PR2) were used. The same ap-
proach was applied for assigning taxonomy to the 17 Hurtigruten & Helmer Hanssen
OTUs.
Some of the OTUs were assigned the same taxonomic denition. These OTUs were
however not pooled, since the accession number was not the same. Also, the fact
that the OTUs were clustered in separate OTUs indicated that they were likely to be
dierent species, even if the same taxonomy was assigned.
Based on the blast searches against the two databases PR2 (Guillou et al., 2012) and
CLJ (Lovejoy et al., 2016), the taxonomies assigned to the 250 most abundant OTUs
were used to make a stacked barchart to further investigate the community composi-
tion.
Lastly, a heat map was produced from the 25 most abundant OTUs, to visualize and
investigate the abundance and distribution of these OTUs among the samples.
An R script with details can be found in appendix E.2.

4. Discussion
Some clear patterns in water masses in accordance with literature were observed from
the CTD data, and taxonomies assigned to the various OTUs were generally within
groups commonly found in the Arctic (e.g. Luo et al. (2009), Lovejoy et al. (2006),
Marquardt (2016)). Dinoagellates was the most abundant group in the Micro Fun
dataset and accounted for 59% of all read counts. Four out of the 17 OTUs obtained
from the Hurtigruten & Helmer Hanssen dataset was assigned to the group, suggesting
high abundance in these samples as well.
The following points seek to address the most important ndings in this study.
Clear dierence in temperature and salinity between
eastern and western sample stations
One clear result from the Hurtigruten & Helmer Hanssen dataset was the distinction in
temperature and salinity between the eastern and western sample stations. Even the
sample stations on the northern side of the archipelago, which receive input of both
ArW and AW, could be geographically north-east and north-west divided based on
their hydrographic characteristics. As expected, there was a pattern if higher salinities
and temperatures at the AW inuenced stations, and lower salinities and temperatures
at the ArW inuenced stations. Moreover, the dataset indicated a change in salinity
and temperature from June to August, as the SW appeared to become fresher and
warmer during the summer. This is again as expected, since the fjords receive fresh-
water input from glacier run-o as the summer advances.
For the Micro Fun stations, a more precise determination of hydrographic inuence
was obtained from examining the temperature and salinity through the entire water
column. Sampling set up (i.e. all samples where collected within one week) prevented
the examination of any temporal patterns. The observed dominance of AW in Fram
Strait was as expected (e.g. Saloranta et al. (2001)), and likewise the observed condi-
tion in Hinlopen Strait, which is alternately inuenced by AW (or rather TAW) and
ArW (Daase and Eiane, 2007). The inuence of TAW in Hornsund suggested a recent
input of AW from the WSC, which could be explained by a dominance of the WSC
over the SPC, enabling intrusion of warm water into the fjords. This was in agree-
ment with (Promi«ska et al., 2017) that reported the presence of AW at the mouth of
Hornsund during summer 2013.
The observed ArW dominance in Erik Eriksen Strait was also in accordance with the
ocean current patterns, with a strong ESC inuence at this station (Saloranta et al.,
2001). The occurrence of primarily LW in Wijdefjorden indicated the inuence of
glacier run o into the fjords, and the presence of ArW reected its latitude. No clear
inuence of AW nor ArW was observed in Bockfjorden and in Isfjorden, presumably
sinces both fjords stretch deep into the landscape and were therefore dominated by
local processes rather than intruding water of the time of sampling (Svendsen et al.,
2002; Walczowski et al., 2006).

Evenness dominated by three bloom events
The communities at the various stations sampled for the Micro Fun project were di-
verse in terms of number of OTUs, but some stations were very uneven. This was in
large part explained by three dierent bloom events.
The two most noticeable of these were explained by particularly high read abundances
of several OTUs assigned to Gyrodinium spp. dinoagellate, accounting for 34% of
the reads in the whole dataset. The most abundant of these OTUs were assigned to
the species Gyrodinium helveticum and Gyrodinium fusiforme, which were in fact both
found in the Hurtigruten & Helmer Hanssen dataset as well.
The abundance of Gyrodinium spp. in Arctic regions has been demonstrated in var-
ious previous studies (Kubiszyn et al., 2016; Levinsen et al., 2002; Marquardt et al.,
2016). In this study, the genus had highest abundances in Fram Strait and in Hin-
lopen Strait, G. fusiforme being particularly abundant in Fram Strait and at 35m
in Hinlopen Strait. The relative abundances within each of these three samples were
15%, 16% and 26% respectively, reecting blooms at these two stations. The bloom
of these OTUs at the sampling depths was not detectable on the CTD proles from
Fram Strait and Hinlopen, since the Gyrodinium spp. are heterotrophs and therefore
do not contain chlorophyll a (Takano et al., 2004).
Some OTUs were assigned to the species Gyrodinium helveticum. This is however a
freshwater species (Takano et al., 2004), and the presence of this specic species is
therefore unlikely. OTUs with the same taxonomic assignment have been reported
in multiple previous studies from the Arctic (Piquet et al., 2010), where it has been
suggested that this may in fact be the closely related dinoagellate species Gyrodinium
rubrum. Forming a single clade with G. helveticum (Piquet et al., 2010; Takano et
al., 2004), G. rubrum might be the actual species observed in the samples. Like G.
fusiforme, the G. helveticum/rubrum OTU was abundant in Fram Strait and at 5m
in Hinlopen Strait.
The high abundances of G. fusiforme and G. helveticum/rubrum at the two stations
could be related to AW and TAW, seeing that both Hinlopen and Fram Strait were
inuenced by these water masses. The same OTUs were however also relatively abun-
dant in the ArW inuenced Erik Eriksen Strait sample, and studies such as Bachy
et al. (2011) have found G. rubrum in high abundance close to the geographical North
Pole. No specic connection between water mass and to the occurrence of the Gyro-
dinium genus could therefore be made.
Studies on especially unarmored Gyrodinium species by traditional methods (i.e. light
microscopy) are often complicated and uncertain, since the organisms are fragile and
easily destroyed (Gómez, 2007). There is therefore a gap in knowledge regarding dis-
tribution of these species (Kubiszyn et al., 2016). The nding of the genus in this
study suggests that this gap can be lled with the aid of molecular tools. A system-
atic approach is however needed, and it would be highly benecial to include other
fast-evolving genes that can divide better at species level.

The third and last bloom event was due to an unidentied pelagophyte observed in
very high abundance in Hornsund. With a relative abundance of 66% in the Horn-
sund sample and accounting for 8% of all reads in the entire dataset, the pelagophyte
assigned "pelagophyte clone DH114 3A43" in the CLJ database was one of the most
dominant OTUs and was clearly in bloom at the time of sampling. The uorescence
prole supported the presence of a bloom at 5m, and so did the chlorophyll a value
of 9.81 µg/l (0.45-10µm fraction) at 5m (table D.5). The same OTU was found in
Isfjorden, although seemingly in lower abundance. The chlorophyll a value here was
2.38 µg/l. This could suggest a pre- or post-bloom stage at this station, or the lower
biomass could be due to other factors such as predation.
The same pelagophyte (i.e. identical sequence) has only been recorded a few times
in the fjords around Svalbard, e.g. at 5m depth in Billefjorden in June 2011 (Vader
et al., 2018) in a lower relative abundance than found in this study. The results from
the Micro Fun samples are (to the best of our knowledge) the rst time a full bloom
of this pelagophyte has been recorded. The specic species is unidentied, and its role
in the Arctic ecosystem has not been described; the species is currently being cultured
for taxonomic description (personal correspondence, A. Vader). It is neither known
whether it has always been present in Arctic waters but just never observed, or if it is
new to these latitudes. A possibility is that the species has been present all along, but
only now has gotten optimal conditions for blooming with the increased AW inuence.
If this is the case, chances are that we will see more recordings of this pelagophyte in
the future. Possibly supporting this was the interesting observation of the identical
sequence among the OTUs from the Hurtigruten & Helmer Hanssen samples.
Micromonas polaris in ArW
Only one genus of chlorophytes were among the most abundant OTUs, but this was
however quite abundant.
The OTUs were assigned to the green algae genus Mi-
cromonas, specically "Micromonas CCMP2099 Arctic", as the only Micromonas
among the assigned OTUs. The species has only recently been described as a sep-
arate species from Micromonas pusilla by Simon et al. (2017), and has been assigned
the species name Micromonas polaris. M. polaris is widespread in the Arctic Ocean
(Connie Lovejoy, Vincent, et al., 2007), and has even been reported in the Southern
Ocean and in the deep currents leading water from the Arctic to the Southern Ocean
(Simmons, 2015). Simon et al. (2017) has described the species as strictly associated
with polar water, and in this study, the OTUs assigned to M. polaris were especially
abundant at 5m in Bockfjorden and in Wijdefjorden, where ArW and LW dominated.
The abundances in these two samples were as high as 19.1% and 19.6%, which could
in fact indicate a bloom at these station. The chlorophyll a proles did not support
this, although the chlorophyll a measurements did show some biomass, respectively
1.07µg/l and 0.7µg/l (table D.5).
Micromonas plays an important role in the Arctic ecosystem as the baseline pri-
mary producer; a function carried out by cyanobacteria in more temperate ocean
provinces (Balzano, 2012; Sherr, 2003). In addition to being phototroph, a study by

Comparison between the two datasets
Including the secondary dataset provided a basis for discussing the results obtained
from the original dataset. Although the poor quality of the original Illumina dataset
precluded in depth analysis of community diversity and composition across water
masses, the secondary provided information on the identity and abundance of tax-
onomic groups and pointed out questions that could be addressed with the original
dataset in the future.
Although similar, the two datasets had some dierences that need to be considered
when compared. Firstly, the lack of a pre-ltration step during sampling on Hur-
tigruten & Helmer Hanssen meant that all organisms larger than 0.45 µm were col-
lected. This was seen from the original dataset by four of the 17 OTUs being assigned
to copepods. Another dierence that could aect the results was the use of dierent
PCR primer sets during marker gene amplication.
Possibilities for further studies using the original dataset
The results obtained from the Micro Fun data on both water masses and community
composition emphasize the relevance of the samples collected on Hurtigruten & Helmer
Hanssen, and the potential for further work with these data. The fact that the latter
dataset was composed from a large number of stations (24 versus the 7 Micro Fun
stations) gave a much better spatial basis for assessing the water regime. The samples
collected in the eastern part of the archipelago are especially interesting, since few
studies have been carried out in these locations due to logistical challenges. The fact
that the Hurtigruten & Helmer Hanssen data showed clear dierences in water masses
between east and west Svalbard underlines the importance of studying the eastern
region further.
The other aspect of the Hurtigruten dataset was the temporal scale. The collection of
samples during multiple cruises over a large part of the summer poses a great oppor-
tunity for assessing changes over several months. The hydrographic results from this
study showed a pattern of warming and freshening during the summer. This is inter-
esting in regard to both water masses and community composition - and ultimately the
correlation between the two - in a future warmer Arctic. The CruiseScience project
provides new and updated information on summer conditions, while most previous
studies on the microbial communities in Svalbard have been carried out during spring
conditions.

5. Conclusion
The rst partial goal of this study was to determine the water masses dominating
at the sampled stations at the time of sampling, which was in part achieved on the
original dataset by looking at temperature and salinity measurements at the sampling
depths. A clear pattern appeared when comparing western stations to eastern stations,
dominated by respectively AW and ArW. The entire water columns were investigated
for the secondary dataset, from which a more in-depth understanding of the origin of
the water at each sampling station could be obtained for further interpretation. The
main ndings were that Hinlopen and Hornsund were inuenced by TAW at the time
of sampling, while Erik Eriksen Strait was clearly dominated by ArW.
The second partial goal was to assess the diversity and community composition of
marine microbial eukaryotes in the Hurtigruten & Helmer Hanssen samples. Due to
poor quality sequence data, only an assessment of presence/absence of a small number
of species could be carried out. By inclusion of the secondary dataset, it was how-
ever possible to address some of the interesting ndings in the original dataset: for
example the presence of the "pelagophyte clone DH114 3A43". Diversity was instead
investigated of the secondary dataset, from which the most interesting ndings were
the blooms of two Gyrodinium species and the pelagophyte, all identied in the origi-
nal dataset.
The third partial goal was to assess potential dierences in community composition
between AW and ArW. The only clear observation made on potential dependency of
water mass was the high abundance of Micromonas polaris in the ArW and LW inu-
enced fjords Bockfjorden and Wijdefjorden, suggesting an association with cold tem-
peratures in accordance with previous studies. The original dataset however showed
great potential for such patters, given that a sequence dataset of better quality could
be obtained.

5 Conclusion
In conclusion, this study managed to investigate the community composition in two
datasets, although these were too incomplete to draw clear correlations to the water
regime. Comparing the species composition of the summers of 2013 and 2018 ad-
dressed a number of interesting ndings, some of which were in accordance to previous
studies. Most remarkable was the high abundance of heterotrophic Gyrodinium species
in 2013 both at sampling stations dominated by ArW and at stations inuenced by
AW. High relative abundances of the green chlorophyte Micromonas polaris in Erik
Eriksen Strait supported previously documented preference for ArW by this species,
and also notable was the abundance of the unidentied pelagophyte in samples from
both years. Further studies on this species will be valuable for assessing its role in the
ecosystem, and especially the nding of the species in the brand-new samples from
2018 underlines the importance of this.
Due to the predicted continuation of AW input to the waters around Svalbard, the
marine ecosystems will doubtlessly experience changes in terms of diversity and com-
position. Studies on the dependencies of community composition on water masses
therefore continue to be of high relevance, as an aid to understand and predict pos-
sible consequences of an increased warming of the Arctic for microbes as well as for
higher trophic levels. The ndings in this study underline the importance and relevance
of studying the marine communities around Svalbard, and emphasize the potential of
further studies on the CruiseScience project.

C Appendix - data processing
C. Appendix - data processing
C.1. OTU pipeline incl. unsuccessful tests
Running the data through the pipeline that is most often used by the biology depart-
ment at UNIS was not very successful, as the vast majority of the sequences were
weeded out. The pipeline primarily uses QIIMETM (v1.8.0 (Caporaso et al., 2010)),
which is open-source and therefore relatively developed. In qiime, the sequences were
joined, with 35% error in overlapping region of 50 bases, demultiplexed according to
barcodes only and dereplicated, still keeping singletons. Clustering at the end (at 98%)
resulted in only 18 OTUs. At every step of the pipeline, several alternative ways to
join, demultiplexed etc. were tested, including changing restrictions in the commands
and including other tools such as USEARCH (Edgar, 2010) and VSEARCH (Rognes
et al., 2016). The pipeline shown in gure C.1 was the one nally used to produce the
18 OTUs. Below are some of the issues connected to the work with the pipeline; most
issues were presumably due to the poor sequence quality.
One of the major errors observed was the cut-out of the reverse primer plus about
50 bases into the sequence in the R2 reads, without cutting o the attached barcode.
This presumably happened during the rst join_paired_ends.py step, probably
further inuencing the processing of already dicult data.
A whole other pipeline using the updated version of QIIME, QIIME2TM (Caporaso
et al., 2010) was attemped used, but failed since the function
split_libraries_fastq.py used in QIIME2 requires a specic barcoding le dif-
ferent from the mapping le at hand.
