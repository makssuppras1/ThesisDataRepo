Abstract 
 
 
I
Abstract 
Research on production of fuels from biomass is becoming more and more prominent as the stockpile 
of fossil fuels is becoming scarcer with time. Sugar beets are a promising source for production of 
first generation biofuels, as they contain large amounts of sugars which can be fermented to 
bioethanol. For the production of bioethanol from sugar beets to be feasible though, the sugar extract 
-which is the liquid obtained after pre-treatment- has to be concentrated. The general objective of this 
project is to define a membrane technology method to efficiently concentrate such stream.   
The extraction of the sugar from the sugar beets consisted of several steps. After slicing the beets, an 
alkaline treatment with sodium hydroxide was conducted to decompose the cell wall structure. 
Subsequently, an acidic treatment with nitric acid was performed to release the sugar fraction. During 
the acidic treatment, the sucrose was hydrolysed to glucose and fructose, which were the main 
components of the liquid sugar fraction.  
The potential of nanofiltration to perform such concentration was evaluated in this thesis, as it is an 
inexpensive method compared to evaporation. For that purpose, three commercial nanofiltration 
membranes were selected (NF270, Desal-5 DK and BW30). The process was evaluated in four steps. 
Firstly, the performance of the three membranes was evaluated and the ones offering the best results 
were selected. Subsequently, pressure (40, 50 and 60 bar) and temperature (ambient temperature and 
50 °C) were also optimized, in terms of flux and retention through the membrane. Lastly, the effect 
of the pH of the feed solution (pH 2, 4 and 6) was also assessed. 
The results revealed that concentration with NF270 at 60 bar, 50 °C and pH 2 provided the highest 
flux together with a high retention of sugars. The results also suggested that nanofiltration is a 
promising technique to concentrate sugars in liquid streams from pretreatment, although the 
concentration variables probably need to be optimized depending on the biomass and pretreatment 
conditions.

1 Scope 
The aim of this MSc thesis is to develop and optimize a method to concentrate a liquid stream 
containing glucose and fructose obtained by extraction from sugar beets by using membrane 
technology.  
The development of the method requires a literature search to determine possible membranes, which 
can be used for concentration of the sugar extract. From the literature search the three best membrane 
candidates would be selected for further evaluation. The selection should be based upon the 
membrane performance and the technical knowledge of the membrane group at DTU. The 
optimization will be performed on the parameters: membrane type, pressure, temperature and pH of 
feed solution. 
The candidates will first be evaluated against each other to determine the best membrane type to 
concentrate the sugar extract. Subsequently, the most promising membrane’s performance will be 
investigated for operation at various pressures. The optimal pressure should afterwards be applied in 
the concentration of the sugar beet extract at ambient and high temperature. Lastly, the pH of the feed 
solution from acidic to neutral will be considered.

2 Sugar beets 
Sugar beets are from the species Beta vulgaris where also red table beets and fodder beets are from. 
The beet is buried in the ground and is 15-35 cm long whereas the leaves are above the ground. The 
sugar in the sugar beets are produced by photosynthesis and only produced the first year. The sugar 
is stored in the beet and if the sugar beet is not harvested in time, the sugar would be used to produce 
seeds [1]. 
The sugar counts for 15 to 21 % of the total weight of the sugar beets which corresponds to a sugar 
content of 12 % to 20 %. The beet itself consists of 75 % water and 25 % dry mater where 75 % of 
the dry matter is sugar and 5 % is pulp [1]. The pulp consists of cellulose (22-30 %), hemicellulose 
(22-30 %), lignin (1-3 %) and pectin (24-32 %) [2].The sugar is the value added compound in the 
sugar beet while the pulp is primary used as animal feed [1]. 
The main sugar in sugar beets is sucrose (342 Da) which is also known as table sugar. Sucrose is a 
dimer that consists of monomer glucose (180 Da) and fructose (180 Da). The monomers are linked 
together at the reducing ends of both monomers. The C2 of fructose is linked to C1 of the glucose 
and forms a α-1,β-2 glycosidic bond [3], see Figure 1. Thus, sucrose is a sugar which does not contain 
a reducing end and therefore have limited reactivity. The low reactivity is beneficial since sucrose is 
a transport and storage molecule in plants [4]. 
 
 
Figure 1 – Formation of sucrose from glucose and fructose [5]. 
 
Sucrose in sugar beets is mainly used to produce table sugar in Europe. Production of table sugar can 
be divide into the following activities [1]:

Sugar beets

1. Delivery of sugar beets to production site. 
The storage time for sugar beets is essential, since the sugar content decreases rapidly 
when cuts have been made in the beet due to microbial attacks which decompose the 
sugars [6], [7]. The sugar beet fields are therefore always within a 30 km radius to the 
factory [1]. 
2. Washing of sugar beets. 
Sugar beets are washed for soil, and weeds and stones are removed [1]. 
3. Slicing of the beets. 
The beets are cut into cossettes to enhance the extraction [1]. 
4. Extraction of sugar. 
Sugar is extracted by hot water of approximately 70-80 °C in a countercurrent mode [1]. 
5. Purification of raw sugar juice. 
Beside of the extracted sugar, impurities have also diffused into the raw sugar juice. These 
impurities are commonly removed by carbonation followed by filtration with activated 
carbon [1].   
6. Concentration of sugar juice. 
The raw sugar juice is concentrated by evaporation from a concentration of approx. 13 % 
sugar to 65-70 % [1]. 
7. Crystallization. 
Sugar crystals are crystallized by the addition of already crystallized sugar and the 
solution consists of syrup and sugar crystals [1]. 
8. Centrifugation. 
Centrifugation is used to separate crystals from syrup. The sugar is cleaned by washing 
with hot water to give white crystals [1]. 
9. Drying. 
A hot-air dryer dehydrates the sugar crystals and afterwards the crystals are cooled [1] 
10. Packaging. 
Finally sugar is packed and shipped to the customers [1]. 
Instead of production table sugar from sugar beets, sugar beets are also a good source to produce 
bioethanol due to the high content of sucrose. Sucrose can easily be converted to ethanol in a 
fermentation with microbes as the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae and since fossil fuel stockpile is 
soon depleted new ways to produce fuels have to be investigated [8], [9].

3 Production of bioethanol 
The need for alternative energy sources was motivated by the oil crisis in the early 1970s, where 
many countries wanted to be less dependent on oil import. Today the reason is to decrease the 
emission of greenhouse gasses and be independent on the oil countries [10] together with overcoming 
the energy demand in the future where the fossil fuel is limited [11]. Since the sources for fossil fuels 
are decreasing the European Commission has planned that 20 % of the fossil fuel in the transport 
sector should be replaced by alternative fuels by 2020 [6]. To reached the goal diesel engines could 
use diesel with 5-10 % bioethanol, combustion engines could use a mixture of petrol with 10-85 % 
bioethanol or bioethanol could replace petrol to vehicles completely [12]. 
Bioethanol produced from biomass could be a promising alternative since bioethanol is a renewable 
fuel with a positive effect on the environment [11], [13]. Biomass crops also function as a carbon 
sink due to the photosynthesis which add positive to the carbon balance [14].  
Biomass is cheap and in large quantities all over the world, but lignin, pectin and inhibitors have to 
be removed by a pre-treatment before fermentation which is complicated together with the 
purification of the ethanol afterwards [11], [13].  
Above 10 million gallons of bioethanol is each year produced from starch extracted from maize, sugar 
produced from sugar canes and sugar beets in the world [14]. In Europe the main feedstocks to 
produce biofuels are sugar beets, wheat and rapeseed [10]. 
Bioethanol produced from food crops is called first generation biofuel whereas non-food crops are 
the feedstocks used for the second generation of biofuel. Bioethanol from food crop is easy to convert 
whereas more steps are needed to produce second generation biofuel as seen in Figure 2. Sucrose 
from sugar beets can be used directly in fermentation, whereas starch, which consists of chains of 
glucose monomer linked together with α-1,4 glycosidic bond, have to be hydrolyzed before 
fermentation as seen in Figure 2. Lignocellulosic biomass is used to produce second generation 
biofuels and needs to be pre-treated to disrupt the biomass before the sugar chains can be hydrolysed. 
Lignocellulosic biomass consists mainly of cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin. Cellulose consists of 
glucose monomers like starch, but is linked together by β-1,4 glycosidic bond (as compared to the 
alpha bond in starch). Hemicellulose is constructed with the monosaccharides xylose, arabinose, 
rhamnose, glucose, mannose and galactose and the uronic acids glucuronic acid and galacturonic 
acid. Lignin has a complex structure and consists of phenolic monomers.

Even though less production steps are needed for first generation biofuel, a competition between food 
and energy is created when using food as biomass for the production bioethanol. This can give an 
inflation of the food price. Thus, to produce first generation biofuel can have some ethical issues [14].

The current bioethanol production from lignocellulosic biomass is not economical feasible with the 
current technologies compared to the prices from the petroleum industry even though the feedstock 
is cheap and available in large amount. The reason is that the production of bioethanol has a high 
energy demand together with water and chemicals. Another disadvantage, which adds to the costs, is 
the large amount of wastewater from the production [13], [15].  
 
3.1 Bioethanol from sucrose 
There are four main groups of bioenergy crops: Cereal crops, sugar producing crops, lignocellulosic 
biomass and oilseed crops for biodiesel. Sugar producing crops are sugar beets and sugar canes. The 
two sugar crops are grown in different areas and therefore the production of bioethanol from sucrose 
can be adopted to most of the world. Sugar canes are grown in warm temperate to tropical areas 
whereas temperate areas as Denmark are optimal for sugar beets [14].  
Production of sugar beets is already common in the European crop rotation and the high sucrose 
content of app. 170 g/kg wet beet makes them suitable for bioethanol production. After extraction of 
sucrose the sugar beet pulp contains mostly cellulose, hemicellulose, pectin and traces of lignin and 
the liquid raw juice from the sugar beets contains mostly sucrose (13 %(wt./vol.) and water [1], [9], 
[15], [16]. The liquid raw juice does not contain many inhibitors which makes production of

bioethanol from sugar beets advantageous compared to other biomass. Another advantages are the 
sugar from sugar beets can be used directly after the extraction in the fermentation because the 
enzyme invertase, found in for example the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae [9], degrades sucrose to 
fructose and glucose, whereas starch and cellulose have to be degraded by enzymes to its monomer. 
This also reduces the production cost, but the pH is an important factor to control [6]. 
A detailed flow diagram of the different unit operations in bioethanol production from sugar beets 
are depicted in Figure 3. The initial process is the same as for producing table sugar. First the sugar 
beets are sliced into cossettes and sugar is extracted with hot water at 70-80 °C. The pulp is separated 
from the raw juice and sold as animal feed after it has been dried. The raw juice has to be concentrated 
before fermentation. The concentration can be conducted with evaporation or with nanofiltration as 
illustrated in Figure 3. The concentrated juice is afterwards used in the fermentation to produce 
bioethanol. Ethanol is then purified and concentrated before the final product is sent on the market. 
Some sugar could also be lost in the permeate if nanofiltration is used. To reduce loss of sugar the 
permeate is purified and also used in the fermentation [12]. In case the concentration of the 
saccharides in the permeate is very low and in order to maintain the feasibility of the process, the 
recovery of the saccharides in the permeate should be evaluated.  
Sugar beets are planted in spring and harvested in autumn [1]. This gives challenges to the storage of 
sugar beets or the sugar juice, because sugar beets have low storability and high risk of microbial 
attack which reduces the sugar content. Therefore the storage of the sugar beets are obtained by 
concentrating the sugar juice to reduce storage volume and avoid microbial growth [6], [14]. As 
mentioned the concentration can be conducted with membrane technology instead of evaporation as 
evaporation requires large amount of energy and sugar can decompose by the heat [17]. The aim of 
this project is to evaluate which membrane and operation condition would be preferable to apply. 
Thus, an introduction to membrane technology will be given hereafter.

4 Membrane technology 
The interest for membrane technology has increased in the last decades, because this technology is 
cost efficient and has a high separation efficiency to produce a product with high quality compared 
to other unit operations as e.g. evaporation where a phase change is necessary [18], [19].  
Membranes are used for many purposes as for wastewater treatment, concentration of products, 
recovering of product, and as a reactor [20] in industries as e.g. the pharmaceutical-, dairy-, waste 
water and petroleum [19].  
In membrane processes, a feed steam is led into the membrane, where the feed is divided in two 
streams called retentate and permeate as depicted in Figure 4. The retentate is retained by the 
membrane whereas the permeate passes through the membrane. The permeate stream is removed 
from the process on the other side of the membrane. The product can either be the permeate stream 
as in wastewater treatment or the retentate stream as in concentration processes. In purifications 
processes, the product stream depends on the molecular weight of the impurities compared to the 
product and can therefore be both the retentate or the permeate [21]. 
 
Figure 4 – Schematic illustration of membrane process. The dashed line indicates the membrane. 
There are both advantages and disadvantages for using the membrane technology, which are listed in 
Table 1. Membranes have a general low energy consumption compared to other separation processes 
and the footprint (area the unit occupy in a production hall) for the modules is small. The membrane 
module is easy to scale up, however the up-scaling factor is linear, meaning that if the double flow is 
needed the active membrane area have to be twice the size [20], [21]. 
Membranes are in general selective by molecular weight as compared to many other separations 
methods where phase change is prerequisite, but the selectivity can be low depending of the 
composition of the feed and the membrane material. Nevertheless, many different membrane 
materials with different configurations are in the market to suit the many demands [21]. 
Throughout the separation with membranes, the fouling and concentration polarisation will, in most 
filtrations, increase, leading to a decline in the flux. The time required for the separation will therefore 
increase. The consequence of fouling is a lower performance in the actual separation, reduction of

the membrane lifetime and the need for cleaning procedures. However, the separation process is 
conducted at mild conditions and without any additional chemicals, which is an advantage to other

have advantages compared to batch processes. Economically, the process would be cheaper and a

Membranes for separation of liquid mixtures are traditionally divided into microfiltration (MF), 
ultrafiltration (UF), nanofiltration (NF) and reverse osmosis (RO). The difference in these membranes 
is mainly the molecular cut-off as seen in Figure 5. The difference in molecular cut-off also indicates 
which application the membranes can be applied in. RO is primary used in wastewater treatment and 
desalination, where small molecules as salts have to be retained. NF is also used in separation of 
organic acids and wastewater treatment, but the cut-off size is larger than RO. Thus, NF is also used 
to concentrate sugar solutions. UF is used in the pharmaceutical and dairy industry as it is used for 
protein concentration or fractionation [21]. Tight UF, NF, and RO membranes operate at high 
pressures over 2 bar [20]. MF membrane has the largest cut-off size and is for example used in 
sterilization of solutions [21]. MF and loose UF operates at low pressure of 1-2 bar [20]. 
In this thesis the focus has been on NF membranes and the rest of the thesis will mainly concerns NF 
technology.

4.1 Membrane modules 
Industrially mainly four membrane modules have been developed, which all have the purpose of 
packing a large area of membrane in less volume as possible [21]. They are in most cases operated in 
cross-flow mode, whereas in the laboratory dead-end filtration is normally applied due to high 
operational control, easy to clean and change membrane. The different modules will be explained 
below and is compared in Table 2. 
 
4.1.1 Plate-and-frame module 
The configuration of the plate-and-frame module is a flat sheet in a frame as seen in Figure 6. The 
plate-and-frame module is developed so the feed flows across the membrane. The permeate is 
collected in the middle of each plate or outside the plate depending on the ease of cleaning. Many 
plates can be assembled together and the permeate from each plate is collected through the permeate 
outlet. Gaskets are needed in this configuration to keep the construction sealed, but still the main 
problem is that it leaks. In the industry the plate-and-frame module is mainly used in electrodialysis 
and in pervaporation. However, if fouling is severe this module is preferable [24]. 
==PAGE:17==
Membrane technology

Figure 6 – Illustration of plate-and-frame module [24]. 
4.1.2 Spiral-wound module 
The spiral-wound module consists of membrane envelops as depicted in Figure 7, where each 
envelope is comprised of spacer and membrane. Each envelope is wounded around a permeate 
collection pipe. The feed flows across the membrane whereas the permeate flows in a spiral to the 
collection pipe in the middle. If only one envelope is used, the pathway for the permeate to the 
collection pipe would be longer than if more envelops are used to obtain the same membrane area. 
This would result in a large pressure drop from the initial starting point of permeate to the collection 
pipe. It is therefore important to have more than one envelope in the module. The spiral-wound 
module is placed inside a pressure vessel [24].

4.1.3 Tubular module 
The tubular module is made of porous or fiberglass support where the membrane is located on the 
inside of each tube as seen in Figure 8. The feed is led inside each compartment in the module and 
the permeate is collected on the outside of the module. Tubular modules are very resistant to 
membrane fouling, are easy to clean and is therefore primary used in ultrafiltration systems [24].  
 
Figure 8 – Illustration of tubular module [25]. 
4.1.4 Hollow fiber module 
The hollow fiber module (Figure 9) consists of many hollow fibers which are enclosed in a pressure 
vessel. The model can have two configurations; the shell-side feed and the bore-side feed 
configuration. In the shell-side feed configuration the feed is on the outside of the hollow fiber which 
allows for a large membrane area because the membrane is located at the outer diameter of the hollow 
fiber. However, the fiber’s wall has to be thick to overcome the hydrostatic pressure [24]. If a fiber 
breaks in the module the whole module has to be replaced, which is a huge disadvantage when using 
a hollow fiber module. 
In the bore-side feed the feed flows inside the fibers and the permeate is collected outside the fibers. 
To minimize the pressure drop inside the diameter of the fibers is usually larger than the one used in 
the shell-side feed configuration. Hence, the membrane area is smaller. The pressure drop inside the 
hollow fiber also effects the operation pressure as the module cannot be operated above 150 psig. 
Pressure drop on the permeate side is instead a problem for the shell-side feed configuration if the 
feed solution contains relative impermeable compounds. Thus, the permeate cannot leave the fibers 
[24]. 
Concentration polarization is better controlled in the bore-side feed configuration compared to shell-
side feed configuration as no stagnant flow or dead-zones are impossible [24].

4.1.5 Comparison of modules 
The main reason for developing the modules was to obtain a high area-to-volume ratio. Of the four 
modules the hollow fiber module has the highest ratio followed by the spiral-wound module. The 
tubular module has the third highest ratio whereas the plate-and-frame module have the lowest ratio 
[24]. In Table 2 are the most important criteria for selecting a module listed. Manufacturing cost is 
always an important factor as it affects the purchase price of the actual module. Hollow fiber module 
is the cheapest module to produce but only in large quantities, since the advanced technology to 
manufacture the fibers is limited to a few producers. Many manufacturers produce the spiral-wound 
module resulting in a cheap price due to the competition. The most expensive modules are the plate-
and-frame modules together with the tubular modules [24].   
Another important criterion for selecting a module is the concentration polarisation and fouling 
control. Hollow fiber modules require extensive pre-treatment of the feed to eliminate all particulars 
otherwise the concentration polarisation and fouling will be too severe. Consequently, hollow fiber 
modules are only used in reverse osmosis and nanofiltration where the cut-off is small. However, 
backflushing can be applied to reduce fouling in the hollow fiber module. Plate-and-frame modules 
and tubular modules can handle separations where fouling and concentration polarisation are severe 
due to the design of the modules [24]. 
Since membrane separation is a pressure-driven process the modules have to withstand high pressure 
and the pressure drop on the permeate side effects how quickly the permeate can leave the module 
[24]. 
All material can be used in the plate-and-frame-, spiral-wound and tubular modules, however the 
hollow fiber is limited in material because of the technology of producing the fibers [24].

4.2 Membrane structure 
Synthetic membranes are produced either by organic matter as polymer or inorganic matter as 
ceramics. The structure of the membranes can further be divided into two groups as illustrated in 
Figure 10. Symmetric membranes consist of cylindrical porous, porous and dense membranes and 
have a thickness of 10-200 µm. The mass transfer resistance is dependent on the thickness of the 
membrane. Thus, the thicker the membrane the higher is the resistance through the membrane. 
Asymmetric membranes consist of a porous membrane and a membrane with a dense skin layer and 
a porous support. The invention of asymmetric membrane was a huge breakthrough in the membrane 
technology as the mass transfer resistance in the membrane only is depending on the thickness of the 
skin layer, which typically is in the range 0.1-0.5 µm. The support layer has a thickness of 50-150 
µm and contain large pores. The support gives the membrane its mechanical strength. With 
asymmetric membrane with the dense skin layer the high selectivity is maintained while the flux is 
increased [21], [24].

4.3.2 Fouling 
Fouling results in the loss of membrane permeability [28]. Fouling is the largest disadvantage in 
membrane technology, because the membrane after a certain time is blocked and has to be cleaned 
or changed, which is costly. Fouling also introduces higher operation costs since the running time is 
increased as the flux decreases [20]. In order to minimize the extent of fouling the foulants and the 
fouling mechanisms have to be identified. However, this is extremely difficult as several of the feed 
solution consists of many different components [29]. Many studies have been conducted over the last 
decades to identify foulants and analysis the fouling mechanism to better understand and overcome 
fouling [20], [29]–[31].   
The three well-known fouling mechanisms are pore blocking, gel layer formation and adsorption 
[28]. Fouling can be external or internal in the membrane. The external fouling is normally reversible 
and can be removed by cleaning with or without chemicals whereas internal fouling is more difficult 
to remove and can be irreversible [20]. 
Many factors as the feed composition, pH, temperature, ionic strength, membrane composition and 
operation modes have influence on fouling. Therefore, the optimal condition for the separation of 
membranes also have to be determined to minimize fouling [20]. 
 
4.3.2.1 Pore blocking 
Pore blocking occurs when particles of a similar size of the membrane cut-off get stock inside the 
membrane [20] as seen on Figure 12. 
There are three different kinds of pore blocking; the particles block the pore partially, the particles 
block the pore completely and a combination of these. Pore blocking is the initial step to gel layer 
formation [20]. 
 
4.3.2.2 Gel layer formation 
Gel layer formation or also called cake layer fouling is occurring when particles larger than the 
molecular cut-off deposit outside the membrane, see Figure 12. When the gel layer is formed on top 
of the membrane, it will control the mass transfer and become an extra membrane. The permeability 
of the gel layer is determined by the size, structure and deformability of the particles, but also the 
pressure applied in the separation [20]. As pressure is increased the thickness of the gel layer will 
increase too [21].

4.3.2.3 Adsorption 
Solute can adsorb on the surface of the membrane which causes the permeate flux to decline. 
Adsorption can occur both inside the pores resulting in directly blocking or narrowing of the pores 
and on the surface membrane which would increase the osmotic pressure [21] (See Figure 12). The 
extent of fouling caused by adsorption is dependent on the membrane properties and the properties 
of the feed solution. Both chemical and electrical attraction between the membrane and the solute 
could result in fouling [20], [32].

4.3.3 Concentration polarization 
The phenomenon concentration polarization, CP, is concentration of solute near the surface of the 
membrane, thus the concentration of the solute is higher at the surface of the membrane compared to 
the concentration in the bulk liquid, as seen in Figure 13. In a certain distance, δ, from the membrane 
the solute concentration will start to increase. The increase is due to a convective flow near the 
membrane and is descripted as J·c. On a distance > δ the solute concentration will be the same as in 
the feed solution, cF. CP will result in a back diffusion of the solute from the δ-layer to the bulk liquid 
which can be explained by Fick’s law. The concentration of solute near the membrane, cm, will 
increase until steady state is reached. Steady state is reached when the convective flow (J·c) equals 
the back diffusion of the solute plus the solute flow (J·cp) through the membrane [20], [21], as seen 
in Eq. (4-2). In Eq. (4-2) is J solute flux, c is the solute concentration in δ-layer, cp is the concentration

4.3.4 Fouling mitigation 
Fouling can be reduced with different methods. Pre-filtration of the feed solution before the 
membrane module would remove larger particle, but also changing the pH of the feed solution can 
reduce fouling as neutral compounds do not absorb to the membrane surface as easily. The membrane 
can also be pre-absorb with an easily removed compound. The hydrophilicity of the membrane also 
effects the fouling as protein foulants are easier absorb and more challenging to remove on a 
hydrophobic membrane surface compared to a hydrophilic surface [21]. 
The mass transfer through the membrane can also be increased by changing the flow pattern in the 
system. In dead-end filtration the flow is perpendicular on the membrane which causes high 
concentration polarization and fouling as a cake layer is build (see Figure 14). The flux will decrease 
==PAGE:25==

Page 19 of 81
as the cake layer is build. By changing from dead-end to cross-flow filtration the flow is instead 
tangential on the membrane (see Figure 15). A cake layer would not be build due to the turbulent 
flow which would reduce concentration polarization and fouling [21]. 
Lastly, the membrane can be cleaned either hydraulically, mechanically, chemically or electrically 
depending on the type of membrane and foulant. Backflushing is typically applied as hydraulic 
cleaning where the flux through the membrane is reversed by releasing the feed pressure. Sponge 
balls are applied in mechanical cleaning. However, this cleaning method can only be used in tubular 
membranes. For chemical cleaning acidic solutions, alkaline solutions, detergents, enzymes, 
disinfectants and gases can be used. The cleaning time is very important due the resistance of the 
membrane. Electric cleaning can be applied when the foulant is charged as the foulants would be 
repulsed by the membrane [21], [33].

4.4 Operational conditions impact on membrane separation 
The efficiency of the separation with membrane technology is effected by operation conditions such 
as the transmembrane pressure (TMP), the temperature of the operation and the pH of the feed 
solution. 
 
4.4.1 Pressure 
A NF process is a pressure-driven membrane process where the pressure normally is high (10-60 bar) 
to overcome the osmotic pressure that can be very high in this process. The flux increases with 
increasing pressure and at a certain point a maximum flux would be reached [21]. The extent of 
fouling can be a problem at the high pressure, especially in dead-end filtrations, as the thickness of 
the gel layer increases with increasing pressure. At high pressure the retention can also decrease due 
to the concentration driving force is increased [21].

4.4.2 Temperature 
When the temperature is elevated the flux through the membrane increases due to swelling of pores, 
higher permeability and decrease in viscosity of the feed solution [34].  
The polymer in the membrane re-orientate when the temperature is elevated as the polymers become 
more flexible which changes the structure of the membrane as the pores swell. Pore swelling 
accomplishes a higher flux through the membrane of both the solvent and the solute. Thus, the 
rejection will also decrease with increasing temperature [19], [26]. The change in the membrane 
structure by temperature elevation will affect uncharged solutes mostly, but a charged solute near the 
isoelectric point could also be affected [26]. However, swelling of the membrane does also decrease 
the membrane lifetime [35]. 
The diffusion of solvent into the pores can be thought as a reaction where the activation energy for 
the reaction/diffusion has to be overcome before the solvent can permeate the membrane [34]. The 
Arrhenius equation (Eq. (4-5)) indicates that by raising the temperature the permeability through the 
membrane would increase [19], [26], [36]. Ai in Eq. (4-5) is the pre-exponential factor, Rg is the gas 
constant, T is the temperature, Ei is the apparent activation energy component i for transport in and 
to the membrane and Pi symbolise the permeability for component i [21], [36].

4.5 Nanofiltration 
Nanofiltration membranes are in between reverse osmosis and ultrafiltration in cut-off size, thus 
mono-valence ions are to some extent not rejected whereas multi-valence ions are. Nanofiltration 
membranes as NF270, NF90, Desal-5 DK, Desal-5 DL or BW30 have also shown nearly 100 % 
retention with low molecular weight compounds as glucose and has the possibility to separate organic 
compounds with different molecular weight from each other [19], [35], [39]. 
NF membranes are used in industries as wastewater treatment, food and biotechnology and 
pharmaceutical [40] and NF modules applied in industry are mainly spiral-wound or tubular modules. 
Spiral-wound has a high area-volume ratio and is cheaper compared to tubular modules, but tubular 
modules have possibility for backflushing to overcome fouling and have low fouling potential [39]. 
To mitigate fouling in spiral-wound modules spacer is incorporated to enhance turbulent flow and 
avoid dead-zones [41]. Selected manufacture of commercial available NF membranes [40] are listed

Two competitive phenomena occur when pressure is applied in NF processes. The solvent flux

The lifetime and chemical resistance of a NF membrane are dependent on the application and the 
fouling. To ensure a long lifetime and high resistance the correct membrane material has to be chosen 
together with optimal operation conditions and cleaning chemicals. Cleaning is necessary if fouling 
is present in the separation. Cleaning is harsh for the membrane and causes deterioration as the 
cleaning is primarily alkaline or acidic [35]. The efficiency of the cleaning is determined by the 
recovery of the water flux or the change of resistance of the membrane [35]. Even though cleaning 
affects the lifetime of the membrane this parameter is not considered important for the efficiency of 
the cleaning [35]. 
Alkaline cleaning uses mostly chemicals as sodium hydroxide or sodium carbonate which removes 
organic foulants or inorganic colloids. It has been shown by 50 % of all foulants are organic 
compounds that can be found in the nature which indicates the importance of alkaline cleaning in 
membrane technology [35]. Together with the alkaline chemicals, a surfactant is applied usually. The 
surfactant can be anionic or non-ionic and should remove fat and prevent already removed foulants 
to adhere to the surface of the membrane again [35]. The pH of the alkaline treatment is usually higher 
than the pH the membrane can resist for longer time without degrade. The cleaning time therefore has 
to be quick to not damage the membrane. However, multiple alkaline cleaning will destroy the 
membrane over time. It have been showed that in special cases the flux through the membrane have 
increased after alkaline cleaning without the rejection decreased [35]. In this project exploration of 
alkaline treatment will be evaluated as the foulant could be sugar which is an organic foulant. 
Acidic cleaning is at pH 1-2 and the low pH is obtained by using nitric acid, citric acid or phosphonic 
acid. Acidic cleaning is used when precipitated salts have to be removed as they are easier dissolved 
in an acidic environment. Acidic cleaning also destroys the membrane, as pH is lower than the 
membrane can resist. As for the alkaline treatment the cleaning time has to be short [35]. 
If the foulant is a polysaccharide enzymatic cleaning is a possibility. The operation condition is mild 
and will not damage the membrane, but it only removes one kind of foulant. If the fouling is caused 
by biofoulants biocides have to be used. These biocides are typically based on an oxidizer as chlorine 
or ozone. The oxidation also damage the membrane to some extent [35].

5.3 Pretreatment of sugar beets  
The pretreatment of the sugar beets was conducted at Strukturforsyningen’s own facility and not at 
DTU. The sugar beets were sliced and treated with 0.7 %(wt./wt.) sodium hydroxide adjusted with 
water to a 0.5 M solution. The alkaline treatment decomposes the structure of the sugar beets. 
Subsequently, an acidic treatment of 2.2 %(wt./wt.) nitric acid was performed to release the sugar 
fraction. The concentration of acid is adjusted to 0.31 M in the solution with the sugar beets. The 
acidic treatment degrades sucrose to both glucose and fructose in the ratio 1:1 and the final pH of the 
solution is 2 0.2. Beside the sugar fraction, the extract also contains approx. 4.7 g/L sodium ions 
and 9.2 g/L nitrate. Finally, the solution was filtrated with a filter cloth of 200 µm before shipped to 
DTU. After arrival the solution was filtered again with a 0.2 µm filter and 0.02 %(wt./wt. dry matter) 
sodium azide was added to the solution to ensure that no microbial growth could occur.

5.6.5 Cleaning of membrane  
A cleaning procedure to NF270 was suggested where 0.25 M sodium hydroxide was used as cleaning 
agent. The vendor of NF270 suggests an alkaline cleaning as first step as an acidic cleaning can 
destroy the membrane [73]  Luo et al. has succeeded to recover must of the initial water permeability 
by cleaning with sodium hydroxide after separation of soy sauce [74]. The cleaning was conducted 
after the final water permeability test (after filtration and water rinsing) had been performed. 
200 mL 0.25 M sodium hydroxide was added to the cell and 100 mL was filtered through the 
membrane at ambient temperature, 60 bar and 200 rpm agitation. After 100 mL of solution was 
permeated, the filtration was ended and the cleaning solution was left in the cell for half an hour.

5.7 Reducing sugar assay  
The concentration of sugars in the sugar extract, retentate, permeate and washing water were 
determined by the reducing sugar assay. Such assay is based on the fact that the acid hydrazides react 
with the reducing sugars. The most reactive acid hydrazides have been found to be 4-
hydroxybenzhydrazide (PAHBAH) [75]. The reaction between both results in the formation of yellow 
anions which can be detected at 410 nm in a spectrophotometer. 
 
5.7.1 Preparation of samples 
The standard curve was determined from glucose solutions of 0, 0.05, 0.15, 0.23 and 0.3 mM, which 
were prepared in milli-Q water. The standard curve was repeated on every microplate measured as 
the colour reagent could change a bit for every time. An example of the standard curve to indicate the 
accuracy and the influence of the colour reagent is given in appendix 10.1 and 10.2 respectively.  
Before analysis, all samples were filtrated with a 0.45 µm filter to remove prospective particles (as 
the sugar extract is obtained from sugar beets). The sugar extract and the retentate were diluted 
10,000-fold whereas the permeate and the washing water were diluted 100-fold in milli-Q water (in 
order to fit the measurements in the range of the calibration line). Exceptions were the permeate 
samples from the pH experiments at pH 4 and 6 and the 6, 8 and 10 mL permeate samples from the 
optimal experiment which were diluted 1,000-fold and 12 and 14 mL permeate samples from optimal 
experiment which were diluted 10,000-fold.

6.1 Water permeability 
Water flux was measured in two rounds. First at low pressures of 1, 2, 3 and 4 bar and afterwards at 
high pressure of 20, 40 and 60 bar. It was necessary to conduct the measurement in two rounds as 
two different cells were used. The water flux as a function of the pressure is depicted in Figure 22 for 
the three membranes NF270, Desal-5 DK and BW30. In theory the water flux should be proportional 
with the pressure with the water permeability as the proportional factor. However, as seen in Figure 
22 the fluxes began to decline at 20 bar. The decrease of the flux was likely due to compression of 
the membrane at high pressure. The pore size in the membranes decreases in the compression which 
decreases the flux. The compression was most severe for Desal-5 DK and BW30 compared to NF270.

at low pressure (1-4 bar) to obtain the correlation between the water flux and the pressure. As

6.3 Selection of membrane 
The three chosen membranes, NF270, Desal-5 DK and BW30, were all evaluated under the same 
conditions i.e. at TMP of 60 bar, ambient temperature and using the feed solution from the same batch 
(pH 2). The results from these experiments were used to select which membrane(s) offered the best 
performance, and therefore should be utilised in further evaluation of the optimal process conditions.  
 
6.3.1 Separation  
The decrease in permeate fluxes during concentration of the feed solution on the retentate side, along 
with the inherent fouling -concentration polarisation- in the filtration are depicted in Figure 24 for the 
three membranes. 
The highest flux was mostly observed in the NF270 membrane, followed by Desal-5 DK and BW30 
at 1, 3 and 7 mL obtained permeate volume. This order of the average permeate fluxes was also found 
by Lyu et. al., as they investigated how the rejection of monosaccharides, monophenols and acids 
with different NF and RO membranes were affected by different operation conditions [19]. However, 
there is no significant difference between the initial average fluxes at 1 mL permeate obtained for the 
membranes as indicated in the letters (one-way ANOVA analysis) on Figure 24. A significant 
difference in the average fluxes was first seen after 3 mL of permeate was obtained. Indeed, the 
NF270 membrane performance was found to be general significantly different from BW30, whereas 
Desal-5 DK was not significant different from any of the two other membranes.  
Desal-5 DK had initially a lower permeate flux than NF270, but as more permeate was collected, the 
difference in the fluxes decreased. This tendency was also exhibited by Luo et. al. when they 
evaluated the membranes to concentrate dairy waste at 40 bar TMP, 2000 rpm and 35 °C [82]. At 9 
mL obtained permeate the flux for Desal-5 DK was higher than NF270. 
NF270 and BW30 laid parallel with each other, given the almost same percentages of decrease from 
the initial flux to the flux at 7 mL, whereas Desal-5 DK does not decrease as much as NF270 and

6.3.5 Selection 
The membrane chosen for further evaluation was selected based on three criteria; high solvent flux, 
high retention and low fouling. NF270 and Desal-5 DK provided similar results for all the three 
criteria, whereas BW30 was more affected by the resistances and had a lower average permeate flux. 
Nevertheless, the retention for BW30 was higher than the two other membranes.  
NF270 was selected (instead of Desal-5 DK) because even though the permeate fluxes were similar, 
the tendency in Figure 24 indicates a NF270 in general has a high flux. The low water contact angle 
for NF270 also indicates that the flux would be higher for this membrane. 
BW30 was also selected due to its high retention, as it is quite important to minimize the amount of 
sugar that is lost during filtration.

6.4 Selection of operational pressure 
To select the optimal pressure, the concentration of the solution was performed with the NF270 and 
BW30 membranes at a TMP of 40, 50 and 60 bar. The high pressures were selected as the initial and 
final osmotic pressure at 20 °C were estimated to be app. 12 bar and 24 bar respectively (see appendix 
10.3 for calculations). As the osmotic pressure increases with increasing concentration of the sugar 
extract (which also increases during concentration), the selected pressures should be considerable 
higher than the osmotic pressure. Another reason for increasing the pressure considerably compared 
to the initial osmotic pressure was the effect of concentration polarization. Indeed, the osmotic 
pressure can be higher than the calculated values as the concentration near the membrane was 
normally higher than in the bulk solution. 
 
6.4.1 Separation  
The decrease of fluxes as a function of obtained permeate volume for the NF270 and BW30 
membranes at TMP of 40, 50 and 60 bar are plotted in Figure 28.   
Initially there was no significant difference between the fluxes at different pressures for NF270 
whereas BW30 has a significant high flux initial at 60 bar compared to the flux at 40 bar.   
If the performance of the membranes at the same pressure is compared to each other, the highest flux 
was observed for NF270, which was expected after results from previous evaluation of the selection 
of membrane(s), cf. section 6.3. The highest flux was observed at 50 and 60 bar for concentration 
with NF270 followed by the concentrations at 40 bar with NF270 and 60 bar with BW30. The 
concentration at 50 bar with BW30 had the second lowest flux and the concentration at 40 bar with 
BW30 was the one providing the lowest flux. Tang et al. evaluated the effect of pressure on the flux 
during concentration of humic acid with different membranes, including NF270 and BW30. They 
also showed that NF270 at all pressures had a higher flux than BW30, concluding that the membrane 
was more hydrophilic, as explained in the previous section [31]. 
For the individual membrane the flux increased with increasing pressure for BW30 whereas the flux 
only increased from 40 to 50 bar for NF270 and was the same at 50 and 60 bar. In theory a maximum 
flux is obtained at a certain pressure, at the flux is in competition with the compaction of the 
membrane and the fouling [21]. This indicates that the maximum flux for NF270 is obtained at 50 
bar at ambient temperature and a pH 2 of feed solution, which is more clearly depicted in Figure 29 
as the flux decline between 50 and 60 bar.

no difference in fluxes were observed at ambient temperature at these pressures for NF270. 
A temperature of 50 °C was selected to be able to detect a possible increase in flux, as the theory 
predicts, as compared to the experiments at ambient temperature. This same temperature had been 
used by Sjöman et al. to separate xylose from glucose with NF membranes, and it was reported that

6.5.1 Separation  
Theoretically the flux should increase with increasing temperatures, as the pores swell, the 
permeability increases and the viscosity of the solution decreases. As seen in Figure 33, the flux 
increased in all cases with increasing temperature (just except the case at 9 mL of obtained permeate 
at concentration at 60 bar with BW30).  
The largest increase was detected at 60 bar with the NF270 membrane, where the flux initially app. 
doubled from ambient temperature to 50 °C. The flux obtained with NF270 at 60 bar was primarily 
significantly larger than any of the other fluxes obtained at different operation conditions. Previously, 
no difference in the fluxes at 50 and 60 bar were observed at ambient temperature. Nonetheless, at 
50 °C, a significant difference in the fluxes at 50 and 60 bar were observed for the NF270 membrane. 
At 50 °C, the flux was dependent on the pressures as the flux increased with increasing pressure. At 
ambient temperature the flux achieved at 60 bar was possibly in competition with the compaction of 
the membrane and therefore no increase in flux was detected compared to the flux achieved at 50 bar. 
As the temperature was raised, the pores swelled, which balanced the resistance from the compaction 
of the membrane and the flux would increase.   
At 50 bar the flux increased initially app. 50 % as compared to the flux at ambient temperature and a 
significantly difference was observed when the temperature was elevated.  
At 50 °C, the lowest flux was observed for the concentration with the BW30 membrane at 60 bar. 
Even though the flux increases from ambient temperature to 50 °C it was only significantly different 
at 3 and 5 mL of obtained permeate. Furthermore, the average flux at 9 mL of permeate obtained at 
50 °C drops below the value of the flux at ambient temperatures. Thus, the increase in flux by the 
temperature could not overcome the resistances as osmotic pressure, concentration polarisation and 
fouling at the final part of the concentration. 
It is clear from Figure 33 that a rise in temperature affected the NF270 membrane more than the 
BW30. The effect of change in viscosity of the feed solution would be the same for both membranes. 
Hence, it was the swelling of the pores and the increase in the permeability -both dependent on 
membrane type- which caused the larger increase in flux for NF270.

more solute could deposit inside the pores -as fouling-, which was the tendency seen in Figure 35 for 
BW30 at 60 bar and NF270 at 50 bar. A higher loss in water permeability was expected at higher

membrane. However, as the pressure was increased, the gel layer formation could possibly counteract 
the extent of internal fouling. This was the reason for larger difference in the extent of fouling at 50 
bar for NF270 and no difference at 60 bar. Hence, at a certain pressure the swelling of the pores would 
not affect the extent of fouling for NF270, presumably due to gel layer formation. 
The extent of fouling increased with increasing pressure for NF270 at ambient temperature. This 
phenomenon was also detected at 50 °C.

6.5.5 Selection 
As previously, the results are evaluated against the criteria of high solvent flux, high retention and 
low fouling, in order to decide which level of temperature was the optimal one.  
Elevation of the temperature to 50 °C increased the flux through the membrane for both NF270 and 
BW30. However, the increase for BW30 was not as significant as for NF270. The retention decreased 
for both membranes as the temperature was increased, but the retention was still high enough to retain 
most of the sugar. The internal fouling was not significant different from each other, but a general 
tendency of high fouling with high temperature due to pore swelling was noticed.  
As BW30 performs poorly on all parameters -except on retention- when the temperature was raised, 
this membrane was discarded. By selecting 50 °C as the optimal temperature together with a TMP of 
60 bar, the fouling would not increase, the flux would be higher and the time required for the 
concentration would decrease. 
6.6 Selection of pH in the feed solution 
The pH of the feed solution changes the performance of the membrane, as the charge density of the 
membrane can change with pH. In this section the results for pH values of 2, 4 and 6 (of the feed 
solution) were evaluated. The pH range from 2 to 6 was selected based on the initial pH of the feed 
solution (pH 2) and considering that the solution should not have a too basic pH -when the solution 
is meant to be used for fermentation into bioethanol. All experiments were performed with the 
membrane NF270 at a TMP of 60 bar and 50 °C, as these conditions have previously been proven to 
be optimal.

6.6.1 Separation  
As the pH was increased, the flux during filtration increased concomitantly, as depicted in Figure 37. 
The NF270 membrane’s isoelectric point has been evaluated by different authors to be app. 3-5.2 
[50], [65]. Thus, at pH 2 the membrane was positively charged, at 4 it was neutral and 6 it was 
negatively charged. The sugars in the feed solution were not affected by the change in pH as the 
sugars pKa’s are above 12 at 25 °C [85]. Even though the pKa decreases with increasing temperatures, 
as dissociation of an hydrogen bond requires energy, the pKa would still be high at 50 °C as 
monosaccharides are quite stable [50].  
 
Figure 37 – Decrease in average permeate flux during concentration of the feed solution (pH 2, 4 and 6) at a

different from each other with a statistical significance of p < 0.05 (one-way ANOVA with a Tukey’s test). If

The results were not as expected as the lowest flux was expected at pH 4 since the membrane would 
be neutral, and high at pH 6 and 2 due to the electrical repulsing in the pores resulting in pore swelling. 
Swelling of the pores has also been reported by Nordvang et al. at alkaline pH [38]. However, the 
effect of pH on membrane performance is complex, as pH can alter the membrane and the solute and 
therefore the interaction mechanism between them [37]. Both Boussu et al. and Lin et al. measured 
the zeta-potential for NF270 at various pH. They both found that the zeta-potential at pH 6 was -15 
mV whereas the potential at pH 3 it was 5 mV. Hence, more negatively charged functional groups 
were attached to the membrane compared to positively charged functional groups. The electric 
repulsing in the pores were therefore large at pH 6 compared to pH 2 which was the reason for a high 
flux at pH 6 [86], [87].

7 Conclusion 
The results shown in this study suggest that nanofiltration is a promising technique to concentrate 
sugar from sugar beets in biorefinery. From the optimization experiments it was proven that NF270 
was the most suitable membrane to concentrate the sugar extract from sugar beets at operation 
conditions at 60 bar, 50 °C and pH 2. 
The flux was primarily influenced by the temperature and to some extent the pressure. As the 
temperature was increased from ambient temperature to 50 °C the flux also increased significantly. 
At ambient temperature no difference in the flux was seen at 50 and 60 bar. However, at 50 °C a high 
flux was observed at 60 bar compared to 50 bar. 
No large difference was detected in the retention when the pressure and temperature were varied. 
However, as the pH was increased, the retention decreased significantly. 
NF270 can be purchased as a pilot plant scale spiral-wound module which is a compact, inexpensive 
module suitable to treat high feed solution volumes with a good control. 
The concentration process could be improved by attempting to increase the flux. Increasing the flux 
could be achieved by increase the membrane hydrophilicity or mitigate fouling. Moreover these 
improvements would also prolong the lifetime of the membrane.

Further work 
 
  
 
Page 68 of 81
8 Further work 
There are different aspects of the project that could be further improved in the next future, particularly 
considering that this process has to be implemented at a large scale. Some of them are: 
 
1. Optimization of the cleaning of the membrane: In this project the membrane was cleaned with 
0.25 M sodium hydroxide. This cleaning procedure was conducted to prove that it is indeed 
possible to recover at least part of the initial water permeability. However, the cleaning 
procedure was not optimized due to the limiting time. By attempting to optimize the cleaning 
procedure, it would be possible to recover some more or even all of the initial water 
permeability of the virgin membrane. However, as the cleaning of the membrane can be a 
harsh process for the membrane, the performance of the membrane has to be evaluated 
afterwards. The search for the optimal cleaning procedure should also involve the 
optimization of some operational variables inherent to the cleaning i.e. time, temperature and 
pressure. Different cleaning agents, also the commercial mixtures, should also be evaluated. 
As the solution contains high amounts of sugars, an interesting alternative could be the use of 
enzymatic cleaning -instead of alkaline cleaning-, which is typically used to organic foulants. 
2. Scale-up: The next step would be to evaluate the process at pilot plant scale. In industrial 
processes, the flow regime that is normally used is cross-flow -as it helps to remove fouling-. 
Therefore, the first step would be to shift the operation mode –at small scale- from lab to pilot 
plant. The performance of the membrane is expected to be different from the one in the 
laboratory.  
3. Incorporation of strategy for energy optimization: In this project the energy requirements have 
not been evaluated. Calculation of the energy needed to operate the process at 50 °C should 
be conducted. As the concentration is performed at 50 °C, heat integration to pre-heat the feed 
solution from e.g. the fermenter would be a possibility which should be evaluated.  
 
The evaluation of the whole process of producing bioethanol from sugar beets should result in a large 
scale plant. Thus, the requirements to integrate the nanofiltration unit in large scale have to be 
investigated together with a proper maintenance procedure for the nanofiltration membrane.

10.5 Water permeability and comparison range 
The membranes used for all the experiments conducted had to be within ±10 % of the average water 
permeability of the membranes for all experiments conducted at the same pressure, before the results 
from the experiment could be compared to other results. The average water permeability and the 
comparison interval of the water permeability are given in Table 15. The water permeability given in 
Table 15 is not strictly the true water permeability, but the water flux obtained at that specific pressure 
(expressed in the unit of water permeability). The water permeability is not dependent on the pressure. 
However, due to compression of the membrane at high pressure, the pore size decreases which 
modifies the membrane. Hence, the water permeability decreases indirectly with increasing pressure 
and in this case it is more relevant to indicate fluxes rather than water permeabilities.
