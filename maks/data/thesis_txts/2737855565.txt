Abstract
The shallow water equations model a wide variety of physical phenomena.
Examples
include flooding and dam breaks, which being able to accurately predict is becoming
increasingly important [3].
In this thesis, we solve the shallow water equations using the nodal discontinuous Galerkin
finite element method (DG-FEM) [16]. We design a solver with the ability to capture
shocks through various stabilizing techniques, namely slope limiting and artificial viscosity.
Furthermore, high-order cubature integration is used for anti-aliasing purposes and the
solver has support for curvilinear geometry.
The solver is implemented in two different software frameworks.
The nudg MATLAB
framework [20] and the libParanumal c++ framework [2]. The MATLAB framework is
simple to work with but it lacks performance. Hence, the MATLAB framework is used
to develop and test several different techniques before the best ones are implemented in
libParanumal. Specifically, a solver with averaged modal decay artificial viscosity [31] is
chosen as it performs the best in terms of removing the oscillations without being overly
dissipative.
The solver is validated for smooth problems through convergence tests. The tests show
high-order convergence thus verifying the theoretical properties of DG-FEM. The shock-
capturing capabilities of the solver are challenged through various test cases and the results
are compared against results the from existing literature. The excellent agreement further
supports the correctness of the solver.
We highlight that libParanumal is designed for massively parallel computations on multiple
GPUs.
This is tested by running simulations on the largest European supercomputer
LUMI with up to 128 GPUs in parallel. The great scaling results show the potential of
the solver for solving large scale problems and computing in high resolution.

Following this, the novel contributions of this thesis are presented. Finally, a thesis outline

1.1
Motivation
A large variety of increasingly important problems can be modelled by the shallow water
equations. Some of the more obvious examples include flooding, dam break problems and
flow in rivers. However, since the shallow water equations are based on the assumption
that the length scale is far greater than the depth scale, and not that the water is shallow
in an absolute sense, less obvious applications such as tidal movements in the ocean can
also be considered [29].
Figure 1.1 shows the catastrophic forces created in a real-world dam break. Being able
to accurately predict the forces involved is crucial. Furthermore, As oceans rise and the
climate becomes more unpredictable [3], these types of events are only becoming more
common.
To construct the most accurate and cost-effective solver we turn to the discontinuous
Galerkin finite element method (DG-FEM). The high-order accuracy, geometric flexibility
and the highly parallelizable nature of the algorithm are all attractive features of the
scheme.
High-order schemes have several benefits compared to their low-order counterpart. Typ-
ically, they achieve a higher accuracy, at a lower computational cost, and with much less
numerical dissipation [30].
The high-order nature of DG-FEM does come with its challenges when it comes to cap-
turing discontinuities in the solution. A shock is an example of a discontinuity that often
arises in shallow water problems. For example, a shock travelling downstream is formed
in a dam break. When simulating shocks, solvers may produce excessive smearing of the

shock or produce oscillations, sometimes enough to crash the simulation [16]. This is why
shock-capturing is emphasized in the title of this thesis.
Many large complicated flow problems require more resolution than what a single processor
is capable of to obtain a satisfactory result. We exploit the parallelizable nature of DG-
FEM and construct a solver capable of running on multiple GPUs in parallel. Thus the
only bottleneck is the number of GPUs available.
The recent surge in deep learning
means that multi-core hardware suited for parallel problems is more readily available than
ever and supercomputers with thousands of GPUs can be used to to run simulations of
previously unimaginable sizes. In this thesis, in addition to running our code on the DTU
cluster, we will run our code on LUMI, the fastest supercomputer in Europe and the fifth
fastest in the world [25].
1.2
Literature Review
DG-FEM has been the focus of an increasing number of research papers in the last decades.
The basics of DG-FEM are thoroughly described in [16], which also provides the MATLAB
framework nudg [20], which is used in this thesis for the MATLAB part of the code. For
parallel multi-GPU computing we instead use the C++ framework libParanumal [2], which
is built on the same theoretical background as the one provided in [16]. In [19], they use
libParanumal to design a nodal DG-FEM simulator for room acoustics. They show the
great multi-GPU scaling potential of DG-FEM.
One of the biggest challenges with DG-FEM is handling discontinuous solutions robustly,
while retaining the high-order accuracy. Three overall strategies have been proposed to
achieve this: limiting, filtering and artificial viscosity.
Limiting consists of a strategy to detect oscillations and then flattening or carefully re-
constructing the solution at the troubled area. A widely used slope limiter is given in [4],
which we also include in this thesis. Many other limiters have been proposed throughout
literature, e.g. [17] and [23]. However, they all face challenges with retaining high-order
accuracy.
The basics of filtering is described in [16]. The idea is that oscillations are mostly present in
high frequency modal values, so a filter is applied which systematically lowers the higher
frequencies. Filters are generally easy to implement, but they are often dependent on
correctly choosing a number of parameters. Furthermore, to completely avoid oscillations,
one needs to be overly dissipative as even small oscillations can be detrimental. In the
case of shallow water, oscillations can lead to negative water heights, which in addition to
not having any physical meaning, also leads to a number of mathematical issues.
Artificial viscosity is similar to limiting in the sense that it also consists of a strategy
to detect oscillations, but it differs when it comes to how to deal with the oscillations.
Artificial viscosity is added as an extra dissipative term in the governing equations. An
overview of different artificial viscosity methods is presented in [31]. Theoretical justifica-
tions for three of the methods are given in [21], [18] and [32]. A different approach uses
neural networks to detect oscillations [24]. The different artificial viscosity methods are
generally both robust and preserve high-order accuracy. One drawback is that they are
expensive. They place extra constraints on the time step and each time step becomes
more expensive, however, this becomes less of a problem as computational hardware be-
comes increasingly powerful. Another drawback is that the methods unavoidably smear
shocks, at least slightly. This is once again mitigated by computational resources, as extra
resolution reduces the smearing.

There is a substantial amount of existing literature concerning the shallow water equations.
[29] uses the finite volume method, but since it essentially is a low-order version of DG-
FEM, many of the same principles can be applied.
One example is the approximate
Riemann solvers used to construct the numerical flux, which can also be used with DG-
FEM. Using DG-FEM to study the shallow water equations has been the subject of several
studies. In [8], a solver is constructed which uses slope limiting to capture shocks. In [1],
another solver is implemented on a GPU, with the additional properties that it is well-
balanced, i.e. it preserves trivial steady-state solutions, and it is also capable of handling
wet-dry fronts. Both of these properties are a result of careful treatment of the numerical
flux and the slope limiting. The amount of existing research means that there are a number
of simulations we can use to validate our own implementation, as each article typically
presents a few test cases to showcase their solver. Furthermore, [5] presents a large number
of test cases including analytical solutions.
1.3
Contributions
With an overview of the existing literature in mind, we are ready to present the contri-
butions of this thesis. As the title suggests, the main contribution is a new shallow water
DG-FEM solver capable of handling shocks on a large scale. The individual techniques
used are described in various publications, but to our knowledge, no one has yet combined
them to create a similar solver.
While we implemented the codes in the existing frameworks nudg and libParanumal,
neither had a solver specifically for the shallow water equations. Additionally, neither
had support for the stabilizing techniques considered in this thesis. The current standard
version of libParanumal also does not support curvilinear elements, which we implemented
with anti-aliasing considerations.
To our knowledge, no one has previously solved the shallow water equations on multiple
GPUs, only solvers built on single CPU/GPU systems are available in the literature. The
Message Passing Interface (MPI) aspects involved, when transferring information between
GPUs, substantially complicates the implementation.
While the basic MPI structure
already existed in libParanumal, extra considerations were necessary when adding artificial
viscosity.
Finally, we have validated our solver against existing results from the literature and
through scaling tests we have shown the potential of solving the shallow water equations
on multiple GPUs.
1.4
Overview
The rest of the thesis is structured as follows. The shallow water equations along with
some of its key properties are derived in chapter 2. Chapter 3 presents the nodal DG-
FEM spacial discretization in sufficient detail for readers unfamiliar with the technique.
Chapters 4, 5 and 6 present extensions to the basic DG-FEM solver, leading to an even
more accurate solver also capable of handling curvilinear geometry.
The temporal discretization is presented in chapter 7. The chapter consists of multiple
schemes with different properties and a condition for a stable time step.
Chapter 8 explores techniques for shock-capturing. Specifically, it describes the techniques
of slope limiting and artificial viscosity, which both aid in stabilizing solutions involving
shocks.

Discretization of Space
To discretize space, we will use a discontinuous Galerkin finite element method (DG-FEM)
[16]. DG-FEM has the benefits of being geometrically flexible, highly parallelizable, and it
provides high-order accuracy. As the name suggests, the method consists of dividing the
computational domain into a discontinuous set of elements. Figure 3.1 shows the structure
of a possible solution on top of the divided domain.
Figure 3.1: A possible DG-FEM solution. The domain is split into elements which are
discontinuously connected with each other.
The parallelizability comes from the locality of the method, as each element only depends
on itself and the edges shared with neighbouring elements. Thus allowing us to perform
operations on multiple elements simultaneously.

element and finite volume methods might recognize DG-FEM as a clever way of combining
the strengths of the two methods.

Curvilinear Elements
When using straight-sided elements, one major issue is how to represent curved geometries,
say a circle. One can approximate the circle with straight sides and this approximation
gets better the more resolution is added.
However, this increases computation times.
Furthermore, it decreases the convergence rate in some cases. For these reasons we will
consider curvilinear elements.
In this chapter, we first discuss the creation of curvilinear elements. Subsequently, we
discuss what changes are required to perform computations with these elements.
5.1
Creation of Curvilinear Elements
Figure 5.1 shows how an element neighbouring a curved boundary is updated.
First,
the face nodes are moved onto the curvilinear boundary, and then the interior nodes are
updated according to the deformation.
Figure 5.1: The two steps involved in the creation of a curvilinear element. The blue
circles show the previous location of the interior nodes.
Due to the possibility of minor numerical errors in meshing, we first ensure that the vertices
are exactly on top of the curved boundary. Then, the relevant face nodes are pushed onto
the curved boundary, such that they have a Gauss-Lobatto distribution by arc length.
Finally, to maintain the stable numerical properties obtained in section 3.1.3, we blend
the boundary deformation onto the volume nodes, see [11] for details.
Pushing the face nodes onto the boundary becomes an involved procedure for boundaries
more complicated than a simple circle. For this reason, we will mention that there is
an alternative approach that utilizes the high-order meshing in Gmsh [10]. Gmsh is able
to construct an equidistant high-order grid for curvilinear elements, which can then be
interpolated onto the Gauss-Labatto-like nodes we constructed in 3.1.3. This approach
was pursued in [26], however, when using curvilinear elements, an error of the order of
10вИТ7 was introduced, which might be due to small issues with this approach. Thus we did
not pursue this method ourselves.

8.2.4
Smoothing
The obtained artificial viscosity is piecewise-constant for averaged modal decay and en-
tropy viscosity. The dilation-based viscosity does vary within each element, but the dis-
continuous nature of all three methods may cause oscillations. Thus we take one additional
step in smoothing the viscosity. As in [31], we do a simple C0-smoothing which consists
of the following three steps:
1. Interpolate the ¬µav-values onto the nodal point set corresponding to N = 2.
2. For each point that is shared between multiple elements, i.e. on an edge or vertex,
set that point equal to the average between all elements shared.
3. Interpolate the averaged ¬µav-values back to the Np original points.
The interpolation is done with Vandermonde matrices. The procedure is identical to the
interpolation to cubature nodes, see chapter 4.

when you keep the workload constant for each processor. Thus we keep the work per GPU
constant while varying the number of GPUs, instead of keeping the total work constant

Conclusion
In this thesis, we have constructed a shock-capturing DG-FEM solver for the shallow water
equations and introduced the necessary theory and methods.
In chapter 8, we introduced two approaches for stabilizing the solution, the most interesting
of which was the artificial viscosity type stabilizers. Here we introduced three different
ways of determining the viscosity and in chapter 11 concluded that the modal decay
averaging was the most promising of the three.
We implemented the solver in two different frameworks. The MATLAB framework nudg
was used to develop and test several different techniques before the best ones were imple-
mented in libParanumal. In Chapter 9, we presented a DG-FEM algorithm that accounts
for the necessary data transfer in a multi-GPU solver. The Libparanumal implementation
allowed us to perform computations on a much larger scale.
In chapters 10 and 11, we validated our solver, both using an analytical solution and some
well-known test cases from the literature which include shocks. We observed that our solver
could handle shocks and got the expected convergence rates for the analytical solution.
Also in chapter 11, we showed the importance of curvilinear elements as we observed
different flow patterns when using curvilinear elements instead of affine elements.
The performance of our libParanumal implementation was investigated in chapter 12,
using both strong and weak scaling. Here we observed great scaling in both cases, even
when moving up to 16 GPUs for the weak scaling and 128 GPUs for the strong scaling.
We concluded that our solver scales greatly if the problem size is big enough to justify the
number of GPUs.
13.1
Further Work
As mentioned in chapter 11, we have only implemented the modal decay averaging viscosity
in libParanumal. For more detailed comparisons of the three different ways of calculating
the viscosity, it would be useful to compare them on larger meshes. This would require
all of them to be implemented in libParanumal.
In chapter 5 we introduced a way of constructing curvilinear elements. One issue with this
approach is that each new curvilinear boundary requires a separate function to construct
the curvilinear elements. A more general approach for how to construct the curvilinear
elements would make the solver more versatile. As already mentioned in chapter 5, [26]
utilizes the higher order element function in Gmsh. This approach had some accuracy
issues, but in [26] multiple possible causes for these accuracy issues are presented.
The performance of the solver was analyzed in chapter 12. However, We did not measure
the number of floating point operations performed. This would be of great interest, since
it would reveal whether or not we can extract more performance out of the same hardware
with further optimizations. Furthermore, a more detailed investigation of the different
kernels would be interesting, to explore where we could perhaps optimize the code. Finally,
the strong scaling of the algorithm was only investigated for polynomial order N = 6, it
would be interesting to investigate if the great results persist for different polynomial
orders.
In chapter 2 we stated that the shallow water equations using the primitive variables are
also well-defined for h = 0m. Extending the solver with the ability to handle wet/dry

fronts would allow it to be useful in more applications such as flooding. There are several
aspects one has to consider when implementing dry fronts, one approach can be found in
[1].
Another issue, which we have not investigated, is whether our solver is entropy-conserving.
This is important in certain applications to achieve correct results. One approach is de-
scribed in [9] and it requires a different formulation of the shallow water equations.
We also did not look at constructing a well-balanced solver, i.e. a solver that is guar-
anteed to preserve a trivial steady-state solution, e.g. ensuring that no sudden movement
is created in a lake at rest.
A well-balanced solver is created in [1] and [9].

the formulation of the shallow water equations and the flux type.
