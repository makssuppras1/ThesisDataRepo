Abstract 
Aquaculture is an important part of the World’s food production and it is expected that the industry 
will grow as a food sector in the future, due to several advantages of producing aquatic animals in 
aquaculture. However, bacterial infection that can cause huge losses in fish and crustacean farms 
are a major concern. Many marine pathogenic bacteria can be controlled with antibiotics, but the 
use of antibiotics in the aquaculture is not sustainable due to the risk of antibiotic resistance 
development. Therefore, alternative strategies are needed. The focus of this thesis is the emerging 
aquaculture pathogenic bacteria Tenacibaculum. Although vaccines have been developed, they are 
not very effective, and a better understanding of the pathogen and possible alternative disease 
control strategies are needed. So, the aim of this study was to characterize virulence factors in 
Tenacibaculum strains isolated from gills of sea bass, sea bream, and Senegalese sole from Spanish 
and Greek sea farms and to investigate whether probiotic bacteria from the Roseobacter group 
could antagonize Tenacibaculum.  
Samples were taken from diseased fish and a total of 48 strains were received.16S rRNA gene 
amplification and sequencing revealed that 15 out of 48 isolates belonged to the Tenacibaculum 
genus, and four isolates belonged to the flavobacterial genus Joostella. The Tenacibaculum and 
Joostella isolates as well as Tenacibaculum type strains were whole genome sequenced for 
identification of virulence genes, and since biofilm formation can be an important virulence factor, 
the biofilm formation ability of the isolates and type strains was quantified using a biofilm 
formation assay. To test whether probiotic Phaeobacter species could be used to antagonize 
Tenacibaculum and Joostella isolates, an agar-based antagonism assay was used. Furthermore, it 
was tested whether a Phaeobacter piscinae biofilm could inhibit the growth of the Tenacibaculum 
and Joostella strains.  
Analyzing the whole genome sequences revealed that the Tenacibaculum strains mainly encoded 
virulence factors associated with iron acquisition, biofilm formation, heavy metal resistance, 
collagen degradation, and reactive oxygen species resistance. All of these are consistent with the 
symptoms in diseased fish. Calculation of average nucleotide identity between the isolates revealed 
that there was a high genetic diversity between isolates from the local outbreak in Greece. 
Therefore, the antigenic diversity among the isolates could be high, indicating that developing 
vaccines that offer protection against all isolates is challenging. T. maritimum and T. skagerrakense 
were the most pronounced biofilm formers among the flavobacteria tested, but a Phaeobacter

Akvakultur er en voksende fødevaresektor, og det er forventet, at flere fisk vil blive produceret i 
fiskefarme i fremtiden i takt med, at Verdens befolkning vokser. Der er dog flere problemer

bæredygtigt at bruge antibiotika i fiskefarme, da det øger risikoen for udvikling af antibiotika 
resistens. Derfor bør der udvikles alternative strategier, så brug af antibiotika kan undgås. 
Bakterie-slægten Tenacibaculum er blevet almindelig i flere fiskefarme, og formålet med dette 
projekt var at karakterisere virulens faktorer i Tenacibaculum stammer isoleret fra gælder af

1. Introduction 
 
Aquaculture is a growing part of the World’s food production. In total, about 80 million tons of 
aquatic animals are produced each year in aquaculture globally (FAO, 2018), and the first-sale 
value of aquatic animals from aquaculture exceeds 160 billion US dollars (FAO, 2016). At present, 
the amount of fish produced in aquaculture is about equal to the amount of fish caught in wild 
(Figure 1), but it is expected that the amount of fish produced in aquaculture will exceed the amount 
of fish caught in wild in the future. Also, a wider range of fish species are expected to be included 
in the future aquaculture (FAO, 2016). Production of animal protein and fatty acids in aquaculture 
is more sustainable compared to several other types of animal protein productions, and it is an 
important part of feeding the growing World population (Engle et al., 2017; FAO, 2016).  However, 
when fish are produced in aquaculture, there is a risk of economic losses due to bacterial infections. 
Vibrio, Aeromonas, and Yersinia are common pathogens in aquaculture, but flavobacteria from the 
Tenacibaculum genus are emerging is several fish farms (FAO, 2016; Done et al., 2015; Avendaño-
Herrera et al., 2006a).

Traditionally, marine bacterial pathogens have been controlled with antibiotics, but the use of 
antibiotics in aquaculture (or other food production systems) is not sustainable due to the increased 
risk of antibiotic resistance development (Done et al., 2015; Marti et al., 2014). Vaccines have been 
developed against several major pathogens and have been very successful as pathogen control, e.g. 
in the Norwegian salmon industry. However, for some (emerging) pathogens, vaccines are not 
effective and also, at the younger larval stages, the fish immune system is not fully developed and, 
hence, vaccines are inefficient (Gudding & Muiswinkel, 2013; NVI, 2017). 
Therefore, there is a need to develop alternative strategies in order to control pathogens in 
aquaculture. One of these strategies could be the use of probiotic bacteria. A promising candidate is 
bacteria from the Roseobacter group due to their production of the antimicrobial compound 
tropodithietic acid (TDA) (Brinkhoff et al., 2004). Furthermore, bacteria from the Roseobacter 
group have strong adhesive properties and can be attached to surfaces in aquaculture as probiotic 
biofilms (D'Alvise et al., 2010).  
One group of emerging fish pathogens for which no efficient vaccines are available, are 
flavobacteria from the genus Tenacibaculum (Småge et al., 2018). In this study, a number of 
Tenacibaculum isolates and type strains were characterized, and to investigate whether TDA-
producing roseobacters had an antimicrobial effect against these Tenacibaculum spp., assays that 
tested the inhibitory effect of roseobacters against Tenacibaculum were used. 
Prior to the start of this project, 48 bacterial isolates from sea farms in Spain and Greece were 
received. All isolates were obtained from diseased fish. Furthermore, four Tenacibaculum type 
strains were included in this study. To confirm if the isolates belonged to the Tenacibaculum genus 
or other flavobacterial genera, 16S rRNA gene amplification and sequencing was carried out. To 
gain a better understanding of why Tenacibaculum are pathogenic to fish and a problem in 
aquaculture, the bacteria belonging to the Tenacibaculum genus or other flavobacterial genera were 
whole genome sequenced in order to identify potential virulence factors and antibiotic resistance 
factors. 
Biofilm formation can be an important virulence factor of pathogenic bacteria, since bacteria in 
biofilms have an increased resistance against the host immune defense, and biofilms help the 
bacteria establish a chronic infection (Phillips & Schultz, 2012). It is known that some 
Tenacibaculum species form biofilms, and it has been suggested that biofilm formation on the 
surface of fish could be a prerequisite for infection with Tenacibaculum (Levipan et al., 2019;

Avendaño-Herrera et al., 2006b). Therefore, the biofilm formation of the Tenacibaculum isolates 
and type strains was quantified with the O’Toole-Kolter biofilm formation assay.  
To investigate whether bacteria from the Roseobacter group had an antagonistic effect against 
Tenacibaculum spp., both a spot inhibition assay, where roseobacters are spot inoculated on a

It was expected that the whole genome sequencing of the Tenacibaculum isolates and type strains 
would give a greater understanding of the virulence and resistance factors of these pathogens and if 
the Roseobacter group bacteria indeed had an antagonistic effect against Tenacibaculum, it would 
be a step in the right direction towards antibiotic-free aquaculture and the implementation of 
roseobacters in aquaculture should be considered.

2. Background 
 
2.1 General characteristics of the Tenacibaculum genus 
 
Bacteria from the Tenacibaculum genus are Gram-negative bacteria that belong to the class 
Flavobacteria that belongs to the phylum Bacteroidetes. The Tenacibaculum genus was previously 
called Flexibacter (Suzuki et al., 2001). Flavobacteria are well-known filamentous and non-spore 
forming bacteria. They live in extremely diverse habitats and several flavobacteria are well-known 
pathogens, especially in the aquatic environment, and diseases caused by flavobacteria have been 
known since 1922. Besides Tenacibaculum, other emerging pathogenic flavobacteria are 
Chryseobacterium, Elizabethkingia, and Ornithobacterium (Loch & Faisal, 2015). Tenacibaculum 
are rod-shaped bacteria, up to 30 µm long, and are motile by gliding (Avendaño-Herrera et al., 
==PAGE:14==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
14 
 
2006a; Yoon et al., 2005; Piñeiro-Vidal et al., 2008a; Pérez-Pascual et al., 2017). However, 
spherical cells can be observed in old cultures (Piñeiro-Vidal et al., 2008c). They are halotolerant, 
grow under aerobic conditions, and are positive for catalase, oxidase, and degradation of casein 
(Loch & Faisal, 2015; Piñeiro-Vidal et al., 2008c; Yoon et al., 2005; Avendaño-Herrera et al., 
2005a). 
Tenacibaculum spp. thrive in several marine niches including sediments, surfaces of tanks, and tidal 
flats and in some cases the natural microbiota of marine animals. The fact that some Tenacibaculum 
spp. are part of the natural microbiota of some marine animals could indicate that some bacteria 
from the Tenacibaculum genus are opportunistic pathogens (Avendaño-Herrera et al., 2006a; Yoon 
et al., 2005; Piñeiro-Vidal et al., 2008a). 
 
2.2 Tenacibaculum in aquaculture 
 
Many different fish species are susceptible to infection with Tenacibaculum spp. These fish species 
include red and black sea bream, yellowtail, seabass, turbot, Atlantic salmon, Chinook salmon, 
Japanese flounder, Dover sole, Senegalese sole, gilt-head seabream, tub gurnard, rainbow trout, 
striped trumpeter, pacific sardine, yellow-eye mullet, greenback flounder and northern anchovy 
(Avendaño-Herrera et al., 2006a; Piñeiro-Vidal et al., 2008c; Castro et al., 2007; Avendaño-Herrera 
et al., 2006b; Kolygas et al., 2012; Handlinger et al., 1997). Several of these fish species are 
important in aquaculture, so Tenacibaculum spp. have the potential to cause huge economic losses 
in sea farms, and since Tenacibaculum is not strictly host specific, new fish species in aquaculture 
could also be at risk for infections with Tenacibaculum (Avendaño-Herrera et al., 2006a). However, 
some fish species are more susceptible to infections with Tenacibaculum spp. compared to others. 
For example, salmon are more susceptible to tenacibaculosis caused by T. maritimum compared to 
greenback flounder (Soltani et al., 1996).

2.3 Tenacibaculosis 
 
In general, there is a lack of understanding of infections in fish caused by Tenacibaculum 
(Avendaño-Herrera et al., 2006a). However, some researchers have established infection models 
that give some indications of the infection mechanisms during the development of tenacibaculosis. 
Most of these models use Tenacibaculum maritimum as challenge bacteria, but the infection 
mechanisms of this species might not correlate with infection mechanisms of other Tenacibaculum 
species. Usual signs of tenacibaculosis in fish are rotten fins, tail rots, scale loss, shallow skin 
lesions (Figure 2), paleness of internal organs, hyperemia and eroded mouth (Figure 3). In some 
cases, necrosis on gills and eyes have been seen (Avendaño-Herrera et al., 2006a; Piñeiro-Vidal et 
al., 2008a; Levipan et al., 2019; Handlinger et al., 1997; Powell et al., 2004; van Gelderen et al., 
2011). In the early phases of infection, superficial skin erosions appear, but as the infection 
progresses eroded ulcers develop (Soltani et al., 1996). However, the development of tissue damage 
and lesions is dependent of the infected fish species (Faílde et al., 2013).

Figure 3. Mouth erosion in Atlantic salmon suffering from tenacibaculosis (Småge et al., 2017) 
 
Even though tenacibaculosis usually occurs at the surface of the fish, cases of tenacibaculosis in 
internal organs have been reported (Olsen et al., 2017). For example, Piñeiro-Vidal et al., (2008a) 
reported tenacibaculosis in the kidneys of a diseased sole from an aquaculture system. 
Faílde et al., (2013) established an infection model in which turbot (Psetta maxima L.) were 
intraperitoneally injected (injection into the body cavity) with Tenacibaculum maritimum. This 
injection resulted in extensive tissue damage in the kidneys, liver, spleen, and pancreas, and 
necrosis and hemorrhage were observed in the gastrointestinal tract. The infected organs were 
extensively damaged from the serous layer to the central parenchyma. However, in the same study, 
subcutaneous injections (injection in the dorsal median sinus) were used as well and the authors 
reported that the T. maritimum was less virulent when it was injected intraperitoneally compared to 
being injected subcutaneously. Similar findings were reported by Avendaño-Herrera et al., (2006b), 
who found that an intraperitoneal injection in turbot did not establish an infection. In the same study 
by Avendaño-Herrera et al., (2006b), a bath challenge with T. maritimum was used and infection of 
the turbots were established and signs of tenacibaculosis, like jaw erosion and mouth hemorrhage 
became apparent.

When Tenacibaculum spp. invade the fish surface, the first signs of infection are usually scale loss, 
scale pocket edema, discrete skin ulcerations, blister-like swellings, and inflammation. Sometimes, 
anorexia and lethargy can be observed among the infected fish (Faílde et al., 2013; Handlinger et 
al., 1997; van Gelderen et al., 2011). In a study by Avendaño-Herrera et al., (2006b), it was 
suggested that formation of bacterial mats (biofilms) on skin and gills could be a prerequisite for 
establishment of tenacibaculosis and that healthy fish that are exposed to Tenacibaculum for short 
time periods are not likely be infected. 
After establishment of infection, Tenacibaculum starts degrading the epidermis followed by 
degradation of the dermis where it feed on the collagen (Figure 4), and in some cases the 
hypodermis has been reached by the bacteria (Småge et al., 2017; van Gelderen et al., 2011). When 
the epithelium is removed, lesions exposing the musculature will erode across the surface of the fish 
with the bacteria feeding from the periphery of the lesions (van Gelderen et al., 2011). 
Tenacibaculum does not seem to invade the musculature of infected fish, and van Gelderen et al., 
(2011) suggested that it could be due to the musculature being well-vascularized with low Na+ 
levels. Thereby, the growth conditions in the musculature are not ideal for Tenacibaculum. 
Erosive gill lesions and necrosis will often develop as a result of Tenacibaculum biofilms attached 
to the gills (Handlinger et al., 1997; Powell et al., 2004). The necrosis will usually start at the tips 
of the filaments and thereafter progress to the gill arch (van Gelderen et al., 2011). Severe gill 
damage often leads to mortality of infected fish, and Powell et al., (2004) reported that plasma 
lactate concentrations were elevated in salmons that had died from tenacibaculosis, indicating that 
acute respiratory failure could be the cause of death. However, in the study by Powell et al., (2004), 
it could not be concluded whether the acute respiratory failure was due to decreased perfusion in the 
infected gills or diffusive limitations lowering the gas exchange in the gills.  
In severe cases of tenacibaculosis, petechial hemorrhage can be observed and at some points of 
infection, erosion of head and fins can expose superficial bones, and flashing swimming behavior 
can be observed (Handlinger et al., 1997).

The size of the fish seems to influence the fish’s susceptibility to infections with Tenacibaculum 
spp. In general, small fish (below 100 grams) are more susceptible to tenacibaculosis compared to 
large fish, due to a more rapid tissue destruction in small fish (Avendaño-Herrera et al., 2006a; 
Småge et al., 2017). Furthermore, dislodged scales can increase the risk of tenacibaculosis, so 
especially young fish are susceptible to infections, since scales are easier dislodged in young fish 
compared to older fish (Handlinger et al., 1997). It has also been reported that the progress of 
infection is very dependent on the concentration of Tenacibaculum in the water. Therefore, 
mortality of infected fish can be seen two days to several weeks post-infection, depending on the 
concentration of Tenacibaculum (van Gelderen et al., 2011). 
 
2.4 Factors influencing tenacibaculosis 
 
The reasons for tenacibaculosis outbreaks in fish farms are not fully understood, but there are 
indications that changing environmental conditions can increase the frequency and severity of 
tenacibaculosis outbreaks (Handlinger et al., 1997). There are also indications that mechanical 
==PAGE:19==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
19 
 
damage on the surface of fish increases the risk of tenacibaculosis (Powell et al., 2004). The 
damage to the fish surface can be a result of wrong feeding strategies, where the fish create wounds 
in the skin of other fish and thereby make them more susceptible to invasion by Tenacibaculum 
(Handlinger et al., 1997; Powell et al., 2004; van Gelderen et al., 2010a).  
Handlinger et al., (1997) also suggested that sunlight can increase the risk of tenacibaculosis, since 
outbreaks in Australia have occurred simultaneously with cloud-free weeks with high UV-radiation 
that can damage the skin of fish and thereby ease the establishment of infection. 
It has also been suggested that jellyfish can increase the severity of tenacibaculosis, because of the 
ability of jellyfish to create wounds in the surface of fish due to jellyfish having cnidocytes on their 
tentacles. Cnidocytes contain subcellular organelles called nematocysts that contain toxins and are 
used to sting the jellyfish’s prey. The toxins produced by jellyfish can cause several kinds of 
damage on fish including hemolytic, dermonecrotic, neurotoxic, cardiotoxic, and cytotoxic damage 
(Småge et al., 2017; Ferguson et al., 2010).  
The influence of jellyfish was suggested in a study by Småge et al., (2017), who reported that a 
tenacibaculosis outbreak caused by Tenacibaculum finnmarkense in Atlantic salmon farms in the 
Finnmark County in northern Norway in 2015 occurred simultaneously with a jellyfish-bloom, and 
that jellyfish of the species Dipleurosoma typicum were observed inside net-pens. Nematocysts 
from the jellyfish were observed in the skin of some of the fish (Figure 5), and in cases where 
nematocysts were likely ripped out, holes that could provide an entry for Tenacibaculum were 
present in the fish skin. Likewise, Tenacibaculum bacteria could be observed in close proximity to 
these holes, and it was hypothesized that Tenacibaculum used them as an entry into the collagen 
rich dermis layer. The authors suggested that both the Tenacibaculum outbreak and jellyfish bloom 
could be the result of a phytoplankton bloom one to two months prior to the tenacibaculosis 
outbreak, since Tenacibaculum levels increase with elevated levels of phytoplankton and jellyfish 
blooms usually occur as a result of the elevated zooplankton levels that follow phytoplankton 
blooms. In the study by Småge et al., 2017, it was concluded that Dipleurosoma typicum were 
unlikely to be vectors for Tenacibaculum finnmarkense, but in other studies it has been suggested 
that some jellyfish species indeed could act as vectors for Tenacibaculum. For example, Ferguson et 
al., (2010) suggested that jellyfish of the species Phialella quadrata could have been vectors for 
Tenacibaculum maritimum in an outbreak in a salmon farm near the Shetland Isles, since T. 
maritimum were present on both gills of salmons and in the mouth region of the jellyfish. The 
==PAGE:20==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
20 
 
authors suggested that the proteolytic activity of T. maritimum helped Phialella quadrata digesting 
fish tissue. The findings by Ferguson et al., (2010) are supported by a study by Delannoy et al., 
(2011), in which it was also suggested that jellyfish could act as vectors for T. maritimum since the 
bacteria was found in the mouth region of the jellyfish Pelagia noctiluca.

Jellyfish are not the only organisms that can increase the risk of tenacibaculosis. There are also 
indications that other pathogenic bacteria can co-infect with Tenacibaculum spp. and increase the 
effect of tenacibaculosis. For example, Tenacibaculum can infect simultaneously with Vibrio spp. 
and thereby increase the damage on the infected fish. For example, the muscle layer of fish can be 
damaged when T. maritimum co-infect with V. splendidus (Handlinger et al., 1997). 
 
2.5 Tenacibaculum virulence factors 
 
Tenacibaculum spp. have several genes encoding virulence factors. However, focus have primarily 
been on the well-known pathogen Tenacibaculum maritimum. In a study by Pérez-Pascual et al., 
(2017), the complete genome of T. maritimum type strain NCIMB 2154 was investigated in order to 
identify virulence genes. The T. maritimum genome encodes peptide/amino acid catabolic 
==PAGE:21==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
21 
 
pathways, secreted proteases and peptide/amino acid transporters. These genes allow T. maritimum 
to take up and degrade proteinaceous compounds from the host tissue. The T. maritimum genome 
contains a polysaccharide utilization locus (PUL) that is predicted to be involved in the harvesting 
of glycans from host glycoproteins. Furthermore, a siderophore biosynthesis gene cluster predicted 
to be involved in synthesis of macrocyclic hydroxamate class siderophores and genes encoding 
HmuR and HmuY homologous proteins that are predicted to be involved in heme uptake were 
identified in the T. maritimum genome. These virulence factors are part of the iron acquisition 
systems which allows T. maritimum to retrieve iron sequestered by host proteins (Pérez-Pascual et 
al., 2017). 
Adhesion factors and biofilm formation are important in the establishment of infection. Genes 
encoding exopolysaccharides, adhesins and proteins displaying lectin or carbohydrate binding 
motifs were identified in the T. maritimum genome. However, the genes involved in quorum 
sensing and quorum quenching were not identified in the genome. The genome also encodes three 
superoxide dismutases (SodA, SodB and SodC) as well as two catalase/peroxidase (KatA and 
KatG) that protect the pathogen against reactive oxygen species produced by host macrophages. 
Furthermore, the genome contains loci encoding a drug efflux system, a putative arsenate reductase, 
and a heavy metal efflux pump-type ATPase that are predicted to be involved in resistance against 
cationic heavy metals (Pérez-Pascual et al., 2017).  
The twin-arginine, the ABC-type and the sec-dependent transport systems as well as the type IX 
secretion system (T9SS) were identified. These systems are important for the transport and delivery 
of toxins and for the virulence in some pathogenic bacteria (Pérez-Pascual et al., 2017). The T9SS 
has also been reported in Tenacibaculum species like Tenacibaculum discolor and Tenacibaculum 
ovolyticum and could be a common feature for the Tenacibaculum genus (Kee et al, 2019; 
Teramoto et al., 2016).  
Genes encoding the exotoxin ceramidase with a signal peptide and a cytotoxic sphingomyelinase 
were also identified in the T. maritimum genome. The sphingomyelinase hydrolyses phospholipids 
to phosphocholine and ceramide in the plasma membrane of eukaryotic cells. The ceramide is then 
further hydrolyzed to sphingosine and fatty acids by the ceramidase. A predicted hemolysin, which 
is a pore-forming toxin, was also identified in the T. maritimum genome (Pérez-Pascual et al., 
2017). 
==PAGE:22==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
22 
 
The genome also encodes a PL8_3 family chondroitin AC lyase that might influence degradation of 
chondroitin sulfate which is an important component of cartilage and fish connective tissue. 
Thereby, chondroitin AC lyase could be an important virulence factor in the invasion by T. 
maritimum into the fish tissue. A predicted sialidase is encoded in the siaA gene. Sialic acids are 
especially present in mucosal surfaces of mammalian cells, and therefore, sialidases are important 
in a pathogen’s colonization and damage of these mucosal surfaces. It is predicted that the sialidase 
is exposed on the surface of T. maritimum which allows the bacteria to process sialic acids directly 
from glycoproteins. Furthermore, several cell-surface exposed proteases were predicted in the 
genome (Pérez-Pascual et al., 2017). 
Many of the virulence factors encoded in the genome of T. maritimum seem to be extracellular. A 
study by (van Gelderen et al., 2009b), demonstrated that extracellular products from T. maritimum 
had a damaging effect on Atlantic salmon. In the study, the fish were intraperitoneally injected with 
extracellular products isolated from a T. maritimum culture. All fish injected with high doses of 
extracellular products died and internal organ damage and hemorrhaging in the peritoneal cavity 
could be observed in the injected fish, confirming the toxic effects of T. maritimum extracellular 
products. 
 
2.6 Treatment of tenacibaculosis and antibiotic resistance 
 
Tenacibaculosis can be treated with several antibiotics. For example, Tenacibaculum maritimum is 
sensitive to ampicillin, enrofloxacin, nitrofurantoin, amoxicillin, oxy-tetracycline, trimethoprim-
sulfamethoxazole, florfenicol, chloramphenicol, and erythromycin (Florio et al., 2016; Handlinger 
et al., 1997; Cepeda & Santos, 2002). However, T. maritimum is resistant to flumequine, oxolinic 
acid, kanamycin, streptomycin, gentamicin, colistin and neomycin (Florio et al., 2016; Avendaño-
Herrera et al., 2006a; Handlinger et al., 1997; Cepeda & Santos, 2002). 
There are several challenges in using antibiotics in aquaculture. Sometimes, antibiotics are not 
efficient in aquaculture, even if the pathogens show in vitro sensitivity to them. For example, 
Cepeda & Santos, (2002) reported that an attempt to treat tenacibaculosis in a fish farm failed and 
mortalities reached almost 100%, even when antibiotics with in vitro efficiency were used. 
Furthermore, when antibiotics are used for treatment of tenacibaculosis it should be taken into 
consideration whether the fish have become anorexic as a consequence of infection. In this case 
==PAGE:23==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
23 
 
bath treatment should be used instead of oral treatment with medicated feed (Avendaño-Herrera et 
al., 2006a). 
Data about the global use of antibiotics in aquaculture are lacking. However, globally there is an 
increase in the use of antibiotics in the food production sector. Especially, oxolinic acid, 
chloramphenicol and oxy-tetracycline are used to a large extent in aquaculture (Done et al., 2015). 
By using antibiotics in aquaculture, there is a risk of antibiotic resistance development and the 
antibiotic resistant zoonotic pathogens are a major concern for the food industry (Done et al., 2015; 
Marti et al., 2014). Many of the same classes of antibiotics are used in both human medicine, 
agriculture, and aquaculture. So, if bacteria from aquaculture develop resistance to antibiotics, there 
is a risk that this resistance can be transferred to bacteria in humans or farm animals. This is 
indicated by having similar resistance mechanisms in aquaculture and agriculture (Done et al., 
2015).  
In aquaculture, antibiotics and antibiotic resistant organisms can be spread to the surrounding 
environment through the water pathway (Done et al., 2015). Furthermore, the aquatic environment 
offers ideal conditions for horizontal transfer of antibiotic resistance genes, since bacteria in water 
easily can interact (Marti et al., 2014). Therefore, the risk of spreading antibiotic resistance between 
bacteria in aquaculture could be higher as compared to the risk in agriculture. For example, 
tetracycline resistance is already common in some aquaculture units (Done et al., 2015). These risks 
of spreading antibiotic resistance genes calls for action to decrease the use of antibiotics. So, 
alternative strategies to control pathogens in aquaculture should be developed. 
 
2.7 Vaccination to prevent tenacibaculosis 
 
Several attempts to develop vaccines against Tenacibaculum infections have been carried out. 
However, it is challenging to achieve full protection against tenacibaculosis by vaccinating fish, but 
in some studies vaccines have decreased the mortality frequency of fish infected with 
Tenacibaculum. van Gelderen et al., (2009a) demonstrated that formalin-inactivated T. maritimum 
cells and Freund’s adjuvant injected intraperitoneally, decreased the mortality of Atlantic salmon 
significantly when the fish were challenged with the pathogen after vaccination. It was also 
demonstrated that the adjuvant was necessary for effective vaccinations, since vaccines without 
adjuvant did not decrease mortality significantly. However, some vaccinated fish that did not die as 
==PAGE:24==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
24 
 
a consequence of infection, developed lesions when challenged with T. maritimum. In a similar 
study, Småge et al., (2018) vaccinated Atlantic salmons with T. finnmarkense cells that had been 
inactivated with formaldehyde. The cells were intraperitoneally injected with oil as an adjuvant, but 
the vaccine did not decrease mortality in fish infected with T. finnmarkense and the authors 
suggested that the infections developed to rapidly for the vaccinated fish’s immune systems to 
establish a protective immune response. 
Initiation of an immune response against pathogens is an important prerequisite for vaccine design 
and in several studies, it has been demonstrated that some fish species indeed initiate an immune 
response when infection with Tenacibaculum starts. Faílde et al., (2013) observed an inflammatory 
response at the point of inoculation, 6 hours post-infection in a turbot subcutaneously inoculated 
with T. maritimum. Faílde et al., (2014) found a significant increase of lymphocytes in turbot 
challenged with T. maritimum and suggested that a humoral immune response against the pathogen 
was established. Similar findings were reported by Costas et al., (2014) who detected a response 
from macrophages from Senegalese sole exposed to T. maritimum, indicating that macrophage 
pattern recognition receptors were activated. Likewise, Salati et al., (2005) measured increased 
antibody titers in sea bass that were intraperitoneally injected with formalin killed T. maritimum 
cells and LPS preparations. However, a similar increase of antibody titers was not observed when 
fish were injected with T. maritimum extracellular products. The study by Salati et al., (2005) also 
showed that a second injection with antigens increased the antibody titers compared to one 
injection. So, using multiple injections should be a consideration in the development of vaccine 
strategies against Tenacibaculum. However, there have also been cases in which fish have been 
infected with Tenacibaculum without developing an inflammatory response. For example, van 
Gelderen et al., (2011) reported a lack of inflammatory response in Atlantic salmon smolts infected 
with T. maritimum. 
One of the challenges in the development of vaccines against tenacibaculosis is antigenic 
heterogeneity within species of Tenacibaculum (Olsen et al., 2017). For example, within the species 
T. maritimum, three different serotypes exist (Avendaño-Herrera et al., 2005b). Likewise, it has 
been reported that more than one serotype exists for T. soleae (Castro et al., 2014). Furthermore, 
different extracellular product groupings and different lipopolysaccharide groupings in T. 
maritimum have been reported (van Gelderen et al., 2010b). In addition, Olsen et al., (2017) 
suggested that local tenacibaculosis outbreaks in Norway could consist of several different 
==PAGE:25==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
25 
 
Tenacibaculum strains and the diversity of the strains in an outbreak makes selection for vaccine 
candidates harder. 
Price of vaccine production should also be taken into consideration when designing a vaccine. 
Therefore, vaccines used in aquaculture often have to be restricted to simple preparations like 
formalin killed cells (Salati et al., 2005). In addition, some vaccines can have side-effects. For 
example, mineral oil-adjuvanted vaccines can cause melanin pigmentation (Figure 6), granolomas 
inflammatory response, and abnormal accumulation of fat, which can decrease fish welfare and 
flesh quality of the fish. Therefore, these side effects should be avoided in the vaccine development 
(van Gelderen et al., 2009a).

in attempts to develop vaccines against the flavobacterial pathogens Flavobacterium psychrophilum 
and Flavobacterium columnare, even though the vaccine attempts offered some degree of 
protection against infection without offering full protection. So, there could be common problems in 
the development of vaccines against flavobacteria (Wahli & Madsen, 2018).

2.8 Detection of Tenacibaculum and diagnostic tools 
 
Several different tools that can be used in detecting tenacibaculosis have been developed. Fatty acid 
composition analysis, enzyme profile characterization using API ZYM and DNA-DNA relatedness 
levels can be used in species determination (Yoon et al., 2005; Piñeiro-Vidal et al, 2008b; Yardimci 
& Timur, 2016; Castro et al., 2014). Furthermore, Piñeiro-Vidal et al, (2008b) reported that fatty 
acid methylesters profiling could be used to distinguish between T. maritimum strains isolated from 
different sources. 
Amplification of the 16S rRNA gene followed by sequencing is a widely used method for 
identification of bacteria, and it can be used to identify bacteria from the Tenacibaculum genus, but 
it is not always sufficient for identification at species level (Yoon et al., 2005). 
In aquaculture, there is often a need for rapid detection of infecting bacteria in order to plan an 
effective treatment strategy. However, if the bacteria need to be cultured in the detection procedure, 
the slow growth of Tenacibaculum on media can hinder a rapid detection of tenacibaculosis. 
Therefore, detection methods where the bacteria do not have to be cultured prior to specie-
determination are useful (Avendaño-Herrera et al., 2004; Avendano-Herrera et al., 2018). 
In several studies, it has been shown that nested PCR procedures can be used for rapid diagnosis of 
tenacibaculosis caused by T. maritimum. Avendaño-Herrera et al., (2004) demonstrated that T. 
maritimum from fish mucus could be identified using a nested PCR approach where universal 
primers were used as external primers and specie-specific primers for T. maritimum were used as 
internal primers. Diagnosis using this method took about 7 hours and T. maritimum could also be 
detected in fish carrying the bacteria in their mucus without showing signs of disease. Specie-
specific primers have also been successful in detection of Tenacibaculum dicentrarchi from 
infected fish from aquaculture, and detection of T. soleae from infected sea bass (Avendano-Herrera 
et al., 2018; Castro et al., 2014). 
Real time RT-PCR procedures can also be used in the detection of bacteria on fish. In a study by 
Småge et al., (2018) two different RT-PCR procedures, targeting the housekeeping genes tuf and 
rpoB, were developed for detection of Tenacibaculum spp. The rpoB assay, where a limited number 
of Tenacibaculum species could be detected, was more specific than the tuf assay, in which a wider 
range of Tenacibaculum species were detected, but neither of the assays were restricted to a single 
==PAGE:27==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
27 
 
species of Tenacibaculum. Therefore, these RT-PCR procedures can be useful in non-specie-
specific detection of tenacibaculosis, but they are not useful in specie-specific detection.  
Serological methods can also be used in the detection of Tenacibaculum. Yardimci & Timur, (2016) 
demonstrated that an indirect fluorescent antibody test could be used in a rapid and efficient 
detection of T. maritimum directly in the tissue of sea bass. 
 
2.9 Probiotic activity of roseobacters 
 
As a consequence of the development of antibiotic resistance due to heavy use of antibiotics, and 
the lack of efficiency of some vaccines, attention has moved towards developing alternative 
strategies to control pathogens in aquaculture. One of these strategies is the use of probiotic 
bacteria, and in several studies probiotic bacteria have had an antibacterial effect against pathogens 
such as Vibrio spp. (Nogami et al., 1997; Grotkjær et al., 2016). Bacteria from the Roseobacter 
group have shown a promising potential as probionts in marine aquaculture being inhibitory against 
marine pathogens, due to the production of the antimicrobial compound tropodithietic acid (TDA) 
(Figure 7). The use of probiotic Roseobacter group bacteria has been recommended in several 
studies (Bruhn et al., 2005; D'Alvise et al., 2010; Brinkhoff et al., 2004). Roseobacter group 
bacteria could especially be used as probionts in fish larval rearing units, since fish are very 
susceptible to infections at this stage (Bruhn et al., 2005).  
 
 Figure 7. Structure of tropodithietic acid (TDA) (Bruhn et al., 2005). 
 
Roseobacter group bacteria are well known for their symbiosis with algae, in which the bacteria 
utilize the algae-produced dimethylsulfoniopropionate (DMSP) as a carbon and sulfur source and in 
turn produce TDA to kill potential pathogens (Seyedsayamdost et al, 2011, Wang et al., 2016). 
==PAGE:28==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
28 
 
TDA-producing genera within the Roseobacter group include Phaeobacter, Pseudovibrio, and 
Ruegeria (D'Alvise et al., 2010; Brock et al., 2014; Penesyan et al., 2011).  
The mode of action of TDA is not well-studied, but Wilson et al., (2016) suggested that TDA kills 
bacteria by collapsing the proton motive force like the structurally divergent polyether antibiotic 
nigericin. In the working model created by Wilson et al., (2016), the carboxyl group of TDA is 
protonated outside the cell membrane and then TDA diffuses across the cell membrane and releases 
the proton in the cytosol. After releasing the proton, TDA chelates a positive charged metal ion and 
diffuses out of the cell (Figure 8). This exchange of protons for metal cations is suggested to cause 
the collapse of the proton motive force. Since highly conserved cellular components are attacked by 
TDA, it is antimicrobial against a wide range of bacterial species and resistance mechanisms are not 
likely developed through mutations (Wilson et al., 2016).

and then transports the proton across the membrane and releases it in the cytosol. After releasing the proton, TDA chelates a 
monovalent cation and diffuses out of the cell.

Genes responsible for TDA biosynthesis are encoded in the tda gene cluster and the biosynthesis is 
regulated by the tdaA gene in response to 3-OH-decanoyl-homoserine lactone. However, there are 
some indications that metabolites in the TDA biosynthesis pathway are also part of the pathway for 
the biosynthesis of a roseobacticide, the bacteria produce in order to kill the algae host at certain 
points in the symbiosis between the algae and bacteria (Wang et al., 2016).  
Several studies indicate that TDA production by bacteria from the Roseobacter group peaks when 
the cells are at high densities under static conditions where the cells will form fibril-anchored star 
shaped structures (Figure 9) that merge into biofilms (Bruhn et al., 2005; D'Alvise et al., 2010; 
Brinkhoff et al., 2004). However, the bacteria have a higher grow rate under aerated conditions 
where they are in a planktonic state (Bruhn et al., 2005).  
 
Figure 9. The star shaped structure of a Roseobacter biofilm (Bruhn et al., 2005) 
 
Bacteria from the Roseobacter group are not affected by the TDA they produce and Wilson et al, 
(2016) suggested that the tdaR1-R3 locus is responsible for TDA resistance in roseobacters and that 
the locus is upregulated by TdaA. It was suggested that the enzymes encoded in tdaR1-R3 
counteract the TDA-induced proton influx with a proton efflux mechanism. Furthermore, in the 
study by Wilson et al, (2016) it was demonstrated that an E. coli transformed with the tdaR1-tdaR3 
genes achieved resistance to TDA, further indicating the role of the tdaR1-R3 locus in TDA 
resistance.

In several studies, bacteria from the Roseobacter group have shown a great antagonistic effect 
against Vibrio spp., like V. anguillarum, V. vulnificus, V. harveyi and V. splendius (Bruhn et al., 
2005; D'Alvise et al., 2010). Likewise, it has been demonstrated that TDA-producing bacteria are 
able to increase the survival of Artemia, an important live feed in aquaculture (Grotkjær et al., 
2016). 
Since bacteria from the Roseobacter group have excellent biofilm-forming and surface-colonizing 
abilities and since the highest TDA synthesis is achieved under static conditions, it has been 
suggested that they can be attached to surfaces in aquaculture in order to antagonize pathogens, and 
that even low levels of TDA can function as biocontrol against pathogens by establishing a 
bacteriostatic state (D'Alvise et al., 2010). 
It has also been demonstrated that TDA, to some degree, has an antimicrobial effect against some 
Tenacibaculum species as well as other flavobacterial species (D'Alvise et al., 2010; Brinkhoff et 
al., 2004). Therefore, it could be hypothesized that bacteria from the Roseobacter group could be 
used to combat pathogenic Tenacibaculum spp. in aquaculture settings.  
 
3. Materials and Methods 
 
Bacterial strains: bacterial isolates were received from Stolt sea farm in Galicia in Spain and from 
Selonda sea farm in Greece. From Stolt sea farm, 13 bacterial isolates from Senegalese sole (Solea 
senegalensis) were received and from Selonda sea farm in Greece, 35 isolates from gills of seabass 
and seabream were received. Furthermore, for this study, the type strains Tenacibaculum 
maritimum DSM 17995, Tenacibaculum amylolyticum DSM 13766, Tenacibaculum mesophilum 
DSM 13764, Tenacibaculum ovolyticum DSM 18103, Tenacibaculum skagerrakense DSM 14835, 
and Tenacibaculum skagerrakense DSM 14836 were ordered. However, the type strains 
Tenacibaculum amylolyticum DSM 13766 and Tenacibaculum ovolyticum DSM 18103 did not 
grow in Difco 2216 Marine Broth (MB) or on Difco 2216 Marine Agar (MA) plates. Therefore, 
they were not used further in this study.  
All isolates and type strains were stored in a –80 °C freezer in freeze medium containing 3 % 
Tryptone Soy Broth (Oxoid), 0.5 % glucose, 2 % Skim Milk Powder (Difco), and 4 % glycerol.

3.1 16S rRNA gene sequencing for bacterial identification 
 
For identification of the received bacterial isolates, polymerase chain reaction (PCR) amplification 
of the 16S rRNA genes followed by sequencing was used. Bacteria were streaked on MA plates and 
incubated two days at 25 °C. Following incubation, a plastic inoculation needle was used to transfer 
a small amount of colony mass from each isolate to 50 µL 0.05 M NaOH in 1.5 mL Eppendorf 
tubes. The Eppendorf tubes containing bacteria were boiled at 100 °C for 10 minutes in a heat 
block. After boiling, the Eppendorf tubes were centrifuged at 11337 × g for 120 seconds. The 
supernatant was removed and used as template for the PCR reaction. From each isolate, 1 µL of 
template DNA was mixed with 24 µL PCR Mastermix consisting of 2.5 µL 10x Hot Start PCR 
buffer, 2.5 µL 2 mM dNTP mix, 0.8 µL 12.5 µM Forward primer 27F (Lane, 1991), 0.8 µL 12.5 
µM Reverse primer 1492R (Lane, 1991), 4 µL 25 mM MgCl2, and 0.125 µL Maxima Hot Start 
polymerase. After addition of the Mastermix to the DNA template, the following PCR steps were 
performed in a BIORAD T100TM thermal cycler: 1 cycle for 300 s at 95 °C followed by 35 cycles 
of 45 s at 95 °C, 60 s at 51 °C, and 120 s at 72 °C. The PCR ended with 1 cycle of 420 s at 72 °C. 
The PCR procedure was validated with gel-electrophoresis for 1 hour at 100 V, using a 1 % (w/v) 
agarose gel. 5 µL PCR product was mixed with 1 µL 6x loading buffer, before addition to the wells 
in the gel. 2 µL 1 kbp ladder was used. After gel-electrophoreses, the gel was stained in ethidium 
bromide for 30 minutes and washed with water, before bands were visualized using a BIORAD Gel 
Doc XR+. A successful PCR amplification of the 16S rRNA gene was determined by a single clear 
band around 1500 bp. For isolates where a clear band was not present, the PCR procedure was 
performed again. After the PCR amplification of the 16S rRNA genes, the PCR product was 
cleaned by addition of 0.5 µL Exonuclease I (EN0581, XX) and 0.5 µL Fast AP (EF0651, X), and 
then incubated at 37 °C for 15 minutes followed by incubation at 85 °C for 15 min. From the 
cleaned PCR product, 5 µL was mixed with 5 µL 27F forward primer and 5 µL was mixed with 5 
µL 1492R reverse primer and send to sequencing at Macrogen, Amsterdam in the Netherlands. The 
received sequences were trimmed with a trim quality score of 0.05 using CLS Main Workbench 8.1. 
Forward and reverse sequences were assembled with the following parameters: minimum aligned 
read length of 20 and conflicts resolved by vote between A, C, G, T. Assembled sequences were 
aligned using the NCBI BLAST database. For sequences that could not be assembled using the 
described parameters, both the forward and reserve sequence were aligned using the NCBI BLAST 
database.

3.2 Whole genome sequencing of flavobacteria 
 
Isolates that had been identified as flavobacteria by their 16S rRNA gene sequences as well as the 
type strains Tenacibaculum mesophilum DSM 13764, Tenacibaculum skagerrakense DSM 14835, 
Tenacibaculum skagerrakense DSM 14836, and Tenacibaculum maritimum DSM 17995 were used 
for DNA extraction and whole genome sequencing (WGS). Bacterial cultures that had been grown 
in MB for 2 days at 25 °C were used for DNA extraction using the NucleoSpin® Tissue kit 
(MACHEREY-NAGEL) following a protocol for DNA extraction from bacteria. According to the 
manufacturer’s instructions, 1 mL of cell culture was spun down with centrifugation at 5433.48 × g 
for 5 minutes. After centrifugation, the supernatant was removed, and the pellet was resuspended in 
180 µL Buffer T1 and 25 µL Proteinase K followed by incubation at 56 °C for 3 hours with shaking 
in order to pre-lyse the samples. After incubation, 20 µL Sigma RNase A (20 mg/mL) was added 
and the samples were incubated for 5 minutes at room temperature in order to remove RNA. The 
samples were lysed by addition of 200 µL Buffer B3 followed by incubation at 70 °C for 10 
minutes. After lysing the samples, the DNA binding conditions were adjusted by adding 210 µL 96 
% ethanol to the samples. The samples were then transferred to NucleoSpin® Tissue Columns and 
placed into collection tubes and centrifugated for 1 minute at 11337 × g. The silica membrane of the 
columns was then washed twice, first with 500 µL Buffer BW followed by centrifugation for 1 
minute at 11337 × g and a second wash with 600 µL Buffer B5 followed by centrifugation for 1 
minute at 11337 × g. An extra centrifugation step was used to dry the silica membrane. At last, the 
DNA was eluted with 100 µL elution Buffer BE using centrifugation for 1 minute at 11337 × g. The 
concentrations of the eluted DNA were determined using a DS-11+ nanodrop spectrophotometer 
(DeNovix) and the Qubit 2.0 kit (Invitrogen) and purity of the DNA was determined using 
nanodrop and gel-electrophoresis. The DNA concentration measurements revealed that too low 
DNA concentrations were eluted (below 10 mg/mL), so the DNA extractions were performed again. 
This time, 2 mL bacteria cultures were spun down instead of 1 mL and the final elution was 
performed in two steps, where 50 mL of 10 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.5 was used twice. In cases where 
this procedure did not yield the required DNA concentration of 10 mg/mL, 2 mL of a bacteria 
culture that had been grown for three days instead of two days were spun down, which yielded the 
required concentrations of DNA, except for the type strain Tenacibaculum maritimum DSM 17995, 
which was not sequenced. The extracted DNA was diluted to concentrations between 10-15 mg/mL 
in 10 mM Tris-HCl, pH 8.5 and sequenced using Illumina sequencing with MiSeq V3, 2×300

3.4 Biofilm formation assay 
 
Quantification of the biofilm forming ability of the bacteria was measured with the O’Toole-Kolter 
assay (O'Toole & Kolter 1998), with modifications described by Jensen et al., (2007). All 
flavobacterial isolates, the type strains T. maritimum DSM 17995, T. skagerrakense DSM 14835, T. 
skagerrakense DSM 14836, and T. mesophilum DSM 13764 were grown in 10 mL MB at 25 °C 
overnight. The strains Phaeobacter inhibens DSM 17395 and Pseudomonas aeruginosa ATCC 
15442 were used as positive controls in the biofilm formation assay. All bacterial cultures were 
diluted to an OD600 of approximately 0.01 and 100 µL of these dilutions were transferred in 
triplicate to a 96 wells plate (Thermo Scientific NucleonTM Delta Surface). Sterile medium was used 
as negative control. The cultures were then incubated for 21 hours at 25 °C. After incubation, the 96 
wells plate was shaken for 10 seconds before OD600 was measured in a SpectraMax i3 
==PAGE:34==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
34 
 
spectrophotometer. Then, all culture medium and bacteria were removed, the wells were washed 
three times with 100 µL dH2O, and the plate was led to dry for five minutes. After drying, 125 µL 
of a 1 % crystal violet (Sigma) solution was added to each well of the plate, followed by incubation 
for 15 minutes. The crystal violet solution was removed, and the wells were washed three times 
with 200 µL dH2O. The plate was then led to dry for 15 minutes. After drying, 200 µL 96 % ethanol 
was added to each well and the plate was incubated for 30 minutes. After incubation, 100 µL of the 
ethanol mixed with crystal violet was added to a new 96 well plate. The plate was shaken for 10 
seconds followed by OD590 measurement. To determine if there was a significant biofilm formation 
in the flavobacterial cultures compared to the sterile medium, a F-test for variance followed by a 
one-tail T-test was carried out. Significant biofilm formation was determined as p<0.05. 
 
3.5 Phaeobacter antagonism of Tenacibaculum in agar diffusion assay  
 
An agar-diffusion (spot inhibition) assay was performed to determine the ability of TDA-producing 
and TDA-deficient Phaeobacter to inhibit the growth of flavobacteria. All flavobacterial isolates, 
the type strains T. maritimum DSM 17995, T. skagerrakense DSM 14835, T. skagerrakense DSM 
14836, and T. mesophilum DSM 13764 were inoculated in 10 mL MB and incubated for two days at 
25 °C under stagnant conditions. The strain Vibrio anguillarum serotype 90-11-286 was used as a 
positive inhibition control. The strains Phaeobacter inhibens DSM 17395, S26 (Phaeobacter 
piscinae), and Phaebacter inhibens tda deletion mutant that was unable to produce TDA (Wang et 
al., 2016) were retrieved from freeze cultures. S26 was isolated from a biofilm in the outlet of a fish 
tank from a Greek sea bass aquaculture unit by Grotkjær et al., (2016), who also demonstrated that 
this strain had a great antibacterial effect against V. anguillarum. Phaeobacter inhibens DSM 17395 
and S26 were streaked on MA and the P. inhibens tda deletion mutant was streaked on MA with 25 
µg/mL gentamycin in order to select for strains deficient in TDA production. Colonies from the 
Phaeobacter strains were inoculated in 10 mL MB and incubated for one day at 25 °C with shaking 
at 200 rpm. After incubation, 10-4 dilutions were prepared from the flavobacteria and Vibro 
anguillarum 90-11-286 cultures and 100 µL of these dilutions were plated in duplicate on MA 
plates using a Drigalski spatula in order to create a bacterial lawn on the plates. From the 
Phaeobacter cultures, 5 µL were spot inoculated on top of the flavobacteria layer. Furthermore, 
dilutions of the bacteria were plated on MA plates for enumeration of the bacterial concentrations. 
All plates were then incubated for two days at 25 °C. However, after incubation, it was observed

Furthermore, the enumeration of the bacteria revealed several orders of magnitudes in differences

the flavobacteria. Inhibition zones were measured from the edge of the Phaeobacter colonies after 
both one and two days of incubation at 25 °C. This method was sufficient for creating a bacterial 
lawn in which inhibition zones could be measured. 
 
3.6 Minimum inhibitory concentration assay with Tenacibaculum and S26  
 
It has been demonstrated that T. maritimum is resistant to gentamycin, kanamycin, and streptomycin 
(Florio et al., 2016; Cepeda & Santos, 2002). To determine if this resistance pattern was common 
for all the flavobacterial isolates and type strains used in this study and to find an antibiotic 
candidate (that allowed for growth of flavobacteria, but not for S26) for the following “Antagonism 
of Tenacibaculum by Phaeobactor biofilms” assay, a minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) 
assay was carried out. All flavobacterial isolates, the type strains T. maritimum DSM 17995, T. 
skagerrakense DSM 14835, T. skagerrakense DSM 14836, and T. mesophilum DSM 13764 were 
incubated in 10 mL of MB for two days at 25 °C under stagnant conditions. The Phaeobacter 
piscinae strain S26 was incubated for one day in 10 mL of MB at 25 °C with shaking. On the day of 
use, 10-2 dilutions of all bacterial cultures were prepared in MB and mixed with gentamycin, 
kanamycin, and streptomycin in duplicate in 96 wells plates (Thermo Scientific NucleonTM Delta 
Surface) to yield concentrations of gentamycin and streptomycin of 25, 12.5, 6.25, 3.125, 1.5625, 
and 0 µg/mL, and kanamycin concentrations of 50, 25, 12.5, 6.25, 3.125 and 0 µg/mL. Sterile MB 
was used as control. The 96 wells plates were incubated for three days at 25 °C under stagnant 
conditions. After incubation, OD600 was measured, with 10 seconds of shaking before the 
measurement. A one-tail T-test was used to determine if there was a significant reduction in the 
bacteria exposed to the highest concentration of antibiotics compared to the unexposed bacteria. 
Significant difference was determined as p<0.05.

3.7 Antagonism of Tenacibaculum by Phaeobactor biofilms  
 
The assay was performed following procedures described by D'Alvise et al., (2010). Phaeobacter 
piscinae strain S26 was incubated in 10 mL MB overnight at 25 °C under stagnant conditions. After 
incubation, 5 µL of the culture were diluted in 195 µL MB in the wells of a 96 wells Innovotech 
MBEC P&G assay plate with the lid having pegs going into the wells. The plate was incubated for 4 
days at 25 °C under stagnant conditions. After incubation, pegs were cut off in duplicate and 
transferred to 2 mL of 3 % instant ocean (IO) and sonicated for 4 minutes at 28 kHz and vortexed 
for 30 seconds. 10-1 to 10-7 dilutions were prepared and plated on MA plates for enumeration. The 
remaining pegs were transferred to wells containing 6.4·102 to 4.8·103 CFU/mL of the strains 
Stolt_2, Selonda_1, Selonda_9, Selonda_11, Selonda_29, T. skagerrakense DSM 14835 and T. 
maritimum DSM 17995 in triplicate. Sterile pegs and MB were used as a negative control. The 
flavobacterial cultures exposed to pegs with S26 biofilm were incubated for four days at 25 °C 
under stagnant conditions. After incubation, 10-2 to 10-7 dilutions were prepared in PBS and plated 
on MA plates containing 25 µg/mL streptomycin. Furthermore, 20 µL of the flavobacteria exposed 
to the S26 biofilms were plated directly on MA plates containing 25 µg/mL streptomycin. All plates 
were incubated for two days at 25 °C before enumeration. Colony counts were used to determine 
CFU/mL that was then log-transformed. To determine whether the concentrations of flavobacteria 
exposed to S26 biofilms were significantly different from concentrations of flavobacteria exposed 
to sterile control pegs, F-tests for variance followed by two-tail T-tests were carried out. Significant 
difference was determined as p<0.05.

virulence and resistance factors and to determine the genetic diversity between the isolates. The 
assembled genomes had lengths in the range 2,900,000-3,900,000 bp, except for Selonda_25 that

belonged to the species Tenacibaculum discolor, and the isolate Selonda_29 belonged to the species 
Tenacibaculum gallaicum. Average nucleotide identity (ANI) between the isolates revealed that

5. Discussion 
 
Aquaculture has become an important food sector, but tenacibaculosis is a concern due the ability of 
Tenacibaculum spp. to cause fish loss in sea farms. Therefore, this project set out to characterize the 
virulence factors of Tenacibaculum isolates from a Spanish Stolt sea farm and a Greek Selonda Sea 
farm, and to test whether probiotic Phaeobacter species could be used to control Tenacibaculum. 
The flavobacterial isolates received from both Stolt Sea farm and Selonda Sea farm were whole 
genome sequenced. Average nucleotide identity of the sequences revealed that the three Spanish 
Tenacibaculum strains were genetically identical and belonged to the species T. soleae. However, 
there was a larger genetic diversity among the Greek isolates and the isolates fell into four species 
of Tenacibaculum. The isolates from the Selonda sea farm can be considered as strains from a local 
outbreak and it could indicate that high genetic diversity among Tenacibaculum in local outbreaks 
is not uncommon. Similar findings are reported by Olsen et al., (2017), who found high genetic 
diversity among Tenacibaculum strains from local outbreaks in Norway. Since high genetic 
diversity can result in high antigenic diversity among isolates, it might be challenging to develop a 
low-cost vaccine that offers full protection against all the Tenacibaculum isolates from the Selonda 
sea farm. 
No antibiotic resistance genes were found in the flavobacterial isolates and type strain by alignment 
to the “Comprehensive Antibiotic Resistance Database”. However, all the isolates and type strains 
were resistant to the antibiotics’ gentamycin, kanamycin and streptomycin in the MIC assay in this 
study, except for T. skagerrakense that was intermediate susceptible to streptomycin. In addition, 
resistance to the antibiotics among Tenacibaculum strains have been demonstrated in several other 
studies (Florio et al., 2016; Avendaño-Herrera et al., 2006a; Handlinger et al., 1997; Cepeda & 
Santos, 2002). The lack of known antibiotic resistance genes in the isolates from this study, indicate 
that the resistance of the isolates to several antibiotics could be due to natural features such as lack 
of target binding rather than horizontal gene transfer of known antibiotic resistance genes. 
Furthermore, the results from the MIC assay in this study correlates wells with a study by Chang et 
al., (1997), who found that most flavobacteria are resistant to aminoglycosides, the class of 
antibiotics that gentamycin, kanamycin, and streptomycin belong to.  
Virulence genes were present in all of the flavobacterial isolates. All Tenacibaculum isolates had 
genes encoding iron acquisition factors and most of the Tenacibaculum isolates had genes encoding 
==PAGE:52==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
52 
 
heavy metal resistance, adhesins, and exopolysaccharides. Iron acquisition factors are important for 
many pathogenic bacteria (Payne, 1993) and it could be expected that all pathogenic Tenacibaculum 
species have iron acquisition factors that make the bacteria able to acquire iron from host proteins 
or other cellular components. Genes encoding heavy metal resistance factors and superoxide 
dismutases indicate that the Tenacibaculum isolates are able to survive in hostile environments and 
resist reactive oxygen species (Pérez-Pascual et al., 2017). All Tenacibaculum isolates, except for 
Selonda_11 (T. discolor), encode biofilm formation factors like adhesins and exopolysaccharides. 
However, Selonda_11 (T. discolor) showed significant biofilm formation in the biofilm formation 
assay, indicating that this strain has biofilm formation factors not determined in this study. 
Therefore, adhesion and biofilm formation could be important virulence factors in the 
Tenacibaculum isolates. This is consistent with the fact that the isolates in the present study were 
isolated from the gills of sea bass, sea bream, and Senegalese sole. Likewise, it is consistent with 
findings in other studies, where it has been demonstrated that Tenacibaculum form bacterial mats 
on the skin and gills of fish during infection (Avendaño-Herrera et al., 2006b; Handlinger et al., 
1997; Powell et al., 2004). Most of the T. soleae isolates encoded collagenases, which could 
indicate that these isolates target the collagen containing dermis layer, and it is supported by a study 
by Castro et al., (2014), who reported that T. soleae caused skin lesions in sea bass and that disease 
could be reproduced with a bath challenge, but not with an intraperitoneal injection.  
No single virulence gene was present in all of the Tenacibaculum isolates indicating that virulence 
genes, in general, not are part of the Tenacibaculum genus core genome. This finding is supported 
by a study by Habib et al., (2014) where it was suggested that pathogenicity had evolved in parallel 
in different lineages of species from the Tenacibaculum genus. The genes encoding the Por 
secretion system (PorSS), which is a common virulence factor in Bacteroidetes were present in the 
T. discolor isolates and could indicate that secretion of extracellular products takes place during 
infection with T. discolor. T. skagerrakense encodes several factors that could be virulence factors 
if the bacteria infect fish. However, based on a literature search, T. skagerrakense has only been 
isolated as free-living bacteria in a seawater sample and have not been described in association with 
aquatic animals (Frette et al., 2004), so it remains unknown whether T. skagerrakense actually has 
the ability to infect fish. 
A single virulence gene (stp) coding a serine-threonine phosphatase was identified in the Joostella 
atrarenae isolates.  None of the Tenacibaculum isolates or type strains had this gene, indicating that 
virulence mechanisms in J. atrarenae are distinct from those in Tenacibaculum. Based on a 
==PAGE:53==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
53 
 
literature search, J. atrarenae has not been isolated from aquatic animals before and have not 
previously been described as a pathogen (Kim et al., 2011), so further studies, eventually with in 
vivo tests, should be conducted before the association of J. atrarenae with marine animals can be 
determined. 
In the present study, the biofilm formation ability of all the flavobacterial isolates and type strains 
was quantified. T. maritimum and T. skagerrakense were the flavobacterial strains with the highest 
biofilm forming ability. The P. aeruginosa positive control did not form as much biofilm as 
expected, but that could be because it was grown in marine broth. The P. inhibens positive control 
formed much more biofilm compared to all of the other strains. These results indicate that 
Phaeobacter is a stronger colonizer of abiotic surfaces compared to bacteria from the 
Tenacibaculum and Joostella genera. However, the biofilm formation of a bacteria is dependent on 
the type of surface the bacteria attach to (O'Toole & Kolter, 1998). Therefore, the biofilm formation 
on biotic surfaces, like the skin or gills of fish, can be very different from biofilm formation on the 
abiotic surface used in the biofilm formation assay in this study. In general, the results from the 
whole genome sequencing and the biofilm formation assay indicate that the Tenacibaculum isolates 
have virulence factors that make them able to infect the surface of fish, but there is diversity in the 
virulence factors as well as a high genetic diversity. 
In this study, the antagonizing effect of Phaeobacter against Joostella and Tenacibaculum isolates 
and type strains were tested, with both an agar inhibition and biofilm inhibition assay. In both 
assays there was a great variation in the susceptibility of different strains to TDA. In both assays, 
the most pronounced antimicrobial effect of TDA-producing P. inhibens and P. piscinae was 
against T. maritimum and T. skagerrakense and the least inhibition was seen against T. mesophilum, 
T. discolor, and T. gallaicum. In the spot inhibition assay, the TDA deficient mutant did not inhibit 
any of the flavobacteria, confirming that TDA production was responsible for the antagonistic effect 
of the Phaeobacter species. However, competition for growth in the media could also have 
influenced the effect of Phaeobacter against the flavobacteria in both assays. In general, this study 
demonstrates that there is a great intra-genus variety in the susceptibility to TDA produced by 
Phaeobacter since some of the Tenacibaculum strains were much more sensitive to TDA as 
compared to others. These findings correlate with a study by Pintado et al., (2010) who found that a 
Phaeobacter strain did antagonize T. maritimum but had no antagonistic effect against T. discolor. 
The antimicrobial effect of TDA against Tenacibaculum has also been tested in other studies. 
D’Alvise et al., (2010) stated that TDA had an antimicrobial effect against T. maritimum, but the 
==PAGE:54==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
54 
 
quantity of the effect was not stated. Likewise, Penesyan et al., (2011) reported that a 
Tenacibaculum sp. was sensitive to TDA, but the sensitivity was lower compared to other bacteria 
like V. harveyi. 
Intra-genus susceptibility to TDA is not uncommon among marine bacteria. In a study by Dittmann 
et al., (2019a), it was found that changes in bacterial abundances in the presence of TDA-producing 
P. inhibens seemed to be species-specific and might even be strain specific. Similar findings were 
reported by Harrington et al., (2014), who found that some marine strains that lived in association 
with a marine sponge were resistant to TDA, and that some of these strains were not TDA-
producers themselves. 
The reasons why some bacteria are sensitive to TDA and some are resistant are poorly understood. 
Wilson et al., (2016) demonstrated that TDA is antimicrobial by collapsing the proton motive force 
in bacteria, and that the TDA-producing P. inhibens is resistant to TDA due to the tdaR1-R3 genes 
that makes P. inhibens counteract the TDA-induced proton influx. Wilson et al., (2016) also 
suggested that non-TDA-producing strains could become TDA resistant be receiving the tdaR1-R3 
genes through horizontal gene transfer. However, the tdaR1-R3 genes or homologs to these genes 
were not present in any of the flavobacterial isolates or type strains from this study, indicating that 
TDA resistance can be due to other bacterial features. Furthermore, Dittmann et al., (2019b) found 
that genes encoding factors for oxidative stress response and biogenesis of the cell wall and cell 
membrane were upregulated in V. vulnificus in the presence of TDA, indicating that collapsing the 
proton motive force in bacteria might not be the only antibacterial effect of TDA and that the mode 
of action of TDA could differ from one target organisms to another. Therefore, the mode of action 
of TDA in Tenacibaculum and Joostella remains to be determined. 
If TDA-producing roseobacters are used to a large extent in aquaculture to control pathogenic 
bacteria, it could be hypothesized that the pathogens will develop resistance to TDA over time, 
either through horizontal gene transfer of resistance genes or by mutations that change the bacterial 
components in a way that they cannot be targeted by TDA. However, studies by Porsby et al., 
(2011) and Rasmussen et al., (2016) indicate that the risk of TDA resistance development in 
bacteria is low. Porsby et al., (2011) found that few bacteria developed resistance upon exposure to 
TDA, and the bacteria that did develop resistance, lost the resistance mechanisms when they were 
cultured in a medium without TDA. In addition, Rasmussen et al., (2016) found that a Vibrio 
anguillarum strain did not develop resistance to TDA or had an increased mutation rate upon long 
==PAGE:55==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
55 
 
term exposure, further indicating that TDA-sensitive strains are unlikely to develop resistance upon 
continuous exposure. 
Based on the results from this study, it cannot be concluded why some of the flavobacterial isolates 
and type strains were more sensitive to TDA compared to others. There was no correlation between 
TDA susceptibility and virulence factors identified in the genomes of the flavobacterial isolates and 
type strains.  
In a study by Dittmann et al., (2019b), it was found that there was an upregulation of genes 
encoding biofilm formation factors in Vibrio vulnificus exposed to TDA, indicating that these 
bacteria might form biofilm in protection against TDA. However, in the present study, T. 
maritimum and T. skagerrakense were the bacteria most sensitive to TDA, and these species also 
showed the highest biofilm formation among the flavobacteria tested, indicating that biofilm 
formation in flavobacteria does not offer protection against TDA. 
The findings in this study, that some flavobacteria are less sensitive to TDA as compared to others 
could indicate that some flavobacteria naturally inhabit ecosystems with increased TDA 
concentrations. Roseobacter group bacteria are known to live in symbiosis with algae, especially 
with the microalgae Emilaniania huxleyi (Seyedsayamdost et al., 2011). In a study by Suzuki et al., 
(2001), T. mesophilum was isolated from algae. The authors did not state the species of the algae or 
whether roseobacters were present on the algae as well. In addition, Jang et al., (2006) isolated a 
Tenacibaculum sp. from the algae Chondrus ocellatus. These studies could indicate that some 
Tenacibaculum spp. are part of the natural microbial communities of some algae and might live in 
the same habitats as TDA-producing roseobacters, which could explain why some Tenacibaculum 
spp. showed little sensitivity to TDA produced by Phaeobacter in the present study. 
Even if TDA-producing Phaeobacter species have an antagonistic effect against some 
flavobacterial species in vitro, it cannot be confirmed if the probiotic Phaeobacter will have an 
antagonistic effect against pathogenic flavobacteria in aquaculture settings. It has been suggested 
that roseobacters can be attached to abiotic surfaces to form probiotic biofilms to control pathogens 
in aquaculture settings (D’Alvise et al., 2010). However, if roseobacters are used in aquaculture, the 
concentration of TDA in the sea water is likely to be much lower compared to the concentration of 
TDA in the medium in the Phaeobacter biofilm inhibition assay in this study. The MICs of TDA 
for the flavobacterial isolates and type strains were not determined in the present study, so it is not 
known how high a concentration of TDA that is needed to control flavobacteria in aquaculture 
==PAGE:56==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
56 
 
settings. Furthermore, the access to nutrients in an aquaculture setting is very different compared to 
the in vitro tests in this study where marine agar and marine broth was used as growth media. For 
example, the concentration of iron is much higher in Marine Broth compared to sea water in fish 
farms (D'Alvise et al., 2016). In a study by D'Alvise et al., (2016), it was demonstrated that the 
concentration of iron influences the probiotic effect of Phaeobacter, since there was a lack of 
antibacterial activity in the supernatant of a P. inhibens culture that was starved from iron. The 
bacteria instead produced a compound which was suggested to be a precursor for TDA. Therefore, 
it could be expected that Phaeobacter species will have a decreased antimicrobial effect against 
TDA-sensitive pathogens in aquaculture due to the lower concentrations of iron. Furthermore, in the 
present study, it was demonstrated that the Tenacibaculum isolates and type strains had 
sophisticated iron acquisition factors that could make them compete for the limited iron sources in 
an aquaculture setting. Therefore, Tenacibaculum spp. might be able to protect themselves against 
some of the antagonistic effects of Phaeobacter by sequestering iron and thereby limit the 
antimicrobial effect of TDA. 
Another important aspect of the use of probiotic Phaeobacter bacteria in aquaculture is the effect of 
subinhibitory concentrations of TDA on the pathogens and the microbial communities in 
aquaculture settings. In a study by Beyersmann et al., (2017), it was found that a sub-inhibitory 
concentration of TDA influenced global gene regulation in P. inhibens similar to the regulatory 
effect of N-acyl-homoserine lactone. In the study, it was indicated that TDA functioned as a 
quorum sensing molecule, and that genes responsible for motility, biofilm formation, and 
chemotaxis were downregulated. It is not known whether a sub-inhibitory concentration of TDA 
will affect gene regulation in Tenacibaculum spp., but both motility and biofilm formation could be 
virulence factors for Tenacibaculum. Therefore, if subinhibitory concentrations of TDA do 
downregulate motility and biofilm formation, TDA might lower the virulence of Tenacibaculum, 
even if the TDA concentration is not high enough to have a bactericidal effect. However, Dittmann 
et al., (2019b) found that genes encoding motility were downregulated, but iron acquisition and 
biofilm formation factors were upregulated when V. vulnificus was exposed to a sub-lethal 
concentration of TDA. If TDA upregulates biofilm formation and iron acquisition factors in 
Tenacibaculum spp., it could make the bacteria more pathogenic.  
The environmental conditions in aquaculture settings should also be taken into consideration before 
probiotic Phaeobacter or other roseobacters species can be introduced. Tenacibaculosis can occur 
as a result of extreme weather conditions. Handlinger et al., (1997) reported that a tenacibaculosis 
==PAGE:57==
Jens Edward Tesdorpf 
S143821 
 
 
57 
 
outbreak in Australia occurred as a result of an extended period of cloud-free days, where sunlight 
created wounds in fish and thereby made them more susceptible to tenacibaculosis. Likewise, in a 
study by Olsen et al., (2011), it was reported that a tenacibaculosis outbreak in Norway was 
associated with winter ulcers in Atlantic salmons in the winter months where the temperatures 
usually are below 10 °C. Bruhn et al., (2005) reported that TDA only has an antimicrobial activity 
in a limited temperature range, and that the antibacterial effect decreased in temperatures above 30 
°C. In the Stolt sea farm in Galicia in Spain and in the Selonda sea farm in Greece, temperatures are 
not likely to raise above 30 °C (Nykjaer, 2009), so the risk of thermal destabilization of TDA is 
low. However, if TDA-producing Phaeobacter are used in warmer waters, for example in equatorial 
aquaculture, there could be a risk of thermal destabilization of TDA, thereby lowering the probiotic 
activity. The TDA molecules are not destabilized when temperature decreases, but the growth and 
TDA-production of Phaeobacter can decrease with decreasing temperatures (Bruhn et al., 2005). 
Therefore, if Phaeobacter species are going to be used as probiotic bacteria in cold water 
aquaculture, for example to inhibit Tenacibaculum associated with winter ulcers, it is of importance 
to isolate a Phaeobacter strain that grow and produce TDA at low temperatures.  
This study has demonstrated that Tenacibaculum isolates from Greek and Spanish Sea farms have 
virulence factors that fit well with the tendency of these bacteria to adhere to the surface of fish and 
cause tissue damage in the outer layers of the skin. Furthermore, this study demonstrates that 
Phaeobacter show stronger biofilm formation on abiotic surfaces compared to Tenacibaculum spp. 
Therefore, it is suggested that Phaeobacter could be used in aquaculture settings to outcompete 
Tenacibaculum on abiotic surfaces. This study also demonstrated that TDA produced by 
Phaeobacter spp. has an antagonistic effect against some of the Tenacibaculum isolates, but since 
some of the isolates seem to be resistant or have a low sensitivity to TDA, Phaeobacter cannot be 
seen as the sole solution to control Tenacibaculum spp. in aquaculture units. Therefore, additional 
strategies are needed. These strategies could be the use of probiotic bacteria beside roseobacters. 
For example, Burkholderia, Psychrobacter, Acinetobacter, and Enterovibrio have antagonistic 
effects against Tenacibaculum (Li et al., 2018; Wanka et al., 2018). Whether these bacteria can be 
used in combination with roseobacters, or even be combined with roseobacters in probiotic multi-
species biofilms, remains to be tested, but using multispecies probionts in aquaculture should be 
taking into consideration and be a topic for future research.

6. Conclusions and future perspectives 
 
The aim of this study was to determine the virulence factors that make Tenacibaculum species able 
to cause disease in fish and to test whether TDA-producing bacteria from the Roseobacter group are 
able to antagonize these Tenacibaculum species. Out of 48 bacterial strains isolated from the gills of 
Senegalese sole, seabass, and seabream, 15 isolates belonged to the Tenacibaculum genus and four 
isolates were also flavobacteria like Tenacibaculum but belonged to the Joostella atrarenae species. 
Whole genome sequencing of the isolates and type strains revealed that the Tenacibaculum isolates 
had virulence factors involved in iron acquisition, biofilm formation, collagen degradation, heavy 
metal resistance, reactive oxygen species resistance, and enzyme secretion, which correlates well 
with the findings that these bacteria usually attack the surface of fish. The only virulence factor 
determined in the genomes of the J. atrarenae isolates was a serine-threonine phosphatase. Average 
nucleotide identity between the Tenacibaculum isolates from the Selonda Sea farm in Greece 
revealed that there was a high genetic diversity among the isolates, which could result in high 
antigenic diversity. Therefore, it will be challenging to develop a vaccine that offers protection 
against all the isolates. A quantification of the biofilm formation ability of the J. atrarenae and 
Tenacibaculum strains revealed that the J. atrarenae and several of the Tenacibaculum strains 
showed significant biofilm formation. The highest biofilm formation was seen with the T. 
maritimum and T. skagerrakense strains. However, all the flavobacterial isolates and type strains 
showed significantly less biofilm formation compared to a P. inhibens positive control, indicating 
that Phaeobacter species could have the ability to outcompete Tenacibaculum and Joostella in 
colonization of abiotic surfaces.  
TDA-producing Phaeobacter had the ability to antagonize the J. atrarenae and several of the 
Tenacibaculum strains. The highest antimicrobial effect was measured against T. maritimum and T. 
skagerrakense and the lowest antimicrobial effect was measured against T. mesophilum, T. discolor, 
and T. gallaicum, confirming that there is an intra-genus variety in the susceptibility to TDA.  
It can therefore be concluded that probiotic Phaeobacter species or other roseobacters can be used 
as part of a strategy to control Tenacibaculum and Joostella in aquaculture settings due to the 
potential ability of Phaeobacter to outcompete Tenacibaculum and Joostella for colonization of 
abiotic surfaces and to antagonize Joostella and some Tenacibaculum species through the 
production of TDA. However, further tests are needed, including tests of the probiotic activity of
