Abstract
This master thesis investigates the performance of index and enhanced index tracking models when
applied to an ESG-focused subset of stocks from the S&P 500, comparing them against portfolios selected
from the entire S&P 500. Addressing a research gap, this study specifically excludes poorly ESG-rated
companies prior to portfolio optimization.
The methodology involves developing and testing index and enhanced index tracking models, drawing
inspiration from established literature. MSCI ESG ratings are used to systematically create an ESG-
filtered dataset. Model performance is evaluated using metrics such as tracking error, Sharpe ratio, and
cumulative returns. This evaluation was conducted across various market conditions and time periods
(2006-2024). The impact of rebalancing frequency and UCITS (Undertakings for Collective Investment
in Transferable Securities) regulations on portfolio performance is also analyzed.
Key findings indicate that for index tracking, portfolios composed of all available S&P 500 stocks gener-
ally achieved better tracking accuracy than their ESG-focused counterparts, particularly with frequent
rebalancing that lead to significant transaction costs.
However, the enhanced index tracking model
demonstrated that ESG-focused portfolios could outperform the broader index. Specifically, the ESG-
focused enhanced model without rebalancing exhibited a superior risk-return profile compared to the
S&P 500 and non ESG-focused model. While frequent rebalancing and strict UCITS compliance posed
challenges for both enhanced models, modified handling of UCITS regulations significantly improved
their performance, allowing both to surpass the index. The performance of ESG-focused portfolios was
notably influenced by broader market interest and performed well in years with large capital flows into
ESG funds.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
In recent years, environmental, social, and governance (ESG) views have become an increasingly impor-
tant part of financial decision-making for private investors and fund managers. While some of it is due
to moral concerns, other reasons stem from evidence that ESG-oriented investments can provide com-
petitive financial returns when compared with purely monetary-oriented investments (Kabderian Dreyer
et al., 2023). This change reflects broader market trends, where ESG factors are now considered part of
long-term risk management and value construction rather than just an additional screening criteria.
Furthermore, some investment funds have been criticized in the media for sticking with ’black’ invest-
ments in companies and have been accused of contributing to climate change and human rights abuses
(Watts, 2024).
Regulatory requirements, such as ESG reporting, obligations of the European Union and the Sustain-
able Finance Disclosure Regulation (SFDR), have led to more transparency in sustainability disclosures.
These rules ensure that companies provide more standardized and verifiable ESG data, allowing for more
consistent assessment of corporate sustainability practices (Danish Industry, 2024). With the increasing
availability of ESG data, it is now possible to quantify ESG factors and utilize them to construct a
portfolio.
Based on the work of Markowitz (1952) on mean-variance portfolio optimization, index tracking has been
studied as an investment strategy trying to reproduce the risk-reward profile of an index, but without
the need to buy all the individual stocks in the index. Traditional index tracking models aim to copy the
performance of a benchmark index with minimal tracking error, while enhanced index tracking models
try to achieve higher returns by making selective changes to the portfolio composition (Mansini et al.,
2015).
By combining ESG scores and linear programming models, investors can optimize a portfolio’s perfor-
mance while aligning with moral standards.
1.1
Objective and Motivation
This thesis will look into index and enhanced index tracking models, specifically when used on a selected
group of stocks from companies with good environmental, social, and governance (ESG) ratings.
The focus will be on understanding how these models perform when applied to a subset of stocks from
the S&P 500, ensuring that all selected companies meet high ESG criteria relative to the sector.
To investigate the performance of the models on this subset of stocks, their performance will be evaluated
across periods with different market conditions. The models will be compared to the index as well as
the same model that is allowed to invest in all stocks from the S&P 500. The selection of an ESG rating
method to assess the stock before selecting an ESG subset of them and the collection of historical prices
is a key part of the foundation for the study.

1.2
Research Question
Index tracking models follow a structured approach to try and replicate the performance of a benchmark
index. Enhanced index tracking models expand on the index model to outperform the benchmark index.
To test a model’s performance, many have utilized the S&P 500 as the benchmark index, given its status
as one of the most well-known indices and its historically strong returns (Robertson, 2023). However,
not all stocks in the index can be considered equally sustainable, and with the growing focus on ESG
investing, it can be expected that investors would look towards other indexes or funds that can yield the
same performance as the S&P 500 but exclude companies with relatively low ESG ratings. This is the
foundation behind the research question.
How do index and enhanced index tracking models perform on
the S&P 500 when restricted to an ESG-focused subset of its stocks?
To help answer the research question, two follow-up questions will need to be answered.
Q1: How do index and enhanced index tracking models differ in methodology?
Q2: How can an ESG-focused subset of stocks be systematically selected?

From Mansini et al., 2015 the index tracking models’ objective functions minimize the mean absolute

Also in Mansini et al., 2015, they describe an approach for the enhanced index tracking model. The
enhanced index tracking problem is described as a bi-objective model maximizing the portfolio’s excess
return over the index it tracks, while also minimizing tracking error. They use a principle called index-
plus-alpha. This term refers to a portfolio that outperforms its benchmark by a certain amount α where
the negative deviation from the index is also never more than a predefined tolerance. This bi-objective
model seems, from the literature, to be one of the most used ways to create the enhanced index models,
however, (Cesarone et al., 2022) have tested a more novel approach to expand the objective of the
model to have a third optimization parameter. Here, they added to a mean-variance analysis a third
non-financial goal represented by the ESG ratings.

2.3
ESG, Portfolio Optimization and Research Gap
It was found that a lot of the literature on ESG was under Category 3, "ESG Portfolio Management
Strategies", which covered how fund managers have started to focus on using ESG in constructing
portfolios. However, these papers lacked quantitative data and instead focused on qualitative methods,
such as interviews with the fund managers. From the literature on portfolio optimization, it was found
that a lot of research has gone into implementing and testing both index and enhanced index tracking
models.
Enhanced models, while typically bi-objective, have also been theorized and tested as tri-
objective models, implementing ESG ratings as an optimization parameter, where ESG ratings have
already been made by MSCI (Utz et al., 2015). For these models, a concern arises as although the
model optimizes for the ESG rating, its simultaneous optimization for two other parameter can lead to
outcomes where companies with poor ESG performance still become part of the portfolio because they
are found to have a high economic return or help the model achieve better tracking accuracy.
There seems to be little research on optimizing a portfolio by excluding the worst ESG-rated stocks
before running the optimization models on the data, thereby ensuring that no poorly ESG-rated stocks
could become part of the portfolio.

3
Theory
This section provides the theoretical foundation for understanding index and enhanced index tracking
models with and without rebalancings. It begins with an overview of ESG principles and their relevance to
investment decisions, followed by an exploration of financial theories, including asset valuation, portfolio,
and investment fund structures and regulations. Additionally, fundamental concepts such as market
indexes, their role in financial markets, and specific methodologies like the MSCI ESG rating system are
introduced.
3.1
ESG Rating
An ESG rating is used as a tool to look at a company from a non-financial perspective. The ESG rating
of a company gives insight into its focus on sustainability and ethics (Edwards & Hadley, 2024).
The environmental pillar of ESG looks into the companies’ influence on climate change, their use of
natural capital, and how they handle pollution and waste.
The social pillar focuses on the companies’ impact on people, including employees, customers, and the
communities they operate in. It includes safety measures and looks not only at the company itself but
also at the standards of its supply chain.
The last pillar, governance, looks at the systems and structures that make up the company and how
management and decision-making in the company happen.
ESG reporting is not standardized worldwide and can be done following different guidelines and by
different ESG providers. However, different organisations, like the EU and UN, have made regulatory
sustainability standards to increase comparability and consistency (Edwards & Hadley, 2024).
3.1.1
MSCI ESG Rating Methodology
MSCI is one of the largest providers of ESG ratings in the world (Lovas, 2023), and provides the ESG
ratings used in this thesis following what was found in section 2.2. The ESG rating is a combined score
reflecting how well a company performs over the three pillars, adjusted relative to its industry.
The overall structure of the MSCI ESG rating methodology can be seen in figure 3.1. A score is found
for each of the three pillars after which a weighted average is calculated to give a Weighted Average Key
Issue Score. This is then adjusted relative to industry peers, and the score is translated into a final ESG
Letter Rating (MSCI ESG Research Products and Services, 2024).

Figure 3.1: Overview of MSCI’s ESG rating method (MSCI ESG Research Products and Services, 2024)
The Governance pillar is similar across industries, whereas the Environment and Social pillars can differ
a lot based on the company and industry. The MSCI method of calculating an Environmental Pillar
Score and a Social Pillar Score follows the same methodology.
For the Social and Environmental pillars, each company is evaluated on two to seven Key Issues out of a
total of 33. These Key Issues depend on the sub-industry the company belongs to. MSCI has created 163
different sub-industries, all with a selection of Key Issues that are relevant based on industry-specific and
market-specific factors, with each company assigned to exactly one. Some companies might differ from
the standard of their sub-industry on essential matters and can then get a Company-Specific Key Issue
added to their evaluation. The Key Issues in each sub-industry are weighted based on their contribution
to negative externalities.
The Key Issues in these two pillars are calculated based on Exposure Scores and Management Scores.
These cover the companies’ risk exposure and ability and commitment to address opportunities and
risks.
The Governance Pillar Score is found differently from the other two since it is not impacted by the
industry the company belongs to. A company’s Governance Pillar Score is composed of a weighted sum
of its Governance Key Issue Scores, where each Key Issues are scored between 1 and 10. These scores
are found by looking at a checklist of Key Metrics that the company can either comply with or not.

3.2
Financial Theory
Financial theory covers the understanding of assets, transaction costs, portfolios and investment funds
and the connection between these. The interaction between these and market indexes, specifically the
S&P 500 is examined over different market conditions.
3.2.1
Assets
An asset is something of financial value that can be traded and is currently owned by a company or
person (Kindness, 2024).
The value of an asset comes from the expectation of its future performance, which is partly based on past
performance, perceived value, and sometimes the strength of the underlying brand. The current value
of an asset is its market price. This value changes with time, and the percentage change over a specified
time period is called its rate of return (Mansini et al., 2015). The higher the rate of return, the more
money is gained from an asset. However, the future rate of return is uncertain, and so there is a risk
connected to investing in any asset. Some assets have higher uncertainties in their returns and therefore
carry more risk whereas others are more predictable and stable. The fluctuation in an asset’s price over
time is known as its volatility, which is an indicator of the risk associated with the asset (Mansini et al.,
2015).
Some assets might be less volatile, which may appeal to many investors, but at the same time, be more
difficult to turn back into cash. These are known as illiquid or non-liquid assets and require longer
12
==PAGE:23==
3
THEORY
periods of time, typically days or weeks, but sometimes even years before they are sold. These types of
investments are typically real estate, antiques, jewelry or collections such as cars or coins. On the other
side, there are liquid assets that can quickly be converted to cash, from seconds or, in the worst case,
a few days. Typical liquid assets are stocks or government bonds (Penfed, 2023). Stocks and anything
else that can be invested in or traded on financial markets have a ticker symbol. This is a unique way
of identifying them, typically a short abbreviation that sometimes includes both letters and numbers
(Hayes, 2025).
3.2.2
Transactions Costs
When an asset is bought, the associated cost of buying it is known as transaction costs. Transaction costs
may include searching for a product or service, planning, cost analysis, and commissions (GEP, 2025).
From a financial investment, these costs are typically the broker fees, where a broker is commissioned for
facilitating the transaction of a stock or fund (CFI, 2025). When a private person buys stocks, the main
transaction fee is the commission charged by the stockbroker. This cost is typically set as a percentage
of the invested amount and is typically below 0.3% (Saxo, 2025). If a bigger investment fund is buying
stocks then the fees a lower than 0.1% (Saxo, 2025). But since funds buy stocks with a large amount
of capital, it must also account for market impact. This refers to the cost associated with a stock price
increase while the transaction is happening. For example, a stock priced at 12 dollars per share might
increase to 12.02 dollars due to the demand that the transaction immediately creates (Dr. Ferraris,
2008).
3.2.3
Portfolios
Portfolios are collections of assets that aim to balance risk and return based on the risk profile of the
investor.
When constructing a portfolio, the goal is to maximize returns while minimizing risk. This can be done
in a multitude of ways, depending on the risk aversion of the investor. The line where risk is minimized
and return is maximized is called the risk-return frontier and can be seen in figure 3.3.
The points A, B and C cover three different portfolio compositions. Portfolios A and C have the same
return, but portfolio C carries a higher risk, making portfolio A more attractive. Portfolios B and C
have the same risk, but portfolio B has a higher return making it more attractive than portfolio C. A
portfolio such as C, where there is a portfolio with higher return for the same level of risk or lower risk
for the same level of return, is said to be dominated by these portfolios (Mansini et al., 2015).

3.2.4
Rebalancing
When stocks are added or removed from a portfolio or fund, so that it more closely aligns with the
intention of the fund, this is called a rebalancing (Croome, 2024).
Funds change over time, even when no stocks are added or removed, because the value of the stocks
changes, and so the composition of the fund changes too. This can lead to a different picture of risk than
initially accepted, and rebalancing might be necessary to bring the portfolio back to the desired level.
A rebalancing can also be used to secure a specific composition of stocks in the fund. For example, if a
fund has half its assets in technology stocks and the other half in healthcare stocks. If the technology
stocks increase in value while the healthcare stocks value decreases, the composition will no longer be
evenly split. Therefore, it is necessary to sell some of the technology stocks and buy more stocks in
healthcare to regain a 50-50 split.

3.2.6
Investment Funds
Investment funds are based on capital from many different investors used to buy assets collectively. It is
a shared portfolio for all the investors, and it gives more investment opportunities and lower fees since
costs are spread across multiple participants. Diversification is also improved because funds can invest
in a broader range of assets than an individual investor might be able to afford on their own (Chen,
2025b).
The individual external investors in an investment fund do not influence what assets are a part of the
fund. Instead, investors choose funds based on, among other things, the assets they hold, the associated
risk, and the fees.
Investment funds can be either actively or passively managed. Actively managed investment funds have
a fund manager who is in charge of what assets are in the fund and what changes should be made to
the composition of the fund. These funds can be focused on assets from specific areas, like technology
assets or CO2 friendly assets. The fund managers’ responsibility is to try and construct a portfolio that
outperforms the market (Chen, 2025b). Passively managed funds also called index funds, try to track

an index, (see section 3.2.8) and instead of having a human managing the fund and making decisions on
when and what to buy/sell, an algorithm makes these decisions by mirroring the index in question (Chen,
2022). Exchange-traded funds (ETFs) are funds that can be bought and sold on an exchange. Investors
buy a share in the fund, and this share then holds multiple other shares of underlying companies (Chen,
2025a).
3.2.7
UCITS
The Undertakings for Collective Investment in Transferable Securities (UCITS) is a regulatory frame-
work established by the European Union to facilitate cross-border investment while ensuring investor
protection and market stability. Introduced in 1985 under Directive 85/611/EEC, UCITS has undergone
several revisions to adapt to financial market developments. (EU, 2024).
Being UCITS compliant can be attractive for a fund. This is because two of UCITS features are harmo-
nization and passporting. This allows a UCITS fund authorized in one EU member state to be marketed
and sold across all EU and European Economic Area (EEA) countries, without requiring additional
regulatory approvals. The funds that are UCTIS compliant provide a high level of investor protection
and liquidity by having to adhere to a set of rules such as. (EU, 2024)
1. At least 90% of the fund’s investments must be placed in liquid assets
2. No single assets must represent more than 10% of the funds assets
3. All assets of more than 5% of the fund’s assets under management (AUM) must not accumulate
to more than 40% of AUM.
4. Investors must be able to redeem their funds twice a month
These rules, which try to reduce the risk of investments, as well as the transparency in the requirements
to be UCITS compliant, have made investing in UCITS funds popular not only in Europe but also in
South America and Asia (James, 2023). After the 2008 financial crisis, Busack et al., 2014 describes
that investors sought to find more liquid, transparent, and regulated investment products, and in 2016
the European Commission estimated that UCITS funds had e 9 trillion of assets under management,
accounting for around 75% of all investments by small investors in Europe (Kozlowska-Sugar, 2016).
This value has increased by 37% to e 12.3 trillion in 2023, showing a continuous interest in investing in
funds labeled as UCITS compliant (EFAMA, 2023).

of stocks can be selected as representatives of that market segment, and based on their performance,

How much an individual stock influences the performance of an index is determined by its weight (J.
Young, 2023). The weight is typically corresponding to the value of the company, where large companies
have a bigger weight on the index.
3.2.9
S&P 500 Index
The Standard and Poor’s 500 index, commonly known as S&P 500, was introduced in 1957. It was
intended to gauge the performance of the U.S. stock market by tracking the value of the top 500 most
valuable companies listed on the New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) and the NASDAQ exchange. Today,
it covers approximately 80% of the U.S. equity market capitalization (market cap) and over 50% of the
global equity market (S&P 500 Global, 2025a).
The index composition is adjusted quarterly to reflect changes in the underlying companies. Stringent
criteria for companies are set to be eligible for the index, and if these criteria are not met, companies
will be removed from the index and replaced by others. The criteria are mainly focused on the economic
performance of the company, such as having a minimum market cap of $20.5 billion and having positive
earnings over the last 4 quarters following the General Accepted Accounting Principle (SPDJIl, 2025).
The weight of each company on the index is determined by its float market capitalization, which means
that the market cap is multiplied by the percentage of its shares that are available to be traded (SPDJIl,
2025). This results in bigger companies with high float market capitalization having a greater influence
on the performance of the index compared to smaller companies.
3.2.10
Market Conditions
While the price of a share in the S&P 500 has increased from $ 40 to above $ 5000, increasing on average
7% every year, the price has not been on a constant upward trajectory. Throughout its history, the
index has experienced 12 bear markets defined as the price of the index falling 20% or more since the
previous bull market’s top. These bear markets have, on average, seen the index fall by 33% and took
approximately 13 months to return to the previous highest value. However, during bull markets, defined
as the index increases 20% or more since the previous bear market’s low, the index has seen on average
a 160% gain over 5 years (S&P 500 Global, 2025a).

3.3
Portfolio Optimization
In this section, a dispersion method is chosen and introduced for use in the optimization model. An
index tracking model and an enhanced index tracking model are also presented, both with and without
rebalancing. Lastly, the modeling assumptions underlying these approaches are discussed.
3.3.1
Comparison of Dispersion Methods
In a comparison of the three dispersion methods, mean-variance, MAD and CVaR, the paper Pavlou
et al., 2019 outlined some findings on the different approaches. It was found that CVaR models on
average combined portfolios with fewer stocks than the other models. This leads to lower diversification
in these portfolios. On the other hand, the MAD model made the most diversified portfolios. Another
paper, Kasenbacher et al., 2017, compared the MAD model and the mean-variance model, and found
that the mean-variance model was outperformed by the MAD model in 5 out of 6 cases. It was found
that the MAD model gave a greater rate of return, without having greater risk and thus is the more
robust model. There was not found any comparison with VaR, but it is assumed that the performance
would be relatively close to CVaR, given that they are based on the same foundation (Mansini et al.,
2015).
Although it was found in these comparisons that MAD had a more diverse portfolio than CVaR and
outperformed the mean-variance model, it is also important that this is probably highly dependent on
the specific scenarios and so the result might differ if other stocks are used or additional constraints are

3.3.4
Index Tracking Model with Rebalancing
Rebalancing is introduced in the model to combat multiple challenges with the original model. Firstly,
compliance with UCITS rules 2 and 3 is only ensured at the time of purchase. If the market value of
the chosen stocks changes in such a way that the rules are no longer satisfied, a rebalancing is needed to
find a new composition that complies with the rules.
Secondly, to comply with UCITS regulations, investors must be able to redeem their funds twice a month
(rule 4 section 3.2.7). Since the model invests all available capital, some will have to be pulled out for
people to be able to get their money out. One way of handling this is by introducing biweekly rebalancing.

3.3.5
Enhanced Index Tracking Model
The index tracking model aims to choose a portfolio that is as close as possible to the chosen index.
When looking at the deviation from the index, upside and downside deviations are weighted equally.
In reality, most investors would find upward deviation to be a good thing as this would mean that the
portfolio outperforms the index. This is the idea that the enhanced index tracking model builds on.
The model goes from merely trying to find a portfolio with a minimum difference between that and the
index, to finding one with the maximum possible outperformance, but still ensuring that the tracking
error does not exceed a given limit.
In Mansini et al., 2015, an index-plus-alpha portfolio is described as a portfolio that outperforms its
benchmark by a percentage amount α.
α is a variable that is maximized in the objective function.
The index value is multiplied by α to create an index-plus-alpha line. The sum of downward deviation
from the index-plus-alpha has to stay within a certain dollar amount, hence the tracking part of the
model. This model strives for an outperformance of the original index with a constant α throughout the
period. Having this constant α throughout seems counterintuitive since the portfolio and the index start
with the same value before deviations can begin and accumulate. This makes it so that the first days
after selecting a portfolio, it is always down from the index-plus-alpha line, counting towards the limited
downward deviation dollar amount.
Because of this behavior, the model was changed to reflect a growing outperformance of the index over
time by introducing a weight, wt, which is multiplied by α. Defining wt as wt =
t
T −1 for t = 0, . . . , T −1,
makes the weighted index-plus-alpha a linearly growing line above the index. Furthermore, only focusing
on downward deviation for tracking error seemed unintuitive and so a tolerance Ωwas introduced that
only allowed a percentage deviation form the index-plus-alpha line, on both upward and downward
deviation.

The enhanced index tracking model works by putting together a portfolio that outperforms the index
as much as possible while keeping the tracking error within acceptable limits. This follows the premise
that stocks that have outperformed the index in the past will continue to outperform it in the future.
However, in practice, stocks that have experienced significant gains may be overvalued and could revert
to more sustainable price levels rather than continuing their upward trajectory, also known as mean
reversion. Mean reversion is a financial theory that suggests that assets will eventually revert to their
long-term average prices (Chen, 2025c).
3.4
Modelling Assumptions
From the proposed models, there are some inherent assumptions made that might not be truly accurate
in real-world investing. This includes, for example, how much of each stock is bought. Here, it is assumed
that buying fractions of stocks is possible. This is not always possible, as some stock brokers do not have
this option. Furthermore, the stock brokers that have the option limit it to certain stocks (G. Young,
2024). The presented index and enhanced index tracking models could be changed to allow only buying
an integer number of a stock, by only allowing κ to be integer values. But instead, κ will be rounded to
an integer after a model is run to decrease running time. If κ changed to only allow an integer amount
of a stock, the model would potentially need to look through more solutions after having found an LP-
relaxed version of the problem to find the optimal one. This would result in increased running time in
nearly all cases.
The model will be used from the perspective of a large-cap fund. This influences the available capital
to be much larger than if seen from a private investor’s perspective and the commissions that funds pay
are also lower than what a private investor is paying (Saxo, 2025). It is assumed that the transaction
costs are paid out of the investors’ investment account rather than through another account, also known
as a self-financing portfolio (Strub & Baumann, 2017). This does have a small influence on the invested
capital compared to externally financed portfolios, where costs are paid for by another account.
Figure 3.7: Comparison of self-financing and externally financed portfolios
The model does not take into account that a stock might pay dividends. If a portfolio contains a stock
that pays a dividend, it will not be calculated into the final value of the portfolio. Only increases in the
stock price can increase the portfolio value.

4
Modelling Methodology
This section explains the methodology for evaluating the model regarding parameter selection and model
evaluation. The acquisition and preparation of data is also described. Then, the parameter selection for
both the index model and the enhanced index model is further explored, leading to a selection of the
data-specific optimal parameters. Lastly, the portfolio selection period is chosen for the models.
4.1
Evaluation Methodology
To evaluate the model, an adequate timeline is needed, together with relevant performance scores that
can be used to compare the models.
4.1.1
Timeline
The analysis will be based on data from the years 2005 to 2024. These years cover periods with different
market trends and can therefore be used to train and test the model under different conditions. First,
the parameters of the models will be determined by looking at a period that spans 2006-2017 (Parameter
selection period). 2005 is excluded since no data from 2004 is available to run the model on. For both
the index tracking model and the enhanced index tracking model, the parameters to be determined are
c and ¯C, while the index tracking model also needs a value for pt and for the enhanced index tracking
model, the parameter Ωmust also be specified.
After using the first period to determine the parameters, the years 2018-2024 (Model evaluation period)
will be used to analyze the performance of the model. The selection and evaluation method is based on
the rolling window approach, where each time period, except for the first and last, will be used both to
form a new portfolio and evaluate the portfolio from the last time period. This is illustrated in figure
4.1 as an example with one year used for the portfolio selection and evaluation period. In this example,
the two-year periods use the first year to put together a portfolio, where only stocks that have been part
of the index the whole year will be considered. The portfolio will be constructed on the last day of the
year. The second year will be used to analyze the performance of the constructed portfolio. In section
4.5 the time frame of the portfolio selection period is investigated, so instead of looking at only one year,
multiple lengths for the period are looked into.

4.1.2
Evaluation of Model Performance
To evaluate the models, their tracking and return compared to the index will be studied. The index
tracking model should ideally track the index with zero error, whereas the enhanced index tracking model
should have higher returns than the index.
The models will be further analyzed by looking into years affected by bull and bear markets, giving
insights as to whether the models only perform well in good or bad times, or are stable in both. How
the tracking evolved over the entire period (2006-2024) will also be studied to see if the models improve
over time. The actual return of the models will also be investigated to see what returns can be expected
and how that return would have accumulated over time. The standard deviation of the daily return
is also important, as it shows the models’ volatility and gives insight into the risk associated with the
constructed portfolios.
From the standard deviation and daily portfolio returns, the Sharpe ratio will be calculated, as it is
a measure that combines the return of an investment and the risk into a single score. This supports
investors in assessing whether the portfolio’s performance is worth the associated risk.
Lastly, the number of stocks selected by the model will be looked at from a stability perspective, as a
large or small number of different stocks might indicate how sensitive the model is to market changes.

4.2
Data Preparation
To begin, the list of constituent companies in the S&P 500 as of 2024 was collected from SP Global
(S&P 500 Global, 2025b). Historical information on companies added to and removed from the S&P 500
index from the beginning of 2005 to the beginning of 2024 was also obtained (Keith, 2025). From these
two lists, it was possible to extract the companies that had been on the S&P 500 from the years 2005
through 2024.
To maintain consistency, company names were standardized to avoid issues with the same company
having two different names, such as "Workday" and "Workday, Inc.". In addition, the ticker symbols
were updated if the company had been acquired by another company or rebranded with a new ticker
symbol and no data could be found from the relevant time periods for the original company.

stock prices were used instead, since the values for the old ticker symbol could not be found. This does
alter the historical performance of a company compared to what it was, but is still included since it
resulted in a larger number of stocks that could be used for the performance analysis of the models.
Daily stock prices for all stocks during the period in which they were part of the S&P 500 from 2005 to
2024 were gathered. Companies where no data could be found, for instance, because they went private
or went bankrupt, have been removed.
In cases where the company is acquired by another company during a year and there is no stock data for
the rest of the year, the stock price is kept constant to simulate that, the money that was in the stock,
has been paid out to the investor who now would have that value in cash rather than having it invested.
An ESG rating was assigned to all S&P 500 stocks, using data from MSCI when available. MSCI ESG
ratings are recalculated each year, but cannot be found for the whole parameter selection period and

Selection of Parameters - Index Tracking
Three parameters in the index tracking model need to be selected. These are ¯C, c and pt. For the
selection of parameters, the portfolio selection and evaluation period is set to one year each.
4.3.1
Available Capital, ¯C
To find a fair value for ¯C, the average assets under management of the 100 largest ETFs were found.
From these, the average was found to be $73,659,413, which was chosen as the value of ¯C (VettaFi, 2024).
4.3.2
Transaction Cost, c
The transaction costs for the model should be based on the transaction costs for a large-cap fund given
the chosen ¯C. The average cost of a US large cap fund (years 2003-2008) was 23 basis points (bps),
divided between 9 bps for commissions and 14 bps for market impact (Dr. Ferraris, 2008), and this is
therefore the value used for c. Basis points (bps) express percentage changes typically for interest rates,
yields, or fees and are defined as 1bps = 0.01%.

4.4.1
Tolerance, Ω
Certain values were tested and evaluated to find the tolerance that the model should be allowed to deviate
from the weighted index-plus-alpha line. A too tight tolerance might hinder the model unnecessarily by
not allowing stocks that have minor price swings throughout the year. On the other hand, too large a
tolerance can make the investor nervous if the portfolio becomes highly volatile. Therefore, the Ωvalues
in the range 1% to 5%, incrementing by 0.5%, are tested.
To evaluate what Ωis optimal, a performance score is created.
Performance score = 2 AverageTrackingPerf −StandardDeviationTrackingPerf
(4.12)
The tracking performance is the percentage the portfolio value is above or below the index in the
portfolio evaluation period. Similar to the performance score for the index tracking model, the tracking
performance is weighted more than the standard deviation. Here, however, a high performance score is
preferred. There is still an emphasis on trying to have a consistent model from year to year in the form of
minimal standard deviation tracking performance. This is important as varying performance can make
it unattractive to investors.
By utilizing this performance score and finding the maximum value, emphasis has been placed on having
a portfolio that throughout the year is outperforming the index (Average tracking performance) and
consistent from year to year (Standard deviation tracking performance).

companies that have narrowly outperformed it.
The same plot is made for the All Stocks model and can be seen below.
Figure 4.15: Performance score of All Stocks model with different values for Ω
From figure 4.15, the performance score reveals that an Ωvalue of 4.5% is the optimal for the All Stocks
model, closely followed by 3.5%. This marks that there is a clear difference between the this and the
ESG Selected model with it’s narrower 2% tolerance. This could perhaps be down to the fact that the
All Stocks has many larger companies to choose from and therefore it needs much greater tolerances
before it starts choosing larger positions in smaller companies. These smaller companies might then have
more upside potential in the following year.
Based on the analysis above. For the ESG Selected portfolio, the chosen value for Ωis therefore 2% while
it is 4.5% for the All Stocks model. This is a significant difference in the tolerance levels for deviation
from the weighted index-plus-alpha line, meaning that the ESG Selected model has stricter tracking
constraints compared to the All Stocks model.

5.2
Index Tracking with Rebalancing - Results
In the first rebalancing model, the model makes rebalancings biweekly and when any of the UCITS rules

5.2.1
Changed Handling of UCITS Regulations
Figure 5.7 shows that the ESG Selected model consistently struggled to keep up with the index, especially
after periods of a lot of rebalancing caused by violations of UCITS constraints. In order to try and improve
the performance of this model, a revision was made in which the constraints regarding the UCITS rules
in the model were tightened. This was implemented by decreasing the 5% constraint to 4.5%, the 10%
constraint to 9% and finally the 40% constraint to 36% during rebalancings. That way, when the model
decides what portfolio to construct, the stocks can have larger price increases or decreases before the
program forces the model to perform rebalancing, as decided by the original UCITS rules, which have
not been changed. This was tested with both biweekly and quarterly rebalancings to see which model
would give the most satisfactory performance.

DISCUSSION
6
Discussion
In this section, the key decisions made throughout the report are discussed. This includes the handling
of data, the selection of time periods looked at, and the chosen way of handling UCITS regulations.
Additionally, the parameter selection method was looked at together with overall modeling assumptions
on how to identify stocks with potential for future outperformance.
6.1
Data
When preparing the data, it was decided that if the historical prices of a stock were not available, then it
was looked into why that was. If a company was acquired by another company, the stock prices for the
acquired company were then set to the prices of the company that bought it. Especially in the earlier
years, this will have created portfolios where the performance is not truly accurate to what it would
have been if all prices were available. Alternatively, the acquired companies with no original stock prices
could have been excluded from the data. This would have resulted in a more accurate price change in
the portfolio, but would have given the model fewer companies to choose from, which could also impact
the performance.
If the models bought a stock that was then taken off the S&P 500 during the year, the price was kept
constant to simulate that the stock was sold and the fund would instead have the stock value in cash.
Alternatively, it could have been implemented that the model would put that money into increasing the
number of stocks already part of the portfolio.
When selecting what periods to use for parameter selection and model analysis, it was chosen to go with
consecutive periods of 2006-2017 for the parameter selection and 2018-2024 for the analysis period. The
later years had fewer stocks with missing price information, therefore, the data would be more accurate.
The years could have been sampled more randomly so that both parameter selection and analysis periods
had a combination of earlier and later years. This would have increased data quality in the parameter
selection period, but reduced it for the analysis period. With a different sampling method, it could
also be more difficult to analyze the model’s performance over several years, without analyzing both
evaluation and training data at the same time.
6.2
Rebalancings - UCITS Regulations
Transaction costs were challenging for both the index and enhanced index tracking models. Both planned
rebalancings and those triggered by UCITS regulation breaches were a cause of high transaction costs,
which made the model diverge from the index. This was particularly evident in the ESG Selected model,
86
==PAGE:97==
6
DISCUSSION
where regulatory-driven rebalancings had a notably negative impact.
To address this, while still complying with the UCITS rule "Investors must be able to redeem their funds
twice a month", an alternative approach could be used. Withdrawals could be distributed proportionally
based on the existing stock weights in the portfolio, and the same for newly introduced capital.
6.3
Parameter Selection
Both the index tracking model and the enhanced index tracking model had a parameter that differed
depending on whether it was the All Stocks model or the ESG Selected model. This was chosen so that
the parameter was optimal for each dataset, and a comparison could be made between optimal models.
For the enhanced index tracking model, the differing parameter was Ω. Ωdescribes the tolerance level
permitted for the model to deviate from the weighted index-plus-alpha line. This tolerance level influences
how much risk a portfolio can contain, which could influence the number and composition of stocks in
the portfolio.
Differentiating Ωfor the models, therefore, means that the models can take different risks, which can
restrict one model more than the other.
Instead of having different values for Ω, an Ωvalue could have been chosen that was the same for each
model. This would restrict the models in the same way, but also limit them, as the chosen tolerance
level would not necessarily be optimal for both models.
When selecting how many years the model should look at for the portfolio selection period, the number
of years was decided after the optimal parameters had been found. However, these parameters were
optimal for the model when running with one year for both portfolio selection and evaluation. This
might have influenced the outcome of how many years were optimal for the portfolio selection period.
The parameters could instead have been optimized together so that the number of years and pt for the
index tracking model were optimized simultaneously. The same would then have to be the case for a
number of years and Ωfor the enhanced index model.
6.4
Model Assumptions
The enhanced tracking model is based on the assumption that creating a portfolio that had increased
the most the year before and is still within a certain tracking tolerance would be the best portfolio for
the next year. The index-plus-alpha approach was quite common when studying the literature, but in
this thesis, it was modified with the term wt so that the model could start at the same value as the
index without it counting as being below the alpha-plus-index value. This did in return significantly
increase the alpha values to a minimum of 39%, equivalent to a compound annual growth rate of 11.6%
compared to the mid to low single-digit alpha values that the normal index-plus-alpha models got. This
shows that the model is now able to find portfolios that have significantly outperformed the index in
the portfolio selection year. With the significant outperformance, there is a risk that the concept of
87
==PAGE:98==
6

mean reversion has a high influence on the model. So by making a model that buys stocks that have
significantly increased in the prior years, the model might more often buy stocks that revert to an average
long-term price. To try and combat this theoretical problem, a parameter could have been introduced
to limit how many percent alpha could go to. That way, the model is more incentivized to buy stocks
that have only moderately outperformed the index.

CONCLUSION
7
Conclusion
In recent years, ESG considerations have become more integrated into investment strategies, driven by
ethical concerns, environmental consciousness and competitive returns. The tradeoff between investing
for higher returns or investing in companies with a focus on sustainability has long been discussed,
centering around the competitiveness of strong ESG companies compared to ’black’ companies. Funds
with varying focuses have been developed to address a wide range of investor needs, and this project
has focused on comparing ESG-focused portfolios with portfolios without ESG concerns. To create the
portfolios, index and enhanced index tracking models that either try to track or outperform the index
can be used. This led to the central research question:
How do index and enhanced index tracking models perform on
the S&P 500 when restricted to an ESG-focused subset of its stocks?
From the literature study, it was found that the theoretical foundation for index and enhanced index
models has been studied since 1952, when Markowitz introduced mean-variance portfolio optimization.
From a selection of theory books and recent written studies that were analyzed, the methodology of the
index tracking model and the enhanced index tracking model were found.
The index tracking model tries to replicate the performance of an index. Different dispersion methods
can be used to track the distance between the index tracking model, and in this thesis, the Mean Absolute
Deviation (MAD) approach was used. The index model minimizes the deviation between the portfolio
returns and index returns, subject to constraints such as budget and, in this case, UCITS regulations.
The enhanced index tracking model takes it one step further and tries to outperform the index by a
percentage amount α while still ensuring that the downward or upward tracking error does not exceed a
given limit, Ω. This answers the first follow-up question.
Q1: How do index and enhanced index tracking models differ in methodology?
When trying to find studies on how ESG-focused portfolios compared to indexes, there were no satisfying
studies that had excluded poorly ESG-rated companies but instead allowed the models to optimize
for ESG rating, tracking, and outperformance of the index simultaneously. This raised concern that
portfolios could contain companies with poor ESG ratings if they help optimize the other two parameters.
Instead, this project composed a dataset based on ESG ratings from MSCI, one of the leaders when it
comes to assigning ESG ratings to companies. Finding the assigned MSCI ESG rating for all companies
in the S&P 500 from 2005 to 2024 and excluding companies below an A rating ultimately answered the
second follow-up question.
Q2: How can an ESG-focused subset of stocks be systematically selected?

CONCLUSION
An index tracking model and an enhanced index tracking model were set up and run on both a dataset
with a focus on ESG (ESG Selected) and one without (All Stocks), to look into the performance impli-
cations and trade-offs of investing with an ESG focus.
To test and analyze the performance of the models, a dataset containing all companies that have been
on the S&P 500 from 2005 to 2024 was created, and if available, the daily stock prices were found in
the years they were a part of the index. When data was not available, it was studied why and if the
companies had been acquired, the prices from the company that acquired them were used. From this
dataset, the All Stocks dataset, an ESG Selected dataset, was created, containing only companies with
an ESG rating of A or above. This excluded companies with no ESG rating and a lower than A rating,
resulting in the ESG Selected dataset having 46.8% fewer companies than the All Stocks dataset.
The index tracking model and the enhanced index tracking model were expanded to comply with some
fundamental UCITS regulations designed to provide strong investor protection and maintain a high de-
gree of liquidity in the funds.
To ensure a fair evaluation of the performance of the ESG Selected and All Stocks models, adjustable
parameters were individually optimized for each model. Moreover, it was found that the index tracking
model performed best when data from one year was used for selecting a portfolio, whereas the enhanced
index tracking model performed best when three years were used.
When analyzing the performance of the models, the years 2018 to 2024 were used. For the index tracking
models, the focus was on how far the created portfolios’ returns deviated from the S&P 500 (tracking
error). For the enhanced index tracking models, the focus shifted to how much they outperformed the
index (tracking performance). A comparison of the number of stocks bought, total value invested in
top 10 most influential positions, average tracking error or performance, yearly return, accumulated
return, Sharpe ratio and the trend of average tracking error or performance, was made between the ESG
Selected models, All Stocks models and the S&P 500, both for the index tracking and enhanced index
tracking models. For the trend of average tracking error or performance and number of tickers bought,
a comparison of the parameter selection period (2006-2017) and the analysis period (2018-2024) was
performed to get a longer horizon to look at. Furthermore, it was looked at how the models perform in
different market conditions. First, the analysis focused on models with no rebalancing, and afterward,
rebalancing was implemented to see how it influenced the return and Sharpe ratio of the models.
For the index tracking model, the results from the non-rebalancing models are clear. The All Stocks
model had better performance than the ESG model. For all years, the return profile of the portfolio
resembled that of the index, not only in yearly return but also Sharpe ratio. The trend from 2006 to 2024
showed that the All Stocks model was generally better and was improving the tracking error at a faster
rate than the ESG model. The model’s performance showed no dependence on the market condition.
When implementing rebalancing to try and satisfy UCITS criteria, the performance suffered greatly due
to the number of rebalancings needed to comply with the criteria. To compensate for this, changes were
90
==PAGE:101==
7
CONCLUSION
made to the model by tightening the UCITS constraints, allowing for bigger price changes in individual
stocks before UCITS criteria were violated. This was done to try to get satisfactory results from the
models. After the changes had been made to the rebalancing models, the final results were a little more
mixed. Looking at the yearly returns and the Sharpe ratio, the performance of the All Stocks model was
the better option. But when looking at the cumulative returns, the ESG Selected model had more years
where it was closest to the index.
The non-rebalancing enhanced index tracking model outperformed the index for both the ESG Selected
dataset and the All Stocks dataset. The ESG Selected model had both a higher mean return and a
lower standard deviation than the All Stocks model, indicating that the ESG Selected model would be
the more favorable financial investment. The market conditions did not have any general impact on the
models, but the yearly performance of the ESG Selected model generally coincided with the performance
of the broad ESG segment.
The enhanced index tracking model for both the ESG Selected dataset and the All Stocks dataset, when
doing standard rebalancings, underperformed compared to the index. When the changed handling of
UCITS regulations was introduced, both models performed better but still did not manage to outperform
the index. Only when solely doing quarterly rebalancings did both models manage to outperform the
S&P 500. The All Stocks model did, however, have a higher average return and a better Sharpe ratio,
making it the better investment opportunity from a purely financial point of view.
All in all, restricting an index and enhanced index tracking model to an ESG-focused subset of stocks
does have varying impacts on their performance. The index model suffered the worst impact from this
limitation. The ESG-focused subset did not in this thesis allow it to choose between many of the largest
companies in the world, which seemed to be a cornerstone for the All Stocks model’s better and more
stable performance both with and without rebalancing.
The ESG Selected enhanced index tracking
model without rebalancings managed to outperform both the index and the All Stocks model. When
implementing the final rebalancing criteria, the ESG Selected model did perform well and managed to
outperform the index and the All Stocks model for many years, when looking at the return. However,
when calculating the Sharpe ratio, the ESG Selected model’s performance does come with a worse risk-
reward profile than the S&P 500 and the All Stocks model.

FURTHER WORK
8
Further Work
In this paper, the ESG Selected and All Stocks models were implemented and tested relative to each
other and the S&P 500. This made it easy to see how the models’ performances were influenced by
excluding companies with a less than desired ESG rating. This did, however, only create conclusions
based on the work performed in this thesis. For further work, it could be interesting to look into how the
models compare to models in other papers, especially given that changes were made to the rigorously
tested index-plus-alpha method. The inclusion of UCITS constraints was also absent in previous papers
and so a deeper investigation of how this has altered the performance could be interesting.
The choice of when a company can be considered good in terms of ESG could be further investigated to
see what impact it would have on portfolio performance. Adjusting the selection thresholds for the ESG
Selected dataset, varying the minimum ESG score required to be a part of it. A comparison could be
made to see the tradeoff between return and sustainability-focused investing.
