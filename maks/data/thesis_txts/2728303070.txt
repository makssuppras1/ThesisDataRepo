Abstract 
 
 
As greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions in the maritime industry are increasing, it is projected that 
the emissions from this sector will considerably increase by 2050 by around 50% compared to 
the recorded emissions levels of 2008. Therefore, the need for decreasing and subsequently 
eliminating the GHG emissions from international shipping is considered to be crucial to 
protect the environment and maintain balance in the ecosystem. Decarbonizing shipping is a 
subject that started being widely discussed in the end of the previous decade, with more 
emphasis given during the last few years, on projects and actions that could accelerate 
reaching low- and zero-carbon emissions across the maritime sector. One of the new 
pathways towards decarbonization is the establishment of maritime green corridors, which 
constitute ambitious routes showcasing alternative fuel utilization and clean technologies in 
all phases and aspects of the respective supply chain. Due to the recent declaration of interest 
from various countries and stakeholders for such a project on a global scale, international 
organizations have initiated official agreements and partnerships aiming at collaboratively 
transitioning to alternative fuels and thus eliminating carbon emission in shipping. In this 
study, an assessment of the feasibility of such a concept is attempted. The green corridors 
concept is defined and a methodological framework for establishing a green corridor is 
displayed based on already published recommendations by relevant entities. The existing 
initiatives around the world are recorded and a case study is conducted for a specific initiative. 
Eventually, based on the research findings, a discussion is conducted assessing whether the 
green corridor concept is feasible. It is shown that a final conclusion is hard to be made 
especially due to the lack of already established and operational green corridors, as well as 
based on the hypothetical nature of the anticipated challenges to be faced in the process of 
creating a worldwide network of green corridors. It is also concluded that more and faster 
collaborative actions would be needed, in order to achieve the decarbonizing goals by 2050.

Chapter 1:  Introduction  
 
1.1 Sustainability in the maritime industry 
Since the United Nations announced in 2015 the 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), 
many countries expressed their intensions in adopting the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable 
Development through which it is desired within this 15-year plan to achieve the Goals.  
To achieve this, countries are required to take action worldwide. Also, the International 
Maritime Organization (IMO) who is part of the United Nations family -and is also the 
specialized agency that is responsible for maintaining safety and security in the shipping 
industry while also responsible for the prevention of marine and atmospheric pollution by 
ships- has actively engaged in complying with the SDGs (IMO, 2019a). 
Working towards sustainable transport requires facilitating the global economy and 
supporting world trade. Specialized committees at the IMO work on developing and launching 
policies, regulations, operations and partnerships based on the different objective of each 
Goal. An indicative example is Goal no. 7 which aims at achieving affordable and clean energy 
by 2030. IMO has entered into partnerships for accomplishing this goal by launching initiatives 
such as the Global Maritime Energy Efficiency Partnership and the Global MTTC (Maritime 
Technologies Cooperation Centre) Network (GMN) project where each project is focused on 
building capacity to address maritime energy efficiency (IMO, 2019b). 
Initially, during the 72nd session of the Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC) in 
London in 2018, IMO embraced the goal of achieving a minimum of 50% reduction in 
greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from shipping industry on worldwide level by 2050, 
compared to the 2008 recorded emissions. This vision was promoted as an urgency, enhancing 
the necessity of adopting strategies as soon as possible in order to progress successfully. This 
goal however was recently revised during the most recent MEPC 80 held in July 2023. The 
revised goal is set on achieving net zero GHG emissions by or close to 2050. To ensure this 
vision is feasible, the uptake of zero and near zero marine fuels -of around 5%- are aimed by 
2030 too. The motivation behind this strategy is IMO’s desire to maintain an active role in the 
global efforts in addressing climate change, while aligning with Goal 13 of the SDGs which 
aims at “taking urgent action to combat climate change and its impacts” (SDGS, 2023). 
 
1.2 Definition of the problem 
Maritime transport plays an essential role in the global economy as it is one of the most 
energy-efficient modes of transport. However, it is also accompanied by a large and increasing 
amount of GHG emissions. In particular, based on the 2018 recorded global shipping 
emissions, 1076 million tons of CO2 were emitted, which corresponds to approximately 3% of 
the global anthropogenic CO2 emissions that are resulted from human activities (European 
Commission, 2023c). 
More specifically, getting a bit more into numbers, the global fleet currently comprises of 
around 100.000 ocean-going cargo ships (more than 100 GT), with each of them designated

to operate approximately 280 whole days per year. According to Vidal, (2009), a single large 
ship can generate around 5000 tons of sulfur oxide (SOx) pollution annually while overall 
shipping industry is responsible for a notable portion of global pollution, emitting 9% of the 
global sulfur oxide emissions and 18-30% of the global nitrogen oxide (NOx) emissions. Lastly, 
it is notable that around 85% of the pollution attributed to shipping occurs in the northern 
hemisphere.  
Pollution is expected to increase in the upcoming years and these emissions are expected to 
rise further too. More specifically, an increase of around 90-130% by 2050 is estimated in the 
2008 recorded emissions in a wide range of possible long-term energy and economic scenarios 
(European Commission, 2023c). In the unfortunate case that these scenarios become reality, 
it would mean that the environmental impact of shipping industry is detrimental, undermining 
the objective of the Paris Agreement.  
The Paris Agreement that was adopted in 2015 at the Paris UN Climate Change Conference 
(COP21), represents the first international agreement with binding obligations focused on 
global climate change. Its primary objective is to establish a comprehensive global framework 
that will aim at preventing dangerous climate change by restricting the increase of global 
warming to levels significantly below 2°C above the pre-industrial level, while also striving 
towards a more ambitious effort of limiting the temperature increase to 1.5°C (European 
Commission, 2023b). 
During the United Nations Climate Change Conference (COP26) that was held in 2021, a total 
of 19 countries countersigned the Clydebank declaration. Some of them include Chile, 
Australia, the United Kingdom and the United States. The objective was to promote the 
development of six or more green corridors by the mid-2020s, with possibilities to expand 
these corridors by the end of the decade (ABS, 2022). 
To make effective efforts towards sustainability and mitigation of climate change, the various 
stakeholders associated to the maritime industry must engage in collective initiatives. 
Decarbonizing shipping is one of the initiatives that is believed to be crucial in fighting climate 
change. Some of the related stakeholders may be cities, regions, authorities, society and also 
the private and public sector. Their responsibility lies upon their abilities to foster regional and 
international cooperation, while simultaneously enhancing resilience and reducing 
vulnerability to the detrimental impacts of climate change (European Commission, 2023b). 
 
1.3 Research objective 
To achieve decarbonization in maritime, several challenges are presented in a wide range of 
pathways that currently exhibit varying degrees of preparedness in operational and 
technological advancement. Most of the decarbonization strategies focus on expediting the 
efficiency of operations and implementing utilization of low- and zero-carbon fuels (ABS, 
2022). 
Taking into consideration the aforementioned global goals towards sustainability and 
decarbonization, significant doubts raise in the maritime industry in what regards the 
feasibility of achieving decarbonization in shipping, as well as the methods required. Through

Chapter 2:  Definition of maritime GCs 
 
2.1 What is a Green Corridor  
As defined by the Mærsk Mc-Kinney Møller Center for Zero Carbon Shipping & McKinsey & 
Company, (2022b), in their feasibility phase blueprint, “Green corridors are shipping routes on 
which there are commercially operating ships using exclusively alternative fuels”  
New technologies and net-zero fuels will be required to be used in the green corridors. 
Therefore, a need to develop and test new technologies is necessary to eventually accelerate 
the reduction of greenhouse gas emissions (Government of Canada, 2022). By investing in and 
adopting this new concept between two or more ports, the green corridors will be established 
in the maritime industry. 
 
2.2 Why are green corridors important? 
As the need for decarbonization is urgent and the first milestone of the goal is set to be in, the 
green corridors may help industry stakeholders to trust the idea through an alternative design 
and approach compared to the traditional operations, and therefore achieve decarbonization 
as fast as possible. The concept also constitutes a way to “initiate an end-to-end 
decarbonization within the supply chain” (Mærsk Mc-Kinney Møller Center for Zero Carbon 
Shipping & McKinsey & Company, 2022b). To achieve this, a close and efficient collaboration 
between public and private parties will be encouraged. This way, the overall ecosystem within 
maritime can be benefited through better supply chain procedures and by maintaining a 
dialogue between the involved stakeholders.  
The nature of this concept is wide, and both developed and developing countries can 
participate in this global decarbonization strategy, by taking significant actions. Hence, green 
corridors may have a great contribution in eliminating climate change by “catalyzing specific 
actions and serving as a mechanism to stimulate actions” (Marine Environment Protection 
Committee, 2022). 
 
2.3 Transport green corridors  
Not long before the maritime green corridors were introduced, green transport corridors for 
freight were discussed for the first time at the Freight Transport Logistics Action Plan in 
Brussels in 2007. According to the European Commission, 2009, Green Transport Corridors 
are “a European concept denoting long-distance freight transport corridors where advanced 
technology and co-modality are used to achieve energy efficiency and reduce environmental 
impact”.  
The main focus of the green corridors concept is to reduce the environmental impact of freight 
transport. To prioritize sustainability, reduction of carbon emissions is aimed, as well as less 
pollution and lower energy consumption by initiating and adopting new measures and 
technologies.  
==PAGE:18==

Apart from the environmental ones, there are a few other reasons why transport green 
corridors are needed. One of the most profound ones is the increasing traffic congestion in 
urban areas which calls for shifting cargoes from the European roads. Through green corridors, 
alternative choices for the network users can be offered, mitigating this way the congestion 
problems. Subsequently, traffic flow can be improved, and hence better road safety is also 
promoted. Another reason for enhancing the need for green corridors is the necessity for 
transporting large volumes of freight over long distances (Psaraftis & Panagakos, 2012). 
Improved connectivity and international trade can be enhanced this way too, which 
consequently can increase the competitiveness of more environmentally friendly modes -such 
as the rail transport-, lead to more job opportunities and contribute to an overall economic 
development in the countries or regions that the green corridors will be part to.  
To make a green corridor work, multimodal connectivity is a key characteristic. Multimodal 
transport includes a variety of modes of transport which are integrated within a corridor and 
offer an efficient freight transport network. These modes may include a combination of road, 
railways, air and/or sea. Through modal shift, switching from one mode to a less pollutant one 
is encouraged, creating this way green routes. 
For an efficient and sustainable multimodal network, correspondingly well-organized and 
developed infrastructure is crucial in order to minimize any potential risks. Some of the actions 
that are suggested are improving the efficiency of road transport, intermodal terminals 
located based on a strategic plan as well as use transshipment hubs and logistics centers to 
enable the supply and move of goods among the different modes of transport. Therefore, 
logistics operations need to be optimized to facilitate the coordination between them and to 
make their collaboration as smooth as possible while overcoming any challenges. Up-to-date 
information-sharing systems can enable this, together with the use of Electronic Data 
Interchange (EDI) – an electronic format of communication between businesses to share 
documents such as purchase orders and invoices (IBM, 2023).  
An example of transport green corridors is the Brenner Green Corridor, which is the first zero-
emission corridor in Europe (Open ENLOCC, 2021). Connecting the northern part of Europe 
with the southern part, it starts from Malmø, Sweden, then to Trelleborg, Sweden and follows 
a route through Germany passing through Rostock, Sassnitz, Berlin and Munich, moving to 
Salzburg, Austria to end in Italy and particularly passing through Verona, Bologna, Naples, 
Messina and Palerma. It also has two branches, one starting from Salzburg and moving to 
Villach, Austria to end in Trieste, Italy. The other branch starts from Bologna and moves on to 
Ancora, Bari and Brindisi and then to Greece, in Igoumenitsa, Patras and Athens (SuperGreen, 
2013). 
Another example is the Finis Terrae Green Corridor starting from Madrid, Spain and ending to 
Paris, France with a branch from Madrid to Lisboa, Portugal. The Cloverleaf is one more green 
corridor starting from Cork, Ireland and ending to Stranraer, Scottland. 
 
2.4 How Maritime Green Corridors will operate? 
To make the green corridor concept feasible, ships that will operate on zero GHG emissions 
are needed. From a technical perspective, this requires several measures that will have a 
positive environmental impact and that will eventually lead to decarbonation of shipping.  
==PAGE:19==

6 
 
A first factor in making a green corridor feasible to be operational is new vessel design or 
modification of already existing vessels. As new alternative fuels will be introduced, new 
engines and propulsion systems will be required to comply with the new standards. The goal 
is to operate on vessels that minimize fuel consumption and use energy efficient technologies. 
In what regards the alternative fuels, many different options are discussed. However, the use 
of them is dependent not only on the extent of their CO2 emissions but also on other factors 
such as the geographical frame they can be produced in and thereafter distributed, their 
safety properties and level of supply. 
To enhance the decarbonization goal, digitalization will also be needed so that data can be 
collected and treated in such a way that will allow for optimizing routes, fuels, supply and the 
general performance in the green corridor. Digital solutions can nowadays more easily be 
integrated into the maritime industry, as data analytics has shown a rapid development in the 
last decade and could be used in promoting vessel efficiency. Maritime management could 
benefit also by sharing this data and information between the different stakeholders, allowing 
them to track the progress of their actions and modify their strategies according to the 
findings. Data might include tracking emissions from the vessels by using new technologies. 
Remote sensing, global ship tracking intelligence or data logging sensors are possible ways of 
collecting data.  
To make a green corridor possible to operate, development of port infrastructure is also 
required. Due to the new fuels and technologies, proper sustainable facilities call for ports 
that support the new changes such as suitable bunkering stations, storage facilities, safety 
compliance and adequate capacity. Charging facilities will also be needed in case of a green 
corridor operating with electric ships.  
As green corridors are a complex venture, constant development is essential. Thus, even after 
the completion of creating a green corridor that is operational, supporting actions for 
improvement should be continued. Collaboration between the stakeholders must be 
maintained to further improve the performance of the green corridor for as long as it is 
needed.   
 
2.5 Expected benefits of green corridors regarding the supply chain  
After the establishment of green corridors, several benefits are expected to be offered that 
will have an impact in the overall maritime supply chain. This is based on the fact that, to 
initiate a new green corridor many first movers need to be united that will share the 
responsibilities and the risks that such a large project will have. Green corridors are by nature 
collaborative projects that offer the possibility for tests and commercial trials of technologies, 
fuels and market solutions. To eliminate some of the risks and uncertainties, new regulations 
and offtake agreements will be needed, to make the planning and the implementation of the 
corridors more effective. Additionally, forming partnerships between both the private and the 
public sector, such as consortiums, are going to be more than necessary to help in sharing 
costs and for a better collaboration across the overall value chain (Mærsk Mc-Kinney Møller 
Center for Zero Carbon Shipping & McKinsey & Company, 2023).

2.6 Potential stakeholders and collaboration 
In any green corridor initiative, several parties might be involved. For a project to flourish, 
collaboration between them is necessary in order to distribute the responsibilities, the 
challenges, the costs and to cover all the key issues that will be faced throughout the project’s 
study and execution duration. 
To make a collaboration successful, the different actors must ensure that there is mutual 
involvement, sharing of experience and knowledge, coordination and transparency during 
their engagement to the project. For this purpose, a high level of understanding is essential, 
as well as making upfront agreements about crucial aspects of the project such as cost and

importantly how to deal with them without facing conflicts or interruptions (Slotvik et al.,

By identifying the barriers in an early stage, trust between the stakeholders - which is a key

a. Port Authorities: As ports constitute a key node for maritime shipping and

decarbonization of shipping.  
c. Shipping companies: With new technologies to be implemented along with the

2.7 Need for alternative fuels 
According to the European Parliament and of the Council, (2014), alternative fuels are “the 
fuels or power sources which serve, at least partly, as a substitute for fossil oil sources in the 
energy supply to transport and which have the potential to contribute to its decarbonization 
and enhance the environmental performance of the transport sector”.  
Or else, as defined in the Feasibility blueprint of Mærsk Mc-Kinney Møller Center for Zero 
Carbon Shipping & McKinsey & Company, (2022), an alternative fuel is “derived from sources 
other than petroleum - some derived from renewable sources - and they usually have a lower 
environmental impact than fossil-based hydrocarbons.”  
It is becoming more and more necessary to start switching to alternative fuels mainly for 
environmental reasons. As pollution and climate change are rapidly growing, measures and 
actions need to be taken immediately in order to help mitigate the negative effects of 
pollution and reduce GHG emissions. Conventional fossil fuels are a major pollutant and thus, 
new sustainable fuels are needed to replace them, by emitting the lowest possible or zero CO2 
emissions.  
There are four key milestones in the life of an alternative fuel according to the blueprint of 
Mærsk Mc-Kinney Møller Center for Zero Carbon Shipping & McKinsey & Company, (2022). 
These are the following with a chronological order: 
a. Feedstock: Feedstock refers to the extraction, production or collection of raw 
materials that will be used for the production of these fuels. Depending on the fuel 
that is desired, there are various sources that can be used. Some examples are 
biomass, biowaste, natural gas or green electricity.  
b. Fuel production: After the collection of feedstocks, various chemical processes take 
place to produce the fuels. Depending on the form of raw materials, different 
procedures are followed. For example, biomass and biowaste go through a biofuel 
synthesis to give the final product which is the fuel - bio-oils. On the other hand, 
natural gas undergoes a more complex chemical processing which includes steam 
methane reforming and carbon storage, which lead to producing the final product - 
hydrogen. Hydrogen has the possibility of further processing in order to produce other 
more sustainable fuels. 
c. Fuel: After the processing in the phase of fuel production, the final result is the 
formation of the alternative fuels. Some of the possible fuels are e-hydrogen, e-
ammonia and e-methanol which can be created from green electricity, other fuels are 
blue ammonia and blue hydrogen which can be produced from natural gas, and lastly 
bio-oils and bio-methanol which can be produced from biomass and biowaste.  
d. Emissions: The purpose of creating these alternative fuels is the reduction of 
emissions. Indeed, by using these fuels, it is expected that less emissions will be 
emitted compared to the emissions of Low Sulphur Fuel Oils (LSFO) - marine oils with 
above 0.10% of sulfur content but meeting the 0.50% sulfur limit (Marine Insight, 
2020). More specifically, 1% less emissions are expected to be released by using e-
hydrogen, e-ammonia or e-methanol, 17% and 19% less emissions are expected by 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
Definition of maritime GCs 
 
10 
 
using blue hydrogen and blue ammonia respectively, and 12% less emissions are 
expected to be released by using bio-oils or bio-methane. 
 
2.7.1 Types of alternative fuels  
Several novel alternative fuels have been introduced in recent years, which can play a key role 
in decarbonization of the shipping industry. These alternative options present viable 
alternatives to fossil fuels, aiming at reducing emissions throughout the entirety of their usage 
lifecycle.

The transition to a more sustainable and carbon-free future could be actively benefited by 
embracing these or some of the above-mentioned alternative fuels.  
 
Hydrogen  
Hydrogen is a fuel that could be a sustainable energy resource for shipping purposes. It can 
be produced directly from fossil fuels or biomass. When it is combined with oxygen, it 
produces electricity and heat. Also, hydrogen combustion only produces water vapor as a by-
product (NREL, 2023) and emits no CO2 when it is used to generate power (Clark et al., 2021). 
However, a large number of emissions are emitted during the production of hydrogen (Q. 
Wang et al., 2023).  
Hydrogen has an advantage in what regards storage, as when intended for storage on ships, 
it can be stored as a compressed gas under exceptionally high pressure or as a cryogenic liquid 
at extremely low temperatures (Lloyd’s Register Maritime Decarbonisation Hub, 2021). 
However, attention must be paid while storing, bunkering or using this fuel. Due to its 
flammable and diffusible nature, safety measures must be taken to avoid any potential burns 
or explosions. From a technical aspect too, it is likely that hydrogen molecules which are very 
small, might leak through pipes and cause health issues to crew members or pose risk for 
burns (Q. Wang et al., 2023). Thus, adequate ventilation and advanced systems that can detect 
hydrogen gas are imperative. Fire detection technology systems are also crucial as there is a 
high risk of fire due to the low thermal radiation emitted in case of hydrogen fire and most 
importantly because of the inconspicuous nature of the flames under daylight conditions 
(Lloyd’s Register Maritime Decarbonisation Hub, 2021). 
 
==PAGE:24==

Green hydrogen 
Green hydrogen exhibits a significant advantage of not emitting any carbon dioxide (CO2) 
when it is utilized for power generation. The production of green hydrogen, which is done 
through electrolysis of water powered by renewable electricity, relies on commercially mature 
technologies that have been successfully implemented at scale. Compared to the production 
of ammonia and synfuels, the easier one-step production of hydrogen is evidently less energy 
intensive. Additionally, hydrogen possesses a prevalent cost advantage between them. In 
terms of energy storage, less energy is required, where more specifically, every megawatt- 
hour (MWh) of hydrogen demands approximately 60-70% less input energy compared to e-
methanol and 16% less than ammonia (Clark et al., 2021). 
Several challenges are on the other hand faced in regards of storage and transportation of 
green hydrogen. For the storage of liquid hydrogen, cryogenic conditions are necessary, which 
consequently lead to substantial cost escalations for both onboard and onshore storage 
facilities. An increased flammability risk also arises with hydrogen, necessitating this way 
costly measures ensuring the safety of storage. From an energy perspective, these 
requirements result in a reduced overall process efficiency and to add on that, the lack of 
existing infrastructure for distribution and bunkering complicates even more the utilization of 
hydrogen as a widely preferable fuel source (Clark et al., 2021). 
 
Ammonia 
The production of ammonia involves the combination of hydrogen with atmospheric nitrogen. 
Despite the fact that ammonia is already extensively produced and utilized for various 
applications - such as industrial use - the current prevailing practice involves the use of 
hydrogen that is developed from fossil fuels, leading to the production of what is called the 
“grey ammonia”. 
Ammonia can be stored in two forms: as a gas when it is at room temperature and as a liquid 
when it is at low temperatures. One of the disadvantages of ammonia is its high accident risk 
due to its natural toxic corrosive gas form (Q. Wang et al., 2023). This makes transport of 
ammonia extra dangerous and exposure to it should be extremely careful.  
When it is in gaseous form, it is colorless and is characterized by a notable unpleasant smell. 
If substantial amounts of ammonia are discharged, a considerable ammonia gas cloud can be 
formed which creates a significant inhalation hazard on either short or even greatly longer 
distances from the release site. Also, prolonged exposure to high concentrations of ammonia 
in gas form can cause severe health issues including lung failure and in more severe cases can 
lead to fatality (Lloyd’s Register Maritime Decarbonisation Hub, 2021). 
Additionally, liquid form of ammonia can cause serious health problems too. Exposure to 
highly concentrated vapors can lead to rapid tissue damage. Therefore, effective gear is 
essential to crew members. To ensure safety, the implementation of a robust system is 
necessary so that liquid or gas leakages can be detected, and people can be prevented from 
being found in contaminated rooms (Lloyd’s Register Maritime Decarbonisation Hub, 2021).

Green ammonia 
Green ammonia shows a prospective long-term fuel solution aligning with the goal of 
achieving zero carbon emissions in shipping. Ammonia has the capability to be stored as liquid 
under atmospheric pressure and at relatively ordinary temperatures. It also has a significantly 
reduced risk of fire compared to hydrogen. Advantages over hydrogen are also demonstrated 
in terms of energy and cost, as ammonia requires less energy and is less demanding on storage 
and transportation requirements. Benefits are also enhanced due to the already established 
distribution network and the global logistics that support ammonia supply chains. Moreover, 
despite the corrosive and toxic nature of ammonia, already established safety regulations and 
standards enable its effective handling and bulk transportation (Clark et al., 2021). For all 
these reasons, ammonia possesses a key role in the decarbonization pathway.  
 
Biofuels  
Biofuels are an alternative source of energy that can be made from waste, oils or sugar and 
can contribute to zero-emission shipping (Clark et al., 2021). Some of these biofuels can be 
used along with conventional fuels together as a mixture for operating vessels (European 
Commission, 2023a). Such a biofuel is Hydrotreated Vegetable Oil (HVO) which is a renewable 
diesel fuel and can be produced by processing waste of cooking oil, vegetable oil, rapeseed oil 
or tallow (ETIP Bioenergy, 2020). Other biofuels are biodiesel, bioethanol, and biomass-to-
liquids (BTL) (Clark et al., 2021).  
Such a type of fuel does not require new refueling infrastructure, as it is compatible with the 
existing facilities in what regards bunkering infrastructure, engines and onboard systems. 
However, due to applications of these fuels in other sectors too - for example in industry or 
road transport - there is high competition on the demand and questionable supply possibilities 
(Clark et al., 2021). 
 
Liquefied natural gas (LNG) 
Liquified natural gas (LNG) is primarily composed of methane and is categorized as a gaseous 
blend of hydrocarbons. It has witnessed in the last few years an increased adoption as a 
marine alternative fuel because of its ability to response responsibly to the evolving 
regulations related to the emissions and to the aim of eliminating the carbon intensity in the 
vessel operations. Currently, over 10% of the worldwide fleet possesses the capacity to utilize 
LNG either it is already in operation or on the order stage. On a positive note, too, this fact is 
also accompanied by the growing availability of LNG bunkering services in various regions 
globally.  
However, there are also some negative aspects in the utilization of this alternative fuel in 
shipping. LNG, which is classified as a fossil fuel, only provides a restricted potential for 
eliminating GHG emissions. In a comparison scale between LNG and other fossil fuels, LNG 
can offer a maximum of 23% lifecycle reduction. Thus, widely adopting LNG as a shipping 
alternative fuel is a risky option that may lead to failure in the objectives outlined by the Paris 
Agreement towards decarbonization (Oceans North Conservation Society, 2023).

Green methanol 
Green methanol is the result of the chemical reaction between green hydrogen and CO2. This 
fuel can be considered as a low carbon fuel since it utilizes renewable raw materials to be 
produced. Even though carbon may be released during its usage/combustion, it can be 
considered as part of the carbon life cycle, as an amount of carbon is also used during the 
production of green methanol. Thus, a balance on the carbon emissions can be maintained.  
According to Strandesen, 2023, this fuel shows so far the most feasible profile and this is due 
to several factors. Firstly, methanol engine technology has already progressed, and some 
engines are already available in the market; a fact that renders this fuel solution as one of the 
most advanced and mature among the other alternative fuels (Getting to Zero Coalition, 
2021). Apart from the engine advancements, green methanol has a low level of toxicity 
towards both the environment and humans. Additionally, storage and infrastructure 
requirements are not demanding. Green methanol can be stored and utilized in the already 
existing infrastructure for conventional methanol. It is therefore easy to transport and handle 
onboard.  
However, even though green methanol seems to be one of the less costly methods to convert 
biomass to fuel (Strandesen, 2023), the production process is still an ongoing effort and more 
research is needed in order to improve its efficiency and costs. 
 
Electricity  
One way to produce electricity apart from fossil carbon is through renewable sources. Such 
resources include wind and solar power, biofuels and hydropower plants (European 
Commission, 2023a). It is anticipated that around 60% of the global electricity demand by 
2050 will be catered by renewable resources. As the advancements on the technologies in 
renewable energy generation are continued, the respective share of renewable electricity 
within the global energy mix also increases. Ships that are battery-powered utilizing electricity 
show the capability to entirely eradicate emissions that are resulted from the operational 
phase (Y. Wang & Wright, 2021). However, the composition of electricity may vary across 
countries depending on the regional demands as well as the availability of natural resources.  
 
Wind  
Wind technologies have emerged as a viable approach to mitigate emissions and to enhance 
energy efficiency in the shipping industry, mainly under small-scale utilization. A lower level 
of fuel consumption is anticipated to result from such wind propulsion technologies, scoring 
a 10-30% reduction with respect to CO2 emissions.  
In what regards the associated costs for the development and installation of such wind 
technologies, a great variation is shown depending on the type of it. It is also important to 
note that for more effective utilization and to fulfill the energy demands of the modern 
shipping vessels, complementary zero carbon fuel solutions are necessary (Clark et al., 2021).

2.7.3 Need for well-to-wake emissions analysis 
The technological advancements in recent years make the transition to alternative fuels easier 
than a few decades ago. The new fuels provide a more competitive solution which is also 
viable and is expected that in the long run, alternative fuels will be more affordable and will 
contribute to the reduction of costs.  
On the other hand, based on the regulations established by the International Maritime 
Organization (IMO), current analysis of the effect that fuels have on the environment take into 
account only tank-to-wake (TTW) emissions. These are the direct downstream emissions from 
shipping that are the result of using the fuels once they are in the tank. This way the well-to-
tank (WTT) upstream emissions that are released while extracting producing, processing and 
transporting the fuels are not taken into consideration.   
This approach can be considered as an admissible one for conventional fuels since most of the 
emissions are released during the process of burning them. However, this approach does not 
seem to be appropriate for the alternative fuels too. This is linked to the fact that TTW 
emissions for alternative fuels may be much lower than the WTT ones, as zero or minimum 
emissions are expected in contrast to the emissions that occur while producing or 
delivering/moving them between the different parts of the world. Hence, a holistic analysis of 
the overall well-to-wake (WTW) emissions must be introduced. The methodology would 
demand a life cycle assessment (LCA) which is also suggested by IMO and the related 
guidelines were considered for finalization and adoption at the latest MEPC 80 (Marine 
Environment Protection Committee) of the IMO held in July 2023.  
For an LCA analysis, the availability of qualitative and quantitative data is crucial in order to 
be able to make an informed assessment about the environmental sustainability of fuels. Such 
an analysis enables a comprehensive comparison of various fuel pathways throughout the 
energy value chain (Moirangthem, 2016).

2.8 Types of maritime green corridors 
As explained in the feasibility blueprint of Mærsk Mc-Kinney Møller Center for Zero Carbon 
Shipping & McKinsey & Company, (2022b), the green corridors can be classified into three 
categories:

Single-point corridors: These are the corridors within which the activities are focused in a 
certain location with vessels operating on zero-emissions. These must include a port hub and 
bunkering facilities too.  
Point-to-point corridors: This type of corridor includes only two ports, and all activities are 
aggregated into a single-route corridor. 
Network corridors: This type of corridor includes more than two ports. These constitute a 
more complex network that may include more bunkering stations and longer routes between 
different ports around the globe.   
To choose what type of corridor will be initiated, the various stakeholders assess several 
factors that will affect the feasibility of such a green corridor. Natural resources, demand for 
fuels, capacity of facilities, TCO (Total cost of Ownership) are some of the parameters that 
play a key role.  
Most of the already initiated or proposed green corridors belong into the network category

too (Getting to Zero Coalition, 2021).

Chapter 4: Steps for establishing a GC 
 
4.1 Methodology 
Currently, there is only a limited number of green corridors initiatives that have started 
conducting research, resulting in the absence of already established green corridors. 
However, a few first feasibility and prefeasibility studies conducted by stakeholders and/or 
organizations around the world, provide a preliminary framework for approaching such an 
initiative. Furthermore, they propose a methodological pathway that can theoretically lead to 
the establishment of new corridors.  
Some of the organizations that have published preliminary studies or suggested blueprints are 
the Mærsk Mc-Kinney Møller Center for Zero Carbon Shipping, Getting to Zero Coalition, the 
Zero-Emission Shipping Mission and the Lloyd’s Register Maritime Decarbonization Hub. 
The sections below present a consolidated methodology approach that integrates the 
collected research outcomes from the published reports of the aforementioned organizations. 
It is notable that these projects exhibit a compelling alignment in the objectives of their 
studies, affirming in this way the uniformity of their goals in what regards to establishing this 
new idea of green corridors. Additionally, these studies underline the necessity of considering 
specific parameters that need to be examined and later implemented in order to foster 
sustainability in shipping. 
 
4.2 Key Performance Indicators 
The following criteria are presented as a suggested fundamental framework for the 
establishment of a green corridor based on the recommended approaches of the already 
published reports. A great part of the steps presented below are derived from the Green 
Corridor Feasibility Phase Blueprint that is conducted by the Mærsk Mc-Kinney Møller Center 
for Zero Carbon Shipping together with McKinsey & Company and which was published in 
August 2022.  
Stakeholders interested into getting involved in or initiating a new green corridor project can 
use the following guidelines to examine whether the idea is feasible. This involves 
investigating any step in the supply chain that is closely related to establishing and making 
such a corridor operational and sustainable. This means a deep assessment of the features of 
each selected green corridor must be examined, evaluating the potential scenario that will 
allow the technical, regulatory and economic capabilities to arise while at the same time all 
potential risks and corruptions in the value chain will be eliminated.   
The fundamental steps into establishing a green corridor are presented through the criteria 
listed in the below sections of this chapter.

4.2.1 Alternative fuels 
4.2.1.1 
Fuel production - Feedstock 
One of the key parameters in establishing a green corridor is the use of alternative fuels. The 
first step of the methodology is to conduct an assessment and estimate of the fuel/fuels 
demand that is expected to arise in the corridor once it is established. In this assessment, 
demand in the regions that are directly associated with the specific corridor have to be 
considered. All sectors that might use the fuels must be considered too with a high focus 
placed on the shipping industry.  
Alternative fuel production may have already previously been initiated around the corridor 
for prior projects or uses. Either existing or planned, an overview of the fuel production must 
then be created for each type of fuel that is closely accessible or easily transported to the 
corridor. The production may be done outside of the corridor facilities and then imported to 
the corridor, or directly produced at a location near the corridor, depending on the feedstock 
and the infrastructure available. The records of this phase must include information about the 
capacity of facilities, production levels and timelines, expected available fuel volumes for 
consumption, locations where production will be executed and also import plans in case of 
moving alternative fuels into the corridor. It is notable also that the estimate of supply and 
demand is subject to the fuel properties and their compatibility to the various vessel types 
(Mærsk Mc-Kinney Møller Center for Zero Carbon Shipping & McKinsey & Company, 2022b).  
It is therefore crucial to attempt an alignment between the supply and demand of alternative 
fuels, to avoid any disruption in the supply chain and to ensure the continuation of operations. 
Therefore, analysis should be conducted to identify the capacities of the alternative fuels and 
the expected range of volumes. Moreover, it is needed to record the availability of raw 
materials or the available renewable resources to which new green corridors might be 
exposed/connected and that could effectively be used to produce fuels following the zero 
emissions goal. Wind and solar energy nowadays form a great potential, hence specialized 
geospatial analysis could indicate the locations where energy could be produced and whether 
the corridor could benefit from it. All the aforementioned alternative fuels (Section 2.7.1) 
constitute potential sources of energy, however the selection of them is dependent on many 
factors, not only the availability of feedstock but also economic factors that affect all phases 
of an alternative fuel’s life - from feedstock to final end user. 
To effectively achieve the decarbonization goal in a corridor by transitioning from traditional 
fuels to an alternative one may necessitate employing more than one or a mix of them. Also, 
the varying productions techniques may vary over time depending on technological 
advancements and range of volumes required (Lloyd’s Register Maritime Decarbonisation 
Hub, 2021).

Cost identification and estimation 
In the next step, analysis should be conducted to identify the primary cost determinants in 
the value chain of alternative fuels, outlining the factors that serve as starting points for cost 
calculations. These costs encompass the costs associated with new fuel and feedstock 
production technologies, costs of electricity that are a big part of the production process and

expenses associated with storage and transport of fuels. Also, it is expected that costs will vary 
from one year to another leading to 2050, therefore, the progress of costs must be 
incorporated in the analysis to provide a more thorough understanding of the cost chain 
evolution.

4.2.1.3 
Finance and investments 
More expenses that are associated with the investments in infrastructure are expected to 
arise in the value chain, either it is for building new facilities or capex investments. Hence, a 
Capital expenditure (Capex) analysis must be done to acknowledge to the various 
stakeholders the projected costs throughout the fuel value chain. It might also be needed to 
examine the possibility of offtakes, to ensure that the produced volumes of alternative fuels 
will be certainly purchased by the end users, either they are located around the corridor or 
shares of fuels are destined to be moved to other customers. This is a considerable proposal 
as in some cases, the natural resources may provide a surplus of the fuel produced that might 
surpass the demand in the corridor.  
Additionally, other investment strategies may be considered in the financial analysis. 
Examples may include private fundings from stakeholders that are interested in investing in 
green corridor projects and in which they foresee future profits. On the other hand, the public 
sector may be interested too in investing in local or national initiatives that will provide 
improved environmental and life quality.

4.2.1.4 
Feasibility of alternative fuels 
Upon the completion of the aforementioned stages of the fuel production analysis, the final 
step in this sequence of criteria is to acknowledge the various stakeholders engaged in every 
aspect of the fuel value chain. These include manufacturers, organizations, authorities, energy 
suppliers, logistics teams, utilities providers and other relevant companies capable and willing 
of getting involved into potential partnerships. Through this step, it is aimed to discern the 
ability if each player to invest in the green corridor project and to what extend they are able 
to contribute (Mærsk Mc-Kinney Møller Center for Zero Carbon Shipping & McKinsey & 
Company, 2022b).  
The outcome of these fuel-based criteria is an overall assessment of the feasibility of 
producing alternative fuels that will be used in the green corridor. What will be derived from 
this assessment is the identification of available sources to produce alternative fuels or the 
possibilities to import them. The supply and demand in the corridor will be projected and then 
technical details will be addressed such as production locations, volumes and capacity. 
Additionally, an economic analysis will reveal the investment possibilities and the expected 
costs of the fuel production in the present and expected cost alterations over time. The final 
step is the structure of policies and regulations regarding all phases in the production chain, 
as well as guidelines for safety, health and use.  
  
4.2.2 Vessels 
4.2.2.1 
Vessel size requirements  
Following the indication of the capabilities for alternative fuel production, it becomes 
necessary to also address the vessel requirements. Diverse demand for alternative fuels 
means expected variations in vessel utilization too. A comprehensive study should be 
conducted to quantify the number of vessels that will be deployed in the green corridor to 
enable sufficient transport of goods, the type of vessels based on the specific fuel they will be 
operate on, the requisite capacity requirements and the corresponding vessel sizes that are 
needed to meet these needs and new specifications. 
 
 
4.2.2.2 
Newbuilds and retrofitted vessels 
Due to the distinctive properties of alternative fuels in contrast to the traditional fossil fuels, 
new requirements for vessels are emerging. Modifications and adjustments will be needed in 
order to adapt the existing available fleet, including changes to the propulsion systems, 
technologies, engines and onboard storage. Therefore, it becomes crucial to estimate the 
associated costs of these modifications while considering potential economic and technical 
implications that might arise in the first phases of the transition or even throughout the 
utilization of the vessels in the long-term. Simultaneously, the prospect of acquiring newbuild 
vessels also arises. The utilization of both the newbuilds and the retrofitted vessels indicates 
the need to develop a comprehensive vessel renewal schedule over time. Such a plan should 
include all relevant possible implications and evaluate the capacity and preparedness of all 
stakeholders involved in the shipbuilding value chain. 
==PAGE:50==
 
 
Steps for establishing a GC  
 
37 
 
 
4.2.2.3 
Capex requirements 
As some of the existing vessels may undergo retrofitting, it becomes crucial to estimate the 
investments that are associated to these retrofitting efforts. Hence, the Capital expenditure 
(Capex) must be quantified including the costs attributed to the implementation of new 
propulsion technologies and storage systems. Additionally, conducting a comparative analysis 
of the capex between the traditional amenities and those of the newly introduced elements 
is necessary. This assessment will allow the involved parties such as ship owners and operators 
to evaluate the financial and funding options they will encounter. Consequently, this will allow 
them to appraise their abilities and determine the extent to which they can invest in this 
emerging value chain.  
 
4.2.2.4 
Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) analysis 
One of the essential components in this vessel criterion is undertaking an economic 
assessment. Conducting a Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) analysis will facilitate the evaluation 
of the decarbonization options by incorporating key inputs and assumptions in the model. 
TCO is a kind of analysis that targets to an assessment of the projected costs related to the 
acquisition, utilization and retiring of a product or service. According to Mærsk Mc-Kinney 
Møller Center for Zero Carbon Shipping & McKinsey & Company (2022b), in the analysis, 
various cost-related parameters must be incorporated such as: 
• 
Attributes of the fuel including its characteristics  
• 
Vessel specifications and characteristics 
• 
Costs associated to fuel production 
• 
Fuel price 
• 
Expenses associated with logistical aspects of storage and bunkering 
• 
Capital expenditure (Capex) prerequisites for vessels 
• 
Supplementary operational costs  
• 
Assumptions regarding efficiency  
 
4.2.2.5 
Feasibility of vessel decarbonization pathway 
Based on the findings of the TCO analysis, it is then needed to outline the financial gaps 
between each decarbonization option and fossil fuel. This way, the main cost drivers can be 
identified showcasing the biggest gaps. Through this step, policies and technological 
developments that may reduce these gaps can be discussed between the stakeholders. 
In summary, the outcome of all the aforementioned steps within the vessel decarbonization 
pathway leads to the assessment of converting vessels to operate on alternative fuels. 
Through a comprehensive technical, economic and regulatory review, a well-structured plan 
can be formulated, including key considerations such as the number of vessels, requirements 
for modifications, TCO analysis and Capex requirements. These parameters collectively 
contribute to determining the viability and practicality of the vessel conversion as a 
fundamental part of decarbonization of shipping by forming a green corridor.

4.2.3 Port, Storage and Bunkering Infrastructure  
4.2.3.1 
Expected demand and capacity for alternative fuels 
After the selection of alternative fuels that will be utilized in the green corridor and the 
identification of the requisite vessel types, an examination and mapping of the storage and 
bunkering requirements becomes necessary too.  
In this phase of assessment, the initial step involves recording all the parameters that 
influence the demand for storage and bunkering, which are related to the fuel properties, 
vessel type and the travel characteristics. More specifically, these parameters include various 
aspects such as the physical properties of fuels including their density or state, the designated 
bunkering locations, the anticipated demand for bunkering for each fuel type, as well as the 
projected storage needs based on the expected fuel volumes and the physical state of each 
fuel.  
 
4.2.3.2 
Existing infrastructure capacity 
Part of the existing infrastructure that is currently under utilization might still be viable for 
accommodating the needs of the new alternative fuels. It is significant thus to compile a 
comprehensive record of the currently available facilities and their condition, specifying the 
capacity possibilities of each port or region included in the corridor. This will facilitate the 
identification of locations where increased capacity might be required. Additionally, an 
investigation of potential new infrastructure is necessary to ascertain whether establishing 
new bunkering sites would better serve the fuel demand in the corridor.   
 
4.2.3.3 
Investments and retrofitting/building new storage  
Just as vessels may require retrofitting, the bunkering infrastructure may also demand 
modifications to align with the requirements of alternative fuels. Since some fuels may be 
imported for utilization in the corridor, the existing facilities need to have the technical 
capacity to accommodate these fuels. This will require building potential new 
storage/bunkering sites or alternating the existing ones. Therefore, this step includes the 
overall assessment of the total infrastructure needed, the technical feasibility of it and the 
potential modifications, as well as the associated cost estimates and the financial implications 
these may entail. Once again Capex analysis is necessary in this regard. Furthermore, an 
analysis of Opex can reveal the recurring operating costs such as inventory, equipment and 
funds for development that are inherent to regular business operations. Moreover, the 
possibility of sharing infrastructure can be explored as a means to alleviate financial burdens 
within the logistics framework.

4.2.3.4 
New regulations and standards 
Standards and safety conditions must also be examined given the fact that the utilization of 
alternative fuels will necessitate different handling procedures. Detailed and adequate 
guidelines must be developed, to ensure that the bunkering and storage systems comply with 
the new conditions. Also, the suitability between infrastructure and alternative fuels must be 
verified, making sure that all processes in the life cycle of the new fuels follow the zero-
emission requirements (Mærsk Mc-Kinney Møller Center for Zero Carbon Shipping & 
McKinsey & Company, 2022b). 
 
4.2.3.5 
Feasibility of infrastructure  
The ultimate objective of this methodological step is to obtain a detailed overview of the 
storage and bunkering infrastructure. By considering the existing infrastructure as well as the 
analysis conducted for creating new facilities, an assessment of the potential gaps between

assessment involves a thorough examination of the technical, economic and regulatory

4.2.4 Mobilization of Demand and routes   
4.2.4.1 
Influence on cargo demand and Sensitivity to changes 
After initiating all the aforementioned variations in fuels, infrastructure, utilization and 
regulations, it is crucial to conduct an evaluation of the cargo’s responsiveness to these 
modifications that are directly associated with the costs of shipping throughout the whole 
lifespan of the green corridor. Through historical analysis and research, a clear picture of the 
cargo demand can be withdrawn that will give a better understanding of how the changes in 
prices and shipping costs may affect future demand. More detailed analysis entailing 
seasonality or trends can show discern patterns and fluctuations that may have an impact on 
future shipping costs.  
Since a green corridor is a project that is intended to last infinitely, it is also important to 
examine and evaluate cargo’s ability to sustain top level decarbonized shipping services over 
time. One way to do so includes recognizing the potential market dynamics and economic 
feasibility of incorporating decarbonization efforts into the cargo’s transportation.  
 
4.2.4.2 
Customer willingness to pay & mechanisms 
One of the key indicators in the feasibility assessment of a new green corridor is to determine 
the factors that motivate customers, end consumers and cargo owners to pay for these 
environmentally friendly solutions given the fact that the costs for establishing a green 
corridor will be significantly increased.   
The first step here is to generate a comprehensive outline specifying the commitment each 
stakeholder has made towards decarbonization, and the formation of commercial alliances 
with the maritime sector. The willingness of the end consumers must be investigated by 
implementing surveys which will aim at presenting their inclination to financially support the 
sustainable shipping services that will lead to decarbonization. An input to estimate 
willingness to pay can also be the consideration of offtake agreements and the length of such 
contracts. Finally, a stakeholder mapping can enable categorizing them based on the range of 
their willingness to pay and the corresponding volume of cargo they represent in the corridor.  
To support stakeholders to engage in such a large project despite the high costs and to 
enhance their willingness to pay, some mechanisms can be assessed too. An example of these 
mechanisms is the offering of long-term offtake agreements. It must be assessed here 
whether this solution will lead to cost savings that will de-risk alternative fuels costs. Finally, 
the possibilities for consolidating demand from various end consumers and cargo owners 
must be explored. To do so, potential alliances and collaborations between them must be 
identified, as well as the total demand that is generated by these collaborations must be 
assessed through estimations.

Potential alternative routes and modes

4.2.5 Economic assessment and partnerships 
4.2.5.1 
Economic assessment and synergies  
To evaluate the economic feasibility of establishing a green corridor, economic evaluations 
must be done for all steps of the value chain. It is crucial to also examine whether the various 
stakeholders are willing to share the decarbonization costs through establishing formal 
partnerships and joint ventures.  
To better address the various sustainability requirements, it is expected that the different 
green corridors will share common needs despite the economic circumstances of each one 
which will vary from one corridor to the other. Such needs may include financial measures 
that need to be taken to support fuel production or infrastructure investments. Other actions 
may include regional or national incentives that could for example enable ships to operate 
commercially in a sustainable way. This could be very beneficial during a transitional phase 
where the ships in a corridor may use still the traditional fuels, others might use low or zero 
emission fuels or some others may use alternative energy sources (Marine Environment 
Protection Committee, 2022). 
 
4.2.5.2 
Technical challenges for the implementation  
Technical challenges are also expected to arise throughout the whole process of creating a 
green corridor. It is important to conduct a comprehensive assessment of the technical 
challenges that may interrupt the realization of the project in order to identify the key gaps 
between the current and the target state across all the steps in the value chain and get an 
overview on how these are expected to evolve over time.  
Through assessing technological trends or market reports, the expected challenges need to 
be outlined, so that a clear picture can be drawn regarding the overall project timeline. For 
example, this could regard the timing of fuel availability and how this may affect the following 
phases and the overall evolution of the project. When the technical challenges are identified, 
a categorization of them needs to be done based on their severity in order to discriminate

approximation of the chronological placement of solutions can be estimated, allowing this

This particular step of the feasibility assessment emphasizes the importance of prioritizing 
actions that will expedite the technical facilitation of green corridors, making sure the 
engagement of the appropriate relevant stakeholders is obtained.  
 
4.2.5.3 
Regulatory and policy constraints 
It is also equally important to assess whether the regulatory framework is adjusted to the new 
changes that will take place moving towards decarbonization. An assessment must be done 
to examine whether policies and regulations are following the project needs while also 
promoting closing the cost gaps. Additionally, it must be validated that there is constant 
alignment with the UN directions and commitments. 
 
4.2.5.4 
Potential risks  
Risks that may affect the implementation and evolution of a green corridor project lurk in all 
aspects of it, such as the technical risks, organizational, economic, executional and others. It 
is required in this step to evaluate the probability level and extent of impact each one can 
have. To get an overview of this, interviews with stakeholders is a suitable way, Also, another 
way can be studying other projects comparable to a green corridor that can be used as 
examples from history and can highlight easier the challenges faced before.  
By recognizing the possible risks, actions can also be considered that will aim at lessening the 
occurrence of them. The stakeholders are finally responsible in this phase to suggest metrics

Chapter 5: Case study: Halifax Port Authority and Hamburg Port 
Authority 
In this chapter, a case study is conducted exploring the potential of creating a green corridor 
between the Halifax Port Authority in Canada and the Hamburg Port Authority in Germany. 
Through this study it is desired to see the impact this green corridor could have on 
decarbonizing shipping by examining the regions’ settings and resources.  
 
5.1 Green corridor between the Halifax Port Authority and the Hamburg 
Port Authority 
In September 2022, an agreement was established in Hamburg between the Halifax Port 
Authority and the Hamburg Port Authority, manifested as a Memorandum of Understanding 
(MoU). The core objective of the collaboration was to make concerted efforts to reduce 
carbon emissions within the shipping corridor connecting them. By aligning their endeavors 
that aim at expediting the global energy transmission, the ports intend to promote the 
advancement of renewable hydrogen technologies (SAFETY4SEA, 2022). 
One of the primary focus areas of the initiative includes the development of port 
infrastructure in the Port of Halifax to facilitate the provision of bunkering services as well as 
the exporting of green hydrogen and its derivatives. Similarly, the focus is also placed on 
enhancing the development of port infrastructure in the Port of Hamburg to also support the 
bunkering operations and the import of green hydrogen and related byproducts (SAFETY4SEA, 
2022).  
A significant part of the initiative is the promotion of collaboration among stakeholders within 
the value chain, with the objective of advancing the utilization of green energy along the 
designated shipping corridor. Establishing aligned goals and measures such as regulations, 
safety measures and financial incentives are aimed through close engagement with local 
communities. Finally, emphasis is also placed on exchanging technology, knowledge and 
experience, alongside creating industry connections to speed up decarbonization within this 
specific route (SAFETY4SEA, 2022).  
Despite the substantial geographical distance of more than 5000 km between the two ports 
and their shared aspiration to transition towards green sustainable fuels, the level of activity 
between them has been not so highly extended, with a limited number of vessels sailing 
directly from one port to the other. More specifically, in 2022, a mere number of four vessels 
arrived at the Port of Halifax directly from the Port of Hamburg (MarineTraffic Blog, 2022).  
On the other hand, being one of the most bustling oceans in the world, the Atlantic witnesses 
an astounding flow of vessel activities on a daily basis with hundreds of influxes. Therefore, 
the forthcoming green corridor is expected to play a pivotal role in adopting green hydrogen 
as a prominent fuel source in a great number of vessels and routes connecting North America 
and Europe (MarineTraffic Blog, 2022). 
Through the establishment of the MoU between the two ports, it is anticipated to engender 
the proliferation of direct business opportunities between the two nations so that both 
==PAGE:58==

that this MoU will serve as a catalyst for Canadian companies to facilitate the deployment of 
their technologies in the German market, while simultaneously presenting German companies

(MarineTraffic Blog, 2022). 
 
5.2 Commitment to Sustainability  
One of the fundamental pillars of the strategic vision and corporate objectives of the Halifax 
Port Authorities is sustainability, which is framed in a cohesive framework that includes 
various aspects and activities. These entail economic considerations, environmental 
administration and community engagement. In this context, any prospective initiative or 
action within the project is required to demonstrate a clear commitment to the tripartite 
elements of the sustainability model, ensuring it generates positive outcomes in all three of 
them.  
It is one of the greatest goals of the Port of Halifax to prompt sustainability principles into its 
operations, aiming at generating economic advantages for the local community, the broader 
region and the country of Canada as a whole. These endeavors are geared towards creating a 
strong, effective and cost-competitive supply chain that promotes economic growth (Port of 
Halifax, 2022a).

Carbon Emissions

aiming at reducing the emissions in the various areas and operations in the port. These

the lighting systems, and focusing on the building energy performance. Additionally, in 2021,

5.4 Port characteristics 
• 
Port of Halifax  
Cargo 
Located at the east coast of Nova Scotia, the Port of Halifax is a multifaceted port which 
provides a wide range of services. It has the ability to accommodate substantial quantities of 
containerized vessels and cargo regardless of the scale. This is due to its robust infrastructure 
and the naturally deep harbor that offers the possibility to handle sizable vessels and cargo. 
Additionally, the port offers efficient connections to the Central Canada and the US Midwest 
through seamless transportation links, such as truck and intermodal rail. With access to these 
locations within a two-day timeframe, the logistics capabilities are also enhanced. In 2021, the 
port experienced an unprecedented increase in the throughput volume. Almost 5 metric tons 
of cargo arrived in the port’s facilities during that year, which is the highest recorded tonnage 
volume in the history of the port (Port of Halifax, 2022b). 
 
Connecting routes 
The Port of Halifax offers the possibility of reaching various destinations, by serving as a 
prominent gateway for key cargo destinations across North America. Because of its strategic 
location, it attracts some of the world’s largest shipping lines, with more than 150 countries 
around the world being vitally connected to the port. Currently, 19 global ocean carriers are 
being accommodated by the port, enhancing the accountability of the port and the 
significance of maintaining the engagement in international trade. Some of the countries 
between which the port has highly substantial trade are Singapore, United Kingdon, Unites 
States and Germany (Port of Halifax, 2022b).

Port of Hamburg  
Cargo and terminals 
The port of Hamburg is considered a significant maritime hub in Europe, demonstrating great 
magnitude and scale. In 2019, approximately 137 million tons of goods were successfully 
handled by the port, from which a significant proportion accounted for containerized goods, 
comprising 69% of the overall cargo volume. The port has four primary container terminals

by containerships. The terminal operators are mainly focused on cargo management and also

the port serving the largest retail companies of the world. An indicative example is the 
company Otto Group, a Hamburg-based retailer that conducts business in over 20 countries

The aim of this indicator is to evaluate the possibility of reducing and subsequently completely 
eliminating the emissions of carbon dioxide CO2, sulfur oxide SOx and nitrogen oxide NOx in 
the corridor. To assess this potential, an evaluation of producing and using alternative fuels is 
to be done, as well as assessing the possibilities for vessel optimization and usage of energy-

Alternative fuels and energy consumption 
To evaluate the energy consumption, as an indicator of the feasibility of the project, the 
energy efficiency assessment is needed for both the vessels and the corridor. To achieve 
sustainable and optimal energy consumption, renewable energy sources are required such as 
usage of wind and solar energy.  
In pursuit of its objectives, the collaboration between the Hamburg Port Authority and the 
Halifax Port Authority intends to explore a range of selection of alternative sustainable fuels

5.5.3 Economic Viability 
Cost savings and investments  
One of the most crucial KPIs in the new green shipping corridor is the economic viability of the 
project. As an increased cost is expected to arise in the first years of the initiation of the 
project, it is essential to indicate whether cost savings can result from the adoption of the new 
practices. Such savings may include reduced maintenance costs or fuel savings.  
To get an understanding of the investment scale in this project, an example is examined. In 
the scenario of utilizing ammonia as one of the alternative fuels, a cost of CAD $250-500 
million is projected to arise for the utilization of a bunkering vessel, a facility for the fuel 
storage and a loading jetty (Oceans North Conservation Society, 2023). 
However, no cost savings have been calculated yet. As the project is still in an early stage, no 
detailed economic analysis is evitable.  
 
Circularity in economy  
To achieve circularity within the economic environment, the possibilities of collaboration 
between the Port Authorities and other industries must be examined.  
An indicative example could be the utilization of substantial volumes of fresh water as 
feedstock for the production of ammonia and hydrogen. To make sure environmental 
sustainability is considered, additional attention must be paid when selecting the sources of 
fresh water in order to eliminate possible adverse effects on natural water systems and not 
having negative impact on the ecosystem. Therefore, collaboration with industries that 
possess water supplies can present a promising avenue for accessing a significant value with 
this context (Oceans North Conservation Society, 2023). 
Another example is the aforementioned collaboration of the Hamburg Port Authority with 
other organizations through the MoU with Air Products company for the production and 
utilization of hydrogen. As this alternative fuel is a considerable source of energy and can be 
utilized from other industries too, this partnership will facilitate the decarbonization goals of 
this project by sharing responsibilities and financial investments with other stakeholders too.

5.5.5 Regulatory Framework and Safety 
Finally, it is necessary to establish harmonized regulations between the two respective 
governments. There needs to be consistency and regulatory frameworks across both ends of 
the corridor to avoid situations where ships encounter different sets of rules upon entering 
Hamburg and Halifax. Therefore, complying with a shared collaborative regulatory set up is 
essential (CBC, 2022).  
In what regards the safety perspective of bunkering and storage, it is crucial to demonstrate 
safety measures for the related operations. As most of the industrial zones within the harbor 
of Halifax are located close to residential areas, this spatial arrangement introduces challenges 
conserving the storage and move of alternative fuels in the port. Potential inconveniences to 
the local communities might arise, warranting careful attention from a safety standpoint. Such 
concerns also arise within the bunkering operations that will take place at the port terminals 
and anchorages in other parts of the region. To ensure therefore the successful 
implementation of practices and the effective engagement of all stakeholders in the project, 
safety measures are necessary as they will play a pivotal role in addressing potential concerns 
and earning support for the initiative context (Oceans North Conservation Society, 2023).

hydrogen in the plans and implementing bunkering option. These modifications are destined

towards hydrogen utilization (World Ports Sustainability, 2023b). 
More specifically, the CMF Working Group has created standardized bunker checklists which 
can be tailored to follow the specific additional demands by each port regarding the needs for 
bunkering activities of new alternative fuels within or in close proximity to the ports. There 
are two types of checklists: the Ship to Ship and the Truck to Ship checklist. With either of the 
two types of checklists, bunkering of liquified gasses can be reflected within the port setting 
as marine fuels, including gasses such as liquid hydrogen and Liquified Natural Gas (LNG) 
(World Ports Sustainability, 2023a). 
 
5.5.6 Scalability and technology advancements 
Digitalization 
Through a Port Digitalization Strategy from the Port of Halifax, (2022b), efforts have been 
commenced on supporting the overall business strategy by fostering innovation and 
enhancing operational efficiency and resilience. Every tier of this strategic plan is designed to 
develop on and complement the others, with a principal focus on leading to the final 
transformation of the Port into a data-centric hub. The plan consists of four fundamental 
ideas: 
• 
Digitalization of the Halifax Port Authorities through adopting digital systems and 
technologies for the internal operations 
• 
Digitalization of the wider community in the Port of Halifax by integrating and utilizing 
digital solutions and platform by the involved stakeholders in the port ecosystem 
• 
Participate and engage in the initiative of creating a Global Shipping forum that will focus 
on establishing a standardized digital infrastructure in the global shipping industry 
• 
Close collaboration with the Canadian Port Authorities in a joint effort to foster 
digitalization practices and share knowledge across the Canadian port industry. 
 
Supply chain efficiency 
As new conditions will apply in all aspects of the corridor, the supply chain operations will also 
be heavily impacted. Therefore, the evaluation of logistics is also necessary to estimate the 
feasilbility of the new operations in the wider supply chain.  
It is anticipated that the demand for low and zero-emission shipping fuels will experience a 
rapid and substantial growth in the forthcoming decades as the decarbonization of shipping 
industry evolves over the years. Nonetheless, challenges might be presented while scaling the 
in-port infrastructure to align with the evolving demand. In the early phases of the project, 
during which the demand remains relatively low, one of the most cost-efficient solutions is

employing a bunker vessel that will directly transport fuel from the remote production plants. 
It must be noted though that this will have an adverse impact on the overall efficiency of the 
supply chain. In the long term and as more vessels will be utilized in the corridor, the bunkering 
frequency will also increase, the effectiveness and efficiency of the fuel supply chain will need 
to be enhanced and thus, establishing in-port storage facilities will become a more favorable

5.6 Assessment of the project potential  
The overall progress of the project is relatively limited so far as the project is still in its early 
stages. Information about undertaken actions and potential research remains scarce in 
published sources. It is premature to draw definitive conclusions about the feasibility of the 
project given the absence of any conducted tests within the various aspects of the plan. 
Nevertheless, there are several ways through which both ports could navigate their efforts in 
pursuit of the objectives of the project.  
One way to facilitate the project evolution would be by encouraging significant port users to 
align their emission reduction targets with the ultimate zero carbon goal of the two ports. This 
could be done by formulating a diverse display of initiatives towards achieving zero carbon 
shipping. Some indicative measures could be the implementation of pilot projects to foster 
innovation, the creation of supportive infrastructure, sharing technology programs and the 
introduction of incentive schemes. Moreover, the two ports must also pay attention and 
prioritize the exploration of viable long-term solutions and not only rely on promoting 
transition alternative fuels. This is because it still remains uncertain whether the ambitious 
target of zero carbon by 2050 is able to be achieved just by giving emphasis on facilitating 
alternative fuels such as hydrogen.  
Incentive schemes could be incorporated into the port strategy to implement the project. The 
potential impact of incentive schemes could be increased when substantial price differences 
are introduced to the existing schemes which typically involve modest deductions from port 
fees for ships, leading to questioning whether they have any effect. For example, bonuses or 
penalties could be incorporated to the port users who undergo sustainable practices or 
undesirable practices respectively, to help successfully implementing incentive schemes (ITF, 
2021). A similar action is being held at the Halifax Port Authority where a reduction in port 
fees is offered as a bonus for utilizing sustainable port power solutions that aim at improving 
the air quality at the port (Hamburg Port Authority, 2023). This shows that there is a 
considerable correlation between the application of financial discounts on the port utilization 
fees and the sustainable-oriented performance of ships.  
A fundamental factor in this project of achieving zero carbon shipping is represented also by 
the engagement and involvement of the two cities’ administration. The city-states can hold 
an important role as key driving forces of decarbonization in shipping. By aligning the emission 
reduction goals between the ports, the cities and the various port operators, better outcomes 
can be achieved in the port activities as the city-states can utilize their influence to motivate 
companies in embracing more active strategies towards reducing the GHG emissions (ITF, 
2021).

Governments can also play a supporting role in facilitating zero carbon shipping through 
promoting the discontinuation of fossil fuels subsidies and enabling a smooth transition to 
zero carbon operations. Currently, the shipping industry benefits from a fuel tax exemption, 
which leads to fossil-fuel energy direction in the shipping industry as at the same time no 
exception is yet applicable to renewable energy (ITF, 2021). Similarly, fuel tax exemptions are 
also applied to the terminal operators. It is easily understood that these measures significantly 
affect the use of alternative fuels and the motivations for the different operators, making the 
transition to alternative fuels less enticing. Thus, the role of government should be enhanced 
in this project, making sure the appropriate supporting measures are taken to move towards 
zero carbon shipping. An indicative example would be the inclusion of the carbon emission 
reduction factor in the tonnage tax, which is a tax that is applied in only a few countries to 
shipping companies when environmental performance is incorporated into the scheme, e.g., 
when younger ships are utilized.  
To enable faster transition towards zero carbon shipping the European Commission could also 
play an important role by reassessing the regulations about exemption on shipping fuel 
taxation. By removing this exemption, potential convergence between the conventional and 
the alternative fuels could be fostered.

Chapter 6: Evaluation of the GC idea and discussion  
 
6.1 Discussion on the research findings 
The primary goal of green shipping corridor initiatives is to enhance the advancement of 
efficient supply chains for alternative zero emission fuels that are presently in a very early 
development stage (Oceans North Conservation Society, 2023). The overall concept of 
establishing green corridors is still not widely investigated and the real possibilities of creating 
such corridors are still not known.  
Establishing a green corridor is a complex endeavor that entails a wide range of factors to be 
considered. Through the research it is found that suggested guidelines have been published 
by maritime organizations that aim at decarbonizing shipping, while also providing a 
fundamental framework for other stakeholders around the world that share the same vision 
too.  
Initiatives have been recorded around the world with good prospectives on establishing green 
corridors within the 2050 zero carbon target goal. However, since none of these projects are 
finalized, the anticipated challenges have not been faced yet and all the development is still 
on a theoretical level.  
 
6.2 Advantages and disadvantages 
There are several positive and negative aspects in the effort of transitioning to more 
sustainable practices in the maritime industry and specifically by institutionalizing the creation 
of green corridors.  
The positive outcomes cover a wide range of environmental advantages with the most 
profound being climate change mitigation. Adopting cleaner practices and technologies in the 
maritime corridors can help achieve the goals towards sustainability and positive climate 
change. The overall environmental benefits are also significant by promoting the utilization of 
eco-friendly fuels which can improve air quality and reduce GHG emissions. Another positive 
outcome is related to regulatory compliance, as through the various green corridor initiatives 
the alignment to regulations and environmental standards can be enabled on an international 
level between countries and organizations. Economic viability is also promoted for the 
maritime sector as the anticipated reduced fuel consumption and the improved operational 
efficiency can lead to cost savings for the shipping companies in the long term. Lastly, a 
significant competitive advantage is offered to the involved shipping companies and ports. 
This advantage is related to the higher position they can hold in the market since engaging in 
environmentally responsible operations and solutions. 
On the other hand, there are also disadvantages related to the green corridor idea. One of the 
most important is the high need for initial investments. Substantial upfront funds are 
necessary to initiate a green corridor project to be invested in research, production of 
alternative fuels, infrastructure, vessel fleets and renewable energy facilities. Moreover, 
currently the lack or the scarce availability of zero-carbon fuels significantly limits the supply 
==PAGE:70==
 
 
 Discussion 
 
57 
 
chain and thus, transitioning fully from conventional fuels to alternative ones is more 
challenging. Related to the new alternative fuels is also another challenge that regards the 
compatibility issues in vessels. As the new fuels will have different properties, vessels might 
need to be retrofitted or even completely replaced by new advanced ones. This is an obstacle 
that may lead to further logistic challenges in the supply chain. Logistics might also be 
challenged by the effect of the new fuel and vessel properties on trade routes. This is related 
to the fact the new routes might require longer transit times depending on these new 
properties, and further resulting in increased costs in some shipping routes. 
Finally, one of the most concerning challenges in this whole concept is the lack of cooperation 
willingness from some stakeholders. This is related to the fact that some companies or 
operators might be resisting on adopting new technologies and policies as this will potentially 
have a negative effect on their business models and disrupt their profits based on various 
collaborations with other partners.  
 
6.3 Controversy and IMO 
A lot of discussion is being held in the maritime industry about the role that IMO holds in this 
new idea of green corridors. As of now, IMO has not demonstrated a clear position in this new 
concept, leaving stakeholders and organizations free to solely work on new initiatives and 
without providing any guidance or specific support on how to establish a green corridor.  
Complaints from different actors have been expressed especially after the recent completion 
of the Marine Environment Protection Committee (MEPC 80) that was held in July 2023. 
Among the key agenda items in this session was the much-anticipated adoption of an 
upgraded IMO greenhouse gas strategy. However, many organizations and governments 
fiercely declared their disappointment in IMO’s inability to address critical ways and policies 
that will make a positive effect on the objective of the Paris Agreement temperature goal of 
1.5oC.  
Prominent Non-Governmental Organizations such as Opportunity Green - an organization that 
works on supporting climate vulnerable countries - believe that IMO lost a crucial last 
opportunity to align with the objectives of the Paris Agreement. This alignment was essentially 
vital in facilitating a fair transition for some of the world’s most susceptible to vulnerability 
nations and safeguard global biodiversity (Harvey, 2023b).  
Over recent months, an escalated demand and pressure has grown upon the international 
shipping industry to adopt and embrace the introduction of carbon levy on vessels. This 
implementation would serve as a funding mechanism to move towards climate actions in the 
developing nations. Through these levy charges that are related to greenhouse gas emissions 
generated during maritime activities and shipping, companies would be greatly incentivized 
to improve their operational sustainability by upgrading their fleets, running them more 
effectively and exploring cleaner technologies and alternative fuels (Harvey, 2023b). 
A proposition regarding levy indicates that shipping companies could contribute to a fund that 
is proportionate to the carbon emissions generated by their fleets. The collected proceeds 
could then be allocated to fund projects and initiatives focused on climate adaptation and 
eliminating emissions in the shipping industry.

However, one of the challenges in developing a levy and the corresponding fund is the 
alignment between the different members of IMO. As each member might have different 
priorities and preferences on the allocation of funds, reaching a consensus on disbursement 
could be more complicated. Additionally, there is a big number of businesses that are not 
interested in engaging in such a measure but prefer to rather go on with their current setups 
in their operations until 2030, and then gradually implement new fuels (Harvey, 2023a).  
During negotiations, many countries have actively opposed to adopting such a measure as a 
levy. Among them are countries with a profound role in the shipping industry like Chile, China 
and Saudi Arabia. It is also interesting that against this tax measure are also some nations that 
are widely known for their dedication to climate change such as Australia and United Arab 
Emirates (Harvey, 2023b). 
On the other hand, several EU members are actively advocating the levy establishment. 
Among them are notable shipping nations like Greece. Also, other worldwide nations, such as 
the USA and Japan have expressed their willingness in adopting a levy. Regrettably, the 
inefficient results of the last MEPC 80 held in July 2023, had led to the unfortunate 
circumstance that any potential levy can only be subject to agreement after the year of 2027 
at the earliest. Consequently, this outcome will inevitably cause further delay of potential 
benefits for the developing countries that aim to accelerate decarbonization through their 
efforts.   
After the completion of MEPC 80, organizations expressed their disappointment about the 
lack of actions and initiatives taken by IMO. Since global GHG emissions are not directly 
included in the Paris Agreement, it is believed that IMO which is branch of the UN, is the only 
responsible for efficiently addressing the required steps and ways that will contribute to 
decarbonization in the international shipping industry. It is also believed that if IMO had taken 
responsibility earlier, a 50% reduction in emissions could be achieved by 2030, indicating that 
with no formal measures taken already, this goal is no longer feasible.  
 
6.4 Financial challenges and risks    
Some financial challenges or concerns may arise with the implementation of this new concept. 
These are related to the increased initial costs and the implications that might be caused as 
the ongoing expenses for establishing a green corridor will be maintained high too. These are 
costs that are necessary for the modifications and upgrades in most aspects of a corridor 
including port infrastructure and alternative fuel utilization. Funding sources are therefore 
vital for the execution of such a major initiative.  
However, questions may arise here regarding the willingness of taking on such a financial 
responsibility and investing in such a risky endeavor. A strong commitment is needed by the 
various stakeholders involved such as port authorities, governments, international 
organizations and shipping operators. To also address these financial risks, the potential 
uncertainties related to making large-scale investments and adopting new technologies must 
be assessed, so that the involved stakeholders acknowledge and avoid them to the level that 
this might be possible.  
Therefore, given the increased costs incurred during the early phases of transitioning to green 
corridors, it becomes imperative to answer who will be willing to embrace the higher costs 
==PAGE:72==

when there are other more cost-effective transportation alternatives. Based on the responses 
by individuals involved in maritime industry and who participated to the questionnaire survey 
conducted for this study (see Appendix), it is notable that presently, stakeholders show a 
varying range of willingness, with an extend of influence being reported by Corporate Social 
Responsibility (CSR), which is a business approach for companies that encompasses societal 
and environmental aspects into the operations and decision-making procedures. However, to 
establish universal commitment, a clearly outlined rule-based framework is necessary. 
A factor that could also possibly prompt stakeholders to encounter higher costs concerns the 
potential advantages resulting in their branding and customer image. By attracting 
environmentally conscious customers, they can promote their reputation in the industry and 
motivate this way more customers to invest in their eco-friendly practices. At the same time, 
though, a great concern is also associated with the economic competitiveness of such an 
initiative. It is unknown whether the shipping companies will still be competitive in the case 
of operating in green corridors, while others will still remain in and use the traditional 
maritime routes. It might be more difficult for companies to maintain their competitive 
nature, which is also a part of the overall supply chain.  
One of the greatest financial challenges of investing into a green corridor is also associated 
with the uncertainty surrounding the infrastructure for producing economically viable 
alternative fuels and its reliable availability. Investors in this field are exposed to high risks, 
with shipping companies facing every day the challenge of deciding on different fuels such as 
ammonia and methanol, all representing choices of a speculative bet on the projected fuel 
that will dominate the industry in the future.  
Finally, concerns are expressed about the prospective changes in fees, taxes and tariffs for 
shipping companies following the introduction of green corridors. Debates concerning 
fairness and equitable distribution of the financial burdens may arise. To effectively address 
and resolve all these potential financial controversies, thorough planning and robust 
collaboration between the various stakeholders is essential. Such efforts must be 
complemented by fostering supportive regulations and policies with the prospect of 
maintaining an equitable transition to green corridors.   
 
6.5 Credibility of green corridors as a business case  
To establish a viable economic proposition for a maritime green corridor, one of the main 
prerequisites is the widespread availability of low carbon fuel options deliverable on a large 
scale, while also being offered at financially viable prices. Costs associated with fuels and 
facilities must be balanced against potential benefits. This is tightly connected to a key 
challenge of utilizing proper infrastructure for production of alternative fuels timely.  
A critical determinant is also the brand and commercial dimension that emerges, with the 
ability of operators to effectively transfer the costs of decarbonization to the end-users along 
the value chain. This cost transfer could promote feasibility of the concept with containerships 
potentially having a comparative advantage over the energy sector through attracting more 
businesses and securing long-term partnerships with other environmentally conscious 
stakeholders.

Additionally, according to the responses to the questionnaire survey conducted for this study, 
financial long-term sustainability is also required to achieve a credible business case for a 
green corridor. Stakeholders involved in a green corridor project will want to ensure an 
adequate Return of Investment (ROI). This can be achieved through several ways such as 
maintaining potential cost savings or accessing green markets and incentives. Such incentives 
may include an advantageous treatment of companies by markets or regions after meeting 
certain environmental criteria. Also, risk mitigation may enhance ROI by addressing potential 
risks related to the changing consumer preferences or alternating regulations, as a sustainable 
business model can be more resilient to future challenges. 
 
6.6 Regional vs global measures towards decarbonization 
Both scales of measures, regional and global, are necessary for a comprehensive approach of 
decarbonization. The unique characteristics of a region can promote initiatives on a local level 
that act as laboratories for practicing and testing innovative solutions.  
An indicative example of regional measures can be observed in the package of measures 
undertaken by the European Union (EU) towards decarbonization of shipping in alignment 
with the principles outlined in the European Green Deal and the “Fit for 55” package. Among 
other things, these include a Directive aimed at integrating shipping into the EU Emissions 
Trading System (ETS), as well as the FuelEU Maritime Regulation that seeks to promote the 
adoption of sustainable fuels. Starting in 2024, these measures are intended to be enforced 
on ships of 5000 GT (gross tonnage) or above, that call at ports within the European Economic 
Area (EEA) and thus constituting a regional framework. Nonetheless, the way these regional 
measures will interact with the global measures implemented by IMO is yet to be seen, with 
limited or no coordination among the two regulatory levels. The expectation is that the 
regional measures will contribute to speeding up the discussion of measures on the global 
level, without undermining the global approach.  
Since the green corridor concept is heavily tied with specific ports and regions, it can also be 
a regional approach. In that sense, working on such measures in the early stages can be 
considered as a good first approach. At the same time, this approach should ultimately be tied 
up with global measures that can provide a constructive framework that ensures uniformity 
in the progress of the goals across the maritime industry. Therefore, a combination of the two 
is necessary in achieving sustainable solutions as the regional initiatives can act as building 
blocks for establishing global agreements and subsequently contribute to strong commitment 
to sustainability.  
It is also notable that certain Pacific Islands such as Marshall Islands, Solomon Islands, Fiji etc., 
that hold a significant sensitivity and vulnerability to the impacts of climate change and are 
advocates of the global levy on carbon emissions, have expressed their concerns that the 
implementation of maritime green corridors might run the risk of bypassing these regions and 
further aggravating the various inequities that are associated with their distant geographical 
location from the major markets. The principle of a “just and equitable transition” is one of 
the main pillars of the revised IMO Strategy and it is vital to ensure that the establishment of 
green corridors is in close alignment with this principle.

6.7 Withdrawal of old vessels and pollution  
Another significant aspect of the overall decarbonization concept which is not extensively 
discussed from the various stakeholders or the organizations that fight towards establishing 
green corridors, is the handling of old vessels that will no longer be utilized. As new fuels will 
start being introduced, the requirements for vessels are also going to be different. Some 
vessels might be able to undertake modifications and be adjusted according to the needed 
standards. However, a large number of vessels are expected to be useless as the green 
corridors are starting to be established, leading to withdrawal of them.  
Concerns therefore arise about the way these old vessels are going to be handled as none of 
the announced green corridor initiatives mentions in their strategic plan or has examined the 
effect this withdrawal may have on the environment.  
End-of-life ships are toxic and cause significant pollution to the environment. They can contain 
big amounts of toxic materials in their structure which require special treatment when they 
have to be removed, disposed of or properly located.  
When a ship’s operational life is getting closer to its end, the ship is becoming a waste too. 
One of the most environmentally friendly ways of handling them is recycling due to the 
reusability it offers for materials such as plastics, iron or steel. In reality though, it is only a 
really small fraction of ships that are handled in the proper way when they reach the end of 
life and follow clean and safe procedures.  
In the last few decades, shipbreaking consists a hazardous process that takes place in some 
specific places around the world, especially in beaches of some South Asia counties. Some of 
these countries are India, Bangladesh and Pakistan, in which ship owners from Europe or East 
Asia sell the ships for breaking. The majority of the ships arriving there are full of toxic 
substances. An unfortunate and dirty industry takes action there by breaking down the ships’ 
parts without proper safety or environmental rules being followed (NGO Shipbreaking 
Platform, 2019).  
Some of the hazardous materials people are exposed to while shipbreaking are asbestos, 
heavy metals and mineral oil. Due to poor operations, risks are faced not only by the workers 
that are executing the breaking down but also there are extreme environmental risks for both 
the people that reside in the surrounding areas but also for the environment itself. The ocean 
and the atmosphere get heavily polluted but the toxic materials and the flumes disturbing the 
ecosystem (NGO Shipbreaking Platform, 2023).  
Due to the lack of control and accessibility in these areas, it is difficult to assess the level of 
pollution resulting. Ship owners that are interested in adopting sustainable practices and 
joining ventures towards decarbonization need to include ship withdrawal in their studies. As 
mentioned in Chapter 2.7.3, an LCA analysis is suggested when discussions begin for new fuels 
and new/modified vessels. However, an LCA for a green corridor project will be complete 
when this kind of analysis is also conducted for the old vessels too that might end up being no 
longer needed for utilization in the corridor.

6.8 General conclusions based on the research 
All in all, to achieve a profound shift towards sustainable operations and policies in the 
maritime transport sector, a greater effort is required despite the already undertaken actions 
worldwide. This is necessary to ensure the long-term resilience and preservation of our 
ecologically delicate ecosystems, as well as safeguarding the prosperity and well-being of 
communities (European Environmental Agency, 2021). 
When compared to other modes of transport such as air or rail, shipping only causes the 
lowest GHG emissions per ton of cargo and per kilometer of transport, making it the most 
efficient mode (Wiacek et al., 2018). Discussions therefore arise on whether focusing on 
decarbonizing shipping is vital while the other sectors cause significantly greater damage to 
the environment by emitting enormous amounts of carbon.  
It also remains uncertain whether creating green corridors is feasible due to the absence of 
formally formed guidelines and regulations yet from IMO, leading to wasting valuable time 
towards reaching the zero-carbon target by 2050. Progress is still slow and as IMO is yet to 
take lead in the execution of the overall concept, the number of already announced initiatives 
around the world is currently insufficient to catalyze complete decarbonization in shipping. To 
achieve efficient reduction results, greater emphasis must be placed on collective global 
efforts, as relying solely on only a few green corridors would have limited impact on the 
elimination of emissions.

heavily tied with specific ports and thus regional regulators. The carbon adjustment mechanism of EU is 
one measure in that direction. 
 
6. Given the increased costs in the early phases of switching to Green Corridors (e.g., for new 
vessels, port infrastructure, new alternative fuels), why would stakeholders be willing to pay 
more if more cheaper options to carry cargo are available? * 
The ability of the stakeholders to pass the decarbonization cost across the supply chain is different for each 
market segment (e.g. containerships, energy, dry, etc) and that depends on the characteristics of the end 
consumer. Regulations like the carbon adjustment mechanism or other carbon pricing measures can help 
to make green transportation options comparable to cheaper but dirtier alternatives. 
 
7. What is the biggest challenge in terms of financial investment for the stakeholders 
involved in a maritime green corridor? * 
At this stage the uncertainty involved in the development of viable alternative fuel production 
infrastructure and availability is a high risk factor for investors in this space. From the shipping

and LNG, each taking a bet on the alternative fuel option to dominate. 
 
8. A potential goal would be to achieve complete decarbonization by 2050. Is this timeframe 
feasible given the continuous increase in maritime greenhouse gas emissions every year? * 
This decarbonization trajectory is a very realistic scenario to be adopted soon as a target. Although quite 
challenging by conventional technologies and approaches, decarbonization could be achieved if only major 
transformations happen in the industry including alternative low and zero carbon fuels adoption and 
Carbon Capture technology on board, on shore and offshore.
