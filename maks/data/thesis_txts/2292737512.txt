Abstract 
High voltage stabilizers are compounds used to significantly increase the dielectric 
breakdown strength of polymers. To reduce power loss there has come an increasing 
demand for high voltage power cables in the power grid. As high voltage insulating 
materials are subjected to large electric fields, the ability to improve the electrical 
properties of polymers is needed. The process of fabricating insulation materials can 
leave defects in the material which cause breakdown when subjected to high voltages. 
This thesis focuses on exploring the capability of improving PDMS by implementing high 
voltage stabilizers which have previously been used with other polymers. By analyzing 
the dielectric breakdown strength and permittivity of different stabilizers, the trends for 
additive compatibility with PDMS has been evaluated. Furthermore the impact of 
additive size and different functional groups have been determined.

Introduction 
With an increasing demand for high voltage (HV) cables in the power grid, it is 
important to make sure these cables are as efficient as possible. With the present 
technology, the power loss over a 100 km HV alternating current (AC) cable is 
approximately 5 %, the longer the cable the higher the loss. HV direct current (DC), 
doesn’t suffer as much loss and is the main resource for long range or subsea cables. 
Reducing the loss can be attained by lowering the current input and increasing the 
voltage, but for this to work an insulating material capable of working at high voltage is 
needed. Currently pure crosslinked poly ethylene is the main resource for insulation, but 
investigations of new materials are being undertaken. To improve upon the insulating 
materials, it is important to understand the relationship between morphology, chemical 
structure and electrical properties. PDMS as a dielectric electro active polymer (DEAP) 
has been investigated, as the theoretical properties of PDMS should be excellent for an 
insulating polymer.  
In the “battle of the currents” of the late 1880’s, alternating current advocated by 
Nikola Tesla and Westinghouse, became the standard method of power over DC, 
advocated by Thomas Edison. Because of ACs capability of transforming between 
different voltage levels, it was safer to use, power plants could be further away from 
settlements and be operated at a lower cost. DC however is better suited for very small 
distances, computer circuitry for example, and very long range cables, mostly subsea. 
By increasing the transmission voltage (V), power loss can be reduced for both AC and 
DC. The main loss source of power loss and heating is joule loss, which is proportional to 
the square of the current (I), looking at the equation for power (P) P = I * V, it is obvious 
that the same power can be transmitted by raising the voltage and lowering the 
current, effectively reducing losses from the current.  
With growing demand to cut CO2, an increase in power supply from electrical energy 
produced by renewable energy sources has emerged. With offshore windmill, hydro 
plant or potentially wave farms, the need for introduction of more long range subsea 
HV cables in the power grid is apparent. As cable length increases, the power required 
to charge an AC cable becomes less favorable compared to DC, thus DC is the ideal 
for subsea, long range or offshore cables. 
To enhance the efficiency and lifetime of HVDC cables, improved insulation is a key 
factor. Previously the main material for insulation of long range subsea cables has been 
paper, impregnated with insulation oil. XLPE has been used since 1968, the first 420 kV 
subsea cable was introduced in 2006. Most cables today are made from XLPE and they

have reached up to 500 kV. For XLPE to be a good insulator it needs to be extremely 
pure, raising the cost significantly. The most important properties required for good 
insulation are high electrical breakdown strength (DBS) and high dielectric permittivity.  
Insulation failures are commonly caused by voids or defects in the polymer where 
electrical discharges can occur. As polymers inherently have low dielectric permittivities, 
certain inorganic fillers have been used in attempts of raising this attribute in the 
polymers. Fillers in the nanometer size range have successfully been implemented and 
proved to improve the tree initiation voltage (TIV) and permittivity. Dielectric loss is 
either due to conduction or conversion to thermal energy through vibration. 
Conduction (common failure for DC) and increased young modulus is often found as a 
side effect when using inorganic fillers. Increasing DBS has been attempted using high 
voltage stabilizers, organic compounds capable of absorbing rogue electrons in a 
conjugated electron system and thus preventing damage to the polymer. These are 
only needed in small quantities and have easy procedures for incorporating them in the 
polymer matrix. 
Throughout this study, high voltage stabilizing additives previously used in polyethylene 
are investigated and mixed with PDMS to explore their effects on the electrical 
properties and synergy with PDMS.

Literature Study 
Throughout the literature study the focus will be on exploring the theory behind 
electrical properties of polymers. By bringing the attention towards the structural 
properties of PDMS, then moving into the properties of dielectric electro active 
polymers and looking at different electric effects, such as space charge and polarity. 
Understanding how these properties affect the breakdown mechanisms of polymers 
and what can be done to prevent it, by controlling these effects with the addition 
additive molecules. 
Polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) 
PDMS is the most widely used silicon based organic polymer, it is a part of the 
polysiloxane compound class and was discovered early in the 1900’s. The polymer is 
known for its unusual rheological properties and visco elastic behaviour. The properties 
include, transparency, low young modulus, bio-compatibility, low surface tension, low 
internal tension, high hydrophobicity, high temperature range of application, high 
breathability and resistance towards many common forms of degradation. Since its 
discovery it has found many applications such as contact lenses, cosmetics or 
shampoos, water repellents, lubricants, anti-fouling agents, medical devices, insulation 
material and elastomers. 1,2 
The chemical formula for PDMS is (CH3)3SiO[Si(CH3)2O]nSi(CH3)3, n being the number of 
repeating monomer units, Fig 1.

Dielectric electro active polymers 
Electroactive polymers (EAPs) consist of soft polymeric materials with large energy 
densities, fast response times and the ability to undergo large deformations when 
subjugated to an electric field. Dielectric EAPs (DEAPs) is a subclass of the EAPs and is 
interesting due to the obtainability of high actuation speeds, strains, work densities and 
degree of electromechanical coupling.3,4 
DEAPs are currently being researched with regards to advancing the fields of 
electromechanical applications such as actuators, generators and sensors. They work 
by encasing an elastomeric film between two compliant electrodes, forming a 
capacitor. Upon the application of an external voltage, electrostatic forces with 
different polarities on each side of the film are created. The electrostatic forces acting 
on the elastomer, will squeeze film subsequently causing an expansion in the planar 
directions.5 
PDMS is one of the most commonly used materials for DEAPs due to its inherent low 
viscous loss and higher temperature range. PDMS does however possess a low 
dielectric constant resulting in the need for a high electric field to cause actuation.6,7 
The dielectric permittivity or dielectric constant is an important parameter for DEAPs, as 
it represents the materials capacity to store electrical energy. Upon subjugation to an 
electric field, all materials become polarized, if the material is conducting, an electric 
field will induce charge transport. Dielectric permittivity of elastomers range from 2-8, 
PDMS has approximately 2-3, whereas certain ceramics can be in the region of 
thousands. 
Inherently all molecules contain an electron cloud, when an applied electric field is 
present these electrons will be attracted to the anode thus creating the polarized film,

Opposite the permittivity is the dielectric loss, which is given as the loss tangent, tan δ, 
tan δ = ε’’/ε’, where ε’’ is the imaginary part of the dielectric permittivity and ε’ is the 
real part. The imaginary part is mainly attributed to dipole relaxation, while the real part 
is ε’ = ε0εr, ε0 being vacuum permittivity and εr is the relative permittivity.8–10 
The dielectric breakdown strength of PDMS is approximately 100-130 V/µm versus 
polyethylene which is 40-60 V/µm, though highly pure industrial PE can reach 150-160 
V/µm. The possibility to increase the DBS of PDMS is important, especially in the search 
to find a new, better or cheaper insulation material for high voltage cables.  
Pure PDMS films have been investigated by Geratt and Bergbreiter. These studies 
focused on PDMS films in the low µm range from 2-14 µm, with the use of silicon 
electrodes in different shapes and with different gap sizes, breakdown energies from 
250-635 V/µm were found. This suggest that PDMS has the potential to reach higher 
breakdown strengths than PE.11 
 
Breakdown Mechanisms 
Breakdown can occur through different mechanisms, dependent on temperature, time 
and 
environment. 
The 
different 
mechanisms 
are 
avalanche, 
thermal 
and 
electromechanical which occur quickly, while erosion and water treeing happens over 
longer periods of time. Most common breakdown in high voltage cables is caused by 
fast avalanches. Fast avalanches dominate at low temperature, while thermal and 
electromechanical dominate at higher temperatures, erosion dominates at low 
voltages and water treeing in aqueous environments. 
Intrinsic breakdown strength is the theoretical value at which the electric field raises 
electrons to the conducting band. The intrinsic value is dominated by which kind of 
breakdown occurs in the polymer. Due to defects and impurities in the sample, the 
experimentally measured value will be lower than this true value, which can only be 
obtained for a perfect material.  
In 1932 it was theorized that electrons gain energy due to the applied electric field, but 
they also lose energy due to interactions with phonons (excited states), i.e. inducing 
lattice vibrations, this theorem is called the low energy criterion. This criterion describes 
that breakdown can only occur if the average energy received by the electrons is 
greater than the average losses.12 This theorem has been studied and it has been 
concluded that DBS is higher for materials with large cut off frequencies and large band 
gaps.12

It has been shown that impurities create energy states within the band gaps. These 
states are called traps and the depth of a trap refers to the amount of energy required 
to release a charge carrier from a trap. The further away from the respective band the 
state is, the deeper the trap, i.e. unoccupied from the conduction band, and occupied 
from the valence band. An unoccupied energy state close to the conducting band is 
called a hopping trap if it’s within 0.2 eV. Shallow traps are defined between 0.2-0.6 eV 
and deep traps between 0.7-1.3 eV, deepest traps are above 1.4 eV. The deepest traps 
are responsible for the distribution of space charges in the material. The effect of deep 
traps on space charge and conduction has been investigated for PE, and it was found 
that these influence the amount of space charges that develop near the electrodes. 
Either homo or hetero space charges are observed, i.e. a buildup of negative charge 
at the cathode and positive charge at the anode (Homo) or vice versa (Hetero). If only 
shallow traps are present, over time the injected electrons will conform to a homo 
charge system, but if deep traps are present a hetero charge system will appear where 
there’s a large buildup of positive charge at the cathode. In a hetero charge system, 
the buildup of negative charge at the anode is preventing further injection of electrons. 
As a result, smaller conduction currents are present i.e. decreased charge mobility at 
high electric fields. It is theorized that space charges which conform to a HOMO 
arrangement decease DBS by enhancing the electric field locally thus initiating 
breakdown. It has also been concluded that deep traps which conform to a hetero 
space charge, suppress injection of electrons and thus decrease mobility of charge 
carriers, effectively increasing DBS13–16. There are however inconsistencies and different 
opinions on this matter in the literature and thus other properties probably play a role in 
this matter. 
Avalanche is a name for different breakdown mechanisms, these include: fast 
avalanche, water treeing and erosion. Avalanche breakdown can occur slowly or 
quickly, depending on the magnitude of the applied electric field. Fast avalanche 
breakdown happens when electrons gain sufficient kinetic energy to ionize other 
electrons through collision, increasing the amount of free electrons exponentially. Two 
categories of trees/slow avalanches exists in polymer materials, water trees created by 
degradation due to the presence of water in the polymer under high voltage and 
electrical trees caused by erosion due to gas discharge i.e. bombardment of the 
polymer by electrical arcs. Treeing is usually created under low electric fields over a 
longer time.17,18 
Fast avalanche breakdown occurs when an electric field ionizes electrons, raising their 
kinetic energy. Electrons are accelerated into impacts with other electrons and if they 
have sufficient kinetic energy, ionize the impacted electron. This raises the electrons to

==PAGE:12==
the conducting band through impact ionization. The released electrons are also 
accelerated by the electric field, if this continues an avalanche is created. By 
interacting with the polymer matrix electrons can lose energy, thus preventing 
avalanches. The length between electron interactions and the polymer matrix is known 
as the mean free path. If the electrons receive sufficient energy from the applied field, 
this initializes a conducting path that cause rapid breakdown in the polymer due to the 
high amount of mobile electrons. The mean free path in a solid insulation materials is 
usually 5-20 Å, which makes it difficult for the average applied electric field to 
accelerate electrons to a kinetic energy that causes impact ionization. Fast avalanches 
are usually initiated in areas of high local fields, such as the tip of electrical trees, at 
polymer matrix defects or by high space charges.19 
The mean free path can be increased by impurities, defects or voids, these can in turn 
be caused by water treeing or erosion. An amorphous polymer generally have small 
voids due to its unordered characteristics, larger voids can be created through the 
manufacture of films, i.e. gas bubbles or trapped air.  The mean free path inside gas 
pockets is generally much longer than in the polymer, and the ionization energy much 
lower, therefore making it easier to initiate avalanches in these regions. Fast avalanche 
breakdown is dependent on the thickness of the sample, the thinner the sample the 
higher the DBS, this is due to a lower probability of defects in thinner samples, this is 
described by the inverse power law equation:  
E(d)=kd-n

derived experimentally, d is the thickness20.

water diffuses into the polymer at local defects where the electric field is enhanced, this

diffusion causes stress in the polymer and thus creates micro sized cavities which grow 
into channels. Water trees are most common in underground and submarine cables 
where water has a large presence and can diffuse into the polymer over time. Water 
tree formation is dependent on the size and number of spherulites in the sample, the 
smaller and higher amount of spherulites the harder it is for water to penetrate. Water 
propagation occurs easiest in the amorphous domains of the polymer, and the water 
trees tend to extend between the spherulite regions.17,21–23 
Erosion is a mechanism in which electrical trees are slowly formed at low electric fields 
due to partial discharges, which damage the polymer locally. The process of electrical 
treeing has been described in three stages by Tanaka et al. First the tree is initiated by 
charge injection, followed by channel formation caused by solid dielectric breakdown 
from the top of the tree and finally the channels are widened by erosion of the polymer 
from electrical arcs24,25.  
Dispersion of nanoparticles can influence the resistivity towards erosion. The interface 
region between particles and polymer becomes a high volume of the composite 
because of the large surface area of particles. Depending on the characteristics of the 
particles this interface region can improve the resistance towards electrical arcs, but is 
not completely understood yet, more on this in the Filler paragraph.  
Thermal breakdown occurs when the polymer is damaged because of elevated 
temperatures caused by too slow dissipation of heat. During DC conditions, heating 
under an electric field is caused by Joule heating, while under AC conditions it’s both 
joule heating and dielectric losses. Joule heating is initiated by small conduction 
currents that generate heat due to the resistivity of the polymer. Generally the heating 
is mainly supplied by joule heating as insulating materials such as polymers have low 
thermal conductivity and low dielectric losses, making the heat generated by dielectric 
losses negligible.

Even at low fields, conduction currents exist in insulating materials and increase with 
increasing field strength and temperature. Impurities and the disordered characteristics 
of amorphous domains enable the insertion of new energy states in the band gap 
which increases mobility of charge carriers27.  
The electrothermal breakdown of thin dielectric elastomers from PDMS has been 
investigated and modelled by Zakaria et al.28 With the purpose of predicting the 
operating range of PDMS dielectric elastomers and evaluating the thermal mechanism 
with regard to temperature at a given electric field. Furthermore the effect of 
temperature on the dielectric properties of different PDMS elastomers and different films 
with different percentages of silica and permittivity enhancing fillers was studied. 
Increased loss permittivity with temperature was attributed to increased dissipation of 
heat and further joule heating was caused by a proportional increase in conductivity. 
Through modelling and breakdown measurements it was theorized that electrothermal 
breakdown is not a major factor in electrical breakdown, as the electrothermal 
breakdown value is at about 5 times higher than the electrical breakdown strength.28 
Electromechanical breakdown is dependent on the mechanical strength of the 
polymer. At elevated temperatures, the polymers mechanical strength decreases due 
to softening of the polymer. Compression of the polymer by electrostatic forces caused 
by the applied voltage, increase the electric field in the narrowing polymer, further 
increasing the electrostatic forces between the electrodes and eventually leading to 
breakdown of the polymer through mechanical failure. The difference between 
crosslinked and non-crosslinked PE has been tested to investigate the effect on 
mechanical and electric strength. And it was found that increased crosslinking, 
radically increased the resistivity towards electromechanical breakdown29. A theory has 
been proposed where electromechanical breakdown is caused by a local 
deformation by shear forces attributed to local increases in the electric field. The area 
which is affected creates an indentation in the polymer further increasing 
inhomogeneous electric field and shear forces and eventually results in breakdown. 29 
 
Fillers 
As polymers have an inherent low permittivity, experiments have been attempted by 
mixing polymers with high permittivity ceramic/metallic fillers. The availability of ceramic 
fillers with permittivities in the thousands, theoretically make for very appealing polymer 
composites. Often high weight percentages of filler is necessary to increase the 
dielectric strength, however this in turn usually decrease breakdown strength or 
increase dielectric loss. Difficulty in making a homogenous interphase between the 
10 | P a g e  
 
==PAGE:15==
organophilic polymer matrix and the hydrophilic metal oxides leads to large contrasts in 
permittivity between phases in the matrix, which give rise to inhomogeneous electric 
fields, thus the filler starts acting like defects instead. 
The interface region between particles and polymer matrix has been theorized to 
attribute a lot to the effectiveness of fillers. The smaller the particle size is, the larger the 
relative surface area becomes, Fig 5. This is in turn inhibited by the fact that a large 
relative surface area increases the risk of particle interaction and agglomeration. To 
prevent the agglomeration of particles, incorporating polymer compatible coatings or 
side groups can improve the dispersion in the matrix. As particle size decreases, the 
interfacial region increases and correspondingly the interaction between the coating 
of the particles and the polymer matrix increase. This in turn increases the impact of 
supportive groups on the dielectric properties of the entire material16,30,31. 
Size of fillers has been investigated and it has been found that fillers in the conventional 
micron size have been shown to decrease breakdown strength. This is believed to be 
because of aggregation of particles, leading to increased local electric fields. Fillers in 
the nanometer range has been researched and proved more successful, believed due 
to the increase in interface area and dispersion over the micron sized fillers. Fillers in the 
nano-size range can improve DBS by scattering the charge carriers, thus reducing their 
energy. Incorporation of particles also influence the morphology of the polymer, and 
can lead to increases in DBS if a less crystalline structure is reached, as the mean free 
path is shorter in amorphous matrixes19,24.  
In a study done by Roy et al.32 using XLPE with different modified and unmodified fillers, 
it was theorized that increases in DBS was attributed to two main effects: for non-polar

As with the size of fillers having an influence, so does the shape. Nanolaminates 
consisting of modified montmorillionite have been implemented in a polymer matrix of

laminate layers are perpendicular to the electrodes the dense packing will inhibit the 
mobility of charge carriers33. 
As previously mentioned, PDMS has a low dielectric permittivity of about 2-3, the 
undertaking of improving this has lead through the use of many fillers and additives that 
previously have had success in increasing the permittivity, when mixing with other 
polymers and they have been attempted incorporated in PDMS; alumina trihydrate34, 
acetylene carbon black/BaTiO3 composites35–39, TiO240–42, expanded graphite43, 
graphene44, carbon nanotubes45, organically modified montmorillonite33,46, lead 
magnesium niobate-lead titanate38, lead-zirconate titanate47, cyanopropyl48 and 
calcium copper titanate49, amongst others. Generally all of these improve the 
permittivity of their respective polymer, but at the cost of either decreased breakdown, 
increased dielectric loss, increased conductivity, particle agglomeration or increases in 
young modulus. These drawbacks usually outweigh the positive effects of the increased 
permittivity. 
 
High Voltage Stabilizers 
Voltage stabilizers consist of compounds that contain delocalized π electrons which 
work by trapping energetic electrons and creating anion radicals. Electrons collide with 
the aromatic compounds resulting in a reduction of the electrons energy, leaving it with 
less energy than is required to damage the polymer bonds. It is important that the 
excitation energy of the electron traps are lower than those of the polymer backbone, 
as these are more likely to be targeted before the polymer. The aromatic compounds 
which undergo electrical transition to aliphatic cations can transform into stable 
aromatic cations due to resonance stability caused by the delocalized pi electrons.

In 1994 Ieda et al. wrote a review on the research of conduction and breakdown in 
polymers. Few conclusions were reached, it was however noted that under high field 
DC conditions, electric field distortions caused by space charge, is one of the main 
contributors to breakdown and it is suggested that electron carrier traps and artificial 
control of carrier injection are investigated to understand this effect.50 
The first recorded attempts of purposely using high voltage stabilizers as additives for 
increasing electric properties were performed by Yamano et al. in 1998. Building on the 
theory of the 1994 study, they investigate the effect of different aromatic 
azocompounds. Six different additives were used, containing different combinations of

a molecular level in polyethylene with various additives. It was found that aromatic 
carbonyl compounds have important characteristics for this purpose. The π-systems can 
trap electrons by obtaining energy from them and transform aliphatic cations to stable 
aromatics to prevent degradation of the polymer matrix.

Throughout the exploration of improving the electrical properties of PDMS it is apparent 
that high voltage stabilizers has a good potential, without causing side effects like 
conduction or defects. Many of the stabilizers show no changes in the morphology of 
the polymer and are only needed in small quantities to give a good effect. Di-aromatic 
compounds are shown to be highly effective, especially benzil derivatives, ester and 
amine groups are used a lot.  
The main dielectric breakdown mechanism is avalanche breakdown, which is caused 
by ionization of electrons in environments of higher than average electron mobility, due 
to longer mean free path, defects or gas traps. To inhibit this breakdown mechanism 
stabilizers can obtain the energy from electrons by excitation of delocalized aromatic 
π-electrons. The core compounds mainly consist of different diaromatic systems with 
different functional groups. The trap depth and excitation energies of the stabilizers are 
affected by the radicals attached to the systems, para substitutions of electron 
withdrawing groups induce an electropositive polarity in the aromatic system, making it 
more electrophilic. It is important to note that the electron affinity must be higher than 
that of the polymer matrix and that the ionization potential must be low so the electrons 
are being inhibited before they reach an energy capable of degrading the polymer. 
Low distance between the HOMO-LUMO energy gaps is also a big factor as this 
dictates how easily electrons are trapped by the system. Uncertainties in the effect of 
space charge distribution, suggest that several properties influence the DBS together.

Experimental Work 
Experimental Plan 
As the properties of different high voltage stabilizers have been investigated, it is 
apparent that structure and radicals play a big role. Benzils have had an increasingly 
important role as a stabilizer due to the twin aromatic system of delocalized π-electrons. 
The effect of different side groups contribute to this delocalization and affect the 
electrical properties of the material.  
To investigate if PE compatible HVS’s can be applied directly in PDMS films, anthracene, 
benzil and some benzil derivatives have been chosen as the compounds of choice for 
this study. Benzil is easy to work with and some benzil derivatives have been provided 
by Englund et al. Benzil’s compatibility with PDMS through dependence on functional 
side groups and compound size, will be examined by producing polymer films with 
different wt% of the additive. These films will have their breakdown and dielectric 
permittivity measured as an initial attempt of investigating their direct compatibility. As 
previous studies have found no differences in morphology, rheological studies will not 
be performed. Benzil derivatives with tertiary amine functionalities have also been 
provided, but they have not been tested as the amine inhibits the crosslinking.

Results & Discussion 
The different additives have been experimentally tested and the results will now be 
examined to explore their influence on the electrical properties of PDMS. Building on the 
theory, the precursor anthracene and several additives of the benzil derivative have 
been subjected to dielectric tests. With an emphasis on researching the side group’s 
effect on the synergy and incorporation with the PDMS matrix, their effect on the 
electrical properties of breakdown and permittivity has been measured. 
Specifically the ether and ester functional groups along with different length sidechains 
have 
been 
investigated. 
Sidechains 
are 
believed 
to 
primarily 
affect 
the 
stabilizer/polymer matrix interface, while the different functional radicals affect the 
electric properties of the aromatic system.  
Through breakdown measurements it can be visualized how efficient the stabilizers are 
at capturing rogue electrons thus inhibiting avalanche breakdown. This information can 
be used to estimate compatibility of the polymer/stabilizer interfaces along with giving  
insight into other properties such as electron movement, mean free path length and 
charge carrier mobility. 
Film production 
For the first half of the study Elastosil RT 625 was used as the core polymer, the polymer 
was chosen because it has low viscosity making it easy to work with. Because of a 
shortage of Elastosil RT 625, another polymer, Elastosil 3043/50, was used for the 
remaining experiments. Elastosil 3043/50 is a polymer which is more stable, but also a lot 
more viscous, it has a higher dielectric breakdown, but lower permittivity. Due to the 
high viscosity it is a lot more difficult to work with. For thin films it was observed that the 
increased viscosity would sometimes cause small local clumps with increased viscosity, 
which when the film was being dragged would cause differences in thickness. 
Initial attempts at mixing the polymer with solid additive filler proved unsuccessful due 
to agglomeration and inhomogeneous dispersion of particles. Thus toluene was used as 
a solvent, attributing to its compatibility with PDMS and high solubility of benzils. Mixing 
toluene with PDMS did however prove difficult, as high and low viscous materials don’t 
conform particularly well to each other, this was not so much a problem with RT 625 as it 
was with 3043/50. As RT 625 has a rather low viscosity, it was easy to mix with toluene 
after a few initial attempts at finding an appropriate method. With the added toluene 
easily pourable films of consistent viscosity were made and the low viscosity allowed air, 
either from bubbles or evaporating toluene to escape easily. However Elastosil 3043/50 
proved more difficult, as the increased viscosity required an increased amount of 
toluene to be pourable, 14 mL vs 2-4 mL for RT 625. This increase in toluene resulted in an 
37 | P a g e  
 
==PAGE:42==
approximately 50/50 mixture of polymer and toluene, which during speed mixing would 
form a high viscous phase of PDMS and a semi liquid phase of toluene/PDMS, Fig 28.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
The solid phase would have to be dissolved by hand mixing, difficulties in getting a 
consistent viscosity with this method could be observed when pouring the mixture, as 
small clumps of locally increased viscosity would gather at the bottom of the container 
If inefficiently mixed. Furthermore upon speedmixing of the toluene/PDMS blend, heat is 
generated which causes the toluene to start evaporating. In the speedmixer this is not a 
problem as the air is separated by centripetal forces, but once the blend is removed 
from the speedmixer, the evaporating toluene will start creating gas bubbles inside the 
mixture. This was especially a problem for elastosil 3043/50 as the high viscosity caused it 
to need 2-3 times longer mixing times at elevated rpm’s, generating high amounts of 
heat, thus toluene readily evaporated inside the viscous medium, Fig 29.

retracted to anywhere between 500 µm and 300 µm, for both elastomers.   
The films containing 4,4-bismethoxy benzil (Et1) had the additive conform into several 
small crystal structures within the film in the shape of trees which were visible to the 
naked eye, Fig 29. The trees were mostly initiated at larger particles larger than 
approximately 5 µm. 
 
 
Dielectric Breakdown Strength 
The DBS has been tested for polymer films in the range of 40-60 µm, made from either 
elastosil RT 625 or elastosil 3043/50 containing anthracene, benzil or different benzil 
derivatives. As a shortage of elastosil RT 625 occurred, the last three benzil derivatives 
were measured in elastosil 3043/50, Es2, Es12 & Et30. Samples with benzil were made for 
both elastomers to examine if there were any significant differences in how the 
stabilizers behaved in the different PDMS’s. The data has been analysed using weibull 
and DBS/thickness plots, a linear relation between DBS (V/µm) and thickness (µm) has 
been assumed above 90 V/µm and it’s assumed going towards an asymptote at 70-80 
V/µm for increased thickness. It correlates well with the experimental data, Fig 30.

Conclusions 
The aim of this thesis was to explore the capability of improving the electrical properties 
mainly dielectric breakdown strength (DBS) of polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) through 
addition of different high voltage stabilizing additives. Through dielectric breakdown 
and dielectric permittivity test the different additives effect on the electrical properties 
have been explored.  
A method for integrating the stabilizer in the polymer was developed by dissolution in 
toluene and mixing with PDMS. 
It was found that unmodified benzil additives had little to no effect on the DBS while 
added in a weight percent below 0.5. Benzil derivatives substituted with different ether 
groups gave small increases in DBS. Increasing additive size was found to have a 
drastically negative effect on the DBS.  
The permittivity was increased upwards of 10 % by 0.3 wt% anthracene with increasing 
filler content of anthracene, benzil, Es2, Es12 and Et30. The results of the remaining two 
additives, Et1 and Et12 were corrupted so it is unknown they have the same effect.

Future Work 
It is suggested that future research should focus on investigating the compatibility of 
compounds with PDMS, either by functionalizing existing stabilizers with PDMS 
compatible side groups or examining other core compounds.  
It is also recommended to investigate the mechanics of dielectric breakdown in PDMS 
to build an understanding of what kind of stabilizers would be most effective.
