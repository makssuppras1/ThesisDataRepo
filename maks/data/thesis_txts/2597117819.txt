Abstract
Campylobacter jejuni poses an increased risk for public health worldwide, being one of the
most common gastrointestinal pathogenic bacteria in humans in the EU. Phage therapy
could present a solution to this emerging threat as they have eﬃciently reduced Campy-
lobacter levels in chicken. However, there is still a lot of research to be done, specially
because this therapy could have diﬀerent eﬀects on humans and the human microbiome.
For this reason, in vitro human models are being developed to test the eﬃcacy of phage
therapy in humans and their potential eﬀect on commensal bacteria residing the intestinal
tract. Here we used Campylobacter jejuni NCTC12662 strain to study the feasibility of
developing an eﬃcient in vitro phage therapy against this pathogen in humans. A phage
cocktail containing ﬁve diﬀerent bacteriophages (F350, F356, F375, F386 and F389) was
successfully developed using plaque assay analysis. Furthermore, phage cocktail and bac-
terial dosages were tested in three diﬀerent growth conditions by using a simpliﬁed TSI
in vitro model. Results from tests using optimal growth condition (41.6°C) presented a
bacterial reduction of 1.04 log units (90.85% reduction) after 2 hours of cocktail injec-
tion, however at 24 hours CFU/ml counts increased to its initial values. A similar case is
the results from the test using 41.6°C for Campylobacter propagation and 37°C for TSI
incubations. In this case, a bacterial reduction of 0.48 log units (67.2% reduction) and
CFU/ml counts also increased after 24 hours of phage dosage. On the contrary, when
using human physiological temperature (37°C) CFU/ml counts did not increase after 24
hours of phage cocktail dosage. Here, the initial bacterial reduction (comparing time 0 to
time 2), was log 0.52 (70% reduction). However, when comparing time 0 with time 24, a
ﬁnal bacterial reduction of log 0.93 (88.3% reduction) was observed. Resistance experi-
ments using single colonies were performed to evaluate the development phage resistance
during phage therapy. Both colonies presented low PFU/ml values in comparison with the
control (log 7.61 and log 7.51 versus log 9.58). Our results indicate that resistance occur
after 24 hours phage dosage at 37°C and possibly even before at 41.6°C.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Campylobacter jejuni
Risks for public health
Campylobacter are motile, gram negative bacteria ﬁrst described by Theodor Escherich in
1886, which he described as a non-culturable, S-shaped bacteria [1]. Since then, awareness
of Campylobacter role in human diseases has only increased. In 2017 it was reported as the
most common gastrointestinal bacteria pathogen in humans in the EU, accounting for 70%
of all cases in that year [2]. Even though 17 species and 6 subspecies have been assigned
to Campylobacter genus, approximately 90% of the infections are caused by C. jejuni [2,
3]. Infection by C. jejuni in humans is usually presented as an acute gastroenteritis, which
includes symptoms such as inﬂammation, abdominal pain, fever and diarrhea. Electrolyte
replacement and re-hydration are the main treatments used to cure the infection. However,
if the infection is not treated rapidly, campylobacteriosis can lead to bacteremia, at which
point the bacteria can travel and infect other parts of the body. In these cases, patients
are treated with antibiotics.
Campylobacter antimicrobial resistant strains poses a major concern, specially in the devel-
oping countries, where the use of antimicrobial drugs in humans and animals is relatively
unrestricted. Currently, medical interventions can lead to serious complications of resistant
bacteria which do not respond to conventional antibiotics [4]. Speciﬁcally ﬂuoroquinolones,
which have been used in the US and Europe for growth promotion in chickens, are now
commonly used in most developing countries, expanding ﬂuoroquinolones-resistant strains
[5]. For this reason, ﬂuoroquinolones and also macrolide resistance has been increasingly
described.
A surveillance of antimicrobial resistant strains carried out in 2010 by the
World Health Organization, identiﬁed that in the US, 1% of C. jejuni isolates from hu-
mans were resistant to erythromycin, 43% to tetracycline, and 22% to ciproﬂoxacin [6]. In
2019, the World Health Organization published that around 2.4 million people could die
in high income countries between 2015 and 2050 without a sustained eﬀort to contain an-
timicrobial resistance. Therefore, there is an urgent need to ﬁnd alternative and eﬀective
solutions to combat these resistant bacteria.
Campylobacteriosis not only produces acute symptoms but it can also develop chronic
1
==PAGE:14==
diseases, such as arthritis, inﬂammatory bowel disease, coeliac disease or the Guillain-
Barré syndrome, a polio-like form of paralysis that can result in respiratory and severe
neurological dysfunction. An estimated total amount of 9.2 million cases of Campylobacter
infection were diagnosed through 27 European states in 2009 [7]. This high prevalence
of campylobacteriosis is closely linked to very large on-costs, such as medical expenses,
product recalls, legal costs, and others. A study conducted in 2012, presented key data re-
garding economical losses due to food-borne pathogens. Campylobacter related infections
were estimated to cause losses of 1.7 billion dollars annually in the US [8].
Growth and transmission
Most food-borne microbes are considered to be strong and robust organisms due to their
necessity to survive in hostile conditions. Surprisingly, C. jejuni have very speciﬁc growth
requirements and sensitivity to environmental stress. It appears to lack many adaptive
responses that can be associated with resistance to stress in other food-borne microbes [9].
C. jejuni is considered micro-aerophilic, requiring low concentrations of oxygen to survive
(5-10 %). It is also considered a capnophile, therefore requiring high concentrations of
carbon dioxide in its environment (1-10 %) [10].
When it comes to growth temperatures, C. jejuni feels comfortable at 37°C to 41°C,
being 41.5°C its optimum temperature. Above 55°C or below 33°C it cannot survive, the
latter due to the absence of the genes coding for the heat-shock proteins necessary for low
temperature adaptations [11].
Regarding its unique metabolism, Campylobacter lacks some of the common metabolic
pathways necessary to degrade glucose and other carbohydrates. On the other hand, it
obtains energy from amino acids or tricarboxylic acid intermediaries [12]. These intrigu-
ing survival characteristics can aﬀect the ability of bacteria to multiply outside the host
organisms, not only during processing and storage of food but also while working in the
laboratory.
Campylobacter’s preferred environment is the gastrointestinal tract of warm-blood ani-
mals. Poultry in particular is the main reservoir for this bacteria. As it is considered
a commensal for poultry, campylobacteriosis symptoms are not present in these animals.
Broilers are often colonized, especially with C. jejuni specie. It is estimated that 80% of
human campylobacteriosis cases are associated with poultry contamination [13]. Transmis-
sion to humans is mainly associated with the manipulation and consumption of contami-
nated poultry, yet it can also involve direct contact with domestic animals or contaminated
waters.
Host colonization
In order to grow in a restricted ecological niche such as chicken or human gastrointestinal
tracts, C. jejuni has evolved speciﬁc strategies for successful host colonization. Motility
is a necessary property in order to infect the mucous layer of the gastrointestinal tract.
Having a corkscrew-like motility allows this bacterium to remain motile in a highly vis-
cous environment such as intestinal mucus. This gives a competitive advantage against
2
==PAGE:15==
motile rod-shaped bacteria which ends up paralysed in this environment [14]. Another
colonization strategy is its attraction for mucin. Mucins are heavily glycosylated proteins
produced and secreted by epithelial tissues, which makes them principal components of
mucus. Campylobacter aﬃnity to fucose (constituent of mucin) allows fast association
with the intestinal mucus during colonization [9]. When it comes to adhesion to the host
cells, C. jejuni protein CADF binds speciﬁcally to ﬁbronectin located on the epithelial
cells, ending in invasion of epithelial intestinal cells.
Asymptomatic colonization in chickens can be achieved in high numbers, up to 109 colony-
forming units (CFU) per gram of infected intestine. Conversely, infective colonization in
humans takes only 500-800 bacterial cells, which demonstrates completely diﬀerent path-
ways of colonizations and interaction with the human versus avian host [15]. In humans,
the mechanism that controls this invasion is trying to be understood experimentally but
complete comprehension is diﬃcult due to strain diﬀerences. Furthermore, it still has not
been determined how the internalized bacteria is related to parthenogenesis and the exact
mechanisms of internalization (possibly immune evasion or establishment of a protected
reservoir)[15].
Phase variation
Bacteria can adapt to fast changing environments through a process called phase variation.
This process does not involve random mutagenesis, but variation of protein expression of
one or more proteins between individual cells of a clonal population [16]. The cells can
switch between two phenotypes in a heritable and reversible manner, usually referred to
as an all-or-none (on/oﬀ) expressing phase. This type of variation can be distinguished
from genetic noise or classical gene regulation because there is a genetic or epigenetic
mechanism that allows this variability to be heritable, but reversible between generations.
This change in protein expression is a random event. However, this switch frequency can
be inﬂuenced or modulated by external factors, such as the characteristics of the gene,
the bacterial species, and the regulatory mechanism. The probability of phase variation
to happen can be as high as 1 every 10 cells per generation, but more often it is 1 every
103 cells per generation [16].
Campylobacter jejuni presents extensive genetic variation. In its genome, this bacteria
presents hypervariable sequences that consist of homopolymeric tracts. This is deﬁned as
a stretch of DNA containing multiple repetitions of a single nucleotide, which often results
in slipped-strand misparing potentially leading to phase variation [15]. This hipervariable
sequences have been found in regions encoding for proteins involved in the biosynthesis or
modiﬁcation of surface carbohydrate structures, such as the capsule, lipooligosaccharides
(LOS) and ﬂagellum. These three structures have vital roles in C. jejuni host-bacterium
interactions (Figure 1.1).
3
==PAGE:16==
Figure 1.1: The Campylobacter jejuni glycome and surface structures. Extracted from: Young
et al. 2007 [15]. The C. jejuni cell surface displays several structures, including many polysac-
charides, which have vital roles in C. jejuni biology, particularly hostbacterium interactions.
The capsule, a highly variable polysaccharide, is important for virulence, epithelial cell ad-
herence and invasion. The lipooligosaccharide (LOS) is also highly variable and has a role
in serum resistance, epithelial cell adherence and invasion. LOS structures of C. jejuni can
display molecular mimicry of neuronal gangliosides, which is linked to Guillain-Barré syndrome
and Miller-Fisher syndrome. The ﬂagellum is required for colonization, virulence and epithe-
lial cell invasion and also acts as a secretion apparatus for invasion antigens. The ﬂagellin is
modiﬁed by O-linked glycosylation. This modiﬁcation is required for ﬂagellar assembly and
is, therefore, important for motility, virulence and epithelial cell adherence and invasion. The
N-linked-glycosylation system modiﬁes some periplasmic and outermembrane proteins. The
N-linked glycan is also important for colonization and epithelial cell adherence and invasion,
but the role of this glycan in these processes is unclear. [15]
C. jejuni capsule is important for serum resistance, adherence and invasion of epithelial
cells and virulence. The Penner Serotyping scheme is based on diﬀerences in capsule struc-
tures, which indicates that the capsular polysaccharide (CPS) is accessible to the immune
system and that this extensive variation has probably a key role in host immune response
[15].
This serological typing method recognizes a total of 47 C. jejuni serotypes [17],
which shows the high diversity in CPS in this bacteria. CPS composition can change due
to variation in sugar composition (diﬀerent carbohydrate backbones), and modiﬁcations
such as O-methyl, ethanolamine, aminoglycerol and O-methyl phosphoramidate (MeOPN)
[18]. To challenge even more CPS variability, phase variable expression results in more
variations by turning on and oﬀgenes in CPS loci. This mechanism allows structurally
related CPS structures to be expressed by strains with a given serotype complex. For
this, phase variation is likely to be a mechanism that provides C. jejuni predatory phage
escape [17].
All in all, phase variation has been associated with strain virulence and with phage re-
sistance. Phages bind to unique host-speciﬁc structures, allowing them to recognize their
host amongst a mixed bacterial population [19]. In section 1.3, we will talk more about
the relationship between phage and bacteria, and resistance mechanisms.

1.2
Bacteriophages
Phage Therapy
Bacteriophages or phages are bacteria-infecting viruses. They are considered the most
abundant organisms on Earth, with an estimation of 1031 phage particles on the planet
[20]. Like viruses, phages require host cellular machinery to reproduce. Infection starts
with the attachment of the phage by recognising very speciﬁc receptors on the host’s cell
surface. Once binding is completed, phage genetic material is incorporated into the cell,
using its machinery and cellular components and blocking its defense mechanisms. If the
phage follows a lytic cycle, it will replicate its own genetic material to create new phage
particles, ending up in cell lysis. If the phage follows a lysogenic cycle, this will integrate its
genome to the host cell, being transmitted to bacterial daughter cells. However, diﬀerent
stimuli (e.g. UV or chemicals) can cause lysogenic phages to change to lytic cycle. Most
of isolated phages have a linear, double stranded DNA (dsDNA) packed into a tailed
proteinaceous capsid.
There are others that can have non-tailed capsids with dsDNA
genome or non-tailed capsids with single-stranded DNA (ssDNA) or RNA genomes [21].
Since phage discovery, researchers have been using them to understand biological and
molecular processes. A rising interest in new therapeutic strategies as a result of emergent
drug-resistant bacteria has brought the spotlight to phages. Phage therapy is based on the
use of phages to treat pathogenic bacteria. Those that follow a lytic cycle have favourable
characteristics for therapy usage, such as host cell lysis, no genetic transfer between phage
and host cell genome, and high host-receptor speciﬁcity [22]. This new therapy presents
some advantages compared to antibiotic treatments. Since phages are highly speciﬁc they
only replicate inside its target host which avoids microbiome damage. Furthermore, phage
activity occurs inside the host cell, which allows precise action, small doses, and removal
after bacteria is eliminated.
As previously seen, speciﬁcity is one of the main advantages of phages, enabling them
to reach areas where antibiotics cannot have an eﬀect (e.g. central nervous system) [4].
However, this same speciﬁcity can also become a drawback when it comes to targeting
a speciﬁc pathogenic bacteria. Typically, a given phage can only infect a fraction of the
strains making up a single bacterial species [23]. This can be a problem if the speciﬁc
infecting bacteria has not been determined. For this reason, phage cocktails represent a
feasible and eﬃcient solution.
Phage cocktails
Monophage therapy (single phage type) has been demonstrated to be very useful for
elaborating proof of concept during development of phage therapy experimental models,
and also for simplifying treatments and steps.
However, more promising results were
seen when using simultaneously more than one phage type, commonly known as “phage
cocktails” [24]. These cocktails ensure that phage formulations possess a diversity of host
range, widening their scope of action. They oﬀer several advantages compared to the use
of single phages in bacteria treatments: i) it is less likely to develop bacterial resistance
(not like individual treatments), resulting in an eﬃcacious product, ii) they are likely to
5
==PAGE:18==
be more eﬃcient (hitting bacteria with more than one phage type). Even though these
phage cocktails seem to be more eﬀective against speciﬁc bacterial populations, they also
have limitations. These include the fact that they could not inhibit bacterial evolution
to phage resistance, or that they could target diﬀerent bacteria and therefore decreasing
phage-infection productivity [25].
1.3
Campylobacter jejuni bacteriophages
Receptor recognition
Phages can infect their host by a highly speciﬁc recognition of host surface receptors.
Studies have shown that changes in the capsular polysaccharides (CPS) or changes in
motility are involved in phage resistance development in C.jejuni. This indicates that CPS
and ﬂagella could be the main phage receptors in C.jejuni [26]. As seen in section 1.1,
ﬂagella and CPS are important structures for host colonization and virulence. Isolation
of C. jejuni strains have shown to inﬂuence both phage type and the receptor recognized
by C. jejuni phages.
There are several ways to classify C.jejuni phages: morphology, genome size and genetic
constitution.
Morphologically, the majority of Campylobacter-targeting bacteriophages
described so far are lytic and belong to the family Myoviridae, and to a lesser extent,
some are designated to the Siphoviridae family. Lytic Campylobacter phages are divided
in three groups according to their genome size: the ﬁrst one is composed of rare phages
with large genomes (320-425 Kb), while the second and the third groups contain most of
Campylobacter characterized and isolated phages to date [27]. Group II and group III have
small genomes (175-183 Kb and 131-135 Kb respectively) and they both have insensitivity
to cleavage by many restriction endonucleases. While group II phages can infect both C.
jejuni and C. coli, group III are exclusively speciﬁc to C. jejuni and have stronger lytic
activity [27]. Finally, the last classiﬁcation type is based on whole genome sequencing and
protein homology. This classiﬁes, group II and III into the Eucampyvirinae sub-family
of Myoviridae as "Firehammervirus" and " Fletchervirus." [28]. Moreover, there is also a
connection between receptor dependency and grouping of C. jejuni. For example, most of
group III phages recognize CPS receptors, while group II targets mostly ﬂagellar receptors
[26, 28, 29].

Phage resistance in C. jejuni
A large number of the identiﬁed and characterized phages are isolated from C. jejuni
NCTC12662 as indicator strain due to its high sensitivity towards phages. The continuous
usage of this indicator strain might have promoted Eucampyvirinae phages isolation and
therefore narrowing down C. jejuni receptor recognition [19].
It is essential to use a
broad range of strains for the selection and isolation of Campylobacter phages, because
primary phage isolation strain largely determines both phage type and receptors (CPS or
ﬂagella) [28]. For this reason, several studies have isolated and characterized numerous
Campylobacter phages in order to build a representative and diverse phage collection that
could target numerous receptors in C. jejuni [19, 26]. Not only it is important to have a
diverse phage collection for receptor targeting, but also to avoid bacterial-phage resistance.
The latter can be a major drawback in the development of therapeutic phage applications.
There are several mechanisms by which C. jejuni can escape from phage infection. Exter-
nal methods include blocking initial phage binding or preventing phage DNA entry. The
ﬁrst line of defense of a bacteria is to prevent phage binding to the receptor, which ends
up being the most common mechanism of resistance to phages in bacteria [30]. A study
conducted by Sørensen et al. ﬁrst described a mechanism by which Campylobacter je-
juni could commonly prevent or inhibit phage adsorption [19, 26]. This mechanism called
phase variation was also reported by other studies [26, 28, 30] and plays an important
role, as it can modulate phage inactivity in of C. jejuni [28]. These variations in bacterial
structure arise from constant exposure to phages in chicken gut, resulting in continuous
and dynamic phages-host co-evolution [19]. Speciﬁcally some phase variations examples
in Campylobacter would include the mispairing of homopolymeric G (polyG) tracts dur-
ing replication which leads to a truncation of a gene present in the biosynthesis locus of
CPS [26]. Another example, would be the recently discovered phase variable expression
of MeOPN modiﬁcation of the CPS [30].
Other mechanisms, such as internal inhibition mechanisms, would include degradation of
phage genomic DNA by restriction-enzymes or CRISPR-Cas interference preventing phage
propagation. Furthermore, C. jejuni strain diversity has showed to also induce resistance,
as several strains can coexist in one broiler ﬂock at any given time [30].
As previously seen, there are many diﬀerent mechanisms by which Campylobacter jejuni
can escape phage infection. Two diﬀerent strategies exist in order to handle resistance,
such as control in farm environments and modiﬁcation of phage products [28]. This latter
approach encourage mostly using phage cocktails instead of monophage applications. A
study conducted by Hammerl et al. demonstrated that using a combination of group II
and group III phages was eﬀective in reducing bacterial counts in 20-day-old chickens,
reaching a reduction up to 3log10 count levels. On the contrary, when they tested those
phages alone, phage treatment was not eﬀective [31]. By using a multiphage application
the selective pressure in the bacteria is maximized, which means that the bacteria needs to
acquire or develop multiple mutations in order to survive. However, these mutations come
with a ﬁtness cost and could eventually be outgrown by a non-resistant phenotype [28].
As an example, two diﬀerent studies noted that phage resistant C. jejuni strains showed
7
==PAGE:20==
a decrease ability to colonize birds [32, 33]. Another reason why multiphage applications
should be used instead of monophage ones is that they decrease development of cross-
resistance when diﬀerent phages target the same bacterial surface receptor [28]. Eﬃciency
of phage cocktails could be obtained by alternation or by eventually, replacing some phages
from the cocktails for new ones.
1.4
Small scale in vitro gut model systems
Current status
The human gut has been studied intensively throughout the last decades, due to the
increasing evidence of the relationship between human health/disease and the gut mi-
crobiome. Human intervention studies and animal studies provide valuable information
on physiological processes and the interaction between gut microbiota and its host [34].
Despite their physiological relevance they are limited for screening the eﬀects and mech-
anisms of action of tested compounds, such as as antibiotics, prebiotics, probiotics and
drugs, as they lack an easy access to the gut [35]. Aside from being technically diﬃcult,
in vivo models are costly and limited by ethical constraints when potentially harmful sub-
stances are involved (e.g. xenobitics or pathogens) [36]. Furthermore, according to the 3R
framework (replacement, reduction and reﬁnement), the usage of animals in experimental
studies should be reduced and replaced in order to limit their use in experiments [37].
For these reasons, in vitro models are being developed and optimized in order to simulate
and mimic the human gut, oﬀering some advantages compared to in vivo models. For
example, the diverse steps of a fermentation process can be evaluated by using a dynamic
sampling over time in diﬀerent consecutive regions of the human colon. Also, high repro-
ducibility is achieved because the models are standardized. Finally, there are no ethical
constraints regarding pathogens, toxic or radioactive compounds. As a result of these
advantages, in vitro models can be used to develop hypothesis that needs to be proven in
human clinical trials [35].
Overall, even though some aspects of these models are artiﬁcial and do not fully mimic
the whole in vivo process, they represent attractive tools for example in phage therapy
research, because they provide continuous monitoring, standardized sampling conditions
and unlimited screening possibilities [34]. Furthermore, they facilitate the obtention of
large data sets, increasing the statistical signiﬁcance of the obtained data.
In vitro models resembling the colon and small intestine can be classiﬁed in two groups
based on the sample movement: dynamic and static models. The ﬁrst ones, are based on a
single reactor where the pH is usually ﬁxed during the simulation (pH: 1-3 for stomach, pH:
6-7 for small intestine, pH: 5.4-7 for colon [37, 38]. Static models include a limited number
of parameters and do not simulate the complex physicochemical and physiological events
occurring in the digestive tract. As they do not mimic "meals", they do not represent
the diﬀerent stages of digestion with diﬀerent pH, enzymatic conditions and transit times
[36]. An example of a static model already developed is the Oomen, a gastric and small
intestine model [39]. The second type of models, dynamic ones, can reproduce physiological
processes such as progressive acidiﬁcation of gastric content, temperature and pH changes
8
==PAGE:21==
and peristaltic movements. Furthermore, some dynamic models can help to understand
the fate of digested compounds by using diﬀerent compartments as the stomach and the
intestines are divided in diﬀerent compartments that mix, degrade and absorb nutrients.
Some examples of dynamic models are the TIM model, Mainville model, and the HGS
model [40–42]. To date, the TIM is the only model characterized as dynamic and multi-
compartmental ( includes stomach, duodenum, jejunum and ileum). It also integrates key
parameters of human digestion and so far is the system that allows the closest simulation
of in vivo dynamic events occurring within the human gastrointestinal lumen [36].
Even though the presented in vitro gut models are quite promising, most of them operate
in large volumes, not always being an advantage in research where the studied compounds
are only available in small amounts due to its high costs. This can lead to low-throughput
models with few or no technical replicates, lowering down the statistical strength of the
analysis [34]. Consequently, small scale in vitro models are preferred when working with
rare, expensive or low-volume biocompunds. In this project, we will work with two dif-
ferent small scale in vitro gut models, the CoMiniGut and the TSI models, previously
described by Wiese et al., [34] and Cieplak et al., [37] respectively.
Mimicking the human gut
Prior to turn the digestive system into a model, it is fundamental that the human phys-
iologic conditions are understood. Digestion starts in the mouth, where the food will be
converted into the bolus (ball-like mixture of food and saliva) thanks to mechanic and
enzymatic degradation. The salivary glands, the teeth and the tongue act together to
degrade the food. The bolus will then travel through the esophagus to the stomach by
peristalsis. The stomach, a key organ in digestion, is divided in several areas. The upper
part is joined to the esophagus end, and it collects the travelling bolus. Gastric acid con-
tains pepsin and lipase enzymes which degrades proteins and lipids, it also contains HCl
and NaCl which causes a decrease in pH (from 5-6 to 1.5 [36]). Even though this highly
acidic pH could damage the cell wall, this low pH promotes the formation of a protective
layer of mucus secreted from gastric glands. The bolus will move through peristaltic waves
to the distal part of the stomach, where it will continue to degrade. At the end of the
stomach, the pylorus, highly secreted gastrin will degrade the bolus and forming a thick
semi-liquid called chyme.
The chyme will enter the ﬁrst compartment of the small intestine, the duodenum. Here,
the acidic chyme is neutralized with NaHCO3 to give an appropriate pH for enzyme
activities [36]. Furthermore, chyme will be degraded by pancreatic enzymes (proteases,
amylases and lypases) and by the bile salts produced in the liver. The small intestine will
degrade the chyme by a mechanical contractions called segmentation. When chyme is fully
degraded, it will be absorbed into the blood, providing nutrients to the body, speciﬁcally
carbohydrates, lipids, and proteins and some vitamins (e.g. vitamin B12), minerals and
water.
Overall, the average transit time of the small intestine is around 4 hours. When all the
nutrients of the chyme are absorbed, this will turn into a semi-solid material (faeces)
9
==PAGE:22==
which will enter the large intestine or colon.
The large intestine provides the perfect
conditions for gut microbiome, including slow gut transit time, nutrient availability and
favourable pH. Up to 1012 microbes per gram inhabit the colon, a very high number
compared to the bacterial counts in the stomach (103) or the small intestine (104-107)
[38]. The highly acidic conditions in the stomach, and the fast transit movement, bile
salts and pancreatic juice secretion in the small intestine makes these two organs a non-
optimal place for bacterial growth. The gut microbiome main function in humans is the
fermentation of non-digestible dietary residues, synthesis of short-chain fatty acids (e.g.
butyric and acetic acids) , production of vitamins K and B, and absorption of ions and
xenobiotics, protection against pathogens and development of the immune system [43].
Once nutrients have been absorbed by the colon, the remaining material will be excreted
through the rectum as faeces.

TSI model
This model was developed by Cieplak et al. 2018, [37]) as a potential screening platform
for modelling microbial survival in the digestive tract of humans. The TSI in vitro model
simulates the passage through the human small intestine by adjusting the pH, bile salts
concentration, pancreatic enzymes and dialysis to mimic absorption. The design of this
model consists of ﬁve batch reactors simulating ﬁve diﬀerent small intestines, allowing the
test of ﬁve diﬀerent samples at the same time. Time spent at each condition (e.g. injection
of bile salts concentration, dialysis) and pH variability are fundamental in order to mimic
the three main regions of the small intestine: the duodenum, the jejunum and the Ileum.
• Temperature: Controlled by the main unit of the TSI, a composite climate box. The
temperature is set at 37°C by using a water ﬂow warmed up by a heat exchanger
10
==PAGE:23==
inside the box. The temperature is monitored constantly and recorded.
• Reactors: The climate box contains ﬁve stirred-fused quartz glass reactors, each one
representing an individual passage through the intestinal tract. The working volume
of the reactors is 12 ml each. The reactors are separately closed bin PVC chambers
and are ﬂushed continuously with nitrogen to provide anaerobic conditions. Alter-
natively, Anaerogen compact sachets are also used to achieve anaerobic conditions.
These chambers are placed on a ﬁve-unit magnetic stirrer plates to equally mix the
samples. Reactors are closed with septa lids containing a pH probe inlet and nee-
dle inlets for the addition of enzymes and bile salts, as well as sampling, input and
output for the dialysis chamber. The bile is absorbed during dialysis using dialysis
cassettes. These cassettes are maintained at anaerobic conditions and they are ﬁlled
with dialysis ﬂuid. A multichannel peristaltic pump ensures constant ﬂow of the
chyme between reactor and dialysis cassettes. The dialysis has a cut-oﬀof 10 KDa
nutrients.
• pH: The pH is measured at each module every 20 seconds. It is controlled with 0.1 M
NaOH according to the pre-programmed experimental set-up using syringe pumps,
which are automatically controlled using an in-house made scripts with Matlab soft-
ware.

CoMiniGut is an in vitro human colon model with a working volume of 5ml mimicking an
average colon transit time of 24 h. It was developed by Wiese et al., 2018 [34] with the
objective of facilitating investigation of rare and expensive compounds with an increased
throughput. CoMiniGut is based on a 5-plex-CoMiniGuts that can be aligned and consti-
tute, an eﬃcient screening platform to investigate the gut microbiota and the interactions
between phages and bacterial microorganisms. This model simulates the passage through

the colon by using a continuous increment on the pH through a multistage setup. This de-
sign facilitates the simulation of the colon properties using three chemostats that simulate
the proximal, transverse and distal colon regions.
• Temperature: The climate box is kept at 37°C by using a water ﬂow connected to a
heat exchanger inside the box. An external temperature probe is placed in the box
for feedback control. A ventilation system secures the temperature distribution.
• Reactors: The climate box contains ﬁve-stirred fused quartz glass reactors, with a
working volume of 5 ml each. Each reactor is positioned in a 150ml polymethyl-
methacrylat (PMMA) compartment, placed on a magnetic stirrer. Anaerobic condi-

conditions. PMMA compartments are composed of a PMMA ring and an exchange-

can be penetrable by needles for pH control, sampling and substrate addition.

Case Study
Small scale in vitro gut models are a great platform to study phage therapy, as they
operate in small amounts and can provide high-throughput results.
However, there is
currently only one study, conducted by Cieplak et al. that developed and eﬃcient phage
therapy test using the TSI model [44]. This study will serve as a starting point for the
proposed theoretical predictions and conducted experiments in this project, and for this,
the methodology and results of this study will be thoroughly examined.
State of the art
Nowadays antibiotics are widely used to treat bacterial pathogens, both in humans and
animals, enabling the treatment of life-threatening infections. However, their extensive
use has led to an increasing population of antibiotic-resistant bacteria, threatening the
healthcare system.
Moreover, most of the antibiotics used are broad spectrum, which
means that they cannot only kill pathogenic bacteria, but also commensal bacteria found
in the human gut. Loss of beneﬁcial bacteria can cause an increased presence of resistant
bacteria through selective pressure and may facilitate horizontal transfer of resistance
genes [45]. Adherent-Invasive E. coli in the ileum has been associated with Ileal Crohn’s
disease (ICD) and it has been found to manifest multidrug resistance [46]. Administra-
tion of phages targeting E. coli might be a potential therapeutic alternative to antibiotic
treatment, improving ICD management.
In this context, Cieplak et al. developed an eﬃcient phage therapy against E. coli in gut
simulated conditions using the TSI model [44]. With the objective of analysing the per-
12
==PAGE:25==
sistence and viability of bacteriophages in the human gastrointestinal tract, they tested a
phage cocktail targeting E. coli in the TSI model. Furthermore, they also compared the
impact of the cocktail vs. a widely used broad-spectrum antibiotic, ciproﬂoxacin on the
E. coli levels and commensal bacteria in the small intestine system.
Mimicking the intestinal microbiota

Preparation of bacteriophage cocktail
In this study, Cieplak et al. formulated a phage cocktail Ec17B153DK1 which speciﬁcally
targeted E. coli DSM 1058 strain, representative from the ileal consortium. The phage
cocktail was design to lyse this strain in the Spot test assay, and it was composed by 3
phages with potent lytic activity. All phages were individually tested to lyse E. coli DSM
1058, as well as collectively tested against 607 E. coli isolates. From the later, the phage
cocktail was able to lyse 408 isolates (67%). Each bacteriophage was propagated in their
respective E. coli host strains at 37°C with a Multiplicity of Infections (MOI) ranging from
2×10-4 to 1×10-1. After propagation, each phage was ﬁltered through a 0.2-micron ﬁlter
and concentrated/buﬀer exchanged in a 0.85% saline solution. To prepare the cocktail,
the three monophages were mixed in approximately equal concentrations.

In this study, the small intestine was mimicked by using the TSI model, already described
in section 1.4. For each of the ﬁve reactors, parameters such as pH, temperature, and bile

(10.81 log PFU/ml) and controls were added to the model. Because the small intestine is
divided in three compartments, the TSI conditions had to be adapted to simulate each of
them. Passage through duodenum was set to 2 hours, with an pH increase from 6.5 to 6.8

tine conditions
With the objective of testing the persistence of the phage cocktail during small intestinal
transit and its eﬃcacy against E. coli DSM 1058 strain, the phage cocktail was inoculated
(0.5 ml; 10.81 log PFU/ml) in four reactors. The last reactor was used for the control

and after the duodenum, and after the ileum stage. Plaques were counted and measured
in Plaque forming units (PFU) in the E. coli host strain grown on selective agar media
(Violet Red Bile Agar) to determine the persistence of phage cocktail.
The eﬃciency
of phage cocktail was studied by comparing bacteria counts (CFU) at the end and be-
ginning of the ileal stage in phage treated samples and control samples. The procedure
to simulate small intestine digestion was performed as described in Cieplak et al. 2018[44].
Relevant ﬁndings
This study reveals important insights into phage therapy, speciﬁcally in in vitro gut phage
therapy such as:
• Phage stability is not aﬀected through the simulated small intestinal passage. This
ﬁnding suggests that oral administration of phage cocktail could be possible without
aﬀecting the cocktail performance. However, phage viability could be reduced in vivo
conditions due complex factors not found in the model (e.g. peristaltic movement,
diet or complex microbiota).
• Phage cocktail administration had no detectable impact on the six commensal bac-
terial species used in the model (Table 1.2). On the contrary, ciproﬂoxacin adminis-
tration resulted in a 1 log-reduction of the bacterial consortia. These results suggest
that phage cocktails could be a potential therapy against E. coli with an eﬀectiveness

Problem deﬁnition and aims of the project
Campylobacter is the leading cause of food-borne bacterial diarrhea worldwide. For more
than a decade, Campylobacter has remained the most common zoonosis in EU accounting
for 70% of all reported cases in 2017 despite implementation of several strategies [2].
Furthermore, presence of antibiotic resistant Campylobacter in the developed countries
poses an increased risk for public health worldwide.
Campylobacter jejuni is considered a commensal of the gastrointestinal tract in chicken.
However, once it colonizes the human gastrointestinal tract through contaminated meat
consumption, campylobacteriosis infection rapidly develops.
By reducing C. jejuni in
chicken, the number of human campylobacteriosis could also decrease substantially [27].
For this reason, various biosecurity procedures have been used in order to reduce the
numbers of C. jejuni through the food chain, specially by applying measures after chicken
slaughter. However, some of these measures are expensive and not always eﬃcient [48].
In humans, Campylobacter is able to colonize the colon and the small intestine. To study
its pathogenicity, current research relies on cell cultures and animal models. However, cell
cultures are often phenotypically diverse and cannot be used to study interaction between
several microorganisms, while animal models are still physiologically diﬀerent from human
models, expensive and with ethical constraints. These points to the urgent need of ﬁnding
new solutions to combat this bacterium.
Campylobacter-targeting bacteriophages could present a solution to this problem, as they
have been isolated since 1985 for the purpose of combating this C. jejuni in poultry
and its products [49]. Campylobacter phages have been administered in vitro (chicken
skin) and in vivo (broilers) [32, 50–52], resulting not only in eﬃciently reducing C. jejuni
colonization but also showing minimal impact on the normal gut microbiome of chickens
[53]. Furthermore, the possibility of using phage cocktails by testing experimentally two
and four phages combinations has also been explored [31, 54].
Even though C. jejuni phages have been experimentally tested and studied in vivo, it still
has not been tested if they have the same impact on humans and human microbiome. It
is therefore extremely important to test it before phages can be used as therapeutics in
humans.
Small scale in vitro gut model systems allow studies of microbial communities under con-
dition closer to be in vivo in fast and low maintenance setup with less sample material
needed. The TSI (The smallest intestine) and CoMiniGut (Copenhagen MiniGut) models
[34, 37] can simulate microbial fermentation in human physiological conditions. However,
gut model systems that allow Campylobacter growth do not exist because of the highly
speciﬁc growth requirements of this bacterium. In this project we will aim at optimizing
simulated in vitro gut model systems (TSI and CoMiniGut) to test the eﬃcacy of a phage
cocktail against phage sensitive C. jejuni NCTC12662 strain. This model has the objec-
tive of evaluating the outcome of using phages as an intervention against Campylobacter
infection in humans, as well as to provide insights on small scale in vitro models to recreate
15
==PAGE:28==
human infections.
The speciﬁc objectives of this project will be:
• To successfully develop a phage cocktail capable of reducing Campylobacter jejuni
NCTC12662 levels.
• Test and establish C. jejuni NCTC12662 survival and colonization in the TSI (the
smallest intestine) and CoMiniGut (small volume colon) models.
• Test phage survival (cocktail of phages F350, F356, F375, F386 and F389) in the
TSI and CoMiniGut models.
• Test the eﬃcacy of phage therapy against C. jejuni in the TSI and CoMiniGut
models.

Bacteriophage Propagation and puriﬁcation
Propagation and puriﬁcation of phages by spot assay was conducted as described in [55]
(see complete protocol in Appendix A.2). Propagating strains were grown overnight onto
Blood agar Base II (Oxoid) plates under microaerophilic conditions at 41.6°C and har-
vested in BHI supplemented with 1 mM CaCl2 and 10 mM MgSO4 (CBHI). Bacterial
suspensions were adjusted to an optical density of (OD600) of 0.350 and then incubated
4 h at 41.6 °C under microaerophilic conditions (6% O2, 6% CO2 and 88% N2H2). After
bacterial lawns were prepared, 400 µl of bacteria was mixed with 200 µl of bacteriophage
(SM-buﬀer in controls) and incubated for 15 min at 37°C to allow phage adsorption.
These suspensions were then mixed with 5 ml of previously warmed NZCYM overlay agar
(NZCYM broth with 0.6% agar) and then poured onto NZCYM 1.2% agar plates. After
overnight incubation at 41.6°C, plates with lysis were ﬂooded with 5 ml sterile SM buﬀer
and placed at 4°C with gentle shaking overnight to elute phages into the buﬀer. Phage
suspensions were harvested and ﬁltered through a sterile 0.2 µm membrane ﬁlter. Follow-
ing, phage stocks were stored at 4°C and titrated.
Bacteriophage Titration
Titration of phages by spot assay was conducted as described in [55]. Bacterial lawns
were prepared as described above and 0.5 ml was added to 5 ml of NZCYM overlay agar
previously tempered to 45°C. The overlay agar then was poured on a previously prepared
and dried NZCYM basal agar plate (1.2% agar, 10 µg/ml vancomycin) and dried for ex-
actly 45 min in a ﬂow hood. After plates were dried, phage solutions were spotted in
their respective propagating strain plate. Three times 10 µl of 100 - 107 phage dilutions
in SM buﬀer were spotted manually in each plate. Plates were dried and incubated under
microaerophilic conditions at 41.6°C or 37°C (depending on the conditions used) for 20-24
h. After this, the number of plaques formed per milliliter of undiluted phage suspensions
(PFU/ml) was calculated and pictures of the plates were taken.

• Eﬃciency of plating (EOP): EOP was used to assess the productive infection. In
our study, it was used to determine the susceptibility of C. jejuni to the phage
cocktail.
Conceptually, the EOP is the relative number of plaques that a phage
stock is capable of producing. The EOP was calculated by dividing the PFU/ml of
the test strain by the PFU/ml of the control strain. The average EOP value for the
phage-bacterium combination was classiﬁed as previously done by Viazis et al. 2011
([59]) and Mirzaei et al.2015 ([60]). The classiﬁcation was made as following: "High
Eﬃciency" when the ratio was 0.5 or more; "Medium Eﬃciency" when the ratio was
bigger than 0.1 but smaller than 0.5; "Low Eﬃciency" when the ratio was bigger
than 0.001 and smaller than 0.1; "Ineﬃcient" when the EOP was under 0.001.
• Multiplicity of infection (MOI): The MOI served as a measurement for knowing the
ratio of phages to bacteria used. This measure was estimated for the TSI modelling
results, therefore the data used was from the TSI experiments. The MOI was cal-
culated by dividing the total PFU by the total CFU. The total PFU was obtained
by multiplying the PFU/ml by the volume of added phage and then by the total
working volume. On the other hand, the total CFU was obtained by multiplying the
CFU/ml by the volume of bacteria used and multiplying by the total working volume.
Statistical analysis
In order to evaluate the statistical diﬀerence between TSI modelling samples (NCTC12662
+ phage cocktail, NCTC12662 control tube and Phage cocktail control tube) a T-test
was performed using Microsoft Excel 2010. T-test is a parametric test widely used to
determine statistical diﬀerences between groups of data and in this study, T-test was used
to determine if the means of two sets of data are signiﬁcantly diﬀerent from each other. One
of the assumptions of this test is that the dependent variable follows a normal distribution.
To know if the data was normally distributed, skewness and kurtosis parameters were
analysed (range of values: -2 to +2) and assumed normal distribution depending on them.
Furthermore, depending on the values of the variances of the groups, the T-test was
assumed with equal or diﬀerent variances. Finally, the null hypothesis was rejected when
p-value < 0.05 and then accepting the alternative hypothesis of statistical signiﬁcance.
Due to insuﬃcient data size and not normally distributed data, the third condition of the
TSI modelling was analysed using the non-parametric test Mann-Whitney U test together
with the Lavene Test for Equality of Variances. These two tests were performed using
the SPSS Statistics software from IBM. Mann-Whitney U test was selected as it is the
non-parametric equivalent of the 2-sample T-test. This test has the assumption that the
variances of the samples are equal, therefore before using it the Lavene Test was performed
to check if the data meet this requirement. Similarly to the T-test, when the p-value
obtained from the Mann-Whitney U test was p < 0.05 the null hypothesis was rejected,
accepting then the alternative hypothesis of statistical signiﬁcance. For both tests, the

to quantify the uncertainty in the estimate of the mean [61].

3.1
Lytic activity of the bacteriophage cocktail
As previously seen, phage cocktails allow the simultaneous application of diﬀerent bacte-
riophages, widening their range of action by targeting diﬀerent receptors. This can beneﬁt
phage therapy by being more eﬀective than monophage treatments. For this reason, in
this project ﬁve diﬀerent phages were used (F350, F356, F375, F386 and F389). Three of
them (F350, F356 and F375) depend on CPS for infection while F386 and F389 depend on
motility for infection. These phages were obtained from the PhageBio group laboratory
collection and all of them proved to successfully infect NCTC12662. This strain is a widely
used indicator strain due to its high sensitivity towards phages. Propagating strains from
each individual phage are presented in (Table 3.1). Brieﬂy, F350 is propagated from
NCTC12568, both F356 and F375 are propagated from NCTC12662, and both F386 and
F389 are propagated from RM1221.
Determination of the phage cocktail titer was used for determining the quantity of in-
fectious phages in Campylobacter NCTC12662. When a determined volume of infective
phages is poured over a lawn of susceptible bacterial cells, the bacteriophage will attach
to the cell, use its machinery to replicate inside and kill it during lytic release. In the
best case, each plaque will be formed by a single phage (infectious unit) [62]. The num-
ber of plaques that will develop can be used to calculate the number of bacteriophages
(plaque-forming units: PFU) in our sample. The higher the PFU/ml counts, the more
viable phage particles are capable of infecting susceptible host cells.
In order to develop an eﬃcient cocktail, ﬁve diﬀerent phages were propagated in their
respective propagating strains until high titers were achieved and new stocks were created
(Table 3.1). The next step was to mix in equal proportions all phages to prepare the
phage cocktail and test it with Campylobacter jejuni NCTC12662 strain. Individual phages
were also spotted at NCTC12662 but this data was lost due to the Covid-19 lock down
and because of limited time, we focused on obtaining results from the phage cocktail.
Nonetheless, Gencay et al. demonstrated that all phages are able to successfully infect
NCTC12662 [30].
As seen in Table 3.1, PFU/ml values resulting from NCTC12662
infection are as high as the PFU/ml values from their propagation hosts, indicating that

the fact that this strain is both propagating and tested strain. Finally, our phage cocktail

3.2
TSI modelling results
The TSI model developed by Cieplak et al.
represents a great screening platform for
modelling microbial survival in the digestive tract of humans. This model can simulate
both bacteriophage and bacterial passage through the human small intestine by adjusting
the pH, bile salts, pancreatic enzymes and dialysis to mimic adsorption.
This model,
previously used in E. coli, allowed the study of the eﬀect of a bacteriophage cocktail
against its target bacteria and also, its eﬀect to the commensal microbiota of the intestine.
This could be an excellent platform to study the eﬀect of our phage cocktail against
26
==PAGE:39==
campylobacter jejuni. However, prior to its use, it was important to determine several
parameters and conditions to be applied in this in vitro system as our objective was to
adapt the TSI to Campylobacter model of infection.
So, in order to evaluate if the phage cocktail and bacterial dosages selected were appro-
priate for making a reduction in Campylobacter growth, as well as for evaluating how well
Campylobacter was adapting to human physiological temperature, a more basic version of
the TSI model was recreated in the laboratory (see Materials and Methods for details).
Brieﬂy, centrifuge tubes were used to maintain liquid cultures and recreate the bioreactors
used in the TSI model. After the bacterial lawns were prepared and adjusted to the de-
sired concentrations, both bacteria and phages were added to the centrifuge tubes and left
for an incubation period of 24 hours. Sampling times were at 0, 2 and 24 hours. Phage
cocktail dosage was based on the amount of phage cocktail used by Cieplak et al. [44]
and Wagenaar et al. [52]. Also, concentration of Campylobacter used was based on the
numbers of bacteria required to result in positive infection in humans [58]. Regarding on
Campylobacter growth conditions, this was grown at microaerophilic conditions and two
diﬀerent temperatures were tested.
In total, three diﬀerent temperature conditions were tested. Figure 2.1 illustrates the
temperatures used for each condition. Summarily, Condition I used Campylobacter opti-
mal temperature (41.6°C) for both propagation and the incubation periods. In Condition
II, 41.6°C was used for propagation and then, 37°C for incubation periods. Finally, Con-
dition III used 37°C for both propagation and incubation.
In Condition I, phages survive and stay active as they are able to infect C. jejuni to the
same extent from 0 to 24 hours (Figure 3.2). Even though phages seem to survive for
24 hours, clear diﬀerences between the control and the sample can be observed when it
comes to lytic activity (Figure B.4).
In the control, bacterial counts steadily increase over time. Despite starting with a smaller
amount of present bacteria compared to the sample containing the phage cocktail (time 0),
the control CFU/ml counts rapidly increases overcoming the sample that contains the bac-
teriophage cocktail. It has to be noted, that this initial diﬀerence is not quite understood,
since theoretically there should be the same amount of bacteria in both samples (phage
cocktail was added after the 0 hour measurement). Further analysing the lytic activity, the
phage cocktail had the ability to signiﬁcantly reduce the levels of C. jejuni NCTC12662
after 2 hours of incubation, speciﬁcally reducing them by 1.04 log units (90.85% reduction)
as seen in Figure B.4. However, after 24 hours of phage cocktail induction, NCTC12662
strain seems to recover, as its levels increase to the point that it overcomes the initial
bacterial numbers (0 hours). This result suggests that NCTC12662 may be able to oﬀer
resistance to the phage cocktail before reaching 24 hours post-infection.
As mentioned before, the second set of experiments was conducted using 41.6°C for the
propagation of NCTC12662 strain. After this, the temperature was set to 37°C for the
incubation periods. Condition II was designed to see how well Campylobacter jejuni would
respond to growing at human physiological conditions.

Resistance experiments
Development of phage resistance can be promoted when bacteria is constantly exposed
to phages. In Campylobacter, phage resistance has been identiﬁed as one of the possible
mechanisms by which this bacteria escapes phage predation [26]. Speciﬁcally, Campylobac-
ter jejuni NCTC12662 has been demonstrated to develop resistance due to phase variable
expression of MeOPN modiﬁcation of the CPS. Knowing this, and also by analysing the
results obtained from the TSI experiments, we decided to perform a series of experiments
to test if phage resistance could help us explain Campylobacter behaviour during the TSI
modeling test.

Chapter 4
Discussion
Bacteriophage dosage is relevant to Campylobacter reduction levels
In order to develop an eﬃcient phage therapy, phage cocktail dosages need to be adjusted
to each infection model. This dosage should contain enough phage numbers to signiﬁcantly
reduce bacterial counts. In this study, dosages ranging from log107.8 to log108.2 PFU/ml
were applied initially, maintaining more or less those levels during the whole experimen-
tation period. Even though phages seem to be active for 24 hours, phage replication does
not seem to occur due to the lack of an increase in phage numbers. Bacteria encountering
high phage densities can create a lot of debris to which phages adhere in competition with
live bacterial cell, disabling phages to replicate [63]. However, this would be the case if a
drop in phage titer was visible which is not the case.
While studying phage-host dynamics, Kittler et al. highlighted the importance of the
inoculum size and time in phage therapy, especially its eﬀect on the type of bacterial
reduction this would provoke [64]. Reduction of bacterial numbers can take place by two
diﬀerent type of infections: primary and secondary infections. The ﬁrst one (also referred
to as passive inundation or passive phage therapy) happens when reduction of bacteria is
caused by the initial phage dose, and it does not require phage replication (it does need
high density of phages initially: MOI >10) The inoculated phage are numerous enough
then the ﬁrst round of primary infection and lysis can by itself remove signiﬁcant numbers
of bacteria [38]. The latter takes place with a lower initial phage dose but it requires
phage self-replication, and results in increase of phage numbers. Replication of phages
is possible at a determined bacterial density, referred to as proliferation threshold and
greatly inﬂuences phage adsorption [65]. With all of this in mind, we hypothesise that the
dosage used during the TSI modelling were high enough to result in a non-reproductive
state of our phages, leading to a passive reduction of NCTC12662 strain. This would cause
an initial cell death due to high phage density but as phages would not be replicating, its
numbers would not increase through time. This could explain our steady phage number
results.
C. jejuni NCTC12662 increase its numbers at 41.6°C but not at 37°C
In both Condition I and II, Campylobacter colony counts reduction was achieved by 90.85%
and 70% respectively. Even though we achieved 1-log reduction in the ﬁrst case, we could
35
==PAGE:48==
have expected a higher reduction if we take into account that ﬁve phages were used for
the cocktail. Furthermore, in both cases Campylobacter counts increased surpassing its
initial numbers by the end of the experimental time (24 hours).
This rise in Campylobacter numbers after reduction has been registered in other studies
[32, 52] and it has been hypothesised to be the result of replication of new-non suscep-
tible strains. After application of phages, susceptible strains might disappear and non-
susceptible strains can easily grow and lead to a rise in Campylobacter counts subsequent
to an initial reduction [64]. This hypothesis could explain the rise in Campylobacter counts
at in-vivo systems in which bacterial populations are genetically diverse. However, in this
study only one bacterial strain was used within a controlled in-vitro system where there
is not a co-existence of strains with diﬀerent susceptibilities as in in-vivo systems.
On the other hand, resistance evolutionary studies have shown that simultaneous phage
exposure imposes a great constraint on bacterial resistance mechanisms, leading to few
mutational routes that allow bacteria to survive in the presence of multiple phages [66].
Wright et al.
were unable to identify resistance mutations in some hosts exposed to
simultaneous phage strains and they suggested that bacterial phenotypic responses to
stressors might be the cause. These stressors could be environmental changes, such as
temperature changes which aﬀect the rigidity of host cell membrane or nutrient variability
that can reduce expression of phage receptors [66]. For instance, it has been noted that
Campylobacter jejuni 1118 substrate utilization is temperature dependent, speciﬁcally
aminoacid use is increased when growing at 42°C compared to 37°C [67].
These environmental stressors could also aﬀect receptor expression levels in bacteria, which
could potentially limit phage adsorption enabling survival of the host under simultaneous
application of phages without leaving a genetic trace. Such is the case of E. coli quorum
sensing defence mechanism against lambda phages [68]. A study conducted by Sørensen et
al. ﬁrst described a mechanism by which Campylobacter jejuni could commonly prevent
or inhibit phage adsorption [19, 26]. This mechanism called phase variation, was further
studied by Gencay et al. with Campylobacter strain NCTC12662. The development of
phage resistance was proposed to be due to phase variable expression of MeOPN modifying
CPS proﬁle of the bacteria [30].
Phase variation precise biological signiﬁcance remains speculative but it is hypothesized
that it could primarily serve as a strategy to provide adaptive responses to more than one
particular environment [69] , or as mention before, it could also serve as a defence mech-
anism against bacteriophage adsorption. Whatever the reason is, phase variation mecha-
nisms in some bacteria do seem to depend on bacterial environment. A well-characterized
example of phase variation by genomic rearrangement which depends on if the environment
is type 1 ﬁmbriae from E. coli. This type of phase variation is mediated by sitespeciﬁc
DNA inversions and depending on the promoter position (located within the invertible
region), transcription will occur or not (ON-OFF system). This inversion is favoured to
ON when E. coli is growing at a temperature of 37°C [69]. Taking into account all of this
information, it could be that the rise of NCTC12662 strain after a reduction in CFU/ml
counts is due to phase resistance.
36
==PAGE:49==
Environmental changes may aﬀect C. jejuni capacity to oﬀer phage resistance
Results from TSI modelling experiments provided useful information regarding Campy-
lobacter capacity to grow in diﬀerent conditions. During Condition III, bacteria was prop-
agated and incubated at 37°C for 48 hours, double the amount of time need to achieve
dense growth compared to the cases in which Campylobacter was grown at 41.6°C (Con-
dition I). As previously mentioned, Campylobacter aminoacid utilization is temperature
dependant, which explains the diﬀerence in bacterial growth between temperatures.
TSI modelling results not only illustrated diﬀerences in growth rates between conditions,
but also diﬀerences in resistance trends. CFU/ml counts from experiments conducted at
41.6°C displayed an initial drop at 2 hours after phage injection, and a latter increase
surpassing the initial values at 24 hours. On the other hand, CFU/ml counts from the
experiment performed at 37°C did not increase, but rather decreased during time. Diﬀer-
ences in these results lead us to further investigate the possible causes of this change in
bacterial counts. For this, resistance experiment were conducted using individual colonies
from TSI modelling at 37°C (Table 3.3). Plaque counts (PFU/ml) were obtained from
individual colonies picked at 0, 2 and 24 hours after phage injection. Interestingly, colonies
that resulted from 24 hours after phage cocktail dosage presented lower PFU/ml counts
than the rest, indicating phage resistance. To assist these results we also calculated ef-
ﬁciencies of plating (EOP) to determine productive infections. EOP values are tricky to
discuss as they could explain multiple situations: from a reduced phage viability to de-
layed plaque initiation or even a reduced infection "vigor" [62]. However, EOP values from
colonies at 24 hours determine low eﬃcient infection from these samples.
Taking all this information into account, it is therefore possible that Campylobacter phage
resistance was starting to develop at 24 hours, when grown at 37°C. This phage resistance
could result in Campylobacter eventually being more resistant and increasing its CFU/ml
numbers overcoming CFU/ml initial counts, as happened in Condition I and II. It is also
important to notice that if phage resistance occurred, this appeared diﬀerently depending
on the temperature used. Our results suggest that phage resistance occurred before 24
hours post-phage dosage at 41.6°C, and after 24 hours post-phage dosage at 37°C. To
support this, another round of TSI modelling experiments should be performed with longer
sampling times, and using both temperatures to be able to compare PFU/ml, CFU/ml
counts and eﬃciencies of plating from both experiments.
Because temperature does not aﬀect motility [69], phages dependant on CPS seem to
produce a more signiﬁcant eﬀect in infecting NCTC12662 strain than motility-dependant
phages. Campylobacter jejuni NCTC12662 encodes four genes at the CPS locus that can
undergo phase variation. One of these genes includes a MeOPN-transferase that has been
demonstrated to be a cause for phage resistance in Campylobacter jejuni NCTC12662[30].
Furthermore, our cocktail consists of ﬁve bacteriophages, three of them (F350, F356 and
F375 ) depending on CPS for infection but not completely on the previous mentioned
MeOPN [30]. It is therefore possible that NCTC12662 undergo phase variation during the
TSI modelling experiment, obtaining phage resistance and therefore growing over its initial
numbers. It has to be noted the diﬀerence between Condition I and II versus Condition
37
==PAGE:50==
III. While in the ﬁrst two it can be clearly seen the increase in NCTC12662 strain numbers,
this is not the case in the latter condition, where NCTC12662 numbers keep decreasing
over time. Having used the same dosage of phage cocktail and NCTC12662 concentrations
in all conditions, the only variable parameter between them is temperature.
This might suggest that Campylobacter has more diﬃculties to oﬀer resistance to phages
when is not growing in its optimal environment. A recent paper published by Taylor et
al. demonstrated that the ﬁrst step in the biosynthesis of the MeOPN modiﬁcation to the
CPS of C. jejuni is catalyzed by the gene Cj1418, where ATP is utilized to phosphorylate
the amide nitrogen of L-glutamine [70].
We could suggest that Campylobacter metabolic state could be linked to its capacity to
escape phage predation could be plausible. When present at 37°C, Campylobacter growth
would be slowed down due to a less eﬃcient aminoacid utilization (lowered metabolic
state). This change in its phenotypic state could lead to a change in its capacity to oﬀer
resistance to phages to some unknown extent. This hypothesis has not been discussed in
the literature that we are aware of. Therefore, more experiments should be performed to
either conﬁrm it or deny it.
Further experiments and model perspectives
Phage and bacterial dosages seem to substantially aﬀect phage treatments outcomes. It
has been studied that the probability of phage adsorption is highly inﬂuenced by the
density of both bacteria and phages. If bacterial density reaches a certain level known
as proliferation threshold, phage replication becomes possible [64]. On the other hand,
phage density will inﬂuence phage capacity to replicate. As mentioned, phage ability to
self-replicate will inﬂuence the type of infection that will develop. Passive infection is
based on the phages that are present on the inoculum, whereas active infection is based
on the phages released by lysis of already infected cells.
development of one type of
infection will inﬂuence which kind of bacterial reduction will occur.
Active reduction
has been proposed as an appropriate strategy to use for phage therapy as it reduces the
probability of acquired host resistance over time, as well being the most probable scenario
of Campylobacter colonization in their hosts [32]. As we can see, ﬁnding the equilibrium
between phages and bacterial densities may be highly relevant to develop an eﬃcient phage
therapy strategy. Further experiments would include testing diﬀerent sets of bacteria and
phage concentrations to test optimal combinations for phage therapy conditions.
CPS components of NCTC12662 strain play an important role on phase resistance. In
this aspect, more research should be done to understand better Campylobacter jejuni
phages CPS components and its molecular function in phage infection. This is vital for
the development of long-term eﬃcient phage therapies. For this, TSI modelling and resis-
tance experiments should be repeated using the individual phages to comprehend phage
interaction and cocktail behaviour against NCTC12662 strain. Furthermore, phenotypic
variation should be also considered and experimentally tested to understand host-phage
dynamics taking into account the environment in which they reside. For example, Bull
et al. suggested through mathematical models, some mechanisms by which phenotypic
resistance could occur [71]. Three possible mechanisms were suggested as mechanisms of
38
==PAGE:51==
penothypic resistance: Induced, Intrinsic and Dynamic. The ﬁrst is based on the fact
that phage adsorption could be due to a change in uninfected bacterial gene expression
after exposure to rests of phage-lysated bacteria in the environment. The second mech-
anism, explains that the reduced adsorption is due to a physiological or gene expression
states that exists in some bacteria present in a population prior to phage introduction.
This would be a consequence of normal environmental variation or high mutation rates.
The last mechanism, explains low adsorption as a result of the degradation or blocking
of bacterial receptors by phage proteins released by lysed cells [71]. Even though these
mechanisms could explain phenotypic variation, they are still merely speculative as not
much is known about it.
However is the case, it is already clear that experimental data needs to be produced for
each strain, phage cocktail and condition applied as results can be highly variable.
For these reasons, it is proposed to continue the research on the aforementioned issues
illustrated in Figure 4.1 before the usage of the human gut in vitro models. Even though
it was not possible to succeed in using small scale in vitro models for Campylobacter
phage therapy, data obtained during this project help us realize the high complexity of
it. This was the ﬁrst attempt to use these models on Campylobacter jejuni and a lot of
essential data had to be generated prior to their use. Even with it, we can only speculate
and suggest diﬀerent outcomes which keeps the whole process in a turbid light. Data
generation and condition testing are going to be fundamental to understand all these
underlying mechanisms. Nevertheless, once more results are available, it is highly advisable
to continue with this research using the in vitro human gut models. Even though data
from these models are not easily transferred to in vivo conditions, they could help us
understand Campylobacter behaviour and capacity to adapt to a new environment.

Chapter 5
Conclusions and perspectives
In this project, we have studied the feasibility of developing an eﬃcient in vitro phage
therapy for Campylobacter jejuni NCTC12662 strain. To study this, several tests were
conducted during this project. Plaque assays were performed to test lytic activity of our
phage cocktail against NCTC12662. High PFU/ml values (log 9.75) were obtained from
this test, indicating great lytic activity from the cocktail. After this experiments were
concluded, TSI model conditions were mimicked using a more basic experimental set-up
in the laboratory, which allowed us to test bacterial and phage dosages, as well as to
study infection evolution through time. When using optimal growth conditions (41.6°C),
Campylobacter showed a CFU/ml reduction of 1.04 log (90.85% reduction) during the
ﬁrst 2 hours after cocktail dosage was applied. However, at 24 hours Campylobacter was
able to increase and overcome its initial CFU/ml counts. This trend was also observed
in the experiment were two diﬀerent temperatures were used: 41.6°C for Campylobacter
propagation and 37°C for TSI incubations. In this case, an initial drop of 0.48 log units
(67.2% reduction) was observed after 2 hours of cocktail application. Nevertheless, at 24
hours, CFU/ml had already increased and surpassed its initial counts. This trend was
not visible while using the human physiological temperature (37°C). CFU/ml counts keep
decreasing over time, having an initial bacterial drop of log 0.52 (70% reduction) and, if
comparing time 0 with time 24, a total bacterial reduction of log 0.93 (88.3% reduction).
Finally, resistance experiments were useful to determine development of phage resistance
throughout the TSI experiments.
Single colonies were picked and re-streak two times
before applying several dilutions of the phage cocktail to evaluate its lytic activity. Two
colonies presented low PFU/ml values in comparison with the control (log 7.61 and log
7.51 versus log 9.58). Both colonies were picked from the sample taken after 24 hours of
phage dosage at (37°C). Our results indicate that resistance occurs after 24 hours phage
dosage at 37°C and possibly even before at 41.6°C.
Implementation of phage therapy against Campylobacter jejuni infection takes into account
a wide variety of factors, such as inoculum size and timing, host range and density of target
bacteria [64]. Phage cocktails have proven to be successful at decreasing Campylobacter
numbers thanks to their increased host range compared to monophage therapies [51, 53,
54].
In this study, we have demonstrated to develop a phage cocktail containing ﬁve
41
==PAGE:54==
diﬀerent phages, capable of reducing Campylobacter jejuni NCTC12662 levels using in
vitro systems. With this, we have successfully achieved one of our main objectives for this
project.
In vitro models are vital to make an initial assessment of testing phage therapies, as they
provide continuous monitoring, standardized sampling conditions and unlimited screening
possibilities [34].
One of our main objectives was to optimize the simulation of an in
vitro human gut model to provide insights on Campylobacter phage therapy. The current
situation with Covid-19, limited our project by reducing the time that was given for the
experimental part. For this reason, we could not obtain any results from the in vitro gut
models. Despite not being able to succeed in this goal, we were able to develop and perform
a series of experiments that helped us elucidate important aspects of Campylobacter phage
therapy in the context of in vitro gut models. These experiments had the objective to
test NCTC12662 concentrations, phage cocktail dosages and eﬃciencies, test incubation
times similar to the TSI model, and adaptability of Campylobacter to human physiological
temperature.
Even though these experiments helped us examine the feasibility of a phage therapy against
Campylobacter jejuni, these encounter several limitations. First, the TSI modelling ex-
periments do not take into account variables present in the models such as bile salts, bile
acids, gut commensal bacteria and dialysis systems. This could aﬀect at the dosages of
bacteria and phages that should be used in the models. Furthermore, survival tests for
the phage cocktail should be done to test its variability levels within the system. Another
future limitation concerning in vitro models is the fact that these models do not represent
several physiological aspects of in vivo models. This could restrict the usability of future
results due to the fact that the models would not take into account factors such as vir-
ulence, movement to crypts or intestinal mucosa or the capacity of phages to infect the
bacteria within this new environment, amongst others. All of this adds uncertainty in our
results and would need further assessment and/or new experiments.
Next steps would include experimentally testing TSI in vitro models and comparing the
data obtained with the present one. Furthermore, coMiniGut model should be also tested,
although a more basic laboratory set-up should be performed ﬁrst. Other biological ques-
tions should also be addressed as mentioned in the Discussion section such as functionality
of CPS structure in NCTC12662, phenotypical variations in NCTC12662, the eﬀect of en-
vironmental conditions such as temperature in NCTC12662 and the concentrations at
which phages are able to actively reduce NCTC12662.
