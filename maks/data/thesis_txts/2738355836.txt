Abstract
The purpose of this thesis is to benchmark the Hybrid Converter Control (HCC) in a Power Converters
(PCON) compared to a parallel configuration of two PCONs, a GFM and GFL, to identify if as stated in
[10], they are equivalent. Additionally, a hardware implementation and tests of both the HCC and Parallel
GFM-GFL were done in the DTU Risø AC/DC WindPowerLab PCON’s. All models were implemented in
Matlab-Simulink 2023b © with Simscape Electrical © package.
In chapter 1:Introduction, an overview of the Variable Renewable Energies (VRE) and Inverter-Based Re-
sources (IBR) is made, as well as an overview of the study cases and models to test.
In chapter 2:Background, the theory behind the modelling of a PCON using Voltage Source Converter (VSC)s
to implement a Grid Following Converters (GFL) and a Grid Forming Converter (GFM) is developed. Also
the external power loops using droop controllers are described for both the GFL and GFM, ending up the
chapter with the theory applicable to the HCC as described in [10].
In chapter 3:Modelling, the DTU Risø AC/DC WindPowerLab PCONs are described and the full circuit
models are shown and described for both simulations and hardware tests.
Chapter 4 is divided in two sections. Section I shows the simulation-only results based on a 1-to-1 comparison
by using the 1-to-1 circuit equivalent from both HCC and Parallel GFM-GFL. In Section II, results are shown
for the hardware tests and the results by using the hardware circuits described in Chapter 3.
Finally in Chapter 5, the conclusions and future work for the thesis are shown. It has been demonstrated
through out the course of this document that the HCC can be implemented in hardware and that it can follow
setpoints with an emerging response very similar to that of a Parallel GFM-GFL, albeit it shows small but
significant differences when it comes to the transient response tested with a resistive load of 0.6pu, connecting
and disconnecting. It was also observed that the HCC shows flexibility by allowing the change of its k1 and
k2 parameters, altogether with its corresponding virtual R and L parameters and PI compensator tunning,
to exhibit responses more or less alike a GFM without changes in hardware. Herein recommendations are
made for future work regarding its transient response and the effects of harmonics.

Introduction
Scope
As many countries, including Denmark and other EU members, race to transition away from fossil fuels
for different environmental, economic and geopolitical reasons, and as the technology to produce electricity
from wind and solar energy is cheaper than ever [8], it is predicted that the growth trend for renewable
energies will continue to grow at an accelerated pace globally, as seen in Fig. 1.1. Due to the previous, it has
become clearer that the power grid of the future will have a high penetration of Variable Renewable Energies
(VRE) connected to it. However, one of the biggest challenges with sustained VRE growth is the insufficient
investment in grid infrastructure, preventing faster expansion. More than 3,000 GW of VRE capacity is
estimated to be in grid queues, and half of these projects are in advanced stages of development [8].
Figure 1.1: Electricity generation by technology globally [8].
The reasons behind the delayed permitting are complex and are due to different reasons. Among the chal-
lenges that VRE present are performance issues when integrated into the power system [5] e.g. capacity
for transmission line loss or inadequate generation, and also the inability of portfolio generation available to
match the demand for power to the needs at any time.
Some traditional solutions can be implemented to tackle these challenges, for example, expanding transmission
networks or building more energy storage. However, there are different technological solutions to typical
problems of a power system with high penetration of VRE, among which the "Changes to distributed VRE
generators" is one of the most sought-after, as improving this could address a wide range of problems with
integration of the power grid [5].

VRE generators are usually used in conjunction with modern Power Converters (PCON); this in turn has
created low-inertia power systems with fluctuating power generation [13]. This is an inherent characteristic
of PCON’s as there is little to no energy stored in a rotating mass behind it, as it would happen with a
conventional power plant where huge rotors turning at above synchronous speeds store important amounts
of energy that the system can dispose of instantaneously. Due to the previous situation, a more specific
term for VRE which use a PCON to inject energy into the power grid has emerged. They have been called
Inverter-Based Resources (IBR).
In [13] some countries such as Ireland or the United States have been reported to face barriers during certain
time slots due to stability problems. Due to this, certain new technologies have been proposed, as the Grid
Forming Converter (GFM); which can better mimic a Sinchronous Machine (SM) response [13].
The GFM behaves as a voltage source, and with the use of droop equations it can create the desired response
regarding oscillations and inertia in power systems; although it can present stability issues to provide voltage
support in weak grids. On the contrary, while Grid Following Converters (GFL)’s lack the the capability
to provide inertia, they presents other desired characteristics, such as very accurate active and reactive
power injection.As stated previously, the integration of IBR is a consequence of increased Renewable Energy
Sources (RES) penetration. Hence, deep knowledge will be required to change the operation paradigms of
power systems and therefore extensive research is required to fully understand the functioning and application
of IBR’s. In this context, a novel approach to make the most of IBR technologies is to design one physical
converter to present the behaviour of two independent GFM and GFL converters, as presented in [10]. In this
thesis, the model in [10] and the study case with a step increase and decrease of load will be simulated and
analyzed to explore the characteristics this "hybrid" control for PCON’s may present. This will be compared
with a GFM and a GFL connected in parallel to identify any differences, if any, and to better understand
the Hybrid Converter Control (HCC) in [10].
There are already some additional publications involving the HCC in [10]. In[7] a pair of GFL-only and
GFM-only converters are bench-marked against an HCC case. However, this work does not benchmark the
performance of the HCC against a case in which a GFL and a GFM function in parallel. Here in [7], it is also
not used the same architecture of HCC voltage, current and power droop controllers proposed in [10], further
making it difficult to compare in a 1-to-1 accurately. In [16], the HCC is cited only to use an internal current
loop, which is pretty standard and out of a text book [9].In [6], the HCC is cited in the context of a Double
Fed Induction Generators (DFIG) synchronization technique. This is not relevant either for the comparison
of the parallel GFM-GFL case with the Hybrid control. Also not very useful to determine the performance in
modern full-size-converter turbines, which is the industry standard nowadays. In [12], the HCC is mentioned
to make reference to the good characteristics of the GFL to follow reactive power references, which is a well
known characteristic of GFL in literature.
As seen previously, there is not any previous work done to benchmark the characteristics of an HCC compared
to a parallel GFM-GFL in simulations nor tests done in real hardware to further investigate its characteristics
in a real environment.

Formulation of the Problem
The main objective will be to test the differences between Hybrid Converter Control (HCC) control and two
independent real PCON’s, a GFM and a GFL, operating in parallel. There could be a case where there is only
one physical converter available in a system, and having two converters would double the cost in hardware,
hence knowing if an HCC presents the same characteristics as a Parallel GFM-GFL could be an advantage
by itself. Further investigations will be made e.g. differences with f-P droops activated for the GFL and
with V-Q droops activated for both the GFM and GFL, this to test if the droops are required as showed in

Limitations
In this work there will not be a further development of a small-signals analysis for the hybrid converter, and
it will centered only in the study of the hybrid HCC model as developed in [10] by implementing it in the
Simulink 2023b software, and the analysis of its results in comparison to the Parallel GFM-GFL case in a
limited set of cases. A small-signal analysis was executed in [10], however it is limited to the internal current
control loop and the plant. This as it assumes the voltage control loop of the GFM is very slow and hence
constant.
The omission of a small-signal model will not allow to verify the limits of instability of the HCC. It will also
not allow to test the HCC across the range of possible f-P and Q-V droop gains, voltage control loop and
Current Control Loop PI compensator tunning or test different types of compensators, external power control
loop strategies or the interactions with other techniques widely used in PCON’s to improve its performance
as a virtual impedance. In other words, it will test the converter in a specific operating setpoint. However,
this work will be a starting point to observe if its worth to explore later on the HCC response in different
operational ranges.
This work will not include a rotor-side converter model, and the DC side of the converter will be modeled as
an ideal VDC source with constant voltage.Additionally, tests will be executed in islanded mode.

Background
Voltage Source Converter
The Voltage Source Converter (VSC) theoretical background will be briefly reviewed as it is the converter
model used in [10]. As the control strategies used in this work are solely for the VSC, the control can be
implemented independently of whether it is a two-level or a three-level converter [9], and as the switch-
ing algorithm and internal electronics of the converter will be disregarded, it only concerns this work the
understanding of the VSC.
In Fig. 2.1 the general diagram of a VSC converter system with an RL filter can be observed. This model
can also be used for a controlled-frequency VSC.
• Grid imposed VSC. Grid imposed means that this type of VSC requires to be connected to a voltage
source and measure its frequency to couple with it, and hence can’t function in Island Mode. Although
two different types of grid-imposed-VSC’s are possible: Current-Mode and Voltage-Mode control, the
later will be dismissed as it doesn’t protect the converter from current excursions, and it can be an
issue due to the sensitivity of a PCON power electronics to currents above its rated power, due to its
fast thermal time constant
• Controlled-frequency VSC. This is an alternative name for a GFM. The difference with the grid-
imposed VSC is that the ρ is not measured at the terminals of the filter, but it is internally set. It can
work in island mode or connected to a grid with the help of a self regulating algorithm as a f-P droop
controller. This converter will also require an internal voltage control loop in addition to the current
control loop. The later feature will be explained further in the GFM Converter section.
The general VSC converter model shown in Fig. 2.1 consists of:
1. Averaged ideal three phase VSC. It accurately reproduces the voltage reference provided to it by a
control system with no losses or distortions on its terminals. There are many different methods to
provide the voltage reference to the VSC. The Averaged-model reference for a VSC will be used in this
work.
2. Filter. Modeled as a resistance and an inductance, which represents the real hardware filter that would
exist in a PCON to filter out the harmonics of the voltage produced by the high-frequency switching
at the terminals of the VSC.
3. DC voltage source. Represents the DC link coming from another AC/DC PCON or from a DC grid. It
will be modeled in this work as an ideal voltage source with no losses. Opposed to the losses modeling
in Fig. 2.1.

upon which frequency to measure and synchronize with. This is accomplished by the PLL mechanism in

GFM Converters
The main characteristic of a GFM is that it is able to set the voltage and frequency that will be supplied to
the loads. For this it requires to add additional controllers in its structure and as well to generate an internal
voltage and frequency reference that does not depend an external stable voltage source. For this reason, one
of the main advantages of a GFM is that it contains black-start capabilities [11]. It also works as an ideal
AC voltage source and thus represent a low-output voltage impedance, requiring an accurate synchronization
system to operate in parallel with other grid-forming converters. Another characteristic is that it has faster,
inherent grid support capabilities [11], as it will try to maintain the internal frequency values by regulating
its power whenever there are any deviations.
This later fact is very important, as a power grid with a 100% GFL converters has been proven unviable and
requires a certain amount of inertia provided by traditional synchronous machines. Also, studies have shown
that the requirement of Synchronous Machines can be eliminated with a certain share of inverters in GFM
with a sufficient energy buffer [11].
Regarding the fig. 2.9 can be observed that the GFM uses the same measurement points as the GFL in the
previous section, but instead of a PLL to obtain its internal frequency and angle for the Park Transforms,
it generates an internal angle represented as θ that maintains its frequency independent of the existence of
an external grid. There are many ways of implementing the internal generation of a θ for a GFM, the two
main ones being PLL-based and active power droop based. The latter is of special interest as it is a desirable
aspect of a GFM controller to not contain a PLL, as the requirement of a PLL is usually considered one of
the main drawbacks of a GFL [9]. However, the mechanism through which θ is generated internally will be
discussed in a later section when discussing the external power loops.

f-P Droop for GFM PCON
This droop control works by measuring the active power Pmeas at the PCON terminals to adjust the difference
between the virtual electric angle difference (δ) of the PCON, and the actual angle of the grid voltage at the
PCON terminals θ, mimicking the way a SM would behave to regulate its active power output. This makes
reference to the known formula that models the behaviour of a voltage source E behind a reactance XL
connected to a voltage source that models the grid V , and it is expressed for active power as P = EV
XL sin(δ),
where δ is the angle difference between the PCON voltage source E and the grid voltage source V . Hence,
with this simplified equation that disregards the effect of a real component in the impedance XL, it can be
observed that in a highly inductive grid, the angle difference δ can be used as input to regulate the power
transfer assuming constant E, V, XL [3].

The explanation of how such a system works, can be better understood if looking at the fig. 2.16, where the
D and Q axis are the common reference axis, which in this case is the resulting dq-frame transformation of
the voltage signal at the PCON terminals or V , and that is rotating with a radial frequency wcom, while any
other number of GFM converters will be rotating at their own dq-frames named as dqi, dqj...dqn. The angles
δi, δj...δn are the phase displacement of the dq-frames in respect to the common reference frame DQ-frame V
of the voltage signal at the terminals of each PCON, and ωi, ωj...ωn are the angular frequencies of the GFM
PCON’s.
If all GFM PCON’s in a system are perfectly aligned with the DQ-frame, this is δi, δj...δn = 0, and have the
same angular speed ωi, ωj...ωn = wcom, then there will be not any power transfer between the GFM PCON
and the system. As a consequence, it is required to generate a non zero ∆δ between a the internal voltage E
of a GFM PCON and the grid’s voltage V to inject power, and that fact can be used for a f-P droop control
works in a GFM that uses active power for synchronization, and not a PLL.
Hence, the f-P droop function in a GFM is used to regulate the frequency of the grid, and the main idea
comes from mimicking the self-regulation capability of the SM in a grid connection mode, decreasing the
active power delivered when the frequency of the grid increases and vice versa [14].
One implementation of the previous theory can be observed in fig. 2.17, where the difference between reference
and measured powers are scaled by a droop constant kp, which equation is similar to kP in 2.35 if all values
are in per unit. The differences in nomenclature in this figure are that the frequency is measured in angular
speed dω, and is added to the internal reference ωref, to be latter integrated and provide the required δi = ρ
as shown in fig. 2.1. This is done to create the appropriate δi = ρ angle to the internal dq-frame to balance
Pref with Pmeas, this later also called PP CC in 2.17. This balancing out will be displaced by the kp droop
constant, allowing the GFM’s to share the load increases and decreases when there’s a frequency deviation,
but will still perform in the foreseen directions increasing power when the frequency drops, and vice-versa.

V-Q Droop for GFM PCON
The V-Q droop is possible thanks to the ability of the GFM to regulate the voltage amplitude at its terminals.
While the f-P droop is in charge of synchronizing and regulating the electric angle difference (δ), the V-Q
droop will regulate the voltage amplitude.
It can be observed in Fig. 2.18 that, in contrast to a GFL, which can only regulate the output currents
and hence can only contribute to regulate the terminal voltage amplitude through a droop control; the GFM
VQ-droop can effectively set a voltage terminal reference and regulate it with a PI controller. This as it can
also be observed that there is a Qref tracking mode and a VP CC tracking mode. The first follows the reactive
power reference and modifies the terminal voltage by a factor determined by kq to achieve its objective, while
the latter measures the amplitude of the terminal voltage and compares it to the reference voltage VP CC−ref
to modify the voltage reference Vref or V in the figure discussed. The equation to determine the gain droop
for the V-Q droop is similar to that of kQ in eq. 2.35.

IGF L−Hybrid−virtual and the GFM-Parallel does not, and hence the GFM-Parallel it is only concerned about
absorbing the Q from the capacitors to control the voltage, and does not participate in the dynamics of the
current control of the GFL-Parallel.

overall effect for the R-Load is zero, and from its point of view the voltage is the same for both controls.

To explain this, it is first important to notice that an increase in frequency will also increase the Q produced
by the capacitors at the load and will induce the GFM-Hybrid to consume more Q as well. This is true for
both Parallel and Hybrid. Hence the GFM-Parallel and GFM-Hybrid voltages will change to compensate
for this. However, as explained in the previous subsection, the GFM-Hybrid voltage output plays a part in
the current control of the GFL-Hybrid by calculating IGF L−Hybrid−virtual through eq.11, and has to further
reduce its voltage to make IGF L−Hybrd−virtual = 0. Due to the double function of GFM-Hybrid voltage
output, the GFL-Hybrid voltage output is forced to change as well to regulate IGF L−Hybrd−virtual to 0.
The previous still does not explain the transient behaviour but only the steady state response. At t=2.5s, the
GFM-Hybrid and GFM-parallel are both oscillating around its new operating frequency setpoint thanks to
the f-P droop with power synchronization outer loop control. This oscillations make the IGF M−Hybrid−virtual

Conclusions and future work
Conclusions
During the course of this thesis and while reproducing and implementing in hardware the HCC in [10],
it was observed that the GFM PCON is currently a very busy field of research, as opposed to the GFL
which has been the standard for many years for a stable and known PCON control architecture for low-IBR-
penetration power grids. Both the GFM’s desirability for its capacity to not depend on traditional SM and
some challenges it presents in operation as non-accurate injection of active and reactive power, has lead to
proposals such as the HCC. The HCC model demonstrates a way in which the desired characteristics of a
GFM can be blended with the known GFL properties so the PCON can produce an emergent and flexible
response to different possible scenarios without hardware changes, in order to comply with grid codes in
high-IBR-grid penetration cases. The model proposed in [10] has been first successfully implemented in real
hardware at the DTU AC/DC WindPowerLab as a result of this project, in collaboration with the staff of
the Lab, while basic set-point changes and the connection and disconnection of a resistive load of 0.6 pu in
islanded condition were successfully performed in hardware as well. This opens the possibility of using the
HCC in the future, as an additional tool om top of the existing ones, to further increase the penetration of
IBR’s in the grid. Following up, a number of specific conclusions are made as a result of the observation of
the HCC test results, which also backup the statements made herein-before.
1. It was observed in Study Cases section, Chapter I and II that the f-P droops and V-Q droops for
the GFM and GFL, as implemented in [10] are not fundamentally required for the implementation of
HCC. The f-P droop with power synchronization for the GFM has been kept as the base case as it was
included in [10] and it is required in the case of a grid synchronized case.
2. The simulation cases run in Study Cases, section I, showed that the Hybrid can follow reference setpoint
changes as if a GFM and a GFL PCON’s were connected in Parallel in steady state. This was concluded
as it exhibited the same final steady state after reference changes as the Parallel GFM-GFL. However,
it was also shown that the two models present small but significant differences in overshoot, rising time
and settling time when the connection and disconnection of a resistive load of 0.6 pu was executed.
Additionally with the Hybrid, the virtual GFM and GFL voltages for the hardware implementation do
not match with virtual GFM and GFL voltages for the simulation results. AS the power circuit was
carefully reproduced and all the other measured variables: frequency, active power and reactive power,
match; one of the most likely reasons for this mismatch is due to the hardware PCONÂĺs high frequency
oscillations that the voltage and current present in steady state for the hardware implementation, and
how this oscillations interact between the Hybrid eq. 2.39 and the modulation used by the Lab PCON’s.
This last reason is mentioned as the modulation of the PCON’s at DTU AC/DC WindPowerLab differs

with the used in the simulations. This could not be thoroughly verified during this work as not all the
internal control signals were able to be recorded during the executed tests, as to verify that the high

3. During both simulation cases and hardware tests, the transient response of the GFL in Parallel ressem-
bled a stiff current source when a sudden change of RLoad = 0.6pu was connected and disconnected. On
the other hand, the virtual GFL component of the Hybrid control did deviate and showed to contribute
to the final emerging response of the Hybrid controlled PCON. This difference in the GFL ’s response
between Hybrid and Parallel GFL was shown to be caused by the slower response of the inner current
control loop for the Hybrid GFL, when compared to the Parallel GFL. This could be due to the effect
of the back-calculation of the Hybrid GFL-measured-current, that the internal current control loop of
the Hybrid GFL is looking at, as done by eqs. 2.39; as opposed to the Parallel GFL which performs a
direct measurement of the output voltage controlled by the GFM.
4. It was shown that during the transient response studied during the simulations, in Study Cases chapter,
Section I; that the normal k1=0.5 and k2=0.5 Hybrid control as shown in [10] presents a larger voltage
overshoot when the load disconnection occurs than in the parallel GFL-GFM case. However, this voltage
overshoot was improved so to be even smaller than the Parallel GFL-GFM case when the k1=0.25 and
k2=0.75. This indicates that a bigger contribution of the virtual-GFM Hybrid to the overall Virtual
HCC reference voltage during transients aids in maintaining the voltage level at the terminals. The
improved voltage measurements after the RL filter was of V = +0.07pu higher for the connection and
voltage dip, and of V = −0.09pu lower for the disconnection and voltage surge, when compared to the
Parallel GFM-GFL voltage signal.
5. During the course of this project it was demonstrated that it is not a hard requirement for the R and L
values to match the real filter as k1+k2 = 1 and virtual −filter1||virtual −filter2 = Hybrid−filter
to be implemented in hardware and to follow references. However, for the correct emulation of [10],
these principles were followed and herein showing that even by doing so, the HCC does not match
perfectly the Parallel GFM-GFL configuration, more even so during transient response.
6. The transient response in hardware was studied with the same time constant τ for both Hybrid GFM
and GFL virtual-PCON’s, but with a different τ for the Parallel GFM-GFL PCON’s. This was done
due to the process of implementation in hardware, to achieve a similar performance experimentally
from both circuits. However, this could have affected the results when the connection of the RLoad
tests in hardware were run, and further have produced different results than what it could have been
done with the exact same tuning for the voltage loop controller in both HCC and Parallel GFM-GFL.
Another important difference is that, due to the impossibility to change the RL filter resistance and
inductance at the Lab PCON’s, the 1-to-1 comparison executed in Study Cases, Section 1, can not be
said to be completely achieved in hardware.

Future work
The results in this work could be advanced further if more study cases are to be performed to benchmark
the HCC with grid requirements, such as Fault Ride Through (FRT). Additionally, the model developed
in [10] could be expanded to include a PCON with an RLC filter and feed-forward. Furthermore, a small
signal analysis that includes the voltage loop and external active and reactive power loop dynamics could
be devised, so as to understand further the stability limits of the HCC under grid-connected mode and the
effects of the connection impedance. Herein-after a list of specific tasks or recommendations is made so to
aid in any new work in this topic.
1. Different variations of the k1 and k2 parameters, as well as R and L parameters of the virtual filters,
could lead to different responses of the HCC and observe different modes. Exploring different combi-
nations could lead to different characteristics of the GFL and GFM responses to emerge in the Hybrid
response as seen at the PCC. This changes could also be performed in real time to address different
transient responses with different emerging characteristics. E.g. in the chapter Study Cases, Section I,
k1=0.25 and k2=0.75 case, when the sudden connection and disconnection of load, the voltage response
improved but the frequency and active power took longer the reach steady state.
2. Post processing of the voltage, frequency, active and reactive power signals when testing for transient
responses such as connection and disconnection of a load could provide with easier analysis and clearer
results for hardware.
3. It is recommended to test the Hybrid, and perform the appropriate comparisons with a parallel GFM-
GFL, in grid connected modes so to test the hybrid response and stability altogether with grid dynamics
observe the effects of the impedance link between the PCON and a voltage source.
4. Run hardware tests to study and compare the transient response of the Hybrid and Parallel with the
same time constants for the GFM voltage inner loop, so to achieve a 1-to-1 comparison as in the Study
Cases chapter, Section 1.
5. Record the internal variables in eq. 2.39 and 2.40 and run harmonics analysis to understand how these
hybrid equations interact with the modulation-produced high-frequency switching signals, and how this
affects the steady-state and transient responses.
