Abstract
This thesis explores the effects of selected polyols on the protein association behavior and thermal
unfolding of two globular enzymes: hen egg white lysozyme, which serves as a well-characterized
model system, and Subtilisin Savinase from Bacillus lentus, provided by Novonesis. Protein as-
sociation is quantified using intrinsic fluorescence-based methods, with Taylor Dispersion-Induced
Phase Separation (TDIPS) serving as a key technique for screening the protein association-modifying
effects of polyols. Thus, a central contribution of this study is the demonstration of TDIPS as an
effective method for investigating protein association behavior beyond disease-related phase sep-
aration, with applications within enzyme formulation and stability assessment. To complement
TDIPS, Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS) and nano-differential scanning fluorimetry (nanoDSF)
are used to examine changes in protein size and thermal stability, thus providing a more nuanced
understanding of how polyols influence the folded protein. The effects of different polyols on
protein solubility, association, and thermal stability were examined by systematically varying the
solution conditions, namely the concentration of polyol or the presence of an association-inducing
salt. The polyols studied include 1,6-hexanediol, glycerol, sorbitol, sucrose, trehalose, ethylene
glycol, and PEG-200.
While most polyols are found to reduce the liquid-liquid phase separation (LLPS) of lysozyme at
sufficiently high concentrations, with sucrose being exceptionally efficient, their effects on sav-
inase varied. Notably, sorbitol does not significantly reduce the salt-induced association of sav-
inase. Nonetheless, glycerol, ethylene glycol, PEG-200, sucrose, and trehalose effectively reduced
protein association in both enzymes when added in sufficiently high concentrations. However, this
may be attributed to increases in viscosity influencing the measurements.
A key finding of this study is that 1,6-hexanediol promotes phase separation behavior in savinase,
destabilizing the protein and lowering its thermal stability, as evidenced by a decrease in melting
temperature both in the presence and absence of salt.
These results highlight the delicate balance between solvation effects, charge interactions, and de-
pletion forces reigning in enzyme-polyol systems, thus emphasizing the need to carefully fine-tune
formulation conditions based on the specific protein’s surface properties and solution environment.
As protein-polyol interactions are highly dependent on the system in question, this reinforces the
importance of carefully selecting polyols and their concentrations for optimizing enzyme stability
for biotechnological and industrial applications.

Introduction
1.1
The Biological Role of Protein Association: Amorphous
Aggregation and Liquid-Liquid Phase Separation (LLPS)
Protein aggregation, a process driven by the self-assembly of misfolded protein species, is a univer-
sal phenomenon that has profound implications for both disease pathogenesis and biotechnological
applications [1]. One of the most fundamental types of protein aggregation is amorphous aggre-
gation, in which proteins cluster together without forming a well-ordered structure. The formation
of these irregularly shaped aggregates can be induced by several factors, such as changes in tem-
perature, pH, and solvent composition [2]. Depending on the physiological conditions, however,
this aggregation may be reversible if induced by changes in the pH rather than thermal denatura-
tion [1]. Moreover, whether a protein undergoes amorphous aggregation depends on its properties,
such as its solubility and amino acid composition, notably its hydrophobicity [1].
In many cases, protein aggregation does not lead to solid precipitation but the formation of a dense
liquid phase coexisting with a dilute phase. This phenomenon is known as liquid-liquid phase
separation (LLPS). LLPS is a fundamental physical phenomenon in which a homogenous mixture
of at least two components spontaneously separates into two distinct liquid phases with different
concentrations [3]. The importance of LLPS has gained an increasing amount of attention due to
its key role in the organization of biological systems, such as in the formation and organization of
crucial membrane-less organelles (MLOs) e.g. stress and P granules [1, 4].
Biomolecular LLPS, specifically, is the process by which biomolecules, such as proteins and
RNA, condense into liquid-like droplets. Their lack of a defined barrier facilitates the dynamic
exchange of the droplets with the surrounding environment [5]. The condensates are not only able
to fuse upon contact or dissolve, but they can also reorganize in response to changes in the cellu-
lar conditions. This responsiveness allows LLPS assemblies to rapidly respond to environmental
changes, facilitating biomolecular interactions and the regulation of cellular processes [6].
Beyond cellular organization, LLPS is implicated in numerous pathologies when aberrant [7, 8].
For instance, phase-separated condensates can undergo a liquid-to-solid transition under certain
pathological conditions, forming amyloid fibrils, as observed in many neurodegenerative dis-
eases. This is exemplified by the aggregation of tau and α-synuclein proteins, as implicated in
Alzheimer’s and Parkinson’s disease [8, 9].
From an industrial perspective, LLPS can be a highly versatile process with many potential appli-
cations, including biomolecular purification, drug formulation, and drug delivery. For instance, it
can be used to encapsulate small-molecule drugs and protect them from degradation. It can also be
used to improve the efficacy of drug formulation without altering the molecule’s chemical structure
[10]. In recent years, there has also been growing attention to the potential of LLPS to help regulate
enzymatic activity. This can, for instance, be achieved by increasing local reactant concentrations
within the dense phase [11]. By virtue of the compartmentalization, the enzymatic activity can be
improved by enhancing reaction efficiency, reducing limitations for diffusion, and ensuring that
the substrates are within close proximity. However, it must be noted that one must aim to achieve
a delicate balance, as biomolecular crowding within condensates can also have the opposite effect
and reduce the enzymatic activity due to increased viscosity, thus restricting the mobility of the
enzymes [11].

1.1.1
Biophysical Principles of LLPS
Whether or not LLPS condensates form is influenced by several factors. One crucial aspect is the
multivalent interactions between the individual biomolecules [12]. The clustering of proteins can
be facilitated through weak, transient interactions, such as electrostatic or hydrophobic interac-
tions. Hydrophobic interactions, in particular, drive and stabilize the self-association of proteins
in aqueous environments, such as the cytoplasm [9, 13].
In addition, phase separation can be promoted by the presence of π−π-stacking interactions,
particularly when involving the side chains of aromatic amino acid residues such as tyrosine and
phenylalanine [9, 14]. These interactions are often found in intrinsically disordered regions (IDRs)
of proteins, which have a highly dynamic polymeric structure, lacking a fixed three-dimensional
structure [12]. However, π −π-stacking can also occur in folded proteins if the aromatic residues
are exposed on the surface [9, 15].

Phase separation can be achieved by regulating specific external conditions, such as the pH, tem-
perature, or the introduction of macromolecular crowding agents [16]. The process and particular
conditions under which the biomolecule in question transitions from a homogenous single phase
to a demixed, two-phase system can be described via. a phase diagram, such as in Fig. 1.1.

dilute phase. Below the line, biomolecules demix into dense and dilute phases.

The spinodal curve marks the regions at which phase separation begins to occur sponta-
neously without the need for a triggering nucleation event. This process is dubbed ”spinodal de-
composition” as there is no kinetic barrier [18]. In the region between the spinodal and binodal
curve, referred to as the ”metastable state”, droplets start to form through slower nucleation. Fur-

the central difference is that the main component of the phase now consists of the dense phase.
The orange line is referred to as the tie line, which indicates and connects the equilibrium
concentrations between the dense and dilute phase (cD and cL, respectively) [17]. Along the tie
line, the concentrations of the dense and dilute phases remain the same and fixed at cD and cL.
However, the relative volume fractions of the two phases may vary. These can be determined by
using the lever rule. According to the lever rule, for a system undergoing phase separation, the
fraction of the light phase is given by the distance from the total concentration T to the cD, fL = L
T .

1.1.1.2
Thermodynamics of LLPS
Understanding the thermodynamics of LLPS is crucial for being able to modulate the degree of
LLPS in a system [3]. The thermodynamics of LLPS is governed by a balance between the system’s
enthalpic interactions and entropic contributions. A system will only undergo LLPS if the demixed
state has a lower Gibbs free energy (∆G) than the mixed state:
∆G = ∆H −T∆S
(1.1)
where ∆H is the change in enthalpy, ∆S is the change in entropy, and T is the absolute temperature
in Kelvin. In the occurrence of favorable biomolecular interactions, such as hydrogen bonding or
electrostatic interactions, which promote condensate formation, ∆H is negative. Conversely, if
there is a positive contribution to the entropy of the system, T∆S, mixing is favored. However,
it is essential to note that although one may think the condensation of biomolecules into the dense
phase opposes the entropy in the system, the increased entropy in the dilute phase from the excluded
large biomolecules can offset the entropy loss in the dense phase, making LLPS favorable [6].
This can likewise be described via. an energy landscape, cf. Fig. 1.2. The illustration shows the
free energy of the system (secondary axis) of the possible states (primary axis). If the system is in a
metastable state region, a small energy barrier keeps the system mixed. However, nucleation may
cause the system to demix. Once the system is pushed into the spinodal region, cf. Fig. 1.1, the
transition energy (i.e. nucleation energy) is so low that phase separation occurs without the need
for nucleation. The system finally enters the most stable state, where both the dilute and dense
phases coexist [18].
Figure 1.2: Illustration of a free energy landscape featuring different molecular states. Proteins can exist in
different energy states, associated with stable, metastable, or unstable conformations. Source: Etibor et al.
(2021), Fig. 1B [18].

Lastly, although initially developed for polymer solutions, Flory-Huggins theory may also provide
a useful framework for describing LLPS behavior [16]. This theory also considers the entropy
and enthalpy of mixing two components, such as a dense and dilute phase, to predict whether
the system will phase-separate. Particularly, the free energy of the system can be determined by
the energy needed for dispersing the dense and dilute phase and the volume fraction of the two
components. The theory also introduces the Flory-Huggins interaction parameter (χ) and states
that if χ < χcritical, no phase separation occurs, and the system remains in a one-phase regime.
Vice versa, if χ > χcritical, LLPS occurs [16].
1.1.2
Salt-Induced Aggregation: Hofmeister Series
Whether or not a protein aggregates in aqueous environments may depend on the salt concentration.
This is used advantageously, as salt-induced precipitation is frequently used in the initial steps of
protein purification [19]. The effect that salts have on protein solubility may be understood within
the framework of the Hofmeister series. The Hofmeister series is a specified order of ions in terms
of their ability to affect the solubility of proteins [20]. The salt’s effect on solubility is associated
with its ability to cause proteins to become ”salted in” or ”salted out”. Salting out occurs when
high concentrations of a kosmotropic salt reduce the solubility of the protein. This is due to
strengthening the hydration shell surrounding the protein, which enhances the surrounding water’s
hydrogen bonding network [21]. Consequently, as the solubility is reduced, the proteins precipitate.
Among the kosmotropic salts, ammonium sulphate ((NH4)2SO4) is one of the most effective and
widely used due to the kosmotropic sulphate ions.
On the other hand, chaotropic salts increase the solubility of proteins by disrupting the wa-
ter structure around the protein, thus weakening its hydration shell [20]. Although the chloride
ions in NaCl are considered more chaotropic than sulphate, they do not significantly destabilize
protein hydration at moderate concentrations. This is attributed to charge screening, as NaCl re-
duces the electrostatic repulsion between e.g. lysozyme molecules [19, 22]. This allows them to
interact more favorably. Therefore, NaCl mainly induces lysozyme association through electro-
static screening, as opposed to the strong hydration effects of ammonium sulphate that promote
salting-out.
1.2
Taylor Dispersion-Induced Phase Separation (TDIPS)
The well-recognized biological relevance of LLPS has resulted in a swiftly increasing interest
in, and thus need for, efficient and accurate analytical methods for its research and quantifica-
tion [17]. One such technique is Taylor Dispersion-Induced Phase Separation (TDIPS), which
enables the sensitive and precise examination of the phase separation behavior of biomolecules
under strictly controlled conditions [23]. TDIPS experiments can be conducted using a Flow-
Induced Dispersion Analysis-1 (FIDA-1) instrument, i.e., a commercially available microfluidic
platform that leverages Taylor Dispersion Analysis (TDA) and makes it possible to perform high-
throughput screening (HTS) of LLPS-modifying compounds. To fully understand the principles
behind TDIPS, however, it is necessary first to examine the fundamentals of TDA and the fluid
behavior within these capillary systems.
1.2.1
Taylor Dispersion and Molecular Transport: Hagen-Pouisseuille Flow
Taylor Dispersion (TD) is a fundamental phenomenon in fluid dynamics that describes the disper-
sion of solutes in a liquid flowing through a confined system, such as a capillary. The dispersion
that takes place stems from a combination of two factors: The radial diffusion of molecules and
the convection in the system [24].
4
==PAGE:18==
Figure 1.3: Illustration of Taylor Dispersion in a capillary flow system. (a) A solute is introduced into the
capillary. (b) As the solute plug moves through the capillary, it will be elongated because of the velocity
differences across the laminae. (c) The flow follows a parabolic profile, with the highest velocity (umax)
at the center and the lowest velocity (umin) near the walls. Radial diffusion further redistributes molecules.
This illustration was created using Biorender.com and is inspired by Malvern Panalytical, Fig. 1 [24] and
Donath et al.(2019), Fig. 4 [25].
TD occurs under laminar flow in the system, in which the fluid moves in structured layers referred
to as laminae. There is a difference in the flow velocities across the different laminae, as illustrated
in Fig. 1.3. The flow velocity is the highest at the center of the capillary (umax) and the lowest
near the walls (umin). [24]. This velocity distribution curve follows a parabolic shape, which is
characteristic of Hagen-Poisseuille flow, as found under laminar conditions:
u(r) = ∆P(R2 −r2)
4ηL
(1.2)
where u(r) is the velocity at the radial position r, ∆P is the pressure drop across capillary length L,
R is the capillary radius, and η is the dynamic viscosity of the system. At the center of the capillary
(r = 0), velocity is at a maximum. Vice versa, at the capillary walls (r = R), the velocity will be
zero [26]. Thus, the velocity gradient causes any solutes present in the fluid to spread along the
capillary. This dispersion increases over the length of the capillary, as elaborated upon in Sec. 1.2.2
[24]. Moreover, it can be seen that the flow velocity is inversely proportional with the viscosity,
resulting in a longer residence time for any solutes present in the capillary in the case of increased
viscosity.
1.2.1.1
Molecular Size and Dispersion: Stokes-Einstein’s Equation
The molecular dispersion observed in the capillary is also influenced by the solute’s ability to dif-
fuse radially across the laminae. This depends on the solute’s molecular size and diffusivity. Larger
molecules diffuse more slowly, which results in less radial spread across the laminae. Conversely,
smaller molecules diffuse more quickly, enabling them to transition between laminae more easily
and rapidly. This results in a more uniform distribution of smaller molecules across the flow and,
thus, a more symmetric, average-size distribution, with a lower deviation of the signal measure at
the end [27]. This is the principle in TD theory, as one can correlate the dispersion profile to the
changes in the size, i.e., hydrodynamic radius. The exact relationship between the molecular size
and diffusivity is described by the Stokes-Einstein equation:
D =
kBT
6πηRH
(1.3)
where D is the diffusion coefficient, kB is the Boltzmann constant, T is the temperature, η is
the dynamic viscosity, and RH is the hydrodynamic radius of the molecule [28]. This equation
is particularly useful when conducting experiments with a FIDA-1 instrument. As the diffusivity
is measured, it can be used to determine the hydrodynamic radius of the solute of interest in the
liquid, cf. Sec. 1.2.2. Furthermore, in regards to the viscosity, it can be seen from Eq. 1.3 that
there is an inversely proportional relationship between the viscosity and diffusion coefficient. This
means higher-viscosity fluids decrease the diffusivity of the solutes.

1.2.2
FIDA-1 Instrument
The FIDA-1 instrument is a commercially available, microfluidics-based platform that efficiently
establishes Hagen–Poiseuille flow in a long capillary. This can then be used for Taylor Dispersion
Analysis (TDA) and to separate and analyze the solutes in the liquid being pushed through the
capillary.
The system consists of a temperature-controlled sample tray, which stores the sample solution(s)
in either a 96-well plate or 1.5 mL vials. The sample loaded is then pushed through a fused silica
capillary, which is precisely 1 m long, with a diameter of 75 µm. The capillary is placed in a
thermostatted chamber, thus ensuring complete temperature control. Lastly, a UV-fluorescence
detector measures the intrinsic protein fluorescence by using LED excitation at 280 nm and mea-
suring the emission at 300 nm using a long-pass filter precisely 84 cm from the sample inlet [29,
23].
1.2.2.1
Taylor Dispersion Induced Phase Separation (TDIPS) Experiment: LLPS
Quantification
Figure 1.4: Illustration of the molecular dispersion occurring in a TDIPS experiment. The green region
represents the protein plug containing i.e. indicator (optionally containing NaCl). As the plug is pushed
through the capillary, protein disperses, as indicated by the medium blue color. The black dots indicate

The FIDA-1 instrument can advantageously be used to investigate the association of proteins. This
will be utilized in this thesis to examine the effect of different industrially employed polyols on the
association of two globular enzymes. For the setup of the TDIPS experiment, the protein sample
and compounds to be screened are organized so that the protein plug, referred to as the indicator,
is placed between the surrounding buffer containing the tested compounds. The surrounding buffer
will be referred to as the analyte [23].
When conducting the TDIPS experiment, the analyte is first loaded into the capillary, fol-
lowed by the indicator/protein plug. The subsequent injection of the buffer is injected at a pressure
that causes the analyte to disperse under the influence of TD, yielding a more Gaussian dispersion
profile. If the protein does not aggregate or phase separate, the monomeric protein will succumb
to a higher degree of dispersion and reach the detector at Tavg, resulting in a sharper dispersion
profile, see Fig. 1.5, b. On the contrary, if the surrounding buffer contains a compound that in-
duces aggregation or phase separation, aggregates or condensates will form, respectively. Due to
the large size of the aggregates or condensates, they remain in the center of the capillary and are
detected first. These large, non-diffusive particles can be detected the earliest at T 1
2 and appear as
spikes in the dispersion profile [23].
However, there are still some effects that may be difficult to predict and control, meaning the ve-
locity distribution of the molecules will not always yield a perfect Gaussian graph. For instance,
the formation of aggregates or condensates may be non-uniform, resulting in some smaller con-
densates succumbing to some degree of diffusion. Hence, they may be detected later than T 1
2 .

A specific phenomenon that may affect the curve is the Segré-Silbeberg effect, which results
in flow focusing. Some larger particles formed near the capillary center may diffuse to equilibrium
positions toward the capillary walls, causing them to be detected later. Conversely, molecules
formed close to the capillary walls may diffuse inward and be detected earlier than expected. This
results in the formation of a ”stable ring” within the capillary at about 0.6 times the capillary radius
[30]. Lastly, highly viscous solutions can change the flow properties by decreasing the velocity
whilst simultaneously slowing down the diffusion of the molecules [31].
Overall, the FIDA-1 instrument inherently presents several advantages within the field of protein
aggregation and LLPS quantification compared to past alternatives. Firstly, simplicity and ac-
cessibility: The simplified operating system makes protein aggregation and LLPS research much
more accessible for researchers with limited practical experience working with protein condensates
[23]. Secondly, minimal sample requirements: Only 39 nL of sample is needed per measurement
[29]. This is exceptionally little compared to bulk methods, and even less is needed if employing
a thinner capillary [23]. This makes FIDA-1 a valuable instrument for investigating rare or ex-
pensive proteins, such as for neurological diseases [23, 29]. Thus, the inherently practical set-up
permits HTS and the semi-qualitative quantification of protein phase behavior.
1.3
Other Methods for Biophysical Characterization: Prometheus
Panta
Although TDIPS using the FIDA-1 instrument is highly efficient in investigating phase separation
events, other methods have been included in this study. This is partly to substantiate the FIDA-1
findings and complement the TDA with parameters for protein thermal stability. Since monomeric
stability and aggregation are not always directly correlated, analyzing both may provide a more
nuanced view of the overall effects the additives may have on the protein.
Prometheus Panta is a multi-functional instrument that integrates both nano-Differential Scan-
ning Fluorimetry (nanoDSF) to conduct thermal stability measurements and Dynamic Light
Scattering (DLS) investigations to analyze the size distribution of particles. These techniques
can provide further insights into the protein stability and unfolding behavior in real-time upon the
absence and addition of the selected polyols.

The subsequent analyses in Sec. 3 will be based on the parameters mentioned below, along
with the approach taken in a study by Hamborg et al. (2020). Here, nanoDSF was utilized to assess
protein stability using both temperature and chemical denaturation to characterize the unfolding
dynamics of different proteins [32].
1.3.1
Experimental Setup
A Prometheus Panta instrument can be operated by loading samples into capillaries. The instru-
ment accommodates up to 48 standard capillaries or 24 capillaries in a chip format, requiring as
little as 10 µL of sample per measurement, thus allowing HTS with minimal sample consumption.
Specifically, only 5 µg/mL is needed for nanoDSF, whilst a minimum of 0.5 mg/mL is required
for a 15 kDa protein for DLS [33].
1.3.2
Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS)
Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS) is a biophysical characterization technique that can determine the
hydrodynamic radius (RH) of proteins in a solution. This is achieved by measuring the fluctua-
tions in the light scattering intensity over time. The intensity fluctuations are caused by the random
Brownian motion of the particles suspended in the solution. As smaller particles move and dif-
fuse more rapidly than large ones, their scattering intensity changes more rapidly, cf. Fig. 1.6, a.
This results in shorter-lasting fluctuations. The fluctuations are compared to generate an autocor-
relation (ACF) decay plot. Seeing as rapidly diffusing molecules lose correlation more quickly,
the decay occurs earlier compared to a sample with large, slowly diffusing molecules, e.g. large
aggregates. Thus, the diffusion coefficient can be obtained from the decay curve. Subsequently,
Stokes-Einstein’s equation, cf. Eq. 1.3 can advantageously be used to calculate the hydrodynamic
radius (RH) from the diffusion coefficient (D) [33, 34].

in solution. The figure is made in Biorender.com with strong inspiration from Auld et al. (2017), Fig. 9
[35]. (b) Schematic of the unfolding process of proteins due to heating, revealing the Trp and Tyr residues

1.3.3
Nano Differential Scanning Fluorimetry (nanoDSF)
nanoDSF relies on the intrinsic fluorescence of the amino acids tryptophan (Trp) and tyrosine
(Tyr). When a protein is in its native conformation, Trp and Tyr residues are buried within its core,
protected from the surrounding aqueous environment. In this state, the protein emits fluorescence
primarily around 330 nm. However, if the protein is gradually heated, it begins to unfold, and
the Trp residues become increasingly exposed to the surrounding solvent. This results in a red-
shift of the fluorescence emission spectrum towards 350 nm. Therefore, the fluorescence intensity
measurements and shift in emission wavelengths allow one to determine parameters indicative of
the thermal stability of proteins. For instance, the melting temperature, Tm, is the temperature
at which 50% of the protein has unfolded, and 50% remains folded [36]. The exact Tm can be
determined by plotting the ratio between the fluorescence at 350 and 330. The midpoint on the
obtained sigmoidal graph corresponds to Tm. In addition, one can infer information from the slope
of the midpoint: A steeper curve typically indicates a protein with increased stability, whereas a
more gradual slope suggests more gradual unfolding, indicating a more unstable protein.

1.4
Selected Polyols
Polyols are organic compounds that contain multiple hydroxyls (-OH groups). Depending on the
polyol, different functionalities are observed. However, many are widely used in industrial and
biotechnological applications due to their unique physicochemical properties and effectiveness in
protein stabilization [37, 38]. For instance, many are used as excipients in liquid formulations
to enhance the shelf life of active enzymes [39]. Although polyols are known to enhance the
stability of proteins, their exact role and impact on the aggregation behavior and thermal stability
of enzymes are still not entirely understood [39, 40]. Many operate by preferential hydration,
which will be elaborated upon further below, but this is not the only mechanism that reigns. For
example, preferential hydration, and thus protein stabilization, may be increased when combined
with excluded volume effects in the presence of more -OH groups in the polyol [37]. This will also
be briefly touched upon in Sec. 1.4.3.
Overall, this lack of understanding presents a bottleneck in the most productive selection and
utilization of polyols in the liquid formulation and shelf stability optimization of enzymes [39].
Hence, understanding the effects of industrially applied polyols on protein aggregation and stabil-
ity is imperative to modulate said aggregation in an industrial context.
The polyols investigated in this thesis have been chosen based on their structural diversity, func-
tional roles, and relevance in industrial and biochemical applications. The selection has also been
highly inspired by studies such as Ahmad et al. (2022) and Nasiripourdori et al. (2009) [37, 41].

1.4.1
Small Polyols
1,6-hexanediol is often used in the polymer-based material industry for synthesis [42]. In addition,
it is well-known for its ability to dissolve LLPS-based condensates both in vitro and in cells. Thus,
it is frequently utilized for this in biochemical studies. Although the precise mechanisms are not
fully understood, it is known that 1.6-hexanediol interferes with weak hydrophobic interactions,
which drive LLPS [13, 43]. For example, Soltys et al. (2021) demonstrated that 1.6-hexanediol
disrupted LLPS in the AB region of human Retinoid X Receptor γ (hRXRγ), with lower concen-
trations only partially preventing phase separation [44].
Glycerol, in contrast, is well-known for its ability to stabilize proteins, such as during their refold-
ing [45]. The stabilization is primarily explained by two mechanisms of interaction: preferential
solvation and preferential hydration. Regarding solvation, glycerol directly interacts with hy-
drophobic patches on protein surfaces. In the process, they form amphiphilic interfaces, which
stabilize partially unfolded intermediates. This prevents their association into aggregates by pro-
tecting the hydrophobic regions from further solvent exposure. For preferential hydration, glycerol
is excluded from the immediate proximity of the protein surface. This leads the water molecules
surrounding the protein to become more ordered. In turn, the exposure of hydrophobic surfaces be-
comes more thermodynamically unfavorable. Consequently, the protein adopts its more compact,
native structure [39, 45]. All in all, the two mechanisms work together, with preferential solva-
tion stabilizing unfolded intermediates, while preferential hydration pushes the protein toward its
native state by making the unfolded state less thermodynamically stable.
An example of these stabilizing effects during, for instance, thermal denaturation of proteins
has been exemplified in a study by Ali et al. (2022). Here, they demonstrated that the structural
stability of catalase was improved with increased secondary structure content and higher melting
temperature by 15.2 C upon the addition of 1M glycerol [38]. Similarly, in a study by Ahmad et
al., the aggregation of α-amylase from Bacillus amyloliquefaciens was prevented in the presence
of 5M glycerol. However, the number of hydroxyl groups seemed to play a crucial role, as higher
concentrations of glycerols were required than for polyols containing more -OH groups [37]. This
is likely explained by volume exclusion effects, which will be further explained below.
Sorbitol is a sugar alcohol used for various purposes, including in the food industry, and as an
additive for heat-processed protein-based gels. It functions similarly to glycerol but has addi-
tional effects on the compactness of the protein. In a study by Chanasattru et al. (2008), sorbitol
increased the thermal stability of the globular protein, β-lactoglobulin (β-Ig), raising its denatura-
tion temperature by 8 C when 30 wt.% was added [46]. This was partly attributed to preferential
hydration, as sorbitol is strongly excluded from the protein surface. Another study by Baier et al.
(2003) demonstrated that sorbitol effectively improved the stability of bovine serum albumin. In
addition to being preferentially excluded, it exhibited steric exclusion and hydrophobic effects, as
it was physically excluded from the hydration shell around the protein. In effect, this reduced the
solvent penetration and reinforced the folded state of the protein as well [46, 47].
1.4.2
Sugar Polyols
Both trehalose and sucrose are very similar disaccharides known to possess significant protein-
stabilizing effects. Because of this, they are widely utilized in both research and industry to prevent
protein denaturation [48]. Although they both contain the same number of hydroxyl groups and
exhibit preferential hydration, there are noticeable differences in how they interact with water and
their effectiveness in protein stabilization [49].
10
==PAGE:24==
Trehalose is often used for the complete inhibition of polyglutamine-mediated protein aggregation
[50]. Its mechanism of action involves several key phenomena, such as preferential hydration,
surface tension effects, and its ability to form extensive hydrogen bonds with the surrounding
water. For the latter, the structured water layer acts as a ”protective shield,” which helps maintain
the protein’s hydration shell. This effectively protects the protein from, e.g., thermal denaturation
upon heating [51].
Sucrose is widely used in industry as a stabilizer, which enhances protein shelf life and stabil-
ity. A study by Jonsson et al. (2024) compared the effects of sucrose and trehalose for protein
stabilization of the two globular enzymes, lysozyme and myoglobin, using differential scanning
calorimetry. They discovered that sucrose appears to exceed the protein stabilization effects of
trehalose at high temperatures due to sucrose’s higher binding affinity to the proteins’ surfaces
and by directly hydrogen bonding to the protein [48]. Ahmad et al. (2022), however, found that
when sucrose is added in high concentrations, it leads to reduced protein refolding efficiency due
to increased viscosity and hindered molecular flexibility. This effectively slowed the protein’s
rearrangement into its native conformation [37].
1.4.3
(Poly)Ethylene Glycols
Polyethylene glycol (PEG) is a hydrophilic, non-ionic polymer with extensive biochemical and
commercial applications [52]. It is widely recognized as one of the most effective precipitating
agents for crystallizing globular proteins [53]. However, the effects of PEG depend strongly on
the size of the polymer chain. As a result, different PEG molecules may exhibit varying effects on
the aggregation of proteins.
Polyethylene Glycol-200 (PEG-200) is a short-chain polymer that is widely used as a crowd-
ing agent [54, 55]. In a study by Annunziata et al. (2022), the effects of PEG-200 and other
PEG molecules of increasing size on bovine γD-crystallin were investigated. They observed that
adding PEG-200 induced phase separation at higher temperatures as the concentration of PEG-
200 increased [56]. This effect was consistent and became more pronounced with larger PEG
molecules. This behavior is best explained by volume exclusion and the depletion zone induced
by the PEG molecules. When PEG is added to a solution, it excludes itself from the space between
two protein molecules in close proximity. This effectively creates a region wherein the PEG con-
centration is lower than in the bulk solution. Due to the significant size of PEG molecules, the
system attempts to minimize the excluded volume, leading to an entropic effect that drives the
proteins closer together. This promotes the formation of protein-dense phases and lowers the free
energy barrier for phase separation, enabling LLPS to occur at higher temperatures [56].
The relationship between PEG size and its effects on protein behavior is further illustrated
by a study featured in Gunton et al. (2007). This study showed that low-molecular-weight PEG
decreases the cloud point temperature of lysozyme, while high-molecular weight PEG has the
opposite effect [57]. This size-dependent behavior highlights the importance of selecting the ap-
propriate PEG molecule for specific applications. For this reason, the monomeric form of PEG,
ethylene glycol (EG), and PEG-200 have both been included in this study to investigate whether
the salt-induced aggregation is enhanced or reduced by differently sized (P)EG molecules.
11
==PAGE:25==
Ethylene glycol (EG) is the monomeric unit of PEG. It is often used as a cryoprotectant to prevent
cold denaturation [58]. However, its influence on protein stability has, interestingly, been shown
to be temperature-dependent. While EG protects proteins at low temperatures, it has been shown
to destabilize them at higher temperatures [58]. In Naidu et al. (2020), this was attributed to
EG preferentially interacting with the unfolded state at higher temperatures (340 K, i.e., 66.85 C),
almost acting like a denaturant comparable to urea. At lower temperatures (-10 C), EG exhibited
preferential hydration and accumulated around hydrophobic residues, thus reducing hydrophobic
interactions. However, the findings in this article were obtained by a combination of differential
scanning calorimetry and molecular dynamics simulations. Thus, it may be of interest to examine
if the effects of EG on the aggregation and thermal stability of the enzymes featured in this thesis
also share the same behavior [58].
1.5
Structural and Biochemical Properties of Enzymes of Interest
The enzymes investigated in this thesis, hen egg white (HEW) lysozyme and Subtilisin Savinase,
are both globular proteins. However, they exhibit distinct differences in their structural and bio-
chemical characteristics, which, in turn, influences their propensity to aggregate.

1.5.1
HEW Lysozyme
HEW lysozyme (lysozyme) is globular glycoside hydrolase that hydrolyzes β-linkages in pepti-
doglycans and chitodextrins [59]. This makes it a highly valuable enzyme in cellular lysis and
antimicrobial applications [57]. Due to its well-characterized structure, lysozyme has been chosen
as the model system for investigating the effects of polyol additives.
Regarding its size, lysozyme has a molecular weight of 14.3 kDa. It consists of 129 amino acids,
with 6 of these being Trp residues (28, 62, 63, 108, 111, and 123). Moreover, Trp-62, Trp-64, and
Trp-108 are known to be located in the active site [60]. In addition, it contains 6 Tyr residues.
The extinction coefficient of lysozyme was determined to be 38940 M−1cm−1, making it highly
intrinsically fluorescent and suitable for both nanoDSF and TDIPS.
Lysozyme is known to have a compact structure due to containing many alpha-helices and rela-
tively few beta-strands, cf. Fig. 1.7, a. Its structure is stabilized by four disulfide bonds. Consid-
ering this combination of its alpha-helical structure and the presence of disulfide bonds, it can help
explain the general robustness of lysozyme.
12
==PAGE:26==
The enzyme is known to operate efficiently over a broad pH range (from 6.0-9.0) [61]. More-
over, as lysozyme has an isoelectric point (pI) of 11.35, it is highly positively charged under most
physiological conditions. This includes at pH 4.4, i.e., the pH-value at which the experiments of
this thesis have been conducted [59, 61]. However, albeit positively charged, lysozyme readily
undergoes LLPS and even forms gels at high protein concentrations [57, 62]. This is due to the
presence of NaCl in the solution, which plays a crucial role in screening electrostatic repulsion.
As the negative chloride ions interact with the positively charged lysozyme molecules, the surface
charge is reduced, and repulsion is minimized. Hence, phase separation occurs under the given
experimental conditions.
1.5.2
Subtilisin Savinase
Subtilisin Savinase (Savinase) is a broad-spectrum alkaline serine protease secreted by Bacillus
lentus. It is widely used in many industrial detergents produced by Novonesis [63]. Its molecular
weight is 26.7 kDa, consisting of 275 amino acids [64]. This makes it twice the size of lysozyme.
Savinase contains 7 Trp and 3 Tyr residues, meaning it is, like lysozyme, highly intrinsically fluo-
rescent. Its extinction coefficient has been calculated to be 26930 M−1cm−1 [65].
Although over 50% of its 275 amino acids have been mutated across different variants to im-
prove thermal stability and proteolytic resistance, its stability remains poorly understood. In its
native form, it is structurally stabilized by calcium binding sites rather than the disulfide bonds in
lysozyme [66]. Savinase’s conformational stability is strongly dependent on the presence of these
calcium ions, and it is a universal feature of all savinase mutants to contain one or more calcium
binding sites [67].
Savinase has been shown to function optimally at moderately alkaline pH (pH 7-10) and exhibits
high activity at highly alkaline pH (pH 8-12) [41, 66]. This feature of high activity at alkaline pH
makes it profoundly suitable for detergents [66]. In addition, savinase has a pI of 9.3. Hence, it
should carry a moderate positive charge in neutral to slightly basic conditions, such as around the
experimental conditions of this thesis (pH 4.5 for savinase), making it less prone to self-aggregation
unless in the presence of a salt, which can screen the charges.
The protein core of savinase contains many β-sheets, which are highlighted in yellow in Fig. 1.7, b.
These β-sheets provide a high degree of structural stability in its core, where the solvent-exposed
active site is located. The active site contains the catalytic triad Ser-His-Asp, which is surrounded
by charged and polar residues [66, 68]. Moreover, one of its calcium-binding sites is found near
the active site. This helps stabilize the active region and maintain the enzyme’s structural integrity
[66]. In contrast, the numerous α-helices on its surface contribute to the enzyme’s flexibility and
broad specificity [67].
A more in-depth computational analysis of the two enzymes will be conducted in Sec. 3.1 to com-
pare their surface characteristics and estimate how they differ in both their propensity to aggregate
or phase separate. It must be noted, however, that the subsequent analysis of empirically obtained
data does not directly compare these two enzymes, as they are inherently different and exhibit
different kinds of aggregation by different mechanisms, which is further induced by different con-
ditions, such as the salt.

2.1
Protein Stock Preparations
A flask with 10 g of crystallized, white hen egg white (HEW) lysozyme powder was purchased
from MP Biomedicals (enzyme activity ≥20,000 u/mg solid) and stored in a -20 C freezer [69].
The Bacillus lentus-derived Subtilisin Savinase enzyme was obtained in the form of a crystal slurry
from Novonesis. This was expressed and purified before crystallization and washing of the enzyme
using CaCl2. The expression, purification, and washing was performed by Novonesis before the
crystal slurry was obtained.
To prepare a lysozyme stock of 20 mL with a desired concentration of a minimum of 100 g/L, 2921
mg HEW powder was weighed off using a Sartorius scale and dissolved into 19.5 mL 0.1 M NaAc
buffer at pH 4.4 in a 100 mL Blue Cap glass bottle. This solution was set onto a magnet stirrer for
1 hour at 550 rpm to increase dissolution.
To prepare a savinase stock, 1029 mg of the crystal slurry was weighed using the Sartorius scale
and dissolved into 9.75 mL 0.1 M NaAc buffer pH 4.5 in a beaker. The beaker was placed onto a
magnet stirrer at r.t. for 5 hours at 500 rpm.
For both enzyme stocks, 200 µL of the solution was extracted and centrifuged at 16.9 krcf at 20
C for 10 minutes to remove any insoluble fractions. Subsequently, the supernatant was pipetted
into a separate 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube. 10x and 100x dilutions were made using the 0.1 M NaAc
buffer at pH 4.4 for lysozyme and pH 4.5 for savinase. The final concentration of the supernatant
was then determined to be 124 mg/mL for lysozyme and 35 mg/mL for savinase by measuring
their UV absorbances, cf. Sec. 2.1.1. The individual protein stocks were separated into 200 µL
aliquots, flash-frozen in liquid nitrogen, and immediately stored at -80 C. Before every experiment,
one aliquot was thawed, centrifuged at 16.9 krcf at 20 C, and the concentration determined.
2.1.1
Concentration Determination
The protein concentrations were quantified by measuring the UV absorbance at 280 nm using a
NanoDrop spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific). In order to ensure accurate measure-
ments and minimize errors caused by, e.g., detector saturation, the samples were diluted 10x and
100x and taken in triplicates. Subsequently, the actual concentration was calculated using Lambert-
Beer’s law, using the extinction coefficients of the proteins. The extinction coefficients were cal-
culated based on their protein sequences using the online tool, ProtParam (ExPASy) [65].
2.1.2
Size Exclusion Chromatography (SEC)
Size Exclusion Chromatography (SEC) was performed on a sample of the savinase crystal slurry
obtained from Novonesis to confirm its purity and remove any potential impurities. The protein
sample for SEC was made by dissolving 13.3 mg of Savinase crystal slurry into 300 µL of 100
mM buffer pH 4.5. The elution was performed using the same buffer, which had been filtered
and stored at 4 C for 24 hours prior. The purification was performed using a HiLoad Superdex
200 Increase 10/300 GL CV preparative size exclusion column (Cytiva), with a volume of 24
mL. The preparative column was integrated into an Äkta pure fast protein liquid chromatography
(FLPC) system (Cytiva). The chromatography was run under a constant flow rate of 1 mL/min
using a 500 µL injection loop. The eluted fractions were concentrated using a 6 mL spin filter with
a 3 kDa molecular weight cut-off via. centrifugal up-concentration, resulting in a final protein
concentration of 26.6 mg/mL. The obtained chromatogram can be found in Appendix A.2.

Buffer and Salt Preparation

anhydrous sodium acetate for final volumes of 500 mL. The pH was adjusted to 4.4 and 4.5, re-
spectively. The solutions were filtered before being stored at r.t.
A 4M sodium chloride (NaCl) stock was prepared by dissolving 23.658 g of NaCl into 100 mL of
milli-Q water. The solution was thoroughly mixed and stored at r.t.
In order to make 1 L of saturated ammonium sulphate stock (5.7M at 20 C, 754 g/L), 750 g of
ammonium sulphate was weighed off. The solution was prepared by slowly dissolving ammonium
sulphate in a beaker containing milli-Q water and a magnetic stirrer. The salt was added gradually
in increments. At first, the salt dissolved easily, but as saturation approached, dissolution became
progressively slower. Gentle heating was applied to ensure the temperature remained below the
boiling point to prevent water loss. The process took several hours prior to dissolution. Once fully
dissolved, the solution was transferred to a 1 L blue cap bottle and left overnight at r.t. By the next
day, crystals had formed at the bottom of the bottle.
In addition, four different buffer stocks were produced: 1M sodium phosphate (NaPi), 0.5M
HEPES, 0.5M Tris, and 0.8MM Glycine-NaOH. 1M NaPi buffer at pH 7.0 was made by dissolving
12.309 g of sodium dihydrogen phosphate monohydrate in 90 mL milli-Q water while stirring. The
1M HEPES buffer at pH 8.0 was produced by dissolving 24.868 g of HEPES in 90 mL milli-Q
water. The 0.5M Tris buffer at pH 8.8 was prepared by dissolving 2.581 g Tris in 40 mL milli-Q.
The 0.8M Glycine-NaOH buffer at pH 10.6 was made by dissolving 12.335 g of glycine in 160
mL milli-Q water. 4.295 g of sodium hydroxide was added to the solution.
After ensuring all solids were fully dissolved, following magnet stirring for 30 minutes until
complete dissolution, the pH values were adjusted by titrating with either HCl or NaOH. Once the
desired pH values were reached, the volumes were adjusted to reach 100 mL for NaPi, 100 mL for
HEPES, 50 mL for Tris, and 200 mL for Glycine-NaOH. All buffers were filtered and stored at 4
C before use.

2.5.1.2
Aggrescan3D Analysis
Aggrescan3D (A3D) is an online, structure-based computational tool designed to predict protein
aggregation by analyzing three-dimensional structural data and thermodynamic principles. The
structural data for each individual enzyme was uploaded to A3D as a Protein Data Bank (PDB)
file. Unlike sequence-based prediction tools, A3D identifies solvent-exposed aggregation-prone
regions (APRs) and evaluates their impact on protein solubility and stability [70].
A3D calculates an A3D score for each amino acid, which accounts for the conformational context
of the structure while excluding contributions from hydrophobic residues in the protein core, as
these are considered negligible [70]. The results of the analysis are summarized in Tab. 3.3.
Key parameters chosen include the Minimal Score Value (representing the most soluble residue),
the Maximal Score Value (identifying the most aggregation-prone residue), the Average Score
(a normalized indicator of overall aggregation propensity), and the Total Score Value (a global
indicator of aggregation propensity that accounts for protein size). More negative values for the
Minimal and Average Scores indicate higher solubility, while more positive Total Score Values
suggest a greater likelihood of protein aggregation.

3.1
Computational Comparative Analysis of Lysozyme’s and
Subtilisin Savinase’s Potential for Protein Association
In order to make a computational assessment of the potential for both enzymes to aggregate and
undergo LLPS, their structures have been examined in terms of the extent of hydrophobic patches
on their surfaces, along with the presence of polar residues that can potentially interact with the
surrounding solvent molecules in an aqueous solution. This is because exposed hydrophobic re-
gions typically drive aggregation or phase separation by promoting protein self-association, thus
enhancing aggregate or condensate formation [71, 72].
3.1.1
PyMOL Analysis
Figure 3.1: Surface structures of lysozyme (left) and savinase (right), respectively. The coloring scheme is
as follows: Hydrophobic residues: Grey; Positively charged residues: Blue; Negatively charged residues:
Red; Polar residues: Orange. The structures were visualized using PyMOL (Version 3.1.3.1).
Structural visualization in PyMOL was used to map surface charge distributions and hydrophobic
patches, providing insight into surface characteristics and solvent accessibility. The generated im-
ages can be seen in Fig. 3.1. In savinase, hydrophobic residues can be seen slightly more exposed.
In contrast, lysozyme’s hydrophobic regions are predominantly buried, possibly contributing to its
solubility. Moreover, the electrostatic surface map shows that lysozyme’s surface is more positive
compared to savinase’s. On the other hand, savinase exhibits a more balanced charge distribution
and a seemingly more dominating presence of polar patches (cf. Fig. 3.1, b).
At a quick glance, one might assume that the distribution is comparable and relatively uniform for

3.1.3
Overall Assessment of Protein Association Propensity
Lysozyme and savinase’s likelihood of association will likely be influenced by a combination of
their distinct charge distributions, hydrophobic region exposure, and possible solvent interactions,
all of which respond differently to polyols. 39.2% of lysozyme’s surface area is composed of
charged residues. Most of its hydrophobic regions are buried. Hence, it should remain highly
soluble due to possible electrostatic repulsions. This may limit association except under conditions
that neutralize charge interactions (e.g., pH close to pI) or induce depletion interactions, such as
upon the addition of high PEG concentrations. On the other hand, polyols, such as glycerol and
sorbitol, might further stabilize lysozyme by enhancing hydration shells, making association less
favorable.
In contrast, only 15.8% of savinase’s surface is charged. Moreover, it has more exposed large
hydrophobic regions (30.8%). This suggests that it is more prone to association than lysozyme,
particularly in crowded environments. Similarly, stabilizing polyols will likely increase the sol-
ubility of savinase, though this may be to a lesser extent than lysozyme due to savinase’s greater
potential for hydrophobic interactions and clustering. The results from A3D possibly substanti-
ate this theory about savinase’s association being driven by hydrophobic interactions, particularly
considering savinase scored higher values for its aggregation-prone residues (-2.3365 vs. -3.2002
in lysozyme). Thus, savinase will likely be more prone to association under the proper conditions,
whereas lysozyme may remain stable unless, e.g., strong depletion forces, such as from PEG-200,
are introduced.

the center and walls.

for lysozyme’s outer hydration shell, resulting in a ”salting-out effect” for the protein at high NaCl
concentrations.

Savinase
3.3.1
Optimization of Buffer Conditions
Figure 3.4: Concentrations achieved by dissolving savinase in six buffers with varying pH values.
By simply considering the typical non-linear concentration dependence of association reactions,
one can reason that if the protein concentration in the plug of a TDIPS experiment is too low to
facilitate protein-protein interactions, protein association will not occur. Thus, it is of the utmost
importance to determine the conditions that allow one to reach a sufficiently high protein concen-
tration prior to conducting the TDIPS experiments.
As seen in Fig. 3.4, the solubility of savinase highly depends on the surrounding pH, with the
maximum concentrations achieved at lower pH values far from its pI of 9.3. Its charge state can
explain this.
At low pH values around 4.5, savinase reaches a concentration of 15.03 mg/mL without the
additional help of stirring or heating. This also provided the highest yield (of 4.73%). The reason
for savinase’s high solubility at lower pH is attributed to its net positive charge in the acidic en-
vironment, which promotes electrostatic repulsion between the proteins. However, it is important
to note that this value does not necessarily reflect the absolute solubility of savinase, as no undis-
solved solid was observed under these conditions. True solubility requires equilibrium with the
solid phase, which was not assessed here. Rather, this data demonstrates the pH dependence of
solubility.
As the pH in the surroundings approaches neutral conditions at pH 7.0 using the sodium phosphate
buffer, solubility decreases by more than half (6 mg/mL), likely due to a reduced net positive
charge. The solubility is at an all-time low when using buffers with pH values close to its isoelectric
point, such as the HEPES, Tris, and Glycine-NaOH buffers. The protein will not dissolve because
savinase carries almost no charge here. Notably, at these higher pH values, some undissolved
savinase was still observed after 1.5 hours of incubation at r.t. This suggests that the equilibrium
solubility limit was reached or that the dissolution process was slower under these conditions.

After continuing to explore methods to increase the concentration at pH 4.5, a maximum concen-

acidic pH. This is likely attributed to lysozyme’s considerably higher pI value around 11. Seeing
as lysozyme’s surface is much more dominated by a positive net charge, this will also influence
its association behavior, as it is highly soluble. This might prevent association in comparable con-

Before exploring which additives might prevent or reduce the aggregation of savinase, it is essential

peak under salt-free conditions. While the spikes might be interpreted as representing different
clusters, they likely arise simultaneously in reality. This is supported by the significant shift in the
curves after adding 2.25M ammonium sulphate. The increased viscosity may delay peak arrival
due to a higher effective protein concentration caused by the formation of large aggregates. If this
is the case, the increase in spike fluorescence suggests that the aggregates grow in size with higher
ammonium sulphate concentrations.

increasingly asymmetric. Moreover, an elongated ”tail” appears in the signal. This suggests that

kD Analysis
To further investigate the effects of ammonium sulphate on the self-association of savinase, DLS
was used to measure the hydrodynamic radius of the protein and examine if its size was concentration-
dependent. Five different protein dilutions, ranging from 1 to 10 mg/mL, were prepared to ensure
a minimum of five data points. Moreover, two sets of conditions were tested: one with ammo-

insights into the isolated effects of the protein concentration but in the presence and absence of
ammonium sulphate. All measurements were conducted at a constant temperature of 20 C.

In Fig. 3.7, it can be seen that there is no significant increase in size at savinase by itself. The
cumulant radius is relatively stagnant and stable at around 2.668 ± 0.057 nm. This is congruent
with the estimate by FIDA-1, which was 2.2 nm. However, the cumulant radius seems to increase
upon adding 2.5M ammonium sulphate. It ranges from 2.59 ± 0.45 nm to almost double its original
radius at 3.99 ± 0.1 nm. This suggests attractive interactions between the savinase molecules are
promoted in the presence of ammonium sulphate.

The diffusion coefficient, D, has been calculated from the Stokes-Einstein equation, cf. Eq. (1.3).
The kD, also known as the interaction parameter, can be derived from the linear regression slope
of D vs. the protein concentration. This is a valuable parameter, as a negative slope indicates that
the protein likely participates in attractive protein-protein interactions, leading to self-association
or aggregation.
The kD is negative, both with and without ammonium sulphate. This indicates that savinase is
inherently likely to self-aggregate. Without ammonium sulphate, the kD is -4.25·10−13m2/s per
mg/mL. In the presence of the salt, the kD is -3.66 ·10−12m2/s per mg/mL, meaning it is kD
is almost ten times more negative, suggesting a much higher likelihood of self-association. This
confirms that savinase aggregates form much more readily in the presence of ammonium sulphate,
likely due to electrostatic screening effects that reduce the electrostatic repulsion of the protein
surface.
One important consideration when interpreting these results, however, is the effect of increased
protein concentration on the solution viscosity. The only viscosity correction applied to these
measurements is the default correction performed by the Prometheus Panta software, achieved
by selecting the solution buffer. Thus, the viscosity increase caused by both ammonium sulphate
and high protein concentrations has not been fully accounted for. In actuality, the decrease in kD
reflects the combined effects of intermolecular interactions and changes in viscosity. A higher
protein concentration leads to a higher viscosity and, vice versa, a lower diffusion coefficient.
Thus, without knowing the exact change in viscosity, it may be argued that one cannot determine
the kD.
That being said, one could argue that the plot itself suggests such corrections may not be
critical. In the absence of ammonium sulphate, the diffusion coefficient remains relatively stable,
showing only a slight linear decrease as protein concentration increases. Furthermore, if viscosity
presented an indisputable issue, the offset should be identical in both graphs. This is not observed,
meaning the reduction in diffusion is likely attributed to intermolecular interactions rather than
bulk viscosity changes. On the other hand, there is the question of absolute vs. relative viscosity:
As a first approximation, the absolute change in viscosity is likely to be the same in both scenarios,
as the same amount of protein is added in both plots. However, the relative change in viscosity due
to increasing protein concentration might not be the same. In the absence of ammonium sulphate,
the baseline viscosity is inherently lower. Thus, the relative increase in viscosity will be more
pronounced. When ammonium sulphate is present, the baseline viscosity is higher due to the
presence of salt, resulting in a less pronounced relative increase in viscosity as a result of the
increasing protein concentration.

3.4.1
Preliminary Comments for Data Analysis
In order to investigate how different polyols influence the self-association behavior of lysozyme
and savinase, TDIPS experiments were conducted under conditions known to induce association
i.e., in the presence of at least 1.75M NaCl and 2.0M ammonium sulphate for lysozyme and sav-
inase, respectively. The concentration of the NaAc buffer was kept constant in both the protein
plug and the surrounding analyte buffer (0.1M NaAc), while the selected polyols were added in in-
creasing concentrations in the analyte. Thus, the impact of polyols on the dispersion profiles could
be examined to determine whether their effects enhance or reduce self-association in the two dif-
ferent enzyme systems. In addition, data from DLS experiments were included to complement the
TDIPS findings, providing further insight into whether the polyols stabilize or disrupt the native,
folded state of the proteins.
28
==PAGE:42==
It must be emphasized, however, that TDIPS remains a semi-quantitative method, and there is no
standardized way to analyze or quantify the data. Despite this, specific qualitative features and
quantitative parameters have been included to indicate the degree of self-association and explain
the impact of the polyols.
Based on the article by Norrild et al. (2024), spike formation i.e. discrete signals occuring
after T 1
2 is a clear indicator of protein aggregation, as the appearance of large spikes in the disper-
sion profile directly reflects the presence of large particles. Consequently, the front integral will
increase due to the formation of spikes. Hence, this parameter is also highly indicative of aggre-
gation or phase separation and, hence, included [23]. As additional parameters, the decrease in
fluorescence for the monomeric peak has been included in this specific analysis, as a reduction
may indicate a lower concentration of the monomeric species. However, this decrease is not al-
ways due to the formation of aggregates, as it could also be caused by increases in the viscosity or
challenges with normalization, depending on the specific plot and given experimental conditions.
Lastly, the total sum of peak heights has been calculated by summing the peaks detected across
all concentrations for a given polyol and averaging them over the number of files. Arguably, this
parameter could be normalized according to the monomeric peak without salt (i.e. the negative
control) to standardize comparisons across polyols for future studies.
A significant challenge in this analysis is the normalization of data while accounting for significant
changes in viscosity, particularly considering the high-viscosity compounds utilized for this study.
In some systems, a high viscosity itself may stabilize or destabilize condensates by slowing the
stochastic Brownian motions of solutes, as discussed further in Sec. 3.4.1.1. To address potential
over-correction in the context of viscosity normalization, the detection location was slightly ad-
justed (e.g., from 0.84 m to 0.88 or 0.9 m in the tube) for highly viscous samples. This adjustment
is justified because the normalization point can be considered somewhat arbitrary, and more mean-
ingful information is generally obtained by analyzing the changes in monomeric peak heights and
widths.

summarizing the lowest and highest added concentrations, along with the decrease in monomeric

for both enzymes (cf. Tab. 3.5 and 3.6). In the analysis, possible molecular explanations will be
provided, and references will be made to these empirically obtained values.
In addition, throughout the analysis, references will be made to the calculated melting tem-

mechanisms behind these two phenomena, i.e., protein association and thermal unfolding, are not

3.4.1.1
Discussion of Viscosity Correction Approach
Although TDIPS has proven highly effective for HTS and investigations on the aggregation or
phase separation behavior of biomolecules, challenges remain. One pressing issue is proper vis-
cosity correction. A critical aspect of TDIPS analysis is the comparison of dispersion profiles,
which can provide valuable information through features such as broadening of the monomeric
peak. This can be more easily compared if the monomeric peak occurs at approximately the same
time. However, Tavg depends on flow velocity, which is influenced by both pressure and viscos-
ity. Therefore, proper normalization is essential for accurate analysis and comparison across the
different conditions.
Two major factors must be considered for viscosity correction: Firstly, calculating viscosity changes
based on the type and concentration of the additive, and secondly, accounting for the combination
effects of different components in the solution, which can further alter the viscosity. To address
this, the normalization of time in this thesis has been performed by using TDIPS viscosity reference
measurements with a protein plug in a surrounding buffer without salt but with the polyol added
in identical concentrations as during the screening experiments. However, the limitations of this
method must be acknowledged, and this thesis does not claim that changes in the viscosity follow
a quadratic relationship with increasing concentration. Viscosity is influenced by multiple factors,
such as dilution, temperature, and molecular interactions, making it highly difficult to establish
a mechanistic correlation. Instead, the polynomial fitting used here primarily serves to align and
compare the curves with the specific enzyme and polyol and extract relevant information. Despite
these limitations, meaningful data can still be obtained by investigating features such as the num-
ber of peaks, but even integrating the front integral has proven slightly challenging, as this also
depends on proper time-axis corrections.
Some viscosity corrections in this project may be inaccurate due to experimental challenges. For
instance, certain viscosity reference measurements had to be repeated because the original sam-
ples were too viscous to pass through the capillary within the required time using TDIPS Method
1. Additionally, slight variations in protein concentration after thawing stock solutions (e.g., 32
mg/mL vs. 34 mg/mL) likely introduced slight errors in the polynomial fits, contributing to over-
or under-correction for viscosity. However, the effect of such small concentration differences is
expected to be minor compared to the impact of the additives.
Several alternative strategies could be explored to improve viscosity normalization. One option
is to rely on literature values for the viscosity of additives, but this approach does not account for
molecular interactions between solvent components. Thus, experimental discrepancies are to be
expected. Another option is the inherent FIDA-1 software, which offers an automated viscosity
correction method, but this assumes the system behaves similarly to water, which does not always
hold true, especially in the presence of high-viscosity additives. Thirdly, using a small, fluores-
cent tracer molecule, such as fluorescein, could provide a more accurate measure of viscosity, as
its residence time should be proportional to the viscosity of the surrounding solution. Lastly, a
potential strategy is to normalize based on the time T 1
2 , as it is established that no signal should be
detected, though this approach is complicated in cases with either significant spike formation and
or nothing occurring at T 1
2 .

has been initiated. Thus, higher concentrations of trehalose are needed for both enzymes to pre-

3.5
Overall Tendencies: Thermal Unfolding Analysis
To complement the data obtained from TDIPS experiments and to explore how polyols influence
the thermal stability of the enzymes, i.e. the stability of the folded protein, screening was conducted
using nanoDSF to assess their impact on the thermal unfolding of the enzyme. An overview of the
general trends observed across the different additives will be provided. All the raw data plots used
to calculate the melting temperatures under each condition have been included in Appendix A.6,
organized in the order they appear. An overview of the obtained melting temperatures can be seen
in Tab. 3.7 and 3.10.
Initially, the proteins were diluted to a maximum concentration of 10 mg/mL for loading into
capillaries. The polyols were then added at both low and high concentrations (as indicated in Tab.
3.7 and 3.10), followed by the addition of NaCl or ammonium sulphate at levels known to induce
self-association. However, during sample preparation, precipitation was observed in the some of
the Eppendorf tubes even before loading. This issue compromised the homogeneous and accurate
measurement of some of the data sets, particularly the measurements of scattering and turbidity.
This is caused by the large particles from the precipitate, which, overwhelmingly, dominate the
scattering signal, thus skewing the results. Hence, some comparisons cannot be made as data is
lacking.
This was indicated by the Prometheus Panta software when running the tests, as it was stated the
the sample(s) exceeded the acceptable acquisition limits. For instance, as a rule of thumb, the
scattering intensities should lie between 1 and 1E+09 and within 0.2 of the median value for the
capillaries, but it was far outside the range for these samples. In addition, the signal-to-noise ratios
during sample acquisition should have been above 125. However, it was close to 0 for e.g. 0.5M
1.6-hexanediol upon addition to 10 mg/mL savinase in ammonium sulphate. Thus, certain graphs
and data obtained on the thermal unfolding, scattering, and turbidity data have been excluded for
the inaccurate samples.
The filtering criteria were partly based on sigmoidal fitting of the scattering and turbidity
measurements obtained. Moreover, the data was excluded if the raw data points were highly ran-
domly scattered, if the signal-to-noise ratios were exceedingly low, or if the R2 after fitting was
below 0.9. The fitted scattering plots have also been included in Appendix A.7. The melting
temperatures have been generated by plotting the measurements of fluorescence at 330 and 350,
respectively, and calculating the ratio between them. Afterwards, the point at which the minimum
value of the first derivative occurs has been determined to be the melting temperature, i.e., when
50% of the protein is in an unfolded state. Here, it must be noted that it was difficult to determine
initially, as some of the curves increased more gradually, leading to less steep slopes. Thus, it
was necessary to impose a range at which the midpoint was clearly seen to occur. Nonetheless,
this should be taken into consideration, as it means the reported melting temperatures may not be
completely accurate. Instead, the plots of the ratios should be taken into consideration and used to
assess the overall tendencies, not necessarily the exact melting temperatures.

presence of 1.0M sucrose, the melting temperature exceeds that of the negative control, increasing

surprising. On the other hand, sorbitol has a stabilizing effect, increasing the melting temperature

effect cannot be observed in Fig. 3.34, a, without ammonium sulphate. Meanwhile, 1.6-hexanediol

findings from the TDIPS experiments.

bilizing polyols like 1.6-hexanediol promote unfolding and destabilization. The fact that PEG-200
and ethylene glycol exhibit moderate stabilization suggests they may act as co-solvents by affect-
ing the protein’s hydration shell and reducing aggregation without being directly stabilizing of the
folded state, such as the sugars.

both in terms of what is measured and in what way. The thermal unfolding experiments can be
used to derive information on how the additives influence the folded, native vs. unfolded state.
Moreover, the experiments are conducted over a range of temperatures. In TDIPS experiments,
however, aggregation and/or phase separation behavior is assessed under different conditions but
at one set temperature. Sucrose can be considered as a case study, considering its ability to sta-
bilize proteins via. preferential hydration and reduce solvent-exposed hydrophobic regions, thus
increasing the energy barrier needed to unfold the protein.

interactions, crowding effects, and viscosity, must all be considered when interpreting the resulting
data. For instance, if sucrose stabilizes the hydration shell of lysozyme but increases the local
viscosity, leading to kinetic trapping of the protein, it might not prevent self-association. Hence,
it is crucial to keep in mind that different phenomena and parameters are investigated in thermal
unfolding and TDIPS experiments, and the findings from one another may not be translatable.

ever, with the exception of 1.6-hexanediol, which is strongly destabilizing of lysozyme’s folded
state. Noticeably, the trajectory of the plots for 0.5M and 1M 1.6-hexanediol indicates another

Future Work
There are still many aspects to be further explored as part of this project and in the broader inves-
tigation of protein association-modifying compounds. One example is how to improve and ensure
more accurate viscosity normalization, which would allow clearer and more meaningful compar-
isons between different aggregation- or LLPS-modifying conditions. For instance, in many of
the samples, it was observed that the monomeric peak flattened out and spikes disappeared at
high concentrations of added polyol. This could indicate either that there was an effect on the
association-behavior of the protein in the plug or that there was effectively less protein reaching
the detector due to the increased viscosity, as the same pressure was applied.
One potential approach to compensate for this issue could involve calculating the ratio be-
tween the viscosity of a reference solvent, such as water, and the sample. This ratio could sub-
sequently be used as a correction factor to the fluorescence measurements. For example, if the
sample’s viscosity is twice that of water, the fluorescence intensity could be multiplied by this fac-
tor to account for the smaller injection volume when using high-viscosity compounds. Although
this method does not precisely account for differences in the actual protein concentration, it may
offer a practical way to enable clearer comparative analyses, particularly for examining monomeric
peak broadening.
Additionally, more sophisticated computational models and simulations to predict protein aggre-
gation and phase separation will be invaluable. This study primarily relied on PyMOL and Ag-
greScan3D to estimate aggregation propensity. However, these tools are highly simplified and
lack the ability to, for instance, capture the dynamic molecular interactions over time. In a re-
cent study by Yang et al. (2024), coarse-grained molecular dynamics (MD) simulations were used
to predict the LLPS of repetitive polyproline and polyarginine peptides with varying lengths and
sequences in solution whilst accounting for different concentrations and temperatures [76]. The
predictions aligned well with the experimental observations. For instance, they were able to ac-
curately predict the necessary salt concentration of 2.7M for PR25-induced LLPS. In an extension
of this, machine-learning-assisted protein association predictors could perhaps be incorporated to
capture non-obvious patterns that current models cannot address.
Lastly, it is highly recommended that the phase separation behavior of savinase be investigated
further upon the addition of 1,6-hexanediol. This could be accompanied by spectroscopy tech-
niques, such as NMR or Circular Dichroism (CD). In particular, CD spectroscopy could be used
to examine if the structure remains unaffected. Overall, such methods could provide insights into
how polyols interact with enzymes, in particular 1,6-hexanediol-induced phase separation on a
molecular level.
From a broader perspective, the findings on the polyols should be further validated by exploring a
wider range of parameters, such as long-term stability and enzymatic activity, to better understand
their practical implications. Lastly, to thoroughly highlight the differences between the two en-
zymes, the experiments conducted in this thesis could be repeated using alternative combinations,
such as savinase with NaCl and lysozyme with ammonium sulphate. This could even be extended
to include different salts, pH ranges, or known mutants, thus providing a more comprehensive
understanding of how these enzymes respond to varying conditions.

5
Conclusion
The aim of this study was to investigate and quantify how different polyols influence the association
behavior and thermal stability of two enzymes, hen egg white lysozyme and Subtilisin Savinase.
This was achieved using biophysical characterization methods, including Taylor Dispersion In-
duced Phase Separation (TDIPS), nano-differential scanning fluorimetry (nanoDSF), and dynamic
light scattering (DLS).
Both enzymes, though globular, exhibited markedly different responses to the selected polyols,
which can be attributed to their inherent structural differences, such as their surface properties and
the different salts used to induce aggregation or phase separation. A common observation for both
enzymes is that glycerol, ethylene glycol, and PEG-200 effectively prevented aggregation, as ev-
idenced by the TDIPS experiments and the disappearance of spikes in their dispersion profiles.
The disaccharides, sucrose, and trehalose, also prevented association, with the most pronounced
impact on lysozyme. However, the required concentrations and effectiveness of the polyols varied
between the two enzymes, highlighting the importance of considering the exact molecular proper-
ties of the specific enzyme and the given solution conditions.
For lysozyme, 1,6-hexanediol, sucrose, and PEG-200 were particularly effective in prevent-
ing NaCl-induced liquid-liquid phase separation, as indicated by the significant reduction, or even
disappearance, of spikes in their dispersion profiles when added in relatively low concentrations.
Sorbitol also demonstrated a significant effect at 0.5M, as the fluorescence signals from the con-
densates completely disappeared. Furthermore, sorbitol not only improved protein solubility but
also increased the melting temperature of lysozyme by 3.02 C when 0.5M sorbitol was added in the
presence of 1.75M NaCl. This is in stark contrast with savinase, where the spikes stemming from
the aggregates persisted even after adding 1M sorbitol. However, in the presence of ammonium
sulphate, 1.5M sorbitol increased savinase’s melting temperature by 2.35 C. This is a key example
of the mechanisms driving the thermal unfolding of and protein association of an enzyme being
highly distinct.
In general, savinase responded much more variably to the polyols. In particular, savinase had
an exorbitant response to 1.6-hexanediol, which significantly reduced its stability and promoted
phase separation, even at relatively low concentrations of 0.125M. Although 1.6-hexanediol is
most frequently used to dissolve liquid-liquid phase-separated assemblies, light microscopy and
the TDIPS experiments indicated that it induced LLPS in savinase. In addition, an impactful ef-
fect was observed during the thermal unfolding analyses as well, as the melting temperature of
savinase was decreased by 9.62 C upon adding 1.5M 1,6-hexanediol without salt. Similarly, the
melting temperature was decreased 9.76 C after adding 1.0M 1,6-hexanediol in the presence of
2.0M ammonium sulphate. This suggests that 1.6-hexanediol not only affects savinase’s associa-
tion behavior but inherent thermal stability. This is, without doubt, a phenomenon that warrants
further investigation.
A key takeaway from these findings is that selecting polyols for enzyme stabilization is not a
straightforward “one-size-fits-all” approach. The effectiveness and impact of the polyols highly
depend on the enzyme’s surface properties and the surrounding solution conditions. This is demon-
strated by the vastly different responses of lysozyme and savinase to the same additives, particu-
larly 1.6-hexanediol and sucrose. Future work should focus on developing more accurate methods
for viscosity normalization, seeing as viscosity remains a significant challenge during the analy-
sis of protein association behavior. Additionally, the findings on the effects of polyols should be
substantiated by exploring a wider range of conditions, e.g. by conducting long-term stability and
53
==PAGE:67==
enzymatic activity assays. Nonetheless, elucidating and bringing attention to the molecular mech-
anisms behind polyols’ stabilizing or destabilizing effects will be crucial for optimizing enzyme
formulations in both industry and academia.

A.3
SDS-PAGE of Subtilisin Savinase following and without SEC
Figure A.2: SDS-PAGE gel of savinase taken directly from crystal slurry without SEC-purification. Gel
run with lysozyme and napin as controls. Lanes 1-4: Savinase. Lanes 5-9: Lysozyme. Lane 10: Napin.
It can be seen that the band for the savinase is clearest in lanes 1 and 2, in which the highest
concentration of 25 mg/mL and 8 mg/mL savinase is found. The top band occurs around 27 kDa,
which matches the theoretical size of savinase. However, a smaller band under 10 kDa appear. This
may be attributed to savinase’s role as a broad-spectrum protease. In crystal form, CaCl2 is used
as a crystallization agent, which helps maintain savinase in its crystalline form. Here, autolysis is
minimized as the enzyme is not in its active soluble state. However, it was informed by Novonesis
that, upon being re-solubilized, the risk of autolysis increases. This was also observed on the
SDS-PAGE gel that was run following the performance of size exclusion chromatography.
The main lysozyme band (lanes 4-8) appears at around 14 kDa, which matches its known molecular
weight. The intense band observed in lane 5 may be due to the overloaded gel, causing oversatu-
ration and the bands to appear thicker and smeared, also indicated by the streaking from the well.
The napin used as a control was included in lane 9 to reference whether gel staining and loading
were successful. Napin comprises two polypeptide chains, making up a small and large subunit.
The gel was run under reducing conditions, so the 9 kDa and 4 kDa chains were separate. It
was also informed that there was less of the 9 kDa chain for this specific sample. However, the
location of the bands may be skewed due to lysozyme having been overloaded and bleeding into
the neighboring lanes, including lanes 4 and 6 to 9, making it appear skewed upwards.
Note: For the first SDS-PAGE of the sample following SEC, the fractions eluted at 17.80 were
deemed to correspond to the molecular size of savinase. The gel and showed the exact pattern and
size of the degraded protein, even following ultra-centrifugation with a 3 kDa filter, indicating a
larger size. Since the bands were consistent with the ones that had been through SEC, the protein
stocks were made directly from the crystalline solution without SEC.
