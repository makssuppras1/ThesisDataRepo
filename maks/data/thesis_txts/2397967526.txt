Abstract
The hybridoma platform for antibody generation is widely used. However, the under-
standing of the origin of the B-cells from immunized mice that result in antibody secreting
hybridomas following electrofusion, is poorly described. This project aims to investigate
the origin of the hybridomas, by assessing VH genes from plasma cells and memory B-cells
and compare these with those isolated from hybridomas. To examine this, we selected
antigen-speciﬁc and IgG producing hybridomas using ELISA, after electrofusion with a
myeloma cell line and subsequently cloning and sequencing of these clones. Hybridoma
CDRH3 from VH sequences were compared to CDRH3 sequences obtained from sorted
plasma cells and memory B-cells.
We show that some of the CDRH3s in plasma cells and memory B-cells were identical to
CDRH3s identiﬁed in the hybridomas. This indicates that it is possible that both plasma
cells and memory B-cells can fuse with myeloma cells and generate antibody secreting
hybridomas. Although both cell types contributed to the broad antibody repertoire, we
found no overlap between CDRH3 sequences in 86-95% of the unique CDRH3s, identiﬁed
in the clones isolated by cell sorting and hybridoma clones. Thus for antibody discovery,
a signiﬁcant larger antibody repertoire can be isolated if a complementing platform is
included in addition to the hybridoma platform.

Resume
Hybridomplatformen anvendes i vid udstrækning til genering af antistoﬀer, men viden
om B-cellers oprindelse fra immuniserede mus der efter elektrofusion, resulterer i anti-
stofsekreterende hybridomer, er begrænset. Her undersøger vi oprindelsen ved at se på
plasmaceller og memory B-celler, og sammenligner disse cellers VH gener med tilsvarende
gener isoleret fra hybridomer. Antigenspeciﬁkke og IgG-producerende hybridomer blev
selekteret ved ELISA efter elektrofusion med en myelom cellelinie og efterfølgende klonet
og sekventeret. Hybridom CRDH3 fra VH sekvenser blev sammenlignet med CDRH3
sekvenser fra sorterede plasmaceller og memory B-celler.
Vi viste at nogle af CDRH3 sekvenserne i plasmaceller og memory B-celler var identiske
med CDRH3 identiﬁceret i hybridomer. Dette indikere at det er muligt, at både plasma-
celler og memory B-celler kan fusionere med myeloide celler og generere antistofudskillende
hybridomer. Selvom begge celletyper er med til at bidrage til bredden af antistofreper-
toiret, havde 86-95% af de unikke CDRH3, identiﬁceret i dette projekt, ingen overlap i
CDRH3 sekvenser mellem kloner isoleret ved cellesortering og fra hybridomkloner. Derfor
kan et større antistofrepertoire blive isoleret, hvis en komplementerende platform bliver
benyttet i forlængelse af hybridomplatformen.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Immunology
Immunology is the study of the host’s defence against infections and is divided into two
main groups; the innate and the adaptive immune response. Innate immunity is the ﬁrst
line of defence against foreign invaders, whereas adaptive immunity will be initiated while
the innate system tries to limit the damage of the invasion.
During an infection the
immune response undergo three phases; innate immunity, early induced innate response
and adaptive response. The innate immunity consists of physical barriers, such as skin and
epithelial mucus, to prevent invasion by pathogens. Furthermore, production of soluble
molecules, such as cytokines and antibodies, mediate activation of the complement cascade
to initiate clearance of pathogens. The second phase, the induced innate response is the
recruitment of eﬀector cells such as macrophages and phagocytes (neutrophils, eosinophils
and basophils) by activation of pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs). These
cells migrate to the site of infection and mediate their eﬀector functions to eliminate
the pathogens. The third phase is the adaptive immune response where non-activated
(naive) T-cells and B-cells are activated by antigens and undergo clonal expansion and
diﬀerentiation to obtain their eﬀector functions, and thereby be able to eliminate the
pathogen. The adaptive response provide long term immunity and comprise the memory
of the immune system [42].
Antibodies (Abs), also called immunoglobulins (Igs), are a part of both the innate and
the adaptive immunity. Antibodies have three main functions; 1) by binding to cognate
antigens (Ag) to mediate a response against this speciﬁc antigen by recruiting eﬀector cells
and other molecules, 2) neutralization of pathogens and 3) mediate complement activation
and thereby enhance lysis of pathogens. Antibodies are produced by activated B-cells

These B-cells have the possibility to undergo somatic hypermutation

1.1.1
Immunoglobulins
Structure
Immunoglobulins consist of two heavy-chains (H-chain) and two light-chains (L-chain)
with a mass of approximately 50 kDa and 25 kDa, respectively.
The H-chain of the
IgG isotype is formed by four domains and the L-chain is formed by two. Both the L-
and the H-chains have one variable domain (V) and one or three constant domains (C),
respectively [20]. These polypeptide chains are linked together with disulphide bonds and
hinge regions to form a "Y" shaped molecule, as seen in Figure 1.1. The top of the two
arms of the "Y" represents the V-region of the antibody with the amino-terminal ends of
the polypeptide chain. The bottom part of the "Y" is the C-region, named Fc fragment
(Fragment crystallizable) containing the carboxy-terminal end of the polypeptide chain.
The C-region is responsible for Fc receptor- and complement binding, and deﬁnes the
isotype of the antibody which depends on the gene rearrangement. Matured naive B-cells
co-express antibodies of the isotypes IgM and IgD, and after activation of the B-cell the
antibodies can undergo CSR which leads to isotype switching.
In addition to natural Ig formats, smaller fragments of Igs can be useful within research
and as therapeutics. The V-region of both the variable light-chain (VL) and variable
heavy-chain (VH) together with the constant light-chain (CL) and the constant heavy
domain 1 (CH1) is forming the Fab fragment (Fragment antigen binding), which has the
antigen binding activity. Another widely used fragment is the scFv (single-chain variable
fragment) which consists of the VL and VH linked together with a ﬂexible linker [42, 50].
Fab and Fc fragments are illustrated in Figure 1.1.
Each V-domain contains three complementarity-determining regions (CDRs) with high
diversity. CDRs are ﬂanked by gene segments called framework regions (FR) which con-
tributes to the right folding of the antibody. The FR regions contain multiple β strands
that are aligned in parallel and linked with disulphide bonds, forming a barrel shaped
molecule called a β barrel. The ﬂexible loops between the β strands are formed when the
β strands change direction and it is within these loops the CDRs are located. How these
β strands and loops are arranged can be seen in Figure 1.1A.

1.1.2
Gene organisation and V(D)J recombination
The diﬀerent V- and C-domains that Igs consist of are encoded in endogenous gene seg-
ments of approximately 330-390 base pairs (bp) corresponding to 110-130 amino acids
(aa). The gene assembly by recombination gives the antibody its speciﬁcity and diversity.
The human antibody genes are divided into three genetic loci; λ, κ and H-chain locus
[57]. Each locus contains diﬀerent gene segments, and joining these segments form an
immunoglobulin gene [27]. The V-domain of the H-chain is assembled by three diﬀerent
gene segments; the variable gene segment (V-gene), diversity gene segment (D-gene) and
joining gene segment (J-gene). The V-domain of the L-chain is composed of two gene
segments; V- and J-gene segments. The V-gene segment is the variable gene segment
that encodes most of the V-domain. The H-chain V-gene segment is denoted VH-gene,
and VL-gene for the V-gene segment on the L-chain. The J-gene is the joining segment
as it joins the V-domain with the C-domain and are denoted JH-gene or JL-gene. The
third gene segment is the diversity segment denoted DH-gene and is only located on the
H-chain. This extra segment gives the V-domain of the H-chain its hypervariability [34].
A fourth and smaller gene segment is located in front of the V-gene segment and is a signal
peptide called a leader-gene segment. The joining of these gene segments take place in
a process called V(D)J recombination, where exons are assembled and the combination
of the multiple genes of V,(D) and J results in the variable domain [50]. A schematic
illustration of the diﬀerent segments can be seen in Figure 1.3.

1.1.4
Development of B-cells
B-cells, also known as B-lymphocytes, are a part of the adaptive immune system. These
cells play an important role in the humoral immune response as they produce and secrete
antibodies, and retain an immunological memory as long-lived memory B-cells. Some
sub-populations of B-cells are short-lived and carries out their function for several weeks
and are then deleted by apoptosis. Others, as long-lived plasma cells, have a longer life
span and circulates within the peripheral system or are retained and maintained in the
bone marrow (BM), by continuously stimulation of various chemokines and cytokines [44].
If later infection of the same pathogen occur, memory B-cells can be activated due to the
immunological memory and this reactivation elicit a rapid secondary immune response.

In this chapter the main focus will be on the two major sub-populations of activated and
diﬀerentiated B-cells; plasma cells (PCs) and memory B-cells (MBCs).
The maturation of B-cells occur primarily in the BM, but also in the fetal liver and the
neonatal spleen. B-cells are derived from hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) and these cells
are multipotent progenitor cells (MPPs) that give rise to all blood cells by diﬀerentiating
into speciﬁc cell types, depending on which signals they receive through diﬀerentiation. A
surface bound tyrosine kinase receptor is expressed on MPPs, which binds to a ligand on
connective tissue cells (stromal cells) in the BM. It is in close connection to these stromal
cells that MPPs diﬀerentiate into common lymphoid progenitor cells (CLP) and further
into Early progenitor B (pro-B) cells. At this stage the cell starts D-J recombination of
the Ig heavy-chain (IgH) and with a successful outcome, the cell proceeds to the next stage
designated a Late pro-B-cell. At this stage, rearrangement of the V-DJ gene segments
of the VH-region occurs.
The gene rearrangement occurs on one chromosome and if
the rearrangement is not a productive joint the process will be repeated on the other
chromosome [5, 25]. The development of B-cells in the BM is illustrated in Figure 1.7.

1.1.5
Activation of naive B-cells
Naive B-cells circulate within the secondary lymphoid organs, such as lymph nodes (LNs),
tonsils and spleen etc. They travel through the blood vessels into the spleen, and through
the high endothelial venules (HEVs) into the LN. B-cells are led to the spleen and LN by
diﬀerent chemokines expressed by e.g. HEVs [16]. Here the naive B-cell can encounter
and recognise antigens presented by APCs such as follicular dendritic cells (FDCs) and
macrophages. B-cells can also encounter antigens in the blood, in the LN entering through
the HEV, in the marginal zone within the spleen etc.
Following antigen encounter, the B-cell internalize and degrade the pathogen and then
present small peptides from the antigen through MHC class II (MHC II) [16]. Afterwards,
the B-cell migrate towards the LN and the border between the T-cell zone and a follicle
consisting of a cluster of naive B-cells. MHC II is a surface bound receptor on the B-cell
that presents foreign degraded peptides from the antigen to the T-cell receptor (TCR)
on the surface of an activated T-cell, responding to the same antigen. These T-cells have
earlier on encountered an APC with the same antigen resulting in diﬀerentiation into e.g.
a helper T-cell which are able to activate a naive B-cell. The engagement of the surface
receptor CD40 on the B-cell with the ligand CD40L on the T-cell, along with the MHC
II:TCR interaction, mediates the T-cell to produce cytokines that stimulates the B-cell
to be activated, see Figure 1.9.

antigen aﬃnity and is outperformed by high-aﬃnity cells, the low-aﬃnity B-cell will not

CXCR4 or CXCR5. In the LZ, FDCs express the ligand CXCL13 which attract both TFH
cells and B-cells with CXCR5 on the surface [10]. The B-cells also express CXCR4 and
when CXCR4 is up-regulated, the cell will migrate to the DZ where the ligand CXCL12
is produced. When the CXCR4 expression is down-regulated, the cells re-enter the LZ
[2, 4].
1.1.6
Somatic hypermutation
Somatic hypermutation is a process where the Ig V-region (IgV) is mutated to obtain
a higher aﬃnity towards an antigen. These alterations are often point mutations, yet
deletions and insertions are also observed. These mutations are limited to the rearranged
Ig genes and starts approximately 150 bp downstream of the transcription start/promotor
of the V-gene segment with exponential decreasing mutation rate [40, 45]. The SHM
process has a higher frequency in nucleotide motifs as WRC (W = A/T and R = A/G),
and motif AGY (Y = T/C) occurs in CDR regions with a high frequency [9, 40]. SHM
occurs during the cell cycle and the transcription of the DNA in the dividing cell. The
process especially relies on the enzyme Activation-induced cytidine deaminase (AID) that
is located in the cytoplasm of activated B-cells only. It has been indicated that it can be
actively transported between cytoplasm and nucleus where it carries out the conversion
of a cytidine to an uridine in the sequence [45]. It relies on the transcription of genes

on pyrimidine rings on a cytidine and deaminates it, resulting in a transformation to an

pathway called base-excision repair, where the enzyme uracil-DNA glycosylase (UNG)

1.1.7
Class switching rearrangement
CSR is the process where the B-cell can undergo isotype switching from IgM and IgD to
IgA, IgG and IgE and thereby altering the eﬀector function of the antibody. The process
of isotype switching by class switching rearrangement is illustrated in Figure 1.11.
Isotype switching is stimulated by encounter with an antigen and the CD40:CD40L inter-
action with TFH cells. As for SHM, the enzyme AID plays an important role in CSR. AID
uses non-template strands of repetitive DNA sequences, the switch regions (S-regions),
that lies downstream the JH segment and upstream of each C-gene segments, except Cδ-
chain which is the only one missing a S-region. These S-regions are rich on G nucleotides

and contain multiple repeats of AGCT and GGGG [62]. In CSR, AID starts to function
when the double stranded DNA is open in two of the S-regions while being transcribed.

to abasic sites as in SHM. Hereafter, the enzyme apurinic/apyrimidinic endonuclease 1

1.1.8
Memory B-cells
Memory B-cells are one of the sub-populations that naive B-cells can diﬀerentiate into
upon activation by antigen, either by a free circulating antigen or presented by APCs.
MBCs holds the immunological memory from earlier infections and are located in the BM,
and secondary lymphoid organs and they circulate within the blood system, waiting for
re-exposure of its cognate antigen.
The primary response to an antigen is the process where a naive B-cell recognizes a speciﬁc
antigen and starts to diﬀerentiate. It takes a couple of days before diﬀerentiation of naive
B-cells and antibody production occurs, see Figure 1.12.
The secondary response is much faster with a higher eﬃcacy and antibody production.
Re-exposure of an antigen to long-lived MBCs elicit a diﬀerentiation of these into antibody
secreting PCs with a much shorter lag phase and with a larger production of antibodies.
This is due to the already existing antigen-speciﬁc high aﬃnity BCRs on the MBCs. It is
thought that one reason for the fast and highly eﬃcient secondary response can be caused
by immune complexes formed by antibodies from the primary response and re-exposure of
antigens. Immune complexes can activate the classical complement pathway. Interaction
between the complement B-cell co-receptor CD21 with the complement proteins from
the complement pathway, accelerates and enhances re-activation of MBCs [23]. Thus,
presence of antibodies from the primary response and interaction with antigens from the
re-exposure leads to formation of immune complexes, which can initiate a fast re-activation
of MBCs and a faster response.

1.1.9
Plasma cells
Diﬀerent subsets of PCs and plasmablasts exist and these diﬀerentiate at diﬀerent stages in
the B-cell development. Some become short-lived and others long-lived, but they all have
in common that the diﬀerentiation is a terminal diﬀerentiation and thereby an irreversible
tightly controlled process. The PCs secrete antibodies of varying aﬃnity depending on
whether or not they have undergone aﬃnity maturation. PCs or plasmablasts can orig-
inate from naive marginal B-cells, activated follicular B-cells, post-GC B-cells and from

1.2
Antibody Technology
As antibodies are antigen-speciﬁc and can be directed against speciﬁc targets, they have
over the last decades been an area of signiﬁcant research and commercial interest. Various
technologies and platforms have been developed to obtain better antibodies with higher
aﬃnity, larger repertoires and higher production eﬃciency. Also, antibodies have been
developed to obtain antibodies, or fragments, with various functions and lower immuno-
genicity.
1.2.1
Use of antibodies
The use of antibodies cover ﬁelds of diagnostic, experimental research, therapy and drug
delivery within medicine.
Due to the positive treatment outcome in various diseases,
it has become one of the biggest areas within biologics.
As antibodies are composed
of polypeptide chains with diﬀerent functions and conformations, scientists have been
experimenting with diﬀerent formats such as Fab, scFv and bi-speciﬁc antibodies. In the
laboratory, antibodies are widely used in immunoassays, where they can target speciﬁc
molecules to quantify serum levels of drugs or antibodies. Identiﬁcation of cell populations
and development stages by cell surface receptors and ligands with antibodies as markers,
are also widely used within research [46].
Antibodies generated in for example mice are immunogenic when used in humans. Un-
wanted immunogenicity is the induction of an immune response towards a therapeutic,
as the substance is considered foreign for the body. Here, the body starts to produce
neutralizing antibodies against the therapeutics. These immunogenic antibodies can be
less useful as therapeutics as they can have a reduced eﬃcacy due to eg. neutralization by
the immune system. To overcome the problem with immunogenicity, humanized antibod-
ies have been developed. When humanizing antibodies, various domains and sequences
are changed from murine to human domains, to minimize the immunogenicity and still
retaining the binding properties.
1.2.2
Antibody discovery platforms
Diﬀerent platforms are used to generate antibodies and some of the most applied platforms
are phage and yeast display, and the hybridoma platform.

Display platforms
The display of antibody fragments on the surface of a cell or a phage has been used
to discover antibodies for more then 30 years.
Advantages with these platforms are
the possibility to build large libraries, easy engineering of the fragments and no use of
animals and thereby no issues regarding tolerance.
A disadvantage with the display
platforms is that low-aﬃnity monoclonal antibodies are usually obtained, which often
need optimization. In addition, antibodies from in vitro systems tend to be associated
with biophysical issues such as aggregation, low yield and unstability, since selection has
been done ex vivo. Two of the most widely used display platforms are phage and yeast
display, which will be described in the following sections.
Phage display
The phage display technology was ﬁrst utilized by G. Smith in 1985 [54] with the display
of a peptide fragment on ﬁlamentous phages.
He demonstrated display of an EcoRI
endonuclease fragment on the surface of a phage by insertion of the fragment into gene
III, that encodes the pIII coat protein of the phage. By inserting the fragment between
the carboxyl-terminal and the amino-terminal end of gene III, its infectious function of
pIII binding to the F-pilus of the Escherichia coli (E.coli) was not disrupted and the
phage was able to infect and insert genes into the E.coli for ampliﬁcation. This method
was proposed as a way of cloning gene segments when an antibody speciﬁc for the encoded
protein was available, so it was possible to capture the phage with the displayed fragment.
The antibody phage display platform utilize displaying of antibody fragments eg. scFv or
Fab on the surface of a ﬁlamentous phage, to select fragments with the desired speciﬁcity.
With this technology, fragments can be expressed in bacteria such as E.coli and thereby
generating phage particles displaying an antibody fragment on the surface while containing
the coding DNA in the phage particle.
The process of selection of fragments with a
certain speciﬁcity often starts with a Fab or scFv library. These large libraries, ideally
109 to 1011 diﬀerent fragments, can be generated in diﬀerent ways either as a synthetic
library, generated based on the antibody repertoire from naive B-cells obtained from the
lymphoid organs, or from repertories from immunized animals [12].
To generate display on phages, the gene sequence of the fragment is inserted in a plasmid
in connection with an encoded part of pIII, which are transfected into E.coli. Larger
proteins such as Fab’s are often inserted in the amino-terminal end of pIII. This inter-
rupts the phage natural way of infecting bacteria and helper phages are needed to produce
functionally phages. By transfection of the plasmid into E.coli and introduction of helper-
phages, multiple phages are produced. To select the fragments with the right speciﬁcity,

Yeast display
Similar to phage display, antibody fragments have been displayed on mammalian cells,
such as E.coli and Saccharomyces cerevisiae (yeast), and of these yeast display is the most
utilized. Similar to phage display, yeast display uses the technique of insertion of fragments
to a surface anchor protein of the host to be able to present up to 10,000 fragments
on the cell surface. With this platform it is possible to display full antibodies on the
surface and not only fragments. Yeast display uses the same principles as phage display,
where selection of displayed fragments or antibodies by immobilized phases, elution and
subsequent ampliﬁcation is utilized. As for panning, the selection of fragments by yeast
display also takes several cycles of selection and ampliﬁcation to obtain a high fraction of
speciﬁc clones [8, 13].
Hybridoma platform
The hybridoma technology was developed by Köhler and Milstein in 1975 [32]. They were
the ﬁrst to use hybridoma technology to produce large amounts of monoclonal antibod-
ies by fusion of spleen cells with a myeloma cell line. Ever since, this technology has
been extensively used worldwide to generate high aﬃnity monoclonal antibodies. The hy-
bridoma platform utilizes the antibody repertoire generated by primary B-cells, combined
with an immortal cell line (myelomas) for continuous cell growth. Within the formation
and selection of hybridomas, three cell types are present; unfused primary cells, unfused
myelomas and fused hybridomas. Primary cells can not replicate in cultures, thus un-
fused cells will die. Addition of aminopterin blocks the de novo DNA nucleotide synthesis
pathway in myelomas. Usually, when blocking the de novo pathway, the salvage path-
way of nucleotide synthesis is the alternative pathway, when hypoxanthine and thymidine
(HT) are present along with the enzyme hypoxanthine-guanine phosphoribosyltransferase
(HGPRT). Myelomas do not express HGPRT and are not able to produce nucleotides by
any of the two pathways by it self, and thereby can not replicate in cultures.
Fused
myelomas inherit the HGPRT from the primary cells and are able to replicate in HAT
(hypoxanthine-aminopterin-thymidine) rich medium. Once all unfused cells have been
eradicated, there are no longer need for aminopterin selection supplement and medium
containing HT is used as a ’rescue medium’ for the hybridomas until they are able to
synthesise nucleotides by them self [32, 48].
Splenocytes are primary cells containing diﬀerent subsets of B-cells that by fusion with
myeloma cells generates hybridomas, which can be cultured and grown continuously while
secreting antibodies. A widely used myeloma cell line is the X63-Ag8.653 (X63), which is
22
==PAGE:36==

an aminopterin-sensitive cell line derived from the BM. X63 does not express heavy- and
light-chains, thus all antibody generation will originate from the fused B-cells [29].
Fusion of cell membranes are achieved by either polyethylene glycol (PEG) or electrofu-
sion. PEG was previously the preferred method as this procedure was easy to conduct and
did not require special equipment. It utilizes the exclusion of water molecules between
cells in close contact and thereby exploits the osmotic pressure to merge membranes.
Contrary, the method of electrofusion is a less invasive technique and gives a higher yield
of fused cells. For the fusion to take place, electric impulses are used to mediate the fu-
sion of cell membranes. By the process of electroporation of the membrane, permeability
is increased by application of electric pulses. When cells are in close encounter due to
alignment of the polarized cells by the electric ﬁeld, and the energy barrier is lowered,
hydrophilic pores are formed in the membranes and they are able to fuse. To obtain a
high fusion yield and ensure the viability of fused cells, a number of parameters such as
amplitude, number of pulses, pulse duration, fusion media composition etc. needs to be
adjusted depending on cell size. The ratio of splenocytes and myelomas, and cell concen-
tration is also important to ensure that cells are close enough for the fusion to take place
[31, 47].
Despite the wide use of the hybridoma platform, fusion yield is relatively low and is
usually only 1:1,000 to 1:10,000 per used splenocyte [47]. Another limitation is that it is
a very labour intensive platform with maintenance of cell cultures, extensive screening,
ampliﬁcation and antibody puriﬁcation.
1.2.3
Development of antibodies by immunization
Production of antibodies in animals utilize the inherited process of the immune system,
where foreign antigens will mediate a B-cell response, resulting in the generation of an-
tibodies. By immunization of animals with an antigen, the animals immune system will
start an innate response followed by an adaptive response. Naive B- and T-cells with
receptors able to bind the antigen will be activated, GCs will be formed, and the aﬃnity
maturation and class switching process will be initiated. By this process, antigen-speciﬁc
antibodies are generated and these can be measured in the blood serum. Immunized an-
imals are used for hybridoma generation, to generate polyclonal antibodies, and to build
immune libraries for phage and yeast display.
To obtain the right immune response from the immunized animal, it is important to con-
sider various aspects of the immunization strategy. Antigen concentration, dose, route

of immunization, adjuvant systems, interval between immunizations, boosts etc. are ad-
justable parameters for optimization of immunization protocols [14].
Adjuvant systems are used to stimulate the animals immune system by diﬀerent mecha-
nisms; a slower release of the antigen to the immune cells by depot formation, recruitment
of immune cells and increased antigen uptake, which activates APCs and thereby initiat-
ing the production of immunomediators such as cytokines. APCs such as dendritic cells
will migrate to the site of injection and by stimulation of their pattern recognition recep-

Activated APCs will interact with naive T-cell and activate these by receptor:ligand in-

adjuvant to boost the immune system with no depot eﬀect to obtain a high antibody titer.
To examine whether immunized animals have responded eﬃcient to an antigen, screening
of antibody speciﬁcity and expression can be tested by immunoassays.
1.2.4
Transgenic animals
Transgenic animals have been used for generation of high aﬃnity antibodies that can be
used as therapeutics. To minimize the risk of immunogenicity caused by some antibodies,
transgenic animal models can be used. Here, Ig gene segments have been replaced by
human Ig gene segments. This process has become widely used in the past few decades
[11].
In early transgenic models, it was diﬃcult to insert large amounts of human Ig loci into
the host endogenous Ig loci and this limited the diversity of the antibody repertoire. The
use of embryonic stem cells have enabled integration of larger gene segments with the
help of yeast artiﬁcial chromosomes or bacterial artiﬁcial chromosomes (BACs). These
methods were used by various researchers. However, transgenic models containing fully
human antibodies exhibited impaired antibody generation compared to wildtype (wt).
This problem was proposed to be caused by less eﬃcient BCR signalling, due to the
human C-region. BCRs are involved in the signalling to activate naive B-cells and also
for the B-cell to undergo SHM and CSR in the GC. This problem was solved by chimeric
antibodies with human V-regions combined with endogenous C-regions to maintain the
right BCR function and a high cellular signal [11, 35].

Transgenic KymouseTM
The company Kymab c⃝has developed a chimeric antibody platform called KymouseTM.
This platform enables generation of antibodies with human V-regions and murine C-
regions, for antibodies to recognise antigens as regular fully human antibodies and still
with an eﬀective BCR signalling inside the mice, leading to activation of naive B-cells and
subsequent SHM and CSR [11, 35].
Kymab c⃝overcame the problem with insertion of full human Ig loci by developing a
technology they called sequential recombinase-mediated cassette exchange (S-RMCE),
where they were able to insert full V-regions from human genomes into HGPRT deﬁcient
mouse embryonic stem cells, in precise locations without deleting any genes from the
mouse. This resulted in fully fertile and functional mice expressing antibodies with aﬃnity
maturated and class switched antibodies with human V-regions and murine C-regions.
Kymab c⃝originally produced two diﬀerent strains, the HK with the κ light-chain and
HL with the λ light-chain. As these two L-chains diﬀer in their orientation of genes,
the process for engineering the two KymouseTM strains diﬀered. For HK KymouseTM,
human H-chain and κ L-chain V-gene segments were inserted upstream the C-gene loci of
the mouse. For HL KymouseTM a full human λ L-chain (both the variable and constant
gene loci) were inserted downstream the murine J- to C-gene cluster.
To silence the
endogenous V-region loci in the mice, a method of chromosomal inversion was used to
invert and reposition the murine gene far away in the chromosome, in order to prohibit
it from interacting in recombination [35].
The process in which human Ig loci were inserted in the mouse genome was based on
insertion of BACs, which worked as a package containing a speciﬁc amount of the human Ig
loci. Several BACs were used to insert full human variable genes and after each insertion,
a check point of successful BAC incorporation was performed before the next BAC was

Chapter 2
Project aim
Isolation of monoclonal antibodies by hybridoma technology relies on fusion between B-
cells with an immortal myeloma cell line. In theory, a multitude of cells can fuse and
give rise to immortal clones, of which a subset will produce antibodies against a target of
interest.
For the generation of antigen-speciﬁc hybridomas, mice are traditionally immunized re-
peatedly until an antibody titer has developed against the antigen, after which the mice
are given a booster injection and the spleen is harvested for fusion. The outcome of a
fusion can vary despite the presence of a good titer against the antigen. Conﬂicting re-
ports exist about the origin of the B-cells giving rise to antibody producing hybridomas
following fusion. Both MBCs and PCs have been suggested to be the origin [49, 51], yet
no study has convincingly shown which diﬀerentiation state B-cells have when giving rise
to IgG producing hybridomas.
In the present study we seek to examine which B-cell populations give rise to IgG pro-
ducing hybridomas. This will be pursued by comparing the VH-gene sequences from the
repertoire of antibodies isolated by hybridoma technology with the VH sequences from
sorted plasma cells and memory B-cells of immunized mice.
Very often a highly diverse and high aﬃnity antibody repertoire is desired in order to
isolate an antibody with the desired properties. If the B-cell origin of antibody produc-
ing hydridomas can be identiﬁed, it extends the insight of how broad a repertoire the
hybridoma platform covers.
It has been described that the hybridoma platform only
generates viable hybridomas from a small fraction of B-cells used in the fusions [47], sug-
gesting that additional diversity can be achieved by including complementing antibody
generating platforms.

Cell sorting by ﬂow cytometry

possible to conﬁrm the exact identity of the sorted MBCs based on the selected surface
marker, as no good marker set exists for murine MBCs, but cells will be referred to as
MBCs in the following.
At each sorting a PE-isotype control was included as a control for PC sorting. If unspeciﬁc
binding of the PE-ﬂuorophore occurred, false positive cell counts will appear in the ’PC
gate’.
Similar, a control for the MBC sorting was included, where a cell sample was
unstained with biotinylated antigen. If unspeciﬁc binding of CF555-Strep occurred, cell
count would be seen in the ’MBC gate’. No unspeciﬁc binding of FITC was observed. A
ﬁnal optimized staining protocol for MBCs and PCs is included in appendix A.

3.4.1
RNA puriﬁcation
Total RNA was puriﬁed by using QIAshredder column (QIAgen, cat. no. 79654) and

from Thermo Scientiﬁc.

SMARTer II A nucleotides is illustrated in Figure 3.1, along with PCR ampliﬁcation.

3.5
Data analysis
After sequencing of the samples, sequences along with chromatograms and quality scores
were downloaded from Euroﬁns Genomics’ website. First, all sequences were processed
with the software AbAnalyzer (in-house software at Novo Nordisk) where sequences were
analysed against a database of VH genes from a selected specie. Data were here compared
to sequences of human and mouse VH genes, and CDRs were identiﬁed by AbAnalyzer
based on the Kabat numbering system. The number of SHM in CDRs and FRs were
calculated by AbAnalyzer for each sequence by comparing the similarity of the sequence
with the database in the software. To assess the quality of the obtained sequences, four
diﬀerent requirements were set:
• If higher similarity to murine VH sequences than human VH, the sequence was
analysed in BLAST to investigate the origin.
If the origin of the sequence was
non-human the sequence was discarded from the data set
• If the quality score was below 49 (no unit provided), the sequencing data was dis-
carded (based on review of chromatograms to assess the limit of the quality score)
• If the sequence included a stop codon (X) the sequence would not be functional and
was discarded
• If the calculated number of SHMs were ≥20 SHM, sequences were discarded
First, the quality of the data was assessed in regards to above mentioned requirements.
Second, remaining CDRH3 sequences were aligned in groups in regards to the sample
type (antigen-speciﬁc hybridomas, IgG hybridomas, PCs and MBCs) using the software
Geneious version 10.2.2.
Heat matrix of each group were generated to easily identify
sequences with similar CDRH3 (≥90% similarity).
These selected samples based on
CDRH3 similarity were collected and aligned together to assess whether they originated
from the same B-cell clone, based on point mutations, deletions and insertions in the
CDRH3.

Discussion and conclusion
The overall goal of the project was to examine the origin of B-cells that, following electro-
fusion with myeloma cells, resulted in antibody secreting hybridomas. This widely used
platform produces a broad repertoire of antibodies, yet there are conﬂicting reports re-
garding the B-cell origin of antibody secreting hybridomas. M. Seifert et. al. reports that
hybridomas phenotypically are similar to PCs [51], whereas E. Schmidt et. al. reports that
surface markers on hybridomas, such as CD19 or Ig, supports the claim that MBCs are
also an origin of hybridomas [49]. To investigate and answer the overall aim of the project,
the gene repertoire of four sample types were examined and compared; antigen-speciﬁc
producing hybridomas, IgG producing hybridomas, PCs and MBCs. This information
would be valuable for antibody discovery using hybridoma technology, as more knowl-
edge about which cell type that is the major source of antibody producing hybridomas,
could provide useful information about the value of including complementary antibody
discovery platforms.
In this project, a total of seven fusions were conducted and ﬁve of these were used in sorting
of PCs and MBCs, with subsequent cloning and sequencing of all ﬁve sorted fusions. From
these cloned and sequenced fusions, two (fusion #5 and #7) yielded sequences from all
four sample groups with an acceptable quality, which were necessary to obtain a complete
comparison between the two discussed platforms; cell sorting and hybridoma platform.
The quality of the data sets was evaluated and within the ﬁnal data sets, 516 sequences
constituted the data from fusion #5 and 371 sequences from fusion #7, distributed on
all four sample types. The sequence comparisons were based on the CDRH3, as it is
a combination of both V-, D- and J-gene segments, and this region is unique to each
antibody. CDRH3 was used to identify clones, and possible overlapping clones between
sample groups. Comparisons of the CDRH3s were used to generate a heat matrix based on
66
==PAGE:80==
CHAPTER 6. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
similarity between sample types from the two data sets. The heat matrix illustrated the
diversity and how broad a repertoire each sample type was comprised of. From the heat
matrix, CDRH3s with a similarity of minimum 90% were selected for further alignment
to clarify the origin of the hybridomas. The obtained overlaps of the CDRH3s between
diﬀerent sample types indicated that both MBCs and PCs in the two fusions could be
involved in hybridoma generation. From fusion #5 one sequence, overlapping between
MBCs and hybridomas, was identiﬁed along with ten overlapping sequences between PCs
and hybridomas. Thus, for fusion #5 it seems that PCs were the major contributor to
the hybridoma generation. In fusion #7, six diﬀerent CDRH3s were observed between
MBCs and Ag2-speciﬁc hybridomas, but only one CDRH3 overlapped. Thus, in total for
fusion #7 two overlaps were identiﬁed between MBCs and hybridomas. For comparison,
four diﬀerent overlapping CDRH3s were identiﬁed between PCs and the hybridomas in
fusion #7. It seems that PCs here also contributed with the highest diversity, but as
indicated by the results both PCs and MBCs are able to fuse with myeloma cells. Since
MBCs can diﬀerentiate into PCs after being processed in the GC or at reactivation when
re-exposure of an antigen, it is not possible to clarify whether the overlapping CDRH3 is
from a hybridoma fused by a MBC or a hybridoma fused by a PC, that was a diﬀerentiated
clone of the MBC [5, 25].
Based on the results, the hybridoma platform does not seem to be entirely limited to one
B-cell type. Although it is not completely clear which B-cell type is the major contributor
to IgG producing hybridomas, it is clear that a signiﬁcant number of cells do not get fused,
as it has been shown that only 1:1,000 or 1:10,000 produce viable fusions per splenocytes
[47].
The identiﬁcation of shared CDRH3s between the hybridoma platform and the
sorting platform were between 5-14% based on the obtained unique CDRH3s in the data
sets. Thus, a large portion of the repertoire from the immunized mice would not have been
acquired if only the hybridoma platform had been used. It was expected to be impossible
to identify overlap for all of the repertoire between the two used platforms, as there can
be some bias in the cloning process. Furthermore, the number of obtained sequences from
the PC and MBC samples were smaller than the counted number of sorted cells, thus some
of the repertoire was not represented. The use of cells pooled from three mice in fusion
#7 was expected to increase the repertoire and thereby limit the discovery of overlapping
CDRH3s between sample types. This is a possible reason for the lower counts of overlaps
of 5% instead of 14% as in fusion #5. Furthermore, 35% of the included sequences in
the data set from fusion #7 contained unique CDRH3s, compared to only 13% in fusion
#5. This corresponds to the expected result of more unique CDRH3s in fusion #7 but
no increase in observed overlaps.

CHAPTER 6. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
It should be noted that the diversity within some of the samples were small and results
should therefore be interpreted with caution. To increase the quality of the data, other
cloning or sequencing approaches could be pursued. For example, next generations se-
quencing could circumvent the problem with few sequences in the sorted samples, as
millions of reads can be obtained in a single run. Another suggestion would be to perform
single cell cloning on sorted cells. This could prevent any ampliﬁcation bias that could
be present when cloning in pools.
We chose to use two diﬀerent primers for the VH ampliﬁcation with a nested primer for
the second PCR, as a nested primer will result in a more speciﬁc PCR product. We did
however observe multiple bands after gel analysis, indicating the presence of possible un-
speciﬁc PCR products. These were not sequenced and it is unknown whether these bands
could contain VH genes with a longer UTR, as the length of the upstreaming UTR in
KymouseTM can vary and is unknown to us. Although the used primer set should be gene
speciﬁc, it is possible that some unspeciﬁc annealing could occur, resulting in unspeciﬁc
bands. Optimization of the primer set could therefore be considered. Furthermore, a
negative control was included in the PCR followed by gel analysis (for fusion #7), which
resulted in a band at 650 bp where GOI was expected. The origin of the band from the
control should have been examined to look into possible contamination risks and it is
recommended to consider this in the future and take necessary precautions.
In conclusion, this project supports the claim that both PCs and MBCs can fuse and
generate antibody secreting hybridomas as suggested by Schmidt [49].
The degree of
participation of the cell types can not be concluded by the obtained results due to the small
data set. Further studies needs to be conducted in order to determine the contributions
from the two cell types. Even though it seems that both PCs and MBCs can participate
in cell fusion, it is clear that a signiﬁcant portion of the antibody repertoire present in PCs
and MBCs, is not captured in the hybridoma repertoire. Therefore, an introduction of a
complementing antibody discovery platform to the hybridoma platform could signiﬁcantly
increase the antibody repertoire.

Appendix A
Optimized staining protocol for
sorting of MBCs and PCs
Cells were thawed and washed twice with FACS buﬀer (1xPBS + 2%FCS + 0.01%
NaAzid) and re-suspended in 3 mL FACS buﬀer containing 5% rat serum to block Fc
receptors on cell surface. Cells were incubated 15 minutes on ice before staining with
antibodies. Cells were counted on a cell counter Vi-cellTM XR (Beckman Coulter) and
distributed on ﬁve diﬀerent samples with 0.5 x 106 cells for sample 1-3 and the rest split
between sample 4 and 5 for cell sorting (see below).
Sample (no. 5) was incubated with 2 µg/mL biotinylated-Ag1 or 50 µg/mL biotinylated-
Ag2. Samples which were not subjected to Biotin-Ag incubation were added FACS buﬀer.
Samples were incubated for 30 minutes on ice. After incubation the cells were washed
with FACS buﬀer, followed by centrifugation at 300 x g for ﬁve minutes. The supernatant
was discarded and the cells stained with following concentration:
• Sample 1 (Negative control): No staining
• Sample 2 (Plasma cell staining with PE isotype control): 1 µg/mL FITC-CD45R
and 1 µg/mL PE-isotype
• Sample 3 (Memory B-cell staining with CF555 conjugate, ÷ biotin-Ag1/biotin-Ag2):
30 µg/mL FITC-IgG and 1 µg/mL CF555-Strep
• Sample 4 (Plasma cell staining): 1 µg/mL FITC-CD45R and 1 µg/mL PE-CD138
• Sample 5 (Memory B-cell staining): 30 µg/mL FITC-IgG and 1 µg/mL CF555-Strep
The samples were incubated for 30 minutes on ice protected from sunlight, centrifuged
at 300 x g for ﬁve minutes and supernatants discarded.
Cells were washed twice in
FACS buﬀer and re-suspended in 250 nM Sytox Blue (DNA binding dye) in FACS buﬀer.
