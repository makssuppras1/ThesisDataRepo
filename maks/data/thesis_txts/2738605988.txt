Abstract
Listeria monocytogenes and Escherichia coli are two pathogenic bacteria present in milk.
A pasteurization of raw milk used for any kind of product meant for human consumption
is by law required. If an alternative unit operation is used instead, then it is required to
document that the level of food safety obtained would be as sufficient as pasteurization.
UVC treatment is a promising alternative, but understanding different factors affecting the
enumeration of microbial survivors and, therefore, the UVC resistance is important to doc­
ument to determine the level of food safety.
The influence of four different recovery media, two selective media (MacConkey/Palcam
agar and Petrifilm) and two non­selective media (TSA and Blood agar) on the UVC re­
sistance parameters shoulder length, inactivation rate, and dose for 4D were tested in a
collimated beam apparatus with a mixing batch reactor. The three individual inoculum
were E. coli ATCC 25922, L. innocua ATCC 51742, and L. monocytogenes ATCC 19115.
MacConkey for E. coli and the two selective media for Listeria spp. were found to predict
a lower resistance than for the non­selective media. E. coli ATCC 25922 required a min­
imum UVC exposure of 4.88 mJ/cm2 ± 0.10 (MacConkey) compared to a minimum UVC
exposure of 5.34 mJ/cm2 ± 0.35 for non­selective media (Blood agar). Similarly for L.
innocua ATCC 51742 required up to 7.85 mJ/cm2 ± 0.23 of for selective media (Listeria
petrifilm) compared to a minimum of 8.57 mJ/cm2 ± 0.46 for non­selective media (TSA).
However, the inactivation rate was not affected by the recovery media. Furthermore, Lis­
teria spp. was more UVC resistant than E. coli, by a minimum UVC exposure of 2.85
mJ/cm2 ± 0.62 to reach 4D reductions.
Six strains of Listeria monocytogenes (ATCC 19115 + five isolates from dairy products)
tested on TSA for the difference in UVC resistance by comparing an estimation of log(cfu/ml)
reductions after 8 mJ/cm2 UVC exposure. Up to 1.52 log(cfu/ml) reductions ± 0.64 were
observed in survival between the most and least resistant strains. Furthermore, was the
influence of anaerobic incubation versus aerobic on the enumeration of survivors tested,
showing four out of six strains showed an increase in survival after anaerobic incubation
with a maximum difference of 0.69 mJ/cm2 ± 0.53, while two strains were not affected.
Lastly was a cocktail of the six L. monocytogenes strains tested alongside the individual
strains, indicating a cocktail portraying the majority of the strains.
This study suggests that different factors, such as recovery media, incubation conditions,
and strains can influence the enumeration of surviving bacteria following a UVC treatment.
This knowledge is essential to keep in mind when analyzing the effectiveness of UVC
treatment on e.g. milk to document the level of food safety.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Food safety has been around for decades and is the most important aspect to control and
document when it comes to producing food. It is to protect the consumers, not having to
worry about getting ill or worse. Good food safety management can also decrease food
waste and therefore minimize the costs. All types of food contain microorganisms, some
do not affect the product, some are pathogenic and some cause spoilage of the food,
and this occurs regardless of whether the food is liquid or solid, or if it is a vegetable or
meat. To better control these microorganisms a safety system has been developed, called
HACCP (Hazard Analysis Critical Control Point) (Gayan et al., 2013a). It is a system es­
tablished to identify the important hazards and know how to eliminate/reduce and/or inhibit
them to minimize or remove the risk, through different process steps, and the most used
preservation method for microbial inactivation is heat treatments (Gayan et al., 2014). A
well­known heating process step is pasteurization. This step was adopted in the dairy
industry in the 19Th century when producing milk (Tadini and Gut, 2022). This is due to
raw milk having high contamination of different pathogenic microorganisms and among
those are Listeria monocytogenes and Escherichia coli (Vasavada, 1988), which needs
to be controlled. Due to the effectiveness of pasteurization and the contamination of raw
milk, governments, almost globally, have by law made pasteurization of milk used for any
kind of consumption mandatory, which also applies in Denmark (Veterinary and Adminis­
tration, 2024).
Science has tried to develop new methods, which can obtain the same safety level of food
as pasteurization using less energy and better preservation of wanted compounds like vi­
tamins, color compounds, or enzymes. Ultraviolet (UV) treatments, especially UVC, have
been called one of the most promising alternatives for pasteurization, with the benefit of
being less energy­consuming and without the formation of harmful compounds. UVC is a
subgroup within the UV spectrum that covers the light span of 180­280 nm. UV treatments
have existed for more than 60 years, but for many years the idea of application of UV treat­
ments mainly focused on surface treatment and treatments of water and water­like fluids,
which are UV transparent, but more and more recent studies indicate more considerable
promises for the safety of liquid foods, such as juices and soft drinks (Koutchma, 2019),
(Gayan et al., 2011), (Lopez­Malo and Palou, 2005).
In recent years an increase of publications regarding UVC treatment of milk and its effect
on dairy products have been published. Studies must prove that a certain UVC process
step can ensure the same level of food safety as pasteurization, due to regulations stating
that pasteurization must be done on all raw milk for consumption before a company can
implement such a process step.
1.2
Aim
LYRAS wants to develop a UVC treatment step to replace the pasteurization of milk.
Therefore, they must document to the Danish Veterinary and Food Administration, that
a UVC treatment can reach the same food safety as pasteurization. A risk assessment
for this project was developed by the DTU National Food Institute to determine, which
pathogenic microorganisms were relevant to include, to show that a UVC treatment would
not leave any food safety concerns. This master thesis was made as a subproject, to
optimize the recovery of the bacteria and thereby ensure the estimations of the UVC re­
sistance parameters and therefore the survival count reflected the reality.

The overall question of the project is:
How to optimize recovery of injured bacteria following UVC treatment?
To answer this question, a literature search was conducted to see which aspects have
been covered. Experiments will established to confirm and widen the knowledge, and
sub­questions have been developed:
Do recovery media affect the survival count?
Is there a difference in UVC resistance between bacteria?
Does incubation time affect the survival count?
Is there a difference in UVC resistance between strains belonging to the same species of
bacteria?
Is there a difference in UVC resistance between strains and a cocktail?
Does the presence of oxygen affect the survival count (aerobic vs anaerobic)?
UVC treatment was the only treatment used in this project, so when UV is mentioned,
UVC is what’s referred to.
1.3
UV light
UV light is a part of the electromagnetic spectrum and spans over the wavelength 100­400
nm and covers the wavelengths in between x­ray and visible light. The UV light spectrum
is divided into four groups from short to long wavelength and the intervals vary a bit de­
pending on the journal. The four groups of UV light are: Vacuum UV (VUV) 100­180
nm, UVC 180­280 nm, UVB 280­315 nm, and UVA 315­400 nm (Koutchma et al., 2019),
(Guerrero­Beltrán and Barvosa­Canovas, 2004).
UVA is naturally encountered through sunlight and causes pigment darkening, while also
being essential to produce vitamin D in the skin. It is possible to inactivate cells by inflict­
ing high doses of UVA light and it damages cells by oxidizing DNA, enzymes, proteins,
and lipids by generating reactive oxygen species. UVB is also naturally encountered
through sunlight and can cause skin burning if the skin gets overexposed. UVB can re­
act with DNA, protein, and enzymes and can cause extensive protein damage and has
been used for decontamination, however, it has lower germicidal effects than UVC, due
to lower penetration capacity. UVC is primarily absorbed by the ozone in the atmosphere
and therefore not encountered in nature, it is known as the germicidal part of the UV
spectrum, which is the reason that lamps have been designed to emit light at a certain
wavelength within this spectrum. It reacts with DNA and has a high penetration capacity
since it is close to DNA’s absorption peak for microorganisms at 260 nm. It reacts with
cytosine and the two thymine neighbor base pair by creating a cross link and creates
thymine dimers or pyridine nucleoside bases as shown in Figure 1.1. This DNA modifi­
cation changes bioactive cell properties and can further generate cyclobutane­pyrimidine
dimers which is a highly dominating byproduct. These changes in the cell properties,
change the ability for DNA transcription and replication, by blocking them, which com­
promises the cellular functions leading to cell death eventually. This cross­link effect is
proportional to the amount of exposure to UVC light. The UVC resistance varies between
cells and sometimes even between strains, and an order from least to highest resistance
between microorganisms are in general: Gram­negatives < Gram­positives < yeast <
bacterial spores < molds < virus, but it is not a rule without exceptions. (Ahmed, 2017),
(Kebbi et al., 2020), (Koutchma, 2019), (Bintsis et al., 2000), (Lopez­Malo and Palou,
2005), (Guerrero­Beltrán and Barvosa­Canovas, 2004).

1.3.1
UV lamps
The two overall types of used UV lamps in the industry are UV LEDs and UV mercury
lamps. There are different types of lamps within these two overall types, which have
different wavelengths and when comparing the two overall groups, then it is important to
compare the overall cross benefit to a specific wavelength. The still most used UV lamp
in the industry is a low­pressure mercury lamp (LPM) and is considered the benchmark
when it comes to UV disinfection (Koutchma et al., 2019). The focus of this thesis is on
LPM lamps since it is used in this project.
A LPM lamp is a quartz tube with transparent glass, consisting of two main electrodes, a
starting electrode, and filled with fluid mercury and argon gas (Figure 1.2).
Figure 1.2: A low­pressure mercury lamp (LPM) from (Koutchma et al., 2019)
When ignited, the lamp emits light with a wavelength of 253.7 and 184 nm, but 184 nm
can only be transmitted in a vacuum and is absorbed by the glass, and therefore light is
only emitted at 253.7 nm. The surface temperature of the lamp is 40 °C, it emits light and
heat in the same directions (as opposed to UV LEDs) and a big advantage of the LPM is
that 40% of the energy usage is converted into UV radiation, where it for other mercury
lamps and UV LEDs is lower than 12% (Koutchma, 2014).
1.4
UV experiment: Bench scale collimated beam apparatus
The two bench scale setups for UV experiments are either a flow­through UV reactor or
a collimated beam apparatus with a mixing batch reactor, where different kinds of UV
lamps can be installed, mercury or LED with different wavelengths. The second setup
with a low­pressure mercury lamp is what this section will focus on.
1.4.1
Parameters and terminology - UV experiments
To calculate the UV exposure of the microorganism, some corrections were needed to
be made, since some energy will be lost in different places in the system. This section
is based on the article (Bolton and Linden, 2003). The calculations to estimate the time
needed to reach a specific UV dose were based on these equations.

The Germicidal Fluence (UV dose) in a bench scale collimated beam apparatus with a
mixing batch reactor can be calculated from equation 1.1:
UV dose(mJ/cm2) = Eavg(mW/cm2) × t(s)
(1.1)
From equation 1.1 can it be seen that the Average Irradiance (Eavg) and time determines
the UV exposure, but first must Eavg be calculated. The Eavg is based on five factors:
Incident Irradiance (E0, mW/cm2), Reflection Factor (RF), Divergence Factor (DF), Petri
Factor (PF) and Water Factor (WF). Eavg is calculated from equation 1.2, and it is only
valid on stirred suspension, which should be initiated 10 seconds before UV exposure
(Bolton and Linden, 2003):
Eavg = E0 × RF × DF × PF × WF
(1.2)
Irradiance and fluence rate are the same in a collimated beam setup and are the total
radiant power incident (Bolton et al., 2015). The Incident Irradiance (E0, mW/cm2) is
measured with a UV radiometer in the center and the same level as the surface of sus­
pension and if a low­pressure lamp is used in a collimated beam setup, then the measure
of the irradiance at the surface is highly accurate.
The Reflection Factor is RF=(1­R), where RF represents that part of the UV light which
enters the suspension and R represents the part of the light that is reflected. This is be­
cause when a light beam changes from one medium (air) to another (water), a part of the
light will be reflected. R = 0.025 from air to water, meaning the RF = 0.975.
The Divergence Factor is a correction of the light beam not being perfectly collimated,
meaning not all light reaches the surface. It is recommended to use a dark and rough
surface to collimate the beam to minimize reflection from the walls. It is dependent on the
height between the surface and the lamp (L) and the depth of the suspension (l) (height
of the petri dish). Equation 1.3 is used to calculate DF:
DF =

L + l
(1.3)
The Petri Factor is calculated for different UV lamps, wavelengths, and petri dish sizes
since PF is a ratio between the average incident irradiance of the surface area and the
center. This correction is used to account for variations of irradiance distributed over
the surface area. It is calculated with a Radiometer and a sensor measuring the incident
irradiance, and by moving the sensor across the surface area equal to the petri dish used.
The sensor is moved 5 mm between each measurement in a XY calibration. A good
design should have a ratio greater than 0.9.
The Water Factor is a correction of the amount of light, which is in contact with the bacteria
since a part of the light will be absorbed by the suspension media without damaging the
organisms. The WF is based on the wavelength (254 nm for this project), and is dependent
on the depth of the solution (l) (height of petri dish), the absorption coefficient (a, cm−1)

1.4.2
Experiment protocol
The setup of the experiment is based on the protocol suggested by (Bolton et al., 2015).
A microbial suspension with a high initial concentration should be made with the target
organism. A minimum of 6 UV doses should be applied including 0, the range of doses
should cover at least 4 log reductions, this is determined by the UV sensitivity of the target
organism. The time to the targeted doses is calculated as in section 1.4.1.
Duplicates or triplicates should be made for each dose and are prepared by adding the
same volume in the amount of petri dishes corresponding to the number of replicates. A
random order of execution is recommended to convert systematic errors to random er­
rors. It is recommended to treat the 0­sample at the same time in the apparatus as the
shortest, medium, and longest time with the lamp turned off or with the shutter, if more
than one 0­sample is used. Samples should be treated with the same stirring speed initi­
ated approximately 10 seconds before starting and without creating a whirlpool.
The incident irradiation should be measured as close to the start and end of the exper­
iment as possible to verify the stability. Dilutions are made for each dose on a chosen
recovery media and incubated under specified conditions, to validate the UV experiment
(Bolton et al., 2015).
1.4.3
Modelling
Different inactivation kinetic parameters are used to compare UV sensitivity between mi­
croorganisms, such as the inactivation rate constant k (cm2/mJ) and UVCD value. UV
dose­response curves are used to estimate these values.
UV dose­response curves
mostly follow first­order kinetics described by (Chick, 1908):
dN
dt = −k × N
(1.5)
and can be written as:
log10(N0/Nt) = −k × UV −dose
(1.6)
where N0 = the initial concentration, Nt = Concentration after UV exposure, k (cm2/mJ) =
the inactivation rate or slope of the curve (Vitzilaiou, 2021).
However, frequently a UV dose­response curve can consist of up to three phases (Fig­
ure 1.3, which are important for understanding the inactivation kinetics and for modeling
data. The first part: is low to no reduction (shoulder), the second part: is the log­linear
(k), and the third part: is low to no reduction (tailing). The inactivation rate is indepen­
dent of both shoulder length and tailing, but a change in k means a change in the esti­
mation of the shoulder and tailing (Geeraerd et al., 2000). To model these parts of the
curve and for better estimation of the log­linear part, (Geeraerd et al., 2000) suggested
a novel inactivation model, which then got developed into a tool to assess non­log linear
microbial survivor curves, called GIna­FiT. This tool is designed as an add­in for Microsoft
Excel. This program can provide model estimates for the initial concentration (log10(N0)),
shoulder length, the inactivation rate (kmax), dose to 4D reduction, and the concentration
at tailing (log10(Nres)), through four models: log­linear regression, log­linear+shoulder,

1.4.3.1
Shoulder and Tailing
The shoulder is often seen in a UV dose­response curve and is related to DNA repair,
which suggests that the organism has a threshold of repairs that needs to be exceeded be­
fore the inactivation will occur. Also, aggregation of microorganisms is suggested, where
the external layer needs to be killed before the inner layer can be damaged (Lopez­Malo
and Palou, 2005) (Gayan et al., 2013a).
The expectation when applying UV would be a constant log­linear decrease of the con­
centration of surviving bacteria when comparing the concentration with UV exposure, but
a deviation towards the higher UV doses is often seen. This part is called the tailing phe­
nomenon as mentioned in section 1.4.3. There exist different theories to explain why his
phenomenon exists and it is seen for various bacteria, viruses, spores, and different ex­
perimental setups.
There can be biological reasons and errors, which can explain the phenomenon. Known
theories are existing subpopulations with different UV resistance, shielding, and shielding
caused by aggregation. Tailing can also occur due to non­homogeneous light distribution
to the surface, this can be caused by a rough surface or high absorbance. A last explana­
tion can be due to an artifact in the experimental setup (Gayan et al., 2013a), (Mamane­
Gravetz and Linden, 2005), (Vélez­Colmenares and Nebot, 2011), (Chick, 1908).
1.4.4
Repair mechanisms
The repair mechanisms can be divided into two overall groups: the first group has repair
mechanisms, which restore the DNA damages before the replication of DNA, and the two
repair mechanisms, that are mentioned the most are dark repair and photoreactivation.
The second group has a repair mechanism, which restores the DNA post­replication, such
as SOS response. The occurrence of the different mechanisms is dependent on the
recovery conditions and the microorganisms (Gayan et al., 2013a).

1.4.4.1
Dark repair
Dark repair is a name referring to nucleotide excision repair (NER), which is a DNA re­
pair mechanism that happens without the presence of light, and it is a mechanism that
can correct a broad variety of DNA lesions. The repair is made by three enzymes: UVr
ABC endonuclease detects a disturbance in the DNA caused by UV exposure and cuts the
DNA strand on both sides of the lesion, then DNA polymerase synthesizes a new comple­
mentary DNA strand and as the final step, the DNA ligase attaches the new synthesized
complementary DNA strand, where the DNA lesion was before. This repair mechanism
is also known as liquid holding recovery due to the ability to repair in a minimal medium
without light exposure. It is suggested that this mechanism is better in a minimal growth
medium rather than in a rich growth medium since the organism then has more time to re­
pair the lesions before replications are initiated, without using the SOS response (Beggs,
2002), (Gayan et al., 2013a).
1.4.4.2
Photoreactivation
Photoreactivation is a repair mechanism that is light­dependent and is considered the
main UVC defense mechanism for microorganisms. Most bacteria can do photoreactiva­
tion, but it is strain dependent as it has been shown e.g. E. coli (Sommer et al., 2000).
It is dependent on the type of enzyme DNA photolyase, that the microorganism contains.
The photoreactivation has two steps: DNA photolyase reacts with the pyrimidine dimer
in the DNA and forms an enzyme­substrate, this step happens in the absence of light,
the enzyme­substrate which then is light dependent, gets activated by light between 320­
410 nm. It reacts with the pyrimidine dimer and restores the original DNA base binding
(Beggs, 2002).
1.4.4.3
SOS response
The SOS response is a last­resort repair mechanism that occurs when DNA lesions get too
extensive and is controlled by LexA and the protein Rec A. It is an error­prone system,
where DNA polymerase inserts random bases without an accurate base pairing, which
then allows the DNA to be replicated past the DNA lesions. This DNA replication leads
to mutations, but this repair mechanism can also lead to the memorization of pyridine
dimers, which further can lead to an increase in UV resistance (Beggs, 2002) (Gayan et
al., 2013a).

Discussion
The results from the three experiments presented in chapter 3 will in this chapter be dis­
cussed in the same order as the results were presented before conclusions will be drawn
in the next chapter (5). All samples were kept as unexposed to visible light as possible
to decrease the risk of photoreactivation, but no experiments or changes between exper­
iments were done to test if photoreactivation affected the results.
4.1
Role of recovery media in enumeration of surviving
bacteria following a UVC treatment
This experiment was divided into four subsections, where the three bacteria on the four
recovery media were discussed separately, and then comparing the UVC resistance pa­
rameters from the three bacteria with the data estimated from the recovery on TSA.
4.1.1
E. coli ATCC 25922
It was determined to fit the UVC resistance parameters with the Geeraerd model log­
linear+shoulder due to a better fit, based on R2 and RSME, and a different model would
give different results as illustrated in Table A.1, Furthermore, have a shoulder been ob­
served before for the same E. coli ATCC 25922 strain (Gayan et al., 2011).
Longer incubation time (24 hours extension) did not affect the recovery of E. coli ATCC
25922 at any doses on any recovery media, which confirmed the results from (Gayan
et al., 2011). The determination of the Sl was affected by the few data points at the be­
ginning of the curve. The Sl was significantly (p < 0.05) shorter estimated on MacConkey
plates than on TSA with Blood agar and E. coli petrifilm, the latter two not resulting in
significantly (p < 0.05) different recovery. These differences in Sl did not affect kmax
when estimated from counts on the different recovery media. This was expected since
increasing UVC resistance by visual light or changing the growth phase of the culture
tested only affected Sl in the study of (Gayan et al., 2011). The effect of the extension
of Sl was reflected in the dose required to obtain 4D reductions, where a significantly (p
< 0.05) lower UVC exposure was required when the Sl was shorter. This indicates that
it would be problematic if the wrong selective medium was used for the purpose of de­
termining the UVC doses needed to reach 4D or higher. More studies need to be made
to determine if this varies between E. coli strains, since these results might differ from
other strains since different repair mechanisms have an effect for some strains and not
for others (Sommer et al., 2000). The initial concentration was not significant (p > 0.05)
different on all recovery media, which was intended and also expected since no stresses
were inflicted on the bacterium in advance, and from these observations, an estimation
of sublethal injured cells can be made. However, the petrifilm was expected to illustrate
lower resistance, since significantly (p < 0.05) lower recovery has been proven for both
heat­ and acid­stressed (Smith et al., 2013). This underlines the importance of testing
different strains.
From the Sl estimates and Figure 3.1, it can be deduced, that all recovery media resulted
in Sl to be in between the first two doses (0 and 2 mJ/cm2). Although kmax was not af­
fected by changing the recovery media, an increased deviation in the tailing part of the
curve, from dose 8 mJ/cm2 was observed for all recovery media.
The tailing phenomenon occurred earlier for recovery on MacConkey plates compared to
32

the other recovery media. The tailing was determined by concentrations close to the de­
tection limit for all recovery media, which makes this part of the curve difficult to interpret.
Petrifilm was better to determine the occurrence of tailing since a higher sample volume
was used, and therefore, has a lower detection limit. Estimating the survival count close
to the detecting limit resulted in large deviations as illustrated in Figure 3.4. The chances
of getting a representative count reduce, as the bacterial concentration decreases, and
if aggregates of cells were formed, then the distribution of bacteria would be uneven in
the suspension. This would result in a slower inactivation since not all bacteria would
be exposed to UVC light. Shielding from dead organic material could also have an ef­
fect since the inactivated bacteria still were in the suspension. These artifacts could also
have caused an effect but were not investigated. However, the tailing does not affect the
estimations of UVC resistance parameters (Geeraerd et al., 2000).
4.1.2
L. innocua ATCC 51742
Longer incubation time did not affect the recovery of L. innocua ATCC 51742 at any dose
on any recovery media. The Geeraerd model log­linear+shoulder was also used for fitting
UVC resistance parameters for this organism.
No significant (p > 0.05) variation between the initial concentration was observed on all
four recovery media, determined with a paired t­test on the observed bacterial concentra­
tions. The Sl were significantly (p < 0.05) shorter on both selective media compared to
non­selective media, in contrary to E. coli, underlines that selective media has a lower re­
covery on stressed L. innocua, which have been observed previously thermally processed
L. innocua (Miller et al., 2010), and can again be used to estimate the sublethal injured
cells. This could vary among strains but indicates that selective media cannot be used
alone. The non­selective TSA and Blood agar were performing the same as expected.
The estimates of kmax were not significantly (p < 0.05) affected by the length of the shoul­
der. The estimated dose to get 4D reductions was significantly (p < 0.05) lower for the
selective media compared to the non­selective media which was due to the change in Sl.
It is important to remember that a significant (p < 0.05) change in Sl does not necessarily
change the dose for 4D reductions (Gayan et al., 2014).
An increased deviation between enumerated bacterial survivors on selective and non­
selective recovery media was observed when 2 mJ/cm2 of UVC exposure was exceeded
and until tailing occurred on all four recovery media around dose 10 mJ/cm2. Larger de­
viations were observed, at the tail part of the curve, as was also observed for E. coli. The
survival counts were not close to the detection limit, meaning tailing did occur. The devia­
tion can be due to aggregations, which would result in bacteria being unevenly distributed
in the suspension. It could also be caused by artifacts showing at lower concentrations,
which would be artifacts affecting the distribution of the bacteria in the suspension, such as
whirlpool, shadows from the magnet, or a combination of not being able to have sufficient
stirring without creating a whirlpool. A whirlpool would change the depth by pushing water
towards the site, increasing the depth towards the edge of the petri dish, and decreasing
the depth in the middle. Too rough stirring can also create an uneven surface thereby
resulting in uneven UVC light distribution. These artifacts might be hidden at high con­
centrations of bacterial survivors since a high concentration would statistically increase
the chance of an even distribution.

L. monocytogenes ATCC 19115
The Geeraerd model log­linear+shoulder was also used for fitting UVC resistance param­

Only two biological replicate experiments were made and variations between the two repli­

any significant (p > 0.05) difference between the estimates of Sl on the four recovery
media. The small SD for kmax, illustrated that kmax was independent of the shoulder. No
difference in UVC resistance between selective and non­selective recovery media would
be observed if no significant (p > 0.05) difference between log(N0), Sl, and/or kmax, which
was the case for Sl and kmax. However, a significant (p < 0.05) difference in initial con­

looking at the trend of the UVC dose­response curve (Figure 3.5), a similar pattern, for
an increasing deviation on the enumeration of bacterial survivors on selective and non­
selective recover media, compared to L. innocua ATCC 51742 was observed. It should be
noted, that Figure 3.5 depicts the inactivation from the same initial bacterial concentration.
The increasing deviation between selective and non­selective recovery was observed for
UVC exposure higher than 4 mJ/cm2 (Figure 3.6. This late deviation compared to L. in­
nocua, can be explained with there was no observed significant (p < 0.05) difference in Sl
between the four recovery media. This suggests that Palcam and Listeria petrifilm have
a lower recovery on both stressed and unstressed L. monocytogenes ATCC 19115.
Compared with L. innocua ATCC 51742 and E. coli ATCC 25922, did L. monocytogenes
ATCC 19115 not end with the same survival counts for all four recovery media, but no
significant (p > 0.05) difference of log(cfu/ml) reductions between 0 and 12 mJ/cm2 was
observed between selective and non­selective recovery media.
Data obtained from these three bacteria suggest that different recovery media can have
a significant (p < 0.05) impact on the initial concentration, Sl, and dose to 4D reductions,
while estimation of kmax was independent.
4.1.4
Comparison of UVC parameters between E. coli, L. innocua and L.
monocytogenes on TSA
Both L. innocua and L. monocytogenes were more UVC resistant than E. coli, which was
expected since Listeria spp. is Gram­positive and E. coli is Gram­negative, further, this
has been illustrated by (Gayan et al., 2014) and (Lu et al., 2011). A difference in estimation
of kmax 2.85 mJ/cm2 ± 0.62 for L. innocua and 2.96 mJ/cm2 ± 0.77 for L. monocytogenes
obtained on TSA, were observed compared to E. coli. A significantly (p < 0.05) higher
kmax, means a higher inactivation with the same UVC exposure, which is the case for E.
coli. A significantly (p < 0.05) shorter Sl and higher kmax for L. monocytogenes compared
with L. innocua, resulting in the same UVC resistance for 4D reductions. Fitting the model
(log­linear+shoulder) with equation 2.2, with the estimated parameters from Table 3.4
showed the comparison with the data obtained (Figure 3.7). There can be argued for a
longer Sl and a higher kmax for L. monocytogenes. The deviation of the two biological
replicates of L. monocytogenes at dose 4 mJ/cm2 affected the Sl, and since the fit ends
(dose 10 mJ/cm2) higher than the observed concentrations and the concentration was the
same as for L. innocua. Both E. coli and L. innocua have better fits, but both exhibited
larger deviations towards the higher UVC exposure.

4.2
Comparison of UVC resistance among Listeria
monocytogenes strains
It is important to remember, that the results were shown as a log(cfu/ml) reduction be­
tween the initial concentration and the concentration after a UVC exposure of 8 mJ/cm2.
Therefore, a lower reduction means that a higher survival count was observed on the re­
covery media.
The treatment dose was chosen by looking at the UVC dose­response curve illustrated
in Figure 3.1.4, from both the L. innocua and L. monocytogenes. Dose 8 mJ/cm2 was
chosen since a significant (p < 0.05) reduction was needed, furthermore, this dose gave
a reduction close to the tailing part, more likely to have the correct difference in resistance
between strains. E.g. if the difference between L. innocua ATCC 51742 and L. monocy­
togenes ATCC 19115 at dose 4 mJ/cm2 was investigated, then a difference between the
bacteria would be observed, while no difference would be observed at dose 10 mJ/cm2.
This should give a more realistic distribution of UVC resistance between the strains and
the cocktail since no full dose­response curves were made.
The pH has been proven to not affect the UVC resistance, while the absorption has a
great effect (Gayan et al., 2014). The change of absorption from the second biological
replicate was considered when the time was estimated. This change meant an increase
of one second in treatment time. The incubation times for the tested cultures were noted
to ensure all strains were in the same growth phase. The SD for petrifilm was less varying
than for TSA, this could come from the difference in sample volume, where 100 times the
volume was used on Listeria petrifilm compared to the spot test on TSA plates. The SD
for the cocktail was the same for both recovery media, and the reason could come from
a relation between the strains that differs a bit each time. E.g. Lis 26 with an OD600 of
0.111 an initial concentration of 7.31 log(cfu/ml) ± 0.04 in the first biological replicate and
the second biological replicate an OD600 gave 9.19 log(cfu/ml) ± 0.07 for the same strain.
The strains were sorted from lowest to highest log(cfu/ml) reductions in Figure 3.8, to il­
lustrate that there was no pattern between the difference from TSA to Listeria petrifilm.
A significantly (p < 0.05) higher reduction was seen for five out of six strains and for the
cocktail, this was also observed for L. innocua ATCC 51742 in the prior experiment (data
not shown).
There must be an even number of observations when doing a paired t­test. For Strains
with an uneven number of reductions (observations) resulted in ”healthy” reductions were
removed, which could be done since the ”healthy” observations were from the same di­
lution. A p­value of < 0.05 from a two­tail was used for all samples. Lis 26 was the only
strain, not being significant (p > 0.05) more UVC resistant on Listeria petrifilm compared
to TSA plates. This suggests intraspecies viability.
The absolute difference in Table 3.6 illustrated how many reductions were between the
estimated log(cfu/ml) reduction on the two recovery media. It is important to understand,
that the factor difference is a percentage deviation between two reductions and not the
survivals count, which would give a different result. It illustrates if the absolute difference
is proportionally higher or lower for a strain.
Comparing the resistance between the strains indicates intraspecies viability between the
strains which was expected (Gayan et al., 2014), but this grouping of resistance changes,
dependent on whether TSA or Listeria petrifilm was used. This emphasizes the impor­
tance of determining the UVC resistance with a proper recovery medium.
Intraspecies viability in UVC resistance between the L. monocytogenes strains was ex­
pected since it has been proved for both E. coli (Gayan et al., 2011), L. monocytogenes

4.3
Role of aerobic vs. anaerobic incubation in enumeration of
surviving bacteria following UVC treatment
The incubation time differed within an hour between the three biological replicates and
the absorption did not influence the time, but pH differed within approximately 1 pH for
this experiment, but pH should not have any effect on the UVC resistance (Gayan et al.,
2014). The increase of pH did not show any effect on the results when comparing SD
from TSA Table 3.6 and aerobic Table 3.9.
Four out of six strains did show a significantly (p < 0.05) lower log(cfu/ml) reduction after
anaerobic incubation conditions. This underlines intraspecies viability within the species
of L. monocytogenes, and indicating the removal of oxygen (O2) can affects the enumer­
ation of bacterial survivors.
ATCC 19115 and Lis 26 performed the same under both aerobic and anaerobic incubation
conditions (relative SD > difference), which suggests that not all strains are affected by
the removal of O2. Intraspecies viability of UVC resistance among strains was expected
since it has been observed for numerous bacteria, under different conditions and for dif­
ferent repair mechanisms following UVC treatment (Gayan et al., 2013a). A higher UVC
resistance under anaerobic conditions could be explained with dark repair, having less
energy available to grow compared to aerobic incubation, and therefore having more time
for repairing DNA before replications were initiated, leading to fewer repairs made from
SOS response. The colonies were smaller after anaerobic incubation compared with the
colonies recovered from aerobic incubation (Figure A.1), which indicates less energy was
available under anaerobic conditions. Another explanation could be, that the restrictions
of O2, experienced under anaerobic incubation, enhance survival for some L. monocy­
togenes strains in stressed conditions. Enhanced growth and better survival have been
observed for L. monocytogenes under anaerobic conditions in a low pH environment com­
pared to aerobic conditions (Lungu et al., 2009), but more studies are needed to verify
these two theories for UVC treatment.
The cocktail behaves again as the majority of the strains. This suggests a cocktail can be
used to illustrate the average behavior for L. monocytogenes, but can hide intraspecies
viability.

Conclusion
For optimizing the recovery of injured bacteria following a UVC treatment, it can be con­
cluded that the extension of the incubation time did not affect the survival counts, sug­
gesting that the bacteria were inactivated and not injured. Furthermore, it was found that
selective recovery media can have a negative influence on the recovery of survivors. Both
MacConkey agar for E. coli and Palcam agar as well as Listeria petrifilm for L. mono­
cytogenes ATCC 19115 and L. innocua ATCC 51741 showed lower recovery than the
non­selective TSA and Blood agar. In particular, this observation affected the shoulder
length of the dose­response curve, which affected the dose needed for obtaining a 4D re­
duction, whereas the inactivation rates kmax were not affected by the choice of recovery
media. Increased deviations in the number of survivors enumerated on the selective ver­
sus non­selective recovery media were observed for increasing UVC exposure reaching
the same enumeration at the tailing. The two non­selective media, TSA and Blood agar
were equally good for all three bacteria. Furthermore, it can be concluded that Listeria
was more UVC resistant than E. coli, this suggest L. monocytogenes as the photogenic
bacteria, which determines the overall exposure needed for a safe UVC treatment.
An intraspecies variability of UVC resistance was observed, which must be considered
when e.g. choosing a surrogate or when testing in a pilot facility. Anaerobic incubation
had a positive influence on recovering L. monocytogenes on TSA, since four out of six
strains were found more UVC resistant after anaerobic incubation compared to aerobic
incubation. Finally, a cocktail of L. monocytogenes was found to behave as the majority
of the strains, but it can hide intraspecies viability.

Perspectivation
The biggest issue of this study was to control the shoulder. Knowing the shoulder is very
sensitive, with different factors affecting this aspect, a standard of propagation should be
developed to minimize differences between each replicate. A solution would be to bet­
ter understand the effect of the growth phases on the UVC resistance of the test strains.
The application of a standardized incubation time interval should ensure that differences
between biological replicate experiments are small. Another future aspect would be spec­
ifying those small areas, that could influence the results, to the technician helping to ex­
ecute the experiment, such as initiating the mixing and or mixing speed. A less deviating
performance between the experiments would reduce the number of replicates needed, to
determine the difference between different strains and bacteria. This would also make
it possible to determine if L. innocua ATCC 51742 is a good surrogate, which is missing
from this project. Furthermore, would a third replicate of L. monocytogenes ATCC 19115
would have enabled a better estimation of the UVC parameters.
After discovering, that the recovery media, can influence certain UVC resistance parame­
ters, the next would be finding the best selective recovery media or establishing a margin
of safety for the recovery medium that the industry prefers.
The next step would be to research which recovery conditions trigger the repair mech­
anisms for both dark repair and photoreactivation. A better understanding of this would
help develop storage conditions for UVC treated products. Especially photo reactivation
which has been shown to have an effect, but to use it, a conversion into a real scenario,
for light exposure and time.
