Preface 
 
This thesis has been prepared at the department of Electrical Engineering at the Technical University of 
Denmark as partial fulfilment of the requirements for acquiring the degree of Master of Science in Electrical 
Engineering. 
The original purpose of the thesis was to investigate the causes and effects of different electric, magnetic and 
mechanical faults, occurring within and in the close vicinity of the common generator topologies used in 
wind turbines today. Two common generator types were initially considered, namely a specific type of the 
Doubly-Fed Induction Generator (DFIG), having two one-turn windings per rotor phase and the common 
Permanent Magnet Generator (PMG). The initial approach to modelling generators was to utilize the Finite 
Element Method by means of MagNet by Infolytica. However, after a long working process, it became 
apparent that the inherent difficulties related to FEM modelling of induction machines in general and an 
extensive lack of information about the machine design, made the developed model very difficult to verify. 
Several parts of the geometry, component materials and electric parts of the model were based on 
assumptions, which introduced too much uncertainty. The most prominent issues were very high rotor 
currents and modulated signals, which made it clear that plausible fault simulations were not obtainable. 
Since the time frame at that stage had become an issue, it was decided to abandon the FEM modeling 
approach and save it for future fault simulations and research. As such, the PMG was not implemented for 
FE-analysis either. The attention was then turned to mathematical modelling, but it was found that the 
common mathematical models of induction machines were somewhat insufficient for fault simulation 
purposes. Thus, it was necessary to develop a new model that resembled the specific DFIG type better.  
Eventually, a mathematical model was obtained, which was considered sufficient for fault investigations, but 
the timeframe of the project had become an issue. The time left for fault simulations was not sufficient for 
thorough fault investigation and eventually the PMG simulations had to be put aside as well. 
The unforeseen event of not being able to utilize the FEM came at a critical point in the project period. It was 
necessary to reconsider the basis of the thesis and it was necessary to suddenly turn the attention to new 
modelling and investigation methods. This was a time consuming process and the main focus of the project 
eventually became the development of mathematical models, suitable for fault investigations, as opposed to 
the fault investigations themselves. The thesis has thus undergone some changes in relation to the initial 
purpose. Some initial tasks have not been solved, but mentioned as future work in the conclusion. 
Nevertheless, the main purpose of investigating different fault types in wind turbine generators have been the 
primary focus of the thesis. The final result includes two models of the Doubly-Fed Induction Generator; a 
model in terms of voltage equations and machine variables, and a model in space-vector form, ready for 
reference frame transformation and suitable for control purposes. In addition, steady-state equations have 
been developed on the basis of the equivalent electric circuit diagram. An expanded model in terms of 
voltage equations and machine variables proved to be useful for simulating several common fault conditions 
and unbalances. Common fault types, including fault conditions specific to the Doubly-Fed Induction 
Generator with two windings per rotor phase, has been investigated and the results have been presented. 
Some of the work carried out, including the initial work with the FEM, has been included in the appendices.

Introduction 
 
Wind power has proven an efficient sustainable energy source and the global market has grown considerably 
within the last decade or so [11], [29]. Large off-shore wind farms consisting of several multi megawatt wind 
turbines have been introduced and established, and today, the wind turbine contributes with a considerable 
energy production [27]. A common choice of wind turbine generator has for many years been the Doubly-
Fed Induction Generator due to its properties in context of variable-speed wind turbine systems. However, a 
tendency toward using Permanent Magnet Generators are emerging, because of the more simple construction 
compared to Doubly-Fed Induction Generators. Because of this increasing main role of wind turbines in the 
power production, the reliability and robustness of the wind turbines are quite important. As an example, off-
shore wind farms are not readily accessible and maintenance can be quite difficult. It is important that faults, 
defects and other problems affecting the normal operation of these wind turbines are detected and solved 
before they propagate, and cause serious damage to the turbine. Not detecting faulty conditions in due time 
leads to long downtimes and high expenses. 
Knowledge of how faults propagate is an important step towards detecting faulty conditions. This thesis has 
considered the issue of fault diagnosis and detection in the most common wind turbine generator, the 
Doubly-Fed Induction Generator. Different electric, magnetic and mechanical faults, their causes and effects 
have been investigated through model simulations. Lots of research has been conducted within the area of 
wind turbine generator failures and diagnostics [45]. However, the research tends to have a focus on 
induction, or synchronous machines in general and not specific to wind turbine generators. But since the 
basic structure of the stator and rotor are similar in electric machine, the results can be used in the context of 
wind turbine generators as well. Several approaches to fault simulations have been proposed in literature, but 
the Finite Element Method seems to be the most efficient and preferred tool [20]. However, mathematical 
models have also been utilized with good results [10]. Mathematical models of electric machines are 
common in literature and the so-called phase-coordinate model of the induction machine, derived from 
voltage equations in terms of machine variables is well established. The thesis has taken the mathematical 
modelling approach and the reason for this is the difficulties associated with the modelling of induction 
machines by means of FEM. Small design flaws due to modelling errors or lacking knowledge of the general 
machine design, makes it quite difficult to verify the FEM model and when the goal is to simulate faults, the 
level of uncertainty associated with the model has to be as low as possible. Mathematical models are of a 
higher level of abstraction and the possibilities for fault investigation is more limited compared to FEM 
modelling. However, the possibilities of obtaining a more generic and practical model, requiring less 
machine design knowledge, has been considered better.  
The thesis proposes an alternative mathematical model of the Doubly-Fed Induction Machine with two 
windings per rotor phase. The common mathematical model only considers one winding per rotor phase 
under the assumption that this approximation is sufficient for modelling the basic functionality of the 
machine. However, this assumption limits the possibility of simulating some rotor specific faults. On the 
basis of the developed mathematical model, some of the common electric, magnetic and mechanical fault 
characteristics have been investigated by means of simulations and the stator current spectrum has been 
obtained in order to relate a specific fault to unwanted harmonics. Many different diagnostic techniques have 
been proposed, but according to [22] and [27] vibrational and stator current spectral analysis has proven to 
be some of the most efficient diagnostic techniques. The stator current is a practical machine quantity for

The report has been divided into six chapters. Chapter 1 covers a brief explanation of the common wind 
turbine systems; the fixed-speed system and the variable-speed system, and the general generator topologies 
associated with the systems. In addition, the basic construction and operation of the Doubly-Fed Induction

Chapter 2 presents a review of fault types and defects associated with wind turbine generators. The chapter

Chapter 3 presents the modelling problem and provides a discussion of the FEM versus mathematical

1 Common Generators in Wind Turbine Systems 
 
The purpose of this chapter is to provide an overview of the common wind turbine systems and to present the 
specific generator topologies associated with these specific systems. Section 1.1 presents the two different 
systems used in large wind turbines and the generators associated with these systems. Section 1.2 presents 
the basic construction and operating principle of the DFIG. 
1.1 Wind Turbine System Topologies 
Large wind turbines can be divided into two topologies depending on how they respond to variations in the 
wind: Fixed-speed systems and variable-speed systems. Both induction machines and synchronous machines 
can be used in wind turbine systems. Induction machines can be used in fixed-speed wind turbine systems, as 
well as in variable speed systems, while synchronous machines are normally used in power electronic 
interfaced variable-speed systems. 
1.1.1 Fixed Speed Wind Turbine 
Fixed speed systems usually make use of three-phase asynchronous generators. The turbine rotor shaft is 
directly connected to the generator rotor via a fixed ratio gearbox. The generator output is tied directly to the 
local power grid and the rotational speed of the generator is more or less fixed. The same goes for the speed 
of the turbine rotor and a variation in the wind naturally causes variations in the mechanical power of the 
turbine rotor, leading to variations in the torque as well. These torque variations on the turbine rotor will 
increase significantly with only a small variation in the rotor speed, resulting in a lot of stress on the 
mechanical parts (especially the gear box) and large fluctuations in the generator output power to the grid.  
The construction and performance of fixed speed systems very much depends on the mechanical sub-circuits 
such as pitch-control time constants, main breaker maximum switching rate, etc. The response time of some 
mechanical circuits may be in the range of tens of milliseconds, thereby giving the large sudden variations on 
the electrical output as mentioned before. Sturdy and rigid mechanical design is necessary and thus leading 
to high construction expenses, especially at high rated power.  
1.1.2 Variable Speed Wind Turbine 
As opposed to the fixed-speed wind turbine system, the variable-speed system allow for variations in the 
wind. The variations are allowed to propagate through the turbine rotor shaft and gear box, all the way to the 
generator rotor. Because of the varying generator rotor speed and the direct connection of the generator stator 
terminals to the power grid, this configuration excludes the use of the basic cage rotor induction generators. 
Instead, Doubly-Fed Induction Generators (DFIG) and Permanent Magnet Synchronous Generators (PMG) 
are commonly used in two slightly different systems incorporating power electronics. These generator types 
make it possible to maintain the generator output voltage and frequency, independent of the generator rotor 
speed and thus independent of wind fluctuations.  
Among the advantages of the variable speed topology is the ability to absorb a sudden increase of 
mechanical power caused by wind gusts and thus reducing the mechanical stress. The energy can be stored in 
the mechanical inertia of the wind turbine, creating an “elasticity” that reduces torque variations. Though 
more expensive, the variable speed systems are cost effective and provides simple pitch control, reduces 
mechanical stress on sub circuits and improve overall system efficiency. Figure 1.1 shows a typical variable 
speed wind turbine system, incorporating a DFIG, power electronics etc.

1.2 DFIG Construction and Operation 
In a DFIG, both stator and rotor are fed with AC currents, hence the name “Doubly-Fed”. The stator contains 
three-phase, sinusoidally distributed windings, embedded in slots around the inner periphery of the stator. 
The stator is made of thin silicon steel laminations, stacked on top of one another and thereby forming the 
stator core. The laminations are insulated in order to minimize iron losses caused by eddy-currents. The rotor 
of the DFIG has a three-phase winding similar to the stator winding. The windings are embedded into slots 
on the exterior periphery of the rotor core and connected to external wires through slip-rings on the rotor-
shaft. The design and construction of DFIG’s vary from model to model, but the basic function is the same. 
Both stator and rotor windings are held in place by wedges. With these winding configurations, the air-gap 
can be considered uniform and thereby there is no saliency in the generator. The stator is connected directly 
to the grid and the rotor is connected through slip-rings to a three phase electric source. By adjusting the 
amplitude and frequency of the currents fed to the rotor, it is possible to keep the voltage and frequency of 
the stator constant. The DFIG has three modes of operation depending on the rotational speed of the rotor 
shaft:  
 
Sub-synchronous speed  
 
Synchronous speed  
 
Super-synchronous speed 
For variable speed systems where the requirements to the speed range of the generator are small, the speed 
variations are typically within +/- 30% of the synchronous speed [11], [12]. When the DFIG runs at sub-
synchronous speed, the stator continues to deliver power to the grid. The rotor on the other hand, absorbs 
power from the grid under this condition. When the DFIG runs at super-synchronous speed both the rotor 
and the stator deliver power to the grid. Super-synchronous speed operation is usually considered as a brief 
overload condition caused by wind gusts and if the conditions persists, the pitch-control dump the blade load.

2 Faults in Electric Machinery 
 
Based on a literature review within the subject of fault diagnostics and detection methods, this chapter 
provides a description of faults in Doubly-Fed Induction Generators and Permanent Magnet Synchronous 
Generators used in wind turbines. The main focus of the thesis has been on the DFIG with two windings per 
rotor phase. However, faults related to the PMG are presented as well, such as to provide a perspective on 
generator faults as a whole and to provide reference for future work. 
The first section 2.1 provides a presentation of different fault types and presents a brief perspective on fault 
diagnostics. The two following sections 2.2 and 2.3 presents stator and rotor specific faults, which is 
typically magnetic or electrical related faults, as well as faults related to the mechanical parts of the 
generators. The final section 2.4 presents the theory behind stator current analysis and the current status of 
fault diagnosing methods.    
2.1 Fault Types 
Fault detection in electric machines is imperative to prevent large break-downs, causing extended down-time 
and high expenses. The severity of a fault can be classified by how much damage it causes to the machine 
and how quickly it propagates through the system. In the context of wind turbines, especially those 
incorporated in large wind farms at sea, fault detection in an early stage is important since maintenance can 
be a difficult task. The major fault types of electric machines can in a wide sense be classified as listed in

The major faults related to the stator and rotor electric circuits are inter-turn open or short circuits, abnormal

The inter-turn faults results in an asymmetry of the machine impedances, which results in unbalanced phase

Missing wedges are another common cause of stator faults. The use of magnetic wedges has led to an 
increase in loose, broken or missing wedges, because they are not as strong compared to epoxy glass wedges. 
Missing stator slot wedges can degrade the efficiency and cause localized temperature rises. The presence of 
wedge material on the stator causes contamination and can result in increased end-winding discharge, but the 
condition rarely causes severe machine failure. It has been observed that faults caused by magnetic wedges 
are likely to occur in high-stress applications.  Missing stator wedges imposes asymmetries in the machine 
because the open stator slots cause disturbances of the magnetic air-gap flux and higher harmonic 
components are induced [38].

2.3.1 Bearing Related Faults 
Bearing faults are some of the most common faults in electric machines. Bearing related faults can be caused 
by several other types of faults, both mechanical and electrical. An example could be excessive vibrations 
caused by magnetic imbalances or mechanical asymmetries of the rotor, which in turn causes heavy wear on 
the bearings. The typical type of bearings used in rotating electric machines is the rolling element bearing (or 
anti-friction bearing). It consists of:

2.3.1.1 
Flaking (Spalling) and Pitting 
Flaking or spalling is a condition where the bearing surface turns scaly and peels off due to the repeatedly 
received contact load received on the raceway and the rolling surface during operation. Pitting is very similar 
to flaking and can be seen as small holes of approximately 0.1 mm in depth are generated on the raceway 
surface. These conditions indicate metal fatigue and normally occur when the bearing has reached its normal 
life span. However, flaking and pitting can develop under faulty conditions such as a cocked or tilted bearing, 
a bent rotor shaft, a misalignment of the bearing or any other case where the bearing has been subjected to a 
load, which is not uniformly distributed on the bearing as intended.  
2.3.1.2 
Fractures: Cracks and Chips 
Cracks include slight cracks and splitting, while chips are a type of failure occurring at a certain part of a 
bearing race or a corner of a roller (as the rolling element). Cracks and chips are caused by improper 
mounting, insufficient internal clearing between the bearing elements or shock loads. If the fit between the 
inner race and shaft is too tight, cracks in the axial direction, i.e. parallel to shaft centreline on the inner race 
can develop. 
2.3.1.3 
Other Effects 
 
Seizing. Seizing (or Seizure) is one of the most common fault conditions when a bearing is first put 
into operation. It occurs when the rotation of the rolling elements for some reason has been inhibited, 
causing a rabid and excessive rise in temperature. The hardness of the bearing races and the rolling 
elements (rollers or balls) is thus reduced and the bearing is rendered unusable. Factors such as 
improper lubrication of the bearing, excessive mechanical load or improper clearance among the 
bearing parts causes seizing. 
 
Cage or housing. Failure in the cage may be caused by foreign particles in the bearing, such as dirt. 
Metal particles produced by flaking or crack can also lead to cage failure. 
 
Rust. The most predominant cause is improper care or maintenance of the bearing when stored or 
when the bearing for some reason is out of operation. 
 
Wear. A bearing normally goes through a wear period of several hours after initial operation, after 
which the raceways have adapted to the working conditions and perceptual wear ceases. However, 
wear may continue under abnormal running conditions leading to an unusable bearing. Several 
conditions may lead to excessive wear such as contaminated lubricant, seizing and dirt. 
 
Electrical erosion. Stray currents can cause damage on the bearing. Even small voltages of 2-3V 
may cause arcs, which creates small pits into races or the rolling elements. 
 
Roughening. Foreign material intrusion into the bearing lubricant can cause roughening of the load 
carrying surfaces. When a hard particle is crushed into the metal surface of the raceway, a small dent 
is left resulting in a roughening of the surface. 
 
Brinelling. An excessive sudden impact on the bearing will drive the rolling elements into the 
raceway hard enough to create small indentations at the contact point. As an example, this could 
happen if the bearing was dropped.

2.3.1.4

When a bearing spins, any irregularities in the raceway surfaces or in the roundness of the rolling element

measuring the vibrations of the bearing housing. The earliest possible warning of a defect can be found from 
comparison of the constant-percentage-bandwidth (CPB) of the vibration spectra. Bearing faults show up in 
such spectra as increases in a band of high-frequency components. When a fault starts to develop, the 
vibration produced starts to change. The resulting vibration pulses repeat periodically with a rate determined 
by the location of the fault and by the bearing geometry. These rates are known as the bearing frequencies, or 
fundamental defect frequencies: 
 
Bearing cage defect (FTF), the fundamental train frequency for faults on the cage. 
 
Ball Pass Frequency Inner (BPFI), ball passing frequency of the inner race of the bearing for a fault 
in the inner race. 
 
Ball Spin Frequency (BSF), the ball spinning frequency for a fault on the ball itself. 
 
Ball Pass Frequency Outer (BPFO), ball passing frequency of the outer race of the bearing for a fault 
in the outer race.

2.3.2 Eccentricities 
A non-uniform air-gap between the stator and rotor results in an unbalanced magnetic flux due to the varying

with the rotor movement, it is called static eccentricity. A combination is termed mixed eccentricity.

lack of an exact circular shape of the rotor or stator, imbalances caused by bearing wear, etc. Static rotor

condition.

necessarily related to eccentricity conditions propagates to the stator currents as harmonics very similar to 
those caused by eccentricities. Additional measurements are thereby needed in order to verify a fault caused 
by eccentricities. In [21] it was concluded that vibrational analysis seems to be the best indicator of static and 
dynamic eccentricities, yielding a clear distinction between the two cases. A last fault condition which has 
similarities with eccentricities is “structural looseness”. It occurs when the generator is not properly fastened 
to the wind turbine bed plate. The faults can typically be detected by means of vibration measurements.

2.4.1 Stator Current Analysis in Comparison  
Before going into a deeper explanation on stator current analysis, its advantages and drawbacks, etc. a quick 
review of other common diagnostic systems has been presented. The condition monitoring system, which is 
the most straightforward, is based on temperature analysis. Some faults and errors result in temperature rises 
and it is relatively easy to measure the temperature of the windings by means of embedded sensors. However, 
the problem is that not all defects can be detected as well as the origin is hardly ever indicated. Acoustic and 
vibrational analysis is based on almost the same principle. If a mechanical fault arises, some additional 
specific vibrations will in general be introduced in the machine. These vibrations can be detected by means 
of acoustic or vibrational transducer, and the most common method is by vibration analysis. As mentioned in 
section 2.3.1 different bearing faults can be correlated with a specific vibration frequency. This is also the 
case with some rotor and stator specific defects, and thus it is possible to distinguish different faults and 
derive a specific fault signature. However, it is not always possible to differentiate between all plausible 
causes of a certain fault and in addition the transducers are sensitive to the interference of other 
environmental conditions. Vibration monitoring systems are also relatively expensive, which introduces an 
economical drawback. Stator current analysis has advantages of vibrational analysis, but not the same 
drawbacks. With stator current analysis, it is possible to distinguish electrical and mechanical faults. The 
transducers (i.e. current sensors) are more robust and less sensitive to environmental conditions, and 
furthermore, the transducers are already implemented in the drive systems as standard, thus reducing costs. 
However, the big challenge with stator current analysis is to make a correct and interpretable signal analysis 
of the stator current.

3 Machine Models for Fault Investigation Purposes  
 
This chapter presents the initial considerations associated with the modelling problem. Section 3.1 presents 
some of the common modelling methods of electric machines. Section 3.2 and 3.3 presents and discusses two 
modelling methods suitable for fault simulations. Section 3.4 presents the overall design of the DFIG 
considered in the thesis and explains the choice of modelling method.  
3.1 Modelling Methods 
Ideally, modelling an electric machine for the purpose of fault investigations require that the developed 
model resembles the actual machine as much as possible. The closer the model can be made to resemble the 
actual physical machine and its behaviour, the more knowledge can be obtained about the machine behaviour 
through simulations. As such, a low level of abstraction is desired when developing the machine model, 
thereby keeping model uncertainty at a minimum. Depending on the modelling approach, some modelling 
methods are more suitable than others. In this thesis, the following modelling methods have been considered.  
 
Finite Element Analysis (also referred to as the Finite Element Method (FEM)) by means of MagNet 
by Infolytica 
 
Mathematical modeling using Matlab/Simulink by MathWorks® as the simulation platform 
The FEM has been utilized in various research on fault diagnostics with satisfying results, e.g. [20], [55]. 
The FEM provides the opportunity to utilize detailed knowledge of the machine design and structure, to 
obtain a highly detailed and unique model. However, obtaining an accurate model that works as intended can 
be a difficult task. When modeling for FE-Analysis, it is necessary to know the design specifications and 
dimensions of the stator and rotor, including materials, teeth, slots, winding layouts, etc. in order to obtain a 
reliable model. Not having the sufficient amount of information increase the amount of uncertainty in the 
model. Another issue is the computational time, which can be rather extensive depending on the required 
precision of the results.   
Mathematical modeling is another useful method, which is used in many areas of electric machine analysis. 
As an example, the control systems of electric machines are based on mathematical models obtained from 
somewhat idealized and usually linear versions of electric machines. Mathematical models are often rather 
generic and they do not require extensive knowledge of the actual machine design. Several design and 
construction aspects are not taken into account and non-linearities are usually neglected. Even so, they still 
provide the possibility of obtaining models, which resembles the actual machine with a precision sufficient 
for many types of machine analysis. Mathematical models tend to be less complicated to implement in 
suitable software environments, because of their simplicity compared to FEM models. As such, 
mathematical models are based on a higher level of abstraction and usually only requires common machine 
information, such as inductances, resistances and basic machines quantities like voltages, currents and power. 
FEM models may very well be the most suitable models for fault investigations, but mathematical models 
may yield satisfying results, if sufficient detail is included in the equations.

3.2 The Finite Element Method 
The Finite Element Method (FEM) is a valuable tool when it is desired to analyse problems over complicated 
domains such as computing complex magnetic and electric fields in electrical machines and components. 
The FEM utilizes numerical techniques for finding approximate solutions to boundary value problems of 
differential equations. It uses the calculus of variations to produce a stable solution by minimizing an error 
function. A typical progression of the FEM can be described in two steps. First, the domain of the main 
problem is divided into a collection of subdomains, where each subdomain is represented by a set of element 
equations related to the original main problem. The second step involves a systematically recombination of 
all the element equations into one global system of equations with known solution techniques, which can be 
solved using the initial values of the main problem and thereby giving a numerical solution to the problem. 
The FEM software uses mesh generation to divide the complex problem into the above mentioned 
subdomains and then utilizes an FEM algorithm combined with the underlying physics of the main problem. 
The mesh generation can be specified for the entire model or be specified differently for each component, 
thus giving varying detail in the solution, as desired.  
3.2.1 Modeling for FE-Analysis 
Creating a model in a FEM software environment MagNet by Infolytica, can be expressed by the following 
step-wise procedure: 
 
Build the geometric model (define a drawing space, draw the geometry, define components and 
materials) 
 
Define coils (winding components or other flux-generating components, define excitation) 
 
Define boundary conditions of the model (boundaries of components and boundaries of the entire 
model) 
 
Customize the mesh generation (modify the mesh edge subdivisions in order to increase the 
precision of the calculations) 
 
Define the model solver (e.g. “Newton-Raphson. Set solver for steady-state evaluation, transients, 
transients with motion, etc.) 
The first step in FE-analysis is the construction of a model that resembles the actual machine as much as 
possible. As such, the model can be considered as being specific for each machine. The total model consists 
of several machine parts such as windings, wedges, stator and rotor. The machine parts are recognized as 
“components” in MagNet and the materials, dimensions and winding configurations have to be specified. 
When the geometric model has been created, mesh-densities, field boundary conditions and definition of the 
electrical circuits of the machine has to be defined. The mesh-density specifies the accuracy of the calculated 
fields in the model or in parts of the model, depending on the requirements for detail. Having a finely defined 
mesh results in high precision of the calculated values, but it prolongs the computational time quite a lot. On 
the contrary, a mesh too large results in increased uncertainty in the calculations. The boundary conditions 
provide the boundaries of the model and its surroundings. When the modelled machine is symmetric in its 
construction, for instance a four-poled synchronous machine, the geometric model can be simplified by only 
constructing half of the model and by means of the boundary conditions, the symmetry of the magnetic fields 
can be defined. This saves computation time and lowers the complexity of the model. However, simulating 
asymmetric conditions require a full model, which is the case when rotor eccentricities yielding an 
unbalanced magnetic pull are to be investigated. The electric circuits of the machine can also be defined by 
means of sources, resistances, switches, coils and capacitances. The electric circuits are used to define how 
==PAGE:31==
 
22 
 
the machine windings are connected, for example, delta or star connections. The circuits also define the 
supply circuits of the machine, such as converters etc.  
3.3 Mathematical Models 
Mathematical models of electric machinery are well developed and common in literature. The common 
mathematical model used to describe synchronous machines is based on voltage equations in terms of 
machine variables. As mentioned in section 3.1, magnetic saturation has been neglected, as well as several 
design aspects. Any resistance in the path from source to winding has been lumped into a single equivalent 
winding resistance. The winding inductances express the relationship between the current in the windings 
and the resulting flux-linkages. The model is also referred to as the phase-coordinate model, since all 
quantities are referenced to their specific machine component. 
The most common model of synchronous machines is the 𝑑𝑞0
 model. This model is derived by first using the 
direct Clarke’s transform to obtain a space-vector representation of the phase-coordinate model (known as 
𝛼𝛽𝛾 transform). Then, Park’s transformation is applied to obtain a reference frame, rotating at the 
synchronous speed, which is known as the 𝑑𝑞0 model representation. The 𝑑𝑞0 representation provides 
constant machine quantities, which is ideal for control system purposes. The reason why space-vector 
representation and reference frame transformation is preferred in many areas of machine analysis is because 
of the complexity of the phase-coordinate model, which makes it rather cumbersome to utilize in practical 
applications, such as control systems. Even for simulation purposes, it is in general considered to be too 
impractical [6]. It requires numerical methods for solving and symbolic expressions are very difficult to 
obtain, although a phase-coordinate model in symbolic form has been presented in [17].

3.4 Modelling the DFIG 
In the case of the DFIG considered in this project, it differs from the more common DFIG design with a 
single winding per rotor phase. Instead of one winding per rotor phase, it has been designed with two 
windings per rotor phase. In addition, each winding is formed by only one turn. Each winding is decoupled 
from one another and thus forms six electrically independent electric circuits, supplied by the rotor side 
converter via slip-ring units. Detailed description of the design, construction and behaviour of this DFIG 
topology is rare in literature, but the subject has been addressed in a fault diagnostic perspective in [27]. 
Figure 1.1 shows a schematic representation of the winding arrangement. 
 
Figure 3.1: Rotor winding configurationn of the DFIG with two windings per rotor phase. 
 
Comparing the DFIG design seen on Figure 3.1 with the model outlined in section 3.3.1, it can be seen that 
the common model only considers one set of three-phase rotor currents and thereby only one phase winding. 
Theoretically, the windings could be considered as a parallel connection, where the rotor currents are split up 
into two branch currents. However, since each rotor winding are fed separately, this simplification cannot be 
justified. Ideally, the model should be expanded by an extra voltage equation, representing the currents 
running in the secondary rotor phase winding. This would yield an extra flux term in eq. 3.5 and also new 
inductance sub-matrices.  
Modelling this specific DFIG in an FEM environment was found to be rather complicated, since several 
important aspects of the machine design was not known and was not available. As discussed in the previous 
sections, this implies that some uncertainty is added to the model, making verification difficult. Using 
mathematical modelling required a new model in order to obtain a more reasonable level of abstraction. 
Since the machine has three additional windings, three additional input voltages, currents and resulting flux-
linkages should ideally be accounted for in the model. Not doing so would exclude the possibility of 
imposing and simulating several fault conditions related to the rotor windings.  
Eventually, the approach of obtaining a generic mathematical model in phase-coordinates was chosen, 
because it provided some interesting possibilities in the sense that a similar model have not been addressed 
as such before. The phase-coordinate model provides a lower level of abstraction in comparison to models in 
space-vector representations. It better facilitates simulations of un-balanced and asymmetric conditions and 
the model is not constructed on the assumption of healthy and normal working conditions. This is important, 
since the space-vector representation is built upon assumptions of healthy conditions and this assumption 
cannot be used when fault conditions are present. Space-vector representation can be used for simulating 
machine fault conditions, but the connection between an imposed fault and its output may be become unclear. 
In addition, some faults may not be simulated. 𝑎𝑟1

4.1 The Basic Design of the DFIG

parallel connected windings per phase, two rotor currents per phase will be present instead of one rotor 
current per phase. From a control point of view, this construction actually yields three extra state-variables.

6.3.2 Discussion on Fault Simulation Possibilities 
The purpose of the fault simulations has been somewhat two-fold. It has been important to assess the fault 
simulation properties of the model at the same time as the fault conditions were investigated as part of the 
verification of the model. The following discussion presents the faults that have been simulated and the 
reason why these fault types have been chosen. 
Broken (completely disconnected) rotor winding (bar): The broken or disconnected rotor winding is a 
quite severe fault. The developed mathematical model facilitates extensive investigations of this fault type, 
since all rotor windings and rotor currents have been represented in the model. The fault may occur in the 
case of a broken slip-ring unit, thus disconnecting the phase winding or in the more problematic case, as a 
completely broken winding inside the machine. In theory, a broken rotor phase winding would increase the 
resistance of the winding infinitely. It is important to remember that additional connections and components 
of the machine windings have been lumped into a common winding resistance, which is the reason why 
several issues are associated with the same model parameter. As such, the main parameter of the model, 
which could be altered, is the winding resistances. Other faults basically results in the same condition, such 
as minor short circuits, worn brushes or other factors, which results in an increase of the winding resistance. 
 Stator winding electrical fault: Short-circuits and open-circuits: Short circuit of the stator winding 
results in much the same conditions as explained above. A short-circuit would result in a change of the 
impedance and result in an un-balanced load. Depending on the severity of the short-circuit, the imbalances 
would be more or less profound.  
Un-balanced rotor voltages: This fault can be considered as an external fault, resulting from several 
possible fault conditions. If the rotor-side converter (RSC) has become faulty, the rotor voltages might 
change. Unbalanced rotor voltages may be due to several different fault types that, in some way or the other, 
result in impedance changes of the machine.  
Eccentricities - Dynamic eccentricities: Mechanical faults such as rotor eccentricities, faulty bearings or 
faults related to the structure of the generator are difficult to distinguish from each other, because they all 
propagate to the stator current through air-gap variations. Simulating a faulty bearing would require some 
assumptions about the frequency of the fault, which thereby could be imposed as a cyclic variation in the 
model. Instead, a dynamic rotor eccentricity has been simulated by using the mechanical angular position of 
the shaft as the variation imposed on the parameters. 
Brushes: Faults related to the slip-ring units, such as worn brushes and rotor short-circuits are related to the 
same fault conditions, resulting in impedance changes of the rotor windings, as mentioned in the discussion 
on broken rotor bars. Thereby, these fault types are difficult to distinguish and simulate. However, it is 
possible to investigate the general case, where the rotor winding impedance changes gradually.   
Other faults: Several other faults presented in Table 2.1, section 2.1 have not been mentioned and 
considered in the further fault investigations, because they either result in the same fault conditions, as the 
faults considered in the simulations, or because they are too difficult to simulate by means of the developed 
mathematical model. As such, a more unique and detailed model are required. An example is gear-box 
related faults, which may result in vibrations of the rotor shaft and thereby result in similar effects as those of 
==PAGE:58==
 
49 
 
bearing faults. Missing or broken wedges have an impact on the magnetic flux, since they affect the air-gap 
of the machine.

7 Conclusion 
 
Through literature reviews, different fault types associated with the electrical, the magnetic and the 
mechanical parts of DFIG’s and PMG’s have been approached. The causes and effects of different fault 
types have been investigated and a theoretical approach to some of the most common faults has been used to 
derive methods for imposing faults on the developed mathematical model. The concept of fault signatures, 
which relates the fault type, the resulting machine condition and how the fault propagates, has been 
presented. In addition, discussions on diagnostic techniques and fault monitoring in general has been 
addressed. There is still a need for research on international standards and research on existing international 
standards and certification guidelines regarding monitoring of wind turbine generators and fault 
classification. 
A mathematical model in phase-coordinates of a DFIG with two windings per rotor phase, has been 
developed and tested for its properties within fault simulations. In spite of missing information on machine 
design and parameters, it was possible to utilize the model for simulations and fault investigations. However, 
it is important to recognize the uncertainty associated with the missing full verification of the model. The 
model has been used to simulate common electrical and mechanical faults with plausible results, but there is 
a need for further investigations in order to remove uncertainty from the results and to fully verify the model.  
The fault simulations show that electrical and mechanical faults can be observed in the stator current 
spectrum and that the frequency, at which the faults can be observed, is largely dependent on the slip. Thus, 
it has been concluded that the fault investigation methods used, yields plausible information about fault 
causes and effects. Future work includes a full verification of the developed model and once this has been 
done, additional fault simulations and investigations should be carried out. Some faults of particular interest 
are bearing related faults, since they are common fault conditions. Eccentricity faults should also be further 
investigated, since the possibility of overlapping fault signatures in connection with bearing faults are present. 
It should also be investigated further how electrical faults propagates in order to verify the fault imposing 
methods.  
A part of the thesis originally included an investigation of the PMG as well, but due to time pressure, the 
model was not completed. As such, the PMG model should be completed and verified, and faults in 
connection with de-magnetized permanent rotor magnets should be investigated. The FEM modelling 
method could be investigated for improvements and perhaps made useable, given that sufficient time and 
knowledge about the machine design is available.  
The working process has in general been subjected to time pressure, which unfortunately has resulted in 
missing aspects of the final work. However, all results obtained and material developed in addition to the 
work presented in the main part of the thesis, have been included in the appendices and the hope is that the 
entire work of the thesis can be used as a basis for future work and reference.

9.3 Appendix: Flux-linkage and Inductances 
Many AC machines are designed on the concept of distributed windings. With three-phase distributed 
windings carrying balanced currents, the goal is to establish a continuously rotating magnetic field with 
constant amplitude in the air-gap and with a speed corresponding to the frequency of the stator voltage, i.e. 
the synchronous speed. Given the distributed winding arrangement, the air-gap MMF mainly contains a 
space-fundamental sinusoidal component and higher order harmonic components have been minimized. 
Thus, a continuously rotating set of north and south poles on the stator are interacting with an equal number 
of north and south poles on the rotor, resulting in a uniform torque. The flux-linkages and inductances are 
based on this functionality.

9.4 Appendix: The PMG 
The PMG was initially a part of the fault investigations, but it had to be disregarded because of time pressure 
and unforeseen problems with the modelling work. The PMG have been considered to a certain extent, but it 
has to be considered as future work. This appendix section presents the PMG, the mathematical modelling 
and the implementation procedure. 
9.4.1 The PMG 
PMG’s have almost the same stator configuration as that of the DFIG; three-phase and sinusoidally 
distributed windings, embedded in slots in the stator core material. In PMG’s, the rotor flux is generated by 
permanent magnets and thereby, no brushes are required as opposed to the Wound Rotor Synchronous 
Generator. High power density can be achieved since there are no rotor windings and thereby the size and 
weight of the generator can be reduced. As a further consequence, there are no rotor winding losses, thus 
reducing the thermal stress on the rotor. The drawbacks however, lie in the fact that the permanent magnets 
are more expensive and prone to de-magnetization. Depending on how the magnets have been mounted on 
the rotor, the PMG can be further classified into Surface-Mounted PMG and an Inset PMG. 
Surface-Mounted: The permanent magnets have been mounted on the rotor, with a non-ferrite material 
separating each magnet. The permeability of the permanent magnets is very close to that of the non-ferrite 
material and the effective air-gap between stator and rotor can be considered uniform. This is called a non-
salient PMG. The main advantage of the Surface-Mounted PMG is its simplicity in contrast to the Inset PMG, 
but since the permanent magnets are mounted on top of the rotor, the magnets are subjected to centrifugal 
forces, which can cause a detachment from the rotor. Normally, the Surface-Mounted PMG’s are used in low 
speed applications. 
==PAGE:98==
 
89 
 
Inset: In Inset PMG’s, the permanent magnets are embedded into the rotor core. Saliency is created because 
of the different permeability of the magnets and the rotor core material. The rotational stress caused by 
centrifugal forces is reduced compared to that of Surface-Mounted PMG’s and the Inset PMG’s can thus 
operate at higher rotational speeds. 
The rated speed of the PMG is dependent on the rated stator frequency and the number of poles. As an 
example, a 72 pole PMG with a rated stator speed of 13.2 Hz has a rated generator speed of 22 rpm [5]. As 
mentioned in section 1.1.2, high ratio gearboxes are used to match the low speed of the wind turbine rotor 
blades with the required speed of the generator. In direct drive systems, the gearboxes have been removed 
and the speed difference has been accounted for by a high number of poles. This results in a bigger and 
heavier construction.

9.6.2.4 
The Voltages 
function V = DFIG_voltages(t,PARAM) 
% Function to calculate the machine voltages by using the magnitudes, the 
% frequencies and phase angles. 
  
% Parameters 
a = PARAM(12); 
Ws = PARAM(1); 
Wr = PARAM(2); 
VS = PARAM(4); 
VR = PARAM(5)*a; 
phase = PARAM(6:11); 
 
% Voltages 
Vas = VS*cos(Ws*t); 
Vbs = VS*cos(Ws*t + 2*pi/3); 
Vcs = VS*cos(Ws*t - 2*pi/3); 
  
Var1 = VR*cos(Wr*t + phase(1)); 
Vbr1 = VR*cos(Wr*t + phase(2)); 
Vcr1 = VR*cos(Wr*t + phase(3)); 
  
Var2 = VR*cos(Wr*t + phase(4)); 
Vbr2 = VR*cos(Wr*t + phase(5)); 
Vcr2 = VR*cos(Wr*t + phase(6)); 
  
V = [Vas Vbs Vcs Var1 Vbr1 Vcr1 Var2 Vbr2 Vcr2]';          
  
end

function R = DFIG_resistance(PARAM) 
% Function to calculate the machine resistances on the basis of the turns 
% ratio. 
  
% Parameters

end

Simulation of Electric Faults in Doubly-Fed 
Induction Generators Employing Advanced 
Mathematical Modelling 
Nenad Mijatovic, Joachim Holbøll, Georgios A. Skrimpas, Christian W. Sweeney, Sebastian Martens 
 
Abstract: For the past two decades, the most common generator topology used in multi-MW wind turbines 
has been the Doubly-Fed Induction Generator (DFIG), because of the advantages it provides in the context of 
variable speed systems. A machine design often encountered in wind turbines contains two parallel windings 
per rotor phase. This configuration is called the Doubly-Fed Induction Generator with Double Rotor Coils 
(DFIG DRC). 
Early detection of abnormal generator behavior due to internal faults and errors, as well as external errors in 
the close vicinity of the generator are crucial, since some faults develop and propagate quickly, causing 
severe damage to the generator. A lot of research within fault diagnostics and condition monitoring systems 
has been carried out, especially within the field of measuring techniques and fault detection methods. 
Typically, mathematical models and FE-analysis has been utilized for fault investigation and simulation, and 
the proposed detection techniques have been tested and verified on live DFIG’s.  
Investigating how faults and defects manifest through the DFIG DRC requires an accurate representation of 
the machine, which allows for asymmetrical and unbalanced conditions. Modelling and simulating faults in 
an FE-environment requires a full machine model, because faults may result in asymmetric phenomena. This 
increases the complexity of the model and thus the computation time. The common mathematical 
representation of a DFIG is based on voltage equations in terms of machine variables, with one winding per 
phase. The model provides the possibility of simulating asymmetrical and unbalanced conditions as long as 
no machine variables are lumped together under the assumption of healthy conditions. By modifying the 
model to represent the DFIG DRC, it can be used for fault simulations with significantly less computation 
time. 
The modified model has been implemented in Matlab and used to simulate some of the faults most common 
to DFIG DRC’s. These include stator winding inter-turn short-circuits, broken rotor coils, eccentricities and 
bearing related faults. The faults have an impact on the magnetic air-gap flux and it is thereby possible to 
impose a specific fault by manipulating the machine inductances in the model. The faults can thus be traced 
to the stator current as unexpected frequency components. These frequency components can be detected by 
means of frequency analysis and it is thus possible to define a specific signature for each fault.
