Abstract
In this thesis, the Bayesian non-negative matrix factorization (NMF) model in conjunc-
tion with nonparametric modeling has been studied. Speciﬁcally, the Indian Buﬀet Process
(IBP) prior in both its ﬁnite approximate form in terms of a Bernoulli Process param-
eterized by a Beta Process and exact inﬁnite form has been considered. The aim is to
automatically infer model complexity while inducing sparsity. Hence, the latent feature
association with observations is attempted captured in a binary matrix Z ∼IBP, such
that E [X] = H(W ⊙Z), with X being observed data, H denoting latent features, and
W denoting the weights accompanying Z. Inference is carried out by means of Variational
Inference (VI) and Markov-Chain Monte-Carlo (MCMC) methods, and a new proposed
VI and MCMC combined method (CGVB).
Experiments are carried out on two diﬀerent kinds of datasets, and with three diﬀerent
hyperprior conﬁgurations placed on H and W . The ﬁrst dataset allows for qualitatively
determining the performance in terms of ﬁnding an accurate part-based solution. The
second dataset measures how well the observed data is inferred to be described w.r.t
inferred latent features. This is achieved by building a classiﬁer based on each models
and/or methods inferred data representation, and subsequently comparing classiﬁcation
accuracies.
It is demonstrated that using the IBP indeed allows for automatic complexity deter-
mination and sparse solutions. However, compared against existing probabilistic NMF
models the results show that the considered models of this thesis regardless of inference
method carry no advantages. The inferred solutions are competitive at best, while the
models based on MCMC or CGVB methods perform signiﬁcantly worse, and it is demon-
strated that careful prior considerations are required in order to achieve reasonable results
using such methods.

Brief introduction:
This thesis deals with Bayesian nonparametrics in conjunction
with Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF) [1]. NMF models have become increas-
ingly popular, due to their part based representations [2], hence justifying the focus in
this thesis.
The NMF model is a speciﬁc type of factor analysis models, which are generally clas-
siﬁed as latent feature models. A general problem associated with latent feature models,
is determining the complexity - i.e. number of latent features. This also applies to NMF
models, and has previously been addressed in various ways; model selection using Akaike
information criterion (AIC) or Bayesian information criterion (BIC) [3], or through Au-
tomatic Relevance Determination (ARD), which allows the model to automatically deter-
mine the number of features provided a speciﬁed upper bound [4, and citations therein].
However, as neither approach is unbounded, a nonparametric inﬁnite NMF has been pro-
posed by means of Markov-Chain Monte-Carlo (MCMC) methods [5], which allows for a
potentially inﬁnite number of latent features.
Further it may be advantageous to consider sparsity into the NMF model, in order
to accommodate for the general non-uniqueness of the NMF solution [6, 7].
Various
methods imposes sparseness using diﬀerent approaches, alas these methods do not allow
for a potentially unbounded NMF model.
Thus, this thesis researches the eﬀect of combining sparsity and Bayesian non-parametrics
into the NMF model. Additionally, this thesis investigates diﬀerent means of drawing in-
ference - i.e. using MCMC and/or variational inference (VI) methods.
Finally, applications will be considered in relation to synthetic data, such as the swim-
mer dataset [6], in order to investigate general feature extraction. Additionally, real world
experiments will be carried out on EEG data, available from e.g. PysioNet.org [8–10].

Speciﬁcally, this thesis investigates the possibility of identifying active frequencies in a

Thesis objectives:
The main research questions regarding the NMF model considered
in this thesis;
• Can model complexity automatically be inferred for a NMF model provided an
appropriate prior?
• What are the beneﬁts and drawbacks of using non-parametric Bayesian modeling as
opposed to existing Bayesian approaches?
• What are the beneﬁts of using a Bernoulli process parameterized by a beta process
over using an Exponential distribution, whose parameters are associated with a
Gamma prior when considering a sparse probabilistic NMF model?
• What are the beneﬁts and drawbacks of using VI compared to using MCMC sampling
methods?
• Can VI and MCMC be combined in order to decrease computational time and/or
increase inference precision?
Overarching learning objectives:

addresses each objective is subsequently elaborated upon.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This thesis investigates non-negative matrix factorization (NMF) [1] in conjunction with
Bayesian nonparametrics.
The NMF model is a speciﬁc type of factor analysis models, generally classiﬁed as
latent feature models. It considers the decomposition of X ∈RD×N
+
into a factorization
with added noise, X = LR + E, with positive matrices L ∈RD×K
+
and R ∈RK×N
+
. The
columns in L make up latent features and the columns in R deﬁnes the linear combination
of these features, for each observation n. Hence, K deﬁnes the number of hidden features
to be inferred, and as such K determines model complexity.
The model has become increasingly popular due to its part-based representation [2],
allowing attractive data exploration analysis and easy interpretable feature extraction re-
sults. Thus, the NMF has proved widely applicable, and has been used on a range of
diﬀerent problems such as music analysis [12–16], bioinformatics [17, 18], neuroinformat-
ics [19], and document clustering [20, 21]. However, despite its success, the original model
comes with certain limitations, such as the NMF model being generally non-unique [6, 7]
and how to determine the appropriate complexity being non-trivial.
Endeavours towards solving these issues can be divided into the maximum likeli-
hood/posterior approach, and the probabilistic approach deﬁned in terms of the Bayesian
statistical framework.
Common between them, is that the non-uniqueness issue is at-
tempted solved by introducing sparsity, while complexity determination is addressed in
diﬀerent ways.
In the maximum likelihood/posterior framework, sparsity is controlled by e.g. sparsity
levels [22], and regularizers like the l1- or l2-norm. The complexity has been deduced
using Bayesian information criterion [23], Akaike information criterion [24], or automatic
relevance determination in conjunction with MAP [25, 26].
The later research towards a probabilistic NMF (or Bayesian NMF) model, allowed
for a more expressive model by accounting for parameter uncertainty and noise. Through
the Bayesian formulation, the probabilistic NMF is controlled through appropriate priors.
Hence, sparsity has been introduced by placing e.g. Exponential priors on each factor el-
ement with Gamma hyperpriors [27] - aptly forcing certain individual elements towards
zero. Furthermore, the probabilistic NMF model allowed the complexity to be automati-
1

inﬁnitely many hidden features, K →∞. The former case includes automatic relevance
determination, which places e.g. an Exponential prior on each factor element, but with
each column in L and row in R sharing the same hyperprior [4]. This allows entire la-
tent features to be ”turned on/oﬀ” as needed. The latter case, with K →∞, an inﬁnite
NMF model has been proposed, using Markov-Chain Monte-Carlo (MCMC) methods [5].
Speciﬁcally, this method considers a ”birth/death” procedure for new features, where a
joint set of model parameters θ and the number of latent features, {θ, K} is drawn from
some proposal distribution.
The set is subsequently accepted or rejected, based on a
Metropolis-Hastings (MH) scheme.
While each solution has proven eﬃcient, they were developed towards solving either
sparsity or complexity determination. Thus, deﬁning a uniﬁed model, aimed at solving
both issues simultaneously is a core objective of this thesis. A possible solution is found by
looking towards research made in the ﬁeld of unrestricted factor analysis - i.e. the general
case with L/R ∈R(D×K)/(K×N). By introducing the concept of Bayesian nonparametrics,
both sparsity and automatic complexity learning can be induced.
One of the ﬁrst and widely popular uses of Bayesian nonparametrics is the Dirichlet
Process [28, 29] and its use for mixture modeling. The Dirichlet Process allows for the
analysis of observations belonging to one of an inﬁnite number of latent mixture compo-
nents. In factor analysis, observations is assumed to be composed of many latent features
rather than belonging to a single mixture component. Speciﬁcally, each observation is
assigned a set of Bernoulli variables describing the probability that each feature is present
or not. Such feature assignments are represented using a binary matrix Z ∈{0, 1}K×N,
hence introducing sparsity. In the limit of inﬁnitely many latent features, K →∞, this
representation deﬁnes the nonparametric formulation of factor analysis.
Griﬃth and Ghahramani [30] were among the ﬁrst to deﬁne a stochastic process on in-
ﬁnitely many latent features for factor analysis models, known as the Indian Buﬀet Process
(IBP). This process was originally described using a single parameter, but was later gen-
eralized as a two-parameter IBP [31]. The IBP was later proved to be a marginalization
of the Beta Process [32], which was ﬁrst derived by Hjorth [33] for survival analysis, and
the process is a measure theoretical approach to Bayesian nonparametrics. The IBP and
Beta Process have found success in a range of diﬀerent ﬁelds, such as social network link
prediction [34], automatic exploration analysis for heterogeneous databases [35], pattern
discovery in medical disorders [36], and for independent component analysis [37].
Substantial research has been made towards how to draw inference using the IBP and
Beta Process using factor analysis. Initially, inference for IBP was made using a Gibbs
sampler with nested MH steps [30]. Later Doshi-Velez and Ghahramani [38] improved
the sampling speed by introducing ”data windows”, thus drawing inference by iterating
through random subsets of the observed data.
A Stick-breaking construction for the
IBP was introduced by Teh, G¨or¨ur and Ghahramani [39], allowing slice sampling, and a
Variational Inference (Variational Bayes) approach has also been explored [40]. Similarly
for the Beta Process, a Stick-breaking construction has been developed allowing for Monte-
Carlo integration [41], as well as Variational Inference (VI) approaches [42].
Following the work concerning Bayesian nonparametrics for general factor analysis,

1.1
Thesis contribution
This thesis is dedicated to the analysis of nonparametric priors placed solely on R as
opposed to [46], which placing inﬁnite priors on both factors. Speciﬁcally, inference will
be based on the original Gibbs sampling in conjunction with an MH step approach, and
will be be compared to the ﬁnite approximation of the Beta Process using VI in order to
shed light on the cost and/or beneﬁts of using such approximations.
Furthermore, a new approach, combining VI with Gibbs sampling, will be proposed as
an alternative to the standard Gibbs sampler. The main objective is to perform approxi-

Such methods could provide a mean for eﬃcient sampling in cases where the parameter
space for the MH step become very large. Such attempts have not been found in the
literature, which further motivates this thesis.
Finally, applications will be considered in relation to synthetic data, such as the swim-
mer dataset [6], in order to investigate general feature extraction. Additionally, real world
experiments will be carried out on EEG data, available from e.g. PysioNet.org [8–10].

The core of Bayesian statistics relies on the posterior Eq. (2.4), where uncertainties about
quantities governing the data distribution is captured. However, the posterior is often
infeasible to calculate - especially for complex Bayesian models. Even if the posterior
itself is calculable, the posterior is oftentimes subject to marginalization when future data
prediction or data generation is desired,

addressed through approximate methods, it still requires means of sampling from p(θ|D),
and while it is calculable, it can be impossible to sample from directly.
In order to accommodate these issues, how to draw inference about the posterior
needs to be carefully considered. Two widely used strategies that are used when exact
inference about the posterior is infeasible are VI also known as Variational Bayes (VB) and
MCMC sampling. They each come with their own beneﬁts and drawbacks, but essentially
VB attempts to approximate p(θ|D) in order to make Eq. (2.12) analytically tractable or
simply to arrive at a computable posterior. On the other hand MCMC methods are means
to draw from the posterior, thus arriving at exact inference in the asymptotic limit.
In relation to NMF, where the posterior is indeed diﬃcult to compute, methods such
as those mentioned above are required. Hence, in this thesis both VB and MCMC shall
be considered. However, only the special cases known as the mean-ﬁeld variational family
(VB) and Gibbs and Metropolis-Hastings sampling (MCMC) will be dealt with.

Chapter 3
Bayesian Nonparametrics
Bayesian nonparametric (BNP) modelling addresses the issue of complexity determination
in a canonical manner. This approach diﬀers fundamentally from typical methods, such as
inferring model complexity through model comparison. The model which best describes
any observed data is assumed the appropriate model. BNP modelling, on the other hand,
is deﬁned by a parameter space of unknown size, whose distributions are to be inferred.
Thus, priors can easily be placed on such distributions, in order to promote any a priori
believed behaviour, assuming that distribution is deemed needed according to the BNP
model. This property provides for more freedom in choosing appropriate priors, in cases
where the priors are selected in order to e.g. achieve ARD behaviour - such priors were
introduced in Section 2.1.2 with the purpose of pruning unneeded latent features.
For the purpose of this thesis and the analysis of Bayesian NMF, the branch of BNP
methods aimed towards the ﬁeld of factor analysis is of particular interest. Speciﬁcally,
the Beta Process [33] (Indian Buﬀet Process (IBP) [30]) allows for inferring the number
of hidden features, up to a potentially inﬁnite number. This is the main justiﬁcation of
considering a BNP approach to inferring the NMF model.
Thus, this chapter initially presents the Beta Process, which is deﬁned using a measure
theoretical formulation. Then, in order to deviate from the measure theoretical notation
and operate in a more “standard” probabilistic formulation, how to obtain the IBP from
the Beta Process is presented. This will reveal how the IBP and Beta Process is eﬀectively
identical, and thus allow the terms “IBP” and “Beta Process” to be used interchangeably.
The IBP will then be discussed in both its original one-parameter form [30] and the
later two-parameter form [31].
This will be followed with a discussion of some of its
desirable properties.
Finally, motivated by the desire to apply VB inference, a ﬁnite approximation to the
IBP will be introduced, as a ﬁnite Bernoulli Process parameterized by a Beta Process.

Thus, the npbNMFi model allows sparsity to be exactly introduced - i.e. Z governs the

perform worse than other hyperprior conﬁgurations [27]. Further, sharing the hyperpior
has the property that if an entire feature is deemed unnecessary during inference, i.e. hk →
0, the model forces any observation association to vanish, wk →0 (i = shared). For
comparative purposes, sparsity promoting priors on W in conjunction with the IBP-
prior are considered (i = sparse). Finally, in order to reintroduce the property of removing
observation association with ”dead” features, the shared hyperprior is placed on W along
with the sparsity promoting prior (i = ss), as a third considered conﬁguration.
At this point it would be appropriate to argue for introducing a binary factor Z upon
which an IBP-prior is placed (aside from the exact sparsity property). Recall from Chap-
ter 3, that for the IBP-prior, the probability for observation n to have feature k active,
depends on which features are active/inactive for all other observations. Speciﬁcally, the
more observations that have the feature k active the more likely it is to be active for
observation n also.
This dependency is arguably desired in some cases.
For instance
assume a group of children reads a subset of D books. In this case, the observed data
would be a list with the total number of books every child has read. which can be written
X = HZ, where H being a 1 × D matrix of ”hidden features” (i.e. the book names) and
Z ∈{0, 1}D×number of children. Clearly, Z holds exact information about which book in H
has been read. Now, assume almost all of Z is available such that zn is unknown for child
n. If one were to guess which books the n’th child has read given the knowledge of what
every other child have read Z−n (Z with the zn columns removed), it would perhaps be
reasonable to guess those books which were found popular by the other children. In other
words, the prior belief of zn depends on Z−n. This is parallel to customers trying dishes,
and so an IBP-prior appears appropriate, as this prior captures the notion of popularity.
In relation to the npbNMFi model proposed in this thesis, the use of such a prior is indeed
desired - as the model is envisioned to explain systems where observations would likely
share many features, and thus the prior belief for features being active is not independent
across observations.

The challenge associated with the npbNMFi model, is how to draw inference about the
posterior in the inﬁnite limit K →∞. Evidently, this can be achieved using MCMC sam-
pling methods, however, such sampling methods can prove notoriously slow and ineﬃcient
(i.e. the sample size may be needed large), especially for large dimensional models. Thus,
the often more eﬃcient (but approximate) VB approach will also be considered. Yet this
enforces a ﬁnite model approximation. This is due to the mean ﬁeld family, which places
a dedicated distribution on each parameters, which is ill-deﬁned in the inﬁnite limit.
Consequently, this chapter seeks to derive the MCMC sampling and VB methods -
i.e. exact inference (in the limit of inﬁnite samples) and approximate methods in con-
junction with a ﬁnite model. Clearly, the disadvantage of the VB method is the loss the
model’s ability to potentially learn its complexity in the inﬁnite limit. The VB method
requires an upper bound on K, and should this bound prove too small the model fails
by deﬁnition. On the other hand the MCMC sampler can ”easily” handle the inﬁnite
limit, because the sampler will be able to iteratively evaluate the need for new features.
The upside to the VB method is the speed at which inference is drawn (relative to the
sampler), as well as exact deﬁnition of each parameter’s distribution, allowing analytical
expressions for their statistical moments. These moments will always be estimates for the
MCMC sampler.
Before diving into each inference method, the reader is referred to the work of Brouwer
et al. [4]. They draw inference about a probabilistic NMF (without an IBP) model using
both VB and a Gibbs sampler (MCMC). Their update and sampling equations for all
parameters, except those associated with the IBP, are much similar to those derived here
(with a few minor exceptions). Further, a VB treatment of the ﬁnite approximation to the
Beta Process for general factor analysis is found in a paper by Paisley and Carin [42], and
an MCMC sampler is employed for the IBP-prior used for independent component analysis
(ICA) by Knowles and Ghahramani [37]. All three references are the main inspiration for
the inference methods proposed in this thesis.
Finally, a list distributions and of their moments can be found in Appendix C. These
are used to identify inferred distributions and moments.

Potential issues and caveats with VB and npbNMFi
While the theoretical derivation is sound, the practical implementation pose several po-
tential issues - especially the Truncated Normal distribution.
Due to the Truncated Normal relying on the cdf and pdf of a parent Normal distribution
numerical instabilities may arise. Hence, if lower bound ≪µ ≪upper bound (i.e. parent
mean being far outside either the upper or lower bound), all quantities of the Truncated
Normal are calculated far out in the tails of the parent Normal. In such cases the moments
of the Truncated Normal proved to be numerically unstable - despite exact analytical
expressions for the mean, variance and entropy.
A practical implementation for alleviating such issues have been introduced by Cun-
ningham1, who employs an eﬃcient approach to ﬁnding the mean and variance of the
Truncated Normal.
Alas, even this method may fail and yield negative variance and
mean. In such cases the ﬁnal fallback method is to estimate these quantities from samples
drawn from the Truncated Normal - see Section 4.2.2. These issues become especially
prominent when using Exponential priors, in conjunction with the IBP prior. The issue
lies with features being “turned on/oﬀ” more strictly when using the IBP prior - i.e. ei-
ther ∃{k, n} : zkn →0 or ∃k : hk →0 ∨wk →0, such that wkn, or hk ∨wk does not
appear in the likelihood respectively. In such instances, their posteriors (Truncated Nor-
mals) defaults to their priors. However, if the prior is diﬀerent from a Truncated Normal
(e.g. Exponential), the iterative transitions (in a VB or sampling scheme) towards the
prior from the Truncated Normal posterior becomes troublesome. Such issues are allevi-
ated almost completely by choosing the prior equal the posterior, (i.e. a conjugate prior
being the Truncated Normal).

4.3. FINAL COMMENTS ON VB AND GIBBS SAMPLING
43
The reader should note how parallel sampling is a direct extension to the parallel
update scheme from Section 4.2.1.
Similarly, all hyperparameters can be sampled in
parallel in a straightforward manner. Parallel sampling is not possible for Z, as the prior
implies dependencies across observations, while the likelihood implies dependencies across
latent features.
Feature pruning
When sampling active features, it should be noted how previously active features that
becomes turned oﬀcan never be turned on again - i.e. mk,−n = 0 ⇒zk,n = 0 ⇒zk = 0.
Whenever such instances are encountered two options are possible.
1. Keep the k’th inactive feature in memory - i.e. let Z be matrix which can only grow
when sampling new features, but inactive features will be kept as zero columns.
2. Prune the k’th inactive feature from all parameters. Then relabel the remaining
active features. Thus Z dynamically grows and shrinks as new features are sampled
and old features die.
If the pruning approach is selected one should keep in mind, that diﬀerent sampled features
cannot be compared across shared labels. e.g. , if feature k = 5 suddenly dies, and all
features k′ ∈{k > 5} are relabelled k′ −1, the new k = 5 is not to be compared with the
dead feature. The distribution of a feature can only be considered in the duration of its
active period. Consequently, when pruning is made, careful track of the feature labels is
paramount a subsequent analysis.
If however the ﬁrst approach is selected, and dead features is kept in memory, the need
for careful label tracking becomes obsolete, but at the cost of memory.
Potential issues for the Gibbs sampler
Just as for VB inference, the Truncated Normal poses issues for the Gibbs sampler. Stan-
dard samplers cannot handle, whenever sampling in the tail domain of the parent Normal
- i.e. when lower bound ≪µ ≪upper bound. Fortunately, Botev [63] has developed a
multivariate sampler, which is not only stable in (most) extreme cases, but further allows
for parallel (multivariate) sampling.
4.3
Final comments on VB and Gibbs sampling
Applying to both the Gibbs sampler as well as the VB approach to inference, speciﬁc
features labelled k cannot be compared across inference attempts. In fact both cases are
due to the inherent exchangeability of features in the model.

Chapter 5
Combined Gibbs-VB inference
This chapters presents the main contribution of this thesis. The aim is to maintain the
desired ability of the Gibbs sampler to infer a nonparametric Bayesian NMF model using
the IBP-prior, while alleviating its inability to marginalize parameters when sampling new
latent features. The proposed inference method builds on Gibbs sampling and variational
inference - hence the name Combined Gibbs-VB inference (CGVB). Such an approach
has not been found in the literature. If proven successful, could provide an alternative
way of dealing with Bayesian nonparametric models, where marginalization is assumed
paramount despite the cost of introducing approximations.
The ﬁrst part presents the proposed inference method in its most general form. Further,
due to the large training complexity associated with CGVB problem dependent heuristics
are necessary in order to make inference computationally feasible. Thus, the second part
of this chapter present the CGVB applied to the npbNMFi
∞model, along with needed
inference heuristics.
5.1
CGVB
CGVB aims at drawing inference about an unknown and unobtainable marginalized poste-

features and infer their appearances.
Further, the hyperpriors are sampled from their
prior distributions with an expected value, E [λ] = 10, i.e. a fairly small variance. Thus,
when sampling new features and associated parameters ξ, the factor elements in H or
W are likely to be small. This in turn makes them accepted with reasonable probability,
as the likelihood is indiﬀerent to ”turned oﬀ” features.
This in turn provides for the
MH sampler to ﬁnd an acceptance ratio close to one (rξ→ξ∗≈1). These features are
subsequently, likely to be kept, but as insigniﬁcant and zero-driven, or later lost again.
This would explain why K+ shows high ﬂuctuations, and also why the model seems to
sample large K+, which simply are just turned oﬀthrough either H or W . When looking
at the learned parts in Fig. 6.5, the parts which are eﬀectually active do not quite show
the true parts. This occurs even though K+ ≫Ktrue. It may be that, when sampling
features, how they are shared across observations becomes too diﬃcult when starting oﬀ
with only one active feature, as the sampler has to explore a combinatorial number of
ways features could be shared (sampled w.r.t. observed data).
As such, a similar issue may be present for CGVB. With Kinit = 1, the marginalization
of the parameters will iteratively be optimized w.r.t. K+. It may be that the VB part
get stuck in local minima, which are too diﬃcult to sample out of. Instead, new features
being accepted are simply zero-driven.
However, as both CGVB and npbNMFshared
∞
are shown to ﬁnd K+ to increase, both
models have not found a satisfactory stationary point. Rather, both models attempts to
sample better features, but instead of redistributing the part-based representation among
existing features, they attempt to sample new features, which are in turn simply driven
to zero and eﬀectively turned oﬀ. Yet, how K+ is increasing is completely diﬀerent for
npbNMFshared
∞
and CGVB. It seems as if npbNMFshared
∞
attempts to infer the appearance
of the latent features for K+ ≤25 for about the ﬁrst ∼200 iterations (representing
the ﬁrst mode of the histograms). However, not being able to infer the correct latent
features the sampler “escapes” and begins to sample new features, but is clearly unable
to redistribute/infer missing latent features.
Once K+ ∼40 the prior p(Z) probably
makes sampling any additional features unlikely - and the second mode is reached. It
seems that with the IBP, how features are shared is too diﬃcult.
It is likely due to
the combinatorial sampling problem of ﬁguring out how features should be shared among
the observations. On the other hand, CGVB keeps on accepting new features, and the
reason why is signiﬁcantly more diﬃcult to assume. However, recall that if ELBO∗of
some associated number of proposed new features is larger than ELBO the proposal is
accepted. However, as this is done sequentially, perhaps the CGVB model can better
redistribute the erroneous previously found latent features when the latent feature space
grows. It may very well do so a little by little, hence continuously accepting new features.
This behaviour may perhaps be addressed by initiating CGVB with more active features
as a more appropriate part-based solution could be found earlier in the sample path.

Additionally, these results provide further insight into how IBP aﬀects the sampler.
When uninformative priors are chosen, the sampling of and keeping new proposed features
becomes almost impossible (as expected - after all the features and weights priors are not
even remotely reminiscent of stick limbs). Further, with Kinit > Ktrue it is clearly easier for
the sampler to infer the correct number of active features and their appearances. Moreover,
a trade-oﬀbetween inferring Z and the latent features is found as a functions of the data
size. When N is small, the accurate number of active features is inferred, yet more noise is
found in H. As N grows this noise reduces and the features are more accurately inferred,
but at the cost of inferring Z. Two reasons are likely to cause this. First of all, as N
grows so does the expected number of active features, due to the IBP prior. Secondly,
the combinatorial complexity for the sampler to explore when inferring how Z looks like
increases exponentially with N, (2N×K+). This could explain why reducing the number
of features becomes increasingly diﬃcult, and hence why especially the body duplicates,
as it is present for all observations.
Then, how come that for CGVB, all observations are assigned all features? The only
plausible explanation must be that it is more expensive for the model to turn oﬀfeatures
in Z rather than through W . In other words, say feature k is redundant, CGVB can either
remove that feature through W or Z (or H) by setting the k’th row to zero in either one.
However, for CGVB to do so in Z it has to sample the representation sequentially, while
VB-optimization may be able to deduce that it can be turned oﬀin W much faster and
optimally by VB-optimization.
Results summary
From the results found associated with the swimmer data it has been shown that deﬁning
appropriate priors when employing either CGVB or npbNMFi
∞is of utmost importance in
order to compete against VB-based inference. Particularly in terms of the initial number
of active features. Yet, considering the diﬃculty of engineering such priors in general,
npbNMFi
∞is deemed inferior, and thus these results suggests that the VB-based methods
outperform the sampling-based methods. Still, compared against existing Bayesian NMF
models, a nonparametric NMF, based on VB inference, was only competitive and provided
no apparant advantages.

6.2
EEG - frequency detection and sleep stage prediction
A quantitative evaluation of the performance of each model, will be assessed by their ability
to ﬁnd informative data representations. To this end, SC recordings from the sleep-edfx-
database that is publicly available on PhysioNet.org [8–10] will be used. The data contains
a pair of PSG recordings from two consecutive nights. These recordings contain EEG data
from 19 subjects along with expert-annotated sleep stage labels. The data was labelled in
intervals of 30 s epochs. The annotated labels were done according to the Rechtschaﬀen
and Kales (R&K) classiﬁcation rules [66]. These subject can be considered independent,
and for the purpose of this thesis only the ﬁrst four subjects will be considered. These
subject will be referred to by an id counting from 0 to 3 - four subjects.
The data contains various information, but only the signal running on the Pz-Oz
channel was used, due to its success in previous classiﬁcation tasks [67, 68]. The data
further contains points well outside the sleep period, thus the period of interest for both
nights was deﬁned as 15 min prior and post sleep.
The rest of the original data was
discarded. Additionally, any epoch with missing labels or labels denoted Body movment
was also discarded. The two nights were subsequently concatenated, making one dataset
for each subject
Finally, the dataset for each subject was decomposed into the time-frequency do-
main using multi-tapered spectrograms based on the Discrete Prolate Spheroidal Sequence
(DPSS) taper functions [69]. The multi-tapered spectrograms has the property of reduc-
ing both bias and variance of the spectrograms. This produces a dataset for each subject
Spec ∈RD×N, with D = 1024 being the number of frequencies constituting an observa-
tions, and N the number of 30 s epochs making up two nights of sleep. These spectro-
grams was then log-transformed, as the low frequencies otherwise completely dominate the
datasets. Hence, the datasets used for classiﬁcation for each subject is S = ln (Spec + 1).
The log-transformed dataset covering two nights for subject with id 3 is found in Fig. 6.21.
In terms of the labels, there was original six (6) diﬀerent labels, of which two of them
were often non-existing or barely present. Thus, to gain a dataset more balanced w.r.t.
the classes, the two “underrepresented” classes were merged with the remaining four. This
leaves the four labels, Awake, REM, Light sleep, and Deep sleep to be classiﬁed.
6.2.1
Dimensionality reduction
The basic idea of employing the NMF model in order to build a classiﬁer, is that the
feature space can be reduced signiﬁcantly. This in turn reduces the number of parameters
to be trained in a classiﬁer, which lowers the risk of overﬁtting. Additionally, the new
representation would hopefully prove more informative by ﬁltering out noise and redundant
information.
As such, the classiﬁcation accuracy based on each models part-based solution serves as
an additional metric for comparison. As was the case when testing the swimmer dataset,
the ARD-NMF and ML-NMF (with K = 16, 25, 50, 75, 100) will be employed to com-
pare against standard models. Additionally, another popular method of dimensionality
reduction will be considered, namely Principal Component analysis (PCA) [47].

≈showed no
superior behaviour. The hypothesis was, that the assessment of active and needed latent
features would be captured by the binary matrix Z. Yet, the results show, that it is the
product, W ⊙Z, which captures the complete features association for each observation.
While Z was found to better encode feature association for npbNMFi
∞, it heavily depends

∞was never found

≈. As discussed earlier, one possible
explanation for the poor performance of the sampling-based model, is the combinatorial
complexity of feature assignment, which is also discussed by Doshi-Velez et al. [40]. For
each K+ there exists O(2K+×N) possible feature assignments (hard assigned). Such models
(as the IBP) provide for less ﬂexible samplers, hence they are prone to getting stuck
in local maxima of the posterior. This behaviour can somewhat be controlled through

Chapter 7
Conclusion and Outlook
The experiments showed similar results across all hyperprior conﬁgurations for each spe-
ciﬁc type of model regardless of inference method. Firstly, it was shown that automatic
complexity determination was indeed possible when choosing appropriate priors (and hy-
perpriors). Furthermore, is was shown that a uniﬁed NMF model, able to simultaneously
induce sparsity and determine model complexity, can be developed using the IBP prior.
Speciﬁcally, the approximative model, npbNMFi
≈, proved to be excellent. On the other
hand, for the exact model, npbNMFi
∞, the task was immensely more diﬃcult when in-
ference was made using both Gibbs sampling and CGVB. Speciﬁcally, inference required
carefully engineered priors (i.e. properly setting hyperprior parameters) before good per-
formances was observed. Yet regardless, of how well the latent features was inferred, only
through Gibbs sampling was feature association aptly placed the feature association in Z.
However, it was typically found in W ⊙Z. For npbNMFi
≈and when using CGVB feature
association was always found to be encoded in the product W ⊙Z.
Alas, when compared to existing probabilistic NMF models, as those considered in [4,
27], no inference advantage was found when considering a nonparametric approach. It was
found that npbNMFi
∞using Gibbs and CGVB faired signiﬁcantly worse across all exper-
iments, while npbNMFi
≈performed only competitively. Yet, because the latent feature
association was found in W ⊙Z rather than Z alone, npbNMFi
≈provide no advantages
over existing models. Only npbNMFi
∞may hold theoretical beneﬁts (by means of Gibbs
or CGVB), as it have the ability to increase the number of latent features (to potentially
inﬁnity) as needed. However, this property was never seen to be of any aid and addi-
tionally npbNMFi
≈was found both faster by about one order of magnitude per iteration,
and inference precision being vastly superior. Thus, VI proved to be better compared
to the MCMC methods. The proposed CGVB method was also not found to be able to
increase the inference precision or speed. Rather, it was found that by initializing the
number of latent features larger than the true number, CGVB defaulted to a VI scheme.
Conversely, by initializing the CGVB with fewer latent features, it showed some of the
same short-comings as when using Gibbs sampling.
The work of this thesis suggests that nonparametric Bayesian NMF is competitive at
best compared to other existing Bayesian models. The theory concerning nonparametrics

Outlook
Joint sampler for Z ∼IBP(α, β)
To address the combinatorial sampling issue associated with the IBP prior, a diﬀer-
ent inference scheme with a joint sampler on Z may be considered. If such a sampler
could be derived, e.g. by proposing Z from some reasonable proposal distribution and
accepting/rejecting it by MH sampling, how latent features ought to be shared across ob-
servations might be easier inferred. A better sampling of Z could in turn allow for better
inference about the latent features.
Diﬀerent hyperprior conﬁgurations
As seen throughout the results of this thesis, the feature association for each observation
proved to be found as the product W ⊙Z, of the weights W and binary matrix Z. Yet
it would be desirable to capture that association in Z alone. However, the variance of
the weights and latent features was attempted inferred individually for each latent feature
hk shared with the corresponding weights wk.
It was also attempted with placing a
separate element-wise variance on each element in W . One conﬁguration not considered
was placing a single variance (to be inferred) shared by all latent features in H, and a
single ﬁxed variance on all weights in W , which is done by Knowles and Gharahmani [37].
Such a conﬁguration may give further insights into the behaviour of the NMF model with
an IBP prior as the IBP has proven successful for other models.
