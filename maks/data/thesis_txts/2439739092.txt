Abstract
In this thesis a framework for using model predictive control to control a reactor in terms of pH and
temperature is presented. A model of pH and temperature is obtained as index-1 differential algebraic
equations where it was shown that pH determination can be transformed into an eigenvalue-problem and that
temperature can be related to the internal energy by the means of enthalpy. It is shown how to transform the
index-1 differential algebraic equations formulation into standard ordinary differential equations which is
used for the model predictive control design.
The MPC framework requires state estimation which was obtained using a stationary Kalman ο§¹lter and
an output disturbance model that ensures offset-free control.
The regulator design gives a step-by-step procedure of implementing common objectives, namely the
reference tracking objective, the disturbance rejection objective, the regularization objective, and the target
objective of the control signals.
The unconstrained controller is exempliο§¹ed in a simulation environment that resembles a real setup of the
reactor. Explicit examples are made on how tuning may be done intuitively to obtain various objectives. The
performance of the MPC algorithm is compared to classic PI-control where it is shown that the performances
are quite similar in this system.
Finally, a simulation environment and controller design is provided in Matlab code along with a proposal
for python implementation of unconstrained model predictive control feedback.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This study is motivated by the desire to establish an experimental setup where temperature and pH may
be controlled using model predictive control (MPC). In addition, the control system should be robust to
disturbances such that offset-free reference tracking is achieved. It is the purpose of this study to provide a
derivation and an analysis of a general framework for pH and temperature control using a Continuous Stir
Tank Reactor (CSTR) model with a cooling/heating jacket. This framework may be of particular interest for
various chemical and biological production industries.
The task is two-fold; deriving a model of the system and constructing the MPC algorithm necessary to
control temperature and pH. The block diagram in Fig. (1.1) illustrates the objectives of these tasks. The
plant is controlled using control inputs u and the measured outputs are z. Furthermore the plant is subject to
disturbances d. Naturally, this study will start with the region marked "Modelling" concerning making a
linear model of the plant ie. the CSTR. The modelling is conducted using index-1 Differential Algebraic
Equations (DAE) in semi-explicit form. They appear in the modelling of many systems and among those,
equilibrium governed chemical processes. Such systems may be described in the form:

That is, the system is governed by both differential equations and algebraic equations; Eq. (1.1a) are the
differential state equations, Eq. (1.1b) are the output equations and Eq. (1.1c) are the algebraic equations. In
the following setup there will be four differential equations and eleven algebraic equations. The ο§¹rst three
differential equations will govern the evolution of mass and the fourth differential equation will govern the
evolution of enthalpy. The algebraic equations will among other things govern the equilibrium fractions that
are valid when the species are at equilibrium for pH determination and the relation between enthalpy and
temperature. When the linear model has been achieved it is discretized according to a speciο§¹ed sample time
since the MPC strategy is implemented on a computer.
After modelling the focus is shifted towards the region marked "MPC". It consists of two blocks; an
estimator block and a regulator block. The estimator block contains a Kalman Filter that uses u and z to
provide an estimate of the states ΥÜx in the linear model which is used in the regulator block. The estimator
model is augmented to enable offset-free control in the presence of disturbances. The regulator block solves
the optimization problem of ο§¹nding the optimal control signal u by solving a linear quadratic problem. These
blocks will be elaborated in their coming respective sections.

Setup description
One of the many applications of the desired framework is to achieve a simpliο§¹ed model of a segment of
the human digestive system where the most interesting segments may be the stomach. However, due to the
complex nature of the stomach (Loomis Jr. 2018) it is unlikely the proposed framework will provide satisfac-
tory resemblance. Instead it is attempted to model the small intestines in hope for a decent resemblance.
This way, the control objectives are deο§¹ned in terms of desired set-points; that is, keep pH equal to seven and
temperature equal to 37 β½ΠC as is approximately the conditions in the small intestines of a human. (Evans et al.
1988) The model will not take biological content into account. It only considers the chemical conditions,
that in turn are essential for all biological activity. To manipulate pH and temperature to follow a reference it
is necessary to introduce actuators and sensors. pH is manipulated through the inlet of acid, base and buffer
solution while the temperature is manipulated by means of the temperature in a heating/cooling jacket. pH
and temperature are measured directly using designated sensors. The experimental setup is illustrated in
Fig. (1.2).

2.2
Modelling the evolution of pH
It is customary to model pH using either the dynamic model approach or the algebraic equation ap-
proach(Hofmann et al. 2008) (Glaser et al. 2014) (YlΟ©n 2001) (Ganesh et al. 2017). The dynamic model
approach involves writing the kinetic rate equations for each species in the reaction. Combining the dynamic
equation they constitute a system of Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs). The variables are the con-
centrations of the species in the solution and their steady-state values are the equilibrium concentrations.
The ODEs are founded from the general mass action kinetics theory. An inherent part of this approach is
knowing the forward and backward rates related to the equilibrium constants. They are usually not known or
hard to estimate correctly. However in this case, the pH dynamics are much faster than the ο§²ow dynamics.
The transients are therefore of less importance since equilibrium has been reached. It is thus tolerable that
the guessed rates are not perfectly matching the true rates. The equilibriums can be solved for with a feasible

3.1.1
Offset-free control
To achieve offset-free control in classic PID control it is known that P-control is not sufο§¹cient to guarantee
offset-free control in the presence of unmodelled disturbances or model mismatch. In the PID framework it is
thus customary to include an integrator in the regulator, that integrates the tracking error and thereby achieves
offset-free tracking. Much analogous to the classic P-control, an MPC algorithm by itself cannot achieve
offset-free control in the presence of unmodelled disturbances or model mismatch. Hence in the MPC
framework offset-free control is achieved by augmenting the system states with integrating disturbance states.
It was shown in (Pannocchia and Rawlings 2003) that a number of integrating disturbance states equal to the
number of measured variables is sufο§¹cient to guarantee zero offset in the controlled variables. With nz = 2 it
is thus known that augmenting the nominal model with two integrating disturbances is sufο§¹cient to guarantee
zero offset in the controlled variables. The number of disturbance states will be denoted np = 2. The ο§¹rst
integrating state integrates disturbances on pH and the second integrates disturbances on temperature. One
can say that the various disturbances and model mismatches have been lumped into either a disturbance on
pH or a disturbance on temperature where the origins of the disturbance is unknown.

3.2
The regulator design
Now that the estimator block has been elaborated, the focus will be shifted onto the regulator block in
Fig. (1.1). This section serves the purpose of presenting one approach of formulating the MPC problem.
In perspective to classic PID control, control objectives as overshoot and control effort are indirectly
fulο§¹lled by means of PID gains. The immediate problem is that it is not intuitive what gains that ensure the
limits of the system are not violated.
The MPC framework solves the optimization problem of ο§¹nding the optimal control sequence that fulο§¹lls
the control objectives in compliance with desired constraints. It effectively means that constraints can be
included in a tangible way while also giving the means for intuitive tuning. The transparency of constraints is
desirable for many reasons; it increases safety, it may be used to increase output quantity and quality, and it
takes actuator limits into account that effectively gives a more desirable response. In the MPC framework the
control objectives may be formulated as weighted Least Square (LS) problems. A generalized representation

necessary to cover the key dynamics. So one way of decreasing the size of the prediction horizon is to
decrease the sampling frequency if the control objectives allow for it. This way fewer predictions ahead
are necessary. Another approach is to develop better solvers for the individual problem. This study will
determine a reasonable size of the horizon based on sample frequency and will not elaborate on different
solvers.

3.2.3
Input- and soft output-constrained MPC
In addition to inputs constraints it is often desired to add output constraints to the QP Problem since
many systems have output boundaries that should not be violated. These constraints will be denoted rmin,n
and rmax,n and will therefore be part of the QP Problem as additional constraints.
In some cases one may impose too strict constraints on the QP Problem, rendering it non-convex. That is,
the solver cannot ο§¹nd a control sequence that satisο§¹es all constraints. Therefore, it is customary to introduce
soft constraints to the output when input constraints already are present. The soft constraints mean that the
solver is allowed to violate the output constraints by some margin, sn or tn, to provide more robustness to
the solution of QP Problem; Hence the name soft output constraints. However, even though the constraints
are softened, it is the desire to only break the constraints in a minimal sense. Hence the marginals sn and tn
should be minimized too for n = 1, 2, ..., N.

One may argue that the response on pH is rather slow even though the tracking error is highly penalized
compared to control effort. However this is simply the nature of the deο§¹ned disturbance. Whenever a ο§²ow
disturbance like the simulated disturbance of 0.1 M HCl occurs, then the ο§¹rst state CA in the reactor starts
to change. What should be noted is that the change does not happen immediately. It will have the largest
change in the beginning which will decrease until steady-state is reached. This means that even though a
constant step disturbance is applied to the system it has to go through the dynamics of the system before it
can be completely rejected. So it does not matter how aggressive the weights are tuned towards disturbance
rejection. In fact, the weights may be too aggressively tuned.
If the desire is to have faster responses on the step disturbance responses there are a couple of different
options. One easy cheap possibility is to lower the volume of the solution of the reactor to well under one
liter. Then changes in concentrations happen much faster. However it should be noted that industrial process
probably do not ο§¹nd this idea appealing due to less being produced. Another approach is to employ faster
ο§²ows. The available pumps used in this study are not rated for high ο§²ow rates, hence it would be necessary
to invest in bigger pumps. A third approach would be to use a higher concentration of solutions. The second
and third option are more expensive in the long run, though.
Looking at the temperature response it is evident that it is faster than the pH dynamics. If even faster
responses are required for the temperature it is necessary to ο§¹nd alternative approaches for heat transfer.
Two options could for instance be transferring heat using advection or be acquiring another reactor with a
much higher heat transfer coefο§¹cient, Uca, through the jacket. On the other hand, the controller does not
include the dynamics of the circulation heater loop that would cause the temperature response to be much
slower. Refer to Appendix (B.3). Even though the dynamics in the circulation heater loop are faster than the
dynamics of the loop controlled by the MPC algorithm they are not negligible. It will have huge effect on the
performance of the MPC controller because it assumes the temperature in the jacket will reach the desired
temperature instantaneously and will unquestionable render the response on temperature in Fig. (4.2) too
optimistic and is most likely even unrepresentative for the real experiment. Hence, from this point on it is
asked that the reader will comply with the assumption that the jacket temperature can change instantaneously.
Speaking of temperature changes, it may be seen in Fig. (4.2) that the jacket temperature does not change
even though there is a disturbance on temperature. However it is seen that the inlets adjust to remedy the
change in temperature. This is generally not the desire, as the reactor jacket should govern controlling the
reactor temperature, not to mention that larger disturbances would demand unreasonable behavior from the
inlets. One way to remedy the shortcoming of control action of the jacket temperature is to divide the MPC
controller into two separate controllers. However, the framework does allow another approach, namely to
change the weight on the fourth control input. Lowering the penalization of control action on TJ dramatically
will ensure that the temperature in the jacket will be used instead of the inlets to control the temperature.

4.2
Unconstrained MPC reference tracking
In addition to the discussed properties of MPC, it was also mentioned that the framework provided
the means to follow reference signals in an optimal sense. If the reference is known in advance, the MPC
algorithm can utilize this information for improved tracking performance. An example of this is shown
in Fig. (4.7) where it is compared to the performance of the designed PI controller. Depending on the
requirements of the system one could argue that both controllers provide satisfactory reference tracking.
There is slightly more overshoot in the PI-control response, though. It can be seen that the control inputs
for both the PI-loop and the MPC-loop both saturate to obtain the best reference tracking. Once again it
should be mentioned that the temperature response and the control input TJ are rather optimistic and is far
from what would be realizable with the current setup. To obtain tracking that takes the input constraints
into consideration one could naturally try implementing an input constraint controller using the provided
framework.
Up until this point no attention has been shown to the size of the prediction horizon. In Sec. 2.6.3, it was
mentioned that the sampling time was chosen to be 60 seconds. This number came as a result of conducting
step reference changes to the nonlinear plant model and evaluating how many steps ahead that must be
predicted to cover the key dynamics of the system. In Fig. (4.7) it may be seen that a prediction horizon of N
= 20 is sufο§¹cient to encapsulate the dynamics of the system.
In case the prediction horizon turned out to be too large, the sample time could be increased. Based
on the slow dynamics of the system and the ο§¹tting prediction horizon it is warranted that the sample time
settles at 60 seconds. For the linear case, one may argue that a large sample frequency causing a large N
does not matter since the gain matrices are computed ofο§²ine. However, too long predictions may cause
the results of the ofο§²ine calculated matrices inaccurate. Far out predictions also beneο§¹t from having very
accurate modelling of the plant.
As a ο§¹nal general remark on the results, it is evident that the tuning parameters are not optimal in a
general sense. It highly relies on the available equipment and the objectives of the plant. The provided
tuning and simulations serves the purpose of showing that the framework works in a simulated environment.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
It has been shown that pH and temperature can be modelled as index-1 DAE. The derivations started with
modelling the mass balance that yielded three differential equations governing the evolution of concentrations
of HCl, NaOH and H3PO4. Using the equilibrium equations for the species, it was shown how pH could be
computed by transforming the problem of ο§¹nding the oxonium ion concentration into an eigenvalue-problem.
It was then shown how temperature could be related to the internal energy of the system using enthalpy
yielding a fourth differential equation. Thereafter the DAE was transformed into ODE that was used for
controller design.
The Kalman ο§¹lter was used to obtain estimates of the states that are used in the MPC framework. The
output disturbance model was used to integrate disturbances in an augmented matrix representation, such
that offset-free control was acquired.
It was shown how the framework of MPC is used to achieve various objectives, namely the reference
tracking objective, the disturbance rejection objective, the control effort limitation objective and the target
objective. It has furthermore been shown how the MPC formulation can be extended to include input
and output constraints such that nonlinear control feedback may be obtained. The implementation of the
simulations has been exempliο§¹ed with Matlab code and an example on how to implement linear MPC
feedback is exempliο§¹ed in a python function.
The results of the unconstrained MPC has been exempliο§¹ed with the presented setup in Fig. (1.2). It
has been discussed that the prediction horizon should at least cover the key dynamics of the system to yield
the correct predictions. It was shown how to tune the weights in the MPC framework intuitively and how
they inο§²uence the pH and temperature responses. It was seen that the present setup has redundant actuators
and that the task of controlling the reactor in terms of pH and temperature could be simpliο§¹ed to classic
PI-control. The MPC algorithm did better on couplings in the system, though. Finally it was shown how the
MPC algorithm is superior to classic PI-control when following a reference and the reference is known in
advance.
It was deemed that classic PI-control would sufο§¹ce for the presented setup, however the framework for
MPC still stands and may be employed in more complicated systems where either constraints are about to be
violated or there are strong couplings in the system.

CHAPTER 6
Future Work
An interesting and necessary topic for future work would be to model the actuator dynamics with ο§¹rst
order dynamics and perhaps even include time delay in the model. This would yield a much more realistic
simulation result when compared to the real system. Regarding modelling it could also be interesting to
model the temperature dependency of the equilibrium constants. This way a more accurate model is achieved
that reο§²ects the true plant better.
In addition it is also of interest to test the system using an input constrained and an input- and soft output
constrained controller where the limits of the actuators would normally have been violated to display the
nonlinear control feedback provided by the MPC algorithm. It was additionally shown in (Prasath and
JΟΗrgensen 2009) that the soft output constrained MPC provides signiο§¹cantly better control performance than
nominal MPC in the face of plant-model mismatch which could be ideal to test in this setup.
One general challenge when trying to control pH in a reactor with living organisms is that one is not
guaranteed linear operational space. In this setup the buffer capacity of H3PO4 provided the conditions
that made it possible to implement linear controllers like classic PI-control to provide satisfactory control.
One may also imagine that the buffer capacity may change over time which would make a ο§¹xed linear
controller undesirable. It comes back to the general trade-off between performance and robustness. It
could be interesting to try estimating the buffer capacity of a system and then adjust the controller gains
accordingly.
It is also of interest to explore how the QP problem subject to constraints can be solved in the python
environment, as it is a cheaper alternative to Matlab. As of yet, it was possible to solve the QP Problem
using the cvxopt-package, however an error arose when the constraints on the control sequence al and au
were included.
