Abstract
In this project we synthesize size-selected Ni-doped MoS2 nanoparticles and study their
shape, structure and chemical states. The particles are produced with a new physical syn-
thesis method based on magnetron sputtering and in-ﬂight sulﬁdation of the nanoparticles,
which are then mass-selected with a quadrupole mass spectrometer (QMS) afterwards. By
x-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) we found that we were able to tune the sulﬁdation
degree of the nanoparticles, i.e. we could make Ni-doped MoSx. With transmission elec-
tron microscopy (TEM) we made size distributions of three diﬀerent size of nanoparticles,
concluded that we indeed could size-select our nanoparticles in the nanometer range. The
nanoparticles were visualised by scanning tunnelling microscopy (STM). Diﬀerent sub-
strate was used for diﬀerent experiments and we found that Ni-doped MoS2 nanoparticles
form platelets when deposited on an Au(111) surface but transform into spherical particles
upon annealing. On TEM grids of graphene we found spherical particles, but also a few
examples of platelets for the smallest particles with a diameter of 3 nm. On these platelets
we were able to get atomic resolution revealing information about the atomic structure.
However these particles were not fully sulﬁdized. At the very end of this project we did
a preliminary activity measurement of our nanoparticles. The nanoparticles were tested
for ultra deep hydrodesulphurization in which they shown some activity encouraging more
research on this system.

Chapter 1
Introduction
It is a well-known fact that the global temperature increase is, to some degree, caused by
increasing CO2-emission by humans. The CO2-emission is partly generated by the burning
of fossil fuels, i.e. oil, coal and natural gas. Our current worldwide energy consumption is
∼17 TW and in 2050 the number is estimated to be 30 TW. The large increase in energy
consumption is mainly due to the increasing population and general increase in living
standards worldwide. This is known as the ”tera-watt challenge” named by R. E. Smalley
[2]. We clearly need to reduce our emission and prepare for the increasing worldwide energy
consumption. On the COP21 meeting in Paris the involved parties signed an agreement
to keep the temperature increase less than 2 ◦C compared to that of pre-industrial level
and an increase less than 1.5 ◦C is pursued. [3]
If we really want to create a sustainable solution we have to turn our interest to renewable
energy sources. 23000 TW of energy is hitting the surface of the earth coming from the
sun, more than enough to keep our wealth increasing without any CO2-emissions. These
23000 TW can be captured directly as solar energy or indirectly by wind and water energy.
The main problem is that only half of the earth is illuminated by the sun at a given time,
so if we also want to use energy during the night we most certainly have to look into a
way of storing the energy. if not for energy consumption during night then for the part
of the infrastructure where we are going ”oﬀ-grid” and have to carry the energy with us.
Other parameters like weather and seasons also have an eﬀect on our ability to produce
power from sustainable sources.
One way to come around this problem is to store the solar energy in chemical fuels. This
could be done by making hydrogen or methane or even methanol which we can almost
use directly in our current combustion engines. For all of these energy storage methods,
we need to optimize the process to get a high eﬃciency, so we do not need to cover all
of our land with solar cells and make the solution economically feasible. The eﬃciency is
often quite low and involves a lot of extra energy for the process. In all of these processes
a catalyst is used to decrease the energy cost. A catalyst is a material that increases the
process rate without being consumed. If we want to increase the eﬃciency or decrease the
cost, we need to ﬁnd new and better catalysts.

Another issue is the scarcity of materials, as an example, platinum can be used to catalyze
the electrolysis of water to oxygen and hydrogen which works at a fairly high eﬃciently,
but the downside is that platinum is expensive and a scarce material which is not suitable
to larger scale remembering the ”tera-watt challenge”. [4]
Emission of other gasses than CO2 can also cause problems for nature. NOx from diesel
fuel is the most known gas to pollute the air. This ﬁeld is known as environmental catalysis.
The emission of sulfur from natural gasses and petroleum have a negative eﬀect on nature.
When too much sulfur is released into the atmosphere consequence is acidic rain which is
not good for biology of nature. Here a catalyst is used to remove the sulfur before the
combustion of the fuel. As the more and more sulfur is removed it gets more diﬃcult to
remove the rest, hence a need for a better catalyst.
Motivation and Goal for this Project
In this project, we investigate Ni-doped MoS2 nanoparticles, which are expected to be
a good catalyst for hydrodesulfurization, i.e. the removal of sulfur in natural gas and
petroleum. MoS2 is a well-known catalyst for hydrodesulphurization and previous work
has indicated that it can be used as a cheap catalyst for Hydrogen Evolution Reaction
[5]-[6].
In this project, we will use a new synthesis method based on magnetron sputtering with
in-ﬂight sulﬁdation nanoparticle, which is then mass-selected, to produce diﬀerent sizes of
nanoparticles and investigate their shape and structure. This include varying the sulfur
content when doing in-ﬂight sulﬁdation during the aggregation of Ni-doped Mo nanoparti-
cless. We will study how precise we can size-select the particles and investigate the anneal-
ing process. All these operations are made in a Ultra High Vacuum (UHV). The particles
are characterized by scanning tunnelling microscopy (STM), scanning transmission elec-
tron microscopy (STEM), transmission electron microscopy TEM and X.ray photoelectron
spectroscopy (XPS).

2.1
Heterogeneous Catalysis
A catalyst is used to promote a chemical process without being consumed. In this thesis,
we look at heterogeneous catalysis, i.e. where the phase of the catalyst and the reactants
are diﬀerent. In this case, the reactants are gaseous and the catalyst is solid. The catalytic
process is taking place at the surface of the catalyst. One example of a catalytic process
could be
AB+∗⇔AB∗
(2.1)
AB∗⇔A∗+ B∗
(2.2)
A∗+ B∗⇔A + B + 2∗
(2.3)
Where the asterisk is refers to a site on the catalytic surface. Equation 2.1 shows that
a molecule of element A and B is absorbed onto a empty reaction site of the surface.
Equation 2.2 shows that the two elements are dissociated and equation 2.3 shows that the
products are desorbed from the surface again. For a catalytic process, the energy needed
to drive the process is less than the same reaction in pure gas phase. The diﬀerent steps
are also sketch in an energy diagram in ﬁgure 2.1.
The turn over frequency (TOF) is the number of reactions at a reaction site per time.
When optimizing a catalyst preferable either the number of active sites or the TOF of each
site should be increased. A model has been develop by B. Hammer and J. K. Nørskov [7]

Sabatier principle
The requirements for a good catalyst is that it binds the reactants to the surface and
release the products in a suitable way. It is clear that if the reactants do not bind properly
to the surface there will be no reaction on the surface. Likewise, if the products of the
reactions can not be released from the surface, the reaction site is then blocked reducing
the activity. This principle is known as the Sabatier principle which can be visualized by
having the activity of the catalyst on the y-axis and some parameter, e.g. Gibbs free energy
for binding on the x-axis. The top of the so-called volcano will be the best candidates for
a catalyst to a given reaction. An example is shown in ﬁgure 2.2, where the parameter
is the Gibbs free energy of adsorbed hydrogen on the surface, and the activity measure is
the resulting current in a electrochemical set-up.

Nanoparticles
The argument for using nanoparticles as catalysts is two-fold. The ﬁrst is an optimization
of the catalyst because we know that the catalytic process takes place on the surface, so
by increasing the surface area less catalyst is needed. The other argument is the change in
reactivity of the catalyst when we introduce defects like steps, corners and kinks. When
considering a corner atom or step, the number of nearest neighbours are lower than for
atoms on a ﬂat surface. Since the atoms are missing neighbours, they have less electrons
in the d-band, which changes the reactivity. An example is gold nanoparticles. As seen
in the above section, gold has a fully ﬁlled d-band and is therefore not reactive.
But
for Au nanoparticles, the local d-band is not full and the particles get reactive.
It is
shown that the diameter, and hence the amount of defects, inﬂuence the reactivity of the
nanoparticles. [9]

2.2
Hydrodesulfurization(HDS)
In the last part of this project we were able to get an activity measurement on our Ni-doped
MoS2 nanoparticles. In the following we will brieﬂy go through the relevant reaction.
HDS is a hydrotreating process were sulfur is removed from natural gas or petroleum. If
the sulfur is not removed, then SO4 gas is released upon burning of the fuel. High concen-
trations of SO4 in the atmosphere causes acid rain. As the production of comsumption of
oil and gas is increased, the need for HDS increases as well to keep the level constant. As
the sulfur contents of natural gas or petroleum decrease, the sulfur is found in more and
more complex molecules resulting in increasing steric hindrance, i.e. making the reaction
more diﬃcult due to the increasing size of the molecules. The process of removing the
last small amount of sulfur is called ultra deep HDS. Sulfur is found in many complex
compounds but one of great interest in the ultra deep HDS regime is dibenzothiophene
(DBT). There are many diﬀerent ways that DBT can decompose, but one of the main
reactions is DBT into biphenyl (BP). This process is shown below:

2.3
Literature on MoS2
MoS2 consists of molybdenum, which is a transition metal with the d-band half ﬁlled, and
sulfur, which is a non-metal. MoS2 is a layered material like graphite held together by van
der Walls forces.
The band structure of a layered MoS2 material is shown in ﬁgure 2.4.
Depending on
the layer structure the d-band is divided into 2 or 3 bands. In ﬁgure 2.4 b) we see the
hexagonal structure that we most often expect when we see layers of MoS2. Here we see
the d-band divided into two bands of which one is half full and one is empty. [11]
In this project we will be focusing on nanoparticles, so we will not go into detail about
MoS2 as a 2D material.
MoS2 has been investigated with great interest in literature. MoS2 has many applica-
tions ranging from lubrication to catalysis. Nanoparticles in particular have been showing
promising properties and have been investigated by diﬀerent groups. In the following sec-
tions, we will go through some of the literature on MoS2 divided into synthesis, substrate
dependence, shape, structure, theoretical predictions and activity for diﬀerent chemical
reactions.

Synthesis
Synthesis of MoS2 particles can be divided into a chemical method and a more physical
method.
To have a feasible and cheap method of producing MoS2 particles, chemical
methods is deﬁnitely the way to go, but physical methods is also of great importance
because it enables more precise control of the properties of the nanoparticles. We will not
dig into diﬀerent chemical methods, since this a whole chapter for itself and is not relevant
for this project.
Synthesis methods are investigated in the work of E. Stoﬀels et. al. [1]. Vapours of MoCl5
and S are introduced into a thermal oven with a carrier gas of argon. When introduced
and mixed in the thermal oven at temperatures up to 460 ◦C, the MoS2 particles are
allowed to form. The particles are collected either on a radio frequency electrode or a
silicon disk. A sketch of the set-up is found in ﬁgure 2.4. The produced particle size were
ranging from 100 nm and up when using the silicon disk. To increase the production rate,
a radio frequency electrode, which makes a ring shaped plasma, is introduced instead of
the silicon disk. The temperature of the thermal oven was in this case 150◦C. They found
that particles were either deposited in the center of the ring shaped plasma or trapped in
the plasma itself. When the plasma is turned oﬀparticles are deposited where the plasma
is on the electrode. By controlling the duration of active plasma they could control the
deposition.
The particle sizes varied with the plasma time, where particles with sizes
smaller than 100 nm were found for plasma times less than 1 min and micrometer sizes
for longer times.
In almost all literature, post-annealing of the nanoparticles is done to ensure proper sulﬁ-
dation and structure. In work of A. J. Jacobson [12], the eﬀect of annealing temperature is
examined. By X-ray diﬀraction (XRD), they found that the untreated synthesized MoS2
was amorphous. By doing XRD and heating to diﬀerent temperatures, they found that
crystallization of MoS2 begins between 200 ◦C and 300 ◦C, but is not completed before
800 ◦C. This work gives us an indication of how MoS2 behave during diﬀerent anneal-
ing temperatures, hence we can estimate the structure of our particles by knowing the
annealing temperature.
Another XRD study of MoS2 is found in the work of E. Borsella [13].
Nanoparticles
are chemically synthesized by using a CO2 laser and precursors of Mo(CO)6 under an
atmosphere of H2S. By XRD, they estimate the nanoparticle size to be 3 nm with a
spherical shape. Since they are able to do XRD, we may assume that the samples was
somewhat crystalline. By using XRD, they found the grain size of the crystalline domains
and not particle sizes, which is not mentioned in the article. Afterwards, the samples are
annealed for 24 hours at 1000 ◦C under argon. Here they found that the particle diameter
(or grain size) has increased to 20-24 nm, which is what one could expect due to increasing
crystallinity or particle sintering. So as expected, we clearly have sintering at 1000 ◦C.

as Pt, but better then found in the article below by T. F. Jaramillo et. al. [6]. By cycling
the catalyst 1000 times and measuring the i-V curves with no notable loss they concluded

Doped MoS2 nanoparticles
In the work of A. Vazquez [19], MoS2 doped with Ni and Co were investigated using
high resolution TEM. In this article, the doped MoS2 was made with a wet chemistry
method with a dopant percentage of 30 % and afterwards heat treated at 400 ◦C for 4

Summary
The structure and shape of MoS2 nanoparticles have been investigated in many diﬀerent
studies. In most studies, particles have been annealed at temperature above 400 ◦C, where
it has been shown that MoS2 is typically found in crystalline phase. Especially smaller
triangle-shaped particles have been studied both theoretically and experimentally by STM
and activity measurements. MoS2 in nanoparticle form has shown activity in HDS, HER,
ORR and in CO2 reduction. In HER, the particles were found to have high activity, but
not as high as platinum. The argument for sticking with MoS2 and not using Pt instead is
that MoS2 is much cheaper and more abundant than platinum. Generally, the sulﬁdized
Mo-edge has been found to be the active site. MoS2 nanoparticles has been produced
chemically and by more physical methods including PVD, which allows size selection of
particles in some range. Activity measurement has been carried out for ”smaller” and
larger particles, but size-dependent studies for particles in the nano-range has not been
properly examined for activity. Diﬀerent promoting elements have been examined and it
has been found that Ni- or Co-doped particles could have higher activity, but there is no
study of what the optimal doping is.
Despite much work in investigating MoS2, many questions still remain. Is there an optimal
particle size for diﬀerent reactions?
What doping degree is the most optimal?
How
important is the annealing for diﬀerent reactions?

2.4.2
X-ray Photoelectric Spectroscopy
The photoelectric eﬀect can be used to determine chemical elements and their oxidation
state in a material. An X-ray lab source is used to produce X-rays with a well-deﬁned
wavelength. The wavelength is deﬁned by the material used as anode. A common source
is Mg, where the Kα line is used. The energy of the emitted lab source Kα photon is
1253.6 eV ± 0.7 eV.
A ﬁlament is heated to a certain temperature and by applying a voltage between the
ﬁlament and the anode in the order of tens of kilovolts, the electrons of the ﬁlament is
pulled out and accelerated into the anode. By relaxation of the electrons in the anode,
X-rays are emitted. In the Kα case, the electrons are relaxed from the 2p orbital to the 1s
orbital. The emitted photon is used to kick out electrons of interest in the sample. Due to
the quantized electron binding energies it is possible to get information about the sample
content by analysing the photoelectrons.
The energy of the emitted electron is given by
Ekin = hν −φ −Ebinding
(2.9)
where hν is the incoming photon energy and φ is the work function for the material and
the detector. The work function for the material is the energy that is needed to excite
an electron from Fermi level to vacuum level. The work function for the detector follows
the same principle, as this is the energy gained by going from vacuum level and into the
detector. Ebinding is the binding energy of the given electron in a given electron shell
respect to the fermi level. The discrete binding energies of the electrons are diﬀerent for
every atom, so this is a way of discovering which chemical compounds there is in the
sample. The principle is sketched in ﬁgure 2.7.

at the change of the peaks in the spectrum, one can evaluate the chemical state of the

compared. The area of each peak is measured and divided by the sensitivity factor which
correct the area due to the fact that some peaks gives mores intensity than others. In our
case the sensitivity factor of the C 1s is set to 1. The intensity for a given material k is

Interpreting an XPS Spectrum
The mechanism described above will give rise to a set of main peaks for each element
present in the sample. The ”ﬁnger print” of an element is thus deﬁne by the electron
structure and binding energy.
Some peaks are more intense than others, which is all
contained in the sensitivity factor. Beside main peaks, there is also some other mechanism
that will give rise to features in the XPS spectrum. We will go through the relevant ones
in this section.
Auger Peaks
Auger peaks arise from a process where an electron is emitted from an orbital. Afterwards,
the hole is ﬁlled with an electron from a higher orbital. To compensate for the energy
gained from this process an electron from the same shell or a higher shell are ejected. The
notation for the Auger peaks refers to which shells are in play, e.g. Ni LMM is an electron
ejected from the L shell, one electron going from the M to L shell and one electron ejected
from the M shell.
Satellites
When using a lab source like Mg as anode, the brightest peak is used. There is, however,
some less intense peaks going out from the anode as well. These less intense peaks will
give rise to smaller peaks at lower binding energy in the ﬁnal XPS spectrum and are well
17
==PAGE:26==
characterized for the diﬀerent lab sources. For an example, the most signiﬁcant one is
located 8.4 eV lower than the main peak with an intensity of 8 % compared to the main
peak. The feature is know as a ”satellite” peak.
Shake-up Features
When ions has released an electron there is a probability that the ion is left in an excited
state. The energy used to put the ion in an excited state is subtracted from energy of
the electron. This will cause the out-going electron to have a lower kinetic energy than
normal. In the XPS spectrum, this eﬀect is seen as a small broad bump before larger
peaks on the higher binding energy side of the peak.
Ghost Peaks
If the anode is somehow contaminated with another anode material, ghost peaks can
appear in the spectrum. Instead of having one well-deﬁned X-ray there will be one from
the contamination as well. The eﬀect will appear as shadows of the other peaks lower or
higher in the spectrum. The diﬀerence in energy between the original peak and the ghost
peak is given by the diﬀerence in energy of the X-rays from the two materials. In a typical
XPS set-up, it is possible to switch between two diﬀerent anodes. By changing the anode,
ghost peaks can be identiﬁed indicating contamination of the anode.
2.4.3
Scanning Tunneling Microscopy
STM is a surface sensitive technique used to visualize materials on nanoscale. With good
conditions and a suitable sample it is possible to get atomic resolution.
STM is a technique, where a tip is scanned over a surface to visualize properties of the
surface. By applying a bias voltage between the sample and the scanning tip it is possible,
due to a quantum mechanical phenomena, to have a tunnelling current if the tip is suﬃ-
ciently close to the sample. By either measuring the variations in the tunnelling current
when moving the tip at the same height over the sample, or measuring the position of the
tip to get a constant current, it is possible to map the electronic density of states (DOS)
of the surface, which in some cases can be translated into describing the morphology of
the surface.
Tunnelling is a quantum mechanical eﬀect, that is very sensitive to variations in distance,
governed by the design of the tip. The formula for the tunnelling current J is:
Jr ∝exp(−Aψ1/2s),
(2.11)
where A = 1.025˚A−1eV−1
2 , ψ is the average height of a planar tunnel barrier and s is the
distance between the tip and the sample.
18
==PAGE:27==
A change of distance between the tip and the sample of 2-5 ˚A will give rise to a change
around 3 orders of magnitude in the tunnelling current.[24] It is reasonable to assume
that a tip really can end up in a single atom, which is needed to get atomic resolution.
Deviations from this assumption is known as tip folding, which makes objects appear
larger than they are in the xy-plane of the sample. Generally the resosultion of better in
the z-direction, since it will not be eﬀected tip folding.
The STM can be driven in diﬀerent modes, e.g. constant current or constant height, which
are the modes we are using. Typically, constant height is used to get the best resolution,
but the possibility of tip crash is high if the sample is not well-known or ﬂat. The most
used mode is constant current mode where the tunnelling current is kept constant via a
feedback loop adjusting the height of the tip. By plotting the position of the tip, an image
is produced. If the work function is constant over the surface, the image can be interpreted
as the morphology of the surface.
With STM it is possible to get atomic resolution on the surface of the sample. The atomic
resolution can be used to determine crystal structure and step size for larger particles or
platelets. By using STM, a size distribution of synthesized nanoparticles can be achieved
by scanning a larger area, but other methods are better and more suitable techniques like
TEM.
Due to the need of a tunnelling current, possible samples are limited to electronically
conducting materials. The sample has to be relatively ﬂat, i.e. surface structure has to
be in the order of nanometers in the z-direction to avoid tip crash. Materials having a
lower electronic conductivity will appear as a hole, since the distance has to be smaller
to support the same current. For an example, Mo and Au have conductivity in the same
range so there will be no problem in getting the correct z-dependence, but if pure sulfur
is observed, it will appear as a hole since the conductivity is much lower than that of Au
and Mo.

Electron microscopy
The resolution of an optical microscope is given by the wavelength of the light, which is
in the order of 400-700 nm. Our nano particles are in the order of a few nanometer, so
an optical microscope is clearly not good enough for us. Instead of using light to visualize
our particles we can use electrons which, due to their small mass, have a much smaller
wavelength given by the de Broglie wavelength:
λ = h
p
(2.12)
Where h = 6.62607004 · 10−34 Js

is a function of the speed of the electron, which will be given be the acceleration voltage
in an electron microscope. The acceleration voltage is in the range of hundreds of keV
which gives us a wavelength in the order of a few pm. This is the theoretical resolution
limit.
The practical resolution is mostly limited by the quality of the electron lenses.

The practical limit is ranging from hundreds of nanometers to atomic resolution for high
quality microscopes. Scanning electron microscopes (SEM) is were an electron beam is
diﬀracted on the surface, which have the advantages that thick material can examined.
These microscope have a resolution of hundreds of nanometers. If we want to have a even
better resolution we need to shoot the electron beam through the sample.
Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM)
In transmission electron microscopy, the sample is illuminated by a parallel electron beam
and the image is detected by a CCD. The intensity is depending on how much of the
electrons are absorbed in the material which is depending on the thickness of the material
and the composition. Since a large area is illuminated interferences after the sample is
possible. Scattering on the atoms in the sample, gives a phase shift of the electron produc-
ing interference patterns which can be used to analyse crystals. Due to this interference
it can be diﬃcult to interpret, since the scattering mechanism is very complex and often
need complex simulations to analyze the image correctly.
Scanning Transmission Electron Microscopy (STEM)
The scanning transmission electron microscope (STEM) is a combination of SEM and
TEM where the electron beam is focussed in a point and scanned across the sample.
Each pixel on the image corresponds to the signal acquired in that point of the sample.

Methods and Experimental Details
In this section we will go through the experimental set-ups and explain the methods used
in this project. The chapter is divided into two parts, where the ﬁrst one is focussing on
the nanoparticle synthesis and the second one on the characterization of the nanoparticles.
We will start with an introduction to the vacuum chamber.
3.1
The STM312 vacuum chamber
The STM312 vacuum chamber is divided into a cluster source, load lock, high pressure
cell (HPC), garage stage and main chamber. All parts of the chamber can be isolated
from each other by gates. The cluster source is used to produce clusters and is pumped
by two turbo pumps. A typical pressure of the cluster source after baking is 2·10−9 mbar.
The load lock is pumped by one turbo pump and serves as loading lock for samples and
is used when depositing clusters on a sample. The garage stage is used to park samples
for later use. The HPC can be used for heating or high pressure experiments with inlets
of diﬀerent gasses. The HPC and the garage stage are sharing a turbo pump.
The main chamber is used for STM, XPS and ISS experiments. Annealing, sputtering
and storage of samples can also be done here. The main chamber have a larger turbo
pump with magnetic bearings minimizing vibrations, which is useful when doing STM
measurements. A typical pressure for the main chamber is 3 · 10−10 mbar.

Sample holders and substrate cleaning
Diﬀerent types of sample holders are used for diﬀerent substrates.
The sample holders have been cleaned before they are introduced into the chamber. The
cleaning process for all sample holders is sonication for 5 minutes in acetone and afterwards
in ethanol and rinsed in MilliQ water 3 three times afterwards. Acetone and ethanol is
organic solvents which dissolves the carbon on the surface loosened during sonication.
GC sheets
The glassy carbon sheets (GC sheets) used for XPS experiments are electro polished in
a chemistry lab to remove earlier deposited particles before being introduced into the
chamber. The GC sheets are mounted in a sample holder made of ”aisi 304” stainless
steel, which contains some amount of Cr, Mn, Ni and Si, beside iron. The GC sheets are
sometimes degassed, which is examined in section 4.3.1.
Au single crystal
A gold single crystal oriented in the (111)-direction (AuSC(111)) is used for STM exper-
iments. The crystal was ﬁrst mounted in a sample holder made of molybdenum, which
can be an issue when doing XPS measurements. After we realized this, the sample holder
was changed to one made of tantalum. After the AuSC is introduced in the chamber, it is
degassed by heating it up to at least 400 ◦C. When we were doing annealing experiments,
it was degassed to at least 50◦C higher than the annealing temperature. Depending on
the dirtiness of the crystal, it was through a sputtering-heating cycle up to 6 times. The
sputtering was done with a pressure of 1 · 10−7 mbar argon accelerated into the sample
by an ion gun with an energy of 1000 eV for 20 min. The AuSC is the heated to the

Heat treatment
The samples that needed annealing was ﬁtted with a C-type thermocouple on the sample
holder. The thermocouple was placed on the backside of the substrate and wired out of
the chamber enabling temperature readings.
In our chamber, we have two diﬀerent methods of heating our sample. One method is in
the main chamber in the manipulator, where a ﬁlament is located behind the sample. The
ﬁlament has a shield on the backside to shield the heating towards the sample. The ﬁlament
is heated using a power supply which we control via a labview program. The maximum
temperature that can be reached is approximately 550 ◦C. If higher temperature is needed,
a bias between the ﬁlament and the sample holder can be applied. The bias accelerate the
emmited electrons towards the sample holder resulting in a higher temperature.
Our second option is to heat our sample in the High Pressure Cell (HPC). Here a metallic
cylinder is put in contact with the backside of the sample holder. By sending current
through the cylinder, joule heating is produced. This process have a longer response time
than the heating in the main chamber, since the whole metal cylinder has to be heated.
The temperature is controlled manually by adjusting the current. This method can reach

Characterization
In the following sections we will go through the methods and details of the diﬀerent

3.4.1
XPS
The XPS spectra is acquired by shooting into the sample with photons from a Mg anode.
The acceleration voltage of the x-ray gun is 12 kV and the emission current is 20 mA. The
x-ray gun is a Specs RQ 20/38. The out-going electrons are measured by a Specs HSA
3000 plus with a pass energy of 100 eV for survey scans and 20 eV for detailed scans, and
a detector voltage of 3000 V.
A survey spectrum is acquired by scanning the binding energies from 1067 eV to 0 eV in
steps of 1 eV. Depending on the sample, this scan is collected between 4 and 10 times and
summed to minimize noise. Detailed scans are made of relevant regions with a smaller
step size of 0.1 eV and summed over up to 40 scans depending on how the signal-to-noise
ratio.
The collected spectra is handled in the software CasaXPS. The spectra are ﬁrst aligned
by calibration to a known peak position belonging to the substrate. For the GC sheets
the spectra is aligned to the C 1s peak and for the AuSC the Au 4f 7/2 peak. Firstly all
peaks in the survey spectrum are accounted for to know which elements are on the sample.
This analysis is based on a combination of the database of CasaXPS and the ”The XPS
handbook” [22].
In the detailed scans, a shirley background is ﬁtted to the peaks. The peaks are ﬁtted
with a Lorentzian lineshape. The Mo 3p peaks are ﬁtted with the constrain that the ratio
between the two peaks should be 1:2 and the Mo 3d peaks with a ratio of 2:3. The Mo
3d peaks will also have the constraint that the distance between the two peak positions is
3.15 eV in agreement with [16]. In some samples, two sets of peaks are found which are
both ﬁtted so the convolution of all peaks ﬁt properly with the data. By dividing the peak
areas with the corresponding sensitivity factors, we are able to calculate the ratio and
relative percentages of the analyzed elements. An example of peak ﬁtting with multiple
peaks is found in ﬁgure 4.6.
3.4.2
STM
Our Aarhus STM located in the main chamber is placed on a metal block which is sus-
pended by two sets of springs to act like a high/low pass ﬁlter for vibrations from the
surroundings. The, at ﬁrst, ﬁxated block is carefully released and the block is allowed
29
==PAGE:38==
to settle for 5 minutes before approaching of the tip is initiated. The tip is carefully ap-
proached to the sample by an automated ”inchworm” movement by ﬁrst driving through
the range of the piezo motors which is then retracted, if no tunnelling current is measured,
and the whole piezo motor is moved a step towards the sample. This is repeated until
a tunnelling current is measured and then the tip is retracted a little from the sample.
During approaching a threshold for the measured tunnelling current is selected. A too
high threshold current will allow the tip to dig into the sample before the threshold is
reached, so a reasonable current threshold should be selected.
After the initial approaching and ﬁne approaching is made by selecting reasonable values
for the gap voltage and the tunnelling current, the surface is approached by scanning a
small area (5 ˚A x 5 ˚A) until the tunnelling current is established. Before being able to get
good images, a good scanning tip is needed. In the following section diﬀerent methods for
getting a good tip is explained.
Getting a good tip
A good tip is essential to achieving good images.
A bad tip can cause the image to
over-/underestimate sizes. I a hole on the sample is scanned, the diameter of the hole is
underestimated. A simple way of evaluating the quality of the tip is to look at a step in
the surface. A step should be as close to a step function as possible. If the step is not
clearly visualized, a better tip is needed. Another eﬀect is having a double tip where all
features will have a shadow due the the fact that one is scanning with two diﬀerent parts
of the tip. Before moving on the get a good tip diﬀerent parameters for the voltage and
current should be tried. By slowly change these parameters a good image can suddenly
be acquired.
There are diﬀerent approaches to achieving a good tip, some rougher than others. The
methods are listed in order of increasing ”roughness”.
Give the tip time to stabilize or move to a new area
The tip can improved over time just by scanning at the same location. Atoms attached to
the tip can fall oﬀand thereby increase the sharpness. It is preferred to scan over a step
since this will increase the possibility of losing attached atoms. Increasing the scan speed
increases the probability of tip crash which can have an improving eﬀect on the tip. This
can also have the eﬀect that the tip is scraping the dirt of the surface away from the area
of the image. Simply moving to a new area with less dirt can also be a solution.
Change the polarisation of the tip
If charged particles or atoms is attached to the tip a change of polarity between tip and
sample can cause them to fall oﬀ. The charged particles or atoms is attached by the tip
having the opposite charge so when the charge of the tip is changed, they are repulsed
instead. It is important to move to a now area so the dirt is not picked up again.
Brieﬂy increasing the voltage or current
By giving short bursts of either high voltage or high current can change the tip to the
better, giving the tip diﬀerent tunnelling conditions for a short while. This could enable
30
==PAGE:39==
diﬀerent parts of the tip to be part of the tunnelling phenomena which will change the
conditions.
Increasing the voltage or current for a longer period of time
Increasing the voltage or current for longer time periods puts more power through, which
can cause local joule heating of the tip. When the temperature increases, it is more likely
that the tip will change as consequence of a tip crash.
Dipping the tip in a metallic surface
By dipping the tip in a metal surface the a sharp tip can be pulled out. If nothing else
work dipping the tip can be a last solution. It is prefered to do the tip dipping in a gold
surface since the gold is very soft compared to that of the tip, so there is minimal chance
of making to tip worse.
When a good tip is acquired the safest method is to get good resolution on larger scale and
then zoom in on interesting objects. Optimization of the tunnelling current and voltage
should be done often to ensure optimal resolution.
Data analysis of STM images
The images made with the STM, is analyzed by using SPIP. The images are imported and
a background is subtracted. It could either be a global plane background, or a line-wise
background, if features like herringbone structure is of interest. Some images had a some
high frequency noise which was ﬁltered by applying a FFT band ﬁlter. The height of
steps, particles and platelets was found by examine a line scan. If the line scan was too
noise a average over a few lines was made. To highlight diﬀerent features of the images
the colour scale is adjust accordingly.

Activity result
Activity measurement on the removal of DBT to bisphenyl (BP) was made on 3.5 nm Ni-
doped MoS2 with a coverage of 50 % resulting in a mass loading of 374 ng. Due to a nearly
empty H2S bottle we estimate that the particles to have a low sulﬁdation degree around
0.5, i.e. MoS0.5. The measurements were made by Ann-Louise Nyg˚ard Christoﬀersen. To
compare the measurements with something well-known, a commercial Pt catalyst with a
mass loading of 900 ng was tested. The activity measurements are shown in ﬁgure 4.18
and 4.19. These are made by measuring the peak area of the relevant peaks in every TOF
spectrum, i.e. one data point is the area of a given peak at a given time.
For the Pt catalyst we see that full conversion is reach in the last shown temperature
step going from 210 ◦C to 300 ◦C. The interesting region for the measurements are from
approximately 10000 s to 30000 s on the time scale. Here we see that the signal of DBT
goes down as the temperature ramps up and that BP is increasing correspondently. When
reaching full conversion the signal is 0.1 for BP. If we compare this signal to the signal we
ﬁnd at 210 ◦C the conversion rate is roughly 80 %.
For our own particles we do not see a stepwise increase in the BP as we did with Pt. We
do see an increase in the BP signal as the temperature is increasing. The change in DBT
is diﬃcult to see, so to say something about this measurement we consider the increase
in BP. If we assume the intensity at full conversion is the same for the two samples at
any temperature we calculate that we at 320 ◦C have 3.5 % compared to full conversion.

experiments.

Chapter 5
Conclusion and outlook
We have in this project shown that we can produce size selected Ni-doped MoS2 nanopar-
ticles by sputtering and afterwards doing in-ﬂight sulﬁdation. We have shown that we can
tune the sulﬁdation degree of the particles by changing the H2S partial pressure. With
STEM and TEM we have shown that we in fact are making size selected particles and we
can control the diameter of the nanoparticles. The standard deviation from mean is found
to be between 1.0 nm and 1.5 nm for particles with a diameter in the range of 3 nm to
8 nm. By STM we found the particles in shapes of platelets. Truncated shaped particles
were compared to a study made by another group showing similar ﬁndings showing an
eﬀect of Ni-doping. Our particles and this study did not show quite the same, but the
experimental conditions were not all the same. We also saw that Ni-doped MoS2 nanopar-
ticles on an Au(111) surface are transformed from platelets to particles upon annealing.
We were also able to calculate a lattice distance for a Ni-doped MoS2 platelet, which was
signiﬁcantly smaller compared to literature. We suspect the reason is due to oxidation of
a not fully sulﬁdized sample. We have investigate the eﬀect of air exposure of metallic
Ni-doped Mo nanoparticles and Ni-doped MoS2 nanoparticles. We found that if Ni-doped
Mo nanoparticles were exposed to air, around 80 % of the molybdenum is found in two
diﬀerent oxidation states. For the Ni-doped MoS2 nanoparticles an increase in oxidized Mo
was found. This sample was not fully sulﬁdized. We made one activity measurement for
ultra deep HDS reaction which shown some activity, but less than a commercial platinum
catalyst. This should motivate further activity measurements.
5.1
Outlook
We have found that by varying the H2S partial pressure it is possible to tune the sulﬁda-
tion degree. However much more experiments are needed, before being able to conclude
a precise link between these parameters. Our experiments indicates each size of nanopar-
ticles have a diﬀerent connection between H2S partial pressure and sulﬁdation degree. So
more XPS experiments is needed to clarify this.

Appendix A: D-band model
In this section we will present the d-band model describing the interaction between a
transition metal and an absorbent. The electrons in an atom are divided into discrete
energy levels given as 1s, 2s, 2p etc. orbitals deﬁned by diﬀerent quantum numbers. We
consider two hydrogen atoms, which each has a single electron in the 1s orbital when they
are far from each other. When approaching each other, the orbitals are split into two
orbitals with diﬀerent energies. The split orbitals are called bonding (lowest energy) and
anti-bonding (highest energy) orbitals. The orbitals are ﬁlled by the electrons of the two
atoms from lowest to highest energy. In this case, the two electrons goes into the bonding
orbital which makes the bonding of two H atoms energetically favourable.
We now consider metals, where the electrons are divided between core and valence elec-
trons; only the valence electrons contribute to bonding.
If we consider the transition
metals, the valence electrons are in the outermost s and d orbitals. Instead of just joining
two or three atoms we now join many atoms that can share their electrons. This will
give rise to energy levels between the lowest bonding orbital and the highest anti-bonding
orbital. Instead of getting discrete energy levels between the lowest bonding orbital and
the highest anti-bonding orbital we get a continuum of energy levels since the distance
between the levels gets inﬁnitesimally small as we ﬁll in more atoms.

reactivity falls. These are the two factors that determinates the nobleness of metals. Gold
is the one element with the largest coupling element and a ﬁlled d-band, which then can
be considered as the noblest of the metals, since it will not react with it surroundings.[29]
As the absorbent is nearing the surface the interaction with the sp-band gives rise to a

splitting of the bonding and antibonding levels. Depending on the ﬁlling degree, some of
the bonding orbitals are emptied and some of the antibonding orbitals are ﬁlled as seen

are ﬁlled the result is that it is no longer energetically favourable for the absorbent to
bond and we can have dissociation. This is know as the d-band model. So by looking at
diﬀerent ﬁlling of the d-band it is possible to tell something about the trends for reactivity.
