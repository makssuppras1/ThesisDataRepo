Abstract
In a context of global energy transition, wind turbines must be designed and optimized
according to their environment to be cost effective. Significant loads applied on wind turôÙ
bines are induced by the wind and the wake generated by upstream wind turbines in a
wind farm. Many ways to compute the wake of wind turbines exist. It can be either anaôÙ
lytical using engineering wake models or numerical using Computational Fluid Dynamics
(CFD) such as Large Eddy Simulations (LES). The analytical approaches are not able to
capture dynamic wake interactions neither the turbulent properties of the flow whereas the
numerical approaches remain too costly and time consuming. Thus, intermediate modôÙ
els as the Dynamic Wake Meandering models are able to provide flow realisation taking
into account turbulence at various scales. On the other hand, Reduced Order Models
(ROM) are being developed to simulate turbulent wind turbine wake more accurately in
a faster way using proper orthogonal decomposition (POD). Afterward, this turbulent flow
is applied on a wind turbine model to realise aeroôÙelastic simulations using FLEX5. From
this simulation, many quantities are computed such as the power production, the deflecôÙ
tions or the Damage Equivalent Loads (DELs) on wind turbines. The aim of this thesis is
thus to study the wake loads on wind turbines using the ROM generated turbulent flows.
Hence, the project is to build data driven models, called surrogate models, to predict the
power production and the loads knowing few statistical information on the inflow. This
will replace both the flow realisation and the aeroôÙelastic simulations. To this purpose,
a database must be built to train these models from both LES and ROM inflows. Then,
the surrogates will take as inputs either statistics of the overall wind speed or statistics
of the POD modal timeôÙseries, corresponding to flow scales decreasing with their ranking
made by the decomposition. Analysing the performances of the surrogates reveals that
such models are able to estimate quickly and accurately the power production and the
load distributions on wind turbines in wake operating conditions with an expected error
below 1.5% of the maximum observed value and a low deviation. Subsequently, the load
surrogates are improved by using statistics of the first POD modes statistics except for
the blade root edgewise load being gravity dominated. However, the power production is
already well predicted by the overall statistics meaning that its surrogate is not affected by
small turbulent scales. Moreover, the modal based surrogates are even able to estimate
accurately loads on turbines operating in atmospheric conditions contrary to surrogates
based on integrated statistics. Finally, there is plenty of room for improvement, in parôÙ
ticular to train the load surrogates on various wind conditions to take into account partial
wake or extreme events.
Keywords: Wind Turbine, Power production, Damage equivalent load, Proper orthogonal
decomposition, Gaussian process, Surrogate models

Introduction
In a context of global energy transition, wind energy needs to be scaled up by four times
in this decade in order to meet the Paris Agreement goal of 1.5ãÎC [1]. To this purpose,
wind turbines must be designed and then optimized according to their environment using
modelling tools. That is the reason why the loads on wind turbines must be estimated
during the development stage of wind farms. One of the main loads comes from the
wind, in particular from the wake generated by upstream wind turbines in a wind farm.
Therefore, understanding and modelling turbulent wakes is of utmost importance to be
cost effective for wind farm developers and wind turbine manufacturers [2] [3]. This master
thesis takes place in the ongoing research on wake models to estimate accurately and
quickly the fatigue loads on wind turbines. This type of load corresponds to a progressive
and accumulative deterioration of the material induced by cyclic loading such as the forces
and moments acting on the wind turbines.
1.1
Background
Broadly speaking, gathering wind turbines into clusters, in order to reduce operation and
maintenance costs, causes several modifications of the wind turbine inflows depending on
their position in the wind farm. Indeed, the interaction between the atmospheric boundary
layer (ABL) and the blades of the front wind turbines are responsible for flow disturbances
downstream: it induces a wake flow. Indeed, the blades create a pressure drop, causing
the air to accelerate around the blades and geerating a region of low pressure and high
turbulence behind the turbine. Defining the inflow u(t) with its mean component ô₤u and
the fluctuations uãý in a Cartesian coordinate system according to the Equation 1.1, the
turbulence intensity (TI) can be expressed depending on the standard deviation of the
velocity fluctuations ü(uãý) in the Equation 1.2 which is increased in the wake region.

The wake is characterized by a turbulent flow that can extend several rotor diameters D
downstream of the turbine [4]. As a result, the wake significantly impacts the performance
of downstream turbines in a wind farm. One of the main wake effects is the reduction of the
power production, but it also consequently modifies the fatigue loads on the downstream
wind turbines.
Flow around a wind turbine
The flow around a wind turbine is highly complex, but it
can be divided into two main regions for the sake of simplicity: upstream and downstream
the wind turbine, according to the Figure 1.1.
ãÂ The upstream flow corresponds to the inflow of the wind turbine. It can be divided
into two subôÙregions depending on their distance to the wind turbine:
ã The freeôÙstream region: This is the region where the incoming wind is undisôÙ
turbed by the presence of the wind turbine. The flow in this region is typically
characterized by the ABL depending on the surface roughness.
ã The induction region: As the wind approaches the rotor blades, the ABL is
modified by the presence of the wind turbine leading to a wind speed reduction.

is also defined by high turbulence intensity and complex flow structures, including

Wake meandering
Several phenomenon can take place in the wake region such as
wake meandering which has a non negligible impact on the wind turbine loads. This
flow phenomenon refers to the lateral and vertical oscillations of the wake behind a wind
turbine as seen on the Figure 1.1 in the farôÙwake region. Wake meandering occurs due
to the interaction between the wake and the surrounding flow conditions [4]. Indeed, the
wake is subjected to the ambient turbulence and wind shear, which can cause it to deviate
from a straight and steady path [5] [6]. The main reasons of wake meandering are:
ãÂ The atmospheric turbulence can impact the wakeãs trajectory. In fact, these turbulent
structures can interact with the wake and induce lateral motions.
ãÂ The wind shear, referring to the change in wind speed and direction with respect to
height, can be responsible for different forces acting on different parts of the wake
in case of variations, and thus, causes it to meander.

ãÂ The ambient wind conditions such as the direction and the speed of the inflow can
affect the wake meandering. For example, a yawed wind turbine (rotated away from
the wind) can create a skewed wake that tends to meander more.
ãÂ Some wake interactions inside a wind farm result in complex wake patterns and
meandering behavior.
Turbulence distribution
Modelling the turbulence has raised many challenges as a
complex and chaotic phenomenon. Traditionally, turbulence in the ABL has been modôÙ
eled as Gaussian, or normally distributed. However, recent researches have shown that
turbulence in atmospheric flows is often nonôÙGaussian, or intermittently distributed, which
means that the turbulence can occur in bursts [7]. Simulations using both Gaussian and
nonôÙGaussian turbulence models has been conducted to investigate the impact on wind
turbine loads according to Jacob Berg. The results demonstrated that the nonôÙGaussian
turbulence model led to higher turbulence intensity and increased loading on the turbine
blades compared to the Gaussian model. Furthermore, this study also found that the
impact of nonôÙGaussian turbulence was more significant for turbines with larger rotor diôÙ
ameters that are more sensitive to changes in turbulence intensity. Thus, nonôÙGaussian
turbulence models provide a more accurate representation of the turbulence in atmoôÙ
spheric flows.
Wake models
There has been a significant amount of research into wake physics in
wind farms over the past few decades. This research has focused on developing modôÙ
els and simulations that can accurately predict the behavior of wakes and their impacts
on downstream turbines. As a result, several approaches to model far wake flows are
available with various level of accuracy.
The most basic models are engineering wake models that provide average flow of a
single wind turbine. The common models are from N. O. Jensen [8], Bastankhah and
PortûˋôÙAgel [9] and Park2 wake model [10]. To perform wake models inside wind farms, a
supperposition model has to be implemented from these engineering wake models [11].
One of the many tools used to implement these engineering wake models in wind farms is
PyWake [12], an openôÙsourced and PythonôÙbased wind farm simulation tool developed at
DTU. These models have all been developed over the past few decades and have been
shown to provide accurate results when compared to experimental data. Having a low
computational cost, these models are efficient for initial assessment of wind farm layouts.
However, the engineering wake models are unable to provide wake dynamics which is
essential for the load predictions.
In order to take into account the dynamic effects of wakes in engineering wake models,
the Dynamic Wake Meandering (DWM) model has been built [5] [13]. It breaks down the
wake into three main components: the velocity deficit, the turbulence within the wake,
and the wake meandering. Each component is modeled separately and the overall flow
is obtained by summing the three different contributions to the atmospheric flow. As a
result, some limitations appear due to the assumptions made by this model:
ãÂ Assuming the independence among the contributions from each subôÙmodels does
not account for the energy cascade from large turbulent structures to smaller eddies
neither the influence of small scales on larger ones.
ãÂ Assuming that the wake meandering is a result of the wake acting as a passive tracer
is not relevant for modelling wake flows in wind farms as the wake is periodically
interrupted by turbines [14].

Furthermore, another approach to model the interaction between the wind flow and
the wind turbine rotor are the actuator disc (AD) and actuator line (AL) methods [15].
The actuator disc method models a wind turbine as a disc that extracts energy from the
incoming wind flow. The disc is represented by a momentum source that imposes a thrust
force on the wind inflow. In contrast, the actuator line method models the individual blades
of a wind turbine as lines with distributed forces that act on the inflow. As a result, the wake
is generated from the AD/AL forces. The main advantage of the actuator line method is
that it provides a more detailed representation of the flow around the blades, which can be
important for accurately predicting blade fatigue loads. Both methods are computationally
efficient and provide a more accurate prediction of wake behavior than engineering wake
models, making it a popular choice for the industry.
Finally, one of the most significant breakthroughs in modelling wake physics has been
the use of computational fluid dynamics (CFD) such as Large Eddy Simulation (LES) to
model flows around wind turbine [16]. Indeed, LES is able to provide highly detailed inforôÙ
mation on the flow structure, in particular for turbulent flows inside wind farms. However,
LES models are computationally expensive and require significant resources, which limits
their practical use for wind farm design and optimization.
Alternative to reduce wake effects
On the ground, new technologies are developed
to mitigate the impact of wake interference on wind farm performances. For instance,
some wind farms use advanced control systems that can adjust the blade pitch and yaw of
individual turbines to minimize wake interference. Other technologies under development
include wake steering, which involves directing the wake away from downstream turbines
and reduce the wake effects on downstream turbines.
Surrogate modelling
To find a compromise between too simple wake flow representaôÙ
tions and detailled representations which are too computationally expensive, some dataôÙ
driven methods, also named surrogate models, are developed [13] [17] [18]. It consists in
a simplified mathematical representation of a complex model to be replaced [19]. As the
goal is to predict the loads on wind turbines knowing inflow characteristics, the complex
model corresponds here to the flow generation plus the aeroôÙelastic simulation. Indeed,
two subôÙmodels are necessary to estimate loads: the flow model and the wind turbine
model. The principles of such models are summed up in a box diagram displayed on the
Figure 1.2. These model are usually built using a large database made up of detailed
CFD simulations to fit the available observations.
One example of load surrogate model has been realised by Nikolay Dimitrov in order to
estimate loads on wind turbines in wind farms using the DWM model as flow model [13].
Building the load surrogate involves generating a set of input/output data using an aeroôÙ
elastic simulation tool and the DWM model to provide inflows to the wind turbine model.
The input parameters include wind speed, wind direction, and the distance between turôÙ
bines. On the other hand, the output data consists of the power production and the loads
on each turbine in a wind farm. Afterwards, the input/output data are then used to train a
surrogate model, represented by the oranges arrows on the Figure 1.2, being a simplified
mathematical representation of the complex flow and turbine models. To this purpose,
two different methods have been used to obtain the functions giving the power and the
loads: the Polynomial Chaos Expansion (PCE) and the Artificial Neural Network (ANN).
The surrogate model is then used to predict the loads on wind turbines for different and
unseen input parameters.

Different flow models based on the DWM theory have been tried to best predict the fatigue
loads on a row of four turbines [20]. The results demonstrated that this surrogate model is
able to predict the fatigue loads with deviations within 25% of the LES predictions. Thus,
it makes this load surrogate computationally efficient and suitable for wind farm design
optimization. However, surrogate modelling tends to reproduce the model used for the
training dataset. Hence, this load surrogate will present the same limitations as the DWM
model. Nevertheless, it concludes that surrogate modeling is a promising approach for
predicting the loads on wind turbines in wind farms and has the potential to significantly
reduce the computational cost of wind farm design optimization.
Another alternative for flow realisations consists in using a decomposed inflow into
modes in order to emphasizes certain features of the flow and find new relationships
between the modes and the fatigue loads. Using a Proper Orthogonal Decomposition
(POD), Sû¡ren Juhl Andersen and Juan Pablo Murcia Leon from DTU developed a PreôÙ
dictive and Stochastic ReducedôÙOrder Model (PSôÙROM) [21]. This has been achieved in
four different steps from a database built on LES flows used to realise aeroôÙservoôÙelastic
computations thanks to EllipSys3D and FLEX5 [22]: POD, projection of the flow in the
database, interpolation of the CrossôÙSpectral Density (CSD) of the modal time series,
decomposition of the CSD and Fourier transformation. Finally, it resulted in a predictive
model of the dynamic flow and the ROM is made up of two different surrogate models:
ãÂ Mean flow surrogate model ùô₤V(ö¡) which predicts the mean flow for a given parameter
ãÂ CSD surrogate model that predicts the change of the modal spectra across the paôÙ
rameter space ùS(f, ö¡). This spectral surrogate can be used to produce realization
of modal time series for a new parameters ùöÎi(t, ö¡).
This ROM owns both a Predictive aspect thanks to the interpolation of the CSD as well
as a Stochastic aspect as random flow realizations can be obtained with correct statistics.
As a result, the first twenty POD modes seems to be sufficient to capture the power proôÙ
duction and the fatigue loads are found with an accuracy comparable to the LES results
for fifty POD modes. Unfortunately, the outputs follow a Gaussian distribution whereas
the distributions given by the LES contain two peaks according to violin plots. Moreover,
the model is very sensitive around the rated wind speed of the wind turbine and requires
sufficient data to solve the flow at low frequencies even though the ROM is faster than
the LES to get load estimations.

Contrary to the surrogate models using various flow models based on the DMW theory
realised by Nikolay Dimitrov, this thesis aims to test several types of surrogates to improve
load predictions using the same flow model based on both LES and ROM inflows. By
making use of the stochastic property of the PSôÙROM, various surrogate models will be
built taking various numbers of inputs. In this way, it will define the requirements for
accurate load predictions as well as the relationship between the flow structure, the POD
modes and the wind turbine loads.
1.2
Problem analysis
With the POD as starting point and having flows realised by LES and the PSôÙROM,
this thesis aims to build two types of surrogate models to estimate the power production
and the damage equivalent loads (DELs) acting on wake operating wind turbines. To
this purpose, a turbine model is defined and the training datasets for the load surrogates
are created using the aeroôÙelastic tool: FLEX5. An integrated quantities based surrogate
model will be built on overall inflow statistics and used as a benchmark for other load
surrogates. The second type of surrogates is a modal based scheme using statistics of
the inflow POD modal timeôÙseries. Comparing the results from these two kinds of load
surrogates will provide some insights on the relationships between the POD modes, the
flow structure and the loads on the wind turbines.
The aim of this research work is to answer the following question:
From the inflow decomposition into modes, can the loads applied by wake dynamics on
the wind turbine be accurately estimated by surrogates trained on modal time series?
To this purpose, three subôÙquestions have been defined to guide the investigations:
1. Can an accurate load surrogate be significantly improved by training on statistics
of the modal time series from proper orthogonal decomposition rather than overall
statistics of the inflow?
2. How many modes from the proper orthogonal decomposition, based on the size of
turbulent structures, are necessary to achieve accurate power production and loads
predictions?
3. How do wake dynamics affect the load surrogates comparing the loads of the first
turbine with wake operating turbines?

2.1.1
FLEX5 methodology
FLEX5 is an aeroôÙelastic software originally developed by ûye [22] and further develôÙ

interacting in a turbulent wind field. Thus, FLEX5 can use turbulence boxes as inputs and
by running in the time domain gives time series of power production, deflections and loads
on the wind turbine as outputs [24]. Aerodynamics are calculated using the Blade Element
Momentum (BEM) method. The structural behavior of the wind turbine is modeled using
the principle of virtual work and degrees of freedom (DOFs). Indeed, modal shape funcôÙ
tions are used for the deflections of the blades and the tower, while stiff bodies connected
by flexible hinges model the nacelle, the rotor shaft and the hub. FLEX5 contains a total
of twentyôÙeight DOFs and models the complete wind turbine using six different coordinate
system as seen on the Figure 2.1.

In the following investigation work, only 13 DOFs will be considered for the blades, the
tower and the nacelle. Indeed, the foundations are considered stiff enough not to move
during the aeroôÙelastic simulations. Furthermore, the time series of the power production
and the loads on the wind turbine will be extracted form the FLEX5 outputs of aeroôÙelastic
simulations given turbulence realisations as inflow.

2.4.1
Process overview
Building a surrogate model relies on five main steps which are independent of the kind
of scheme depending only on the training dataset given. All of them are listed below in
chronological order:
1. Definition of the parameters: The first thing to build a surrogate model is to define
the outputs wanted to be estimated using the inputs available. This will set the
structures of the database and the model afterwards.
2. Creation of a dataset: Knowing the parameters of the models, a database of obôÙ
servations has to be created in order to train the model on it, being in the case of
supervised learning models. The database has to contain a large amount of obserôÙ
vations of both the inputs and outputs of the future models. Finally, the database
will be postôÙprocessed (cleaning ...) in order to simplify the next steps.
3. Development of the surrogate model: Based on the relationships between the
inputs and outputs, a surrogate model per output (power production and loads) will
be trained. Gaussian Processes (GP) will be used along with a squared exponential
kernel being defined by a length scale as the main parameter.
4. CrossôÙvalidation of the model: The training dataset is divided into k folds to comôÙ
pare the performances of k models trained and tested on different parts of the availôÙ
able dataset. This step aims to calibrate the modelãs parameter on the database.
5. Estimation of the modelãs performances: The final step is to train the model with
the best calibrated parameters on the entire training dataset giving the final model.
This model is then tested by comparing its prediction to the observations to assess
its performances.

A common practice when training surrogate models is to scale the training dataset for

2.4.5
Gaussian process
Having a normalized database, the surrogate model will now be built using systems that
can adapt to their environments and learn from their experience. Indeed, some superôÙ
vised learning models will be applied to the database containing both inputs and outputs
training the model to best predict unseen data afterward. Moving from a discrete training
dataset to a continuous function, an inductive process will be applied to build the right
function fitting the given dataset: it is a regression process. Instead of assuming directly
specific functional form for the relationship between inputs and outputs, the process will
Load surrogates based on reduced order modelling of wake dynamics
21
==PAGE:33==
consider all the possible functions that are consistent with the observed data. Therefore,
an assumption has to be made about the characteristics of the underlying function in orôÙ
der to reduce the possibilities. The stake is to avoid restricting the function class which
could lead to poor predictions as well as increasing too much the flexibility of the functionsã
classes which could result in overfitting (fitting well the training dataset and badly unseen
data). Thus, a Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) giving a prior probability to every
possible function will help finding a compromise.
A Gaussian process is a probabilistic model that is commonly used for building surroôÙ
gate models in various applications, including optimization, regression, and uncertainty
analysis [19] [37]. It is a powerful tool for modeling complex relationships between input
variables and output values, especially when the data is limited or noisy. As said previôÙ
ously, the basic idea behind a GP is to define a prior distribution over functions, where
each function represents a possible mapping between input and output variables. FurôÙ
thermore, higher probabilities are given to functions that are considered to be more likely.
For example, smoothest functions will have a higher probability than other functions. The
problem of dealing with an infinite set of possible functions in a finite time can be solved
by using GP, which is a generalization of Gaussian probability distributions. Indeed, it
assumes that the distribution over functions is Gaussian meaning every point in the input
space is associated with a random variable that follows a Gaussian distribution, as shown
on the Figure 2.15 for a two dimensional regression. Thus, GP enables to effectively work
with functions by considering only a finite number of points and still obtaining consistent
results, making it both computationally tractable and conceptually powerful.
Figure 2.15: Gaussian Process Regression sketch
Going more into mathematical concerning, a Gaussian process assumes for each data
point a normal distribution in order to fit the training dataset and predict on the testing
dataset. Thus, a GP is fully characterized by its mean function and its covariance function:
ãÂ The mean function represents the average behavior of the underlying function.
However, it is commonly assumed a zeroôÙmean translating that the average value
over the sample functions at each data point is zero. Although the specific random
functions do not have a mean of zero, the mean of the predicted values for any fixed
inputs would become zero, independent of the inputs as more functions are being
drawn. This is the assumption made by the Python library scikit-learn used to
compute the surrogate models. Thus, it will not provide explicitly the mean function.

Furthermore, the variability of the sample functions for any inputs can be defined
by computing the pointwise variance. Hence, a GP specifies that the prior variance
does not depend on the data point.
ãÂ The covariance function, also called kernel, represents the assumptions about the
mean function. A kernel function defines the similarity between different function
values at different input locations. Indeed, the output value is higher if the two data
points given as inputs are similar. Commonly used covariance functions include the
Radial basis Function (RBF) defined by a squared exponential,the Matûˋrn function
(a generalization of the RBF), and the exponential function among a wide choice of
functions.
The RBF kernel will be used for all the surrogate models built for the sake of consisôÙ

consequence, GPs using this kernel have mean square derivatives of all orders giving a

2.4.6
CrossôÙvalidation of the model
So far, the dataset has been defined and an appropriate covariance function has been
selected to fit as well as possible the training dataset using a GPR. Now, the parameters
of the kernel function have to be calibrated on the training dataset by performing a crossôÙ
validation, as seen on the Figure 2.14. Only the length scale l has to be calibrated for the
GP using a RBF kernel according to the Equation 2.17.
It is assumed that the data points are Independent and Identically Distributed (IID). This
means that all samples stem from the same generative process and that the generative
process is assumed to have no memory of past generated samples which is the case
for aeroôÙelastic simulations performed on various inflow chunks. As a consequence, the
KôÙfold method can be applied dividing all the training dataset into k groups of data points
as shown on the Figure 2.16.
For the k folds, one different fold is removed each time to train the model on k ôÙ 1 folds.
Then, the model is tested on the fold set apart constituting unseen observations. As a
result, the parameters of the model can be calibrated by computing the error between the
prediction against the observation for different folds. It assumes that the best parameter
corresponds to the one giving the lowest error. As a result, k models are built giving a
large range of performance estimatorãs values. Thus, the crossôÙvalidation method proôÙ
vides more robust estimations of performance by mitigating the impact of data variability

As a consequence of the grouping performed on the database to compute the load

ãÂ Grouping the database
A wellôÙknown technique used to reduce the complexity of the data and facilitate the modôÙ
eling process is to bin the database, especially when dealing with large datasets or when
the relationships between variables are not easily captured by a simple model. Binning
involves dividing the continuous input variables into discrete intervals, also named bins.
Thus, the advantages of binning the data are:
ãÂ Reducing complexity by reducing the number of unique values in a variable. Thus,
it simplifies the relationships between variables by replacing continuous values with
discrete categories.
ãÂ Handling noise by smoothing out noisy data as a result from grouping similar values
together.
ãÂ Handling nonôÙlinear relationships by capturing nonôÙlinear relationships between variôÙ
ables. Indeed, grouping values into bins results often in capturing patterns and
trends that may not be apparent in the raw data.
ãÂ Dealing with sparse data presenting too low data points in certain regions. In fact,
binning the data can ensure that each bin has a sufficient number of data points for
modeling.
For all these different reasons, it has to be wondered if the database available needs
to be binned on the input variables of the model to create a simplified representation
of the input space. Nevertheless, the main goal of this investigation work is to improve
the performance of load surrogates by adding inputs (standard deviation of modal timeôÙ
series) to the models. Thus, it does not seem feasible to bin the database with respect to
the inputs in the cases where the model has more than three inputs.
Nevertheless, the database needs to be grouped based on a criteria in order to compute
the percentiles in each of the groups of data points as the surrogates will be build for
different percentiles of loads. Hence, the data points have been grouped depending on the
flow simulation chunks. In fact, it is the most consistent way to group the data points. As a
result, the initial database of 85,680 data points has been reduced to 2040 groups of data
points. For example, the Figure 3.10 shows the comparison between the original database
and the grouped database for the median of the DELs of the towerôÙbottom foreôÙaft bending
moment MTb,y,eq scaled according to the inputs used for the integrated quantities based
surrogates.
According to the Figure 3.10, an important loss of data points occurs when grouping
the database for the percentile computation which also enable to simplify the database
to train the models. After grouping the database with respect to the simulation chunks,
the median and the 25th and 75th percentiles are computed for all the outputs of the
surrogate models in each group. As a consequence, the groups containing less than five
data points are removed because the percentile computation is not accurate enough with
few data points. Fortunately, any of the groups were made up of less than five data points
and 100% of the data points are used to train the models. Thus, the surrogate models
were trained using 2,040 data points for the integrated quantities based scheme (LES and
ROM inflows) and 2,000 data points for the modal based scheme (only ROM inflows).

4.4
Sensitivity analysis on the number of folds
All the results shown in the previous sections have been obtained from a crossôÙvalidation
using twenty folds with the scikit-learn python library. As a consequence, the results
are comparable and the models obtained provide a good approximation of the perforôÙ
mances of the final surrogates. However, the expected error given by the RMSE or the
predicting time might change with the number of folds used to perform the crossôÙvalidation.
That is the reason why a sensitivity analysis on this parameter has been realised with usual
number of folds for crossôÙvalidating data driven models.
Generally speaking, the number of folds used in the crossôÙvalidation has to be wisely
chosen as it can impact significantly the performances of the surrogates even more when
models are trained on small datasets. In fact, if the dataset is divided into too few folds,
the model risks not to be trained on enough scenarios to give accurate predictions. On
the contrary, if the dataset is divided into too many folds, the performances of the models
are assessed of few predictions which is a risk if these predictions are made on outliers.
As a result, the modal based surrogate models have been calibrated and trained using
four different number k of folds during the crossôÙvalidation: k ã[10, 20, 30, 40] folds. For
each scenario, the same performances of the models have been reported.
The first parameter to check is the variation of the errors in the model predictions inôÙ
duced by the number of folds. Thus, the RMSE representing the expected error of the
model have been displayed on the Figure 4.9a for the 50th percentile of the DEL of TBF
bending moment surrogates based on model time series. As a result, the RMSE curves
follow the same converging trend with the number of modes very closely to each otherãs.
The RMSE given by the crossôÙvalidation using ten folds seem to be slightly higher when
the modal based surrogates use few POD modes. Nevertheless, the impact of the numôÙ
ber of folds on the errors made by the surrogates is not significant. Thus, the results
related to modelsã errors presented previously represent well the performances of the
load surrogates.
The second parameter under study is the predicting time of the surrogates taken out
of the crossôÙvalidation as no testing dataset has been extracted at the beginning due to

5
Discussion
This master thesis aims to elaborate on the concept of surrogate models to estimate
load distributions from inflow statistics. To this purpose, different research questions have
been defined and some boundaries have been set in order to realise the experiment able
to answer them. Indeed, the results presented in the previously have been reached thanks
to some assumptions. As the results have already been analysed, the assumptions and
choices made to reach them will be now discussed. The purpose is to challenge their
limits and investigate to what extent the models can be modified for improvements.
To accomplish the tasks targeted by this thesis, two different kinds of surrogates differing
by their inputs have been built. They use a single wind turbine model and have been
trained on a database made from inflows of ten inôÙline wind turbines with a particular
method of machine learning, called a Gaussian process including a squaredôÙexponential
kernel to predict three different percentiles of both the power production and the damage
equivalent loads.
First of all, the database on which have been trained the load surrogates is dependent
on the wind turbine model used to run the aeroôÙelastic simulations on flow realisations
available. In fact, the mean power production and the DELs predicted are corresponding
to the expected values for the NM80 turbine experiencing the inflow defined by the inputs
provided. Thus, the surrogate models are particular for the turbine model, the load sensor
and its percentile. In the end, the surrogate models are efficient, but very specific to the
output wanted.
All the different steps in the construction process of the load surrogates can be disôÙ
cussed to find some alternatives that could improve the surrogate performances. For
instance, another kernel like the Matûˋrn kernel, which is a generalization of the RBF kerôÙ
nel adding a parameter controlling the smoothness, can be tried or even another machine
learning method such as the artificial neural networks (ANN) can be implemented to comôÙ
pare the performances. However, the most easiest way to improve the surrogates would
be to extend the training dataset. Indeed, it would increase the diversity and quantity of
training samples helping to improve the generalization capability of the surrogate models.
Concerning the performance assessment of the load surrogates, the performance estiôÙ
mators have been computed over the predictions realised by the models during the crossôÙ
validation, after having defined the best length scale. Indeed, any testing dataset has
been put apart due to the too low amount of data reduced by the grouping step for the
percentiles computation. As a result, it does not correspond to the predictions made by
the final surrogate even if these estimations are very close to the final ones. Hence, augôÙ
menting the dataset would enable to save a testing dataset with unseen data to assess
the final performances.
It has to be kept in mind that the final goal of these load surrogates is to be used by the
wind industry in order to design wind turbines in the best way possible. To this purpose,
these models must be efficient by providing quickly accurate predictions. As a result,
the industry might prefer using the integrated quantities based surrogates for the sake
of simplicity. In this case, it has been seen that it provides already good prediction with
low expected error. Unfortunately, the overall statistics as inputs are not enough for the
load estimations on the front turbine. By adding the turbine number in the wind farm

6
Conclusion
This thesis aimed to train surrogate models for predicting the mean power production
and several damage equivalent loads applied on the wind turbine knowing some inflow
statistics. To this purpose, two different kind of surrogates have been built: the integrated
quantities based surrogates using overall inflow statistics and the modal based surrogates
using the inflow POD mode statistics.
By building these surrogate models, it has been demonstrated that both power producôÙ
tion and loads can be accurately and quickly estimated by data driven models. FurtherôÙ
more, the load predictions are more accurate based on POD mode statistics that provide
more information on the flow structures. It results in an expected error lower than 1.5%
of the maximum observed value and a low deviation. Only the first five POD modal time
series are usually enough as further modes correspond to too small turbulent flow strucôÙ
tures to affect the loads. On the contrary, the power production is not affected by the flow
decomposition and is already well estimated by overall statistics of the inflow. In addition,
a difference is made between turbines operating in atmospheric conditions and turbines
operating in wakes. Indeed, loads on front turbines require the modal based surrogates to
be accurately predicted. Analysing these results helps answering the research questions.
Can an accurate load surrogate be significantly improved by training on POD
statistics of the modal time series rather than overall statistics of the inflow? The
modal based surrogate models provide load estimations with an expected error reduced
between 10% and 45% compared to the integrated quantities based surrogates. However,
this improvement is not as significant for the power production with a slight reduction of
the expected error on the quartiles and no improvement for its median.
How many POD modes, based on the size of turbulent structures, are necessary
to achieve accurate power production and loads predictions? Knowing that large
flow scales correspond to the first POD modes and further POD modes represent small
turbulent scales, the number of POD modal timeôÙseries needed to accomplish both accuôÙ
rate and quick load estimations is between three and five depending on the load sensor
and its percentile, except for the 25th quartile of the DEL of TBS that requires six POD
modes. However, the DEL of BRE surrogates are not significantly affected by the number
of POD modes. On the other hand, the 25th and 75th percentiles of the power producôÙ
tions are well predicted with eight and four POD modes statistics respectively and the
more accurate predictions of its median are provided by the integrated quantities based
scheme, thus the overall statistics are sufficient.
How do wake dynamics affect the load surrogates comparing the loads of the first
turbine with wake operating turbines? Comparing the loads and power production
estimations for the ten inôÙline turbines, it has been seen that the integrated quantities
surrogates underestimate the loads on the front wind turbine contrary to the modal based
schemes using at least three POD mode statistics. Thus, the flow decomposition enables
to predict also the load on the front turbine whereas the majority of the training database
is made up of wake operating turbines. However, the DEL of BRE bending moment is well
predicted for all the turbines by the several surrogates as well as the power production.
To conclude, this thesis demonstrated the usefulness of surrogate models based on
modal timesôÙseries statistics of the the inflow to predict accurately and quickly the load
distributions as well as the power production.
