Abstract 
Dark chocolate is one of the most popular chocolate products in Denmark. The 
contamination of cadmium (Cd) and nickel (Ni) in dark chocolate may pose a potential 
threat to public health. The study aims to evaluate the risks of Cd and Ni exposed in the 
Danish population due to the consumption of dark chocolate. The estimated 
consumption rate of dark chocolate among the whole population aged 4-75 ranged from 
2.82 to 3.76 g/day, while 5.31-6.84 g/day for consumers. Estimated chronic exposures 
to Cd and Ni from dark chocolate consumption were 0.0069-0.0093 μg/kg b.w./day and 
0.19-0.22 μg/kg b.w./day, which contributed approximately 3-5% and 5-8% of the total 
dietary exposure of Cd and Ni respectively. EFSA derived a tolerable weekly intake of 
2.5 μg/kg b.w./week (corresponding to 0.36 μg/kg b.w./day) for Cd and a tolerable daily 
intake of 2.8 μg/kg b.w./day for Ni. Overall, there is a low concern of health risks from 
Cd and Ni chronic exposure regarding the current consumption of dark chocolate. The 
margin of exposure (MOE) approach was applied to evaluate the acute risk of Ni 
exposure. The estimated MOE values were far below 10 across all age groups, implying 
that Ni-sensitized individuals may develop eczematous flare-up skin reactions for dark 
chocolate consumption.

Chapter 1 Cadmium 
1. Introduction  
1.1. General introduction 
The heavy metal cadmium (Cd) is a naturally existing element with high toxicity [1]. 
Cd is rarely presented in its elementary form, but rather appears in the oxidation state 
as Cd2+ in the natural environment. Therefore, Cd is frequently found in other metals 
ores, like zinc, lead, iron, and copper [2]. Apart from natural sources (e.g. earth’s crust), 
Cd emitted from anthropogenic activities (e.g. nonferrous metal mining) has 
continuously increased in the environment over the past century [3]. As far as we know, 
Cd is not recognized as an essential element for plants, animals and human life [4]. On 
the contrary, excessive Cd exposure has caused adverse health effects like kidney 
damage, bone disease, and cancers [5-7]. Cd is very persistent and easily accumulates 
in plants and aquatic animals, which means a long-term management approach is 
necessarily required to reduce potential human exposure [8]. 
 
1.2. Route of exposure  
Food is the primary source of Cd uptake for nonsmoking general population in the 
absence of occupational exposure [9-11]. Cd concentration in food crops is associated 
with uptake from soils [12]. Direct application of phosphate fertilizers (around 300 mg 
Cd per kg dry products) is one of the major sources of cadmium contamination in the 
soil. Not the total amount of Cd in the soil would be assimilated by plants, the 
bioavailability relies on the known physical and chemical properties like redox 
potential, organic matter content, pH, and cation exchange capacity [13, 14]. The long-
term application will increase Cd uptake by agricultural crops through roots, which will 
eventually enter the food chain [15]. 
 
For cigarette smokers, inhalation is the main route of exposure. Tobacco plant 
(Nicotiana tabacum) can concentrate Cd in leaves regardless of soil Cd content. Cd 
concentration varies (0.5–1.5 μg/cigarette) dependent on different brand types and 
geographical origins. Cadmium oxide is formed during smoking, then is inhaled and 
accumulated in our lungs and blood circulation systems. [16-18]. Meanwhile, Cd is 
used in many industries, which means that workers are at higher risk of Cd exposure 
compared with the general population. They are exposed to Cd during the 
manufacturing process involving metal smelting, batteries production, electroplating, 
==PAGE:7==
 
6
and welding [7, 19].  
 
1.3. Cd in chocolate products 
Chocolates are very popular in the European market and half of the world cocoa 
products are consumed by European consumers. The potential health benefits of 
consuming chocolates are mostly associated with the antioxidant effects of the high 
phenolic content (procyanidins, catechin, and epicatechin) [20-22]. Nevertheless, 
chocolates can be contaminated by Cd and pose health threats. Cd contamination level 
in cocoa beans is decided by soil properties [23], geographic origin [24], and cocoa 
cultivar varieties [25, 26]. During chocolate production, the manufacturing process 
could either concentrate or dilute Cd content influenced by processing methods, raw 
materials, and products variates [27]. It is pointed out that winnowing and conching are 
two steps that can significantly decrease Cd contamination [28]. In the end, dark 
chocolates have higher Cd content than milk and white chocolates because of the higher 
cocoa proportion [29].   
 
The new Commission Regulation (EU) No 488/2014 about the maximum Cd levels in 
cocoa and chocolate products was adapted from 1 January 2019 after 4 years of 
preparation time for companies. The highest Cd tolerance level is 0.1 mg/kg in milk 
chocolate with ≤ 30% total dry solids, while 0.3 mg Cd/kg in milk chocolate with ≥ 30% 
total dry solids or chocolate with ≤ 50% total dry solids and 0.8 mg Cd/kg in chocolate 
with ≥ 50% total dry solids. Higher cocoa content results in higher Cd maximum levels 
[30].  
 
2. Hazard identification 
2.1. Basic structure and function of kidney 
The kidneys are paired bean-shaped organs that are located on the posterior abdominal 
wall behind the peritoneum. Two kidneys are positioned on either side of the vertebral 
column in the retroperitoneal space. The left kidney lies slightly higher than the right 
one because of the vicinity of the liver on the right side [31].  
 
The kidneys are very important for maintaining homeostasis in the human body. First 
of all, harmful waste products are filtered and removed from the blood, then excreted 
in the urine. The balance of body fluid composition (water and electrolytes) and volume 
is also regulated by our kidneys to maintain normal cellular function. Kidney diseases 
==PAGE:8==
 
7
can be caused by excessive fluids building up in the body. Moreover, blood pressure is 
in the control of the renin-angiotensin system (RAS). Renin is released in the 
juxtaglomerular apparatus of the kidneys. Angiotensinogen (AGT) is cleaved by renin 
to form angiotensin I (Ang I), and then is transformed into angiotensin II (Ang II) by 
angiotensin converting enzyme (ACE). The secretion of Ang II hormone results in 
elevated blood pressure by stimulating blood vessel constriction and water and sodium 
retention (see in Figure 1). Besides, increased sodium level in the blood is also induced 
by the release of aldosterone in the adrenal glands under Ang II stimulation. Finally, a 
variety of hormones are produced from the kidneys, such as erythropoietin. 
Erythropoietin is responsible for the production of mature red blood cells in the bone 
marrow [32].  
 
Two main regions can be observed when kidneys are cut open in half: the outer region 
cortex and the inner region medulla (see in Figure 2). They consist of nephrons, which 
are the basic functional units of the kidneys. The nephron is mainly composed of a renal 
corpuscle connected to the renal tubule. The renal corpuscle refers to the glomerulus 
surrounding by the Bowman's space, while the renal tubule is known as the constitution 
of proximal convoluted tubule, loop of Henle, and distal convoluted tubule [33]. 
 
Blood plasma filtration is the main function of the glomerulus and also the first step of 
concentrating waste products in urine. Water and small solutes (e.g. glucose, amino 
acid) are able to pass through, while albumin and red blood cells in blood plasma are 
blocked by the glomerular capillary wall. There are three layers in the glomerular 
capillary wall, as the endothelium, the basement membrane, and podocytes [34]. The 
glomerular filtration rate (GFR) is commonly recognized as the best indicator of kidney 
function, especially very important for the diagnosis of chronic kidney disease [35]. 
GFR is defined as the volume of plasma that is filtered through the glomeruli in the 
kidney per unit time for urine excretion. It is measured based on the renal clearance rate 
of certain substances, such as inulin and endogenous creatinine [36].    
 
The proximal tubule is responsible for reabsorbing more than 60% of the ultrafiltrate, 
including sodium and water. Na+ in the tubular fluid is driven to moves down the 
chemical concentration gradient into the cell. Then, Na+-K+-ATPase pump located in 
the basolateral membrane senses the increasing level of Na+ and pumps Na+ out of the 
cell to maintain intracellular sodium concentration. Na+ that is pumped into the 
interstitial spaces eventually diffuses into the peritubular capillary [37]. Water is 
reabsorbed into the peritubular capillaries through the transcellular route and 
paracellular route. Water can diffuse across the proximal tubule cells through aquaporin 
in the apical and basolateral membrane (transcellular pathway). Meanwhile, water also 
==PAGE:9==
 
8
flows into the lateral intracellular space through the tight junctions (paracellular 
pathway). Water movement is driven by the increased oncotic pressure of the 
peritubular capillaries and the reabsorption of Na+ along with other solutes from the 
tubular fluid [38]. 
 
2.2. Toxicokinetics  
2.2.1. Absorption 
Dietary intake is the main route of long-term Cd exposure, but only a small amount of 
ingested Cd is absorbed. The gastrointestinal absorption of Cd is considerably much 
lower compared with respiratory absorption (10%-50%), varying from 1% to 10%. In 
general, average gastrointestinal absorption of Cd is 5% for female and 10% for male 
in western countries [39]. The absorption rate is also affected by the body store of 
essential nutrients. Especially, iron deficiency results in the increased absorption of Cd 
[40, 41]. 
 
2.2.2. Distribution and metabolism 
Cd enters the systemic circulation and binds to albumin (Alb) and metallothionein (MT), 
after being absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract. Cd is primarily delivered to the 
liver during the initial exposure, while the kidney is the major body burden target for 
the long-term exposure. The cadmium-albumin (Cd-Alb) complexes are taken up by 
hepatocytes through endocytosis and then split by lysosomal degradation. The 
increased intracellular accumulation of Cd induces the synthesis of MT. Even though 
the formation of cadmium-metallothionein (Cd-MT) complexes protects liver cells 
from Cd hepatotoxicity effects, the excessive amount of Cd can induce oxidative stress 
and produces lipid peroxidation damage in the plasma membrane. Eventually, Cd-MT 
is released back into the circulation from injured hepatocytes. Besides, Cd also binds 
to glutathione (GSH) for protection oxidative stress and then is excreted into the bile 
[42].  
 
Cd-MT complexes released from hepatocytes following the bloodstream are filtered 
through the renal glomerulus. Then, the complexes are reabsorbed by the epithelial cells 
of the proximal tubule through endocytosis. The lysosomes release Cd2+ from Cd-MT 
complexes in proximal tubular cells, where a limited amount of renal MT is synthesized 
to protect against renal dysfunction. Cd concentration in the kidney exceeding 50 mg 
kg−1 would pose a significant threat of renal tubular cell injuries [43].

9
 
2.2.3. Excretion 
Cd is generally retained in the kidney with an extremely long biological half-life of 10-
30 years [44]. It is mainly eliminated from our body through urine and feces. Only a 
very small amount of Cd, around 0.005% to 0.01% of the total body burden, is excreted 
via urine on a daily basis [45]. The excretion rate is dependent on age and associated 
with exposure level. Tubular dysfunction and damage also result in the increased Cd 
excretion rate [43]. Fecal Cd content can be used as a measurement of dietary Cd intake. 
It is well known that a large amount of Cd from oral exposure is unabsorbed and about 
90% of ingested Cd is excreted in the feces [46]. 
 
2.3. Toxicological effects 
The kidney is a primary and susceptible organ affected by chronic Cd toxicity, while 
the damage is usually associated with Cd accumulation in proximal tubules [44]. Renal 
tubular dysfunction has been observed among exposed subjects [47-50]. The early 
indicator of tubular abnormalities includes the increased excretion of low molecular 
weight proteins (LMWP) in urine, such as β2-microglobulin (β2M), α1-microglobulin 
(A1M), and retinol-binding protein (RBP) [51]. Impaired reabsorption of glucose, 
amino acids, bicarbonate, and phosphate also occurs during the development of tubular 
injuries, which are the characteristics of Fanconi syndrome [52]. The renal damage 
induced by Cd can be progressive and irreversible [53, 54]. Chronic kidney disease 
(CKD) has always been a major global health concern with around 11% to 13% 
prevalence [55]. Some epidemiological studies have indicated that Cd exposure is a risk 
factor of CKD development [56] [57], especially in patients with hypertension or 
diabetes [58]. A global burden study estimated that dietary Cd exposure contributed 
0.2% of total DALYs in regard to CKD [59]. 
 
Besides the adverse effects on the kidney, many studies have suggested a connection 
between low-level Cd exposure and bone damage [60-65]. In the OSCAR 
(Osteoporosis-Cadmium as a risk factor) study, low-level Cd exposure was associated 
with the increased risk of forearm fractures in participants who were 50 years of age or 
older [61]. In a Swedish population-based prospective cohort, dietary Cd exposure was 
associated with low bone mineral density. A higher risk of osteoporosis and fractures 
was also observed at minor exposure to Cd from diet among 2,676 postmenopausal 
women [62]. Benchmark dose (BMD) of urinary Cd (U-Cd) for osteoporosis was 
significantly lower in women compared with men [65]. 
==PAGE:11==
 
10
 
It is well known that the itai-itai disease is a severe form of chronic Cd poisoning, which 
was first discovered in the Jinzu River basin region of Japan. The patients exhibited 
clinical symptoms of renal tubular dysfunction, osteomalacia and osteoporosis [66]. 
The occurrence of itai-itai disease was caused by consuming rice with high Cd 
concentration [67]. It was certain that the source of Cd pollution was from the Kamioka 
mine, which was located at the upstream of Jinzu River. The contaminated river water 
was used for the irrigation of paddy soils and direct consumption [68].  
 
2.4. Biomarkers of exposure 
Blood Cd (B-Cd) has been identified as the indicator of recent exposure [69, 70]. The 
median blood Cd level for 105 non-smokers in southern Sweden was 1.8 nmol/L, 
ranging from 0.38 to 18 nmol/L. 1.4 times higher B-Cd level was found in women than 
men, which could be explained by the low iron storage in women [16]. In another 
Swedish study, the median value was reported as 0.19 μg/L and the B-Cd concentration 
was negatively associated with meat consumption [71].  
 
The U-Cd level is generally used to estimate long-term exposure and total body burden 
[72]. It is usually adjusted with creatinine concentrations. Creatinine is a waste product 
produced from muscle metabolism, then filtered through glomerulus and excreted in 
urine. Intra- and inter-individual variability in the urine flow rate was discovered, which 
leading to the substantial variations in the concentration of biomarkers. The increasing 
amount of water consumption would lead to decreasing concentration of Cd and 
creatinine in urine. The creatinine is excreted at a relatively constant rate compared to 
the urine (water) flow rate. Therefore, the U-Cd level is expressed as micrograms of 
analyte per gram of creatinine [73]. Cd concentration in urine is proportional to its 
accumulation in the kidney [6]. The high degree of temporal stability suggested that U-
Cd is a good biomarker reflecting cumulative and long-term cadmium exposure [74]. 
High U-Cd concentration was associated with increased all-cause mortality [75]. On 
the other hand, there was a relatively low risk of renal dysfunction development when 
the U-Cd excretion was below 2 μg/g creatinine [49]. Smoking status and age are 
important factors for U-Cd concentration. People who never smoke (0.30 μg/g 
creatinine) has significant lower concentrations compared with current (0.59 μg/g 
creatinine) or former smokers (0.40 μg/g creatinine) [76]. It was reported that U-Cd 
firstly decreased until the beginning of adulthood, then continuously increased and 
finally decreased around 60 to 70 years old [77]. In a Danish postmenopausal women 
cohort, the mean U-Cd value was 0.70 ± 0.62 μg/g creatinine. A significant positive 
==PAGE:12==
 
11
association was found between dietary Cd intake and urinary Cd level among non-
smokers [78].  
 
2.5. Biomarkers of renal effects 
Tubular damage can be determined by the urinary excretion of LMWP (i.e. β2M, RBP, 
A1M) and N-acetyl-β-d-glucosaminidase (NAG) activity. β2M has been recognized as 
the most useful biomarker regarding renal effects by the European Food Safety 
Authority (EFSA) panel [12]. The proximal tubules normally reabsorb the most of β2M 
filtered through the glomerulus. However, the impaired tubular function would result 
in the elevated urinary excretion of β2M [79]. From the clinical perspective, three 
reference values were established for β2M, as 300, 1,000 and 10,000 μg/g creatinine. 
The excretion of β2M within 300 μg/g creatinine is considered as a normal condition. 
As the β2M excretion levels increase to 1,000 μg/g creatinine, the tubular dysfunction 
is irreversible. Over 10,000 μg/g creatinine of β2M excretion in the urine is associated 
with the Cd nephropathy [51]. Moreover, several studies have established the dose-
response relationship between U-Cd concentration and β2M level [80-83]. The only 
problem would be that β2M is not stable in acidic urine on the condition of pH less than 
7. To avoid the rapid degradation of β2M, the pH control of samples is required [84]. 
On the other hand, RBP is equally sensitive as β2M but more stable [85]. A1M has 
been recognized as a less sensitive or specific biomarker for tubular function [12]. NAG 
is a lysosomal enzyme present in the proximal tubule. The high molecular weight makes 
it difficult to filter through the glomerulus, however, NAG is secreted into the urine 
when proximal tubular epithelial cells are injured [86]. 
 
2.6. Health-based guidance value 
The health-based guidance value (HBGV) has been derived from the threshold dose of 
renal effects in Cd. The provisional tolerable weekly intake (PTWI) of 7 μg/kg b.w. 
was established by the Joint FAO/WHO Expert Committee on Food Additives (JECFA) 
at the thirty-third meeting in 1988. Due to the long half-life of Cd, the weekly-based 
HBGV was changed into a monthly value. The original PTWI was withdrawn and 
JECFA established a new provisional tolerable monthly intake (PTMI) of 25 μg/kg b.w. 
in 2010, which corresponding to 5.8 μg/kg b.w. expressed as weekly intake [87]. On 
the contrary, a different HBGV was proposed by EFSA in 2009. The tolerable weekly 
intake (TWI) value of 2.5 μg/kg b.w. was recommended from the risk assessment of 
Cd. It was believed that TWI could provide effective protection for all consumers [12].

3. Dose-response assessment 
The HBGVs from JECFA and EFSA were established on the basis of dose-response 
assessment. The first part of the dose-response assessment focused on toxicodynamic 
modelling, which illustrated the relationship between biomarkers reflecting renal 
effects and biomarkers reflecting Cd concentration in kidneys. The second part was a 
toxicokinetic model that linked renal Cd accumulation to dietary exposure to Cd. The 
graphical presentation of dose-response assessment is in Figure 3. 
 
3.1. Meta-analysis 
A meta-analysis is a statistical method that combines and then analyze the results from 
different studies [88]. The systematic review of a total of 35 epidemiological studies 
was performed by EFSA to link U-Cd and β2M levels. The database consisted of 165 
pairs of summary data that cover 30,000 participants, while individual data was not 
available. Several important covariates were also mentioned, including gender, age, 
ethnicity, working status, and co-exposure. The basic characteristics of the main 
covariates are presented in Table 1. It was noticeable that the majority of participants 
were non-occupationally (99%) exposed women (75%), who were Asian (93.5%). 
These factors were considered to be important in determining the dose-response 
relationship. Particularly, the ANCOVA analysis indicated that there were significant 
effects of age and ethnicity on β2M level and its interaction with U-Cd [12]. Only one 
alteration was made in the dataset by JECFA. The reported mean values from Hotz et 
al. (1999) study should be regarded as geometric means, and there was no need for 
transformation [87]. 
 
3.2. BMD approach 
The hybrid BMD approach was applied to the summary data gathered from meta-
analysis within the Bayesian framework by EFSA [12]. Several studies also calculated 
BMD or its lower bound of the left-sided 95% confidence interval (BMDL) by using 
the hybrid method [89-92]. The hybrid approach is described as a probabilistic model 
that fits continuous data to estimate the percentile of population distribution falling 
above or below a cut-off point at a certain dose [93] [94]. In this case, it was assumed 
that the β2M level followed a log-normal distribution at a given U-Cd concentration. A 
benchmark response (BMR) of 5% (and 10%) extra risk compared to the background 
response was selected for the BMD determination. [95]. There are two different types 
of risk estimates, as extra risk and additional risk. Additional risk corresponds to an

risk (X%) is defined as the absolute change in risk (meaning additional risk) divided by 
the proportion of the non-affected population in the control population. The equation is

Three cut-off values were used for BMD and BMDL calculation, consisting of two 
pathological based values (300 and 1000 μg β2M/g creatinine) and one statistical based

targeted for the BMD analysis: (a) Total population without any covariates adjustment; 
(b) total population with ethnicity (Caucasian) and gender adjustment; (c) focus 
population consisting of subgroups that are non-workers with average age above 50 
years, adjusting for ethnicity [95].

questionnaire (FFQ) was designed to calculate dietary intake, while the information 
about Cd contamination level in food items was gathered from the Swedish National 
Food Administration. First-void urine samples were collected and tested. The cohort 
data were fitted into the one-compartment model. The mean estimate of Cd half-life 
was 11.6 years ranging from 5 to 35 years. In Figure 4, corresponding to U-Cd 
concentration of 0.5, 1, 2, and 3 μg/g creatinine, the maximum amount of Cd dietary 
intake after 50 years of life-time exposure at the cumulative population proportion (i.e. 
95th percentile) was established [99]. 
 
The RP of 1 μg Cd /g creatinine in urine reported by EFSA was chosen to derive the 
HBGV based on Amzal study. Therefore, the maximum daily intake of 0.36 Cd/kg b.w. 
in respect of 50 years exposure was established to guarantee the Cd excretion in urine 
lower than 1 μg/g creatinine for the protection of 95% of the population, corresponding 
to TWI of 2.52 μg Cd/kg b.w. [12]. On the other hand, some statistical modifications 
were made by JECFA. The modelling was performed through a two-dimensional Monte 
Carlo simulation. A statistical parameter concerning variation in Cd half-life was 
involved in accounting for the individual variability. The cumulative distribution of 
dietary Cd exposure is presented in Figure 5 with 95% confidence intervals calculated 
==PAGE:17==
 
16
from parameter estimates (i.e. fk × fu). As a result, the dietary intake of 1.2 μg Cd/kg 
b.w. (95% CI 0.8-1.8) per day was estimated corresponding to the breakpoint of 5.24 
μg/g creatinine (at the 5th population percentile). The 5th percentile (lower bound) of 
0.8 μg Cd/kg b.w. per day, as equal to 25 μg Cd/kg b.w. per month (PTMI), was chosen 
for the protection of those susceptible individuals [87]. 
 
3.5. The similarities and differences between EFSA and JECFA 
There were some similarities and differences in both parts of the dose-response 
assessment from EFSA and JECFA (in Table 3). For the toxicodynamics part, the 
systematic review (35 studies) of Cd and β2M concentration conducted by EFSA was 
used by JECFA with one alteration in the dataset. As far as I'm concerned, a single 
change in a paired of data wouldn’t result in much difference. However, different 
approaches were taken for the identification of RP. The hybrid BMD approach was 
applied by EFSA combining with the Hill model to estimate BMDL5. By contrast, the 
breakpoint between two slopes from the biexponential model was selected by JECFA 
as the RP. For the toxicokinetics part, the same one-compartment model from Amzal 
study that connected the U-Cd level to dietary Cd intake was used by both EFSA and 
JECFA. Nevertheless, a statistical parameter was added for log-normally distributed 
half-life of Cd by JECFA. Confidence intervals associated with the population 
distribution of dietary Cd intake were also applied and performed by two-dimensional 
Monte Carlo simulation [98] [100].  
 
3.6. Conclusion 
As can be seen, there was a two-fold difference in HBGVs between EFSA and JECFA. 
The approaches adopted for the evaluation were fully explained above. Even though 
the assessment of HBGVs was reasonable and appropriate, the approach taken by EFSA 
is preferred considering the adjustment of ethnicity. The TWI of 2.5 μg Cd/kg b.w. 
established by EFSA was chosen for further hazard characterization in this study.

Chapter 2 Nickel 
1. Introduction 
Nickel (Ni) is also a naturally existing trace metal that widely distributed in our 
environment (water, air, and soil). Several adverse health effects are caused by Ni, 
including lung cancer, allergic reactions, kidney, and cardiovascular diseases [101]. 
Inhalation, ingestion, and absorption through dermal contact are the three exposure 
routes to Ni. Inhalation is a major route for occupational individuals, especially for 
those who work in industries producing stainless steel and nickel alloys. For the general 
population, Ni contaminated food and water are the main sources [102, 103].  
 
Some foodstuff contains a high-level concentration of Ni, including chocolate, 
soybeans, legumes, nuts, oats, certain canned food, etc. On the other hand, Ni contents 
in cereals, milk products, meat, poultry, and eggs are considerably low. Plant-based 
food accounts for significant contribution to the total dietary Ni exposure, mainly 
because Ni content is higher in plant tissue than animal tissue. The concentration of Ni 
in plant-based food is associated with soil conditions (i.e. soil type), seasonal changes, 
and plant parts (i.e. leaves). On the whole, Ni is widely distributed in all different kinds 
of food with large variations. It is impossible to completely avoid dietary intake of Ni, 
but the selection of low Ni diet could help to decrease the risk of allergic reactions for 
sensitized individuals. Moreover, vitamin C supplement and iron-rich food have been 
recommended to reduce dietary absorption of Ni [104]. The existence of Ni in drinking 
water also needs to be noticed. The contamination level in the drinking water or tap 
water is determined by water sources (i.e. ground water, surface water) and water 
treatment systems (i.e. taps, tubing). In general, the average content of Ni in drinking 
water is below 10 μg/L in European countries, and less than 5 % of the population is at 
risk of exposure exceeding 20 μg/L [105]. Within the EU legislation framework, no 
regulatory limit of Ni in food has been established. However, for drinking water, a 
maximum limit of 0.02 mg/L was determined by the Commission Directive 
2003/40/EC. 
 
2. Hazard identification 
2.1. Toxicokinetics 
Ni in food and drinking water is absorbed via the gastrointestinal tract, ranging from 
1 % to 40%. The absorption rate is affected by factors like fasting status and diet 
==PAGE:19==
 
18
constituent. A relatively high absorption rate of Ni is observed in fasting individuals. 
Also, Ni is easier absorbed from drinking water compared with food [106]. Ni is uptake 
by cells via different transporters, including transferrin which is mainly responsible for 
iron transportation and homeostasis [107]. Once entering the bloodstream, Ni is bound 
to plasma albumin and α-2-microglobulin in the circulatory system, and then widely 
distributed in the body. It is clear that Ni is predominantly found in the kidneys, but 
also in the liver and brain [108]. From dietary exposure, absorbed Ni is primarily 
excreted in the urine, while most ingested as well as unabsorbed Ni is eliminated in the 
feces [107, 109]. The estimated biological half-life of dietary absorbed Ni was 
determined as 28 ± 9 hours in healthy volunteers [110]. 
 
2.2. Toxicological effects 
The difference in health effects caused by Ni is dependent on the routes of exposure. In 
this study, we focused on the damage induced by oral exposure to Ni. Reproductive and 
developmental toxicity were recognized as critical endpoints from chronic exposure of 
Ni by EFSA based on animal studies [106]. Reduced weight of reproductive organs 
(testes, epididymis, seminal vesicles, and prostate gland), fertilizing ability of 
spermatozoa (sperm count and sperm motility), and increased sperm morphological 
abnormalities were observed in Ni treated male mice [111, 112]. Reproductive effects 
were reported in Wistar rats (both males and females) exposed to Ni from drinking 
water. When the males were exposed to 30 ppm NiCl2 via drinking water for 28 or 42 
days before copulation, a decreased number of pregnancies and pups born were 
confirmed [113]. It has been proven that Ni can be transported across the placental 
barrier, leading to teratogenesis and embryonic effects in the developing fetus. Lipid 
peroxidation induced by Ni can cause damage to the placental membrane. Moreover, 
cellular death during fetus development is partially due to mutations in the mitotic 
apparatus caused by prenatal exposure to Ni [108]. The high pup mortality rate was 
observed during the lactation period when female only or both parents (female and male) 
were exposed to NiCl2 [113]. Overall, it is evident that developing fetuses and neonates 
are the main targets of developmental toxicity from oral exposure [114]. 
 
Ni is one of the most common causes of allergic contact dermatitis (ACD), which is 
defined as a type IV hypersensitivity response caused by the immune system reacting 
to environmental agents through dermal contact. There are two phases of ACD, the 
sensitization phase and elicitation phase (see in Figure 6). Ni ions penetrate the skin 
and activate epithelial cells to release cytokines (TNF-α and IL-1β) and chemokines. 
The ions also are taken up by antigen-presenting cells (APCs) like Langerhans cells 
==PAGE:20==
 
19
(LCs) and dendritic cells (DCs). Then, APCs regulated by cytokines migrate to the 
draining lymph nodes where haptens are delivered to naive T cells. At the end of the 
sensitization phase, hapten-specific T cells are produced and move out of the lymph 
nodes toward the epidermis. In the elicitation phase, re-exposure to Ni ions induces the 
activation of hapten-specific T cells to produce inflammatory cytokines and 
chemokines. The allergic response of localized dermatitis is observed [115]. In general, 
women are more sensitized compared with men. The prevalence of Ni sensitivity 
among the European population is 8-10% in children and adolescents and 8-19% in 
adults [116]. The decreased prevalence of Ni allergy is identified after the 
implementation of Nickel Restriction by REACH (former as EU Nickel Directive), 
however, Ni allergy and dermatitis are still substantial problems in EU [117]. In a 
Danish cross-sectional population-based study, approximately 11.4% of the young 
women aged 18-35 years old were allergic to Ni in 2006, reducing from 19.8% in 1990 
[118]. 
 
Oral exposure to Ni can provoke systemic contact dermatitis (SCD) among Ni-
sensitized individuals, and systemic reactions including hand eczema and eczematous 
flare-up skin reactions are developed [119-121]. SCD is defined as the inflammatory 
skin disease that occurs after systemic exposure (i.e. oral, intravenous, inhalation, or 
percutaneous) to a hapten in already sensitized individuals. The mechanism of SCD 
remains unclear [122]. Several studies indicated that the ingestion of Ni elicited allergic 
reactions in the skin among some Ni-sensitized individuals [123-125]. 7 out of 10 Ni-
sensitized participants reacted to oral administration of 4.0 mg Ni, however, the dose is 
much higher than normal dietary Ni intake of 0.1-0.5 mg/day. Meanwhile, few 
participants reacted to the doses of 0.3 or 1.0 mg Ni [126]. A meta-analysis study was 
performed to estimate the threshold dose of Ni ingestion that can cause SCD in Ni-
sensitized individuals. In total, 9 studies along with 171 participants were categorized 
into three groups. The effective doses (EDs) of 50% response rate among the high, 
intermediate, and low Ni sensitivity groups were 1.27, 2.00, and 3.06 mg Ni, 
respectively. 1% of sensitive individuals were expected to respond with SCD for the 
oral dose of 0.22-0.35 Ni mg per day [127]. The EFSA committee concluded that SCD 
was selected as the critical endpoint for acute exposure to Ni. Also, Ni-sensitized 
individuals are at the risk of developing eczematous flare-up skin reactions considering 
the current acute exposure to Ni from diet [106]. 
 
3. Dose-response assessment 
==PAGE:21==
 
20
To evaluate the risk of dietary Ni exposure, both chronic and acute effects need to be 
considered. The reproductive and developmental effects observed in animal studies 
were identified as endpoints for chronic exposure among the general population, 
whereas allergic reactions were selected as the endpoint for acute exposure among Ni-
sensitized individuals.   
 
3.1. Reproductive and developmental toxicity 
Two animal studies published by the Nickel Institute were used to derive the RP for 
chronic effects [106]. In the one-generation dose-range finding (DRF) study, Ni sulfate 
hexahydrate was orally given to rats at six dose levels of 0, 10, 20, 30, 50, and 75 mg/kg 
b.w./day, corresponding to 0, 2.2, 4.4, 6.6, 11, and 17 mg Ni/kg b.w./day. There were 
one solvent control and five treatment groups consisting of eight males and eight 
females in each group. For F0 parental rats and F1 offspring, the doses were 
administrated two weeks before mating and at post-natal day (PND) 22. The results 
were presented in Table 4. For F0 generation (parents), no statistically significant 
differences were found in copulation and fertility indices between the control and 
treatment groups. Copulation index was defined as the number of animals successfully 
paired with copulation dividing by the number of mated animals, while fertility index 
was defined as the number of pregnant females dividing by the number of animals 
successfully paired with copulation. The fertility index was 100% in all six groups, 
while the copulation index was also 100% except in the treatment group of 50 mg kg 
b.w./day (87.5%). Significantly increased mean post-implantation loss was observed in 
30, 50, and 75 mg/kg b.w./day groups compared with the control group. As for F1 
generation (offspring), a significant increase in the incidence of dead pups was 
observed at the exposure of 75 mg/kg b.w./day. No effects in the growth or survival of 
pups were found for several weeks after weaning. Based on the results from the DRF 
study, dose levels of 1.0, 2.5, 5.0, and 10.0 mg/kg b.w./day were selected for the 
following two-generation reproduction toxicity (2-GEN) study [128]. The lowest-
observed-adverse-effect level (LOAEL) of 2.2 mg Ni/kg b.w. per day was identified by 
EFSA because of the number of dead pups at PND 0 [106].  
 
In the 2-GEN study, there were 28 rats (F0 and F1 generations) in the control group 
and each treatment group (four in total). Ni sulfate hexahydrate were orally 
administrated to rats at five dose levels of 0, 1.0, 2.5, 5.0, and 10.0 mg/kg b.w./day, 
corresponding to 0, 0.2, 0.6, 1.1, 2.2 mg Ni/kg b.w./day. F0 generation was gavaged 10 
weeks before mating, while F1 generation was gavaged starting on PND 22. The results 
were presented in Table 5. There were no statistically significant differences between 
==PAGE:22==
 
21
the control group and treatment groups in the mean post-implantation loss of F0 and F1 
generation. Furthermore, no statistically significant effects were noted in pup viability 
of F1 and F2 generation at any dose level. In summary, the highest dose of Ni sulfate 
hexahydrate had no adverse effects on reproductive and developmental functions in rats. 
Therefore, the no-observed-adverse-effect level (NOAEL) was identified as 10.0 mg/kg 
b.w./day of Ni sulfate hexahydrate, which is equal as 2.2 mg Ni/kg b.w./day [129]. 
 
BMD analysis was performed for dose-response assessment. The incidence of litters 
with post-implantation in treatment groups (F1 generation) was the endpoint to assess 
the developmental toxicity of Ni in BMD calculation. The main results were presented 
in Table 6. The BMDL10 value of 0.20 mg Ni/kg b.w./day was estimated by the 
multistage model based on the DRF dataset, while 0.25 mg Ni/kg b.w./day was 
estimated by the quantal-linear model based on the 2-GEN dataset. However, no 
statistically significant increasing trend (Cochran-Armitage test) was found in the 
incidence of litters with post-implantation for both DRF (P = 0.3) and the 2-GEN (P = 
0.13) studies. Both studies were conducted by the same research group following the 
same experiment protocol (the same experimental conditions and methods). The 
BMDL10 values from the two studies were quite similar. Thus, DRF and 2-GEN data 
were combined for BMD analysis. The BMDL10 was calculated by the multistage 
model, as 0.28 mg Ni/ kg b.w./day. A statistically significant (P = 0.00013) dose-
response relationship of combined DRF and 2-GEN dataset was observed. The 
BMDL10 of 0.28 mg Ni/ kg b.w. per day was chosen as the RP. As a result, considering 
the default uncertainty factor of 100, the TDI of 2.8 μg Ni/ kg b.w. per day was derived 
for chronic exposure [106]. 
 
3.2. SCD 
Meanwhile, Ni-sensitized individuals were not considered to be protected from acute 
allergic effects (i.e. hand eczema) of SCD by the TDI of 2.8 μg Ni/ kg b.w. per day. 
Seventeen studies included in a meta-analysis were assessed for the dose-response 
relationship. Studies designed with no placebo tests, positive responses in placebo tests, 
or double exposure were excluded for the calculation of ED values [127]. Even though 
estimated ED values for 10% response could be used to establish the RP of acute 
exposure to Ni, the meta-analysis conducted by Jensen was not considered by EFSA. 
The committee concluded that it was reasonable to include studies with positive 
responses in placebo tests, as background exposure is required in the dose-response 
assessment. Besides, there was no scientific supportive information regarding 
individuals categorized into different sensitive groups. A total of seventeen studies were

examined by EFSA, and three studies [125, 126, 130] were selected as suitable for 
BMD analysis [106].  
 
In the Gawkrodger study, twenty-four women and two men (aged 19-67) sensitized to 
Ni were orally given Ni salt (NiSO4∙7H2O) in lactose capsule or the placebo at first, 
and the complementary set of capsules were given later. 0.4 (n = 10) and 2.5 (n = 10) 
mg Ni were administrated on two successive days (double exposure), while 5.6 mg (n 
= 6) Ni were administrated as a single dose. In double exposure groups, three 
participants reacted to Ni only and two reacted to Ni and placebo at a dose level of 0.4 
mg Ni, while two participants reacted to Ni only and three reacted to Ni and placebo at 
a dose level of 2.5 mg Ni. All six participants had reactions with Ni only or Ni and 
placebo at a single dose of 5.6 mg Ni [130]. The incidences of participants with allergic 
reactions to Ni exposure per treatment group (5/10, 5/10, 6/6) were used for BMD 
calculation by EFSA. The BMDL10 of 0.18 mg Ni per person was estimated from the 
quantal-linear model, corresponding to 2.6 μg Ni/kg b.w. assuming a body weight of 
70 kg [106].  
 
In the Hindsén study, thirty Ni sensitive female participants were patch tested with a 
serial dilution of Ni sulfate hexahydrate ranging from 0.0013% to 12.5%. One month 
after the final patch test, all participants were randomly categorized into three dose 
groups, as 0 (placebo), 1.0, and 3.0 mg Ni. In the placebo group, no flare-up reactions 
were detected on the previous patch test sites. Two participants had flare-up reactions 
at a dose level of 1.0 mg Ni, and nine participants at a dose level of 3.0 mg Ni [125]. 
The incidences of participants with flare-up reactions to Ni exposure per group (0/10, 
2/10, 9/9) were used for BMD calculation by EFSA. The BMDL10 of 0.11 mg Ni per 
person was estimated from the quantal-linear model, corresponding to 1.6 μg Ni/kg b.w. 
assuming a body weight of 70 kg [106].  
 
In the Jensen study, forty Ni-sensitized individuals and twenty non-Ni-sensitized 
individuals participated in patch testing with 0.55%, 1.66%, and 5% Ni given as Ni 
sulfate hexahydrate. One month after patch testing, Ni-sensitized participants were 
randomly categorized into four dose groups, as 0, 0.3, 1.0, and 4.0 mg Ni. Non-Ni-
sensitized participants were randomly divided into two groups, orally administrated to 
either a placebo or 4.0 mg Ni. Skin reactions, itching, and general symptoms (headache, 
dizziness, nausea, and metallic taste) were closely monitored. The positive cutaneous 
reactions included widespread clinical reactions, flare-up reactions in previous sites of 
contact dermatitis, and flare‐up reactions of previously positive patch test sites. No 
clinical cutaneous reactions were observed among non-Ni-sensitized participants. Only 
one Ni-sensitized participant had clinical cutaneous reactions in placebo groups, four

reactions were only observed at a dose level of 4.0 mg Ni among Ni-sensitized 
participants [126]. The incidences of Ni-sensitized participants with clinical cutaneous

Chapter 3 Risk assessment 
1. Exposure assessment 
In this study, exposure assessment is defined as the intake level of Cd and Ni that 
consumers exposed through the consumption of dark chocolate. Occurrence and 
consumption data collected from literature and public reports were used for calculation. 
Scenarios were designed to reflect potential exposure with the influence of different 
factors, especially for groups who are more vulnerable.  
 
1.1. Occurrence of cadmium in chocolate 
Africa (68%), Asia (17%), and America (15%) are the major regions to produce cocoa, 
and the yield has been continuously increasing to satisfy the demand. The geographic 
origin of cocoa beans is considered as an important factor of Cd contamination in 
chocolate [131]. High Cd concentration was found in cocoa beans produced in South 
America (1,388 ± 1,089 μg/kg), followed by Central America (544 ± 302 μg/kg) and 
East Africa (508 ± 587 μg/kg). Relatively low Cd content was detected in cocoa beans 
originated from Asia (328 ± 176 μg/kg) and West Africa (92.6 ± 41.8 μg/kg). The large 
variation in occurrence data is very likely due to the large biological variation.[132]. 
Moreover, the mean Cd concentration observed in chocolate bars using cocoa beans 
from South America (0.58 ± 0.52 mg/kg) was much higher than Central America (0.33 
± 0.30 mg/kg), Africa (0.20 ± 0.11 mg/kg), and Asia Pacific (0.12 ± 0.07 mg/kg) [133]. 
Therefore, it is certain that the origins of cocoa beans could result in large variations in 
Cd concentration in chocolate. Particularly, cocoa beans produced from Ecuador have 
been used in some top-selling dark chocolate in Denmark, including Ritter Sport Edel-
Bitter 73% [134]. 
 
A study from Italy indicated Cd concentration in chocolate was far below the maximum 
level of 0.80 mg/kg. The median Cd concentration of chocolate with ≥50% total dry 
cocoa solids was only 0.116 (± 0.015) mg/kg [135]. Cd concentration in dark chocolate 
is dependent on cocoa content. In general, the cocoa content of dark chocolate is above 
50%, possibly up to 70-85%. About 0.037 and 0.194 mg/kg Cd were detected in dark 
chocolate (50% cocoa) and very dark chocolate (70% cocoa) from the Austrian market 
[136]. For consumers, elevated risk of Cd exposure can be expected for the regular 
consumption of dark chocolate with high cocoa content produced by cocoa beans 
originated from South American countries.

2. Risk characterization 
Risk characterization is the final step of risk assessment. The results from previous 
dose-response analysis and exposure assessment were combined to quantify the health 
risks of Cd and Ni from consuming dark chocolate. On the basis of risk estimation, 
decisions are made by risk managers or policy makers to ensure the safety of consumers. 
Regarding the chronic effects due to the long-term exposure of Cd and Ni, the risk 
characterization was carried out by comparing the established HBGVs from dose-
response analysis with estimated exposure. As for the allergic reactions caused by acute 
exposure from Ni, the risk characterization was conducted by estimating the MOEs.  
 
2.1. Risk characterization of chronic Cd exposure  
The dietary exposure to Cd from dark chocolate consumption among the Danish 
population aged 4-75 ranged from 0.0069 to 0.0093 μg/kg b.w./day, while the exposure 
among consumers was 0.0134-0.0198 μg/kg b.w./day. Estimated exposure among 
children aged 4-9 ranged from 0.0176 (whole-level) to 0.0370 (consumer-level) μg/kg 
b.w./day, 0.0084-0.0194 μg/kg b.w./day among adolescents aged 10-17, and 0.0057-
0.0147 μg/kg b.w./day among adults aged 18-75. The dietary exposure from dark 
chocolate consumption was far below the TWI of 2.5 μg/kg b.w. per week (0.36 μg/kg 
b.w./day) and accounted for approximately 3-5% of total dietary exposure to Cd among 
the general population in Denmark. It can be concluded that there is a low concern of 
health risks from Cd exposure regarding the current consumption of dark chocolate. 
 
==PAGE:36==
 
35
2.2. Risk characterization of chronic Ni exposure 
The dietary exposure to Ni from dark chocolate consumption was 0.19-0.22 μg/kg 
b.w./day among the Danish population aged 4-75, and the consumer-level of exposure 
ranged from 0.36-0.47 μg/kg b.w./day. Estimated exposure among children aged 4-9 
was observed as 0.47 (whole-level) to 0.87 (consumer-level) μg/kg b.w./day, 0.22-0.46 
μg/kg b.w./day among adolescents aged 10-17, and 0.15-0.35 μg/kg b.w./day among 
adults aged 18-75. Based on the results of exposure assessment, the estimated exposure 
was much lower than the TDI of 2.8 μg/kg b.w./day, and only contributed 5-8% of total 
exposure to the Ni among the whole Danish population. Therefore, the risk of Ni 
exposure from dark chocolate consumption is considered to be acceptable. 
 
2.3. Risk characterization of acute nickel exposure 
The margin of exposure (MOE) is generally used to assess the risk of substances that 
are genotoxic and carcinogenic. In general, MOE of 10,000 or higher is recognized as 
a low public health concern. The BMDs (or T25) derived from animal studies are 
considered as standardized RPs for the calculation of MOE. The equation is shown 
below [148]: 
 
𝑀𝑂𝐸 =  𝐵𝑀𝐷𝐿10 / 𝑒𝑥𝑝𝑜𝑠𝑢𝑟𝑒 𝑒𝑠𝑡𝑖𝑚𝑎𝑡𝑒𝑠 
 
It was determined that the MOE approach was adopted for the evaluation of acute risk 
from Ni exposure, considering no thresholds were established for sensitization of 
dermal contact to Ni as well as allergic reactions in sensitized individuals. The RP was 
established based on studies that fasting participants were orally administrated to Ni 
through lactose capsules, while much lower absorption rate is expected from food 
consumption. The allergic reaction is not considered to be a severe health concern 
compare to other toxicological effects. On the contrary, substantial inter-individual 
variability in the immune response to Ni exposure was not examined. In summary, the 
MOE of 10 or higher reflected a low health concern [106]. 
 
The MOEs were calculated based on the RP of 1.1 μg Ni/kg b.w. and the estimated 
exposure level ranging from 0.49 (adults) to 1.40 (children) μg/kg b.w./day. The MOE 
values were far below 10 across all age groups, as 0.8-0.9 among children aged 4-9, 
1.5-1.7 among adolescents aged 10-17, and 2.0-2.2 among adults aged 18-75. Overall, 
Ni-sensitized individuals in Denmark may develop eczematous flare-up skin reactions 
due to acute exposure to Ni from the dark chocolate consumption.

Chapter 4 Discussion 
Consumption scenarios 1 and 2 were designed for the calculation of dark chocolate 
consumption among the whole population in Denmark. Similar results of consumption 
rate were obtained from two different scenarios (see in Table 9). Therefore, it seems 
that the estimation of consumption rate is considered reliable, despite large uncertainty 
from scenario assumptions (further discussion in chapter 5). The estimated 
consumption of dark chocolate among the Danish population aged 4-75 ranged from 
2.82 to 3.76g/day, while 5.31-6.84 g/day among consumers. 
 
In consumption scenario 3, theoretical consumption rates were discussed. It is 
necessary for the protection of consumers, since no consumption data at consumer-level 
was available. One or two dark chocolate bars per week (14.29 or 28.57 g/day) were 
much higher than the regular consumption among the whole population as well as 
consumers, but they are highly possible assumptions for a certain group of consumers. 
The results (see in Appendix Table S2 and S3) illustrate that high consumption level 
(14.29 or 28.57 g/day) of dark chocolate is of concern across all age groups considering 
the significant increase in the exposure of Cd and Ni. 
 
In this study, it is obvious that the estimated exposure of Cd and Ni from the dark 
chocolate consumption is significantly higher in children compared to adults, meaning 
that children are at higher risk of adverse health effects. The mean difference is the 
change in body weight, whereas the consumption level is similar across different age 
groups. Additionally, 5 or 10 years of exposure period among the young population 
have limited contribution of lifetime exposure compared with adulthood, especially that 
renal damage induced by Cd is the chronic effect. Even though it is unlikely for children 
to develop diseases and show symptoms at such a young age, high-level exposure 
remains a threat to health when they reach their adulthood stage.  
 
Moreover, cocoa contents and cocoa beans origins can be adjusted by dark chocolate 
producers, which lead to varied contamination levels of Cd and Ni. Based on the results 
from exposure scenarios (scenarios 3 and 4 in section 1.4.; scenarios 2 and 3 in section 
1.5.), significant yet small differences were observed. Therefore, cocoa contents and 
cocoa beans origins are not determinative factors that affect Cd and Ni exposure from 
dark chocolate consumption. It can be concluded that there are no worries for 
consumers to hesitate about cocoa contents and cocoa beans origins when they purchase 
chocolate regarding health concerns.  
 
==PAGE:38==
 
37
To address the public health concern of Cd contamination, the European Regulation No 
488/2014 about the MLs of Cd in foodstuffs including chocolate products was 
implemented in 2019. The effects of established ML of 0.8 mg Cd/kg in chocolate with 
≥ 50% total dry cocoa solids on Cd exposure were discussed. The exposure from other 
sources was assumed as a fixed value, as total dietary exposure of Cd minus estimated 
exposure from dark chocolate consumption. Therefore, the maximum exposure of dark 
chocolate consumption was calculated as the difference between TWI and exposure 
from other sources. As a result, estimated MLs targeting two population groups (young 
population and adults) with different consumption rates were established and presented 
in Table 13. The results indicated that the ML of 0.8 mg Cd/kg was able to allow adults 
to consume dark chocolate up to 14.29 g/day (one chocolate bar per week). On the other 
hand, the estimated ML for the protection of young consumers aged 4-14 was 0.4 mg/kg, 
as a half of regulatory ML. It needs to be considered that the contamination level of Cd 
in dark chocolate from the Danish market was far below 0.4 mg/kg.  
 
In the matter of Ni exposure, no regulatory limit within the EU legislation framework 
was available. Based on the EFSA report, total dietary exposure to Ni among the Danish 
population was very close to or exceeded the TDI of 2.8 μg/kg b.w./day, ranging from 
2.66 to 5.64 μg/kg b.w./day [106]. Therefore, it is important to establish MLs to reduce 
excessive exposure of Ni from various foodstuffs. Estimated MLs of Ni in dark 
chocolate across three age groups were shown in Table 14. Considering the whole 
population-level (consumer-level) of consumption rate, the estimated MLs of 1.3-1.7 
(0.8-1.0) mg Ni/kg was calculated for children aged 4-9, while 2.3-3.0 (1.3-1.6) mg 
Ni/kg for adolescents aged 10-17, and 2.8-3.6 (1.4-1.7) mg Ni/kg for adults aged 18-
75. Considering the Ni contamination in dark chocolate was found ranging from 3.41 
to 3.8 mg/kg, it might be hard to establish a possible ML for consumers’ protection. 
 
In summary, high-level consumption of dark chocolate is not recommended for 
consumers concerning potential excessive total dietary exposure to Ni.

Chapter 5 Uncertainties 
The occurrence of hazards in dark chocolate: No Danish data was found available 
regarding the hazards (Cd and Ni) concentrations in dark chocolate, therefore, the 
occurrence values in bitter-sweet chocolate from Petersen report was used. The terms 
“dark chocolate”, “bitter chocolate”, and “bitter-sweet chocolate” are quite imprecise, 
for the reason that no regulatory standards have been established. It is expected that the 
main differences are the sugar and cocoa contents. Considering cocoa content varied 
widely in different brands (dark chocolate) and very low concentrations of the hazards 
in added sugar, the magnitude of the uncertainty is recognized as small. The direction 
of the uncertainty is not clear, causing either over- or under-estimation of exposure. 
Furthermore, sampling uncertainty can also affect the estimation of exposure to hazards 
due to the limited sample size (20 samples) of bitter-sweet chocolate. Considering no 
methods are applicable to reduce uncertainties, the occurrence values reported by EFSA 
were introduced as supplementary information for exposure assessment.  
 
The consumption of dark chocolate: The consumption of dark chocolate was not 
investigated in the Danish Diet Survey. Therefore, two scenarios were established to 
extrapolate the consumption rate of sugar and candy (consumption scenario 1) and 
chocolate (consumption scenario 2) to dark chocolate. The magnitude of extrapolation 
uncertainty is expected to be large due to the assumptions made in scenarios, 
particularly for children. Compared to adults, children are generally more attracted to 
candies or chocolates with relatively high sugar content instead of dark chocolate. In 
summary, the exposure to hazards for children in this study is very likely to be over-
estimated. The consumption rate of dark chocolate among consumers (P90) applied in 
acute exposure assessment was calculated based on the FFQ reflecting for chronic 
dietary habits. For example, there is a high possibility that a chocolate bar (100g) is 
consumed in one day a week rather than over the week. Therefore, it’s very likely to 
under-estimate the consumption level for acute exposure.  
 
Hazard characterization: The meta-analysis of human studies was performed for the 
dose-response relationship between U-Cd and β2M. There were no controlled selection 
criteria for publications included in the database. These studies were also aimed at 
different purposes with different approaches. More importantly, statistical significance 
doesn’t necessarily represent biological significance. It is difficult to identify the 
direction of the uncertainty, but the magnitude could be large. The HBGV of Ni 
exposure was derived based on animal studies. An uncertainty factor of 100 was applied 
for interspecies and interindividual differences. Ni was administrated to rats by the 
==PAGE:40==
 
39
ingestion of water. The differences in the absorption rate of Ni via the ingestion of food 
or water could result in over-estimation.  
 
In summary, there is a rather small uncertainty on the occurrence data of Cd and Ni in 
dark chocolate. A relatively large uncertainty is discovered on the consumption rate. 
Considering dark chocolate consumption had a limited contribution to the total dietary 
exposure of Cd and Ni, the impact of uncertainties on the risk assessment is considered 
to be small.

Chapter 6 Conclusions and recommendations 
Conclusions  
• 
There is a low concern of health risks from Cd and Ni chronic exposure regarding 
the current consumption of dark chocolate. 
 
• 
The estimated MOE values were far below 10 across all age groups in Denmark, 
meaning that Ni-sensitized individuals in Denmark may develop eczematous flare-
up skin reactions due to current levels of acute exposure to Ni from the dark 
chocolate consumption. 
 
Recommendations 
• 
The regulatory limits of Ni contamination in chocolate products including dark 
chocolate should be established for the protection of consumers.  
 
• 
Avoiding dark chocolate is recommended for Ni-sensitized individuals.
