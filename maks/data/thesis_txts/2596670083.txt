Abstract
The Niger transboundary river basin hosts over 130 million people in nine countries and
fragile ecosystems.
It is facing pressures driven by a growing number of water users,
socio-economic development and climate change. Decision-makers have agreed on an in-
frastructure development plan prioritizing a big expansion of irrigated agriculture to secure
food supply as well as multi-purpose dams.
Previous studies have investigated the impacts of this investment plan on the basin under
climate change scenarios. Yet, many studies did not represent interactions between the
Water-Energy-Food (WEF) commodities. Moreover, the use of hydro-economic optimiza-
tion approaches is scarce in existing literature on the topic.
In this study, we intend to fill the knowledge gaps by adopting a holistic representation
of the WEF Nexus interrelations and by using a hydro-economic optimization approach
based on a single economic objective function to model water allocation. We aim to assess
the impacts of infrastructure development both at basin and country scales, considering
economic impacts as well as impacts on flow dynamics and water availability. Additionally,
we investigate whether there are any trade-offs or synergies between different investment
plans. We evaluate the combined impacts of a drier climate and infrastructure development
on the extent of the flooded area in the Niger Inner Delta floodplain located in central
Mali.
We consider two infrastructure plans : the Irrigation Plan which is an expansion of ir-
rigated area all over the basin, and the Hydropower Plan which consists in building the
three dams of Fomi in Guinea, Taoussa in Mali and Kandadji in Niger. We build a simple
conceptual node-based model of the basin. We model water allocation using the Water
Hydropower Agriculture Tool for Investment and Financing (What-if), a hydro-economic
optimization model developed by the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), the Mas-
sachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) and the international environmental consulting
company COWI. This tool is documented in Payet-Burin et al. (2019) [39]. To assess
impacts on the Inner Delta, we set up a simple linear reservoir model and we link it to
the conceptual model of the basin. The extent of the flooded area is investigated un-
der 25 scenarios combining 5 scenarios of irrigation expansion and 5 scenarios of runoff
decrease (between -10% and -30%). We find that investment plans would generate impor-
tant agricultural benefits for Nigeria and Mali and moderate hydropower benefits for the
countries where new dams are to be built. Investment plans are found to generate a total
hydropower benefit of 50 M$/year in Guinea, Mali and Niger but a loss of 5 M$/year in
Nigeria. This trade-off is due to a reduction of inflow into hydropower production units
in Nigeria because of upstream increased agricultural abstractions and reservoir volume.
We do not identify other significant synergies or trade-offs between the two investment
plans. We conclude that climate change is the main driver affecting the flooded area in the
Inner Delta. A massive expansion of irrigation also affects the floodplain area especially
if combined with a drier climate (runoff reduction). Additionally, we find that the impact
of dam development on the flooded area is very mild. However, this study comes with a
number of data and modelling uncertainties. We do not represent crop and power market
interactions and we assume perfect cooperation among basin stakeholders. Further work
should be conducted to stengthen decision-making in the basin.

1
Introduction
1.1
Context
Ensuring human socio-economic development while maintaining environmental sustain-
ability is a growing challenge in a context of rapid population growth, changing lifestyles,
ecosystem degradation, increasing water scarcity and uncertainty in future climate [43].
In particular, West Africa is facing numerous pressures driven by a fast population in-
crease as the population in the region is expected to be multiplied by at least two by 2030
(compared to 1990) and by four in case of constant fertility rates. [35]. Moreover, due
to climate change and erratic rainfall, the region experiences regular yield losses which
threaten food security [62]. Rivers are key resources in this area, providing water to agri-
cultural, industrial and domestic users. Therefore, water resources management-related
decisions need to be made based on a thorough understanding of eco-hydrological processes
and climate change projections. Such decisions involve numerous stakeholders, especially
for transboundary river basins.
Furthermore, as they may impact not only the water sector but also other water-dependent
sectors such as power and agriculture, these decisions need to be made following a holistic
vision instead of a sectoral approach. “The Water, Energy and Food Security nexus –
Solutions for the Green Economy” conference, held in November 2011 at Bonn, laid the
foundation for new approaches to address water resources issues [56]. As shown in Figure
1.1, the nexus approach highlights the inter-dependencies between achieving water, energy
and food security for human well-being while ensuring ecosystem protection. It is based
on an understanding of the synergies and trade-offs between competing water, energy and
food users.

The transboundary Niger River Basin, called NRB in the following, shared among nine
West African countries, hosts fragile ecosystems [19] and a growing number of water users.
To sustain economic growth and human well-being in the region, decision-makers plan to
develop water, power and agricultural infrastructure.
This infrastructure development
plan includes a big expansion of irrigated agriculture and the development of large reser-
voirs to meet growing water supply, food and energy needs as well as to control the high
inter-annual variability in water availability [62]. This study aims to analyse these infras-
tructure investment options within the WEF nexus using a hydro-economic optimization
model. This introductory section presents some of the previous similar works and their
findings as well as the study area. Additionally, it shows how this study intends to fill
some of the knowledge gaps and to offer an innovative and holistic approach to answer
the research questions.
1.2
Presentation of the study area
The Niger is the third longest river in Africa after the Nile and the Congo River.
It
covers a total area of 2.13 million km², shared by over 130 million people in nine countries
(Benin, Burkina Faso, Cameroon, Chad, Guinea, Ivory Coast, Mali, Niger and Nigeria).
It supplies water for domestic, agricultural, industrial, mining and power uses within
the basin which faces fast population increase (estimated annual average growth rate
of 3.2%).
The nine riparian countries of the basin are ranked at the bottom third of
the 189 countries according to the United Nations Development Programme’s Human
Development Index (2018), with 5 in the last 15. 30% of the population do not have
access to an improved water source and only 35% have access to electricity. Agriculture
is a major economic activity within the basin, employing 80% of the population, and
the area is still highly dependent on small-holder agricultural production [19]. Among
the nine riparian countries, seven (all except Cameroon and Mali) are part of the West
African Power Pool (WAPP) which aims to promote and develop power generation and
transmission infrastructure as well as to coordinate power exchange among the Economic
Community Of West African States (ECOWAS) Member States [59]. Moreover, the Niger
River provides important ecosystem services, especially in the NID. The Niger River is
therefore a key resource, yet very unreliable considering climate and river flow variability
exacerbated by climate change [6]. The following sections present the hydrology, climate,
agriculture, socio-economic profile, power production and power market of the basin.
1.2.1
Hydrology & Climate
The Niger River originates from the highlands in Guinea and Sierra Leone and flows
northeast towards the Sahara and the NID central floodplain in Mali. After the NID,
the river flows towards the South and reaches a confluence point with its tributary, the
Benue river, before reaching the maritime delta in the Atlantic coast of southern Nigeria.
The northern section of the basin, extending across the Sahara desert into Algeria, is
hydrologically inactive. The active basin modelled in the study and called hereafter the
basin, covers 1.27 million km² [35]. Most of the basin area is shared between Guinea,
Mali, Niger and Nigeria. However, this study will also consider Benin, Burkina Faso and
Cameroon.

As shown in Figure 1.2, the Niger River receives water from different tributaries. According
to the Food & Agriculture Organization (FAO) [15], the main tributaries are the Sankarani
River (around 400 m³/s), the Bani River (500 m³/s) which flows into the NID, the Kaduna
River (500 m³/s) and the Benue River (3,400 m³/s). The average discharge measured at
the Gauging station Koulikouro, located at the end of the upper part of the basin, is
around 1,348 m³/s on average (measures taken from 1946 and 1992) [20]. Olgivie et al.
(2010) find an inflow in the NID of 36 km³/year, which corresponds to 1,141 m³/s [35]. The
NID is responsible for big evaporation losses accounting for between 30% and 50% of the
inflow depending on the years. This correspond to total losses between 220 and 950 m³/s.
At the gauging station Niamey in the middle part of the basin, mean annual discharge is
around 875 m³/s. In this part of the basin, flow values can reach very low levels below
10 m³/s. In the lower part of the basin, due to inflow from tributaries, the discharge is
bigger and reaches an average yearly value of 2,500 m³/s. After receiving water from the
Benue river and heavy rainfall [35], the annual mean discharge of the Niger River at its
end point in South Nigeria is around 5,589 m³/s.

Irrigation
The NRB has a high groundwater potential. Groundwater could supply both drinking
water and water for agricultural use. There are large aquifers in sedimentary strata in
Mali, Niger, Chad, Nigeria and Cameroon, and discontinuous aquifers in Guinea, Mali,
Ivory Coast, Burkina Faso and Niger. Groundwater recharge is variable and depends on
the geology, topology, climate and crop cover. Recharge rates vary from 20 mm/year in the
Sokoto region of Nigeria to 136 mm/year in Katchari in northern Burkina Faso. However,
both recharge and withdrawal data remain scarce [35].
The Niger Basin is under the influence of a varying climate, Sahelian in the North and more
tropical in the South. Rainfall has a high temporal variability and is unevenly distributed
across the basin. Values of precipitation range from 250 to 750 mm/year in the sahelian
zone to over 2,000 mm/year close to the river mouth in the Guinean zone [36]. Overall, the
rainy season is single-peaked and spans four to five months each year (i.e., May/June to
September/October). Figure 1.3 shows the typical yearly pattern for runoff, precipitation
and evapotranspiration in the basin.

Potential evapotranspiration is high across the basin, especially in the North, due to
advection from the Sahara. This causes high losses in water reservoirs [35]. During the
rainy season, precipitation exceeds evapotranspiration and runoff is produced, causing a
peak discharge in the NID and in the maritime delta and occasionally flooding the basin
[8]. The surplus also infiltrates the soils and recharges groundwater.
Environmental flow policies in the basin remain scarce although there is a growing recogni-
tion that minimum flow targets should be ensured, especially in the NID. In its Sustainable
Development Action Plan (SDAP), the Niger Basin Authority (NBA) considers a scenario
in which flooded area level in the inner delta should not drop below 90% of the current
levels under new infrastructure development [1].
1.2.2
The Niger Inner Delta
The NID is an important feature of the basin. It is a 40,000 km² floodplain containing
numerous wetlands, lakes and streams. According to Ogilvie et al. (2015) [34], the NID can
be divided into two major units: (1) the upstream delta between the gauging stations Ke-
Macina and Akka characterised by large independent channels in the dry season, which
become totally flooded during the wet season and (2) the downstream delta between
Akka and Koryoume composed of multiple small lakes and ponds separated by East–West
oriented sand dunes. Figure 1.4 shows the location of the delta and a satellite view of it.

According to Ogilvie et al. (2010), the annual mean flow into the NID from Guinea and

The NID is facing numerous pressures driven by increasing population, new dams de-
velopment projects upstream of the delta such as Djenne and Fomi and increased risk of
extreme hydrological events due to climate change [27]. In the 1980’s, a significant drought
in the delta resulted in famine amongst riparian populations. Moreover, the development
of dams upstream of the delta including Markala, Selingue and Talo has already affected
the natural flow, reducing the amplitude of the annual flood [34].
1.2.3
Socio-economic profile & Governance
Population in the NRB was estimated at 100 million inhabitants in 2008 by the World
Bank (WB) [7], with 80% living in Nigeria.
Overall, population density is increasing
when moving downstream, towards the maritime delta, and it varies from 4 to 25 inhabi-
tants/km² upstream of the NID to 516 inhabitants/km² in the maritime delta [1]. Most of
the population is young, with more than 50% being less than 15. Urbanisation is uneven
and varies from 20% in Niger to 65% in Nigeria. The basin is experiencing a fast urban-
ization, although 64% of its population was still rural in 2010. Moreover, the proportion
of people living under the threshold of poverty is high throughout the basin: 70.3% in
Burkina Faso, 70.1% in Guinea and 65.9% in Niger [35].

The Niger River is a key resource, especially in Mali and Niger. Mali, with around 30%
of the basin within its borders, has developed irrigated agriculture along the river as well
as rainfed crops. Additionally, according to the NBA, one and a half million people fully
depend on exploitation of the natural resources of the NID floodplain. This includes rice
farmers producing 5,000 to 170,000 tons of rice each year, cattle breeders with 2 million
cattle and 5 million small ruminants, as well as fishers producing 50,000 to 100,000 tons
of fish each year. Niger, with around 23% of the basin within its borders, strongly relies
on river navigation to reach the sea through Nigeria. Nigeria is a major agricultural user,
with both irrigated and rainfed fields [7].
The NBA, which was created in 1980 to replace the Niger River Commission established
in 1964, is the organization in charge of basin-scale cooperation on water resources [7].
Due to insufficient funds, the NBA first lost power and credibility. However, in the Paris
Declaration of April 2004, the basin Member States leaders committed to ensure socio-
economic development and a better sustainable shared management of water resources
throughout the basin. As a result, the NBA published its SDAP in 2007. This action plan
contains operational measures and approaches to tackle water resources challenges in the
basin and to plan infrastructure development. The NBA must ensure the engagement of
numerous actors, sometimes with competing interests.
1.2.4
Agriculture
Agriculture plays a central role in the NRB. It employs the majority of the people in
the basin and it represents 40% of the basin-wide Gross Domestic Product [62]. Yet, the
agricultural potential is still under achieved. There are over 2.5 million ha of arable land
in the basin but less than 20% of them are currently exploited. Agriculture in the basin
is characterized by a lack of mechanization and other inputs such as fertilizers, with most
of the labour provided by women and children [35]. Subsistence agriculture represents
78% of the total agricultural production. The region exports irrigated vegetables but this
industry is poorly organised. [16].
Major rainfed crops grown in the basin, both in terms of physical area and production, are
cassava, yams, maize sorghum, vegetables, rice, millet and groundnuts. Major irrigated
crops grown in the basin are rice, vegetables, sugarcane, potatoes, maize, sorghum and
millet [25] [62]. Detailed data on crops production, yields and areas for both irrigated and
rainfed crops is provided in Appendix D.
Overall, 85% of the cultivated area in the basin is rainfed. Crop patterns have adapted to
rainfall patterns: seasonal pasture is grown in the North whereas banana, cassava, sorghum
can be found when moving further South. Rice is mostly grown in the South and in the
NID [35]. According to the NBA, 265,000 ha are under full control irrigation, of which
117,000 ha are in Mali, 84,000 ha in Nigeria and 46,000 in Niger. Niger, Mali and Burkina
Faso are dominant in the basin in terms of irrigation development, with respectively 32%,
31% and 19% of their irrigation potential developed against less than 10% for the other
riparian countries [16]. The biggest irrigation withdrawals occur around the NID and in
the extreme North of the basin as well as in the Eastern parts in Nigeria [35].

1.2.6
Other water-dependent activities
Other activities in the basin are dependent on the water system. These include industry,
mining, fishery, livestock, navigation and tourism. The mining sector is mostly developed
in the upper and middle parts of the basin, yet not well regulated, which causes harm to
ecosystems [1]. Fishery and aquaculture are critical for jobs and food security, especially
in the two deltas and around big dams [1]. Around 240,000 tons of fish are produced each
year. Livestock also plays a significant role in the basin, accounting for 10% to 15% to
the basin Gross Domestic Product. The basin hosts 50 million herders who maintain 138
million livestock units [16]. Tourism and navigation are still underdeveloped, although
the basin hosts several ecosystem hot-spots where tourism could be developed such as the
NID, the Upper Niger National Park or the National Kainji park [1].
1.2.7
Drivers of change and challenges
The NRB is experiencing fast population growth. The growth rate is estimated at around
3% [35] [1], which implies that the population could reach more than 400 million by 2050.
This rise in population coupled with a fast economic growth result in projected increased
demands for WEF commodities. Electricity demand for the WAPP is estimated to raise
from around 100 TWh in 2015 to 350 TWh in 2040. Irrigation demand is also planned
to increase, although estimations are scarce and highly dependent on expansion scenar-
ios. According to the European Union Joint Research Centre (JRC) (2019), irrigation
requirements for crop production may vary between 3,280 Mm³/year and 4,133 Mm³/year
depending on agricultural scenarios [16]. Additionally, livestock numbers have increased
in all countries over the last 30 years and are expected to continue to rise, responding to
the projected population increase and the increased demand for meat and dairy products
[16].
To address the challenges due to this rising population and demands, decision-makers are
planning infrastructure developments covering the WEF sectors. As stated by JRC (2019),
irrigation investments in the Sahel are concentrated in North Nigeria, and the Office du
Niger in Mali [16]. Hydraulic infrastructure such as dams is not well developed in West
Africa even though there is great potential for large dams in the rivers Senegal and Niger
[5]. According to the NBA [2], the whole investment programme for the NRB covers the
period 2008-2027 and is divided into 4 five-year ”sub-programmes”. In 2007, the total
investment amount was estimated at 6,459 M$. Around 80% of investments are planned
to go to Component 1 of the investment plan which is Development of Socio-economic
Infrastructure (the two other components cover ecosystem conservation and resources pro-
tection as well as capacity-building and stakeholder involvement). The NBA Master Plan
[1] includes the construction of big multi-purpose dams as well as the development of both
hydropower and hydro-agricultural infrastructure. Power investments could include the
development of renewables and the extension of distribution networks.
According to the International Renewable Energy Agency (IRENA), the share of renew-
able technologies in the WAPP region could increase from the current 22% of electricity
generation to as much as 52% in 2030 [41]. Transmission lines between countries could
also be further developed [5]. Committed projects include the Dorsale line between Ivory
Coast and Ghana, the CLSG line connecting Ivory Coast, Liberia, Guinea and Sierra
Leone and the OMVG line connecting Senegal, Guinea, Gambia and Guinea Bissau.
Moreover, the NRB is expected to face challenges due to climate variability in the years
to come. The region has experienced extreme events in the past decades, such as big
rainfall decreases that led to the droughts and famines of the 1970’s and 1980’s. Floods
are also becoming a growing concern in the basin, both in terms of human safety and

• Poor regulation of the upper and middle parts of the basin;
• Under-exploitation of the hydropower potential;
• Uneven distribution of water resources across the basin;
• Poor knowledge and screening of groundwater resources;
• High dependency of the NID floodplains to upstream flow variability;
• Lack of environmental flows regulations and policies.
1.3
Literature review & Knowledge gaps
1.3.1
Hydro-economic Optimization
As explained by Payet-Burin et al.(2019), hydro-economic optimization models have re-
cently developed into decision-support tools for basin-scale water resources management.
The novelty of such tools is that they assign an economic impact to each decision to trans-
form complex multi-objective problems into single-objective problems [39]. These models
include representations of the water system and users. Each water user has a demand for
water and a Willingness To Pay (WTP). In most of the existing hydro-economic models,
demand and WTP are exogenous which means that they do not vary dynamically with
the availability of WEF commodities. Yet, representing such interactions may lead to a
more realistic representation of basin dynamics. For instance, developing low cost solar
power in the basin would increase power availability, which may lead to a reduction of
energy prices over the basin and to a lower WTP for water for hydropower.
1.3.2
Water-Energy-Food nexus
Over the past decade, several papers using a nexus approach to model inter-dependencies
between WEF systems have been published. According to Harwood (2018), this approach
can handle the complexity of having multiple stakeholders with different viewpoints [22].
Payet-Burin et al.
(2019) underline that some of the work relying on a WEF nexus
approach actually uses single-system models where the output of one model serves as
input for another model. On the contrary, other works couple models and represent all
the interactions within a single model. The former method allows different stakeholders
to use their own tools whereas using a coupled model requires a holistic view which might
not be part of their field of expertise. However, a coupled model is more relevant for
optimization frameworks [39].
Nevertheless, according to McCarl (2017), switching to a WEF nexus paradigm raises a
number of challenges. Indeed, it requires that traditionally domain-focused stakeholders

1.3.3
Previous findings on the Niger River Basin
Hydrological modelling
Previously published literature provides numerous hydrological models of the NRB. Ac-
cording to Eisner et al. (2017) [12] and Krysanova et al. (2017) [28], the following hy-
drological models of the NRB were available in 2017: ECOMAG, HBV, HYMOD, HYPE,
mHM, SWAT, SWIM, VIC, WaterGAP3. For example, Aich et al. (2014) [6] compared
impacts of climate change on streamflow in four large African basins including the NRB
using the Soil and Water Integrated Model (SWIM). In 2015, the WB and the AFD
published a climate resilience study [4] using the Water Evaluation And Planning system
(WEAP) which is a Windows-based decision support system for integrated water resources
management and policy analysis. This study will be called ECRAI in the following (for
Enhancing the Climate Resilience of Africa’s Infrastructure). Andersson et al. (2017) [8]
applied the Hydrological Predictions for the Environment model, originally developed for
Sweden, to the NRB. Recently, Liersh et al. (2019) relied on the SWIM model to evaluate
the feasibility of water resources planning in the upper Niger River basin.
The recent development of satellite technologies has brought innovative hydrological mod-
elling methods. For example, Oyerinde et al. (2017) modelled runoff dynamics in the
NRB using satellite-based rainfall estimates [37]. They used rainfall data from the Global
Precipitation Climatology Project. Based on their findings, they proposed the integration
of this satellite dataset in runoff modeling, especially in data scarce river basins.
Specific hydrological models have been developed to represent the NID. Ibrahim et al.
(2017) conducted a water balance analysis over the NID focusing on spatio-temporal dy-
namics of flooded area and water losses [24]. They relied on datasets from the QuickSCAT
satellite. Ogilvie et al. (2015) performed a decadal monitoring of flooded area within the
NID using the Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer (MODIS) satellite dataset
[34].
Climate change impacts on hydrology
Numerous studies have investigated the impacts of climate change on the NRB hydrology.
Oguntunde et al. (2013) quantified the impact of increased greenhouse gases on the hydro-
climatology of the NRB [36]. They concluded that under elevated emissions for the period
2031–2050, the NRB climate would be warmer in all the months, drier during the rainy
months (April–September), but wetter during the dry-wet and wet-dry transition months
(March and October). Aich et al. (2014) analysed the impacts of climate change on river
flow in the upper Niger basin and identified adaptation measures [6]. They found a high
uncertainty in flow modifications both in terms of direction and magnitude. Angelina et
al. (2015) reported similar findings. They quantified the modifications of streamflow at
the Koulikouro gauging station (located downstream of the upper NRB) under climate
change and found that changes in annual mean flow and annual maximum monthly flow
were not statistically significant at the 95% confidence level. Eisner et al. (2017) studied
the whole basin and found a decreasing streamflow at the beginning of the wet season with
the largest reduction of −22 mm (−37%) occurring in August [12]. Vetter et al. (2017)
evaluated sources of uncertainty in projected hydrological changes under climate change
projections in the NRB and noted a high uncertainty for this river basin, thus calling
for cautiousness while interpreting hydrological projections resulting from climate change

Infrastructure development
Literature about water resources management in the NRB also covers infrastructure in-
vestment plans and water management policies, often under climate change projections.
For example, Ghile et al. (2014) performed a bottom-up climate risk assessment of in-
frastructure investments focusing on the upper and middle parts of the basin [18]. They
found low to moderate climate change-related risks on infrastructure considering targets
set by stakeholders of the basin, the highest risk being for environmental flow level tar-
gets. Furthermore, the ECRAI study published in 2015 evaluated the resilience of planned
power and agricultural infrastructure in a context of climate change in several African river
basins including the NRB [4]. According to their findings, the current water allocation
rules in the NRB Sustainable Development Plan prioritize irrigated agriculture to secure
food production and alleviate poverty. Additionally, they concluded that the irrigation
infrastructure development plan is insensitive to projected climate changes: some agricul-
tural production decreases may occur, but would generally be less than 3% of the output
projected. They projected mild (<10% decrease) to moderate (<20% decrease) impacts
of climate change on hydropower production, navigation, and flooding of the NID. Since
the approaches for most of the previous studies were mainly sectoral or bilateral, Yang et
al. (2018) intended to fill the gap by assessing trade-offs within the WEF nexus. They
assessed the impact of climate and anthropogenic changes on the water, energy, food and
ecosystem sectors and they characterized the resulting trade-offs using water availability
metrics. Results suggested that dam development in the NRB could mitigate negative
impacts from climate change on hydropower generation and also on ecosystem health to
some extent.
As the NID provides significant human and ecosystems services [57], it has been an area
of focus for infrastructure development and climate impacts assessments. Liersch et al.
(2013) investigated the vulnerability of rice production in the NID under water resources
management decisions coupled with climate variability and change [29]. Their main find-
ing is that both projected climate change and upstream reservoir management lead to a
significant reduction of peak discharges during the rainy season and hence to serious losses
of potential agricultural areas within the NID. Zare et al. (2017) evaluated the influence
of the Bani river on the annual and seasonal variations of floods within the NID. Their
findings suggested that climate change will lead to earlier floods during dry years and later
floods during wet years. This is due to the smaller extent of the flooded area during dry
years and to a large amount of lateral flow that slow the spread of the flood during wet
years. Kassambara et al. (2018) assessed the impact of agricultural water allocation on
the ecosystems in the NID [26]. They found that the expansion of irrigated area in the
Office du Niger irrigation scheme, located upstream of the NID, had a significant impact
on water availability in the NID during the low-flow period despite recent increases in
rainfall and upstream flows.
Overall, impacts of both climate change and infrastructure development on the NRB have
been investigated. However, while some studies have not considered the entire basin [9]
[12] [18], others only focused on a sectoral point of view without considering interactions
within the WEF nexus, as pointed out by Yang et al. [62]. Moreover, among the studies
assessing investment plans, outputs such as economic impacts, cost-benefit analyses and
economic valuation of synergies and trade-offs between scenarios seem to remain scarce.
The dynamics of the NID have been assessed under both climate change and investment
projections. Yet, there is a need to connect the dots by implementing a thorough reser-

1.4
Research questions
Transboundary integrated water management is needed to achieve development in the
NRB in accordance with the Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) [44]. Multiple SDGs
could be impacted: End Poverty (1), Zero Hunger (2), Clean and available water for all (6),
Clean and affordable energy for all (7), and Sustainable economic growth (8). One major
challenge is to provide decision-makers with tools to evaluate infrastructure development
plans while considering climate change scenarios.
This study aims to perform a hydro-economic analysis of infrastructure investment options
within the WEF nexus in the NRB. The objective is to analyse these options in terms of
economic feasibility by assessing basin-scale and country-scale benefits, and sustainability
by considering environmental flows in the NID. The project will cover the entire basin and
will aim at answering the following questions:
• What are the economic impacts and combined impacts of the selected investment
plans both at basin and country scales? Are there any trade-offs or synergies?
• What is the combined impact of climate change and infrastructure development on
the NID dynamics?
• What are the trade-offs between selected environmental flow policies in the NID and
economic benefits of investment options?
This study intends to fill the knowledge gaps mentioned in the previous section by pro-
viding:
• A holistic representation of the interactions between the water, power and agricul-
tural systems in the NRB;
• A simple conceptual model of the NID dynamics to represent interactions with the
rest of the basin;
• A hydro-economic optimization approach based on a single objective economic func-
tion to model water allocation;
• An economic analysis of the infrastructure investment plans, considering trade-offs
and synergies at basin and country scales.

2.2
Optimization framework
This study uses the What-if tool and methodology [39] which have been developed by the
Department of Environmental Engineering of DTU, the international consulting group
COWI A/S and the MIT through its Joint Program on the Science and Policy of Global
Change.
What-if is an open source hydro-economic optimization model.
Its purpose
it to provide technical assistance to solve complex decision issues in the field of water
infrastructure investment planning. It is based on a holistic approach with representations
of the water, agriculture and power systems, so that the inter-dependencies within the
WEF nexus can be taken into account. The river network in What-if is described by a
node-based approach, where the modelled area is divided into catchments corresponding
to real hydrological units. Water is stored and released from reservoirs and is allocated
to water users. What-if includes modules for agricultural production, crop market, power
production and power market. Additional details about the modules used for this study
are provided in the following sections.

The optimization process of What-if relies on a single-objective function. The inputs are
the production costs, transaction costs and supply benefits of WEF commodities. The
tool maximizes the sum of all consumer and producer surpluses. As shown in Figure 2.1,
the consumer surplus is the difference between the consumers’ WTP and the market price,
and the producer surplus is the difference between the market price and the producers’
production costs [39].

The objective function to be maximized is expressed as follows:
WSB −WSC + CSB −CSC −CPC + ESB −ETC −EPC

where WSB represents the water supply benefits; WSC is the water supply costs; CSB
is the crop supply benefits; CSC is the crop supply costs; CPC is the crop production
costs; ESB is the energy supply benefit; ETC is the energy transmission costs; and EPC
is the energy production costs, which are the sum of the energy operational costs, fuel
consumption and CO2 emission costs, and the capacity expansion costs.
The outputs are mainly economic indicators (market prices, consumer and producer sur-
pluses, shadow prices), as well as water, energy, and agriculture management decisions
(supply, consumption, storage, production, transport).

The water module represents hydrological characteristics of the basin as well as water

This study considers two ecosystem spots on which environmental flow targets can be set:
the NID and the maritime delta. In the baseline scenario, no environmental flows are
applied to these two wetlands. A set of scenarios of environmental flow policies is then
developed for the NID. The NID is modelled as a linear reservoir and incorporated in the
hydrological model. This provides a more realistic representation of the river dynamics
by taking into account evaporation and storage in the NID. The modelling method of the
NID is detailed in the following section.
Additionally, a specific environmental constraint for the NID is incorporated to the model.
Instead of focusing on the outflow volume, a constraint on the flooded area is added, thus
allowing to represent flood restoration policies. Environmental constraints are defined at
a monthly time step and do not change over the years. The area constraint can be either
on the minimum or on the average area. In case of a ”minimum” constraint, the model
ensures that the flooded area at each time step is bigger than the minimum value set as
constraint. In case of an ”average” constraint, the area is averaged on all time steps and
the resulting average has to be bigger than the average constraint.

2.6
Energy production module
The energy module focuses on electricity, or ”power”. Only hydropower is represented
in this study. Hydropower production units are linked to specific catchments. They are
either linked to reservoirs or represented as run-off-the-river plants with no storage. Each
hydropower unit has a production factor (in kWh/m³) which corresponds to the amount
of energy produced by water flow through hydropower turbines. In this study, because of
lacking data, a fixed head is assumed for hydropower plants. The module uses values for
the hydropower capacity of turbines (in MW) and efficiency values. Hydropower plants
also have operational production costs.
In this study, hydropower plants and their associated turbine capacities are taken from the
ECRAI study [5]. Five hydropower plants are represented in the baseline scenario, includ-
ing four reservoirs (Selingue in Mali, Kainji and Jebba in Nigeria, Lagdo in Cameroon) and
one run-off-the-river plant (Markala in Mali). They account for a total capacity of 24,939
MW. The water-energy equivalents (production factors) and the operational production
costs are taken from IRENA (2013) [41]. Production factors range from 0.04 kWh/m³ at
Selingue to 4.76 kWh/m³ at Lagdo. The efficiency is set to a default value of 0.9 for all
hydropower plants which is the average efficiency of existing hydropower projects in the
WAPP area according to IRENA [41]. Operational production costs are assumed to be
the same for all power markets, based on an average of the values provided by IRENA.
The resulting mean value is 0.002 $/kWh.

2.8
Sensitivity analysis of flooded area in the Inner Delta
To assess the combined impacts of infrastructure development and climate change on the
NID, five scenarios of runoff modifications and five scenarios of irrigated area expansion
are developed, which is a total of 25 scenarios. Runoff is reduced by 0%, 10%, 20%, 30%
and 40% while area equipped for irrigation is multiplied by 1, 2, 4, 6 and 8. The runoff
modification range choice is based on Ghile et al. (2014) [18].
Under these 25 scenarios, the state of the NID is tested in terms of flooded area extent.
Three indicators are developed for this analysis: one wet season indicator, focusing on the
maximum annual flooded area, and two dry season indicators, focusing on the minimum
annual flooded area:
• Wet season area indicator: the maximum annual flooded area should be strictly
above 20,000 km²;
• Dry season area indicator 1: the minimum annual flooded area should be strictly
above 8,130 km²;
• Dry season area indicator 2: the minimum annual flooded area should be strictly
Hydro-economic analysis of infrastructure
investment options within the Water-Energy-Food nexus in the Niger River basin
27
==PAGE:40==
above 4,715 km².
The threshold value 8,130 km² corresponds to the mean of all the annual minimum areas
for the 23 years simulated in the baseline scenario. The threshold value 4,715 km² is the
minimum annual area of all 23 years simulated in the baseline scenario.
The threshold value 20,000 km² is based on previous findings made by Liersch et al. (2013).
They estimated that the desired wet season state condition for the NID is reached when
the maximum annual flooded area is superior or equal to 20,000 km² [29]. It is assumed
that above this threshold, the demands of the population regarding food production can
be maintained by the ecosystem.
For each of the three indicators, the number of years where the indicator desired threshold
is not fulfilled is expressed as percentage of the total number of years simulated.
2.9
Opportunity costs of restoring flooded area in the
Inner Delta
This study assesses the trade-offs between restoring flooded area in the NID and basin-
scale economic benefits. For this purpose, an environmental constraint of average flooded
area is set at 10,000 km² using the newly defined constraint option in What-if. Existing
information sources including NBA reports do not provide values for flooded area average
values so this constraint is based on observed data of flooded area from previous years.
The same 25 scenarios as defined in Section 2.8 are tested. For each scenario, the constraint
is applied and the opportunity costs of the constraint are calculated. Opportunity costs
are defined as the difference between the basin-scale total benefit without constraint and
with contraint.

Results & Discussion
This section presents and discusses the results of the hydro-economic optimization process.
The first part is a model validation performed by comparing results for the baseline scenario
to other literature findings. The second part analyses the economic impacts and combined
impacts of the selected investment plans.
The following parts focus on the combined
impacts of infrastructure development and climate change on the flooded area in the NID.
This section intends to answer the questions presented in the introduction: (1) What are
the economic impacts and combined impacts of the selected investment plans both at basin
and country scales? Are there any trade-offs or synergies? (2) What are the combined
impacts of climate change and infrastructure development on the NID dynamics? (3) What
are the trade-offs between selected environmental flow policies in the NID and economic
benefits of investment options?

According to the model outputs in the baseline scenario, the main water consumptive

and domestic only account for 1%. As shown in Table 3.1, the gross irrigation consump-

irrigation requirements estimates are highly dependent on input data, especially evap-
otranspiration and crop coefficients, on the method used to quantify crop irrigation re-

area is estimated at 860,000 ha in this study, which also matches the range provided by
JRC (550,000-900,000). Hydropower production is bigger than found in the literature.
However, the value of 6,000 GWh/year comes from the NRB profile made by GIZ which
mentions that the hydropower potential of the basin is around 30,000 GWh and that only
20% are currently exploited. This profile also provides a table of hydropower generation
for each dam but the values are contradicting most of the other existing literature. There-
fore, values of hydropower generation for each dam were disregarded. The 6,000 GWh are
only an estimate of the production based on the share of the potential that is exploited,
which makes it harder to compare with the value found in this study. Reservoir evapo-
ration values are compared with values from the section 3.2.4.2 of the NBA Action Plan
[1]. These values are higher than the ones found in this study for Selingue, Kainji and
Jebba. However, it is not clear whether the Action Plan considers gross evaporation or net
evaporation (i.e. gross evaporation minus rainfall), which could explain the differences.
Flow at the outlet of the river in the maritime delta is estimated at 180,323 Mm³/year
which is comparable to previous findings. For instance, Ogilvie et al. (2015) found a
value of 180,000 Mm³/year [34]. The NID dynamics are consistent with previous litera-
ture modelling attempts in terms of average annual flooded area and maximum annual
flooded area. However, the minimum flooded area is overestimated in this study: 3,614
km² vs. 2,203 km² in Ogilvie et al. (2015) [34].
In general, the model reproduces the trends observed in the baseline scenario for the
water, energy, and agriculture systems to some extent. Yet, it differs from other previous
work. Therefore, in the following, most of results will be expressed as a difference between
scenarios instead of absolute values.

3.4
Opportunity costs of flooded area restoration
As detailed in the Methods, the code of What-if has been modified to add the possibility
to put a constraint on the extent of the flooded area in the NID. This novelty allows
to perform trade-off analyses between selected environmental flow policies and economic
benefits. In this study, an average area of 10,000 km² is set as an environmental constraint.
Under the base, semi-dry (-10% runoff) and dry (-20% runoff) scenarios, this environmental
flow constraint is not found to generate significant losses in benefits at basin scale: less than
1% of the benefit without constraint regardless of the expansion of irrigation. However,
under the very dry scenario (-30% runoff), the constraint generates losses in benefits, called
opportunity costs in the following.

4
Conclusions & Recommendations
This study shows that connecting WEF commodities in a holistic approach and using a
single objective hydro-economic optimization approach allows to evaluate the sustainabil-
ity and economic viability of planned investments in the transboundary NRB. However,
there are some limitations to this work and potential improvements which are discussed
in the following section.
4.1
Uncertainty analysis
4.1.1
Data uncertainty
A major limitation of this study is data availability and accuracy. Most of the data inputs
come from global open source datasets which are less accurate than local datasets. For
instance, runoff is estimated based on the GRUN dataset [17] which differs from local
gauging station datasets as shown in Figure 4.1. There is a change at Douna after the
construction of the Talo dam (in 2006) and a clear overestimation at Nantaka (Mopti). At
Koulikouro and Ke-Macina, GRUN overestimates local values for some years (especially
since 2006 at Ke-Macina). GRUN values were much lower than local values at Koulikouro
before 2006. Details about the comparison of these datasets are provided in Appendix C.

Furthermore, because of lacking data, strong assumptions are taken to provide all nec-

essary inputs to the optimization model. Data is missing on area/volume relationships
for reservoirs and on hydropower plants head. In this study, a fixed head is assumed for
hydropower. Yet, head can have a high influence on hydropower generation: a higher
head means a higher water pressure across the hydro turbine and more power generated.
Local knowledge of heads for each hydropower unit (existing and planned) would be an
important information to add to the model. Moreover, the estimation of water demands
is very uncertain. Irrigation water demand is estimated using the FAO 56 method which
requires numerous assumptions about crop growth stages and calendars as well as crop
coefficients which have been assumed equal to the ones for crop in areas with similar cli-
mate. Data from local surveys conducted among agricultural stakeholders would provide a
better picture of the agricultural system. As for other irrigation demands, they have been
assumed equal to the non-consumptive use from a global gridded water use dataset even
though water use and demand may differ significantly. However, this second assumption
does not impact significantly model outputs since water use other than hydropower and
irrigation account for a very small share of basin-wide total water use.
4.1.2
Model uncertainty
In this study, the conceptual model of the NRB is set up based on some strong assumptions
that need to be discussed. First of all, even tough nodes have been placed on country
boundaries, some of the catchments are split between two countries and were linked to
one single country (the one that contained most of their area). Therefore, country-scale
impacts have to be taken carefully, as a first picture of potential impacts of infrastructure
investment plans that would need further investigations to support decision-making.
Additional model uncertainties come from the NID model. In the following, the capacity of
the model to predict flooded area in the NID is evaluated relying on the following statistical
indicators: the Normalized Root Mean Squared Error (NRMSE), the correlation coefficient
(R) and the Mean Biais Error (MBE). The Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) represents
the sum of the differences between simulated and observed values. The NRMSE is its
normalized version by the range of measured data values (maximum value minus minimum
value). The MBE is an indicator of whether the model is over or under predicting the
observed. Positive values of MBE indicate under prediction while negative values indicate
over prediction. R quantifies the correlation between measured and observed data, i.e.
how much of the measured data variance can be predicted by the model.
As shown in Table 4.1 the correlation coefficient for the volume/area relationship is R=0.62
with a normalised RMSE of 5% and a negative MBE of -1,984.3 km². Considering that
NRMSE should be strictly under 10% of the mean of observed values to guarantee a
satisfactory level of accuracy of a model, this requirement is fulfilled by the NID model
used in this study. The correlation coefficient is moderate to strong [40]. However, the
negative MBE indicates that the flooded area is over predicted in this study. Therefore,
results of the stress test need to be interpreted carefully.

The infrastructure development plans investigated in this study are based on the latest
available data and reports, yet updates would be needed from local stakeholders to get a
better picture. Notably, there is a need to locate more precisely future irrigation schemes.
For this study, the total planned expansion of irrigated area per DZ is known, and dis-
tributed among the catchments based on existing area equipped for irrigation: catchments
with a bigger area equipped for irrigation will be prioritized for irrigation expansion. This
is a strong assumption that overlooks potential expansion of irrigation in currently under-
equipped areas. As for crop choice, each crop has a maximum area per farming zone and
per culture which is set as a constraint for the optimization model. So, crop distribution is
also assumed to correspond to the current situation: a catchment that grows mostly rice
and that is supposed to increase its irrigated area will mostly add rice. This is debatable
since other decision-making processes could be just as, if not more realistic (for example,
the new crop distribution is based on yield maximization).
As for climate scenarios, there is a scientific consensus that runoff scenarios in the Sahel
are highly uncertain [6] [9] [18]. According to Ghile et al. (2014), a tropical region such
as western Africa is affected by deep uncertainties in climate change projections emerging
from multiple sources: parameterization and untested assumptions in climate models,
inherent variability of the Earth’s climate system and uncertainty of future emissions [18].
Furthermore, this study assesses opportunity costs of applying an environmental flow
constraint on the average monthly flooded area in the NID. Yet, future environmental
flow policies depend on the willingness of various stakeholders to cooperate and on socio-
political changes that are hard to predict.
4.2
Suggestions for further improvements
In this study, the flooded area is not part of the objective function, which is purely
economic. It is only modelled as a constraint. Approaches including the flooded area
in the objective of the hydro-economic optimization process should be applied in future
work. This would give a better view on the impact of dam management decisions on the
extent of the flooded area.
A number of key elements of the basin are not modelled in this study. For instance, the
model does not represent groundwater aquifers because of limitations in data availability.

Yet, groundwater has been assessed as a key resource in the basin, still under-exploited.
According to the FAO (2005), groundwater is the main source of irrigation water for most
of the small farms. It is mainly abstracted from hand-dug wells, and mainly applied by
manual watering. A minority of small farms use drilled boreholes. However, most (over
90%) of the large private or government-owned irrigation schemes use surface water [13].
The power market and crop market modules are available in What-if but are not used
in this study, which implies that crop and energy prices are estimated at the farm and
power-plant levels and do not depend on demand or transport. Hydropower being the main
connection between the power system and the water/agriculture system, the choice of not
modelling the power market can be explained by relatively low current and predicted
hydropower production values in the basin. As detailed by Payet-Burin et al. (2019),
not representing the crop market induces some limitations such as not representing crop
demand, trade, transaction costs and losses or food security constraints. Yet, this type
of analysis would be interesting in the NRB where policies seem to be prioritizing food
security. Moreover, interactions with rainfed agriculture cannot be represented and the
WTP for crops is calculated as exogenous. Non-market and market approaches can be
similar if irrigated crops are a marginal share of the total production, and if supply,
demand, and trade of crops remain stable. In the NRB, irrigated crops are a small share
of the total production. Yet this study analyses combined impacts of climate change and
irrigation development. Under climate change, rainfed production could be significantly
impacted and therefore a crop market approach would allow to get a better overview of
future challenges in a context of rising demand for food. Further work should consider a
holistic representation of the crop market, and of the power market if hydropower is to be
developed more in the basin.
Renewables could play an important role in the NRB for the years to come. IRENA’s
assessment shows that the share of renewable technologies in the WAPP region could
increase from the current 22% of electricity generation to as much as 52% in 2030, provided
that the cost of these technologies continues to fall and fossil fuel prices continue to rise
[41]. Further work should include a capacity expansion model (available in What-if).
In terms of water demand, this study does not consider future changes in water demands
other than hydropower and agriculture. The impact of this modelling choice is limited
since most of the demand is agricultural. Yet population development and demand increase
should be considered because a growing population would have an impact on the definition
of the threshold for the desired state in the NID [29].
Although this study considers interactions between WEF commodities, a number of water
resources-dependent sectors are not considered in this study, such as fishery, navigation,
livestock and tourism.
Economic outputs of this study include internal return rate for selected investments and
economic impacts of investments both at basin and country scales. Yet, a more thorough
economic analysis would be needed to support decision-making, with detailed cost/benefit
analyses of investments.

5
General conclusion
The Niger is the third longest river in Africa, exceeded only by the Nile and Congo rivers.
It faces multiple pressures driven by a growing population, socio-economic development as
well as climate change. The NBA has identified key challenges including the poor regula-
tion of the upper and middle parts of the basin, the under-exploitation of the hydropower
potential, the uneven distribution of water resources across the basin, the poor knowledge
and screening of groundwater resources, the high dependency of the NID floodplains to
upstream flow variability and the lack of environmental flows regulations.
Therefore, transboundary integrated water management is needed in the NRB to sustain
economic growth, takcle poverty and protect ecosystems in accordance with the SDGs.
This study has intended to support decision-making at basin scale by using a single ob-
jective hydro-economic optimization approach. This work has been performed with a new
open-source decision support tool for economic valuation of water infrastructure develop-
ment in the WEF nexus. This tool fills a gap in existing planning tools which are mostly
single-resource focused, or do not have an optimization framework [39].
Two infrastructure development investment plans have been considered: the Irrigation
Plan, consisting in a big expansion of irrigated area, and the Hydropower Plan, including
three multi-purpose dams (Fomi in Guinea, Taoussa in Mali and Kandadji in Niger). These
two plans correspond to priorities identified by the NBA [1]. The impacts of these plans
have been investigated both in terms of cost-efficiency and water availability. A focus
has been made on the NID floodplain in central Mali, for which a sensitivity analysis of
flooded area to combinations of climate change and infrastructure development scenarios
has been performed.
The following research questions have been investigated:
What are the economic impacts and combined impacts of the selected invest-
ment plans both at basin and country scales?
Are there any trade-offs or
synergies? The plans would benefit mainly to the agricultural sector and especially to
Nigeria. The Irrigation plan is found to generate 2,704 M$/year among which more than
90% would benefit to Nigeria. Benefits of the hydropower development are low since the
expansion of hydropower remains limited. The Hydropower Plan is found to generate
basin-scale benefits of 39 M$/year.
The trade-offs identified between both investment
plans are very low to not significant both at basin and country scales.
What are the combined impacts of climate change and infrastructure devel-
opment on the NID dynamics? According to the findings of this study, the extent
of the flooded area in the NID is more sensitive to runoff modifications than to irrigation
development. The impacts of dams on both the wet season and dry season flooded area
are very mild. Climate change, through runoff modifications, is found to be the main
driver affecting the wetland area. However, massive expansion of irrigation also affects
the wetland especially if combined with a drier climate.
What are the trade-offs between selected environmental flow policies in the
NID and economic benefits of investment options? Opportunity costs of constrain-
ing the model to maintain an average flooded area of 10,000 km² on the whole simulation
period are not significant compared to total benefits since they account for less than 1%
of it under most scenarios. However, under the ”worst case” scenario, with the driest cli-
mate, there are significant trade-offs between the fulfillment of the area requirement and

basin-scale benefits. Opportunity costs are around 50 M$/year for the current irrigated
area and reach more than 550 M$/year for a massive expansion of irrigation. Dams seem
to worsen the situation when irrigated area is increased by more than a factor 2.
Although further investigations are needed, this study is a first step in applying this new
decision-support methodology to the NRB area.
As stated in the United Nations 2020 World Water Report [43], reducing both the impacts
and drivers of climate change requires significant changes in the way we use the Earth’s
limited water resources, and improved water management offers numerous opportunities
to decision-makers for adaptation, mitigation and resilience.

C
GRUN dataset performance
This study assesses the performance of the global open dataset GRUN by comparing it
to data from local gauging stations. This assessment focuses on four stations located in
the upper part of the basin: Koulikouro, Douna, Ke-Macina and Nantaka (Mopti). As
shown in Figure C.1, a simple node-based conceptual model is set up placing a node on
each of the four stations of interest and generating the corresponding hydrological units
(catchments). By definition, for each catchment, all the runoff generated in the catchment
ends up in the river and flows into the station.
Figure C.1: Gauging stations used to compare GRUN and local datasets. Nodes are placed on each of the
gauging stations of Koulikouro, Douna, Ke-Macina and Nantaka (Mopti). Four catchments are generated out
of these nodes.
GRUN is a gridded dataset, so pixel values are summed over all the pixels of each catch-
ment and multiplied by the area of the catchment. The resulting flow value can be com-
pared to the inflow measured at the station since the model has been built to place the
station at the outlet point of the catchment.
For each station and for each month of each year between 1980 and 2014, values from
GRUN are compared with local values from the station. The correlation coefficient is
calculated as well as the ”rotation correlation coefficient which is the highest correlation
coefficient obtained when matching one time series with all the rotations (shifts) of another
time series.
The ”rotation correlation coefficient” can indicate a potential delay effect
between GRUN and local measured data.
