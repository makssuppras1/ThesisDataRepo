Abstract
3D unsteady RANS CFD simulations are used to determine how sensible are urban areas, located
in a generic city, to a variation at the inlet boundary conditions.
Firstly, a reference case is deﬁned using an inﬂow velocity with a logarithm proﬁle and then 11
diﬀerent variations of the wind ﬂow are performed. The focus of this study is how the velocity
magnitude is varying in courtyards, corridors and boulevards, previous the assessment of the wind
ﬂow quality. The deviation between the diﬀerent conﬁgurations and the reference case is used as an
answer.
The outcome is that there are important discrepancies between points belonging to the same area
of the city which can change signiﬁcantly the outcome of a comfort study.

Introduction
The world is becoming increasingly urbanized, this phenomenon is resulting into unplanned urban
sprawl with worsening air pollution and overburdened services.
According to the latest UN report of 2018, see [1], world urban population will increase by 2 billions
of people in 30 years and 83.2% will be in less developed regions [ﬁg. 3.1].
United Nations included into the Sustainable Development Goals for 2030, see [2], the Goal 11 which
aims at making cities inclusive, safe, resilient and sustainable.
Figure 3.1: Projected urban populations until 2050, see [1] p 13
To successfully address this goal, it’s important to make an investigation of micro climate in cities.
These studies can illustrate how cities will change their liveability in the next years, so they provide
a valuable answer to questions regarding, f.ex. thermal and wind comfort, air pollution and rainfall
impacts.
In this way, pedestrian and cycling activities are incentivised as means of transport, so nowadays
wind comfort and safety is an important requirement in urban areas and many city authorities
request studies of pedestrian wind comfort and safety before authorizing new constructions and
developments in their cities.
A successful setup of a wind comfort study can be summed up by the following steps:
1. Firstly, it’s necessary to determine correctly the wind proﬁle present at the site, so one has
to select the appropriate wind statistics and typically these are characterized by a Weibull
probability density function.
Usually, the investigated wind studies should be carried on many wind directions.
2. After this, it’s necessary to model the new site and its immediate surroundings. So, building
features that project on pedestrian areas for more than 0.5 m can inﬂuence wind conditions,
as well as building geometry near entrances and important pedestrian areas.
While, small landscape features should be ignored but care should be taken to pre-assess their
impact.
3. To assess the impact of the proposed construction, one has to use CFD and/or wind tunnel to
calculate speed-up, for each investigated wind direction, deﬁned as the ratio of the local wind
speed at pedestrian-level to the undisturbed reference wind speed at the same locations. The
locations used to determine speed-up ratios are critical pedestrian areas, such as balconies,
sitting areas, walkways and frequently used zones.
1
==PAGE:8==
4. Then, it’s required to combine speed-up ratios with the wind statistics and this will return the
probability of exceedance of a given wind speed. So, these are also deﬁned as comfort ratings
and they have to be compared with the comfort criteria required by the municipality.
Standards and best practice guidelines have been written to improve the quality and the assurance
of micro-climate studies. The most recent documents on these regards are:
1. COST action 732: Best practice guideline for the CFD simulation of ﬂows in the urban envi-
ronment by Frank et al [3]
2. NEN 8100 : Wind comfort and wind danger in the built environment [4]
3. Wind microclimate guidelines for developments in the city of London [5]
4. Pedestrian wind environment around buildings: literature review and practical examples by
Blocken & Carmeliet [6].
The aim of this thesis is to investigate how sensitive are pedestrian level locations, located in a
generic city, to a change in boundary conditions which are directly changing the approaching wind
proﬁle.
The case study is FORCE city, a generic neighbour made by civil and industrial buildings. Typical
building conﬁgurations can be recognized, for example there is an high rise building (number 10),
two condominium (number 9 and 8), three factories (number 1, 7, 3 and 4) and two wide boulevards
[ﬁg 3.2].
This urban design can look unusual because there are plant and dwelling areas placed close by, so
it’s important to say that this city does not exist in reality but these buildings have been taken up
from diﬀerent existent cities.
Figure 3.2: The analyzed "FORCE city" geometry
The domain for the CFD model is a computational wind tunnel. The overall meshing is a compromise
between quality of the results and simulation time.
Regarding the latter, trial simulations are
performed to test how long could a simulation takes to complete and thus to estimate how many
conﬁgurations can be examined.
The wind ﬂow proﬁle is investigated using inﬂow plane conditions and changing them between three
cases: uniform smooth, smooth proﬁle (reference case) and proﬁle turbulent. Then, the reference cell
size is modiﬁed two times compared to the reference case and, ﬁnally, it’s placed a ﬂow processing
unit and, in this way, wind ﬂow is modiﬁed in 7 diﬀerent conﬁgurations.

The assessment of the quality of the wind ﬂow is done with the monitoring of its velocity component
U along y axis and z axis and then averaged. So, u(z) and u(y) proﬁles are used as indicators of the
wind proﬁle quality .
While, the eﬀect of these diﬀerent conﬁgurations on the wind urban comfort are monitored along
diﬀerent monitor probes and the averaged magnitude velocity. These monitor points are positioned
and grouped in four diﬀerent areas: courtyards, corridors and two boulevards.
3.1
Background literature
The signiﬁcant improvements in CFD software and hardware components during last years has made
it possible to predict and assess urban comfort in design stage. But CFD may be applied with inade-
quate information on the impact of the many parameters which are inﬂuencing the result prediction.
To compensate this there have been numerous in literature on microclimate in urban areas. In order
to improve the accuracy of CFD simulation for coupled outdoor and indoor air ﬂow, Ramponi [7]
investigated the impact of CFD parameters on building cross-ventilation and Toparlar et al. [8]
conduct a study which conﬁrms that CFD can accurately predict surface temperature.
Another aspect of microclimate is wind comfort which has become an issue because the adequate
achievement of it can be problematic in most urban areas. Its evaluation is based on the eﬀect on
the wind force on humans and, in order to evaluate in real cases, many wind comfort criteria have
been written (Davenport, 1972 [9], Isyumov 1978 [10], Melbourne 1978 [11]).
Previous studies investigated discomfort conditions caused by the high wind velocities around build-
ings ([12], [13]). There is an increasing concern about the adverse pedestrian wind environment
caused by low velocity wind around buildings in dense urban area. In [14] Blocken et al. are pro-
viding a framework which integrates CFD best practice guidelines using an iterative procedure.
While in this study, Blocken et al. [15] are doing a comparison of the results obtained applying the
Duct wind nuisance standard NEN 8100 [4], which is correctly validated, with those got from a sim-
pliﬁed procedure used in earlier studies. The comparison of the two results shows that the simpliﬁed
procedure provides an overestimations by up to 25% for the highest discomfort probabilities, so this
type of large discrepancies can heavily aﬀect the outcome of wind comfort studies.
Zhan et. al[16] studied numerically the wind ﬁeld around diﬀerent building conﬁgurations. While,
Ramponi et al. [17], evaluated the eﬀect of diﬀerent urban densities on outdoor ventilation.
The eﬀect of diﬀerent building geometry on pedestrian level wind comfort has been investigated by
Du et al. [18] who are studying the eﬀects of lift-up design in four common building conﬁgurations
on the wind comfort using CFD simulations. Their results show that this design can improve the
wind comfort but its inﬂuence is highly dependent on the wind direction.
The main objective of this thesis is to investigate wind comfort in a generic city geometry imple-
menting in the numerical model the state of the art of the most recent guidelines. In COST action
2007 [3], authors are providing a set of useful guidelines for choosing the correct set of equations to
solve the model, how to size the computational domain, how to assess the boundary conditions and
how to set up correctly the numerical study.
While, NEN 8100 [4] is meant for application of statistic wind speed. So, its contribute is not ex-
tending to the numerical setup, which is mostly the core of this thesis, but they have been useful to
learn the recommended approach for wind micro-climate studies in the Netherlands and what are
the relevant matters to include in a technical report for numerical simulations.
Finally, the city of London [5] recently published its own general guideline required as part of the
planning design of new proposals and its focus is on the factors that directly aﬀect the quality and
consistency of wind micro climate studies. Its main contribute to the thesis is in the CFD require-
ments.
The literature review made by Blocken & Carmeliet [6] is focusing on more than 200 studies related
to outdoor wind environment for pedestrian. Its major contribution to this work is in the numerical

The CFD model, including the city, the inﬂow and the outﬂow volumes, is investigated under

The quality of the wind ﬂow is examined using the along wind component on the vertical and

Diﬀerent points have been monitored in the city, detecting the velocity magnitude, categorized in

and absolute deviation too see which points are more sensible to wind ﬂow variations and how much
is the over / under estimation of the velocity magnitude

4.3
Outﬂow
The Outﬂow boundary condition is used to model the ﬂow where the ﬂow is exiting the computational
domain so the velocity (and other variables of interest) is not known priorly.
It is declared as a pressure outlet with a constant value of 0 Pa. As explained into COST [3], this
boundary condition is setting at zero the derivatives of ﬂow variables and it creates a fully developed
ﬂow, which has a velocity proﬁle (and/or proﬁles of other properties such as temperature) that is
unchanging in the ﬂow direction. But it is important to highlight that gradients in the cross-stream
direction can exist at the outﬂow boundary, so it’s only the diﬀusion ﬂuxes in the perpendicular
direction to the exit plane which are assumed to be zero.
4.4
Top, lateral and walls
These boundary conditions are used to link solid and ﬂuid regions, thus in viscous ﬂows moving
close to walls the no-slip condition is considered the default option. This indicates that the ﬂuid, in
this case air, is sticking to the wall and it moves with the wall velocity, which is zero m/s in this
case.
6
==PAGE:13==
This is also found in the recommendations of COST [3], so building walls have been all declared as
smooth with no-slip conditions.
Since the results should have been compared with wind tunnel measurement of a closed test section,
see 5.1, then top and lateral boundaries are treated as a solid and stationary walls.
5
Numerical setup
The dimensions of the computational domain in the ﬂow, lateral and vertical directions are related
to the area of interest and to the boundary conditions that are used. So, it’s important to place
the domain boundaries to a suﬃcient distance from the analysed region to avoid a contaminated
solution.

5.2
Numerical wind tunnel
The numerical wind tunnel dimensions are derived from the CWT dimension and then scaled up
using the scale factor.
To assess their precision and correctness, they are compared with the recommendations in the
analyzed guidelines and in chapter "5.5: Choice of computational domain" in COST action [3],
where the authors are giving an overall suggestion based on the state of the art:
1. Vertical and lateral extensions: it’s recommend to size it as the cross section of the wind
tunnel, if the height is much larger than 6Hmax (vertical) and 5Hmax (lateral), where Hmax
is deﬁned as the height of the tallest building, then it’s possible to use a lower value.
2. Inﬂow extension: there are two diﬀerent suggestions. At p. 18, it’s recommend to have a
distance of 8H when there is a blockage ratio at 10%, while at p.47, in a practice case made
by the University of Siegen using MISKAM, it’s stated that is enough to have 5Hmax for the
inﬂow when only one wind ﬂow direction is investigated.
3. Outﬂow extension: for urban areas, it can be used a smaller distance than 15H. In the appendix
B.3, it’s suggested to use a distance of 10H when one wind ﬂow direction is investigated.
As said before, given a scale of 1 : 200 cm, the numerical wind tunnel dimensions are written into
5.2. The tallest building in the geometry is n.10, see ﬁgure 3.2, so its height is Hmax and it’s 50 m
(in the CFD model).
Since the physical wind tunnel dimensions, once the scale factor is applied, are complying with the
previously stated COST requirements for this numerical wind tunnel, then the vertical and lateral
extension are both declared as 400m.
While, the inﬂow length used for the reference case, mesh and inﬂow proﬁle conﬁgurations is 400 m,
see chapter 5.6, and for all the other conﬁgurations is 800m.
For the outﬂow length, it’s always long 600 m and this is due to don’t have a reversed ﬂow conditions
during the solving processes.

It’s possible to represent the atmospheric boundary layer using the wind tunnel boundary layer.
There are diﬀerent methods to achieve this, in ﬁgure 5.3 some of them are shown.

roughness elements.

technology. As said before, this has been done because, in a ﬁrst moment, it was planned to do a
validation test in the wind tunnel.

are placed at the inﬂow also their height is responsible for the height of the boundary layer that one
wants to create. This one have a trapezoidal shape, with a base length of 360 m and a top length of

5.4
Computational grid
The meshing designates the cells (or elements) where the ﬂow is solved, so it’s a discrete represen-
tation of the problem geometry.
It’s crucial that the computational grid is designed in a way that doesn’t introduce large errors, so
it’s ﬁne enough to describes turbulence and to reduce truncation errors. Generating a poor quality
grid can lead to highly inaccurate simulation results or there can even be a lack of convergence, on
11

the other hand the mesh density can’t be too high given the computational resources.
COST [3] is giving a large set of recommendations in chapter 5.8 "Choice of the computational grid".
It follows a summarized list of the main points highlighted in the guide and how these requirements
are met in the model:
1. "Grid stretching has to be be small in regions with high gradients, so the expansion ratio is
kept under 1.3."
To satisfy this requirement, the speed of cell transition is slow at the core, while close to
surfaces the transition has a medium velocity.
2. "Hexahedral cells are preferred over tetrahedral", see table 5.3. Hexahedral make up the 84%
of the total cell types, while tetrahedral are totally absent.
3. "Grid lines are parallel to the walls", see ﬁgure F.1 for the ceiling, ﬁgure F.2 for the ﬂoor,
ﬁgure F.4 for the inlet and ﬁgure F.3 for the outlet. In all cases, the lines are parallel to the
walls and thus they are satisfying this requirement.
Looking at the cell types for "1 Conﬁguration", see 5.6, the majority of the cells are made up of
hexahedral, then there is a small percentage made of wedges and polyhedral, while tetrahedral cells

5.5
Model choices for ﬂow
The choice of the equations has the largest inﬂuence on modelling errors and their uncertainties.
Firstly, one has to decide if the study requires a steady or unsteady treatment and which turbulence
model is more appropriate for its case.
12
==PAGE:19==
In the guideline COST [3], there is chapter 5.3 "Choice of approximate equations describing the
physics of the ﬂow" which is explaining how to choose the correct set of equations to describe the
physics.
Hereafter a summary of the most important suggestions for this study:
1. Unsteady state is required to solve the turbulent atmospheric boundary layer. Also, since this
study is focusing on an industrial and urban environment, it’s necessary to use Navier-Stokes
equations.
2. Temperature and humidity equations need to be considered if relevant for the study, which is
not the case.
3. Unsteady RANS with standard k-epsilon model. These models are a good compromise between
robustness, computational cost and accuracy and they are generally suited to industrial-type
applications that contain complex recirculation ﬂows, which is the object of this thesis.
Regarding the latter, City of London [5], which is a more recent guideline, is recommending to use
the realisable k-epsilon model since nowadays is a robust industry standard.
Having said that, the principal model choices applied in the CFD model are:
1. Three dimensional ﬂow, to capture the variations in the three directions
2. First 5000 iterations with steady RANS and then 10000 with Unsteady RANS. This helps to
approach the converged solution and to reduce the computational time.
3. Realisable k - epsilon turbulence, which is better than the Standard K-Epsilon model for many
applications, and can generally be relied upon to give answers that are at least as accurate.
4. Two-layer all y+ treatment, which is used due to the ﬁne meshes present close to the buildings
and it’s able to resolve the viscous sub layer.
5.5.1
Steady state
Once the initial guess is decided, the ﬂow variables are calculated at each iteration until the equa-
tions are satisfying a user-speciﬁed error.
To achieve faster a convergence for the results, the equations have been solved before with steady
state for 5000 iterations so that the all residuals are dropping and then stabilizing, which can be
clearly seen into 5.6.
In section 5.11 of COST [3] "Choice of iterative convergence criteria", it’s suggested to have a re-
duction of at least 0.0001 in residual’s order of magnitude and this condition is well satisﬁed by all
of the plotted quantities.
As is stated into section 5.7 "Choice of initial data" of COST [3], this part is used as an initialization
so that it’s taking the solution closer to the ﬁnal results to both reduce the computational eﬀorts
and the inﬂuence of initial data which is more relevant with a large domain or a low wind speed.

Time step size
The transient phase is time dependent and the time-step is chosen as a trade oﬀbetween the
computational time and Courant number ≃1 in the area of interest, deﬁned as ∆t = CFL ·
∆xmin/Umax.
Given that this estimate is conservative, the ﬁrst trial has been with time-step= 0.5, ﬁgure 5.7, then
a reduction to 0.25 sec, ﬁgure E.2, and then an increase to 1 sec, ﬁgure E.1.
For 0.25 sec, there are only the areas around the spires and the ﬁrst blocks which have a CFL at
2 and all the rest of the wind tunnel as a CFL of 1. So this can be considered a reference case
regarding the CFL number but the computing time is around double compared to 0.5 sec.
Looking at ﬁgure 5.7, there is a larger number of areas which have a CFL around 2 in the city, while
in the ﬂow processing unit the area with CFL of 2 in ﬁgure E.2 has expanded with parts going up
to 4, there is also a small stripe with CFL=5. Overall, the area of interest has CFL = 1.
Finally, the case of time step=1, see ﬁgure E.1, has too many areas with CFL around 3, now there
appearing also after the city.
So, the best trade oﬀfor both the computational time and the accuracy of the simulation is given
by the time step of 0.5 sec.

Iterative convergence criteria
The steady state conditions, chapter 5.5.1, are used as an initial guess so that the equations are
solved up to lower the error and this is represented by the residuals, ﬁgure 5.6. During the transient
phase, they are ﬁrstly slightly increasing and then they stabilize around an acceptable order of
magnitude.
Additionally to the residuals, COST [3] suggests to record the target variables and see if they are
constant or if they are moving around a constant value to determine if the convergence has been
achieved.
To this extent, in ﬁgure E.3 is shown an example of the whole velocity trend of sample points for
the transient state. It can be seen that during the ﬁrst 100-150 iterations, no turbulence is recorded
while after that all the points are oscillating.
As a rule of thumb, the time used by a turbulence to travel from the entrance to the end of the
domain can be determined with a relation between the domain size and the wind speed. Indeed,
during the initial period, the results are highly dependent on the initial data and they don’t have to
be taken into account for the ﬁnal solution.
Given the CFD wind tunnel length of 1800 m and an input velocity of 10 m/s, a turbulence takes
around 180 sec to travel from the inﬂow to the outﬂow of the domain. For this reason, the recorded
velocities are kept in the interval from 800 sec to 1000 sec, as shown into 5.8.

Results
To assess the impact of diﬀerent inlet boundary conditions on the pedestrian wind ﬂow, the veloc-
ity magnitude, for each conﬁguration, is monitored in diﬀerent areas (boulevards, courtyards and
corridors) and then compared with the reference case analyzing the relative and absolute deviation.
It’s helpful to analyse both deviations because there are cases where the reference velocity is low,
f.ex. under 1 m/s, so the relative deviation will spike up and the absolute deviation will be a better
reference.
The following graphs are showing the relative / absolute deviation and the velocity magnitude per
each monitored location. The reference case velocity is plotted with a straight black line so in the
following graphs the Y axis has the correct scale also for this velocity. Additionally, conﬁgurations
are plotted using the ID assigned to them in table 5.4 and the color code for each group is showed
in table 8.2.

Conclusion
The target of this thesis is to evaluate how sensible are diﬀerent urban areas at pedestrian level, like
courtyards, corridors and boulevards, to a change at the inlet boundary conditions.
This question is answered using a CFD model which is built using the recommendations of the latest
guidelines on this matter (COST action 732[3], NEN 8100 [4] and Wind microclimate guidelines from
City of London [5]). 3D unsteady RANS simulations with realizable k-epsilon are used to solve the
ﬂow.
This model includes a ﬁctional, but realistic, city geometry and its wind ﬂow is monitored in diﬀerent
areas. The incoming wind ﬂow is varied in 11 diﬀerent conﬁgurations: two variations for the reference
mesh cell size, two inﬂow plane variations and seven diﬀerent ﬂow processing units. Their wind ﬂow
quality is assessed monitoring the horizontal and vertical velocity distribution along the domain.
While, the monitor areas are compared with the reference case in terms of relative and absolute
deviation.
The following conclusion can be made:
• The two boulevards, present in the city, have really diﬀerent results. One of them is a straight
street without any interference from the surrounding, while the other one has many more
buildings which are obstructing the wind ﬂow.
• Re circulation areas have the highest deviations but this is heavily aﬀected by the surrounding
