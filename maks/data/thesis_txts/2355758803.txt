Abstract
Abstract
Abstract
Abstract 
The main purpose of this thesis is to study the integration of bearing sleeves in 
components made from fibrous composites, develop a toolbox of calculation and 
experimental methods for aiding the design of such components. The interest in 
the application of fibrous composites, is in the project, derived from the maritime 
industry. The fibrous composites are utilized as structural components because of 
their high stiffness-to-weight and strength-to-weight ratio. The analysis of the 
problem is based on a component from a maritime satellite antenna produced by 
Cobham Satcom.   
The relevant literature is reviewed in this thesis, to provide the needed back-
ground knowledge. From the literature review it is found that none of the existing 
test standards and fixtures are suitable for the desired bearing sleeve tests. The 
problem is simplified in to radial and axial bearing loading and therefore two test 
fixtures with related test specimens are developed. With the fixture for the radial 
loading both tension and compression is possible.  
Initial testing of different bearing sleeve incorporation methods is carried out. 
Bearing sleeves bounded by glue to the composite is compared to non-glued bear-
ing sleeves with a flange geometry. The non-glued bearing sleeves was found to be 
the best solution in both radial and axial bearing sleeve tests.  
Three different Finite Element models of the radial bearing sleeve tests are de-
veloped in ANSYS Mechanical APDL. The three models provide the base for in-
vestigation of the significance of different assumptions and simplifications. The 
in-plane material parameters for the FE models is found by material tests. Ten-
sion, compression and shear material characterization tests are carried out and 
clear results are obtained.  
Further tests of the radial loaded non-glued specimens, for both tension and 
compression loading, are carried out for three different bearing hole sizes, to test 
the robustness of the FE models. A DIC system is used to measure the strains in 
the test specimens. Comparisons between the radial bearing sleeve test strains 
and the FE models shows good resemblance in the linear-elastic region. The first 
failure of the laminate is predicted well by the use of Max. stress and Max. strain 
failure criterions.  
The developed calculation tools are useful in aiding the design of radial loaded 
bearing sleeves. Through the performed tests, both axial and radial bearing load-
ings are studied, and failure modes are analyzed, as well as the parameters central 
to designing bearing sleeve inserts are discussed.

1 Introduction
Introduction
Introduction
Introduction 
The application of fibrous composite materials has in the recent decades spread to 
many industries where high strength-to-weight and stiffness-to-weight ratio is desired. 
The widening of application area is due to development in the production technology of 
fiber-reinforced resin-matrix composite materials. Originally the use of fibrous composites 
materials were limited to the aerospace industry where weight optimization was the driv-
ing factor for implementing composites materials. This project originates from the mari-
time communication industry, were weight optimization also is the driving incentive for 
implementing of fibrous composites. The project is centered on the challenges of incorpo-
rating bearing sleeves in fibrous composites.

1.1 Background and relevance to industry
Background and relevance to industry
Background and relevance to industry
Background and relevance to industry 
This project is a collaboration with Cobham Satcom, in studying the strength of bear-
ing sleeves in carbon fiber reinforced polymers (CFRP). Cobham Satcom develops mari-
time satellite antennas with load carrying components produced in CFRP. The need for 
embedding bearings in the maritime antennas, arises from construction of the servo stabi-
lized antennas. The Sailor 600 is a 3-axis stabilized antenna and is designed with CFRP 
parts as load carrying components. The CFRP parts are attached to an aluminum bear-
ing-house by glue connections. The Sailor 600 antenna and x-elevation arm are shown in 
Figure 1.1 and Figure 1.2. The x-elevation arm is one of the parts where embedding bear-
ings directly in the CFRP structure is desired by Cobham Satcom. This could eliminate 
the aluminum bearing houses, and consequently reduce the weight and the number of 
manufactured parts. Cobham Satcom seeks to replace the bearing houses with bearing 
sleeves inserted directly in to the CFRP part. Bearing sleeve is the name used, in this 
thesis, for a part connecting the CFRP to the bearing. In order to design such parts, cal-
culation tools for strength prediction of embedding bearings in CFRP must be developed. 
This thesis focuses on the strength of the CFRP in close proximity to the inserted bear-
ings sleeves.

2 Problem definition
Problem definition
Problem definition
Problem definition 
This project is based on developing a toolbox of calculation and experimentation meth-
ods for aiding the design of CFRP parts with integrated bearing sleeves. The calculation 
tools will be based on the finite element method and the theory of lamina failure in com-
posites. The experimental work will be used to validate the accuracy of the calculation 
tools and to investigate the essential parameters of the failure mechanism.  
 
The following subjects are relevant to investigate in the process of developing a 
toolbox for bearing inserts in CFRP:  
• 
Developing relevant test methods of the practical problem. 
• 
Exploring and testing different bearing sleeve insert techniques. 
• 
Identifying predominant failure modes and investigating the mechanism by 
utilizing the calculation tools.  
• 
Quantifying strength and stiffness of the bearing connections.  
• 
Investigating the effects of change in bearing sizes.

3 Thesis outline
Thesis outline
Thesis outline
Thesis outline 
The chosen approach for this thesis is centered on developing tools and methods that 
are directly applicable to the industry. In the following, the chosen outline for this pro-
ject will be discussed.   
This project is delimited to study the strength of CFRP laminates loaded through in-
serted bearing sleeves in the axial and radial direction. The problem will be simplified 
and broken down in order to design test specimens and develop mathematical models. In-
itial tests of glued connections between bearing sleeves and the CFRP, are compared to 
non-glued fastening methods. These initial tests are purely empirical, and are carried out 
to get an understanding of what parameters are of most importance, and how the differ-
ent bearing mountings and loading situations behaves in practice.   
It is chosen to further focus the study around testing and modeling the radial bearing 
loading in the CPRF laminate. The effects on strength of different bearing sizes are in-
vestigated. The failure of the test specimens will be compared to the failure criteria ob-
tainable by the developed mathematical models. The results of the mathematical models 
are compared to the results from the experiments with the use of Digital Image Correla-
tion (DIC) technique, by strain values and strain distributions. Any discrepancies be-
tween the mathematical models and the experimental results will be analyzed and de-
scribed in order to understand the limitations of the models.  
Failure criteria are calculated for the FE models in order to validate the accuracy of 
the failure predictions found in the tests.

4.1.1 Introduction to composite materials
Introduction to composite materials
Introduction to composite materials
Introduction to composite materials 
A composite material denotes a combination of two or more materials that forms a 
beneficial third material [1]. The advantage of combining materials is that a composite 
typically exhibits the best properties of each component it consists of. Properties e.g. 
strength, stiffness, fatigue life, corrosion resistance etc. can be improved by combining 
materials. Hence, composite materials can be designed to have the needed properties to 
perform a specific design task.  
Normally, composites can be characterized into four different types; ‘Fibrous compo-
sites (short fibers, long fibers and whiskers)’, ‘Laminated composites’, ‘Particulate com-
posites’ or a combination of the first three types [1], see Figure 4.1. A combination of a 
long fibrous composite and a laminated composite is addressed in this section due to the 
desires and interests from Cobham Satcom. The type of composite is referenced as CFRP 
(Carbon fiber reinforced polymer) laminate.

A long fibrous composite consist of fibers and a matrix [1]. A fiber is characterized by 
its great length-to-diameter-ratio and its near-crystal-sized diameter. Lengthways, a long 
fiber is much more stiff and strong in comparison to the same material in bulk form due 
to a more perfect structure. The crystals in fibers are aligned lengthwise with the fiber 
axis and have smaller amounts of internal defects compared to the bulk form. Generally, 
fibers shows a linear elastic behavior [1]. Carbon fiber can be interwoven into fabric/cloth 
due to its easy bending properties [1]. The fibers can be interwoven in different woven 
patterns (e.g. 2/2 twill weave and plain weave) [3]. Carbon fibers have a limited usage 
without a binding material that bonds the fibers together and fixates the fibers in a 
==PAGE:28==
Page | 8  
 
structural shape designed to carry loads [1]. The binding material in composites is called 
a matrix. The matrix has multiple purposes including; fiber supporting, fiber protection, 
stress transfer between fibers etc. Figure 4.2 shows how loads are transferred from a bro-
ken fiber to the matrix and afterwards to the adjacent fibers and the other segment of 
the broken fiber. The load transfer mechanism progresses by the shearing stress in the 
matrix which repels the fiber from being pulled out of the laminate [1]. 
 
Figure 4.2 Fiber failure in relation to matrix and fiber stress in unidirectional tension [1] 
Normally, the matrix has a lower stiffness, strength and density compared to the fiber 
material. Despite this, the combination of fibers and matrix can have great strength, 
stiffness and low density [1]. Different materials such as polymers, metals or ceramics can 
be used as a matrix material in a composite. In this report a cross-linked polymer epoxy 
resin is used as matrix. Epoxy is a thermoset polymer, that when chemically irreversible 
set/cured, forms cross-links between molecules in three-dimensional interconnected chains 
[1]. The formed internal structure provides great stiffness and strength properties com-
pared to other polymer resins.  
Carbon fiber reinforced polymer laminates consist of fibrous composite materials that 
are laminated. The hybrid composite is composed of carbon fiber plies/layers bonded to-
gether, usually with the fiber direction of each ply oriented in different directions. Hence, 
the stiffness and strength of the laminate can be varied and controlled in numerous direc-
tions in relation to the specific design task [1] see Figure 4.3. The same matrix/resin used 
to bond the fibers together in each ply are typically also used to bond the plies together 
in a laminate [1].

Winding, laying and molding are three different process methods used to layup CFRP 
composite laminates. Laying includes tape laying and cloth laying. The layup process 
method is dependent upon e.g. size, geometry, expertise etc. [1]. Cobham Satcom uses the 
cloth laying method for some of their present laminate composite parts and desires this 
method to be used and analyzed in this project. Therefore, this section will only address 
the cloth laying method due to application purposes.  
The specific cloth laying method used is based upon using pre-impregnated (pre-preg) 
carbon fiber interwoven cloth. The pre-preg consists of an interwoven carbon fiber ply 
imbedded in an epoxy matrix resin [4]. The pre-preg is oriented and laid in plies in the 
desired shape obtained by using e.g a mould, a mandrel, plates etc.  
The orientation of a CFRP laminate is specified by a standard laminate code [5]. The 
laminate code specifies the orientation in degrees of each ply in relation to a general geo-
metric x-axis of the laminate. E.g. a six ply symmetric laminate can be specified as 
[+45°, 0°, -30°, -30°, 0°, +45°] or [45/0/-30]S.

Different methods can be used to cure laminates based on pre-preg with epoxy includ-

pre-preg interwoven cloth solidifies. This process forms a permanent bonding between the 
resin and the fibers in all plies [1].

In relation to application purposes, predictions of load-carrying capabilities have high 
importance. As of present time, no stand-alone failure criterion exists that has the ability 
to predict failure in all combinations of composites and loading scenarios. Three common

In this project the calculations of the fiber laminate test specimens is done with the 
commercial FEM code: ANSYS Mechanical APDL 17.0. Different approaches can be 
taken when modeling laminates in ANSYS depending on the desired level of accuracy and 
computational cost of solving. ANSYS has specialized elements simulating laminated ma-
terials and they are generally referred to as layered structural elements. Layered struc-
tural elements are able to have different sections with different materials and orientations 
through the thickness of an element. Two of ANSYS’s layered structural elements is cho-
sen to be studied because they each inflicting different assumptions to the resulting 
mathematical model. The shell and solid layered structural elements will be described in 
the following.  
4.2.1
4.2.1
4.2.1
4.2.1 Shell elements 
Shell elements 
Shell elements 
Shell elements  
In ANSYS Mechanical APDL 17.0 two different shell elements are suited for modeling 
composites, SHELL181 and SHELL281 [7]. SHELL181 is a 4 node element with nodes in 
each corner of the quadrilateral element. Each node has six degrees of freedom, three 
transitional and three rotational. SHELL281 is an 8 node element with notes in each cor-
ner and on the midpoints of each line of the quadrilateral element also with six degrees of 
freedom at each node. For both elements the shell can be defined with layered properties 
by section commands. Individual layer thickness, orthotropic material properties and ma-
terial orientation can be controlled for the shell elements. The shell elements are built on 
Mindlin-Reissner shell theory, which assumes a linear distributed deformation through 
the thickness of the element. Further are through-thickness stress of the element ne-
glected i.e. plane stress condition is assumed. The layered shell element are suited for 
modeling thin to moderately thick shell structures.

In this section the relevant information about bearings and their load distribution, in 
relation to application in composite materials, is reviewed. 
Different types of bearings exist, common for all, they restrict unwanted movement of 
moving parts so only controlled movement remains. Among the most common types are 
rolling element bearings where several different types are available. For example ball 
bearings, roller bearings and needle roller bearings, each with their own strengths. Rolling 
element bearings are, in metal constructions, often fitted in the housing by close-fitting 
fits between the outer race and the housing and the inner race and the shaft [8]. The high 
accuracy in the mounting of the bearing is done in order to achieve the best bearing per-
formance and lifetime. By accurate machining of the mating parts, misalignment of the 
bearings and deformation of the inner and outer race is reduced to a minimum. The need 
for accurate mounting, drives the desire for imbedding bearing sleeves in the CFRP 
parts.

In order to make the production of test specimens and test fixtures possible within rea-

uni-axial test fixtures and test specimens, in order to reduce the complexity of the test

The test specimens for the radial and axial tests are simplified from the complex and 
curved geometry of the application (x-elevation arm) to a flat plate with a hole for the 
bearing sleeve. This simplification builds on the assumption that stress in the CFRP in 
close proximity to the bearing will be less dependent of the curvature of the part and 
largely controlled by the contact between the CFRP and the bearing sleeve. The plate 
simplification is necessary, to make the production of the specimens feasible. The holes 
for the bearing sleeves are machined in the plates after they are cured, as opposed to co-
curing the bearing sleeves in the lamination process. The machining of the bearing holes 
is desired by Cobham Satcom as it is the simplest to implement.  
The project focuses on the stresses and strains in the CFRP, therefore the resulting 
stress and strains located in the bearing elements are not investigated. The axel, bearing 
and bearing sleeve are simplified to a solid axel for the radial non-glued test, in order to 
reduce complexity. The simplification is depicted in Figure 5.1. The simplification builds 
upon the assumption that the close fitting concentric steel rings of the axel, inner race, 
balls, outer race and bearing-sleeves stiffness’s are proximate to the stiffness of one solid

The radial test fixture consists of a foundation plate with a base plate mounted per-
pendicular to the surface with four M12 screws, see Figure 6.2 and Figure 6.3. The foun-
dation plate is developed to be mounted on the T-slot table with eight M10 screws on the 
MTS 90kN test machine. The base plate has a fit hole located at the center. The hole 
makes it possible to insert three different pins with diameters: 15 mm, 20 mm and 25 
mm. The pins represent the simplified bearing sleeve, bearing and axel for the non-glued 
specimens. The reason for the choice of the three specific pin diameters is due to realistic 
application sizes, handling and specimen sizes. Pin adapters are used to utilize different 
pins with different diameters in the same fit hole located on the base plate. A M8 screw 
is located at the top of the base plate which is able to lock the loading pins in place if 
necessary. Three different plate test specimens exist to accommodate for the three differ-
ent pin diameters. The specimens contains a fit hole that makes it possible to use the 
pins to load the specimens. The specimens can be loaded by the grips of the MTS test 
machine at the top surface. At the top of the specimen, specimen holders are located. 
The holders secures that the clamping force from the test machine’s 80 mm wide grips 
are distributed to the whole width of the specimen due to the high thickness of the hold-
ers. The specimen holders are also machined to have a rough surface that secures a high 
friction coefficient between the grips and the specimen. The specimen holders and the 
specimen are clamped together by the grips on the test machine. When the specimen is 
loaded in either tension or compression, the pin mounted in the base plate loads the spec-
imen in-plane and simulates a pure radial loading condition. Both the non-glued and 
glued test specimens can be used with the fixture. The machining tolerances for the pins, 
pin adapters, and pin holes in the specimens are ISO clearance fits that minimizes the 
clearance between the components to a certain extent and still makes assembling and dis-
assembling possible. The clearance fits are assumed to simulate the fit between a bearing 
sleeve and a CFRP component. The fits between pins and specimens have a great influ-
ence of the loading scenario according to Section 4.3, even though it is assumed that due 
to the softness of the specimens and great hardness of the pins, the use of ISO clearance 
fits are a good approximation of a loaded bearing sleeve.

center of the pin, in order to reduce computational time. The pin-base plate and the 
foundation-base plate connection is a bounded contact to reduce the complexity of the 
simulation. Boundary conditions for the model are defined as: 
• 
Displacement of the screw holes in the foundation plate which connects the 
plate to the T-slot table of the testing machine are fixed in all d.o.f.  
• 
Only compressive forces between the bottom of the foundation plate and the 
T-slot table of the test machine exists. 
• 
Loading of the model is done through the pin with a distributed pressure, by 
the bearing load functionality.

SYS Workbench. Convergence studies are shown in Appendix 15.7.

The purpose of the initial tests, is to investigate the differences between glued and 
non-glued bearing-sleeves incorporated in CFRP. The non-glued solution is chosen to be 
a bearing sleeve with a flange geometry. The flanged sleeves are designed with a tight fit 
between the hole in the CFRP and the sleeve, in order to seat the sleeve properly. The 
two solution’s load carrying capacities are investigated in the bearings radial and axial 
directions. From the tests, it is desired to find the failure modes for the different fas-
tening solutions in the two load scenarios. The failure modes and the ultimate load carry-
ing capabilities are found and used to expand the understanding on how practically im-
plementation of bearing sleeves in CFRP components can be carried out.

One of the main objects addressed in this project is to develop FE models that are ca-
pable of simulating a CFRP plate loaded from a bearing sleeve insert in the radial direc-
tion and thereby be able to predict failure in the plate. To validate the FE models, com-
parable tests are conducted. To test the scalability and robustness of the FE models, the 
comparable test are conducted for three different sleeve insert sizes (Ø15, Ø20 and Ø25 
mm) in both tension and compression loading. The non-glued specimen type is used 
throughout these tests. The Aramis DIC system is used to measure the strain distribu-
tion in the specimens and can therefore be directly compared with the results from the 
FE models.

Test procedure 
The test procedure of the following tests are closely related the ones used for the initial 
radial tests. The dissimilarity includes the introduction to the 12M Aramis DIC system, 
change of pin sizes on the fixture and added tension loading. The specimens’ preparation 
and insertion are conducted according to Section 8.1.1. The following test procedure is 
identical for all three bearing sizes: 
• 
The test fixture is mounted in the test machine without a pin inserted. 
• 
The desired specimen is pushed down in the fixture opening. 
• 
The compatible pin is then inserted in the fixture and further into the fit hole 
in the specimen. 
• 
The specimen is then grabbed by the grips of the test machine. 
• 
The Aramis DIC system is configured to measure the surface of the specimen 
around the pin hole. 
• 
The desired loading direction is chosen to either tension or compression load-
ing. 
• 
While the test is conducted, the Aramis system measures the movements of the 
specimens, and the applied force and displacement are logged from the test ma-
chine. 
• 
After a test has been conducted, the post-processing of the data recorded by 
the Aramis system can begin where the strain distribution can be established. 
Also, the data from the load cell and measurement rod can be used to obtain 
stress-strain curves and force-displacement curves.

The shell model uses the Shell181 element and is the simplest layered structural ele-
ment available. The model is modeled as a simple open-hole laminate with an applied 
load in the hole. The model is fully constrained/clamped on the top edge of the specimen 
which simulates the constrains from the clamps of the test machine. It is not possible to 
load the specimen with a sinusoidal distributed load due to non-variable load limitations 
in ANSYS. Instead, an linear load distribution is used as bearing loading which is rela-
tively similar to a sinusoidal distribution, illustrated in Figure 10.2. The load can either 
be applied normal to the top half circumference (compression) or at the bottom half cir-
cumference (tension) in the hole. The load increases linear from zero to maximum mir-
rored around the center of the hole as see in the figure. The integral in relation to y of 
the linear distributed load is calculated and is equal to the load determine from the tests. 
All three FE models are able to change hole size.

The Solid w. contact FE model is a further development of the previous model with

steel pin and the lubricating properties of carbon. The model is loaded on the top surface

The failure mechanism
The failure mechanism
The failure mechanism 
By using the Max. stress and Max. strain criterion for all three FE models, the results 
show that the solid FE models agree and state that for all load cases and hole sizes, both 
the offset shear strength and the limit shear stress are exceeded as first failure parameter. 
This indicates that the initial failures occur due to shearing in the loaded half of the hole. 
The shell FE model disagrees and states that the failures occur when the offset compres-
sion strain and the limit compression stress in the loading direction is reached. Due to 
the limitations of the shell elements, the solid elements are believed to be more suitable 
for this problem. The failure criteria can only be used to describe the failure mechanism 
in the linear loaded region due to the restriction in ANSYS. Therefore the ultimate fail-
ure mechanism can only be based on failure tendencies determined from the material 
characterization. In Figure 10.37 a radial force-displacement curve is compared with a 
material compression test and a material shear test. It is observed from the comparison 
that after the force-displacement curve starts to deviate from the linear region, the curvy 
failure shape shows comparability with the shear test, which complies with the failure cri-
teria described previously for the solid models. The shear test has a large plastic defor-
mation region from initial failure to ultimate failure which is assumed to explain the de-
viation from linearity for the radial test. The compression test shows no initial failure be-
fore an abrupt ultimate failure occurs. After the radial test reaches initial shear the defor-
mation becomes larger and at some point the compression limit is reached and the test 
suddenly fails as seen for the compression test. This understanding of the ultimate failure 
mechanism can be characterized as an estimate based on tendencies, as it is not possible 
to validate it by analyzing the failed specimens due to the small failure scale and the DIC 
results’ restricted measurements close to the hole where failure occurs.

11 Discussion
Discussion
Discussion
Discussion 
The radial bearing tests showed loading curves with a linear region followed by a curvy 
region and then a sudden failure. The different regions indicate different failure mecha-
nisms in the laminate. In the linear region no failure is assumed. The curvy region indi-
cates internal failure which has been related to a shear failure and is supported by the 
Max. stress and Max. strain criterion. At the ultimate failure the load drops suddenly. 
The sudden drop indicates a compression failure due to the high compression values lo-
cated in this area. The ultimate failure mechanism is only based on tendencies and has to 
be investigated further before a conclusion can be determined. Due to limitations of AN-
SYS, only the linear behavior can be evaluated. Therefore, to investigate and evaluate 
the ultimate failure mechanism, failure values and strain distributions, the FE models 
must be further developed to include non-linear behavior. Based on the hole size com-
pared to the load values it was not found possible to conclude a clear relation due to the 
limited test spectrum of three hole sizes, even though, the results show an increase of 
load capacity when the hole size increases. The comparison also shows that the radial 
tension test has slightly greater values for larger hole sizes compared to the compression 
tests. This indicates that the laminates are more suited to be loaded in tension loading. 
By testing more hole sizes, more representable relations can be determined.  
The DIC showed good results with some limitations. The DIC results presented errors 
around the hole and at edges. The errors arise due to the missing facets located at the 
edges. A solution to conquer this problem is to move the DIC system closer to the test 
fixture. By moving the system closer, the pixel density of the specimen is enhanced which 
could result in smaller sized facets in cooperation with a smaller sized dotted stochastic 
pattern. By reducing the facet-size the error measurement located at the contact between 
the laminate and the steel pin could be diminished. Even though some errors will always 
exist due to the DIC system’s mode of function. Unfortunately it was not possible to 
move the DIC system closer to the test fixture due to conflict between the size of the test 
machine and the DIC setup. Modifications of the DIC system nor the test machine were 
not a possibility for this project. The FE models’ strain distributions were compared to 
the DIC results which showed great comparability. The FE model results have further-
more most values located between ±1 standard deviation from the DIC results. This indi-
cates that the FE models are capable of capturing the tendency of the actual loading sce-
narios. The Shell and Solid FE models show great results for the smallest and medium 
hole size, but as the hole size increases the fit starts to deviate where the contact model 
stays fitted to the tendency and actual strain values. This indicates that the linear bear-
ing load used for the Shell and Solid FE models is a respectable simplified assumption 
compared to the contact model however with some deviation. To further validate the as-
sumed linear bearing loading for larger holes, a load investigation must be conducted for 
larger holes to confirm the scalability and robustness of the FE models. The downside of 
using the contact model is the choice of friction coefficient. As described previously the 
==PAGE:95==
Page | 75  
 
Solid w. contact FE model is simplified to not include friction due to time constrains of 
this project. The comparison between the DIC section strain results and the FE model 
has shown great comparability in both tendencies and actual values. This indicates that 
the simplification is close to the actual value which shows that low friction is present. 
The high resemblance is believed to be caused by the high content of carbon in the con-
tact area and the high polished surface of the pin which creates a low friction coefficient. 
To optimize the accuracy of the contact model, the model must be further developed to 
include friction. The friction coefficient can be determined by an experiment where the 
contact material surfaces are sliding against each other and force measured. By including 
friction, the use of the model can be expanded to include other surface finishes and lami-
nate compositions which will increase the range of application.  
The FE models have showed good failure values localized around the failure value of 1 
for the Max. strain and Max. stress failure criterion. The Tsai-Wu criterion showed inac-
curate results, which can be described as conservative. However, the shell and solid ele-
ments do not agree where the initial failure starts to occur. It is not possible to conclude 
exactly where the initial failure occurs but the force-displacement curve results reflects a 
shearing failure which the solid elements confirm. The resemblance between the test and 
FEM results found in this report indicates that the fixture, FEM loads and constrain as-
sumptions are consistent between each other. The fixture has small margins of displace-
ment under load but has shown to have no significant influence on the results. As pre-
sumed, the tested laminates indicates in-plane failure. The assumed out-of-plane material 
properties from the ANSYS library have revealed to be sufficient for the in-plane bearing 
loading. If the test is further developed to also include out-of-plane-loading, the out-of-
plane material cannot be assumed and has to be determined by material characterization 
tests. The strain distribution found for the DIC results indicates no significant edge effect 
from the dimensions of the specimen laminates. The chosen design parameter, 푤/퐷≥4, 
indicates a valid assumption. If larger hole sizes than Ø25 mm needs to be further inves-
tigated, the laminate specimens and the radial test fixture needs to be modified in order 
to comply with the design parameter.  
Due to the limitation in ANSYS it was not possible to add different stiffness properties 
in both tension and compression. Therefore, the model was limited to one stiffness for 
each principal direction. For the material characterization tests it was found that the 
stiffnesses were different in tension and compression. A parameter investigation was 
therefore conducted which determined that the stiffness found in tension provided the 
most accurate results for the radial tests. To further optimize the FE models and avoid a 
parameter investigation, different stiffness properties in tension and compression must be 
integrated into the models. Even though the FE models have predicted good accurate re-
sults, the choice of stiffnesses are based on a parameter investigation which is not opti-
mal for application purposes.

Conclusion
Conclusion 
From the findings of the initial tests it is determined that the non-glue flange bearing 
sleeve solution provided the highest ultimate load properties in both axial and radial 
loading compared to the glued solution. By using the non-glued solution the failure loads 
are repeatable for both first failure values and ultimate values which is desirable for ap-
plication purposes. The FE models are found to be a powerful calculation tool that are 
able to predict both strains and failure values in radial bearing loaded laminate speci-
mens in the linear elastic region with respectable error margins. Based on the compari-
sons with the DIC strain section results, it has been determined that all three FE models 
captures the strain tendencies with marginal errors. According to these results the Solid 
w. contact FE model shows best comparability between strain tendencies and actual 
strain values for the largest hole size. The investigated failure criteria results indicate 
that the Max. Strain and Max. Stress criterion are the most suited failure criteria to use 
for pure radial bearing loading. It is concluded that by using both criterions a relative ac-
curate failure value can be determined. According to the two criteria the initial failure is 
initiated by a shearing failure. To determine strains and stresses at the ultimate load for 
bearing loaded laminates, the FE model has to be further developed to include non-elas-
tic behavior.

13 Further work
Further work
Further work
Further work 
Besides the further work described in the previous sections additional investigations 
are needed to validate and increase the understanding of loaded bearing sleeves incorpo-
rated in laminates. To further validate the FE models, more hole sizes must be tested. 
By testing more sizes, the robustness and scalability of each model can be validated. The 
FE model could be further developed by replacing the linear bearing loading with a sinus-
oidal bearing loading and introducing friction in the contact model which could enhance 
the accuracy. A bearing insert is rarely only loaded purely in the radial direction. To ac-
commodate and widen the application area of the FE models, a further development of 
introducing bi-axial loading by adding axial loading to the models. This requires that 
both test fixture and test procedure is further developed or modified to make comparable 
experimental test results. By analyzing bi-axial loaded bearing inserts a more generic FE 
model can be determined. It is assumed that when the radial bearing test load curves de-
viates from the linear region the initial failure occurs. Due to the uncertainties of this as-
sumption an investigation may be conducted. By measuring the position and the circular-
ity of the holes before and after different loadings in the linear and non-linear region, a 
more accurate failure point can be determined and compared against the FE models. The 
radial bearing tests conducted in this report have been limited to clearance fits between 
the laminate and the pin. However, bearing inserts are commonly press-fitted into struc-
tures. The introduced pre-tension may affect the failure mechanism and needs to be in-
vestigated further. Therefore the use of actual bearing sleeve inserts may be an ad-
vantage for these tests due to ability to simulate a realistic mounting.
