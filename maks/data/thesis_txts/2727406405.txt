Introduction
Climate change is one of the most important issues for the humanity today.
With
global temperatures continuing to rise, it is crucial that we take drastic action to reduce our
greenhouse gas emissions and prevent the worst effects of climate change.
Circular economy concept describes a model where the dependency between consump-
tion and economic growth is not linear, products are not necessarily becoming waste at the
end of the life but staying in circulation longer, decoupling in this way economic growth and
consumption of natural resources. It is based on sustainable economic growth. This is an
important point since population and consumption per capita are still growing ([1]), and the
over consumption of natural resources has to stop.
According to the Circularity Gap Report ([2]) assessed globally, circular economy has
the power to decrease global GHG emissions by 39% and cut virgin resource use by 28%,
while also creating economic opportunities and reducing inequality.
The Circularity Metric used in the Circularity Gap Report globally, define circularity as
the share of secondary materials that are cycled back into the global economic system over
all the material input in the global economy ([3]).
The current global economy has a linear behaviour (take-make-waste economy), it is only
7.2% circular ([3]), and decreasing among the years, meaning that there is high potential
to cut the emissions by the adoption of a circular economy, reducing waste and increasing
resource efficiency. Additionally, 70% of the greenhouse gases (GHG) emissions are related
to use of materials, thus, in order to significantly reduce emissions we must change the way
we use materials to meet our needs.
The company Circle Economy is assessing the circularity in different countries with the
aim to promote circular practices on a large scale. Countries play a key role in driving
the transition to a circular economy, they need to take action, they have the authority to
create national laws, establish supportive policies and incentives, and collaborate with other
countries on circular initiative. Circle Economy is assessing the circularity of Denmark in
2023.
The circular economy concept is based in three principles, eliminate waste and pollution
by reducing and ultimately eliminating waste and pollution from production and consump-
tion process, circulate products and materials by keeping the materials circulating for as
long as possible aiming to create closed loop systems, and regenerate nature by adopting
sustainable practices that align human activities with regenerative capacities of the natural
environment ([4]).
The circularity metric employed in this report primarily emphasizes the first and second
principles of the circular economy.
It quantifies the proportion of total inputs into the
economy derived from secondary sources, specifically recycled materials reintroduced into
the system. However, it is important to note that this metric does not evaluate the value
or quality of the recycled materials. Instead, it measures the mass of materials recycled
regardless of their ultimate purpose or potential downcycling. In essence, the circularity
metric treats all recycled materials equally, regardless of whether they undergo a process of
downcycling.

The goal of this master thesis is to assess the circularity of Denmark from a resource
perspective using the circularity metric previous described, developing a dynamic Material
Flow Analysis (MFA) model capable to qualitatively describe the behavior of the Danish
consumption of materials.
By using the Circularity Metric used in the Circularity Gap
Report globally it is possible to see how is Denmark contributing to the global assessment.
Dynamic material flow analysis is a method of studying the flow of materials within an
economy or system over time. It involves tracking the movement of raw materials, interme-
diate products, and waste through the various stages of production, use, and disposal. This
approach allows for the identification of inefficiencies and waste in the system, as well as
opportunities for resource conservation and reuse. Dynamic material flow analysis is a tool
for understanding the environmental and economic impacts of different materials and for
identifying potential pathways towards a more sustainable and circular economy. It can pro-
vide valuable insights for policymakers, businesses, and individuals seeking to reduce their
environmental footprint and promote more efficient resource use.
The model under this project explores qualitatively possible future quantities of different
material resources inputs to the economy and outputs as solid waste flows (material coming
out from the use stage) as well as the amount of the material that is collected to the different
treatment methods and actually recycled under some scenarios. The system for the model is
be defined by the domestic material consumption (DMC) in Denmark. It starts in 1769 and
projected to 2060, this is in order to build the stock through the years and have better results
for the present years. It is projected to 2060 due to data availability in DMC projections.

Theory
The theory section of this project aims to provide understanding of the key concepts
and tools relevant to assess the circularity of Denmark. It will explore important definitions,
discuss the tools employed in the project and clarify crucial data collected and managed
in the model building, specifically Material Flow Analysis (MFA), Economy-Wide Material
Flow Accounts (EW-MFA) data and Solid waste management in Denmark.
To begin, it is essential to establish a common understanding of key terms and definitions
related to the circular economy. This will enable a coherent discussion of the circularity
assessment and its implications for Denmark.
Next, attention will turn to the tools utilized in order to measure the flows of materials
within the Danish economy. This play a vital role assessing circularity. By exploring the
theory behind this tools it is possible to gain insights into the strengths and weaknesses of
the assessment.
Furthermore, this section will delve into the most significant data collected and managed
within the Danish economic system. Specifically, the focus will be on EW-MFA data. This
data encompasses different levels of categories of materials of important understanding for
the model building.
Additionally, waste management practices in Denmark will be discussed as an essential
aspect of the circular economy. Understanding the waste management system in Denmark
will contribute to build coherent and possible scenarios for the case of Denamark.
2.1
Definitions
• Material losses: it refers to all the material that is not collected for the collection
system. It represents losses in terms of material to the air, water, soil (emissions) or
materials with low probability of emerging as wastes or secondary resources because
they are dissipated during or after use or because there are not demolished (e.g. tunnels
or dums, potentially dissipated materials [5]).
• Waste flows: material collected to final treatment from the industries and households.
• Waste fractions: different waste materials sorted by industries and households (paper,
glass, plastics, etc.).
• Circularity Metric: amount of material recycled over the total input to the economy.
• Collection rates: fraction of material collected for final treatment, e.g. fraction collected
for recycling (not necessarily recycled).
• Recycling rates: fraction of the material sent to recycling actually recycled.

Material Flow Analysis
Material flow analysis (MFA) is a widely used methodology for assessing the flows of
materials within a well-defined system. MFA provides a comprehensive understanding of

material inputs, outputs, and stocks, enabling the evaluation of the system, based on mass
balance equations, providing insights and hotspots.
Dynamic Material Flow Analysis (MFA) is a tool used to analyze the behavior of ma-
terial flows within a system over time. This study uses dynamic MFA with the purpose
of understanding the Danish domestic material consumption (DMC) in terms of resources,
investigating and managing flows of materials between the economy.
The flow of materials investigated includes domestic material extraction, imports, ex-
ports, consumption, end of life of materials and recycled materials within the Danish econ-
omy. By incorporating a temporal dimension, it enables the examination of material flows
over time and provides insights into the dynamics and changes within the system. This ap-
proach offers a more comprehensive understanding of resource utilization, waste generation,
and the circularity of an economy.
One of the main outcomes of dynamic MFA is the quantification and visualization of
material flows and stocks and the development over time ([6]). It allows researchers and
policymakers to identify key trends, patterns, and hotspots in material consumption, waste
generation, and recycling rates. Additionally, dynamic MFA enables the assessment of mate-
rial efficiency and circularity, highlighting opportunities for improvements and optimization
in resource management.
Investigating the behavior of material flows in an economy is crucial for several reasons.
First, it provides valuable insights into the drivers and determinants of material consumption
and waste generation. By analyzing the patterns and dynamics of material flows, policy-
makers can identify factors that contribute to resource inefficiencies, waste generation, and
environmental impacts. This knowledge can inform the development of targeted policies and
strategies to enhance resource efficiency and transition towards a more sustainable, circular
economy.
Furthermore, dynamic MFA allows for the projection and scenario analysis of material
flows into the future. By modeling different scenarios, policymakers can evaluate the po-
tential impacts of policy interventions, technological advancements, and behavioral changes
on material consumption and waste generation. This proactive approach assists in setting
targets and designing strategies to steer the economy towards a more sustainable path.
The Open Dynamic Material System Model (ODYM) is a material systems framework
programmed in Python. ODYM MFA models possess the capability to effectively manage
various levels of flow and stock specifications. These specifications encompass a wide range
of elements, including products, components, sub-components, materials, alloys, waste, and
chemical elements, all of which can be traced at the same time. ODYM introduces a data
structure designed specifically for material flow analysis. This structure ensures that all in-
put and output data are stored in a standardized file format, enabling data exchange across
multiple projects. Additionally, ODYM offers an expanded library for dynamic stock mod-
eling.Common techniques and formulas utilized in Material Flow Analysis (MFA) comprise
various types of equations involving linear and nonlinear parameters, employing transfer or
other process coefficients. These equations can include regression models, dynamic stock
models, or optimization models ([6]).

Economy-wide material flow accounts
The European Strategy for Environmental Accounting (ESEA) recognizes Economy-
wide Material Flow Accounts (EW-MFA) as a fundamental module within Environmental
Accounts, intended for regular and timely production to support policy formulation.The
material flow accounts provide a foundational framework for understanding the general re-
lationship between the production and consumption of materials, as well as the generation
of waste and other undesirable residuals. The incorporation of EW-MFA as one of the three
modules in Regulation (EU) No 691/2011 on European Environmental Economic Accounts
highlights its importance ([7][8]).
The classification of materials reported by EW-MFA has a distinction in four different
levels browken down from the main material flow category (1-digit level):
• 1-digit: material category
• 2-digit: material class
• 3-digit: material group
• 4-digit: material sub-group

Domestic extraction refers to the material inputs obtained from the natural environment
for use within the economy. It represents a distinct form of economic production, involving
the deliberate extraction or movement of natural materials by human activities or technology
under human control.[9]
Biomass generally encompasses organic, non-fossil materials of biological origin. How-
ever, not all generated biomass is considered as domestic extraction in Eurostat’s EW-MFA.
The domestic extraction of biomass encompasses all plant-based biomass that is harvested
by humans and their livestock, as well as the capture of wild fish and the biomass of hunted
animals. However, the biomass derived from livestock and livestock products such as milk,

To ensure clarity regarding the assignment of biomass generation to domestic extraction,

Metal "ores" can be defined as deposits of metal compounds found in the Earth’s crust
that can be economically processed to extract desired metals. Typically, ore deposits consist
of rocks, although in certain significant cases, they can also be specialized soils or sand
deposits. In the context of EW-MFA, only the portion of excavated rock that undergoes
processing to extract the desired metals should be considered. This means that any soil or
rock that is simply excavated and moved to access the metal ore itself should not be classified
as ore. The majority of soil and rock excavated in an open-cut operation is not considered
as part of EW-MFA. Ore is selectively transported for further processing, while waste rock
is removed and deposited on a separate waste pile. Ideally, only the rock contained within
the "ore bodies" should be considered as metal ore ([10]).
Accounting for the domestic extraction of metals and non-metallic minerals always refers
to the run-of-mine production, which accounts for the total quantity of extracted crude
mineral submitted to the initial processing stage.
The Non-Metallic minerals accounts for stone quarries and clay and sand, chemical
and fertilizer, salt, quartz, gypsum, natural gem stones, asphalt and bitumen, peat and
other non-metallic minerals other than coal and petroleum ([9]). The report of non-metallic
material categorized as "3.10 Excavated earthen materials (including soil)" is optional and
it is not reported in Denmark (see Table 1).
Regarding the domestic extraction of fossil energy materials it is important to note that
EW-MFA focuses only on the extraction of fossil energy carriers. Consequently, primary
renewable energy carriers such as hydro, wind, solar, and geothermal energy are not included,
however, the materials used for the indrastructure of these energy carriers are reported in
their respective categories.
Biomass extraction intended for energy purposes is reported
under the biomass category, while the extraction of the energy carrier uranium is reported
under metals ([10]).
In terms of imports and exports, there are some differences between the Domestic Ex-
traction tables and the Imports and Exports tables, e.g. instead of 1.4 and 1.5 materials
in the 2-digit both are reported in the same flow. Even though these minor differences,
EW-MFA organizes traded products into material groups that align with the classification
of domestic extracted materials. This classification helps combine and summarize informa-
tion about domestic extraction and trade to create indicators. It is important to recognize
that this grouping based on the materials in traded products is a simplified approach, as
products are typically made up of different materials. Finished products, in particular, vary
enormously. Therefore, traded products are grouped based on the main material component
they contain. For example, motor vehicles are categorized as "products mainly from metals"
([9]).

2.4
Waste management in Denmark
As population, wealth and hereby consumption increases in Denmark ([11]), so does the
waste generation. It is therefore of the utmost importance to deal with waste in a way that
is environmentally sound, using robust technologies that are able to deal with unforeseen
events and treat many different types of waste well. Denmark produces approximately two
tons of waste per person annually, which potentially results in a significant loss of resources.
The handling and disposal of waste also require financial expenses, and have an impact on
the environment and climate ([12]). Promoting a decrease in waste quantities and adopting
efficient, sustainable practices for utilizing that waste are fundamental aspects of advancing
towards a circular economy. Within a circular economy, the emphasis is placed on enhancing
resource productivity that can lead to environmental and economic benefits.Furthermore
prolonging the lifespan of materials and products can additionally lead to reduced emissions
that would come from waste treatment, material transport and reduce the use of virgin
materials. Waste is defined as substances or objects which the owner or holder discards,
intends to or is required to discard’, according to the EU’s directive on waste from 2008. [13]
Based on the fact that waste has a potential of being a resource, in combination with
the negative impact on the environment and health conditions, waste management has been
of high importance in Denmark for decades. Initial conditions of limited amounts of natural
resources, limited space available for landfilling and the case that most of the danish water
supply system is based on the direct use of groundwater after minimum treatment, set a
strong base for a highly developed waste management system. [14]
The set infrastructure allows the managing system to take the following form:
• Waste Hierarchy: Denmark follows a waste hierarchy approach that prioritizes waste
prevention, followed by reuse, recycling, and energy recovery, with landfilling consid-
ered as a last resort.[15] There are possible deviations of the hierarchy. In that case,
deviations should be justified with a life cycle consideration. Used deviations should
still be done with overall best possible environmental results, the principle of propor-
tionality and socio-economic considerations.[16]
• Source Separation: One of the fundamental principles in Denmark’s waste management
is source separation. Households and businesses are encouraged to separate their waste
into specific categories such as organic waste, recyclables (e.g., paper, plastic, metal),
and residual waste. This enables efficient sorting and recycling processes.
• Recycling: Denmark has an extensive recycling infrastructure and aims to maximize
recycling. Various materials, including paper, cardboard, glass, metals, plastics, and
textiles, are collected separately and processed for recycling. The government promotes
awareness campaigns and provides easily accessible recycling facilities throughout the
country. From 2020 recycling is calculated as it is in the EU targets, meaning only the

• Organic Waste: Organic waste, including food and garden waste, is collected separately
and treated through composting or anaerobic digestion. The resulting compost is used
for agricultural purposes, while the biogas generated from anaerobic digestion is utilized
as a renewable energy source.
• Waste-to-Energy: Denmark heavily relies on waste-to-energy incineration plants, where
non-recyclable waste is burned under controlled conditions to produce heat and electric-
ity. The energy generated from incineration is used for district heating and electricity
production. Advanced technologies are employed to minimize emissions and ensure
environmental sustainability.
• Landfilling: it is the least preferred option in Denmark’s waste management strategy.
The country has significantly reduced its reliance on landfills and has imposed strict
regulations on waste disposal. The remaining residual waste after recycling and energy
recovery processes is carefully managed in landfills engineered to prevent environmental
contamination.
• Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR): Denmark implements an EPR policy that
holds manufacturers responsible for the entire life cycle of their products, including
their disposal. This encourages manufacturers to design products that are more envi-
ronmentally friendly and easier to recycle.
• Public Participation: Denmark places a strong emphasis on public awareness and
involvement in waste management. Citizens are encouraged to participate actively in
waste separation, recycling, and other sustainable waste practices. Education programs
and campaigns are conducted to raise awareness about waste reduction and recycling
([17] [18]).
By integrating these strategies, Denmark’s waste management system aims to minimize
waste generation, optimize resource utilization, and create a closed-loop system where ma-
terials and resources circulate within the economy, reducing the reliance on virgin resources
and minimizing environmental impacts.
2.5
IPAT equation
The IPAT equation, alternatively referred to as the IPAT formula or the I=PAT model,
represents a conceptual framework to show the intricate connection between human activities
and their environmental repercussions. Comprising three fundamental components, namely
population, affluence, and technology, the IPAT equation provides a way to analyze and
understand the environmental impacts of human activities ([19]).
I = P × A × T
Page 9 of 49
==PAGE:12==
Master Project Report
2.5
IPAT equation
The "P" within the IPAT equation signifies the size of the human population. As the
global population continues to grow, so does the demand for resources, consequently putting
increased pressure on ecosystems, energy reserves, and natural habitats. This escalating
pressure has as consequences, deforestation, heightened pollution levels, resource depletion,
and loss of biodiversity.
The "A" element in the IPAT equation represents affluence, integrating the average
income, consumption patterns, and lifestyle choices exhibited by individuals within a society.
Enhanced affluence consistently corresponds to increased resource consumption and waste
generation. In societies characterized by affluence, the tendency is to have higher levels of
energy use, carbon emissions, and ecological footprints. Increased consumption, particularly
within industries that heavily rely on resource-intensive practices, directly contributes to
environmental degradation and worsens the effects of climate change.
The "T" component of the IPAT equation denotes technology, encompassing the tech-
niques, methodologies, and systems employed in the production of goods and services. Tech-
nological advancements have the potential to both improve and exacerbate environmental
impacts. Sustainable and efficient technologies can reduce resource consumption, minimize
waste generation, and promote the utilization of renewable energy sources. On the other
hand, inappropriate or outdated technologies can lead to pollution, habitat destruction, and
increased resource extraction.
The IPAT equation suggests that environmental impact "I" is a function of the in-
teraction between population, affluence, and technology. It highlights the need to address
these factors collectively to achieve sustainability and minimize negative environmental con-
sequences. Through a comprehensive understanding of the interplay between these variables,
policymakers, researchers, and organizations can develop strategies and solutions to mitigate
environmental problems and promote sustainable development.
It is important to acknowledge that the IPAT equation, despite its utility as an analytical
framework, it does not capture all aspects of human-environment interactions. It simplifies
the complex relationship between human activities and the environment. Factors such as
governance structures, cultural dynamics, and value systems play significant influence over
environmental impacts. Consequently, it is imperative to recognize the supplementary roles
played by these factors. Nonetheless, the IPAT equation remains a valuable tool for exploring
the fundamental drivers of human impact on the planet.

3.2
Data collection
To construct a model for evaluating resource circularity in Denmark, it was imperative
to gather data from various national sources.

3.5
Qualitative uncertainties
As mentioned earlier, the model is constructed based on numerous assumptions and
uncertainties surrounding data collection.
First, the data presented in the EW-MFA relies on modeling and assumptions when
there are gaps in the available data to report between the country, for example some flows
are estimated or converted from volume to mass with average density of the materials.
Nevertheless, the EW-MFA data has been used as an average for the given period, allowing
for use of the different split between the 1-digit and 2-digit level materials.
Secondly, the potentially dissipated materials, particularly in terms of the 2-digit mate-
rial "Sand and gravel" is not based on studies conducted in Denmark and as the use of these
materials are in the construction sector mainly, with long lifetimes, it is not very certain the
fraction considered potentially dissipated.
Domestic Material Consumption per capita was estimated for the beginning of the time
period as mainly use of biomass, for food and feed which results in low consumption per
capita, this is rough estimation and then it is considered linearly increasing until 1970.
Additionally, some estimations had to be done in order to have the same data recorded from
different sources (IRP, OECD, Statistics Denmark).
The biomass consumption of products derived from animals cannot be estimated from
the EW-MFA tables since the animals are not accounted as domestic extraction.

Explored Scenarios
Scenarios are possible future developments, the scenario making helps in the under-
standing of complex systems and allows for improved decision-making by considering differ-
ent conditions, interventions, and outcomes. The scenarios are not derived by extrapolating
past trends but aim to explore possible, not necessarily likely, outcomes.
Different scenarios encourage exploration of possibilities beyond current trends and con-
ventional thinking. They help in understanding the dynamics of complex systems and how
they might evolve and interact in different ways.
By considering a range of scenarios,
decision-makers can incorporate multiple perspectives and stakeholder viewpoints, raising
more inclusive and comprehensive decision-making processes.
There are two distinct types of scenarios commonly used, forecasting scenarios and
backcasting scenarios. By considering both decision-makers can gain valuable insights into
the potential future developments and identify effective strategies to achieve their desired
objectives.
Forecasting Scenarios involve starting from the current or historical situation and ex-
ploring various possible future paths to deduce potential end-states. They are descriptive
or exploratory in nature, aiming to understand where we might end up if we follow specific
trajectories or trends. Forecasting scenarios consider a range of factors and variables to
project the potential outcomes or consequences of different paths.
On the other hand, Backcasting Scenarios start with a desired end-state or a defined
goal and work backward to determine the possible pathways to reach that specific outcome.
This approach is normative in nature, focusing on identifying the options and strategies
available to achieve a desired future state. Backcasting scenarios consider the current or
historical situation as a starting point and then explore different actions, interventions, or
policies that can be implemented to move towards the desired outcome.

Sensitivity analysis (SA) is a tool that serves in the first place to identify the factors
that have a significant influence on the variability of the output in a model or system.
Determining the most important parameters, it is possible to gain a deeper understanding
of the system’s behavior.
Secondly, it helps to detect the parameters with less impact in the model outputs, which
can help to put less attention on these and the uncertainties they have.
Additionally, sensitivity analysis, when is done globally, allows the recognition of regions
in the model’s parameter space that exhibit high variation in the model results. This infor-
mation helps researchers identify areas where the model may be particularly sensitive. By
understanding these regions, further investigation or data collection efforts can be focused
on improving the reliability and robustness of the model.
Lastly, it could be helpful to assess the interactions between different parameters within
the model.

Discussion
The circularity under the baseline scenario implies low amount of recycled materials
going back into the cycle again. Given the fact that the amount of materials becoming waste
are the response of an earlier consumption, which was lower in material mass, it is expected
that the circularity of the materials is not high. The DMC per capita and consequently
overall consumption of materials have been growing in the lasts decades globally ([3]) and
the waste being generated and collected nowadays was introduced in the system in the past
years.
On the other hand, if the material consumption was able to maintain or decrease for
the following decades, then the material recovered in the future would be the response of a
higher material mass, and consequently the potential circularity would increase.
The global circularity is now 7.2% and decreasing the last years [3]. Five out of nine
planetary boundaries are being transgressed today and it could be assessed by a shifting
toward a circular economy ([3]). The goal of circularity globally is 17% ([33]). Denmark is
a country that is committed to environmental sustainability and historically it had imple-
mented policies to promote sustainable practices. Additionally, it had set ambitious targets
in terms of energy production transitioning to renewable sources, aiming to be fossil energy
independent by 2050 [34]. Given this proactive approach, it would be reasonable to expect
that Denmark will aim to achieve circularity goals as well.
Furthermore, there are countries with a higher circularity as Netherlands which uses
24.5% of the resources from secondary materials. Netherlands is, however a different case
than Denmark, where the material demand for the construction sector accounts for 14.6%
of the total material inputs ([35]) while in Denmark this percentage is almost half the total
input to the economy.
From the model results and scenarios, it is possible to see that the DMC per capita
needs to decrease in order to increase the circularity and reduce the gap to a circular econ-
omy. DMC per capita was assumed under the baseline scenario to increase due to economic
growth. However, GDP per capita is often used as a proxy for economic growth and de-
velopment, as it provides an indication of the total value of goods and services produced
within an economy. Economic growth is typically associated with an increase in GDP over
time, indicating an expansion of economic activity. As an economy grows, there is generally
an increase in the consumption of materials, including fossil energy, metallic minerals, and
non-metallic minerals. This increased material consumption is often associated with infras-
tructure development, industrial production, and the expansion of manufacturing sectors,
all of which contribute to GDP growth.
Higher GDP levels usually correlate with increased industrialization and urbanization,
which require more raw materials for construction, energy production, and manufacturing
processes.
These materials, such as metals and minerals, are crucial inputs for various
industries, including construction, automotive, electronics, and machinery, which are drivers
of economic growth.

The analysis of the scenarios provides valuable insights into the necessity for a change in
DMC and its reduction. Presently, Denmark exhibits a relatively high DMC of approximately
24 tons per capita annually, signifying substantial resource utilization. Although the Figure 7
has shown a declining trend in recent years, it was previously higher and had demonstrated
historical increases. Notably, Denmark’s DMC is one of the highest in Europe ([36]). It
is important to acknowledge that Denmark’s climate is colder than other countries and
consequent higher energy inputs are required, when this is met by fossil energy sources, it
may contribute to the elevate DMC. However, if Denmark successfully transitions towards
renewable energy sources, aiming to become fossil independent by 2050, there will be a
significant reduction in per capita DMC.
Additionally, population density plays a crucial role in determining the DMC per capita
of a country. The relationship between population and the required infrastructure is not
directly proportional, as higher population density tends to result in lower consumption per
capita.
When a country has a higher population density, the proximity of people and resources
facilitates efficient resource utilization and distribution. This can lead to reduced per capita

consumption as individuals have access to shared amenities and infrastructure, promoting
efficient land use and transportation systems.
Conversely, countries with lower population density may face challenges related to in-
frastructure development, such as the provision of essential services, transportation networks,
and resource distribution. This can lead to higher per capita consumption as individuals
require a greater amount of resources and infrastructure to meet their needs.
It is important to consider the interplay between population density and DMC per
capita when analyzing resource consumption patterns.
Even though it could be expected that Denmark decrease the DMC per capita due
to the fact that much of the needed infrastructure has been already built, effectuating a
decline in DMC necessitates a collective commitment involving governmental policies, shifts
in energy production towards renewable sources, the adoption of circular business models by
companies, and individual responsibility.
The role of the government lies in implementing policies that facilitate the transition to
sustainable practices. Companies, on the other hand, can contribute by adopting alternative
business models that prioritize longevity and repairability over increased sales and consump-
tion. Consumers, as the final agents, must develop a conscientious understanding of resource
consumption and the underlying societal constructs that create needs. Companies, driven by
profit motives, perpetuate a cycle of overproduction and consumption. A paradigm shift is
necessary, where conscious decision-making is prioritized to distinguish genuine needs from
artificial ones. Additionally, companies should explore alternative production methods, such
as designing products easily repairable, encouraging customers to opt for repair rather than
purchasing new items.
By aligning governmental policies, company strategies, and individual choices, a trans-
formative shift can be achieved towards reduced DMC and a more sustainable utilization of
resources. This concerted effort will contribute to a circular economy and a more environ-
mentally conscious society.
In the context of the potential dissipated materials, and specially the construction mate-
rials, present a huge influence in the overall circularity metric, representing were most of the
resources are not being recovered. This means on the one side that there is a big potential to
increase the circularity of these materials, however, one of the limitations of the circularity
as it was defined is that it considers the mass of materials recycled without the consideration
of the use given to those materials. In this aspect, the circularity aims to reduce the impact
of the economy to the environment, however, if the material recycled is used with a lower
quality, the original input to the economy could be not seen reduced due to the need of high
quality materials.
There are now techniques to recycle concrete in a closed loop ([37]) avoiding the depletion
of aggregated resources and emissions in the cement and concrete production processes. It
is important to first, recover higher amounts of construction materials to recycling, there are
high amounts of dissipated material that could be recover. The exact amount of potential
collection of these materials requires further investigation.
In terms of recycling, Denmark has a strong culture of recycling and the population is
encouraged to separate their waste.

The developed model is valuable for understanding the consequences of various actions
and identifying areas where policies should be focused. However, there are certain material

to gain deeper insights into individual materials.
It’s important to note that the collection of different waste fractions does not necessarily
mean that all the materials collected under a specific category, such as metals, originated

waste treatment processes.
Even though the uncertainties of the model are significant, the study focusing in the level

Conclusions
The circularity indicator chosen defined as the total material recycled over the total
amount of material input to the economy has a limit to grow that do not allow the economy
to be 100% since materials are used for example for nutrition and there are material emissions
to air or water as a consequence of production processes.
The resource circularity of Denmark in 2023 is 2.9%. Denmark consumes 146 million
tonnes of materials and only 2.9% of this is coming from a secondary resource, while the rest
is virgin materials. If energy from waste incineration is accounted as energy substitution in
Tonnes of Oil Equivalent (TOE) the circularity is 3.5%. The addition of the energy input in
the circularity metric is debatable since it is not directly substituting fossil energy and there
are still emissions associated with waste incineration. Additionally, there are many resources
being incinerated that could be added to the recycling processes by improving the sorting
and designing of the products for an easier collection.
Material inputs were growing in the last decades, in the context of a growing economy
and need of infrastructure building as well as increased consumption of materials for the
population in general. Consequently and particularly for the construction materials, the
amount of material leaving the system nowadays is the consequence of an earlier consumption
and thus less amount of patential recovered materials. Nevertheless, for the future it could
be expected higher amount of waste generated, consequence of the actual consumption and
better techniques to recycle these materials in a closed loop. Besides, once saturated the
infrastructure needed, the material consumption would decrease greatly, and the materials
being recovered could potentially match the material inputs needed.
Under the baseline scenario projection the circularity is increasing by 0.1% only from
2023 to 2060. However, exploring different scenarios it was found a possible 17% circular
economy (14% increase) by changing the future behaviour. Many efforts have to be done in
the DMC, shift of energy consumption towards renewable energy and technology improve-
ments in collection and recycling rates, specially for the recycling of construction materials
since they represents a big amount of the total waste generated.
The most difficult and necessary shift is in terms of domestic material consumption
(DMC) per capita. For the case of Denmark where the population density is not really
high, every material used for building infrastructure means high DMC per capita. In theory,
after saturating the system the need would be more for renovation than for building new
infrastructure and by improving the recycling of the construction sector the need of virgin
materials should be minimum. In reality, similar as the rebound effect for energy savings, the
needs of the population in terms of comfort are usually increased, meaning that it is usually
possible to continue build infrastructure, specially if there are economically possibilities to
do it.

Perspectives
In order to get more insights from this study it would be better to have data in terms
of different materials in the waste fractions. This could be done by applying picking analysis
of waste composition for the different fractions.
Additionally, better data and sort of the waste fraction categorized as "soil" since it is
not reported what is included in this fraction and probably it would include used sand and
gravel.
Understanding and quantification of potentially dissipated materials in the context of
the Danish economy would be interesting and essentially influence the results of this study.
Further investigation in the lifetimes of the 2-digit level materials is needed and the
developed over time.
A conducted global sensitivity analysis to understand the co-relation between the dif-
ferent parameters is necessary. To conduct it, better understanding of the relation between
the different materials and the use of each of them in the economy is needed.
