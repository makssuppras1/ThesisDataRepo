Summary (English)
The goal of the thesis is to explore possibilities of a rule based algorithm ap-
proach in the treatment of heart failure involving mobile technologies. On the
one hand, to provide patients with relevant self quantiﬁed data and trends which
could allow them to have an accurate knowledge of their disease during the
treatment.
And on the other hand, to facilitate physicians and nurses with
information of possible decompensation in patients, and to provide advice for
medicine titration.
The product of this project is based on two mobile applications prototypes devel-
oped in iOS. The ﬁrst, a patient application that uses the frameworks HealthKit
for allowing to collect data using state of the art devices; and ResearchKit for
evaluation of symptoms.
The second, a physician/nurse application that re-
ceives daily patient’s data. Additionally, a real time database using Firebase
was implemented in order to send patient’s data to the nurse app and vice-versa.
Finally, the project was designed in 6 iterations using Lean user experience
prototyping. A doctor in Peru helped to validate ideas regarding the develop-
ment of the questionnaire of symptoms and rule-based algorithms, while the
user interface (UI) was validated by four testers in 2 iterations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Heart failure is a global disease that aﬀects approximately 26 million people
worldwide. Estimations in the United States account for 5.7 million cases, whilst
in Europe for 15 million[1]. In Denmark, a country of around 5.5 million inhab-
itants, the number of diagnosed patients range between 60.000 to 100.000, with
11.000 admissions per year and an average patient age of 72 years[2]. Also in
2013, Denmark registered 18,4% re-admissions for patients with 67+ age[3].
Furthermore, despite advances in studies of heart failure medication to relieve
patient’s symptoms and improve quality of life, the actual treatment has not
changed much for the past two decades. This represents a threat to health care
expenditures, since global population of age 60 years or over will grow by 56%[4]
by year 2030, and with it the need to innovate and improve the treatment of
chronic diseases. The problematic is already evident in Denmark, where the
fastest a patient is discharged, the cheapest the treatment will become. This
leaves a patient almost alone with high possibilities to be re-admitted at the
hospital, and to even inﬂate the cost of the treatment.
Today, heart failure patients in Denmark do not count with an oﬃcial mobile
solution to manage their requested metrics nor to annotate their medication
adherence. Many patients feel lost at the beginning of the treatment due to
the lack of proper communication between patient and nurse. However, mobile
health and telemonitoring could become an important tool for better treatment

1.1
Heart Failure
Heart Failure can be deﬁned as a chronic, progressive condition or as an ab-
normality of the cardiac structure in which the heart has a decreased pumping
function of blood. This abnormality leads at ﬁrst to the heart could enlarging
its chambers to make stronger contractions and pump suﬃcient blood, however
this results in irregular heartbeats, ﬂuid retention and lung congestion. Also, af-
ter a period, it increases the heart’s muscle mass, therefore producing weakened
heart pumps, and faster pumping which results in tachycardia.
There are two main types of heart failure:
• Left-Sided heart failure. This occurs on the left ventricle which is respon-
sible for receiving oxygen-rich blood coming from the lungs and pumping
it to the rest of the body. There are found two types:
Systolic failure, the heart fails to pump enough blood due to its lost
ability to contract as a normal heart.
Diastolic failure, the muscle has become stiﬀand has lost elasticity for
allowing blood to ﬁll up the ventricle.
• Right-Sided heart failure. Also known as right ventricular HF, usually is
the result of the left ventricle failure because the excessive ﬂuid pressure
that is transferred through the lungs damages the right side of the heart.
Since the right ventricle does not have enough pumping power, the blood
"back-ups" in the body veins. The results are swelling and congestion
within the lower parts of the body producing edemas.
1.1.1
Heart failure and Left ventricular ejection fraction
Heart failure deﬁnition is based on measurement of the left ventricular ejec-
tion fraction (LVEF). LVEF compares the amount of blood in the heart to the

amount of blood pumped out in each heart beat. A normal LVEF may range
between 50% - 70%. Furthermore, there exists diﬀerences in the disease within
patients with LVEF. The diﬀerentiation is important as it helps to select speciﬁc
therapies, and it may encourage new studies. These are known as[6]:
• Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF), they typically present
a LVEF < 40% and present diastolic dysfunction.
• Heart failure with mid-range ejection fraction (HFmrEF), term recently
introduced in the 2016 ESC and 2016 AHA guidelines. The LVEF range
is between 40% - 49%.
• Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF). The diagnosis re-
quires a LVEF >= 50%.
Even though a patient diagnosed with HFpEF might seem to pump out a nor-
mal percentage of blood, the amount of pumped blood is not enough to meet
the body’s needs. Unfortunately, there is not clear evidence of the eﬀective-
ness of actual treatments in patients diagnosed with HFpEF or HFmrEF[6].
These patients are generally highly symptomatic older adults, might have being
diagnosed already with other chronic disease, and have impaired tolerance to
exercise, therefore the treatment’s goal is to alleviate symptoms and resistance
training to improve exercise capacity[6].
1.1.2
Causes, symptoms and diagnosis of heart failure
There are many conditions that could lead to developing heart failure. In many
cases it is about lifestyle factors represented in unhealthy habits. The main
causes are behind several other conditions:
• Coronary artery disease, obstructed arteries blocked the blood stream.
• Past heart attack,
• High blood pressure, high pressure in blood vessels leads the heart to pump
harder.
• Faulty heart valves, the heart workload is harder than normal.
• Heart muscle disease, diabetes, obesity, and others.

Symptoms at an early stage may be diﬃcult to identify and therefore many
cases are diagnosed when the damage has already occurred. However, the most
typical experienced symptoms are [7]:
• Dyspnoea: Sudden shortness of breath
• Orthopnoea: Shortness of breath when laying down
• Severe nocturnal dyspnoea
• Reduced exercise tolerance
• Fatigue, tiredness, and increased time to recover after exercise
• Ankle swelling
For diagnosis, the European Society of Cardiology mainly recommends the use
of an echocardiogram test which allows immediate evaluation of the heart cham-
bers, ventricular and valve functions. And, an electrocardiogram (ECG) that
provides heart rhythm information and electrical conduction[6].
1.1.3
Treatment of heart failure
Unfortunately, there is no cure for a patient diagnosed with heart failure. How-
ever, it can be treated according to the degree of damage in the heart. Usually,
the goal of the treatment is to relieve symptoms, prevent re-admissions and
improve quality of life.
Heart failure patients are classiﬁed based on their limitations and symptoms
according to normal breathing and degrees of shortness of breath, and or angi-
nal pain. The classiﬁcation method used is the New York Association (NYHA).
Also, patients might be classiﬁed under Stages of heart failure using the Ameri-
can College of Cardiology/American Heart Association (ACC/AHA) classiﬁca-
tion (see Tables 1.1 and 1.2).
Generally, patients with heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HF-rEF)
Stage C, start pharmacologic treatment with ACEI or ARB plus Beta Blocker,
consequently according to their NYHA classiﬁcation II-IV patients are pre-
scribed Loop Diuretics, Hydral-Nitrates, Aldosterone or Antagonist1. The dosage
of the drugs are increased every second week according to the patients symp-
toms and response evolution of the treatment. The duration of the treatment

1.2
Information and Communication Technology
(ICT) and Health
The use of ICT within the healthcare sector may include the implementation
of an electronic health records (EHR) database, clinical decision support sys-
tems and telemonitoring. The implementation of above technologies is often
related with innovation and hence with opportunities to improve eﬃciency, cost-
eﬀectiveness and quality of healthcare. Unfortunately, these implementations
may fail, be unsuccessful or obtain negative insight from doctors. Therefore,
factors such as training of users, regular evaluation and measurable outcomes
may be followed carefully up[8].
1.2.1
Telemonitoring and Clinical decision support system
(CDSS)
Telemonitoring refers to remote monitoring of patients using together a telecom-
munication medium and electronic information. For heart failure, it may include
monitoring and sharing clinical data, such as measurements, and the feedback or
guidance of the patient in episodes of decompensation, which might be essential
elements to treat the chronic disease on time. Telemonitoring can be integrated
with CDSS and can enhance a treatment by titration of medicine remotely.
In heart failure, such a system may provide advice based on collected data from
the patient, e.g. weight, blood pressure, heart rate, symptoms and others, and
in some cases the system can integrate the patient’s medical history, with the
previous diagnosis from EHR.
1.2.2
Mobile Health
Mobile Health (mHealth) is known as a sub-segment of e-Health, and according
to the World Health Organization it can be deﬁned as:
A medical and public health practice supported by mobile devices, such as mo-
bile phones, patient monitoring devices, personal digital assistants (PDAs), and
other wireless devices.[9]
Today mHealth serves many purposes; from using the core features of a smart-
phone (voice calls and SMS), to the more advanced use of mobile applications

which interact with in-built mobile and/or external Bluetooth sensors which
measure vital body signs, such as heart rate, heart EKG, blood pressure, weight,
body mass index, daily activity and many more relevant measurements.
Moreover, mHealth could aid to improve the treatment of a heart failure patient
and reduce the number of readmissions. On the one hand, it can help patients to
change their behavior positively when given the opportunity to self manage their
health and condition. And on the other hand, it could enable more accurate
diagnosis and treatment since the collection of data from the patient is more
frequent comparing to a conventional treatment.

2.1
Problem Statement
In the context of the treatment of heart failure in Denmark, a patient when
meeting with a nurse every second week is to generally be measured relevant

3.1
Self Management and Remote Monitoring stud-
ies
There are a few studies that have explored the feasibility of self-management of
heart failure patients making use of a smartphone and rule based algorithms.
Most of them refer to remote telemonitoring and have reviewed absolute risk
reductions of all-cause mortality from 1.4% to 6.5%, and hospitalizations from
3.7% to 8.2%[10].
Ledwidge et al(2013)[11], evaluates patterns in sensitivity of remote weight mon-
itoring on high risk patients by using a system called HeartPhone. The Heart-
Phone system includes a mobile application that could receive weight values
from a bluetooth-scale and share the data to a remote web server. Moreover,
the study discusses diﬀerent points of view regarding weight monitoring in heart
failure patients. One of them suggests that it exists poor sensitivity to observe
==PAGE:26==
12
Related Work
clinical deterioration when weight changes e.g 2 to 3 kg over 2 or 3 days. Another
suggests that subtle weight changes within one week could conﬁrm heart failure
decompensation. Furthermore, the HeartPhone weight monitoring algorithm is
based on using moving averages to daily weight data which triggers alerts based
on weight lectures beyond the threshold. The results provided more sensitivity
when predicting Heart failure events (82%) compared to the normal thresholds
of 2 kg over 2 to 3 days (21%), and a not exact measure threshold of 1.36 kg
over 1 day in 46% of the cases.
Another case of study is found at the Massachusetts General Hospital (MGH).
Zan et al (2015)[12], which evaluated the feasibility of using a system called
iGetBetter, for low intensive heart failure remote monitoring of patients (oppo-
site from highly intensive: close clinical oversight and follow-up). iGetBetter
is an HTML 5 web platform that resembles a mobile application and consists
of two apps, one for clinician and one for patient. 20 patients completed the
study, and were instructed to measure their weight, blood pressure and heart
rate every morning using a Bluetooth weight scale and a blood pressure cuﬀ
from Withings. Measurements were sent onto a web platform and patients were
provided with an iPad Mini to access their measurements online. In case the
patient missed to register any parameter, they would receive a call from an In-
teractive voice response (IVR) system as a reminder, and allow them to register
their weight manually with the phone keypad. A signiﬁcant ﬁnding was that
80% of patients were consistently engaged, by reporting and viewing their self
measurements over a course of 90 days. Also 19/20 (95%) participants reported
more conﬁdence when doing home care activities and 16/20 (80%) believed that
their disease was being controlled better. Finally, no signiﬁcant diﬀerence were
observed for hospital resource utilization of frequency in 30 day re-admissions.
A randomized controlled Trial in Finland telemonitored a group of 47 patients[13].
They were provided of a mobile phone with a pre installed mobile application,
a weight scale and blood pressure meter. They were requested to measure their
weight, blood pressure and pulse, and to answer symptom related questions once
a week. All data was shared to a secure remote patient monitoring server. A
nurse followed up on the patients and contacted them if thresholds were acti-
vated or if the patient did not comply in fulﬁlling the weekly report. Although
adherence for submitted weekly reports was close to 90%, there were not signif-
icant diﬀerences in their health or self-care behavior.
Lastly, there exists indications that the 6 minute walk test (6MWT) could pre-
dict heart failure severity which leads to death and possible hospitalizations.
Though the test is very simple and consists of walking from point A to B at
a distance of 30 meters, the test needs to be controlled by a trained clinician.
Brooks et at[14] intended to validate a study of self administered 6MWT where
103 patients, limited to NYHA III and less severe symptoms, independently

4.1.3
The nurse and up-titration of medicine
In Denmark, heart failure is treated in nurse-based heart failure clinics. Nurses
are usually involved in[20]:
• Patient education, with the goal of providing knowledge about the disease,
the treatment, possible risk factors and necessary lifestyle changes.
• Follow-up on the state of the patient regularly through telephone consul-
tations.
• Drug up-titration. This takes place at the medical institution and nurses
are responsible for making changes in medications, such as ACE inhibitors,
beta-blockers, angiotensín, aldosterone-receptor blockers and diuretics.
For drug titration, nurses make their decision based mostly on the actual symp-
toms of the patient. Nurses, normally, ask symptomatic questions and assess
which level in the NYHA functional classiﬁcation the patient belongs to. Also,
metrics as weight, blood pressure, and pulse rate are important to determine
necessary dose’s increments. Other tests such as a blood analysis could be often
be needed for evaluating the health of the kidney and renal system.
4.1.4
How does the patient feel during the up-titration
period?
Heart failure means that the heart muscle is not able to perform as it used to do
when it was healthy. For many diagnosed patients this situation is diﬃcult to
swallow at once. The symptoms could be part of the problem, as breathlessness
and fatigue, and liquid accumulation and swollen legs require for them to change
routines in their lives. The disease may aﬀect patients’ self-esteem, therefore
negative feelings, social isolation and depression could also take part during
their treatment[21].
Another matter to consider are the possible side eﬀects from the drugs. Many
patients believe that medications are harming their health and they stop taking
them. A cause to this issue could be represented as the lack of knowledge about
their disease, because patients who know their medications and the side eﬀects

According to the Fundamentals of Nursing Assessment[23, ch 6], patient’s data
can be classiﬁed in two types:
4.2.1
Objective data
Objective data are observable and measurable data that can be seen, heard, or
felt by someone other than the person experiencing them.
Objective data observed by one person can be veriﬁed by another person ob-
serving the same patient. For heart failure patient, examples of objective data
are sudden weight gain or loss, very high or low blood pressure, high or low
heart rate, an EKG result, blood analysis, and others. Objective data can also
be referred to as signs or overt data.
4.2.2
Subjective data
Subjective data are information perceived only by the aﬀected person.
Subjective data cannot be perceived or veriﬁed by another person. In this con-
text, examples of subjective data are feeling nausea, fatigue, shortness of breath
while doing certain activities, therefore subjective data can also be referred as
symptoms or covert data.
4.3
Clinical decision support systems
Clinical decision support systems (CDSS) are computer programs that assist
medical decision making processes by providing a second opinion or improving
the decision outcome in an interactive way.
A CDSS can assist to any well
structured decision problem of a clinical matter task, such as in:
• Diagnosis assistance, typically based on statistical likelihood.
• Therapy planning, typically from knowledge in clinical guidelines.
• Risk assessment
• Treatment monitoring

5.2.1
A brief review at the available literature regarding
self management
In Chapter 3, there were discussed academic studies regarding self management
and monitoring. Independently, they used one daily measurement of weight,
blood pressure before breakfast or during the morning, and of heart rate while
the patient was at rest[11][12][13].
The American Heart Association (AHA) and the European Society of Cardi-
ologist (ESC) guidelines, encourages to keep track of daily weight measures in
order to avoid decompensation due to sudden weight gain of +2 kg in 3 days, in
this way patients may increase their diuretic dose or alert their physician [6][25].
Moreover, for blood pressure, the ESC guideline recommend to keep control
of it, speciﬁcally in cases of hypertension where the blood pressure targets are
similarly applicable to the ones in hypertension guidelines[6]. There are 3 grades
of hypertension, and it is recommended targeting and maintaining a measure
to < 140/90 mmHg with blood pressure drugs with the standard treatment[26],
however, a recent SPRINT trial has found that an intensive treatment to reduce
systolic blood pressure SBP to < 120 mm Hg was possible, during the trial those
with lower SBP had a better survival rate within the next 5 years[27].
Regarding the Resting Heart Rate measurement, the ESC guidelines consider to
target a resting value <= 70 bpm or <= 75 bpm if taking Ivabradine[6]. This
guideline or the ACC/AHA one do not mention whether it is important or not
to self measure this metric in a daily basis.
Finally, from interviews conducted to nurses by ex DTU Master students at the
Herlev Hospital in Denmark last year[28], nurses agreed that daily measurements
were ﬁne, however regarding blood pressure measurements were advisable to be
taken every second day to avoid wrong focus of the disease.

Dyspnea refers to breathlessness, a subjective sensation of diﬃculty when breath-
ing, while Fatigue and tiredness a feeling of being tired and diﬃculty with every-
day activities. From Figure 7.1, Braunwald discusses that the New York Heart

Metabolic Equivalent Task (MET) chart. MET expresses the energy cost of
physical activities in intensity as Light, Moderate, and Vigorous.
Summarizing the previous, there are four degrees regarding symptoms in heart
failure:
• Grade 1 None breathlessness or fatigue
• Grade 2 Breathlessness, fatigue on severe exertion
• Grade 3 Breathlessness, fatigue on mild exertion
• Grade 4 Breathlessness, fatigue at rest

From these grades it is then possible to identify that they are evaluated according
to the intensity of physical activity, and to both shortness of breath and fatigue.
Therefore, to get a more accurate information from a patient, dyspnea and
fatigue should be questioned in regards of the intensity of their physical activity.
7.2.2
Coughing
Coughing is another frequent symptom in heart failure, and it can be related to
two main causes:
• The adverse reaction to Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE) inhibitors[33]
which produces a dry coughing in the patient as a side eﬀect. In this case,
Angiotensin II receptor blockers (ARB) should be prescribed instead.
• Another cause might be due to ﬂuid accumulation in the lungs and results
in a cough that produces frothy sputum. Having in mind the context of
congestive heart failure in the left ventricle, this coughing may be caused
by the higher pressure inside the left atrium of the heart, then in the veins,
and capillaries in the lungs. This leads to the ﬂuid being pushed through
the capillary walls into the air sacs (alveoli)[34]. The alveoli’s job is to
transfer oxygen to the bloodstream, and if they are ﬁlled with ﬂuid, then
they will not be able to provide the required amount of oxygen to the
body.
7.2.3
Edema
It arises when parts of the body become swollen due to gathering of ﬂuid in
the tissue.
It usually becomes evident with sudden weight gain.
Normally,
congestive heart failure can cause[35]:
• Peripheral edema, it may happen that legs, ankles and arms might suﬀer
of swelling.
• Abdominal edema, ﬂuid builds up in the abdominal area and it may pro-
duce pain.
• Sacral edema, swelling on the back due to lay down.

7.2.4
Paroxysmal Nocturnal Dyspnea (PND)
PND refers to the sensation of breathlessness that awakens a patient and that
may be experienced after 1 or 2 hours of sleep. This symptom is usually re-
lieved in the upright position and adding extra pillows in the back. PND is a
serious symptom, and it may be caused by pulmonary congestion, therefore the
improvement of symptoms when adopting a vertical position.
7.2.5
Other symptoms
Dizziness, it is an often common symptom and it might happen when blood
pressure drops. The main reason is that most medication (ACE-I, ARB and
Beta blockers) in heart failure will reduce blood pressure.
Nausea or loss of appetite, might be due to building up ﬂuid around the liver
and intestines which may interfere with digestion. It might also be due to side
eﬀects of the medicine.

11.2
HealthKit
HealthKit is an iOS framework that works as a sort of database for storing
personal health data. Thus, it is possible to save, query and make statistical
analysis. In order to populate Health Data such as Weight, Blood Pressure, and
Heart Rate, there are two options. One could choose to add the values manually,
or could make use of third party apps such as Withings. These third party apps
may get the data from devices connected via Bluetooth to the iPhone. Then,
the data collected is sent by those apps to the HealthKit Store using the class
HKHealthStore in order to save the new samples. Similar case is done when
retrieving health data. The Patient application can therefore retrieve health
data regardless if they were stored by third party applications or manually.
Before saving or retrieving data from and to HealthKit, permission to read or
write data needs to be granted. Values from HealthKit may have diﬀerent at-
tributes, but the ones used for weight and heart rate were: Measurement, date
and data source. While for Blood Pressure: Systolic, diastolic, date and data
source. Also, these values are being collected in background mode, and each
time a new value appears in the HealthKit Store by using a custom function,
startObservingChanges(), which triggers the method enableBackgroundDelivery-
ForType(). Moreover, a query observer is created, HKObserverQuery, to receive
updates when HealthKit data changes. Later values are anchors compared be-
tween actual values and the new ones. At last, the new value is sent to the
Firebase database (Figure 11.2).

Firebase
In order to manage patients and their data, a Firebase project was implemented.
Firebase[37] is a cloud services provider and backend as a service company that
provides several development tools for web and mobile development. It oﬀers
a very comfortable free plan called Spark, which for prototyping purposes is
enough. For this project, there were used two services Firebase Authentication
for storing patients, and Real Time Database for storing patient’s data.
11.4.1
Patient authentication and Realtime database
Although currently Firebase does not encrypt any data nor has any short term
plans to add this feature, the Firebase’s Realtime Database uses certiﬁcations
SSL with a 2048 bit keys and handles secure authentication tokens (included in
the free plan). However, tokens were not implemented for this project due to
time constraint.
In order to set up Firebase, ﬁrst, one must create a new Project using a Google

Implementation of Rule based algorithms
Since it was an exploratory project, the algorithms were implemented on the
Patient application instead of doing the analysis in the cloud server side. This
was due to time constrain and feasibility, since setting up the project aside

path. The implementation, however, simulates well how a clinical decision sup-
port system (CDSS) may function. The algorithms implemented communicate
with the Nurse application in Real Time and the other way around, therefore
attention to patients may succeed on time.

Future work and discussion
This chapter describes a reﬂection and discussion of diﬀerent areas and sub-

however, are important for the ﬁeld of this study.
12.1
Testing with Dataset and real patients
One of the main challenges faced in going forward within this project is to test
the set of rules in real life, specially for medicine titration. Due to complexity
of the subject other factors were avoided in this project and only symptoms,
weight, blood pressure and resting heart rate have been considered. Therefore
one way to test might be to use datasets from relevant heart failure randomized
trials. However, accessing these datasets is not an easy task and several forms
and requirements may be necessary to ﬁll up. One of the main repositories are
the Biologic Specimen and Data Repository Information Coordinating Center
from the U.S. Department of Health & Human Services (e.g. HF-ACTION).
Besides, testing a prototype system such like this with real patients may be
subjected to ﬁlling several protocol conditions up or bureaucracy, therefore prior
to deployment a test with a Dataset is recommended. The dataset may contain
at least part of an entire up-titration period of patients. Also, it would be very
important to count at least with:

• Symptomatic values such as Dyspnea and fatigue during exertion, body

Feature improvements
Another main challenge is to understand the Heart Failure guidelines and trans-
late some of their rules into a mobile application. Also, there is a challenge
regarding the limitations of access to other types of patient’s data, such as
Sinus Rhythm from an Electrocardiogram (EKG), or B-type natriuretic pep-

to change from ACE-I to ARB[6]. Nevertheless, to maximize the eﬃciency of
use from the data that was analyzed in the project, it should be important to
consider the following:
• Improve data cleaning
Previously, it was mentioned that considering non-wireless devices, they
might send erroneous values, and that the application would need to han-
dle this in order to minimize errors in the automatic evaluation for the
patient’s evaluation and nurse’s summary, avoiding false alarms or mes-
sages. Although, the application handles two use cases (patient has already
a vast number of measurements in HealthKit prior installing the app and
Only ﬁrst measurement is considered), there are several others that could
require to extend this project immensely, but they would be important to
consider in future developments.
• Symptoms and context Asking for symptoms, may be improved by
the use of wearable devices. An Apple Watch may help to assess more
frequent resting heart rate measurements, as well as step counting. Also,
the 6 minutes walk test can be implemented and can assess the functional
capacity of the patient. An example of a use case can be: a patient that
has walked for 6 minutes (approximately 300 - 500 meters), step counts
and heart rate values can be recorded automatically from the iPhone’s
and Apple Watch accelerometer, later a notiﬁcation can ask the patient

• Other symptoms

Patient empowerment
Patient empowerment is a very confusing term, however, the European Patients
Forum (EPF) uses the following deﬁnition of empowerment for an individual
level:
A multi-dimensional process that helps people gain control over their own lives
and increases their capacity to act on issues that they themselves deﬁne as
important[39].
Additionally, the EPF describes diﬀerent aspects of patient empowerment. These
aspects should at least be partially covered in further development of heart fail-
ure monitoring systems for satisfaction of the stakeholders. Nevertheless, the
Patient education aspect might one of the most important, and hence the most
accessible one to support. Therefore, these features might need to be considered
in future releases.
• Add a "Lifestyle" feature.
It is a requirement for patients diagnosed with heart failure to adopt a
healthy lifestyle. Among the recommendations are to: do moderate exer-
cise, immediately stop smoking, lower the alcohol consumption to speciﬁc
quantities per day, reduce considerably the salt consumption, and in some
cases reduce the liquid intake per day. If such a feature is implemented it
may provide patients with important knowledge about living with heart

Data Privacy
Data privacy was not covered in this project. However, it is an important and
a sensitive topic that might need to be pursued in future releases due to the
fact that this application intends to collect personal data from a natural person.
Personal data corresponds to: Full name, address, contact information, device
identiﬁers, location data. Additionally, it corresponds to any data concerning
physical or mental health that has close relations to the person’s health status.
The Draft Code of Conduct on privacy for mobile health applications from the
European Commission, strictly mentions that if an app processes collected data
in order to measure o predict health risks (e.g. risk to injury or heart attacks)
and/or stored in order to analyze and evaluate the user’s health[40], then it may
be subject to regulation of data privacy. Among the main issues covered in the
Draft are:
• User’s consent
• Purpose limitation and data minimization
• Privacy by design and by default
• Data subjects rights and information requirements,

• Security measures,

Chapter 13
Conclusions
The project consisted of six iterations exploring the necessary elements that
would allow to apply and implement a set of rules for management of a chronic
disease. The focus for applying a rule based algorithm approach was to support
patients and clinical staﬀin order to improve the current treatment. At the end
of this project, a data driven system prototype was implemented consisting of
two native mobile applications for iOS.
The ﬁrst three chapters describe important deﬁnitions and concepts regarding
Heart failure as a chronic disease.
Moreover, the state of the art provided
inspiration since some of the studies have approached telemonitoring together
with clinical decision support systems, and hence a set of rules regarding the
management of the disease during the treatment period.
Chapter 4, provided insights on the actual treatment of heart failure, the goal
regarding the titration of medication, and acknowledgment of the two main
stakeholders, the Patient and the Nurse, if the heart clinic was nurse based.
Furthermore, the initial analysis of the type of data that would allow to help
stakeholders during the treatment process was deﬁned.
The further development of the project took place from Chapter 5 to 10 in cu-
mulative lean iterations that were validated with constant feedback. Moreover,
working closely with a Doctor and being able to early validate ideas proved to
==PAGE:92==
78
Conclusions
be eﬀective. A reason for this may be that one of the biggest challenges is the
existing gap of knowledge regarding the disease and the broadness of the topic
from the developers side. Therefore, for future releases, this approach should
also be followed with patients’ collaboration.
The analysis of objective data was made in the ﬁrst iteration. Besides, Heart fail-
ure is a frequently symptomatic disease. Thus, the analysis of these symptoms
in the second and third iterations represented the foundation of the project. As
a result from previous iterations, during the fourth one, a set of rules were evalu-
ated based on the European Society of Cardiologist (ESC)[6] and the American
Heart Association (AHA) guidelines, together with evaluation and feedback from
a Doctor in Peru specialized in Internal Medicine. The results were to count
with a patient classiﬁcation algorithm for assessing stability in symptomatic and
asymptomatic patients. And for the development of messages for patients and
clinical staﬀaccording to thresholds in values of subjective and objective data.
Furthermore, in iteration ﬁve, the set of rules were developed in the a Patient
and Nurse prototype, and with this providing both stakeholders with important
tools to make sense of the gathered data by giving access to summaries and
evaluations and two weeks insights of graphical information.
For the sixth and ﬁnal iteration, a new algorithm was evaluated based on the
ESC guidelines, the Spanish Society of Cardiologist (SEC) and Martinez Kropf,
these were validated again by a Doctor. The rules were implemented in the
Patient app and in the Nurse app, the clinical staﬀ, was provided access to vali-
date whether they agree with the system’s recommended titration or if they were
willing to customize the medication. Both applications can share information
due to a cloud database implementation in Firebase.
The ﬁnal product represents a system that resembles a clinical decision support
system based on collected patient’s measurements and symptoms.
There is
still more work to do and extra tuning needs to be rendered, specially in data
cleaning, reﬁnement of the algorithms, data privacy and data security. However,
the results of this project may encourage others to continue working within this
area.
