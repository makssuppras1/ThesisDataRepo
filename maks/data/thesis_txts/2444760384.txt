Abstract 
Liquid activated carbon (LAC) combined with donor and bacterial cultures is a new technology, 
aimed at in situ remediation of contaminated groundwater plumes. The LAC is injected into the 
subsurface to create a permeable reactive barrier (PRB), where contaminants are sorbed and de-
graded. The success of the technology is dependent on the distribution of LAC in the target treat-
ment area (TTA). However, the newness of the technology means that challenges with injection and 
monitoring methods remain. The Capital Region of Denmark has initiated a technology develop-
ment project to assess LAC technology in typical Danish conditions. The tested products were 
PlumeStop® and hydrogen release compound (HRC) targeting a plume of chlorinated ethenes (CE) in a 
mildly heterogeneous sand aquifer. The injection took place in December 2017. 
The aim of this project is to evaluate the distribution of PlumeStop® and donor through injection 
wells and provide recommendations for future use. Multiple lines of evidence are included: Visual 
interpretation using contour plots, transport processes by estimating breakthrough curves (BTC), 
depth discrete sampling and borehole logging.  
Transport processes were evaluated based on BTC of non-volatile organic carbon (NVOC) concen-
trations. Local variations in pore water velocities occurred; within a 2.6 m distance a factor 4.3 dif-
ference was seen. It was possible to distinguish the PRB into three zones dependent on donor distri-
bution and permeability. Uneven distribution of volatile fatty acids (VFA) released from HRC was 
indicated by NVOC concentrations with higher concentrations detected in the deepest part of the 
TTA. Depth discrete samples and estimated BTC indicate that HRC-X is unevenly distributed caus-
ing areas without continuous release of lactic acid; hence, the needed long-term reduced conditions 
for microbial degradation of CEs are not ensured.  
Contour plots indicate limited sorption by PlumeStop® within the barrier even 351 days after the 
injection. One out of twelve monitoring wells indicated the presence of PlumeStop® due to sorption 
71 days post-injection. Depth discrete sampling determined varying radius of influence (ROI) ≤ 0.3 
m instead of the designed 1.7 m and showed vertical variation in both distribution of PlumeStop® 
and NVOC concentrations. The observations reflect that the injection had been conducted at a too 
low pressure. By applying borehole logging in the injection wells it was confirmed that the amend-
ment products could be distributed to areas at least 1 m above the TTA. Indications of a build-up of 
PlumeStop® above and in the top of the TTA were found based on a high increase in formation 
conductivity complying with the expected conductivity of PlumeStop®. Based on Archie’s law a 
suitable formation factor for the area was found to be 3.15 prior to injection; however, it was con-
cluded that Archie’s law is not applicable to a system disturbed by PlumeStop® and HRC. To con-
clude, injection wells are not suitable for injection of LAC. Guaranteeing homogeneous distribution 
of LAC through a gravel pack in an injection well is not possible. Monitoring distribution of 
PlumeStop® can be improved by using more level specific monitoring wells, than the 2 m screens 
at the field site as well as including depth discrete sampling in monitoring rounds soon after injec-
tion. New methods including core sampling combined with the sorption test developed by 
Fjordbøge et al. (2018) to detect the presence of PlumeStop® is recommended for future research.

1 Introduction 
Groundwater is a primary source of water for agriculture, industry and drinking water (Rolle, Maier 
and Grathwohl, 2011). Chlorinated ethenes (CE) such as trichloroethene (TCE) are the cause of 
groundwater contamination in 38 % of 10,421 confirmed contaminated sites in Denmark 
(Søndergaard et al., 2018). CE have, due to physicochemical properties such as high density and 
volatility, been proven difficult and costly to remediate (Li et al., 2012). Research and development 
of sustainable remediation techniques to target CE are therefore highly important.  
Remediation techniques are categorised according to whether they target the source zone or the 
downgradient-contaminant plume (Wiedemeier et al., 1999). Contaminated plumes consisting of 
CE can cover large areas and penetrate deep into the subsurface as seen with CE contamination at 
Rødekro (Broholm et al., 2015). In situ plume remediation techniques are therefore more favourable 
than ex situ. A permeable reactive barrier (PRB) is an in situ remediation technique that intercepts a 
contaminant plume (ITRC, 2005; Mackenzie et al., 2008). The challenges related to PRBs include 
the longevity of the contamination; hence, the lifetime of the PRB may not be equivalent to the life-
time of the contaminated plume (Jeen, Gillham and Przepiora, 2011). A novel method known as an 
AC-based technology is gaining ground as a more cost effective method that prolongs the life ex-
pectancy of a PRB (US EPA, 2018). The AC-based technology comprises the contaminant removal 
utilising the processes adsorption and degradation. AC is applied to adsorb CE and reactive 
amendments are responsible for the degradation (Mackenzie et al., 2008, 2012; Liang, Lin and 
Shin, 2009; Georgi et al., 2015; Fan, Gilbert and Fox, 2017; US EPA, 2018). PRBs utilising the 
AC-based method are installed through distribution of AC and amendment products via injection 
wells or by direct push methods (Mackenzie et al., 2008; Fan, Gilbert and Fox, 2017). This injec-
tion of the products, avoiding excavation, causes the technology to be cost effective. Several types 
of AC products can be applied, differentiated by their size; the types include granular, powdered or 
liquid (US EPA, 2018). Longevity of the remediation technique is unknown due to its novelty (Fan, 
Gilbert and Fox, 2017). Major benefits of AC-based technology include its initial fast removal of 
contaminants through adsorption, as well as its long term ability to adsorb a persistent contaminant 
flux (from diffusion, desorption, and dissolution) when combined with bioaugmentation (Regenesis, 
2016; Fan, Gilbert and Fox, 2017; US EPA, 2018).  
PlumeStop® Liquid Activated Carbon TM (PlumeStop®) is a commercially available product by Re-
genesis, which uses suspended liquid activated carbon (LAC) particles (1-2 µm) to adsorb contami-
nants (Regenesis, 2015e, 2015f; Birnstingl et al., 2016). The particles are coated with an organic 
polymer in order to change the surface charge, keeping them in suspension 1-3 months after injec-
tion (Regenesis, 2015a, 2015g). When PlumeStop® is co-applied with a donor and bacteria cul-
tures, the PRB is capable of degrading CE and remediates groundwater through a two-step process 
of adsorption and biodegradation (Birnstingl et al., 2016). Geological heterogeneities, can cause 
preferential pathways and further complicate distribution of LAC (Lewis, 2014; Busch et al., 2015). 
Achieving a homogeneous distribution that covers the target area is a challenge (Fan, Gilbert and 
Fox, 2017). Therefore, gaining a better understanding of the distribution and distribution techniques

is important. Furthermore, a challenge with AC combined with bioaugmentation is the difficulty as-
sociated with differentiating between degradation and adsorption (Fan, Gilbert and Fox, 2017).  
The Capital Region of Denmark has initiated a technology development project to investigate the 
new technology: Establishment of a PRB utilising the new AC-based technology. PlumeStop® was 
chosen as the AC product, three hydrogen release compounds (HRC-Primer, HRC and HRC-X) 
were chosen as the donors and BDI+ was chosen as the bacterial culture to be investigated at field 
scale at the location Industrivej 2 (Rambøll, 2018b, 2018a; Rambøll and Regenesis, 2018). Indus-
trivej 2 has been the site of various industrial activity since 1962 and several fires have resulted in 
TCE soil and groundwater contamination in a sand aquifer (NIRAS, 2019). The aim of the project is 
to document the distribution and dechlorination by PlumeStop® and the amendment products and 
evaluate whether the AC-based technology as a remediation technique is suitable for further use at 
other contaminated sites in Denmark (Rambøll and Regenesis, 2018). Permanent injection wells 
were installed and PlumeStop® was injected (Rambøll and Regenesis, 2018). The field test has 
been monitored since 2017. 
1.1 Scope 
The main scope of this project is to assemble a quantitative and conceptual understanding of how 
the chosen injection method at Industrivej 2 distributed PlumeStop® and amendment products 
(HRC-Primer, HRC, HRC-X). This will be accomplished by: 
 review of the applicability of monitoring methods for assessment of PRB distribution, 
 evaluation of whether PlumeStop® and donor have created a PRB through investigation of 
non-volatile organic carbon (NVOC) distribution and sorption of chlorinated ethenes, 
 evaluation of transport processes at Industrivej 2 to distinguish how heterogeneities affect 
the distribution of both PlumeStop® and amendment products through examination of esti-
mated NVOC breakthrough curves, 
 assessment of PlumeStop® distribution via permanent injection wells, gaining a quantitative 
understanding through multiple lines of evidence including visual inspection, borehole log-
ging and depth discrete sampling, and 
 provision of recommendations for future use of PlumeStop®, future applied injection meth-
ods and monitoring tools.

croorganisms gain different chemical energy yields; depending on which electron acceptor they uti-
lise (T H Jørgensen et al., 2005). Gibb’s free energy quantifies the energy yield (see Table 2).

2.4 Volatile Fatty Acids 
In reductive dechlorination of CE in the subsurface, there must be hydrogen present to act as an 
electron donor. One process that produces hydrogen, is fermentation of organic matter (Wiedemeier 
et al., 1999). Volatile fatty acids (VFAs) are byproducts of the natural fermentation of organic mat-
ter where short-chain fatty acids (SCFA) and hydrogen (H2) are released. When the acids undergo 
secondary fermentation, additional electron donors are generated (Christensen et al., 2000; Lee, Bae 
and McCarty, 2007; Weatherill et al., 2018). The acids released are classified as weak acids based 
on their dissociation constant (pKa) (see Table 3) (Nazaroff and Alvarez-Cohen, 2001). VFAs can 
also be used as direct electron donors in order to stimulate natural degradation or bioaugmentation 
of CE. This includes substrates such as lactic acid, that when fermented releases hydrogen (H2) and 
SCFA such as butyric, propionic and acetic acid (He et al., 2002; Aulenta et al., 2007; Lee, Bae and 
McCarty, 2007). Fermentation processes entail the same substrate acting as both electron donor and 
acceptor (Wiedemeier et al., 1999; T H Jørgensen et al., 2005). Dehalogenating microorganisms 
can use both acetate and H2 as electron donors in the reductive dechlorination of CEs (Lee, Bae and 
McCarty, 2007). The hydrogen released from these reactions can be used in reductive dechlorina-

3.1 Distribution and Injection of Activated Carbon 
This section presents different types of activated carbon (AC) and their flow characteristics. Fur-
thermore, injection methods for introducing AC and amendment products into the subsurface are 
presented.  
3.1.1 Activated Carbon 
Activated carbon (AC) is a compound, which after a two-step heated process consists solely of car-
bon (Nazaroff and Alvarez-Cohen, 2001). AC is nonpolar and the large surface area of the porous 
media results in a high sorption capacity for organic contaminants (Nazaroff and Alvarez-Cohen, 
2001; Hale et al., 2017). When AC is co-applied with other amendments such as electron donor and 
bacterial culture the processes of sorption followed by degradation are enabled (US EPA, 2018). 
Various AC products exist either in powder, granular or liquid form (GAC, PAC and LAC). The 
latter also referred to as colloidal AC. Particle size of GAC range from 0.2 to 5 mm (Desotec, 
2007), PAC ≤ 75 µm (Desotec, 2019) and LAC ≤ 2.5 µm (Mackenzie et al., 2012; Regenesis, 2016).

3.1.2 Flow Characteristics of Powdered or Liquid Activated Carbon 
Injecting AC includes injecting a suspension. Suspensions consist of two phases a liquid phase and 
a solid phase. If the solid phase stays dispersed it is a suspension (Vassoughi, 1999). 
PAC Lewis (2014) investigated the flow characteristics of suspended PAC injection by direct push 
injection in a coarse grained porous medium. When PAC is injected, the larger carbon particles can 
become trapped in small voids. The PAC flow characteristics very near the injection tip are domi-
nated by grain displacement flow. Grain displacement flow is where the flow of the injected sus-
pension displaces soil grains and creates new pathways (see Figure 2A). Radial flow describes the 
flow moving along a right angel to the injection tip displacing liquid; however, not the grains (see 
Figure 2B). As the PAC-particles travel further away, radial flow dominates. Eventually the veloci-
ty of the PAC-particles will decrease and settle into the aquifer. Furthermore, Lewis (2014) de-
scribes possible events that can occur if PAC settle and plug the soil pores and decrease the hydrau-
lic conductivity locally as a result. The radial flow can be compromised causing the PAC-particle to 
pack around the injection. Hence, the designed volume is not covered with PAC-particles. Second-
ly, too much carbon injected at one location cause increase in pressure. This forces the PAC-
particles to create new pathways, which results in uneven distribution. Lastly, the groundwater flow 
will bypass the areas plugged with PAC-particles due to lowered hydraulic conductivity. As a re-
sult, the CEs cannot be adsorbed to the PAC-particles.  
 
LAC LAC particles have a smaller particle size compared to PAC, which will increase the ability to 
avoid entrapment in smaller voids. Surfactants are often added to make the particles stay in suspen-
sion and avoid agglomeration (Vassoughi, 1999; Georgi et al., 2015). Opposite PAC, LAC with a 
surface coating is designed to overcome settlement due to decrease in velocity post-injection and in-
stead settle into the aquifer due to lost effect of coating (Regenesis, 2015a).

3.1.3 Description of Applicable Injection Methods 
Direct injection, is where the amendment products as a specified volume are mixed prior to injec-
tion and often include water from an external source. The volume is injected directly into the sub-
surface, which causes corresponding displacement (Rosansky, Condit and Sirabian, 2013). This is 
possible with LAC (see Figure 3). 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 3 depicts direct injection, introducing amendment products including LAC or solutions di-
rectly into the subsurface. The amendments flow with the groundwater, making it possible to moni-
tor the amendments in downstream monitoring wells (ISRRIWR et al., 2009; Rosansky, Condit and 
Sirabian, 2013); however, LAC such as PlumeStop® will stop migrating with groundwater and set-
tle after around 3 months (Regenesis, 2015g). This injection can be done through permanent wells 
or temporary injection points (Rosansky, Condit and Sirabian, 2013).  
Injection Wells ISRRIWR et al. (2009) has investigated subsurface injection of in situ remedial re-
agents. They conclude that if injection wells are applied their screens must intersect only the TTA. 
Furthermore, that a proper injection well design must include filter pack, annular sealing and grout-
ing features (Fetter, 1992a; ISRRIWR et al., 2009). Fetter (1992) describes that keeping detailed 
records of the process is regarded as sound professional practice. Installing wire-wrapped screens 
instead of slotted screens is recommended as they provide a greater surface area flow; however, 
they are more expensive (Rosansky, Condit and Sirabian, 2013). Moreover, employing pressure 
gauges is important to achieve the necessary pressure and careful flow rate control into each well is 
important (Rosansky, Condit and Sirabian, 2013).  
Direct Push Injection Technology (DPI) Rosansky et al. (2013) describe DPI as common practice 
when injecting amendment products into an aquifer. DPI pushes a rod through the formation using 
static hydraulic pressure or a dynamic percussion hammer. DPI is low cost compared to installing 
injection wells and facilitates targeting amendments into discrete depth intervals. Limitations of 
DPI include that the screen can be clogged and smeared resulting in a non-uniform distribution.

Spin Injection Spin injection is developed by the company Injectis as a method, which overcomes

Rosansky et al. (2013) has investigated different types of injection methods and have provided the

use. Only these methods will be described and evaluated with respect to the degree of applicability 
in Table 5, together with a classification of the site (Industrivej 2), which is properly presented in 
Chapter 5. The classification in Table 5 indicate that DPI is better suited for the range of hydraulic 
conductivities at Industrivej 2 compared to injection wells; however, injection wells are indicated 
more suitable for the depth of the TTA. This indicates that DPI is the most suitable in respect to a 
proper distribution in a sand aquifer with relatively low permeability. DPI does not involve remov-
ing aquifer material out of the subsurface as implementing injection wells does, instead DPI pushes 
a rod through the formation; hence, more sensitive to greater depth and changing subsurface geolo-
gy. Therefore there is a higher certainty of reaching the desired depth when establishing injection 
wells.  
3.2 Review - Tools for Evaluating Distribution of Liquid Activated Carbon 
This section presents a review focusing on applied methods to investigate the distribution of LAC. 
Distribution monitoring is closely connected to performance monitoring. Performance monitoring 
evaluates whether and where the products work accordingly, thus indicate the distribution of the 
products and is therefore briefly described. Table 6 provides an overview of the case studies, in-
cluding the targeted contaminant, remediation technique, injection methods and the applied moni-
toring methods. Various AC-products are included: products combined with zero valent iron (ZVI) 
and AC products co-applied with or without bioaugmentation. Furthermore, two different AC-
products are applied (PAC and LAC), which distribute differently. The case studies provide 
knowledge on monitoring methods that can be applied at the field site. 
Performance Monitoring US EPA (2018) suggests the following tools for performance monitoring 
of in situ AC technologies: redox, VFA, compound specific isotope analysis (CSIA) and specific 
degraders. Specific degraders are important, when it comes to assessment of the biodegradations. If 
specific degraders are injected, mapping of the microorganisms can support evaluation of the distri-
bution caused by the performed injection. Birnstingl et al. (2016) also mentions an increase in dis-
solved iron post-injection of LAC and HRC; hence, monitoring of iron can support the distribution 
of LAC and HRC. VFA give indication of donor distribution and fermentation stage. Lastly, com-

toring less relatable to the distribution of LAC and amendment products. 
Distribution Monitoring Distribution monitoring of LAC is necessary in order to guarantee that a 
successful transport to the TTA has been achieved (e.g. treatment grid or PRB). If spacing between 
the injection points has been too big or distribution has been heterogeneous then a PRB will not 
have been created and contaminants will continue to spread in areas where LAC is not coating the 
sediment. PAC is applied by trenching or hydraulic fracturing and more easily monitored by visual 
detection (Redell et al., 2011). Visual detection of LAC on sediments has been attempted using 
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM). Birnstingl et al. (2016) (Regenesis) used SEM to document 
PlumeStop® coating of sand particles. This method was under further development at Nano-
Science Centre Copenhagen University (KU) in order to enable the methods to be used at field 
scale. The methods from KU for determining PlumeStop® quantities in a soil sample, were initiated 
but not developed to completion, and they have stopped further research in this. The methods in-
clude SEM, Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDXS) and thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) 
(Tobler, 2018). Figure 4 and Figure 5 present a comparison of SEM images produced by Regenesis 
and KU respectively. On the Regenesis images, the sand particles without PlumeStop® appear 
smooth compared to the two images coated in PlumeStop®; however, it is important to note that the 
scale size of the two pictures are not comparable. The SEM produced by KU used sediment from 
the study site and a concentration of PlumeStop® representative of that applied in the field (see 
Figure 5). Initial KU results found that PlumeStop® dimensions are too similar to fine-grained ma-
terials to be identified in SEM and that EDXS was non-conclusive. Comparing SEM images from 
KU and from Regenesis is difficult, thus the exact sediment size and PlumeStop® concentrations 
used to coat sand particles by Regenesis are unknown. All methods need further development 
(Tobler, 2018).  
Core sampling has been applied to determine the radius of influence (ROI) of LAC (Fan, Gilbert 
and Fox, 2017; McGregor, 2018) (see Table 6). The core samples have been used to visually detect 
whether LAC has coated the sediment. However, at some field sites LAC may only be visibly de-
tectable in areas where too much LAC is present; thus an indication of unsatisfactory distribution 
(Busch et al., 2015). Therefore, other methods to determine the amount and presence of 
PlumeStop® is under development. McGregor (2018) used an increase of TOC and foc to indicate 
that PlumeStop® was present in the soil sample. Leth (2018) also assessed TOC as a method for de-
tecting PlumeStop®, by measuring TOC in an experimental setup before and after a trial injection. 
However, it was not possible to distinguish between PlumeStop® and the carbon in the sediment 
due to the similarity between the carbon content of PlumeStop® and the sediment. A new sorption 
test has successfully been developed by Researcher Annika Sidelmann Fjordbøge at DTU Environ-
ment, to determine distribution of PlumeStop® based on sorption (Fjordbøge et al., 2018). The 
method determined the sorption isotherms for PCE and TCE on PlumeStop®, including sediment 
from the field site. Comparison with sorption to aquifer material allows for quantification of LAC 
(i.e. PlumeStop®) in sediment samples. Thereby, LAC distribution can be assessed in combination 
with core sampling, by adding a known amount of TCE or PCE to quantify the adsorbed. The 
method is new and not yet tested at the field site.

Groundwater sampling can be employed to monitor and evaluate the distribution of LAC. An im-
mediate decrease in contaminant concentrations is a direct indication of LAC present. The sampled 
monitoring wells need to be representative of the treated aquifer. Preferential pathways between in-
jection points and monitoring wells (Lewis, 2014), and displacement of contaminant from injection 
can make the monitoring well unrepresentative (Birnstingl et al., 2016; Fan, Gilbert and Fox, 2017). 
The concern is that a monitoring well where AC has been visually detected may not be representa-
tive for the aquifer as well as may be subject to extreme carbon loading (US EPA, 2018). When this 
is the case Fan et al. (2017) suggest installing new monitoring wells within close proximity to the 
affected well. Under construction of a new well soil samples can be collected and distribution can 
be determined as well as determining whether the initial well is representative. Heterogeneities in 
the geology can also cause preferential pathways (Busch et al., 2015). Depth discrete sampling of-
fers a high vertical sampling interval. For the benefit of determining ROI it can be implemented in 
locations with varying distances to the injection points. This can indicate the ROI based on sorption 
when reduced concentrations are observed.  
Borehole logging can be applied as a monitoring method when injection wells have been used for 
injection of LAC and amendment products. Borehole logging is a non-invasive method to investi-
gate wells. It consists of lowering a sonde, which logs physical parameters that can be related to the 
well, the surrounding geology or the properties of the fluids. The sonde data are given as a function 
of depth as the sonde is lowered or hoisted out of the well (Telford, Geldart and Sheriff, 1990; 
Kearey, Brooks and Hill, 2002). The method is generally used for petroleum exploration and to in-
vestigate the geology surrounding a borehole in relation to drinking water resources (Telford, 
Geldart and Sheriff, 1990), and has not previously been used for evaluating LAC distribution. Ad-
vantages of borehole logging in injection wells include determining the construction of the well in 
order to assess whether there could be preferential pathways for the LAC and donor to other areas 
than the TTA. Additionally, gain knowledge on local changes in the geology, which will have af-
fected the distribution.

4.1 PlumeStop® - Liquid Activated Carbon (LAC) 
PlumeStop® Liquid Activated Carbon TM is a commercially available product. The 1-2 µm AC par-
ticles are suspended as colloidal, where approximately 4 % is particles and 75 % is water (see Table 
7) (Regenesis, 2015e, 2015g; Birnstingl et al., 2016; Regenesis and Leonard, 2018). The particles 
surface is coated, altering their surface charge and keeping them in suspension, which should enable 
PlumeStop® to be distributed widely in the subsurface (Birnstingl et al., 2016). The particles 
should remain in suspension for 1-3 months (Regenesis, 2015g). The aim of PlumeStop® is to in-
stantly remove contaminants from groundwater by adsorption. When co-applied with an electron 
donor/acceptor and bacterial cultures degradation of contaminant should also occur (Regenesis, 
2015a; Birnstingl et al., 2016). PlumeStop® creates a greater surface area for contact between the 
bacteria and contaminant, which leads to the destruction of contaminants. Destruction frees up the 
sorption sites and thereby, regenerates PlumeStop®, which increases the longevity of the remedia-
tion technique (Regenesis, 2015e). Dependent on which amendment product PlumeStop® is co-
applied with it can remediate various organic contaminants (i.e. CEs or petroleum hydrocarbons) 
(US EPA, 2018). PlumeStop® enables low pressure injection, which ought to avoid extreme carbon 
loading from occurring (Regenesis, 2015e). This is most suited geology with higher permeability.

4.2 Hydrogen Release Compound (HRC) – Donor 
Three donors are injected: Hydrogen Release Compound (HRC), Hydrogen Release Compound 
Primer (HRC-Primer) and Extended Hydrogen Release Compound (HRC-X) all commercially 
available products by Regenesis. HRC, HRC-Primer and HRC-X are designed specifically for en-
hanced, in situ anaerobic bioremediation of CEs in groundwater. HRC is a viscous poly-lactate ester 
material, which when hydrated become available for microbial degradation producing release of 
hydrogen in a time frame from 18-24 months after application (Rambøll and Regenesis, 2018). 
HRC-Primer is thinner and less viscous (see Table 8) and releases after hydration lactic acid result-
ing in production of hydrogen over a period of several weeks (Rambøll and Regenesis, 2018). 
HRC-X is the most viscous of the three donors. It is designed to release lactic acid for periods of up 
to five years (Rambøll and Regenesis, 2018). The difference in viscosity is what makes the three 
donors release hydrogen at different rates. Bacteria can more easily degrade the donor with the low-
est viscosity; hence HRC-Primer will provide lactic acid faster and as a result fully consumed first 
(Regenesis, no date). The most viscous donor HRC-X will be degraded slower, which explains the 
longer time frame for releasing lactic acid (Regenesis, no date). All three donors are denser than 
water and are stated to have a relative density ranging from 1.1 to 1.3 (Regenesis, 2015d, 2015c; 
Regensis, 2015).

5.3 Applied Injection Method at Industrivej 2 
This section is based on Rambøll & Regenesis (2018). The injection design consists of 12 injection 
points creating a PRB with the dimensions of 30 m in length, 9 m in depth (12-21 m bgl) and a 
width between 2.5 and 3 m. Regenesis were responsible for the injection of PlumeStop® and 
amendment products. Regenesis found spin injection as the optimal method for injection into a deep 
sand aquifer (12 - 21 m bgl ≈ 30 - 21 m DVR90), since this method is expected to be able to 
achieve depth discrete distribution without creating new preferential pathways; hence, an even dis-
tribution. This method was attempted at injection point I12 on the 1st October 2017. The injection 
tip was damaged and unable to drill further than 8 m bgl. This was followed by two attempts at I7 
and I8, reaching 4 m bgl. The failing of the spin injection was most likely due to the geology from 
43 - 30 m DVR90, which consist of a clay formation with sand and silt lenses (see Figure 7). If con-
sidering spin injection for further use in Denmark, this is a problem as this type of geology is repre-
sentative for typical Danish conditions. Furthermore, large cobble/gravel were encountered at this 
depth, which completely sheared of the tip of the spin injector. As a result, an auger with a diameter 
of around 75 mm was applied the next day to ‘pre- drill’ to around 12 m bgl through the clay layer 
prior to spin injection at I12. Hereafter the spin injection reached 13 m bgl and injected around 50 L 
of PlumeStop®; however, the injection tip became blocked twice and the rods had to be withdrawn 
from the ground, the tip cleared and the point re-drilled. The third day the spin injection reached 16 
m bgl and around 100 L of PlumeStop® was injected before it was deemed too difficult to advance 
any deeper.  After 3 days of attempting to use spin injection it was concluded that the target treat-
ment depth of 21 m bgl would not be reached due to the geology. As a result, the second option of 
injection via permanent injection wells was applied. The specific design of the injection wells is 
shown in Figure 8.

The injection method consisted of a double packer system containing two inflatable packers (Figure

diluted mixture of PlumeStop® was mixed (see Figure 9A), and then injected using the diaphragm

tions and was injected subsequently. After the injection of PlumeStop® and BDI+ the system was 
flushed with 10-20 L in order to force the amendments into the formation. This process was repeat-
ed until the bottom of the well, and then donor was injected from the bottom and up to 12 m bgl.

6.4 Methods for Cleaning Boreholes 
A method for cleaning boreholes was developed but only tested in one I-well (I6). Materials used 
for cleaning boreholes were a pressure washer, spray nozzle and a MP1 pump. The pump was low-
ered to 11.5 m bgl, just above screen opening, and with a pumping rate to match the pressure wash-
er. The pressure washer was then lowered up and down the borehole. The spray nozzle directed the 
water to spray the sides of the borehole. The duration and extracted volume of water was moni-
tored. The well was visually inspected before and after cleaning. The method successfully cleans 
the well to a certain degree. However, some hard donor residue remains in the plastic pipes.  
6.5 Packer Test 
A borehole packer test was conducted. This includes placing an inflatable packer below the area of 
interest. Secondly, the area above the packer was filled with water. Lastly, it was observed whether 
the newly established water level stayed constant over time. A water level drop indicates that the 
plain pipe is not sealed properly. The applied packer was a pipe plug (Plugy, long model series 
L710) with a minimum and maximum diameter of 40 and 70 mm respectively. A packer is well-
known equipment for investigating boreholes (Brassington and Walthall, 1985). Figure 10 shows 
the materials used at the field site. Prior to conducting the packer test water was pumped from 15 m 
bgl from the I-well. This was in order to avoid disturbing the aquifer with aerobic tap water, since 
once the packer is released to terminate the test the water cannot be retrieved. The method was 
trialled in I6 and a full packer test were conducted in I2, I4, I7 and I9. The plugy was placed 8 m 
bgl and after the pipe was filled with water, the change in water level was monitored after 15 and 45 
minutes.

6.6 Borehole Logging 
Borehole logging was conducted in all injection wells (except I4) and M5-1 on the 2nd January 2019 
(393 days after injection) (see Figure 11). The description of the logging programme was written 
based on correspondence with Rambøll in relation to the offer of conducting the borehole logging 
as well as verbal and email correspondence with Gert Andersen, Senior Engineer at Rambøll. 
Borehole logging makes use of physical properties of geology and the groundwater in order to log 
differences occurring at different depths. Logging should be done as the sonde is hoisted out of the 
well. The speed in which the sonde is pulled out of the well is typically slower and more constant 
resulting in a more accurate log (Telford, Geldart and Sheriff, 1990; Kearey, Brooks and Hill, 
2002).  It is an advantage to use several sondes when borehole logging as they may supplement 
each other (Telford, Geldart and Sheriff, 1990). Some of the fundamental assumptions of borehole 
logging are that the borehole is centered and does not vary in diameter. Alternations in this can have 
geometrical implications and may affect results. Furthermore, it is assumed that the borehole casing 
are made of homogeneous materials and that the variations being logged by sondes (ex. BHTV) re-
flect variations in the surrounding formation.  
Limited sondes were available since the equipment normally is used in monitoring wells in drinking 
water aquifers. Furthermore, some sondes were excluded due to the 63 mm diameter of the I-wells; 
the inner diameter was approximately 55 mm. All sondes applied are described in this section. The 
sondes that were considered but not possible to apply are described in Appendix C. Table 15 pre-
sents an overview of the methods investigated and applied.  
Prior to borehole logging a dummy log with dimensions 237 x 3.8 cm was tested in each borehole. 
The dummy log was made of metal, and designed with sharp edges in order to clear the borehole of 
any hard donor residue, which may inhibit borehole logging sondes from entering the borehole.

NGRS The natural gamma sonde (NGRS) estimate the formations content of unstable isotopes, 
which include 40K, 238U and 232Th (potassium, uranium and thorium); hence, NGRS measure the 
overall radioactivity (French Oil and Gas Industry Association, 1986; Mwenifumbo, 1993). Particu-
larly the unstable isotopes of potassium (40K) are emitted by shales and clays and not by gravel or 
sand (Kearey, Brooks and Hill, 2002). NGRS gives the gamma radiation in cps (counts per second), 
which reflects the clay content in the surrounding geology of a borehole. 
BHTV An acoustic televiewer (BHTV) uses an ultrasonic transmitter to emit an ultrasonic beam, 
which the borehole walls reflect and then is detected by an ultrasonic receiver in the sonde (Telford, 
Geldart and Sheriff, 1990). The time it takes for the reflected signal to reach the receiver is depend-
ent on the material and diameter of the well casing (Horn, 2003). The BHTV obtains logs that are 
relative in hardness value of the borehole. Therefore, when measuring the hardness of the inside 
plastic pipe (pipe) and outside well casing (borehole) it is important to have certain knowledge of 
the well in order to assess the BHTV log. BHTV can only obtain values when placed below the wa-
ter table. The BHTV creates a 360 degree ultrasonic image of the hardness of the well casing. It 
may detect fractures in open boreholes (Srinivas et al., 2018). From this, assessment of whether the 
casing is consisting of sand/gravel, bentonite or cement is made. The method assumes that the di-
ameter of the borehole is constant. The orientation of the sonde is kept on track by a magnetometer 
(Horn, 2003).

ment of the formation; hence the reading is a mean value, where it is not possible to determine the

6.7 ICP-OES Analyses of Particle Samples from Transect 4 
Investigation of black precipitates observed with visual inspection in M-wells (M5-2, M6-2, M7-1, 
M7-2 and M8-2) in Transect 4 was performed. This was in order to determine whether the black 
precipitate was PlumeStop® or something else such as iron sulfide. The precipitate samples were 
collected in 250 ml PEHD plastic bottles. A tube was placed in each screen in Transect 4. The par-
ticles from these black tubes were sampled and stored under aerobic conditions for one day. These 
particles where then moved to a glass tray and dried at 60 ºC over night in an oven. The dried sam-

extracting iron sulfide formations. The mixture applied in this study, which is a (3+1) nitric-

alysed in an Inductively Coupled Plasma Optical Emission Spectrometer (ICP-OES). The samples 
are exposed to 9000 degrees and the intensity measured in cps (counts per second) for each wave-
length. The minerals used for comparison can be seen in Table 16. For more detailed information

7.2.1 Interpretation of NVOC Breakthrough Curves 
 
 
  
The sampled NVOC concentrations are used to estimate BTC in order to determine the pore water 
velocities (m/year) and the longitudinal horizontal dispersivity (m). These values will contribute to 
understanding the distribution of donor downstream from the injection wells. Two interpretations, 
which combined are applied to interpret the distribution of donor based on the observed changes in 
NVOC are presented and applied to create the BTC.  
Interpretation 1 The different properties of the three HRC-donors injected at Industrivej 2 are pre-
sented in Section 4.2. Figure 18 shows how BTC is estimated based on NVOC. The reasoning be-
hind the interpretation of BTC is the following: Bacteria metabolize the low viscosity materials 
more quickly. As a result HRC-Primer start VFA release almost instantly. Moreover, HRC-Primer 
is expected to release lactic acid over several weeks. In a one-year time frame this will result in an 
instantaneous release. HRC and HRC-X is expected to give a more slow release of lactic acid in a 
timeframe of 18 months and 5 years respectively. The total release of lactic acid from all three HRC 
donors is expected to result in a continuous release. NVOC concentration is expected to correspond 
to the release of VFA (Rambøll, 2018a). 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Interpretation 2 The release from the donor mass distributed upstream of the I-well later moves 
downstream and causes the VFA release from HRC-Primer to increase the pulse duration. In Figure 
19, this scenario is explained with the pink line as the donor pushed the furthest upstream and with 
the green line as the donor pushed the furthest downstream closer to the M-well; hence, the donor is

7.3 Distribution of Donor Based on BTC  
The BTC have led to an understanding of how the donors are distributed in Transect 2. The inter-
pretation is shown by illustrations of Transect 2 and Transect 3 in Figure 26 and Figure 27 respec-
tively. The distribution of donors can be divided into three different zones, which indicate uneven 
donor distribution. 
The blue area represents locations where distribution of all three HRC-donors has been ideal. In the 
blue areas a long-term release of VFA is presented achieving NVOC concentrations, which remain 
high for longer periods of time, ensuring conditions ideal for reductive dechlorination. This is indi-
cated by concentrations above 200 mg/L starting from day 71, which stay above 200 mg/L at day 
271 and indicate a continuous release of NVOC. 
The yellow area represents the most permeable locations where the flow is expected to be higher 
than 10 m/year, resulting in the HRC-Primer having already passed Transect 2 and arrived to Tran-
sect 3 (see Figure 26 and Figure 27). This explains why the NVOC concentrations are low but 
above 20 mg/L in Transect 2 71 days post-injection and already detected in Transect 3 in concentra-
tions above 400 mg/L after 271 days post-injection. Since the NVOC concentration in Transect 2 
does not stay above 200 mg/L less HRC-X is expected to be present upstream from this location. 
Uneven distribution leaving areas without HRC-X cause areas with instantaneous release, which 
does not allow the designed 5-year longevity of the donors within the PRB. 
The red areas represent low permeable zones, which during injection received high concentrations 
of NVOC. The BTC show that estimates in this area achieved pore water velocities higher than the 
slug test estimated. It is assessed that the high pore water velocity is a cause of the injection itself. 
This explains why a comparable increase in concentration is not yet observed downstream; hence, 
the slug tests obtained velocities are better estimates in this area. Resulting in the NVOC front as-
sessed to be located between Transect 2 and Transect 3. The pore water velocities estimated when 
forcing the high NVOC to be caused by injection found in the red area a pore water velocity = 3.65 
m/year. Applying this rate predicts the comparable high concentration to travel 5 m in around 500 
days. High concentrations in the area are present at day 71 in Transect 2 and there is 5 m between 
Transect 2 and 3. Hence, the NVOC concentration is expected to increase in Transect 3 downstream 
the red areas around day 571 post-injection. Furthermore, the initial high NVOC concentration at 
day 71 post-injection declines to one sixth of the original, indicating that the majority of the NVOC 
concentration was produced by VFA release from HRC-Primer.

7.4 Correlation Between NVOC and pH 
The contour plots and depth discrete samples show a correlation between NVOC and pH. This cor-
relation is investigated with a scatterplot and the spearman correlation test. pH and NVOC results 
from screens in all monitoring rounds and depth discrete samples 256 days post injection are in-
cluded. Figure 28 presents a negative correlation between NVOC and pH. This was expected since 
Rambøll (2018a) determined based on results 71 days post injection that VFAs account for up to 80 
% of the NVOC (VFA are weak acids) (see Section 2.4). The pH and NVOC data have a normal 
and non-normal distribution respectively (see Appendix F). As a result the spearman correlation test 
is applied and found a correlation value = -0.72. This correlation is significant as the p-value is < 
0.05. By applying linear regression the relationship between NVOC and pH is determined to be a 
log normal distribution; hence a relative change in NVOC leads to an absolute change in pH (see 
Appendix F). This was expected since pH is a logarithmic scale. This indicates that the outliers are 
affected by something else than the increased NVOC value. The four outliers are from a distance ≤ 
0.5 m from the nearest I-well and the pH is around 5.5. The pure donors have a pH from 2-3 and are 
distributed from 0.1 to 0.27 m from the I-wells. This may cause the further decrease in pH.

The Borehole Casing The acoustic televiewer (BHTV) measured inside the plastic pipe (on the 
way down) and the borehole (on the way up). The measurements are relative to each well, distin-
guishing between hard and soft material. In order to correctly determine what materials the changes 
in hardness corresponds to a certain degree of knowledge about the well construction is needed. 
When comparing the image of the pipe and image of borehole obtained by BHTV for I11, the areas 
with increased hardness are the same for both logs (column 6 and 9 in Figure 39). On the inside, 
BHTV shows the hard white range is present between 9-10 m bgl and again from 11-11.5 m bgl. 
The screen is also clearly visible as vertical stripes and the solid plastic in between the screen open-
ings is within the white (hard) range. These areas of increased hardness corresponds well with the 
BHTV for the borehole and hardness curves, which also has areas of increased hardness at these 
depths (column 10, 11 and 12 in Figure 39). This pattern does not correspond to the expected bore-
hole construction (see Figure 8). The 3D image of the inner borehole shows a systematic increase in 
diameter of the boreholes. Figure 40 shows the comparison of all 3D images of the boreholes in-
cluded in the logging programme. The hardness and expansion of the pipe occurs 9-10 m bgl (33.5-
34.5 m DVR90) with the exception of I3 and I12. Interpretation of the BHTV is made more com-
plex due to the increased hardness inside the plastic piping, and that this corresponds to areas where 
the diameter of the pipe appears to increase (see Figure 39 column 8 and 9). There are two possible 
interpretations:  
1. The diameter of the pipe has expanded; if the diameter of the plastic pipe has been altered at 
certain depths this affects the fundamental assumptions of the acoustic televiewer, which as-
sumes the pipe and borehole have a constant diameter.  
 
2. The increased hardness of the borehole is causing the BHTV to show an increased hardness 
and increased diameter of the pipe.  
Arguments supporting interpretation 1 include that a change in diameter within the plastic pipe will 
also affect the results of the BHTV for the borehole (compare Figure 40 and Figure 43) since the 
fundamental assumption of a constant diameter is not withheld. Interpretation 1 is discarded due to 
the following; a calliper log was not included in the logging programme and is instead based on the 
hardness curve from BHTV imaging. The increase in diameter is systematic (see Figure 40), and if 
the expansion has occurred in relation to the injection method it is assumed that it would have 
caused the change throughout the borehole in 1 m intervals. Additionally, the 63 mm boreholes are 
pressure tested by the manufacturers to withstand greater strain than the 8 bar applied by the double 
packer (Rotek, 2019). For instance, BHTV estimates an 8 mm variation in diameter between 9-12 m 
bgl in I11 (Figure 39), which is much greater than the 0.7 mm tolerance of the pipe (Kjeldsen 
11/02-2019). Furthermore, when comparing with the visual inspection (Section 7.5.1) no indication 
of an expansion of the pipe in this interval is shown.

can be gained about the distribution of PlumeStop® and donor from logging the physical parame-

trend variations between natural gamma and formation conductivity is assessed.   
To investigate this, boreholes M5, I11 and I6 have been chosen to illustrate the tendencies from the 
borehole logging. The natural gamma and formation conductivity for M5, I11 and I6 can be seen in 
Figure 43. When examining M5, deviations between the two logs occur at depths; 2-4 m bgl, 8 m 
bgl and 12 m bgl. At these depths, the formation conductivity does not follow the same trend as the 
gamma log. Conductivity increases between 2-4 m bgl without any clear increase in cps. Compar-

as a section of clay. A decrease in gamma log occurs at 8 m bgl, which is precisely the location of 
the sand lens according to the geological profile. The sand lens is not clearly visible in the for-

and Sheriff, 1990). Lastly, a notable increase in induction also occurs in M5 around 12 m bgl with-

later sand. For comparison, the gamma and formation conductivity for I11 appears to correspond

the increase in conductivity to be caused by a build-up of donor and PlumeStop® between 11-13 m 
bgl (30.32-32.32 m DVR90).

Conductivity and Porosity The aim of this section is to assess whether the injection of 
PlumeStop® and donor has made any systematic alterations to the aquifer and whether Archie’s 
law can be applied to borehole logging data from an aquifer with amendment products.  
Since borehole logging, was only conducted post-injection, the bulk conductivity (σbulk) and for-
mation factor (F) pre-injection were estimated based on porosities and electrical water conductivi-
ties (σw). The available porosities from Frederiksen and Rosenberg, (2018), were determined from 
sediment samples from Transect 1 and 2 in the sand aquifer. The σw from baseline water samples 
were used from screens located where the sediment samples were taken. The water conductivities 
varied between 85-130 mS/m. See Appendix I for the spatial variation in water conductivity. 
Archie’s law was applied to the porosities (Equation 6) to determine the F and subsequently σbulk 
predicted for cementation factor 1.3, 2.5 and the average value of 1.9 (Equation 7). The correlation 
between σw and the predicted σbulk are shown in Figure 46. The ranges of F are 2.91-3.51, 4.75-6.29 
and 7.70-11.23 for m=1.3, 1.9 and 2.5 respectively. The two lower values for cementation factor 
seem most suitable for a sand aquifer. The inverse of the slopes seen in Figure 46 are equivalent to 
the formation factor (Balbarini et al., 2018). This results in formation factors of 3.15, 5.34 and 9.04 
for varying m factor.  
F has previously been determined at a Danish field site, Grindsted landfill and stream, with similar 
geology: a mildly heterogeneous sandy aquifer. Two formation factors were determined for Grind-
sted landfill and stream, 4.5 and 5.1 respectively. Balbarini et al., (2018) states that a fixed for-
mation factor of 4.7 is representative for the entire area. The range reported for F for unconsolidated 
sand from samples taken from sand/silt floodplain sediments in Weller et al. (2015) are from 2.93-
8.66 and for sand and gravels from 8.20-14.62. These are the ranges that could be expected based 
on bulk conductivity measurements from borehole logging. Based on these assumptions a constant 
F factor of 3.15 is assumed suitable for the Industrivej, 2. Moreover, it will be applied to estimate 
the changes in the aquifer caused by the injection of PlumeStop® and HRC-donor.  
Firstly, borehole logs for formation conductivity and pore water conductivity was used to determine 
F in areas where the data from the logs overlapped (see Appendix I). Except for I5, I11 and I6 the 
overlap was limited. Based on the two borehole logs the values of F were determined to range be-
tween 2.18-12.38. There was no linear relation. The PlumeStop® and donor residue in the injection 
wells affected the sonde measuring water conductivity (TCME).

8 Overall Assessment of Future Recommendations  
This chapter will present an overall assessment of the distribution of PlumeStop® and donor at In-
dustrivej 2 along with future recommendations.  
8.1 Distribution of Amendment Products and Injection Method 
PlumeStop® Uneven distribution of PlumeStop® via injection wells is observed. This is based on 
the observation of PlumeStop® in some groundwater samples from M-wells within and outside the 
barrier. This indicates uneven PlumeStop® distribution since visual detection is only possible if the 
AC loading is above the intended. Uneven distribution due to preferential flow paths is likely due to 
the dependence on geological heterogeneities, which are more difficult to counteract in injection 
wells. Close to one I-well a depth discrete sample indicated sorption by PlumeStop® at 0.3 m 
downstream of the respective I-well. At another location depth a descriptive sample 0.3 m down-
stream an I-well did not indicate sorption due to the presence of PlumeStop®. This concludes that 
the observed ROI achieved through the applied I-wells is varying and ≤ 0.3 m, which is below the 
intended ROI of 1.7 m. This is supported by the difference in appearance of the I-wells, which was 
made clear by visual investigation. Furthermore, conclusions based on borehole logging indicate a 
vertical variation in the distribution of PlumeStop®. At the top of the sand aquifer an increase in 
formation conductivity was determined in 7 out of 12 boreholes. This increase in formation conduc-
tivity deviated from the natural gamma log. This indicates that the increased conductivity was not 
related to geology and coincides with the high conductivity by the surface coating of PlumeStop®. 
Furthermore, data from monitoring rounds presented as contour plots determines that sorption by a 
complete a PRB is not occurring. This conclusion about the PlumeStop® distribution is consistent 
with flaws in the injection design, which have been discovered post-injection. At the field site the 
injection of PlumeStop®, HRC and bacterial cultures through I-wells was done at too low pressures 
(<1 bar). This pressure were suitable for spin injection, but not injection through injection wells 
(Rambøll and Regenesis, 2018).  
HRC Donor The distribution of HRC donors (i.e. NVOC) within the PRB was assessed via contour 
plots and depth discrete sampling. NVOC concentrations in Transect 2 showed spatial variations. 
NVOC was found most dominant in deeper screens in M-wells within the PRB. The donors are 
denser than water and injected bottom-up, which may have caused preferential pathways down the 
gravel pack causing more, spread in donor the deepest part of the TTA. Depth discrete sampling 
gives a clearer indication of the vertical variation of the donor distribution, by capturing local peaks 
in NVOC and pH concentrations.  
Transport processes The transport processes at Industrivej 2 have been assessed by BTC based on 
NVOC concentrations. Three types of zones were distinguishable at the site based on the BTC: 
1. Low permeability layers where NVOC concentration increases after 71 days without ad-
vancing to downstream M-wells 351 days post-injection.  
2. High permeability zones where NVOC concentrations present an instantaneous release and 
where NVOC concentrations after 165 days are transported beyond Transect 2.  
3. Areas that showed good distribution of all donors and signs of continued release of NVOC 
(this zone included all screens in M1).  
==PAGE:89==
79 
 
In the highly permeable layers presenting instantaneous release, the NVOC concentration decreases 
271 days post-injection in one screen to below baseline concentration and several screens show rap-
id NVOC decrease. This indicates that an uneven distribution of donor causes some parts of the bar-
rier to be without continuous NVOC release, which results in NVOC concentrations equal to base-
line concentrations. Hence, it is concluded that the distribution of HRC-X is not even. This is criti-
cal, as it will impact the longevity of the barrier. HRC-X is injected to give a 5-year continuous re-
lease to ensure long-term suitable redox condition (iron and sulphate reducing conditions). 
Applying NVOC as a tracer has successfully provided further knowledge about the spatial distribu-
tion of donor and transport of the released VFA. This indicates the benefits of injecting a conserva-
tive tracer alongside with a donor. A conservative tracer would be more accurate than assuming 
HRC®-Primer lactic release as a tracer, because the lactic acid undergoes fermentation and is im-
pacted by other factors than a conservative tracer. Combining this with a conservative tracer along-
side the injected donor would enable a further understanding of how the lactate that is released from 
the various HRC donors is transported. Fluorescein is a popular tracer; however, fluorescein tracers 
can be affected by field conditions reducing their signal (Busch et al., 2015), a conservative tracer 
such as bromide (i.e. as sodium bromide) is recommended (Riis et al., 2006). Bromide is a frequent-
ly used groundwater tracer and has the following advantages: it can be easily analysed in groundwa-
ter samples; hence, it does not occur naturally in groundwater aquifers, it has very low to no sorp-
tion and recovery after injection is expected to be greater than for fluorescent tracers (Riis et al., 
2006). Disadvantages include a high detection limit of 0.6 mg/L (Riis et al., 2006). Even in mildly 
heterogeneous aquifers, slight variations in geology cause preferential flow. Injecting a tracer would 
enable more accurate evaluation of transport as well as the improved possibility of modelling 3D 
transport. This would provide a more accurate dispersivity and pore water flow estimation.  
Bacterial Injection Some contradicting factors regarding the injection of bacterial cultures were 
observed. The HRC donors have a low pH, which creates conditions unfavourable for Dhc bacterial 
cultures. Yang et al., (2017) states the pH range suitable for Dhc in BDI consortium is 6.5 – 8 and 
found that lasting damage to Dhc’s ability to degrade VC occurred when exposed to pH reduced to 
5.5 for more than 40 days. Investigation of the injection performed reveals that it is unclear how 
conditions are kept anaerobe during bacterial injection. This is critical since Dhc needs strictly an-
aerobic conditions (Löffler et al., 2013).  PlumeStop® is diluted with aerobic tap water and inject-
ed. The bacterial cultures are kept anaerobic in deoxygenated water and injected alternately with 
PlumeStop® top down. It is unclear whether the low groundwater flow at the field site will cause 
the Dhc to be in acidic aerobic conditions for a duration long enough to irreversibly inhibit Dhc. 
Documentation This project has shown the importance of comprehensive documentation during 
the construction and execution of injection using injection wells. Making use of the opportunity to 
characterise the geology under construction increases the number of geological sampling points 
within short proximity (in comparison to only characterising geology under construction of M-
wells). It is ideal for increasing knowledge of geological variation in the area of the PRB where 
even mild heterogeneities affect the distribution of LAC. Furthermore, it is beneficial to document 
the construction and well casing of the injection wells, to the same degree as M-wells. These miss-
ing documentations complicate the interpretation of how the heterogeneity and borehole condition 
affect the distribution through injection wells. Typically, the sand/gravel pack of the well casing 
==PAGE:90==
80 
 
will extend 0.5 - 1 m above the screen openings. Since the double packer system, only seals off the 
plastic pipe and not the entire borehole/gravel pack, amendments will have access to shallower and 
deeper zones of the aquifer through the borehole. The gravel pack is constructed with homogene-
ous, higher permeability sand than the aquifer material. Thorough documentation will save a lot of 
effort and eliminate unknowns in relation to the well casing. Lastly, documenting pressure of the in-
jection and ensuring that the pressure is suitable for the designed injection method is recommended. 
Permanent Injection Wells Despite amendments having been injected under too low pressure at 
Industrivej 2, knowledge has still been gained. This can be applied in future use of PlumeStop®, 
HRC and bacterial cultures for plume remediation under Danish field conditions. The levels of un-
certainty related to using permanent injection wells make the method less favourable for future use. 
Guaranteeing homogeneous distribution through a gravel pack in an injection well is not possible. 
Bottom-up injection will most likely result in a preferential flow of HRC products to the bottom of 
the TTA.  
8.2 Monitoring Strategy  
It is recommended that data is processed and evaluated soon after a monitoring round has been 
completed (e.g. visual interpretation by contour plots). This will enable fast feedback and 
knowledge about the success of the injection. The visualisation of monitoring data through contour 
plots has been key to understanding the distribution of donor and see the indications of the missing 
PlumeStop® sorption.  
Depth discrete sampling was very a beneficial monitoring method to apply since it enabled evalua-
tion of the variations in donor distribution and gave indication of PlumeStop® distribution through 
local reduction in CE concentrations. Depth discrete sampling can be used to indicate both ROI and 
vertical variations. This is an advantage compared to the M-wells, which each consist of three 2 m 
screens. The data collected in these screens are averages over the screen depth. When compared to 
depth discrete sampling peaks in local NVOC concentrations are not detected in the 2 m screens. 
Additionally, bentonite seals between each screen depth in M-wells are not included at the field site. 
An option for better depth specific monitoring is to use multilevel wells such as Continuous Multi-
channel Tubing (CMT) wells. CMT can include 7 screens, with screen openings down to 2 cm and 
each screen is separated with a bentonite seal (Alexander, Berg and Illman, 2011). Furthermore, a 
monitoring well closer to the I-wells than the designed ROI would be beneficial. When the ROI is 
not achieved it is difficult to assess, how far the distribution was from achieving the desired ROI, 
since the first M-well is placed at the desired ROI. Alternatively, depth discrete sampling could 
have been performed at different distances within the desired ROI to assess the distribution of 
PlumeStop®. 
Including a test of the chosen injection method prior to establishing an entire Transect is beneficial. 
The Spin injection method was tested and did not succeed and as a result the injection wells was in-
stalled. Instead of installing and injecting through 12 wells it would have been beneficial just to in-
stall one, perform an injection in this well and assess whether the method can achieve the desired 
ROI. If this had been done, it would have been observed that the applied pressure was not enough to 
achieve the designed ROI.  
==PAGE:91==
81 
 
The inclusion of a monitoring round sooner than 71 days post-injection in Transect 2 would have 
been beneficial, since the injection itself was designed to distribute PlumeStop® 1.7 m 
(ROI=1.7m). However, caution must be taken when doing so, as this can disturb the distribution of 
the established PRB. Nonetheless, this would be beneficial and is possible despite low flow, since 
the injection itself is designed to distribute PlumeStop® all the way to the first M-well. The benefits 
from an earlier initial sampling round furthermore include an investigation of the transport of lactic 
acid within the PRB indicated by HRC®-Primer release and inspection of variations in donor dis-
tribution. Moreover, an early monitoring round should include depth discrete sampling close to I-
wells to supplement the data from M-wells and show initial response and possible variation with 
depth.  
Based on literature reviews TOC/foc determination is not the most reliable method to determine 
LAC distribution. Core sampling has been assessed at the field site and was found to be applicable 
but time consuming and expensive (Rambøll and Regenesis, 2018). However, the combination of 
core sampling and the sorption test by Fjordbøge et al., (2018) is a new method developed and yet 
to be tested in the field.  
8.3 Ideas for Future Research  
Overall, further development of injection methods is needed for the current methods to be applica-
ble in Danish geology. It is ideal to test the new sorption test by Fjordbøge et al., (2018) by apply-
ing it to core samples as a tool to assess the distribution of PlumeStop®.   
Future research to investigate the suitability of injecting a tracer along with LAC and donor prod-
ucts is required. As well as the feasibility or possibility of including a pH stabilizer (Yang et al., 
2017), or making use of injection wells and injecting bacterial cultures later after aerobic neutral 
conditions are established.  
Based on independent porosity measurements, a formation factor for the aquifer was determined to 
be 3.15. However, it was concluded from borehole logging that Archie’s law was found unsuitable 
for aquifers disturbed by LAC and HRC donor. It could be an advantage for future development of 
borehole logging as a monitoring tool for LAC and HRC to include boreholes outside of the TTA 
and including more sondes in the logging programme (i.e. dual resistivity sonde and calliper log) as 
well as conducting a before and after comparison of the aquifer. The further use of borehole logging 
would ideally include borehole logging in baseline investigation at a site prior to constructing a 
PRB. In order to evaluate changes in the aquifer due to injection of amendment products. It is not 
recommended to attempt borehole logging at sites with limited 63 mm boreholes.

9 Conclusion 
Applying multiple lines of evidence determined uneven PlumeStop® distribution at the field site. 
Contour plots of CE concentrations presented spatial variation in the distribution of PlumeStop®, 
evident by sorption (i.e. reduction in CE concentration) only occurring in limited monitoring 
screens. Depth discrete sampling supports uneven distribution of PlumeStop®. Only one depth dis-
crete sample observed sorption in a distance of 0.3 m downstream an injection well. Near another 
well sorption was not observed 0.3 m downstream. This indicates uneven distribution and a ROI ≤ 
0.3 m; significantly lower than the designed ROI of 1.7 m. Last in the line of evidence was borehole 
logging, which support uneven distribution by indicating PlumeStop® build-up present in the top 
section of the sand aquifer. Borehole logging furthermore confirm that amendment products could 
have been distributed to an area above the TTA.  
The NVOC distribution caused by release from injected donor further supports uneven distribution 
using permanent injection wells. NVOC concentrations were most dominant in the deeper screens 
of monitoring wells within the TTA. This was most likely due an applied bottom-up injection. Bot-
tom-up injection results in a downward preferential flow within the well casing particularly since 
the donor products are denser than water. Depth discrete sampling further found vertical NVOC 
variations, which is not detected in 2 m screens in monitoring wells. This indicates that determining 
vertical variations are just as important as ROI and that depth discrete sampling/wells are preferable 
for distribution monitoring.  
The transport processes at the field site were successfully evaluated based on BTC fitted on NVOC 
observations caused by donor release of lactic acid. Three types of zones were distinguished within 
the PRB; low permeable zones where high concentrations of NVOC are distributed, high permea-
bility zones where an instantaneous release of lactic acid cause low NVOC concentrations within 
the barrier 351 days post injection and zones with good distribution indicating continuous release of 
lactic acid from donor. Downstream of the barrier (Transect 4) the pore water velocities determined 
based on BTC are consistent with the average pore water velocities determined by slug tests. The 
BTC determined average pore water velocities varying with a factor 4.3 within a 2.6 m along flow 
line. 
The final assessment of permanent injection wells, as an injection method for PlumeStop® has 
found too many uncertainties related to this method. Therefore, permanent injection wells are not a 
recommended injection method for future use of PlumeStop®. The double-packer inside the pipe is 
unable to evenly distribute amendment products. The gravel pack allows distribution outside of the 
TTA, which if applied in future needs to be included in the design and well documented. Further-
more, the bottom-up distribution of HRC donor is not suitable since the HRC cause preferential 
flow to the deeper sections of the aquifer. For future use of PlumeStop® it is recommended to in-
clude depth discrete sampling in the first monitoring round post injection, as well as apply core 
sampling with the newly developed sorption test.

CXT-fit Description and Chosen Estimation Method In Excel, the model setup is a parameter 
estimation problem in a spreadsheet, which involves input parameters, observations and formulae 
(see Figure A3). The analytical solution is implemented in the excel function CDE(L,t,t0, v, D, 
[R,µ, 𝛾, strFluxConc, ci,c0]), where arguments in square brackets are optional. If no arguments are 
given in square brackets, the values will be R=1, 0, 𝛾=0, strFluxConc=”flux” ci=0,c0=1 (Tang et al. 
2010). The cellnumber containing the value of a fixed parameter is entered in the function. The pa-
rameters that have to be estimated are given a cellnumber in Excel, where the parameter estimation 
will be given as output. In the CDE function, the equation for D is entered instead of just D; hence, 
dispersivity (αL) is estimated instead. Once the CDE function is applied, it predicts a concentration 
for each time step. The Excel spreadsheet will have three columns located side by side containing the 
time step, observation and prediction respectively (see Figure A3). In the column next to the predic-
tions, a column containing the residuals, which is the difference between the predicted and observed 
values are estimated. An objective function cell (called SSR) is created, which calculates the sum of 
squares of the residuals (SSR). The SSR is the chosen estimation method, where the model parameters 
are estimated by minimizing the sum of the residuals defined as:

This method assumes that the residuals are normally distributed and residuals independent of each 
other at different time steps (Löwe et al. 2018). The CXT-fit code contains a Solver, when applying 
the solver it is given the objective function, the parameters to estimate and a range with the predic-
tions. The solver applies the chosen estimation method given in the Equation and gives the estimated

standard deviation and lower (LCL) and upper (UCL) confidence level is calculated to evaluate the 
estimated parameters.  The RMSE is presented instead of SSR as the unit is easier to interpret; hence, 
the unit of RMSE is C/C0 percentage and SSR is the C/C0 percentage squared.

Appendix C 
Borehole logging 
OPTVAn optical televiewer (OPTV) creates a 360 degree continuous image using a camera. The 
exact depth and images are logged similar to all other sondes enabling good comparison (Horn, 2003; 
Williams and Johnson, 2004). The exact effects of residue and traces of PlumeStop® and donor in-
jection on the different sondes are unknown. Hence, OPTV would be a good supplement to the other 
data in order to know whether changes in logs could be due to residue coating the inside of the bore-
hole at the exact locations. OPTV was not available to be used in the 63 mm wide boreholes. Below 
the water level visibility is expected to be very low during borehole-logging due to the residue from 
PlumeStop® and donor being disturbed and put in suspension. The optical televiewer is dependent 
on good visibility below water surface.  
Dual focused formation resistivity (DLL3) The dual focused formation resistivity sonde (DLL3 
aka dual lateral log), measures resistivity of the formation, by sending out a current into the formation 
(French Oil and Gas Industry Association, 1986). The resistivity is measured at both deep and shallow 
depth in the formation, which enables distinguishing influences from the borehole casing and those 
from the formation. The induction log is not able to pass through plastic and thereby cracks or joints 
with leaks appear in the log, enabling evaluation of the plastic pipe in boreholes. This sonde has 
greater accuracy than measuring the electromagnetic induction. However, the sonde cannot be used 
at contaminated sites due to the surface of the sonde, which cannot be cleaned after use.   
DILS Dual electromagnetic induction sonde (DILS) apply electromagnetic induction to determine 
the electrical conductivity of the formation. DILS achieve both a reading at shallow and deep distance 
in horizontal distance to the well. A formations electromagnetic conductivity increases or decreases 
dependent on whether it consists of clays (increase) or sand (decreases). The water content further-
more impacts the conductivity. Water content increases the conductivity. Obtaining both a shallow 
and deep reading, enables an interpretation of the both electrical conductivity of the well-casing and 
the formation (Telford, Geldart and Sheriff, 1990). 
TRIS (Density) Slimhole density sonde (TRIS) is able to log the density of the formation. The density 
logged by the TRIS sonde is relative but can determine changes in the formation with depth. Other 
sondes can be calibrated with standards in order to enable geology to determined based on the density. 
This is the sidewall density sonde (FDSB). This sonde is 55-56 mm and would not fit into a 63 mm 
borehole. Whereas, the TRIS sonde is 38 mm but unfortunately, not available to be used at a contam-
inated site.  
CAL3 Caliper log measures the diameter of the borehole as the sonde is raised out of the borehole 
(Kearey, Brooks and Hill, 2002). The caliper log can be used to correct data from sondes sensitive to 
changes in diameter (Mwenifumbo, 1993). Caliper sondes can consist of various flexible arms and 
can be used to detect fractures in open boreholes (French Oil and Gas Industry Association, 1986; 
Telford, Geldart and Sheriff, 1990). This sonde would be useful in determining whether disturbances 
in the BHTV was due to changes in the diameter of the borehole. The caliper log was not available 
to be used at contaminated sites. However, it is possible to determine a caliper log for the borehole 
based on the hardness log.

The pH and NVOC correlation Histograms showing the distribution of the two datasets are shown. 
The pH observations show normal distributions as most of it histogram is covered by the red line 
showing the shape of normal distributed data. However, the NVOC dataset is right skewed. Due to 
the peak located to the left resulting in a tail with a positive skew to the right.   
 
 
The scatterplot indicates correlation; hence, a correlation test is performed. It is a test that gives the 
correlation a score. The spearman correlation test is chosen as it can be used on continuous data both 
normal and non-normal distributed data.  The spearman correlation number rho has a value from -1 
to 0 or from 1 to 0. The value 0 states that there is no correlation. 1 and -1 show that there is perfect 
positive and perfect negative correlation respectively. Among with the rho-value the spearman test 
gives a p-value. If the p-value is < 0.05 the rho-value presents a significant correlation between 
NVOC and pH. The results from the spearman correlation test are shown in Table A3Table A3. n 
presents the number of observations from each NVOC and pH that is applied in the test. The rho-
value is -0.83. Hence, this value shows a valid negative correlation. This correlation is significant as 
the p-value is < 0.05.
