Chapter 1
Introduction
In this project acoustofluidics is used to focus and position micro particles e.g. human white cell so they
can be imaged for medical and pharmaceutical applications. The scope of this project is to simulate the
physics and optimise the acoustofluidic device design for handling of such particles.
Acoustofluidics is positioned to provide enormous potential in such application, as it provides a
gentle, fast, and easily controlled handling of microbiological organisms.
This project is in partnership with ChemoMetec A/S, whom will stand for the experimental part of
realising the device and setup. A feed-back loop between the simulation part and the experimental part
of the project is implemented to ensure the reliability of the devices studied and analysed.
More concretely, this thesis presents an optimised way of focusing particles in either a plane or a
line. When a solution containing particles is injected into a channel, the particles are positioned using
acoustophoresis in either a certain plane or a line inside the channel, such that the concentration of
particles in said plane or line is increased.
The device is built up using a piezoelectric actuator as the source of the acoustic waves, and it is
placed under the glass channel, where the fluid solution is injected.
The project analyses and optimises the parameters of the device. Parameters such as the frequency
of the acoustic waves and the geometrical dimensions of the piezoelectric actuator and the glass channel,
for instance.
The thesis first presents the theory behind the physics studied in this project and the governing
equations used in the simulation. The results of simulations conducted using Comsol Multiphysics is
then presented, compared, and discussed.

Theory
This section presents the theories used to model the physical setups and the governing equation describ-
ing the physical phenomena considered in this project.
To model the physical phenomena describing the devices worked with in this project, the continuum
hypothesis is employed. This implies that the solid or fluid matters are modeled as perfectly continuous
structures, regardless of the fact that matter is made of discrete molecules.
This also implies that
small volume elements of matter (compared to the macroscopic length scale) in a system are in fact so
large that they contain a very big number of molecules, so that molecular fluctuations in the physical
quantities are neglected. This volume element is called a fluid particle when considering fluid dynamics
and a solid element when considering the theory of elasticity in solids. [1, 2, 3]
The physical properties are studied and modeled in terms of fields, considered in the Eulerian picture.
This means that the time evolution of a field value is studied at a certain fixed position. The quantity of
the field is defined as the average equivalent molecular quantity in the finite volume element considered
at that position. [1, 3]
The following sections present the basic fundamental theory utilized in the simulation and study of
the acoustic device. The mathematical notation adopted in this thesis follows from Bruus [1].

Chapter 3
Device design and setup
In this project, acoustics will be used to position and control bio-particles for imaging purposes. Imaging
the particles requires consideration of the optical setup surrounding the acoustofluidic device.
The
optical setup taking the images of the particles will be explained in this section together with the
constraints and considerations the device has to adopt.
3.1
Optical considerations
An optical imaging setup, has a characteristic field of view (FOV) area at the object plane of the
sensor/camera.
This is the area of the object, that will be imaged using a certain sensor/camera.
Objects outside of this area will not be seen or sensed. FOV has also a certain depth by which objects
outside of this depth are considered out of focus, and will usually be seen as blurry objects. Optical
imaging requires a light source and the positioning of the light source with respect to the objective lens
of the sensor/camera can vary.[17]
3.2
Current System
In this project, bright field transmission light microscopy is considered. The light source is positioned
opposite to the camera, and the specimen to be imaged is in between. This optical setup is currently
used by the partners in this project, ChemoMetec. Furthermore, the bio-particles are injected into a
glass channel that is transparent for the light wavelengths used. The channel is considerably big with
respect to the FOV area of dimensions 881 µm×662 µm of the camera. The objective plan is aligned to
the bottom of the glass channel and the bio-particles are waited to sediment out of their suspension in
the fluid to the bottom of the channel. This sedimentation of particles takes a considerable amount of
time, and the setup is calibrated to wait until the particles are settled at the bottom. After this waiting
period, the optical setup is activated and an image is taken for further analysis of the bio-particles. See
Fig. ?? for an illustration of the process.

3.3
Design of acoustic device
In this project, the acoustic devices are designed and optimised to be adopted for the optical setup
presented above. The focusing of the particles into a FOV area suited for a certain camera is achieved
using acoustofluidics. Two types of focusing are studied; plane focusing and line focusing.
Building up the acoustic device is based on the standards and literature available in the field. The
components are chosen in terms of availability, viability, suitability, and feasibility.
The devices chosen for simulation are presented in the following. Some devices are simulated in
2-dimensional (2D) numerical simulations, others in 3-dimensional (3D) numerical simulations. This
implies that an acoustic device focusing bio-particles in a plane can be simulated in both a 2D and 3D
simulation.
3.3.1
Components of common acoustic devices
An acoustic device requires an actuation source inducing vibrations in the device at ultrasonic frequen-
cies. Standard actuators used in acoustofluidics are components made of piezoelectric materials. One
of these common materials is the piezoelectric material Pb(Zr1−xTix)O3 (PZT). PZTs are components
manufactured in different shapes and sizes. Electrodes are deposited at certain surfaces of the PZT to
allow for mechanical actuation using electrical oscillations. Their bulk driven actuation is assumed to
respond linearly to the electrical AC frequency applied to the electrodes, which are in the single digit
MHz range. See Lenshof et al. [18] and Dual et al. [10] for more detailed consideration of the acoustic
actuation. Two types of PZTs are used in this project with the main one being Pz27 and the other
being Pz26. The properties of Pz27 are that it is a soft material having high sensitivity and high power
loss. Pz26 is a hard material having low sensitivity and low power loss. The thickness of the PZT
is what defines the actuation resonating frequency.[19] This means that for the same type of a PZT,
different PZT thicknesses can be chosen depending on the frequency of actuation needed for a certain
application. See Table 2.1 for Pz27 material parameters.
Common microfluidic channels used in acoustofluidics are made of Pyrex glass.

3.3.2
Line focusing device design considerations
In the paper Antfolk et al. [20] two-dimensional acoustophoresis (line focusing) is numerically shown
possible in an ”almost-quadratic” channel. The article shows the importance of the streaming field
in line focusing. The article reports line focusing of sub-micrometer bio-particles, where the acoustic
radiation force is notably weakened due to the particle’s radius. So the drag force due to streaming
and the radiation force work together to focus the particle transversely in a point in the microchannel
cross-section. The actuation frequency of the transducer (a single transducer is used) is set to match
half a wavelength of pressure in the fluid in the quadratic microfluidic channel.
The leanings taken from [20] to build the acoustic device is the use of a quadratic channel and the
viability of using only a single transducer to focus particles in a line. However, the particle size is not
limited to sub-micrometer in this thesis. See Fig. 3.3 for an illustration of one of the configurations.
3.3.3
Plane focusing device design considerations
Taking an image with a considerable FOV area requires particles to be focused in the objective plane of
the sensors/camera. An acoustofluidic device is designed to utilise acoustophoresis to position particles
in a plane at the objective plane having a minimum area equal to the FOV area.
The article Antfolk et all. [21] reports a considerably uniform plane pressure node in a rectangular
glass channel. A disk PZT is used with an actuation frequency matched to half a pressure wave in the
height of the rectangular channel cross-section. This is taken as a starting point for the design of the
acoustic device. The uniformity of the planar pressure node is restricted to places above or not too far
from the disk transducer. However, instead of disk transducers, ring transducers are used instead.
For imaging applications using transmission light microscopy, the planar pressure node has to occur in
the transparent channel without having the transducer blocking the line of sight of the light transmission.
Therefore, ring transducers are considered for the design of the acoustic device. See Table 3.1 and Fig. 3.3
for an illustration and description of some the geometrical configurations and parameters chosen in this
project.

Chapter 4
Numerical Modeling
In this project, the finite element modeling (FEM) method is used to approximate solutions of partial
differential equations (PEDs).
This is a huge advantage when considering complex geometries and
phenomena that do not necessarily have simple analytical solutions. The governing equations that are
considered in this project are all suited to be modelled using FEM. The software used for the simulations
is Comsol Multiphysics.[22]
4.1
The Finite Element Method of solving PDEs
In the following, a brief presentation of the theory behind FEM is given. The formulation in this section
is followed from the lecture notes ”Numerical studies in physics” of Henrik Bruus [23].
4.1.1
Strong Form
In continuum physics, the governing dynamics are represented by equations of the form
∇· J −F = 0,
(4.1)
where J is known as the generalised flux and it can be of a vector or tensor form, and F is known as
the generalised force that drives the flux and it can be of a scalar or vector form.
J and F can depended on several variable fields, but in the following we consider the case where
they depend on a single scalar field u(r) that is stationary in time. So that the flux is expressed as
J[u(r)] and the force is only space dependant as F(r). In general multiple vector or scalar fields can
play a role. A solution u(r) satisfying eq. (4.1) in the domain Ωwith respect to a certain boundary
condition at the domain boundary ∂Ωis called the strong solution of the strong formulation of the PDE.
This type of solutions is not always attainable analytically.
4.1.2
Discretization, test functions, and weak form
Instead of solving the very difficult strong formulation, the FEM introduces an easier method to solve
the governing PDEs.
First the space-time continuum is discretized.

the equations are to be solved for is discretized using a large but finite number N of points rn where
n ∈1, 2, 3, .., N. Each point is considered a vertex for connections creating a finite number of geometrical

4.2
Implementation in Comsol
4.2.1
Domains
When simulating a device in Comsol we implement the geometry needed to model the devise as close
to reality as possible. The geometry implemented is then divided into domains corresponding to each
component of the device.
Each domain has certain physical phenomena that can be modelled and
simulated. Each physical phenomena in each domain has a governing equation describing the physics
of it. See fig. (4.1) and table 9.1 for illustration and desricption. Sometime a component of the device
is modelled using physical models with more than one field. This is understood in Comsol as a domain
with several fields to be calculated. This can be for example the piezoelectric component of the device.
To simulate the piezoelectric component, the numerical model has to solve a PDE corresponding to
the equation of elasticity of the solids and a PDE corresponding to the electrical potential necessary to
actuate the piezoelectric material. Those two PDEs are solved in the same domain corresponding to
the geometry of the piezoelectric component.

Chapter 6
Results: Plane focusing
In this chapter, plane focusing devices are studied. These kind of devices are very useful when considering
application of imaging wider and bigger FOV areas.
The focusing of particles in a plane allows to
image a considerable number of particles simultaneously, increasing the throughput of imaging optical
instrument. The magnification in such optical setups is worsened for the purpose of including as many
particles as possible in a single image.
The previous chapter shows the importance of mismatching between the actuation frequency of the
actuator and resonance frequency of the fluid channel, as a condition to achieve good and reliable line
focusing. It is observed that when actuator’s resonance is matched to the fluid’s, the Frad is too strong
to allow for the suspended particles to sufficiently circulate the channel and be focused in a single spot
in the middle. This large Frad, however, can be an advantage, when the task is to focus particles in a
plane, as opposed to a line.
To avoid confusion with regard to the nomenclature, the naming convention is revisited. The term
plane focusing is used for focusing of particles in 2 dimensional plane in the real physical geometrical
space. However, when simulating the dynamics in 2D, showing the dynamics in a plane perpendicular to
the focusing plane, the particle focusing corresponds to a line of particles positioned beside each other.
Not to confuse this with the term line focusing used for focusing particles in a line in the real physical
geometrical space. 2D simulation shows the dynamics of this focusing as a crowded spot (point) of
focused particles. In the following, the Circulation Probe is also disregarded, when presenting sweeps,
and only the acoustic energy density of the device will be probed.
For plane focusing purposes, matching the actuation frequency with the mentioned resonance fre-
quency of the fluid, is ideally implemented when the actuator itself resonates at the same frequency. For
PZT actuators the resonance frequency is correlated with a certain characteristic PZT height. Therefor,
the height of the Pz27 actuators used in this chapter corresponds always with the resonance of the fluid
channel.
All results in this chapter are presented for particle’s radius of 5 µm.

A plane focusing device having a 300 µm capillary glass positioned at the center of Pz27, having
width 10 mm and height 0.8 mm and actuated with 1 V potential difference across the actuator is
presented in Fig. 6.1. The device shows maximum acoustic energy density at the resonance frequency
f = 2.485, which is close to the theoretical expectation. The particle motion for the device is presented
in Fig. 6.2. The particle motion is considerable in the vertical direction focusing the particles in a plane
parallel to the actuator. The focusing of all the particles is relatively fast, occurring at around 0.05
s. The time scale, however, is not uniform across the plane of focusing. The particles in the middle
of the channel focus faster than those close to the vertical sides of the channel. It is important to
consider the streaming field in this device. As observed in the previous chapter, when positioning the
capillary channel in the middle of the actuator, the streaming field’s motion is circulating in two circles
centered to the sides of the center of the channel. This was the basis for achieving 2 lines focusing
when using a mismatching frequency in the previous chapter. In regions where the streaming field push
with the radiation force field, the motion is fastest. When the two fields act against each other, the
motion becomes slower. The uniformity of the focusing plane (straightness of the plane) is effected by
the streaming in this device. The radiation force, seems otherwise very uniform. The plane is bent
downward in the middle of the channel and upward on the sides. A bending height parameter hbend is
defined as the vertical distance between the upper most positioned particle and the lowest positioned
particle. For this considered device hbend ≈11µm, corresponding to a bit more than the diameter of
the particles considered.

Summary
We can conclude from this discussion, the importance of the walls of the capillary glass channel in fitting
half a pressure wave inside the fluid channel that starts and ends with a crest and a trough, to focus
particles in plane perpendicular to the pressure gradient. The condition is to at least fit a quarter of a
stress wave in the solid walls of the channel. It is also apparent from the previous discussion that the
width of the radiation force hot-spot is not the only indication of the width of the focusing plane of the
particles. Streaming has to be taken into consideration as well. Narrower hot-spots can still result in a
wide straight plane of focused particles. However, wall thickness of channel and distance between the
two actuators effect the hot-spots shapes. Different time scales of particle focusing in a plane, results in
the need to wait for the plane formation across the whole width of the focusing plane. This is a draw
back in the case of strong streaming fields dragging and splitting the particles’ plane formation.

9.1
Comsol
The implementation of the streaming vector field v2 PDE is typed in Comsol as follows
-(test(vf2Xx)*Jvf2XX+test(vf2Xy)*Jvf2XY+test(vf2Xz)*Jvf2XZ)
- test(vf2X)*Fvf2X,
(9.1a)
-(test(vf2Yx)*Jvf2YX+test(vf2Yy)*Jvf2YY+test(vf2Yz)*Jvf2YZ)
- test(vf2Y)*Fvf2Y,
(9.1b)
-(test(vf2Zx)*Jvf2ZX+test(vf2Zy)*Jvf2ZY+test(vf2Zz)*Jvf2ZZ)
- test(vf2Z)*Fvf2Z,
(9.1c)
and the p2 field PDE is typed as
-test(p2x)*Jp2X-test(p2y)*Jp2Y-test(p2z)*Jp2Z-test(p2)*Fp2.
(9.2)
This is however not the only way to implement the p2 scalar field. In Comsol an auxiliary variable
can be defined for a certain domain of a certain variable (v2 in this case) where p2 and its governing
equation is defined under the same variable domain.
