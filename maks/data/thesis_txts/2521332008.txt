Summary
The increasing demand for more powerful rare earth magnets has led to the research
into new manufacturing methods which are able to produce these magnetic components
without the need for resin binders. Conventional sintering and powder metallurgy are
able to deliver parts with the required magnetic and physical properties. However these
production methods require very long process times. Resistance Sintering methods were
developed to increase the speed of producing powdered metal components with the
same properties as conventional sintering. This project applies the techniques used in
Resistance Sintering to attempt to produce magnetic disks. With special focus being
given to understand the effects that the current profiles and grain size distributions of
the powdered metal have on the properties of the final part.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Rare earth permanent magnets are used by various different industries in applications
ranging from sensors, to motors, electrical generators, computer hard disks or any other
application that requires a strong magnetic field in a small package.
Due to their brittle nature, rare earth magnets are only manufactured using powder bind-
ing technologies. The most common method used today is bonded magnets, in which
the magnetic powder is mixed with a resin or binder, which is then placed into a mold
and cured. Due to the curing time the process cycles are quite long, and the mechanical
and thermal properties of the components are also limited by the resin matrix. As the
resin matrix is non magnetic, the final product will have weaker magnetic properties
than the pure rare earth alloy.
Sintering is another manufacturing process commonly used to produce magnets which
have higher magnetic and mechanical properties due to the absence of the resin matrix.
Sintering is a process which utilizes thermal energy to bind together metallic or ceramic
powders creating parts whose properties approach that of the base material. The most
common form of sintering can be broadly described in two phases, powder compaction
and sintering. In the first phase the powdered material is compacted using molds, once
compacted the powder holds the shape of the mold but has very poor mechanical prop-
erties. During the second phase the powder compact (Green Body) is then heated for
several hours at very high temperatures, causing the powder to bind together through
diffusion phenomena driven by a reduction in surface energy. The part shrinks a con-
siderable amount during sintering and because of this, dimensional tolerances are often
difficult to meet and post processing is generally required.
In recent years, research has been done on the use of Electrical Resistance Sintering
for the production of rare earth magnets. This method belongs to a family of sintering
processes collectively known as hot pressing, which combines the pressing of the powder
and the sintering into one operation. Electrical resistance sintering uses high electrical
currents to heat the powder as it is being compressed within a mold. This thermo-
electro-mechanical process has shown some promise in producing components with high
mechanical and magnetic properties with very short process times, with the potential to
produce net shape parts.
The main processing variables used to control the outcome of a sintering process can be
divided into two categories: Material Variables and Process Variables. Material variables

2.1
Electrical Resistance Sintering
2.1.1
Process Overview
Electrical current assisted sintering (ECAS) was first used by Arthur Bloxam in 1906,
when he patented a process for producing tungsten filaments for use in lightbulbs[Gag18]
and the process continued to be refined throughout the 20th century. Electrical resistance
sintering is just one process from the ECAS group of processes, which are all based on
the principle of compressing a conductive powder material while subjecting it to one or
several pulses of high current. This group of processes is characterized by very short
cycle times when compared to conventional sintering, as the high heating rates and
pressures greatly facilitate densification.
Figure 2.1: Schematic representation Resistance Sintering tooling system
Figure 2.1 shows a representation of the tool configuration used in the process. Tooling
consists of a non conductive die, eletrodes/punches, an electrical power supply and a
hydraulic system to provide the compressive load. This system requires that the powder
be made of a conductive material in order to establish electrical contact between the
electrodes. This allows heat to be generated inside of the powder,allowing for higher
heating rates as the heating is not dependant on heat conduction.

The feeding of the powder can be done manually or with the help of a feeding machine.
As the powder is handled it becomes agitated, intermixed with air, this results in poor
packing and a very low apparent density (35-50%), once in the die. The use of mechan-
ical vibrations can allow the powder to settle further leading to better packing and an
increase in apparent density.
During the pre-compaction step, the powder is compressed in order to further increase
it’s density prior to sintering. A high starting density is important as it directly effects
the electrical resistance of the powder and also impacts the sintering speed and final
density of the part [KS05][Orr+09]. Pre compaction is also known as the squeeze time.
The sintering step consists of a simultaneous application of compression and current
which both work to enable different densification mechanisms. The current may be ap-
plied in one or more pulses and serves to heat the part. The pulses may be of varying
length and strength. These pulses can be used to clear way oxide films and control
heating and cooling. Figure 2.3 shows an example of a sintering process cycle.

2.1.3.2
Deformation and Creep
The application of a compressive load to the powder compact during the sintering pro-
motes densification through several different means [KS05]:
• Elastic deformation of contact points between particles
• Plastic deformation of particles
• Creep
These series of effects greatly increase the speed of densification as plastically deforming
the material is a much faster method than diffusion in transporting material into the
space left by porosity. They also directly contribute to increase contact area between
particles which in turn causes more locations where interfacial area driven diffusion can
occur.
2.1.3.3
Electromigration
Electromigration is a diffusion mechanism that is driven by the passage of a large elec-
trical current through a conductor.
The large quantity of electrons flowing through
the conductor suffer elastic collisions with the material’s matrix causing atoms to be
shifted into nearby vacancies or causing the creation of protrusions when located near
an outer surface, effectively creating a very slow material flow from the cathode towards
the anode[Jak+10].

2.1.4
Control Parameters
The Resistance sintering is a complicated process as it involves electrical, thermal and
mechanical aspects. The interaction between these factors can be complex and changing
a parameter in one domain can have unintended effects in the others. The main input
parameters used to control the sintering process are:
• Powder material selection and treatment
• Compression Load F
• Electrical Current I
• Current Pulse Length P L
• Number of pulses P n
• Cooling Time C
These input parameters have a direct effect on the outcome of the sintering process by
determining the heating in the system, the presence and severity of temperature gradi-
ents as well as impacting the mechanisms which govern densification. Other factors may
influence the process as well, such as the tool material, whether the tool is preheated or
not, the current source, etc. however these factors can be difficult or costly to change
and are not considered key to the process.
The selection of the powder material is the most important parameter as it sets a con-
straint on the process by requiring the powder to reach a certain activation temperature
to promote the atomic mobility necessary for diffusion.
In general this temperature
requirement for sintering to occur is in the realm of 0.7-0.8 of the materials melting
point. The powder’s particle size can also effect the driving force for densification by
determining the amount of surface energy γv-s in the system. A powder with smaller
particles will have a larger surface area, more contact points between particles to initiate
necking.[Kot65].
The current parameters I, P L, P n and the Cooling time, are responsible for the heating
of the powder through the Joule Effect. These properties are selected such that they
bring the powder up to temperature quickly and ideally are able to maintain the temper-
ature at the proper temperature for the required amount of time. For longer processing
windows temperature control can be a challenge as the heat that builds up in the powder
is constantly being lost through the tooling. This can lead to temperature gradients in
the powder which can cause the temperature on the outer layers of powder to fall bellow
the sintering impeding densification in that zone.

The compression load also has a beneficial effect on the sintering time, by promoting
plastic deformation and creep which are a much faster method of moving material and
increasing density than necking and grain coarsening. Compression load also reduces
the electrical resistivity of the material by increasing the contact area between particles.

2.1.5
Heat Generation
When an electrical current flows through a conductor it generates heat whenever it en-
counters resistance, this is due to the electrons colliding with atoms in their path causing
them to vibrate more. The electrical resistance R is inversely proportional to the cross
section of the conductor through which the current flows.
This dependence on cross section of the causes there to be an increased resistance
at the interface between two conductors which are touching, due to the real contact
area between the two objects consisting of smaller contact zones due to the presence of
asperities. These cross section reductions cause a spike in electrical resistance and and
increase in heat generated in that zone. See figure 2.6.
The point contact between particles in the powder cause the current to be constricted
several times as it flows through the powder generating. The resistance of the powder
changes based on the pressure applied to it as the grains deform under load, increasing
their real area of contact and reducing the heat generated as shown in 2.7.
The compaction of the powder can also cause it to reorganize itself causing a greater
number of particles to contact each other, creating more routs for the current to flow
through thereby reducing the electrical resistance further.

2.2.1.1
Loosening effect
The loosening effect occurs when the powder ratio Dr = Dc/Df is such that the fine
particles are too large to fit within the empty spaces created between the coarse particles.
This impedes the coarse particles from packing tightly together increasing the powder’s
over all volume as shown in figure 2.10. Large differences in particle sizes greatly diminish
the loosening effect[Ye+18][Rie91].

Wall effect
The wall effect, depicted in figure 2.11, causes reduced porosity near the surfaces of the
container which constrict the powder’s volume or in the case of multi-modal packing,
the presence of large particles which serve as a wall for areas of fine powder.

This interaction is caused by fine particles becoming trapped between coarse particles,

Blending different sized powders can have a dramatic effect on the packing density of

2.2.3
Particle Shapes
Powders can be made of particles of a variety of different shapes varying from flakes, to
rods to spheres. These shapes can greatly impact the powders’ density and flowability
by increasing friction, enclosing pores or promoting agglomeration. The most common
method of characterizing particle shapes is through the use of the Dimensionless Param-
eters[Cho+06] described below. Figure 2.14 shows how these parameters are extracted
from a particle.

4.5
Other Observations
The ejection of the sintered disks was the most problematic step throughout the pro-
cess. The use of a manual arbor press as the method of ejecting was responsible for the
cracking of multiple samples during ejection and possibly weakening the samples that
did not break. The level of wear on the dies used for the experiments caused many of
the disks to stick in them, and ejection would often require a great deal of force, in many
cases it was required to shock load the samples to get it to move, which could cause
internal cracking that would weaken the part. The vast majority of the disks sintered
throughout this project were not able to be tested as they could not survive the ejection.
This problem is amplified further by wear on the dies. Over the course of several
sintering cycles adhesive wear causes degradation of the ceramic dies which increase the
channel at the height where sintering occurs as shown in figure4.14. This causes the
disks to be larger than the channel through which they have to exit. This means that
ejection force needs to shear off the material present in the volume of the wear. This
shearing action can cause craking through the disks as the sintered portions are torn
apart.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
The aim of this project was to manufacture magnetic disks through the resistance sin-
tering project with a focus on using grain size distributions and current profiles as key
control parameters. The intent was to improve the mechanical resistance of the mag-
netic disks while trying to eliminate the presence of melted zones to ensure that magnetic
properties of the powder are not lost.
Current profiles proved to be key to producing high performing magnets. The effect
of the different profiles are clearly seen in the micrographs and density measurements.
The use a low currents and long process times showed to be quite beneficial in increasing
compression resistance. The LC and P series showed nearly doubled the compression
resistance of the 80xx series. With the M series of samples close behind.
Grain size distributions appear to have a negligible effect on the process, as parts that
were produced using powder mixes do not seem to have noticeable difference from the
other samples in their group, even though the powder itself can have density increases
of up to 15% as shown in the literature. While the grain size distributions can greatly
improve performance of conventional sintering, field assisted sintering techniques appear
to rely much more on the mechanical compression, which works equally well regardless
of the powder composition.
The increases that were achieved in the compression resistance along side the reten-
tion of the powder’s initial microstructure show that the resistance sintering of magnets
with both High mechanical resistance and High magnetic properties is possible, and that
the path to achieving that will most likely come through more experimentation with cur-
rent profiles to fine tune the heating of the part powder compact so that it can remain
at sintering temperatures for a longer window.
5.0.1
Future Work
The full effect of the current profiles has yet to be explores, due equipment limitations,
dynamic change in compression load and curret values could not be explored. The equip-
ment also limited cycle time to approximately 7 seconds. experimenting with longer
process times could yield better results, as the samples series LC and P have shown that
