Abstract
In the presented thesis, vortex dynamics and interactions are studied using tools from topo-
logical fluid dynamics. Vortex patterns described by the vorticity criterion and the Q-criterion
are investigated.
In the first part of the thesis, the dynamics of vortices identified using the vorticity criterion are
studied. Vortex interactions in a fluid medium are described as bifurcations up to codimension
two. A connection is made between the theoretical results and physical observations. A novel
algorithm is developed to track vortex cores and saddles in order to analyse flows using the
vorticity criterion.
The implemented numerical method is employed in analysing the flow
past an asymmetrically placed cylinder in a confinement. The wall effect is investigated and
characterised using the developed theory.
The wall is shown to operate in three different
regimes: the wall-preparation phase, the wall-effect phase, and the wall-destruction phase.
In the second part, a theory is formulated which describes vortex dynamics identified by the Q-
criterion. The theory is valid both away from boundaries and near a no-slip wall. A complete
description of all possible codimension one bifurcations is given, which accurately identifies
many important vortex phenomena. Vortex creation, destruction, merging, and separation are
described qualitatively. Additionally, the time evolution of vortex rings and hairpin vortices
are formulated using the developed theory.
Keywords: topological fluid mechanics, vortex dynamics, vortex interactions, vorticity crite-
rion, vortex core tracking, Q-criterion, bifurcations in vortex patterns.

1. Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Fluid mechanics is a branch of physics that deals with the motion of fluids. It has applications in
a wide range of disciplines such as aerospace, mechanical engineering, chemistry, astrophysics,
medicine, and biology to name a few. The dynamics are governed by conservation laws which
state that quantities such as mass, momentum, and energy should be conserved.
Navier-
Stokes equations model fluid flows for many engineering problems and are proven to portray an
accurate representation of flow physics. Due to the nature of these partial differential equations,
the dynamics are very interesting but also very hard to comprehend. Fluid flows often contain
structures and patterns of many length scales and time scales of different orders of magnitude.
Therefore, it is crucial to extract the most important features from a flow.
Topology is concerned with the properties of geometrical objects under smooth deformations.
The main focus is on the structure of the object. In topological fluid mechanics, the aim is to
identify the most important features of a fluid flow. Dynamical systems theory becomes useful
when doing this, as the physics are governed by PDEs. Critical events that mark structural
changes in the flow can be examined using bifurcation theory, which studies qualitative changes
in a dynamical system.
In this thesis, vortex dynamics are treated using tools from topological fluid mechanics. Vortices
are regions in a flow where swirling motion is dominant. These structures are found to take
on key roles in different fluid mechanical phenomena. Vortices are not only observed in nature
such as in the motion of jellyfish, or planetary flows; but are also exploited in cutting-edge
technology such as in designing aircraft bodies, engines, and cooling systems. The theories
formulated in this thesis are qualitative in nature, that is the structures that can transform
into each other by continuous deformations are treated as equivalent. However, the theoretical
results depict a very detailed picture of critical events that cause structural changes in fluid
flows. It is the aim and the desire of this thesis to contribute to the science of fluid mechanics
in understanding vortex dynamics better. The importance of vortices in fluid mechanics and
the power of topological fluid mechanics in providing descriptions of fluid mechanics problems
motivate the studies that follow.
1.2
Aims and Objectives of the Thesis
This thesis started around the idea of using the vorticity criterion to systematically analyse
vortex dynamics in two-dimensional flows.
The first objective was to develop a numerical

method to extract the pathlines of vortices from computational fluid dynamics simulations
and investigate vortex dynamics. This would allow one to analyse a wide range of flows with
industrial and technological importance. In order to guide the analysis of the numerical results,
a theory for possible vortex interactions in a fluid medium is developed using bifurcation theory.
The second idea was to develop a bifurcation theory for Q-vortices with the hope to understand
possible vortex interactions in three-dimensional flows. The main objective of this study was
to use tools from dynamical systems theory to gain insight into vortex flows and investigate
complex dynamics seen in fluid mechanics problems. It is believed that these would contribute
to understanding vortex flows better and provide a valuable contribution to analysing a broader
range of fluid dynamics problems.
1.3
Outline of the Thesis
The thesis is divided into two main chapters where the focus is on different aspects of vortex
dynamics. In chapter 2, vorticity topology is examined. The aim of the chapter is to develop
a novel numerical method to systematically track vortex cores using the vorticity criterion.
The vorticity criterion is used to investigate the dynamics of vortices in two-dimensional flows.
Bifurcation theory is used to derive formulations for vortex interactions inside a fluid medium.
A novel numerical algorithm is developed to track vortex cores and their mathematical duals
inside the flow to analyse vortex interactions. Computational fluid dynamics simulations are
performed to showcase the developed theory and test the tracking algorithm. Flow past an
asymmetrically placed cylinder in a confinement is analysed. Local bifurcations in the flow are
reported and wall-induced vortex interactions are discussed.
The focus is switched to three-dimensional flows and vortex boundaries in chapter 3. A vortex
is identified using the Q-criterion. Using bifurcation theory, all codimension one bifurcation
are identified and classified. The theoretical results are explained using physical reasoning to
portray vortex dynamics and interactions both away from boundaries and close to a no-slip
wall.
In chapter 4, a brief summary of the main results of the thesis is presented. The main conclu-
sions are highlighted and some ideas for future work are given.

2. Vorticity Topology
2.1
Introduction
A vortex is an important feature of fluid flows, which describes flow patterns in a local region.
In the close proximity of a vortex, whirling motion is dominant. This feature of a vortex has
been exploited in mathematics, physics, and engineering to investigate a wide range of phe-
nomena. Vortex dynamics has found its applications in the aerospace industry [1, 2], medicine
[3, 4], biology [5, 6], astrophysics [7, 8], and many more. Describing and analysing the flow
topology in terms of vortices provide many advantages. Any definition of a vortex should give
a description of the flow that is Galilean invariant. This allows comparison and analysis of
flows that are described in different inertial frames. Furthermore, analysing vortices focuses
attention on certain regions in the domain which carry the key important characteristics of the
flow. Therefore, vortex analysis helps in understanding fluid flow. The versatility and many
advantages of vortices have motivated studies on vortex dynamics and interactions.
Although the essence of a vortex lies in its simplest definition, a universal definition of a vortex
has yet to be reached. Since Helmholtz published his theorems on vortex dynamics [9], there
have been many attempts to define a vortex. The first generation of vortex identification meth-
ods was inspired by the Helmholtz Theorems for vorticity and made use of vorticity magnitude
in order to locate a vortex [9, 10, 11]. All of these methods suffered from needing a threshold
that is flow dependent. These methods were also unable to distinguish shear layers from vor-
tices, due to contamination of vorticity magnitude in regions of high shear. As pointed out by
Liu et al. [12], threshold-based criteria fail to identify vortices in boundary layers.
The following generation of definitions was derived from invariant quantities of some important
flow variables, such as velocity gradient. Some of the popularly used ones include the Q-criterion
[13], λ2-criterion [14], and ∆-criterion [15]. These define a vortex as a region where a certain
quantity is positive (or above a threshold), and as a result, give a description of a vortex
boundary instead of its core. The caveat is that often the vortex boundary alone does not
provide enough information.
Therefore, these methods are generally extended to visualise
“boundaries” of inner regions of a vortex. However, this approach again introduces thresholds
to identify vortices and suffers from the same problem. More recent methods try to address the
shortcomings of these definitions, such as the MZ-criterion [16], which is an objective definition
of a vortex.
Most of the modern definitions of vortices seem to have forgotten about the historical and rather
intuitive connection between a vortex and the vorticity field. Although these methods have
other physical interpretations of what a vortex is, it is still useful to identify a vortex using local
circulation. An alternative definition of a vortex, which we call the vorticity criterion, brings a
modern approach to vorticity-based identification, using the power of dynamical systems theory

and bifurcation theory. According to the vorticity criterion, a vortex is a local extremum of the
scalar vorticity field [17]. This approach is only valid for two-dimensional flows but has been
proven to be powerful in identifying important flow features [18, 19, 20].
It is shown that the vorticity criterion can be applied to analyse the flow in the wake of an
object. Recently, the vorticity criterion was successfully used to explain the formation of the
Kármán vortex street [18] and investigate boundary layer eruption [20]. However, the dynamics
of vortices have not been previously studied in a systematic way. The aim of this study is to
develop a novel numerical method to track vortices in the flow and use the vorticity criterion to
analyse vortex dynamics and interactions. In order to achieve this, a mathematical investigation
of vorticity topology is pursued and possible bifurcations in vorticity topology are derived.
2.2
The Vorticity Criterion
Regardless of the definition chosen, vortices are associated with local flow spin and are seen as
a measure of local rotation in the velocity field. In this aspect, vorticity (defined as the curl of
the velocity field, Equation 2.1) is one of the most natural parameters to locate and quantify
vortices.
ω = ∇× u
(2.1)
Vorticity is a function only of the derivatives of the velocity field. Therefore, it is Galilean
invariant. As the definition of the curl operator suggests, vorticity measures the infinitesimal
circulation of the velocity field. A positive vorticity describes a flow that locally spins in the
positive direction (counter-clockwise), and a negative vorticity is associated with a flow rotating
in the negative direction. However, only quantifying local rotation does not provide any useful
information in terms of vortex dynamics and interactions.
Instead of considering the whole vorticity field, one can focus on where the vorticity is concen-
trated or how the field changes its properties in space. This can be achieved systematically by
studying the topology of vorticity, specifically its critical points. Notice that vorticity is a vector
field and its gradient is a tensor field for three-dimensional flows. A definition for the critical
points of a vector field does not exist, let alone a theory for classifying and analysing them.
However, for two-dimensional flows, vorticity reduces to a scalar and the current dynamical
systems and bifurcation theory can be applied to track changes in its topology.
For a scalar vorticity field, a vortex is defined as a local extremum of the vorticity field. This
definition is consistent with the intuition that vortices are flow structures where local rotation
is dominant. Classifying only the critical points of vorticity gives a concise description of the
flow field. Furthermore, important flow phenomena can be identified by tracking the critical
points and investigating topological changes in the vorticity field. This is done in the following
sections with a detailed investigation of bifurcations in the vorticity field.

2.2.3
Topological Bifurcations of Vorticity
The equation of motion for the critical points is defined only if the Hessian is regular, as
can be seen from Equation 2.12. Alongside the dynamics of critical points, it is interesting
to identify the conditions for its existence and violation of these conditions. If the Hessian
becomes singular, i.e. det Hω
0 = 0, the non-degeneracy condition of Equation 2.4 is violated.
This signals a bifurcation when possibly t or another parameter is varied. A local bifurcation
occurs when a smooth change in the parameters results in local structural changes.
Fluid flow problems are often comprised of multiple parameters. Thus, the flow topology is
expected to be a function of multiple parameters, aside from time t. Some of these parameters
are a Reynolds number, and parameters characterising the geometry of the problem. If more
than one of the problem parameters approach a critical value such that a smooth variation
of the parameters results in a different flow topology, a higher-dimensional bifurcation occurs.
The codimension of the bifurcation is defined as the minimal number of parameters needed to
display the bifurcation in a robust way. An investigation of local bifurcations of the vorticity
topology that will be encountered in this study is presented below.

of the same type, and B is of the opposite type. At a critical time, a creation event occurs above
point A, creating B and A∗, as shown in 2.10. In the range γ∗< γ < γ∗∗, three bifurcation
events are observed. At the first critical time, a creation occurs above point A. Points B and
A∗are created in this event. At the second critical time, point A is destroyed with point B.
Point A∗survives these bifurcations. At the third and last critical time, yet another creation
event occurs. Point A∗lies above the creation event. Note that point A∗becomes the point A

of the last creation event. In this γ-range, the bifurcations are comprised of one hysteresis and
one extra creation event.
At γ = γ∗, point A experiences a creation event above it. Different from higher γ-values, the
second and third bifurcation events occur at the same time. Point A is destroyed with point B,
at the same instance of a creation event. The topology is similar to that of a simple bifurcation.
The newly created points are marked in red. Points A∗and B survive these events. At the
second critical γ-value, γ∗∗, the first and second bifurcation events occur at the same time.
Point A becomes momentarily degenerate, but continues its motion. At a later critical time, a
creation event occurs below it.
For high γ-values, the critical point patterns are the same as that observed for γ > 0 in
a pitchfork bifurcation. At a critical time, a creation event occurs below the initial critical
point. In summary, two types of pitchfork bifurcations admit similar flow topologies in certain
γ-regimes. γ < γ∗range of a perturbed pitchfork is structurally the same as γ < 0 range of an
unstable pitchfork. Similarly, γ > γ∗∗range of a perturbed pitchfork describes the same flow
patterns as γ > 0 of an unstable pitchfork.
The main difference between these bifurcations is the critical cases. The symmetric critical point
pattern of an unstable pitchfork perturbs into two critical cases. Consequently, the creation
of two new critical points perturbs into one creation event, followed by a destruction event,
followed by another creation event. Since the difference between the two types of pitchfork
bifurcations lies in the range γ∗< γ < γ∗∗, computationally it is not easy to distinguish
these two bifurcations. It is highly likely that only the earliest and the latest topologies will
be captured. Since symmetry is a very strong requirement, it is assumed that the unstable
pitchfork bifurcation will almost never be the case for observed vortex patterns.
Depending on the type of the initial critical point, point A, the bifurcation describes two
different phenomena.
If point A is an extremum, i.e.
a vortex, the bifurcation is termed
supercritical perturbed pitchfork bifurcation. In this case, an exchange of the pathlines of vortices
occurs over a wide range of bifurcations. This kind of bifurcation can make substantial changes
in vortex topology as it can considerably alter the pathline of the vortex. If point A is a saddle,
the bifurcation is termed a subcritical perturbed pitchfork bifurcation. In this case, a change of
saddles occurs through the generation of one short-lived and one new vortex.

Global Bifurcations
The study of local bifurcations deals with local stability changes, and topological changes
in local invariant sets, such as the critical points.
On the other hand, global bifurcations
concern larger invariant sets [25]. Changes in the level-set curves of vorticity are studied under
global bifurcations. As mentioned in subsection 2.2.1, the saddle-level curves of vorticity are
especially interesting as they can be interpreted as vortex boundaries. If there are two saddles
in the close proximity of a vortex, the homoclinic orbit from one saddle would enclose the
vortex, whereas the level curve of the other saddle would be following the homoclinic orbit
from outside. However, the vortex might change the saddle it uses as its “boundary”. In such

Hopf Bifurcation
Hopf bifurcation describes the change in the stability of a dynamical system from a steady
solution to a time-periodic solution. Consider the linearized dynamical system around a critical
point. If all the eigenvalues of the linear operator have negative real parts except for two purely
imaginary ones, a Hopf bifurcation occurs when these eigenvalues cross the imaginary axis
[25]. Therefore, this is not a topological bifurcation but a dynamical bifurcation. The system
exhibits a selected frequency as soon as it crosses into periodicity.
The normal form of a supercritical bifurcation is described by Equation 2.41, where r is the
amplitude of a variable, and γ is the bifurcation parameter. The critical point is stable against

The stable zero solution for γ < 0 loses its stability and becomes unstable. A stable limit
cycle appears for γ > 0. The bifurcation diagram of a Hopf bifurcation is given in Figure 2.12.
The flow around an unbounded cylinder undergoes a Hopf bifurcation at the critical Reynolds

2.3.1
Problem Definition
The flow around a surface submerged in a confinement is important for many engineering
applications. The confinement may be due to space restrictions such as in wind tunnels or
water ducts; or the hydrodynamic interaction with the confinement could be exploited as in
cooling systems [26], turbomachines [27, 28], building sections [29], and bridge piers [30]. It
is, therefore, essential to systematically study such flows. A standard way of doing this is to
analyse the flow around a cylinder.
The flow around an unconfined cylinder has been studied extensively in the past. For very
low Reynolds numbers up to Re = 4, the flow is in a creeping flow regime where streamlines
follow the cylinder body [31]. At low Reynolds numbers, fixed pairs of vortices appear in the
wake of the cylinder [32]. The steady regime breaks into a time-periodic flow through a Hopf
bifurcation.
The critical Reynolds number is reported as Rec ≈40, by Roshko, and Ji et
al. [33, 34]. As the Reynolds number is further increased, 3D effects become prominent, and
transition into turbulent flow occurs [35]. This study only concerns the range where the flow is
two-dimensional.

2.3.2
Numerical Setup
CFD Setup
The simulations are performed using the open-source spectral element framework Nektar++
version 5.2.0 [41]. The Navier-Stokes operator is converted into the variational formulation
using the continuous Galerkin method. For the solution basis, triangular Taylor-Hood elements
with 6th-order Lagrange basis functions are used. The variational problem is integrated using a
higher-order splitting method, which allows explicit advection of the non-linear terms, followed
by solving the pressure Poisson equation and a Helmholtz problem to enforce the viscous terms
and the velocity boundary conditions [42, 43].
In order to preserve the accuracy and resolve time changes in the flow, a second-order accurate
implicit-explicit time integration scheme of order two is used. The time step is chosen so that
the maximum CFL number over the whole domain does not exceed 0.4. The matrix equations
are solved up until a tolerance level of 1e-9.
The simulations are performed on the High
Performance Computing Center of DTU on LSF10 cluster. sixty-four cores are used for each
simulation case, and 8 cores are used for the post-processing of the data. The computations on
LSF10 cluster used a mix of Intel Xeon Gold 6226R, Intel Xeon Gold 6342, AMD EPYC 7542,
and AMD EPYC 7543 CPUs. The final processing of the critical points to track the vorticity
criterion is performed on a laptop with six AMD Ryzen 5 5500U processors.
The boundary conditions are set equivalent to that described in Figure 2.13. The inlet profile
imposes the same flowrate that would be set by a flat velocity profile of U∞. Setting a fully-
developed velocity profile at the inlet reduces the length of the mesh needed in front of the
cylinder, as well as reducing the errors emanating from the corners of the domain. In order
to obtain a determinate system of algebraic equations, pressure is only needed at the outlet.
However, higher-order pressure boundary conditions are employed at all the Dirichlet-type
boundaries to improve solver stability. For outflow, a higher-order outflow condition is used to
remove any reflection from the outlet boundary.
The simulations are started from an initial condition same as the inlet profile at each vertical
section of the domain. Aerodynamic forces on the cylinder are recorded at each time step.
The time-averaged vorticity field is recorded every 50 time steps. For each value of the gap
parameter, an initial simulation is run until the deviation in the time-averaged vorticity field
is less than 3%. The flow is assumed to converge into a periodic solution, and a second run is
performed to investigate local bifurcations. The second simulation is run for 18 vortex-shedding
periods. Post-processing of the results is explained in detail in subsection 2.3.3.

Shedding Frequency Calculation
Vortex shedding is the main phenomenon that needs to be resolved with high accuracy. The
physics of shedding involves many mechanisms which are active in different regions of the
domain. The pressure is high in the aft of the cylinder, and there is a low-pressure zone in the
wake. At the beginning of the shedding, flow is accelerated above the cylinder. Flow separation
occurs at a certain angle, after which the accelerated fluid enters a zone where the pressure is
high further outside the cylinder and the pressure is low down towards the centreline. Therefore,
the flow is pushed towards the wake by the pressure and changes direction.
This creates
a negative/positive recirculation in the wake, which creates a vortex with negative/positive
vorticity.
Vortex shedding can be divided into two main mechanisms: a suction phase where fluid is
sucked from upstream and a vortex is created; and a blowing phase where the flow is ejected
towards the wake and the vortex fully separates from the cylinder. The pressure difference
between the aft and the wake increases during suction, and consequently the drag increases.
However, the pressure difference drops during the blowing phase, which decreases the drag.
Therefore, the drag experiences one cycle of fluctuations during the creation of one vortex. On
the other hand, both of these phases have the same impact on the lift. Pressure decreases where
the vortex is created, and increases on the opposite side.
The same processes are repeated for the other half of shedding, where the suction direction
is changed. If the fluid is sucked above the cylinder in the first half, a vortex with negative
vorticity is created. The creation of the vortex decreases the pressure above the cylinder and
increases the lift as a result. During the second half, a positive vortex is created. The creation
of a positive vortex decreases the pressure difference, and thereby the lift decreases. During
one period of vortex shedding, the lift force undergoes one period of oscillation.
The cross-stream components of vortex shedding patterns are opposite to each other. Thus,
the lift force has only one dominant frequency which is the same as the shedding frequency.
However, the streamwise components of these flow patterns are in the same direction. Their
effect on the drag force depends on the strength of the vortices created. So, the drag force
has two dominant frequencies: the shedding frequency and double the shedding frequency to
distinguish two halves of shedding. Using Fourier expansion, the aerodynamic forces can be
expressed as a function of their most dominant frequencies, as given in Equation 2.45.

As

Fourier Transform (FFT), least-square fitting, and Poincaré mapping.
The easiest way of decomposing a signal into its frequency components is by applying FFT
to the signal. By FFT, the amplitude and phase shift of all frequencies in the signal can be
obtained.
As stated earlier, the simulations are run for 18 shedding-periods after the flow
field has converged into a time-periodic solution. This is not always long enough to accurately
predict the signal frequency. Although simulating for an extra period has always the same
computational cost, it has diminishing marginal benefit for the FFT results. Therefore, it is
hard to get an accurate prediction of shedding frequency under moderate computational costs.
One other drawback of this approach is that it is more susceptible to noise compared to other
methods. As the force signal will likely have some noise, a more heuristic approach could lead
to better results.

squares sense can be performed to calculate the shedding frequency. Four parameters are chosen
to best-fit the lift coefficient signal: mean lift µ, the amplitude of the oscillation A, shedding
frequency f, and phase shift of the signal ϕ. As a starting guess for the fitting algorithm, the
mean of the signal, half its range, the frequency prediction from FFT, and 0 are given. This
approach is shown to be much more accurate and precise in finding the shedding frequency, but
still not up to desired level. It is also found that this approach gets more accurate for smaller
gap parameters.

2.3.3
Methodology for Tracking Critical Points
The vorticity criterion requires the calculation of the derivatives of vorticity up to second-
order. Since the numerical solutions are obtained for the primitive variables u, v, and p, a
post-processing routine is developed to first calculate the necessary quantities, then to use
them to locate vortices, and finally to track the motion of the vortices.
The first step of
post-processing is performed in the Nektar++ environment. Using vorticity and gradient
modules of FieldConvert utility, vorticity and its partial derivatives up to second-order are
calculated. In order to make use of the higher-order metadata in the Nektar++ environment
and to increase the accuracy of further post-processing that will follow, the obtained solutions
are projected onto a finer grid.
Locating critical points from the CFD results is performed using the open-source scientific data
analysis and visualisation software ParaView version 5.10.1 [46]. In order to locate the critical
points of vorticity, zero-level curves of its gradient vector are drawn. Intersections of these
curves are marked as the critical points, as shown in Figure 2.19. The critical points’ positions,
values of the vorticity, and the determinant of its Hessian at these points are exported for each
time step. A snapshot of the flow field is also recorded separately. This process is automatized
via a Python-interface script, given in Appendix C. For the rest of the analysis which involves
tracking the critical points in time, a Python post-processing script is written.
Each step in the generation of the final data has associated errors. Discretisation errors associ-
ated with the CFD results stem from the order of basis functions and the computational grid.
The polynomial order and the mesh parameters are selected according to a grid independence
study, and the discretisation errors in the CFD setup were concluded to be within an acceptable
range. Moreover, usage of Taylor-Hood elements gives optimal convergence for basis functions
of order higher than two [47]. The numerical errors stemming from matrix solvers are predicted

to distinguish noise and the actual geometry of the vorticity. Although locating the vortices is
predicted to contribute the most to the gross error, it is assumed that the grid is refined enough
to result in a reasonably smooth tracking of the critical points.
The goal of the tracking algorithm is to extract the pathlines of the critical points from the
data at each time step. To achieve this, the pathlines of the critical points are described in
space-time as a parametric curve (x(t), y(t)). The functional dependence of the position on time
t is modelled by a polynomial fit in the least-squares sense. Every critical point is assumed
to either be (i) a new point in an “active” pathline, (ii) the first point of a new pathline, or
(iii) a spurious point that should not exist but has been created during a prior post-processing
routine. Here, an active track is a list of ordered points (ordered in time) where the time step
interval between any adjacent elements is less than an allowed limit, maxIdle. The aim is to
capture data points of type (i) and (ii), and disregard points of type (iii).
It is assumed that the points close to the endpoints of an active track can be used to predict
the future position of the critical point. The Euclidean 2-norm between a prediction made
using these points and a data point that lies on the pathline should be small. Conversely, if
a point is a spuriosity, it should not be followed by new noisy data points in later time steps
that when combined result in a pathline. Therefore, points belonging to pathlines are assumed
to come from ordered data with lower noise than that created as pure noise. The proposed
algorithm matches the critical points with active tracks in two main steps: a prediction step,
and a matching step based on predictions and nearest distance.

xp,i and elements of the data vector xdata,j, where i and j denote the indices of the said points
in the associated vector, and should not be confused with coordinate indices. In order to ease
the matching process later on, an extra term is added to the distance based on the type of the
critical point. If the active track (and hence the predicted point) and the point from the data
are of the same type (vortex-vortex or saddle-saddle), the distance is not changed. Otherwise,
the distance is changed such that it is effectively infinite. This is performed in the second term
of Equation 2.50, where the sign of the determinant of the vorticity is denoted by sgn Hi for
the active track i, and by sgn Hj for the data point j. This allows to distinguish a vortex from
a saddle near a bifurcation. Notice that D is not necessarily a square matrix. Its number of
rows is equal to the number of active tracks, and its number of columns is equal to the number
of data points.
Dij = |xp,i −xdata,j|2 + (sgn Hi −sgn Hj) · 1099
(2.50)
Once the difference matrix is assembled, the next task is to find the best matches between the
predictions and the data points. The gist of the method is to find tuples of points where the
prediction point is the closest point to the matched data point, and the data point is the closest
point to the matched prediction point. A minimum search algorithm is implemented for this
purpose. The search starts from the first row of the matrix. The minimum element of that row
is found, and the search direction is switched to “column”. Then, the minimum element of that
column is found, and the search direction is switched to “row” again. Following, the minimum
of the row is found. This search is continued recursively, and each row and column where a
turn is taken is recorded. Once the search algorithm reaches a dead end, a tuple of a prediction
point and a data point has been found, where these two points are closest to each other. This
tuple is recorded and the corresponding row and column are taken out of the matrix. The
search algorithm goes one step back and continues until it reaches a dead end again. Each time
a tuple is found, they are taken out of the matrix and the search continues from where it is
left. This is continued until all possible matches have been made. Note that, not all matches
made in this manner are meaningful. The algorithm will continue matching until the matrix
runs out of rows or columns.
==PAGE:39==
2.3. APPLICATION TO FLOW AROUND A CONFINED CYLINDER
29
If the number of critical points is larger than the number of active tracks, then the non-matched
critical points are immediately added as new active tracks. If the converse is true, the non-
matched active tracks are marked under a list containing the indices of those tracks. Following,
each tuple is investigated if the distance between them is under a set tolerance. The maximum
tolerance is set as dmax = 0.1D. If this condition is not satisfied, the index of the track is
added to the non-matched tracks, and the data point is added as a new active track. After this
check is made for all tuples, the non-matched tracks are checked for idle activity. If an active
track is not able to find a match for longer than an allowed time step duration, it is taken
out of the workflow and put inside a list of passive tracks. The maximum allowed idle time
is selected to be 4 time steps. So far, the algorithm is able to both find matching pathlines
for data points and identify them if they do not belong to any pathline but are the first point
of a new pathline. However, spurious data points are not eliminated yet. This is done in the
active-to-passive track transition state. As mentioned, it is assumed that the noisy data is not
ordered enough to be modelled as least-squares polynomials. Therefore, it is less likely that
noisy data will produce longer pathlines. A cap on minimum pathline length is introduced to
eliminate all short pathlines which are produced by noisy data. A proper pathline is selected to
contain at least 4 points. This ensures that the algorithm is able to distinguish between noise
and proper data.
The prediction and matching steps are repeated for every time step until all the time steps
are processed and a list of pathlines is obtained. The algorithm is initiated by labelling all
data points in the first time step as new active tracks. After the last time steps, all the long
enough active tracks are put inside the final pathlines list. This list is then plotted and local
bifurcations of critical points are spotted, as shown for γ = 2.0 in Figure 2.23. The main
operations performed by the algorithm are shown below. The algorithm is implemented into a
Python code, given in Appendix D.

2.4
Conclusions
Vortices are strong tools for understanding fluid flows. They convey the most important infor-
mation in the flow concisely. The aim of this study is to develop a novel numerical method to
investigate vortex dynamics and their interactions. The vorticity criterion is employed which
defines a vortex as an extremum of the scalar vorticity field. Therefore this criterion is only
valid for two-dimensional flows. Possible bifurcations up to codimension two are identified.
The resulting vortex topologies are formulated and presented with illustrations. The bifurca-
tion patterns are demonstrated by their physical interpretations.
A numerical algorithm is developed to track the vortices inside the flow. The algorithm con-
structs the pathlines of vortices, displaying their dynamics and interactions. Additional sub-
==PAGE:54==
2.4. CONCLUSIONS
44
routines are implemented to deal with noise and errors caused by various post-processing steps.
The theoretical results and the developed numerical method are used to analyse the flow around
a confined cylinder that is asymmetrically placed inside the confinement. The wall effect on
the vortex structure is examined in detail, identifying every codimension two bifurcation as the
cylinder approached the wall.
It is concluded that the wall has two main mechanisms to interact with the flow structures. In
early phases where the cylinder is further away from the wall, the wall creates weak vortices
which only interact within themselves. In this so-called wall-preparation phase, the interactions
are at most indirect, if not at all.
In this regime, isola bifurcation is commonly observed
which is responsible for creating a vortex only interacting with itself and its saddle. In an
intermediate phase termed the wall-effect phase, the wall structures are comparably stronger
than some of the main flow structures. The vortices created by the shedding either get trapped
between wall vortices or are weakened substantially by interacting with them. It is seen that
subcritical hysteresis is an important bifurcation for trapping vortices and exterminating them.
Perturbed pitchfork bifurcation is observed to take the role of changing the pathlines of strong
flow structures and binding them with wall structures. These often occur by interchanging the
saddle that destroys a main vortex with a saddle created by the wall. Similarly, a vortex that
destroys a main flow saddle can be interchanged with a wall vortex through a supercritical
perturbed pitchfork bifurcation.
These help to trap main flow structures and show direct
interactions between the wall and the flow. Wall effects are able to completely stop vortex
shedding below the cylinder at the end of the wall-effect phase.
During the wall-destruction phase, all the vortices and saddles are destroyed regardless they
are created by the wall or not. The wall simply kills any transients during this regime. Isola
bifurcation is commonly observed as most of the structures form vortex-saddle pairs and com-
pletely get erased from the flow through an isola. The flow converges to a steady solution for
a gap parameter of γ ≈0.64.
Through solving this problem, the power of employing bifurcation theory in analysing fluid flows
is demonstrated. The saddles of vorticity are interpreted as the duals of vortices that take
on important roles in creation and annihilation events. The developed numerical algorithm
is tested and found to be very useful in analysing two-dimensional flows using the vorticity
criterion. It is observed that noisy data close to codimension two events push the capabilities
of the algorithm, but it is in most cases still able to consistently draw vortex pathlines and
show the local bifurcations of codimension one and two.

3. Bifurcations of Q-Vortices
3.1
Introduction
Fluid flows exhibit a multitude of interesting dynamics. Therefore, it is essential to develop tools
in order to discover the key mechanisms leading to the complex dynamics we observe. Vortices
are among the commonly observed fluid mechanical structures, which carry compact and concise
information about the flow. These structures can convect with the flow, as well as stretch, twist,
and interact in ways that affect the flow. Previous research focusing on understanding these
effects have found that vortices are responsible for the creation of aerodynamic forces such as
the lift force [48, 49]; the dynamics of atmospheric phenomena like cyclones or polar vortices [50,
51]; and play major roles in mixing [52], and energy transport between turbulent structures [53,
54]. Thus, it is important for engineering science to understand and describe vortex dynamics.
Although simply defined as a region of whirling motion, there has not yet been an exact def-
inition of a vortex. Defining and locating vortices has been an open debate. However, one
consensus on the properties of a vortex has been reached: Galilean invariance. In continuum
mechanical sense, a Galilean invariant definition is invariant under the change of inertial refer-
ence frames. Therefore, these kinds of vortex definitions depict the same flow field irrespective
of the observer. Galilean invariance is a key property for engineering applications, as usually
a real-world problem is analysed through their Galilean transformed equivalent. For example,
aerodynamics tests are performed in wind tunnels whereas the real-world problem is of moving
bodies in a still medium.
One approach called the vorticity criterion defines vortices as critical points of the scalar vortic-
ity field. This definition investigates the topology of the vorticity field to locate and characterise
vortices and their interactions. It makes use of scalar vorticity which is only valid for 2D flows,
and a generalisation of it to three-dimensional flows does not exist. Popular choices for vortex
detection, which are also valid for both two- and three-dimensional flows, are the Q-criterion
of Hunt et al. [13], MZ criterion of Haller [16], λ2-criterion of Jeong [14], and ∆-criterion of
Chong [15]. All of these definitions locate the vortex as a region inside the fluid medium. This
study analyses the most popular of them: the Q-criterion.
The Q-criterion defines a vortex as a region where rotation locally dominates deformation. As
a consequence of this definition, vortex interactions happen through the boundaries enclosing
the vortex region. It is crucial to understand the topology of the boundary to resolve vortex
interactions. Topological changes in the boundaries of Q-vortices have been studied in the past
by Nielsen et al. [55] where the theory is developed only for vortices in two-dimensional flows.
To capture the full dynamics of vortices, a theory that is valid in 3D is required, which is
developed for incompressible flows in the following parts of this study.

3.5
Conclusions
In this chapter, a theory describing the dynamics of vortices in incompressible flows is formu-
lated. The Q-criterion is used to identify vortex regions, although the theory is valid for any
scalar function with similar requirements. Therefore, the formulated dynamics can be gener-
alized to other identification criteria such as the λ2-criterion or the ∆-criterion. All possible
codimension one bifurcations are identified, which covers the evolution of vortices. The theory
provides a systematic and robust way of characterising the dynamics and interaction of vortices
in three-dimensional flows.
The theory captures structural changes in a vortex through bifurcations in the vortex boundary.
Away from boundaries, the condition for a bifurcation is Q = 0 and ∇Q = 0. This results in two
possible bifurcations: punching and pinching. A punching bifurcation describes four possible
physical events, depending on the direction of time and where Q is positive. The creation and
destruction of a vortex is through a punching bifurcation. The creation of a hole inside the
vortex or the implosion of the hole are also associated with a punching bifurcation. Separation
and merging of vortices are described by a pinching bifurcation, as well as the creation and
destruction of a vortex ring. Vortex ring events describe a transition from a vortex with a
genus-zero surface to a genus-one surface.
Near a wall, the bifurcation condition is more complex due to the boundary conditions. A
bifurcation occurs when ∇eQ = 0 where eQ = Q
z2. Possible bifurcations are classified under
two names: wall-punching and wall-pinching, where each bifurcation can correspond to eight
physical events. Creation and destruction of a vortex by the wall, separation and merging of
a vortex with the wall, and creation and destruction of a hole inside a vortex (from either
side) near a wall are all described by wall-punching bifurcation. These events illustrate pos-
sible cases of single vortex-wall interactions and depict a picture of how a wall can weaken
or strengthen a vortex via energy transfer. They also show that for every vortex event, there
exists a corresponding “hole” event.

3.5. CONCLUSIONS

Wall-pinching bifurcations describe two different vortex structures where the vortex boundary
is shaped like a saddle. If the saddle-shaped boundary encapsulates the vortex, then the event
is a vortex merging/separation near the wall. Similar to a pinching bifurcation, the geometry
of the boundary during merging is described in a wall-pinching bifurcation.
If the vortex boundary lies between the vortex region and the wall, the bifurcation describes
the evolution of a hairpin vortex. The early time dynamics of a hairpin is identified as follows:
the vortex region lies on the wall. Its contact with the wall thins out in a specific place, almost
separating the vortex into two distinct wall supports. After the critical time, the wall supports
become separated. The main body of the vortex lifts and stretches into the flow. It has contact
with the wall through its two legs.
These theoretical results summarise the possible vortex interactions. They fully describe the
early dynamics of vortex creation, destruction, separation, and merging, as well as the unique
dynamics of vortex rings and hairpins.
These findings can provide useful insights for fluid
mechanics problems by portraying mathematically correct pictures of vortex dynamics and
their interactions.

4. Summary and Future Work
In this thesis, vortex dynamics are examined using dynamical systems and bifurcation theory.
Two different vortex identification criteria are used which allowed handling the problem from
different aspects. Nonetheless, the overall methodology remained the same: structural changes
in the vortex patterns are identified through topological bifurcations of a quantity describing
the flow. Therefore, the results obtained can be reproduced by applying the same approach to
other vortex identification criteria. The important events marking physical changes in vortices
are mathematically formulated and associated with the underlying physical mechanisms. These
allowed the construction of the bifurcation diagrams of these events to illustrate the complete
picture of vortex patterns.
From the study on vorticity topology, it is found that many events such as vortex creation,
destruction, merging, and separation can be explained by codimension one and codimension two
bifurcations. Furthermore, it is shown that tracking the pathlines of the critical points allows
the application of the vorticity criterion to analyse fluid flows. An accurate representation and
investigation of vortex interactions are achieved by developing a novel tracking algorithm. Wall-
effect is investigated in a flow past an asymmetrically placed cylinder in a confinement. The
physical mechanisms that the wall induces on the vortex patterns are reported as codimension
two bifurcations.
The systematic approach for tracking critical points using the vorticity criterion is proved to
be useful. This approach can be used in analysing other two-dimensional flows with industrial
importance. The theory and the algorithm can also be applied to rotating reference frames,
such that axisymmetric flows can be analysed. These flows have applications in turbomachinery
and understanding transition to turbulence. Another direction of research is generalizing the
vorticity criterion for three-dimensional flows.
In 3D, the vorticity becomes a vector, and
defining its critical points is a vague problem that could be addressed.
The capabilities of the developed algorithm are reported, one of which is being particularly
sensitive to noise near certain codimension two bifurcations. The predictor step can be improved
using not only the available coordinates of the critical points but also the velocity field. Even
if the flow field in close proximity of a critical point can be incorporated into the calculations,
the accuracy of the predictions should increase. For time-periodic flows, the periodicity can
be used as an improvement for the algorithm. Using a statistical averaging method can prove
beneficial to draw the pathlines of critical points in a more robust way.
Characterisation of vortex evolution using Q-criterion provided a full picture of vortex events
in three-dimensional flows. The analysis covers all codimension one bifurcations. The theory
for vortices away from boundaries explains not only elementary vortex phenomena such as
creation and annihilation, vortex merging and separation, but also the evolution of vortex
rings. The theory for vortices near a no-slip wall describes wall-vortex interactions. Again,

In conclusion, these studies are believed to provide a systematic approach to analyse vortices.
In line with the main objective of this thesis, vortex dynamics are mathematically modelled and
