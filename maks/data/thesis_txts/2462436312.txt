Abstract 
The purpose of the thesis is to explore entrepreneurial decision-making through 
normative, descriptive, and prescriptive theory application. Being exposed to difficult 
and complex decisions, the motivation for the thesis is based on the author’s own 
experience as a founder of a tech startup. The thesis investigates how accurately 
leading descriptive decision theories portray entrepreneurs. The thesis also investigates 
how probabilistic tools (Bayesian belief networks) can be used in both normative and 
prescriptive decision theory. The findings of the thesis are obtained by examining the 
academic literature and compiling qualitative data from surveying entrepreneurs and 
professionals. The thesis points out several shortcomings in current descriptive decision 
theory regarding entrepreneurs as it mainly refers to overconfidence, loss aversion, 
status quo bias, and prospect theory. Nevertheless, only small adjustments were 
needed for Prospect Theory to describe entrepreneurial decision-making well. The 
thesis also provides insights into how Bayesian belief networks yield substantially 
different scenario estimations compared to normal heuristics. The findings in the thesis 
pose questions for further investigation in both descriptive and prescriptive decision 
theory.

Introduction 
Originally I was inspired to pursue the subject of entrepreneurial decision-making and 
opportunity evaluation because of the types of decisions I was facing on a regular basis 
in my own tech startup. I recognized the unique nature of decision-making in a startup. 
Freedom to innovate and pivot, plus the lack of good hard data on the business, makes 
for very different frames for decision-making compared to large enterprises.   
I wished to learn more about the nature of decision-making to help myself and others 
make better decisions with their companies.  
The entrepreneurial journey is filled with uncertainty, and navigating through these 
uncertainties seems to be fundamental to the success of one’s business.  
Gaining insight into such uncertainties could potentially help create better decision-
making, and this was to me a sound goal for a master’s thesis.  
 
Research objectives:  
The objective of part 1 is to explain why entrepreneurship is important to research. In 
part 1 I want to explore the nature of entrepreneurs and to dissect entrepreneurial 
decision-making and opportunity evaluation. This will serve as a guide when navigating 
through the normative and descriptive theories of decision-making and how they 
pertain to entrepreneurial decision-making.  
 
The objective of part 2 is to explore relevant normative theories that may aid in 
entrepreneurial decision-making. In this part I will also explore the probabilistic 
graphical modeling tool, Bayesian belief networks, as an alternative normative decision 
aid. 
 
==PAGE:6==

The objective of part 3 is to explore relevant descriptive decision theories and 
understand how well they describe entrepreneurs. Through my own research I will 
challenge the dominant theories to increase my understanding of entrepreneurial 
decision-making in action.  
 
The objective of part 4 is to utilize the knowledge gained from the previous parts to 
argue for presenting a new prescriptive decision-making tool. Part 4 will also look at the 
conflict between modelled and intuitional decision-making.  
1.1 Why is entrepreneurship important? 
1.1.1 Entrepreneurship hype  
Before I argue for why entrepreneurship may be some of the answer to future 
challenges, I will recognize that like technologies, movements in culture can also follow 
Gartner's hype curve as seen below. Even though entrepreneurship is not a new 
phenomenon or a new technology, I think it is prudent to be wary of the overhype of 
any phenomenon in society. There has been an extraordinary glorification of 
entrepreneurs in media and politics that has resulted in a surge of new aspiring 
entrepreneurs--most prominently in tech. As I will explain, I think the mindset and 
culture of entrepreneurship have great potential to change and help the world, but I 
also think we might be somewhere near “the peak of inflated expectations” for what it 
can provide our society. Entrepreneurship is the new normal.

Survivorship bias refers to our tendency to focus on successes in a particular area and 
we try to learn from them while we completely forget the failings through the same 
strategies. Countless aspiring basketball players are likely to follow Michael Jordan's 
training regime but never made MVP or even the NBA. The issue here is that we really 
do not hear about the thousands of athletes that never make it, and for good reason--
it's not as good of a story. We therefore mistakenly overvalue the strategies, tactics, and 
advice from the “winners” and overhype professions that most likely will kick you out 
faster than you came in.  
A good example of this is the many extremely successful entrepreneurs that dropped 
out of university--Richard Branson, Bill Gates, Mark Zuckerberg and Elon Musk, to name 
a few. Does their story suggest you should just drop out and start a business, or are you 
more likely to be average and to actually benefit enormously from finishing your 
degree? According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics the average earnings of people with 
a college degree is still 38.5% higher than people without a degree (Bureau of labor 
statistics1). In other words, looking at earning statistically, definitely a good idea to finish 
- at least if you started in the first place.

1.2.1 Entrepreneurial alertness   
Being ready and alert for when an opportunity presents itself is a key factor and a 
common theme among successful entrepreneurs. That is what (Ardichvili 2003) found in 
a study exploring personality traits of successful serial entrepreneurs. 
If you are not actively attuned to the possibility of opportunity, you are far less likely to 
recognize it when it comes your way. Entrepreneurial alertness was a term coined by 
economist Israel Kirzner. The term describes the entrepreneurial state of mind, a 
heightened state of informational alertness necessary for recognizing opportunities. 
The term has been explored further by many scholars and in Entrepreneurial Alertness 
and Discovery (Tony Fu-Lai 2001) the concept is further described: “an opportunity 
exists only if it is perceived by the entrepreneur”. Even the most obvious opportunity 
can be ignored by a person who is not motivated to see it. In other words, individuals 
will not discover any profit opportunity if they ”switch off” their alertness systems. This 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
is something any aspiring entrepreneur should recognize and try to hone within 
themselves.  
An entrepreneur is traditionally described by (Kirzner 1993) as an arbitrage agent. In the 
arbitrage theory of profit (Kirzner 1973), he argues that the job of an entrepreneur is to 
correct the misallocation of resources by specializing in the discovery of missed 
opportunities. Simply put the nature of being an entrepreneur is discovering 
under/overvalue commodities/goods and supplying the market with a cheaper/better 
alternative.  
This can be interpreted solely as buy low, sell high--which it certainly is for some--but it 
can also be interpreted as the reallocation of silicon from silicon polymers into the 
silicon semi-conductors that drive the informational revolution of today. 
Though I think Kirzner's definition will be true for a long time, the nature of being an 
entrepreneur has certainly changed dramatically, from more arbitrage (buy low, sell 
high) businesses to much more innovation-driven.  
It is probably more likely that the change has come from the marketplace and the 
access to potentially disruptive methods like the internet and AI, than from a change in 
the nature of the entrepreneurs themselves. 
Alertness itself must come at a cost, maybe not in the traditional sense, but 
entrepreneurship sacrifices their attention which could be spent elsewhere. Kirzner 
sees alertness to opportunity as the core of entrepreneurial contribution to the 
economy in the paradigm of the division of labor (Adam Smith 1776). 
There have been attempts in cognitive science to understand entrepreneurial alertness.  
A bold attempt made by (Dave Valliere 2013) was the cognitive schemata theory from 
cognitive science. It was used to explain the ability of serial entrepreneurs to recognize 
an opportunity. Entrepreneurs like all others develop a framework of how the world 
works in their view. The schema organizes their perception of new information.   
The ability of individuals to spot opportunities will be highly dependent on their 
schemata of the world. Having an entrepreneurial schema will result in the allocation of 
attention towards spotting opportunities.  
Many of these ideas and interpretations adhere to the thinking of the Austrian school of 
economics. Carl Menger was one of the first economists to deeply explore the nature of 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
entrepreneurs, and because Austrian economics has had an interest in entrepreneurs 
as important drivers of the economy, they are often cited as an authority on the 
exploration of entrepreneurship.    
1.2.2 Prior knowledge  
Entrepreneurs often start ventures in fields in which they have some prior knowledge. 
This knowledge can generally be divided into three categories.  
1. Prior experience of operating in a certain market.  
2. Knowledge of ways to serve a certain market.  
3. Knowledge of customer problems.   
Due to prior knowledge the entrepreneur will recognize opportunities in that specific 
market that entrepreneurs without the knowledge would not be able to (Kirzner 1997).  
Prior knowledge allows for information asymmetry, a term explored vicariously by 
(George A. Akerlof 1970). The asymmetry of information in favor of the entrepreneur 
can be leveraged into a business opportunity. In other words, if you know something 
others do not, and you have the means to act on it, there is an opportunity. 
  
(Ardichvili 2003 and Sigrist 1999) propose two types of prior knowledge relevant to the 
identification process. The first type pertains to knowledge areas of special interest to 
the entrepreneur. These are areas toward which the entrepreneur naturally gravitates, 
has fun with and is fascinated by.  
Driven by his/her fascination, the entrepreneur engages deeply with the subject and 
becomes an expert. Becoming an expert and being on the cutting edge of knowledge in 
the field, affords the entrepreneur a huge advantage in taking the next incremental step 
or leap within this field, not afforded a layman. 
However, being an expert can generally, but not always, hinder your ability to see larger 
opportunities and connections between fields.   
  
The second domain pertains to knowledge accumulated over the years of work/study 
experience. This may not hold the same passion for the entrepreneur but is often more 
practical and structured in nature. Combining passion with a practical approach often 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
leads to the discovery of a new opportunity, a new market, or a new solution to 
customer’s problems. 
Prior knowledge is also found to have a significant impact on entrepreneurial alertness 
and learning as mentioned above. It is the interplay of the heightened alertness to 
opportunity and the heightened quality of recognition of the opportunity that makes 
prior knowledge an incredibly valuable piece in the formation of a successful 
entrepreneur (Hajizadeh 2016). 
1.2.3 Social network 
The opportunities that are afforded an entrepreneur are closely tied to the social 
network the entrepreneur keeps. This is not to say that it is impossible to begin 
ventures in fields with no network, but underestimating the power of a well-functioning 
network can very well lead to the demise of the project. The accuracy of the statement 
above is easily seen in places like Silicon Valley--everyone who wants to do anything in 
software comes here, if not to live, then at least to pay their respects to the almighty 
Sand Hill Road (location of the biggest venture capital firms).  
 
 
(Nasdaq) 
 
The Bay area is by far the leader in venture capital investment and supersedes the 
entire venture investment amount in Europe as a whole. Beijing is catching up fast, and 
this is where Chinese developers are moving. Being around opportunity is extremely 
important for opportunity recognition.  
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
In 1973 (Granovetter 1973) introduces the world to the concept of “The strength of weak 
ties”. In here he argues that most people hold more casual (weak) relationships then 
familiar (strong) relationships, and that the weak relationships have a much larger 
impact on the information we are exposed to. The weak ties are often selected from 
your immediate environment, so your location or exposure to people is important.  
Therefore the people you surround yourself with greatly impact the information you are 
exposed to.  
1.2.4 Effectuation 
The point above serves as a fine segway into one of the more important concepts in 
entrepreneurial opportunity recognition and evaluation: Effectuation, focusses on 
studying entrepreneurs, with the intent of using the knowledge to guide future 
entrepreneurs in how to approach a new venture. In effectuation there is a distinction 
between the normal managerial approach of causation and what the theory believes to 
be the entrepreneurial approach of effectuation.  
Definition: “Causation, processes take a particular effect as given and focus on selecting 
between means to create that effect. Effectuation processes take a set of means as 
given and focus on selecting between possible effects that can be created with that set 
of means” (Sarasvathy 2001) 
I will quickly take you through the principles of effectuation as I think they complement 
the rest of the review well. Much of the information below can be found on 
(www.effectuation.org). Effectuation holds an important position in entrepreneurial 
studies but will not play a significant role going forward.  
  
The “Bird in Hand principle” aligns well with the thought that a social network plays a 
large role in entrepreneurship. The principle is simple: Start taking action and use what 
is readily available to you. 1. Who you are and what makes you special, 2. What you 
know (prior knowledge and experience), and 3. who you know (social network). 
  
The “Affordable Loss principle” deals with evaluating opportunities based on the 
acceptability of the downside rather than on the attractiveness of the predicted upside. 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
By allowing estimates of affordable loss to drive decisions about which venture to start, 
entrepreneurs stop depending on predictions. Instead, they focus on cultivating 
opportunities that have a low failure cost and that generate more options for the future. 
  
The “Lemonade Principle” embraces surprises that arise from uncertain situations, 
remaining flexible rather than tethered to existing goals. In most contingency plans, 
surprises are undesired, but because entrepreneurs do not tie their idea to any 
theorized or preconceived “market,” anything and everything is potentially a surprise 
that can lead to a valuable opportunity. 
  
The “Crazy-Quilt Principle” forms partnerships with people and organizations willing to 
make a real commitment to jointly creating the future with you. Do not worry so much 
about competitive analyses and strategic planning. Obtaining pre-commitments from 
key stakeholders, suppliers, or customers helps reduce uncertainty in the early stages of 
creating an enterprise. Finally, since the entrepreneur is not wedded to any particular 
market for their idea, the expanding network of strategic partnerships determines, to a 
great extent, which market or markets the company will eventually end up entering or 
creating.  
  
The “Pilot in the Plane Principle” deals with the four specific principles above, 
representing different ways entrepreneurs interact with the environment to shape the 
environment. Of course, not everything can be shaped or controlled, but effectuation 
encourages you, as the pilot of your venture, to focus on the aspects in your 
environment that are within your control. 
  
The effectuation principles serve as an appealing summary of the point made above--
the importance of the social network, prior knowledge, and flexibility.

1.2.5 Psychographics and personality traits 
Is there such a thing as an entrepreneurial personality and what does it mean for 
opportunity recognition?  
It has been difficult for academia to nail the personality of a successful entrepreneur. In 
the last decades, researchers have primarily gravitated toward the Big-5 factor 
personality model to explain the entrepreneurial personality. The Big-5 traits also 
known as the OCEAN model, was famously used by Cambridge Analytica, to aid in 
Donald J. Trump's win in the 2016 presidential election targeting a specific content to 
specific users based on the OCEAN model. That was certainly an example of the model's 
correctness and effectiveness. However, for the purpose of entrepreneurial personality, 
several additional traits have been fused into the Big-5 to provide a deeper and more 
nuanced picture.  
The OCEAN model is the basic building block of most psychographic models, and a brief 
overview is all I can give you on the space available. 
O--Openness to experience, C--Conscientiousness, E--Extraversion, A--Agreeableness, N-
-Neuroticism.  
In a extensive meta-analysis Zhao and Seibert (2006) found entrepreneurs to be more 
open to experience, more conscientious, similar for extraversion, less agreeable, and 
less neurotic compared to people in managerial positions (or in the Big-5 lingo, O+, C+, 
E, A-, N-). They suggest that higher degree of Conscientiousness is the most significant 
difference between entrepreneurs and managers (C+). Of course, not all studies agree 
on this specific combination of personality for entrepreneurs but especially two of the 
five traits recur often. O + and N- mean high in Openness to experience and low in 
Neuroticism. 
O describes the breadth, depth, originality, and complexity of an individual’s mental and 
experiential life. This has some comparability to entrepreneurial alertness, explained 
not as a state of mind but more as a trait of personality.  
N describes the tendency to experience feelings, such as feeling anxious, nervous, sad, 
and tense. It is understandable if the turbulent and uncertain life of an entrepreneur 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
better suits those with a mild and even-tempered personality compared to the nervous 
and neurotic.       
  
A common critique of the Big-5 model when analyzing entrepreneurs is the generality of 
the traits in the model. To create a more coherent and customized portrait of the 
entrepreneurs, researchers have shifted toward creating a multidimensional 13 
personality framework that incorporates other qualities like self-efficacy, 
innovativeness, locus of control, need for achievement and risk attitudes.  
  
Especially self-efficacy and risk attitudes play a large role in the recognition, evaluation, 
and excursion of opportunities. Self-efficacy is the belief we have in our abilities, 
specifically our ability to meet the challenges ahead of us and complete a task 
successfully (Akhtar 2008). Self-efficacy is in the opinion of many researchers the 
quintessential trait of entrepreneurs. In the uncertain environment of venture creation, 
entrepreneurs need a strong sense of personal self-efficacy to execute their visions. 
Remaining steady in their beliefs, when faced with difficult situations, one can 
understand why this would be an important trait.  
Researchers have found entrepreneurs to have a higher self-efficacy rate than 
managers (Chen 1998), though I would say the amount of empirical evidence in this field 
is relatively sparse. It has been difficult for researchers to agree to a standard for testing 
and surveying these traits; that is why the evidence is sparse. 
Studies found that optimistic individuals are more likely to enter into entrepreneurship 
and also exert more effort after having entered (Manove 2000). The same paper also 
underlines how prevalent over-optimism (optimism toward positive event coming to 
fruition compared to the real probability) is among business executives. 
Researching this area, Krueger and Dickson (1994) found an interesting correlation 
between feelings of self-competence, self-confidence, and risk-taking, which is again 
closely related to opportunity recognition. Subjects in the study who were encouraged 
and led to believe that they were very competent in decision-making and opportunity 
evaluation, were far more willing risk-takers. The team similarly found that the subjects 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
who received the opposite treatment reacted with less willingness to risk-take. (Krueger 
and Dickson 1994) 
I think this synergy between traits nicely explains why entrepreneurs with high self-
efficacy jump on opportunities that normal people would not.  
I think some of the entrepreneurial alertness may stem from the core knowledge that 
you as an entrepreneur with high self-efficacy are ready to grab an opportunity if it 
presents itself. 
Their research also provides evidence that the perceived likelihood of an event 
happening is based on whether the predicted outcome is positive or negative.  
Your willingness to take risks influences your ability to correctly predict the outcome of 
a certain situation. The more risk-willing the entrepreneur, the more likely they are to 
predict events as positive.   
The willingness to take risk is not exclusively a positive trait in an entrepreneur. It is 
obvious that a higher risk profile also increases the number of entrepreneurs that fail 
and make incorrect predictions about how profitable their idea is. According to the 
Bureau of Labor Statistics only around 30% of businesses started are still operational 
after 10 years (BLS survival rate). This is not to say that the business did not make 
money along the way and pay good salaries during this time; nor does survival mean 
that the company was profitable. In other words, a lot of entrepreneurs definitely either 
lack the ability to execute or the ability to evaluate an opportunity correctly.  
  
(Åstebro et al. 2014) examined the relationship between optimism, overconfidence, and 
entrepreneurial activity. They found like other researchers that there may be a link 
between self-efficacy and overconfidence when estimating opportunity--this could 
explain the willingness to engage in such risky behavior as venture creation. 
  
High self-efficacy and overconfidence are traits commonly found in entrepreneurs, and 
it would seem that the traits themselves foster entrepreneurial activity. This reminds 
me of the aspiring pop stars on shows like American idol. So many try their luck at 
becoming singers and believe they have talent, but in reality, they are just average or 
worse. Only a few have what it takes to make it. 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
Even if the same is true for venture creation, it does seem like a must to believe in 
yourself and what you are doing in order to find your opportunity--the risks are simply 
too great to venture into the unknown without. 
  
In short, the above presentation was my overview and understanding of 
entrepreneurial opportunity recognition and evaluation as seen in academia. The 
review prepares you for the next chapters.  
1.2.6 Self efficacy and overvaluation  
I wondered how I could take a closer look at how self-efficacy manifests itself in the 
current business world. Understanding the impact of self-efficacy in the entrepreneurial 
pursuit is possibly the most important aspect when trying to get a comprehensive 
picture of the drives that direct the entrepreneurs. It is obviously not easy to enter the 
mind of a large number of early-stage entrepreneurs. I feel lucky that entrepreneurship 
has become entertainment as well as reality, and the negotiation between 
entrepreneurs and investors are broadcasted on TV weekly.  
In TV-shows like Shark Tank, Dragons’ Den, and even the Danish Løvens Hule, we get an 
inside look at the negotiations between the investors and the hopeful entrepreneurs.   
I am well aware that it is not actually how negotiations take place in real life, but the 
program does contain real people and real money. Since we do not get to be a fly on 
the wall at normal negotiations, this is by far the largest sample size of equity

2.1 Introduction 
In this chapter I will analyze and compare the most relevant and dominant theories of 
decision-making, looking at 1. Normative decision theory, which describes what 
people ought to do, the optimal decisions given certain constraints and assumptions, 2. 
Descriptive decision theory, which analyzes how actors actually make decisions in real 
life. The latter should give us some insight into the decision-making of entrepreneurs. 
These two branches may be merged into a prescriptive model that could benefit the 
entrepreneurs, joining the understanding of optimal decision-making and the nature of 
decision-making. I will examine prescriptive models in part 4. 
2.1.1 What kind of decisions are we looking at? 
Some decisions do have a correct or a incorrect answer, but most important decisions 
are inherently probabilistic in nature. These types of decision are therefore much more 
susceptible to biases and other decision pitfalls. 
  
Everyday decisions are often learned over time and programmed into our daily actions 
in ways where most do not even notice. Here I am referring to mundane decisions such 
as the route you take to work, the letterhead on your email or whether or not you greet 
your neighbor in the morning. These types of decisions are called programmed 
decisions and these decisions often occur frequently enough for us to develop an 
automated response to them. 
  
On the other hand, it's hard to find a more complex and less programmed decision than

Uncertainty: The lack of certainty is the existence of multiple possibilities.

Risk: Probabilities are observable–there are normally 38 slots on a roulette wheel. This

2.2.1 Expected value theory  
Expected value theory was the starting point for both the descriptive and normative 
theories and was at one point the best way of modeling decision-making. 
Expected value theory explained simply: If there is a p% chance of winning X$, the 
expected value is equal to p*X. If there is a p% chance of X and a q% chance of Y, then 
Expected Value = pX+qY. 
Daniel Bernoulli, and his work with the St. Petersburg paradox2, proved expected utility 
theory violated the way people made choices and it could therefore not be the theory 
that anticipated the best decisions. (Sommer 1954) 
Expected value theory states that you should and would always choose the option with 
the highest expected value. 
2.2.2 Expected utility theory EU 
Bernoulli later developed Expected utility theory EU (Bernoulli 1738) that views the 
actors as wanting to maximize expected utility and not expected value. Utility is 
fundamentally personal, and this gave the theory a new dimension compared to 
expected value theory.

2.2.2.1 Logical axioms  
Expected utility is predicated on logical axioms4 that served as constraints for the 
theory.   
The Axioms in EU define a rational decision-maker, and they are relevant to all rational 
decision-making models and are logically correct.  
 
The Completeness axiom assumes an actor to have a well-defined preference between 
alternatives. The actor either prefers A > B or B > A or is indifferent when A = B.  
 
The Transitivity axiom assumes an actor to decide according to the completeness 
axiom and for the actor to decide consistently. In terms of utility transitivity, it means 
that if A = B and B = C then A = C or if A > B and B > C then A > C.  
 
The Independence axiom assumes an actor picking between two relevant alternatives 
introduced to a third irrelevant alternative, will still maintain the same order of 
preference toward the two relevant alternatives as if the third alternative was never 
presented. In other words, the preference order of relevant choices is independent of 
irrelevant choices.

2.2.3.1 Bayesian probability modelling     
Bayesian inference is an alternative to the classical frequentist statistics.  
Bayesian inference interprets probabilities as a degree of belief or confidence in the 
occurrence of a certain event. When looking at an event we may have a prior belief 
about the event, but to make sound predictions we will have to update those beliefs 
when presented with new information. Bayesian statistics allows us to incorporate our 
prior beliefs and evidence to produce new posterior beliefs. 
 
The Bayesian approach is much more useful in cases with little or no data available 
because of its ability to incorporate expert judgment into the model. Expert judgment 
or “belief” is used as a subjective understanding of a probability in cases where evidence 
of data is lacking. Understandably this is dangerous for the robustness of the model, 
but it gives us the opportunity to incorporate the heuristics of the entrepreneur into our 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
models. Remember we are interpreting probability as a reasonable expectation 
represented by the state of our current knowledge.  
 
Bayesian inference has two approaches: 1. It simply evaluates the joint probability of a 
particular assignment. 2. In this approach we find P(x|e), meaning we can find the 
probability of a variable, given a certain event happens. This allows us to evaluate 
different scenarios in the same model, and can actually give us insight into the 
underlying event that we did not possess previously.  
2.2.3.1.1 Bayes theorem 
Everything Bayesian is based on Bayes theorem, named after its inventor 
mathematician Thomas Bayes. 
Bayes theorem simply describes the probability of an event, based on knowledge that 
related to this event. 
Time spend at work may be related to being wealthy. In Bayes theorem, time spent at 
work would be treated as a clue, and by incorporating that knowledge we can make a 
better prediction of whether the person is wealthy or not.

2.2.3.2 Bayesian belief networks BBN 
 
 
 
 
 
 
                    
A BBN is a probabilistic directed acyclic graphical model that is utilized when dealing 
with complex problems. The networks come in many forms depending on the case and 
objective. The network can help visualize the relationships between the nodes in the 
network (direction and strength of links between nodes).  
Each node in the network has a state or a value–in our scenario above the state of the 
world can either be s1 or s2; each has an assigned probability.  
   
A simple BBN – Figure 5 
 
 
Edges and links are used to represent the relationship between the nodes, and the 
direction of links indicates causality. Creating a useful network relies heavily on correctly 
estimating the strength of the links between the nodes. 
The network will allow us to make inferences about a seemingly unrelated event and 
observe the impact of nodes on each other–the probabilistic relationships between 
multiple events. 
BBNs rely highly on prior beliefs but will adjust any falsehoods as new data comes in. In 
cases were data is limited, the initial assumptions become very important (as in our 
case).  
==PAGE:38==

BBNs are usually used for machine learning problems, with continuous input of 
information. BBNs perform very well in cases of reliable prior beliefs coupled with a 
feed of information to update and fine-tune the models. Naive Bayes algorithms are 
very powerful for classification tasks.  
For simplifying this inquiry, the networks we will be looking at are causal networks that 
follow Markovian axioms, which means that each event depends only on the state of 
the previous event (parent and children nodes, i.e., nodes need a link to impact each

3.1 Introduction  
SEU was the first real attempt at making a descriptive model for decision-making, but 
the theory could still only account for rational actors and must therefore still be seen as 
a normative theory that takes the actor’s viewpoint into account. 
This brings us to Bounded rationality, which is less a theory of decision-making than it 
is a constraint on the theories that try to explain descriptive decision-making. 
Herbert A. Simon proposed an alternative to the view of rational actors in his work 
Models of man (Simon 1957). He explains how actors, in reality, cannot act rationally as 
the circumstances for producing well-thought and rational decisions are rarely present. 
Decision-makers are cognitively as well as time limited and this reduces their ability to 
be completely rational actors.  
Simon suggests that actors are only partly rational, and their cognition and environment 
can restrain their rationality.  
Adding this understanding about the decision process developed descriptive decision 
theory and contributed to a vast array of descriptive theories that are more involved 
with the psychological and cognitive aspects of decision-making than the  
mathematical ones. 
3.2 Systems for decision-making  
Segmenting decision types into cognitive processes can help us further understand 
what mechanism and theories to focus on to progress.  
The two major theories in this field come from behavioral economics/psychology and 
cognitive and computer science. First is Daniel Kahneman's Systems theory from his 
famous book Thinking, fast and slow (Kahneman 2011). 
  
==PAGE:46==
46 
 
The theory is founded on countless experiments and builds upon other solid theories 
from the hand of Kahneman and his colleague Amos Tversky. The title refers to the two 
systems in the theory. System 1 is the automatic, fast, and often unconscious way we 
think. System 1. is regarded as the autonomous system, it is efficient, requiring little 
energy or attention, but is prone to certain biases, systematic errors, and heuristics. 
System 2 is slow, requires cognitive effort and deliberate thinking. Mental energy has to 
be allocated toward the process and it will not work without attention. System 2 has the 
ability to mitigate some of the flaws that come with system 1, but system 2 comes with 
its own set of biases.  
The second relevant theory is the LIDA model (Learning Intelligent Distribution Agent), 
one of several theories in the field of computer science (Franklin et. al 2016). LIDA is a 
theory of cognitive architecture, that has been fairly influential in the pursuit of 
computer science to understand the human mind. Underlying the theory are two 
hypotheses: 1. Human cognition employs frequent iteration called cognitive cycles 
which involve interaction between conscious thinking, memories, and input; 2. The 
cycles act as “atoms” of cognition, from which higher-level cognitive processes are built. 
- It is the content and the amount of the cycles that determine what kind of mental 
process is being carried out. 
 
The LIDA cognitive cycle phase diagram 
    
Figure 9 (Franklin et. al 2016) 
 
 
The LIDA model has two “forks” representing the dual directions of the theory - science 
and engineering. - Through empirical science the science direction describes how the 
==PAGE:47==
47 
 
mind works, creating a hypothesis for testing and learning about the biological mind. 
The engineering direction is devoted to implementing functionalities resembling 
biological cognition. (Franklin et al. 2016) 
The credibility of LIDA comes from the successful projects created on the basis of the 
model. LIDA is the model many scientists use in research in which they create AGI 
(artificial general intelligence) and is currently used in the pursuit of autonomous 
vehicles. 
  
These two theories may themselves fall under the category of normative and 
descriptive theories. As Kahneman’s Fast and Slow theory is mostly derived from 
studying behavior (descriptive), Franklins LIDA model is developed from multiple 
disciplines and mainly developed with the purpose of creating intelligent systems 
(normative). 
Determining where strategic decision-making fits into these theories will help us 
identify the correct biases, heuristics, and other cognitive loopholes that may interfere 
with the entrepreneurial decision-making.  
In the Fast and Slow model, the obvious answer seems to be that strategic decisions 
would be associated with system 2: Strategic decisions are often deliberate decisions 
and may involve probabilistic thinking. The LIDA model suggests that human cognition 
operates on a continuing iteration of cognitive cycles, out of which complex processes 
are built. Reconciling the two theories can be done by observing the fast and slow 
system 1 as a process with few cognitive cycle iterations and by viewing system 2 as a 
process with a multitude of cognitive cycle iterations. This does not completely reconcile 
the theories, as the Fast and Slow model separates the systems into two separate 
processes and the LIDA model sees them in a continuum. (Franklin, S., Madl, T., D'Mello, 
S., & Snaider, J., 2014). When learning from these theories, we must remember to be 
nuanced in our selection of which cognitive biases to investigate, and not only examine 
system 2 biases but also asses biases that may serve as the basis for larger, more 
complex structures.

3.3.1 Theoretical answer: Descriptive decision theory 
3.3.1.1 Prospect theory 
Prospect theory is the descriptive decision-making theory developed by psychologist 
and economist Daniel Kahneman and mathematical psychologist Amos Tversky, 
intended to supersede SEU as the dominant descriptive theory. The theory originates 
from the work Kahneman and Tversky did in the 1970s and presented in their paper” 
Prospect Theory: An Analysis of Decision under Risk“(Kahneman, Tversky 1979), and it is 
regarded as a seminal piece in the puzzle of descriptive decision theory.   
Prospect theory tries to explain the very scenario that we are interested in, namely how 
actors decide between alternatives that involve risk and uncertainty.  Kahneman and 
Tversky use the term prospect to represent a gamble or a chance of a monetary gain, 
but in their original paper (Kahneman, Tversky 1979) the authors call for the theory to 
be used as a general decision-making theory: “it can be extended to more involved 
choices.”.  
Entrepreneurial opportunity evaluation and strategic decisions are very closely related 
to the original “prospect” scenarios laid out in the theory. The theory was discredited 
==PAGE:49==
49 
 
through its inbuilt violation of dominance, a flaw that was later corrected with 
Cumulative Prospect Theory (Kahneman, Tversky 1992). 
The original Prospect Theory only dealt with risk, but the new 1992 version also dealt 
with uncertainty. Prospect theory is obviously an evolution of subjective expected utility 
theory, where bounded rationality, for example, is taken into account.  This 
incorporation, among others, adds a layer of complexity that makes it more relevant as 
a descriptive theory. 
From a critical perspective, it seems to me that the formerly discussed bounded 
rationality in some sense mirrors Kahneman and Tversky’s description of Heuristics6. 
The theory is backed by empirical research on general decision-making and is still a 
leading theory.  
The main difference in Prospect Theory relative to SEU seems to be its ability to model 
risk attitudes and uncertainty attitudes, not only the action of maximizing utility. The 
two components that make that change possible are namely loss aversion and 
decision weighting. 
  
Loss aversion inhabits a pivotal role in prospect theory.  
It was Tversky and Kahneman who in their paper loss aversion in riskless choice 
(Tversky, Kahneman 1991) introduced loss aversion to behavioral economics. They 
found reference points to play a significant role in the interpretation of loss and gain. 
Furthermore, they found their subjects’ interpretation of losses to have a greater impact 
on value than gain. This irrational and illogical interpretation shapes the actor’s 
behavior toward opportunities. Loss aversion results in a tendency to prefer avoiding 
losses to acquiring equivalent gains, which greatly impacts the value curve (utility curve). 
Prospect Theory includes all the previous concepts in one theory: subjective utility, 
reference points, diminishing marginal utility, and loss aversion.

The weighting function in Prospect Theory was created to better model how actors 
assign weights to different prospects with different probabilities. To offer a more 
descriptively precise measure of the interpreted utility of an outcome, the utility of each 
outcome is multiplied by the decision weight. In our previous function walkthrough, the 
weighting of the outcome was assumed (not shown) in the value outcome represented 
by x. Subjectivity is infused into the utility function this way. The decision weighting is 
not a probabilistic tool that measures degree of belief. It is a tool that measures the 
impact that probabilities have on the weighting of the utility of a prospect.  
 
The certainty and pseudo certainty effect bias play an important role in the shape of 
the weighting curve for Prospect Theory, according to (Kahneman, Tversky 1986, 1981). 
This phenomenon describes how certain outcomes or outcomes perceived as certain, 
garner disproportionately more weight in decision-making compared to slightly less 
certain outcomes.  
Therefore, the weighting curve is concave near probability 0 and convex near 
probability 1.   
An increase of 0.1 probability of a company achieving a certain goal has a stronger 
impact when the probability changes from 0.9 to 1 then from 0.3 to 0.4. This, in essence, 
shows the certainty effect.  
The weighting function also accounts for diminishing sensitivity in the region 0.3-0.7, 
where the rate of change in weighting is significantly slower, thus representing a lesser 
impact on decisions.  
The increasing weighting near the endpoints does not always hold true. In (Kahneman, 
Tversky 1986) they find that very small probabilities can either be greatly overvalued or 
neglected altogether.

Prospect theory is described in two phases. (Kahneman, Tversky 1979) First, the editing 
phase consists of a preliminary analysis of the prospect or opportunity.  
This will manifest an initial understanding of the opportunity, which is a simple version 
of the prospect. Second, in the evaluation phase, the edited prospects are evaluated 
and chosen on their perceived value; the highest value prospect is chosen over others. 
To create a less mentally strenuous evaluation phase, when choosing which prospects 
have the highest value, the editing phase helps organize and simplify the prospect 
options.  
What makes Prospect Theory unique is the operations the actors undergo between the 
editing and evaluation phase. These operations change the perceived value of 
outcomes and probabilities associated with the available prospects. Value is viewed as a 
function with two arguments: Argument one - Is the asset position of the actors, which 
serves as the reference point, argument two – Is the magnitude of change (positive or 
negative) from that reference point. 
  
Kahneman and Tversky found that people normally perceive prospect outcomes as 
gains and losses and not a final value. 
==PAGE:53==
53 
 
Gains and losses and their impact on value are defined by the reference point on which 
the actor interprets the prospect. The reference point usually corresponds with the 
asset level of the actor: How much can be gained/lost and how much do I already have?  
The operation for determining the reference point is known as Coding. The coding 
performed by the actor can be affected by the formulation of the offered prospect and 
the state of mind of the actor in the coding moment.  
This is also known as the Framing effect.  Kahneman and Tversky found that actors 
tend to favor certain gain over higher probabilistic gain and probabilistic loss over a 
lower certain loss. From expected utility theory the framing effect may also contribute 
to a violation of the independence axiom as an irrelevant factor may influence the 
utility of different choices.  
In Prospect Theory it is possible to change the outcome of a decision made by an actor, 
by changing the frame (order or manner) in which the decision is presented, for 
example presenting a prospect as a loss instead of a gain. The three main operations 
pointed out by Tversky and Kahneman are Coding, Combination7 and Cancellation8. 
Many in academia have chimed in on Prospect Theory, and one of the stronger voices is 
David Gal. He argues for loss aversion being explained by the Status quo bias alone 
(Gal 2006).  
The status quo bias is based on empirical studies and supported by the Psychological 
Law of Inertia9 and the Endowment effect10, showing that individuals tend to prefer 
remaining at status quo in transactions.  
Tversky and Kahneman instead attributed the Status quo bias to loss aversion. This is 
based on the assumption that the loss of the status quo has a greater impact on 
subjective value/utility than an equivalent gain from the status quo (Kahneman, Tversky 
1991).

3.3.1.2 The role of heuristics in strategic decision-making  
Heuristics is a set of rules based on past experiences–an innate form of human 
inductive reasoning used to make predictions. We use heuristics as a cognitive adaptive 
toolbox with various tools tailored for specific classes of problems much like a hammer 
and drill in a handyman’s toolbox (Gigerenzer, Gerd 2008). Without this tool, life would 
be pretty exhausting as we would have to deliberately make every decision in our lives. 
Although it may be difficult to determine what factors exactly inform heuristics, 
heuristics can be tested as a mental model that makes predictions.  
Herbert Simon developed the first heuristics model, which he called the Satisficing 
heuristics: satisfy and suffice. Heuristics is used when deciding between alternatives in 
situations involving uncertainty. The model is defined as: 
 Step 1. Determine a target level of utility an alternative must reach. 
Step 2. Choose the first alternative that satisfies the target level. If no alternative 
reaches the target level, adapt parameters. 
Step 3. If after time T no alternative has reached the target level, decrease the level of 
utility and return to step 1. 
(Simon 1956).  
  
Because heuristics is used in the majority of our decisions, they are very important for 
every entrepreneur and can be either beneficial or detrimental depending on how they 
are used. Heuristics helps prevent the decision-maker from developing “Decision 
fatigue”.  
The objective of heuristics is to produce a solution within a reasonable time frame that 
is reliable enough to solve the problem at hand, “Satisficing”. 
  
==PAGE:55==
55 
 
Decision fatigue describes how individuals’ decision-making ability decreases in quality 
after a decision intense period. (Hirshleifer, Levi, Lourie, Teoh, 2019) found that after 
making multiple forecasts in the same day, financial analysts did indeed suffer from 
decision fatigue. Forecasting is essential for making strategic decisions. 
In the study, they found in accordance with the academic literature on decision fatigue 
that the analysts had a higher probability of deferring to their heuristics the longer the 
decision session went on. This indicates that heuristics functions as a sort of default set 
of decisions that we make when we lack the necessary mental surplus to make 
deliberate thoughtful decisions. There are no studies suggesting that decision fatigue 
affects entrepreneurs in any different way than other decision-makers. When 
considering prescriptive methods for decision-making two points about decision fatigue 
should be included.    
1. Spending time on implementing high performing heuristics may be an advantage for 
entrepreneurs, as all entrepreneurs have to make an extraordinary amount of complex 
decisions.  
2. Making important strategic decisions should be done alone and not in combination 
with many other decisions. If decision fatigue is a product of mental fatigue, and the 
complexity of a decision affects the mental cost, the most complex decisions should 
mostly be made in isolation from other decisions. 
In decisions involving risk (known unknowns), heuristics faces an accuracy vs effort 
trade-off where the simplified decision process often leads to reduced accuracy. This 
makes perfect sense since risk is a game of known probabilities, and deliberately 
calculating and thinking will almost always yield a result with higher utility than using 
heuristics.  
Strategic decisions are likely to involve some element of uncertainty (unknown 
unknowns). In these cases, heuristics may have a less-is-more effect on the more 
complex decisions with many unknown variables. The actor's instincts can serve as a 
better decision tool than analytic thinking or computer models, assuming the actor has 
a wealth of knowledge in the field. This seems to be true mainly because of the risk of 
estimation errors in models when dealing with uncertainty (Gigerenzer, Gerd 2008).  
==PAGE:56==
56 
 
According to Gigerenzer, to foster robustness11 decision-making in an uncertain world 
requires ignoring part of the available information.  Gigerenzer argues that a small 
amount of accurate information is better than a larger amount of less accurate 
information (there is certainly also a threshold for that). Since more uncertain problems 
need more information to predict, the risk of using irrelevant information increases 
(overfitting)12. Remembering also that uncertainty is not exclusively linked to 
probability, it may also include utility both subjectively and generally (you do not always 
know what you want), which adds to the complexity of most decisions.  
These findings suggest that there should be a threshold for decisions on a spectrum of 
uncertainty, in which high uncertainty decisions are better suited to be made by 
heuristics (instincts/intuition) than models or even deliberate thinking and vice versa. 
 
The system 1 vs system 2 discussion, as it pertains to heuristics may be a little played 
out. Heuristics is certainly more prevalently used in system 1 decisions, but as shown by 
(Brighton, 2006) simple heuristics can perform with greater accuracy than regression 
models in system 2 type decisions. It all comes down to using the right tool at the right 
time. Secondly, the LIDA model puts even more pressure on the understanding that the 
mind works in two distinct systems and not on an iteration loop or something else 
entirely.

There are countless biases recorded in academia, but I will only focus on the ones 
relevant to entrepreneurs in strategic decisions. When a person accredits a 
disproportionate amount of importance to a variable compared to objective reality, it 
represents a bias toward that variable.

Biases affect every decision we make to various degrees, and it is almost certain that all

Biases may represent an opinion as in subjective utility theory, which is not looking for

Constructing artificial biases may be used to correct or optimize decisions in models, as

Strategic decisions made by entrepreneurs will come with a higher degree of 
uncertainty than established businesses. Managers in larger organizations will usually 
have historical data on trends, past performance, and other information that will reduce 
the level of uncertainty in strategic decisions (Mintzberg 1973). 
Because of this discrepancy in available information entrepreneurs have to be more 
reliant on heuristics and decision biases. The people who are comfortable with the use 
of heuristics and biases are the same people who are most likely to become 
entrepreneurs (Busenitz, Barney 1997). 
  
(Cooper et al. 1988), among others, found entrepreneurs assigning a higher probability 
of success to their own ventures compared with competing ventures, and found that 
entrepreneurs generally display more overconfidence than the general population in 
decision-making. This observation only reinforces the belief that self-efficacy and 
overconfidence are correlated to some extent.   
Evaluating opportunities is a cornerstone activity for entrepreneurs, and in these 
assessments, overconfidence will play a role. Is there a real economic opportunity? How 
should the opportunity be exploited? How competitive is the space? These are all 
questions that will be affected by overconfidence. This overconfidence may start the

entrepreneur on a dead venture or have them focus on an inappropriate target 
market/feature, but as mentioned earlier it may also be the juice that is needed to 
convince potential stakeholders such as investors, suppliers, and customers that the 
venture is worthwhile.  
  
Decision-makers of all kinds consistently underestimate the errors and lack of 
representativeness in small samples sizes (Payne et al. 1992). This willingness to not 
follow statistical standards results in what is called the Representativeness bias. This 
bias plays a larger role in decisions with a larger portion of risk (known unknowns), 
since faults can mostly be mitigated by research. The most common form of 
representativeness bias is the willingness of decision-makers to generalize from small, 
nonrandom samples (Tversky and Kahneman 1971) –most commonly one’s personal 
experience (Kahneman et al. 1982). 
The law of large numbers gives us the blueprint for dealing with a decision under risk, 
and how large random samples through inference can inform us of the world. The 
problem arises when decision-makers are willing to use that same type of inference and 
confidence in small, nonrandom samples.  
There is certainly reason to believe that the representativeness bias is more prevalent 
among entrepreneurs compared to managers at larger companies (Katz 1992). 
Entrepreneurs may be forced to make judgments upon a smaller set of data than what 
would be accepted in larger companies. 
This particular bias may not only arise from personality traits but also from 
circumstances and environmental factors such as lack of information, time, and 
resources.  
The data collection needed to make decisions that do not involve representativeness 
bias might prematurely reveal an entrepreneur’s products and technologies to 
competitors, and this knowledge is in many cases the entrepreneurs greatest advantage 
compared to the established market (Porter 1980). Therefore, it becomes a tradeoff 
between reducing the potential of the company and making less risky and uncertain 
decisions.

3.4 Key takeaways and a new look at prospect theory for 
entrepreneurs  
  
Prospect Theory as a theory of decision-making that includes entrepreneurs supports 
this investigation well. One of the major aspects of Prospect Theory, namely loss 
aversion, was showed to be not only extremely accurately predicted from the theory but 
explained the behavior of entrepreneurs and non-entrepreneurs equally well. 
The connection between loss aversion and status quo bias was scrutinized, and new 
insights were gathered revealing that status quo bias may not be a product of loss 
aversion but may operate in its own domain. This insight does not change the 
fundamental aspects of Prospect Theory much; it only adds some complexity that may 
have been ignored in the past.  
  
Confidence does not play a large role in Prospect Theory, and my view of the quality of 
Prospect Theory as a descriptive theory for entrepreneurs is therefore not impacted 
greatly by the new findings in the study. I would, however, advocate for including 
entrepreneurial confidence in the probabilities of the utility functions in Prospect 
Theory when describing entrepreneurs.

4.2 Tractability  
Heuristics is still an incredibly important tool in strategic decision-making, because of 
the intractable nature of many real-world problems. As shown in (Brighton 2006) 
inductive human inference (heuristics in other words) can outperform algorithmic 
machine learning models, in problems with high uncertainty. Computational models can 
with great success be applied to well defined problems. Models can find the optimal 
route or create the most accurate representation, by using either brood force 
computation or more clever means as neural networks. The problem with using 
computational models on strategic decisions, is that those types of decisions are very 
hard to defined and encapsulate in a way that makes them computationally tractable. 
We are far from being able to simulate entire worlds, there are simply to many 
uncertain variables. Dealing with intractable scenarios, means that both mind and 
machine must change their aim from perfection toward satisfying and sufficient – or as 
(Simon 1956) describes satisficing. There are not yet any artificial intelligence tools that 
can be appropriate applied to wide range of strategic decisions even with abounded 
data, and there probably won’t be until general AI is developed. AI can be incredibly 
powerful in well defined solution spaces, like medical diagnostics where probabilistic 
==PAGE:74==
74 
 
ranking of possible illnesses can be created based on medical history and symptoms. 
Business related strategic decision are never going to be this well-defined.  
Tractable and intractable problems 
 
Figure 12 (Green & Mehr, 1997)) 
Figure 12 can both be used to illustrate both tractable and intractable problems. The 
figure shows a fast-and-frugal tree for coronary care unit allocation. The model was 
created to assists emergency physicians in deciding whether a patient with severe chest 
pain should be sent to the coronary care unit or a regular nursing bed. The fast-and-
frugal tree is a classification tree that has n number of cues and allows for a 
classification after each cue. In the example above the tree performs well and will most 
likely be a good decision aid for the physicians. But a tree like this suffers from the same 
limitations as the BBNs I proposed in part 2. The number of end nodes or number 
factors included in intermediary nodes (weighting nodes) in the tree increases 
exponentially for each layer of additional cues – This makes the model computationally 
intractable very fast. This is why creating a more precise representation of the scenario - 
like I did in part 2 BBN scenario - is key to using a model that is susceptible to 
intractability.

4.4 BBNs and heuristics as a prescriptive tool 
4.4.1 Introduction  
The inspiration for combining BBNs and heuristics in a prescriptive tool came from the 
US intelligence company Palantir. Palantir’s core innovation was to utilize the sorting 
power of the fraud detection algorithms from PayPal (Peter Thiel was the owner of both 
companies) and coupling it with human analysts using heuristics, mostly used for 
national defense purposes. By bridging the gap between algorithms and human 
heuristics, Palantir created the most effective antiterror tool to date. Perhaps something 
similar may be achieved for strategic business decisions, by combining the power of 
BBNs (a forced probabilistic method that may mitigate some of the flaws and biases 
associated with strategic decision making) and entrepreneurial heuristics. When using 
BBNs we are forced to define a problem space, knowing full well that it does not 
represent the whole truth but hopefully it does include the most vital parts, much like 
you would do in standard data analysis, in which a principal components analysis would 
revile the x-number of features that represents 90% of the spread in a dataset. 
4.4.2 Prescriptive tool  
BBNs function great as a documentation tool for such things as user-research, much 
like a mind map of sorts. BBNs forces the decision maker to estimate the importance of 
a certain piece of information, which can be very helpful in mitigating representative 
and availability biases. The BBN is still mostly based on the entrepreneur’s own 
==PAGE:82==
82 
 
judgment, but by fragmenting the estimation, the entrepreneurs can reflect and 
mitigate underlying biases that may affect his decision-making in more complex 
decisions. By forcing the entrepreneur to first create a believable problem space, the 
method promotes the weighing and judgment of all important variables in the case. 
BBNs also warrants tangible data to be used in the model and is treated without bias 
(known probabilities concerning the industry or research conducted can be entered in 
the model). 
BBNs can be built out to a greater extent than what I have done in this thesis, though it 
still has to abide by the limitations of tractability. While learning with the model as your 
business grows and more data or information/research is procured, the model can be 
adjusted to fit that. The more nodes that can be added with quality judgment and data, 
will increase the accuracy of the model. We can easily imagine a BBN that is 
considerably more complex than the ones shown in the thesis.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:83==
83 
 
Expanded BBN - Figure 14 
 
I think a BBN also promotes an awareness of the quality of your own heuristic 
predictions. It cannot be overstated how important self-awareness is in decision-
making. Being aware of your own tendencies and prediction power is powerful in itself. 
4.4.2.1 Learnings and prescriptive tools 
To incorporate some descriptive elements into BBNs, we can use what we have learned 
from the studies in part 3. First, I would like to note that a personal test of one’s own 
tendencies and biases will work better than insights gained from studying 
entrepreneurs as a group. Entrepreneurs do have some similarities, but it is also a 
heterogeneous group that most likely will have more differences than similarities. 
Performing personality, heuristics, and biases tests and incorporating them into the 
model can really bolster the model. For incorporating more general descriptive 
==PAGE:84==
84 
 
behaviors, the most obvious place to start is in the weighting function in the nodes. This 
is similar to neural networks, in which we can incorporate intentional biases to even out 
other unintentional biases. At the same time we can use the weighting functions as a 
way to promote the strengths of the entrepreneurs. To take an example of this, we can 
return to part 3., which demonstrated how the entrepreneurial confidence might be an 
advantage. We can use this in the weighting function to further promote judgments that 
have been assigned a high probability. Skewing the weighting in this way will increase 
the importance of high probability estimations. This will, in turn, put more emphases on 
heuristics compared to tangible data, which, in fact, may not be a good idea. A suitable 
threshold should be developed from further study or individual tests. Other traits can 
be incorporated into the weighting function through similar factorization.
