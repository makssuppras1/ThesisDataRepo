Chapter 1
Introduction
Data mining is broadly used in many aspects of industry supporting researchers
to get insight of particular incident based on massive data, often with very high
dimensions. It is often done by applying a combination of knowledge such as
machine learning, statistics and data science.
Text mining is a subcategory
of data mining which primarily deals with human perceivable languages. We
are living in an information exploration era, where text data is everywhere and
many of them exist in unstructured form. This is specially true in modern in-
dustries, which have collected massive data in various formats. For instance,
a system maintenance report could be a set of unstructured text data, which
is not easy to have a clue about what it implies by human eye. However, nat-
ural language processing [1] techniques are good at dealing with this kind of
data. Techniques like vectorization, feature extraction, clustering, topic mod-
eling, sentiment analysis are commonly applied on text data. Moreover, with
help of dimension reduction techniques like PCA, LSA, NMF, and models like
Bayesian, SVM and Randomforest, one could make prediction for a certain in-
cident, so as to support decision making. The results should be evaluated using
techniques like cross validation, precision, recall, F score, entropy and so on, in
order to chose the most reliable result.
The oil and gas oﬀshore industry is an example that is exploring“big data”tools

to deal with unstructured and large amounts of data to improve technical deci-
sions and preventing downtime etc. Maersk Oil has the historical data involving
both sensor and operational data as well as plentiful of unstructured data such

1.0.1
Project plan
The original problem of this project is to explore whether observations made in
inspections and maintenance reports can be linked to the later formation of slugs
or slug-induced damages in the so-called Produces Water Treatment. As such
the idea was to detect reported incidents or pre-incidents of anomalies related to
slug formation by performing text-mining in a historical database from Maersk
Oil and as a next step exploring whether this reported events could be correlated
with actual patterns of sensor and alarm recordings. In the ﬁrst phase of the
project it unfortunately appeared becoming apparent that conﬁdentiality issues
hindered the access to the data especially because of the data being unstructured
(implying Maersk Oil was unable to judge what kind of information they would
provide access to). This in turn just underlines the need to cluster the texts
into diﬀerent types for further analysis.
Since the massive unstructured text data exists in Maersk Oil, there is a urgent
demand(the reason is explained in Projectplan) to get insights of these data,
particularly grouping the unlabeled texts with similar meaning into categories.
Moreover, in order to keep the relationship with the original problem of interest,
papers and reports dealing with slug related issues has been part of the data
materials used in this thesis. Concerning real world cases, where new texts with
similar or diﬀerent semantic meanings will expand the existing data. So this
thesis will conduct experiments on the target text data consisting of two Cor-
pora (introduced in section 3.1) respectively, with the purpose of investigating
the changes of clustering when adding a new group of texts. In order to make
cluster analysis of this text collection, the very ﬁrst step is to convert the DPF
documents to plain text, followed by tokenizing each document, lowercasing each
word, removing non-English and stopwords, which result in a collection of clean
tokenized documents. The next step is to convert the tokenized documents to a
vector representation. There are many ways to derive a vector representation,
such as applying hash function, tf and tﬁdf. For this project, I apply tﬁdf, as
it takes consideration of both local and global frequency [2]. The vector repre-
sentation is often a very sparse matrix with very high dimension and including
many correlations between the words [5], as such I need to make dimension
reduction, so as to make the clustering more sensible. This thesis will cover

Nonnegative Matrix Factorization is a dimension reduction method, which has
been applied in a wide range of ﬁelds including text mining [7] [8], computer

non-negative attribute leads to inherent advantages in practice including intu-
itive interpretation. Given a word-document matrix A ∈RM×N, we expect a

The resulting matrices W and H are very intuitive to interpret in the sense that

NMF deﬁnes the vectors {wi, . . . , wk} to be linearly independent [11].

2.3
Clustering
Since the problem of this project is to cluster a collection of unstructured texts,
clustering methods should be considered. Document clustering is a common
task in text mining with the intention of categorizing a large collection of text
ﬁles into several conceptually meaningful clusters, which enables users quickly
browsing and retrieving document.
Often times people would not know the
labels of a set of data in prior, thus there is a need to explore the relation of
each samples in the data. Clustering methods are based on some measurement
of similarity among the data points. In this section I will explain a couple of
clustering methods focusing on the principle and algorithm.
2.3.1
Kmeans
Kmeans clustering performs hard assignment of unlabeled data points to clusters
based on the Euclidean distance between each data points and their nearest
centroid. It starts by randomly assigning K desired number of data points as
cluster centroids, which follows by an iterative process to minimize the total
within cluster variance. The iterative process consists of two steps:
1. For each centroid assigned, identify the data points that are closer to it
than any other centroid(clustering step)
2. Compute a centroid for each new formed cluster, then replace the old
centroid with the new one.
Convergence is obtained when the mean vectors become stable. The two steps
are identical to those in EM (Expectation Maximization), in which they are
called E-step and M-step respectively [12]. We can consider K-means algorithm
as an optimization problem, let xi = (xi1, ...xip) refers to each data point,
m1, m2, ...mk refers to centroids, and clusters are c1, c2, ...ck, we state that K-
means converges to a local minimum:

2.3.2
Hierarchical-clustering
Hierarchical-clustering oﬀers a more informative structure than the ﬂat clus-
tering such as K-means. In addition, it does not need a pre-speciﬁed number
of clusters. But all these advantages make it low computational eﬃciency [2].
Hierarchical-clustering measures the distance between two clusters in the con-
densed distance matrix. Ward is a Hierarchical-clustering algorithm based on
variance minimization. It minimizes the variance at each agglomerative step,
where the criterion for selecting pairs of clusters to merge at each step is deter-
mined by the minimum value of the objective function below [14]:

3.3.1
Estimate of ONC using NbClust
The intractable issue in cluster analysis is probably the choice of the optimal
number of clusters (ONC). This section will depict the estimate of ONC us-
ing K-means assessed by 30 indexes. The implementation follows the R func-
tion ”NbClust” [17], which oﬀers a wide range of clustering methods as well as
30indexes for judging clustering quality. The function returns an ONC based
on votes of the 30indexes. To mention a few, gap, silhouette, KL, sdindex and
CH, see the full list and details at [18]. For practical purpose, this section will
only describe Silhouette and Gap statistics in details.
Although dimension 22 can trade oﬀoverﬁtting and underﬁtting in terms of
reconstruction error, there is no theoretical evidence for that this dimension can
directly indicate the optimal dimension of LSI in attempt to estimate the ONC.
Therefore, with intention to ﬁnd the ONC I have to check a sequence of LSI
dimensions with a range of numbers of clusters. A ONC will be computed for
each LSI dimension, as a result, a sequence of ONCs are computed, which draws
a tendency showing the most frequent ONC throughout the dimensions. The
experiment for estimating ONC by using NbClust follows the steps below:
1. According to section 3.1, pre-process and split the data to three sets(type
I), followed by computing three document-word matrices for each set re-
sulting in Atrain ∈R70×1000, Avalid ∈R36×1000 and Atest ∈R35×1000.
2. Train LSI model at k dimension on the training set to obtain Atrain ∈
R70×k and derive Avalid ∈R36×k, where k = {2, . . . , 22}, followed by
conducting K-means on Avalid ∈R36×k and compute the 30 indexes.
3. Obtain votes from the 30 indexes for each dimension k, and compare these
votes to identify the best ONC.
The ONC derived from the 30 index votes vary much according to diﬀerent LSI
dimensions. Figure 3.2 illustrates that the ONC has got the majority (12 out
of 21) votes for 3. Figure A.1 (in Appendix) shows details of the votes for each
LSI dimension.

Discussion
This project is derived from a demand that Maersk Oil need to cluster the un-
structured texts, so as to easily recognize what can be shared and what should
remain high conﬁdentiality. As such, my study focuses on how to cluster the
unstructured text with high semantic sensibility while comparing diﬀerent meth-
ods for extracting latent semantic features and methods for clustering. I use two
corpora in order to detect the changes of clusters when adding more topics in a
corpus.
I started with converting the raw PDF ﬁles to plain text followed by applying
natural language processing techniques including ﬁltering out non-word sym-
bol, removing non-English word and stop-word and extracting stem of word. I
split the corpus in two ways, Type-I for easy computation but waste some data,
while Type-II for using data suﬃciently but require much more computation
than Type-I. Moreover, Type-I could contribute testing bias because the testing
result could be based on a particular random splitting. Then I performed recon-
struction error check for LSI model among 80 dimensions, ﬁgure 3.1 indicates
that dimension 22 will best trade oﬀunderﬁtting and overﬁtting issue. The
ONC is always a delicate issue for K-means clustering. First of all, it the data
does not kind of matching K-means assumption, the clustering will risk to fail.
Secondly, there are many methods to assess the quality of clustering, some of
them based on information of intra cluster dispersion and between cluster dis-
persion such as silhouette, while others based on the diﬀerence between pooled

intra cluster dispersion and the cluster dispersion of a uniformly distributed data
in the same space such as gap statistics. I elaborated silhouette and gap, which
are straightforward to interpret when looking at the ﬁgures. However, NbClust
provides 30 indexes for checking the quality of clustering, there are many more
to be discovered. In K-means clustering experiment for Corpus-I, LSI stands
out in the model selection process, but the ﬁnal prediction based on NMF is

This implies that both clustering method tend to merger small cluster to other

make the prediction coherent to the ground truth. I have the original document
partitioned in diﬀerent folders according to their themes, thus I can manually
assign labels to each document. For instance, the testing set has in total 35
samples consisting of 3 Slug + 4 Bio + 13 GIS + 15 Slug:

0, 0, 0, 0]

1, 1, 1, 1]
