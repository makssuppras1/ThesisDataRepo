Abstract 
Hydrogen storage solution, proposed by Klenergy, is simulated in TRNSYS (Transient System 
Simulation Tool) in order to design and validate the feasibility of the technology. The storage 
system consists of electrolyzer, hydrogen storage tank, fuel cell and the heat storage tanks that 
collect the waste heat from the fuel cell. The analysis is performed for a household unit located 
in Copenhagen, Denmark, which is equipped with a photovoltaic system to generate sufficient 
amount of energy in order to cover all the electricity consumption. The excess energy is stored 
in form of hydrogen and later consumed in the fuel cell to provide electricity and heat. The 
system is able to cover all the electricity consumption of the household and partially cover the 
heat demand. Due to the storage system inefficiencies and combined heat and electricity 
production of the fuel cell, the photovoltaic system with great capacity is required. For the 
purpose of the electrical energy storage, a new design is proposed and evaluated in the study. 
The combination of electrochemical electricity storage for a daily cycle and hydrogen storage 
solution for a seasonal energy storage performs with higher energy efficiency. The economic 
analysis of the solution shows the current high investment cost of the hydrogen technology, 
although the trend of price reduction can be observed and further expansion of the system in 
near future is be expected.

1 INTRODUCTION 
The investigation of Klenergy storage solution was motivated by the effort to reduce our 
carbon footprint and promote the renewable energy. Currently, the vast majority of primary 
energy consumption in the world is provided from the fossil fuels. Fossil fuels are cheap and 
reliable source of energy, however, high amount of carbon emitted to the atmosphere during 
their burning is the main contributor to the global warming. On the other hand, renewables are 
a sustainable source of energy with minimal impact on the environment. The principal 
disadvantage of the solar and wind energy is its fluctuation in energy production. While the 
operation of traditional power plants can be controlled according to the demand, the energy 
production from renewable sources is strongly dependent on the weather conditions of each 
specific location. In order to be fully independent of the fossil fuels, reliable and efficient 
energy storage has to be introduced. 
Hydrogen as a secondary energy carrier can be stored for a long period with no energy 
deterioration. The electricity is consumed in electrolysis, while the water is split into hydrogen 
and oxygen. The power is later generated on demand by oxidizing hydrogen in the fuel cell. 
Hydrogen gas has high mass energy density and high volumetric energy density under 
pressure. Hydrogen is a non-toxic and non-poisonous element, which does not present any 
hazard for groundwater contamination, neither atmospheric pollution (The National Hydrogen 
Association, & U.S. Department of Energy, 1875). 
Klenergy energy system is a small household unit that can store the excess renewable energy 
in form of hydrogen and later produce electricity and heat. The simulations of the system in 
this thesis aim to validate the feasibility of the storage system, in order to later build and test 
the storage solution in a laboratory environment. The capacities of the system components are 
designed to cover all the electricity consumption of the household located in Copenhagen, 
Denmark. The photovoltaic installation is considered to be a source of renewable energy in 
the simulation. The economic analysis based on the current investment cost and operating cost 
of the technology is performed, in order to evaluate the possible economic benefits for the end 
user of the system. 
The simulation of the energy system was performed in TRNSYS (Klein et al., 2010). The 
hydrogen system analysis is performed in the software, because of the flexibility in behavior 
simulation of transient systems and Hydrogems library of components created by Ulleberg 
(2002).

2 THEORY 
The vast majority of houses is dependent on energy supply to run their facilities, such as space 
heating and cooling, water heating, lighting, ventilation, cooking and much more other 
equipment. Over the past decade, there has been a strong emphasis on a renewable energy use 
and energy efficiency through the whole spectrum of the energy supply chain, all the way to 
the end-users. Significant progress in a low energy design can be observed, nevertheless, the 
energy still has to be supplied to the households. There are a few different alternatives for 
household consumers to cover their energy demand. 
2.1 Energy supply 
Denmark has one of the most developed energy grids in Europe, where 63% of households is 
connected to district heating (State of Green, 2016) and nearly every house is connected to the 
electricity grid. Therefore, a typical scenario in Denmark can be described as a household 
where 100 % of the electricity consumption is covered by the electricity grid as well as 100 % 
of the heat consumption is covered by district heating. Simplified scheme of the energy supply 
is shown in Figure 1, where the energy production is shown on the left-hand side and the 
consumption in the household is presented on the right-hand side. Electricity grid and district 
heating bring several advantages for the consumers; they typically provide high quality and 
reliability of distributed energy, large-scale energy generation propose lower-cost according 
to the theory of economy of scale, what delivers economical benefits of the operation. In 2015, 
nearly 68 % of all district heating in Denmark was produced by combined heat and power 
units, this cogeneration increases energy efficiency and reduces carbon emissions as a result 
of fuel saving (DBDH, 2016). On the other hand, centralized power plants and district heating 
production has to deliver the energy to the end-users, what presents higher transmission losses 
between the power plants and consumers, as well as high initial investment costs and 
complexity of the system. 
 
Figure 1. Electricity grid + District heating 
With a development of sustainable energy systems and tendency to reduce a carbon footprint, 
more investments into renewable energy can be recognized. The amount of solar energy 
potential and the falling prices of the photovoltaic technology present solar as the brightest 
sustainable energy for the future. Increasing number of houses are being equipped with PV 
panels on their roofs to reduce their electricity consumption. The capacity of the system can 
be designed to generate the corresponding amount of electricity that is used in the house, 
therefore to cover all the consumption. But due to the weather conditions, the PVs cannot 
cover all the consumption of the household independently. Since the sun does not shine 
constantly, it cannot follow the energy demand of the end-users. Therefore, there is an excess

power during the day and no power supply during the night. The excess power from the PVs 
has to be stored in an energy storage system or it can be exported to the grid. The energy policy 
of Denmark back in 2009 allowed the electric meter to run backward for photovoltaic power 
plants smaller than 6 kW, which provided full compensation of electricity that was exported 
to the grid (Stenkjaer, 2009). In other words, the household would get back every kilowatt-
hour exported to the grid in the period with no energy production from the PVs. This means 
that the electricity grid would operate as an “energy storage“ for a household. Such a scenario 
is shown in Figure 2.  
 
Figure 2. Electricity grid + PVs + District heating 
This system is difficult to maintain due to increasing share of renewables in electricity 
generation. The energy produced from photovoltaics or wind sources inherently varies and 
present a specific challenge to keep power grid stable. Therefore, the subsidy policy has 
changed since 2009. So-called 60/40 aid scheme for PV systems was introduced. The 
settlement price of exported electricity from solar panels was 60 øre/kWh for the first ten years 
and 40 øre/kWh for the next ten years with no limit on the system capacity. On May 3, 2016 
the Danish Parliament has repealed this support and new energy policy has been announced 
(Energinet, 2017). This amendment doesn’t affect the commitments that have been received 
before May 3, 2016. With no aid scheme for the PV systems, the owners of the PV systems 
have to sell the electricity at the market rates or deliver a surplus to the grid free of charge. 
The market price in the Nordic power exchange fluctuates throughout the year according to 
supply and demand on a day-ahead and intraday trading basis. The market electricity price in 
Copenhagen area for every hour of the year in 2016 is shown in Figure 3, the price has varied 
from -39.86 øre/kWh at the end of December up to 159.88 øre/kWh in January. An annual 
average market price was 21.89 øre/kWh (Nord Pool AS, 2017a).

2.2 Energy Storage 
The three principal functions of Electrical Energy Storage are to reduce electricity costs by 
storing the electricity generated at the off-peak period, improve the reliability of the electricity 
grid and to improve power quality (International Electrotechnical Commission, 2016). EES is 
one of the crucial technological areas to resolve the future of renewable energy. Renewable 
energy installation in grid power supply experience complications such as power demand 
fluctuation and power generation undependability due to the changing weather conditions. 
Specifically, solar irradiation variation has to be considered on both daily and seasonal basis. 
These power demand and power generation fluctuations can be observed on graphs below. 
Where Figure 8 shows typical daily electricity profile (Godoy-Shimizu, 2014) and an example 
of solar irradiance from June 11, 2016 (The Technical University of Denmark - Department 
of Civil Engineering, 2017). It can be recognized that the solar energy peak in the midday 
cannot supply power demand peak in the afternoon. The energy storage working on a daily 
charge and discharge cycle could hold an energy accumulated during the day and cover the 
energy consumption during the night.

The electricity consumption variation for a typical household with the annual consumption of 
4,000 kWh can be seen in Figure 9, where the demand is slightly higher in winter season than 
in summer season. The electricity production of 4 kW PV system, which would produce 
approximately the same amount of energy in Copenhagen region, is also shown in Figure 9

the energy of approximately 1500 kWh to cover year around energy consumption. The state 
of charge of this energy system is shown in the graph and its function has sinusoidal character

EES systems are classified into mechanical, electrochemical, chemical, electrical and thermal 
energy storage systems (Figure 10). The mechanical storage systems store the excess energy 
as kinetic energy in flywheels, potential energy in water or compressed energy in the air. 
Electrochemical energy storage, known as secondary or flow batteries, converts chemical 
energy contained in its electrochemical cells into electric energy by an electrochemical 
reaction. Chemical energy storage systems store the energy in secondary energy carrier 
hydrogen or synthetic natural gas. They store the electrical energy via electrolysis of water 
and later generate the power by oxidizing hydrogen in the fuel cells or in a gas or steam turbine. 
Electrical Double-layer Capacitor store the energy at an electrode-electrolyte interface of high-
surface-area carbon electrodes. Superconducting Magnetic Energy Storage systems store 
energy in the form of a magnetic field created by the current in a superconducting coil. The 
coil is cooled to a temperature below its superconducting critical temperature. Thermal storage 
systems are typically not fed with electricity, but the heat from renewable sources can be stored 
into the storage and used to produce electricity on demand.

The requirements on the energy storage vary according to the storage application. The main 
feature of seasonal storage is discharge time, which in the case of solar energy requires to be 
in weeks up to months. The electrical energy storage technologies can be divided according 
to their discharge time into following categories (International Electrotechnical Commission, 
2016): 
Short discharge time (seconds to minutes): double-layer capacitors (DLC), 
superconducting magnetic energy storage (SMES) and flywheel (FES). The energy-to-
power ratio is less than 1 (e.g. a capacity of less than 1 kWh for a system with a power of 
1 kW). 
Medium discharge time (minutes to hours): flywheel energy storage (FES) and – for larger 
capacities – electrochemical EES, which is the dominant technology: lead-acid (LA), 
Lithium-ion (Li-ion) and sodium sulfur (NaS) batteries. The technical features of the 
different electrochemical techniques are relatively similar. They have advantages in the kW 
- MW and kWh - MWh range when compared to other technologies. Typical discharge 
times are up to several hours, with an energy-to-power ratio of between 1 and 10 (e.g. 
between 1 kWh and 10  kWh for a 1  kW system). Batteries can be tailored to the needs of 
an application: tradeoffs may be made for high energy or high power density, fast charging 
behavior or long life, etc. 
Long discharge time (days to months): hydrogen (H2 ) and synthetic natural gas (SNG). 
For these EES systems, the energy-to-power ratio is considerably greater than 10.  
Pumped hydro storage (PHS), compressed air energy storage (CAES) and redox flow 
batteries are situated between storage systems for medium and long discharge times. Like 
H2 and SNG systems, these EES technologies have external storage tanks. But the energy 
densities are rather low, which limits the energy-to-power ratio to values between 
approximately 5 and 30.

2.3 Chemical energy storage - Power-to-Gas 
Based on the previous paragraphs, the chemical energy storage can be identified as the most 
promising approach to store a large amount of energy for a long period of time. With the 
combination of power-to-gas and gas-to-power technology, sustainable seasonal electricity 
storage can be accomplished (DENA, 2015). Renewable energy can be stored in the secondary 
energy carriers, such as hydrogen gas or synthetic natural gas. The excess electricity is used 
to produce hydrogen in a chemical reaction which is called water electrolysis. The efficiency 
of the reaction can reach 80-95 % on the basis of the higher heating value (Munkholt, 2013). 
The hydrogen gas can be stored in pressurized vessels or cavern storages. Furthermore, it can 
be liquefied at a very low temperature or combined with carbon dioxide into synthetic natural 
gas by the reaction called methanation. Chemical energy stored in the form of hydrogen can 
be later used in transport, heat supply and the industry (Figure 12). For the purpose of electrical 
energy storage, the gas-to-power energy can be generated with several technologies, fuel cells 
(less than 1 MW) and gas motors (under 10 MW) can be used for combined heat and power 
generation. Gas and steam turbines can operate as a peak power plant with design power of 
several hundred MW. The overall electricity-gas-electricity efficiency is around 40% 
(International Electrotechnical Commission, 2016). The lower electrical efficiency of the 
system can be compensated by combined heat and power generation in decentralized 
installations.

2.4 Klenergy energy storage 
Klenergy storage system Helios is designed to store the energy from a primary renewable 
energy source in the form of hydrogen. Solar-hydrogen system based on Micro-CHP 
residential fuel cell energy storage delivers an advantage of local energy production and 
consumption. Energy stored in the form of hydrogen can be stored for longer periods than 
conventional batteries with no energy degradation. The long-term storage solution with 
minimal energy losses presents extensive improvement and a considerable advantage in 
seasonal storing of energy. The schematic model of the system is shown in Figure 13. The 
Klenergy system storing renewable energy (PVs, WECS, etc.) consists of the electrolyzer, 
hydrogen storage, fuel cell, heat storage and two independent control systems for electricity 
and heat management. Secondary components are electrical converters, inverters, compressor 
and boiler shunts. The excess power from the photovoltaic panel is consumed in electrolyzer 
to produced hydrogen, the hydrogen is stored in a pressurized tank at the pressure of 200 bar. 
The hydrogen is used to produce electricity on demand in the fuel cell, while the excess heat 
is extracted by the fuel cell cooling system and stored in a heat storage. Collected heat is later 
used for space heating or hot water production. While the electricity-storage-electricity 
efficiency is expected to reach 40%, the overall energy efficiency with waste heat recovery is 
aimed to achieve approximately 80% energy efficiency.

PEMFC are well developed and proven technology, the cost reduction in last years and 
dynamic operation which allows accurate following of load demand present an important 
advantage in mobile as well as stationary applications. The operation temperature is generally 
around 80°C. DMFC is a derivative of PEMFC, which use methanol instead of hydrogen. 
Liquid Phosphoric Acid is the electrolyte of the PAFC. Typical application is in the industrial 
and commercial combined heat and power. Potassium Hydroxide liquid electrolyte of AFC is 
being replaced with an anion-exchange membrane in recent development. The performance 
of alkaline fuel cell is limited by pure hydrogen fuel and CO2 free air. A molten mixture of 
lithium, sodium and potassium carbonate is used as an electrolyte in the MCFC. The high 
temperature of MCFC (600 - 700°C) and typical power range of 50 kWe – 5 MWe naturally 
leads to the industrial and commercial combined heat and power application. Solid electrolyte

The fuel cell electrical efficiency is defined as the ratio of the electrical energy produced in 
the cell and the heat that would be produced by burning the fuel. The generation of heat is 
mainly due to electrical inefficiencies, yet a significant share of this heat can be utilized in a 
well-insulated fuel cell. Therefore, the overall efficiency is described as the ratio of the 
electrical energy and energy of utilized heat to the enthalpy of fuel formation.

commercial implementation (Cleghorn, 2006). The operating lifetime over 60,000 hours have 
been reported (Barsukov, 1996) and The U.S. Department of Energy 2020 target for 1-25 kWe 
Residential and Light Commercial Heat and Power and Distributed Generation Fuel Cell 
Systems Operating on Natural Gas operating lifetime is set to 60,000 hours as well (The U.S. 
Department of Energy, 2015).

3 METHODS 
Model of the Klenergy storage system has been developed in software TRNSYS (Klein et al., 
2010). The sensitivity analysis was carried out to improve the overall performance and 
optimize the size of the components, which involves in reducing the initial cost of the system. 
Additionally, an economic analysis of the system has been carried out based on the results of 
the energy simulations and other economic indicators.  
3.1 Software 
TRNSYS was used for its flexibility in behavior simulation of transient systems and also for 
comprehensive library of components. Hydrogems created by Ulleberg (2002) are 
mathematical models of components necessary for the simulation study. TRNSYS was also 
previously used to simulate hydrogen systems comparable to the Klenergy system (Ulleberg, 
1997a). For the sake of this report, two control systems for electricity and heat management 
had to be created. The process of the programming was carried out by following TRNSYS 
documentation (Klein et al., 2014) and the source code of the components is enclosed in the 
Appendix A and Appendix B. The time step of the simulation was set to 15 minutes, what 
ensure sufficient resolution of the simulation.  
3.2 Design of Klenergy CHP storage solution 
The hydrogen storage solution use the excess electrical power and store it in a form of 
hydrogen. Variable renewable energy sources (such as solar, wind, water, geothermal) can be 
used to provide green energy, the solar photovoltaic system was chosen for the purpose of this 
study. The excess energy is transformed to hydrogen in the electrolyzer and stored in a 
pressurized storage tank. Subsequently, the chemical reaction of hydrogen and oxygen 
produce energy in the fuel cell according to energy demand of users. The electrical efficiency 
of the fuel cell is generally between 40-60%, while the overall efficiency can be up to 95% 
(Napoli, 2015). This includes utilization of waste heat. Optimal operating temperature of PEM 
fuel cell is between 50°C and 90°C (Balasubramanian, 1999), which provides sufficient 
temperature to cover both domestic hot water temperature requirements as well as temperature 
demand for radiant space heating elements. Due to simultaneous production of heat and 
electricity, control strategy either covering electricity or heat demand had to be applied. In 
order to achieve all the electricity demand to be covered by renewable energy production, 
electricity load following strategy was used in the model. Therefore, the heat that is not 
consumed instantaneously in the house would have to be stored in a thermal energy storage. 
The scheme of the system is shown in Figure 15. The electricity power, which is generated in 
the photovoltaic system, is converted in a maximum power point tracker (MPPT) to match the 
required voltage and increase the efficiency. The energy is either immediately converted in 
DC/AC inverter and consumed in a household or when there is no demand for the electricity, 
the excess power is used to produce hydrogen in the electrolyzer. The hydrogen is compressed 
and stored in the pressurized storage tank. The hydrogen is later consumed in the fuel cell 
according to the electricity demand of a user. The waste heat from the fuel cell is collected 
and stored in domestic hot water tank or space heating tank and later it is supplied to the 
==PAGE:27==

household on demand. The system can supply electricity, hot water, and space heating. The 
connection to the electricity grid shall be preserved in the case of insufficient energy capacity 
of the storage system or for the purpose of electricity export to the grid. Auxiliary heating 
elements for both domestic hot water and space heating are preferred as well, for the purpose 
of the simulation district heating presents auxiliary heat source for the household.

systems instantly follows the demand and minimize the amount of energy that is either 
imported or exported to the electricity grid.The operation with load following strategy has 
been evaluated by Napoli (2015) as the most effective for micro-CHP systems and therefore 
it has been chosen for the simulation.

has to prioritize and select the most efficient energy strategy.  
3.2.2.1 Local renewable energy 
Electricity demand of the users is primarily covered by the local renewable energy source 
(PVs). The excess power is send to electrolyzer and stored in form of hydrogen. If the 
hydrogen storage is at full capacity and cannot store any more energy, the surplus of electricity 
is exported to the grid. 
3.2.2.2 FC energy 
If the energy production of the renewable source is not sufficient, then the fuel cell is in 
operation and follows the electricity demand. The main operation time of the fuel cell is from 
the late afternoon until the morning of the next day, when there is no energy produced by PVs. 
3.2.2.3 Grid 
The grid serve as a backup power system in the case there is not enough renewable energy, 
nor stored hydrogen. The grid also delivers advantage to cover occasional power peaks, which 
would otherwise result in a much bigger fuel cell unit.

3.2.7 Heat storage tank– Domestic hot water and space heating 
Nearly all of the excess heat can be utilized in a well-insulated fuel cell, the overall efficiency 
of the fuel cell with heat utilization can reach 90-95% (Ulleberg, 1997a; Napoli, 2015). Heat 
losses towards the ambient or evaporation can be recognized in the system, but nearly all the 
excess heat from the fuel cell is stored in two heat storage tanks. The stratified domestic hot 
water tank is equipped with immersed spiral heat exchanger. The spiral heat exchanger 
transfers supplied amount of heat from a fuel cell loop to domestic hot water loop. The space 
heating thermal storage consists of a 4-pipe buffer tank. The system is controlled by thermostat 
(T) situated in the domestic hot water tank. According to determined set point temperature and 
temperature deadband the hot water flow from the fuel cell is directed to one of the tanks. The 
scheme of heating system is shown in Figure 31. 
 
Figure 31. Heating scheme 
==PAGE:35==

Two models of storage tanks were used in the simulation: Cylindrical Storage Tank with 
Immersed Heat Exchanger (Type 534) and Stratified Storage Tank with Fixed Inlets and 
Uniform Losses (Type 4).  
Cylindrical Storage Tank with Immersed Heat Exchanger (Thorton, 2000): 
This subroutine models a fluid-filled, constant volume storage tank with immersed heat 
exchangers. This component models a cylindrical tank with a vertical configuration. The 
fluid in the storage tank interacts with the fluid in the heat exchanger (through heat transfer 
with the immersed heat exchangers), with an environment (through thermal losses from the 
top, bottom and edges) and with up to two flow streams that pass into and out of the storage 
tank. The tank is divided into isothermal temperature nodes (to model stratification 
observed in storage tanks) where the user controls the degree of stratification through the 
specification of the number of “nodes”. Each constant-volume node is assumed to be 
isothermal and interacts thermally with the nodes above and below through several 
mechanisms; fluid conduction between nodes, and through fluid movement (either forced 
movement from inlet flow streams or natural destratification mixing due to temperature 
inversions in the tank). The users have the ability to specify one of four different immersed 
heat exchanger types (or no HX if desired); horizontal tube bank, vertical tube bank, 
serpentine tube, or coiled tube. Auxiliary heat may be provided to each isothermal node 
individually; through the use of inputs to the model. The model also considers temperature-
dependent fluid properties for either pure water or a propylene glycol and water solution 
for both the tank and heat exchanger fluids. 
Storage Tank; Fixed Inlets, Uniform Losses (Klein, 2011): 
The thermal performance of a fluid-filled sensible energy storage tank, subject to thermal 
stratification, can be modeled by assuming that the tank consists of N (N <= 100) fully-
mixed equal volume segments. The degree of stratification is determined by the value of 
N. If N is equal to 1, the storage tank is modeled as a fully-mixed tank and no stratification 
effects are possible.  Type 4 models a stratified tank having fixed inlet positions defined 
within the code. Fluid entering the hot side of the tank is added to the tank node below the 
first auxiliary heater. Fluid entering the cold side of the tank enters the bottom node. The 
node sizes in this instance need not be equal. Temperature dead-band on heater thermostats 
are available. This instance further assumes that losses from each tank node are equal. 
The fuel cell operates at temperature of 65°C. Due to the heat extraction effectiveness the 
temperature of the fluid flowing into the heat exchanger and heat tank is assumed to be 60°C. 
This temperature is sufficient for domestic hot water supply at 50°C and radiant space heating 
at 30-40°C. The heat storage and ambient interacts through thermal losses of the storage 
boundary. The heat loss coefficient for both of the tanks was assumed to be 0.69 W/m2.K, heat 
loss coefficient for a specific size of heat storage is shown in Figure 32. Simulation of fluid 
stratification in the tank is a model in 20 isothermal temperature nodes in domestic hot water 
tank and 40 isothermal temperature nodes in space heating tank. Auxiliary heaters were 
assumed to be connected in series after the heat storage tanks and supplied additional required 
amount of heat to the system.

4.3 Economic results 
For the purpose of the economic analysis a brief summary of different electrical energy 
strategies and potential saving for the end user are presented in Table 5. The table shows the 
comparison between the four selected approaches of energy management independently of 
time and investment cost. With assumed local electricity production and 100 % efficiency of 
three usage strategies, the concepts compared below present different saving potential for the 
customer based on the retail price of supplied energy. The electrical energy in the household 
can be used to supply either electricity demand or heat demand. The electricity price per 
kilowatt-hour for the household consumer in Denmark is generally 4 times higher than the 
price of heat energy provided by district heating. The market rate for electricity exported to 
the national electricity network fluctuate on an hourly basis, however, the energy price for 
exported electricity is typically around half of the price of district heating energy. According 
to the current energy tariffs in Denmark, Klenergy storage system with an electrical efficiency 
of 44 % and heat efficiency of 35 % present a solution with higher potential saving for the 
customer than by utilizing the excess electrical energy for heating or export. The main saving 
potential can be recognized due to the big difference in the electricity price for the consumer 
and heat price of district heating. Nevertheless, the low electrical efficiency of 44 %, recovered 
electricity still presents a possible saving of 95.87 øre/kWh compared to 50.00 øre/kWh for 
the district heating.

6 DISCUSSION 
6.1 Electrical energy storage 
The urgency to resolve the electrical energy storage question as a key factor of the future 
reliability on the renewable energy sources has been introduced in previous chapters. Among 
other EES technologies that are already commercially available or the technologies which are 
currently being developed, chemical energy storage or Power to Gas system solution is 
considered to be the most applicable technology for a long-term energy storage. Even thought 
the hydrogen storage technology is still expensive and further development is needed to 
achieve more mature outcome, the recent trends and the economy of scale indicate that the 
expansion of the technology in near future will emerge. Power to Gas application on a mega-
watt scale, such as E.ON power-to-gas energy storage project in Germany (Hydrogenics, 
2012) or BioCatProject in Denmark (Skøtt, 2016) has already been launched and operate in 
cooperation with local existing natural gas pipeline network. The operation of hydrogen 
storage on a smaller scale has been investigated in Vestenskov, where the first hydrogen 
village in the world was built in 2008. At the end of the project in 2014, the conclusion stated 
that the fuel cell generating combined heat and power can replace existing electricity and heat 
supply in the households, however only the reduction of the technology price will achieve 
economic benefits of the operation (EWII, 2017). 
Further discussion on local energy storage shall be stimulated. Decreasing cost of 
photovoltaics technology will present an inexpensive and environmentally friendly source of 
energy. With an opportunity to locally store the excess energy, the high cost of electricity 
transportation and grid maintenance can be avoided. Together with no transmission losses in 
the electricity network, the personal EES could deliver economic benefits for the consumers. 
On the other hand, centralized energy storage with great capacity requires lower investment 
cost per stored unit of energy.  
The operation of hydrogen storage in a household environment raises a question of safety 
concerns regarding the gas flammability. The hydrogen as a gas is difficult to detect since it 
does not possess any odor, color or taste. The hydrogen concentration in the air presents a very 
wide flammability range (4 – 74 %) and the energy required for ignition is low, however, the 
hydrogen gas is lighter than air and has a rapid diffusivity. The bottom line is that hydrogen’s 
properties and behavior are different than the fuels we use now, although it can be handled 
safely if the user understands the hydrogen gas behavior and simple guidelines are followed 
(The National Hydrogen Association, & U.S. Department of Energy, 1875).  
6.2 The system performance  
The operation of the hydrogen system on a small scale evaluated in this study has declared the 
feasibility of the Klenergy storage system. The system in combination with photovoltaic 
installation was able to cover all the electricity demand in the household and supply more than 
30 % of the DHW and SH demand. The amount of supplied heat from the Klenergy storage 
system depends on the capacity and proposed strategy of the system. Due to the main goal of 
the system to cover all the electricity consumption of the household, the heat demand was 
==PAGE:72==
60 
 
covered by the waste heat explicitly. The difference in electricity and heat load profile of the 
household defines the system performance. The electricity demand and domestic hot water 
demand fluctuate through the year, but required amount of energy is relatively stable compared 
to space heating demand. The seasonal variation in electricity production from the Klenergy 
storage solution involves in higher heat production during the winter season what corresponds 
to the tendency of higher heat demand in winter months. Nevertheless, the amount of the heat 
produced in the fuel cell was rather limited and could not cover all the heat demand in the 
simulation. The reference house in the simulation with the heat demand of 30 kWh/m2.a 
presents efficient and well-insulated dwelling. Therefore, the household that could be heated 
by the Klenergy storage system exclusively would require to be greatly efficient. 
A significant amount of energy losses in the Klenergy system were caused due to the 
inefficiency of the electrolyzer and its performance under low current. The low current 
performance of the photovoltaic installation under low light intensity cannot be avoided in the 
system. With a small system as a domestic energy storage, only one electrolyzer is considered 
to be used to produce hydrogen. For the energy system performing on a large scale, more 
electrolyzers connected in parallel would deliver higher efficiency of the system. A different 
control strategy of the energy distribution in the system, where the low excess power from the 
PVs is utilized in the household instead of the electrolyzer, could deliver higher efficiency of 
the energy management. This operation strategy prevents the electrolyzer from running under 
low-efficiency conditions and preserves the lifetime of the unit. Even though the alkaline 
electrolyzer was used in the simulation and probably will be used in Klenergy storage as well, 
the advantages of PEM electrolyzer in following the power fluctuations designate this 
technology to be used in photovoltaic systems once the price will be sufficiently reduced to 
make the system more competitive. 
The overall efficiency of the fuel cell in the simulation has achieved more than 93 %, what 
corresponds to the efficiency 90-95% from the literature (Ulleberg, 1997a; Napoli, 2015). The 
flexibility of PEM fuel cell has certain benefits in combined heat and power production. While 
low-temperature PEM fuel cells operate at the temperature around 70°C, high-temperature 
PEM fuel cells or solid oxide fuel cells operate at much higher temperature what presents a 
certain advantage in the utilization of waste heat. With the progress in the fuel cell technology, 
both of the mentioned systems should be considered for the future design of combined heat 
and power systems. 
Even though that the hydrogen storage system is the most promising technology for a long 
term energy storage and the simulation of the Klenergy system has achieved the round-trip 
efficiency of nearly 80 %, more efficient and cost-effective technologies are available for the 
day-night operation of the energy system with photovoltaics. Specifically, electrochemical 
batteries, which are already present in many forms in almost every household, deliver certain 
advantages in energy efficiency and power density. The combination of two technologies 
presented in the report has introduced efficient and possibly economically more effective 
system. The higher electrical efficiency of the system evolves into the smaller capacity of all 
the components in the hydrogen storage system. The battery delivers an advantage in day-
night operation and peak load coverage, while the fuel cell operation in winter months 
distribute both electricity and heat to cover the household demand. Even though the storage 
==PAGE:73==
61 
 
system does not produce sufficient amount of heat to cover all the DHW nor SH demand, the 
trend of the heat production from Klenergy storage solution presents the solar collectors as the 
efficient technology to cover the DHW demand from a renewable source in summer months, 
during the operation of PVs and battery.  
The estimated cost of the system currently represents the major drawback of the technology. 
However, the price reduction in recent years indicates, that the hydrogen technology is 
becoming more competitive among other technologies. According to the economy of scale, 
with a higher number of produced units, the retail price of the technology will decrease as it 
was observed in the case of photovoltaic systems. 
6.3 Limitations  
The lack of data associated with the hydrogen technology has presented great limitation in the 
modeling of the system. Even though the electrolyzer and fuel cell technology have been 
around for several decades, the technical parameters and performance of contemporary units 
are often qualified as a confidential information of the manufacturer. Therefore, the existing 
components from HYDROGEMS library (Ulleberg, 2002) have been used in the simulation. 
For the purpose of the simulation two different energy systems have been evaluated, while the 
heating system generally presents a technology with relatively long response time, electric 
power system works with a much higher frequency of load variation. Therefore, the time step 
of 15 minutes chosen for the annual simulation presents an important limitation in regards of 
smoothing the electricity consumption peak loads. The electricity consumption profile 
retrieved from Godoy-Shimize (2014) presents a relevant source of information on a daily 
electricity consumption, however it possesses the lack of data on the household peak loads. 
For the future research, the electricity profile with higher time resolution and detailed peak 
load record shall be evaluated.  
6.4 Future research 
For the future exploitation of the technology, a system able to cover both the electricity and 
the heat demand should be investigated. Different strategies and technologies to cover the heat 
demand are possible to be combined with hydrogen energy storage. The fuel cell cooperation 
with either electric heater, heat pump, solar collector or hydrogen burner shall be investigated 
and compared. However, each of these strategies is assumed to require more energy stored 
from the PV system, which would resolve in a greater capacity of the system and therefore 
higher investment cost. The economic analysis of the Klenergy system able to cover all the 
electricity demand in the house has shown, that the current price of the photovoltaics and 
hydrogen technology do not deliver economic benefit for the  consumer, therefore the 
hydrogen storage system able to cover all the energy demand of the household presumably do 
not introduce economic performance with benefit. However, the trend of the cost reduction 
observed for both of the technologies presents the opportunity for the hydrogen system to be 
employed in near future. 
In order to obtain a more detailed insight into the hydrogen storage performance, more analysis 
shall be carried out. Due to the time restriction of the study, only one energy profile for 
electricity, space heating and domestic hot water was analyzed. For a better understanding of 
==PAGE:74==
62 
 
the system performance under different conditions, more electricity and heat profiles should 
be investigated. Combined heat and power production of the unit under various conditions of 
energy demand, specifically annual fluctuating of heat-to-electricity ratio, introduce specific 
requirements on energy system. 
The performance of the hydrogen storage has to be further considered for different locations. 
The simulation in Denmark has shown a great difference in the seasonal solar irradiation, what 
involved in large hydrogen storage tank. The same size photovoltaic installation place in South 
Europe would produce nearly twice as much energy as the one in Denmark while the heat 
demand of the household would be significantly reduced compared to the household located 
in the cold climate of Northern Europe. On the other hand, a high retail price of the electricity 
in Denmark presents a high saving potential of the household energy storage. 
The technology of regenerative fuel cell has been investigated recently. In a current design, 
the energy is stored by the chemical reaction of electrolysis, where the water is split in the 
electrolyzer and stored in hydrogen storage. The reverse process of splitting water is occurring 
in the fuel cell, where the energy and water are produced. The units are usually optimized for 
a specific reaction. The closed loop of the operation is shown in Figure 114. The technology 
of regenerative fuel cell is a fuel cell operating in a reverse mode. Therefore, only one unit is 
required in the storage system, what resolve in lower investment and maintenance cost. 
Currently, the largest reversible SOFC system by Boeing is being tested in California (Februar, 
2016). Simplified scheme of the system is shown in Figure 115.

7 CONCLUSION 
The study has proved the feasibility of Klenergy hydrogen storage with micro-CHP fuel cell 
system, nevertheless a few points have to be addressed before the expansion of the technology. 
The hydrogen as an energy carrier is a proven technology which delivers many advantages in 
a long-term electricity storage. The large scale application of Power to Gas approach is 
expected to grow and take advantage of the excess renewable energy as well as existing natural 
gas network. For the hydrogen to be used in the households as an energy storage, the safety 
concerns and the economic benefits of the hydrogen system have to be considered. 
The analysis confirmed that the household equipped with the photovoltaic installation and 
Klenergy storage was able to supply all the electricity demand and partly cover the heat 
demand of the consumer. The household energy storage based on the hydrogen technology 
evaluated in the study has achieved the round-trip energy efficiency of 78.7 %, where the 
electricity efficiency has counted for 43.7 %, while the heat efficiency was equal to 35.0 %. 
Remaining energy was lost in the system because of the inefficiencies and parasitic loads of 
the components. Due to the low electricity efficiency of the hydrogen system, a combination 
with electrochemical battery presented in the study has introduced a more efficient way of 
storing the electricity in the household. While the hydrogen can be stored in a pressurized tank 
for a long period, the batteries with electrical efficiency up to 98 % delivered important 
advantage on a day-night cycle.  
Based on the results of the economic analysis, the cost of hydrogen system, as relatively new 
commercially available technology, has to be further reduced in order to deliver economic 
benefit for the consumer. Even thought the hydrogen system as an energy storage has been 
proven to be feasible, the practical application of the technology in the household has yet to 
be demonstrated on a real case study.
