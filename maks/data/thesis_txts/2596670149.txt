Abstract 
The continuously emerging antibiotic resistance crisis calls for discovery of novel antibiotic com-
pounds.  Terrestrial microorganisms have historically been the source of many bioactive compounds; 
however, the marine environment has proven a promising supplier of natural products as well. This 
study investigated the genomic potential for secondary metabolite production of a selection of marine 
Vibrionaceae strains from the Galathea Collection of bacteria. Twenty strains were whole-genome-
sequenced using Oxford Nanopore Technologies MinION sequencing platform and multiplexed with 
the Rapid Barcoding Kit. Species identification and phylogenetic analyses based on the genome 
sequences were carried out using online tools such as The Galaxy Webserver, SpeciesFinder, 
KmerFinder, JSpeciesWS and FurIOS. The 16S rRNA gene is not a reliable phylogenetic marker for 
species delineation in Vibrionaceae strains, and therefore the species identification was based on a 
combination of 16S rRNA gene sequences, fur gene sequences and whole genome sequence com-
parisons. The use of the 16S rRNA gene as a phylogenetic marker of the Vibrionaceae family was 
discussed and found to be mainly hindered by the high interspecific similarity, more so than the high 
copy number and high intraspecific variation. The strain S0693 was identified as Vibrio kanaloae, 
S0788 and S2043 as Vibrio coralliilyticus and S1072 as Vibrio nigripulchritudo. Six strains were po-
tentially representing novel species and ten strains were not identified due to lack of sequence data. 
The nanopore sequencing technology used in the MinION has been deemed highly error-prone but 
was found suitable for species identification and phylogenetic analyses of marine Vibrionaceae, as 
the results were consistent with Illumina sequencing. The genomes of the strains were mined for 
biosynthetic gene clusters using AntiSMASH 5.1.2, revealing a great potential for production of bio-
active compounds in most strains with up to nine predicted BGCs in a strain. The strains dedicated 
2.4-6.1% of their genomes to BGCs, and most of the predicted BGCs were not similar to any known 
compounds. In conclusion, this study likely found several novel Vibrionaceae species and confirmed 
previous studies indicating a large chemical potential in Vibrionaceae strains, and so encourages 
further elucidation of the predicted BGCs.

Dansk resumé 
Antibiotikaresistens er et fortsat voksende problem der nødvendiggør opdagelsen af nye antibiotika. 
Mikroorganismer isoleret fra jord har historisk set været kilde til mange af de antibiotika, der bruges 
i dag, men det marine miljø har også vist sig at rumme en lang række producenter af bioaktive 
molekyler. Dette studie har undersøgt det genomiske potentiale for produktion af sekundære meta-
bolitter hos en række Vibrionaceae stammer fra Galathea samlingen af marine bakterier. Tyve stam-
mer er blevet helgenomsekventeret med Oxford Nanopore Technologies’ MinION sekventerings-
platform og multiplekset med ‘Rapid Barcoding Kit’. Genomsekvenserne blev brugt til at artsbe-
stemme stammerne og undersøge fylogenetiske sammenhænge ved hjælp af en række onlineværk-
tøjer, herunder ‘The Galaxy Webserver’, ’SpeciesFinder’, ’KmerFinder’, ’JSpeciesWS’ og ’FurIOS’. 
16S rRNA genet er ikke en pålidelig fylogenetisk markør for artsidentifikation i Vibrionaceae familien, 
og derfor blev der i dette studie både brugt 16S rRNA gensekvenser, fur gensekvenser og sammen-
ligninger af hele genomsekvenser. Brugen af 16S rRNA genet for artsidentificering og fylogenetiske 
analyser af Vibrionaceae blev diskuteret og det blev fundet at det største problem er den store lighed 
mellem genet på tværs af arter, mere end det er et problem at der kan være flere kopier af genet i 
et genom og at den intragenomiske variation kan være høj. Stammen S0693 blev identificeret som 
Vibrio kanaloae, S0788 og S2043 som Vibrio coralliilyticus og S1072 som Vibrio nigripulchritudo. 
Seks stammer repræsenterer måske nye arter og ti stammer kunne ikke identificeres pga. mang-
lende sekvensdata. Nanoporesekventeringsteknologien brugt I MinION har en højere fejlrate sam-
menlignet med andre sekventeringsteknologier, men blev i dette studie stadig fundet egnet til brug 
ved artsidentificering og fylogenetiske analyser af marine Vibrionaceae, da resultaterne var sam-
menlignelige med Illuminasekventering. Derudover blev arternes genomiske potentiale for produk-
tion af sekundære metabolitter undersøgt ved brug af værktøjet AntiSMASH 5.1.2, det viste et stort 
potentiale for de fleste af stammerne med op til ni forventede biosyntetiske genklynger i en stamme. 
Stammerne tilegner 2,4-6,1% af deres genomer til biosyntetiske genklynger, og de fleste af de for-
ventede genklynger er forskellige fra kendte genklynger. Afslutningsvis har dette studie sandsynlig-
vis fundet nye arter af familien Vibrionaceae og bekræftet det kemiske potentiale, der tidligere er 
blevet set i Vibrionaceae stammer samt vist at yderligere undersøgelse af disse stammers sekun-
dære metabolitter kan lede til nye antibiotika.

1. Introduction
Microbial natural products play a crucial role in modern day society. They are found in industrial 
processes as biocatalysts and biomaterials, in foods to make them safer or more appealing to eat 
and in the healthcare system as a multitude of drugs including antibiotics and anticancer drugs. 
Especially the antibiotics have changed our lives.  
During the so-called ‘golden age’ of antibiotics discovery from the 1940s to the 1970s, scientist dis-
covered more than 20 classes of antibiotics [1]. It changed the way we treated infectious diseases 
and made sure that simple bacterial infections are no longer death sentences. Unfortunately, this is 
changing again as the emergence of antibiotic resistance increases. Today, resistance has been 
seen against every antibiotic placed into clinical practice (Figure 1) [2].  
Figure 1 A timeline of the introduction of antibiotics and following emergence of resistance. The top 
panel shows the year some important antibiotics were introduced to the clinical setting, and the lower panel 
shows when resistance was first observed. Resistance has been observed for all antibiotics used in the clinical 
setting. Figure from Clatworthy et al. 2007 [3]. 
A well-known example is methicillin resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA), a pathogen causing 
skin infections among other things, that has become resistant to a list of drugs including all available 
penicillins and other β-lactams and is widely spread in both hospital and community settings includ-
ing animal husbandry [4]. Other serious examples are Klebsiella pneumonia that often shows re-
sistance towards carbapenem, a last choice drug for treating infections with Gram-negative bacteria, 
or extensively drug resistant tuberculosis (XDR-TB) where the infecting agent is resistant to four of 
the key anti-tuberculous drugs [5], [6]. This, in combination with the fact that only few new antibiotics 
have been discovered the last couple of decades (Figure 2), as few pharmaceutical companies work 
in this area, shows the urgent need for new solutions for treating or avoiding microbial infections [2]. 
==PAGE:11==
Side 2 af 74 
Figure 2 A timeline of the discovery of the largest classes of antibiotics. Bottom panel: year of discovery. 
Top panel: year of introduction into the clinical setting. Figure from Lewis 2020 [7]. Most of the antibiotic com-
pounds used in the clinical setting were discovered more than 50 years ago, and in the last couple of decades

New solutions can either focus on completely different strategies such as use of phage therapy or 
probiotics or focus on finding new antibiotic compounds for example by chemical synthesis of com-
pletely novel compounds, chemical modifications of known compounds or searching for novel natural 
compounds. For a period of time, the focus was on developing chemically synthesised compounds, 
as the search for natural products is more expensive and cumbersome. It did however prove difficult 
to produce synthetic compounds as efficiently as natural products. Therefore, the focus is now re-
turning to natural product discoveries [8]. 
Terrestrial microorganisms have been the main source of novel natural products, where more than 
80,000 compounds have been discovered [9]. The marine environment on the other hand, has been 
less utilised as a source for novel bioactive compounds. Different natural products can be found in 
the marine environment as compared to the terrestrial niches, as the conditions differ significantly 
[9]. Marine bacteria often survive or even thrive in conditions with lower temperature, higher pres-
sure, dissolved oxygen, high salt concentration and so forth [10] and thus it is expected that their 
secondary metabolites could have chemical structures different from those produced by terrestrial 
organisms [11]. The potential for production of bioactive compounds by marine bacteria have been 
supported by several studies from this group [12]–[14]. Gram et al. 2010 even showed that the ability 
of producing antibacterial compounds is found in marine bacteria on a global scale, especially among 
surface-associated bacteria [12].

This thesis will be one more small step in the direction of discovering useful bioactive compounds, 
to participate in the fight against the antibiotic resistance crisis. Specifically, a selection of Vibri-
onaceae strains from the Galathea Collection of marine bacteria will be investigated. The strains 
were sampled based on antibacterial activity, and the selection covers a wide variety of sampling 
sites, preliminary species IDs and antibacterial potential. Whole genome sequencing will be per-
formed using the Oxford Nanopore Technologies MinION for species identification, phylogenetic 
analyses, and genome mining for potential bioactive molecules. 
2. Background
2.1 The Galathea Collection of marine bacteria 
The Galathea Collection was created with the aim of utilising marine bacteria for bioprospecting. It 
was established by Lone Gram during the global research expedition Galathea 3 on the Danish Navy 
vessel Vædderen in 2006-2007 (Figure 3) [15]. The collection is located at the Technical University 
of Denmark [12]. It contains 519 bacterial strains isolated from seawater and biotic or abiotic surfaces 
from locations across the globe, that demonstrated antibacterial activity against the Gram-negative 
fish pathogenic bacteria Vibrio anguillarum and/or the Gram-positive human pathogenic bacterium 
S. aureus [12]. Of these strains, 301 were identified as belonging to the family Vibrionaceae by initial
sequencing of the partial 16S rRNA gene. This was also supported by phenotypic tests showing that
the strains were glucose-fermenting, could grow on TCBS and were sensitive to vibriostaticum [12].
Figure 3 The route of the Galathea 3 expedition on which marine bacteria with antibiotic activity were sam-
pled from around the globe. Figure from www.galathea3.dk [15]. 
2.2 The Vibrionaceae family 
With 301 strains, the Vibrionaceae family is the best represented bacterial family in the Galathea 
Collection. Bacteria belonging to the Vibrionaceae family are Gram-negative, usually motile, curved 
rod-shaped, gammaproteobacteria and they are most often oxidase positive [14], [16]. Many species 
are mesophilic, meaning they usually live in moderate temperatures, which is reflected in that they 
are more abundant in subtropical and tropical regions, but psychrophiles also exist and are found in 
polar environments [17]. They are facultative fermentative and as chemoorganotrophs they utilise 
organic molecules for both energy and carbon source [16]. Different Vibrionaceae are able to utilise 
a wide range of carbon and nitrogen sources, one of the most prominent being chitin [17]. Chitin is 
==PAGE:13==
Side 4 af 74 
the most abundant biopolymer in the marine environment, as it is the main constituent of e.g. crus-
taceans’ exoskeletons. Degradation of chitin is often linked to specific phenotypes [17]. Vibrionaceae 
are usually easily cultivated in the lab, and can grow on e.g. marine agar and the selective media 
thiosulfate-citrate-bile salt-sucrose agar (TCBS agar) [16], [17]. By mimicking natural conditions in 
the lab, it is possible to significantly change the phenotype of cultured Vibrionaceae [18]. 
New species of the Vibrionaceae family are continuously identified, expanding the size of the family, 
and as techniques for determining similarities and differences between strains are improved, typi-
cally by genome analyses, the organisation of the family also changes. At the moment, the family 
consists of 190 species (Figure 4) with valid nomenclature distributed between 11 genera: Aliivibrio 
(6 species), Catenococcus (1 species), Echinimonas (1 species), Enterovibrio (5 species), Grimontia 
(3 species), Listonella (2 species), Paraphotobacterium (1 species), Photobacterium (35 species), 
Salinivibrio (6 species), Thaumasiovibrio (2 species) and Vibrio (128 species) [19], [20].  
Figure 4 Phylogenetic tree of Vibrionaceae strains based on 16S rRNA gene sequences by neighbour-
joining method. Figure from Al-saari et al. 2015 [21] .

Previously, the preferred techniques for novel species identification of Vibrionaceae strains have 
been DNA-DNA hybridisation, repetitive extragenic palindromic PCR and amplified fragment length 
polymorphism (AFLP), as it is difficult to identify species with other methods. The difficulties arise 
from the fact that several Vibrionaceae share similar phenotypic traits and 16S rRNA gene se-
quences and that some strains carry several copies of the 16S rRNA gene with high variation to a 
degree, where these typical measures are not able to differentiate all Vibrionaceae species [16]. 
These methods were followed by sequencing and comparison of housekeeping genes in for example 
a multi locus sequence analysis (MLSA) [16]. The many shared features are found because many 
species are closely related. Examples of closely related groups that are often difficult to distinguish 
are V. brasiliensis-V. tubiashii-V. xuii- V. coralliilyticus-V. neptunius, V. alginolyticus-V. parahaemo-
lyticus- V. campbellii-V. rotiferianus and V. lentus-V. splendidus [14], [16]. Closely related strains 
form groups called clades (Figure 5). 
Figure 5 Phylogenetic tree of Vibrionaceae strains showing how they group into clades. The tree is a 
concatenated split network tree based on eight gene loci. The ftsZ, gapA, gyrB, mreB, pyrH, recA, and topA 
gene sequences from 96 taxa were concatenated, and a tree was reconstructed using the SplitsTree4 pro-
gram. Clades indicated by a solid red line were the “new” clades. Clades indicated by a dotted red line or by a 
dotted black line are the clades “emended” and “un-changed,” respectively. Figure and description from 
Sawabe et al. 2013 [22].

Vibrionaceae commonly possess two chromosomes, of which the large chromosome is usually 
within 3.0-3.3 Mb and the small chromosome varies more in size from 0.8-2.4 Mb [23]. Genomes of 
up to 6.4 Mb have been reported [24]. The larger chromosome is usually more conserved, containing 
housekeeping genes involved in translation, replication, and motility, whereas the smaller chromo-
some is more variable and contains genes involved in pathogenicity, antimicrobial resistance, and 
host avoiding mechanisms [17]. In addition to the variability found on the smaller chromosome, Vibri-
onaceae is also suspected to be able to vary the copy number of their chromosomes [25]. Some 
Vibrionaceae become naturally competent when grown in the presence of chitin [26], [27]. All these 
possibilities for diversity allow for niche speciation. 
Vibrionaceae are found in great numbers in almost all marine and brackish environments around the 
world [16], [17]. Here they often live together in commensal, symbiotic or pathogenic relationships 
with eukaryotic organisms such as corals, mussels, fish, plants, sponges etc. [14]. Their pathogenic 
nature is for example seen when vibrios are involved in coral bleaching [28], and when V. anguilla-
rum, V. harveyi, V. splendidus and V. tubiashii often cause diseases in fish and shellfish [16]. On the 
other hand, some Vibrionaceae have potential as probionts in fish rearing, e.g. some V. halioticoli 
strains are potentially mutualists to abalones [16]. 
An important component for the ability of Vibrionaceae to live in such close contact with other organ-
isms, is the ability to form and grow in biofilms. The biofilm mode of growth makes it possible for 
microorganisms to stay attached to surfaces, and often confers a range of benefits for the producer, 
such as increased resistance towards harmful substances such as antibiotics [29] and acid stress 
[30]. Another good reason for staying surface attached is the high nutrient concentrations present in 
and on eukaryotic hosts [17]. Another important factor of the relationships between Vibrionaceae 
and eukaryotic host organisms, is the use of quorum sensing. Examples of vibrios using quorum 
sensing are V. fischeri that senses the density of cells in the light organs of their host squid and 
regulate bioluminescence accordingly or the pathogens V. anguillarum, V. cholerae and V. harveyi 
among others that regulate expression of virulence genes based on cell density [31]. 
The family of Vibrionaceae has been known since 1854 [31], and today some species are especially 
well studied. One example is the aforementioned V. fischeri, a bioluminescent species that has a 
symbiotic relationship with a bobtail squid [32]. Perhaps the most well-known is V. cholerae, an im-
portant human pathogen. V. cholerae have been responsible for several cholera epidemics, often 
spreading through contaminated drinking water. It is found as a natural inhabitant of marine environ-
ments attached to surfaces of e.g. plants, zooplankton and crustaceans, and is therefore a facultative 
pathogen [33]. Other species of Vibrio cause diseases in humans as well, for example V. parahae-
molyticus that can cause severe seafood poisoning and V. vulnificus that causes infections [17]. The 
ability of being such potent human pathogens, has resulted in Vibrionaceae being researched mostly 
due to their pathogenicity, including research on virulence factors. For a long time, little was therefore 
known on secondary metabolite production not involved in pathogenesis [17].  
This does not mean however, that no interesting secondary metabolism exist in Vibrionaceae spe-
cies. More than 90 different compounds have been isolated from Vibrionaceae [14], among these 
are antibiotics such as andrimid [13], holomycin [13] and prodigiosin [34] and compounds that inter-
fere with virulence or other important systems in other bacterial species such as the ngercheumicins 
[35] and the solonamides [36]. Furthermore compounds have been isolated from Vibrionaceae, with
anticancer, antifungal and antimalarial activity [14].

2.2.1 Vibrionaceae strains in the Galathea Collection 
Some of the Vibrionaceae strains from which bioactive compounds have been isolated are found in 
the Galathea Collection, which indicates the chemical potential of this collection. Previous genome 
mining of Vibrionaceae strains from this collection, such as Photobacterium galatheae S2753 and 
V. coralliilyticus S2052 and S4053 indicated a larger bioactive potential than found by chemical anal-
yses, with up to 12 biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) identified by antiSMASH 2.0 and more hits
using other genome mining software [37].
P. galatheae S2753 (Figure 6) was at first identified as P. halotolerans [12], but was later discovered
to represent a novel species [38]. This type strain for the species P. galatheae was obtained from a
mussel in the Solomon sea [38]. The closed genome of S2753T contains 12 copies of the 16S rRNA
gene, of which nine copies shared great similarity [38].
Figure 6 Photobacterium galatheae S2753. Cells of S2753T are straight, rod-shaped and have a polar fla-
gellum. Picture from supplementary materials to Machado et al. 2015 [38]. 
S2753 produces the known antibiotic holomycin (Figure 7.1), that was previously found in terrestrial 
Streptomyces spp. and a fish pathogenic species, Yersinia ruckeri [37], [39]. Holomycin is a dithio-
lopyrrolone compound [37]. It is a potent antibiotic with a broad spectrum of antibacterial activity 
against pathogenic bacteria e.g. S. aureus, S. pneumoniae, S. epidermis, Enterococcus faecalis, 
Escherichia coli as well as marine strains such as V. harveyi, V. vulnificus and V. parahaemolyticus 
[14]. Holomycin is produced by an NRPS biosynthetic cluster [14]. Its antibiotic activity is inhibiting 
cellular processes by disrupting the metal, particularly Zn2+, homeostasis [40]. 
S2753 also produces several kinds of cyclodepsipeptides, the solonamides and ngercheumicins, 
which interfere with the virulence of S. aureus [37]. The ngercheumicins A-E have a depsipeptide 
macrocycle and either a fatty acid or peptide tail (Figure 7.3 and  7.4 ) [14]. 
Other examples of strains with interesting secondary metabolism are the strains S2052 and S4053. 
They have been identified as the coral pathogen Vibrio coralliilyticus based on MLSA of three house-
keeping genes in addition to 16S rRNA gene similarities [12], [13]. The two strains were sampled 
from distant geographical locations, but both produce the known antibiotic andrimid [13]. Not all V. 
coralliilyticus strains produce this antibiotic and neither does the type strain of the species [14]. Apart 
==PAGE:17==
Side 8 af 74 
from Vibrio spp., distantly related bacteria including Enterobacter sp., Pseudomonas fluorescens 
and Pantoea agglomerans produce andrimid [14].  
Andrimid is a NRPS-PKS hybrid peptide that impacts fatty acid biosynthesis in target organisms [14]. 
It consist of a pyrrolidinedione head that has been shown to be essential for activity, and a fatty acid 
tail that can be more variable (Figure 7.2) [14]. 
When grown on nutrient rich medium, the strain S2052 produced many different secondary metab-
olites, but by mimicking natural conditions, the metabolite synthesis changed. When grown on chitin, 
S2052 focuses its metabolite production on andrimid [18]. 
Even though both holomycin and andrimid were already known antibiotics before they were detected

2.3 Whole Genome Sequencing 
Today, one of the first steps in the process of bioprospecting is often genomic analyses, as it is 
possible to screen large numbers of microorganisms for genomic potential, before determining which 
strains to investigate more thoroughly in the laboratory.  
Whole genome sequencing of bacteria has been around for 25 years now, as the first shotgun se-
quencing of a bacterium was performed in 1995 on Haemophilus influenzae. During the 2000s whole 
genome sequencing evolved with the possibility of doing high-throughput sequencing, and the field 
continues to progress. Today it is even possible to sequence single DNA molecules and obtain se-
quencing data in real time during sequencing directly in the field [41]. 
2.3.1 Types of sequencing 
DNA sequencing methods are split into generations based on characteristics of the techniques [9]. 
The 1970s saw the first techniques developed for sequencing DNA. In 1975 Sanger and Coulson 
presented the “plus and minus” method, where DNA is synthesised in the absence and presence of 
specific nucleotides and the length of the resulting fragments are used to determine the sequence 
[42]. In 1976 Maxam and Gilbert published a method termed “chemical sequencing method”, where 
the DNA is treated with different chemicals that affect specific nucleotides, the DNA strand is broken 
at the affected site and again the length of the resulting fragments is used to determine the sequence 
[43]. In 1977 Sanger et al. presented a new technique called “chain termination sequencing”, where 
DNA synthesis by DNA polymerase is conducted in the presence of regular nucleotides as well as 
the dideoxy derivative of one of the nucleotides at a time, producing DNA fragments of different 
lengths with a known last nucleotide and the sequence can then again be determined by the length 
of the different fragments by gel electrophoresis [44]. 
The chain termination sequencing method proved to be less complex and more scalable and was 
therefore the type of first-generation sequencing that became most widely used and is often referred 
to as Sanger sequencing [9], [45]. Sanger sequencing was used for e.g. the first draft sequence of 
the human genome, a project that was estimated to cost between 0.5-1 billion dollars [46]. 
As ground-breaking as first-generation sequencing was, it is however quite low-throughput, expen-
sive and time consuming. So even though the technique is pretty precise and give high quality reads 
of up to 1000 bp, it was not long before the work on second generation sequencing started [9], [45], 
[47].  
Second generation sequencing (also called Next generation sequencing, NGS) first saw the world 
with the introduction of parallel sequencing by 454 Life Sciences’ (now Roche’s) pyrosequencing in 
2005 [45]. This technique generate reads of up to 1000 bp and base calling is done by measuring 
fluorescence [48], [49]. Single-stranded DNA is immobilised on beads where DNA polymerase can 
synthesise the complementary strand. One nucleotide is added at a time and afterwards washed 
away prior to the addition of the next nucleotide. When a nucleotide is incorporated, pyrophosphate 
is released and can be converted to ATP by ATP sulfurylase and further used by Luciferase to gen-
erate light, that can be measured [50]. 
Soon after came Solexa’s (now Illumina) sequencing-by-synthesis based Genome Analyzer platform 
in 2006 and Applied Biosystems (later Life Technologies, now ThermoFisher Scientific) sequencing-
by-ligation based SOLiD (Sequencing by Oligo Ligation Detection) in 2007 [9], [45], [46], [49].  
==PAGE:19==
Side 10 af 74 
 
The technique used by Illumina is based on incorporation of modified nucleotides during DNA syn-
thesis. The nucleotides are modified in a way, that only allows the addition of a single nucleotide, 
that can be made fluorescent to measure the incorporation of the nucleotide. Afterwards the modifi-
cation can be removed to allow for the addition of the next nucleotide [48]. These sequencers can 
generate larger amounts of data than the pyrosequencers at roughly the same price per run, but the 
reads are shorter at around 300 bp [49]. Since the introduction of the first machine, Illumina have 
introduced newer platforms optimised for different use, and Illumina machines still dominate the high-
throughput sequencing (HTS) market as they offer the highest throughput and the lowest per-base 
cost [46], [49]. 
In SOLiD sequencing, target DNA is fragmented, adapters are ligated to the ends and the DNA 
fragments are linked to beads where they are amplified. Sequencing primers binds to the adapters, 
and on these primers fluorescently labelled di-base probes are ligated. This process is repeated 
several times with primers of different lengths, until all bases are covered and the sequence can be 
determined [51]. 
Iron Torrent (later Life Technologies, now ThermoFisher Scientific) introduced their semiconductor 
sequencing technology in 2010, where DNA strands are amplified and linked to beads on where 
DNA synthesis can occur under the sequential addition of individual nucleotides, and the incorpora-
tion of nucleotides is detected by pH changes induced by the release of hydrogen ions during DNA 
synthesis [46], [49]. This process produces reads of up to 400 bp [49]. 
In NGS, the DNA to be sequenced is fragmented and often amplified by PCR [9]. The characteristics 
of the NGS technologies is the ability to produce large amount of data through higher throughput 
and lower prices, but with the downsides of the reads being very short due to the fragmentation and 
the quality a bit poorer than of first generation sequencing [9], [45]. The short reads lead to more 
fragmented assemblies, but this can to some extent be combatted with increasing coverage [48]. 
Some problems cannot be solved with higher coverage however, it is for example difficult to se-
quence repetitive regions longer than the reads produced [48]. PCR amplification is also prone to 
have a bias against extreme GC content [52]. 
The need for longer reads produced at high speed and low cost drove the development of third-
generation sequencing (TGS) [45]. TGS is provided commercially by Pacific Biosciences (PacBio) 
with Single Molecule Real Time (SMRT) sequencing released in 2010 and by Oxford Nanopore 
Technologies (ONT) with nanopore sequencing introduced in 2014 [9]. TGS technologies sequence 
single DNA molecules, and therefore no amplification is necessary [45]. This has several benefits, 
including the ability to produce long reads, avoid PCR bias, cutting down sequencing time and the 
possibility of including DNA modifications in sequencing [45], [46]. As with all technologies, the ben-
efits are coupled with some downsides, here including less accuracy and throughput [9]. 
In SMRT sequencing, hairpin adapters are ligated to double stranded DNA to create a circular tem-
plate, allowing for multiple sequencing of the same molecule. Sequencing is done by DNA synthesis 
inside a very small chamber using nucleotides with different fluorophores, that gets excited and emits 
light of different colours upon incorporation, which can be measured continuously. By doing this, 
nucleotide incorporation is measured in real time [46]. The average read length is >14 kb and long 
reads can be up to 60 kb, and a single SMRT cell can generate up to 1 GB data in 4 hours [46]. The 
error rates are high at around 11%, but at least they are randomly distributed, so that increasing 
coverage aids in achieving accurate sequences [46]. 
==PAGE:20==
Side 11 af 74 
 
In Nanopore sequencing, whole DNA strands are pulled through a nanopore through which also runs 
an ionic current. When the DNA strand is pulled through, the current is disturbed in a specific way 
for each nucleotide, which can be translated to the DNA sequence [53]. This generates very long 
reads, but as with SMRT sequencing the error rate is higher than for NGS. As nanopore sequencing 
is the technique that has been used in this thesis, the next section will cover ONT nanopore se-
quencing in more depth. 
2.3.2 Oxford Nanopore Technology nanopore sequencing 
Oxford Nanopore Technologies (ONT) is a United Kingdom-based company with the goal of enabling 
“analysis of any living thing, by anyone, anywhere” through accessible and easy-to-use real-time 
nanopore-based DNA/RNA sequencing technology [54]. Their nanopore technology is available in a 
range of formats, from the small Flongle and MinION, through the GridION able to run five sequenc-
ing experiments at once to the large PromethION for population-scale sequencing [54].  
Common to the ONT devices is the use of nanopores for sequencing, making it possible to produce 
very long reads, theoretically only limited by the length of the prepared DNA fragments [55]. Reads 
of more than 800 kb have been produced in the sequencing and used in assembly of a human 
genome [56], and reads of up to 2 Mb have even been reported [57]. ONT uses engineered protein 
nanopores embedded in a membrane with very high electrical resistance. By applying a potential 
across the membrane, an ionic current is passed through the nanopores. When a biological molecule 
such as DNA passes through or near the nanopore, the current is disrupted in a characteristic way, 
and the change can be measured and used to identify the molecule [53]. During library preparation 
an adapter DNA sequence and a motor enzyme is connected to the DNA strands, so that the motor 
enzyme can pass the DNA strand through the nanopores (Figure 8) [53].

In some experiments, a hairpin adapter joining the template and complementary strands is used to 
sequence both strands in one go, as they pass through the nanopore in succession [59]. This is 
called “2D” reads, and they produce more accurate base calls than “1D” reads (the template or 
complement strand alone) [59]. Addition of the hairpin structures followed by denaturation results in 
a single stranded fragment of DNA that consist of the forward strand, followed by the adapter and 
then the reverse strand. When this fragment moves through the nanopore, reads are acquired of the 
“template” strand first and then the “complement” strand. Afterwards the base calling software at-
tempts to call a consensus sequence based on both reads, referred to as the 2D read [55].  
The MinION sequencer used in this thesis is a pocket-sized sequencing device developed by ONT 
capable of producing data in real-time (Figure 9) [55]. It weighs only around 100 g, and it is powered 
through a USB 3.0 connection to a standard computer or laptop [47]. Scaling the size down to this 
level is possible because no imaging equipment is necessary [60]. Using the MinION, it is currently 
possible to sequence bacterial genomes at a price of around 50 USD pr. genome. 
The small size, easy portability and low initial investment means the MinION has been used for 
sequencing experiments in remote locations or unusual and extreme settings like during the Ebola 
outbreak in West Africa [61], in the middle of cassava-fields in Sub-Saharan Africa [62], deep into a 
rainforest [63], during an ice cap traverse expedition [64] and even in space on the International 
Space Station [65].

Nanopore sequencing have been deemed highly error-prone, but the technology is still under inten-
sive development, and both chemistry and data processing is improving [47]. When the MinION was 
first introduced, a study assessing the performance found the error rate after base calling to be 
38.2% [55]. Already four years later in 2018, the error rates had improved to 15-25%. This is however 
still high when compared to the 1% error rate of short-read platforms [66]. 
Even though Nanopore reads have high error rates when compared to Illumina reads, it is not nec-
essarily to an extent where it obscures the strain-level phylogenetic differences [52]. Tanaka et al. 
compared overall genome relatedness indices (OGRI) and multi locus sequence analysis (MLSA) 
based on sequencing of five closely related Vibrionaceae species with Nanopore only, Illumina only 
and hybrid assemblies in a study in 2018 [67]. As previous studies, they found that Nanopore se-
quencing showed higher error rates than Illumina sequencing, but the errors did not influence aver-
age nucleotide identity (ANI), in silico DNA-DNA hybridisation (DDH), G+C content or MLSA tree 
topology and low frequencies of mismatch and indels were observed in the eight genes used for 
MLSA [67]. The high error rates means that Nanopore sequencing is perhaps still not suitable for 
allele type or small variant studies, but the study shows that Nanopore sequencing can be used for 
species identification based on whole genome similarity [67]. 
When assembling genomes based on Nanopore reads it is generally possible to produce more con-
tiguous assemblies than when using Illumina, but the assemblies are often less accurate due to the 
higher error rates [52]. Assembly of MinION-reads requires a relatively low coverage around 30x to 
produce high quality genomes, except for strains with extreme GC content, where a higher coverage 
might improve quality [52]. Annotation of genomes assembled from Nanopore reads is able to predict 
more biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) in repetitive or bias-prone regions as compared to assembly 
of Illumina reads, and is able to reduce the number of BGCs that are fragmented by being located 
at a contig end. On the other hand, annotation of assembled Nanopore reads might miss some BGCs 
due to erroneous sequences with e.g. introduction of stop codons or frameshifts, and might even 
predict false positive BGCs as well [52].  
Nanopore reads can also be used for hybrid assemblies. The combination of short reads with low 
error-rate (such as Illumina) and long reads, even though error-prone (such as Nanopore), is able to 
provide high quality assembled genomes with low number of contigs and high N50 [52]. Especially 
regions with repetition and genomes with extreme GC contents are better resolved using Nanopore 
reads. This means hybrid assemblies of MinION and Illumina reads are able to construct gap-closed, 
finished-grade circular genomes of Vibrionaceae [67].  
To increase throughput and lower the per-genome price of sequencing, ONT offers a barcoding 
solution enabling sequencing of several samples on the same flow cell. DNA barcodes are unique 
oligonucleotide sequences attached to sample DNA [68]. During the library preparation for barcoded 
sequencing, the DNA is fragmented, so the sequenced fragments differs from the previously de-
scribed 2D sequences. The DNA is barcoded using a barcoded transposome complex, that cleaves 
the genomic DNA (gDNA) and adds barcoded transposase adapters. Afterwards the barcoded sam-
ples are pooled, and the sequencing adapters are attached (Figure 10) [69]. After sequencing, the 
samples can be demultiplexed using different softwares. The EPI2ME platform by Metrichor Ltd (a 
subsidiary of ONT) offers a workflow for demultiplexing among other things [69].

2.4 Sequence data treatment and analyses 
DNA sequencing generates large amounts of data, and careful data analysis is key to obtaining 
biological insights from it. However, not all biologists are experts in data analysis and bioinformatics, 
and to ensure high quality research many easy-to-use, accessible online tools exist for doing bioin-
formatics workflows. They enable microbiologists to profit from bioinformatics tools without the need 
to learn a programming language, use a command line interface or familiarise themselves with Linux 
package management. The Galaxy platform is an example of a web-based platform for analysis of 
biological data in areas such as genomics, proteomics and metabolomics [70]. It is a free, public, 
internet accessible resource with more than 5,500 tools available [70].  
When sequence data has been obtained, one of the first steps in the sequence data treatment is 
quality control. When sequencing DNA, there is always a risk of introducing errors in the sequence. 
The type and frequency of errors differ between techniques and platforms, but the impact of all of 
them can be partly mitigated by proper quality control. Often, sequence data (when produced in the 
FASTQ format) contains a quality score for each base, in addition to the nucleic sequence. These 
quality scores can be analysed to assess the overall quality of the reads using for example the pro-
gram FastQC, an open-source tool that is available at the Galaxy Server [71]. FastQC gives a list of 
measures describing the quality of the reads such as the ‘per sequence quality scores’ and ‘per base 
sequence quality’. For Illumina reads, ‘per base sequence quality’ should be above 20 or else 
trimmed. The cutoff is not valid for Nanopore reads, as the quality of Nanopore reads is generally 
lower and MinION quality scores do not follow the Phred scale [55]. Quality control of Nanopore 
reads can be done using a tool called NanoPlot, that is also available on Galaxy and can visualise 
sequence quality by using either the sequence and quality scores in FASTQ format or a sequencing 
summary provided by the MinKNOW software. NanoPlot illustrates various aspects of the sequenc-
ing data in different plots and diagrams, as well as providing numbers on read length, quality scores 
and more [72]. 
==PAGE:24==
Side 15 af 74 
 
High-quality reads can be assembled into contigs or ideally into complete genomes. An example of 
a tool for this is Unicycler that is available on Galaxy. Unicycler is an assembler that can take both 
Illumina and Nanopore reads and make a hybrid assembly. It is also possible to use only one type 
of reads. Unicycler relies on SPAdes for producing an assembly graph and on Pilon for polishing the 
contigs with the short reads [73]. SPAdes also does some error correction of the reads to increase 
the quality of the finished assembly [73]. 
After assembly, the contigs or complete genomes are ready to be analysed. An analysis that provide 
valuable biological insights is annotation of the genome. In general, annotation means to add a note 
of explanation or commentary to something e.g. a text, and gene annotation specifically means to 
identify the location, name and potential function of genes and coding regions on a genome. Prokka 
is a tool for annotation of genomes from bacteria, archaea, and viruses, also available on Galaxy. 
Prokka is a wrapper of several feature prediction tools, and it predicts protein-coding sequences 
using Prodigal, rRNA genes using RNAmmer, tRNA genes using Aragorn, signal leader peptides 
using SignalP and non-coding RNA genes using Infernal [73]. An assembled genome can also be 
submitted to tools that search specifically for other features such as antimicrobial resistance genes, 
genes involved in secondary metabolite production, virulence genes etc. Another use for assembled 
genomes is taxonomic or phylogenetic analyses, which will be dealt with in the next section. 
2.5 Taxonomy and phylogeny 
Taxonomy is the systematic classification of living organisms, including microorganisms [74]. The 
correct identification and naming of isolated bacteria is an important part of microbiological work, 
whether the purpose is diagnosis of infectious diseases, biotechnological utilisation of microorgan-
isms or fundamental research, as it allows for clear communication and comparison of results. In 
1965 Cowan divided taxonomy into three parts: classification, identification and nomenclature [74]. 
Classification means to sort organisms into taxonomic groups based on their differences and simi-
larities. Identification can be done by comparing an unknown species to previously described taxa. 
Nomenclature is to name organisms in a systematic way [75]. Even though much has changed since 
1965, the core ideas are still relevant. Cowan stated, that both classification and identification are 
based on characterisation, but with different use of the features. In classification, all independent 
characters are equally important, whereas for identification some traits will be more important than 
others [74].  
One of the most fundamental ways of classifying and dividing organisms into separate groups is by 
species. From the animal kingdom, we know species to be defined by the ability of two individuals 
to produce fertile offspring. This definition does not work in the microbial world, however. The micro-
biologist’s definition of species has changed with time as it was originally based on physiology and 
was later expanded to include staining, biochemical profiles, and genetic analyses. In the 1960s the 
first methods for genome comparison was introduced [76], and later, in the 1970s, it became possible 
to sequence DNA fragments to use for genealogical reconstruction and taxonomy [76]. The definition 
of species can vary depending on the area of microbiology. For example, in ecology a relative re-
laxed circumscription such as by 16S rRNA gene variation might be adequate, whereas more details 
are necessary for evolutionary microbiologists [76]. Rosselló-Móra and Amann defined a species as 
“a category that circumscribes monophyletic, and genomically and phenotypically coherent popula-
tions of individuals that can be clearly discriminated from other such entities by means of standard-
ized parameters” in 2015 [76]. 
==PAGE:25==
Side 16 af 74 
 
 
In addition to taxonomic analyses, gene sequence data can also be used for phylogenetic analyses. 
Phylogeny is the evolutionary development and diversification of a group of organisms. Taxonomy 
and phylogeny is interlinked in some way, as one of the main criteria today for designating a new 
taxonomic rank is that phylogenetic clustering suggests doing so [77]. 
Molecular methods are useful in taxonomy and phylogeny, as they allow microbiologist to investigate 
organisms that cannot be cultivated in current laboratory settings, or that do not utilise their full ge-
nomic potential in the lab [78]. Today, a polyphasic approach is taken when identifying new species. 
This means basing the definition on a wide set of parameters to fulfil the three requirements mon-
ophyly, genomic coherence and phenotypic coherence [76]. Monophyly means that the group of 
organisms consist of all the descendants to a common ancestor (a group that does not include or-
ganisms with different ancestors or lack (groups of) organisms that share the same ancestor) and is 
usually demonstrated by phylogenetic inferences based on housekeeping genes [76]. Genomic co-
herence means a great degree of similarity in gene content and gene sequence, and is demonstrated 
by comparison of several phylogenetic marker genes (e.g. MLSA) or entire genomes (e.g. dDDH or 
ANI) [76]. Phenotypic coherence is a great degree of similarity of the observable characteristics that 
results from gene expression, and can perhaps in part be demonstrated by genome annotation, but 
a thorough phenotypic characterisation is necessary to get the full picture [76]. The parameters used 
for a polyphasic approach can include a combination of 16S rRNA or other phylogenetic marker 
genes similarities, DNA G+C mol%, DNA:DNA hybridisation, morphology in/on different media, 
spore forming abilities, nutrient requirements, antimicrobial susceptibility testing, enzyme activities 
and stress tolerance among others [75]. For a gene to work as a phylogenetic marker, it needs to 
fulfil a set of criteria. The gene needs to be present in all the organisms to be compared, it should 
carry out the same function in all the organisms, it should preferably only be present as one copy in 
each genome, the gene sequence should be long enough to allow for discriminatory information to 
be contained, but short enough to be easily sequenced and it should be possible to predict whole-
genome relationships with precision and accuracy that can be compared to the use of e.g. 16S rRNA 
gene data or DNA-DNA hybridisation [79].  
Different parameters have different boundaries for determining if two strains are of the same species. 
Some example of boundaries are AFLP pattern similarity ≥60% [16], DNA-DNA similarity ≥70% [16], 
16S rRNA gene sequence ≥97.5% [16] and ANI ≥95% [38]. The boundaries for some genes often 
used for MLSA of Vibrionaceae is ≥98% rpoA gene sequence, ≥94% recA gene sequence and ≥94% 
pyrH gene sequence [16]. 
 
2.5.1 Tools for species identification and phylogeny based on gene sequences 
As previously mentioned, a common method for bacterial taxonomy and phylogeny is 16S rRNA 
gene sequence comparison [9]. The genes can be compared by aligning them and drawing a phy-
logenetic tree based on the alignment. It is also possible to use tools that attempts to species identify 
organisms by comparing the 16S rRNA gene sequence, like the tool SpeciesFinder (available at 
https://cge.cbs.dtu.dk/services/SpeciesFinder/) [80]. 
As also previously mentioned, many Vibrionaceae species are not easily identified using 16S rRNA 
gene homology however, as their genomes often contain several copies of the gene and the simi-
larity between different species can be very high [81]. To solve this problem, Machado et al. devel-
oped a tool called FurIOS in 2017 (available at http://www.cbs.dtu.dk/services/furIOS-1.0/) [82]. 
==PAGE:26==
Side 17 af 74 
 
FurIOS is a tool for species identification of Vibrionaceae strains using the ferric uptake regulator 
(fur) gene. The ferric uptake regulator is responsible for maintaining iron homeostasis in many bac-
terial species, by blocking transcription of genes involved in iron acquisition when intracellular iron 
levels are high [83]. The fur gene is suitable as a phylogenetic marker gene in Vibrionaceae, as all 
Vibrionaceae carry the gene, it has been found to be present in only one copy in the genome , it has 
the same function in all organisms and it is able to differentiate different species [83]. Species iden-
tification based on single genes has the advantage, that the technique is more available even for 
smaller labs, as it is based on PCR and single gene sequencing.  
Sometimes, a single gene does not provide high enough discriminatory power however, and tech-
niques involving several genes are necessary to get good interspecies resolution [21]. An example 
of such a method is multi locus sequence analysis (MLSA). In MLSA, the sequences of a set of 
phylogenetic marker genes are compared to references and used to build a phylogeny. Which genes 
to compare and what thresholds to use varies depending on the genus [76]. Sawabe et al. (2007) 
developed an MLSA scheme for the family Vibrionaceae using nine gene sequences (ftsZ, gapA, 
gyrB, mreB, pyrH, recA, rpoA, topA and the16S rRNA gene), but after further testing it was discov-
ered that it is not critical to include the 16S rRNA gene [22]. An example of a tool for MLSA analysis 
is autoMLST (available at https://automlst.ziemertlab.com/) that creates a tree from MLSA calcula-
tions.  
To take it even further than comparing a handful of genes, the so called Overall Genome Related-
ness Indices (OGRI) have been developed to compare whole genomes for species circumscription 
including digital DNA-DNA hybridisation (dDDH) and average nucleotide identity (ANI) [76].  
DDH is a laboratory technique that was for a long time acknowledged as the gold standard for spe-
cies delineation. DNA from two closely related organisms is fragmented and DNA from one of the 
organisms is labelled. The single-stranded labelled DNA from one organisms is combined with an 
excess of the single-stranded DNA from the other organism, and the amount of labelled DNA incor-
porated into double-strands is compared to when the labelled DNA is combined with DNA from the 
same organism. An incorporation of more than 70% indicates that the two organisms belong to the 
same species [78]. As this wet-lab technique is difficult to carry out, an in silico counterpart dDDH 
was developed, to calculate genome-to-genome distances based on whole genome sequences [84]. 
An example of a tool for dDDH is the Genome-to-Genome Distance Calculator (GGDC) from the 
DSMZ Leibniz Institute (available at http://ggdc.dsmz.de) [85]. 
ANI compares the shared genes between two genomes and has been shown to correlate well with 
DDH. ANI can be calculated by two different algorithms through the JSpecies software package e.g. 
at the JSpecies Web Server (JSpeciesWS) (available at http://jspecies.ribohost.com/jspeciesws/) 
[76]. The first algorithm is called ANIb and is calculated by BLAST searches of 1 kb genome frag-
ments against the target genome [76]. The second algorithm is called ANIm and is based on the 
MUMmer algorithm and is much faster [76]. ANIm values of 77%-93% (in 94% of cases) corresponds 
to intra-genus but inter-species measures, and values >93% correspond (in 95% of cases) to intra-
species values [76]. The interpretation of values between 93%-96% can be a bit uncertain, as both 
intra-species and inter-species examples exist [76]. For strains with high identity (80% to 100%), the 
two algorithms provide similar results, but they differ when used for distantly related genomes [76].  
In addition to ANI calculations, the JSpeciesWS offers tetranucleotide signature regression (TETRA). 
TETRA is a very fast way of comparing genomes without aligning them, and it is therefore suitable 
==PAGE:27==
Side 18 af 74 
 
for screening large sets of genomes [76]. Regression values above 0.999 has been shown to corre-
spond to ANI values >94%. 
All these tools can be used for identification purposes or to investigate relationships between differ-
ent strains. Bioinformatic analyses of genomic data can provide great insight but should in some 
cases be used together with wet-lab analyses. For example, when describing novel species, it is 
very useful to have the entire genomic sequence, but it is also of utmost importance to provide rele-
vant data on morphology, physiology, biochemistry etc. 
 
2.6 Genome mining 
Another area of microbiological research that has benefited from the development of gene sequenc-
ing is natural product discovery. Today, the search for novel natural products usually includes ge-
nome mining. Genome mining is based on the idea, that the genetic material of a microorganism 
can reveal the potential for production of interesting compounds such as natural products, enzymes 
etc. [9]. Genome mining of bacterial genomes is used as a way of screening strains before doing 
time consuming laboratory experiments to investigate the potential. To search for natural products 
in the laboratory typically includes culturing large number of strains in a limited number of conditions 
and hoping that something of interest can be detected, which is both expensive and relatively inef-
fective [86]. Furthermore, these experiments often lead to re-isolation of already known compounds 
[87]. Traditional methods for product discovery is also hindered by the fact that most bacterial spe-
cies cannot be cultivated in the laboratory currently, and so it is necessary to learn how to cultivate 
new organisms before their biosynthetic potential can be assessed using laboratory based tech-
niques. With mining of genome sequences, promising strains can be prioritised before extensive lab-
work is commenced [86]. Genome mining can be used for dereplication (to figure out the production 
of already known compounds) or to predict unknown compounds. A bottleneck occurs when it comes 
to finding actual natural products, after a pathway has been predicted [86]. When a promising strain 
has been identified, it is often necessary to perform detailed fermentation studies and chemical anal-
yses in order to get the strain to produce the compound at all and especially in detectable amounts 
[86]. This means, that the laborious investigation in the laboratory is not completely avoided, but the 
number of strains to include can be cut down significantly, or the experiments can be tailored to 
specific compound types or organisms.  
Genome mining is dependent on high-quality, contiguous genome assemblies. Genome mining of 
Illumina assemblies is challenged by the facts, that natural product biosynthetic gene clusters (BGC) 
often contain repetitive sequences and are often longer than the read length, which makes it difficult 
to assemble BGCs using short reads [86]. This means that long read sequencing such as Nanopore 
sequencing is better if the purpose of sequencing is genome mining. BGCs are groups of genes that 
are located in close proximity to each other on a chromosome and are involved in the same biosyn-
thetic pathway for production of natural products [87]. 
Natural products are also called secondary metabolites [88]. Microbial secondary metabolites are 
organic compounds that are not directly involved in growth and proliferation of the producer organ-
ism. Instead secondary metabolites play important roles in e.g. defence or communication [89]. Nat-
ural products are of great interest, since they include toxins, pigments, and antibiotics, among others. 
Natural products have a range of biological activities, including antibacterial, antifungal, antiviral and 
antitumor activities [89]. The diversity and complexity of microbial natural products are very high, but 
the principles of the biosynthesis are often quite similar, which makes it possible to predict BGCs

[88].The biosynthesis of natural products consists of three stages. The first stage is to acquire the 
building blocks for the product. The building blocks can either be the same as for some primary 
metabolite production, such as malonyl-CoA or proteinogenic amino acids, or they can be specific 
for the secondary metabolite. Specific building blocks will usually be produced by enzymes encoded 
for in the same BGC. Next, the building blocks are used to make precursor molecules. Lastly, the 
precursor molecules can be modified by e.g. methyltransferases [88]. 
For the purpose of using natural products as medicine, non-ribosomal peptides (NRP) and polyke-
tides (PK) are considered especially valuable classes of microbial secondary metabolites [87]. NRPs 
and PKs are produced by non-ribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPS) and polyketide synthases 
(PKS). Both NRPS and PKS are modular enzymes that are responsible for assembling precursor 
molecules from building blocks. Modular enzymes often have repetitive motifs, which means the 
genes encoding the enzymes have repetitive sequences, which is why they are difficult to assemble, 
but also the reason why they have recognisable sequences [9]. NRPS consist of an adenylation (A) 
domain, a thiolation (T) domain, a condensation (C) domain and a thioesterase (TE) domain or re-
ductase (RE) domain. The A and T domains binds and activates amino acids and other carboxylic 
acids. The C domain links the amino acids together. The TE domain cleaves the amino acid chain 
produced so that either a linear or cyclised molecule is released. Sometimes the cleaving is per-
formed by an RE domain instead [90]. A PKS is composed of an acyltransferase (AT) domain, a 
thiolation (T) domain and a ketosynthase (KS) domain. The AT domain binds and activates extender 
units such as acyl-CoA. The T domain links to the activated acyl chain of the extender unit. The KS 
domain decarboxylate and condense the extender units [90]. As mentioned, both NRPS and PKS 
consists of several modules consisting of these domains. Specific amino acid motifs on the mono-
mers used as building blocks by NRPS and PKS determine which A or AT domain will bind and 
activate [90]. In addition to NRPS and PKS, there also exists NRPS-PKS hybrids [90]. 
An example of a tool used for genome mining for natural products is called antiSMASH, the ‘antibiotic 
and secondary metabolite analysis shell’. AntiSMASH offers a pipeline for automated genome min-
ing of all major classes of antibiotics and is freely available at https://antismash.secondarymetabo-
lites.org/ [88], [91]–[96]. AntiSMASH combines several algorithms and tools and can detect both 
known and unknown BGCs and BGC-like regions [87], [88]. AntiSMASH finds putative genes with 
Prodigal and then searches these identified genes and gene clusters against different databases 
[96]. KnownClusterBlast analysis searches identified clusters against the MIBiG database, that con-
tains hand curated gene clusters which have been experimentally characterised [96]. ClusterBlast 
analysis searches identified clusters against a large database of clusters to identify similar clusters 
[96]. Subcluster Blast analysis searches identified clusters against a database containing operons 
involved in the biosynthesis of common secondary metabolite building blocks [96]. smCOG analysis 
compares each gene of identified clusters to a database of orthologous groups of proteins involved 
in secondary metabolism, to annotate the putative function of the gene products [96]. Active site 
finder detects active sites of highly conserved biosynthetic enzymes [96].  
Using a tool like antiSMASH it is possible for wet-lab microbiologists to utilise advanced bioinformatic 
pipelines to guide their experimental work, for more efficient and fruitful research activities.

2.7 Aim of the study 
The research objectives of this thesis have been based on the knowledge described in the previous 
sections. The overall aim of the study is to investigate phylogenetic relationships and genomic po-
tential for secondary metabolite production of Vibrionaceae strains from the Galathea collection 
based on Nanopore whole genome sequencing. Originally, the plan was to include 60 strains in the 
analyses to be able to detect patterns across a larger selection. Due to the restricted access to 
laboratories during the COVID-19 pandemic, only 20 strains were sequenced. 
More specifically, this thesis will investigate the following hypotheses: 
1) It is hypothesised, that nanopore sequencing with ONT MinION can be used to produce as-
sembled genomes from the Vibrionaceae strains of the Galathea collection of high enough 
quality to perform taxonomic and phylogenetic analyses, as this has been successfully used 
in Vibrionaceae strains before. 
 
2) It is hypothesised that the strains of the Galathea collection will show taxonomic and phylo-
genetic diversity, as they have been sampled from across the globe, from different conditions 
and using different sampling methods. 
 
3) It is hypothesised, that the Vibrionaceae strains of the Galathea collection will show great 
genomic potential for secondary metabolite production, as they have been selected based 
on antibiotic activity, similar strains from the collection has done so previously and because 
the genomes will be assembled based on Nanopore reads, which are better at resolving 
biosynthetic gene clusters than shorter reads. 
 
These hypotheses will be tested by 
• 
Investigating how ONT MinION sequencing and online tools for sequence analysis can be 
used for bacterial whole genome analysis without extensive bioinformatics skills.  
 
• 
Investigating how specific marker genes (16S rRNA gene, fur gene) and OGRI can be used 
for species identification and phylogenetic analysis of the sequenced Vibrionaceae strains. 
 
• 
Investigating phylogenetic relationships between the sequenced Vibrionaceae strains and 
where possible assign a species name. 
 
• 
Investigating the genomic potential of the sequenced Vibrionaceae strains.

3.2 
DNA extraction 
Genomic DNA extraction was performed with the Promega Wizard® Genomic Purification Kit (cata-
logue no. A1120, Denmark) based on a slightly modified version of the protocol for isolating genomic 
DNA from Gram-negative bacteria in the technical manual from the kit [97]. Two mL of liquid cultures 
grown to the late exponential phase or early stationary phase in MB (200 rpm shaking at 25°C for 
14-24 hours) were collected and used for genomic DNA extraction. The pelleted cells were treated 
with the Nuclei Lysis Solution and incubated at 80°C for 15 minutes. After cooling to room tempera-
ture, the cell lysate was treated with the RNaseA Solution and incubated at 37°C for 60 minutes. 
Three strains (S0568-1, S0787 and S0788) were treated with RNaseA from another kit of the same 
concentration (Sigma-Aldrich, R4642-10MG, Denmark). After cooling to room temperature, the cell 
lysate was mixed with the Protein Precipitation Solution by vortexing thoroughly for 30 seconds and 
then incubating on ice for 30 minutes. The insoluble components were spun down and the superna-
tant containing the DNA was transferred with a pipette to room temperature isopropanol. Visible DNA 
aggregates formed in the isopropanol was transferred with a glass needle to room temperature 70% 
ethanol. The DNA was spun down, the ethanol was removed, and the pellet was left to dry completely 
before being resuspended in DNA Hydration Solution O/N at 5°C. The amount of all reagents used 
corresponds to the original protocol. 
Quality of extracted genomic DNA was assessed by gel electrophoresis with GelRed® Nucleic Acid 
Gel Stain (5x) (Biotium, 41003) and with DS-11 spectrophotometer/fluorometer (DeNovix, US). 
Amount of extracted genomic DNA was quantified using a Qubit 2.0 analyser (Invitrogen, United 
Kingdom) with the Qubit dsDNA BR kit (catalogue no. Q32853, Invitrogen, United Kingdom). 
If the quality of extracted gDNA was too low, an additional purification step was included [98]. Sev-
enty μL gDNA was mixed with 7 μL 3M NaAc and 222 μL 99.7% ethanol, until DNA was visible. The 
DNA was transferred to a new Eppendorf tube with 1 mL 70% ethanol. After centrifugation, the su-
pernatant was discarded, and the DNA was dried at 45°C for 20 minutes. The dried DNA was resus-
pended in 30 μL elution buffer from the Promega Wizard® Genomic Purification Kit (catalogue no. 
A1120, Denmark). 
3.3 
PCR amplifications of 16S rRNA and hlmE genes 
As a further quality check of the extracted gDNA and to confirm that the cultivated species are be-
longing to the Vibrionaceae family, the 16S rRNA gene was amplified from a random selection of 13 
strains. The universal primers 27f (5’-AGAGTTTGATCCTGGCTCAG-3’) and 1429r (5’-GGTTAC-
CTTGTTACGACTT-3’) were synthesised by Macrogen Europe (Amsterdam, the Netherlands) in the 
concentration of 5 μM and were used for the amplification of 16S rRNA genes [99].  
To search for molecular signatures in the strain collection, a PCR amplification of the hlmE gene of 
the holomycin biosynthetic gene cluster was done on a selection of strains of interest. Primers de-
signed for the hlmE gene of P. galatheae S2753 were used (hlmE fw: 5’-GGAATTCCATATGAACC-
CTGATCACGTTGGCAC-3’ and hlmE rv: 5’-GCTCTAGATCAATGGTGGTGGTGATGATGCAA-
GCTG-ACTCCGTCCGGTTG-3’). The primers were designed to produce a 3.7 kb product.  
The TEMPase Hot Start 2x Master Mix A Blue (cat. no. 5200600, Ampliqon, Denmark) was used in 
all PCR reactions in this study. The reactions furthermore included 5 μM or 10 μM of both forward 
and reverse primer respectively for the 16S rRNA gene and hlmE gene amplification, template DNA, 
and distilled water up to the desired volume. The T100 Thermal Cycler (BioRad, cat. no. 1861096) 
was used for PCR amplification.

3.4

experiments were performed. The first sequencing experiments included the strains S0562-1, 
S0568-1, S0693, S0788, S1072, S1073-1, S1100, S2606, S2155-1 and S3301-1. The second se-
quencing experiment included the strains S0787, S2043, S2056, S2226, S2394, S2537, S2668-2, 
S3728-1, S3789 and S4051.

3.4.2 Flow cell preparation 
Before sequencing, a flow cell was prepared. The number of active pores in the flow cell was checked 
according to the Flow Cell Check protocol [100]. The minimum number of active pores covered by 
the warranty is 800 for MinION flow cells. The flow cell was inserted into the MinION device that was 
connected to a computer with the MinKNOW software. Successful connection of the device was 
confirmed by seeing a light and hearing a fan in the device. In the MinKNOW software, the “Check 
Flow Cell” workflow was chosen, and it ran for approximately 30-45 minutes. When finished, the 
software showed the number of active pores and indicated the health of the flow cell. 
After the flow cell check, the flow cell was primed with the Flow Cell Priming Kit (EXP-FLP002, Oxford 
Nanopore Technologies, United Kingdom). The reagents were thawed on ice. A small amount (ap-
proximately 20-30 μL) of the buffer in the flow cell were removed by inserting the tip of a P1000 
pipette into the priming port and turning the wheel up. The flow cell priming mix were prepared by 
adding 30 μL Flush Tether to a tube of Flush Buffer and vortexing. Of the prepared priming mix, 800 
μL was loaded into the priming port without introducing air bubbles. After five minutes, the SpotON 
sample port cover was lifted and further 200 μL priming mix were added to the priming port. 
3.4.3 Loading DNA sample 
The library was prepared for loading by combining 11 μL DNA library, 34 μL Sequencing Buffer, 25.5 
μL Loading Beads and 4.5 μL nuclease-free water (75 μL in total) and mixing gently by pipetting. 
The 75 μL sample were added to the flow cell via the SpotON sample port in a dropwise fashion. In 
the second sequencing run, the loading of the sample was not completely successful. The SpotON 
sample port was blocked by a combination of small bubbles and settling loading beads, preventing 
the sample drops from entering the flow cell. The blockage was attempted removed, and the sample 
was eventually loaded into the flow cell, but the flow cell was damaged in the process. The flow cell 
had 1467 active pores before this and 1151 after this. During sequencing, this number decreased 
steadily and by the end of the experiment, no pores were active. 
3.4.4 Sequencing 
To start the sequencing the MinKNOW was used. The appropriate flow cell and kit were selected, 
and the run length was set to 48 hours. Otherwise, standard settings were used. 
3.4.5 Flow cell washing 
After sequencing, the flow cell can be washed and reused or returned to Oxford Nanopore Technol-
ogies for recycling. The flow cells were washed using the Flow Cell Was kit and protocol (Exp-
WSH003, Oxford Nanopore Technologies, United Kingdom) [101]. The Wash Solution A (DNase I) 
was placed on ice while Wash Solution B (exonuclease buffer) was thawed at room temperature 
before being thoroughly vortexed and placed on ice. 20 μL Wash Solution A and 380 μL Wash So-
lution B were mixed and placed on ice. From the Waste Port 1 on the flow cell all fluids were removed 
while the Priming Port and SpotON sample port were closed to prevent air from being drawn across 
the sensor array area. The priming port was opened, a small volume of liquid was removed as de-
scribed previously. Afterwards, the 400 μL Wash Mix was loaded into the flow cell via the priming

port, the port was closed, and the flow cell was left for 30 minutes at room temperature. With both 
ports closed, the waste channel was again emptied through waste port 1. Afterwards, a tube of 
Storage Buffers was thawed at room temperature and 500 μL was added through the priming port. 
With the priming port closed, the waste channel was once again emptied via Waste Port 1, and the 
flow cell was stored at 5°C for further use or returning. 
3.5 
Sequence data analysis  
3.5.1 Whole genome sequence data quality control and assembly 
The sequencing data generated during the ONT sequencing was collected using the MinKNOW 
software which also provided a range of sequencing statistics. The sequencing summary text files 
generated by MinKNOW were submitted to NanoPlot on Galaxy Version 1.28.1 [70], [72] to investi-
gate the sequencing statistics.  
After MinKNOW finished base calling, the reads were demultiplexed based on the barcodes using 
the FASTQ Barcoding workflow version 3.10.4 in the EPI2ME (Metrichor Ltd) software. The demul-
tiplexing also included a quality control step of filtering out all reads with a quality score <7. 
After demultiplexing, the reads from each strain was assembled using Unicycler on Galaxy Version 
0.4.8.0 [70], [102]. The reads were submitted in FastQ format. For most strains, only the nanopore 
reads produced in this thesis was used for assembly. For those, the option “Paired or Single end 
data?” was set to “None”. Contigs shorter than 100 bp was excluded. Other settings were as default. 
The three strains S0787, S2043 and S2394 have previously been Illumina sequenced by this group. 
These reads in FastQ format was included in the assemblies of these strains, to produce hybrid 
assemblies. Before assembly, the Illumina reads were quality controlled using FastQC on Galaxy 
Version 0.72+galaxy1 [70], [103] and trimmed using Cutadapt on Galaxy Version 1.16.5 [70], [104]. 
The minimum length and quality cut-off were both set to 20, and other settings were as default. 
For the hybrid assemblies, the Unicycler setting “Paired or Single end data?” was set to “paired”, 
and the two set of Illumina reads were submitted as forward and reverse reads respectively, and 
nanopore reads were submitted as long reads. Otherwise settings were as for nanopore-only as-
semblies. 
The quality of the assemblies was assessed with QUAST (Quality Assessment Tool) on Galaxy 
version 5.0.2 [70], [105]. The software was run without reference genomes and with no estimated 
reference genome. All other settings were as default.  
The Unicycler assemblies were annotated using Prokka on Galaxy Version 1.14.5. using default 
settings [70], [106]. 
To further assess the quality of the assembled genomes based on Nanopore reads compared to 
partial 16S rRNA gene Illumina sequencing, one copy of the 16S rRNA gene from each assembled 
genome was aligned by BLAST to the originally published 16S rRNA gene sequences from these 
strains by Gram et al. 2010 [12]. 
3.5.2 Genome based species identification and phylogeny 
3.5.2.1 
Investigation of 16S rRNA gene sequences 
16S rRNA gene based species identification was carried out by submitting the Unicycler assembled 
genomes to the online tool SpeciesFinder 2.0 [80]. 
==PAGE:39==
Side 30 af 74 
 
For investigation of the copy number and variation of the 16S rRNA genes of the strains, all copies 
of the gene were found using the annotation by Prokka visualised in JBrowse. The sequences for all 
copies of the gene in each strain were downloaded in fasta-format. A BLAST search of all the copies 
of the 16S rRNA gene from each strain was run against all sequences in the NCBI standard nucle-
otide collection (nr/nt) database and against only type material (other settings as default), to investi-
gate whether the variation in each strain lead to different species ID of different copies of the 16S 
rRNA gene. To further investigate the variance between different copies of the 16S rRNA gene from 
the same strain, all copies from each strain were aligned using ClustalW in BioEdit 7.2 and the 
alignments were visually inspected. To visualise the variation, an example of a strain with low varia-
tion (S1072) and a strain with more variation (S3301-1) was used. The copies of the 16S rRNA gene 
from each strain and the 16S rRNA gene sequence of all Vibrionaceae type strains downloaded from 
EZBioCloud [107] was aligned in CLC Genomics Workbench 20.0.4 and a maximum-likelihood tree 
was generated with CLC Genomics Workbench 20.0.4 using the neighbour joining construction 
method, the Jukes Cantor nucleotide substitution model and bootstrap values based on 1000 repli-
cates. The 16S rRNA gene from E. coli was used as an outlier to root the trees.  
For phylogenetic comparisons, a representative 16S rRNA gene copy from each strain in this study 
and the 16S rRNA gene sequence from the type strains were aligned as described above. 
3.5.2.2 
Investigation of the ferric uptake regulator gene 
Ferric uptake regulator protein (fur) genes were identified in the annotations by Prokka and the nu-
cleotide sequence were extracted from the genomes in JBrowse. 
For three strains, S1072, S1073-1 and S3301-1, two fur genes were found, but they were located 
right next to each other with a small overlap and in total the length of the two genes were the ex-
pected length of a fur gene. Therefore, the sequences were merged to obtain a full fur gene. 
For species identification, the fur genes in FASTA format were submitted to http://www.cbs.dtu.dk/ 
services/furIOS-1.0/ [83]. 
Type strain fur genes were found by doing a BLAST search of all the fur genes found in this study 
against only type strains, and then doing a new BLAST search of the results from this search against 
only type strains. Furthermore, fur genes from additional strains were found using the search string 
“Vibrionaceae fur gene” on the NCBI webpage and doing yet another BLAST search against type 
strains of the results from this. This resulted in both specific entries for fur genes, entries for smaller 
contigs including the fur gene and entire chromosomes including the fur gene. To obtain the actual 
fur gene sequence in all cases, the contigs/chromosomes were annotated using Prokka on Galaxy 
as described previously. The fur gene is not sequenced to the same extent as the 16S rRNA gene, 
so it was not possible to include as many fur genes as 16S rRNA genes. 
For phylogenetic comparisons, the fur genes from type strains and the strains in this study was 
aligned using CLC Genomics Workbench 20.0.4 and a maximum-likelihood tree was produced as 
described previously. 
3.5.2.3 
Whole genome based species identification 
For species identification, assembled genomes in fasta format were submitted to the online tool 
KmerFinder 3.2 (https://cge.cbs.dtu.dk/services/KmerFinder/) [80], [108], [109], and the database for 
bacterial organisms were chosen. KmerFinder divides the sequence into k-mers (where k is a 
==PAGE:40==
Side 31 af 74 
 
number, in this case 16-mers) and searches these k-mers against databases of known genome 
sequences to find the species with the highest number of k-mer matches. 
For species identification, assembled genomes in fasta format were also submitted to the JSpe-
ciesWS (http://jspecies.ribohost.com/jspeciesws/) [110]. A tetra correlation search (TCS) was con-
ducted, where the query genomes are searched for highest correlation against the genomes in the 
reference database GenomesDB. 
For ANI calculations, genome sequences of relevant type strains was downloaded from the EZBio-
cloud database [107]. A genome sequence was not available for all relevant type strains. The JSpe-
ciesWS was used for ANIb calculation, and the genomes of strains in this study was compared to 
genomes of other strains from the study and genomes of type strains, that was closely related based 
on the other analyses. 
3.5.3 Genome mining 
The assembled genomes were mined for secondary metabolite production using antiSMASH 5.1.2 
[95]. The genomes were submitted in Fasta format as they were outputted by Unicycler. Detection 
strictness was set to relaxed and all features were on (KnownClusterBlast, ClusterBlast, SubClus-
terBlast, ActiveSiteFinder, Cluster Pfam analysis and Pfam-based GO term annotation).

4. Results 
4.1 
Culture and DNA extraction 
The strains were selected to include a wide variety of species with a high potential of producing 
different natural products by selecting based on different sample sites, sample types or preliminary 
species ID or on high inhibitory activity in the original screening. Some strains were included based 
on results from other work of this group. S2753 have been whole genome sequenced [111] and was 
included as a sequencing control. S2043, S2054, S2055 and S2056 were sampled from the same 
site as the andrimid producer S2052 and S2754 from the same site as the holomycin producer 
S2753. These strains were included to investigate the correlation between bioactivity and the sam-
pling site or the occurrence of horizontal gene transfer. S2226, S2541-2, S2545, S3362, S3704, 
S3856, S3901-1 and S4640-1 were preliminary identified as belonging to the genus Photobacterium. 
As P. galatheae S2753 produce bioactive compounds [13], it was interesting to investigate other 
potential Photobacterium spp. in this collection. Only some of the strains ended up in the final anal-
yses however, as the access to university laboratories was restricted due to COVID-19 before all 
experiments had been conducted. It was random which strains were sequenced, as this was not 
planned.  
After incubation at 25°C for 3-5 days, the colony morphology on MA was observed (Table 3). Most 
strains were pale cream/colourless, while a few were dark blue/grey (Figure 12). Some of the cream 
coloured colonies turned slightly red after storage at 5°C for a week (Figure 12). The colony sizes 
varied greatly, but so did the density of colonies on the plates, perhaps accounting for some of the 
differences in colony size. No strains showed luminescence when observed in a dark room. From 
the plate cultures, liquid cultures were inoculated and incubated at 25°C for 14-18 and/or 20-24 
hours. Incubation for 14-18 hours proved more appropriate for gDNA extraction. For some cultures 
it was observed that it was impossible to separate proteins and DNA solution if incubated for longer 
than 18 hours, as a layer of a white, slime-like material formed on top of the liquid after attempted 
protein precipitation (Table 2 and Table 3). In other cultures the issue was not as pronounced, but 
the concentration of extracted gDNA was still higher when incubated for 14-18 hours as compared 
to 20-24-hour (Table 2 and Table 3). It was not investigated what caused this.

4.3 
Whole genome sequencing  
Twenty Vibrionaceae strains were whole genome sequenced using ONT MinION during two rounds 
of sequencing of ten strains. Before sequencing, all flow cells were checked with the MinKNOW 
software ‘check flow cell protocol’. All flow cells had 1300-1500 working pores, which is above the 
lower limit for returning at 800 active pores. The sequencing was controlled by the MinKNOW soft-
ware, which also performs base calling and outputs the sequenced reads in .fast5 and FASTQ format 
as well as a sequence summary report. 
The summary reports were submitted to NanoPlot on Galaxy Version 1.28.1 to investigate the se-
quencing statistics. In general, the statistics for sequence run one was much better than for se-
quence run two (Table 5). The reads generated during run one was longer and of better quality and 
almost six times as many reads were generated during run one (Table 5). 
==PAGE:49==
Side 40 af 74 
 
After base calling with MinKNOW, the reads were demultiplexed, as ten barcoded strains were se-
quenced on each flow cell. For this, the tool FASTQ Barcoding in EPI2ME (Metrichor Ltd.) was used. 
The reads were filtered to only include reads with a quality score above seven, sorted according to 
the barcodes and the barcodes and adapters were removed. For the strains sequenced in sequenc-
ing run one, this resulted in 74,179-219,978 reads per strain, whereas for the strains sequenced in 
the second run, the number of reads were only 2,147-23,123 per strain (Table 6). These differences 
in numbers were also reflected in the total yield, which is 265.2-975.9 Mb for strains sequenced in 
run one, and 4.5-72.7 Mb for strains sequenced in run two. This means the coverage is up to 200-
fold higher for run one than run two (Table 6). All strains sequenced in run one has a coverage of 
>50, which is enough to construct high-quality genomes [52]. All strains sequenced in run two has a 
coverage of ≤15, which is not enough to construct high-quality genomes necessitating extra care to 
be taken when assessing the results from further analysis of these genomes.

4.4.1 16S rRNA gene based phylogeny 
For phylogenetic comparisons, a representative 16S rRNA gene copy (copy 1 in all cases, see ap-
pendix 8 for a full description of the different copies) from each strain in this study and the 16S rRNA 
gene sequence from type strains were aligned in CLC Genomics Workbench 20.0.4 and a maximum-
likelihood tree were generated with CLC Genomics Workbench 20.0.4 using the neighbour joining 
construction method, the Jukes Cantor nucleotide substitution model and bootstrap values based on 
1000 replicates. The 16S rRNA gene from E. coli was used as an outlier to root the tree. The strains 
of this study are widely distributed across the 16S rRNA gene based tree, with some clustering two-
three strains together (Figure 17 and Table 8). One strain cluster together with Photobacterium spp. 
and the rest clusters together with Vibrio spp. No strains cluster together with other genera of the 
Vibrionaceae family. Three strains (S0788, S2043 and S2056) do not cluster together with one spe-
cific species but groups into a small branch of a larger cluster with V. tubiashii, V. coralliilyticus, V. 
neptunius and S2394. The assembled genome of S0788 is based on good quality reads, whereas 
S2056 and S2043 is based on low quality reads and a combination of low quality Nanopore reads 
and good quality Illumina reads, respectively. Some clusters have low bootstrap values, and not all 
are to be trusted (Table 8).

4.4.2 Fur gene based phylogeny 
The fur gene has been suggested as a phylogenetic marker for species identification of Vibrionaceae 
strains [83], and so the fur genes were extracted from the whole genome sequences of 12 strains. 
In nine strains, a single copy of the fur gene was found by annotation with Prokka (Table 9). The 
size of the fur gene in most of these strains was approximately 450 bp, but in two strains, it was 
shorter and was therefore suspected to be only a partial fur gene. In three strains two copies of the 
fur gene were detected by Prokka. Upon further inspection, each strain was found to likely carry only 
one copy of the fur gene, as the genes were located next to each other with a small overlap, and the 
total size of the two genes in each strain was approximately 450 bp. In the genomes from six strains, 
no fur gene was detected by Prokka. One of those genomes was based on good quality Nanopore 
reads, and a fur gene was found when aligning the genome sequence and a previous fur PCR am-
plicon sequence published by Machado et al. 2017 (accession KU756375) [82] using BLAST. The 
five other genomes in which no fur gene was found by Prokka were based on bad quality Nanopore 
reads, and so the lack of a fur gene might be due to the assemblies being incomplete.

The fur genes found in the strains of this study was included in a phylogenetic tree with fur genes 
from Vibrionaceae type strains (Figure 18). The fur genes from type strains were found by doing 
BLAST searches of fur genes from this study against type strains in the NCBI nucleotide database, 
and then again doing a BLAST search of the fur genes found by this. The fur gene is not sequenced 
and published at the same extent as the 16S rRNA gene, and therefore the fur gene based phylo-
genetic tree has lower resolution than the 16S rRNA gene based phylogenetic tree. 
The pattern that the strains of this study created in the fur gene based phylogenetic tree and the 16S 
rRNA gene based phylogenetic tree were relatively similar, but with some differences. The strains 
S1072 and S1073-1 clustered together in both trees with V. nigripulchritudo. S0562-1 and S0568-1 
clustered together in both trees. In the 16S rRNA gene based tree they clustered next to V. chagasii 
and shared a branch with V. splendidus. V. chagasii was not included in the fur tree, but V. splen-
didus was located in the same larger cluster as S0562-1 and S0568-1 together with a few other 
strains. S3301-1 clustered together with V. splendidus in both trees. S0693 clustered with V. cycli-
trophicus in 16S rRNA gene based tree, but this was not included in the fur gene tree. The closest 
neighbours in the fur tree were S0562, S0568-1, S3301-1, and V. splendidus. In the 16S rRNA gene 
based tree these strains and species all shared the same main branch as S0693, so the differences 
could be due to lower resolution in the fur tree. S1100 clustered with V. crassostreae in the 16S 
rRNA gene based tree, which is not included in the fur tree. In the fur tree the closest neighbours 
were the same as for S0693, and again they share the main branch on the 16S rRNA gene based 
tree, so the differences could be due to different resolutions. S0787 clustered together with V. med-
iterranei in the 16S rRNA gene based tree, but this species was not included in the fur tree. In the 
fur tree, S0787 did not cluster with a specific species. S0788, S2043 and S2394 clustered relatively 
close together in both threes and was located on the same main branch as V. coralliilyticus, V. tubi-
ashii and V. galatheae. In the fur tree however, V. cidicii and V. vulnificus was located closer to the 
cluster than V. tubiashii and V. galatheae was, whereas in the 16S rRNA gene based tree, V. cidicii 
and V. vulnificus was on a different main branch. It should be noted that the fur gene of S0788 was 
not complete (360 bp long). S2056 was included in this cluster in the 16S rRNA gene based tree but 
was not included in the fur tree as no fur gene was found. S2606 was the strain that exhibited the 
biggest difference across the two trees. It clustered with V. galatheae in the 16S rRNA gene based 
tree in a larger cluster with V. brasiliensis, V. sinaloensis and V. caribbeanicus. V. galatheae was 
also included in the fur tree, but the other species were not. In the fur tree, S2606 did not cluster with 
one specific species, but was next to a cluster consisting of Candidatus V. antiquarius, V. alginolyti-
cus and V. harveyi. In the fur tree V. galatheae was in a completely different cluster with V. tubiashii. 
All these species are located on the same larger branch in the 16S rRNA gene based tree, however. 
These differences might be due to the fact, that the fur gene of S2606 was not complete (216 bp 
long). 
It was difficult to obtain certain results from this fur gene based phylogenetic tree due to the low 
resolution, but the general picture was consistent with that from the 16S rRNA gene based phyloge-
netic tree.

4.4.3 In-strain variation of 16S rRNA genes 
As comparison of 16S rRNA genes is a key part of bacterial species identification, they were included 
in this analysis, even though previous studies show, that the 16S rRNA gene alone cannot be used 
for a conclusive species identification of Vibrionaceae strains. 
The 16S rRNA genes were extracted from the whole genome sequences of 17 strains based on 
annotation by Prokka. In assemblies based on low quality Nanopore reads (coverage <30), 1-3 cop-
ies of the 16S rRNA gene were found (Table 10). In hybrid assemblies based on both low quality 
Nanopore reads and Illumina reads 1-6 copies of the 16S rRNA gene were found. In assemblies 
based on good quality Nanopore reads, 9-15 copies of the 16S rRNA gene were found. In the as-
sembled genome of the strain S4051, the annotation by Prokka did not find any 16S rRNA genes. 
However, this genome is based on low quality Nanopore reads and the annotation by Prokka only 
found two genes in total, indicating issues with the sequence. 
To further assess the quality of the assembled genomes based on Nanopore reads compared to 
partial 16S rRNA gene Illumina sequencing, one copy of the 16S rRNA gene from each assembled 
genome was aligned by BLAST to the originally published 16S rRNA genes from these strains by 
Gram et al. 2010 [12]. For most of the strains, the alignment resulted in >99% identity (appendix 4). 
For three of the strains with genomes based on good quality reads, the identity was <97%, which is 
the threshold for species identification. For the strain S1072 the identity is 96.84%, which is close 
the species threshold. Perhaps this small difference can be due to the difference in sequence 
lengths. For the strain S1073-1 the identity is only 92.4% and for the strain S2155-1 all copies of the 
16S rRNA gene was aligned to the originally published sequence, as the first copy only showed 
80.9% identity. The identity varies from 80.9-94.4% for the different copies. These two strains sus-
piciously low values, but overall, this confirms the accuracy of the genomes based on Nanopore 
reads, as the sequence of the 16S rRNA gene in most cases were very similar to the Illumina am-
plicon sequence of the gene. 
As Vibrionaceae strains have previously been shown to contain several copies of 16S rRNA genes 
with high variance, the variance of the different copies in each strain was assessed. First, a BLAST 
search of all copies of the 16S rRNA genes against type strains in the NCBI nucleotide database 
was done. For most strains, the top hit was the same for all copies of the gene, but for S0693 and 
S3301-1 different copies matched with different type strains (Table 10 shows the number of different 
hits for each strain, more details on this can be found in appendix 8).  
To further asses the variance, the 16S rRNA genes from each strain was aligned with ClustalW in 
BioEdit 7.2 and the alignments were inspected visually to investigate the variance within each strain. 
For most of the strains, the different copies aligned well and were very similar. An example of this is 
S1072 (Figure 18). For a few strains there were quite a lot of variance between different copies 
however and different copies of the gene clustered with different type strains (S3301-1 as an exam-
ple in Figure 18).

4.4.4 Gene sequence based on-line tools for identifying bacterial species 
For species identification, a range of different bioinformatic tools were used. When the strains in this 
study were sampled, preliminary species identification based on partial 16S rRNA gene amplicon 
sequences was done by Gram et al. 2010 [12]. The results from this will be considered in addition to 
the analyses performed in this study.  
In this study, species identification based on 16S rRNA gene sequences was also done, using the 
online tool SpeciesFinder 2.0 and BLAST search. This identified most of the strains as Vibrio spp. 
(Table 11). For eight strains, SpeciesFinder did not find a close enough match for confidence of the 
result to pass. For one strain (S4051), the run failed. 
Species identification was also performed based on the fur gene using the online tool FurIOS. This 
also identified most of the strains as Vibrio spp. (Table 11 and Appendix 6). Five strains matched 
with one species with an identity value of ≥95%. Three strains matched with more than one species 
with an identity value of ≥95%. Four strains did not have any matches with an identity value of ≥95%. 
Two of those with very low values, were the two strains with only partial fur gene sequences as the 
identity value was also dependent on the length of the match. One of them was S2155-1, with the 
highest match being 93%. The fur gene of S2155-1 could not be found by Prokka and was instead 
found manually using BLAST search of a known fur gene, so it is possible that the sequence contains 
a small number of errors. The last one was S2394, whose genome was based on Nanopore and 
Illumina reads, which could point to it being a novel species. For five strains, no fur gene was found, 
meaning these could not be included in this analysis. 
The assembled genomes were submitted to KmerFinder 3.2 that performs species identification 
based on comparison of the entire genomes in 16 bp fragments. This identified most of the strains 
as Vibrio spp., and one tentatively as Photobacterium sp. (Table 11). For three of the strains with 
assembled genomes based on low quality reads, there were no results.  
The assembled genomes were also submitted to the JSpeciesWS and the tool TCS was used for 
species identification. TCS compares tetra-nucleotide signatures of the query genome with that of 
known genomes. Only type strain results have been included here, whereas the above-mentioned 
tools were not limited to type strains. TCS also identified most of the strains as Vibrio spp., and one 
tentatively as Photobacterium sp. (Table 11). S0787 and S2043 and S2394 were identified as be-
longing to the species V. coralliilyticus, S1072 and S1073-1 were identified as belonging to the spe-
cies V. nigripulchritudo For S0787, the z-score of one or more species-matches was >0.999, which 
is considered good, rendering the results inconclusive. For six strains the z-score of one or more 
species-matches was >0.989, which is considered acceptable by the tool and with no matches 
>0.999, meaning these results were also inconclusive. For six strains, there were only matches with 
a z-score <0.989, which is too low for species identification. The genomes of two of the strains that 
could not be identified (S2155-1 and S2606) were assembled with good quality Nanopore reads, 
meaning that the results could indicate the strains belong to novel species. The genomes of the other 
strains, that could not be identified, were assembled using low quality Nanopore reads, and the lack 
of matches can be caused by this. The genome of one strain was not possible to submit, because 
the sequence was shorter than the requirement of the tool.

The previously mentioned tools for species identification did in most cases provide inconclusive re-
sults, as several strains were either identified as different species by different tools, or one tool pre-
dicted several species IDs for a strain with equally high probability. To assess how closely related 
the strains of this study is to the type strains of the species different tools identified them as belonging 
to, ANI comparisons were calculated using the ANIb algorithm on the JSpeciesWS. Genome se-
quences of type strains were downloaded from EZBiocloud (accession numbers in appendix 7). Un-
less otherwise stated, the percent alignment listed was from comparing the type strain to the strain 
from this study. For most of the strains the percent alignment was approximately the same when 
comparing the strain from this study to the type strain. However, for the strains with incomplete 
assembled genomes, the comparison of the strain from this study to a type strain could give a false 
positive result in that the entire genome of a strain from this study could be aligned to the genome 
of a type strain, but only cover less than 20% of the genome of the type strain. The genome of each 
strain in this study was compared to the genomes of other strains from this study, if they clustered 
closely together in the phylogenetic trees or shared the same results from different tools, as well as 
to genomes from type strains of species they were predicted to belong to. Two strains are considered 
belonging to the same species if they share more than 95% ANI. 
S0562-1 and S0568-1 most likely belong to the same species, as they share 99.8% ANI based on 
98.3% alignment and they share identical results from all other analyses. It was not possible to de-
termine for certain, which species they belong to, as no tools showed output with values above their 
respective species threshold. The two strains are at least closely related to and perhaps belonging 
to the species V. chagasii, as both the TCS (0.996) and ANI (93% based on 77% alignment) values 
are within the range where both intra- and inter-species examples exist [76]. 
S0693 is most probably belonging to the species V. kanaloae, as they share 98% ANI based on 
92.21% alignment. The strain is closely related to V. toranzoniae too (ANI of 94.4%) and this is the 
top hit from the TCS analysis. The reason why V. toranzoniae and not V. kanaloae is the top hit in 
the TCS analysis is most likely that the GenomesDB database used for TCS contains no type strain 
of V. kanaloae. The database contains one strain of V. kanaloae that was the best match in the TCS 
analyses if non-type strains were included. 
The strains S1072 and S1073-1 seemed to belong to the same species, with 99.7% ANI based on 
99% alignment and the species was most likely V. nigripulchritudo as the two strains shared 96% 
ANI with the type strain based on 91% alignment and all other analyses suggested this species as 
well. It should be noted however, that S1073-1 was suspected to perhaps be wrongly identified in 
this study due to an error during the laboratory work. The two strains S1072 and S1073-1 were very 
similar in most of the analyses (similar colony morphology, exactly the same GC% content, the fur 
gene in both strains were found as two partial fur genes, identified as the same species with all tools 
and even with the same z-value in the TCS analysis). This could either show that they are two clones 
of the same strain, as they were isolated from the same sea turtle (supplementary material to [12]), 
or it could show that S1072 have been used twice in the lab by mistake, labelled as both S1072 and 
S1073-1. Furthermore, the BLAST search of the 16S rRNA gene of S1073-1 did not correspond to 
the originally published 16S rRNA gene amplicon sequence [12]. It is not the case that the same 
sequence data has been used twice under the two labels, as there are also some differences in the 
sequence data (the two strains did not share exactly the same ANI when compared to V. nigripulchri-
tudo and had different number of contigs). Preferably, this would have been examined in the lab,

Genome mining 
The genomes were mined for potential biosynthetic gene clusters using antiSMASH 5.1.2 [95] (Table 
12). All strains except S3789 and S4051 were predicted to contain at least one BGC. More than half 
the strains were predicted to harbour more than five BGCs and the maximum number of predicted 
BGCs were nine. The strains were found to dedicate 2.4-6.1% of their genomes to BGCs (excluding 
the strains with genomes assembled with low quality Nanopore reads, as the assemblies do not 
represent entire genomes), except the strain S2155-1, in which only one BGC is predicted account-
ing for 0.2% of the assembled genome. The number of predicted BGCs or the proportion of the 
genome dedicated to BGCs did not correlate to genome size or inhibitory activity in the original 
screening. Most of the BGCs were predicted to encode NRPSs or bacteriocins, but also PKSs, beta-
lactones, aryl polyenes, terpenes, siderophores, polyunsaturated fatty acids, ectoins, microvidins 
and homoserine lactones were predicted. Ten BGCs across eight strains shared >75% similarity 
with a known BGC, and probably share chemical properties with known compounds.  Most of the 
predicted BGC were not very similar to known compounds, and 55 of the predicted BGCs shared 
less than 25% similarity to any known compounds, indicating that they could be provide novel chem-
istry. 
Some of the compounds known to be produced by strains from the Galathea collection was predicted 
in strains in this study as well. S2043 (identified as V. coralliilyticus) and S2056 (tentatively identified 
as V. coralliilyticus) were both predicted to carry the BGC for andrimid production. S0788, that was 
also identified as V. coralliilyticus was not predicted to produce andrimid, however. S2754 was 
shown by PCR amplification to carry a gene in the holomycin BGC like S2753. The two strains were 
sampled from the same site and were identified as the same species according to 16S rRNA gene 
amplicon sequencing, but S2754 was not whole-genome-sequenced so further verification of the 
species ID was not carried out.

5. Discussion 
Since the discovery of microorganisms, microbiologists have desired to categorise and characterise 
them. Systematic ways of doing so facilitates clear communication as well as forming the basis for 
comparisons between studies and more exhaustive investigations. Techniques and gold standards 
for species identification and characterisation is constantly changing as new technologies are devel-
oped. Examples of new technologies that have changed this recently are high throughput gene se-
quencing that can generate large amounts of data and powerful computers that can handle and 
analyse these large amounts of data. Third generation sequencing, or long-read sequencing, is one 
of the latest developments in this area [112]. This study used the long-read sequencing platform 
Oxford Nanopore Technologies MinION to whole-genome-sequence Vibrionaceae strains for cate-
gorisation and characterisation. Twenty strains were sequenced, of which four could be identified to 
species level, six could potentially represent novel species and the rest could not be identified due 
to lack of sequence data. The genome sequences were also mined for BGCs. Several BGCs were 
predicted in most of the strains, of which a few were very similar to known BGCs, but most were not 
very similar to anything known. 
5.1 
Assessment of Nanopore sequencing for species identification and phyloge-
netic analyses of Vibrionaceae strains 
As Nanopore sequencing have been deemed highly error prone [47], emphasis was put on the qual-
ity of the sequencing results generated. In this assessment, a distinction was made between the 
first, successful sequencing experiment, and the second, unsuccessful sequencing experiment 
where issues with loading the flow cell resulted in less reads and lower quality reads that were not 
representative of Nanopore sequencing. Under normal circumstances, the results from the second 
experiment would not have been included, and the experiment redone. This was not possible due to 
the restricted access to university laboratory facilities during the COVID-19 pandemic, and therefore 
the results were included to provide a wider base for the report.  
First, the general quality statistics for sequencing and assembly was examined. This revealed that 
the mean read length was 5-10 times the Illumina read length which is consistent with existing liter-
ature [113], and that the quality scores of the majority of the reads were acceptable (Appendix 1). 
Another indicator of acceptable quality was the size of the assemblies and the number of contigs. 
The size of the assemblies from the first sequencing experiment was 4.7-6.2 Mb, which corresponds 
to the previously reported size of genomes of the strains in the Galathea collection [18]. For three of 
the strains sequenced in the first experiment, the assembly produced only two contigs, which is 
consistent with the number of chromosomes commonly found in Vibrionaceae [18].  
Tanaka et al. investigated Nanopore sequencing for determining overall genome relatedness indices 
(OGRI) and for doing MLSA on Vibrionaceae strains [67] as described in section 2.3.2. They found 
that even though Nanopore sequencing is error-prone, it did not influence these parameters to a 
great extent, as similar results could be obtained with Nanopore only, Illumina only and hybrid as-
semblies [67]. This would have been interesting to replicate in this study, but no strains that have 
previously been Illumina sequenced, were successfully sequenced in this study before laboratory 
access was restricted. There was some overlap, however, between strains sequenced in his study 
and strains where the 16S rRNA gene and/or the fur gene has been amplicon sequenced, allowing 
for some examination of this.  
==PAGE:71==
Side 62 af 74 
 
Three strains sequenced in this study were used by Machado et al. to test the FurIOS database [82]. 
The same matches were obtained when the fur gene sequences from the present study was sub-
mitted to FurIOS, showing that Nanopore sequences can be used for fur gene species identification. 
Thirteen strains were sequenced in this study providing at least one copy of the 16S rRNA gene from 
a Nanopore-only assembly and were preliminary identified to species level by Gram et al. by BLAST 
of 16S rRNA gene PCR products [12]. To assess the use of the Nanopore sequencing for 16S rRNA 
gene comparison, a BLAST search for the 16S rRNA gene sequences of the present study was 
compared to the original preliminary identification. In the case of seven strains, the same results 
were obtained when including non-type strains in the BLAST search. Five strains were identified as 
belonging to a different species in this study as compared to the preliminary identification, but still as 
species that is closely related to the species found in the original study. The differences could be 
due to the inadequacy of the 16S rRNA gene for precise species identification of Vibrionaceae strains 
[31], or that new entries have been added to the database since [114] or the differences in length of 
the sequences, as the original preliminary identification was based on partial 16S rRNA gene se-
quences, and the sequences in the present study are in most cases representing the entire gene. 
Only one strain (S1073-1) was in the present work identified as belonging to distantly related species 
as compared to the identification in the original study, but this is thought to be due to an error during 
the laboratory work. Together these results show that Nanopore sequencing is also suitable for spe-
cies identification based on 16S rRNA gene sequence when compared to Illumina amplicon se-
quences, as the observed differences for the most part are within the expected when working with 
Vibrionaceae 16S rRNA genes. 
A thorough comparison of Illumina sequencing and Nanopore sequencing for use in phylogenetic 
analyses or species identification was not possible to include in this study, but the obtained results 
support the conclusion by Tanaka et al. [67], that Nanopore sequencing is appropriate for these kind 
of analyses despite the higher error rates. Assemblies of a size corresponding to genome size and 
based on few, large contigs were obtained, from which it was possible to extract phylogenetic marker 
genes as well as perform genome wide comparisons to type strains. 
5.2 
Species identification 
For species identification, a combination of several tools was used, as none of them were both fast 
and easy to use while providing convincing results alone. 
The original preliminary species identification based on 16S rRNA gene amplicon sequencing by 
Gram et al. [12] was only used to guide the identification, as the databases of gene sequences have 
been updated with new data since the study was published in 2010 [114], so it might differ from 
newer results.  
Two single-gene-based species identification tools, FurIOS and 16S rRNA gene BLAST search, 
were used. These kind of tools are very useful in some settings, as they do not rely on whole genome 
sequencing, but can be used in combination with PCR amplicon sequencing as well, which might be 
more easily available for e.g. smaller labs [83]. However these tools have the respective disad-
vantages that the 16S rRNA gene is not always appropriate for differentiation of Vibrionaceae spe-
cies [16], and the FurIOS database was last modified in 2017, it contains genes from non-type strains 
and it contains relatively few genes, as the fur gene is not sequenced and published to a great extent 
[115]. Therefore, these tools only guided the species identification as well.  
==PAGE:72==
Side 63 af 74 
 
The fur gene is not a widely used phylogenetic marker, however it works well for differentiation of 
Vibrionaceae species [83]. This was verified in this study, as the patterns of the phylogenetic trees 
based on the fur gene and 16S rRNA gene respectively were relatively similar and the output from 
FurIOS was comparable to the output from SpeciesFinder, that does species identification based on 
16S rRNA gene and also includes non-type strains in the output. Hereby, the fur gene met the re-
quirement for a phylogenetic marker to “predict whole-genome relationships with precision and ac-
curacy that can be compared to the use of e.g. 16S rRNA gene data” (section 2.5). 
KmerFinder and JSpeciesWS TCS, were also used for species identification. Basing the species 
identification on entire genomes avoids most of the issues pointed out for the mentioned single-
gene-based tools. KmerFinder has one of the same disadvantages as the FurIOS database as it 
includes non-type strains, however, the database is continuously updated, last in July 2020 [116]. 
Also the database of JSpeciesWS was updated in May 2020 [117]. The TCS outputs non-type strains 
as well, but information is provided about which strains are type strains. Furthermore, JSpeciesWS 
provides thresholds for the z-score calculated in the TCS, to determine if two strains are the same 
species or just closely related [117]. KmerFinder provides a measurement of the match between two 
strains in the form of number of matching k-mers, but no threshold for species identification. Because 
of these differences, the output from TCS is considered the most credible.  
To further verify the species identifications obtained through the different tools, ANI comparisons 
were done on the strains and type strains of species that were the closest matches in previous 
analyses. ANI is in some ways similar to KmerFinder and TCS, as the entire genome of a query 
strain is compared to the entire genome of a reference strain. More information is obtained through 
ANI analyses however, as the JSpeciesWS ANI calculator outputs both the ANI percentage as well 
as the alignment percentage, making it possible to determine how large a part of the genomes is 
comparable. Furthermore, ANI between two genomes mirrors DDH, the previous gold standard for 
bacterial species circumscription, with an ANI value of 95% corresponding to a DDH of 70% [118]. 
ANI was therefore used as the main indicator of species identification in this study. 
Overall, the strains examined in this study belongs to different Vibrio spp. as well as a tentative 
Photobacterium sp., verifying the hypothesis, that the Galathea collection shows taxonomic and 
phylogenetic diversity. Four strains were identified as known species, six strains potentially repre-
sented novel species and ten strains were not identified due to lack of sequence data. 
5.3 
Evaluation of the issues regarding use of 16S rRNA gene sequence similari-
ties for Vibrionaceae species identification 
The 16S rRNA gene sequence was included in the species identification and phylogenetic analyses 
in this thesis, but as previously mentioned, this gene is not always a suitable marker for differentiating 
Vibrionaceae species [31]. With up to 15 copies of the gene present in a single genome and intra-
genomic variation up to 5% reported [24], it is easy to imagine that it is difficult to base conclusive 
analyses on this. In addition to being very variable within a species, the 16S rRNA gene is sometimes 
very similar across species [81]. An example of this is V. splendidus, where intraspecific 16S rRNA 
gene sequence variability is up to 2%, while some V. splendidus strains are only 0.8% different from 
some closely related V. lentus strains based on 16S rRNA gene comparisons [81]. To guide the use 
of the 16S rRNA gene sequence data in this study, it was examined if the main issue is the high 
copy number with high intraspecific variation or the interspecific similarity.  
==PAGE:73==
Side 64 af 74 
 
Analysis of the intragenomic variation of the 16S rRNA gene in the present study was done using 
the 10 high-quality Nanopore based genomes, as they were believed to provide the best represen-
tation of the high copy number. As the 16S rRNA gene is longer than the read length of Illumina, it 
is probable that several copies of the gene is present in the actual genome, but they have been 
combined to a single or few different copies in the hybrid assemblies.  
Two strains could be identified as different species based on BLAST searches of the different copies 
of the 16S rRNA gene against type strains. However, a majority of the copies in both strains gave 
the same species match, with only a minority of the copies pointing towards other species (Figure 
18 and Appendix 8). The eight other strains show less intraspecific variation and a unanimous spe-
cies identification. This means 20% of the strains in this study have high enough intraspecific vari-
ance to obscure species identification and phylogenetical analyses based on 16S rRNA gene simi-
larities. This issue is less critical if a closed genome or whole genome based on long reads is ob-
tained like in this study, from which it is possible to determine which 16S rRNA gene sequence is 
the dominant. The same approach was taken by Machado et al. 2015 [38], when describing the type 
strain S2753 of P. galatheae, that carried 12 copies of the 16S rRNA gene of which nine shared 
great similarity and were considered the representative sequences. 
The second issue, the interspecific similarities, was evaluated using the phylogenetic trees and 
BLAST results. The phylogenetic tree of Vibrionaceae type strains based on the 16S rRNA genes 
showed branch lengths of <0.01 for several species (detailed phylogenetic tree in Appendix 5), indi-
cating how closely related they are based on the 16S rRNA gene. The same issue is seen when 
BLAST searching the 16S rRNA gene, as the resulting output often contains several different type 
strains with more than 97% identity, in some cases even several with more than 99% identity (full 
BLAST results in Appendix 8). For the strains in this study, the most prominent challenge in species 
identification is therefore the high interspecific similarities, as this cannot be resolved by changing 
sequencing strategy or something similar, only by using other characteristics for species circum-
scription, which is why the array of different tools were used. It is common to use a combination of 
methods for species identification of Vibrionaceae species, examples of this are Li et al. 2020 de-
scribing the novel species V. ouci and V. aquaticus using 16S rRNA gene phylogeny, ANI, dDDH 
and phenotypic characteristics [119] as well as Li et al. 2019 that described the novel species V. 
albus by a combination of 16S rRNA gene comparison, MLSA, ANI, dDDH and a range of phenotypic 
and chemotaxonomic characteristics, where the 16S rRNA gene similarities indicated the strain be-
longed to known species, but other methods indicated a novel species [120]. 
5.4 
Genome mining 
Most strains in the study showed a promising potential with up to nine BGCs in a genome predicted 
by AntiSMASH. This was not unexpected, as other Vibrionaceae strains and specifically strains from 
the Galathea Collection produce bioactive compounds and are predicted to have potential for several 
additional BGCs [31]. Vibrionaceae is not the only family of which marine specimens are potential 
natural product producers. Other examples are marine, pigmented pseudoalteromonads that is pre-
dicted to carry up to 20 BGCs [37] and Saccharomonospora sp. CNQ‐490 isolated from marine 
sediment that is predicted to carry 19 BGCs [121], solidifying the rationale behind including marine 
bacteria in the search for novel bioactive compounds. The strains of this study have significantly less 
potential than the most prolific producers of secondary metabolites such as Streptomyces spp., of 
which some strains are predicted to carry up to 83 BGCs [122]. However, only a few of the predicted 
BGCs in this study were very similar to known BGCs, and most of them shared little to no similarity 
==PAGE:74==
Side 65 af 74 
 
to any known clusters, suggesting that many of them produce novel compounds. These strains are 
therefore still relevant in the search for novel bioactive compounds, even though the number of BGCs 
is lower than for some genera. A predicted BGC does not prove the presence of an actual BGC in 
the genome however, as AntiSMASH is prone to predict false positive BGCs [96]. This is an inten-
tional feature of the tool, as it is deemed more useful to over-identify than to miss a relevant BGC. 
This means, that WGS and genome mining can never stand alone as tools for natural product dis-
covery. Furthermore, many genomes possess “silent” or “cryptic” gene clusters, meaning that it is 
not enough to discover the BGC to obtain the natural product [114]. Analyses like these therefore 
needs to be followed by laboratory experiments further investigating, among other things, the actual 
potential of the strain, how to activate production of the compounds and the properties of the com-
pounds. Tools that can be used to further elucidate the biosynthetic potential include gene editing 
using CRISPR-Cas9, heterologous expression-based strategies, changing environmental factors, 
inducing stress etc. [114]. 
6. Conclusion 
Ten Vibrionaceae strains from the Galathea Collection was successfully whole-genome-sequenced 
using Oxford Nanopore Technologies MinION sequencing platform, and additionally ten strains were 
attempted sequenced with less success. Ten strains were identified based on genomic sequences 
using a range of online bioinformatic tools. S0693 was identified as belonging to the species V. 
kanaloae, S0788 and S2043 as belonging to V. coralliilyticus and S1072 as belonging to V. nigrip-
ulchritudo. S0562-1 and S0568-1 were identified as belonging to the same species potentially V. 
chagasii or a novel species closely related to V. chagasii. S1100, S2155-1, S2606 and S3301-1 were 
not identified despite being represented by high quality sequences, and possible represent novel 
species.  S0787, S1073-1, S2056, S2226, S2394 S2537 S3668-2, S3728-1, S3879 and S4051 were 
not identified due to issues in the lab or lack of sequence data. The nanopore sequencing technology 
used in the MinION is still a new technology under rapid development, and continuous assessment 
and evaluation is needed to ensure high quality sequence data. This study builds on the study by 
Tanaka et al. 2018 [67], and found that Nanopore sequencing is suitable for species identification 
and phylogenetic analyses of marine Vibrionaceae, despite the higher error rates when compared 
to Illumina sequencing. The genomes of the strains were mined for bioactive compounds using an-
tiSMASH 5.1.2 revealing a great potential for production of bioactive compounds in most strains with 
up to nine predicted BGCs in a strain, as was expected based on previous studies of marine Vibri-
onaceae. The strains dedicated 2.4-6.1% of their genomes to BGCs, and most of the predicted BGCs 
were not similar to any known compounds. In conclusion, this study likely found several novel Vibri-
onaceae species and confirmed previous studies indicating a large chemical potential in Vibri-
onaceae strains, and so encourages further elucidation of the predicted BGCs. 
7. Further perspectives 
As this study was impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic limiting the access to laboratory facilities, a 
series of experiments that would have been appropriate to include could not be performed. The first 
step in taking this work further, would therefore be to conduct these experiments. The strains se-
quenced in the second, unsuccessful sequencing experiment should be re-sequenced to properly 
identify them and assess their genomic potential for secondary metabolite production. The remaining 
strains, not sequenced in this study, should also be sequenced, to obtain a bigger picture of the 
==PAGE:75==
Side 66 af 74 
 
potential and diversity of the strain collection, and to be able to observe any patterns in chemical 
potential, species identity, geographic location or sampling type. 
The strains that were not identified as belonging to a known species, should be further investigated 
to determine if they represent novel species. This investigation could include re-sequencing with 
MinION for strains sequenced in the second experiment, sequencing with Illumina to produce high 
quality hybrid assemblies and potentially closed genomes, phenotypic and chemotaxonomic char-
acteristics such as cell morphology, Gram testing, catalase and oxidase activity, vibriostatic sensi-
tivity, salinity requirements, temperature optima, oxygen requirements, fatty acid content, chemotyp-
ing and more [123]. 
The strains that showed high genomic potential for secondary metabolite production, should also be 
investigated further, to find any potentially novel compounds or discover what known compounds 
are produced by the strains. The array of produced compounds can be assessed with LC-UV/MS, 
and interesting compounds can be isolated by bioassay-guided fractionation and elucidated by NMR 
[13]. If a strain does not produce the predicted compounds, it is still possible that the strain is able to 
produce bioactive compounds but is not doing so under the current conditions. Experiments could 
then be carried out to induce the production for example changing culture conditions such as media 
formulation, temperature or pH, co-culturing the strain with other species, engineering the transcrip-
tion or translation machinery to change gene expression or heterologous expression of the BGC in 
more well-studied production organisms [124].
