Abstract
Miniaturization of products in several industry sectors is pushing the plastic injec-
tion moulding industry towards the production of micro-scale parts. Micro-injection
moulding poses new challenges in terms of precision and replicability of micro-features,
particularly when using a conventional injection moulding machine. Among those
challenges, key concerns are the determination of the process parameters, as conven-
tional processing is often not able to provide the requirements for micro-replication
and stability; as well as the industry’s need for a reliable, easy and fast method to
provide the operation parameters.
In the present project, two methodologies for the determination of process parame-
ters for injection moulding were tested on a conventional injection moulding machine
and compared for the processing of a plastic component moulded in cyclo olephi
co-polymer that counts with micro-features on its surface.
One of the two methodologies tested in this research project is based on the concept
of maintaining a constant plastic pressure at the tip of the screw during the injection
phase, which is achieved by using a decreasing injection speed proﬁle. The second
methodology is based on a constant injection rate during the injection phase and is a
well-known and accepted method in the industry.
The injection moulding tool and moulded plastic parts’ dimensions were characterized
by means of Light Optical Microscopy. Parts were also characterized in terms of mass.
A Process Window Approach was used to study the inﬂuence of several process pa-
rameters on the replication of micro-features.
In-cavity temperature and pressure sensors were used to characterize the pressure
and temperature of the thermoplastic during the injection and holding phases of the
injection moulding process.

Background and
Project Introduction 1
1.1 Introduction and Project Overview
The present project was developed as a MSc. Thesis for the degree of MSc. in Mate-
rials and Manufacturing Engineering within the Mechanical Engineering Department
of the Technical University of Denmark.
Miniaturization of components in several industry sectors is pushing manufacturing
techniques towards the micro-scale. Being injection moulding one of the most promi-
nent processes for shaping of plastic parts, injection moulding of micro-components
or components with micro-features will play a decisive role in the manufacturing of
modern products.
Conventional processing is not always capable to adapt to the requirements of micro-
injection moulding components in terms of precision and stability. Because of this, a
review of the methodologies used to generate the parameters for the injection moulding
process is necessary.
The present project attempts to tackle some of those issues by the generation of a
comparison of a traditional injection moulding methodology and one of recent de-
velopment in terms of their ability to replicate micro-structures on the surface of a
plastic component, in this case fabricated in cyclo olephi co-polymer.
The novelty of the present research project resides in three key aspects: First, the
study of a novel method which, to the knowledge of the author, has not been studied
using a scientiﬁc approach by the scientiﬁc community. Second, a special focus on
the injection pressure for the selection of the parameters for the injection moulding
process. Third, and most important, to test methodologies that were developed for
conventional-sized injection moulding parts on micro-injection moulding type B parts.
1.2 Goal and Objectives
The main objective of the present project is to implement an injection pressure con-
trolled injection moulding process aiming to optimize the replication of mould features
at a micro-scale. The process covers all main phases of the injection moulding cycle
including: ﬁlling, packing, dosing and cooling.
==PAGE:13==
7
1.2 Goal and Objectives
Secondary objectives include:
• A comparison of two methodologies for process parameters generation.
• Extraction of information about the process from in-cavity sensors installed in
the mould.
• Installation and use of temperature and ﬂow monitor equipment for the cooling
water system.
• Characterization of moulded parts in terms of mass and dimensions at micro-
and meso-scales.
• Generation of a DOE study with some of the most important process variables
to compare their inﬂuence on micro-replication.

State of the Art 2
2.1 Plastic Injection Moulding
In 1985, in the United States, the volumetric consumption of plastic was greater than
that of steel, copper and aluminium combined. [1] This consumption has risen ever
since. Today, plastics are used in plenty of applications being the most important
packaging with almost a third of the consumption followed by construction and tex-
tiles. The automotive industry is utilizing more and more plastics every year and
plastic has found applications in many industries as an alternative to a wide range of
materials.
Injection moulding (IM) is the most common method for mass manufacturing of
plastic products. A very ample range of products from truck dashboards to micro-
components can be produced using this method. IM for plastics was ﬁrst conceived
to manufacture billiard balls in celluloid as a replacement for ivory by John Wesley
Hyatt in the late 19th century. Before the mid 20th century, the process consisted in
an externally heated barrel and a plunger was used to push the resin into the mould.
Because of the poor thermal conductivity of plastics, this lead to uneven heating of the
resin in the barrel, resin degradation and products with in-homogeneous properties.
This problem was solved by the introduction of the reciprocating screw in 1956. [2]
The introduction of this mechanism revolutionized the process and allowed plastic
injection moulding to become the most used forming process for plastics. In the last
30 years, IM has had a very rapid growth in new areas such as automotive, electronics,
medical, packaging, etc. [3] Nowadays, approximately two thirds of plastic parts are
manufactured using IM. [2]
The modern IM process is a cyclic (semi-continuous) process.
In Figure 2.1, an
schematic of a typical process cycle is shown.
The cycle begins with an open mould:
1. The ﬁrst stage is the closing of the mould: During this stage the two or more
parts of the mould are brought together and the clamp force is applied.
2. Stage number two is optional as in modern processes the injection unit is always
in contact with the mould, if not, the injection unit moves forward into contact
with the mould.
3. Next, the plastic is plunged into the mould by the injection piston, this is known
as ﬁrst injection, ﬁll or, simply, injection. This stage is controlled by speed and
position of the screw.

4. Fourth, is the holding, packing or second injection.

5. Next, is the backwards movement of the injection unit. This stage is optional

6. Plasticating, dosing or recovery stage: After the piston stops its movement, the
screw rotates and is pushed backwards by the plastic itself. During this stage,
the next injection shot is prepared.
- Cooling stage ends. At this point the part has cooled down and is ready to
eject.
7. The last stage is the mould opening and ejection of the part. The moulds open
and the part is ejected mechanically, pneumatically or manually.
The screw has four main functions: To eliminate the central cold region and to only
have a thin layer of hot plastic in the barrel; to transport the material from the throat
to the nozzle using ﬂights and rotation; to heat the material by compressing it and
introducing shear forces which give the material between 60 and 90 % of the heat; [4]
and, to plunger the plastic into the mould using a check valve with ﬂutes and a ring
that allows the plastic to ﬂow in the right direction.
2.1.1 Micro-Injection Moulding
Several industry sectors such as IT, medical or automotive are pressing plastics injec-
tion moulding towards the micro-scale for the development of miniaturized products.

Figure 2.2: Schematic of the injection moulding process phases. [2]
When IM is used to fabricate parts in the microns or submicrons size range, it is
deﬁned as microinjection moulding (µ-IM). This is a broad term and divisions have
been proposed. Yao and Kim [5] proposed two categories for µ-IM parts. Parts A,
with a size of less than 1 mm and parts B, larger in size but with micro-features.
Other categorizations take weight into account.
Injection moulding machines have also been adapted for µ-IM parts. Adaptions in-
clude the use of a melting chamber and a plunger instead of the reciprocating screw to
ensure pellet melting, or a higher injection speed to ensure ﬁlling before solidiﬁcation
and, more precise control units. [6]
The use of conventional IM machines for µ-IM parts poses several challenges: material
degradation can occur because of the light weight of the moulded parts; higher mould
temperature is often required; small strokes demand great precision of movements;
part ejection should be done carefully; etc. [7]
Processing techniques normally employed in IM machines cannot always be used di-
rectly for µ-IM parts because of scale eﬀects [8]. For this reason, there is a lack of
knowledge about processing strategies and methodologies for this kind of products.
2.1.2 Pressure-driven Injection Moulding
The term pressure-driven refers to how the injection stage or ﬁrst injection is con-
ceived. As mentioned before, during the ﬁrst stage the process is controlled by speed
and position of the screw. This movement will be reﬂected as an increment in the
pressure of the melt as it ﬂows along its path.

2.1 Plastic Injection Moulding
In the old days, injection moulding machines were controlled by oil valves that directed
ﬂow from a pressurized reservoir to the required mechanisms. During the ﬁlling stage,
in order to maintain the pressure at a safe level, machines were equipped with a relief
valve which acted when pressure reached a certain level. By triggering this safety
measure, processors were unconsciously modulating the injection speed.
In newer machines, it is possible to view the behaviour of the pressure at the front
of the screw as the injection cycle proceeds. This development lead to a new way of
moulding in which a constant speed was used and a resulting pressure would occur
without being limited by the machine followed by a pressure-controlled stage. This
allowed improvements in the control of the process, the most important methodology
based on this approach is called the Scientiﬁc Moulding method [4], which will be
deﬁned in great detail in following sections.
In recent years, a new paradigm has been established. By intentionally modulating
the speed during the ﬁlling stage, it is now possible to control the pressure to achieve
new objectives and solve some of the common problems with traditional techniques.
Because of this, some new techniques and methodologies have been developed and
proposed.
One of the ﬁrst is called the ACG method [9], which will be detailed
in following sections. Another very recent example is the method developed by a
company named iMFLUX R
⃝. The methodology developed by this company is based
on the use of an external controller system that automatically modulates the ﬁlling
rate to achieve a deﬁned pressure. [10]
2.1.3 Injection Moulding Process Setting
At a ﬁrst glance, IM can be seen as a very simple process. Plastic is melted, injected
into the mould, cooled down and removed as a ﬁnal part. In reality, it is a very intri-
cate and complex process with high inter-dependency of the most important process
parameters.
Pandelidis and Zou [11] illustrate this behaviour with some examples: if the melt
temperature is increased, a decrease in melt viscosity occurs, this will lead to a reduced
pressure and stress, but a higher temperature may increase the risk of the material
degrading while requiring an increase in the cooling time and, thus, increasing the
mould temperature. Increasing the mould temperature will reduce heat losses and
the plastic will ﬂow more easily resulting in a reduced ﬁlling time. A short ﬁlling
time increases the ﬁlling pressure resulting in a higher shear rate and shear stress. It
is clear that these are conﬂicting process parameters and that it is diﬃcult to ﬁnd a
balance between these variables.
Because of the huge inﬂuence of process parameters in the ﬁnal quality and economics
of the part and, the complexity of the process, the determination of the machine
settings is critical. The processing parameters are determined by highly experienced
and skilled personnel and the process is based on the “know-how” of the moulder.
The worker will probably recall a previous setting for a similar part and use intuition
==PAGE:18==
2 State of the Art
12
to adjust the parameters by a trial-and-error approach until the desired quality and
cycle time is obtained. The time required for this process is dependent on the skill
and experience of the moulding personnel.
In academic research, there have been many attempts on process parameter selection
and optimization but, typically, only a few parameters are studied in a much idealized
system, among those are: injection speed and time; packing pressure and time; and,
barrel and mould temperature.
However, in a modern injection moulding machine there are tenths and even hundreds
of parameters that the operator must select when setting up a process. Coupled with
this situation is the fact that, in the industrial environment, time is one of the most
important constraints. The mould should be running as soon as possible.
There are several approaches for process parameters and selection of the IM process.
Among these approaches are: mathematical models, numerical simulations, design
of experiments (DOE), process window approach (PWA), artiﬁcial neural networks
(ANN), etc. [12] The speciﬁc diﬀerences and characteristics of these approaches are
outside of the scope of the present project. In Table A.1, a summary of the diﬀerent
methods for selecting process parameters and their advantages and disadvantages is
shown.
One of the most interesting aspects of the new approaches is their aim to achieve
an online control of the process. This means, automatically adjusting the machine’s
parameters in order to maintain a deﬁned variable at a certain value. The variables
used to control could be the cushion size or a pressure curve, among others.
These kind of developments are in their early stages and are, probably, only useful for
processors that have few tool changes and very long runs. In a dynamic environment
with very short runs (sometimes less than 4 hours), it would be too diﬃcult and costly
to implement them because of the need of a high number of experiments.
It is clear that there have been many attempts on tackling the problem at hand.
Although there are guidelines and techniques to determine the processing parameters,
there is not a single technique that can be used to satisfactory solve the problem.
Another aspect to consider, is the shortage of experienced personnel because of the
huge increment in demand of injection moulded parts. It is evident that there is a
need for a method that can be easily learned, used on the shop-ﬂoor and quickly
set up at the machine.
There have been attempts to establish methodologies for
the selection of process parameters focused on the shop-ﬂoor with its constraints of
personnel, equipment and, most important, time.
The pioneers in this ﬁeld were John Bozzelli and Rod Groleau [4], the principles that
they established are still widely used in the industry. Their methodology was named
Scientiﬁc Processing and Scientiﬁc Moulding (SM).
Scientiﬁc Processing refers to the whole process of transformation of plastic from the
reception of raw material until shipping [4], this includes, among other stages: storage,
pre-processing, processing, peripheral equipment, etc. While SM, speciﬁcally, refers
to the process stage in which plastic is transformed from pellets into a part at the

injection moulding machine. In other words, SM is a part of Scientiﬁc Processing.
SM is the most used methodology in the industry as it allows good consistency and

In recent years, a new methodology for setting up processes was developed in Mexico

2.2 Scientiﬁc Moulding Methodology
2.2.1 Introduction
The term Scientiﬁc Moulding was coined by John Bozzelli and Rod Groleau who
were pioneers in the ﬁeld of modern injection moulding. John Bozzelli developed the
method at DOW Chemical c⃝in the 70s and 80s. [13]
At its beginnings, SM was conceived as a response to the lack of knowledge about
processing and materials that plastic processors had at the time.
Which lead to
quality and consistency problems. Nowadays, SM is the reference and standard in the
moulding industry and is widely used. SM is a very powerful technique for process
development and optimization as it requires very little equipment; can be done at the
machine; has the advantage that the parameters can normally be translated from one
machine-mould combination to another machine, and gives parts of good quality with
acceptable cycle time.
Some of the early stages in the production process can heavily inﬂuence the process
and the quality of the moulded parts, e.g., some resins should not be exposed to
sunlight to avoid degradation, some must be kept in dry environments, etc.
Many resins must be dried before processing, this is a consequence of the hygroscopic
nature of some polymers and the possibility of hydrolysis inside the injection moulding
machine. This is especially important for polymers which are a result of condensation
polymerization. If heated in the presence of water, the polymerization reaction could
revert and cause chain scission. [4] [14] Normally, the resin manufacturer will specify
the drying conditions of the resin in terms of temperature and time. It is important
to follow these guidelines as a shorter time could result in moisture getting into the
barrel and causing degradation, aesthetic defects or bubbles, whereas a longer time
could result in the loss of volatile additives such as lubricants, ﬂame retardants, etc.

2.2.2 SM Process Development
SM assumes that the material has been prepared in a correct manner and, that
the machine-mould combination has been properly selected (the relation between
shot size and screw diameter is adequate, the size of the mould is appropriate for
the platens, etc.).
Another assumption is that the clamp force has already been
determined or approximated with an estimate of the injection pressure and projected
area or following instructions from the mould maker.
SM can be divided into the following steps:
• Setting dose and transfer positions
• Setting melt temperatures
• Setting mould temperatures
• Rheology study - Injection phase
• Determining process window
• Determining gate seal time
• Determining cooling time
• Optimization of screw speed and back pressure
2.2.2.1 Setting dose position
The dose position should be selected according to the part’s weight, screw diameter
and melt density of the material. In some cases, it can be diﬃcult to know the melt
density of the material, then, a try-and-error process must be done. It is important to
ensure a large enough cushion (material left in the barrel after injection and holding)
to prevent the screw from hitting the barrel and to give stability to the process.
However, the cushion should be small enough to allow the front of the plastic to
"feel" the pressure from the screw during the holding and dosing phases.
2.2.2.2 Setting melt temperatures
Plastic must be melted before been formed by the injection process, i.e., going beyond
the glass transition temperature and, in the case of semi-crystalline materials, above
the melt temperature to reach the "liquid" state of the resin. This is done by heating
the plastic with friction and heating bands inside the barrel.
It is important to
note that an excessive heating could lead to thermal degradation and chain scission,
especially, in the case of semi-crystalline materials which normally have a narrower
processing temperature range.
The barrel is divided into at least 3 or 4 sections that can be programmed with individ-
ual temperatures. Material manufacturers supply information about the temperature
setting in the material data-sheet. Commonly, the temperature is proﬁled from a
==PAGE:21==
15
2.2 Scientiﬁc Moulding Methodology
lower value at the throat (to prevent melting and to improve dosing), to a higher
value at the end of the screw. Sometimes, especially for semi-crystalline materials
the recommended proﬁle will have a bump at the compression region of the screw to
overcome the huge amount of energy necessary to melt down the crystals in the resin.
In the SM methodology, the temperatures in the barrel are set following the in-
structions of the manufacturer and aiming to the mean value of the speciﬁed range.
Normally, the nominal values, displayed by the machine, will not accurately represent
reality. To overcome this issue, a test is performed using a direct contact pyrometer
previously heated to a value 20 ◦C over the expected value, doing an air shot or a purge
and quickly measuring the temperature using the pyrometer. Then, adjustments can
be done on the machine to meet the desired temperature.
2.2.2.3 Setting mould temperatures
Mould temperature is a critical factor as it can inﬂuence the ﬁlling stage, as well
as the crystallization (for semi-crystalline materials), aﬀects surface properties and,
also gives the plastic molecules the energy necessary to reach an equilibrium stage
without introducing internal stresses. Resin manufacturers invest considerable eﬀort
into the study of optimal mould temperatures and establish an operation range that
is presented in the material data-sheet. In SM, it is recommended to use the mean
value of the supplied range as an starting point and modify if necessary.
2.2.2.4 Rheology study - Injection phase
During the ﬁll stage, also called ﬁrst injection, plastic is pushed into the mould by
the screw at a certain speed resulting in plastic pressure. In this stage, the technician
must indicate the speed at which the screw should move, a limiting pressure that the
machine should not exceed and a staring and ﬁnishing position.
Plastics are viscoelastic materials [14], i.e., their viscosity depends on the shear rate.
A higher shear rate will be translated into a lower apparent viscosity. At high shear
rates, there is a plateau of viscosity, i.e., viscosity does not change signiﬁcantly with
changes of shear rate. SM takes advantage of this fact and sets an injection speed in
this region. This means that the eﬀects of small variations in the injection speed that
could happen because of poor control of the machine or changes in the condition of
the plastic will be minimized and the process will be more stable.
In the SM methodology, an experiment is conducted to construct the "rheology curve"
and ﬁnd the stability region. This is done by following this procedure:
1. Set the melt and mould temperatures according according to Sections 2.2.2.2
and 2.2.2.3.
2. Set holding phase parameters to zero.
3. Set a delay for screw recovery long enough to allow the gate to freeze.

5. Set a cooling time long enough to achieve resin solidiﬁcation and allow extrac-

6. Set a transfer position in order to achieve a part of about 50 % in volume.
7. Set a "slow" injection speed.
Although not speciﬁed in the methodology, a

pressure.

2.2.2.5 Process window
After the injection phase has been performed, the mould is ﬁlled with molten plastic
that will start to solidify and shrink. The second phase, also known as packing or
holding phase, adds more plastic material to compensate for the volumetric shrinkage
caused by the cooling. During this phase, the part achieves its ﬁnal mass. If a part is
under-packed it could present defects such as voids, excessive shrinkage or sink marks.
If a part is over-packed it could have internal stresses that could lead to failure or
warpage.
In SM, the optimal packing pressure is determined by the use of a process window. In
this approach, two variables are changed and their inﬂuence on the part is studied. For
amorphous materials, the normal variables to be changed are the melt temperature
and the packing pressure. For semi-crystalline materials, it is normally the packing
pressure and mould temperature which are changed.
The procedure for determining the packing pressure of an amorphous material is as
follows:
1. Set the mould temperature to a medium value of the range established by the
manufacturer.
2. Set the barrel temperatures to the minimum recommended by the manufacturer.
3. Set the injection speed to the value found in Section 2.2.2.4.
4. Set the holding time and holding pressure to zero.
5. Set a screw delay so that the gate is ensured to be sealed.
6. Set a higher than necessary cooling time.
7. Mould a part ﬁlled about 95 % in volume, determined by a visual approximation.
8. Set the holding time to a value that ensures the gate to be sealed.
9. Using small steps, increase holding pressure until an aesthetically acceptable
part is obtained, this will correspond to the low temperature, low pressure corner
of the process window.
10. Keep increasing the holding pressure until the part exhibits defects. This will
correspond to the low temperature, high pressure corner of the process window.
11. Set the barrel temperatures to the maximum recommended by the manufacturer
and repeat the process to ﬁnd the two missing corners of the process window.

2.2.2.7 Cooling time
Cooling is an important factor in the injection moulding process. During this stage
the plastic molecules stop their movements and, in the case of semi-crystalline resins,
crystals form. A part must have enough mechanical stability to be ejected from the
mould without damages. To ensure this, the part is cooled down below the resin’s
heat deﬂection temperature (HDT). HDT is a commonly used standard in the industry
and is deﬁned as the temperature at which a moulded part deforms under a certain
stress. It is reported in the material data-sheets. Cooling can also inﬂuence the ﬁnal
dimensions of the injected part.
The processor can manipulate the cooling rate by letting the part cool down inside the
mould with an increment in the cycle time or let the part cool down in the atmosphere
after ejection.

2.2.2.8 Screw speed and back pressure
In the SM methodology, there are no experiments designed for the determination of
the dosing stage parameters. Both are inﬂuenced by many factors, such as design
of the screw, state of the check valve, machine design, sensibility of the resin, etc.,
moreover, some machines allow the option of proﬁling as a function of the position of
the tip of the screw.
Still, as a rule of thumb and good processing practice, these parameters should be
selected so that the dosing stage is completed at least a couple of tenths of a second
before the cooling time is over. Back-pressure can be set as the lowest pressure that
can keep a consistent dosing and ensure that the cycle is not restricted by the dosing
time. It is important to ensure that during dosing no mechanical degradation of the
resin happens. Normally, this could only be a problem with very short cycles and
very sensitive resins.

2.3.2.2 Selection of barrel zones temperatures
Resin manufacturers spend a considerable amount of time and eﬀort in the preparation
of processing data-sheets that include a range of recommended process temperatures.
Normally, the zones of the barrel are also speciﬁed. The temperatures are commonly
proﬁled, from a cold throat to a hot front of the barrel. Obviously, the proposed
processing temperatures are very conservative in order to prevent degradation since
the resin manufacturer does not have information about the machine in which the
resin is going to be processed.
In ACG, a simple logic is followed. If the manufacturer recommends a range of tem-
perature, then the midpoint of that range should be close to an optimal value for
==PAGE:29==
23
2.3 Pressure-Driven Injection Moulding
processing, moreover, the temperature at the front zone of the screw is the tempera-
ture that the manufacturer uses as moulding temperature.
In traditional process setting, there are two beliefs about why lower temperatures at
the back side of the barrel are recommended:
• The ﬁrst case is to prevent a "thermal shock" that could damage the resin. This
is a wrong reasoning as plastic materials have a very poor thermal conductivity
[14] and most of the heat needed to melt the resin comes from the work exerted
by the screw.
• The second belief is inherited from the time when IM machines were commonly
over-dimensioned in terms of barrel size. Plastic was easily degraded because of
the high residence time.
Nowadays, injection moulding machines are much better selected and cycles are much
shorter. The residence time in the barrel is, now, not as long and plastic will not
degrade as easily in most of the cases.
Because of this, in ACG, the barrel temperatures are set with a ﬂat proﬁle at the
center of the recommended temperature range for the front zone of the barrel. Doing
so, the resin will have more time to reach the desired temperature and the melting
process will be more homogeneous and stable. This setting will be used for the process
development and in most cases will be adequate. However, the temperature proﬁle
will be revised in a further experiment. If the residence time is found to be too long
then the temperatures will be proﬁled in similar mode as in SM. If the residence
time is found to be too short, then the barrel zones temperatures will be proﬁled in
a reverse mode with a higher temperature at the back of the barrel and the desired
temperature at the front.
2.3.2.3 Mould temperature
There are no speciﬁc recommendations within the ACG methodology for the selection
of the mould temperature. The general recommendation is to use the mean value of
the range presented by the resin manufacturer. [9] Small deviations from this value
can be used to inﬂuence factors such as demoulding or aesthetics of the part. However,
it is well known, that in the case of µ-IM, a higher mould temperature is beneﬁcial
for the ﬁll of sub-micron and micro-features. [18]
2.3.2.4 Pressure test
The key aspect of ACG is the process of selection of a speed proﬁle for the injection
phase. The logic behind the ACG method comes from the observation that when a
mould is ﬁlled freely, i.e., without a forced injection speed, the pressure would quickly
rise and then stabilize for the whole injection. In the ACG methodology, this is called
the "natural ﬁlling pressure", other authors refer to it as "stable ﬁlling". [19]
==PAGE:30==
2 State of the Art
24
To determine the injection speed and injection screw positions, it is ﬁrst necessary to
ﬁnd out the ideal injection time. In SM, this is done by using the rheology curve as
explained in Section 2.2.2.4. In ACG, this is done in a similar fashion by performing a
pressure test and creating a pressure versus ﬁll time curve. This is done by following
this procedure:
1. Set the melt and mould temperatures according to Sections 2.3.2.2 and 2.3.2.3.
2. Using a ﬂat proﬁle set a high speed, i.e., around 80 % of the maximum nominal
speed of the machine.
3. Set the dose and transfer positions according Section 2.3.2.1.
4. Set a low pressure limit. This value will depend on the geometry of the part
but it is recommended to start with a very low pressure (20 % of the maximal
nominal pressure of the machine). If an incomplete part cannot be ejected, it
might be necessary to set a high enough pressure to ﬁll the part and allow eject.
5. Make a shot and record the nominal pressure and ﬁll time.
6. Gradually increase the injection pressure and record the ﬁll times.
7. Stop the test when, after increasing the pressure, the ﬁll time remains the same
or the part exhibits ﬂash.
8. Create a pressure versus ﬁll time plot.
9. Select a ﬁll time in the left part of the plot in the region where pressure changes
do not considerably impact the ﬁll time. This time will be the objective ﬁll time
for the injection phase.
The objective of the injection speed proﬁle in the ACG methodology is to replicate
the natural movement of the screw and achieve an injection phase with a constant
pressure, while at the same time achieving the desired ﬁll time. It is impossible to
determine the exact positions and speeds that should be programmed at the machine
as they heavily depend on the geometry of the mould and the machine’s acceleration
ramps but a good approximation can be calculated and then manually adjusted until
the curve has the desired shape.
For most of the cases, when limiting the injection pressure during ﬁll, the shape that
the injection rate curve takes is close to a parabola. Since the desired injection time is
known (from the Pressure Test), it is possible to create an injection curve discretized
into proﬁles that simulates a parabola.

2.3.2.6 Holding pressure
After the injection stage is ﬁnished, there will be a completely full part inside the
cavity, as the plastic cools down it will start to shrink and material will be needed to
compensate for this. The holding pressure should be selected at approximately 80 %
of the transfer pressure to ensure that plastic will not ﬂow out of the mould and back
into the barrel. During this stage, it is necessary to pay attention to the position of
the screw (or speed of the screw) as a function of time. The screw should only move
slightly forwards and should not be allowed to bounce back. It can be concluded that,
if the screw is not moving forward or backwards the volume of plastic inside the cavity
is kept constant. In some cases, it might be necessary to make small adjustments to
the holding pressure to manipulate aspects such as gloss, dimensions or if there are
problems with the ejection system.

2.3.2.7 Barrel temperature test
As explained in Section 2.3.2.2, it is desired to achieve the mean of the temperature
operation range at the front of the barrel while having a complete melting of the
pellets. In the industry, it is often seen that although molten regions of the resin have
the desired temperature, there are still small pellets that have not reached a liquid
state.
It can be complicated to measure the real melt temperature since the internal tem-
perature sensors of the barrel often have insuﬃcient precision [9] and other methods
==PAGE:36==
2 State of the Art
30
for measuring it, such as measuring the temperature of an air-shot or purge with a
probe thermometer are also unreliable because of the fast cooling of the plastic and
the impossibility of detecting small un-melted pellets inside the purge.
In ACG, an experiment is performed to deal with this uncertainty and is based on the
indirect measurement of temperature by using the ability of the machine to reﬂect
viscosity changes on the injection pressure and cushion position.
The experiment is explained by the following steps:
1. Let the machine run and reach an steady state (until the shot-to-shot variations
on the cushion position are not considerable, the variation considered acceptable
would depend on the screw diameter and the precision of the machine) and take
note of the cushion position and transfer pressure.
2. Increase the cooling time so that the overall cycle is 10 % longer.
3. Let the process stabilize and take note of the cushion position and transfer
pressure.
4. Reduce the cooling time to the original value.
5. Increase the barrel temperature if needed.
6. Repeat the experiment if necessary until, with the new proﬁle, no variation is
observed on the position of the cushion.
By doing this test, two contrary eﬀects are introduced. The mould gets colder so
an increase in the cushion position is expected (less material can enter the mould)
while an increase in residence time means that the plastic had more time to melt and
the viscosity to decrease, leading to a decrease in the cushion position. The transfer
pressure also gives information about the viscosity of the material.
If a decrease in the cushion position is observed, it can be concluded that the melting
of the resin was improved and the viscosity of the resin was lowered.
Then, the
temperature proﬁle in the barrel can be modiﬁed by using steps with increments from
the desired value at the nozzle to higher values at the hopper end, i.e., an increasing
proﬁle until no variation in the cushion position is observed. The required increments
are normally between 1 ◦C to 5 ◦C. Moreover, this would normally only occur for very
fast cycles.
It will not normally be needed but the temperature proﬁle can also be set in a de-
creasing fashion if plastic degradation has been observed. This could happen if the
barrel size is over-dimensioned and the residence time too long.

As a starting value, the back-pressure can be selected as 10 % of the maximal value of
the machine. This value will normally give the best results in terms of stability and is
a common practice in other methodologies as well. [4] However a simple experiment
can be performed by varying the back-pressure from 5 % to 20 % of the maximal

amorphous nature comes from the fact that the bulky cyclic part in the backbone
reduces the mobility of the chains and impedes crystal formation.
Among the many properties of COC, there are: excellent clarity in the UV and visible

insulation; high glass transition temperature (up to 250 ◦C); good recyclability and no
generation of harmful gases when destroyed and, low resistance against hydrocarbons,
in which it dissolves easily. [20]

3.3 Preliminary Process Development
Before the present project, it was not clear whether it was possible to mould COC
in the Sensor Mould as several attempts had been previously made without success.
The problem arose during the ejection of the part when the pillars would stick inside
the cavity and break during the ejection. If that happens, it is necessary to remove
the mould from the machine, disassemble it and clean it with solvents.
It was hypothesized that the origin of this problem radicated in the compression and
packing during the injection and holding stages and possibly due to the temperature
of the mould. It was decided by the author to attempt a fast and, as unstressed as
possible moulding to test the ejection. The cooling water system was set at 70 ◦C.
A pre-existing program was used as a template for the process parameters. The press
movements were optimized to improve the cycle and to ensure a consistent ejection
of the part. Special care was taken for ensuring a proper mould safety by setting a
strict time limit for the closing stage and a limit on the force during the last stage of
closing. Mould safety was tested by placing a bent piece of paper on one of the mould
faces and attempting closing.
The injection speed was risen and no holding stage was programmed. A ﬁrst part was
moulded using dried resin and the ejection was found to be adequate.

3.4 Clamp Force Experiment
Clamp force is an important parameter in injection moulding. It refers to the force
that the press unit applies during the ﬁlling, holding and cooling stages to keep the
mould closed. It is normally expressed in tonnes and the maximal tonnage of the
machine is commonly displayed within its model.
The force used to close the mould can inﬂuence the quality of the part in several
ways: if the force is too high, it can actually squeeze the mould and damage the
venting channels or other components such as tilted beams or sliding inserts. If vents
are pressed too much, air will be impeded from escaping the mould and could lead
to part defects such as air bubbles or diesel eﬀect in the plastic. If the force is too
low, the mould could be opened by the plastic pressure during the ﬁlling and holding
phases, resulting in ﬂash.
==PAGE:44==
3 Experimental Procedures
38
It is diﬃcult to estimate the required force for a determined mould but a rule of thumb
is normally used:
ton = (AC · Cn + Ar) · P
(3.1)
Where, ton is the required clamp force; AC is the projected area of the cavities; Cn
is the number of cavities; Ar is the projected area of the runner system and, P is
the maximal injection pressure when the part is ﬁlled, this could happen during the
injection stage or during the holding stage depending on the geometry of the part.
The term projected area refers to the area of the part normal to the injection unit. In
reality, the force required to keep the mould closed will also depend on the thickness,
material, temperature, etc. It is diﬃcult to estimate the injection pressure without
knowledge of the process but there are some guiding values that are accepted by the
industry:

cm2

4.3 Clamp Force Experiment
An experiment was conducted using the setting described in Section 3.4. The experi-
ment was started using 400 kN (40.14 ton) as nominal clamp force with the real force
being close to 415 kN (41.6 ton). During injection, there was no noticeable movement
of the mould. Because of this, it can be concluded that the plastic pressure does not
open the mould.
The clamp force was lowered gradually using 300 kN (30.1 ton), 200 kN (20.1 ton), 100
kN (10.05 ton), 80 kN (8.03 ton) and 60 kN (6.02 ton) as steps. It was determined by
means of a face dial that the mould was not opened by the plastic pressure in any case.
However, when 60 kN was chosen as the nominal value, the actual value measured by
the machine was 72 kN (7.22 ton). This means that, at that force level, the machine
is not capable of keeping the mould closed and it compensates by applying a higher
tonnage.
Part thickness was measured at both cavities for samples of all conditions. The results
are shown in Table A.7. A small, non-statistically representative, batch of samples
was measured, by means of LOM, to investigate if signiﬁcant diﬀerences could be seen
for pillar heights (see Table A.6).
It is clear that there is no direct inﬂuence of clamp force on the thickness of the part
or pillar height. This means that the mould does not open during the injection and
hold stages. This reinforces the observations made using the face dial.
Because of the results of both experiments, it was determined that subsequent ex-
periments should be conducted using a clamp force of 100 kN. This is, in itself, an
improvement to the process as less force is applied to the mould. Less force translates
to a better preservation of both mould and toggle system and less energy is applied
by the machine. It could also improve the venting of the mould.

4.7.4 Plastic Pressure
The injection moulding machine used in this study has the ability to record some key
features of the process. It is equipped with a pressure sensor located on the front of
the barrel that gives information about the pressure on the plastic resin. From Tables
A.4 and A.5, it can be seen that the maximal plastic pressure is lower for ACG1,
approximately 567 bar, compared to the approximately 710 bar of SM1. The fact
that the plastic pressure rises is a consequence of a higher injection speed.
A lower injection pressure is desirable because the stress applied on the mould is
lowered, thus, the clamp force required to keep the mould closed is reduced. This
improves the mould’s and machine’s service-life, and reduces the machine’s energy
consumption.
However, it is interesting to see that this higher pressure for SM1 is not reﬂected by
the in-cavity measurements in which ACG1 showed a higher pressure.

in the in-cavity sensor recordings.

hand, the pressure integral for the ﬁrst 7 s of ﬁll and holding phases is considerably

4.7.7 Process Stability
Both optimized processes were used to mould parts on diﬀerent days (at least 3) to
test the stability of the results found. It was determined that for both, the 3 key
parameters: maximal pressure, switch-over pressure and cushion position were stable
for all runs. The deviations are shown in Tables A.4 and A.5.
The process window experiment can be seen as an equivalent of introducing drastic
variations into the process. For example, rising the temperature of the melt can be
seen as reducing the viscosity of the resin which would also be the eﬀect of, e.g.,
a batch with a higher melt ﬂow index (shorter chain length and lower viscosity);
lowering the hold pressure can simulate a machine failure; a change in the mould
temperature could be a consequence of a cooling channel been blocked; etc. By doing
this mental exercise, and, revising the results from the interaction plots for ACG1
and SM1 (Figures 4.2 and 4.3) it can be seen that ACG1 is more robust in terms of
micro-replication when dealing with perturbations to the original process.
As mentioned in Section 4.7.1, ACG1 process exhibited for all measured pillars a lower
standard deviation than SM1. This is, directly, a measure of process stability within
one batch of samples.
For a better comparison of the stability of the two processes, more experiments are
required.

Conclusion 5
Mould characterization showed that at a meso-scale, the mould has dimensions that
are consistent to the nominal dimensions. At a micro-scale level, the real dimensions,
as characterized by the pillar heights, are systematically shorter than the nominal
dimension.
It was demonstrated that is possible to injection mould COC successfully with the
studied mould using cooling water at 70 ◦C and 85 ◦C, which is considerably lower
than the parameters recommended by the resin manufacturer.
Using COC resin, both, ACG and SM methods proved that are capable of generating
a reliable set up of process parameters. The processes were obtained by following
meticulously the instructions detailed in Robust Process Development and Scientiﬁc
Molding [4] for the SM method and, Inyección de Plásticos Básico [9] for the ACG
method.
It is possible to characterize the micro-structures on a microinjection moulding part
in COC by means of LOM with enough accuracy and precision to allow conclusions
to be drawn.
It is possible to replicate micro-features on the surface of a plastic part in COC using
injection moulding processing techniques.
SM proved that is possible to replicate micro-features to a certain extent following a
simple procedure that takes an skilled operator a few hours to perform. By studying
the process window corners obtained during the SM process development, it can be
concluded that the center of the process window was not optimal in terms of micro-
replication.
ACG Method proved that is possible to replicate micro-features by following simple
and clear instructions and without any external aid at the machine. The procedure
takes an skilled operator less than a couple of hours. Although, process windows are
foreign to the ACG method, it can be concluded, from the pillar height measurements
of the process window corners, that the original process can be improved in terms of
its micro-replication ability.
Both optimized processes showed consistent shrinkage when compared to reported
shrinkage rates and, no evident warpage was observed.
No signiﬁcant diﬀerences in terms of mass or meso-scale dimensions (thickness, length
and width) were observed between parts obtained following the SM and ACG method-
ologies.
==PAGE:85==
79
The ACG optimized process exhibited a wider process window in terms of replication
of micro-features as showed by the measurements at the process window corners and,
in general, exhibited a better ability to replicate micro-features.
No clear and direct eﬀect of mould temperature, melt temperature and hold pressure
could be seen for both methods. This shows the huge inﬂuence of the injection speed
and pressure on the micro-replication ability of a process. This is a consequence of the
complicated interactions between these parameters as showed by the Carreau-Yasuda
and WFL models.
Regarding the pressure inside the cavity, which is a parameter that is closely related
to the micro-replication ability of the mould, it was observed that the sensors located
on the part stopped sensing the eﬀect of the hold pressure before the sensors located
on the runner. This is a consequence of the gate freezing. When comparing ACG1
and SM1, it was seen that the pressure diﬀerence among the sensors was smaller for
ACG1, indicating a more homogeneous pressure inside the cavity. Another diﬀerence
that was observed was a higher pressure at all sensors for the ACG1 process.
In general, ACG1 process showed a good replication ability and the recorded in-
cavity pressure curve could be used as objective for an online control system to ensure
replication during manufacturing.
ACG methodology proved to be an easy and reliable method for setting-up injection
moulding machines and demonstrated its ability for the replication of features at a
micro-scale level, aspect that had not been tested before.
The main objective of the project was fulﬁlled, as it was demonstrated that is possible
to optimize the injection moulding process by controlling the injection pressure to
improve the replication of micro-features.
Secondary objectives were fulﬁlled: two injection moulding methodologies for process
parameters setting were compared; micro-features were characterized on cavity and
parts; in-cavity sensors were used to characterize the processes; two DOE studies were
performed to study the inﬂuence of three major injection moulding parameters on the
micro-replication.
In general, this project demonstrated that a pressure-driven approach to injection
moulding has advantages over traditional methods in terms of micro-replication and,
that there is an important area of opportunity for further research in this ﬁeld.
==PAGE:86==
Future Perspective 6
Being the present topic novel and, this study considerably ambitious, there is a great
area of opportunity for research and experimentation with the techniques presented
and the knowledge generated.
First, a better characterization of the mould is recommended. In the present study,
only 8 out of the 100 micro-pillars on one of the cavities and none of the 36 pillars
on the runner system have been characterized.
It is, especially, recommended to
characterize pillars on the other cavity and on the runner system.
Second, to improve the comparison of the two methodologies presented and used in
this study, it is recommended:
• To mould and characterize more batches of samples with the same optimized
process parameters for both methodologies to generate a study of capability
indexes.
• To test both methodologies for diﬀerent resin families. It would be especially
interesting to test on semi-crystalline materials with a larger tendency to shrink.
• To test the methodologies for diﬀerent machine-mould combinations, for IM and
µ-IM parts.
• To compare the processes in terms of mechanical properties of the parts, stability
as measured by process capabilities, eﬃciency and scrap.
• To test both methodologies for the replication of features at a nano-level.
Third, to improve the replication of micro-features on the tested mould some aspects
could be taken into account:
• Modify the mould for the addition of vents.
• Process at higher mould temperature.
Fourth, in order to increment the knowledge about the injection moulding process:
• Develop a setting for the Moldﬂo R
⃝to exploit its capabilities and extract infor-
mation about the cooling and heating of the mould within one injection cycle.
• To use the melt ﬂow index (MFI) as an indirect measurement of chain length
and molecular weight and relate it to processing degradation.
Fifth, to use the obtained knowledge to generate a system to control process variables
in order to maintain a desired pressure curve to ensure replication of the micro-
features.
