ABSTRACT 
Properties of the fiber/matrix interface greatly influence the properties of composite 
materials. Thus, knowing its characteristics is of key importance. A new experimental 
method was developed to obtain parameters of a micromechanical model 
characterizing the interface in terms of shear stress, debond energy and residual 
thermomechanical stresses. The model addresses both debonding and pull-out. The 
method utilizes digital image correlation and tracking as a principle and will be used 
to measure debond length and relative displacement between fiber and matrix. 
Parameters from the obtained data will be extracted two separate ways; substituting 
values derived from measurements into the model and through nonlinear regression. 
The method will also be used for extensometry in tensile testing. 
In this work, the model will be presented, extracted parameters will be shown, 
values to be measured, necessary equipment and methods will be listed. Details of 
the development process will disseminated step by step. Ways of processing 
measurement data will be demonstrated, along with evaluation of said data; possible 
error sources will be identified and methods to overcome them will be suggested. 
The method will be validated three separate ways: (i) it will be used for extensometry 
to obtain the stress-strain curve of fibers with known Young’s moduli, (ii) 
displacement data obtained via the method will be compared to that from a tensile 
testing machine and (iii) parameters extracted through different procedures will be 
compared to both one another and to results obtained during previous 
investigations.

1. INTRODUCTION 
1.1. Context 
Composites gain more and more ground as structural materials. Characterization of 
the fiber/matrix interface is of key importance in order to assess and predict 
mechanical properties, behavior, lifespan, and eventual failure of composite 
materials, both on micro and on macro scale. Throughout the history of composite 
materials, numerous models have been established to characterize the fiber/matrix 
interface with ever-increasing accuracy. New and improved measurement methods 
thus need to be developed to cope with the demand for precision of contemporary 
micromechanical models. 
1.2. Problem definition 
The micromechanical model developed at DTU Wind Energy (henceforth Risø 
model) is fairly simple, consistent and well-researched. However, being a theory 
developed fairly recently, it lacks the sufficient amount of experimental data to back 
it up. To gather more data, a robust experimental setup needs to be built. These two 
problems will be addressed in this work, where we develop a reliable measurement 
method and use it to gather experimental data. 
1.3. Objective 
The aim is to develop an experimental setup and method based on digital image 
correlation and tracking to provide reliable, reproducible and relatively simple 
means for determining parameters in the Risø model. The method is a direct 
descendent and augmentation of that developed and used by Durai Prabhakaran and 
his coworkers [2] between 2011 and 2013. The second goal is to use the developed 
method to collect, evaluate and interpret interface parameters described by the 
model. 
1.4. Scope and limitations 
1. This work primarily focuses on developing the experimental method and not 
the model. Implications of the model will not be challenged in any shape or 
form. 
2. Number of measurement-grade specimens was scarce. 
3. Specimens used for the experiments are 5-7 years old and little is known about 
manufacturing circumstances, let alone storage conditions and aging 
==PAGE:9==
  
9 
properties (e.g. water uptake, UV and oxidative degradation, etc.). According 
to supervisor Hans Lilholt, odd and inconsistent datasets are to be expected. 
4. According to the author’s knowledge, steel fiber is not widely used as 
reinforcing material in polymeric composites. 
5. Being restricted to the visible range of the electromagnetic spectrum, the 
method can only be used on composites with transparent matrices. 
6. The development process involved a lot of trial and error. 
7. Due to circumstances beyond the reach of the author, three different cameras 
were used for a total of 16 measurements, which is assumed to have reduced 
consistency. 
8. Lens distortions were not accounted for.

2. BACKGROUND 
2.1. Composite materials 
Composites in general represent a material family along with ceramics, metals and 
polymers [3]. Composites are multiphase materials consisting of one or more 
reinforcing or filling materials (typically fibers) and embedding materials, also 
known as matrices. There exists an excellent adhesive connection between the two; 
said connection persists at sustained stress and deformation, provided loads remain 
within boundaries the material was designed for. Fibers are usually hard and rigid 
while matrices are usually softer and more ductile [4]. 
According to Callister [5], a composite is considered to be any multiphase material 
that exhibits a significant proportion of the properties of both constituent phases 
such that a better combination of properties is realized. Phases differ significantly in 
terms of structure and properties even if they are chemically similar [4]. Since 
composites form and occur naturally (e.g. wood, bone), another criterion has to be 
added in the present context apart from those above: a composite material is 
artificially made, i.e. manufactured [5,6]. 
2.1.1. Typical reinforcements/fillers 
Reinforcement materials are typically fibers, though they come in various other 
shapes as well like particles or flakes. The main reason for them being fibrous is due 
to their applications – the direction in which they have the greatest strength needs to 
be aligned with the direction of the load [4–7]. As for materials, the most widely used 
reinforcement is, by far, glass fiber due to its low cost and high strength-to-density 
ratio [6,7]. Other common fibrous reinforcing materials include carbon fiber (low 
density, high Young’s modulus, highly anisotropic), polyethylene fiber (low density, 
high tensile strength, expensive, not suitable for high temperature), aramid fiber 
(high strength, high stiffness, good thermal resistance, expensive) [4,6,7]. 
Typical particle reinforcements/fillers are carbon black in plastics and elastomers and 
gravel in concrete [5]. 
2.1.2. Typical matrices 
Polymeric matrix materials can be divided into two groups: thermoplastics and 
thermosets. Common thermoset matrices are unsaturated polyester & vinyl ester 
resins and epoxies [5,6]; for high service temperatures, polyimide resins are typically 
used [5,6].

2.1.3. Applications 
Composite materials are utilized on a wide variety of fields. They are highly sought 
after where low weight, high strength, customizability and corrosion resistance is 
needed, like recreational and professional sports (canoes, oars, skis, fishing rods), 
aerospace (airplane and rocket parts, ultralight aircraft, gliders), transportation 
(lightweight chassis, tires), renewable energy (wind turbine blades), military (firearm 
grips and stocks, bulletproof vests). Composites have also known to supersede other 
materials on various fields – think of composite propane cylinders or wood-plastic 
composite outdoor benches. Non-polymeric composites are also widely used, e.g. 
plywood, reinforced concrete, high-end brake disks and gas turbine blades (inspired 
by [8]). 
2.2. Micromechanical models of the fiber/matrix interface 
The behavior of a composite material is a result of the combined behavior of the 
following three entities: fiber or reinforcing elements; matrix; fiber/matrix interface 
[6]. There is a general consensus in the literature that mechanical properties of the 
fiber/matrix interface strongly influence those of the product, e.g. strength, 
toughness, elastic and flexural moduli and more [2,9–12].  
According to Zinck et al. [13], micromechanical models can be divided into two 
groups: stress criterion (most commonly stress peak)-based ones and energy-based 
approaches. Early interfacial shear stress (henceforth IFSS)-based models include 
Cox’ shear lag model [14], the constant shear stress model by Kelly and Tyson [15], 
Greszczuk’s stress criterion model [16], the model of Takaku and Arridge [17], and so 
on. These models have been improved over time by taking one or more of the 
following phenomena into consideration: stresses in the matrix, friction, residual 
stresses due to differences in thermal expansion between the fiber and the matrix and 
Poisson effects. These improvements were motivated by observations indicating that 
fiber/matrix debonding takes place progressively [9], and is nonlinear in nature 
beyond a certain critical length [18]. While these models are widely used due to their 
simplicity, there are disputes over their accuracy [8]. Zinck et al. [13] conclude that 
models based on either average or maximum IFSS alone can lead to biased results, 
thus the use of energy-based models is preferred. Hutchinson and Jensen [19] 
showed however that shear-lag-based models can be good approximations once the 
debond length is longer than about 2 … 3 ⋅𝑟𝑓 [9]. 
==PAGE:12==
  
12 
Energy-based models utilize fracture mechanics, account for energy stored in the 
embedded fiber, and usually aim to determine interfacial fracture energy as a 
function of maximum load or a derived value thereof [8]. Notable models include 
those by Jiang et al. [20], Gao et al. [21], and numerous models and improvements by 
Nairn with various coworkers. These too, may or may not incorporate effects of 
certain phenomena like residual stresses, friction, Poisson effect and pre-crack [8]. 
Models accounting for said phenomena however are usually quite complicated and 
require not-so-efficient numerical studies to obtain parameters from [22]. 
 
As a summary, IFSS-based models are simple but sometimes inaccurate, energy-
based models are more accurate but usually complicated, thus the need for a 
compromise has arisen. 
2.3. The Risø model 
This section is based on [2,9,22,23]. Other references are cited in-text. 
The Risø model is a simple analytical shear-lag model that concerns pull-out and 
single-fiber fragmentation, has similarities to an advanced shear-lag model by 
Hutchinson and Jensen [19] and a fracture mechanics-based model by Kessler et al. 
[24], and aims to characterize the fiber/matrix interface in terms of three parameters, 
namely (i) fiber/matrix debond energy 𝐺𝐼𝐼𝑐, (ii) interfacial shear stress 𝜏𝑠 and (iii) 
fiber/matrix mismatch strain Δ𝜖𝑇, through which the model incorporates residual 
stresses. The model neglects Poisson effects, thus making it possible to be simplified 
to one dimension. 𝐺𝐼𝐼𝑐 is assumed to represent the chemical nature and bonding of 
the fiber surface to the matrix, 𝜏𝑠 is assumed to represent topography and frictional 
sliding of the fiber relative to the matrix, and Δ𝜖𝑇 is caused by the thermo-elastic 
differences between fiber and matrix, i.e. it accounts for different coefficients of 
thermal expansion of the fiber and that of the matrix.  
The model has the following experimental parameters: the load on the fiber or on the 
composite 𝑃𝑓/𝑃𝑐, the debond length 𝑙𝑑 and the relative displacement between the 
fiber and the matrix 𝛿, ergo these are to be measured. In order to obtain model 
parameters 𝜏𝑠, 𝐺𝐼𝐼𝑐 and Δ𝜖𝑇 based solely on measurements, one must first express 
model parameters with experimental parameters as independent variables.

2.4. Exposure in digital photography 
This section is but a very brief introduction to the topic that needs to be provided in

A digital photo is created when light passes through the camera lens and strikes the

three main factors [25,26]:

d. The lighting at the scene: ambient and supplementary light.

3.4. Methods 
3.4.1. Single-fiber pull-out test 
There are multiple methods for measuring fiber/matrix adhesion and interfacial 
properties, e.g. microbond test, single fiber fragmentation test, fiber push-out test, 
etc. Single-fiber pull-out test is an experimental method for characterizing the 
fiber/matrix interface. As its name suggests, a single fiber is embedded in the matrix 
material then pulled out while measuring force, displacement, and possibly other 
parameters. These tests can provide information about IFSS, but are not very useful 
when it comes to commercially available composites. The most common result is a 
peak load corresponding to fiber/matrix debonding and a frictional load 
corresponding to pull-out [6]. In case of the Risø model, it can also be used to 
determine 𝐺𝐼𝐼𝑐 and Δ𝜖𝑇 (Section 2.3), which is one of the key points of this work. 
In the Risø model, two types are addressed [9,23]. 
a. Specimen is clamped at its end opposite to the one where the fiber is loaded in 
tension (matrix is stress-free), denoted as PO-1. 
b. Specimen supported in the matrix end where the fiber is loaded in tension, 
denoted PO-2 (composite at the uncracked end is stress-free). 
See Section 2.2 for more information on interface characterization. 
3.4.2. Digital image correlation 
Digital image correlation (DIC) is a class of non-contacting methods that acquire 
images of an object, store images in digital form and perform image analysis to 
extract shape, deformation, rotation and motion data [51]. The idea behind the 
method is to derive the displacement of the object (material) being tested by tracking 
the motion of a unique pattern applied to the to the component’s surface [52]. DIC 
may be performed in two or three dimensions [51–53]. 
In general, it is not possible to find the correspondence of one pixel in a subsequent 
image. Instead, a small neighborhood around the pixel of interest is considered to 
form a more characteristic pattern to detect in the subsequent images. This small 
neighborhood is usually referred to as subset, window, or facet [51,53]. Facet 
matching has been performed with many types of patterns, e.g. lines, grids, dots, 
with the most common one being random patterns [53].  
Apart from applied mechanics and materials research, DIC is important in many 
practical applications like process control, geological mapping and robotics [53]. 
 
Theoretical aspects of DIC bear no significance in this work, hence they are not 
disclosed. 
==PAGE:26==
  
26 
3.4.3. Curve fitting with nonlinear regression 
Curve fitting, also known as regression analysis, is used to find the "best fit" line or 
curve for a series of data points [54]. For a wide range of engineering problems, there 
are three usually three reasons why one might want fit a curve to a given set of data 
[55]: 
a. Just plot the data and draw a smooth curve through the points to aid 
visualizing functional dependency. 
b. Fit a mathematical model to the data and analyze the goodness of the fit. 
c. Fit a particular physical model with unknown parameters to the data and 
determine said parameters. 
In this case, c. is required. MATLAB’s Curve Fitting Toolbox uses nonlinear least 
squares curve fitting [50], the method of which will be briefly introduced. 
 
Nonlinear least squares curve fitting allows fitting a general nonlinear equation with 
one or more unknown coefficients to a set of experimental data and minimize the 
square error between the fitting equation and the experimental data. The process 
begins by an initial guess of the fitting parameters then the equation is linearized 
around the parameters and solved for minimal square error, yielding a new set of 
fitting parameters. The process is iterated until a consistent set of parameters is 
obtained. While this method is powerful and known to yield accurate values in many 
cases, one should never blindly accept the results of a curve fit – engineering 
judgment should always be used [55].

Search for suitable DIC software then began. Three were tried initially: Ncorr, an 
open source MATLAB plugin [56]; TrackMate [57], a plugin for the open source 
image processing program ImageJ [58] and finally GOM Correlate which can be 
regarded as the DIC module of the proprietary motion and deformation measuring 
system ARAMIS [47], and is free to download. The former two were (subjectively)

Literature states that a highly contrasted, stochastic and isotropic pattern should be 
applied to the area of interest for high-quality DIC measurements [51,53,56,59]. This 
was deemed unfeasible for the following reasons: 
a. Fibers have diameters – and hence apparent widths when projected to two-
dimensional space – in the sub-millimeter range. Speckles would need to have 
diameters of 0.001…0.01 mm which requires specialized depositing 
equipment. 
b. The debond front [19], one of the features to be tracked, cannot have any 
pattern applied to. 
c. Applying a pattern along the to-be embedded length of the fiber would alter 
its surface conditions, thus introducing a bias to model parameters. 
An alternative had to be found. Based on a distinction between different image 
matching methods, i.e. those based on brightness change, gray values, and features 
in consecutive images as described in [53,60,61], a simple experiment was carried out 
on legacy footage in order to test whether GOM Correlate was able to track the 
debond front without any pre-applied speckles, based on differences to its 
surroundings or on its shape only. The test was successful, as can be seen in Figure 8.

4.2. Test procedure – force and image acquisition 
With the setup complete, actual measurements could take place. A total of sixteen 
specimens from two different batches had been tested; test parameters for individual 
specimens can be found in Table 15. Pictures of tested specimens are in Appendix B. 
 
 
Figure 20 As-manufactured geometry of the specimens. 
 
Having an as-manufactured geometry as depicted in Figure 20, specimens had been 
cut into two pieces to increase the possible number of tests and thus data yield. A 
typical test sequence was carried out as follows: 
1. Setup was prepared for measurement: camera was attached to the tripod 
head; screen (i.e. that for the camera) and flashlight were switched on and the 
camera was connected to the screen via an HDMI cable. Test protocol in 
Bluehill 3 was modified if necessary in terms of maximum load, sampling rate 
and crosshead speed, and the test program was loaded. Overhead lights in the 
laboratory were switched off. 
2. Specimen was clamped into the lower grip vertically, preferably with the fiber 
end being located under the frontmost groove of the capstan. 
3. Force in Bluehill 3 was balanced, i.e. its apparent value was set to zero. This 
step was incorrectly omitted while testing the first batch of specimens. 
4. The fiber was wound around the capstan twofold then its end was threaded 
into the tangent bore located at the base of the capstan’s top cross-beam. 
5. Holding the fiber end under slight tension so that it does not unwind from the 
grooves, the øM6 bolt on the front of the capstan was tightened – first 
manually then with a hex key – anchoring the fiber. 
6. The fiber and the matrix were cleaned and degreased with 96 V/V% ethanol. 
7. Marking agent (Section 3.1.4) was applied at four loci: 
a. On the front of the matrix, near the top 
b. On the fiber, close to the matrix 
c. On the fiber, approximately halfway between the bottom part of the 
capstan and the matrix 
d. On the fiber, near the bottom part of the capstan – in the camera’s field 
of view, the smudge should not overlap with the capstan 
Stroking the uncured marking agent with a thin object increases its roughness, 
thus its traceability. Marking agent was then allowed to cure for 1-2 minutes. 
8. Exposure settings of the camera were modified if necessary and the camera 
was focused on the fiber. Horizontal axis of the viewfinder was aligned with 
the matrix. Synchronizing LED was brought into the camera’s field of view.

4.3. Processing raw data, DIC, data integration and parameter extraction 
With the first iteration, it was tested whether it was better to use a video as-is or use 
individual frames as pictures. As frame extraction did not provide any benefits, 
increased processing time and disk space usage, importing videos directly was 
deemed sufficient. 
4.3.1. Preprocessing 
If the testing machine’s sampling rate and the camera’s frame rate were different 
during the test, there are two tested ways to synchronize them: 
a. Converting the video to another frame rate 
b. Fitting a linear curve to the 𝑃(𝑡) data points acquired by the testing machine 
then substituting video frame timestamps into said curve’s equation (only 
used on BK 11/048 Long01 due to rate mismatch: the testing machine recorded 
data at a rate of 23.81 [1/s]; frame rate of Nikon D7000 was 23.98 fps during the 
same measurement while both devices indicating 24 [1/s] / fps). 
Frame rate conversion can be carried out via various pieces of software – in this 
work, VLC Media Player (Section 3.3.3) was used. It is worth mentioning that audio 
tracks are of no use in these measurements and can be discarded – or not recorded in 
the first place – in order to reduce disk space usage. 
 
==PAGE:42==

This process can also be used if the video format recorded by the camera cannot be 
loaded into either Tracker or GOM Correlate directly. 
4.3.2. Digital image correlation and tracking 
This section branches into two: using Tracker to track 𝑙𝑑 and 𝑙𝑑 and GOM Correlate 
to track 𝛿 and Δ𝐿𝑓. 
 
a. Using Tracker to obtain 𝒍𝒅(𝒕) and 𝒍𝒔(𝒕) 
After preparing the video as described in the previous section, it was loaded into 
Tracker. Calibration was carried out via the physical measuring tape attached to the 
lower grip. The virtual coordinate system’s origin was then moved to the matrix’ 
non-gripped end and the horizontal axis were aligned with the fiber, with the 
direction toward the grip being positive. After defining the origin and the scale based 
on a known length, visual cues, i.e. the LED signal, were looked for. Once the first 
frame in which the LED is fully extinguished was located, that particular frame could 
be set as the first, and the timer set to zero, thereby synchronizing data from Tracker 
and data recorded by the testing machine. A visible debond front was then searched 
for; once it propagated sufficiently far from the end of the matrix, tracking could 
begin via defining the debond front as a pattern to trace and starting the Autotracker 
feature (Figure 21). Tests were carried out to determine which Autotracker 
parameters to use: evolution rate (changes in color and shape of the template) should 
be kept at 0% and Automark score (determining how good matches need to be in 
order to get accepted) should be at least 6/10. Once tracking had been finished, 𝑙𝑑(𝑡) 
could be copied and pasted into a spreadsheet. 𝑙𝑠(𝑡) (Figure 27) could be obtained in 
a similar fashion via tracking the embedded fiber’s end sufficiently far from the 
clamp (Figure 22). 
 
In case of specimens from batch BK 11/047, debonding was unstable. The debond 
front did not propagate as a linear function of force but in a start/stop (“jump”) 
fashion. Due to this, autotracking failed in most cases and debonding had to be 
followed manually (still in Tracker). Number of data points obtained in this fashion 
was scarce – this issue was overcome by fitting a linear function onto said 𝑙𝑑(𝑡) 
points (as 𝑙𝑑(𝑡) was still linear overall) then substituting timestamps of other 
measurements into the 𝑙𝑑= 𝑎⋅𝑡+ 𝑏 equation.

5.4.2. Evaluation of extracted model parameters: issues and possible solutions 
If one looks at extracted model parameters (Table 17 and Table 18), some errors are 
immediately apparent. 𝜏𝑠 𝑆𝐿1 and 𝜏𝑠 𝑆𝐿2 are not equal while they should be, and 
some values downright make no physical sense, namely: if 𝜏𝑠 was indeed negative as 
some tests suggest, that would imply that interfacial shear stress aids debonding and 
pull-out rather than obstructing it, which would contradict the second law of 
thermodynamics. In crude terms: if that was indeed true, the fiber would come flying 
out of the matrix all by itself. 
 
As for Δ𝜖𝑇 being negative with some tests, this also implies an incorrect physical 
meaning, i.e. coefficient of thermal expansion of the fiber is greater than that of the 
matrix. While thermal expansion data is not provided in the TDSs of either the fiber 
or the matrix, according to Granta CES [3], coefficient of thermal expansion of 
unsaturated polyester is 7…16 times greater than that of steel.  
Plotting Δ𝜖𝑇(𝑃𝑓) curves also revealed they were not constant (Figure 33) despite [2] 
stating they should be after the initial unstable debonding phase. 
 
Figure 33 Non-constant Δ𝜖𝑇(𝑃𝑓) curve (example). Specimen: BK 11/048 Short 06.

5.4.3. Possible explanations for deviations 
There are a few possible explanations for deviations in 𝐸𝑓 and the need for zero 
correction in 𝛿. As for 𝐸𝑓, 𝑃𝑓 (and hence 𝜎𝑓) values are assumed to be correct so error 
is assumed to originate from length measurements. Aside from that already 
mentioned in Section 5.4.2, i.e. 𝛿 not being infinitesimal with no load applied, some 
others are proposed below.  
 
a. Angular error 
Looking at both legacy and recently taken footage, one might see that top of the 
specimens – and sometimes the side – is clearly visible during some tests (Figure 38). 
This indicates that the plane of interest and the projection plane, i.e. the camera’s 
sensor, are not parallel: there exists an angular error between them in azimuth 
direction and possibly elevation direction as well. Change in apparent length caused 
by this phenomenon is illustrated in Figure 39. Proposed solution: alignment of 
planes. 
 
 
Figure 38 Angular error: measurement plane and projection plane (camera sensor) are not parallel: top 
of specimen is clearly visible. 
==PAGE:63==
  
63 
 
Figure 39 Illustration of the effect of angular error and change in apparent length caused by the 
relative positioning of camera and specimen (exaggerated). 
 
b. Length calibration error 
A measuring tape attached to the front side of the bottom grip was used for 
calibration. Said tape was not located on the plane of interest but slightly closer to the 
camera. Its precision is not known and it was slightly crooked. On a smaller scale, 
marks denoting millimeters and centimeters on the tape have a finite width (Figure 
40), the order of magnitude of which is comparable to that of 𝛿.  Proposed solution: a 
more accurate calibration tool located on the plane of interest. 
 
 
Figure 40 Measuring tape used in tests magnified: marks used for calibration have non-infinitesimal 
widths. 
 
c. 𝜹(𝒕= 𝟎) being indicated as negative 
GOM Correlate interprets 𝛿 as negative at the beginning of measurements (Figure 41) 
despite the length being defined such as 𝛿(𝑡= 0) = 0. Whether this is due to noise, 
software error or the fiber tightening on the capstan is not known and requires 
further investigation. 
==PAGE:64==

Figure 41 𝛿(𝑡= 0) < 0. Raw output from GOM Correlate. Specimen: BK 11/048 Short 06. 
 
d. Lens distortion  
Digital consumer cameras have lens distortions which may vary with zoom, focal 
length and other parameters [64]. These errors cause changes in apparent length in 
the picture/video (Figure 42) yet were not taken into consideration during this 
investigation. Proposed solution: lens calibration or using metric lens [64].

5.4.5. Justification of 𝜏𝑠 values obtained from pull-out data 
While it is clearly stated in [9] that decrease in 𝑙𝑠 does not originate from pull-out 
action alone due to contraction, results presented in Section 5.3 might still be correct 
for the following reasons. If one takes a look at measurement data depicted in Figure 
28, 𝜎𝑓 at the (quasi-)beginning and the (quasi-)end of the pull-out phase assumes 
these values: 𝜎𝑓,𝑏𝑒𝑔𝑖𝑛𝑛𝑖𝑛𝑔= 1101 𝑀𝑃𝑎 and 𝜎𝑓,𝑒𝑛𝑑= 385 𝑀𝑃𝑎. Difference: 
 Δ𝜎𝑓= −716 𝑀𝑃𝑎, which corresponds to Δ𝑃𝑓= −54 𝑁. While initial free distances of 
the fibers were not measured exactly, an upper bound estimate of 300 mm can be 
obtained from GOM Correlate for certain measurements. Length change of a 300 mm 
long piece of fiber due to a decrease of 53 N in load: 
Δ𝐿= Δ𝑃𝑓⋅𝐿0
𝐴𝑓⋅𝐸𝑓
= (… ) = −1.073 [𝑚𝑚] 
In case free fiber length at the beginning of the pull-out phase was indeed 300 mm, 
contraction is responsible for a 1.073 mm decrease, which is a mere 1.7 - 3.1% of the 
corresponding initial embedded lengths (~35 −65 𝑚𝑚). 
5.4.6. Drawbacks of the method 
Main drawbacks of the method are listed below. 
1. Only works on transparent specimens. 
2. Difficult to use if debonding is unstable. 
3. Requires a high-quality digital camera with minimal lens distortion and low 
added digital noise to yield accurate data.  
4. Poor quality footage is hard to process and may produce false results. 
5. Sensitivity to ambient light conditions, light source and camera exposure 
settings. 
6. Sensitivity to misalignment and errors in calibration. 
7. GOM Correlate, Tracker and MATLAB require considerable computing power 
and RAM. If the computer used for DIC does not have a dedicated graphics 
card with parallel computing platform (CUDA), processing time increases 
significantly. 
8. Even with sufficient computing power, processing takes a lot of time. 
9. High-resolution videos, as well as GOM Correlate container files, occupy 
sizable amounts of storage space. 
10. Relies on some trial and error at every step. 
11. Fitted curves sometimes have wide confidence bounds. 
12. Curve fitting requires guessing boundaries and starting points to converge, 
which may lead to false results.

6. CONCLUSION 
This work concerns the development of a method designed to obtain displacement 
data via digital image correlation (DIC) so that parameters of a micromechanical 
model of fiber/matrix interfaces can be determined. Context, aim, limitations have 
been clearly shown. Theoretical background to work with has been presented and 
elaborated. Values to be measured and parameters to be obtained have been pointed 
out. Materials, equipment and methods used have been identified and introduced. 
Development phases were described step by step, with detailed explanations as to 
why individual steps have been carried out. Means to extract and process data 
procured from measurements have been shown. Processed data has been evaluated 
and presented along with parameters calculated from said data. Errors in parameters 
have been pointed out; possible error sources were identified and suggestions have 
been made to overcome them. Final results have been compared to one another and 
to results from previous works on the topic. 
 
Overall, the developed method, while having some drawbacks, has proven to be 
repeatable, reliable and reasonably easy to use. Necessary equipment can be found in 
any standard-equipped materials testing laboratory and required software is 
available for free (except for MATLAB – however, open source regression software 
does exist). Based on the results, the method is suitable for measuring 𝑙𝑑 and 𝛿 values 
to be used for parameter determination in the Risø model; the model itself accurately 
describes fiber/matrix debonding and pull-out phenomena. The method performs 
best when 𝜏𝑠 needs to be obtained, as values determined via different methods 
matched best in the case of this variable, but the other two parameters are somewhat 
close to one another as well, and are at the very least in the same magnitude as those 
from previous experiments. Considering the low number of specimens with 
unknown manufacturing conditions, this is a formidable result. With a higher 
number of specimens manufactured in a well-known and uniform way and with 
error sources minimized or eliminated, it is safe to assume that the other two 
parameters could be determined with high confidence as well. The possibility of 
obtaining model parameters via curve fitting has also been shown. 
 
It has also been demonstrated that the setup can be used for extensometry, even in 
the sub-millimeter range, as displacement indicated by the testing machine and 
GOM Correlate deviated only by 0.08% and length changes as small as 𝑛 𝑥 10 𝜇𝑚 
could be distinguished. Young’s modulus of the fiber obtained from measurements 
only deviates by 3% from the nominal value, which again proves the precision of 
length measurements, as force values originating from the testing machine are 
assumed to be accurate. 
==PAGE:70==
  
70 
 
In order to compensate for differences between 𝜏𝑠 values obtained from SL1 and SL2 
and non-constant Δ𝜖𝑇(𝑃𝑓), a zero-correction factor in 𝛿 has been introduced. Results 
modified this way had shown good match with those obtained via curve fitting and, 
in case of 𝜏𝑠, those obtained from pull-out experiments as well. As for pull-out 
experiments, it has also been demonstrated that 𝜏𝑠 can be obtained with a reasonable 
margin of error without knowing the free fiber length at the beginning of the pull-out 
phase.  
 
Miscellaneous measurement equipment crafted during the development process can 
be used in other experiments, namely: the modified computer mouse can be used to 
precisely synchronize test start and stop. The camera fixture can reliably 
accommodate cameras to be used for visual measurements at the testing machine 
Instron 3343. Fluorescent paint combined with UV light provides high-visibility 
traction points without significantly altering overall light conditions of the scene.

7. FUTURE WORK 
Based on the results, the following future researches are proposed: 
1. Repeating measurements with a greater number of specimens with similar 
fibers, matrices, sizings and manufacturing conditions while accounting for 
identified error sources. 
2. If the zero correction in delta approach turns out to be correct, it can be 
automated. 
3. Building the setup in a controlled-light environment, possibly a in a 
darkroom. Using a light source with adjustable brightness is highly advisable. 
4. Measuring debond energy (partially or in whole) directly via an infrared 
camera, possibly in combination with DIC. Underlying principle: heat release 
during debonding [65]. 
5. Extending the method to non-transparent matrix materials, possibly in 
collaboration with DTU Civil Engineering, where micro-CT combined with 
DIC has been used to study pull-out resistance of steel fibers from concrete 
[66].
