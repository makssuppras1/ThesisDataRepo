Abstract
This dissertation focuses on the challenging problem of recovering the 3D geometry of
an opaque object embedded in a refractive object using RGB images. The conventional
solution to this problem is to employ the technique of Refractive Multi View Stereo (RMVS),
which is based on Multi View Stereo (MVS).
In order to explore new methods on this problem, we investigate the newly method, Neural
Radiance Fields (NeRF) for 3D reconstruction of refractive objects. NeRF is a state­of­
the­art technique for novel view synthesis that uses an implicit neural representation to
encode the geometry and appearance of a scene. The core idea behind NeRF is to render
images by integrating the density and the color values along a ray, which is provided by
the implicit representation.
However, the original NeRF model assumes that ray travel along straight line in an ho­
mogeneous scene. This assumption does not hold for refractive objects, which distort the
path of the light rays. To account for this, we extend the NeRF method by incorporating
the eikonal equation, enabling to simulate the refraction of light through the object.
We compare two different implementations of our extended NeRF method and select the
most effective one for reconstructing the opaque object embedded in the refractive ob­
ject. We evaluate the performance of our approach using synthetic dataset. In particular,
we generate synthetic data using Blender and demonstrate that the approach is able to
reconstruct an explicit representation of the opaque object inside the refractive object with
high resolution.
Although the results are promising, there are still some challenges that are required to
address. One of the key issues is the pose estimation, which is crucial for accurate 3D
reconstruction. Furthermore, there is no ground truth data available for the real dataset,
which makes it difficult to quantitatively evaluate the performance of this method. These
limitations suggest the need for future work to further refine this method and address these
challenges.
In summary, our work demonstrates that the NeRF method can be extended to handle
the challenging problem of 3D reconstruction involving refractive objects. It represents a
significant step forward in the field of 3D reconstruction and offers new method for preser­
vation of ambers, human tissues etc.

Introduction
This chapter begins by outlining the motivation of the master project and dissertation. In
section 1.2, a brief literature review is presented. It ends by describing the overall structure
of this dissertation.
1.1
Motivation
Reconstructing 3D geometry from photographs has been a longstanding problem that
has received significant attention from researchers. The applications of image­based 3D
reconstruction are vast, ranging from the preservation of historical artifacts [1] and medical
imaging [2] to 3D mapping for autonomous driving [3] and various other domains. As a
result, numerous solutions have been proposed to tackle this challenging task.
MVS has been extensively researched and proven to be an effective solution for image­
based 3D reconstruction [2]. In recent years, deep learning architectures for MVS have
been proposed [4], which have greatly improved the quality of geometry reconstruction
and transformed the MVS pipeline into a data­driven approach. Another popular method
that has been receiving significant attention is NeRF [5], which was first introduced in
2021 as a practical solution for Novel View Synthesis (NVS). While originally developed
for NVS, NeRF has also shown possibility in 3D geometry reconstruction by providing
volumetric density outputs for specific point coordinates in the encoded scene.
In addition to the aforementioned methods, there are several other intriguing techniques
for image­based 3D reconstruction, such as shape from silhouette [6] and differentiable
rendering [7]. However, these methods primarily focus on reconstructing the geometry of
opaque objects while ignoring refractive objects. Few of them concern about the refractive
objects. When dealing with refractive objects, the surface geometry between two medi­
ums becomes crucial for computing the refraction of light. Refraction, which is governed
by Snell’s law, bends light and violates the assumption of classical 3D reconstruction in a
homogeneous medium, as illustrated in figure 1.1. Before starting this project, I undertook
a special course and studied various methods for acquiring the geometry of transparent
objects, both with and without opaque objects inside, which will be introduced in section
1.2.

and red points in a refractive object that are projected onto the same coordinates on the
image plane, but follow different projection rays once exposed to air.

This project aims to address the challenge of digitizing fragile amber­encased insects,
which are valuable specimens collected by evolutionary scientists and natural history mu­

It is a special case of 3D reconstruction where the target is the opaque object inside re­

1.2
Literature Review
This section provides a literature review of topics related to this dissertation, including 3D
reconstruction for refractive object and opaque object inside refractive object.
1.2.1
3D reconstruction for refractive object
Before starting this dissertation, I studied some novel methods for reconstructing the re­
fractive object without opaque object inside. This subsection will introduce these methods
and involved techniques.
Environment matting is a common technique used to capture how refractive objects dis­
tort the background. By leveraging the distorted background, it is possible to estimate the
geometry of light paths transmitting through the transparent object. Following the work
by Wu et al. [8], Lyu et al. [9] implement a ’ray tracer’ using PyTorch, differentiating the
process of ray generation, ray­scene intersection and ray refraction. It is noted that this
approach does not involve the imaging from the intersection between rays and scene.
This allows the geometry of transparent objects to be be optimized in an automatic dif­
ferentiation manner by back­propagating the loss between simulated refracted rays and
ground truth refracted rays decoded from environment matting. The drawback of this
method is apparent ­ it requires a complex images capturing setup. Xu et al. [10] pro­
posed a novel approach for reconstructing refractive objects using a hybrid mesh­neural
representation. Similar to Lyu’s work, it also requires environment matting to capture the
distortion caused by the refractive object. However, instead of using ray­pixel correspon­
dences, the method uses ray­cell correspondences, which allows for a hand­held captur­
ing setup. The proposed approach utilizes Multilayer Perceptron (MLPs) to approximate
the displacement of the vertices on the initial mesh of the refractive object. By updating
these MLPs, the geometry of the refractive object can be optimized more efficiently. Li et
al. [11] makes the capturing setup less restricted using a small number of images from
a mobile phone camera, observed under a known environment map. They utilize neural
networks to estimate normal for the hit points and a rendering layer to model the image
formation.
While aforementioned methods have nice results on reconstructing refractive object, they
have a limited assumption that restricts light refraction to only two bounces, which is not
suitable for some concave refractive objects. NEMTO [12] and NeTO [13] are two meth­
ods adopting Signed Distance Functions (SDFs) network to represent object geometry
and solving the self­occlusion problem using different techniques. NeTO designs a strat­
egy based on law of reversibility to detect and exclude those rays with self­occlusion in the
optimization. NEMTO introduces a ray bending network, mapping the incident ray with
surface normal and first intersection to the final refracted ray exiting the refractive object.
Another interesting branch for computing the geometry of opaque and refractive objects is
Differentiable Rendering (DR). The process of rendering is basically to project 3D scene
into 2D image. On the other hand, Inverse Rendering (IR) aims to compute the attributes
of 3D scene (geometry, bsdf, lighting etc.) from 2D images. Differentiable rendering is

one of the inverse rendering methods. The idea of DR is to design a differentiable ren­
derer mathematical model and its corresponding implementation to differentiate the ren­
dering process, so that the derivatives of the loss between rendered image and ground
truth image with respect to different scene attributes are feasible to compute. It is clear
that the most challenge parts of DR are how to differentiate rendering equation and de­
sign the gradients. In recent years, some differentiable rendering systems are proposed,
including differentiable rasterizer(pytorch3D [14], soft rasterizer [15]) and physics­based
differentiable renderer (redner [16], mitsuba [17], psdr­cuda [18]). Since rasterizer could
not process complex light­transport effects, differentiable rasterizer are out of considera­
tion when dealing with refractive objects. Physics­based differentiable rendering can be
used to accurately estimate interior and boundary integrals [18]. The estimation of inte­
rior integrals is based on existing Monte Carlo methods for forward rendering. And the
estimation of boundary integrals is a new question for differentiable rendering. The three
physics­based differentiable renderer mentioned above solve this question in different
ways.
1.2.2
3D reconstruction for opaque object inside refractive object
Although there are limited works that specifically address the problem of combining re­
fractive objects with opaque objects, some existing methods can be adapted to address
this issue. This subsection aims to present some related methods that can be useful in
addressing this problem.
Cassidy et al. [19] provide a RMVS mathematical model for reconstructing the objects
inside a homogeneous refractive object with arbitrary geometry. Yoon et al. [20] propose
a novel method for estimating the depth from stereo cameras through a curved refractive
thin medium. Yan et al. [18] develop a physical­based differentiable rendering scheme to
solve the inverse problem.
NeRF is a method combining implicit representation of scene with volumetric rendering,
originally developed to solve the problem of novel view synthesis for a specific scene. It
encodes the scene with some MLPs and can estimate the density for specified points in
the scene. Therefore, we can compute the density for a desired range and obtain the
shape of opaque object inside refractive object by setting a density threshold. However,
due to the assumption of camera model in the homogeneous medium, NeRF is not de­
signed for scene with refractive objects. Although some works [21] have attempted to
embed NeRF as a module for grasping refractive objects, the reconstructed geometry of
the refractive object are rough. In order to solve this issue, some methdos are developed
based on the original NeRF [22] [23] [24]. LB­NeRF [22] learns the light refraction by
using a separate network to predict the displacement δx for compensating the sampling
point on the straight ray x to the point on the refracted ray x + δx. However, different dis­
placement on the same point on the straight ray will mislead the estimation of the original
NeRF. In order to reduce the ambiguity in the estimation of displacement, LB­NeRF intro­
duces a regularization term punishing the estimated excessive displacement. Eikonal [23]
proposes a multi­step training scheme to model the lights transmitting refractive object.
Assuming the light radiance is constant inside the refractive object, it simplify the com­
plete model of light interaction to an eikonal­only model. It utilizes a neural ODE solver to
solve this initial value problem. Similar to Eikonal, SampleNeRFRO from Pan et al. [24]
follows the ray equation of geometric optics derived from the Eikonal equation for a vol­
umetric scene [25]. Without using a multi­step training scheme, SampleNeRFRO follows
the original NeRF but with a hierarchical path sampling technique. ReNeuS [26] aims at
the same problem as ours. Instead of using NeRF as their base method, ReNeuS follows
NeuS [27] to represent the scenes with a neural surface representation of SDFs and a

volumetric radiance representation.
Traditional methods such as RMVS, which redesign the camera model and image projec­
tion algorithm based on the model of refraction, have achieved some interesting results
but are not good enough. Physical­based DR involves redesigning the entire physical­
based rendering system, which helps solve many ill­posed inverse problems in a manner
of differentiable rendering. However, it is difficult to design the mathematical model and
implement it. On the other hand, NeRF­based methods utilize volume rendering, which
is originally differentiable, to build a data­driven solution. The results from NeRF­based
methods are inspiring, and most importantly, they can be easily scaled without the need to
design complex mathematical models. While the training of NeRF­based models is usu­
ally slow, this is not a significant issue when dealing with offline problems. Furthermore,
with more researchers working on this area, techniques for improving the training speed
of NeRF are being proposed [28].

Background
This chapter provides a brief overview of the background knowledge used in our project.
We first introduce two categories of geometry representation, followed by the mathemat­
ical model of volume rendering. This model is an important technique for extracting RGB
images from the implicit geometry representation studied in this project. Next, we intro­
duce the eikonal rendering model dealing with refraction. Finally, we present the concept
of neural ODE, which is a novel method for estimating gradients for an ODE system.
2.1
Representation of 3D geometry
In computer graphics, 3D geometry is encoded in different methods. These methods can
be classified into two categories ­ explicit representation and implicit representation. This
section will introduce the concept of these methods and discuss their pros and cons.
Figure 2.1: Explicit representation for 3D geometry. (a) Point cloud. The size of point is
meaningless. Point only encode the information of coordinates and other material prop­
erties. (b) Voxel grid. The size of voxel is an important property for voxel grid. With a
smaller voxel size, the voxel grid has a better accuracy. (c) Polygonal mesh. Decoded
as planes, mesh can only represent the surface of objects.
2.1.1
Explicit representation
There are three common explicit representation for 3D geometry. Point cloud is a set of
3D points. Each point encodes its coordinates or other information such as color, density
etc. Point cloud is straight forward and easy for storage. They are used to represent the
surface and the structure of object. Voxel grid is another explicit representation method
for 3D geometry. First of all, voxel derives from the concept pixel and image is a two­
dimensional grid of pixels. Voxel grid is a three­dimensional grid of voxels, where each
voxel represents a small cube of space in object or scene. Similar to point clouds, voxel
grids can contain information such as color, density and other material properties. Due
to its convenience for encoding exterior and interior of objects, a voxel grid is useful in
medical imaging. However, both of two aforementioned methods are lacking description
towards the geometrical relation between elements. Polygonal mesh (mesh for short) is
a common structure storing the 3D geometry with the relation between elements. A mesh
is a collection of vertices and faces that define the surface of a 3D object. The vertices
are connected by the edges and the edges weave the faces. It is a versatile and efficient

==PAGE:16==
way to represent complex 3D shapes, and can be easily manipulated and transformed
using various algorithms and techniques. However, for an object with complex shape and
high resolution, meshes also require a large amount of storage as point clouds and voxel
grids.
2.1.2
Implicit representation
Implicit representation cover a set of methods that are used to describe a geometric object
without explicitly defining its surface or volume. It includes methods such as SDFs, implicit
neural fields etc. a SDFs assign a distance value to each point in 3D space, indicating
how far away that point is from the surface of the object. Positive distance values indicate
that the point is outside the object, while negative distance values indicate that the point
is inside the object. The surface of the object is defined as the points where the distance
value is zero. SDFs are useful when dealing with boolean operations. Implicit neural
fields are a recent development in implicit representation that use deep neural networks
to learn the implicit function that describes the shape of an object. The neural network
takes a point in 3D space as input and outputs a scalar value that represents color, density
and other material properties.
2.2
Volume Rendering
Volume rendering is a distinct technique to surface rendering. It is used to visualize the
interior of volume (e.g. smoke, cloud) by describing how light interact with materials. This
section will introduce the mathematical model of volume rendering.
2.2.1
Radiometry on Volume
Radiance L is a quantity measuring the amount of light [29]. For volume rendering, the
key idea is to trace how the radiance changes along the light within the volume. In order to
model and visualize the process of light within volume, people represent the volume as a
small cylinder with a differential distance travelled by light ds. The light radiance changes
from Li to Lo when marching ds along the direction ω. It is noted that Lo may be smaller
or bigger than Li depending on the property of lights involved in this volume and the light
source.
Figure 2.2: Model of light transmitting volume.
There are four types of radiance variation. Assuming there are some particles inside the
volumes. When some of light is absorbed by the particles, the light radiance decreases.
The absorption coefficient σa defines the probability that the light is absorbed by the parti­
cles inside the volume per unit distance. However, some volume medium such as candle
flame can emit light, which will increase the light radiance along the volume. There are
also some lights travelling along other directions, which result in in­scattering and out­
scattering. The scattering coefficient σs defines the probability that the light is scattered
by the particles inside the volume per unit distance. It is noted that both out­scattering

and emission have the same effects on the loss in radiance. Therefore, we can define
extinction coefficient σt = σa + σs to represent both of them.
Figure 2.3: Absorption, emission and scattering. Absorption and in­scattering lead to
an increase to the light radiance while emission and out­scattering decrease the light
radiance.
2.2.2

Neural Radiance Fields
This section will first introduce the concept of NeRF proposed by Mildenhall etc. in 2020

NeRF related to refractive objects will be introduced specifically in the next chapter. Before

NeRF is an implicit representation to encode a static bounded scene S using a much
smaller capacity compared to the collection of images used to train it. Hence, it can be
considered as a method of data compression. More interestingly, NeRF can generate new
views of the scene, which can be seen as interpolation based on the input view images.

Basically, a NeRF is a MLPs FΘ encoding the information of a scene. The input of the

direction will terminate at this point. Higher σ indicates higher probability that the point is
opaque.

and volume density of every point in the encoded scene at specific view direction. The next
step is to render images based on these points using sampling techniques and volume
rendering.

2.5
Training the NeRF
Before the details of training the NeRF, it is necessary to focus on some properties of
neural network. Neural network is well known for its ability of approximating arbitrary
function while there’s evidence show that it tends to learn the function with less complexity,
which is called spectral bias[33]. Intuitively speaking, neural networks always try to find a
’lazy’ way to fit the data used to train it. It is a good property when we need a global model
to generalize as much as possible unseen data with similar distribution. However, NeRF
is expected to be an over­parameterized model for a specific scene and required local
fluctuations to tackle details or high frequency information. The authors of NeRF propose
a way to alleviate spectral bias of NeRF, which is introduced in the next subsection.

its proven effectiveness, using this tool for masking refractive objects can significantly
increase efficiency compared to manual labeling. However, it’s important to note that
while refractive object segmentation is a necessary component of the project, it is not the
primary focus. Therefore, we label the mask manually to ensure the accuracy of segmen­

Neural Radiance Fields for Refraction
This chapter presents two methods based on NeRF for simulating refraction. The first
method, EikonalFields [23], proposed by Bemana et al.
utilizes a three­step training
scheme. The second method, SampleNeRFRO [24], introduced by Pan et al., trains a
NeRF with a sampling technique that is specifically designed for refraction. Both methods
utilize the eikonal equation to simulate the path of light within refractive objects. How­
ever, they differ in their approaches to solving this equation. The main difference arises
from the assumption made regarding the index of refraction (IoR) field. Specifically, the
first method assumes that the IoR field is both unknown and inhomogeneous, while the
second method assumes that the IoR values are known. In this project, the IoR value is
given, making the SampleNeRFRO method more suitable. Nevertheless, it is important
to introduce the concept and techniques of the EikonalFields.
4.1
EikonalFields ­ Estimate IoR Fields with unknown IoR
The initial stage of EikonalFields is identical to that of the original NeRF in obtaining a
representation for the whole scene. However, as a result of light refraction, the estimated
density and radiance estimations of points within the refractive object from original NeRF
are inaccurate. Therefore, the subsequent step of EikonalFields is to build a MLPs repre­
senting the IoR field, which is utilized to determine how the ray change inside the refractive
object.

4.1.3
Problem of EikonalFields for our problem
In an ideal scenario, the trained IoR fields should output the correct IoR values for the
queried position, and the neural ODE should generate accurate derivatives of the ray’s
position, direction, and radiance. However, based on the tests conducted by the original
author, it was observed that although this method can solve NVS, the trained IoR fields
do not provide actual physical values, and the change in the direction of the rays is also
not physically correct [23]. Unfortunately, the author did not elaborate much on this issue.
From my understanding after reviewing the paper and their codes, I believe that it might
be challenging to use a neural ODE to simulate the eikonal equation and converge to a
correct state. Additionally, training another MLPs to represent the IoR fields, along with
the neural ODE, may be unstable. To address this issue, I suggest providing a rough but

6
Discussion
6.1
Conclusion
In conclusion, we have presented a novel approach to address the challenging problem
of 3D reconstruction involving opaque objects embedded in refractive objects using RGB
images. Our approach is based on extending the state­of­the­art NeRF method to handle
refractive effects by incorporating the eikonal equation. Our experimental results demon­
strate that our approach can effectively handle synthetic datasets rendered in Blender
and shows potential for real­world datasets captured using a camera.
While our approach shows promising results, there are still some challenges that need
to be addressed. One of the key challenges is accurate pose estimation for real dataset,
which is crucial for 3D reconstruction. Additionally, lack of ground truth data for real­world
dataset makes quantitative evaluation of our approach difficult. For the Blender dataset,
we need more experiments and results to validate the robustness of the method. While
writing the conclusion, we notice some newly published works related to our problem. It
will be encouraging if we can evaluate these new methods with ours on the same bench­
mark.
Nonetheless, we believe that our work represents a significant step forward in the field of
3D reconstruction and has the potential to enable new applications in computer graphics,
robotics, and augmented reality.
6.2
Future
Future work will focus on refining our method to address the limitations and challenges we
have identified. Also, more experiments are required to test the effectiveness and robust­
ness of this method. Furthermore, we plan to investigate the application of our approach
to a wider range of domains, such as medical imaging and mapping for autonomous driv­
ing, where 3D reconstruction from RGB images plays a critical role. Besides these, there
are some interesting problem to be solved.
6.2.1
Influence of the refractive object’s geometry
While the refractive object is set to a cube in this project, there are different shape of
amber in real life. Therefore, it is necessary to investigate how the shape of refractive
object influence the quality of reconstruction. It will be more beneficial if this method can
apply to arbitrary convex object.
6.2.2
Texture of the target object
Currently, we only focus on the 3D geometry of the target object. However, the texture of
the insect is also an important part for the digitization of amber. Extending the techniques
of NeRF, we believe that it’s possible to align the view­dependent color from NeRF to the
reconstructed object.
