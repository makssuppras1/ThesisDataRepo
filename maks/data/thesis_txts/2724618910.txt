Abstract 
The annual catch of the shrimp, Pandalus borealis, is about 255 tons; half of the shrimp is a by-
product (head and shells). This project is a subproject of the ReMess project, which aims to utilize 
shrimp production side streams in a sustainable way and at the same time create an increased 
economy. Therefore, it is interesting to look at the recyclability of shrimp residue, which can be 
divided into different components: chitin, minerals, lipids and proteins. Chitosan and protein are 
used in the food, cosmetic and pharma industries.  
This project examines the functionality of enzymatic hydrolysis of chitosan and protein coming 
from shrimp waste (H&S). For enzymatic hydrolysis, two enzymes, trypsin, and alcalase, are used 
to investigate whether they had a difference in protein % and degree of hydrolysis at different 
hydrolysing times. There was no significant (p>0.05) difference in protein % and degree of 
hydrolysis within the group and between them, with one exception at 2 h which showed 
statistically significant (p <0.05) differences between trypsin and alcalase. Chitosan and protein are 
used as emulsifiers in different mixing ratios in the emulsions with and without ultrasound, where 
they are analysed on different parameters: particle size, zeta potential and stability. Emulsion with 
chitosan is more stable than emulsion with protein alone. For the emulsions Ch1:Pr1, Ch1:Pr2 and 
Ch1:Pr3 with and without ultrasound, it was expected that the emulsions with ultrasound had a 
better stability, however, the only sample treated with ultrasound that had a better stability than 
without was Ch1:Pr2 ultrasound compared to Ch1:Pr2. The samples were very close in the stability 
index, and it can be concluded that none of the emulsions were was stable, as they were all above 
3 on the stability index. From the results, it appears that creaming had occurred in the emulsions.

1 Introduction 
 Introduction 
The most commercially caught shrimp is Pandalus borealis, which in 2020 alone constituted 
255,389,018 ton (FAO, 2023) 
The edible part of the shrimp constitutes about 1/3, while shrimps side stream (head and shell) 
about 2/3 (personal communication Mikkel Knudsen of Launis A/S), and of the latter, only 5 % are 
utilised – typically for fish feed.  
For greater sustainability and market value, this report, which is part of a larger project called 
ReMeSS, focuses on increasing the value of those parts of the shrimp that have not previously 
been commercially exploited. 
ReMeSS, which stands for Resource effective multi-extraction of sustainable high-value 
compounds from the shrimp production side stream, aims to streamline the utilization of shrimp 
for environmental and economic reasons. In the first part of ReMeSS, a method has been found 
where the shrimp residue is divided into different components and identified as proteins, chitin, 
minerals and lipids – and how large proportions they represent.  
Many of the essential amino acids (Dang et al., 2018a) are found in the shrimp's proteins, and 
these can, for example, be an important source in dietary supplements, which is relevant in a time 
with a focus on minimizing meat intake. In addition, H&S is a good mineral source.  
This part of the ReMeSS project focuses on the functionality of hydrolysed proteins and chitosan 
from shrimp side streams, including how good emulsifiers they are. Sources show, for example, 
that chitin in the form of chitosan is a good emulsifier in connection with sunflower oil (Liu et al., 
2012). Based on this, it will be investigated how different combinations of chitosan and protein 
from shrimp side streams behave in emulsions that can be used in different industries, e.g. food, 
pharma or cosmetics. 
 
This project examines the functionality of hydrolysed protein and chitosan from shrimp side 
streams in food systems. Protein and chitosan are studied as emulsifying in emulsion and their 
stabilities. Chitosan and protein are the product after enzymatic hydrolysis of head and shell (H&S) 
from shrimp side streams. The protein will be investigated for the degree of hydrolysis, using the 
OPA method and protein content. The functionality of protein and chitosan as emulsifiers will be 
==PAGE:9==
 
2 
 
expressed in the parameters: surface in diameter, interactions between the particles and stability 
over time in emulsions. Emulsions with protein and chitosan are set up with different ratios to 
examine how the stability is between them and chitosan and protein. 
Some experiments are emulsions with ultrasound with the same ratio of chitosan and protein as in 
emulsions without ultrasound.  
All the different parameters measured in emulsion, protein and chitosan are used to assess both 
functionally and the properties of protein and chitosan as emulsifiers in the different emulsions. 
The results will be compared and discussed. 
 Hypotheses 
Based on the introduction of the project, experiments will be carried out that will help to elucidate 
the hypotheses. Data from the experiments will be processed, and statistics will be applied and 
analysed.  
 
H1: A longer time of hydrolysis results in a higher protein content.  
H2: A combination of protein and chitosan has a higher stability in an emulsion compared to 
protein alone.  
H3: Pretreatment of chitosan by ultrasound before production of the emulsion with proteins will 
result in a more stable emulsion.  
H4: Hydrolysis by alcalase will result in a higher degree of hydrolysis compared to treatment by 
trypsin.  
H5: The degree of hydrolysis and the protein content will increase when the H&S is dried and 
ground before hydrolysis compared to the treatment of wet H&S.  
 
2 Background  
 Composition and Structure of the Shrimp 
 
Pandalus borealis is a cold-water shrimp which is considered one of the most commercially 
important species in the world within wild shrimps (Dang et al., 2018b). The shrimp has different 
names: pink shrimps (Shumway et al., 1985), deep-water shrimp (UK, Canada and USA), and deep-
sea shrimp also called the Northern Shrimp (Holthuis, 1980). P. borealis lives in the North Atlantic

and especially in the waters around Greenland, Iceland, The Gulf of St. Lawrence, The Gulf of 
Maine, The Gulf of Alaska and the North Pacific (Shumway et al., 1985; Holthuis, 1980). The red 
colour of the shrimp stems from the fat-soluble carotenoid astaxanthin (Høegh, 1989). 
   
P. borealis lives at a depth between 
150 and 400 m, where the seabed 
is slightly soft and muddy and at a 
temperature between 0-8 °C

is a protandric hermaphrodite. The 
shrimp is male for 1-2 years in 
Skagerrak, and 4-5 years in 
Greenland after which it changes to 
a female. The reason for the slower 
growth around Greenland is the 
colder waters (Høegh, 1989). 
Fertilization of the female shrimp 
occurs once a year when the eggs in 
the ovaries are bluish-green, which can be seen through the shell. Then the male shrimp places its 
sperm on the female shrimp's pereopods, and thereby the eggs become fertilized when the 
female spawns. From August to April, the female bear fertilized eggs on the pleopods, and the 
female shrimp cannot change shell during that period (Høegh, 1989).  
Shrimps have a divided body into a head part that includes the digestive system and gills, and the 
tail part includes the edible part. The shrimps are invertebrates and have an outer skeleton as the 
insects, exoskeleton, called shells. Throughout the life of a shrimp, it changes the shell about 25-30 
times (Høegh, 1989).

The exoskeleton of shrimps is called the integument and comprises three layers. The outer layer is 
the epicuticle (1-3 μm thick), and the middle layer is the procuticula (10-several hundred μm 
thick), divided into the outer exocuticula and the inner endocuticula. The inner layer is a cell layer 
of 1-3 μm consisting of living epidermis cells. The epicuticle consists of lipoprotein (cuticulin) that 
is covered with a wax layer. Procuticula is built up of calcium carbonate with chitin and protein, 
the calcium carbonate contributes to the strong structure of the cuticle. The endocuticular 
structure is more open, and the exocuticula is denser in structure. A plasma membrane separates 
the procuticula from the epidermis, which is a single-cell layer, in addition, the epidermis is 
separated from the muscle tissue (Høegh, 1989). The distribution of the components in crustacean 
shells is: calcium and magnesium carbonate 20-50 %, protein 20-40 %, chitin 15-40 % and 
constituents as lipids, astaxanthin and minerals (Antonino et al., 2017). 
This project is working with the head and shells from shrimps after press, called press cake and 
focusses on the proteins and the chitin, and not lipids and minerals.

The supernatant protein %, which is derived from protein extraction, is determined using the

2.1.2 Use of Proteins (from Shrimps H&S)   
Protein is found in many places; it is the building block of cells in all living organisms, thus, through 
the diet, proteins and especially the essential amino acids, therefore, it is found in many forms in 
the food industry. Enzymes are also proteins, which act as biological catalysts.  
Protein is used in emulsions as emulsifiers and mostly in the food industry (Berton-Carabin & 
Schroën, 2015). In this project, protein from H&S of shrimps is used as an emulsifier together with 
chitosan as the second emulsifier in emulsions. 
2.1.3 Enzymatic Hydrolysis  
The extraction of protein from the head and shell of shrimps is performed with enzymatic 
hydrolysis in which enzymes cleave a protein into smaller peptides or even amino acids. It is a 
chemical reaction where the covalent bonds between monomers are broken. Breaking the bonds 
occurs with the help of water. This project uses the enzymes trypsin and alcalase (described under 
section 2.2 Enzymes) for hydrolysis. The reaction proceeds by the enzymes catalyse with excess 
water and attack a water molecule on the covalent peptide bond between two adjacent amino 
acids, it is cleaved between the carboxyl and amino groups (Wouters et al., 2016. The product 
output of the hydrolysis is the supernatant (protein) and pellet (chitin, lipid, and minerals). The 
supernatant and pellet are freeze-dried, and the product is without water. The enzymatic 
hydrolysis has broken the peptide bonds, and therefore the proteins are separated from the H&S 
product, and the rest is the pellet.  
The protein % in the supernatant is determined by the DUMAS method. The DUMAS method 
measures the total nitrogen content from the sample to calculate protein percentage with the 
help of a conversion factor multiplied by the total nitrogen in the sample. The factor used for 
calculations for the protein determinations in the supernatant is 6.25. 
The autosampler transports the sample into the combustion pipe, where the samples are burned 
at 800 °C in an oxygen-rich atmosphere, and nitrogen from the samples forms N2 and NOx. First, in 
the combustion process, the halogens bound to the sample react, thus carbon forms CO2, sulphur 
forms SO2 and SO32-, and the bound hydrogen forms H2O. The process continues past the 
==PAGE:13==
 
6 
 
absorption columns where CO2 is removed, and then a drying step follows where all the water is 
removed. The rest of the combustion sample is transported into the EAS-reductor, and the output 
from NOx is reduced to N2, SO2 is absorbed, volatile halogens are bound, and a drying step 
removes any residues of water. The last step is where a thermal conductivity detector (TCD) reacts 
to the same application of the analytical gas mixture (i.e. carrier glass +N2) with an electrical signal 
and the sample nitrogen contents are measured (Afdeling for Fødevareteknologi, 2019. 
Fast Performance Liquid Chromatography (FLPC) is a type of liquid chromatography where the 
solvent flow rate is controlled by pumps. There is a mobile phase that is liquid and a stationary 
phase that can be liquid or solid containing particles. FLPC can use different columns that are 
prepacked for different options, such as hydrophobic interaction, ion exchange, affinity 
chromatography, and gel filtration. It is gel filtration that is used in this project as the purpose is to 
separate macromolecules based on their size. The solvent or buff carries the sample into the 
column after it is injected, where the sample diffuses in or adheres to the gel, and the sample is 
separated into different zones or bands. The detector can detect all separated compound bands 
from the background in the mobile phase through a flow cell (Walker & Loughran, 2011). The 
samples were detected at 215, 254 and 280 nm and expressed by the absorbed sample and its 
volume (mL). 
 Enzymes 
Enzymatic hydrolysis uses enzymes, which has the advantage that it is more sustainable, and the 
enzymes have different properties. Enzymes are biomolecules, almost always proteins, which are 
biological catalysts that exist in all living organisms (Berenbaum et al., 2011. Enzymes perform a 
chemical reaction, which is expressed by the enzyme starting and/or increasing the speed, without 
changing or damaging the protein, called the energy of a reaction (Berenbaum et al., 2011. The 
shape of the enzyme is important for its function. When it has a three-dimensional shape, there is 
an active site. Overall, the molecule is called a substrate when it binds to an active site, and an 
enzyme-substrate complex occurs when a reaction between a particular substrate binds to a 
specific enzyme (Berenbaum, 2011).  
The enzymes used in this project are trypsin and alcalase, both endoproteases, which are also 
called endopeptidases. They are proteolytic peptidases that cleave proteins by cleaving their 
==PAGE:14==
 
7 
 
peptide bonds. Endopeptidases do not cleave the peptide bonds from terminal amino acid but 
inside the polypeptide chains. Trypsin was extracted from the pancreas of cows, but now it can 
also be excreted from the pancreas of different mammalians, the type that is often used is the 
bovine trypsin. Trypsin is an enzyme employed in several industries, e.g. leather, food and 
pharmaceutical industries (Gong et al., 2015. Trypsin cleaves the peptide bonds on the carboxyl 
side of the amino acids arginine and/or lysine, however, there is an exception when it is bound to 
a carboxyl (C-terminal) proline.  
Abiotic factors influence the reaction of the enzymes, these factors are pH and temperature, and 
every protein and substrate complex has its optimum. The optimum temperature and pH for 
trypsin, which is catalysing during the hydrolysis of protein, is 40 °C and pH 8, respectively, but it 
can have an optimum range between pH 7-9 (Adler-Nissen, 1985 and a temperature maximum of 
65 °C (Gong et al., 2015. Unlike trypsin, which breaks peptides into larger pieces, specifically after 
the carboxyl side, alcalase cleaves the protein into many smaller pieces and more randomly during 
hydrolysis. Alcalase is extracted from Bacillus subtilis (a protein-digesting bacterium). The 
optimum range of alcalase is pH 8 and for temperature 50 °C up to 55 °C (Shu et al., 2016. If the 
temperature increases, there will be more movement in the molecules and a faster reaction, but 
over a certain level, it can lead to denaturation of the enzyme (Robinson, 2015. 
The enzyme/substrate ratio (E/S) influence the reaction rate, therefore, it is an important 
parameter. The relationship between the reaction rate of hydrolysis and enzyme/substrate is 
linear if the substrate is soluble, and opposite if the substrate is insoluble (Adler-Nissen, 1985. 
2.2.1 Degree of Hydrolysis  
OPA is the method used to determine the degree of hydrolysis of protein hydrolysates. The 
method describes the degree of hydrolysis in percentage of cleaved peptide bonds relative to the 
total number of peptide bonds in the sample. For every hydrolysed peptide bond, when a free α-
amino group is formed, it reacts with OPA (o-phthaldialdehyde) and an SH-compound 
(dithiothreitol, DTT) and forms a complex. For this complex, a spectrophotometric method is 
applied, and it can be measured at 340 nm on a plate reader. The measurement of the detection 
limit is 3.8 % and is based on the lowest concentration in the calibration curve (L-serine). For the L-
Serine, a 7-point standard curve is applied. The standard solutions are prepared by a 2-fold serial 
dilution in distilled water with factor 64 between the highest and the lowest standard, and the

8 
 
highest standard is 0.5 mg/mL L-Serine. The sample used in the OPA method is the supernatant 
after centrifugation and cognate protein contents measured from DUMAS. After the sample is 
measured on a plate reader, the result is used to calculate the degree of hydrolysis with the 
following formula:

 Chitin in Shrimps  
Chitin is also called poly β-(1,4)-N-acetyl-D-glucosamin. It is a natural polysaccharide, and chitin is 
a biopolymer that has crystalline microfibrils, which are found in structural components in the 
exoskeleton of insect and marine sources or from cell walls in fungi and yeast. This biopolymer is 
the most abundant natural polymer after cellulose and is synthesized by a large number of living 
organisms (Khoushab & Yamabhai, 2010 Younes & Rinaudo, 2015). Chitin is one of the main 
components of shrimp; in the exoskeleton, it is found in the linings of their respiratory, excretory, 
and digestive systems and tendons (Khoushab & Yamabhai, 2010). Shells from crabs and shrimps 
are the main source of chitin at present. Chitin has different properties such as biodegradable, 
biocompatible, bio-absorbable with an antibacterial effect, the ability to heal wounds, and 
immunogenicity is low. Chitin is used in many different fields: microbiology, wastewater 
treatment, material science, agriculture, tissue engineering, food technology, drug delivery 
systems, and bionanotechnology (Khoushab & Yamabhai, 2010; Younes & Rinaudo, 2015). 
Chitin comes in three forms α, β and γ, depending on the source. The α form most often comes 
from crab and shrimp shells, but also from yeast and fungal cell walls, and the β form occurs along 
with protein in squid pens and in the tubes synthesized by vestimetiferan and pogonophora 
worms (Khoushab & Yamabhai, 2010; Younes & Rinaudo, 2015). In α-chitin, there are two anti-
parallel molecules per unit, whereas β-chitin has only one parallel arrangement, and γ-chitin 
contains one anti-parallel thread and two parallel (Khoushab & Yamabhai, 2010). The β and γ 
forms can be changed to α form, but not the opposite. The α- and β-chitin are commercially 
available, here in the project it is α-chitin from shrimp shells. The intermolecular hydrogen bonds

Chitosan: structural characterization and applications 
Chitin is transformed into chitosan by deacetylation of chitin. Chitosan is a linear polysaccharide 
that consists of b-(1,4)-linked glucosamine units (2-amino-2-deoxy-b-D-glucopyranose) and a 
portion of N-acetylglucosamine units (2-acetamino-2-deoxy-b-D-glucopyranose). In Figure 2, the 
chemical structure of chitosan is shown (Antonino et al., 2017; Younes & Rinaudo, 2015). The 
structure of chitosan with free amino- and hydroxyl groups along its backbone make the chitosan 
a potential polymer with a pH response. When the pH is low, the amines of chitosan are positively 
charged, protonated, and cationic polyelectrolytes in water solutions (Liu et al., 2012). The 
protonation of the amino groups of chitosan is why chitosan is soluble in diluted acid solutions 
(pH<6). Solubility of chitosan is best in 1 % acetic acid, pH about 4.0-4.4, but soluble up to pH 6.4 
(Ansorena et al., 2019, Younes & Rinaudo, 2015). 
The polycationic chitosan can interact with polyanions forming polyelectrolyte complexes. 
Chitosan can possess micro/nanoparticles, fibres, and hydrogel, and it has an excellent ability to 
form films (Antonino et al., 2017).

Characteristics of chitosan are its non-toxicity, biodegradability and biocompatibility. In addition, 
chitosan has biological properties such as antibacterial, antimicrobial and bioadhesiveness, 
coagulating activities, and wound healing capacity. It makes chitosan highly relevant for 
applications in medicine and pharmacy, agriculture, the food industry, cosmetics, and wastewater 
treatment (Antonino et al., 2017).  
Chitosan synthesis of chitin is in different steps: deproteinization, demineralization and 
deacetylation. The extraction of chitin and protein starts by H&S from shrimps performed by 
enzymatic hydrolysis progress (described in section 4.2). The end product has two outputs, one is 
protein (supernatant), and the other is the pellet that contains chitin, lipid and minerals. 
Deproteinization of chitin is achieved by enzymatic hydrolysis, and before demineralization, the 
chitin will be defatted by extraction with SCO2.

 Emulsion Based on Protein and Chitin from Shrimp H&S 
Emulsion stability (unstable system)  
An emulsion is a mixture of two immiscible liquids, e.g. oil and water. When droplets from one 
phase are dispersed in the second phase, it is called emulsion. The phases will after some time be 
separated. The emulsion systems are thermodynamically unstable. For example, it is known from 
mayonnaise, milk, cream or salad dressing, which is O/W. Water in oil (W/O) emulsion is for 
example butter and margarine. During emulsion, the triglycerides in the oil phase of milk can be 
semi-solid, depending on the temperature of the dispersed fat crystals. Dispersed phases are 
found in other products in the form of starch granules (cake batter), gas bubbles (whipped cream), 
and ice crystals (ice cream) (Berton-Carabin & Schroën, 2015; Dickinson, 2009). 
In order for the emulsion to be stable or postpone phase separation, an emulsifier must be added. 
Emulsifiers are biopolymers, like protein (here from the shrimps), or they can be low molecular 
==PAGE:20==
 
13 
 
weight emulsifiers as surface-active substances, including chitin in the form of chitosan. 
Emulsifiers have a hydrophilic and a lipophilic end, and this is called amphiphilic, this means that 
they are typically absorbed in the boundary surfaces of oil and water (Berton-Carabin & Schroën, 
2015; Dickinson, 2009). The four important factors are flocculation, coalescence, creaming, and 
Ostwald ripening, which describe the instability processes exhibited by O/W emulsion and in W/O 
emulsion processes are nearly the same, except for the creaming, where it is sedimentation 
instead. The stability (TSI) in the emulsion is measured in the Turbiscan. In this project, it is the 
characteristics and functionality of the emulsion of chitosan and protein of H&S from shrimps 
which are measured by Turbiscan. 
Flocculation 
The definition of flocculation is that emulsion droplets are sticking together without fractures on 
the protective stabilizing layer. It happens when the pair interaction energy can be different 
charged or hydrophobic on the surface and becomes negative (weak or larger and irreversible 
flocculation). When the aggregated molecules have a total in a certain quantity, they will be 
insoluble and precipitate. It can be flocculation in various ways during emulsion by: The density of 
the surface charge is lower by pH change or binding of ions, e.g. Ca2+, and the probability is greater 
when the system is electrostatic repulsion. The ionic strength is increasing with added salt to the 
aqueous phase, and it has effects on the electrostatic repulsion. The solvent quality is lower with 
e.g. where ethanol is added or pH is changed, some particles are induced under thermodynamic 
conditions. During emulsification, polymer bridges could be formed when the emulsifier is 
insufficient, where adsorbed polymers connect with the neighbouring droplets. Formation of 
polymer bridges after emulsification occurs when the heat treatment and the process are the 
adsorbed protein molecules cross-link. Non-adsorbed species have an influence that can explain 
the extra attractiveness where a narrow gap between droplet surfaces occurs (Dickinson, 2009). 
Coalescence 
Coalescence is when several emulsion droplets fuse and form a larger single droplet. It is not 
desirable in food emulsion products. Coalescence is often found in cream layers or flocculated 
systems. It often occurs when adsorbed protein layers are weakened by an emulsifier, which is a 
surfactant, or stirring is intense and/or air enters the emulsion. The process is partial coalescence 
where more than one crystalline oil droplet are together. When a droplet forms a solid fat crystal, 
==PAGE:21==
 
14 
 
a drop consisting of liquid oil enters. Partial coalescence is the process used during the churning of 
cream. The factor affecting fusion is the same as for flocculation, but in addition, cycles in the 
temperature of the cream layer influence the melting and growth of fat crystals, and stirring 
(Dickinson, 2009).  
Creaming 
Creaming in most emulsions is to be avoided, but not in all the processes. In the emulsion, 
creaming can occur. In an O/W emulsion, creaming is where the oil droplets move under gravity, 
forming a concentrated cream layer at the top. In the creaming, the particle will migrate to the 
dispersed phase.  
There are three ways to slow down creaming: reduction of density difference between phases, in 
an aqueous medium increase in effective viscosity, and reduction of the droplet size (Dickinson, 
2009). 
Oswald ripening 
Oswald ripening is a process where the smaller particle or droplet is more soluble than the larger 
ones, and small particles have a higher radius of curvature, and the larger particles have a lower 
radius of curvature. Over time in the Ostwald ripening (disproportionation), smaller particles or 
droplets will dissolve and be deposited on the larger particles after their molecules diffuse in the 
bulk.  
Ostwald ripening can be avoided by the following changes: monodispersity should increase, an 
increase in droplet size, the interfacial tension should be reduced, and into the dispersed phase, 
insoluble material will be incorporated. The latter is the best strategy in practice (Dickinson, 2009)

Measurement of the particle size and the zeta-potential 
The zeta potential is measured with an electric field across the dispersion, thus the particles are 
headed against the electrode of opposite charge with a velocity proportional to the degree of the 
zeta potential. The zeta potential is defined as the charge that measures the distance from the 
particles and not directly on the surface. It is an important factor concerning the interactions 
between particles in dispersions with properties such as viscosity, dispersion stability, flocculation,

and film-forming properties. The particle size is an important parameter because when the 
particle size is reduced, the volume becomes considerably smaller compared to the surface, which 
influences the dispersion properties. The zeta potential value can explain how the particles 
interact in relation to the size. The particles with a high zeta potential can both be negative and 
positive. The zeta potential is considered high or at a level where it is stable, when it is smaller 
than -30 mV and higher than +30 mV (Queiroz, et al., 2021). If the particles have the same charge, 
they will reject each other. The particles and small molecules with low density remain in 
suspension. A high zeta potential contributes to stability, and it can explain if a dispersion or 
solution will avoid aggregation. It is the interaction between the chitosan particles and protein 
particles that defines the stability of an emulsion (Malvern, 2022; Malvern Panalytical a spectris 
company, 2023).

 Extraction of Protein from Shrimps H&S via Enzymatic Hydrolysis 
Protein hydrolysis on press cake was obtained using the proteases alcalase and trypsin 
(Novozymes, Denmark, kept at 4-5 °C). Before enzymatic hydrolysis, the press cake was dried in an 
oven at 40 °C for 6 h and ground in a coffee miller to a fine powder (dry-H&S). Of dry-H&S, 10 g 
was mixed with 100 mL distilled water (w/v 1:10) and heated in a water bath to 55 or 40 °C for 
treatment with alcalase and trypsin, respectively. The pH of the heat-dried H&S was adjusted to 8, 
with 1M HCI and mixed with proteases to a final concentration of 0.5 % for both alcalase and 
trypsin.  
Samples were hydrolysed for 2 h, 30 min, and 10 min with both proteases. The reaction was 
stopped by heating the sample in a water bath for 15 min at 90 °C. Subsequently, the samples 
were vacuum filtrated (very fast filter paper from “Whatman”, 20-25 µm) and separated into a 
solid (pellet) and a liquid (supernatant) fraction. The pellet was freeze-dried (referred to as lipid, 
chitin and mineral fraction), and the supernatant was centrifuged at 3750 x g at 10 °C for 10 min. 
From the centrifuged supernatant, 5 mL was collected and stored at 5 °C for analysis of protein 
content (DUMAS) and the degree of hydrolysis (OPA). The remaining supernatant was mixed with 
the centrifuged pellet and freeze-dried (referred to as protein fraction).

 Hypothesis 1: A longer time of hydrolysis results in a higher protein percentage 
The purpose of hypothesis 1 was to verify if the time of hydrolysis an influence the protein 
content. Earlier studies have a hydrolysis of two h or more with high protein content (Kong et al., 
2007). When the hydrolysis time is shorter, a lower protein content is expected, as the enzyme in 
the hydrolysis does not have the same time to work. To test this hypothesis, the protein content is 
recorded after three different hydrolysis times; 10 min, 30 min and 2 h, using two different 
enzymes; trypsin and alcalase. The result is seen in Figure 6.

Mastersizer, and lastly, to analyse the stability index for the different emulsions by Turbiscan.

 Hypothesis 5: Pretreatment of chitosan by ultrasound before the emulsion with 
proteins will result in a more stable emulsion. (Turbiscan, Zetasizer, 
Mastersizer)  
Based on theory, where interaction between particles will be greater at several smaller particles, 
as they have more surface overall compared to few larger particles with a slightly larger surface. 
The purpose of the hypothesis is to examine if it is likely to get a more stable emulsion using 
ultrasound to make the particles of chitosan smaller and thereby create a greater interaction in 
the emulsion and thereby a more stable emulsion. In addition, ultrasonication can help to improve 
the emulsifying property of chitosan, because it breaks chitosan into smaller particles. To examine 
the effect of ultrasound, Turbiscan is applied, which can measure the stability index and how the 
emulsion develops over time. In addition, Zetasizer and Mastersizer have been used to measure 
particles, zeta potential and size, so that all the measurements can be compared with emulsion 
without the use of ultrasound.

The application of ultrasound wave in a liquid can result in microbubbles that will collapse, where 
the result of a succeeding propagation of wave is in a high shearing effect, a phenomenon called 
cavitation (Queiroz, Silva, et al., 2023). This may be a possible explanation for why emulsion with 
ultrasound has a lower stability than the emulsions without ultrasound. 
The ultrasound treatment has an increase in surface hydrophobicity, which can arise from 
unfolding of the protein (Kumar et al., 2022), and the results from whey protein concentrate, egg 
white protein and soy protein isolates are similar to Arzeni et al. (2012), where a rise in surface 
hydrophobicity was observed.  
It may also be a possible explanation that ultrasonication on chitosan may affect its surface 
hydrophobicity. Another reason is the degradation of large aggregates, such that the 
intermolecular hydrophobic interactions are smaller and their hydrophobic amino acid groups are 
"stored" inside the aggregates (Kumar et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2019). If it has arisen in the chitosan 
that is used in emulsion, it can help to explain the emulsion instability, since interaction between 
the particles will be less frequent.

6 Conclusion 
In this project, the functionality of hydrolysed protein and chitosan from shrimp side streams in 
food systems has been investigated. The enzymatic hydrolysis (trypsin and alcalase) was used on 
press cake of H&S, and the polypeptide chains were reduced in size, and the output was a protein 
(supernatant) and chitin, mineral, lipid (solid). Protein content % after enzymatic hydrolysis over 
time cannot demonstrate significant difference (p>0.05) in content of protein % in the groups, 
alcalase and trypsin. Therefore, hypothesis 1 cannot be verified. The degree of enzymatic 
hydrolysis has in samples with alcalase no significant difference compared to the sample with 
trypsin. The hypothesis 2 is not verified because the result from the degree of hydrolysis of 
alcalase is not higher than trypsin. During the project, the protein % and the degree of hydrolysis 
of "wet" H&S with alcalase compared to H&S with alcalase and trypsin were also investigated. The 
hypothesis was that the dried and grounded H&S will increase in the degree of hydrolysis and 
protein % compared to treatment of wet H&S. The result from the work with hypothesis 3 is not 
significant, and therefore, it cannot be confirmed. For the further work with emulsions, protein 
and chitosan from trypsin 10 min were used to investigate their functionality. It is investigated 
whether emulsions with protein and chitosan in different mixtures are more stable than the use of 
protein alone. The results of emulsion with protein alone have a lower stability in the emulsion 
than with both chitosan and protein, thus hypothesis 4 can be confirmed. Results from emulsion 
with and without ultrasound on chitosan are compared in relation to emulsion stability. With the 
fact in mind that the particles become smaller when exposed to ultrasound and their effect leads 
to hypothesis 5, where emulsions using ultrasound on chitosan should become more stable than 
emulsion without ultrasound. The only emulsion with ultrasound that has a better stabilized 
emulsion is Ch1:Pr2, the rest is more unstable. Based on the overall results, hypothesis 5 cannot 
be confirmed.  
 The work with the functionality of chitosan and protein of shrimp waste continues as the 
potential in utilizing their property as e.g. emulsifier of food and in the pharmaceutical industry is 
vast. Since it will also be both economically and sustainably responsible to utilize residual products 
from the shrimp industry, which is somewhere between 45-60 %.

7 Perspectives 
Based on the results, new experiments are required to clarify the potential of protein and 
chitosan, of H&S from shrimp. It should be examined whether: 
• Longer hydrolysis time for alcalase and trypsin does not provide a higher protein % and which 
gives the highest yield.   
• The degree of hydrolysis for alcalase and trypsin must be examined at shrimp H&S to uncover 
whether alcalase and trypsin are located at the level of H&S from shrimp.  
• Study of chitosan quality relatively to stability in emulsion. 
• Study of protein molecule weight by Sec-male. 
Other things that can be looked at in emulsion are viscosity. There may also look out 
concentration of chitosan and protein and additionally tests with other enzyme.  
An exciting thing is to investigate "wet" H&S, because if it turns out that yield will be the same as 
H&S, it will be a more economical and sustainable process since less energy is spent on drying H&S 
and thus also time.  
In 2020, 255,389,018 tons of shrimp were caught, and out of a shrimp, about half is waste in the 
form of heads and shells. It is a large amount of residual waste that can be recycled in another 
form. Today, only a small part is used for fish feed and does not have such a large return. There 
may be good economy in getting the resources for new products for e.g. the food industry in the 
form of emulsifiers, mineral supplements, and protein supplements.
