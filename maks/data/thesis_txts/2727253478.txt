Abstract 
 
The transition to sustainable and circular business models is essential to reach the 
environmental targets outlined in the Paris Agreement. The textile industry, known for its 
significant environmental footprint, has been a focus of increasing interest in environmental 
assessments. This study aims at complementing the existing literature by assessing the 
environmental impact of fibre-to-fibre recycling of cotton and polyester fibres, using a Life-
Cycle-Assessment approach. The analysis compares the benefits of recycled yarns to virgin 
yarns and identifies the primary environmental hotspots in the production chain for different 
sorting scenarios. Results demonstrate that recycled scenarios generally have lower 
environmental impacts than virgin scenarios across a broad range of impact categories, 
including Climate Change, water use and human toxicity. Moreover, the sorting of textile 
waste into colour fractions shows a significant environmental benefit compared to mixed 
colours sorting with systematic lower values. Nevertheless, the implementation of recycling 
comes with many challenges, technological, economic and societal. Consequently, the 
involvement of all stakeholders, including end-consumers participating in post-consumer 
textile waste collection, the collection and sorting industry providing sufficient feedstock to 
recycling operators, and textile manufacturers sourcing recycled materials, is essential for a 
successful transition to a circular business model. 
Moreover, while the upstream processes are the major contributors to most of the impacts 
of virgin yarns, the downstream phase (including spinning, bleaching/pre-treating, dyeing and 
drying) contributes significantly to the environmental impact in recycled scenarios. As a result, 
recycling alone cannot fully address the industry's environmental footprint, and decarbonizing 
the textile industry necessitates holistic circular solutions. While recycling has a significant 
role, closing the loop more downstream in the textile chain by promoting new circular 
business models such as reuse and repair should be explored to achieve a sustainable future 
for the industry.

1. Introduction  
 
The transition from linear business models to more sustainable and circular ones throughout 
the industries is a requisite if we are to respect our environmental targets defined in the Paris 
agreement. Decarbonising industrial supply chains first requires understanding them and 
identifying where the main environmental impacts lie. In that respect, the interest for 
environmental assessment either across sectors or focused on specific industries is growing. 
The number of industrial reports investigating solutions to reduce emissions has increased but 
still strongly focuses on the CO2 emissions of sectors and their impact on Climate Change. 
Methodologies such as life-cycle assessment (LCA) have enabled to conduct more holistic 
assessments about specific products or industrial supply chains, including all the steps from 
cradle to the grave as well as taking into account other environmental consequences such as 
water use, emissions of particulates, or land use among others.  
 
1.1. 
The textile industry & its impacts 
 
The textile industry is one of the most polluting industry and at the same time one of the most 
embedded sector in our society (Köhler, 2021). Textile is an essential component of multiple 
items of our daily life, from clothing, household linens to many industrial and institutional 
products. Semantically, textiles are defined by the European Parliament and the Council 
(2011) as any product exclusively composed of textile fibres, regardless of the mixing or 
assembly process employed. Additionally, products containing at least 80% textile fibres (by 
weight) are also considered textile products (Trzepacz, 2023). The textile industry is divided 
into three main types: clothing, industrial textiles and household textiles, and is characterised 
by its extensive global supply chain. Production of textile fibres in the world amounted to 109 
million metric tonnes in 2020 and is forecasted to reach almost 150 million metric tonnes by 
2030 (Statista, 2023). In terms of textile imports (including fibres, fabrics and garments), the 
European Union (EU) is the second-largest global importer after the United States (US). The 
total combined value of textile imports into the EU reached $125 billion in 2021, a figure that 
nearly doubles when intra-EU trade is included (Köhler, 2021). Moreover, the global demand 
for textiles is increasing as a result of growing population and increasing wealth and 
consumerism trends in emerging market (Sandin G. &., 2018) (Textile Exchange, 2021). 
Consumers in private households are the dominant buyers of clothing and household textiles. 
In 2018, private households consumed 5.4 million tonnes of textiles, with clothing accounting 
for 4.4 million tonnes (81%), and household textiles accounting for just over 1 million tonnes 
(19%). This data underscores the crucial role textiles play in our daily lives and highlights the 
significance of the textile industry to the global economy (Köhler, 2021). 
 
The textile industry is a prime example of a linear economy, with a business model based on 
selling high volumes of low-cost, easily accessible products (Re_fashion, 2021). Over the last 
decades, this phenomenon has been further aggravated by the lowering of production prices 
==PAGE:7==
 
6 
leading to even greater volumes of low-quality textiles and resulting in high per capita 
consumption of material resources and associated waste generation (Köhler, 2021) (Hot or 
Cool, 2022). The fashion industry is hence being characterised by increasing numbers of yearly 
collections of products of rapid obsolescence, with for instance the largest European clothing 
brand rolling-out 24 collections per year (Köhler, 2021). The consumption of textiles in EU has 
increased by 40% during the last decades and accounts for 2-10% of the environmental impact 
(Hole, 2020). 
 
As a result, the textile industry has a massive social and environmental footprint, of which the 
fashion industry represents the greatest part. The Ellen MacArthur Foundation estimated that 
the global greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from textile production reached 1.2 billion tonnes 
of CO2 equivalents in 2015 (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2017). In Europe, consumption of 
clothing, footwear and household textile is the fourth largest contributor to GHG emissions, 
after housing, transport and food (Hot or Cool, 2022). In addition to its contribution to GHG 
emissions, the consumption of textiles is responsible for around 4 to 6% of the EU’s 
environmental footprint across a range of impact categories such as resource depletion, water 
use, toxic chemicals and generation of waste (Köhler, 2021), (Sandin G. &., 2018).  
The environmental strain of the industry depends on the type of fabric used and is scattered 
unheavenly throughout the production chain. For instance, the production of cotton requires 
high use of water and pesticides while the production of polyester is less water-demanding 
but encourage fossil-resources extraction.   
 
1.2. 
Solutions to reduce the impacts of the textile industry 
 
The Paris Agreement sets a target to limit the increase in global average temperature to well 
below 2 degrees Celsius (°C) above pre-industrial levels, and to pursue efforts to limit the 
temperature increase to 1.5°C. To achieve this goal, the agreement calls for a global reduction 
of GHG emissions by at least 50% by 2030 and reaching net-zero emissions by 2050. Although 
the Paris Agreement does not have specific targets for individual industries, each sector is 
expected to contribute to the global effort to reduce emissions to meet the overall targets. 
The textile industry being one of the largest contributors to global carbon emissions, it is 
expected to implement measures to reduce its GHG emissions and transition to a low-carbon 
economy. More precisely, as reminded by Sandin & al., for several environmental impact 
categories, staying within the planet boundaries would require to cut-off the impact of textile 
supply chain from 30 to 100% depending on the category (with for example 100% reduction 
for Climate Change) (Sandin G. &., 2018). To achieve such a reduction, multiple solutions must 
be pursued simultaneously:  
- 
Promote more sobriety, which means consuming less goods and hence producing less, 
- 
Increase overall efficiency of the chain by optimising the energy use, water use or 
chemical use of processes, implementing cleaner means of production or using more 
sustainable fibre types, 
==PAGE:8==
 
7 
- 
Close the loop of this linear industry either upstream by recovering textile waste into 
new potential resources, or downstream by promoting new circular business models 
such as reuse. (Köhler, 2021) 
 
In particular, textile waste is the fastest-growing waste stream in municipal solid waste (MSW) 
across the globe (Juanga-Labayen J. P., 2022). It hence represents a tremendous loss of 
economic and material value and make the third bucket of solutions a particularly interesting 
challenge to take up. Recycling is one of the multiple solutions falling into the last category 
and consists of re-processing textile waste into new products. It has the potential to lower the 
demand on virgin textile raw materials, close the loop to reduce the number of steps of the 
processing chain necessary and hence resources needed, and detract valuable resources from 
end-of-life. It is embedded in the paradigm shift towards a more circular economy which aims 
at preserving the value of the materials and resources as long as possible in the form of 
functional products, and reintegrating used products considered as waste inside the economic 
cycle (Köhler, 2021) (Re_fashion, 2021). According to McKinsey, increased recycling and 
collection could drive annual emissions abatement of around 18 million tonnes (McKinsey, 
2020). 
 
Currently, few initiatives have been implemented to improve textile waste collection and 
valorisation throughout the world, or in Europe. The end-of-life pathways of textile are mostly 
landfilling and incineration. Between 65 to 92 million tonnes of textile are wasted every year 
of which less than 20% is recycled on average (Wang, 2022). More precisely, less than 1% of 
the clothing waste is being recycled back into clothing in what we refer as fibre recycling 
(Trzepacz, 2023) (McKinsey, 2020). Under current conditions, textiles are mostly turned into 
low-grade non-woven products like filling or insulation. Indeed, the demand for recycled fibres 
is still very low and the current recycling technologies are too expensive to enable recyclers 
to offer fibres at competitive prices. Moreover, the lack of regulatory framework until now led 
to unstandardised, scattered initiatives. Nevertheless, the regulatory framework is now 
evolving in the EU, with the revised Waste Framework Directive requiring EU member states 
to collect separately textile waste by 1st January 2025 and to consider the introduction of 
textile waste reuse and recycling policies (Köhler, 2021) (Hole, 2020). This regulatory 
framework will be a key enabler of textile recycling as it will promote a centralised collection 
solution that will help to increase the textile waste feedstock volumes.  
 
The textile chain and its linear business model have been the subject of a growing number of 
studies during the last decade, either investigating the environmental hotspots of its 
production chain, or assessing the environmental benefits of solutions to reduce its impact, 
such as recycling. Nevertheless, the lack of data due to the lack of transparency of the 
industrials, as well as the restricted scope of some papers limit the number of exploitable 
studies in the literature. There is hence room for further assessment of the environmental 
impact of the textile chain.

1.3. 
Existing literature on the environmental impact of textile recycling  
 
This section examines the existing literature on textile recycling and highlights its limitations. 
Despite the increasing interest in textile recycling, there is a notable scarcity of literature on 
the subject overall, particularly when compared to the extensive research available on 
recycling of other materials. For instance, a ScienceDirect database search conducted by Hole 
(2020) revealed that out of 1817 papers focused on recycling, only 25 (1%) specifically 
addressed textile recycling, with the majority of studies centred around paper and waste 
recycling. While recent attention has been given to the production and consumption stages 
of textile life cycle, research regarding the end-of-life stage is noticeably lacking (Hot or Cool, 
2022). Additionally, the environmental impact of textile recycling is a subject that has 
sometimes been inadequately studied, as underlined by the literature review conducted by 
Sandin et al. (2018). Furthermore, we attempted to gather literature from industry sources, 
such as environmental assessments from clothing companies like Patagonia, Decathlon, and 
PeakPerformance, to provide insights on recycled fibres. However, none of these companies 
were willing to share their materials with us. 
 
The number of papers dedicated to investigating the environmental impact of textile recycling 
is remarkably low. According to Sandin et al. (2018), a total of only 34 studies addresses this 
subject. Their research further reveals that the type of textile recycling the most extensively 
studied is fibre recycling, accounting for 57% of the studies, followed by polymer/oligomer 
recycling (37%), monomer recycling (29%), and fabric recycling (14%). This focus on fibre 
recycling in academic research aligns with the industrial scalability and maturity of the 
technology, which will be elaborated upon in Section 2. 
 
Most studies rely on Life-Cycle Assessment methods (LCA) to evaluate the environmental 
impact of technologies, underlining the widespread interest in this type of assessment and 
the relevance of the methodology. However, as emphasised by Sandin et al. (2018), most of 
these studies predominantly focus on assessing the impact on Climate Change alone. For 
instance, the article "Carbon footprint reduction in the textile process chain" examines the 
impact of recycling materials but solely investigates the carbon footprint (Muthu, 2012). There 
is however a risk associated with not considering a broader range of impact categories. By 
solely focusing on one aspect, there is a possibility of not capturing the full benefits of a 
recycling method or inadvertently shifting impacts to other not investigated impact categories 
(Sandin G. &., 2018). Nevertheless, it is noteworthy that even in such cases, LCA methods are 
still employed to calculate the carbon footprint of different scenarios. 
 
Despite the scarcity of comprehensive and detailed LCAs in the field (Sandin G. &., 2018), some 
studies have conducted in-depth LCAs and have provided valuable supporting material for the 
current study. For instance, Kazan & al. conducted a garment oriented, cradle-to-grave LCA 
==PAGE:10==
 
9 
(Life cycle assessment of cotton woven shirts and alternative manufacturing techniques, 
2020). It assesses exhaustively the environmental impact of, among others, conventional 
cotton and recovered cotton fibres, including their impact on global warming, acidification of 
oceans and eutrophication of water. However, there are some limitations to consider. First, 
the study does not specify the recycling technology employed, which makes it challenging to 
assess the accuracy of the recycled model. Additionally, it does not include the collection and 
sorting steps in the recycling chain, and there is a lack of discussion regarding the recycling 
efficiency.  
Alternatively, Bianco & al. conducted a yarn-oriented, cradle-to-gate LCA (Life Cycle 
Assessment (LCA) of MWool® Recycled Wool Fibers, 2022). This study specifically compares 
the environmental impact of virgin and recycled wool fibres, encompassing factors such as 
Climate Change potential, ozone depletion, eutrophication potential, land use, water use, and 
more. However, it is important to note a key limitation of this study: it focuses on a specific 
patented process for producing recycled wool. As a result, the findings are highly specific and 
cannot be easily extrapolated to the broader textile market, limiting their generalisability.  
Lastly, Sandin et al. conducted a comprehensive report titled "Environmental assessment of 
Swedish clothing consumption-six garments, sustainable futures" to compare the 
environmental impact of six different garments (2019). The study encompasses various factors 
such as climate change, energy use, water use, and toxicity. They present results for different 
functional units, including the impact per one use of each garment, per garment, and for the 
annual clothing consumption in Sweden. This study offers a highly detailed overview of the 
environmental impact of these specific garments and stands as an example of a meticulous 
and well-documented LCA. However, it is worth noting that the focus on specific garments in 
this study poses challenges when it comes to industry-wide applications and arguing in favour 
of the overall benefits of recycling fibres. While the study provides valuable insights into the 
environmental impact of individual garments, it may not directly translate into a conclusive 
argument for the broader advantages of fibre recycling within the textile industry. 
 
While comprehensive LCAs in this field remain scarce, the studies aforementioned provide 
valuable insights and form a supportive basis for the present research. 
 
1.4. 
Identified gaps of literature  
 
Through our analysis, we have identified several gaps in the existing literature on the 
environmental assessment of textile recycling. First, it is important to note that most studies 
adopt a garment-oriented approach. This means that they primarily estimate the 
environmental impact of different fibres or scenarios at a final product level, such as for 
example, by comparing the environmental impact of a virgin polyester dress with that of a 
recycled polyester dress.  
In addition, many existing studies have overlooked the inclusion of the collection and sorting 
processes in their assessments of textile waste. This omission limits the comprehensiveness 
==PAGE:11==
 
10 
of their evaluations and results in an incomplete understanding of the environmental impact 
of textile recycling. Furthermore, it hinders professionals in the sorting industry from 
transparently utilising the results of these studies to highlight the environmental benefits of 
their activities. 
Moreover, a significant gap in the existing literature lies in the limited analysis of the impact 
of chemicals in textile recycling. Studies often provide a simplified view of the effects of textile 
chemicals on the environment, potentially leading to an underestimation of their overall 
impact.  
 
Consequently, the following study will aim at bridging the above-mentioned literature gaps 
while incorporating the best practices identified from existing studies. By doing so, it aims to 
offer a more comprehensive understanding of the environmental impact of textile recycling 
and contribute valuable insights to inform future sustainable textile practices. 
 
1.5. 
Aim of the study 
 
The aim of this study is to assess the environmental impact of fibre-to-fibre recycling in the 
textile supply chain, with a specific focus on cotton and polyester fibres. To achieve this, we 
aim to address the following key questions: 
1) What are the benefits of recycled yarns compared to virgin yarns 
2) What are the primary environmental hotspots within the production chain for virgin and 
recycled materials respectively? 
3) How does the quality of input materials and the sorting process influence the overall 
environmental outcomes? 
To facilitate a comprehensive analysis, it is essential to have a clear understanding of the 
textile supply chain and its various stages. Therefore, the following section will provide 
detailed information on the different steps involved in the textile supply chain.

The following chapter will provide an overview of the textile production chain first and of the 
textile recycling chain.

Industry reports often draw on internal data collected by companies within the textile 
industry. These reports may provide valuable insights into specific aspects of the industry, 
such as production volumes, market trends, and company-specific environmental data. 
However, the data within industry reports may be limited to specific companies or industry 
segments, and their methodologies might not always align with scientific rigor. Moreover, the 
access to the underlying data is rarely public which makes it difficult to check or reproduce 
calculations. On the other hand, academic studies typically follow established scientific 
methodologies, such as life-cycle assessment (LCA), and utilise data from a range of sources. 
These sources may include publicly available databases, scientific literature, government 
reports, and industry collaborations. Academic studies strive to ensure the highest standards 
of data quality, reliability, and representativeness. However, they may face limitations due to 
data availability, access, and the need to make assumptions or extrapolations. 
 
Nevertheless, industrial reports and academic literature align on the fact that the production 
stage is a key highlight of the textile chain. Indeed, a report by McKinsey focused on 
quantifying the GHG emissions at each stage of the chain and revealed that approximately 
70% of fashion's annual GHG emissions occur during production and processing stages, 10% 
during transport and retail, while the remaining 20% are generated during the use phase 
(McKinsey, 2020). In a more holistic approach, the report by Sandin & al. (2019), employing 
LCA methodologies, highlights that the production of raw materials, wet treatment processes, 
and the use phase are major contributors to environmental consequences (Sandin G. R., 
2019). 
 
2.2. 
Textile recycling chain 
 
After their initial use, textile products can follow different paths: either being re-looped into 
circular models or disposed of through landfilling and incineration. Circular models encompass 
reuse, where the product is transferred to a new user without significant changes, either 
through donation or sale, and recycling. Textiles can be recycled through two distinct 
pathways: downcycling and closed-loop recycling. 
 
Downcycling involves the conversion of textile waste into lower-value products, such as 
insulation wipes, industrial rags, low-grade blankets, or upholstery (Sandin G. &., 2018) .This 
process primarily relies on mechanical recycling methods. On the other hand, closed-loop 
recycling encompasses all recycling processes that transform textile waste into a similar 
product within the same industry. In this case, materials are reprocessed to retain their 
properties and value for reuse within the same product value chain (Wang, 2022).  
When discussing recycled fibres, bottle-to-fibre recycling is often included. This process 
involves breaking down plastic bottles made of polyethylene terephthalate (PET) into small

of recycling does not involve textile waste, it is not the focus of the present study. 
 
Recycling of textile waste into new textile products includes fabric recycling, fibre recycling 
and polymer/monomer recycling, all achieved through a mix of mechanical, chemical and 
thermal recycling processes (Sandin G. &., 2018). The systematisation of recycling into these 
three was introduced by the Ellen MacArthur Foundation (2017) and taken over by Sandin & 
al (2018) in their review. Currently, the majority of textiles are being downcycled as it 
represents the most cost-effective and feasible solution (Trzepacz, 2023). In Europe, less than 
1% of textiles undergo fibre-to-fibre recycling (Circle Economy, 2020).

3. Methodology 
 
This section outlines the methodological choices adopted to address the objective of this 
paper: evaluating the environmental impact of fibre-to-fibre recycling within the textile supply 
chain focusing on cotton and polyester fibres. 
 
3.1. 
Overall methodological choices 
 
This study aims at providing a high-level assessment of the impact of recycling processes that 
is not restricted to a specific product. Therefore, we propose a yarn-oriented study that 
examines the production phase of the yarn in its entirety while excluding downstream 
processes that are specific to garment production. Consequently, all steps occurring after yarn 
production and yarn wet treatment are not within the scope of this study as they are assumed 
to be identical for all scenarios. This means that the virgin and recycled yarns, after undergoing 
yarn processing and wet treatment, would follow the same processes to achieve the same 
final output. Additionally, we assumed that the subsequent wet treatment performed on the 
fabric during the textile production process was highly dependent on the product, since colour 
and design of the dyeing and printing depend on the intended use or style. Therefore, wet 
treatment could be excluded from the scope of the system. By excluding these unit processes, 
the study provides results at the yarn level, independent of product-specific life cycle stages. 
Besides, all production of accessories is excluded as intervening later in the product 
manufacturing process. Figure 4 visually highlights the process steps that are the focus of this 
study and those that are outside its scope.

3.2. 
Technological choices  
 
This study extends beyond the clothing supply chain to encompass the entire textile industry. 
Therefore, the word “textile” here refers to both clothing, household textile and industrial 
textile. Besides, the focus of this study is on two types of fibres: polyester and cotton. Indeed, 
this choice of fibres enable to focus on both a plant-based fibre and a synthetic one, and 
together, those two fibres dominate the fibre market, representing 75% (respectively 51% and 
24%) of it (Trzepacz, 2023) (Ellen MacArthur Foundation, 2017) .  
 
In the case of recycled scenarios, the study includes the collection and sorting of textile waste, 
the recycling step, and the subsequent yarn production and wet treatment. While collection 
and sorting steps are often overlooked in LCAs, they are crucial for accurately assessing the 
replacement rate between textile waste and recycled yarn and providing a comprehensive 
understanding of the recycling chain. The study considers all sources of textile waste, including 
both pre-consumer waste (such as production waste and excess stock from retailers) and post-
consumer waste (such as residential and industrial/institutional textile waste). 
 
Regarding recycling technologies, the study focuses specifically on textile-to-textile recycling 
and excludes bottle-to-textile recycling and downcycling. This choice is driven by the fact that 
textile-to-textile recycling represents the most circular solution and re-allocates the highest 
value to textile waste. When selecting the recycling technologies to study, the aim was to 
strike a balance between circular and beneficial practices and scalable, available technologies 
that the industry could adopt in the near future. Therefore, the study solely focuses on fibre-
to-fibre recycling technology, thereby excluding fabric recycling, monomer, oligomer, and 
polymer recycling. Indeed, fibre-to-fibre recycling is the most commonly used recycling route 
and is already implemented at a commercial scale. Fabric recycling, while more circular, has 
limited scalability and is only applied in specific cases. Polymer/oligomer recycling is still a 
niche technology and is not widely implemented due to technological barriers. Additionally, 
the study only considers mechanical recycling processes as part of fibre-to-fibre recycling.

3.3. 
LCA methodological choices  
 
In this study, Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) has been chosen as the preferred method to 
evaluate the environmental impact of textile recycling. The study aims at bridging the gap in 
existing literature by addressing a wide range of environmental impacts beyond solely climate 
change. The selected impact categories for detailed assessment are Climate Change, 
Terrestrial ecotoxicity, and Human toxicity (non-cancer). Other impact categories have also 
been investigated and are detailed in next section. To model the production chain and 
compare the LCA results, we used a combination of industrial and academic data, and 
employed EASETECH as LCA modelling software. 
 
Furthermore, this study aims to take a comprehensive approach to model chemicals by 
incorporating a detailed assessment of textile chemicals within the LCA framework. Building 
upon the framework proposed in Roos's article (Advancing life cycle assessment of textile 
products to include textile chemicals. Inventory data and toxicity impact assessment. , 2016), 
this study intends to provide a more accurate evaluation of chemicals role in the textile 
production chain.  
 
4. LCA - Goal and scope of the study 
 
In this section, we provide further details on the LCA approach adopted in this study. 
 
4.1. 
Overall Approach  
 
==PAGE:22==
 
21 
The methodology employed in this study is based on a quantitative approach known as Life 
Cycle Assessment (LCA) which allows us to assess, quantify and compare the environmental 
impact of both virgin and recycled yarns. More specifically, we used a process-based LCA, 
meaning that the modelling of the environmental impacts relies on the individual and 
separate modelling of each constituting steps of the overall life cycle. It is important to note 
that this study adopts a gate-to-gate LCA approach. This means that we focused solely on 
modelling and analysing a specific part of the entire life cycle, rather than considering all unit 
processes from cradle to grave. Our assessment encompasses the relevant stages from yarn 
production to wet treatment, excluding downstream processes that are considered common 
for both virgin and recycled yarns. 
 
The functional unit of the LCA is the following:  
1kg of yarn of 150 dtex, of middle-intensity colour, used to produce textile consumed in the 
Nordics today, produced using the Best Available Technology (BAT).  
 
The LCA performed is a consequential comparative analysis that evaluates the differences 
between virgin and recycled under different collection and sorting scenarios. For each type of 
fibre, four scenarios have been investigated, one virgin yarn scenario and three recycled 
scenarios: 
- 
A baseline recycled scenario (high quality input and no colour sorting) 
- 
A best-case recycled scenario (high quality input and colours sorting) 
- 
A worst-case recycled scenario (high quality input and no colour sorting).

4.3.2. Geographical boundaries  
The environmental impacts of the textile industry vary significantly depending on the country 
and its income level. Countries with higher income tend to have a greater impact due to 
increased consumption of textile goods, and therefore play a crucial role in decarbonising the 
textile industry (Köhler, 2021). Recognizing this, the European Union (EU) is currently 
implementing regulations concerning textile end-of-life.  
 
Given these considerations, this study focuses on textile consumption in the Nordics, which 
serves as an excellent case study for high-income developed EU countries. The location of yarn 
production processes has been determined based on main exporters of textile products to the 
Nordics, while sorting and collection processes are assumed to occur within the Nordics. 
Recycling processes, on the other hand, take place with the main recycling partners of Nordic 
sorting companies. In this context, collection and sorting data have been provided by a Danish 
partner company, and life-cycle inventory data have been sourced from the Mistra report 
(Sandin G. R., 2019), which is based on data from the Nordics, specifically Sweden. More 
information on the geographical model used in the study can be found in Section 4.2.1. 
 
Given these considerations, the focus of this study is on the consumption of textile in the 
Nordics, which provide an excellent case study of high-income developed EU countries. Hence, 
the location of the yarn production processes has been determined based on the exporters of 
textile products to the Nordics, the sorting and collection processes take place in the Nordics, 
and the recycling processes occur in the min recycling partners of the Nordic sorting 
companies. In that regard, collection and sorting data have been provided by a Danish partner 
company, while life-cycle inventory data have been sourced from the Mistra report (Sandin G. 
R., 2019), which is based on data from the Nordics, specifically Sweden. Further details on the 
geographical model used in the study can be found in Section 5.2.1. 
 
4.3.3. Temporal boundaries 
The study aims at providing an overview of the areas of improvement in the textile supply 
chain, therefore it is based on the BAT. It nonetheless focuses on the recycling technologies 
that are already mature and to a certain extent spread in the recycling industry. It is important 
to note that the study uses the current data on textile production and might not reflect the 
volumes collected in the future. Moreover, the LCIA data extracted from ecoinvent is slightly 
outdated and might not be applicable anymore in a few years.  
 
4.3.4. Limitations due to methodological choices  
It is important to acknowledge the limitations of the study due to the methodological choices 
made in the scoping of the LCA. First, the gate-to-gate LCA approach adopted only considers 
a specific part of the entire life cycle of the product, which means that the study cannot 
provide a full picture of the environmental impacts of the textile industry and cannot be used 
==PAGE:25==
 
24 
to identify hotspots in the chain. The study is also based on specific technological assumptions, 
which means that it does not reflect all current recycling practices. The choice of technologies 
and focus on specific scenarios limit the generalisability of the study's findings. Additionally, 
the geographical focus on the Nordics further restricts the applicability of the results to the 
rest of the world.  
 
Furthermore, it is important to acknowledge that the validity of the ecoinvent data used in 
this study is limited over time. As technology and industry practices evolve, the data and 
results obtained may become outdated. However, it is worth noting that the chosen processes 
in the study were intended to reflect the Best Available Technologies. As technology continues 
to advance, the study has the potential to become more representative of the deployed 
technologies in the textile industry in the coming years.

4.5. 
Multifunctional processes & system expansion  
 
An important consideration in conducting a LCA is how to allocate the environmental burdens 
of multi-functional processes.  
 
In transportation processes, impacts were assumed to scale proportionally to mass, with the 
environmental impact of transportation shared among the different products transported in 
the same truck/ship. Similarly, processes shared among different fractions, such as post-
recycling waste handling (i.e. treatment of textile waste, dust and metals), were allocated 
based on mass. All other processes were obtained from the ecoinvent 3.8 database using 
allocation at the point of substitution. 
 
System expansion with substitution was employed for all processes including by-products. For 
example, in the case of cotton cultivation which yields both seeds and fibres, the by-product 
was considered by including the avoided production of cottonseeds. In the case of post-
recycling textile waste, the avoided production of filling materials was taken into account. 
Regarding the recycling scenarios, it was assumed that the incineration of recyclables leads to 
heat and power generation, replacing the average market mix in the Nordics.  
 
One key challenge was determining how to allocate impacts to the textile waste feedstock in 
the recycling scenarios. The default cut-off allocation method was used. This means that the 
environmental burdens of primary material production are allocated to the primary user of 
the material, and the material input is considered free of environmental burden when 
entering the recycling chain. Although this assumption is debatable, as it likely underestimates 
the environmental impact of the recycling scenarios, we considered the economic value of 
textile waste to be zero currently. This implies that textiles are not produced specifically for 
recycling but are produced regardless of their end-of-life scenario, as recycling textile waste 
is currently more costly than incineration or landfill disposal.

5.2.2. Fibre Production Modelling 
The model for fibre production in this study is based on inventory data from the Roos et al. 
report (Roos, 2016) , which is also utilized in the Mistra report (Sandin G. R., 2019).  
 
Cotton fibre production.  
For virgin cotton fibre production, data from the Cotton Inc report implemented in ecoinvent 
3.8 (Cotton Incorporated, 2016). This dataset focuses on the production of cotton fibre in 
China, India, and the US, which may not align with the main exporters mentioned earlier. 
However, it was considered a suitable approximation of the average global cotton production. 
It was assumed that the raw cotton fibres are exported to be spun into yarn. 
 
Polyester fibre production. 
For the polyester scenario, it was assumed that the polyester used in the virgin scenario was 
100% fossil-based and of virgin origin. The data used for modelling of the melt spinning of 
polyester (PES) into fibres is based on the description by Sandin et al. (2019). Best Available 
Technologies (BAT) were employed for the process, and average technologies were utilised 
for the chemicals involved. In terms of electricity mix, a fictional mix was created to represent 
the countries included in the geographical model. 
 
Details on the activities and chemicals used to model the production process steps are 
provided in Appendix B.  
 
5.2.3. Yarn Production Modelling 
In the yarn production phase (i.e., spinning and wet treatment), the unit processes considered 
are the same for the virgin cotton and recycled cotton - mixed colours scenarios, as well as for 
the virgin polyester and recycled polyester - mixed colours scenarios. Indeed, it is assumed 
that these processes take place in the same production location and involve identical 
operations. However, for the sorted colours recycled yarns, there is a difference in the wet 
treatment applied to the yarn. 
 
Yarn Spinning. 
The environmental impact of yarn spinning is dependent on the size of the yarn. Thicker yarns, 
indicated by higher dtex values, require less energy during the spinning process. More 
specifically, according to Van der Velden (2013), the environmental burden of spinning is 
inversely proportional to the yarn size (1/yarn size). Therefore, to calculate the impact of yarn 
production, we need to define the yarn size for our functional unit. 
 
In this study, we have chosen to use an average yarn size instead on an interval. A yarn size of 
150 dtex was selected for all scenarios. This value represents an average yarn size comprised 
70 and 300 dtex, encompassing a wide range of textile products. For example:  
- 
A cotton T-shirt typically uses yarn of approximately 150 dtex, 
==PAGE:32==
 
31 
- 
Uniforms, such as those worn in hospitals, usually have yarn sizes between 150 and 
200 dtex, 
- 
Polyester dresses are typically made with yarns of approximately 120 dtex. 
Results can then be extrapolated to other yarn sizes by using the function between yarn size 
and environmental impact mentioned above.  
 
The yarn spinning processes for the cotton scenarios are modelled based on the ring spinning 
method, specifically for yarn of 150 dtex, using the inventory data from Roos et al. (2016). The 
BAT are considered for the spinning processes, while average technologies are used for 
chemicals. In the case of virgin polyester, t the production process involves melting the PET 
directly into yarn without the need for an additional spinning step. For the recycled polyester 
scenarios, the air-jet spinning method, as described in the inventory data from Roos et al. 
(2016), is utilized. 
 
Details on the activities and chemicals used to model the spinning process steps are provided 
in Appendix B.  
 
Wet treatment. 
It is important to highlight that the study assumes that the quantity of dyes and chemicals 
applied during wet treatment at the fabric level is the same for all scenarios, as the wet 
treatment is primarily considered to occur at the yarn level. As a result, the functional unit 
considers that all scenarios result in a yarn of a same colour intensity. The model used for the 
wet treatment of yarn hence depends on whether the yarn is already coloured or not, which 
differs between the different sorting scenarios. 
All processes presented in this section utilise BAT and average chemicals. 
 
Virgin cotton and recycled cotton - mixed colours scenarios:  
The yarn undergoes a bleaching process followed by dyeing and drying. The data used is based 
on the Roos et al. dataset (2016), specifically the yarn bleaching of cotton/elastane yarn in an 
OBEM machine and the dyeing of cotton/PES weave (used as a proxy for yarn dyeing).  
 
Recycled cotton – sorted colours scenario:  
In that case, the yarn is already coloured after the recycling step, so it does require bleaching. 
It is then slightly dyed to reach the target final colour, and then dried. The complementary 
dyeing process is modelled by using half of the dyeing processes compared to the mixed 
colours scenario. 
 
Virgin polyester and recycled polyester - mixed colours scenarios:  
The polyester yarn undergoes a pre-treatment process, followed by dyeing and drying. The 
data used for these processes is based on the Roos et al. inventory (2016), specifically the pre-
treatment in a jet machine of PES weave before printing and the dyeing of cotton/PES weave. 
==PAGE:33==
 
32 
 
Recycled polyester – sorted colours scenario:  
In that case, the yarn is already coloured after the recycling step, but it still requires pre-
treatment. It is then slightly dyed to reach the target final colour, and then dried. The 
complementary dyeing process is modelled by using half of the dyeing processes compared 
to the mixed colours scenario. 
 
For all yarn production processes, the electricity mix used is based on the fictional mix 
including the countries of the geographical model.  
Detailed tables of processes and data sources can be found in Appendix B.  
 
5.2.4. Collection and Sorting Model 
Currently, the collection of textile waste is far from being centralised, which constitute a main 
obstacle for a consistent model definition. It is not determined yet how the collection of the 
textile waste fraction will be rolled-out in Denmark and in the Nordics more generally. We 
therefore assumed that the collection step could be modelled by a simple transportation step 
from hypothetical collection stations to sorting plant. The distance is estimated based on the 
average radius of urban areas in Copenhagen, Stockholm, and Oslo, which is approximately 
10 km. Assuming around 20 collection points scattered across the area, the pick-up step is 
modelled as a 50 km truck transportation, followed by a 100 km transportation from the urban 
area to the sorting facility. This transportation step is modelled using an ecoinvent dataset for 
a 16-32 tonne EURO 4 lorry transportation. 
 
Additional assumptions are made during the sorting phase, which are common to all 
scenarios: 
- 
Receiving, unloading and inbound storage of the waste after its collection are mostly 
manual and represent a negligible amount of energy. They are therefore excluded 
from the system. 
- 
Pre-sorting of textile waste into recyclable and non-recyclable fractions is 
predominantly done manually. As a result, no energy consumption is considered for 
this step, but a sorting ratio is applied, specific to each quality scenario. A ratio of 10% 
of non-recyclables is selected for the high-quality scenarios and a ratio of 20% is 
selected for the low-quality ones. The non-recyclables materials sorted are assumed 
to be incinerated, which is accounted for in the model as avoided generation of heat 
and electricity (NewRetex, 2023). 
- 
Material sorting is modelled based on the energy consumption of the sorting line. 
Based on industrial data, the sorting line can treat 550kg of waste per hour. The sorting 
line has a nominal power usage of 73.3 kW, but the estimated usage factor ranges 
from 50% to 75%. Therefore, the operating power usage is approximated to be around 
40 kW, and the energy consumption is approximately 0.07 kWh/kg of waste 
==PAGE:34==
 
33 
(NewRetex, 2023). The energy mix used for this process is the weighted average of the 
Nordics. 
- 
Baling is considered negligible and is not included in the model (refer to appendix C. 
for calculations).  
 
It is important to note that the assumption is made that the textile waste received at the 
recycling plant is not suitable for re-use. Therefore, textiles are either classified as non-
recyclable and incinerated or recyclable and treated accordingly. This assumption disregards 
textiles that are pre-sorted for re-use or textiles that are not suitable for fibre recycling but 
are downcycled into products of lower quality. 
 
5.2.5. Recycling Model 
The recycling step is modelled based on the Life Cycle Inventory (LCI) data provided in the 
Study on the technical, regulatory, economic and environmental effectiveness of textile fibres 
recycling: final report by Duhoux & al (2021). The best-case scenario data for mechanical 
recycling of both cotton and polyester are selected, which include the following inputs for the 
mechanical recycling of cotton and polyester: 0.5 kWh/kg of electricity, using the European 
average electricity mix from ecoinvent 3.8, 0.02 l/kg of water.  
The output of the recycling step depends on the fibre processed. 
 
Recycled cotton scenarios. 
The output is: 
- 
0.19 kg of spinnable fibres (assumed efficiency of 20% for all scenarios),  
- 
0.03 kg of dust, 
-

- 
0.73 kg of filling materials/fluff and 0.05 kg of scrap iron for low-quality scenarios 
(Duhoux, 2021). 
 
Filling materials/fluff are modelled as avoided production of polyethylene terephthalate 
granulate. Dust is incinerated and modelled as municipal incineration of waste paperboard.  
 
Recycled polyester scenarios. 
The output is: 
- 
0.55 kg of spinnable fibres, (assumed efficiency of 55% for all scenarios),  
- 
0.03 kg of dust,  
- 
0.42 kg of filling materials/fluff for high-quality scenarios with no findings, 
- 
0.37 kg of filling materials/fluff and 0.05 kg of scrap iron for low-quality scenarios 
(Duhoux, 2021).

granulate. Dust is incinerated and modelled by municipal incineration of waste polyethylene

since they are often already accounted for in factories data on heat and electricity (Sandin G.

The recycling efficiency is assumed to be the same for both high-quality and low-quality 
scenarios because it is considered that the low-quality portion of the textile feedstock is

5.2.6. Transportation Model 
In the analysis, transportation is considered at three different steps within the scenarios: 
1. Between raw materials production and yarn processing:  
The shipping distance is calculated by taking the average distance between 3 main 
exporters of raw material and 3 main locations for yarn production. It is estimated as: 
o 7,500 nautical miles for cotton (13,890 km) 
o 2,250 nautical miles for polyester (4,167 km) 
2. Between sorting and recycling:  
The transportation of textile waste from sorting facilities to recycling facilities within 
Europe is estimated to be approximately 2,000 km (specifically, the distance between 
Spain and Denmark). The transport is modelled using the ecoinvent data on a 16-32 
tonne EURO 4 lorry transportation. 
3. Between recycling and yarn production:  
The shipping distance from recycling facilities to yarn production facilities is estimated 
to be 7,500 nautical miles or approximately 13,890 km.

4. Life Cycle Impact Assessment 
 
This section presents the results of the life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) based on 
characterised midpoint impact categories. A process contribution analysis was conducted for 
each scenario to determine the share of impact allocated to each unit process, considering 
the midpoint categories modelled in ReCiPe2016. The results of this analysis can be found in 
Appendix D. 
The process contribution analysis reveals that the impact of each process step varies 
significantly depending on the impact category and differs between the scenarios.  This 
indicates the need for further investigation and comparison of scenarios based on specific 
impact categories. Consequently, the following sections provide a detailed analysis of the 
impacts for Climate Change, Terrestrial ecotoxicity, Human toxicity (non-cancer). Additionally, 
a broader comparison is conducted, encompassing a selection of other impact categories: 
Fossil Resource Scarcity, Freshwater eutrophication, Freshwater ecotoxicity, Human (non-
cancer) and Water use. It is worth noting that Land Use was excluded from the scope of the 
LCA as the model did not provide representative values for this impact category, according to 
our judgment. All results presented in the following sections are calculated per FU, meaning 
per kg of yarn.

6. Interpretation & limitations 
 
6.1. 
Interpretation of the results  
 
The analysis of the results for both virgin cotton and virgin polyester yarn highlights the 
recurring environmental hotspots in the upstream phase of production, such as cotton 
cultivation and ginning and baling for cotton, and melt spinning for polyester. These upstream 
processes indeed contribute significantly to the environmental impact across various impact 
categories. In that sense, textile recycling offers a notable advantage by eliminating these 
upstream processes and thereby reducing the overall environmental impact. For almost all 
impact categories under study, the recycled scenarios had a lower impact than the virgin 
scenario thereby presenting an environmental benefit.  
 
In the case of the recycled scenarios, the results show that most of the environmental impact 
lie in the downstream phase, the collection, sorting and recycling steps having rather 
negligible contributions. Regarding the low impact of recycling compared to other process 
steps, it contradicts the discussion we had with industrials who stated that recycling was a 
very energy-consuming step. This discrepancy can be explained by the use of best available 
techniques (BAT) for mechanical recycling in the analysis. Indeed, this results in lower energy 
consumption during the recycling stage compared to current industry practices. Additionally, 
the electricity mix used for mechanical recycling is based on Europe's lower environmental 
impact electricity mix, further reducing the overall impact. In contrast, other process steps 
may utilise electricity from sources with higher environmental impacts, as seen in regions like 
Asia. As a matter of comparison, the average CO2 footprint of Europe’s electricity mix is 250 g 
CO2-eq/kWh compared to India’s (used as a proxy for Asia) which is approximately 590 g CO2-
eq/kWh (European Environment Agency, 2023), (Electricity Maps, 2023). This highlights the 
importance of adopting sustainable practices, including the use of BAT and clean energy 
sources, to minimize the environmental impact of textile recycling and other production 
processes. It is nevertheless important to note that the introduction of a transportation phase 
by truck in the recycled scenarios does contribute significantly to the terrestrial ecotoxicity 
potential. This highlights the need for careful consideration and optimisation of transportation 
methods to minimize the environmental impact associated with recycling. 
 
The results also shed light on the benefits of colour sorting. Indeed, sorted colours scenarios 
generally exhibit lower impact compared to mixed colours scenarios for a given impact 
==PAGE:55==
 
54 
category. This is attributed to the elimination of bleaching (for cotton) and dyeing steps, 
reducing the demand for chemicals and energy while no additional energy use is linked to the 
sorting of textile waste into colour fractions (NewRetex, 2023). Notably, the advantage of 
colour sorting is more pronounced for cotton than for polyester, as the latter still requires pre-
treatment for half-dyeing. Nevertheless, for this benefit to be worth, brands need to accept 
to use coloured recycled yarn and not necessarily re-dye them again after recycling.  
 
Furthermore, the results demonstrate that, in most cases, recycled scenarios help bridge the 
impact gap between cotton and polyester. While the virgin scenarios often exhibit significant 
differences in impact between the two fibres (with either cotton or polyester demonstrating 
higher impact according to the category), the recycled scenarios show a more consistent order 
of magnitude. This indicates that recycling plays a crucial role in equalizing the environmental 
impact between cotton and polyester. 
 
Despite the advantages of recycling, it is important to acknowledge that a significant portion 
of the environmental impact remains in spinning, bleaching/treating, dyeing, and drying 
processes. Thus, recycling alone cannot fully address the environmental footprint of textile 
production. It is crucial to consider holistic circular solutions, such as repair, reuse, and to close 
the loop as closely as possible in the production chain, through for instance, fabric recycling. 
These alternatives should be explored to effectively reduce the overall environmental impact 
associated with textile production. 
 
6.2. 
Limitations and opportunities for further research  
 
This study aimed at conducting a life-cycle assessment (LCA) comparing the environmental 
impact of virgin and recycled yarns for cotton and polyester. While the results and conclusions 
have been presented, it is important to discuss the limitations of the study and identify 
opportunities for further research.  
 
Method choice and selection of impact categories. 
The chosen method provided a holistic overview of the environmental impact of the textile 
chain. Nevertheless, the study focused on cradle-to-gate assessment, which limited the 
identification of environmental hotspots to a specific part of the textile production chain. In 
particular, it would be interesting to compare the impacts of the production chain to those of 
the use phase. Future research could hence consider expanding the system boundaries to 
include the use phase and end-of-life considerations to provide a more comprehensive 
assessment. 
Moreover, the LCA performed could have been more precise by using different models and 
comparing the results together. Indeed, ReCiPe2016 was chosen because it included a broad 
scope of environmental parameters and had a global perspective that was adapted to the 
globalised textile chain. Nevertheless, the model of some impact categories presented some 
==PAGE:56==
 
55 
limitations. For instance, land use was considered poorly modelled and was consequently 
excluded from the scope of the study. Additionally, even though we tried to model as 
thoroughly as possible the chemicals used in process steps, the modelling of the toxicity still 
probably underestimates the impacts. Future research could explore alternative methods and 
compare the results with this study or focus on specific impact categories using more 
dedicated methods. 
 
System boundaries and functional unit.  
The decision to conduct a yarn-oriented assessment led to difficulties in defining a relevant 
functional unit and drawing the system boundaries. For instance, the choice of a kilogram-
based functional unit does not fully capture the difference in quality between recycled yarn 
and virgin yarn. Indeed, one of the main challenges of recycling is to obtain high-quality yarns 
from textile feedstock of various quality due to wear and tear, laundering, and the presence 
of disruptors. As a result, if the yarn is of lower quality, a higher quantity might be needed to 
produce the same final textile product, and to ensure a similar life expectancy. Moreover, it 
would be interesting to investigate if the use phase of a recycled product is the same than of 
a virgin product (shorter lifetime, lower number of uses etc.). Further investigation is needed 
to assess the quality of recycled yarn and determine appropriate modelling approaches.  
Additionally, the assumption of 150 dtex yarn size may not be representative of all textile 
products and exploring different yarn sizes would provide a more accurate reflection of reality. 
 
Besides, the textile supply chain involves various processes, alternatives to spinning, and a 
range of chemicals. The granularity of these different processes was not captured in the 
current study as we had to choose specific processes that cannot mirror all the combinations 
of processes that can happen. Moreover, we considered that the production chain of both 
virgin and recycled fibres was identical which is debatable. Indeed, the same processes might 
not be used on virgin and recycled yarns, and they might not occur in the same locations 
necessarily. Further research could explore different process models and compare their 
impact on the results.  
 
Finally, except for the high quality/low quality differentiation, we did not take into account in 
this study the influence of findings (i.e., accessories) on recycling scenarios. A more granular 
assessment of the collection and sorting phase has been conducted by Valentina Rossi (2023) 
and details the different collection, sorting and reusing/recycling scenarios according to the 
quality of textile waste. We hence recommend combining the reading of the present 
environmental assessment of the textile production chain with this thorough analysis of the 
upstream phase.  
 
Geographical assumption. 
While the geographical scope did not significantly influence the virgin scenarios, it played a 
role in the recycling scenarios. The location of spinning and processing facilities for recycled 
==PAGE:57==
 
56 
fabrics may differ from those for virgin processes, potentially influencing the overall 
environmental impact. Further investigation into the geographical aspects of recycling 
processes would be beneficial. 
 
Multifunctionality Limitations. 
The assumption that textile waste is free from environmental burden due to its lack of 
economic value needs further consideration. Alternative approaches could involve 
considering a fraction of the upstream impact and incorporating the diversion from 
incineration. High uncertainties also exist regarding the modelling of by-products, particularly 
the overestimation of the impact of avoided cottonseed production. Further research on 
these aspects would contribute to more accurate assessments. 
 
Data quality.  
The modelling of the recycling process was subject to the highest uncertainties in the study, 
despite not being the most sensitive process in most scenarios. Industrial claims of a 75% 
efficiency for mechanical recycling contrast with literature suggesting efficiencies closer to 
20% for cotton and 55% for polyester. Further research is needed to address these 
discrepancies and validate the representativeness of the model for the recycling process.

7. Conclusion 
 
Recycled yarns display a significant environmental benefit compared to virgin yarn. Indeed, 
they reduce the need for fossil resources and water and diminish the strain on climate change 
and toxicity of all ecosystems and human health. Nevertheless, besides the technological 
challenge, implementing large scale recycling 
 is challenging as switching from the current linear textile business model to a more circular 
one requires the contribution of all stakeholders along the value chain. First and foremost, the 
involvement of end-consumers is crucial in enabling the collection of post-consumer textile 
waste. Encouraging consumers to participate in recycling programs and properly dispose of 
their used textiles is essential for creating a steady supply of feedstock for recycling operators. 
The collection and sorting industry also play a central role in the recycling process. By 
establishing efficient and effective collection and sorting systems, they can ensure that 
recycling operators have access to an adequate amount of textile waste for processing. 
Furthermore, the textile industry itself needs to embrace the use of recycled materials. By 
sourcing and incorporating more recycled materials into their production processes, they can 
help drive the development and growth of the recycling sector. Overall, the challenges of 
textile recycling are complex and require a collaborative effort by all stakeholders to overcome 
them and achieve a sustainable future for the industry. 
 
Moreover, while recycling plays a significant role in reducing the environmental impact of the 
textile industry, it is not the sole solution to achieving sustainability. Taking into account the 
benefit if switching the whole cotton and polyester supply in 2020 from virgin yarn to HQ 
mixed colours recycled yarns, the emissions avoided would account to 430 million tonnes per 
year. This is approximately equal to the sole yearly emission of CO2 of Poland in 2021 (UNEP, 
2023). This shows that even an idealistic implementation of recycling technologies will not 
enable us to reduce the impact of the textile industry sufficiently. To achieve the goals set out 
by the Paris Agreement and decarbonize the textile chain, a comprehensive approach is 
necessary. This includes optimizing energy use, reducing chemical usage, improving process 
efficiency, lengthening the life of products via circular models such as repair and reuse and 
improving the end-of-life by increasing the share of textile collected and recycled into new 
products.  
Reducing consumption of textile products, particularly fast fashion, and embracing the service 
economy by promoting renting or borrowing instead of single-use purchases are also 
important steps. Additionally, designing products with longer lifetimes and for easier 
recyclability, such as avoiding fibre blends and complex constructions, contributes to a more 
sustainable industry. Initiatives like the Ecodesign for Sustainable Product regulation provide 
an optimistic outlook for the future of the industry by promoting sustainable design practices 
and encouraging the adoption of circular principles. 
 
==PAGE:59==

Overall, addressing the challenges of textile recycling and achieving sustainability in the 
industry requires collaborative efforts and the integration of multiple strategies across the 
entire value chain.

C. Justifications for negligible processes 
 
Baling of textile can be realised with a dedicated baler with the following characteristics 
(Bramidan, 2023):  
- 
Bale weight: 25 to 80 kg of textile  
- 
Motor: 1.5 kW  
- 
Cycle time avg: 28 sec  
The resulting energy input to bale 1kg of textile waste is comprised between 0.00015 kWh 
and 0.00047 kWh which is negligible compared to the other processes of the sorting phase.

Figure 25 - Breakdown of processes' contribution to each midpoint impact category (virgin 
cotton scenario) 
Figure 24 shows that the ginning and baling of cotton fibres is the major contributor for most 
of the impact categories (16 out of 18), followed by cotton cultivation (13 out of 18). For the 
latter, it must nevertheless be considered that the avoided production of cottonseed, by-
product of cotton cultivation, reduces drastically the impact of the cultivation step.  
We notice that spinning and bleaching also contribute to a significant share of various impact 
categories. Indeed, spinning contributes to more than 15% of the impact for 7 impact 
categories, and bleaching for 8 impact categories.  
Incineration of material losses is inferior to 0.1% for all impact and is hence negligible. 
Transportation also has a very low impact, representing less than 5% for most categories 
except Photochemical oxidant formation for ecosystem quality and human health.

Figure 26 - Breakdown of processes' contribution to each midpoint impact category (recycled 
HQ mixed colours scenario) 
In the case of the recycled cotton yarn production, contribution to each impact category is 
more shared between the different processes and there is less of a main contributor to all of 
them. In this case, transportation (by truck from the sorting to the recycling facility) represents 
a large share of impacts, being the main contributor to 7 out of 18 categories. Bleaching still 
contributes to the biggest share of 5 categories out of 18 and spinning to 4 out of 18. Recycling 
contributes to more than 20% of the impact for 14 categories and is the main contributor of 
the impact in ionizing radiation potential (59%) and water use (40%). Sorting has a low 
contribution to impact categories, inferior to 4% for all categories except Ionizing radiation, 
Land use and Water use. Incineration of dust also has a low impact with a contribution of less 
than 2.5% in all impact categories. The avoided production of filling materials linked to the by-
product of the recycling step provides a significant negative contribution to almost all the 
impact categories and hence strongly influences the impact of the scenario in each category.  
 
The combined contribution of transportation, spinning, bleaching and recycling make up more 
than 75% of the positive contributions to all impact categories, underlining that they are the 
principal hotspots of the chain.

colours scenario given the absence of yarn bleaching and the limited impact of yarn dying.
