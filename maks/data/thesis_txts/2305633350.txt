Abstract  
The first aim of this thesis is to investigate the connectivity between salmon farms in the 
Faroe Islands with respect to the sea louse, L. salmonis, and see if the connectivity varies 
between summer and winter conditions as well as between spring and neap tide. A numerical 
model based on tidal forcing was constructed to study the dispersion. Connectivity matrixes 
were constructed as well as several distribution maps of the mean age and weighted 
infectious time. Results indicate a complex connectivity network with highly variable 
connection patterns between salmon farms in the Faroe Islands. Farms could be identified as 
being emitters, receivers or isolated. And we were able to identify key farms in the 
connectivity network. A complex dispersion circulation was also identified. There were some 
noteworthy differences between L. salmonis released during summer and winter as well as 
between neap and spring tide. The second aim of this thesis was to see if the obtained 
information could contribute to a Faroese treatment management plan. Farms identified as 
emitters should be strictly managed to prevent unnecessary external infection pressure on 
other farms. Farms located in exposed areas can act as catalysers amplifying the connection 
in the whole farm network system. The obtained information emphasized that a holistic 
approach is needed in any treatment management plan for the Faroe Islands to ensure steady 
and sustainable growth of the industry.

1. Introduction  
1.1. Aquaculture and Sea Lice 
Aquaculture is a vastly expanding industry and at the moment the fastest growing food 
industry worldwide (Subasinghe, 2005). In 2013 aquaculture production worldwide increased 
by 5.6 % compared to 2012 and annual growth is still forecasted to continue (FAO, 2015). 
The total aquaculture production (finfish, crustaceans, molluscs and other aquatic animals) 
was 70.2 million tonnes in 2013 (FAO, 2015). The global production of Atlantic salmon 
(Salmo salar) was 2.08 million tonnes in 2013 (FAO, 2013). Norway is by far the largest 
producer of salmon in the world with about 1.2 million tonnes (FAO, 2013). Faroe Island is 
the fourth largest producer of Atlantic salmon in the world with 70 893 tonnes of Atlantic 
salmon in 2014 (Hagstovan.fo, 2015), 25 % higher than the peak production in 2003 before 
Atlantic salmon farming industry almost crashed due to an epidemic caused by Infectious 
Salmon Anemia (ISA) (FAO, 2014). 
Currently one of the major challenges in growing salmon at sea is the infection of sea lice. 
Sea lice are marine ectoparasitic copepods. Copepods or Copepoda is a subclass of 
Maxillopoda, they are a group of small crustaceans present in all kinds of aquatic habitats and 
is described as the most abundant metazoans on earth. Copepoda contains over 250 described 
families with 2600 genera and 21,000 described species (Walter & Boxshall, 2016). They are 
an important ecological link in any marine food chain. Copepods graze on microscopic algal 
cells and serve as food for larger crustaceans, juvenile fish and even whales. Thereby they 
link the vast phytoplankton biomass present in marine and fresh water environments to the 
rest of the food chain. They are also important as marine parasites as approximately one third 
of copepod species spend their lives as associates, commensals or as parasites on 
invertebrates and fish (Humes, 1994).  
What we commonly refer to as sea lice are parasitic copepods belonging to the family 
Caligidae, especially the genera Caligus and Lepeophtherius. In salmon production in the 
North Atlantic there are two species of major importance, the Lepeophtheirus salmonis and 
Caligus elongates. Especially the former species, L. salmonis, causes the greatest damage and 
the majority of literature on sea lice in salmon aquaculture is also focused on this particular 
species. L. salmonis is only found on salmonids, where as C. elongates has been reported on 
80 different species including salmon (Kabata, 1979).

This thesis primarily focuses on L. salmonis by investigating the connectivity between 
salmon farm sites due to tidal currents with regard to this particular species. L. salmonis feeds 
on the fish mucus and scales (Pike & Wadsworth, 1999). The erosion of the fish epidermis 
harms the host by lowering its immune system, altering the osmotic pressure resistance and 
stressing the fish at many levels and in worst case this can led to the death of the host (Pike & 
Wadsworth, 1999). These effects cause the salmon to be more susceptible to diseases, 
experience lower growth rates and lower overall quality of meat. This compels the salmon 
farmers to treat their fish to control the degree of infection. The elevated number of sea lice in 
the natural environment due to salmon farming causes an increased infection pressure beyond 
what is regarded natural to wild salmon leading to a depression in the abundance of wild 
salmon stocks (Costello, 2009a; Heuch, et al., 2005; Krkošek, et al., 2007a; Krkošek, et al., 
2007b; Todd et al., 2006). As for the Faroe Island, there is no knowledge of any indigenous 
wild salmon population, however, there has been a stocking program since the early 1960’s. 
The knowledge of this population is nevertheless very limited (Føroya Sìlaveiðufelag, 2016). 
It is estimated that on a global scale the salmon farming industry experiences, directly and 
indirectly, losses of approximately €300 million annually (Costello, 2009b), making sea lice 
the most economically significant marine parasite worldwide. These values are nonetheless 
most likely an underestimation of the total cost as they do not include labour involved in 
management and treatment of sea lice, negative publicity, compromised health, the effect of 
the treatments (pharmaceuticals/toxins) to the local environment, and the wild salmon 
decreasing stocks due to sea lice epidemic etc. The Faroese farming industry alone spends € 
20 million on sea lice treatment based on the same calculations done by Costello (2009) using 
a treatment costs of € 0.19 per kg of fish (www.fiskaaling.fo, 2015). 
How sea lice larvae spread or disperse and transmit in their environment is still an ongoing 
scientific question. The dispersion and transmission patterns between farms are highly 
dependent on the hydrographical variations in the respective region. There are even 
differences within individual farms and between cages. Some farms can regularly experience 
severe lice epidemics while others do barely get affected (Pike & Wadsworth, 1999). The 
explanation for such variations is complex, it is primarily due to location specific physical 
and biological conditions and secondarily, the farm animal husbandry practices including 
disease management. There has been a growing trend in recent years to explain and predict 
the dispersion and transmission patterns using numerical models based on hydrodynamic 
models and particle tracking algorithms (Amundrud & Murray, 2009; Salama et al., 2011; 
Gillibrand & Willis, 2007; Adams et al., 2012, Stucchi, et al., 2011; Asplin et al., 2011). The

numerical studies investigate very different aspects of the sea lice dispersion. Gillibrand and 
Willis (2007) found that larvae behaviour as avoidance of low salinities and phototactic had a 
significant effect on the dispersion pattern in an idealized coastal inlet. Amundrud and 
Murray (2009) investigated the Loch Torridon, a fjordic sea loch on the west coast of 
Scotland, and found that local winds had the dominant influence on the dispersion patterns of 
particles. Salama et al., (2011) applied a numerical model at the Loch Linnhe system in the 
western Highlands of Scotland and found that sea lice can be transported over large distances 
before becoming infectious (up to 30 km). Stucchi, et al. (2011) investigated the Broughton 
Archipelago on the coast of British Columbia, Canada, and showed that their numerical 
model could to some degree predict the abundance of sea live larvae. Adams et al., (2012) is 
the most relevant numerical study with regard to this thesis as they examined the connectivity 
between 9 farming sites in Loch Fyne, on the west coast of Scotland. They found that such an 
approach enabled them to gain insight into the sea lice dispersion patterns between 
aquaculture farming sites and thereby could aid in management decisions. All the research 
applying numerical models and referred to above concluded that more validation is needed. 
However, they all agree that the information that can be obtained can serve as bases for 
treatment management plans, placement of new farm sites and how wild salmon stocks are 
potentially affected.   
1.2. Treatment in Faroese farms  
Faroese law states that farmers are obliged to delouse the entire fish farm if numbers of adult 
female or mobile stages per fish in any fish pen, exceeds values of 2 or 10, respectively 
(www.logir.fo, 2009). Treatment of sea lice in Faroe Islands salmon farms has mainly been 
the use of antiparasitic pharmaceuticals. The most widely used are baththerapeutants 
pyrethroids and Hydrogen Peroxide as well as the in-feed compound emamectinbenzoate and 
diflubenzuron. In recent years Hydrogen Peroxide has been the preferred treatment for larger 
fish in sea cages. Emamectinbenzoate and diflubenzuron are normally used as treatment for 
smolts and smaller fish. The Faroese Fish Farmer Association has made an agreement on 
chemical use which entails the use. The agreement states that tat each group of compounds 
must only be used once for a generation of salmon at a specific site with the exception of 
Hydrogen Peroxide (Patursson, 2014). 
Sea lice, like most parasites, develop resistance to chemical treatment especially when used 
heedlessly, this is observed throughout the salmon farming industry (Jimenez et al., 2011).

1.3. Water and Tides around Faroe Island 
The Faroe Island are an island archipelago in the middle of the Atlantic Ocean with 
complicated coastlines, multiple embayment’s and connecting sea straits, see figure 1 upper 
picture. The sea around Faroe Islands can be characterized as a tidal energetic system. Due to 
the North Atlantic Current water derivative from the Gulf Stream, the sea around Faroe 
Island is dominated by relatively warm and saline waters entering the Faroe shelf on the 
western side. On the shelf there is a steady current clockwise around the islands, partly driven 
by tidal rectification and possible also density-driven (Larsen et al., 2008).There is intense 
mixing on the Faroe shelf caused by strong tidal currents, wave and wind action, creating a 
homogenous water masses. The average temperature on the shelf ranges from 6 °C in 
February to approximately 10 °C in August and September. The sea temperature on the shelf 
is close to uniform. However, temperature differences has been observed in periods to be up 
to 0.5 °C warmer in the southernmost island in winter, most likely due to eddies originating 
from the Scottish Slope Water penetrating through the North Atlantic Current onto the shelf 
(Larsen et al., 2008). The salinity on shelf water around the Islands normally ranges from 
35.1 ‰ to 35.2 ‰, very seldom exceeding 35.30 ‰  (Gaard & Hansen, 2000; Hansen & 
Østerhus, 2000; Larsen et al., 2008).

1.4. The thesis 
The knowledge of how individual salmon farms in the Faroe Islands are connected by sea is 
restricted. The current sea lice treatment management plans may therefore be ineffective. 
This thesis seeks to answer two central questions: 
1. What is the connectivity between the Faroese salmon farms?  
a. Does the connectively pattern vary between: 
i. Summer and winter 
ii. The spring-neap cycle 
2. How can this information aid in a treatment management plan for Faroe Islands? 
 
To date there is no numerical particle tracking model for the Faroe Islands and was therefore 
developed for this thesis to address the questions above. Connectivity is basically measured 
by recording particles, simulating L. salmonids larvae, released at one site and entering 
another site and staying in or re-entering the initial release site. The movement of particles 
will be determined by the tidal current velocities. Only tidal currents are included as this is 
believed to be the most significant parameter in Faroese on shelf water, however currents in 
fjords and straits are not well reflected in tidal currents alone as other parameters like wind 
and freshwater have a major influence.    
Basic knowledge about tides and a relatively thorough elaboration of tidal constituents will 
be outlined in the following sections. The sea lice simulated will be L. salmonis. A section of 
the basic biological information of this species will be included, and the life stages of special 
interest in a modelling perspective will be elaborated on further.       
As we will see there are some clear trends obtained from the results and this information 
could be implemented directly in a treatment management plan.

2. Background 
2.1. Tides  
One of the most important and regular ocean phenomena are the tides. Tides are the rhythmic 
rise and fall of the sea water, observed twice a day in most places around the world. Tides are 
generated because the earth rotates on its axis and spatially varying gravitational forces 
exerted by the moon and the sun force an ocean elevation and create a tidal current response. 
The vast deep ocean water masses are directly affected by these astronomical forces. The 
tidal effects in coastal regions are not directly generated by the attraction of these 
astronomical bodies but by tidal waves or a combination of tidal waves, which propagate 
from the deep ocean basins through shallower coastal waters influenced by the shape of the 
ocean basin, the variable being the bottom topography and the Coriolis force (Pawlowicz et 
al., 2002). 
Over the centuries there have been several theories attempting to both explain and predict 
oceanic tides. The first real breakthrough came with Newton’s theory of gravitation. Later on 
Laplace made major contributions by mathematically explaining the astronomical forces that 
cause tides, also called tidal potential. Furthermore he formulated the equations of tidal 
motion on a rotating earth and solved the unique case where all of the Earth’s surface is 
covered by water. Tidal analysis and prediction using the harmonic method was 
simultaneously and independently developed by Lord Kelvin and Ferrel. This method was 
further expanded by Darwin, Harris and Doodson (Foreman & Henry, 1989). 
The tidal potential is set up by the Earth-Moon and the Earth-Sun system. The principles are 
the same in both systems. If we take the Moon as an example, it creates two equally large 
tidal bulges on Earth, one facing the Moon and one on the side facing away from the Moon. 
The tidal bulges are a result of gravitational and centrifugal forces. Together, the Moon and 
the Sun create the spring-neap tidal cycle resulting in high-high tides and low-low tides every 
fortnight (14.8 days). Although the Sun has a mass 27 million times that of the Moon, the 
Moon has by far the largest influence on tides on Earth. This is because the Moon is a lot 
closer to Earth than the Sun, 390 times closer. The tide-generating force of the Sun is 
calculated to be about 46 % to that of the Moon (Defant, 1961). The tidal potential is 
relatively weak on a small scale, however when active over a large area the oceans respond 
by setting up a tidal wave which, as mentioned above, is determined by the shape of the 
ocean basin, their topography and the Coriolis force. The Coriolis force makes the tidal wave 
travel clockwise in the Northern Hemisphere and counter clockwise in the Southern

Hemisphere. The rise and fall of the sea surface in a tidal wave causes the water to move 
back and forward creating tidal currents. It should be noted that at high and low tide the tidal 
currents are low. The tidal currents are highest midway between a high and low tide.    
2.1.1. Tidal Constituents 
Tides are usually separated into their different frequencies and each frequency or constituent, 
is described by its own amplitude and direction. Although there are about 390 tidal 
constituents, in practise only a handful of constituents will be enough to predict most of the 
tidal behaviour (Defant, 1961). These tidal constituents can be divided into astronomical tides 
and compound tides or overtides, sometimes referred to as shallow water tides. Astronomical 
tides can be traced back to the gravitational forcing by either the moon or sun and are those 
that appear in the tidal potential. They are dominated by diurnal and semi-diurnal 
frequencies; the semi-diurnal frequencies because there are 2 tidal bulges on the earth – one 
on the near side and one on the far side of the sun or moon; the diurnal frequencies because 
the earth is tilted with respect to the orbital planes of the earth-moon and earth-sun systems. 
Overtides are secondary components which are the product of nonlinear interactions between 
astronomical tides (Foreman & Henry, 1989). The largest tidal constituent is M2 exerted by 
the Moon while in most places S2 is the second largest exerted by the Sun, both are semi-
diurnal components and are called principal lunar and principal solar, respectively.

The combined tidal wave in a location or area in the ocean can be diurnal, semi-diurnal or 
mixed semi-diurnal, expressed as tidal height. A diurnal tide cycle has only one high tide and 
one low tide every lunar day. A semi-diurnal tide cycle experiences two high and two low 
tides of approximately equal amplitude every lunar day. An area with a mixed semi-diurnal 
tide cycle has two high and two low tides which are unequal in amplitude every lunar day.  
At any given location, the form of the tide is given in part by the tidal potential, but also 
depends on local basin geometry (their shape and depth). Some basins “resonate” close to 
some tidal frequencies amplifying these components while damping out others.   
There are also points or locations in the ocean where there is no or almost no tidal elevation, 
these are called amphidromic points. These points arise because the earth is not solely 
covered by water but by landmasses preventing the tidal wave in propagating around the 
globe. The tidal wave instead travels around the ocean basin with an amphidromic point 
located in the centre. A simple way to categorize the tide in an area as being diurnal, semi-
diurnal or mixed is done by the using the “form ratio”. The “form ratio” summarizes two 
main diurnal constituent’s amplitudes and two semi-diurnal constituent amplitudes e.g: 
 
 
       
         
(1) 
 
F = 0 to 0.25 indicates semi diurnal tides. F = 0.25 to 1.5 indicates a mixed, mainly semi-
diurnal tides, large inequalities in range and time between the highs and lows each day. F = 
1.5 to 3.0 means mixed, mainly diurnal tides, frequently only one high water per day. F > 3.0 
indicates diurnal tides, generally only one high tide per day.  
The sea around the Faroe Islands has of all types of tides. In the west region there are mainly 
semi-diurnal tides where as the east region is a combination of mixed semi-diurnal and 
diurnal tides. There is also a local amphidromic point near Tórshavn, the capital of the Faroe 
Island, located at the centre of the islands. This means that several of the fjords around the 
center of the Faroe Island experience very little difference in the tidally induced amplitude,

The constituents used in our model are from a numerical simulation of the barotropic tides

involved constituents as well as a residual current in every grid point. The generated tidal 
constituents generally agreed well with the measurement data, but was less accurate in fjords

some cases insufficient.

2.2. Sea lice – L. salmonis 
In the Faroe Islands, L. salmonis is often referred to as “laksa lús”, meaning ''salmon lice''. L. 
salmonis will be the primary focus in this thesis and with particular attention to their 
planktonic stages. Therefore the following sections will include basic biologic information 
about L. salmonis and an elaboration on the planktonic stages. 
2.2.1. Life cycle 
The life cycle of L. salmonis has 5 phases and 10 stages (Schram, 1993), see figure 11. The 
first phase contains 2 free swimming stages which are non-feeding called napulius I and 
napulius II. The second phase includes a free swimming infectious stage, the copepodid 
stage. At this stage the sea lice larvae are still non-feeding. The third phase is when the sea 
lice are attached to the host but cannot move around the host, they are non-motile. This phase 
includes 4 stages, the Chalimus I, II,II and IV. In the fourth phase the sea lice are mobile and 
==PAGE:21==
Tróndur J. Kragesteen 
DTU Aqua 
2016 
16 
 
thus can move around the host. This phase includes 2 stages called pre-adult I and II. The 
fifth phase and tenth stage is the reproductive phase and is the adult stage. All stages include 
a renewal of the exoskeleton. 
In recent years there have been disputes about the total number of stages in the life cycle of L. 
salmonis, because all currently described copepod species have 6 copepodid stages except the 
genus Lepeophtheirus which has been found to exceed that number (Hamre, et al., 2013). 
Hamre et al. (2013) illustrates evidence that L. salmonis has in fact only 2 chalimus stages 
instead of 4. However, I will only consider the traditional L. salmonis life cycle with 10 
stages and 5 phases.   
 
Figure 11. The life cycle of L. salmonis with the 5 phases (Nauplius I-II, Copepodid, Chalimus I-VI, Pre-
adult I-II and Adult) and 10 stages (Schram, 1993). 
2.2.2. Phase I  - Napulius stages 
The first stages of sea lice life are the nauplius I and II. At this stage the eggs on an adult egg 
string hatch as naupulii and are released into the environment. The sea lice larvae at this stage 
display active swimming, however free swimming is restricted to vertical diurnal movement 
in the water column (Johannesen, 1978).  
The development time of L. salmonis nauplius I and II depends on temperature at full 
strength sea water, defined as water with salinities between 33-36 ‰ (Oglesby, 1969). The 
mean duration time of nauplius I is 53.0, 30.5 and 9.0 hours at 5, 10 and 15 °C, respectively. 
Nauplius II has a mean duration time of 170.3, 56.9 and 35.6 hours at 5, 10 and 15 °C, 
respectively (Johnson & Albright, 1991). The combined duration time of both stages at 
different temperatures is plotted in figure 12 as well as a fitted curve.

2.2.3. Phase 2  - Copepodid 
After the nauplius II stage L. salmonis moults into a copepodid. The free swimming 
copepodid has enhanced swimming abilities and can, as well as nauplius I and II, adjust its 
vertical position in the water column, but the horizontal movement depends mostly on ocean 
currents (Pike & Wadsworth, 1999). It can be expected that L. salmonis will, as other marine 
parasites, display behaviours in their planktonic stages that will increase their chances of 
finding a host. There is very limited knowledge of their behaviour in their natural 
environments although many attempts to acquire insight into their behaviour in and around 
sea cage cultures have been conducted. It has been demonstrated that napulii and copepodids 
are photosensitive and migrate upwards during day light and downwards during night time 
(Heuch et al., 1995). This may nevertheless seem a bit arbitrary as salmon kept in cages 
migrate to the surface layers during night time and stay at relative deeper layers at day light 
(Oppedal et al., 2011). Heuch et al., (1995) suggest that it might be this crossing over 
between the paths of salmon and sea lice larvae that allow transmission to take place. 
Copepodids have also been shown to be sensitive to vibrations, where vibrations induce 
swimming activity towards the vibration source. Chemical cues and the olfactory system 
have as well been shown to aid and direct the sea lice larvae in finding a host (Mordue & 
Birkett, 2009). Copepodids are generally found in the upper layer of the water column 
(Heuch, Parsons, & Boxaspen, 1995; Costelloe, et al., 1998; Penston et al., 2008). This is 
==PAGE:23==
Tróndur J. Kragesteen 
DTU Aqua 
2016 
18 
 
supported by observations by Hervøy et al., (2003) which found that salmon held a 0-4 
meters depth were approximately an order of magnitude more infected than salmon held at 4-
8 or 8-12 m. 
Current velocity at the farm cages is important for the infection attachment rate of 
copepodids. Samsing et al., (2015) found that copepodid attachment rates were highest at 
current velocities corresponding to typical swimming speeds of caged farmed salmon, less at 
low current velocities and lowest at higher velocities. They explain that this may be due to 
the interplay between two factors: encounter rate and contact time.  
The copepodid stage remains non-feeding although a functional gut and developed external 
mouthparts (Pike & Wadsworth, 1999). This means that the copepodid, being the infectious 
phase, has a limited time to detect and infect a host which is dependent on the internal energy 
reserves composed mainly of lipids. The depletion time of the internal reserves is determined 
by the initial size of the reserve and the metabolic rate of the copepodid larvae which is 
controlled by the sea temperature and their movement activity.  
The scientific literature on the longevity of the copepodid stage is inconsistent. Wooten et al., 
(1982) suggest a maximum period of 4 days at 12 °C, where Johannesen (1978) found values 
as high as 30 days. In an extensive thesis by Gravil (1996), a study on the influence of 
temperature on the longevity of copepodids was conducted. Temperatures of 5, 10 and 15 °C 
with full strength sea water were investigated and copepodids where found to be able to live 
for 17, 13 and 9 days, respectively. Gravil (1996) also found that 1 day old copepodids are 
more infectious than copepodids that are 7 days olds, with infection success of 22 and 14 %, 
respectively.  Gravil (1996) suggests that this may be due to the higher swimming activity of 
the 1 day old copepodid larvae compared to 7 day old larvae which is again linked to the 
level of internal energy reserves. However, the potentially reduced infection success will not 
be included in the model. In the model it will assumed that the lifespan of the copepodids will 
follow the results found by Gravil (1996). The longevity of the copepod stage at different 
temperature is plotted in figure 13.

2.2.4. Phase 3  -  Chalimus I to IV 
When a copepodid finds and attaches to a host it undergoes a major metamorphosis and it 
moults into the chalimus I and develops the ability to feed. Further development to the 
following stages is primarily determined by the physical environment (Pike & Wadsworth, 
1999). The chalimus stages are not as damaging to the host as the pre-adult and adult stages, 
however they can disrupt the skin of their host while using their toothed ridge while feeding, 
which can be seen as a non-selective rasping apparatus like the molluscan radula (Pike & 
Wadsworth, 1999). In the chalimus stages the L. salmonis is attached to the skin of their host 
by a frontal filament and therefore referred to as non-mobile stages. L. salmonis in the 
chalimus stages grows from a length of less than a 1 mm to an average of approximately 2.5 
mm at the end of the last chalimus stage. The time it takes to develop from the chalimus I to 
the end of the chalimus IV is on average 12 day for males and 14 days for females at 10 °C 
(Hamre, et al., 2013).        
2.2.5. Phase 4 - Pre-adult I and II 
At the end of chalimus IV the L. salmonis molts into the pre-adult I stage. Both pre-adult I 
and II are mobile. The shape of the pre-adult body is designed so that the water flow presses 
them down onto the host skin and makes them be able to move around the host by use of jet 
propulsion (Costello, 2006). The length of the pre-adult I and II female is 3.7 mm and 5.2 
mm, respectively. The length of pre-adult I and II males is 2.9 mm and 4.3 mm, respectively 
(Pike & Wadsworth, 1999).

2.2.6. Phase 5 - Adult 
The morphological shape of the adult L. salmonis is the same as their pre-adults counterparts. 
However they are larger in size and posses genital complexes. The natural lifespan of an adult 
L. salmonis has proven difficult to document. The survival rate of adult L. salmonis in 
laboratory conditions is generally low, which may be due to the handling of the fish, small 
space or pure water quality (Costello, 2006). Measuring the longevity of adults on wild 
salmon has proven especially difficult. However, there is good circumstantial evidence that 
adult female L. salmonis can overwinter on wild salmon as demonstrated by (Jacobsen & 
Gaard, 1997) and thereby be able to survive for over 6 months.    
2.2.7. Reproduction  
Mating is observed between adult male and preliminary pre-adult II females. Males can be 
observed moving around the fish displaying “mate searching” behavior (Ritchie et al., 1996). 
After a fertilized pre-adult II female molts into an adult female she begins producing egg 
strings which protrude from their genital complex. The egg string can grow to be twice the 
length of the female. The egg strings contain a high number of fertilized eggs which develop 
and hatch and are released as Nauplius I into the surrounding environment. Once a female as 
been fertilized and the spermatophores have been successfully attached, the female louse is 
capable of producing multiple egg string pairs (Pike & Wadsworth, 1999). A study by Heuch 
et al., (2000) showed that a female can produce 11 successive pairs of egg strings in its life 
time. Several studies investigate and attempt to quantify the number of eggs a female can 
produce in its life time or per string. Wooten et al. (1982) found egg numbers up to 350 per 
string, Ritchie (1993) reports 439.5 ± 56 eggs per female and Tully (1989) investigated egg 
production at different times of the year and found 315 ± 135 eggs per string in January and 
107±19 eggs per string in August. Eggs strings produced at low temperatures (7.2 °C) have a 
greater total length and on average contain more eggs compared to development at higher 
temperatures (12.2 °C). However, the eggs produced at low temperatures have a higher 
percentage of non-viable eggs and the eggs are significantly smaller (Heuch et al., 2000). By 
taking basis in Heuch et al. (2000) egg numbers a female can potentially produce is 285 × 2 ×

2.3.1 Tidal dispersion

diffusion etc. However, in a tidal energetic system as the one in the Faroe Islands the 
horizontal particle dispersion will primarily be due to the effect of tides. (Ridderinkhof & 
Zimmerman, 1992 ) analysed a two dimensional tidal model of the Wadden Sea and show

2.4. Modelling sea lice  
A useful way to supplement information relating a system where practical and empirical 
observation is unsuitable due to factors such as cost, methodological and ethnical restrictions, 
is the use of numerical models (Salama & Rabe, 2013). As stated above, numerical models 
have been used to investigate how sea lice larvae dispersion in their environment (Amundrud 
& Murray, 2009; Salama et al., 2011; Gillibrand & Willis, 2007; Adams et al., 2012, Stucchi, 
et al., 2011; Asplin et al., 2011) 
The numerical modelling can be split into two parts, a hydrodynamic model and a particle 
tracking model. The hydrodynamic model expresses the direction of the current velocity in 
time and space. All the mentioned hydrodynamic models that are currently used are 3D 
models providing velocity vectors in each dimension. They are based on years of data 
collections combined with numerical simulations from tidal, wind, precipitation, temperature, 
salinity, bathymetry etc. The hydrodynamic models used in the studies above are Finite-
Volume, primitive equation Community Ocean Model (FVCOM) for Broughton Archipelago 
(Stucchi et al., 2011) and Loch Fyne (Adams et al., 2012), Bergen Ocean Model (BOM) in 
Hardandgerfjord (Asplin et al. 2011) and the eighth format of the Georgia-Fuca system (GF8)

model of Loch Torridon (Salama & Rabe, 2013). The grid spacing varies from 100 x 100 m

of unfavourable conditions like low salinities (Johnsen et al., 2014). This can be justified as 
the current velocity in this model is an average velocity over the entire water column and 
therefore vertical movement would have no or negligible effect on the horizontal 
displacement. This assumption is reasonable for L. salmonis movement outside fjords but it 
leaves out any behaviour that might occur inside fjords.

3.1.6. Boundary conditions 
There are two kinds of boundary conditions in the model. One is the outer boundary of the 
grid. If a particle advects out of the initial grid they are not allowed to re-enter the grid. This 
is an open boundary. The particle is therefore taken out of the simulations. The second 
boundary condition is between the sea and the shoreline. This boundary condition is “sticky” 
as particles getting closer to land where the velocity vector is zero slow down and if they 
cross over a particular point they get permanently stuck or stranded. The accuracy of this 
boundary condition can be debated, however can be justified because water currents do slow 
down near the shore.

5. Discussion 
In the last decade there has been an international gradual increase in the use of numerical 
models to study the dispersion of L. salmonis in all leading Atlantic salmon producing 
countries and are now at a state where fairly realistic results can be obtained (Asplin, et al., 
2014). Although the Faroe Island by quantity is the fourth largest producer of Atlantic salmon 
this is the first constructed numerical model investigating the dispersion of L. salmonis and 
farm connectivity in the Faroe Islands. 
In our model the passive particles were given lifespan features to simulate planktonic L. 
salmonis larvae and simulations were set up to investigate the connectivity between farms at 
summer and winter conditions and at spring and neap tide. The presented results clearly 
indicate a highly variable degree of connectivity between salmon farms in the Faroe Islands. 
There are some noteworthy differences between summer and winter conditions as well as 
between spring and neap tide which will be highlighted in the farm connectivity section   
5.1. Farm Connectivity  
All matrixes contain a lot of information on their own, but when interpreted together the 
information becomes very interesting and useful. Outlining all the information about specific 
connections between sites is too extensive for this thesis, instead some general trends and 
specific connections of interest will be highlighted. 
We showed that farms can be divided or ranked into three groups: receivers, emitters or 
isolated (figure 31 and 32). The five most emitting farms are 18, 16, 14, 8 and 21. 
Interestingly, all except farm 8 are close in space to farm 15 which is by far the largest 
receiving farm. If we look at the connectivity matrix (figure 30) farm 15 also receives the 
largest proportion from the top emitting farm sites. Farm 15 also has the largest receiving 
area, which indicates that the manually defined receiving area around a farm site influences 
the results. 
There are some interesting differences in emitting patterns between summer and winter as 
well as between spring and neap tide. Farms located at the center of the Faroe Island emit 
more in winter compared to summer where as the northern and southernmost farms emit 
more in summer compared to winter. A possible explanation for this could be that farms 
located at the northern and southern parts have dispersion patterns that lead away from the 
Faroe Islands. Therefore in winter conditions when the larvae stay in the napulii stages for a 
longer period a larger proportion of larvae disperse away from the islands before becoming 
==PAGE:69==
Tróndur J. Kragesteen 
DTU Aqua 
2016 
64 
 
infectious and are therefore lost to the open ocean. As for the farms located at the center they 
may have dispersion paths that cause larvae to stay within the islands and thereby emitting to 
more farms in winter conditions due to the prolonged longevity of the infectious stage. 
Between spring and neap tide there seems to be a predominant trend that farms emit more at 
spring tide, see figure 46. This makes sense as higher currents disperse larvae faster around 
the islands. 
The connectivity matrixes show that most farms are predominantly self-infectious. The 
degree of self-infection is stronger in summer conditions than in winter conditions, likely due 
to the prolonged length of the non infectious stage in the winter scenario. There are however 
some fjords and straits that hold on to all L. salmonis in both scenarios. Most likely this is 
because the release sites are placed in tidally “dead zones” that express unrealistically long 
retention times. This could be resolved by including more forcing parameters and will be 
discussed later on. Many of the highly self-infectious farms are also very isolated (figure 44, 
see farms 10-12 and 20). It was more difficult to identify any obvious trend between L. 
salmonis released at spring and neap tide, however farms seemed to experience more external 
infection at spring tide and more self infection at neap tide.    
Three farms 5, 9 and 13 express a low degree of self-infection. Farms 5, 9 and 13 also emit to 
a higher or lower degree infectious larvae to all other farms. This can be explained as these 
farms are in tidally exposed areas, see figure 29, where the L. salmonis larvae are carried with 
the currents away from their source shortly after they are released and only re-enter after 
almost 2 weeks (summer scenario). Farms 5 and 9 also receive a large amount of external 
infectious larvae where farm 13 does not. By looking at the location of farm 13 we see that it 
is located at the start of the large narrow strait on the north side. The only significant 
contributor to the external infection in farm 13 is farm 11. And if we look at the dispersion 
patterns in figure 41 and 42, we see that there is a relatively high concentration of weighted 
infectious time dispersing right by farm 13 without really passing through farm 13. This 
emphasizes that a slight change in the dynamics of the system can significantly influence the 
connection outcome.  
The connectivity matrix showing the weighted time the infectious L. salmonis spend in a 
farm area is somewhat misleading in the current simulation setup, because it overestimates 
large farm areas. And farms like 5 and 9 which receive a considerable amount of infectious L. 
salmonis larvae get a weak signal because the larvae travel right through these small farm 
areas. But the strength of this connectivity matrix is that it directly accounts for the mortality 
and time they spend in a farm area. This is certainly a useful and more accurate way to 
==PAGE:70==
Tróndur J. Kragesteen 
DTU Aqua 
2016 
65 
 
investigate farm connectivity, especially in a model where the current dynamics in the fjords 
and the large narrow strait (farm area 11 and 12) are more realistic and where the actual area 
of the farm sites could be incorporated. 
The distribution maps shown in figures 36-38 and 40-42 clearly illustrate the dispersion 
pathways of L. salmonis larvae around the Faroe Islands and give a good intuitive 
understanding of the whole system. There seems to be two large clockwise dispersion 
circulation cells, one around the main islands and the other around the southernmost island. 
These circulation cells are also connected throw a smaller circulation cell around the island 
located between the main islands and the southernmost island. In summary these result 
indicate that the Faroe Islands are surrounded by dispersion “highways” transporting L. 
salmonis around the islands. 
There was an attempt to group farms together, see figure 33, 45 and 48. This can be useful in 
a management perspective as such zones should have coordinated treatment plans. Farms 1, 2 
and 3 (group 1) are strongly connected and isolated from the other farms by emitting and 
receiving negligible amounts of infectious larvae to and from other farms, however the 
internal connection is weaker at winter conditions (figure 45). Farms 4-9 (group 2) and farms 
13-19 and 21 (group 3) were also considered to be internally well connected. However, there 
was a fairly strong connection between these two groups indicating that group 2 and 3 should 
perhaps not be grouped separately. The degree of connection between the three groups can 
also be explained by dispersion patterns we see in figures 36-38 and 40-42.  
5.2. Study Comparison 
It is somewhat difficult to directly compare our results to other numerical modelling studies 
on L. salmonis as the obtained results are highly area specific. The Faroe Islands are quite 
unique being an island archipelago in the middle of the Atlantic Ocean with homogeneous 
water with respect to salinity and temperature and where shelf water currents are mainly 
determent by tides and are generally high. The other numerical models also include many 
other parameters. But generally we found that L. salmonis larvae released at tidally exposed 
sites disperse far and wide and L. salmonis larvae released inside tidally “dead zones” stay in 
their respective release area, which is somewhat trivial. The only numerical study that also 
directly examines the connectivity between farms is (Adams et al., 2012) and they as well 
found that most farms are to some degree self-infectious, and that some farms serve as 
emitters and other as receivers. They also identified some important farms that could be key 
==PAGE:71==
Tróndur J. Kragesteen 
DTU Aqua 
2016 
66 
 
farms in the control of the whole sea lice population in the region. A thesis on sea lice 
population dynamics in Faroese salmon farms by (Patursson, 2014) agrees fairly well with 
our results where he found the same fjords expressing internal and external population 
dynamics. 
5.3. Management Implications 
The insights into the connectivity of farms can be used as input into management 
considerations related to reducing sea lice infections. Means of reducing sea lice infection in 
salmon farms could be to place them in higher current velocities by placing the farms further 
out in the fjords (Samsing et al., 2015). This will reduce the contact time and the rate of self-
infection. However, based on our results this would amplify the connection between farms in 
the Faroe Islands and increase the encounter rate. Therefore care should be taken for such 
measures. Farms 5, 9 and 13 are currently placed in exposed areas and in some sense they act 
as catalyser to the whole connection network. The effect of these farms as “emitters” should 
therefore be evaluated before placing new farms in these locations. On the other hand, by 
placing farms in more isolated areas will increase the degree of the self infection emphasizing 
that there is no simple solution to this problem. 
Another alternative way of possibly reducing the connectivity of the whole system could be 
to place filter feeders like mussels at key locations around the Faroe Islands to remove sea 
lice from the water column (Davenport et al, 2000; Molloy et al., 2011). Our model could be 
used to estimate the optimal locations for such filter feeders. 
The current Faroese aquaculture legislation states that farmers should undertake sea lice 
treatment measures if there are 2 adult females or 10 mobile L. salmonis per salmon. And 
there is currently a heated debate in the Faroese media about this legislation because there has 
been an unusually high number of L. salmonis observed from September to January 
2015/2016. The results presented here clearly indicate that the current allowed sea lice per 
salmon should be lowered as it would reduce the strength in all connections and thereby 
lowering the self-infection of the whole farm system, so to speak. If our model was improved, 
validated and could simulate more realistic results it could be used directly to estimate the 
level sea lice per salmon that should be permitted.

5.4. The Model 
Our model is a two dimensional numerical model where the water velocities are solely 
created by tidal forcing and L. salmonis larvae are passive particles with lifespan features. 
The following sections will highlight the effect of the included parameters. The only 
sensitivity test we conducted was on the time step in the numerical integration, but there 
could have been conducted other sensitivity test to see the effect of the placement of 
individual farm sites, diffusion, mortality, development time, longevity of the infectious stage 
ect. Due to insufficient time these tests were not conducted, but the effects will be discussed. 
5.4.1. Grid 
The grid is a horizontal map with 100 m spacing. This resolution is relatively high and few 
numerical models use a higher horizontal resolution (Salama & Rabe, 2013). However, as the 
grid is two dimensional only resolves the mean velocity in a water column. This is not likely 
a problem in the shelf currents around the Faroe Island as there is no stratification in these 
areas. But inside fjords and narrow straits where stratification might occur and where upper 
water layers may travel in one direction and lower layer in another direction which cannot be 
resolved in a two dimensional horizontal grid. Increased accuracy of the fjord dynamics 
would therefore require a stratified model. 
5.4.2. Time Interval 
Determining what time interval to apply in the simulations was a critical step in the model 
development. The particle tracking model is based on the Euler scheme method. A time 
interval of 0.005 h (18 s) was found to be sufficient by looking at the movement and 
dispersion pattern of particle clusters from three different locations simulated with different 
time intervals and as well as considering the CLF condition. Although the simulations 
became more similar with a decreasing time interval there was still a visible difference 
between the cluster movement and the dispersion pattern. Adams et al., (2012) also applied a 
simple Euler scheme and used a time step of 0.621 s for their simulations. In further 
development of the numerical model the particle tracking model should be reconsidered and 
possibly adapt the 4th order Runge-Kutta method which is the most widely used method in the 
particle tracking model and will increase accuracy and probably allow a larger time interval. 
==PAGE:73==
Tróndur J. Kragesteen 
DTU Aqua 
2016 
68 
 
5.4.3 Tidal Forcing 
In the tidal dispersion section we showed how fast and wide tides can disperse particles 
around the Faroe Islands. In contrast to the other numerical models investigating Loch 
Torridon, Loch Linnhe, Broughton Archipelago and Loch Fyne which to some degree are 
enclosed water bodies where the tidal effect is less evident compared to the other forcing 
parameters. It can be argued that the dominating factor contributing to the movement of shelf 
water and particle dispersion is tides and therefore it can be justified to only include tidal 
currents in the model. The tidal model was validated by (Simonsen & Niclasen, 2011) and 
they found the water currents velocities around the Faroe Islands generally fit well. However, 
the fjords are less resolved, meaning there are relative few data points inside many of the 
fjords and narrow straits and the current velocities inside these areas, although representative, 
have a relatively large error, percentagewise. This issue should be improved in any further 
development of the model. 
5.5. Model Extensions 
The model can be extended in many aspects. All the mentioned numerical models that also 
simulate particles imitating planktonic L. salmonis larvae build on a 3D hydrodynamic model 
coupled with a particle tracking model. And they included tidal and wind forcing, freshwater 
input, varying salinity and temperature as well as particle turbulent diffusion. Some included 
larvae movement behaviour while others assumed a passive particle drifting with the water 
currents. The following sections will discuss how possible model extensions would 
potentially influence the obtained results.    
 
5.5.1. Diffusion 
Particle dispersion due to horizontal turbulent diffusion was not included in the model. We 
showed that particle dispersion around the Faroe Islands will mainly be caused by tides and 
therefore decided not to include turbulent diffusion to save time and not to add an uncertainty 
to an already uncertain model. However, turbulent diffusion will have a significant effect in 
areas were the tidal effect is less evident and e.g. would allow particles to advect out of and 
into these currently isolated areas. The effect of including diffusion in the model should 
under all circumstances be investigated further.      
==PAGE:74==
Tróndur J. Kragesteen 
DTU Aqua 
2016 
69 
 
 
5.5.2 Wind 
We did not include wind forcing in the model although the Faroe Island is a windy place with 
strong wind all year around (Larsen et al., 2008). Wind will have a significant influence on 
surface layer movement of the shelf water assumed to withhold the highest concentration of 
L. salmonis. Periods of inward wind into a fjord will force outside water to be pushed into the 
fjord and hold the existing water inside the fjord. In this case fjords will experience a period 
of a higher degree of self infection and possibly a higher degree of external infection 
pressure. In periods of outward wind in the fjords there is often observed an increased out 
flow of surface water on one side of the fjord and an increased inflow of surface water on the 
other side of the fjord. In this case we would observe a decrease in the self infection pressure 
and a potential increase in external infection pressure. Again, this points to a need for better 
fjord modelling.    
5.5.3. Freshwater Input 
Freshwater input can have a large influence on salinity and water circulation in an area 
especially in semi enclosed water bodies like fjords and narrow straits. Freshwater input has a 
negligible effect on shelf water salinity and does not directly affect current velocities on the 
shelf water around Faroe Islands. However, freshwater input greatly influences the 
circulation dynamics in fjords and narrow straits. Freshwater input into a fjord creates a less 
dense surface layer which advects out of the fjord forcing deeper and denser water to the 
surface in the head of the fjord replacing outwards flowing water. By not including 
freshwater input in the model leads to an underestimation of the outward flow in the fjords 
and straits, thereby possibly overestimating the self-infection pressure we observe in the 
results. (Gaard et al., 2011) studied Kaldbaksfjord (number 10 in figure 29) which they 
characterized as a typical Faroese fjord. They estimated a residence time between 5 and 14 
days and the long term average exchange rate of about 10 days due to freshwater input. In our 
model particles placed in the middle of this fjord did not escape the fjord within 24 days. This 
emphasizes the need for incorporating freshwater input into the model in order to more 
accurately estimate the fjord circulation.  
5.5.5. Biological Parameters 
Validating the biological parameters of L. salmonis is somewhat difficult as the knowledge of 
sea lice in their natural planktonic environment is virtually nonexistent and the existing data 
==PAGE:75==
Tróndur J. Kragesteen 
DTU Aqua 
2016 
70 
 
is based on laboratory experiments (Pike & Wadsworth, 1999). The validation of numerical 
models would greatly benefit from stronger evidence about these stages especially in their 
natural environment like studies done by á Norði, et al., (2015). Nonetheless, the L. salmonis 
planktonic stages were assumed to follow the naupulii development time, copepodid lifespan 
and mortality presented by Johnson and Albright (1991), Gravil (1996) and Stien et al., 
(2005), respectively. As shown in the reproduction section the potential number of naupulii 
that can be released by one single farm is vast (50-100 million/day). Therefore the particles 
simulating the planktonic L. salmonis larvae can be seen as ‘meta-particles’ representing a 
cohort of larvae (Gillibrand & Willis, 2007).  
5.5.6. Naupulii Development  
The mean duration time of newly released naupulii to develop to an infectious copepodid is 
assumed to follow the data by Johnson and Albright (1991) and is dependent on temperature 
at full strength sea water. The duration of the naupulii stages is approximately similar to the 
other numerical models. In our model all naupulii develop to copepodids at exactly the same 
time at a given temperature. This is in contrast to reality as the development time varies, 
where some nauplii will develop to copepodids at an earlier stage and some at a later stage. 
This can present a problem e.g. if we consider that naupulii which are very close to the 
copepodid development stage are carried through a farm area, they will not be recorded, but 
in reality some of these naupulii will have developed to infectious copepods at this point and 
will be highly infectious. This information is lost by having all L. salmonis turn “on” at 
exactly the same time. Although this does not present a major problem it should be taken into 
consideration when the model will be further developed by having a variable development 
time.      
5.5.7. Copepodid 
Viable information about the copepodid stage is difficult to come by and the existing 
information is inconsistent. We made the chioce to follow the values found by Gravil (1996). 
Again all copepedids are turned “off” after a given time at a given temperature. The longevity 
of the copepodids found by Gravil (1996) is longer than found by several others. 
Overestimating the longevity was considered better than underestimating it. Because by 
underestimating the longevity excludes possible connections between farm sites that might be 
of interest. Therefore it was decided that it was better to take a radical longevity and 
==PAGE:76==
Tróndur J. Kragesteen 
DTU Aqua 
2016 
71 
 
simultaneously record the age leaving the viability of the copepodids in a given connection 
up for debate.   
5.5.8. Mortality 
The mortality of the L. salmonis larvae was set to be 0.17 d1 throughout the planktonic life, 
although Stien et al (2005) proposed a 0.22  d-1 mortality for the copepodids. Other numerical 
models use a 1 % h-1 mortality (0.24  d-1) throughout their planktonic lifespan (Adams et al., 
2012; Amundrud & G., 2009; Salama et al., 2011). Gillibrand and Willis (2007) use a 
variable mortality dependant on salinity and life stage. The salinity in Faroese shelf water 
ranges between 35 – 35.3 ‰ and occasionally observed to drop below 34 ‰ in some fjords, 
therefore increased mortality due to low salinity was not considered in our model. As well 
with other biological parameters mortality is an uncertain estimate of the real mortality 
especially for an elusive copepod like L. salmonis. Therefore more research is needed to 
make a better estimate of this important parameter.   
5.5.9. Vertical Movement / Sea lice Behaviour  
One aspect that was not considered in our model was the vertical movement or behaviour of 
the L. salmonis larvae. L. salmonis larvae were assumed to have a vertical position in the 
upper water layers where transmission is more favourable. Gillibrand and Willis (2007) states 
“Larval behaviour is complex, and models that depict sea lice as passively dispersing 
particles are over-simplistic and cannot be used as management tools with confidence”. This 
may be true for fjords and narrow straits where stratification occurs and water currents are 
generally low. But simulating L. salmonis as passively dispersing particles in the shelf water 
around Faroe Islands where the vertical position of particles will virtually have no effect on 
the horizontal movement should be a justified simplification.  
5.6. Perspectives 
The presented results can have many implications that can benefit the Faroese aquaculture 
industry. First of all the results indicate that a holistic management approach must be taken in 
any treatment management plan for the Faroe Islands. And farms that are emitters should be 
strictly managed to prevent unnecessary external infection pressure on other farms. Currently, 
there is no coordinated management plan for the Faroe Islands, however our results clearly 
indicate that such a plan should be developed as salmon farms are not isolated but connected 
==PAGE:77==
Tróndur J. Kragesteen 
DTU Aqua 
2016 
72 
 
to each other. The farms can be grouped into at least two cluster as farms in the southernmost 
island are practically isolated. 
In this thesis we have focused on the dispersion of L. salmonis larvae, but the numerical 
model can also be adjusted to investigate the dispersion of any disease agent or pollutant 
related to aquaculture or any other industry.

6. Conclusion 
The first aim of this thesis was to investigate the connectivity between Faroese salmon farms 
and see if the connectivity varied between summer and winter conditions as well as between 
spring and neap tide. In summary, we found that salmon farms in the Faroe Islands display a 
complex connectivity network with highly variable connection patterns. And we were able to 
identify farms as being emitters, receivers and isolated as well as identify key farms to the 
whole connectivity network (farm 5, 9 and 13). The farm connectivity pattern was roughly 
the same in both summer and winter conditions, however farms located at the center of the 
Faroe Islands emit more in winter compared to summer conditions where as farms located in 
the northern and southern islands emit more in summer compared to winter conditions. A 
clear difference between L. salmonis released at spring tide and neap tide was difficult to 
identify, although there was a predominant trend that farms emit more at spring tide as well 
as farms seemed to experience more external infection at spring tide and more self infection at 
neap tide.   
The second aim of this thesis was to see how the obtained information could aid in a 
treatment management plan for the Faroe Islands. We showed that the obtained results can be 
directly implemented and contribute as information to a treatment management plan. First of 
all, a holistic management approach must be taken in any treatment management plan for the 
Faroe Islands. Farms identified as emitters should be strictly managed to prevent unnecessary 
external infection pressure on other farms. Farms located in exposed areas can act as 
catalysers amplifying the connection in the whole farm network system. The effect of such 
farms should therefore be evaluated before placing new farms in these locations. 
Although the presented model can to a certain degree simulate the realistic dispersion pattern 
in the shelf water around the Faroe Islands it lacks the necessary resolution in the fjords and 
the large narrow strait in order to better predict the realistic dispersion in these areas. Also 
parameters like wind and freshwater input are not included and will highly influence the 
dynamics of the system. However these problems can be resolved with better fjord 
modelling. Larvae behaviour was not considered although it has been shown to have a 
significant influence on the dispersion in an idealized coastal inlet (Gillibrand & Willis, 
2007). The current lack of knowledge of the L. salmonis biology prevents realistic modelling 
of salmon lice and therefore the need for better process understanding of lice biology must be 
emphasized.

All leading producers of Atlantic salmon in the world have been and are developing realistic 
numerical models to predict the dispersion of sea lice. Even though the Faroe Islands is the 
fourth largest producer of Atlantic salmon there was up to this point no such numerical 
model. In this sense this thesis is a pioneer study. By including more parameters and 
validating the model to measured data this numerical model can, with enough resources, be 
developed into a invaluable tool for the aquaculture industry in the Faroe Islands and ensure a 
steady sustainable growth of the industry.
