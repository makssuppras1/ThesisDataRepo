ABSTRACT 
Construction Site Layout Planning has been historically considered by construction managers a 
task that can be performed during the construction activities, without any specific strategy. Only 
in the past few decades, the effect that a well-planned construction site has on productivity and 
safety has been appreciated. Nowadays contractors not only are aware of the importance of this 
task but also its complexity. It is difficult to consider all the parameters that contribute to an 
optimized construction site layout. Furthermore, since the layout planning is usually performed 
just before the construction phase, the designers cannot consider the buildability of the design 
at the early design stage, affecting the quality of the end product. 
In the past 20 years, many researchers have developed models and algorithms to automatically 
optimize the geometry of the construction site. However, in the majority of these researches, 
the productivity is only associated with a single parameter which is the travel path distance 
between facilities on the site. Although this is an important factor, the productivity of the 
construction site is affected by multiple parameters, due to its complex nature. Furthermore, 
the productivity of the performed activities should not be the only optimization goal, as the 
quality of the end product and the working conditions for the workers on the site, along with 
their safety, should also be considered. 
In this thesis, Generative Design was identified as the most suitable technology to perform multi-
objective optimizations. Therefore, each step of its optimization process was separately 
analyzed, identifying the software to be used to develop a generative design algorithm for the 
generation of optimized construction site layouts. 
Following the chosen methodology, a tool was developed through an iterative process in which 
feedback was collected by testing the functionalities of the tool on a case study and by 
communicating with professionals from both academia and the industry. This led to an accurate 
evaluation of the approaches to use for the generation of the geometry and to determine the 
measurable parameters that contribute to the performance of the construction site towards the 
goals of the optimization. 
Furthermore, the tool was made suitable to be used at the early design stages. This is possible 
thanks to the early available information that the algorithm uses as input and the integration 
provided for multiple software commonly used at the conceptual design phase by designers. By 
providing feedback on the construction phase at early design stages, this tool has the potential 
of improving the collaboration between designers and contractors.

1 INTRODUCTION 
1.1 CONSTRUCTION MANAGEMENT – PAST AND CURRENT PRACTICE 
The Architecture, Engineering and Construction (AEC) industry is composed of multiple 
organizations that collaborate in the design and the construction of buildings or civil 
constructions such as bridges, dams or urban spaces. The players who are involved in this 
industry are architecture firms, engineering firms and contractors. This last player is central to 
the framework of this thesis. The main objective for a contractor is physically building the 
construction. The management of the construction process is known as construction 
management. 
The role of the construction manager is of importance for the smooth proceeding of the site 
operations. The tasks assigned to it include such as the scheduling of activities and delivery of 
supplies, the site layout planning and the monitoring of performance. Among these, the site 
layout planning has been historically the most neglected aspect as engineers have been treating 
it as a task that can be performed during the construction, without any kind of pre-planning 
(Heap, 1987). This task consists of locating all the facilities used to support the construction 
activities on the construction site, such as cranes, storage and utilities in the most efficient 
manner. 
The first report stating that the construction site planning affects the productivity was published 
in 1986 by the Construction Industry Institute (CII), where this task was recognized as critical for 
the success of the project (Construction Industry Institute, 1986). Simply reducing the travel 
distances among facilities would result in a reduction of time and costs on the entire project, as 
it is estimated that the labour costs constitute approximately 30% of the total project cost 
(Moore, 2005). Other positive aspects of a well-designed site layout such as the conditions of 
the working environment, which contributes to the quality of the final product and, more 
importantly, to the safety of the workers are also appreciated nowadays. 
Construction Site Layout Planning (CSLP) is commonly performed by the construction manager 
assigned to the project, who draws the plan of the construction site including the most 
important facilities that need to be placed in it during the construction phase. As it is easy to 
guess, the efficiency of the layout is strongly dependent on the experience of the construction 
manager and the company. The organization usually gives the construction managers access to 
a database of layouts of previous projects, from which they can get ideas that can be adapted 
to the current case. However, common sense and experience are usually not enough to consider 
every factor that affects the efficiency of the operations and working conditions on the site.

1.2 ZÜBLIN A/S PRESENTATION 
Züblin A/S is a Danish construction company, which is part of one of the leading European 
technology groups in the construction industry: STRABAG (Zublin A/S, 2020). 
The importance and the complexity of CSLP are well-known among contractors and it is, in fact, 
one of the most challenging and time-consuming tasks for the construction managers. 
Therefore, research projects to provide support for handling a complex task such as CSLP is of 
interest for many construction companies. Züblin A/S has shown particular interest in this goal 
and it is for this reason involved in this thesis project. Collaborating in this project for Züblin A/S 
firstly means, having the chance of optimizing its construction site layouts, thereby increasing 
the productivity on the construction site and ensuring improved working conditions for its 
employees. Secondly, reducing the time that the construction manager spends on CSLP, who 
can focus on other aspects of construction management such as scheduling activities or planning 
material deliveries.

1.3 PROBLEM DEFINITION AND RESEARCH QUESTIONS 
The complexity of CSLP is derived from the number of variables that need to be considered. 
These are related to the selection of the elements to include in the plan, their locations and their 
interactions. Specifically, the main problems arising on the construction site are derived from 
the distance between facilities and workplace, the location of the material stacks, the 
equipment and the site facilities and the spacing dedicated to both workspace and access roads 
(Mamat & Zin, 2016). In Figure 1, these problems are classified on a scale from 0 to 5 according 
to their recurrence.

productivity such as increased travel distance or double handling of materials, others to the

Moreover, the site plan is not a stand-alone task, it is correlated to the scheduling, the 
construction method, the materials used in the construction, manpower and equipment 
available for the project and the financial planning (Elbeltagi, 2014). This means that all these 
other planning tasks can be affected by a poorly performing construction site layout. This is 
indicative of how impactful a well-planned layout is for the construction process and, on the

other hand, how challenging it is for a construction manager to consider all these factors. 
Therefore, the main research question of this thesis is: 
How can the construction manager be supported while performing CSLP? 
Another interesting aspect to evaluate is the integration of construction planning with the 
previous phases of the design process. Lacking consideration of the construction phase 
requirements during the earlier design phase means that the construction manager needs to 
adapt the layout to fit the design. This often leads to poor buildability and therefore, poor quality 
of the end product as, at this stage, any suggested changing on the design would be very 
expensive to be applied. This is a common issue for every discipline in the traditional design 
process, which often leads to design changes at later design stages, causing an exponential 
increment of the project cost. The use of Integrated Project Delivery (IPD) and Building 
Information Modelling (BIM) has the potential to improve communication and interoperability 
among the stakeholders and therefore, to support the decision-making at the early design stage 
(Glick & Guggemos, 2009).

However, CSLP is commonly performed just before starting the construction phase and 
therefore, it is not included in the information stored in the BIM model. For the designers to use 
the construction management feedback as one of the criteria for the design, CSLP needs to be 
included at the early stages of the design process and in the list of requirements along with 
requirements for daylight, structure and energy consumption etc. This leads to a second 
research question: 
How can CSLP be integrated at the early design phase?

1.4 THESIS OBJECTIVE 
This project aims to respond to the research questions posed in the previous section and to 
develop a tool to address them concretely. The original idea proposed by Züblin A/S was the 
developing a tool capable of auto-generating optimized construction site layouts in terms of 
time, cost, safety and quality, given the final model of the building; a specific tool to substitute 
the construction manager on the CSLP. However, acknowledging the poor collaboration 
between contractors and designers, it was decided to instead proceed towards the development 
of a tool that could fill this gap. The tool should not only support the construction manager 
decision-making but also provide feedback to the designer who can thus adapt the design at 
early design stages, avoiding higher costs of change at later design stages. This means that the 
tool needs to be used at the early design stage and therefore, its input cannot be based on the 
final model of the building but rather on simple inputs available at the beginning of the design 
process. 
Therefore, the tool developed in this thesis project does not substitute construction managers 
but rather supports their work through the generation of a range of coarse geometries of 
optimized construction site layouts in terms of time, cost, safety and quality, from which the 
construction manager can start detailing. Keeping a low Level of Detail (LOD) is the key to having 
a generic system, usable for a variety of projects, and that does not require detailed inputs. This 
allows the tool to be used at the early design stage, providing designers with the possibility to 
check the design concept from a construction management perspective and to consider the 
received feedback in the next design iteration. 
This gives the possibility of reaching multiple objectives: 
 
Supporting the construction manager to produce optimized construction site layouts 
 
Reducing the time spent by the construction manager for CSLP 
 
Improving the collaboration between construction managers and designers 
 
The chapter structure presented in this thesis report is created after the steps of the chosen 
methodology. Each chapter contains the adopted procedures and the results obtained at each 
step of the research and development process. This facilitates a continuous flow of information 
for the reader.

2 BACKGROUND 
This chapter describes past researches and studies on the optimization of the construction site 
layout. The different techniques and used software are described and categorized along with 
the limitations that had to be overcome during the development of this project. 
2.1 CONSTRUCTION SITE LAYOUT MODELLING 
The optimization of the construction site layout planning has been addressed in a vast variety of 
ways in the past decade, ranging from mathematical models to knowledge-based systems 
(Papadaki & Chassiakos, 2016). Despite the diversity among the methods, all the studies include, 
even if in different forms, the site space modelling and the site objects modelling. This consists 
in translating the inputs for the CSLP into a geometry that can be read and used by the 
optimization algorithm. The different approaches used by the authors for the modelling of these 
elements are categorized by Sadeghpour and Andayesh (2015) and therefore, the following 
subsections reflect the same structure used in their article. 
2.1.1 Site space 
The definition of the site space is the first step towards the realization of the construction site 
model. In analyzed studies, three different approaches that have been adopted: predetermined 
locations, grid system and continuous site space (Sadeghpour & Andayesh, 2015). 
The predetermined locations approach consists of pre-allocating spaces onto the layout where 
eventually the objects can be placed. These spaces can vary both in shape and in size. This 
approach is simple and it is optimal for cases where only specific spots are available for placing 
objects, however, it reduces the flexibility of the model as the space can only be filled in a 
specific way. Furthermore, it requires a pre-examination of the site when the locations are 
determined and therefore, it is not applicable without an initial user evaluation. 
The grid system approach consists in dividing the site space by orthogonal lines to create a grid. 
Each cell of the grid is a possible location for the objects to be placed. In contrast to the previous 
approach, with the grid system, all the site space can be used, however, the accuracy of the 
approach is dependent on the size of the cells of the grid. Indeed, the available spots to place 
objects are the centre of the cells and locations such as the lines in between the cells are not 
considered in the search for the location. Furthermore, after an object is placed, even if it only 
partly fills the cell, that cell would still be considered unavailable for the following objects to be 
placed. Of course, the finer the grid is, the less impactful these limitations become. On the other 
hand, the finer the grid is, the longer the computational time gets. 
==PAGE:19==
 
7 
 
The continuous site space approach is the most accurate and the one that best represents 
reality. In this case, the positioning of the objects is not limited by predetermined locations or 
grid lines, every point on the site surface can be used for placing an object. However, using this 
approach, the algorithm gets heavier due to the checks that have to be done for avoiding 
conflicts among objects. While in a grid, a finite number of cells are labelled as filled when an 
object is occupying them, with a continuous space, an infinite number of points is occupied by 
the objects and therefore, a different checking method is required. 
2.1.2 Site objects 
In this thesis, site objects mean all the elements that need to be placed on a construction site to 
ensure the correct proceeding of the activities on the site such as the crane and the site offices. 
In the following paragraphs, these objects are classified by typology and mobility, and different 
approaches for their representation are presented (Sadeghpour & Andayesh, 2015). 
2.1.2.1 
Typology 
The objects that need to be placed on the construction site during the planning phase are 
multiple and vary from project to project. According to Sadeghpour and Andayesh (2015), these 
objects can be categorized in 6 typologies: construction equipment, material lay-down and 
storage areas, temporary facilities, access roads and on-site paths, workspace and site obstacles. 
Construction equipment constitutes all the machinery that needs to be installed on the 
construction site for performing the construction operations as cranes, elevators and concrete 
pumps. 
Material lay-down and storage areas are spaces where the materials to be used during the 
construction are placed. The lay-down area is usually the part of the site where delivery trucks 
discharge their supplies. This area is dedicated to the materials that have to be used not long 
after the delivery on the site and therefore, can be used for the just-in-time technique. This is 
one of the main points of the LEAN management, a management approach that aims to 
eliminate every factor that waste time or resources from the process, keeping it lean, indeed. 
The material storage area instead, contains materials that can be stored for a long time. 
Containers are usually used for storing these materials so that they are not affected by weather 
conditions and are theft safe. 
Temporary facilities include all structures that function as services for workers and managers 
such as offices, toilets and changing rooms. These facilities are usually installed in containers 
that can be stacked on top of each other to minimize their space utilization on the construction 
site. 
==PAGE:20==
8 
 
Access roads and on-site paths are the spaces of the construction site dedicated to the 
movement of both workers and vehicles. The placement of these is important to define the 
travel distance among objects, which affects the efficiency of the site. 
The category workspace includes the required space to perform activities on the construction 
site such as carpentry, form-making and concrete preparation. Buffer space around objects can 
also be included in the workspace category. 
Finally, site obstacles constitute non-movable objects on the construction site, which can be 
existing buildings or natural obstacles like trees. 
2.1.2.2 
Mobility 
Another characteristic to take into account when modelling an object on the construction site is 
its mobility. Regarding this aspect, three are the possibilities: stationary object, movable object 
and moving object. 
An object is classified as stationary when its position is fixed throughout the whole period it is 
present on the construction site or it is very inconvenient to be relocated. A typical example of 
a stationary object is the tower crane. The cost for the relocation of the crane is so high that it 
is not desirable to move it around the site. This makes the position of the crane crucial for the 
efficiency of the planning and it has to be carefully chosen. 
Normally, the relocation of objects is to be avoided. However, in some cases, this waste is 
compensated by the increased efficiency of certain tasks at a certain stage of the construction 
process. In that case, the object is classified as movable. For example, a rebar workshop yard 
assumes high importance at the early phases on the construction, but when the structure is 
complete, it could be replaced with a pipe storage yard, for instance. What affects the efficiency, 
in this case, is the travel distance between objects. Therefore, the goal is choosing locations that 
minimize the distance between objects that are most related to each other. Since this 
relationship can vary at different stages of the construction process, their locations can vary 
accordingly. 
When an object has to change location to perform tasks, it is classified as a moving object. The 
most classic example of a moving object is a truck. On the construction site, these objects still 
require space that can assume the shape of a path or area on which these objects can freely 
move across. However, moving objects turn into stationary when not in function, which means 
that unless they leave the construction site as soon as their task is completed, they also need a 
location to be still. In the case of trucks, a parking lot.

2.1.2.3 
Representation 
The objects, like the site space, can be modelled in different ways by using different approaches. 
In this case, the approaches from the simplest to the most accurate are: dimensionless objects, 
approximate geometry and actual object shape. 
The dimensionless object approach is a very simple approach in which the objects are 
represented as single points. Since the elements do not have any shape or size, checking conflicts 
with other elements is impossible. Therefore, this approach only makes sense when the site 
space is divided into predetermined locations, as in that case, each available location is filled 
with a different object and overlaps are not possible. 
The approximate geometry approach consists of a representation of the objects considering 
their size but approximating the actual shape with a simple geometrical shape such as a square, 
a rectangle or a circle. This approach allows much more flexibility of the construction site layout 
compared to the previous method but it still requires less computational power compared to 
considering the actual shape of the objects. This is because the objects are represented by fewer 
edges and curves, which makes it easier to make them fit together and to analyze the overall 
geometry. This is the most used approach as it has a good balance between calculation 
complexity and accuracy. 
The actual object shape approach is the most realistic one. With this approach, objects are 
represented with their actual shape. This method is particularly useful when the construction 
site sits in a tight space such as a narrow road in the city centre. In this case, every inch is 
important for the efficiency of the site and approximating the shape would mean losing such 
spaces. The actual object shape approach expresses its maximum potential when applied 
together with a continuous site space approach. Using the grid system approach would reduce 
the accuracy of the model as the object, even if placed with its actual shape, would be adapted 
to the grid, simplifying its shape according to the size of the cells. On the other hand, this way 
of modelling is very demanding for the computer that runs the analysis. 
The combination of the approaches used by different studies for modelling the site space and 
the site objects can be represented by a matrix. In Figure 4, the representation of the site objects 
is called object boundary. For each of the possible combinations, a study that used that approach 
is cited.

2.1.3 Time dimension 
As mentioned above, the objects in the construction site can not only change position but can 
also appear and disappear from the site at different time stages as different tasks are performed 
over time. Although it is obvious that the construction site has a dynamic nature, almost none 
of the past studies have modelled the construction site to be time dynamic. Two simplified 
approaches can be used to reduce the complexity of the model: static and phased (Sadeghpour 
& Andayesh, 2015). 
The static approach does not consider the dynamics of the construction site. Every object stays 
on the site for the entire duration of the construction. Considering that normally, objects are 
moved or replaced over time, this is considered an extreme simplification that can only be 
applied in construction sites with an abundant area, where the construction manager does not 
need to fill every single square meter and the layout can be looser. 
The phased approach is midway between the static and dynamic approach. The phasing method 
entails dividing the construction process into phases, each of them referring to a certain 
timespan. This approach consists of analyzing the optimal position of the objects on a 
construction site at different times for each phase of the construction. This means that the 
accuracy of the method depends on the number of phases. From this point of view, the static 
==PAGE:23==
 
11 
 
approach can be seen as a single-phased approach and the dynamic approach as an infinitely-
phased approach. This approach is more accurate than the previous one but it adds complexity 
to the computation. The complexity is higher not only because the analysis has to be done more 
than one time, but also because the layout outputted from an analysis affects the following one. 
Subsequently to the discussion on the mobility of the objects, most of the objects on the site 
are either fixed or not convenient to be moved. Therefore, the layout at a successive phase 
should always take into account the location of these objects in the previous phases. 
The dynamic approach is the one that best reflects the reality of the operations on the 
construction site. This is also called 4D optimization and it can only be used when all the tasks 
of the construction process have been scheduled. Results of previous studies show that the 
dynamic approach has a higher chance of allocating the objects in optimal locations considering 
the whole construction process. In fact, with this approach, objects can be removed from the 
layout as soon as the task that they are supporting is terminated and can thus get replaced by a 
new object. It is recommended to use this approach especially if the available space on the site 
is narrow and therefore has to be carefully used.

2.1.4 Optimization method 
In the mentioned studies, a variety of models for the analytical optimization of the construction 
site layout have been used. These models are usually characterized by nature-inspired 
algorithms, which can be classified into Artificial Intelligence (AI), Evolutionary Algorithms (EA) 
and Swarm Intelligence (SI) methods (Papadaki & Chassiakos, 2016). One of the most famous AI 
methods is the Neural Network (NN), which is largely used in machine learning. NN imitates the 
functions of the human brain by a network of nodes for the exchange of information which 
simulates the neuron activity (Kim, 2017). Within the EA methods, the Genetic Algorithms (GA) 
are the ones that most capture the attention of researchers. These are algorithms based on 
evolutionary principles and simulate the evolution of a species generation after generation 
starting from an initial population (further explained in Chapter 2.2.3). Finally, two of the most 
used among the SI methods are the Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) and the Ant Colony 
Optimization (ACO). The first one is inspired by the social behaviour of flocks of birds and schools 
of fish, where the position of the single particles is iteratively updated to find the globally best 
path for the entire swarm. The second one, instead, is inspired by the behaviour of ants when 
looking for food. In this case, each ant chooses a random path to reach the food until the ant 
travelling the shortest path releases the highest concentration of pheromone within the colony. 
This makes the other ants starting following the shortest path (Vu, et al., 2016). 
The effectiveness of all these methods has been demonstrated in the last decade by multiple 
studies on the optimization of the construction site layout. However, the majority of these 
studies focuses on optimizing a single parameter, which is commonly the travel path distance. 
This is indicative of how specific but also limited these studies are. The factors that affect the 
efficiency on the construction site are multiple and minimizing the travel distance among 
facilities should only be one of the goals of the optimization. On top of this can be added 
parameters not related to efficiency, but safety on-site or quality of the working environment. 
Since the goal of this project is to perform a multi-objective optimization, the capability of 
simultaneously optimizing more parameters is the one to be researched in the chosen method. 
Among the presented methods, Genetic Algorithms (GA) is the best performing in this regard 
(Farmakis & Chassiakos, 2018) and thus, it was chosen for the development of this project. 
Furthermore, GA is the foundation of one of the most promising tools in the AEC: Generative 
Design (GD), which is investigated in-depth in the next section. 
2.2 GENERATIVE DESIGN 
Generative design is an iterative process that generates multiple design alternatives and uses 
evolutionary principles to select and optimize the best-performing ones. Recently, this approach 
==PAGE:25==
 
13 
 
is making its way both into the manufacturing and construction industry, especially in the field 
of architectural design (Autodesk Inc., 2020). 
Among the many benefits of generative design, there are two of particular impact on the 
construction industry. First, the construction industry is still operating with old practices and 
most of the times, companies prefer to stay in the comfort zone, leaving little room for 
innovation. Moreover, designers, after years of experience, tend to prefer efficiency over 
creativity, tending to reapply previously used solutions in new projects. The GD allows new ideas 
to be quickly tested so that designers have the freedom to experiment and explore new, 
innovative solutions. 
Second, there are many requirements that designers need to meet when designing a building. 
It has always been a challenge to optimize every aspect of the design such as structural design, 
energy consumption, daylight etc. and most of the times, the overall optimal solution is a 
compromise among the singular optimized solutions. The GD allows designers to quickly explore 
a wide range of solutions and it is capable of optimizing these solutions through iterations. 
Within the generative design vocabulary, the generated design alternatives are called 
population, while the iterations to which the design is subjected are called generations (Krish, 
2011). Figure 6 shows the iterative nature of the generative design process along with its steps: 
Generate, Evaluate and Evolve, which are separately analyzed in the next subsections. 
 
Figure 6 - The iterative nature of the generative design process 
2.2.1 Generate 
The first step of the generative design process is the generation of alternatives. The different 
solutions are generated through random variations of parameters value within certain ranges. 
The nature of these parameters and the ranges are defined by the user and they depend on the 
objective of the optimization. The number of generated alternatives is equal to the population 
set by the user. The setup of this process is commonly done through a parametric model, which 
allows designing by using parameters instead of the traditional drawing tools (further explained 
in Chapter 2.3.2). 
==PAGE:26==
14 
 
Taking as example one of the sample files published by Refinery Team and The Proving Ground 
(2018), the object of the generation is three cuboids to be assembled in different ways to create 
different building compositions. In this case, the parameters are the height and location of the 
three cuboids. The height ranges from 20 to 200 meters and the location can vary from 0 to 50 
meters both in the X and the Y direction on the XY plane. 
 
Figure 7 - Sketch of the sample optimization objective (Dunne, 2019) 
2.2.2 Evaluate 
After the generation, the alternatives are analyzed and ranked in the evaluate phase. In this part 
of the process, the geometry is queried to evaluate the performance of each alternative. Each 
parameter that affects the performance is broken down into factors and separately analyzed. 
Each of the analyzed parameters contributes to defining the score that the evaluated alternative 
reaches towards the goals of the optimization. In this passage, there is also the possibility of 
giving different weights to the contribution of the factors to the overall score, in case they have 
different levels of importance. The overall score of each alternative is used to rank them from 
the best to the worst performing one. 
Following the previous example, the goal of the design is to find the best alternative to maximize 
the volume of the composition and at the same time, minimize the surface area. This way, the 
designer maximizes the profit because the rental area is maximized and the cost of the façade 
is minimized. 
2.2.3 Evolve 
The last part of the process consists of optimizing the result by using GA. In practice, with the 
use of these algorithms, it is possible to push the design towards the goals that the user wants 
to achieve. Optimizing the design means maximizing certain characteristics and minimizing 
==PAGE:27==
 
15 
 
others. Once the goals are defined, the algorithm starts looking for the characteristics carried by 
the geometries that better fit the goals and quickly teaches itself to create new geometries 
similar to those. This process is iterative and it creates more geometries at each generation until 
the user is satisfied with the result. Creating a GA from scratch is possible, but it is common 
practice to use an optimization engine that works with validated algorithms both to save time 
and ensure the validity of the result. 
From the previous example, to optimize the solution towards the defined goals, the algorithm 
isolates the inputs which affect these parameters. In this case: height of the cuboids and their 
locations of the plane. The algorithm starts to change these inputs within their ranges, selects 
the best performing alternatives and applies the principles of GA to optimize the solution. 
Of course, in a simple example, the optimized solution can be obtained with simple 
mathematical calculations.  However, the reality is always more complex than the theory. For 
instance, if one were to include in the equation the goal of having a certain daylight factor in a 
given percentage of the floorplan or maximizing the visibility towards a certain direction, the 
solution would not be so obvious. When dealing with multi-objective optimizations, the optimal 
solution is expected to be a compromise among the given goals. Here is where the potential of 
GA can be truly appreciated. 
2.2.3.1 
Genetic Algorithms 
As GA is the foundation of generative design, the purpose of this paragraph is to describe their 
origin and to explain the principles that are applied to the optimization of the design when using 
them. 
Since the 1950s, several research groups have been interested in taking inspiration from nature 
when solving problems. In particular, Darwin’s theory of evolution was taken as a foundation for 
studies regarding optimization problems. The evolution of organisms and the adaptations that 
these organisms undergo, depending on the environment they live in, is a very clear example of 
optimization. This idea led to the development of various Darwinian-like evolutionary systems. 
Among them, there are Evolutionary Strategies, Evolutionary Programming and Genetic 
Algorithms (De Jong, 2009). 
Genetic Algorithms, exactly like an evolutionary process, has an initial population and a certain 
number of generations that are supposed to evolve the initial population into a population more 
adapted to the conditions of which the system is built. There are three base mechanisms in 
Genetic Algorithms that work to change the initial population, all of them taken from nature: 
selection, crossover and mutation (Gegundez, et al., 2007). 
==PAGE:28==
16 
 
The selection is the process that drives the evolution towards the best individuals of the 
population or the ones that best fit the conditions that the system imposes. In biology, the 
function that drives this mechanism of selection is called fitness. The fitness is not trivially 
determined by a single statement such as “the strongest survive”, instead it is a function 
determined by a combination of characteristics that affect the reproductive success of the 
organisms. These can be, for instance, the degree of health or even the attractiveness of an 
individual (Byerly, 1986). A certain gene that determines a characteristic of an individual 
becomes dominant in a population if the individuals that carry it are the ones with the highest 
reproductive rate so that the gene is passed to the next generation through breeding. 
The crossover is the mechanism used to combine genes of selected individuals to generate 
better offspring. This only happens for sexually reproducing species and the fact that many 
species have independently developed sex to recombine genes is proof that it is a successful 
strategy. Organisms that have asexual reproduction can only give birth to copies of themselves. 
This means that if a virus or a disease can affect one individual, it can also affect the entire 
population. Crossover, by comparison, gives diversity within the population and different 
capabilities by recombining genes from the selected individuals. A fact that highlights the 
importance of the crossover is that males only exist to bring crossover into a population. Hence, 
it is more convenient to have only 50% of individuals that can give birth (females) and having 
crossover, rather than having 100% of those individuals without crossover (Ridley, 2011). 
Finally, the mutation is a mechanism that introduces random modifications of an individual, not 
related to ancestry. This has the potential to further increase the diversity within the population 
and provides the possibility for new characteristics to spawn, that none of the others has ever 
had previously. Mutations have a certain probability to happen in different species and they are 
usually related to the number of generations. For example, mutations might happen on average 
every 10 generations in one species and on average every 100 generations in another species. 
These three mechanisms adapted to GA provide the potential to simulate natural evolutionary 
processes to solve and optimize computational problems. The fitness function is determined by 
the parameters that are evaluated in each alternative. The selection happens when the 
alternatives are rated and sorted according to their performance. The new generation process 
considers the characteristics of the alternatives that best performed in the previous generation 
and mixes these characteristics through the crossover. Furthermore, mutations are useful to 
create diverse alternatives in case of stagnation in improvement. Of course, if low performing 
alternatives are created through the mechanisms of crossover and mutation, those alternatives 
are discarded in the following generation through the selection mechanism (Osman, et al., 
2003). 
==PAGE:29==
 
17 
 
2.2.4 Limitations 
Even if the use of generative design seems to be helpful for designers in many ways, other 
studies have demonstrated its limitations. Specifically, Davis (2020) lists four limitations of the 
generative design that deserve to be mentioned. 
Building a generative design algorithm is like building a factory that produces design outcomes. 
This means that to obtain good outcomes and explore a wide range of alternatives, the factory 
needs to be accurately designed. Creating the factory is more difficult and requires more skills 
than designing manually and if the developer is not skilled enough, there is the tendency of 
setting too many limitations. This leads to a generative design algorithm unable to explore a 
vast variety of solutions and that instead, creates hundreds of similar-looking alternatives. 
The creation of alternatives is random and the generation part of the algorithm cannot recognize 
a good from a bad solution. This means that to obtain a good solution, hundreds of non-viable 
solutions are normally created. This tendency of these algorithms to prioritize quantity rather 
than quality can be described as buying hundreds of lottery tickets. The user knows that an 
enormous amount of computational power is used to create non-viable design options but 
he/she confidently waits until the winning ticket is pushed out. 
Making a comparison among hundreds of solutions is difficult. In literature it is known as “the 
paradox of choice” and it refers to the fact that humans are afraid to make a choice when dealing 
with lots of options. This is simply because the chances of choosing a wrong solution are higher. 
Furthermore, it has been demonstrated that humans particularly dislike when the alternatives 
are similar to each other, making the decision process even harder. This is often the case of 
generative design, where the outcomes are similar to each other but not identical. 
It is not possible to measure everything that matters. When querying a geometry, only 
geometrical parameters can be quantified. For example, in the case of construction 
management, the evolution and changes of the object on the construction site and all the other 
aspects dependent on time cannot be appreciated in a singular optimization. To do that, the 
user should make more optimizations at different phases of the construction process and then 
make evaluations aside from the generative design algorithm. 
An acute awareness of the limitations of generative design informed the development of this 
project. By applying an accurate evaluation of the approaches to apply to the generation of 
alternatives, it was made possible to leave to the developed algorithm the freedom of exploring 
a wide range of solutions while ensuring the viability of the generated geometry. Furthermore, 
a careful evaluation of the parameters to be analyzed was done to include all the factors that 
affect the performance of the construction site in the evaluation, while still narrowing them 
==PAGE:30==
18 
 
down to a few main objectives in the final step of the process for easy identification of the best 
performing solutions.

2.3.1 Building Information Modelling 
In 2020, the use of Building Information Modelling (BIM) is widespread, and its usefulness 
unquestionable. During the past decade, BIM has demonstrated its potential to support 
architects, engineers and contractors. With the use of BIM technology, an accurate 3D model of 
the building is constructed in a digital environment which supports all the stakeholders to 
visualize what is to be built and therefore, to identify any potential design and construction issue 
that, without this technology, would likely only be noticed at later stages, in the construction 
phase (Azhar, et al., 2008). On the contrary to the previously used technology, which was only a 
digital drawing of the building, BIM contains a database in which data of each modelled 
component can be stored. This creates a collaborative environment where all the stakeholders 
involved in the project can exchange information through the BIM model, without the addition 
of any other document. This new paradigm encourages the collaboration between parties, 
which is the key to increase productivity and quality and to decrease cost and delivery time of 
the project (Jeong, et al., 2009). 
In construction management, BIM is a valuable support for many areas. Figure 8 shows the 
results of a survey published by (Ku & Taiebat, 2011), where construction companies which have 
integrated the use of BIM in their routines could state in which field BIM has been implemented. 
According to the survey, constructability (including clash detection) is the aspect of the 
construction management that makes the most use of BIM. Visualization, intended as the 
possibility of checking the design, both in a pre-construction phase and during the construction, 
obtained the second position, while the third place is occupied by site planning, which is 
confirmed to be one of the aspects that can benefit the most from BIM.

Many BIM software developed by different companies are on the market. Some of them are 
mostly used in the conceptual phase of the design process such as SketchUp, Revit and Tekla, 
others are used for analysis, for example, Navisworks and Solibri. 
In Denmark, BIM has been used for more than a decade as the Danish government made it

2.3.2 Computational design 
The term computational design has emerged in the AEC industry in the last decade (Caetano, et 
al., 2020) and has rapidly become popular among architects and engineers. Computational 
design is a parametric representation of geometry, which can be modelled not by drawing tools, 
==PAGE:32==
20 
 
but indeed by setting the parameters that describe it. Such technology is extremely useful for 
exploring different design solutions by adjusting parameters, especially at the beginning of the 
design phase, where the size and the shape of the geometry are not well defined yet (Yiwei & 
RenXia, 2010). 
Furthermore, the computational design allows users to create tools to generate geometries. 
Therefore, whenever the problem that the geometry is designed to solve, is not a one-time 
problem but it is recurrent, it is smart to use computational design to create an “industry” which 
produces solutions to that problem. This technology brings a revolution in the design process, 
which passes from being manual drafting based, to an automatic process. 
Software such as Dynamo and Grasshopper are open-source computational design platforms 
where the users can build their own tools through visual programming. Considering that the 
users of these software are mainly architects and engineers, hard coding a computational design 
tool is extremely difficult for the average user (Charntaweekhun & Wangsiripitak, 2006). Visual 
programming helps this process by allowing the user to write code through flowcharts, where 
the nodes are pre-coded to perform certain tasks, and the user is only called to connect inputs 
and outputs of consecutive nodes to create logics. 
Dynamo is directly connected to Revit and developed by the same company: Autodesk, while 
Grasshopper is a plug-in of Rhinoceros. Both Dynamo and Grasshopper are suitable for 
developing the tool for this project but since Revit is the chosen BIM software, the choice of the 
software for building the tool was Dynamo. However, Grasshopper for Rhinoceros is widely used 
during early, conceptual design phases, both by architects and engineers. Therefore, within the 
development of the tool described in this thesis, the possibility of integration between the two 
software was explored (described in Chapter 4.6). 
Dynamo can operate both as a plug-in for Revit and as a stand-alone application, which makes 
the software very versatile. It includes a large variety of nodes that can be used in visual 
programming for many different tasks such as designing sketches or managing data. Dynamo 
also gives the user the possibility to create custom nodes both by using a combination of the 
existing nodes or by Python scripting, for more expert programmers. These nodes can be 
included in packages and published, to make them available for other users, who can simply 
download the nodes and integrate them into their flowcharts. Dynamo’s creators have built an 
online community where learning material and tutorials are available, study cases are shown, 
issues and errors can be reported and material for developers can be found (Autodesk Inc., 
2016).

To support the development of the tool, two software were used as plug-ins of Dynamo. The

2.3.3.1 
Generative Design 
Generative Design was launched in 2016 under the name of Project Fractal with the idea of 
implementing generative design into Dynamo and giving users the possibility to automate the 
process of generating design options. Due to the vast audience that the software collected in 
the first year, the team invested in the development of it, changing the name to Project Refinery

Once Generative Design is installed in Dynamo, there is the possibility of including a node, which 
functions as the output of the evaluation phase and that Generative Design will optimize 
generation by generation, in the flowchart. The inputs for the flowchart are sliders, the values 
of which are changed by Generative Design to automatically generate all the different design 
alternatives. Once ready, the Dynamo script can be exported to the Generative Design 
application, which can be launched directly from the Dynamo interface (Generative Design, 
2020). In the Generative Design application, the user can choose either to create random 
solutions or to run an optimization, which can be associated with the Evolve stage of the 
generative design process. The user can define what inputs and outputs should be used in the 
optimization, the goals of the optimization, the population size and the number of generations. 
The GA used by Generative Design is a second version of the Non-dominated Sorting Genetic 
Algorithm (NSGA-II). This algorithm, compared to other multi-objective genetic algorithms, has 
demonstrated both to be able to get closer to the optimal solutions of complex problems and 
to better preserve diversity among the proposed solutions (Deb, et al., 2002). 
The result of the optimization in Generative Design can be visualized in different ways but two 
are the most interesting ones. The design grid is a grid of 3D views that shows all the different 
solutions of the optimization that can be sorted by input or output. Each of these views can be 
individually explored by rotating, zooming and panning. The parallel coordinates is a graph in 
which inputs and outputs are represented by parallel vertical lines and each solution by a 
polyline that intersects each vertical line in the point correspondent to the values of inputs and

outputs of that specific solution. In this visualization, the solutions can also be filtered by

Speckle 
Speckle is an open-source data platform for AEC, which allows architects and engineers to 
upload and share design data (Speckle, 2020). This data is streamed and therefore, automatically 
updated into the working environment of whoever has permission to receive the data. This 
makes the collaboration among the designers easier and quicker as the changes are visualized 
in real-time in all the team members’ machines. Moreover, no data is lost as it is saved in the 
cloud. 
Speckle can be installed as a plug-in to both Dynamo and Grasshopper and it works with two 
basic components: a sender and a receiver. The sender takes as input the data the user wants 
to share, the format of which is flexible and may include, but is not limited to, text, numbers, 
lists or even geometry. When the sender is defined in the canvas, an ID is assigned to it. This ID 
needs to be inputted in the receiver node which then, starts to receive in real-time the data sent 
by the sender.

3 METHODOLOGY 
This chapter describes the methodology applied in the development of this project, breaking 
down all the methodological steps. The importance of following a methodology is found in the 
support that researchers receive by adopting standards and good practices. Not only does a 
methodology point the researcher in the right direction, but it is also an insurance for the 
readers, who then know how the study has been conducted and can judge its validity as a result. 
3.1 DESIGN SCIENCE RESEARCH 
The chosen methodology for this project is the Design Science Research Methodology (DSRM). 
This outcome-based methodology is commonly used in engineering and computer science 
disciplines as it is usually applied when dealing with the development of algorithms and human 
interfaces (Dresch, et al., 2015). The DSRM involves five steps: problem identification, define the 
objective of the solution, design and development, demonstration and evaluation, and 
communication. As shown in Figure 11, the process is iterative so that each step can lead the 
researcher to reconsider and readjust previous steps. Each step of the methodology is described 
theoretically and in the context of this research in the following subsections. 
 
Figure 11 - The Design Science Research Methodology followed in the project 
3.1.1 Problem identification 
The first step of the DSRM is the identification of the problem. In this step, the current practices 
of a certain process are researched and criticisms are identified. The defined issues are the ones 
that need to be addressed by the solution and therefore, by the product that is developed 
further ahead in the methodology. This step is also where the relevance of the problem is 
demonstrated through links with blind spots in both the industrial and academic field (Conboy, 
et al., 2015). 
==PAGE:37==
 
25 
 
Within this project, the identification of the problem started with the first focus group at Züblin 
A/S, where Ole Berard, head of digitalization, and Adham Al-Nachar, BIM specialist, described 
the obstacles that the team faces when dealing with CSLP. They explained how challenging it is 
to consider every aspect and to find the right compromise between efficiency and safety on the 
site. Furthermore, Patrick Zangerl, LEAN coordinator, spends too many hours on the site 
planning when he could focus on other aspects of construction management such as scheduling 
tasks and supplies delivery. The research continued with the literature review from which it 
became apparent that the issue described by Züblin A/S is common for contractors and that 
plenty of studies were developed in the past decade on the argument. Moreover, since the CSLP 
is commonly performed at the end of the design process, the designers cannot include this 
aspect in the list of the criteria for the design at the early design phase. This often leads either 
to poor buildability and a final product characterized by a lower quality than expected, or too 
high costs due to changings on the design at late stages. 
3.1.2 Solution objective 
At this step of the process, all the objectives to be addressed to solve the defined problem need 
to be identified. The identification of the objectives often requires detailed background research 
where the gaps between the solutions presented in past studies and the problem to be solved 
are identified. These objectives are then used to define a possible solution that will be developed 
in the following step (Conboy, et al., 2015). 
The analysis of the previous studies highlighted that CSLP is one of the aspects of construction 
management that can benefit most from the use of new technologies such as BIM and 
generative design. The research also led to the identification of gaps in the background that the 
proposed solution for this project needed to fill. Plenty of models have been developed to 
automate the optimization of the layout but most of them were only finalized for research 
purposes, without a practical use in the industry. Furthermore, either the models only contain 
one element on the site (e.g., crane) losing focus of the full picture, or the solutions are 
evaluated by taking only one parameter into account (e.g., travel path distance). Moreover, 
these studies, being accurate in the evaluation of that parameter, require detailed inputs that 
can only be available at a late stage of the design process, making it impossible for the designers 
to consider CSLP at the early design stage. 
Therefore, the proposed solution for this project is a tool that supports the construction 
manager in his/her decision-making on the CSLP, which is capable of a multi-objective 
optimization by analyzing many aspects of the construction site. The tool has to be developed 
using software that can be easily integrated into the current contractors’ common practices and 
==PAGE:38==
26 
 
that can be used at the early design stage by designers, who can benefit from the feedback on 
CSLP.

the generative design.

of the tool includes all the steps described in Chapter 2.2: Generate, Evaluate and Evolve, with

4.1 MODELLING 
Before starting the development of the algorithm, the approach to follow, out of all the 
possibilities explained in Chapter 2.1 for modelling the construction site layout, had to be 
defined. 
Since the objective is to create a tool that is usable at the first stages of the design process, the 
most reasonable way to model the space is by using the grid system approach. In fact, the 
predetermined location approach is too limiting and it does not allow to explore a wide range 
of alternatives. On the other hand, the continuous space approach seems to be too 
computationally heavy for an initial evaluation of the construction site layout. Furthermore, the 
==PAGE:42==
30 
 
grid system approach is the most flexible of the three as the user is allowed to change the size 
of the cells to adjust the accuracy of the results and computation time. This means that whether 
the user needs a quick approximation or an accurate evaluation, the model can fulfil the request, 
according to the size of the cells. 
The same observations apply to the definition of the object boundary. The dimensionless object 
approach is too coarse and does not give the user a good enough approximation of the layout, 
especially if used without synergy with a predetermined location approach for the 
representation of space. Conversely, the actual object shape approach is too demanding in 
terms of computational power for the purpose of the developed tool. With minor exceptions, 
the degree of accuracy reachable by this approach is not needed either for supporting the 
construction manager decisions or for an evaluation at the early design stages. Therefore, the 
choice is to model the objects with an approximate geometry approach. This means creating the 
new objects with a limited LOD and adapting the ones already included in the BIM model such 
as the building to be built with the same LOD. Therefore, it was decided to follow the approach 
used by Ning, et al. (2011) described in Chapter 2.1. 
Finally, the time dimension was modelled with a static approach, considering only one phase of 
the construction, where the building is already built on site. To have the possibility of modelling 
time with a phased approach, the phases of the construction need to be defined in the BIM 
model. If the phases are defined, it is possible to use generative design more times starting from 
different BIM models, each representing a specific phase, and then to put the results together 
by building rules for keeping the locations of fixed objects from one phase to another. However, 
Züblin A/S asserts that having a BIM model with the information of the phases is rare and 
therefore, the hypothetical function of the tool could not be used often. This is also the case for 
the dynamic approach, where a schedule needs to be connected with the BIM model which, if 
it happens, it is at the very late phases of the design process. Furthermore, with the static 
approach, the model needs fewer inputs to be built and the tool is more likely to be used at the 
early design stage, when the schedule of the tasks to be performed on the construction site is 
not yet defined. 
The biggest limitation of the static approach is the fact that it is not possible to reuse spaces on 
the construction site as the information related to the timespan of each object is missing. 
Instead, with that information, it is possible to reuse the same location once the objects 
occupying it finishes all its tasks. However, the generalization of the spaces can help to reduce 
this limitation. For instance, calling an area material lay-down area does not specify which 
material should be placed in and therefore, the area can host different materials during the 
construction process. This way, a hypothetical cement warehouse and a rebar storage yard can 
==PAGE:43==
 
31 
 
be placed on the same spot without the need of having two separate areas. Of course, with this 
simplification of the time dimension, it is not possible to know if those two areas need to exist 
at the same time on the construction site and therefore, the problem is shifted to the 
determination of the size of that area, which is treated in Chapter 4.3. 
4.2 SETUP 
This initial step was introduced to take information from the BIM model and translate it into 
readable data for the first step of the generative design process: the generation.  
As previously stated, the input for the contractor which has to plan the construction site layout 
is generally the BIM model obtained from the designer. Therefore, the BIM model was taken as 
input for the developed tool as well. However, the BIM model contains plenty of information 
regarding many aspects of the project that, if included in later stages, would be an unnecessary 
load for the machine that performs the generative design in terms of computational power. For 
this reason, the user is asked to only select the elements of the BIM model that are important 
for the optimization so that only those elements are imported into Dynamo, where the 
algorithm was developed. Therefore, as a developer of the tool, it is of importance to evaluate 
which elements are relevant. After an accurate evaluation, these elements turned out to be the 
topography, the building to be built, the existing fixed objects on the site and the access to the 
urban roads. 
The topography is usually modelled in the BIM model with a mesh which may contain several 
points dependent on the LOD of the model and on the actual differences in surface height on 
which the model stands. 
CSLP is normally seen as a 2D problem. Researchers rarely consider the third dimension and 
construction managers typically only do so in the detailing phase of the planning with few 
exceptions. In this project, it was therefore decided to avoid considering the Z-axis and to 
translate the model into its 2D version. This implies that all the points of the construction site 
are placed on the same plane and that the cranes and lifters can reach every altitude. To follow 
this line, after the mesh representing the topography is selected and imported into Dynamo by 
the user, it needs to be flattened to the reference plane, which was chosen to be the XY plane. 
The result is a plane surface delimited by boundary lines. 
The flattened topography is the base for the creation of the grid. Here, the user has the 
possibility to change the size of the cells to adjust the accuracy and the velocity of the generative 
design optimization. The grid is created by splitting the surface into squared surfaces 
representing the cells of the grid. Of course, the chance that the total length and width of the 
topography is a multiple of the cell size is low and therefore, the cells adjacent to the external 
==PAGE:44==
32 
 
boundary are normally smaller in order to fit the boundaries. To avoid placing objects on cells 
that are reasonably smaller than the chosen size, the algorithm cleans the list of surfaces from 
the cells that are smaller than the 80% of the normal size, thereby defining an acceptable size 
variation of 20%. 
The building to be built is probably the most important object as it not only represents an 
obstacle for placing other objects but it is also the object that is taken as a reference to evaluate 
the performance of most of the other objects. Here, one of the limitations of the static time 
approach is encountered. The building should not represent an obstacle for the placement of 
other elements for the whole duration of the construction process. For example, at the 
beginning of the operations on the site, that space could be used as a material storage area. 
However, because of the simplification, the space occupied by the building at the end of the 
construction process needs to be considered occupied from the start. 
The LOD of the BIM model determines how many elements compose the building. In the 
simplest case, the building is represented in the model by a box, in the most detailed, potentially 
by thousands of solids. The user has to have the possibility to select the building independently 
from the LOD and therefore, it was provided with a selection tool capable of selecting multiple 
elements to import into Dynamo. 
What is relevant to the construction site layout design is the footprint of the building as the 
model is designed in 2D. An easy way to obtain the approximate geometry of the footprint of 
the building through Dynamo would be creating the bounding box containing all the elements 
composing the building and taking the face parallel to the XY plane from it. However, it was 
noticed that in many cases this is not an acceptable simplification for the building to be built. 
The example in Figure 13 shows that the resulting rectangular shape includes a very important 
space to be used on the site layout design. Therefore, although the approximate geometry 
approach is the chosen one for representing the site objects, it has been decided to make an 
exception for the representation of the building to be built and to use its actual shape. 
To do so, all the solids composing the building are moved along the Z-axis to intersect the XY 
plane and merged to create one unique solid. From this solid, it is now possible to obtain the 
intersection with the XY plane which corresponds to the actual shape of the footprint. Since the 
site space is represented in the model as a grid system, all the cells of the grid which contain the 
footprint of the building are marked as occupied. This means that even if the footprint is 
represented by its actual shape, the adaptation to the grid system simplifies its shape towards 
a more approximated geometry according to the size of the cells.  
==PAGE:45==
 
33 
 
 
Figure 13 - Modelling of BIM model objects onto the grid system 
As described in Chapter 2.1, obstacles such as natural impediments or existing buildings that are 
placed within the site represent one of the main elements to be modelled as they can influence 
the whole layout. These objects are often present in the BIM model unless it is a very conceptual 
model and therefore, the user can select and import them into Dynamo. Contrary to the building 
to be built, the actual shape of these objects can rarely influence the site planning. Therefore, 
the site obstacles are represented on the grid with their approximate geometry. After importing, 
a bounding box is created to include all the elements composing the obstacle, one of the faces 
of the box parallel to the XY plane is taken, moved along the Z-axis and placed on the grid. Also, 
in this case, the shape adapts to the grid system as shown in Figure 13. 
While the location of the equipment or the temporary facilities are optimized through the 
generative design process and therefore not fixed, the access to the site from public roads is 
forced by the conditions of the urban area. This accessibility parameter also extends to 
conditions other than public road access, any condition that might make access not suitable 
should be included. For instance, if a school is placed in front of the construction site, it may be 
more convenient to have the access on the opposite side so that the drivers do not need to 
constantly consider the possibility of a child crossing the street unsafely. For this reason, the 
responsibility of choosing the access points to the site is given to the user, who can either select 
one or two access points on the boundary lines of the topography. These points are used during 
the generation phase, where they function as starting and ending points of the vehicular paths 
on the site. 
In the final version of the developed tool, after tests and iterations, Speckle was introduced to 
divide the Setup script from the rest. This choice made the optimization much lighter and faster 
as Dynamo, in this case, does not need to handle a considerable number of solids from the BIM 
model, but only the data needed for the optimization. After the selection of the elements by the 
user, the Setup script translates the selection into simple geometry and sends it to the 
optimization script through a Speckle sender. As shown in Figure 14, the stream of data consists 
==PAGE:46==
34 
 
of the building footprint, the workspace, which is the flattened topography, the site obstacles, 
the access to the site and the number of storeys. This last input is useful for the 3D visualization 
of the result and for determining the size of the temporary facilities as it is based on the building 
gross area (Chapter 4.3).

4.3 GENERATE 
The generation of geometries is the first actual step of the generative design process. In this 
stage, the alternatives are generated creating the initial population for the genetic algorithm 
optimization. At the Setup stage, the boundaries of the construction site were defined and the 
grid generated in it. Furthermore, the location of the building to be built, the obstacles and the 
accesses to the site were defined. In this section, it is described how the elements to be 
generated are selected and sized and how the generation of those elements was approached. 
4.3.1 Objects selection and sizing 
Before starting the generation of the elements, the typology of the elements to be generated 
need to be defined. According to Sadeghpour & Andayesh (2015) and based on Patrick Zangerl’s 
experience, the objects on the construction site can be divided into the following typologies: 
equipment, material lay-down and storage areas, accesses and on-site paths, temporary 
facilities, workspace and site obstacles. To keep the model generic, these categories are not 
further subdivided, which means that, for instance, the type of material laying in different 
storage areas is not defined as well as the nature of the workspace (e.g. rebar fabrication yard 
or concrete mixing area) and it will be the task of the construction manager to detail the layout 
in the following design phase. 
The site obstacles are defined by the selection of the user in the Setup script, while once all the 
other elements are placed, any remaining space on the construction site can be considered 
workspace. Therefore, only four elements need to be generated: equipment (crane), material 
==PAGE:47==
 
35 
 
lay-down and storage areas, on-site paths (connected to the accesses already defined) and the 
temporary facilities. 
Estimating the size of the objects to be generated is a complex task as most of the elements on 
the construction site do not have fixed dimensions, but they are dependent on the management 
of the site. Underestimating the size can lead to a loss of productivity, while overestimating it 
can lead to tight areas with problems in quality and safety. 
In the case of this project, the size of the crane is one of the main factors that influence time 
and cost of the layout and therefore, it is one of the variables to determine in the evaluation 
phase of the generative design. As more extensively described in Chapter 4.4, its size depends 
on the percentage of the footprint of the building to be built covered by the crane radius. The 
size of the paths is based on the size of the vehicles on the site with, if possible, the addition of 
an extension to host a separate lane for walking personnel. 
The size of the material lay-down and storage areas, and the size of the temporary facilities have 
to be estimated before the beginning of the generation. For an accurate estimation, models 
have been studied in the past years such as the one proposed by (Razavialavi, et al., 2014). The 
application of these models requires the project schedule as the size of the objects is dependent 
on it. The size of the material lay-down and storage areas and therefore their capacity, are 
dependent on the volume of materials simultaneously present on the site. This depends on the 
delivery rate of the materials and on how long they stay in the dedicated areas before being 
used on the construction. The size of the temporary facilities depends on the number of workers 
simultaneously working on the construction site as they are the users of facilities such as offices 
and toilets. Of course, this is also dependent on the schedule and which activities are performed 
at each time during the construction. As shown in Figure 15, the space required for a certain 
facility on the site varies during the construction process and with a dynamic optimization, space 
can be saved. However, even by using a static approach, the schedule of activities is needed for 
accurately estimating the maximum required space. 
==PAGE:48==
36 
 
 
Figure 15 - Space required on the construction site for material storage overtime (Razavialavi, et al., 2014) 
Since the tool developed in this thesis is mainly meant to be used at the early design stages, 
when the schedule of the activities is not yet available, it was decided to use a rule of thumb, 
specifically the one proposed by Elbeltagi (2014). This rule allows one to determine an 
approximate size for those objects based on the cost of the construction and the building gross 
area. In this project, it was decided to use the budget as it is always one of the client 
requirements and therefore, available at the early design stage. To introduce it, a slider 
indicating the millions of Danish crowns is provided to the user in the Dynamo Graphic User 
Interface (GUI). 
The total resulting area for the materials is divided into lay-down area and a covered material 
storage according to a percentage that is then optimized during the generative design process. 
Züblin A/S places the temporary facilities in pre-assembled containers. These containers are 
stackable on top of each other and therefore, one of the variables to consider is the number of 
layers on which the containers are stacked. This number divides the area for temporary facilities 
resulted from the rule of thumb and it is also one of the variables to be evaluated in the 
optimization process. 
4.3.2 Self-learning and forced learning approach 
During the development of the generation part of the algorithm, it was important to define clear 
guidelines to follow. The first decision to take was the choice related to the learning approach 
that the algorithm should follow in the generation. During the initial testing of the algorithm, it 
became apparent that it was entirely possible to create unreasonable geometries and illogical 
solutions. An example of this is the relative location of the crane and the material lay-down area. 
A solution in which the area where materials to be used in the construction activities is placed 
==PAGE:49==
 
37 
 
outside the crane radius range (Figure 16) is not convenient as the materials need to be moved 
before being reachable by the crane. This results in an increase in time and cost to move the 
objects and a higher risk of damaging the transported elements, decreasing the quality of the 
construction. A construction manager, therefore, would not even question the position of the 
materials, which should be within the crane reachable area. However, the way the algorithm 
has to handle these situations is not obvious and the used approach needed to be accurately 
evaluated. 
 
Figure 16 - Example of a poorly performing solution due to the wrong location of the material area 
A first approach was called self-learning. With this approach, the algorithm has the freedom of 
creating geometries and it does not have any limitation given by the developer. In the case of 
the crane and the material lay-down area, it means giving the possibility to the algorithm to 
generate the two elements in whatever available spot on the grid and only later on, in the 
evaluation stage, badly rating the geometry alternatives with the material area outside of the 
crane radius. With the use of this approach, since there is no limitation to the generation, the 
algorithm would produce a large number of alternatives that would be discarded later on 
because of poor performance, using a significant amount of computational power. On the other 
hand, leaving freedom in the generation, the generative design is used at its maximum potential, 
the evolution process would learn generation after generation which alternatives perform best 
and define the general rules through evolutionary principles rather than from initial settings 
given by the developer. 
The other approach was called forced learning. This approach does not let the algorithm learn 
entirely by itself, rather, a set of rules are defined to limit the generation according to common 
==PAGE:50==
38 
 
sense and to what normally performs best. Using the same example, the application of this 
approach would mean forcing the generation of the material area in one of the available spots 
within the radius of the crane. Here, problems arise when also other elements benefit from that 
location, but due to the strict rule, no alternatives can be evaluated. Therefore, this approach 
leads to higher performing alternatives in the first generations, but giving limitations makes the 
algorithm predictable and not capable of experimenting with new solutions, different from the 
one that the construction manager would normally propose. 
To draw a parallel to a common social situation, parents can decide either to let their children 
experiment, make mistakes and learn themselves what is best to do in life, or to keep them safe 
from mistakes and teach them their point of view of what is right or wrong. Both approaches 
have their positive and negative sides and probably, in real life, the optimal solution stands in 
the middle. 
In the framework of this project, it was evaluated that leaving freedom to the algorithm to 
experiment with new solutions is more important than saving computational power. This way, 
the algorithm is more flexible and even if some of the alternatives perform badly, there is the 
possibility of creating innovative solutions that the construction manager would not have 
developed without an algorithm. Therefore, the first approach is the chosen one for this project 
as it is the one which best uses the full potential of the generative design. 
4.3.3 Parallel and sequence approach 
Another choice that had to be taken during the development of the generation part of the 
algorithm, was whether to generate all the elements at the same time or one after another. 
The first approach features a parallel generation, in which all the elements are created at the 
same time. This approach is fast in the generation phase but an enormous number of 
alternatives are discarded in the evaluation phase. This happens not because they perform 
badly, but because they are not viable. In fact, if there is no sequence in the generation, at the 
moment of the creation, each element can be located in every cell of the grid and there is a big 
chance that at least two of them are overlapping. This creates non-viable geometries that need 
to be handled after the generation phase. Since only a small percentage of alternatives out of 
the generated ones are sent to the final part of the generative design process, the starting 
population is low and many more generations are needed in the evolution phase to obtain high-
performance outputs. Compared to the following, with the same initial population and the same 
number of generations, this approach is faster but the quality of the result is lower. 
The second approach follows a sequence in the generation. After one element is placed on the 
grid, the availability of the cells is checked and the ones that have been occupied by the last 
==PAGE:51==
 
39 
 
created element are marked as occupied, before the placement of the following element on the 
grid. This approach makes sure that all the generated alternatives are viable and therefore, the 
entire initial population of geometries is sent to the final stage of the generative design process. 
However, this approach presents two limitations. First, the computation of the generation phase 
is heavier and slower as after each generated element, the algorithm needs to check which cells 
are occupied and to create a new list of available spots. Second, by following a sequence in the 
generation, an involuntary hierarchy of the elements to generate is created. This is because 
during the generation, cells are populated by the created elements and therefore, the location 
of the elements later generated is dependent on the location of the previous ones. 
 
Figure 17 – Comparison between the parallel generation and the sequence generation approaches 
Despite the required computational power, the second approach was chosen for this project as 
it allows the user to have more control over the whole process. With the first approach, the 
number of alternatives that reach the final step of the generative design process is based on 
chance and this also means that the number of generations required to obtain a good 
performing result is unknown. On the other hand, with the second approach, the number of 
generated alternatives is consistent and by testing the algorithm, it is possible to estimate the 
population size and the number of generations needed to balance accuracy of the result and 
computational time. This approach also turned out to be more reflective of how the construction 
manager would normally approach CSLP. Furthermore, the fact that there is a hierarchy, in most 
cases, is not a limitation as in reality the location of certain elements is actually more important 
than others; a natural hierarchy is present. 
==PAGE:52==
40 
 
4.3.4 Generation process 
As previously described, the generation of the elements is done in sequence and therefore, the 
hierarchy of the elements has to be created. After discussions with Patrick Zangerl, LEAN 
coordinator at Züblin A/S, the sequence was defined as equipment, material lay-down area, 
paths and temporary facilities. This is because the priority should be given to the crane, which 
should reach as much area as possible of the footprint of the building. After that, the materials, 
which have to be picked up by the crane, followed by the paths, which are used by vehicles to 
supply the area in which the materials are stored. Finally, the temporary facilities, which are 
placed wherever free space is left. 
Except for the path, each element is placed on the grid following four main steps: creating the 
geometry of the element, defining the location where the geometry should be placed, placing 
the geometry in the chosen location and checking which cells are turning unavailable for the 
next element location due to the placement of the current element. Each of these steps was 
developed in Dynamo by one or more groups of nodes connected to each other by using the 
Dynamo GUI. 
As shown in the example of the material lay-down and storage area in Figure 18, to create the 
geometry, a Python script is used (Appendix III). The created geometry is approximated and 
therefore, the output of the python script is one or more rectangular shapes representing the 
material lay-down and the storage area, if present. As input, this script requires the total area 
required for materials calculated at the beginning of the generation part of the algorithm, where 
the size of the object is defined, the percentage of lay-down area over storage area and two 
random numbers. These numbers are used in the Python script to define the division ratio 
between the two sides of the rectangular shapes to be created. The first number is for the shape 
of the material lay-down area and the second one is for the shape of the storage area. The 
randomness is necessary for creating different geometry alternatives every time the input 
changes (extensively described in Chapter 4.5). Finally, the rectangular shapes outputted by the 
Python script are turned into surfaces ready to be placed on the layout.

The location where the shape is supposed to be placed is defined by the group of nodes shown 
in Figure 19. At first, a point in the centre of each available cell of the grid is created and the list 
of possible points to be chosen is sent to the following group of nodes. The choice of the point 
to locate the geometry is also random. The dedicated node, in fact, randomly chooses as many 
numbers as the number of geometries to be placed. The rounded numbers correspond to the 
indices of the chosen points among the ones included in the list of available points. 
 
Figure 19 - Sequence of nodes in Dynamo GUI for the definition of the location of lay-down and storage areas 
The placement of the geometry on the chosen point is apparently simple but, in reality, is the 
most complex of the four steps. The complexity of this step is due to the fact that the size of the 
element is not the same as the size of the cell in which the element should be placed. The 
element is normally bigger than the cell and therefore, the chosen point in the previous step 
only represents the spot where the centre of the geometry should be placed, but also the 
surrounded cells will be occupied by the newly placed element. The final developed version of 
this step is represented by a Python script (Appendix III), which places the geometry at the 
chosen location and relocates it on the following cell in the list in case of found intersections 
with existing geometry until it finds a spot that does not lead to intersections. To do this, the 
Python script needs the list of points on the grid, the chosen location, the geometry to be placed 
and the existing geometry for checking intersections as inputs. The outputs of the Python script 
are the placed geometry and the final chosen location. The development iterations that led to 
the final version of this part of the algorithm are described in Chapter 5.1. 
==PAGE:54==
42 
 
 
Figure 20 - Sequence of nodes in Dynamo GUI for the placement of temporary facilities 
The last step of the generation of an object consists of intersecting the newly created geometry 
with the cells of the grid. The aim of this is to split the list of cells into occupied and available by 
checking which of them have been filled by the new geometry and which are still available for 
the placement of the following object. The outputted updated list of available cells is taken as 
input to create the list of points for setting the initial location of the following object to be 
generated. On the other hand, the newly occupied cells are added to the existing geometry that 
is inputted in the Python script for the placement of the following object. 
 
Figure 21 - Sequence of nodes in Dynamo GUI for splitting occupied and available grid cells after the placement of 
the temporary facilities 
The generation of paths was handled differently from the steps previously described as the 
geometry to create is not an area, but a series of curves across the site. The main node that was 
used to generate the path curves on the grid is a pathfinding node included in the Refinery 
toolkit, which is a collection of packages for Dynamo to accelerate generative design workflows 
(Generative Design, 2020). This node is capable of finding the shortest path between a starting 
and an ending point, taking into account the boundary within which the path can be created and 
the obstacles that need to be avoided. The existing geometries, in this case, represent the 
obstacles and the outer lines of the construction site represent the boundary. The starting and 
ending points of the path are represented by the accesses to the construction site defined at the 
Setup stage. To make sure that the path would also connect other locations on the site, the 
complexity of it is increased by including one randomly chosen point among the available points 
on the grid between the starting and the ending point.

4.4 EVALUATE 
The evaluation of the created geometries is the step of the generative design where all the 
alternatives are analyzed and ranked according to their performance. Analyzing the geometries 
means investigating them, and finding the characteristics that are favourable and unfavourable 
to reach the goal of the generative design. By ranking is intended the process of giving a score, 
positive or negative, for each analyzed characteristic and thereby sorting them according to their 
fitness. 
The challenge of this step is to recognize which characteristics are worthy of inclusion in the 
analysis, and how to make these measurable. In the case of CSLP and specifically in this project, 
it was decided together with Züblin A/S that the goal is to optimize the geometries in terms of 
time, cost, safety and quality. Therefore, before starting the analysis, it was necessary to define 
which measurable parameters related to the geometry influenced those four optimization goals. 
To do so, beyond the literature review and the discussion with Patrick Zangerl, it was decided to 
publish a survey to obtain different opinions on the subject from a larger pool of professionals. 
Time and cost on the construction site are usually strictly correlated as both labour and rents 
are a high percentage of the total cost. Therefore, if an activity takes longer than expected, the 
cost also increases. Taking the example of the travel path distance, the greater the distance 
between two facilities, the longer the travel is, and since the workers are paid by the hour, costs 
increase as a result. This is the reason why time and cost are often analyzed together as 
efficiency. However, in this project, it was decided to keep them separated and to analyze them 
singularly as, in certain cases, they do not have the same trend. One example is having two 
==PAGE:56==
44 
 
cranes instead of one. Paying double rent for the equipment make costs increase but since two 
elements of the construction can be lifted simultaneously, the time decreases. 
The goal of quality not only refers to the quality of the built construction, which of course should 
be as high as stated in the client requirements, but also to the quality of the working 
environment. In fact, along with safety, good working conditions is an important aspect for the 
workers on the site which often is forgotten. 
4.4.1 Analysis 
In this subsection, the factors that, according to this study, affect the goals of the optimization 
are described. While in most studies, the travel path distance between objects is the only 
considered parameter for the optimization, in this project, the parameters taken into account 
are multiple, each of them related to a different aspect of the construction site layout geometry. 
Each of the parameters is made measurable by dedicated Dynamo scripts that are included in 
Appendix III. 
As previously mentioned, one of the most important factors is the percentage of the footprint 
reached by the crane. To evaluate this, a circular area representing the area reached by the 
crane is created in the model and its intersection with the footprint of the building is noted. The 
ratio between the intersection area and the total footprint area is the percentage of the 
footprint reached by the crane. 
Checking whether the material lay-down area lies within the crane radius is also of importance. 
The double handling of materials not only affects time and cost, but it increases the risk of the 
workers getting hurt and the material getting ruined, influencing both safety and quality. This 
check is addressed in the same way as the previous one, considering the lay-down area within 
the crane when its percentage is above 80 per cent. 
With the same method, it is also checked whether the temporary facilities are within the crane 
radius. This is deemed undesirable for safety reasons. 
The travel path distance between the facilities and the workplace is also a variable and it is 
calculated by using the same node used for the generation of the site path. In this case, the path 
is generated to connect the centre of the temporary facility area to the closest workplace to the 
building. The length of this path is compared to the dimensions of the construction site to 
evaluate whether it is short enough to allow an agile journey for the workers. 
Regarding the site path, there are many factors to take into account. One of them is its minimum 
width. To calculate this in the model, a series of lines perpendicular to the path is created along 
==PAGE:57==
 
45 
 
the whole length of the path. These lines are cut if they intersect another object and therefore, 
the length of the shortest line corresponds to the minimum width of the path. 
The second factor related to the path is its proximity to the lay-down area. If the trucks can 
deliver the supplies directly in the dedicated area, the material does not need to be moved a 
second time. To check the distance between the two, the closest point on the path to the lay-
down area is isolated and the distance between this point and the lay-down area is calculated. 
The final aspect related to the path to be checked is whether the vehicles need manoeuvring, as 
this affects time and safety, especially if dedicated areas are not provided. Each angle formed 
between the end of a line of the path and the start of the following one is calculated. It is 
assumed that if this angle is lower than 60 degrees, the vehicles need to manoeuvre. 
With the last iteration of the development of the tool, two more factors were added to include 
the findings of the published survey. The buffer distance from the building to be built and site 
objects from the generated elements is one of them. To check this, an external offset of three 
meters from the boundary of the building and the objects are taken. Forming a surface by loft 
between the original boundary and the offset, it is possible to check if this surface intersects any 
generated object. 
The second factor highlighted in the survey is the evaluation of how tight the site becomes after 
the generation of all the elements as it influences the safety and the quality of the workplace. 
Therefore, the overall area of the generated elements is compared to the remained area on the 
site. If the ratio is below 50 per cent, the area is considered adequately spaced out. 
4.4.2 Ranking 
The part of the algorithm assigned to ranking the different alternatives takes the result of the 
analysis as input and gives a score to each of the analyzed parameters.  
By testing the algorithm on a case study (Chapter 5.1), it was possible to weigh the different 
factors according to their importance to the geometry alternatives performance. It was decided 
that some of the factors are considered to be a boolean, hence a true or false statement, for 
example, the condition of the temporary facilities laying within the crane radius. Others were 
considered in percentage such as the area on the site dedicated to the workspace. In this case, 
the score increases with the increase of the percentage. 
Within this project, it has not been possible to obtain datasets for the calculation of actual time 
required and real costs. Instead, a scoring system was developed on a scale from 0 to 10 for 
measuring the performance towards the four goals of the optimization (time, cost, safety and 
quality). The different parameters have different weights of contribution to the overall score of

4.5 EVOLVE 
The Evolve step of the generative design process in this project is carried out by the use of 
Generative Design. This tool, presented in Chapter 2.3, is a plug-in of Revit connected with 
Dynamo, which uses genetic algorithms to optimize the geometry generated and evaluated in 
Dynamo. 
The optimization of Generative Design plays with input values that determine the generation of 
geometry to create all the alternatives that, applying the genetic algorithm principles, allow the 
user to reach the goal of the optimization. Therefore, the first step to use the software is to set 
the inputs and the goals of the optimization. The inputs need to be sliders so that Generative 
Design can change the value of the input within the range determined by the slider. For this 
project, 10 sliders were included in the Dynamo script. These sliders determine not only the 
position on the grid of the elements to be generated but also other characteristics such as size 
and shape. 
 
Figure 23 – Input variables for the optimization in Dynamo GUI (left) and “Define study” window of Generative 
Design (right) 
The goals of the optimization are the score outputted by the ranking phase and as previously 
mentioned, are time, cost, safety and quality. Along with these, a fifth goal was added, 
representing the average score of the geometry on the other four goals of the optimization. In 
==PAGE:60==
48 
 
the “Define study” window of the Generative Design (Figure 23), it is possible to choose whether 
the goal of the optimization should be maximizing or minimizing these scores. In this case, in the 
ranking phase, all the desirable conditions were set as positive contributions to the overall score 
and therefore, the goal is to reach the maximum value possible, which was set to 10. 
Furthermore, the user can set constraints to the goals, hence a minimum and a maximum value 
that determines a range within which the solution is acceptable. In the case of this study, it does 
not make sense to set a maximum as the goal is to maximize the scores, but it is interesting to 
set a minimum value to avoid poorly performing solutions. This is a useful tool to filter out 
alternatives that performed favourably in a single goal, but highly unfavourably in the others. 
Such solutions would not necessarily be filtered by the genetic algorithm (NSGA-II), but would 
likely be considered unacceptable. In fact, this algorithm works with non-dominating principles, 
which keeps every result that performed better than others in at least one of the goals. For a 
geometry to be eliminated, there should be at least one alternative that performs better in each 
of the goals. 
For example, a geometry that has a score of 9 in one goal and 3 in each of the other goals, would 
be kept by the algorithm along with a geometry that has a score of 8 in all the goals, even though 
the second geometry has a definitively higher overall score. This means that, following the 
evolutionary principles, the first geometry can be replicated and have crossovers with other 
geometries, passing on bad “genes” to be inherited by the next generation. Setting a minimum 
score of 5 for each goal means that such a geometry would not be considered in the population 
for the creation of the next generation. 
Finally, before starting the optimization, the user is asked to set the population size, the number 
of generations and the seed. These parameters determine the number of cycles and the number 
of alternatives generated at each cycle and therefore, they strongly influence the accuracy of 
the result. Testing the developed tool with a multitude of construction sites, it was possible to 
determine the optimal values to insert for an optimal compromise between accuracy and time 
of computation. 
Once the optimization is over, the user can visualize the results of the study, sort them and select 
the desired one to open directly in Dynamo. The possibility of sorting the solutions obtained 
from the optimization is of importance to the construction manager when a particular objective 
need to be addressed. For instance, when a construction needs to be built as fast as possible for 
an emergency, time needs to be optimized even if the other goals are not performing as well. 
Examples of this could be the construction of temporary houses for inhabitants after an 
earthquake or the construction of new hospitals capable of hosting more patients during a 
pandemic.

5 DEMONSTRATION AND EVALUATION 
The demonstration and evaluation stage of the DSRM is the stage where the developed tool is 
used to solve real problems. This is done to test and demonstrate the applicability, efficiency 
and effectiveness of the tool. Within this project, this stage was carried out primarily by the use 
of a case study which led to a series of iterations of the design and development stage for the 
improvement of the tool. Secondly, the most advanced version of the tool that was developed 
in this thesis was tested on multiple construction sites for a better definition of the capabilities 
and potential of the tool, but also its limitations and the improvements that could be achieved 
with further development. 
5.1 CASE OF STUDY 
The case study chosen for this project is a BIM model of a building shared by Vermeulen (2019) 
in one of his classes for Autodesk University. The model was originally dedicated to the 
demonstration of the potential of Project Refinery, which in that case, was also used in the 
construction layout planning field, specifically to find the optimal position of the tower crane. 
The code object of the demonstration was very limited as the crane position was the only 
evaluated parameter and only predetermined locations along a path could be chosen. 
Therefore, to make the model more generic and suitable for the optimization presented in this 
thesis, all the objects present in the model were deleted, except for the building to be built and 
the site topography. Since one of the main object categories listed in Chapter 2.1 is the site 
obstacles, it was decided to place a box in the 3D model representing an existing building on the 
site. 
 
Figure 27 - 3D view of the BIM model used as a case study 
==PAGE:65==
 
53 
 
Even though the development of the tool started from the generation, a basic evaluation script 
needed to be coded to test the algorithm. Without this, it would not be possible to check the 
quality of the generation part of the algorithm. However, adding the evaluation script made it 
more difficult to identify the source of issues in the algorithm. Therefore, it was decided to base 
the optimization on the evaluation of a single parameter: the percentage of the building covered 
by the crane radius. Therefore, the parameters that determined the performance were the 
location of the crane and its size. This solution made it easy for the developer to distinguish a 
good-performing algorithm from a bad one, also due to the shape of the building of the case 
study, where the best position for the crane is very intuitive. 
5.1.1 Placement of geometry 
The first version of the tool followed the parallel generation approach described in Chapter 4.3, 
in which all the elements are simultaneously generated. The result of the test was qualitatively 
poor even if the number of elements to be generated was limited to one crane and one material 
lay-down area. It fact, the overlap of geometry that was randomly created made most of the 
alternatives unviable for the generation of new offspring. This issue did not allow the algorithm 
to perform as intended as the size of the actual population used by the genetic algorithm was 
only a small percentage of the one set at the beginning of the optimization. For example, when 
generating 100 geometry alternatives, intersections were found in 83 of them, meaning that 
only 17% of the generated geometries were viable additions to the population. The busier the 
construction site gets, the lower the chance of obtaining viable geometries, as the risk of 
intersection increases. 
Therefore, it was decided to switch to a sequence generation approach, in which the elements 
are generated one after another, checking at every step which cells on the grid are available. 
This results in a computationally slower approach compared to the first version, but with the 
capability of outputting higher quality results. However, the problem of overlapping geometries 
was only partially solved. Figure 28 shows that even though the cell was included in the list of 
available locations, due to the size of the element to be generated, a substantial number of non-
feasible solutions were created. It is not possible to define a constant safety distance from other 
objects because the shape and the size of the element to be placed changes every time a new 
alternative is created. Therefore, a significant part of the generation script was dedicated to 
determining which of the listed points were actually available for placement, considering the 
size of the element. In short, this was done by placing the geometry in every point of the list and 
checking which of the points led to an intersection of geometries. By removing these points from 
the list, a new list was created, which only contains the actual available points. Since every 
element has a different shape and that shape is different for each generated alternative, this 
==PAGE:66==
54 
 
passage had to be repeated for every element and for every member of the population, making 
the generative design process very slow and computationally expensive.

5.1.2 Input initialization and visualization 
More tests on the case study led to the decision to abandon the mapping of the inputs and to 
instead adopt a random number generator. Translating the inputs is necessary because the 
range of the sliders do not necessarily correspond to the length of the lists the values are taken 
from, for example, the available locations on the grid. Since the sliders do not accept any input 
for defining the ranges, these have to be constant, while the number of locations depends on 
the size of the cells and the existing elements of the site. By mapping the inputs, the chosen 
input number would be translated into the corresponding number in the possibilities range. 
However, the result gets less accurate as the number of possibilities gets further from the input 
range. If the input range is bigger than the number of possibilities, some numbers would have 
more chances of getting picked than others due to approximation, and vice versa, some 
numbers would be skipped and never be picked. 
Introducing the random number generator, the input functions as a seed for the definition of a 
random number within the chosen range. This solution evens out the chances for all the 
numbers to be selected. More importantly, the same seed can generate as many numbers within 
the range as desired and therefore, define more locations from a single input, while prior to this 
implementation, every location needed to be defined as input. This means that to give the user 
the possibility of adding a second crane, there would be the need for a “crane1 location” and a 
“crane2 location” input. This not only makes the algorithm more complex because of the 
increased number of inputs, but also limited, as the user could only generate as many cranes as 
the number of dedicated inputs. This means that the number of objects to generate has to be 
defined by the developer and not by the user. This problem was also noticeable in the material 
shared by Vermeulen (2019), where two separated Dynamo scripts are included, one for the 
single crane solution and one for the double crane solution. On the other hand, with the use of 
the random number generator, a single input allows the definition of as many locations as the 
number of cranes. This solution makes the algorithm more flexible and gives the possibility of 
defining the optimal number of cranes through the generative design process, instead of fixing 
the number beforehand.

5.1.3 Scoring system 
Through the iterations, the scoring system was improved. At first, all the parameters contributed 
to the overall score of the geometry alternatives in equal measure. However, the importance 
and weighting of the different parameters quickly became a central point of discussion. For 
example, the number of access points is relevant both for time and safety, however, the position 
of the material lay-down area is much more impactful because if it is wrongly located, it leads 
to double handling of material. Therefore, the parameters needed to be weighted based on their 
importance. The result of the optimization increased in quality but the position of the crane was 
still an issue to address. Even if a high weighting was given to the parameter regarding the 
percentage of the built footprint within the radius of the crane, the alternatives could still obtain 
a fairly high score without the fulfilment of this condition and therefore, most of the outputted 
footprints would have a low area percentage covered by the crane. 
For the final version of the tool, it was decided to create a separate output for this parameter 
and do not include it into the scoring system. This allows the user to set a minimum value for it 
during the optimization and therefore, all the generated alternatives that have a lower 
percentage of footprint coverage than the set value are discarded. However, setting the 
minimum percentage to 100 per cent would be too discriminating and since only a few 
alternatives would be kept in the population, the genetic algorithm could not count on enough 
diversity and it would not be able to use its maximum potential. Therefore, it is suggested to set 
the minimum for this parameter to 70.

6 COMMUNICATION 
Communication is the step of the DSRM in which the result of the development is exposed to 
the opinion of a network of professionals. The feedback obtained from this passage is necessary 
to validate the developed tool and to refine it with more iterations towards a final version. In 
this project, the communication step was performed in the form of meetings with professionals, 
a survey and the publication of the developed tool. While the focus groups and the survey 
determined iterations in the methodology, the publication of the developed tool will obtain 
feedback after the end of this project and therefore, it will be the foundation for future 
development.

During the whole project, regular focus groups with Züblin A/S and DTU were scheduled. 
Specifically, Adham Al-Nachar and Patrick Zangerl, respectively the BIM specialist and the LEAN 
coordinator at Züblin A/S, Tim Pat McGinley, DTU professor and Ole Berard, former head of

6.2 SURVEY 
The survey was designed to support the evaluation part of the algorithm. Finding all the 
parameters that could influence the performative evaluation of the generated alternatives is a 
challenge. Furthermore, the weights of the different factors that influence the score of each 
alternative are not intuitive to define. Therefore, the purpose of the survey was to obtain 
feedback and opinions from the professionals working in the construction industry to define 
what to analyze and what importance to give to each analyzed condition. 
The template of the survey form (Appendix I) was divided into five sections. The first section 
aimed to describe the project and the purpose of the survey to the readers as well as getting 
information about their profession. This was done to make sure the survey was filled out by a 
professional in the construction field. Each of the other four sections was dedicated to each of 
the four goals of the optimization: time, cost, safety and quality. 
Each of these sections included two parts. In the first one, different conditions on the 
construction site were described and the professional needed to select how much the described 
conditions would affect the goals of the optimization (in positive or in negative). The second part 
could be freely filled in with text where the professional could describe more relevant conditions 
to be evaluated with regards to the performance of the four optimization goals. 
The survey was published on LinkedIn and it collected 11 answers. Unfortunately, the result of 
the first part of each section was too confusing to be considered valuable for research purposes. 
In fact, some of the described conditions were considered positive by some professionals and 
negative by others and considering an average of those scores, it would result in an irrelevant 
condition. It is difficult to gauge why the professionals would think of opposite outcomes from 
the same condition as in the survey no space for an explanation was provided. Besides that, the 
number of received answers was too small to consider the result reliable and therefore, the 
weights of the different factors had to be evaluated by testing the tool with multiple different 
construction sites (Chapter 5.2). 
The second part of the survey collected a considerable number of suggestions from the 
professionals. Not all of them could be implemented in the algorithm, confirming its limited 
capability in analyzing all relevant parameters (Chapter 2.2). However, certain suggested 
parameters were deemed relevant and possible to implement. This led to a new iteration to the 
design and development step of the DSRM where the analysis of the buffer distance among 
elements and the analysis of the tightness of the workspace were implemented.

7 CONCLUSION 
In conclusion, with the knowledge gathered during the research and the experience obtained 
during each phase of the adopted methodology, an answer for the research questions identified 
in the problem definition is given together with an overall analysis of what has been 
accomplished during this thesis and what might be attainable with future development of this 
project. 
7.1 CONTRIBUTION 
The research carried out in this thesis addresses the complex task of CSLP. Within the AEC 
industry and especially among contractors, the impact that the planning of the construction site 
has on the productivity of the operations is well known. Construction managers spend a large 
amount of time on it, as considering all the factors that affect the performance of the site is not 
intuitive.  Furthermore, CSLP is usually performed at the late design stage and the construction 
managers need to adapt the layout to the design. The difficulty of setting up a well-performing 
construction site has an impact on the quality of the end product as it affects its buildability. 
Including this task at an early design stage would mean an improved collaboration between 
designers and contractors and a lower cost for changes to the design. The identification of these 
problems led to two questions which were addressed as the research area for the thesis and 
which are answered in the following paragraphs. 
1 - How can the construction manager be supported while performing CSLP? 
During the past decades, much research has been carried out on the use of algorithms to 
optimize the construction site layout. These studies followed different approaches with different 
degrees of accuracy to model both the topography of the site and the objects used to perform 
the activities such as cranes and material storages. Moreover, the techniques used for the 
optimization were multiple, spanning from neural networks to genetic algorithms. Although the 
results of these studies seem promising, the optimizations were only focusing on one parameter: 
the travel path distance. Of course, minimizing the distance among facilities, the productivity of 
the site increases but that is not the only parameter to take into account. 
In this thesis, the identified goals of the optimization were time needed for the operations, cost 
of the operations, safety for the construction workers and quality of the end product and the 
working environment. The best method to use to perform a multi-objective optimization was 
identified to be generative design (GD). The GD iterative process generates an initial population 
of geometries, evaluates their performance and uses genetic algorithms to select the best 
results and optimize them through generations. 
==PAGE:81==
 
69 
 
Therefore, within this thesis, a tool based on generative design to support the construction 
manager on CSLP was developed. Due to the limitations of generative design and the complexity 
of the evaluation system, it was necessary to adopt an iterative process in which the developed 
tool was revisited and improved several times. By carefully finding the right approach for the 
generation and by testing the optimization with different settings to compare the performance, 
the resulting algorithm does not limit the generation of the generative design and, at the same 
time, outputs viable solutions. 
The algorithm takes into account multiple parameters that contribute to the four goals of the 
optimization and therefore, to the overall performance of the generated geometries. These 
parameters have been made measurable in the algorithms by querying the geometry itself. 
2 - How can CSLP be integrated at the early design phase? 
The task of CSLP usually requires data that is only available at late design stages. The needed 
inputs are not only the model of the building but also the schedule of activities to be performed 
on the site and the plan of the supply delivery. The BIM model is used to extract volumes and 
quantities, while the delivery plan is required to determine the size of the material storages. The 
schedule of the activities is needed to perform an optimization that respects the dynamic nature 
of the construction site. This is because the objects on the construction site can not only change 
position but can also be replaced by a new facility if there is no need for it during the entire 
construction process. 
If CSLP is performed at the early design stages, this information is not yet available and 
simplifications need to be made. The proposed tool does not need detailed inputs to perform 
the optimization as it is represented by a static model (a simplification of the dynamic nature) 
and an estimation of object sizes is made through rules of thumb. Furthermore, it does not need 
a detailed model, but only a simple geometry of the site topography, the footprint of the building 
to be built, the site obstacles and the indication of the accesses to the site. 
This means that the developed tool can be used early in the design process and that the 
designers can have an agile feedback loop on the buildability of the design. However, the tool 
cannot produce a detailed solution and the construction manager should take the result of the 
optimization as the foundation for the detailed plan. 
Since designers use various software to conceptualize the design, a multiple-input system was 
developed through the Speckle stream data that facilitates the integration of the tool with three 
different software: Revit, SketchUp and Rhino/Grasshopper. This allows the designer to quickly 
send the input geometry from the design concept and run the optimization of the construction 
site layout whenever the design changes.

7.2 LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK 
The research and the developed tool present limitations that, due to the relevance for the AEC 
industry, are worth analyzing and addressing in further development. 
7.2.1 Dynamic optimization and real-world data collection 
The simplification to the static representation of the dynamic nature of the construction site is 
useful for the integration at the early design stage but it drastically reduces the accuracy of the 
analysis. Because of this, the tool only allows one to obtain an approximate geometry that the 
construction manager needs to detail. This does not mean that the tool is not a support for the 
contractors, but developing the time dimension is crucial to define an optimized layout, 
especially when the available space on the site is tight. A future version of CANTIERE could 
include a toggle that allows the user either to use or not use the schedule of the activities so 
that it can still be used at the early design stages, but it could also provide a more accurate 
evaluation of the site at the late design stages to the contractor. 
The scoring system was developed by testing the tool on the case study. Although this is a good 
approximation to make an initial evaluation of the site layout, a real database could be used to 
improve it. A neural network could be introduced in the evaluation phase of the generative 
design in which Züblin A/S and other contractors could connect their database of collected real-
world data. 
7.2.2 Stand-alone platform and dashboard 
During the development of this research, the tool needed to be updated more than once 
according to the updates to Dynamo and Generative Design. The continuous development of 
these platforms makes the nodes used in the visual programming obsolete and therefore, they 
need to be changed with each new version release. This represents a problem as the tool would 
need continuous updates. With future development, the tool could be made as a stand-alone 
so that it would not rely on any other platform. This is a big step as it requires significant 
development, but that it would give independence to the software. 
Along with the development of a stand-alone platform, the visualization of the result could also 
be improved from the one offered by Generative Design. The possibility of visualizing the design 
grid and filtering the results through the parallel coordinates chart is of importance, however, 
the tool does not provide specific feedback to the designers. In fact, in case of use at the early 
design stages, designers need to interpret the result of the optimization themselves and try to 
change the design to improve it. It would be helpful if, as output, the tool would provide 
concrete advice to the user on what to change to obtain an improved buildability of the end 
product, such as the material, the position or the dimensions of the building.

GENERAL NOTES 
CANTIERE optimizer was developed to support the complex task of construction site layout 
planning. It is capable of producing high-performance construction site geometry alternatives 
by using generative design optimization. The performance of the site layout is evaluated 
considering the time needed for the site activities, the cost of the operations on the site, the 
safety of the site and the quality of the end product and the working environment. 
The simplicity of the inputs needed for the optimization and the integration with more design 
software such as Revit, Rhino/Grasshopper and SketchUp, make CANTIERE an agile tool to use 
at the early design stage. This gives the possibility to the designers to have feedback on the 
buildability of the design, which is often a parameter considered only later on in the design 
process. 
CANTIERE comes as a folder containing the scrips to set the inputs of the optimization (Revit IS 
and SketchUp IS) and two versions of CANTIERE optimizer (Revit 2021 and earlier versions).

RECOMMENDATIONS 
By testing CANTIERE in multiple scenarios, some guidelines for the correct use of the tool have 
been identified. 
1- Before starting the optimization with Generative Design, it is recommended to set the grid 
size to obtain an accurate result within an acceptable computing time. The balance is found 
when the number of cells constituting the grid is in the range of 300 – 1000. This means that 
the bigger is the construction site, the bigger should be the size of the cells. 
 
2- During the creation of a new study in Generative Design, it is suggested to set the minimum 
of the % of covered footprint in a range between 70 and 80. This is to ensure the output of 
a solution in which the crane covers the majority of the building footprint, but still keeping 
a large enough population that allows the genetic algorithm to work correctly. Similarly, to 
keep a balance between performance and diversity in the generated population, it is 
suggested to set the minimum acceptable value for the other optimization goals not higher 
than 5.
