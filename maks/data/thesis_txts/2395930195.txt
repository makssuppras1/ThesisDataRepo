Abstract
Resistance emergence in Mycobacterium tuberculosis is a serious obstacle for eﬃcient
tuberculosis treatment. Due to slow growth rates of M. tuberculosis, drug susceptibility
testing is highly time-consuming. However, advancements in next-generation sequenc-
ing technologies have made it possible to develop fast in silico methods for resistance
prediction.
At Center for Genomic Epidemiology we have developed PointFinder, an in silico
method for predicting resistance associated with chromosomal mutations based on
whole genome sequencing data. In this study, PointFinder was improved with com-
plete features for detecting insertion, deletion, and substitution mutations associated
with antimicrobial resistance. Additionally, PointFinder was re-designed to perform
the resistance analysis from both raw sequencing reads and assembled data.
Assembled data was analyzed using blastn to detect genomic regions of interest for re-
sistance prediction, whereas a novel mapping method called k-mer alignment (KMA)
was used to map raw reads directly to the database sequences. A comparison of the
two methods was performed. It was found that the resistance prediction was improved
when introducing a depth threshold required for gaps to be accepted in the KMA con-
structed alignments. With this threshold the prediction performance of PointFinder
with KMA became equally good or slightly better than blastn.
The performance of PointFinder’s prediction of resistance to 9 anti-tuberculosis drugs
was evaluated based on two M. tuberculosis data sets containing 3574 and 123 isolates,
respectively. A mutation library listing more than thousand M. tuberculosis muta-
tions predictive for resistance was optimized as part of the performance evaluation.
Through the optimization of the mutation library we identiﬁed several non-predictive
mutations, of which many were also reported in the literature to be misclassiﬁed re-
sistance markers. Additionally, we discovered that we could improve the prediction by
also evaluating resistance based on premature stop codons. The ﬁnal performance of
PointFinder showed a varying sensitivity ranging from 0.5 to 0.896 and a high speci-
ﬁcity above 0.9 for 8 out of 9 evaluated drugs.

Introduction
1.1
Antimicrobial Resistance
The discovery of antimicrobials in the 20th century has been the most important event
in the history of modern medicine [1].
With these eﬀective drugs, previously non-
treatable infectious diseases were easily cured, and it is beyond comprehension how
many lives have been saved.
Today we are absolutely dependent on antibiotics and antimicrobials in general. The
extensive use of antimicrobials has, however, caused these drugs to become less eﬀec-
tive for treating infections. Bacteria and other microbes are progressively becoming
resistant to the existing antimicrobials, and the development of new drugs does not
keep up with the pace of resistance emergence [1].
Antimicrobial resistance occurs when bacteria or other microbes change their genome
composition to reduce or eliminate the eﬀectiveness of the antimicrobial compound.
The changes can either be due to transfer of an entire gene from one organism to an-
other, or be caused by de novo chromosomal mutations in genes involved in the same
pathways as the drug targets [2]. Bacteria genomes are highly plastic and our overuse
of antibiotics creates a selective pressure that challenged microbial populations [3]. The
selective pressure favors growth of organisms that, either by chromosomal mutations
or gene transfer, acquire resistance to the antimicrobial compound in their environment.
Our extensive and incorrect use of antimicrobials has allowed resistance emergence
to occur in an increasingly high pace and for a wide range of drugs. This antimicrobial
crises has caused that we now again, many years after the great discovery of antibiotics,
must consider microbial infection as a major health thread.
1.2
Antimicrobial Resistance in Mycobacterium tuberculosis
Resistance to anti-tuberculosis drugs is a major obstacle to eﬀectively treating tuber-
culosis and prevention of the disease worldwide [4]. According to the World Health
Organinsation (WHO) tuberculosis is the 9th leading cause of death worldwide and the
leading cause of death from a single infectious agent [4].
Tuberculosis is a contagious disease that is caused by infection of Mycobacterium tu-
berculosis. The bacteria are airborne and usually attack the lungs. Recent estimates
reveal that approximately 1 out of 4 individuals are infected with M. tuberculosis glob-
ally [5]. Tuberculosis can be latent in which case the host’s immune system controls
the infection by generating multi-cellular granulomas that encapsulate the bacteria.
Within this multi-cellular complex the bacteria can survive in a dormant state. [6].
The life time risk of developing active tuberculosis for latent infected individuals is
5-15 % [4]. Active tuberculosis implies that the immune system is no longer able to
ﬁght the disease.
Most cases of active tuberculosis can be cured with anti-tuberculosis treatment. How-

Isoniazid was observed in 4.1% of all new tuberculosis cases. Moreover 19% of pre-

The anti-tuberculosis drugs target essential pathways for the bacteria existence, such as

is mediated exclusively by chromosomal mutations [7], and the mutations causing re-
sistance emerge in genes associated with the essential pathways. These mutations can
for example hinder drug binding or the initial activation of a pro-drug[9].
1.3
Overcoming Antimicrobial Resistance
To prevent antimicrobial drugs from becoming completely ineﬀective, we must keep
new resistance from developing and prevent already existing resistant bacteria from
spreading. In order to do so, we need to track antimicrobial resistance. Rapid de-
tection of resistant bacteria, can facilitate the initialization of an eﬀective treatment
immediately after the diagnosis of an infection. Hence reducing the misuse of drugs.
Additionally, rapid detection of resistant bacteria is important as their occurrence call
for extra attention and precaution.
Next-generation sequencing (NGS) rapidly yields accurate whole-genome sequencing
(WGS) data, and with suﬃcient knowledge about resistance occurrence and an appro-
priate bioinformatic tool, antimicrobial resistance can be predicted directly based on
WGS data [10].
At Center of Genomic Epidemiology (CGE) we have developed ResFinder [11], an in
silico method for detection of acquired genes associated with antimicrobial resistance
based on WGS data. ResFinder is species independent, but as mentioned above, drug
resistance is not only predictive from the presence or absence of genes. Species speciﬁc
chromosomal mutations can also be predictive of drug resistance. Therefore ResFinder
was recently expanded to include another tool called PointFinder [12]. PointFinder is
a species speciﬁc tool that search for point mutations associated with drug resistance.
The ﬁrst version of PointFinder was only designed with the purpose of searching for
substitution mutations, hence leaving out the detection of insertions and deletions as-
sociated with resistance.
The main focus of this project was to improve the existing PointFinder source code.
This included rewriting the existing source code into the Python programming language
and developing a general method for detection of not only substitution mutations but
also insertions and deletions predictive for drug resistance. As an additional improve-
ment, the speed of the program was increase by implementing an alternative method,

called KMA, for detecting genes of interest directly from raw sequencing data. The
predictive power of PointFinder was evaluated in this project based on two tuberculosis

M. tuberculosis is a well-suited organism for the purposes of testing the new version
of PointFinder as resistance in tuberculosis is exclusively mediated by chromosomal
mutations [7]. Additionally, resistance in tuberculosis is caused by all types of chro-

Moreover, resistance testing for tuberculosis is remarkably time consuming due to slow

great precaution and bio-safety laboratories of class 3 are recommended [13]. This
makes laboratory testing cumbersome and expensive. Thus, an alternative method for
identifying resistant tuberculosis strains is highly needed.

Methods and Materials
2.1
Data Sets
Two data sets were used for evaluating the performance of PointFinder’s resistance
prediction. Both data sets contain raw sequencing reads from M. tuberculosis isolates,
and all isolates are associated with laboratory-determined resistance phenotypes for
up to 9 drugs. The two data sets are independent, hence no samples are contained in
both data sets.
2.1.1
TDR TB Data Set
The TDR TB data set is held by the UNICEF/UNDP/World Bank/WHO Special
Planning Agency for Tropical Disease Research and Training. It was obtained from
pathogenseq.lshtm.ac.uk under ’tuberculosis’ and under ’TDR TB Strain Bank’.
The data set contains 127 isolates, of which 123 were accessible from the European Nu-
cleotide Archive (ENA) at www.ebi.ac.uk/ena/data/view/PRJEB11653. The
samples are associated with laboratory-determined resistance phenotypes for 4 diﬀer-
ent drugs, namely Rifampicin, Isoniazid, Streptomycin, and Ethambutol.
For all drugs, resistance phenotypes are given as the minimum inhibitory concentration
(MIC), which is a clinical measurement of resistance. It describes the lowest concen-
tration of an antimicrobial drug that is necessary for preventing visual growth of an
infectious organism in vitro. To distinguish between susceptible and resistant strains
based on a series of MIC-values, a clinical breakpoint is set. Any strains with MIC-
values smaller than or equal to the breakpoint are said to be susceptible whereas all
strains with MIC-values above the breakpoint are considered resistant. The clinical
break point vary with the species, drug, and growth medium used. The isolates con-
tained in the TBR TB data set were grown in L¨owenstein–Jensen medium which has
the following clinical breakpoints for tuberculosis: 40 µg/ml for Rifampicin, 0.2 µg/ml

2.1.2
ReSeq Data Set
The ReSeq data set was obtained from the Relational Sequencing TB Data Platform
available at platform.reseqtb.org [15].
From here we obtained 3574 tuberculosis
isolates with determined resistance phenotypes for up to 9 diﬀerent drugs, namely,
Rifampicin, Isoniazid, Streptomycin, Ethambutol, Amikacin, Capreomycin, Ethion-
amide, Kanamycin, and Pyrazinamide. The number of isolates with determined drug
resistance varies for each drug. For this data set, the determined resistance phenotype
was given as a R for resistant, S for susceptible or B for samples that were determined
with one test as resistant but susceptible with another test. If a sample was determined
to be both resistance and susceptible to a given drug it would not be included in the
prediction of resistance to the speciﬁc drug.
2.2
Assembly and blastn
In order to use blastn for gene detection, the provided sequencing data were assembled
using SPAdes.
2.2.1
SPAdes Assembly
SPAdes constructs directed Bruijn graphs based on k-mer fragments from the sequenc-
ing reads [16]. SPAdes can use diﬀerent sets of k-mers to build the graphs. Here, we
use k-mers of length 21, 33, 55, and 77 default for SPAdes. The genome sequence is
constructed by ﬁnding the optimal path through the Bruijn graphs, this will be an
eulerian path if possible [16]. An eulerian path goes through each edge in a graph
once. For each continuous path a sequence is composed, and this sequence is called
a contig. SPAdes outputs a ﬁle in fasta format that contains all composed contigs.
SPAdes version 3.6.0 was run with the following command.
\$ spades.py -k 21 ,33 ,55 ,77 --careful -t 4 -m 20
--pe1 -1 FORWARD READS --pe1 -2 REVERSE READS
-o OUTPUT FILE
The careful argument was used to reduce the number of mismatches and short inserts
and deletions as the PointFinder algorithm is very sensitive to gaps and mistaken
mismatches. The input data was paired end Illumina reads, thus the pe1-1 and pe1-2
arguments were used to indicate which ﬁles contained the forward and reverse reads,
respectively.
2.2.2
N50 Calculation
The N50 value is often used in genome assembly to judge the quality of an assembly.
From the contigs, the N50-value is calculated as the shortest contig found in the subset of

2.2.3
blastn
The nucleotide-nucleotide bacis local alignment search tool, blastn, was used to search
for matches between database templates and query nucleotide sequences found in the
contig ﬁles. blastn uses k-mer seeding and the Smith-Waterman algorithm to gener-
ate an alignment[17]. A scoring matrix is used to decide for each position whether a
match, a mismatch, or a gap would beneﬁt the alignment the most. When running
blastn in PointFinder, the chosen parameters for the alignment are at default settings:
gap opening -5, gap extension -2, match 1, and mismatch -3.
The CGE module blaster.py is used to call blastn and to collect the required alignment
data. The module is available at bitbucket.org/genomicepidemiology/blaster.
2.3
KMA
KMA, short for k-mer alignment, is a novel in-house developed k-mer based mapping
algorithm, that directly maps raw sequencing reads to a given database [18].
In order to use KMA on a speciﬁc database, the database must be indexed. The in-
dexing consists in extracting k-mers (by default 16-mers) from the database template
sequences. The k-mers are compressed to quinary numbers with a reference to the
position and template to which the k-mer belongs.
Both the main kma script and the kma indexing program are available at bitbucket.org/
genomicepidemiology/kma/
KMA works in 5 steps. In the ﬁrst step the raw reads are trimmed, and low quality
bases are removed (default minimum Phred score is 30). The second step serves to ﬁnd
matching k-mers between the trimmed reads and the template k-mers. Both reads and
templates are scored according to the number of matching and non-matching k-mers
that they share. A perfect match adds k to the score whereas non-matching k-mers
are penalized with -1. For a read and a template to be accepted for further alignment
their score has to be positive. In step 3, alignments between the accepted reads and
templates are created. As one read can have perfectly matching k-mers belonging to
diﬀerent template sequences, some reads might be aligned to more than one template,
and the read will be assigned to the template that it aligns to with the highest align-
ment score. The alignment score is calculated using a scoring system where matches,
mismatches, gap opening, and gap extension can be user-deﬁned. The k-mers that
match the reads perfectly are used as seeds for the alignment and the stretches be-
tween matching k-mers are aligned using the Needleman-Wunsch algorithm [19]. In
step 4 the best overall alignment score for each of the complete templates are calcu-
lated. This is calculated as the sum of score of all the best read-alignments generated
on the template. If a read has more templates that it aligns equally well to, the read is
assigned to the template with the best overall alignment score. The last step, step 5,
generates the ﬁnal consensus alignmeant by merging all the read-alignments created in
step 3. The nucleotides in the consensus sequence are called based on majority voting.

2.3.1
McNemar’s Test
Signiﬁcant base calls are evaluated with McNemar’s test where the diﬀerent base and
gap counts on each position are compared. A base is said to be signiﬁcantly over-
represented if the following equality is met.
(x −y)2
x + y
> 3.84
Where x is the number of reads containing the most common base and y is the number
of reads with alternative bases. The McNemar’s test follows a χ2 distribution [21].
2.3.2
Running PointFinder with KMA
Prior to running PointFinder with KMA all reference sequences from the PointFinder
database are indexed using the kma program kma index. Kma is run with the following
optional settings: -e 1.0, we accept all templates found independently of their e-value,
-mrs 0.5, the minimum alignment score for accepted reads must be above 0.5 when
normalizing to the read length. Additionally, the scoring matrix for alignment is set
with the same parameters as for blastn: gap opening -5, gap extension -2, match 1,
and mismatch -3.
2.3.3
Minimum Threshold for Gap Depth
KMA can take a matrix argument. This provides the user with a matrix ﬁle, that con-
tains the base, gap, and ambiguous base counts on each position in all found templates.
This matrix is constructed from the read-alignments on the templates, and it is used
in the 5th step of the KMA algorithm to generate the consensus sequence. However,
instead of using the KMA constructed sequences, the matrix is used to customize a
consensus sequence with a reduced occurrence of gaps at low sequencing depths.
Because the PointFinder algorithm is very sensitive to gaps in the alignments, it has
to be assure that gaps are only inserted when it is highly certain that an insertion or
deletion did occur. We introduce a threshold of the minimum number of reads (also
referred to as depth) that agree on the occurrence of a gap.
Majority voted bases or gaps should be signiﬁcantly overrepresented based on Mc-
Nemar’s test, additionally, the depth of a signiﬁcantly overrepresented gap should be
above the set minimum threshold for gap depth. In case this threshold is not reached,
the outcome depends on whether the gap is observed in the read sequences or in the
template sequence.
In case a gap is seen in the template sequence, an insertion is potentially occurring,
however, if the threshold is not met we consider the gap insigniﬁcant, and the gap
in the template will be ignored together with the aligned base. In case a gap is seen
in the query sequence, a deletion is potentially occurring, however, we again consider
the deletion insigniﬁcant. Instead of calling a gap a lowercase n is called at this position.

2.4
PointFinder
PointFinder is a web and command-line application for predicting resistance associated
with chromosomal mutations based on WGS data. PointFinder composes the script
pointﬁnder-3.0.py and an associated database. The web-service exist as a common Res-
Finder and PointFinder platform available at cge.cbs.dtu.dk/services/ResFinder.
The PointFinder sources code is available at bitbucket.org/genomicepidemiology/
pointﬁnder-3.0. The dependencies are KMA or NCBI-blast and BioPython.
2.4.1

lobactor jejunum, Salmonella enterica, and Klebsiella pneumoniae. The database ﬁles
are available at bitbucket.org/genomicepidemiology/pointﬁnder db.

All reference sequences are stored in separate fasta ﬁles with the preﬁx .fsa. The cod-
ing gene sequence must start at position 1 with the ﬁrst codon being the start codon.
The only exception occurs when the promoter region of a gene is predictive for drug

The size of the promoter region will be given in the ﬁle name of reference gene ﬁle.
This is necessary in order to locate the start codon and to detect the correct reading
frame of the gene sequence.
The additional ﬁles required in each species directory are the following three ﬁles:
genes.txt, RNA genes.txt and resistens-overview.txt.
The ﬁle genes.txt is a small conﬁg-ﬁle containing the ﬁle name (without the preﬁx) of
the reference sequences. Only the sequences listed in this ﬁle will be included in the
analysis.
The ﬁle RNA genes.txt is used to distinguish protein coding sequences from non-protein
coding sequences and must contain all sequences, including genes with promoter re-
gions, where at least part of the mutation detection should be performed on nucleotide
level instead of amino acid level.
The ﬁle resistens-overview.txt is the mutation library. This ﬁle list all mutations as-
sociated with drug resistance. The ﬁrst part of the ﬁle must include all substitution
mutation, whereas insertion and deletion mutations are included in the end of the ﬁle.
The two parts of the ﬁle are separated by the hash-tagged line: # Indels.
Each line in the ﬁle represents a mutation. Mutations are identiﬁed by gene name and
a position, corresponding to a position in the reference sequence. The line also contains
information about which alteration will cause which type of drug resistance, and this
will be associated with a PubMed ID.
The tuberculosis mutation library was obtained from pathogenseq.lshtm.ac.uk, un-
der Tuberculosis and Rapid DR Study [22]. The library consists of more than thousand
mutations located in the 32 genomic regions listed in table 1. These mutations had
been found through a systematic review of resistance mutations in M. tuberculosis [22].
2.4.2
Program Usage
PointFinder.py detects chromosomal mutations predictive for drug resistance based on
WGS data. PointFinder can use either KMA or blastn to detect the genes and genomic
regions of interest for the resistance prediction. Dependent on the method of choice
the input ﬁle has to be either one fasta ﬁle or raw reads ﬁles in fastq format from one
single isolate. PointFinder outputs 3 ﬁles, 2 diﬀerent ﬁles listing the found mutations,
and one listing the resistance phenotype predicted by the program.

2.4.3
Program Design
As a main part of this thesis PointFinder was rewriten in Python while designing a solid
algorithm for handling of insertion and deletion mutations in the resistance prediction.
Furthermore, an additional method for gene detection based on raw reads, namely
KMA, was implemented.
The following section describes the program design in details.
9
==PAGE:14==
Student ID: s160509
Extraction genes of interest from input ﬁle(s)
After initial argument parsing and user input error handling, the program starts by
detecting the genes and genomic regions of interest for the following drug resistance
analysis.
Dependent on the method of choice, PointFinder will call either blastn or KMA as
described above (see section 2.2.3 and 2.3.2). KMA and blastn will detect sequences
similar to the database reference sequences in the input ﬁle(s). The detected sequences
must be more than 90% identical to the reference sequences and have a coverage above
60%, unless other coverage and identity thresholds are deﬁned by the user.
Both
methods will return alignments of the database reference sequences and the sequences
found in the input ﬁle(s).
KMA returns one hit per found database sequence, however, blastn can give several hits
per gene. Overlapping hits are joined into one sequence. If the overlap of two sequences
varies from one another both sequences are saved. Hits with a non-mapped stretch
between them are joined with N’s in both sequences of the alignment corresponding to
length of the non-detected region.
Finding mismatches from alignments
In order to ﬁnd all mismatches between the reference sequence (subject sequence)
and the sequence detected in the input ﬁle(s) (query sequence), the aligned sequences
are compared nucleotide by nucleotide or codon by codon depending on what type
of sequence the alignment represents.
Sequences can be either non-protein coding
sequences (i.e. rRNA genes) or coding gene sequences. Furthermore, coding genes
can have promoter sequences. If an alignment contains both a promoter and a coding
region, the alignment of the promoter region is separated from the coding sequence and
the promoter region is evaluated nucleotide by nucleotide while the coding sequence is
evaluated codon by codon.
Finding nucleotide mismatches
Promoter sequences will be assessed from the -1 position relative the the start codon,
thus from right to left, whereas the remaining non-protein coding sequences are assessed
from left to right with positive positioning. The aligned sequences are looped over to
compare one nucleotide at a time. Insertions and deletions are detected as gaps in
the subject sequence (i.e. the reference sequence) or the query sequence (i.e. input
sequence), respectively. The entire stretch of nucleotides aligned with the gap will be
reported as one insertion or deletion.
Finding codon mismatches
Protein encoding genes are assessed codon by codon. The reading frame of the gene
sequence is set using the start position of the subject sequence relative to the start of
the corresponding reference sequence in the database.
If a codon in the query sequence diﬀers from its aligned subject codon, it is checked
whether a gap occurs in one of the codons. Gaps occurring in the subject sequences
10
==PAGE:15==
Student ID: s160509
indicate an insertion, whereas a deletion is indicated by a gap in the query sequence.
The inserted or deleted region is corresponding to the stretches of nucleotides aligned
to the gap. For insertions and deletions the nucleotide sequence and the nucleotide
position, thus not the codon number, will be used as identiﬁers for the mutation for
later look-up in the mutation library. The nucleotide level is used since insertions and
deletions can occur out of sync with the reading frame. Thus, for the purpose of distin-
guishing the position in the codon at which the gap occurs, the nucleotide level is more
appropriate. When a gap is detected, the reading frame is adjusted corresponding to
the size of the gap.
If the query and subject codons diﬀer and no gaps are detected, the codons are trans-
lated into amino acids.
If the amino acids also diﬀer, the mutation is stored as a
substitution mutation. In this case, the alternative amino acid and the codon position
will deﬁne the mutation. In case of the occurrence of a premature stop codon in the
query sequence, the search for mutations will end before the end of the alignment is
reached.
Looking up mutations in the mutation library
The observed mutations are compared to the known resistance predictive mutations
stored in the mutation library. The gene name, mutation position and nucleotide/amino
acid variation are used as keys to look up speciﬁc mutations contained in the mutation
library. In the case of deletions, the stretch of deleted nucleotides can be longer than
the deletion stored in the database, and it is assessed whether the found deletion cov-
ers the deletion from the database. In this case, the deletion mutation is considered
resistance predictive.
If a mutation exist in the mutation library, the drug(s) and PubMed ID (PMID) as-
sociated with the mutation are returned. The mutations are named according to the
recommendations from the Human Genome Variation Society [23].
Output ﬁles and resistance prediction
PointFinder has 3 output ﬁles. One ﬁle is a tab-separated ﬁle with 5 columns; mutation
name, nucleotide change, amino acid change, drug, PMID. Each line in the ﬁle represent
a mutation found any of genes contained in the input ﬁle. The second ﬁle contains the
same information as the tab-separated ﬁle but it is easier to read and easily converted
to HTML when running PointFinder from the web-server.
The last ﬁle only contains one line and is used to report the input samples resistance
phenotype for each drug in the database. The prediction of resistance to each drugs is
performed using the following criteria: If any mutation associated with drug resistance
is found in the sample, the sample is predicted to be resistant to this drug. On the
contrary, if no mutations associated with drug resistance is detected and all genes of
interest for resistance to the speciﬁc drug are found (above the minimal coverage and
identity thresholds), the sample is predicted to be susceptible to the drug. However,if
no mutations associated with drug resistance are found, and one or more genes of
interest for a speciﬁc drug resistance are not found the resistance phenotype for the
speciﬁc drug can not be predicted and a ? will be written to the ﬁle.

2.5
Measurements for Prediction Performance
PointFinder performs binary classiﬁcation when predicting whether a sample is resis-
tant or susceptible to a given drug. In the assessment of the quality of the this binary
classiﬁcation task 4 diﬀerent groups arise:
1. True positives (TP). The samples that are correctly predicted to be positive (resis-
tant).
2. True negatives (TN). The samples that are correctly predicted as negative (suscep-
tible).
3. False positives (FP). The samples that are incorrectly predicted to be positive when
they are in fact negative.
4. False negatives (FN). The samples that are incorrectly predicted as negative when
they are in fact positive.
The quality of PointFinder’s resistance prediction are evaluated using these 4 groups
with 3 diﬀerent measurements, the true positive rate, true negative rate, and the
Matthews correlation coeﬃcient (MCC).
2.5.1
True Negative Rate
The true negative rate (TNR), also referred to as the speciﬁcity, measures the fraction
of negative samples that are correctly predicted as such.
It is calculated from the
following equation [24].
TNR =
TN
(TN + FP)
2.5.2
True Positive Rate
The true positive rate (TPR) are the fraction of correctly classiﬁed positive samples
over the total number of positive samples. This measurement is often referred to as
the sensitivity and is calculated from the following equation [24].
TPR =
TP
(TP + FN)
2.5.3
Matthews Correlation Coeﬃcient (MCC)
The Matthews correlation coeﬃcient takes both the true and the false positives and
negatives into account. Thereby, it considers all groups arising from the prediction task,
and it is applied to balance the sensitivity of the method relative to the speciﬁcity. The
MCC is calculated with the following equation.
MCC =
TP · TN −FP · FN
p
(TP + FN)(TN + FN)(TP + FP)(TN + FN)
The MCC range from -1 to 1, where 1 means that the the predicted values and the
true values are perfectly correlated and the prediction is perfect. -1 means that the

prediction is completely opposite the true values. and a 0 indicates a prediction no
better than random [24].
The MCC is used as the general measurement of the quality of PointFinder’s pre-
diction. The true negative rate and true positive rate will be given a-long-side with
the MCC to show the sensitivity and speciﬁcity of the prediction.
2.6
Optimization by Forward Selection of Mutations
To optimize the performance of PointFinder’s resistance prediction we investigate
whether the mutation library includes non-predictive mutations.
For this task we
only consider mutations from the mutation library that are found in the ReSeq data
set and more than 5 times. This is done to assure that we only eliminate mutations
from that library that are considerably presented in the data set.
In order to distinguish non-predictive and predictive mutation contained in the muta-
tion library, we perform forward selection of the mutations. We ﬁrst consider a model
that does not include any mutations. This model will not predict any resistant isolates
and thus the predictive power of this model is very low. We evaluate the quality of the
prediction using the MCC. We then add one mutation to the model. The one mutation
that will be added is the mutation that beneﬁts the prediction the most. In the next
step of the forward selection we add one more mutation. The mutation to be added
is again the mutation that together with the previously added mutation will give the
best prediction. We keep on adding mutations in this manner until the addition of
any remaining mutations will only decrease the quality of prediction. The resulting
model then contains the mutations that seem to be predictive of resistance whereas
the mutations that are not include will be considered as non-predictive mutations.
While generating the model we will use 3-fold cross validation to ensure that we do not
overﬁt to our data. In each step of the forward selection we calculate the MCC on the
training and the test set. In each fold of the cross validation a model will be generated.
In the end all three models are used to give the ﬁnal model. Only mutations that are
excluded in all the models will be excluded in the ﬁnal model, and these mutations will
be removed from the mutation library.

3.1
Comparing KMA and blastn in PointFinder
PointFinder has been designed to use two diﬀerent methods, KMA or blastn, for de-
tecting the genes of interest for resistance prediction.
Earlier versions of PointFinder only used blastn and was consequently dependent on
assembled data. As KMA can map raw reads directly, the data processing is drastically
reduced with KMA. Since KMA is a novel method, the performance of PointFinder
with KMA on raw reads was evaluated and compared to using blastn on assembled
contigs from the same reads.
The performance of KMA has previously been evaluated [18] however, this work was
mostly focused on KMA’s ability to detect speciﬁc genes. For the PointFinder appli-
cation, where speciﬁc point mutations are of interest, not only the gene detection but
also the precision of the KMA alignment must be evaluated in greater detail.
PointFinder was run for all 123 tuberculosis isolates from the TDR TB data set using
both methods. Raw reads was inputed when running with KMA whereas with blastn
the raw reads was ﬁrst assembled using SPAdes (see section 2.2.1) and the resulting
contig fasta ﬁle was used as input.
PointFinder predicts each isolate’s resistance phenotype for all the drugs included
in the mutation library. The predicted phenotype was compared to the laboratory-
determined phenotype provided together with the TDR TB data set, here deﬁned as

3.3
Evaluating the Predictive Power of Mutations
The prediction performance was not perfect and especially not for some drugs such
as Ethambutol, Amikacin, and Pyrazinamide the prediction was fairly poor. There-
fore, we aimed to optimize the performance by evaluating the mutations found with
PointFinder.
3.3.1
Prediction on Premature Stop Codons
From the work above it was noticed that frameshift mutations did occur, and these
would most likely lead to the occurrence of premature stop codons. Moreover, the mu-
tation library also lists a few premature stop codons as being predictive for resistance.
These premature stop codons were, however, only considered as predictive markers if
found in a speciﬁc position in the gene. The eﬀect of a premature stop codon - gene
truncation - is, on the other hand, more or less the same independent of its exact
position in the gene. Therefore, in the attempt of improving the resistance prediction,
21
==PAGE:26==
Student ID: s160509
all premature stop codons was considered predictive for resistance if found in a gene
already annotated in the mutation library with a premature stop codon.
Assuming that a premature stop codon has the same eﬀect independent on its position
might not be entirely correct. If a premature stop codon is located very close to the
end of a gene, the eﬀect of the gene truncation might be insigniﬁcant. Therefore, we
also considered a more conservative approach where we only predicted resistance if a
premature stop codon was found downstream of the position of the premature stop
codons described in the mutation library.

Discussion
PointFinder has been developed to predict resistance associated with chromosomal mu-
tations based on WGS data. In this study, the PointFinder source code was improved
with new features for detecting insertions and deletions. Additionally an alternative
mapping method, KMA, was implemented, as a potential replacement for blastn. To
assess the quality of PointFinder’s prediction a mutation library listing chromosomal
mutations in the M. tuberculosis genome associated with anti-tuberculosis drug resis-
tance was evaluated.
4.1
Comparison of KMA and blastn
At ﬁrst glance the prediction performed by PointFinder with blastn seemed slightly
better than PointFinder with KMA (table 2), and we looked into the diﬀerence be-
tween the two methods. The number of mutations observed with one method but not
the other, was larger for KMA than blastn (ﬁgure 2). This can be explained by the
fact that KMA uses a global alignment approach and blastn generates local alignments.
blastn cuts oﬀparts of the alignment that decreases the alignment score, whereas KMA
always aligns to the entire template sequence. Therefore KMA is likely to ﬁnd more
mutations than blastn. Furthermore, the assembly may be incomplete and contain
gaps due to low sequencing depth. In case a database sequence is located in-between
contigs, blastn will ﬁnd two partial hits and will miss some of the gene in-between.
KMA on the other hand is highly sensitive and can ﬁnd sequences covered by only one
read [18].
Diﬀerences in base-calling may also be due to the use of assembly-alignment versus
mapping. blastn aligns to contigs which are constructed by puzzling together all reads
in order to assemble the entire genome. KMA only considers reads that map to the
database sequences. KMA might detect unwanted signals from reads representing ge-
nomic regions not contained in the database. Fore example, if two homologous genes
exist but only one of the genes is predictive for resistance and therefore only this gene
is contained in the database, the reads representing the other non-predictive homolog
might mistakenly map to the database sequence. Such cross-talk can lead to base mis-
callings. The assembly is more robust in this manner since all reads are evaluated in
the context of reconstructing the entire genome sequence.
The above mentioned diﬀerences indicate that running PointFinder with blastn on
assembled data might be more accurate as the assembly algorithm resolve the great
puzzle of connecting and compiling every read. However, KMA is a global alignment
method and will therefore always align to the entire template. Additionally, KMA can
map to low depth regions, which can be hard to assemble. Thus, with KMA we might
therefore obtain more information than with blastn.
We observed an accumulation of method-speciﬁc mutation calls caused by frameshifts
observed with one method but not the other. These mutations mainly occurred when
sample depth was low and the assembly was of poor quality indicated by the N50-value

Frameshift mutations have large eﬀects on the PointFinder algorithm.
Frameshift
mutations disrupt the reading frame of the sequence and will most likely result in a
premature stop codon. If a frameshift mutation is mistakenly observed it can poten-
tially cause a false positive or false negative prediction. False positive predictions occur
if a predictive mutation arises in the changed reading frame. False negative predictions
can likewise occur if a predictive mutation is hidden because the reading frame is set
incorrectly. Additionally, a premature stop codon can occur at a position before a
predictive mutation is reached.
Since it seemed that the occurrence of frameshifts was biased at low depths (ﬁgure 3)
and because frameshift mutations have a large eﬀect on the PointFinder algorithm.
We introduced a minimum threshold for position depth required for gap calling, as
described in section 2.3.3.
Figure 4 showed that increasing threshold values gradually changed the depth distribu-
tion of frameshifted gene towards the general distribution of gene depth. The optimal
value for the minimal threshold of position depth was found to be 30 for the TDR TB
data set. Using KMA with a threshold set to 30 resulted in a slightly better prediction
when running PointFinder with KMA than PointFinder with blastn. This indicates
that using a depth threshold for when to introduce gaps improves the alignments.
The minimum threshold of depth required for gap calling served to get around the
unwanted eﬀect of erroneously observed gaps at low depth. We would, however, prefer
to prevent them from occurring in the ﬁrst place, and this led to speculations in the
reason for their occurrence. Since both KMA and blastn were biased to insert more
gaps at low sequencing depths, we assume that the problem must, to some extend,
arise from the sequencing itself. The TDR TB samples were sequenced using Illumina
MiSeq which has an accuracy of 99.9% when all reads containing Phred scores below
30 are removed [25] - this was done by KMA. Approximately 1 out of 1000 base calls
would be incorrect, and at low depth it is harder to distinguish sequencing error from
true sequence variants [26]. We found that a gap depth of 30 was most optimal for
the prediction. Even though, Illumina errors are not randomly distributed [27] the
found threshold seemed high. With an error rate of 0.1% the probability that all the
sequencing errors would pile up in 30 reads around the same position is low. Thus,
there might be something more to this than just the occurrence of sequencing errors.
We should take into account that the threshold was optimized based on a small data set
to which we could be overﬁtting. A re-adjustment of the threshold could be performed
by ﬁnding its optimal value based on the larger ReSeq data set.
We validated whether running PointFinder with KMA using the threshold at 30 on
the ReSeq data increased the prediction performance compared to having no threshold
for gap calling. Figure 5 and table 4 showed that for all drugs, except Ethionamide,
the prediction was either equally good or better when applying the threshold. The
decrease in the prediction performance of Ethionamide resistance could indicate that
by introducing the threshold we ignore insertions or deletions that do cause muta-
tions reﬂecting the true resistance phenotype. However, the Ethionamide sample set
is the smallest sample set with around 240 isolates. We did not calculate a weighted

Ethionamide on the average performance is rather large compared to its small sample

4.2
Predictability of Premature Stop Codons
After correcting for potentially erroneous frameshift mutations, we continued to look
at the predictability of premature stop codons. A premature stop codon can occur as a
result of a frameshift. Thus, we indirectly assess whether the frameshift mutations that
we consider trustworthy also seem to have functional eﬀects on the resistance emer-
gence. The occurrence of a premature stop codon is likely to be associated with loss of
function of the protein encoded by the gene and may lead to a great ﬁtness loss. This
suggests that a premature stop codon may have a functional eﬀect that compromises
this ﬁtness loss e.g. surviving the drug exposure.
We compared the quality of prediction when applying diﬀerent settings for when to con-
sider a premature stop codons as predictive of resistance. The best quality of prediction
was achieved when considering all premature stop codons as predictive for resistance if
they were found in genes previously described with predictive premature stop codons
(approach 3 table 5). Premature stop codons predictive for resistance were described in
the mutation library for 5 drugs, namely Rifampicin, Isoniazid, Capreomycin, Ethion-
amide, and Pyrazinamide. 8 Rifampicin resistant and 22 Isoniazid resistant samples
showed premature stop codons in rpoB and katG, respectively (table 6). None of the
Rifampicin or Isoniazid susceptible strains contained premature stop codons in these
genes. This strongly implies that the observed premature stop codons were correctly
called and the functional eﬀects of gene truncation directly aﬀect the resistance pheno-
type. In fact, rpoB encodes the β-subunit of the DNA dependent RNA polymerase [9].
The evolution of M. tuberculosis strains with truncated RNA ploymerase β-subunits
could possibly only be driven by a strong selective pressure such as the one existing
in the presence of Rifampicin. katG, a catalase-peroxidase enzyme, is required for the
activation of Isoniazid [28]. If katG activity is lost, (e.g. by gene truncation) Isoniazid
cannot be converted to its active form and the harmful eﬀects of Isoniazid on M. tu-
berculosis are lost.
For Capreomycin, Ethionamide, and Pyrazinamide, the association between the occur-
rence of premature stop codons and the resistance phenotype was not as clear as above.
For all 3 drugs, including premature stop codons increased the true positive rates of
the resistance prediction, yet at the same time the true negative rates were reduced.
When considering the MCC, the beneﬁts of including premature stop codons, however,
exceeded the costs of false predictions for all drugs (table 5).
Two genes, panD and pncA, were described with premature stop codons predictive for
Pyrazinamide resistance. Table 6 indicates that resistance was correlated with prema-
ture stop codons in pncA. 11% of all resistant strains showed a premature stop codon
31
==PAGE:36==

in pncA whereas these only occurred in 0.2% of all susceptible strains. As for Isoniazid,
Pyrazinamide is a pro-drug which is activated by the pncA encoded enzyme [9]. The
expected biological eﬀect of a premature stop codon in ethA is loss of the Pyrazinamide
activating enzyme, and this was reﬂected in the resistance phenotype. On the other
hand, panD premature stop codons were observed with nearly the same frequency in
resistant and susceptible strains (0.03% and 0.02% respectively). Later investigations
of non-predictive mutations implied that premature stop codons in panD were not pre-
dictive for resistance (table 7). Other studies have also questioned the role of panD
mutations in Pyrazinamide resistance [29, 30]. Dillon et al. showed Pyrazinamide re-
sistant strains with panD deleted, and they postulated that panD is not a target for
Pyrazinamide resistance [30]. Hence, it might beneﬁt the quality of prediction if all
panD mutation were excluded from the mutation library.
Several premature stop codons in ethA were, based on the mutation library, associ-
ated with Ethionamide resistance. Ethionamide is also a pro-drug and ethA encodes a
mono-oxygenase that converts the pro-drug into its active form [9]. However, 33 strains,
presumably without the ethA mono-oxygenase activity, were still susceptible to Ethion-
amide (table 6). This indicates that the premature stop codons are either not correctly
called, ethA is not the only enzyme able to activate Ethionamide, or Ethionamide also
has antimicrobial eﬀects as a pro-drug. 38% resistant and 17% susceptible strains had
premature stop codon in ethA. Assuming that they were all correctly called, the ethA
premature stop codons did occur with a high frequency. It could be that Ethionamide
constitutes a selective pressure that favours premature stop codon in ethA leading to
intermediate levels of resistance. Moreover, it is possible that the intermediate levels
of resistance will not always reach the threshold for deﬁning resistance.
One premature stop codon in tlyA was described in the mutation library associated
with Capreomycin resistance. Only 5 out of 1157 strains showed premature stop codons
in tlyA. 2 were found in resistant strains and 3 in susceptible strains, representing 1.7%
and 0.3% of the two phenotypic populations, respectively. This might imply that tlyA
premature stop codons to some extend relate to Capreomycin resistance, however the
infrequent occurrence of premature stop codons in tlyA made it diﬃcult to draw any
conclusion.
In summary, Rifampicin, Isoniazid and Pyrazinamide resistance were associated with
premature stop codons in rpoB, katG, and pncA respectively, and the functional eﬀect
of the premature stop codons could be related to resistance development.
Due to the lack of determined resistance in the TDR TB data set we were not able to
assess the impact of premature stop codons on resistance prediction for this data set.
However, we did notice that 15 out oﬀ16 observed premature stop codons occurred in
genes that were described in the mutation library with premature stop codons. The
tendency of premature stop codons to arise in genes where we suspect that they have
a functional eﬀect in resistance progression indicates that the premature stop codons
does not occur randomly due to sequencing or alignment errors.

4.3
Detection of Non-predictive Mutations
Resistance mutations are often associated with a ﬁtness cost and secondary muta-
tion can be seen to compensate for this decrease in ﬁtness [31]. These compensatory
mutations can be hard to distinguish from the true resistance causing mutations. Addi-
tionally, phylogenetic single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) have several times been
misclassiﬁed as resistance markers [32]. The ﬁnal eﬀort to improving PointFinder’s
prediction consisted in detecting such non-predictive mutations that mistakenly had
been included in the mutation library.
We performed forwards selection of mutations based on the mutations cumulative ef-
fect on the MCC. With the forward selection of mutations, we did not consider the
predictive power of the mutations individually, instead we considered the predictability
of each mutations in the context of the other predictive mutations found in the same
sample. This would allow us to detect compensatory or linage speciﬁc mutation as
non-predictive, if they existed in the susceptible isolates.
For 6 out of 9 drugs we observed an improvement of the prediction when removing one
or more mutations from the mutation library. Many of the mutations that we found
to be non-predictive have also been reported elsewhere to be misclassiﬁed as resistance
predictors, for example kasA G269S and kasA G312S [33], rrs 492C>T [34, 35], rrs
517C>T [36], and embB E378A and embC T270I [37, 38, 39].
Based on the fact that many of our ﬁndings are reﬂected in the literature, it seems that
forward selection of mutations is good approach to scan for non-predictive mutation.
Yet, we have to be aware that the optimized mutation library could be overﬁtted to our
training data. We used the smaller TDR TB data set for validation of the optimized
mutation library for 4 drugs; Rifampicin, Isoniazid, Streptomycin, and Ethambutol.
The validation showed that for 3 out of 4 drugs the prediction performance was im-
proved by removing the mutation detected as non-predictive (table 9). However, two
additional false negatives were found when omitting the 3 mutations assessed to be
non-predictive for Rifampicin resistance (ﬁgure 10). The two false negatives had I491F
or L430P substitutions in rpoB. This ﬁnding indicates that in the case of Rifampicin
we actually overﬁtting our prediction model to the ReSeq data set. From table 7 it can
be seen that omitting I491F, L430P and a third mutation rpoB H445N resulted in a
true negative gain of 37 and a false negative gain of 7. The ratio between true negative
gain and false negative gain was the smallest seen for any of the drugs, and the beneﬁt
of omitting these 3 mutations was actually not very notable (table 7 and appendix B).
We therefore chose to re-include the 3 mutations in the mutation library.
For Amikacin and Pyrazinamide we did not have an independent validation set. How-
ever, the true negative gain relative to the false negative gain obtained when omitting
the non-predicted mutations was rather large. Additionally as mentioned a above the
rrs 517C>T and panD mutations were previously reported to be misclassiﬁed resis-
tance markers for Amikacin [36] and Pyrazinamide resistance [30], respectively.
The forward selection of mutation was only applied for mutations contained in the
mutation library. The same procedure could be applied for all found mutations in
order to also detect predictive mutations not included in the mutation library.
Mutations causing resistance might also occur in other genes than those included in

Suspected target genes should be included in the database, so that
PointFinder could be used to detect mutations in any gene anticipated to inﬂuence

As an additional comment, it is important to stress that the development of resistance

available with PointFinder, should be updated periodically to be keep up-to-date with
new ﬁndings in the ﬁeld of resistance development.
4.4
Clinical Impact of PointFinder and Future Perspectives
The ﬁnal performance of PointFinder showed that, for all drugs, we obtained a true
negative rate above 0.9, except Ethionamide with a true negative rate at 0.797 (table
8). The true positive rates ranged from 0.5 to 0.896. In other word, PointFinder is
generally highly speciﬁc, few susceptible samples are falsely predicted as resistant. The
average true negative rate, which was unweighted, was 0.945. This indicates that if
a sample is susceptible it will also be predicted as so in roughly 19 out of 20 cases
when evaluated with PointFinder. In a clinical setting, where this prediction could be
used to manage treatment, it would rarely happen that the intended treatment would
be avoided because of a false positive prediction. On the contrary, PointFinder is less
sensitive. Overall, based on the average unweighted true positive rate at 0.734, ap-
proximately 1 out 4 resistant samples will not be predicted as resistant. In a clinical
setting a high sensitivity is highly desired as this mean that most resistant samples
are detected and extra precaution can be taken. For Rifampicin and Isoniazid the
sensitivity is moderately high (> 0.87) and the clinicians could be fairly conﬁdent that
it would be save and eﬀective to prescribing any of these drugs when the bacteria
are predicted susceptible to them. However, for Capreomycin and Pyrazinamide ap-
proximately every second resistant sample was not detected. Several tools similar to
PointFinder have been developed for resistance prediction in M. tuberculosis among
others, CASTB [40], PhyResSE [41], TBProﬁler [22], KvarQ [42], and Mykrobe Predic-
tor TB [43]. A comprehensive study comparing these ﬁve tools was recently performed
by V. Schleusener et. al. [44]. In this study, 91 strains was analysis and the sensitivity
and speciﬁcity of the tools were measured for the resistance prediction of Rifampicin,
Isoniazid, Streptomycin, Ethambutol, and Pyrazinamide.
The results of the ﬁve predictor tools were compatible with PointFinder’s prediction.
As for PointFinder’s resistance prediction of Rifampicin and Isoniazid showed for all
tools both high speciﬁcity (Rifampicin 0.94-0.96, Isoniazid 0.84-0.98) and sensitivity
(Rifampicin 0.93-1, Isoniazid 0.79-0.93). The resistance prediction for the remaining
drugs diﬀered more and was less accurate, which was also the case with PointFinder.
The tool that generally seemed to perform best was the PhyResSE tool. The mutation
library associated with this tool contained all the same genomic regions as PointFinder,
yet it included two additional genes, ndh and gidB, of interest for Isoniazid and Strep-
34

tomycin resistance [44]. These genes should be included in PointFinder’s database and
mutations associated with resistance in these genes should be investigated and included
in the mutation library. The advantages of PointFinder over the ﬁve assessed tools are
that PointFinder serves as a larger platform together with ResFinder (detects acquired
genes resistance) for resistance detection in multiple species. Additionally, PointFinder
explicitly reports frameshift mutations, mutation restoring frameshifts, and premature
stop codons, which makes it easier to interpret of the function eﬀect of the diﬀerent
sequence variants.
In order to achieve a perfect prediction the reference method for determining the
resistance phenotype must be accurate. A systematic review from 2013 thoroughly
examined the accuracy of diﬀerent drug susceptibility tests (DST) in tuberculosis [45].
They concluded that for Rifampicin and Isoniazid the agreement between DST are
high, above 90%, and highest for the most used and commercial test. Streptomycin
testing were associated with high sensitivity for detecting resistant isolates but only
moderate speciﬁcity for identifying susceptible strains, ranging from 76.7 to 98.8. This
is in agreement with our ﬁndings. We showed true positive rates of 0.545 and 0.799 for
Streptomycin resistance prediction on the TDR TB and ReSeq data sets, respectively.
We observed many false negative, however, if the DST are identifying too many truely
susceptible isolates as resistant, the false negatives might in fact be true negatives in
some cases. Likewise, for Ethambutol the agreement between diﬀerent DST was poor
due to moderate sensitivity of the tests in detecting resistance. In our Ethambutol
resistance prediction, we saw many false positive (298). However, what we observed as
false positives might in fact be true positives, if the determined phenotype was wrong.
For the remaining drugs data was limited, thus the reviewer did not conclude anything
regarding reliability of DST for the remaining drugs.
One of the main problems associated with the varying agreement between tests was
related to the deﬁnitions for when to consider an isolate to be resistant. They conclude
that there is a need for a more thoroughly evaluation of when to clinically consider
a M. tuberculosis strain resistant to Ethambutol and Streptomycin in order to detect
susceptibility accurately for these drugs [45].
Until now, we have considered drug resistance categorically as susceptible or resis-
tant. However, drug resistance can be described with more precision as a continuous
variable where the degree of resistance is taken into account. DST measure resistance
continuously e.g. the minimum inhibitor concentration (MIC) measures the drug con-
centration needed to eliminate the bacteria in vitro. The selected threshold, called
the critical concentration, for when to consider a isolate resistant, is essential for the
determined phenotype and must follow international standardization. However, the
systematic review from 2013 revealed that only 90.6% of 405 studies included in the
review chose a critical concentration according to the standards [45].
The ReSeq data set did not always provide information about what test was used for
resistance testing nor the numeric results of the test. The results from the systematic
review imply that we might have reason to believe that errors occur when determining
the true phenotype and due to diﬃculties in susceptibility testing we should not expect
a perfect correlation between predicted phenotype and DST determined phenotype.

One way to improve the precision of the resistance prediction would be to consider
resistance as a continuous variable. Such approach has been taken for resistance pre-
diction in other species among others N. gonorrhoeae [46]. Here, WGS data was used
to perform multivariate linear regression for MIC prediction. A similar tool that pre-
dicts the degree of resistance to anti-tuberculosis drugs based on the chromosomal
mutations might be relevant to improve the precision of prediction. Diﬀerent muta-
tions might contribute to the resistance progression to a varying extend. Hence, such
predictor could give more reﬁned answers to the role of ethA premature stop codons
in Ethionamide resistance, and it might likewise reveal whether the 3 rpoB mutations
detected as non-predictive in the forward selection of mutation did in fact cause inter-
mediate levels of resistance. Additionally, measurements for the degree of resistance
would be useful for monitoring the progression of resistance over time and improving
antimicrobial surveillance especially when the resistance level is close to the critical
concentration.
5
Conclussion
Through this study a new version of PointFinder was designed, optimized, and eval-
uated. PointFinder was rewritten in Python with improvements of a general method
for detection of insertion, deletion and substitution mutations predictive for resistance.
Additionally, PointFinder was designed to use an alternative method for gene detec-
tion, namely KMA.
We found that method-speciﬁc mutation calls occurred to a higher degree with KMA
than blastn, presumable due to KMA being more sensitive in low depth regions and
thereby including more, yet less accurate, information in the gene alignments.
We found that both KMA and blastn was biased to insert gaps at low depths. However,
introducing a minimum threshold for position depth required for gap calling prevented
the low depth supported frameshifts from occurring and the quality of the prediction
of PointFinder with KMA increased above the quality achieved with blastn.
We found that premature stop codons in rpoB, katG and pncA were predictive for resis-
tance to Rifampicin, Isoniazid and Pyrazinamide, respectively. Premature stop codons
in ethA and tlyA did to some extend show correlation with resistance. Whereas pre-
mature stop codons in panD were not predictive for resistance.
To exclude non-predictive mutation from the mutation library we performed forward
selection of mutations, which was a good method to detect misclassiﬁed resistance
markers. However, for Rifampicin we did seem to overﬁt to the training set based on
the validation of the optimized mutation library on an independent data set.
The ﬁnal resistance prediction showed a general high speciﬁcity and a moderate sen-
sitivity of PointFinder. The performance was compatible to other similar predictors
for resistance prediction in M. tuberculosis. In light of varying accuracy of the refer-
ence methods for drug susceptibility testing, PointFinder’s prediction has potential for
clinical use. Even though, the moderate sensitivity can result in undetected resistance

tance predictions performed by PointFinder would rarely lead to unnecessary avoidance
of intended treatments due to false positive, since PointFinder is highly speciﬁc.

Figure 11: 3-fold cross validation of forward selection of mutations predictive for resistance. All mutations
predictive for resistance found in the ReSeq data more that 5 times were included in the forward selection of mutations
for the following drugs; Rifampicin (a), Isoniazid (b) Streptomycin (c), Ethambutol (d) Kanamycin (e), Ethionamide
(f) Amikacin (g) Capreomycin (h) and Pyrazinamide (i). The ReSeq data set was divided into 3 subsets. For each
fold of the cross validation one subset was excluded from the forward selection and used as test set for validation. The
mutation selection performed on the training set was optimized based on the Matthews correlation coeﬃcient (MCC).
The x-axis shows the number of mutations added and the y-axis the MCC obtained in each step of the forward selection.
The blue line represents the training set and the red line the test set. The vertical dashed line represents the best model
for resistance prediction on the training set. The remaining mutations were added randomly to show the eﬀect on the
MCC when adding these mutations.
