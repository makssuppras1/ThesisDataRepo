Abstract
Steel pylons on a suspension bridge consist of several box sections placed on top of each other. The box
sections are connected by two kinds of connections; overlap connections and welded connections. The
welded connections are very time consuming and expensive and a new solution is sought instead of the the
welds. The new solution investigated in this thesis consist of a bolted ﬂange connection between the box
sections.
The bolted ﬂange connection and the skinplate, which is the outer shell of each box section, result in a
L-stub. The L-stub and the overlap connection are investigated for stiffness and strength where simple cal-
culation models are derived by use of assumptions. Only elastic theory is considered.
Using the theory of beam on elastic foundation and the force method it is possible to derive the stiff-
ness model and obtain an expression for the stiffness for each connection as well as the stiffness for the two
connections considered as a joint. With the determination of the stiffnesses the distribution of forces in the
joint can be obtained as well as the deﬂections in the joint. The analytical stiffness model is compared with
simple numerical models which have been derived using the software tool Abaqus/CAE.
The derivation of the strength model presents new theory of the resistance of a L-stub based on the original
theory of the T-stub. One of the main attempts is to determine the yield line patterns for failure mode 2
where the bolts are yielding and the ﬂange is partially yielding.
The derived stiffness model showed satisfying results when determining the force distribution where a
larger part of the affecting force was obtained by the bolted ﬂange connection. Speciﬁc deﬂection curves
were assumed for the L-stub and it was possible to verify the deﬂection curves with the derived stiffness
model. The simple numerical models showed results for the deﬂections that were not entirely comparable
with the analytical results. The assumptions for the numerical model was assumed to be the reason for the
differences in the results as well as the neglect of the plastic theory.
The derived strength model revealed that it to some extend was possible to ﬁnd the resistance of the L-
stub by using the original theory of the T-stub. Several assumption should be considered when deriving the
new theory and it was therefore not possible yet to conclude that the derived strength model was ﬁnal.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction and hypothesis
The joints in a steel pylon on a suspension bridge usually consists of a bolted overlap connection and a
welded connection between the box sections as illustrated in Figure 1.1a. Because the welding process
is very time consuming and has to be performed offshore the pylons become very expensive. It is there-
fore desired to ﬁnd a new solution for a connection between the box sections such that welding can be
minimized offshore saving time and thereby money. A proposal for a solution implements a bolted ﬂange
connection between the box sections giving one of many advantages that all welding can take place on land.
This thesis investigates the new type of joint structure for the box sections, see Figure 1.1b, for stiffness and
strength. The aim is to analytically derive simple theoretical calculation methods for stiffness and strength
based on assumptions for the overlap connection and the bolted ﬂange connection. One of the most impor-
tant assumptions is the neglect of the bolt elongation. It is desired to obtain fair distributions of the stiffness
and strength with use of the derived methods. The analytical model is veriﬁed by using simple numerical
modelling. The purpose of the derived simpliﬁed methods is to use them as a validation tool in determining
the design of the pylons. Furthermore the report considers future investigations necessary to optimize the
model for more accuracy.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION AND HYPOTHESIS
1.1
State of the art
When building a new suspension bridge the steel pylons are the most time demanding because they have
to be erected offshore. The pylons consist of several box sections which are fabricated on land and placed
on top of each other on the foundation with a crane on site, see Figure 1.2. These box sections have several
vertical stiffeners on the inside which are used in the overlap connections and the sections are welded along
the edge of the skinplate.
Figure 1.2: Erection of the pylon.
Because of the very time consuming and expensive welding process the pylon joints are therefore something
that could be optimized in order to save time offshore. A proposal of a new design of the joint between
the box sections could be to implement a bolted connection instead of the welded connection. A bolted
ﬂange should be welded to the end of the skinplate becoming a part of the box sections fabricated on land.
This results in all welds in the box sections being performed on land saving time offshore. The ﬂange plate
at the end of the skinplate is then bolted to the ﬂange plate at the skinplate in the next box section. By
using this kind of connection instead of welds along the skinplate other advantages are obtained besides
time saving. When avoiding welds a higher strength of steel can be used which results in thinner plates
in the box sections and a larger element can therefore be lifted by the crane offshore. If the elements are
larger, then the amount of joints along the pylon can be reduced because of a lower number of sections. The
painting job of the pylon can also be performed on land instead of offshore because of no welding between
the box sections. So by introducing a new design of the joint both time and material can be saved.
By introducing this new type of joint it is therefore wished to investigate the stiffness and strength as
the existing theory does not provide any information of the calculations for this type of joint.
1.2
Objective
The objective in this thesis is to investigate the stiffness- and strength interaction between two connections
- a bolted end-plate connection and an overlap connection with bolts - that is located in a steel pylon on a
suspension bridge. The connections are investigated for stiffness and strength individually and afterwards
as a joint. A fair distribution of stiffness and strength is wanted such that the capacities are not exceeded.
==PAGE:17==
1.3. SCOPE OF WORK
3
Two simple theoretical calculation methods are developed based on assumptions in order to obtain a model
that can estimate the stiffness and strength for this particular joint.
Hypothesis:
Is the assumptions for the simple calculation methods possible for calculating this particular problem in
order to ﬁnd fairly distributions of stiffness and strength?
First an analytical approach is derived followed by a simple numerical model to compare and verify the
analytical stiffness model.
1.3
Scope of work
The work in this thesis primarily consists of three parts; an introduction to the problem case and the as-
sumptions for how to derived the models, a theoretical study and derivation of the solved problem case and
last, a numerical analysis.
The introduction of the problem case describes the investigated structure and the procedure of how to
develop the simple calculation methods in order to estimate the stiffness and strength. Furthermore it states
the assumptions used throughout the thesis.
The theoretical study and derivation of the solved problem ﬁrst explains background theory for the al-
ready existing theoretical methods used in order solve the problem case. Secondly it shows the theoretical
expressions obtained in order to ﬁnd stiffness and strength when following the simple calculation method.
The numerical analysis consists of simple modelling of the joints with use of the software Abaqus/CAE
to verify the analytical stiffness model.
At last, the discussion and conclusion are based on the obtained results.
The problem case is provided by COWI together with all the information of the joints.

A normal force will affect the pylon running in the outer shell where it will be distributed between the
skinplate and the overlap connection. How much of the force that will be distributed to each part in the
pylon depends on the stiffness of the parts. Because the overlap connection is very stiff compared to the
skinplate and the bolted ﬂange the main part of the normal force will be obtained by that connection.
The overlap connection can be categorized as a friction connection where the force will be transferred
between the plates by shear. The distribution of the force will vary along the length of the overlap connec-
tion, [9]. The plates are assumed inﬁnitely stiff and only the bolts will deform, [4].
The skinplate and the bolted ﬂange can be considered as one connection. This connection is an end-plate
connection in bending subjected to tension which is the part transferring the tensile forces. The end-plate in
bending can be categorized as a L-stub. This is a bolted connection subjected to tension, [13]. The ﬂanges
are anticipated to yield before the bolts and the bolts are therefore anticipated to develop yielding after the
ﬂanges have started to yield. This results in elongation of the bolt shaft which will affect the resistance of
the connections.
2.1
Stated assumptions
Because the purpose of this thesis is to derive simple calculation methods to determine stiffness and strength
for the two connections, assumptions and certain choices have been necessary in order to derive the meth-
ods.
As described earlier in this chapter the welds in the joint are completely neglected and only the bolted
connections are considered. The calculation model for the stiffness only considers the elastic theory ne-
glecting the plastic theory. The calculation model for strength of the L-stub assumes the theory of the
original T-stub using plastic theory, [4].
In general the elongation of the bolts in the L-stub is neglected. Because the bolts are preloaded it is
assumed that the preload will result in a signiﬁcant smaller elongation of the bolts than if not preloaded.
This elongation is assumed insigniﬁcant in the simpliﬁed models and therefore not studied in this thesis.
Moreover, the preload is not considered further in the calculations.
For the overlap connection the bolts are neglected and therefore completely ignored both for the stiff-
ness model and the strength model.

CHAPTER 3
Stiffness
3.1
Modelling of stiffness
This section gives an introduction to the modelling of the stiffness of the joint. Assumptions important
for the modelling are presented where the assumptions stated in Chapter 2 should also be remembered and
considered.
The purpose of the stiffness modelling is to ﬁnd an expression of the total stiffness of the considered
joint part shown in Figure 2.4. To be able to ﬁnd the total stiffness which is a combination of the overlap
connection and the L-stub, the stiffnesses of the individual components are found ﬁrst starting with the L-
stub, second with the overlap connection and last with the overlap connection and L-stub as a joint. These
stiffnesses are used to ﬁnd deﬂections as well as the force distribution in the joint.
3.1.1
L-stub
Before the explanation of the calculations can take place, an important notice for the moment of inertia
for the L-stub is presented. The notice is that three different moment of inertia are used for the different
calculations for the L-stub and they are illustrated in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Different moment of inertia for the L-stub.
The two strips illustrated in Figure 2.5 and 2.6 each have a stiffness that will vary per running meter. The
stiffness for the longitudinal strip will vary in the y-direction where the stiffness for the transverse strip will
vary in the z-direction (when considering the coordinate system in Figure 2.4). When choosing a value for
z and y the stiffness for each strip will result in the total stiffness in that exact point on the skinplate.

to [10].

3.1. MODELLING OF STIFFNESS
13
Figure 3.4: Submodel A: Beam on elastic foundation with inﬁnite length.
Figure 3.5: Submodel B: Bolted ﬂange.
At ﬁrst, Submodel A is investigated with the theory of beam on elastic foundation, [17]. To be able to
determine the rotational stiffness, Kθ, in the corner between the bolted ﬂange and the skinplate, it is ﬁrst
desired to derive an expression for the deﬂection of the skinplate. This deﬂection is derived by solving a
fourth order differential equation and hereafter the rotational stiffness can be determined.
This states that two contributions to the total stiffness of the L-stub are known; kb(xb) which is from
the continuous spring and Kθ which is from the deﬂection of the skinplate.
Secondly, Subsection B is investigated with the theory of force method, [11]. This theory is used to de-
rive an expression of the stiffness of the L-stub strip with contributions from both the longitudinal strip, the
transverse strip and the bolted ﬂange. A deﬂection of the bolted ﬂange in the vertical direction is derived by
use of the force method and the stiffness contribution from the derived deﬂection, Ku, can then be found
by using Hooke’s Law, F = k · u.
The fourth and last contribution, Kn, to the stiffness of the L-stub is from the normal force in the steel
pylon. The normal force is running in the outer part of the pylon and thereby in the skinplate resulting
in normal stresses which results in elongation of the skinplate. And it is this elongation that contributes
to the ﬁnal stiffness of the L-stub. The ﬁnal stiffness of one strip on the L-stub, Ktot,s(xb), can then be
determined by use of the adapted component method, see Section 3.2.

overlap connection. The structural part is therefore now considered as illustrated in Figure 3.6 when con-
sidering the overlap connection.

As the normal force runs in the outer part of the pylon, it also runs in the overlap connections and results in

4.1
Modelling of strength
This section gives an introduction to the modelling of strength for the L-stub and the overlap connection.
The assumptions for modelling the strength are presented both in Chapter 2 and this chapter, ﬁrst for the
L-stub and then for the overlap connection. The strength is only found for the two joints separately.
The purpose of the strength modelling is to derive a simple calculation method for the L-stub based on
the already known theory for the T-stub and with this simple method determine the resistance of the con-
nection.
4.1.1
L-stub
To be able to model the strength of the L-stub it is important to obtain knowledge of the theory of the origi-
nal T-stub. The method procedure of the T-stub theory is used to ﬁnd the resistance of the L-stub where the
same assumptions are made. The T-stub theory is therefore presented in Section 4.2 before the derivation
of the theory of the L-stub begins. The strength of the T-stub as well as the L-stub is found by the upper
bound method which uses yield line theory and the principle of virtual work. The T-stub theory uses plastic
theory, [4], [7].

the L-stub compared to the original T-stub is that the L-stub only has one bolt per bolt row. The numbering

Theory of overlap connection
The strength of the overlap connection is determined by the deﬁnition of force
Fo,Rd = σ · A
(4.78)
The overlap connection then consists of a stiffener and two overlap plates resulting in three plates. The
area is taken as a mean value because of different thickness of the plates. The area is therefore assumed the
same as Aave in Eq. (3.58). The stress is assumed as the normal stress, σn, and the strength of the overlap
connection is therefore equal to the normal force in the connection, Fn,o.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
A new optimization of the joint between box sections in a steel pylon on a suspension bridge is sought by
implementing a bolted ﬂange connection. This results in the joint consisting of a L-stub and an overlap
connection. The joint has been investigated for stiffness and strength by deriving simple analytical calcula-
tion models by use of analytical theories and modelling a simple numerical model with use of the software
Abaqus/CAE. All models derived only consider elastic theory.
The analytical stiffness model is derived stating assumptions and using already existing theory such as
beam on elastic foundation and force method. It is desired to ﬁnd a total stiffness of the joint by deriving
the stiffness of the two connections individually. The total stiffness is veriﬁed by the force distribution in
the joint. Because the overlap connection is very stiff it is assumed that this connection will obtain most of
the normal force affecting the pylon. This assumption is concluded to be valid since the overlap connection
is discovered to obtain around 58% of the normal force and the L-stub will obtain around 42%. Furthermore
it can be concluded that the bolted ﬂange has the desired effect of obtaining a large part of the normal force.
The importance of the stiffness of the transverse strip on the skinplate is investigated where two cases
consisting of a simply supported beam and ﬁxed supported beam are compared. The stiffness of the trans-
verse strip is not shown important for the original thickness of the ﬂange. If the thickness of the ﬂange is
deﬁned as a lower value than the the original thickness, larger deﬂections will occur and a larger variation in
the force distribution is observed. The deﬂections are observed to be lower for the ﬁxed supported case and
the lower the thickness of the ﬂange, the larger a parabolic variation in force distribution will be obtained.
It can therefore be concluded that the stiffness kb(xb) is of some importance for total stiffness of the joint
and should not be neglected.
Speciﬁc assumptions are conducted in order to obtain the assumed deﬂection curve for both the skin-
plate and the bolted ﬂange. The derived stiffness model can therefore be concluded to comply with the
assumption for the deﬂection curves of w(xs) and u. This states that the derived analytical stiffness model
is considered valid in verifying the stiffness of the joint. However, it should be noted for the conclusion
marks for the stiffness model that the derivation of the model is new theory and the assumptions must be
considered.
The numerical model of the joint shows some differences from the analytical stiffness model. The two
models are therefore not entirely comparable and this can be because of the simplicity of both models. In
order to obtain a better comparison between the models, it is assumed that a more detailed numerical model
can obtain better comparable results. However, the results for the deﬂections for the two models are not of

The analytical strength model is as for the stiffness model derived using already existing theory such as
the T-stub theory. Yield line patterns are derived for Mode 2 for the L-stub where existing theory only
uses yield line patterns for Mode 1 as reference. Because the derived strength model is new theory and the
assumptions from the T-stub theory are considered, it can be difﬁcult to conduct a ﬁnal conclusion of the
validity of the strength model due to the necessity of experiments. However, it is assumed from the derived
yield line patterns that Mode 2 will be dominating and this is also concluded to be valid. It can to some
extend therefore be concluded that it is possible to derive a strength model for the joint by using the T-stub
theory.
A ﬁnal conclusion mark can be stated. The two derived models for stiffness and strength are just one
solution to the problem. By experiments and further investigations on deriving the analytical models more
accurate results will be expected.
7.1
Further work
To optimize the two derived models as well as the numerical model an implementation of plastic theory can
be applied. This will take yielding into account in the joint.
The models can also be optimized by considering the elongation of the bolts. This could be done by
modelling the support for the bolt as a spring instead of a simple support in the stiffness model. It can be
investigated whether the deﬂection of the bolted ﬂange corresponds to the elongation of the bolts.
The validity of the T-stub theory applied on the L-stub can be investigated by experimental work. By
conducting experiments the real deﬂection of the L-stub can be determined as well as the criteria for the
dimension n. Or maybe it will result in other criteria. At last the yield line patterns for Mode 2 can be
investigated.
