PREFACE 
 
This report focuses on the development of a method for chemical bottom-up synthesis of a 
nanoporous gold (NPG) film equivalent to films produced from dealloying methods. The work 
presented is based on a long chain of developments, stemming from a nanoparticle synthesis called 
the SAMENS (Saccharide-based approach to metallic nanostructure synthesis) method, developed in 
the DTU NanoChemistry group since 2007. 
During my bachelor project, previously worked with the SAMENS synthesis method in 2015, and 
developed nanoporous gold films during a special course. 
In addition to incorporating Pt in the synthesized nanoporous gold, the synthesis method for forming 
pure nanoporous gold has also been greatly improved and an article concerning this has been 
composed and will be submitted very soon 
 
ABSTRACT 
 
Nanoporous metals are metallic structures consisting of pores and ligaments on the nanometer scale. 
In general nanoporous metals are of great interest because of their catalytic characteristics which are 
closer to nanoparticles than to bulk metals, and also the surface areas for this type of porous catalysts 
are high. An example of the enhanced catalytic activity of porous metals is nanoporous gold, it is 
especially interesting due to its potential application as catalysts in many important reaction systems. 
The most commonly used method to make nanoporous metals is dealloying, in which an alloy is 
stripped of its less noble part during an etching procedure. This way, because of surface diffusion 
effects, a nanoporous structure of noble metal is left behind. Compared to common dealloying 
methods of making NPG, a pure bottom-up method of chemically synthesizing NPG would be 
preferable, because only a single metal precursor is needed, hence it is possible to avoid any trace 
metal impurities which have previously been found in dealloyed nanoporous metal. Chemically 
synthesized NPG (cNPG) lends itself to investigations into what is responsible for the high catalytic 
activity of dealloyed NPG, previously it has been hypothesized that the presence of trace metal atoms 
contribute to the catalytic activity. 
The method used to create cNPG, is a modification of the SAMENS synthesis method to create Au 
nanoparticles. By reducing gold metal precursor with the molecule 2-(N-morpholino)ethanesulfonic 
acid (MES), which plays a role acting as a reducing agent, AuNP capping agent and pH buffer, the 
particles are not stable for longer periods, and some assemble at the liquid-air interface, into a 
nanoporous structure. The morphology and porosity of cNPGFs are controllable by experimental 
conditions such as temperature, chloride concentration ionic strength and protonation of the buffer. 
By modifying the surface composition of the cNPG film with Pt, it has been attempted to develop a 
nanoporous film mimicking the structure and properties of conventional Au@Pt core-shell 
nanoparticles. The film produced by the method proposed in this report show has been characterized 
==PAGE:3==

in several different ways, the physical structure has been thoroughly investigated by microscopic 
techniques including TEM, SEM and STM. The elemental composition of the film has been 
characterized by EDX. Cyclic voltammetry (CV), has been applied to probe the electrochemical 
characteristics, as well as confirm the presence of both Au and Pt in the surface layers of the film. 
The synthesized film, being a porous platinum surface structure could very likely make it a suitable 
fuel cell catalyst. The synthesized film is called AuPt-NPF in the rest of the thesis.

1. INTRODUCTION 
 
Gold has been used for art, decoration, dental materials and currency for decades due to its relative 
chemical inertness.  
The first known historical example of a dealloying technique developed by humans is the depletion 
gilding techniques of pre-Columbian Indians from around 300 AD.1 They produced golden ornaments 
from a copper-gold alloy, and found that by etching the surface Porous gold was left. This was 
because of the selective dissolution of copper. 
In contrast to the inertness of bulk gold, gold nanomaterials have shown excellent catalytic activity 
towards many chemical reactions.2–5 unique electronic, catalytic and optical properties and offer 
potential for applications in molecular electronics, bioimaging, biomedicine, sensors, catalysts and 
nanotechnology.5–11 It is believed, that many factors influence the increased catalytic activity when 
particle sizes decreases. One main theory is, that it is the drastic increase in the number of low-
coordinated Au surface atoms, which act as catalytic sites.12 
Gold nanoparticles (AuNPs) in the range of 1-100 nm can be synthesized in solution by various 
recipes, which commonly contain two key steps, i.e.  chemical reduction of gold precursors such as 
HAuCl4 to nanoparticles, and formation of stabilizing coating layers around the particles.4,5 From 
building block AuNPs, production of gold nanorods, nanostars, sponges, core-shell nanoparticles 
with different elemental compositions and functional AuNP hybrids as well as their functionalities 
have been developed and investigated profoundly.13–18  
Nanoporous gold films (NPG) are three-dimensional solid films, consisting of bi-continuous 
networks of gold ligaments and pores with extraordinarily large specific surface areas.19 NPG possess 
a high degree of low coordinated surface atoms, which give rise to surface stress as well as enhanced 
reactivity.19,20 NPG is electrically conductive and represents an ideal electrode materials for 
electrochemical reactions.21,22 Recent discoveries show that catalysts based on NPG promote 
selective reactions at low temperature.6 Many functional molecules can be introduced on the NPG 
surfaces via surface engineering serving as platforms for design and construction of new 
functionality. 
Nanoporous metals were not used as catalysts until 1925 when Murray Raney patented the method 
for manufacturing Raney metals.23 Raney metals are made from a wide selection of alloy metals, but 
one of the components are always aluminum which is the less noble constituent and selectively 
dissolved.24 Raney metals have been very successful catalysts, and is still used for many processes 
today, especially for hydrogenation reactions. 
The first TEM investigations of dealloyed nanostructures were made in 1963, which was an important 
step.25 An understanding of dealloying mechanisms was developed in 1960’s by people working on 
general corrosion phenomena, in order to protect metals from corrosion. Not until 2001 was a more 
thorough physical mechanism of the dealloying of Ag-Au described26 
==PAGE:7==
7 
 
Today, we have three different mechanisms used to describe the selective dissolution of a metal in an 
alloy.27 
(i) both metals ionize followed by redeposition of the more noble metal; (ii) only the less noble metal 
ionizes and enters the solution while the atoms of the more noble metal aggregate by surface diffusion; 
(iii) only the less noble metal ionizes and enters the solution and atoms of both metals move in the 
solid phase by volume diffusion. 
The formation of nanopores by dissolution of metal is a highly complex mechanism, and very 
dependent on the alloy system. Therefore, not all systems are completely understood and it is difficult 
to attribute the pore formation to a single mechanism. Still, some systems have been more thoroughly 
investigated than others, for example the Cu-Au alloy system, it has been proven that no dissolution 
of Au takes place, and that interdiffusion of Cu and Au is occurring.28 Mechanism (ii) cannot be 
neglected since it has not been disproven. 
The main reason to develop a synthesis method for a bimetallic Au Pt nanoporous film, is to use it in 
catalytic reactions, it is especially interesting as a possible catalyst material in fuel cells, platinum is 
currently one of the favored materials for fuel cells. The advantages of using a porous bimetallic film 
is potentially multiple. If a film with a perfect shell layer of Pt around a backbone of Au can be made, 
the utilization of Pt can be significantly increased compared to ordinary Pt nanoparticles currently 
used. Also the electron transfer is likely improved as the metal film is highly conductive, and unlike 
other catalyst systems, there is no boundary between the catalytic part and the conductive carrier 
material.  The lack of a boundary between the catalytic part and a carrier is also an advantage 
considering the stability of the film, usually Pt nanoparticles can detach from a carrier material and 
leach out of the catalyst. The thickness of the nanoporous film is also significantly less than a common 
catalyst layer found in in fuel cells, which are in the micrometer range. 
 
1.1 PREPARATION OF NANOPOROUS GOLD FILMS USING DEALLOYING METHOD 
 
Fabrication of dealloyed nanogold is a field which have been studied quite extensively, and a lot of 
different preparation routes exist. Here is presented a general preparation method for dealloyed NPG, 
and some considerations. 
Ag/Au gold leaves have long been used as a convenient starting material, to fabricate NPG 
electrodes29. Usually a 12-carat gold leaf can be used, but other compositions are also used. There are 
also other methods for preparing the alloy, such as sputtering, evaporation and electroplating 
The procedure is to dissolve the less noble metal, commonly Cu or Ag, by etching. Commonly HNO3 
has been used, but other solutions could also be used. This depends to some degree on dissolution 
selectivity of the solvent toward the less noble component.30 The choice of alloy components are also 
dependent on the mechanical compatibility of the metals, since big differences in coefficients can 
increase the cracking of the film. Electrochemical etching with perchloric acid is another approach to 
the dissolution, and can give a finer porosity.30 
The gold leaf can be handled with a roller, and transferred to the etching solution. Surface tension 
supports the gold leaf on the surface of the solution, and makes it easier to handle after etching. The 
==PAGE:8==
8 
 
gold leaf can be left in the bath for a varying amount of time, the time it is left in the solution can also 
affect the final structure, usually 30 – 60 minutes are used. It is quite common to modify the structure 
of NPG after fabrication, often this will be a heat treatment which will coarsen the pores and 
ligaments, and thereby the features can be accurately controlled.31 Other methods of post modification 
can also be used. 
 
1.2 CHEMICAL SYNTHESIS OF NANOPOROUS GOLD FILMS 
 
The chemical synthesis of cNPG starts by HAuCl4 reduction with MES buffer, forming AuNPs. MES 
has a multipurpose role in the synthesis, acting as: (1) a pH buffer, (2) a reducing agent and (3) a NP 
capping agent. The procedure has been modified from a previously reported synthesis of MES-
stabilized AuNPs32, and is summarized in Figure 1. KCl is added before AuNP formation, which 
serves to increase the ionic strength of the solution and screening of the electrostatic repulsion 
between the NPs as well as reducing the surface tension at the air-liquid interface. Consequently, 
AuNPs are driven to the interface due to the created energy difference (E).33 After AuNP formation, 
adsorption of nanoparticles to the air-liquid interface is observed as an initial diffuse and incoherent 
film, which behaves very fluid-like. During the following 30 min, a NP concentration gradient from 
the surface towards the bulk of the solution is created due to AuNP adsorption to the interface. This 
becomes the driving force for the NPs in the solution bulk to migrate towards the surface. The 
character of the film gradually changes over time, and separate patch-like structures can be seen 
trapped at the interface and in constantly motion due to convection in the hot solution. The final 
reaction step involves the addition of HCl. The acid acts as a MES stripping agent, effectively 
destabilizing the AuNPs. As a result, AuNPs in the solution precipitate while AuNP surface structures 
at the air-liquid interface sinter together to form robust cNPG. 
 
Figure 1. The cNPG film synthesis procedure and mechanism. MES buffer and KCl are preheated prior to Au precursor 
addition. MES reduces HAuCl4 to AuNPs while KCl increases ionic strength, decreasing both electrostatic repulsion 
between AuNPs and the sorption barrier, allowing interfacial adsorption of AuNPs. Driven by the concentration gradient, 
bulk NPs diffuse towards the surface creating interfacial AuNP assemblies. Finally, addition of HCl induces deprotection 
of NPs by removal of MES, resulting in structural sintering and cNPG film formation.

(H2Pt6 • xH2O, Aldrich, ≥99.9%), 2-(N-morpholino)ethanesulfonic acid hydrate (MES)

1.2.2 fabrication 
 
In a standard synthesis, 12 mL of ultrapure water from Sartorius water system, (18.2 MΩcm), 4 mL 
100 mM KCl and 2 mL 100 mM MES (pH 8.0) were mixed in a Ø 50 mm beaker, which was then 
placed in an oil bath for pre-heating at 80 °C for 5 min. Then, 2 mL 20 mM HAuCl4 was added while 
the beaker was kept at 80 °C. AuNPs were formed almost instantly and the reaction was left to proceed 
for 30 min. At this point, an oily “pre-film” consisting of individual microscopic gold assemblies at 
the air-liquid interface had formed. Upon addition of 75 µL of 4.0 M HCl, the NPG morphology 
started to change into a single coherent piece of film. This process was allowed to occur for 10 min 
at 80 °C after which the beaker was removed from the oil bath. After the synthesis, it is important to 
separate the film from the AuNP solution to stop the process completely. The reaction mixture was 
partly removed from underneath the cNPG by pipette followed by addition of ultrapure water. This 
was repeated twice to pre-clean the solution. Then, the beaker was filled to the brim with water, and 
the whole beaker was immersed carefully into a large glass bath with ultrapure water, taking care not 
to break the cNPG in the process. From this container, the NPG was transferred to a 3 L beaker with 
ultrapure water with a clean plastic spoon for final cleaning through diffusion and storage.

1.3 METHODS FOR CHARACTERIZATION 
 
For the characterization of the cNPG, several different methods were used, for the structural 
characterization. An initial check of the film on microscale was performed using an optical 
microscope, connected to an optional camera. The fine nanostructure and pore structure was 
investigated using a TEM. The porosity of the sample is a key parameter to know, but it is not a 
simple parameter to investigate, mainly because the sample quantity is very small. TEM can give 
very accurate measurements of the pore sizes, but it is very limited when the sample isn’t thin enough 
to be assumed to be 2-dimensional. In case there are inhomogeneities in the structure, the TEM image 
can’t be assumed as a representation of the whole sample. The pore sizes determined from TEM 
images was 20 ± 10 nm, and the ligament sizes 30 ± 10 nm. The method most often used for 
determination of surface area, and hence porosity, is physisorption of nitrogen or argon, but it is not 
suitable in our case, since much larger quantities of sample is needed for this method. Instead we 
decided to use a quartz crystal microbalance (QCM), which is suited for determining masses of films, 
commonly self-assembled monolayers or biofilms. The weight determined using this method was 100 
± 50 µg/cm2. The QCM is not specifically suited for porous films, so we chose to also use another 
method for determination of the sample mass, atomic absorption spectroscopy (AAS). AAS gave a 
weight of 50 µg/cm2. 
When we know the mass of a specific area of the film, we know the density of the sample. Since the 
height of the sample can be measured by Atomic force microscopy (AFM), the height has been 
determined to be 500 ± 200 nm. 
 
2. CHEMICAL SYNTHESIS OF BIMETAL NANOPOROUS FILMS 
 
2.1 DEVELOPMENT OF RECIPE FOR CHEMICAL SYNTHESIS OF PT-AU POROUS FILMS 
 
The initial attempts at developing a synthesis method for making a combined AuPt film, was based 
on things learnt during the development of the synthesis method used for the nanoporous gold film. 
It was initially believed that to synthesize a film from core-shell nanoparticles, the core-shell particles 
could form initially, followed by the formation of a film. This presents a problem, since the formation 
of the NPG film is quite fast, whereas Pt reduction is a slow process. A method is therefore needed 
to both inhibit nanoporous film formation during core-shell formation, and initiate the film formation 
again after core-shell particles have been formed. This was attempted by running the initial steps of 
the synthesis in a flask filled completely with solution and fitted with a glass stopper, so as to limit 
or prevent any air-water interface, on which the nanoparticles could form a film. Afterwards the 
formed core-shell particles, are poured out into a beaker and heated at 90 OC for 1½ hours, afterwards 
only modest amounts of surface film was formed, and it lacked any structural integrity. 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
It was hypothesized that the Pt shell of the particles might interfere with the formation of a strong 
matrix, therefore it was chosen to abandon the idea of synthesis in a flask, and instead synthesize in 
an open beaker, and adding the precursors shortly after one another, so that the reduction and film 
formation happen simultaneously. It was thought that Au particles might form an initial film in this 
way, which could act as a template for further particles. 
 
2.2 CHEMICAL SYNTHESIS 
 
To find the chemicals list, see section 1.2.1. 
The synthesis vessel used is a low Ø70 mm beaker, and the beaker is placed in an oilbath at 90 oC, 
the reaction volume is 40 ml. First step is to add 20.8 ml of ultra-pure water, 8 ml 100 mM glucose 
and 4 ml 100 mM MES pH 8.0, to the beaker, together with a small magnet, and place it on the oil-
bath. After 5 min. 4 ml 20 mM HAuCl4 is added and the solution immediately changes color to very 
dark red, after a further 30 seconds 3.2 ml of 20 mM H2PtCl6 is added. Remove the magnet after the 
H2PtCl6 has been added. Let the beaker stand on the oil-bath for 1½ hour, by this time almost all of 
the water have evaporated, and a film forms on the surface of the solution.

2.2.1 Cleaning procedure 
 
In order to test and characterise the film, it has to be clean. The film is in contact with the solution 
which contains MES, glucose, unreduced metal precursors and nanoparticles. Because of the relative 
fragility of the film, mechanical cleaning was found unsuitable. The idea of chemical cleaning was 
also not realistic, because of potential risks of altering the sample structure. The decision was made 
to clean the film by dilution of the solution using ultra-pure water. A setup was made with a peristaltic 
pump, see Figure 3. First the solution was drained almost completely, then a flow of water into the 
solution was made by a second tube, at the same flowrate. After displacing the solution volume a 
couple of times, the draining tube is removed, and the beaker is filled with water to the brim. The 
flowrates of the pump are very low in order to minimize convection in the solution, and minimize 
risk of breaking the film.

Next, the small beaker is gently immersed in a big 2-3 L beaker of water without breaking the film. 
The film floats on the surface in the bigger beaker, and is ready for sampling. The sampling is 
explained in detail for each characterization method, but the general approach is to lift the film off 
the surface of solution by immersing the substrate into the solution, and lifting the film off the surface.

3.1 PRINCIPLE OF STM 
 
 
 
Figure 9, A) STM principle34, B) STM sample holder mounted with HOPG sample. 
 
Scanning tunneling microscope (STM) is an advanced microscopy technique designed to investigate 
samples on the atomic level. STM is based on the concept of quantum tunneling, and depicts the local 
density of state for any structure of electronically conductive samples. 
Quantum tunneling is a phenomenon based on quantum mechanics. When particles meet a potential 
barrier higher than their own potential, some of them can pass through the barrier. In relation to STM, 
the particle described is an electron, which is being driven through a circuit by a potential bias, in 
vacuum, air or liquid. 
STM requires that the sample is conductive. The current is measured from the sample to a conductive 
scanning probe, usually a sharp tip made of tungsten or a platinum-iridium alloy, the end of the tip 
has to be as sharp as possible, preferably the tip of the probe will be a single atom, this will in theory 
result in the highest resolution. The tip sharpness is usually achieved by either mechanical cutting, or 
electrochemical etching.35 
The current runs when a potential bias between the sample and the probe is applied. The distance 
between the sample and the tip is controlled by piezo elements, which can expand and contract with 
great precision under a potential, see figure 9 A) for a visualization of the setup. 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
STM is limited by the choice of samples, which have to be conductive. On the other hand, STM has 
an advantage regarding in situ measurements. STM can even be performed in aqueous solutions. STM 
also has the possibility to perform electrochemistry in-situ while imaging the sample, making it 
possible to study the reactions on a single molecule level.36 A STM can be operated in two different 
ways, either constant height or constant current mode. In constant height mode, the tip is scanned 
across the sample at a fixed height. As a result the current has to be controlled during the scanning, 
and the image attained is then a map of current changes. In constant current mode the voltage is set 
as constant and the tip is moved relative to the sample to keep this constant, the image attained this 
way is then a map of the tip height relative to the sample. Both current changes and height changes 
can be related to the charge density of the sample surface. 
Also the samples must be very flat and the surface must be positioned perpendicular to the tip, because 
the piezo elements have a limited operating range, in the z-direction.

separation. The tunneling current relies exponentially on the tip-sample separation.

A specialized application of STM is scanning tunneling spectroscopy (STS) where the local density 
of states at a specific position can be investigated by changing the voltage while measuring the 
current37. STS is not a scanning technique, but measured at a local position. 
 
3.2 SAMPLE PREPARATION OF AU-PT POROUS FILMS FOR STM 
 
For the STM characterization of the sample, it was chosen to immobilize it on highly oriented 
pyrolytic graphite (HOPG), HOPG is a synthetic graphitic material, and a single crystal form of 
graphite, with a minimal amount of impurities and defects in the structure. HOPG takes the form of 
a solid with stacked parallel layers of carbon atoms. A single layer of HOPG atoms, is also called 
graphene when it is separated from the other layers. Interatomic forces are much stronger within a 
plane of carbon atoms than with atoms from adjacent planes. To clean and prepare HOPG for 
experiments, ordinary Scotch tape can be used to peel off the top layers of HOPG, revealing a pristine 
surface underneath. 
In order to immobilize the AuPt-NPF, no modification of the HOPG was needed. When the film is 
transferred and dried, it has a good contact with the substrate for STM imaging. A clean HOPG wafer 
was submerged under the surface in the beaker with clean water, by holding it in a tweezer. By 
positioning the wafer underneath a piece of film and gently withdrawing the HOPG from the solution, 
the film can be lifted from the surface of the solution. It should be mentioned that HOPG is very 
hydrophobic, and it can be difficult to lift the film from the surface, by tilting the wafer and trapping 
the film alongside the wall of the container, this step becomes easier.

4.1 PRINCIPLE AND INSTRUMENTATION OF TEM 
 
 
Figure 14, Structure and components in TEM39 
 
Transmission electron microscopy (TEM), is a microscopy technique developed for imaging samples 
in the nanometer range, achieving close to atomic resolution. The working principle of TEM is based 
on electron transmission and scattering. The resolution of conventional visible light microscopy is 
restricted by the wavelength of light. The wavelength of electrons on the other hand is much smaller, 
enabling a much higher resolution for electron microscopies. 
TEM was invented by Max Knoll and Ernest Ruska, and the first TEM equipment was demonstrated 
in 1931. Later the equipment became commercially available. The invention of TEM was awarded 
the Nobel prize, in 1986.40 
TEM equipment is complicated and contains many parts, see Figure 14. The facilities are expensive, 
and the running costs are high. I briefly explain the components of the microscope starting from the 
electron source, following the electron path through the column, sample, and ending up at the imaging 
device. All parts of the TEM used to manipulate the electrons are contained within the electron 
column, which is a housing containing the electron gun, magnetic lenses, apertures and sample stage. 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
In addition, most of the probes applicable to TEM, have been integrated with the column, such as 
energy dispersive x-ray spectroscopy (EDX) and electron energy loss spectroscopy (EELS). The 
whole column is kept in high vacuum during imaging, to prevent the electrons from scattering within 
the column due to collisions with molecules in air. 
The electron source, which is also called an “electron gun”, uses a power supply of up to 200 kV, to 
release electrons from a filament, usually made of tungsten. Other materials can be used with different 
advantages, such as LaB641 and a field emission gun (FEG) made of a small tungsten crystal.42 The 
source in the TEM used for this project is a LaB6 source. The magnetic lenses employed in a TEM, 
are made of massive copper coils, which can generate a large and very precise magnetic field to focus 
the beam of electrons. Apertures are metallic plates with holes of varying diameters, inserted into the 
path of the electron beam. Specific electrons can be included or excluded. The electrons distance from 
the optic axis, by adjusting the apertures the beam may be focused, or used for other methods such as 
dark field microscopy. The sample stage, is the holder which contains the sample of interest. The 
stage can be tilted around its own axis, perpendicular to the electron beam, and the cavity surrounding 
the sample within the microscope is connected to a liquid nitrogen cold-trap, in order to eliminate any 
vapors. When the electron beam passes through the sample, it enters a chamber with a window, and 
a retractable screen of fluorescent material, which are used for quick visualizing of the sample and 
adjustments. When the images are required, the screen is retracted and the beam passes to a CCD 
camera, which records the electrons as an image. 
Transmission microscopy is only feasible because electrons pass through material by slipping 
between atoms, sometimes the electrons inevitably collide with atoms, resulting in a scattering of the 
electrons. The denser or thicker the material result in higher degree of scattering, and cause a higher 
contrast of the acquired images. Since TEM imaging is based on electrons transmitted through a 
sample, the images acquired is 2-dimensional representations of 3-dimensional objects. A real 3-
dimensinal image can be acquired on some TEM machines, equipped with a tilting sample stage, by 
acquiring a series of images at different rotational degrees, a single 3-dimensional image can be 
compiled, this is called electron tomography43. A TEM can do much more than simply visualizing 
the sample, the interaction of the electrons with the sample, makes the electrons carry a lot of different 
information about the sample. 
EDX spectroscopy is an element analysis technique, based on detecting characteristic x-rays which 
are created when the sample atoms are excited by the electron beam. This method is very useful, and 
gives information from localized areas, at the same time of the TEM imaging44,45. Dark field imaging 
shows the scattered electrons as opposed to ordinary bright field imaging. It is achieved by changing 
the aperture within the microscope, so as to block out transmitted electrons and allow scattered 
electrons to pass. It is a very useful method to investigate crystals and stacking defects within samples. 
It can also be used as a way to acquire electron diffraction patterns, for crystallographic purposes. 
EELS is a technique in which the energy levels of electrons scattered by the sample is investigated. 
The instrument is not a separate unit, but has to be incorporated within the TEM. The energy loss can 
tell many different things about the sample, only electrons which has passed through the sample and 
been deflected (inelastic scattering) are observed. EELS can be used to generate enhanced TEM 
images, by filtering off electrons that is incorporated in the image, based on lost energy, this is called 
energy filtered TEM46, and increases the resolution of acquired images. EELS is inherently a 
spectroscopic technique, and the energy loss spectra can be used to interpret sample parameters such 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
as specimen thickness, plasmon peaks, elemental composition, bonding states, oxidation states, and 
other more intricate information46. As previously mentioned TEM is commonly done where the 
sample is in a vacuum. This has been a severe limitation of the method as in situ studies are hard to 
perform. As early as 1935 attempts have been made, to develop a way of overcoming these 
limitations, by separating the sample from the vacuum47. Recently there has been an increased focus 
on trying to perform in-situ measurements on TEM, today this method is called ETEM or 
“environmental TEM”. Small cells are built to fit into the TEM, and are permeable by electrons. The 
cells are fitted with gas inlet and outlet system, in this way materials can be studied in-situ in many 
different conditions and environments. The temperature of the gas can also be controlled up to several 
hundred degrees, and small pressures can also be applied47. Scanning TEM (STEM) is a version of 
conventional TEM, in which the electron beam is focused to a very fine point and raster scanned 
across the sample, the real power of STEM becomes evident when it is coupled to spectroscopic 
instruments. In this way maps of EDX or EELS measurements can be created, and it can even be 
combined with sample tilting to get 3-dimensional elemental maps of samples48. 
 
4.2 SAMPLE PREPARATION FOR TEM  
 
For TEM investigation, the sample has to be transferred to a TEM grid. In this thesis, grids with pure 
carbon 300 µm copper mesh (TED PELLA inc.) were used. Similar to STM sample preparation, it is 
important to transfer the film without any alternations of the structure. The simplest approach is to 
lift the film off the surface of solution by immersing the grid into solution and withdrawing the grid 
from underneath the film. TEM grids with the target samples are then air-dried for 24 hours, in a dust 
free environment.

4.4 CONCLUSION 
 
Electron microscopy techniques provide many useful information, which makes it possible to 
evaluate the samples not only on structure, but also compositions. 
In the synthesis recipe, the molar ratio of platinum to gold was 0.8, this is a rather high ratio 
considering the attempted goal to create a film of core-shell or alloy nanoparticles. EDX 
measurements have provided concentrations of platinum and gold for each of the samples made, see 
Figure 23 for the relative ratios. It is clear that there are significant differences in the platinum content, 
of the different samples. Based on the color of the film floating of the surface, assumptions about the 
platinum content of the film were made. Because of previous experience with chemically synthesized 
nanoporous gold film, which have a striking golden color, it was assumed that Sample 1 which was 
golden, contained less platinum than Sample 2 which was black, platinum nanoparticles or Au@Pt 
core-shell nanoparticles are usually black. As can be seen in Figure XXX, Sample 2 does contain 
more platinum than Sample 1, although not nearly as much as could be expected. The platinum 
content of Sample 2 is only half of the 0.8 Pt-Au ratio in the synthesis solution. The EDX 
measurements of Sample 3 is deviating from each other, as described previously, but this is in 
accordance with the TEM observations of distinctively different nanostructures in the sample. The 
different measurements of Sample 3 match well with the measurements of Sample 1 and 2 
respectively.

5. ELECTROCHEMISTRY INVESTIGATION OF PT-AU POROUS FILM 
 
5.1 PRINCIPLE OF CYCLIC VOLTAMMETRY AND 3-ELECTRODE SYSTEM 
 
For electrochemical characterization, cyclic voltammetry has been used. In brief, the principle in 
cyclic voltammetry (CV) is to apply a switching potential to a system and observe the redox behavior 
as a function of the current. The potential of the electrode is usually cycled many times to achieve 
good statistics. The scan rate of the system usually has a great influence on the shape of the signal. 
The potential versus time function is a triangular potential waveform. 
The data acquired from CV experiments can give valuable information about he electrochemical 
system. Values such as surface coverage, diffusion coefficients, rate constants, specific surface area, 
specific capacitance etc. can be calculated. The system investigated in this thesis, is not an analyte in 
solution, but an immobilized film, which means that some of the usual calculations can not be applied. 
The system used to investigate the NPF is a common 3-electrode electrochemical cell setup, as shown 
in Figure 24.  This system consists of reference, counter and working electrodes, the cell is purged 
with argon and kept in a Faraday cage during measurements to eliminate oxygen interference and 
electric noise. 
 
Figure 24, Electrochemical 3-electrode cell used in experiments. 
The working electrode used is a ϕ 4mm disc of glassy carbon (GC) mounted in a Teflon cylinder, 
upon which the AuPt-NPF is loaded. The reference electrode is a saturated calomel electrode. 
And the counter electrode used is a coiled Pt wire. In preparation for all experiments the 
electrochemical cell and all other components of glass or teflon are thoroughly cleaned in a boiling 
15% HNO3 solution and washed thoroughly with ultra-pure water followed by 30 minutes of repeated 
sonication. The Pt counter electrode is cleaned in a hydrogen flame.  
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
The first step of preparing the working electrode is polishing. The polishing process starts off with a 
rough polish using wet grit 2000 and 4000 sandpaper, this is followed by polishing by hand on a wet 
polishing cloth using abrasive alumina particles. 3 different alumina grain sizes are used for polishing, 
1 µm, 0.3 µm and 0.05 µm, the electrodes are polished for 10 min. with each of the 3 grain sizes, by 
alternating clockwise and counterclockwise rotations on the polishing cloth, keeping the electrode 
perpendicular to the polishing surface, this is followed by triple sonication in ultra-pure water. 
The AuPt-NPF is then deposited on the GC electrode, by immersing the electrode in solution, and 
lifting the film off the surface with the electrode. After this step, the electrode is dried at 40 OC for ½ 
hour. The AuPt-NPF only has to cover the area of the GC electrode, not the teflon part. In order to 
remove the excess film, we carefully use a nitril glove to rub it away. See Figure 25 for a visual 
presentation of the sample and electrode. 
 
 
Figure 25, A) image of the AuPt-NPF prior to deposition on working electrode. B-C) images of the working electrode 
after film deposition, and after removal of excess film.

The presence of both exposed Au and Pt in the surface of the film has been confirmed by comparing 
the CV of our AuPt-NPF with CVs of each pure element, see Figure 26. I interpret the weak hydrogen 
adsorption and desorption peaks as a sign that only a smaller amount of Pt is present on the surface

the oxides of Au and Pt is stabilized by the new structure.

6. CONCLUSION AND PERSPECTIVES 
 
The scope of this project was to modify chemically synthesized nanoporous gold, into a bimetallic 
structure containing both gold and platinum. This has been achieved as a novel Au-Pt bimetallic 
nanoporous structure has been made using a simple chemical synthesis procedure. 
Separate types of structures within the film has been identified and characterized using TEM and 
SEM, they have been proven to have different elemental compositions by EDX, and the presence of 
both AU and PT has been proven. Electrochemical investigations using cyclic voltammetry have 
shown the presence of both Au and Pt electrochemical signals, proving that both Au and Pt is present 
in the surface layer of the structure. A formation mechanism for this chemically synthesized 
nanoporous structure has also been proposed. 
Other approaches to fabricating Au-Pt bimetallic nanoporous structures exist, most of them are based 
on electroless plating and electrochemical plating50 on a NPG structure formed by dealloying method. 
One example of such a structure is presented in the work by Yi Ding et al.50 and the structure has 
pores of approximately 15 nm, which is very comparable to the dimensions of our synthezied AuPT-
NPF. 
 
6.1 FUTURE WORKS 
 
During this project, a lot of effort was put into developing and characterizing the AuPt-NPF, but as 
the project has a limited scope and time-frame, many things which could have been improved or 
characterized were left for later study. Some ideas and missing parts are described here. The most 
notable thing is a general optimization of the synthesis itself. Time during the project was limited and 
many attempts at developing the synthesis were unsuccessful, therefore the final synthesis method 
which is presented, has not gone through a thorough procedure of optimization. Most notably only 
very few tests have been performed with different concentrations of metal precursors. 
Regarding the characterization of the film, there are still some main questions which are unanswered, 
the exact position of the different metals in the nanoparticles is unknown, the method to answer this 
question is element mapping in a TEM. It can be undeniably proven whether the particles are AuPt 
alloy nanoparticles, or core-shell nanoparticles. This would tell us what kind of surface is responsible 
for the catalytic effect of the material. The element mapping images would also provide important 
information helping to determine the exact formation mechanism of the film, and in turn provide 
knowledge of which parameters to change and improve in the synthesis. 
Improving the material can be done in many ways, one being the porous structure, another being the 
utilization of the platinum in the synthesis, the current EDX measurements performed has shown that 
the amount of platinum in the nanoparticles is far below what is expected. The first step to improving 
this would be to investigate where in the current synthesis the platinum is in excess. Both the film 
formed in the air-water interface, and the bottom of the synthesis vessel has been investigated, hence 
it is very likely that the platinum is present in the synthesis solution, which should be investigated. 
==PAGE:38==
38 
 
Following this investigation, effort should be put into determining how to minimize waste of Pt 
precursor. 
Some measurements previously made for determining the characteristics of synthesized NPG, should 
also be made. For example measurements of the film conductance, to evaluate the efficiency of 
electron transfer within the film. Also proper determination of the surface area and porosity of the 
film is needed. Furthermore the film has to be tested as electrocatalysts for oxidation of fuel molecules 
and for oxygen reduction reaction carried out in electrochemical systems.

Chemically Controlled Interfacial Nanoparticle 
Assembly into Nanoporous Gold Films for 
Electrochemical Applications  
Mikkel U.-B. Christiansen, Nedjeljko Seselj, Christian Engelbrekt, Michal Wagner, Frederick N. 
Stappen, Jingdong Zhang*  
Department of Chemistry, Technical University of Denmark, Kemitorvet 207, 2800, Kongens 
Lyngby, Denmark  
Keywords: Nanoporous gold, self-assembly, chemically controlled, CO oxidation, CO2 reduction, 
acid-induced.   
Abstract  
Nanoporous gold (NPG) is conventionally made by dealloying methods. We present an alternative 
approach for chemical synthesis of nanoporous gold films (cNPGFs). The method starts from gold 
salt through formation of gold nanoparticles (AuNPs) in 2-(N-morpholino) ethanesulfonate (MES), 
and ends with homogenous cNPGFs. MES plays a role acting as a reducing agent, AuNP capping 
agent and pH buffer. Replacing MES molecules around AuNPs by chloride ions make AuNPs 
hydrophobic and trigger them sinter into a long-range continuous gold networks and further assemble 
into uniform cNPGF at the liquid-air interface. Morphology and porosity of cNPGFs are controlled 
by experimental conditions such as temperature, chloride concentration, ionic strength and 
protonation of the buffer. cNPGFs up to 20 cm2 are achieved routinely and mapped by atomic force 
microscopy (AFM), transmission electron microscopy, quartz crystal microbalance (QCM), atomic 
adsorption spectroscopy (AAS) and electrochemistry. An average thickness and density of cNPGFs 
are reassured 500 ± 200 nm and 50-150 µg/cm2 respectively, suggesting 85-95% of the film is porous. 
In addition, cNPGFs show excellent electronic properties and catalyze both CO2 reduction and CO 
oxidation electrochemically.
