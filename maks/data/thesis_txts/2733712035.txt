Abstract
With water scarcity posing a significant threat to Morocco, and billions of US$ being invested in new water infras-
tructure within the country, the relevance of hydroeconomic optimisation modelling for decision-making processes is
increasingly apparent. Utilising the WHAT-IF decision support tool in combination with climate input data from DHI’s
Global Hydrological Model, this study establishes a national-scale single-objective hydroeconomic optimisation model
within the water-energy-food (WEF) nexus, with the goal of enhancing infrastructure selection and decision-making
within Moroccan water resource management. This is done with the intention of remedying the absence of a national-
scale hydroeconomic model adapted to the Moroccan domain. The model optimisation framework operates by finding
the solution which maximises aggregated net economic benefit generated from water allocation to different sectors
within the WEF.
Initially, a baseline scenario was formulated using simulated historical climate data and a representation of current wa-
ter infrastructure in Morocco. The remaining WEF sectors were represented using input from freely available global
datasets and incorporated into a simple workflow for model conceptualisation. The resulting water balance of the base-
line scenario was found to be largely in agreement with values from literature. The baseline scenario allowed for the
identification of key deficiencies in water availability on both spatial and temporal scales, which provided the rationale
for the selection of investments with the potential to alleviate said deficiencies.
Findings from the baseline scenarios indicated that spatio-temporal variation in water availability and water value are
key issues in Morocco. The North-Western region is abundant in water resources, while catchments further south expe-
rience extreme water scarcity, highlighting the importance of inter-basin transfer schemes for the spatial distribution of
water across catchment boundaries. The high temporal variability in water values is particularly apparent around agri-
culturally abundant regions, which emphasises the necessity of dams for increased temporal control of water allocation
to farmlands.
In light of these findings, the following infrastructure investments featured in Morocco’s national water strategy plan
(PNE, PNAEPI) were selected: (i) expansion and extension of the North-South Inter-Basin Transfer (NS-IBT), (ii)
Casablanca desalination plant, (iii) construction of M’dez Sefrou dam and (iv) capacity increase of Mohammed VI dam
for further analysis. Subsequent iterations of the model were run under different investment scenarios, the results from
which were compared by analysing relative changes in key indicators from the baseline scenario. The expansion of
the NS-IBT and capacity increase of the Mohammed VI dam proved to have the largest effect on net system benefit,
with annual increases of 2.1% and 3.4%, respectively, relative to the baseline scenario. Simulations also showed the
potential synergistic effects of multiple investments working in tandem, with a combination of the upgraded transfer
scheme, Casablanca desalination plant, and M’dez dam showing a ∼7% annual increase compared to their respective
singular investment scenarios, corresponding to US$ ∼5 million per year.
This study presents the capabilities for hydrologic prediction and optimal water allocation in Morocco and functions
as a prospective tool for investment selection. Future studies in the Moroccan domain should take steps to optimise
input data to allow for a more nuanced and accurate representation of the modelled water system and consider refining
modelling approaches to address limitations and mitigate bias. Overall, this study demonstrates the potential of using
simulated hydrological data in combination with hydroeconomic optimisation modelling to enhance decision-making
and investment planning across vast spatial extents, which lays the foundation for a workflow with global applicability.

Introduction
Water holds a distinct position among natural resources, serves as a cornerstone for the development of life and is
a fundamental resource of economic prosperity and food security (Alitane et al. 2021). The North African region
ranks as one of the most water-scarce in the world and is confronted with major challenges associated to water stress
(Nelson et al. 2010, World Economic Forum 2023). Growing demand driven by demographic growth and economic
development, combined with a 30% decline in annual average inflow from surface water from 1945-1978 compared
to 1979-2018 has pushed Morocco into a situation, where water scarcity is an urgent concern and action on water
resource management is required (World Bank 2022). In line with this, Morocco has since 2015 experienced annual
rain deficits, causing weak runoffs and a reduction in water supply to dams, leading to groundwater overdraft, which has
put an increased pressure on the already scarce water resource (International Trade Administration U.S. Department of
Commerce n.d.). This is further highlighted by the fact that the overall reservoir storage amid yet another severe drought
reached an unprecedented low 33% of the total storage capacity, with certain dams registering critical levels below 10%,
in 2022 (World Bank 2022). The increasing occurrence and severity of droughts, together with future climate change
projections, strongly suggest that Morocco is shifting towards even warmer and drier climate conditions, potentially
approaching the absolute water scarcity threshold of 500 m3 yr−1 per person (Taheripour et al. 2020). Assessments
based on varying climate scenarios formulated by both the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) and
United Nations Development Program (UNDP) expect an overall decline in rainfall by approximately 8-20% relative
to levels of 1979-1999 by 2050 in the most pessimistic projections (Cardarelli & Koranchelian 2023, McSweeney et al.
2009, Ouraich 2010, Taheripour et al. 2020).
1.1
Background and context
Similar to other countries in the Maghreb region, demands for drinking water and human sanitation are quite modest
in Morocco in comparison to other water uses. Irrigated agriculture, which is by far the largest water consumer in Mo-
rocco, represents approximately 80-85% of the total water withdrawal (The World Bank Group 2021). Despite growing
urbanism and the fact that the agricultural sector provides, on average, approximately 15% of the total gross domestic
product (GDP) of Morocco, the sector employs approximately 35% of the labor force (DEPA 2022, Taheripour et al.
2020). Hence, in addition to posing a threat to food security at national level, water scarcity constitutes a considerable
source of macroeconomic volatility and has the potential to impose significant constraints on the socioeconomic devel-
opment of the country (Verner et al. 2018, World Bank 2022). The risks associated with water stress have long been
recognised in Morocco. Traditionally, Morocco has responded to these risks by deployment of hydraulic infrastructure,
exemplified by increasing the total reservoir storage capacity more than ten-fold, from approximately 2 Bm3 to 19 Bm3
through construction of 126 dams in the period between 1960 and 2020 (Cardarelli & Koranchelian 2023). However,
these measures alone have proved inadequate as a robust and sustainable solution to address water challenges in the
long term. This is mainly due to the lack of available water resources and complications related to the spatio-temporal
climate variability in the country (Tahiri et al. 2022). The notion of climate variability in combination with water re-
source scarcity is not a recent phenomenon. Temporally, Moroccan climate is divided into two distinct seasons, with
86–92% of the annual rainfall falling in the wet season (October-April). In addition to seasonality, substantial year-
to-year fluctuations contribute to an irregular and unpredictable temporal rainfall pattern (Doukkali 2005). The spatial

climate variation is mainly caused by the Atlas mountain range, acting as a divider between the two main climatic zones,
the semi-tropical productive northern and western coastal regions and semi-arid to arid southern areas bordering the
Sahara Desert (Tahiri et al. 2022).
To address the challenges related to climate variability and the expected reduction in future water availability, a range
of additional strategies and initiatives to improve water infrastructure, enhance water use efficiency, and promote inte-
grated water resource management have recently been implemented in Morocco (Dahan 2017, Doukkali 2005, Othman
et al. 2022). A milestone in Moroccan water policy was achieved with the approval of the 1995 Water Law 10-95, the
purpose of which was to optimize water utilisation and mitigate regional disparities to guarantee universal access to
water throughout the country (World Bank 2018). More recent water policies include, the Integrated Water Resource
Management Master Plan (PDAIRE), an instrument for water planning at the river basin level of the ten water basin
agencies (ABHs) in Morocco, and the National Water Plan (PNE), an ambitious action plan to invest nearly US$ 40
billion in the water sector between 2020-2050. The PNE subset, the National Priority Program for Drinking Water and
Irrigation (PNAEPI), is set to accelerate these investments to mitigate climate impacts and guarantee water security
in the foreseeable future with a budget of approximately US$ 11 billion from 2020-2027 (DEPA 2022, Royaume du
Maroc 2009). Additionally, a development strategy, the Green Morocco Plan (PMV, was launched in 2008 aimed
at modernising the Moroccan agricultural sector to increase exports and competitiveness on the world market, while
accounting for sustainable rural development and water use (Kingdom of Morocoo 2008, Ouraich & Tyner 2018).
In the past few decades, hydroeconomic models (HEMs) have emerged as a helpful, analytical tool to provide insights
on water system dynamics and support management of water resources to find the optimal solution on a complex
decision problem (Bekchanov et al. 2017, Harou et al. 2009). Without such tools and analyses, decision-makers may
be left without a systematic and quantitative foundation to evaluate the costs and benefits associated with different
managerial options. Ultimately, this may lead to sub-optimal decision-making. These HEM tools either utilise the
economic value of water to drive water allocations and management or economically evaluate them to offer policy
insights and identify opportunities for more efficient water management (Harou et al. 2009). Moreover, they contain
a representation of the water system and use algorithms to back-calculate a set of decision variables to achieve the
best feasible outcome aligned with specifically defined model objectives (Kahil et al. 2018). Under decision-making
processes in water systems, including water infrastructure investment planning, competing demands across different
sectors must be considered. The importance of fully integrating the interconnections within the water–energy–food
(WEF) nexus in HEMs has become more recognised with time (Albrecht et al. 2018, Tahiri et al. 2022). Here, WEF
nexus is understood as a systems-based perspective, where each commodity is recognised as being interdependent and
interacting around available water resources. Essentially, HEMs, through the interdisciplinary WEF approach, aim to
evaluate and optimise the efficiency of water allocation mechanisms that mutually benefit water resource management,
considering cross-sectoral feedback mechanisms. Another advantage of HEMs is their ability to assess potential future
changes to water resource systems, including the introduction of new infrastructure or climate change impacts (Kahil
et al. 2018). Therefore, a HEM provides the means to spatio-temporally reveal the optimal water allocations within the
existing infrastructure in Morocco. In doing this, it accounts for the complexities introduced by climate variability and
also allows for a feasibility assessment of the planned investments outlined in the PNAEPI.

1.2
State of the art
Applications of hydroeconomic modelling in literature are numerous and cover various different water resource prob-
lems, both on evaluation of water allocation efficiency (Booker et al. 2005, Cai et al. 2003, Kahil et al. 2015) and
infrastructure investment analysis (Acquah & Ward 2019, Gohar et al. 2013). More specifically, previous applications
involve studies on; the conjunctive use of groundwater and surface water to reveal the potential of groundwater bank-
ing (Harou & Lund 2008, Pulido-Velazquez et al. 2004), in-stream and off-stream intersectoral allocation and use, e.g.
operation of a multipurpose, cascade multireservoir system in the Euphrates basin (Tilmant et al. 2008), as well as
estimations of the direct and indirect economic costs of floods (Jonkman et al. 2008). However, with the exception of
a few national and global scale HEMs (Draper et al. 2003, Cai & Rosegrant 2002, Kahil et al. 2018), a common trait of
typical hydroeconomic modelling studies is that they rarely span across large spatial scales (Hossen et al. 2021). This
is also valid for previously conducted work of similar character with HEMs in Morocco, which include hydroeconomic
analyses on the relationship between irrigation water and agricultural production in the Middle Draa valley (Heidecke
et al. 2010), impacts of climate change-induced drought on water uses in the Ait Ben Yacoub region (East Morocco)
(Elouadi et al. 2020), investment planning in relation to flood risk management of the Sebou River (Benchbani et al.
2021), improving climate resilience of the Loukkos basin (Benchbani et al. 2022), and understanding the interactions
between water supply and demand to successfully manage water resources in the Souss-Massa basin (Guemouria et al.
2023). Consequently, a national-scale HEM adapted to the Moroccan domain is lacking. Given Morocco’s high vulner-
ability to climate variability and change, coupled with the country’s extensive investment portfolio, the need for such a
model is evident.
1.3
Purpose
Considering the water resource challenges in Morocco, this study aims to develop a national-scale hydroeconomic op-
timisation model (HOM) within the WEF nexus to support decision-making in Moroccan water resource management.
This is achieved by (i) regionally predicting water availability, (ii) revealing optimal water allocation mechanisms and
(iii) evaluating feasibility of new water infrastructure investments. The water situation in Morocco calls for method-
ologies that can support the design of sustainable water management, which aligns perfectly with what hydroeconomic
modelling can offer. Hence, the study seeks to fill the gap of the currently missing national-scale HEM in Morocco.
Due to the highly diverse climatic conditions and uneven distribution of available water resources in the country, the
larger spatial scale of this model, in comparison to previous HEMs, aims to enhance the understanding of feedback
processes and dynamics of the entire Moroccan water system. In terms of water infrastructure planning, the developed
national-scale model may be considered particularly helpful for Morocco, since it can provide new perspectives on
system bottlenecks and consequently identify where new infrastructure would be most beneficial, ensuring that invest-
ments are made in a synergistic manner. In this way, the model can assist in realising the potential of water resources
and support prioritisation of water infrastructure investments in Morocco. The study seeks to provide answers to the
following research objectives:
■Identify deficiencies in water availability and areas with potential for improvement under existing water infras-
tructure.
■Based on these deficiencies, select five investment projects with the potential to alleviate water stress in the

problem areas for further investigation.
■Provide economic feasibility review of selected planned infrastructure investments in the water sector
The main focal point of this study is expanding the current knowledge in the field of water resource management in
Morocco. The purpose is to demonstrate the prospects of the methodological approach rather than provide accurate,
ready-to-use recommendations for operational use and investment planning. Despite this, results obtained from the
developed model offer insights into potential managerial strategies and could be implemented into a broader framework
for integrated water resource management. Hence, this study intends to establish the groundwork for future research on
national-scale hydrological modelling in Morocco. In line with this, the study’s use of climate data from the DHI-GHM
seeks to demonstrate the capability of simulated hydrology for use in large, data-scarce areas as input for hydroeconomic
optimisation and showcase the potential of building a global hydroeconomic model in the future.
This paper is structured as follows. Firstly, in Section 2 the principles of hydroeconomic modelling and details on the
general framework and economic optimisation of the developed model are presented. In addition, conceptualisation of
the water system, parameterisation of model inputs, the representation of each sector of WEF nexus, and the selected
investment opportunities are evaluated. Subsequently, in Section 3.1, the model output results under existing water
infrastructure, i.e. a baseline scenario, are examined and discussed. This includes spatio-temporal insights on water
availability, agricultural crop production, reservoir fill percentage, as well as marginal values of water and capacity
constraints. Section 3.2 answers the last research objective, as outcomes from an investment analysis based on cost-
benefit principles are shown. Finally, limitations and improvement opportunities of the methodological approach, as
well as future perspectives, are discussed.

Methods and data
The following section will first present the fundamental principles of hydroeconomic modelling and provide details on
the specific hydroeconomic model used in this study. Furthermore, the conceptualisation of the Moroccan water system
and the methodological approaches applied to catchment delineation are described. Finally, details on the representation
of the various modules within the WEF nexus and related processes in the model and input data used to populate these
will be introduced.
2.1
Hydroeconomic modelling
The management of water resource systems involves regulating and finding optimal solutions on a highly intricate de-
cision problem with resource challenges amongst multiple interconnected subsystems, namely the water-energy-food
(WEF) nexus (Fig.1). Hydroeconomic models (HEMs) couple the fields of hydrology and economics to provide a
solution-oriented tool aimed at supporting decision-making for such water resource management systems by revealing
economic trade-offs and benefit-sharing between competing water users within the WEF nexus. The aim of hydroeco-
nomic analysis is to offer guidance on intertemporal water allocation in real-world operational systems (Bauer-Gottwein
et al. 2017). Fundamentally, these HEMs represent a complex water system by a simplified conceptual model that out-
lines the connectivity of the river flow network and includes a representation of the main spatially distributed hydrologic
features, i.e. reservoir storage compartments and water-generating activities such as desalination plants, water demand
(i.e. domestic, industrial, agricultural) and economic water valuation. Using these, HEMs attempt to characterise the
spatially distributed water availability and demand within a water system (Kahil et al. 2018).

Hydroeconomic analysis and modelling can be subdivided into two fundamentally different approaches, namely the
simulation approach and the optimisation approach. The first is rule-based and seeks to predict real-world system be-
haviour. It typically evaluates how the system performs under one or multiple alternative scenarios, so-called ’what-if’
scenarios (Harou et al. 2009). An example of such an alternative may be testing water infrastructure system performance
under different reservoir operation policies, i.e. how the system performs if a reservoir is operated in a particular way.
Hydroeconomic simulation models are thus used to examine very specific conditions and quantitatively explore the
effects of a particular water resource management strategy. In contrast to simulation-based models, Hydroeconomic
optimisation model (HOM)s help identify the optimal solution among numerous alternatives, by answering ’what is
best’ in a broad sense (Harou et al. 2009). HOMs find the optimal solution with respect to a defined objective function.
With an increasing number of alternatives, simulation modelling is quickly surpassed by the algorithms of optimisa-
tion models regarding computational processing requirements and time consumption, in that numerous combinations
of alternatives can become unfeasible to compute by a simulation model. Exploring vast complex solution spaces for
potentially promising combinations of solutions thus necessitates the use of optimisation modelling (Heinz et al. 2007).
The integration of economics in HOM, ensures that each managerial decision has either an economic benefit or cost.
generally implies that the common model objective of HOM is to allocate water to maximize the aggregated net eco-
nomic value, or alternatively to minimize the total cost of water allocation and water supply curtailment cost. Hence,
in HOM the water allocation is driven and or assessed by the economic value that it generates (Draper et al. 2003).
In this study, the open-source hydroeconomic optimisation model (HOM) Water, Hydropower, Agriculture Tool for
Investment and Financing (WHAT-IF) is adapted to the Moroccan domain to answer the research objectives listed in
Section 1. Similar studies on a national scale, including transboundary river basin networks such as the Zambezi River
basin, have previously applied the WHAT-IF model to reveal optimal water allocation and act as a decision support tool
for water infrastructure investment planning (Payet-Burin et al. 2019). The WHAT-IF model uses linear programming
(LP) as its mathematical backbone for optimisation, which requires that both the objective function and the constraints
that the function is subject to are linear. The WHAT-IF connects the WEF nexus in a holistic, economic optimisation
framework. It has an intuitive modular formulation in that each subsystem (e.g. water, agriculture) of the nexus is
represented by individual modules, which can be modified, activated, or deactivated separately. This means that the
interactions between subsystems are internally represented in a single model, and not coupled by multiple single-system
models as is the case for compartmental models, ensuring that the economic principles act as the core component of
the optimisation framework (Payet-Burin et al. 2019). The advantage of having all model aspects embedded in one
consistent model, is that it allows for great flexibility to optimize on large-scale water systems, at the expense of
simplifying the representation of certain aspects of the water system, e.g. the surface-groundwater interactions (Heinz
et al. 2007). An illustrative representation of the main features of each subsystem in the model and their relative
interactions are shown in Figure 2. More details on how the individual components in the figure are parameterised and
how the objective function applied in the model is formulated are elaborated on in Section 2.1.1.

To summarize, the WHAT-IF model identifies the best combination of water supply and demand management options
within a consistent framework. This means that the model can both simulate optimised water allocation mechanisms
under existing water infrastructure and adaptations to new infrastructure investments. In line with the research objec-
tives in Section 1, the model is therefore very useful to quantitatively evaluate the economic impact and feasibility of
the planned investments in Moroccan water infrastructure. The investment assessment is performed by a welfare cost-
benefit analysis (CBA) to systematically evaluate the economic efficiency of the proposed investments by comparing
the project costs and incremental benefits attributed to the particular investment project. The foundation of the CBA
lies within welfare economics given that the economic optimisation framework of the model represents the costs, bene-
fits, and surpluses of all individuals within the economy. This builds on the concept that incremental benefits result in
economic growth, subsequently benefiting every individual within the economy (Griffin 1998). Note that in terms of
CBA, an investment is essentially considered desirable if the benefits exceed the associated costs.
Within this context, the CBA employs a with-and-without analysis. This analysis entails evaluating the relative dif-
ferences between two scenarios: first, a ”business as usual” model representing the optimal allocations for the current
water system (referred to as ”baseline scenario”), and second, model scenarios that explore the implementation of one
or a combination of multiple investments in the existing water infrastructure. The evaluation centres on incremental
changes in system performance, including benefits and costs across the scenarios. In this way, the analysis provides a
structured framework for decision-making, since the economic feasibility of each planned investment can be compared.
One significant advantage of employing a HOM for investment analysis in the Moroccan water resources system lies
in its convenient ability to go beyond evaluating individual investment projects. Instead, it enables the determination

of the optimal set of investments, considering both synergistic and antagonistic interactions among various individual
investments. Hence, by this approach, it is possible to evaluate a comprehensive ensemble of investment projects in a
convenient manner, while still offering well-informed insights to facilitate the decision-making process.
Listed below are additional characteristics and assumptions of the WHAT-IF model setup, which have not previously
been addressed in preceding paragraphs, but remain critical for understanding the structure and limitations of the model.
Note that the list is not exhaustive, but more detail on the model setup will gradually be introduced in the following
sections.
■The model operates at basic hydrological timescale at monthly time steps to justify the assumption of instanta-
neous water balance throughout the entire water system. This relatively short time interval is chosen to suitably
reflect seasonal changes throughout the system and a typical timeframe of managerial decisions.
■The spatial extent of the model is on a national-scale and covers most of the Moroccan domain, including the
Spanish mainland exclaves (Melilla, Ceuta etc.), but excluding the disputed territory of Western Sahara. See
Section 2.2 for more details.
■Runoff generation and groundwater recharge are assumed decoupled from water uses. Hence, changes in water
use have no effect on either runoff or groundwater recharge.
■The model provides insights on sustainable water resource management, well aware of the fact that groundwater
resources are presently overexploited (DEPA 2022).
■Water quality is not considered. Consequently, all sources of available water can be allocated to serve all water
use purposes in the system.
■The model is based on perfect foresight formulation. Consequently, optimal decisions are found with full knowl-
edge of hydrological variations, economic information and other parameters throughout the entire modelled time
series.
■Water allocation follows the principles of an ideal water market situation, in which transaction cost and risks are
neglected, and allocation is therefore only restricted to physical, environmental, and demand constraints.
2.1.1
Objective function
In hydroeconomic modelling, the objective function serves as what is to be considered the most crucial component,
since it defines the overarching goal of the model (Harou et al. 2009). Optimization on management of water resource
systems quickly becomes an intricate task given that managerial objectives are typically plentiful and contradicting.
The contradiction in objectives stems from the competition between users, manifested both geographically and in terms
of water utilization. For instance, there may be a conflict between priorities, such as hydropower production and
irrigation agriculture, highlighting the inherent challenge in reconciling diverse interests (Kahil et al. 2019). However,
as previously described in Section 2, the hydroeconomic approach involves assigning either a benefit or cost to each
decision within the modelled system, allowing the managerial objectives to be combined into a single commensurate
monetised objective function for optimisation purposes. Hence, monetisation translates a complex multi-objective

In practical terms, the optimisation model makes decisions on variables (called decision variables) influencing the indi-
vidual components of the objective function for each spatially discretised unit and time steps defined within the model,
with the ultimate goal of maximizing the objective function. These decision variables represent typical water system
operations, including reservoir releases, groundwater pumping among others. Hence, the main outputs of the economic
optimisation are the optimal decisions in terms of decision variables defined for the WEF commodities (Payet-Burin
et al. 2019), which are summarized in Table 1. However, as with any other LP problem, there exists a corresponding
dual problem to the primal problem (expressed in the objective function) in which the shadow prices of the constraints
(also called dual values) are the decision variables. The shadow price of a particular constraint is per definition the
change in objective function value per unit increase in the right-hand side of that constraint. In other words, the shadow
prices quantify the marginal value of relaxing a constraint on the optimal objective function value, in this case total
economic value generated from optimal water allocation (Pulido-Velázquez et al. 2006). This implies that the shadow
price of binding constraints in the optimal solution are non-zero, precisely reflecting their marginal value. Conversely,
non-binding constraints consistently carry a shadow price of zero. The shadow price outputs are therefore considered
equally as important as the optimal decisions, in that they provide valuable insights on capacity constraints and are used
to assess resource availability, as well as identify bottlenecks in the water system (Harou et al. 2009). In addition, other

2.2
Conceptual model
Conceptualisation of the water resource system is a key task in HOMs since it defines the spatial discretisation and
complexity of the system representation. Moreover, it dictates the spatial resolution of the model output. The choice
of discretisation should thus reflect the intended model application, while simultaneously setting the prerequisites for
the spatial resolution of the input data. In this study, the water resource system is represented by a lumped node-link
network architecture, in which nodes (in this case catchments) are linked by a connectivity matrix. The catchment
delineation was derived through a step-wise methodological process, in which the listed steps below were performed
to delineate the final catchments applied in the model. The following paragraphs elaborate on the details of each step.
1. Digital Elevation Model (DEM) hydroprocessing and catchment delineation on selected river outlets (pour points).
2. Delineation of catchments associated with a reservoir, satisfying Degree of Regulation (DOR) > 50%.
3. Supplementation of defined catchments from HydroSHEDS database in coastal areas.
The first step of the catchment delineation process was to divide the study area into catchments that could accurately
represent the climatic and demand variation across Morocco, without making the system representation exceedingly
complex. This was accomplished through traditional DEM hydroprocessing, utilizing the pysheds library—a fast, open-
source tool designed for hydrological analysis (Bartos 2020). Catchment delineation was performed in combination
with a DEM from the USGS Hydrosheds V1 project, with a spatial resolution of 15 arc-seconds (approximately 0.5 km)
(HydroSHEDS 2023). The DEM was conditioned by filling pits, depressions, and resolving flats. Using the D8 routing
scheme, which routes each cell to the neighbouring cell with steepest descent, a flow direction grid and subsequently
a flow accumulation grid were computed (see Appendix A). By this, we obtained a delineation of the river network,
which was used to delineate catchments of user-selected pour points, i.e. cells with high flow accumulation values, by
finding all the cells draining to a particular pour point.

2.3.1.1
Climate and runoff data
Runoff, evaporation, and precipitation were obtained from the Danish Hydraulic Institute - Global Hydrological Model
(DHI-GHM). The DHI-GHM uses a gridded rainfall-runoff model with a spatial resolution of 0.1° (approximately 10
km) and a kinematic routing model in combination with ERA5 precipitation and Priestley-Taylor potential evaporation.
From this, the model is able to provide simulations of climatic data for historical time periods, real-time, 10-day short-
term forecasts, and 9-month seasonal forecasts (Murray et al. 2023). In the context of this study, the DHI-GHM data of
interest was historical datasets of precipitation, evaporation, and runoff, for tiles 196, 207, and 208, which collectively
encompass the study area of Morocco. A 25-year time series was used for the baseline model, from January 1997 to
December 2022. Using the geometries of the delineated catchments as a mask layer, a subset of each climate variable
could be extracted for each catchment and aggregated to monthly time-steps. Runoff is then input to the WHAT-IF
model as Mm3 month−1, precipitation and evaporation in mm month−1. It is important to emphasize that the exact
timestamps of the climate and runoff data are of secondary importance, as the primary focus is on observing the water
system’s response to variations in water availability. In line with this, the HOM was also run in cyclic mode, meaning
that the model operates in a continuous, repeating loop, where the last time step serves as input for the first time step of
the subsequent cycle. Consequently, there is no need to specify initial conditions, which particularly applies to storage
in reservoirs. The input climate data is visualised on a spatial and temporal scale in Figure 6 and 7, respectively.

2.3.1.3
Reservoirs
Reservoir scheduling stands out as one of the most crucial operational problems in water resource management. This is
because it addresses multiple objectives, including the fulfilment of water demand, irrigation, hydropower production,
flood control, and the preservation of recreational value. Given that reservoirs provide the opportunity to store water
for future use, allocation problems in river systems typically entail navigating inter-temporal trade-offs. This is because
reservoir operation and decisions on water allocation have a lasting impact on future water availability. In line with this,
reservoirs play a crucial role in smoothing out temporal fluctuations in water availability and aligning water availability
with water demand variations in time, since these may be out of phase (Harou et al. 2009). A common example involves
the contrast between the crop calendar and rainfall seasonality. For instance, crop irrigation demand is typically highest
during summer, while the wet period dominates in winter and late autumn.
The selection of dams included in the modelled system is based on registered dams in the GRanD that fulfil the DOR
> 0.5 criteria, as explained in Section 2.2. This criterion ensures that only reservoirs with a sufficient capacity to store
water far beyond the model’s monthly time step are part of the conceptualised water system. Further, the outflow from
reservoirs is not represented as being proportional to storage volume and a monthly constant reservoir evaporation rate
is assumed due to the absence of measured data and relationships between reservoir surface area and volume. The
evaporation rate for each reservoir is derived from the monthly averaged evaporation rate in the associated catchment
obtained from the DHI-GHM data (1997-2022), as shown in Figure 6b. The most reliable available data on representa-
tive reservoir surface area is sourced from GRanD, based on the classification scheme defined in Beames et al. (2019).
However, despite applying data on representative surface area, rather than maximum surface area, the assumption on
time-constant evaporation rate may likely still lead to an overestimation of the evaporation in periods with low reservoir
storage levels. On the other hand, the local open water evaporation rate from reservoirs is believed to be higher than

2.3.1.4
Inter-basin transfer schemes
Water transfer channels provide an additional option for linkage between catchments besides the upstream-downstream
relations determined by the natural river flow direction. The purpose of IBT schemes is to improve the connectivity of
the natural water system, enabling the transfer of water from regions with surplus availability to those facing shortages.
Hence, transfer schemes intend to even out spatial heterogeneities in a water resource system. The detail at which
IBT links are implemented in HOMs again depends on the spatial scale of the modelled system. To properly align
with the catchment discretization of the Moroccan domain in this study, only a single IBT scheme is included in the
conceptualisation of the water system, namely the North-South Inter-Basin Transfer (NS-IBT) or also called Water
Highway, linking catchment Sebou to Bou Regreg and further to Al Massira, as seen in Figure 5. The NS-IBT scheme
has a current annual capacity of 392 Mm3 y−1 according to Nabil et al. (2020), which is uniformly distributed to the
monthly time step in the model (32.7 Mm3 month−1). In addition, Nabil et al. (2020) suggests that an increase of the
transfer capacity to 860 Mm3 y−1 is possible. Further, a time-constant transfer loss rate of 10% is assumed.

Incorporating the trade-off between water allocation for human use and water use for ecosystems is crucial in hydroe-

incorporation of benefits generated from ecosystems and ecological value into HOMs is difficult, and a well-defined

2.3.1.6
Groundwater aquifers
Groundwater and surface water are often interconnected systems, and effective water resource management requires an
integrated approach. Groundwater management is thus a crucial component of water resource management, involving
the sustainable use, protection, and regulation of groundwater resources (Bauer-Gottwein et al. 2017). The character-
istics of groundwater (storage, wide spatial distribution) give greater flexibility to integrated water management, as
groundwater resources can compensate for the temporal variability of rainfall runoff, similar to the impoundment of
surface water. In this model, the groundwater reservoirs are decoupled from the runoff-generating system and are
represented in the form of additional available water sources, where the abstraction capacity is constrained by a time-
constant groundwater recharge. Annual recharge is obtained on a river basin level from Royaume du Maroc (2009).
The groundwater recharge is distributed amongst the discretised catchments within each basin in proportion to their
relative size. For instance, Moulouya Ouest makes up 35% of the total area of the Moulouya basin and, therefore, was
allocated 35% of the available groundwater, corresponding to approximately 15 Mm3 month−1.
The spatial distribution of groundwater resources is seen in Figure 10. This is obviously a significant simplification,
as in real-world systems there would be high spatial variability in groundwater recharge within a river basin. In line
with this, the model lacks representation of groundwater storage capacity, meaning that only the annual recharge is
constraining abstraction, while overall groundwater storage levels are disregarded. This means there is no incentive for
the model to curb groundwater abstraction in order to allow aquifers to refill to a more sustainable level. Ideally, the
model should include the flexibility to vary abstraction based on storage, especially given that groundwater overdraft is
already a major issue in Morocco (Ahmed et al. 2021). While one could argue that the groundwater recharge constraint
may not accurately reflect the current state of overexploitation of groundwater in Morocco, the constraint is not deemed
excessively restrictive. This is due to the fact that well-field drawdown will most likely be constraining before the used
recharge constraint, which covers an entire groundwater aquifer. Further, the pumping cost of groundwater (푐gw) is
assumed constant in time and space and is estimated to 0.05 $ m−3 based on data from Alam (2016) and Hellegers et al.
(2007). Hence, total groundwater cost (GWC) is given by Eq. (13).

Agricultural module
The supply of water for irrigation depends largely on decisions made by farmers regarding, crop mix and timing, water
application, and irrigation technology. This study attempts to model the interactions between water resource manage-
ment and agricultural systems by combining input values for farming zones, crop types, yields, growth phases, crop
prices, and more to provide optimal solutions on crop choice, agricultural water allocation and cultivated area. Crops
were selected amongst the highest production volume crops in Morocco based on data from the FAO FAOSTAT database
(FAO n.d.). For the purpose of this study, it was decided only to include irrigated crops in the model. The reason for
this being that rainfed crops do not affect the overall water balance and since crop markets are not taken into consider-
ation, they unnecessarily increase the complexity of the model. It should be noted that irrigated crops only contribute
to 20-25% of total crop production, although in terms of monetary value, they constitute 65% of the total produced
crop value in Morocco (Taheripour et al. 2020). Further, an important factor in irrigation agriculture relates to the fact
that water allocation spans the entire growing season, with harvest and associated economic benefits realized only at
its conclusion. Consequently, these delayed yields contribute to inter-temporal trade-offs between water allocation to
crops, water release and storage in reservoirs and time delays in network routing (Bauer-Gottwein et al. 2017).
Land capacities of farming zones are computed on a catchment basis using data on area equipped for irrigation (AEI)
from FAOSTAT (Fig.11). Given that crop markets are kept exogenous in this particular application of the WHAT-IF,
an added constraint was required to ensure that the model diversified its crop selection and did not exclusively produce
the most high-valuable crops (in this case, fruits and vegetables). Through this approach, we attempt to integrate
overall tendencies of real-world farming practices and decisions made on crop mix, which are influenced by dynamic
crop selling prices and risk management considerations among others, into the model. This was achieved by way of
introducing a maximum culture area constraint for each crop. The maximum cultivated area for each crop was obtained
from FAOSTAT on a national scale and subsequently distributed amongst the catchments in accordance with each
catchment’s AEI. A multiplier of 1.3 was then applied to every crop cultivation area to allow the model some added
flexibility in crop choices (Tab.5). For instance, the Sebou catchment constitutes 18% of the nation’s AEI, meaning that
it is assigned 18% · 1.3 = 23.4% of the max culture area for each crop. Hence, a uniform restriction on crop cultivation
area is applied to all catchments in the system.

2.3.4
Investment selection and analysis
As outlined in the research objectives, a prerequisite to the selection of an investment portfolio for the investment anal-
ysis was to first identify key areas with water stress under existing infrastructure that, we believe, have the potential
to be alleviated by new, already proposed, infrastructure projects. The final selected investments and formulated in-
vestment scenarios will therefore first be presented in Section 3.1.5, subsequent to the sections elaborating on the main
findings of the baseline model results. The metrics used to identify water shortages and system bottlenecks are namely
shadow prices of the model constraints, but also the interplay between different decision variables. As described in
2.1.1, shadow prices of this model refer to the marginal economic value of an incremental change of a model constraint.
Hence, the water balance shadow price, often referred to as water value, tells us when and where in the modelled system
we would economically benefit most from an increase in available water. The shadow price of individual capacity con-
straints, e.g. a specific desalination plant, can highlight where added infrastructure capacity would be most beneficial
for the overall system performance, according to the defined objective function.
The investment analysis is performed by a with-without analysis and applies principles of CBA, as described in Section
2.1. The analysis centres on evaluating the relative differences between the baseline scenario and each investment
scenario, which thereby also allows for a comparison between the investments and their feasibility. The climate data
used to populate the model first and foremost serves to capture variations in water availability, rendering the specific
timestamps irrelevant to the interpretation of model outputs. Hence, the with-without analysis for each investment
scenario is conducted as though the investments defined for a particular scenario are implemented in addition to the
existing water infrastructure throughout the entire temporal domain of the model. This implies that details on investment
planning such as timing of investments and investment sequencing are not part of the analysis. Any investment budget
constraints are therefore not taken into account. Consequently, discounting, i.e. adjusting future benefits and costs to
their equivalent present value in accordance with the concept that a dollar spent today is worth more than a dollar spent
in the future, is not included in the investment analysis. This is because it as such irrelevant when the costs and benefits
occur in time in the context of the conducted analysis on relative changes between scenarios, and since the model is
Regional Hydrologic Prediction and Optimal Water Allocation in Morocco

run for a so-called arbitrary temporal domain. In essence, the investment analysis offers insights into the performance
of the system and the alterations in managerial decisions resulting from the implementation of various infrastructure
investments. This comparison is made against the baseline, considering the given climate and runoff data that populate
the model.

Water availability and water value
Water availability, defined as the sum of local runoff and inflow from upstream catchments, varies highly both in
time and space across Morocco, as displayed in Figure 12 and later seen in 14, respectively. As expected from the
spatial variability in runoff (Fig.6c), the North-western regions (Centrale and particularly Nord-Ouest) have a signifi-

the percentage difference exceeding 4000%, in certain areas. Figure 12a also highlights the influence of connectiv-

upstream-downstream connection. Ultimately, this characteristic makes these regions more vulnerable to dry periods,
as their dependence solely on local runoff is not alleviated by the capability to meet water demands with resources from
upstream catchments. This is exemplified in the Sud region, where the water availability of the connected catchments
along the Draa river exceeds that of the remaining isolated catchments, namely Chbika, Guelmin and Noun.

either irrigation, groundwater abstraction, or desalination production, the water value thus always remains zero, even if
water resources are completely absent. Hence, although water value serves as a suitable indicator for water scarcity, it
is essential to acknowledge that it depends not only on water availability, but also water demand, as is further discussed
in relation to Figure 17. The high water values observed in Chbika, Zelmou, Oujda and Zelmou are due to domestic

The fact that Oum er Rbia has a surprisingly low water value according to the model output might be explained by the
simplifications made relating to the water system representation and catchment delineation. This applies mainly to the

basin-scale transfer links are missing. In addition, catchments are primarily delineated based on DEM hydroprocessing
(Sec.2.2), which does not necessarily imply that runoff generation within a catchment precisely corresponds to the area
to which the water is supplied to users. The considerable discrepancies in water availability and water demand of the
two neighbouring catchments, Oum Er Rbia and Safi, are examples of the model’s limited representation of water-

the sensitivity related to system conceptualisation. Oum Er Rbia is connected to a large upstream network with three

catchment isolated from the remaining water system. Meanwhile, Safi has both higher user water demand (Fig.9) and
higher agricultural water demand, in that the near-boundary AEI primarily lays within Safi instead of Oum Er Rbia

model’s economic optimization framework. Although this variation might be valid and reflect the current real-world
state, the magnitude is believed to be too significant and caused by model limitations. However, in line with this, the
water value of Oum er Rbia reveals the obvious advantages of IBT schemes to homogenise spatial variations in water
availability, which is further elaborated in the following paragraphs and Figure 13.
Figure 12b also shows the typical trend seen in HOMs, where water value gradually increases when moving upstream
in the system. This is because upstream water resources have the potential to serve multiple purposes and have more
flexibility for efficient allocation both in time and space to optimize the economic benefit that the upstream water
may generate. This especially applies to catchments associated with hydropower plants, given that the availability of
additional water can generate economic benefit, both through hydropower production and agricultural crop production
in downstream catchments. As an example, this trend can be seen in the Centrale region.

Groundwater and desalination
As expected, we observe an increase in groundwater abstraction and desalination production during the period from June
to October with lowest water availability (Fig.23,24). The spatial distribution of groundwater shadow prices (Fig.23)
is quite consistent with the map of overall water value in the study area (Fig.12b), suggesting that water resources are
largely constrained by available groundwater. This is due to the low pumping cost assigned to groundwater, making it
the preferred beneficiary of water to the system. The additional benefit generated by an extra cubic meter groundwater
will almost always out-value the associated abstraction cost. Given that groundwater resources in Morocco already
are in serious decline (Ahmed et al. 2021, Hssaisoune et al. 2020), it would be inadvisable to increase the amount of
groundwater available for abstraction in the model, despite the fact that our model currently uses less than real-world
data would suggest (Tab.7). There is a lot of uncertainty surrounding the true exploitation of groundwater in Morocco,
compounded by the prevalence of illegal drillings which have greatly accelerated the depletion of certain aquifers (Food
and Agriculture Organization 2023). Considering these factors, it would be advantageous to concentrate investment
efforts on infrastructure with the potential to increase surface water resources and thereby alleviate the stress placed on
groundwater aquifers.

Summary and investment selection
The previous sections have highlighted that spatio-temporal variability in water shortages could be a cause for concern
in the Moroccan water resource system. More importantly, the developed HOM has specifically pinpointed locations
and times where these shortages are most pronounced. This is for example seen from the high spatial variation in water
value (Fig.12b) suggesting that there is an abundance of water resources in some regions and major scarcities in others.
In line with this, the outputs of the baseline model scenario have provided identifications on system bottlenecks and
where infrastructure investment would potentially be most beneficial. Table 8 summarises key indicators of the baseline
scenario, which clarify the spatial variations across the different regions in Morocco for not only water availability, but
also agricultural productivity and reservoir storage capacity among others. Note that the key indicators are in principal
outcomes of the decision variables deducted from the model’s single objective optimisation formulation, and should
not be misunderstood as a multi-objective optimisation problem. Another finding from the baseline scenario was that
shadow prices of the annual EFR were zero throughout the entire time period, indicating that the requirements were
satisfied each year as the constraint was non-binding.

Scenario A: From the water shadow prices in the western regions (Fig.25), we observe an increase moving from
north to south and additionally, the IBT capacity is constraining the transfer outflow on a annual basis (Fig.13).
Together, this advocates for the proposed capacity expansion of the existing NS-IBT from Sebou to Bou Regreg
to Al Massira, namely from the baseline transfer capacity of 392 Mm3 yr−1 to 860 Mm3 yr−1 (Nabil et al. 2020).
Hence, the change between this investment scenario and the baseline is simply an increased capacity of the NS-
IBT scheme.
Scenario B: In continuation of Scenario A, this scenario extends the upgraded NS-IBT to the Tensift catchment (Nabil
et al. 2020). We assume that the Tensift extension is coupled to the capacity expansion of the IBT. The Tensift
catchment shows both a relatively high water value (Fig.12) and low crop productivity relative to its AEI as
observed in Figure 19. This investment scenario thus seeks to examine the system’s response to introducing
added connectivity between catchments by an extension of the NS-IBT with the expectation that the investment
alleviates the water stress in Tensift, ultimately leading to improved crop production in the area.
Scenario C: The Bou Regreg catchment is by far the most populous in the study area, accounting for almost a quarter
of the total domestic and industrial demand in the system. This is largely due to the fact that it encompasses
the major cities, Casablanca and Rabat (Fig.9). The planned desalination plant in Casablanca, with an expected
capacity of 300 Mm3 yr−1, will be the largest of its kind in Africa and supply vital drinking and irrigation water
to the area (Magoum, Inès 2023, Agenzia Nova 2023, Redondo, Raúl 2023). By including this into the modelled
investment scenario, the intention is to reduce the Bou Regreg catchment’s dependency on the NS-IBT from
Sebou, allowing more water to be transferred to the water-stressed catchments further south.
Scenario D: In close proximity to the NS-IBT is the proposed M’dez dam in the Sefrou area upstream the Sebou
catchment (Fig.25) with a reservoir capacity of 700 Mm3. In this scenario, we are interested in exploring the
interactions between introducing added reservoir capacity in the Nord-Ouest region and the transfer outflow of
the NS-IBT, ultimately to investigate whether the water stress in the catchments to the south may potentially be
alleviated by the dam investment.
Scenario X: In the Nord-Est region of the country, we observe high temporal variations in water shadow price (Fig.14).
In addition, the water values fluctuate homogeneously across the connected catchments along the Moulouya River
(Fig.16), as the highest potential for agricultural production is found furthest downstream, namely in Moulouya
Nord-Est. This investment scenario will therefore evaluate the impacts of a capacity expansion of the Mohammed
VI dam at Taurirt to 980 Mm3, downstream Moulouya Nord-Ouest, on water allocation and capability to mitigate
these temporal variations by ensuring that more water resources are available during dry periods (Taouil 2023).
Note that Mohammed VI is an existing dam, but is not represented in the baseline model in that its current capacity
of 730 Mm3 (Lehner et al. 2011) does not satisfy the DOR criterion (Sec.2.2).
Scenario BCD: This scenario combines Scenario B, C and D to reveal whether they exhibit any synergistic or an-
tagonistic interactions. This scenario will, offer insights into whether one should advocate for the simultaneous
implementation of investments or recommend, within the framework of this model, that it is more economically
justifiable to invest in just one of the projects or pursue a different combination of investment projects.

beneficial. As described in Section 2.3.4, the investment analysis focuses on quantification and understanding of the
incremental benefits or changes brought about by the different investments outlined in Section 3.1.5. This analysis
elaborates on the model results of a selected subset of the total modeled scenario runs, specifically six scenarios. Five of
these scenarios involve individual investments being included in the model. Meanwhile, one scenario, named Scenario
BCD, combines the NS-IBT capacity increase and extension to Tensift with the construction of the M’dez dam and the

into the system comes with desalination costs. Additionally, the allocation is predominantly restricted to satisfying
demands in Bou Regreg, given that the capacity of the NS-IBT constrains the water transfer to Al Massira, particularly
during the economically productive seasons, as illustrated in the baseline results (Fig.13). Hence, the model results
suggest that the desalination plant in Casablanca has a seemingly low return on investment (ROI) and extremely long
payback period, even without discounting the net benefits. On the other hand, the results of Scenario C and Scenario
BCD show that the Casablanca plant capacity is constraining only during summer periods (Appendix C), also showing
high inter-annual variability throughout the simulated time period.

overestimation of the system performance, and is also believed to be partly influenced by the simplified nature of the
water system conceptualisation. This aligns with the notion that model scenarios introducing new water infrastructure
in areas without any prior representation in the model generally result in larger incremental changes to the baseline.
In contrast, scenarios investigating investments in areas where other infrastructure is already in place through capacity
expansions tend to yield comparatively smaller changes. Hence, the fact that no dams or other water infrastructure are
included in the the baseline system representation in the Moulouya region leads to an exaggerated cost-benefit ratio in
Scenario X. The fact that the Mohammed VI dam is already in place, but at a lower reservoir capacity as described in
Section 3.1.5, further substantiates that the suggested feasibility of the investment in Scenario X is somewhat biased by
the baseline system representation. Ultimately, this showcases the sensitivity of the HOM outputs to the choices made
on system conceptualisation in the context of investment analysis.

3.3
Limitations
3.3.1
Conceptualisation choices
A study of this nature will always be limited by the spatial and temporal resolution of its input data. Given the limited
time constraint placed upon this study, decisions regarding such elements as the size of delineated catchments needed
to strike a balance between complexity and simplicity. While increasing the spatial resolution of the model would allow
it to capture more detail, there are limitations on data availability, computational power, and model complexity in terms
of result interpretation. In certain cases, given the granularity of input data such as the AEI, it would not make sense to
delineate even smaller catchments. There can also be issues with scale mismatch if the size of the modelled catchments
does not reflect the scale at which decisions are made, limiting the model’s ability to provide useful recommendations.
In the end, the size of the delineated catchments represents a compromise between these competing factors. Many of
the same considerations apply to the representation of groundwater resources and omission of endogenous crop and
energy markets in the model.
Water user representation is another area with the potential for refinement given additional time and data sources. The
WTP for water users in the system was greatly exaggerated to ensure that all user demands were met before any other
sector demands. A finer user representation could have discerned between rural and urban users, and various types of
industrial sectors, further enhancing model performance by allowing for a more nuanced approach to user allocation
and WTP. This could also include the addition of a dynamic WTP for users based on supply-demand mechanisms. The
same could be applied to the agricultural sector, by incorporating rainfed crops into the model as well as irrigated.
The availability and quality of input data also represents a significant challenge for this study. While much care and
consideration was taken to obtain data from trusted, reputable sources, there will always be instances where extrapola-
tions are required due to missing data, or the validity of a source comes into question. Especially information regarding
infrastructure investments was often found to be conflicting regarding important factors such as cost, scale, and time-
line of investments. Ideally, the input climate data from the DHI-GHM would be validated against additional measured
datasets for all climate parameters in order to properly assess its accuracy and dependability. Another limitation of

56
==PAGE:65==
using the DHI-GHM in this context is that its kinematic routing model already accounts for water use and irrigation
based on withdrawal data from Huang et al. (2018), with water withdrawn for these purposes being stored at catchment
outlets (Murray et al. 2023). This essentially means that our model may potentially be double-counting some user and
agricultural use, however, looking at the water balance from the baseline model (Tab.7) and comparing DHI-GHM
runoff to measured runoff (Fig.8b), this does not appear to be reflected in the available runoff. Our baseline model
scenario even overestimates the amount of runoff in the system available for users compared to data from other studies.
Therefore, it was evaluated that no bias correction of the DHI-GHM was required. The incorporation of water use into
the DHI-GHM also means that we are not able to simulate a completely ”natural” flow regime for our EFR calculations,
as flows are already inextricably linked to human activities.
The primary limitation in our approach to EFRs is that they are placed exclusively at a limited number of major river
outlets within the study area. Furthermore, the flow requirements are determined on an annual basis, meaning that
they do not account for the timing of flow events (Bauer-Gottwein 2023). The method also relies on stationary data to
predict future climate patterns and does not consider climate change scenarios in calculating EFR.
The application of the Smakthtin method for determining environmental flows (Smakhtin et al. 2004) comes with the
inherent disadvantage that it simply reflects a constant fraction of the natural flow regime (Korsgaard 2007). This sim-
plified approach might fail to capture the complexity of local hydrological conditions and ecosystem dynamics. It also
does not take into account the spatial variability in flow requirements across river basins, making it largely dependent
on the spatial resolution of the catchment delineation. The environmental flow requirement approach used also does not
consider ecological diversity between ecosystems. Depending on geography and climate, the ecological requirements
of various river systems and the organisms living within them can vary greatly and the system may therefore not meet
species-specific requirements.
3.3.2
Result interpretation
The benefit-based approach used to evaluate system performance within this study is limited in that it does not consider
feedback loops between the agricultural and energy sectors. For example, the value of crop production remains fixed
and does not consider external trans-system boundary market effects, which would allow it to endogenously adjust
production values. It should also be noted, that agricultural production is dependent only on the availability of water
for irrigation. In reality, many factors influence the production of crops, such as soil characteristics, fertilisation and
nutrients, and pest and disease management. For these reasons, the financial benefits presented by the model output
must be interpreted with a degree of circumspection.
The model’s reliance on perfect foresight can also lead to an overestimation of system performance, as it allows it
to predict future trends, which cannot necessarily be replicated in real-life management scenarios (Payet-Burin et al.
2019). This could potentially bias the predicted benefit associated with an investment project. An alternative to the
perfect foresight approach could be Model Predictive Control (MPC), which has been shown to model more realistic
reservoir operation compared to perfect foresight (Payet-Burin et al. 2021). MPC serves as an alternative approach
that mimics potential operations. It achieves this by iteratively solving optimal management decisions based on the
available foresight, such as forecasts, at each time step (Payet-Burin et al. 2021). By doing this, MPC addresses the
Regional Hydrologic Prediction and Optimal Water Allocation in Morocco
57
==PAGE:66==
limitations of perfect foresight, at the cost of increased computational power. Payet-Burin et al. (2021) did however,
find the impact of perfect foresight to be < 2% on total system benefits, meaning that its effect is likely overshadowed
by larger uncertainties present in other facets of our model.
3.3.3
Investment feasibility
For the purpose of this study, the infrastructure investment projects modelled are all assumed to be feasible across
various facets, including legal compliance, ecosystem services, and social considerations, considering their integration
into Morocco’s PNAEPI. In reality, this model lacks the complexity to accurately represent a number of the effects that
new infrastructure could have on the WEF nexus. In its current state, the model is unable to consider the timing and
sequencing of investments. It simply reflects how the system performs with or without a given investment scenario
over a historical hydrologic time period and is unable to ”build” an investment at the optimal time, since investment
costs are neither included in the objective function or defined in terms of a investment budget constraints. If long-term
climate forecasts were to be applied along with investment pricing and discounting, there would be potential to increase
the model’s applicability for present-day and future investment planning.
While reservoirs can be an extremely important tool for reducing temporal fluctuations in water availability, they also
come with significant societal and environmental impacts, which have been the cause of great criticism for many dam
projects. Dams have been shown to decrease biodiversity in river systems and greatly restrict fish migration to spawning
grounds upstream (Tahmiscioğlu et al. 2007). Many of the same considerations apply to inter-basin transfer schemes,
as these require significant alteration of the natural state of river systems. Siltation of dams is also a major issue, with an
estimated 75 Mm3 of sediment being trapped in dams every year in Morocco alone, drastically reducing the country’s
reservoir storage capacity (Royaume du Maroc 2009). Furthermore, the construction of new dams is capable of creating
reservoirs with large surface areas, that can cause displacement of both inhabitants and nature in the surrounding area.
The study also does not consider the flood-safety benefits provided by reservoirs or their maintenance costs.
The quality and intended use of desalinated water is not considered. Some desalination processes are intended for
industrial use only and do not meet drinking water standards (El-Ghzizel et al. 2021). Desalinated water in our system
is simply added to the overall water availability, meaning that it can be used by any water user. This leads us to an overall
limitation of our model, being its exclusive focus on water quality and neglecting the critical dimension of water quality.
A prime example of this is the potential applications of groundwater resources, a large proportion of which we know
from literature to be abstracted from small, local wells by farmers and used directly for irrigation (Food and Agriculture
Organization 2023). This water is most likely not potable, however the model assumes it to be applicable to any type
of user in the system. Future enhancements to the model could incorporate water quality considerations, providing a
more holistic and accurate representation of the complex interplay between water quantity and quality in effective water
resource management.
3.3.4
Multi-objective optimisation
A way to factor in conflicting objectives and stakeholder interests involved in investment planning is through a multi-
objective optimisation approach. This allows for the consideration of a multitude of stakeholders and instead of a
single monetary benefit objective, as used in this study, the multi-objective approach can consider economic benefit,
environmental sustainability, and social equity (Bauer-Gottwein 2023). This can be achieved in two ways: Either
Regional Hydrologic Prediction and Optimal Water Allocation in Morocco
58
==PAGE:67==
by the valuation followed by optimisation approach, which first evaluates or assigns weights to multiple objectives,
then proceeds to single-objective optimisation, or by the optimisation followed by valuation approach, which initiates
multi-objective optimisation without a predefined valuation of the objectives. The results from the multi-objective
optimisation are then passed to decision makers as a set of Pareto-optimal solutions (Bauer-Gottwein 2023). While
the optimisation followed by valuation approach is theoretically preferred, it often comes with increased computational
costs. Having multiple solutions available allows policy makers to apply a more holistic approach to decision-making,
but also involves subjective choices and requires a higher level of information (Bauer-Gottwein et al. 2017). An example
of where this could be applied is on the decision to expand the NS-IBT. While our model shows that transporting water
from a water-abundant region to a water-scarce region will provide the most economic benefit, it fails to consider some
of the detriments discussed above, such as the effect on ecosystems and people reliant on the water. By applying the
multi-objective approach, such stakeholders can be given weight in the decision-making process and be involved in a
more holistic manner.

4
Conclusion and outlook
In the conducted study, we presented a national-scale hydroeconomic optimization model (HOM) within the water-
energy-food nexus (WEF) adapted to the Moroccan domain to support decision-making processes in the management of
water resources. The model was used to spatio-temporally predict water availability and water deficits within a baseline
model scenario, reflecting the existing water infrastructure in Morocco, which formed the basis for selecting investments
for subsequent investment analysis. This analysis was based on a comparative assessment between investment scenarios
(including combinations of five different infrastructure investments) and the baseline, also applying basic principles of
cost-benefit analysis (CBA) to suggest the economic feasibility in the context of the model optimization framework.
The primary method of result interpretation involved evaluating relative changes between model scenarios in terms of
outputs on decision variables and dual values of model constraints, demonstrating the following results:
■The major trends of the resulting water balance in the baseline model align with values reported in the literature,
with the most notable disparity observed in groundwater abstraction.
■The lack of connectivity and uneven distribution of naturally available water resources causes high spatial vari-
ation in water values, namely observing water surpluses in the Nord-Ouest region and substantial shortages in
the Ouest region, suggesting critical water stress. With the exception of occasional curtailments of domestic and
industrial users in a few catchments, we generally find relatively low water values east of the Atlas Mountains,
primarily to the south due to comparatively low total water demand in this region.
■The seasonal climatic patterns and inter-annual variability in water availability are observed to cause temporal
variations in water value in most regions of the study area. This especially applies to areas with inadequate water
supply to meet agricultural demands, leading to peaks in water values during summer, where crop irrigation
demand coincides with reduced runoff and precipitation levels. Along the Moulouya River, this trend is also
apparent due to insufficient reservoir capacity.
■The baseline model proved to be a robust and valuable tool for identifying problem areas and system bottle-
necks in time and space, thereby facilitating an initial prioritization of water infrastructure investments within a
comprehensive investment portfolio.
■Of the evaluated investments, the proposed capacity expansion of the North-South Inter-Basin Transfer (NS-IBT)
from Sebou, in tandem with the construction of the M’dez dam and Mohammed VI dam capacity increase show
the highest increase in yearly averaged net benefit, with a relative change of ∼3% compared to the baseline.
However, the suggested benefit of the Mohammed VI dam is considered somewhat overestimated and biased by
the water system conceptualization of the developed model.
■Synergistic interactions are mainly observed between the inter-basin transfer and M’dez dam investments, yield-
ing a ∼7% relative increase in the net benefit change compared to baseline when these investments are made
collectively (3.2%) rather than individually (3.0%).
■In the context of the model’s optimization framework and water system representation, the economic feasibility

60
==PAGE:69==
of the planned desalination plant near Casablanca appears questionable. This is evident when considering the
sensitivity to desalination production cost, revealing an underwhelming return on investment and a payback
period exceeding 100 years.
■The potential benefit of transferring surpluses of water from Sebou to the more water-scarce southern areas is
underscored by the fact that the outflow from both Sebou-Bou Regreg and Bou Regreg-Al Massira is constrained
by the IBT capacity, even with the invested capacity increase. This is evident in the relatively high shadow prices
for the transfer capacity constraint.
Based on the above findings, it is consolidated that this model underscores the potential advantages associated with
employing a national-scale HOM. It enables the analysis of infrastructure investments on a large spatial scale and
provides insights into typically overlooked system dynamics and feedback mechanisms in water resource management
across water basins. Furthermore, the model shows the possibility of combining the global DHI-GHM product and
hydroeconomic modelling. Hence, we have established a relatively simple workflow to develop a national-scale HOM
that is applicable globally.
Future work should involve refining data input and system conceptualisation to enhance the representation of the real-
world system, as reflected in the baseline model scenario. This effort aims to ensure that the model output can potentially
serve as the foundation for a more tangible decision-making framework. This could be addressed by a sensitivity
analysis of model input parameters or by representing their uncertainty via Monte Carlo simulation. In line with this,
projections on future climate and socio-economic development and refinement of ecosystem valuation could be areas of
investigation to expand the assessment of the baseline system performance and feasibility of investments. The practical
applicability of the investment analysis could further be enhanced by integrating mixed-integer programming (MIP)
and discounting into the HOM to enable the model to determine the optimal timing and selection of investments. The
HOM may also be redefined to a multi-objective optimization problem, e.g. to include objectives on social equity and
environmental sustainability, and thereby allow for mapping of the trade-offs between multiple objectives and provide
entire Pareto fronts to support decision-making. Implementation of model predictive control (MPC) in the modelling
framework could also be a subject for future investigation to cancel the bias of the perfect foresight approach used in
the model. Populating the model with short-term climate forecasts provided by the DHI-GHM could further provide
insights into the model’s utility in a more operational context to support optimal water resource management in the
short term.
