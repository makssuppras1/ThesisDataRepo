Abstract
In the search for improving the activity of the Hydrogen Evolution Reaction (HER), this study introduces
nickel and molybdenum alloy catalysts. These catalysts were produced using a simple electroplating
technique to create high­performance electrodes. Machine learning was utilized to optimize the HER
overpotential at ­10mA/cm2 (η10) by adjusting the synthesis parameters. It was discovered that the elec­
troplated catalyst layer was mechanically unstable. As a result, two optimization investigations were
conducted: one aimed at optimizing performance only, and the other at optimizing both performance and
mechanical stability of the electroplated catalyst. The electrode optimized for performance only exhib­
ited an η10 of ­59mV vs. the Hydrogen Reference Electrode (RHE) potential. When optimizing for both
performance and mechanical stability, the optimal electrode demonstrated an η10 of ­63mV vs. RHE. The
overall best­performing electrode was found to have an η10 of ­36mV vs. RHE. The machine learning
model used for optimizing solely for performance was found to be inadequate in predicting overpotentials
with synthesis parameters. However, the model used for optimizing both performance and mechanical
stability was found to be able to guide the laboratory experiments, leading to stable and good­performing
electrodes. The thesis also found that large porous­like structures, along with a significant amount of
molybdenum in the catalyst layer, were the main contributors to good performance. Furthermore, it was
established that the Electrochemically Active Surface Area (ECSA) is linearly correlated to the overpo­
tential on a semi­logarithmic scale.

Introduction
As the global population continues to grow and prosper, the demand for energy increases at a rapid pace.
Traditionally, this demand has been met through the combustion of fossil fuels such as coal, oil, and gas.
These fuels, while cheap and readily available, contribute significantly to the accumulation of greenhouse
gases in the atmosphere, leading to global temperatures rising. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate
Change (IPCC) warns that if greenhouse gas emissions are not significantly reduced within this decade,
we could face a 3°C temperature increase by the year 2100, leading to severe consequences such as
extreme weather events, climate­induced refugees, and food scarcity [1].
In response to the climate this crisis, significant investments have been made in renewable energy sources
like wind turbines, photovoltaics, and hydroelectricity. These technologies have significantly lowered
electricity prices in Europe, reduced grid emissions, and led to the closure or transition of fossil fuel
power plants. However, due to the intermittent nature of renewables, utility providers are forced to use
fossil fuels to maintain a consistent supply of electricity and hot water.
One way to store renewable power is the production of green hydrogen gas (H2) via electrolysis of water.
Alkaline electrolysis, being the most affordable and widely available electrolysis technology, is partic­
ularly suitable for large­scale green energy transition due to its scalability. However, as alkaline elec­
trolyzers have relatively low efficiency compared to other electrolysis technologies, the low efficiency
proposes an area of improvement [2]. One particular aspect to improve is the electrodes and catalysts used
in electrolyzers, in this study, the catalyst will be improved by electroplating nickel (Ni) and molybdenum
(Mo) on a perforated nickel plate as support.
Traditionally, the development of catalysts for alkaline water electrolysis has relied on empirical labora­
tory experimentation. This approach is time­consuming, resource­intensive, and explores only a limited
set of electrode synthesis parameters. As it has been demonstrated by other research projects [3], com­
bining laboratory research with machine learning­guided experiments can yield promising results. In
context of this study, a machine learning model has been trained and optimized with respect to two sepa­
rate sets of data. One set of data was used to optimize for pure performance, and another to optimize for
mechanical stability and performance. In total, 44 samples were made, of which 6 samples were control
samples with identical synthesis parameters. This approach not only accelerates the process of finding
the best parameters but also makes it less expensive and more efficient for developing new and better
materials.

Theory
Among other electrolysis technologies, alkaline water electrolysis (AWE) is the most mature, cheap, and
readily available technology [2]. However, AWE is also the least efficient, which is why it was chosen as a
topic for further investigation in this study. The following section will provide the theoretical background
for alkaline water electrolysis.
2.1
Alkaline Electrolysis
Alkaline water electrolysis employs the basic principles of electrochemistry­ and catalysis to produce
gaseous hydrogen (H2), and oxygen (O2) from water (H2O) in an alkaline solution usually consisting of
potassium hydroxide (KOH) or sodium hydroxide (NaOH) [4, 5]. Through the application of an electrical
current, it is possible to split water into gaseous hydrogen and oxygen.
An alkaline electrolysis cell consists of four main components: an anode, a cathode, an electrolyte, and
a separator (or diaphragm), see Figure 2.1. Reduction and oxidation occur at the cathode and anode,
producing H2 and O2 respectively. The electrolyte acts as an ionic conductor, enabling current to pass
through the cell. The separator is placed in between the two electrodes to separate the product gases
while ensuring that the electrolyte can flow freely between the two electrodes thus maintaining ionic
conductivity [5]. In Equations 2.1 ­ 2.3 the two half­cell reactions and the overall reaction can be seen.

2.3
Electrochemical Losses During Operation
In any electrolyzer, operating at Urev/Uth is nearly impossible due to various losses of the system. The
overpotential (η) is defined as the difference between the actual potential measured and the theoretical
potential required for an electrochemical reaction to occur [6]. In the case of electrolysis, the theoretical
minimum potential to drive the reaction is 1.23V versus the hydrogen electrode potential, as described
in Section 2.2. The standard hydrogen electrode potential is by convention 0V, thus the overpotential
for hydrogen evolution reaction will be the measured potential minus voltage spent on various losses.

Resistive Overpotential
The resistive overpotential refers to voltage losses in the electrodes, resistance from ions moving in the

other losses in the electrical components [6].

2.4
Catalyst for Alkaline Water Electrolysis
In order to lower the overpotential described in the previous section, a catalyst can be used to lower the
overpotential and increase the reaction rate. In this section, a brief introduction to catalysis with a specific
focus on the catalysis of water splitting will be given.
In general, a catalyst is a material that facilitates a reaction without being consumed and in doing so,
increases the reaction rate and lowers the activation energy [8]. An illustration of how a catalyst lowers
the activation energy barrier can be seen in Figure 2.3.

A good catalyst is defined by its ability to lower the activation energy of the reaction, having a high
selectivity toward the desired product, and often having a large surface area to increase the number of
active sites for the reaction. In terms of catalysis for HER through electrolysis, it is also important that
the electrode has a high electrical conductivity [8].
2.4.1
Catalyst Reaction (Elementary Steps)
The reaction pathway for HER can be divided into two steps the Volmer and Heyrovsky steps. In the
Volmer step, the initial and rate­determining stage, water is electrochemically split and adsorption of a
hydrogen atom on a free active site occurs.
H2O + e−+∗−→H∗+ OH−
(Volmer Step)
Where * denotes a free active site on the catalyst. This is followed by either the electrochemical Hey­
rovsky step where the adsorbed hydrogen atoms react with electrons to release molecular hydrogen or
the chemical Tafel step [10].
H2O + e−+ H∗+∗−→H2 + OH−+∗
(Heyrovsky Step)
2H∗−→H2 + 2∗
(Tafel Step)
In general, it has been reported that at high overpotentials the Tafel step is negligible, while at low over­
potentials the Tafel and Heyrovsky steps happen in parallel [10].

Electroplating
In this study, to lower the overpotential a catalyst consisting of Ni and Mo was introduced by the method
of electroplating. In principle, electroplating is a way to deposit and reduce electronically conductive
species onto a cathode from an aqueous solution by supplying energy. As it can be seen in Figure 2.4, the
basic components of an electroplating cell are illustrated. The cathode, or working electrode, is where
the desired metal is deposited, leading to the reduction of metal species on its surface. The counter
electrodes can either be made from a consumable or inert material. In this study, the counter electrodes
were made from nickel, which means nickel was consumed from the counter electrodes and dissolved into
the electroplating solution. In an ideal system, the same amount of nickel consumed from the counter
electrodes is electroplated onto the working electrode, thus keeping the nickel concentration stable in
the electroplating solution. The electroplating solution is an aqueous solution containing the metals one
wishes to electroplate. The solution can also contain possible complexing agents, and other substances
to control the pH of the solution [7].
The surrounding environment has a great impact on the electroplating process and therefore also the
structure of the electroplated material. A study made by Alexander W. Black et. al. [11] investigated
the effect of synthesis temperature on morphology and crystallinity. This study concludes that increasing
temperature increases the electron transfer rate and mass transport during electroplating, thus influencing
the structure of the deposited material. Another study made by Sajjad Sadeghi et. al. [12] found that
increasing current density generally increased the thickness of the deposited material. Investigating the
reasons behind the influence of the synthesis parameters on the characteristics of the deposited layer is
out of scope of this study.

AI­Machine Learning
As the electroplating process is complex, optimizing the overpotential by changing the synthesis param­
eters one by one would require a significant amount of work in the laboratory. Fitting a machine learning
model onto carefully selected data could provide a faster way to optimize the overpotential compared to
regular linear investigations. While traditional machine learning approaches often require large datasets

to effectively capture complex patterns, Gaussian Processes (GP) provide a suitable method for utilizing
machine learning on small datasets. In this study, the amount of data generated for the machine­learning
algorithm was relatively small (30 data points from 30 samples). In this context, GP is an insightful and
powerful tool for understanding and predicting the complex relationships within the synthesis process.
The reason GPs were chosen as the method for regression is due to their ability to capture complex, non­
linear relationships in data. Making it possible to predict sets of synthesis parameters that are likely to
offer good overpotentials. Furthermore, as GPs rely heavily on probabilities, this means the regression
incorporates uncertainties into its predictions, which is a valuable feature when limited experimental data
is available [14].
2.6.1
The Statistical Model (Gaussian Process)
In traditional regression, a single function is fitted to describe the data best. The Gaussian process ap­
proaches the regression problem in a slightly different manner, as it fits a probability distribution over
multiple functions that best describe the data. Therefore, it is possible to describe the mean and variance
of all the fitted functions within the distribution. This is an important attribute as it allows for a quan­
tification of the uncertainty of a predicted point. Figure 2.5 provides an example of how the GP works,
showing three random functions from a generic GP fit. Here, the black lines represent three random func­
tions in the GP fit and the gray shaded area represents the uncertainty of the prediction. It can be seen
that the uncertainty is large in areas where no points are present, and vice versa where data is available.

shaded area represents the uncertainty of the mean prediction. Illustration taken from [14].

should pass through the data points. If the distribution of functions is required to pass through all data

smoothness, a prior (or kernel) should be determined.
In machine learning, a prior is a probability distribution that represents an underlying understanding of

data point is removed from the dataset, and an entirely new model is fitted on the remaining data. This

By plotting the actual and the predicted values against each other, a good fit will have data points on

RMSE has the same units as the target value (in this study, the overpotential) and it shows the average
difference between the actual and the predicted overpotential at any given point.
In general, a low RMSE is desired. In the context of this study, a reduction in the RMSE after the 2nd
round of machine learning during the optimization loop described in Section 2.7.3 is considered a success.
Throughout this study, an RMSE below 30mV is assumed to be sufficient. This particular threshold was
chosen because of inherent uncertainties discussed in Section 5.4. Since the GP model was used to guide
the experimental work, the absolute error between any data point and the prediction was not the main
focus in terms of utilizing the model.
2.7
Optimization Technique
Once a machine learning model has been fitted and validated, the subsequent step involves optimizing
the function to identify synthesis parameters for high­performing electrodes or to discover new synthesis
parameters for experimental testing in the lab. This section will describe how Bayesian Optimization
(BO), can be used to optimize functions that are expensive to evaluate.
2.7.1

Bayesian optimization is a sequential optimization technique that aims to find the global optimum of a
black­box objective function that is costly to evaluate [17]. In the context of this study, the black­box
objective function is the GP fit explained in Section 2.6.1, as can be seen in Equation 2.15.

The Bayesian optimizer then iteratively selects sets of parameters and evaluates the acquisition function.
In the context of this study, the aim is to minimize the overpotential. The optimizer tries out sets of
parameters automatically, evaluates the parameter set that maximizes this acquisition function and repeats
until a sufficiently high­performing solution is found [19]. The inherent drawback of this method is that
there is no way of knowing if the optimizer found a global or a local extremum. Therefore, the optimizer
will retry the method n times to reduce the risk of the extremum being a local one [18].
However, as this study is trying to utilize machine learning on a limited amount of data, it becomes
apparent that it’s important to have data points uniformly distributed in the parameter space to ensure that
all the parameters’ influence is captured by the data. Thus, General­purpose Information­Based Bayesian
Optimisation (GIBBON) is utilized to suggest multiple points that, if provided to the algorithm, will not
only reduce the uncertainty of the fit but are also likely to have a good target value.
2.7.2
General­purpose Information­Based Bayesian Optimisation
GIBBON is a novel acquisition function for Bayesian optimization. GIBBON is based on the concept
of max­value entropy search [19]. This approach aims to reduce the uncertainty in the maximum value
of the objective function by querying points which would not only reduce uncertainty but also gain the
most amount of information. Thus, by supplying the queried points to the model, will, in theory, reduce
the uncertainty and fit a better model. This is different from other methods that focus on the maximum
target value of the expensive­to­evaluate function, as explained in Section 2.7.1, without considering the
uncertainty at that point.
The advantage of utilizing the GIBBON acquisition function is its ability to query multiple informative
points at a time. This is particularly useful in the context of this study as the generation of data for the
machine learning algorithm is done by hand in the laboratory. Thus by using the GIBBON acquisition
function, multiple sets can be generated and synthesized in one laboratory session.
2.7.3
Optimization Loop
As the synthesis process is conducted manually in the laboratory, the optimization is done iteratively,
which can be seen in the flow chart in Figure 2.7. The initial investigation consists of synthesizing several
electrodes with synthesis parameters uniformly distributed in the parameter space to properly describe
the intricate relationships between the synthesis parameters. This is followed by an analysis step in which
the electrodes are characterized and the HER overpotential is determined. The GP is then fitted to the
data using the RBF kernel and is validated using leave­one­out cross­validation. From here, the RMSE
is evaluated to be either sufficient or not. If not, GIBBON is used to suggest several new candidates
to be synthesized in the lab. These are then synthesized and analyzed, and the loop continues until a
sufficiently low RMSE is achieved.
Once the RMSE is sufficiently low, sensitivity analysis can be conducted on the fit to gain insight into
which synthesis parameters influence the predicted overpotential. The sensitivity analysis is done by
keeping all but one synthesis parameter fixed, and recording how it affects the predicted overpotential.
Next BO is conducted on the UCB acquisition function to find the single most optimal set of synthesis
parameters.

3.3
Electrochemical Testing Procedure
The electrodes synthesized in this study were tested using a Gamry600 potentiostat. This device allows
the user to perform a sequence of tests predefined by the user, enabling automatic testing. The testing cell
was a 3D­printed zero­gap cell. The working electrode, separator, and counter electrodes were placed in
a holder, with screws to keep the three components tightly pressed against each other. This minimizes the
ohmic resistance caused by mass transport between the electrodes. A reference hydrogen electrode was
placed in a separate chamber next to the separator to measure the working electrode potential against the
reversible hydrogen potential. The RHE was calibrated at pH 14 and regularly checked against a Hg/HgO
reference electrode. When not in use the RHE was stored in 1M KOH.
Placing the reference electrode in a separate chamber next to the reaction is advantageous because bubble
interference and oxygen leakage are less likely to cause misreadings. The reference electrode can provide
accurate readings from next to the main reaction, because the electric field created by the cell in operation
is assumed to be uniform near the working electrodes and separator, see Figure 3.2.
The testing procedure commenced with preconditioning of the electrode using cyclic voltammetry (CV)
coupled with potentiostatic electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (PEIS) to measure resistance. This
was then followed by another set of CV used to determine the electrochemical active surface area (ECSA),
followed by two linear sweep voltammetry (LSV) used to estimate the overpotential. Finally, PEIS and
chronopotentiometry (CP) were conducted to check resistance and overpotential at steady­state operation.
The final CP was conducted for 15 minutes to check for short­term stability of the electrode. A detailed
description of the testing procedure can be seen in Appendix A.3

3.3.4
Chronopotentiometry
Chronopotentiometry is an electrochemical method that measures the voltage over time while applying
a constant current to the electrode. This method can be used to study how the electrode performance
varies over time. Ideally, the potential should remain constant over time. The CP can also be used to
evaluate how the electrodes perform under steady­state operation. To do this, the corrected potential
from the CP can be compared with the corrected potential from the LSV at the same current density. This
comparison will indicate if the LSV is performed under steady­state conditions. In this study, several CP
tests were performed for each electrode. For overpotential validation, CP was done at ­5, ­10, ­20, ­50,
and ­100mA/cm2 for 1 min. For short­term stability testing, CP was done at ­100mA/cm2 for 15min.

3.4.1
Scanning Electron Microscopy
Scanning Electron Microscopy is a useful technique used to characterize the surface of a sample. In
principle, the technique utilizes a finely collimated beam of electrons which is scanned across the surface
of a sample. The interactions between the electron beam and the sample result in the emission of photons
and electrons which carry information about the topography and composition of the sample [27] as seen
in Figure 3.7. The signal of interest in SEM are secondary electrons (SE) backscatter electrons (bSE),
and element­characteristic X­rays, all emitted from the sample. Other types of electrons and X­rays are
also emitted when using SEM, these signals were not used in this study.
Secondary electrons (SE) are low­energy electrons (0–50 eV) that are generated from the primary electron
beam hitting the surface of the sample and through an inelastic collision event have been released from
the surface of the sample and picked up by the secondary electron detector [28]. Secondary electrons are
generally used to generate topographical images of the surface. This is because the electrons originate
from the surface region of the sample due to their low energy.
In contrast to the secondary electrons, backscatter electrons are high­energy electrons that have interacted
with the sample through elastic scattering. When electrons are elastically scattered they interact with the
atomic nuclei in the sample and are deflected at angles between 90­180° during which the electrons lose
energy. As the energy of the deflected backscattered electrons is proportional to the atom number, the
back­scattered electrons can be used to gather information about the crystallography, topography, and
magnetic field of the sample under investigation. A figure showing the basic principle behind SEM­EDS
can be seen in Figure 3.6 [27].
Figure 3.6: Figure illustrating how different signals the SEM­EDS instrumentation detects from the
atom nucleus. The illustration shows electron movement as a dashed line with grey circles showing
electrons from the electron beam and blue circles showing electrons from the atom. An example of
an inelastic scattering event can be seen from the dashed line circumventing the atom nucleus. An
example of an elastic scattering event can be seen from the electron beam knocking out an electron
from its shelf, causing an electron from a higher energy state to release characteristic x­rays to move
closer to the nucleus.

3.4.2
Energy Dispersive Spectroscopy
Energy Dispersive Spectroscopy is a technique that can be coupled with SEM to provide elemental analy­
sis of the sample. The signals of interest in this study are the secondary electrons, which carry topograph­
ical information about the sample, backscattered electrons carry information about the crystallography,
topography, and magnetic field of the sample and finally, characteristic x­rays which carry information
about the element from which it came. When electrons from the electron beam interact with the sam­
ple, some electrons will have enough energy to cause an inner­shell excitation event. This means that
an electron gains enough energy to be excited to another shell, further away from the atomic nucleus.
As this will create an electron vacancy, and all electrons seek to have the lowest possible, the excited
electron will emit an x­ray photon in order to lose enough energy to go back to its previous shell, see
Figure 3.6. The energy of the emitted x­ray is element­specific, and therefore it is possible to extract
a spectrum containing the amount of x­rays at the specific energies which are counted by the detector.
From recording the spectrum of x­rays, it is possible to create an elemental map in which it can be seen
where the elements are located on the sample [29].

4.2.2
Fitting and Validation of the GP Model
The synthesis parameters and η10 for the first 30 samples were stored in a .csv file. This file was used
as the input for the GP fitting script, which performed both training and validation of the fit. Figure
4.4a shows the results of training the GP on the data. The x­axis (y­true) represents the true measured
overpotential in normalized values, while the y­axis (y­pred) represents the predicted overpotential, also
in normalized values. If the model accurately describes the data, the plotted data points will resemble a
linear line with an R2­value close to 1, as indicated by the black line in Figure 4.4a and 4.4b. In Figure
4.4a, it can be observed that the RMSE for the training evaluation is 29.57mV, which suggests that the
model was able to describe the data during training with an average uncertainty of 29.57mV in any given
point. This is further supported by the fact that the R2­value of the trained model, is 0.8. However, when
conducting leave­one­out cross­validation, as seen in Figure 4.4b, the RMSE significantly increases to
65.5mV while the R2­value is 0.028. The fact that the R2­value is low indicates that the fitted GP model
was not able to accurately predict the true overpotential with corresponding synthesis parameters. It is
worth noting that a significant number of data points are clustered together in the top right corner of the
plot. This suggests that many electrodes had similar overpotentials, despite having different synthesis
parameters. This could potentially explain why the GP fit is not able to predict overpotentials with syn­
thesis parameters, because points located close to each other appear to be uncorrelated. In Figure 4.5, a
visualization of the fitted GP model can be seen, the color bar shows regions of predicted high overpo­
tential (blue), medium overpotential (yellow), and low overpotential (red). Interestingly, the temperature
parameter appears to have no influence on the overpotential, as there is no variation in the color bar as
seen in the figure. This could be explained by the fact that the fitted model does not describe the data
adequately. It is generally expected that the synthesis temperature has an influence on the overpotential
to some extent. One possible way to improve the fit is to supply more data to the fitting algorithm, which
could reduce the RMSE and thereby improve the fit.

Discussion
Even though the machine learning optimization technique was able to guide the experimental work during
the thesis, this technique had inherent limitations and shortcomings. In this chapter, Section 5.1 compares
the performance of selected electrodes to published data while Section 5.3 discusses the limitations and
shortcomings from the methods used in this study. Finally possible sources of errors are highlighted in

5.1
State of the art comparison
To validate the performance gains achieved by optimizing the overpotential, a comparison can be made
with other non­precious HER catalysts. Generally, the catalysts produced in this study, prior to ultra­
sonication, exhibited a lower overpotential than most other non­precious catalysts found in the literature,
with the exception of a NiMo catalyst on a nickel foam support, as indicated in Table 5.1. However,
it’s important to note that the best­performing sample created pre­ultrasonication was unstable and thus
unsuitable for industrial use. In contrast, the optimal electrode post­ultrasonication demonstrated an η10
of ­63mV vs. RHE, ranking among the top­performing electrodes identified in the literature review. In
fact, this η10 of ­63mV vs. RHE is only 19mV higher than that of a NiCoP/carbon cloth electrode [32].
Given that the price of cobalt and platinum is 28,550USD/kg and 28,680USD/kg respectively and the
price of molybdenum is 47USD/kg as pr. 27/02/2024 [33]. This means the NiMo electrodes produced in
this study present an attractive alternative in terms of raw material cost.
When comparing the optimal post­ultrasonication sample, Sample No. 37, in this study to the best­
performing NiMo sample on a Ni­foam support [34], it’s clear that the nickel foam electrode outperforms
with an η10 of ­30mV. This superior performance is expected, given that the nickel foam support inher­
ently has a larger surface area than the perforated nickel plate used in this study. Therefore, by changing
the support to a nickel foam support and using the optimal synthesis parameters found in this study could
be an interesting investigation.

5.3
Critical Review and Limitations of the Methods Used
5.3.1
Scanning Range for ECSA Estimation
The ECSA for the synthesized electrodes was estimated using the method outlined in Section 3.3.3. This
method assumes that only the charging current flows through the electrode within the selected voltage
scanning range. The scanning range, chosen to be between ­40mV and 10mV vs. RHE, was based on
initial tests of NiMo alloy electrodes as seen in Figure 5.1a. However, it was discovered that this scanning
range was not applicable to all electrodes. Some electrodes exhibited a significant current at ­40mV vs.
RHE, indicating that the scanning range was, in fact, not non­Faradaic. For instance, as can be seen
on the y­axis in Figure 5.1a, no significant current flows through the electrode, suggesting that only the
charging current was present during the CV test. This contrasts with Figure 5.1b, where a substantial
current of up to 0.4A flows through the electrode, indicating the occurrence of electrochemical reactions
during the test. This resulted in poor linear fitting on the scan speed vs. charging current, and the double­
layer capacitance determined using this method was not representative for all electrodes. The R2­values
of the fit made using this method can be found in Appendices B.1 and B.2. To overcome the limitation
of improper voltage scan range, each electrode could be synthesized twice. The first electrode would be
used to determine an appropriate scanning range for the CV, which would then be applied to the second
electrode. An alternative method for estimating the double­layer capacitance is to fit the impedance curve
described in Section 3.3.1 to an appropriate electrochemical equivalent circuit.

5.3.3
Steady State During Test
Reflecting on the testing sequence, see Appendix A.3, it becomes apparent that the placement of the CP
tests could have implications for the steady state operation of the electrodes. Specifically, the CP tests
were conducted at the end of the sequence, after the LSV tests. This implies that when using the LSV
tests for overpotential determination, the electrode might not have been in steady­state operation. If any
species on the surface of the electrodes is reduced, it will impact the voltage readings thereby introducing
an uncertainty. In fact, by investigating the LSV compared to the CP measurements of Sample No. 37 as
seen on Figure 5.3 it becomes evident that the CP measurements do not align directly with the LSV curve.
The fact that the LSV and CP measurements do no align suggests that the LSV measurements may not
have been recorded during steady­state operation. However, given that the CP measured at ­10mA/cm2
is in close proximity to the LSV curve, it is reasonable to assume that the overpotentials determined using
the LSV method are acceptable. However, as the deviation is larger at high current densities, this could
have implications for determining the Tafel slope and exchange current density. Conducting the CP tests
before the LSV would increase the likelihood of the electrodes being in steady­state for the LSV test, and
thus also provide a more accurate overpotential and Tafel slope determination.
Figure 5.3: Figure showing the LSV from Sample No. 37, with a scatter plot of the CP measurements
done a ­5, ­10, ­20, ­50 ­100 mA/cm2. Here, it can be seen the the CP measurements do not align
directly with the LSV curve, indicating that the LSV test was not conducted during steady­state

The same point can be made about the PEIS tests. The PEIS_2 (measured at 0V vs. RHE) tests were
used to determine the ohmic resistance. However, as PEIS_3 (measured at ­5mV vs. RHE) is more likely
to be conducted during steady­state operation, it could provide a more accurate ohmic determining step.
Nevertheless, when comparing the ohmic resistance found by PEIS_2 to PEIS_3, it was generally found
to be the same value. Thus, the ohmic resistance correction using PEIS_2 is considered reasonable. The
ohmic resistance measurements from PEIS_2 and PEIS_3 can be seen in Appendix C.

5.3.5
Gamry600 Instrument Limitation
This study utilized a Gamry600 potentiostat for the electrochemical tests, which had a limit to the max­
imum voltage and current that could be drawn from the instrument [37]. Given that the electrodes used
for testing were cut to an area of 4.5cm2, it was not possible to determine the overpotential at high current
densities for poorly performing electrodes, because the voltage required to reach those current densities
lay beyond the capability of the instrument. This limitation affected the determination of the Tafel slope
for these electrodes. The Python script developed for determining the Tafel slope for poor­performing
electrodes selected the highest available four current density points and their corresponding overpoten­
tial, each separated by 10mA/cm2, for fitting. However, this approach resulted in the poor­performing
electrodes appearing to have a lower Tafel slope than expected which was likely due to the overpotentials
at these lower current densities not being linear on a semi­logarithmic scale. To mitigate this issue, the
electrodes could have been cut to a smaller size, thereby enabling the equipment to draw a larger current
density.
5.4
Potential Sources of error
In any experimental study, it is important to recognize potential sources of error that may have influenced
the results. The errors can originate from various factors and significantly impact the accuracy, reliability,
and reproducibility of the findings. This section will discuss possible sources of error which may have
impacted the findings of this study.
Inaccurate Cutting of Electrodes
During this study, the perforated nickel plates were cut by hand using a pair of scissors and a ruler. This
resulted in an uncertainty of the area of the electrodes being introduced because measuring perforated
nickel plates with a ruler is inherently uncertain. Therefore the electrodes were cut to approximately
1.5x3.5cm. Thereby introducing uncertainty to any calculation relying on results per electrode area.
Submerging Electrical Connection in Deposit Bath
Amplifying the aforementioned electrode area uncertainty is the fact that the top part of the electrode
used for electrical connection, see Figure 3.1a, could also be partly submerged into the nickel strike and
deposit solution. Consequently, if submerged in the solution, the electrical connection part could also
be deposited onto, thereby altering the applied current density. This source of error was particularly
a problem in the deposit solution as the solution was not transparent, and thus made it impossible to
determine how much of the electrical connection was submerged.
Degradation of Counter Electrodes During Deposit
During the synthesis process, it was found that depositing at high voltages (>2V), caused the counter
electrodes to degrade fast. An unidentified green substance was found on the counter electrode suggesting
the deposition of a nickel compound. If it’s assumed that the green substance on the counter electrodes
originates from the formation of nickel and chloride species, the fact that it is deposited on the counter
50
AI­Assisted Optimization of Electrodes for Alkaline Water Electrolysis
==PAGE:61==
electrodes could have an impact on the nickel concentration of the deposit bath. A possible solution
to mitigate this source of error is to change the source of nickel to e.g. NiSo4, however, due to time
constraints this was not done in this study.
Nickel Strike Concentration
Another source of error is the nickel concentration of the nickel strike bath. As the nickel strike bath
did not have a lid on when in use, this means that HCL could evaporate and thus the HCL concentration
could change over time. Furthermore, when conducting a nickel strike, a small amount of hydrogen was
produced on the working electrode, meaning that over time the concentration of water also decreased.
Since the nickel strike bath was never remixed or checked during the project, this posed an unaccounted
uncertainty.
Ohmic Resistance of Current Collectors and Sensing Electrodes
Another source of error, is the fact that the testing cell had 4 connections for the Gamry600, See Appendix
A.1. These connections were made from nickel and would contribute to the overall overpotential of the
cell. However, as the connections were small compared to the working­ and counter electrodes this con­
tribution was assumed to be negligible. Furthermore, as electrical connection wires were not exchanged
during the project, the contribution from these connections has been identical for all tests.
Unstable Temperature Readings
Throughout the course of the thesis, it became apparent that the stirrers used in this project had hardware
and/or software issues. In some cases, the display appeared to show a different temperature (±0.5°C)
than the actual temperature of the water bath. In other cases the stirrer and heating element returned errors
on separate occasions, leading to possible uneven heat distribution.

Conclusion
In conclusion, this study demonstrated that machine learning is a viable and powerful tool for guiding
experimental work aimed at optimizing the HER overpotential by altering the synthesis parameters for
producing NiMo alloy catalysts by electroplating on a Ni­support. During the course of this study, it
was decided to optimize for both performance only and to optimize for mechanical robustness along with
performance. To achieve these objectives, two separate datasets were created. The first dataset contained
performance data from electrodes tested immediately after synthesis, while the second dataset included
data from the electrodes, tested a second time, this test being after 30 minutes of ultrasonication.
For the optimization of performance only, Bayesian optimization was applied first to the GIBBON acqui­
sition to produce candidate samples to reduce uncertainty. After the results from the candidate samples
were incorporated into the dataset, the UCB acquisition function was optimized to find the optimal set
of synthesis parameters. The GP model’s predictions of overpotentials with synthesis parameters were
found to be less accurate, evident by the fact that the RMSE found during in­sample leave­one­out cross­
validation was 59mV and the R2­value from the linear fit conducted on predicted versus true values was
0.1. Despite this, the electrode synthesized using the optimal set of synthesis parameters (219 mA/cm2,
22°C, 22min) achieved an η10 of ­59mV vs. RHE with a Tafel slope of 156mV/dec. Although this was not
the lowest overpotential found in this study, the performance gain compared to an untreated Ni­support
was still significant. The best­performing electrode achieved an η10 of ­36mV vs. RHE with a Tafel slope
of 43mV/dec.
When optimizing both performance and mechanical stability, the GP model was trained using post­
ultrasonication data. The in­sample leave­one­out cross­validation of this model yielded an RMSE of
33.5mV and an R2 value of 0.78. These results suggest that the model was capable of predicting over­
potential based on synthesis parameters. Upon optimization, the electrode synthesized with the optimal
parameters (459 mA/cm2, 49°C, 22min) exhibited an η10 post­ultrasonication of ­63mV vs. RHE and
a Tafel slope of 183mV/dec. Notably, this electrode was identified as the best­performing electrode
post­ultrasonication. In investigating the causes of performance improvement, it was found that good­
performing electrodes had a sponge­like porous electroplated structure. Further investigation through
EDS analysis revealed that good­performing electrodes also had a significant amount of molybdenum
present in the electroplated structure.
On the microstructural level, the structures of the poorest­performing electrode and the best­performing
electrode were found to be identical. Thus, the increase in performance was attributed to both the amount
of molybdenum in the electroplated structure and a high­surface­area porous structure. However, it was
also observed that in many instances, the electroplated surface structure did not adhere properly to the
support surface. This meant that during the handling of the electrodes, there was a risk of the electroplated
catalyst delaminating. This introduced a significant degree of uncertainty into the performance data for
the pre­ultrasonication dataset

Furthermore, an investigation into the relationship between the ECSA and the overpotential revealed a
clear relationship. On a semi­logarithmic scale, the linear relationship between ECSA and overpotential
had an R2 value of 0.94.
Finally, it was found that if future research identifies a method to improve the adhesion of the catalyst
layer, the efficiency gains that can be obtained from using electrodes synthesized using the methods
from this study under industrial current densities could significantly enable green hydrogen to become
a viable resource. This would further enable reductions in greenhouse gas emissions on a global scale.
Furthermore, the electrodes produced in this thesis utilize raw materials which are significantly cheaper
than electrodes made using e.g. cobalt and platinum.

7
Recommendations for Future Work
As it was found in this thesis that the machine learning algorithm is able to guide the experimental labo­
ratory work toward high­performing electrodes, several new research topics emerge.
Firstly, the sponge­like structure observed in the best­performing samples has been found to be beneficial
for the HER due to its high surface area and molybdenum content. However, a major challenge is ensuring
that this structure adheres well to the electrode surface. Poor adhesion can lead to the detachment of the
structure, resulting in a loss of activity. Therefore, developing methods to improve the adhesion of the
sponge­like structure could lead to more stable electrodes with lower overpotentials. This could involve
modifying the electrode surface or the deposition process to enhance the interaction between the deposited
material and the electrode.
Secondly, it would be worthwhile to investigate how the Ni:Mo ratio in an alkaline pH influences the
hydrogen evolution reaction overpotential. This could potentially lead to a more resource­efficient elec­
trodeposition process if the machine learning method is utilized to optimize for the overpotential.
Finally, the optimal synthesis parameters identified in this project have been determined on a small scale.
It is important to verify whether these parameters also apply when the process is scaled up. This could
involve conducting experiments in larger electrochemical cells or even pilot­scale facilities. In addition,
long­term stability testing is crucial to assess the durability of the electrodes under realistic operating
conditions. This could involve running the electrodeposition process for extended periods and monitoring
the performance of the electrodes over time.
