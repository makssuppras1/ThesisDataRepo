Abstract
Cut element formulations can be used to model arbitrary crisp interfaces on
a structured mesh. This thesis uses cut elements to model Fluid-Structure
Interaction (FSI). The modelling of the underlying physics are presented indi-
vidually. The ﬂuid modelling includes the use of Nitsche’s method for weakly
imposing boundary conditions on cuts.
Furthermore, a method for stabil-
ising the ﬂuid solution using Continuous Interior Penalisation (CIP) with
ghost penalisation is presented. The non-linear behaviour of the structural
domain capable of modelling large displacements is modelled using a Total
Lagrangian (TL) formulation. The ﬂuid and structural solvers are coupled to
an FSI solver. Two ways of creating an FSI solver are presented. The ﬁrst
method deforms the mesh for each FSI step. This limits it to small deforma-
tions. Based on this an alternative method is suggested where a structured
mesh is used throughout the FSI simulation. This is done by using a mapping
method based on the Arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian (ALE) method. The ALE
method requires the use of an Updated Lagrangian (UL) formulation of the
structural non-linearities. This is not implemented in this thesis, but the po-
tential of the method is demonstrated using the TL formulation. Finally, the
strengths and weaknesses of the mesh deformation and mapped mesh method
are demonstrated on diﬀerent test cases.

Resumé
Cut elementer kan bruges til at modellere skarpe grænseﬂader mellem ar-
bitrære geometrier på et struktureret net. Denne kandidatafhandling bruger
cut elementer til at modellere Fluid-Struktur Interaktion (FSI), hvor modelle-
ringen af de underlæggende fysiske elementer bliver præsenteret hver for sig.
Fluidmodelleringen bruger Nitsche’s metode til at indføre grænsebetingelser
på svag form. Ydermere stabiliseres ﬂuidmodellen vha. Continuous Interior
Penalisation (CIP) med ghost penalisation. En Total Lagrangian (TL) formu-
lering bliver brugt til at håndtere den ikke-linearitet, der følger af de store
udbøjninger i den strukturelle model. Modellerne for ﬂuiddomænet og det
strukturelle domæne bliver koblet til en samlet FSI-model. To versioner bliver
præsenteret, hvor den første deformere nettet for hvert stridt i FSI-løseren.
Denne metode er begrænset til små deformationer og derfor præsenteres en
alternativ metode. Den alternative metode bruger et struktureret net under
hele FSI-simuleringen. Dette gøres ved at bruge en projiceringsmetode baseret
på en Arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian (ALE) metode. For at denne metode kan
bruges skal en Updated Lagrangian (UL) formulering bruges til at håndtere
ikke-lineariteten. UL metoden er ikke implementeret i denne afhandling men
potentialet af ALE metoden demonstreres ved brug af TL-formuleringen. Til
sidst testes styrker og svagheder for de to FSI-løsere på en række forskellige
testopsætninger.

1 Introduction
Fluid-Structure Interaction (FSI) is highly relevant in many engineering dis-
ciplines. It describes the interaction and balance between e.g. deformation of
a structure vs. velocities and pressure in a ﬂuid. This can be in anything from
aortic valves to wind turbine wings as illustrated in Figure 1.1. Aortic valves
play an important role in the cardiovascular system of humans and other liv-
ing things. The valves are found throughout the circulatory system. Their
purpose is to allow blood ﬂow in one direction while hindering backﬂow. This
means that there is a strong coupling between ﬂuid and structure. The ﬂow
is governed by the position of the valve and the pressure in the ﬂow. Cor-
respondingly, the deformation of the valve is governed by the load from the

On a much larger and industrial scale FSI also plays a role in how wind
turbines work. Here the load from the ﬂow is both driving the rotation and
bending of the turbine blades. Hence the FSI dictates how much power the

This thesis aims to provide an immersed boundary method using cut ele-
ments for FSI problems. The method is suitable for topology optimisation, as
it can easily be used in parallel computing. This thesis does not cover the cut
element method in topology optimisation. However, topology optimisation is
a strong motivation for developing the cut element method presented here.
Therefore, the method will be related to topology optimisation through the
report.

Diﬀerent approaches can be used when modelling interface problems like
FSI. Traditional FEM uses a boundary ﬁtted mesh. It is possible to use in
FSI but it requires re-meshing to make sure that interfaces are represented
by element edges. This is both computationally heavy and requires that the
numbering of the Degrees of Freedom (DOF) is updated which is cumber-
some in parallel computing. Furthermore, it is diﬃcult to apply to density
based topology optimisation as intermediate densities at interfaces are com-
mon.

interface between material phases such as ﬂuids and structures. The lack of

The XFEM method, which is described in Fries and Belytschko, 2010,
[4], uses an enrichment technique in the regions of interest e.g. at a crack or
material phase interface. By doing this DOF’s are added to the system even
though the mesh is not reﬁned. The result is a larger system of equation with
a correlation between computational time and level of enrichment. An XFEM
method applied to ﬂuid problems is presented in Villanueva and Maute, 2017,
[5] where it is used for topology optimisation. The method presented in this
thesis diﬀers by not using enrichment to model interfaces and thereby it does
not change the dimensions of the system.
CutFem is an alternative to XFEM where enrichment is not used. Instead
cut shape functions are used and the method is in many ways similar to the
method proposed by Andreasen and Aage, 2019, [6] which is used in this thesis.
In Burman, Elfverson, Hansbo, Larson, and Larsson, 2019, [7] CutFEM is used
in a way with similarities to the cut elements used in this thesis, but where
the ﬁctitious domain is not included in the calculation. This thesis aims at
including the entire domain Ωto ensure optimal design freedom.
Finally, FCM which was introduced by Parvizian, Düster, and Rank, 2007,
[8] oﬀers an approach where the ﬁctitious domain is included. Similar to the
method from Andreasen and Aage, 2019, [6] a contrast parameter is used.
This is in many ways similar to the Solid Isotropic Material with Penalisa-
tion (SIMP) method used in density based topology optimisation. In a purely
structural simulation it includes the ﬁctitious domain with a very low stiﬀness.
Thereby, the ﬁctitious domain is included but has negligible eﬀect on the so-
lution in the physical domain. The diﬀerence between FCM and the method
used in this thesis is the way cuts are handled. As described in Groen, Lange-
laar, Sigmund, and Ruess, 2017, [9] FCM consists of cells divided into voxels.

The advantage of the method used in this thesis is that the dimensions
of the system are constant with no new DOF’s introduced during the solving
process. This reduces the amount of bookkeeping required and reduces com-
puting time. The entire domain is included providing more design freedom.
Furthermore, interfaces are modelled with linear cuts which works well for
interaction problems.
The cut element method provided in this thesis is used for both the ﬂuid
and structural domains. The aim is that the motion of the structural domain
is mapped through an Arbitrary Lagrangian-Eulerian (ALE) based method.
This means that both the ﬂuid and structure uses a ﬁxed and structured
background mesh at all times as illustrated in Figure 1.4(c). This allows a
very ﬂexible design domain as the entire domain can take the characteristics
of either structure or ﬂuid at any time in the optimisation process.
Among other immersed boundary methods speciﬁc for FSI is the method
presented by Jenkins and Maute, 2016, [10]. The principle of this method

An alternative approach where a solid structure with a ﬁtted mesh is
immersed into the ﬂuid domain was presented by Schott et al., 2019, [11]. An
illustration of this approach is available in Figure 1.4(b). The method allows
the solid structure to have large displacements by using a non-linear element
formulation. The large displacements of the solid structure has no inﬂuence on
the ﬂuid solution as this is always calculated on a ﬁxed mesh. This is ensured
by an ALE-based approach. The diﬀerence from the method presented in this
thesis is that only the ﬂuid mesh is mapped to a ﬁxed mesh. The downside to
using a ﬁtted mesh for the structural domain is that the mesh is now restricted
to only this domain. This is not a problem when known designs are evaluated
and optimisation is not desired. But when used in topology optimisation it
will introduce some restrictions to the design freedom. It is more diﬃcult to
allow material anywhere in the domain.
There are several advantages to using the method suggested in this thesis
compared to the methods presented by Jenkins and Maute, 2016, [10] and
Schott et al., 2019, [11]. First, the use of ﬁxed meshes means that there is
very little bookkeeping in the set-up. The amount of elements and element
connectivity stay the same throughout the entire computation. Therefore the
method is good for parallel computing which is important as computational
time is often an issue in optimisation problems. Finally, the method does not
require re-meshing. If the method by Jenkins and Maute, 2016, [10] is used
to model large deformation it is necessary to re-mesh skewed and inverted
elements. Re-meshing is also necessary if new structural regions should be able
to develop in the method by Schott et al., 2019, [11]. The method suggested
in this thesis can handle large deformations and will be able to develop new
structural regions in the domain without the need of re-meshing when used in
topology optimisation.
1.1
Motivation
Using cut elements to get a geometrically accurate indication of boundaries is
very useful when applied to multi-physics problems where interface conditions
are crucial. It allows a ﬁxed structured background mesh to be used in the
entire domain while still being able to describe a non-conforming boundary.
==PAGE:22==
6

This is useful especially in shape and topology optimisation of FSI problems.
Cut elements provide a way of applying proper boundary conditions without
re-meshing for every optimisation step and allows the solid structure to deform
according the forces applied by ﬂuid ﬂow. In density based topology optimi-
sation, element phases are averaged and therefore it is diﬃcult to determine a
proper boundary. This makes it hard to determine exact interface conditions
and often very ﬁne meshes are required to compensate.
One crucial point is the application of boundary conditions between ﬂuid
and structural phases. This project investigates how existing weak boundary
condition formulations, such as Nitsche’s method by Nitsche, 1971, [12] per-
form. Furthermore, the Continuous Interior Penalty (CIP) method presented
by Douglas and Dupont, 1976, [13] is used to stabilise pressure oscillations.
This is necessary because of an instability issue that occurs when ﬁrst order
elements are used for both the velocity and pressure ﬁelds. These instabilities
are not problematic when second order elements are used for the velocity ﬁeld
and ﬁrst order is used for the pressure. However, there are advantages of us-
ing ﬁrst order elements in both ﬁelds. Simpler and faster solvers can be used
compared to when second order elements are used. The beneﬁt is that compu-
tational power can be used on mesh reﬁnement and not solving. In topology
optimisation this is important as more detailed designs can be obtained with
less computational power. Therefore, this thesis focuses on using only ﬁrst
order elements.
The structural domain will be calculated using the Total Lagrangian (TL)
method. This is used to handle non-linear deformations. The Updated La-
grangian (UL) method required for the ALE method is also brieﬂy introduced
in this thesis. A method for mapping the structural solution back to the initial
ﬁxed mesh is presented. The method is based on the ALE method with an
operator-split technique described in Nazem, Sheng, and Carter, 2006, [14].

1.3
Contributions and Layout
This thesis contributes to the ﬁeld of FEM of FSI by presenting the element
matrices used in the CIP method. The matrices can be directly implemented
in a discretised FEM code. Only appropriate bookkeeping between local and
global DOF’s is needed. The thesis also presents one way of doing this.
Furthermore, a method for simulation of FSI problems with small displace-
ments is presented. The method uses the cut element formulation presented
by Andreasen and Aage, 2019, [6], where the ﬁctitious domain is included in
both the ﬂuid and structural domains. This makes the method very suitable
for topology optimisation. Furthermore, a method capable of handling large
displacements without re-meshing is suggested.
The thesis starts by presenting the cut element formulation in Chapter 2.
Next, an introduction to FSI is provided in Chapter 3. Chapter 4 presents the
governing equations of ﬂuid mechanics and the discretised element matrices
obtained by the Galerkin method. Furthermore, it introduces how Nitsche’s
method is used to weakly enforce boundary conditions on interfaces.
The
chapter also presents how the ﬂuid solution is stabilised using the CIP method.
The ﬂuid modelling is validated against results from Comsol MultiPhysics 5.4.
The solid modelling is presented in Chapter 5. First the geometrical non-
linear TL formulation is presented and validated against Comsol. The UL
formulation is introduced in relation to the TL method. To handle ﬁctitious
domains consisting of both solid and void regions an interpolation scheme is
introduced. The scheme interpolates between linear and non-linear element
formulations to ensure that the system can be solved.
The ﬂuid and solid modelling is coupled in a segregated solver in Chapter 6.
First, the load on the interface is determined from the converged ﬂuid solution.
Then a solver using mesh deformation is presented. The mesh deformation
method is tested and the results are discussed. Next, a mapping method based
on the ALE method is suggested. The interpolation process used for this is
described and discussed.
Finally, the potential of the mapping method is
demonstrated on test similar to those used on the mesh deformation method.
Both methods are demonstrated on diﬀerent test cases in Chapter 7. The
chapter illustrates the strengths and weaknesses of both methods.
The challenges encountered during this thesis are discussed in Chapter 8.
Finally, Chapter 9 sums up the conclusion from this thesis and future work
directions are proposed.

2 Cut Elements
This Chapter introduces the theory behind the cut element formulation used
in this thesis. Cut elements are essential when a submerged boundary method
is used to model interaction in FSI problems. Both the ﬂuid and structural
domains in this thesis use cut elements to describe the interface. This allows
the use of an unﬁtted mesh where both an active and an inactive domain
are present at all times. This approach is also known as a ﬁctitious domain

present and therefore the stiﬀness is zero.

3 Overview of Fluid Structure
Interaction
Methods for modelling the underlying physics, i.e. ﬂuid and structure, are
necessary when modelling FSI. Figure 3.1 illustrates an example of an FSI
problem. The ﬂuid domain is illustrated with blue streamlines ﬂowing around
the structural region indicated by light blue colour. The interface between
these two regions is marked by a red line. This interface is where the interac-
tion is happening. The ﬁgure also show a dashed red line indicating the initial
undeformed position of the structural domain. The example in Figure 3.1
can act as a simpliﬁed 2D model of one side of an aortic valve. It is an over
simpliﬁcation of the problem as it requires 3D models to really describe the
physics. However, it illustrates the potential of modelling FSI.
FSI introduces a variety of challenges, mainly because the behaviour of
the ﬂow is strongly dependant on where the structure occupies the domain.
Similarly, the deformation of the structure is dependant on the load applied
by the ﬂuid. This load is a result of the pressure in the ﬂow and skin friction.
The pressure results in a force acting normal to the surface of the structure.
Skin friction is a result of the no-slip condition. This creates a boundary layer
close to the structural surface. The boundary layer goes from zero velocity at
Γfsi to the free stream velocity at some distance from Γfsi. As a result shear

Traditionally FEM has rarely been used for ﬂuid problems. The reason
is that the method has issues with stability due to oscillations in convec-
tion dominated problems. However, with the introduction of the Streamline-
Upwind/Petrov-Galerkin (SUPG) method for convection dominated ﬂows by
Brooks and Hughes, 1982, [19] and later the Pressure-Stabilising/Petrov-
Galerkin (PSPG) method for advection dominated ﬂows by Tezduyar, Mittal,
Ray, and Shih, 1992, [20] this issue is solved. Unfortunately, these stabilisa-
tions do come at a price as described in Burman, Fernández, and Hansbo,
2006, [21]. Among their undesired features are that they introduce artiﬁcial
boundary conditions on velocities and pressures. Furthermore artiﬁcial non-
symmetric terms are added to the system and a non-physical pressure/velocity
coupling is introduced.

The simplest way to enforce boundary condition is to do it strongly. This
is done by prescribing the desired conditions for the individual boundaries
directly. For a Dirichlet type velocity boundary condition this could be done
by setting u = gu on ΓD, where gu is the prescribed velocity. However, for

is illustrated in Figure 4.4 which shows an element subject to a cut.

on the interface where the weak boundary condition is enforced. It should

4.3
Continuous Interior Penalty
The system of equations described in the earlier sections is derived with the
standard Galerkin method. The matrices use an equal order approximation
for the velocity and pressure ﬁelds. This method is widely known to have
stability issues when used on problems where large gradients can occur. One
way to deal with this problem is to use a suﬃciently ﬁne mesh to capture
these gradients, Douglas and Dupont, 1976, [13]. This increases the need for
computational power and therefore this approach is often not beneﬁcial.
Several other methods have been developed to deal with the instability is-
sue. One of the ﬁrst and most widely used is the Streamline-Upwind/Petrov-
Galerkin (SUPG) method introduced by Brooks and Hughes, 1982, [19]. How-
ever, this method does include some undesired features.
First, the least
squares method used in the method introduces a velocity/pressure coupling
that is not physical. Second, artiﬁcial non-symmetric terms are added to the

amount of damping. It works by evaluating the residual at three diﬀerent
perturbations of η. Then a second order polynomial is ﬁtted to the three data
points. The resulting polynomial is used to estimate the optimal step length.
The method should help minimize the risk of overshooting the solution and
thereby reduce the risk of getting stuck in undesired local minima. For a more
thorough description of the line-search method please refer to Section 4.1 in
de Borst, Crisﬁeld, Remmers, and Verhoosel, 2012, [29] or Section 5.1.4 in
Wriggers, 2008, [30].

5.2
Interpolation Scheme for Fictitious Domain
A geometrically non-linear element formulation combined with a ﬁctitious
domain approach is prone to have convergence issues. The problem occurs
because of the very low stiﬀness in the void region as illustrated in Figure 5.4.
The result is that void elements are likely to experience large deformations.

6 Coupling of Fluid and
Structure
To model the interaction between ﬂuid and structure the two individual solvers
are combined to form a segregated FSI solver. This introduces a variety of
challenges. Mainly because the behaviour of the ﬂow is strongly dependant on
where the structure occupies the domain. Correspondingly, the deformation
of the structural domain is dependant on the load applied by the ﬂuid. An
illustration of this coupling can be seen in Figure 6.1. The result is that the
systems are highly coupled and non-linear. Therefore, an iterative process is
needed to ﬁnd a solution where the ﬂow and structure are in equilibrium.
This chapter presents these challenges and describe how they are handled
in this thesis. First, a method for transferring loads from the ﬂuid domain to
the structural domain is presented. Here traction forces from skin friction are
neglected and therefore only pressure loads are acting on the structure.
Next, two approaches for obtaining a solution for the interaction problem
are presented. The ﬁrst uses one mesh for the ﬂuid domain and a separate
mesh for the structural domain.
The ﬂuid solution is found and used to
enforce loads on the structural domain. This is then solved and used to deform
the ﬂuid mesh. The structural mesh is unchanged as the structural solver
always refer to the initial conﬁguration in the TL formulation. Then a new
ﬂuid solution is found on the updated mesh. This process is repeated until a
converged solution is reached.
The second approach suggests how one single mesh for both the ﬂuid and
solid domain can be used. Again the ﬂuid solution is calculated and used to

with Matlabs built-in interpolation function griddata.

bilinear interpolation to map data from a scattered set of data points to a
structured grid. The function can be used to transform relevant data from
the deformed mesh present after the Lagrangian step, back to a structured
background mesh.

lapping itself. To avoid this being a problem only values in nodes related to
solid or cut elements from a structural point of view are mapped. Doing this
with the function griddata requires the approach illustrated in Figure 6.13.
It works by adding one node in each corner of the domain.
This ensures
that all node in the void region take appropriate values. As a result all void
nodes are not mapped and will loose the reference to previous iterations. The
method makes sure that any void values appearing inside the solid domain as
a result of large displacements are not mapped and thereby do not obscure
the mapping.

now purely lies in how the structural non-linearity is modelled.

8 Discussion
This thesis has encountered numerous challenges. Solutions have been found
to some of them, while others still need to be solved. The diﬀerent challenges
will be discussed in this chapter. Suggestions on how to handle the reaming
unsolved problems are presented and discussed.
The thesis presented how to model the individual physics of an FSI prob-
lem using cut elements. The method used in this thesis includes the ﬁctitious
domain in both the ﬂuid structural models. Especially, the ﬂuid model was
aﬀected by this approach. Nitsche’s method was used to enforce boundary
conditions on cuts. The solution was stabilised using the CIP method. This is
also the case in Massing et al., 2017, [22], but without including the ﬁctitious
domain. In this thesis the CIP terms from Massing et al., 2017, [22] were ex-
tended to the ﬁctitious domain while the ghost penalty terms were kept at the
cuts. This alone was not suﬃcient to produce accurate ﬂuid results. A variety
of tests were performed to attack the problem from diﬀerent angles. From
these tests it was seen that one method was able to produce very good ﬂuid
results. This method used CIP in the entire domain and ghost penalties at the
cuts. Furthermore, strongly enforced no-slip conditions were imposed on all
nodes related only to elements fully submerged in the ﬁctitious domain. Even
though one good method was found from the tests performed they also indi-
cated that the ghost penalisation was not strong enough. In Appendix D.2
a test was performed without the ﬁctitious domain. This set-up should be
similar to Massing et al., 2017, [22], but it showed streamlines going into the
weakly enforced no-slip boundary. Furthermore, the pressure dropped signiﬁ-
cantly just in front of this boundary. The problem was not seen when no cuts
were used and boundary conditions were enforced strongly. It suggests that
the ghost penalisation is too weak when interacting with Nitsche’s method.
This should be examined more thoroughly in future work.
The second major challenge encountered in this thesis was to set-up an
ALE based method for simulation FSI problems with large displacements.
Work on an ALE method using the operator-split technique was commenced.
It was possible to show its potential by using the TL formulation for the ﬁrst
Lagrangian step and then take an Eulerian step using ﬁrst order interpolation.
However, it was not possible to obtain converged FSI solutions using the TL
formulation. A considerable amount of work was put into implementing the

9 Conclusion
The aim of the thesis was to extent the cut element formulation presented
by Andreasen and Aage, 2019, [6] to FSI problems. This required the use of
Nitsche’s method to enforce boundary conditions weakly on cuts. This was
implemented successfully for both velocity and pressure boundaries.
The motivation for extending the cut element method is to use it for topol-
ogy optimisation in the future. For this reason, it was desired to use equal
order elements for the velocity and pressure in the ﬂuid domain. Doing this
means that computational power is used on a ﬁner discretisation than it would
be possible with higher order elements. This means that more detailed design
can be obtained. Using equal order elements introduces instabilities to the
solution. Therefore, it was stabilised with the CIP method including ghost
penalisation and strong no-slip conditions in the ﬁctitious domain. This was
able to produce very good ﬂuid results.
In addition the element matrices
used in the CIP and ghost penalty methods was presented. To the authors
knowledge these have not been explicitly presented in existing literature.
The ﬂuid solver was coupled to a non-linear structural solver using the TL
formulation to form an FSI solver which used mesh deformation. This was
able to produce converged FSI solution and thereby fulﬁlling the basic aim
of this thesis. Through tests it was seen that the mesh deformation method
in general was restricted to small deformations due to the ﬂuid solver being
used in the deformed mesh. Based on this a mapped mesh method based on
the ALE method with the operator-split technique was presented. It required
the use of an UL formulation for the structural non-linearities. It was not
possible to implement a working version of the UL formulation in this thesis.
However, the potential of mapping the mesh with an ALE based method was
illustrated using the TL formulation.
Summed up, this thesis provides an FSI solver capable of handling small
structural displacements. This is done using cut elements where the ﬁctitious
domain is included in both the ﬂuid and structural domain. To the authors
knowledge this has not been done before. Furthermore, the overall precess for a
mapping method capable of handling large displacements has been presented.
Only a working UL formulation is required to simulate FSI problems with
large displacements.

9.1
Future Work
This thesis has encountered many challenges. Some have been solved and some
are still unsolved. To further develop the method presented in this thesis two
challenges has to be prioritised.
First, the performance of the ghost penalisation in the ﬂuid solver should
be examined thoroughly. It would be interesting to see if it was possible to
produce good ﬂuid solution without using strongly enforced no-slip conditions
in the ﬁctitious domain.
Second, to reach the full potential of the cut elements method in FSI prob-
lems a working UL formulation has to be implemented. With a working UL
formulation it is rather straight forward to map the level set ﬁeld and dis-
placements to a ﬁxed background mesh. This means that the UL formulation
is key to being able to simulate FSI problems with large displacements.
Finally, for the method to be used in topology optimisation the sensitivities
have to be determined. Most of the required sensitivities are already described
in Andreasen and Aage, 2019, [6]. However, the sensitivity of the mapping
performed in the Eulerian step needs to be determined. When this is done all
building block needed for topology optimisation are constructed.
