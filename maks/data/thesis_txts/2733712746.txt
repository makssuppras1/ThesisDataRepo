Abstract
Eukaryotic translation initiation is a highly regulated process that marks the beginning of
protein synthesis. Thousands of proteins possessing important structural and regulatory
roles are encoded in eukaryotic genomes, and precise identification of translation initiation
sites thus constitutes an important task for the comprehensive understanding of gene ex-
pression, protein synthesis and related processes. In eukaryotes, translation of main open
reading frames from an mRNA transcript is frequently initiated at an AUG alternative to
the first one. This observation demonstrates that local context information influences the
choice of translation initiation site. Prediction of translation initiation sites in eukaryotes
is thus a complex and non-trivial task.
In this project, I introduce NetStart 2.0, a novel deep learning-based model for predicting
eukaryotic translation initiation sites of main open reading frames. Utilizing pretrained
nucleotide and protein language models, NetStart 2.0 operates by understanding both the
local start codon context of a translation initiation site while identifying shifts from non-
coding to coding regions, thereby investigating whether nucleotide sequences upstream or
downstream of a start codon can be translated into protein-like structures. Being trained on
sequences from a broad spectrum of phylogenetically diverse eukaryotic species, NetStart 2.0
is able to accurately predict translation initiation sites in species all across the eukaryotic
domain. Solely relying on the information embedded within the mRNA sequence itself
as well as the organism origin, we find NetStart 2.0 to outperform both its predecessor,
NetStart 1.0, and the most recent state-of-the-art method TIS Transformer.

Introduction
1.1
RNA Processing in Eukaryotes
In eukaryotic cells, protein-coding genes are transcribed by RNA polymerase II in the
nucleus to produce precursor mRNA (pre-mRNA) (Figure 1A, ”Transcription Region”).
Pre-mRNAs undergo a series of critical processing events in the nucleus to acquire its mature
form (Figure 1B) [1][2]. Three key modification events occur in processing of pre-mRNA,
namely 5’ capping, splicing and 3’ polyadenylation [1][2][3].
Figure 1: Hypothetical structures of A) a gene and B) a mature mRNA, respectively. The
figure is made with inspiration from M. Kalkatawi et al. (2019) [4].
The 5’ capping process involves enzymatic addition of a 7-methyl guanosine (m7G) not
encoded in the DNA itself, to the 5’ end of the transcript [1]. The 5’ cap modification con-
sists of a series of enzymatic activities that take place in the early stage of transcriptional
elongation, typically when the pre-mRNA is around 25-30 nucleotides long [1][2][3]. The 5’
cap serves multiple important functions, including facilitating transport from the nucleus
to the cytoplasm, aiding translation initiation, and protecting the mRNA from degradation
by 5’→3’ exonucleases present in both the nucleus and cytoplasm [1][2][3].
Many eukaryotic pre-mRNA transcripts contain non-coding regions known as introns. Pre-
cise removal of introns is required for proper protein expression and occurs via splicing [2]. In
most eukaryotic organisms, splicing is catalyzed by the spliceosome, which consists of a com-
plex of small nuclear ribonucleoproteins along with additional proteins [2]. The intron-exon
1
==PAGE:10==
boundaries are usually identified by conserved sequences at the 5’ and 3’ ends of the introns,
known as splice sites (usually the dinucleotide GU at the 5’ end and the dinucleotide AG at
the 3’ end) [1]. Within the spliceosome, a series of interactions take place, such that intronic
regions are excised and exons joined together to produce the mature mRNA, see Figure 1 [2].
Many eukaryotic mRNA transcripts are subject to alternative splicing, a process in which
a single pre-mRNA undergoes differential exon joining events to yield different functional
mRNA variants, known as isoforms, and, consequently multiple distinct proteins [1][2]. This
process contributes complexity to the transcriptome and is particularly prevalent in or-
ganisms like humans, where it has been estimated that more than 95 % of multiple-exon
pre-mRNAs are subject to alternative splicing [1][5]. The human genome comprises approx-
imately 25.000 genes, from which approximately 90.000 different proteins are produced,
emphasizing the impact on genetic diversity that alternative splicing contributes [5]. Like
humans, other metazoans predominantly employ alternative splicing as a way to diversify
their gene products [1]. In contrast, unicellular eukaryotes exhibit limited use of alternative
splicing. For instance, the number of protein-coding genes in Saccharomyces cerevisiae is
similar to the actual number of resulting proteins [1].
Polyadenylation occurs at transcription termination and is the final step in pre-mRNA
processing. It involves the addition of multiple adenosine monophosphates, known as the
poly(A) tail, at the cleaved 3’ end of the transcript [1][2]. The poly(A) tail can reach lengths
of up to several hundred nucleotides in mammals and enhances RNA stability as well as
possesing an important role for translational efficiency of the mRNA [1][2].
1.2
Translation Initiation in Eukaroytes
1.2.1
The Cap-Dependent Pathway
Eukaryotic translation initiation is a highly regulated process that marks the beginning
of protein synthesis. It is orchestrated by various eukaryotic initiation factors (eIF) in
an energy-dependent environment. The canonical (cap-dependent) pathway of eukaryotic
translation initiation can be divided into 8 stages (see Figure 2, step 2-9), beginning with
the assembly of the 43S Pre-Initiation Complex (PIC) and culminating in the formation of
the functional 80S ribosome, which is essential for facilitation of protein synthesis [6].

1.2.2
IRES-Mediated Translation Initiation
The vast majority of eukaryotic translation initiation events occur via the cap-dependent
pathway. However, under certain conditions, mRNAs cannot be translated in the cap-
dependent fashion. For example, during cellular stress and viral infections, cap-dependent
translation is inhibited [10]. Additionally, some viral mRNAs do not contain a 5’ cap
structure, while they are still efficiently translated in their host cells [10]. In such cases, an
alternative, cap-independent mechanism driven by internal ribosome entry sites (IRES) is
often used. IRESs are structural elements found in the 5’ UTR of certain cellular or viral
mRNAs. They have been found to promote ribosome assembly and initiate translation by
recruiting different trans-acting factors, thereby removing the need for recognition of the 5’
cap [10].
5
==PAGE:14==
It has been demonstrated that the endogenous genes being capable of cap-independent
translation are usually involved in biological pathways that function in response to cellular
stress or viral infections, which implies that IRES-mediated translation plays an important
role under these cellular conditions [10].
1.3
Identification of Translation Initiation Sites
Thousands of proteins possessing important structural and regulatory roles are encoded in
eukaryotic genomes. A substantial amount of cellular energy is allocated to the transcrip-
tion of genes into mRNAs and their subsequent translation into proteins [11]. Errors in the
translation process can result in various dysfunctions in produced proteins, such as inactive
or deleterious proteins that misfold, aggregate or lack regulation, among others [11]. Thus,
it is critical that ribosomes initiate at the appropriate codon, such that the proper amino
acids are incorporated into the polypeptide chain. To ensure that the ribosome initiates
translation at the right position of an mRNA, the choice of start codon used for TIS is
carefully selected, and depends on position along the mRNA molecule (proximity to the
5’-end) as well as context [12]. Further, the presence of a secondary structure at or near
the TIS of an mRNA transcript can also decrease efficiency of recognition. In addition to
these cis-elements, the stringency of TIS selection is also regulated by trans-acting factors
such as eIF1 and eIF1A [13].
Historically, translational initiation have been described to most often take place at the
first occurring AUG in an mRNA transcript (corresponding to ATG in DNA), while a start
codon further downstream is selected in some cases instead [7][12][14][15]. This phenomenon
was explained by the scanning mechanism proposed by Marilyn Kozak in the early 1980s
[16][12][17]. According to the scanning hypothesis, the 43S PIC scans the mRNA in the
5’→3’ direction until it finds the first initiation codon in a favorable context for initiating
translation [12][14].
In the early years of investigation (around the 1980-1990s) the importance of position was
highly emphasized based on analyses of hundreds of vertebrate mRNA sequences, for which
translation was found to be initialized from the first AUG in 90-95 % of cases [12][15].
However, it has more recently been reported that approximately 40 % of the mRNAs
taken from GenBank include one or more upstream AUGs [18], whereas it has been found
that more than 50 % of all human mRNAs have at least one AUG upstream of the anno-
6
==PAGE:15==
tated TIS [14]. Additionally, M. Kumar et al. (2015) [19] conducted a study on the AT-rich
malaria parasite Plasmodium falciparum, in which computational methods were used to esti-
mate that 97.8 % of the parasite’s mRNAs contained at least one AUG upstream of the TIS.
In relation to the importance of the local context surrounding the start codon, it has
previously been demonstrated that 95 % of selected AUGs have an optimal context [20][21].
A weak first AUG start codon not in a proper context can induce leaky scanning, in which
the 40S ribosomal subunit can bypass this AUG, leading to translation initiation at a further
downstream start codon. This kind of occurence provides evidence that local context greatly
influences the choice of TIS, and is a common escape mechanism when an AUG triplet is
present in the 5’ UTR [8][21].
1.3.1
Patterns of Local Start Codon Context in Vertebrates
M. Kozak performed key studies on vertebrate mRNA with the purpose of characterizing
the optimal TIS consensus sequence. She established that the sequence GCCRCCAUGG
(R being a purine (A or G) and AUG being the start codon), known as the Kozak sequence,
is both the consensus sequence flanking the TIS and the one most optimal for translation
in vertebrates [9][14][17]. In particular, conservation of a purine at position -3 (relative to A
in the AUG start codon) and a G right downstream the initiating codon significantly con-
tributes to the efficiency of translation initiation in vertebrates, while the degree to which
the rest of the motif adheres varies [7][9][17][22]. Site-directed mutagenesis experiments on
vertebrate sequences have shown that translation decreases 5- to 10-fold when either an
A at position -3 or the G right downstream the initiating codon is replaced by C or U,
and 20-fold when pyrimidines (C or U) are substituted in at both positions. Further, it has
been demonstrated that particularly the presence of a purine at position -3 plays a crucial
role in TIS recognition, and in its absence, the G right downstream the initiating codon
significantly contributes in promoting translation [9][16]. The importance of conservation
of a purine at position -3 for efficient translation initiation was also emphasized by H.
L¨utcke et al. (1987) [23], who found that translational efficieny of an mRNA with presence
of an A, G, U and C at position -3 was 100 %, 85 %, 61 % and 38%, respectively, in a
vertebrate system [9]. Additionally, A. G. Pedersen and H. Nielsen (1997) [15] investigated
the importance of individual positions surrounding the TIS during development of NetStart
1.0, by leaving out data from an individual position at a time. The experiments showed
that predictive performance markedly decreased when the nucleotide at position -3 was
7
==PAGE:16==
left out, and slightly decreased, when the nucleotide at the position right downstream the
initiating codon was left out.
Experimentally, the interaction between purine bases at positions -3 and +4 (in which
the initiating codon begins at position +1) and the translational machinery have been
demonstrated by A. V. Pisarev et al. (2006) [24], in which interactions between an A at
position -3 and the α subunit of eIF2 were shown, as well as between a G right downstream
the initiating codon with ribosomal protein S9 (rpS9) and 18S rRNA.
1.3.2
Conservation and Variability of Local Start Codon Context in Various
Eukaryotic Groups
The mRNA transcripts used for deriving the Kozak sequence were primarily of vertebrate
origin, as noted above. Subsequent analyses of phylogenetically distant, non-vertebrate
phyla have suggested that signals can differ considerably among distinct eukaryotic groups
[9][15][25][26]. S. Nakagawa et al. (2008) [25] conducted a comprehensive comparative anal-
ysis of nucleotide sequences surrounding initiation codons, utilizing data from 47 eukaryotic
species, which encompassed both animals, fungi, plants, and protists. From the systematic
investigation of sequence motifs in this broad spectrum of eukaryotes, it was observed that
the purine base at position -3 was universally conserved among phylogenetically distant
groups. Additionally, the study found a strong bias for either A or C at position -2, as
well as a pronounced preference of C two positions downstream the initiating codon across
the entirety of the 47 species. Furthermore, G. Hern´andez et al. (2019) [9] later revisited
published experimental studies as well as in silico data on TIS consensus sequences from
diverse eukaryotic lineages, with the conclusions being in accordance with those of S. Naka-
gawa et al. (2008) [25]. Besides these above-mentioned nucleotide positions, the remaining
positions surrounding the initiation codon differ to a greater degree when comparing dis-
tinct eukaryotic groups. Several analyses emphasize that while the preferred nucleotide
sequences are generally quite diverse among different species, the differences between pat-
terns of nucleotide bias roughly reflect the evolutionary relationships of a given species
[9][25][26]. In Figure 3, the most conserved positions of the local TIS context as concluded
by G. Hern´andez et al. (2019) [9] are shown for various systematic groups of eukaryotes.

1.4
Non-AUG Translation Initiation
It was long thought that eukaryotic translation almost always initiates at an AUG triplet in
the right context, as defined by the scanning hypothesis [12]. However, ribosome profiling
techniques (deep sequencing of ribosome-protected mRNA footprints as a readout of trans-
lation) at TISs have revealed that translation initiation in eukaryotes is considerably more
complex, showing that non-AUG initiation arises rather frequently and that the selection of
start codon is highly regulated by the cellular levels of different initiation factors [13][14][27].
While functional non-AUG codons used for translation initiation are more frequent than
previously appreciated, it should be emphasized that they typically perform at a markedly
reduced efficiency compared to AUG codons [11].
1.4.1
Functional Groups of Non-AUG Translation Initiation
The major functional groups of non-AUG initiation can be seen in Figure 4. One group
includes Proteoforms with Alternative N-Termini (PANTs), encompassing initiation events
arising from a non-AUG TIS located in the same reading frame as the main AUG-initiated

is VEGFA (encoding vascular endothelial growth factors), whose extended proteoform is
secreted, initiating at an upstream in frame CUG codon [14]. Generally, PANTs with
non-AUG TISs located upstream of main AUG TISs are more frequent than PANTs
with non-AUG TISs located downstream. This is as expected according to the scanning
mechanism, as fewer PICs would be available for non-AUG initiation downstream of AUG

It is likely that more novel proteins as well as proteoforms of known proteins are yet to be
discovered as a result of non-AUG initiation of the main ORF. However, it is evident that
the substantial majority of functional non-AUG initiation leads to translation upstream the
main TIS, introducing translation of upstream open reading frames (uORFs). Translation
of uORFs usually result in synthesis of short polypeptides. Most often, uORFs are shorter
than 100 codons and referred to as small ORFs (sORFs) [27]. uORFs are encoded in RNA
regions outside the annotated protein coding regions, which in some cases overlap with
the main ORF, and in other cases are placed completely in the 5’ UTR [11][13][14][27].
Sequence-based computational analyses have predicted that about 50 % of mammalian
transcripts contain at least one uORF [13][14]. Strikingly, it has been estimated that up to
approximately half of detected uORFs initiate at a codon alternative to AUG [27].
Historically, eukaryotic coding uORFs have evaded discovery through traditional genomic
tools, in part because they are often shorter than standard gene annotation length cutoffs,
but also due to an enrichment in sequence properties previously assumed to be unusual, such
as initiation at non-AUG start codons [11][27]. Even though many reported uORFs remain
uncharacterized, some of them are known to represent cis-translational regulators, rather
than encoding functional microproteins. An increasing number of uORFs have nevertheless
been demonstrated to play significant roles in biological processes, such as regulation of
protein complexes, membrane channels, and transcription factors, among others [27].
Overlapping uORFs are located in a different reading frame than that of the main ORF (see
Figure 4). Initiation of overlapping uORFs usually lead to quite distinct proteoforms. An
example is found for the gene POLG, which encodes the catalytic subunit of mitochondrial
DNA polymerase. Initiation at an out of frame CUG located upstream of the POLG Coding
Sequence (CDS), results in the long protein POLGARF (260 amino acids in humans). The
complete function of POLGARF is not clear, but there is evidence that it may contribute
in extracellular signalling [14][29].
Non-overlapping uORFs are encoded entirely in the 5’ UTR of mRNAs, frequently func-
tioning as cis-translational regulators (see Figure 4) [27]. Near-cognate1 translation of a
non-overlapping uORF has a complex relationship to translation of the downstream gene.

neurodegenerative diseases, such as Huntington’s disease and fragile X-associated tremor
ataxia syndrome, among others [14][27].
1.5
The Role of Initiation Factors in Start Codon Recognition
Alterations in eIF stoichiometry is known to influence TIS recognition patterns. In a study
carried out by D. Barth-Baus et al. (2013) [31], a reporter mRNA from rabbit reticulocyte
lysates able to initiate from both an AUG and an upstream CUG start codon showed that
the addition of eIF1 and eIF1A strengthened start codon fidelity, resulting in less initiation
at the CUG triplet and more initiation at the AUG. In contrast to this observation, adding
additional eIF5 and eIF5B loosened start codon fidelity, resulting in more initiation at
the CUG codon [11][31]. Additionally, haploinsufficiency of wild-type eIF1 or mutations
in eIF1 have been found to increase non-AUG initiation in yeast cells [32]. Consistently,
overexpression of eIF1 in mammalian cells have been shown to inhibit initiation from
non-AUG codons [11]. All of these findings particularly highlight the importance of eIF1
expression in recognition of main AUG-initiated translation.

1.6
Language Modelling With Biological Sequences
Language models (LMs) can be described as models that track the grammatical and se-
mantic relationships between tokens, such as words in a sentence, assigning a probability to
unseen tokens based on some training data [33][34]. These models serve as a fundamental
component in Natural Language Processing (NLP), a field that has undergone a signifi-
cant evolution throughout recent years, and integrates linguistics and machine learning to
comprehensively understand human language. NLP tasks involve not only understanding
individual words but also the contextual relationships between them [34].
Similar to the contextual relationships and diverse patterns found in natural languages,
biological sequences, such as DNA, RNA and protein sequences contain complex and con-
voluted information. Consequently, approaches for modelling biological sequence data has
greatly benefited from deep learning techniques originally developed for NLP [35].
1.6.1
The Transformer
For many years, Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) with Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
architecture have been established as state-of-the-art approaches for prediction tasks on
sequence data. However, the introduction of the Transformer architecture by A. Vaswani et
al. (2017) [36] has profoundly changed the landscape of sequence analysis and modelling. By
employing a self-attention mechanism, the transformer excels at capturing context across
different positions in a sequence, surpassing the capabilities of RNNs. Additionally, the
transformer architecture has significantly increased parallelization capabilities, leading to
more efficient training compared to RNNs [36].
The complete transformer network is characterized by several sequential layers with identical
design, consisting of two main components, namely an encoder and a decoder [35][36].
Both the encoder and the decoder utilize stacked self-attention layers and point-wise, fully
connected layers [36]. The encoder processes the input through several encoder blocks,
resulting in a contextualized representation of the input sequence. Subsequently, the decoder
takes these encoded representations and generates an output sequence [36].

Preparation of Inputs
Before sequences can be fed into the encoder, they need to be represented in a numer-
ical format that the model understands. Initially, individual sequences are tokenized by
dividing them into smaller components based on specific criteria or rules. For instance,
protein sequences could be tokenized such that each amino acid corresponds to a token.
The input tokens are then converted into embeddings, which are the vector representations
of dimension dmodel of each respective token in a vocabulary [36]. The embeddings are
learned during training, aiding the model in comprehending the meanings and relationships
between tokens. Consequently, similar tokens are often placed close to each other in em-
bedding space, enabling models to better generalize based on context [39].
In contrast to recurrent networks, transformer models do not have a natural notion about
the order of tokens in an input sequence. Information on relative positions in a sequence is
dealt with by adding positional encodings to the input embedding vectors. The positional
encodings are vectors with values that follow a specific pattern, such as sine and cosine
functions of different frequencies [36][39]. Additionally, some models employ positional
encodings that are learned rather than being fixed [36]. Positional encodings have the same
dimension dmodel as the embeddings, and the two are thus summed at the bottom of the
encoder, see Figure 5 [36].

in which a model is trained in a self-supervised setting on extensive amounts of unlabelled
data, initializing weights randomly and initiating training without prior knowledge [40].
This unlabelled data could comprise sentences or biological sequences, for example. The
approach of LM pretraining greatly benefits learning of biological languages without the
need for prior annotation, and is particularly convenient considering the vast amounts of
unlabelled biological sequences available. During pretraining, for instance with a model
training on amino acid sequences, the model learns to predict the subsequent amino acid
in a sequence, or amino acids that are randomly masked out. [40].

1.7
History of Translation Initiation Site Prediction
Over the years, various computational methods have been developed for accurately pre-
dicting TISs in eukaryotic organisms. In 1997, A. G. Pedersen and H. Nielsen introduced
NetStart 1.0 [15], utilizing a feedforward neural network with one hidden layer. The net-
work employed a combination of local start codon context and global sequence information,
aiming to identify AUG TISs of main ORFs in vertebrate species and Arabidopsis thaliana
[15][48].
In 2017, Zhang et al. developed TITER (Translation Initiation site detectoR) [49], a deep
learning-based framework for predicting TISs both in main ORFs and in sORFs initiated
20
==PAGE:29==
at either AUG or non-AUG codons in humans [49]. Utilizing an ensemble of hybrid deep
convolutional and recurrent neural networks, TITER processed transcript sequences of fixed
length around the TIS, emphasizing local sequence context. TITER also incorporated prior
preference of TIS codon composition to predict non-AUG start codons [49].
In 2019, Kalkatawi et al. developed DeepGSR [4]. DeepGSR employed convolutional neural
networks to predict various genomic signals and regions including TISs, in the eukaryotic
species Homo sapiens, Mus musculus, Bos taurus and Drosophila melanogaster [4]. In 2022,
DeepGenGrep was developed by Q. Liu et al. (2022) [50], also aiming at predicting differ-
ent types of genomic signals and regions, including TISs, using the datasets prepared for
DeepGSR. DeepGenGrep employed a combination of convolutional layers and recurrent
layers with LSTM architecture [50].
In 2020, Goel et al. developed a model for TIS prediction of AUG TISs in main ORFs
based on human genomic sequences [18]. This model utilized a combined feature extraction
method with a support vector machine using both global and local sequence-based features
[18].
Recently, Clauwaert et al. introduced TIS Transformer [35] (2023). Employing self-attention
and the transformer architecture, TIS Transformer was trained from scratch on human
transcript sequences from the entire human genome, and exclusively utilizes the information
embedded in the transcript nucleotide sequence to predict TISs in human transcripts. TIS
Transformer uses full processed transcripts as input, excluding introns and intergenic regions
[35]. The model was trained to detect multiple coding sequences on a transcript, including
micropeptides encoded by sORFs, either alongside a canonical coding sequence or within
long non-coding RNAs [35].
1.8
Data Redundancy
In the context of developing machine learning models for biological sequence data, it is
vital to consider sequence homology when partitioning data into subsets. This is due to the
evolutionary or functional relationships between sequences, which make them dependent of
one another [51]. Consequently, biological datasets often exhibit redundancy. When working
with redundant data, the assumption of independence among datapoints across randomly
split train, validation and test partitions does not hold. Randomly splitting the data can
21
==PAGE:30==
thus cause highly similar sequences to be distributed across different partitions, which could
result in an overestimation of the model’s performance. In such a case, the model would be
evaluated on sequences highly similar to those used for training the model itself, and thus
not reflect the model’s ability to generalize to unseen sequences [15].
1.8.1
Dealing with Data Redundancy
One way to deal with redundant biological sequence data involves homology reduction.
In this approach, closely related sequence pairs are removed from the dataset before par-
titioning the data into subsets [51]. While homology reduction reduces redundancy in a
dataset, it may also remove sequences that could have contributed valuable information to
the model. In contrast, the approach of using homology partition [51], aims to come around
this issue by allowing closely related sequences in the final dataset, as long as they are
grouped in the same partition. F. Teufel et al. (2023) [51] defines the problem of homology
partitioning as follows: Given a set of sequences, a maximum similarity threshold and the
number of partitions to generate, an assignment of sequences to partitions should be found
so that (i) no sequence in a partition has identity above the threshold to any sequence in
another partition, and (ii) as many sequences as possible are retained in the partitioned
dataset overall.
1.9
Principal Component Analysis
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is a multivariate statistical method for reducing
dimensionality and thus complexity of a dataset, while preserving as much information
from the original dataset as possible. By concentrating the information in fewer variables,
it becomes easier to detect patterns in data [52]. The dimensionality reduction is accom-
plished by creating a new basis constituted by uncorrelated, orthogonal variables called
Principal Components (PCs), which are constructed in such a way that the chosen change
of basis ensures as much information as possible to be kept within the first PC, decreasing
monotonically [52].
Standardizing variables to have a mean of 0 and a standard deviation of 1 is common
practice prior to performing PCA. This is because PCA is very sensitive to different scales
and will prioritize variables with a large variance, in order to preserve as much information
within as few PCs as possible [53].

expected value for a random classifier [56].

dependent on the class distribution in a dataset and can thus cause misleading comparisons
between datasets with varying levels of class imbalance. Hence, the MCC does not describe
the binary classifier objectively, but rather the performance for a given dataset with a given
imbalance [57].

The Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve and AUROC
The Receiver Operating Characteristic (ROC) curve depicts the trade-off between FPR and
the TPR at various decision thresholds. Each point on the curve corresponds to a specific
threshold, and the TPR and FPR are calculated across multiple thresholds to form the
complete ROC curve [58]. By interpolating the points on the ROC curve, the Area Under
the ROC Curve (AUROC) is derived, providing a singular metric that reflects the overall
performance of a binary classifier. The AUROC thus demonstrates how well the classifier
distinguishes between positive and negative classes across all thresholds [58][59].
The Precision-Recall Curve and AUPRC
The Precision-Recall (PR) curve illustrates the relationship between the precision and recall
of a binary classifier. Similar to the ROC curve, each point on the PR curve corresponds to
a particular decision threshold, and the Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (AUPRC)
is calculated as the area beneath this curve [58]. The AUPRC assesses the model’s overall
performance in correctly identifying positive instances while minimizing false positives and
negatives [59].
24
==PAGE:33==
Applying ROC and PR to Class-Imbalanced Datsets
While the ROC curve employs all four categories of the confusion matrix, the PR curve
focuses on precision instead of the FPR, thereby not emphasizing true negatives [58]. The
ROC curve is established to be invariant to class imbalance, and provides a universal,
random baseline of AUROC = 0.5, whereas lower AUROCs indicate misclassification, and
higher AUROCs indicate better classification abilities. In contrast, the random baseline for
AUPRC is equal to the imbalance of the dataset [58].
Despite the establishment that ROC curves are invariant to class imbalance, the PR curve
and AUPRC has widely been recommended over ROC when having highly imbalanced
datasets [58]. However, through careful mathematical and logical reasonings, as well as
simulations, novel preprints by McDermott et al. (January 2024) [59] and Richardson et
al. (December 2023) [58] challenge this interpretation. The claim that AUPRC is better
than AUROC in cases of class imbalance is widespread in literature [58][59]. Richardson
et al. [58] argue that the common recommendation of using PR over ROC for imbalanced
datasets is the result of a misunderstanding, with the PR curve’s use of precision making it
reflect a classifier’s performance on a specific dataset with its specific imbalance, but being
limited to generalize across distinct datasets. Through extensive automated and manual
reviews on arXiv papers, McDermott et al. [59] finds that this claim is frequently either
being stated without citation or being attributed to papers that do not make the claim
in the first place, and further, that papers making variations of the claim are frequently
misunderstood, mis-cited or outright wrong [59]. McDermott et al. [59] also argues that the
AUPRC can be significantly more indicative of a classifier’s precision compared to AUROC,
whereas the major advantage of AUROC lies in its capacity to offer a more generalizable,
unbiased assessment of performance.
In this project, model performance is assessed in terms of multiple metrics in order to get
as complete an evaluation as possible, including both AUROC and AUPRC.

The RefSeq database was chosen as the source of data, as it provides a comprehensive

2.3.4
Construction of the Non-TIS-Labelled Datasets
The non-TIS-labelled dataset consists of 3 types of sequences in which a non-TIS ATG was
labelled, namely 1) mature mRNA with ATGs upstream or downstream of the TIS, 2) intron
sequences and 3) sequences from intergenic regions. All sequences in the non-TIS-labelled
dataset were extracted based on a window size of 200 nucleotides upstream and downstream
of the labelled non-TIS ATG, respectively, resulting in sequence samples of 403 nucleotides.
As with the TIS ATG-labelled dataset, sequences with one or more unknown nucleotides
were removed. Along the way, several checks were carried out to ensure that sequences were
extracted properly and that a given non-TIS ATG had been labelled accordingly. Below,
the specific approaches for constructing datasets for each type of sequence will be detailed.
Mature mRNA with Non-TIS ATG as Label
One type of sequence extracted for the non-TIS-labelled dataset includes data from the
mature mRNA, but where non-TIS ATGs were labelled rather than the ATG at the TIS
(see Figure 9B). The mRNA sequences were individually extracted in the same way as
done for the TIS-labelled dataset (see section 2.3.3). The TIS in the mRNA transcript was
identified, to be able to locate and label upstream and downstream ATGs, respectively.
All ATGs upstream of the TIS in every transcript where the 5’ UTR was known (and
35
==PAGE:44==
not estimated) were extracted and made into separate samples, extracting 200 nucleotides
upstream and downstream, respectively, of the individual ATG, and labelling that ATG
as a non-TIS sample. The decision of including all ATGs upstream of the TIS for each
transcript, was made as it is critical that the model efficiently learns to distinguish an
upstream ATG from a downstream TIS. The ATG at TIS is frequently one of the first
present in a transcript, and therefore learning the context surrounding such ATGs is very
important. In all transcripts containing ATGs downstream the TIS, we randomly picked
out one downstream in frame ATG and one downstream out of frame ATG (comparing
to the reading frame of the TIS ATG), and made them into samples in the same way as
described for the upstream ATGs.
Introns
For the intron part of the non-TIS-labelled dataset, the intron-exon boundaries in a gene
were located from the exon coordinates, and individual introns were extracted. For every
transcript stored in the nested dictionary (see Table 3), the process involved the overall
steps (see Figure 9C):
1. The set of intron coordinates in a gene was stored, and 1 intron sequence was randomly
picked out.
2. All ATG indices in the randomly selected intron sequence were located and 1 ATG
index was randomly chosen.
3. A segment of the transcript comprising 200 nucleotides upstream and downstream,
respectively, of the chosen ATG in the intron was extracted.
Sequences placed on the complement strand were reversed and taken the complement of,
prior to locating ATG indices. Our goal was to ensure a balanced dataset with approxi-
mately as many samples from intron sequences as there were TIS-labelled samples for each
species. This was ensured by extracting one intron sample per transcript as detailed above.
Additionally, this approach ensured that intron sequences across the entire genome were
picked out. Some eukaryotic species, particularly some protozoans such as Trypanosomes
and Leishmania species have very few genes that contain introns [65][66][67]. For such
species, the few intron sequences available were extracted.
36
==PAGE:45==
Intergenic Regions
Sequences were collected from intergenic regions based on the coordinates of annotated
gene positions. Intergenic regions were defined as the sequence between the end coordinate
of one gene, and the start coordinate of the next gene. The overall process is visualized in
Figure 9D, and involved the steps:
1. For each intergenic region, all ATG codon indices were located within the sequence.
2. 2 ATG indices were randomly chosen within each intergenic region.
3. Once the ATG indices were selected, a segment comprising 200 nucleotides upstream
and downstream, respectively, of each of the chosen ATGs was extracted.
As with the intron samples, we wanted the number of intergenic samples to approximately
correspond to the number of TIS-labelled samples per species. In cases where more than
twice as many intergenic samples were collected, we randomly selected a subset of these
to match the number of TIS ATG-labelled samples. The choice of 2 ATG indices picked
out per intergenic region was made to ensure a balance between obtaining an adequate
number of intergenic samples while avoiding extracting unnecessary sequences that would
later be discarded. Another important aspect of this choice is that we wanted the extracted
sequences to be spread across the entire genome, which was ensured by not picking out
too many samples per intergenic region, thus maintaining a representative distribution of
intergenic regions throughout the genome.

3.1
Analysis of Local Start Codon Context
Prior to developing NetStart 2.0, the local start codon context was analysed systematically,

3.1.2
Construction of Distance-Based Tree
In order to directly encapture the relationships among species in regards to their local start
codon contexts, a Neighbor-Joining (NJ) tree was constructed on the basis of positions
comprising the defined start codon context (20 nucleotides upstream and 10 nucleotides
downstream of the TIS) in each of the 60 species. As the data comprises a lot of sequences
from each species and we wanted to make a representation on a species-level, the overall
approach was to (1) calculate nucleotide frequencies at each position of the local start
codon context across all sequences for each species, (2) correct the nucleotide composition
at each position for background frequencies, (3) calculate Aitchison distances of Centered
Log-Ratio (CLR)-transformed nucleotide frequencies at each position between each pair of
species and (4) sum Aitchison distances at each position to construct a 60 × 60 distance
matrix that is used for (5) constructing a distance-based tree using the NJ algorithm. Steps
1 and 2 are described in Section 3.1.1 above, and steps 3-5 will be detailed below.
Performing CLR-Transformation and Calculating Aitchison Distance

frequencies of the 4 nucleotides at position i, and is thus compositional. The compositional
nature signifies that nucleotide frequencies are not independent. For example, if the fre-

decrease consequently [69]. Due to the nucleotide frequencies’ dependency upon each other,
calculating distances directly between compositions can be very misleading [69]. Thus, in
order to quantify the distance in local codon context patterns between each pair of species,
the Aitchison distance was calculated between two species for each respective position of
the site-specific frequency matrices.

Selection of Species for Machine Learning Dataset
Initially, we wanted to include all the extracted sequences from the 60 species in the dataset
for training the models. However, due to time constraints in terms of the time it takes to
partition the dataset and train the model, we decided to reduce the datasets for training
the model, using only a subset of species. The selection of species was based on the results
obtained from the analysis of the local start codon context (see results in Section 4.1).
As emphasized, most vertebrates have highly similar start codon contexts, and for that

3.3
Homology Partitioning
The extracted datasets were comprised of various sequence types, including highly similar
sequences, such as genes belonging to the same gene family, mRNA splice variants of the
same gene, or homologous genes from different organisms. This intrinsic similarity intro-
duces redundancy in our data [15][51]. As mentioned in Section 1.8, randomly splitting
the data poses a risk of introducing a bias and potentially overestimating the model’s
performance due to this redundancy [15].
To address the issue of data redundancy, we employed the homology partitioning algorithm
GraphPart [51] for partitioning the data prior to training the model. GraphPart is a graph
clustering algorithm that divides a dataset into a user-defined number of partitions, ensur-
ing that closely related sequences are consistently placed within the same partition while
retaining as many sequences as possible in the dataset [51]. Briefly outlined, the algorithm
operates by performing pairwise alignment of sequences, finding and merging clusters and
then iteratively reassigning and removing sequences until a criterion of complete separation
is achieved, based on a user-defined threshold [51]. Additionally, GraphPart takes into con-
sideration the distribution of class labels to ensure that class-labelled sequences are evenly
distributed across all partitions [51].
To align the sequences using GraphPart, we chose MMseqs2 as the alignment program [75].
When applying GraphPart with MMseqs2 to nucleotide data, MMseqs2 uses a prefiltering
step [51]. This step involves comparing k-mers between sequences, with only the highest-
scoring pairs being selected for pairwise alignment. This filtering reduces the number of
43
==PAGE:52==
sequences that need to be aligned, effectively decreasing computation time [76].
A pairwise sequence identity of 50 % was chosen as threshold for partitioning the data,
such that no pair of sequences with a higher sequence identity would end up in different
partitions [51].
Before running GraphPart, the sequences were prepared in fasta format, which GraphPart
takes as input. We extracted a subsequence from each TIS-labelled sequence consisting
of 403 nucleotides with 200 nucleotides upstream and downstream the labelled ATG, re-
spectively, correspondng to the extracted length of all non-TIS-labelled samples. For each
sequence, the species name was labelled to facilitate an even distribution of sequences
from each species across the partitions. We created separate fasta files for each of the four
sequence types (see Figure 9A-D). We further split the non-TIS-labelled mRNA sequences
into two fastafiles, respectively containing sequences upstream and downstream the TIS.
In our dataset, we have included all splice variants of a given gene within each species.
During the extraction of the non-TIS mRNA sequences, we ended up with some duplicates,
caused by some splice variants having the same TIS. Specifically, our approach involved
extracting all ATGs situated upstream of the TIS for each splice variant. Consequently,
this process introduced multiple duplicate upstream ATGs within the dataset for each
individual species. Furthermore, certain genes have multiple TISs due to varying splice
variants. Due to complexities in annotation of these variants, some ATGs were erroneously
labelled both as a non-TIS and as a TIS, despite their functional status as a TIS. For such
cases, we removed all sequences labelled with a non-TIS ATG and kept sequences with a
TIS-labelled ATG.
GraphPart was then run separately on the datasets for each of the 5 fastafiles. Each dataset
for a specific sequence type was divided into k = 5 partitions, and a partition from each
sequence type was randomly concatenated to create complete partitions. With this ap-
proach, we assumed that sequences of the five distinct sequence types would not exceed
the sequence similarity threshold.

twofold. Firstly, we wanted to ensure an approximately even distribution of sequences in

3.4
Modelling
3.4.1
Model Architecture
The aim of the model developed in this project, NetStart 2.0, is to predict ATG-labelled
TISs within sequences of diverse species across the eukaryotic domain. The modelling
objective can thus be described as a binary classification problem, in which the purpose is
to predict whether an ATG is a TIS or not.
Figure 10: A conceptual representation of the features emphasized by individual windows
of the model, exclusively based on the input organism and sequence.
The model architecture comprises three primary windows, each designed to extract different
kinds of relevant information for the prediction of TISs, as conceptually illustrated in Figure
10. As shown in the figure, the model’s predictions rely exclusively on knowledge of the
input organism, and the information embedded in the input sequence itself. Each window
processes input independently:
• A window for representing taxonomical levels of a given species (Fig. 10, purple
window),
46

• A global sequence window for identifying shifts from non-coding to coding regions
(Fig. 10, blue window),
• A local sequence window for detecting patterns of the local start codon context (Fig.
10, green window).
A high-level overview of the full model architecture is depicted in Figure 11. The model
is designed to take two inputs: an organism name and an mRNA sequence. It processes
these inputs through the three above-mentioned windows, and the representations from
each window are subsequently combined in a downstream feedforward network.
Briefly explained, the organism is represented by incorporating taxonomic embeddings
based on 7 taxonomic levels, each with the same dimension. These embeddings are summed
position-wise, resulting in the organism embedding. The input mRNA sequence is used for
two purposes: Firstly, a nucleotide subsequence capturing the local window around an ATG
is tokenized and encoded by NT-MS. This generates a contextualized representation of the
start codon context pattern, which is subsequently fed into a feedforward layer. Secondly, a
longer nucleotide subsequence representing the global window around an ATG is encoded
into its corresponding amino acid sequence. The amino acid sequence is tokenized and
processed by ESM-2, generating a contextualized representation of the amino acid sequence,
which is also fed into a feedforward layer.
The two separate feedforward layers placed downstream NT-MS and ESM-2, respectively,
are employed to reduce the embedding dimensionality and aid in more balanced feature
extraction from the three windows. The resulting embeddings from the three windows are
then concatenated and fed through another feedforward layer. Rectified Linear Unit (ReLU)
was applied as activation function in the feedforward layers, which is the identity function
f(x) = x for positive input and 0 for negative input [77]. The output from the feedforward
layer is directed to a binary classification layer using sigmoid activation, which converts any
real-valued number into a number between 0 and 1, making it ideal for assessing a model’s
prediction in terms of probability. The sigmoid activation function is given as [77]:

Identification of Shifts From Non-Coding to Coding Regions
As mentioned, the model integrates a global sequence window (Fig. 11, green window) with
the aim of identifying shifts in a sequence from non-coding to coding regions, investigat-
ing whether subsequences upstream or downstream of an ATG could be translated into
protein-like structures. For this purpose, the pretrained protein LM, ESM-2, with 8 mil-

A pre-specified window of nucleotides (see Section 3.4.3) surrounding the labelled ATG
in an input sequence was extracted and translated into the corresponding amino acid
sequence. The amino acid sequences were tokenized, employing the tokenizer designed for
ESM-2 models. Stop codons were encoded by an underscore character and represented as
an unknown token. The tokenized sequence was then used as input for ESM-2, in which
each token is represented by an embedding vector of dimension 320 (see Section 1.6.4).

the ESM-2 encoder model (see Figure 5). As the dimension of the contextualized sequence
representation is rather large (corresponding to the number of tokens multiplied by hidden

network to reduce embedding dimensionality before combining information from each of
the three windows.
Detection of Local Start Codon Context
In addition to detecting shifts from non-coding to coding regions, the model also incor-
porates a local sequence window with emphasis on learning the local start codon context
surrounding a TIS (Fig. 11, blue window). For this purpose, we employed the pretrained
DNA LM, NT-MS, with 50 million parameters and 12 layers, for encoding the nucleotide
sequences (see section 1.6.4).
A small, pre-defined window of nucleotides (see Section 3.4.3) surrounding the labelled
ATG in an input sequence was extracted. Subsequently, the nucleotide sequences were
tokenized using the tokenizer designed for NT-MS. The tokenized sequences served as input
for NT-MS, in which each token was represented by a 512-dimensional embedding vector.
Similar to the process with ESM-2, we obtained a contextualized sequence representation
of the nucleotide sequence by extracting the last hidden state from the NT-MS encoder
model. We then fed the NT-MS-encoded embeddings into a feedforward network to reduce
embedding dimensionality.

in development by treating their embeddings as missing values, following the taxonomic
classification as far down the tree as possible.
3.4.2
Training Setup
As the model solely concerns prediction on whether a given ATG is a TIS or not, the
classification problem is binary, and Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) was used as the loss
function during training. In our case, the BCE measures the dissimilarity between the
actual labels (0 for non-TIS, 1 for TIS) and the predicted probabilities of the datapoints
belonging to the TIS-labelled class. Importantly, it penalizes predictions that are confident
50
==PAGE:59==
but wrong [79]. The BCE loss for a given sample is calculated as:
L(y, ˆy) = −(y · log(ˆy) + (1 −y) · log(1 −ˆy)),
(21)
with y being the actual label (0 for non-TIS, 1 for TIS) and ˆy being the predicted probability
of a datapoint belonging to the TIS-labelled class.
We used the Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation) algorithm as optimizer, which employs
stochastic optimization using first-order gradients. Adam computes individual adaptive
learning rates for different parameters based on each parameter’s gradient history, which
enables faster convergence compared to standard gradient descent employing a constant
and global learning rate [80].
Cross Validation Setup
Figure 12: Cross validation setup for training and testing the model.
As mentioned, GraphPart was used for splitting the data into 5 partitions to make sure that
no pair of sequences with a pairwise sequence identity above 50 % would end up in different
partitions. The 5 partitions were split into 3 subsets, including (1) a training set used
to optimize model parameters, (2) a validation set for hyperparameter optimization and
(3) a test set to assess the final model’s performance. We employed 4-fold unnested cross
validation, in which one partition served as the overall test set, whereas four models were
trained on the remaining four partitions, each utilizing a different partition as the validation
set, see Figure 12. The outlined approach allowed us to iteratively train and validate
models on distinct subsets of the data, resulting in a more comprehensive assessment of the
model’s performance across various data distributions. The final model was implemented
as an ensemble of the 4 models trained on different data subsets, averaging the predicted
51
==PAGE:60==
probabilities from each model. The test set (partition 5) was then used for quantifying how
well the model generalized to unseen data [51].
Dealing With Overfitting
Overfitting occurs when a model starts fitting noise in training examples rather than general
patterns, resulting in decreased performance on unseen data [51]. To reduce overfitting,
early stopping was implemented in the training process, monitoring the BCE loss on the
validation set. The training was stopped when the performance on the validation set started
decreasing monotonically for a pre-specified number of steps [81]. Additionally, dropout
was applied to each of the feedforward layers, in which a predefined fraction of neurons and
their connections are randomly dropped during each training iteration [82].
3.4.3
Training
The training procedure was divided into three stages:
• Fine-tuning ESM-2 and NT-MS separately,
• Hyperparameter tuning,
• End-to-end training with fine-tuned LMs and optimized hyperparameters.
The below sections detail the complete training process.
Fine-tuning
Both ESM-2 and NT-MS were fine-tuned separately prior to tuning hyperparameters and
training the full model end-to-end. For fine-tuning each LM, we simply added a classifi-
cation head directly downstream of ESM-2 and NT-MS, respectively. These models were
then trained on our labelled datasets, allowing the pretrained LMs to adapt specifically
to the task of predicting TISs. We employed the training setup described in Section 3.4.2
excluding dropout, as this is applied to the feedforward layers further downstream in the full
model. The classification head was then removed from both ESM-2 and NT-MS and both
of the fine-tuned encoder models were saved. The fine-tuning procedure was independently
executed for each of the 4 training and validation set combinations (see Figure 12), resulting
in distinct and adapted models corresponding to each data split.
52
==PAGE:61==
The approach of initially fine-tuning ESM-2 and NT-MS separately, was taken to avoid
potentially, highly increased computational time as well as issues that might arise from
simultaneously fine-tuning while performing hyperparameter optimization.
Hyperparameter Tuning
Machine learning models, including neural networks, are powered not only by the data they
are trained on but also their related hyperparameters. Hyperparameters are configuration
settings whose values cannot be estimated from data [83]. Given their potentially significant
influence on a model’s learning ability, tuning these parameters to find optimal values is
an important task prior to training the final model [83]. For this purpose, we employed
Optuna [84], a specialized hyperparameter optimization framework particularly designed
for machine learning workflows, including PyTorch-based models. Utilizing Optuna, we
defined an objective and specified ranges of values for the hyperparameters, from which
the parameter search space was dynamically constructed [84].
Optuna adopts various state-of-the-art algorithms for sampling hyperparameters and prun-
ing unpromising trials, which in concert greatly improves the cost-effectiveness of opti-
mization [84]. We defined the BCE loss of the validation set as the objective for Optuna.
Employing the default hyperparameter sampler, the Tree-structured Parzen estimator al-
gorithm, and the default search space pruner, the Hyperband algorithm, 50 trials were
conducted. The specific hyperparameters that were tuned, along with their descriptions
and value ranges to optimize from, are shown in Table 6. The ranges in number of extracted
nucleotides respectively upstream and downstream an ATG were purposely selected to
be small, as we wanted the nucleotide-encoded window to focus on the patterns of the
local start codon contexts. Similarly, the ranges in number of amino acids upstream and
downstream an ATG (translated into Methionine) were chosen to be larger, as we wanted
the amino acid-encoded window to focus on global sequence patterns related to identifying
shifts from non-coding to coding regions.

3.7.1
Considerations in Benchmark Against TIS Transformer
Due to the way TIS Transformer is developed, we took a few considerations into account
in order to get as unbiased a benchmark evaluation as possible:
• As TIS Transformer is trained only on human sequences, a separate analysis exclusively
on the human sequences from the test set was carried out.
• As TIS Transformer is trained to detect multiple coding sequences on a transcript,
including micropeptides encoded by sORFs, predictions on all sequences with an
upstream, non-TIS-labelled ATG were excluded.
It should be noted that there also exist some downstream sORFs, but we decided to keep the
sequences with a downstream non-TIS labelled ATG in the benchmark for various reasons.
Firstly, these are generally much less frequent than upstream sORFs [27][35]. Secondly,
the way downstream non-TIS ATG-labelled sequences were extracted included randomly
selecting one sequence in frame and one sequence out of frame, relative to the TIS ATG
for each TIS-labelled sequence. Thus, as we only extracted a minor, random subsection of
possible ATGs downstream the annotated TIS, the likelihood of that ATG being the TIS
of a downstream sORF is quite small, but may nevertheless introduce a slight bias in the
benchmark study. It should also be emphasized that TIS Transformer is developed to take
full transcripts as input, while our test set is based on sequences each with a length of 403
nucleotides.
Another bias is introduced in the benchmark study as TIS Transformer is trained on se-
quences from the human transcriptome, which means that there will most likely be some
overlap between the sequences used for training TIS Transformer and the human sequences
used in our test dataset. This may potentially lead to a slight overestimation of the perfor-
mance of TIS Transformer on the H. sapiens test set.
In addition to the benchmark on the H. sapiens test set, we assessed each model’s per-
formance for various systematic groups, including vertebrates, invertebrates, plants, fungi,
and protozoa, based on the test set.

3.8
Investigating TIS Efficiency in Relation to Predicted TIS
Probability
A study conducted by W. L. Noderer et al. (2014) [85] aimed to investigate the efficiency
of start codon recognition in mammals for all possible sequences surrounding the TIS
and utilizing ATG codons. The researchers employed FACS-seq, an approach combining
fluorescence-activated cell sorting and high-throughput DNA sequencing and defined the
sequence surrounding the TIS to span positions -6 to +5, with the A of the translation-
initiating ATG codon corresponding to position +1. Using FACS-seq, the study measured
translation from a genetic reporter library representing all 48 = 65.536 possible TIS se-
quences. In the study, each sequence was used to initiate translation of green fluorescent
protein inserted into the pCru5-IRES-mCherry-F2A-Puro plasmid, utilizing an upstream
primer with a stop codon in each reading frame to prevent upstream translation initiation.
Based on the FACS-seq data, a dinucleotide position weight matrix was then used to gen-
erate a reference table of translation initiation efficiencies for each TIS sequence [85].
The correlation between the TIS efficiency and predicted TIS probability with NetStart 2.0
was investigated. We used the TIS efficiencies obtained by W. L. Noderer et al. [85] for each
of the 65.536 possible TIS sequences, and constructed sequences with a constant mRNA
context, only varying nucleotides in positions -6 to -1 and +4 to +5. For the construction,
we utilized the same primer as the one used in the study and extracted the downstream
sequence for monomeric enhanced GFP (mEGFP) from the pCru5-IRES-mCherry-F2A-
Puro plasmid. The established mRNA context and additional information is provided in
Appendix 4.
The generated sequences were fed to NetStart 2.0 with H. sapiens as input organism, and
the relationship between the measured TIS efficiencies and the predicted TIS probabilities
was analysed.

Discussion
In this project I introduced NetStart 2.0, a model that integrates taxonomical information
with information embedded in the mRNA sequence. Utilizing learned sequence represen-
tations obtained from pretrained protein and nucleotide language models, NetStart 2.0
aims at accurately predicting translation initiation sites in species all across the eukaryotic
domain.
Local Start Codon Context
Our analysis of local start codon contexts showed that while some systematic groups have
very similar start codon contexts within the respective groups, species from other systematic
groups express more diverse contexts. The phylogenetic tree constructed on the basis of the
obtained start codon contexts of the 60 species in our dataset displayed clear monophyletic
clusters for vertebrates, monocot plants (including the lycophyte plant), and dicot plants.
These clusters, characterized by short branch lengths, align well with existing literature,
which states that differences between patterns of nucleotide bias in start codon contexts
roughly reflect the evolutionary relationships of a given species [9][25][26]. In contrast,
the patterns of nucleotide bias varied more across the fungal, protozoan and invertebrate
species, reflecting evolutionary relationships only to a certain degree (Figure 15).
As highlighted in various studies, including works by M. Kozak [16][17], S. Nakagawa et
al. (2019) [25] and G. Hern´andez et al. (2019) [9], our analysis found a purine base to be
universally conserved three positions upstream of the initiating ATG across phylogenetically
distant groups. Adenine was most conserved in all species, except for the monocot plants,
where guanine predominated across all seven investigated species. Furthermore, our analysis
agrees that there is some universal bias towards adenine or cytosine two positions upstream
of the initiating ATG, which we found to be the most conserved in all 60 species. However,
the conservation was not particularly pronounced (Figures 16, 17, and 18). We found a
slight discrepancy comparing our results to the findings of G. Hern´andez et al. (2019) [9],
who concluded that uracil (thymine in DNA) is conserved at the position right downstream
of the initiating codon in fungal species (Figure 3). Our dataset, including different fungal
species, did not support the universal conservation of uracil across the fungal kingdom (Fig-
ure 17). The inclusion of different fungal species can be discussed to influence the distinct
findings, but nevertheless, we cannot conclude that uracil is universally conserved at this
94
==PAGE:103==
position across the fungal kingdom.
Well in line with the findings of S. Nakagawa et al. (2019) [25], our analysis highlighted
a pronounced preference for cytosine two positions downstream of the initiating codon in
the majority of the 60 species (Figures 16, 17, and 18). This tendency was particularly
significant in our analysis of TIS efficiency and predicted TIS probability in a H. sapiens-
defined system. The sequence logos of sequences with high TIS efficiency, high predicted
TIS probability, or both, exhibited large conservation of cytosine two positions downstream
of the initiating ATG (Figure 38).
While our analyses for most parts align with existing studies on the topic, expanding the
analysis to cover more organisms would be valuable, in order to see if more well-defined
motifs for distinct systematic groups would appear.
Evaluation of NetStart 2.0 and Benchmark
NetStart 2.0 was evaluated on an independent test set, for which we had used GraphPart
to perform homology partitioning. This approach ensured that the model’s performance
was not estimated based on homologous sequences used during training. We generally ob-
tained a high performance on the test set across all 35 species. However, investigating the
error rates on individual sequence types (Figure 26) as well as the distribution of highly
confident but wrongly predicted non-TIS sequences (Figure 23), we saw that the model
has the greatest challenge in classifying downstream in frame labelled ATGs from labelled
TIS ATGs. We suspect that there may be more downstream, in frame translation initiation
sites of alternative ORFs giving rise to distinct proteoforms, than what is annotated in
the RefSeq database. Such potential unannotated translation initiation sites would possess
similar start codon contexts to that of the labelled TISs, and thus be wrongly labelled as
non-TIS sequences in our dataset contributing to the increased error rate on this sequence
type. Thus, this speculation could contribute to the explanation of why our model generally
performs the worst in differentiating downstream in frame ATGs from labelled translation
initiating ATGs. Definitively, a model can only be as good as the data it has been trained on.
In our benchmark study, NetStart 2.0 outperformed TIS Transformer across all measured
performance metrics in each defined systematic group of eukaryotes, including the test set
based exclusively on H. sapiens sequences. Highly noticeable performance differences were
95
==PAGE:104==
observed on the test sets for the protozoan, fungal and plant species, whereas the smallest
difference in performance was found for the H. sapiens and vertebrate test sets, for which
TIS Transformer also obtained a high performance (Figures 34, 36 and Table 10). These
observations align with our expectations, as TIS Transformer was specifically developed
using human sequences only, and vertebrates generally express similar start codon contexts.
Consequently, for species more distantly related to H. sapiens, it is reasonable to anticipate
a decline in TIS Transformer’s performance. This emphasizes the limited generalizability
of TIS Transformer to diverse eukaryotic organisms, making it less suitable for applications
beyond the vertebrate context. In contrast, NetStart 2.0’s strategy to train on diverse
eukaryotic species and implementing taxonomical information enables it to offer robust and
accurate predictions across a broader spectrum of eukaryotic organisms.
Despite achieving better performances with NetStart 2.0 than with TIS Transformer, it is
important to acknowledge the distinct advantage of TIS Transformer, being that it is devel-
oped to also predict translation initiation from sORFs and start codons alternative to ATG.
In comparison to the simple, implemented baseline model, we observed a drastic difference in
performance to that of both NetStart 2.0 and TIS Transformer. This observation indicates
that the task of predicting TISs is highly convoluted, emphasizing the need for more
sophisticated prediction models in order to accurately capture patterns related to identifying
translation initiation codons.
Learned Model Patterns
Based on qualitative assessments, fine-tuning of both ESM-2 and NT-MS noticeably en-
hanced the modelling objective of classifying ATGs as TISs or non-TISs. Based on the
principal component analyses performed on the sequence representations from both these en-
coders, we observed a significant difference in the ability to separate TIS-labelled sequences
from non-TIS-labelled sequences when comparing the principal component-projected data
before and after fine-tuning, respectively (Figures 19 and 20). Based on these projections,
the fine-tuning of particularly ESM-2 contributed markedly to the classification of TIS
from non-TIS sequences and further expressed a separate cluster of downstream, in frame
non-TIS samples (Figure 19). This observation suggests that downstream, in frame ATGs
exhibit distinct patterns compared to the other non-TIS sequence types. This is expected,
given that stop codons will not be present in the downstream in frame non-TIS samples
96
==PAGE:105==
while they also express more protein-like patterns, which ESM-2 picks up on.
For obtaining a quantitative assessment of the impact fine-tuning had on the overall perfor-
mance, we could have trained a version of the model in which we did not fine-tune either
of the pretrained language models. This approach would also provide information as to
whether the downstream feedforward layers in themselves would be able to adapt to the
specific task of classifying translation initiation sites.
Considerations in Implementing Organism Information
In the initial stages of developing NetStart 2.0, various methods for implementing emphasis
on sequences from a broad spectrum of eukaryotic organisms were considered. Initially, we
considered training distinct models for organism groups expressing highly distinct start
codon contexts. However, the choice of including learned organism embeddings in the model
architecture proved effective, allowing for the training of a single shared model. This im-
plementation highly simplified the training process while still obtaining good performances
across all species in the test set. Additionally, our experiment that involved randomly shuf-
fling the input organism labels, validated that providing the input organism information
increases performance slightly (See Table 8). This observation aligns with our expectations,
considering the significantly different patterns in start codon contexts distantly related
eukaryotic organisms express.
Investigating TIS Efficiency in Relation to Predicted TIS Probability
From our correlation analysis of TIS efficiency and predicted TIS probability of the 65.536
constructed sequences, we saw a slight, positive correlation between TIS efficiency and
predicted TIS probability (Figure 37). Additionally, it was observed that the vast majority
of sequences had a relatively high predicted probability of being labelled as a TIS. This
result is expected, considering that we used a constant mRNA context (thus only varying
the motif from position -6 to +5) from a coding transcript inserted into a plasmid. This
result emphasizes the influence that the entire mRNA context in itself has on a prediction
with NetStart 2.0, in addition to the local start codon context.
The patterns detected in the sequence logos of sequences with i low predicted TIS probability
showed that NetStart 2.0 is very unlikely to predict an ATG as a TIS, if there is an in
frame ATG right upstream (Figure 38D, F, G).

Evaluation of Predicting on Truncated Sequences
When evaluating NetStart 2.0 predictions on truncated sequences, performance was vaguely
decreased (Figure 28). We found the impact to be rather small when removing nucleotides
upstream of the ATG predicted upon, while a slightly more pronounced effect was observed
when removing downstream nucleotides. These observations align with our expectations,
given that NetStart 2.0 relies on the sequence downstream of an ATG to assess the protein-
likeness of the open reading frame. This validation is especially relevant considering that the
5’ UTR is shorter than 200 nucleotides in many mRNAs (see Figure 8), thus demonstrating
that predicting on such mRNAs will not affect the outcome significantly.
Furthermore, we specifically wanted to evaluate the performance of NetStart 2.0 when based
on actual mRNA transcripts, thus using the individual, annotated 5’ UTRs as the input
subsequence upstream of TIS. This evaluation would allow us to accurately quantify how
well our model performs on actual mRNA sequences, accounting for the inherent differences
in the 5’ UTR among transcripts. Unfortunately, due to time limitations, we could not
carry out this evaluation.
Optimization of Amino Acid Input Size
During the hyperparameter optimization process, we saw that three out of the four models
trained on distinct combinations of training and validation subsets resulted in the best
performance when utilizing the largest possible window of amino acid sequence (Table 7).
This observation thus excites a discussion of whether increasing the input size of amino
acid sequences would lead to better performance. In this discussion, a concern is whether
increasing the amino acid input window size could potentially lead to worse predictions
on sequences with short 5’ UTRs. However, increasing the window size and testing these
concerns would pose an interesting experiment for further potential improvements.
Evaluation of Predicting on Species Not Used in Development
NetStart 2.0 performed noticeably well on the 25 vertebrate and plant species not used in
model development, obtaining very low error rates on the various non-TIS sequence types at
the specified MCC-optimized threshold of 0.425 (Figure 30). We suspect that this tendency
may be attributed to a range of sequences from the 25 species that are likely orthologous
to some used for training, resulting in the observed increase in performance. We intended
98
==PAGE:107==
to apply GraphPart to the entire dataset with all 60 species, evaluating NetStart 2.0 solely
on the sequences of the 25 species in the test set. However, due to time constraints, we only
executed GraphPart on the 35 species used in model development.
Comparison to NetStart 1.0
The primary objective of this project was to develop an improved version of NetStart 1.0,
enhancing performance while covering a broader range of eukaryotic species in the training
set, making the model broadly applicable across the eukaryotic domain. It was attempted to
download NetStart 1.0 for conducting predictions on our test set, but due to compatibility
issues with system versions arising from NetStart 1.0’s development in 1997, it was not
doable. A. G. Pedersen and H. Nielsen (1997) [15] reported that NetStart 1.0 obtained an
MCC of 0.6208 on their vertebrate dataset and an MCC of 0.7122 on their A. thaliana
dataset. In comparison, NetStart 2.0 obtained an MCC of 0.8246 on the vertebrate test
set, and an MCC of 0.8553 on the A. thaliana test set, outperforming NetStart 1.0 on this
comparable metric.
Benchmark Challenges
What turned out to be a bigger challenge than expected, was being able to include other
of the eukaryotic TIS-predicting models described in Section 1.7, in the benchmark study.
Some of the models were simply not available, including the model developed by Goel et
al. [18] and DeepGSR [4]. Both TITER and DeepGenGrep were available on GitHub78,
but each with limited instructions as to how to run them, whereas we got errors trying
to run predictions on their own data. Reaching out to the authors of both TITER and
DeepGenGrep, we did not get a response. Furthermore, DeepGenGrep requires nucleotide
sequence inputs of length 603, meaning that our extracted datasets are not applicable in the
current form. However, as mentioned, we did try downloading DeepGenGrep and running
it initially using their own data to test the accessibility, which was not possible in the first
place. One could argue that the limited accessibility of these models is a benchmark in
itself.

Limitations to NetStart 2.0 and Future Perspectives
NetStart 2.0 is trained on TIS-labelled sequences based on mature mRNA transcripts,
lacking consideration for introns in predictions on genome sequences, potentially leading to
a decreased prediction performance on such sequences. If time had allowed so, evaluating
NetStart 2.0 on genome sequences and potentially incorporating genome data into training
would be useful.
Further enhancements could involve incorporating the capability to predict initiation sites
for start codons alternative to ATG. However, for this version of NetStart we decided only
to include ATG predictions due to the current limited number of annotated non-ATG start
codons as emphasized in Table 1. Additionally, a valuable expansion of NetStart would
include the ability to predict translation initiation sites in sORFs.
In the development of NetStart 2.0, we decided to employ small versions of both ESM-
2 and NT-MS. While larger pretrained language models could potentially enhance the
model’s ability to learn accurate sequence representations, various reasons made us decide
on the smaller versions. Notably, larger models would significantly extend training time,
considering the substantial number of sequences in our training set. We carried out initial
experiments with larger versions of ESM-2, which resulted in highly increased training time
with minimal improvement in validation performance. However, had the time resources
been there, further exploration of using other pretrained language models to potentially
increase performance would be interesting.

Appendix 1. Description of Scripts
All below described scripts used for this project are attached in a zip-file. Data can be
made available upon request.
Scripts involved with preparing datsets (./scripts/data preparation/):
• extract annotations.py: Extracts the relevant genomic annotations (mRNA, exon,
CDS) from a genomic GFF-formatted file. The script takes as input one genomic
GFF-file and is run for every species in the dataset.
• check annotations.py: Checks that annotations have been extracted correctly.
• extract mRNA.py: Extracts samples from mature mRNA sequences. The script ex-
tracts both positive samples, consisting of mRNA sequences with a labelled ATG TIS,
and negative samples, consisting of mRNA samples with a labelled non-TIS ATG,
both upstream and downstream of the TIS.
• extract introns.py: Extracts samples from sequences with a labelled non-TIS ATG
in introns.
• extract intergenic.py: Extracts samples from intergenic regions of a genome with
labelled non-TIS ATGs.
• number sequences.py: Numbers sequences in the generated datasets.
• check datasets.py: Makes an additional check that all datasets have been extracted
correctly.
• partition sequences.py: Extracts subsequences of a predefined number of nucleotides
upstream and downstream an ATG, and saves them in a fastafile. The fastafile is used
as input for GraphPart. GraphPart is run from the script, carrying out homology
partitioning on each sequence type in the dataset.
• create model datasets.py: Takes the information on which sequences goes into
which partitions, and creates files with sequences assigned to each respective partition.
• sort taxonomy.py: Creates a file with all eukaryotic organisms’ taxonomical distri-
bution across several ranks.
109
==PAGE:118==
• map taxonomy.py: Maps eukaryotic organisms’ taxonomy on various taxonomical
levels. The mapping is used for tracking a species’ taxonomy in the model.
• prepare testdata benchmark.py: Merges data from all sequences to carry out dif-
ferent tests on, including the test set from the development dataset (partition 5) and
sequences from the 25 species not used in model development.
• get dataset sizes.ipynb: Shows dataset sizes after homology partitioning (used
for Table 5).
Scripts involved with data analyses (./scripts/data analysis/):
• extract local context info.py: Extracts relevant sequence information based on
the annotations from the 29 species selected for the preliminary dataset.
• data plots preliminary analysis.Rmd: Data analysis plots based on the prelimi-
nary dataset.
• extract local context info.py: Calculates nucleotide background frequencies and
frequencies of every position in the local start codon context for all 60 species in
dataset.
• calculate distance.py: Performs the CLR-transformation and calculates Aitchison
distances between each pair of species.
• make information logos.py: Creates sequence logos for the local start codon con-
text of every species, both creating the version that does and does not correct for
background frequencies.
• make tree.R: Generates the neighbor-joining tree constructed for the local start codon
context analysis.
• visualize tax embeddings.py: Extracts the learned organism embeddings from the
final trained models, concatenates them and calculates cosine similarities between
each pair of species, visualized as a heatmap.
• PCA pretrained models.py: Extracts the contextualized sequence representations
from either pretrained language model and performs the PCA.
Scripts involved with modelling: (./scripts/modelling/):

• Finetune ESM2.py: Finetunes ESM-2 on each train and validation data split.
• Finetune NT-MS.py: Finetunes NT-MS on each train and validation data split.
• hyperparameter tuning netstart model.py: Hyperparameter tuning for NetStart
2.0 on each train and validation data split.
• train netstart model.py: Final training of NetStart 2.0 on each train and validation
split, with finetuned language models and optimized hyperparameters.

the test set, in which input organism labels are shuffled.

• construct TIS sequences.py: Constructs TIS sequences in constant mRNA context
for the analysis of TIS Efficiency compared to predicted TIS probability.
• get predictions TIS constructs.ipynb: Runs NetStart 2.0 on constructed TIS
sequences.
• evaluate netstart.ipynb: Assesment of evaluation metrics and plots for evaluating
NetStart 2.0.
• evaluate models.ipynb: Benchmark results.
• plot TIS efficiency vs preds.ipynb: Plots and data for sequence logos involved
with the TIS efficiency and predicted TIS probability analyses.

Appendix 4. mRNA Context Used for Predicting All Possible TIS
The pCru5-IRES-mCherry-F2A-Puro plasmid was accessed at https://www.addgene.org/
49225/ the 12th of January, 2024.
We utilized the forward primer given in the study conducted by W. L. Noderer et al. (2014)
[85]:
5’-CATCCTCTAGACTGCCGGATCTCGAGTAACTGACTAGT NNNNNN ATG NN
CGAATTCAGCAAGGGCGAGGAG-3’
The Ns indicate the 8 positions where the nucleotides were varied to construct all 48 = 65.536
possible TIS sequences. The forward primer was then merged with the 200 nucleotides
downstream of the mEGFP ATG start codon from the pCru5-IRES-mCherry-F2A-Puro
plasmid, such that the final mRNA context utilized was:
5’-CATCCTCTAGACTGCCGGATCTCGAGTAACTGACTAGT NNNNNN ATG NN CGAATTCAGCAAGG
GCGAGGAGCTGTTCACCGGGGTGGTGCCCATCCTGGTCGAGCTGGACGGCGACGTAAACGGCCACAAGTT
CAGCGTGTCCGGCGAGGGCGAGGGCGATGCCACCTACGGCAAGCTGACCCTGAAGTTCATCTGCACCACC
GGCAAGCTGCCCGTGCCCTGGCCCACCCTCGTGACCACCCTGAC-3’
