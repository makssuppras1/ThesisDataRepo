I 
 
ABSTRACT 
For decades, thin-walled steel sections have been used in building construction both as 
infill and as load bearing elements. A method was later developed for optimizing the 
thermal resistance, of e.g. exterior walls, by slotting the web part of the sections. The slots 
however, reduce the load bearing resistance of the section, which today is still not fully 
covered by any Standards or Codes, why further research on this topic is essential. In 
Denmark the slotted cold-formed steel profiles is today mostly used in exterior walls in 
residential buildings (up to two storeys), but may have a great potential for use in higher 
and bigger spanning buildings. The main aim of this Thesis was to get an improved 
understanding of the influence, which the slotting of the web has on the structural 
behaviour of thin-walled C-sections in order to access whether these profiles are suitable as 
load-bearing elements for larger framing systems.  
 
The stiffness properties of the slotted web are studied numerically and analytically resulting 
in a proposed analytical model for calculating the rotational spring stiffness in the junction 
between the web and flange. This model is incorporated in the existing relevant European 
Standards, to include the effects of slots when calculating the load capacity of C-sections.  
 
Experimental work was performed consisting of 12 columns all with slotted C-sections 
with a height of 2.8 m and a thickness of 2 mm. The 12 tests were divided into two test 
series, each with six columns. The first test series consisted of simple supported columns 
and the second included a mid-height lateral support. Only axial compression was 
investigated, disregarding any transverse or eccentrically loading. Furthermore, any lateral 
restraints or stabilizing effects from plasterboards or cladding are neglected. The results on 
the compression load bearing capacity, from the experimental tests were compared with 
the analytical calculations and showed reasonable agreement for the first Test series. 
However, for the second Test series the analytical calculation overestimates the load 
capacity. During the tests it was further observed, that the use of a web-stiffener, in the 
connection of the column, influenced the global buckling direction of the column. 
 
Non-linear finite element simulations of the performed experimental tests were performed 
using the commercial FE program ABAQUS. The simulations included geometrical 
imperfections according to the European Standards as well as non-linear material 
behaviour from material tests. The results showed that the geometrical imperfections has a 
considerably influence on the ultimate load capacity for such long elements.  
 
Lastly, the results from the experiments were compared to a Case Study concerning the 
magnitude of loading in a typical office building. Based on this simple comparison, the 
slotted steel columns are assessed to be suitable as load bearing elements in buildings above 
two storeys, however, only when some kind of lateral restraint is present.

RESUMÉ 
I årtier er tyndvæggede stål profiler blevet brugt i byggeri, både som udfyldning og som 
bærende elementer. Senere blev der udviklet en metode hvor langsgående slidser blev 
introduceret i kroppen af profilet for at optimere varmeledningsmodstanden. Slidserne 
reducerer dog samtidig bærevenen af profilet, hvilket stadig ikke er omfattet af nogen 
Standard i dag, hvorfor yderligere forskning er af stor betydning. I Danmark er de slidsede 
kold-formede profiler i dag primært brugt i ydervægge i boligbyggeri (op til 2 etager) men 
har muligvis et stort potentiale ved brug i såvel højere som større bygninger. 
Hovedformålet i dette speciale var at opnå en bedre forståelse af den indvirkning, som 
slidserne i kroppen har på de strukturelle egenskaber af tyndvæggede C-profiler, for derved 
at kunne vurdere om disse profiler er brugbare som bærende elementer i større byggerier. 
 
Gennem numeriske og analytiske studier er stivhedsparametrene af den slidsede krop 
fastlagt, og en analytisk model er foreslået for at bestemme rotationsfjederstivheden i 
hjørnet mellem krop og flange. Modellen er brugt til at modificere de relevante Europæiske 
Standarder således at effekten af slidserne inddrages, når bæreevnen bestemmes. 
 
Eksperimentelt arbejde omfattende 12 søjler blev udført, alle med slidsede C-profiler med 
en højde på 2,8 m og en tykkelse på 2 mm. De 12 tests blev inddelt i to Test-serier, hver 
med seks søjler. Den første serie bestod af simpelt understøttede søjler, hvor der i den 
anden serie også var en sidevers understøtning i midtpunktet af søjlen. Forsøgene bestod 
udelukkende af aksialt tryk, idet der ses bort fra tværgående eller excentrisk belastning. 
Endvidere er eventuelle laterale fastholdelser eller stabiliserende effekter fra gipsplader eller 
beklædning udeladt. Resultaterne på tryk-bæreevnen fra forsøgene blev sammenlignet med 
de analytiske beregninger og viste rimelig overensstemmelse for den første Test-serie. Men 
for den anden Test-serie viste den analytiske beregning sig at overestimere bæreevnen. 
Under forsøgene blev det endvidere observeret, at anvendelsen af en krop-afstivning 
(koblingsbeslag) i samlingen påvirkede retningen på søjlens globale udknækning. 
 
Ikke-lineære finite element simuleringer af de udførte eksperimentelle tests blev foretaget 
ved hjælp af det kommercielle FE-program ABAQUS. Simuleringerne inkluderede 
geometriske imperfektioner i henhold til de Europæiske Standarder såvel som ikke-lineær 
materiale adfærd fundet ved materiale tests. Resultaterne af disse modeller viste, at de 
geometriske imperfektioner havde en betydelig indflydelse på den endelige bæreevne for 
disse lange tyndvæggede stål profiler. 
 
Til sidst belv resultaterne fra forsøgene sammenlignet med en fiktiv Case Study vedrørende 
størrelsen af de verticale belastninger i en typisk kontorbygning. Baseret på denne enkle 
sammenligning vurderes de slidsede stål profiler til at være egnede som bærende elementer i 
byggerier over to etager, men kun når en eller anden form for lateral fastholdelse er tilstede.

1. INTRODUCTION 
For decades, thin-walled steel sections have been used in construction both as infill and as 
load bearing elements. A method was later developed for optimizing the thermal resistance, 
of e.g. exterior walls, by slotting the web of the sections. In the past decade slotted thin-
walled sections has become more attractive, especially in the Nordic countries. The slots 
however, reduce the load bearing resistance of the section, which today is still not covered 
by any Standards or codes, why further research on this topic is essential.   
 
Cold-formed thin-walled steel members have been widely used in load bearing walls for 
many decades because of the very high strength to weight ratio. In the past decades 
systematic research, as well as improved manufacturing technology, protection against 
corrosion and increased material strength have led to wider use of cold-formed sections 
within the building industry [11]. A composite construction consisting of the thin walled 
steel studs together with plasterboards also known as the light steel framing system or LSF 
has become a competitive alternative to traditional constructions of materials like wood or 
concrete. The system typically includes thin-walled steel sections, mineral wool, 
plasterboards and cladding as illustrated in Figure 1.1.

The LSF system is a total building component element, comprising of external- and 
internal walls as well as floors.  The system has been developed as a response to the 
increasing need for more efficient building construction, like faster construction, less use of 
material and better energy/thermal resistance in buildings. 
 
In the last decades, the use of thin-walled members with slots/perforations in the web part 
of the profile is gaining increasing attention. The perforations are typically longitudinal 
holes that are either punched or cut in the web part of the section. The slotted steel 
member may be included in the LFS system as wall columns connected at the top and 
bottom to slotted U-channels tracks as illustrated in Figure 1.2. 
 
 
 
Figure 1.2: Illustration of wall system with slotted steel members 
Studies including T. Höglund & H. Burstrand (1998) [9] have shown that the slotting of the 
web in the thin-walled steel members has proven to decrease the thermal bridges by 
increasing the length of which the cold must travel as shown in Figure 1.3, thus increasing 
the thermal resistance. This offers great advantages in countries where insulation of 
buildings is of high priority due to the cold climate and even more, where the ambition of 
improved energy efficiency is prioritised. Furthermore, the improved thermal properties of 
the slotted steel members have also shown to provide increased fire resistance.

Because of the perforations, however the structural behaviour of the slotted steel member

Most of the fabricators of lightweight structures, such as Gyproc, Knauf Danogips and 
Norisol to mention a few, now have a variety of slotted members in their product range. 
These slotted members are used as external load bearing walls in single-family houses or as 
infill walls in blocks of flats and industrial or office buildings. In Denmark the slotted cold-
formed steel profiles is today mostly used in exterior walls in residential buildings (up to 
two storeys), but may have a great potential for use in higher and bigger spanning 
buildings.

1.1 Objective 
The overall objective of this Thesis is to get an improved understanding of the influence 
the slotting of the web has on the structural behaviour of thin-walled C-sections, in order 
to access whether these profiles are suitable as load-bearing elements for larger framing 
systems (above 2-storys with floor height of approx. 2.8 meters). This assessment is done 
by comparing the load bearing capacity with the type and magnitude of loading in a typical 
office building. 
 
The immediate objectives of the Thesis are: 
 
 Through numerical investigation of the slotted web an analytical model for 
predicting the rotational spring stiffness, which is directly related to the transverse 
bending stiffness, will be proposed. This analytical model could potentially make 
any finite element modelling unnecessary when determining the stiffness of the 
slotted web. 
 Additionally the compression load bearing capacity of slotted C-sections, used as

1.2 Structure of Thesis 
Having given an introduction in Chapter 1, including a description of the background and

Ife, L.W. 
One of the first studies concerning slotted thin walled studs where conducted by Ife, L.W

plywood and gypsum boards attached on each side as seen in Figure 2.1.

performed. The test setup consisted of both axial and horizontal load cases. Ife concluded: 
that the load bearing capacity of the web perforated steel element was approximately 9 % 
lower than that of a solid stud.

Höglund, T. 
Höglund, T. (1998) [8] has developed an analytical calculation method for design of slotted 
steel members. The calculation methods are primarily based on Swedish Code for Light 
Gauge Metal Structures 79 (StBK-N5).  
 
Several possible types of failure modes are introduced by Höglund, see Figure 2.2 d). In all 
cases the load bearing resistance is found to be mainly influenced by the modules of elastic 
shear support of the slotted web or/and by the reduced transverse bending stiffness of the 
web.

The effect of the modules of elastic shear support can be taken into account by using a 
reduced shear modulus Gred for the web. The value of Gred can be determined either by 
FEM calculations or by experiments. The reduced transverse bending stiffness together 
with local buckling is treated using the effective area approach. 
 
The calculations of two specific failure types are treated more in detail. The failure types 
are: 1) buckling in the plane of the web taking the shear deformation of the slotted web 
into account and 2) lateral buckling of the flanges where the gypsum boards are assumed to 
act as elastic supports. 
 
Höglund also addresses the failure type where buckling of the flange stiffeners occurs in 
the free span or at the support. The screws in the plasterboard mainly prevent this failure 
type. By testing, the effective buckling length was found to be 0.72 multiplied by the 
distance between the screws. The analytical calculation method is compared and verified 
against the results of e.g. Frederiksen and Spange from the Danish Technological Institute, 
the Norwegian Building Research Institute and the Department of Structural Engineering, 
Steel Structures, Royal Institute of Technology, Stockholm.

Kesti, J. 
Kesti, J. (2000) [13] studied the local and the distortional buckling behaviour of the flange 
and web-stiffened compression members, both numerically and experimentally, particularly 
when the web part is perforated. In this research two types of web-perforated sections 
were investigated, sigma-shaped sections and C-channel sections. The thickness of the 
analysed sections varied between 1 to 2 mm. 
 
Kesti treats the perforations of the web by finding the reduced stiffness value from linear 
FE analysis. This reduction factor indicates the ratio between the stiffness of the perforated 
part of the web and that of a solid plate. To model the perforated web in numerical analysis 
two methods using reduced thickness were introduced by Kesti. The two methods, which 
was used to consider the reduced stiffness were: 1) Reduced thickness of the whole web 2) 
Reduced thickness of only the perforated web part, see Figure 2.4. 
 
 
Figure 2.4: Two ways to model the perforated web [13]. 
The test results showed that the restraint of the perforated web with respect to the 
distortional buckling mode had considerable influence on the failure load. The failure loads 
of the whole sections were approximately 10% higher compared to those of the tested 
sections with only the flange part when the lips of the whole section were failing outwards 
and 27% higher when the lips were failing inwards. 
 
Guidelines for taking the resistance of the gypsum sheeting into account were also 
presented. It was found that all the specimens tested with gypsum board failed in 
distortional buckling mode. The screw pitch was found to have no influence on the 
buckling mode or the number of buckling half-waves, however the screw pitch was found 
to influence the failure load. The failure load was found to be 20% higher (t=1.2mm) when 
using 200 mm screw pitch compared to 600 mm, and 30% higher than that of a plain 
section without gypsum boards. Kesti found that the thinner the wall thickness of the 
section, the bigger the influence of the gypsum board has on the failure load. In the design 
guidelines, the flexural stiffness of the plasterboard and rotational stiffness of the 
connection between the board and the stud were taken into account, which improves the 
distortional buckling strength of the slotted wall stud.

3. ANALYTICAL CALCULATION 
METHOD (THIN-WALLED PROFILES) 
Having presented the existing literature on the subject of slotted thin-walled steel members 
it has become clear that the slots influence the behaviour of the member in several ways. 
The slotting of the web reduces the transverse bending and shear stiffness of the web as 
well as the compression load bearing capacity of the member. However, the presented 
studies show that the primary effects of the slots is on the distortional buckling mode, 
which is directly related to the transverse bending stiffness of the slotted web. This Thesis 
focus on the axial load bearing capacity of long slotted wall steel studs for which the global 
buckling mode is of most interest. Knowing now that the slots primarily affect the 
distortional buckling, this mode is further investigated because it affects the global buckling 
by reducing the effective area of the cross section.  However, this chapter deals with the 
procedure for calculating the compression load bearing capacity of thin-walled members, 
according to the European Standards where the effects of the slots are not included.  
 
3.1 General 
Structural elements made of steel tend to be slender, in particular thin-walled members 
fabricated from steel plating or sheeting whose thickness is much less, than the other 
structural dimensions. When a structure undergoes visibly large displacements transverse to 
the load then it is said to buckle. Elements subjected to compression forces or secondary 
bending effects (imperfections, nonlinear material or asymmetric loading) are particularly 
prone to buckling. Buckling can proceed in manner, which may be either: 
 
 stable       -   in which case displacements increase in a controlled fashion as loads are 
increased, i.e. the structure's ability to sustain loads is maintained, or 
 unstable   - in which case deformations increase instantaneously, the load carrying 
capacity curve dives and the structure collapses. 
==PAGE:26==
ANALYTICAL CALCULATION METHOD 
13 
If the buckling deflections become too large, the structure fails - this is entirely 
a geometric consideration, completely separated from any material strength consideration. If a 
component or part thereof is prone to buckling then its design must satisfy both strength 
and buckling safety criterions. Thin-walled steel sections can induce sectional instability in 
part of the cross section (local buckling), such as the web, flanges or lips, or of the total 
cross section (global buckling). There exist a number of potential buckling modes that can 
occur, of which some are listed below: 
 
 Local buckling 
 Distortional Buckling 
 Flexural Buckling 
 Torsional-flexural buckling 
 Torsional buckling 
 Lateral torsional buckling 
 Shear buckling. 
 
All these buckling modes affect the shape and the load bearing resistance of the member's 
cross section. This Thesis will only address the local, distortional and flexural buckling 
modes, which are presented in the next section. 
 
 
3.2 Eurocode approach  
The analytical method, used to determine the compression load bearing capacity of the 
slotted members, is based on the Eurocode Standards; EN 1993-1-1 - General rules for steel 
structures [25], EN 1993-1-3 - Rules for cold-formed members [26] and EN 1993-1-5 - Plated 
structural elements [27]. In the following the calculation procedure for determining the load 
capacity of non-slotted cold-formed thin-walled C-sections is given.

3.2.1 Local buckling 
Local buckling is characterized by local deformation of very slender cross-section elements 
involving only rotation at the nodes/junctions (web, flanges or lips) as shown in Figure 3.1. 
 
 
Figure 3.1: a) Local buckling mode with lips/flanges rotating inwards b) Local 
buckling mode with lips/flanges rotating outwards 
Plate elements will not collapse when its theoretical buckling stress, σcr, is reached. This is 
in contrast to independent columns, which develop large deflections and fail once buckling 
starts. The ability to support loads beyond the buckling load is known as post-buckling 
strength. Essentially, the centre strips of the plate deflect the most and have a tendency to 
transfer part of their loads into the less deflected neighbour strips. The strips closest to the 
edge can resist increasing load with hardly any deflections. Consequently, the element 
experiences a redistribution of stresses in the section. In the pre-buckling phase, the 
stresses are uniformly distributed, while after buckling they become non-uniform as shown 
in Figure 3.2 and continuously concentrate near the supported edges as the load increases, 
until the yield strength is reached and the plate starts to fail. 
 
The theoretical solution to this problem, taking into account the post-buckling strength, is 
not practical for design purposes, for which the "effective width" design model has been 
developed. The model was first introduced by Von Karman et al in 1932 and later modified 
by Winter with provision for unintended geometrical imperfections. In the "effective 
width" model, instead of considering the non-uniform stress distribution σx(y) over the 
width b, the total load P is assumed to be carried by fictitious effective widths beff  
subjected to uniform stresses equal to the edge max stress σmax. The effective widths beff is 
chosen so that the area under the curve of the non-uniform stress distribution σx(y) is 
equal to sum of the fictive uniform edge stress distributions as shown in equation (3.1) and 
Figure 3.2.

3.2.2 Distortional buckling 
Distortional buckling of thin walled sections includes rotation of the lip/flange component 
around the flange/web junction. This rotation can occur inwards or outwards, see Figure 
3.3 a) and b). This buckling mode is also known as "flexural stiffener buckling" or "local 
torsional buckling".  
 
 
Figure 3.3: Distortional buckling mode with lips/flanges rotating a) outwards 
b) inwards 
Distortional buckling is typically associated with the presence of an edge stiffener. Without 
the stiffener, only local buckling dominates because the element has only one supported 
edge. The presence of the edge stiffener improves the resistance of the member, but 
consequently leads to the occurrence of the distortional buckling mode.  
 
Distortional buckling of compressed thin-walled members with C-sections is governed by 
the rotational spring stiffness at the junction between the web and the flange. Wider webs 
are more flexible i.e. has less stiffness and thus provides less rotational stiffness to the 
junction, which ultimately results in lower distortional buckling strength of the cross 
section. Longer lips are beneficial against flange distortion but become more sensitive for 
local buckling. The wavelength of distortional buckling is usually halfway between that of 
local and global buckling modes as illustrated in Figure 3.4 from the European Standard.

3.2.3 Global buckling 
Global buckling is characterized by out-of-plane movement of the cross section at the 
critical load and is the predominant buckling mode of long and slender elements. The 
modes of global buckling are seen as flexural, torsional or flexural-torsional buckling, as 
shown in Figure 3.8. Global buckling modes are sometimes called rigid-body buckling 
because any given cross-section moves as a rigid body without any cross section distortion. 
The calculation procedure for torsional buckling or torsional-flexural buckling is not 
included in this Thesis.

PERFORATIONS AND 
ANALYTICAL MODEL 
Having introduced the slotted thin-walled members and the possible buckling modes as 
well as presented the calculation method according to the European Standard this Chapter 
deals with the investigation of the influence of the perforations/slots in the web part of the 
section. Introducing the perforation in the web part of the section greatly affects the 
stiffness properties in all directions. The slotted web plate no longer acts as an isotropic 
material, but adapts more of an anisotropic behaviour, having different stiffness in all 
directions. As earlier mentioned studies have shown that, the slots have the greatest 
influence on the distortional buckling of the profile. Because the distortional buckling 
mode, to a large extent, is governed by the rotational spring stiffness in the junction 
between the web and the flange, this stiffness is now further investigated. The stiffness 
properties of the slotted web are studied numerically and analytically. Lastly, an analytical 
model for calculating the rotational spring stiffness is proposed in Section 4.3.2.2. 
 
4.1 General 
The analytical method, used to determine the compression load bearing capacity, is based 
on the Eurocode Standards. The Standard do not take the perforations in the web into 
consideration. The reduced rotational spring stiffness in the junction between the 
perforated web and the flange is thus incorporated in the calculation procedure for 
distortional buckling given in the Standard. The reduced stiffness also affects the global 
buckling capacity by governing the effective area of the cross section from the distortional 
buckling calculation.

Two finite element models are simulated to determine the reduction of the transverse 
bending stiffness due to the perforations in the web. The first model is a solid plate and the 
second model is a perforated plate, both representing the web of a C-section profile. Both

length and width. A ”S3” element mesh type is chosen, which is a linear 3-node triangular 
shell element type. Triangular elements are preferred when meshing complicated geometry,

considered reliable and accurate. Once the element size chosen is small enough that the 
solution, that is the deformation, begins to converge, that element size is used. For the 1 m 
long model a coarse seeding is used, however, in the centre of the model a refined mesh is 
made to be able to investigate the stress more precisely. The plates are supported along the 
long edges with simple supports as to represent the flanges of a C-profile. The plates are

4.2.2 Influence of the number of perforations 
From the results found in the previous Section, one may conclude that the number of slots 
in the web do not have much impact on the transverse bending stiffness. Therefore, this 
Section further investigates the influence of the number of slots in the web. The perforated 
web-part of the C-section profiles are available in a variety of geometries and a varying 
number of slots. To study the influence, of which the number of perforations in the web 
has on the transverse bending stiffness, six finite element models are simulated. The FE-
models are similar as the one used in the previous Section, apart from the varying number 
of slots and a plate width of 200 mm. The slots in the models with less number of slots are 
reduced from the outer part and inwards towards the centre of the web. 
 
In Figure 4.4 is shown that the reduction of the transverse bending stiffness is much more 
pronounced going from a solid plate to the first number of slots (100% stiffness equals 
solid plate). Furthermore, the reduction of stiffness becomes lesser and lesser when 
increasing the number of slots. This corresponds very well with the results obtained in the 
previous Section where the stiffness of a web with ten slots was found to be almost equal 
to one with six slots. All the calculations regarding the transverse bending stiffness is given 
in Appendix A.

 number of slots in the web.

a high number of slots without losing much stiffness percentage wise. This may be 
advantageous when compared to the positive effects gained, i.e. thermal properties and fire 
resistance when increasing the number of slots.

5. EXPERIMENTAL WORK 
Now the modified analytical method, including the author’s rotational spring stiffness-
model, for calculation the compression load bearing capacity of slotted steel sections, has 
been presented. In this Chapter a brief summary of the experimental work will be given. 
The details and the procedure of the tests performed are documented in a separate report 
named "Jacobsen, J.H. (2015) Slotted thin-walled steel members - Experimental work" [12]. 
 
5.1 Column tests 
5.1.1 General 
The experimental work consists of two Test series both with slotted THR 245/2.0 C-
profiles with a length of 2.8 m. The two Test series each consist of six column tests. The 
difference between the Test series lie in the effective column length as shown in Figure 5.3. 
The first test series consisted of simple supported columns and the second included a mid-
height lateral support. The purpose of performing two Test series is to investigate the 
benefit of a lateral restraint on the ultimate load bearing capacity. The expected failure-
buckling mode is the flexural buckling. The flexural buckling resistance is greatly influenced 
by the buckling length. When the columns have no lateral restraints, the buckling length of 
both in-plane and out of plane direction is equal to the true length of the profile (2.8m). 
The thin-walled C-profiles are characterized by a small inertia in the wall plane (z-axis) and, 
in order to improve the load bearing resistance of the studs in this direction, studs can be 
restrained with lateral supports e.g. by steel U-profiles attached lengthwise to the profiles 
flanges at mid-height as shown in Figure 5.1. This system allows a buckling length of the 
stud to be only half the wall height.

5.2.1 Performance 
The mean value of the failure load is 23.6 kN for Test series 1 and 48 kN for Test series 2. 
As seen from Table 5.2 and Table 5.3 the relative standard deviations is very small only 
3.81 % for Test series 1 and 2.54 % for Test series 2, which indicates a very small variability 
in the data.  
 
All the specimens in Test series 1 failed in global flexural buckling with interaction of 
distortional buckling mode. In the distortional buckling phase, the flanges and lips of all the 
specimens buckled outwards. All the specimens in Test series 2 failed in global flexural 
buckling with some interaction with distortional buckling. The flanges and lips buckled 
outwards in the top half of the specimen, inwards in the middle and outwards in the lower 
part respectively. 
 
The load-displacement curves (performance curves) for all the specimens are given in 
"Jacobsen, J. (2015) Slotted thin-walled steel members - Experimental work" [12]. As an example, 
the performance curve from test specimen no. 1 (Test series 1) is given in Figure 5.9. 
Throughout the loading-sequence the specimen experience local- distortional- and global 
buckling modes. Initially the perforated web undergo local buckling with increasing 
wavelengths at a load of about 8-12 kN. The specimen experience a buckling mode 
transition from local- to distortional or a so called "snap through" at a load of about 12-15 
kN as indicated in Figure 5.9. The flanges of the cross sections deflect outwards during the 
distortional buckling mode in all the tests performed. The global buckling mode identified 
as flexural buckling, initiate as the distortional buckling becomes more severe, and the 
specimen reaches its failure load with an interaction between the distortional and flexural 
buckling.

manly determined by the location of the web stiffener.  
 
5.2.3 Connections 
The load is transferred mainly through the web of the profile due to the use of a web 
stiffener as shown in Figure 5.13. This causes an eccentricity in the load transferred to the

around 8.6 mm. This eccentricity is present until the magnitude of the load is high enough 
to deform the shrews/holes so that the entire cross section has contact with the base (U-
profile). To determine exactly at which time or load the entire cross section get full contact 
with the base profile is very difficult. The best estimation is done based on the observations 
during the tests, which indicate that the deformation in the connection occurs somewhere 
near the transition from local- to distortional buckling mode.

6. NUMERICAL ANALYSIS  
In the previous Chapter, a short summary of the conducted experimental work as well as 
examples of the performance and displacement curves for the test specimens were 
presented. In this Chapter, a linear elastic and a non-linear finite element analysis, of the 
same C-sections as investigated in the experimental work, is performed in the ABAQUS CAE 
application. The purpose of the FE-analysis is to simulate the performed column 
compression tests, and to obtain a better understanding of the buckling modes and failure 
type of the slotted C-section.  
 
The load bearing capacity found from the FE-analysis using initial imperfections given by 
the European Standard EC 1993-1-5 [27] is compared with the mean value of the failure 
load of the experimental work. Furthermore, the influence of the magnitude of the 
geometric imperfections on the failure load is investigated by running FE-simulations with 
a "scale factor" of 0.8, 0.5 and 0.1 on the imperfections. 
 
6.1 FEM input 
The FE-model is built with deformable 3D shell extrusion because the C-profile is very thin 
compared to its length. The element is meshed with a ”S3” element type, which is a linear  
3-node triangular shell element type. The triangular elements are superior when meshing 
complicated geometry, which is the case in the perforated web. The geometric order is 
chosen as linear because it provides sufficient integration points for this type of analysis. 
The element size of the mesh is chosen on the basis of observations on whether the results 
obtained is reliable and accurate. Once the element size chosen is small enough that the 
solution, using the eigenvalue buckling analysis, begins to converge, that element size is 
used. The chosen mesh is shown in Figure 6.1.

6.3 Non-linear analysis 
6.3.1 Imperfections 
In ABAQUS, the "Riks" method can solve the postbuckling problems both with stable and 
unstable behaviour. To analyse a postbuckling problem one need to introduce a geometric 
imperfection pattern in the "perfect" geometry so that there is some response in the 
buckling mode before the critical load is reached. In ABAQUS there are three ways to 
introduce these imperfections, 1) as a linear superposition of buckling eigenmodes 2) from 
the displacements of a static analysis 3) or by specifying the node number and imperfection 
values directly [1]. In the present work the first option is chosen, where an eigenvalue 
buckling analysis is performed with the "perfect" geometry to establish probable collapse 
modes.    
 
The geometrical imperfections of the test specimens are incredible difficult to accurately 
measure, and vary much from one specimen to another. So instead, the imperfections used 
as input in the non-linear analysis, is chosen as the recommendations given in the 
European Standard EC 1993-1-5 [27]. The load bearing capacity found from the FE-
analysis using these imperfections is compared with the mean value of the failure load of 
the experimental work. The influence of the magnitude of the imperfections on the failure 
load is investigated by running multiple FE-simulations.

6.4 Results 
A number of simulations are carried out to determine the ultimate failure load, and to study 
the influence of the initial imperfection magnitude on the failure load. The results of the 
FE-simulations together with an example of the experimental data from Test series 1 are 
shown in Figure 6.6. The load-deformation curves given in Figure 6.6 is made by 
combining the load proportionality factor (LPF) - arc length curve with a plot showing the 
deformation of the nodes in the bottom of the profile - arc length. The FE-curves roughly 
fit the experimental curve having nearly the same deformation at failure-load; however, the 
FE-curves have a larger inclination (stiffness). As Figure 6.6 shows, the magnitude of the 
initial imperfection has a considerable effect on the ultimate failure load, which is also 
expected for such long elements, where the ultimate load capacity is more likely to be 
governed by instability failure rather than by material failure. The failure load with the 
initial imperfections, given by the Standard EC 1993-1-5 [27], predicts lower values of the 
failure load compared to the results from the experiments, thus being on the safe side. 
Furthermore, Figure 6.6 shows that with an adjustment factor of 0.1 (10 % of the EC) the 
failure load coincides very well with the experimental results. This might encourage the use 
of an initial imperfection for global elements of L/2000 and local elements of a/2000 when

9. CONCLUSION 
During this Thesis, an introduction was given of the utilisation of the light steel framing 
system (LSF) in building construction together with a brief history and purpose of the 
slotting of the web as well as the objective of the this Master Thesis. Furthermore, the 
procedure for calculating the compression load bearing capacity of thin-walled members, 
according to the European Standards, where the effects of the slotting are not included, 
were presented. The importance of including the reduced stiffness, due to the slots, in the 
calculation was emphasized, in particular regarding the distortional buckling mode.  
 
The transverse bending stiffness of the slotted web was investigated numerically. One 
results showed that the number of slots in the web affects the stiffness in a tendency that 
as a result enables the use of a high number of slots without losing much stiffness 
percentage wise. This may be advantageous when compared to the positive effects gained, 
i.e. thermal properties and fire resistance when increasing the number of slots. 
 
The influence of the perforation/slots in the web was further investigated; in particular, the 
reduced transverse bending stiffness was studied by performing numerical models to 
follow the stress distribution across the perforated web. From this study, an analytical 
model, based on the principles of virtual work, for calculating the rotational spring stiffness 
in the junction between the web and the flange, was proposed. The model showed varying 
precision in predicting the stiffness for different cross sections compared to FE-analysis – 
between approx. 4 to 24%. However, the model provided a reasonable good estimate of 
the stiffness, leaving the designer with the possibility of not having to use a finite element 
program for every new project concerning slotted thin walled profiles. Nevertheless, the 
model needs to be further developed to reduce the deviation. 
 
The experimental work consisted of long column tests, with slotted steel C-sections, loaded 
in compression. The tests were designed to resemble a column used as part of a wall 
system in a load bearing exterior wall. The test columns were the "THERMOnomic 
THR245/2.0" with a length of 2800 mm and a thickness of 2 mm. Two Test series, with 
==PAGE:99==
 Conclusion 
86 
each six test-elements were investigated to compare the effect on the ultimate compression 
load bearing capacity of a column with no lateral support (Test series 1) and with a mid-
span lateral support (Test series 2). The results of the tests showed that all the columns in 
both Test series failed in an interactional global-distortional stability failure-mode, with 
max. mean load capacity of 24 kN and 48 kN for Test series 1 and 2 respectively. Thus, the 
lateral mid-support provided about 50% more load bearing capacity. 
In Test series 1, in the distortional buckling phase, the flanges and lips of all the columns 
buckled outwards. In Test series 2, the flanges and lips buckled outwards in the top half of 
the specimen, inwards in the middle and outwards in the lower part respectively. 
 
Based on the tests, it was clear that the load was initially transferred mainly through the 
web of the profile due to the use of a web stiffener. This causes an eccentricity in the load 
transferred to the profile. This eccentricity was present until the magnitude of the load was 
high enough to deform the shrews/holes so that the entire cross section has contact with 
the base profile, creating a forced rotation in the connection. Observations from the tests 
suggest that this partial initial rotation thereby dictates the flexural buckling direction of the 
column. Furthermore, the boundary conditions of the columns in the analytical calculations 
were assumed simply supported, however, during the column tests, some degree of fixed 
end-conditions was observed. The fixation occurs because the connection is moment-stiff 
to some extent due to the screws as well as the end contact pressure between the profiles. 
 
To simulate the conducted experimental column tests, finite elements models of the two 
Test series were performed.  In the numerical analysis, a linear elastic analysis was made to 
determine the possible buckling modes of the slotted profile. Additionally, a non-linear 
analysis with geometrical imperfections, and material non-linearity were performed. The 
results showed that the load-displacement curves from the FE-analysis roughly fit the 
experimental curves of Test series 1, having nearly the same deformation at failure-load. 
Furthermore, it was found that the magnitude of the geometrical imperfections has a 
considerable influence on the ultimate compression load bearing capacity of the columns. 
Moreover, it was found that with an adjustment factor of 0.1 (10% of the EC 
recommendations) the failure load coincides very well with the experimental results. This 
might encourage the use of an initial imperfection for global elements of L/2000 and local 
elements of a/2000 when simulating these kinds of long perforated C-sections exposed 
only to axial force. In Test series 2 the FE-curves generally overestimates the failure load 
with about 25% compared to the experimental data, thus being on the unsafe side. 
Additionally, the failure load of the FE-curves occurs at much smaller deformation, which 
might be because any additional deformations from the test machine are not accounted for 
in the load-displacement curves.  
 
Analytical calculations, based on the Codes with the modification of the reduced rotational 
spring stiffness, calculated according to the author's model, were performed on the 
columns used in the experimental work. Two calculations of each Test series were 
performed to include what was observed in the experimental test, namely, the eccentricity 
==PAGE:100==
 Conclusion 
87 
caused by the web stiffener. The calculations where this eccentricity was included generally 
predicted the load capacity with greater accuracy, however, all analytical calculations proved 
to overestimate the load capacity. The general overestimation of the load capacity from the 
analytical calculation was not considered related to the inaccuracy of the author’s model for 
the rotational spring stiffness, since the maximum deviation in the model only affects the 
result of the load capacity of up to 1%. Whereas the neglecting of any torsional 
contributions in the calculation may have a greater influence. Some degree of torsional-
flexural buckling mode was observed from the tests, which may reduce the load bearing 
capacity. Likewise were the experimental tests associated with some sources of errors that 
possibly reduced the ultimate load capacity, which are further specified in "Jacobsen, J.H. 
(2015) Experimental work" [12]. 
 
To evaluate the suitability of the slotted steel studs, as load bearing elements in a higher 
building of more than two storeys, the axial load capacity determined by the experimental 
work is compared to the resulting load from the fictive Case Study of a three and four-
storey office building. If comparing the design load bearing capacity from Test series 1 to 
the resulting load of a column at level 0 from a three-story office building, it is evident that 
the slotted steel member fall short of being applicable as a load bearing element. However, 
when the design load bearing capacity from Test series 2, where the column is supported 
laterally at mid-height, is compared to the same load, the capacity surely exceeds the 
resulting load in a column at level 0 from a three-story office building, making the slotted 
steel member suitable as load baring elements. In fact, the capacity of the slotted steel stud 
from Test series 2 is very close to the resulting load from a four-story building.  
 
The comparison was made based upon a compression load capacity found from tests of a 
single standing element not considering any lateral restraint from e.g. plasterboards or any 
other stabilizing effects or stiffness from the wall system. If considering these, one could 
argue that the slotted steel element is suitable as load bearing elements for buildings as high 
as four storeys. However, the tests were also performed not considering transverse loading 
from e.g. wind as well as eccentricities from vertical loading, which surely reduces the 
acceptable compression load. Another consideration, which affects the use of the slotted 
profiles in higher buildings, is the fire resistance demands. In Denmark external walls must 
be classified as R120 (fire resistance in 120 min) when the floor in the top storey is located 
above 12 m.

FUTURE WORK

10. FUTURE WORK 
The above given work gives rise to a number of potential future work within the presented 
areas of research, some of them being: 
 
Further study of the influence of a web stiffener in the connection is needed. From the 
performed tests, the web stiffener clearly has an influence on the connection. Observations 
suggest that the web stiffener dictates the flexural buckling direction of the column. 
Unfortunately, only tests with the web stiffener present in the connection was conducted. 
This limits the possibility of commenting on the web-stiffeners effect on the failure load of 
long columns in compression. However, in the paper by Andreassen, M. J. and Jönsson, J. 
(2015) [3], it was concluded that the web stiffener had no effect regarding the compression 
load capacity of columns with a length of 500 and 1000 mm. Consequently, further 
research and testing of long columns with and without web stiffeners should be studied.  
 
The column tests performed in this Thesis were rather simple regarding loading scenarios 
only including axial loading, why further study of the limit value of transverse load is of 
interest. Furthermore, it could be of interest to study columns with multiple or different 
types of lateral supports to increase the ultimate load capacity. Likewise it could be of 
interest to study measures for strengthening the weak axis bending (around the z-axis) of 
the slotted profile, since the failure around this axis showed to be determining for the 
compression load bearing capacity. Another point, which could be of interest for further 
research, is the interaction between the columns and gypsum boards. Whether the boards 
can be used as lateral restraints and stabilizing elements of a wall system, and ultimately if 
this restraint is sufficient to make any extra elements for lateral support superfluous.  
 
Lastly, further fire simulations of wall systems, including slotted thin-walled steel members, 
to simulate the behaviour of the slotted steel stud at elevated temperatures, and to verify a 
wall system for 120 min of fire, so that these can be used in multiple story buildings.
