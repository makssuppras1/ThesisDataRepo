Abstract 
In this study laser-based micro-stereolithography (µ-SLA) was used to 3-dimensionally (3D) print 
poly(ethylene glycol)diacrylate (PEGDA) with the intention of creating a chemotaxis in vitro 3D 
microfluidic model. The model had two channels and was based on diffusion-based source/sink 
system. Furthermore, a set-up for perfusion was created to investigate the reproducibility of 
perfusion of the printed microchannels in the model. The intention was to generate a 
concentration gradient of chemoattractants between two channels, where tumor cells/cluster 
could be embedded to study their migration. The methods used were µ-SLA of PEGDA to fabricate 
the hydrogel microchannels with the smallest possible cross-sectional dimension. Microscopy was 
used to validate the channel shape and dimension against the channel design. For the set-up of 
perfusion, syringe needles were used as external connectors to the model which were placed at 
the microchannels vertical inlet and outlet using two 3-axis positional stages. Tubing were 
connected from the peristaltic pump to the syringe needles to generate perfusion of solutions 
with dissolved dye for visualization of the microchannels. Experiment was done both for one- and 
two-channel perfusion. COMSOL Multiphysics 5.2 simulation program was used to model the 
development of the concentration gradient of chemoattractants between the two channels. The 
results of the fabrication and design of microchannels gave printed models with only slight 
deviation between models. However, the design of the model and the printed models gave a 
larger deviation due to swelling and pixilation through fabrication. The perfusion of the 
microchannels had a low success rate and only one out of 19 two-channel models was able to be 
perfused. Through COMSOL Multiphysics 5.2 simulation, it was shown that the model with 
source/sink channels was able to generate a linear concentration gradient of the selected 
chemoattractant after 16 hours. The deviation from the model design to the printed hydrogel 
model was due to swelling of the hydrogel and pixilation when building the hydrogel model 
through µ-SLA. The low success rate of perfusion was due to the fact that the hydrogel models 
had low toughness, which made the hydrogels propagate cracks or hydrogel was not sealed tightly 
around the inserted needles, both situations led to leakage of dyes. Furthermore, the external 
connectors’ set-up gave only little visualization of the hydrogel model, which made it difficult to 
insert the needles into the inlet/outlet of the model without penetrating the surrounding 
hydrogel. The penetration of surrounding hydrogel in the funnel led also to leakage of dyes. The 
simulated model gave an indication of the time it would take to generate the concentration 
gradient between the two channels in the model. The conclusion was that the printed two-channel 
model was reproducible, but the design with vertical funnels did not work with the selected 
syringe needles. Thereby, the success rate of perfusion through the microchannels was limited 
and could not provide a perfusion through the channel for the wanted ~ 16 hours for the given 
chemoattractant to generate a concentration gradient.

Resume 
I dette projekt blev laser baseret mikro-stereolitografi (µ-SLA) anvendt til at 3-dimensionelt (3D) 
printe poly (ethylenglycol) diacrylat (PEGDA) med det formål at skabe en in vitro 3D microfluid 
model til kemotaksi via diffusion. Modellen havde to kanaler og var baseret på et source/sink 
system. Endvidere blev der opstillet et forsøg til væskegennemstrømning af mikrokanalerne. 
Dette var til at undersøge reproducerbarheden af gennemstrømning af mikrokanaler i de printede 
modeller. Hensigten var at generere en koncentrationsgradient af kemoattraktanter mellem de to 
kanaler, så tumorceller/klynge kunne blive indlejret i rummet mellem kanalerne og cellernes 
migration vil kunne blive studeret. De anvendte metoder var μ-SLA med materialet PEGDA for 
fabrikation af hydrogelmodeller med mikrokanaler. Mikrokanalerne var printet med det mindst 
mulige tværsnit. Mikroskopi var anvendt til at validere kanalformen og dimension mod kanalens 
design. Kanylenåle blev brugt som ekstern forbindelser mellem slange og model. Nålene blev 
indsat i en vertikal bevægelse i mikrokanalernes vertikale indgang og udgang med to 3-akset 
mikromanipulatorer. Der var slanger forbundet til den peristaltiske pumpe til kanylenålene til at 
generere gennemstrømning af vand med opløst farvestof til visualisering af mikrokanalerne. Der 
blev lavet forsøg af væskegennemstrømning med både en og to kanaler. COMSOL Multiphysics 
5.2 simulation program blev anvendt til at modellere udviklingen af koncentrationsgradienten af 
givne kemoattraktant mellem de to kanaler. Resultaterne af fabrikation af de printede modeller 
gav kun lille afvigelse mellem selve modellerne. Design af modellen og de printede modeller gav 
en større afvigelse. Væskegennemstrømning af mikrokanaler havde en lav succesrate og kun én 
ud af 19 to-kanal modeller var i stand til blive gennemstrømmet med væske. Det blev vist via 
simuleringer at det ville tage cirka 16 timer for den valgte kemoattraktant at genere en linear 
koncentration gradient mellem de to kanaler. Afvigelsen fra modellens design til de printede 
hydrogelmodeller skyldes hydrogel kvældning og fabrikations pixelering. Den lave succesrate for 
væskegennemstrømning skyldtes hydrogels lave sejhed som gjorde, at hydrogelen revnet eller 
hydrogelen ikke blev forseglet tæt omkring de indsatte kanylenåle i indgangene og udgangene af 
kanalerne. Begge situationer førte til lækage af opløst farvestoffer. Endvidere gav de ekseterne 
forbindelseopsætninger med kanyler kun begrænset visualisering af hydrogelmodellen. 
Opsætningen af eksterne forbindeler gjorde det vanskeligt at indsætte nålene i indgangene og 
udgangene af kanalerne i modellen. Dette gjorde, at hydrogelen omkring indgangene og 
udgangene af kanalerne blev penetreret af nålen, som også kunne føre til udsivning af opløst 
farvestof. Den simulerede to-kanal model gav en indikation af, hvor lang tid 
væskegennemstrømning af den givne kemoattraktant skulle være for at genere en 
koncentrationgradient mellem kanalerne. Konklusionen var, at den printede to-kanal model var 
reproducerbar i design, men de vertikale indgange og udgange af hydrogelmodellen virkede ikke 
med de valgte kanylenåle. Derved blev succesraten for væskegennemstrømning af kanalerne 
begrænset og der kunne ikke skabes gennemstrømning i en længere periode hvilket var 
nødvendigt for at genere en koncentration gradient.

Thesis Objective  
The objective of this project is to assist in the development and establishment of a reliable and 
reproducible in vitro chemotaxis three-dimensional (3D) model with perfusable vasculature in 
close proximity of tumor cell clusters/cells. The main focus will be on the early stages of cancer 
metastasis, intravasation into the blood vessels. This is done through perfusion of the synthetic 
vasculature with tumor cell chemoattractants that generate a spatiotemporal concentration 
gradient by diffusion, which polarize the tumor cells to migrate. The main steps in this project are 
1) to design and fabricate two stable, closed microchannels with funnels in synthetic polymer that 
can be perfused with a liquid medium. 2) To build a stable external fluidic connector set-up and 
give reproducible perfusion through the microchannels by an external pump. 3) To generate the 
concentration gradient between the two microchannels by diffusion. The method used is micro-
stereolithography (µ-SLA) of poly(ethylene glycol) di-acrylates (PEDGA) to fabricate the hydrogel 
microchannels with the smallest cross-sectional dimension possible. Microscopy is used to 
validate the channel’s shape and dimension against the channel design. The perfusion through the 
channels requires external fluidic connectors to be located at the microchannels inlet and outlet 
using 3-axis positional stages. A peristaltic pump is used with tubes connected to the external 
fluidic connectors to generate perfusion of solutions with dissolved dye for visualization of the 
microchannels. COMSOL Multiphysics 5.2 simulation program is used to model the concentration 
gradient between the two microchannels.  
Following section introduces the concept of a source/sink system to generate a concentration 
gradient and through a few assumptions, how the concept of source/sink can be integrated in a 
microfluidic platform. The source/sink is a diffusion-based method, which can establish a 
chemoattractant concentration gradient that is stable in steady state. In Figure 1, the traditional 
source/sink model is illustrated. The chemoattractants are secreted from the source tissue and 
are degraded in the sink tissue. This generates a concentration gradient between the two tissues, 
which makes the cells migrate towards the higher concentration.1   
 
Figure 1: Adopted from Cai D et al. 1 The traditional source and sink model in direct cell migration. The green dots are 
chemoattractants that are secreted from a tissue (source) and are degraded within a sink tissue, thus creating a 
concentration gradient. The migrating cells polarize in the gradient and migrate to the source.

A schematic of how to integrate source/sink channels into a microfluidic model and generate a 
concentration gradient is shown in Figure 2. The microfluidic system has two channels with an 
inlet and outlet in each channel. The microfluidic channels are in a material, where predetermined 
molecules can diffuse through. The source channel is perfused with fluid through convection. 
Thereafter, the molecules from the source channel goes through the porous material through 
diffusion into the area between the two channels. The second channel has the function as a sink, 
with fluid such as water and provides an output for the diffused molecules through convection. 
The source/sink combination generates a pseudo-steady state in between the channels, wherein 
the concentration of molecules does not change dramatically with time.2 The concentration 
gradient can be generated in a matrix such as extracellular matrix (ECM). ECM should be 
embedded into the area between the two channels before perfusion together with the given 
migration cells.1,2 However, the ECM and cells embedding is beyond the scope of this project.  
 
Figure 2: Illustration of the source-sink channels. The predetermined particles/molecules introduced through 
convection in the source channel. The sink is filled up with water through convection. The particles diffuse from the 
source towards the sink and generate a concentration gradient between the two channels.  The volume between 
channels is filled with a matrix such as ECM.

Introduction 
Metastases, which represent the end product of a multi-step cell-biological process of a primary 
malignant tumor, are responsible for the most cancer-related deaths worldwide.3 Tumor cell 
migration is essential for invasion and dissemination from the primary tumor. Research have 
shown that tumor growth is not only dependent on the malignant tumor cells themselves, but 
also on the highly complex microenvironment of the tumor.4,5 Therefore it is imperative to get a 
better understanding of the chemical and mechanical signals from the complex microenvironment 
surrounding the tumor cells to aid metastatic research as well as cancer drug research.5 There are 
many ways to investigate tumor cell migration and a long range of techniques to mimic cancer cell 
microenvironment.5 The current metastatic research is dependent on in vivo tumor models in 
mice, which is suited to investigate the relevance of signaling pathways during different steps of 
the metastatic cascade5,6. However, in vivo studies have disadvantages, include the complexities, 
unpredictabilities and ethical issues when using mice.5,6,7,8 For these reasons, establishment of 
reproducible in vitro tumor models is needed, i.e., models that capture the essential properties of 
tumors microenvironment and are able to isolate single signals in tumor cell migration.  
In this project, a reliable in vitro chemotaxis model was developed through reproducible 
technology. The model had perfusable synthetic vasculature (two channels). Moreover, there was 
designed a space between the vasculature to embed ECM and tumor cells/cluster. Perfusion set-
up was developed for the perfusion of the microchannels. The microchannels of the model were 
tested by perfusion with dyes. The intention was to generate a stable spatiotemporal 
concentration gradient between the two microchannels, which could be used for chemotaxis 
purposes.    
Cell Migration 
In tumor cell trafficking, the cells can move randomly or directionally, where they are most 
efficient when they are involved in directed migration. There are different types of direct cell 
migration, such as haptotaxis, electrotaxis, durotaxis and chemotaxis.9 In this section the latter is 
elaborated on. The concept of chemotaxis is shown in Figure 1. Chemotaxis is the directed 
migration of cells due to a concentration gradient of compounds also called chemoattractants. 
The chemotaxis mediators for tumor cells, tumor-associated inflammatory cells and stromal cells 
are often chemokines, chemokines receptors, growth factors and growth factor receptors.9 
Convection can create spatial gradients and is defined as a molecular aggregation moving 
collectively in a fluid. Where gradients such as gases, growth and hormonal factors are all confined 
by the proximity to the vascular system and diffusion.10 Migration cells can travel long distances 
and can get multiple source signals, since different chemoattractants can change in production 
and location over time, which means that the cell can take multiple turns. Further, the migrating 
cells can alter their responsiveness towards the chemoattractants.1 Multiple turns are needed to 
navigate through the complex path that can include paths through interstitial matrices, along 
individual ECM fibers, on flat basement membranes and/or squeezing in between cells to cross 
barriers like blood vessels, lymph vessels and epithelium.1

Migration Assays 
The polarity in cell migration is not well understood and there are many different methods and 
models, both two-dimensional (2D) and 3D, used to investigate the directed cell migration. In this 
section, there is an elaboration on the advantages and drawbacks of current 2D and 3D methods 
and models. 
The 2D techniques, like Boyden Chamber, is a horizontal endpoint assays, see Figure 3A.11 This 
technique has proven to be effective to some degree for explaining the behavior of tumor cells 
and developing hypotheses of the tumor cells’ possible mechanisms. It is a horizontal assay and it 
is not possible to take live images of the cell migration.12 For Under-agarose gel, see Figure 3B,  it 
has overcome the problem with visualizing the migration since the migration is on a flat surface.13 
The assay is very simple and can have multiple gradients simultaneous in the same assay. The main 
disadvantage in both assays is the lack of ability to generate a stable chemoattractant 
concentration gradient.11,12 The control of the microenvironment is inadequate due to insufficient 
structural, mechanical and biochemical properties, and can lead to wrongful interpretation of the 
collected data.5

chamber make the migrating cells. Cells are located in the upper chamber, transmigrate across the membrane and

The use of 3D models gives researchers the opportunity to tailor the microenvironment as they 
want and provides a more realistic model of the physical in vivo microenvironment than 2D 
assays.8 There are different experimental 3D in vitro approaches with various applications to 
mimic the in vivo tumor microenvironment.5,8,13,16,17,18 Animal models for cancer studies are also 
used to capture and replicate the microenvironment of tumor cells.19 However, in vivo tumor 
models face significant problems due to difficulties isolating the effect of single signals and 
unwanted signals in the models.5 Furthermore, there are challenges in observations with high 
spatial and temporal resolution, the species variation among human and mice, reproducibility due 
to variability between animals and the ethical challenges of sacrificing large number of animals.6,7,8 
However, the animal models are used as a point of reference when developing in vitro 3D models. 
The main challenge for 3D cellular models is to maintain consistency and reproducibility in cell

The primary requirement is that the fabrication and perfusion should be reproducible. The model 
should have microchannels which mimic vasculature in the body, since the gradient of gases, 
growth and hormonal factors is confined by the proximity to a vascular system and diffusion.10 
The diffusion gives a requirement for the material to be permeable to chemoattractants. The 
porosity is defined as the pore size in the material, which makes it possible for particles from e.g. 
serum, to diffuse passively through the material. The model should be created by a material that 
is non-degradable, since it should be used for 4-7 days or longer. Further, it should be bioinert and 
be able to culture endothelial cells in the microchannels. There should be perfusion through the 
channels and the material of the model should resist the mechanical stress induced by perfusion. 
The microchannels should be able to be perfused at least for 4-7 coherent days, since endothelial 
cells are eventually going to be cultured in the microchannels and a concentration gradient of 
chemoattractants by diffusion needs to be established.14 The model should have enough space 
between the microchannels to embed ECM and tumor cells/clusters, and to generate 
concentration gradient. The concentration gradient should be a stable linear chemoattractant 
gradient, which induces migration of the embedded tumor cells. The secondary requirements are 
not investigated through experiments in this project, but will be elaborated on in Section 
“Conclusion and Outlook”.

subdivided into capillaries and then into a larger vessel again, venule.22 In the capillaries the 
oxygenated blood supplies the tissue with oxygen and nutrients. Intravasation of tumor cells 
is through venules, capillaries and lymph vessels.23 To mimic the vascular system around the 
tumor, the structural and physical parameter of vessels need to be implemented into the in vitro

Engineering Design 
In tissue engineering, it is important to be able to control the size, shape and physical features of 
the in vitro model.27 Rapid prototyping and hydrogel has opened new doors for tissue engineering 
scaffolds in the last decade, since it produces design models with fully interconnected 3D channels 
with predetermined dimension and porosity.28,29 Hydrogel is defined as polymer network gel, 
which is swollen with water.24 They can closely simulate living tissue due to tissue-like elasticity, 
high porosity and high water content, which enable transportation of nutrition, oxygen and other

Photopolymerization 
Photopolymerization is a process where photocurable/photoreactive monomers get converted 
into crosslinked network. Monomers, like PEGDA, have photoreactive and crosslinkable di-
acrylate groups. PEGDA is mixed together with a light-sensitive compound called photoinitiator. 
The photoinitiator absorbs ultraviolet (UV) or near-UV light (depends on the light source and the 
photoinitiator), which makes free radical species. The free radical reacts with the acrylate groups 
on the monomer and new radical is subsequently formed, therefore triggering a chain reaction, 
since PEG-di acrylate is used, the extra acrylate group functions as a cross-linker; it connects 
individual polymer chains and thus effectively creates a cross-linked network. This process is also 
called curing. The polymer chain and cross-linked network keep growing until two radicals are 
combined, which make the polymerization subsequently terminated. In Figure 4, the chemical 
reaction between the photoinitiator Lithium phenyl-2,4,6-trimethylbenzoylphophinate (LAP) and 
PEGDA is illustrated, when they are exposed to UV or near-UV light.24,36,37

Perfusion of Microchannels and the Perfusion Set-up 
In microfluidic models, the channel should have an inlet that leads into the channel, and an outlet 
that leads away from the channel, so perfusion can be created.47,48 The connection between 
external pumps to the microfluidic model by tubing is an important part of the perfusion set-up. 
Connectors such as needles, fittings or directed tubing to inlet/outlet of the model can be used. 
To keep perfusion running for several hours or days external pumps can be used.5,6,49,50 In 
microfluidic-based models external pumps make it easier to control the flow rate and enable 
better control over chemical and physical environment.

Diffusion 
The behavior of convection of fluid through the microchannels has been stated through Hagen-
Poiseuille flow. The porosity of the material of microchannels has an impact on the mass transport 
properties. The convection can be ignored if the pore size in the material is less than 100-200 
nm.58 In other words, there will not be advection of fluid flow through the pores of the material 
and only the time-dependent diffusion will apply.58 Diffusion is a random transport mechanism 
and is defined as the stochastic process, where there is a net movement of molecules or atoms 
from a region of high concentration to a region of low concentration. If there are no external 
forces applied, the molecules will move in all directions, and it will change direction if it hits 
another molecule or obstacle on its way.

The Selected Materials and Methods for the Project 
 
The following materials and methods are used to achieve the requirement, from Table 3, in this 
project.  
The design of the model is built on the concept of a diffusion-based source/sink model. The two 
microchannels are therefore horizontally parallel to each other and had an inlet and an outlet in 
both channels, where perfusion is generates. The horizontal location of channels gives the model 
easy accessibility to direct visualization of the migration of tumor cells compared to conventional 
vertical indirect assays like Boyden chambers. There is space between the channels for the 
concentration gradient to be generated. There are vertical inlets/outlets of the channels, so the 
insertion of connectors into the inlets/outlets can be executed in a vertical movement.  
The fabrication method selected is µ-SLA, since it gives a higher printing speed than LDM. In 
addition, it has a higher resolution than LDM and inkjet printing, which gives the wanted 3D space, 
geometry, and flexibility in in vitro vasculature model. The porosity in the material is also higher 
when using µ-stereolithography. A high porosity is necessary since the diffusion-based source/sink 
channels depend on molecules diffusing through the material. The material, PEGDA, is selected to 
build the hydrogel model. PEGDA is bioinert.24 It is an important feature in all chemotaxis models, 
that there is a complete overview of which signals are presented to the cells culture and/or the 
cells embedded into the model.24 Further, PEGDA is a cheap material, has a low degradable rate 
and higher mechanical strength compared to naturally-derived polymers. A high mechanical 
stability and low degradable rate is needed for the model to withstand days of perfusion in the 
channels.24 
Because the hydrogel is relatively fragile, connectors with desired mechanical strength that 
accommodate tubing can currently not be integrated into the design model. Therefore traditional 
injection needles are utilized to connect inlets/outlets. The insert of needles needs to be a very 
precise execution. This gives a better visualization, since a vertical microscope can be used to the 
insertion of the needles. The needles are connected to tubing, which goes to the external pump. 
The idea for the external pump was inspired by Wang et al.7, where they are modeling 
transvascular migration of tumor cell through artificial blood vessel. They used a peristaltic pump 
since it was necessary for growing endothelial cells in the channels for 2-4 days. Therefore the 
peristaltic pump is selected due its ability to perfuse over a long period of time compared to the 
syringe pump. The external and interchangeable tubing made it easy to keep the experiments 
sterile and change between different fluids. The material of the tubing is tygon, since it is the 
tubing that is available. However, it is not required to have tubes with high biocompatibility or 
high chemical resistance since there are no highly aggressive media perfused through the tubes 
in this project. A reliable perfusion through the channel needs to be created before a 
concentration gradient can be generated between the two channels. Therefore dyes are used as 
“source” through the channels to study the fluid flow.

Perfusion Set-up 
One of the model requirements is that the microchannels are able to be perfused. This 
requirement is two-fold:  1) The perfusion of the channels should be reproducible and 2) the 
channels should be perfused for 2-4 days. Firstly, tests are done with three-channel models, where 
dyes are perfused in only one channel to ensure that the channels of the hydrogel models are able 
to be perfused. A syringe pump was used following a peristaltic pump. The set-up for this perfusion 
is called the one-channel perfusion set-up. Afterwards, both of the channels in the two-channel 
models are perfused with dyes. The set-up for this perfusion is called the two-channel perfusion 
set-up. Every model is perfused only once. Table 12 gives an overview of the design of the model

Overview of the Two-channel Perfusion Set-up 
The perfusion through the two-channel hydrogel models are generated through the two-channel 
perfusion set-up, see Figure 14C for the simplified overview of the set-up.  The overview shows

hydrogel model. The outlet needles are inserted into the outlet funnels of the model. The outlet 
needles are attached to the outlet adapters, which are connected to the outlet tubes. The 
peristaltic pump makes it possible to suck out the dyes with the same flow rate as the dyes are 
pumped into the channels.    
The set-up of external fluidic connectors is a part of the perfusion set-up and is showed in Figure 
15. The needle adapters are connected to two 3-axis positional stages, two adapters on each 
stage. The 3-axis positional stages are placed in front of each other on a breadboard, see Figure 
16A. One each stages, two adapters with needles are placed on a metal plate fixed with clamps 
and connected to the stage via perpendicular metal joint. There are placed two Poly(methyl 
methacrylate) (PMMA) needle holders under the metal plate. The needle holders have two holes 
each. The distance between the two holes is 2.9 mm as the distance between funnels in the two-
channel model. The 90° bent needles is placed and fixed in the needle holders. This keeps the 
needles in place vertically and with the right distance from each other, see Figure 16B. The correct 
distance of the needle is important to ensure that the needles do not penetrate the hydrogel 
around the funnels. The 3-axis positional stage can be manually adjusted to lower the needles 
down into the vertical funnels of two channels hydrogel model as seen in Figure 16B. Between the 
two 3-axis positional stages, the rotation stage is placed. On the rotation stage, there is placed 
and fixed plate with a well, which contains the hydrogel model and water. The rotation stage 
makes it easy to rotate the well, so the funnels of the hydrogel model are aligned with the needles. 
The rotation stage is fixed at the wanted position by screws and thereby makes the model fixed 
before the needles are inserted into the funnels. The well can be viewed in Figure 15 and Figure

Finite Element Modeling 
In element modeling, it is only the “active” part of the model that needs to be simulated.  The 
active part in this project was the microchannels from funnel to funnel (6.3 mm) with cross section 
300 µm x 300 µm and the area between the channels (2.0 mm x 6.3mm). See Figure 19. The 
simulations were performed with COMSOL Multiphysics 5.2. See Appendix E for elaboration on 
the COMSOL Multiphysics 5.2 and the physics used in the element modeling. 
 
Figure 19: Drawing of the simulated model from COMSOL Multiphysics 5.2 with corresponding parameters. The black 
arrows give the function of the given part of the model or the length.

steady state. Additionally, time-dependent transport of diluted species for the source/sink model 
is employed to follow the formation of concentration gradient over time in the area between the 
channels. The model is also simulated without a sink to compare to the source/sink model. The 
source channel is modeled as having constant concentration (perfect sink). The geometric 
parameter and define values in the simulations is listed in Table 15.

Discussion 
Limitations of the Design due to Materials and Fabrication 
The method, µ-SLA, and the PEGDA 700 resin, made the models reproducible and they were 
produced with only a slight variation among the models themselves, see Table 17 and Table 18 p. 
45-46. The design of models and how the materials and fabrication affected the design are 
discussed in this section, whereas the reproducibility of the perfusion will be discussed in Section 
”Perfusion of the Microchannels and the Limitations of the Perfusion Set-up”.  
Table 4 p. 21 shows the parameter of the capillary and venule, which was a starting point for the 
design. Different channels sizes and wall thicknesses of the microchannel were investigated to 
determine how small the microchannels could be, under the assumption that they still would be 
stable and without high deformation. The test of Model A, Figure 20A-B p. 45, 200 µm x 200 µm 
square channel in cross section with 200 µm walls, had a high standard deviation, σ= 85 µm, of 
the walls, Table 16 p. 45. In comparison to Model B, Figure 20C p. 45, 300 µm x 300 µm square 
channel in cross section channel, had a lower standard deviation, σ=41 µm. Furthermore, Model 
A had a factor ~ 2 wider channels than the design parameter. Therefore the 300 µm x 300 µm 
cross section channels with 300 µm wall thickness was selected as final design of the two channels 
model. 
The deviation in the dimensions of the printed test models and also of the two-and three- channel 
models was due to swelling and the physical pixilation of the hydrogel. The DMD in the µ-SLA 
consists of small mirrors. It could build e.g. wide of channel of 100 µm and the slice software 
Magics 16.2 EnvisionTec could construct so the 2D images was place in the center of a pixel and 
illuminated, so 100% intensity in the middle pixel and less intensity of the neighbor pixel. Whereas 
PolyChrome 3D printer, the SliceSTL software will calculated the outer-pixel should, which will lead 
to full exposure of the channel. The optimal situation for the 100 µm channel would be if two first 
pixels got full intensity and the last would get a small dose (EnvisionTec slice) or no dose at all 
(SliceSTL).41 Pixelation could give uncertain parameters in the dimensions of the hydrogel model. 
In this project, the volumetric equilibrium degree of swelling of 20 % PEGDA 700 were ~76 % from 
relaxed state to swollen state of the hydrogel model, see Table 20 p. 52.  As seen in Table 17 p. 45 
and Table 18 p. 46, the dimension of the printed models was deviated approximately with 29-183 
µm from the designed model parameters. The swelling effect of hydrogel had shown uncertainty 
in structure size in models. In Neiman et al.70, deviation of the 300 µm spacing of the channel was 
measured. This was due to the degree of swelling by 20% PEGDA, which were fabricated in SLA. 
There were deviations from 219 ± 10 µm to 297 ± 8 µm. The different values of deviation of wall 
sizes were due to different concentrations of photo initiator and energy density. The swelling of 
hydrogel and the mechanical properties will be elaborated on in section “The Properties of the 
Resin and Seeding cell culture in the Microchannels”. The final printed two channels design of the 
model were more stable than the channels in Model A and Model B, Figure 20 p. 45, since there 
were more hydrogels to support the channel structure.   
The design of square cross section channels instead of circular cross section channels was decided 
from the start. This was due to the building blocks of EnvisionTEC Perfactory® Micro 3D printer 
being pixilated which affected round shape to be more corned and uneven, see appendix I for a 
picture of circular cross section channel. To see how the shape affected the fluid flow, the two 
==PAGE:64==
 
 
64 
 
different shapes channels were compared in the COMSOL Multiphysics 5.2 simulation. The velocity 
fields of the two different shaped channels were compared in Figure 25A p. 48 and Figure 26A p. 
49. In the square channel, the velocity in the corners seems to be slower. Shear rate of position in 
the channels is illustrated in Figure 25B p. 48 and Figure 26B p. 49. The evolving shear rate seems 
to be identical in every direction in the circular cross section, whereas in the square cross section, 
shear stress is higher at the walls than the corners of the channel. To investigate this further, a 
diagonal and horizontal cut line were created in both cross section of the channels. In Figure 27 p. 
50, the two graphs of horizontal and diagonal cut line of the shear stress of position is shown for 
both channels. This concluded that shear rate evolved identically in every direction in the circular 
cross section channel, which is predicted, since there is rotational symmetry. The horizontal and 
diagonal cut line of the shear rate of position of square cross section channel is not increased 
identically in the two directions. This meant that flow velocity was affected by the square shape 
and there was not the same fluid friction in the edges as in the sides of the channel. The inner 
layer in venule is composed of endothelial cells. The diameter of endothelial cells is around 8-12 
µm and the mean diameter was used to define the height of the cut line used in Figure 28 p. 51.71 
This was to study the shear rate at the wall if  a layer of cells where to be cultured on the square 
cross section channel, see Figure 28 p. 51.  The shear rate is decreased with a factor ~3.7 both at 
the surface and 10 µm height from the surface and there is a factor 4 difference between the two 
cut lines. The 10 µm height from the surface gives the relevant shear rate at the upper side of the 
cells. Shear stress is an important element when culturing viable endothelial cells in 3D channels.22 
Researchers have reported that shear stress in the vessels has a positive effect on cells regarding 
differentiation, stimulation and survival. However, if the shear stress is too large it can lead to 
damage and/or death of the cells.22 
In Zheng et al.53, they fabricated a type 1 collagen microfluidic network system with 100x100 µm 
square channels and 150x120 µm rectangular channels fabricated by soft lithography. Despite the 
corners in their models, the endothelial cells seeded within the channels had showed to form 
approximately cylindrical vessels.14 A passive gravity driven flow pump was used and the average 
shear stress (wall) was 0.01 Pa, which was a factor 30 lower than the physical shear stress in 
venule. In Price et al.72 , they showed that the type I collagen channels (with cell growth) stability 
was a function of pressure and shear stress. A slow flow gave short-lived (5 days for 80% of the 
vessels) leaky vessels. However, in Zheng Y. et al.53, they could keep them stable despite the slow 
flow since there were other factors affecting the vessels such as cell type, the ECM growth 
medium, fabrication method and the geometry, network vs. single channels.  
There is a limitation of oxygen diffusion of 100-200 µm for cells in the body; however, the design 
of the two-channel model with perfusable channels and porosity in the hydrogel material, which 
is elaborated on in section” Chemoattractant Concentration Gradient”, made it possible for cells 
to get oxygen and nutrients through perfusion of medium in the microchannels, thereby can 
increase the potential size of the tissue.

The main concern in building the set-up to perfuse the microchannels was the stability of the set-
up since the slightest movement or misadjustment can affect the hydrogel model by tearing the 
hydrogel, which could lead to leakage of fluid from the funnels. Therefore the main requirements 
for the set-up were stability, ability to adjust the set-up parameters in the micro scale and visibility 
of the funnels and channels. Two 3-axis positional stage and a vertical microscope (without a 
degree angle) were used to navigate the 90 degree bent needles into the funnels of the model in 
the one channel perfusion set-up, see Figure 14B p. 38 and Figure 18 p. 42. This set-up provided 
the wanted stability for the perfusion set-up. There was only one inlet needle, one outlet needle 
and no PMMA needle holders for perfusion of model 1-5, see Table 23 p. 55. The vertical 
microscope made it possible to see the needle tip and funnel at the same time. This made it 
possible to insert the needle with lower risk of destroying the hydrogel around it, which also 
showed in the success rate of perfusion in Table 25.   
There were two needles inserted into two funnels at the same time in the perfusion of two- 
channel model. There was 2.9 mm distance center point to center point from the two funnels. The 
two needles needed to have precisely this distance between them to be inserted without 
destroying the hydrogel around the funnels. The needle holders were made for giving this stability 
and keep the needle completely vertical. The material PMMA was selected, since it is 
transparent.73 A transparent material would have made it possible to see through the holders via 
a microscope and visualize the needle tips and funnels. Thereby, visualize the navigation of the 
needles into the funnels without penetrating the hydrogel around the funnels.  However, the 
design of the PMMA holders was created through a laser and when the PMMA was irradiated, the 
material got cloudy, see Figure 30 p. 53. This made it impossible to see through the PMMA with 
the vertical microscope and thereby not see the funnels or the needles tips. The microscope was 
therefore angled by approximately 25°, see Figure 17 p. 41. The angle made it possible to see one 
needle tip and one funnel at the same time. This made it important that the hydrogel model was 
completely aligned with the needles, since there was no view to the neighbor needle or the 
neighbor funnel. This was done by the rotation stage with the fixed well, which contained the 
model, see Figure 15 p. 40.  In order to not ruin the hydrogel models with the needles pointy tips, 
they were cut off, as seen in Fig. 31 p. 53. However, they were a little rough around the edges, 
which were filed to make them smoother. The perfusion set-up with angled microscope and the 
PMMA needle holders was an unsustainable method of visualizing the needles and the funnels in 
the model. This should be carefully reconsidered if redesigning the perfusion set-up from this 
project.  
In total, there were 8 models that never got perfused. This was due to the inner side of models 
funnels getting penetrated by the needle, which propagated cracks in the hydrogel model, see 
==PAGE:66==
 
 
66 
 
Figure 37C p. 58.  Six of them got ruined even though there were no PMMA holders obstructing 
the view. However, most of the models were first showing leakage when perfused with dyes, see 
Figure 37A and Figure 37B p. 58. The leakage was due to the inner sides of the funnels getting 
penetrated, but not propagating any cracks. Instead there was backflow when the dyes reached 
the inlet funnel or there were leaked dyes when it reached the outlet funnel. This was due to the 
hydrogel not being tightly sealed by the needle. It was investigated if the flow rate was too high 
and it was decreased from 200 µl/min to 20 µl/min. However, the rate did not have any effect on 
the leakage.  
The time in water before perfusion was approximately 24-72 hours after printing. This was due to 
the model needing to reach a steady weight, see Figure 29 p. 52, and the non-polymerized resin 
needed to be rinsed from the channels. The perfusion of model 8, see Table 23 p. 55, was used to 
investigate if the model could be perfused after a longer period of time in water (32 days). It was 
the longest perfusion (~3 hours) of all the models. Numerous of perfusions of channels in the 
model were after only 24 hours in water. The time in water, like in model 8, needs to be 
investigated further with more hydrogel models to give any sustainable conclusion about the 
relationship between the time in water and the success of perfusion through the channels. In 
Figure 34 p. 56, the optical micrograph shows model 8 after perfusion. Picture A) shows the funnel 
got penetrated on the inner side but still could perfuse without leakage. This was most likely due 
to the needle being at a slight angle when inserted into the channel and could have closed for any 
leakage of the funnel. This indicated that the needle holders was not as stable as wanted.  As 
discussed, there were some limitations with the visibility of needles and funnels in the two-
channel perfusion set-up and there were only one successful two-channel perfusion generated in 
this project. Model 9, see Table 23 p. 55, were perfused for 27 seconds, see Figure 35 p. 57.  In 
Figure 36 p. 57, it was clear to see that all four needles went into the funnels without penetrating 
the inner hydrogel of the funnel. This indicated that it was possible to make two-channel perfusion 
if all factors of precision of the perfusion set-up were in place. The two-channel model were also 
printed using the PolyChrome 3D printer to investigate the success rate of perfusion of hydrogel 
models illuminated with a near-UV light source compared to a hydrogel model printed with a UV 
light source. However, there were too many limiting factors of the perfusion set-up to conclude 
anything substantial from the trials with these models.  
The COMSOL Multiphysics simulation, see Figure 38 p. 59-60, showed the stable and linear 
concentration gradient would be established after ~16 hours with diffusion coefficient of CCL21. 
This means the hydrogel model needs to be placed in the well in the perfusion set-up for at least 
~16 hours. Model 8 was placed in the well for approximately 15 hours (3 hours perfusion, 12 hours 
of waiting to be perfused again). There were water covering all surfaces of the model but was not 
covered with a lid, since the needles were in the way. The hydrogel model could not be perfused 
again after 12 hours. This could be due to evaporation of water. The hydrogel kept its steady 
swollen state by water diffusing in and out of the crosslinked structure. If the water had to be 
evaporated from the model, it would eventually make the model shrink, which leaded to change 
in dimensions of the model and made the hydrogel model more brittle. The well in the perfusion 
set-up was designed to keep the model hydrated at all times but as seen in Appendix D, it was 
only 1 mm higher than the model. To keep the hydrogel hydrated for 2 days or more, a solution 
could be to have a larger volume of water covering the surface of the hydrogel or a systematic 
refueling of water in the well.

The stress-strain relationship in thin-walled in vivo microvessel can be described as having 
exhibited viscoelastic behavior under time-dependent condition.74 In Larsen et al. 75, the 
compression modulus was determined for 20% PEGDA to be 0.68 MPa. Because the PEGDA was 
cured in a diethylene glycol diethyl ether (DGDE) solvent and thereafter placed in Millipore Milli-
Q™ water, this only gives an approximation of the compression strength of the hydrogel model 
compared to cure in water. This gave an indicator for what pressure the microchannels can 
experience without permanent deformation of the material. This was important to know since a 
change in radius in the channel affected the pressure difference between the channel ends and 
the shear stress, see Equation 6 and Equation 7 p. 26. The cross section of model 10 from Table 
23 p. 55 was investigated afterwards and can be viewed in Appendix J. There did not seem to be 
any permanent expansion/deformation of the channel after a flow rate of 200 µl/min. The 
deformation length was calculated for the 300 µm wall of the channel and gave 0.03 µm, which 
indicated that the PEGDA 700 hydrogel did not have a low elastic modulus.  
Pumps Functions  
The syringe pump was first tried in three channels test models, see Figure 32 p. 54. There was only 
pumped fluid in the inlet funnel, but not sucked out of the outlet funnel. The outlet tubing went 
directly to a waste container, see Figure 14A p. 38. There was only one-channel perfusion  in one 
model out of the 6 three-channel model. The fluid could not be perfused through the 
microchannel due to overpressure. The peristaltic pump was selected based on its ability to 
generate perfusion in a long period of time. However, it also solved the overpressure problem in 
the channels by having fluid pumped into the channel in the same pace as it was sucked out of the 
channel. The peristaltic pump gave an oscillatory and pulsating flow rate. This could give problems 
for perfusion with low flow rate. However, in this project the flow rate did not matter in the light 
of chemotaxis purpose. In the Einstein-Smoluchowski relation, Appendix B, the movement of 
molecules due to diffusion is described as the distance the molecule travels proportional to the 
square root of time. The flow rate does not affect the diffusion of molecules, which means it would 
take the molecule the same time of crossing the area if it perfused with 200 µl/min or 20 µl/min. 
Thereby, the flow rate with an oscillatory and pulsating flow would not affect the diffusion of 
molecules. However, if cells were to grow in the microchannels with a low flow rate, the selection 
of the peristaltic pump needed to be evaluated again regarding the effect the pulsating flow have 
on the given cells type.  
Properties of the Resin and Seeding cell culture in the Microchannels 
There was a requirement of mechanical stability to be able to perfuse the microchannels in this 
project and when inserted the needles into the funnel. Hydrogels are known for its low Young 
modulus and low stress to failure.76 There were two main issues with the use of PEGDA 700 
hydrogel. 1) The swelling affected the parameter of the dimension of the printed model, as 
mentioned earlier in Subsection “Limitations of the design due to Materials and Fabrication”. 2) 
The crosslinked hydrogel lead to low toughness of the model, which was shown when the needle 
was inserted, it induced stress on the hydrogel model that leaded to cracks or propagation of 
cracks, Figure 37B-C p. 58. PEGDA molecular weight, crosslinking, the solvent and the amount of 
polymer determined the swelling and mechanical properties of the hydrogel model. The 
properties of the resin and how it could affect the mechanical properties of the hydrogel and 
seeding of cells in the microchannels is elaborated on in this section.       
==PAGE:68==
 
 
68 
 
In Chan et al.77, they studied the relationship of molecular weight of PEGDA and how it affected 
volumetric swelling ratio and elastic modulus. The molecular weight of PEGDA was investigated 
with a fixed light dose. The conclusion was that the volumetric swelling ratio increased and the 
elastic moduli decreased with increasing PEG molecular weight. This means that PEGDA 700 had 
a higher elastic modulus and limited swelling properties compared to PEGDA with higher 
molecular weight. However, the PEGDA 700 is much cheaper than PEGDA with higher molecular 
weight.78 
The polymerization condition (photo initiator and the light dose) affected the swelling ratio and 
mechanical properties in hydrogel model. Different photo initiator concentration affects the 
material properties, since it gives different crosslinking density due to different of amounts of free 
radicals that is created by the photo initiator during the UV or near UV photopolymerization 
process.79 Khandaker M. et al.79 investigated the relationship of photo initiator concentration (α-
α-dimethoxy-α-phenylacetophenone) with constant light dose and the mechanical properties of 
PEGDA. The study concluded that the maximum failure stress were increased significantly by 
increased photo initiator concentration. This would mean if increasing the concentration of 
photoinitiator in the resin in this project, it would increase the stiffness of the model and thereby 
increase the probability for propagating cracks in the hydrogel. On the other hand, a decreased of 
photo initiator will increase toughness of the hydrogel but decrease it stiffness, which is needed 
keep microchannels and funnel stable for perfusion. Further, an increase of crosslink density 
would also give a smaller mesh size of the polymer network and reduce the diffusivity. Moreover, 
a high concentration of photo initiator had showed that the cells viability decreases due to 
cytotoxicity.80 Cell viability, pre-seeded, could also be affected by a too high light dose, which 
induces DNA damage in the cells. In Panda et al.81, the relationship of the amount of PEGDA (10-
40%) and cell viability was investigated with a constant photo initiator (2-hydroxy-1-(4-
(hydroxyethoxy) phenyl)-2-methyl-1-propanone) and constant light dose. The cell viability with 
PEGDA 10% and 20% were found to be high (>80%), whereas the concentration beyond 20% gave 
a decrease of cell viability.   
Chemoattractant Concentration Gradient  
The concept of the source/sink model is that a molecule would be released from the source and 
diffuse to the sink, which establishes a uniform concentration gradient, Figure 2 p. 14. As 
mentioned, the time of the longest perfusion was a one channel perfusion for approximately 3 
hours. Simulations of the source/sink were used to investigate the concentration gradient in the 
hydrogel model. The development of concentration gradient in a source/sink model, see Figure 
38 p. 59-60 was compared to a model without sink, see Figure 39 p. 60-61 through simulations. 
The concentration gradient could not be achieved by the model without sink, since the 
concentration gradient begins to even out as viewed in Figure 39B p. 61. However, the model 
without sink could be used for chemotaxis purpose approximately the first hour of perfusion with 
a very unstable gradient before the concentration begins to even out in the 2x4 mm area. The 
model without sink is not a better solution compared to Boyden chamber or Under-agarose gel 
assay, since these assays have the ability to keep an unstable gradient for 4-6 hours.  
The predicted mesh size of the hydrogel was investigated by using the results from the degree of 
swelling by Flory-Rehner theory, see Equation 3 and 4 p. 24. The predicted mesh size was found 
to be 7 nm, see Table 20 p. 52. The molecules with a diameter under 7 nm would be able to diffuse

Conclusion and Outlook 
The design of the channels in the model were horizontally paralleled to each other which meant 
it was possible for one linear gradient to be developed in the visible 2mm x 4mm area between 
the two channels and visualized (if using dyes) through a microscope. The designed channels in 
the model should mimic the vasculature geometric properties. However, there was a limitation of 
41 µm in resolution of the EnvisionTEC Perfactory® Micro 3D printer and the swelling of the 
hydrogel. The fabrication method and the resin with PEGDA 700 gave reproducible hydrogel 
models. The slight deviation from design dimensions to the printed models was due to pixilation 
and post swelling of the hydrogel after printing. Through test models and test microchannels the 
mechanical stability of the microchannel was found to be 300 µm x 300 µm square cross section 
with wall thickness of 300 µm, which was the final dimension of the microchannels in the two-
channel model. Through COMSOL Multiphysics simulation, the square cross section channel was 
shown to affect the shear stress of fluid at the wall in the channel with a significant factor. The 
fabrication of square cross section channels should be reconsidered if culturing endothelial cells. 
The selected peristaltic pump could provide a pulsating flow rate in the microchannels. However, 
this had no effect on the pace of diffusion since diffusion was not dependent on flow rate of the 
fluid. Moreover, the peristaltic pump could pump fluid in the microchannels with the same flow 
rate as fluid was sucked with. This solved the overpressure problem in the microchannels. The 
perfusion of the two-channel models had a low success rate due to two main issues. 1) The 
visibility of the funnels of the model and needles were poor due to the needle holders, which 
made it difficult to navigate the needles into funnels without penetrating the hydrogel around. 2) 
The low toughness of the hydrogel. The cracks created by the needle could propagate and leakage 
could be generated. The relationship between PEGDA molecular weight, crosslinking, the solvent 
and amount of polymer were surveyed in the literature to see if any parameter could be changed 
to give the wanted properties of the hydrogel model. The elastic property is affected by the 
molecular weight, whereas PEGDA 700 had a higher elastic modulus than PEGDA with higher 
molecular weight. A PEGDA with a higher molecular weight could be selected to be used for 
chemotaxis modeling, however, higher molecular weight is more expensive than PEGDA 700.  The 
amount of PEGDA should be 20 % or under to keep a high cell viability. PEGDA 700 hydrogel had 
a too low toughness to be used in the perfusion set-up in this project. However, the perfusion set-
up should also be reassessed since the PMMA holders were not an optimal solution to keep the 
needles fixed. The mesh size of the 20 % PEGDA, in this project, was compatible with diffusion 
medium with chemoattractants, CCL21, through the walls. The time of the longest perfusion was 
~3 hours and only through one channel perfusion. There could not be shown a concentration 
gradient between the two channels. Therefore, COMSOL Multiphysics simulations were used to 
show the generation of concentration gradient in the source/sink model. It showed the 
concentration gradient could be generated after ~16 hours. In comparison, the sink was removed 
and it was clear that the concentration gradient never would be linear and the area between the 
two channels would have an uniform concentration after ~ 2 days. In this project, the 
concentration gradient could likely have been generated if 1) the two-channel model could be 
created with high value of toughness but simultaneously with high enough stiffness value to be 
perfused through the microchannels or 2) other external connectors, which can be inserted into 
the funnels without penetrating the hydrogel around the funnel and be sealed tight around the 
funnels.

The further work and the secondary requirements can be fulfilled in different ways of the PEGDA 
hydrogel two-channel model. A solution to create mechanical stability of the inlet/outlet in the 
channels is to utilize two different resins in the PolyChrome 3D printer. By utilizing different 
polymerization methods it would allow for the creation of microchannel structure in PEGDA 700 
hydrogel and use, e.g. epoxy, to create a harder structure of the inlet/outlet. The PolyChrome 3D 
printer make it possible to photopolymerize with two different light sources and by selecting two 
different photo initiators, which have distinct absorption, the 3D model can be built in two 
different materials. However two-component µ-SLA 3D printing has at this time not been 
attempted. Through the assumption that the perfusion of the two-channel model could generate 
a concentration gradient the following should be considered and investigated. PEGDA is bioinert, 
which prevents the cells from attaching to its surface of the PEGDA hydrogel. However, addition 
of ECM protein-derived cell adhesive peptides, such as RGD peptide sequences, to PEGDA have 
showed to improve cell viability, proliferation, and spreading.82 The coating of adhesive peptides 
or molecules can be done pre- or post-grafting. The design of horizontally paralleled channels 
makes it possible to observe through vertical microscope, which makes it easier to observe 
migration and intravasation of the tumor cluster/cells into the channel. Intravasation of channel 
is not possible with the porosity of PEGDA 700 hydrogel in this project. Pores needed to be 
integrated in the wall of the channel, which makes it possible for cells to migrate into the vessel. 
The design between the two channels needs also to be redesigned with marks or micron pillars 
into the middle of the 2.00 mm x 4.00 mm area. These marks or pillars can be viewed through a 
microscope and aim in placing the tumor cells/cluster in the middle of the two channels.

Appendix E: COMSOL Multiphysics 5.2 and the Physics used in the 
Project 
The fluid motion in the microchannel can be modeled and simulated in the software program 
COMSOL multiphysics version 15.2 that uses computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD). CFD is a platform 
for simulating devices and system that involves fluid flow, and it has a ready-made physic 
interface. This makes it possible to configure the model input through graphical user interface 
(GUI). When selecting an input, there is formulate the relevant equations for the given input and 
model.  For microfluidic fluid flow and diffusion of molecules through material the following 
physics can applies. 
The Creeping Flow: Creeping flow is also a referred as the Stokes flow, where systems have a low 
viscosity or small geometrical length scale. The equations applying to the creeping flow is the 
Stokes equations for conservation of momentum, which states that the total momentum of a 
closed system does not change over time, and the continuity equation for conservation of mass, 
which states the mass of a closed system does not change over time. 83,84 The transport of Diluted 
species: Simulation of the concentration field of a dilute solute in a solvent. It is used to see how 
transport and reactions of the given species is dissolved in a liquid. The transport is a driving force, 
such as diffusion by Fick’s laws.83,84
