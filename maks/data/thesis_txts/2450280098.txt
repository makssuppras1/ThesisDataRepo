Abstract
O
ver the course of two decades a phenomenon of overproduction of turbulence for
the use of Reynolds averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) equations including turbulence
models has been known. The consequences of this behavior are severe pollution
of turbulence both pre- and post-breaking, premature wave decay as well as alterations of
undertow proﬁles. Previous research has found conditional instability which was extended
to unconditional instability of well-known two-equation turbulence closure models.
This
thesis has extended the review to the more advanced Reynolds stress models which also
show the same tendency of over-production of turbulence even in the nearly potential ﬂow
region. An analysis ﬁnds that the equations are likewise unconditionally unstable for the
Reynolds stress models and a subsequent simulation of a propagating wave conﬁrms this
for the present stress−ϵ model while the implemented stress−ω model stays practically
stable. The two-equation models had the whole potential ﬂow region being unstable while
the stress−ϵ model is only unstable in the surface region. The growth rate of turbulence
was not possible to predict due to known simulation issues where the crest surface velocities
are overpredicted, giving inaccurate velocity gradients.
Both a ﬂow modiﬁed closure coeﬃcient for the production term of the dissipation equa-
tion and an added buoyancy term in the k- and ϵ-equations are implemented for stability.
Only the buoyancy works for simulation purposes as the ﬂow modiﬁed closure coeﬃcient is
passive in sheared ﬂow and this happens to be true exactly in the surface region where the
model is unstable. The stable models could keep a nearly constant wave form propagating
without exponential growth of turbulence.
Extending the simulations to breaking waves the diﬀerences between stable and unstable
Reynolds stress models are clear in that unstable models pollute the whole computational
domain with turbulence killing the wave even before breaking while the stable models behave
close to the two-equation models. They give accurate surface elevation plots and predict the
undertow and turbulent kinetic energy from pre-breaking to outer surf-zone though the inner
surf-zone is like standard closures. The best model overall is the stress−ω model though it
functions as a more expensive version of the k −ω model.
Keywords: Computational ﬂuid dynamics; Turbulence modelling; Reynolds stress mod-
els; Surface gravity waves; Breaking waves; OpenFOAM.

Introduction
T
he natural phenomenon of breaking waves plays an important role in the morpho-
logical dynamics in and around the surf zone. The balance of sediment transport
is easily tipped to either side depending on the inﬂuential factors present in a given
area. A major contributor is the eﬀect of breaking waves that has qualitatively diﬀerent
undertow proﬁles depending on the breaker type. This is one of the determining factors in
the formation of breaker bars. Therefore it is of the essence to describe the velocities and
turbulence levels over the duration from pre-breaking to inner surf zone.
Ting & Kirby (1994) is one of the more well-known experimental studies on the eﬀect
of breaking waves for a spilling and plunging breaker.
In newer times however, where
the usage of computation has become more and more widespread all while computational
power is increasing, it is natural to use computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD) to model
near-shore coastal dynamics. While direct Navier-Stokes simulation (DNS) is possible, it
is both costly and time consuming. For this very reason a simpliﬁcation of the problem
has been achieved through the Reynolds averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) equations. This
approximative method models the average ﬂow at the beneﬁcial eﬀect of decreased mesh
grid reﬁnement needed. The cost is that a turbulence closure model is needed to bridge the
gap. Many diﬀerent types of turbulence closure models have been established over the years
including one-equation models, two-equation models like the widely known Wilcox (1988)
k−ω model and the Launder & Sharma (1974) k−ϵ model [in cartesian coordinates by Jones
& Launder (1972) and Jones & Launder (1973)], with k being turbulent kinetic energy, ω
speciﬁc dissipation rate and ϵ dissipation. Furthermore, some versions of the more advanced
Reynolds stress models have also been established.
Over the course of the last two decades the problem of over-production of turbulence
in a nearly potential ﬂow has been known. The problem has resulted in pollution of the
turbulence both pre- and post-breaking possibly leading to premature wave decay and al-
terations of the surf-zone undertow proﬁles. Devolder et al. (2017) combated this with the
introduction of a buoyancy term in the k-equation, but it did not solve the inherent prob-
lem. Mayer & Madsen (2000) proved that the Wilcox (1988) k −ω model was conditionally
unstable resulting in exponential growth for relatively low values of ω. Recently Larsen &
Fuhrman (2018) has extended this to an unconditional instability in the nearly potential
ﬂow region for a wide array of two-equation models. They also proposed a modiﬁcation of
the Wilcox (2006) k −ω model to include a second ﬂow-dependent stress-limiter active in
nearly potential ﬂow while reducing to the Wilcox (2006) model in a sheared ﬂow region.
This closed a long-standing problem for the two-equation models.
Brown et al. (2016) also acknowledged the over-production problem, where in addition
to studying two-equation models they also implemented a Reynolds stress model resembling
the best known of the stress models, the Launder et al. (1975) Launder-Reece-Rodi (LRR)
model. While the stress models are superior to the two-equation models in that they do not
assume isotropic turbulence and in turn are expected to give results resembling experiments

Launder-Reece-Rodi Model
The Launder et al. (1975) LRR model is the most well-known of the stress-transport closure
models and takes basis in the ϵ-equation for dissipation along with all 6 of the Reynolds
stress equations.
It is an extension of the two equation closure models in that it uses
Reynolds stresses directly instead of the turbulent kinetic energy and most notably includes
a pressure-strain relation term as diﬀerence to for instance the Launder & Sharma (1974)
k −ϵ model.
Sadly, there are limitations to the way of implementing terms in the turbulence models
of OpenFOAM as the software keeps keen track of the variable types (due to the basis in the
C++ language), ﬁeld types (scalars compared to tensors) and units rather than simply being
a numerical tool. Therefore it has been deemed too diﬃcult to implement the diﬀusion term
for the stress equation as stated in Eq. (2.2.1). Instead the diﬀusion term implemented
in the “out of the box” OpenFOAM version of the LRR model seen in Eq. (2.2.11) is used.
Furthermore, there have been encounters with many issues of numerical instability trying
to run the model with the modiﬁed dissipation term, even for low Courant numbers. The
reason for the instability is still unknown to the author, but it has to do with the dissipation
term in the ϵ-equation wherefore the model is further modiﬁed to have the same dissipation
term as the OpenFOAM version of the LRR model seen in Eq. (2.2.12).

This means that if the period and depth averaged value of s is small enough
compared to the total simulation time, it would actually seem like the model is stable in the
potential ﬂow region which it deﬁnitely is not.
As mentioned previously in the equilibrium analysis, there are several solutions to the
analytical problem and using a constant negative value for s in the simulation with same
absolute value as shown in Figure 3.1.1 would also give way to a positive growth rate of
equal magnitude which contradicts the result of Eq. (3.1.24) or Eq. (3.1.29). The analysis
tells that a negative s would give a negative growth rate. This is because the solution was
sought in the intention of using a positive s value. If looking for the equilibrium solution to
Eq. (3.1.25) and Eq. (3.1.26) having a negative s in mind, the correct solution found would
be exactly as given by Eq. (3.1.29) just with a negative sign. This way a negative s value
would still yield a positive growth rate and with the same magnitude as given by its positive
s counterpart.
Even though a period and depth averaged value of the vertical derivative, ∂u/∂y, is
never expected to be negative when working with potential ﬂow or stream function theory,
the time varying values of s would still be negative for about half the period depending on
where in relation to the crest and trough one might look.

T
his chapter deals with the analytical stabilization of the Launder et al. (1975) LRR

closure coeﬃcients needed for the buoyancy terms are also found.

Buoyancy Stabilization
It turns out that the ﬂow modiﬁcation of the dissipation production closure coeﬃcient
does not inﬂuence the stability of the stress turbulence closure models as the turbulence is
produced in the crest to trough region. This is also the place where pΩ/p0 ≈1 and hence
no stabilization is achieved through the modiﬁed coeﬃcient even when using a high value
for λ2. Only the already stable region of the water column is altered through the use of a
ﬂow modiﬁed closure coeﬃcient. This means that a diﬀerent way has to be established to
stabilize the stress turbulence models. It turns out that the Wilcox (2006) stress−ω model
is stable for practical purposes. Then, only the Launder et al. (1975) stress−ϵ model is of
interest in this regard.
Since the turbulence production is only located near the surface, a possible solution
to the problem is to insert a buoyancy term into the equations, as already presented in
Eq. (2.2.2) and Eq. (2.2.5). Going by a buoyancy term only for the equivalent k-equation
as found in Larsen & Fuhrman (2018) would be the simplest introduction of a buoyancy
term. This has already been accomplished by Burchard (2002), Rodi (1987), and Ruessink
et al. (2009) for the k −ϵ model; Fuhrman et al. (2013), and Umlauf et al. (2003) for the
k −ω model; and Devolder et al. (2017) for the k −ω SST model. Introducing the buoyancy
in this way only modiﬁes the diagonal stress equations. However, this has the implication
that a numerical instability is met when running an OpenFOAM simulation. Overcoming this
numerical disadvantage is achieved by turning to Burchard (2002, p. 18, 21) for the stress−ϵ
implementation of buoyancy. It is necessary to implement terms both for the stress-equation
as well as for the ϵ-equation. Still, only the diagonal components of Reynolds stresses have
been implemented as the derivation of closure coeﬃcients for the buoyancy terms is only
based on the k-equation rather than the full set of stress equations.
As Burchard (2002) is using a diﬀerent set of coordinate axes and coordinate system di-
rections as well as notation, the exact relation for the buoyancy term described in Eq. (2.2.2)

5.2 |
Wall Functions
The momentum equation and turbulence model equations do not in themselves put limita-
tions on what is practically possible with respect to modelling over the whole of a boundary
layer, but Wilcox (2006) and Menter (1993) both describe the problem for most turbulence
models that they cannot integrate through the viscous sublayer without severe loss of ac-
curacy. For the models reviewed in this report there are going to be limitations on the
ϵ-equation, while the ω-equation does not experience this problem. The problem can be
mitigated by the introduction of damping functions utilizing Reynolds-number dependent
corrections. Introducing these terms it is possible to model all the way down to the vis-
cous sublayer and usually a y+ ≤1 is necessary to obtain proper results. Another way of
obviating the problem is to make use of wall functions based on the theory of boundary
layers.
A perturbation analysis of the log-layer equations give way to boundary conditions for
k, ω, and ϵ such that the ﬁrst cell can be placed in the log-layer thus reducing the nec-
essary computational work drastically as the mesh acceptably can be much coarser. The
analysis and consequent relations are described mainly by Wilcox (2006, pp. 156, pp. 181,
pp. 343) while Bredberg (2000) additionally explains the concept of an eﬀective viscosity.

5.3.1
Smooth Data
The model validation results for the smooth case are presented in Figure 5.3.1 through
Figure 5.3.5. Starting with the k −ω SST and k −ϵ models which have already been tested
by previous studies [Fuhrman et al. (2010) for the k −ω model] it shows that there is good
agreement with the Grass (1971) experimental results with the exemption of some known
problems.
Looking at the k-ω SST model the ω-equation has the advantage that it can integrate
through the viscous sublayer which has been utilized as seen in Figure 5.3.1.
This has
the beneﬁt that the whole range of the water column can be determined by this kind of
numerical study. In general the data for velocity and shear stress ﬁts the expected results
and for turbulent kinetic energy this is also reasonable except in the near wall area as the
spike in turbulence is not found. This story has already been told by Fuhrman et al. (2010)
and Wilcox (2006, Fig. 4.25 & Fig. 4.29) stating that the k −ω model experiences this
short-coming for a smooth turbulent ﬂow.
For the k −ϵ model the ϵ-equation does not have the same possibility of integrating
through the viscous sublayer because the standard version of the ϵ-equation does not include
viscous damping as stated by Wilcox (2006, ch. 4.6.3), giving way to unrealistic values for
velocity and turbulence if used in the same manner as the k −ω model.
Instead, wall
functions must be implemented to avoid integration through the viscous sublayer. These
are, as stated previously, only applicable in the log layer wherefore the ﬁrst cell center is
required to be located in the log layer of some (30 −70) < y+. Looking at Figure 5.3.2 it
is seen that the chosen position of the ﬁrst cell resulted in a y+ = 50.16. This is evident
as there is a discontinuity in gradient at this exact location which explains the part of the
curves under the ﬁrst cell center near the bed where there is a jump from the bed to the
ﬁrst large cell. The rest of the cells onward are all relatively small compared to the ﬁrst cell.
The discontinuity in gradient is seen especially for the velocity proﬁle and the shear stress
proﬁle, the latter of which is calculated based on the ﬁrst. Knowing that it is calculated
from Eq. (5.3.3), it is seen that the discontinuity in velocity proﬁle gradient will impact the
results of the calculated shear stress distribution.
To avoid “wiggles” in the velocity- and turbulent kinetic energy proﬁle, the mesh must
be kept fairly ﬁne. The eﬀect of change from a ﬁrst large cell to smaller cells has not been
investigated, but could potentially explain some discrepancy seen for the velocity proﬁles,
especially for the smooth LRR model in Figure 5.3.4.

both simulations the level of dissipation is sustained while the analysis would quickly decay
a possibly high ω or ωeq to reach its equilibrium value. In this case it is apparent that the
modiﬁed LRR model keeps an ωeq that is lower than the equilibrium value thus resulting
in turbulence while the stress−ω has a suﬃciently high ω above the equilibrium value that
turbulence growth is not initiated.

Larsen & Fuhrman (2018) explained that the larger eddy viscosity found for the case
with the Wilcox (2006) stress limiter λ1 = 0 gave a more “correct” ﬂow resistance giving
way to the more correct velocity proﬁles in the inner surf zone, while the stabilized models
seemingly gave a too low value of eddy viscosity at the surface which in turn would result
in a faster surface roller and subsequently larger undertow. The new results found for the
stress models exhibit the same qualitative exaggeration in the inner surf zone and raises the
question that maybe the stabilized turbulence models predict the correct eddy viscosity, but
is it the shortcoming of the interface velocities present in OpenFOAM that causes the exagger-
ated proﬁles? As mentioned in Larsen et al. (2018) and seen from Figure 6.3.4, OpenFOAM
can severely overestimate the velocities at the very surface and it takes a very small Courant
number to correct this extreme behavior. The modiﬁed Launder et al. (1975) LRR model
including buoyancy does give lower eddy viscosity at the surface compared to the Larsen
& Fuhrman (2018) case with Wilcox (2006) stress limiter λ1 = 0, but still higher than the
stabilized two-equation models, and yet it shows the same exaggerated behavior.

8
Conclusions
T
he scope of this thesis has been to study the apparent instability of Reynolds stress
models in the nearly potential ﬂow region as seen in Brown et al. (2016) leading to
exponential turbulence and eddy viscosity growth, premature wave decay, as well
as pollution of the surf-zone interfering with the undertow proﬁles. The results have been
divided into three main parts: An analysis of the model equations and subsequent stabi-
lization, a simulation of a propagating wave train to show the instability problem, and a
simulation of breaking waves for both the unstable and stabilized models to show the eﬀect
of instability on breaking as well as trying to attain better results with the stress models
than their two-equation counterparts.
8.1 |
Analysis
After the Reynolds stress models had been established, an analysis was performed on the
model equations. Convective and dissipation terms were neglected for the use in the potential
ﬂow region. Then the normal stresses located in the diagonal of the stress matrix were
combined into an equivalent k-equation. Along with the oﬀ-diagonal τ12 and the dissipation
through either ω or ϵ a simpliﬁed system of equations was deduced. It was shown that ω or
an equivalent ωeq = ϵ/ (Cµk) went to a constant equilibrium value along with an established
utility variable Ψ = k/τ12. In the equilibrium event the three equations decoupled, and
being linearised, the solution for k and τ12 was that of exponential growth. The growth
rate was calculated analytically only depending on the closure coeﬃcients and the velocity
gradients through the factor (2s −Ω). The analysis then proved that both the stress−ϵ
and the stress−ω models were unconditionally unstable as found similarly by Larsen &
Fuhrman (2018) for the two-equation models. Though the analysis had roots in the isotropic
turbulence assumption, an extended numerical analysis of the model equation showed that
the results for constant equilibrium values and growth rates were not signiﬁcantly diﬀerent
to make up for all the trouble of not assuming isotropic turbulence when the simple analysis
proved much easier and also gave analytical results for the instability.
Since the analysis showed that the models were unstable, the next logical step was to
ﬁx the problem. This was done in the stabilization chapter by modiﬁcation of the produc-
tion term closure coeﬃcient for the dissipation equation, either α for the stress−ω model
or Cϵ1 for the stress−ϵ model. This ﬂow modiﬁcation of the closure coeﬃcient eﬀectively
resulted in negative growth rates rendering the model stable. Still, this had the implication
later in the propagating wave train simulation chapter that it was not feasible for practical
purposes. As the stabilization was based on the shear production modulus, p0, to rotation
modulus, pΩ, ratio, it was eﬀective in all regions except the near surface interphase region
where the turbulence instability was located. Since the simulation showed pΩ/p0 ≈1, it
made stabilization impossible to achieve. Instead, a buoyancy term was introduced for both
the k- and ϵ-equation, though only for the stress−ϵ model as the stress−ω model through

8.2 |
Wave Train
Before a proper wave train simulation and subsequent simulation of breaking waves including
boundary layers was to run, a validation of the stress models on a simple steady ﬂow case
was needed. This validation was performed for both a smooth and rough bottom like for
the Grass (1971) experiments. The two versions of LRR model did show to have diﬃculty
in predicting the velocity- and turbulent kinetic energy proﬁles while the stress−ω model
performed generally better giving very similar results to the k −ω SST model. The ω-
equation also had the advantage that it could integrate through the viscous sublayer for the
smooth case while the ϵ-equation needed wall functions for both smooth and rough case.
Regarding the individual stress-components the stress−ω performed close to the ex-
pected log-layer analysis of Wilcox (2006) resulting in an approximate 4 : 3 : 2 relation for
τ11 : τ33 : τ22 while the two other stress−ϵ models did not come close. Part of the explana-
tion for the results is found in the implementation. Both the modiﬁed LRR and OpenFOAM
version of LRR model used a diﬀerent diﬀusion term for the k- and ϵ-equation similar to the
Launder & Sharma (1974) k −ϵ model. This way it is not the exact Launder et al. (1975)
LRR model used which is why it does not ﬁt well with neither the Wilcox (2006) analysis
nor the Grass (1971) experimental results.
Moving on to the actual propagating wave train the models did partly conﬁrm the anal-
ysis result of instability as the stress−ϵ models exploded with turbulence decaying the wave
within a couple of periods. The wave, that should be gradually decreasing only due to nu-
merics, disappeared in the matter of seconds thereby proving the extend of the problem. On
the other hand the stress−ω model did not go unstable as the analysis suggested. It showed
results like the other stabilized models, including the modiﬁed LRR model with buoyancy.
The qualitative diﬀerence between the stress−ϵ and stress−ω model was discussed using
snapshots from OpenFOAM. It was shown that the over-production of turbulence was linked
to the interaction between the water- and air phase where the production in the interphase
was much larger than just below in the water. This large production along with a relatively
low ωeq for the stress−ϵ model results in exponential turbulence growth while a vastly higher
ω located at the turbulence production in the surface prevented the initiation of turbulence
growth.
8.3 |
Breaking Waves
The breaking wave case was set up as a real wave situation that included boundary layers and
a slope to either result in spilling or plunging breakers. Again, the unstable stress−ϵ models
gave way to serious problems with production of turbulence in both the nearly potential
ﬂow region as well as in the sheared ﬂow region of the surf-zone. It even gave much worse
results than Brown et al. (2016) predicted, but exponential growth depends on the initial
conditions.
Looking at the eddy viscosity the whole domain was polluted with turbulence while only
the surf-zone was expected to have any signiﬁcant turbulence. This resulted in wave break-
down for the spilling breaker eﬀectively killing the wave before even nearing the breaking
point as well as messing up the breaking process for the plunging breaker. With this in

velocity proﬁles as they both exaggerated the wave drift and undertow in the inner surf-zone
resembling the results of the stabilized Larsen & Fuhrman (2018) k−ω model. This suggests
that the eddy viscosity calculated near the surface might not be wrong as previously stated
by Larsen & Fuhrman (2018), but could be attributed to the shortcoming that OpenFOAM
simulates velocity proﬁles with an overestimation of velocity under the crest while the proﬁle
below in turn is underestimated because of continuity.
In summation of the results for all chapters the stress−ω model is the best of the models
tested, but it performs much like the k −ω model making it a more expensive tool to get
the same results knowing that it works on all 6 stress-components rather than a combined
k-equation.
8.4 |
Further Works
Since this thesis did not include the correct version of the Launder et al. (1975) LRR model
it is worth a try to simulate at least the steady boundary layer ﬂow including the correct
dissipation terms to get a close match to the Wilcox (2006) analysis in the log-layer. This
could in turn have beneﬁcial eﬀects on the wave breaking results to give as good results as
the stress−ω model or maybe even better.
Only a quick look was taken on the explanation of turbulence growth for the stress−ϵ
model contra the stress−ω model. A more in depth explanation could give way to stabilize
the LRR model in a diﬀerent fashion such that it does not produce unwanted turbulence
right before or at breaking. This could help the results located further into the surf-zone.
It was mentioned that the stress−ω model behaved like the k −ω model in the inner
surf-zone exaggerating the velocity proﬁles due to what is believed to be an erroneous eddy
viscosity at the surface which would let surface rollers be faster and then create more under-
tow. It was also mentioned that since so many models predict the same kind of behavior, this
might rather have to do with the overprediction of velocity under the crest due to OpenFOAM
simulation shortcomings. A study of this could be of interest to determine whether the
faults stem from the simulation or the model.

Structure of the Appendices
A guide of how the appendices are structured is presented in the overview below. The guide
serves as a quick overview of the appendices and brieﬂy describes the main content of each
appendix.
Appendix A Unstable Models Wave Train Simulation with Turbulence Model Turned Oﬀ-
Contains the simulation results of a propagating wave with unstable turbulence models
turned oﬀ. Essentially it shows that the wave propagates without signiﬁcant decrease
in wave height.
Appendix B Snapshots of Propagating Wave Train Simulation - Contains snapshots of
normalized turbulence parameters for a propagating wave over the OpenFOAM simula-
tion domain. Figures are shown for both stress−ϵ and stress−ω model with diﬀerent
color scales and at two time steps. They show the exponential growth for the stress−ϵ
model and the high ω values for the stress−ω model.
Appendix C Surface Elevation Time Series for Breaking Waves - Contains time series
of surface elevations for the breaking wave simulations at the probing locations in
Table 7.1.3 and Figure 7.1.2. They are shown for the ﬁrst 60 periods during the warm-
up phase and it can be seen how the surface elevations go to equilibrium values over
time. It is also seen how the wave turns more and more asymmetrical as it propagates
up the slope.
Appendix D Reynolds Stress Proﬁles for Breaking Waves - Contains the Reynolds stress
proﬁles for the breaking wave simulations supporting the observations made for the
turbulent kinetic energy proﬁles while also showing the evolution of the individual
components as the wave moves from pre-breaking through the outer surf-zone to the
inner surf-zone.
