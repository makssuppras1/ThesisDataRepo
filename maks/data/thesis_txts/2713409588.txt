Abstract:
This study focuses on characterising the acoustic outlet of a hear-
ing instrument (HI) through the development of an analytical
and a numerical models of a generic behind the ear (BTE) device.
The analytical model is set to reproduce the frequency response
of the sound pressure measured at the microphone. Initially, the
geometry of sound path is simplified to a single tube so it could
be solved using the one-dimensional Helmholtz equation. Then,
the lumped element method (LEM) is utilised to model the differ-
ent parts of the sound path individually and put them together
into a single system. Afterwards, as the LEM fails to model the
higher frequency behaviour of the parts of the system, a trans-
mission matrix method (TMM) is developed, which also allows
the introduction of the visco-thermal losses in small tubes. The
analytical model’s frequency response is comparable to the meas-
ured one, but is inaccurate.
The numerical model is developed in order to introduce the
vibro-acoustic interactions to the system. It is developed trough
the finite element method (FEM) using COMSOL. The geometry
of the system is designed to replicate the sound path and its
physical properties are defined accordingly. Boundary conditions
are imposed on the system to be solved, and points and frames of
evaluation are defined. A convergence study is done in order to
find the optimal mesh of element sizes between 0.285 −0.5 mm.
The pressure frequency responses of the FEM and TMM models
correlate well, while the vibrations in the system are introducing
resonances in the low frequencies. A study of the system in the
mechanical domain reveals that the vibration are caused by the
bends in the system, which is also confirmed by a straight version
of the FEM model.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Hearing aids and hearing instruments (HIs) are intricate devices with many
components interacting with each other. When designing a hearing aid there are
many considerations to be taken, acoustically these include the pure acoustic be-
haviours and vibroacoustic interactions of the different components in the sound
path. Design and prototyping of new devices can be an expensive and time con-
suming procedure, therefore there is a demand for high precision models of the
acoustic behaviour of HIs. These models allow simulation and analysis of the
predicted acoustic characteristics of a HI, thus reducing the time and costs of
design and prototyping. Currently, there is a motion to create a complete model
of the whole sound path of a HI by combining high precision models of the
individual components in the path. Such a model of the complete sound path
will allow detailed systematic investigations of the effects of the different system
parameters and their configuration on the acoustic performance of HIs. This in
turn would improve the optimisation of the different systems, their interactions
and the overall performance of the devices.
In general, HIs are divided to three types: behind the ear (BTE), in the ear (ITE)
and receiver in the ear (RIE). The receiver is the loudspeaker of the hearing aid.
This name comes from the telecommunication terminology.
BTE:
As the name suggests, this type of HI sits behind the ear. All the com-
ponents of the HI are contained within a casing that sits behind the ear and the
sound from the receiver goes into the ear through an acoustic tube.
ITE:
Here, unlike in the BTE, the casing sits in the ear canal, so no additional
tubing is needed.
RIE:
This type’s casing is sitting behind the ear like the BTE, but the receiver
does not sit inside of it, it sits in the ear canal instead.

A big concern in HI development is the issue of feedback. Because of the close
proximity of the microphones and the receiver, the signal from the receiver can
be picked up by the microphones and is fed back to the system, making it un-
stable. Feedback can occur in different domains (e.g. electrical, mechanical or
acoustic domains) and ideally, should be eliminated in all of them.
Models
such as the one developed in this project are a valuable tool in recognising the
source of these feedbacks. All components in the sound path and around it may
resonate with the output from the receiver, begin vibrating, and transmit the
vibrations back into the microphones. Hence, the vibro-acoustic coupling is a
source of feedback of high importance. This is especially prominent in the BTE
type HIs, as a significant part of the sound path from the receiver is sitting inside
the casing, specifically in the curved earhook. Thus, the scope of this project is
limited only to the BTE type HI.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.1
Project Description
This project concerns the modelling and characterisation of the sound path from
the receiver of the HI to the ear. That means that through this report, several
methods will be utilised in order to replicate the measured behaviour of this
system.
As can be seen in Fig. 1.1, the sound path consists of three main parts, the
suspension which is housed within the HI’s casing and is connected to the re-
ceiver, the earhook, and the BTE tube. The end of the BTE tube connects to an
earmold or dome that sit in the HI user’s ear canal.
The investigation of the behaviour of the sound path will be divided into two
models
1. Analytical model
2. Numerical model
The analytical model will first attempt to describe the purely acoustic behaviour
of the system through gradual consideration of more elements of the system.
The additional elements will be introduced through employment of more cap-
able methods. Then, a numerical model will be developed. This model will use
the finite element method (FEM) and will be designed in COMSOL Multiphysics
6.0[1].
The pure acoustic model is expected to provide insight into the direct effect
of the sound path on the sound wave propagating through it. This includes
the filtering introduced by the shape and size of the tubes, the effects of the
transition between tubes, and the visco-thermal losses from the inside of the
tubes. Then, the same behaviours will also be attempted to be replicated through
a FEM model. This would allow both visualisation of the behaviour which may
provide more intuitive understanding of it and consideration of conditions that
are too cumbersome or complex to be solved analytically, such as the vibro-
acoustic interactions. The vibro-acoustics will help describe the deformation of
the tubes due to the propagating sound wave and the influence of the casing
and earhook on the acoustic behaviour of the system. This will possibly provide
insights on feedback resulting from the transmission of sound through vibration
in the HI, specifically the earhook, back into the microphones.
The models will be compared and validated with the a preliminary measure-
ment of the system that is shown in Fig. 1.3. A picture of the measurement set-up
can seen in Fig. 1.2. The outlet tube dimensions of the HI used for the measure-
ment are listed in Table 1.1. The outlet tube is connected through an earmold
substitution to a 2cc coupler, which consists of a cylindrical cavity with effect-
ive volume of 2 · 10−6 m3[2]. Because of the coupler’s standardised volume and
shape, it is simple to include its effect on the system in the model. Its impedance

Analytical Model
In this chapter, the behaviour of the system will be modelled analytically. Spe-
cifically, the purely acoustic response of the system will be described based on
the plane wave equation with considerations of losses and discontinuity inside
and between the tubes. First, the system will be described analytically. Then,
a model will be developed through lumped element method (LEM). Finally, a
transmission matrix model (TMM) of the system will be derived. Each method
will also introduce additional considerations to represent the system more pre-
cisely.

As defined in Section 1.1, the outlet tube consists of three sections. Each of these
sections is an acoustic tube with a specific cross-section diameter.
While possible, it is cumbersome to construct the equation for the response
between the source and output pressures as done in Eq. (2.12), especially as
the termination impedance of each section is the total acoustic impedance of
the following sections. Hence, another method is employed here. Lumped ele-
ment model (LEM) is based on the transmission line method that allows to take
systems in one domain and describe them as analogous systems in another do-
main.
This is done by decomposing the system into discrete elements.
The
most common application of LEM is the description of multi-domain systems
using analogous electrical circuits, because it allows the use of well established
design and processing methods from that domain. The method requires the do-
mains of interest to have an analogous mathematical behaviour, so the equations
constituting the behaviour in each domain are similar. Here, the domains have
similarity in their harmonic behaviour.
The discrete lumped elements are described based on the following assump-
tions:
• The physical dimensions of the elements are significantly smaller than the
wavelength of the frequencies of interest. It is commonly considered that
the results of a LEM are valid when the wavelength is at least ten times
larger than the largest dimension of the component.
• All elements are ideal, which means that non of the elements show para-
sitic effects and that the bodies and walls of the elements are rigid and
in-compressible.
==PAGE:17==

• The elements have homogeneous properties, meaning all their properties
are concentrated into a single point. Hence, the shape of the element does
not have an effect on the response of the system, only its volume.
• The behaviours of all elements have analytical solutions.

same domain, such as coupled pipes, as the system is divided into discrete
elements. Additionally, as the receiver is an electro-acoustic component, an ana-

Chapter 3
Numerical Model
This chapter deals with the development of a finite element method (FEM)
model of the system. The following will discuss the considerations taken in
the design.
The field of acoustics deals with the wave equation, a partial differential
equation, under different boundary conditions. As showcased in Chapter 2, a
system can be solved analytically, but only to a certain level of complexity, hence
some simplification are needed. In order to solve the full differential equations,
a different approach has to be taken. Numerical models are utilising numer-
ical methods to approximate the solutions of the equation systems describing a
model.
The way this is done is by dividing the system into smaller domains and ap-
proximate the solutions for the relevant boundary conditions. The main methods
used in numerical acoustics are boundary and finite element methods, where
the boundary element method divides the domain into continuous boundary
domains and FEM divides the whole geometry of the system into discrete ele-
ments.
It should be emphasised that numerical models are not perfect. The division
of the system into domains introduces errors, hence the quality of the discret-
isation of the model will be investigated in Section 3.4. Additionally, numerical
simulation can be computationally expensive because its complexity increases
rapidly with the amount of calculations. [10]

Figure 3.1: A screenshot of the full geometry as defined and shown in COMSOL. From left

The first step in the creation of the model is the definition of the geometry of
the system. The geometry is the physical dimensions of each part of the system.
FEM geometry can be defined in three main methods: Three-dimensional (3D),
two-dimensional (2D) and axisymmetric. The decision of which type geometry
to be used relies mainly on how efficiently the system can be described. For
example, an axisymmetric geometry is defined by a 1D or 2D shape that is
revolved around an axis, which means that the computations are done on one
dimension less than is actually modelled and are simply replicated around the
axis of symmetry. Of course, this can only be done on axisymmetric geometries.
The system under investigation in this project is not axisymmetric because of
both the bends in the tubes and the rectangular shape of the receiver. Hence, the
geometry is defined in 3D space.
The final geometry, as can be seen in Fig. 3.1, was defined mainly through the
concatenation of boxes and cylinders. Because it is assumed that the structures of
the coupler and the earmold substitute do not have an effect on the behaviour of
the system, they are defined only by the air volume inside of them. In addition,
in an attempt to represent the system to the best degree, the acoustic tubes of the
system were defined with outer walls, as can be seen in Fig. 3.2. The highlighted
areas on the left and right ends of the geometry are coupling channels for the
receiver spout and the earmold suspension. While the highlighted length for

Boundary conditions are constraints imposed on partial or ordinary differen-
tial equations in order to solve them. There are four types of boundary condi-
tions: [10]
• Dirichlet - imposed directly on the governing equation,
• Neumann - imposed on a derivative of the equation,

A major consideration when creating a FEM model is the mesh. The mesh is
the discretisation of the geometry into smaller elements, on which the governing
equations are solved. The main two considerations when creating a mesh are the
order of the elements, and their density. Both higher order and higher density
meshes will increase the quality of the results, but in the cost of increased com-
plexity and therefore increased computation time. A short convergence study
was done in order to find the optimal mesh for this model. This was done by
computing the model with meshes of increased density until the results show to
converge to a certain "true" value.
The model was evaluated at f = {50 Hz, 100 Hz, 500 Hz, 1 kHz, 5 kHz,
10 kHz}. The mesh density in COMSOL is controlled through the minimum
element size, which is its inverse.
It was varied from 200 to 3600 elements
per meter which correspond to a minimum element sizes of 5 to 0.278 mm.

curves of the RE are plotted on a logarithmic scale to show the convergence of
the system. A couple of them are shown in Fig. 3.9. All convergence plots can
be seen in Appendix A.

Chapter 4
Discussion
In this chapter further comments about the models and their results will be
made.
The discrepancy between the models’ sound pressure level frequency re-
sponses and the preliminary measurement’s response is a sign that the models
are not accurate enough, most significantly in the resonance frequencies above
1 kHz. There are several possible areas in which the models might be lacking.
First, the device the models are based on is closed inside its casing and therefore
there is limited access to the sound path and its dimensions. Hence, they were
approximated from measurements made outside the transparent casing. This
means that some of the assumptions made about the geometry of the sound
path could have been wrong, or some smaller features were missed. Although,
such small changes should not have such a significant effect on the resonances
of the system. A part of the system that has large effect on its response is the
source. If the estimated source pressure had significantly different frequency
content, even a perfect model of the sound path would have provided incorrect
results. The receiver model used in this project is a curve fitted model of a re-
ceiver of the same series as the one in the device, but a possibility exists that
the model it was fitted to has a different frequency response than the one in the
device. More precise information regarding the receiver could provide insight
about the accuracy of the source. Moreover, instead of using a curve fitted model
of the receiver, the receiver could be modelled as well.
The other area in which the models were lacking is the low frequency reson-
ances observed in the response of the FEM model. While it was identified that
these were a result of the bends in the system, they do not appear in the meas-
urement even though it is bent as well. The vibrations are heavily influenced by
the physical properties of the system. Two big property of the device that were
not introduced in the FEM model are the earhook and the casing, both would
naturally dampen the vibrations in the system and consequently attenuate their
effect on the sound pressure within the sound path. While the casing was com-
pletely neglected from the model, the earhook was modelled as a tube, which
36
==PAGE:41==
37
misrepresents its physicality. By introducing the real geometry of the earhook,
a more accurate prediction of the vibrations in the system could be approxim-
ated. Also, by introducing the casing into the model, the feedback to the HI’s
microphone could possibly be estimated.
Lastly, it was suggested that each bend in the system caused one of the
low frequency resonances, this could be further studied by introducing another
bend in the system or removing one of the existing bends, and comparing the
responses.
Furthermore, designing a simplified model of a bent tube could
identify the effect of the bend and its angle on the resonances of the system,
which could potentially help finding an expression of these effects as a function
of the bending angle and allow more informed design of future models.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
This study was set to characterise and model the sound path of the acoustic
outlet of a HI. An analytical and a numerical model were designed in an attempt
to replicate its acoustic behaviour. It was found that the transmission matrix
method was most equipped to construct the analytical model as it was capable
of easily concatenating different sections of a system and including its visco-
thermal losses. The analytical model produced a frequency response similar to
the response of the HI, but was inaccurate.
As the analytical model only considered the purely acoustic behaviour of
the system, the numerical model was used to introduce the vibro-acoustic in-
teraction of the system into the model. The numerical model’s response was
very similar to the one of the analytical model with two distinctions, its reson-
ance peaks above 1 kHz were higher by about 3 dB and it had low frequency
resonances. While the origin of the difference in the resonance peak was not
identified, it was established that the low frequency resonances were caused by
the vibrations in the system. Through the investigation of the displacement and
force magnitudes at the point of interest in the model it was proposed that each
of the mechanical resonance frequencies is caused by one of the bends. The
performance of the models was examined and improvements were proposed.
