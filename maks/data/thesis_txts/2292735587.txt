Abstract
In comparison with conventional gravity based breakwaters, the ﬂoating breakwaters represent
an alternative solution to protect harbour areas from wave impact. The beneﬁts are applica-
bility regardless of bad soil conditions and/or great water depths, while the disadvantages are
higher cost in terms of construction as well as maintenance and being ineﬀective for long waves.
Hence, ﬂoating breakwaters are well suited for protecting marinas in areas exposed to a mild
wave environment.
The aim of thesis is to investigate various designs of ﬂoating breakwaters, where especially two
properties have great interest; hydrodynamic eﬃciency and dynamic response.
The hydrodynamic eﬃciency is a measure for the eﬀectiveness of a ﬂoating breakwater ex-
pressed by the transmission coeﬃcient, CT. This coeﬃcient reveals the amount of the incident
wave height that is transmitted ”through” the breakwater. Associated with the aim of a calm
wave environment on the lee side of the ﬂoating breakwater, CT is an important design param-
eter.
The dynamic response refers to the harmonic motions of a system excited by periodic external
forces expressed as the Response Amplitude Operator (RAO).
The concepts of various ﬂoating breakwater designs are analysed in order to reveal the eﬃciency
and dynamic response by experimental setups and numerical models carried out in WAMIT.
The analyses are based on realistic dimensions and wave conditions from the coastal area
near Kaplankaya, Turkey, which data have been provided by Niras A/S. The diﬀerent design
types are based on a regular pontoon, where modiﬁcations such as a wider bottom plate and
various thicknesses of synthetic porous material attached on the sides are performed.
The
latter design option serves the purpose of increase the dissipation of wave energy due to viscous
damping. Decay tests are submitted in order to evaluate the viscous damping eﬀects and natural
frequencies and thus, implementing the viscous damping for all designs in WAMIT.
A general good agreement between the two approaches including the experimental and numer-
ical model is concluded. The reﬂective models (without porous media) provides almost similar
achievements in regard to the response and eﬃciency. The results for the dissipative models
(with porous media) diﬀers a bit in the two approaches, as permeable structures are diﬃcult to
model in WAMIT. The ﬁnal results indicate that the wing plate option provides a huge stabi-
lization in terms of the the dynamic response, while the dissipative models tends to provide the
lowest transmission of the wave energy due to the great dissipation of the wave energy.

Preface
This thesis was conducted with the purpose of obtaining the academic degree of Master of
Science in Civil Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark. The master thesis was
submitted at the Institute of Mechanical Engineering and in cooperation with the Danish
consultancy company Niras A/S.
The thesis covers a thorough analysis of various designs of ﬂoating breakwaters including phys-
ical experiments and numerical modelling carried out in the computer software WAMIT. The
focus is to evaluate the performance of the models in terms of ability to attenuate the incident
wave energy and motions of the models in the respective degrees of freedom.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This chapter introduce the topic of this Master thesis ”Analysis of ﬂoating breakwaters”. The
background and motivation for this study including objectives and approaches of the research
will be presented. Furthermore, the basic principles of ﬂoating breakwaters will shortly be
discussed.
1.1
The background and motivation for the study
The need for new harbour constructions or expansions of existing harbours are an obvious
consequence of a constantly growing world trade and increasing globalization. Breakwaters
serve the purpose of protecting harbours and marina facilities in general from undesirable wave
heights by attenuating as much as possible of the incoming wave energy. However, not all
marine areas are suitable for constructing conventional breakwaters. In areas with deep water
or bad soil conditions the traditional gravity-based systems such as caisson, composite or rubble
mound breakwaters become expensive and diﬃcult solutions. This is one of the main reasons
why interest has turned to alternative solutions like ﬂoating breakwaters.
This thesis considers the heavy-duty type of ﬂoating breakwaters, in the form of a large ﬂoating
concrete structure, as an idea to design a marina independent of the circumstances mentioned
above. However, ﬂoating breakwaters are not without challenges since the ﬂoating structure is
non-stationary and does not cover the entire height of the water column, from seabed to water
surface, and thus, allowing wave energy to be transmitted underneath the breakwater. Strictly
speaking, the aims of the design phase of a ﬂoating breakwater are to satisfy two criteria; limited
movements of the structure and suﬃciently reduction of the wave action inside the marina area
to ensure a stable wave environment, preventing unwanted vessel movements.
The Danish consultancy company, Niras A/S, is working with a marina project located in
Kaplankaya, Turkey, where the use of a ﬂoating breakwater is considered as a suitable solution.
In line with this, the wave environment at the current location has been analysed by Niras A/S
and it provides the basis for the investigations of ﬂoating breakwaters in this study. The design

1.3
Approach to the research
The approach in order to reach the objectives of this study is divided into 3 steps including a
literature review, physical experiments and numerical modelling. The experimental and numer-
ical models are used to analyse the behaviour of the FB, why a continuous consistency between
dimensions and proportions is essential in order to obtain comparable results.
Literature review
An initial literature review is carried out to acquire general and technical information about
FBs and to obtain knowledge about existing experiences with such type of structures.
Experimental setup
The experimental tests take place in a 25 m long wave ﬂume in Building 114 at DTU Campus.
The design of the FB test models are based on a downscaled model of the preliminary design
performed by Niras A/S. By adjusting parameters such as the draft, general geometry and/or
ﬁtting the models incident and lee side with porous material, a wide range of diﬀerent designs
can be tested. The tests of the downscaled FB models serve the purpose of measuring the
motions, performance in the sense of wave transmission as well as reﬂection, natural frequency
and damping eﬀects. All these registrations are used to validate the results from the numerical
model and furthermore, to improve this model in order to reﬂect the conditions taking place
in reality.

1.4. GENERAL UNDERSTANDING OF FBS
Following measurements are carried out for the experimental model:
 The motions are measured by the use of video recordings and accelerometers detecting
the three relevant degrees of freedom including sway, heave and roll.
 In order to evaluate the eﬃciency of the FB model, the surface elevation is measured with
wave gauges placed at the incident and lee side of the model. By these measurements, the
surface elevation and the reﬂected as well as transmitted wave heights are determined.
 The natural frequencies and damping ratios for sway, heave and roll are determined by
decay tests, where the diminishing motions are detected by video recordings.
The experimental model will be exposed to waves with wave heights based on three diﬀerent
wave steepnesses for a range of various wave periods.
Numerical modelling
The numerical model makes it possible to compute the behaviour of diﬀerent FB designs without
building them physically.
The calculations are executed in the software program WAMIT,
which is a radiation/diﬀraction panel program developed for linear analysis of the interaction of
surface waves with various types of ﬂoating and submerged structures. The numerical model is
optimized based on the measurements and the actual behaviour experienced associated with the
experimental tests. Having a reliable numerical model will provide an opportunity to simulate
the full size structure and a range of slightly modiﬁed design options and wave climates.
1.4
General understanding of FBs
The general objective for a FB is to protect marinas and shore areas from incident waves in
order to create an appropriate wave environment at the lee side of the structure.
In that
respect, a FB is not very diﬀerent from a conventional gravity-based breakwater such as rubble
mound breakwaters or concrete caissons. However, there are some fundamental advantages and
disadvantages of FBs which will be elaborated later in this section.
In a historical perspective, this alternative solution for shore and harbour protection is not a
new concept but a rather old system used in Great Britain back in the 1800s, [13]. In the
last decades, the logistical need for larger ships and thus, bigger port areas has increased,
which can create diﬃculties for establishing suitable sheltered locations. This development
leads to a greater need for breakwaters, which has increased the interest in FBs rapidly in the
sense of achieving a coastal protection system which is cheap and eﬀective even at challenging
locations.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
1.4.1
Types of structures
There are many diﬀerent types of FBs with various designs in order to attenuate the wave
energy. The way these diﬀerent FB designs operate are divided into two groups of structures,
[1].
1. Reﬂective structures: The incident waves are reﬂected so only a small amount of the
energy may pass beyond them.
2. Dissipative structures: The incident wave energy is destroyed by friction, ﬂow separa-
tion and turbulence.
However, these classiﬁcations are arbitrary since a FB typically will be a combination between
these two classiﬁcations as there is many complex phenomena involved in the interaction be-
tween the waves and FBs, [1]. This means, that a FB partially reﬂects, transmits and dissipates
the energy from the incident wave, however, emphasis is often placed on either the reﬂective or
dissipative system. In this thesis both reﬂective and dissipative systems are investigated.
Reﬂective system
A reﬂective system is typical based on the idea of solid vertical wave barriers, where the most
common type of FB is the single pontoon or barge ﬁxed to the seabed with mooring lines.
Figure 1.1: Cross-section of a single pontoon ﬁxed with mooring lines, [1].
In Figure 1.1, it is seen that the eﬀectiveness of a FB system depends on the mechanical
parameters such as the mass, center of gravity, and mass moment of inertia, denoted as M,
hG and I respectively. Furthermore, the geometrical shape of the FB, restoring eﬀects and
properties of the incident waves are all of great importance to the FB.

2.1
Linear wave dynamics
Waves in water come in lots of diﬀerent shapes and sizes depending on their surroundings. They
do however have one thing in common which is the ability to transfer energy from one point
to another with relatively little displacement of the water particles and thus, mass transport.
In order to describe the waves analytically, the complex behaviour of waves has been idealized
into more simple motions by what is known as the linear wave theory. This section introduces
the wave concepts and deﬁnitions including a brief explanation of the linear wave theory and
the panel method in WAMIT. Then follows, a presentation of the theory applied to determine
wave parameters in practice and the rules of scaling.

2.1.2
Irregular waves
Wave observations in the open ocean show that the surface elevation is a random process. It
is far from regular because the real sea state is a combination of waves with various directions,
heights, periods, etc. This irregular wave signal can be modelled by superposition of harmonic
regular waves, cf. Figure 2.3 where the principle of superposition is illustrated. From this
it follows that irregular waves can be described by the basics of elements from linear wave
theory put together with elementary probability theory.
The irregular wave signal can be
decomposed by Fourier Series into a number of harmonic regular waves. The result is a series of
amplitudes and phases where each amplitude and phase value belongs to a frequency described
in a frequency spectrum.
By collecting all waves in a spectrum, the ”probability density”
indicates where most of the wave energy is concentrated and reveals the dominating wave in
the whole wave ﬁeld, cf. Figure 2.4 for illustration of a frequency spectrum based on various
regular wave series.

2.1.4
Linear wave theory
The linear wave theory is based on two fundamental equations including the mass balance
equation and momentum balance equation, which describes the kinematic and dynamic aspects
of waves. The linear part of the name refers to the possibility of scaling all parameters linearly,
but within some well deﬁned boundaries. Linear wave theory is only applicable for deep water
waves with small amplitudes. If this criterion is not satisﬁed, non-linear eﬀects will inﬂuence
the behaviour and shape of the wave. This is why the linear wave theory is the core theory of
ocean surface waves. The theory assumes the waves to have permanent form and propagate
over a horizontal seabed. Furthermore, is it assumed that the water is an ideal ﬂuid which
implies that the ﬂuid is incompressible, inviscid (no viscosity) and irrotational (no rotation
of the water particles). The interaction between these assumptions and the two fundamental
equations is brieﬂy outlined below.

2.1.5
WAMIT - Panel Method
The numerical model of the FB is carried out in the computer software WAMIT, where the
objective is to examine the unsteady hydrodynamic pressure, loads, motions and velocity po-
tentials of a ﬂoating body interacting with plane progressive waves in water, [18]. WAMIT is
based on the linearised ﬁrst-order free-surface body boundary conditions and the ﬂow is as-
sumed potential where separation and lifting eﬀects are ignored, cf. Section 2.1.4. The panel
method, also refereed to as the boundary integral equation method (BIEM), is based on Green’s
theorem where the velocity potential at any point in the ﬂuid is represented by surface distri-
butions of singularities over the ﬂuid boundary surfaces, [19]. The method is used to solve the
diﬀraction and radiation problem, when the waves interact with the ﬂoating body.
In this thesis, the higher-order method is used, where Green’s velocity potential integrals over
the body surface and computational domains are carried out numerical by using Gauss quadra-
tures in parametric space, [19].

2.2.5
Damping and natural frequency
The energy dissipation of an oscillatory system occurs through the damping force, B ˙x(t) and
thus, inﬂuence and reduce the response of the structure. Traditionally, the damping force is
divided into three types; structural, material and ﬂuid damping. All three types of damping
are present during the FB motions, however, the most prominent is the ﬂuid damping, which is
subdivided into linear wave damping and viscous damping. Structural damping counts external
friction in e.g. structure joints while material damping covers the internal friction of a material
at molecular level, [23].
The viscous damping appears due to viscous ﬂow phenomena from appendices such as sharp
edges which creates turbulent ﬂow including separation. Due to ﬂow separation at sharp edges
of the FB, the pressure forces caused by the viscous eﬀect become more important than the
wall skin friction (shear forces). The point where the skin friction becomes zero is referred as
the detachment point, [7]. The ﬂow separation creates vortex formations due to the structures
oscillations relative to the water. These vortices or eddies cause the viscous damping eﬀect.
The viscous damping eﬀect is usually determined experimentally based on a decay test, cf.
Section 4.2.4.
The viscous damping is not linear in reality, however, an approximately linear damping coef-
ﬁcient will be used in WAMIT. Hence, assuming the damped motions to be described by the
linear equation of motion, cf. Equation (2.52), the damped oscillations can be characterized by
the decreasing amplitudes as function of time, cf. Figure 2.11.

2.3
Mooring system
The design of the mooring system used for this study is based on the method provided by O.
M. Faltinsen, [7]. A mooring system is designed to prevent the FB to drift away, and thus,
maintain a precise position. The mooring system consists of a number of lines attached to the
FB where the lower ends of the lines are anchored to the seabed. These lines can be made
of various types of material depending on the requirements of the structure design. However,
the most frequently used material for the lines are chains, which also is the material used for
this study. The anchors of the mooring system are gravity based, thus the mooring system is
stabilized due to its own weight, which demands a signiﬁcant part of the mooring lines to lie
on the seabed. Due to the weight and elasticity of the mooring lines, restraining tension forces
are applied to the FB. This eﬀective stiﬀness that the mooring chains add to the FB should be
able to withstand the hydrodynamic drift forces acting on the FB.

3.3.2
Oscillation of the mooring lines
The natural frequency for the three DOFs in the mooring system are important parameters
due to the risk of resonant oscillations between the FB and the mooring system. In order to
minimize the risk of resonance, the mooring system is aimed to obtain a natural frequency for
sway, heave and roll, which are located outside the FBs resonance frequency. However, the
extension of the mooring lines and thereby, the stiﬀness of the mooring system is dynamic and
varies with wave frequency and wave steepness. As the mooring system is a gravity based type
with catenary chains with a very small mass compared to the FB, the natural frequency of the
mooring system is estimated to be quite high compared with the wave frequencies. A thorough
analysis of the oscillation of the mooring lines are not included in this study.

Chapter 4
Methodology
This chapter presents the two approaches which form the basis for the research carried out
in this study. A numerical model has been set up with the purpose of analysing the general
dynamics of the FB models.
In addition, the FB models have been tested experimentally
serving the purpose of not only validate the numerical model but also serve as a supplement to
the numerical model since much of the inputs are based on the empirical work.
4.1
Numerical calculations - WAMIT
The numerical modelling makes it possible to compute the behaviour of diﬀerent FB designs
without building them physically. The numerical models were based on the circumstances and
objectives earlier presented. Furthermore, the model are optimized based on the measurements
and behaviour experienced from the experimental tests of the FB models.
The numerical calculations were carried out in the software program WAMIT version 7.0.
WAMIT is a radiation/diﬀraction panel program developed for the linear analysis of the inter-
action of the surface waves with various types of ﬂoating and/or submerged structures, [18].
The program is based on a three-dimensional panel method and a variety of options making
it possible to perform a dynamic analysis of all kinds of structures in ﬁnite or inﬁnite water
depths, located on the water surface or submerged and mooring options such as freely ﬂoating,
restrained or ﬁxed etc.
WAMIT computes the free surface elevation at speciﬁed ﬁeld points and the motions of a FB
including the Response Amplitude Operator (RAO). Furthermore, valuable information about
drift forces acting on the FB due to the wave action and added mass, linear wave damping and
hydrostatic restoring coeﬃcient are determined.
A run of WAMIT is divided into two subprograms called POTEN and FORCE, which runs
sequentially. POTEN evaluates the ﬂow velocity potentials of the diﬀraction and radiation on
the body surface. The Potential Control (POT) ﬁle is used as the input ﬁle for this subprogram.

and pressures acting on the body surface at speciﬁed positions. The Force Control (FRC) ﬁle

Before a test could be initiated, the 7 wave gauges needed to be calibrated.
To ease this

The calibration was performed for all 7 wave gauges in completely calm water and the process
consisted of following three steps.
1. 30 s data acquisition in the lowest position, 0 mm.
2. 30 s data acquisition in the highest position, 50 mm.
3. Linear interpolation between the two voltage signals obtained by the two data acquisitions
and converted it to the unit voltage per meter.
Appendix D.3 contains a validation of the linear relationship.

Decay test
In order to determine the natural frequency and the damping ratio for sway, heave and roll
motions, decay tests were performed. The concept of a decay test, cf. Section 2.2.5, is based
on the idea of measuring the free natural response for the initial conditions
x(0) = x0
and
˙x(0) = v0
where x0 is the initial position/displacement and v0 the corresponding velocity which is followed
by the decreasing motions as function of time, t. The response was measured by use of camera
recordings and Particle Tracking, cf. Section 4.2.2. The motions were recorded until the FB
model was at rest again.
Before the decay tests could take place, the experimental model and the water in the ﬂume
were brought to absolute rest. The decay test was performed for all respective 3 DOFs.
 Sway: The experimental model was gently pushed along the positive y-axis, careful
not to induce any roll motions at the same time. The slow motions of the model were
63
==PAGE:79==
4.2. EXPERIMENTAL TESTS
only associated with a small amount of wave generation, which however, made some
disturbance which will be discussed later.
 Heave: The experimental model was gently pushed vertically downwards, still avoiding
any introduction of roll motions. The initial push generated waves travelling away from
the model but as this wave returned due to reﬂection by at the ends of the ﬂume, it
caused the model to start rolling. This last mentioned scenario was not a major problem
since the relatively fast heave motions were almost done oscillating. The very last part of
the measurements, inﬂuenced by the reﬂected waves, was of course not considered part
of the free response and thus, neglected in the further process of the analysis.
 Roll: The experimental model was gently pushed on one edge of the model. As in the
case of heave, the waves generated in connection with the roll motion, returned to the
model just as the motion was about to stop and thus, accelerated the motions once again.
In the process of calculating the logarithmic decrement, only the free response was considered
which of course meant discarding the ﬁrst part of the measurement including the initial position
and forced displacement. The measured response was not expected to ﬁt perfectly with the
linear equations of motion since the free response was aﬀected by forces from the mooring
system and viscous damping, which are non-linear in reality.

5.1
Damping and natural frequency
This section presents the results from the decay tests, i.e. the natural frequency and damping
ratio for each FB model in the respective DOFs. The main results are collected in tables and
supported by ﬁgures of the measured response for some selected models. The tendencies are
discussed in details and the external viscous damping coeﬃcients, Bvis, are determined. The
viscous damping is determined in order to improve the WAMIT model as this does not take
viscous damping eﬀects into account. Each DOF is treated separately and presented in the
order of roll, heave and sway.
The concept of logarithmic decrement was utilised to determine the damping ratio ζ, cf. Section
2.2.5 where the theory is described. The damping ratio describes how fast an initially induced
motion decreases towards zero and thus, its initial position.
The experimentally measured
damping ratio includes the damping due to wave generations, ζwave, and the viscous damping
due to ﬂow separation, ζvis. Thus, the damping ratio is written as ζtotal.
ζtotal = ζwave + ζvis
(5.1)
where ζwave = ζWAM because ζWAM is the damping ratio estimated from the linear wave
damping calculated by WAMIT.
Viscous damping covers ﬂow separation and thus, vortex formation which are associated with
large ﬂow velocities around large bodies and sharp edges. The presence of the viscous damping
is linked directly to the response where the logarithmic decrement was calculated for the ﬁrst
and fastest motions recorded in the decay test. Therefore, the decrement analysis includes only

Natural frequency
By comparing the natural frequencies for FB model Reg Ff and Reg it is seen that the
frequency was not aﬀected by the 2 mm mooring system.
This was expected due to the
small pre-tension, TH, of the mooring system. Recalling, that adding stiﬀness to a system will
increase the natural frequency but when the pre-tension, and thus, the stiﬀness, Cij, is minor in
comparison to the inertia mass, Mij, of the free oscillating system, the inﬂuence of the mooring
system is relatively small.
For FB model Reg 6mm where the pre-tension was attempted to be the same as for the 2 mm
system, the natural frequency was slightly larger due to the increased stiﬀness which is aﬀected
by the large self-weight, w, of these chains.
The mounted wing plate on FB model Wp slowed the roll motion considerably and thus,
increased the period Td by approx. 33 %, which decreased the natural frequency signiﬁcantly.
The small natural frequency is explained by the contribution of added mass, Aij. As the draft,
D, was kept constant for all models, the mounted wing plate only caused a small decrease of the
inertia mass, Mij, due to the submerged wing plate volume. However, the wing plate increased
the surface area which aﬀects the added mass, Aij. This larger inertia as well as added mass

and a nearly non-aﬀected constant stiﬀness, Cij, caused the decrease of the natural frequency,

The porous media attached on the sides of the FB models reduced the eﬀect of the wing plate,

Sway damping and natural frequency
The free response in sway was in contrast to roll and heave, not aﬀected by hydrostatic restoring
forces, constantly trying to bring back the system into equilibrium. The forces which kept the

was however, a slack system with a small horizontal pre-tension, TH, and thus, stiﬀness, Cij.
As a consequence, the period of oscillation became extremely long compared to roll and heave
periods, cf. Figure 5.7. Furthermore it was discovered that the periodic response was not as
harmonic as experienced for roll and heave. Two factors were thought to be the reason.

sway is large and the mooring lines tighten up and the horizontal tension grows exponen-
tially.

the test a small amplitude wave was generated along with the initiation of the model.
Due to the long period, this wave became relatively persistent and travelled at high speed
so that a certain amount of the energy got reﬂected at the end of the ﬂume.

increase signiﬁcantly. Even-though the pre-tension was equal in relation to the 2 mm mooring
system used for the other FB models, the stiﬀness contribution, Cij, increases a lot faster along
with the horizontal displacement of the FB. The natural frequency for FB model Reg 6mm
is increased by factor of 3 which requires a stiﬀness 9 times as big, assuming the mass of the
system is constant, cf. Equation (2.59). This emphasize, that the sway response is mainly

Equation (2.54).

It was experienced in WAMIT that there was a deviation between the experimental and WAMIT

some challenges in order to make a WAMIT model which was representative for the reality.

in the porous media which is attempted illustrated in Figure 5.29. These water columns on
each side of the FB are dynamic entities, where the amount of water changes continuously
due to the motions and incident waves. This captured water aﬀected the inertia mass, Mij,

With regard to the eﬀective area of the wing plate it should be understood as the area of the
top surface of the wing plate, which was covered equivalent to the attached porous media.
Due to this decreased free area of the wing plate, the added mass, Aij, associated with the
top surface of the plate decreased which increased the heave and roll response. The natural
frequency of the FB model is aﬀected by this added mass, Aij meaning that the deviation of the
frequencies between the experimental and WAMIT model was in direct relation to the reduced
added mass.
In order to obtain some more comparable porous FB models in WAMIT, these two presented
phenomenons for the experimental model were attempted to be implemented in WAMIT. How-
ever, these implementations were based entirely on assumptions and experience gained by dis-
coveries in the wave ﬂume and from the numerical model. The eﬀective volume of the porous
media was around 11% of the total volume, and thus, it was estimated that the volume of the
attached porous media was equal to the amount of captured water. This was included in the
inertia mass, Mij, and center of gravity considerations. The covering eﬀect of the porous media
on the wing plate was implemented by expanding the width, W, of the FB model equivalent
to the thickness of the porous media. The wing plate was correspondingly reduced in the same
magnitude as the width was increased.

Chapter 6
Discussion and design proposal
This chapter serves as a summary of some of the important discoveries made from the results
treated in Chapter 5. The general trends for the experimental and numerical models will be
considered as well as the reﬂective and dissipative structure types.
6.1
Experimental tests and WAMIT modelling
There were some diﬃculties in order to establish a numerical model in WAMIT representative to
the experimental tests. WAMIT puts a natural limit as the program is based on linear analysis,
which do not always represent the reality. These non-linear relations includes the hydrodynamic
stiﬀness provided by the mooring system and the potential viscous damping. The latter is
considered as the reason for the consistently overestimated roll response prevailing around the
natural frequencies of the diﬀerent FB models. The additional viscous damping contribution
determined experimentally and implemented in WAMIT were only suitable for the adjacent
wave frequencies where the movements are limited. This indicates that the viscous damping
for roll has a non-linear trend which prevails for large responses at resonance frequency.
The connection between the experimental tests and the WAMIT models were generally good
for the reﬂective FB models without porous media attached including FB models Reg and
Wp. Here, the dynamic response as well as the performance were almost identical of course
with the exception of the roll response. This indicates that the determined inertia mass, Mij,
additional viscous damping, Bij, and additional stiﬀness, Cij, matrices are based on reason-
able assumptions and correct calculations. In contrast to these two reﬂective FB models, the
dissipative models including Reg P50, Wp P30, Wp P50 and Wp P100 were diﬃcult
to model properly in WAMIT. The permeable membrane is hard to model in WAMIT, which
diﬀers the WAMIT model from the results obtained in the wave ﬂume. Besides the additional
damping determined by decay tests, a geometric modiﬁcation was made in an attempt to sim-
ulate the physical properties of the porous media. These modiﬁcations based on impermeable
walls resulted in some relatively good response results especially in heave which were very much
aﬀected by the porous media’s inﬂuence on the eﬀective area of the wing plate. However, the

performance based on the reﬂected, CR, and transmitted, CT coeﬃcients were not possible to
get into accordance with the experimental results. The tendencies were almost in correlation

6.2
Reﬂective FB models
Two types of reﬂective structures were investigated including a regular barge, Reg, and a
barge with a wing plate attached on the bottom, Wp. The regular barge had some large
dynamic responses compared to the barge with attached wing plate, which was more suitable
for low frequency waves as the wing plate reduced the natural frequency of the FB model. The
only damping for these reﬂective systems were the vortex formation around sharp edges and
the linear wave damping due to the radiation potential. As the amount of vortices increased
by the magnitude of the response, the regular barge was actually more eﬃcient around its
natural frequency due to the large responses which increased the viscous damping as well as
the radiation potential is approx. zero. Though, the wing plate in general included a greater
viscous damping coeﬃcient, the small roll response and thus, minimized viscous damping, made
the FB less eﬃcient for high frequency waves compared with FB model Reg. However, a FB in
operation in a marine areas, often have multiple functions that require a calm structure, why
the dynamic responses is an important parameters as well as the transmission coeﬃcients. This
taken into account, it is concluded that the wing plate improves the FB model signiﬁcantly.
The wing plate model Wp is the model with smallest motions overall.
6.3
Dissipative FB models
In order to verify the eﬀect of a dissipative structure, four FB models were tested with porous
media attached.
This includes a regular barge with 50 mm porous material and a wing-
plate-modiﬁed barge with 3 diﬀerent thicknesses of porous material. Regarding the FB model
Reg P50, the porous media was a great improvement in order to reduce the responses and
furthermore, the eﬃciency primarily in terms of the transmission coeﬃcient, CT, was improved
signiﬁcantly. The reﬂection coeﬃcient, CR, was decreased due to the increased viscous damping
in the porous media. An absolute beneﬁcial modiﬁcation of the FB design.
However, the function of the porous media was more ambivalent for the FB models with the
wing plate mounted, Wp P30, Wp P50 and Wp P100. It was conﬁrmed that the wing
plate had a major impact regarding the motions of the FB. In contrast, the mounted porous
media actually increased the motions of the structures as a function of its thickness. As the
area of the wing plate was covered up by the porous media, the responses tended to increase in
heave and roll due to numerous factors discussed in Section 5.3.3. The increased response led to
increased the viscous damping and thus, the transmission, CT, and reﬂection, CR, coeﬃcients

Chapter 7
Conclusion and recommendations
Conclusions have been drawn through the whole thesis where this chapter presents the main
conclusion in conjunction with the objectives of the research. Finally a number of recommen-
dations for further research are listed based on the gained experiences from the work of this
thesis.
7.1
Conclusion
FBs serves the purpose of either reﬂect and/or dissipate the incident wave energy, ensuring a
calm wave environment on the lee side. This thesis has dealt with various FB models, all tested
experimentally and numerically, in the frequency domain in order to evaluate their dynamic
response and hydrodynamic performance.
In the FB design phase, the wave period represents the most essential parameter because any
ﬂoating structure becomes insigniﬁcant if the wave period is superior in magnitude. Therefore
it is important to analyse the wave environment in order to estimate the most frequent waves
including wave periods, heights etc. representative for the given location. The dimensions of
the FB should be estimated aiming for a design which natural frequency do not interfere with
the most frequent wave periods. In this procedure inertia mass, added mass and stiﬀness are
governing parameters. The stiﬀness covers the hydrostatic restoring stiﬀness combined with
the external stiﬀness provided by a mooring system while the added mass accounts for the
water which must be accelerated in order to move the entire system. Changes in the geometry
do not necessarily inﬂuence the inertia mass or the stiﬀness in any signiﬁcant way but the
added mass might change considerably. Modifying the geometry by means of a mounted wing
plate therefore caused the FB to behave diﬀerently.
Wave damping and viscous damping
are parameters mainly aﬀected by the magnitude of response as the hydrodynamic damping
is proportional to the velocity.
The linear wave damping is the energy dissipation due to
wave generation due to the radiation potential while viscous damping represents the energy
dissipation due to the process of ﬂow separation and vortex formation. The latter eﬀect had a
huge inﬂuence and depends on the geometrical shape of the submerged part of the structure.
113
==PAGE:129==
7.1. CONCLUSION
It has been proven trough this study that the viscous damping is diﬃcult to determine exactly
and varies non-linearly in roll.
It was possible for the reﬂective FB models Reg and Wp to obtain good agreements between
experimental model and the predictions from the WAMIT model. The regular barge, Reg,
reached larger motions compared with the wing plate model, Wp, regardless of the considered
DOF. This great motion especially around the resonant frequency of the structure seemed to be
an advantage in terms of the transmission coeﬃcients because a lot of the wave energy was lost
as a result of the intensiﬁed viscous damping due to the large motions. However, the regular
barge, Reg, is a very unsustainable solution, since a wave environment in reality never comes
with a constant frequency. In an application-oriented view of a new marina, large motions are
also mostly unwanted in respect to e.g. the comfort level of the people staying at the ﬂoating
structure, the risk of very large forces in mooring system and damaging the vessels moored to
the structure. Looking speciﬁed at the heave and roll responses, the wing plate structure, Wp,
provides massive stabilization while also reducing the natural frequency. The reduced natural
frequency of FB model increased its performance for longer waves compared with FB model
Reg. Furthermore, it had a stable and continuously performance in terms of the transmission
coeﬃcients in regard to various frequencies where the FB model Reg performance tended to
ﬂuctuate depending on the frequencies.
Improving the dissipative properties of the FB models were easy done for the experimental
models by the attachment of porous material. However, it turned out to be quite a challenge
to model it numerically in WAMIT because it is diﬃcult too model permeable structures in the
program. Thus, the model was based on the assumption of some of the water is captured in the
porous media and thereby contributes to the inertia mass while likewise extending geometry in
relation to simulate the gained or reduced added mass of the FB. However, the reﬂection from
porous surfaces were not modiﬁed in WAMIT, why the reﬂection and transmission coeﬃcient
were hard to predict numerically for these dissipative FB models.
Looking at the FB model Reg and Reg P50, the porous attachment optimized almost all
features of the regular model. The heave and roll responses were reduced for all frequencies
while sway motions seemed to increase in case of very small frequencies otherwise also sway
responses were decreased as well. The transmission coeﬃcients decreased signiﬁcantly parallel
to a massive decrease of the reﬂection coeﬃcient testifying a considerable amounts of energy
were dissipated due to the porous media.
Combining the porous media with the wing plate model should obviously be the ideal solution
but the synergy eﬀects sort of fails. Considering the response in heave and roll for FB model
Wp, Wp P30, Wp P50 and Wp P100, the respective responses increased by the amount
of porous material attached to the model. This tendency was very clear and it was most likely
explained by the reduction of the eﬀective area of the wing plate which reduced the added
mass. Furthermore, contributed the captured water in the porous media not only to a greater
inertia mass but also a completely load distribution which led a center of gravity was located
higher and thus, increased responses especially in roll.
The aim of this thesis was to develop a suitable design proposal for the marina area in Ka-
plankaya. The ﬁnal design proposal was based on a analysis of the FB models exposed to

a wave environment present in Kaplankaya with a wave period T = 8 s and a wave height
H = 2.44 m. The wave period was raised to T = 8 s instead of T = 7 s due to lack of good
experimental results for small wave periods. The FB model with a wing plate mounted on the
bottom of the structure and a 100 mm dissipative arrangement on the sides i.e. FB model
Wp P100 was the ﬁnal design proposal. In spite of the increased responses for this particular

D.4
Validation of motion detection
Two methods have been used to measure the motions experimentally including Particle Tracking
and two accelerometers. In order to validate each of these methods, the respective results are

Associated with the use of Particle Tracking to track the particles using individual video frames
and thus determine the motions of the FB, it is important to be acquainted with the phe-

Distortion is an optical aberration, which deviates from rectilinear projection, [25]. It is often
associated with the zoom lenses and distortion can follow many patterns, however, the most
common distortions are tangential and radial symmetric. Often, the contribution from the
tangential component is very small and may well be neglected, [11]. The radial component
are classiﬁed as Barrel, positive radial distortion, Pincushion, negative radial distortion, and
Mustache, which is the mixture of the two aforementioned.

1
Intro
The need for new harbour constructions or expansions of existing harbours are an obvious
consequence of a constantly growing world trade and increasing globalization. Breakwa-
ters serve the purpose of protecting the harbours from undesirable wave heights by atten-
uating as much as possible of the wave energy. However, not all marine areas are suitable
for constructing conventional breakwaters. In areas with steep seabed, deep water or bad
soil conditions the traditional gravity-based breakwater systems such as; caisson, compos-
ite or rubble mound types become expensive solutions. This is one of the main reasons
why interest has turned to alternative solutions like ﬂoating breakwaters.
This thesis considers the heavy-duty type of ﬂoating breakwaters in the form of a large
ﬂoating concrete structure, considered as an obvious idea to design a marina independent
of the circumstances listed above. Floating breakwaters are, however, not without chal-
lenges since the ﬂoating structure is non-stationary and does not cover the entire height
of the incoming water column; allowing wave energy to be transmitted under the break-
water. Strictly speaking, the design phase of a ﬂoating breakwater aims for satisfying
two criteria; limited movements and suﬃciently reduction of the wave action inside the
marina area. The focus of this thesis will be on optimizing the design and the anchoring
of a ﬂoating breakwater targeting a speciﬁc wave climate in the Mediterranean sea.
Floating Breakwater will hereinafter be referred to as FB.
2
Background
The FB design will be based on a wave climate, provided by Niras A/S, recorded outside
the coast of Turkey where a new marina in Kaplankaya is intended to be build in the
coming years. Here the signiﬁcant wave height is Hs = 2.44 m with a peak period of
Tp = 7 s for a return period of 50 years. The water depths in the area varies between
40 −60 m, which is classiﬁed as deep water since the ratio between water depth h and
wave length L, fulﬁll the criterion
h
L ≥0.5, for maximum wave length Lmax = 76 m.
This means the waves can be considered linear which allows linear wave theory to be
utilized.
To obtain a stable environment for the ships, maximum 20 % of the incident waves heights
are allowed to be transmitted inside the marina area, which corresponds to a transmission
coeﬃcient of CT ≤0.2.

This includes both slack and taut chain systems and the design of the chains and axial

Methodology
The approach to reach the above objectives is divided into 3 steps; literature review,
numerical calculations and physical model tests, each elaborated below. Numerical and
physical modelling is used to analyse the behaviour of the FB, why a continuous con-
sistency between dimensions and proportions is essential in order to obtain comparable
results.
Literature review
An initial literature review is necessary to acquire technical knowledge about FBs and to
search existing experiences with such type of structures. The study also serve the purpose
of making sure that already existing thesis or research projects, have not already covered
equivalent topic and objectives.
Numerical calculations
Numerical modelling makes it possible to compute the behaviour of diﬀerent breakwater
designs without building them physically. The calculations are executed in the software
program WAMIT, which is a radiation/diﬀraction panel program developed for linear
analysis of the interaction of surface waves with various types of ﬂoating and submerged
structures. The numerical model is optimized based on the measurements and behaviour
found in the physical model test. Having a numerical and physical model behaving identi-
cally, will lead to reliable numerical calculations of a full size structure and an wide range
of slightly modiﬁed design options and wave climates.
Physical model tests
The physical model tests take place in a 25 m long wave ﬂume in Building 114 at DTU
Campus. By building a downscaled FB with adjustable partition walls it is possible to
test diﬀerent ballast distributions and thereby changing the moment of inertia.
Fur-
thermore the model has the opportunity to be ﬁtted with wings, changing the geometry
signiﬁcantly. The tests serve the purpose of measuring the motions, the eﬃciency and

The motions are measured by use of an accelerometer detecting the three relevant de-

Results
The results from the numerical part will be compared with the physical model tests and
vice versa, which should lead to a consistence between the results. The damping eﬀect of
the porous media should be implemented in the numerical model, making it possible to
calculate and predict the behaviour of such a full size FB type.
The eﬃciency of wings and the distribution of the ballast will be looked into. How the
center of gravity eﬀects the motion of the FB and the attenuation of the wave energy.
Important parameters to evaluate will be the transmission coeﬃcient, CT, reﬂection coef-
ﬁcient, CR, damping coeﬃcient, ξ, total energy loss, Eloss, response amplitude operator,
RAO, anchor-line force, and associated natural frequency.
Diagrams, graphs, tables and 3D ﬁgures will be used to illustrate the results and to
identify tendencies. Finally the most optimal design will be concluded.
