Abstract
As deep learning start to reach high stakes applications such as medical image diagno­
sis, and their failures modes start to be uncovered, increasing attention has been directed
towards explainability. Counterfactual explanations tackle the question: ”what would the
input look like had the prediction been different?”. We consider the task of medical im­
age diagnosis in the case where the disease is correlated with a demographic attribute.
A black box model might exploit this correlation and make a prediction based on this at­
tribute. Counterfactual explanations are typically used to uncover such biases. In this
thesis, we instead propose a VAE­based classifier that is robust to such bias and is by
design counterfactually explainable. We show that this classifier can extract a feature that
encodes the disease label in the data while being uninformative of the demographic at­
tribute. This objective is typically implemented using marginal independence constraints.
However, we show that under certain assumptions on the causal relationships between
the demographic attribute and the label, conditional independence constraints can be
derived from this objective. These constraints can be relaxed in two ways, using the
conditional mutual information and the Maximum Mean Discrepancy. We evaluate our
two models on variations of the Colored MNIST dataset and compare them with versions
of the models implementing marginal independence constraints, and a baseline model
without independence constraints. We show that marginal independence constraints are
inadequate when the label is correlated with the attribute, and that our approach using
conditional independence constraints should be preferred over the alternative versions,
when the correlated attribute is as predictive of the label as the label­specific features in
the image.

Introduction
The development of machine learning has revolutionized the field of computer vision,
which moved from traditionally hand­crafted features to learning­based methods in a
range of applications such as image segmentation, registration and classification, due
to the increased accuracy. In the last years, the deep learning revolution, enabled by the
advances in hardware and the larger availability of data, provided further improvements
by enabling more complex models through deep architectures, as demonstrated by the
performance of deep learning models on the challenging ImageNet classification task [1].
However, the vast majority of benchmarks like ImageNet measure model performance
by assessing validation performance on a test set that is independent and identically
distributed (i.i.d.) with respect to the training set, which is rarely justified in real­world
settings. Indeed, deep learning models often learn shortcuts in unpredictable ways and
consequently suffer from unexpected generalisation failures [2]. Shortcut solutions per­
form well on i.i.d test data but fail on datasets that are systematically different from the
i.i.d. training and test data, also called out­of­distribution (o.o.d) data. For example, a
deep classifier was able to detect pneumonia from X­ray scans of a number of hospitals
but failed for scans from new hospitals: it learned to identify a hospital­specific metal token
on the scans and used the hospital’s pneumonia prevalence rate to adjust its predictions
[3]. Relatedly, deep learning models replicate, and sometimes amplify, human biases in
the data, when a sensitive attribute that is spuriously correlated with the target is picked
up as a shortcut feature for the prediction. Examples include algorithms used to predict
criminal recidivism, such as COMPAS which was reported by ProPublica to be ”biased
against blacks” [4], or to select candidates based on their résumés, like Amazon’s hiring
tool which was found to be biased in favour of men [5].
These failures, in addition to the black box nature of deep learning models, have slowed
down their adoption in high stakes applications such as healthcare and justice. That
is why, explainability in machine learning has attracted considerable interest from the
research community. In particular, counterfactual explanations are an example­based
approach which explains a specific prediction from a black box model by providing an
example that is close to the original input but that would result in a different prediction [6].
For example, in the context of medical imaging, the outcome of a disease classifier for an
image presenting a disease can be explained with an example of the same image without
the disease according to the classifier. After a model is deployed, this method can be
used to help build trust in a black box system by giving the user an understanding of how
the predictor makes a decision. Before that can happen, however, one should ensure
that the model is free of biases to the extent possible. Towards this aim, counterfactual
explanations have been used to uncover model biases [7, 8, 9]. For example, if the
classifier learned to predict the disease based on an attribute that is spuriously correlated
with the disease, such as a demographic attribute, a counterfactual example could be an
image in which this attribute has been changed. However, although this method can help
detect biases, it does not generally provide a method for mitigating them.
Bias mitigation has been tackled in the field of invariant representation learning [10, 11,
12], and has been linked to causality [13, 14, 15]. Indeed, bias arises when a model
is trained on a specific domain, but doesn’t generalize to other domains. Therefore, in­
variant representation learning aims at learning representations that are invariant under
changes in domains. The domains can be sensitive or nuisance attributes. When the

domains are nuisances, as in the case of X­ray scans from different hospitals, we are
dealing with robustness; when they are sensitive attributes, such as race or gender, we
are dealing with fair representations [10, 16]. This is usually done by encoding an input
into a representation that is uninformative of the bias variable, while still being optimal
for the supervised task under consideration. This objective is typically implemented as
an independence constraint, which is in practice relaxed to other measures, such as the
mutual information [11, 17, 12] and the Maximum Mean Discrepancy [10].
In this thesis, we combine counterfactual explanations and invariant representation learn­
ing, in the aim of building a fair and robust classifier with an in­built capability to generate
counterfactual explanations for the task of disease diagnosis in medical images. We pro­
pose a VAE­based generative model which learns a latent space W×Z where W encodes
information about the disease and Z encodes the remaining information in the image. A
classifier predicts the label based on W, and a counterfactual example can be gener­
ated for an input image by changing the latent variable belonging to W in a way that flips
the prediction of the classifier. However, when the presence of a disease is correlated
with a demographic attribute, this attribute might be encoded in W, and consequently,
the classifier might use this information to make predictions. This is problematic because
the demographic attribute is first of all sensitive, and might even be spuriously correlated
with the disease, hindering the robustness of the classifier. Therefore, we would like to
learn representations in W that are invariant to the demographic attribute, and represen­
tations in Z that are invariant to the target label. Under certain assumptions on the causal
relationship between the label and the correlated attribute, we show that this objective
translates to conditional independence constraints on the latent variables, the label and
the attribute. We obtain two versions of our model by extending the common relaxations
of the independence constraints using mutual information and Maximum Mean Discrep­
ancy to the conditional case. We evaluate our method experimentally on the Colored
MNIST dataset by comparing our two models to their equivalent implementing marginal
independence constraints and to a baseline model without independence constraints.
The thesis is organized as follows. The present chapter introduced the content of the
thesis. We introduce the relevant background for this thesis in chapter 2 before presenting
our methods in chapter 3. Chapter 4 presents the datasets and the experiments which
were carried out during the thesis. Finally, the results obtained in the experiments are
discussed and concluded in chapter 5 and 6.

2.1
Latent subspaces in Variational Autoencoders
Our approach to classification and counterfactual generation relies on learning latent sub­
spaces using Variational Autoencoders. This section introduces Variational Autoencoders
(VAE) and the Conditional Subspace VAE.
2.1.1
Variational Autoencoder
Variational Autoencoders (VAE) are based on the auto­encoder setup, which consists
of encoding an input x into a low­dimensional representation z and decoding it into a
reconstruction ˆx. However, while the encoder and decoder networks of a simple auto­
encoder return values for z and ˆx, the corresponding networks in a VAE return values for
the parameters of the distributions over z and x. A VAE models the latent variable z using
a probabilistic framework, and defines a generative process
z ∼pθ(z), x ∼pθ(x|z)
where the prior pθ(z) is a chosen distribution, typically a standard normal distribution
N(0, I), and the observation model pθ(x|z) is a distribution depending on z, typically
a diagonal Gaussian distribution N(x|µθ(z), σθ(z)) in the case of real­valued data or a
Bernoulli distribution B(x|gθ(z)) in the case of binary data [18]. Loaiza­Ganem and Cun­
ningham [19] introduce a new distribution called the continuous Bernoulli for [0,1]­valued
data, which improves on the Gaussian and Bernoulli VAE commonly used for modelling
MNIST images.
Parameter estimation can be performed by maximizing the likelihood pθ(x) over a dataset.
However, pθ(x) =

2.2
Invariant representations
Invariant representations are representations that are invariant under changes in do­
mains. Domains can be nuisance or sensitive attributes. When the domains are nui­
sances, we are dealing with robustness; when they are sensitive attributes, we are dealing
with fair representations [10, 16].
The objective is usually to encode an input x into a representation z that is uninformative
of the nuisance or sensitive attribute c, while still being optimal for the supervised task
under consideration. This objective is typically implemented as an independence con­
straint z ⊥c. In practice this constraint is relaxed to other measures, such as the mutual
information I(z, c) [11, 17, 12] and the Maximum Mean Discrepancy [10].
However, this approach assumes that P(Y |X) does not change across domains. There­
fore, class­conditional independence constraints z ⊥c | y have been proposed for the
general case when this distribution can change [20]. The present section introduces two
approaches to implement these constraints in order to learn conditional invariant repre­
sentations.
2.2.1
Adversarial training
Adversarial training has been proposed to learn invariant representations by making the
learned representations on different domains indistinguishable for an adversarial network
[21]. Subsequently, the adversarial loss has been mathematically derived using the mu­
tual information [17, 11], as presented in section 2.1.2.
Adversarial training has also been used to learn conditional invariant representations by
training an adversarial network to predict the domain from the representation for each
value of the label [20]. We will derive the adversarial loss similarly to Klys et al. using the
conditional mutual information.
The conditional mutual information is the expected value of the mutual information of two
random variables given the value of a third [22]. It can be rewritten in terms of mutual
information [23]:
I(X; Y |Z) = I(X; Y, Z) −I(X; Z)
(2.4)
Another approach to learn invariant representations uses the Maximum Mean Discrep­
ancy, which will be presented next.

2.2.2
Maximum Mean Discrepancy
The Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) [24, 10, 21] quantifies the distance between two
distributions by comparing all of their moments. It can be efficiently implemented using
the kernel trick. For any positive definite kernel k, the MMD between two distributions p
and q is
DMMD(p ∥q) = Ep(z),p(z′)[k(z, z′)] + Eq(z),q(z′)[k(z, z′)] −2Ep(z),q(z′)[k(z, z′)]
and DMMD(p ∥q) = 0 if and only if p = q [21].
The MMD is used by the Variational Fair Autoencoder (VFAE) [10] to learn fair represen­
tations. The model is based on a VAE with priors that encourage independence between
sensitive variables s and latent variables z. However, they argue that there could still
be some dependence in the approximate marginal posterior qϕ(z|s), in particular if the
label y is correlated with the sensitive variable s. Therefore, VFAE maximizes a penal­
ized lower bound which impose a regularization on the marginal qϕ(z|s) through the MMD
penalty DMMD(qϕ(z|s = 0) ∥qϕ(z|s = 1)). We will adapt this penalty to obtain conditional
invariant representations.
2.3
Counterfactual explanation
Explainable machine learning can be categorized into two approaches: model explanation
and outcome explanation [6]. Counterfactual explanations are an instance of the outcome
explanation approach, which explain an explanation for a specific prediction from a black
box model. They provide an example that is close to the original input but that would result
in a different prediction. These explanations are typically generated post­hoc by training
a separate VAE or Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN). In particular, counterfactual
explanations generated with VAEs are often based on gradient descent in latent space,
requiring an optimization for each input [25, 26]. In contrast, our approach uses a VAE
to both make predictions and generate counterfactual explanations, without requiring any
additional optimization. This approach is based on two previous works by Downs et al.
and Zhang et al, which we will now present.
Downs et al. created Counterfactual Recourse Using Disentangled Subspaces (CRUDS)
[27] which uses the CSVAE to generate counterfactual data that satisfy desirable out­
comes. CRUDS generates counterfactuals for a data point (x, y = 0) with correspond­
ing approximate posteriors qϕ(w|x, y = 0) and qϕ(z|x) by sampling z from the posterior
qϕ(z|x) and w from the prior p(w|y = 1) and decoding the latent representation into the
input space. However, according to Zhang et al., the counterfactuals generated for dif­
ferent inputs following this method tend to be similar. In addition, this method is limited
to counterfactual recourse and does not provide a classification model, nor does it aim at
explaining one.
Zhang et al. [28] share our goal of simultaneous classification and counterfactual gen­
eration, but use the latent space of a VAE with normalizing flows (NF). The proposed
NF­VAE uses normalizing flows in between the encoder and decoder of the VAE to create
a latent representation that can be classified using a probit regression model. Figure 2.2
illustrates the pipeline of the model. We will present the model in more detail in the rest
of the section.

3
Methods
The methods in this thesis will now be presented. First, we describe our assumptions
about the considered variables using a causal graph and derive two conditional inde­
pendence constraints. Then, we describe the general architecture of our model, before
focusing on two versions of the model which implement the conditional independence
constraints. Finally, we present our counterfactual generation method.
3.1
Causal graph
We consider a binary attribute c which is correlated with the label y. In particular, we
assume that c and y have a parent­child relationship or have a common parent. For
example, c could be the age of a patient, causing the presence of a disease y. We aim
to learn a representation of the data in the latent space W × Z that encodes information
about y in W and the remaining information, including information about c, in Z.
Our assumptions about the relationships between the latent variables, the label and the at­
tribute can be modelled by three possible causal graphs represented in Figure 3.1, where
we assume that the latent variables w and z are the generative factors of the image x. For
example, w could be clinically­relevant information about the disease and z could be in­
formation about the demographic attributes of the patient and the other factors of variation
in the image. Each causal graph corresponds to one of the three considered relationships
between c and y.
From the causal graphs in Figure 3.1, we can derive the following conditional indepen­
dence relationships between the latent variables, the label and the attribute [29]:
w ⊥c | y
z ⊥y | c.
These independence constraints are implemented through a regularization term in the
loss function of our model, which will be presented in the next section.

3.3.1
Adversarial
The first version, which we call the conditional adversarial model, uses the conditional
mutual information.
The conditional mutual information measures the conditional de­

3.4
Counterfactual generation
Once the model has been trained, counterfactual examples can be generated for an input
image using the weights of the logistic regression model. The logistic regression model
has a Bernoulli posterior qγ(y|w) = B(y|πγ(w)) where πγ(w) = σ(wT aγ +bγ). To generate
a counterfactual example for an image x, it is first encoded into w and z by sampling from
the approximate posterior distributions qϕ(w|x) and qψ(z|x). Then, the latent vector w is
projected into the counter­class region using a step size larger than 1

where αi = −wT aγ+bγ

as derived in section 2.3. Finally, the latent vectors wc and z are

tion pθ(x|wc, z) = CB(x|λθ(wc, z)), following the approach of Loiza­Ganem to reconstruct
MNIST images [19]. Indeed, we find that using the mean of the distribution instead results
in paler images because of the infinite derivative of the mean function at 0 and 1, whereas
sampling from the distribution gives noisy images, as shown by Loaiza­Ganem et al. [19].

In this chapter, we describe the experiments and their results.
We first conduct a series of experiments comparing the models with different levels of
correlation (none, medium, high) between the target label and the correlated attribute,
using variations of the Colored MNIST (CMNIST) dataset [14]. The purpose is to deter­

All experiments were conducted on the gpuv100 nodes of DTU High­Performance Com­

We start by presenting the datasets that we constructed for the experiments, before pro­

4.1
Datasets
We experimented with variations of the Colored MNIST dataset, introduced by Arjovsky et
al. for out­of­distribution generalization [14]. The goal is to predict a binary label assigned
to each image based on the digit. Each image is colored either red or green in a way that
correlates spuriously with the label. Therefore, the color is an effect of the label.
In our experiments, the datasets are obtained from MNIST by subsampling and transform­
ing each example of 4 and 9 as follows. First, assign the preliminary binary label ˜y = 0 for
digit 4 and ˜y = 1 for digit 9, which we call the true label. Second, obtain the final label y,
which we call the noisy label, by flipping ˜y with probability pn. Third, sample the color id
c by flipping y with probability pe. We call this probability the color flip rate. Finally, color
the image red if c = 1 or green if c = 0. We use pn = 0 and pe ∈{0.05, 0.2, 0.5} in the first
series of experiments and pn = pe = 0.2 in the second experiment. For each experiment,
a training set is created from the MNIST training set using the chosen pe and two test sets
are created from the MNIST test set, with a color flip rate of pe and 1 −pe respectively.
The first test set has the same correlation between the label and the color as the training
set, and will be called i.i.d, whereas the second test set has a correlation in the opposite
direction, and will be called o.o.d. The training set is then split into train/validation sets
with as many validation samples as test samples. This results in a train/validation/test
split of 9800/1991/1991 samples.
Our datasets differ from the original Colored MNIST in two ways. While the original dataset
consists of all digit numbers, we restrict our datasets to only images of 4 and 9 following
Zhang et al. [28]. Additionally, the original training set contains two environments with
pe = 0.2 and pe = 0.1 while the test set has pe = 0.9 and both sets have pn = 0.25.
Indeed, while Arjovsky et al. learn representations that are invariant to the environment,
our aim is to learn representations that are explicitly invariant to the color. For this reason,
we include the color id c in the data in addition to the image x and the label y, whereas
Arjovsky et al. include the environment id e. Furthermore, we find that setting pn = pe is
sufficient to make the color useful enough for predicting the target label.

Hyperparameters
All models were trained for 200 epochs with a batch size of 64 and the Adam optimizer

4.2.3
Evaluation
Since our aim is simultaneous classification and counterfactual generation, our evaluation
is geared towards classification accuracy and invariance of the representations.
The classification accuracy of the logistic regression model is computed on both i.i.d and
o.o.d. test sets, to evaluate the classifier’s performance in these two scenarios. Further­
more, since the classifier takes latent variable w as input, the accuracies on both test sets
also indicate whether the representations w are invariant to the color. Indeed, if they are
not invariant to the color, the classifier could use the information about the color that is
encoded in the representations to predict the label, resulting in decreased generalization
performance.
The invariance of the representations is also measured quantitatively in four ways on the
i.i.d test set.
A typical measure of invariance is the adversarial accuracy [10, 11]. Indeed, represen­
tations that are invariant to an attribute are minimally informative about this attribute, so
we evaluate the accuracies of adversaries which predict c from the latent variable w and
y from the latent variable z. These adversaries have the same architecture as the auxil­
iary classifiers in the marginal adversarial model and are trained post­hoc on the training
data using the same optimizer during 20 epochs. We will therefore call them marginal
adversaries. In our experiments, y and c are correlated, so even if the representations
are invariant, the adversary could predict the desired attribute based on the correlated
one with an accuracy greater than 0.5.
Additionally, one way to evaluate that representations w are invariant to the color, is to
count the number of counterfactual examples that have their color changed, since coun­
terfactual generation only involves changes to the representations w. More precisely, we
measure the number of counterfactuals for which the sum of the normalized pixel values
in the channel corresponding to the color that is not the color of the original image is more
than a chosen threshold. We set this threshold to 0.01 after inspecting the normalized
pixel values of a number of MNIST images. We call this measure N. appear. It should be
as close to 0 as possible.
The last two measures are based on the invariance penalty in the loss function of our two
conditional models.
Based on our adversarial model, we propose to evaluate the adversarial accuracies in
predicting c from (w, y) and y from (z, c). This choice is motivated by preliminary experi­
ments where we observed that the predictive posterior of the auxiliary networks converges
to pe when the learned representations are invariant, whereas the entropy, which appears
in the invariance penalty, converges to a value that is more difficult to interpret. These

4.4
Experiment 2: Noisy labels
To make the colors more useful in predicting the labels, we create a dataset with noisy

4.4.1
Hyperparameter tuning
The weights in the loss function of the models are tuned to achieve good classification
accuracy and counterfactual generation. The loss function of the MMD and adversarial
models is composed of the loss of the baseline model with an invariance penalty. There­
fore, we first tune the weights in the loss of the baseline model, before tuning the weights
of the invariance penalty in the loss of the MMD and adversarial models while keeping
the weights of the baseline fixed. Hyperparameter tuning is performed by training models
with random seeds 0, 1, 2 and evaluating them on the validation set.
Baseline model
The loss of the baseline model is made of four components, weighted by the coefficients
β1 to β4: the reconstruction loss, the KL divergences between the posterior and the prior
in the subspaces W and Z, and the classification loss. We set β1 and β3 to 1, and tune
β2 and β4.
We first tune β2 ∈J1, 5K by setting β4 to 0. We train the corresponding baseline models
and evaluate the mean KL divergence ED(x,y) [DKL(qϕ(w|x) ∥p(w|y))] on the training and

5
Discussion
The results of the two experiments will be summarized and discussed with an emphasis
on the limitations of our method. This discussion will serve as a basis for envisioning
future work.
5.1
Experiment 1: Varying levels of correlation
In the first experiment, which involved variations of the CMNIST dataset with true labels
and three different levels of correlation between the label and the color, it was found
that the marginal models are not able to learn representations that exhibit the desired
invariances when the correlation is high or medium. However, this result should be treated
with care because the hyperparameter tuning was only carried out with the baseline and
conditional models. Thus, results related to these two models are more trustworthy, even
though trials involved values of the hyperparameters that were chosen based on human
optimization, which could be imperfect compared to a more systematic hyperparameter
tuning like in the second experiment. It was shown that surprisingly, even for a high
degree of correlation between the label and the color, the representations learned by the
baseline model were almost as invariant as those learned by the conditional models. This
was evaluated on the i.i.d. test set but this is likely to stay true on the o.o.d. test set, given
that the accuracies on this test set are still higher than 0.9 for all models. We hypothesize
that in order to be exploited by a model, a spuriously correlated features must not only be
predictive of the outcome but also significantly improve predictions.
5.2
Experiment 2: Noisy labels
To make the colors as predictive of the labels as the shapes of the digits, we introduced
label noise to the CMNIST dataset with a moderate degree of correlation in the second
experiment. We found as expected that the conditional models were better able to learn
the desired invariances in the representations while having a good classification accuracy
compared to the marginal models, which were also superior to the baseline model. Among
the two conditional models, the MMD model was found to be superior to the adversarial
model in terms of generalization performance and invariance of the representations. In
addition, the MMD model is faster to train since it doesn’t require training an additional
network contrary to the adversarial model.
A systematic hyperparameter tuning was carried out on all models, giving us more con­
fidence in these results than in the first experiment. Interestingly, the hyperparameter
tuning of the marginal models showed that the lowest weight for the marginal invariance
penalty was actually the best, suggesting that the results for the marginal models in the
first experiment could be improved by decreasing these weights. The hyperparameter
tuning consisted in starting with the simplest model possible and adding components one
by one to the loss function and tuning the corresponding weights, one at a time. This
method of hyperparameter tuning was chosen instead of grid search for time reasons, be­
cause model training is time­consuming, taking up to 30 minutes for the adversarial model.
In addition, hyperparameter tuning could not be automatized using grid search, random
search or bayesian optimization, because there isn’t one unique criterium to choose the
values of the hyperparameters, which are the coefficients of the different components in
the loss function. In addition, the criteria for choosing them are actually part of the loss

5.3
Future work
As suggested above, future work could include repeating the tuning of β5, with β6 = 0.
Indeed, although our conditional models performed as desired on the i.i.d. test set, this
was less the case on the o.o.d. test set, where we found that the classification accuracy
was considerably diminished, especially for the adversarial model, suggesting room for
improvement in terms of generalization performance.
A natural continuation of this thesis would then be to apply our method to a real­world
dataset. In particular, this thesis was motivated by the task of disease diagnosis in medical
images, where the presence of a disease could be correlated with a demographic attribute.
In this section, we describe the dataset, experimental setup and evaluation which could
be used for this task.
5.3.1
Dataset
CheXpert, a large dataset of chest X­rays labelled with the presence of 14 radiographic
observations, would be appropriate for this task, because it provides two demographic
attributes, the age and sex, in addition to the labels. Among these 14 observations, car­
diomegaly, which refers to enlarged heart, is particularly well suited to the purpose of
counterfactual explanation, because the validity of the explanation can easily be evalu­
ated by computing the difference map between the original image and the counterfactual
example.
That is why, cardiomegaly was also chosen as the target class for explanation by Singla
et al. [30] who proposed a method of counterfactual explanation which gradually exagger­
ates the semantic effect of a given class predicted by a black box model. They show that
their method can discover confounding bias in the data by training a smile classifier on a
biased version of CelebA, a benchmark dataset of celebrity images, confounded with gen­
der such that all smiling images are of male faces. Our method has similar capabilities,
because it can generate a sequence of counterfactual examples that look increasingly like
the counter­class, by increasing the step of the projection onto the decision boundary, as
shown in Figure 2.3. However, contrary to Singla et al., our goal is not to explain a biased
classifier, but to create a classifier which is at the same time robust to a known source of
bias, and explainable through counterfactual generation. Indeed, cardiomegaly could be
correlated with age and gender because it is associated with heart diseases, whose inci­
dence increases with age and male gender [31]. In addition, images are labelled with the
presence of ”Support Devices”, which could also correlate with cardiomegaly, and com­
pared to demographic attributes, this label has the advantage of being easily recognized
on the image, which facilitates the evaluation of the invariance of the representations.
Therefore, we created a balanced dataset using a subset of CheXpert consisting of chest
X­rays with an anteroposterior (AP) view. In CheXpert, labels for the presence of the
14 observations were extracted automatically from free text radiology reports as positive,

few, as opposed to images with an unmentionned label, so our dataset consisted of all AP

make the dataset balanced with respect to the label, amounting to 40950 images. In our

for the gender. Therefore, future work could include the creation of biased datasets, where

Evaluation
We can directly reuse the quantitative and qualitative measures which we proposed to
evaluate our models on the CMNIST dataset, to the exception of N. appear. During the
experiments on the CMNIST dataset, we actually considered an alternative measure,
named N. switch, which counts the number of counterfactual examples which have their
color switched compared to the original image. An image colored red is said to switch color
if the sum of pixel values in the green channel of the counterfactual example is larger than
that of the red channel, and this is reversed for a green image. However, N. appear was
chosen instead for the CMNIST dataset because N. switch doesn’t account for the cases
where the original color is still predominant, but where the other color also appears in the
image, which is a sign that the label­specific representations are not invariant to the color.
In the case of chest X­rays, similar measures could be devised by training separate clas­
sifiers for age, gender and support devices. N. switch can directly be adapted to this new
dataset, by inferring these attributes from the counterfactual examples using the trained
classifiers and counting the number of images which switched value for the correspond­
ing attribute. N. appear could also be adapted, although less obviously, by considering
the confidence of the classifier which is trained to predict the attribute. Since the consid­
ered attributes are all binary, we can evaluate how much the confidence of the classifier
changed from the original input to the counterfactual example and define a threshold of
significance. If the threshold is reached for an image and its counterfactual example, we
consider that they contribute to N. appear, similarly to CMNIST images.

Conclusion
In this thesis, we combined the theory of invariant representation learning with variational
autoencoders and counterfactual explanations to create a method for simulatenous clas­
sification and counterfactual generation, in the presence of correlation between the target
label y and a known attribute c. We proposed a generative model which learns a repre­
sentation W × Z of the data, where W encodes information about the target label y and
Z encodes information about c along with the remaining information in the image. This
separation of information is achieved by learning representations w which are invariant
to the attribute c and representations z which are invariant to the label y. Invariant rep­
resentations are often implemented using marginal independence constraints between
the latent variable and the attribute, through adversarial training or minimizing the MMD
penalty. However, we have shown using a causal graph that when y and c either share
a parent­child relationship or are confounded by a third variable, the desired invariances
should be implemented using conditional independence constraints. Therefore, we ex­
tended adversarial training and the MMD penalty to the conditional case, to create two
different models. To evaluate the relevance of our approach, we proposed to compare
our conditional models to their marginal equivalents and a baseline model without any
independence constraints. These models were evaluated on variations of the CMNIST
dataset, where the color of the digits is caused by their label, using quantitative and qual­
itative measures of invariance.
First, we investigated the effect of the correlation strength between the color and the label
by varying the flip ratio pe to create the datasets. It was found that the baseline learned
the desired representations as well as the proposed, conditional models, even when the
correlation is high. However, this was not the case for the marginal models, which were
shown to be appropriate only when the variables are not correlated. We hypothesized
that the model did not learn to rely on the color to predict the label because the shapes of
the digits were more predictive of the label.
Therefore, we made the color as useful as the shapes of the digits by introducing label
noise to a CMNIST dataset with a moderate level of correlation. We argued that adding
noise to the labels could simulate the more complex task of diagnosing medical images.
We proposed a method of hyperparameter tuning and showed that marginal indepen­
dence constraints are not adequate for learning invariant representations. However, re­
sults show that they still outperform the baseline, suggesting that these constraints are
still better than none, which explain why they were proposed in previous work. In con­
trast, our proposed approach using conditional independence constraints proved to be
well suited to the task, especially when we relax these constraints using the MMD.
Finally, we discussed the limitations of our approach and suggested directions for future
work. In particular, we proposed to apply our method to detect cardiomegaly in chest
X­rays images using the CheXpert dataset. We highlighted the relevance of this dataset
for our method, discussed how to preprocess the dataset for it to be used in future exper­
iments, and how the experimental setup used for the CMNIST dataset could be adapted
to this more challenging, real­world dataset.
