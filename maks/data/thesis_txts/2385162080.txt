Abstract
Online advertising is, for many sites on the web, the primary, often the only, source of
income, making it possible for web sites to keep running and pay for the hosting fees.
Targeted ads are a type of advertisement built from behavioral profiles of everyone
browsing the web, which means that the information upon which the targeted ads
are created is of a private nature, and as such should be protected. This thesis is an
investigation of how targeted advertisements from Google can be exploited to infer
the information that Google uses to target said advertisements. Furthermore, it is
an investigation of how currently published research in this area can be improved, in
order to build an attack that demands attention from Google.
During this thesis, a novel method was developed; a method that enables an attacker
to target arbitrary amounts of people browsing the web.
The method has been
implemented in code, and thus the foundation for a scalable attack capable of inferring
Google targeted advertisement information has been laid.

Introduction
Everything from our social lives to interactions with our governments is becoming
increasingly digitized. It is possible to find information, love, friends, material goods
and information online. Doctor’s appointments can be scheduled online, social secu-
rity cards can be ordered online, and in Denmark, most of citizens interactions with
the municipality is done online. The world has become centered around people and
their data. This is increasing productivity and efficiency of a wide variety of services,
even enabling new and previously unheard services, that make lives easier for many
people. It has made the world smaller, connected us to each other in ways we could
have never imagined only a couple decades ago. However, this all comes at a price.
Each individual’s life is being recorded; what she likes, who she likes, where she goes
and much, much more. This leaves a great responsibility on the holders of the data
being gathered. Companies like Google, Facebook, governments, municipalities. The
amount of information gathered is unprecedented throughout currently known human
history, and the importance of data protection is just recently becoming apparent to
at least some of the world’s legislative body, judging by the European Commission’s
phasing in of the European Data Protection Directive1. This directive states that ’Ev-
eryone has the right to the protection of personal data’ and ’...persons or organisations
which collect and manage your personal information must protect it from misuse and
must respect certain rights of the data owners which are guaranteed by EU law’ which
gives a good impression of what the directive is about. The legislation also defines a
fine for breaking this law; a fine of up to 4% of global yearly income to a company
that breaks the law. However, the need for legislation speaks for itself; we cannot
trust the holders of data to regulate themselves, to live up to their responsibility.
Data protection is technically difficult, time consuming and expensive. It does not
necessarily, and certainly not quickly, improve earnings. When information is stolen
(as it regularly is2), commonly called data breaches, data owners admit to there being
a problem. Until this occurs, ’there is no problem’. The possibilities for the use of
the data depends on the nature of the data, and some of the usages of the data are
less severe than others. Blackmail and identity theft may cause economic trouble
for individuals, perhaps social out-casting or division of families, but it will not cause
death. However, in certain political climates, some data can cause societies to change,

CHAPTER 2
Literature review
In this chapter we first explain how the online advertisement ecosystem works, and
then go through the academic literature and compile the currently publicly research
into the concept of Web Personalization Security Vulnerabilities. We describe what
the papers have done, how they have done it and finally what their results were.
Furthermore, we classify their attacks and describe the classifications that we have
chosen and explain why they have been given that classification.
2.1
The Google advertising ecosystem
The google targeted advertising system consists of two main components, AdSense and
AdWords. AdSense is the system which enables websites to become part of the Google
Display Network(from here on referred to as GDN), which is the network consisting
of all the sites that give their availability to host ads.
These websites are called
publishers. AdWords is the system which allows for advertisers to create multiple
types of ads and have them displayed on GDN websites. The ads are either location-
based, content-based(contextual), generic or profile-based(targeted) and they are either
shown on GDN websites(display-based), search results on Google(search-based), in
the YouTube platform or in the Gmail service. The advertiser can use a graphical user
interface to create the online advertisements. In order to configure an advertisement
campaign, the advertiser first chooses the destination URL of the user that clicks
the ad. Then the advertiser defines the advertisement to show and the keywords
describing it (in the case of search based ads). Afterwards, the placement needs to
be set, which is the set of websites where the ad will be displayed (in case of display
based ads), and the time and geographical location where the ad should appear. The
next steps are to select an audience to reach, setting the total budget for the ad and
the bidding type and price. Bidding options need to be set for each ad. AdWords
provides multiple different bidding options including cost per click(CPC), cost per
1000 impressions(CPM), cost per acquisition(CPA) and automatic bidding. Google
makes online auctions, based on the received bid values, among all the advertisers.
The winning ad is then displayed to the user when she visits the publisher page.
The interface provided by AdWords has counters showing what audiences the ads
have reached and how often the ads have gotten impressions and/or clicks. This is
illustrated in Figure 2.1.

The TRAP attack
[Con+15] introduces an attack (named TRAP) that makes it possible to extract
private information from the GDN. The information they (partially) extract is the
affinity audience. The type of ads they are targeting are the display ads.
TRAP utilizes a position as both the publisher and advertiser of a set of targeted
ads. The ads are created in a campaign created by TRAP, on a GDN website it
controls6. When a user visits the TRAP website, the increase in impressions on the
TRAP ad is correlated with the visit. The steps performed to realize the attack are:
Set up a website which will be part of GDN, register it on AdSense. Monitor visitors’
accesses. Create an ad campaign, through AdWords, with ads that target specific
affinity audiences(comprising the interests to be discovered). Ensure they are only
displayed on the attacker website. Compare AdWords data with web user access to
infer affinity audience.

2.3
Castellucia et. al’s attack
[CKT12] explores how much of the Google interest categories and Google ads profile
of arbitrary web users, it is possible to extract by looking at targeted advertisements.
They explain how the GDN works, how the Google Ad Profiles are created and
describe an attack which extracted 79% of users’ Google interest categories, along
with 58% of the Google ads profiles.
In order to get these advertisements, they eavesdrop on a network. This is possi-
ble because ads are almost always served with HTTP, even when websites use SSL.
When they have obtained the ads, they analyze them and from there they are able
to efficiently extract the private information. The details are explained below.
To perform the attack they follow these steps: Collect all ads served to a target user,
filter the collected ads to only retain targeted(profile-based) ones. Classify targeted
ads into categories, and reconstruct profiles.
There are two ’building blocks’ needed for this attack to be successful, the method
for web page categorization and the method for category comparison.
Their web page categorization works by deriving interest categories from a given web-
site. It relies on the tool used by Google to generate the Google Ads Preferences10
pages.
The algorithm is as follows: A webpage is visited and the resulting Dou-
bleClick cookie is saved. A request is made to the Google ads preferences page with
the saved DoubleClick cookie. Because Google does not always update the Google
ads preferences page for a single web visit, and because the ad preferences are up-
dated after a period of time ranging from 1-2 minutes, they repeat this step 5 times
every 2 minutes. Finally, they parse the Google ads preferences page and retrieve the
corresponding categories.
To evaluate the performance of their categorization method, they scraped 5000 ads
from Google search page, and 2000 sites from the GDN, classified them using the
tool and reviewed the results. They found that Google can categorize more than
90%, most of which are relevant categories. They then tested another method which
does not rely on the Google ads preferences page (cosine similarity as used by [toub])
and compared the performance. They found that 60% of the categorizations overlap.
Figure 2.8 illustrates an example categorization.
In order to compare categories, they developed three different classifications. Same
category: Two categories are considered equivalent if they have an exact match. Same
parent: Two categories are considered equivalent in this category if they have the same
parent. For example the two categories ”Arts & Entertainment →Entertainment
Industry →Film & TV Industry →Film & TV Awards” and ”Arts & Entertainment
→Entertainment Industry →Film & TV Industry →Film & TV Production” have
the same parent category ”Film & TV Industry”, so they fall into this category. Same

2.4
Facebook and it’s advertisement ecosystem
Facebook from the user’s perspective
When a person signs up on Facebook, she is required to provide her real first and last
name, email, gender and date of birth.11 She is also encouraged to upload a picture
and to fill out a set of more detailed information about herself. This set includes basic
information: current city, hometown, interested in men/women, looking for friend-
ship/relationship/networking, political and religious views, relationships: relationship
status (single, it’s complicated, married etc.), education and work information contact
information: address, phone, IM screen names, emails, likes and interests: favorite
activities, music, books, movies, TV as well as Pages which could be StarBucks or
Coca Cola. Events like the Olympics, websites, AIDS. Any user can like any site
about any topic.
Users are also able to Like many different things on the web, such as webpages,
blog posts, articles, products. They are able to post status updates, videos, pictures
and ask questions. All these possibilites to express themselves enables Facebook to
learn a lot about their users, by data mining this content.
Facebook provides the ability to limit who can see the information a user shares
on Facebook through a privacy setting specific for each category of information. The
user can distinguish five significant levels of privacy settings specifying the visibility
of a particular type of information: Everyone, Friends of Friends, Friends Only, Hide
from specific people and Only me.
Facebook from the advertiser’s perspective
An ad created using Facebook’s self-serve advertising system consists of the destina-
tion URL, Title, Body Text and an optional image/video. The advertiser is offered
a targeting criteria to specify their ads. Targeting parameters include location (in-
cluding a city), gender, age (including an age range and ’Target people on their
birthdays’), interested in (e.g. men or women), relationship status (e.g. single or mar-
ried), languages, likes & interests, education (including specifying a specific school
and graduation years) and workplaces.
These parameters can be combined in any way. An example of a targeting criteria
could be ’men who live within 50 miles of San Francisco, aged 24-30, single, interested
in men, Like skiing, graduated from Harvard and work at Apple’. During the ad
creation process, Facebook provides the advertiser with a real-time ’Estimated Reach’
box, that estimates the number of users who fit the currently entered targeting criteria.
To launch the ad, the advertiser needs to choose what time of day the ad should be
displayed, daily budget and max bid for Cost per impression(CPM) or Cost per
click(CPC) campagins. Figure 2.9 shows the advertiser interface.

2.5
Korolova’s attack
[Kor10] explores how much and what private information, about arbitrary Facebook
users, can be extracted by an attacker posing as an advertiser. They discover that
Facebook uses private and ’Friends Only’ user information to determine whether the
user matches an advertising campaign’s targeting criteria; the default privacy settings
lead to many users having a publicly visible uniquely identifying set of features; the
variety of permissible targeting criteria allows microtargeting an ad to an arbitrary
person; the ad campaign performance reports contain a detailed breakdown of infor-
mation, including number of unique clicks, respondents’ demographic characteristics,
and breakdown by time, which they show leads to an attacker posing as an advertiser
being able to design and successfully run advertising campaigns that enable them to:
1. Infer information that people had posted on Facebook using the ’Only me’,
’Friends only’ and ’Hide from X people’.
2. Infer private information not posted on Facebook.
3. Display intrusive ads to individuals.
Below is the detailed description of the two attacks [Kor10] describes. Their pur-
pose is to exploit the details of the advertising system’s design in order to infer private
information.
Infer information posted on Facebook with ’Only me’, ’Friends Only’, and
’Hide from these people’ privacy settings through ad campaign match
Attack 1: Using inference from impressions, it aims at inferring information
that a user has put on Facebook, but has used the privacy settings to restrict to e.g.
’Visible only to me’ or ’Visible to friends only’. The attack bypasses this restriction
by running several advertisement campaigns targeted at the victim (a user) differing
only in the targeting criteria corresponding to the private information the attacker
is trying to infer. The difference in the campaign performance reports of these ad-
vertisement campaigns enables the attacker to infer the desired private information.
The necessary conditions for the attack to be successful are: the ability to choose
targeting criteria that identify the user uniquely; Facebook’s user to ad matching
algorithm showing the ad only to users who match the ad targeting criteria exactly
and using the information of whether the user satisfies a criteria when determining
the campaign match; the user using Facebook sufficiently often so that the ads have
a good chance of being displayed to that user at least once over the observation time,
if the user matches the targeting criteria; the advertising system treating all the ad-
vertising campaigns equally.
In order to perform the attack, these steps are followed: assuming as input a
user U and a feature F whose value from the possible set of values f1,..., fk the
attack desires to determine, if it is entered by U on Facebook, observe the profile

2.7
What is the optimal attack
In this section we try to define what an optimal attack should look like. We discuss
the optimal attack in the context of the following properties: scalability, victim ac-
tions necessary, attacker actions necessary, attack noise and information revealed. It
should be noted that the focus here is on the optimal attack, which may or may
not be entirely possible. There are two different types of actors to consider in this
discussion, malicious and non-malicious actors. The potential malicious actors in-
clude regular websites wanting to retrieve as much information possible, in order to
monetize it by e.g. showing more personalized search results or by using it to sell
services to a third party; any malicious actor who can use the information to sell to
other third parties, or use the information to blackmail users. It should be noted
that even though a company running a website deploying the optimal attack might
not break the law, it still raises ethical concerns. The non-malicious actors include
the owners of the sensitive information collected from people on the web, e.g. Google,
Facebook; it also includes the browser vendors, e.g. Google, Mozilla, Microsoft. The
non-malicious actors are the ones that we, with this research, are trying to convince
that there is a real problem in the current advertising ecosystem, through the dis-
playing of the severity of this attack, done by taking the perspective of the malicious
actors.
For an attack to be a major threat to common people browsing the web, it needs
to be scalable to a point where attackers can farm information from a large group of
users in an automated, reasonably fast and cheap way. A non-scalable attack is still
a threat, but more so for socially/politically high ranking people in society, seeing as

2.8
Which research direction and why
Looking at the three current attacks, a promising approach seems to be a revamp
of [CKT12].
None of the attacks are truly scalable, which, to us, seems like the
single most important factor holding them back from achieving excellent efficiency.
Therefore this research focuses on how to improve the scalability of current attacks,
while remaining invisible to the victims and still retrieving the same information
about the victims. Instead of having a tight coupling between the attack and the
interest we wish to infer, or between the location from where the attack is being
executed, the attack should be loosely coupled to every possible interest out there,
independent of physical location. To achieve this, we have come up with an attack that
simply probes victim browsers about all the targeted advertisements they have been
displayed. The attack is a re-composition of previously researched components, along
with a novel component that to our knowledge has not been used in this field before,
the cache timing component. To probe victim browsers, we rely on web cache timing
attacks[FS00][VJN15][BB07]. This kind of attack is difficult and possibly costly to
fix, it works very well and thus it is a solid component in this attack. In order to
find the advertisements to probe for, we leverage the research of [CKT12] to classify
the web pages pointed to by the advertisements. This way, the attack consists of two
decoupled phases: finding the advertisements to probe for, and actually probing the
browser for them. This approach has the potential to solve the scalability issue of the
current attacks, and thus to draw attention to (and acknowledgement of) the issue
of private and possibly sensitive information, about people browsing the web, leaking
to third parties.

3.1
Revealing interest categories of arbitrary victims
The question this attack tries to answer is: It is possible to create an attack that
retrieves the same information as the previously mentioned attacks, while allowing
the attack to scale to an arbitrary number of victims?
The attack works by probing a victim’s browser cache to see if it has a resource
cached. The resource we are probing for is a resource associated with a targeted
advertisement.
If the resource is cached, we say that the ad represented by the
resource has been displayed to her. In order to know what to probe the victim’s
browser cache for, we farm advertisements on publisher pages. These ads are then
filtered to retain only the targeted ones, and then classified to a topic or a group
of topics. When we know that an ad has been displayed to a victim, that the ad
displayed is a targeted ad and that the ad is classified to a certain topic, we can say
that the network displaying the ad to her has classified her as interested in the topic
of the advertisement. This means that we are actually inferring personal interest
categories from, in our case, a Google profile. It is worth noting that we do not know
every source of information that Google uses to target advertisements, which means
that what we are inferring might not be limited to personal interest categories. In
the following we describe the attack in terms of scalability, victim actions necessary,
attacker actions necessary, attack noise and information revealed, and compare it to
similar attacks described in the academic literature: [CKT12][Con+15][Kor10].

The entire attack relies heavily on being able to farm as many of the ads in rotation

gives us a greater chance of getting a browser cache hit (elaborated later) and thus re-

need the matching UCR. We cannot simply take anything with some image extension

Step 2: Targeted ad classification
In order to filter out advertisements that are not targeted, and thus not useful in this
attack, we use the approach of [CKT12]. There are four different types of display ads,
location-based, contextual, generic and targeted ads[CKT12]. We are only interested
in the targeted ones, because their presence can reveal information collected from
Google about a person. The filtering method (ab)uses the Google AdWords platform
to classify arbitrary web pages. Adwords has a tool called Display Planner. It enables
a customer to perform multiple actions, including getting targeting ideas based on
a keyword or a web page. Figure 3.3 shows the Display Planner interface. When
collecting the advertisements, each ad collection contains the LPU and UCR, and the
webpage that the ad was collected from (called the publisher here on out). During the
filtering process we classify, using the Google Display Planner, the publisher and the
LPU which leads to the advertisers web page, and thus represents the advertiser. If
an advertisement and a publisher have at least one category in common, we consider
the ad contextual and discard it. It is worth noting that an advertisement can both
be classified by us as contextual, and yet be targeted. This is because any targeted
advertisement that shares a topic with the publisher will be classified as contextual.
This is sub-optimal, but we must be strict with the targeted classifications to reduce
pollution in our results. Because of this, we must not use pages with topics over-
lapping with the topic of the ad we are trying to farm, in order to minimize this
occurrence.
To filter out the generic advertisements, we use a number of different profiles (Dou-
bleClick cookies) with no overlapping interests (with each other and the ad being
filtered), visit the publisher of the ad in question 10 times8, and if the ad is displayed
to either of the profiles, we consider the ad to be generic. We do not take action to
filter out location-based advertisements, because we do all our experiments from the
same location. This way, we will only be, possibly, shown ads that are based on the
same location.

over time, we might get her interests wrong. A scenario could be that Google has
classified Alice as liking Sports on January 1, based on her navigation pattern. Alice
never clears her cache. On February 1, her pattern has changed so much that Google
now thinks she does not like Sports, but Movies instead. When we start farming the
ads, if a company has had the same Sports ad running for a month, we would pick it
up, and if Alice has that ad cached (from when she liked Sports), we would think she
is interested in Sports, which would be wrong as of February 1. As described before,
this can also be looked at as a bonus, and not an issue. We could set a threshold for
how long an ad was farmed before we consider cache hits as an indicator of historic
interests, as opposed to the current interests of the victim.

site long enough for us to check her cache for all the ads we have collected. The task

4.3
A different direction
In this research, we have focused on attacks targeting average web users, however,
another direction of this research could be to target specific advertisers. An attack
could focus on creating as many fake clicks for an advertisement owned by a specific
advertiser, let’s say Microsoft. This would cost Microsoft money, gaining them no
traffic, and at the same time undermine the business model of e.g. Google. As an
attacker we would use our AdFarmer to farm as many advertisements as possible,
and then check the LPU to see if the advertisements have a connection- or belong
to Microsoft. This farming would have to be done by different Google profiles, until
we found a profile with the interests that make the GDN show me advertisements
from my target. Then we could construct a Google profile that has a high probability
of being shown ads from said advertiser, and then, using the AdFarmer, click these
advertisements, thus generating fake traffic. As mentioned before, Google does take
the issue of fake traffic very serious, and it is not possible for us to, at this point, say
how difficult it would be to generate fake traffic beating the Google detection sys-
tem. However, it could potentially be a significant blow to the current advertisement
system, which could lead to an increased focus on the issues in the current implemen-
tation, thus leading to a higher probability of it receiving the necessary funding and
man power to be fixed / improved sooner rather than later.

The purpose of this experiment is to get an idea of how best to farm advertise-
ments. It is unknown which type of profile is best suited to get as large as possible
an amount of targeted advertisements.
In this experiment, we use five Google profiles, in which we manually set a single
top level topic as a positive interest for each profile. No gender or other information is
set for any of the profiles. The topics (from here on out called Farmer Topics) chosen
for the profiles are: Shopping, Jobs & Education, Home & Garden, Autos & Vehicles
and Beauty & Fitness. The physical location of the machine gathering the data is
Copenhagen. A set of base pages are chosen to be visited and navigated around on.
They serve as a starting point for the Ad Farmer, which collects the advertisements.
Specifically, the Ad Farmer will visit each page, click 5 links within the same domain,
search on Google for the name of the base page plus the Farmer Topic, and visit one
of the search result links. The pages are chosen so that their topics are not directly
related to the Farmer Topics, and so that they are popular websites that many people
visit (according to Google). The pages are http://startpage.lenovo.com, http:
//yahoo.com, http://dailymail.co.uk, http://reddit.com, http://imgur.com
and http://9gag.com.
The Ad Farmer is then run 10 times with each of these
profiles. The only pre-processing performed on the data gathered before creating the
graph is the translation of some of the topics from Danish to English1.
Figures 5.1, 5.2, 5.3, 5.4, 5.5, 5.6, 5.7, 5.8, 5.9 and 5.10 depict the data gathered.
The advertisements farmed with each profile have two graphs associated with them,

reason for this could be that the advertisers in these topics are simply willing to pay
a higher CPC, thus winning the bidding auctions more often. If this is indeed the
case, it would seem that the outcome of the bidding auctions is the largest influence
on what advertisements are displayed. Positive interests are another such influence,
and they could weigh less. It is important to note that even though a Farmer Topic
is not logically related to an advertisement topic, it is possible for an advertiser to
target any given audience. The advertiser might have done her own research and
concluded that their target audience is in fact usually interested in their topic, even
though it might not seem reasonable at first glance. To get the ads we want, we
might need to overemphasize the influence of interests as compared to bidding auc-
tions. This is because if we are to infer that a victim is interested in e.g. Autos
& Vehicles, the probability of us getting a cache hit is low if we only have a small
number of advertisements related to that topic. To figure out how to improve on this,
we believe more experiments need to be done to explore the lower level mechanisms
of the advertisement bidding.
Another possibility as to why some topics are over-represented could be that some
advertisers focus more on audiences that visit a specific web location. So Autos &
Vehicles could be a topic of which the advertisers behind might focus more on which
web pages are visited, and on some specific websites they are willing to bid high
enough to win.
5.3
Illuminating experiments
In this section we describe possible future experiments that could further illuminate
how to get closer to being able to get the advertisements needed to infer specific
interests.
We have seen that the influence of a single positive interest is not exceptionally
significant. However, it might be that negative interests carry more influence than
their positive counterparts. So an interesting experiment could be to set a single
positive interest, and then set the topics that are over-represented in Experiment 0
as negative interests. If Google adheres to negative interests in a stricter way than
==PAGE:73==
5.3 Illuminating experiments

positive interests, it could be that the negated interests are not allowed to bid in the
advertisement auction, possibly lowering the CPC price and thus allowing advertisers
with lower budgets to prevail.
An extension to this experiment could be to set a single, or a small set, of pos-
itive interests, and then negate all other interests. This might simulate the opting
out of targeted advertisements all together, an action that Google has enabled with
the click of a button3. Google states that ’When you opt out, you’ll still see ads by
Google – they just won’t be based on topics that you like, your visits to advertisers’
websites or demographics’. So the optimal result in this experiment might be that
all targeted advertisements farmed belong to the positive interests set, and all other
advertisements farmed should be either contextual or generic. This would allow us
to get advertisements from a specific topic, and make it easier to get advertisements
from topics that more often come from advertisers with smaller budgets.
To test whether some advertisers target audiences on specific web sites more than
audiences with specific positive interests, we could choose base pages that are related
to the Farmer Topic(s). This might also be a way to overemphasize the influence of
other factors than money in the bidding auction. However, to do this, an extension
must be made to the way we filter advertisements. This is because we consider an
advertisement that shares a topic with the web page we found it on contextual (as
has already been mentioned).
A slightly different experiment might focus on how precisely we can farm adver-
tisements associated with lower level topics. For example it could be interesting to
see if we can farm all the advertisements related to Smart Phones which is a sub topic
of the top level topic Internet & Telecom. If this is possible, it would mean that we
will be able to very precisely infer low level interests in victims.
