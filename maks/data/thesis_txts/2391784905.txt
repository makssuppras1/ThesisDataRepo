Abstract
The thesis studies the inversion capped neutral (conditionally neutral) atmospheric boundary
layer.
The eﬀect of the potential temperature inversion on the mean wind speed proﬁle is
analyzed using the data from seventeen LES simulations. The averaged turbulent quantities
proﬁles are presented, explained and the relevant dimensional groupings to obtain dimensionless
functions are investigated.
Based on this observations three solutions for the mean speed
proﬁle have been obtained: analytic, semi-empirical and from similarity theory. The last is
the most reliable and is obtained using Buckingham Π theorem twice.

Motivation
In the last decades thanks to the eﬀort of individuals and whole countries large scale production
of electricity from wind has became a reality. Astonishing progress has been made from the ﬁrst
commercial wind turbines on every aspect. Improving eﬃciency, lowering costs and maximizing
energy yield have driven the design of wind turbines and wind farms. In order to ensure a
constant decrease in the levelized cost of energy (LCOE) manufacturers have developed along
the years increasingly larger turbines that today reach hundreds of meters at their highest point
and projects in the pipelines continuously grow in size.
In parallel with the development of wind turbines a branch of meteorological sciences has fo-
cused on wind resource assessment with the aim of obtaining the best possible prediction of
wind resource at turbine sites, crucial to determine the annual energy production (AEP) and
loads that a turbine will experience along its lifetime. As the industry grew the need of harmo-
nization and common rules lead to the development of the IEC standard [1] that collects the
best practices followed by the industry when it comes to the design of wind turbines. These
rules arose from a need to synthesize scientiﬁc validity and simplicity of application and they
represent the guidelines followed in the design of a wind turbine. For what concerns the wind
proﬁle, at the present day the standard [1] includes two diﬀerent equations, namely the power-
law and the logarithmic-law (log-law). While this equations deﬁnitely satisfy the criterion of
simplicity, they may not reﬂect the actual wind experienced by a wind turbine. The ﬁrst one
relies only on the assumption that the the wind proﬁle is exponential while the second one is
obtained through similarity theory and its applicability, in homogeneous terrain and in absence
of thermal eﬀects, within the ﬁrst part of the atmospheric boundary layer (ABL) is conﬁrmed
by experience, measurements and by a careful study of the turbulent kinetic energy budget
(TKE). Its simplicity relies on the fact that it determines the wind proﬁle based solely on two
surface based correlated parameters: the frictional velocity u∗and the roughness length z0.
Now, even if the standard includes only those simple formulations, in order to perform a careful
wind resource assessment thermal eﬀects in the ABL cannot be excluded. The log-law resem-
bles the physics and is an acceptable solution close to the surface in neutral conditions (no heat
exchange between air and earth surface) but it is has been shown and formalized by Monin and
Obukhov [2] that the thermal eﬀects on the wind proﬁle within the ABL are not negligible.
They have ﬁrst developed a similarity theory that predicts the wind proﬁle based on frictional
velocity u∗, roughness length z0, the heat ﬂux at the surface Q0, and a universal function φm,
which coincides with the log-law in absence of heat ﬂux. From the ﬁrst introduction of the the-
ory in scientiﬁc community a lot of progress has been done and reliable expressions for φm have
been found [3] for the stable (downwards heat ﬂux from air to surface) and unstable (upwards
heat ﬂux from surface to air) boundary layers. It has to be kept in mind that this similarity
theory, as much as the log-law, holds only close to the surface. It depends strictly on surface
parameters and this questions its applicability to the whole height of modern wind turbines.
The physical interpretation of the upper part of the ABL lags the one of the close to surface ﬂow
due to an extremely challenging measurement environment and complex ever-changing physics.
Nevertheless as wind turbines keep growing it is crucial to obtain a deep understanding of the
whole boundary layer.
As the upper part of the ABL is considered, the inﬂuence of additional parameters has to
be included into the physical model: the potential temperature inversion and Coriolis param-
eter.
By potential temperature inversion it is meant a positive gradient of mean potential
temperature ∂Θ
∂z above the ABL, while the value of this gradient is normally referred as the
6
==PAGE:7==
’strength of the inversion’. A potential temperature inversion is most of the time responsible for
extinguishing turbulence and hence dictates the height of the ABL rather than M-O dynamics.
The traditional classiﬁcation of ABLs is based on surface heat ﬂux and may lead to misleading
results when applied to inversion capped boundary layers. This is especially true for boundary
layers which do not have a surface heat ﬂux and would be considered ’neutral’ i.e. no buoyancy
eﬀects. In order to account for the eﬀects of a positive gradient of potential temperature a new
terminology is proposed in [4]: ’truly neutral’ and ’conditionally neutral’ ABL. A ’truly neutral’
ABL is characterized by a constant mean potential temperature proﬁle (neutral stratiﬁcation)
while a ’conditionally neutral’ ABL is capped by a positive gradient of potential temperature
which extinguishes the turbulence (stable stratiﬁcation). The Coriolis parameter and hence the
inﬂuence of the Coriolis force is normally excluded in surface-based theories and its inﬂuence
is disregarded under the assumption that close to the surface the its contribution is negligible.
However, moving further up from the surface its relative contribution increases up to the top
of the ABL where it will be in balance with the pressure force term establishing the geostrphic
balance.
The objective of the present work is to present, observe, study, explain and ultimately pre-
dict the physics and the wind proﬁle of a conditionally neutral ABL and determine the inﬂu-
ence of geostrophic wind, inversion of potential temperature strength, Coriolis parameter and
roughness length exert on this kind of ABL.

Introduction
This thesis studies the physics that governs the conditionally neutral boundary layer based on
data from Large Eddy Simulations (LES) and this drives the structure of the document that
resembles the logical path followed by the author. The ﬁrst chapter presents the indispens-
able equations that govern dry atmospheric ﬂows. In the second chapter the physics of the
atmospheric boundary layer is presented together with its most classical solutions and these
are compared to the results of the LES simulations. The third chapter brieﬂy presents the LES
code and explains the results of the simulations. This is done by using the plots to explain the
equations derived in the ﬁrst chapter. Extraordinary attention is dedicated to the turbulent
kinetic energy budget (TKE) as it is considered the key to reveal the physics of the ABL. A
similar analysis is presented in the fourth chapter, this time showing the inﬂuence of geostrophic
wind, roughness length, inversion strength and Coriolis parameter.
The observations made in the ﬁrst chapters will be the basis of Chatper 5 and 6 which repre-
sent the core and ﬁnal point of the thesis. Here using dimensional analysis and semi-empirical
arguments an equation to predict the proﬁle of wind up to 80% of the height of the ABL is
derived and compared to the results obtained from the simulations.

their large scales and their consequent extreme sensitivity to buoyancy forces. The governing
equations for dry atmosphere are here presented following [5] with special focus on the kinetic
energy budgets and their physical implications.

Chapter 2
The atmospheric boundary layer
(ABL)
Introduction
The boundary layer is the part of a ﬂuid ﬂow which is strongly aﬀected by the presence of the
surface. When considering atmospheric ﬂows even weak winds will create large shear stresses
at the surface and will lead continually to the creation of eddies. These are very eﬀective in
transferring heat and momentum across the whole boundary layer depth, which vary from few
tens of meters to few kilometers from the ground depending on the thermodynamics of the
system. Wyngaard in [5] deﬁnes the atmospheric boundary layer (ABL) as:
• ’A region of continuous turbulence, instantaneously bounded by a thin, sharp top, the con-
voluted, ever-changing interface between the turbulent motion and the stably stratiﬁed, non
turbulent ﬂow above. As a free stream boundary of a turbulent ﬂow its local, instantaneous
thickness could be of the order of the Kolgomorov microscale η.’
• Averaging produces a much thicker interfacial layer at the ABL top; in it the mean po-
tential temperature increases smoothly with height, the mean-wind components transition
to their free-stream values and the turbulence level decreases near zero. Its thickness can
be as much as 20% −50% of the mean depth of the ABL.
• Mean ﬂow in the steady, horizontally homogeneous ABL, the result of a mean balance
among turbulent stress divergence, pressure gradient, and Coriolis forces.’
Few but important diﬀerences divide the ABL to boundary layers typical of engineering ﬂows.
Firstly, due to Earth’s rotation a ﬁctitious force, the Coriolis force, has to be included into
the model. This deeply aﬀects the structure of the boundary layer and its relative importance
increases moving away from the surface until the height in which, solely with pressure force,
establishes the geostrophic balance. Another feature typical of the ABL is its diurnal cycle
caused by Sun radiation on the earth surface. While air is transparent to this radiation the
earth surface is not and therefore it will start heating. When the surface will have a higher
temperature than the air above, a heat transfer from the surface to the air is established which
drives buoyant convection and as a result the mean height zi of the ABL increases. On a clear
night the ABL the picture is reversed. The air is warmer then the below surface and the heat
ﬂux goes from the air down to the surface. As a result the ABL is much shallower and stably
stratiﬁed while the interface between the turbulent ABL and the above non turbulent ﬂow may
be complicated by gravity waves. Traditionally three types of ABL are identiﬁed based on the
surface energy balance [6]:
• An unstable boundary layer develops as a result of a heat ﬂux from the surface to the air.
• A stable boundary layer develops as a result of a heat ﬂux from the air to the surface.
14
==PAGE:15==
• A neutral boundary layer develops when there is no net heat exchange between the air and
the surface. This case however is unusual since small variation in potential temperature
can create considerable buoyancy eﬀects. In real world application it is therefore more
common to use the expression ’nearly neutral boundary layer’ to indicate a boundary
layer which is not strongly aﬀected by buoyancy eﬀects.
It has to be said that even though a truly neutral boundary layer rarely exists, it does represent
in many cases a good approximation especially in high winds/ cloudy locations. A last classiﬁ-
cation has to be made based on the presence of positive potential temperature gradient at the
top of the ABL i.e. potential temperature inversion. As mentioned the scope of this thesis is to
study ABL with null surface heat ﬂux, capped by an inversion of potential temperature which
following [4] the terminology is deﬁned as: conditionally neutral ABL. What determines the
boundary layer structure is again buoyancy eﬀects, but this time rather than from the surface,
buoyancy forces act from above. Then, the last type of ABL and the topic of the thesis is:
• Conditionally neutral ABL. It results from a heat and mass exchange at the top of the
ABL, caused by an inversion of potential temperature.
In this chapter the physics of neutral, convective and stable boundary layer are introduced and
their most classical theoretical solution are presented and the applicability of these solutions to
the the results of simulation ’A’ in Table 3.1 is tested.

Stable and unstable ABL
Considering again equation (1.9) it can be seen that in the presence of a negative vertical
gradient of mean potential temperature ∂Θ
∂z < 0 an upward motion of a parcel creates a positive
potential temperature deviation ˜θ′ and therefore a positive buoyancy force. A downward motion
creates a negative buoyancy force. It can be concluded that in case of a negative vertical gradient
of mean potential temperature every vertical motion of a parcel in enhanced by the eﬀect of
buoyancy forces: this is called unstable stratiﬁcation. The opposite picture, stable stratiﬁcation,
results from a positive vertical gradient in mean potential temperature. Every positive vertical
motion is opposed by a negative buoyancy force that acts to restore the particle in place and
vice-versa.

Stable ABL
Following [5] three diﬀerent kinds of stably stratiﬁed ABLs can be identiﬁed:
• The ﬁrst develops when the wind is advected on a cooler surface downstream.
• The second, most common and studied, develops in night of clear weather where the
warmer air ﬂows on a cooler surface and establishes a downward ﬂux of heat.
• The last one is the so-called ’inversion-capped neutral’ addressed as conditionally neutral
which is the topic of this thesis and will be addressed as ’conditionally neutral ABL
following [6].
It will be shown that the solution for the classical stable ABL (clear weather) based on M-O
similarity is not applicable to conditionally neutral ABL. Nevertheless these two types of ABL
share negative buoyancy forces which causes similar eﬀects on the ﬂuid ﬂow.
A stable ABL is normally shallower than the convective ABL by an order of magnitude and
the largest eddies are much smaller and weaker than in the CBL. The reason of this diﬀerent
behaviour can be found in equation (1.17) i.e. in the TKE budget. Under stable stratiﬁcation
the buoyancy term represents a loss and therefore will be labeled as Buoyant Destruction (BD)
which increases the losses through dissipation ϵ. The other principal term in the TKE budget
is the production of turbulent kinetic energy through shear. In this case the TKE budget will
read:
SP ≃BD + ϵ
(2.11)
Since normally [5] the dissipation rate and shear production terms are of the same order of
magnitude, turbulence can survive a rather small rate of buoyant production. It is possible
deﬁne a ’critical state’ the case in which the sum of dissipation and buoyant destruction balance
the shear production are in balance. If the buoyant destruction term becomes more negative
turbulent kinetic energy will decrease and ﬁnally die out over time.

In Chapter 1 it has been showed that in stationary conditions the turbulent transport term TT
and the pressure transport term PT are responsible solely for transporting the TKE throughout
the ABL, the shear production term SP is a source and ﬁnally the dissipation term a sink. The
buoyant production BP term instead can be either a source or a sink depending on the gradient
of potential temperature. In presence of a positive gradient of potential temperature a particle
moving upwards w > 0 will have a lower temperature than the neighboring particles causing
θ < 0 to be negative, and vice-versa if the particle is moving downwards. This means that if
∂Θ
∂z > 0 then the buoyancy term in the TKE budget is negative, which physically means that
the buoyancy forces are acting to extinguish the turbulence.
The picture is reversed for a negative gradient of potential temperature which enhances the
turbulence and represents a gain term in the TKE. It has been previously shown in Table 2.1
how slight gradients in potential temperature heavily aﬀects ABL ﬂows and leads to two very
diﬀerent ﬂows. Stable ABLs are carachterised by low wind speeds with high shear and can be
as shallow as tens of meters. Unstable ABLs instead can reach few kilometers in height and are
characterized by high wind high turbulent ﬂows with eddies that spans the whole ABL depth zi.
This drastic diﬀerence in behaviour is caused solely by the sign of the BP term and therefore the
analysis of the TKE may be alone a signiﬁcant and powerful starting point to analyze ABL ﬂows.
In a neutral ABL there is one source term SP that steals kinetic energy from the mean ﬂow and
provides it to the turbulent ﬂow. This happens at the largest scales, in the energy containing
range, and activate the energy cascade i.e. the energy transfer from the large scales to the
smallest scales where the TKE will be dissipated by the action of viscous forces represented in
the TKE budget by DISS. In a stable ABL instead there are two loss terms: DISS and BP.
Clearly the diﬀerence in behaviour between neutral and stable ABL are not caused solely by
the higher value of TKE loss but rather by the diﬀerent natures of ϵ and BP.
Buoyancy forces act on the largest energy containing eddies and therefore acts directly on
the input of the energy cascade decreasing/extinguishing the turbulence.

conditions can be done analyzing the component energy budgets following [5]. In stationary
conditions, under the assumption that transport, pressure and Coriolis terms are negligible it

In the inversion layer, shown in Figure 3.5, all the terms are in the same order of magnitude and

almost completely extinguished at the ABL top zi and as a consequence turbulent transport dies

to more negative values and becomes the most important term of the TKE budget. As discussed
previously the buoyancy forces act on the largest energy containing eddies which represent the
input to the energy cascade and therefore aﬀects directly the other term of the TKE that
belongs to large eddies: SP. It can be seen that as the slope of BP increases in modulus, the
rate of decrease of SP is enhanced, and goes down to a value close to zero in zi.
Now, the TKE has two loss terms and one gain term of the same magnitude. This causes
two contemporary eﬀects. First, as the input to the energy cascade shuts down close to the
inversion the dissipation term will go to zero. But this happens after it has dissipated all the
turbulence still in the cascade when the input term shuts down, and this may cause the delay
of DISS with respect to SP. The second eﬀect of the rapid decrease in BP is that changes
the sign of the imbalance term in the TKE budget revealing both the progressive decrease of
turbulence and its time dependent behaviour

(3.25) and (3.26). This time the shear production term is inactive and stationarity cannot be
assumed, however the picture is the same as the one presented previously: due to buoyancy
eﬀects close to the inversion an intercomponent exchange of TKE is established by the pressure
transport terms.

moving up in the ABL it appears to be a three term balance between SDM, PM, TTM. It is
interesting to note that IMBM is close to zero up oscillating between negative and positive
values to 0.5. Here this term becomes strictly positive and increases with height reaching its
maximum at the ABL top zi. This is due to the buoyancy forces that extinguish the large
eddies and therefore the shear and transport terms. The pressure term decays with a vertical
delay with respect to SD and TTM and close to the inversion coincide with the imbalance term.
This means that the buoyancy forces extinguish the large eddies, which result in lower stress and
hence lower (modulus) SD term. As a consequence the ﬂow is subject to a positive acceleration
which is maximum at the inversion and gradually decreases.

The basic principles of the NCAR code and the set up of the simulations have been shown.

Chapter 5
Dimensional Analysis
Introduction
In the previous chapters the physics of the conditionally neutral ABL have been studied and
the relevant dimensional groups needed to normalize the ensemble averaged proﬁles have been
investigated. The SP terms of the TKE budgets for the diﬀerent simulations have shown similar
qualitative functional behaviours up to z/zi = 0.8 which enabled a ﬁrst non-dimensionalisation
of the proﬁles. The same applies for the other large scale term BP.
In the following chapter these observations are formalized through dimensional analysis and
lead to an expression for the heat ﬂux proﬁle wθ(z) and for the mean wind speed proﬁle S(z).
The obtained function for the mean wind speed follows the results from the LES simulations
up to z/zi = 0.8 with a maximum error within few percents as seen in the appendix.

Chapter 6
Analytic and semi-empirical
solutions
Introduction
An equation for the mean wind proﬁle for conditionally neutral boundary layer has been derived
using dimensional analysis. As it has been showed dimensional analysis even if it represents
a powerful and practical tool is subject to certain restrictions and does not own the universal
applicability typical of an analytic solution. The purpose of this chapter is to obtain an analytic
solution for the wind proﬁle for a conditionally neutral boundary layer. This is done following an
analogous reasoning to Derbyshire in [16] using the expression derived in the precedent chapter
for the heat ﬂux proﬁle and parametrising the Richardson ﬂux number.
As in Derbyshire
however, in order to obtain a solvable analytic solution a simple assumption on the stress
proﬁle has been made: T = −u2
∗(1 −z
h)(a+ib). The solution obtained through this assumption
admits only an ageostrophic angle of π/3, which is too high with respect to what observed in
the LES simulations. Also in this solution the angle at the surface and stress proﬁle is not
inﬂuenced by any of the parameters of the simulations which is in clear contrast to what shown
in the previous chapters.
With the purpose of studying in depth the stress proﬁles and to test a new ’smart guess’,
an empirical function for the stress proﬁle is derived. Using this expression a solution for the
mean speed proﬁle is obtained which is well within 5% of error within the interval of the ABL
of interest.
A ’smart guesses’ is most of the time the only way to solve non linear partial diﬀerential
equations.
Most of the time rather than being based on a sudden enlightenment they are
suggested by data and experience. This chapter shows one iteration of the process that leads
to a ’smart guess’ for the stress proﬁles: observation, modelling and testing.

Conclusions
In this thesis the conditionally neutral ABL have been studied through the analysis of data
from seventeen LES simulations. This kind of boundary layer is capped by a positive gradient
of potential temperature that induces a downward heat ﬂux, which established the ABL height
and induce stable stratiﬁcation. The heat ﬂux wθ has a linear proﬁle up to approximately
z/zi = 0.8; it starts from zero at the surface (boundary condition of the simulations) and de-
creases monotonically to its minimum close to the inversion. As a result, the buoyancy eﬀects
on the mean wind speed and turbulent quantities are highest at the inversion and minimum
close to the surface, in clear contrast with classical stable boundary layers.
As a consequence the logarithmic proﬁle is a good approximation for the mean speed proﬁle
in the near surface region up to z/zi = 0.2. This means that the simulations with the strongest
inversions and hence the lowest ABL, show signiﬁcant disagreement with the logarithmic proﬁle
already at 75m. This questions the applicability of the log-law for conditionally neutral ABLs.
