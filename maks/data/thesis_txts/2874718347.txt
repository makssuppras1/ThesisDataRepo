Abstract
Microrobots are emerging as a powerful technology in biomedical research due to their tiny
size and ability to carry out complex functions such as targeted drug delivery, microscale sens-
ing, and minimally invasive surgical manipulation. By integrating smart, stimuli-responsive
polymers and advanced fabrication methods like two-photon polymerization and 4D print-
ing, these microrobots offer unprecedented control, biocompatibility, and adaptability within
complex biological environments. Despite these advances, it remains to be determined how
to achieve robust fabrication of multi-material microstructures, ensuring reliable actuation
in physiological conditions, and optimizing biocompatibility and biodegradability to make
clinical applications feasible.
Using two-photon polymerization (2PP), scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and optical
microscopy, this thesis focuses on the designing, fabrication, and characterization of multi-
material polymer microrobots with integrated smart hydrogel components to enhance their
stimuli-responsive actuation and functional performance for biomedical microscale sensing
applications. .
The three design iterations allowed for the improvement and optimization of microrobot
fabrication, achieving the desired and expected outcomes. Based on the acutaiton result of
the hydrogel test structures fabricated using AAc and PBA enabled the study of swelling
behaviour when exposed to different chemical conditions.
In conclusion, the findings of the present study suggest that smart materials printed with
the correct size, shape and design and under certain parameters are suitable as functional
sensing elements, that can be incorporated in multi- material microrobots.

Introduction
pH exerts significant effects on protein structure, hemoglobin-mediated oxygen transport,
enzyme activity, and overall biochemical equilibrium. Thus, pH is essential for healthy bio-
logical function. A small pH range f 7.35-7.45 is essential for the optimal activity of numerous
enzymes systems. Even minor deviations from this range can result in disruption of home-
ostasis and impair metabolic processes. To maintain the reasonable range our body ought
to produce an opposite situation as a counterbalance. Tradtional materials aor fabrication
techniques are not suitable for achieving this level of control. Therefore, the use of smart
materials etc. is required. [39].
PH-responsive materials, such as hydrogels and smart polymers, use protonation and depro-
tonation to alter their mechanical behaviour, swelling, or degrading properties in response to
local pH levels. This allows for controlled deformation or payload release [64]. Acrylic acid
(AAc), which swells at higher pH values, is frequently used in hydrogel-based microrobots
[8].
The manufacturing of microrobots requires advanced technology such as 4D printing. Among
the most advanced and precise techniques is two-photon polymerization (2PP), which is a
high-resolution printing method. It fixes photosensitive liquid resin by capturing two photons
of near-infrared light, leading the resin to polymerize. Certain Microrobots are developed by
combining pH-responsive hydrogel "muscles" with structural skeletons by 4D laser printing,
which allows for exact shape change and controlled actuation [8] [43].
2PP has expanded significantly over the last 20 years, with uses spanning from the manu-
facturing of optical, drug delivery, tissue engineering, and microfluidic devices. Notably, the
area has grown beyond conventional photoresists based on acrylate and epoxy to include
specially made monomers intended to alter the hydrophilicity, surface chemistry, mechanical
characteristics, and other aspects of the subsequent formations [30].
2.1
Background and Significance of Smart Materials
Traditionally, products have been fabricated using top-down approaches where excess ma-
terial is removed from the bulk to obtain the final product. However, recent technological
advancements and the demand for advanced applications, bottom-up approaches have be-
come essential, enabling modification of the materials chemical structure at the atomic level.
To adress these requirements, new smart engineered materials have been invented such as

acrylic acid (AAc) and polybutyl acrylat (PBA). The aforementioned materials play a signif-
icant role in advanced applications of biomedical devices, sensors and actuation systems due
to their unique ability to respond to external stimuli including pH, temperature, humidity,
light and glucose. Their responsiveness enables nano- and micro level structuring, making
them highly suitable for advanced fabrication techniques. [14].
2.2
Motivation for Using 2PP and 4D Printing
Traditional fabrication techniques such as casting, forging or 3D printing are inadequate
for the precise production of complex and micro- and nano-structured products designed
to external stimuli. To overcome this limitation, the Two-Photon Polymerization method
can be used. This method enables high-resolution micro-fabrication [29]. The 2PP method
uses 4D printing to precisely fabricate nano/micro-level 3D structures using smart polymers.
It is the 4D printing which enables micro/nano structured products for stimuli responses.
4D printing is a result of 3D printing + time-dependent change. The smart polymers are
designed in a way that their molecular structure responds to stimuli. For instance, the shape
memory polymers change their shape when exposed to heat. Therefore, the 2PP method
represents the most suitable fabrication technique for producing smart products of micro-
and nano-structured products. Furthermore, the 2PP method can also be utilized for the
fabrication of products related to drug delivery, tissue scaffolds and biosensors [11].
2.3
Objectives of the Study
This study is primarily focused on the fabrication of multimaterial microrobots by using the
2PP method and using properties of acrylic acid and Phenylboronic aci as smart polymers.
Furthermore, it focuses on elucidating the stimuli-actuation mechanism and assessing the
role of smart polymers in 4D printing. This study explores the use of advanced software, i.e.,
DeScribe and NanoWrite, for designing and printing the test structure of microrobot design.
Lastly, this study demonstrates the use of characterization techniques including SEM and
OM, for characterizing microrobotic structures.

3.1
Two-Photon Polymerization (2PP)
Two-photon polymerisation (2PP) is used for creating products of complex micro and nano
structures. It is the advanced method of additive manufacturing to fabricate 3D micro and
nano structured products with higher precision than traditional 3D additive manufacturing
methods [6]. This following section emphasizes the various aspects of two-photon polymeri-
sation.
3.1.1
Principle of Two-Photon absorption
The principle for the 2PP machine is the two-photon absorption. It is a non-linear optical
phenomenon, first theorised by Maria Goppert-Mayer in 1931. According to this principle,
the molecule of the respective polymer material absorbs two photons simultaneously to get
into the excited state. This process requires photons of very high density, which can only
be achieved at the focal point of ultrafast laser beams. As this process of absorption and
excitation occurs only at the focal point, the polymerisation process is confined to a very
small 3D volume, which ultimately helps achieve ultra-high-resolution fabrication [35] [12].
This phenomenon is rare under normal illumination conditions because the probability of
this event occurring is proportional to the square of the light intensity. If the two-photon
absorption is compared with a single-photon absorption, it is quite different. A single photon
with the energy of E ≥EElectronics is directly absorbed on the surface, while two photons
require the energy of E ≈EElectronics
2
to get absorbed in the surface and excite the molecules.
In Figure 1, the two-photon absorption process is depicted. The galvo mirrors direct the
laser beam towards the resin, where the polymerisation process occurs [41] [7].

3.1.2
Photopolymerization process
The main components of the advanced 2PP system, such as Nanoscribe GT+, used in this
project, include the laser source, optical setup and focusing system and the motion control
stage [7].
A complete setup of the two-photon polymerisation system is shown figure 2.
Figure 2: Schematic illustration of a 2PP system
The basic requirement in the two-photon polymerisation method is the laser source. To
get photons of very high intensity, an Femtosecond (FS) laser source is used. FS means
femtosecond, the time in which photons hit the resin and molecules absorb these photons
and get into the excited state.
• A λ
2 wave plate controls how light interacts with subsequent optical elements in the
system. It rotates the polarization plane of the laser beam.
• The polarization beam splitter works with the wave plate to control the intensity and
the direction of the photon. It is used as an attenuator to precisely control the laser
power reaching the samples surface.
• AOM (Acousto-optic modulator) is used for precise control over exposure and pattern-
ing. It allows us to control the laser beam ultra-fast (MHz). This precise control helps

to achieve precise on/off toggling for exposure patterning and modulation during the
writing process [7].
• Stages are used to rotate the specimen in the x-y-z direction, but they work to-
gether with the galvo-scanner. The galvo-scanner moves the specimen in the x- and
y-directions precisely, while the motion control stage slowly moves the specimen in the
z-direction [15].
The optical setup and focusing system involve multiple components. The printing resolution
depends on the precision of the system. The system is responsible for directing and focusing
the laser beam to the sample [24].
Galvo-scanner: These are the motorised mirrors that reflect the laser beam in x–y direc-
tions for fast scanning and tracing out the desired pattern layer-by-layer.
Objective lens: Focuses the laser tightly into the photoresist for polymerization. This is
the most important element of the system because of its function to tightly focus the laser
beam to the smallest possible voxel. A very high-numerical-aperture (NA) immersion objec-
tive lens of NA = 1.4 is required for this purpose [24].
Immersion Oil: During the directing of the laser beam, a reflective index mismatch can
occur. To eliminate this issue, immersion oil is used between the lenses and the substrate in
the system. The elimination of the reflective index mismatch helps in preventing spherical
aberrations and ensuring optimal focus and resolution [24].
Glass substrate: Holds photoresist layer for fabrication
Photoresist: Material that polymerises at laser focus via two-photon absorption
CCD camera and Illumination System: The CCD camera and illumination system are
installed in this system for real-time monitoring of the substrate and photoresist. It is an
important element because it allows to precisely align print relative to the substrate struc-
ture [24].
Power meter: Measures laser power at the sample plane.

3.2
Smart materials
Smart materials and structures that are manufactured in four dimensions (4D) are intricately
structured mechanisms that act without any requirement for computerized control systems
by detecting environmental stimuli and adjusting to their surroundings [5]. To fabricate
microrobots for biomedical applications, smart materials are required for the 2PP additive
manufacturing method.
Material properties and structural design must be carefully taken into account when fabri-
cating bio-integrated microrobots (BIMs). Materials with biocompatibility, biodegradability,
excellent mechanical characteristics, and stimuli response capability are all essential for the
effectiveness of in vivo manipulation. The tissue mimicking capabilities of hydrogels, elas-
tomers, and native biomaterials such as collagen and chitosan are frequently used because
they help maintain cellular viability and functionality while engaging tissues in vivo in a
non-invasive manner. BIMs can incorporate dynamic features like adaptive shape-morphing
due to stimuli-responsive materials [43] .

3.2.1
General Characteristics of Smart polymers
Smart polymers are also called stimuli-responsive materials. These polymers are refered as
smart or stimuli-responsive because of their ability to change physical or chemical properties
in response to external stimuli. External stimuli can be pH, light, temperature, or even
electric fields.
Their ability to respond to external stimuli enables them to be used for
biomedical applications such as drug delivery, actuators, or soft robotics [23] [30].

Acrylic Acid (AAc)
Acrylic acid-based materials are smart polymers due to their ability to ionise depending on
the pH of the environment. This ionisation gives them unique swelling and responsiveness
properties. The reason behind pH-dependent ionisation is the carboxylic group (—COOH)
[30].
Acrylic acid CH2 = CH - COOH is a vinyl monomer. After polymerisation, it becomes a
high molecular weight polymer with the repeating unit (CH2 = CH – COOH)n [30].
Figure 4: Chemical structure of AAc
• Primary Stimulus: pH
– Low pH (Acidic): Polymer chains are neutral and collapsed due to hydrogen
bonding.
– High pH (Basic): Polymer chains become charged and swell due to electrostatic
repulsion.
– The switch occurs at its pKa (˜4.5 - 5.5), making it a perfect actuator for biological
environments [19] [10].
• Secondary Stimuli:
– Ionic Strength: High salt concentrations suppress swelling by shielding charges.
– Temperature: Can be engineered for a dual response by co polymerization.
• Core Mechanism: Reversible, pH-triggered swelling/deswelling is the fundamental
actuation principle for 4D printing and microrobotics [30].
Acrylic acid-based polymers are synthesised by the formation of interpenetrating polymer
networks (IPNs) combined with acrylamide polymer, which provides enhanced structural
strength.

3.3.1
pH-Responsive Actuation
A pH-responsive actuation means the ability of a material to change shape when the pH
of the environment changes. In polymers, this phenomenon leads to swelling and shrinkage
when the pH of the environment changes [30].
When the pH of the environment is low, the environment becomes acidic. In such an environ-
ment, the carboxylic groups remain protonated (–COOH). The hydrogen bonding between
the chains and the hydrophobic nature of the groups cause the polymer network to collapse
into a compact state. When the pH is high, the environment becomes basic. Due to which
the acid groups deprotonate, forming negatively charged carboxylate anions. The resulting
electrostatic repulsion between these similar charges forces the polymer network to expand
and absorb water, leading to significant swelling [30].
Drug Delivery: As AAc can shrink or expand depending on the pH of the environment,
this property can be used for drug delivery applications.
It can be used on a “gate” of
microcarriers. When these microcarriers enter the stomach, due to low pH, the hydrogel
remains sealed to protect the drug. When it enters the neutral intestine, due to pH close to
7, the hydrogel swells, opening the gate to release the drug [30] [33].
Sensors: AAc swells in response to a change in pH. This property can be used for sensors
in wound healing or inflammation. The relevance for this application is that the physical
swelling of the material can be transduced into a measurable signal. If the pH-responsive
hydrogel is integrated into a cantilever, on swelling, the cantilever will bend due to stresses.
This bending enables a quantitative measurement of local pH [13].
3.3.2
Glucose-Responsive Actuation
When a material or a system responds to a change in the level of glucose in the blood, it
is called Glucose-Responsive Actuation. This phenomenon is mainly observed in the smart
delivery of insulin to manage diabetes [44].
In the designing of microrobots or hydrogels, this concept is often used at certain glucose
levels because of its ability to swell [22]. PBA is a common glucose-sensing element, because
when it interacts with diol molecules like glucose, it forms reversible covalent bonds. The in-
teraction causes changes in properties of the material, such as swelling or contraction, which
allows glucose-responsive actuation [40].

Glucose-sensitive actuation works by employing an enzymatic or molecular recognition mech-
anism. Glucose sensitivity uses the Glucose Oxidase (GOX) enzyme, which oxidises the
oxidation of glucose to gluconic acid. This oxidation results in lowering the pH. This change
in pH causes swelling or deswelling in the glucose-responsive material like PBA, leading to-
wards glucose-mediated actuation [3].
This is the reason why glucose-responsive materials is ideal for biomedical application, be-
cause of its specificity in the presence of glucose, meaning they can work without any external
power. The swelling or contraction in microrobots can be translated into measurable values,
making it suitable for biosensing [22] [44]. In the world of biomedicine, the glucose-responsive
actuation is used in smart insulin delivery systems. A glucose-responsive polymer is fabri-
cated with the miniaturised device and insulin. When the glucose level changes, the polymer
material shrinks or swells, allowing miniaturised devices to perform with great efficiency [44].
3.3.3
Integration of Multiple Stimuli in Smart materials
In smart systems, multiple stimuli-responsive materials can be used in a single product for
different environments [46]. For instance:
Sequential Operation: The smart polymers can be used in a product for sequential op-
erations. For example, a microrobot can use one stimulus for navigation while the other for
the action [42].
Logic-Gated Actuation: This application of multiple stimuli-responsive materials can
lead towards specificity and reduce the off-target effects. It is done by using multiple stimuli-
responsive materials, which can respond to changing pH or temperatures [26].
Enhanced Feedback: Multiple stimuli in smart systems can be used for precise thera-
peutic inventions. The use of multiple stimulus-responsive materials allows a more robust
diagnostic picture than a single stimulus-responsive material product [27] [31].

Microrobots must be precisely characterized in order to confirm their robustness, functional

3.5.1
Optical microscopy
Optical microscopy is one of the oldest techniques to magnify tiny objects. It has a magnifi-
cation range between 20× and 2000×. It is easy to operate and widely used in the research
facilities worldwide. It uses the light to magnify the tiny objects. A light falls on the sample
by passing through the condenser lens. Then, the objective lens will collect the light from
the sample and create a magnified image with different magnifications. Magnification and
resolution of optical microscopy depend on the wavelength of light and the numerical aper-
ture (NA) of the objective lens [34].
The key advantages and limitations of optical microscopy are discussed below:
Advantages: It is easy to operate, affordable, and provides results immediately. It allows
viewing samples in a magnified size without the need for extra preparation of the sample. It
can study living cells and biological processes in real-time. It is portable and easily used in
most laboratories.
Limitations: Its magnification and resolution are less than those of the electron microscope.
It is unable to focus on thick specimens at high magnification. It cannot provide very fine
structural details like SEM or TEM. Image quality relies heavily on the type and intensity
of illumination [28].

4.1
Design process
The design phase is fundamental for the creation of multi-material polymer microrobots for
use in microscale sensing. Several concepts, limitations, and requirements were obtained
from previous work on microrobots, including research group meetings and ongoing related
work. The functionality of the microrobots were defined by several factors, such as shape,
size, visualisation, structural integrity, and responsiveness of the material. To obtain the
best desired design, several iterations were utilised for improvement to optimise and modify
the design. This sections highlights the progressively refining of the design through several
iterations, each iteration focusing on one or several factors affecting the functionality of the
microrobots.
4.1.1
First design iteration - Testing
The first design iteration was designed with inspiration of previous designed microrobots, to
decide how many sensing elements to incorporate in the next design and to solely test the
functionality of the design and parameters.
A triangle shaped design was created with three spheres and three connecting tubes, with a
sensing element in the middle. One of the goals of this iteration was to find out the stability
of the spheres and connecting tubes when printing with IP-L backbone material. The size
and shape of the spheres and connecting tube was examined for the purpose of generating
ideas for the next designs. The second goal of the design was to test different factors that
can influence the print quality of the printed microstructures. Four different prints with
the laser power set to 20, 25, 40 and 70 mW was conducted to test what the best suitable
parameters when printing with IP-L.

4.1.2
Second design iteration - IP-L Printing
In the second iteration, designs with four spheres were explored to incorporate 2 × 2 sensing
elements. Three different designs, each with four spheres, was created and printed from
IP-L material to test the stability of the design compared to three spheres, both with and
without counter mode. Each design were printed in a 3×3 array on the same substrate. The

were characterised using optical microscopy. A more detailed analysis of the print quality
and geometric properties was conducted using SEM. The acquired images were analysed
and used to determine different failure modes. The subjective assessments obtained from
the analysis were used to determine any improvements or changes to future iterations.

After the second iteration, the properties of hydrogel sensing elements were investigated.

4.1.4
Third design iteration - Optimized design
The results obtained from the initial test structures revealed several limitations that required
modifications to the sensing element design. The results revealed that the sensing elements
scaled up to 10 µm was the most suitable for actuation testing, considering the size limi-

and reproducibility.
However, increasing the size of the sensing elements introduced new spatial constraints.

total robot designs were refined and simplified alongside the fewer sensing elements. The
concept of four spheres was based on the 2×2 sensing array at that time, and as size increase
of the sensing elements had to be incorporated to the original design, different alternatives
were explored. Three distinct designs were selected for evaluation:
• A triangle design with three spheres, adapted from previous concepts.
• A design with four spheres, carried over from the original plan.
• A newly developed four-sphere design, specifically modified to accommodate the up-

These design were selected to determine which microrobot offers the highest print quality
when combining various materials.

5.1
General Design Requirements
To design and fabricate the best suitable microrobots for biomedical applications, a set
of requirements are needed to ensure reliable and well-functional microrobots.
Most of
the requirements were defined during the research phase or obtained from previous work.
Depending on the outcome and the findings of the iterations, the requirements are adjusted
to meet the objective of this project. The most important and essential requirements are
specified below:
• Core Functional and Structural Requirements:
– Mechanical integrity: The microrobot must retain its structural stability dur-
ing operation, with the IP-L backbone providing sufficient rigidity to withstand
fluidic currents, mechanical interactions, and external stresses.
– Hydrogel sensitivity: Hydrogel sensing elements must show predictable, re-
versible, and repeatable volumetric changes in response to physiological or ex-
perimental levels. The response time must be sufficiently fast for the intended
application.
– Sensing structures: Responsive features should remain small, as reduced di-
mensions enhance size-dependent responsiveness. Designs should also allow qual-
itative, visually detectable changes.
• Material and Biocompatibility Requirements:
– Material compatibility: IP-L and hydrogel must bond effectively without de-
lamination or interface failure.
– Biocompatibility: Only non-toxic, biomedically safe materials should be used
for applications such as biosensing.
• Fabrication and Printing Requirements
Page 28 of 46
==PAGE:36==
4D printing of multimaterial polymer microrobots
– High resolution and feature size: 2PP must achieve fine features, with mini-
mum feature sizes not smaller than 2.0 µm. Printing parameters should be indi-
vidually optimized to ensure precision.
– Overlap limitations: Structural designs must minimize unsupported overhangs
to prevent deformation during printing. Materials should overlap sufficiently to
provide mechanical stability without overexposure.
• Performance and Design Considerations
– Optical trapping limitations: Microrobot designs should incorporate 8–12 µm
trapping spheres, with a minimum of three spheres included to enable multidirec-
tional motion.
– Scalability and batch size: The production process should allow consistent
fabrication of multiple identical units, maximizing yield per batch.
– Process efficiency: Design and printing steps, including alignment, should be
simplified to enhance throughput for multimaterial microrobots.
– Design space adherence: Final structures should remain within the predefined
design space established in prior work.

As seen in figure 11 the spheres, tubes and sensing elements displayed visible fabrication ar-
tifacts and surface roughness. The spheres displayed distinct print lines and micro-texture,
which shows the voxel-by-voxel nature of 2PP in microfabrication process. The tubes showed
non-unformity along their length, rather than the smoothness that might be expected with
optimal fabrication. This could be a result from the slicing and layer accumulation. As
IP-L enables intricate feature definition, the process setup and selected parameter can affect
the surface quality, specially at the edges. By adjusting the slicing and hatching parame-
ters, the 2PP fabrication changes how each structure is formed voxel-by-voxel. As slicing
distance control the vertical separation between the layers, smaller slicing distance lead to
more densely packed vertical voxels, which produces smoother surfaces, but extends the
printing time. The hatching distance sets the horizontal separation between the scan lines
within a layer. With smaller hatching distance, the voxels overlap more by filling the gaps,
but also extends the printing time.
For the sensing elements, the squares and triangles connected at the midpoints of the tubes,
there appears to be a larger fabrication error. The alignment of the surfaces is inconsistent.
The start of each small section is positioned at a slightly different height, creating a staircase-
like effect along the element.
While the edges remain well defined, the surface of each
individual section appear smoother than those of the spheres and tubes. One explanation
for the smoother surface could be the geometry of the design, when comparing the flat surface
of the square and triangle with the spheres and tubes.

6
Conclusion
This master thesis explored the design, fabrication and characterization of multimaterial mi-
crorobots using IP-L photopolymer and two-photon polymerization. Through a structured,
multiphase design process, different microrobot concepts and hydrogel based test structures
were conceived and iteratively refined based on experimental feedback and fabrication suc-
cess. The workflow in this project emphasized the importance of progressive optimization
for both design and process parameters in micro scale fabrication.
Starting with three promising designs, the final model was obtained after three design it-
erations. The 2PP fabrication parameters was systematically optimized, during multiple
studies to explore the influence of laser power and scan speed on the structure fidelity and
geometric stability. Careful attention to design choices was essential throughout the 2PP
printing process. Strategically shaping and dimensioning critical features, proved funda-
mental for successful fabrication. Each design cycle contributed valuable insights, enabling
process refinements and optimization of both design and fabrication parameters
The characterization of the fabricated structures, using both optical and electron microscopy,
demonstrated reliable print quality across multiple shapes and designs, great geometric preci-
sion and repeatable dimensional fidelity when printing within well defined parameter ranges.
Extensive parameter screening led to the findings of optimal fabrication conditions, with
laser power of 90 - 100 and scan speeds between 8000 and 10000, when printing with acrylic
acid, enabling consistent microstructure production while balancing surface quality and flex-
ible and mechanical stability.
Further the hydrogel test structures fabricated using AAc and PBA enabled the study of
swelling behaviour when exposed to different chemical conditions. These experiments demon-
strated that smart materials printed with the correct size, shape and design and under certain
parameters are suitable as functional sensing elements, that can be incorporated in multi-
material microrobots.
Future experiments should build upon the constraints and findings in this project, focusing
on identifying specific fabrication parameters for both AAc and PBA hydrogels. Based on
the work done in this project, fabrication of microrobots combining multiple materials could
be achieved.
