Executive Summary. 
 
The health industry is a peculiar business line. The development of new products is conducted 
within a harsher regulatory and social environment than other areas, due the sensitive & risky 
consequences the products may have over their user-base’s wellbeing. Therefore, is no 
strange that most of the major industry actors have enabled systems of production that ensure 
the product viability, most of the time under greatly public scrutiny. However, R&D in the 
pharma industry remains a game of uncertainty, a chance call into a roulette game, expecting 
hitting the right option before losing all the chips. 
 
Is remarkable that, considering the amount of pharma products developed during the last 70 
years, the market is still very dynamic (B. Munos, 2009). However, the evolution the area 
has undergone is by no means small. According to the FDA, only in 2016, they received 
around “36 NME applications; [while the] average NME filings for past decade is 
35”(Jenkins, 2016). Overall, the FDA (arguably, the most important regulatory agency in the 
world, given the market relevance of the USA) has, as of 2016, 6741 commercial NME with 
activity (Jenkins, 2016).  
 
Why then, most of the specialists are warning on the increasing difficulties of innovating in 
this sector? There are plenty of explanations, ranging from the current research & 
development model costs (DiMasi, Hansen, & Grabowski, 2003; Grootendorst, Hollis, 
Levine, Pogge, & Edwards, 2011; Jayadev & Stiglitz, 2009; Saadi & White, 2014), to a 
reduced focus on specific therapeutic areas (Berggren, Møller, Moss, Poda, & Smietana, 
2012; J A Dimasi, 2001; Jakobsen, Wang, & Nwaka, 2011), increasing regulatory 
requirements & red tape (Bergsland, Elle, & Fosse, 2014; Kola, 2008), decreasing ROI and 
patenting issues (Bujak, Geilinger, & Leo, 2016; Grootendorst et al., 2011; Saadi & White, 
2014), up to concentrated technologies and reduced/limited know-how (J A Dimasi, 2001; 
Lendrem & Lendrem, 2014).  
 
Though, one thing is true: the pipeline is getting smaller (Berggren et al., 2012; Pammolli, 
Magazzini, & Riccaboni, 2011) whilst the major actors are constantly draining their capital 
resources (Kaitin, 2010); with it, the chances to increase the quality of life of an increasing 
eldering population are getting slimmer, even if they will surely require new types of medical 
care for which we, as a society, are not prepared to provide (Goldman, 2005). 
 
The present work is looking to review the current innovation environment in the global health 
industry, incorporating it through a Schumpeterian approximation to understand how the 
market innovation has been driven, as described by Malerba & Orsenigo (2015). After 
stablishing a general scenario of the industry, the intent will move towards characterizing the 
biological submarket (arguably the driving force in the industry) using a perspective of 
evolutionary economics and learning why and how is innovative still. The third section 
centers on providing specific cases and showing how successful companies have 
approximated to develop products, focusing in the Danish experience. Finally, the work 
provides general recommendations and innovation patterns suggestions based on the cases 
explored and the literature research.

Reading Guidance  
 
A brief reading guidance is offered as reference to the content of this work. Chapter I 
Product Development process starts by giving definitions that will be useful throughout the 
thesis. It later goes to discuss the current state of the innovation model in the pharmaceutic 
industry; it also provides context for the main stakeholder’s stances. The reader then will be 
able to identify the major variables involved in the drug discovery and development, and will 
be able to relate the problematic inside the R&D at the industry. 
Then, on Chapter II Schumpeterian and Evolutionary Economics, the author moves into 
describe, using the evolutionary theory, why the innovation is at the center of economic 
development of the pharma industry. The author will intent to characterize the biological 
submarket in terms of Schumpeterian innovation patterns. This will serve to verify the 
assumption that there is a “biological revolution” founded on empirical evidence from patent 
review and other sources. Once that characterization is asserted, the work will move into 
Chapter III Cases review, where the author provide examples, based on the Danish local 
industry, of two success cases and one outsider perspective that can highlight best practices, 
which can be later generalized for more companies. Further insights and general 
recommendations based on both, the literature research and the empirical cases will be 
presented in Chapter IV Proposals, along with general conclusions of the present work.

Working Hypothesis:  
 
One model has dominated the drug production, described by Kaitin (2010) and Kola (2008). 
 
However, this model was useful for the small molecule NME product is starting to need 
adjustments, showing that it is not one fit all solution (Malerba & Orsenigo, 2015). 
 
This industry reached an innovation-peak around the year 2000 (Jenkins, 2016), and since it 
can be considered it a Mark II industry, with the development curve in decline, and the 
consequential paradigm in the market since. In Evolutionary economics terminology, there 
is still an adaptation process of the innovation paths in the industry, but the predominant 
actors have developed mainly through an incremental innovation process, preventing new 
actors to enter the market. 
 
However, a second wave of innovation should be differentiated, based upon the biological 
products (NTBP) development were developed as early as 1976, but that started to take force 
around the same time the small-molecules innovation started its decline; this submarket could 
be characterized still as having a Mark I innovation pattern. There might be evidence that 
shows a higher success rate in the R&D of biological NMEs, specially in some therapeutic 
lines (e.g. orphan drugs and oncology). 
 
The problem arises when the “roulette-model” - meaning the model used to develop and 
select small molecules (NMEs)- is replicated for NTBPs discoveries: the vices are kept. It is 
a misunderstood paradigm change that might be restraining the potential offered by the 
biological revolution.

Chapter I Product Development process 
 
Arguably, there are 3 main industrial sectors in the world that satisfy the most basic needs of 
the population: energy, food and health industries. From them, the food industry is probably 
the one with the greatest number of products and possibilities in terms of diversity, but also, 
complexity variables in a global market. The energy sector is dominated by a major player 
product, with few alternatives that, nonetheless, will become more and more relevant due the 
technological shifts and environmental concerns.  
In a broad sense, health, is a very strange market. When focusing on the pharmaceutical 
market, it can be appreciated its wide variety of products, like the food industry, but most of 
them require an enormous amount of investment, whilst a majority of potential innovations 
will likely never reach the consumer or have a lasting impact on the sector. From those 
fortunate enough to reach the market and have a significant impact on it, chances are that 
will be often marketed aggressively, and will be presented as the new revolution on its 
therapeutic area, namely a “blockbuster” (B. Munos, 2009), when most of them are not. 
 
1.1 
Introduction 
Of course, in general terms, the development model is the same one imposed by the health 
industry since after the WWII. Since then, the history of medical innovation, embedded in 
ethic, economic and social considerations, should not be taken from granted when discussing 
innovation strategy for this industry. On his recent article depicting the history of the industry 
from an evolutionary economics perspective, Malerba & Orsenigo, (2015) characterize the 
drug discovery strategy in the aftermaths of the war time as a ‘random screening’, where 
“little a priori knowledge of what could work and why”. Interestingly, they also asserted that 
such a model could be “… usefully represented as a lottery”. The model was refined as the 
decades passed, trying to rationalize the search for medications, but it remains a gamble 
embedded on high uncertainty. 
Therefore, is amazing how scarce is the current research on the adequacy of the entire 
innovation model, with notable exceptions (among others Grootendorst et al., 2011; Khanna, 
2012; Malerba & Orsenigo, 2015; Moors, Cohen, & Schellekens, 2014; Paul et al., 2010; 
Schuhmacher, Gassmann, & Hinder, 2016); their research, however, mostly focus on analyze 
the state of the art, propose ways to keep in control the soaring costs or advise on regulatory 
frameworks that facilitate the process. While Kaitin (2010), Moors et al. (2014) and Paul et 
al. (2010) do offer more comprehensive approaches on specific steps toward a sustainable 
model, their reach or depth on specific proposals is somewhat limited. 
The likeliness is that such a major shift onto model reformation is nowhere near to be seen; 
after all, this depends in part on having relevant actors who perceive a gain from any potential 
change, and then a coordinated effort to achieve it. Hence, the status quo remains in good 
shape, with changes made at an internal level (Schuhmacher et al., 2016). It is fair to ask if 
the current process is causing a systemic stagnation (or worst, a loss) of innovation on the 
sector, especially when there is evidence of a technological change that could have the sector 
process (namely, biotechnologically sourced products). 
 
The aim of the present work will be to present a mere glimpse to a relevant questioning, 
asking if the path used over 70 years should be deeply amended based empirical evidence.

1.2 
Definitions 
It becomes important to define the product types and terminology that will be dealt with in 
this work. The author decided to use FDA terminology since the USA is the biggest market 
for innovations and the major source of new products.  
First, is important to differentiate between two major drug sub-sets, small-molecules and 
biologics. Morrow & Felcone (2004) offered several interpretations of both terms, but based 
on their definitions, they should be understood as follows: 
A small molecule (SMD) is a compound chemically synthesized, usually not big in size. The 
FDA also denotes that these products “have a well-defined structure and can be thoroughly 
characterized” (Food and Drug Administration [FDA], 2015a).  
On the contrary, Therapeutic Biological Products (TBP), usually are protein-based 
products which “only living systems can produce […] and [...] are relatively large molecules” 
(Morrow & Felcone, 2004). A size comparison can be appreciated on Figure 1. Additional 
differences can be appreciated at Appendix 1. 
In technical terms, they usually are polypeptide macromolecules that are not obtained 
polymerization (Samanen, 2013). A non-comprehensive list of TBPs include hormones 
(growth hormone, parathyroid hormone, insulin), interferons, interleukins, growth factors, 
monoclonal antibodies (MAbs), vaccines, immunomodulators, blood, blood components, 
plasma derived products (e.g. albumin, immunoglobulins, clotting factors, fibrin sealants, 
proteinase inhibitors, etc.), gene therapy products, etc.

Amgen Inc. (2017)

The TBPs meet the definition of drugs described in the Federal Food, Drug and Cosmetic 
Act (FDC Act). However, most of them1 are also regulated under the “Public Health Service 
(PHS) Act [that] defines a biological product as a ‘virus, therapeutic serum, toxin, antitoxin, 
vaccine, blood, blood component or derivative, allergenic product, or analogous product, … 
applicable to the prevention, treatment, or cure of a disease or condition of human beings’” 
(Food and Drug Administration (FDA), 2015a). The main difference that this creates is the 
need of requesting a biologics license application (BLA) instead of a new drug application 
(NDA) for those products regulated under the PHS Act. 
 
1.3 
New Molecular entity and New Therapeutic Biological Product. 
This work will make use of the definition of New Molecular entity (NME) used by the FDA 
to refer new drugs where a new drug application (NDA) is the first submitted for its kind 
(either for the indication or by its formulation) (Samanen, 2013). Munos (2009) defined a 
NME as “A medication containing an active ingredient that has not been previously approved 
for marketing in any form in the United States”. Munos also mentions that the term NME is 
usually associated only to small-molecules. The author will follow that association, unless 
otherwise is explicitly stated in the text. For the biologics, the author will favor the use of the 
term New Therapeutic Biological Product (NTBP) over the one of new biological entity 
(NBE), since that could create confusion over the text.

1.5 
Industry Current state. 
Now that the author has commented on the innovation development model and the definitions 
to be used on this work, the author intends to frame the state of the art in the drug industry, 
which will provide a work frame to identify its relevant actors and problems. 
One characteristic challenge of the drug innovation model is, evidently, that in order to move 
forward onto the next stage, it requires the completion of the previous milestone. Usually, in 
other industries a failure on meeting the expectative can be managed using several planning 
tools and management systems (e.g. Six-Sigma, lean, etc.).  
However, there critical sections on the health product development are dependent on several 
outcomes that are linked to the very nature of each molecule. A non-comprehensive list of 
these key factors includes its discovery, safety (toxicity, associate adverse reactions, etc.) and 
efficacy (therapeutic dosage, targeted population, indication) in preclinical and clinical 
phases, but also the research company’s long term strategy.  
Reviewing the “rate of success” (Paul et al., 2010) for each of the model stages, it can be 
appreciated that is “…51 % for discovery research, 69 % for preclinical development, 12.8 
% for the clinical development phases and 91 % for the submission phase, resulting in an 
overall probability of technical and regulatory success (PTRS) for drug R&D of 4.1 %” 
Figure 5 The drug development process timespan for each stage. Source: Wikibooks (2014) 
==PAGE:15==
 
14 
   
(Schuhmacher et al., 2016). What show is that the critical attrition occurs on the clinical 
phases, with high problems as well on the discovery research phase. 
 
Nonetheless, the success rate is astonishingly low in productivity terms, especially 
considering the amount of investment that large corporations pour into R&D efforts, and 
which can be as high as “US $1 billion dollars ($0.8–1.8 billions) when adjusted for post-
approval Phase IV expenses and costs linked to approval in non-US markets” (Munos, 2009; 
cited in Khanna, 2012 – this data has not been adjusted to 2017 prices). Several explanations 
have been raised on why is the case, including accounting the “…intrinsic difficulty in 
discovering new drugs for increasingly complex pathologies” and that  “…the low hanging 
fruits have already been picked and now the challenge [of discovering new products] 
becomes harder” (Malerba & Orsenigo, 2015).  
To make the scenario even worst, timeframes to get the products to market can be as high as 
14 years (Paul et al., 2010), but evidence shows that there is a visible increment on the 
clinical-phase completion timelines (Schuhmacher et al., 2016), since the average of 6.4 
years for drugs approved between 2005-2009, increased to 9.1 years for the approved ones 
in the period 2008-2012. A similar trend was seen for the “interval durations in preclinical 
development (+17 %, 2004–2012)” (Schuhmacher et al., 2016). The same authors also 
mention a triple effect due this process increment: reduction on the patent-protected life of 
the product, a rising in the development costs and a decrease in the market-share potential 
due increasing possibility of similar-molecules’ competition. Available data cited by the 
same paper by Schuhmacher et al. refer that most of the failures in product development for 
clinical phases II and III in the year 2012 were related to “efficacy (56 %), safety issues (28 
%)”. Since these 2 phases are well into the half of the R&D process, the ROI of every attempt 
is decreased and risked by each failed product.  
Kaitin (2010) warned that the current financing model of R&D based companies depended 
deeply on the revenue of several major “blockbuster” products (B. Munos, 2009)- namely, 
products with revenue sales exceeding 1000 million USD annually-, some of which would 
lose their patent in the period 2010-2012- most notably “Lipitor” (atorvastatin) and “Plavix” 
(clopidogrel), with limited products able to replace the revenue loss.

Innovation models  
With that in mind, is difficult to take a radically innovative approach, and the safe move is 
sometimes promoting early research, either by externalize, sponsor or acquiring it. The 
industry actually has encouraged several of these innovation models, and Schuhmacher,

Figure 7), depending on its proportion of externally sourced innovation acquisition and the

amount of R&D development management that those companies carry out to deliver that

• Knowledge creator: characterized by a preference of internal innovation management

• Knowledge integrator: this model reflects the preference of using externally

• Knowledge leverage: This type of model acquires drug candidates, technologies and

internal and external resources.

Consolidation and concentration of the sector 
As seen on the previous section, the innovation model of most companies requires acquisition 
of technology in some degree. That acquisition can be done by the purchase of a specific

technology, patent or product licensing. However, consolidation is also a crucial activity on 
the market. 
Consolidation, meaning the acquisition & merger of players, is one of the most common 
ways of acquisition of technology that companies deem necessary to potentiate their 
development. For example, a recent exchange between GSK and Novartis included the 
handover of the vaccine branch from Novartis for the cancer development division at GSK 
(Glaxo Smith Kline plc., 2015). Both companies launched a consumption venture together 
as well. What becomes interesting of this process is that big pharma is focusing more and 
more on specific therapeutic areas, and abandon those where they do not foreseeable near 
future profit or where they perceive a poorer performance against competition.  
 
And the stakes are really being concentrating on biological-R&D companies. Samanen  
(2013) enlist the most recent big acquisitions made by big pharma. The four top are fairly 
recent and eclipse most of the rest, starting by the 2009’s landmark acquisition of Genentech 
(the preeminent and first biotech firm) by Roche, and the Schering-Plough purchase by 
Merck. Both operation ranked almost at the same level of about $40,000 million USD. The 
following operation, the acquisition of Wyeth by Pfizer in the same year, for a cost of up 
$68,000 million USD. Finally, the 2013’s Genzyme purchase by Sanofi-Aventis for over 
$20,100 million USD show how far companies are willing to spend to ensure access to 
technology. 
 
All of the latter mentioned operations derail in comparison with the 2016’s averted merger 
of Pfizer and Allergan for up to $160,000 million USD, who failed to pass the regulator test. 
If happened, the new emergent company would have been a behemoth of the industry, while 
not ensuring Pfizer many pipeline or synergy gains (which could have been gained by the 
Wyeth’s operation), but the possibility of domiciling its revenue partly in Ireland, which 
could save the pharma-giant up to $1,000 million in tax cuts (Humer & Banerjee, 2016). 
Pfizer, as the research on this work will show, has been outpaced on R&D development 
recently. 
 
Smaller pharma has a different set of strategies regarding consolidation. Usually, they are the 
companies being acquired, and therefore several companies are eager to demonstrate their 
added value to become attractive to bigger players or to prevent smaller enters to their field, 
in a blue-ocean market strategy described by Kim & Mauborgne (2015).  
And this is by no means a small market. HBM partners, a Swiss health-investing firm

further prevented the establishment of monopolistic positions in the industry” (Malerba & 
Orsenigo, 2015). The concentration ratio CR4 index  (meaning the % of market share of the 
largest four firms in the global industry) “was equal to 28% in 1947, 24% in 1967 and 22%

1.6.2.1 
What is a regulatory agency and what is its role? 
During the review of the literature for this work, the author did not found a more concise and 
simple description of the role of an actor than this one for the regulatory entities: “the main 
goal of state intervention has always been to guarantee the access to safe and efficacious 
drugs by the largest possible share of the population” (Malerba & Orsenigo, 2015). However, 
the implications of such assertions are not easy to grab. For example, what does it mean that 
drugs are safety? Or at which safety level? And what about efficacy? how is clinically 
defined? 
This is a shared problem by major world regulatory entities. As leading EMA’s officers 
asserted about its responsibility: “[our] role is to safeguard public health by being 
gatekeepers—by ensuring the safety and efficacy of drugs to be licensed—and, at the same 
time, to contribute to public health by being enablers” (Ehmann et al., 2013). Though is a 
very politically savvy statement, it is also revealing that they want to enable innovation.  
 
Is just convenient to define some general health-authority competencies, which evidently are 
quite different from country to country. In general, a regulatory agency is a branch of the 
government in charge of providing structure to the legal framework (either enacted by the 
legislative or by executive branches) but also to enforce it. Beyond these very broad 
definitions, there are plenty of different approximations, even between the two entities to be 
focused on this work: FDA and EMA. Hence, let us discuss very briefly both entities: 
• FDA: The Food and Drug Administration is an agency within the U.S. Department 
of Health and Human Services, a branch of the executive power of the US federal 
government. Born in 1906, it was until 1938 with the signature of the Food, Drug, 
and Cosmetic Act that was mandated to require companies for drug's safety before it 
could go on the market. The approval deadline for the request was 2 months. (Swan, 
2013). Nowadays, the FDA reviews and grant drug market approvals through mainly 
one department, Center for Drugs Evaluation and Research (CDER), though some 
==PAGE:22==
 
21 
   
limited cases are submitted to the Center for Biologics Evaluation and Research 
(CBER)2(Samanen, 2013). Its legal enforcements are binding, and most are contained 
either on the US pharmacopeia, the Code of Federal Regulations (CFR) Title 21, Parts 
1-1499; or on Guidance Practices & Documents issued by the agency (McGee 
Pharma International, 2016). 
• EMA: In contrast, the European Medicines Agency is relatively new entity. Born in 
1995, is a decentralized and independent agency headed by an Executive Director and 
a Management board of 36 members. EMA claims to be a network agency, and as a 
supranational entity, does not links to a specific authority (EMA, 2015). It does not 
grant market approval but rather advises the European Commission (EC), upon its 
scientific review, if the drug candidate should be approved. Once the EC issue its 
approval, the “the centralised marketing authorisation is valid in all European Union 
(EU) Member States, Iceland, Norway and Liechtenstein” (EMA, 2017). Is 
noteworthy that the EMA does not oversees not mandate over the national agencies 
of the European Economic Area (not only the EU), and is aimed to work with them 
to manage the market. The legal basis for that management is enclosed on a set of 
Regulations of mandatory fulfillment on all member state; Directives, that lay down 
outcomes that must be achieved, and for which each member state may interpret when 
transposing into national laws; and rules, such as Eudralex, Volume IV (McGee 
Pharma International, 2016). 
 
So, in summary, the regulatory schemes and operations are not standardized, though they 
tend to be more over the time, the adaptation process is not easy or fast. Some agencies tend 
to have harder requirements than others, and some have high standards. And it seems that 
having a hard-regulatory enforcement focused on risk management is not always perceived 
as a problem; for example, Munos (2009) claims that “more demanding regulatory apparatus, 
such as the United States and the UK, have fostered a more innovative and competitive 
pharmaceutical industry”.  
The rationale behind it being that is that “force companies to be more selective in the 
compounds that they aim to bring to market” while “stimulate R&D investment and promote 
the emergence of an industry that is research intensive, innovative, dominated by few 
companies and profitable” (B. Munos, 2009). This view is also supported by Malerba & 
Orsenigo (2015), who comment that “stringent drug approval process in the US […] 
significantly increased barriers to imitation, even after patents expiry”. Meanwhile, 
“submission of risk- minimization action plans in the United States have surged by 75%, as 
has the number of risk evaluation and mitigation strategies subsequently approved” (Kaitin, 
2010). 
However, Moors et al. (2014) provides a contrast view since “there is evidence that the 
introduction of regulatory demands has led to a marked decline in new drugs, [and] there are 
no data showing whether regulation has led to better drugs both in the USA and Europe”. 
And there is also the problem of the drug efficacy and its evaluation. Moors et al. (2014)

1.6.2.2 
Reimbursement and payers schemes 
Undoubtedly, a key issue nowadays is how the drug pricing is devised. This is also a major 
issue, since the ROI of most innovations need to be high, so investors have a certain assurance 
that they will see resources to cover the hefty development bills and still render attractive 
gains. Contrastingly, the user population entrust the authorities with the contradictory 
responsibility of approve safety, innovative & affordable products.  
 
Malerba & Orsenigo, (2015) comment that countries where “national health service exists or 
when in any case there is a third payer (typically, an insurer), demand elasticity to price tends 
to be lower than it would otherwise have been”, and its because “governments may act as 
monopsony and through various instruments tend to reduce drug prices”.  
 
The role of private care providers and insurers, managed care organizations (MCOs) and 
pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs) and government programs (like Medicaid and Medicare) 
in the US bear the equivalent responsibility exerted in other countries by national public 
entities, but without a similar leverage & negotiating power nor the limits on the cost existent 
in almost the rest of the markets. Evidently, the US market present cost schemes that are 
significantly higher from the rest of the world. 
 
Academic authorities, like Jayadev & Stiglitz (2009) have proposed ways to cope with 
increasing costs of medicines by the massive support R&D public funding of new drugs, but 
also by promoting that governments should evaluate the pricing of products based on their 
value-benefit and efficacy results. The authors provide the example of UK’s Office of Fair 
Trading, whom base its assessments on the “marginal clinical benefit of additional drugs 
through the impact on quality-adjusted life-years (QALYs)”. The proposal was received 
rather skeptically (Huttin & Lopes, 2009), but the important point is that there is need to 
==PAGE:25==
 
24 
   
generate feasible alternatives for the cost issue. And this is not news, since nowadays most 
“pharmaceutical companies are partly ‘subsidised’ through publicly funded research” 
(Malerba & Orsenigo, 2015). 
 
1.6.2.3 
IPP environment 
The perfect way to deter competitors is through publicly enforced protection. This concept 
is not new, but the mechanism to the modern version of intellectual property started in Europe 
in the mid 16th century. By late 19th century, synthetically-produced drugs were patented 
widely by German and UK manufacturers, and licensed for use and production in the US and 
elsewhere. However, for biological drugs is until 1946, with the US Patent Office grant of a 
patent for streptomycin that the previous unpatentability of natural sourced medicines was 
overturned, and henceforth drug remedies expended its potential commercial sourcing. Since 
then, “patent regime in pharmaceuticals has become increasingly tight”, culminating with the 
1994 adoption of the Agreement on Trade-Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights 
(TRIPS) for the participant countries in the World Trade Organization (WTO) (Malerba & 
Orsenigo, 2015). Nowadays, the system provides “patents covering the discovery process, 
the products themselves, and their production, formulation, delivery and indications” (Moors 
et al., 2014). 
There are controversies on the patent use for pharma. The two most known arguments are 
that patents hurt drug innovation potential. Moors et al., (2014) support that idea since for 
them, patents “reward incremental low-risk innovation more than high-risk ground-breaking 
innovative research” while companies tend to base its IP strategy on making patents that 
block the entrance to innovative competitors.  
Malerba & Orsenigo (2015) provide an inconclusive support to this argument, since they 
mention that the IP laws passed in the 1960 decade in Japan, Italy and France without a 
product protection (only protecting production processes) had a severe impact, discouraging 
the local companies to innovate; however, no impact whatsoever on the R&D output was 
seen on Germany, Switzerland, Sweden who had similar laws. The same authors say that 
further reforms to patent laws have not improved the innovative capabilities of Italian and 
Japanese companies, rather the opposite.  
 
On the other side, the second argument, as explained by Dimasi & Grabowski, (2004) is that 
patents make drug’s “price will be set above marginal cost (the price that would prevail in a 
perfectly competitive market [i.e., a scenario with no patent protection]) and, as a result, the 
quantity consumed will be below that which would result under perfect competition”. Both 
authors offered rejections of the argument based on the need to offer innovators with means 
to compensate their work, though both welcomed the idea of proposals including prizes and 
R&D partnerships as alternatives to a pure patent-based IP system. 
 
In strict sense, patents are the most global IP form used for pharmaceuticals. However, they 
are not the only one and other forms are also important, but the focus will be on one: 
exclusivity terms granted by government agencies. Exclusivity terms are granted by countries 
in the light of specific developments on certain areas, usually underdeveloped or loosely 
researched and in need of therapeutic solutions (i.e. some oncologic, infectious and rare 
diseases).  
==PAGE:26==
 
25 
   
Grabowski, DiMasi, & Long (2015) conducted a second review and concluded a similar 
stance as in their 2004 paper, saying that “patents and regulatory exclusivity provisions are 
likely to remain the core approach to providing incentives for biopharmaceutical research 
and development”, while aiming to use prizes and other motivators to help with the still 
unmet health needs. 
 
1.6.3 
Society and users 
Meanwhile, the costs of drug treatments for patients continue to soar, specially but not only 
in the US, where it does not exist a regulatory overview of the drug-costs, relying on the 
market power of exerted by the insurance companies. But, as the nefarious “Epipen” case 
(Rosenthal, 2016) reveals, this system is subjected to serious difficulties when entrusting 
private companies with moral decisions. This problem is named access, and it is present in 
different forms over different markets. 
 
Throughout the rest of developed countries, most of which have monopsonic powers on their 
national health systems, most consumers do not put much attention to the actual cost of their 
medicines. This cause several problems, since, as Malerba & Orsenigo (2015) stated, “[t]he 
market for drugs is inherently characterised by information asymmetry”, since the consumer 
and the physicians in charge of choosing and administering the treatments receive the 
information of drug efficacy and safety from the manufacturer. Therefore, even with the far-
reaching powers exerted by national entities, the consumers and the responsible of 
administering the products are severely subjected to the influencing of the companies.  
 
The same authors affirm that users and physicians tend to be loyal to the same brands and 
follow routinely habits despite the costs (Malerba & Orsenigo, 2015). When added that fact 
to aggressive market practices like the ones recently used by Pfizer against the introduction 
of the generic version of Lipitor® in the US in 2011, coercing their competitors and actively 
engaging to prevent market loss (Jackevicius et al., 2012), the scenario is tilted against the 
entrance of new innovators. Therefore, is hard to enforce drug price controls. 
 
Here is worth to dedicate some lines to the indiscriminate promotion of use of some 
medications by the industry with support (if not in connivance) of the medical community. 
Two examples are at hand: the misuse of antibiotics and the opioid epidemic ravaging the 
US at the moment. Although both cases are complex and different in their causes, a similar 
shared component is that involved pharma companies tried to maximize their returns by 
expanding the therapeutic use of their products. The results are devastating: the appearance 
of antibiotic-resistant pathogen strains is a major concern, and countries like Denmark have 
regulated the prescription of antibiotics to the minimum necessary. On the US opioid 
epidemic, is estimated by the U.S. Department of Health & Human Services that in 2015 
around 12.5 million abused prescription medication, while more than 15,000 deaths were 
attributed to overdosing on commonly prescribed opioids, and another 13,000 deaths were 
related to overdosing on heroin; the latter is a substitute favored by drug addicts when their 
access to prescribed medication is cut. Is clear that controls based solely on trust of 
physician’s judgment against corporate interest failed the test, at least on these cases.  
 
==PAGE:27==
 
26 
   
An alternative approach to the drug price control is on the optimization of use. The process 
of reimbursement and pricing has already been discussed on the regulatory section, but is 
important to mention its link here on the effect of drug use. One of the key parameters used 
to indicate a medication are the symptoms referred by the patients. However, this way attends 
to the actual disease consequences rather the causes, making the medicine approach a reactive 
activity rather than a prevention process. The “reactive” approach also makes difficult to 
identify a sustainable use of medication since some therapies are more effective (and cheaper) 
if administered before a symptom is manifested (e.g. the diabetes consequences can be 
avoided if a more rigorous control of glucose levels is followed). However, Moors et al., 
(2014) also discuss that not all diseases can benefit from using a preventive approach and 
that ultimately, that approach does not promotes innovation.  
 
Reviewing now the effects of the access problematic in poor and developing countries, it can 
be seeing that many of the diseases that affect their population have not been properly 
researched nor therapeutic options developed; even if treatments exist, the supply and 
production is concentrated on the markets where their manufacturers ensure profits by their 
introduction. This is a key factor, since companies will divert resources in search of profit 
optimization. Is important to remember that companies are eager to capitalize their returns in 
the less possible amount of time, not only due IP constraints, but also to repay the leveraged 
costs of their R&D and operation. Hence, as any market, pharma tries to avoid risk. As part 
of the data review performed for the patent search in this work, the author checked that at 
least 5 out of 15 revised pharma companies stated on their financial records the struggles for 
maintain supply and sell on the Venezuelan market along 2016. Some of them even pulled 
off entirely, while others (as TEVA) suspended money transfer activities preventively. The 
consequence is that market access for the population is almost non-existent. Although this is 
a radical and unusual situation, it reflects the problems of a globalized network where even 
a once-attractive market such as Venezuela (which was at a time even considered a hotspot 
for the Caribbean logistics of companies like Novo Nordisk) can drag the revenues and 
investment from companies. 
 
Situations as the one referred above disincentivize the entry on less developed markets with 
economic problems and/or a reduced reimbursement scheme. At the same time, markets 
considered to be on safer and more interesting markets, due its number of potential “virgin” 
patients, like China and the southeast Asia countries whom are having a transformation of its 
population consumption habits and diseases prevalence. In sum, it makes less attractive to 
invest on the less researched diseases & populations in poor markets. 
 
1.6.4 
Investors, and Market financing 
DiMasi, Grabowski, & Hansen (2016) made an update research on the costs related to 
development, including resources coming from public and private funds. A striking data 
provided by them is that nearly half of the amount of the cost of research had to be capitalized 
while the rest was out-of-the-pocket money from the company. In other words, “the ROI 
needed to be financed by future endeavors would provide a rate of 2:1 to the investors, a 
tendency that has continued since…” (DiMasi et al., 2003).  
 
==PAGE:28==
 
27 
   
Upon review of which are the phases that are more resource-intensive, it is found that “[…] 
clinical development (Phases I–III) accounts for approximately 63% of the costs for each 
NME launched (53% from Phase II to launch), and preclinical drug discovery accounts for 
32%.” (Paul et al., 2010). That means that a cost for NMEs is heavily concentrated on the 
middle section of the model, but a third of those costs are expected to happen up front. That 
is a heavy weight for companies that half-function on borrowed money for its discovery 
endeavors.  
 
An inadvertent consequence is that “…pre-clinical and phase I companies were sold at a 
(non-weighted) average multiple of around 11x* (upfront value divided by investment) vs. 
an average of 5x for the later-stage companies. Thus, early-stage companies with ‘hot’ drugs 
or discovery platforms have generated great investor returns recently.” (Bujak et al., 2016). 
In other words, companies think that the front-value of potential discoveries worth more than 
more developed products. This is an amazing consideration, since the common sense would 
indicate the contrary; however, a reason could be that the later-stage discoveries have a more 
defined market potential and its value has been priced better than early-stage discoveries. 
Higher uncertainty, as in any gamble game, pays-off more.  
 
It is worth to mention that “[w]hile European returns have also improved recently, the data 
indicate a widening of the gap between US and European returns.” (Bujak et al., 2016) with 
a US-7.0X return, compared to an Europen-4.3x average. This could be due the higher 
development costs in the US, but also is an indication of the investment environment and 
return expectative for the US market, linked to its drug pricing. 
 
And yet, as Hay, Thomas, Craighead, Economides, & Rosenthal (2014) state that 
“[a]chieving FDA approval for only one-in-ten drug indications that enter the clinic is a 
concerning statistic for drug developers, regulators, investors and patients”. Munos, (2009) 
assert that the “NME output […]is essentially linear, and likely to remain so, and a cost of 
producing NMEs that is increasing exponentially […] could tempt investors to force 
wholesale change onto the industry, unless the industry pre-empts them with radical 
initiatives”. 
 
The above scenario adds as evidence of an increasingly difficult environment to invest and 
obtain financing for new developments. Smaller companies obtain the needed money usually 
through venture capital sources (Malerba & Orsenigo, 2015), but in order to do so, their 
development should show an interesting (and often difficult to prove) potential. And is likely 
that the investor will be eager to capitalize rapidly its investment, even if the product is still 
on an early phase, without committing to develop the product further. A clear consequence 
is that early-phase attrition could rise due the double effect of low investment-development 
delay. 
 
However, is also important to remember that pharma has the advantage of being a low 
demand elasticity market (Malerba & Orsenigo, 2015), which means that once a product has 
asserted its entrance to the market, consumers (and prescribers) will likely to prefer it over 
other options, even if they are pricier. Thus, having a product on the market is usually a safe 
==PAGE:29==
 
28 
   
bet, which is attractive for investors. And one difference on this preference is the aim to have 
more biologics, since, as Samanen, (2013) states, the pricing for these products can be ranged 
quite differently, from some hundred USD annually for a small-molecule generic drug 
treatment,  up to thousands or hundreds of thousands USD in biomolecular drugs costs (e.g. 
around $12,000 for rheumatoid arthritis or $200,000 USD in the case of Gaucher’s disease). 
Hence, there could be an incentive to capitalize on biomolecular therapies.  
 
1.6.5 
Academia and basic research 
There is no doubt that the most important discoveries of the pharma industry have a clear 
precedent on the academia or the clinical experience. University hospitals and institutes 
supply a constant flow of innovations adopted over time in the industry. However, a general 
view on the industry is that within academia, most “don’t understand what it takes to translate 
great research into a drug.”(B. H. Munos & Orloff, 2016). In that sense, most ‘start-up’ 
pharma companies from universities ended as suppliers or purchased by consolidated firms, 
mainly due lacking vertical integration of downstream activities (i.e. drug development, 
regulatory affairs, manufacturing and marketing) (Malerba & Orsenigo, 2015) 
 
It has also been discussed the public funding of innovation. However, a survey reveals that 
the academia feel that a “limited pool of NIH funding has made basic research 
ultraconservative”, and that they are “not funding bold and deep ideas”. At the same time, 
“basic research is being neglected. It cannot get published in the top journals or get adequate 
funding” (B. H. Munos & Orloff, 2016). It appears to be a disconnection between the industry 
and the academia, at least at some degree, both in the importance of basic research funding 
and the understanding of the knowledge transformation into valuable assets. 
 
1.7 
How a product is selected for development? 
The way that products are devised is long and complex, and would require at, at minimum, 
worth several written volumes to be covered in depth. Suffice to say that, from the perspective 
of the two pharma cases that will be presented on Chapter III of this work, the products are 
selected on the basis of: 
a) Previous knowledge of the disease and its action mechanisms, and  
b) The commercial perspective within the company.  
 
The latter point depends greatly on the marketing profile of the company, the business case 
that can devise for the product and the opportunity-cost that it has against the competitors 
and the market environment. The paradigmatic case of the indefinitely postponement of oral 
insulin development (which will be discussed at length in the Novo Nordisk case) reflects 
that the science is an important aspect, but not the only one, when devising a new product.

Chapter II Schumpeterian and Evolutionary Economics 
 
The next step in this work, once the current state and the relevant actors have been presented 
and analyzed in Chapter I, will be to perform a thorough analysis of the innovation process 
for the pharma industry. In doing so, the author will make use of a NeoSchumpetarian 
perspective. The rationale in doing this is that this economic theory has at his center the 
concept of “innovation” as the driver of economic development; this principle serves well to 
characterize the pharma industry, which is crucially dependent (at least on its present 
business model) on the perennial development of new products to replace the ones that 
become less profitable, either because there are new and more effective therapies, or due IP 
protection loss.  
The evolutionary economics theories are based on the principles exposed by the Austrian 
economist Joseph Alois Schumpeter in the early years of the 20th century. On his 1912 book, 
Theory of Economic Development, he already states the key role of innovation in the 
disruption of economic cycles, and highlighted the importance of the entrepreneur as the 
source of the economic development. The advances of his theory have been done mainly in 
the years after 1980, where the Evolutionary economics theory was proposed. Evolutionary 
economics takes an historic perspective to identify “dependencies and irreversibilities” 
(Hanusch & Pyka, 2006). Its interest is based on explaining how the creation, selection and 
retention forces affect the appearance and diffusion of novel products.  
 
2.1 
Fundamental concepts. 
As mentioned before, economic NeoSchumpetarian theories, specifically the evolutionary, 
takes focus specially on the innovation as central driver of the developments in socio-
economic systems, recognizing that uncertainty and adaptability to change are inherent 
phenomena associated to it (Hanusch & Pyka, 2006). This uncertainty makes impossible a 
prediction of the innovation outcomes, whilst it relies heavily on the different actors’ 
learning, interaction and environmental perception as source of innovation process 
explanation. In this approach, actors do not have to behave on a rational and predictable 
manner, much to the contrary to the classic economic theories. Actors are “behavioural 
entities which are primarily characterised as sets of specific and idiosyncratic productive, 
technological and organisational capabilities” (Malerba & Orsenigo, 2015). 
 
Though there is an emphasis on the change as constant in the economic processes, two main 
paradigms, the punctuated equilibria and the gradualist paradigms (Hayes, 2014) have been 
proposed to explain the change process dynamic. However, disregarding which one is 
preferred, is clear that changes and the adaptations on the system produce a “pattern 
formation” to structure the economic activities. Hanusch & Pyka (2006) assert that these 
patterns have inherent properties, which reinforced by positive feedback effects, promote 
radical change and are responsible for the observed qualitative change (i.e. change type seen 
at all economic levels, not only on a structural manner, but in the removal of constrains to 
attain a new status quo). 
 
 
==PAGE:31==
 
30 
   
Malerba & Orsenigo (2015) explain find the forces that define the innovation pattern of each 
industry: 
• Selection push towards the reduction of differences among firms. This is carried out 
by “promoting the growth of more efficient firms, by suppressing less efficient 
behavior and by providing incentives to adopt more efficient practices”. However, 
this process faces the inertia to change of established systems within the firms. 
• The force creation of novelties relies on the capabilities acquired by the companies. 
These capabilities are learned over time, by incorporating external knowledge (e.g. 
imitation, mergers, acquisitions), or by internal development.  
• This learning process is however constrained by the nature of the firms, by the 
environment and the previous learning. Hence, it can be argued that retention force 
can be seen in the success of the firms, which “is linked to the ability to continuously 
innovate and adapt”.

whilst the selection process reduces it. Is in the learning process that Schumpeter described,

as Mark I and II. Based on their work, Breschi, Malerba, & Orsenigo (2000) described them 
enumerating their characteristics. Table 2 enlist them.

Now is useful to review their proposed set of dimensions to describe “technological regimes”, 
which are ways to synthetize “some fundamental structural conditions that contribute to 
define the requisite competencies, the incentives and the dynamic properties of the innovative 
process” (Breschi et al., 2000). These dimensions are explained as follows: 
 
==PAGE:32==
 
31 
   
• Technological opportunities (OPP): is the measure of how likely is to develop an 
innovation for a given amount of money invested. In more innovative environments, 
there would be higher incentives to invest due more possibilities of innovation.  
• Appropriability of innovations (APP): Is the degree of protection possibilities that 
an actor has to prevent competitors to imitate their innovation.  
• Cumulativeness of technological advances (CUM): It is referred to the level in 
which an industry has developed their knowledge as a continuity stream of 
consecutive and related innovations, improving gradually from the original one, or 
alternatively, diverging they knowledge into/from new development areas.  
• Properties of the knowledge base (KBA/KAP): This dimension refers to the degree 
of specialization, complexity, interdependence, etc. that the technological knowledge 
has within the field. The relation between ‘generic’ (KBA) vs ‘specific’ (KAP) 
knowledge is determined depending if a given technology has matured or not, but 
also exist an additional relation to review if these knowledge base, wither KBA or 
KAP, is related to ‘basic’ or ‘applied’ sciences, being the latter obtained through 
experience whilst the first is general and broad understanding of issues.  
 
The same authors revisited a set of dimensions (used on theoretical models constructed by 
Dosi (1982); Iwai (1984) and Nelson & Winter (1982), among others) to assess the status of 
several markets and categorize within a distinctive innovation pattern. The author will make 
use of these characteristics (including their referred notation) to review the NTBP submarket, 
so it becomes important to review them now. 
• Technological entry and exit (ENTRY): All other things being equal, higher 
technological opportunities (OPP) benefit the entry of new innovators to the market. 
In this case, there exist a direct relation to the OPP rate, including the growth of the 
already established firms. In contrast, cumulativeness (CUM) has a inverse relation, 
since less amount technology constructed by the incumbent innovators benefit the 
entrance of new actors, which in consequence would also benefit the entry of 
innovators that have a knowledge base orientation of specific ‘KAP’ on applied 
sciences, while a generic ‘KBA’ knowledge base on basic sciences would have a 
negative effect on the potential entry of innovators. 
• Rate of concentration of innovative activities among firms (CONC): All other 
things being equal, it has been said that high OPP benefit entry of innovators, and in 
consequence that lower the concentration level of the market. It is worth to mention 
that the rate OPP-CONC is dependent also on the environment interaction of OPP, 
APP and CUM, resulting in scenarios where over time, high OPP results in higher 
CONC levels. This scenario has been seen in the pharma consolidation over time, 
especially on the early stages of the industry, where OPP helped to build CUM of 
technologies whilst better enforcing of APP increased (though not drastically) the 
CUM on the industry. Inherently, by limiting the diffusion of the knowledge base 
maintains and increase the advantage of the established innovative actors, which 
provides a direct relationship between its APP and CONC levels. A similar 
relationship is established between CUM and CONC rates. Finally, a specific ‘KAP’ 
knowledge base on applied sciences is usually connected to intensive R&D activities, 
which can limit the number of innovators that can develop them, increasing CONC, 
==PAGE:33==
 
32 
   
though is important to have access to “generic” knowledge in basic sciences at initial 
stages in the market. 
• Degree of stability in the hierarchy of innovative firms (STAB): The inference of 
STAB, APP and CUM, is direct, benefiting the existing status quo when there are 
means to assert ownership of their proprietary technology and its surrounding and 
derive knowledge. This in turn tends to reduce OPP for non-incumbent innovators, 
since its presence results in disruption within the market, marking an inverse 
relationship between these rates; however, a similar effect as with high levels of CUM 
and OPP could result in that high OPP rates leads ultimately to a higher STAB levels.

Based on the latest 3 reports, Novo Nordisk has been consistently named among the most

3.2 
The Case for rare diseases. 
As mentioned before, there are areas where pharma productivity has not stagnated, and one 
is on the “orphan drug” category. It has been reported that “[a]bout 47% of the new drugs 
approved by U.S. FDA in 2015 (21 of 45) were approved to treat rare or orphan 
diseases.”(Kataria et al., 2016). What is more, in 2015 and 2016 “… the same percentage of 
drugs (41%) were prized first-in-class therapies targeting novel modes of action, which was 
also the percentage of 2016 agents approved with orphan indication status.”(Idea Pharma, 
2017). And, at least for 2015, the majority of these products were for oncology indications 
(Norman, 2016), ranging from 30–40% of the orphan developments (Tambuyzer, 2010). 
==PAGE:45==
 
44 
   
 
Before starting diving on the case particularities, is worth to define what is an “rare or orphan 
disease” and “orphan drug”. According to Aronson (2006), the term orphan disease “…has 
been used to describe diseases that are neglected by doctors” but also to “designate diseases 
that affect only small numbers of individuals (so-called health orphans)”. He also states that 
there is no clear definition of what qualifies as such, since there are different country-
specifics on the amount of people affected by it to be considered (US around 200,000; whilst 
Australia put it on 5000), or prevalence (EU 5 in 10,000, the WHO 6.5-10 in 10,000). The 
lists of orphan diseases also differ, but accounts range from 6,000 up to 8,000, with around 
an 80% of them with a genetic basis; it is calculated that around 6% of the population (almost 
30 million in the EU) could suffer from an orphan disease (Tambuyzer, 2010). 
 
The FDA and EMA have different standards to qualify a product as orphan drug, being them: 
• FDA5- The disease or condition for which the drug is intended has a user population 
(either treated or administered) limit of up to 200,000 people in the United States. In 
the case that the user population surpass that limit in the United States, then the 
product still can seek this denomination if there is no reasonable expectation that costs 
of research and development of the drug for the indication can be recovered by sales 
of the drug in the United States. 
• EMA6: First, the product must be intended for the treatment, prevention or diagnosis 
of a disease that is life-threatening or chronically debilitating; the prevalence of the 
condition in the EU must not be more than 5 in 10,000 or it must be unlikely that 
marketing of the medicine would generate sufficient returns to justify the investment 
needed for its development; and that no satisfactory method of diagnosis, prevention 
or treatment of the condition concerned can be authorized, or, if such a method exists, 
the medicine must be of significant benefit to those affected by the condition. 
The EMA, which is overseeing a market that has multiple legal entities embedded (due the 
multinational nature of the EU), tends to center its definition more in the effects rather than 
in the numbers (which still have presence in the prevalence requirement). But is notable the 
intent on emphasize the effect on the population condition that is not mentioned on the CFR 
text.  
 
It is clear that Orphan drugs are a big niche-market in the industry. One reason could be that 
they appear to have greater clinical-phases success rate average7, with slightly higher rates 
for the not oncology-related, (Hay et al., 2014), combined with the fact that “molecules 
approved for orphan indications were investigated in a substantial number of indications prior 
to original marketing approval.” (DiMasi et al., 2016). The last assessment is directly linked 
to the market exclusivity that the companies can obtain, and that will be discussed further.  
The regulatory entities do provide incentives for the development of this products; the 
rationale behind them is that most of these diseases, though are limited in the population,

pediatric investigation plan (PIP). There are almost the same limitations to this 
protection mentioned by the FDA, including permission from the authorization holder 
for approval of other products, lack of capability to supply enough product to meet

3.2.1 
Orphazyme case 
Orphazyme ApS is a Danish pharmaceutical company founded on 2009. It is mainly focus 
on the development of treatments for lysosomal storage diseases, which are considered 
orphan diseases. According to its web page, the first indication for its drug arimoclomol is 
the “Niemann-Pick Type C disease (NPC) [which] is one of several fatal lysosomal storage 
diseases and is affecting around 1 in 150,000 newborns” (Orphazyme A/S, 2016). However, 
they are seeking applications and/or in several other orphan indications, such as Gaucher’s 
disease, sporadic Inclusion Body Myositis (sIBM) and Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS) 
(Orphazyme A/S, 2017a). 
In 2011, the worldwide rights to arimoclomol were bought by Orphazyme from CytRx Corp 
(GEN, 2011). This small-molecule “which stimulates diseased cells to produce their own 
HSP70, also reduced the accumulated sphingolipids and dramatically improved the disease 
symptoms” (Orphazyme A/S, 2016). The agreement included a payment up to $120 million

(that depended on milestones achievement) and royalties from its sales, if gets to be 
commercialized. Recently, Orphazyme concluded the recruitment for their pivotal clinical 
trial (ClinicalTrials.gov, 2017; Orphazyme A/S, 2017b), which represents a landmark on the 
development of their product.  
The author conducted an interview with Anders Mørkeberg Hinsby, PhD, cofounder and  
CEO of Orphazyme and board member of other biomedical companies (Linkedin, 2017a). A 
fuller version of this interview can be found on the Appendix 5. The following are the 
highlights of the meeting. 
 
Incentives of enter on the orphan drug area are crucial to generate more innovations, and 
Orphazyme takes advantage of them (both the EMA and FDA have granted the orphan 
denomination to arimoclomol). The wave respond to the availability of “low-hanging fruits” 
available still on several indications. Since the era of blockbuster is being replaced by value-
based medications, the developments must show clinical improvement of the patient 
condition. The pricing and clinical trials design will be related to the efficacy of the product, 
and that is a reality that Orphazyme acknowledges on its own clinical development strategy. 
This approach has been already discussed in Chapter I, but is already being implemented. 
Treatments will evolve to be more personalized and the targets will be identified through 
precision medicine. The predominant paradigm in the industry is the treatment of the disease 
made by a physician based on its interpretation of signs and symptoms9. The paradigm then 
moves into section a complex “disease” for which a single product cannot completely treat 
it, into smaller “smaller diseases”, for which a specific treatment can be diagnosed more 
precisely. This approach has already been adopted by other of the pharma productive areas: 
oncology. Cancer is in fact a multi-variant, multi-factor array of diseases, which have 
sometimes very little in common, other than the name. A single type of treatment for breast 
cancer would possible not render the same result in treating a skin-sarcoma, or leukemia. 
Hence, research had to be very niche-focused, and specialized, almost like the same set 
present on the orphan-disease world. Nonetheless, this approach does not have to be 
constrained only to those two, and bigger-complex diseases (e.g. diabetes, neurological or 
heart diseases) could be approached on the same sense. 
 
Orphazyme appears could be characterized in a “knowledge leverager” open innovation 
model. Orphazyme has been able to attract investment for its product development. A single 
series-round for fund raising would be of $50 million USD, while they have conduced 3 up, 
the first for a maximum of €20 million.  
Whilst this is small to US standards, they have ben efficient in terms of operation cost-
spending, expecting to hit the market with a product for around $100 million USD, around a 
20th of the current cost development tag for big pharma. However, the high risk of failure 
with only one project still exists, and a company of over 35 persons cannot handle more than 
one development project at a given time. A key insight is the notion of a possible coordination 
of small-biotech companies that provide resource support while not being integrated into a

The innovation is not (always) the problem. 
When the author started thinking about cases to include in this work, he selected NN due his

However, this picture is not entirely complete. In this section, the author will present a review 
of the NN strategy based on an interview held with Gerasimos Koulouris, Strategic R&D 
Business Analyst at Novo Nordisk (Cruz Contreras, 2017b; Linkedin, 2017b), and 
complemented with videos from broadcast interviews of the NN’s recently appointed CEO

First, let us review their innovation stance. One of the holy grails in the endocrinology 
medicine is the development of oral insulin. There are plenty of diabetes therapies, both oral 
and injectable. NN, in fact, is also a leading manufacturer of devices to deliver its products. 
But proper insulin delivery has always been a problem. Insulin is a hormone (thus, a protein), 
that is segregated by the pancreas into the blood stream to regulate the levels of glucose, 
which is the fuel the cells use to make their processes. When there is not insulin production 
(like in DM1 or progressive stages of DM2) or when the organism has developed a resistance 
to it (like in DM2), it becomes necessary to provide it artificially. DM2 can be treated with 
alternative therapies, but the main therapy for DM1 is insulin (Lisa & Hess-Fischl, 2017).  
==PAGE:53==

Insulin-dependent patients require a constant supply of the medication, generally more than 
once intake daily. Therefore, is problematic for them to carry with their medication and apply 
it when not at home or at work. The ideal would be dosage it orally. And that is the quid of 
the issue: insulin is a protein, and the gastric system has evolved to process proteins. Making 
a pharmaceutical formulation that not only is capable of being stable at temperature levels 
but also resistant to the digestive process and able to be absorbed into the blood stream in 
therapeutic dosage levels, is to say the least, an innovation conundrum.  
Well, NN and other companies have been on the race to develop it for years (now even 
decades). In recent times, at an internal company level (the author witnessed), the confidence 
of having an oral technology in the near future was high. But in October 2016, the Chief 
Scientific Officer, Mads Krogsgaard Thomsen announced that NN was terminating its efforts 
on it for the time being. Its change of strategy included continue with oral semaglutide (a 
weekly analog GLP1) and repurposing their research efforts on adjacent areas, such as the 
beginning of an “intensive R&D program in NASH (Non-Alcoholic Steatohepatitis), 
cardiovascular diseases and diabetic kidney diseases”(Lawrence, 2016). Discovery failures, 
as described throughout this work, are the norm rather than the exception.  
In fairness, NN is still very productive for industry standards, but what this case (along with 
others, such as the 2014's discontinuation of their inflammatory disorders bet) reveals that

A way forward by integration. 
The transition into a job takes time. For Lars Fruergaard Jorgensen, however, his first day in 
office as NN’s CEO was already a busy day. Since late 2016, he had to take part on the major 
decisions for facing a turbulent 2017. So far, the company has succeeding in containing 
dangerous bleeding on its financial perspective, and is on its way to adjust the R&D efforts 
by launching new projects. So, again, the discovery phase gets complicated as it takes time 
to detect and select a candidate based on the company’s knowledge and business profile. 
During a televised interview in May (Bloomberg, 2017), Mr. Jorgensen discussed several 
topics, but interestingly, focused on the access problem. The context is somewhat different 
from last year, since now the market is immersed on the AHCA proposal and the Obamacare 
repeal, but the NN mentality has adapted to offer a response to the environment: price rebates 
are here to stay, at least in the foreseeable future. 
 
Thus, the industry must face a new reality that shows that the US, either by a marginalization 
of health coverage or by a more aggressive stance on pricing by MCOs/PBMs, will no longer 
be receptive of high costs. The argument that said that the US was, in a way, subsidizing the 
world’s pharmaceutical innovation is likely to be put at test. The message offered by Mr. 
Jorgensen and the one provided by Mr. Koulouris during the interview is one of 
==PAGE:57==
 
56 
   
acknowledgement of this reality, while both are showing hints of a new phase centered on 
integration, something that companies like J&J, Roche or Abbvie have started to embrace. 
The path for NN goes, apparently, to evolve into a sort of service company. The idea is to 
enhance the relationship with customers, through more dedicated and personalized 
treatments, platforms and customer service. Some of these practices are already known to the 
company, because their “biopharma” branch is very specialized in small populations that 
require constant product supply and service attention, some for years (growth hormone 
patients), some for life (like hemophilia patients). So, should not surprise that diabetes care 
would evolve into a personalized approach as well, empowering the patients and 
strengthening customer relations. One approximation is through the world Diabetes 
Foundation (worlddiabetesfoundation.org), an independent trust that builds ties with the 
society and educates the public in the diabetes treatment and consequences.  
 
Technological efforts for integration should account the need to acquire further knowledge 
of the diseases. One way to approach is through big data acquisition tools that allow 
physicians and medical practitioners into devise more effective treatments. In technical 
terms, these systematic data collections are called observational clinical trials, which are 
different from interventionist trials (the ones needed for drug market application) in that 
subjects agree only to grant access to their medical records once they fulfill certain 
parameters to be considered in established cohorts. Trials like these ones are regularly carried 
out, especially on populations where vast medical records are available, such as in Denmark, 
Sweden and Switzerland. Electronic medical record systems allow to have a wider and more 
controlled span of a patient evolution, and can dramatically improve our knowledge of 
diseases, along with therapies for their treatment.  
 
Another effort on the technology side is the device manufacturing. As mentioned, some of 
NN’s molecules are very sensitive. The methodology to deliver the molecule to be 
biologically available is not trivial. As matter of fact, Novopen® was the first insulin device 
of its class, and a revolution on the market at a time. Mr. Koulouris alluded to efforts to 
enhance delivery systems and pharmaceutical formulations are key to this integration, but 
also on rendering devices that (like eye implants or similar devices) can gather clinical info 
from the body on a continuous basis. Is not far beyond to hear such initiatives, since “…[NN] 
announced its collaboration with California based Glooko, providing remote monitoring 
software for type 1 and type 2 diabetes patients” (Idea Pharma, 2017). 
 
Thus, NTBPs innovation are just a part of the whole process. One of the key comments during 
the interview with Mr. Koulouris was his personal view that the products that NN deliver 
have a quality standard that is increasingly difficult to overcome (they are, in fact, getting 
closer to replicate the actual biological behavior), and hence the emphasis should not be put 
in the drug alone, but on the whole development around the range of product integration.

Peter Harper is a Managing Partner at Harper & Vedel consultancy, specializing in

First, there is an impression of a change from small molecule’s manufacturing focus since 
around 40 years ago, which used to have a chemical synthesis approach. The Industry has 
redefined itself into a biological-products manufacture, mainly due the perception that there 
is where financial gains can be made. A major part of that SM production has shifted into 
India and China, where is produced by a low cost-high volume scheme. Almost all pharma 
companies in Denmark have adopted the same approach. 
 
The old facilities in Europe are being abandoned or purchased at discounted prices. These 
facilities are of “zero value” to the companies dedicated to biologics production. Biological-
product manufacturing is a game of scales: other than the insulin production, most products 
are manufactured in small facilities and in reduced quantities/volumes. That is why 
repurposing of facilities is also not an option, since most manufacturing processes have also 
evolved to make it more efficient and disposable.  
 
The capital expenditure in construction is considerable lower than it used to be. People in 
Denmark tend to consider Novo Nordisk as an example of what a pharma company looks, 
but they forget that NN is supplying around 60% of the world-market insulin, that is why 
their facilities are big (and their construction investments are too). But for the rest of the 
manufacturers, the requirements are far less big. Mr. Harper commented the case of a vaccine 
facility in Siena, Italy for a big pharma that are comparable to house-size, and the pre-made 
generic biopharma plants developed by GE (Cogley, 2016). Therefore, the barrier of entry 
for small companies is lower than in the past, making the clinical development the (already) 
major development cost. Hence, it has never been more affordable to product a drug than 
today. But, it appears that the excitement of biological revolution has not lived to the 
expectations generated, and that is also seen on the facilities construction side.  
 
On the regulatory side, Mr. Harper does not see a problem on the entities nor the legal 
framework, but rather on the persons and teams dedicated to respond at the companies. He 
alluded to the underestimation by new companies of the paperwork and experience needed 
to meet the regulatory requirements and pass from a R&D into a production setting. He 
considers this as a major internal competence issue. Companies fail to recognize that a 
different team should oversee production once R&D is completed. Also, these companies 
tend to try to internalize all processes, and do not seem to foresee that many of these activities 
are temporal and can be performed by external people.  
 
In a way, Mr. Harper favors a specialized knowledge outsource, and indicates that companies 
should identify what is that differentiates from others and concentrate on it, and hire 
==PAGE:59==
 
58 
   
externally the rest (i.e. contract manufacturing organizations - CMOs). The key issue is that 
if the company’s product requires unit operations that are consider “standard”, with high 
margin and small volume, then it does not make sense to own the facilities. That mentality

4.1 
Personalized medicine 
One of the key aspects of the new scientific understanding of the concept of disease is that 
although modern medicine could pin point most of the reasons that cause a specific disease, 
they have generalized the consequences suffered by most individuals, as well as the solution. 
This was basically because there were not methods of particularize the specific variables to 
be accounted on each individual case. The human body, as any complex system, is affected 
by so many things that it made sense to have standard treatment outlines. 
 
The starting points for a personalized approach to diseases were based on the use of the 
physician’s recommendations for subject’s treatment, based on their medical history and the 
results of a determined set of diagnostic tests. But this was a complicated and debatable 
solution because it relied heavily on the expertise of the physicians and their interpretation 
of a limited array of data, which could have a limited scientific back up and possible low 
replicability. Of course, this scenario also implies a thorough knowledge of the disease, 
which in many cases is still limited, to generate those specific diagnostic tests. 
However, the development of informatics data tools, medical devices (e.g. implants and 
continuous monitoring devices) and specific tests targeted for therapies (i.e. biomarkers and 
genetic tests) are increasingly allowing the medical professionals to identify and provide a 
more dedicated treatment to patients’ needs. Additionally, one of the areas studied in this 
work is actually helping in developing strategies to make the personalized medicine approach 
a reality, as Tambuyzer (2010) comments: “[t]he field of rare diseases is a ‘societal 
laboratory’ that is predicting future trends in patient-centered human health care, and as such 
it is a model for personalized medicine for some aspects. For chronic diseases, such as 
diabetes or some neurodegenerative diseases, this aproach seems to be ideal, due the wide 
==PAGE:62==
 
61 
   
range of population’s type and characteristics, since “personalized medicines are aimed at 
subgroups of mostly well-known large patient populations often already addressed by the 
health-care systems and with well-established infrastructures” (Tambuyzer, 2010). 
Additional efforts, like the ones supported by NGO’s like the Personalized Medicine 
Coalition (personalizedmedicinecoalition.org) 
The promise still miss to render the desired outcome, i.e. accountable measured 
reduction/improvement of disease rate/control, but so far there are some clear examples that 
encourage its use, like blood pressure control and dietary recommendations made for elderly 
people. Further clinical evidence is needed. 
 
4.1.1 
Slice the pie: scope and reduce the complex diseases 
An additional aim resulted directly related to the perspective of Mr. Anders Hinsby is the 
notion of understanding the diseases in such manner that is possible to scope them into 
smaller sets of syndromes, each one of them treatable separately (or conjunctively, if 
possible, but on a more comprehensive manner).  
 
This simplification of diseases treatment based on their effect rather than the 
symptomatology/signs require, as already mentioned, a deep understanding of the action 
mechanisms of the disease, but also on the interaction of drugs and other treatments 
administered to treat them. Hence, there are no shortcuts on the learning process, it must be 
connected to the knowledge acquisition. In a way, the rare disease case is also connected 
here. Tambuyzer (2010) says that when “common diseases and syndromes are being divided 
into smaller patient sub-populations and that these subpopulations may be eligible for orphan 
status”, which entails the benefits of exclusivity and IP protections, and prevent disruptive 
competition. Once an orphan designation is granted it“… allows for more predictive and 
targeted treatments, with earlier intervention and improved patient outcomes” (Tambuyzer, 
2010). Similar strategies could be devised for the least identifiable syndromes.   
 
4.2 
Translational medicine 
Translation medicine can be defined as “a research discipline aimed to improve the 
predictability of success of drug discovery and development” (Day, Rutkowski, & 
Feuerstein, 2009). In a sense, the intention of this discipline is to identify methodologies that 
enhance and ensure the specificity of a therapy based on biological mechanisms. Such 
mechanisms are then related to desired behaviors. A good example for this process is found 
in the case of the arimoclomol, a molecule that promotes a desired production of HSP70, a 
stress protein secreted as part of the heat shock response. This desired response is then taken 
in advantage to treat diseases that have that mechanism interconnected in their causality. 
Additional strategies include the use of biomarkers, “biological substances or biophysical 
parameters that can be monitored objectively and reproducibly and used to predict drug 
effector outcome” (Day et al., 2009). In a way, biomarkers help not only to identify targets. 
Additional categories (as seen in Appendix 7) include indicators of how a drug candidate 
interact and react (in pharmacodynamical terms) with its biological target, the consequences 
of these interaction, etc. 
==PAGE:63==
 
62 
   
Ultimately, the potential expected from these technologies is that the ability “to identify and 
validate new biomarkers will ultimately increase the power and utility of the discovery 
technologies” (Kaitin, 2010).  
Here is important to mention the need of build these models in silico, i.e. from computational 
models, that allow to foresee the product component without implementing it on a live 
organism, but rather using databases available. Thus, this deeply depend on the expansion of 
knowledge of each disease mechanism; Kant, Ibberson, & Scheer (2010) have discussed the 
issue of the current amount of scientific development and the impossibility for many scientist 
to keep with the academic and industry publications, and then use it to generate further 
knowledge. Their approximation pass by development of knowledge management systems 
that allow scientist to systematize and construct the scientific knowledge database of 
tomorrow. 
 
4.2.1 
Clinical development optimization: quick win/fast fail approach 
One of the uses of the Translational Medicine Paradigms (Clinical Research Society, 2012), 
is the use of biomarkers to make an improved clinical development process based on the 
quick win/fast fail approach. The idea would be to build clinical trials based on specific 
outcomes for tests. Paul et al. (2010) developed this different approach for clinical 
development, “shifts the proof of concept (POC) stage from Phase II to just after the start of 
clinical development” (Clarke, 2010). This process, now widely known but not widely used 
yet, is depicted on Figure 18. 
 
 
Figure 18  Traditional paradigm of drug development (a) contrasted with an alternative development paradigm 
referred to as quick win, fast fail (b). CS, candidate selection; FED, first efficacy dose; FHD, first human dose; 
PD, product decision. Source (Paul et al., 2010) 
The authors explain that this alternative design incorporate a decrease in the technical 
uncertainty (of the drug usefulness) to establish the POC before entering in the more 
expensive clinical phases (II and III). Fewer candidates will advance, but they will have 
==PAGE:64==
 
63 
   
higher possibilities of being launched into the market; this will also have the benefit to save 
resources that can be reinvested into other developments. An interesting connection is that to 
have clear and measurable endpoints to determinate the viability of candidates, the use of 
biomarkers or other forms of landmark points should be used in these types of trials. 
 
The question would be, how feasible is this? So far, it appears that only few companies and 
trials have conducted this approach. The problem again is the definition of endpoints. Not all 
companies have the expertise to develop biomarkers, and when they do exist, it is still a 
matter of standardization within the industry. After 7 years of its original proposal, it remains 
to be seen if the renewal and wider adoption of the model can deliver improved success rates, 
which so far is not the case. In addition to this, Mr. Hinsby motioned a good insight in the 
sense that the fast fail approach should” be instead a fast fail efficiently, to allow the 
innovative companies to prevent useful resources to go in a dead-end development, and 
survive a failure in product development.  
 
4.3 
Regulatory needs 
We have described the regulatory environment of the FDA, and to a lesser degree the EMA 
stance. Both entities have incentive programs and development standards measured by goals 
stated by their executive branches (the US government- PDUFAs & the EU commission) and 
the. Initiatives like the FDA’s critical path and fast track, and EMA's PRIME are programs 
to promote the speed ways to attain market approval. 
 
4.3.1 
Regulatory approval review 
However, the major challenge of the regulatory entities is not efficiency on the review (which 
is still important), but the adequacy of the requirements for the approval, and the inclusion 
of a third dimension, the patient’s life-quality improvement, rather than only the safety and 
the efficacy of the drug. On that regard, Eichler et al. (2013) comment that a higher “emphasis 
on post-marketing surveillance of safety and effectiveness in the real-world allows regulators 
to rely more on monitoring than on prediction” while “all licensing decisions are based not 
only on interpretations of data but also on value judgments, including the willingness to trade 
benefits for harms”.  
If the regulator has more resources available, that will mean more requests can be reviewed. 
Moors et al. (2014) comments that “earlier approval would not affect the risks of drug 
development, and would lengthen the exclusive marketing period (and, therefore, the costs) 
of such drugs”. 
 
Another recommendation is make less expensive the marketing authorization process, which 
will promote the entrance of more small actors, and improve competition. This idea is 
especially relevant for the areas where innovation is stagnated or heavily dominated for a 
single actor, and should be considered in the fast track programs, perhaps including a lock 
on the proven attainment of a major POC.  
 
Following the trend on specificity and POC treatments, is important the validation and 
standardization of these endpoints based on biomarkers and other  “novel methodologies, or 
the framework for biosimilars” (Ehmann et al., 2013). Ultimately this will enable innovators 
==PAGE:65==
 
64 
   
to acknowledge they are following trends that the regulatory authority (being also the main 
scientific arbiter) has studied and recognized.  
The author would like to close this section by acknowledging an interesting position by the 
FDA-CDER’s Deputy Director for Regulatory Programs, Douglas Throckmorton, (2012), 
whom provides the following set of directives for the authority to function, and  that very 
well could be a guidance for its present and future activities: 
 
• Understand the nature & challenges of the issue [the role of the authority] 
• Improve the efficiency of development through collaboration and communication 
• Improve internal FDA processes to support new paradigms 
• Stay open to change 
 
The case of Japan’s overregulated and bureaucratic drug approval process (Tanimoto, 2015) 
should be an additional proof that the authority has to ease the process to the minimum 
necessary to ensure its mandate to bring safety, affordable and effective products to the 
market. 
 
4.3.2 
Rethink the role of the IP restrictions, increase access. 
There are many points to be made on this issue, but the author consider two things are needed 
urgently to enter in the discussion: 
 
• Cost control: Perhaps the most socially relevant and sensitive issue is drug pricing. 
Unfortunately, there are not simple or general solutions, and each country (and in 
some cases, each hospital or state) particularities need to be considered. An option is 
to include the effectiveness parameter in the market authorization and reimbursement 
decisions; by acknowledging that this dimension is an important parameter to 
measure the relevance (and innovativeness) of a drug, the regulatory authority would 
introduce a minimum treatment standard. In that sense, Moors et al., (2014) thinks 
that including the effectiveness assessment will bring a “shift from unit-pricing to 
value-based purchasing, [so that] new drugs will be priced based on the value 
healthcare payers give to them”.  
If the regulatory authority could have this assessment, the problem of “me-too” drugs 
and “slightly-better” drugs could also be challenged, since medication seeking for 
exclusivity, market approval or other IP protection methods would be forced to show 
their added value. Is wort to note that this process is challenging, since in a way is a 
barrier and complicates an already difficult process, which is one of the reasons the 
industry would oppose it. 
 
• IP protection schemes: This is perhaps the most controversial issue. Malerba & 
Orsenigo (2015) and Moors et al. (2014) state that the international standardization 
of the IP protection system has further been complicated and its structure “do not 
appear to have had a visible and significant impact on the innovative capabilities of 
industries”. Moors et al. even take it to a higher degree, saying that their ideal model 
would be “a world without drug patents, [where] the research and development would 
be performed by public funds and would be accessible to all parties”. In contrast, both 
==PAGE:66==
 
65 
   
Malerba & Orsenigo, and Dimasi & Grabowski, (2004, 2015) recognize the 
importance of having a way to incentivize the industry to innovate; in special, their 
2015 paper comments that the patent scheme will continue to be the main IP 
protection, but that additional incentives, such as exclusivity, need to be present as 
well to promote innovation. 
 
The author tend to consider that the world’s intellectual protection system is very centered 
(and contructed) for the major and rich markets, and therefore it further pose a struggle 
in less developed countries where innovation is limited. The author would favor a less 
universal approach for IP protection laws and pricing schemes, allowing countries to 
enter diffeentiation on a case-by-case review, which would also improve access. The 
author consider that intellectual protection has been a cornerstone for the economic model 
of innovation (from the Schumpeterian economics perspective), but that does not mean 
is the only way to reward the development of novel inventions. Further ideas and better 
approaches, from public funding and cooperative collaborations, etc. should be 
considered. 
 
4.4 
Reuse of molecules, including discarded candidates. 
Oprea & Mestres (2012) propose an interesting algorithm (included on Appendix 8) on drug 
repurposing from different stages of the drug development. The chart applies to both NMEs 
and NTBPs. Is interesting that the strategy refers to the identification of additional therapeutic 
indications for the product (even at early stages), taking advantage not only the specificity of 
potential targets (using the above named in silico developments) but also the safety profile 
obtained from previous development. The concept is not new since, as mentioned in the 
orphazyme case, it is widely used for rare disease drugs. Tambuyzer (2010) offer also the 
example of sildenafil, a erectile-dysfunction bluckbuster repurposed for orphan indications 
in pulmonary arterial hypertension (which incidentally, was the initial indication was 
developed for). The FDA has followed suit and now have a database of repurposed 
medications that have now also an orphan designation for a different indication. 
 
4.4.1 
Creation of a world platform for trade of discarded molecules 
Within the repurposing proposal, an important part was mentioned by Mr. Hinsby, and is the 
potential abundance of false negatives in early stages using the quick win/fast fail model. 
Therefore, the market should be prepared to trade on this kind of early developments based 
on the work already made for each one, allowing for fast repurposing. In a way, is a model 
where the “failed” candidates will be identified for new developments. The author considers 
that this market could even be considered a new type of commodity, trading in for the best 
buyer in the major stock exchange places. In a way, making this as open as possible, and 
allowing governmental supervision to prevent non-innovator actors to monopolize it, could 
help the competition and increase the rate of desirable profiles.

4.5 
Reorganization of the industry 
The industry is composed by a myriad of different companies, so is hard to make specific 
assessments on how it should be structured. But the author consider that are several 
generalizable recommendations, named as follows:  
 
o Review the future viability of the big pharma: since this work has shown the end of 
blockbuster business-model as a main income for the big-pharma, the author suggest 
that these companies should rethink if their current size and structure is convenient 
in the long run. A downsize in the scale (not necessarily, but desirable, on the 
operational side) should be considered to become efficient. The author thinks that a 
gradual approach to this restructure is the most desirable approach, since downsizing 
will require job loss, and is a sensitive problem that companies would face better, 
allowing these people to enter other companies or sectors.  
 
o Introduction of a Chief Dead Drug Officer (CDDO). Scannell et al., (2012) proposed 
the concept of this charge within corporate structure, for a person dedicated to 
assessing and improving the drug fail rate, so specific problematic in the company is 
properly identified and followed. The idea is simple and reasonably achievable for 
most big-medium size companies, with a comprehensive R&D portfolio. 
 
o Evolve into service companies and platform integrated services: A trend detected in 
the Novo Nordisk case is their approach to integrate a product platform, less centered 
on the drug development and introducing the collection & use of data, and allow the 
company to offer a more personalize treatment, while enhancing their relationship 
with the patient (ergo, increasing its loyalty). This appear to be the way for companies 
centered on chronic diseases. However, other companies should use a similar 
approach to identify the ranges of services that can render to population, which is a 
way of preventing/rethinking the downsize suggestion, already discussed. In this 
respect, the enhance of NTBPs is crucial, since they ensure a biological specificity 
and efficacy that patients can consider in their value assessment 
 
4.6 
Embrace open innovation 
One recommendation that most authors support is the use of external knowledge to enhance 
the competences of the company. In a way, there are little arguments to oppose open 
innovation, and usually they are in the line of knowledge protection. Science is intrinsically 
collaborative and constructed upon previous development, so this argument selectively omits 
the fact that most advances can and are achieved by different teams. Therefore, the question 
is not if a company should incorporate open innovation, but up to what degree.  
Peter Harper offered a bold recommendation, which is that companies must to concentrate 
on their key differentiating competences, and use external knowledge for all the rest. In a 
way, this recommendation is in line with Schuhmacher et al., (2013) model of knowledge 
leverager. The author considers that companies (big and small) should follow suit, which in 
turn will slim their organizations and make them more efficient.

Conclusions 
During this work, the author described the current problematic of the pharma industry, 
identifying the actors and the interest they have in the innovation process. The author showed 
a tendency of sustained NTBP development against a decrease of NMEs production in the 
period, characterizing as a Schumperterian Mark II innovation patter for the period 2000-
2017. Thus, biologicals are at the center of innovation in the modern-day drug development, 
which confirms the initial hypothesis, though not in a way of a surge.  
 
The author presented cases of success that highlighted actions taken by some companies to 
overcome the R&D stagnation. He was able to provide recommendations to try to overcome 
this situation based on those highlighted actions. In some cases, like the translational 
medicine and the quick win/fast fail model are intrinsically dependent to work. But other 
authors have identified most of them as a comprehensive approach to solve the industry R&D 
problems, while concentrating in specialized and integrated product platform. The author 
support the rethinking of the industry role in innovation terms, centering the core 
competences of companies into a more open and collaborative approach for discovery and 
early development, and an efficient process of clinical development. POC remains a promise 
that need to be tested and expanded to verify its feasibility, though development of targeted 
endpoints is proven possible, though rather complicated.  
 
Is also important to mention that the presented work have limitations, both in the systematic 
methodology and in the scope of results. It can be argued that the patent search shows clear 
trend in terms of recording, but the timespan could be done differently (for example, a yearly 
production rather an averaged one) and this info correlated with spending information on 
R&D. On the other hand, some of the recommendations should be put at test on individual 
cases to see if they match with the reality and up to what extend they can be applied in the 
real life. Finally, the author is considering the willingness for model change of the industry 
as a given fact, when probably is not. Facing the need to generalize the interest of so many 
actors is difficult and will ultimately lead to oversimplifications; however, is true that most 
big pharmas are actively looking for options to solve the R&D problem. 
 
In the end, the roulette model is still active and uncertainty as a key factor will not disappear 
from drug development. A deeper understanding of the diseases will help to minimize it up 
to a degree, but deep refinements are needed to unleash the potential of the long overdue 
biological revolution.

Appendix 5 Oprhazyme’s Interview extended Summary. 
 
Orphazyme 
Anders Mørkeberg Hinsby, PhD, cofounder and  CEO of Orphazyme and board member of 
other biomedical companies (Linkedin, 2017a), granted the author an interview held at 
Orphazyme’s facilities (Cruz Contreras, 2017a). The following is a recount of the highlights 
of that conversation. Next is a summary of such conversation. 
 
He started the chat sharing his view of why more companies are looking at orphan 
indications: “Absolut number of orphan drugs is given by the higher number of companies 
working on orphan diseases […] and companies are working on stratifying bigger diseases 
[…]” and that “companies are now motivated and incentivized on the exclusivity and rebates 
obtained on orphan drugs”.  
In his opinion, the old model of “blockbuster products” is being replaced by a new version 
of them, “whereby medicines are valued based on the effect it has on the patient treated 
population”, and governments are focusing more and more to ensure access treatment for all 
population. That incentivize the development on fields that where not previously properly 
researched, which provide higher revenues (due higher treatment costs) that allow a company 
to seek commercial benefit from developing a product, as long as the effect on the population 
is good.  
In his opinion, in the future, the price of a drug and the endpoints in clinical trials to test them 
will be closely linked to the efficacy of a product, which has not been the case up to the 
moment.  
He also referred to the current wave of “low hanging fruits”, which is a consequence of 
relatively low saturated therapeutic areas, the increasing number of relevant actors and the 
incentives offered by governments. Another key factor is a better understanding of the 
disease’s mechanisms and genetics, which could lead to a way of “personalized and 
precision medicine” (meaning treatments that are suited to attain specific characteristics of 
the disease and the patient) and this is a game-changing approach.  
 
On the product development, Orphazyme is centered on the development a product called 
Arimoclomol. Mr. Hinsby mentioned that their approach was not based on patent a product 
but rather to patent “knowledge” (e.g., their knowledge of how use the specific cell’s heat-
shock response to promote a protein-production behavior). Then, they were “luck” to find a 
set of specific small-molecules that targets specifically the mechanism to promote such 
desired behavior, and which were available to be acquired and had been developed already 
to the clinical stage.  
Mr. Hinsby agrees on the characterization of Orphazyme’s model as a “knowledge 
leverager”, given that the current size of the organization is 35 persons and relies deeply on 
several key external vendors. He disagrees on be described as a “virtual pharma” because, he 
argues, such companies “do not build on their knowledge or on their platform”, though they 
may work. He considers that one of the key aspects of being successful in the industry is the 
capability of learn of their mistakes, and been backed by supporters (meaning, investors) that 
are willing to help when those poor choices arise. On the contrast, the “virtual” model usually 
targets a single opportunity, which often is not possible to take until the final stage at the first 
==PAGE:90==
 
89 
   
attempt. On the same side, Mr. Hinsby considers that Orphazyme is still small enough to be 
flexible and efficient on the resource allocation.  
On the resources aspect, Mr. Hinsby mentions that for European standards, the company is 
“extremely well-funded and cushioned”. However, from a US perspective, they are not, since 
they would also have money to outsource or acquire early-developed technology and know-
how. A single series-round for fund raising would be of $50 million USD, while they have 
conduced 3 up, the first for a maximum of €20 million.  
On the cost side, he mentioned that Orphazyme operates on a much lower cost from big 
pharmas, since they usually cover (as mentioned extensively on the first chapter of this work) 
for a set of product development program comprising several candidates at the time that will 
suffer from attrition, while they are mainly focused on one candidate. He does admit that 
whilst this is a much lower cost operation, that implies a higher risk (Mr. Hinsby locate the 
risk on a maximum of 10x-20x the one that have on big pharma). But he also argues on its 
higher efficiency of the company, demonstrated on his expectative of bringing the product 
to the market for a cost of around $100 million USD, around a 20th of the current cost 
development tag for big pharma. 
In terms of future development, Mr. Hinsby mentioned that as long as the company continues 
with a similar structure, only one product-per generation can be expected to be delivered, but 
he doesn’t discard a joint venture or even a sell-out of the company. However, he mentioned 
that a key motivator on this work-field is the sense of reward, of accomplishment that the 
persons receive from developing a product that can improve the living conditions of a patient. 
In his opinion, people tend to work harder because of their commitment to the project, and 
that in back increases motivation and efficiency. In a way, he sees a connection of that to a 
scale-reduction of more biotech companies, and he foresee the same time of approach, maybe 
under a larger coordination organization that provides support and resources to small-sized 
biopharma companies. He talked about the importance to center on the core knowledge inside 
the company, which is its intrinsic value to build upon. 
He also talked about the difficulties to change in big pharma, rising a comment on how those 
changes could be perceived by potential stock investors, which in turn could damage the 
company market value. Hence, he concludes that the willingness to adopt principles such as 
quick win, fail fast, by those organizations is limited. On that development model, he 
considers that the “fail fast” part should also be extended to “fail fast efficiently”, since 
companies should be aware and do not be afraid to “ask the right do-or-die questions” and 
determinate the adequate end-points to ensure that the failure of a candidate is promptly 
detected.  
The conversation entered a very interesting point: he mentioned his hypothesis on the 
possibility of false-negative abundance on the discarded pile of candidates due to the 
increasing endpoint severity in early phase, resulting in an increasing number of projects that 
were terminated too early. He said that this is a natural consequence of this development 
model, and that smaller companies that lacked the resources to carry on with their 
development can discard that candidate, while another company can repurpose it in due time. 
With that, he contravenes a regular big-pharma approach, that is that companies would “sit” 
on non-developed candidates to prevent competitors on succeeding to develop a new product. 
He mentioned that it could be also due the risk of look “stupid” by not being able at 
developing something that another company refurbished into a success.

On the patent aspect, and regarding patent strategy for orphan drugs and the approach to look 
for several indications as DiMasi et al. (2016) has already explained, Mr. Hinsby  mentioned 
that the above mentioned approach is the one that orphan drugs for oncology diseases have 
used, whilst the non-oncology ones (or “generic”) have not “sliced” onto the potential several 
indications. He also discussed that the patent strategy is not always efficient, because once 
you obtain the orphan denomination by the regulatory authorities (FDA or EMA), they 
receive a period of exclusivity for each indication as soon as they enter on the market. 
However, that exclusivity is not always safe-proof, specially on off-label use (meaning use 
of the product for indications other-than the one approved by the regulatory entity) after the 
exclusivity for one indication has expired. He then referred to being used a “picket fence”13 
strategy as patent safeguard. He mentioned that these two aspects (namely orphan exclusivity 
and patent strategy) are their defense line (he mentioned a certain aversion to a company’s 
secret approach), but also try to have as much indications developed in parallel as possible. 
One big challenge the “generic” orphan drugs manufacture has is the potential number of 
patients. Mr. Hinsby referred that oncology orphan diseases can have a patient population 
ranging from 50,000 up to 200,000 per region, whilst the “generic” orphan diseases usually 
range from 1,000 up to 2,000 potential patients per region. This put in major threat small 
generic-orphan companies because, once they run out of exclusivity, they cannot compete 
commercially with the price of generic manufacturers for a market of 2,000 patients; generic 
manufacturers know that, and are eager to have ready generic products to out-compete 
companies on those niche markets; generally, they will also reach to a price equilibrium, 
allowing to manufacturers continue having a profitable business, though on a reduced price.  
The biological model has been shaping the new developments along with small-molecules 
on the terms of the concept “precision medicine”. A case he mentioned as clearly 
representative is the one of antibodies, which can be used to identify a very-specific wide 
range of targets on the surface of the cell; along with that, nowadays small-molecules also 
can be developed very precisely for targets inside the cell. He still sees advantages on the 
small-molecules approach (e.g. bioavailability). He considered that the marriage of 
biologicals and small-molecules was difficult in the past because it was very difficult to 
predict (based on “in silico” models or on big data) how they would behave. But he thinks 
that nowadays it is something that is certainly possible to do. Orphazyme must have this 
approach in mind to compete within his market, since most competitors also have a similar 
approach.
