Abstract
Pseudomonas aeruginosa is one of the most prevalent opportunistic pathogens in the cystic
ﬁbrosis lung environment. If not eradicated early on, this bacteria often evolve to become a
persistent coloniser of the lungs leading to chronic infection, eventually leading to lung failure
and death. A recent study of clonetype data from P. aeruginosa isolates has shown that strains
that were thought to be eradicated from the lungs after antibiotic treatment were persistent
strains that had gone undetected in routine follow-ups. In this project, ’true’ ﬁrst isolates of
persistent and eradicated strains of P. aeruginosa from 50+ young patients are analysed with
the aim to identify traits that might predict persistence. This was done in high-throughput
phenotypic assays analysing persister cell formation, adhesion, minimum inhibitory concentration,
and growth rate in three diﬀerent media. The data was then analysed using statistical tests and
logistic regression models. Large phenotypic diﬀerences in clonetypes were identiﬁed between and
within patients. High growth rate and low adhesion, along with low presence of Haemophilus spp.
in the lungs after P. aeruginosa isolation were identiﬁed as possible factors for higher persistence
in isolates.

Hyothesis and goal of this study
In cystic ﬁbrosis (CF) a defect mucociliary clearance leads to infections of opportunistic pathogens,
such as Pseudomonas aeruginosa (PA). Unsurprisingly, CF patients undergo intensive and long-
running antibiotic treatments in order to eradicate infecting pathogens. Infections by PA if not
eradicated may turn persistent in the lungs which leads to chronic infections eventually causing
lung failure and death. Few tools are available to predict which strains will ultimately persist in
CF patient lungs from initial detection of a particular bacterial strain.
In this study, we attempt to identify traits that may predict persistence in strains with the basis
of a hypothesis:
Initial phenotypic traits are diﬀerent between persistent strains and eradicated strains of PA
in Danish CF patients and can be used to predict persistence.
We investigate this hypothesis by high-throughput screening of a large collection of ﬁrst isolates
from the CF Centre at Rigshospitalet, Copenhagen, Denmark. Through statistical analysis and
logistic regression, signiﬁcance of the investigated traits will be assessed.

Introduction
The genetic disease cystic ﬁbrosis (CF) was ﬁrst
described in 1938 (1) and the gene behind the
disease CFTR was identiﬁed in 1989 (2). When
the disease was ﬁrst described patients usually
died in the ﬁrst year of their life. In the 80
years since, research and treatment has evolved
enough for patients to be able to expect to live
into their ﬁfties.
Therefore, CF has evolved
from being a serious illness in children to now
also being a serious disease among adults (2, 3).
In CF, a viscous mucus layer in the lungs
leads to infections by diﬀerent opportunistic mi-
croorganisms during the patient’s lifetime. One
of these is the highly adaptable bacterial species
Pseudomonas aeruginosa (PA); PA infections
can turn persistent and then over time chronic
where the bacterium is consistently cultured
from longitudinal airway samples over years of
infection. In many cases this leads to pulmonary
exacerbation for the patient and eventually lung
failure and death (4).
When PA is isolated for the ﬁrst time in a
CF patient, the goal of the clinic will be erad-
ication by antibiotic therapy to prevent devel-
opment of persistent and chronic infections (5).
Another goal is to avoid development of antibi-
otic resistance and other concerning pathogen
adaptions as the persistent pathogen is exposed
to repeated treatments and extended time in
the CF lung environment. However, few tools
are available to predict which strains will ul-
timately persist in the CF patient lungs from
initial detection of a particular bacterial strain.
1.1
Cystic ﬁbrosis
CF is a monogenic, autosomal recessive disease
most commonly found in Caucasian populations.
The incidence rate of CF changes from region
to region, with a rate of 1/353 in Ireland and
1/25,000 in Finland. In Northern and Central
Europe the average rate is 1/6000 (2). In 2019,
approximately 550 patients underwent monthly
care at the Danish CF centres in Copenhagen
and Aarhus (6).
In healthy individuals, particles and micro-
bial cells are removed from the upper airway
through a process called mucociliary clearance.
Pathogens are trapped in the airway surface
liquid, which consists of two layers: The mucus
layer on the top and the less viscous periciliary
liquid (PCL) layer at the bottom. The mucus
layer traps and inactivates inhaled pathogens,
and cilia on the epithelial cells then move the
mucus (7, 8). During maintenance of the lung
surface environment, the protein known as cys-
tic ﬁbrosis transmembrane conductance regulator
(CFTR) plays an important role as the con-
ductor of chloride transport in epithelial cells.
However, in individuals with CF, mutations in
the CFTR gene cause malfunction or loss-of-
function of CFTR.
This leads to dysfunction of the chloride ion
transport across epithelial cell surfaces resulting
in lower volumes of PCL, making the mucus
layer more dominant. Consequently, the airway
surface liquid becomes more viscous and less
movable. Thus, pathogens cannot be cleared as
eﬃciently in CF patients, facilitating bacterial
lung infections (4, 6, 9, 10).
To date, more than 2000 diﬀerent CFTR
mutations have been identiﬁed. A classiﬁcation
system has been set up, in which mutations are
divided into six classes according to their impact
on protein function. Classes I, II, and III are
regarded as the more severe mutations and are
associated with the classical form of CF while
classes IV, V and VI confer a milder phenotype
(2). Class I and Class II both lead to prema-
ture degradation of the protein by producing
defective and misfolded proteins, respectively.
Class III inactivates the protein by changing
ATP binding or hydrolysis domains (11, 12). In
Denmark, the CF population is characterised
by having the highest proportion of the Class
II mutation ∆F508 in the world (6).
1.1.1
Microbiome of the CF lung
As mentioned, mutations in the CFTR gene can
lead to a lung environment colonisable by oppor-
tunistic pathogens. Several of these pathogens
have been identiﬁed and studied for years while
others are so-called emerging pathogens. The
pathogens most commonly associated with CF
lung infection are PA, Staphylococcus aureus,
and species of the Burkholderia cepacia complex.
The emerging pathogens are Stenotrophomonas
maltophilia, Haemophilus inﬂuenzae and non-
tuberculosis Mycobacteria (9, 10, 13).
In a

on A. fumigatus because of its association with
worsened prognosis and pulmonary function (13,

1.1.2
Deﬁnitions in CF infections
When discussing infections in CF, particularly
PA infections, there are diﬀerent terms for the
nature of the infection: Chronic, intermittent,
and persistent. Aside from this, eradication is
used for describing successful treatment of an
infection and eradicating it completely. These
terms are applicable to other pathogens in the
CF lungs but in this project we focus on PA. The
basic clinical approach to infection monitoring
is to take airway samples (usually expectoration
of lung sputum by the patient or collection of
samples by bronchoalveolar lavage) and evalu-
ate for bacterial species presence on selective
agar plates. However, monitoring of CF patients
can vary signiﬁcantly between treatment centres.
Diﬀerent deﬁnitions have therefore been devel-
oped for each stage of infection. Two general
deﬁnition of chronic infections are used: the
Leeds and the Copenhagen criteria. The Leeds
criteria are based on 50% of airway samples
being positive for PA over a year where sam-
ples should be taken at least every 3 months.
As the Copenhagen CF Centre takes airway
samples on a monthly basis, the Copenhagen
chronic infection criteria are based on 6 months
of positive PA airway culture, but also consid-
ers increased antibody response or mucoidity of
cultures as a marker of chronicity. In intermit-
tent colonisation, PA is cultured erratically but
consistently from the next sputum sample af-
ter antibiotic treatment (5). Similar to chronic
infections, the term of eradication has diﬀer-
ent deﬁnitions with some calling for a negative
culture at one week after treatment and others
calling for three negative cultures in a 6-month
period. At the Copenhagen CF Centre, eradi-
cation is deﬁned as 6 months of PA-free airway
cultures. Common for these deﬁnitions is that
eradication presumes elimination of an organism
from the airways (17). Reaching a deﬁnition for
persistent infection is generally trickier than the
three others, as diﬀerent studies use diﬀerent
forms of the word. In much of the literature,
“chronic” and “persistent” are used interchange-
ably. To be clear in this study’s discussion of
persistence, we deﬁne persistent infections using
genomics as well as positive culture records and
consider three increasingly stringent classes of
persistence. This is based on implementation of
genomic analysis in the Copenhagen CF Centre
showing that in patients with ’eradication’ of PA
infection, the same clone type is re-cultured af-
ter the eradication period (more than 6 months
with no positive PA sample) in 43% of a 42
patient cohort (5). We focus on recurrence of
isolates of the same clone type, with the least
stringent category being recurrence of the same
clone type after an ‘eradication’ period or past
1 year since the ﬁrst isolation of that clone type,
regardless of the presence of other clone types.
We also use the term persistence in reference
to these persistent infections, which is diﬀerent
to the term persister phenotype, which refers
to small subpopulations of persister cells that
are able to survive high concentrations of an-
tibiotics, while the entire population does not.
These cells show very slow growth and once
the stressor is removed, they are able to revert
back to normal growth and then emerge (18, 19).

1.2
Pseudomonas aeruginosa
When entering the CF lung, the pathogen meets
a harsh environment with oxidative and nitro-
sative stresses, along with antibiotics and an
ever-changing environment due to airway inﬂam-
mation and extensive and irreversible structural
changes in the lung. This forces the pathogens
to adapt in order to survive and successfully in-
fect the lung surface. For PA, this might lead to
intermittent (but persistent) and then chronic
infections (4).
PA is a Gram-negative bacteria found ubiq-
uitously in soil and water. Aside from being one
of the most important opportunistic pathogens
in infections in CF patients, it also causes other
serious infections such as skin and soft tissue
infections, gastrointestinal infections, and recur-
rent urinary tract infections (3, 13, 20). It is
a serious concern for the immunocompromised,
and routinely causes nosocomial infections (21).
Chronic infection with PA is associated with
more rapid lung function decline and therefore
signiﬁcant morbidity and mortality. So, when
PA is isolated from a patient the infection will
be treated aggressively with antibiotics, such
as inhaled colistin or tobramycin and peroral
ciproﬂoxacin. Despite the treatment, eradica-
tion is not achieved in 10-40% of patients. The
prevalence of PA is generally low in children and
increases with the age of the patients. Some
studies have found that some children less than
a year old may become PA positive (9, 22–24).
One study, using samples from 86 patients, re-
ported a mean age of PA acquisition of 7.7 years,
ranging between 0.8 years and 24.1 years (25).
It has also been found that children with less
functional CFTR are more prone to early PA
colonisation (24).
1.2.1
Adaptation to the CF lung environ-
ment
Since PA is such a versatile bacterium found in
several moist environments, sources of PA infec-
tions in CF patients are plentiful. Not only are
the environmental sources a factor, also home
environments, hospitals, and cross-infection be-
tween patients and family members have been
identiﬁed as sources of infections. When talking
about initial infections, the strains are usually
unique, originating from the environment but
depending on frequency of contact between pa-
tients, transmission might occur (4, 24, 26). One
study found that 41% of CF patients with early
infections in their cohort had shared strains,
and one third of these were potentially between
patients (27) and another study identiﬁed three
possible transmission events with a cohort of 34
young CF patients (28). In the Danish CF Cen-
ter, recent studies have suggested that changes
in clinic cleaning policies and patient segregation
has resulted in most current initial infections
originating from the environment (6).
If the source of an infection by PA is the
environment, the bacteria will meet a harsh en-
vironment in which adaption is key for survival.
If adaption is successful, the new infection might
someday become chronic. In a study investigat-
ing the diﬀerences in groups of PA isolates, they
found that the chronic infections from CF pa-
tients were signiﬁcantly less diverse than those
of the other groups. The other groups contained
isolates from ﬁrst infections from CF patients,
urinary tract infections, chronic suppurative oti-
tis media, and intensive-care immunodeﬁciency
(26). This makes sense in that speciﬁc changes
in phenotypes and mutations have been associ-
ated with chronic infections. The most common
mutations occur in regulatory genes leading to
a wide range of changes in the expression of
genes. These are loss of motility, loss of viru-
lence factors, slow growth, antibiotic resistance,
change in metabolic proﬁle, bioﬁlm formation,
and overproduction of alginate, leading to a
mucoid phenotype. Bioﬁlm and the mucoid phe-
notype are both regarded as strong markers of
chronic infections (4, 24, 25, 29, 30). A recent
large study of strain adaptation using longitu-
dinal airway isolates from young Danish CF
patients conﬁrmed many of these ﬁndings at a
much earlier stage of infection. It showed that
most early isolates of a PA strain exhibit naïve
characteristics: a fast growth rate comparable
to model laboratory strains (in in vitro condi-
tions), high antibiotic susceptibility, and low
adherence ability (adherence ability is linked
to bioﬁlm formation). Within 2 years of lung

1.2.2
Bioﬁlm and mucoidity
Bioﬁlms are communities of bacteria imbed-
ded in a self-produced matrix of exopolysac-
charides, DNA, proteins, and lipids. Production
of bioﬁlms is often regulated by quorum sensing
and depends on nutrient availability and energy
sources present in the environment (32). In PA,
the most important components of the bioﬁlm
are the polysaccharides alginate, Pel, Psl, and
extracellular DNA (33). Strains isolated from
the CF lung predominantly produce alginate for
their bioﬁlm, while strains found in any other en-
vironment than the CF lung produce Pel and Psl
instead. Both Pel and Psl have been found to be
regulated by quorum sensing (34–36) while the
main players in regulation of alginate produc-
tion are the proteins MucA and AlgU, although
quorum sensing has also been shown to regulate
alginate production through other pathways (4,
37).
Alginate overproduction is often caused by
mutations in mucA, which encodes an anti-sigma
factor regulating the alternative sigma factor en-
coded by algU, σ22. With no MucA to sequester
σ22, the sigma factor will upregulate the algD
operon, resulting in alginate production. Aside
from this, σ22 will upregulate the responses to
heat shock, osmotic stress, and oxidative stress
along with downregulating several virulence fac-
tors, such as ﬂagella, pili, the type III secretion
system, and Rhl quorum sensing (4).
The beneﬁt of switching to the mucoid phe-
notype seems to be related to protection of the
cell envelope against the environment. For ex-
ample, alginate producing bioﬁlms are more
resistant to both antibiotics and to phagocytic-
mediated killing (3, 38).
In later stages of
chronic infections, some strains may revert to
a non-mucoid phenotype. This might suggest
that the production of alginate has a high energy
cost, and if no selective pressure is put on the
bacteria, the production will cease (39, 40). In a
review by Folkesson et. al., speculation was put
forward as to whether the mucoid phenotype in
itself is important for survival in the CF lung
environment or if it is a by-product of muta-
tions in mucA leading to upregulation of other
favourable systems, such as the cell envelope
stress response (4).
1.2.3
Resistance
The versatility and adaptability of PA is widely
known to be extraordinary, and can be attributed
to its complex and large genome of 5-7 Mb. The
bacteria can develop resistance to a wide range
of antibiotics by combination of both intrinsic,
acquired, and adaptive resistances (41).
The intrinsic resistance of PA provides a low
basal susceptibility to several antibiotic groups.
The primary driver is the low permeability of the
outer membrane, while inducible cephalospori-
nase and constitutive or inducible expression of
eﬄux pumps also confer low susceptibility to
several classes of antibiotics. Speciﬁcally, the
constitutive expression of MexAB-OprM pro-
vides low susceptibility to most β-lactams and
ﬂuoroquinolones (41–43).
PA can acquire new antibiotic resistance
genes through a number of mechanisms. Through
horizontal gene transfer plasmids, transposons,
integrons, and prophages are all able to carry
resistance genes from one bacteria to another.
Transferal of aminoglycoside and β-lactam re-
sistance between bacteria have been reported
through integrons and plasmids (43).
Although mobile genetic elements are impor-
tant, mutational changes are the main driver
of development of resistance in PA. These can
cause reduced antibiotic uptake, modiﬁcations
to antibiotic targets, and overexpression of eﬄux
pumps and antibiotic-inactivating enzymes (3,
43). In this project, ciproﬂoxacin has been used
for the assays. Resistance against ciproﬂoxacin
and other ﬂuoroquinolones are thought to be
driven by mutations altering the antibiotic tar-
get - the gyrase. More speciﬁcally, resistance-
inducing mutations tend to occur in the genes
gyrA and gyrB. Furthermore, mutations in the
eﬄux pump repressor nfxB and topoimerase IV
are also theorised as a cause of ciproﬂoxacin
resistance (31).

1.3
The persister phenotype
The third category of resistance is adaptive re-
sistance. Adaptive resistances are the ones that
are dependent on stimulus from the outside. In
these cases, outside stimulus causes a change in
gene and protein expression that is reversible
when the stimulus is gone. One such stimulus
is bioﬁlm (See Section 1.2.2) and the other is
the presence of persister cells. Persister cells
are, as mentioned in Section 1.2, cells within a
population that are tolerant of antibiotics, while
the majority of the population is not (18, 19).
Several possibilities for the mechanisms under-
lying formation of persister cells have been put
forward, but common for all of them is that for-
mation is often triggered by an outside stress re-
sulting in heterogeneously dormant cells within
the genetically identical population. Most com-
monly this stress is starvation, but oxidative
stress has also been mentioned. Stringent re-
sponse is one of the more popular proposals
to what mechanisms might be behind persister
formation. In stringent response, the alarmone
(p)ppGpp prevents DNA replication and tran-
scription, resulting in slower growth. Another
popular suggestion is the use of toxin-antitoxin
systems such as the HigB/HigA system which
has been shown to be necessary for ciproﬂoxacin
induced persister formation in PA (18, 19, 43,
44). Much research about persister formation
has been done in Escherichia coli, where some
reliable causative mutations have been charac-
terised, but much is still yet to be discovered
about persisters in PA. However, there is no
doubt about the importance of research in per-
sister formation. One high throughput longitu-
dinal study of 460 isolates from 39 young CF
patients showed the presence of high-persisters
(deﬁned by survival of at least 75% of replicates)
in 22 patients with 19% of the isolates being
high-persisters based on regrowth of at least 3
of 4 replicate cultures after treatment with more
than 100x the MIC for ciproﬂoxacin (45).
1.4
Context of this project
Persistent and chronic infections pose a great
threat to CF patients. Several studies have set
out to identify phenotypes and genotypes which
may predict what strains could lead to failure
of eradication, and the establishment of persis-
tent and chronic infections. A limiting factor
has been the ability to conﬁrm that a strain is
in fact a new colonisation to negate the bias of
prior exposure to and adaptation within the lung
environment. While many studies of longitudi-
nal adaptation in CF exist, fewer have focused
on initial isolates and their persistence as con-
ﬁrmed by both culture records and genotyping
of follow-up isolates. Recently, the adaption
of whole genome sequencing have provided re-
searchers with a wider toolbox for studying PA
infections by being able to tell infections from
one another at clonetype level. A study found
that in patients with infections that had been
pronounced eradicated (See Section 1.1.2 for
deﬁnition of eradication) in the clinic may still
carry the same clone type as this ‘eradicated’
isolate, without it being detected by the clinic in
sputum samples. This means that ‘ﬁrst isolates’
or ‘initial isolates’ in some studies collected in a
patient after a lengthy eradication period may
still be strains that have been surviving since
the last PA positive sample culture and thus
have adapted. This will have an impact in the
way the infection should be treated (5).
Thankfully, studies focused on isolates from
young children have a reduced likelihood of bi-
ased ‘ﬁrst isolates’. Several such studies have
connected the mucoid phenotype with eradica-
tion failure. One study found that 50% of the
mucoid ﬁrst isolates in patients below the age
of 6 were eradicated, in contrast to the 77% of
the non-mucoid isolates in other patients in the
same study (46). Another study associated the
typical characteristics of chronic infections with
eradication failure as well (22). A third study
with a cohort of mixed ﬁrst isolates and non-
ﬁrst isolates identiﬁed wrinkly colony surface
and irregular colony edges as associated with
eradication failure (47).
One of the studies that inspired this project
was conducted by Tramper-Stranders et. al..
Here, the authors analysed virulence factors of
initial isolates in order to characterise eradi-
cated and persistent strains. In this study, the
isolates used were ﬁrst isolates, many of which
were the very ﬁrst isolates of PA in a patient
ever, others being the ﬁrst isolate detected af-
ter at least 1 year free of PA. The phenotypes
that were analysed were focused on virulence
factors, such as swimming motility and twitch-
==PAGE:12==
Page 11 of 44
ing motility. The deﬁnition of persistence used
was based on failure to eradicate after several
treatment cycles. Using these factors, it was not
possible to identify any characteristics of the iso-
lates that could predict failure to eradicate (25).
As mentioned previously, in a study analysing
evolutionary trajectories in isolates from initial
colonisation to persistent infections, new pheno-
types of interest were identiﬁed that included
‘lifestyle’ traits in the isolates. It was found that
adhesion and aggregation did not consistently
correlate with colonisation time, though a sub-
set of adapted isolates did show high adhesion
ability. This was surprising, considering the gen-
eral understanding of bioﬁlm being beneﬁcial
to persistence. Furthermore, growth rate and
ciproﬂoxacin showed cross-patient convergence,
suggesting that these two traits have an impor-
tance in adaption to the CF lung environment.
Modelling also showed combinatorial adaptive
paths to persistence, and an especially clear
diﬀerence between the multi-trait phenotypes
of multi-year adapted isolates compared to the
20 naïve isolates conﬁrmed to be the ﬁrst ever
PA isolated from a patient’s lungs (31). In this
study, we characterise the naïve adaptive state
for an additional 58 ﬁrst isolates, phenotyping
all 78 isolates using an improved high through-
put approach detailed in the methods with the
aim of mapping the phenotypic characteristics
of a much larger initial strain population, and
evaluating whether any trait diﬀerences within
this uniform population can serve as predictors
of strain persistence.
In this project, we attempt to characterise
phenotypes in the ﬁrst ever isolates of PA that
might predict future persistence in young CF
patients. Seeing as these isolates are the ﬁrst
ever detected PA isolates, they should not have
adapted to the CF lung environment yet, pro-
viding us with naïve strains not seen before.
Building from the newly identiﬁed phenotypes
of interest, we run high-throughput assays on
the isolates, investigating the growth rates in dif-
ferent media, adhesion, ciproﬂoxacin resistance,
and persister cell formation and compare them
to persistence calls within one, two and three
years based on the presence of speciﬁc clone-
types sampled from patients. We think these
three factors (ﬁrst ever isolates, new phenotypes
of interest, and three-fold follow-up on persis-
tence of isolates) will improve our understanding
of the characteristics of early isolates and our
power to predict persistence.

2.2
Creation of cryo replicates
In total, 5 biological replicate plates were pro-
duced and for each biological plate replicate,
three technical plate replicates were produced.
This totalled to 15 96-well microtiter plates in
cryostock. The biological replicate plates were
named BR1-5 and each of the technical repli-
cates were assigned a letter ABC. So, the ﬁrst
plate produced was BR1A and the last was
BR5C. BR1-4 were used in the assays while the
BR5 plates were used as back-ups.
Common for all plates were the positions
of the controls: 6 blanks, 6 PAO1 and 6 PA14.
The design is seen in Figure 2 with the grey
squares corresponding to the positions of the
blanks, light blue to the positions of PAO1 and
dark blue to the positions of PA14. The white
squares symbolise the positions of the strains in
the collection. For each biological replicate, a
new randomised order was used. (See Appendix
B for the randomised designs of the biological
replicates).
When creating the cryo plates, each isolate
was re-streaked from its cryo stock onto an agar
plate and incubated O/N at 37◦C. A single
colony from each plate was then picked using a
P100 pipette tip and inoculated at its assigned
randomised position in a 96-well deep-well plate.
The deep-well plate was then incubated O/N at
37◦C. After incubation, the OD of slow-growing
isolates was corrected by up-concentrating to an
OD630 between 0.3 and 0.5 (75 µl volume) by
spinning the overnight culture down in a cold
(4◦C) microcentrifuge and resuspending (This
was performed for two consistently slow growing
isolates). The culture was then mixed in equal
amounts with 50% glycerol to a ﬁnal concentra-
tion of 25% glycerol and a volume of 150 µL per
well in a 96-well microtiter plate. All replicates
were sealed using a sterile alum-seal for 96-well
microtiter plates and stored at -80◦C.
When an assay was to be run, the replicate
plate was taken out of the freezer and stamped
onto a square LB agar plate using a 96-well
metal spot replicator (cleaned by brushing and
then sterilised in an ethanol bath and burn-oﬀ
between uses). The plate was then incubated at
37◦C for 16 hours to let the colonies grow large
enough to use but not large enough to interact
with each other.

Simple phenotypes
Simple phenotypes such as mucoidity, pigmenta-

2.4
Growth assays
Three diﬀerent growth assays were run: One
using Luria-Bertani (LB) medium, one using
Synthetic CF sputum Medium (SCFM) (48),
and one using M9 minimal salt medium with 20
mM glucose (MM) (49). The method was the
same for each of the assays only diﬀering in the
medium used.
Using a 96-well autoclavable pin replicator,
the stamped colonies from the replicate plates
were transferred into a 96-well microtiter plate
containing 40 µL media and diluted.
Then,
using the same pin tool the dilution was trans-
ferred into 150 µL media in a second 96-well
microtiter plate. This plate was then put in
an ELx808 Absorbance Reader (BioTek Instru-
ments, Winooski, VT, USA) microplate reader
and incubated at 37◦C at slow shaking speed
for 20-24 hours. The reader was set to read
once every ten minutes at 630 nm. Additionally,
isolates that did not grow in the MM Glucose
medium were noted as ’Auxotrophic’ (50).
2.4.1
Growth rate analysis software
To calculate the growth rates and generation
times of the isolates, an R script was used.
This script was modelled after a sliding win-
dow method (51).

the entire growth curve of the replicate it is
analysing. Using a window of 3 or 5 data points,
the programme parses over the growth curves,
moving one data point at a time. For each win-
dow, the slope is calculated and saved. When all
slopes have been calculated, the highest slope is
designated as the maximum growth rate. From
this, the generation time is calculated.
For all calculations of growth rates, a window
of 3 data points was used. These windows were
selected to minimise erratic ﬁts. All growth ﬁts

Minimum eradication concentration
assays
To determine a minimum eradication concentra-
tion (MEC) for the isolates, the antibiotic plates
from the inhibition assay was used further. Af-

2.7
Adhesion assays
Adhesion was estimated by incubating the repli-
cates in 96-well plates in 150 µL LB using NUNC
peg lids instead of standard plate lids. They
were incubated at 37◦for 20 h.
After incubation, the peg lids were washed
with 180 ul 1X M9 salts to remove non-adhering
cells. The peg lids were then transferred to a
microtiter plate containing 160 µL 0.01% crystal
violet (CV) and left to stain for 15 minutes.
After staining, the lids were washed three
times in three individual washing plates with
180 µL 1X M9 salts. Finally, the peg lid was
put in a 96-well plate containing 180 µL 99%
ethanol and left for 10 minutes for the adhering
CV stained cells to detach from the peg lid.
The ﬁnal plate along with the original cul-
ture plate were used for measuring OD of the
plates, at 590 nm to detect crystal violet and 630
nm to detect planktonic growth respectively.
To determine adhesion, the OD of the CV
was divided by the OD of the culture plates.
2.8
Persister cell assay
Using a 96-pin replicator to pick up colonies
from the square solid LB agar stamped plates,
a 96-well microtiter plate containing 150 µL LB
was inoculated. The plate was incubated stati-
cally at 37◦for 24 h until the bacteria reached
stationary phase.
To determine initial viability of the bacteria
in each well, a replicator was used to stamp the
wells onto solid agar. Ciproﬂoxacin was added
to the plate to a concentration of 100 µg/mL.
The plate was then incubated statically for 20-24
h at 37◦C.
The treated plate was diluted to 10−1, 10−2,
10−3, and 10−4 in 96-well microtiter plates con-
taining 1xM9 salts using the same automated
ﬂuid handling robot as in Section 2.5.
Each dilution was stamped onto solid LB
agar using a replicator. Approximately 0.5 µL
was transferred with the replicator. The plates
were incubated at 37◦for 48 h. Visible colonies
were counted at the 24 h and 48 h mark and
noted down. Isolates were classiﬁed as persister-
forming isolates if at least 2/4 replicates regrew
from 10−1 or higher dilution culture.
An isolate was recorded as having the persis-
ter phenotype, if at least 50% of the replicates
produced persister cells.
2.9
Data analysis
All collected data was compiled into a data
frame, along with patient IDs, clonetypes, and
persistence calls for each isolate. For the per-
sistence cells, four categories were used: if the
isolate recurs at least once after ﬁrst isolation,
the isolate is determined to be persistent (Y).
If samples from patients has had other clone-
types than the one recorded as ﬁrst isolate or no
PA cultures, the isolate will be assigned as non-
persistent (N). One group are unknown, where
follow up PA isolates have not been sequenced,
so it is not certain if the isolate has recurred
(unk). Last, some strains need a more extended
follow-up period for determination of persistence
within a given time frame (i.e. the patient has
only been followed for a year after its ﬁrst PA, so
persistence calls for the 2 year and 3 year cate-
gories are impossible), so the category cannot be
determined (NA). Furthermore, three diﬀerent
calls have been used: persistent after the ﬁrst
==PAGE:17==
Page 16 of 44
eradication period or 1 year (whichever occurs
ﬁrst), recurring after 2 years, and recurring after
3 years.
All pre-processing and analysis of data was
run in R (version 3.6.3).
Data was grouped
according to their persistence call in the three
diﬀerent classes. A Wilcox-test was run on the
continuous data while a χ2 test was run on the
categorical data. A principal component anal-
ysis (PCA) was run on the growth rates in LB
and SCFM, adhesion data, MIC, and MEC data.
In the end, univariate and multivariate logistic
regression models were set up. Univariate mod-
els were also run on co-cultured pathogens data
from the clinic (Data on presence of Staphylo-
coccus and Haemophilus).

Results
The collection contained 78 isolates from 72 pa-
tients. The isolates used in this project are all
available and veriﬁable ﬁrst isolates from the
Copenhagen CF Centre from 2002 until 2018.
Follow-up data for the isolates diﬀer as some
patients have persistence calls for all three cat-
egories used, while other patients simply have
not been followed for two or three years as of
yet. This means that when analysing the data,
gradually more isolates will switch to the un-
known and not available categories, which will
also be visible in the plots in this section. In
Table 2 an overview of the number of persis-
tent, non-persistent, unknown and not available
isolates in each of the persistence datasets is

3.1
A reusable cryo-collection
To study the collection, randomised libraries in
96-well microtiter plates were created. This was
done to improve our high-throughput screening
platform and make these libraries useful in fu-
ture experiments as well. Due to the origin of
the isolates in the collection, an additional eﬀort
was put into the reusability of the libraries and
reproducibility of the assays. This was done by
randomising the replicates on the 96-well plates,
so plate eﬀects (such as possible edge eﬀects)
would play a smaller role in the variability of the
isolates. Aside from the randomisation, the OD
was measured for the plates of each library to
ensure that cell density in the wells were in the
same range across all wells. Initial cell density
has been shown to have an impact on maximal
growth rate (53), which only adds to the impor-
tance of normalising the OD in the cryo-libraries.
Evaluation of the reproducibility of assays using
the libraries was done by calculating the stan-
dard deviation of growth rates in LB for the
controls, PAO1 and PA14, both of which were
positioned on the edges as well as in the middle
of the plate (See Section 2.2 for explanation).
After several rounds of optimisation, the growth
rates of PAO1 and PA14 were assessed to de-
termine if the observed growth rates matched
the expected growth rates for the two controls.
As the growth rates corresponded passably, it
was decided to move forward with further phe-
notypic assays. Furthermore, the goal of having
created reusable cryo-libraries with all strains
growing reliably had been achieved with pass-
able variability in the controls.
For further control of the results attained in
this study, the MIC of 25 of the isolates (includ-
ing PAO1 and PA14) were compared to MICs
determined by E-test from a prior study (31),
performed in Mueller-Hinton media. 6/25 of the
reported MICs in this study do not correspond
to the E-test results. The 2 were were too low,
while 4 were exceeded by E-test MICs. Given
the diﬀerences in method and media, we assessed
this was an acceptable outcome of our high
throughput approach. Deviant strains should
be followed up on in the future by re-assessing

4
Discussion
This is one of the largest studies of ’true’ ﬁrst iso-
lates with follow-up data that also incorporates
whole genome analysis to deﬁne clonal persis-
tence. The collection consists of all available
ﬁrst isolates from a single CF centre’s cohort
with infection persistence assessed for 62 pa-
tients. We identiﬁed multi-patient clonetypes
that showed unexpected variation in isolates
from diﬀerent patients. This study did not iden-
tify any stand-out predictive traits that were
consistent across all persistence classes. How-
ever, adhesion and growth rate in LB did prove
to have potential for further investigation.
4.1
Patients
Of the patients we were capable of calling in
persistent past eradication or 1 year, 31/62 pa-
tients were categorised as persistent. This ﬁnd-
ing diﬀers from other studies where lower rates
of failure of eradication has been reported. In
one such paper, 69% of patients achieved erad-
ication. In this study, eradication was deﬁned
as PA-negative cultures for 12 months after ini-
==PAGE:26==
4.2
Traits
Page 25 of 44
tial antipseudomonal therapy (57). Such a high
proportion of persistent infections in patients
is worth noting since even if some isolates do
not survive past 2 or 3 years, they have still
survived treatment and an eradication period
of 6 months.
The three diﬀerent persistence categories
used in this study were separated into persistent
past eradication or 1 year, persistent past 2 years,
and persistent past 3 years. The category based
on persistence past eradication or 1 year was
created on a desire to be able to account for all
isolates in one persistence category while the two
categories based on 2 and 3 years were possible
because of available follow-up data. This follow-
up data is currently not available for all isolates
but increasingly more follow-up data will be for
future studies.
Even if the availability of follow-up data
provides the opportunity for further study of the
isolates and their persistence, it also exposes a
risk in these categories. The persistence datasets
are more likely to have false negatives than false
positives as we do not know if the presumed non-
persistent isolates might resurface in later follow-
ups on the patients (5). If they were to reemerge,
that would mean that the current persistence
call is false which is something to take into
account in later studies using these datasets.
However, no similar study has a solution to this
problem and we at least have a high frequency
of sample collection and sequencing attempts to
resolve this.
4.2
Traits
The traits found to be most important in pre-
dicting of future persistence in ﬁrst isolates were
adhesion and growth rate in LB. When looking
at the continuous data of the growth rates, the
boxplots eluded to an advantage of a ﬁrst iso-
late being faster growing than the others in LB.
Historically in CF, slow growth rate has been
a typical trait of chronic and persistent infec-
tions with PA , so it is surprising to ﬁnd that
the faster growing ﬁrst isolates might have an
advantage (31). Because of slow growth rate
being important in the later stages of infection,
studies in PA infection of CF patients have gen-
erally focused on growth rate in this perspective.
This study suggests that a fast growth rate also
might have a role to play in persistent infections
of CF patients. One thought is that a faster
growth rate might be an advantage in competi-
tion with other infecting pathogens in the CF
lung, enabling the initially faster growing iso-
lates to survive past the second and third year
of infection.
Another trait that has been identiﬁed as
possibly important in predicting persistent in-
fections is adhesion. For the persistence dataset
persistent past eradication or 1 year, the persis-
tent isolates were signiﬁcantly less adhesive than
the non-persistent isolates while it evens out in
the later follow-ups. Adhesion in PA is driven
by ﬂagella and pili (58), which are virulence fac-
tors known to be downregulated in later stages
of infection by the same alternative σ factor
that upregulates alginate production, causing
the mucoid phenotype (4). Of the three mucoid
isolates in this collection, one has low adhesion
while the other two are average compared to the
rest of the collection. As this study does not
analyse the genomic data of the isolates, future
studies might check the genes encoding ﬂagella,
pili, or the alternative σ factor or test gene ex-
pression in order to possibly ﬁnd an explanation
for this ﬁnding.
A third trait that is interesting to discuss is
the persister phenotype. In this study, 22/78 iso-
lates have the persister phenotype, correspond-
ing to 28% of ﬁrst isolates in this collection
producing persister cells. Isolates may adapt to
form persisters over time, as has been shown in
other studies (45,59), but the high amount of
persister formation in these isolates is surprising.
This may in part be attributed to the method of
recording persister cell forming isolates. With
4 replicates, 2 were needed for the isolate to be
assigned as persister forming.
4.3
Multi-trait interactions
In this study, we have investigated the associa-
tion between three traits: growth rates, sensitiv-
ity to ciproﬂoxacin, and persister cell formation
(See Figure 5). Despite the ﬁndings of earlier
studies, which indicate that slow growth, re-
sistance and persister cell formation is linked
(60-63), here we ﬁnd that slow growth could be
linked to high sensitivity and no formation of
persisters. When discussing the fast, average,
==PAGE:27==
4.4
Clonetype-patient interactions
Page 26 of 44
and slow growing isolates, it is important to keep
in mind how these three groups are deﬁned: by
the 33rd and 66th percentiles. This means that
the slowest fast growing isolate and the fastest
average growing isolate are very close to each
other. The same goes for the slowest average
isolate and the fastest slow isolate. In an eﬀort
to take this caveat into account, the data was
checked manually to see if the high sensitivity
isolates were gathered in one end of the slow
growing group. They were not. Furthermore,
the two resistant isolates that were found in
this study are fast growing in LB and average
growing in SCFM, which also goes against the
earlier associations. One limitation of the MIC
results is the low amount of highly sensitive and
resistant isolates. This inequality in the distribu-
tions may very well have an eﬀect on the results
of this test. Therefore, a larger collection of ﬁrst
isolates would likely contribute to a better test
of the association than what was achieved here.
4.4
Clonetype-patient interactions
Among the 78 isolates in this project, 50 dif-
ferent clonetypes have been assigned. Of these
50 clonetypes, 11 are present in more than one
patient (See Table 5) with DK54 having been
isolated from 7 diﬀerent patients. Interestingly,
for some of these multi-patient clonetypes, the
phenotypes of the isolates are very diﬀerent.
For example, looking at the DK54 isolates, two
isolates stand out: F076 which is the only persis-
tent isolate among the DK54s (persisting past 2
years, with data for the third year not available)
but this isolate was the only isolate that was
consistently unable to grow in SCFM - which is
a medium speciﬁcally made to mimic CF spu-
tum - and was also one of the 5 auxotrophic
isolates unable to grow in Glucose MM. The
other isolate that stands out in the group of
DK54 is F018. This isolate is the only mucoid
isolate that is not a DK06 and is also the least
adhesive of the DK54s.
Another interesting group of isolates is the
clonetype DK06. Here, we have four isolates
spread across 3 patients. Between the four iso-
lates, great diversity is observed with a wide
spectrum of growth rates in SCFM, while growth
rates in LB are similar save for one isolate. Two
of the collection’s three mucoid isolates are in
this group. Most interesting about this group,
however, are the isolates F055 and F056 that
are both from the same patient. These isolates
both persist past 3 years and are both persister
cell forming. This is where the similarities in
phenotypes stop between the two isolates. F055
is slow growing in both LB and SCFM and com-
pletely unable to grow in Glucose MM, while
F056 maintains the same growth rate in LB and
SCFM and is in the average group in growth
in Glucose MM. Furthermore, F056 is the third
mucoid isolate in this collection.
These dramatic diﬀerences within interpa-
tient and intrapatient clonetypes raise questions
as to how this can be. Earlier studies with some
of the same isolates as in this project have sug-
gested possible transmissions between patients
for three clonetypes (28). Another inﬂuencing
factor could be the conditions within the CF
lung environment such as the presence of other
pathogens and severity of disease. The preva-
lence of PA has been found to correlate inversely
with the incidence of S. aureus in the CF lungs
(64). A French study found a range of factors
that they correlated with a consistent lack of PA
colonisation. One of them was more frequent de-
tection of Heamophilus inﬂuenzae (65). In this
study, it was found that less frequent isolates of
Haemophilus spp. was associated with persis-
tence of PA in patients, corresponding with the
ﬁndings that the French study made. Another
study demonstrated that niches in the lung of
CF patients may lead to clonally related PA
evolving diﬀerently and therefore diﬀering in
phenotypes (66). This could be a suggestion as
to why there is such a large diﬀerence between
the two DK06 isolates from the same patient.
Genomic studies of the isolates may be the ﬁrst
step to ﬁnding out exactly what the diﬀerences
within the clonetypes might be. This could be
done by investigating speciﬁc genes that are as-
sociated with the phenotypes that the isolates
diﬀer in or the expression of these genes and
their diﬀerences.
4.5
Logistic regression models
In the logistic regressions in this project, growth
rate in LB, adhesion, and amount of Haemophilus
spp. positive cultures were found to be signif-
icant when trying to predict persistence and
==PAGE:28==
Page 27 of 44
non-persistence. The univariate models of these
three all came out signiﬁcant, however the OR
and their 95% CIs for LB growth rates were
generally very high in both the univariate and
multivariate models, making them unreliable.
The same was not true for adhesion and pres-
ence of Haemophilus. The high ORs for LB
growth rate can be attributed to the nature of
this metric and the deﬁnition of ORs. ORs de-
cribe the odds of an outcome if the variable has
a one-unit increase. Growth rates in this project
range from 0.372 h−1 to 1.4148 h−1, meaning
that a one-unit increase of the slowest growing
isolate would move it up to be one of the fastest
growing isolates in the entire collection. When
considering this, the high ORs are not very sur-
prising but they are also not very precise - which
is also reﬂected in the large CIs of the ORs.
None of the multivariate logistic regressions
in this project came out signiﬁcant. These ex-
planatory variables were chosen on the basis of
suggestions of relevance from previous studies
(5,60-63). As these models were not signiﬁcant,
no attempts were made with more than these
ﬁve variables. We leave the rest of the optional
variables for future work.
5
Limitations and future perspec-
tives
This study used a large collection of conﬁrmed
ﬁrst isolates of PA from CF young patients.
Using a high-throughput setting, the isolates
were assayed in an array of diﬀerent traits in
order to identify phenotypes that might predict
eradication or persistence.
Choices made in
design of the study lead to limitations that must
be accounted for in further studies.
The logistic regressions could beneﬁt from
more isolates.
The lack of signiﬁcance in the
multivariate models could be the result of a
limitation in the collection itself. Even if the
collection is a large collection of conﬁrmed ﬁrst
isolates, it may not be large enough to identify
speciﬁc traits that can predict future persistence.
A larger collection may be needed to deﬁnitively
identify traits in initial isolates that may predict
which will persist and which will be eradicated.
This could be done by collecting initial isolates
from other CF centres than the one at Rigshos-
pitalet. Another expansion of the collection will
come in time when further follow-up is carried
out on the patients and the unknown and not
available isolates are sampled, sequenced, and
the respective isolates are designated as persis-
tent or non-persistent in all three categories. A
power analysis to determine the needed sample
size may also be a small but helpful addition to
the statistics of this project.
Genomic data could add to understanding of
diﬀerences in clonetypes.
This study did not
touch upon genomic diﬀerences in the isolates
apart from the pre-determined clonetypes. The
isolates have been sequenced and clonetypes
have been determined in previous work (28,45)
but due to time constraints, this study did not
examine the sequences. This presents a limita-
tion as further investigation in diﬀerences be-
tween the persistent and non-persistent isolates
were not possible. In the future, a "pangenome"
for the entire collection can be set up. Further-
more, pangenomes for the persistent and non-
persistent groups in each persistence dataset
can be set up to possibly identify diﬀerences
in the groups. Aside from this, the diﬀerences
in supposedly closely related isolates can be ex-
amined further, and possible diﬀerences in the
genome might explain why their phenotypes are
as diﬀerent as they are even if they have the
same clonetype.
Assays in laboratory setting do not always
reﬂect real conditions
A limitation that mi-
crobiologists always must keep in mind when
working with organisms from samples is that
in vitro experiments and assays in controlled
laboratory settings do not always reﬂect what
happens in vivo. In a study of the PA tran-
scriptome, it was found that several pathways
including the primary quorum sensing system
has signiﬁcantly lower expression in human infec-
tion than in laboratory conditions. Furthermore,
genes known to confer antibiotic resistance had
higher expression in human infection than in
laboratory conditions (67). In an attempt to
negate this limitation, SCFM has been used in
this project to simulate the nutrients of the CF
sputum. Microﬂuidic systems have been devel-
oped to mimic the CF airways (68) and may be
==PAGE:29==
Page 28 of 44
another option for simulating the CF airways
more precisely.
Scope of assays could be expanded to raise
chance of identifying interesting phenotype.
When looking at one of the studies that in-
spired this project, Tramper-Stranders et. al.
tried to identify characteristics of eradicated
and persistent isolates in a collection of ﬁrst
isolates (25). The two most notable diﬀerences
between the study by Tramper-Stranders and
the present study lies with the collection used
and the phenotypes tested for (See Section 1.4).
The phenotypes they examined were linked to
virulence factors, while the phenotypes we have
examined are more ’life-style’ phenotypes. In
further studies, a combination of the two groups
of traits could be added to the traits examined.
The study by Tramper-Stranders et. al. exam-
ines swimming and twitching motility, which - if
added to the present study - could be compared
to the adhesion assay run in this study, as both
ﬂagella and pili are a part of these traits (58).
Furthermore, additional replicates of the assays
run in this project will add to the reliability of
the results.

6
Conclusion
This study has used high-throughput methods for analysing a large collection of ’true’ ﬁrst isolates
of PA from young Danish CF patients. With the data collected, we tried to determine if initial
phenotypic traits are diﬀerent between persistent strains and eradicated strains and in that case,
which ones. This was done using pairwise statistical tests and setting up logistic regressions -
both multivariate and univariate.
This study identiﬁed multi-patient clonetypes and multi-clonetype patients. Among these
clonetypes great variation was observed despite the isolates being clonal. Future studies could
investigate these diﬀerences by analyses of the genomes and transcriptomes of these isolates to
identify diﬀerences that may lead to diﬀerences in phenotypes.
Although the multivariate models did not confer any signiﬁcance, the univariate models and
statistical tests did. Here, three variables were signiﬁcant: growth rate in LB, adhesion and
amount of Haemophilus positive cultures 1 year after PA isolation. Even if growth rate in LB was
found to be signiﬁcant the ORs were not very precise. Adhesion and presence of Haemophilus
did not have the same unprecise ORs and were both related to less odds of persistence. However,
none of the identiﬁed traits were consistent across all three persistence categories. Nevertheless,
they may be subject for further investigation in the future. Expansion of the collection with more
follow-up on the isolates whose persistence has not been determined yet, addition of more isolates,
and possibly addition of more phenotypes such as virulence factors may give rise to stronger
models to either conﬁrm or reject the ﬁndings of this study.
