Abstract
This thesis focuses on the strategy for deploying electrolyzer stacks to maximize power dispatch
in a Power-to-X system for ammonia synthesis, powered by renewable energy sources (RES)
like solar and wind. It compares the performance of two electrolyzer models, alkaline and PEM,
focusing on their ability to handle fluctuating renewable power inputs. The study also explores
the operation of ammonia synthesis under flexible operating conditions, optimizing the inte-
gration of electrolyzers and required subsystems like air separation units and separation cycle
to properly operate the system. By analyzing different configurations and geographical loca-
tions, the thesis provides insights into maximizing hydrogen production, storage, and ammonia
synthesis eﬀiciency while addressing technical challenges in dynamic RES environments.

Introduction
1.1
Problem Definition
The problem with renewable energy sources lies in their variability and volatility, which do not
ensure a stable and constant load needed by the power consumption side.
In recent years, energy storage has become a hot topic as a solution to manage the surplus
energy coming from infrastructures like PV and Wind Farms. Aside from batteries, which still
represent a significant cost, green hydrogen has attracted considerable attention from both the
public and private sectors.
Green hydrogen is produced through the electrolysis process, which splits water into hydrogen
and oxygen, powered by renewable energy sources such as wind, solar, and hydro. However,
the challenge with hydrogen is its low energy density and the high energy consumption required
to convert it into a liquid form, which is more convenient for storage.
The solution to overcome this challenge exists, and the technology that can resolve it is called
Power-to-X. Green hydrogen production itself can be considered a Power-to-X product, as it
involves using renewable power in a process to produce chemicals. However, the term ”Power-
to-X” more specifically refers to derivatives of hydrogen, such as ammonia and methanol.
Even though also methanol has market potentials, it has been decided to focus on ammonia as
the ”X” component for this project. However, the focus of the Thesis is not solely on evaluating
storage potential; instead, ammonia synthesis is viewed as a tool to prevent RES curtailment.
The entire system required to produce this ”X” is quite complex and expensive, so it cannot
be used only when surplus wind and solar energy are expected.
The goal of the thesis is to evaluate the entire process involved in a Power-to-X system, to
illustrate and explain how all systems and subsystems have to be modeled and designed to
operate synchronously together. Here lies the first challenge of the project: how to operate
a conventional chemical plant under renewable energy power. It must be considered that a
conventional chemical plant operates under steady-state conditions, meaning that a specific
nominal mass flow rate of reactants is consumed in a reactor, which produces a specific sample
rate of products, every day for 90% of the year.
The idea to simulate the reaction mechanism occurring in the ammonia production system
under different and variable load is one of the main goals of the project. The second main part
of the scope regards the control of the machine which produces hydrogen, the electrolyzers.
Even though the electrolysis process is already a well-known process, the deployment of the
respective machine for a green process is something quite recent. The number of projects is
growing worldwide, but still the operation of such a system is not well known. Points like power
allocation strategy as well as the optimal size of the system to accommodate the RES power
are still uncertain. There is no optimal configuration that guarantees the best performance.
This is complicated to evaluate, also for the two most mature technologies in the market: alka-
line electrolyzer (AEK) and proton exchange membrane electrolyzer (PEM), which differ from
each other both from an operational and cost perspective. The first one is cheaper but slow in
following the renewable energy profile, whereas the PEM is expensive but has far outstanding
performance.
In addition, the RES technologies differ noticeably from each other. Solar power is the cheaper
1

RES energy, but it features a specific power profile not only throughout the day but also during
the whole year. Wind energy does not stop as frequently as the former, but its costs are higher,
especially for offshore wind. It can be easily noted that a combination of both is needed to
guarantee power production as close as possible to steady conditions by working complemen-
tarily.
Europe and the rest of the world are characterized by different RES resources. In fact, it is
undeniable that the south of Europe has far more available solar resources compared to the
north, and the opposite is true for wind energy cases.
1.2
Scope of the Project
The challenge represented by the complicity of a Power to X project is what draw this project.
The first point to be addressed is understanding the feasibility of running the ammonia synthe-
sis production under different loads. Modeling the synthesis reactor is necessary to understand
the operational mechanisms of the plant in terms of equipment performance. The process itself
must be integrated with equipment such as compressors, pumps, heat exchangers, evaporators,
etc., which are typically designed and optimized to manage a fixed working stream. Under dif-
ferent loads, various effects can occur, resulting in the need for extra equipment and controls.
Hence, analyzing the performance and different working conditions of these other units in the
entire system is essential to evaluate potential technical barriers and obstacles from conven-
tional settings.
The second main analysis involves the operation of the electrolyzers, clustered in a so-called
electrolyzer farm. The goal of this part is to assess how different configurations of these ma-
chines perform differently in terms of hydrogen production and power consumption.
Both
alkaline and PEM technologies will be tested to evaluate the resulting performance response,
both in terms of their eﬀiciency in absorbing available renewable energy power, due to the
difference in operating parameters, and the respective amount of hydrogen produced.
The ability to model the entire system, including auxiliary components such as compressors,
pumps, and air cooling systems, is an objective of the project to assess the general performance
of the system under changing power input and highlight the main energy consumers and key
equipment process parameters. Moreover, it is important to highlight the off-grid nature of
the system, requiring oversizing in terms of hydrogen production and storage to both cover
seasonality and lack of RES power, accurately covered by a hydrogen gas turbine.
To sum up, the project aims to evaluate the performance of a Power-to-Ammonia system,
analyzing different electrolyzer stack configurations under different geographical locations to
assess performance results. The analysis will focus on the utilization capacity of the electrolyzers
under various configurations, total hydrogen produced, and hydrogen storage levels required
using technologies like AEK or PEM. In terms of the energy source, the study will investigate
how the deployment of PV and Offshore Wind assets results in different outcomes in terms of
energy produced, due to varying capacity factors and economic cost differences. Finally, the
best outcomes in terms of overall ammonia production and the respective levelized cost will be
evaluated.

Alkaline vs PEM Differences
The overall reaction describing the electrolysis process has been introduced with Eq.1, but in
an electrolyzer cell, the anode and cathode reactions represent half of this equation. Alkaline
electrolyzers (AEK) and Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) electrolyzers are the two technolo-
gies deployed for this reaction, but they differ from each other in terms of electrode reactions,
materials, and performance.
Alkaline electrolysis is currently the most mature and commercially employed electrolytic tech-
nology. As shown in Fig. 3, these electrolyzers need an aqueous solution containing KOH as
the electrolyte, with a concentration of 20-30% by weight in the solution. The charge carriers
are OH−ions, which move through a NiO diaphragm. The working temperature cannot exceed
90°C to avoid affecting ohmic conduction in the electrolyte and the stability of the diaphragm,
which needs to be maintained humid to guarantee low ionic and ohmic losses. For atmospheric
alkaline electrolyzers, the lowest energy requirement is reported to be 53.4 kWh
kgH2 , with a related
maximum eﬀiciency of 73.8%. Compared to PEM models, the AEK version is not the best
in terms of fast performance, as it operates within a limited current density range of 200-600
mA
cm2. Moreover, the load range is also quite restricted to 20-100% in the best case. Hydrogen is
produced at the cell outlet with very high purity, containing only residual amounts of oxygen,
which must be removed in a catalytic converter due to the poisonous effect of oxygen traces on
the Haber-Bosch synthesis catalyst.
Even though increasing pressure has a negative effect from a thermodynamic perspective, high-
pressure alkaline systems are often adopted. The main advantage of these configurations lies in
the higher attainable production rate and the significant reduction in compression requirements

According to Le Chatelier’s Principle, increasing the temperature shifts the equilibrium position
toward the reactants (N2 and H2), reducing ammonia yield. However, high temperatures are
necessary to increase the reaction rate, described by the Arrhenius equation, as the dissociation
of nitrogen molecules’ triple bond requires significant energy.
In the Haber-Bosch process, iron-based catalysts are employed to enhance reaction rates by
lowering the activation energy. Iron is chosen for its abundance, cost-effectiveness, and high
catalytic activity for ammonia synthesis. However, the industrial reaction temperature has to
be kept below 830 K to avoid the catalyst’s structure degradation. Hence, by considering all
these temperature effects on the overall process performance, a compromise temperature of
around 650-770 K is normally used, optimized based on overall yield and reaction rate.
Also, pressure has a significant effect on the equilibrium disturbance. The reaction involves a
net reduction in the number of gas molecules: 4 moles of reactants form 2 moles of ammonia.
Therefore, increasing pressure shifts the equilibrium toward the products, favoring ammonia
formation. Industrially, pressures of 150-250 atm are applied to achieve higher yields, as pre-
dicted by Le Chatelier’s Principle, by considering proper optimization in terms of economic
and engineering constraints.
Finally, the equilibrium can also be manipulated by changing the concentrations of reactants
or products. Removing ammonia as it forms shifts the equilibrium toward the production of
more ammonia. Similarly, maintaining a high concentration of N2 and H2 in the reaction vessel
promotes the forward reaction. Quenching flows after reactor beds is a way to mix fresh syngas
and partially convert hydrogen and nitrogen, establish higher concentrations of reactants, and
lower ammonia concentration in the stream.

2.4
Air Separation Unit and Desalination
Nitrogen production is required to isolate nitrogen from air, serving as a feedstock reactant in
the ammonia synthesis process. Conventional plants primarily use air liquefaction and separa-
tion, with cryogenic distillation accounting for 90% of global nitrogen production. The second
technology, based on Pressure Swing Adsorption (PSA), produces nitrogen through compres-
sion stages and membrane filtration, featuring a more modular design.
Cryogenic distillation is the most mature technology used for oxygen and nitrogen production.
It is based on the different vaporization temperatures of nitrogen and oxygen. The main steps
include air compression, followed by water and CO2 removal, and cooling through a heat ex-
changer to cryogenic temperatures. At this point, the difference between nitrogen and oxygen
saturation temperatures allows their separation in a distillation column.
Energy consump-
tion for cryogenic distillation is approximately 0.11 kWh/kgN2, with capacities of up to 10,000
tons/day. However, it operates within a 60–100% load range and has slow response times,
making it less flexible for dynamic power inputs.
The second technology, illustrated in Fig.5, is Pressure Swing Adsorption (PSA). This technol-
ogy is compact and well-suited for small-scale nitrogen production. The steps include initial air
compression to a few bars, followed by the passage of the air stream through membranes where
oxygen binds to a Carbon Molecular Sieve (CMS) at high pressure. After a few cycles, oxygen
8
==PAGE:21==
is desorbed at lower pressures, leaving pure nitrogen. PSA systems require 0.11–0.29 kWh/kgN2
and operate within a 30–100% load range, offering rapid response times. However, they are
limited to 2,000–5,000 Nm3/h production rates and yield lower-purity nitrogen compared to
cryogenic distillation.
Thus, these two nitrogen production technologies are deployed based on the application: cryo-
genic distillation is ideal for large-scale production due to its high purity and capacity but comes
with high capital costs and low flexibility. PSA, in contrast, is more flexible and compact, with
faster start-up times, though it is less eﬀicient at large scales and produces lower-purity nitro-
gen.

Regarding desalination technology for fresh water feed consumption, SeaWater Reverse Osmosis
(SWRO) and Mechanical Vapour Compression (MVC) are the main two technologies deployed.
The first one is a widely used desalination technology that converts seawater into potable water
by removing salts, minerals, and other impurities through a semi-permeable membrane. The
process works by applying pressure to seawater, forcing it through a membrane that allows
only water molecules to pass through, while blocking larger molecules such as salts. The clean
water that emerges is collected as permeate, while the concentrated brine is typically disposed
of or further treated.
On the other hand, Mechanical Vapour Compression (MVC) is an energy-eﬀicient desalination
and evaporation technology that compresses and recycles vapor within a system to aid in water
distillation. The process begins with seawater evaporation, followed by compressing the vapor
with a mechanical compressor, which raises its temperature and pressure. The vapor is then
condensed back into water, with the condensed water collected as fresh distillate.
A range of 8-16 and 5-13 kWh
m3 represent the respective energy consumption for MVC and SWRO.
The product water purity in ppm TDS is below 20 for MVC, whereas it falls between 250-700
for SWRO. Additionally, the feed water quality does not require specific parameters for either
technology.

Offshore wind farms offer several advantages over their onshore counterparts. First, the wind

more reliable energy production. Second, offshore turbines can be larger and taller, as there

noise pollution and visual impact on communities, addressing some of the common concerns

coastal areas with favorable conditions have already been utilized. Consequently, new projects
will need to be located farther offshore, increasing installation and maintenance costs due to
greater depths and harsher environmental conditions.

3.2
Power System Management and Process Control
The core of power control lies in the power system management. A clear strategy has been
developed and implemented to maximize the power dispatched to the electrolyzer, taking into
account a process system that consistently consumes a certain power level, with some flexibility.
The entire Power-to-Ammonia system can be divided into three main areas:
• Area A: ELZ Farm
This is the true flexible and dynamic area, aiming to maximize the utilization of power
from PV and Offshore Wind systems while dynamically adjusting the power level of each
electrolyzer stack. The goal is to follow the RES power profile as closely as possible,
based on the operational capabilities of the electrolyzer stacks. This section includes the
maximization of hydrogen production and allows a broad range of flexibility, depending
on the type of electrolyzer deployed, as will be described in Section 3.3. This subsystem’s
model is particularly detailed, accounting for many operating conditions such as ramping
rates, different start-up durations, and control strategies for shutdown and standby pro-
cess settings. From the PFD, this area includes the ELZ subsystem with the respective
Balance of Plant.
• Area B: Ammonia Synthesis
Only after hydrogen production has been determined in the previous main area are the re-
maining system parameters, both in terms of power and mass streams, defined. However,
the subsystems included in this area cannot dynamically follow the RES power profile as
closely, so a restricted operating range is imposed. From the PFD, this area includes the
ASU, Ammonia Synthesis, Separation, IP Steam Turbine, NH3 Storage, Backup Power,
and Heat Recovery Subsystems. The sensitivity of the final process to this degree of
flexibility will be discussed in Section 3.7.
• Area C: H2 Storage
Hydrogen storage plays a critical role in managing periods of peak power generation
and when the RES power reaches zero. It ensures a continuous flow of hydrogen to the
ammonia synthesis process, even when the renewable energy sources are not generating
power. The eﬀicient operation of the hydrogen storage system is key to balancing and
15

maintaining the ammonia synthesis process, as the ammonia system can only operate
within certain parameters and cannot be shut down without affecting the overall produc-
tion. By eﬀiciently managing the storage and release of hydrogen, the system ensures
a steady supply of hydrogen for ammonia production, thereby guaranteeing the ammo-
nia synthesis process continues smoothly despite fluctuations in power availability. The
storage subsystem thus serves as a stabilizer, helping to maintain the operation of the
ammonia system while providing the necessary flexibility to accommodate varying energy
input from the RES assets.

5
Conclusion
The project aims to develop and design an off grid Power-to-Ammonia system, focusing on its
dynamic operational behavior under different power loads, relying solely on power from PV
and offshore wind systems, without any grid connection.
The system can be divided in three main area. the first one, constituted by the ELZ subsystem,
is where dynamics occur through optimization between power and hydrogen production rate.
The second part regards the ammonia synthesis production, designed with all the auxiliary
equipment to operate under a specific load range of 30-100%. The third part is the one consti-
tuting the linking between the two, namely the hydrogen storage subsystem, managing properly
the flow of hydrogen to the reactor, between actual production from the ELZ subsystem and
the operating range allowable, by controlling the respective storage level for this purpose.
The heart of the system is the electrolyzer farm. This subsystem has been modeled in high
detail, implementing an algorithm to optimize power allocation from the available RES assets.
Additionally, its dynamic operation has been defined by considering key performance param-
eters such as ramping rates, load flexibility, and cold and hot startup. For this purpose, the
project tested both PEM and alkaline (AEK) models (one with high and one with low stack
capacity) using two locations, Denmark and Spain, to have a complete overview also based on
different geographical availability in terms of solar and wind energy.
Secondly, particular attention has also been devoted to the Haber-Bosch reaction for ammonia
synthesis. In this regard, the kinetics and thermodynamics of the reaction have been developed
to capture key differences across the allowed load range of 30-100%. By doing so, the effects on
temperature and concentration at the reactor outlet were analyzed to assess system operation,
ensuring technical feasibility based on the design of the remaining equipment in other subsys-
tems.
Beyond the electrolyzer and ammonia synthesis subsystems, additional units have been inte-
grated, including an air separation system, hydrogen storage, separation units, and an intermediate-
pressure steam turbine cycle. Thanks to this comprehensive approach, it was possible to have
a comprehensive overview of their integration from a holistic perspective, accessing as well the
main performance of a complex system under change in power inputs.
The final piece of the system includes a Backup Power Subsystem to provide baseload power
to allow process operation. For this purpose, a gas turbine run by surplus green hydrogen has
been modeled to cover the main lack of power from RES, with the integration of a battery
subsystem to facilitate buffering and cover smaller power shortages.
The system outcomes highlight interesting results, with some degree of improvement, both
technically and economically.
From the AEK and PEM simulation outcomes, it is evident that the PEM model can absorb
power more eﬀiciently, adjusting its power levels more freely thanks to favorable operational
parameters, compared to the more constrained operation of AEK models. The utilization fre-
quency differs between the two, with PEM being activated more often and for longer periods,
with a higher yearly capacity factor. Even though stacks belonging to the same electrolyzer
model are deployed differently, it has been found that their full capacity factor is never reached.
63
==PAGE:76==
However, despite the better dynamic performance of PEM, the AEK model with low stack ca-
pacity achieves higher hydrogen production. Moreover, in the same scenario, stacks with lower
capacity perform better than their higher-capacity equivalents. Having a similarly flexible op-
erating range and the ability to capture lower power during periods of unfavorable renewable
energy availability allows for continuous hydrogen production and a higher stack utilization
capacity factor.
The impact of varying reactant flow rates on reactor performance is evident through the de-
tailed ammonia synthesis model. This analysis is crucial for understanding the overall system
behavior, considering fluctuations in product rates, concentrations, and temperatures. A key
finding of the simulation is the reactor utilization and flow rate distribution. The reactors
operate at only a 54% capacity factor, with the lowest flow rate limit being the most frequently
observed, ultimately resulting in a final ammonia production of approximately 45 kton.
Thanks to the detailed model of the synthesis reactor and all the equipment included in the
plant, along with the defined capacities and selected sizes, the simulation confirms the proper
functioning of the system, maintaining adequate temperature and steam conditions during both
peak and low-demand periods
The hydrogen gas turbine effectively compensates for major gaps in renewable power supply,
while the battery system is primarily used to cover short transition periods between the activa-
tion and deactivation of the gas turbine. Although the gas turbine operates frequently to offset
the lack of renewable power, it has been figure out that it exhibits a relatively low capacity
factor in terms of overall utilization
Finally, it can be concluded that from a technical perspective, all performance indicators sug-
gest that the Spanish case with a low-capacity stack configuration yields the best results in
terms of hydrogen and ammonia production while requiring the second-lowest hydrogen stor-
age capacity. Also, from an economic perspective, the case reports the best Levelized Cost
of Ammonia (LCOA), with a final value of 3.228 $/kg, despite its uncompetitiveness with the
current cost found in the EU market, which is nearly five times cheaper.
However, it is important to note that this project was not developed as an optimization model
aimed at minimizing the final LCOA. Instead, the primary objective was to design and model
the system to evaluate its dynamic performance and assess its feasibility, focusing on the inte-
gration of key components from an holistic perspective.
