Abstract 
Genetic defects and diseases are an everlasting problem and new medicine needs to be developed 
to treat them. Once the medicine/drug has been developed optimization of the pathway and 
production are the top priorities. Drugs of the sorbicillinoid family such as sorbicillin and 
sorbicillinol could treat cancer and HIV, among others. This project will focus on Trichoderma 
reesei for the production of sorbicillinoids. The main objective will be on a predicted gene 
cluster that Jørgensen found during his PhD. The gene cluster expresses sorbicillinoids and needs 
to be understood and validated before it can be used for production. The production of 
sorbicillinoids in Trichoderma reesei has been increased by overexpression of a transcription 
factor. The effects of sorbicillinoids are favourable in order to fight diseases and should therefore 
be optimized for production. To optimize the production an understanding of its expression and 
host organisms is required. To understand the sorbicillinoid production of the cluster, deletion 
mutants are constructed and they are validated through chemical analysis with high-perfomance 
liquid chromatography (HPLC) and mass spectrometry (MS).

Introduction 
In recent years a lot of different diseases and sicknesses have circulated the Earth. Some people 
suffer from genetic defects and require treatment their entire life. Other diseases are terminal and 
untreatable. To help less fortunate human, medicine is a necessity. As a result the industry 
produces a variety of different pharmaceutical drugs. One of the production organisms used by 
the industry is Trichoderma reesei. Trichoderma reesei is used because it has secretion of 
proteins, making purifications easier. Furthermore it has a high production capacity and can 
tolerate high concentrations of homolog or heterologous proteins. 
The focus of this project will be on a sorbicillinoid cluster in Trichoderma reesei that has been 
activated by overexpression of a transcription factor. The work is a continuation of the work 
done by Jørgensen [1], and this article is thus used as the starting point. The Trichoderma reesei 
is a good production organism [2] making it interesting to genetically modify and setup 
experiments. Some of the products Trichoderma reesei produces are sorbicillin and sorbicillinol, 
which belong to the sorbicillinoid family.  
Sorbicillinoid is a product from a secondary metabolite, which is one of the major contributors to 
modern medicine. This thesis will look into a predicted cluster by looking into some 
overproduction strains constructed by Jørgensen [1]. Jørgensen overexpressed a transcription 
factor and it resulted in a formidable increase in the production of sorbicillinoids. Regrettably, he 
never completely validated the cluster – this thesis will pick up where he left. In the predicted 
sorbicillinoid cluster there are 7 genes; SorA, SorB, SorC, SorD, SorE, SorF and SorG. In 
between SorC and SorD is an internal gene, which Jørgensen predicts is not part of the cluster.  
Out of the 7 genes in the predicted sorbicillinoid cluster Jørgensen constructed deletion mutants 
of SorA, SorB, SorC and SorD. This leaves deletion mutants for SorE, SorF, SorG and the 
internal gene to be done. These deletion mutants were constructed during this thesis. To fully 
validate the cluster, the internal gene is also included in the experiments; therefore a deletion 
mutant of the internal gene is also constructed. Some surrounding genes will also be subjugated 
to chemical analysis to see whether they interact with the cluster. 
To verify the effects of the overexpression on the Trichoderma strain chemical analysis must be 
carried out. Through qPCR (quantitative polymerase chain reaction) the up- or downregulation

Theory 
The next sections will look into the theoretical aspects needed to understand the thesis subjects. 
After a brief introduction to filamentous fungi, and in particular Trichoderma reesei, the 
chemical aspects of the secondary metabolite studied, this thesis producing sorbicillin and 
sorbicillinol (both sorbicillinoids) will be outlined.  
Why are fungi relevant for modern society? 
Fungi are a vast and heterogeneous group of eukaryotes that populate different biological 
environmental niches. Fungi are walled heterotrophy organisms and therefore require energy [7]. 
Through cellulases and other enzymes the fungi decompose organic material to gain resources. 
Fungi also produce several useful or harmful compounds that improve the everyday life of 
humans. For example, they can be used for production of enzymes, dyes, medicine or ferment 
sausages or soybeans. Some species are opportunistic pathogens and can invade other organisms 
– such as Aspergillus fumigatus, Candida albicans or Tinea corporis. The respective infections 
from these fungi are aspergillosis, which causes inflammation in the lungs and sinuses but can be 
more severe in patients with weak immune system, candidiasis, which is a yeast infection that 
ranges from blood, genitals, mouth, skin, blood, and lastly ringworms, which prefer to infect 
glabrous skin but can infect all superficial skin areas.  
Some produce mycotoxins (aflatoxins, citrinin, fumonisins, etc.), which are harmful for 
mammals directly or indirectly, e.g. by destroying crops and other food sources. Fungi usually 
reproduce through asexual spores (conidia), but some also reproduce sexually. Knowing how 
they behave and reproduce is a necessity when trying to cultivate or genetically modify fungus. 
The vast variety of fungi is an advantage so they can colonize different environmental niches, 
and as a result it contains different regions with asymmetrical distributions of cellular 
compartments. The sporulation begins in the vegetative phase consisting of branched hyphae or 
polarized cylindrical cells that form pseudohyphae or, if dimorphic, in both forms [7]. The 
hyphae form stalks by apical extension, and at the end of the stalk a conidiophore vesicle is 
formed. The condiophore vesicle forms metulae (uninucleate cells) and phialides are formed on 
top of the metulae.

The asexual reproduction cycle 
The asexual reproduction cycle starts after the vegetative phase by germination of a spore. The 
germination starts by the spore taking in water, which then starts to swell (increasing its size). 
The cells are then duplicated and follow the 4 general phases (G1, S, G2 and mitosis). After the 
first mitosis the growth pattern changes to form germtubes – this is the start of the multicellular 
hyphae. Within the first 4-8 hours cells that are 10 times bigger than the conidia will form – 
these are called germlings. After the third mitosis the germlings will progress to postdivisional 
hyphal cells. The hyphal cells are separated into different compartments by the formation of 
septa. Septa are internal crosswalls with septal pores that allow transport between cells. The 
hyphae are extended by Spitzenkörpers (an apical cluster of cytoskeletal elements and vesicles). 
When the nutrients have been depleted, stalks with spores are formed to repeat the asexual or 
sexual cycle [7].

The sexual reproduction cycle 
The sexual reproduction cycle starts after the vegetative phase by the formation of ascogenous 
dikaryotic mycelium. The mycelium closest to the initial cell (cleistothecial primordium) will 
produce Hülle cells, which will form fruiting bodies (cleistothecia). Sacs will form on the 
cleistothecia and will contain sexual spores. The cells will start to divide and form new spores 
that have either hetero- or homokaryon cores. Homokaryon is a necessity for industrial 
applications to prevent strain instability. Therefore a homokaryon strain with high production 
capability is favourable – Trichoderma reesei would be an excellent choice. 
Trichoderma reesei 
Trichoderma reesei is a fast growing fungus that is inexpensive to cultivate and has a broad 
habitat to be cultivated in. The QM6a strain was first characterised as a Trichoderma viride since 
they are so closely related [8, 9], but was renamed to Trichoderma reesei. It has GRAS 
(generally recognized as safe) status in regards to producing heterologous proteins, produces 
cellulases and has a secretion capacity of up to 100 g/l homologous protein, making it a great 
industrial production organism [8, 10]. In its native environment it produces cellulases to 
decompose plant material and is often found in the rhizosphere of plants or decaying plant 
material [8]. Although its genome comprises only of a few genes that encode cellulases they are 
very effective at producing cellulases [8].

Trichoderma reesei holds a large potential for dominating the environmental niche through 
enzymes, chemicals, efficient mycoparasites, antagonists and biocontrol agents [8]. It reproduces 
both sexually and asexually. Its optimal growth temperature is from 25 to 30°C – typically there 
is no growth above 35°C. Furthermore it can grow with or without light and on many different 
carbon sources [8, 9, 11].  
Trichoderma reesei form highly branched conidiophores as small compact or loose tufts. The 
main branches produce lateral side branches and phialides arise near the central tip. The 
phialides are enlarged near the center, can be arranged in a whorl and can be cylindrical or 
somewhat spherical [9]. The conidia has a lime green colour and will form within a week usually 
as ellipsoidals [8]. 
Interaction with roots of plants leads to advantages for the plants, such as root proliferation, 
better growth protection from other fungi and higher tolerance to toxic chemicals [8, 12, 13]. The 
protection from other microorganisms is obtained by increased toxicity towards germination of 
other pathogens or inactivation of the pathogens enzymes [13]. The higher tolerance is by the 
introduction of xylanase and other elicitors which enhance synthesis of various compounds 
inside the host organism to protect it [13]. 
Trichoderma reesei can produce secondary metabolites such as polyketides, pyrones and 
terpenes. It also has a high productivity and high capacity of protein secretion and it is thus a 
very favourable organism for the industry [1, 8, 14]  to genetically modify to optimize different 
processes or physiological needs. 
Primary and secondary metabolites 
Primary metabolites are the essential ones directly involved during growth and reproduction of 
the organism. They are typically energy efficient ways of turning substrate into product and 
restoring adenosine triphosphate (ATP) and nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide (NAD). Loss of 
the primary metabolites would result in very limited or no growth. On the other hand, secondary 
metabolites do not influence growth as directly as primary metabolites but are expressed in the 
beginning of the stationary growth phase to give competitive advantages, which indirectly help 
growth. Secondary metabolites are involved in interactions between its host and everything 
outside the cell wall - such as the environment, neighbouring cells, other organism etc.

Secondary metabolites sometimes require a certain state of the host to be expressed – e.g. there is 
no reason to produce fungicides if there is no competing fungus. Humans have learned to exploit 
secondary metabolites for medicinal purposes by knowing their specific requirements to be 
produced. Secondary metabolites produce many different pharmaceutical drugs, amino acids and 
chemicals needed for organic synthesis etc. Some examples are: cocaine, erythromycin, fatty 
acids, glycosides, lovastatin, morphine, penicillin, steroids and other useful products – most of 
the known ones probably being antibiotics, such as penicillin. 
The families range from alkaloids, non-ribosomal peptides (NRPS), polyketides (PKS), shikimic 
acid pathway and terpenoids, among others.  
The focus of this study will be on polyketide synthase (PKS), a type of secondary metabolite that 
creates some very interesting pharmaceutical drugs – such as erythromycin, enediynes, 
doxorubicin and lovastatin [15]. 
Polyketide synthase  
Polyketide syntase (PKS) are composed of different domains. The order and function of the 
domains characterise which product can be produced – also whether the PKS is iterative or 
reducing. This diversity leads to a broad variety of natural products [16]. The most essential 
domains are acyl carrier protein (ACP) and ketosynthase (KS), which together catalyse the chain 
elongation and acyl transferase (AT) since they transfer an acyl group onto the chain. Next are 
cyclase (CYC) and thioesterase (TE) – their function is to release the chain once it is fully 
elongated. A reducing PKS contains ketone reductase (KR), dehydrogenase (DH) and enoyl 
reductase (ER) domains, which individually reduce ketone to alcohol (KR), alcohol to alkene 
(DH) and alkene to alkane (ER). The non-reducing domain starter unit acyl-carrier protein 
transacylase (SAT) is for loading another starting unit than a ketone [17]. The product template 
(PT) is catalysing specific cyclisation(s) of the chain. The methyl transferase (MT – CMT/C-Met 
or OMT) metylates either the carbon or the oxygen [3, 16, 17]. The focus of this study will be on 
sorbicillinoid, which is a hexaketide metabolite, produced by a PKS cluster through either an 
endo-, exo-, Diels-Alder ([4+2]-cycloaddition) or Michael reactions [3, 4, 16, 18, 19].

Sorbicillinoid 
The sorbicillinoid family is a large family of hexaketide metabolites and a sorbyl sidechain 
(C5H7). The precursor of the sorbicillinoid family is either sorbicillin [5, 20] or sorbicillinol [5] - 
their structures are shown in Figure 3 and Figure 4. 
 
              
 
Figure 3. Sorbicillin. 
Figure 4. Sorbicillinol. 
One of the reasons why it has not yet been determined which one is the precursor is that 
sorbicillinol is not stable enough to be isolated alone [4]. The respective structures of 
sorbicillinoids and its sorbyl side chain leads to anticancer activity. Some sorbicillinoids, such as 
trichodimerol [4], inhibits the production of antioxidants and tumour necrosis factor-α from both 
macrophages and blood monocytes [3, 18, 21]. Tumour necrosis factors regulate immune cells, 
induce tumour cell apoptosis, but also have host-damaging effects in septis, tumour cachexia and 
autoimmune diseases [6]. Others protect the T cells from HIV-1 [3-5] and inhibits expression of 
viral proteins [4]. Some induce antioxidant activity [3]. A study done by Lee  et al. [21] also 
report sorbicillinoids having agonist activity on PPARγ (peroxisome proliferator-activated 
receptors γ) while not disrupting PPARα or PPARβ. The PPARγ is a human receptor mainly 
present in the adipose tissue stimulating the lipid uptake in fat cells. Activation of PPARγ 
inhibits the growth of different cancer cell lines. Agonist activity can potentially be used as a 
treatment for diabetes and some cancer types.  
Sorbicillinoids can be categorised into 4 subfamilies: monomeric sorbicillinoid derivatives, 
bisorbicillinoids, trisorbicillinoids and a combination of different biosynthetic precursors [5, 19]. 
Some sorbicillinoids are produced through shikimic-related metabolites rather than polyketide.

Some of the sorbicillinoid families are characterised by a yellow colour [3, 18, 19] – sorbicillin 
and sorbicillinol also share this characteristic. 
 
 
Figure 5. Trichodimerol. 
 
The subclass trichodimerol contains a characteristic cage-like structure (an example can be seen 
in Figure 5); the structure is often formed by the Michael reaction. Sorbicillinoids often form a 
cyclic structure leading to various structures [5]. Another characteristic is its many free radicals, 
such as 2,2-diphenyl-1-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH), 2,6-di-tertbutyl-4-methylphenol (BHT) and α-
tocopherol (vitamin E) [5].  
A lot of research in this field has been done with sorbicillinoids, and sometimes compounds are 
published with different names by different authors. Sorbicillinoids are often called 
bisorbicillinoids, since they are rarely seen as monomers. Sorbicillinol is the most likely 
precursor to sorbicillin and bisorbicillinoids [5].  
The composition of sorbicillinoid genes 
The sorbicillinoid gene cluster in this project was predicted by Mikael Skaaning Jørgensen (his 
PhD thesis can be found as article [1]). The article also describes the introduction of a new 
expression system in Trichoderma reesei using pyr2 as a selectable marker.  
Jørgensen used bioinformatics to predict that the sorbicillinod cluster contained 7 genes; SorA,

Gene targeting 
For manipulation of different organisms, precision and accuracy are 2 very important factors. 
The location of the target in the organism is important in order to precisely adjust the organism 
on a molecular level. A genetic replacement strategy called gene targeting uses homologous 
recombination to exchange highly similar or identical DNA sequences [22]. Once the DNA is 
inside the organism it will eventually be integrated into the chromosome either by non-specific 
integration or homologous recombination. Non-specific integration is random breaks in the 
chromosome that are ligated together by non-homologous end joining (NHEJ). Homologous 
recombination (HR) considers the homology between the recipient DNA and foreign DNA [22]. 
To improve a strain to become efficient for specific gene targeting non-homologous end joining 
can be reduced [22]. The E. coli strain DH5α has been genetically modified to allow for high-
efficiency cloning in the laboratory by changing the ratio between NHEJ and HR, among other 
genetic modifications [23]. 
The technique Uracil Specific Excision Reagent (USER) fusion uses homologous recombination 
and the E. coli DH5α strain, which results in specific gene targeting.

Uracil Specific Excision Reagent (USER) fusion and cloning 
Uracil Specific Excision Reagent (USER) cloning allows assembling PCR fragments into a 
plasmid that can be used for target specific integration into a gene. The novelty is a deoxyuracil 
at the 5’ primer end, which is further extended with a tail sequence. The fragments desired to 
ligate together have a complimentary tail sequence. Once enough plasmids have been assembled, 
they are transformed into DH5α strain. The more fragments used in assembling, the more 
effective the USER transformation will be [24].  
4 plasmids are constructed using USER cloning in this thesis. The plasmids are assembled by 4 
fragments; pMJ-023 vector backbone, pyr2, up and down fragments of the respective 4 genes 
(SorE, SorF, SorG and the internal gene). The four plasmids will be inserted into the 
Trichoderma reesei strain obtained from the QM6a strain. 
The descendants of the QM6a strain 
The original Trichoderma reesei strain was isolated as a Trichoderma viride during World War 2 
and was called the QM6a strain [2, 8, 25]. The war ended and new use for cellulases arose, and 
the QM6a strain was subjected to classical mutagenesis so it could be applied for industrial 
purposes. 
The Trichoderma reesei used in this project has its acetamidas gene (amdS) disrupted (from A. 
nidulans – article [11]) and auxotrophic marker pyr2 is added for selective purposes. The 
bidirectional pyr2 marker leads to uridine auxotroph and 5-Fluoroorotic Acid (5-FOA) resistant 
mutants. The mutants show some instability when transformed with pyr marker [14]. 
Overexpression of a transcription factor has also been induced to facilitate sorbicillinoid 
production. To ensure control over the strain it also has an adenine deficiency, which results in 
required supplement of adenine and uridine to the cultivation media. Deletion mutants with the 
uridine deficiency grow extremely slow and should therefore after verification grow in presence 
of uridine (Mikael Jørgensen, personal communication). The Uracil Specific Excision Reagent 
(USER) cloning carried a functioning pyr2 gene which should result in uridine autotrophic 
strains.

For chemical analysis the method High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) and Mass

High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) 
The HPLC was performed in reverse phase, which uses a non-polar stationary phase, thereby 
detaining of less polar molecules. This is called the retention time. The mobile phase is a liquid 
solution composed of 2 solvents – one to load and one to elute. The solvents influence the peaks 
by increasing or decreasing the retention time of the retained molecules. Typical solvents for 
reverse phase are water, acetonitrile and methanol. Water is added to increase the affinity of the 
hydrophobic stationary phase where acetonitrile and methanol are more organic/less polar 
solvents and makes the retention time less and thereby elutes the molecules faster. Since HPLC 
uses ions, the pH is important so that the molecules have the correct charge – else it will not be 
detected. Therefore the HPLC is either done under acidic or basic conditions – this depends on 
the columns, as high or low pH can damage some of the equipment. Therefore columns are 
designed capable of tolerating pH and are constructed to have specific selectivity [26]. One way 
to limit the affinity is by using polymeric resins, since they have homologous surface and thereby 
lowering the unspecific reaction with hydroxyl residuals [26]. 
Mass Spectrometry (MS) 
Mass spectrometry is only capable of measuring ions, therefore the molecules needs to have a 
charge. The method used is depending on whether the molecule is positively or negatively 
charged. A molecule often has more charges than one and therefore the mass spectrometer will 
then measure in mass over numbers of charges (m/z). Mass spectrometry uses the monoisomeric 
mass, and from this a very exact estimate of the molecular composition of the molecule can be 
calculated. The most common adducts are H+, Na+, NH4
+, K+, H+CH3CN and Na+CH3CN. 
During MS runs H2O and CO2 can also be lost from the molecules.

In the following section the significance of the chemical analysis is discusses while deducting 
knowledge related to the work conducted in the thesis. For the quantitative Polymerase Chain 
Reaction (qPCR), cDNA is needed and while extracting the RNA the strain 031 remained yellow 
until the final step of purification, indicating a high level of sorbicillinoids.

Figure 30. The transcriptional profiles of the 8 genes with their function. 
The transcriptional profiles of the 8 genes with predicted involvement in the sorbicillinoid cluste.  
 
From Figure 30 it is illustrated that the PKS genes (SorA and SorB) are upregulated and the 
monooxygenase (SorE) is 2 to 4 times more upregulated than the PKS genes. Monooxygenase 
catalyses the incorporation of one oxygen (hence the mono) and reduces another oxygen to 
create water [29]. Since both precursors (sorbicillin and sorbicillinol) contain 3-4 integrated 
oxygen, an increase is to be expected unless the enzymes are not fully saturated. The high fold 
increase could indicate that these were the natural bottlenecks of the cluster. 
The predicted transcription factors (SorC and SorD) are being increased; this probably means the 
cluster contains a positive feedback mechanism or that the cluster is not regulated as efficiently 
as it could have been. This would lead to a further increase in the genes expression level, since 
they are upregulated moderately.

The predicted transporter gene (SorF) is from a major facilitator superfamily and is therefore 
most likely involved in transportation of the product between the 2 PKSs (SorA and SorB). It 
might also be involved in the transportation of sorbicillin or sorbicillinol across membrane. 
The predicted short chain dehydrogenase/reductase (SDR) that is being expressed from SorG 
might be moderately downregulated, but according to the concentration (C(t) values – from the 
qPCR – the levels of mRNA are almost the same compared to SorE. 
Looking back at article [1] the predicted biosynthesis of sorbicillin and sorbicillinol (Figure 6) 
requires both SorA and SorB to synthesise the first intermediate, which is also supported by [20]. 
For the last intermediates both SorE and SorG would be required. It seems that SorE is 
upregulated to match the concentration of SorG, which might indicate SorE being the natural 
bottleneck of the pathway. With this in mind the deletion mutants of SorE and SorG would 
probably have their growth hindered – unless the predicted transporter (SorF) is able to transport 
sorbicillin and sorbicillinol equally. Sorbicillinol is rather reactive so it might not need as much 
transportation as sorbicillin. 
Another reason for the transportation of the sorbicillinoids could be that if a too high 
concentration is reached inside the cell, it is toxic to the cell. If this was the case, the organism 
would probably have some sort of system to take care of the threat. A system like that could be 
composed of enzymes that are degrading the sorbicillinoids to control the toxicity level. If this 
system is a reality the transcription factor could also be inhibiting this, which could also explain 
why there are transcription factors inside the sorbicillinoid cluster to both activate it but also for 
antagonist activity against the degradation of the sorbicillinoids. 
Every organism has its limits in regards of producing products, but if there are no limits the 
organisms might be self damaging. If this is the case it comes down to how much damage the 
process is inflicting and how much damage the organism can handle. Maybe the transcription 
factor forms a complex with the sorbicillinoids and makes it less toxic, if at all. In that case little 
expression of the transcription factor in the wild type has to do with the degradation rather than 
the generation of the sorbicillinoids. In any case, the industry would probably care little for this 
as long as the organism can produce useful drugs such as sorbicillinoids.

The NM-T-055 strain is observed in Figure 20 to be more yellow than the other strains, which

Looking back at Mikael Jørgensen's prediction about SorE being needed to convert sorbicillin 
into sorbicillinol this may explain why. But Mikael Jørgensen also predicted that SorG and SorE 
were entwined in this conversion, which is interesting since SorG looks a more or less like one of 
the 3 other strains.

verification, but the strains should be supplemented with uridine for growth once they have been 
verified, else their growth would very limited, which is observed from Figure 25 to Figure 28.

Conclusion 
The aim of this thesis was to investigate the predicted sorbicillinoid cluster in Trichoderma 
reesei. To do so, deletion strains were constructed and subjected to chemical analysis. Firstly, 
expression vectors were generated by the use of PCR and USER cloning to construct a target 
specific expression cassette, and through homologous recombination the deletion mutation 
would occur.  
As a result 4 expression vectors were constructed and were verified by restriction analysis. The 
expression vector for the deletion of the SorF gene was not as expected and was therefore 
submitted to more analysis. The expression vectors were correctly assembled. The insertions of 
the 4 fragments into the transformants were tested by tissue PCR, but were not working. 
To conclude more upon the deletion strains they were subjugated to growth experiments with 
adenine and either with or without uridine. There was a small growth of the control strain MJ-T-
039, but the deletion strains grew up and were observed to be growing differently. 
The mutant strains were subjected to HPLC and the results were very similar, which indicates 
that they either have the same effect or were unsuccessful. The preceding evidences indicate that 
the latter is the more plausible one. Also the internal gene had an upregulation, whereas the SorG 
had a downregulatio, so not all genes were upregulated by this transcription factor. The SorG 
may be because of a positive feedback mechanism that ensures that there will not be too much 
product to inhibit or inflict problems for the host organisms. It would have been very educational 
to have the neighbouring genes “flanking” the sorbicillinoid cluster, so that it might have spread 
some light over the case. 
Either the fragments have been inserted correctly and the HPLC shows no difference, implying 
the internal gene has as much influence on the sorbicillinoid cluster as the other investigated 
genes, or the fragments are inserted wrongly and they somehow influence the MJ-T-039 
transformants in another way than predicted or not at all. But if it has been integrated wrongly, it 
would not be a reliable source of information/knowledge.
