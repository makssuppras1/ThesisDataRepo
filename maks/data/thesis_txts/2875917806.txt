Abstract 
Laser Powder Direct Energy Deposition, LP-DED, is an additive manufacturing process 
capable of producing near-net-shape components at high deposition rates. Numerous studies 
have investigated the geometry, microstructure, and mechanical performance of LP-DED 
parts. However, the results vary for each LP-DED setup due to the large number of 
influential parameters, and modeling tools can therefore be a challenge. 
This thesis presents a literature review of current knowledge on LP-DED with a particular 
focus on the deposition of 316L stainless steel, identifying guidelines and the influence of 
different parameters on geometry, microstructure, and mechanical properties. These 
insights are subsequently tested experimentally on an industrial LP-DED system equipped 
with a KUKA KR 30 HA, using a TRUMPF TruDisc 16002 200 μm fiber laser at FORCE 
Technology. A tool for determining the melt pool geometry is developed for the specific 
setup, and state of the art computational fluid dynamics simulations are assessed for its 
utility. The experimental workflow, illustrated in Figure 1.2, follows an iterative 
methodology, where all samples are systematically characterized for relevant findings before 
determining their inclusion in the subsequent stage. 
The investigations combine Design of Experiments, DOE, computational fluid dynamics 
simulations using FLOW-3D, light optical microscopy, LOM, confocal microscopy, electron 
backscatter diffraction, EBSD, energy-dispersive x-ray spectroscopy, EDS, Archimedes 
porosity testing, Vickers hardness testing, and tensile testing for a comprehensive study of 
the process.

Nomenclature 
Build direction - The direction of the build. Denoted as the z direction, see Figure 1.1 below. 
CFD - Computational fluid dynamics. In this work, CFD is carried out using the software 
FLOW-3D 2025R1 
Cuboid - The naming for the rectangular cuboid samples used for examining multilayer 
prints. 
DOE - Design of Experiments, DOE, is a framework for designing a setup that can test for 
multiple parameters’ effects and interactions. 
EBSD - Electron backscatter diffraction, EBSD,  is a characterization technique used in 
conjunction with SEM for defining the crystallographic orientation of the individual grains.  
Ed - Specific energy density, Ed, describes the amount of energy available for melting by using 
laser power, scan speed, and spot size. 
EDS - Energy dispersive x-ray spectroscopy, EDS, is a characterization technique used in 
conjunction with SEM for measuring the elemental composition. 
EL - Engineering elongation denotes, in % of original length, how much a material can 
deform before fracturing.  
EVED - Volumetric energy density, EVED, describes the amount of energy available for melting 
by using laser power, scan speed, hatch spacing, and layer height. 
G-code - The code used to control the robot arm movement.
HDR - Heating depth ratio, HDR, is an expression for how far the heat reaches into the 
previous layer. 
HV - Vickers hardness, HV, is a measure of the hardness of a material. Higher hardness, 
higher HV. 
IPF - Inverse pole figure is a visual representation of the EBSD scanned area, allowing for the 
grain orientation with respect to the chosen sampling orientation to be assessed.  
LOM - Light optical microscopy, LOM, is a characterization technique using visible light.  
LP-DED - The “LP” stands for laser powder, while “DED” stands for direct energy deposition. 
It refers to the specific setup of the direct energy deposition process used in this project. 
p% - Penetration percentage, p%, is a term used to indicate the penetration depth, p, per 
overall height, h, from the bottom to the top of the melt pool. It is given as p% = p/h * 100. 
PF - Pole figure is a plot of the crystal orientations from a scanned area. 
Point cloud - Three dimensional objects are digitally represented by a series of points in 
space. A collection of such points is referred to as a “point cloud”. The more points for a 
given area, the higher the spatial resolution.

1 Background and introduction 
Metal additive manufacturing, AM, is a technique that has rapidly grown in recent years 
(Fayazfar et al., 2018). Among those, Laser Powder Direct Energy Deposition, LP-DED, is a 
common choice and has recently been used in aeronautical, petrochemical, and automotive 
industries. One of the aspects of LP-DED that makes it stand out from other AM technologies 
is its capability to produce large parts, functionally graded materials, and repairing worn 
components (Aversa et al., 2021). However, the microstructure and mechanical performance 
of the LP-DED manufactured samples remain poorly understood (Zheng et al., 2019). Some 
studies even indicate that with every powder batch change, a full study of parameter 
influence may be required (Aversa et al., 2021). 
This project presents guidelines adapted from the literature to support the choice of laser 
power, scan speed, and mass flow in order to control geometry, microstructure, and 
mechanical properties for printing 316L using LP-DED.  
The aim of this project is to investigate, in collaboration with FORCE Technology, how these 
guidelines perform in an industrial setup, as well as the influence of process parameters on 
part geometry, melt pool geometry, microstructure, and mechanical performance. 
Furthermore, the potential for the use of modeling tools to assist the initial stages of design 
and process optimization is assessed.  
Multiple methods are employed for investigations of the process, including confocal 
microscopy, LOM, EBSD, EDS, Archimedes test, Vickers hardness test, and tensile test. 
Furthermore, DOE, using JMP, and CFD simulations, using FLOW-3D, are employed for 
analysis and a deeper understanding of the process. All manufacturing of the samples is 
carried out at FORCE Technology’s facility at Lindø on a KUKA KR 30 HA, using a TRUMPF 
TruDisc 16002 200 μm fiber laser, printing with 316L powder, supplied by GTV. A roadmap 
of the methods, relevant samples, and their location in this report is given in Figure 1.2 
below.

2.1 Stainless steel and grade 316L 
Stainless steels were first discovered and produced in England and Germany around 1910, 
where production was modest. Since then, the production has increased each year, and 
stainless steel is now used all around us. Even so, the production has never exceeded 1,5% of 
the yearly steel tonnage production in the US (Washko & Aggen, 1990). 
Stainless steel is an iron based alloy with a Cr content of at least 10,5%, and up to around 
30%. Due to its composition, it forms an adherent and self healing Cr-oxide film on the 
surface, which inhibits corrosion. Multiple grades of stainless steel are available, such as the 
300 series. The 300 series is primarily austenitic, meaning that it has a face centered cubic, 
fcc, structure. These steels cannot be hardened by other methods than cold working, and do 
not possess magnetic properties. Their alloying content, compared to other stainless steels, 
contains higher amounts of Ni and up to 2% Mn. 316L is a specific grade of stainless steel in 
the 300 series, with added Mo for increased corrosion resistance. The “L” in 316L indicates a 
low carbon content, which increases corrosion resistance after welding (Washko & Aggen, 
1990).  
Stainless steel, and thereby 316L with its well known weldability, is a popular material choice 
in the context of AM. In 2018, more than 50% of all metal powder shipments for AM were 
grades of stainless steel (Campbell et al., 2020). The nominal alloying composition of 316L is 
given in Table 2.1.

2.2 Geometry of LP-DED 
2.2.1 Geometry of parts 
Parts produced by LP-DED are often considered to be near-net shape, meaning that although 
they are close to the final part geometry, they may not meet the specified tolerances 
(Campbell et al., 2020). 
The geometry of a part can vary throughout the building process. The width for each track 
can change as the build progresses. This is typically due to heat accumulation in the part, 
resulting in a widening of the melt pool, further described below (Zheng et al., 2019).  
Due to the many heating cycles, high residual stresses are common for LP-DED parts. 
Residual stress can lead to geometrical changes, which is why a stress-relief treatment is 
often performed. This may be done while the part is still attached to the substrate, allowing 
for safer removal without distortion (Campbell et al., 2020).  
Under/over-building is a commonly observed phenomenon in LP-DED. This is when a layer 
is either higher or lower than expected, causing an accumulation of height differences. Even 
though this might seem like a problem, the complex process of LP-DED makes it 
self-stabilising, eliminating to some extent the buildup of under-/over-build layers (Aversa et 
al., 2021). Hatch spacing, i.e., the distance between adjacent tracks, is one of the parameters 
that impact height. A proposed tool for defining the optimal hatch spacing is to determine 
the spacing that causes the overlap area of the tracks to equal the unfilled area between the 
track peaks, as depicted in Figure 2.2.

2.2.2 Geometry of melt pool 
A study by Zhang et al. (2013) investigates the effect of laser power, scan speed, and mass 
flow on width and height of 316 LP-DED single-tracks. They find that higher track height is 
achieved with higher laser power, topping out at 1,2 kW, lower scanning speeds, and higher 
mass flow. Deposition width follows the exact same trend, with wider tracks achieved by 
higher laser power, up to 1,2 kW, lower scanning speeds, and higher mass flow. Their 
findings are depicted in Figure 2.3. 
 
 
 Figure 2.3: Plot of how laser power, scan speed and mass flow affect width and height of 316 single-tracks made 
by LP-DED. Figure based on Zhang et al. (2013). 
Other studies find similar results, while also documenting that the spot size has an effect on 
track width (Martukanitz, 2020). 
The penetration of the melt pool into the underlying material is an important parameter for 
optimal metallurgical bonding. Dass & Moridi (2019) report that a penetration percentage of 
10% - 30% results in optimal bonding. Higher than 30% leads to a risk of keyhole formation, 
but lower than 10% may result in lack of fusion. For the definition of keyhole and lack of 
fusion, see Section 2.3.1. Dass & Moridi (2019) report the penetration percentage to increase 
with a decrease in powder feed rate or an increased energy density.

2.3 Microstructure in 316L LP-DED 
The LP-DED process involves a multitude of heating cycles, rapid melting and solidification, 
as well as possible vaporization. All of these factors make the process complicated, and the 
microstructural evolution is therefore still far from understood (Zheng et al., 2019). The 
following is an attempt to give an overview of the general knowledge about the 
microstructure and its development in 316L samples produced by LP-DED. 
2.3.1 Porosities 
Porosities in relation to LP-DED can, in general, be categorized as either process induced or 
a result of the environment. In both cases, there are many different origins. Figure 2.4 
highlights the most common ones, their mechanisms, how to identify them, and how they 
may be avoided. Each of the porosities is described in more detail below.  
Gas porosities can be a product of the environment. They may originate from the shielding 
gas, which can be incorporated into the melt pool, and gas entrapped in the powder from the 
atomization process, which is transferred to the sample during printing (Campbell, et al., 
2020). In case of gas entrapped in the powder, the resulting porosities can be reduced by 
printing with a high energy density for complete melting of the particles. They are, however, 
more difficult to eliminate than process induced porosities (Fayazfar et al., 2018). Gas 
porosities are typically characterized by being randomly distributed and spherical in shape 
(Zheng et al., 2019).  
Lack of fusion, or interlayer porosities, is a typical process-induced type of porosity that 
arises when the energy density is insufficient. This may result not only from too low a laser 
power and too high a scan speed, but also from an elevated mass feed rate. As a result, the 
powder particles are not fully melted, leading to porosities. This type of porosity is 
characterized by its non-spherical form, the presence of unmelted particles near the 
porosities, and its size, ranging from sub-micrometer to millimeters (Campbell et al., 2020). 
Interpass inclusions are also a cause of lack of fusion. This happens when not fully melted 
powder particles fuse together on the surface, creating a void. If they are not melted by the 
subsequent layer, the void persists, leading to a porosity. Particles on the surface can be 
avoided by, besides a proper choice of energy density, correct laser and feeder alignment. If 
they are not aligned or if the laser is out of focus, particles on the surfaces can easily form 
(Zheng et al., 2019). Layer thickness and especially optimal hatch spacing, as described in 
Section 2.2.1, are reported to be controlling factors for lack of fusion (Zheng et al., 2019).  
Interlayer porosities might also include what Fayazfar et al. (2018) describe as shrinkage 
porosities. There are three reasons for this type of porosity: different layers having different 
mechanical properties, a big difference in temperature causing a difference in thermal 
elongation, and chemical reactions between different materials being applied. If the material 
flow is not enough to compensate for the differences, a void is formed.  
While low energy input can pose a problem, so can a high energy input. Keyhole formation 
can be a product of excessive energy input. If the energy of the laser is too high, splatter 
ejection occurs. This forms a void at the root of the melt pool known as a keyhole. This can 
cause turbulent flow in the melt pool, as well as the formation of a void before the melt has

2.3.2 Grain morphology 
As LP-DED, as well as many other metal AM techniques, uses an approach where each layer 
is built upon a previously deposited layer of the same material, epitaxial growth dominates. 
This means that the grains in the solidifying layer near the melt pool boundary adopt the 
orientation of the underlying grains. The preferred growth direction for austenite, as in 
stainless steel, is <100>. This direction aligns with the direction of maximum heat flow 
(Campbell et al., 2020). If the <100> is not aligned with the heat flow direction, the grains 
cease to grow as they impede upon the faster growing grains. The direction of the heat flow 
can change as the build progresses due to the buildup of heat and complicated scan paths 
(Martukanitz, 2020). Scan strategies can be used to some extent to control the grain growth.  
Further away from the melt pool, if undercooling is significant, epitaxial growth is no longer 
possible (Campbell, et al., 2020). Instead, a general relationship between the temperature 
gradient, G, and the solidification rate, R, for the microstructure applies. Fayazfar et al. 
(2018) report the grain morphology of LP-DED in 316, and provide a map of the equiaxed 
and columnar grains as a function of exact values for G and R, see Figure 2.5a. The figure 
illustrates how the ratio G/R describes the grain morphology, while the cooling rate, G·R, 
describes how coarse the microstructure is; higher cooling rates result in finer 
microstructures. Yazar et al. (2022) report that the region near the free surface exhibits low 
values of G/R, causing, in general, equiaxed grains. They implement a transition zone, 
indicating the change from columnar to equiaxed grains close to the free surface. Care should 
be taken when concluding about grain morphology. If a cluster of columnar grains is looked 
at from above, it will resemble an equiaxed structure.

2.3.3 Solidification mechanisms 
Alloys can exhibit many forms of solidification structures. One of the main mechanisms 
behind the growth types is constitutional supercooling. This phenomenon occurs due to 
solutes being pushed out into the melt. The higher concentration of solute at the melt front 
causes the liquidus temperature to drop, resulting in a nonequilibrium solidification 
temperature profile from the solid into the melt. If the gradient of the actual temperature 
profile is below the critical gradient of the nonequilibrium solidification temperature profile, 
protrusions from the solid/liquid interface will start to grow. If the actual temperature 
gradient is low enough, the protrusions develop secondary arms, and at even lower 
gradients, these can develop tertiary arms. These protrusions with multiple arms are known 
as dendrites (Porter et al., 2021).  
The solidification structure, as explained by the constitutional supercooling, can be 
simplified by interpreting the temperature gradient and solidification rate as depicted 
schematically in Figure 2.5b. It illustrates that at high values of G/R, planar growth 
dominates, but as the ratio decreases, the growth type will switch to cellular. At even lower 
ratios, the growth becomes columnar dendritic. Finally, at the lowest G/R, the columnar 
dendritic growth transitions into the equiaxed dendrites (Campbell et al., 2020).

2.3.5 Local compositional changes  
As mentioned above, constitutionally supercooling results in solutes being pushed into the 
melt. As the solidification front progresses, the composition gradually changes, causing the 
last melt to solidify to have the highest concentration of solutes. For dendritic growth and 
other types of growth, there will therefore be a high solute concentration between the 
solidification structures (Porter et al., 2021).  
Another important factor for the composition is the build environment. Reactive species 
should be avoided so as not to form carbides, nitrides, or oxides during the printing process. 
A vacuum or inert gas is therefore generally applied to the deposition area (Martukanitz, 
2020).  
Due to these factors, LP-DED produced parts tend to have highly different local 
compositions (Campbell et al., 2020). This can cause the phases and precipitates to differ 
from those expected. For 316L, it is found that it mainly contains austenite, with some 
indications of retained ferrite, but no martensite (Fayazfar et al., 2018). Due to the low 
carbon content in 316L, no carbides are expected to form (Campbell et al., 2020).

2.4 Mechanical properties 
This section aims to outline the effect of the processing parameters, which can be tailored to 
achieve the desired mechanical properties through the above mentioned microstructural 
mechanisms. 
 
2.4.1 Hardness 
Hardness is related to grain size by the Hall-Petch relationship, i.e., smaller grains result in 
higher hardness, as explained in the overview by Yazar et al. (2022). This is due to the grain 
boundaries' ability to hinder dislocation movement (Campbell, et al., 2020). It is therefore 
not only the grain size that influences hardness, but also the dislocation density, as well as 
the amount of precipitates. Although finer grain structures result in higher hardness, scan 
speed seems to have little effect (Yazar et al., 2022), while higher laser power is found to 
reduce hardness in 316 (Zhang et al., 2013).  
Fayazfar et al. (2018) describe a unique micro-hardness evolution in 316 LP-DED produced 
parts. Due to the residual heat from the underlying layers, an annealing effect occurs. 
Further, the heat buildup causes the solidification rate to slow down over time, allowing the 
formation of larger grains. This results in high micro-hardness near the substrate, which 
gradually decreases towards the center, only to start increasing again towards the top layers. 
Furthermore, the surface of the melt pool might experience a quenching effect from the 
shielding gas, causing even higher micro-hardness at the free surface. This results in a much 
higher localized micro-hardness than conventional cold-rolled material. The micro-hardness 
is further reported to increase with time in between layers (Fayazfar et al., 2018).

3.2 Statistics - Design of Experiments 
In the context of Design of Experiments, DOE, an experiment is defined as a test of the 
effects certain parameters have on an outcome. It can be parameters that are known to have 
an effect, but where the magnitude is unknown, or it can be an investigation of a set of 
parameters to quantify their relative importance. In both cases, the aim is to develop a 
predictive model between the input and the output. It is therefore also important to note that 
the model can describe only the effects within the experimental setup. It is not a simulation 
(Kulahci, 2024).  
When designing the experiment, it is important to randomize it. In this way, errors due to 
the order of the experimental setup are avoided, i.e., there are no effects between being the 
first or third run of the experiment. Replication in the data is likewise sought for, as this 
helps lower the error from the observational method. If many parameters are chosen for the 
setup, it can be beneficial to select the most significant and block the rest, as this can 
otherwise introduce too much noise in the test. The interaction between multiple parameters 
can be important to identify. An interaction is when a change in one parameter changes the 
effect of another (Kulahci, 2024).  
A full factorial DOE encompasses many of the above-mentioned key points. It checks the 
outcome for all possible parameter combinations, as well as the interaction between 
parameters. This can be done by as little as a two-level design, meaning the parameters only 
need to be analysed at a high and low setting. Another possibility is to do a response surface 
design. This type of design is used for analysing the optimal parameter values and can better 
handle non-linear modelling. It does, however, need observations on more than two levels 
(Montgomery, 2013). 
When an analysis is made, it is important to check its validity. By making a plot of the 
residuals, i.e., the difference between the predicted and observed data points, it is possible to 
get a better understanding of the behavior of the model. The residuals should be distributed 
randomly around the centerline, with no outliers and no clustering or shape of the 
distributed residuals. If this is not the case, a transformation of the outcome data might be 
appropriate to adjust for abnormality. One of the transformations that can be made is a 
Box-Cox transformation. Here, the response y is changed so it no longer has a simple linear

3.3. Computational fluid dynamics using FLOW-3D 
Computational fluid dynamics, CFD, relies on software to study how fluids, often in motion, 
influence mechanisms that are influenced by heat transfer. Mechanisms that are highly 
complex and computationally demanding. The use of CFD can provide highly detailed, 
visualized, and comprehensive information about fluid flow to assist analytical and 
experimental analyses. For that reason, it is often employed at the early stages of design and 
production as an alternative to running expensive and time consuming experiments.  
All CFD analyses rely on what is known as the “governing equations”, which represent the 
laws of: 
1.​ Mass is always conserved. 
2.​ Newton's second law: the rate of change of the momentum is equal to the forces 
acting upon the fluid with a constant mass. 
3.​ First law of thermodynamics: Energy is conserved - the rate of change of the internal 
energy of the system is equal to the rate of change of the applied heat and the rate of 
change of the work done on/from the system. 
It is up to the user to apply the governing equations, which control how the fluid behaves 
under the simulation environment (Tu et al., 2018). The equations include the physical 
effects such as solidification, evaporation, the Marangoni, recoil pressure, and multiple 
reflections of the laser (Bayat et al., 2021). 
The computational domain of the simulation is defined with a mesh, which is a grid of cells. 
The mesh allows the software to divide the computational domain into a set of smaller 
subdomains, meaning that the governing equations can be numerically solved for each cell. It 
follows that the mesh is one of the controlling factors for the accuracy of the analyses; a 
smaller mesh size results in higher spatial resolution, but also higher computational cost. To 
allow for continuity of the material, CFD regards all material as a fluid, no matter its state, 
solid or liquid (Tu et al., 2018), with the gas phase being considered as void to ease the 
computation. To track the location of the fluid, the Volume of Fluid, VOF, method may be 
applied. Here, each cell is represented by a variable F, with a value 0 < F < 1. A value of 0 
represents a cell empty of fluid, and a value of 1 represents a cell filled with fluid. 
Intermediate values represent the fluid/void interface (Bayat et al., 2021). By use of the VOF 
method, the physical free surface can thereby be approximately reconstructed within each 
cell, allowing for sub-grid representation of the surface (Margolin et al., 1997). However, the 
spatial resolution of the simulation remains governed by the mesh size.  
CFD analysis consists of three main steps: preprocessor, solver, and postprocessor. As the 
solver performs the numerical calculations, it is not described here. The preprocessor and 
postprocessor, on the other hand, are explained below in relation to the software product, 
FLOW-3D, used for this work. 
The setup of the analysis, including material and geometry, is done with the preprocessor. 
This is handled using FLOW-3D AM 2025R1. To define a material, a geometry is created 
within the user interface, from where a fluid, i.e., the material, is assigned (Tu et al., 2018). 
In FLOW-3D AM 2025R1, the most common materials and their properties are readily

evaluate the physics governing the simulation.   
After running the analysis, the results are assessed. For this, the postprocessor, FLOW-3D 
POST 2024R1, is employed in the project. It allows for analysis and visualization of data 
using animations and plots (Flow Science, Inc., n.d.). 
 
3.4 Light Optical Microscopy - LOM 
A powerful tool for examining, for example, grain morphology and porosities is light optical 
microscopy, LOM. In this study, LOM was used in both brightfield and darkfield settings. In 
brightfield, the light is transmitted and reflected perpendicular to the surface. This results in 
the whole sample being illuminated, allowing for easy characterization of sample features 
such as grains. Darkfield imaging uses an inclined angle of the light source. As the light is 
coming from the side, only features that result in the light being scattered are illuminated. 
Features such as grain boundaries and porosities are therefore illuminated while the rest of 
the sample is not (Vander Voort, 2019). 
For both LOM settings, a careful preparation of the surface of the sample is necessary. In 
conjunction with the grinding and polishing steps, which are described in Section 4.1.4, an 
etching step is needed for revealing the morphology. The etchant works, for example, by 
targeting grain boundaries or coloring distinct phases in the sample (Struers, 2025). The 
etchant can be tailored depending on the material and features investigated. The chosen 
etchant for this project was Kroll’s etchant, consisting of 32 ml 5% HF, 38 ml H2O, and 10 ml 
68% HNO3. The choice of etchant was based on past studies, which had successfully revealed 
grain morphology for laser powder bed fusion 316L manufactured samples. 
Two different LOM microscopes were used in this project. The majority of the work was done 
with a Zeiss Axiolab 5 with superior image quality, while higher magnifications were made 
possible with a Zeiss Axio Vert.A1.

3.5 Confocal Microscopy 
For volumetric measurements of samples, two techniques are often used: “Coherent Confocal 
Microscopy” and “Focus Variation”. Both techniques are based on the principles of light 
optical microscopy, with the added ability to move in the z direction to allow for height 
measurements. In Coherent Confocal Microscopy, a pinhole in front of the detector is used. 
This allows for blocking out the light and thereby the image, which is not in focus. By 
sampling and processing these images, it is possible to determine the height of the image in 
focus. Focus Variation works in a similar way but without the pinhole. Instead, the focal 
depth is changed at each step in the z height. By comparing the maximum contrast at each 
height, it is possible to determine the height of the surface. Both these techniques are “line of 
sight”, meaning that they are not able to detect surfaces that are not directly in front of the 
lens (Turner et al., 2023).  
For this project, the confocal microscope Sensofar S neox was used. The microscope allows 
for measurement with a precision of up to 0,15 μm in line and space. Surfaces can be 
measured at an inclination of 70° to 86° depending on the characteristics, where rougher 
surfaces allow for a higher inclination. The microscope is capable of using and combining 
different measurement techniques, including Coherent Confocal Microscopy and Focus 
Variation (Sensofar metrology). 
 
3.6 Scanning Electron Microscopy - SEM  
In LOM, the limiting resolution factor is the wavelength of visible light. By using electrons 
instead of light, a much higher spatial resolution is obtained. The wavelength of the electrons 
can be tailored by the acceleration voltage, V, with higher acceleration voltages resulting in 
smaller wavelengths. To focus the electrons into a useful beam, a set of magnetic rings is 
used. These allow for magnifying and focusing in a similar way to glass lenses in LOM (Zhou 
& Wang, 2007). 
By focusing a beam of electrons, referred to as the primary electrons, on a material, the 
different signals radiating from the primary electrons interacting with the material can be 
captured by various sensors. If a scan of the sample surface is performed, capturing the 
signals at each location, an image can be produced (Zhou & Wang, 2007). 
For this project, a Sigma 300 by ZEISS, fitted with various detectors, was used for 
performing the SEM. 
 
3.6.1 Secondary electron detector - SED 
When the primary electrons excite atoms in the sample, secondary electrons are released. As 
these electrons have a much smaller energy, only the ones produced near the surface of the 
sample, close to the incident electron beam, are able to escape and be detected by a 
secondary electron detector, SED. This allows for a high lateral resolution with great 
topography information (Zhou & Wang, 2007).

3.6.2 Electron Backscatter Diffraction - EBSD 
Backscattered electrons are primary electrons that have been elastically scattered by 
interacting with the atoms and then re-emitted from the sample. These electrons are 
channeled by the crystal lattice, causing diffraction. The diffraction patterns can be detected 
by an electron backscatter diffraction, EBSD, detector. These patterns are made up of what is 
known as Kikuchi bands, where the angle between the bands is used to determine the 
orientation of the crystal lattice. This information can help to map out the grains, their 
orientation, and the types of grain boundaries in the sample.  
In practice, the analysis is performed by tilting the sample 70° to maximize the number of 
backscattered electrons. The detector is placed as close to the sample as possible for better 
detection. In general, the angular resolution is about 0,5°. 
A common way to represent the collected data is by an inverse pole figure, IPF, map. An IPF 
map uses a color coding of the grains, reflecting their direction in relation to the projection 
axis. One limitation of the IPF mapping, however, is its inability to indicate rotation about 
the projection axis. It is therefore used for the representation of the individual grains and 
their orientation with respect to the projection axis (Zhou & Wang, 2007). Another way to 
show the orientation of the grains, including their rotation, is with a pole figure, PF. The pole 
figures are the normal direction to a chosen family of planes, projected onto a sphere in 2D, 
representing the normal, longitudinal, and transverse directions of the sample. The common 
poles to plot are the {100}, {110}, and {111}. In the case of a completely random orientation 
of the grains, the pole figure shows no clustering of the projected points. This allows for the 
detection of texture in the sample (Central Facility for Advanced Microscopy and 
Microanalysis, 2013). 
 
3.6.3 Energy dispersive x-ray spectroscopy - EDS 
When the primary electrons interact with the atoms of the material, the atoms may be 
ionized, thereby ejecting an electron from the inner shells. When this happens, an electron 
from the outer shell will take up the vacant space. As this results in a decrease in energy, the 
excess energy is emitted as a characteristic x-ray. This x-ray can be detected by an energy 
dispersive x-ray spectroscopy, EDS, detector. Due to the energy of x-rays being elemental 
specific, they can be used to determine the elemental composition of the scan area (Zhou & 
Wang, 2007).  
However, the intensity of the emitted characteristics x-ray increases with the atomic number. 
Therefore, EDS has a disadvantage in detecting lighter elements, such as carbon. Elements 
with an atomic number of 8 or higher are generally considered to be reliably detected (Leng, 
2013).  
Further, the interaction volume for producing x-rays is much larger than that for 
backscattered electrons. This leads to a lower spatial resolution, typically on the micrometer 
scale (Zhou & Wang, 2007).

4.1.5.2 Defining the computational domain 
The substrate material was defined by creating a box measuring L: 36 mm, W: 20 mm, and 
H: 10 mm, and, as for the particle source, defined as “Fluid 1”, i.e., made of 316L. The void, 
i.e., everything inside the computational domain that was not considered to be the material
itself, was set to argon gas, also specified by the FLOW-3D material database.
The mesh was created by considering two domains: the upper, containing the particle source, 
and the lower, containing the substrate and the deposition. The upper mesh was set to L: 36 
mm, W: 20 mm, and H: 27,5 mm, and a cell size of 20 mm. The lower mesh was more 
complicated and defined as a grading as illustrated in Figure 4.11. A grading allows for the 
cell size to gradually increase or decrease. This reduces the computational load while 
maintaining the high spatial resolution where needed. It was set to L: 36 mm, W: 20 mm, 
and H: 12 mm. The cell size was set to 1 mm for the length, gradually decreasing to 0,1 mm 
over the first 8 mm from both ends. For the width, it was likewise set to 1 mm, and decreased 
towards 0,1 mm for the first 7 mm. Finally, the height was set to increase from the top 4 mm 
of 0,1 mm cells to the 1 mm cells of the bottom part, 8 mm below. This led to a total of 
915.832 cells across both mesh domains. The upper and lower mesh were set to overlap by 
0,5 mm, with a "continuous" boundary condition to seamlessly transfer information across 
the two mesh domains. All other surfaces of the mesh were set to symmetry, except for the 
top surface of the upper mesh, which was set to pressure to allow for particles to escape the 
domain.

mm. The red grid indicates the finer cell size of 0,1 mm, while the yellow is the graded region.

4.1.5.3 Physics considered 
The governing equations were applied under the “physics” tab in FLOW-3D. These included: 
-​
Density evaluation, which allowed for the buoyancy effect in the molten state.   
-​
Gravity, for its influence on the particle movement. 
-​
Heat source, from where the reflections were enabled. A maximum of 12 reflections, 
with an attenuation rate for the end of reflection of 0,01, was added. Allowing for 
capturing its contribution to the formation of keyholes. 
-​
Heat transfer was enabled with “first order”, meaning that only the neighbouring 
cells would be considered for the heat transfer calculations.  
-​
Two-way momentum coupling was enabled under particles to allow for calculations 
of the forces acting on the melt pool as a result of the particle impact. 
-​
Phase change to evaluate any change in energy distribution due to the release or 
absorption of latent heat. 
-​
Shielding gas was enabled to account for the forces exerted on the melt pool. With a 
reported shield gas flow rate of 20 L/min, and an exit nozzle area of 1,5 cm2, given by 
the CAD model, the shield gas velocity was found to be 222 cm/s.  
-​
The surface tension model was enabled, which, together with the surface tension 
coefficient for 316L, would allow for calculations of the Marangoni effect. 
-​
Finally, viscosity and turbulence were enabled, with the flow set to laminar for better 
calculations of the liquid 316L. 
 
4.1.5.4 Execution and post processing of the simulation 
A sample, ST_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4_d2,3, was used to calibrate the simulation. This was done 
by changing one parameter at a time, and comparing the result of a simulated cross section 
with a cross section of the sample characterized by LOM, until satisfactory results were 
obtained. The investigated parameters that were gradually changed and assessed were: 
-​
Distance from substrate to powder source. Distances of: 21,5, 22,5, and 25 mm. 
-​
Distance from substrate surface to powder and laser source. Distances of: 22, 23, 25, 
and 28 mm. 
-​
Particle generation rate in relation to the specified mass flow: 100%, 90% and 80%. 
-​
Powder nozzle diameter of the stated 1,5 mm and for 2,7 mm. 
-​
Absorption rate between the laser and the material. For substrate material: multiple 
values between 0,2 and 0,5. For the particles: multiple values between 0,01 and 0,1. 
-​
Tabulated values for surface tension adopted from Ebrahimi et al. (2022), as opposed 
to the linearly temperature dependent coefficient from the FLOW-3D database. 
The most accurate results were obtained with a distance between the substrate and the 
particle source of 22,5 mm, in contrast to the stated 25 mm, and an absorption rate of 0,38 
for the substrate and 0,1 for the particles. Everything else was kept as reported. 
All simulations were set to a runtime of 1,2 seconds, allowing for the melt pool to reach its 
quasi steady state. Afterwards, all simulations were assessed in the postprocessor FLOW-3D 
POST 2025R1. Here, visual representations of the simulation were made, and cross sections 
were extracted with the FLSGRF Slices filter.

direction of the heat flow, see the enlarged image of ST_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4_d2,3 in Figure

caution, as columnar grains, when looked at from the growth direction, will appear equiaxed. 
From the scan direction images in Figure 5.4b, dendritic growth along the free surface can be 
seen, further emphasizing that the seemingly equiaxed grains in the transverse direction may 
in fact be columnar.  
At a higher mass flow rate of 25,9 g/min, the grain morphology changes. Figure 5.5 shows an 
enlarged image of ST_P3,8_v500_ṁ25,9_d5,0. Here, a coarse dendritic microstructure 
growing perpendicular to the free surface is found, see Figure 5.5a and b. For both samples 
printed with a mass flow rate of 25,9 g/min, it is observed that the zone near the substrate 
has a different morphology compared to the bulk. Here, an equiaxed dendritic structure is 
observed from the transverse and directional images, as shown in Figure 5.5a, c, and d. This 
can be explained by the higher mass flow leading to lower energy density, and thereby less 
heat in the melt pool as compared to samples with a lower mass flow. This causes a low 
temperature gradient, which, according to constitutional supercooling, see Section 2.3.3, 
leads to the formation of equiaxed grains.

5.1.3 Melt pool geometry 
5.1.3.1 Penetration 
The penetration percentage is given for all samples in Figure 5.2. It indicates that laser power 
and mass flow has a great impact on both the overall penetration and the penetration 
percentage, while scan speed primarily changes the overall penetration. The figure shows 
that ST_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4_d2,3 and ST_P2,3_v500_ṁ13,4_d2,3 are the only samples to 
exhibit satisfactory penetration with a penetration percentage value of 56% and 33%, 
respectively. This is higher than the recommended 10% - 30% found in the literature, see 
Section 2.4.2. However, the observed deep penetration is highly localized with a surrounding 
lower penetration. For this setup, a penetration of 10% - 30% would therefore, likely, cause 
bonding issues.  
Further, the scan direction cross sectional view in Figure 5.2 shows 
ST_P1,3_v500_ṁ6,4_d2,3 to penetrate into the substrate, even though the transverse cross 
sectional view indicate no penetration. This is attributed to the varying penetration depth 
along the length of the track, which is particularly visible for ST_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4_d2,3 
and ST_P2,3_v500_ṁ13,4_d2,3. These two samples, with clear signs of varying penetration 
depth, were further measured from the LOM results. As both the height and penetration of 
the track are at maximum value near the center, caution had to be taken when measuring the

A full factorial DOE was made to investigate how the process parameters influence the track

The DOE model for width, as seen in Figure 5.8, shows a strong dependence on scan speed,

cause a slight decrease in width, which is unexpected and not in accordance with previous 
studies, see Section 2.2.2. Interestingly, laser power and scan speed, as well as laser power 
and mass flow, have a significant interaction as seen from the effect summary in Figure 5.8.

deposited would result in a wider track. An explanation may be the faster cooling rate 
exhibited by an excessive amount of material flow to the melt pool area. In the case of a low 
mass flow, a higher local energy density per added material is expected. This leads to 
complete melting of the material, with the substrate absorbing more heat. As a result, the 
melt pool has more time to flatten and widen. If, instead, an excessive amount of material is 
being added, the powder cools down the melt pool faster, resulting in less residual heat in the 
substrate and an earlier solidification of the melt pool, giving no time for widening. This 
leads to a significant change in height, as seen in Figure 5.9, a slightly smaller width, and also 
a lower angle. This explanation is further backed by the LOM results seen in Figure 5.2.

ST_P2,0_v500_ṁ13,4_d2,3 had resulted in less penetration of the substrate, indicating a 
decrease in residual heat, and a visible increase in the height, with a slight decrease in width.

point exists, where the mass flow will change from a widening to a narrowing of the width. 
This, however, has not been investigated, as only two values of mass flow were incorporated

5.2.2.5 Layer height invalidating cuboids for testing HDR 
From the literature, an HDR of ⁓ 1,5 is reported to result in the highest density samples, i.e., 
low density of porosities. HDR is controlled by spot size, scan speed, and most dominantly, 
layer height. A set of samples with a parameter configuration for achieving an HDR of ⁓ 1,5 
had been made based on the DOE, see Appendix D. However, the layer heights for these 
samples are found to be far from the predicted values, as seen in Figure 5.22. 
Figure 5.22: Graph showing the actual average layer height, with error bars, and the expected values for samples 
testing for an HDR of ⁓ 1,5. 
The reason for the layer height of the HDR samples being exceptionally far from the expected 
values is to be found in how the parameters were chosen. Using a DOE approach, the scan 
speed needed for achieving a layer height that would fulfill an HDR of ⁓ 1,5, came to be 199 
mm/min and 260,5 mm/min for the two samples, see Appendix D. This is outside the 
modeling space, where the lowest scan speed is 500 mm/min. This, together with an 
approximation for hatch spacing, also found in Appendix D, explains why the layer heights 
are about half of the expected values.  
Based on the measured layer height, the HDR for all cuboids were calculated and are 
summarized in Table 5.2. Also given are the values for specific energy density and volumetric 
energy density. With the latter being dependent on layer height. It shows that the HDR 
values for the cuboids to be tested for the theory of HDR are ⁓ 2,78 and ⁓ 2,89. This is further 
from the ideal value of ⁓ 1,5 compared to C_P2,0_v500_ṁ13,4 and
C_P2,3_v500_ṁ13,4_<H, which had an HDR value of ⁓ 1,98 and ⁓ 2,06, respectively.
Therefore, it was decided not to do further investigations on the samples originally intended 
for HDR, but instead use the samples with an HDR more true to the 1,5 for analysis of its 
effect on porosities.

Figure 5.23 below shows four brightfield LOM images of the cuboids of the transverse

For multi-layer builds, the grain morphology changes as more material and multiple heating 
cycles are introduced, as explained in Section 2.3. The following is a detailed investigation of 
the grain morphology of the cuboids and how they correlate to the process parameters.  
5.2.4.1 Large scale LOM investigations of grain structures 
The LOM images, see Figure 5.23 and Appendix I, reveal that the grain morphology is 
dominated by columnar epitaxial growth in all samples, with indications of equiaxed grains 
near the top surface, as indicated for C_P1,8_v500_ṁ6,4. To further investigate this,

5.2.4.2 Grain growth and orientation 
From LOM, sample C_P1,8_v800_ṁ6,4 and C_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4, with their big difference 
in Ed, have shown to have different melt pool geometries and grain structure. These samples 
were therefore selected for further analysis using EBSD. Note also that only limited EBSD 
was done, as master students are not allowed to operate the SEM, and the investigations 
therefore relied on assistance by more senior scientists. A set of IPF maps of the two samples 
is given in Figure 5.26. 
Figure 5.26: IPF mapping of C_P1,8_v800_ṁ6,4 and C_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4 at the center and bottom near the 
substrate. Also indicated are values for Ed [J/mm2] and EVED [J/mm3]. 
The high number of grains in the bottom layer of Figure 5.26 is due to the many nucleation 
sites provided by the substrate surface. As explained in the literature, when the melt 
solidifies, the grains grow epitaxially, inheriting the crystallographic orientation of the grain 
they nucleate on, see Section 2.3.2. The many different orientations in the first few deposited 
layers are therefore a result of the many grain orientations found in the substrate. As these 
grains continue to grow, those with their preferred growth direction oriented parallel to the 
heat flow direction will grow faster than the other grains, eventually eliminating other 
orientations. This is the cause for the extended columnar grains growing epitaxially across 
multiple layers, resulting in the bigger grains seen in the center of Figure 5.26.

The few smaller grains of the center may be due to turbulent flow in the melt pool, which is 
described in the literature to cause dendrites to break off as they form during solidification, 
see Section 2.3.3. These shattered dendrite tips can act as new nucleation sites, resulting in 
the formation of small grains with different orientations compared to the surrounding 
grains.      
The higher heat input indicated by the Ed of  C_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4 is confirmed to cause the 
formation of the many grains deviating from growing in the build direction, as it has been 
observed with LOM, see the bottom images of Figure 5.26. However, it can be seen that this 
phenomenon did not persist as the build progressed. To further assess the grain orientation 
of the two samples, a set of pole figures were extracted.

C_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4. 
Figure 5.27 shows that both samples had a strong texture with the <100> direction parallel 
to the build direction, z, i.e., the heat flow direction. This is well in accordance with the 
literature and is explained by the preferred growth direction of FCC metals to be <100>, see 
Section 2.3.2. A band around the equator of the {100} figures can be noticed. This is a 
consequence of the <100> preferential growth direction aligning with the z direction, but 
may have any direction along x and y, causing a <100> fibre texture. The lower contrast 
found for C_P1,8_v800_ṁ6,4 might be due to the sample having smaller and thereby more 
grains, due to its lower Ed causing a higher cooling rate, which results in more scattering of 
crystal orientations. It could also be an indication of higher residual stresses, also known to 
be caused by a low heat input, as this also results in scattering of the signal. Note also that 
the relatively low number of high density spots and lack of bands in the pole figure are due to 
the samples only containing a small amount of grains.

5.2.4.4 Solidiﬁcation structures 
The LOM results seen in the high magnification images of Figures 5.24 and 5.25 revealed a 
solidification structure deviating within the melt pool. Figure 5.31 shows an enlarged image 
of a melt pool in the center of C_P1,8_v800_ṁ6,4. Here, cellular structures at the bottom of 
the melt pool and columnar dendrites with wide secondary arms at the top are found. This 
phenomenon is commonly observed in welding as described in Section 2.3.3. While the 
image only shows a melt pool for C_P1,8_v800_ṁ6,4, the same phenomenon is observed 
for all samples, primarily in the center- to the top-region. This is attributed to the high 
temperature gradient that exists at the melt pool boundary. It is known that a temperature 
gradient is one of the controlling factors for solidification structures, with higher gradients 
resulting in cellular growth, and lower gradients allowing for dendritic growth, see Section 
2.3.3.

5.2.5 Porosities 
Porosities can be a fatal flaw influencing performance. Different guidelines for porosity 
control are given in the literature and tested below. 
5.2.5.1 HDR and parameters for porosity control 
Figure 5.23 in the previous section shows that the higher mass flow of C_P2,0_v500_ṁ13,4 
results in lack of fusion porosities near the substrate, which is in agreement with the 
literature, see Section 2.3.1. This, however, is eliminated in subsequent layers. Besides lack 
of fusion, the same sample also shows a high amount of relatively large spherical voids, 
indicating keyhole porosities. However, as for the single-tracks, the amount of porosities is 
reduced by raising the heat input, either with higher laser power as for 
C_P2,3_v500_ṁ13,4 or by lowering the mass flow as for C_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4 shown in 
Figure 5.32. This suggests the porosities to be of another origin than keyhole porosities.  
Figure 5.32: LOM showing large spherical porosities, and how they are mitigated by control of laser power and 
mass flow. Higher resolution images are found in Appendix I. 
To further investigate porosities, an Archimedes density test was performed. The relative 
densities, i.e., the density of the samples compared to wrought 316L, for different values of 
laser power, scan speed, and mass flow, are given in Figure 5.33.

Furthermore, the literature suggests, see Section 2.3.1, that an HDR of ⁓ 1,5 should result in 
the densest parts, proving it to be a tool for predicting porosities. However, the almost 
identical densities of sample C_P1,8_v800_ṁ6,4, with the highest tested HDR of 5,09, and 
C_P2,0_v500_ṁ13,4, with the lowest HDR of 1,98, prove that HDR is not able to predict 
the density of the samples. Additionally, the LOM results from Figure 5.32 showed the same 
sample with an HDR of 1,98 to contain the highest visible amounts of porosities, further 
emphasizing the poor predictability of HDR.

5.2.7.1 Hardness evolution throughout the prints 
A plot was made to show hardness as a function of distance from the substrate for 
C_P1,8_v800_ṁ6,4 and C_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4. For details of the scanned area, see

Figure 5.38: Plot of hardness, in HV, as a function of distance from substrate surface, in mm. Based on data from

Figure 5.38 shows that all samples have the highest hardness near the substrate surface and

hardness. As the build progresses, the accumulated heat reduces the cooling rate, resulting in 
bigger grains and thereby lower hardness as described in Section 2.4.1. The same 
phenomenon, of higher heat causing lower hardness, is likely the cause for 
C_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4, with the highest Ed, to have a lower measured center hardness than 
C_P1,8_v500_ṁ6,4 and C_P1,8_v800_ṁ6,4. In all three cases, the hardness is found to 
have increased for the final layer. Across all samples, not just the above mentioned, an 
increase of ⁓ 5 HV is found between the last two measurement points. This is explained in 
the literature by the shielding gas and the conduit gas causing a quenching effect, leading to 
smaller grains and higher hardness at the top surface, see Section 2.4.1.

C_P1,8_v800_ṁ6,4 and C_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4, was constructed.

6 Discussion 
The following is a discussion of the key observations done in this work. 
6.1 Theoretical guidelines for process control 
From the literature study, Section 2, energy density, HDR, and optimal hatch spacing were 
identified as guidelines for process control. These are further discussed in detail, and a novel 
term, Ed/ṁ, is proposed. 
6.1.1 HDR as a tool for porosity control 
From the literature, an HDR of ⁓ 1,5 is reported to result in the densest samples. From the 
brightfield LOM analysis, the single-track sample, ST_P3,0_v500_ṁ25,9_d5,0, with the 
lowest HDR of ⁓ 1,98, i.e., the value closest to the ideal 1,5, was not found to contain more or 
less porosities than the other investigated samples. Further, this sample was found to have 
the highest degree of lack of fusion compared to other samples with higher HDR values. 
Further analysis of the single-track samples using darkfield LOM showed a high density of 
suspected ⁓ 1 μm gas porosities for ST_P3,0_v500_ṁ25,9_d5,0, with an HDR of ⁓ 1,98, and
even more so for ST_P2,3_v500_ṁ13,4_d2,3, with an HDR of ⁓ 2,4, indicating that HDR
might be useful. However, these suspected gas porosities may also be a product of the 
dendrite arms having been cut off and looked at from the side. Further analysis using EDS, 
see Section 5.2.6, found former oxide particles to leave indentations in the surface, 
corresponding to the same size as the suspected porosities found with LOM. It can therefore 
not be concluded that the sample with a low HDR value contains fewer gas porosities.   
Furthermore, the cuboid LOM analyses of sample C_P2,0_v500_ṁ13,4 and its 
corresponding single-track ST_P2,0_v500_ṁ13,4_d2,3, revealed large spherical porosities 
of unknown origin. Other samples with higher values of HDR were found to contain fewer or 
none of these porosities. 
The inability of HDR was even further emphasized by the density analysis. Sample 
C_P2,0_v500_ṁ13,4 and C_P2,3_v500_ṁ13,4_<H showed to have the lowest HDRs of ⁓
1,98 and ⁓ 1,97, respectively. However, all cuboid samples had similar values for density, 
with the highest HDR having a slightly higher density, see Section 5.2.5.

6.1.2 Hatch spacing for lack of fusion control 
The theory of optimal hatch spacing serves two different purposes: mainly eliminating lack of 
fusion, and predicting the height of multilayer structures.  
Concerning lack of fusion, the cuboid LOM results showed that three different values of 
hatch spacing all produced the same degree of lack of fusion for the first deposited layer. 
Furthermore, the density test, Section 5.2.5 showed that all values of hatch spacing had the 
same density. This indicates that the use of hatch spacing as a controlling factor for lack of 
fusion should be done with caution. The same applies to other types of porosities found in 
this study. 
6.1.3 Predictability of energy density: Ed and EVED 
Two different ways of calculating energy density were identified and tested. The specific 
energy density, Ed, uses laser power, scan speed, and spot size, while the volumetric energy 
density, EVED, excludes spot size and instead relies on hatch spacing and layer height as 
illustrated in Figure 6.2 and described in Section 2. A plot of penetration percentage, grain 
size, and hardness as a function of Ed and EVED is shown in Figure 6.1. Also indicated is the 
expected trend for energy density, Eexpected, found in the literature, see Section 2. Note that 
Eexpected only shows the trend, i.e., higher E leads to higher hardness, and the values are 
therefore not representative of true measurements.

6.1.4  A novel term for parameter control: Ed/ṁ
Figure 6.2: Illustration of the different terms for energy density and what parameters, in blue, and indirectly, in 
green, they capture. P is laser power, v is scan speed, dspot is laser spot size diameter, s is hatch spacing, l is layer 
height, and ṁ is mass flow. Drawn using Affinity Designer. 
Although EVED was thought to be a promising term for parameter control, this was not found 
to be the case. One explanation is that a higher mass flow affects more than just the 
deposition volume. The higher mass flow will inevitably cause a larger amount of material to 
be present within the laser beam at any given time, causing a shadowing effect, leading to a 
lower laser intensity in the melt region. Therefore, a new term based on energy density is 
proposed. By taking the local energy density per added mass, Ed/ṁ is obtained. In doing so, 
not only is the amount of material being deposited captured indirectly, but also the 
shadowing effect, as illustrated in Figure 6.2  
Ed/ṁ was calculated with mass flow given in g/s and is plotted in Figure 6.1 as well. It shows 
that Ed/ṁ predicts all outcomes well. In Figure 6.1, the penetration was determined based on 
the LOM of the single-tracks. But as EVED relies on layer height and hatch spacing, only the 
single-tracks, which were replicated as cuboids, were used. Figure 6.7 shows a trend line of 
measured penetration percentage of all single-tracks as a function of Ed/ṁ, where the values 
are somewhat well predicted, further indicating the predictability of Ed/ṁ. 
Another advantage by incorporating mass flow, is the ability to translate from single-tracks 
to cuboids. As EVED relies on hatch spacing and layer height, it cannot be reliably applied to 
single-tracks. On the other hand, Ed lacks a term for amount of volume being deposited, 
which affects the melt pool size, and thereby the hatch spacing and layer height for 
multilayer structures. The term Ed/ṁ will therefore also help bridge the gap when moving 
from single-tracks to multilayer structures.

6.2 Unmatched layer height 
Using the theory for optimal hatch spacing, it was expected that the height of the 
single-tracks would equate the layer height of the cuboids. However, this was not the case, as 
the cuboids showed a higher layer height, independent of smaller, equal, or higher step-up 
values than the single-track height, see Section 5.2.2.3. Besides the unknown reason for the 
unmatched layer height, it was also expected that a step-up value higher or lower than the 
wanted value would cause a significant change in layer height, as this would cause 
de-focusing issues. Three explanations for this are discussed below. 
6.2.1 Heat buildup leading to bigger deposition volume 
One hypothesis is that during printing, the heat buildup will cause the melt pool to gradually 
widen. The bigger surface area will allow for a larger fraction of powder being deposited, 
leading to a bigger deposition volume, which adds to the height. This also explains why the 
44 layer build has a bigger layer height than the 22 layer build. This is in part supported by 
the DOE analysis, where higher laser power, which is analogous to the higher heat buildup, is 
found to cause a substantial widening with relatively low change in height, indicating bigger 
deposition volumes. While the height remains unaffected for the single-tracks, the effect of 
having an adjacent track to the deposition, as in the case for the cuboids, may force the 
increased deposition volume to add to the height.  
This phenomenon should be indicated by the measurement of the individual melt pool depth 
as in Section 5.2.2.3. However, the accumulated heat would also affect the penetration depth, 
thereby masking the increase in layer height when measuring between the melt pool 
separation lines, see Section 4.2.2 for an explanation on the measuring procedure. A closer 
comparison of the top melt pool, where no separation line exists, of C_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4  
and its corresponding single-track shows an increase in height, see Figure 6.3. This indicates 
that the higher accumulated heat does lead to an increased layer height.

varying number of layers, showing the overall melt pool depth of the first deposit and the last top-layer deposit.

6.2.3 Under-/over-focusing causing difference in layer height 
In Section 5.2.2.3, it was reported that a step-up value compared to the wanted value of -0,2, 
0, 0,2, and 0,4 all resulted in the layer height being greater than expected. It was 
contemplated that the accumulation of the step-up values for each layer would cause a 
defocusing issue over time. To investigate this further, a simulation using FLOW-3D was 
performed on ST_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4_d2,3, where the distance from the substrate to the 
processing head, i.e., to the powder nozzle and laser beam location, was offset by -3, 0, and 3 
mm to simulate the step-up error. The results are presented below. 
Figure 6.6: Simulated cross section of ST_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4_d2,3 with an offset of -3 mm, 0 mm, and +3 mm. 
The simulation in Figure 6.6 shows that for the height to increase, the distance between the 
processing head and the substrate should be increased as well. However, in the experimental 
setup, the step-up value was both negatively and positively offset, and in all cases, it caused 
an increased layer height. Further, the simulation in Figure 6.6 used a pre-defined powder 
source offset of -2,5 mm to account for not all particles coinciding with the melt pool. 
Positively offsetting the processing head by +3 mm caused the powder nozzle to be back in 
focus, resulting in higher powder catchment efficiency for that particular simulation.  
It therefore cannot be concluded how defocusing of the processing head can influence the 
layer height. Instead, it is expected that the adjacent track and the heat buildup were the 
primary contributors to the observed increase in layer height. 
This suggests that for the theory for optimal hatch spacing to predict layer height, it should 
be further developed to incorporate the effect of adjacent tracks and the buildup of heat due 
to multiple layers.

6.3 Modeling of melt pool geometry 
Being able to predict the melt pool geometry based on process parameters will result in 
better estimations and control of part geometry, evidently speeding up production and 
lowering the cost associated with printing. For this, two approaches are discussed. One based 
on the analytical results of the performed DOE, and one on the fluid dynamic simulations 
performed using FLOW-3D. 
6.3.1 Quality of the DOE 
A key part of a well-constructed DOE is randomization, where every experiment is run 
separately. In the case of the single-tracks, the LP-DED was set up with the input 
parameters, and one parameter was changed at a time to achieve the desired samples. 
Ideally, the machine should have been rebooted in between each sample printing, and the 
samples should have been printed in a random order, not grouped into samples with similar 
conditions. To account for this, a split-plot design would have been beneficial, as it would 
allow the software to consider the grouped design. It would also have been possible to 
analyse how significant the lack of randomization was by doing a nested design. However, 
this was realized late in the project, and since the DOE was not a main focus, this was left 
out. 
In line with randomization, replications were not made in an optimal way. For each sample, 
three curves were extracted and analysed as mentioned above. Ideally, one curve extracted 
from three different samples of the same settings, produced in a random order, should have 
been collected. For a lack of fit analysis, replicates produced by repeated measurements of 
the same sample can pollute the data (Kraber, 2022). This explains the poor lack of fit seen 
in Figures 5.8 - 5.10, where it never exceeded 0,012. 
For the printing of the samples, four different parameters were adjusted: spot size, mass 
flow, laser power, and scan speed. However, the preliminary DOE, see Appendix D, only 
incorporated laser power and scan speed. It was observed that the spread in the residual 
distribution for height was greatly improved by incorporating just two levels of mass flow 
into the analysis. It is therefore assumed that other parameters might influence the model, 
which was not incorporated. Such parameters could be the mass flow of the conduit gas, 
which is known to affect the cooling, see Section 2.4.1, and can affect the speed at which the 
powder impedes on the melt pool.

6.3.2 Use of DOE as an analytical and predictive tool 
A tool for predicting the melt pool geometry, the prediction profiler, was developed based on 
the DOE. The prediction profiler allows for the prediction of the geometry in two different 
ways. Either based on the input parameters or by specifying a target geometry value. For the 
latter, the desirability feature is used to define the importance and tolerance for each 
geometrical parameter. From this, the needed input values are estimated. 
The prediction profiler does, however, have its limitations. As already mentioned, it was built 
partly on missing data for 1,3 kW and 1,5 kW laser power at a mass flow of 13,4 g/min. The 
reason for the missing data was due to low quality deposits as described in Section 5.1.1. The 
model should therefore be used with caution when approaching this region of the dataspace. 
It should also be noted that the model is not a simulation, and it is therefore expected to 
perform poorly outside the dataspace. This was also evident from the cuboid printing, testing 
for an HDR of ⁓ 1,5. Here, the models for Box-Cox transformation and 2nd order polynomial 
obtained from the DOE, were used to achieve a height of ⁓ 2,3 and ⁓ 2 mm, respectively, at a 
scan speed of ⁓ 200 and ⁓ 260 mm/min, which is outside the dataspace. However, the true 
layer height of both cuboids was found to be ⁓ 1 mm, see Section 5.2.2.5. There are many 
reasons why the desired layer height was not achieved, such as the circle approximation for 
hatch spacing described in Appendix “D”, but the main reason was likely due to modeling far 
outside the dataspace. However, using the prediction profiler within the dataspace was found 
to yield predictions close to the actual measurements as described in Section 5.1.4.1. 
As the prediction profiler was only based on width, height, and angle measurements, it was 
not directly able to predict if the deposit would have sufficient penetration into the 
underlying material. The ability to estimate the penetration from the already measured 
values was therefore further explored. Both the ratio between the width and the height w/h, 
as well as the angle, all found in Appendix E, were investigated. The already used input 
parameters were also analysed by using the novel term Ed/ṁ. A data plot was made to 
visualize how well each method would predict the penetration, as shown in Figure 6.7. 
Figure 6.7: Plot of penetration percentage as a function of angle, w/h, and Ed/ṁ.

6.3.3 FLOW-3D for simulation on melt pool geometry 
As mentioned above, the prediction profiler is a statistical tool capable of handling and 
evaluating the tested parameters within the dataspace. FLOW-3D, on the other hand, is an 
advanced CFD simulation tool, capable of handling a vast number of governing physics in 
complex setups. In this work, the model was calibrated to one sample,  
ST_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4_d2,3, from which five simulations were run and compared with the 
LOM and confocal microscopy results. The results were that width, height, and angle, and to 
some extent also penetration, were reasonably well predicted for the two samples of the same 
mass flow as the calibrated sample.  
Multiple reasons for the poor prediction of penetration, and for width, height, and angle at a 
higher mass flow exist. From a software point of view, it is possible that some physics in 
relation to the governing equations were overlooked. It is also possible that the values 
imported for 316L from FLOW-3D’s database did not correspond to the experimentally 
tested material, although different values were assessed for their influence. Lastly, powder 
particles are difficult to handle, and for the simulated setup, the powder was assumed to 
follow a straight trajectory from the nozzle to the substrate. This, however, is not the case in 
reality, where the streams of particles interact with each other, the shield gas, and gravity, to 
instead follow a complex and curved trajectory. The distance between the powder nozzles 
and the substrate was offset in an effort to compensate for this effect, thereby lowering the 
catchment efficiency. From an experimental point of view, it is possible that different values 
can have deviated from the stated. The LOM results, see Figure 5.2 and 5.7, showed the 
penetration to be shifted to the side, indicating that the laser beam and powder were not in 
focus. Further, the laser intensity distribution is never truly Gaussian. It would therefore 
enhance the simulation if the intensity distribution is measured and implemented.  
FLOW-3D has already proven to be a useful tool for predicting melt pool geometry in the 
vicinity of the calibrated parameter range. However, with the above implementations, it is 
likely to be an even better predictor, enabling precise simulations for a greater range of 
parameters.

7 Conclusion 
In this project, the influence of key parameters, together with a set of guidelines for 
microstructure and mechanical performance optimization was studied in relation to 316L 
produced by LP-DED. The work included LOM, EBSD, EDS, Archimedes testing, hardness 
testing, tensile testing, and further analysis using DOE and FLOW-3D. The most relevant 
findings related to the key parameters are mapped in Figure 7.1.  
Melt pool geometry
Higher mass flow is found to decrease width, which is unexpected, but can be explained by a 
premature solidification of the melt pool. Height is shown to increase with an increase in 
mass flow, while angle decreases. The mass flow is also found to affect penetration, where 
higher mass flow leads to lower penetration, ultimately causing lack of fusion. 
The increase of laser power is found to largely increase the width and angle, i.e., the contact 
angle between the deposit and substrate. Laser power is also found not to have a great 
impact on height, which contradicts previous studies. Furthermore, higher laser power is 
observed to increase the penetration.  
An increase in scan speed leads to a decrease in width and height, and an increase in angle. 
Higher scan speeds do not have a great impact on lowering the penetration percentage, but 
rather decrease the overall depth from the bottom to the top of the melt pool. 
Modeling of melt pool geometry
A tool, the prediction profiler, for determining the height, width, and angle based on the 
values of mass flow, laser power, and scan speed was developed. This can prove to be a vital 
tool in determining layer height and hatch spacing, without the need for printing and 
measuring single-tracks or relying on heavy computational simulations. The tool can be 
downloaded and opened in JMP, while users without a license can access a limited version of 
the tool through a browser. 
The computational software FLOW-3D 2025R1 was assessed. It was found to be a promising 
tool for predicting melt pool geometry, with the need for calibration of only a few samples.  
Sample geometry
It is found that the presence of an adjacent track, as for the cuboids, together with the 
accumulation of heat, causes an excessive increase in layer height as compared to the 
single-track height. This is attributed to the adjacent track functioning as a guide for the 
stream of powder, and the accumulated heat increasing the melt pool surface area, thereby 
increasing the powder catchment efficiency.

Guidelines 
The theory for optimal hatch spacing is reported in literature to mainly help reduce lack of 
fusion, but also to predict the layer height. However, hatch spacing is not found to impact 
lack of fusion, and is further shown to be suboptimal in predicting the layer height in the 
current setup.  
Heat penetration depth ratio, HDR, is reported in the literature to yield the highest part 
density at a value of ⁓ 1,5. However, all tested samples in the present work, with a varying 
HDR of 1,97 - 5,09, have close to identical densities, with no significant influence by HDR. 
Energy density is reported to help summarize multiple parameters into one term, allowing 
for easier prediction and comparison of results. Both local energy density, Ed, and volumetric 
energy density, EVED, were tested in this work. Ed is shown to explain, to some extent, both 
grain size and hardness. It does, however, not take mass flow into consideration, which also 
plays a significant role and is better captured by EVED. EVED is found to be a poor predictor for 
both grain size and hardness, but performs better than Ed when predicting penetration. Both 
expressions should therefore be used with caution.  
A novel interpretation, Ed/ṁ, of energy density is proposed. Preliminary results indicate that 
considering Ed/ṁ provides a more reliable predictor for grain size, hardness, and penetration 
percentage. Additionally, it facilitates easier comparison and translation of parameters 
between single-tracks and multilayer builds. 
Microstructure
The grain morphology based on the given parameters is found to consist of columnar grains 
that grow epitaxially, with the <100> direction aligning with the heat flow direction. The 
grain size is found to increase with energy input. Within the melt pools, the solidification 
structures are found to change from cellular to dendritic from the bottom towards the top of 
the melt pool. This effect is especially pronounced in multilayer builds. All of these findings 
are in agreement with the literature.  
A high mass flow is found to result in porosities with the same character as keyhole 
porosities, which seems to be mitigated to some extent by an increase in laser power. 
However, further analysis showed these porosities to likely be of another origin. It is 
suspected that higher mass flow can lead to the injection of gas into the melt pool, causing 
macroscopic porosities.  
Additionally, a density test was performed. It showed all samples to have a relative density of 
more than 99%.  
Mechanical properties
For mechanical properties, hardness is found to correlate with grain size. All samples were 
further shown to have an elongation of 50% - 75%, much greater than what is reported in the 
literature.

Outlook 
To further improve the DOE based modeling tool, the prediction profiler, more process 
parameters should be considered, such as processing head distance and conduit gas flow. 
The penetration should further be included as an output parameter for a wide range of 
parameter combinations. It would also be beneficial to have a lower stepwise increase in 
laser power to better capture the induced effect on melt pool geometry. The manufacturing of 
the single-tracks should also be optimized by randomization and replications, i.e., more than 
just one sample per parameter setting should be considered. Both of which would lead to a 
more robust model. Furthermore, the prediction profiler can be used to find height, width, 
and angle, but for optimal hatch spacing, the procedure has so far been to manually find the 
exact distance for realizing A1 = A2, see Section 4.2.1. A tool that automates this task would 
be highly beneficial.  
Further work is needed to understand why and by how much an adjacent track changes the 
melt pool geometry, as well as the effect of accumulated heat from previous layers. This 
would allow for a more precise tool for calculating hatch spacing to be incorporated into 
current slicer software packages for better geometry control, thereby contributing to the 
transition from near-net-shape to net-shape geometries.  
To better simulate the melt pool geometry, a full calibration and assessment of the 
computational fluid dynamics software, FLOW-3D 2025R1, is needed. This would include 
measurements of the actual beam intensity profile and focus point of the powder, as well as 
calibration of a wider range of samples. This could result in the software becoming a highly 
precise tool for predicting melt pool geometry across all parameter configurations. 
For a better understanding of the influence of process parameters on microstructure, a 
broader range of parameters should be investigated with EBSD and EDS. For this work, 
C_P1,8_v800_ṁ6,4 and C_P2,0_v500_ṁ6,4 were chosen based on their difference in 
melt pool morphology and apparent microstructure. This meant that the change in energy 
density was assessed rather than the individual parameters. EBSD should further be used to 
investigate the top surface to see if the observed microstructure, in fact, was equiaxed.  
The EDS analysis had indicated the presence of a higher density of silicon oxides in the high 
energy density sample. This could greatly influence the mechanical properties, and should be 
further assessed by analysing a bigger sample region and for more parameters using EDS.      
The possibility of producing high quality depositions with completely different grain 
morphology should be further assessed. From LOM, the single-tracks with a high mass flow 
of 25,9 g/min showed indications of an equiaxed solidification structure compared to the 
dendritic structures of the cuboids. However, the high degree of lack of fusion found at this 
high level of mass flow made them impractical for the cuboids. It may be possible to get a 
sufficient penetration by raising the laser power to around 18 kW, see Appendix K, however, 
the optics used in the FORCE Technology setup have an upper limit of 6 kW. 
To better understand why the YS did not correspond to the found hardness data and the 
reason for the observed excessive elongation, EBSD should be performed on the fractured 
tensile samples. Further, a tensile test using standard sized samples should be performed.
