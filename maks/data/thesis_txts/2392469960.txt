Abstract
This thesis mainly focuses on modelling the thermal dynamics of a residential building
and an electric water heater, which can support for analyzing how much flexibility
can be provided by those components to the eletrical grid. In this thesis, a control
strategy is designed to control electric water heater to provide services to the grid.
This thesis starts with a case study of modelling PowerFlexHouse1, i.e., one of
the smart houses at DTU Risø. Both grey-box and black-box modelling methods are
implemented and compared. On top of that, several additional elements are added to
existing models in order to test whether the accuracy of the model can be significantly
improved.
Aforementioned modelling techniques are applied to develop the model of electric
water heater which are installed in PowerFlexHouse2 and Nordhavn area, with great
possibility to be controlled in the future. Experiments to discover dynamics inside
water tank are designed and carried out in the lab. A single layer model is firstly
derived in this thesis as a benchmark for subsequent development. Based on the need
for further exploiting the flexibility, a multiple layer model is also developed.
The single layer model developed in the third chapter is used to investigate how
electric water heater can provide flexibility to the grid. The corresponding potential
is demonstrated by two case studies namely energy cost reduction and peak load
reduction.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Renewable energy is now playing an increasingly important role in economy growth
and anti climate change movement. However, the fluctuating nature of renewable
energy resources like PV and wind power can cause instability in the power system.
Thus, multiple types of flexibility are needed in the energy sectors [1].
1.1
Motivation
Today, buildings account for over one-third of total final energy consumption in the
world [2]. A considerable portion of the energy consumed by buildings is used for
heating, especially for countries like the Nordic countries where winter is typically
long and cold.
Correspondingly, the thermal mass of buildings becomes an ideal
storage-alike resource that can balance the fluctuation of intermittent renewables by
offering aggregation-based large scale flexibility at very low cost [3]. Model-based
understanding of the thermal dynamics of buildings is therefore crucial in exploring
and utilizing the flexibility potential of buildings. The degree of its importance is
the same as other key elements such as modelling occupant behavior and modelling
various heating solutions.
Electric water heater (EWH), as an essential household appliance also has a great
potential to provide flexibility to the energy sector. EWHs are used for hot water
supply, which represents a substantial portion of energy from buildings. Meanwhile,
three good properties of EWH make it an ideal choice for energy storage and partici-
pating in demand side management.
1. Water has large heat capacity and the EWH normally has large volume, which
means EWH can store considerable amount of energy.
2. The heating elements of EWHs are resistors, they don’t consume reactive power.
This makes the control of switching actions relatively easy. Besides, there is no
lockout time after each switching action (as with, e.g., air conditioners), enabling
high-frequency control signals can be applied to EWHs to meet certain power
system regulation needs [4].
3. Due to the development of materials, present EWHs have much better insulation
than before. Thus, pre-heating actions of the EWH will only cause little amount
of heat energy loss.

Project description
This project performs system identification for the PowerFlexHouse1 and the EWH
in PowerFlexHouse2 based on real data collected from live experiments, in order
to answer how much flexibility the EWH and the building can provide to the grid.
Then, control strategies for an aggregator are derived to provide grid services based
on the assumption that the residential building has the same type of EWHs. The two
control strategies are designed for two different objectives, the objective for the first
case study is to optimize the energy cost; the objective for the second case study is
to follow the reference signal from BRP (Balancing responsible party).

In this chapter, a literature review of methods applied to model the thermal dynamics
of buildings is firstly provided, followed by an introduction of PowerFlexHouse1 and
its developed models. Several trials for improving developed models are described
and compared with the developed models.
2.1
Review of building modelling methods
Methods applied to model building’s thermal dynamics can be categorized as follows:
1. Black-box modelling is a completely data-driven method, considering only
system inputs and outputs. It is normally applied in systems when little or no
prior knowledge about the system’s physical property is available or the system
structure is too complicated with many unknown influencing factors. In this
case, data collected from the experiments become the only information available
for modelling. Examples of black-box modelling are given by [5] in which neural
network is applied. Quality of black-box models are normally much dependent
on the quality data collected from experiments and whether the experiments
are well designed to detect the system dynamics. Using insufficient or biased
data for model estimation will cause significant modelling error. Another factor
is that process and measurement noise affects the quality of the experimental
results, which also impacts the accuracy of the estimated model. In this case,
model cross-validated with another set of data from a different experiment may
give a significant error. Thus, the experiments for black-box modelling require
==PAGE:16==
4
2 Modelling Methods Review and Case Study Based on PowerFlexHouse1
a well-designed experiment to reflect all system dynamics.
2. White-box modelling, which requires detailed knowledge of the system, for
instance, the physical structure (formulas of convection, ventilation, solar irra-
diance etc.) and relevant parameters like heat capacity and thermal resistance
of the materials of the building. Good examples of white-box modelling are
presented in [6][7], where the authors use RC circuit network to model building
thermal dynamic, with materials and parameter known in advance. The accu-
racy of white-box models can most of the time be improved when additional
influencing factors are considered. In [8], the influence of moisture is taken into
consideration and the building structure and space is calculated precisely. In
[9] ventilation and convection inside the building is accurately modelled, the
space and arrangement inside the room are considered carefully as well.
3. Grey-box modelling is a combination of the previous two approaches(black-
box modelling and white-box modelling). It normally uses prior physical knowl-
edge to determine a general model structure and then applies the experimental
data to estimate unknown parameters. Examples using differential equations
to model the thermal dynamics are presented by [3][10][11], while RC circuit-
based models are also developed in [7][6] using grey-box approach. Comparing
the white-box model, models developed in [7][6] the values of relevant param-
eters are estimated according to their maximum likelihood that were derived
based on experimental data. Since the data-driven approach can be easily influ-
enced by any noise signals. To increase the applicability of the model, a latent
force thermal model (LFM-TM) is introduced in [12] as an add-on to the origi-
nal grey-box model in order to compensate for the deviation caused by unknown
physical part and noise inside the system rather than increasing the complexity
of the model to fit the measured data. As a result, the grey-box model with
LFM-TM term will have a high likelihood of the data without having a too
complicated structure of the model.

Evolving experience learned over time
Ever since 2008, a number of studies have been conducted to model the thermal dy-
namics of PowerFlexHouse1 in order to accumulate the knowledge and experience. Al-
though the intension of modelling the thermal dynamics of PowerFlexHouse1 remains

unchanged, the experiments and the modelling approach do evolve over time due to
the increase of knowledge and the improvement made to the experimental platform
over the years. Table 1 presents a short summary of three major experiments-based
modelling activities which were conducted in 2008 [15] , 2009 [16] and 2015 for some
improvements [17]. Each experiment is made of a number of sub-experimental activ-
ities. The time span between the two early experiments and the last experiment is
quite long because many relevant studies were performed in other PowerFlexHouse

changed. In all these experiments, the RC circuit-based grey-box modeling approach

resistance and heat capacity of different components in and parts of the buildings)
and limited duration for each experiment. Pseudo random binary signals (PRBS) are
applied to excite the system in all experiments. In order to reduce the level of data
complexity, dimension reduction approaches are applied, wherein the measured indoor

(PCA) or simply using the average temperature. Parameter estimation for all ex-
periments is based on maximum likelihood value performed by tools developed for
continuous time stochastic modelling (CTSM) [18], although implemented in different
software platforms. Different models developed in each experiment are statistically
compared to each other by using likelihood ratio tests in order to find a final model
with the best performance. An obvious fact that can be found is the increasing level
of complexity, i.e., to include more relevant state variables and properties into the
model even with nonlinear representations. The representative model structures can

Comparison of model structure and discussion
The model structures of [15][16][17] have many components in common. For example,
the input number is three, and the chosen orders of the system are all third-order,
but some differences still exist.
The model of [15] is more different from the other two models.
There is no

considered instead of the state of heater temperature. No capacitance for the electrical
heater means the interior temperature will respond to the switching actions of the
heater immediately. However, there is always a time delay for the heater to heat
in the real electrical heater, which makes the model in [16][17] are more reasonable.
This could also explain why the deviation in high-frequency part is smaller compared
to the model in [15]. The main difference between [16] and [17] is that air infiltration
and the effect of solar radiation on the envelope is considered in [17], which is more
detailed in the non-dominated structure of the model.

In this chapter, system identification experiments on the electric water heater (EWH)

3.1
EWH modelling methods review
Modelling the dynamics of EWH has been researched for some time to explore the
possibility of implementing demand response (DR) control for frequency regulation.
In [19][20], a single-layer EWH white-box model is presented, which assumes that the
water temperature is uniformly distributed. The author of article [21], proposes a
double-layer white-box model which divides the EWH into an upper layer, a lower
layer and a mixing layer. To increase the accuracy of the white-box modelling, multi-
layer models are developed in [22][4]. Multi-layer model in [22] divides the EWH into
plenty of temperature disks then considers the heat diffusion and water flow effect
between the disks. The multi-layer model introduced in [4] is mainly based on the
partial differential equation, and takes some complex physical effects like convection
into consideration. Black-box modelling is applied in [23], but the model object is a
hot water heat pump.

3.3.2
First PRBS experiment
According the system identification theory in [26], it is better to use a signal like white
noise to excite the system. Since signals such as step response signal, have much more
power at low frequencies, it is difficult to get complete information about the system
at high frequencies. However, white noise input is difficult to realize in real life due to
limitations of the practical system. For instance, in our EWH system, it is not possible
to control the power value of the heating component and only the switch states of the
heating component can be controlled, so giving white noise input is impossible for the
EWH. Thus, PRBS (pseudo random binary sequence) signal becomes a commonly
used signal for system identification. PRBS signal approximates white noise, which
has a flat spectrum, so it can excite all frequencies “equally” in some sense, which
allows us to observe more dynamics of the system in different frequencies.
3.3.2.1
Experiment design and implementation
Two main factors determine the PRBS, which are the basic period of the signal λ
and the maximum length sequence N (N = 2n-1), the total duration of the PRBS
signal is T = Nλ. In the step response experiment, it is noticed that there is a time
delay for the water temperature respond to switch signal, which is around 3.5 minutes.
Hence, 3.5 minutes is chosen to be the basic period λ. The step response result also
shows that the whole heating period last for around 2 hours, so the “on” time of PRBS
should be less than 2 hours, which makes the length of PRBS sequence is chosen to be
N = 26 −1. The designed PRBS signal is generated by Matlab idinput-function, the
PRBS signal and corresponding autocorrelation can be found in the Figure 3.9. The
autocorrelation plot shows that correlation for all time lag (the time lag represents
the distance between the current point and starting point) is within the confidence
interval, so the signal is not periodic and has the property of white noise. Since the
switch of EWH is not possible to be controlled by the computer, the relay connected
to the EWH is controlled instead shown in Figure 3.10. The PRBS signal is copied
to the Python script, and then sent to the relay so that the EWH can be controlled.

A single-layer model has been used in [19][20] which assumes the water temperature
is uniformly distributed throughout the tank. The simple single-layer model equation
is based on the energy balance in Equation 3.1.
C ˙Ti(t) = Q(t) + G(Ta −Ti(t)) + HWD(t)(Tin −Ti(t))
C = ρcpV, H = ρcp, G = UA
(3.1)
where Ti(t) is the water temperature inside the tank (◦C), Tin is the inlet water
temperature(◦C), Ta is ambient exterior air temperature(◦C), Q(t) is the rate of
energy input (W), WD(t) is the water usage (m3/s), ρ is the density of water (kg/m3),
cp is the specific heat capacity of water (J/◦Ckg), V is the volume of water tank (m3),
A is the total surface area of the tank (m2). U is the heat transfer coefficient of the
tank (W/m2 ◦C).
Water usage effect is temporarily not considered in parameter
estimation. The energy balance equation can be rewritten as below.
C ˙Ti(t) = Q(t) + G(Ta −Ti(t))
C = ρcpV , G = UA
(3.2)

CHAPTER 4
Aggregator
In this chapter, the first-order single-layer model obtained in last chapter is used to
explore how aggregated EWHs can provide flexibility and ancillary service to electrical
grid.
The EWH aggregation control in this chapter is based on the scenario of a resi-
dential building. It is assumed that the residential building previously use medium-
temperature district heating to heat the water for the domestic hot water usage and
space heating. However, the medium-temperature district heating is going to be re-
placed by the low-temperature district heating (LDTH) in order to increase the energy
efficiency. Research results show that the LDTH is feasible for space heating in the
well-insulated buildings but not sufficient for preparing hot water. This means water
for domestic use needs to be heated by other facilities inside the residential building
so that the water temperature can meet the requirements for domestic hot water use.
The added facilities can be gas boilers or electrical boilers, we assume a building with
30 apartments which is applied to aggregation-based case studies.
10 apartments
inside the building choose to use the EWH. The potential risk for integrating these
new EWHs into this building is that it may cause the peak load to exceed the power
capacity of the building. Changing the fuse to one with larger power capacity is a
temporary solution and would introduce additional cost to the building owners. Thus,
in this case, a smart way to control the EWHs’ power setting is developed using the
mixed integer programming technique.
There are two case studies for the heating schedule management in this chapter.
The target of the first one is to minimize the energy cost while the target of the second
one is to track a reference signal from BRP (Balancing responsible party). For both
cases in this chapter, it is assumed that the aggragator predicts the families’ energy
consumption requirements and make the energy schedule. However, it is needed to
note that the first case study is at the stage of offline scheduling, which means that
the EWHs will be switched on according to the offline schedule during operation. For
the second case study, the optimization is based on the idea of “receding horizon”
in [27]. Thus, though the data for the second case study is offline, it is able to be
changed to online in the real application.

4.2

Second heating schedule optimization is based on a given reference signal from BRP,
which can be used for balancing the wind farm generation fluctuation. It is assumed
that the EWH owners signed contracts with BRP to follow the reference signal so that
EWH can provide flexibility to the wind farm power plants. The working scenario
is that the BRP firstly receives the power production information from the wind
farm, then the BRP gives a reference signal based on the wind power production to
the EWH aggregator. The EWH aggregator arranges the heating schedule based on
the given signal. The derived schedule by EWH aggregator firstly satisfies the hard
constraint of the customers’ requirements for hot water usage and then follows the
power reference signal given by BRP with maximum efforts. It is known that the
wind power production varies a lot due to the fluctuation of the wind speed, which
makes it is impossible to get an accurate predicted value of wind power production
one day ahead. Therefore, the “receding horizon” based model predictive control is
used to solve this problem. It is supposed that the estimated average wind power
production is reliable on the time scale of one and a half hours, which means that
the BRP can give a suitable power reference signal in the time scale of one and a half
hours. Then the optimizer of the EWH aggregator derives a heating schedule during
the coming period for all the EWHs in the residential building. The heating schedule

EWH aggregator only takes the first step movement of the optimal solution. Then
the optimization window recedes and repeats the action until the end. All the first
step decisions construct the control sequence of the EWHs. The constraints of this
case are the same as that of the first case. The optimization decisions cannot violate
the constraints of temperature and EWH model.

20◦C. Therefore, the discrete EWH model of 15-minute time scale is simplified as
Equation 4.5. The heating power is a binary variable, which can only be 0kW or
2.8kW as is mentioned in Section 4.2.
Tt+1 = 0.9976 ∗Tt + 0.0023 ∗Pt + 0.0488 + Tde,t
(4.5)
With the information above, the EWH aggregator is able to make a specific heating
schedule for the EWHs. In order to increase the calculation speed, the scheduling
period is divided into two parts. The first part is from 00:01 a.m. in the midnight to
12.00 p.m. while the second part is from the 12:01 p.m. to 24:00 p.m. The second
part takes the ending EWH temperature of the first part as the initial temperature.

based optimization (the EWHs simply need to satisfy the hard constraints of the
temperature). It can be noticed that the EWHs brings forward the heating action
in some periods with the price based optimization. The reason is electricity price is

4.4
Chapter conclusion
From the case studies in this chapter, we can draw the conclusion that by aggregating
the EWHs in the building and implementing some control algorithms, the aggregator
can truly provide ancillary services to the electric grid without violating the limit of
the grid capacity of the building. The first case study shows better results compared
to the second one. There are four problems for the second case study. The first
problem is that the number of EWHs under the aggregator is only 10, the flexibility
is limited. One thing needs to be mentioned is that the reference signal should be
within a reasonable range. Since the first designed proportion coefficient for reference
signal is too small, which results in that the aggregator can not track the signal
almost all the time, the results can be found in Appendix C. The second one is that
the EWHs power can only be 2.8kW or 0kW owing to the design of EWH. It is not
possible for the aggregator to track the small variation of the reference signal. The
third problem is that the water temperature drop profile for the 10 EWHs may be too
similar (the initial temperature are the same, and are all derived from the data of July
28th), which also restricts the flexibility that the aggregator can provide. The fourth
problem is that our assumption is that the wind power production is predictable one
and a half hours ahead. If the time of prediction can be increased, which makes the
scale of the optimization window larger, the result could also be improved. Due to the
limit of time, the performance of the second case study cannot be improved further.
But the results at present of the second case study shows the possibility that the
results can be improved when more EWHs are under the control of the aggregator.
In conclusion, the two case studies of this chapter are primary trials for the ag-
gregation control of EWHs. The scale of the aggregator of the case study is still very
small. However, the results of the case studies show that the aggregator of EWHs
really has the potential to provide a larger scale of ancillary services to the power
grid, as long as a massive number of EWHs can be controlled by the aggregator.

5.1
Conclusion
This thesis applied grey-box modelling techniques to model the thermal dynamics of
a residential building and an EWH based on experiments conducted at DTU Risø
Campus. The obtained EWH model is used for investigating the potential for aggre-
gation control.
PowerFlexHouse1 models
After comparing the existing models of PowerFlexHouse1 from the year 2008, several
ideas are tried to see whether the existing models could be further improved. Data
reduction method SVD is applied, and the results show some improvements. Mean-
while, model structures considering wind effect and solar angle effect are also tried.
The fitting results do not show any improvement. However, it is found that the error
of existing models is highly correlated with solar radiation. If more accurate informa-
tion of solar radiation could be accessed like the precise solar angle of the sun, it is
highly possible that the model could be improved.
EWH models
By carrying out a series of experiments and analyzing the obtained data, a model
of the EWH is identified. The final chosen model TiThTe shows a satisfactory result
in predicting the water temperature of EWH. The prediction error of the model is
within 1◦C. The double-layer model T1T2ThTe also performs well in predicting tem-
perature. The reason that T1T2ThTe model is not chosen is because the model TiThTe
and T1T2ThTe have similar results but TiThTe is simpler. Further, the water temper-
ature of lower part of the tank is not known, we cannot validate the accuracy of the
double-layer model. If more sensors can be installed in the tank, a more accurate
EWH model could be developed.
Aggregator
Two case studies of the aggregator are accomplished. One is for the electricity energy
cost optimization and one is the power reference tracking optimization. The first case
study of EWH aggregation control shows that our control algorithm can really let the
EWHs optimize the heating schedule and achieve a better operation economy. The
EWHs will switch on during the price valley so that energy cost can be decreased.
==PAGE:86==
74
5 Conclusion and Future Work
The results of the second case study don’t show very satisfactory optimization results.
The reasons can be conclude as following
1. The number of EWH is only 10, so the capacity for tracking the reference signal
is very limited.
2. The power of EWHs is fixed, which makes EWHs cannot track the small varia-
tion of the reference signal.
3. The water temperature drop profile of 10 EWHs may be too similar.
4. The forecast-based optimization horizon is only one and a half hours.
Further, the EWH model used for the case study is the simplest first-order model,
the delay effect may cause some deviation between simulation and reality.
These
problems will be reconsidered in the future so that the performance of the aggregator
can be improved.
5.2
Future work
PowerFlexHouse1 models
Solar angle information needs to be collected so that the model could be improved.
Two or three more experiments need to be done to have as validation data set. The
heat dynamics of each room will be researched so that more accurate control can be
implemented.
EWH models
At this moment, the performance of model TiThTe is already good enough, but it is
maybe because our EWH is only 92L, the stratification effect is not so obvious. Next
step plan is to install sensors at different heights of the tank so that the stratification
effect on the EWH can be studied. Meanwhile, the accuracy of the double-layer model
can also be validated if more sensors are installed in the lower part of the tank. With
more sensors inside the tank, the effect of district heating on the tank is also able to
be researched. Further, the water consumption can be included in the model if the
water consumption profile is properly measured (there are communication problems
for the water flow mete at present).
Aggregator
The EWH model used in the aggregation control is still the simplest first-order model,
so it will be changed to a more accurate higher order model in the future. However,
the main task for the aggregation control of EWH is to increase the scale of the
aggregation. From the case study results, we have already found that the aggregation
control can cooperate with the power grid and provide ancillary services, but the
capacity is limited. Thus, the next step is to research the control algorithm for a
massive number of EWHs. A new rule for BRP deciding the reference will also be
designed. The rule will consider the mixing integer property of the EWH (the switch
of EWH has only two binary states). When large amount of EWHs can be controlled,
the potential of EWH aggregation control can be fully utilized.
