Abstract
This thesis investigates numerical schemes to compensate for dispersive eﬀects, and to correct
for defocus as well as aberrations in optical coherence tomography. We initiate the thesis by
a theoretical introduction of optical coherence tomography, and the mathematical framework
is laid out with the inclusion of dispersive eﬀects, defocus, and aberrations.
Transversal
scanning is introduced, as well as the eﬀect of digitising the three spatial coordinates, and
the data processing from acquisition of the spectrum to a three dimensional tomogram is
discussed.
Dispersion compensation is performed globally by extracting the non-linear phase through a
Hilbert transform and the resulting phase is conjugated and added back to the spectrum. In-
tensity correlation optical coherence tomography is introduced as a means of depth-dependent
dispersion compensation, and the procedure is applied to simulated and experimental data,
both verifying the depth-dependent dispersion compensation properties of intensity correla-
tion optical coherence tomography. The performance in terms of sensitivity and processing
speed is assessed and compared to conventional optical coherence tomography.
Defocus correction is performed also by adding a conjugated non-linear phase, but in the
transverse spectral domain. The phase added is obtained by: (1) unwrapping the phase in
2-D and ﬁtting a parabola, (2) iteratively applying a parabolic phase and selecting the phase
that maximises an image sharpness metric, and (3) measuring local slopes in the transverse
spectral domain, mimicking a Shack-Hartmann wavefront sensor. The three methods are
employed to refocus a specular USAF resolution test target and a microscope-like en-face
image of a cucumber slice, and the phase from the en-face image is used to refocus the
entire volume, extending the depth of focus to 577 microns. Deeper layers are not refocused
properly due to multiple scattering. The three methods were further employed to identify
any spherical aberrations from the set-up, and slight visual improvement is recorded. The
need for correction of sample induced-aberrations in dermatology is questioned. Finally, the
stability requirements for numerical correction of defocus and aberrations are discussed on
the basis of a literature study, and the pros and cons of numerical corrections versus harware
based corrections are discussed.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Optical coherence tomography (OCT) [1] is an interferometric imaging technique, which
non-invasively provides a depth proﬁle of the reﬂection coeﬃcient of the imaged sample.
The reﬂection coeﬃcient includes both specualar reﬂections as well as backscattering, and it
reveals structural information about the sample. Due to the non-invasive image acquisition,
OCT initially found employment in retinal imaging [1–4], which is diﬃcult due to the retina’s
position behind the vitreous body, and it remains the most widely used application of OCT
even today, 25 years after the ﬁrst demonstration [5]. Subsequently, OCT has been applied
in more or less every medical ﬁeld ranging from embryonic imaging [6,7], through endoscopic
imaging [8] to dermatological imaging [9] and even neurological imaging [10] to name a few.
The success of OCT is cemented by the publication of a 25 year anniversary special edition
of Biomedical Optics Express in July 2017 [11].
OCT was ﬁrst demonstrated using a movable mirror in the reference arm of the interferometer
to transversally scan the sample, a method now termed time domain (TD) OCT [1], but
subsequently, a method without moving parts, termed Fourier domain (FD) OCT, in which
spectral information is used to reconstruct the depth proﬁle, was demonstrated, and shown to
be superior to TD OCT in terms of both imaging speed and sensitivity [12]. For these reasons,
FD OCT is almost exclusively used today, utilising either a spectrometer to obtain the
spectral information, termed spectral domain (SD) OCT, or an optical source sweeping the
optical frequency, and thereby encoding the spectral information in time, termed swept source
(SS) OCT. In both FD OCT methods, the signal from all depths are recorded simultaneously,
and the spectral width of the source is inversely proportional to the axial resolution of the
resulting image [12], and these two facts pose two problems in OCT: Firstly, the broadband
sources used in FD OCT are increasingly sensitive to dispersive eﬀects, which aﬀects the
group velocity of diﬀerent optical wavelengths causing diﬀerent parts of the spectrum to
disagree on the actual position of a scatterer in the sample, thereby degrading the axial
resolution. Secondly, the simultaneous acquisition of information from all depths results in
the depths close to the focal point of the ﬁnal objective being in focus, whereas depths above
and below the focal region will be defocused, degrading the transverse resolution.
Dispersive eﬀects can be suppressed hardware-wise by ensuring that the accumulated phases
in both interferometer arms are close to identical, which is typically done by introducing a
dispersive element, e.g. BK7 glass, in one of the arms [13]. Defocus can also be mitigated
hardware-wise by using multiple probe beams with diﬀerent focus positions [14], or by moving
the focus of a single beam [15]. Both methods require the stitching together of diﬀerent
images, which demand some numerical capacity, hence the later introduction of defocus

purely numerical methods for both dispersion compensation, ﬁrst in TD OCT [16–18] and
subsequently in FD OCT [19–21], and defocus correction [22–25] have been demonstrated,
helping to keep the optical systems simple and robust enough to survive in medical clinics.
The numerical approaches for dispersion compensation and defocus correction for OCT all
revolve around the fact that dispersion, defocus, and aberrations introduce a non-linear phase
in the optical spectrum or in the transverse spectral domain for dispersion, and defocus and
aberration, respectively. Because OCT is an interferometric technique, the phase information
is readily available to be evaluated and linearised.
In this thesis, we investigate the compensation of depth dependent dispersion arising from
the sample, which has so far only been demonstrated either by utilising fourth order correla-
tions in TD OCT-like systems with axial scanning [26–28], or for FD OCT by assuming the
non-linear phase piecewise constant along depth [29, 30]. One exception was demonstrated
experimentally in [31], where a numerical mimicry of fourth order correlations is implemented
in an SD OCT set-up employing two spectrometers. We implement a purely numerical re-
alisation of fourth order correlations by multiplying spectra and address the shortcomings,
also purely numerically. For defocus correction, we implement three methods for estimation
of the non-linear phase, and compare their defocus correction abilities, as well as apply them
to correct system induced aberrations.
1.1
Outline
The thesis is initiated with a chapter on the mathematical formulation of OCT theory start-
ing from the electric ﬁelds returning from the interferometer arms through the acquisition
and ending with the data processing required to obtain a three dimensional image includ-
ing the eﬀects of digitising the spatial coordinates. The sample dispersion is included from
the beginning, whereas system dispersion, defocus, and aberrations are included phenomeno-
logically. The next chapter is concerned with dispersion compensation, and ﬁrst a global
compensation of depth independent system dispersion is introduced before depth dependent
dispersion compensation is investigated. The ﬁnal chapter introduces the three methods for
non-linear phase estimation and apply the methods for defocus and aberration correction.
The chapter is concluded with a discussion on the stability requirements and the limits of
numerical defocus and aberration correction compared to a hardware-based correction.

Chapter 2
Optical coherence tomography
Optical coherence tomography (OCT) utilises white light interferometry in a Michaelson
interferometer to map out the intensity of the light returning from a certain depth, z, of the
test object placed in one of the interferometer arms, termed the sample arm. The light is
returning due to either backscattering, reﬂection, or both depending on the nature of the test
object. For biological samples backscattered light is the dominant part of the returning light,
but for simplicity, we join the backscattering and reﬂection depth proﬁles, and name the joint
term the reﬂection coeﬃcient, r(z). By scanning the illuminating beam transversally across
the sample surface, the reﬂection coeﬃcient can be mapped as a function of all three spatial
coordinates to form a 3-D tomogram of the structural information of the sample.
In this chapter, the theory of SD OCT is laid out, and the approximations usually made
are accounted for and their implications on the ﬁnal image are discussed, in order to gain a
thorough understanding of the process ﬂow from electric ﬁelds to a digital 3-D image.

Transversal scanning
A general formalism for the relation between the reﬂection coeﬃcient and the OCT depth
scan has now been developed, and extending the model to include 2-D transversal scanning
seems a trivial task: Obtain spectra at diﬀerent transversal positions, assemble the resulting
adjacent OCT depth scans to form a 3-D tomogram, and you’re home safe. In practice,

hitherto been assumed to be a plane wave illuminating a sample that only changes in depth.

increasing the data stream beyond the capabilities of the computer used to process the data.

angiography where it can help improve the signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) [36].

the imaging speed with a factor equal to the number of segments, and even if this reduction in
imaging speed is acceptable in a speciﬁc situation, the phase extraction by using the analytic
signal requires a strong, narrow peak in each segment of the A-scan, which is not generally
the case. In many biological samples, only the top surface is suitable for phase extraction,
and so, this leads us to the topic of depth-dependent dispersion compensation.

3.2
Depth-dependent dispersion compensation
Now that depth-independent dispersion compensation is introduced and demonstrated, the
next natural step is depth-dependent dispersion compensation. The non-linear phase shift
from the sample induced dispersion is a function of depth, and therefore, to achieve the
optimal result in terms of image quality, each depth should be compensated by a slightly dif-
ferent phase term. As quickly discussed above, this can be done by applying global dispersion
compensation to an axially segmented volume, and then stitching the individually optimised
segments together to form a piecewise dispersion-compensated volume. This increases the
processing time, and requires knowledge or assumptions about the sample to infer the correct
phase factor for a given depth.
Complete depth-dependent dispersion compensation has not yet been demonstrated, but
depth-dependent compensation of GVD (and all even orders of ¯β) has been demonstrated in
phase-conjugate OCT [27], chirped pulse interferometry OCT [28], and quantum OCT [26].
The two ﬁrst use classical light sources, but require extensive set-ups, and they both operate
in the time domain, making them slow, bulky and costly. Quantum OCT utilises entan-
gled photon pairs to obtain intrinsic GVD cancellation, but it requires entangled photons
and a time domain set-up as well, which in practice limits the applications. Recently, spec-
tral domain OCT with intrinsic GVD cancellation has been demonstrated experimentally by
Ryczkowski et al. [31] by detecting the light with two interferometers, and multiplying the
interferograms together. We term that technique intensity correlation (IC) OCT, and we
present here a purely numerical implementation of IC OCT using both simulated and exper-
imental data from a classical SD OCT set-up, and approach partly conducted by Shirai [38].

intuitively pleasing when performing the Fourier transform ﬁrst: For each Fourier transformed
sub-spectrum, the artefacts have a slightly diﬀerent phase, while the real features have a
phase that is constant with ω0 and thus across all the sub-spectra. Adding these Fourier
transformed sub-spectra together cause the artefacts, that are all out of phase, to add up
destructively, while the real features are in phase, and they will thus add up constructively.

Numerical refocusing
As any optical imaging modality, OCT is aﬀected by the divergence of the Gaussian beam
away from the focal point. A diverging spot causes features of the image outside the focal
region to be blurred similarly to conventional photography. Additionally, imperfect system
components introduce aberrations in the light beam, and the sample itself also distorts the
wavefront. Traditionally, this is mitigated hardware-wise by the use of hardware-based adap-
tive optics (HAO), which works by inserting a deformable mirror into the beam path [44].
The deformation of the mirror is then adjusted to exactly cancel the wavefront distortion,
and the aberration-free beam is retrieved with improved transversal resolution as a result.
However, in order to deform the mirror correctly the wavefront distortion must be identiﬁed.
This is typically done with a Shack-Hartmann wavefront sensor which measures the wave-
front slope, which is then used to construct the wavefront distortion. This precludes the use
of lenses due to the weak reﬂections of light entering and exiting the lenses. The reﬂections
mix with the light returning from the sample, and the phase estimation is obscured. As a
consequence, spherical mirrors must be used, and, in order to reduce the spherical aberra-
tions emerging from a spherical mirror, the focal length of the spherical mirrors are chosen
as several metres [44]. This obviously increases the system complexity and limits the ease of
use and clinical applicability.
As discussed in section 2.2, defocus, system aberrations, and sample aberrations, all introduce
a non-linear phase in the transverse spectral domain, analogous to the non-linear phase
introduced by dispersion in the domain of optical frequencies. In many imaging modalities,
such as two-photon microscopy and photo-acoustic imaging etc., the absolute value of the
returning signal (optical or acoustic wave, respectively) is measured, and the non-linear phase
that aﬀects the light before acquisition is therefore ”frozen” into the image so to speak. OCT,
however, is an interferometric technique, which allows us to access the phase information of
the acquired tomogram, and this opens the door to numerical corrections of the wavefront
distortions, termed digital adaptive optics (DAO). This can easily be veriﬁed by having a
look at the interferogram, ˆI(ω):

Phase estimation in 2-D
As mentioned above, both defocus and aberrations manifest themselves as a non-linear phase
in the spatial frequency domain, which is two-dimensional (x, y) for a 3-D tomogram. With
dispersion compensation, the phase is extracted via a Hilbert transform and the wrapped
phase values, φ ∈] −π; π], of the resulting complex numbers are unwrapped by adding
±2nπ, n ∈N to each individual wrapped phase value such that |φi+i −φi| ≤π, where
φi is the unwrapped phase value of the i’th complex number. This smoothes the disconti-

point. The continuous phase output is used to ﬁt a polynomial to identify the non-linear
phase terms present in the spectrum, and the non-linear phase is then conjugated and added
back to the spectrum to compensate for dispersion. In 2-D the unwrapped phase in kx, ky

both directions. This makes the unwrapping an overdetermined system with more equations
than variables, which in general has no exact solution, and we therefore need other methods
to extract the phase.

4.1.1
Phase extraction by least squares ﬁt
While an overdetermined system has no exact solution, it has an optimal solution, which is
here deﬁned in a least squares sense. The least squares solution to the phase unwrapping
problem can be shown to be identical to the solution to Poisson’s equation with the angle
diﬀerence, α = φi+1 −φi as the driving term [47]. This allows the least squares solution to
be computed rather eﬃciently, but in order to weigh each angle value with the intensity at
that pixel to reduce the inﬂuence of noisy pixels, the equation must be solved iteratively,
increasing the computation time signiﬁcantly [48].
The phase obtained by solving Poisson’s equation can then be ﬁtted to Taylor monomials
up to the desired order. The monomial ﬁtting is done by a non-linear least squares ﬁt in
MATLAB, and the resulting phase is conjugated and added back to the Fourier plane of the
en-face image. The monomial ﬁtting is now in 2-D, which also increases the computation
time compared to 1-D ﬁtting. This increase in processing time making the least squares phase
estimation unﬁt for real time corrections, but for defocus and system aberrations, which are
sample-independent, the processing time is acceptable because the non-linear phases needed
for correction must only be computed once, and then applied to each tomogram.
This method is mathematically equivalent to global dispersion compensation as presented in
section 3.1. The phase is unwrapped, and ﬁtted by a sum of diﬀerent order monomials.
4.1.2
Iterative estimation
In order to avoid extracting the actual phase and ﬁtting a number of 2-D monomials, iterative
procedures have been proposed as well, see e.g. [24], and they estimate the non-linear phase by
optimising the image sharpness by adjusting the monomial coeﬃcients, aJj. This optimisation
of course requires a sharpness metric to quantify the image quality, and in this work, the
variance of the absolute pixel values of the image is used because of the simple calculation
required and the robustness against noise [49]. Iterative methods for optimisation tend to
be slow due to the excessive number of calculations required when the coeﬃcients are varied
in order to ﬁnd the solution that maximises the sharpness metric. The number of values
each parameter assumes in the search can be drastically reduced by ﬁrstly assuming that
all coeﬃcients can be varied independently, i.e. that the optimal value for coeﬃcient 1 is
not inﬂuenced by the values of the other coeﬃcients. This assumption is not valid when
the function basis is not orthogonal, and as a result, only a single monomial is applied at a
time. No ﬁtting is required for this method, so it is relatively easy to change from Taylor
monomials to Zernike polynomials, but to have a better comparison between the methods,
the Taylor monomials are used. Secondly, employing an eﬀective search algorithm for the
coeﬃcient value that maximises the sharpness metric can also reduce the computation time.
With δ being the desired resolution of the variation of the coeﬃcients, the required steps for
a simple linear search for the optimum value scales inversely with the resolution, O(δ−1),
but for a binary or logarithmic search, the required steps scale with the logarithm of δ−1,
O(log(δ−1)). Again, the phase for correction both defocus and sample aberrations can be
calculated on beforehand, so for these two applications the processing time is subordinate.
This method is similar to the dispersion compensation process presented in [19], where the
dispersion terms, ¯βn≥2 are found iteratively.

4.1.3
Sub-aperture correlations
The ﬁnal method for estimation of the non-linear phase included here is a numerical imple-
mentation of a Shack-Hartmann wavefront sensor as presented in [23]. Such a sensor utilises
an array of lenslets which focus diﬀerent parts of the incoming beam onto a 2-D detector
array. The local slope of the wavefront at each lenslet causes a transverse shift of the focal
point. From the shifts of each lenslet image, the local wavefront slope is calculated, and the
slopes are used to estimate the coeﬃcients of the Taylor monomials or Zernike polynomials.
In OCT this can be mimicked numerically, due to the access to the phase information of
the tomogram. As discussed above, the inverse Fourier transform of an en-face propagates
the image backwards, and is proportional to the electric ﬁeld returning from a speciﬁc depth
of the sample at the Fourier plane. The Fourier plane is before the lens that focuses the
light onto the detector, and this is exactly the point where we want to install a wavefront
sensor. It is implemented numerically by splitting the pupil (Fourier) plane into K × K
sub-apertures, as illustrated in ﬁgure 4.1 with simulated data. The blurred en-face image
is created by performing 512 × 512 A-scan simulations as used in chapter 3. The reﬂection
proﬁle is a delta function in depth with amplitude equal to the normalised pixel value of the
sample en-face image, which, in this case, is a single Gaussian dot. A non-linear phase, in
this case a parabolic phase to resemble an out-of-focus image, is added to the sample electric
ﬁeld before the reference and sample ﬁelds are added and the absolute value is taken. The
resulting en-face OCT image is a cut through the 3-D tomogram at the axial position of the
delta function.
When correcting the wavefront, the en-face image is inverse Fourier transformed to numer-
ically propagate the ﬁeld backwards to the pupil plane. The pupil is then split into K × K
sub-apertures, which mimics the lenset array in a physical wavefront sensor.
Each sub-
aperture is Fourier transformed to propagate it forward to the detector, and because each
part of the pupil plane contains spatial frequency components that make up the full image,
the Fourier transform of each of the sub-apertures yields a shifted, low resolution version of
the full image. The shift in each sub-aperture is evaluated by cross-correlating the intensity
of sub-aperture i, j with the central sub-aperture. The shift is thus a measure of the slope
of sub-aperture i, j relative to the slope of the central sub-aperture. This means that linear
phase terms can not be corrected with this technique, but that is no loss since only non-
linear terms cause blur. In ﬁgure 4.1, the central sub-aperture is shown in blue alongside the
other sub-apertures in red to highlight the shift. The slopes in each sub-aperture are used to
construct an estimate for the non-linear phase of the entire pupil by a least squares solution,
as explained in the next section. The non-linear phase is conjugated and added back to the
entire pupil, which is then inversely Fourier transformed to reveal the corrected image. The
K × K sub-apertures need not ﬁll the entire pupil plane, as illustrated in ﬁgure 4.1. This
allows us to discard the data close to the edges where there is little signal, thus increasing the
sub-aperture method’s robustness towards noise, because only the part of the Fourier plane
that actually contains information is used to estimate the non-linear phase.
This method is equivalent to the initial method for dispersion compensation presented in [20],
where a short-time Fourier transform (STFT) is used to evaluate the phase slope in each
segment across the channelled spectrum. Due to the problem being one dimensional, the
STFT approach is discarded in favour of the direct extraction, also demonstrated in section
3.1.

Defocus correction
Defocus is a system-induced aberration, and it presents itself as a radially parabolic wavefront
distortion that scales linearly with the distance from the focal point [50]. Fortunately, we
have access to individual depths from the 3-D tomogram, so in a single en-face plane at depth
z = z0 image, the PSF for defocus, Pdefoc, will be
Pdefoc = F{eia(k2

(4.13)
where a is the coeﬃcient of proportionality, and zF is the depth at which the focal point lies,
and φdefoc(kx, ky, z0 −zF ) = a(k2
x +k2
y)(z0 −zF ) is the parabolic phase present due to defocus.
a is determined solely by the optical system, and so, by careful calculations, it is possible to
determine a theoretically, in which case defocus can be corrected easily [25] by adding the
conjugated phase to the tomogram, which then exhibits all depths in focus. However, for a
single system conﬁguration, a only needs to be determined once, so by using any of the three
phase estimation methods, a can be identiﬁed, and Pdefoc can be determined completely if zF
is known. The z-axis is the OPD between the two interferometer arms, so while a is constant,
zF is not, but must be identiﬁed by the user. In post-processing, this can be assessed visually
and estimated, and in real-time imaging, one can imagine a a slider bar that the user can
use to adjust the estimate of zF such that the image quality is optimised. Bear in mind that
zF only changes when the reference path length is changed, and during imaging, zF is thus
likely to be constant.
In the context of the angular spectrum method, numerical defocus correction operates by
numerically propagating the en-face image, which is proportional to the electric ﬁeld, back-
wards to the Fourier plane behind the ﬁnal lens of the imaging system. In the Fourier plane,
a parabolic phase is added, which corresponds to changing the wavefront of the electric ﬁeld,
which in turn changes the focal point of the lens. When the ﬁeld is numerically propagated
forwards by a 2-D Fourier transform, the focal point is on the detector, given that the proper
parabolic phase was added, and the en-face image is then refocused by being propagated to
the focal point. Applying a parabolic phase changing linearly with the distance from focus
to the diﬀerent en-face planes will refocus all en-face images simultaneously.
The three methods for phase estimation are applied ﬁrst to an image of a United States Air
Force (USAF) resolution target deliberately placed 1 mm below focus, and secondly to a
full 3-D tomogram of a cucumber, from which a single en-face image is selected, and a is
estimated from that and used to correct the entire tomogram.

4.3
Aberration correction
Aberrations are ever present in any optical imaging system, and they are induced by both
the system itself and the sample. In OCT a low NA objective is typically used to increase the
DOF, and as a result, aberrations are expected to have a limited eﬀect due to the large beam
spot size. This section is dedicated to investigating the presence and eﬀect of aberrations in
an OCT system.
4.3.1
System aberrations
Intuitively, the system-induced aberrations should be assessed in the focal point placed at the
top of a sample where both defocus and sample-induced aberrations can be neglected, but
due to the low NA, this approach will only reveal the presence of very large aberrations. As
shown by Adie et al. [52], the presence of aberrations will introduce artefacts either far above
or far below focus when numerically correcting for defocus. For the tomograms presented here
the depths far below focus are dominated by multiple scattering, and it is thus not possible
to assess the aberrations below focus properly, so only en-face images far above focus are
investigated thoroughly. This also minimises the eﬀect of sample-induced aberrations.

4.3.2
Sample aberrations
Sample-induced aberrations are in general space-variant in x, y, and therefore they aﬀect the
en-face image through a space-variant convolution (equation (2.24)), which naturally requires
a space-variant deconvolution if complete correction is the target. In OCT, that is typically
performed by splitting the image into several segments and assuming the aberrations are
constant inside these segments [53].
This is completely analogous to segmentation-based
dispersion compensation as discussed in chapter 3, and it consequently has the same short-
comings: Each segment must have a clear and well deﬁned peak for either of the three
methods to work. This is the case in retinal imaging where the cones and rods can act as
”guide stars” for correction of the space-variant ocular aberrations [22]. As we saw with
dispersion compensation, the step from piecewise depth-independent dispersion compensa-
tion to genuine depth-dependent dispersion compensation required the exploitation of fourth
order correlations which, despite the implementation, increases the complexity of the OCT
system either hardware-wise or software-wise. Likewise, the for aberrations, the step from
piecewise space invariant aberration correction to true space-variant aberration correction is
quite large, and although techniques for space-variant blur correction in conventional photog-
raphy are showing promising results [54], none of them have, to our knowledge, been adopted
for OCT, making this an obvious research subject.
For dermatological imaging, as system 1 is built for, guide-stars for piecewise space invari-
ant correction are not generally present, but the void of research papers on the eﬀect on
dermatological OCT imaging pose the question whether or not correction of sample-induced
aberrations is in fact relevant in dermatological OCT imaging. sample-induced aberration
correction is no doubt still an important subject, but the relevance in dermatological OCT
imaging means that little eﬀort has been put into the subject of sample-induced space-variant
aberration correction in this thesis.

All the corrections applied in this chapter are based on phase corrections through the angular

coordinates change with time, so δφ(t) →δφ(x, y), which is exactly a phase error across the

conﬁgurations where the entire x, y span is covered in one go, termed full ﬁeld OCT. All x, y
points are measured simultaneously, and the phase jitter is thus not a problem [23].
Transverse motion cause phase instabilities due to two eﬀects: Firstly the seemingly axial
motion of features that are not orthogonal to the optical axis caused by transverse motion,
and secondly, the fact that the A-scans are not acquired on a regular grid if the sample moves.
The seemingly axial motion introduces phase noise identical to what is observed for real axial
motion, and the irregularity introduces phase jitter as well.
The obvious way to minimise the phase instabilities, apart from stabilising the set-up, is
to increase the imaging speed, such that the beam covers a large area in a short time.
The employed hardware imposes a maximum scanning speed, that may or may not provide
suﬃcient phase stability. What is suﬃcient is determined by the feature size, scanning speed
and distance from focus [55,56]. Alternatively, a glass plate can be placed in contact with the
sample surface, and the phase used for defocus and aberration correction is then the relative
phase between the glass plate and the en-face plane in question. All sample motion will also
aﬀect the glass plate, and the relative phase is thus free from motion artefacts except those
stemming from the irregular scanning pattern arising from transverse motion. In situations
where physical contact with the sample is undesired, several algorithms for in-vivo numerical
phase stabilisation have been proposed [51,56,57].
In this work, only stationary samples imaged with a stationary set-up have been assessed, and
this naturally eliminates the motion artefacts, but the images are still susceptible to motion
jitter from e.g. the galvano scanners. The results presented in sections 4.2 and 4.3 show that
we are able to restore features close to the diﬀraction limit, and we can thus conclude that
the intrinsic stability of the system is suﬃcient, and when moving to in-vivo imaging, only
the sample motion should be considered as a source of phase instabilities.
4.5
Hardware adaptive optics or digital adaptive optics?
As we have seen so far in this chapter, DAO works excellently for defocus correction, but
for aberration correction, the improvements were less impressive, albeit positive. We shall
conclude this chapter with a discussion on the pros and cons of both hardware and digital
adaptive optics.
For both defocus and aberration correction, DAO has a major advantage: The layers at
diﬀerent depths can be treated individually as we have seen in this chapter, enabling DAO
to perform axially space-variant corrections, as required for defocus correction. That is not
possible with HAO because a single beam probes all depths at once. Introducing a parabolic
wavefront correction in HAO can shift the focus, but a full volume with all depths in focus
simultaneously will require several images with diﬀerent focus positions that are stitched
together digitally [14, 15]. With DAO, the parabolic wavefront error of the light returning
from a single depth can be estimated, and all en-face images can individually be numerically
propagated to the focal plane by the angular spectrum method, to reveal a 3-D tomogram

==PAGE:68==

with all depths simultaneously in focus. Stitching together individual 3-D tomograms will
generally yield a better quality image due to the improved SNR close to the focus despite
the depth, whereas the digital correction can not aﬀect the noise level. In other words: If
little or no light reaches the lower layers of the sample, the image will be noisy both before
and after DAO. HAO, on the other hand, can increase the intensity of light that reaches
a certain depth by changing the focal point and correcting for sample-induced aberrations,
undoubtedly increasing the SNR.
Regarding aberration corrections, both HAO and DAO can correct for system-induced aber-
rations, or sample-induced aberrations that are constant across the en-face image. In HAO
this is performed by introducing a wavefront correction that is constant as scanning occurs,
and in DAO it is performed by adding the non-linear phase to the angular spectrum of the
en-face image. By changing the wavefront correction in HAO as the beam is scanned across
the sample, it is possible to correct for transversally space-variant sample-induced aberra-
tions, which, so far, is not easily achievable with DAO, as discussed in section 4.3.2. In HAO
the beam spot size is minimised by correcting for aberrations, and the resulting spot in the
detector is thus also small. For DAO a complete en-face image is treated, i.e. the sum of
the intensity at all the transverse positions, which means that the aberrations present in
the Fourier plane of an en-face image are a sum of the sample-induced aberrations from all
transverse points. Furthermore, the wavefront correction also happens in the Fourier plane
of the en-face image, and the correction will aﬀect all transverse points evenly. It is thus
clear that if the beam is aﬀected by the same aberration at all transverse points, DAO can
correct the image, but if the aberrations vary across the sample, the average aberration that
can be detected by DAO will not correct all points correctly.
The optimal solution, regarding the image quality, that accommodates the shortcomings of
both HAO and DAO is to implement both and let DAO handle the defocus correction, which
is axially space-variant, and HAO the aberration correction, which is in general transversally
space-variant. As discussed previously, HAO requires bulky, expensive set-ups, but the need
for aberration correction depends on the system and sample as well as the resolution require-
ment for a given application, so depending on the applications of the OCT system, HAO
can be omitted. Alternatively, HAO can be implemented without the wavefront sensor, and
function only by adding a parabolic phase, changing the focus point, and thus aid DAO by
increasing the SNR at layers far from the initial focus.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
In this thesis numerical methods for dispersion compensation, defocus and aberration correc-
tion in OCT have been implemented and investigated. Initially, the mathematical framework
for OCT was laid out, showing that the system dispersion and system-induced aberrations
aﬀect the tomogram as a non-linear phase factor in the spectral domain, axially and transver-
sally, respectively, and can be removed by elimination of this non-linear phase factor. Defocus
manifests itself in the transverse spectral domain as a parabolic phase factor that changes
linearly with the distance from the focus point. Dispersion of the sample and sample-induced
aberrations enter the equations as space-variant convolutions, which can not be compensated
or corrected by adding a non-linear phase to the spectral domain because each part of the
physical domain requires a speciﬁc non-linear phase in the spectral domain.
System dispersion compensation was treated ﬁrst, and we saw that for a silicon wafer the non-
linear phase can be identiﬁed by a Hilbert transform of the channelled spectrum, and applied
by adding the non-linear part of the conjugated phase back to the spectrum. Both the top
and bottom surface of the wafer was compensated by this method, but not at once. Depth-
dependent dispersion compensation was introduced through IC OCT in which a channelled
spectrum is multiplied with itself mirrored, thereby cancelling all even orders of dispersion
while introducing artefacts and halving the image range. The imaging range is restored by
working with the analytic signal, and the artefacts are reduced signiﬁcantly by a novel, generic
artefact reduction algorithm working by splitting the entire spectrum into M sub-spectra and
summing these sub-spectra. The depth-dependent dispersion compensating properties of IC
OCT are veriﬁed by numerical simulations and by application of IC OCT to experimental
images of a silicon wafer. The IC OCT image showed both top and bottom surfaces of 4
microns.
Peak background noise was observed at each peak, and the cause was deemed
the cross term between the signal and noise in the multiplication, and by adjusting the
single parameter, M, of the artefact reduction algorithm, the peak background noise was
also reduced. The performance of IC OCT was assessed, and the processing time relative
to conventional OCT was found to scale linearly with M for M < 300, with a factor of
proportionality of 0.887, showing that the processing time is on the order of hundreds for
reasonable artefact and peak background noise reduction. The SNR of IC OCT was assessed
and found to increase with M and in general decrease with OPD quicker than conventional
OCT, but for M > 100 the SNR was higher for IC OCT than conventional OCT for OPDs
less than 1300 microns. The resolution improvement of a factor of

Chapter 5. Conclusion
Numerical refocusing was implemented through the angular spectrum method, where each
en-face plane was propagated numerically to the focal plane by adding a parabolic phase to
the transverse spectrum. The parabolic phase was identiﬁed by three methods: A direct least
squares ﬁt of the unwrapped phase values, an iterative optimisation, and a method based on
sub-aperture correlations, which mimic a physical Shack-Hartmann wavefront sensor. The
methods were applied to an image of a USAF resolution test target placed 1 mm below focus,
and when artefacts in the transverse spectrum due to the specular sample were removed, all
three methods were able to restore the image to a similar quality as an image taken in focus,
with the iterative method performing slightly better. A cucumber slice was subsequently used
as a scattering sample, and an en-face image 328 microns above focus was refocused by all
three methods, which were all able to restore a small feature to within 6 % of the diﬀraction
limit. A larger feature was restored from a FWHM width of 54 microns to between 10.8 and
14.8 microns, with the direct ﬁtting being the best and the iterative method the worst. The
parabolic phase obtained from the ﬁtting method was then extrapolated to all en-face planes
using a linear depth dependence, and applied to an entire volume, which afterwards exhibited
close to diﬀraction-limited feature sizes for 577 microns. However, the refocusing failed for
layers far below the original focal region due to multiple scattering. The same extrapolated
phase was then applied to another tomogram of a piece of bell pepper acquired with the same
set-up, and the refocused tomogram showed similar traits as the refocused cucumber slice.
Aberration corrections were assessed from a refocused en-face plane far (390 microns OPD)
above focus where even weak aberrations are known to aﬀect the resolution. The aberrations
were not visible in the focal plane due to the now NA used in OCT. A spherical aberration
was used in the ﬁtting and iterative method whereas the sub-aperture correlation method
used all orders up to and including four.
The results were much less dramatic than for
refocusing, but reductions in feature size of 25% were observed, and the spherical aberration
obtained with the iterative method was applied to the en-face plane 86 microns OPD below
the ﬁrst en-face plane, and general visual improvements were observed through the reduction
of shadows in the image. Sample-induced aberrations were quickly discussed and the need
for correcting them outside the ﬁeld of ophthalmology was questioned.
Through a literature study, the stability of the set-up and sample was found to be imperative
for the three methods described here to work due to them applying and working directly on
the phase of the images. Finally, the diﬀerences between numerical techniques and hardware-
based techniques for refocusing and aberration correction were discussed, and numerical
techniques were found to have an advantage due to the access to individual en-face planes,
which can be corrected separately, and a disadvantage when it comes to aberrations that
vary across an en-face plane. Hardware-based methods were found to complement this very
well, and the ideal OCT system would employ both.

Appendix A
OCT systems: Simulations and
experiments
In this appendix, the two experimental OCT systems and the OCT simulation used in the
thesis are described.
A.1
Simulation
The OCT simulation used in this thesis is performed in MATLAB, by creating a linear
frequency axis with 2048 points ranging from 1070 nm to 1470 nm.
A Gaussian source
spectrum is generated symmetric in frequency, giving a centre wavelength of 1238 nm with a
FWHM of 230 nm, providing a theoretical resolution of 3 microns. The source ﬁeld is sent to
a beam splitter, and the reference ﬁeld is reﬂected oﬀa perfect mirror with rR = −1, while
the sample ﬁeld is reﬂected oﬀuser-deﬁned interfaces, and propagating through user-deﬁned
materials. The ﬁelds are added, and the absolute square value is recorded as the channelled
spectrum. The reference intensity is subtracted from the spectrum to remove the DC term.
A.2
Experimental system 1
System 1 is a supercontinuum (SuperK Extreme OCT9, NKTPhotonics, Denmark) based,
all-ﬁbre OCT system. The supercontinuum spectrum is ﬁltered by a longpass ﬁlter which
allows light form 1000 nm to 1700 to pass. The beamsplitter is a 50/50 ﬁbre coupler (Goosch
& Housego, Netherlands) customised for 1300 nm. The sample arm contains a hand held
probe, which for the imaging performed for this thesis was mounted on a table to ensure
stability.
The light is detected by a spectrometer (Wasatch Photonics, USA) with 2048
pixels spaced linearly in wavelength between 1070 nm and 1470 nm, and it acquires spectra
at a maximum rate of 76 kHz.
The non-Gaussian spectral shape together with the window applied (cosine taper) yields an
achievable resolution of 4 microns. The objective employed (LSM02, Thorlabs, Germany)
provides, for an input beam diameter, an NA of 0.1, which translates to a theoretical lateral
resolution of 5 microns. The focused beam spot size depends both on wavelength and scanning
angle, so an achievable resolution of 5.5-6 microns depending on scanning angle is expected.
Operated by: Niels M. Israelsen
