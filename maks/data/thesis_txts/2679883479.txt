Abstract
Fish eye-lenses are known to be a chronological recorder of micro chemical constituents,
making them potentially useful in interpretations of environmental, ecological, and life-
history changes, up until now studies of eye-lenses have been less extensive than those of
otoliths but have the potential to reveal new information since these are protein based
tissues unlike otoliths which are calcium based tissues. Here the trace elements incorpo-
rated in eye-lenses and otolith from Baltic Cod were analysed with laser ablation induc-
tively coupled plasma mass spectrometry (LA-ICP-MS), making a comparative study
that aims to expand existing knowledge on eye-lens trace elements, as well as establish
potential inﬂuences to these concentrations. Additionally this study looks into what the
rings found in eye-lenses could represent and be derived from, together with a general
analysis of periodic behavior observed in the eye-lens data of various elements. Linear and
mixed linear models over various elements showed little overlap between concentrations
in eye-lenses and otoliths suggesting a diﬀerent governance of uptake processes, further
it showed that eye-lens composition have potential in stock discrimination while being
sensitive to sex and maturity stage. In this study only sporadic correlation between lens
K, Zn, P, Cu, Mg and Sr periodic behavior and physical lens-rings was found. The best
correlation was found between eye-lens rings and otolith Sr periodic behavior, where each
peak in the chemical proﬁle corresponded almost exactly to the width of one lens-rings,
found constantly between all included individuals. The rings observed in cod eye-lenses
appears, based on this study, not to represent seasonal ﬂuctuation or other age related
measures directly, but may rather be an artifact of structural changes to the lens as
the ﬁsh grows, whereas the overall trace elements in the eye-lenses seems to reﬂect the
surrounding environment more directly than that of otoliths.

Introduction
Age-structured
ﬁsh
population
models,
gained favour already in the 70s after the
ground work was laid down by Beverton
& Holt and later Ricker [Beverton and
Holt, 1957, Ricker, 1975], and still provides
the basic principles of cohorts for today’s
more advanced statistical models. Within
ICES, an aged based stock assessment is
considered key to having a fully assessed
and sustainable ﬁshery, with modern age-
based models yielding the most stable ad-
vice and best predictions of future stock
dynamics. The basis for an aged based
assessment is still the ability to age in-
dividual ﬁsh, and otoliths has long been
the default choice for creating a good age
key [Maceina et al., 2007, Morison et al.,
1998, Beamish, 1979]. The characteristics
that make otoliths an ideal tool for age de-
termination is that otoliths are inert and
incrementally formed throughout the ﬁshes
life and these increments will, in temper-
ate areas, form visible seasonal rings that
can be directly interpreted as years, further
more the otolith is not subject to resorp-
tion [Campana and Thorrold, 2001, Cam-
pana, 1999]. Although the most widely used
method for ﬁsh aging, otoliths are not the
only tissues that exhibit some of these
traits, the endoskeleton, ﬁn-spines, ﬁn-rays,
scales and eye-lenses do too [Tzadik et al.,
2017, Pourang et al., 2018], of these the
scales have been most widely used in age
determination, probably due to the ease
and non-lethality of sampling. Otoliths, to-
gether with genetics, has also been exten-

sively studied to map-out migration pat-
terns, population and sub-population struc-
tures, for otoliths these features have been
explored through analysis of its chemical
composition, that is indirectly aﬀected by
the diet and environment throughout the
ﬁshes life [Campana et al., 1996, Anon,

ual ﬁsh will constitute a diﬀerence in life
history, making it possible to detect migra-

et al., 2010, Campana et al., 1996, Cam-
pana and Thorrold, 2001, Campana, 2005],
and in recent years even age directly [Heim-
brand et al., 2020]. Other aspects explored
with the various chronological tissues have
included diets and ecosystem reconstruc-
tion [Tzadik et al., 2017].
Otoliths from Gadus morhua/ Cod are
some of the most well studied chronological
tissues of any species, and have for many
years directly contributed to the manage-
ment advice for the various population and
sub populations where aging is done by
counting the seasonal rings, that form as a
result of changes in diet and growth rate,
which are visible in the cross sectioned
otolith going out from the exposed core.
Analysis of the chemical composition of
Cod otoliths has also helped in the separa-
tion and mapping of sub-populations struc-
tures found in the Baltic [Hüssy et al.,
2015], mitigating the risk of local deple-
tion due to the multiple sub-populations
with overlapping habitat [Jonzén et al.,
2002, Sterner, 2007, Heath et al., 2014]. An
expansion of how otolith chemistry could
be used came about as a result of unclear
growth zones in the Eastern Baltic Cod
stock that made the classical age determi-
nation unreliable, which lead to an age esti-
mation method, based on seasonal patterns
in the chemical analysis of these otoliths
[Heimbrand et al., 2020]. Although a lot
has already been achieved using otoliths,
the chemical composition of the diﬀerent
tissues that possesses chronological proper-
ties will diﬀer within the same individual,
since a given element has to travel through
diﬀerent physiological barriers to get to the
respective tissue[Gillanders, 2001, Hüssy
et al., 2020b]. Opening the the possibil-
ity to apply the knowledge and experiences
gained from otoliths, to ﬁnd out more about
the ﬁshes life history and supplementing in-
formation from otoliths with similar analy-
sis of other chronological tissues.
Drawing direct conclusions from the chemi-
cal composition of any chronological tissue,
to the surrounding environment are diﬃ-
cult and should be approached with caution
since, the signal that is seen is the result
of many complex physiological processes
of uptake and tissue transport [Campana
and Thorrold, 2001, Hüssy et al., 2020b].
However, the chemical composition of eye-
lenses in particular, has been shown to be
a possible indicator for heavy metals in
the environment [Kingsford and Gillanders,
2000]. Eye lenses are in general an interest-
ing candidate to ﬁnd out more about ﬁshes
life history, since eye-lenses are not cal-
cium based structures like the other chrono-
logical tissues [Pourang et al., 2018], but
consists mainly of a wide range of pro-
teins bound in primarily α and γ crystalline
that is thought to make up the structural
part of the eye-lens [Wistow and Slingsby,
2016, Dove, 1999]. Studies that look at
ﬁsh eye-lenses in a stock management per-
spective are few, [Quaeck, 2017, Quaeck-
Davies et al., 2018, Tzadik et al., 2017,
Kingsford and Gillanders, 2000, Gillanders,
2001, Dove, 1999, Dove and Kingsford,
1998, Pourang et al., 2018], and no pub-
lications which has analysed eye-lenses in
the same manner and detail as otoliths was
found, with the basic ring structure visible
within eye-lenses still poorly understood
and broadly unexplained. In this study,

Materials and Methods
The data used in this project was collected
and analysed as part of the bigger scientiﬁc
research project TABACOD at DTU-Aqua
[Hüssy et al., 2020a] that works toward
solving the ageing and stock assessment
challenges of the interconnected Baltic Cod
sub-populations. The trace element proﬁles
for otoliths and eye-lenses, was consigned to
this project as ready to use data ﬁles with
all information on the ﬁsh readily available.
The trace element proﬁle for the otolith
data had already been used for research
purposes and had therefore already under-
gone data screening and outlier removal,
consequently only preselected element pro-
ﬁles were available for the analysis concern-
ing otoliths. The element proﬁles of the eye-
lenses was given as "Raw" PPM data, that
contained a broader selection of elements
than for the otoliths, where data screening,
outlier removal and data partitioning was
performed here, detailed under Data prepa-
ration, section 2.3.

Sampling Overview

were collected as part of the TABACOD
project [Hüssy et al., 2020a], had been
tagged with tetracycline leaving a chem-

captured is visible in the otolith chemi-

how much the the otolith have grown in
relation to how mush the ﬁsh has grown
and aids aging validation, it is also used
as a time stamp in the chemical proﬁle so
that each data point within the tagging pe-
riod can be annotated, backtracking time
as "days from tagging" giving a realistic
timescale to seasonal or other trends ob-
served in the chemical proﬁle. The Cods
were captured in the years 2016-2018 and

2.2
Sample Preparation
The sampled otoliths and eye-lenses were
ﬁrstly cleaned by soaking them in an ul-
trasonic bath of deionized water for 10
minutes, rinsed with deionized water and
left to dry on acid-washed trays over
night under a laminar ﬂow hood. Secondly
the lenses and otoliths were embedded in
epoxy (Struers®) and sectioned, with an
Accutom-100 multi-cut sectioning machine,
through the core of the otolith and lens
respectively exposing the complete growth
axis on both sides of the cores. Thirdly
the surface of each section was polished
with 3 µm abrasive paper mounted on
rotating disks (Buehler®) that was then
cleaned again as described above, obtaining
a smooth clean surface to chemically anal-
yse. Lens sections were, after the chemical
analysis, digitized using a Leica DCF290
camera at a magniﬁcation of 380 µm pixel-1
with a standard setup (8 bit/channel, 2048
x 1536 pixel frame).
2.2.1
Chemical analysis
The most robust method for identifying the
chemical proﬁle of any tissue, is to dissolve
it whole and analyse it with e.g. inductively
coupled plasma mass spectrometry (ICP-
MS) giving lots of material to analyse, en-
suring a solid analysis with good oppor-
tunity to detect elements only present in
small amounts. This is also a ﬁrst step when
using LA-ICP-MS, that indicates which
of the many present elements should be
focused on and which are present in so
low concentrations that they will fall be-
low detection limits. LA-ICP-MS, was per-
formed on the transverse cross-section of
otoliths and cross section of the eye-lenses
by "De Nationale Geologiske Undersøgelser
for Danmark og Grønland" (GEUS), tak-
ing points measurements along the growth
axis making a transect that goes from edge
to edge trough the core. An example of
laser paths in eye-lenses seen in ﬁgure 2 by
the yellow-ish line, creating a left and right
side chemical proﬁle mirrored around the
core, that in theory should be identical, and
serve as a general quality check. The LA-

ICP-MS, works by continuously extracting
a thin layer of material, that is then sucked
into a mass spectrometer, where each ele-
ment is "counted" giving a counts pr. sec-
ond along the transect. To get the total
concentration in e.g. ppm, the most abun-
dant element is used as an internal stan-
dard, from which the total concentration of
the remaining elements can be calculated.
For otoliths this internal standard is Ca,
since it is a calcium based structure, for eye-
lenses it was determined that P served as
the best internal standard, while the con-
centration of P itself was calculated us-
ing Mg, as the internal standard, these two
showed the most coherence with the ICP-
MS of fully dissolved eye-lenses used as a
precursor. For the otolith chemical proﬁle
nine elements where chosen as purposeful
for the Baltic Cod, and an additional eight
elements were viewed likely to be of inter-
est in the eye-lenses, included in these eight
was Hg but it was not possible at GEUS to
quantify it and was left out, the elements
used in the analysis are summed up in ta-
ble 2 under Data Overview section 2.4.
2.2.2
Lens Rings Identiﬁcation
Like otoliths, eye-lenses also contain uni-
form rings that go out symmetrically from
the core, however unlike otoliths there is no
consensus as to what these represent or why
they form. For each individual in this study
the images of the cross sectioned eye-lenses
were used to map out these rings along
the laser transect, noting in particular the
distance between rings and the overall dis-
tance of the transect, measured from core
to the left and right edge also giving the
total diameter of the lens. Not all individ-
uals possessed clear concise rings that were
easily identiﬁable, in these cases complete
rings were identiﬁed by using an elliptical
tool, excluding the "half" rings that col-
lapsed into and became part of other rings,
an individual where the rings were consid-
ered easy to identify and one considered as
diﬃcult is illustrated in ﬁgure 2, where each
dot represents a complete ring along the
laser transect, the mapped out rings for all
individuals can be viewed in appendix A.

2.3.2
Partitioning and Scaling
The data was created by LA-ICP-MS, mak-
ing a transect from edge to edge of the eye-
lens, resulting in a data frame which con-
tained values "mirrored" around the core.
To look into details relating to the life his-
tory of the ﬁsh, and to make comparative
analysis with the otolith data, data for each
individual was split into a left and a right
part. Since the data is only mirrored around
the true center of the lens, it was very im-
portant to make sure that data was divided
at exactly this point, just splitting the data
into two equal parts does not necessarily
achieve this, since the lens could be de-
formed or cracked, and the laser transect
was observed to not be exactly straight.
When looking at the chemical proﬁles for
the full dataset, it was observed that some
elements, Mg and K in particular, had a no-
ticeable top and valley respectively at the
same place very close to the middle as illus-
trated in ﬁgure 5. It is well established that
the chemical composition is unique at the
core, due to the ﬁsh being in larval stage
at formation [Dove, 1999, Campana, 1999],
therefore this coherent point for Mg and
K was used as the true center to split the
data, which correlated very well with half
the data when the laser transect could be
considered ideal. After the data was split, it
was for the left part ﬂipped so that all the
data "starts" at the core and goes toward
the edge, Additionally the length of each
laser transect was measured, and scaled to
ﬁt the data, so that the ﬁrst point is taken
at length almost zero and the last point is
taken at the measured radius, which makes
it easy to relate structure seen in the data
directly to physical places in the eye-lens or
visa-versa.

2.5
Data Analysis
2.5.1
Comparison between radii
When viewing the images of the eye lenses,
it was noticed that not all the laser tran-
sects were straight lines and that one indi-
vidual had its transect interrupted on the
right side of the nucleus, e.g. ﬁgure 8. This
could mean that the data is skewed to some
extend, and that some rigs may be over
or underrepresented for the given individ-
ual. To clarify the extent that this could
inﬂuence any analysis, the data from the
left and right part of the eye lenses was
tested against each other since, these in the-
ory should resemble each other closely and
no overall diﬀerences should be detectable.
Two main tests was carried out; ﬁrstly each
element across individuals was tested, using
a multivariate test for diﬀerences between
left and right, as well as a uni-variate test
where individuals was considered a random
factor, thereby considering the ﬁsh to ﬁsh
variation. Secondly each individual across
the elements were tested, to see if any one
ﬁsh had diﬀerences between left and right.

2.5.4
Periodic Behavior and Lens-
Rings
One of the great advantages of the LA-ICP-
MS method of analysis, is that the chrono-
logical properties of the tissue are used to
the fullest, with each data-point theoreti-
cally representing an actual point in time
of the ﬁshes life. This type of data was used
by Heimbrand et al. (2020) to look for sea-
sonal patterns in elements of Cod otoliths,
matching the physical year-rings visible to
element trends. To see if similar seasonal or
periodic variation could be found for Cod
eye-lens elements, the R-function "loess"
[Cleveland et al., 1992] was used to make lo-
cal second degree polynomial regressions to
each element for all individual. For the ele-
ments K, Zn, P, Cu, Mg and Sr a reason-
able regression could be made without too
wide conﬁdence intervals, the R-function
"IDPmisc$peaks" was hereafter used to de-
ﬁne the peaks and valleys present, where
a minimum peak height detection was set
for each element individually dependent on
the scale of the concentration. To corre-
late any periodic variation found in the el-
ement concentration over time to the ob-
served lens rings, the distance between each
lens-ring was used to annotate data back to
the corresponding ring. By overlapping the
13
==PAGE:17==
regression lines with the annotated rings, it
was then possible to look for coherence in
peaks/valleys and the visible rings. To look
for possible coherence between uptake of a
given element to the otolith and the for-
mation of the eye-lens rings, the same pro-
cedure of local polynomial regression was
done for the otolith elements K, P, Mg,
Ba, Mn and Sr with the otolith data pro-
portionally annotated to the lens-rings. For
this annotation it is assumed that both
otoliths and eye lenses grow continuously
throughout the ﬁshes life both in propor-
tion to the ﬁsh growth, making the otolith
and lenses proportional at all increments.
In Heimbrand et al. (2020), it was shown
how the cyclic chemical signal of P and Mg
in Cod otoliths corresponded to the yearly
growth rings seen in otoliths, whereby each
valley in the chemical proﬁle was deter-
mined to represent one year of growth. To
see more directly if any of these trends
could be found in the lens data, the otolith
P and Mg individual valley distances to
the core was scaled in proportion to the
lens data. Proﬁle plots of the lens elements
exhibiting periodic variation, was coloured
according to the valleys of otolith P and
Mg respectively, looking directly for coher-
ence that could correspond either directly
to the yearly growth rings of otoliths, a
seasonal or perhaps biannual growth trend.
Lastly, all the periodic variations analysis
for both lens-rings and otolith-rings was re-
peated for each of the lens element concen-
tration ratios Sr/P and Sr/Mg since these
have the potential to capture more complex
environmental changes that the ﬁsh goes
trough, like in the winter where the Cod
seeks deeper waters, lowering the uptake of
P while at the same time experiencing a
change in salinity that in theory should be
reﬂected in the Sr levels since salinity and
Sr are correlated [Zimmerman, 2005, Maar
et al., 2011].

Discussion
The continued advances in using otolith
micro chemistry, can in large parts be at-
tributed to the advancements in methodol-
ogy. Classically the whole otolith was dis-
solved whereby elements/ isotopes could be
quantiﬁed [Campana, 1999], this method
was improved with more sensitive instru-
mentation making detection limits smaller
with the introduction of inductively cou-
pled mass spectrometry (ICP-MS) [Gillan-
ders, 2001]. Although LA-ICP-MS "pre-
serve" the chronological properties of the
otolith/lens in the data, a drawback is that
less material is analysed at a time com-
pared to dissolving the whole otolith, which
means that some elements although present
comes close to or below detection limits.
At least 50 elements have been detected in
otoliths using these various methods [Stur-
rock et al., 2012, Hüssy et al., 2020b], and
so every new study using otolith micro
chemistry must have consideration of which
elements are of interest. Likewise a lot of el-
ements have been detected in the eye lenses,
but not before analysed using LA-ICP-MS
making the unanalysed data the ﬁrst real
results, where it is apparent at which con-
centrations the various elements can be ex-
pected and which elements might be of too
low concentration or too noisy to be use-
ful in future exploits using the LA-ICP-
MS method. When looking at data repre-
senting the entirety of the ﬁshes life, the
elements K, Zn, P, Cu and Mg were the
best candidates for meaningful interpreta-
tion having lower standard deviation then
mean value. When only considering data
representing the beginning of the ﬁshes life
Li and Ti was additionally considered of
interest, whereas looking at the edge data
only Mg, P, K and Zn was found to be sta-
tistically useful. However, when looking for
periodic variation Sr could in addition to
K, Zn, P, Cu and Mg be shown to ﬁt a
local second degree polynomial regression.
It is a matter of course, that other anal-
ysis methods such as dissolving the whole
lens would change which elements are use-
ful candidates in an analysis [Gillanders,
2001], for example it is well established that
eye-lenses of ﬁsh contain a lot of S [Wistow
and Slingsby, 2016] which was not analysed
here due to technical restrictions. Likewise
Hg was near detection limit but due to lack
of reference material could not be quanti-
ﬁed, an element which could have potential
uses since the Baltic is known to contain a
lot of heavy metals.
From the statistical models over the lens
using all the data, where it was looked into
whether any of the factors stockID, sex and
size as well as their interactions had any ex-
planatory power, it was found that stockID
or the interaction with stockID was sig-
niﬁcant for all analysis except Cu. It was
found that West individuals had a signiﬁ-
cantly higher concentration of Zn than in-
dividuals from East, while the interaction of
stockID and sex for K, P and Mg, made the
picture less clear with only signiﬁcant dif-
ference between combinations of factors for
Mg between sex for West individuals. These
results indicate that Zn is the best can-
didate when it comes to stock separation,
while the other elements looked at might
give the wrong impression if sex is not
factored in since it was indicated but not
fully established, probably due to lack of
data, that males for K and Mg have higher
concentration in East than West while fe-
males have lower concentrations in East
than West while the opposite was true for
P, which essentially means that males and
females might give opposing signals when it
comes to stock discrimination in regards to
the given elements. For both the core and
the edge data none of the factors explored
were found signiﬁcant, with only the model

being aﬀected the same way by maturity,
that logically shifts the intrinsic levels of K
since it is an essential mineral for nerves
and muscles.

that very few points constituted a peak/
valley in the line, and no discernible pat-
tern could be found on its own, however
the similarities observed between Sr and
Mg, were somewhat conﬁrmed when plot-
ting the proﬁle plot over the ratio Sr/Mg
since it resulted in an almost ﬂat line with
single point outliers, which indicates that
the two elements ﬂuctuates at the same
time, this semi correlation seems to be only
on an individual basis since it did not show
up on the Spearman’s rank correlation test
for overall correlations between elements.
When looking for coherence between the
lens rings and the otolith element concen-
tration, otolith Sr was found to correlate
very well with the lens-rings, with almost
full overlap between peaks and rings for
many of the individuals, and partly for
other individuals. For otoliths the Sr con-
centration is known to have a positive re-
lationship to extrinsic environmental con-
centration [Hüssy et al., 2020b], to which
it could be speculated if the Sr concentra-
tions is a governing or triggering factor in
the formation of a new lens-rings. Consider-
ing that none of the lens elements looked at
correlated as well with the rings as otolith
Sr with a few exceptions where single peaks
coincided for a range of elements, and that
no correlation was found between the val-
leys of otolith P or Mg and any of the lens
elements, suggests that the rings observed
in the eye-lenses of Cod do not correspond
to any seasonal variation and thereby not
to the age of the ﬁsh but rather seems to
reﬂect the surrounding environment more
directly, although this is diﬃcult to con-
ﬁrm with the present data. Some notable
observations that can be made when look-
ing at how "nice" the lens looks and how
easy it is to discern the rings in relation
to the element proﬁle plots of the lens,
is that there appears to be a connection
with, how nicely looking the trends of P
and Mg is and how clear the rings appear,
if P and Mg have a continuous opposing
downwards and upward trend respectively
without too large peaks underway the lens
rings stand out more clearly, leaving spec-
ulations of whether these proﬁle trends is
connected to change in crystalline structure
of the eye-lens from alpha to gamma as the
ﬁsh ages [Dove, 1999]. It is further noticed
that the otolith Sr peaks correlated most
successfully with lens rings in the cases were
the rings was unclear, to which it is spec-
ulated whether a "messy" transition from
one crystalline to another leaves "space" for
extrinsic inﬂuences.
The analysis and results of this report
bears the mark of data that leaves want-
ing, the data only contained fourteen indi-
viduals of which two individuals were from
the Western population, though luckily one
of each gender was represented, this has
made for an unbalanced design that makes
any conclusion surrounding stockID less ro-
bust than desired. The analysis also bears
mark of the fact that it is the ﬁrst time
ﬁsh eye-lenses have been analysed with LA-
30
==PAGE:34==
ICP-MS, which means that a lot of noise
was present in the data and that the stan-
dardisation procedures for some of the el-
ements can be improved upon, in partic-
ular it would be ideal if the standardisa-
tion could be done over another of the most
abundant elements present e.g. K which
showed good stability across individuals,
enabling the relationship between lens P
and Mg to be properly explored. Further
it is noted that all results connected to the
mapping of the lens-rings bears the uncer-
tainty that no prior knowledge on how to
identify the proper rings exists, and that
only these fourteen were available for prac-
tice, thus opening up the debate whether
all rings or too many rings have been found,
what does however seem the case is that the
lens-rings do not follow a consistent pattern
of being incrementally closer to one another
like it is observed for otolith-rings.
5
Conclusion and Future
Aspects
The results of the analysis in this study,
leads to the belief that the rings observed
in cod eye-lenses do not represent seasonal
ﬂuctuation or other age related measures
directly, but seems to reﬂect the surround-
ing environment more directly although
what causes the rings to appear seems more
complex. Leaving speculations in whether it
is simply a derived eﬀect from the α/β crys-
talline formation/exchange, where protein
structure reach a given length before cut-
oﬀto start a new "layer". Statistical anal-
ysis of the overall trends found for the var-
ious element concentrations conﬁrms that
the incorporation of trace elements must
be governed by diﬀerent processes than for
otoliths, it also furthers the belief that eye-
lenses could be more representative of the
metals in the environment and thereby have
potential in stock-separation and ecosystem
reconstruction.
More work is needed with this type of data
to conﬁrm the trends seen and, further an-
swer and ﬁll in the gaps to the questions put
forward, a future advantage could be using
a more balanced and larger sample design,
where equal amount of individuals from dif-
ferent stocks are represented allowing an el-
emental ﬁngerprint to be made over each
stock, which would also allow for multivari-
ate analysis on stock discrimination, which
was not possible here. A greater contrast in
the age/ lengths of individuals are also de-
sirable if further inferences about the lens-
ring should be made with greater certainty,
making temporal trends more clear, with
the additional need to quality assure the
readings of the lens-rings perhaps even sta-
tistically prove or dismiss the supposed con-
nection between clarity of the rings and ele-
ment concentration trends. Finally any new
similar study should use the lessons learned
here with regard to LA-ICP-MS procedure
and external reference standards, hopefully
improving the readings of the low concen-
tration elements reducing random noise.
