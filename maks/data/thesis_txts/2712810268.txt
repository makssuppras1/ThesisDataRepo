Summary (English)
This thesis aims to set up a tool which combines the predictions of two famous
AI systems – DeepTMHMM and AlphaFold. DeepTMHMM predicts the topol-
ogy of membrane proteins from its FASTA sequence and AlphaFold outputs a
prediction of the 3d structure of the protein.
The thesis foundation is to make and deploy a system which, firstly, predicts
if the sequence defines a transmembrane protein using DeepTMHMM. Then,
in the case where the protein is a transmembraneprotein, the structure is pre-
dicted. The system’s output is accompanied by a 3D visualization of the joint
predictions. Setting up the system environment requires establishing a server to
make the tool accessible. BioLib, the company hosting DeepTMHMM and part-
ners in this project, facilitate and help with the infrastructure. The last stages
of the project include analysis, optimization, and validation of the solution.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Firstly, this project presents an overview of the current state of the structural bi-
ology field, how the transmembrane proteins are represented, and why building
an API that combines topology and 3D structure could help. Then, an overview
of the API it is presented explaining functioning, user interface and deployment
explanations. Subsequently, the project goes through both algorithms used in
the API, explaining its main techniques and characteristics. Afterwards, the
API and its different stages during development are explained more in detail.
Finally, a research section explains the approaches followed to improve the struc-
ture prediction using the topology information. Additionally, in appendix A, B,
C, D, and E, it is possible to find background about protein structure and trans-
membrane topology, docker system, and detailed explanations about different
machine learning models used in the prediction algorithms.
1.1
Motivation
Transmembrane proteins(TMP) are critical for cellular functions, being involved
in many important biological processes, such as cell signalling, cell-cell commu-
nication, and the transport of molecules across cell membranes. Additionally,
they are also the target of many drugs. Studying TMPs structure can provide
insight into these functions and their role in cell physiology. [10] [11][12]
==PAGE:22==
2
Introduction
Despite their importance, the three-dimensional structures of membrane pro-
teins have been difficult to determine. X-ray crystallography is the usual method
for determining molecular structures. It generally requires proteins to be in solu-
tion and free from other molecules. However, TMPs are embedded in cell mem-
branes, often with segments outside the membrane. Their hydrophobic surfaces,
flexibility and lack of stability make them difficult to extract and purify[10][12].
Nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy (NMR) has been used to determine
the structures of some membrane proteins. However, this method requires more
amounts of protein than x-ray crystallography, and the results are usually less
precise[13]. In the last years, Cryo-electron microscopy (Cryo-EM) has become
an effective technique for obtaining structural insights from biological macro-
molecules, including membrane proteins [14].
In particular, Cryo-EM allows for the determination of 3D structures of proteins
in their natural environment in a way that is not possible with other techniques.
However, the resolution that can be achieved with Cryo-EM is still limited to
about 5 Å, so reconstructing the detailed protein structure is unlikely to be
possible. Still, the Cryo-EM reconstruction of a membrane protein in its natural
environment provides a functional constraint to the protein’s structure [15].
NMR and Cryo-EM are powerful techniques for experimentally determining the
three-dimensional structure of membrane proteins.
However, they share the
same problems, being expensive and time-consuming. Additionally, obtaining
high-quality data for all proteins of interest is not always possible. As a result,
the structures of many membrane proteins remain unknown.
In order to obtain information about the three-dimensional structure of a protein
that is not accessible by standard techniques, one must resort to computational
methods. One of the most successful types of computational methods is the
one that uses structural databases. The main protein structural database is the
Protein Data Bank (PDB) [16]. The PDB is a database of protein structures
determined through experiments. It provides detailed information about the
structure and function of proteins and other molecules. Scientists from all the
areas often search the PDB to understand the structures of molecules, making it
an essential resource for structural biology. Among the computational methods
that use protein databases, Alphafold can predict the 3D structure of proteins
more accurately than any previous method. Alphafold is a machine learning
method that combines deep learning with a physical model to predict the 3D
structure of proteins. It was developed by Google DeepMind, in collaboration
with the University of Toronto and the University of Cambridge[1].
Currently, Alphafold only predicts the protein’s folding, not its topology. There-
fore, by mixing these two methods, it is possible to obtain further information

about protein physiology and function.
One of the most important uses of protein topology predictions is understanding
the proteins’ folds. By understanding the folds of the proteins, it is possible to
identify the functional interactions with other molecules and determine the spe-
cific roles that the folds play. Protein topology prediction is a complex problem,
as proteins can adopt various shapes and structures. However, several meth-
ods and algorithms can be used to predict protein topology, including sequence
analysis, 3D structure prediction, and comparative modelling [17]. Sequence
analysis is a powerful tool for protein topology prediction. It can provide infor-
mation about the sequence of amino acids in a protein and the order in which
they are arranged. [17].
DeepTMHMM has surpassed various well-known topology prediction algorithms
in an overall-type classification test.
It can predict alpha-helical and beta-
barrel transmembrane protein folds throughout the whole proteome. This al-
gorithm employs deep learning to improve the prediction of its predecessor,
TMHMM, one of the first and most commonly used topology prediction algo-
rithms. DeepTMHMM improves in three different aspects: It can predict the
existence of a signal peptide, the topology of beta-barrels, and the presence of
a signal peptide [18].
The information given separately by the predictions of 3D folding structure and
topology has been proved to help accelerate experimental studies, obtention
of insights regarding the functionalities, and drug targeting in the domain of
transmembrane proteins. Therefore, combining these two predictions with state-
of-the-art methods, such as Alphafold and DeepTMHMM, can offer scientists a
more thorough overview of TMPs.

Chapter 2
API Overview
This tool aims to combine the predictions of two famous AI systems. DeepTMHMM
predicts the topology of membrane proteins from its FASTA sequence, and Al-
phaFold outputs a prediction of the tertiary structure of the protein. The struc-
ture is visualized through the well-known Molstar toolkit.
Firstly, it asserts using DeepTMHMM if the sequence defines a transmembrane
protein. Then, in the case where the protein is transmembrane, the structure
is predicted using Alphafold. The system output would come accompanied by
a 3D visualization of the joint predictions where the different topologies are
masked with different colours of the chain. The masks are imposed above a 3d
visualization of the structure. The tool is first deployed in a docker container,
which later is sent to the system environment established in Biolib servers to
make the tool accessible.
Biolib is the company hosting DeepTMHMM and
partners in this project. The tool is mainly developed in python, except for the
visualization part that runs in Java, HTML and CSS.

pass to compute, where the user can visualize the logging information of the

DeepTMHMM
DeepTMHMM is built based on a deep learning encoder-decoder sequence-
to-sequence model, which takes a protein sequence as input and generates a
per-residue label sequence.
Signal peptide (S), inside cell/cytosol (I), alpha
membrane (M), beta membrane (B), periplasm (P), and outside cell/lumen of
ER/Golgi/lysosomes (O) are the per-residue labels (O). The sequence of residue
labels determines the topology of the protein.[18]
The encoder comprises an ESM-1b pre-trained language model, a bi-directional
LSTM(Long short-term memory) model, and a dense layer with drop-out. The
representations of the encoder are input into a conditional random field (CRF)
decoder.
3.1
DeepTMHMM Architecture
This section will describe the main characteristics of the architecture. Figure
3.1 shows a representation of DeepTMHMM architecture.
As mentioned previously, the algorithm is based on a seq2seq encoder-decoder
architecture.
==PAGE:32==
12
DeepTMHMM
Figure 3.1: DeepTMHMM arquitecture
The goal of sequence to sequence (seq2seq) models is to map a fixed-length input
to a fixed-length output, even if the input and output lengths are different. The
encoder-decoder architecture is commonly used for this task.
An encoder-decoder model comprises two blocks: the encoder and the decoder,
which are associated with a vector called the ’context vector.’In the encoder,
each token in the input sequence is processed. It aims to squeeze all of the
information about the input sequence into a fixed-length vector known as the
context vector. After passing through all the tokens, this vector is provided to
the decoder. The vector is created to capture most of the meaning in the input
sequence. The decoder scans, token by token, the context vector and creates an
estimate of the target sequence.[28]

Encoder
3.1.1.1
ESM-1b
The ESM-1b is a transformer protein language model based on the RoBERTa
architecture and its training procedure.
RoBERTa (Robustly Optimized BERT pre-training Approach) is an Natural
Language Processing(NLP) model modification of the BERT model(Bidirectional
Encoder Representations from Transformers) better trained and optimized.[29]
==PAGE:33==
3.1 DeepTMHMM Architecture

developed to pre-train deep bidirectional interpretations from unlabeled tokens
by conditioning on both the left and right sides simultaneously.[29]
To train the model fully unsupervised with the masked language modelling ob-

3.1.1.2
Bidirectional LSTM
Bidirectional LSTMs train two LSTMs rather than one on the input sequence.
The first on an original copy of the input sequence and the second on a reversed
copy. Thus, all the time steps in the sequence should be known. It is an exten-
sion of conventional LSTMs and can enhance model performance on sequence
classification tasks by obtaining extra context and leading to a quicker and even
more thorough learning process. [8]
This model is explained more in-depth in Appendix C.

Conditional random fields are a class of statistical modelling methods.
In
DeepTMHMM, it fulfils a similar task as the hidden Markov model of its pre-
decessor TMHMM. However, rather than treating each point in the output
sequence as a separate classification task, each CRF prediction contemplates
contextual information or the state of the neighbours.
It is also possible, allowing label sequences to follow a basic transmembrane
grammar, to reproduce TM protein attributes, such as segment length dis-

per-residue labels and restricting the learned "interaction" matrix. Viterbi de-

DeepTMHMM was trained and tested using five different types of proteins:
• Alpha-helical transmembrane proteins without a signal peptide (alpha

• Alpha-helical transmembrane proteins with signal peptide (SP + alpha

• Beta-barrel transmembrane proteins (Beta), globular proteins with signal

MSA detects sequences that are similar but not identical to those found in living

4.2.2
Template Search
After receiving the MSA from Uniref90, it is contrasted with PDB70 applying
HHsearch[36]. The related mmCIF file in the PDB database is used to obtain
structural templates for each hit. Each model is supplied with four templates,
sorted by the expected number of perfectly aligned residues. From the template
information, it is built an initial structure representation referred to as the "pair
" representation. In essence, the pair representation explains how likely are each
amino acids to be in contact with one another [1].
The concept underpinning template modelling is that Proteins mutate and
evolve. However, their structures tend to remain similar despite the changes.
On the other hand, conservation usually takes place on a smaller scale, with
some parts of the protein mainly remaining unchanged as their surroundings
change. Alphafold can identify some of these conserved segments and use them
as a guide to building the structure.[35]
4.3
Evoformer
The multiple sequence alignment and templates are processed with a transformer
in the evoformer module, which determines which elements are more informa-
tive. This step aims to iteratively refine the MSA and pair representation while
exchanging information between them. An improved MSA model will increase
the network’s characterisation of the geometry in the pair representation. At the
same time, the pair representation will also assist in refining the MSA model.
This procedure is broken down into 48 blocks that are iteratively repeated until
a certain number of cycles have been completed. In each cycle, the model uses
the current structural hypothesis to improve the multiple sequence alignment
evaluation, which leads to a new structural hypothesis..[35][1]

decrease what would otherwise be an unattainable computing cost. Specifically,

determine which pairings of amino acids are more correlated, and vertically,
deciding which sequences are more relevant.[35][1]

highlight interacting pairs of residues.[35][1]

4.3.4
Structure Module
The protein is treated as a "residue gas" by the structure module. Every amino
acid is represented by a triangle representing the backbone’s three atoms. These
triangles float in space, and the network rotates and translates them to construct
the structure.[35][1].
AlphaFold generates a series of affine matrices that displace and rotate the
residues in space at each step of the iterative process. All residues are placed at
the origin of coordinates at the start of the structure module. As a result of the
lack of physical or geometrical assumptions in this representation, the network
is prone to structural violations [1].

4.3.5
Invariant Point Attention
It is a modification of attention which acts on a set of frames.
The frames
are represented as Euclidean transforms. This representation allows the frames
under global Euclidean transformations to be invariant.
Implementing the same rigid motion to all residues while keeping the embeddings
fixed updates the local frame similarly. Then, a transformation by the same
shared rigid motion will likewise affect the structure update. Therefore, the
model learns that any possible rotation of translation (rigid motion) of the data
will lead to the same answer, saving much computation [1].
A more thorough explanation of this method can be read in Appendix E.
4.3.6
Model of the Side Chains
The model of the side chains is generated whose positions are parametrized by
a list of torsion angles that the network predicts in their standard form and
implements with typical geometric subroutines [1].
4.3.7
Loss Functions
The fact that there is a loss for each of the algorithm’s fundamental components
makes the loss in Alphafold particularly compelling. A weighted total of all the
losses makes up the overall loss.
Under multiple possible alignments, the main Frame Aligned Point Error (FAPE)
loss compares the anticipated atom positions to the actual positions. Aux losses
consist of averaged FAPE and torsion losses on the intermediate structures.
Distogram loss asserts a direct connection between each entry in the pair repre-
sentation and the corresponding pair of residues. The MSA loss is an averaged
cross-entropy loss for masked MSA prediction, which aims to encourage the net-
work to take into account inter-sequence or evolutionary relations to fulfil the
BERT task. The confidence loss" enables the creation of the pLDDT value with-
out sacrificing the structures’ correctness. Lastly, the violation losses drive the
model to generate a physically reasonable structure with proper bond geometry
and clash avoidance.

They can form many different structures in dynamic equilibrium under physi-
ological settings and still function in an unstructured/disordered state. Recent
research into the genome sequences of numerous organisms has discovered that
such disordered areas make up over 40% of any eukaryotic proteome [39].
The long unstructured chains shown in AlphaFold prediction could be due to var-
ious factors related to the underlying approach. Multiple sequence alignments
are required to determine distances between residues. Intrinsically disordered
regions often evolve more rapidly than structured regions. Orthologous IDRs
can cover enormous sequence lengths, causing the alignments of these areas to
be generally imperfect, involving significant gaps and extensions of gaps. As
a result, distance restrictions inside IDRs and between IDRs and the folded
domains are likely to be poorly defined.
Thus, the difference between IDR
and structured regions in quality of information and constraints derived from
multiple sequence alignments possibly contribute to predicting sequences into
extended IDR forms.
In addition, these forms tend to envelop the folded regions by energy minimiza-
tion steps performed during the prediction, maintaining the system close to the
starting structure [40]. Therefore, this represents a possible cause that explains
why this region crosses the membrane or directs adjacent folded regions towards
the membrane.
6.2.2
Information from Experimental Structures

gions often are disordered and not resolved.
Hence, the prediction of these
missing regions can cause unrealistic predictions in these areas.

Template and output methods are the least invasive of the four specified meth-

DeepMind team improved the prediction of the protein S0A2C3 by doing what
they called “crop-then-fold”. They hypothesised that the prediction of the whole
protein generated failures due to “long-chain collapse”. So, the model failed to
generalise to considerable sequence lengths because it made predictions well out-

was improved to fight the long-chain collapse. However, they stated that it still
may happen in long sequences.

The idea of using "crop-then-fold" is to retain inter-domain context when pre-
dicting without suffering from collapse. The process consists of getting the MSA
of the whole sequence split into shorter crops that can have better detail in a
local context level.
Then, crop the MSA, which would belong to the differ-
ent crops in the sequence. Predict each sequence crop independently with the
cropped MSA and finish imputing these outputs as artificial templates when
predicting the whole sequence.
The same idea was used in this project but with a different approach.
The
crops were obtained regarding different topologies to obtain better details of
the local context. The different sequences of crops are folded respect the same
environments (inside, membrane and outside) while maintaining global context
information with the MSA from the entire sequence prediction.

Transformers are important in the architecture of alphafold. It is modified to
fit for different tasks in many parts of the algorithm.
Transformer are based in encoder-decoder structure. The encoder converts a
sequence of symbol representations (x1, ..., xn) into a sequence of continuous
representations z = (z1, ..., zn). The decoder then outputs an output sequence of
symbols (y1, ..., ym) one element at a time, given z. The model is auto-regressive
at each phase, using previously created symbols as extra input to create the next.
The Transformer, as depicted in the left and right sides of Figure D.1, employs
layered self-attention and point-wise, fully connected layers for both the encoder
and decoder.[9]

The encoder is composed of a stack of N identical layers. Each layer has two

Attention
Self attention allows to look at other positions in the input sequence for clues
that can help lead to a better encoding of the current element. It can be defined
as the mapping of a query and a set of key-value pairs to an output, where the
query, keys, values, and output are all vectors. The result is a weighted sum of
the values, with the weight allocated to each value determined by the query’s
compatibility function with the relevant key. By multiplying the input with the
weight matrix learned throughout the training process, the key, query, and value
are obtained.[9]

queries and the keys of dimension dk. Then, the products are divided each by

Multi-Headed Attention
It expands the model’s ability to focus on different positions. In order to create
the inputs of the multi-headed attention, it is linearly projected the different
queries, keys and values h times to dk, dk and dv dimensions, respectively. Then,
in the multi-headed attention, it is implemented parallel attention functions
for each of these projected versions of queries, keys and values, obtaining dv-
dimensional output values. These are concatenated and once again projected,
resulting in the final values.[9]
