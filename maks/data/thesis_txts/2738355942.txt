Abstract
This thesis presents a comprehensive data­driven model for the analysis and manage­
ment of mechanical component complexity within NNE, with a specific focus on reducing
the number of pump variants. The study aims to streamline the pump system by minimiz­
ing variability and improving operational efficiency.
The Shannon method was used to measure the randomness within the system to quantify
the complexity of the pump system, providing a metric to gauge the complexity. The
research utilizes a variety of machine­learning models to achieve the goal of reducing the
number of pump variants.
Specifically, supervised learning techniques such as logistic regression, decision trees,
and random forest were implemented, along with unsupervised learning through cluster­
ing. These models were developed and executed using Python­based libraries, ensuring
robust and efficient computational processes.
To further improve the performance of these machine learning models, hyperparameter
tuning was performed, optimizing the model parameters to achieve the best predictive ac­
curacy. In addition, ensemble methods were applied to combine the strengths of multiple
models, thereby enhancing overall performance.
The evaluation of model accuracy and validation involved rigorous testing and cross­
validation technique. The results demonstrated that the machine learning models are
useful in reducing the number of pump variants. This finding underscores the importance
of comprehensive data collection in achieving optimal results.
The study discusses the results of the kind of complexity, measurement of complexity, and
also that the participation of machine learning models has significant potential to optimize
pump system management in NNE.

Introduction
In today’s fast­changing economy, increasing demand for diverse products necessitates
innovation and adaptability from businesses. Although product diversity fosters innova­
tion, it also introduces complexity, particularly through the proliferation of component vari­
ants.
Today’s greatest challenge of manufacturing is increasing complexity. It manifests itself
in products and manufacturing processes, as well as in company structures [1].
The pharmaceutical sector, known for its intricate processes and severe quality require­
ments, places a high value on the effective management of complex components. Novo
Nordisk Engineering (NNE), a reputable consulting organization in the Danish pharmaceu­
tical industry, maintains a large database containing a wide range of such components.
However, the increasing complexity of this database presents considerable issues, in­
cluding operational inefficiencies, increased costs, and potential quality compromises.
Addressing these difficulties through efficient complexity management is critical for main­
taining NNE’s competitive advantage and assuring peak performance.
1.1
Motivation
The motivation for this research stems from the critical need to address the challenges
posed by increasing component complexity in the pharmaceutical industry, particularly
within NNE. Efficient complexity management can lead to significant improvements in
operational efficiency, cost reduction, and quality assurance. This complexity poses chal­
lenges across multiple operational domains:
• Process Challenges: The presence of various component variants impacts pro­
duction processes, causing inefficiencies in changeovers and resource allocation.
• Operational Challenges: Managing multiple component variants affects inventory,
quality control, and maintenance, leading to tracking complexities and increased
downtime.
• Supply Chain Challenges: Component variances complicate demand forecasting,
logistics, and supplier relations, potentially resulting in supply­demand mismatches
and strained relationships.
• Cost Implications: The proliferation of component variants leads to increased in­
ventory and changeover costs, quality issues, and complexity­driven inefficiencies.
NNE has highlighted the pressing need for streamlined complexity management. The
potential to significantly impact operational efficiency and maintain high­quality standards
in such a demanding sector serves as a strong motivator for this study. By exploring
strategies to manage and mitigate the impacts of component complexity, this research
aims to provide actionable insights and practical solutions to improve the performance
and competitiveness of NNE and similar organizations in the industry.
This research aspires to contribute to the academic understanding of complexity man­
agement, data­driven models such as machine learning while offering tangible benefits to
industry practitioners, ensuring sustainable growth and innovation in the pharmaceutical
manufacturing domain.

1.2
Objectives
The primary objective of this project is to develop a data­driven model for complexity
analysis and management tailored to Novo Nordisk Engineering (NNE)’s database. The
project aims to address the challenges posed by the increase in component variants in
the pharmaceutical industry by focusing on the following key objectives.
1. Review of Related Research: Understanding the complexity. Studying manufac­
turing or industrial complexity. Exploring different types of complexity. Learning
quantifying the complexity of the system.
2. Data Deep Dive: It starts by diving headfirst into the data, exploring the vast land­
scape of component variants within NNE’s database. This initial exploration sets
the stage for our journey ahead.
3. Complexity Check: With the data in hand, the next step is to measure the complex­
ity lurking beneath the surface, identifying the factors adding layers of complexity,
such as interdependencies between component variants, and assessing their im­
pact on operational efficiency.
4. Machine learning model Development: The final act is to harness the power of
a machine learning model tailored to NNE’s database. This model will leverage
advanced algorithms to analyze patterns, forecast trends, and provide actionable
insights, empowering NNE to navigate complexity with confidence.
5. Evaluation of Models: Investigating methods to assess complexity. Validating the
developed model through thorough testing. Engaging with NNE employees to dis­
cuss the model and gather their feedback for further refinement.
By achieving these objectives, this project seeks to provide NNE with a robust framework
and tools to manage complexity effectively within its database, ultimately contributing to
improved operational efficiency, cost­effectiveness, and sustainable growth in the phar­
maceutical industry.

2.1
Complexity
The term ”complexity” lacks a universally recognized, exact (formal, for example), and
commonly accepted definition, leaving its meaning open­ended and confusing. The Latin
term ”complexus” originally meant ”entwined” or ”twisted together.” This meaning of ”com­
plex” is comparable to that given by the Oxford Dictionary, which states that anything is
”made of (usually several) closely connected parts.”
If a system has more elements or components and more connections between them, it
would be considered more complicated. In this case, there are variants of mechanical
components that can cause complexity. What is complicated is not necessarily complex,
and vice versa, and what is complicated for one person may be complex for another less
knowledgeable individual or a group with fewer technological tools [2].
2.2
Types of manufacturing complexity
Certain research studies divide manufacturing complexity into different categories based
on specific criteria. These publications frequently distinguish between two categories of
complexity: functional and physical divisions [2], as shown in Figure 2.1.
2.2.1
Physical domain complexity
The complexity that results from different aspects of an engineering system, such as scale,
coupling, diversity, and multidisciplinary, is termed physical domain complexity [2]. To
deepen our understanding of complexity, we can extend this classification to include two
additional types, refer to figure 2.2[3].

Static complexity
It is defined as the expected amount of information necessary to describe the state of an
engineered system [2].
Dynamic complexity
It is the expected amount of information necessary to describe the state of a system de­

Functional domain complexity
A measure of uncertainty in understanding what we want to know or in achieving a func­
tional requirement[4]. Figure 2.3 represent the categories of this complexity.
Time­ Independent complexity
It is the result of not satisfying the functional requirements of a system at all times, includ­
ing the uncertainty that arises because the designer lacks knowledge or understanding
of the system and its components [1, 2, 4, 5, 6]. Time­ Independent complexity is divided
into categories like real and imaginary complexity.
Time­ Dependent complexity
On the other side, the time­dependent complexity occurs periodic or combinatorial which
depends on the time [6].
• Periodic complexity: In periodic complexity the system returns to its original state
periodically, thus limiting the number of possible combinations of states [1].
• Combinatorial complexity:
Time­dependent combinatorial complexity is a com­
bined result of type uncertainty, coupling, and non­equilibrium causality.

2.3
Manufacturing systems complexity measurements
Multiple methods are used to measure the complexity of the manufacturing systems. It is
divided into multiple categories which are mentioned below [6]:
2.3.1
Chaos and non­linear theory
• Phase Space Reconstruction: Analyze the state space of the system to identify
patterns and predict future behavior. This method helps to visualize the evolution of
the system over time.
• Lyapunov Exponents: Measures the rate of separation of infinitesimally close tra­
jectories, indicating chaos. It is used to assess the stability and predictability of
manufacturing processes.
• Bifurcation Diagrams: Visualizes how a system changes with variations in pa­
rameters, revealing points of instability. This method helps in understanding critical
points where the system behavior changes.
2.3.2
Information theory
• Shanon Entropy: Measures the uncertainty or randomness in the state of the sys­
tem. Higher entropy indicates more complexity. It is used to analyze the variety and
distribution of pump states.
• ε­Machines: Uses hidden Markov models to represent the historical memory of
processes, providing a measure of statistical complexity. This method is useful for
modeling and analyzing the sequence of operations in manufacturing.
• Kolmogorov Complexity: Assesses the complexity of sequences based on their
compressibility. Less compressible sequences are more complex. It is used for
evaluating the predictability and randomness of time series data from pump opera­
tions.
2.3.3
Hybrid Approaches
• Heuristics ­ Indexes and Information theory: Combines information theory with
coding systems to evaluate product and process complexity. These methods are
suitable for systems with intricate interdependencies and high product/process vari­
ation.

2.8
Decision Tree
A decision tree is a data mining and application tool that uses a tree­like structure for
regression through classification. The root, positioned at the top, represents the input,
while the leaves at the bottom signify the results or decisions [16].
2.8.1
Terminology
• Root node: The starting point of the decision tree, where the input is provided. It
splits into internal nodes and is the parent node of these internal nodes.
• Internal node: Intermediate nodes that arise from the root node. These nodes act
as child nodes of the root and are further split into decision nodes, serving as parent
nodes for these decision nodes.
• Terminal node: Also known as decision nodes or leaves, these are the end points
of the tree. They stem from internal nodes and do not split further, having any child
nodes.
• Depth: Refers to the number of splits or divisions within the tree.
• Pruning: The process of selectively removing internal nodes or sub­nodes to sim­
plify the tree.
• Splitting: The act of dividing data into nodes or sub­nodes.
A decision tree is a machine learning technique used for decision­based analysis in fields
such as business, computer science, civil engineering, and ecology. It addresses various
regression data mining problems, aiding in decision making and management practices.
A decision tree processes input, defined as the root node, which splits into decision nodes
recursively based on specific procedures. Essentially, it performs regression through in­
ductive classification, repeatedly splitting data to extract relevant information.
Key advantages of decision trees include simple data preparation, easy interpretation,
robustness to outliers, ability to handle both categorical and numerical variables, and
effectiveness in nonlinear scenarios.
They require minimal computational power and memory. However, they are not suitable
for continuous variables of infinite size and can be unstable, a problem addressed by
techniques like bagging and boosting.
Decision tree algorithms vary in their structure (binary or non­binary) and impurity mea­
sures. Popular algorithms include ASSISTANT, CART, C4.5, CHAID, ID3, and QUEST.

2.8.2
Stability in a Decision Tree
Decision trees group observations with similar response values, but small changes in
the data can cause significant perturbations, affecting their predictability and leading to
unrealistic results. To address these instabilities, various algorithms are used, including
bagging, boosting, and pruning.
Bagging (bootstrapped aggregation) reduces the variance of prediction by creating mul­
tiple data sets through random sampling. Random forest, a popular model, uses this
principle by performing random subsampling and limiting the attributes available at leaf
nodes.
Boosting adjusts the weight of observations iteratively to correct classification errors, with
applications in models like gradient boosting and support vector machines. It focuses
more on incorrectly classified variants to improve accuracy.
Both bagging and boosting are weak learners individually, but together they form a strong
learner. Increasing the depth of a decision tree increases its complexity. Various software
tools are available for decision tree analysis, differing in operational compatibility, compu­
tational speed, data handling, algorithm structure, and usability, typically using datasets
in .txt or .csv formats.
2.9
Random Forest
The random forest algorithm, formalized by Breiman (2001) [17], is a supervised learning
method used for classification and regression. It predicts the class or mean value of a tar­
get variable by constructing an ensemble of ”classification and regression trees” (CART).
These trees model nonlinear relationships and are effective when standard parametric
regression assumptions are not met.
20

==PAGE:31==
Individual decision trees are created through recursive partitioning of the predictor variable
data space, resulting in a hierarchy of nodes and branches. This process refines partitions
to maximize local node homogeneity or optimize predictive criteria.
The random forest algorithm builds multiple decision trees using a training dataset. This
ensemble, or ”forest,” improves predictive accuracy and robustness by averaging the pre­
dictions of individual trees, encompassing more possibilities in the data [18]. Training data
is used to train the algorithm and a separate validation dataset may be used for evaluation
[19].
2.9.1
Description of Algorithm
An ensemble of decision trees by developing each tree from a bootstrap sample of training
data is a random forest algorithm construct. Each tree is built through recursive partition­
ing of the predictor space, selecting a subset of predictors at each split to maximize the
reduction in the impurity of the nodes. For classification trees, this involves minimizing
the Gini impurity, while for regression trees, it involves minimizing the error variance.
In classification, the random forest makes predictions based on the majority vote of in­
dividual trees, while in regression it averages the predicted values. Each tree uses an
out­of­bag (OOB) sample, the part of the training data not included in the bootstrap sam­
ple, for validation. The OOB error estimate indicates the model’s generalization error,
comparable to standard cross­validation.
Key hyperparameters in random forests include the number of trees, the number of predic­
tors sampled at each split, the bootstrap sample fraction, and controls on tree complexity
such as minimum node size and maximum number of terminal nodes. Adjusting these
parameters can balance prediction accuracy and variance reduction.
2.9.2
Model Interpretation
Model interpretation in random forests focuses on the relative importance of predictor
variables and their effects on the target variable. Two key metrics are the importance of
permutation and the importance of Gini.
• Permutation importance measures the impact of predictors by observing the de­
crease in model performance when predictor values are randomly permuted in the
out­of­bag (OOB) samples.
• Gini importance calculates the frequency and effectiveness of a predictor in split­
ting the data, weighted by sample proportions, across all trees. Revised the tra­
ditional Gini importance to eliminate bias towards predictors with many split points
[20].
Other interpretive tools include permutation tests to calculate p­values for predictors, pre­
diction intervals, and methods for causal inference [21, 22]. Visual tools like partial de­
pendence plots (PDPs) and individual conditional expectation (ICE) plots help illus­
trate the effects of predictors on the target variable, showing overall and individual tree
responses, respectively.
The advantages of random forests include their nonparametric nature, strong predictive
performance, capability to determine variable importance, and built­in model validation
using OOB observations. They can also handle incomplete observations through impu­
tation.
Limitations include:
1. Less interpretable results compared to single trees.

K­mean
The K­Means Clustering method is a basic unsupervised learning technique. This tech­
nique is widely used in machine learning and data analysis due to its simplicity and effec­
tiveness. Assisting in the identification of patterns and simplification of intricate systems,
it is especially helpful in dividing a dataset into discrete groups or clusters according to
feature similarity. The clustering of K­Means may contribute significantly to the optimiza­
tion of mechanical models by increasing performance and decreasing the complexity of
the model.
2.10.1
Algorithm
The objective of clustering is to divide a set of ’n’ observations into ’K’ clusters. Each
observation is assigned to the cluster with the closest mean. These iterative steps are
followed by the algorithm:
• Initialization: Select random starting centroids of the K clusters from the data set.
• Assignment: Using a distance measure, usually Euclidean distance, assign each
data point to the closest centroid.
• Update: As the mean of all data points assigned to each cluster, recalculate the
centroids.
• Initialization: When the centroids no longer noticeably vary or a predetermined
number of repetitions is achieved, repeat the assignment and update procedures.

Grid Search
A systematic approach to investigating the hyperparameter space is Grid Search. Using
this method, a predetermined set of hyperparameters is selected, and the ideal set is
determined by testing each conceivable combination of these parameters.
Smaller search spaces benefit from this thorough search, as it finds the optimal set of pa­

ing model’s overall performance.

Random Search
Grid Search is efficient when there are few possible combinations of hyperparameters, but
as the search space expands, it becomes computationally costly. Random searching is
frequently favored in these circumstances. Random Search chooses random parameter
choices for each iteration rather than testing every possible combination.
Although it might not identify the ideal combination, this method is more effective in larger
search areas and can quickly settle on a collection of acceptable hyperparameters. When
there are constraints on time or computer power, Random Search may be quite useful.

Boosting classifier
Boosting is the process of combining many learners into one powerful learner (Figure

principle is to train predictors one after the other to correct their predecessors. There are
several boosting methods but Adaptive Boosting and Gradient boosting are very popular.
2.12.3
Bagging/ pasting classifier
Using several training techniques is one approach to obtain a varied collection of clas­
sifiers, as was previously mentioned. Alternatively, each predictor can be trained using
a different random subset of the training set while still using the same procedure. This

6
Conclusion
In the thesis, the first objective was to analyze the data for different pumps. After the
overview of the data, the second step was to check the complexity and randomness and,
if possible, to measure it. Then, using a machine learning model try to reduce complexity
and the number of components.
While analyzing the dataset, the key observation is that around 40% of pumps are used
once in the system. This means that these pumps are used in the factory for one process,
and the rest are used in multiple processes in the facility. The available complexity falls
under the static physical domain, and a way to calculate the complexity is using Shannon
entropy.
To reduce the complexity of this system, two different types of machine learning are used.
One of them is supervised learning and the other is unsupervised learning. Logistic re­
gression, decision tree, and random forest classification models have been used under
supervised learning. In unsupervised machine learning, the K­means machine learning
model is used.
For supervised learning, the accuracy is good with these machine learning models, but for
validation, the flow rate graph and design pressure are used. The thesis also discusses
how to ensemble methods to improve performance. It also performed decently.
K­means combined with Algorithm 1 worked very well, recommending pumps for pumps
in a cluster. It also includes a funnel and different types of pumps. Using this method, the
number of pumps dropped from 71 to 30. But complexity increased.
In conclusion, Depending on available data, the Shannon entropy worked well but is not
related to the variation of components in the system. Supervised learning could not do
well with the prediction of the right pump. The mentioned k­means and algorithm did well
in reducing the number of pumps. However, funnels created different type of complexity
in the system.
In the future, some improvements are possible. The current data set lacks sufficient di­
versity and volume. By incorporating more comprehensive and varied data sources, the
robustness and accuracy of the analysis can be significantly improved. This includes data
from additional vendors, new operational scenarios, and broader environmental condi­
tions.
Beyond K­means clustering, other unsupervised learning techniques like hierarchical clus­
tering, DBSCAN, and Gaussian Mixture Models should be explored. These methods can
uncover hidden patterns and insights within the data, potentially leading to better opti­
mization and classification of pumps and processes.
Exploring various recommendation algorithms such as collaborative filtering, content­
based filtering, and hybrid models can improve pump selection and optimization. These
models can provide customized recommendations based on historical data, operational
efficiency, process requirements, and improving decision­making and system performance.
