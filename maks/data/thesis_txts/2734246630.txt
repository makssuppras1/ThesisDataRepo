Abstract  
EnzymaƟc reducƟon of CO2 to methanol promises the producƟon of a high-value, widely applicable chemical 
from the primary greenhouse gas. Using speciﬁc enzymes and selecƟve membranes combined into enzymaƟc 
membrane reactors (EMR) promises lower energy consumpƟon and higher product selecƟvity. SƟll, 
convenƟonal EMR provides the enzymes liƩle to no protecƟon from harsh industrial condiƟons, resulƟng in 
deacƟvaƟon over Ɵme. Thus, new EMR designs must be developed. Therefore, this thesis aƩempts to 
improve a method of enzymaƟcally producing methanol from CO2 by using carbonic anhydrase (CA) 
immobilized on a membrane to enhance the ﬁrst step in a 3-step cascade.  For this purpose, two methods of 
immobilizing CA on a membrane bioreactor are compared: ﬁrst, by immobilizing CA on a polysulfone 
ultraﬁltraƟon membrane using polydopamine and polyethyleneimine and second, by encapsulaƟng CA in a 
metal-organic framework and immobilizing it on a membrane. The two methods are compared based on 
acƟvity and thermostability at 60°C.  
CA immobilized on a membrane showed higher acƟvity of 442 mU/mg compared to free CA at 220 ± 28 
mU/mg and slightly improved thermostability. Carbonic anhydrase immobilized in the metal-organic 
framework (ZIF-8) showed lower acƟvity compared to free CA of only 0.028 ±0.007 mU/mg; this was later 
conﬁrmed by SEM and BET to be caused by the agglomeraƟon of the CA@ZIF-8. Nevertheless, the CA 
immobilized in ZIF-8 (CA@ZIF-8) showed to retain 90 % of the iniƟal acƟvity, whereas free CA only retained 
around 8 %, showing improved thermostability aŌer 1 hour of incubaƟon at 60 °C.  
 A novel method of immobilizing the CA@ZIF-8 on a Psf ultraﬁltraƟon membrane was discovered, creaƟng a 
working enzymaƟc membrane reactor using CA in ZIF-8, which showed promising catalyƟc acƟvity. The 
methods were also veriﬁed by using water contact angle, zeta potenƟal, permeate ﬂux, and FTIR spectra, and 
for the ZIF-8, the surface area and morphology were determined.

ObjecƟves

1 IntroducƟon  
In 1896, a Swedish chemist named Svante Arrhenius ﬁrst quanƟﬁed how an increase in atmospheric CO2 
would act as the glass windows in a “hot house” and prevent more of the sun's heat rays from escaping the 
atmosphere; he then speculated that this eﬀect, later named the greenhouse eﬀect could contribute to long 
term changes in the climate. He even went as far as to menƟon the burning of fossil fuels as one of the 
possible sources of CO2 [1].  
Over a decade later, a German chemist and later Nobel Laureate named Fritz Haber patented a process for 
synthesizing ammonia from nitrogen in the air [2]. This invenƟon served as the foundaƟon for the nitrogen 
ferƟlizers used today worldwide and coincidentally as a feedstock for high explosives. Combined with his 
later work in the German poison gas program during WWI earned him the moniker “Father of Chemical 
Warfare”. However, his method of mass-producing syntheƟc ammonia also founded modern agricultural 
pracƟces providing food for the growing populaƟon [3]–[5]. Thus paving the way for the subsequent 
populaƟon explosion [6].  
In 1960, Sidney Loeb and Srinivasa Sourirajan discovered a pracƟcal way of construcƟng a membrane that 
could withstand high ﬂux. This discovery would begin the membrane technology revoluƟon, leading to the 
wide use of membranes in industries today [7], [8]. Around the same Ɵme as Haber, two researchers, Nelson 
and Griﬃn, demonstrated that enzymes, a well-known natural catalyst, could be immobilized while 
maintaining acƟvity [9].  
The research menƟoned above was done without knowing how the work might aﬀect the world within the 
next 100 years. The following thesis is also an aƩempt to understand and solve a part of the climate crisis, 
personiﬁed by Arrhenius and Harber, by using enzymes as a soluƟon to capture and convert CO2 to value-
added chemicals by using the work founded by Loeb and Sourirajan in combinaƟon with the enzyme 
techniques discovered by Nelson and Griﬃn well as countless others.

1.1 CO2 emissions  
The anthropogenic emission of CO2 has risen rapidly since the beginning of the Industrial RevoluƟon, when 
fossil fuels, especially coal, gas, and oil, became the primary energy source for the increasingly industrialized 
society worldwide [6], [10]. The surge in anthropogenic CO2 emissions has signiﬁcantly risen aŌer World War 
II, together with the global populaƟon. This increase in atmospheric CO2 can be documented in ice cores, 
daƟng back 800,000 years, as seen in Figure 1.1 [11], where pre-industrializaƟon levels of atmospheric CO2 
were 200-300 ppm. Nowadays, the post-industrializaƟon levels of CO2 are well above 400 ppm, with 
signiﬁcant increases from year to year, i.e., a rise of 2.13 ppm was observed between 2021 and 2022 – the 
eleventh year in a row. Finally, the latest level equals 420 ppm as of 2023 [10].  
 
Figure 1.1: Carbon dioxide concentraƟon in the atmosphere[11] 
 
This dramaƟc increase in atmospheric CO2 has also led to a rise in global temperatures by roughly 1 °C [12], 
[13]. This is caused by the greenhouse eﬀect, coined by Svante Arrhenius in 1896. The increasing atmospheric 
CO2 also leads to an array of challenges for populaƟons worldwide.  
Higher average temperatures elevate the risk of drought around the equator, which in turn leads to increased 
agricultural challenges, including lower average and more varying crop yields, especially for crops such as 
wheat [6], [14], [15]. Considering global demand for calories and protein is expected to increase by 100 ±11 
% and 110 ±7 %, respecƟvely, towards 2050, driven by the 2.3 Billion person increase in populaƟon and rising 
living standards in developing countries [16], this consƟtutes a severe problem.  
Another serious consequence of the increased atmospheric CO2 is that the oceans are acidifying. Because 
the oceans currently absorb a third of all the CO2 in the atmosphere, and since this concentraƟon has been 
rising, the equilibrium between the sea and the atmospheric CO2 has shiŌed so that more CO2 is absorbed 
into the seawater, acƟng as a buﬀer for the atmospheric CO2. However, oceans can only absorb a certain 
amount of CO2, and once the acidiﬁcaƟon is complete and the oceans are saturated, the atmospheric CO2 
concentraƟon will rise even faster.  When CO2 is dissolved into water, it protonates to form bicarbonate ions, 
causing the pH to drop; this is known as ocean acidiﬁcaƟon and decreases the availability of calcium 
carbonate, used in aquaƟc organisms to create calcium structures such as shells and skeletons [17], [18]. 
Ocean acidiﬁcaƟon also reduces the growth of many calcifying species of aquaƟc life, including shellﬁsh,

primary source of protein [19].  
The increased global temperature also aﬀects average global sea levels, ﬁrstly by increasing the temperature

This is supported by both satellite and in-situ data collected from 1993-2009 and from 1880 to 2009,

the ground/water below will absorb more of the sun's energy, causing the global temperatures to rise even 
further [22]. The increasing sea levels will directly aﬀect a large percentage of the global populaƟon living 
in/close to the ciƟes near the coast and impact ground and freshwater availability [23].  
Fortunately, countless iniƟaƟves aim to prevent further increases in atmospheric CO2, thereby fending oﬀ 
the eﬀects of global warming before temperature and water levels rise to a degree where current socieƟes 
collapse. This includes huge governmental and intergovernmental programs such as the Paris Agreement [23] 
and the InﬂaƟon ReducƟon Act [24]; both programs aim to create economic incenƟves for business to 
transiƟon their producƟon to more carbon-neutral methods. The Paris Agreement focuses on keeping this 
century's temperature increase well below 2 °C above pre-industrial levels. This is done, among other 
iniƟaƟves, by introducing “carbon pricing”, where a tax/fee is placed on the emission of CO2, thus creaƟng 
an economic incenƟve for emiƩers to change toward less CO2 emiƫng methods.  This carbon pricing also 
creates an internal European market where emissions allowances are sold between companies at around 80-
100 EUR/Ton CO2, thus creaƟng an economic incenƟve for each company to lower their emissions wherever 
possible [24].  
Then, the inﬂaƟon reducƟon act aims to lower CO2 emissions by invesƟng in and giving tax reducƟons to 
develop and produce renewable energy within the USA. One of the methods menƟoned in the InﬂaƟon 
ReducƟon Act is carbon capture/storage, where 3 billion USD are given in tax credit to incenƟvize the 
development and use of this technology [25]. This is all to explain that there are signiﬁcant economic 
incenƟves to develop and implement carbon capture technologies worldwide and to show that the 
development of technology in this thesis has global economic support.  
 
 
 
 
 
1.2 Carbon capture  
Carbon capture has been recognized as one of the potenƟal technologies to reduce the atmospheric CO2 
concentraƟon and thus help keep the global average temperature increase well below 2 °C as agreed upon 
by 193 states in the 2015 Paris Agreement [26]. Global emissions from fossil fuels alone exceed 35 Gt CO2

/year in 2022 and are expected to rise 0.5 %/year as countries such as India and China conƟnue to develop. 
This means that to adhere to the goal of staying well below the 2 °C increase, we would need to decrease 
our CO2 emission by 1.4 Gt/year. As a comparison, this is the same decrease we experienced globally during 
the COVID-19 fall of 2020 [27]. As renewable energies are yet to be fully developed to cover our energy 
demand, energy from fossil fuels is expected to account for 78 % of global power by 2040, combined with

the goal of net-zero CO2 emissions by 2050, all methods for carbon capture, storage, or uƟlizaƟon are needed. 
Some of these will be presented in the following secƟons. 
Carbon capture technology provides a method of lowering the atmospheric CO2 concentraƟon by acƟvely 
removing CO2 from the atmosphere, in contrast to other iniƟaƟves focusing only on limiƟng humanity's 
current emissions.  ConvenƟonal carbon capture methods are split into three categories 1) oxy fuel 
separaƟon, 2) precombusƟon capture and 3) post-combusƟon capture. Common for all is limiƟng the amount 
of CO2 released into the air while producing heat and power from burning fossil fuels. The diﬀerent 
technologies for CO2 capture can be seen in a simpliﬁed diagram in Figure 1.2

In Figure 1.2 it can be seen that post-combusƟon is a normal combusƟon where the CO2 is then separated 
from the waste ﬂue gas using various methods, including membranes and monoethanolamine. The 
advantage of this approach is that exisƟng power plants can be modiﬁed for CO2 capture. Pre-combusƟon is 
done by producing hydrogen gas from fossil fuels by ﬁrst gasifying the fuel, producing syngas (H2 and CO), 
and then producing pure CO2 and hydrogen gas, where hydrogen is then burned as a clean fuel to produce 
power and heat. Oxy-fuel combusƟon funcƟons by using almost pure oxygen in the combusƟon of the 
fuels, thus limiƟng the exhaust gas to carbon monoxide and water; this process is the most advanced way 
of CO2 capture [28], [30], [31]. Simply put carbon capture from power producƟon using fossil fuels prevents 
CO2 from going into the atmosphere by separaƟng it from the other combusƟon components and then 
dehydraƟng and pressurizing it to prepare it for transport, storage, or uƟlizaƟon.

The concentrated CO2 can be uƟlized for various applicaƟons, including building materials, fuels, beverage 
carbonaƟon and chemicals. AlternaƟvely, it can be stored away in geological formaƟons such as old mines, 
basalt formaƟons, caves or depleted oil/gas reservoirs by employing enhanced oil reﬁnery (EOR). EOR is the 
most common method of storing CO2 in geological formaƟon, but is done to extract crude oil trapped in 
porous rock formaƟons by displacing the oil with pressurized CO2. Typically, the technologies applied to the 
captured CO2 are split into two categories: carbon capture and storage (CCS) and carbon capture and 
uƟlizaƟon (CCU), where carbon capture and uƟlizaƟon will be presented in more detail later [28], [31], [32].   
1.2.1 Carbon capture and storage  
The convenƟonal way of capturing CO2 is to either use a solid or liquid absorbent, typically in connecƟon with 
a fossil fuel powerplant, cement or steel factory where the CO2 is absorbed from the puriﬁed ﬂue gas; this 
makes the capture easier due to the higher concentraƟon of CO2 [28], [32]. 
One of the most widely used and only commercialized post-combusƟon methods of capturing CO2 is 
monoethanolamine (MEA), a primary amine soluble in water that captures CO2 by forming carbamate in a 
liquid. To release the pure CO2 and regenerate MEA, the compound is heated to somewhere between 70-
200 °C, which requires somewhere between 3.8-4.2 GJ/t CO2, depending on the method. Carbon capture 
using MEA can help remove between 87-100 % of the CO2 released from combusƟon and releases almost 
pure CO2, making it a prevalent capture method [32]–[34]. Disadvantages include thermal and oxidaƟve 
degradaƟon, especially in high regeneraƟon temperatures. Other terƟary amine candidates have the methyl-
diethanolamine (MDEA) and 2-dimethylaminoethanol (DEAE) that react with CO2 to form bicarbonate ions 
and are thermally regenerated at a lower energy expenditure of 1.34 GJ/t and 1.42-1.57 GJ/t CO2 respecƟvely. 
Disadvantages include slow absorpƟon rate and degradaƟon. However, methods using other amines are less 
technologically developed than MDA and are, therefore, less widespread [28], [32]. 
An alternaƟve to the single amine approach is to use a combinaƟon of 7 % (w/w) MEA and 3 %(w/w) MDEA 
with 1 % (w/w) 2-amino-2methyl-1-propanol (AMP). This method forms addiƟonal bicarbonate from MDEA 
and sterically hindered carbamate that reverts into bicarbonate. This method archives higher CO2 absorpƟon, 
lower regeneraƟon energy and higher regeneraƟon eﬃciency compared to a 10 % (w/w) soluƟon[33]. 
However, the process is less well-known and, therefore, less commercialized.  
Even with lower regeneraƟon energy and temperature, carbon capture by liquid amine soluƟon sƟll has the 
disadvantage of requiring enormous amounts of energy, making the process economically reliant on cheap 
energy. Furthermore, the environmental eﬀect of producing the energy convenƟonally for regeneraƟon 
contradicts the purpose of carbon capture. Another disadvantage of the MEA method is the acute toxicity as 
well as the corrosivity of the MEA, especially concerning works and surrounding inhabitants if leaks were to 
occur from a plant [35].

chemical product as the storage, contrary to the amine method, where the binding agent is regenerated at 
high energy expense. The ﬁrst promising method is to use slaked lime (calcium hydroxide) as the solid 
chemical absorbent; the produced calcium carbonate (CaCO3) can then be uƟlized as a construcƟon material 
[32]. The slaked lime process has the major advantage that no thermal regeneraƟon is needed, as the product 
(CaCO3) is also the carbon storage. This process has a higher capacity than amine-based carbon capture but

1.2.2.1 
Methanol producƟon from CO2 
One of the most promising chemicals being produced today is methanol or Wood Spirits. It is primarily used 
as a base chemical to create a variety of other important chemicals, mainly formaldehyde and fuel addiƟves. 
Methanol is also a prime candidate to replace fossil fuels such as gasoline and diesel and rival hydrogen to 
store excess electrical energy from wind, solar and wave power from “Power-to-X” projects [44].  
Therefore, renewable methanol promises to lower CO2 emissions and provide an obvious transiƟon from 
fossil fuels. Advantages include that methanol is a high energy-density liquid fuel combined with the fact that 
methanol can be stored and transported safely and at ambient pressures compared to the more energy-
dense hydrogen that must be stored under high pressures and at greater risk [44]. In comparison methanol 
and gasoline have octane numbers of about 110 and 91-97, respecƟvely, methanol can replace 
gasoline/diesel in otherwise hard-to-electrify transportaƟon such as shipping and aviaƟon. A current example 
is the launch of “Laura Mærsk”, the ﬁrst container ship from the Mærsk ﬁeld by renewably produced 
methanol [45]. Another overlooked advantage of methanol as a fuel is that it can be used in up to 85 % of 
tradiƟonal combusƟon engines, meaning that there would be no need to modify the personal transportaƟon 
ﬂeet if the transiƟon to methanol is made [40], [46].  
The global demand for methanol has been increasing over the last 50 years. In 2016, global producƟon went 
from 92 Mt/y to about 111 Mt/year in 2022, with the annual growth expected to conƟnue as methanol gains 
popularity as a fuel and base chemical [47]. Methanol has become a valuable raw material trading alongside 
gasoline at 270-380 USD/Ton within 2023 [48].  
However today, about 90 % of methanol is synthesized from natural gas, and using methanol as fuel 
contributes negaƟvely to greenhouse gas emissions [46]. Luckily, methanol can be synthesized from 
renewable sources, including biomass conversion and carbon capture. The advantages of using CO2 as raw 
material to produce methanol include the stability of the CO2 molecule, the fact that CO2 as a carbon source 
is not in compeƟƟon with food producƟon, and its abundance in modern energy producƟon. The biggest 
challenge of producing methanol from CO2 is the stability of the linear CO2 molecules, causing strong carbon-
oxygen and standard free energy of (∆G= -400 KJ/mol), meaning a thermodynamically unfavorable reacƟon. 
The process requires 228 KJ/mol CO2 and six electrons to reduce the carbon, making it expensive to run on a 
large scale [40].  This challenge is overcome using a catalyst that decreases the acƟvaƟon energy and 
increases the reacƟon rate.  
This method is commercialized with several plants worldwide using excess CO2 and H2 from power plants to 
produce methanol using a catalyst. The ﬁrst commercial plant was the George Olah Renewable Methanol 
plant in Iceland, where 4000-ton methanol is produced yearly, capturing 5500-ton CO2. This process uses the 
copper–zinc oxide hetero-nanostructures supported on aluminium oxide (CZA)-catalyst, the predominant 
method for commercial-scale thermochemical methanol producƟon. The process is carried out at 250 °C and 
under 50-100 Bar using H2 produced with electrolysis to convert the CO2 to methanol [49]. This also highlights 
the main disadvantages of this process, being that it requires high pressures and temperature, as well as 
disƟllaƟon and electrolysis, which all need vast amounts of energy, which in some cases can be produced 
renewably but mainly stem from burning fossil fuels [50]. Luckily, nature has its way of converƟng CO2, which 
has gained interest in recent years because of its use of ambient temperatures and pressures and green 
chemistry. This approach will be revised in the next secƟon.

1.3.1 Enzymes  
Enzymes are nature's catalysts; without them, no life as we know it would be possible. Humankind has also 
used enzymes since ancient Ɵmes to produce everything from beer, bread, and cheese to medicine[51]. 
Today, the food and pharmaceuƟcal industries, as well as the emerging biofuel industries, rely heavily on the 
use of enzymes. Especially in biotechnology, enzymes have become a vital part of many industries, providing 
a consistent, eﬃcient catalyst with high yields and easy modiﬁcaƟon, and they can catalyze reacƟons under 
ambient condiƟons [52]. Enzymes are used to catalyze various reacƟons, lowering the acƟvaƟon energy and 
creaƟng favorable conﬁguraƟons for the molecules involved, thus increasing the reacƟon rates. Enzymes are 
so important that last year, the global enzyme market reached over 10 billion USD [53].  
Industrial enzymes are expensive to produce and purify; therefore, maintaining an enzyme’s catalyƟc ability 
is crucial to any bio-industrial process. Because most enzymes have evolved to catalyze reacƟons under 
certain condiƟons, they are sensiƟve to biologically incompaƟble condiƟons. Biologically unfavorable 
condiƟons will cause the enzyme to change its terƟary structure, leading to changes in the acƟve site, thus 
lowering catalyƟc ability and, in the worst case, even denaturing the proteins, causing irreversible damage 
[52]. In a cascade reacƟon, enzymes are selected based on their speciﬁc catalyƟc ability so that they produce 
substrates for each other in a cascade reacƟon. That way, relaƟvely complex chemical reacƟons can be 
catalyzed, which no single enzyme could do independently. 
One promising example of this mulƟ-enzyme conversion of CO2 into methanol is the 3-step cascade reacƟon 
ﬁrst shown by [54]. This reducƟon of CO2 to methanol begins ﬁrst by reducing CO2 by formate dehydrogenase 
(FateDH) into formic acid, second formic acid is reduced by formaldehyde dehydrogenase (FaldDH) into 
formaldehyde, and third formaldehyde is reduced into methanol using alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH). All 
steps use the electron donor nicoƟnamide adenine dinucleoƟde (NADH) to provide hydrogen ions to the 
reacƟon and act as a cofactor to all three enzymes. The complete reacƟon can also be seen in Figure 1.4 
below.

1.3.3.1 
EnzymaƟc membrane reactor  
EnzymaƟc membrane reactors (EMR), is a membrane setup where a membrane is used to retain the enzymes 
as the substrates (either as a gas or in a liquid soluƟon), are conƟnuously converted by the immobilized 
enzymes on/in the membrane. Thus, the membrane catalyzes the reacƟon and separates the product from 
the reactants. This approach oﬀers many beneﬁts, including instantaneous product removal,  improved 
biocatalyƟc acƟvity, higher producƟvity, scalability, ﬂexibility and economic feasibility, compared to 
convenƟonal conversion in soluƟon [69], [70]. Especially when combined with the emerging immobilizaƟon 
technology of  MOFs, providing the enzymes with excellent protecƟon from biologically unfavorable 
industrial condiƟons, as well as increasing stability and recyclability of the enzymes [58], [64], [71], [72]. Such 
a state-of-the-art method for CO2 conversion by EMR is presented in secƟon 1.3.3.2 below. The conversion 
of CO2 by EMR oﬀers many beneﬁts, including low temperature/pressure conversion of otherwise inert and 
surplus gas into a value-added chemical without the use of harsh solvents. SƟll, challenges with the method 
exist, prevenƟng it from large-scale commercial applicaƟon. The central challenges include stability and 
reusability of the enzymes in the mulƟ-enzyme cascade reacƟon (MECR), see secƟon 1.3.1.  As well as 
resistance to the external factors of CO2 sequestraƟon, such as temperature and pH [61], [64], [69], [73], [74].  
Challenges with low iniƟal hydraƟon of CO2 from FateDH have also been idenƟﬁed, causing low solubilizaƟon 
of CO2 in the soluƟon and, thus, low reacƟon rates, making the process less economically feasible due to 
CO2’s low solubility in water. This problem is solved by adding a hydraƟon enzyme (such as CA) causing formic 
acid to be produced by FateDH at much higher rates, using bicarbonate as substrate [61], [69], [75].   
Another idenƟﬁed challenge, speciﬁc to the mulƟ-enzyme cascade reacƟon (MECR), is the boƩleneck arising 
from the (Formic acidFormaldehyde) reacƟon catalyzed by FaldDH. This enzyme requires a threshold 
formate concentraƟon to proceed, thus acƟng as a boƩleneck for the whole reacƟon. Proposed soluƟons 
include sequenƟal immobilizaƟon, and enzyme-engineering [57].   
Another challenge speciﬁc to the MRCR reacƟon is the diﬀerent opƟmum temperatures for the enzymes 
involved, where FateDH, FaldDH and CA all have opƟma at 37 °C the enzymes ADH and GDH have opƟma at 
25 °C [76], [77]. This, however, is not a catastrophic problem because either enzyme catalyzes at either 
temperature. This problem is typically diminished by running MECR somewhere between 27-37 °C.   
The challenges of EMR also include more pracƟcal challenges, including maintaining enzyme stability and 
reusability, ﬁnding feasible and scalable reactor setups in addiƟon to methanol removal to avoid both enzyme 
inhibiƟon and backwards reacƟon (MeOH--> CO2) [57], [69], [78].

1.4 Enzyme immobilizaƟon strategies 
Enzyme immobilizaƟon was developed around 60 years ago to improve the acƟvity, stability, and selecƟvity 
of the enzymes under industrial condiƟons, thereby lowering the cost associated with low turnover rate, 
recycling, and reuse [80], [81]. Since then, a variety of common enzymes such as laccase, glucose oxidase, 
lipase, pepsin, dextranase, and carbonic anhydrases have been successfully immobilized on convenƟonal 
membranes using diﬀerent immobilizaƟon methods [64], [70], [74]  
The ﬁve most common enzyme immobilizaƟon methods are entrapment, adsorpƟon, crosslinking covalent 
bonding, and aﬃnity see (Figure 1.5). Methods are used to immobilize enzymes reversibly or irreversibly 
depending on the method. Diﬀerent methods aﬀect the stability, acƟvity and loading of the enzymes and can 
be used alone or in combinaƟon.

crosslinking, and aﬃnity immobilizaƟon [55]

AdsorpƟon immobilizaƟon is a method where the enzymes are adsorbed into a solid inert structure, typically 
a ﬁbre, charcoal, agarose or a porous material [82]. The enzymes are bound to the network by weak 
interacƟons, such as hydrophobic interacƟon, hydrogen bonds, salt bridges, electrostaƟc interacƟons and 
Van der Waals forces. AdsorpƟon immobilizaƟon is the simplest form of immobilizaƟon, done by immersing 
the structure in a soluƟon containing the enzymes for a period of Ɵme. This type of immobilizaƟon oﬀers 
limited loss of enzyme acƟvity because the acƟve site is not hindered or changed because of the weak 
interacƟons, and disadvantages also include easy desorpƟon due to changes in pH, temperature, or ionic 
concentraƟon along with poor general storage ability [73], [83], [84].  
Covalent immobilizaƟon occurs when the enzymes are covalently bound to the funcƟonal groups on the 
surface of the support or a membrane. Speciﬁcally, the amino acid side chains of the enzymes are linked to 
the funcƟonal groups, such as carboxylic acids, imidazoles, and hydroxyl groups, where they form strong 
covalent bonds. Typical surface materials include chitosan, cyanogen bromine-infused agarose and pepƟdes 
modiﬁed surfaces [82]. This leads to irreversible immobilizaƟon with negligible enzyme leakage and 
prolonged reusability but in a trade-oﬀ with low enzyme acƟvity caused by the change in enzyme 
conformaƟon when binding a surface to an amino acid sidechain [84], [85].  
Crosslinking is a method of linking enzymes using a crosslinker molecule, typically glutaraldehyde or an 
inacƟve protein. This simple type of immobilizaƟon produced strong bonds but at the cost of enzyme acƟvity 
due to changes in the acƟve site. However, this method requires a large amount of chemicals for crosslinking 
[70], [86]. 
The aﬃnity immobilizaƟon principle is based on having a tag molecule bound to one party (support or 
enzyme) with an aﬃnity towards the other party (enzyme or support). Typically, a tag such as an anƟbody or 
bioƟn has an aﬃnity towards a speciﬁc site on the enzyme, such as hisƟdine. Typical supports include 
chitosan-coated porous silica beads and agarose-linked with concanavalin protein. This immobilizaƟon 
method provides control over the orientaƟon of the enzyme and avoids acƟve site blocking. Nevertheless, 
the downside includes that speciﬁc groups are needed on the enzyme and the tag; this can be accomplished 
by geneƟc engineering of the enzyme [82], [84].  
Entrapment immobilizaƟon occurs when the enzymes are caged within another structure without chemically 
anchoring. The entrapment structures are typically silica sol-gel, polymers, carbon paste, amphiphilic 
network, or MOF. Entrapment immobilizaƟon is dependent on the diﬀusion barrier between the enzyme and 
substrate, the ability of the substrate to reach the enzyme, as the enzyme is ﬁxated in the pores/gel to avoid 
leaching [83], [84]. The general advantages of this method include no chemical reacƟon possibly aﬀecƟng 
enzyme acƟvity, and several types of enzymes can be immobilized simultaneously. Typical disadvantages 
include a high diﬀusion barrier, meaning only reacƟons with small substrates and products can be catalyzed. 
AddiƟonal disadvantages include enzyme leaching as well as biologically incompaƟble synthesis condiƟons. 
MOFs are an emerging method for entrapment immobilizaƟon because of their mild syntheƟc condiƟons, 
geometric richness, large surface-to-volume raƟo and adjustable pore size [71], [86].

The substance polydopamine (PDA) was discovered by Lee et al. [87], inspired by the composiƟon of the

3.1.1 CA acƟvity using CO2 as substrate    
Free CA was measured using CO2 as substrate in accordance with secƟon 2.2.1. AŌer an iniƟal trial and error 
process, which resulted in thoroughly cleaning the measuring equipment and adding detergent to the

4 Conclusion  
The objecƟves of this thesis were to improve the hydraƟon of CO2 using CA to enhance the 3-step cascade 
producing of methanol from CO2 to ulƟmately reduce greenhouse gas emissions. To achieve this, two 
methods of immobilizing CA on an EMR were compared: immobilizing CA onto a Psf ultraﬁltraƟon membrane 
modiﬁed with PDA and PEI and encapsulaƟng CA in ZIF-8. The two methods were compared based on acƟvity 
and thermostability, and available characterizaƟons were done to verify the methods. Through a series of 
experiments, a viable method of characterizing the acƟvity of immobilized and free CA was determined using 
p-NPA as a pracƟcal surrogate substrate for CO2 while doing the ﬁltraƟons through the membrane.   
Using p-NPA as substrate was determined to be the most viable way of determining the catalyƟc acƟvity of 
CA immobilized on a membrane, compared to using CO2 as substrate. This was due to the low diﬀerence in 
reacƟon rate between the catalyzed and uncatalyzed reacƟons and was determined through a series of 
experiments. 
CA immobilized on the Psf membrane by electrostaƟc adsorpƟon showed higher acƟvity of 442 mU/mg 
compared to free CA at 220±28 mU/mg and improved thermostability by around 27 % compared to free CA. 
CA immobilized in ZIF-8 by entrapment showed lower acƟvity compared to free CA of only 0.028 ±0.007 
mU/mg; this was later conﬁrmed by SEM and BET to be caused by the agglomeraƟon of the CA@ZIF-8 
parƟcles causing lower relaƟve acƟvity per mg enzyme. Furthermore, the CA@ZIF-8 showed an improvement 
in thermostability of 82 % aŌer 1 hour of incubaƟon at 60°C, compared to free CA. Where free CA only 
retained a total of around 8 % aŌer 1 hour of incubaƟon at 60°C.  
A novel method for creaƟng a working EMR using CA@ZIF-8 immobilized on a Psf ultraﬁltraƟon membrane 
using entrapment in PDA was also discovered, and preliminary results indicate that it showed improved 
catalyƟc acƟvity compared to the EMR of CA@membrane, but further study is necessary. It was also 
demonstrated by analysis of the water contact angle that immobilizing CA@ZIF-8 on the membrane 
signiﬁcantly increased the hydrophilicity of the membrane and that immobilizing ZIF-8 only increased the 
hydrophilicity by around 10°.The structure of the ZIF-8 and CA@ZIF-8 was conﬁrmed using FTIR and SEM, and 
the average parƟcle size was determined to be 369 ±187 nm and 633 ± 308 nm for ZIF-8 and CA@ZIF-8, 
respecƟvely. Using BET, the surface area of the ZIF-8 was determined to be 1224.4 m2/g, as well as the porous 
nature of both CA@ZIF-8 and ZIF-8.  
Prospects for future research include quanƟfying the eﬀect on the thermostability of CA@ZIF-8 on the 
membrane using PDA entrapment and possibly redoing the experiment with a newly prepared substrate 
soluƟon. And possibly redo the experiments for thermal stability of CA@membrane to determine the eﬀect 
of membrane immobilizaƟon more accurately. QuanƟfy the water permeability of the CA@membrane and 
CA@ZIF-8 on a membrane to assess the impact of immobilized CA@ZIF-8.
