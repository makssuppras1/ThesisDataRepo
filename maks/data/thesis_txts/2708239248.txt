Abstract 
The round goby (Neogobius melanostomus) is a fish native to the Ponto-Caspian region. It is highly invasive 
and is currently spreading through North Europe and North America. Individual behavioural variation appears 
to affect their spread and the impact on the invaded ecosystems. Among-individual differences in animal 
behaviour (i.e., animal personality) is an important factor that can influence the survival and fitness of an 
individual, its interaction with the environment, and therefore its ecological niche. Behavioural and ecological 
variation among-individuals can also influence the spread and impacts of biological invasions. In invasive fish, 
an individual’s personality traits can influence their dispersal behaviour where personality-biased dispersal 
can drive the spread of invasive species, which may lead to variation in the behavioural composition of 
populations along an invasion front. Trait variation across invasion fronts deserves attention because it can 
influence how those populations impact on the environments they invade, and the appropriate mitigation 
measures against invasive species impacts. This project measured personality trait variation in a novel 
environment and predator response context (i.e., boldness), and directly analysed the relationship between 
personality traits and physiological stress responses in the round goby (e.g., blood cortisol, lactate) to 
determine the role of behaviour and physiological variation in their invasion. The sampled fish were from 
two populations: the first close to the invasion front (<30km) and the second more well-established. The two 
populations have comparable environmental conditions but differ in their time since invasion by 
approximately 5 years. Results showed unexpected population variation in personality, where the more 
recently established population was less bold in a predator response context, and surprisingly found that 
personality variation appear to show no relationship to physiological parameters. Overall, our results 
highlight that personality variation appears to be an important factor in the continuing spread of round goby 
populations in the Baltic Sea, but that the mechanisms underpinning personality trait variation in these 
populations remain unresolved.

1. Introduction 
1.1. Biological invasions: definitions, stages, and drivers 
Biological invasions are complex processes that can deeply impact invaded ecosystems. They consist of a 
species successfully spreading and conquering novel areas due to the absence of their natural obstacles, 
often becoming the dominant population (Valéry et al. 2008). Invasive alien species (IAS) are associated with 
at least 109 of the 156 bird extinctions that occurred in the last 500 years  (BirdLife International, 2017) and 
are the fourth and the third most important threat to respectively endangered amphibians and mammals 
(Vié et al. 2009; Kornis et al. 2012). This is often the result of exploitation and interference competition 
between the invasive species and the native ones (Callaway and Ridenour 2004). These invasions can also 
result in an alteration of the abiotic component of the ecosystems e.g. the turbidity of water in the aquatic 
systems (Carlsson et. al, 2004) and the modification of stream morphology and hydrology (Naiman et. al, 
1986). In coastal and estuarine environments, the impact is extensive. These areas are particularly exposed 
to introduction of invasive species due to their central role in many anthropogenic activities that result as 
vectors for the invasion as shipping, aquaculture and even aquarium trade (Williams and Grosholz 2008). 
These human-mediated invasions consist of four steps: the transport of individuals outside of their native 
range; introduction into the environment; establishment of a self-sustaining population in that new 
environment; and spread beyond the point of introduction. A species must overcome each of these stages 
before it is considered invasive (Blackburn et al. 2011). Dispersal is an important part of the spread phase of 
invasions, and many factors can influence dispersal decisions, including intraspecific competition, predation 
risk, kin interaction, inbreeding risk, mate choice, and habitat (Bowler and Benton 2005, Clobert et al. 2009). 
Behavioural variation among-individuals can also be linked to dispersal, where individuals’ personality traits 
can affect their decisions to stay or depart, and when and where to settle (Weis and Sol 2016). This dispersal 
tendency has often been linked to boldness, aggressiveness, activity, and sociability (Rehage and Sih 2004; 
Cote et al. 2010; Myles-Gonzalez et al. 2015; Hirsch et al. 2017), and these traits also can affect the post-
dispersal success (Weis and Sol 2016). Elliott and Cornell (2012) showed that the invasion rate can double if 
there are two distinct personality types within the invaders: a subpopulation of individuals that differ in both 
their growth rate and dispersal ability could achieve a higher invasion speed than would be possible with only 
one of the two phenotypes. In a study about western bluebirds (Sialia Mexicana), (Duckworth 2008) 
demonstrated that more aggressive individuals lead dispersion at the invasive front, and then following 
establishment they are substituted by less aggressive individuals because of their poor parental care. These 
differences in personality types, initially confined in the original population, are spread along the invasion 
succession (Thorlacius et al. 2015; Myles-Gonzalez et al. 2015), which is likely to influence how the invasive 
populations interact with the ecosystems they invade.

1.2. The invasive round goby 
 
The round goby (Neogobius melanostomus, Pallas, 1814) is a member of Gobiidae native to the Ponto-
Caspian region (Figure 1). It is a bottom-dwelling fish, often found around rocky habitats and other 
substrates in shallow water but it is also reported in open sandy areas and dense aquatic macrophyte 
meadows (Ray and Corkum 2001). It can live in fresh and brackish water up to 30ppt, even with low oxygen 
concentrations and poor water quality (Ellis and Macisaac 2009; Kornis et al. 2012). For these reasons the 
round goby is one of the most diffused invasive fish and it can be found in the Laurentian Great Lakes, the 
Baltic Sea, and many major European rivers (Kornis et. al, 2012).  
 
Figure 1: Round goby dwelling on the sea floor. Photo: Sune Riis Sørensen, DTU Aqua 
 
Most likely introduced from the ballast water of commercial ships, the first appearance of round gobies 
within the Baltic Sea region was in the Gulf of Gdansk in Poland in early 1990 (Sapota and Skóra 2005). During 
the first years, the population grew slowly but, from 1994, it started to spread around the Baltic Sea coastline. 
By the end of the decade, it became the dominant fish in shallow waters of the western part of the gulf 
(Sapota and Skóra 2005). Since then, this predatory fish has been spread west along the coasts of Poland, 
Germany, and now into Denmark. The first round goby in Denmark was observed near the island of Bornholm 
in 2008 (Figure 2). In 2009 the species was first observed in the inner Danish waters south of Zealand in

In the Baltic Sea, round gobies appear to eat the eggs of native species, including flounder (Platichthys flesus), 
eelpout (Zoarces viviparus), and native gobies (Karlson et al. 2007). They are well adapted to feeding on hard-
bodied prey items (Andraso et al. 2011) and are often found to prey on bivalve and gastropod populations in 
the invaded environments (Deurs et al. 2021; Pennuto et. al 2018; Kipp and Ricciardi 2012). Both bivalve and 
gastropod populations provide important functions in aquatic ecosystems (e.g. water filtration, providing 
shelter and habitat structure (Petersen et al. 2016; Koivisto and Westerbom 2010), algal grazing pressure 
(Pennuto et. al 2018; Kipp and Ricciardi 2012). Round gobies thus can impact both the abundance of specific 
species, as well as broader ecosystem processes in the environments that they invade. Therefore, it is 
necessary to understand the drivers of round goby invasions, trait variation in invasive round goby 
populations, and how this influences their interactions within invaded ecosystems. 
 
1.3. Individual behavioural variation: what is it, what causes it and why does it matter? 
For the past century, personality has been a human prerogative and is only with some early studies in 1970-
1980s that personality starts to be described also in animals (Levinson 1978; Buirski et al., 1978). At first,

there was little focus on the study of animal personality, caused by a low understanding of the link between 
behavioural traits and fitness, and a lack of terminology for this field of study (Réale et al. 2007). Populations 
were often treated as a whole, where conspecific individuals were ecologically equivalent. It was not 
considered the fact that individuals from the same species could differ in use of resources (Colwell and 
Futuyma 1971; Pielou 1972; Linton, Davies, and Wrona 1981). 
It is in the 1990s-2000s that personality obtained some significance within behavioural ecology (Sloan Wilson 
et al. 1994), where individual specialization can reflect in differences in physiological, behavioural and 
consequently ecological intrapopulation variation (Bolnick et al., 2002). However, two different definitions 
of personality were used among ecologists: the first is a broad-sense definition, where personality 
corresponds to the presence of consistent behavioural differences between individuals that involve any type 
of behaviour; the second definition focused on the same kind of differences but applied only in specific suites 
of behaviour, typically expressed in a novel or challenging context, and for this reason, is considered a 
narrow-sense definition (Réale et al. 2010). 
The definition given by (Wolf and Weissing 2012) is similar to these, as animal personality is defined as the 
differences in the behavioural tendencies that are consistent over time across contexts. In the present study 
I will use a statistical approach, the most recent one, where personality refers to consistent among-individual 
variation in behaviour (Sanchez-Tojar et al., 2021). Once the definition of animal personality is clear, an 
important new step is to understand the drivers that underpin personality differences in animals. 
The first one is the genetic variation between individuals, which when an individual’s genotype is expressed 
in different environments can produce different phenotypes (Pigliucci 2005). Physiological differences 
between the individuals, e.g. nutritional state (Moran et al. 2021) and metabolism (Careau, 2008) can affect 
the among-individual variation in behaviour too. Moreover, hormone levels (Niemelä and Dingemanse 2018) 
and neurotransmitters (Ferris and Delville 1994) can affect and be affected by personality traits. Physiological 
parameters are discussed in detail in the next paragraphs. 
 
 
1.4. Cortisol and lactate 
Stress in fish (and many other animals) triggers a hormonal response controlled by the endocrine system 
that results in the production of corticosteroids and catecholamines (Wendelaar Bonga 1997). For this 
reason, hormones like cortisol and corticosterone are frequently used to measure the stress response in 
vertebrates. Cortisol is one of the main end products of the brain-pituitary-interrenal axis (HPI), a set of 
influences and feedback interactions between the hypothalamus, the pituitary gland and the interrenal 
cells. This hormone is released in the blood stream to mobilise and convert the glycogen stored as energy

reserves to glucose (Reeder and Kramer 2005). This availability of energy facilitates the necessary 
responses to physical adaptation to the stressor. In Overli (2005) two different lines of rainbow trouts 
(Oncorhynchus mykiss), one high-responding and one low-responding in terms of cortisol production during 
stress, showed different behavioural responses. Another parameter used to measure stress response in 
vertebrate is lactate. Lactate is the conjugate base of lactic acid, a compound formed when the organism 
uses carbohydrates as an energy source during anaerobic activity. This activity may be triggered as a stress 
response (Wells and Pankhurst 1999), leading to an increase of lactic acid production and, thus, to a higher 
concentration of lactate in blood.  
 
1.5 Individual behavioural variation in the round goby 
As already said, round goby is a successful invasive species. Therefore invasions of this species are often 
studied to investigate large-scale invasion patterns (Thorlacius and Brodin 2018). This process has been 
studied from many points of view, with focus on the invasion drivers already described in this study: 
metabolism, size, condition, behaviour and physical parameters as salinity and temperature (Myles-Gonzalez 
et al. 2015; Behrens, van Deurs, and Christensen 2017; Brandner et al. 2018; Behrens et al. 2020; Christensen 
et al. 2020; Moran et al. 2021). Differences in these conditions were found along the invasion gradient, but 
not always and not with the same results. For example, larger body size has been found to be associated with 
the individuals closer to the invasive front in the Danube River (Brandner et al. 2013), but a following study 
showed that biological traits do not differ significantly along the invasion gradient in the Upper Danube River 
(Cerwenka et al. 2017). Fish at the invasion front in the Trent–Severn Waterway (Canada) have been found 
to be bolder in a novel environment context in Myles-Gonzalez et al. (2015). Differently, in another study, 
Behrens et al. (2020) found bolder fish tend to be smaller, due to the metabolic cost of their behaviour that 
results in a slower growth rate. This differs from the general idea the bigger gobies should be found at the 
invasive front. These contrasts may be explained as a lack of uniformity of how boldness is measured: as I 
said boldness is involved in many contexts (e.g., predator response, novelty response etc.) that may apply 
differently at different stages of their ontogeny. Moreover, boldness is often measured indirectly from 
aggression, which can result in a biased result depending on the experiment performed. Groen et al. (2012), 
for example, showed that in the Laurentian Great Lakes round gobies living at the invasive front, in a 
disturbance exposure context, were more aggressive but not significantly bolder. For these reasons is 
important to establish during an experiment the specific variable used to quantify boldness and the ecological 
relevance of the variable being measured.

It is now clear that personality can be driven by physiological, genetic, and environmental variation. All these 
factors affect behaviour in different contexts: in this study I will focus on boldness in both a novel

environment and predator response context. Boldness (and shyness) in fish affects an animal responses in a 
wide range of contexts: it can be the time to emerge from a shelter and explore a novel environment (Brown 
and Braithwaite 2004), the feeding behaviour and the refuge use (Klefoth et al. 2012). Usually bolder fish are 
more responsive to novel environment (Yoshida et al. 2005; Frost et al. 2007; Beausoleil et al. 2008) and 
quicker to recover following a simulated predator strike (van Oers et al. 2005). The common factor in these 
contexts is the willingness of the fish to take risks, to exposed themselves to danger such as predation. In this 
project, the risk consists of the presence of a predator, both visually (in analysis of behavioural variation) and 
by the detection of damaged conspecific chemical cues in the water (in analysis of physiological variation). 
The overarching goal is to measure differences in the boldness composition and physiological stress 
responses of round gobies across their invasion front in Denmark. This study focused on two populations, 
the first is a well-established population (Grønsund, first recorded 2011) and the second is a more recently 
established population (Søvang, first recorded 2016). To determine the level of behavioural variation within 
and between these populations, and the physiological factors influencing behavioural variation, I specifically 
tested the following research questions: 
I. 
Do round gobies within these two populations show among-individual differences in boldness in 
a predator response context? I predicted that I would find consistent individual differences in 
boldness traits both within and across populations, as consistent among-individual variation has 
previously been found in this species, for example in (Myles-Gonzalez et al. 2015).  
II. 
Is there a difference in boldness between the two populations? Which one is bolder? 
Some studies have showed that the populations at the invasive front are bolder (Myles-Gonzalez et 
al. 2015; Thorlacius et al. 2015), but some others found a significant difference only in aggression 
(Groen et al. 2012). I expected, studying the behaviour in the context of a novel environment and 
predator response, to find bolder fish in the population closer to the invasion front, following the 
results in Myles-Gonzalez (2015). 
III. 
Is there a difference between populations in the physiological responses to stress?  
I expect to find lower levels of stress responses in the fish from the invasive front, as I expect those 
fish to be bolder and more active (Myles-Gonzalez et al. 2015). 
IV. 
Is there a relationship between physiological stress response and behavioural stress response?  
Physiology directly affects the activity of the fish and if hypothesis I is confirmed, I expect to find a 
relationship between physiological and behavioural stress responses on an individual level.  
V. 
Do round gobies show condition-dependent (body size, physical condition/condition factor) 
differences in boldness/predator response?  
I expect to confirm the result of a previous study, Behrens et al. (2020), which showed a significant 
difference between bold and shy fish for both weight and length: bold fish resulted to be lighter and 
shorter than the shy ones. Furthermore, Moran et al. (2021) demonstrated that fish in poor

2. Materials and Methods  
2.1. Fish and holding conditions  
The experiments focused on two populations of round goby at different points along their Baltic Sea invasion 
front (see Figure 2, Introduction). The first is a well-established population in Grønsund (54.90371 N, 
12.10367 E, first recorded approx. 2011), while the second population from Søvang is closer to the species’ 
invasion front (55.57146 N, 12.62579 E, first recorded approx. 2016, and within 30 km of the northern edge 
of their range expansion). Both sites are shallow brackish coastal areas, where round gobies are typically 
found in high abundances during spring and summer periods (Azour et al. 2015). 
Fish were collected in May 2021 from both sites (by NPM, for use in a previous experiment). Fish were caught 
using a combination of passive sampling methods set overnight (i.e.,  double funnel fyke nets, baited box and 
cylinder traps) to reduce the potential for behavioural trait-biased sampling (Biro and Dingemanse 2009; 
Michelangeli, Wong, and Chapple 2016). Salinity and temperature measured within the sampling areas were 
very similar when sampling occurred (Søvang 16/5/21: 8.75ppt, 14.1 °C; Grønsund 18/5/21: 8.27ppt, 13.2 
°C). Fish were transported to DTU Aqua, Lyngby, Denmark, and acclimated to laboratory conditions for 48 
hours before being individually tagged with passive integrated transponder (PIT) tags (12 × 2 mm, 0.1 g, 
Oregon RFID Inc.), using previously published methods that have minimal effects of fish health and condition 
(Jørgensen et al. 2017). 
Before the start of this experiment, fish were held in two large mixed sex round tanks (3000 l) connected 
with a recirculating water system; fish were held with other round gobies from two different populations 
along the invasion gradient. Fifteen days before the behavioural experiment, fish from the chosen 
populations were randomly allocated to three round tanks (800 l) in groups of 24 per tank, 12 from each 
population (n = 36 total per population). Only males were included because the number of females in the 
sampled fish was low: this is potentially because the sex ratio in the invasive front of round gobies is male 
biased (Corkum et al. 2004) and because the males are usually more active than females  (Marentette et al. 
2011), facilitating their capture with passive traps. Furthermore, the survivorship of females during the 
holding in the laboratory was much lower than males and showed more frequently scale loss and wounds on 
their flanks. The salinity in the holding tanks was 10.6 ± 0.2 ppt and the temperature was constant at 10.5 ± 
0.1°C with a daily cycle of 12 hours of light and graduated increases/decreases in light intensity at dawn and 
dusk to simulate a natural light regime. During the whole project fish were fed every two days with dry 
pelleted feed (3mm Ivory Ex composite pellets, Aller Aqua, Denmark). Before the behavioural experiment the 
feeding was kept constant, but before the physiological experiment feeding was interrupted for 48 hours 
before the 16 hours of isolation, to decrease the production of feces while being held in a non-recirculating 
water system.

2.2 Experiment A: Behavioural responses to stress 
After 4 weeks in the holding tanks, twenty-four fish were assayed each day and each fish was assayed twice, 
with 7 days between the repetitions. The experiment started at 9:00 in the morning and every 30 minutes 
two fish were moved from the holding tank to individual black tanks (24 x 34 x 15.5 cm) covered with brown 
cardboard and aired with air stones for 2 h to standardize pre-trial handling and stress levels: cortisol levels 
in round gobies, have been found to peak 20 minutes after acute stress and return to normal levels after one 
hour in male individuals when placed in a familiar environment (Marentette, Tong, and Balshine 2013). 
Because the isolation tanks were not a familiar environment, their function was not to let the cortisol in blood 
to decrease to baseline levels, but to standardize the stress level for all the fish, since the continued net 
swoops in the holding tanks would have a larger effect on the last fish sampled. After isolation, the fish were 
moved to the behavioural arenas using the individual tanks still covered and opened only once they are 
immersed in the arena water. 
 
The behavioural arenas consisted of two white tanks (36 x 56 x 21 
cm), A and B, separated by white polystyrene to prevent the fish 
from jumping out of the tanks, and to insulate the arenas from both 
external sound and visual inputs (Figure 3). The experiment was 
performed at the same conditions of light, temperature, and salinity 
of the holding tanks. One camera (Logitech Brio HDR webcam, 
Logitech) connected to a laptop using the software Logitech Capture 
(Logitech.com) recorded the tanks from 1 m above. A simulated bird 
strike was performed by using glass pipes in which fishing lines run 
with a bolt (5.5 cm, 23 g) at the end. The bolt was positioned to 
strike in the middle of the arena, at least within 2 body lengths from 
the fish. The fish were placed in the arenas as explained above, 
making sure that they were never out of the water, and the camera 
started to record when they entered the arena. After 5 minutes of 
acclimatization (Thorlacius and Brodin 2017) the bolts were 
released to hit the water surface and penetrate 5 cm into the water 
column, then were immediately retracted. Recording continued for 5 minutes after the strike before the trial 
was terminated. After every run the arenas were rinsed with DI water and the water was entirely replaced, 
to prevent contamination from the previous fish by conspecific chemical cues that could alter the stress level 
during the experiment (Barcellos et al. 2011).

First freezing was affected by the movement of the structure during the strike on which the camera was held: 
for this reason, I checked manually the videos and the response for all the fish. This resulted in a threshold 
that I used to distinguish fish who froze as a response to the strike and the ones that did not. This threshold

2.3 Experiment B: Physiological responses to stress 
Ten days after the Experiment A last trial, 12 fish were moved each 
day from the holding tank to individual black (24 x 34 x 15.5 cm) tanks 
for 16 hours to standardize stress levels before the blood sampling 
(Figure 4). The isolation time was longer than the one acted for the 
behavioural experiment because this time it was important to let the 
cortisol concentration in the blood decrease to baseline levels. Three 
treatment groups were sampled: 1) Control, sampled after 16 hours 
without the addition of any stressor; 2) Sham, exposed to 10 ml of 
unscented water; 3) Cue, exposed to 2,5 ml of water with chemical 
alarm cue mixed with additional 7,5 ml of unscented water. The 
chemical cues of injured fish were obtained via the method 
described in Smith (1989), reducing the number of cuts on the fish 
flanks but doing them on both sides. One non-experimental fish from 
laboratory stock was euthanized with an overdose of MS222 and

2.5 Statistical analysis 
 
All the analysis was performed in R (4.1.2) in the development environment RStudio (2021.9.1.372). 
 
 
2.5.1 
Experiment A: Behavioural responses to stress 
 
Before the analysis, each variable was inspected and tested for normality (i.e., via qq-plots and Shapiro-Wilk 
tests, Shapiro, 1965) and transformed to get as close as possible to normality where possible. Intraclass 
correlation coefficients were calculated to test the repeatability of the behavioural variables (package “rptR”, 
v0.9.21). The reference distributions were Gaussian for the variables with a distribution close to a normal 
one (i.e., square root transformed pre-strike activity, post-strike activity and difference in activity), Poisson 
was used for Pre- and Post-strike edge use, while first freezing, and latency to resume movement were 
heavily bimodal and were therefore converted to binomial variables (See table X, Results section). 
Intraclass correlation coefficients (ICC) were used to test hypothesis I, i.e., the among-individual differences 
in boldness in a predator response context, using the fish ID as the only fixed effect. The obtained ICC, or 
repeatability coefficients, describe the proportion of between-individual variance relative to the total 
phenotypic variance (Nakagawa and Schielzeth 2010). Moreover, adjusted repeatability was calculated 
adding Population as a fixed effect to control the effect of the two populations on the differences in

behaviour. Adding Population as a fixed effect removed its variance from the proportion, resulting in the 
repeatability of the behavioural responses as if only one population was tested. 
 
Fish 485 and 9765 presented wounds during Experiments B, and for this reason they were removed from the 
analysis. During the sampling in the collection sites, no fish was injured, so the wounds could be a result of 
aggression from other fish in the holding tanks. Moreover, wounds could alter the physical condition of the 
individual and consequently, the behavioural responses. Fish 9924 presented cataracts on both eyes, so they 
were removed from the analysis too. It has been demonstrated that round gobies that present eye-flukes 
have reduced response to aerial attacks due to the cataracts they induce (Flink et al. 2017), therefore data 
from this individual was removed from all behavioural analysis.  
 
Linear mixed-effect models were used to estimate the effect of both fixed and random factors on behavioural 
variables. The extraction of the p-values was performed using the function  lmer() function (package “lme4”, 
v1.1-27.1, Bates et al. 2015). Behavioral variables included here were those that showed significant non-zero 
repeatability, and therefore represent measures of among-individual behavioural (personality) variation in 
these experimental populations (see Table 1).  
The random effects considered were 1) holding tank, to consider the possible effect of sharing the holding 
tank with different compositions of fish in terms of aggressivity and size, 2) experiment day, to take into 
account possible variations based on the weather, 3) trial, to distinguish between the first week of 
experiment and the second, 4) trial arena, to consider possible different conditions of light and visive inputs) 
and 5) fish ID, to take into account differences in individual behaviour. The fixed effects considered were 1) 
total length and 2) body condition, to test hypothesis V, effect of condition on boldness/predator response, 
and 3) population, to test hypothesis II, difference in boldness between populations. Continuous fixed effect 
factors (i.e., total length and condition) were scaled to improve the interpretability of regression coefficients 
within models (Schielzeth 2010). 
 
Generalized linear mixed effect models were used for variables with distributions too far from normality (i.e., 
the ones compared with Poisson distribution during the repeatability tests): the random and fixed effects 
considered were the same as for the not generalized models. Performance tests were used to calculate the 
goodness-of-fit of the models (Nakagawa and Schielzeth 2013).

4. Discussion 
In relation to hypothesis I, I expected to find behavioural differences among individuals in boldness. 
Specifically, I expected to find the fish closer to the invasion front to be more active and further from the 
edge. Repeatability tests showed the effect of among-individual variation in both activity and edge use 
variables. The pre-strike context was a novel environment, where fish had no direct indication of the 
presence of a predator. The post-strike context was still a novel environment, but here with a simulated bird-
strike acting as predator presence. In both these contexts among individual variation seemed to affect the 
behaviour, thus confirming the hypothesis. The result are consistent with what Bell et al. (2009) showed in a 
meta-analysis regarding the repeatability of behaviour across many taxa where roughly 35% of the variation 
in behaviour between individuals could be attributed to among-individual differences.   
Nevertheless, in a study about the relation between personality and metabolism (and also size) in round 
gobies, latency to resume movement was found to be repeatable (Behrens et al. 2020). Interestingly, the 
experimental design was the same of Experiment A, but the results are different. 
In relation to hypothesis II, I predicted that the individuals from the population at the invasion front were 
bolder than individuals from the more established population. I found differences between the populations 
in boldness only in their activity after the predator cue, while no population effect was demonstrated in their 
pre-strike activity, or their edge use variables both before and after the strike. Fish from the population of 
Søvang (closer to the invasion front) appeared to be significantly less active, so less bold, than the fish from 
Grønsund (more established population) after a simulated strike, suggesting that the population closer to 
the invasion front was less bold when exposed to an acute predator stimulus. This is in contrast with the 
hypothesis formulated before the experiment, were I predicted higher boldness in the population closer to 
the invasion front. Boldness can increase the dispersal and the successful outcome of competition between 
invasive and native species (Damas-Moreira et al. 2019). In Thorlacius and Brodin (2018), a positive 
correlation was found between activity and boldness in round gobies. In that study, the experimental design 
was the same described in the present study, where a fake bird attack was used to stress the fish. For this 
reason, in the studied contexts here, more active fish might be considered bolder, but it is still in contrast to 
my original predication that bolder fish were found further from the invasion front. Other studies on round 
gobies and other fish species have demonstrated the presence of bolder fish closer to the invasion front 
whereas others again did not find any significant difference in boldness between old and new populations of 
round gobies (Groen et al. 2012). Notably, both the abovementioned studies about round gobies had in 
common the low age of the newly established population, between two (Myles-Gonzalez et al. 2015) and 
three years (Groen et al. 2012). In the present study, the age of the population considered to be at the 
invasion front (first record 2016) was five years. This could be a reason why my results differ from the 
previous ones. In old populations, the traits linked to dispersal like boldness may be lost over time. This may 
==PAGE:24==
Andrea Galli 
 
DTU Aqua, Master Thesis, 2022 
 
24 
 
be related to differences in density between populations, where it has been suggested that lower densities 
lead to selection of traits that increase reproductive rate rather than dispersal tendency (Duckworth 2008). 
The type of behavioural variables being considered may also be a factor leading to my contrasting results, for 
example only Groen et al. (2012) measured boldness as activity after a stressful event, while Myles-Gonzalez 
et al. (2015) measured it as the latency to emerge from an acclimation shelter. The differences in the variables 
used to describe boldness made it difficult to compare between these two studies and mine. However, it is 
interesting to note that in the present study the younger population sampled was close the invasion front, in 
a range of 30 km. The average rate of round gobies spread along the Danish coastline between 2009 and 
2012 was 30 km yr-1 (Azour et al. 2015). Even considering the slower rate calculated around Bornholm Island 
(10 km yr-1), it seems that the invasion is now slowing down. Since the physical parameters in Søvang were 
very similar to the ones in Grønsund, on reason of this slowing down could be a different predation pressure 
experienced by the fish in the Søvang. Anecdotally, cod are more common in the northern part of the study 
area near the Øresund. Cod prey on gobies (Almqvist et al. 2010; Behrens et al. 2019) and this higher predator 
pressure could influence the behaviour of round gobies, changing the ratio of bold individuals in that area. 
In relation to hypothesis III and IV, I predicted to find population differences in physiological stress responses 
(lactate and cortisol), and that these responses would be correlated with personality trait variables. Contrary 
to expectations, there was no difference between the populations in their physiological responses to a 
predator cue. The experiment design did cause a stress response in the fish, as cortisol concentration in blood 
was significantly higher in fish exposed to the alarm cue as compared to the control fish, demonstrating that 
that this experimental design was appropriate for this species. However, lactate did not differ between the 
treatment groups. As described earlier, lactate is one end-product of cellular anaerobic metabolism. No 
significant changes in lactate level in blood suggests that the fish did not response to stress by undertaking 
anaerobically fuelled activity. The reasons for this could be due to the conditions under which the experiment 
was performed: the fish were in small tanks, resulting in a low possibility to respond to the stressor by 
swimming. Furthermore, the typical response to predator of this species is to freeze, sometimes after a quick 
escape. It is interesting that the Søvang population (closer to the invasion front) did not reveal any difference 
in physiological response compared to the other population. As said above, the behaviour did differ between 
the populations, even if not in the expected way. This could mean that cortisol level changes do not 
necessarily translate into a different behavioural response. In a study about behaviour in a predator response 
context, Lawrence et al. (2018) studied the difference between juvenile pumpkinseed sunfish (Lepomis 
gibbosus, Linnaeus 1758) treated with cortisol. Specifically, some of the fish were treated with surgical 
insertion of cocoa butter pellet containing cortisol into the intraperitoneal cavity, to obtain a stable release 
of cortisol over a period of time and study its impact on antipredator behaviour. This studied demonstrated 
that different levels of cortisol had no influence on antipredator behaviour. The design of the behavioural 
experiment by Lawrence et al. (2018) was similar to the present Experiment A, with a simulated aerial strike 
==PAGE:25==
Andrea Galli 
 
DTU Aqua, Master Thesis, 2022 
 
25 
 
to mimic a predator attack. Thus, although with a different species and with juvenile fish (the size of the fish 
in the present study, all above 10 cm of total length, might suggest that only adult individuals were sampled), 
the result appears to be consistent with what was found in the present study. On the contrary, in Barreto et 
al. (2014) frillfin goby (Bathygobius soporator), a closer species to the round goby, were treated with 
intraperitoneal cortisol implants that decreased the fish activity in response to the presence of a predator. 
This was simulated by conspecific chemical cues in the water, the same design as used in the present 
Experiment B. For frillfin goby, cortisol had effect on the behavioural responses to a predator attack, whereas 
in the present study, cortisol level increased in response to stress, but the effect on the behavioural responses 
was too low to be detected or absent. However, it is important to highlight that the stressor in the present 
two Experiments A and B were different: in Experiment A the predator presence was simulated by a visual 
input, while in Experiment B the stressor consisted of addition of a chemical cue. This could have influenced 
the difference between the behavioural and the physiological responses resulting from the two experiments. 
From the correlation tests, none of the physiological variables studied seemed to be correlated to the 
behavioural ones. This is in contrast with the result from meta-analysis performed by Niemelä and 
Dingemanse (2018). In their study, Niemelä and Dingemanse demonstrated that physiological variables such 
as metabolic rate, hormone levels, body weight and/or size was correlated to aggression, boldness, 
exploration, and activity in animals, with |r| = 0.216 and CI = [0.168, 0.278]. Particularly, hormone levels 
resulted in a correlation value of |r| = 0.141 with CI = [0.042, 0.316]. Notably, these are the overarching 
correlation values that are the result of a meta-analysis of many studies, and outcomes of single studies may 
deviate from this. For example, Thomson et al. (2011) found no correlation between cortisol responses and 
behavioural responses in two populations of rainbow trouts (Oncorhynchus mykiss), although the two 
populations manifested different cortisol levels in the blood in response to stress. Thomson’s study 
supported the results from the present study. However, the species considered were different and the 
cortisol levels in blood were manipulated by anthropogenic processes as aimed for breeding. Whereas in the 
present study, the two populations were sampled to test differences in natural physiological responses to 
stress. 
In relation to hypothesis V, I predicted an effect of condition on behavioural responses to stress, but no effect 
of body condition was found. This result is in contrast with Moran et al. (2021), who showed a negative effect 
of nutritional condition on boldness, where fish in poor feeding treatments tended to be bolder than the 
ones well fed. This suggest that fish that are more in need of food (i.e., the low-condition ones) are more 
willing to take a risk to get food. However, it is important to highlight that in Moran et al. (2021) the 
differences in condition between fish were induced by the differences in feeding regime. In the present study, 
no manipulation on of the feeding regime was performed, the two populations did not show difference in 
condition, and variation within populations was low. This could be because the experimental fish had been 
in the holding facility for approximately five months before the experiment, in an environment with high food 
==PAGE:26==
Andrea Galli 
 
DTU Aqua, Master Thesis, 2022 
 
26 
 
availability, no predators and stable physical parameters such as salinity and temperature. This could have 
affected the general condition of the fish, that in nature is likely to be more variable. Total length, instead, 
showed a very strong negative effect on the activity in a novel environment and a minor non-significant 
negative effect on the activity in a predator response context. After the strike, most of the large fish seemed 
to have problems to fit in the corners of the arenas (where the fish seemed to try to hide), potentially 
resulting in a trend of higher activity. Movement into refuge areas is a typical response of animals to a 
predator presence and moving into the corner areas of the arenas may reflect this behaviour given there 
were no sheltered areas in the arena. However, no significant effect was detected on edge use, both before 
and after the strike. The negative effect of size on behaviour contrasts with the results from the meta-analysis 
by Niemelä and Dingemanse's study (2018). As for hormone levels, Niemelä and Dingemanse found a 
correlation between body size and boldness traits with |r| = 0.132 and CI = [0.077, 0.201]. However, as said 
above, this study involved many different studies performed on different species, and in a species-specific 
case the results could be different. In fact in Behrens et al. (2020), a study about round gobies, the correlation 
between size and boldness resulted to be negative, meaning that shy fish are usually larger than the bolder 
ones. In Behrens et al. (2020), though, boldness was measured as the latency to resume movement after a 
simulated aerial attack. Even if the experimental design was the same as the one described in this study, the 
results are different. It is important to remember that in the present study, latency to resume movement 
was not included in the analysis because not repeatable.

5. Summary and Conclusion 
In summary, the present study found consistent among-individual behavioural variation in two populations 
of round goby from Danish coastal areas of the Baltic Sea. Behavioural differences between populations were 
found only in activity in a predator response context, with fish closer to the invasion front being less active 
and so less bold. Exposure to conspecific chemical cues resulted in an increase of cortisol concentration in 
blood, but with no differences in responses between the two population with different invasion histories. 
Contrary to expectations, no correlation between behavioural responses and physiological responses was 
detected, and likewise there was no effect of body condition on behavior. On the contrary, total length had 
a significant negative effect on activity when the fish experienced a novel environment. Finally, a negative 
trend was detected between total length and activity in a predator response context.  
This study further highlights the importance of among-individual behavioural variation to the invasion 
process of the round goby, and specifically identifies boldness in a predator response context as a key 
behavioural difference in two populations along the species’ Baltic Sea invasion front. Despite this, 
physiological and body condition variation were not identified as factors directly driving personality trait 
variation within or between these two populations. Therefore, the processes that underpin personality 
variation in the round goby, and the processes driving their ongoing invasions in northern Europe and beyond 
are valuable topics for future research.
