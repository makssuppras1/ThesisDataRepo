Abstract
This thesis is concerned with the development and testing of a microfabricated chip
that serves as a platform for experiments involving 2D materials such as graphene
inside a transmission electron microscope (TEM). In order to use graphene in
integrated circuit components such as the transistor, an energy band gap has to
be opened. This can be done by sculpting the graphene into a nanoribbon, for
instance by knock-on damage from the electron beam or by crystallographic etching
of graphene using catalytic metallic nanoparticles. The environmental TEM allows
us to monitor graphene with atomic precision under the inﬂuence of diﬀerent gases.
The TEM chip facilitates heating as well as four-point electrical measurements such
that the electrical properties of graphene can be correlated with the precise atomic
structure, such as information on the edge state (zigzag or armchair) and defects.
The heating from the TEM chip removes contaminants and activates the self-repair
of electron-beam-induced point defects in graphene.
The goal of this project was to fabricate and characterise the TEM chip. This
was done through numerical simulations using Comsol, fabrication of the chip in
the DTU Danchip cleanroom facility, and ﬁnally testing and characterising the chip
in a variety of experiments.
The resulting temperature distribution from the Comsol model was seen to
be quite uniform in a large area, and the highest temperature was achieved in the
center of the membrane as intended with the design. The temperature in absolute
terms was, however, quite oﬀcompared to experiments. Also the deformation proﬁle
was diﬀerent from observations in experiments. Things such as initial stress in the
materials and the diﬀusion of titanium into the platinum layer are not simulated,
and are believed to be part of the explanation to the deviations.
The fabrication of the TEM chip was ﬁnished and was successful. During the
process it was found that the potassium hydroxide (KOH) etched the top silicon
nitride layer, which was further problematic because of a leaking KOH holder. The
solution was found in a KOH resistant coating, which was discovered and bought
during this project.
The Ti/Pt heater was calibrated to ﬁnd the resistance versus temperature under
atmospheric conditions. The resistance was seen to increase permanently after being
exposed to temperatures exceeding 500 ◦C. The lack of a thermal anneal during the
fabrication as well as oxidation of Ti and interlayer diﬀusion of Ti and Pt atoms are
believed to be the cause of the increased resistance. Various stability tests of the
TEM chip was conducted to ﬁnd its limitations. Here the silicon nitride was found
to break down at 54 V. The heater was found to be stable at 70 mW for more than
20 hours. The membrane was observed to go into distinct buckling shapes depending
on the design of the TEM chip. The maximum applied power through the heater was
found to be 130 mW. Finally, a graphene trilayer was transferred to the TEM chip
using the wedging transfer method. Some contamination from was seen to be present
after the transfer process - this was eﬀectively removed by applying 2 V between two
electrodes.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Graphene has been the subject for extensive research ever since it was successfully extracted
from graphite in 2004 [25] due to its exceptional properties such as an extremely high
carrier mobility and thermal conductivity, as well as the existence of ballistic transport
at room temperature [22, 23]. Graphene is a 2D material consisting of a single layer
of carbon atoms arranged in a hexagonal lattice. The crystal lattice gives graphene a
remarkable band structure where the conduction and valence band meet in two points
known as Dirac points. Around these, the two energy bands form two circular cones; thus
the dispersion relation is linear at the Dirac points with zero-energy gap. This means that
charge carriers can be described as massless fermions in these regions; a fact which leads to
some extraordinary electronic properties in graphene [6]. However, the lack of a band gap
in graphene presents a challenge for its integration in the semiconductor industry, since the
operation of electronic components such as the transistor relies on the energy band gap.
Many ideas have been proposed to open a band gap in graphene such as the adsorption
of atomic hydrogen [7], patterning the graphene into nanoribbons to conﬁne the charge
carriers [36], creating a substrate-induced bandgap from hexagonal boron nitride (hBN)
[10], and making graphene antidot lattices [28]. All of the above were theoretical predictions
in the years 2004-2008. Since then, many experimentalists have demonstrated the opening
of a band gap in graphene.
In 2007, Han et al. demonstrated experimentally the ability to engineer the band
gap in graphene nanoribbons (GNR) by means of lithographic processes. The band gap
was found to scale inversely with the ribbon width; the highest gap being 200 meV for a
15 nm wide ribbon [12]. In 2010, Richard Balog et al. observed the opening of a gap in
hydrogenated graphene. In this study they created ”regular patterns of hydrogen-covered
regions, resulting in a conﬁnement potential for the carriers in the pristine graphene regions,
thus leading to a bandgap opening” [3]. In 2013, Hunt et al. showed experimentally that
by stacking graphene and hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) to create what is known as a
van der Waals heterostructure, the band structure could be engineered [14]. In pristine
graphene, the linear dispersion relation at the Dirac points arise from the fact that the
hexagonal lattice is composed of two interleaving triangular sub-lattices, which create the
two degenerate Dirac points [6]. By stacking another crystal on top of graphene, this
sublattice symmetry is broken, and the charge carriers are given a mass [10, 14].
Returning to graphene nanoribbons, numerous methods have been proposed to produce
them, such as ”plasma etching, e-beam lithography, scanning tunneling microscopy (STM)
lithography, and chemical stripping” [38]. The atomic structure of the edges (zig-zag
or armchair) in graphene nanoribbons have long been known to play a central role in
determining whether the nanoribbon exhibits quasi-metallic or semiconducting behavior

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
Figure 1.1: Illustration of in-situ transmission electron microscopy (TEM) sculping of
graphene by knock-on damage from the electron beam. Figure from [5].
[24, 16].
One of the ambitions is to construct atomically precise integrated circuits,
and graphene nanoribbons (GNRs) are promising candidates for components such as
interconnects or transistor channels. It is therefore of great interest to nanostructure
graphene and correlate the electronic properties while monitoring the process at the
atomic scale as it happens. The ability to accurately change the structure at the atomic
level to control the properties of the material, could pave the way for future graphene
nanoelectronics.
An environmental transmission electron microscope (ETEM) allows
exactly for this knowledge to be obtained: using such a microscope, graphene or other 2D
materials can be imaged with atomic resolution under the inﬂuence of diﬀerent gases while
nanostructuring the graphene. The electron beam in a TEM can be used to either image
or sculpt the graphene (see Fig. 1.1) depending on the energy of the beam [5, 30, 44]. The
energy required to knock out carbon atoms from the graphene is ∼80 keV [30]. In-situ
heating can be used to clean contaminated graphene as well as for active self-repair of
electron-beam induced defects created during the imaging process [44]. Another strategy
for sculpting graphene is to use metal nanoparticles such as Ni, Fe, Co, and Ag, which
have proven to etch graphene along crystallographic directions [4, 38]. These particles
act as a catalyst for the hydrogenation or oxidation of graphene at elevated temperatures
(600-850 K).
In order to perform similar experiments to the above mentioned, a proper heating
platform or TEM chip is needed to support the graphene. The chip has to feature both
heating and probes for electrical characterisation of the material under study, as well as a
window for the transmission of the electron beam. The aim of this project is to fabricate
and test such a MEMS-based platform for in-situ TEM experiments involving graphene.

plus various durability tests of the chip.
Ch 1: Introduction
The introduction will motivate the project and give a broad
introduction to the subject of the thesis.
Ch 2: Theory
The second chapter will contain some relevant theory for understanding
heat transfer in solids. This is relevant in order to understand the equations that Comsol
solves and how to set up the boundary conditions.
Ch 3: COMSOL simulation
The third chapter will go through the Comsol model
that simulates the membrane part of the TEM chip. The model solves coupled equations
from three energy domains (electric, thermal, and mechanical) and solves for the currents,
temperature distribution, stress, and deformation. It is of interest to obtain an accurate
model, since it allows for an optimisation of the design through geometry or material
changes without having to build the chip using expensive cleanroom fabrication techniques.
Ch 4: Design
This chapter will present the chosen mask design for the TEM chip. A
special focus is on the chips that facilitate heating. The masks are used in the ultraviolet
(UV) lithography during the fabrication process.
Ch 5: Fabrication
This chapter presents the fabrication process in detail. Apart
from going through the process ﬂow, the chapter includes many tests, discoveries, and
measurements. The fabrication of the TEM chip was done in the DTU Danchip cleanroom.
Ch 6: Characterisation
This chapter contains results from various tests and exper-
iments regarding the TEM chip. It begins with a number of durability tests including:
Stability at high current and voltage levels, stability at high temperatures, and a breakdown
test of the silicon nitride, which is used as an insulator in the design. Then the resistance
versus temperature is obtained in a calibration of the heater in atmospheric conditions.
Furthermore, the TEM chip is imaged while running a large power through the heater to
investigate failure mechanisms. Finally, a graphene trilayer is transferred to the chip using
the wedging transfer method, and subsequently characterised from an IV measurement.
Ch 7: Conclusion
The conclusion summarizes the results obtained in this project as
well as some suggestions on how to improve the characterisation of the TEM chip.

Chapter 2
Theory
This chapter will present some theory concerning heat transfer in solids. This will create
a solid background for understanding how the heat transfer is modelled in Comsol in
the next chapter. There are three mechanisms of heat transfer: conduction, convection,
and radiation. Heat conduction in solids are described by the heat equation, which states
that the total heat energy is conserved. This equation is derived from this observation.
The rate of which an object loses heat to the surroundings due to convection is governed
by Newton’s law of cooling. In this law, the heat transfer coeﬃcient is assumed to be
constant, however in reality it has to be determined for the speciﬁc system under study,
since it depends on both the temperature and the type of ﬂow in the surrounding ﬂuid.
Many studies report a signiﬁcantly higher heat transfer coeﬃcient for microstructures than
at the macroscale, however with a large degree of variation [1, 11, 13]. The chapter end
with a section on thermal radiation, which is described by the Stefan-Boltzmann law.
2.1
The heat equation
The equation that governs all heat transfer in a substance over time is named the heat
equation, which is a partial diﬀerential equation that on the fundamental level expresses
conservation of energy (essentially the ﬁrst law of thermodynamics). The equation can
take many forms depending on whether it is a ﬂuid or a solid under study and depending
on how many terms are included. Heat transfer in liquids is a signiﬁcantly more complex
problem than heat transfer in solids. The reason being that ﬂuids deform when subject
to shear forces, and because of viscosity, energy is dissipated in this process. In many
problems regarding ﬂuids, the ﬂuid is in motion which means that heat can be carried
away by advection.

2.2
Convective heat transfer
Convection is the transfer of heat by the movement of a ﬂuid (e.g. air or water). Convective
heat transfer includes a combination of heat diﬀusion and advection, the latter describing
transport by bulk ﬂuid ﬂow. One can distinguish between two types of convection: free
convection and forced convection. In free convection, the ﬂuid motion is caused by buoyancy
forces. These arise because if the temperature of a given mass of air increases over an
object, the volume must increase by the same factor (cf. the Ideal gas law). Thus, the
air becomes less dense and rises. Conversely, cold air drops and replaces the warmer air,
which creates convection currents that transport heat energy. In forced convection, a ﬂuid
is forced to ﬂow over the surface of an object by an external source or equivalently the
object is moved through a ﬂuid. In the following, when speaking of convection, we are
referring to natural or free convection. Convection is described through Newton’s law of

Chapter 3
COMSOL simulation
Comsol is a simulation software that solves systems of partial diﬀerential equations (PDE’s)
using the ﬁnite element method (FEM) for various physics and engineering applications.
There can be many reasons for simulating a device rather than testing it in the laboratory.
A few advantages in general are: (1) The chip or device can be tested an ’inﬁnite’ amount of
times in the computer, whereas in the real world it can fail and break, (2) Once the model
produces consistent results with experiments, the simulation allows for an optimisation of
the design (through geometry, materials, etc.), before spending time making new mask
drawings and fabricating the device in the cleanroom, both of which are time consuming
and expensive. Thus one gets an early prototype of the design. The downside to numerical
simulations is that it can be very diﬃcult and time-consuming to obtain realistic solutions,
which creates the necessity of verifying the model through experiments.
The simulation described in this chapter models the TEM chip, speciﬁcally a 4 pt heater
chip as seen in the optical image in Fig. 3.1a. The part that we are interested in is the
membrane section which contains the platinum heater embedded in a thin silicon nitride
membrane (400 nm). Through the simulation we seek answers to questions like: How
uniform is the temperature distribution? What is the temperature for a speciﬁc current
through the heater? What is the magnitude of the stress in the heater and membrane?
How does the deformation look like?
3.1
Multiphysics model
The 3D model couples physics from three energy domains: The electric, the thermal,
and the solid mechanics domain (see Fig. 3.2). This makes the model signiﬁcantly more
complex and time-consuming than e.g. only solving for the temperature distribution. The
study is chosen to be stationary, which means that enough time has passed that thermal
equilibrium as well as mechanical equilibrium is established and the solution does not
depend on time. In the real world, the micro heaters reaches a steady-state situation
very fast (maybe on the order of ms, depending on how large a voltage ’shock’ is applied).
Furthermore, given the small thermal mass of the heaters they are expected to be able
to change temperature at a rate of 1 ◦C/µs, however this is still subject to be determined
through an experiment.

3.1.3
Meshing
In Comsol, the mesh can be built from four diﬀerent 3D element types: tetrahedrals (tets),
bricks, prisms, and pyramids. The standard mesh in Comsol is made from tetrahedrals,
mainly because any 3D volume, regardless of shape or topology, can be meshed with this
kind of element [9]. However, if the geometry contains very thin structures in one direction,
a mesh composed of tetrahedrals will become very dense in regions of thin structures,
because the algorithm used to create a tet mesh will try to keep the aspect ratio of the
tetrahedrals close to unity [9]. This motivates building the mesh from prisms, especially
if the solution does not vary much in the thin direction. An example of the diﬀerence
between a mesh composed of tetrahedrals and one made from prisms is shown in Fig. 3.3.
These two meshes are made with exactly the same element size parameters - the diﬀerence
arise because the tets are forced to be smaller to keep their aspect ratio close to 1. While
the tetrahedral mesh consists of 163038 domain elements with 1318130 degrees of freedom,
the prism mesh with two swept layers consist of only 15840 domain elements with 407830
degrees of freedom. The solution time and memory requirements depend strongly on the
number of degrees of freedom (though the exact relation is more complicated). If one swept
layer can suﬃce, the number of domain elements is brought down to 7920 with 241980
degrees of freedom. In addition, the solution is not expected to vary much within the
heater itself: the current density is expected to be nearly constant except for corners, nor
is there expected any strong temperature gradients.
Mesh reﬁnement
Given the chosen mesh element type pictured in Fig. 3.3b, the next
thing to investigate is how large the size of the elements can be without introducing too
large numerical errors. To perform this mesh convergence analysis, all mesh parameters
are held constant except for the maximum element size, which is varied using a parametric
sweep. Two values are tracked during the analysis: the displacement and the stress - both
values are extracted from the same arbitrarily chosen domain point (x = 8.3 × 10−5 m,
y = 7.9 × 10−5 m, z = 4 × 10−7 m). The mesh is gradually reﬁned until the computer runs
out of memory. The relative error on the parameters is then calculated from their value
at the ﬁnest mesh. These errors are plotted against the degrees of freedom in the mesh
in Fig. 3.4. From the graph it is seen that the maximum error is 10 % for the coarsest
mesh - in this case the maximum element size is free in the sense that it is set larger than
the biggest dimension of the model. It is noted that all other mesh parameters are set to
extra ﬁne, e.g. the mesh algorithm is still allowed to make small elements (minimum size
7.95 × 10−7 m). At a maximum element size of 10 µm (229320 degrees of freedom) the
error on both values is approx. 3 −4 %, where they lie all the way up to a size of 4 µm. At
3 µm the error on both parameters is less than 0.5 %, but here the number of degrees of
freedom is also more than a million leading to a long simulation time. The ﬁnal mesh is
shown in Fig. 3.3b.

Discussion
The simulation is idealised in the sense that the geometry is perfect and
the material properties are the same everywhere (homogeneous), i.e. it does not take into
account defects, impurities, contamination, or other fabrication anomalies. In reality, the
materials have residual stresses after deposition due to a mismatch in the crystal lattices:
this causes the membrane to bend when released and the heater can also delaminate. A
misalignment in the UV lithography can also break the symmetry, which is preserved in
the Comsol model. As described earlier, many eﬀects come into play when heating the
Ti/Pt bilayer: recrystallization of the platinum (growth of the mean grain size), relaxation
of intrinsic stress, as well as diﬀusion of titanium into the platinum [32]. These things are
challenging to incorporate into the simulation.

Design
The MEMS platform (or the TEM chip) comes in a variety of designs depending on its
purpose. Common for all of them is that the design is made to be compatible with the
TEM holder (see Fig. 4.1a). This sets a restriction on the size of the chip as well as the
number of contacts, the latter being limited by the number of contacts on the TEM holder.
The present model (Aduro TEM holder from Protochips) has four contacts, which can be
spent in a couple of diﬀerent ways: (1) All four for electrical measurements which allows
for a 4 pt measurement, (2) Two for heating and two for electrical measurements, or (3)
all four contacts for heating. One of the designs includes a gate, which use two contacts.
Some of the designs also feature four contacts on each end, allowing the chip to be used
from both sides, however they have not been used in this project.
While the MEMS platform is designed with in-situ TEM research in mind, the chip
is compatible with multiple experimental setups apart from a TEM. Examples include:
Raman systems, electrical probe stations, cryostats, AFM, SEM, etc. Basically anywhere
the chip can ﬁt. An example is seen in Fig. 4.1b, where it is placed on a heating element
with four probes connected inside the Linkam stage - a setup which is described later.
The TEM chip features either a square membrane of dimensions 61 µm × 61 µm or a
rectangular membrane sized 530 µm × 130 µm. There is a number of options for the hole
in the membrane that provides a window in-situ TEM: (a) A 3 × 3 array of small holes,
(b) A larger hole of diameter 15 µm, or (c) a 4 µm wide vertical slot. The hole designs can
be seen in Fig. 4.6. Table 4.1 summarizes the dimensions and features of the TEM chip.

Chapter 5
Fabrication
The fabrication of the TEM chip consists of ﬁve mask levels and is outlined in brief in
Fig. 5.1. The microfabrication processes used are well-known within the industry and
includes: Chemical vapor deposition (CVD), e-beam evaporation, sputtering, reactive-ion
etching (RIE), wet etching, and photolithography. Since the design requires processing
on both sides of the wafer, double side polished silicon wafers are used. In principle, the
ﬁrst step is the deposition of low-stress Si-rich silicon nitride (Si3N4) through low-pressure
chemical vapor deposition (LPCVD). However, in this project silicon wafers were bought
with approximately 300 nm LPCVD nitride from Chalmers University of Technology.
With these wafers as a starting point, the ﬁrst step is the deﬁnition of the large platinum
electrodes and heaters. These are then encapsulated in RF sputtered nitride (SiNx). Then,
contacts are opened by RIE. The fourth step is the deﬁnition of the top electrodes, which
are used for electrical measurements in the ﬁnal chip. Afterwards, SiN membranes are
deﬁned by etching through the entire silicon substrate by a KOH etch. Finally, as the chip
will be used within a TEM, holes are etched in the membrane by a dry etch. Lastly, the
microchips are diced by a saw.
Since the process consists of ﬁve lithography steps, many process steps in the fabrication
are similar. In order not to create too many repeats, two standard processes are described
ﬁrst. The ﬁrst one is a lift-oﬀprocess, which is used two times: For the buried metal layer
and for the top electrodes. The other process is the deﬁnition of a positive resist mask,
which is used three times in dry etchings of silicon nitride (contact openings, backside
KOH windows, and membrane holes). The same dry etching recipe is used in all three
etchings of SiN.

5.1.1 Spin coating
First, the adhesion promoter hexamethyldisilazane (HMDS) is
applied from the gas phase while the wafer is heated (this is done on a HMDS vapor prime

This particular resist has a negative proﬁle, which makes it well-suited for lift-oﬀprocesses.
The nLOF 2020 is sensitive to the i-line (365 nm), but not sensitive to the g- or h-line.
After spin coating, the resist is soft baked for 60 s at 110 ◦C on a hotplate. This is done to
evaporate the solvent from the resist in order to solidify the resist. The spin coating was
carried out with the recipe named ”T2 nLOF 2020 2um no HMDS” on an SVG 88 Track

5.5
Top electrodes
After the large electrodes are deﬁned and encapsulated in silicon nitride and contacts are
opened through the nitride, the top electrodes used for electrical measurements are ready
to be deﬁned. In this design, both the buried metal layer and the top electrodes are made
of platinum. The method for deﬁning the top electrodes is very similar to that of deﬁning
the buried metal layer. As described previously, it consists of a lithography step followed
by metallisation and a lift-oﬀ. This is described in section 5.1. An example of two diﬀerent
kinds of top electrodes are shown in Fig. 5.9. For the top electrodes, 5 nm chromium is
used as an adhesion layer. Three diﬀerent options for the top electrodes were followed: 95
nm Pt, 195 nm Pt, and no top electrodes. The last option enables us to deﬁne the top
electrodes through a stencil. That has some possible advantages: (1) It provides for a fast
way to change the design, since one can easily create a new stencil using focused ion-beam
(FIB) rather than order a new photo mask, (2) it allows one to place the electrodes on top
of the material to be investigated (e.g. graphene), which might not create as large a strain
on the material as if it is bridged across the electrodes.

KOH etch
The etch rate of KOH when etching <100> Si depends on the concentration of the KOH
and the temperature. The maximum etch rate is obtained at approx. 30 −35 weight %
KOH. In our case, a 33 weight % KOH solution is mixed from pellets by taking 1500 g KOH
and adding 3 L of water - the process is exothermic which means heat that is generated
in the reaction. The solution is then placed on a hot plate and kept at 80 ◦C. The etch
rate can be determined from a Dektak measurement after e.g. 2 hours - from experience
the above method results in etch rates of 66.4-70.5 µm/hr, corresponding to an etch time
between 4 hrs 58 min and 5 hrs 16 min. One can also watch for bubbles in the solution, as
these disappear when there is no more silicon to be etched.

5.7
Membrane holes
The last major step in the fabrication process is etching holes in the membranes. A number
of ﬁndings regarding the processing of the holes were made during the course of the project.
These are summed up in the following:
1. If the holes are made after the release of membranes, the spin-coating of the resist
mask for the lithography of holes has to be made on a vacuum-free spinner. Otherwise
the membranes will break and the vacuum hold on the wafer is lost. The problem is
solved by using an edge handling chuck on the SSE spinner, which applies a vacuum
at the edge instead of in the center.
2. Another option, which was later abandoned, is to crystal bond (CB) the wafer to
a dummy wafer and then spin-coat as usual on a vacuum chuck. Although crystal
bond is soluble in both acetone and water it turned out to be very diﬃcult to remove
from the membranes (see Fig. 5.13b).
3. When the membranes are released quite a lot of tensile residual stress is contained
within them, causing the wafer to bow. This causes the alignment of the holes to be
quite diﬃcult. It is deﬁnitely possible with the risk that not all parts of the wafer
are in focus and alignment with the mask. This problem is avoided if the holes are
made before the membranes (Fig. 5.13a), which can be achieved using the protective
coating.
It should be noted that in the original process ﬂow (and therefore also in the wafer that
made it out of the cleanroom) the membrane holes had to be made in the end, since
the protective coating was not acquired yet. The story in chronological order is this: (1)
vacuum-free spin-coating was possible on the KS spinner, which however was taken out of
use during the processing of the TEM chips, which is why another solution was needed.
(2) Then, crystal-bonding to a dummy wafer was suggested and proved to cause problems
with the removal (Fig. 5.13b). (3) It was then found that an edge-handling chuck for the
SSE spinner was available. (4) The protective coating was found which allows the holes to
be made before the KOH etch.
In the following, it is described how to make the membrane holes. The process is very
similar whether the step is performed before or after the KOH etch. From experience,
however, the lithography turns out better if the holes are made before, because of two
reasons: (1) It allows the use of the Spin-Track, which uses a newer and better resist than
the SSE Spinner (which has had problems with particles in the resist), and (2) no problems
with out-of focus and alignment in the UV exposure.

Characterisation
Since the MEMS heater chip is to be used as a platform for experiments involving 2D
materials etc. it is important that it is working reliably such that the chip: (a) does not
fail during experiments and (b) does not inﬂuence the measurement of the material under
investigation. Therefore, it is tested and characterised thoroughly in order to determine its
performance and limitations. These tests include: I-V characteristics, stability over time
with a large power applied (e.g. 70-100 mW), stability over time at temperatures exceeding
575 ◦C, breakdown tests of the silicon nitride, and mechanical limitations (does the heater
platform fail due to a rupture of the membrane or due to the heater burning over?). To
answer the latter, the chip is observed and imaged under a microscope while running a
large current through the heater. First, the experimental setup will be described.

runaway (an instability), since the dissipated power heats up the resistor causing the
resistance to increase - in turn the current source is forced to apply a larger voltage to keep

Fig. 6.3a this does not have to be the case. Below a certain threshold power level, there is
no thermal runaway and the resistance of the heater is on the contrary dropping over time,
probably due to annealing of grain boundaries in the platinum. It can also be that it is the
contact resistance itself that is decreasing, since this is included in a 2 pt measurement.

6.4
Breakdown of SiN
As earlier described, the MEMS platform comes in multiple variations: 4 pt heating, 2 pt
heating / 2 pt contact, 4 contacts, and 4 contacts plus a gate. In two of the designs there
are two electrode layers separated by the insulator silicon nitride (see Fig. 6.5b). When
using two contacts for heating (buried layer) and another pair of contacts for electrical
measurements (top layer), they need to be separated by an insulator such that they are
not shorted and also because in general there will be a potential diﬀerence between the
two sets of contacts. No insulator is perfect: at a great enough voltage the material breaks
down and a current is allowed to ﬂow. In some cases a small current is leaking through
the insulator before the actual breakdown (see Fig. 6.6c). It is of interest to determine
the breakdown voltage in order to know the limitations of the TEM chip. While the 2
pt heating / 2 pt contact chip is not designed to have an electric ﬁeld between the top
electrodes and the buried layer on purpose, the gate chip is - the silicon nitride should
therefore be able to withstand at least 20 V, which can be encountered in gate sweeps of
graphene. Also, it is important not to have leakage currents from the heater to the top
electrodes, since that can inﬂuence the measurement of the material under study.
Thus using a gate chip, a voltage was swept from 0 to 200 V by applying a positive
potential to one of the four top electrodes relative to the gate, which was grounded. The
images and measurements shown in Fig. 6.6 are from the same experiment and were
recorded simultaneously. In fact, two breakdowns were observed: the ﬁrst one at 54 V and
the second one at 156 V. The process can be outlined as follows:
1. At an applied voltage greater than 25 V, a small leakage current on the order of
0.1-1 µA starts to ﬂow through the nitride (Fig. 6.6c).
2. At 54 V the ﬁrst breakdown occurs leading to the top electrode partly delaminating
(Fig. 6.6a). After this happens, the current gradually increases smoothly (Fig. 6.6a).
3. The current keeps rising till a voltage of 156 V where the second breakdown occurs -
here the current jumps discontinuously from 0.6 mA to the compliance current.
4. On the way back (red curve), the current can easily ﬂow.

6.5
Heater calibration in air
An important feature of the MEMS platform is its heating ability. In experiments involving
atomic scale patterning and simultaneous electrical characterisation and heating it is of
interest to know the temperature for a given applied power. As a ﬁrst attempt, the heater
is calibrated in air. While, atmospheric conditions are very diﬀerent from the high vacuum
inside a TEM, the calibration in air still provides useful information, since the TEM
chip is also used for ex-situ experiments. The major diﬀerence between the two scenarios
regarding the heat loss, is convection, which is driven by air currents around the chip that
carries heat away. Therefore, the TEM chip is expected to be hotter at any given power
when situated in-situ TEM. Performing the calibration both in-situ and ex-situ TEM
allows one to judge the importance of this convection in the heat transfer processes. The
emitted thermal radiation from the chip depends only on the temperature and material
constants, thus it is the same in the two scenarios except that the received radiation from
the surroundings can diﬀer, leading to a diﬀerent net power.

6.5.1
Setup
For the calibration, the experiment was set up as shown in Fig. 6.1b. The calibration was
performed using a 4 pt heater chip, where a source voltage was applied (relative to ground)
between the outer pads and a 4 pt voltage measured between the inner pads, such that the
contact resistance is eliminated. The electrical measurements are made using two Keithley
2400’s - one sourcemeter and one multimeter. The shieldings of the coaxial cables are all
grounded. The chip is placed on a heating element inside the Linkam. A picture of the
chip with the four probes connected can be seen in Fig. 6.9a. The temperature is then
raised at the fastest rate of 150 ◦C/min. Slower rates have been tested as well without any
diﬀerence - the chip can easily adjust temperature at this rate.
6.5.2
Purpose and assumptions
The purpose of the calibration is to establish the relationship between the resistance of the
platinum heater and its temperature, R = R(T). From this measurement the resistivity
ρ(T) of the platinum is also obtained, since the two are related through the heater geometry,
which is known. In the calibration we are assuming that: (1) The temperature of the TEM
chip equals the applied temperature from the stage, and (2) That neither the temperature
nor the resistance is inﬂuenced by the measurement itself. These rules can be generalised
to apply for any calibration. Stating the above assumptions does not mean that we are
naively believing them to be true, it is important however to keep them in mind and to
improve the experimental conditions such that they are ’less violated’.
6.5.3
Probing voltage
As mentioned above, it is important that the voltage used for probing the resistance is
not disturbing the actual property we are interested in, i.e. it should generate as little
Joule heating as possible, without the data becoming too noisy. A good indicator of Joule
heating is the I-V curve becoming non-linear. In case of no self-heating of the resistor, the
slope of the I-V curve, dR = dV/dI, should therefore be constant. Fig. 6.7 displays this
value against the applied voltage for two diﬀerent ranges. It is seen that for the 300 mV
range (Fig. 6.7b), there is a clear bending of the data, i.e. the resistance increases as a
result of Joule heating. However, for the 100 mV data there is no systematic variation -
the points are randomly scattered in a 5 Ωwide band. Therefore it should be safe to use
100 mV as a voltage for probing the resistance in the calibration.

6.6
Buckling membrane
Motivation
Testing the MEMS heater platform to know its limitations include ﬁnding
the maximum power level the heater can endure without failure. Such a test, however,
does not reveal how the chip fails: Does the membrane break or does the heater burn over?
Other interesting questions to be answered are: At what power level does the heater start
to glow visibly? Does it seem to radiate evenly throughout the heater, or are there any
hot spots? Also, what happens to the membrane as it expands due to thermal expansion?
Being ﬁxed at all sides, it has to deﬂect or buckle - the precise behaviour of the deﬂection
versus the applied power is interesting to know. Since the purpose of the chip is its usage as
a platform for 2D materials, the deﬂection of the membrane will naturally cause strain in
the material under investigation (e.g. in graphene). Such a strain will change the electrical
properties of the material (i.e. from bending of the energy bands), which introduces a source
of error that needs attention. The graphene itself might also break from a large strain. If
the deﬂection can be controlled precisely, it might even be turned into an advantage, if
one wants to observe the eﬀects from straining the material. All of the above motivate
observing the membrane while running a high power through the heater.
Experiment
The experiment was set up as described in section 6.1. To capture images
continuously along with the IV measurements, the Linkam stage was placed under a
10x optical microscope with a long working distance and a camera mounted on top (see
Fig. 6.1a). The setup is part of a Raman system and the camera is not capable of capturing
images at a high frame rate. Therefore, images were only taken at a rate of one image per
second using the software named Chronolapse to set the timing between frames. Three
diﬀerent chips were examined: A 2 pt heater without top electrodes and no encapsulation,
a 4 pt heater, and a 2 pt heater with top electrodes (the two latter encapsulated in SiN). In
case of the 4 pt heater, a 4 pt measurement was done using the outer pads for the source
voltage and the inner pads for the 4 pt measurement voltage. The voltage was sweeped
from 6 V to 16 V at the same rate, such that the images and the electrical measurements
could be correlated.
Discussion
Selected images of all three types of heater chips are shown at three diﬀerent
power levels in Fig. 6.12. Interestingly, the membrane does not deﬂect symmetrically
around the center as in the case of a square or circular membrane (without any patterned
electrodes). Rather, it goes into a higher order buckling mode with 4-5 nodes with the
particular shape depending on the type of chip. The onset of this buckling occurs at
approx. 65 −70 mW, i.e. slightly before the images in the ﬁrst row. Inspecting the non-
encapsulated heater (1st column), one can see that the membrane goes into a shape with
multiple nodes on either side of the heater loop. This can probably be explained by a
larger stress in the heater than in the surroundings, which forces the membrane to deﬂect
in these areas. The larger stress in the heater comes from the fact that the heater is the
heat source and thereby warmer than the surroundings - the thermal expansion is therefore
more pronounced in the vicinity of the heater, something which is further accelerated by
the fact that the coeﬃcient of thermal expansion is larger for platinum than for silicon
nitride (8.8 × 10−6 K−1 vs 2.3 × 10−6 K−1). Already from the ﬁrst image at 75 mW, one
can spot four nodes (visible as dark ’shadows’) in the membrane. Once the membrane
decides for a deﬂection shape, only a small redistribution of the nodes occur.
Also interesting is the fact that the three types of heater chips display diﬀerent deﬂection
shapes. While the non-encapsulated chip discussed above has a rather ﬂat stripe in the
middle and ﬁve nodes on each side of the heater loop, the encapsulated 4 pt heater chip

To make a better estimation of the temperature versus applied power, a new IV sweep

6.8
Graphene transfer
Mechanical exfoliation
Transferring graphene to the TEM chip begins with making
the graphene itself. In this project, the graphene is made from mechanical exfoliation
of graphite, a technique also known as the Scotch tape method. This method was for
the ﬁrst time successfully used to extract single-layer graphene from graphite by Andre
Geim and Konstantin Novoselov in 2004 [25]. The technique exploits the week van der
Waals interaction between the graphite layers, which allows them to be easily separated.
Mechanically exfoliated graphene has the highest quality in terms of electronic properties
(e.g. the highest electron mobility and the lowest number of defects) among the various
fabrication methods (epitaxial growth, CVD growth, among others).
In this project, the graphene was made by taking a small piece of graphite, which was
cleaved and distributed in a half-wafer sized area on blue tape. Then the blue tape was
folded to cleave it once more. Statistically, some of the cleaved graphite will be graphene,
some few-layered, while most of the ﬂakes can still be considered graphite. The ﬂakes are
transferred to an index marked wafer, which ease ﬁnding the single-layer graphene (SLG)
afterwards. The index wafer is prepared by ﬁrst baking it at 200 ◦C for 20 min to evaporate
water content, followed by 10 min plasma ashing in O2 (200-400 mtorr at 100 W). The
graphene ﬂakes are then transferred to the index wafer by rolling the blue tape on top of it.
The wafer is then baked at 80 ◦C for 10 s which decrease the adhesion from the tape: thus,
more graphene/graphite will adhere to the substrate when pulling the tape oﬀ. However,
a drawback to heating the index wafer + tape is that the graphene is contaminated by
residues from the glue, which are more pronounced if heated. A compromise exists at
80 ◦C. The graphene ﬂakes are randomly scattered across the index wafer. After the entire
wafer has been mapped by an optical microscope, a program developed by Bjarke Sørensen
Jessen was used to identify single- and bi-layer graphene, etc. The program relies on the
fact that graphene on silicon dioxide (SiO2) creates a small contrast to the substrate.
Wedging transfer method
The actual transfer of graphene to the TEM chip is based
on the wedging transfer method proposed in [33]. In this process the wetting nature of
water is used to lift oﬀa hydrophobic nanostructure (e.g. graphene) from a hydrophilic
surface. The graphene is attached to a hydrophobic polymer, which in our case is cellulose
acetate butyrate (CAB), which allows for the transfer of graphene from one substrate
to another (Fig. 6.15a). Thus, a solution of CAB (25 mg in 100 mL ethyl acetate) is
spin coated on the index wafer. The recipe is: 1500 rpm for 60 s with an acceleration of
200 rpm/s. The wafer with CAB on is subsequently baked at 75 ◦C. The CAB can then be
cut with a scalpel in an area containing a graphene ﬂake. When water is added, the water
spontaneously invades the hydrophilic/hydrophobic interface due to capillary forces [33].
Once completely wetted, the polymer with graphene is lifted and ﬂoats on the water/air
interface. The piece of polymer can then be transferred to the TEM chip (Fig. 6.15a). A
100x optical micrograph of a trilayer graphene transferred to a 4-point contact chip can be
seen in Fig. 6.16a.
Subsequently, the chip is baked at 135 ◦C for 2 min (with a slow temperature ramp
from 80 ◦C, e.g. 10 ◦C/min) to evaporate the water from the transfer process and bring
the polymer with graphene underneath in contact with the substrate (Fig. 6.15b). A
zoom-in on the graphene can be seen in Fig. 6.16b. In this particular case, ripples in the
graphene sheet formed after it was brought into full contact with the substrate, because
it is suspended by the top electrodes. The CAB polymer is then dissolved in ﬁrst ethyl
acetate (10 min), then acetone (5 min), and ﬁnally moved to isopropanol without being
allowed to dry.

Summary
In summary, we have seen that the heaters are stable at high temperatures (575-595 ◦C)
for at least 35 min and probably even longer. Furthermore, the heater is stable for 20 hours
at a power level of 72 mW through the heater. There is some threshold level between
70 mW and 100 mW, which leads to an instability and a thermal runaway. It is believed to
have its cause in the onset of buckling, which occurs at approx. 75 mW. According to the
temperature calibration, the heater is around 600 ◦C at 70 mW. The resistance was seen
to increase permanently after the heater was exposed to temperatures exceeding (500 ◦C).
The increased resistance also happened if the heat came from Joule heating. A trilayer
graphene was succesfully transferred to a four-point chip. After baking the chip at 135 ◦C,
ripples formed in the sheet, but were gone again after critical point drying. The residue
from the CAB was partly removed after two voltage sweeps to 2 V, causing the overall
resistance to drop from 1057 Ωto 414 Ω.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The aim of this project was to develop and test micro fabricated chips for in-situ transmission
electron microscopy (TEM) experiments involving 2D materials such as graphene. The
purpose of the microchip is to constitute a platform for such experiments, and to facilitate
both heating as well as electrical measurements for characterising the material under study.
This project was concerned with the TEM chip itself, which was investigated through four
major studies: Simulating the design through ﬁnite element analysis, fabrication the chip
in the cleanroom, characterising the electrical properties and testing the limitations of the
chip, and ﬁnally transferring graphene to the chip to test its functioning.
A 3D Comsol multiphysics model of a 4 pt heater chip was built from scratch. The
interesting part of the design, speciﬁcally the membrane with the heater, was simulated.
The model solved for the current, temperature, and stresses arising from Joule heating
in the platinum resistor. It was found that a mesh consisting of prism elements was
superior to the standard tetrahedral mesh, and could bring the simulation time down by a
large factor. The results from the model showed a quite uniform temperature distribution
across a large area of the membrane - in particular, the highest temperature was achieved
in the center, which is the intention with the design, since the material under study is
bridged across a hole here. However, the temperature in absolute terms was found to be
substantially lower than what was found in experiments (∼150 ◦C in the model versus
∼400 ◦C in the experiment, both at a power level of 50 mW). The stress was found to be
largest inside the heater as expected: 550 MPa at 10 V. The deformation of the membrane
was compared to an experiment and was found to be quite diﬀerent except for a small part
at the end of the heater loop, which showed the same shape. Things such as initial stress,
the titanium layer, as well as the encapsulating nitride layer are believed to play a role in
the deformation shape.
While the mask design of the chip was not changed, some discoveries were made during
the fabrication process. The wet etch in potassium hydroxide (KOH) caused problems
because the mechanical holder was leaking and the sputtered silicon nitride was easily
etched. The 15 s dip in buﬀered hydroﬂuoric acid (BHF) to remove the native oxide
before KOH was also found to etch the sputtered nitride. The solution to the former
problem was found in a ProTEK R⃝B3 KOH resistant coating provided by Brewer Science.
The preliminary tests were promising: the wafer was immersed for more than 5 hours
in 33 wt% KOH without the sputtered nitride being attacked through the coating (the
color of the nitride depends strongly on the thickness of the layer). The coating was
easily removed using remover 1165 followed by plasma ashing. During the cleanroom
process the cross-section of the heaters was measured to be 0.35 µm2 using atomic force
microscopy (AFM). The width of the heater (3.5 µm) was a bit larger than the mask design

In the characterisation, we have seen that the Ti/Pt heaters are stable at a high
temperature (575-595 ◦C) for at least 35 min and probably even longer. This should be
suﬃcient time for an in-situ TEM experiment involving a high temperature. However, it
should be noted that this temperature was externally applied - the situation is diﬀerent
if the same temperature has to be sustained by Joule heating due to diﬀerent stress
distributions, which under high applied power levels leads to buckling in the latter case.
We have seen that the heater was stable for 20 hours at a power level of 72 mW through
the heater, and that there is some threshold level between 70 mW and 100 mW, which
leads to an instability and a thermal runaway. It is believed to have its cause in the onset
of buckling, which occurs at approx. 75 mW. The buckling was observed and imaged
under a microscope for three diﬀerent heater chips. Each type was seen to have a distinct
deformation proﬁle, probably related to whether or not the membrane has (1) the top SiN
layer, and (2) the top electrodes. All three types were seen to fail at approximately the
same power (130 mW), after which the heater simply burned over, while the membrane
stayed intact in all three cases. Furthermore, breakdown of the top silicon nitride layer
has been observed at 54 V, where after the nitride conducts a small current on the order of
µA due to electrons tunneling through the barrier (Fowler-Nordheim tunneling). At 156 V
the barrier is fully removed and the nitride is conducting a current in the mA range.
The Ti/Pt heater was calibrated in air to ﬁnd the resistance versus temperature.
According to the calibration, the heater is around 600 ◦C at 70 mW, which is a stable power
level. The resistance was found to permanently increase about 100 Ωafter heating the chip
above 500 ◦C - a similar increase was found by heating the chip from Joule heating. The
increased resistivity is believed to be caused by morphological changes in the Ti/Pt bilayer.
Many studies point towards diﬀusion of titanium into the top layer as the main explanation
to an increased resistivity at annealing temperatures above 500 ◦C [32, 27]. The sudden
decrease in resistance and corresponding negative TCR at temperatures exceeding 500 ◦C is
not fully understood. However, it should be emphasized that this eﬀect is only temporary:
the resistance follows the same track when cooled down.
A graphene trilayer was successfully transferred to a four-point chip using the wedging
transfer method. During the transfer process it was found that ripples in the graphene sheet
were created after the chip was baked at 135 ◦C. The ripples disappeared after the sample
was dried using critical point drying (CPD). After critical point drying, residues from the
cellulose acetate butyrate (CAB) were clearly visible on the sheet. This contamination was
eﬀectively removed by sweeping a voltage between the electrodes, whereupon the overall
resistance dropped from 1057 Ωto 414 Ωafter two sweeps from 0-2 V.

7.1. SUGGESTIONS
7.1
Suggestions
Summarizing, the TEM chip has been thoroughly tested and characterised. It has been
found that the lack of annealing the Ti/Pt bilayer during the fabrication process causes
the performance (resistance) to change with use. The optimal annealing conditions have
been investigated in many papers, and it is suggested that a similar analysis is done to
our heaters. Measuring the intrinsic stress in the Pt layer as-deposited e.g. by wafer
bow measurements is also recommended. This measurement could be repeated after the
membranes are released in the etch. The knowledge of the intrinsic stress in the layers can
be used to improve the Comsol model. The lifetime of the heater might be enhanced by
exchanging the Ti with Ta according to [8].
It is also suggested that the heater calibration is repeated in a more controlled envi-
ronment than the Linkam stage. A suggestion could be to heat the TEM chip inside a
vacuum tube within a large furnace, where the temperature is well controlled. According
to [8] the degradation of platinum is enhanced in an oxygen environment. Therefore, the
heater could be calibrated or annealed in a N2 environment in a furnace.
As a ﬁnal remark, the TEM chip could be calibrated inside the TEM by observing the
crystallographic etching of graphene by Ag nanoparticles in an oxygen environment. In a
study by Tim Booth et al. [4], the activation energy of these particles were determined in
such an experiment (applying a known temperature). The velocity of the particles depends
on the temperature in an Arrhenius form (exp(−Ea/kBT)), i.e. by using the determined
activation energy from [4], the temperature on the chip can be estimated based on the
velocity of the moving particles.
