Abstract 
Concerns about depleting fossil resources along with environmental issues have urged the research 
for new technologies to produce sustainable energy. Lignocellulosic biomass is a renewable and 
sustainable resource for carbon-based fuels, which has proven to be easily integrated into the cur-
rent transportation infrastructure. The solid biomass is converted to a bio-oil through fast pyrolysis. 
To test the efficiency of catalytic deoxygenation of a biomass pyrolysis vapor model compound by 
zeolites, a setup at DTU has been modified for these investigations. This thesis is about characteriz-
ing the products from deoxygenation of hydroxyacetone, investigating the acidity of the zeolites, 
and the formation of coke as higher hydroxyacetone to catalyst ratios are reached. 
For this thesis seven zeolites were utilized; three types of CBV30, two types of CBV55, and two types 
of CBV80. The difference between the zeolite types is the presence of only micropores or a mix of 
micropores and mesopores. From the coke combustions it was found that a high rate of coking was 
observed initially, with a decreasing rate as the hydroxyacetone to catalyst ratio increases. Differ-
ences in the release of CO and CO2 between the zeolites during the combustion of coke were ob-
served. It was found that the mesoporous zeolites release a higher ratio of CO2 compared to the 
microporous ones, indicating that the introduction of mesopores results in a more distributed coke 
depositing. From the ammonia TPDs conducted after each deoxygenation experiment is was found 
that the total acidity drops as more coke is deposited on the catalyst with reduced slopes of de-
crease. It was furthermore found, that the mesoporous zeolites have an increased tolerance to-
wards deactivation from coking, as more coke was deposited on these zeolites while keeping a 
higher acidity relative to their starting acidity. 
Analysis of the gas phase produced from the deoxygenation showed that CO and ethene in general 
represents 30 wt% of the product. Comparison of the obtained gas yields at similar hydroxyacetone 
to catalyst ratios for the microporous zeolites with their mesoporous counterpart, showed that the 
mesoporous zeolites have an increased yield of ethene and CO. No remarkable difference in the C1-
C3 alkanes and C4+ yields were determined as an effect of the treatments; however, an increasing 
yield as the hydroxyacetone to catalyst ratio increased to 6, with similar values at a ratio of 12 was 
determined. From chromatograms of the collected liquid products it was determined that the ma-
jority of the compounds produced were found at retention times below 10 minutes. The compounds 
found in this retention time interval were mostly monoaromatics, furans, ketones, and ethers. From 
comparison of the microporous zeolites with their mesoporous counterpart, it was found that the 
mesoporous delay the breakthrough of hydroxyacetone. Comparison of the monoaromatics yield 
showed that the microporous CBV30 and CBV55 had higher yield than their mesoporous counter-
part, while the mesoporous CBV80 showed higher yield than the microporous. This trend was at-
tributed to the level of strong acid sites on the zeolites, as a decreasing level of strong acid sites was 
found to result in a lowered yield of monoaromatics.

Resumé 
Bekymringer om udtømning af fossile ressourcer i kombination med miljømæssige problemer har 
fremskyndet forskning af nye teknologier til produktion af bæredygtig energi. Lignocellulose bio-
masse er en fornybar og bæredygtig ressource til kulstofbaserede brændstoffer, som har vist sig at 
være let at integrerer i den nuværende transportinfrastruktur. Den faste biomasse omdannes til en 
bioolie gennem ”fast pyrolysis”. En opstilling på DTU er blevet modificeret til at undersøge effekti-
viteten af katalytisk deoxygenering af gasfase model komponenter fra biomassepyrolysen ved brug 
af zeolitter. Denne afhandling omhandler karakterisering af produkterne fra deoxygeneringen af hy-
droxyacetone, samt undersøgelser af zeolitterne surheden og koksformation som hydroxyacetone 
til katalysatorforholdet øges. 
Til denne afhandling blev syv zeolitter brugt; tre typer CBV30, to CBV55 og to CBV80. Forskellen 
mellem zeolittyperne er tilstedeværelsen af kun mikroporer eller en blanding af mikroporer og me-
soporer. Fra koksforbrændingen blev der konstateret en høj koksformation indledningsvis med en 
faldende formations hastighed ved stigende hydroxyacetone til katalysatorforhold. Det blev yderli-
gere observeret, at de mesoporøse zeolitter frigiver et højere niveau af CO2 end CO i forhold til de 
mikroporøse, hvilket indikerer at indførelsen af mesoporer resulterer i en mere distribueret koksaf-
lejring. Fra ammoniak TPD'erne udført efter hvert deoxygeneringsforsøg blev det konstateret, at 
den totale surhedsgrad falder med stigende hydroxyacetone til katalysatorforhold, da mere koks 
deponeres på katalysatoren. Det blev endvidere fundet, at de mesoporøse zeolitter har en øget 
tolerance over for deaktivering fra koks, da mere koks blev deponeret på disse zeolitter, mens en 
højere surhed blev opretholdt. 
Analyse af gasfasen fra deoxygeneringen viste, at CO og ethen generelt repræsenterer 30 vægt% af 
produktet. Sammenligninger af de opnåede gasudbytter ved lignende hydroxyacetone til katalysa-
torforhold for de mikroporøse zeolitter med deres mesoporøse modstykke viste, at de mesoporøse 
zeolitter har et forøget udbytte af ethen og CO. Ingen bemærkelsesværdig forskel i C1-C3 alkaner 
og C4+ udbyttet blev konstateret som virkning af behandlingerne. Imidlertid blev et stigende ud-
bytte observeret ved for øgning af hydroxyacetone til katalysatorforholdet til 6, med tilsvarende 
værdier ved et forhold på 12. Fra kromatogrammeren af de opsamlede væskeprøver blev det be-
stemt, at størstedelen af de fremstillede forbindelser blev fundet ved retentionstider under 10 mi-
nutter. Forbindelserne der blev fundet i dette retentionstidsinterval, var hovedsageligt mono aro-
mater, furaner, ketoner og ether. Fra sammenligning af de mikroporøse zeolitter med deres me-
soporøse modstykke blev det konstateret, at introduktionen af mesoporer forsinker gennembrud-
det af hydroxyacetone. Sammenligning af det monoaromatiske udbytte viste, at den mikroporøse 
CBV30 og CBV55 havde højere udbytter end deres mesoporøse modstykke, medens den mesopo-
røse CBV80 viste højere udbytte end den tilsvarende mikroporøse. Denne tendens blev tildelt ni-
veauet af stærke syresteder på zeolitterne, da et faldende niveau af stærke syreområder viste sig at 
resultere i et sænket udbytte af monoaromater.

Background: 
The many years with concerns about increasing greenhouse gas emissions and depleting fossil re-
sources have urged for the research of new technologies for the production of sustainable energy. 
Especially the need for sustainable fuels is of interest, as transportation (automotive, aviation, mar-
itime) consumes a significant fraction of the global energy and contributes substantially to the flue 
gas emissions. Lignocellulosic biomass (e.g. wood, straw, and energy crops) is a renewable and sus-
tainable resource for carbon-based fuels, which has proven to be easily integrated into the current 
transportation infrastructure. The solid biomass is converted efficiently to a bio-oil through fast py-
rolysis. However, using lignocellulosic biomass as the raw material leads to a bio-oil consisting of 
many different species, which are hard to identify. Further treatment is desired in order to use the 
bio-oil in the transportation sector or for chemical manufacturing. 
Description: 
The crude bio-oil currently produced from pyrolysis has a high oxygen content and acidity and is 
therefore not suitable for direct application. Especially oxygenated species are a problem due to 
high reactivity, which leads to polymerization and storage problems. Upgrading of the bio-oil by 
lowering the oxygen content is of interest in order to improve the fuel properties, such as higher 
heating value and stability. Catalytic vapor phase upgrading over an acidic catalyst at elevated

of oxygen. However, poisoning of active sites and rapid coke deposition often challenges the cata-

1 Introduction 
The use of heavy fuel oil has been dated back to the construction of the Babylon tower over four 
thousand years ago and continues to be mentioned throughout history. In 1870 the first gasoline 
powered combustion engine was invented. Since then liquid fuel in the form of gasoline has been a 
very important part of the transportation sector. Today, gasoline mostly comes from fossil re-
sources, but combustion of the fuel generates harmful flue gases and contributes to global warming 
by increasing CO2 levels. Concerns about depleting fossil resources along with the environmental 
issues have urged the research for new technologies to produce sustainable energy. Especially the 
need for sustainable fuels is of interest, as a significant fraction of the global energy is consumed in 
the transportation sector along with a concerning emission level of CO2. 
A goal of a reduction in the greenhouse emission of 50–80 % by 2050 has been presented by the UN 
climate panel [1]. To realize this target, the use of biomass to produce bio-oils have been considered, 
along with other renewable energy sources. It has been found that using lignocellulosic biomass as 
the raw material, a low-cost, renewable, and sustainable carbon-based fuel can be produced. An 
efficient way of turning the solid biomass into a bio-oil is through the process of fast pyrolysis, which 
gives high yields of liquids by utilizing high heating rates and short residence times in an oxygen-free 
atmosphere. This liquid contains a high amount of oxygen species such as water, carboxylic acids, 
aldehydes, ketones, alcohols, furans, and phenols, which results in a lower heating value compared 
to crude oil, and poor stability upon storage due to the high reactivity of these oxygen species. 
Catalytic upgrading of the obtained bio-oil or direct upgrading of the pyrolysis vapors is therefore 
of interest, as this has been shown to improve the overall quality by lowering the oxygen content, 
while removing the most reactive functional groups. The ZSM-5 catalyst has proven to be of great 
interest due to the functionality of its Brønsted acid sites and shape selectivity towards carbon spe-
cies with less than 12 carbon atoms. However, poisoning of active sites and rapid coke deposition 
often challenges the catalytic performance. Regeneration of the catalyst by combustion of the 
formed coke is an option, but if the conditions of the regeneration get too harsh, dealumination can 
occur, resulting in loss of performance, as acid sites in the structure will be lost. Since bio-oil contains 
a wide variety of oxygenated compounds, the upgrading is complex, and it is difficult to point out 
exactly which family of chemical groups contributes to the observed products. Cracking, decarbonyl-
ation, decarboxylation, hydrocracking, hydrodeoxygenation, hydrogenation, and polymerization are 
all reactions that have been reported to occur during the upgrading. In the following a literature 
study is presented first, followed by an overview of the setup used for this thesis, the results ob-
tained from the experiment work conducted, proposals to future work, and lastly a conclusion.

2 Biomass 
In general, the word “biomass” is used as reference to all biological organic materials, but in terms 
of energy applications, biomass refers to the material obtained directly from the plants. Various 
forms of biomass are of interest for the application of energy supply including wood, straw, and 
energy crops [2]. Dry biomass is usually referred to as lignocellulosic biomass, which mainly com-
pose of the two carbohydrate polymers cellulose and hemicellulose, and the aromatic polymer lig-
nin. The composition of these polymers in wood is known to be 30–60% cellulose, 15–35% hemicel-
lulose, and 15–40% lignin [3]. In Appendix A, a table showing the content of cellulose, hemicellulose, 
and lignin in different materials on dry basis can be found. The structure of the biomass is porous, 
where cellulose represents the important backbone, while the other substrates surrounds it. A sche-
matic drawing of the structure of wood can be seen in Figure 1. 
 
Figure 1 – Schematic drawing of the molecular structure of wood [4]. 
The typical functional groups and the IR signal associated with the possible compounds in cellulose, 
hemicellulose, and lignin are listed in Table 1. It is observed that, the three components most likely 
consists of different oxygen-containing functional groups such as alkenes, esters, aromatics, ketone, 
and alcohol. The highest IR absorbance of OH and C–O was found in cellulose, while hemicellulose 
was found to contain a higher level of C=O compounds. Compared with hemicellulose and cellulose, 
a big difference was found for the IR spectra of lignin. A group of complex IR absorbance was found, 
indicating that lignin contains high levels of methoxyl–O–CH3, C–O–C stretching and C=C stretching 
(aromatic ring) compounds [5].

2.1 Cellulose 
Cellulose is the major structural component in the cell walls of a plant cell, and the most widespread 
natural organic compound. The structure is a linear polymer of repeating D-glucose units, also 
known as pyranose. The rings are linked by β-1,4 glycosidic bonds, between C-1 in one ring and C-4 
in the following [6]–[8]. The structure of a cellulose molecule can be seen in Figure 2. 
 
Figure 2 – Molecular structure of a cellulose molecule [7]. 
The properties of the cellulose depend on the number of glucose units that makes up the polymer, 
also known as its degree of polymerization (DP). This degree can be as high as 15 000 [8] or even 17 
000 [7]. The linear structure of cellulose is maintained by intramolecular hydrogen bonds between 
the hydroxyl group at C-3 and the oxygen in the pyranose ring and the hydroxyl group at C-2 and C-
6. These interactions give cellulose a crystalline structure, and its unique properties of high tensile 
strength. The three-dimensional structure is often referred to as cellulose fibers or cellulose micro-
fibrils. The fibers have an approximate thickness of 10–20 nm, with a length of several μm, which 
==PAGE:21==
 
Page 4 of 99 
 
can contain between 30 to 100 cellulose macromolecules [8]. These molecules are arranged in par-
allel microfibrils, which form a planar unit (sheet). An illustration of this arrangement can be found 
in Figure 3. 
 
Figure 3 – Illustration of the sheet structure of cellulose caused by the hydrogen bonds [7]. 
2.1.1 Properties 
Under normal atmospheric conditions (20°C and 60% relative humidity) cellulose has the ability to 
absorb 8–14% water, however it is not soluble in water, but swells. Cellulose is also insoluble in 
dilute acids, bases, and most solvents. However, in concentrated acids, depending on the degree of 
hydrolysis achieved, cellulose can be dissolved, as the polymer is broken down to smaller chains as 
disaccharides or even glucose. The temperature affects the solubility as well, as higher temperatures 
provides sufficient energy to break the hydrogen bonds. If cellulose is heated, it does not melt, but 
it starts to decompose at a temperature range of 240–400°C [6]–[9]. 
2.2 Hemicellulose 
The second structural polymer is hemicellulose, which surrounds the cellulose fibers and stands as 
a connecting link between cellulose and lignin. The term hemicellulose is commonly used for repre-
senting a group of non-cellulose structural polysaccharides, that are found in the plant cell wall [7], 
[8]. The DP for hemicellulose is 50–200 monomers, which is significantly lower than for cellulose, 
also the lack of a crystalline structure is an important aspect of hemicellulose [6]. The amorphous 
structure is mainly due to a highly branched structure and the presence of acetyl groups on the 
polymer. The structure of hemicellulose consists of two main groups: heteroglucans and heteroxy-
lans, which then can be divided into further subcategories. However, the most common type of 
polymer in the structure is xylan, which is linked through xylopyranosyl units with α-(4-O)-methyl-
D-glucuronopyranosyl units attached to anhydroxylose units [7]. An illustration of the molecular 
structure of xylan is shown in Figure 4. 
==PAGE:22==
 
Page 5 of 99 
 
 
Figure 4 – Molecular structure of xylan (the most common polymer of hemicellulose) [10]. 
2.2.1 Properties 
Hemicellulose is, as cellulose, insoluble in water at low temperatures. However, its hydrolysis begins 
at lower temperatures than that of cellulose, and it starts to break down at temperatures of 200 to 
260°C [9]. This is due to the branching in the structure, which makes it soluble at elevated temper-
atures. If acid is added, the solubility of hemicellulose in water is highly improved [7]. The same 
applies if base is added [6]. Since hemicellulose has an amorphous structure the physical strength 
of the structure is very low. 
2.3 Lignin 
Lignin is an aromatic, three-dimensional, and cross-linked copolymer of phenylpropanoid units, 
known as monolignols. Depending on the type of biomass, the structural units in this large molecule 
varies, some common phenylpropanoid units are 4-coumarly alcohol, coniferyl alcohol, 5-hydroxy-
coniferyl alcohol, and sinapyl alcohol. These units are bound irregularly by ether bonds (C-O-C) or 
bonds between two carbon atoms [8]. Depending on the combination of units and the type of 
bonds, the corresponding type of lignin can be categorized as guaiacyl, syringyl and p-hydroxyl-phe-
nyl lignin [6],[8]. The structure of these monomers are shown in Figure 5, while a possible structure 
of lignin is shown on Figure 6. 
 
Figure 5 – Monomer units of lignin. Left: p-hydroxyl-phenyl. Mid: guaiacyl. Right: syringyl. 
As shown in Figure 1, lignin is primarily present in the outer layer of the fibers and is the reason for 
the rigid structure. Lignin greatly affects the endurance and development of the cell, as it plays a 
part in the transport of water and nutrients in the plant cell. It furthermore works as a binder be-
tween cells, thereby affecting the resistance to impacts, compression and bending [7]. In wood, the 
amount and specific category of lignin in the biomass give rise to a division, which consist of a split 
==PAGE:23==
 
Page 6 of 99 
 
into hardwood and softwood. These two types have dissimilar structures, where softwood have 
been found to contain more guaiacyl lignin, a polymer with a high fraction of coniferyl units, and 
hardwood typically contains guaiacyl-syringyl lignin, a copolymer of both the coniferyl and sinapyl 
units. Furthermore, the fraction of sinapyl units is higher in hardwoods than in softwood [6]. Since 
hardwood consists of both guaiacyl and syringyl units, it has a higher methoxyl content, compared 
to that of softwood [11]. 
 
Figure 6 – A possible structure of lignin [6]. 
2.3.1 Properties 
Just as cellulose and hemicellulose, lignin is insoluble in water, however different solvents as low 
molecular alcohols, acetone, and pyridine have been found to significantly dissolve lignin. It has 
furthermore been observed that thermal softening of lignin occurs at elevated temperatures, which 
leads to depolymerization reactions of acidic or alkaline nature [7]. Lastly, it has been found that 
the pyrolysis of lignin generally starts at a lower temperature than that of cellulose, however the 
degradation happens over a wider temperature range, 160–900°C [6],[11]. 
2.4 Inorganic matter  
Biomass sources contains varying amounts of inorganic material (alkali metals, heavy metals, and 
chlorine) which are reported as ash content. The presence of inorganic minerals is also dependent 
on the type of biomass, along with its environment during growth, the use of fertilizers, and the 
amount of inorganic matter that is collected during harvesting. Increased levels of alkali metals are 
generally seen in agricultural biomass and short-rotation energy crops, while lower levels are found 
in woody biomass. The content of inorganic matter in the biomass ranges from less than 2% in soft-
woods to 15% in agricultural waste. Alkali and alkali-earth metals, particularly sodium and 
==PAGE:24==
 
Page 7 of 99 
 
potassium, and sulfur and phosphorous containing ammonia salts have been reported to have a 
catalytic effect during pyrolysis, which favors the production of char and gases, at the expense of 
the condensable vapor product [2],[6],[12]. 
2.5 Moisture content 
Water is commonly found in biomass, and the content varies depending on the type of biomass. The 
water can be present in numerous forms, if adsorbed onto the hydroxyl groups of cellulose and 
hemicellulose molecules through hydrogen bonds, it is typically referred to as “bound” water. Un-
bound water is found occupying the void space of the biomass structure if the moisture content 
exceeds the fiber saturation point, which typically is 30% of the oven-dry weight for most types of 
wood. Water as a gaseous phase can also be found in the voids [2]. A proximate and ultimate anal-
ysis is shown in Table 2 for describing the differences between biomass and coal properties. It must 
be mentioned that the moisture content in the biomass is very dependent on the weather and time 
of harvesting, hence highly variable values can be seen. 
For the process of fast pyrolysis, water will be present in the final bio-oil product. This will reduce 
the heating value of the bio-oil and affect the viscosity. The organic species within the bio-oil are 
highly polar due to the high content of oxygen in the molecules. This makes the bio-oil hydrophilic, 
and the presence of an aqueous phase can be observed if the water content of the bio-oil is above 
30–45 wt% [2]. The water could also have an impact on reaction rates and the product yields. Some 
positive effects have been reported and an apparent reduction in the activation energy required for 
the pyrolysis reactions of wood under steaming conditions have been observed [13], resulting in 
faster reaction rates. However, contradictory results that indicates no impact on either yields or 
kinetics during conversion in a steam atmosphere has also been reported [12].

3 Biomass pyrolysis 
Numerous thermochemical conversion technologies may be applied to convert biomass into heat, 
power, and liquid fuels/chemicals. One of these processes is pyrolysis, which is conducted by heat-
ing the biomass in an inert atmosphere for the production of charcoal (bio-char), liquids (bio-oils), 
or high heating value gases. The relative proportions of the product depend on the operating con-
ditions. Depending on the reaction temperature, heating rate, and residence time, the pyrolysis can 
be classified as either slow, intermediate, or fast. Also, the specific feedstock for the process can 
affect the composition of the produced phases, as different biomasses contains different propor-
tions of cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin. In Table 3, an overview of the different modes of bio-

3.1 Slow pyrolysis 
Wood is the major feedstock for slow pyrolysis, as this process is a technique for treating biomass 
to produce charcoal. However, this process can also be used for producing liquid fuels [15]. The 
vapors formed during this slow pyrolysis are usually not condensed but can instead be used for 
providing the heat for operation, by direct or indirect heating. This process is more tolerant to the 
moisture content in the feedstock and a content of 15–20% is typical for charcoal production [6]. 
The process is carried out with slow heating rates and long residence times to maximize the char 
yields and with enough time for tar condensation. The applied heating rates are as low as 0.1 - 2°C 
min-1 [12], and up to 10–20°C min-1 with a residence time in the range of 5–30 minutes [9] or even 
days. 
3.2 Intermediate pyrolysis 
In this process, the pyrolysis is carried out in the temperature range of 400–550°C under controlled 
heating rates to produce condensable vapors and gas. Due to the controlled chemical reactions, the 
reaction conditions in intermediate pyrolysis offer a broad range of variation for process optimiza-
tion. Large biomass sizes are acceptable in this process, however lower liquid yields are obtained, 
which can be up to 55%. Compared to fast pyrolysis, the liquid obtained from intermediate pyrolysis, 
have low tar yield and viscosity [6],[9]. 
==PAGE:26==
 
Page 9 of 99 
 
3.3 Fast pyrolysis 
This technology has been gaining attention because of the increasing interest in the production of 
liquid fuel from biomass, as the process parameters in this pyrolysis mode are carefully controlled 
to give high oil yields. When utilizing fast pyrolysis, the principle is to prevent further cracking of the 
produced oil into non-condensable compounds. This is done by heating the feed rapidly in the ab-
sence of oxygen at temperatures in the range of 450–600°C for a short residence time of less than 
2 seconds [6],[9],[16]. However, using residence times of up to 5 seconds can give acceptable yields 
[16]. To ensure that the heating is done quickly, high heat transfer rates are needed. These are 
achieved by finely grinding the biomass, to a typically particle size of less than 5 mm [2]. Since the 
reaction takes place for a very short duration, the chemical kinetics and mass transfer play a crucial 
role in determining the product chemistry [6]. By using wood as the feed to the pyrolysis, a maxi-
mum liquid yield of up to 80 wt% on a dry feed basis can be achieved, with an operating temperature 
of 500–520°C and a vapor residence times of no more than 1 s [16]. Using grass tends to give a 
maximum liquid yield of around 55–60 wt% on a dry feed basis at a temperature range of 480–
500°C, depending in the ash content of the grass [14]. 
3.4 Pyrolysis mechanism 
As mentioned before, biomass mainly consists of cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin, and these com-
plex biopolymers undergo various transformations as the reaction temperature increases. The 
weight lost during pyrolysis can be measured with a thermogravimetric analysis (TGA), as shown in 
Figure 7. Initially, the mass decreases due to the evaporation of water, as the biomass temperature 
increases to approximately 100°C. As the temperature increases further to about 160°C, the bound 
water in the biomass structure is removed. At this point, the pyrolysis vapors exhibit a negligible 
heating value. Above 180°C the cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin polymers start to break down 
and liberate a mixture of noncondensable gases and condensable vapors. The condensable tar va-
pors contain water, acetic acid, methanol, acetone, phenols, and heavier tars, which sometimes are 
referred to as pyroligneous acid [2]. Additionally, due to the decomposition of functional groups 
permanent gases (CO2, H2, CO, CH4, C2H2, C2H4, C3H8) are released during char forming reactions [9]. 
At temperatures between 180 and 270°C, the reactions are endothermic, but have also been ob-
served to change to exothermic at temperatures above 280°C. An explanation to the exothermicity 
in this temperature range could be due to the decomposition of vapors to coke and secondary va-
pors. Contradictory results regarding the existence of this exothermic peak have be reported [12], 
as it depends greatly on the biomass examined. 
==PAGE:27==
 
Page 10 of 99 
 
 
Figure 7 – TGA curves for cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin for increasing temperature [5]. 
At temperatures above 400°C, the less volatile components, that still are present in the remaining 
solid char, are gradually released from the lignin derived material, resulting in a solid product that 
has a higher fixed carbon content and a lower volatile carbon content. If the temperature is in-
creased above 600°C, the primary condensable components, that are present in the gas phase, un-
dergo further cracking and polymerization reactions, which results in a reduced bio-oil yield [2]. 
Utilizing very short residence times leads to partial depolymerization of the lignin because of ran-
dom bond cleavage and inter-reaction of the lignin macromolecule, which results in a less homoge-
nous liquid product. Longer residence times can cause secondary cracking of the primary products, 
reducing yield, and unfavorably properties in the bio-oil [16]. 
3.5 Effect of operating conditions 
3.5.1 Heating rate 
Biomass pyrolysis is greatly affected by the heating rate, which may be attributed to the cellulose 
content in the biomass [17]. Furthermore, the heating rate also affects the melting and swelling of 
the biomass, and thereby how the competing processes, bond-breaking, cross-linking, and internal 
mass transport, changes with the temperature for the tar formation. The reaction temperature are 
thereby determined by the heating rate [9]. 
Initially, the process may require a significant amount of heat for drying the moisture within the 
biomass. An indication of the relative heat of reaction is shown in Figure 8, which is generated from 
differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) results. The figure furthermore shows that, the three poly-
mers decompose in distinct temperature ranges. Hemicellulose starts an early decomposition, fol-
lowed by cellulose, which both have a rather defined temperature range with most of the decom-
position. The decomposition of lignin occurs slowly over a wide temperature range, which all

concurs with the previous information about the three polymers. The differences in the chemical 
nature and molecular structures of the three components explains the dissimilar behaviors ob-
served [5]. The devolatilization process is fundamentally endothermic, whereas exothermic char 
formation reactions sometimes are observed. Biomass pyrolysis is typically endothermic at temper-
atures below 280°C, and may be exothermic at temperatures above 280°C [2].

A comparison of the yields and reactivities of chars produced from slow (20 K min-1) and fast heating 
(≈104 K s-1) pyrolysis was conducted by Zanzi [18]. The rapid heating rates resulted in lower char 
yields compared to those under slow heating. Furthermore, the chars generated at fast heating 
rates were more reactive in gasification than under the slow heating, and with a higher porosity. 
3.5.2 Organic matter 
The main gaseous products from pyrolyzing cellulose, hemicellulose, and lignin are rather similar, 
and include CO2, CO, CH4 and some organics. The release of H2 and a total gas yield can be measured 
using a Micro-GC when pyrolyzing the three components. It has been observed that hemicellulose 
has a higher CO2 release, while cellulose generates higher levels of CO. This is due to the high car-
boxyl content in hemicellulose and the higher carbonyl content in cellulose. Since a higher concen-
tration of aromatic rings and methoxyl are present in lignin, its cracking and deformation releases 
more H2 and CH4 [6]. The pyrolysis mechanism of cellulose is characterized by an initial depolymer-
ization step that produces active cellulose. This reaction reduces the polymerization degree without 
releasing any volatile components. Two competitive reactions then decompose the active cellulose: 
a slow reaction that produces char plus permanent gases, and a main reaction which releases 
levoglucosan. The pyrolysis involves cleavage of glycosidic groups by dehydration, followed by a 
==PAGE:29==
 
Page 12 of 99 
 
breakdown of the “anhydroglucose” units. At low temperatures, the dehydration and breakdown 
of sugar molecules result in char formation [3]. 
Hemicellulose is the most reactive component of the three major components in lignocellulose due 
to its less crystalline structure. The pyrolytic decomposition is postulated to go through three steps. 
First the polymer breaks into smaller (oligomeric) fragments, followed by decomposition to mono-
meric units, and finally these fragments decompose to volatiles. The devolatilization of hemicellu-
lose mainly occurs at temperatures around 260°C, leading to a high tar yield. Devolatilization at 
higher temperature produce an increased level of gas at the expense of tar, without the formation 
of levoglucosan as for cellulose [9]. In general, lignocellulosic materials with a high hemicellulose 
content have a lower solid product yield, compared to biomasses with a low hemicellulose content, 
if the amount of cellulose is kept constant. Wu et al. [19] observed that utilizing higher temperatures 
favors the cracking and reforming of volatile components, thereby increasing the CO and H2 for-
mation. Furthermore, the pyrolysis of hemicelluloses produces more methanol and acetic acid than 
cellulose. The multistep kinetic mechanism when pyrolyzing cellulose resembles that of hemicellu-
lose. The structural components of both cellulose and hemicellulose are polymeric sugar chains, 
which releases tar components (levoglucosan and anhydro-sugars), permanent gases, a wide num-
ber of oxygenated species, including methanol, acetic acid, hydroxyacetaldehyde, acetone, acetol, 
furfural, and 5-hydroxymethylfurfural during pyrolysis [3]. 
The decomposition of lignin starts at relatively low temperatures, but the main process takes place 
around 400°C. The pyrolysis causes the formation of aromatic hydrocarbons, phenols, hydroxyphe-
nols and guaiacyl-/syringyl-type compounds [20]. The highly cross-linked structure of lignin affects 
the decomposition and give a predominance for cross-linking reactions, favoring higher char yields 
due to the formation of more thermally resistant condensed structures [21]. Worasuwannarak et 
al. [22] found that significant interactions between cellulose and lignin are present during biomass 
pyrolysis, and that these interactions contributed to a decrease in tar yields, and an increase in char 
yields. Furthermore, it has been found that lignin inhibits the levoglucosan polymerization, thereby 
enhancing the formation of low molecular weight compounds from cellulose, causing a higher char 
yield. Simultaneously, the secondary char formation from lignin is reduced by the presence of cel-
lulose, which also enhances the formation of some lignin-derived products including guaiacol, 4-
methyl-guaiacol and 4-vinyl-guaiacol [23]. 
At temperatures below 230°C the mass loss of lignin is mainly caused by the cracking of hydroxyl 
groups in the side chains. The cleavage of C-C bonds is occurring at higher temperatures, releasing 
CO and CO2. The lighter hydrocarbons (mainly CH4) and H2 are released from primary pyrolysis of 
lignin in the temperature range of 400–800°C [5],[24]. Avni et al. [25] observed that, when the tem-
perature is increased to 900°C during the decomposition of lignin, CO2 is release from the carboxyl 
groups, CO from the weakly bound oxygen groups, H2O from the hydroxyl groups, and H2 from the 
aliphatic and methoxy groups. Furthermore, it was found that tertiary reactions at higher 
==PAGE:30==
 
Page 13 of 99 
 
temperatures may involve breakage and rearrangement of stronger bonds in the aromatic rings of 
lignin, with the release of additional H2 and CO from tightly bound oxygen groups. Utilizing temper-
atures above 500°C and high heating rates, leads to higher yields of volatile components and lower 
char yields from the lignin pyrolysis. 
3.6 Product characteristics 
During biomass pyrolysis, gas, tar, and residual char are always produced, but their proportions sig-
nificantly vary with the operating conditions of the process. It has been well explained that, slow 
heating rates and long residence times of the gas and tar released from the biomass favors the 
production of biochar, while short residence times and moderate to high heating rates optimize bio-
oil yields. In Figure 9 the effect of temperature on the typical yield from fast pyrolysis of wood can

[16],[26]. However, the heating value of the bio-oil is only approximately 40–50% of that of a liquid 
hydrocarbon fuel on a mass basis. This low value is due to the presence of water and a high oxygen 
content [2],[3],[16].

4 Zeolites 
Zeolites are microporous crystalline materials that occur both naturally and synthetically. A common 
feature is that they all have a 3-dimensional, tetrahedral framework structure constructed from 
corner-sharing. The framework-structure consist of silicon atoms, with some substitution of alumi-
num, bridged through oxygen atoms to form an ordered structure. A general structure formula is 
given by TO4, where T is any tetrahedrally coordinated cation. The substitution of silica with alumina 
gives rise to a charge imbalance originating from the difference in charge of Si4+ and Al3+. Introduc-
tion of this charge imbalance leads to acidic properties in the zeolite framework as a hydrogen atom 
can be bonded. Substitution by other di-, tri- and tetravalent metal than Al is also possible, e.g. Zn2+, 
B3+, Ti4+. Doing this with varying concentration could contribute with different properties, such as 
alteration of framework, which arise from difference in ionic radii. Introducing a large cation can 
block or reduce the effective size of a pore opening, while a small cation might alter a pore opening 
influence the catalytic, optical, magnetic, or electronic properties of the zeolite [27]. 
A framework-type, opposite to a framework-structure, is simply used to describe the connection 
between the tetrahedrally coordinated atoms (T-atoms) of the framework in the highest possible 
symmetry. The framework composition and the actual unit-cell dimensions are not considered. This 
gives the opportunity to classify many different materials under one designation. A three-letter code 
is assigned to confirmed framework types. The codes are generally originated from the name of the 
zeolite e.g. LTA from Linde Type A, and MFI from ZSM-5 (Zeolite Socony Mobil - five). The frame-
work-structure is relatively open and characterized by the presence of channels and cavities. These 
pores are of molecular size, meaning that molecules can enter the structure and find catalytically 
active sites and be converted. The newly formed molecule must be of a size which is allowed to 
leave through the pores. This give rise to one of the unique features of zeolite catalysts, the size 
selectivity. Only reactants small enough to enter the pores will be able to react, and only products 
that are able to diffuse out will be formed. This implies that the size of the pores in a specific zeolite 
structure is very important for the product and reactant selectivity [28]. 
Pore openings are characterized by the size of the ring that defines the pore. A ring consists of n-
atoms, where n is the number of T-atoms, in the ring. An 8-ring is considered a small pore opening 
with a size of up to 0.40 nm, a 10-ring a medium one of size 0.50-0.60 nm, and a 12-ring a large one 
up to 0.75 nm. These different types of rings give rise to a number of structural features: cages, 
channels, chains, and sheets, which are common in several different zeolite framework types, such 
as α-cavity, β-cage, and pentasil unit. In Figure 11, these subunits are shown, along with the pentasil 
chain. Between these, the pentasil chain with edge-sharing [58] cages is characteristic for the family 
of high-silica zeolites, such as the MFI structure [27]. The symbolism [58] means that the structure 
is made up of eight 5-rings.

4.1 Properties of zeolites 
The chemical and textural properties of zeolites are of great importance, when applying them in a 
process. Textural properties are generally related to the ability to adsorb organic molecules based 
on the size-exclusion effect, as described above, this phenomenon is called shape selectivity. The 
chemical properties of the zeolites are controlled by the composition, which determines the acidity, 
subsequently affecting their activity and selectivity in transforming organic compounds, their ion-
exchange properties, which governs the hydrophobicity–hydrophilicity of the zeolites, and the abil-
ity to adsorb polar or nonpolar molecules [29]. 
A clear relationship between the chemical properties of zeolites and the molar ratio of Si/Al has 
been reported, whether the ratio is high (small charge on frame) or low (high charge on frame). A 
generalization of the properties is given in Table 7. The importance of the ratio can be reasoned by 
the charge change of -1 when isomorphously substituting from Si4+ to Al3+. Increasing the Al3+ con-
tent increases the number of charged sites within the framework, which leads to a higher hydrogen 
concentration in the crystal structure [30]. In zeolites with a high silica content, the distribution of 
Al sites is rather unpredictable and dependent on several parameters, such as synthesis conditions 
and structural guiding agents. However, the sites are suspected to be far from one another, due to 
the low amount of Al, relative to zeolites with low silica content. This is in agreement with Löwen-
stein’s rule; Al-O-Al linkage is commonly not observed within the framework due to unfavorable 
interactions of negatively charged tetrahedral [29],[31].

The introduction of the negative charged sites in the framework of the zeolite results in highly acidic 
sites, due to the charge being balanced by a proton. These sites are one of the most important 
features, particularly in the aspect of using zeolites as catalysts. A zeolite typically contains both 
Brønsted and Lewis acid sites. The concentration and strength of these sites mostly depends on the 
structural type and its chemical composition of the zeolite. Brønsted acid sites (BAS) are a conse-
quence of the introduced negative charge and are characterized by a proton located on the bridging 
oxygen between the Si and Al tetrahedral. This site is strongest when the aluminate tetrahedral is 
neighboring two silicates tetrahedral (Si-OH+-Al--O-Si) [29]. Figure 12 and Figure 13 illustrates this 
principle between having Al sites in the framework or not. The strength of BAS depends on several 
parameters, such as the relative size of the pores, electronegativity of the trivalent metal atom in 
the framework, distribution and density of BAS, and the local geometry e.g. bond angle between Si-
OH+-Al-. However, Jones et al. [32] found that the characteristics of the zeolite framework and the 
mobility of protons in the presence of catalytic amounts of water, leads to an acid strength that 
solitary depends on the identity of the heteroatom species e.g. Al, and not on its location, distribu-
tion, or density.

Lewis acid sites (LAS) are also an interesting feature in the structure of zeolites. These are related to 
the presence of tri-coordinated (Al, Fe) or tetracoordinated (Sn) framework atoms and/or extra-
framework species, typically Al oxo-hydroxo species. It has been found that, interactions between 
Lewis and Brønsted acid sites in the framework of weakly steamed zeolites can lead to very strong, 
or even super acidic Brønsted sites [33]. Lago et al. [34] found a strongly enhanced catalytic activity 
for the cracking of n-hexane, when utilizing mild steamed H-ZSM-5 zeolite, which was attributed to 
super acidic Brønsted sites. This could be due to the partial hydrolyzation of aluminum atoms in the 
vicinity of bridging OH groups (Si-OH+-Al-). These partially hydrolyzed Al-atoms were regarded as 
strong electron-withdrawing centers for neighboring bridging OH groups, which creates the very 
high strength BAS [34].

catalyst of interest in this thesis. It has a high silicon to aluminum molar ratio, which typically is 
greater than 10 and possibly infinity. Nowadays, ZSM-5 is widely used in the petroleum refining and 
petrochemical industries due to its high degree of thermal and acid stability and high selectivity in 
certain catalytic conversions [35],[36]. The hydrothermal stability of ZSM-5 is however lower

compared with other zeolite types as USY [30]. The framework type of the high-silica zeolite can be

10-ring holes, seen as the gray sheet perpendicular to the x-axis in Figure 14. Each sheet is again 
connected by oxygen bridges in the x-direction to form the 3-dimensional structure. Adjacent sheets 
are related to one another by an inversion center. This produces straight 10-ring channels parallel 
along y-axis, and sinusoidal 10-ring channels perpendicular to the sheets (along x). The latter chan-
nels link the straight channels to one another to form a 3-dimensional 10-ring channel system [27], 
[37]. 
The use of the ZSM-5 zeolite in fluid catalytic cracking (FCC) is of great importance. FCC is the most 
important refining process, where low value, heavy petroleum fractions—typically vacuum gas oils 
are upgraded into distillates, primarily gasoline. It was introduced primarily to satisfy gasoline de-
mands. With the continuously increasing demand for propylene and the gradual shift in preferred 
transportation fuel from gasoline to diesel, product distribution needs to be more flexible. This de-
mand has been achieved by using ZSM-5 as an additive or co-catalyst in the process [38]. 
 
Figure 14 – Conceptual 3-D structure of ZSM-5 zeolite with MFI framework-type, sinusoidal, and straight pores [27]. 
By introducing the ZSM-5 zeolite, the octane number of FCC gasoline can be improved due to the 
selective cracking and formation of n-alkanes in an amount of 0.5–3%. It furthermore boosts the 
yield of light olefins, particularly of propylene and butene. Deep catalytic cracking, a modified FCC 
technology, which uses ZSM-5 as the primary catalyst, have been found to primarily produce olefins 
with gasoline as a by-product. An enhanced production of middle distillate fractions from FCC can 
be achieved, by lowering the Al content in the framework by dealumination, or in other words de-
creasing the acid sites density [29]. The ZSM-5 zeolite can furthermore be used for upgrading lower 
carbon chains into oil-like compounds, especially seen through the methanol to gasoline (MTG) pro-
cess. Here the zeolite catalyzes the deoxygenation of methanol accompanied by chain growth. This 
process is of high relevance for the conversion of biomass pyrolysis vapors.

5 Upgrading of bio-oil from fast pyrolysis 
The bio-oil produced from the fast pyrolysis of biomass contains a high level of oxygenated species, 
and it has been found that the bio-oil in general contains more than 90 different components, which 
mostly is oxygenates. The main interest of the upgrading is therefore to lower the oxygen content, 
thereby producing a more stable oil, which also has an increased heating value. The importance of 
this technology lies in the fact that biomass is an interesting alternative to crude oil from an envi-
ronmental point of view, because of its low contents of sulfur, nitrogen, and metals and because its 
global balance of CO2 is neutral [39]. 
A promising way to obtain the desired stability and a sufficient heating value is through catalytic 
upgrading with an acidic catalyst, where ZSM-5 has proven to be of great interest. The selection of 
this catalyst is due to the functionality of its Brønsted acid sites, high selectivity towards carbon 
species with less than 12 carbon atoms, limited deactivation by coking, and hydrothermal stability 
[40],[41]. Utilizing this zeolite has been found to dramatically change the composition of the bio-
oils, by both reducing the amounts of oxygenated compounds via deoxygenation reactions, and 
simultaneously increasing the aromatic species. A reduction in the oxygen content from 33 to 13% 
has been reported for an upgrading applying ZSM-5, where the oxygen removal was found to take 
place by conversion to H2O at lower temperatures and CO and CO2 at higher temperatures [42]. The 
formation of CO and CO2 is the preferred case, as more hydrogen would remain for the formation 
of hydrocarbons. Consequently, leading to a possible lowered carbon deposition on the zeolite, 
while simultaneously preventing an increased water content in the bio-oil. The oxygen removal, 
primarily in the form of water, comes from the stronger dehydration tendency of the ZSM-5 zeolite 
due to its strong acidity [43]. However, the strong acidity also leads to a decrease in the organic 
fraction (bio-oil) due to over cracking towards hydrocarbon gases or coke formation. The acid sites 
are therefore the essential part in the mechanism for both deoxygenation reactions and deactiva-
tion mechanisms [44].  
Because of the complexity of biomass-derived pyrolysis oil, the upgrading of this is rather difficult. 
Pointing out exactly which family of compounds that is contributing to the observed product is on 
the verge of impossible. It is furthermore difficult to establish suitable conditions for the catalytic 
process, given the different reactivities of the feed components. A division of the bio-oil into chem-
ical groups is therefore a common approach, and from there selecting a model compound that rep-
resents the functionality of that group. This method has been utilized by many for a better under-
standing of the reaction steps involved in the upgrading [39], [40], [45]–[50]. Some investigations 
have also been conducted on a mixture of model compounds [51], and on full pyrolysis oils [26], 
[41]–[43], [52]–[56].

5.1 Reactions and Mechanisms  
The high diversity of compounds in the feed leads to a complex reaction network when upgrading 
the bio-oil. Cracking, decarbonylation, decarboxylation, hydrocracking, hydrodeoxygenation, hydro-
genation, and polymerization are all reaction that have been reported to occur during the upgrad-
ing, with the cracking as the primary reaction [44]. General examples of these reactions are given in 
Figure 15, where it is noticeable which type of reaction that forms CO, CO2, and H2O. Furthermore, 
the formation of carbon is also of great significance. Coke can be formed from different pathways, 
which Figure 16 indicates. Here an overview of the process from biomass to oil derivative and then 
different cracking pathways are shown. Hydroxyacetone is selected as the product from the fast 
pyrolysis (partially dehydrated species in figure), and how this is reformed to either CO, CO2, H2O, 
coke, simple hydrocarbons, olefins, or aromatics. In these reactions hydrogen is a key species, which 
is produced through steam-reforming, dehydrogenation of the carbohydrates or hydrocarbons, wa-
ter gas shift, or decarbonylation of the partially dehydrated species. The hydrogen can then be con-
sumed in reactions that increases the H/Ceff ratio of the products and lead to formation of olefins 
and alkanes. 
 
Figure 15 - Examples of reactions associated with catalytic bio-oil upgrading [44]. 
The exchange of hydrogen is possible directly through hydrogen-transfer reactions between two 
hydrocarbon/carbohydrate chains, which occur on acid sites. Another possibility is through consec-
utive dehydrogenation/hydrogenation processes, that are greatly accelerated by the presence of a 
metal. Aromatics are also formed during the upgrading, possibly by Diels–Alder reactions of partially 
dehydrated/hydrogenated species [57]. For selectively producing olefins and aromatics, the dehy-
dration, hydrogen-forming, and hydrogen-transfer reactions must be properly balanced by choosing 
appropriate reaction conditions. A maximum production of intermediate hydrogen is needed, to 
ensure that the pathway that produces the maximum amount of olefins and aromatics from bio-
mass is followed. The maximum hydrogen available depends on what the released carbon is con-
verted into; the maximum yield of H2 increases in the order C < CO < CO2 [57]. 
==PAGE:39==
 
Page 22 of 99 
 
 
Figure 16 - Reaction pathways for the catalytic cracking of biomass-derived oxygenates [57]. 
The dehydration mechanism of ethanol over a zeolite was investigated by Chiang and Bhan [58] and 
is illustrated in Figure 17. The reaction is initiated by adsorption of ethanol on a Brønsted acid site. 
Two paths are then possible, either a decomposition route or a bimolecular monomer dehydration, 
respectively left and right path in Figure 17. The elimination of oxygen through decomposition and 
release of water, was determined to happen with a carbenium ion acting as a transition state. In 
this state a surface ethoxide is formed, which can desorb as ethylene and simultaneously regenerate 
the acid site by hydrogen-transfer. For the bimolecular monomer dehydration, a second ethanol 
molecule is needed, such that a second adsorption can lead to the formation a diethyl-ether on the 
catalyst. Chiang and Bhan [58] furthermore concluded that the pore structure of the zeolite controls 
the preference for which of the two routes is favored, where smaller pores favors the less bulky 
ethylene product. Pore sizes thereby control the product distribution, where deoxygenation of bio-
oils with medium pore size zeolites (approx. 5–6 Å) results in a production of increased C6 – C9 com-
pounds, and larger pores (approx. 6–8 Å) gives increased production of C9 – C12 [44]. It has further-
more been reported, that the decomposition reactions occurring in the zeolite are accompanied by 
oligomerization reactions. In total, these reactions produce a mixture of light aliphatic hydrocarbons 
and larger aromatic hydrocarbons. Where the oligomerizing reaction mechanism also has been 
found to be based on the formation of carbenium ions as intermediates. The formation of carbe-
nium ions is therefore essential in all of the relevant reaction mechanisms [44],[59]. An overall con-
ceptual complete deoxygenation reaction for upgrading with a zeolite normalized with respect to 
carbon can be characterized by:

5.2 Effect of temperature and residence time 
The reaction temperature during the upgrading is usually in the range from 300–600°C, atmospheric 
operating pressure, and a relatively high residence time to ensure a satisfying degree of deoxygen-
ation, i.e. LHSV around 2 h−1 [44]. The effect of the temperature on catalytic upgrading of bio-oil in 
a fixed bed reactor, when utilizing HZSM-5 as catalyst, was investigated by Williams et al. [60] in a 
temperature interval from 400–550°C. Mortensen et al. [44] treated their data and obtained the 
results illustrated in Figure 18. The investigation showed that, a high temperature was needed in 
order to decrease the oxygen content to a significant degree. 
 
Figure 18 – Comparison of oil and gas yields to the elemental oxygen content in the oil from a zeolite cracking process 
as a function of temperature. Experiments were performed with a HZSM-5 catalyst in a fixed bed reactor. Yields are 
given relative to the initial biomass fed [44].

Page 24 of 99 
 
However, increased temperatures resulted in decreased oil yields and increased gas yields. This ten-
dency can be explained by an increased rate of cracking reactions at higher temperatures, resulting 
in the formation of smaller volatile compounds. Controlling the degree of cracking is therefore of 
great importance for optimizing the oil yield, as a certain amount of cracking is required for remov-
ing oxygen, however it will decrease the oil yield. The effect of the residence time was investigated 
by Vitolo et al. [61]. It was reported that by increasing the residence time, the extent of carbon 
formation also increased. Finding the best compromise between deoxygenation and limiting the 
carbon formation is therefore needed. 
5.3 Kinetics 
The literature describing bio-oil cracking kinetics with the use of zeolites are limited. Instead, Adjaye 
and Bakshi [49],[50] used a series of model compounds to find the reaction network in the zeolite 
cracking, which is described by the sketch in Figure 19. It was suggested that as the initial step, the 
bio-oil separates in two fractions, a non-volatile and a volatile fraction. These two fractions are dif-
ferentiated by determining which molecules evaporates at 200°C under vacuum. The non-volatile 
fraction can then be converted into volatiles as an effect of the cracking reactions present. Besides 
being converted to volatiles, this fraction can either polymerize to form residue or condense/poly-
merize to form coke. The residue is here the fraction of the produced oil which does not evaporate 
during vacuum distillation at 200°C. The volatile fraction can, as the non-volatile fraction, be con-
verted into residue through polymerization. It is furthermore responsible for the formation of the 
three fractions in the final product: the oil fraction, aqueous fraction, and gas fraction. A last possible 
step is the formation of coke from polymerization/condensation of the oil fraction.

5.4 Deactivation 
When catalytically upgrading biomass pyrolysis oils, problems with excessive deactivation of the 
catalyst due to the formation of coke have been reported, along with operating problems of the 
reactor, as coke of thermal origin is deposited inside. The formation of the thermal coke is attributed 
to the condensation of phenolic components, especially those of high molecular weight, which can 
make up to 30 wt.% of the pyrolysis oil [40]. The coke deactivation of the zeolite is reversible, and 
can be removed by combustion, but under severe conditions, high temperatures and presence of 
steam, the silica-alumina matrix of the zeolite undergoes a dealumination process that decreases 
its activity upon reuse after regeneration. During the dealumination, the tetrahedral alumina in the 
framework is converted into so called partially distorted octahedral alumina atoms. These can dif-
fuse to the outer surface where they are converted into octahedrally coordinated alumina atoms 
detached from the framework, which are not acidic [39]. 
Coking is affected both by the Lewis and Brønsted acid sites in the zeolite and is increased with 
increasing acidity. The binding of species to the catalyst surface is done through the Lewis acid sites, 
while the Brønsted sites are working as proton-donor to the compounds of relevance, forming car-
bocations, which are believed to be responsible for the coke formation on the surface [44]. The 
carbon formation is in principle occurring through polymerization and condensation reactions, 
which in the end results in blockage of the pores in the zeolites. Guo et al. [62] investigated the coke 
precursors during upgrading of bio-oil over HZSM-5. It was found, that the precursors of coke on the 
outer surface of the catalyst were saturated aliphatic hydrocarbons, while aromatic hydrocarbons 
mainly were the precursor in the pores. These species expand during upgrading, resulting in block-
age and deactivation of the catalyst. Another investigation of the coke formation on HZSM-5 was 
conducted by Gayubo et al. [63] with synthetic bio-oil in a fixed bed reactor at 400–450°C with tem-
perature programmed oxidation (TPO) of the coke after deposition. They reported the formation of 
two types of carbon: thermal carbon and catalytic carbon. The thermal carbon was found in the 
macropores of the catalyst and described as equivalent to the depositions on the reactor walls and 
causes blocking of pores. The catalytic carbon was found in the micropores and was explained 
formed due to dehydrogenation, condensation, and hydrogen transfer reactions as discussed 
above, which deactivates the catalyst as a result of chemisorption on the acid sites. A lower hydro-
gen content was found in the catalytic carbon compared to the thermal carbon. Another type of 
deactivation was reported by Ramasamy et al. [64] when converting acetic acid in a fixed bed reactor 
at 360°C over HZSM-5. At these moderate temperatures the acetic acid generates the intermediate 
==PAGE:44==
 
Page 27 of 99 
 
acetate ion, which is adsorbed strongly on the active sites. This lead to a deactivation of the catalyst 
via active-site poisoning. 
In an attempt to decrease the extent of formed carbon on the catalyst, Zhu et al. [65] investigated 
the effect of co-feeding hydrogen during anisole upgrading at a H2/anisole molar ratio of 50 in a 
fixed bed reactor at 400°C over HZSM-5. It was found that only a slight decrease in the carbon for-
mation was achieved in the presence of hydrogen. The conversion of anisole as function of time on 
stream is shown in Figure 21. It was thought that adding hydrogen would promote the reaction with 
adsorbed carbenium ions to form paraffins. However, the effect of this was deemed insufficient to 
increase the lifetime of the catalyst to any significant degree. A similar conclusion was reached by 
Ausavasukhi et al. [45] in a study of deoxygenation of benzaldehyde over gallium-modified ZSM-5, 
where it was described that the presence of hydrogen did not influence the coke formation. How-
ever, in the presence of hydrogen, the gallium serves as hydrogen activating sites, which could par-
ticipate in hydrogenation reactions on the catalyst. A large change in yields of benzene and toluene 
was seen when comparing the results for runs utilizing either helium or hydrogen as carrier gas over 
the gallium-modified ZSM-5. When using helium, no toluene was detected, but changing to hydro-
gen increased the toluene yield to as high as 43.71% at 500°C. This effect was not seen for the non-
modified ZSM-5 [45].

5.5 Regeneration 
An important aspect of the catalytic upgrading process is regeneration after coking, etc., thereby 
trying to increase the lifetime of the catalyst. Vitolo et al. [66] investigated the regeneration of a 
HZSM-5 catalyst which had been used in a fixed bed reactor at 450°C and in a time span of 60–120 
==PAGE:45==
 
Page 28 of 99 
 
minutes with a feed of bio-oil. The regeneration consisted of washing of the catalyst with acetone 
followed by drying in air in a furnace at 500°C for 12 hours. However, a lower lifetime and deoxy-
genation degree was found for the regenerated catalyst relative to the fresh. After several regener-
ations this effect became more pronounced, and a decrease of 62% in the availability of acid sites 
over 5 regeneration cycles was determined. Since bio-oil in general has a high-water content, this 
deactivation could be speculated as dealumination, which then unavoidably occurs during zeolite 
cracking of bio-oil, thus making complete regeneration impossible. Another investigation on regen-
eration was conducted by Guo et al. [67] of a catalyst which had been operation in a fixed bed 
reactor with bio-oil as feed at 380°C, however no operating time was reported. Here the regenera-
tion of HZSM-5 was done at 600°C for a duration of 12 hours. After regeneration of the catalyst, 
tests showed an increased oxygen content in the upgraded product as a function of regeneration 
cycles, relative to the fresh. 21 wt.% oxygen was found in the product oil when utilizing the fresh 
catalyst, but after 5 regenerations the oxygen content increased to 30 wt.%. This increase in oxygen 
was suggested to be due to an observed decrease in the number of available acid sites on the cata-
lyst.  
Zhang et al. [54] examined the catalytic upgrading of vapors from fast pyrolysis of corn stover over 
a fresh, spent, and regenerated ZSM-5. The spent zeolite was prepared by using it in a fixed bed 
reactor at 400°C for 40 min, from where it was cooled, and dried at 120°C for 1 hour. The regener-
ated zeolite was obtained from the dried spent zeolite by combustion in air at 600°C for 2 hours. 
The study concluded that among all the parameters of: specific surface area, pore volume, and total 
acid sites (both weak and strong acid sites), the fresh zeolite had the highest values, and the spent 
had the lowest values (fresh > regenerated > spent). Furthermore, both the spent and regenerated 
zeolite lowered the total upgraded product vapor yields compared to the fresh (gas yield order: 
fresh > regenerated > spent). The highest coke yield was obtained for the fresh, while a lower yield 
for the regenerated, and the lowest for the spent was reported. Based on the results it was con-
cluded that pre-coking could be a method to minimize coke yield and promote the formation of 
target products. 
5.6 Product characteristics 
Gayubo et al. [39],[40] investigated the conversion of alcohols, phenols, aldehydes, ketones, and 
acids over HZSM-5 (Si/Al = 24). In Figure 22 an overview of the reaction paths for the different chem-
ical groups described below are provided. The studies showed that alcohols dehydrate rapidly to 
the corresponding olefins at low temperature, approx. 200°C, and that iso-alcohols dehydrate more 
rapidly than linear. Above 250°C, the olefins obtained in the dehydration were transformed into 
higher olefins, either butenes or C5+ olefins. At temperatures higher than 350°C, transformation of 
the olefins into C4+ paraffins and a small amount of aromatics were observed. Ethene, propene, and 
butenes were also final products obtained from the cracking of C5+ paraffins and aromatics. It was 
==PAGE:46==

therefore concluded that, the catalytic conversion of C3 and C4 alcohols over a HZSM-5 zeolite is 
complementary to the conversion of methanol and ethanol from the point of view of obtaining a 
high yield of light olefins or a high-octane index gasoline. Furthermore, it was shown that, phenol 
has a low reactivity on HZSM-5 and produces only small amounts of propylene and butenes at tem-
peratures above 250°C. The catalytic transformation of 2-methoxyphenol was found to have a low 
reactivity to hydrocarbons, but a considerable generation of thermal coke. These results indicate an 
improved upgrading if the phenols are removed prior to the catalytic conversion [39]. For the con-
version of acetone, it was found that, it first dehydrates and then undergoes disproportionation to 
i-butene, although more slowly than alcohols and at a higher temperature, approx. 250°C, and space 
times. The obtained i-butene then converts into C5+ olefins at temperatures above 350°C, which are 
further reacted to C5+ paraffins and aromatics. A much greater formation of aromatics was obtained 
from acetone than from alcohols. Ethene, propene, and butenes were also obtained at high tem-
peratures from cracking. Conversion of acetic acid resulted in an initial formation of acetone, by a 
complex reaction path, which then proceeds as explained before. At high conversion temperatures 
for acetic acid and acetone, around 400°C, the primary route for deoxygenation was observed to be 
decarboxylation, and to a lesser extent, dehydration. Acetaldehyde was found to have a low reac-
tivity over HZSM-5 similar to phenol, and only the trimer trimethyltrioxane and a low proportion of 
C6+ olefins was obtained from this transformation. The thermal degradation of acetaldehyde was 
furthermore reported to cause operating problems in the reactor due to thermal coke deposition. 
Removal of aldehydes prior to catalytic upgrading is, like for phenols, a possible method to improve 
the process. Aldehydes and phenols are economic interesting compounds in a purified form, which 
provide a reason for a pre-separation. Lastly, it was reported that the deactivation by coke was 
considerably higher from conversion of acetic acid and acetone than from alcohols [40].

6 Setup – Catalyst Testing Unit 
The following section contains a detailed description of the laboratory setup used during the exper-
imental part of this project. The laboratory is located at the Technical University of Denmark in 
building 229, and the setup is “137-5B Automated Catalyst Testing Unit for Automotive Catalysts”. 
6.1 Setup overview 
The laboratory setup has been built to test different types of catalysts at varying temperatures, with 
the possibility of making an automated sequence of steps, with respect to temperature changes and 
flow rates. For these experiments a specific quartz reactor has been made, which can be disassem-
bled while the main part remains in the hot furnace. Further details will be given in Section 6.2. A 
connection from a nitrogen flow through a bubble flask containing the vapor model compound has 
also been made for the purpose of evaporating the liquid and thereby transferring it to the gas 
phase. The complete setup consists of: 
• A gas dosing system for the addition of N2, He and NH3. 
• A “bubbling flask” containing the model compound. 
• An oven with three heating zones containing the quartz reactor. 
• Two condensation steps – the first at 4°C and the second at -60°C. 
• A diagnostic system for the analysis of gas composition. 
The PI diagram of the experimental setup is shown in Figure 23, and will be explained in detail in the 
following part. The setup is designed to be used at atmospheric pressure, with a safety pressure 
limit of 1.6 atm, where a safety valve is opened and the mass flow controllers (MFCs) are shut off. 
The control structure of the setup is built in "LabVIEW”. 
The setup is designed such that three different types of experiments can be performed: the catalytic 
upgrading, adsorption of NH3 and subsequently a temperature programmed desorption (TPD), and 
regeneration of the catalyst by coke burning. During an upgrading experiment, valve V1 is used to 
initially bypass the bubble flask to flush the system with nitrogen to remove any atmospheric air 
containing water that could condense in the dry ice bath. After an experiment, V1 is turned back to 
the flushing position to ensure that none of the evaporated model compound is still in the gas lines. 
When witching from an upgrading experiment to an ammonia TPD or regeneration experiment, 
valve V2 must be turned. This valve has been installed to minimize the risk of condensation of the 
model compound in any of the non-heated gas lines. The valve V8 must also be turned for flow to 
the NH3 analyzer when an ammonia TPD is performed, while for upgrading the two condensers must 
be connected for the collection of liquid product. For regeneration a connection directly to the gas 
filter can be made, skipping the two initial condensation steps. 
==PAGE:56==
 
Page 39 of 99 
 
 
Figure 23 – PI diagram for the experimental setup. 
The valve V3 is always open to both connections while the valves V4 and V5 are electronically con-
trolled. V4 works as the safety valve which is open when no experiment is running and closes by an 
input from the LabVIEW setup as V5 opens. V5 is thereby closed when an experiment has completely 
ended or if the pressure exceeds the safety value, where V4 then reopens. Heat tracing has been 
installed to certain lines to avoid condensation of either model compound, product, or ammonia. 
The first heating zone is from the bubble column to the end of the outgoing line from V2. This zone 
is controlled manually and will be kept at 50°C during the upgrading experiments. The second heat-
ing zone is from the ammonia mass flow controller to the connection with the outgoing line from 
V2, where the third heating zone starts and ends at the top of the oven. Heating zone 2 and 3, along 
with 4, which is from the bottom of the oven to the NH3 analyzer, are controlled electronically in 
LabVIEW where set points can be given individually. Heating zone 2 and 3 usually operate with the 
same set point. 
The oven contains three heating zones: RT1, RT2, and RT3. These operate from the same set point 
given in LabVIEW, but each has its own temperature measurement and individual heating. In Lab-
VIEW heating ramps can be sequenced such that a desired heating profile can be executed. A ther-
mocouple can also be inserted in the reactor from the bottom such that a temperature measure-
ment ~1cm below the bottom of the catalytic bed can be performed.  
6.2 Reactor 
Inside the oven a quartz reactor is placed which consist of three parts. These can be seen in Figure 
24. The first part is an outer tube, including the primary inlet tube at the top, which is visible just 
above the oven. At the end of the top inlet tube, the gas exits in radial direction through a number 
of small holes, which ensures good mixing with the gas from the other entry at the bottom of the 
==PAGE:57==
 
Page 40 of 99 
 
reactor. This piece is connected to the third part with a joint just below the furnace. The second part 
is an inner tube, where the upper tube is inserted into, with the catalytic bed holder at the bottom 
and a narrowing for the connection to the third piece. The third part is the bottom with a secondary 
inlet and the outlet. The secondary inlet allows for a gas flow from the bottom of the reactor to the 
top and from there entering the second part of the reactor above the gas nozzle. The gas then flows 
through the bed and down to the outlet. The features of the reactor can be seen in greater detail 
on the drawn model shown in Appendix C where the important elements in the reactor are pointed 
out. A smaller flow from the bottom inlet, compared to the top inlet, will be used during the exper-
iments, as it is used for dilution or to ensure that all gas from the top inlet is passing through the 
catalytic bed. When preparing the reactor for experiments, a weighted amount of zeolite is poured 
into the second part and placed directly on top of the bed holder. On top of the catalytic bed a layer 
of glass wool is placed to ensure that the bed stays even during the experiment.

6.3 Zeolites 
For the experimental work, three different types of microporous ZSM-5 zeolites were utilized, all of 
which were obtained from Zeolyst; CBV30, CBV55, and CBV80, which correspond to different Si/Al 
of 16.4, 29.5, and 39.3, respectively. Prior to use, all zeolites were calcined in a calcination oven 
under synthetic atmospheric air. A temperature ramp of 4°C/min up to 550°C was applied and the 
temperature was kept for 5 hours. The calcined samples were pelletized, crushed, and sieved to a 
sieve fraction of 250–600 μm. Before the three types of zeolites were used for upgrading, they all 
were treated with two different methods to change their characteristics. One treatment was simply 
steaming the zeolite for 5 hours at 500°C with a 30 vol% steam. The steam treatment removes some 
of the weakly bonded alumina atoms, thereby lowering the acidity of the zeolite. The steamed zeo-
lites are referred to as CBV30st, CBV55st, and CBV80st. 
Another treatment of the zeolites was an acid/alkaline wash which forms mesopores in the struc-
ture of the zeolite, such that it contains both the initial micropores and new mesopores. The alkaline 
conditions are used for Si leaching, while the acid conditions are used to remove Al debris resulting 
from the leaching process. Only zeolites that have been steamed will be used in this thesis, and the 
acid/alkaline wash is therefore conducted and then followed by steaming. The zeolites containing 
mesopores are referred to as mesoCBV30st, mesoCBV55st, and mesoCBV80st. One zeolite has been 
treated with a pore directing agent (PDA) during the acid/alkaline wash, causing a better distribution 
of the mesopores in the structure compared to the regular treatment. The zeolite treated with PDA 
is referred to as mesoCBV30st PDA. 
A more detailed overview of the mesoporous treatment of the zeolites and the resulting structures 
is given in Appendix E. 
6.4 Condensers 
Two condensers are placed at the setup for capturing of liquid product. The first condenser is a 
LAUDA E100 which utilizes a cooled water bath and has an operating temperature of 4°C. A metal 
container is placed in the water for the collection of the liquid, as shown in Figure 25. The outlet of 
the metal container is connected to the second condensation step. This step utilizes a dry ice bath 
with ethanol and has an operating temperature of approximately -60°C. Three glass condensers in 
series are placed in the ice bath to ensure that a maximum collection of liquid is achieved. The glass 
condensers contain a solvent which consists of isopropanol with heptane in a concentration of 0.1 
mol/L added as internal standard. The condensers are filled with an amount of solvent that ensures 
that the gas stream bubbles through the solvent, hence the level of liquid must be above the outlet 
of the inner tube in the condenser. The exact amount of solvent used is weighed before each exper-
iment.  A fourth glass condenser without any solvent is placed in a dry ice bath after the initial three 
to collect any entrained solvent. 
==PAGE:59==
 
Page 42 of 99 
 
 
Figure 25 – First condenser operating at 4°C with a metal container for the collection of liquid product. 
The three glass condensers containing solvent are shown in Figure 26, where they are placed in the 
isothermal bath holder. As mentioned above, flushing the system and containers with nitrogen be-
fore starting the cooling is important, as water will be condensed in these condensation steps and 
especially as ice in the dry ice condensation. Here ice is also observed on the outside of the glass 
condensers during operation, as moisture in the surrounding air freezes on the surface. This needs 
to be thoroughly removed before determining the amount of liquid collected in the glass condensers 
by weighing. However, the residual air in the glass condensers containing the solvent cannot be 
flushed without the condenser being cooled, as this will cause the solvent to evaporate. These con-
densers are therefore added to the sampling line when they are cooled properly and just before the 
experiment is started. Thorough care must be taken while these condensers are being cooled, as 
surrounding air can diffuse into the condensers and the moisture content freeze, giving an addi-
tional amount of water in the collected oil, and an offset in the mass balance. The inlet and outlet 
of the condensers are therefore plugged during cooling and the addition to the sampling line is done 
as quickly as possible.

6.5 Mass Flow Controllers 
There are seven mass flow controllers available at the setup which have all been calibrated using a 
bubble flow meter. The MFCs are shown in Figure 23 as arrowed boxes, where the arrow to the far 
left is MFC1 and going to the right follows MFC2 etc. It has been found that all MFCs have a higher 
maximum flow rate than expected, except MFC1 which is accurate. These offsets need to be taken 
into account when calculating flow rates and the expected gas compositions. A summary of the 
MFCs present at the setup is shown in Table 12, where the source of the gas is stated along with the 
measured maximum flow rate and the stated maximum of the MFC. 
MFC1 is used to add nitrogen for dilution of either ammonia or oxygen. It has a maximum flow rate 
of 500 Nml/min. The nitrogen flow is not heated before it reaches the point of mixing with ammonia. 
MFC2 is used to add oxygen and has a maximum flow rate of 558 Nml/min. It is used for regenera-
tion of the catalyst by burning the coke that will deposit during upgrading. The oxygen line is also 
not heated and is mixed with the nitrogen before it reaches a heated zone. 
MFC3 is used to add NH3 for the NH3 TPD experiments, and the line is heated all the way from the 
MFC to the inlet of the reactor. The temperature of the heat tracing is set to 60°C during upgrading 
experiments and 80°C for NH3 TPDs. The maximum flow rate has been found using nitrogen as cali-
bration gas and has a value of 548 Nml/min. 
MFC4 is used for adding nitrogen to the bubble flask and entrain the model compound into the gas 
phase or flushing the system after runs with the model compound. It has a maximum flow rate of 
1110 Nml/min. The heat tracing on this line starts after the bubble column, where it is added to 
avoid condensation and has an operating temperature of 50°C. 
MFC5 is used to add nitrogen for dilution in the reactor and has a maximum flow rate of 277 
Nml/min. It is attached to the secondary inlet at the bottom of the reactor and is included to ensure 
that all the flow from the top inlet is going through the bed.

6.6 Diagnostic system 
Multiple temperature, pressure, and flow measurements are performed at the setup and are logged 
on the attached computer, which is used for calculations. The flow leaving the reactor can be sent 
to two different gas analyzing systems: 
NH3/NO/NO2 analysis: Here the gas is sent through heat-traced pipes (80°C) and the ammonia con-
tent is analyzed. This analyzer is used during NH3 TPD experiments where the data is logged on the 
attached computer. 
CO/CO2/O2 and GC analysis: Here the gas is sent through the two condensation steps for collecting 
liquid product during upgrading. The gas must be water and particle free, hence a gas filter and a 
gas cooler are placed downstream of the condensation train. A laptop has been placed at the setup 
for the data logging from the CO/CO2/O2 analyzer and the micro-GC. 
In the following subsections, the operating principle of each analyzer will be described. 
6.6.1 Agilent 490 Micro GC 
A micro-GC from Agilent was used at the setup for measuring the gas composition in the produced 
gas from the upgrading process. Two columns have been installed in the GC, each with an individual 
pump, carrier gas, and TCD detector. The general principle of the TCD detector is to utilize the dif-
ference in thermal conductivity between the species in the gas and the carrier gas. The choice of 
carrier gas is therefore determined based on the species desired to be detected. The type of column 
can be chosen to achieve separation of the compounds of interest. 
One of the columns installed was a Molsieve 5Å (M5Å) 10m, which is designed to separate: hydro-
gen, carbon monoxide, methane, nitrogen, oxygen, and some noble gases. Using argon as the carrier 
gas enables the detection of hydrogen on this column. However, utilizing argon instead of helium 
as carrier gas gives a smaller peak for oxygen, leading to higher measurement uncertainty. The other 
column is a PoraPlot Q (PPQ) 10m, which is designed to separate: hydrocarbons C1-C6 and CO2. Here 
a peak overlap of nitrogen, oxygen, and methane will be seen, as well an overlap of ethylene and 
acetylene. However, separation of propylene and propane is achieved [69]. For this column helium 
has been chosen as carrier gas. 
The operating parameters of the two columns have been determined based on the suggested pa-
rameters from the user manual provided by Agilent [69], parameters used in the literature [5], and 
by investigating when an optimal separation between peaks was observed. The parameters used 
==PAGE:62==
 
Page 45 of 99 
 
for the two columns during this thesis are shown in Table 13. The pressure and injection time were 
determined based on the suggestion from the user manual, while the temperature was examined 
in more detail using different setpoints. The backflush is applied to keep the column clean, and only 
the M5Å column has a need for this option as CO2 can adsorb in the column and decrease the sep-
aration between peaks. The backflush time for the M5Å column was optimized by using a pure CO 
gas, such that the backflush is initialized shortly after the peak, as this is the last desired compound 
detected in this column. A run time (analysis time) of 600 s has been chosen as this gives approxi-
mately 1 sample every 10 minutes.

connected to a specific peak time, integrating the area under the peak to the baseline, and then 
giving the known concentration of the species. Automated calibration curves were then drawn and 
calculated by the GC software. 
6.6.2 NH3/NO/NO2 Analyzer 
An AO2020 Series Gas Analyzer from ABB of the model Limas11 HW is used at the setup to contin-
uously measure the concentration of NO, NO2 and NH3, where only the NH3 measurement is of in-
terest as no NOx is present during the TPD experiments. A maximum concentration of 500 ppm of 
ammonia can be measured by the analyzer. Calibration of the analyzer was done by first making a 
zero calibration at which helium was fed to the analyzer at a flow of 1 NL/min until a steady value 
was reached. The zero calibration was conducted with helium as this is used during the NH3 TPD 
experiments for indicating the baseline (zero concentration of NH3). Thereafter a span calibration 
was performed. Here ammonia was mixed with nitrogen to give a specific concentration based on 
calculations from the calibration of the MFCs. The span calibration was done at concentration levels 
of 200, 300, and 400 ppm. The analyzer operates with an inlet temperature of minimum 100°C and 
uses ultraviolet light to measure the NH3 concentration.

6.6.3 CO/CO2/O2 Analyzer 
An NGA 2000 MLT analyzer from Rosemount was connected to the setup for continuous measure-

6.7 Liquid product characterization 
The condensed liquids from the two condensers are recovered after upgrading experiments and 
stored in a refrigerator to eliminate any potential ageing reactions. To characterize the content in 
the produced liquid, multiple analysis methods have been used. The composition in the upgraded 
bio-oil was analyzed using a GC-MS coupled with FID, while the water fraction in the oil is deter-
mined by Karl-Fisher titration. In the following subsections the operating principle of each analyzer 
will be described. 
6.7.1 GC-MS/FID 
Analysis of the bio-oil composition was done by using a GC-MS/FID Shimadzu QP 2010 Ultra appa-
ratus equipped with a Supelco Equity 5 column. Identification and quantification of the samples was 
performed by the mass spectrometer (MS) and flame ionization detector (FID), respectively. The 
initial temperature for the GC column was held at 80˚C for 10 minutes. The column was then heated 
to 250˚C with an initial heating rate of 2˚C/min up to 100°C followed by an increased heating rate 
of 5°C/min. The final temperature was held for 5 minutes. A split ratio of 80 was used in the injection 
section. The MS scanning was set to a range of 20 to 300 m/z. The MS was intentionally turned off 
between 2.57 to 2.65 minutes in order to avoid saturation by high concentrations of the solvent.

6.7.3 Karl-Fischer titration 
The instrument used for the water content determination was a 701 KF Titrino. The titrant utilized 
by the instrument is Hydranal Composite 5, which determines the water content in the collected 
bio-oil based on the iodometric titration of sulphur dioxide in water as shown in the reaction below: 
 
I2 + SO2 + 2 H2O ⇌2 HI + H2SO4 
(12) 
The equilibrium is shifted to the right by neutralizing the acid with an Imidazole buffer. The complete 
reaction involves the solvent which contains methanol. The complete reaction scheme for the Karl-
Fischer titration consists of the two following reactions: 
 
CH3OH + SO2 + RN ⟶[RNH]SO3CH3 
(13) 
 
I2 + [RNH]SO3CH3 + H2O + RN ⟶[RNH]SO4CH3 + 2 [RNH]I 
(14) 
Here RN is the base used in the mixture. 
However, when utilizing a solvent containing methanol for the Karl-Fischer titration, aldehydes and 
ketones can react with the methanol to from acetals and ketals respectively with the release of 
water [72]: 
Aldehyde: 
RHC = O + 2 CH3OH →Acetal; RHC(OCH3)2 + H2O 
(15) 
Ketone: 
R2C = O + 2 CH3OH →Ketal; R2C(OCH3)2 + H2O 
(16) 
The reactivity of the two types of compounds decreases as the chain length increases, with alde-
hydes being the more reactive type. For aldehydes, acetaldehyde reacts the quickest and for ke-
tones, acetone and cyclohexanone forms ketal most rapidly [72]. This side reaction for ketones give 
rise to a considerable uncertainty, as the model compound chosen for this study is hydroxyacetone 
(HA). An overdetermination of the amount of water in the feed may occur as a fraction of the HA 
may react with the methanol. Furthermore, if any unconverted HA is collected in the alcohol solvent, 
this could also react with the isopropanol to form water. Proper cooling of the samples is therefore 
needed to avoid this side reaction. However, the ketal formation rate is influenced by the chain 
length of the alcohol present, with the smallest chain giving the highest reactivity [72], hence any 
reaction with isopropanol would most likely be negligible. This side reaction has the potential to 
cause an uncertainty in the water content of the feed and has only a minor influence on the col-
lected oil.

fraction was clear while the heavy fraction had become more intensely yellow. The change in feed 
was done due to discoloring of the first feed after it had been used for several times, and the second 
due to suspicion of a too high degree of polymerization. Cleaning by rotary evaporation was done

7 Experimental sequencing  
All of the three types of experiments that are run on the setup described above are controlled by a 
series of commands, which are collected in a sequence in the LabVIEW control program. For the 
upgrading experiments the sequence is simply to heat the reactor to the desired temperature and 
holding this temperature for a desired amount of time before cooling down. The sequences for the 
ammonia TPD and the coke burning contains more steps and will be described in the two following 
sections. 
7.1 Ammonia TPD 
A sequence for the ammonia TPD experiments has been developed based on some initial experi-
ments with different zeolites and applied amounts. Initially, an amount of 1 g of zeolite was used 
along with a heating ramp of 10°C/min in a helium flow of 500 Nml/min to a maximum temperature 
of 550°C after NH3 adsorption at 70°C. This resulted in concentrations of NH3 above the measurable 
range of the analyzer (>500 ppm). The amount of zeolite was therefore decreased to 0.5 g, and the 
helium flow was increased to 1000 Nml/min. This resulted in some overlapping of the expected 
concentration peaks, with the concentration still being too high. The heating rate was therefore 
lowered to 5°C/min and the helium flow kept at 1000 Nml/min, while including a gas flow to the 
bottom inlet of the reactor in order to ensure no hold up of ammonia in the reactor. Utilizing these 
parameters led to better results and the possibility of using 1 g of zeolite for the upgrading experi-
ments. An overview of the final sequence used for the ammonia TPD experiments is given in Table 
16. Step 1-3 are included to remove any undesired compounds adsorbed on the catalyst during 
upgrading before the NH3 adsorption/desorption steps, while step 9 is included for flushing. The 
starting temperature in step 1 is defined as 0°C as LabVIEW operates with a requested temperature, 
which defines the heating ramp. This requested temperature starts from 0°C by default. The true 
starting temperature varies depending on the experiment run previously, however the heating 
starts when the requested temperature reaches the temperature of the heating zone in the oven.

8 Experimental results 
In this section a description of the results obtained during the experimental work will be given, along 
with a discussion of the results. For all the upgrading experiments a fresh amount of zeolite was 
weighed and placed in the reactor. The two parts containing the catalyst bed holder and the outlet 
of the quartz reactor were cleaned in acetone and dried before an upgrading experiment was con-
ducted. 
8.1 Hydroxyacetone mass balance 
The model compound used for upgrading was chosen to be hydroxyacetone, which has the mass 
composition; 48.64% C, 8.16% H, and 43.20% O. This compound closely matches the elemental com-
position of bio-oil in terms of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen, see Table 6. The mass balance of hy-
droxyacetone was investigated at room temperature over the reactor as an initial experiment. This 
was done to ensure that all the evaporated mass from the bubble column was captured after the 
reactor. Initially, no heat was added to the bubble column. 1 Nl/min of nitrogen was bubbled 
through the HA for 10 hours resulting in an evaporated amount of 10.59 g. During the experiment 
a flow of 0.1 Nl/min of nitrogen was also sent to the bottom inlet of the reactor. These flows of 
nitrogen will be utilized for all the empty reactor tests and upgrading experiments. Out of the 10.59 
g evaporated, 4.39 g was captured in the metal condenser at 4°C and 2.68 g was collected in the 
three glass condensers at -60°C. During this experiment the isopropanol solvent was not used. Most 
of the 2.68 g was captured in the first of the three glass condensers and only a few drops in the 
second and a negligible amount in the last. A total amount of 3.52 g (33.2%) was therefore not 
collected in the condensation train, giving indications of a leak in the system. Valve V7 was therefore 
installed for the purpose of performing pressure tests before each experiment to ensure that the 
reactor is tightly connected to the inlets and outlet. The joint between the top and the bottom piece 
of the reactor was as a consequence of this experiment greased with a silicone paste to minimize 
possible leaks from the reactor. 
A second investigation of the mass balance was conducted at a reactor temperature of 500°C where 
the heating bath temperature was raised to 30°C in order to achieve a higher feeding concentration 
of hydroxyacetone. No solvent was utilized during this experiment. The flow of nitrogen through 
the HA was kept for exactly 5 hours. The higher temperature increased the evaporation of liquid 
from the previous 1.06 g/hour to 1.40 g/hour, giving a total amount of 7.01 g evaporated. For this 
test run a collection of 3.93 g was observed in the metal condenser (4°C) and 2.22 g in the dry ice 
bath (-60°C). An increase in the level of CO and CO2 was also measured at the CO/CO2/O2 analyzer, 
where a total amount of 0.0268 g was detected during the 5 hours. This gave an amount of 0.833 g 
(11.9%) product not detected. No investigation of hydrocarbons in the gas phase was done for this 
experiment.

a bio-oil only to a small degree comes from small ketone molecules. 
Late in the experimental work variations in the water content of the feed were observed. It was 
measured by the Karl-Fischer titration that the water content of the feed was decreasing as the feed 
was used, indicating that relatively more water was evaporated from the bubble flask than hydrox-
yacetone. This also correlates with a 12 times higher vapor pressure of water (3.17 kPa at 25°C) 
comparted to hydroxyacetone (0.255 kPa at 25°C [73]). The actual amount of water fed, and thereby 
the actual amount of dry HA fed, has thereby not been determined accurately as a steady feed rate 
with the initial water content was assumed. This increased amount of water fed corresponds to a 
lower achieved HA:C ratio and an increased level of fed water in the collected liquid, thereby affect-
ing the calculated amount of reaction water. The calculated amount of reaction water is higher than 
the actual value seen in Figure 27 to Figure 29, as all the “extra” water fed appears in the H2Osample 
term in Eq.(17), but not in the H2Ofeed term. Furthermore, yields of oil, gas, and coke would be higher 
than determined and indicated in Figure 27 to Figure 29 since the amount of hydroxyacetone is 
lower than assumed. However, as the water is depleted from the feed, a higher fraction of the fed 
amount will be hydroxyacetone as the feed is becoming pure HA. In the beginning of the experi-
mental work the feed was used for a longer period, with occasional refilling of the bubble flask. 
Estimation of the actual amount of water evaporated during these experiments has not been pos-
sible as the water content is expected to decrease nonlinearly, the length of the individual experi-
ment varies, and the feed stock was found to polymerize during use and storage. This changing 
water content in the feed is deemed to be the reason for the observation of no clear decrease in 
the yield of water and gas, and an increasing yield of oil as the HA:C ratio increases. These trends 
were however expected to be observed, since increasing HA:C ratios leads to a larger degree of 
deactivation of the zeolite, resulting in increasing amounts of unconverted hydroxyacetone.

As mentioned in section 7.2, the coke formed from the upgrading is burned off in 2 vol% oxygen

of CO and CO2 released continuously during the burning, and thereby determining the total amount 
of carbon deposited in the reactor. Initially, the measured flow in Nl/min (V) is converted to a molar

8.4 Insight to the acidity of the zeolites

8.5 Gas phase composition 
The gas phase composition was measured during the deoxygenation of hydroxyacetone over the 
different types of zeolites. However, problems with the micro-GC attached to the setup occurred 
during the initial phase of the experimental work allowing only one of the GC columns to be utilized. 
A pump was therefore installed after the safety condenser seen on Figure 23, allowing sampling of 
a small gas stream, which was collected in a gas bag and then analyzed at a gas chromatograph 
(Thermo Scientific refinery gas analyzer, Trace 1300/1310) with a flame ionization detector (FID) 
and two thermal conductivity detectors (TCD). The gas composition of the collected gas bags was 
analyzed for H2, N2, CO, CO2, C1 to C5, and C6+ hydrocarbons and Chromeleon Chromatography Stu-
dio software was used for analysis of the chromatograms. The gas bags were attached for 30-minute 
intervals in order to obtain an average gas concentration for each interval. The determined HA:C 
ratio for each bag is therefore also the average value between the start of the sampling and the end.

9 Future work 
During this thesis a large effort has been put into modifying the setup for the purpose of using it for 
hydroxyacetone upgrading and screening of the functionality of different zeolites. The future work 
that could be performed is therefore divided into improvements that can be made on the setup and 
the types of experiments that could be conducted. 
9.1 Setup improvements 
9.1.1 Separate feeding line for model compound 
As discussed in section 8.2, the model compound was found to polymerize. This polymerization has 
the potential to form some heavy compounds that can stick to the inside of the tubes which then 
need to be cleaned with acetone. Adding a separate feeding line for the model compound would 
make cleaning of the line easier, but also by making the line shorter and close to the reactor inlet

would lower the extent of polymerization. The addition of this feeding line could be implemented 
by moving valve V2 to just before the top inlet of the reactor and attaching the other gas line going 
to V2 to the mixing zone with the heated ammonia line. Additional heat tracing would be required 
for this new line and for the shortest possible line, a holder for the heating bath would be needed 
near the reactor inlet. This is however located in a height of 2 meters, making a compromise be-
tween shortest possible feeding line and comfortable working height necessary. 
9.1.2 Different outlet on reactor 
After each upgrading, some heavier products were found sticking to the inside walls of the reactor 
outlet. The outlet of the reactor is located approximately 10 cm below the oven and had a temper-
ature around 50°C resulting in condensation of heavier molecules. Some heat tracing and isolation 
was added to the outlet, but the effect was only minor. Making a small reduction in the reactor 
length would locate the outlet closer to the oven, which could decrease the amount of product 
sticking to the walls. Changing the shape of the outlet to a straight drop, instead of the 90° angle, 
could also shorten the outlet, and make it possible for a more straight and shorter line to the first 
condenser. However, removing the 90° angle, removes the location for the insertion of the thermo-
couple that measures the temperature below the catalytic bed. A new location for this thermocou-
ple most therefore be found, to ensure that a temperature measurement close to the bed is made. 
9.1.3 Additional condensation step 
During the upgrading experiments, when evaporating larger amounts of hydroxyacetone, problems 
with formation of ice in the first of the glass condensers in the dry ice bath was discovered. This 
resulted in an increasing pressure in the system, which over time could affect the feeding rate. Add-
ing a condensation step between the 4°C step and the dry ice bath with a temperature of 1°C, could 
capture more water and in best case prevent the clogging due to ice, but at least delay it to an extent 
that experiments with a higher HA:C ratio could be conducted. 
9.1.4 Moisture sensor 
As mentioned in earlier sections, determining the exact amount of water that has evaporated from 
the feed was found to be difficult. Determining the water content of the feed before and after use 
provides a better insight into the water evaporation, however, not a completely exact insight due 
to the continuous polymerization. Installing a moisture sensor on the feed line just before the top 
inlet could give continuous measurements of the water content of the feed going into the reactor. 
A more exact HA:C ratio could thereby be determined, resulting in a more correct yield of the or-
ganic products from the calculations performed. However, it needs to be tested whether the pres-
ence of the model compound interferes with the moisture measurements.

9.2 Experimental 
A large part of the experimental work conducted in this thesis was invested in screening different 
types of zeolites, however a variety of other investigations to get a better insight into the perfor-
mance of a chosen zeolite could also be performed. In the following some ideas for further experi-
mental work are given. 
9.2.1 Upgrading to higher HA:C ratios 
As no stabilization in the formation of coke was seen in this study, increasing the HA:C ratio further 
could provide information of when the activity of the zeolites drops completely. This also provides 
knowledge about the effect of the treatments of the zeolites towards their tolerance and formation 
of coke. Changes in the compounds found in the produced gas and liquid phase could be examined, 
giving indications of further development in selectivity as the HA:C ratio increases. Lastly, investi-
gating the acidity for higher HA:C ratio could give information about the importance of the different 
sites, if both the strong and weak sites deactivate completely or only one type does, and how this 
affects the selectivity. 
9.2.2 Expand the experiments of zeolites with the PDA 
Only one type of zeolite treated with the PDA was examined during this thesis and some interesting 
tendencies compared to the other zeolites tested were found. Conducting experiments for other 
types of zeolites treated with the PDA in a wide HA:C range would therefore be of interest to 
broaden the understanding of the effect of this treatment. 
9.2.3 Regeneration and the effect on acidity 
All the zeolites used in this thesis were regenerated after upgrading, and the possibility of conduct-
ing an NH3 TPD directly after the coke burning is a possibility with the setup used. This could give an 
insight into the effect of the regeneration, and if all acid sites are regained completely or if perma-
nent deactivation has occurred by the upgrading/regeneration cycle and to what extent. Utilizing 
this regenerated zeolite for upgrading, conducting a new NH3 TPD, regenerating it, performing an-
other TPD, and repeating this a couple of times could be turned into a complete study, as conducting 
the NH3 TPDs are very time consuming. Looking into the effect of partial regeneration of the zeolites 
could also be of interest to examine. 
9.2.4 Effect of temperature 
Only one temperature was used during the upgrading experiments in this thesis. By conducting sim-
ilar upgrading experiments at lower and higher temperatures would allow an understanding of the 
effect of the upgrading temperature on yields and selectivity. 
 
==PAGE:102==
 
Page 85 of 99 
 
9.2.5 Examine a new model compound 
During this thesis only one model compound was examined. However, the design of the setup allows 
for the examination of various model compounds and conducting studies with different compounds 
could give an insight into the effect on coking and the resulting product distribution from different 
chemical groups present in the raw bio-oil. Conducting experiments with a mixture of two or more 
compounds could be conducted to investigate whether the presence of different groups interferes 
with the results observed for the individual compounds.

Conclusion 
The purpose of this master thesis project was to investigate the effect of catalytic deoxygenation of 
a biomass pyrolysis vapor model compound with zeolites. At first, a literature survey was made from 
where a general knowledge of biomass, the effect of pyrolysis of this biomass, and the effect of a 
catalytic treatment of the pyrolysis oil was obtained. The literature survey was focused on 
knowledge about the specific zeolite H-ZSM-5, and the characteristics of this catalyst. 
A setup was modified for the purpose of utilizing it for the experimental work conducted in this 
thesis. The addition of two condensation steps played a crucial role in the deoxygenation experi-
ments, while connections to the different analyzers was vital for the gas phase determination, re-
generation, and determination of acidity. 
An empty reactor test at 450°C was conducted to examine the effect of temperature. From this test 
it was concluded that thermal cracking was occurring to a minor extent, as less than 0.5 wt% of the 
fed hydroxyacetone was determined as gas and coke. From the GC-MS/FID analysis it was further-
more determined that the captured liquid product consisted of 98% HA and a small amount (~2%) 
of ethers. 
For the deoxygenation experiments seven different zeolites were tested; three types of CBV30, two 
types of CBV55, and two types of CBV80. It was observed that the catalytic upgrading of hydroxy-
acetone resulted in high gas yield, which roughly was between 40 and 50 wt%, with the mesoporous 
zeolites producing the highest amounts. The yield of water was found to be higher than the yield of 
oil for many of the deoxygenations with no clear trends in the yields as the HA:C ratios changed. A 
changing water content in the feed was discovered and deemed the reason for the observation of 
no clear trend in the yields of water, oil, and gas, which were expected to decrease for the water 
and gas and increase for the oil as the HA:C ratio increases. These trends were expected to be ob-
served as increasing HA:C ratios leads to higher deactivation of the zeolite, resulting in higher 
amounts of unconverted hydroxyacetone. However, correcting for the true amount of water fed, 
showed increased yields of gas, oil, and coke, with an approximate decrease in the calculated HA:C 
ratio of 20%. A coke yield of less than 2.5 wt% was found for all experiments, thus indicating that 
the formation of coke when upgrading pyrolysis vapor compounds only to a small degree comes 
from small ketone molecules, as this value is much lower than values found in literature. 
After each deoxygenation experiment a regeneration of the zeolites was conducted, where the re-
lease of CO and CO2 was measured continuously. The obtained values for the concentrations were 
then converted to molar flows from which the mass of carbon could be determined. A high rate of 
coking was observed initially for all the zeolites, with a decreasing rate as the HA:C ratios increased 
from 2 to 6 and from 6 to 12. However, the mesoCBV30st PDA was observed to keep a steady rate 
of coking up to HA:C = 6, indicating an increased tolerance towards deactivation by coking. This 
==PAGE:104==
 
Page 87 of 99 
 
increased tolerance was attributed to the increased distribution of pores when treated with the 
PDA. Differences in the release of CO and CO2 between the zeolites during the combustion of coke 
were observed. It was found that the mesoporous zeolites release a higher ratio of CO2 compared 
to the microporous ones, indicating that the introduction of mesopores results in a more distributed 
and easier combustible coke. 
Ammonia TPDs were conducted for fresh zeolites and after each upgrading experiment to deter-
mine their change in acidity. The effect on the acidity of the different treatments was investigated 
and it was found that the acid/alkaline wash for generation of mesopores increases the total acidity 
of the zeolite without significantly affecting the temperature of the desorption peak maxima. 
Steaming the zeolite was observed to decrease the acidity, but also shift the desorption peak max-
ima to a lower temperature for both the mesoporous and microporous zeolites. The treatment with 
the PDA was found to result in two peaks with an almost equal maximum value, and with a larger 
fraction of the total acidity being strong acid sites. From the conducted ammonia TPDs after each 
deoxygenation experiment it was found that the total acidity drops as more coke is deposited on 
the catalyst with reduced slopes of decrease. It was furthermore found, that the mesoporous zeo-
lites have an increased tolerance towards deactivation from coking, as more coke was deposited on 
these zeolites while keeping a higher acidity relative to their starting acidity. 
During the deoxygenation of hydroxyacetone over the different types of zeolites, measurements of 
the gas phase composition were conducted. From the analysis it was determined that the largest 
contribution to the gas phase was from carbon monoxide and ethene, with these two species in 
general representing 30 wt% of the product from the catalytic upgrading. Comparison of the ob-
tained gas yields at similar HA:C ratios for the microporous zeolites with their mesoporous counter-
part, showed that the mesoporous zeolites have an increased yield of ethene and CO. While the 
microporous zeolites have a higher yield of C3 olefins, except for the CBV80 types at a HA:C ratio of 
2, where the mesoporous CBV80 has a 18% higher yield. No remarkable difference in the C1-C3 
alkanes and C4+ yields were observed as an effect of the different treatments; however, an in-
creased yield as the HA:C ratio was increased to 6, with similar values at a HA:C ratio of 12, was 
determined. The CO2 yields were found to be higher for the microporous zeolites, comparted to 
their mesoporous counterparts, except for the CBV80 types. 
Analysis of the collected liquid products from the different deoxygenation experiments of hydroxy-
acetone were examined by using a GC-MS/FID. From the obtained chromatograms it was deter-
mined that the majority of the compounds produced were found at retention times below 10 
minutes. The compounds found in this retention time interval were mostly monoaromatics, furans, 
ketones, and ethers. At higher retention times, small peaks of branched cyclic aliphatics, and differ-
ent structures of indane and indene were observed. From comparison of the microporous zeolites 
with their mesoporous counterpart, it was found that the introduced mesopores appear to delay 
the breakthrough of hydroxyacetone. Besides the breakthrough of the reactant towards higher HA:C 
==PAGE:105==
 
Page 88 of 99 
 
ratios, an increased concentration of other oxygen containing compounds were also observed. At a 
HA:C ratio of 6 an increased amount of ethers, with yields of 0.1 to 0.5 wt%, and esters (up to 1.1 
wt%) were determined. Comparison of the monoaromatics yield showed that the microporous 
CBV30 and CBV55 had higher yields than their mesoporous counterpart, while the mesoporous 
CBV80 showed higher yields than its microporous version. This trend was attributed to the level of 
strong acid sites on the zeolites, as a decreasing level of strong acid sites was found to result in a 
lowered yield of monoaromatics. A high fraction of strong acid sites with a moderate total acidity 
was found to result in increased yields of monoaromatics when deoxygenating small ketones with 
the ZSM-5 zeolite. This can be attributed to a lower propensity of coking at moderate total acidity 
while a certain extent of strong acid sites is necessary for the catalytic conversion of cracked hy-
droxyacetone fragments to monoaromatics.

Appendix E 
Preparation of mesoporous zeolites starting from different Si/Al ratios of 16.4, 29.5 and 39.3 has been per-
formed by modifications starting with the ammonium form of the as-received zeolite powders. Table 26 gives 
an overview of the treatment steps for the preparation of hierarchical ZSM-5 from the CBV30, CBV55 and 
CBV80 starting material, respectively. The order of treatment steps is indicated in the sample name: The 
number before “B” refers to the molar concentration of the NaOH base used during the Si leaching. The 
number in front of “A” states the molar concentration used for the conducted HCl acid wash in order to 
remove Al debris resulting from the leaching process, which can block access to micropores. For the more 
acidic CBV30 zeolite, the desilication is more difficult due to the high Al concentration and treatment with 
higher base strength of 0.6 mol/l was conducted. A sample of the CBV30 starting material has been tested 
with a 1:1 mixture of 0.6 mol/l NaOH and 0.2 mol/l of the pore directing agent tetrapropylammonium hy-
droxide (TPAOH). This sample is denoted “0.6B+0.2PDA/0.06A”. Due to the expected reduced kinetic of de-
silication by the addition of the pore directing agent, a longer treatment time of 60 min was used.  
Table 27 gives an overview of the physical and chemical properties of the microporous and mesoporous ZSM-
5. It should be noted that for the calculation of the microporous volume only the pores in the pore size 
distribution below 7 Å are taken into account, even though the range of micropores is often assigned to pore 
width of 0 – 20 Å. The peak of pores plotted in the range 7-20 Å has been suggested to constitute an artifact 
associated with phase transition of Ar and is therefore excluded in the calculation of the volume of mi-
cropores and only the peak around ~5.5 Å is taken into account. It should also be noted that Ar-physisorption 
only allows to probe the lower range of mesopores up to ~300 Å. Using N2 physisorption allows to resolve 
the full range of mesopores up to 500 Å. Table 27 indicates both the lower mesoporous range as probed by 
Ar-physisorption as well as the full range probed by N2. In general, the generation of well-distributed, smaller 
mesopores is more desirable since it yields enhanced diffusion characteristic with little loss of microporous 
volume. In contrast, large mesoporous voids replace a considerable amount of microporous material. In ad-
dition, microporous material in between large mesoporous may still face diffusion limitations. Comparing 
BET surface area it can be observed that for microporous ZSM-5 the Ar-physisorption suggests a slightly larger 
BET surface area compared to N2 physisorption, while for mesoporous ZSM-5 both smaller and higher BET 
surface areas result. The accessible pore volume more than doubles for some of the mesoporous zeolites 
compared to their microporous counterparts. The introduction of well-distributed mesopores can lead to a 
six-fold increase in the ratio of mesoporous to microporous volume compared to the starting zeolite material, 
as observed for the mesoporous CBV30. In contrast, a poor quality of mesopores as observed for 
CBV55/0.02B/0.02A LB may only result in a slight increase of this ratio from 0.55 to 0.77.
