Abstract 
 
Currently, the food system, which includes those processes from production, aggregation, processing, distribution, consumption, 
and disposal of food products, is unsustainable. This unsustainability is driven by factors such as climate change, resource scarcity, 
pollution, water consumption, and loss of biodiversity due to food production as well as malnutrition and diet-related non-
communicable diseases. Many studies have identified that to obtain a sustainable food system, one of the main actions that needs 
to be carried out is to shift our dietary preferences. Animal-based products are one of the main drivers of this unsustainability, due 
to the huge amounts of resources needed for their production. In this context, plant-based solutions are those food products that 
are expected to substitute animal-based products such as meat, milk, cheese, or yogurts.  
 
This master thesis studies how shifting from dairy to plant-based yogurts can provide a more sustainable food system. This 
sustainability is studied by calculating both the health and environmental impact of soy, coconut, oat, and peanut yogurt compared 
to dairy yogurts. Cradle-to-gate life cycle assessment is used to calculate the environmental impact of producing these plant-based 
yogurts. The impact categories analyzed are climate change, land use, water consumption, and biodiversity loss. EcoInvent 3.7.1 
database processes are used for this purpose. Moreover, the dairy yogurt environmental impact is already obtained from this 
database. The health impact is measured by calculating the amount of calcium, protein, the Nutrient Profiling, and the Health 
Nutrient Index of each yogurt type. To define if shifting from dairy to each plant-based yogurt is a sustainable option, three criteria 
are used:  
 
Criteria 1: Food consumed should always provide health benefits to be sustainable. 
Criteria 2: When shifting from dairy to plant-based options, if benefits are obtained both for health and for the environment, plant-
based yogurts are defined as more sustainable.  
Criteria 3: When shifting from dairy to plant-based options, if there are no health or environmental benefits, the environmental price 
per health benefit is calculated. If the environmental price per health benefit is lower for plant-based than for dairy yogurts, there is 
the potential for being a more sustainable option. 
 
Results from this master thesis demonstrate that plant-based yogurts do have the potential to be a more sustainable option than 
dairy yogurts. This sustainability is driven by the fact that all plant-based yogurts' environmental impact is lower than the dairy-
yogurts one, while health performance varies depending on the impact category. However, current recipes and their results vary 
depending on the plant-based yogurt type and health methodology used. For this reason, it is needed to guarantee a high calcium 
content added to plant-based yogurts as well as a low number of sugars and sodium. Moreover, this report does only represent a 
finite number of yogurts, so certain plant-based yogurts recipes might not comply with the criteria of this report.  When it comes to 
global sustainability, yogurts do not represent a key element when trying to reduce the environmental impact of the food system. 
For this reason, even though shifting from dairy to plant-based yogurts might be a more sustainable customer choice, it does not 
guarantee a huge impact on the global food system.

1. Introduction. Sustainability in the food system. 
 
"The biggest driver of nature loss in the world today and for some time has been food production and 
consumption" 
 
This statement was said in June 2022 by the Director of WWF Marco Lambertini (1). But it is not only him, many international 
organizations such as the United Nations are also stating that the current environmental impact of our diets is huge, and a 
change needs to be done to have a sustainable food system (2). But what does it mean? And which ones are the main drivers 
of this unsustainability? 
 
As defined by the European Commission, food systems include all the activities and actors involved in the production, 
aggregation, processing, distribution, consumption, and disposal of food products (3).  According to FAO, a sustainable food 
system should deliver food security and nutrition for all in a way that the economic, social, and environmental bases needed to 
provide this security and nutrition are guaranteed for future generations. Figure 1 represents the main elements that a 
sustainable food system should contribute to (4).  As it can be seen, sustainable food systems should have a positive impact 
not only on the environment but also on the society and the economy.  
 
 
 
Figure 1. Sustainable food system. Main elements. 
  
 
As FAO states in its Sustainable Healthy Diets Guiding Principles (2019) (5): 
 
” Two of the major challenges of our times are malnutrition in all its forms and the degradation of environmental and 
natural resources. Both are happening at an accelerated pace” 
 
This problem has been studied and handled by most international organizations over the past years.  The United Nations (UN) 
Food Systems Summit and N4G meetings in 2021 it was reflected a growing international awareness of the current policies, 
and it was contemplated a need for urgent reforms to deliver sustainable healthy diets (6). In 2022 the focus of the World Food 
==PAGE:9==
   
 
 
Page 8 of 90 
 
Summit was on climate change and how the global food system is affected by global warming. During the World Food Summit 
2 days conferences, topics such as sustainable agriculture or sustainable diets were analyzed by industry experts.  
 
In this context, the global perspective is clear, the current food systems are unsustainable. This is stated by FOOD 2030, the 
EU's research and innovation policy to transform food systems and ensure everyone has enough affordable, nutritious food to 
lead a healthy life. FOOD 2030 states that this unsustainability is driven by factors such as climate change, resource scarcity, 
pollution, water consumption, and loss of biodiversity due to food production as well as malnutrition and diet-related non-
communicable diseases. Population growth also needs to be considered when analyzing this problem. For this reason, the 
goals that FOOD 2030 includes are related to the production of climate-smart food systems, the generation of new business 
models and products, and the support of healthier diets (7).  
 
Food systems form a complex structure where not only food production but also transport, processing, and consumption 
practices of all different aliments need to be considered. During the World Food Summit 2022, three main areas were analyzed 
to combat the food impact on climate change: food waste, good production practices, and dietary shifts. A huge relevance on 
dietary shifts was given by speakers such as Joao Compari from the World Wide Fund for Nature (WWF) who stated that the 
climate change mitigation potential of dietary shifts is around 48%, surpassing the potential of food waste and good practices. 
Also, these dietary shifts can potentially promote healthier nutrition, increasing the sustainability of the food system. Another 
relevant issue pointed out during the World Food Summit by the speaker Susan Chomba is that these dietary shifts cannot be 
a global statement, they should consider different needs such as the different situations between occidental and African diets.  
 
Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) do not directly consider sustainable food systems but indirectly many SDGs promote a 
sustainable food system. One relevant example is the SDG number 2, zero hunger, to achieve a global sustainable food system 
people must have access to food.  Other SDGs that can be applied when talking about sustainable food systems are SDG 3 
(Good health and well-being), SDG 12 (Responsible consumption and production), SDG 13 (Climate change), and SDG 15 (Life 
on land). Finally, SDG 6 (Clean water and sanitation) and SDG 11 (sustainable cities and communities) could also potentially be 
influenced by the promotion of sustainable food systems (8).  
 
In this master thesis, the food system’s sustainability is analyzed considering two main parameters: the environmental impact 
and the health impact of diets. From that perspective, one dietary shift scenario is studied: the sustainability impact of changing 
from dairy yogurts to plant-based yogurts.  Due to the nature of health impact indicators, this study is done considering the 
health impact on an average western diet.  
Thus, this project intends to contribute to the obtention of data related to the sustainable performance of plant-based 
alternatives and to answer the question:  
 
Can shifting from dairy to plant-based yogurts provide a more sustainable food system? 
 
       This question will be solved by combining the answer to the following questions:  
 
Considering the environmental and health impact… 
 
How are plant-based alternatives expected to perform?  (Section 2) 
How is the sustainability of plant-based yogurts compared to dairy yogurts?  (Section 4) 
Which ones are the main drivers? And how certain are these results?  (Section 4) 
Does it represent a relevant improvement in terms of global sustainability?  (Section 5)

2. Background review. Plant-based alternatives 
performance.  
 
As stated in the introduction, it has been studied that dietary shifts towards more plant-based diets can provide a more sustainable 
food system. During the past years, there has been an increased social awareness about the environmental and health impact of 
current diets. This social awareness has created an influence on society’s preferences and the food sector, and many companies 
are providing new solutions that claim to be healthier and more environmentally friendly than other alternatives. In this context, 
plant-based alternatives are introduced as new solutions that can substitute the consumption of animal-based foods such as meat 
or dairy products for new animal-free ones. In this section, a bibliographic study of the current plant-based alternatives’ performance 
compared to the animal-based ones is done.  
2.1 Health performance 
 
Several studies have been made to quantify the impact of plant-based alternatives compared to meat-based ones. Most of these 
studies are relatively new as they have been made over the past 5 years. In general, terms, WHO recommendations suggest 
predominantly plant based as part of a healthy lifestyle. Studies suggest that plant-based meat is healthier than the average meat 
consumed, and that it helps build muscle mass, and can be used to help people with specific health conditions (9). When it comes 
to dairy yogurts, several studies suggest that plant-based milk alternatives have beneficial health effects as antioxidant activity such 
as reducing the risk of cardiovascular diseases. However, they need to be fortified to obtained similar nutritional values as dairy 
drinks (10). Moreover, for other products that substitute dairy products, such as yogurts, costumer decision choosing the right 
option is a key parameter to obtain a similar or better health performance than when taking dairy yogurts (11).  
Finally, in general, terms, even though a shift to more plant-based products is expected to improve the health impact, many 
parameters can affect this result. One common parameter is the amount of sodium or sugars that market plant-based products can 
have to seem as appealing as animal-based ones. 
2.2 Environmental performance 
 
Regarding the environmental impact, the difference in the performance of animal-based and plant-based alternatives strongly differs 
depending on the product, production process, and study. The accuracy of the results will also depend on the impact category and 
the data available. For this reason, several studies have been analyzed to quantify the relationship between the environmental 
performance of the plant-based alternative compared to the animal-based one.  To see specific values, the number of studies 
analyzed, and the different data sources visit Appendix 1. The results are given as the environmental performance of the plant-
based alternative divided by the environmental performance of the animal-based one. In this sense, a value lower than 1 suggests 
a better performance of the plant-based alternative than the animal-based one. In general terms, there are more studies for global 
warming impact assessments and there is a lack of studies related to the environmental performance of plant-based yogurts, cheese, 
and cream.  
 
When it comes to global warming, values represented in Figure 2 have been obtained. As it can be seen, most plant-based 
alternatives’ global warming potential is expected to be lower than the animal-based ones. When it comes to almond milk, there has 
been a huge variability depending on the study and the plant-based alternative performance can be higher than the animal-based 
one. For plant-based yogurts, only 1 study has been analyzed so the accuracy of the result can be considered low.

3. Plant-based yogurts. Data collection and 
methodology.  
 
Plant-based yogurts are those that are made with non-dairy milk. Companies such as Arla or Danone have introduced plant-based 
yogurts on their portfolios which can be produced out of many raw materials such as soy, almond, coconut, and oat. These yogurts 
are said to be more sustainable, but in many cases, this term is used broadly and with little data shared behind it. Moreover, the 
general trend is to expect that shifting from dairy yogurts to plant-based ones will be more sustainable (12).  This study intends to 
quantify the sustainability of plant-based yogurts when shifting from dairy-based ones to give provide numbers to these statements. 
To do so, the environmental and health performance impact is combined and compared among the different alternatives. 
3.1 Scope of the study 
 
This study is a comparative study where the sustainability of the production and consumption of different products will be analyzed. 
The main comparison will be the shift from dairy-based yogurts to plant-based ones but, as four different plant-based yogurts 
alternatives are used, a comparison between them is also discussed. 
 
This report is a collaboration with the company Chr. Hansen, and their Vega cultures, The different possible products that can be 
produced out of these cultures are used as a case study (13).  For this reason, the plant-based yogurts analyzed are:  
 
• 
Soy yogurt 
• 
Coconut yogurt 
• 
Oat yogurt 
• 
Peanut yogurt 
 
Chr. Hansen’s cultures impact is studied from a general perspective, which means that no specific product characteristics, such as 
the possible yield increase or health benefits related to Chr. Hansen-specific products, are considered in this study.  Average yield 
and raw material consumption are used for the calculations. Moreover, the yogurts compared do not have any fruits or flavorings 
components on them. 
 
The sustainability impact is quantified considering the environmental and the health impact of the compared products. 
 
The environmental impact of plant-based yogurts production is calculated according to the LCA methodology and following ISO 
14040/44 recommendations for LCA calculations. The production is calculated from a global perspective, as no data for specific 
markets production is used. For dairy yogurt, an average emission factor from EcoInvent 3.0 Yogurt, from cow milk {GLO}| market 
for | Conseq, U is considered. Several modifications have been done to this process to make a fairer comparison. These 
modifications are explained in section 3.2.2.1 
 
 This LCA is a comparative LCA where the following stages are similar for all scenarios. Thus, they have been excluded from the 
calculations:  
• 
Yogurt shelf-life 
• 
Yogurt waste production and treatment during the production process 
• 
Yogurt packaging 
• 
Yogurt transport

•

The health impact of dairy and plant-based yogurts consumption is analyzed for a limited geographical area which includes the 
“western countries” and their diets. These western countries typically include most countries of the European Union as well as the 
U.K., Norway, Iceland, Switzerland, the United States, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand. The following health parameters are 
analyzed: protein content, calcium content, Nutrient Profiling, and Health Nutrient index. The limited geographical area only has 
influence when analyzing the sustainability performance in terms of the Health Nutrient Index and the discussion of the results.

3.2 Data collection 
 
Data collection procedures are different for the environment and the health impact collection. This is done due to the nature of 
each calculation. While environmental impact accounts for the production process environmental performance, health impact is 
based on consumption. In this context, it needs to be highlighted that this study intends to be a global comparison, the recipes used 
for the environmental impact calculations represent general recipes and do not represent the exact environmental impact of the 
yogurts used for the health impact calculations.  
 
3.2.1 Health impact data collection 
 
Health impact data has been collected from different bibliographic sources where current dairy and plant-based yogurts’ nutritional 
compositions that are on the market have been analyzed. To obtain a more precise sample of results, the following number of 
products are used: 
 
• 
Dairy yogurts: 7 
• 
Soy yogurt: 2 
• 
Coconut yogurt: 3 
• 
Oat yogurt: 3 
• 
Peanut yogurt:1

3.2.2 Environmental impact data collection 
 
The environmental impact data collection procedure varies between the dairy yogurt and the plant-based yogurts’ environmental 
impact. The reason behind it is that databases already provide results for the environmental impact of dairy yogurts, while no 
databases provided results on the environmental impact of plant-based yogurts. Production recipes have been provided by Chr. 
Hansen industry experts and the emission factors are taken from EcoInvent 3.0, Consequential. To see specific recipes, visit Appendix 
2. 
 
This difference in the methodology may lead to different assumptions when it comes to yogurt production processes. Dairy yogurt

results are compared with one animal-based one.  
 
Results are calculated and an environmental impact variation (EIV) using equation [ 1 ].  The environmental performance evaluation

3.3.3 Sustainability performance methodology 
 
Sustainability performance is used to combine both environmental and health impact scores. This methodology follows the 
diagram represented in Figure 12. This methodology is based on the following criteria:  
 
1. 
Criteria 1 (C1): When the health impact score is negative, the most negative value is always less sustainable. Health benefit 
is a mandatory parameter. Thus, if there is a more negative health impact, it will always be less sustainable. 
 
2. 
Criteria 2 (C2): When plant-based yogurts reduce the environmental impact and provide health benefits they are clearly 
more sustainable that dairy-based yogurts. Contrary, if the environmental impact and the health benefits are worse it is 
clearly more unsustainable.  
 
3. 
Criteria 3 (C3):  When plant-based alternatives provide benefits for the environment, but the health impact score is reduced 
it is needed to check whether for the same amount of health impact score the environmental impact is higher or not. This 
environmental impact per amount of health impact is called “environmental price” as it is the “cost” that the environment 
must pay to provide the same amount of health benefits (same amount of protein, of calcium, same % of daily value intake 
or same minutes of healthy life gained). The same occurs when plant-based alternatives provide benefits for the health 
and not for the environment.

dairy-based one.

5. Plant-based yogurts’ global sustainability potential  
In the previous sections the sustainability performance has been studied in a product-specific perspective. In this section, the results 
obtained are analyzed in a global perspective, trying to understand how the total impact and the potential of shifting from dairy to 
plant-based yogurts is. To do so, three different analyses are done: 
 
• 
In section 5.1 the global potential impact is studied. This study is done by calculating the maximum global impact when 
dairy yogurts are substituted by plant-based yogurts. It is done both considering maximum health and environmental 
impact.  
• 
In section 5.2 different possible improvements are deeply studied. These improvements are calculated considering the 
main areas of improvement both for health and environment. 
• 
In 5.3 the impact of changing from dairy to plant-based yogurts on the SDGs is studied. 
5.1 Global environmental and health impact 
 
The global environmental and health impact is calculated to understand the global consequences of shifting from dairy to plant-
based yogurts. To do so, the first step carried out is to understand which ones are the main drivers of the unsustainability of the 
food system. In this way, the plant-based yogurts effect can be analyzed with a global perspective. After, several assumptions are 
done to understand the maximum improvement potentials.  
 
5.1.1 Environmental and health impact drivers 
 
The first step to understand the global environmental and health impact of changing from dairy to plant-based yogurts is to analyze 
which ones are the main drivers of the current unsustainability of the food system. In this sense, it can potentially be determined 
how can the sustainability of the food system be improved when changing from dairy to plant-based yogurts. 
 
Health impact of current diets 
 
Nowadays, unhealthy diets include malnutrition in all its forms: undernutrition, obesity, and other dietary factors (5). Current diets 
are based on the consumption of foods high in energy, fats, free sugars, and sodium. At the same time, many people do not eat 
enough vegetables, fruits, or fiber. Regarding the health impact of these diets, dietary health risks related to this malnutrition, are 
associated with non-communicable diseases (NCD) (17).  
 
The Global Burden of Disease (GBD) provides a tool to quantify health loss from hundreds of diseases, injuries, and risk factors. 
Using this tool, it is determined that in 2019, around 20% of all the global deaths related to non-communicable diseases are 
associated with dietary risks (18) . This value depends on different data sources and can even increase until reaching 26% of all 
deaths related to NCD. This value is growing, and it has grown 10% since 2010 (19).

5.1.2 Plant-based yogurts potential 
 
Plant- based yogurts sustainability global potential is measured by considering its impact both in health and the environment 
when dairy yogurts are substituted by plant-based yogurts. 
 
Global health impact of plant-based yogurts 
 
The first step to analyze the global health impact when dairy yogurts are substituted by plant-based yogurts is to understand how 
many dairy yogurts are taken per day. This value highly changes depending on the country, and it is even a person-specific value. 
Average value for some western countries goes from 21.3 kg per year in France until 6.7 kg of yogurt per year in the US (25). This 
corresponds to average values of 58 to 18 grams of yogurt per person per day.  
 
When it comes to protein content, as it was mentioned before, most western countries do have an excess of protein intake per day. 
Considering this exceed, it can be said that, if this variation does not reduce the protein intake until lower values than the 
recommended, there is not a significant health impact due to the protein content by changing from dairy to plant-based yogurt. 
Specifically, knowing that, the average dairy yogurt has around 81 grams of protein per kg taken the average western consumer 
takes from 5 to 1 gram of protein per day from their yogurt intake. This value corresponds to a maximum of 6 to 2 per cent of the 
total daily protein intake. A reduction of half of the protein intake, might not produce any relevant health impact on the consumer.  
 
As it was mentioned before, contrary to the protein intake, calcium intake in western countries is equal or lower to the daily 
recommendations. Thus, the dairy yogurts substitution by plant-based yogurts impact depends on the yogurt type and its exact 
calcium content. While the fortification of most soy and coconut yogurts with calcium provides equal or higher benefits for the 
consumer health (sometimes the calcium content is higher than in dairy yogurts), peanut and oat yogurts low calcium content might 
lead to a decrease in the calcium intake. In western countries researchers have found that the average daily calcium intake

5.2 Possible improvements for a better sustainable performance.  
 
In section 4.3.1 Table 17 the sustainability performance of plant-based yogurts compared to dairy yogurts is analyzed. In this section, 
it is also concluded that soy yogurts are more sustainable than dairy yogurts, that coconut and oat yogurts have the potential and 
that the peanut yogurt taken on the report is less sustainable than dairy yogurts.  Moreover, different possible improvements were 
mentioned in order to guarantee plant-based yogurts that are more sustainable than dairy ones.  
 
Plant-based yogurts do have one advantage when they are compared to dairy-ones: their raw material is less impactful for the 
environment. Thus, guaranteeing this low environmental impact is a key parameter. When health impact is compared there are 
several points that need to be considered, these are: protein content, calcium content, and the use of additives such as sugar or 
sodium.  
 
Protein content improvement 
 
Under the scope of this study, it was mentioned that the diets considered are the western countries ones. As studied in section 
5.1.2, most western countries protein content does not come from the yogurts. Moreover, western countries tend to have a much 
higher protein intake than the protein recommended values. Thus, even though protein additives could be used if the protein 
content wanted to be increased, there is not relevant health-related impact when this protein content is reduced by half.  
==PAGE:61==
   
 
 
Page 60 of 90 
 
 
Calcium content improvement  
 
Another key parameter in the yogurt’s health performance is the calcium content. Soy and coconut yogurts are already fortified with 
calcium to guarantee its intake. While this fortification might leave to different calcium assimilation ratios, when yogurts are fortified 
with calcium carbonate and vitamin D can provide similar assimilation as the dairy yogurts one (27) (28). While the health effect stays 
similar, the environmental impact is much lower due to the low environmental impact of calcium carbonate.  
 
Guaranteeing calcium content is highly important. Thus, it needs to be guaranteed that calcium is obtained and that it can be 
assimilated from plant-based yogurts. Due to the high variability of these yogurts’ composition, it is important to analyze each 
different case. 
 
Sugars and other additives 
 
Plant-based recipes are highly variant from one brand to another. The addition of sugars and other additives to improve its taste is 
a quite common option. The reduction of these sugars can potentially improve the sustainability performance of plant-based 
yogurts. 
 
Yeast specific scenarios 
 
In this study, average production efficiencies are considered. However, Chr. Hansen experts are aware of the possibility of increasing 
this yield by changing from one type of yeast to another. This increased efficiency might lead to a decrease in the amount of raw 
material needed. As it was studied before, this raw material is the most important environmental driver when producing plant-based 
yogurts. Reducing this quantity, will reduce the environmental impact of the product. Moreover, there are also products that can 
increase the health performance and provide additional benefits to the plant-based yogurts. Specific scenarios and alternatives 
should be analyzed to guarantee the best sustainable performance.  
5.3 SDG’s contribution.  
 
As it was mentioned on the introduction, sustainable food systems are not directly mentioned in the SDGs, but they are indirectly 
mentioned. SDG 2 (Zero hunger), SDG 3 (Good health and well-access to food) and SDG 12 (Responsible consumption and 
production) are some examples of SDG’s that have a clear connection with providing a sustainable food system.  
 
In this section, the conversion from dairy to plant-based yogurts potential impact on the SDG’s is analyzed. This analysis is done by 
considering the application of the research project. It means that, while the production of the plant-based yogurts is considered 
global, the dietary shift is considered to occur in western countries. Thus, the environmental or market impact has a global 
perspective, while the health impact does only consider western countries. The SDGs by themselves have a time perspective until 
2030, thus, the project impact is analyzed in 2030 (8 years after the study is done). However, future impacts should also be 
considered. 
 
The scope of the project does only consider plant-based yogurts as soy, coconut, oat and peanut. However, it was seen that the 
sustainability performance of these yogurts is highly dependent on the yogurt type. In this study the future potential contribution to 
the SDG’s is studied, thus, the best scenarios are the ones analyzed. In this scenarios results suggest that when dairy yogurts are 
substituted by plant-based yogurts they have a high potential to be more sustainable. This sustainability is driven by the lower 
environmental impact and the possible equal or improved health impact.

To do a more global sustainability analysis, a slight market impact analysis is done. This market impact is based on the fact that, 
more raw material is needed if more plant-based yogurts are required, while less dairy milk is needed. 
First, dairy milk is one of the main components in the many world economies and diets. Over 600 million people live on dairy farms 
and dairy milk consumption is expected to increase (29), Milk production directly affects to the reduction of poverty, of hunger and 
the increase of decent work and economic growth among others. Thus, the impact on these economies should be considered. When 
it comes to yogurt production, top yogurt exporters per amount of money generated are European countries such as Germany, 
France, Greece, Austria and Spain (30) (31),  
When it comes to new raw materials production, the market situation is highly different depending on the raw material type.   
lant-based yogurts studied on this report contain the following main raw materials: soya, coconut, oat, and peanut.  
Soya production is mainly in the US, Brazil, and Argentina. It already has a mature market where most of it is used to feed animals. 
There has been an increased awareness of the environmental impact of its production, due to the high amounts produced yearly. 
Thus, it would be needed to promote a change on its use, where less animals are needed to be feed and soya can be used directly 
for consumers.  
Coconuts are mainly produced in Indonesia, Philippines, India, and Brazil. High amounts of it are imported to western countries such 
as European countries or the US. Currently it is a growing market, where most of it is produced by small farmers. Its growing demand 
is leading into a situation where producers are not able to meet the demands. Moreover, due to the relatively slow growth of coconut 
palms, long-term plans would be needed for coconut growth if its demand is also growing. This growing demand could help small 
farmer and new market to grow their economy.  
Oat is mainly produced in Russian Federation, Canada, Australia, Poland, and Spain. Its current use is mainly for animal feeding. Big 
companies tend to be behind this production, and they are already aware of the expected demand growth.  
Finally, peanut is mainly produced in China, India, Nigeria, and other countries such as US and Argentina. The biggest importer are 
European countries, and it is mainly used for peanut butter. There is still a huge potential for increasing its market in many eastern 
countries as well as south American ones.  
 
From this analysis it can be concluded that, when it comes to market impact a potential economy growth can be made in those 
areas where the different raw materials are available. Some of these areas such as eastern countries, could improve their local 
economies by increasing the amount of plant-based raw materials that are produced. However, a more site-specific study needs to 
be done to guarantee economic growth by changing from dairy to plant-based yogurts. Food security, which can be increased by 
increasing the available options, can be increased by increasing the available yogurt types. A clear example of this food security is 
the importance of the Ukraine/Russia war in the global food security system. The world resources institute suggests that, one of the 
short-term decisions that could be done to avoid its impact is the shift towards more sustainable and plant-rich diets. (32).  
 
Environmental impact of this research project is clear: shifting from dairy to plant-based yogurts can reduce the environmental 
impact of our diets. Health impact highly depends on the plant-based yogurt type, but from the results and section 5.2 it can be 
affirmed that they have the potential to both increase and be equal to the dairy yogurt health impact.

6. Conclusion.  
 
Results obtained on this master thesis suggest that plant-based yogurts perform better for the environment than dairy yogurts and 
that, health performance is highly dependent on the nutritional parameter considered and, on the plant-based yogurt type. Thus, 
plant-based yogurts do have the potential to be a more sustainable option that dairy-based yogurts. However, the different available 
options need to be studied individually as there are huge difference on their performance.  
 
The environmental performance is better for plant-based yogurts than for dairy yogurts for the four impact categories, climate 
change, land use, water consumption and biodiversity loss. When it comes to health performance, protein content is always lower 
in plant-based yogurts than in dairy yogurts. However, considering the western diets analyzed on this report, it can be said that, not 
only the main protein source are not dairy yogurts but also that its reduction might not produce any significant negative effect on 
the health. Calcium content is higher in both soya and coconut yogurt than in dairy yogurts. This calcium content is added, and, to 
provide plant-based yogurts that perform as good or better than dairy yogurts is highly important to guarantee the intake and 
assimilation of this calcium. Nutrient Profiling and Health Nutrient Index results provide opposite answers. While Nutrient Profiling 
suggests that dairy yogurts perform better for the health than all plant-based yogurts (with a similar performance of soy yogurt), 
Health Nutrient Index suggests that this performance is much worse for dairy yogurts than for soy, peanut, and coconut yogurts, 
obtaining losses of minutes of healthy life per amount of dairy yogurt taken. This variation is based on the different parameters 
included on each methodology. While Nutrient Profiling provides a positive impact for the protein intake, Health Nutrient Index does 
not consider it. Moreover, sodium impact is a relevant negative parameter for the Health Nutrient Index.   
 
In this report, the objective is to answer the questions whether plant-based yogurts can provide a more sustainable food system 
than dairy yogurts. From the results and the methodology followed, it can be said that plant-based yogurts do have the potential to 
provide a more sustainable food system. This potential does not mean that they will always be more sustainable, for example oat or 
peanut yogurts tend to perform worse than dairy yogurts, but they can easily increase their health performance without increasing 
the environmental performance. Thus, they do have the potential to be more sustainable than dairy yogurts. However, global dairy 
yogurts environmental impact is not high enough to consider dairy yogurts as one of the main drivers of the global food system 
unsustainability. In fact, it is expected that plant-based meat alternatives do have a much higher potential than plant-based yogurts. 
Global food environmental impact might be decreased when dairy-yogurts are substituted by plant-based yogurts, and they might 
also have the potential to encourage consumers to try new alternatives to animal-based products on their diets. In this sense, it is 
considered that not only the direct effects of shifting from dairy to plant-based yogurts might provide a more sustainable food 
system, but also the indirect effects.  
 
SDGs contribution is highly affected by the better environmental performance and the health performance potential. However, the 
market analysis done does not provide information enough to guarantee economic growth due to this dietary shift. For this reason, 
shifting from dairy to plant-based yogurts contributes to the SDGs number 2, 13, 12, 13 and 15.  
 
When this report is applied on a costumer or specific situation, other relevant parameters should be considered. First, specific 
environmental needs might be found. While climate change and biodiversity loss do have a global impact, specific production 
locations and practices might lead to a situation where water scarcity can potentially be a problem. Thus, water consumption 
reduction might be the main driver for shifting from dairy to plant-based yogurts production. Moreover, production environmental 
impact might be reduced due to improved production practices. In this report, average yields are used. However, it is known that 
Chr. Hansen products might provide an increased yield. This increased yield, reduced the amount of raw material needed, reducing 
its environmental impact. Moreover, health performance can also be improved by using Chr. Hansen products.  
 
==PAGE:66==
   
 
 
Page 65 of 90 
 
To provide a more accurate answer to the research question a few points should be deeper studied. These points can be divided 
into:  
 
• 
Deeper analysis of the health impact: When it comes to health impact, calcium, protein, Nutrient Profiling and Health 
Nutrient Index results are considered as equally important. Future studies should provide a more detailed analysis of the 
health impact to study how this dietary shift would affect for different dietary needs 
• 
Market impact analysis: The market study should be done to provide a full social economic analysis of this dietary shift, 
where not only short term but also long-term consequences are analyzed. 
• 
Social study:  A social analysis would also be recommended to understand why people prefer one option among the 
others.  
• 
Broader number of options: New plant-based recipes are emerging from the producers. Also, fortification changes do 
occur with time. This report does only consider a small range of these recipes and it might not consider the latest advances 
on this field. A broader study, where the different producers are involved, should be done to provide a more accurate 
representation of the current market  
 
To conclude, it can be said that shifting from dairy to plant-based yogurts can provide a more sustainable food system. This 
sustainability is driven by the lower environmental impact of plant-based yogurts. However, health impact needs to be carefully 
studied to ensure the equal or better impact on the consumer health. This dietary shift might not lead to huge variation in the global 
food system sustainability but can potentially have an impact of costumer choices and promote alternatives to animal-based yogurts.
