Thesis
Adding low-frequency harmonics in
bone-anchored hearing systems
Abstract
The frequency response of bone-anchored hearing systems is characterised by a
severly limited output at low frequencies. Previous studies have shown that adding
low-frequency harmonics in conventional hearing-aids can improve the perceived sound
quality. In this study, the effect of adding low-frequency harmonics by gain-clipping to
improve the subjective perception of audio quality in bone-anchored hearing systems
was investigated. To this end, four input stimuli were processed with four different pa-
rameter settings and used in a MUSHRA listening test with both trained and untrained
listeners. The results indicate no statistical significance for the combined group, but
preferences towards wide spectrum gain-clipping for the isolated expert group.
1
Introduction
The Bone Anchored Hearing System (BAHS) enables patients with conductive hearing loss
to hear by vibrations through the skull. Instead of an electroacoustic transducer in the ear
canal, the system uses a vibrator connected to an implant fixated in the mastoid of the
temporal bone.

The system can be modelled as two masses connected
by a spring with a transfer function having a very promi-
nent resonance peak around 800-900 Hz with a declining
slope of 20 dBµN per octave towards low-frequencies. This
results in a relatively thin sound.
Conventional hearing aids transmit sound to the tym-
panic membrane by an electroacoustic transducer placed
in the ear canal. Blocking the ear gives rise to the oc-
clusion effect, where the user perceives his own voice as
too loud and hollow and this is mitigated by open-fitting
hearing systems. The trade-off is low-frequency leaking
out through the openings. Conventional hearing systems
mainly address issues of speech intelligibility, with focus on
the higher frequency range. In normal use, low-frequency
sounds enter the ear canal through the vent and comple-
ment the amplified sounds resulting in all frequencies be-
ing properly represented. However, during audio stream-
ing no outside sound source is available and the electroa-
coustic transducer has to produce the entire frequency
range resulting in audio devoid of low-frequency.

Methods
2.1
The auditory system and psychoacoustics
The auditory system is divided into the outer, middle and inner ear. The inner ear can
be described as a frequency analyzer separating out frequencies of complex input stimuli.
Vibrations are converted to a travelling wave moving along the basilar membrane with tono-
topic representation. On the basilar membrane, inner and outer nerve cells are situated
inside the Organ of Corti. The basilar membrane vibrations cause the nerve cells to deflect.
Outer hair cells act as a cochlear amplifier enhancing the vibrations, while inner hair cells
spike and release action potentials in the auditory fibers which travel to the auditory cortex
where the stimuli is converted to what we perceive as sound.

Psychoacoustics is the study of how humans per-
ceive sound. It deals with the physical, physiological
and perceptual correlates of sound production, trans-
mission and reception [12] and describes three impor-
tant attributes of auditory sensation: pitch, loudness
and timbre [4]. Pitch is that attribute of auditory sen-
sation in terms of which sounds may be ordered on a
scale from low to high and is related to the fundamen-
tal frequency of stimuli. Loudness is the attribute of
auditory sensation in terms of which sounds can be
ordered on a scale extending from quiet to loud [8] and
is related to amplitude as demonstrated by the equal
loudness contours [11, p. 91]. Timbre is what dis-
tinguishes two sounds having the same loudness and
pitch. It can, for instance, be described as "harsh",
"bright" and "dull [4, p. 232].
Two theories explain how the auditory system
extracts pitch from an input sound. The place theory
describes how the tonotopic mapping of the basilar
membrane links to frequency: exciting a particular place on the basilar membrane corre-
sponds to a particular frequency. The temporal theory describes how the discharge distri-
bution of neural firing rate in the temporal domain is closely related to the repetition rate

Human pitch detection is not yet fully understood. One pitch detection model is based
on both the temporal and place theory. The auditory filters are logarithmically spaced, and
stimulating the auditory system with a pulse train excites filters near apex by fewer harmon-
ics causing resolved excitation patterns, whereas filters near the base are unresolved. Basilar
membrane movement for resolved filters follows a fine-structure whereas it only follows the
envelope for unresolved high-frequency filters [11, p. 134].
Another pitch detection model is the template model [11, p. 141]. It argues that the
brain learns templates of pitches in the same manner it learns templates of real world objects.
It works best for low frequencies, and can not explain pitch perception where there are no
resolved harmonics. Although, it works very well in combination with the temporal model
to explain musical pitch perception. Pitch perception is extracted from higher harmonics,
and research has shown, that resolved harmonics are most crucial [4, p.
135], and that
the auditory systems perceives the pitch corresponding to the highest common factor of
adjacent harmonics [4, p. 133]. This discovery, is called the "phenomenon of the missing
fundamental" and is demonstrated in figure 2.
Sensorineural hearing loss alters the patients perception of sound due to destruction of
the cochlear amplifier widening the auditory filters. It leads to decreased frequency discrim-
ination which alters subjective perceptions such as timbre, loudness and pitch. Processing
audio, by adding harmonics to a stimuli, does not necessarily translate equally well for per-
sons with sensorineural hearing loss as for persons with otologically normal ears, as studies
have shown that adding lower harmonics can impair the ability of elderly impaired listen-
ers to determine pitch [9]. Other studies have also demonstrated how speech recognition
thresholds are elevated when signals are peak clipped [3]. Evidence also suggests the ability
to use Temporal Fine Structure (TFS) cues is diminished by cochlear hearing loss. This is
problematic, as TFS is used to "listen in the dips" [7]. Pitch perception is not fully under-
stood, but evidence suggests hearing impaired rely more on temporal-envelope cues for pitch
perception, than normal hearing, as they experience limited resolved harmonics [10]. Such
challenges must be addresses when modifying the spectra in hearing systems using signal
processing.

Hard-clipping is at the core of the "PowerBass"
algorithm developed by Pauli Miinnar [6]. It consists
of three steps: 1) Overdriving a low frequency signal
part, 2) Low-pass filtering the part to shape the har-
monics, 3) Combining the low frequency signal with
a high-pass filtered version of the input to form the
final output signal.
A BAHS modified version of this algorithm was
developed for this investigation and is shown in figure
4. The final system, developed in MATLAB, takes the
mono stimuli as input, x, normalized to 10 sones, and
feeds it into the algorithm where it is attenuated by
3 dB. If the signal is PowerBass processed, it is high-
pass filtered at 200 Hz to remove components below
the operating range of the BAHS. Next, the signal
splits into three parts. The upper part is high-pass
filtered at 300/500 Hz and passed on towards the fi-
nal summation point.
The middle part is low-pass
filtered at 300/500 Hz and also passed towards the
final summation point. The third part is a weighted
sum of the low-passed signal and the high-pass filtered
input. This summed signal is waveshaped by apply-
ing a gain factor of either 8 or 12 followed by clipping.
The output is gain attenuated, to reduce possibility
of overload at the final summation stage. The higher
harmonics are attenuated by a series of low-pass fil-
ters, and the saturated signal passed on to the final
summation stage.
Here the three signal paths are
combined to form the final output signal. The last
stage is another saturator block which prevents out-
put signals above full scale to reach the output. The
’reference’ (variation ’F’) is the output from the Power Headroom (PH) gain stage and the
’anchor’ (variation ’E’) is the same signal high-pass filtered at 1 kHz. All output signals are
normalized to 10 sones to reduce loudness perception bias in the listening test. The BAHS
used was the Ponto 5 SuperPower from Oticon Medical with a prescribed a flat gain of 20

Conclusions
This research aimed to answer if the perceived audio quality of bone-anchored hearing sys-
tems can be improved by exploiting the principle of the missing fundamental. Four different
input stimuli were pre-processed through a non-linear system which added odd harmonics
to the stimuli based on four different parameter combinations.
A MUSHRA listening experiment was conducted, and based on the subjective assess-
ments from 18 test persons, seven of which where objective listening experts, no significant
result was obtained. Analysing assessments from the isolated objective listening experts, sta-
tistical significance was found between ratings of the processed and unprocessed (reference)
stimuli. Using Tukey’s honestly significant difference criterion on a multiple comparison pro-
cedure showed population marginal means significantly different from the reference. Most
significance was found for processings were harmonics were added based on a wide frequency
bandwidth. This indicates higher preferences for broader saturation at the cost of timbre
alterations.
This research clearly illustrates that adding harmonics to bone-anchored hearing systems
has some potential to improve the perceptive audio quality, but also raises the question if it
would be more beneficial to add such harmonics more selectively. With today’s processing
power this could be performed nonlinearly in the frequency-domain based on input spectra.
To better understand the implications of these results, future studies could address the effect
of input spectra in relation to assessor preferences as well as physical limitations due to the
Maximum Force Output. Further research is also needed to understand how the inherent
harmonic distortion of bone-anchored hearing systems can be utilized in a more intelligent
system.

Project aim
In conventional hearing aids sound is picked up by microphones where it is processed. Then
the processed sound is transmitted to the Tympanic Membrane (TM) by an electroacoustic
transducer placed in the ear canal. Placing transducers properly blocks the ear canal and
gives rise to the occlusion effect, where the user perceives his own voice as too loud and
hollow. This challenge has been mitigated by the invention of open-fitting hearing systems
in which the ear canal is not completely blocked.
The trade-off with open-fitting is that low-end sounds from the hearing aid leak out
through the openings.
Hearing systems have traditionally been designed to address the
issues of speech intelligibility, with a main focus on the frequency range between 1 to 4 kHz.
In a normal setting, the low-frequency sounds also enter the ear canal through the vent and
in this way complement the amplified sounds from the hearing aid. The end result being
that all frequencies are properly represented when using the microphones.
With the introduction of audio streaming capabilities no outside sound source is avail-
able and the electroacoustic transducer has to produce the entire frequency range. A well
designed electroacoustic transducer has a flat frequency response, but a natural lower cut-off
frequency where the response drops off. The lower cut-off is generally proportional to the
diaphragm size and smaller speakers can not push as much air. The diaphragm size in hear-
ing systems is naturally limited due to the size of the ear-canal. Forcing a small diaphragm
to emit a large Sound Pressure Level (SPL) beyond the lower cut off requires a huge di-
aphragm displacement. This is expensive in terms of battery power and can destroy the
electroacoustic transducer. The effect being that open-fitting design provides audio devoid
of low-frequency content.
This challenge has previously been mitigated by the use of Digital Signal Processing
(DSP) and psychoacoustics [16, p. 2]. Adding higher harmonics to a signal provides the
user with perception of a lower fundamental - even though this lower fundamental is not
physically present.
The introduction of BAHS provided patients with pure conductive hearing loss the abil-
ity to hear. This is achieved by vibrating the sound through the skull to the cochlea. Instead
of placing electroacoustic transducers in the ear canal, the sound processor is connected to
an abutment surgically implanted in the mastoid of the temporal bone. The system can be
modelled as two-masses connected by a spring. The result is a transfer function having a
very prominent resonance peak around 800-900 Hz. The function has a step declining slope
below the resonance and results in a relatively thin sound. Opposed to how low-frequency
components can enter through the open-dome of conventional hearing-aids, adding to the
end result, the full frequency range has to be provided by the BAHS.
The intent with this project is to investigate if adding low-frequency harmonics to bone-
anchored hearing systems can improve the subjective perception of audio quality in the user.
The hypothesis being, that a significant portion of the users will like such a feature enabled
in the BAHS - although preferences may be dependent on the spectral distribution of the
input stimuli. I.e. enabled for certain music but disabled for pure speech.

Background and theory
6.1
The Auditory System
Before we dive into how audio quality can be enhanced by adding low-frequency harmonics,
it first necessary to understand some basic concepts on how the auditory system processes
sound. It consists of the outer, middle and inner ear and is depicted in figure 8.
Figure 8: The auditory system consists of the outer, middle and inner ear. The cochlea
converts the mechanical vibrations to electrical impulses which are transmitted to the brain
where they are perceived as sound.
In a person with otologically normal ears the sound waves first reach the outer ear. Here
they either hit the pinna, and get directed into the ear canal, or, given the right angle, enter
the ear canal directly. The ear canal can be resembled as a closed-ended tube with a unique
resonance frequency. It shapes the frequency spectrum of the sound before it reaches the
middle ear.
Here the acoustical air waves hit the TM which converts the acoustical sound into
mechanical vibrations. The TM is connected to the ossicles, consisting of incus, malleus and
stapes. The mechanical vibrations are passed through the ossicles in which stapes pushes
the oval window situated in the Cochlea in the inner ear.
Page 13 of 83
==PAGE:19==
Thesis
6.2
Hearing loss
The Cochlea is filled with peralymph and endolymph. Thus, the ossicles serve as an
impedance matching device. Inside the Cochlea the vibrations are converted to a travelling
wave, which moves along the basilar membrane. The basilar membrane is narrow and stiff
at the base and wide and sloppy at the apex. This facilitates high frequency vibrations
at the base and low frequency vibrations at the apex, often referred to as the tonotopic
representation.
The Organ of Corti is situated between the basilar and tectorial membrane inside scala
media. Inner and outer nerve cells are located on top of the Origan of Corti and when the
basilar membrane vibrates the nerve cells vibrate as well. The outer hair cells are sensitive to
frequencies corresponding to their tonotopic placement. When excited they begin to vibrate
with the same frequency and amplify the vibrations. This is known as the cochlear amplifier.
When the inner hair cells vibrate, they spike and release action potentials in the auditory
fibers. These electric signals travel all the way to the auditory cortex where the stimuli is
converted to what we understand as sound.
The inner ear can be described as an frequency analyzer effectively separating out the
frequencies of complex input stimuli. The analysis is limited by the critical bands, or width
of the auditory filters. The filter bandwidths increase with frequency which affects frequency
selectivity. Although similar sounding, there is a distinct difference between frequency dis-
crimination and frequency selectivity. The former refers to the ability to detect changes in
frequency over time where as the latter refers to the ability to resolve frequency components
of a complex stimuli [10, p. 204]. Due to the cochlear amplifier the filter tuning is level
dependant. One cause of hearing loss is destruction of outer hair cells which reduce the
effect of the cochlear amplifier. As the basilar membrane is made of organic material it has
inherent non-linearities which is the root cause of Distortion Product Otoacoustic Emmisions
(DPOTE).
It follows, that, for a person with otologically normal ears, vibrations from the vocal
chords also travel directly thorugh the skull to the cochlea. It is the reason why the voice
sounds differently to the speaker when recorded by a microphone and played back through a
speaker. Vibrations from outside can also travel via the temporal bone to the ear canal wall
were they are converted to pressure differences causing the TM to vibrate. This effect, called
the occlusion effect, is especially pronounced when the ear canal is blocked as the pressure
difference in this case only reacts with the TM.
6.2
Hearing loss
Hearing loss can be divided into the following groups: conductive, sensorineural, and mixed.
When it only occurs in one ear it is referred to as single-sided deafness. If the hearing loss is
not caused by neither the outer, middle or inner ear it is referred to as retro-cochlea hearing
loss and the problem is to be found in the nerve path to the brain [24, p. 91].
A conductive hearing loss is caused by problems in the ear canal, tympanic membrane
or ossicles.
The consequences are in the range of minor impedance mismatches causing
attenuation of a particular frequency range all the way to patients born without a pinna and
ear canal. In other words; if the cochlea is correctly stimulated the patient will perceive the
sound as a normal hearing person.

==PAGE:20==

6.3
Hearing systems
If the hearing loss occurs in the inner ear it is sensorineural and often referred to as
cochlear hearing loss. The cochlear amplifier can be destroyed if exposed to loud noises.
This will result in dips in the audiogram. The dips typically occur around the 1-4 kHz range
where the human ear is most sensitive, but can, depending on the noise type, also occur
elsewhere. This is categorised as noise induced hearing loss. Age related hearing loss is
the most common type of sensorineural hearing losses and is refereed to as Presbyacusis.
It occurs naturally for all humans as the outer hair cells deteriorate over time. Here, the
hearing loss will be most prominent for all the higher frequencies. Sensorineural hearing loss
alters the patients perception of sound. This is due to the aforementioned destruction of
the cochlear amplifier which results in wider auditory filters. It leads to decreased frequency
discrimination which alters subjective perceptions as such as timbre and pitch. In case of
the patient having both conductive and a sensorineural hearing loss it is referred to as a
mixed hearing loss.
Processing audio, by for instance adding harmonics to a stimuli, does not necessarily
translate equally for persons with sensorineural hearing loss and persons with otologically
normal ears. It has been shown, that adding lower harmonics can impair the ability of elderly
impaired listeners to determine pitch [20]. Other studies have also demonstrated how speech
recognition thresholds are elevated when signals are peak clipped [5]. Evidence also suggests
the ability to use TFS cues is diminished by cochlear hearing loss. This is problematic,
as TFS is used to "listen in the dips" [18]. The pitch perception in the auditory system is
not fully understood, but evidence suggests hearing impaired rely more on temporal-envelope
cues for pitch perception, than normal hearing, as they experience limited resolved harmonics
[21]. Such challenges must be addresses when modifying the spectra in hearing systems using
signal processing.
6.3
Hearing systems
The various types of hearing losses have given rise to a wide range of hearing system solutions.
Traditional hearing systems dominate a large part of the market. They are aimed at patients
with hearing impairment treatable by acoustical amplification. The BAHS are well suited for
patients having a relatively intact cochlea. If the cochlea is severely damaged, then cochlear-
implants are used. Here, an electrode is inserted into the cochlea and the hair cells excited
by electrical stimuli. Finally, auditory brainstem implants are used on patients suffering
retro-cochlea hearing loss. This project will solely focus on BAHS.

Bone Anchored Hearing Systems
In 1965, the Swedish Brånemark discovered that, if left undisturbed, osteocytes grow
in close apposition to titanium screws. The discovery is called ossointegration and was first
successfully applied in dentistry and craniofacial surgery. This formed the basis for bone-

by a skin-penetrating coupling from an ossointegrated titanium implant in the mastoid.
The invention was further improved and the Bone Anchored Hearing Aid (BAHA) became
commercially available in 1987 [9].

6.5
Psychoacoustics
The following section serves to explain important concepts related to how the human brain
senses and perceives physical properties of sound. Classical psychophysics dates back to
the 18th century pioneered by physiologist E. H. Weber and physicist G. Fechner.
The
field originated from curiosity on how physical stimuli affect the subject perception [23].
Psychoacoustics, a multidisciplinary field within the branch of psychophysics, is the study
of how humans perceive sound.
It deals with the physical, physiological and perceptual
correlates of sound production, transmission and reception.
A musical sound can be physically described by its frequency components but perceived
in terms of melody, harmony, chords, tuning, etc. or by how it differs from a sound coming
from a different instrument.
The first perception terms are generally related to what is
described as the pitch while the second is related to what is described as timbre [10]. At third
important term in psychoacoustics is loudness, which relates to matters such as dynamics.
In the following sections we will take a closer look at these very interesting topics!
6.5.1
Pitch Perception
Pitch perception is one of the most important evolutionary concepts alongside directionality
and loudness as it helps humans estimate the size of the source. Although pitch can be
Page 17 of 83
==PAGE:23==
Thesis
6.5
Psychoacoustics
measured in units of hertz it is not an objectively measurable physically property such as
the concept of frequency. Frequency can be measured and pitch can be heard. The formal
definition by the American National Standard Institute (ANSI) is that "pitch is that attribute
of auditory sensation in terms of which sounds may be ordered on a scale from low to high".
This is a very broad definition and it can be argued to also cover topics like loudness. A less
broad definition is that pitch is "a sensation associated with musical melodies".
As pitches can be rated as "higher" or "lower" and quantified in hertz they are corre-
sponding very nearly to the repetition rate of sound waves. A pure sinus tone is the simplest
example of a pitched sound, but complex sounds also have pitch. It is even possible to create
band limited noise which induces the sensation of a pitch in the listener.
Let us have a look at the physiological basis of pitch perception. Two theories have
been proposed to explain how the auditory system extracts the pitch from an input sound.
They are the place theory and the temporal theory.
6.5.2
The Place Theory
As the Organ of Corti is mechanically vibrated by the basilar membrane the inner hair cells
are exited. This causes the auditory nerve fibers to fire and creates an electrical impulse,
in the form of an auditory action potential, to travel down the auditory pathway and into
the brain where it is perceived as sound.
During no stimulus the auditory neurons are
spontaneous firing, but during stimulus the firing rate is increased in the temporal domain.
The concept of tonotopic mapping tells us, that the mechanical properties of the basilar
membrane is related to frequency. It is stiff and narrow at the base, which causes resonances
at higher frequencies, and at the helicotrema it is wide and sloppy, which causes resonances
at lower frequencies. This gives rise to the place theory: if a particular place on the basilar
membrane is excited then it corresponds to a particular frequency.
6.5.3
The Temporal Theory
Looking at the neural firing rate in the temporal domain it can be observed, that the discharge
distribution is closely related to the repetition rate of the stimuli. Neurons can only fire or
not fire and thus resemble a binary coding, or digitization, of the waveform. They only lock
to the positive cycle of a waveform. This is called phase locking. The neurons are not always
fast enough to fire at every period, but when they do fire, it is at multiples of that particular
period.
As time and frequency are inversely related, the frequency of the stimuli can be extracted
by the firing rate of the neurons. Experiments have shown, that the phase locking properties
of the neurons break down at around 3,5 kHz - which actually corresponds to the end of
musical pitch perception. The highest note on a normal piano tuned to equal temperament
is C8 which corresponds to 4186.009 Hz.[10, p. 131]
6.5.4
The Volley Principle
The auditory system contains thousands of neurons and multiple neurons are reacting to
the same frequency. Each neuron is not fast enough to spike at every period, but one of its

rates for multiple neurons located at the same place on the basilar membrane, and adding
up the firing rates, the result is a close resembling of the input wave form [10, p. 144].

A listener will perceive a pulse train, with a repetition rate of 10 Hz, as
individual clicking sounds. When increasing the frequency to 20 Hz the auditory system is
not fast enough and the perception changes to what is called the "motorboat perception".
A raw and harsh sound with no perceivable pitch. Increasing the repetition rate even more
will induce the sensation of a pitch corresponding to the fundamental frequency. This is
an ingenious way for the auditory system to extract just what is necessary. An analogy
could be how the visual cortex observes individual frames at low frequencies, but they blend
together to a moving film, when the rate reaches 20 frames per second. As of such, pitch is
an ingenious way for our brain to reduce the complexity of the outside world.
6.5.6
Pitch perception models
The auditory filters are logarithmically spaced and widens with increased frequency. When
stimulating the auditory system with a pulse train, the auditory filters close to the apex will
be exited by fewer harmonics than the wider filters closer to the base. In other words, the
excitation patterns of the filters, excited by low-frequency harmonics, will be resolved where
as the filters excited by higher-frequency harmonics will be unresolved.
When looking at how this translates to the movement of the basilar membrane it can be
shown, that low-frequency harmonics follow a fine-structure - the membrane resonates with
the same frequency as the stimuli. For the high-frequency harmonics the basilar membrane
can not resonate this rapidly. Instead the vibrations follow the envelope of the stimuli. This
could argue for a model where pitch detection is based on both the temporal theory and the
place theory [22, p. 134]. The concept is demonstrated in figure 10.

6.5.7
The missing fundamental
The pioneers of acoustic science, such as Ohm (1843) and Helmholtz (1863) thought the
first harmonic, or fundamental component, determined pitch perception. The basic idea
being, that extracting the lowest frequency component of a complex signal would result in
the periodicity of the signal. In most real world complex tones the first harmonic is present
in the signal - and the assumption holds - but, in 1940, Schouten demonstrated how the
pitch perception was left intact even when removing the fundamental component of a pulse
wave.
This discovery, called "phenomenon of the missing fundamental", demonstrated two
important findings: 1) the fundamental does not have to be present for pitch perception
and 2) the lowest component present is not the basis of pitch perception [10, p. 135]. The
phenomenon is demonstrated in figure 12. Later, in 1956, Licklider showed, that the pitch
perception was unaffected when masking the frequency region of the fundamental. This
also indicated that pitch perception is extracted from higher harmonics. Later research has
shown, that resolved harmonics play the most important role in pitch perception [22, p. 138]
[10, p. 133].
A sound consisting of short clicks, or pulse train, occurring 200 times per second will
induce a pitch perception very similar to a 200 Hz pure-tone. The difference is the higher
harmonics in the click sound, which occurs at integer multiples off the fundamental frequency
of 200 Hz. I.e. there will be harmonics at 200, 400, 600,...Hz. These harmonics shape the
sound and give it a harsh timbre. Filtering out the 200 Hz component will still induce a pitch
sensation of 200 Hz, but it will change the timbre. It is even possible to filter out multiple of
the first harmonics, for instance just leaving the 1800 Hz, 2000 Hz and 2200 Hz harmonics,
and the pitch of 200 Hz is still perceived. Schouten called the low pitch associated with

6.5.8
Timbre
The ANSI formal definition of timbre is "That attribute of auditory sensation in terms of
which a listener can judge two sounds similarly presented, and having the same loudness and
pitch, as being dissimilar". Its sound quality descriptions include, among others, mellow,
rich, dull, bright, dark and harsh. When considering musical notes, timbre relates to those
attributes that can be varied without affecting the loudness, pitch or duration [10, p. 232].
As an example, we can look at a pure tone. It has only one single frequency which
is identical to the repetition rate.
A normal square wave, with similar repetition rate,
will contain the same frequency, but also frequencies of odd integer multiples. They will
both induce the same pitch, but will sound differently. These other frequencies are called
harmonics and are what enables us to distinguish the same note played on two different
instruments.
6.5.9
Loudness
The ANSI defines loudness as "that attribute of auditory sensation in terms of which sounds
can be ordered on a scale extending from quiet to loud" [19]. The perception of loudness is
related to its amplitude, but no simple functional one-to-one relationship has been discovered.
For instance, a low frequency sound wave with a larger sound pressure level than a mid
frequency sound wave, will cause a perception of lower loudness [22, p. 91]. This was first
demonstrated by Fletcher and Munson in 1933 by the equal loudness contours. These curves
demonstrate the correlation between perceived loudness of a pure tone at 1 kHz and a tone
at another frequency.
The curves show, how a 1 kHz pure tone presented with a SPL of 40 dB has the same
perceived loudness as a 100 Hz pure tone presented at approximately 60 dB. The loudness
scale used is the phone scale, where, in this example, both sounds have a level of 40 phons. In
other words, our auditory systems need a much greater sensation in SPL at lower frequencies
for us to perceive the stimuli as equally loud as sensations in the mid frequency range. The
equal loudness contours similarly show, that we also need greater sensations at very high
frequencies. Two dominant dips can be observed, one at 3,5 kHz and one at 13 kHz. These
can be ascribed to resonance frequencies in our ear canals.
The sone scale was established in order to avoid possible confusion between comparing
SPL in dB and subjective perceived loudness. Based on a great number of loudness compar-
isons the loudness curve was mapped. The sones are on the y-axis and phones on the x-axis,
where one sone corresponds to 40 phones. There is an almost linear relationship between
the two units where a 10 phon increase in SPL results in a doubling in perceived loudness
[7].

Audio normalization is often necessary when working with digital audio. This is the case
in the broadcast industry where radio stations are broadcasting with similar loudness levels
[28], in the music streaming industry where services like Spotify normalize all the music to

Normalizing is also especially important when conducting listening experiments. For
reasons explained in section 6.5.9 the spectral content and loudness level of different audio
material can induce different loudness perceptions in the listener. If not mitigated, this can
greatly affect the results and make it impossible to state if for instance a new DSP algorithm
is preferred based on its spectral contributions, or if the listener solely preferred the louder
sound.
There are different ways to normalize digital audio. The simplest method is peak nor-
malization, where all the samples are divided by the absolute value of the largest sample.
This will shift everything, such that the largest sample gets a value of +1/-1. As may be
clear, this method does not take into account neither the dynamic range of the entire signal
nor the way the auditory system perceives loudness. Another method is the root-mean-
square. In this method all the samples are averaged and squared. Then the root is taken of
the result. This gives the Root-mean Square (RMS). A linear gain can then be applied to
the RMS to normalize it compared to another signal. Again, this does not take into account
the way our auditory system perceives loudness.
More sophisticated normalization methods are the ISO 532-1 (Zwicker) and EBU R
128. They are provided as part of the Audio Toolbox in MATLAB as acousticLoudness and
integratedLoudness, respectively. Both methods take the auditory system into account and
are thus greatly suited to normalize digital audio. The acousticLoudness calculates the sones
while integratedLoudness calculates and returns the loudness in Loudness Units relative to
Full Scale (LUFS).

6.8.1
Systems
A system describes how a signal passing through it is modified. Another term is the transfer
function. A linear system has a behavior confirming to both scaling and superposition. I.e.
scaling the input by a given amount results in an output scaled at a similar amount. Passing
separate input signals through the system, and then adding the outputs, gives the same
result as would adding together the input signals and then passing that through the system.
Another term is time-invariance, which states that the output of a system does not change
depending on the time of the input. The Linear and Time-Invariant (LTI) class of systems
adhere to both of these two conditions.
This is important, because when using a delta
function as input, the output becomes the impulse response of the system. By exploiting the
linear and time-invariant properties it can be shown, that the corresponding output, to any
other arbitrary input signal, is the convolution between this input signal and the impulse
response.
6.8.2

Joseph Fourier made the claim, that any periodic signal can be decomposed into sinusoids
which are harmonics, or integer multiples, of a fundamental frequency - the inverse of the
period.

6.9
Distortion
A system which adds components to the output, not present at the input, is said to be
non-linear.
As stated in section 6.6, the harmonics are typically generated in the time-
domain using such non-linearities. In the following section we will take a further look at
distortion and harmonics. According to the dictionary, distortion is "the act of twisting or
altering something out of its true, natural, or original state" [37]. Distortion can thus both
be intentional or unintentional - it all depends on context. When the purpose of an audio
system is to amplify a source to make it more audible, the tolerance for distortion is generally
low. When the intention is to "color" the sound to make it sound "richer" or "grittier", as is
the case for guitars in rock music, the distortion introduced by the non-linearities is intended.
6.9.1
Harmonics
One descriptor of the spectral content of sound is harmonics. A pure tone oscillating at 100
Hz has a fundamental frequency of 100 Hz and a spectra with exactly just one component
- the frequency of 100 Hz. This frequency, or fundamental component, is referred to as the
first harmonic. Integer multiples of the fundamental are referred to as higher harmonics. In
this case, that would be 200 Hz, 300 Hz, 400 Hz, and so forth. The harmonics are given
an harmonic number corresponding to their integer multiple of the fundamental frequency
and can be divided into even (200 Hz, 400 Hz, 600 Hz, etc.) and odd (300 Hz, 500 Hz, 700
Hz, etc.) harmonics. Sub-harmonics are fundamental frequencies whose harmonic series also
contain the given components. In this example it could be 50, 25, 20, etc Hz [19, p. 134].
The human ear finds odd-order harmonics more objectionable than even-order harmon-
ics [6]. This can be explained by the pitch relationship between even-order harmonics and
the fundamental where the 2nd, 4th and 8th harmonics are perfect octaves above the fun-
damental. This will, in a musical sense, cause less clash with harmonics from other notes
when playing chords. The odd-order harmonics result in octave-transposed fifths and thirds
relation to the fundamental and this will cause musical dissonance.
6.9.2
Input-output functions
The non-linear properties of a distortion system can be explained in terms of its input-
output function. Here, the input is plotted on the x-axis and the corresponding output on
the y-axis. There are two types of general input-output functions; the even-function and
the odd-function. When an alternating signal is being feed into an even-function half the
signal is being mirrored across the zero-crossings, referred to as mirrored symmetry. In an

6.10
Filters
The necessity to pass some components through a system, while blocking out others, is
a widely occurring phenomenon.
Examples are water filters blocking contaminants and
sunglasses blocking Ultraviolet (UV) rays from hitting the eyes. In this section we will have
a look at digital filters and how they can be used to filter audio signals.
6.10.1
Filter types
There is a wide variety of filters, and different filters are used depending on the task to
be solved. For instance, a high-pass filter can be used to remove Direct Current (DC) or
to increase the perception of brightness while a low-pass filter can be used to increase the
perception of base. A combination of these two filters is the band-pass filter which reduces
both low and high frequencies on either side of a range. A fourth filter type is the notch filter,
which does the opposite - removes frequencies situated in between a specific bandwidth.
6.10.2
Ideal filter
A filter which instantly removes frequencies, when passing a certain threshold, is called an
ideal filter. The ideal filter has full transmission in the pass band, total attenuation in the
stop band and abrupt transition. The filter order specifies how close a realised filter is to
the ideal filter and an ideal filter has infinite order and latency.
In all practical applications, it is not possible to design such a filter. Instead the filter
designer must carefully select a cut off in which frequencies are allowed to pass through the
filter. This is called the passband. The other cut off, in which frequencies are attenuated,
is called the stopband. The region, in which the attenuation changes from the specifications
in the passband to the specifications in the stopband is called the transition band.
The frequency domain has symmetry for real signals, and must be mirrored for negative
frequencies. When operating with discrete signals in the digital domain the full response

accumulate over time.

7.1
PowerBass
Back in the mid 00’s, with the introduction of streaming capabilities, Oticon experienced
complaints from users with open-fitting domes. Generally, hearing impaired are affected the
most by hearing loss at frequencies around 1-4 kHz. This is the most sensitive area and
plays a huge role in speech intelligibility. Lower frequencies are not as easily attenuated
by obstacles on their path from transmitter to the receivers ear. And in this way, lower
frequencies directly contribute to the SPL measured at the TM.
With the introduction of audio streaming, there is no contribution from an outside
source, and the entire sound pressure has to be provided by the electroacoustic transducer.
While the advantage with the open-vent design is a minimization of the occlusion effect,
the drawback is that low-frequency sounds easily leave the ear canal. The end result is a
very thin sound. Oticon called in outside expertise from AM3D - which was a company
specialized in DSP enhanced audio experiences. One of their products was the PowerBass
algorithm which was ported to the ARC Tangent-A4 processor which is part of the legacy
Oticon Inium Sense platform.

The algorithm utilizes gain, filter, summation and saturation blocks.
The algorithm consists of three steps [12]:
1. Overdriving a low frequency signal part
2. Low-pass filtering the part to shape the harmonics
3. Combining the low frequency signal with a high-pass filtered version of the input to
form the final output signal
The system consists of a mono input, x, which is feed into the algorithm and attenuated by
G5. The signal is high-pass filtered to remove components below the cut off frequency of
the electroacoustic transducer. Then the signal splits into three parts. The upper part is
left unprocessed and passed directly towards the final summation point. The middle part is
low-pass filtered and gain adjusted before being passed towards the final summation point.
The third part is a summed ratio between the low-passed signal and the high-pass filtered
input. This summed signal is excessively gained and then clipped by a saturator. The output
is gain attenuated, to reduce the possibility of overload. Then it is feed through a cascaded
network of low-pass filters until it reaches the final summation stage. Here the signals from
the three paths are combined to form the final output signal. The last stage is another
saturator block which prevents output signals above full scale to reach the output.

The PowerBass algorithm contains four different types of blocks; gains, filters, summation
and saturation. Each block plays a vital role in the algorithm. Some have fixed parameters
while others have user adjustable parameters [11].

Filter blocks
There are two types of filter blocks:
• High-pass filter (HPF): A first order IIR Butterworth filter with a user adjustable cut
off frequency. The cut off frequency shall be chosen such that it is one to two octaves
below the Low-pass filter (LPF) cut off frequency. This limitation is introduced as
it is not realistic to boost signal components below more than two octaves below the
natural high-frequency cut-off frequency of an electroacoustic transducer. The purpose
of the HPF is thus to remove these components.
• LPF: A first order IIR Butterworth filter with a user adjustable cut off frequency. The
LPF is applied in two places in the block diagram. Firstly, it is used to filter the
signal into a low frequency component part. The cut-off frequency shall be selected to
be close to the high-pass cut-off frequency of the electroacoustic transducer. This to
ensure the effect is applied below the low frequency cut-off of the transducer. Secondly,
a cascaded series of filters, with similar cut-off settings, are used to generate a fourth
order IIR filter. The function is to greatly attenuate higher harmonics generated by
the saturator block.

There are two types of summation blocks:
• Addition: The first block performs pure addition of the two incoming signals from the
output of G2 and G3.
• Addition and subtraction: The second block also performs addition, but note, how the
sign has changed for the low-frequency component signals. The end result is that a
high-pass version of the original signal is generated for free, while it is also combined
with the saturated low-frequency signal part.

Modified PowerBass algorithm and adjusted parameter settings
Early in the project phase, it was decided to not focus on low-level embedded programming.
That is, the main goal was to investigate how adding low-frequency harmonics affected the
performance of the BAHS - and in the end the perception of the listener. Not to construct
machine code to run on the BAHS. Instead, audio files should be pre-processed in MATLAB
and streamed via Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) to the BAHS during the final listening
experiment. Therefor, it was decided to build a model in MATLAB of the Inium Sense
Platform implementation, described in section 7.1.1.
This project phase had two main challenges:
1. Phase distortion caused by IIR Butterworth filters.
2. Carefully selecting appropriate parameters, well suited for a BAHS.
Early in the process, when the first audio files were processed, it became apparent, that
the filtering caused audible phase distortions. There are two summation points in the block
diagram in figure 24. At the first summation point the output signals from G2 and G3 are
summed. Here G3 has been low-pass filtered while G2 has not. At the second summation
point three signals are being combined. The first is unprocessed, the second one low pass
filtered, while the third consists of the aforementioned summed G2 and G3 signal which then
has been low pass filtered four times. This results in two potential issues: 1) filtering does not
happen instantaneously. It takes a certain time to process all incoming samples and comb
filtering will occur if a filtered output signal is being added to its unfiltered input signal.
2) Butterworth IIR filters are not linear phase. This is shown in the phase response plots
in figure 25. This means, that the time duration, for different frequencies to pass through
the filter, are not the same. Some pass quickly while other take longer to pass through.

The basics behind the vibrator is a mass-spring system with an electromagnetic engine
as shown in figure 30b. It is based on Newtons 3rd law and can be described by the equation
F1 = F2 −→M1a1 = M2a2
(34)
where M1 is the mass of the Ponto and M2 is the skull bone.
The engine works with,
or against, the force that wants to bring the system back to the static equilibrium point
- effectively generating vibrations.
The system does not have a flat frequency response,
because the incoming sound is being transmitted to the ear by vibrations. The relationship
between the mass of the patients head and the mass of the BAHS gives rise to a characteristic
transfer function with a resonance peak in the range of 800-900 Hz. In figure 29a it can be
seen how the Ponto generally shapes the sound. DSP algorithms are implemented in the
Ponto 5 SP which reduce the resonance peak, but it is still prominent.

The Ponto 5 SP has a MFO which is shown in figure 29b. This is the maximum output
the Ponto 5 SP can generate at each frequency. Four separate bands are defined for the
MFO. They are 100-500 Hz, 500-1000 Hz, 1-5 kHz and 5-9,5 kHz. Algorithms are contin-
uously monitoring the processed sound. If the energy level in one band increases a given
threshold, then all frequencies, belonging to this MFO band, will be attenuated according
to a predefined ceiling. This ensures the patient always hears processed sound which is not
overloaded, saturated or clipping. It is important to take into account, that the MFO may be
counterproductive when adding additional low-frequency harmonics to the incoming signal.

7.7
Listening experiment setup
With all the pre-processed files in place the next step was to conduct a listening experiment.
The primary goal was to investigate if the participants would prefer any of the PowerBass
processed stimuli over the unprocessed version. The second goal was to investigate if any of
the four parameter settings were preferred in particular and finally if the stimuli type played
a role in the the preferred type of processing.
MUSHRA stands for MUltiple Stimuli with Hidden Anchor and Reference. It is a
standard developed by the International Telecommunication Union Radiocommunication
Sector (ITU-R) and defined in ITU-R BS.1534-3 [4]. The standard describes a method to
conduct a subjective listening test to asses intermediate audio quality. It was developed with
Page 70 of 83
==PAGE:76==
Thesis
7.7
Listening experiment setup
the aim at assessing the perceived quality degradation caused by the compression from audio
codecs. It is a double-blind test as neither the assessor nor the test operator knows which
stimuli are being ranked.
Opposed to other types of tests, where the assessors are forced to chose between two
or three stimuli, the MUSHRA method lets the assessor cross compare up to nine processed
stimuli simultaneously. The assessor is presented with a screen with push-buttons and sliders.
The assessors task is to press the ’reference’ button and listen to an unprocessed audio snippet
with a maximum length of 12 seconds. Then the assessor shall press the other buttons and
randomly selected processed versions of the reference are played back.
The assessors task is to provide ratings by the use of sliders in the range from 0 to 100.
The scale is divided into five nominal groups as seen in figure 48. The reference, as well as an
anchor, is also hidden among the processed stimuli. In the standard MUSHRA, the anchor
is a low-pass filtered version of the reference with fc at 3.5 kHz, maximum pass band ripple
= ±0.1 dB, minimum attenuation at 4 kHz and 4.5 kHz at 25 dB and 50 dB, respectively.
Two modifications were made for the MUSHRA listening task in this project.
The
major goal with this project was to determine if adding low-frequency harmonics improved
the sound of the BAHS. Therefore the nominal labels were changed to "Best", "Better",
"Okey", "Worse" and "Worst". The reason for this change was, that the assessors were asked
to rank stimuli sounding equal to the reference in the center and stimuli sounding better
and worse above or below, respectively. The other change was that the anchor parameters
were changed to a 1 kHz high-passed filter. The reason for this change was that the BAHS
transfer function rendered the original anchor imperceptible.
Two possible MUSHRA implementations were setup during this project. The first one
was the Oticon OMEXP, which is an in-house developed tool used to quickly setup listening
tests. The other one was a MATLAB based version. Due to potential bugs, and limited
flexibility in the Oticon OMEXP, it was decided to move on with the MATLAB version.

7.7.1
MUSHRA MATLAB
The custom made MUSHRA consisted of a simple Graphical User Interface (GUI) with a
reference button in the left side followed by six stimuli buttons labeled A-F. Beneath each
stimuli button there was a slider. Pressing any button turned it green and played back a
randomly assigned stimuli. This also increased the font size of the associated slider range.
There were four different input stimuli used, and each one was processed by four different
PowerBass presets. Including the anchor and reference this totaled to 24 different stimuli, as
described in section 7.5. This initially added up to four different MUSHRA screens, but it
was decided to include three repetitions which resulted in the final MUSHRA listening test
containing 12 screens.
The assessor was free to press the stimuli buttons in any order and listen as many times

8.1.1
Interviews
Each listening test ended with a short interview. The TP was asked questions on how the

mentioned, the volume being quite low. Most responded to clearly perceive more low-end

of stimuli. Others were divided on whether or not they would trade more low-end for a
reduction in clarity. There was a common agreement that the GUI was user friendly, while
a few persons requested loop functionality or easier methods to quickly skip between the
reference and other stimuli.

It is not recommended is to perform a series of t-tests to determine which pairs of means

in table 13. It showed significance between 39 combinations of the four different stimuli

9
Discussion
The results showed no clear significance for the general population and thus the null hypoth-
esis could not be rejected. Although, this is a very valid result, there are some facets of the
Page 81 of 83
==PAGE:87==
Thesis
experiment which could have been improved.
The MUSHRA standard clearly states to only include experienced listeners [4, p. 5]. As
the listening experiment took place while COVID-19 infection rates peaked in Denmark, it
was difficult to gather sufficient experienced listeners. Due to limited access to audiometers,
it was also decided to trust each participants statement that they had normal hearing.
Incorporating a short learning sequence into the beginning of the experiment could also
potentially have helped all the participants and minimized contamination.
The output normalization nullified the loudness contribution. This effectively forced
the participants to rank the pitch perception caused by spectral changes - and was necessary
to conduct any significant analysis. Although, in market released products, the loudness
perception also plays a crucial role. Thus, the approximately 3 dB difference between the
raw stimuli and the PowerBass process variation ’D’ could play a role in whether or not the
end-users can perceive a difference - and would actually prefer such a feature enabled in the
product.
Another major challenge in this study was the effect of the MFO. It severely compresses
the low end in the Ponto and equalizes all effects at high input levels. One can argue, that
the sensitivity of the auditory system is reduced at low frequencies for low input levels, and
it is beneficial to add a feature such as the PowerBass anyways, but it is recommended to
incorporate feedback from the MFO into a potential PowerBass feature to make it more
adaptive.
Continuing in those lines, as to how a proper PowerBass algorithm for BAHS could
be developed, it should be investigated if another non-linearity as for instance a half-wave
rectifier would be more suitable.
The PowerBass algorithm was developed more than a decade ago, and today’s embedded
systems are now substantially more powerful. Thus, it is also recommended to investigate
whether it could be beneficial to add the harmonics in the frequency domain.
Building
a more intelligent system, would also open up for the possibility to exploit the inherent
harmonic distortion in the Ponto. Another option is to design a system which performs
parallel processing at various bandwidths in order to increase the effect while minimizing
artifacts like IMD.
35 % of the participants expressed they liked the enhancement while 15 % disliked it.
This leaves 50 % with a neutral position. Based on this, it is recommended that such a
feature is not enabled by default. Instead, it should be possible for the user to activate it by
choice.
10
Conclusion
This research aimed to answer if the perceived audio quality of bone-anchored hearing sys-
tems can be improved by exploiting the principle of the missing fundamental. Four different
input stimuli were pre-processed through a non-linear system which added odd harmonics
to the stimuli based on four different parameter combinations.
A MUSHRA listening experiment was conducted, and based on the subjective assess-
ments from 18 test persons, seven of which where objective listening experts, no significant
Page 82 of 83
==PAGE:88==
Thesis
result was obtained. Analysing assessments from the isolated objective listening experts, sta-
tistical significance was found between ratings of the processed and unprocessed (reference)
stimuli. Using Tukey’s honestly significant difference criterion on a multiple comparison pro-
cedure showed population marginal means significantly different from the reference. Most
significance was found for processings with wide bandwidth saturation indicating high pref-
erences for broader saturation at the cost of timbre alterations.
This research clearly illustrates that adding harmonics to bone-anchored hearing systems
has some potential to improve the perceptive audio quality, but also raises the question if it
would be more beneficial to add such harmonics more selectively. With today’s processing
power this could be done in the frequency-domain in a non-linear way based on the input
spectra. To better understand the implications of these results, future studies could address
the effect of input spectra in relation to assessor preferences as well as the physical limitations
due to the Maximum Force Output. Further research is also needed to understand how the
inherent harmonic distortion of bone-anchored hearing systems can be utilized in a more
intelligent system.
