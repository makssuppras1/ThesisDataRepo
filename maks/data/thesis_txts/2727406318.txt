Abstract
For urban stormwater treatment, wet detention basins are considered the Best Available Technology
(BAT) for Stormwater Control Measures (SMCs) in Denmark. However, the foundation behind wet
basins as BAT is based on insufficient reasoning and hinders the development of new SMCs. Due to
the lack of national protocols, Teknologisk Institut (TI) developed and published a new test procedure
for new SCMs to create a common practice for comparing SCMs. In the context of this, this thesis
conducted a performance assessment of the novel treatment train Rensebassin Syd (RS). Water samples
throughout the treatment units were examined and flow sensors were used to model the mass balance in
RS. The monitoring requirements from the discharge permit for RS were evaluated in relation to the test
procedure from TI. The mass balance model was evaluated by historical precipitation data to assess the
long-term performance of RS. RS showed higher removal efficiency of total nitrogen, total phosphorus,
total suspended solids, biochemical oxygen demand, and chemical oxygen demand compared to wet
basins, except for PO4-P. The mass balance model for November 2022-March 2023, based on sensor and
weather data, provided insights into the system’s behavior but was compromised by inaccurate sensor
readings. The current monitoring procedure from the discharge permit did not meet the comprehensive
requirements according to the test procedure from TI. Using the mass balance model in RS, the long-
term simulation results for November 2011-April 2023 were compromised by the inaccurate sensors and
resulted in unrealistic results for accumulated volume and water levels in RS. For further research,
addressing data inaccuracies and refining the model will enhance the reliability of the performance
assessment of RS.

Introduction
As population growth and urbanization pose more complex challenges for urban planning, climate
change also poses a demand for more resilient, flood-proof, and sustainable urban drainage systems
in cities [1]. A particular cloudburst in Copenhagen in 2011 showcased the importance of sustainable
urban drainage systems, as the current sewage system could not manage the amount of stormwater, and
flooding was seen in several places. This particular cloudburst resulted in urgent climate adaptation
measures, with a focus on cloudbursts and storm floods in Denmark [2]. Consequently, the construction
and development of sustainable cities have placed significant emphasis on addressing these challenges
and ensuring effective urban infrastructure in the face of changing climate patterns [1].
As urban stormwater poses potential pollution of surrounding and receiving water bodies, some degree of
treatment is needed. As studies show the quality and quantity of stormwater depend on the catchment
area, existing drainage system, and variations of concentration of solids, nutrients, and metals are seen
in stormwater [3],[4]. Filtration, sedimentation and detention are some of the principles that are applied
in stormwater treatment. To manage and treat the stormwater, different Stormwater Control Measures
(SCMs) and Best Management Practices (BMPs) are applied, such as smaller rain beds along roads or
green roofs in Copenhagen to more area-demanding wet basins for detention and retention of stormwater
[5]. The use of different BMPs depends on many factors, such as the quantity and quality of stormwater,
catchment area, existing drainage system and the recipient.
The current regulatory and legislative framework in the EU requires Water Quality Standards to be met
for the receiving water. In Denmark, the environmental authority has criteria for stormwater discharge
that should be carried out according to the Best Available Technology (BAT), which is a dynamic
standard and not linked to one specific treatment technology. Wet detention basins have been the only
accepted BAT so far according to the Ministry of Environment and Food of Denmark [6]. However,
the decision of BAT has not been based on pollution levels or emissions but is based on the design and
structure of the treatment technology. This verification of wet basins hinders the BAT criteria as a
driver for the development of new technology [6], and critique has been made of the choice of wet basins
as being BAT [7].
Even with comprehensive understanding, testing and ranking of different BMPs [8], there is no clear
outlined approach or procedure on a national basis for testing and documentation of BMPs that also
allows for comparison between different treatment methods. Even with other SCMs available, there
are currently no guidelines or national standards for how to test systems or report the results [6].
Furthermore, this also poses a challenge for the municipality, water supply companies, project managers
and contractors [9]. In the lack of clear procedures on a national level, the implementation of new
and innovative BMPs is complicated.
As a result, Teknologisk Institut (TI), which facilitates and
communicates scientific information within professional settings, published in 2021 their version of a
test procedure for SCMs [10]. This was made in order to obtain a common national guideline for the
testing and documentation of BMPs.

In 2021, while constructing a residential area in Køge Nord, sustainable urban drainage systems were
examined for a separate sewage system. As a result of Spildevandsplanen, a novel treatment train was
planned and implemented in Køge Nord. This treatment train (defined by its Danish name, Rensebassin
Syd - RS), consists of several SCMs, such as small detention ponds along the main road, sedimentation
pipes and a wet filter basin before the outlet to Snogebækken, which discharges into Køge Bugt, a Natura
2000 protected area. Even though 65% of SCMs in Denmark are wet basins [11], it is the combination
of the different treatment units that make RS a novel and unique SCM.
After the construction of RS was done in 2021, water samples were taken and sensors were installed at
the inlet and outlet. With the existing data from sampling and sensors, the objective of the report is to
provide a comprehensive performance assessment of the stormwater treatment train in Køge Nord. In
relation to the lack of national guidelines for the testing and documentation of BMPs, the test procedure
from TI will be applied to RS. This application will allow the gaps, disadvantages and advantages
between the monitoring program from the discharge permit [12] and the test procedure from TI. The
following research questions were considered when analyzing the performance of RS:
1. Assessing the removal performance of RS by estimating the mass balance using sensor data and
water samples.
2. Assessing the feasibility of current monitoring procedures by comparing the test procedure from
TI with the discharge permit from Køge Municipality and practical experience.
3. Assessing the long-term performance of RS by modeling the mass balance to investigate the dy-
namics and patterns over different historical precipitation data.
Køge Nord can be seen as delivering an interesting front-runner to discuss and support the current
discussion about BAT in Danish waters. Through this assessment, this report aims to contribute to the
development of standard procedures for testing and reporting of new and innovative SCMs in Denmark.

Background
In this section, an overview of the chemical composition of stormwater and treatment technologies of
stormwater will be presented. Furthermore, the building stones for BAT for SCMs will be presented, as
well as the case study of RS.
5.1
Components in stormwater
Stormwater consists of different substances, and the quality and quantity are affected by many factors.
The composition of the stormwater is dynamic and depends on the upstream activity, such as stormwater
runoff from high trafficked areas, e.g. highways, often have another composition than runoff from green
infrastructure [13].
Stormwater can be characterized by chemical and physical properties, such as organic and inorganic
substances, nutrients, metals, pathogens, and suspended solids.
For this project, there was a focus
on the compounds that are required from the water samples from the discharge permit: nitrogen (N),
phosphorus (P), total suspended solids (TSS), biochemical oxygen demand (BOD), and chemical oxygen
demand (COD).
Nutrients such as nitrogen and phosphorus are components that are of major concern when treating
stormwater. Nitrogen can be present in stormwater in different forms, and in this study, the focus was
on total nitrogen (TN), inorganic (nitrite (NO2-)+nitrate (NO3)) and ammonia (NH3)+ammoniumN
(NH4-N).
For TN the typical concentration ranges from 1-3 mg/L in stormwater in Denmark, and
the removal efficiency of a wet basin is 20-40% [14]. For NO2-+NO3, a study showed in Denmark a
mean concentration of 0.33 mg/L, and 0.15 mg/L for NH3+NH4-N [13]. High levels of nitrogen can
result in eutrophication, and harmful algal blooms if discharged into water bodies. Furthermore, there
is also a higher risk of an abundance of pathogens [15]. Total phosphorus (TP) is the sum of dissolved
and particulate phosphorus, and sources of TP are different land uses, e.g. lawns in residential areas.
The soluble part of phosphorus is mostly represented by orthophosphate forms. A study found the
concentration of TP and orthophosphate of 0.56 and 0.15 mg/L, respectively [13]. For wet basins, the
removal efficiencies are 60-80% and 50-75% for TP and orthophosphate, respectively [14].
Besides nutrients, organic matter also affects the quality of stormwater. BOD is the amount of dissolved
oxygen consumed by microorganisms during organic matter decomposition. Typical concentrations vary
from 2-10 and 20-100 mg/L for BOD and COD, respectively. For wet basins, removal efficiencies are
20-40% and 30-60% for BOD and COD, respectively [14]. TSS is also an important factor in the quality
of stormwater. The sources of TSS in stormwater are runoff from construction sites, erosion and urban
runoff, and consists of solids, soils, debris, and organic pollutants. Runoff from highways showed TSS
concentrations varying from 116-140 mg/L [13]. For wet basins, the removal efficiency can vary from
70-90% [14]. A summary of concentrations at the inlet and outlet of a wet basin and removal efficiencies
can be seen in Table 1.

5.2
Development of stormwater management in Denmark
Management of urban stormwater has been a focus of research for several decades. The complexity
of the topics has led to several keywords being used in the terminology: BMPs, SUDS, LID, SCMs,
etc.
The keywords overlap, but as pointed out by [16], the terms can be classified by two broad
principles: "(i) mitigation of changes to hydrology and evolution towards a flow regime as much as
feasible towards natural levels or local environmental objectives, (ii) improvement of water quality and
a reduction of pollutants." In relation to the terminology surrounding urban drainage, there is also a
shift from focusing on health and flood mitigation to including economic, environmental, and social
aspects. Even with different dimensions and designs, constructed wetlands and SCMs such as rain beds
can be created to achieve high functionality in combination with aesthetic, recreational and value for
the surrounding nature [17],[18]. In 2020, the distribution of different treatment technologies currently
applied in Denmark was investigated, and wet and dry detention ponds were responsible for 65% and
24%, respectively [11].
In general, there are guidelines and recommendations for stormwater management in Denmark [19],
[17]. The legal basis states that discharge of stormwater to a recipient shall be regulated as diffuse load
according to The Danish Nature Agency [20], and as mentioned previously, the discharge of pollutants
shall be limited by the use of BAT. Which treatment that is applied, is based on the assessment that is
carried out in the project.
As mentioned by TI, it is the lack of a common national procedure that complicates the navigation
between the different guidelines and recommendations. As seen from literature[11], "We need to start
managing separate stormwater discharges at a strategic level, and here there is a good opportunity

5.3

area, which would consist of residential areas with family houses and apartments, innovative stormwater

area. However, during this thesis project’s execution, only the construction of the residential housing
surrounding the main road was carried out, and therefore the thesis only represents the current state of
the catchment area. See Figure 31 an Appendix B for the full construction of the catchment area. The

Stormwater flows from the main road through six Sedipipes in parallel, which are indicated as red pipes
in Figure 2. The Sedipipes function as sedimentation of fine and coarse particles and separate light
liquids from stormwater. Each pipe is 6m long and with a maximum capacity of 30 L/s, giving a total of
180 L/s. It is assumed that up to 97.5% of the annual precipitation can be treated through this setup.
At higher flow, the water flows past the pipes and directly into the delay basin. There are located sand
traps in the inlet to the Sedipipes. After the Sedipipes, the water is led into the first basin, the delay
basin. The basin has a volume of 350 m3, and consists of layers of soil and sand (zone B), and is used for
detention and sedimentation. A smaller pipe then directs the water to the filter basin, consisting of both
Zone A and B. If the delay basin has met its storage capacity, an overflow will direct the water directly
onto the filter basin, as seen on Figure 3b. Zone A consists of different sand and soil with vegetation on
top, and this zone is referred to as the filter zone. The water infiltrates the filter zone and is collected
in drainage pipes, which ensures the water is directed towards the outlet of the filter basin. Under zone

Some of the processes that occur in RS are sedimentation, evaporation, sorption and physical filtration.
Sedimentation is the process of particles settling to the bottom of a basin or well. The process depends
on particle size, viscosity and turbulence in the water. Furthermore, the efficiency of the process also
depends on the detention time, area of the basin, and flow dynamics. For wet basins with 250 m3
volume pr reduced area, there is an expected removal efficiency of 70-90% for TSS [14]. Evaporation is
used for the mass balancing of SCMs as a loss of water. As the water evaporates from the basin, the
concentrations of the compounds can be increased. The potential evaporation depends on the type of
surface and weather conditions [13]. Another process for reducing solids is by the use of filters [5]. In
RS, zone Z and B represent filters, consisting of sand and soil layers, that retain the particles. Besides

filtration, there are also two other processes occurring in the filter: Adsoprtion and absorption, where
dissolved substances in the water are bound to the surface of the particle or in the particle itself [5].
From the consulting engineering company Envidan and utility company Klar Forsyning, there is an
expectation of RS to meet and perhaps even surpass the criteria of BAT. The expected concentrations

efficiencies are 57% for TSS through Sedipipes, and 30-50% for phosphorus depending on the velocity of
water and part of phosphorus bound to particles. The phosphorus in the water entering the filter basin
will then be reduced further by 50% and 10% would already have been removed in the smaller detention
ponds. This gives an expected reduction of 80% of phosphorus. By ensuring a long residence time in
the filter basin, the treatment processes are optimized, and nitrogen is assumed to be reduced to the
same level as wet basins of 40%. Combined with the long residence time, the nutrient uptake in plants,
and anaerobic and aerobic soils, nutrient removal is expected to meet the same removal efficiency as wet
basins. Besides the nutrient removal, RS is designed to have a permanent water table of 5cm which will
create recreational value for the area, as seen in Figure 3c.
As summarized in the discharge permit, there is an overall evaluation from Køge Municipality that the
discharge to Snogebækken will not extend more than T5, stormwater will be cleaned to a minimum
of BAT criteria, protection level from Habitatsbekendtgørelsen will be ensured and there will be no
groundwater infiltration from RS.
5.4
Teknologisk Institut’s test procedure
The test procedure from TI was published in 2021 after the discharge permit for RS was published
by Køge Municipality [10]. It was developed by "Projektnetværket Regnkvalitet der BAT’er" led by
TI in collaboration with 12 municipalities, 14 public utilities, 7 manufacturers, 3 consultants, and 6
institutions. With this test procedure, results from different treatment solutions will be comparable.
The test procedure recommends three approaches to document the effect of the treatment: the diversity
of treatment solutions, different stages of documentation and the practical execution.
For each of
the approaches, there are recommended tests and measurement methods to achieve a uniform and

7.1.3
Sources of errors for sensors
The magnitude of the following error is unknown, but it however important to highlight. During an
inspection on 13/02/2023, lower water levels of 0.5 m difference were observed in four out of six of the
wells at the outlet of Sedipipes. From further investigation, the system was declared leaking, and four
out of the six Sedipipes were shown to have leaky junctions. In April and March, the soil surrounding
the pipes was excavated, and all the junctions were welded together from the outside. It was unknown
how much water had been leaking from the system and to which magnitude it could play a part in the
mass balance but it was an important uncertainty. See Figure 36 in Appendix G for pictures of the
excavation of soil around the Sedipipes.
The difficulties of correct sensor setup are a well-known obstacle when investigating mass balances in
SCMs. It was important to remember that the SCM was designed to detain and attenuate peak flows,
often with high return periods of rain events. However, the outflows were often frequently low. The
great range in outlet flows can be difficult for the sensor to cover [33]. In RS the maximum allowed
discharge is maximum 23.15 L/s, but the observed flows do not go near this value. The maximum
water levels in the pipe have been a few centimeters, meaning that the sensor perhaps can have high
uncertainty with smaller flows.
A way to minimize the uncertainties for outflow measurements could be to use another setup, that can
capture low and high flows without compromising the reliability of measurements. One example of this
is a study using a 10 mL tipping bucket, 1L tipping bucket and electromagnetic flowmeter, where it was
possible to set up an accurate mass balance for a vegetated green roof [33]. Another option would be
to verify the water level in RS. The permanent water table of 0.05 m of water will differ from day to

7.4
Current monitoring procedure in comparison to test procedure
In the following section, the differences between the discharge permit, TI’s test procedure, and practical
experience will be presented and discussed. Recommendations in order to meet the different criteria
will be presented followed by the economic aspects of the sampling expenses. Lastly, a perspective on
other countries’ national guidelines and test procedures will be looked into as to how implementation
has been successful.
The discharge permit from Køge Municipality stated that water sampling at the inlet and outlet has to
be done after the establishment period in order to determine treatment efficiency. The analysis should
include suspended solids, total phosphorus, total nitrogen and dissolved phosphorus, and Sorbicells could
be used. In the first year, four evenly distributed water samples were required at the inlet and outlet.
In the third year, two water samples at comparable dates were required. Lastly, after five years samples
from the filter basin should be taken, and partial replacement of the filter should be done every 20 years.
The four water samples were taken both as grab samples and Sorbicells in the inlet and outlet, and the
requirements for the first year were fulfilled according to the discharge permit. However, the criteria of
"evenly distributed" was not clarified, whether or not it should be evenly distributed over the whole year
or within a certain period. The water samples were representative for the period of November-March,
but it was unknown how the seasonal variation of precipitation, evaporation and continuous construction
upstream would affect the treatment efficiency of RS.
Furthermore, there were noticeable differences in sampling between the discharge permit and test pro-
cedure. For TI, there was a requirement of a minimum of ten rain events for water samples at the inlet
and outlet, and the water samples should comply with the parameters, as seen in subsection D.2. The
total of eight water samples (grab and Sorbicell) did not meet this requirement and the water samples
did not analyze for the parameters according to TI.
Based on the requirements for TI, presented in Table 7, the current sampling methods used for RS did
not meet all the requirements. Flow proportional water sampling and flow measurements were done
at inlet and outlet according to TI and precipitation measurements were taken from KER. However,
as mentioned before, the minimum of 10 rain events was not met.
The requirements from TI also
emphasized that sampling should reflect the seasonal variation, dry and wet weather events and periods
with small and big rain events, which were not represented with the current water samples.

In order to fulfill the criteria for sampling, the following recommendations could be considered:
• Increase the number of water samples to include dry and wet weather events and seasonal vari-
ations. As mentioned before, Rockflow has applied TI’s test procedure to their product and ten
water samples were obtained from December-September with varying rain depths and intensities.
The water sampling was done by flow proportional sampling.

• If automatic sampling is not possible, TI recommends 1L water sample per minute in 20 minutes

A publication from 2017 compared the current frameworks for stormwater quality improvement devices
evaluation protocol [SQIDEP] in Australia, Austria, Germany, New Zealand, Switzerland and the United
States [38]. In Australia and New Zealand, efficient national protocols were yet to be implemented
formally. Several interesting findings were found in relation to the development of guidance protocols
and it was summarized how the development was non-linear. In Australia, the delay in the development

7.5.1
Uncertainties for modeling of mass balance
In light of the inaccurate data available for this project, it was not possible to apply the model and
achieve realistic mass balances. However, there were still uncertainties regarding the mass balance’s
parameters and assumptions that needed to be addressed regardless of the inaccurate sensor data.
For extending the complex modeling of mass balance in RS, the use and implementation of different
software could be explored. The Danish Hydrological Institute (DHI) offers consulting and software
used for integrated hydrological modeling of catchments, and the software MIKE SHE offers modeling
of catchment dynamics, runoff, evaporation, etc.
However, in order to elevate the current model used for the mass balance in RS, several factors could
be investigated and implemented. The mass balance could be expanded to include evapotranspiration
(ET) and not solely include evaporation (E). E is the process of water going from liquid to vapor phase,
and the ET process occurs in the substomatal cavities of vegetation. For vegetated surfaces, plants
withdraw water and nutrients from the soil and roots zone. The water is transported to the stem and
leaves of the plant, where nutrients are processed. The water then evaporates from the hole in the
leaves, called stomata [41]. When modeling the hydrological cycle of RS, two mass balances could be
set up: the energy balance of the Earth’s surface and the energy balance for the ponding water on top
of the basins could be accounted for. Therefore, a more comprehensive mass balance for RS could be
the investigation of the energy fluxes from the surface of RS. In order to investigate ET, flux chambers
could be used for measuring the flux of water vapor from the vegetated surface of RS. Another option
could be to use meteorological data for the calculation of potential evapotranspiration. By including the
ET variation of day and night values, it is also possible to investigate the infiltration rate of the delay
and filter basins [33]. By the inclusion of ET and infiltration rate from RS, the mass balance could be
presented with more accurate data.
7.5.2
Uncertainties and simplification of input data
For simplification of the data requirements for the mass balance, assumptions were made for the used
weather data. The first assumption was the model only had inflows of stormwater runoff from upstream
catchment and precipitation and only evaporation and outlet flow to Snogebækken. Other interactions,
such as soil moisture dynamics, evapotranspiration and infiltration of water through the filter basin were
excluded from the mass balance for simplification purposes. These simplifications introduce uncertainties
in the model’s ability to accurately represent the true water balance of RS, and if they were to be

Conclusion
The objective of the report was to conduct a performance assessment of Køge Nord’s novel stormwater
treatment train that consisted of sedimentation pipes, delay and filter basins. The removal efficiency of
the Sedipipes and basins was estimated through the use of different monitoring methods, such as water
sampling and sensor measurements conducted by other technicians at the inlet and outlet of RS. The
removal efficiency was determined by two different methods of water sampling. A model was developed
to simulate the mass balance for the period spanning November 2022 to December 2023 using flow sensor
data. In relation to TI’s published guidance protocol for testing new stormwater treatment solutions, the
monitoring program from the discharge permit for Rensebassin Syd was assessed according to TI’s test
procedure. To broaden the scope of the analysis, the mass balance model was applied for the assessment
of the performance of RS by long-term simulations using historical precipitation data.
To conclude the findings and results from the report, the following points can be concluded:
• Based on the water samples results, it can be concluded that reductions of TN, TP, TSS, BOD
and COD were higher than expected from the discharge permit and the BAT requirements for wet
basins. However, both grab and Sorbicell sampling indicated lower-than-expected reductions for
PO4-P compared to the discharge permit and BAT requirements. It was possible to set up a mass
balance of RS using sensor and weather data. However, inaccurate results were obtained as the
outlet sensor underestimated the flow, which resulted in an unrealistic accumulation of volume
and water levels in RS.
• Additionally, by comparing the discharge permit from Køge Municipality with the test procedure
from TI and practical experience, the feasibility of the current monitoring procedures was evalu-
ated. It was concluded that the discharge permit did not meet the requirements specified in the
test procedure.
• The modeling of long-term simulations of the mass balance in RS was based on the parameters
estimated on the available sensor flow data from the period 01/12/2022-15/03/2023. In light of
these results, the long-term simulation results from the model were compromised due to incorrect
sensor data and not due to incorrect model setup.
The results demonstrate the performance of Sedipipes and basins in reducing pollutants, as well as
the need for further improvements in monitoring procedures using sensors. The long-term simulations,
although compromised by sensor data inaccuracies, provided insights into the overall behavior of RS.
Addressing data inaccuracies and refining modeling techniques will enhance the reliability of long-term
simulations for the performance assessment of RS.

Further research
In the context of the work conducted in this thesis, further research could be done for the assessment
performance of RS.
• To further investigate the reliability of the outlet sensor, it would be possible to determine the
flow velocity based on the water levels measured in the pipe. This could be done using Manning’s
equation, which is based on uniform flow in open channel pipes and utilizes flow velocity, flow area
and slope.
• The mass balance for RS could be explored further by investigating the water level in the basins.
In order to limit the uncertainty of the magnitude of the water level on top of RS, a water scale
could be implemented in the basins. It would thereby be possible to account to a certain degree
the variations of the permanent water table of 5 cm.
• Water dynamics and porosity in the soil of the filter basin could be included.
Furthermore,
investigating the distribution of TSS from the inlet to the outlet of the basins could support the
assessment of the treatment performance of the filter basin.
• Finally, with the accurate measurement of the outlet flow, the model would be able to provide more
reliable and precise results, enhancing the understanding of the treatment train’s performance in
the area. By correcting the outlet sensor, it would also be possible to apply the model when the
construction of the whole catchment area is completed.
