Abstract
In recent years, Performance Based Design (PBD) techniques have gained prominence
in fire safety design for buildings worldwide, allowing greater flexibility in fire detection
device placement. This thesis investigates the challenges and solutions for modelling smoke
detector responses using Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulations, such as the
Fire Dynamics Simulator (FDS), with the aim of enhancing the accuracy and reliability
of detection design tools for engineers in PBD scenarios.
The study examines the theoretical background of fire modelling and detection, focusing
on spot-type photoelectric detectors. A comprehensive literature review is conducted to
assess the suitability of existing experiments for replication in computational simulations.
In collaboration with Fire Eater A/S, a novel experiment with smoke detectors is de-
signed and executed, yielding mean activation times for various detector distances and
fuel diameters.
The modelling process in FDS involves several technical choices and uncertainties in in-
put property values, such as the soot yield value and the detector’s obscuration threshold.
Additional uncertainties were identified upon validating simulation results with the exper-
iment, extending beyond those reported in the literature. The study ultimately demon-
strates the utility of a bias factor representing smoke detector activation uncertainty for
design engineers, effectively addressing the thesis’s primary questions and highlighting the
significance of accurate and reliable calculation tools in PBD scenarios for optimizing fire
detection and safeguarding human lives and resources.

1.1
History of Fire Alarms and Smoke Detectors
Humans have always had a conflicted relationship with fire. Once the technology to control
it was developed, the advances it brought to society were massive. On the other hand, fire
also brought numerous property and life losses when not controlled. Due to the dynamics
of a fire’s growth and spread, it was clear that detecting and signalling a fire in its earlier
stages makes it much easier to control and extinguish it. For that reason, the first fire
alarms were created.
It is uncertain when the first fire alarm systems began, but before the advent of electricity,
the early towns relied on human sensory (visual, hearing, smell) detection and posterior
manual signalling through ringing bell towers [1], [2]. It was only during the XIX century,
following the invention of the telegraph, that the first alarm system was oﬀicially invented.
William F. Channing and his assistant Moses Farmer patented the “Electromagnetic Fire
Alarm Telegraph” on 19 May 1857 [3]. Even though it was an electric alarm, there was no
automatic detection accoupled to this device. It consisted of two boxes with a telegraphic
key and handle. In the advent of a fire, someone would have to crank the handle, and the
device would send an alert to the alarm station, where an operator would alert the fire
department.
It was only in 1890 that Francis Robben Upton patented the first fire alarm with automatic
detection based on the temperature rise above a certain point [4].
The first device operating on smoke detection was discovered by accident by Swiss physicist
Walter Jaeger in the late 1930s. Jaeger was working on a sensor to detect poison gas but
was failing.
In his frustration, Jaeger lit a cigarette, and to his surprise, his gadget
registered a drop in current, responding to the smoke. This was the discovery of the first
smoke detector [5].
1.2
The Role of Detection in Performance-Based Fire Safety
Designs
In recent years, countries worldwide have increasingly adopted Performance Based Design
techniques for fire safety design in buildings. Unlike prescriptive recommendations, these
techniques allow greater freedom in locating fire detection devices.
The correct placement of fire detectors allows for optimal quantification of devices to
ensure building evacuation before untenable conditions are reached.
When insuﬀicient detectors are designed, the Required Safe Egress Time (RSET) may be
greater than the Available Safe Egress Time (ASET), putting human lives at risk.
A recent study conducted by Runefors [6] revealed that smoke alarms in residential settings
accounted for an 84% and 62% reduction in mortality for individuals within the age groups
of 20-44 years and 45-64 years, respectively, in unintentional fires in Sweden.
Not only that, in industrial facilities, a slight delay in detection and alarm sounding can
cause an uncontrollable fire and, consequently, a major industrial disaster.
In the case of an overestimation of detectors, there is a risk of excessive expenses that
damage the contractor, generating a misallocation of resources that otherwise could have

1.3
Fire Signatures and Detector Types
As mentioned in the previous section, smoke is not and has not been the only means to
detect a fire. Several different products resulting from a fire can change the surrounding
ambient conditions and be used for detection; Custer called these products fire signatures
[7].
The fire signatures can be divided into:
• Energy Release Signatures
• Gas Signatures
• Aerosol Signatures
For a signature to be relevant in a detection mechanism, the signal-to-noise ratio must
be greater than the typical environmental variations. A fire must produce enough of the
specified signature so the detector sensor would perceive an unusual disturbance and,
consequently, trigger the alerting/notification system.
1.3.1
Energy Release Signatures
Throughout its course, fire constantly irradiates energy to the surrounding environment.
This electromagnetic radiation is emitted, usually from the Infrared (IR) to the Ultraviolet
(UV) region of the electromagnetic spectrum.
The combustion reaction increases the energy of the stable molecules involved, breaks
their bonds and creates free radicals. In this process, UV and visible light (280-570 nm)
radiation are emitted to equilibrate the discrete energy levels that the electrons can assume
in the atomic orbit. Also, combustion produces compounds such as CO, CO2 and water
vapour that emit IR radiation due to their increase in kinetic energy.
Moreover, detectors can respond to the convected thermal energy of a fire. It is the case
of heat detectors that can activate due to reaching a fixed threshold temperature or the
rapid rate of temperature change.
Several sources of nuisance, both artificial and natural, can disturb a sensor’s energy release
detection, such as hot objects and sunlight. The flame flickering of a fire can be used to
modulate and improve the signal-to-noise ratio, as it is known that the flame oscillations
are in frequencies ranging from 0.5-15 Hz, and the radiation emitted is expected to have
similar frequencies. Recent advances in Artificial Intelligence have also been shown to
increase the precision of IR detectors, as shown by Xavier and Nanayakkara [8], by filtering
human motion nuisance through a Deep Neural Network algorithm.
1.3.2
Gas Signatures
Furthermore, the combustion reaction absorbs and produces different gases during its
occurrence. The addition or consumption of these gases changes their concentration in
the surrounding atmosphere, making it possible to characterise a fire signature. Typically,
these fire signatures are the expected products in combustion, such as CO2 and CO,
whereas the consumption of oxygen results in a characteristic reduction of its content.
Nevertheless, other gas signatures may be more appropriate in the case where it is known
beforehand that some specific fuels are present in the location, including H20, HCl, HCN,
HF, H2S, NH3 and nitrogen oxides.

1.3.3
Aerosol Signatures
Additionally, every combustion process releases small solid and liquid particles into the
atmosphere, ranging in size from 5x10−4 ￿m to 10 ￿m. As they are suspended in the air,
they are called aerosols.
The quantity and characteristics of the aerosol generated in a fire depend on the fuel com-
position (material being burned), the combustion state (incipient, smouldering or flaming)
and the amount of dilution with air, coagulation from Brownian motion and surface depo-
sition [9]. It is well established that depending on these characteristics mentioned above,
a specific type of detector can better respond to the aerosol generated than other types.
The details governing this detection difference will be further explored in the following
sections of this thesis.

Some devices combine sensors for different fire signatures into one single detector, for
example, smoke + heat detectors, smoke + heat + CO detector, and so on.
The operation principle of the most commonly found types of smoke detectors is defined
in NFPA 72 [11]:
• Cloud Chamber Smoke Detection
The principle of using an air sample drawn from the protected area into a high-
humidity chamber combined with a lowering of chamber pressure to create an envi-
ronment in which the resultant moisture in the air condenses on any smoke particles
present, forming a cloud. The cloud density is measured by a photoelectric principle.
The density signal is processed and used to convey an alarm condition when it meets
preset criteria.

• Photoelectric Light Obscuration Smoke Detection
The principle of using a light source and a photosensitive sensor onto which the
principal portion of the source emissions is focused. When smoke particles enter the
light path, some of the light is scattered and some is absorbed, thereby reducing the
light reaching the receiving sensor. The light reduction signal is processed and used
to convey an alarm condition when it meets preset criteria.
• Photoelectric Light-Scattering Smoke Detection
The principle of using a light source and a photosensitive sensor arranged so that
the rays from the light source do not normally fall onto the photosensitive sensor.
When smoke particles enter the light path, some of the light is scattered by reflection
and refraction onto the sensor. The light signal is processed and used to convey an
alarm condition when it meets preset criteria.
• Video Image Smoke Detection (VISD)
The principle of using automatic analysis of real-time video images to detect the
presence of smoke.
Detectors can also be classified based on their coverage area characteristics, e.g. spot,
linear or volume types. Generally, ionisation or photoelectric light-scattering spot-type
detectors are used in residential buildings, while linear-type photoelectric light obscuration
is often used in large compartments. Their operation mechanism is further described in
the following sections.
1.4
Ionisation Smoke Detection Mechanism
As mentioned above, the basic detection mechanism of an ionisation detector consists of
a radioactive element emitting alpha particles in a chamber containing positive and neg-
atively charged plates. Common elements utilised as the radiation source are Americium
241 or Radium 226. The strength of the sources ranges from 0.05 to 80 microcuries [7].
The alpha radiation released in the chamber hit the oxygen and nitrogen molecules in the
air between the electrodes, ionising them. The positively-charged ions are attracted to the
negative plate, and the negatively-charged ions are attracted to the positive plate causing
a small constant current to flow.
Once the smoke enters the chamber, the aerosol particles interact and bond with the
ionised air, decreasing the electric current and activating the alarm. Figure 1.1 shows a
schematic representation of a typical ionisation smoke detector.
1.5
Photoelectric Light Obscuration Detection Mechanism
Photoelectric light obscuration detectors utilise a light source and a photosensitive device
(Figure 1.2).
When smoke is present, the light beam path is blocked, and the photosensitive device
changes its resistance, thus, altering the electric circuitry and triggering the alarm. The
term obscuration might not be the most precise to describe the phenomena, with some
authors suggesting the term light attenuation, as the light can be absorbed, reflected or
refracted by the smoke [10].
Historically, the light source utilised in most applications was an incandescent bulb; how-
ever, nowadays, the typical source is a light-emitting diode (LED), as modern LEDs have

1.6
Photoelectric Light Scattering Detection Mechanism
As with photoelectric light obscuration detectors, a light source and a photosensitive
device are also required for light scattering detectors. The principle differs a little, as the
photosensor is not normally receiving the light but is at an angle with the light beam path
(Figure 1.3). As the aerosol particles enter the light path, light is scattered and reflected to
the sensor, changing its current, voltage or resistance and causing the detector to respond.
Light scattering and obscuration theories have been extensively studied over the past
century and can be examined in more detail in [14], [15]. The most utilised theories state
that the intensity of the light scattered is a function of the incident light wavelength and
the aerosol particle size.
For small particles (up to 0.05 times the wavelength of the incident light), the scattering
theory developed by Rayleigh points out that the amount of light scattered is directly
proportional to the second power of the volume of the particle and is inversely proportional
to the fourth power of the wavelength of the light [16].

1.8
Limitations
The research will be limited to a specific type of smoke detector, the spot-type photoelectric

subjected to most Performance Based Design dimensioning.
The analysis is limited to the Fire Dynamics Simulator, as other Computational Fluid
Dynamics software apply different models to the calculation of phenomena discussed here.
The study will not focus on the detector’s smoke response model as no current model in
the literature represents this behaviour.

Overview of Smoke Detection Models
To accurately predict the response of a smoke detector is a complex task. Not only the
smoke detector must be modelled, but also the fire and smoke must be taken into account.
This can be seen by the way Schifiliti [18] divided the smoke detection model into sub-
models, as shown in the image below:
Figure 2.1: Smoke Detection Model by Schifiliti [18]
Depending on the smoke production model, the smoke aerosol characteristics can signifi-
cantly change and impact the whole detection model. The smoke production is a function
of the fuel composition, combustion state (smouldering or flaming), degree of vitiation of
combustion air and ventilation, and these parameters can alter the particle size distribu-
tion, concentration, morphology and refractive index [10].
The smoke dynamics model will be defined by how the fire grew and spread, the fuels
included, and ventilation conditions change over time [10].
The smoke transport model should be able to account for the changes to aerosol charac-
teristics due to time and distance from the fire source, dilution and transport time. These
changes are caused by gravitational, turbulent diffusion and thermophoresis transport
mechanisms, and coagulation [19]. The transport time from the source to the detector is
also notably affected by the geometry of the enclosure, such as ceiling height, configuration
and barriers [10].
A smoke entry model refers to how easy it is to pass the smoke through the detector housing
and reach the sensor. Lastly, the sensor response to the smoke must also be modelled. It
also responds to aerosol characteristics, such as the amount and size of particles within an
ionisation detector’s chamber or their amount and reflective properties in a photoelectric
detector [10].
Each of these parameters is diﬀicult to establish and could be the object of a thorough
study by themselves, and therefore, are usually not considered in most current detection
computer models. Instead, smoke detectors are normally modelled as rate-of-rise heat
detectors, or the amount of smoke is correlated to an optical density and percentage of
obscuration/attenuation per unit distance. It is a correlation because, as discussed in the

2.1
Zone Models Smoke Detection
The so-called two-zone fire models or “Zone Models” software, such as CFAST [21] from
NIST and Argos [22] from DBI, utilise these methods for smoke detector activation. The
user can input an activation sensitivity, and the device responds when the smoke concen-
tration reaches the threshold.
A zone model works by dividing the compartment into two zones, an upper layer and a
lower layer resulting from thermal stratification due to buoyancy. Conservation of mass,
energy and species equations are applied to each zone, and the interaction between them
results from transport submodels.
The activation inside the fire room is calculated utilising Alpert’s model [23] ceiling jet
equation to obtain its velocity and, thus, the time to reach the detector. Outside the fire
room, the zone models apply pressure balance calculation or assume constant velocity;
therefore, under these circumstances, the results must be carefully evaluated by someone
who understands the model’s limitations.

2.2
Field or Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) Models
On the other hand, field or Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) models are the most
sophisticated deterministic models for simulating enclosure fires [24]. In a CFD model
software, the volume of interest is divided into many subvolumes or cells, and the model
solves the equations of fluid physics (mass, energy and momentum conservation) inside
each cell.
The model can solve for the viscous forces acting on the fluid, or the models can ignore
the viscous forces (inviscid). The inviscid approach, although faster, cannot capture the
effects of turbulent mixing and dilution of species and therefore is not useful for smoke
detection purposes [25]. The substitution of the viscous stress components in the fluid
flow into the momentum equation yields the Navier-Stokes equations, and their solution
is the key purpose of field models.
Currently, there are two main approaches to CFD simulations of fires: Direct Numerical
Simulation (DNS) and non-DNS. DNS has very limited use since it requires very small
grid cells; thus, non-DNS models are more common for most use cases. Non-DNS models
can also be classified into two approaches: Large Eddy Simulation (LES) and Reynolds-
averaged Navier-Stokes equations (RANS). These approaches refer to the models applied
to the unresolved subgrid dissipative processes.
One of the most utilised software in fire safety designs is an LES model, the Fire Dynamics
Simulator (FDS), created, maintained and disseminated by NIST [26].
The FDS Technical Reference Guide, however, makes a caveat about smoke detector mod-
elling:
“Most computer models, including FDS, do not provide detailed descriptions
of the smoke besides its bulk transport. This last point is the most important.
At best, in its present form, FDS can only calculate the velocity and smoke
concentration of the ceiling jet flowing past the detector.
Regardless of the
detailed mechanism within the device, any activation model included within
FDS can only account for the entry resistance of the smoke due to the geometry
of the detector. Issues related to the effectiveness of ionization or photoelectric
detectors cannot be addressed by FDS.” [26]
This means that contrarily to zone models, FDS does calculate the velocity of the smoke;
however, on the left-hand side of Figure 2.1, the user has control only over the Smoke
Entry Model.

Smoke Detection Experiments
Several experiments containing smoke detector data have been selected from the litera-
ture to be considered potential data for simulating in FDS. The experiments have been
classified based on their reporting of certain parameters, including information about the
test environment, the fire characteristics, and the smoke detectors.
These parameters
are crucial for ensuring that the data from the experiments are relevant and accurate for
simulation. A summary of these parameters is provided in Table 3.1.
Regarding the detailed information about the test environment, the experiments should
report the room’s dimensions, the materials used for walls and other obstructions, and
the location of the smoke detectors, as these can determine the barriers the smoke can
encounter during its flow.
The characteristics of the fire that was used in the experiment must also be considered.
It is important to know the combustion material used, as well as whether the fire was
a smouldering or flaming fire. The fire’s heat release rate (HRR) is also an important
factor to consider, as this is an input that can affect the smoke’s behaviour and the smoke
detectors’ performance in FDS.
Ultimately, it is essential to carefully review the experiment’s results, particularly the
smoke detectors’ response time.
This information can be used to determine whether
the data from the experiment is suitable to be compared with the simulation results.
Experiments that report clear and comprehensive results are more likely to provide reliable
data for simulation.

3.1
NIST Indiana Dunes Research Project, 1975-1977
The famous NIST (then known as the National Bureau of Standards) Indiana Dunes
Research Project was a series of full-scale residential fire tests conducted in its first phase
in 1975 by Bukowski et al.[31] and its second phase by Harpe et al. in 1977 [32]. Due to
its richness in the detailed description, this experiment remained one of the best sources of
residential smoke alarm data for several years. Phase II of the experiment was performed
to gather information on fires under no air conditioning conditions.
The experiment expressed the slightly better response of photoelectric smoke detectors to
smouldering fires and ionisation smoke detectors’ slightly better response to flaming fires.

However, since the difference was minimal, the authors emphasised that there were no
apparent advantages between the technologies regarding life-saving potential.
• Room Geometry: The tests were conducted in three different two-story brick
buildings, the J.R. Whitehouse test site, the Lakeshore test site and the Wabash
Avenue site. The room dimensions are not explicitly stated, but there are floor plans

• Fire: The fire was initiated in different locations of the test sites. Both smouldering

the simulation results.
3.2
Bukowski and Bright, 1975
Bukowski and Bright [33] performed a series of full-scale tests to determine whether pho-
toelectric smoke detectors could respond to the same standardised test fires used to assess
the performance of ionisation smoke detectors.
This experiment was historically important for being one of the firsts to define the differ-
ences between the detection response between the two different technologies, suggesting
modifications in standardised fire testings that would result in a later combination of the
UL-167 [34] and UL-168 [35] standards into UL-217 [36] and UL-268 [37].
• Room Geometry: The tests were conducted in a large open space of 18 m x 18 m
x 5 m.
• Fire: The fire was located in the middle of the room, 1.2 m above the floor. The
experiment utilised six fire materials: Shredded paper, polystyrene, gasoline, wood
brand, cotton and polyurethane. Apart from the smouldering cotton fire, all com-
bustions were flaming fires.
• Smoke detectors: Eleven smoke detectors were utilised, eight photoelectric, two
ionisation and one Taguchi gas sensor (TGS). The light-obscuration sensitivities
ranged from 0.5 to 2.5%/ft.
• Results: The results of the 26 fire tests are reported in tables and charts.
These tests contain all parameters to be considered for simulation in FDS.

3.3
Delichatsios, 1980
Delichatsios [38] performed a series of tests to investigate the effects of forced ventila-
tion and temperature stratification on detecting flaming and smouldering fires. Also, it
attempted to specify spacing requirements of smoke detectors for flaming cable fires in
rooms with flat and beamed ceilings and corridors. This work was especially relevant due
to its consideration of smoke stratification effects on the detector response.
The most relevant tests to evaluate smoke detector response were conducted in the corridor
test site and the information in the following sections is regarding these.
• Room Geometry: The general view of the corridor is represented in the report
and shows dimensions of 12.2 m x 1.22 m x 2.44 m. The corridor was assembled
with 32 interlocking plywood panels.
• Fire: The fuel utilised in this experiment was pieces of XPE/neoprene cable cut
into 15 cm lengths. Both flaming and smouldering fires were tested. Acetone was
also used to sustain a flaming condition for the flaming fires.
The fire location
is approximately in the centre of the corridor. Although the fuel material can be
challenging to model, the report also provides HRR values during the detection.
• Smoke detectors: Eighteen smoke detectors were installed in the corridor ceiling,
nine of which were ionisation and nine photoelectric types. The average values of
optical density at response are provided.
• Results: The results of the 9 fire tests are reported in tables and charts.
Although Delichatsios report the parameters necessary to simulate in FDS, the fuel mate-
rial can be challenging to model since it consists of electric cables of complex composition.
The HRR is insuﬀicient to correctly model the fire, as information regarding the smoke
generation (such as the soot yield) is needed and would be of diﬀicult estimation.
3.4
Spearpoint and Smithies, 1997
Spearpoint and Smithies [39] conducted eleven fire tests to examine the performance of
smoke detectors. They utilised analogous ionisation and photoelectric sensors and com-

and U.S. (UL) standards. It also studied the visibility conditions and optical densities.
• Room Geometry: Unfortunately, probably due to limited space, there is no men-

selected to match BS 5445 or ISO standards.
• Fire: The materials utilised included pine wood, polyurethane foam, cotton wick,
heptane and cooking oil. Both smouldering and flaming fires were tested. The fire
location is also indicated in the report.
• Smoke detectors: During the tests, ionisation and photoelectric with sensitivities
complying with BS/EN/ISO and UL 217/268 standards were utilised.
• Results: The smoke detector and sensor time responses are reported in a table
format.
Due to the lack of information regarding the room geometry, this experiment is not con-

3.5
Naval Research Laboratory, 1999-2000
The U.S. Navy tests [40], [41] were two series of experiments attempting to develop a new
multi-criteria early warning fire detection system. With real-scale laboratory testing, the
navy investigations were able to evaluate the impacts of several real and nuisance sources
in different fire scenarios. A vast number of fire tests were performed, providing a valuable
and detailed database for future research.
• Room Geometry: The first series of tests were conducted in a room with dimen-
sions 4.1 m x 6.5 m x 3.6 m. The second series was conducted onboard the ship
SHADWELL and its dimensions are not available in the original report but are
mentioned in Geiman and Gottuck [20] as approximately 8.5 m x 8.5 m x 3 m.
• Fire: The materials utilised included ordinary shipboard combustibles such as oily
rags, cardboard, paper, sheets, mattresses and flammable liquids. Both smouldering
and flaming fires were tested. The fire location is also indicated in the report.
• Smoke detectors: During the tests, ionisation and photoelectric smoke detectors
with different sensitivities (ranging from 0.82 – 11%/m) were used.
• Results: The smoke detector response of the 126 room fire tests and 29 ship fire
tests are reported in tables and charts.
The second phase of this project, conducted by Wong et al. [41], is unsuitable for simula-
tion due to the challenging estimation of the SHADWELL ship geometry. The first phase,
conducted by Gottuk et al. [40] has a large number of tests performed, and the required
parameters are all reported, making it suitable for simulation.
3.6
Gottuk and McKenna, 2002
In 2002, Gottuk and McKenna [42] conducted 53 full-scale tests in a large telecommuni-
cations oﬀice to compare spot-type to aspirated smoke detectors. Different source types,
locations and ventilation conditions were tested. The detection spacing was also compared
with two independent loops of 200 and 400 ft2 per detector.
• Room Geometry: The large oﬀice was one entire floor of a three-story building,
with dimensions of 100 ft x 180 ft x 15.5 ft.
• Fire: The fire materials utilised were mainly wire and cable samples, as these were
the most common sources, according to reports of past fire incidents in telecommu-
nication facilities. The fire locations are also shown in the paper.
• Smoke detectors: Two different laser-based smoke detectors were evaluated: spot-
type and aspirated smoke detectors. Their location and sensitivity (0.05%/ft Pre-
Alarm and 0.1%/ft Alarm) are stated in the article.
• Results: The smoke detector time responses are reported in a table format.
This experiment, similar to what was mentioned in Delichatsios, would have too many
uncertainties when simulating the complex fuel sources, the electric cables. For this reason,
it is not suitable for simulation.

3.8
Xtralis IT/Server Room Fire Test Demonstrations, 2008
Xtralis [47] performed a series of tests in their Test Facility IT/server room to demon-
strate the very early warning of their VESDA smoke detection system. There are several
challenges in IT/server rooms, as they are usually highly ventilated areas that can cause

4
Experimental Setup Design
By designing a new experiment, it is possible to have more control over the parameters
that would be inputted and simulated in FDS. It is possible to be sure, for example, what
were the geometry/opening conditions of the test room, as well as the fire sources and
smoke detectors utilised.
One of the first challenges to testing smoke detectors is that their activation times must
be long enough to avoid too much uncertainty in the results obtained. That is, in the case
of a sensor activating in 10 seconds, and the instruments have inaccuracies of the order
of 1 second, the error due to uncertainty is of the order of 10% of the result. If the same
instrument inaccuracy is applied to an activation time of 60 seconds, the error is only
1.6%.
As a result, a large compartment is required to ensure that the smoke travels a long
distance to reach the detector. With this objective in mind, the company Fire Eater A/S
[52] offered their testing facilities, which are located in Hillerød, Denmark, to conduct the
experiment.
Prior to the experiment, an FDS simulation was run to find the distances required to
achieve such times.
The simulation, if valid, allows testing numerous smoke detector
positions until the optimal one is found.
Information about the enclosure and fire used are some requirements to make the simu-
lation accurate; thus, a walk-through in the test facility was performed to identify and
collect the necessary data, as well as laboratory tests of fire sources.
The test enclosure, earlier simulations, and laboratory tests used to conceptualise the
experiment are all described in the following sections.

High ceiling jet velocities were identified through slices positioned at the same location
as the detectors.
Figure 5.5 illustrates that within just 12.5 seconds, well before the
alarm is triggered, the velocities surrounding the smoke detector have already reached
approximately 0.2 m/s (the black region in the figure).
Some studies report a critical velocity of around 0.13-0.2 m/s [60], [61] for the alarm to
activate without significant delays due to the entry lag mechanism in flaming fires.
The value of 0.15 m/s is also close to the velocity at which a detector must respond to
become UL-listed. EN 14604 specifies a velocity of (0.2 ± 0.04) m/s. Thus, even without
precise knowledge of the critical velocity at which a detector will activate the alarm, it
is assumed that if a detector is certified by UL or EN, activation will occur at velocities
above 0.15, and consequently with minimal entry lag effects.
For this reason, Heskestad’s model (Equation 2.8) was chosen to simulate the detectors,
as with the velocities achieved at the time of detection, there will be no significant delays
in smoke entry into the sensor chamber, only the characteristic mixing time of the smoke
within the chamber.
The characteristic L value of the Esylux detector is unknown; therefore, the FDS default
value (L = 1.8 m) was utilised in the simulation.
The obscuration threshold for activation used was 31.01 %, assuming optical characteristics
similar to the red laser in Table 4.3.

6
Validation and Sensitivity Analysis
In order to validate the smoke detection model, the simulation results must be compared
with the experimental results considering the uncertainties both in the experimental and
model values.
The statistical methodology employed in this thesis is the same as that used in the FDS
validation process. It will be presented briefly here, while the method is studied in greater
depth in [62], [63], [64] and [44].
6.1
Experimental uncertainties
For each activation time, a pair of experimental and model values exists. The experimental
value, subject to uncertainties, should not be regarded as the ”true” value. Under identical
experimental conditions, activation times may fluctuate upon repetition due to random
and systematic components contributing to measurement errors.
Random errors arise from unpredictable sources and cannot be eliminated; however, their
impact on uncertainty can be mitigated through repeated measurements and statistical
analysis. Systematic errors, or biases, persist under identical conditions and cannot be
fully eradicated either. Nevertheless, their influence on uncertainty can be substantially
reduced by implementing corrections such as calibrations. The method presented here only
accounts for random errors, treating any remaining systematic errors in the experiment
as random.
Apart from measurement uncertainty, experimental uncertainty includes the uncertainty
of each parameter inputted into the model, propagating the uncertainty. For example,
suppose one wants the model to predict gas temperature. In that case, it is known by
empirical relations that gas temperature is proportional to the HRR. The HRR already has
experimental uncertainties, which subsequently propagate to the temperature prediction.
The primary sources of uncertainty identified in the thesis experiment involve thermocou-
ple readings, scale measurements, the heat of combustion model for HRR determination,
heptane fuel purity, and smoke detector activation.
Only thermocouple readings and
smoke detector activation (main objective) represent model output values comparable to
the experiment, while other quantities serve as input parameters within the model, prop-
agating their uncertainties.

relationships between the experimental quantities to be assessed and the input parameters
that propagate uncertainties.

6.4
Validation - Updated Bias Factor
Through the sensitivity analysis of input parameters, their relationship with alarm ac-
tivation times could be assessed. This allows for the calculation of a relative standard
deviation for the experiments of eσE = 12 %.
It is possible to directly calculate the bias factor using only the data from this experiment;
however, there is a limitation in the factor’s conception, which assumes the data pairs
ln(M/E) as normally distributed (Equation 6.2) With only four pairs of points, it is diﬀicult
to assume this normality.
For this reason, the experiment of this thesis was combined with the NIST -already part
of the validation process- and the experiments reported by Cleary et al., 2021 to calculate
a new bias factor for smoke detector activation.
Upon incorporating data from additional experiments, it is no longer feasible to employ the
previously calculated relative standard deviation of 12 %, as the other experiments exhibit
significantly greater dispersions. The adopted experimental relative standard deviation
was that of Cleary’s experiment, computed by averaging activation times for the same
location and then normalizing by their average. This relative standard deviation is 0.80
and can be observed in the outcome of Figure 6.13.
Consequently, a bias factor of 0.26 was obtained, which as previously mentioned, indicates
that the model underestimates activations by 74 %.

7
Conclusion
This study investigated the modelling of smoke detectors in CFD software, specifically
within the Fire Dynamics Simulator.
The activation mechanism of the most common detectors available on the market was
introduced to comprehend their limitations and advantages, leading to a focus on spot-
type photoelectric detectors. It was noted that current theoretical models are limited to
smoke entry models inside the detection chamber rather than the precise response of the
activation mechanism in the presence of smoke.
Subsequently, a literature review sought reports of previously conducted experiments to
assess whether these experiments provided suﬀicient information for replication in com-
putational simulations. To this end, the reports found were categorized into four groups
concerning their provided information on geometry, fire and fuel, essential smoke detector
data, and the ease of extracting results from the report. After the review, it was de-
cided to reproduce the NIST Smoke Alarms experiment, which is already part of the FDS
validation process, and Cleary et al., 2021.
With the knowledge gained from the literature review, an experiment was designed in order
to obtain more practical knowledge and control of the simulation parameters involved. A
total of 16 tests were conducted using two frying pans with different diameters containing
the fuel. After various statistical analyses, the final results were the mean times of 49.8
and 60.1 seconds for the 28-centimetre diameter case, smoke detectors 2 and 3 meters from
the fire, respectively, and 60.4 and 123.2 seconds for the 24-centimetre diameter.
With these results in hand, the modelling in the software was addressed, leading to several
technical choices. Several input property values were calculated using literature references,
which led to uncertainties, such as the soot yield value and the detector’s obscuration
threshold.
Ultimately, upon validating the simulation results with the experiment in the final chapter,
it was observed that there are more uncertainties than those reported in the literature,
encompassing not only uncertainties regarding the measurements taken in the experiment
but also uncertainties in Key Input Parameters that propagate into the obtained results.
In conclusion, with a bias factor of 0.26 representing the model uncertainty of smoke de-
tector activation, the utility of this information for the design engineer was demonstrated,
thereby addressing the primary questions of the thesis and completing the study.
