Abstract 
Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons enter wastewater treatment plants mainly by surface 
discharges, but also all types of wastewater, since they are products of combustion 
processes. PAHs are known to be toxic for humans and nature. Their effects and fate are not 
yet totally investigated. These compounds occur in the environment in low concentrations 
making them difficult to trace in aqueous environments by conventional analytical methods. 
In this study, the passive dosing analytical method is used to investigate the partitioning of a 
mixture of 13 priority PAHs in three types of filtered wastewater in order to investigate 
partition coefficients during a wastewater treatment process. A preloaded polymer is left to 
equilibrate with filtered wastewater samples after primary sedimentation, biological 
treatment and secondary sedimentation (effluent) of a wastewater treatment plant. High-
Performance Liquid Chromatography analysis was used in order to determine the Enhanced 
Capacities of the samples for each one of the PAHs and DOC analysis of the samples was 
used in order to derive the partition ratios KDOC of the PAHs tested. 
The polymer was subject to full loading of the PAHs with more than 98% efficiency for each 
compound. The kinetics of passive dosing revealed that equilibrium with ultrapure water 
was reached within the first hour of passive dosing for all the PAHs. Equilibrium 
concentrations 
varied 
significantly 
for 
Chrysene, 
Benzo(b)fluoranthene, 
Benzo(k)fluoranthene, Benzo(a)pyrene and Dibenzo(a,h)anthracene, the heaviest of the 
PAHs (n=19). These compounds were not further tested due to the lack of confidence of the 
results, since in most cases their concentrations were too low to be determined or very 
close to the limit of detection.  The Enhanced Capacities of the samples were determined by 
normalization of the PAH concentrations in wastewater with those in ultrapure water. 
Enhancement was noted only for Benzo(a)anthracene, with higher values for the influent 
and wastewater processed with biological treatment - twice the capacity of ultrapure water 
- while lowest for the effluent wastewater. The transport of the compound is more 
facilitated through binding to DOC at the initial steps of the waste flow treatment processes 
in comparison with the effluent which is characterized by lower DOC concentrations. The 
Enhanced Capacities of wastewater for Acenaphtylene, Fluorene, Phenanthrene and Pyrene 
did not differ from ultrapure water, while the results for Anthracene are not plausible. In 
order to derive the DOC partition coefficient of the treatment procedure for 
Benzo(a)anthracene, the Enhanced Capacities of the samples were plotted against DOC 
concentration. Nevertheless, the KDOC relationship could not be determined because there 
was not found a linear correlation between Enhanced Capacity and DOC concentration. The 
KDOC values of Benzo(a)anthracene were determined for each stage of wastewater 
treatment and were found to be 0.03, 0.14 and 0.08 times the KOW in the influent, after 
biological treatment and after secondary sedimentation respectively.

1. Introduction 
During the last decade, the concern has been raised among environmental scientists 
regarding the effects of Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons to the environment and human 
health. Increased PAH concentrations in aqueous samples could be the result of the 
industrialization, since comparatively large quantities occur to the environment through 
combustion engines. These days certain PAHs have been recognized as probable 
carcinogens and toxics. Indicative of this concern is the fact that various environmental 
agencies across the world have identified certain of them as a priority to be investigated 
concerning their fate and effects by issuing PAH priority lists. According to the United states 
Environmental Protection Agency (US EPA), 16 PAHs make the priority to investigate, 
namely: 
Naphthalene, 
Acenaphthylene, 
Acenaphthene, 
Fluorene, 
Anthracene, 
Phenanthrene, Pyrene, Fluoranthene, Chrysene, Benz[α]anthracene, Benzo[α]pyrene, 
Benzo[b]fluoranthene, Benzo[k]fluoranthene, Benzo[ghi]perylene, Indeno[1,2,3-cd]pyrene 
and Dibenz[α,h]anthrancene. 
The main aim of this project is to determine the binding of PAHs to Dissolved Organic 
Carbon within the different stages of a wastewater treatment plant. For this purpose, 
samples of influent wastewater, samples before the secondary clarifier and effluent samples 
of one or more WWTPs will be passive dosed with the toxic compounds. Their binding to 
DOC will be determined and KDOC will be calculated for each treatment procedure. 
Samples are loaded with a mix including PAHs that have been declared as priority pollutants 
by USEPA, by use of passive dosing techniques. Passive dosing takes place by equilibration 
of the samples with a preloaded polymer of known concentrations of PAHs. The freely 
dissolved concentrations of the toxic compounds are controlled by partitioning from the 
polymer. The speciation in each sample is determined by measuring their total 
concentration by High-Performance Liquid Chromatography. Onwards, the results are 
normalized by taking into account the freely dissolved concentrations in ultrapure water 
(without SS, DOC) which equal in this case, the total concentrations in the sample. The 
results may provide information for comparison of different WWTPs.

2. Theoretical Background 
2.1 Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons 
Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons, also referred to as PAHs are neutral, nonpolar [1] organic 
contaminants that are found in mixtures of numerous chemicals in the environment [2]. 
PAHs are hydrophobic compounds and as implied by their name, their molecules comprise 
two or more benzene rings in various configurations [1]. This group of organic substances 
apart from having toxic effects  and being carcinogenic, gathers environmental concern 
mainly due to their persistency [2] [3] which could make a threat for living creatures, 
humans and the environment. 
2.1.1 Formation and sources 
They are formed naturally as a consequence of atmospheric deposition and combustion 
processes. PAHs may be formed both by natural sources (i.e. forest fires) and anthropogenic 
sources (i.e. conversion processes) [1]. They are by-products that are naturally generated by 
the burning of fossil fuels and biomass [4] and the incomplete combustion of hydrocarbons 
[1] like coal, oil, gas and garbage [3] materials which contain carbon (i.e. wood, tobacco) and 
are usually found concentrated [2]. The sources of PAHs also include tire particles, vehicle 
exhaust, crumbling of motor oil, parking lot sealants etc. [2]. In inside places, PAHs can be 
formed by smoking tobacco, grilling or charring meat [2]. Nevertheless, PAHs are also be 
created by atmospheric deposition and can therefore be found in the air, bound in the 
particulate matter of air pollution [2]. 
2.1.2 Properties 
PAHs share common physical-chemical characteristics, such as high boiling and fusion 
points, low solubility in water and high lipophilicity [5]. The smaller the number of rings or 
the molecular mass of a PAH, the higher its vapor pressure, although it is still low for the 
case of PAHs [5]. What is more, the PAHs whose molecules are structured in a linear way, 
appear to be less stable compared to those with branched structures [5]. What is more, 
values of LogKow which exceed 3.5 might be a potential danger to the environment [6]. The 
solubility of PAHs may be influenced by several factors. It increases in the case of organic 
liquids present in the water [5]. On the other hand, solubility decreases as molecular weight 
rises and also in the case that salts are present in the solution (salting-out effect) [5]. 
Besides that fact, PAHs are frequently met in river basins, where they arrive by rain wash-
out before they are deposited in river sediments [5].  
PAHs are persistent compounds with complex chemical structures, so most of them do not 
break down easily in water and are persistent in the environment [2] [3]. They have a trend 
to sorb to surfaces, and so when they reach aquatic environments they attach readily to the 
particles of the sediment. This fact explains their high concentrations in sediments [2]. PAHs 
==PAGE:14==
4 
 
with high molecular weights appear an increased tendency to be captured by particulates 
[5]. Degradation of PAHs is caused by the microorganisms, light and chemical agents [5]. 
2.1.3 PAHs into aquatic environments 
In the environment PAHs are often found together in groups of two or more [3], since it is 
unusual that contamination is caused by a single PAH [5]. The mix of PAHs may vary 
according to the source of contamination and the degradation processes that might take 
place [5]. They enter the aquatic environment both through atmospheric deposition to the 
water bodies and by direct releases through spills and petroleum use [2]. They also enter 
estuaries through discharges such as landfill leachates, stormwater runoffs and industrial 
discharges, by wastewater treatment plants and also by surface runoff, which leads to high 
PAH concentrations in the bottom sediments of water bodies [2].  
PAHs are carried along runoffs mainly from parking lots and road surfaces, usually 
originating from tire particles, vehicle exhausts, crumbling asphalt, parking lot sealants, 
leaking motor oil, coal gasification and atmospheric deposition. Recent studies have focused 
on identifying solutions to eliminate the risk of PAH contamination from stormwater 
runoffs, mainly suggesting the substitution of coal-tar based sealants and the effective 
management of stormwater runoffs from parking lots and roads [2]. In this context, it is 
worth mentioning that selling tires with high aromatic oils (which generally contain PAHs), 
has been banned by the European Union.  The estimated reduction of PAHs emitted from 
tires is expected to reach 98% [7]. 
2.1.4 Toxicity of PAHs 
PAHs are also known to be toxic to aquatic life [1] [2]. Scientific studies have documented 
detrimental impacts from PAHs on aquatic organisms [2]. The bigger their molecular weight, 
the more stable the PAHs, in a sense of persisting in the environment for longer periods of 
time, being less soluble in water and eventually more toxic to aquatic life [2]. The toxicity of 
PAHs to some aquatic species can increase by exposure to UV light [8]. 
It is unlikely that humans experience the above mentioned impacts as a result of PAHs in 
streams and lakes, or by drinking water, because PAHs have a preference to sorb into 
particles than dissolve in water [2]. According to United States Environmental Protection 
Agency (USEPA), the maximum contaminant level (MCL) for PAHs in drinking water is 0.2 
ppb [2]. Consuming fish is no more of a threat to humans because PAHs do not readily bio-
accumulate within vertebrates [2]; on the other hand they do bio-accumulate in the tissues 
of bivalve mollusks [8]. Benzo(a)pyrene concentration is considered indicative of total PAH 
concentration, so a maximum allowable level of benzo(a)pyrene for bivalve mollusks on the 
market has been set by the European Union [9]. 
Through inhalation, skin contact and ingestion PAHs may be also absorbed by mammals 
[10]. Some PAHs are also considered to be toxic to humans [2] and their toxicity depends 
==PAGE:15==
5 
 
highly on their structure [4]. In spite of the fact that acute toxicity to humans is really low 
[2], some of the most toxic compounds known are included in the PAH group and are 
thought to be carcinogenic, mutagenic and teratogenic [4]. Among these effects, cancer is 
considered the most detrimental towards humans [2]. Despite the lack of studies regarding 
effects on humans, cancer seems to be associated with skin contact or breathing PAHs [3]. 
Professionals that get frequently exposed to PAHs are more affected by lung, skin and 
bladder cancer [11]. Although not thoroughly studied, other non-cancer effects of the PAHs 
are associated with reproduction, development and immunity [2].

probable human carcinogens, among which Benz(a)anthracene and Benzo(a)pyrene, the 
latter being used in many cases as an environmental indicator for the existence of PAHs [2].

2.2 Passive Dosing 
Passive sampling and dosing techniques have been gradually spread because they offer 
many practical and analytical advantages compared to traditional methods that determine 
exposure in water, sediment, air and soil. They can further provide information on the 
bioaccumulation to organisms and can be used to control exposure in toxicity tests. 
Moreover, these methods may be used to measure sorption and predict diffusion and 
partitioning processes in a system [14]. 
At first, passive sampling was used to measure the exposure to chemicals.  It provides the 
advantage of sampling for continuous periods that leads to information about chemical 
concentrations over time. The sampler is a polymer material that is used for sorption, 
Polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS), which acts as a storage compartment that collects substances 
already existing in aqueous environments over time. 
Afterwards, passive dosing analytical method was developed for the control of freely 
dissolved concentrations of hydrophobic organic chemicals (HOCs) [15]. It is used to control 
the exposure to chemicals and it is based on the same partitioning principles as passive 
sampling. The same material is often used. In passive dosing though, unlike passive sampling 
the sampler firstly acts as storage compartment. When passive dosing analytical method is 
used to measure speciation, no phase separation is required. This makes it more precise 
than other methods used in the past.  
Passive dosing controls the speciation of the chemical which is called ‘Model analyte’ and 
does not require separation steps. As analytes can be investigated one chemical or a 
mixture of hydrophobic compounds such as PAHs. 
2.2.1 Passive Dosing Procedure 
The passive dosing procedure that is followed includes the preparation of the polymer for 
loading, the procedure of loading of the polymer with the analytes, followed by the passive 
dosing of aqueous samples using the loaded polymer. Finally the analyte concentrations in 
the samples are determined. 
Loading of polymer with analytes 
At the beginning of the passive loading procedure, the type of polymer that will be used as a 
means loaded with the analytes has to be determined. In many cases, the polymer is 
prepared in the laboratory by casting it into the vials where passive dosing will take place. 
This has to be done taken into account the volume of the vial and the quantity of casted 
polymer in it. Polymers with specific characteristics can be also custom made.  
After selecting the polymer, the analytes are loaded to it in desired concentrations at the 
laboratory, before the polymer is imported to the sample. The analytes are forced to the 
==PAGE:19==
9 
 
polymer as a solution of known concentrations in a solvent. Pure water is then added to 
force the hydrophobic analytes in to the polymer and mark high precision efficiencies. The 
flow of the analytes from and to the passive sampler due to diffusion results in equilibrium 
partitioning [15] between polymer and the loading solution. The concentration of the 
analytes in each compartment depends on the aqueous solubility of each analyte and is 
described by the polymer-water partition coefficient, Kpw. 
Passive dosing of aqueous samples 
On a second level, the loaded polymer acts as the partitioning source of the analytes firstly 
in ultrapure water and on a second level in the aqueous samples of interest. The polymer is 
inserted in certain volume of sample (<10ml). The concentration in the polymer controls the 
speciation of the analytes. The polymer is left in the sample as much time as needed in 
order the concentrations of the analytes to equilibrate between the sample and the 
polymer.  
Determination of total sample concentrations 
After partitioning equilibrium has been achieved the polymer is removed and the total 
concentrations of the analytes in the aqueous sample are measured either by scintillation 
counting or by high-performance liquid chromatography for mixtures of analytes. 
2.2.2 Analytical Passive Dosing Principle 
Passive dosing has evolved to a popular analytical approach that can determine accurately 
the speciation of hydrophobic organic compounds [15].  
Passive dosing kinetics 
The concentration of an analyte during passive dosing follows an exponential trend. The 
kinetics of passive dosing is described by the following equation [15]: 
Csolution(𝑡) = Csolution,eq(1 −e−kt) 
where:  Csolution(𝑡) is the concentration of the analyte in an aqueous solution at a time 𝑡, 
Csolution,eq is the concentration in the aqueous solution at equilibrium of partitioning and k 
is the rate constant. 
Partitioning equilibrium 
The concentration of a hydrophobic analyte in an aqueous sample is [15]:  
𝐶𝑠= 𝐶𝑓𝑟𝑒𝑒+ 𝐶𝑏𝑜𝑢𝑛𝑑  
where: 𝐶𝑠  is the total concentration of the analyte in sample, 𝐶𝑓𝑟𝑒𝑒 is the freely dissolved 
concentration in the sample and 𝐶𝑏𝑜𝑢𝑛𝑑  is the concentration of the analyte in suspended 
and dissolved matter.

2.2.3 Binding to a 3rd phase                
PAHs, being hydrophobic compounds express a preference to bind in medium constituents, 
rather than dissolve freely in water. At the same time, an amount of the total substance is 
found in dissolved concentrations in the sample, whereas a much higher amount is bound. 
Binding to a third phase depends highly on the medium constituents, which in the case of 
humic substances, colloids or salts affect significantly the speciation of chemicals. The 
existence of medium constituents including Aldrich Humic Acids (AHA), hydroxypropyl-β-
cyclodextrin (β-HPCD) or Sodium Dodecyl Sulfate (SDS) may enhance the capacity of a 
solution (E>1). AHA contributes on the partitioning process of PAHs into DOC aggregates, β-
HPCD hosts PAHs in their hydrophobic cavity and SDS hosts PAHs in the formed micelles. On 
the other hand, certain effects are able to reduce the capacity of a solution, one of them 
being the salting out effect that is observed at the existence of NaCl in a solution (E<1).                  
PAHs have a high affinity for organic matter due to their lipophilic and hydrophobic 
properties and they often accumulate in fatty tissues [17]. Consequently, PAHs tend to sorb 
to humic substances and aqueous samples that contain significant organic matter present a 
high capacity of binding and storing PAHs [18]. Dissolved Organic Carbon (DOC), is a way of 
expressing the dissolved organic matter in water [19] and occurs due to plant 
decomposition and animal material. DOC is the organic carbon that is able to pass through a 
0.7-0.22 μm filter [20]. Organic carbon present in soil may dissolve and then move with both 
surface water and ground water [19]. Yellow to brown color of water bodies indicates high 
loads of organic carbon contained which origins from the leaching of humic substances from 
plants and soil matter [20]. Furthermore, DOC forms complexes with trace metals, creating 
water-soluble complexes which can be transported and taken up by organisms [20].

2.3 Chromatography 
Chromatography is a widely used analytical process which separates mixtures to their 
individual components by forcing them to pass through a column of a solid or liquid [21]. 
There, at any time, each component is shared between two phases: the ‘stationary phase’ 
and the flowing ‘mobile phase’ [22]. The stationary phase can be solid or liquid, while the 
mobile phase is either liquid or gaseous. 
During chromatography, the sample is placed onto the stationary phase and then the 
mobile phase passes through it by a procedure called ‘elusion’. The components of the 
sample, ‘solutes’ migrate at different rates through the stationary phase, which leads to 
their separation. Those rates are imposed according to the affinity of each component to 
each phase. Components are continuously transferred from and to each phase by processes 
of sorption and desorption. The solutes transferred along the stationary phase develop peak 
profiles which broaden as a function of time and distance [22]. By processing these peak 
profiles, the relative proportions of analytes in the mixture are measured. 
Chromatographic techniques are classified according to the type of mobile phase used, the 
type and properties of the stationary phase, as well as the shape of the chromatographic 
bed where the stationary phase is set [23]. Those techniques can be summarized in Gas, 
Supercritical Fluid and Liquid Chromatography, denoted as GC, SFC and LC respectively [23].  
Τhe mobile phase is significant because it determines the kind of samples that can be 
analyzed by each technique [23]. Moreover, the shape of the chromatographic bed varies 
according to the characteristics of the mobile phase. In the case that the mobile phase is a 
gas or a supercritical fluid, it flows through the stationary phase which is contained in a tube 
called column, but planar forms of bed also exist. In liquid chromatography there is also the 
option of a geometry which allows fluids to move through a thin layer by capillary action 
[23]. Respectively, when the bed is a column, a pump or a gas stored in a pressurized 
cylinder forces the mobile phase to move through. The column can be either an open 
capillary or a packed tube [23] as shown in Figure 3.  
The stationary phase may be an inorganic absorbent, a rigid organic polymer, a material 
(organic or inorganic) with a chemically modified surface or a liquid coated with a granular 
material [23]. Gas, supercritical fluid and liquid chromatography may be further subdivided 
according to the type of the chromatographic bed and the stationary phase [23]. 
==PAGE:23==
13 
 
 
Figure 3 Chromatographic bed techniques for (A) packed column, (B) open capillary and (C) plane chromatography [23] 
The main properties of each chromatographic method are based on the values of density 
and viscosity of the mobile bed. The density of the mobile phase affects its solvating power 
and determines the choice of eluent that will favor the separation. Viscosity determines the 
pressure which is needed to force the mobile phase through the chromatographic bed and 
determines the solute diffusion coefficient [23]. If the coefficient is low, the capillary or 
particle diameter needs also to be kept low [23]. 
2.3.1 High-Performance Liquid Chromatography 
Liquid chromatography is operated at ambient temperatures [21]. It uses a granular 
stationary phase [23] and the mobile phase is a liquid solvent that contains the sample [24]. 
This type of chromatography may be further divided according to the types of method 
implemented. In liquid chromatography a pump and a detector are needed for the transport 
of the mobile phase and in order to observe the different analyte fractions [23] with the 
type of analysis of choice. Liquid chromatography can be divided in types according the 
separation mechanism or the type of the stationary phase [24].  
In order to distinguish from conventional liquid chromatography, the term High-
Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) has been introduced to emphasize on the new 
advanced techniques based on finer stationary phases with particles no larger than ~10μm 
[23]. Liquid chromatography includes paper chromatography, thin-layer chromatography, 
and column techniques, which are all considered to be “High Performance” but regard 
different technical configurations and separation processes [23]. HPLC is the most used type 
of elusion chromatography and a well-known tool in environmental science, toxicology and 
multiple other fields. It is widely spread because it can efficiently separate various organic, 
inorganic and biological species [24].  
During High-Performance Liquid Chromatography the solutes migrate through the 
stationary phase found in a column with rates according to the distribution ratios. The 
stationary phase consists of micro-particles in order to achieve efficiency in resolution. 
When the stationary phase is more polar than the mobile phase, the separation is called 
normal-phase. On the contrary, in reversed-phase separation, the stationary phase is less 
==PAGE:24==
14 
 
polar than the mobile.  Elution can occur under isocratic conditions (the mobile phase has a 
constant composition) or with a gradient composition that is preferable for mixtures in the 
case of wide range of polarities in a sample. For reversed-phase separations, usually, 
mixtures with methanol or water with acetonitrile are used and for normal-phase 
hydrocarbons with chlorinated solvents or alcohols [22]. The mobile phase needs to be 
pumped in the column generating a back-pressure [22]. Finally, the data enter a 
microcomputer and recording system that controls and processes data. 
2.3.2 HPLC Instrumentation and Principles 
The schematic configuration of an HPLC is shown in Figure 4 and consists of:  
o the solvent delivery system, 
o pumping systems 
o the sample injection system, 
o the HPLC column 
o the detectors 
 
 
Figure 4 Schematic diagram of a high-performance liquid chromatograph [26] 
The delivery system consists of a mechanical pump, the solvent reservoirs, a de-gassing 
solution and a gradient former that generates mixtures of the solvents. The liquid phase 
usually consists of a mix of two solvents that according to the overall polarity of the eluent, 
the polarity of the stationary phase and the nature of the sample components, aims to 
achieve the desirable elution [22]. Evolved HPLC systems are also provided with valves that 
introduce liquids at various proportions [24].  
==PAGE:25==
15 
 
The solvents are delivered at high pressures (up to 6000 psi) with a constant and 
reproducible flow rate [22]. The main three types of pumps used in this occasion are the 
screw-driven syringe pump, the reciprocating pump and the pneumatic or constant-pressure 
pump [24]. The most common way of sample injection is based on a sampling loop for 
sampling sizes from 5 to 500μL. Usually, either this or in other cases, an autosampler - 
injector makes part of the equipment [24]. The high pressure flow of the solvent which 
carries the sample enters the loop by manipulating the valve [21]. 
The HPLC column is made from stainless steel, but for applications of low pressures also 
glass and Tygon are used. Their usual length ranges from 10 to 30 cm and their inside 
diameters from 2 to 5 mm. The column packing is particles from 3 to 10 μm [24]. Most 
commonly, the packing (stationary phase) in HPLC is chemically-modified silicas, unmodified 
silica or co-polymers of styrene and divinyl benzene. Different modes of HPLC have been 
developed as result of the variety of bonded phases with polymeric materials [22]. 
Aminopropyl (-C3H6NH2) is polar and used in both reversed and normal phases used to 
separate carbohydrates by modified partition mechanisms at a pH range of 2.5 - 7.5 [22]. 
Detectors must be compatible with the liquid flow and able to minimize exra-column band-
broadening. In HPLC the detector depends on the nature of the sample [24]. Many different 
solutes’ detection types are used, the most ideal of which comprise the rapid response of 
the solutes, high sensitivity, stability in operation etc. Mostly used nowadays are the UV-
visible absorbance detector types, Fluorescence detectors, Refractive Index (RI) monitors 
and Electrochemical Detectors [22]. Each type is chosen according to the type of elusion 
desired. In the case of mixtures of PAHs, fluorescence detectors are the proper choice. They 
are very sensitive and selective for solutes as poly-nuclear aromatics and their detection 
ability is based on filter-fluorimeters or spectrofluorimeters [22].

3.1.1 Ultrapure water 
Ultrapure water as implied by its name is the water at the highest possible level of purity 
and differs from de-ionized water. All contaminant types, including organic compounds, 
particles and dissolved matter are at the lowest possible concentrations [25]. Typically it is 
considered to contain only H2O. Ultrapure water is used in the project in order to determine 
the freely dissolved fractions in aqueous samples. It is also passive-dosed for the 
normalization of the PAH concentrations in the samples after they have undergone passive 
dosing.  
3.1.4 WWTP samples 
The WWTP samples used in this project were collected from Lynetten wastewater 
treatment plant of Copenhagen. Its catchment area is about 76 km2 and receives load of 
750000 population equivalent (PE).  
According to the flow diagram of the wastewater treatment plant in Figure 6, the inlet 
passes through mechanical screenings, sand and grease trap and primary sedimentation 
before it is then diverted to the aeriation tank where biological removal of phosphorus and 
nitrogen takes place. Lynetten treatment system is developed to switch nitrification with 
denitrification in the same tank, according to various parameters (time etc). Onwards, the 
wastewater passes through secondary sedimentation, after which the effluent is pumped to 
release in Øresund sea. 
In primary treatment, influent wastewater is led to the grid chamber where particles larger 
than 6 mm are removed. Onwards, takes place the removal of sand and grease in the sand 
and grease trap. Finally, the finer suspended material is left to settle to the bottom of the 
primary clarifiers. The sludge is then removed by scrapers and the sludge is sent after 
thickening to the digesters for biogas production. 
 
==PAGE:29==
19 
 
 
Figure 6 Flow diagram of Lynetten wastewater treatment plant 
In biological treatment, the wastewater is initially led to an anaerobic Bio-P reactor where 
the influent COD is hydrolyzed to volatile fatty acids (VFAs). VFAs are consumed by 
phosphorous accumulating organisms (PAOs). The polyphosphate concentration decreases 
in the anaerobic reactor and the ortho-phosphorous concentration from the anaerobic zone 
is expected to increase 2-3 times compared to the influent. In the Bio-Denitro reactors takes 
place the P uptake at aerobic/anoxic conditions. Then the waste sludge that flows from the 
secondary clarifiers helps remove the phosphate that is stored in the bacterial cells. Also the 
biological nitrogen and the organic carbon are removed by nitrification and denitrification 
which takes place in the Bio-Denitro reactors that function under both aerobic and anoxic 
conditions alternately. At the aerobic mode ammonium gets oxidized by autotrophic 
bacteria. It is a two-step process performed by two types of bacteria: Ammonium oxidizing 
bacteria, Nitrite oxidising bacteria. In unaerated periods happens the denitrification and 
final nitrogen removal by heterotrophic bacteria. The addition of precipitation chemicals, 
i.e. iron salts, helps to improve the removal of phosphorus. The biological sludge is collected 
in the secondary clarifiers and returned to the beginning of Bio-Denitro system, while the 
waste activated sludge is dewatered and sent for digestion. Finally, wastewater is led to the 
secondary clarifier and the effluent is pumped out and released. 
Three types of samples were collected along the waste flow: after the primary clarifier, after 
the aeration tank and after the secondary clarifier. The samples are referred to as 
“Influent”, “Post-Aeration” and “Effluent” respectively. Approximately 0.5 l was sampled of 
each wastewater type. The wastewater samples were filtered with 0.45 μm pore filter and 
kept in +4 ◦C until they were used for passive dosing and DOC analysis.

3.2 Methods 
In this section is described the preparation of the polymer for loading, the procedures of 
loading the polymer with the PAHs and passive dosing as well as the HPLC analysis of 
passive-dosed samples and the determination of DOC concentrations in wastewater 
samples. 
3.2.1 Loading of polymer with analytes 
In order for the polymer to be loaded with the analytes, the silicone rod had to be cleaned 
and subsequently cut in smaller pieces of similar size that will allow more samples to be 
passive dosed simultaneously. 
Preparation of silicone rods for loading 
A silicone rod of approximately 2.5 m long was initially washed under a short dish-washer 
program machine without the addition of soap. This was done to assure that undesired dust 
adhering externally to the silicone is removed. Afterwards, the silicone rod was rinsed with 
tap water, left to dry and then placed in a bottle to be stored.  
The rod was then cut in smaller pieces of similar length in order to fit the 2 ml HPLC vials 
which are used for passive dosing. Cutting the silicone into rods of desirable size before 
loading it with the analytes has been preferred so as to avoid that loaded minor parts of 
silicone are lost by cutting afterwards or that silicone dust is created from the cutting. Each 
of the total 104 cut rods was 0.178±0.009gr and 2.3 cm of length.  
The silicone rods are then placed in an amber vial and ethanol is added. After being left in 
ethanol for at least 24 hours to allow extract any impurities, the ethanol is poured out. 
Subsequently, the silicone rods are rinsed off with water and left in ultrapure water for at 
least 24 hours, a period during which, the water in the vial is changed 3 times.  
Loading of silicone rods with analytes 
Once the rods became totally dry, 19 of them that weighed 0.178±0.004gr each were 
chosen to be loaded. To ensure homogeneity in loading of all pieces and assume all rods are 
loaded with the same concentration of the PAHs, the silicone rods were loaded in 
population. Therefore, all rods were put in the same glass bottle of 100 ml volume and then 
were added the 1ml PAH mix of 500 μg in acetone and a 4ml of methanol. According to the 
procedure the vial has to be filled with extra pure water, so that the PAHs are pushed in the 
rods due to their hydrophobicity. Adding water was done regularly in small steps in order to 
avoid crystallization of the PAHs in the solution.  The first addition of water took place 2 
hours after the input of the PAHs, when 2ml of ultrapure water were added to the bottle. 
The bottle was then put to roll continuously till equilibrium of the analytes between the 
solution and the silicones was achieved in order to reinforce homogeneity of loading for all 
the silicone rods. The following period of 2 weeks, water was added regularly to the bottle 
==PAGE:31==
21 
 
in small volumes (<5ml). In a case when crystals appeared in the bottle, 4ml of methanol 
was added anew. After having 50% of water in the bottle, water dosage was increased, 
being double than each previous time. During those last steps, 10, 20 and 40 ml of water 
were added at subsequent days. In total, the loading solution resulted in a final volume of 
93 ml, of 9.7% methanol in ultrapure water.  
3.2.2 Passive dosing procedure 
Before the passive dosing of the aqueous samples, passive dosing of ultrapure water has 
been performed for each of the 19 rods that have been loaded with the PAHs. The results 
are necessary in order to determine the freely dissolved fraction of the PAHs, since in 
ultrapure water there is no dissolved matter. 
Passive dosing of extra pure water 
The rods were removed from the bottle where they were loaded in population and wiped 
with a wet lint-free tissue to remove any remains of the loading solution from their surface. 
All silicone rods were pigmented due to the sorption of the PAHs as shown in Figure 7. Each 
of the rods has been placed in a 2 ml HPLC vial and 1 ml of ultrapure water was added. 
Then, the passive dosing vials were left to shake at vortex movement at 1000 rpm for 24 
hours with an IKA Vibrax VXR rotation shaker (Figure 7), to ensure equilibrium of the PAHs 
between the solution and the silicone rod has been reached.  
 
Figure 7 Loaded silicone rod in 2 ml HPLC vial and Vibrax VXR rotation shaker 
In the mean-time, 0.1 ml was sampled from 3 of the passive dosing vials at 1.3 and 2.3 hours 
of passive dosing and placed in a 0.2 HPLC vial each. Onwards an even amount of methanol 
was added to them. After the period of 24 hours, 1 ml was sampled from all the silicone rod 
containing vials and 1 ml of methanol was added to them with the same procedure as 
described above. All the HPLC vials containing the extracts were stored at 4◦C in order to 
undergo HPLC analysis that provides information about the kinetics of passive dosing and 
the free fractions of the analytes in ultrapure water at equilibrium with the loaded silicon 
rods.

Passive dosing of wastewater samples 
The three types of wastewater samples were filtrated by 0.45 μm pore filter. Three 
replicates of each sample were used for passive dosing, 1.6 ml each. In total, 9 HPLC vials of 
2 ml volume were put to shake at Vortex movement for 24 hours and then 1 ml of each was 
placed in 2 ml HPLC vials along with an even amount of methanol. The vials were stored at

3.2.3 Chemical Analysis 
HPLC analysis of the samples 
The concentration of the analytes after passive dosing in ultrapure water was determined 
for 19 replicates, each corresponding to one of the loaded silicone rods. Passive dosing was 
performed in 2 ml HPLC vials containing ultrapure water each. The samples, after passive 
dosing were kept in 4◦C until they were analyzed by HPLC for the 13 PAHs. The 
concentrations of the PAHs in ultrapure were determined by recognizing and integrating the 
generated Chromatograms. The peaks in the chromatogram have been identified by relating 
the retention time of each PAH to other PAH respective chromatograms. A peak could not 
be integrated unless above detection limit (Area=1). 
Each analyte was present in the chromatogram at different retention time. Results 
regarding 3 different wavelengths were generated. For each one of the PAHs apparent in 
the chromatogram, the wavelength which generated the bigger area in the chromatogram 
was chosen.  
Following, some of the rods were also passive-dosed with PAHs with the same procedure. 
What is more, the spent loading solution was also analyzed by HPLC in three replicates. In all 
cases the same wavelength was used to record results concerning each particular PAH. 
Determination of DOC concentrations in wastewater samples 
The DOC concentration of the wastewater samples was determined by wet chemical TOC 
Analysis as non-volatile organic carbon (nVOC). The samples were filtered at 0.45 μm pore 
filter before analysis, so that nVOC refers to dissolved non-volatile organic carbon. 
According to the analytical principle for the nVOC measurement, the filtered samples were 
acidified with phosphoric acid (17%) and sparged to eliminate the IC. After stripping of CO2 
the oxidation reagent (Sodium Persulfate) was added. The remaining carbon was converted 
to carbon dioxide and measured by an infra-red detector. Purgeable organic substances can 
be lost during sparging process.

5. Discussion 
Loading PAHs into the polymer 
The analytes were successfully loaded into the polymer. With more than 98% partitioning of 
the analytes into the polymer (Table 6), it is safe to consider that it is a complete mass 
transfer of the analytes. Complete mass transfer was crucial in order to have measureable 
concentrations of the analytes in the aqueous samples tested. The results of the % mass 
transfer to the polymer under full loading are also in accordance with the results of previous 
study concerning the mass transfer of fluoranthene [15]. In this study PDMS was casted in 
10 ml glass vials in laboratory, the percentage of methanol in the solution after the loading 
procedure was 0.86% and the calculated mass transfer reached 99.7%.  
Moreover, the mass transfer from the loading solution to the polymer is calculated based on 
Kpm values of PAHs. A small deviation of the calculated Kpms from the actual partition 
coefficients also induces some uncertainty concerning the final concentrations in the 
polymer. Specifically, the percentage of mass transferred into the polymer depends on Kpm 
values which are model-derived and based on slightly bigger density of polymer (1.11 
instead of 1.09 gr/cm3). Nevertheless, the mass transfer percentage is close to 100% and 
any uncertainties raised from intermediary calculations are not expected to affect 
significantly that outcome.  
An important remark deriving from Figure 13 HPLC profile areas of Acenaphthylene, 
Fluorene, Phenanthrene and Anthracene for passive dosing of ultrapure waterFigure 13 
Figure 14 and Figure 15  is that despite the fact that all 19 silicone rod pieces were expected 
to have the exact same concentrations of analytes having been loaded in population, the 
concentration of certain PAHs in the rods vary significantly. In most cases, they follow the 
same trend and analogy between PAHs tested among the silicone rods. Since the 
concentrations in each silicone rod are calculated from their respective concentrations in 
water, it is implied that there was pollution in some of the testing vials. The existence of 3rd 
phase, either dust or DOC, is the most likely to have happened, while PAH contamination of 
ultrapure water before it was passive-dosed is less expected.  
A loading problem is noted in Figure 15, where the concentration of the most 
complex/heavy PAHs in the rods is depicted. These differences can be explained by the 
formation of crystals noted during passive loading procedure. These crystals adhere to the 
surface of silicone rods resulting in rods of higher concentrations of some of the PAHs. Such 
a result would have been overcome if loading of the analytes to the polymer had taken 
place more slowly, or had they been left to equilibrate for a longer period. This fact is more 
apparent for these PAHs mentioned, since these compounds are heavier, more hydrophobic 
and more likely to form crystals during the passive loading procedure according to the 
partition coefficients, Kpws. 
==PAGE:50==
40 
 
The limit of quantitation for the HPLC measurements, which describes the precision of the 
HPLC analysis, could not be assessed because each tested vial was analyzed only once. 
Often, the quantitation limit is set to be ten times the limit of detection [27]. For such a 
quantitation limit it would be reasonable to exclude those measurements of PAHs in rods 
that have already been excluded. Nevertheless, this HPLC method will be optimized in the 
future in order to be more efficient for low concentrations of PAHs. 
Kinetics of passive dosing of ultrapure water 
The kinetics of passive dosing of ultrapure water was investigated by measuring the total 
concentrations of the PAHs in three replicates at 3 time points. The resulting concentrations 
of one of them had to be discarded due to inconsistency with the results of the other two 
and due to the fact that it generated measureable results for very few of the PAHs. Had it 
been taken into account, the uncertainty levels of the concentrations would have been too 
high to conclude about the passive dosing kinetics of any of the PAHs freely-dissolved in 
ultrapure water. 
According to Figure 9 and Figure 11, PAHs 1 to 6 reached equilibrium within the first hour of 
passive dosing. The uncertainty of the measurements is higher for measurements at 1.3 
hours. During such short passive dosing intervals the time lapse between measurements of 
different vials, is indicative of the concentration differences between vials.  
In order to derive the kinetics curves for those analytes (PAHs 1 to 6), the replicates should 
have been sampled more times within the first hour of passive dosing.  Ιn previous study 
concerning the same polymer and equilibration of ultrapure water under the same 
circumstances, the time when equilibrium was reached, t95% ranged from 11 to 23 minutes 
for anthracene, pyrene and phenanthrene that were investigated [16]. 
The kinetics curves of passive dosing of PAHs 7 to 13 also cannot be generated. For PAHs 11 
and 13 no results are obtained, while for PAHs 7, 8, 9, 10 and 12 the RSD of measurements 
exceeds 20% and no safe conclusions can be reached considering the time of equilibration. 
Nevertheless, equilibration for hydrophobic organics is fast and expected to take place 
within minutes to hours of passive dosing [28], so at 24.3 hours of contact between polymer 
and ultrapure water equilibrium will have been reached. 
For more trustworthy results for all PAHs, more replicates at each passive dosing time lapse 
should be analyzed, as implied by the higher deviations of measurements at 1.3 hours of 
passive dosing. What is more, it is expected that higher concentrations in the polymer 
would have generated less deviating results. 
Passive dosing of wastewater samples 
The passive dosing of wastewater samples was assessed by use of the Enhanced Capacities 
(E) of each type of sample and the Free Fractions (ff) of the analytes. E and ff depend on the 
equilibrium concentrations of the PAHs tested in ultrapure water. Since for PAHs 7-13 such 
==PAGE:51==
41 
 
measurements are not reliable as above mentioned, passive dosing of these compounds is 
not investigated and therefore those parameters are not determined for them. 
The fact that for PAHs the E of influent is the highest, while the one of the effluent is the 
lowest is reasonable since the most dissolved material is expected in the influent, while the 
least in effluent wastewater; hence PAHs bind to it and are transported accordingly. The 
influent which is collected after primary sedimentation is highly loaded with dissolved 
material. After the biological removal of phosphorus and nitrogen, dissolved organic 
material is degraded or removed and the enhanced capacity of the sample is expected to be 
lower. Finally, after the secondary clarifier, effluent water has low dissolved concentrations 
according also to the legislation. 
According to Figure 20 Enhanced Capacities of Acenaphthylene, Fluorene, Phenanthrene, 
Anthracene and Pyrene are close to 1, meaning that no enhancement is noted in relation to 
ultrapure water. On the other hand, a capacity two times higher than the one of ultrapure 
water is noted for Benzo(a)anthracene and therefore, its transport is facilitated with regard 
to ultrapure water and the other PAHs. Free fractions were determined for the 5 PAHs as 
seen in Figure 21. Except for Benzo(a)anthracene the other compounds depicted, are 
expected to have ff values around 1. The concentrations in ultrapure water were higher 
than for the wastewater samples in this case. A possible explanation is that unwanted 
material might have been found in ultrapure water such as particles or dissolved material.  
Otherwise, a different type of HPLC setup might have generated more representative 
results. Compounds 7 to 13 could not be processed, possibly due to the fact that they are 
heavier than the others and are more likely to have formed crystals while loading.  
kDOC determination in wastewater samples 
The inability to determine the partition coefficients has been underlined in previous study 
[27]. It is proved that the degree of aromaticity of the organic matter in samples can 
increase the partition ratios [29] [30]. The failure to correlate DOC concentration to the 
Enhanced Capacity of the samples and therefore determine one partition coefficient that 
applies for all types of wastewater samples implies that during the treatment procedure of 
the wastewater, organic matter composition is not constant. It is possible that each PAH has 
a preference of binding in certain organic substances in contrast to others according to their 
molar weight or degree of aromaticity [30]. No knowledge concerning the types of organic 
DOC substances found in these samples was available, so it is assumed that each point of 
the figure corresponds to different KDOCs. Moreover, Enhanced capacities of the samples, on 
which is based the calculation of the partition coefficients are not completely trustworthy as 
described earlier, which affects the KDOC values generated. The uncertainty for the results is 
also raised by the fact that nVOC analysis does not take account of the total DOC because 
the volatile organic carbon (VOC) is lost during the sparging procedure of the analysis. The 
percentage of an organic substance lost as VOC depends on the types of the organic 
compounds contained in the samples. However, this is not expected to affect significantly 
==PAGE:52==
42 
 
the resulting ratios since it is difficult for PAHs to bind to these fractions which are 
comparatively light. 
Nevertheless, it was possible to determine three different KDOC values for 
Benzo(a)anthracene corresponding to the 3 wastewater samples tested, due to the 
Enhanced Capacity of the samples for this compound. In total these values ranged from 
0.03*KOW to 0.14*KOW which is in accordance with previous study concerning KDOC values 
from various aqueous samples.  
Future developments 
In order for the results of this study to be more representative and with less uncertainty, 
more silicone rods should have been used for passive dosing. Also, samples of the same type 
should have been passive-dosed by the same silicone rod more than once. Moreover, in 
order to deliver trustworthy KDOC values, other methods of determining dissolved organic 
concentration should have been used, more precise than nVOC determination. In addition, 
samples from more wastewater treatment plants should be tested to have an overall 
estimation of KDOCs. It is also necessary that the HPLC method used for measuring the 
concentrations of PAHs in the aqueous samples is optimized in order to be more robust and 
the generated results far above the limit of quantitation also for the most hydrophobic 
PAHs.

6. Conclusions 
The PAHs were loaded to the polymers marking more than 98% efficiency, therefore full 
loading was succeeded. This percentage differs slightly from the realistic because it was 
based on Kpm values from a model which describes the ratios taken into account a polymer 
of the same type but of imperceptibly higher density.  
Partitioning equilibrium between the loaded silicon rods and ultrapure water was achieved 
within the first hour of passive dosing enhanced by vortex shaking for 11 of the PAHs. Out of 
the 13 PAHs, indeno(1,2,3-cd)pyrene and Benzo(ghi)perylene of the initial loading solution 
could not be quantified by HPLC analysis because their concentrations were below the 
detection limit of the HPLC. The equilibrium concentrations varied significantly for the 
heaviest of the PAHs, namely: Chrysene, Benzo(b)fluoranthene, Benzo(k)fluoranthene, 
Benzo(a)pyrene and Dibenzo(a,h)anthracene. This deviation was likely caused by crystal 
formation during the loading procedure. Also, some of the passive dosing vials with 
ultrapure water were quite possibly contaminated by a 3rd phase, either DOC or dust, 
causing deviation of results. 
Reliable 
Enhanced 
Capacities 
were 
determined 
for 
Acenaphthylene, 
Fluorene, 
Phenanthrene, Pyrene which were close to the capacity of ultrapure water, and also 
Benzo(a)anthracene, the capacity of which exceeded notably the one of ultrapure water. 
The Enhanced Capacity for Benzo(a)anthracene was not found to be linearly correlated to 
the DOC concentration of the samples, explained by the fact that DOC quality is not stable 
during the wastewater treatment process. Therefore, three different partition coefficients 
were determined; one for each sample tested. The KDOC was highest for the influent 
wastewater samples and lowest for effluent, suggesting that its transport is more facilitated 
in the beginning of the treatment process by existence of more bound fraction of the 
compound. 
The HPLC analytical method used needs to be optimized in order to be able to quantify also 
the PAHs that are more heavy and hydrophobic or in lower concentrations in the aqueous 
samples and so as to have more robust results.
