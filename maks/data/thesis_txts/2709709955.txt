Abstract
This thesis explores the sound environment in Danish Hospitals and how this is experi-
enced by staff and patients in the units.
Three intensive care units and one acute neurological unit was included in the study, that
investigates the acoustic conditions in the units and whether they meet the current building
regulations. Furthermore, the perceived sound environment by staff is evaluated through
questionnaires to investigate if current conditions are sufficient to create satisfying sound
environments for a workplace and place of healing.
Through informal interviews, questionnaires, measurement of reverberation time, speech
parameters and noise logging it was found that although most of the included units fulfilled
the requirements stated in the Danish Building Regulations regarding reverberation time,
none met the recommended thresholds for noise in patient wards as directed by the World
Health Organisations Guideline to Community Noise(1999), and staff feedback showed
noise as a significant negative impact on the ability to concentrate and feeling of stress.
The above mentioned results were compared to the findings of ethnographic studies per-
formed in each unit to determine recommendations regarding acoustic design of future
hospital units for both architects, acoustical engineers and lawmakers.

1
Introduction
This thesis takes a starting point in the current acoustic environment of hospital units.
Hospitals are a place of healing, but also a workplace for a large group of people who on
many aspects of their work are strained already. Noise can have a large negative impact on
the patients’ healing progress as well as staffs’ work environment satisfaction and stress
level.
The acoustic environment in hospitals is complex and problematic for a number of reasons:
The work environment can be very hectic at times and staff handle life-or-death situations
on a regular basis, why the awareness of generating noise is deprioritised. At the same time,
a noisy work environment contributes to decreased work satisfaction and increased stress,
which further impacts the well-being of hospital staff negatively.[6] Danish hospitals are
currently facing a growing issue with unsatisfied staff, who are requiring improved work
conditions and increased wages along with a trend of migration to the private sector. The
past two years of the covid 19 pandemic have only excited the dissatisfaction further and
emphasised to society, how vital the health care system and its workers are.
A noisy hospital environment can prolong the healing time and hospital stay for patients,
creating a larger workload on staff and spiral the experienced wellbeing of both patients
and staff negatively. With this in mind, improving the sound environment in Danish hos-
pital units can benefit both employees and patients, but first, the current conditions must
be charted and a discussion of the sufficiency of current regulations on hospital acoustics
must be had.
Through a holistic approach, the project attempts to answer the following research ques-
tions through observations, acoustic measurements, noise measurements, and through
questionnaires distributed to staff, patients, and visiting relatives in four different inpa-
tient hospital units in the capital area of Denmark:
• How can the current acoustic environment in Danish hospital units be documented
and does it align with current building regulations on the field of hospital acoustics?

• Does the current regulation on noise and acoustics in Danish hospitals create a sat-
isfying sound environment for staff and patients?
• How can the sound environment in hospital units be improved through legislation
and design procedures going forward?
By answering the question above, the goal is to pinpoint specific recommendations to be
included in regulations on hospital acoustics as well as procedures for the design process,
to implement noise and acoustics in the design of hospital units. Through identifying areas
of improvement with a broader perspective than just than objective, acoustic parameters,
a more nuanced insight can be achieved and serve as groundwork for improving the acous-
tic conditions in hospital units, and in the end, improve the staff and patient experience
altogether.

2
Theory
This chapter consists of three sections. The first presents the relevant literature concern-
ing noise and acoustics in hospital environments and seeks to give a thorough depiction of
the current conditions and previous studies, as well as laying the cornerstone for the more
specific scope of this thesis. The second part concerns current legislation and design re-
quirements regarding hospital room acoustics. Lastly, necessary acoustic parameters and
definitions are presented to have a clear, common point of reference throughout the rest
of the report.
2.1
Noise in hospital environments
In its’ simplest form noise is defined as unwanted sounds. The experience of noise is
greatly subjective and sounds that are pleasant and uplifting to some can be perceived
as extremely annoying to others; some genres of music are often subject to this division.
Moreover, the occasion on which a sound occurs can determine how annoying it is: Loud
music can be a joy if you attend a party, but if you stay in the apartment next to the party,
trying to sleep; not so much. Likewise, collegial conversations are pleasant at times, unless
you are immersed in work or trying to concentrate on a task.
The physiological impact of noise is many and well documented. Permanent damage and
hearing loss can occur when subject to high sound pressure levels of 90 dB continuously
over 8 hours or a single event of 115 to 140 dB.[7] However, other less directly noticeable
consequences are related to noise exposure as well.
2.1.1
Hospitals as a place for healing
From the patients’ perspective, hospital noise is unconditionally interconnected with sleep
disturbance. Interrupted sleep is a cause of both physical and mental discomfort and ill-
ness, and in both clinical settings and through field studies it has been verified that dis-
turbance of sleep for any reason can increase blood pressure, heart rate, cause unsteadily
breathing and more body movement during sleep, which can be problematic to inpatients’
well-being. Furthermore, interrupted sleep will affect people into the following day result-
ing in reduced perceived sleep quality, increased fatigue, depressed mood, and decreased

3
==PAGE:18==
performance([8], p.26), all of which can be harmful to a patient’s ability to get better and
general experience during hospitalisation.
Sleep impairment among inpatients is linked to impairment of the immune system and
regenerative process as well as prolonged hospitalisation and increased mortality.[9] A
case study showed significantly longer duration of hospital stays while nearby construction
work was carried out compared to no construction work.[10] This effect is pronounced in
Intensive Care Units where patients are highly sensitive and sleep disruptions have been
linked to the occurrence of the delirium state.[11]
An over-average use of sedatives and sleeping medications are seen amongst people liv-
ing in areas with higher exposure to night-time noise ([8], p. 26). This aligns with a
study published in 2014, where the effects of noise and light protection, earplugs and eye
masks respectively, were tested in a post-anaesthesia care unit. The results showed that
the protection prevented a decrease in sleep quality and most notably reduced the overall
intake of morphine during the first 24 hours compared to the control group.[12] Consid-
ering that pain is appointed the most dominating cause of sleep loss amongst hospitalized
patients[13], the use of less morphine for patients using noise and light protecting equip-
ment and people using relatively more sedative medicine when sleeping in noisy area
indicate that people are more sensitive to pain during sleep if they are exposed to noise.
Patients in intensive care units(ICU) are especially at risk of developing delirium, which
is an acutely occurring brain dysfunction. The state is a source of mental and emotional
disruption and linked to increased mortality and duration of hospitalisation.[14] The con-
nection between noise in the ward and the development of delirium has been hinted, but
not sufficiently supported by evidence at this point. Nonetheless, both sleep impairment
and delirium are causes of negative experiences in hospital units, with immense impact
on patients’ well-being and health, and in the end, this impacts staff and management too,
putting additional strains on the workforce and financial burden on the health care sys-
tem. Consequently, the incentive all around should be towards decreasing noise impact
on patients as well as staff to improve conditions for both groups.

of the project. The prioritisation was further supported by a priority index and standard of
practice developed to identify the need for acoustic requirements based on the condition

2.1.2
Hospitals as a workplace
Although the research on noise in hospitals mainly focuses on the experience of patients,
staff are affected by loud noise levels throughout the day and night as well. The workday
in a hospital unit can be very hectic and many units have staff in satellite functions coming
and going in the unit, who might not always show consideration to keeping the noise level
down.
Medical staff has been identified as a significant noise source themselves and the knowl-
edge by staff on hospital sound and noise have been examined through questionnaires,
which revealed that health care staff in ICUs have limited knowledge on noise. How-
ever, a wide range of suggestions for improvements has been made including changes in
the daily routine, like reducing noise from conversations and alarms to more extensive
changes e.g. only having single bed wards.[17]
On a general note, the noise impact in an office work environment does not necessarily
immediately reduce cognitive task performance, but has been shown to reduce mental
well-being, mood, and stress through increased heart rate and emotional arousal when the
noise environment is loud, 59 dB(A), compared to quiet at 36 dB(A).[18] Good speech in-
telligibility in open-plan offices has been proved to disturb employees to the extent where
cognitive performance is reduced between 4 and 41% when people are talking in the room
compared to when it is silent. It is a result of irrelevant speech being understandable and
disturbing employees to the extent of work performance being impacted negatively.[19]
Through measured equivalent sound pressure level LAeq and questionnaires, hospital staff
have shown to be exposed to sound pressure levels between 58-72 dB(A) daily, which was
expressed by 80 to 91% of staff to negatively impact their daily work. Noise from phones
and medical equipment was highlighted as most annoying, and the noise during normal
day and night shifts were mentioned as negatively impacting staffs’ ability to concentrate,
communicate clearly and make quick decisions, why the potential for errors increased as
a consequence hereof.[6]

2.1.3
Acoustic design of hospital units
As the previous sections make clear, noise in hospital units is negatively impacting both
patients and staff. A US-based study showed patients’ satisfaction scores increasing by
4-15% as a result of a 3-6 dB decrease in noise levels. Since the health care system in the
US deviates from the Danish model, this cannot be expected to transfer perfectly, but the
method could be adaptable and an improvement in the perception of a hospital stay would
be desirable if achieved.[21]
Evidence-based design regarding hospitals single out issues, challenges, and factors in the
current hospitals to find solutions to implement in the new design.[22] Based on the role of
the physical environment influencing patients and staff, evidence-based design has gained
some attention within hospital design[23], and shown to positively impact end-users, as it
was explored in connection with the construction of a new intensive care unit in Calgary,
Canada. The end-user perceptions were explored through interviews conducted two to
three months after the unit was taken into use and again after 12-15 months. Highlighted
results include atmosphere, natural light, and low noise levels specifically important. Fur-
thermore, a product of the evidence-based design was increased spaces in the wards which
encouraged more family presence by the patient, and although it meant greater distances
between staff and patients, it was outweighed by the inclusion of family members and the
increased benefits of fewer interruptions as a result of less noise. [24]
The above-mentioned case is a simple example of how decisions on design and layout can
impact the soundscape in a room indirectly. More often than not, architects develop the
design and concept first and engineering disciplines are brought in afterward in the order
in which it is relevant. As a result, acoustic engineers are introduced fairly late in a design

2.2.2
World Health Organization recommendations
The guide to community noise published by the World Health Organization touches on
noise levels in hospitals wards.[8] The guideline’s recommendations are based on consid-
eration to the patients’ health. The critical topics within hospital noise are highlighted as
“sleep disturbance, annoyance and communication interference, including interference
with warning signals.”([8], p.44) To minimise sleep disturbance, the report states follow-
ing requirements for wards:
• LAeq should not exceed 30 dB throughout day, evening, and night time.
• LAF,max should not exceed 40 dB for sound event indoors during night.
The equivalent sound pressure level LAeq should be calculated over 16 hours for day and
evening and 8 hours for night-time. Furthermore, the WHO report proposes the maximum
equivalent sound pressure level in rooms where patients are being treated or observed to 35
dB LAeq, enhancing that intensive care units and operating theatres are of particular prior-
ity when concerning sound pressure levels. Consequently, the equivalent sound pressure
levels in these units should be as low as possible. [8])
It is well known and documented, that these guideline values are very difficult to comply
with, and in many hospital settings, the mere background noise from ventilation and other
equipment provides noise levels that exceed the recommended threshold limits. In mea-
surements conducted in five UK intensive care units, LA,eq levels never dropped below 45
dB, with the noise levels exceeding between 52 and 59 dB(A) more than 50% of the time.
Furthermore, peaks above 85 dB(A) were monitored in all units occurring upwards of 16
times per hour during night-time. [28].

Acoustic parameters
2.3.1
Reverberation Time
The most widely used room acoustic parameter is the reverberation time (RT), which ex-
presses the time in seconds, which it takes a sound impulse to fade out in a room. The
reverbing properties of a room depend on size, layout, and furniture and the appropriate re-
verberation time depends on the function of a given room. Examples of this are displayed
in fig. 2.2.
Have you heard?
11
==PAGE:26==
Figure 2.2: Typically recommended reverberation times for different room types([2]).
The experience of reverberation time highly affects the quality of the sound environment
in a room. The lower RT is, the more sound is absorbed in the surrounding construction,
and the experience of sounds in such room can be described as anechoic or “dead” since
the noise immediately dies out which can make the speech intelligibility poor, if you are
not close to and directly facing the sound source or person speaking. This is not a common
problem in smaller rooms, but in the case of auditoriums, the reverberation time can be
too low and result in poor sound perception on the seating rows furthest from the speaker,
as too many sound waves are absorbed before reaching the back rows.
Contrary to this, if RT is too long, the noise will be reflected in a room and occupants
will experience the sound environment as noisier. This can have the additional effect
of occupants raising their voices to compensate for the noise in a room, known as the
Lombard effect,[29] a typical situation arising in restaurants and classrooms that have not
been renovated to current standards. [5, 30]
In more technical terms, the reverberation time expresses the time sound waves reverb
in a room and is defined by the time it takes for a sound impulse to decrease by 60 dB.
If the materials present in a room have highly sound-absorbing properties, like heavily
upholstered furniture and acoustic wall and ceiling absorbers, the sound waves emitted
from a noise source will not be reflected as much, resulting in a low reverberation time. If
the room has hard surfaces like glass, brick, or concrete with walls parallel to each other,
the sound waves can fluctuate between the hard surfaces and create a very unattractive
sound environment. The hard, none-absorbing surfaces are a typical challenge in hospital
settings, where rooms need to be sterile and thus possible to clean. This has resulted in
difficulties with achieving proper sound attenuation in hospitals, although efficient, sterile,
acoustic absorbers have been introduced in the later years.

To depict the noise environment that staff and patients are subject to, different types of

The human ear can perceive audible sounds from 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz, at different sound
pressure levels. Lower frequencies are less perceivable, and the human ear is most sen-
sitive around 1000 Hz([5] P.17). As seen in fig. 2.5 the audible sound level at 50 Hz is
around 70 dB, whereas sounds at 1000 Hz are perceivable down to 2-5 dB.
Decibel(dB), in the sense relevant to this project, refers to the sound pressure level, which
is the most recognised way to express the loudness of sounds detectable to the human ear.
The lowest value of the scale would be 0 dB and not audible to humans. The logarithmic
nature of the decibel scale means that if the sound energy is doubled, the sound pressure
level increases by 3 dB. For example, if a speaker is emitting sound at 60 dB, and another
speaker is turned on, also emitting 60 dB, the total SPL value will increase by 3 dB to
63 dB. Likewise, if the sound pressure level is increased by 10 dB it is perceived to the

Method
In the following chapter, the methodological part of the project will be described as the
different ways of elucidating the topic are presented.
The project takes a holistic approach to determine the sound environment in inpatient hos-
pital units and single out areas of improvement within the regulations and design process
of indoor acoustics. The methodology combines an analysis of the quantitative elements
introduced in the previous chapter with qualitative questionnaires. Inspired by the con-
cept of Design Anthropology, an interdisciplinary ethnographic angle is included to better
depict the real-life context and challenges faced by occupants in hospital units. [37]
3.0.1
Design Anthropology
Design Anthropology as the name suggests is the merging of the design and anthropol-
ogy discipline, where the methods of the two separate fields are combined to liaise with
the other. The interdisciplinary approach creates design solutions rooted in ethnographic
methods but orientated towards the future and social evolution.
Based on this, three models of Design Anthropology were drawn up[38]:
dA
The theoretical contribution is for anthropology rather than design. Design
follows the lead of anthropology in terms of adopting theoretical understand-
ings, or becoming the subject of anthropological study.
Da
Fieldwork is in the service of design. Framing originates from problem orientated
design approaches rather than engagement with peoples. Anthropology is put in
service of design, for example, ethnographic studies are used for establishing de-
sign requirements.
DA
Disciplines of design and anthropology are engaged in a convergence of efforts
each learning from the other each learning from the other.
Based on the DA model, fieldwork will serve to identify design requirements and areas
of interest, which will be supported by the typical methods used in acoustic engineering.
The occupants will in part be included in the observational studies carried out in the units
and through the distributed questionnaire.

3.1
Measurement methods
A distinction is made between noise measurements and acoustic measurements in the quan-
titative data collection on which the analysis is partly based. The first mentioned is sound
pressure levels logged over periods of 24 hours, to disclose the general noise levels in
designated areas of the units. The latter covers the measurements of acoustic properties of
the same or similar rooms and areas in the unit. The procedure of conducting these mea-
surements will be elaborated on below, and prospectively, the two types of measurements
will be referred to as noise and acoustic measurements, respectively.
The project includes four hospital units in the capital region of Denmark: One acute neu-
rological unit and three intensive care units specialised in anaesthesiology in ICU1, neuro-
logical diseases in ICU2, and solid organ transplantation and reconstructive cancer surgery
in ICU3. Due to the severity of patients’ condition in the ICUs, 24-hour observations by
staff are required in these units.

Inaccuracies
It should be noted that the sound level meter was placed in the units and staff and patients
were made aware of the function and purpose, why the results might not be completely
representative of the regular noise levels. Staff might have behaved differently, either more
26
Have you heard?
==PAGE:41==
or less noisy, due to the presence of the equipment. Occupants might also, intentionally
or not, have spoken directly into or touched the device without knowing it could affect
the measurement data. If monitoring took place over more days or even weeks, this could
have been accounted for, and measurements would have depicted general noise levels
more accurately, but this was not possible due to the limited time frame and available
equipment in the project.
Consequently, the noise measurements give an indication of the noise levels in inpatient
hospital units, but especially peak noise levels should be considered with the above men-
tioned in mind.
Additionally, high sound pressure levels do not necessarily mean problematic noise ex-
posure, as some noise might be intentional, like the sound of the TV in a bed room or
relatives talking to a hearing-impaired, hospitalised relative. This can be soothing to the
patient, but annoying to staff or vice versa.
Subsequently, high sound pressure levels might not be directly problematic to patients or
staff but will contribute to the overall sound environment and can indirectly affect staff and
patients negatively in the form of stress or sleep deprivation. Responses to the distributed
questionnaires can help illuminate the experienced noise.

4.1
NEURO
The NEURO unit is an acute neurological unit that treats symptoms and conditions of
apoplexy and other neurological diseases. A large part of the treatment is centered around
thrombolysis, but main work tasks also include blood tests, electrocardiogram (ECG/EKG),

During weekdays’ office hours, the unit also has a consultation function for patients with

This common office area, henceforth named conference/office room, was clearly empha-

room. This poses a significant issue for concentration as both breaks, professional dis-

located in the room, but more occupants can occur.

6.1
How does the acoustic conditions in the unit align with current
regulation?
The current regulations on acoustics in Danish hospitals are limited to reverberation time,
which should be below 0.6 seconds in almost all rooms where staff or patients spend longer
periods of time, except form open-plan offices, where the requirement is that the area of
absorption should be equal to or greater than 1.1 times the floor area of the office.
Measurements of reverberation time in wards, offices or conference rooms in the units
showed that with exception of the ward in the NEURO unit and the conference/staff room
in ICU1 all rooms met the applicable requirements. The ward in the NEURO unit had an
older acoustic ceiling, which was attributed to the lack of acoustic absorption in the room.
The conference/staff room in ICU1 had undergone initiatives to improve the acoustic en-
vironment but without satisfying results, partly because the acoustic absorbers were used
as noticeboards.
The poor acoustic conditions in this part of ICU1 were clearly reflected in the subjective
evaluations, as 24% of staff members highlighted the room as the noisiest room. The
noise level LAeq,1hour in the room was measured to be between 40 and upwards of 70 dB(A)
varying noticeably throughout the day.
The 55% of staff respondents in the NEURO unit expressed that patients were negatively
affected by noise to some extent, although noise levels do not directly correlate with the
reverberation time, higher RT properties increase the experienced noise, why it is objec-
tionable that the reverberation time in wards exceeds the regulations directed by BR18.
The regulations on room acoustics in BR18 can be considered fairly superficial, as they
do not consider the purpose of each room to a greater extent, and as this report has shown,
suiting reverberation times do not necessarily result in comfortable sound environments.
This will be discussed in the following sections.

6.2
Discussion of how occupants experience the sound environment
The responses received from staff in the four units showed that about half of them had
been disturbed to some extent or very disturbed by noise through the past six months. At
least 55% of respondents in each unit finds it difficult to concentrate on work tasks to some
or a great extent and the same applies to staffs’ level of stress, which was expressed to be
negatively impacted by noise to some or a great extent by at least 57% of staff respondents
in all four units. Furthermore, between 58% and 77% of staff respondents find that patients
are negatively impacted by noise to some or a great extent. These responses unanimously
express the extent of noise as an issue in the included units in this project.
Even though the reverberation times measured are generally within the limits directed by
BR18, this is evidently not sufficient conditions to create a satisfying sound environment
for staff. Whether it creates a comfortable soundscape for patients was not possible to
evaluate due to the limited number of responses received from patients, but according to
staff evaluations and noise measurements in wards, it clearly is not. The measured equiv-
alent sound pressure levels in the wards give a better indication of the actual soundscape
patients are subject to. The measured equivalent sound pressure levels in the wards during
day and evening are all above 50 dB(A), at night this is number varies between 43 and 53
dB(A) in the ICUs and 34.6 dB(A) in the NEURO unit. This is above the recommended
guideline value of 30 dB by WHO, however not exceeded by very much in the ward of
the NEURO unit. The WHO recommendations are exceeded to a significant extent in the
ICUs, which is highly problematic as this unit type treats the most vulnerable inpatients.
The project poses an interesting challenge for future studies to investigate: How can the
experience of patients be explored accurately, especially in Intensive Care Units, where
most patients are heavily sedated or unconscious. Patients who are well enough to fill out
questionnaires are not representative of the whole group, alternatively, patients should be
interviewed upon release, but this also excludes the group of patients who do not survive
their hospital stay, and since noise can have an impact on the mortality rate, this can result
in a positive bias in responses if this group is left out. In this project, relatives of the pa-
tients were included to investigate the perceived sound environment in wards, but largely
due to conditions regarding covid-19, hardly any responses were collected. Under other

6.3
What can be done differently moving forward
By acoustic engineers
From the results found in this project, the following trends regarding hospital noise can
be deduced:
The combination of several hospital functions in the same room negatively impacts the
perceived noise environment. Examples of this were seen in all units but ICU3. In the
NEURO unit, the combined conference and office room was almost unambiguously ap-
pointed the nosiest area in the unit, and LAeq,1hour was measured to be continuously around
50 dB(A) through day and night. The function of the conference is task assignment and
treatment discussion which imply talking. This directly opposes the soundscape needed in
an office where people are concentrating and need silence to perform their tasks without
making mistakes.
In ICU1 the room merging is both seen in the combined conference and break room and
the observation/office areas placed in open connection with the hallways. In the latter
example, the negative aspects are two-wayed, as noise from the office areas scatter through
the hallways and nurses by the workstations can hear all the hallway activity like patients
being admitted, equipment moved around or noise from the combined conference and
break room, which is in the middle of the unit and had significant issues both acoustically
and in the combination of conference and staff room which both generated a lot of talking.
Like ICU1, ICU2 has the conference function located in open connection with the hall-
ways, which is designated as the noisiest area by 25% of staff respondents. It cannot be
said whether it is hallway activities like walking and transportation or the talking and of-
fice activities happening in the conference base that makes the hallway noisy, but both
are contributing to the noisy environment. Furthermore, in ICU2 the nurses’ observation
posts were moved inside the patient bed room, which seemed to result in staff being less
annoyed by other staff members talking, as they were subject to this to a smaller degree,
however, equivalent sound pressure levels during night showed the highest levels in the
ICU2 ward of 52.4 dB(A) and a decrease in LAeq in the conference area indicates that staff
activity moves mainly to the wards during night time. This creates significantly worse
conditions for the patients, although staff evaluations in the questionnaires did not reflect
the negative impression of the solution that was perceived during observations in ICU2.

==PAGE:97==
During observations decreased job satisfaction was mentioned as a direct result of the
workstations inside the wards. The last example highlights the importance of supporting
observations with questionnaires and vice versa on this matter.
What this project most of all has made clear is that the challenges of creating satisfying
sound environments in inpatient hospital units are complex, and not achieved in any of the
four included units. To identify issues and the underlying reason for them, measurements
and surveys should be supported by observational studies performed by acoustic engineers
to identify problematic areas and find the underlying reason for this.
The questionnaire responses in this project gave really good insight into the experience
of noise by staff and highlighted important areas of interest, but without the observations
and preliminary information gained through visits to the unit, a deeper understanding of
their importance could not be obtained. By comparing several units, overall trends can be
identified and solutions to one problem might have been tried in another unit, only to foster
new challenges. For acoustic engineers to improve the noise environment in intensive care
units and inpatient units in general, the field must be included earlier in the design phase
to be able to impact more than the choice of acoustic ceiling and wall absorbers but also
identify sources of noise, work patterns of staff and needs of the users, that can impact the
noise environment in the final building.
By legislators
Considering the current regulations in RT by BR18, it can be discussed whether 0.6 sec-
onds is suitable for the majority of room types in hospitals. This project did not identify a
clear link between lower reverberation times and lower noise levels. Based on the observa-
tional studies, the perception and generation of noise are closely linked to the unit layout,
location of different hospital functions, and work patterns. As most of the measured av-
erage RT values were about 0.4 seconds, and in the conference/staff room of ICU1 it was
between 0.5 and 0.7 seconds and noticeably poor according to staff, lowering the require-
ments to 0.4 seconds might be beneficial.
In Danish schools, the BR18 requirement to reverberation time in open classrooms areas
has been lowered to 0.4 seconds, since teaching is more conversation-based today.[27]
Similarly, in the work in hospitals and especially in ICUs, conversation and clear com-
munication are essential for proper treatment and knowledge sharing. Furthermore, with

the objective of bringing noise levels down, the reverberation time should be as low as
possible, why lowering the threshold could benefit this purpose, as long as the speech
intelligibility is not compromised.
It should be noted that only a few rooms in each unit were included in both the noise
and acoustic measurements. They were chosen for their representative nature, but at least
slight deviance in other rooms in each unit is to be expected. Rooms like the hallways
were not included and long reverberation times in these areas can result in noise spreading
through the whole unit. Including more room types of each unit can help obtain a wider
perspective on noise scattering in hospital units but this was beyond the scope of this
project.
The requirement to open-plan offices can be interpreted in different ways, as the open-plan
office as described in BR18 refers more to larger office landscapes and does not consider
how this is converts in hospital environments where many of the office areas are in open
connection to other room types and must be suitable for different activities, not just office
work. However the regulations regarding normal, smaller, offices only include up to 4
office workstations, why several of the rooms included in the acoustic measurements in
this project fits under both categories and at the same time, do not. The unfit definition
of office areas in hospital settings underlines the need for more fitted regulations on this
topic.
Based on this study, the question of including requirements to STI or D50 values can be
raised, however not answered unambiguously. Staff expressed poor speech intelligibility
experienced in the units, but the STI values measured in this project were extremely good
which did not align with the experience of occupants. The background noise in relation
the speech intelligibility should especially be considered further, as it highly impacts the
speech intelligibility properties. Equivalent sound pressure levels have been measured
to be LAeq,1hour over 50 dB(A) in the units at which level excellent speech intelligibility
cannot be expected. The possibility of using low STI values to separate groups of people
talking should be explored further, as staff speaking was emphasised as a general issue in
all the units, why it should require more focus.

By architects and designers
This project clearly underlines the relation between perceived sound environments and
the physical environments as architecture and room layout impact the occupants’ percep-
tion and terms of acting. Based on the findings in this project, architects should include
acoustics and noise as a design aspect to a wider extent, earlier in the design process, to
avoid the acoustic regulation ending up being just a matter of complying with regulations.
As this study has shown, complying with regulations does not equal a comfortable sound
environment for the occupants.
By incorporating what was stated in the suggestions of improvements to acoustic engi-
neers, it can be combined with other engineering fields and be considered in the early
design phases.
A way of doing so could be to create an interdisciplinary survey for hospital units including
questions related to other aspects of the unit relevant to a design phase e.g. work patterns,
what is missing in the current unit, and what staff would wish from a new unit. Similar
questionnaires could be distributed to patients and relatives to shed light on their needs in
a hospital unit. In such a case, the following questions from this project regarding noise
and acoustics should be included:
Which of the following areas of the unit do you experience as the most noisy?
Followed by the option to explain their answer further. Additionally, the noise-matrix
with evaluations of noise sources should be included, as it gave really good insight into
the noise sources more present or more annoying, in the current units.
Additionally, the question of, where the most quiet area in the unit is, should be asked to
pinpoint areas that are better functioning according to staff.
With a starting point in this and observational studies performed by acoustic engineers,
the acoustic properties can be interlaced with the architectural vision and create the basis
for better buildings.

6.4.1
Questionnaires
Several factors impacted the number of responses to the staff questionnaires, but it can
roughly be attributed to the many shift changes happening, why all employees are never
gathered at the same time to receive information e.g., about the questionnaire. Further-
more, the nurses in charge of handing out the surveys pass on the duty when they finish
their shift, but naturally many other tasks are prioritised over this and it can easily be for-
gotten, resulting in many people not being aware of the questionnaires. There might be
a bias towards more negative reviews, as staff with a negative experience of the noise
environment might be more inclined to answer questionnaires on the topic, although the
lacking information about the questionnaire is attributed larger impact to the number of
respondents than staff declining to answer.
The lacking information about the survey amongst staff also made it difficult to get them
to help distribute questionnaires to patients or their relatives. Furthermore, the intensity of
the covid-19 pandemic was rising during data collection and by mid-January 2022 restric-
tions were introduced limiting relatives to visit for only 15 minutes per day between the
hours of 13-15:00, why they cannot be expected to spend time answering questionnaires
during this limited period, nor have a representative impression of the sound environment
in the unit. These are conditions specific to the covid-19 pandemic which has impacted
the project at least to some degree, some more will be elaborated on below.
6.4.2
Covid-19
Although the covid-19 pandemic did not directly affect the project more than restrictions
for visiting relatives as just mentioned, everyone in the health sector has been affected by
this and this influenced the project indirectly.
At the time of the data collection of this project, the pandemic had been impacting the
entire society to a fluctuating degree, but especially intruding the work environment in the
hospitals for almost two years. During December 2021 and January 2022 when visits to
the units and measurements were conducted, the increased number of infected people rose
to new levels, although the number of admitted patients was not overwhelming in the units
included in this project. Nonetheless, the daily routines at the units were impacted by re-
strictions and staff, who could not work as they were in isolation at home. This disrupted

Conclusion
Through the knowledge on hospital environments gathered in this thesis, the overall con-
clusion is that hospital units are complex sound environments. Many aspects contribute
to a noisy environment in the units and the perception of the noises are equally diverse.
It was found that the measured reverberation times in bed wards and office and conference
areas, generally met the requirements stated in the Danish Building regulations and the
speech intelligibility was correspondingly very satisfying, however, the perceived speech
intelligibility expressed by staff was poor, which in part was attributed the high noise
levels in the units.
Noise measurement showed that no patient bed rooms met the recommended noise level
threshold by the Worlds Health Organisation, but a noticeable decrease in SPL during
night hours was detected in two of the units. The noise levels in office and staff areas
showed that staff was subject to noise levels above 50 dB(A) for large parts of the day and
night, and questionnaire responses revealed that over 50% of the staff respondents felt that
noise in the unit affected their level of stress and ability to concentrate negatively.
The project concludes that the experience of noise and its impact on staff is highly influ-
enced by the architectural layout of the units and how work functions are located, relative
to each other. Especially worth highlighting is that combining more room functions in one
room showed to generally create poorer work environments and ill-suited sound environ-
ments.
This study showed the inadequacy of current regulation and building tradition but also
pointed out valuable tools to use in the future design of hospital units. With the use of
questionnaires, staff can highlight problematic areas and sources of noise specific to their
work environment, and combining this with observations and noise measurements, a holis-
tic overview can be gained and through this, better solutions can be found, keeping in mind,
that no perfect solution is out there.
Further studies should be made including other unit types, more respondents and include
the experiences of patients and relatives better. Ideally, noise measurements should be

patients should be monitored, to detect the physical impact noise in hospital units have on
