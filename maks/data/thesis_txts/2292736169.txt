Summary
The purpose of this Master’s thesis is to investigate the feasibility of reconstructing
the ﬂow ﬁeld around an aquatic microorganism based on Particle Image Velocimetry
measurements of the two velocity components on a plane. These experimental data
are used in order to recover the three-dimensional ﬂow ﬁeld, described by the linear,
incompressible Stokes ﬂow. The microorganism used in the experiments is a Dino-
physis acuta phytoplankton and the data were provided by the National Institute of
Aquatic Resources - DTU Aqua.
The thesis ﬁrst examines the reconstruction of the two-dimensional ﬂow ﬁeld based
on synthetic data of the two velocity components on a line. The synthetic data were
created by numerically solving the Stokes equations and then restricting the ﬂow ﬁeld
on the line. The purpose of examining this simpliﬁed problem is to gain a mathemat-
ical and physical insight into the reconstruction task. In particular the eﬀect of the
distance of the data domain from the cell and the eﬀect of the ﬂuid ﬂuctuations on
the surface of the cell are investigated. Afterwards the three-dimensional ﬂow ﬁeld is
reconstructed based on synthetic data of the two and three velocity components on
a plane. The purpose of using the diﬀerent data sets is to evaluate the importance of
the third, out-of-plane velocity component and the eﬀect it has on the reconstruction
quality. Finally, the methodology developed was applied in the reconstruction of the
three-dimensional ﬂow ﬁeld based on the experimental data.
The results of the simulations suggest that the reconstruction quality depends on the
position of the data domain and that the closer this lies to the microorganism, the
closer is the reconstructed ﬂow ﬁeld to the true one. In addition, the results indicate
that the importance of the third velocity component in the data declines as these are
taken close to the microorganism. This provides some support for researchers to con-
tinue using experimental techniques that provide the two velocity components, when
the experimental set-up is such that the data are taken close to the cell. Lastly, the
results from the reconstruction based on experimental data suggest it was useful as
the reconstructed ﬂow ﬁeld retained the basic characteristics of the initial data, and
the morphology of the phytoplankton is evident, at some level, in it. The reconstruc-
tion algorithm can produce more accurate results, however, if some enhancements
in the numerics, e.g. mesh reﬁnement, or modelling assumptions, e.g. geometrical
model for phytoplankton, were made.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
About 71% of the Earth’s surface is water-covered, and the oceans hold about 96.5% of
all Earth’s water. It is therefore reasonable that mankind has invested time and money
into the study of the oceans and marine biology. The microorganism that serves as the
basis of the aquatic life is the phytoplankton. The name comes from the Greek words
ϕυτo (phyton), meaning ”plant”, and πλαγκτo (planktos), meaning ”wanderer” or
”drifter”. Phytoplankton are sometimes called the grasses of the sea. Like land plants,
they produce lots of oxygen through photosynthesis. During photosynthesis they use
the sun’s energy to combine carbon dioxide and water into organic material. This
process removes carbon dioxide from seawater and allows the water to absorb carbon
dioxide produced in the atmosphere. This ”global carbon cycle” helps regulate the
temperature of our planet [Age].
Plankton are also an important source of food for larger animals. Phytoplankton are
the ﬁrst link in the food chain. They are known as primary producers because they
produce the ﬁrst forms of food. Zooplankton and other small animals that graze on
the phytoplankton are known as primary consumers. These, in turn, become food for
larger organisms such as bivalves, crustaceans and ﬁsh. The ﬁsh and other animals
then become food for animals near the top of the food chain, such as harbor seals and
man [New]. There is a wide range of types of phytoplankton in terms of size. Most
of them lie in the nano and micro scales, with an equivalent spherical diameter of 2
to 200 µm. This fact renders phytoplankton a diﬃcult and costly ecosystem to study.
However, marine biologists have studied the swimming, feeding and reproduction of
the phytoplankton in an attempt to better understand the aquatic ecosystem.
One of the areas marine biologists focus on, is the swimming mechanisms of the phyto-
plankton. Depending on the type, phytoplankton have developed various swimming
patterns. There are two predominate phytoplankton types, the diatoms and the di-
noﬂagellates. Both are eukaryotic single-celled algae, but the latter have two ﬂagella,
which can be manipulated for movement and enhance their feeding rates by pulling
the water and hence nutrients, towards them. More on the swimming and feeding of
ﬂagellates can be found in [Nie15]. Due to their small size, phytoplankton inhabit a
world where the inertia forces are negligible and the viscous forces dominate. In terms
of ﬂuid dynamics, this translates into a low-Reynolds, linear Stokes ﬂow around the

microorganism.
A widely used techniques to capture the ﬂow ﬁeld around these microorganisms is Par-
ticle Image Velocimetry (PIV) [Adr10]. This is an optical method of ﬂow visualization
and is used to obtain instantaneous velocity measurements and related properties in
ﬂuids. The ﬂuid is seeded with tracer particles which, for suﬃciently small particles,
are assumed to faithfully follow the ﬂow dynamics. The ﬂuid with entrained particles
is illuminated so that particles are visible. The motion of the seeding particles is
used to calculate speed and direction (the velocity ﬁeld) of the ﬂow being studied.
Although there are three-dimensional stereoscopic PIV methods, most common are
the traditional PIV experiments which capture the two-dimensional ﬂow ﬁeld on a
plane around a swimming cell and further research is based on this two-dimensional
ﬂow ﬁeld.
1.2
Problem statement
The objective of this Master thesis project is to reconstruct the three-dimensional
ﬂow ﬁeld around an aquatic microorganism based on PIV measurements of the two
in-plane velocity components. The phytoplankton used for the PIV experiments is
a type of dinoﬂagellate called Dinophysis acuta, ﬁgure 4.13. The experimental data
were provided by the National Institute of Aquatic Resources - DTU Aqua.

In order to achieve this goal, ﬁrstly it is essential that the nature of the reconstruction
problem is analysed and that the ability of reconstructing three-dimensional ﬂow ﬁelds

1.2.1
Proposed Solution
There is an extensive literature about the Navier-Stokes equations of ﬂuid motion,
both from a purely theoretical (existence, uniqueness, stability of solutions), and a
computational perspective. The reader is referred to the book by G.P. Galdi [Gal94]
for the mathematical background and to [Gir86a],[Reu12],[JWa07] for numerical meth-
ods for the Navier-Stokes equations. There also exist in the literature research papers
and projects that concern boundary control of the Navier-Stokes and Stokes equa-
tions, but the focus is on shape or energy loss optimization, and they do not involve
reconstructing the ﬂow ﬁeld based on a set of data. To the best of our knowledge,
this is an original project, based on various areas and mathematical tools.
The task of reconstruction is a combination of solving the forward Stokes equations
of motion, posed as a Boundary Value Problem (BVP), discretized with a Finite
Element Method (FEM), and an Inverse Stokes problem, which takes the form of
an optimization problem.
Physically, the ﬂow ﬁeld around the microorganism is
described by the steady-state, linear Stokes ﬂow, or creeping ﬂow, a considerable
simpliﬁcation of the full Navier–Stokes equations. The equations are complemented
with a set of suitable boundary conditions in order to obtain a well-posed BVP. Then
the weak form of the equation is discretized by an appropriate FEM scheme, the
mixed Taylor-Hood elements. The resulting system of equations is solved either with
a direct or an iterative solver, depending on the its size. The resulting ﬂow ﬁeld can
be restricted on a plane to produce synthetic data used initially in the reconstruction
in order to check the sanity of the algorithm.
Afterwards, the reconstruction itself, which is an inverse problem, is posed in the
form of a minimization problem with respect to the unknown boundary condition
on the surface of the microorganism. The quadratic functional used represents the
residual of the reconstructed ﬂow ﬁeld on the plane and the given data on the same
plane, in addition to a term indicating the norm of the solution-boundary condition.
The last step is to impose the optimised solution as the boundary condition on the
microorganism, and obtain the reconstructed ﬂow ﬁeld. In the case of the synthetic
data, this ﬁeld can be compared to the true one and the misﬁt can be evaluated. Both
three-dimensional and two-dimensional data on a plane are used in order to obtain
the three-dimensional ﬂow ﬁeld. This serves as a check of the importance of the third,
out-of-plane velocity component and the impact it has on the reconstruction. The
research being done so far by the National Institute of Aquatic Resources in the ﬁeld
of planktology is based on two-dimensional data, and it is of great interest to marine
biologists to determine whether these data can provide an accurate image of the true
three-dimensional ﬂow ﬁeld.

2.1
Fundamental Concepts in Fluid Dynamics
In this section the basic concepts governing Fluid Dynamics are brieﬂy explained
based on the textbook by [Hen06]. These concepts are central to the project and are
presented for completeness.
In the heart of Fluid Dynamics lie the Continuity and Navier-Stokes equations, which
accurately describe the motion of viscous ﬂuid substances. These equations arise from
applying the conservation of mass and Newton’s second law to ﬂuid motion, together
with the assumption that the stress in the ﬂuid is the sum of a diﬀusing viscous term
(proportional to the gradient of velocity) and a pressure term.
The non-dimensionalization of the equations which can lead to simpliﬁcation in their
form is alsopresented.
The modelling assumption of the linear Stokes equations
around aquatic microorganisms is motivated based on this. The assumption is phys-
ically valid in the scale of microﬂows, where the microorganisms, due to their small
sizes, inhabit a world where viscous forces dominate and inertia eﬀects are negligible.
This equation is the framework of this Master’s thesis project, and will presented in
detail in following sections.

2.2
The Stokes Equations
The ﬁrst step to approaching the 3-dimensional reconstruction problem is studying
the two cardinal equations, namely, the Stokes equation (2.21) coupled with the Conti-
nuity Equation for incompressible ﬂuids (2.6). The ﬁrst one describes the momentum
of the ﬂuid as it moves along the pressure gradient. In the Stokes equation, only the
linear diﬀusion term (∇2u) is kept, while the non-linear advective term (u · ∇u) is
negligible, as the non-dimensionalization revealed. Physically one can argue that in
the aquatic microcosm the inertia forces are much smaller than the viscous forces due
to the tiny length scales, giving rise to Stokes equation.
After having motivated this choice of ﬂuid ﬂow both mathematically and physically,
Stokes ﬂow around an aquatic microorganism must be mathematically formulated. In
this section, the problem is posed as a Boundary Value Problem (BVP). Furthermore,
the weak form of the equations and a Finite Element discretization scheme is derived
in order to obtain the approximation to the weak solution of the Stokes problem.

and numerically solved with an appropriate Finite Element Method. Other options,
that are suited for numerically solving the strong formulation of the problem instead,

The Nitsche Method
An issue that often arises when dealing with complex domains is the representation of
the complex boundaries and their boundary conditions. The classical way of dealing
with this challenge is the employment of unstructured meshes, where the boundary is
represented by the mesh’s facets. However generating an unstructured mesh induces
a major computational and storage overhead [J B12],[Mik09].
Therefore, several methods have been developed for representation of complex bound-
aries on structured meshes. The ﬁrst order representations which use the facet cells
of the generated mesh are proven ineﬃcient. Particularly in 3D cases like the re-
construction problem, the mesh reﬁnements techniques employed for achieving the
required mesh resolution lead to a formidable high number of cell numbers.
A widely used method to circumventing this problem was proposed by Nitsche in
[Nit70]. There, the incorporation of Dirichlet boundary conditions in the ﬁnite ele-
ment approximation of the model Poisson problem is discussed. Nitsche introduced
a simple idea for handling Dirichlet boundary conditions weakly without the use of
Lagrange multipliers. In the scope of this paper, Nitsche’s method will be employed
for imposing the Dirichlet BC on the surface of the sphere/microorganism.

In [Mik09] the authors compared the Nitsche method with the traditional Galerkin

of the system matrix for Nitsche’s and the traditional method was compared and it
was observed that this of the traditional method is much higher. For this reason the
traditional method may cause trouble for iterative solvers such as multigrid methods.

A brief introduction to the method of Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) is pre-

and right-singular vectors of A, respectively. The singular values and vectors obey

ill-posed, at least to some degree. A good measure of the degree of ill-posedness is
given by the singular value decomposition of the matrix N. The faster the decay rate
of the singular values, the worse the degree of ill-posedness. If the singular values
decay asymptotically at an exponential rate, the inverse problem is severely ill-posed.
In the forward problem, the small singular values damp out the contributions from the
additive noise on the boundary condition for the cell surface. In the inverse problem,
however, they become an ampliﬁcation for the noise components, drowning out the
original signal, as explained above.
If a problem is ill-conditioned, any standard
matrix inversion algorithm will fail to recover the desired solution.
On the other
hand, large singular values amplify the eﬀect of noise on the cell surface in the forward
problem and they damp it out in the inverse procedure.

the data domain Γ. It can be expected that the further away the measurements are
taken from the cell, the more diﬃcult it will be to reconstruct the respective ﬂow ﬁeld.

domains, making the problem more and more ill-posed. In order to test this theory,

the data, a too large one might have an overly-smoothing eﬀect that leads to loss of
desired properties of the ﬂow ﬁeld. The L-curve of the inverse problem, discussed in
2.3 was created in order to choose the optimal regularization parameter. Figure 3.7
suggest that for a noise level of 1%, λ = 10−4 is the most appropriate choice.

CHAPTER 5
Implementation
The most challenging part of the project was the implementation of the model in
the programming language Python. As mentioned in chapter 1, the FEniCS Project
provided the collection of algorithms needed. In particular, the FEM scheme for the
discretization of the weak Stokes problem and the linear system solver along with
some visualization algorithms were used.
The mesh used in both the 2D and 3D simulations was created with Gmsh [GR09].
This is a 3D ﬁnite element grid generator with a build-in CAD engine and post-
processor. During the construction of the mesh, the data domain (a line in the 2D case
and a plane in the 3D) was explicitly deﬁned. In the 2D simulations, 1600 elements
were used and a 7866 × 7866 linear system was solved with the LU factorization.
In the 3D simulations, 84634 elements were used, resulting in a 354202 × 354202
linear system. The system was solved with the transpose-free quasi-minimal residual
method and a algebraic multigrid preconditioner, as suggested in [L+12] for the 3D
Stokes ﬂow. This problem was far too large for a personal computer to run, and the
DTU clusters were instead used. Speciﬁcally, the code was run on an IBM NeXtScale
nx360 M4 node with 2 Intel Xeon Processors E5-2680 v2 (ten-core, 2.80GHz, 25MB
L3 Cache) and 128 GB memory.
The optimization part of the project was initially carried out with the dolﬁn-adjoint
library, which automatically derives the discrete adjoint and tangent linear models
from a forward model written in the Python interface to DOLFIN. When this method
did not produce the desired results for reasons explained in section 3.2.1, the com-
mercial software package MatLab was used. This was also used for post processing of
the results. The results were visualized either with FEniCs’ build-in plotter or with
ParaView [Hen07], an open-source, multi-platform data analysis and visualization
application.
The main pieces of Python and MatLab code can be found in the Appendix A.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This Master’s thesis project shed some light into the nature of the reconstruction
of the 3D ﬂow ﬁeld around an aquatic microorganism based on PIV measurements,
and provided answers about the feasibility of the reconstruction itself. The analysis
showed that the quality of the reconstruction is greatly dependent on the distance
of the domain where the measurements were taken from the position of the microor-
ganism. It is possible to get very accurate ﬂow ﬁelds, of course depending on the
noise level, when the measurements are taken on a plane passing through the cell.
This is true whether the data contain information about only two or all three velocity
components, as the diﬀerence in the residuals of the reconstructed ﬂow ﬁelds with the
true one is 1.31% for a 1% noise level. However, as the domain is moved away from
the microorganism, the reconstruction abilities deteriorate signiﬁcantly and the extra
component is rendered more valuable. For a far away data domain the diﬀerence in
the residuals is at 48.4% In the course of this project the diﬀerent steps leading to
the reconstruction were presented and discussed.
Firstly the choice of the linear, Stokes equations to describe the ﬂow ﬁeld in the
neighbourhood of a phytoplankton was motivated, see section 2.1.4. Then the Stokes
problem was posed as a Boundary Value Problem with mathematically and physically
suitable boundary conditions, section 2.2.1. The framework used to solve the BVP
was the FEniCS Project, with the DOLFIN Python interface. Therefore the weak
formulation of the Stokes equations were derived and discretized using the stable
mixed Taylor-Hood elements, sections 2.2.2,2.2.3. The resulting ﬂow ﬁelds were then
restricted on predeﬁned data domain in order to generate synthetic data.
The second part of the reconstruction involved using these data in an inverse Stokes
problem, section 2.3, in order to recreate the entire ﬂow ﬁeld. The inverse problem was
posed as an optimization problem, where the residual of the reconstructed ﬂow ﬁeld
on the data domain with the synthetic data generated before had to be minimised
with respect to the boundary condition on the surface of the microorganism. The
Singular Value decomposition of the observation matrix, which maps the boundary
condition to the ﬂow ﬁeld on the data domain, provided insight into the degree of ill-
posedness of the reconstruction, section 3.1.2. The results suggest that the problem
becomes more ill-posed as the data domain lies further away from the animal and the
quality of the reconstruction decreases. This agrees with the physical intuition about
the eﬀect of the position of the data. The ill-posedness of the problem was tackled
==PAGE:74==
60

with the use of Tikhonov regularization on the boundary condition.
The optimization problem was initially solved using the DOLFIN library for Partial
Diﬀerential Equation constrained optimization. However, the results were not satisfy-
ing due to numerical and technical issues arising from the use of a highly automated
algorithm. Therefore, the problem was moved to MatLab, section 3.2.1. The opti-
mised boundary condition on the surface of the cell was subsequently used in the
forward Stokes problem to obtain the optimised ﬂow ﬁeld in the entire domain.
During the optimization synthetic data containing information about both the 3 and
2 (only in-plane) velocity components on the data plane, were used. This served the
purpose of investigating the importance of the knowledge of the third, out-of-plane
component, section 4.2.1.
The results suggest that as the data domain is moved
further away from the cell and the problem becomes more ill-posed, the importance
of this component increases. In particular, the simulations revealed that when the
data are taken to be in the plane passing through the animal, the diﬀerence in the
residual of the true ﬂow ﬁeld and the reconstructed one when using 2D and 3D data
is merely 1.31%.
The ﬁnal step of the project was to put the technique to test, by reconstructing
the 3D ﬂow ﬁeld around an actual phytoplankton based on PIV measurements of
the two velocity components on a plane, section 4.2.1. The ﬂow ﬁeld around the
microorganism is not symmetrical due to the presence of the two ﬂagella, which distort
the ﬂow, pulling water towards the cell in order to facilitate feeding or moving. The
reconstruction algorithm produced a ﬂow ﬁeld that retains the main characteristics of
the original ﬂow ﬁeld. The residual between the measurements and the reconstructed
ﬂow ﬁeld on the data domain was 3.62%. While this result shows that the algorithm
can indeed reconstruct the basic structure of the ﬂow ﬁeld, there is certainly room
for improvement.
Limitations and Future Work
The basic limitation of the mathematical model used is the modelling of the phyto-
plankton. A sphere is the simplest shape that could describe such an organism, but
as ﬁgure 4.13 shows, in reality they are much more complicated. The next step would
be to create a more sophisticated geometrical model to represent the cell. Especially
the addition of the trailing ﬂagellum would help signiﬁcantly with the reconstruction
of the ﬂow ﬁeld. While the current model can capture its’ eﬀect at some level, it
could greatly beneﬁt from an explicit deﬁnition of such a part.
Every numerical model faces certain limitations. The size of the domain used aﬀects
the image of the ﬂow ﬁeld. The boundary condition used in the outer boundaries, i.e.
that they are moving at the same but opposite speed as the cell, is physically true
for the far away boundaries, r →∞. Restricting the domain to a cube with sides
==PAGE:75==

10 times the radius of the animal is acceptable, but not optimal. Simulations based
on larger domains would produce ﬂow ﬁelds that better capture the true ﬂow ﬁeld
around a swimming organism.
Another common limitation when solving Partial Diﬀerential Equations numerically
and using a discretization technique is the size of the discretized mesh elements. The
smaller their size, the closer the representation of the discretized solution is to the
continuous one. As mentioned in chapter 5, in the 3D simulations, 84634 elements
were used in the discretization of the domain. By mesh reﬁnement one could e.g.
double the number of elements and obtain a ﬁner mesh representation. However this
would lead to a signiﬁcantly larger linear system. Solution to large-scale problems like
that have high computational demands in terms of time and storage. In the interest
of eﬃciency, during this project the aforementioned mesh was used, but the fact is
that use of a higher resolution mesh would produce more accurate results.
Despite the aforementioned limitations, this work provides a foundation on which
future projects can be based. The reconstructed three-dimensional ﬂow ﬁeld in the
neighbourhood of the microorganism can be used to derive crucial quantities in the
study of phytoplankton. The mass transport mechanism and rates are dependent on
the ﬂow ﬁeld around the cell, as well as the forces acting on the cell. Based on it the
Peclet and Sherwood number of the mass transfer process can be computed. The Sher-
wood number has considerable practical interest and applicability in oceanography,
since it is a measure of the eﬀect of advection on the nutrient uptake [Kiø08].
