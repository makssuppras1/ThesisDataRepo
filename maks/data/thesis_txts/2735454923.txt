Assignment Formulation
This master’s thesis project aims to explore the mixing properties of a 200L tank used
for fully formulated biologics solutions, through computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simu-
lations. Stirred tanks are widely used in various biological formulations, and knowledge of
gradients, forces, and mixing can be used to better understand such a process, leading to
better product quality, and more efficient use of resources.
The objective is to produce accurate models of two specific 1L and 200L tanks, rep-
resenting their geometry, inlet/outlet configuration, and mixing capabilities. Additionally,
it is desired to model two-phase flows in the instances of vortex formation, as well as for
particle distribution. Simulations and experiments with the 1L tank will reduce the cost of
understanding the 200L tank.
The project will include an in-depth literature review of stirred tanks, related CFD
simulations, and transport phenomena.
This will be followed by thorough modelling of
the two tanks, complete with accurate geometry, boundary conditions, and 3D mesh. The
models will then be used to simulate a variety of process conditions to better understand
the forces affecting the product. Experimental results will be used to verify the performance
of the models, to ensure accurate representation of the mixing process, starting with the
smaller tank. Finally, an analysis of the results is provided, along with recommended mixing
conditions, and the mixing effect on the biological product.
Outcomes:
1. Literature study
2. Two validated CFD models of the 1L and 200L tanks
3. Simulated results, highlighting forces affecting biological products during the mixing
process
4. Experimental results verifying models, ensuring their reliability
5. Recommendations for optimized mixing conditions

Abstract
During the production of monoclonal antibodies, freezing is sometimes employed to pre-
serve the formulated product. Eventually, this frozen and bagged product must be thawed,
during which large concentration gradients can form. For this reason, a mixing tank is typi-
cally employed to collect and homogenize everything before it can be filled into e.g. syringes
or vials.
Especially when working with expensive fully formulated products, it is desirable to
avoid wasted product. For this reason, the use of a 1L tank as scale-down model was explored,
for prediction of the behavior of a full 200L cubical tank employing single-use bag technology
with magnetic impeller drive system. The 1L scale-down tank maintained two important
ratios of impeller diameter to tank width alongside tank width to tank height, which increased
the degree to which the two tanks would display similar mixing characteristics.
Colorimetric and conductivity derived mixing time experiments were completed on the
1L scale-down model mixing tank. A strong exponential correlation between the mixing time
and impeller rotation speed was displayed, taking an hour to become 98.5% homogeneous at
an impeller rotation speed of 50rpm, and an average of 30s at 900rpm. Using the physical
properties of the solutions which were used for mixing time experiments, an accurate com-
putational fluid dynamics (CFD) model of that same tank was created, and a similar model
was created for prediction of the 200L tank properties.
Both the 1L scale simulations and experiments showed similar results for this exponential
decrease in mixing time, with the CFD model predicting a 98.5% mixing time of 2700s at
50rpm and 8.5s at 900rpm. Significantly lower mixing speeds were observed when the flow
was more turbulent, with improvement around an impeller rotation rate of 150rpm in the
1L tank, where the Reynolds number was around 325. For the same mixture, with viscosity
around 6.5 centipoise, the mixing speed in the 200L tank was found to be between 84s for
50rpm and 25s for 150rpm, reflecting the additional liquid displaced by the larger impeller
reducing the impeller rotation speeds necessary to reach turbulent flow.
The shear strain rate, ˙γ, used as an indicator for product damage and surfactant foam-
ing, was found to scale between the two tanks with impeller diameter, Di, and impeller
rotation rate, Ni, according to ˙γ ∝NiD0.3

Introduction
Fujifilm Diosynth Biotechnologies is a Contract Development and Manufacturing Orga-
nization (CDMO)[5], which is a company that specializes in providing manufacturing and
drug development services for other pharmaceutical companies. The company is rapidly
expanding, having announced that it is spending over $7 Billion USD upgrading its manu-
facturing capacity in Denmark, England, and in the United States[2, 3, 4]. With a current
annual revenue of around $21 billion USD as of 2022[3], the company currently has a major
financial opportunity to invest in additional manufacturing capacity. The company produces
a variety of large molecules, such as monoclonal antibodies, recombinant proteins, and vac-
cines, and also offers support for the production pathway, from upstream to downstream[1].
The manufacturing of biopharmaceutical products involves many steps on the way to
a final formulated drug. These steps often rely on parameters that can be difficult, costly,
or detrimental to the product to measure. For instance, the presence of various probes in
a mixing tank can be a source of strain on the drug proteins, impede mixing, be expensive
to purchase, require replacement, and increase the chance of contamination. Especially at
large scale, experiments use vast amounts of resources, and increasingly large vessels are
increasingly expensive to modify. To compound the issue, these experimental batches are
not able to be sold, so minimizing this waste is desirable.
Tools that can further develop our understanding for such processes can be used to both
improve their quality and effectiveness, and are especially valuable if they reduce the need
for physical experiments.
During the production of monoclonal antibodies (Mabs), a critical step is the thawing
of the already formulated product. The freezing process is useful in prolonging the shelf-life
of the drug, but will necessitate a homogenization step due to the possibility of gradients
forming during thawing of the formulated product. This mixing step is typically performed
in a mixing tank directly after thawing, and is critical in the production of a homogeneous
solution for equal doses in the final formulation. Despite the desire for quickly obtaining
homogeneity, proteins can be sensitive to high mixing speed, causing degradation of the
drug product, which is highly undesirable. In order to avoid this, mixing studies should be
completed.
One way of visualizing the behavior of these systems is computational fluid dynamics
(CFD). CFD programs use a collection of formulas to calculate the conservation of mat-
ter, momentum, energy, and other phenomena. Realism is increased by adding terms for
modeling the effect of turbulent flow. These equations are typically solved on the basis of
subdividing the geometry into a series of small elements that make up a larger mesh. Based
on the the physical properties of the fluid, inputs and outputs, and energy input on the sys-
tem, conditions such as pressure, velocity, strain, and temperature can be visualized either
over time or in steady-state.

single-component, such as just water in a mixing tank. This instance will mainly concern

It is desired to explore scale-down possibilities, in conjunction with the implementation

Pharmaceutical Drug Development
In the pharmaceutical industry, there has been clear trends towards the use of single-use
equipment, such as the mixing tank explored in this report. This is useful in a variety of
pharmaceutical processes, among them the mixing of formulated product following a freeze-
thaw cycle, such as for formulated monoclonal antibodies (Mabs) solutions. The mixing must
be carefully balanced to eliminate the concentration gradient that follows such a process
without unnecessarily degrading the proteins.
2.1.1
Mixing
Recent developments in pharmaceutical production has seen a resurgence in single-use
plastics. In the age of increased regulatory requirements regarding cross-contamination and
viral load, manufactured disposable vessels have the advantage of being able to be produced
free of contaminants and therefore bypass the need for strict cleaning validation checks. As
manufacturing of the equipment is improved, the safety with regard to leaching of materials
out of the plastic, and cost efficiency are explored, their use continues to spread to more unit
operations[29]. Alongside removing cleaning requirements, these single use systems display
major advantages in low price to set up, easy scale-out with the possibility of modular

inclusion of baffles to improve mixing quality is difficult. This could be one benefit of a cubical
container, where it has been proposed that a cubical shape improved mixing performance,
with the corners acting as baffles.
Traditional impeller solutions have additionally been
compared to magnetically levitating impeller solutions, with less degradation of the product
in the magnetic solution due to the lack of a narrow gap that could "grind" the proteins
down[23].

2.2.5
Navier-Stokes Equations as a Millenium Prize Problem
Despite the widespread use of the Navier-Stokes equations, the non-linear term for
inertia complicates their analytical solution. Even for simplified versions of the equations,
solutions have only been found for simple geometries where it is possible to ignore the inertial
term, for instance by infinitely long pipes[7].
For the purpose of advancing the field of mathematics as a whole, it is one of seven
problems given a $1 million USD prize fund at the start of the nineteenth century, with the
hopes of inspiring future mathematicians, known as the Millenium Prize Problems[6]. The
terms of the problem as to show that for the incompressible form of the equations, either
"smooth, physically reasonable solutions"[21] exist or that the equations break down[21].
This is mentioned only to show that these equations are complex, hence the need for
methods such as CFD to approximate real-world behavior.

2.3
Computational Fluid Dynamics
The equations used to model the behavior of fluids are nonlinear, complicating the pro-
cess of obtaining numerical solutions for anything but simple systems. The most frequently
used solution to this issue is the implementation of computational fluid dynamics, for ap-
proximating the partial differential equations which are otherwise too complex to solve in
all but the simplest of systems[8].
The process of obtaining numerical solutions involves breaking the overall volume into
smaller mesh elements, iterating on the conservation equations through algebraic approxi-
mations, and repeating until numerical stability is reached, based on convergence criteria or
the maximum allowed iteration limit. For steady-state flow, these calculations are repeated
until the flow no longer changes in behavior. Solving for transient flows involves solving until
the criteria are reached at many different moments in time, each known as a time step, and
repeating until the total desired time difference is reached[8].
2.3.1
Mesh
The mesh in a CFD simulation is many smaller shapes each used to break the domain
up into many small elements, which can be used as the basis of turning the difficult-to-solve
Navier-Stokes into a series of algebraic calculations. For each element, a volume integral
is performed for the conservation equations.
Gauss’s Divergence Theorem is applied to
simplify the time derivatives, and series approximations are performed to further simplify
the equations. For transient terms, an approximation is made where the time derivative is
replaced with a time step, and a given integral by a calculation of the values at the start
and end of the time step[8].
These control volumes and linearizations are performed for each of the control equations

For low Reynolds numbers, it is suitable to model the Navier Stokes equations as they

Direct Numerical Simulation:
Ideally, the modeling of turbulence in CFD is possible directly through analytical solution
of the Navier-Stokes equation. Unfortunately, the computational power required to make a
fine enough mesh with small enough length scale is impossible for modern computers given
industrially relevant scale[8, 18].
Instead, it is more feasible to model the effect of turbulence through a selection of
different models, simulating the effect of actual turbulence. These models mostly recreate the
behavior of turbulent systems, which are inherently time dependent, in a time independent
manner. The models used in this project, the k-ε, k-ω, and SST first utilize the concept of
the Reynolds Averaged Navier-Stokes equations (RANS) and the eddy viscosity concept to
simulate turbulence.
RANS:
Turbulent flows are chaotic, with particles flowing in many different directions, even when
there otherwise is a steady general flow direction. To remedy the difficulty of simulating these
complex flow behaviors, it is possible to implement time-averaged variables instead. This
process creates a form of the transport equations known as RANS equations. The method
is frequently displayed using the velocity vector, as seen in Equation 16. The velocity vector
v is broken up into two components: the time averaged portion ¯v and the fluctuating v′ in
directions i. The time averaged component is the mean, and will be time independent, and
then the fluctuating component gives the time dependent portion. This simplifies solution
of the formulas, on the basis of the assumption that, even though turbulent flow is not
reproducible and highly time dependent, the average behavior of the flow is the same[18, 12].

A wide variety of tools are available for quantification of the mixing time. Between
these are a plethora of quantitative methods, namely conductivity measurements, but also
a very easy qualitative method, colorimetry.

2.4
Mixing Time
The quantitative methods offer easier determination of a specific mixing time, but there
are many to choose from, each of varying merit and difficulty to implement. Among these
are some which require specialized tools to accomplish, such as Electrical Resistance Tomog-
raphy, which uses a ring of electrodes to measure the conductivity of multi-phase systems in
a non-intrusive manner, using the conductivity of the layers to measure mixing. Planar laser
induced fluorescence, uses a laser to excite a dye to a certain wavelength that can then be
measured, as the intensity of the light is strongly correlated to the concentration. Instead of
using specialized equipment, however, it is possible to use less specialized equipment, such
as conductivity/pH probes. These probes can be correlated to the concentration of either a
solute or acid/base, and are used to determine the concentration at the probe over time[9].
Using colorimetry requires no added equipment aside from a dye and the ability to peer
inside the mixing tank. Colorimetry has some benefits in that it does not disturb the flow,
is exceedingly easy to implement in that it requires no equipment, and it becomes possible
to visualize the behavior of the flow. However, it has some major drawbacks in that it is
subjective - depending on the observers decisions, and is only usable in see-through systems.
The use of probes is likewise troublesome in that they inherently disturb the flow, with
the presence of a probe potentially changing the mixing time. Still it is beneficial in being
directly correlated to the concentration of a tracer, is easy to implement, and can be used
in most any setup[9].
Mixing time is usually referred to using the variable tm, which can be associated with
differing degrees of homogeneity. For instance, the mixing time can be defined as the point
where the concentration no longer exceeds ±10% of the total difference between the final
(Cf) and starting concentrations (Ci). The necessary percentage for the mixing time varies
heavily depending on the homogeneity desired from a given system. Either way, a lower
mixing time is typically preferred, but depends heavily on both the nature of the flow -
whether it is turbulent or laminar - but also the impeller rotation speed[19].
With some probes, it is possible to use the conductivity of the fluid as a measurement of
mixedness. For this purpose, it is useful to standardize the values of the conductivity based
on the difference between the initial and final concentrations. The initial concentration can
here be regarded as C0, signifying it as the concentration at time 0, and the final as C∞,
which is the concentration where fully mixed, i.e. at infinite time. The degree of mixing can
be obtained by comparing the instantaneous concentration Ct and infinite, where they both

5
CFD Model Setup
In order to realistically model the behavior of a system in computational fluid dynamics,
a given object must first be faithfully modeled in a 3D program, converted into a mesh, and
then the conditions in the CFD program must be set to approximate reality – all without
excessive computation time.
5.1
Mesh Creation
The discretization performed by the CFD program, when the governing equations are
applied to mesh elements, also causes their size and shape to have some effect on the results
of the simulation. This can be important to keep in mind as the simulation may require
additional resolution where the velocity is high, significant heat transfer is occurring, etc.
Two avenues of mesh creation are available in ICEM for creating these mesh elements,
structured and unstructured. A structured mesh is created by first "blocking" the structure
into parts, and the elements are then created as the user specifies, typically as regularly
shaped hexahedral (cubic) elements. This creates a uniform mesh, which allows very precise
user control of the resolution at desired spots, and when used well can shorten computation
time. To contrast this, an unstructured mesh is irregular and created parametrically, filling
the geometry with either tetrahedral or hexahedral elements.
This can be sub-optimal
where the parametrically formed mesh turns out strange causing incorrect flow behavior,
and requires a certain amount of optimization to achieve a good balance of resolution and
calculation time.
5.2
CFD setup
In order to solve the partial derivatives of the model equations, the program relies on
the user to know the correct settings to translate real world behavior to the program through
initial conditions, governing equations, and acceptable assumptions for simplification. This
involves selection of proper boundary conditions, correct transfer of equations between do-
mains, and setup of rotating elements.
5.2.1
Boundary Conditions
Boundary conditions are the edges of the domain where the conservation equations are
solved, and are used as the initial conditions.
These can also input a transfer of mass,
momentum, and pressure.
Page 22 of 74
==PAGE:37==
Jens Hartvig, Master’s Thesis
5.2
CFD setup
No-Slip walls: In this boundary condition, the velocity of the liquid is equal to the wall.
Where the wall does not move, it will cause the liquid to have a velocity of zero at the wall,
and otherwise can be used to make the fluid flow at a specific velocity at the wall. In a
stationary domain this can be used to make a cylindrical shaft apply a rotational force on
the liquid where it touches, or in a rotating domain it can apply a counter-rotating wall on
the floor, causing it to be stationary in the absolute frame[8].
Free Slip walls: At these boundaries, the velocity normal to the wall are set to zero, as
are the wall shear stress, so the wall exerts no forces on the fluid and no fluid passes into
or out of the system through these boundaries[8]. These are used to model points where
the presence of the walls should not significantly affect the flow, for instance, at the top of
an open container, where no liquid escapes the system, but the flow is not hindered by the
boundary.
Inlet, Outlet, and Openings: These are areas where the fluid can flow across, pertaining
to the continuity equation. For inlets and outlets, this will have a velocity component, and
pressure must also be specified.
5.2.2
Interface Models
Interfaces are the method by which the equations of state are transferred between dis-
tinct domains. These are used as the basis for transferring information between the rotating
domain (rotor) used to model the spinning of the impeller and the stationary domain (stator)
for the bulk liquid.
A general connection is used to initiate a frame change rotating to stationary domain.
Three options are available to simulate this change: frozen rotor, stage, and transient rotor-
stator.
Frozen Rotor: The rotation of the system is calculated but the orientation of the interface
remains fixed. This effectively calculates the average behavior, but all transient effects at
the interface are lost. This is a poor model for areas where the flow then interacts with
stationary components[24].
Stage (Mixing Plane):
The stage model takes an average of the flux through the entire
circumference, in contrast to assuming the position of the rotor/stator is fixed. This model
is more intensive than frozen rotor, and will perform a better average estimate in some
circumstances, but similarly neglects transient interactions[24].
Transient Rotor-Stator:
Where the transient behavior of the interface is important, it is
possible to model the exact behavior of the interaction between the rotating and stationary
domains.
This model requires more computational resources to implement, but can be
assisted by using one of the aforementioned models to give an initial guess for the flow

5.2.3
Domains
In CFD, domains are defined by an area of fluid flow or a heat conducting solid made up
of a mesh, the properties of materials in the region, and its nature – buoyancy, heat transfer,
pressure, frame of reference, etc.
For frame of reference, it is useful to be able to set a rotating frame of reference. Now
the entire domain will rotate with some rotational velocity, and will bypass the issues of
rotating a complex shape in a stationary domain.
The rotation of an impeller would not be correctly modelled as a rotating no-slip wall
due to the stationary nature of the mesh. Instead, it can be useful to simulate the rotation
through an entire domain. This necessitates the interface models, which separately model
the rotating behavior, and the momentum of fluid into the rest of the system. The necessity
of this is demonstrated in Figure 5.

normal to the wall equal to some value will not give realistic results for complex shapes like
it will for a simple cylinder or flat wall, as the complex shape would be stationary over time.

5.4
Mesh Sensitivity Analysis
Proper mesh size is vital to obtaining good results in a CFD simulation. A smaller
mesh will ensure greater precision and higher resolution for complex flows, but the increased
computational power necessary to solve and analyze the additional mesh elements must be
considered[15].
The typical solution is to perform a mesh-sensitivity analysis. A series of simulations

Nevertheless, the Courant number can be used as basis for increasing or decreasing the time
step resulting from the time step analysis as the impeller rotation speed changes. It was seen
that as long as the timestep change and velocity change (resulting from change in impeller
rotation speed) were proportional, the stability of the results was approximately equal. For
instance, for a velocity increase from 50m/s to 150m/s, the time step was decreased by the
same factor 3.
5.6
CFD Setup
5.6.1
Initial Conditions
Initial conditions are required to set the starting values for the variables of the fluid
equations. These can also be estimated by the solver, but a good initial condition will lessen
the time it takes to find steady state. Similarly, a bad initial condition will lengthen the
time it takes, and may lead to poor simulation of real behavior in a transient simulation.
The solver is not able to handle variable rotation speed for rotating domains in the same
transient simulation, so experiments were performed at constant rotation speed, without
simulating the acceleration from 0rpm to the intended impeller rotation speed.
Additionally, the values from another solver result can be used to implement initial
conditions. To find the mixing time, where the results from each time step are relevant, it
is useful to first find the steady state behavior and then start a transient simulation.
To determine the mixing time, the solver was prevented from solving for mass concen-
tration for these initial condition determinations, so the starting velocities and turbulence
could be determined without affecting the mixing time calculation, and then the solver could
be allowed to solve for mass fraction once more at the start of the transient simulation in
order to find the time it took to mix to homogeneity.

5.6.5
Colorimetry CFX
It was then desired to recreate the results of the colorimetry experiments. Because the
experiment resulted in very slow mixing times, greater than an hour, it was decided to k-ε
turbulence model to decrease computational complexity. In addition, sub-optimal timesteps
were used, shown later, with small timesteps at the start to capture the injection of viscous
dye, and then overly large at the end to enable longer total simulation time.
Because the 51cP sucrose dye was determined to be poorly miscible in the 21cP bulk
solution, they were modeled separately as two fluid flows, each as their own continuous fluids,
with homogeneous turbulence model of k-ε. Crucially, buoyancy modelling was active, but
not surface modelling. Interfacial mass flux was enabled but negligible at 1e-11kgm−2s−1.
This was a two-phase flow, so the Navier-Stokes equations were solved individually for each
fluid, greatly increasing the calculation time.
The 1L tank was modeled with an impeller rotation speed of 150rpm, and the following
timestep:
ts1L = if(t < 2[s], 0.05[s], if(t < 30[s], 0.5[s], if(t < 120[s], 1[s], 5[s])))
The 200L tank was modeled with an impeller rotation speed of 100rpm, and the following
timestep:
ts200L = if(t < 2[s], 0.5[s], if(t < 30[s], 1[s], if(t < 120[s], 2[s], 10[s])))

7
Discussion
Two types of experiments were performed on the actual tank; one series using conduc-
tivity to determine mixing time, and one using colorimetry. Between these, the conductivity
experiments were significantly more useful in their scope, both with regards to breadth of
impeller rotation speeds covered as well as focus on pure mixing speed. The colorimetry ex-
periments were plagued by a dependence on sucrose’s solubility on top of the mixing speed,
and covered only low speeds which were not representative of the mixing times at large scale.
Nevertheless, the results seemed useful for showing the distribution of insoluble aggregates.
These experimental results were compared to CFD simulations, showing good agreement
overall, and revealed new information about flow direction, mixing quality, etc. The validity
of these CFD results will be discussed with regards to inaccuracies from the mesh and
timescale.
Using the CFD results as basis, the possibility of using equations as basis for predict-
ing mixing quality and time must be discussed, and compared to the equations used for
other tank types. Future work could be done to improve on the results gathered, involv-
ing additional experiments to improve the scope of the scaling formulas and to ensure their
validity.
7.1
The Mesh
For the two meshes, the one for the 1L tank (Figure 10) and 200L tank (Figure 13),
element size analysis was performed to capture realistic behavior with as few elements as
possible, aiming to decrease computation time required for accurate results. For this reason,
a very fine mesh was desired in the rotating domain to fully capture the flow around the
impeller, and it was desired that these smaller elements would continue into the stationary
domain and around the impeller shaft to capture where the flow is fastest, to then transition
into larger elements towards the edges where the flow is slower.
It appeared that this
increase was well accomplished in the 1L tank, while the mesh size in the 200L tank was
mostly constant through the entire stationary domain.
Nonetheless, the mesh seemed to acceptably capture realistic behavior, with results that
were similar to those seen in physical experiments, with two caveats. Firstly, the mesh was
seen to cause some inaccuracies in the rotating domain, shown in Figure 55a. This relates
to the automatically generated unstructured mesh causing the transfer of momentum from
element to element to be slightly off of what would be expected in reality, so even though
the general behavior is good, its validity at certain small scales must be considered. The
maximum velocity, examined in Table 5 and Table 7, was also seen to be above the impeller
tip speed, but this can be explained by velocity contributions from other directions and
turbulent behavior.

Second was the capture of behavior close to the tank walls, as seen in Figure 55b and

stationary domains. Alternatively, a finer mesh may lessen the effect of the sawtooth, and
better capture realistic flow at the cost of slower simulation.
Some errors may also be attributed to improper selection of timesteps. As mentioned
from the timestep analysis, 1s timesteps were found to be optimal for both tank sizes in order
to achieve a mixture of proper tank behavior and low equation imbalance, but compromises
were made to observe the behavior of the system at different rotation speeds. Because no
major instabilities were observed and the behavior of the systems was seen to be realistic,
it was assumed that the compromise was acceptable. Furthermore, the appearance of the
mixing time graphs between different impeller rotation speeds was similar, so it is believed
that scaling through the Courant number was successful.
7.2
CFD and Experiments
As was seen when discussing the Navier-Stokes equations, the density and viscosity
of a Newtonian fluid is sufficient to explain its behavior with regards to momentum and
continuity. For this reason, it was reasonable that the results for mixing time between the
physical experiments and CFD were in agreement. Further, this relationship allowed for
the use of proxy solutions, as utilized in these experiments. Because Mabs solutions were
seen to be Newtonian even for relatively high concentrations, sucrose made a cheap and
effective substitute, especially as a variety of compositions were used in practice, making it
Page 67 of 74
==PAGE:82==
Jens Hartvig, Master’s Thesis
7.2
CFD and Experiments
impractical to cover every possible combination of active component, excipients, etc. The
use of these proxy solutions did have a drawback in that the effect of strain rate on e.g.
foaming of polysorbates was not seen. Additionally, the density obtained while reaching the
same viscosity was not found to be an ideal match.
The agreement in mixing time between the experiments was not necessarily guaranteed,
as the presence of a conductivity probe measure affected the flow in the tank, but due to its
placement towards the top of the tank, where the velocity was lower, it was not a surprise.
The colorimetric experiment disagreed with the CFD in the the correlation between
viscosity and mixing time. The colorimetric experiments showed an increase in mixing time
based on an increase in viscosity, but this was likely due to the confounding variable of
sucrose solubility. The relationship found in the CFD was a slight decrease in mixing time
based on an increase in viscosity, related to the increased propagation of velocity through
the liquid. This appeared to be the more realistic behavior, and agrees with the decrease
in mixing time expected of increased power to the impeller (caused by more energy being
necessary to move the impeller through the more viscous fluid).
This additional power requirement for moving through viscous fluids is noteworthy at
larger scales, where it may become cost prohibitive.
Similar mixing speeds of 18-33 seconds was found in supplier information for a round
200L tank using the same drive system at 180rpm[16], suggesting that at the least the results
of the 200L CFD experiments will yielded a good estimate of the actual mixing speed. As
a 25s mixing time was found for 150rpm in the 200L cubical tank, despite being lower than
180rpm in this supplier document, it is likely that the corners indeed functioned as baffles,
improving the overall mixing quality.
7.2.1
Absolute Mixing Time
Between the CFD, where monitor points were placed at a variety of positions but may
not capture the worst case scenario, and the conductivity experiment, where only a single
probe was placed towards the top of the tank, it was noted that the time to get the actual
minimum and maximum concentration in the tank within the tolerance for a homogeneous
solution could be well beyond the measured values.
This was reflected in that the different monitor points often differed slightly in how
quickly they reached a proper degree of mixing. This is also typical of measurements with
probes, which often reveal a slight difference between e.g. a probe at the bottom and one at
the top[9]. For this reason, it would have been useful to have access to more probes for the
conductivity experiments, or to repeat the experiments with the probe in different locations.
Investigating the absolute worst case in the CFD experiment, the mixing time was found
Page 68 of 74
==PAGE:83==
Jens Hartvig, Master’s Thesis

to be about 30% higher for the absolute 98.5% mixing time. This is useful to know, as even
when a probe in the 200L tank suggests that the mixture is well homogeneous, it is worth
prolonging the mixing by 30%.
It would have been useful to record the minimum and maximum concentration in all
the CFD simulations.
7.2.2
Tank Shape and Behavior
The presence of an impeller shaft in the 1L tank meant that the flow could be altered
compared to the 200L tank. Some amount of momentum was transferred by the rotation
of the shaft to the liquid, and the shaft physically blocked the movement of fluid into the
center of the tank.
From comparing the streamlines and concentration in figures 27 and 28, it indeed ap-
peared that the mixing dynamics were affected, as much of the downwards momentum in
the 200L tank goes down through where the impeller shaft was located in the 1L tank.
7.3
Colorimetry Experiment
It was seen in the colorimetry experiment that challenges with mixing could be intro-
duced when the solute is poorly miscible in the bulk solution. Examining the case where a
dense sucrose dye was used as proxy for a strongly concentrated area of Mabs or aggregate,
the material was found to collect along the bottom sides of the tank. Using the dense sucrose
as a proxy for this situation was decided based on its slow mixing time and the presumed
heaviness of the protein aggregates.
Even though the mixing time between the 1L tank at 150rpm and 200L tank at 100rpm
was found to be significantly different, also in mixing quality, both tanks found that the
denser insoluble materials collected in the corners of the tank.
In the 200L tank, where there is significantly more turbulence, a larger portion of the
material was found to be suspended by the upwards force of the tank overcoming buoyancy
effects of the fluid.
As seen in these colorimetry experiments, it can be expected that the mixing time will
be much greater than that determined by the CFD simulations when the mixed substance
is poorly soluble in the bulk fluid.

7.4
Scale
Due to the desire of investigating large scale productions in smaller scale models to save
money and effort, the art of scaling is a well studied topic in the engineering field. As such,
a large variety of tools exist for investigating this topic, CFD being just one of variety of
strategies. Another avenue is scaling equations, which offer a rough estimate of parameters
such as mixing time and max shear strain rate, and are much faster to implement – though
less powerful.
A duo of these equations was made for mixing time and max shear strain rate based on
the CFD results at 1L and 200L.
Mixing time is a good indicator of the time necessary to achieve proper homogeneity in
the tank, and shear strain can help indicate the possible issues of protein aggregation and
polysorbate foaming. Unfortunately, the two were found to scale differently. In the situation
where it is desired to predict the qualities of a 200L tank process based on experiments at
1L, it will be necessary to conduct measurements at two different impeller rotation speeds.
Figure 29 and Figure 30 display the velocity and strain at 6 different impeller rotation
speeds for the 1L tank, with proportional maximum values. It can be seen that the profile of
the flows is very similar for 300, 600, and 900rpms, but seems to change for the lower values
of 50, 100, and 150rpms, likely as a response to the changing turbulence of the flow. This
difference is seen to be significantly less pronounced in the big tank, where the relative values
shown in Figure 32 and Figure 31, where the flows are all well developed in the turbulent
area. Villadsen puts forth the relationship tmix ∝1/Ni where the power number to fluid
mass remains steady[35], but the scaling equations revealed that the diameter of the impeller
is vital in finding a good relationship between the two tank sizes.
The power number can be seen in Figure 40 to remain stable after a certain Reynold
number, reflecting the area where the fluid behavior remains similar. The rapid increase
in mixing time seen in Table 5 below impeller rotation speeds of 300rpm is expected to be
caused by the decrease in mixing quality associated with the transition to laminar flow. This
is not a significant worry in the large tanks, where the increased diameter of the impeller
significantly decreases the rotation speed required to obtain the benefits of turbulent mixing.
7.4.1
Mixing Time
Impeller power number, or just the power input to the tank, is typically used to predict
mixing speed through the entire range of Reynolds numbers, using power numbers from the
impeller supplier. In lieu of having this impeller power number, it would have been useful to
measure the power input over different impeller rotation speeds to create a more universal
mixing time formula, or through calculating the torque on the impeller directly in the CFD.

7.5
Future Work
Because no verification experiments were performed on the 200L tank, there remains
some uncertainty as to the validity of the scaling equations and even the 200L tank CFD
results that were used as their basis. To better apply them in practice, it would be interesting
to measure the mixing time in the actual 200L tank.
It would also be interesting to verify the scaling equation with another solution viscosity,
to further test the limit of the correlations. Additionally, testing the equations with another
size tank would help ensure their legitimacy.
It is also noted that the equations are created assuming fully loaded tanks. Most scaling
equations in literature include a term for the height of the liquid to the tank diameter, so it
might be interesting to also relate the fullness of the tank to the mixing time.
Particle distribution was noted in the colorimetry experiment for a relatively viscous
solution that was somewhat dense (1.2823g/mL) compared to the bulk(1.247g/mL). It may
be interesting to use CFD to show the distribution of particles at different particle sizes and

Conclusion
Conditions were investigated for the mixing process after a freeze-thaw cycle of fully
formulated Mabs solutions, which typically induces a concentration gradient over the height
of the tank. This investigation was conducted for the company Fujifilm Diosynth Biotech-
nologies Denmark. It was desired to investigate mixing time, which determines the amount
of time that must be spent mixing the solution, and the shear strain rate, which can have
an impact on both protein aggregation and foaming of the polysorbate used to protect the
protein from damage.
Experiments were performed to yield mixing time on the basis of colorimetry, which
relied on the operator to observe the mixing time based on the gradual dilution of a dye in
the tank, as well as conductivity, which used a single probe to note when the concentration
reached a steady state. From the colorimetry experiments, it was seen that decreasing volume
and increasing impeller rotation speed yielded shorter mixing times, as well as distribution
of less soluble particles, represented by a viscous 51cP sucrose solution. Conductivity exper-
iments on a 40.7% sucrose solution revealed strong correlations between impeller rotation
speed and mixing time.
The CFD results were first prepared by creating a mesh size analysis and time step
analysis to ensure that results would be realistic without being overly computationally de-
manding, using the physical properties of solutions prepared for the mixing experiments.
These results were then confirmed by computational fluid dynamics, which was in good
agreement with the conductivity experiments. A strong correlation was seen between im-
peller rotation speed, impeller diameter, and the resulting max strain rate and mixing speed,
which can be used to guide the determination of mixing speeds between the 1L and 200L
tank.
From the CFD results, the mixing time was found to scale well through N −1

D−3/5

predict two equivalent impeller rotation speeds in the 1L tank that would match either the
shear strain rate or the mixing speed in the 200L tank at 150rpm. Unfortunately, because
the two scale differently, it was not found to be possible to investigate both simultaneously.
The distribution of insoluble aggregates or poorly soluble liquids was found through
simulation with an less-soluble 51cP sucrose solution in a less dense sucrose solution. In
the 1L cubic tank, the dense liquid was found to accumulate in the corners, away from the
strongest axial velocities, before diffusion gradually allowed it to climb up the sides. This
was compared to the 200L tank case using computational fluid dynamics, which similarly
found that the dense solution collected in the corners of the tank.
