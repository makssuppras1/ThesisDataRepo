Abstact 
This study was about finding an air change rate from a vast amount of data from 1068 
apartments in 4 parts of the city of Copenhagen. They were in the departments named 
Haderslev, Brøndbyvester, Svendborg and Valby. 
The concentration decay technique was chosen to be the most viable for this study for 
estimating the ACR, as occupancy status was unknown. Scripts using the program R were 
then chosen to be used for automatic detection of decay curves, as it is the most feasible 
way to go through 1838 years of data. Scripts from previous studies were implemented 
and changed with harsher criteria, so the method of selecting decay curves were more 
precise. The criteria included having a minimum start CO2 start concentration and 
restrictions on positive slopes in CO2 concentration. This reduced the risk of CO2 
production being present during the ACR estimation. 
31,858 ACR estimations were calculated using time series analysis. The median and 
average of the ACR estimations are 0.84 h-1 and 1.14 h-1 respectively. 78% of the ACR 
estimations fulfilled the requirements the ACR requirement of 0.3

apartments were conducted in 4 different parts of the city in Copenhagen.  
Haderslev had the highest ACR estimations which makes sense as it has a balanced 
mechanical ventilation system. Surprisingly Svendborg had lower ACR estimations than 
Brøndby, even though Brøndby had natural ventilation and Svendborg had balanced 
mechanical ventilation. The data might be biased though, as Svendborg has a higher 
amount of ACR estimations when it is colder outside. The hourly variance of the ACR is 
noticeable being highest during the day, and with small peaks when people were cooking. 
There is a small seasonal variance, where ACR in the summer is 1.22 times higher than 
the winter values. This is way lower than another study using active and passive tracer gas 
to find seasonal variance, there it had 8 times higher summer values than winter values 
(Bekö, et al., 2016). 
The variance of the ACR seems to be the highest when considering each apartment, as 
the lowest median is at 0.4 h-1, and the highest median is at 1.8 h-1.

The uncertainty of this study is large, considering that CO2 production can still affect the 
results, but they are mitigated by the criteria of a minimum CO2 value of 450 ppm must be

IntroducƟon 
Knowledge of ventilation rates in dwellings is important as a minimum amount of 
ventilation is required to maintain a healthy dwelling. Low ventilation rates can also cause 
higher concentrations of particulates and chemical compounds in the air, if they 
accumulate indoors. These may negatively affect human health in the short-term causing 
sick building syndrome and in the long term potentially causing respiratory disease or even 
cancer (Wolkoff, 2018). While ventilation can minimize the spreading of airborne diseases 
such as Covid 19 by displacing stagnant air, when done inadequately the air can get 
recirculated contributing the spreading (Correia, Rodrigues, Silva, & Gonçalves, 2020). 
The CO2 concentration is not dangerous in itself at the concentration typically encountered 
indoors in buildings, but when people are the main pollution source, it can be a measure of 
how well a room is ventilated. 
The air change rate also contributes to energy use in buildings. As air gets replaced from 
the outside, that air going in needs to be heated up to the indoor temperature levels. How 
the air gets heated up, depends on the type of ventilation system there is in the apartment. 
Balanced mechanical ventilation systems most often have heat exchangers, and they 
typically have a heat recovery rate of 70-90%. This means that only some of the air 
coming in from outside needs to be heated up (Bohne, 2023) 
The goal of the study was to estimate air change rates (ACR) in dwellings using 
measurements of CO2 concentration in 1097 apartments situated in Haderslev, 
Brøndbyvester, Svendborg and Valby, in an approximate span of 2 years. Previously, two 
studies with the same data set were carried out, where the goal also was to estimate ACR. 
The focus of the current study will refine the methodology and carefully evaluate the 
methods used to estimate ACRs. In this study there is more focus on how a decay curve 
should be, and how precisely an AR1 model estimates air change rates. 
As there needs to be an automated way to calculate the ventilation rates out of the vast 
amount of data given, the calculations for the ACR estimates are done using the program 
R, which is a statistical program language.

Theory 
VenƟlaƟon in dwellings 
There are 3 main different ventilation systems in buildings applied for this study, and they 
are natural ventilation, exhaust ventilation and balanced mechanical ventilation. 
Natural ventilation means that air in the dwelling gets air replaced through cracks of the 
building, window openings or ventilation slots in windows or doors. This way of ventilating 
the home usually depends on the persons living in the home to air out, when cooking or 
showering, or through exhaust vents from the bathroom to the outside. (Bohne, 2023) 
An exhaust ventilation system usually has a mechanical ventilator making negative 
pressure from the bathroom and/or kitchen, but usually both. They can be activated by 
moisture control in the bathroom, or in accordance with the light switch. The exhaust in the 
living room is usually controlled by activating the kitchen hood when cooking. This 
measure of addressing ventilation can minimize the moisture build-ups, but it usually 
doesn’t address the base ventilation problems dwellings have. (Bohne, 2023) 
A balanced mechanical ventilation system has a central unit with fans, heat recovery, 
heater and filter. The balanced mechanical ventilation system takes air from the outside to 
the centralized unit, then the air flows through a filter and a heat exchanger, where 
incoming air and exhaust air have thermal contact, thus trading heat. The air coming from 
the central unit gets supplied to typically bedrooms and living rooms, and the return air is 
usually in the bathroom, kitchen and possibly laundry room (Bohne, 2023).

Tracer gas CO2 
A common way to estimate air change rate is to track and analyze the timecourse of the 
concentration of a tracer gas. Occupant-generated CO2 can be used as a tracer gas and 
was available in this study. (Cui, Cohen, Stabat, & Marchio, 2015).  
The concentration decay method is where gas is injected and stopped when there is 
enough concentration of the gas in the room, and the decrease of that concentration is 
measured over a certain type of period. This decrease in concentration can then be used 
to estimate an ACR in a room (Cui, Cohen, Stabat, & Marchio, 2015). 
The constant injection method injects gas into the room with a constant flow rate, and the 
last method is constant concentration, where gas is injected to maintain a certain 
concentration. These 2 methods are less used as they require a larger quantity of gas, and 
certainly not suitable to use in this study, as you have to know how much gas you’re 
injecting. For the constant injection method, you would have to know specifically about 
how many people are in a room and know their CO2 production rate to reach an estimate 
of ACR and to reach a constant concentration, you would have to control how many 
people and increase their activity level to reach a constant concentration of CO2 (Cui, 
Cohen, Stabat, & Marchio, 2015). 
Mixing 
To obtain a reasonable estimate of ACR the room would have to have good mixing of the 
air. In a controlled study this would usually done by having a fan in that mixes the air, and 
then air is sampled centrally to ensure it is measuring from a place where the air is mixed, 
as corners can have “dead zones” where the gas concentration might not travel enough. 
In this study there is little control on how mixed the air is between the different rooms, as it 
is unknown in which room the occupants are generating the CO2. Despite that, the degree 
of mixing is unknown. An earlier study found that usually there is a decent amount of 
interzonal mixing in between rooms in dwellings, where a room typically reaches 70% of 
the gas concentration generated from another room (Clausen, Bekö, & Toftum, 2016). This 
is only the case when it comes from rooms on the same floor, whereas the gas doesn’t mix 
as well to other floors, which would apply well to this study as the dwellings are all on one 
floor.

Occupant behaviour 
The people living in the dwellings are usually the ones causing the highest variance in the 
amount of heating, air change rates and electricity use in homes.  
In this case with air change rates, the people living in the dwelling will often dictate how 
often they go out of the building, open the windows, decide to sleep with the bedroom door 
open. Occupant behaviour were bigger predictors on the ACR than building 
characteristics, where occupant behaviour accounted for 30% of the ACR variation, while 
9% of it was accounted for by building characteristics (Bekö, Toftum, & Clausen, 2011). 
The occupant behaviour in this study was not controlled/observed.

Previous studies 
Two earlier studies worked with the same dataset to find ACR based on the measured CO2 
concentrations (Dinesen, 2023) (Gylling & Revier, 2022).  
The first study was finished in Dec 2022 based on 303,664 ACR estimates. The study 
found a clear seasonal variation of the median ACR estimates where the summer period 
was 40% higher than the winter period. Using the corrected decay regression technique, 
they found that 73% of the apartments met the requirement of having an ACR of 0.5 h-1 by 
BR18. (Gylling & Revier, 2022) 
The second study found 124,266 ACR estimates. 55,348 of those estimates were 
assumed that the window was closed, which were estimates under 2 h-1, while 49,969 of 
them were assumed to be open at over 4 h-1, while the rest in the middle were not included 
in the modelling. The mean ACR was found to be 3.9 h-1 in the study, but it also removed 
ACR under 0.2 h-1 (Dinesen, 2023). 
In earlier studies focus was not on how appropriate decay curves should be selected, so a 
lot of the decay curves did not actually look like they were decay curves. Figure 2 shows 
20 decay curves randomly picked from the dataset, and there were not set enough criteria 
of how decay curves should look like, as almost all the decay curves randomly picked did 
not even reach a maximum of 450 ppm. Only one of the randomly picked curves looked 
like a decay curve (3rd row 2nd column), but the slope at the start was positive. The result 
of the estimated ACR would be a lot different if there was more focus on constricting the 
criteria of how a decay curve should be. 
 
Figure 2 20 random decay curves from another study (Dinesen, 2023)

Focus in the earlier studies was more on how the decay curves were selected. They made 
2 different methods of estimating ACR, with a regression decay technique which is the 
common way to do it. The other method was the PIT (Parametric iteration technique) 
method. The PIT method was developed to find ACR in buildings with natural ventilation, 
where occupant generated CO2 is present. It finds the best theoretical exponential curve 
on the measured data, by calculating different ACRs within the selected time period, and 
the decay curve with the least square method was chosen. They had 4 different results 
with a corrected and uncorrected version of both PIT and the regression decay technique 
(Gylling & Revier, 2022).  
This current study was done using the scripts made from another study estimating ACR 
with an AR1 model (Dinesen, 2023).  This was done by setting more criteria on what the 
decay curves should look like, and evaluating if the AR1 model method is viable to 
estimate the ACR by comparing it to the analytical logarithmic model of estimating ACR.

Method 
Case building and measuring equipments 
 
The data was acquired from a study about having the heating bill correlated to readings of 
indoor climate sensors placed in the apartment, instead of the traditional reading of energy 
meters from the radiators (Andersen, Trotta, & Gunnarsen, 2022).  
Indoor climate sensors were installed in 1068 apartments in Copenhagen. They were 
distributed in 4 different departments in the city, it was in Brøndby, Haderslev, Svendborg 
and Valby.  
The indoor climate sensors were placed in the biggest room and as centrally as possible in 
the apartment, and the type of room it was placed in varied by how the apartment was 
arranged (Andersen, Trotta, & Gunnarsen, 2022). 
The IC-meter sensors collected indoor climate data in the rooms, specifically: Indoor 
temperature, relative humidity and CO2 concentration.  
In table 1, the number of records measured with the 5 minute intervals all added together 
for all of the apartments corresponds to a total of 1838 years, with a total of 223 million 
datapoints. This only shows that there needs to be an automated way to detect these 
decay curves as it is impossible to manually pick some decay curves from the dataset. 
The apartments were constructed in the 60’s for Brøndby, Haderslev and Svendborg, 
whereas the apartments in Valby were constructed at the start of the 80’s. Brøndby was 
the only department that did not get renovated, which is why the apartments in Brøndby 
only have natural ventilation. The apartments in Haderslev and Svendborg were renovated 
later and they have balanced mechanical ventilation systems. The apartments in Valby 
have exhaust in the bathrooms and kitchens.

CalibraƟon of the IC-meter sensors 
The IC-meter sensors use nondispersive infrared (NDIR) light to measure the CO2 
concentration. The sensor emits infrared light and measures how much of the light has 
been absorbed by the CO2 molecules. This method of measuring CO2 concentration with a 
NDIR sensor needs calibration, as there are particles that could come in the way of the 
light, or the light might get weaker over time, which causes sensor-drift. 
IC-meter therefore had an automatic baseline calibration in the software, that calibrated 
the background CO2 concentration to be the lowest 0.5 percentile measurements, which 
was assumed to be the background concentrations. The automatic calibration did not work 
as it should have in a period from 2020-2021, so a lot of the data had wrong 
measurements, therefore the calibration was done manually using an algorithm doing the 
same, where the lowest 0.5 percentile of the CO2 concentration of the month was set to 
400 ppm (Andersen, Trotta, & Gunnarsen, 2022). Figure 3 shows what the CO2 
concentrations were before and after the calibration.

Comparing models that esƟmate the ACR 
The eﬀects of start- and endpoints on the ACR esƟmaƟon  
Decay curves must start with a negative slope, as the decay regression equation states 
the slope is negative. Theoretically there could be no end to a decay curve, as it could 
always go forever with the decay regression equation, but these were measured 
datapoints where the concentration would build up again at some point.  
In theory it should not matter where the decay curve starts or ends, the ventilation rate 
should be the same, but when handling datapoints, a logarithmic model or an AR1 is fitted 
to the data.  
Decay curves were handpicked with fitted logarithmic models. They all had R2-values over 
0.9, which indicated the logarithmic model fitted well to the datapoints. AR1 models were 
then compared with different start and endpoints, to see if the results are similar. 
Figure 4 shows the effect of the placement of the endpoint on the ACR estimate for the 
logarithmic model and the AR1 model. Ending it early (blue) was where the ACR was 
closest between AR1 and logarithmic estimates. Ending it where the CO2 concentration 
was lowest was when the AR1 resulted in an increase of the ACR estimate in parallel to 
the logarithmic model estimate.

Processing of data 
For the AR1 model to work, the data needed to be prepared as the AR1 model required 
measurements in timesteps of 5 minutes. The data was saved as a datetime format, which 
is a method to store time in the program R (Jones, Guarinello, Soderberg, & Wasser, 
2021). 
The first step after obtaining the data collection was changing the timesteps from showing 
datetime format to minutes, so from the start of the observation started at 1, and it ends at 
the last observation of the last building in the department, with every observation being at 
5 minute distance.

InterpolaƟon of the measurements 
The measurements read from the IC-meter were not always at 5-minute intervals, as it 
sometimes skipped 5 minutes and instead read after 10 minutes. The AR1 method used 
assumes that every data point is at a 5-minute interval, the method would overestimate the 
ACR if a regular 5 minute interval was assumed as shown in figure 8. The logarithmic 
regression model had an ACR estimate of 3.35 h-1, whereas an AR1 estimation resulted in 
3.66 h-1 before an interpolation used with between the missing 5-minute interval, and 3.46 
h-1 after. An interpolation was done for all the data, so there would always be a datapoint 
every 5 minutes, ensuring a closer ACR estimation using the AR1 method.

LimiƟng the ACR calculaƟons with an intercept 
The intercept in the AR1 model formula in this case was the background CO2. An algorithm 
was introduced to the R program, where only estimates of ACR over 0.05 h-1 went through, 
and the estimates must have an intercept between 300-600.  
This intercept range is to ensure estimates close to the background CO2 levels, and the 
that of the IC-meters uncertainty which the company claims is 50± ppm. A further study 
was done to review different sensors and their uncertainties depending on temperature, 
humidity levels and CO2 levels, and it was found that the IC-meter sensors at 20 °C had an 
uncertainty level around 100± ppm (Mylonas, Kazanci, Andersen, & Olesen, 2019), which 
is why an intercept between 300-600 was used. 
Table 6 shows how the intercept range changes the amount of ACR estimations, and the 
average ACR. The amount of ACR estimations got higher as the intercept range got 
looser, while the average also got higher.

Combining ACR calculaƟons with date and outdoor data. 
The next part of the script was combining the estimated ACR calculations with their dates 
and outdoor data. The outdoor data was given from the same company that owns the IC-
meters, depending on the location. Outdoor data was from observation points in grids of 
similar lengths, and they interpolated between those grids to provide weather data for 
different locations. This made it so the Haderslev, Brøndby, Svendborg and Valby had 
different weather data, so 4 different sets of outdoor data had to be connected to their 
respective departments. 
The weather data includes outdoor temperature, humidity, wind speed and wind direction. 
These were given in 1-hour intervals, so the first step was to interpolate between the 1 
hour intervals so it matched with the 5 minute intervals from the indoor IC-meter 
measurements. 
Then the time periods in the extracted decay curves were matched with the interpolated 
outdoor data. This used the date and time where the extraction curve started and ended, 
and matched with the start and endpoint of the outdoor data. 
The average of the outdoor temperature, humidity and windspeed was then found for the 
time period, so there only was 1 value of each representing that period. Additionally, the 
indoor temperature difference from the highest temperature of the extraction of the decay 
curve was taken, and subtracted with the temperature at the last point of the decay curve, 
this was found for the reason to find if there is a correlation between higher indoor 
temperature difference and the estimated ACR. 
All the data with the indoor IC-meter, outdoor data and dates with time periods was then 
combined in a new data frame, which was subsequently used for the results and analysis.

BR18 
The building regulation BR18 states that the ventilation rate in a dwelling should be at a 
minimum 0.3 l/s pr m2 heated floor area. This corresponds to an ACR of 0.5 h-1 assuming 
the room height is 2.3 m (Bygningsreglementet, 2018). This requirement was met by 78% 
of all apartments. Haderslev had the highest number of apartments meeting the 
requirements at 88%, while Valby had the lowest at 72% of the apartments meeting the 
requirement. 
Brøndby having the second highest portion to fulfill the BR18 ACR requirements was 
unexpected as it has natural ventilation. It had higher values than Svendborg, which has 
balanced mechanical ventilation systems.

Comparing studied ACR of dwellings with the ACR esƟmaƟons 
A study was conducted to find air change rates in different kinds of 96 Danish dwellings. 
These dwellings included single family houses, row houses, and apartments with different 
kinds of characteristics. It was conducted using active trace gas in 2-4 day periods, and 
some of the samples were conducted using passive tracer gas. (Bekö, et al., 2016).

apartments, with the seasonal variations. The apartment type was chosen from the study, 
so the dwellings were similar to the apartments available in this study. Another column 
included in table 14 was the passive tracer gas method conducted in the same study, but 
the sensor was placed in the bedroom.

studies. The controlled study with tracer gas had about 9 times higher ventilation rates in

times higher ACR than in the winter. It might be explained by the criteria made for this 
study, where decay curves CO2 increases over 10ppm over 5 minutes get thrown out, so

Uncertainty of the study 
The estimations of the ACR were based on the assumption of perfect mixing between the 
rooms, where the CO2 concentration would be the same between all the rooms. With that 
in mind, the decay method could be applied to have a reasonable estimate of the ACR. 
The decay method is also depending on there being no CO2 production present, and this 
is also the factor that is not totally preventable, but was addressed to some extent by the 
criterion that the decay curve should end at or below 450ppm. This does not totally prevent 
that there is no CO2 production as the people in the dwelling could be going into their 
bedrooms or pets that could be contributing to some CO2 production, which would lower 
the ACR estimate. 
Another thing that was found in a study measuring ACR in different Danish homes, was 
that using measured CO2 as a passive tracer gas to measure the ACR was in average 
twice as high as using active tracer gas (Bekö, et al., 2016). 
There could also be window openings that would set the CO2 concentration down under 
450 ppm, but people could still be present, the only way to measure the “real” close 
estimate of the apartment, in a large scale study like this, is knowing the people left the 
building and ensuring there is no other CO2 production. 
 The mixing of the air inside dwellings can’t be determined from the study and even though 
studies show that concentration of a room in single floor dwellings reach 70% from another 
room where people are emitting the CO2 (Clausen, Bekö, & Toftum, 2016). There could be 
other variables like closed doors, or open windows from other rooms, that would make the 
CO2 concentration way different from where the sensor is collecting the data.

Conclusion 
 With the help of the program R, 33,185 ACR estimates were able to be found from 
the 1068 dwellings in Copenhagen. 
 The median ACR was 0.84 h-1, while the average was 1.14 h-1. 
 78% of the ACR estimations are above the BR18 requirement of 0.5h-1. 
 There is a large uncertainty in the study, but some of it was mitigated by having the 
decay endpoint at maximum 450 ppm, and ensuring no decay curve rising by 
10ppm, to minimize the chances of extra CO2 production. 
 The seasonal variance is less significant than other studies done with active and 
passive tracer gas (Bekö, et al., 2016). 
 The hourly variance is noticeable, and the ventilation type as well when not 
including Svendborg, which showed that it had high amounts of ACR estimations 
when it was colder outside. 
 The highest variance of the ACR was in between the apartments with ACR medians 
varying from 0.4 h-1 to 1.8 h-1. 
 The modelling showed that there was a correlation between outdoor/indoor climate 
data and the ACR, but the effects were very small.
