Summary
Saving for retirement is achievable in different ways, such as through pension
contributions, residential housing, and other investment opportunities. De-
spite the variations of different retirement savings, they all follow a similar
idea, that the proportion of risky assets decreases with time to retirement.
The idea to spread consumption of a consumer over the life-cycle is often
referred to as life-cycle investing. Low consumption periods will be supple-
mented by periods of high consumption. Retirement planning should reflect
the expectations that individuals have towards their retirement years.
This thesis considers the effect of leveraging with human capital considera-
tion for life-cycle investments. Human capital refers to the employees’ human
capital, the potential future earnings adjusted for inflation.
Leveraging in
finance is considered as the act of borrowing capital in order to make more
substantial investments, that yield higher returns. To achieve this, different
leverage strategies were analyzed with their effect on pension portfolios. Pen-
sion portfolios are often categorized for different risk attitudes; risk-averse,
risk-neutral and risk-seeking attitudes. To fulfill this, three different pension
portfolios with different risk levels, formed different asset allocation depend-
ing on risk attitude. Where the ratio of risky assets towards non-risky assets
decreased as a function of time to retirement. The model is leaning on multi-
ple scenarios generated using the Monte Carlo simulation method. The model
is based on an equal weight leveraged strategy, which becomes less leveraged
when human capital decreases. The objective of leveraging was to outperform
the non-leveraged portfolio in terms of risk-adjusted returns. This research
concluded that in certain economic environment, leveraging with human capi-
tal consideration, can enhance life-cycle investing. Supported by investigating
the model sensitivity towards deviations in interest rates. Whether leveraging,
or other precarious strategies are the appropriate methods to use in life-cycle
investing, is dependent on the risk attitude and expectations individuals have
towards their retirement.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Life after retirement is usually not the top priority for people during their
professional careers. Other turning-points such as raising children, marriage,
professional career, homeownership are among the main priorities for many
individuals.
To be able to guarantee a successful retirement, life-planning
is an important element.
The focus of retirement is often organized when
individuals are halfway through their careers.
Retirement planning starts
when an individual begins to contribute an amount of their wealth into pension
funds or other sorts of savings. Despite the awareness of pension contributions,
individuals are unaware of how much capital they will need for their retirement
(Martin 2019).
The pension savings needed for retirement are dependent on each indi-
vidual due to the difference in consumption and expectations towards the
retirement years. For that reason pension savings could be tailored towards
different needs. The consciousness towards retirement planning needs to be
addressed, and individuals should ask themselves, ”how can I live a pleasant
life during retirement?” Human capital, that is all future earnings for an indi-
vidual, also varies among people. Leveraging in investment strategies can be
used to magnify portfolio returns at the same time as it increases risk. For
individualized pension portfolios, the combination of leveraging and human
capital has the potential ability to increase the performance of the investment.
In that strategy, human capital could be used as a guideline for individualized
investment strategies for life-cycle products.
This thesis intends to exemplify if human capital and leveraging can be ap-
plied to individual life-cycle products. To investigate this further, this research
will try to answer if, ”leveraging with human capital considerations, enhances
individual pension savings?” However, using leverage could raise concerns re-
garding risk and if it is relevant for different risk attitudes. Therefore, this
research will try to shed a new light and answer if ”leveraging is convenient for
all types of risk attitude?” Additionally, ”how does the financial environment
affect leveraged portfolios?”

1 Introduction
1.1
Pension Funds
Pension funds are investment funds that provide retirement commitments to
retirees. The funds are garnered from accumulated contributions from either
employers, employees, or a combination of both (Whiteside 2019). There are
two kinds of pension funds; defined benefit and defined contribution funds.
The defined benefit funds promise the employee a certain amount of pension
when they retire, based on age, amount of work, and so forth. Defined benefit
plans have many positives against their comparative. Mainly, that they allow
the employee to have an adequate plan during retirement, as the amount
received is a predetermined amount - or percentage - of one’s salary. The
negatives of such plans are that they put more onus on the employer, the very
definition of accruing money in such a way means that funding the pension
has an impact on company profits and the companies must predict the amount
of money needed to meet obligations (McWhinney 2019). In recent years the
defined benefit pension plans have decreased in popularity with now only 17%
of private-sector workers having access to one (EBS et al. 2018).
On the other hand, defined contribution funds rely on how much the em-
ployee has contributed to the fund and how much interest the money has been
able to accumulate. Therefore, the benefits paid out rely on the performance
of the fund, which can fluctuate between years and funds. Most pension funds
are risk-averse and try to return long term growth with minimal risk (Phipps
2019). Employers have the choice of whether to match contributions of the
employee. The pension funds will use asset allocation methods based on a
ratio of stocks and bonds that is dependant on the consumers’ risk preference
and years until retirement.
1.2
Leveraging
In finance, leveraging is the act of borrowing capital in order to make more
substantial investments that yield higher returns. The leveraged amount can
vary, but a company or individual having more debt than equity would be
classified as highly leveraged. This ratio of debt to equity can be described
using a leverage factor, in equation 1.1. In numerical terms, investment or
portfolio with a leverage factor of 3, has borrowed 200% of the capital.

1.2 Leveraging
3
Leveraging has positive relationship with risk; the higher the leverage, the
higher the risk. Leveraging by nature, also comes with the risk of default, i.e.,
the failure to meet the obligations of repaying a loan. In this thesis, the goal is
not to analyze the leverage factor itself or the probability of default. Instead,
to investigate the relationship between human capital and leveraging for pen-
sion savings. This report will follow from the work of a previous Master’s
student at the Technical University of Denmark, where the findings will be
used as a measure and analyzed further and how it can benefit pension savings.
In their thesis, Kristjánsson (2019) analyzed the optimal leveraged asset allo-
cation strategies. The research concluded that a leverage factor of 3,4 and 5
was optimal, meaning that the probability of default increased rapidly with a
factor of a higher order than 5. The thesis also concluded that a leverage factor
of 3 also had the overall best performance regarding risk-adjusted returns and
other risk characteristics. Figures 1.1 and 1.2 illustrates Kristjánsson (2019)
findings regarding the optimal leverage factor.

1.3
Salaries and Pension Contributions
Salary and wages vary with profession, experience, age, country, sex, inflation
rates, along with others. They will almost exclusively rise as one is promoted
through a company or career path. For the average person, the most significant
increase will occur when a person is transitioning from a role lacking any
experience criteria to an experienced role. The expected pattern of wages is a
gradual increase - that is steeper early in the life-cycle - that steadily tapers
up to retirement, where it will be on a gradual decrease. The rate of which
will be dependent on the pension scheme and how much was invested by an
employee or employer.
In this analysis, the average salary in Denmark was selected. Focusing on
the total for men and women and only fixed salary-earners, the average salary
of 2018 for ages ranging from 25 to 65 can be seen in figure 1.3. The age group
of 45-49 years shows the peak of earning potential; beyond that, the average
salary will steadily decrease, the rate of which slows down with increasing age.
Figure 1.3: Average salary based on age in Denmark (Statistik 2018).
In Denmark and many other countries, it is mandatory to pay into some
form of a pension scheme. Many people will even contribute above the min-
imum requirement. Aside from a defined contribution pension scheme, Den-
mark incorporates a public pension to all people over the age of 65, which
provides a universal income regardless of a retiree’s contribution. However,
this type of pension will not be the focus of the analysis.
Contribution rates in Denmark will vary between 12 and 18 percentage
of their salary.
On average, the total pension contribution is around 15%.

1.4
Human Capital
Human capital has a broad definition; it is the economic implication that is
produced from knowledge, experience, and skills of the employed. With this
definition, investing in human capital would take a more personable approach
than investing in traditional markets, meaning supplying training and educa-

Life-cycle investing is an important topic but has the primary desire to
spread consumption of a consumer over the life-cycle, so that periods of low
consumption will be supplemented by periods of high consumption. The most
optimal way to achieve this is by taking a higher risk in youth, and slowly
decreasing the proportion of assets to the risky side as one ages (Merton

life, to start investing in risky assets. Studies using a model with a mandatory

Volatility
Another measurement for an investment strategy is to measure its volatility.
While the average returns has a focus on the central tendency of returns,
the volatility focuses on deviation from the central. Volatility is a measure
of uncertainty, specifically in the change of the returns and, therefore, the
risk. The most common way volatility is measured, is in its standard devia-

can consequently be spread over a bigger price range, resulting in a higher
risk. Volatility does not measure the direction of price changes, and normally,
investors are more worried about a negative price change from the average
(Jorion 1995). Consequently, volatility is not always seen as a perfect way to
measure risk.

According to the efficient market theory developed by the American economist
Eugene Fama, stocks always trade at their true value. The theory states that
share prices reflect all available information making it impossible for investors
to consistently ”beat the market.” Therefore, the only way to obtain a higher
return is by buying riskier investments (Fama 2013). The assumption for this
study is that the investor has relevant market information for equities in the
Eurozone and uses this information for his investments. Another assumption
is that the investor has the credit needed for the investments. The data is
provided by Fama (2013) in 2012 and consists of 25 equity market indices and
six fixed income indices1.

4.3
Simulation and Scenario Generation
The objective of the simulation is to generate a substantial amount of future
scenarios based on historical data. The generated future scenarios only give
an idea of how the future can look and cannot be taken as gospel.
The scenarios can give a good indication of how the worst, best, base, and
average-case scenarios will look. Section 4.3.2 gives a deeper understanding
of each case. The scenario generation is then compared to the historical data
to ensure its statistical significance. The method used for the generation of
the scenarios is known as Monte Carlo simulation. It is a standard method
for scenario generation and uses known parameters from the training set, the
historical data, to generate the test set, possible future scenarios. One of the
upsides of using Monte Carlo is that it generates scenarios that are not in the
historical data. It can, therefore, generate some extreme scenarios which have
not occurred but have a possibility of happening in the future.
Historical
returns can not guarantee any future returns.
However, due to a lack of
reliable data, an assumption is made that simulation of the historical data
can represent future returns.

Estimating Interest Rates
The leverage model needs interest rates to compute the cost of borrowing.
Government short term bonds with the shortest tenor illustrate the proxy
for interest rates.
The reason for using the government short term bonds
with tenor less than one year is that they can be the proxy for the risk-free
rate. In developed countries, government bonds have a low credit risk due to
government vision in the long term and their possibility of changing the tax
rate (Ejsing et al. 2015). The generated short term bond returns, from the
scenario generation, have a big spread and behave differently than nominal
interest rates. The short term bond returns are volatile. The interest rates
were estimated by calculating the annual average return of the short term bond
every two years. A risk premium is included, which is considered to be 1.5%3
during the investment period. Figure 4.4 illustrates the difference between the

for the non-leveraged one. The reason why, the Sharpe ratio falls or increases

time, the standard deviation is not sensitive towards the deviation in interest
rates, as can be seen by looking at figures 5.16 and 5.21. On the contrary,

Discussion
Having financial security during retirement is an objective everyone should
strive for in order to live a comfortable life during the latter stages of life. It
can be achieved by saving a portion of the income continually. The continuous
savings should be invested to get the effect of compounding interests (Bodie
et al. 1992). As Albert Einstein said: ”Compound interest is the 8th wonder of
the world. He who understands it, earns it; he who does not, pays it” (Candice
2019).
In previous chapters, individuals’ pension portfolios have been examined.
Particularly, to see whether leverage with human capital considerations, en-
hances the individuals’ pension portfolio. This research is based on multi-fold
scenario generations that are trained on historical data. A Monte Carlo sim-
ulation was used to generate the multiple scenarios needed for the analysis,
as explained in section 4.3.
The model introduced in sections 3.1 and 3.2
was used to compute the returns for all the scenarios and strategies over the
investment period. In order to understand the results the analysis compared
different leveraged and non-leveraged portfolios, different risk profiles, and
measures for return and risk.
The comparison applied the same measures
such as NPV, Sharpe ratio, Sortino ratio, CVaR, VaR, along with others. To
ensure that the portfolios were compared on even ground.
The average-case scenario is used to indicate how each portfolio would
perform on average in terms of risk and return. The results from the average-
case affirmed, that higher risk-adjusted returns could be obtained by leverag-
ing when interest rates are low enough, as seen in section 5.3. Nonetheless,
the interest rates are rather high in the scenario generations. Therefore, the
risk-adjusted returns are on average lower for a leveraged portfolio than the
non-leveraged one, as noticed in section 5.3. Leveraging when interest rates
are low, preferably lower than 3.7% on average, can have a positive effect on
final values. Contrarily, when interest rates are higher than 4.7% on average,
it is not in the investors’ favor to use leveraging. In an economic environment
where interest rates are higher than 4.7%, the investor should look into other

final NPV can differ from around 500,000 EUR to approximately 1,500,000

are not as extreme in terms of large spread. Interest rates are an uncontrol-

kind of an interest rate environment is best suited for leveraging.
The leveraged risk profile that gains the most in terms of risk-adjusted
returns is the low-risk portfolio, observable from section 5.3. A risk-averse
investor invests in a low-risk portfolio because of a low-risk tolerance, i.e., to
avoid risk and spread portfolio (Bodie et al. 1992). To only use risk-adjusted
returns to measure whether it is beneficial to leverage a low-risk portfolio is
counter-intuitive. Leveraging a low-risk portfolio increases the spread of NPV
notably. That goes against the values of a risk-averse investor. Therefore,
it can be concluded that leveraged portfolios are more appealing for risk-
neutral and particularly risk-seeking investors. Since there are circumstances,
where leveraged portfolios outperform the non-leveraged portfolios, observed
in section 5.3.2.
As discussed in section 1.2, a former M.Sc. student at DTU, researched
optimized leveraged asset allocation strategies. The main focus in his research

which are important to highlight. The generated interest rates in this research
are significantly higher than the interest rates in Kristjánsson’s research. Es-

to this thesis to some extent, for example, it uses same historical data and
leverage factor. But there is a significant difference between the models where
Kristjánsson uses optimization strategy, while model 3.2 uses equal weighted
strategy that leverages equally during the investment period.

6.1
Future Work
The Monte Carlo simulation used in the thesis is, in principle, a very naive
approximation of the future. A Monte Carlo simulation assumes normality,
and does not use any other statistics other than the mean and covariance.
Therefore, it is not a perfect method since historical returns are not sufficiently
normally distributed. Furthermore, Monte Carlo simulation is unidirectional
(Szmigiera 2019). Therefore, it does not pick up on real-world patterns. Hence,
recessions such as the financial crisis that happened in 2008 would unlikely
materialize.
Improved interest and inflation rate generation would also be of consid-
erable value.
The rates generated in each scenario are estimated using a
profoundly simple method that probably does not reflect the future correctly.
Finally, a leverage optimization model that would take into account inter-
est rates, asset prices, transaction costs, timing of investments and risk would
probably increase the risk-adjusted returns by a fair amount. Model 3.2 has
always the same leverage no matter the interest rates. Figure 5.3.2 shows how
affected the performance is based on interest rates. An optimization model
that improves the current model by taking into account interest rates along
with the other aforementioned measures should therefore be trivial.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Human capital is generally not taken into consideration in life-cycle pension
products. Therefore, this thesis aimed to analyze if leveraging with human
capital could benefit people’s pension savings. By analyzing strategies with
and without leveraging this thesis shows that under the right circumstances
leveraging could benefit people pensions.
Furthermore, the role of human
capital for the leveraged strategies was not as significant as the role of interest
rates, as suggested by sensitivity analysis.
Analyzing the sensitivity for the leveraged strategies gave a comprehensible
insight into what parameters may exert influence on the leveraged strategies.
The sensitivity analysis inquired into how deviation in interest rate affected
the performance concerning its net present value, volatility, and risk-adjusted
returns. Intriguing or not, the role of the variations of human capital did not
affect the performance of the portfolios in the same manner as the interest
rates.
The leveraged and non-leveraged strategies were examined for three differ-
ent risk profiles. Those risk profiles described the risk attitude for individuals
if they prefer risk-averse, risk-neutral, or risk-seeking strategies. A part of the
analysis was to probe into if better performance could be obtained by imple-
menting a more aggressive leveraged strategy. It transpired to be in favor of
the net present value for the leveraged strategies to increase the risk in terms
of changing its predefined asset allocation and the role of the human capital. It
also resulted in an unsubstantial worse performance, concerning risk-adjusted
returns for the risk-neutral and risk-seeking strategies. However, the aggres-
sive leveraged strategy showed considerably better performance, in terms of
net present value, than the normal-leveraged strategy for any deviations in
interest rates. In consideration of the preceding, it is hard to argue which
leverage strategy is better or worse. It depends more on the risk attitude of
the individual.
Although a point to consider for individuals with a risk-averse attitude
that by leveraging a low-risk portfolio increases the spread and decreases risk-
==PAGE:90==
74
7 Conclusion
adjusted returns. Those individuals should, therefore, avoid low-risk strategy
with leveraging since it is the opposite argument for individuals that do not
want to increase the risk towards wealth.
The generated interest rates in this thesis are subject to certain deficiencies
since the aim was not to simulate interest rates for the life cycle. The rates
were based upon short term government bond returns.
Considering those
deficiencies, if the interest rates are reduced, the risk-adjusted returns for the
leveraged portfolios will be superior to the non-leveraged.
Life after retirement is a part of the life cycle that many individuals do
not pay attention to during their professional careers. Where people, usually
do not consider the role of human capital to spread wealth and consumption
from high wealth periods to low wealth periods. It is our opinion that in the
right circumstances, that by leveraging with human capital consideration can
benefit people’s life-cycle pension products. Nonetheless, it depends on the
risk willingness for each individual, whether leveraging their pension savings
is the right approach in their retirement planning.
