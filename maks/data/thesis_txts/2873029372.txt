Abstract
We investigate control variates to reduce the high variance that hinders
optimization in score-based models. We explore approaches using Taylor
expansions, normalizing flows, and the Stein operator. The Taylor expansion
approach achieves substantial variance reduction on a toy dataset, but shows
diminishing returns on MNIST and no effect on CIFAR-10. Higher-order
Taylor expansions provide no performance improvement over the zeroth-order
approach. Normalizing flows prove unsuitable as control variates due to un-
known likelihood expectations during training and the difficulty of making flows
dependent on score model parameters. While the Taylor control variates show
diminishing performance on larger models, the Stein control variates demon-
strate the opposite scaling pattern, performing better as model size increases.
The Stein control variates performs well on MNIST, achieving significantly
lower FID scores than the Taylor control variates throughout training, but
shows limited effectiveness on CIFAR-10. For the Stein approaches, the Stein-
NN method proves most promising due to its greater modeling flexibility, as it
can be easily configured through neural network hyperparameters, unlike the
Stein-Taylor method which is constrained by the computational cost of high-
order derivatives. We find that a learned scalar regression coefficient performs
on par with the analytically optimal coefficients for most experiments, and
suggest neural network modeling as a potential middle ground between the
high-variance analytical approach and the low-variance learned scalar.

Introduction
1 Introduction
In generative modeling, we have several methods to model unknown data distributions.
Approaches such as variational auto-encoders (VAEs), energy-based models (EBMs), and
normalizing flows all aim to maximize the log-likelihood of the data. However, trying to
maximize the log-likelihood gives each method significant limitations: VAEs optimize a loose
lower bound, EBMs tackle the challenge of partition function estimation, which is required to
normalize the energy-based density, and normalizing flows need specialized invertible architec-
tures. Score-based generative modeling offers an alternative approach that learns the gradient
of the log-density (known as the score) instead of the density itself. The goal of this approach
is to learn the score, which can subsequently be used to generate samples from the data
distribution. Unfortunately, this approach faces the challenge that the score objective exhibits
high variance, which hinders optimization (Song & Kingma, 2021). To address this variance
problem, control variates offer a promising solution. Control variates for score-based modeling
were first introduced by (Wang et al., 2020), who used a 0th order Taylor polynomial of the
score objective as a control variate. (Jeha et al., 2024) then generalized Wang’s approach by
deriving the control variate for an arbitrary order Taylor polynomial. Their work has shown the
effectiveness of control variates for low-dimensional data, but also showcases limitations of the
method for high-dimensional data, suggesting that more expressive control variates are needed.
This work builds on top of these contributions. In Section 4, the work by (Jeha et al., 2024)
is presented including additional experiments. In Section 5, we explore using a normalizing
flow as a control variate. In Section 6, we explore how we can use the Faà di Bruno formula
to easily construct higher-order control variates, based on the general Taylor control variate
formula derived by (Jeha et al., 2024). Finally, in Section 7, we investigate the Stein Operator
to construct control variates, which addresses a key limitation inherent in the Taylor-based
approach.

4.6 Experiments
We evaluate the Taylor control variate’s effectiveness in reducing gradient variance for three
different regression coefficients:
• 𝛽loss: The analytical optimal regression coefficient on the loss as given in Equation 15.
• 𝜷grad: Again the analytically optimal regression coefficient, but now computed in the gradient
space. Note that this yields a vector of weights, one for each parameter in the model. We
denote the scalar 𝛽grad as the average of this vector.

7 Stein Control Variate
In Section 6, we observed a major limitation of the Taylor control variate approach. By
discarding all terms with non-zero expectation, we lose most of the approximation’s explanatory
power, resulting in poor performance. The Stein operator offers a promising alternative by
providing a systematic way to transform functions into zero-mean control variates. This enables
us to construct control variates using the complete Taylor expansion, avoiding the information
loss that limits the previous approach. We will also demonstrate how the Stein operator can be
used to transform neural networks into zero-mean control variates. We first introduce the Stein
operator and its key properties, then demonstrate these methods.

Conclusion
8 Conclusion
In Section 4 we evaluated the Taylor control variate from (Jeha et al., 2024) on a toy dataset
with multiple MLP configurations for the score model. The control variate shows high variance
reduction on small MLPs, but performance degrades with deeper architectures. A learned
regression coefficient also proves effective, achieving comparable variance reduction to the
analytically optimal coefficients. This is encouraging since learning a single scalar parameter
is computationally more efficient than computing the optimal values analytically. The control
variate proves effective on MNIST, producing lower FID scores than the baseline, but does not
improve results on CIFAR-10. Increasing the Taylor expansion order from 0 to 1 provides no
improvement on any dataset.
In Section 5 we explored normalizing flows as control variates, inspired by (Müller et al., 2020).
A flow integrates to 1 over its domain, but using it as a control variate in our setting requires
computing the expected likelihood of the flow during training, which is unknown (the likelihoods
increase throughout training). Additionally, to use a flow as a control variate in score modeling,
the flow must be able to affect the gradient of the score model. This means the flow must depend
on the score model parameters, which proves problematic. These limitations led to focusing on
alternative methods better suited for score model training.
In Section 6, we investigated the construction of higher-order Taylor control variates using
the jax.experimental.jet Python library. Increasing the order to 2 and 3 does not improve
performance and appears to add noise rather than provide meaningful variance reduction. Our
analysis reveals that the control variate terms are negligible in size compared to the discarded
terms with non-zero expectation. This is a major limitation of the Taylor control variate.
In Section 7 we explored the Stein operator for constructing control variates. The Stein operator
can be used to generate zero-mean functions, making it ideal for control variate construction.
The toy data results show minimal impact from increasing the number of vectors used in the
Skilling-Hutchinson estimate. However, it would be valuable to test how the number of vectors
affects convergence on real datasets. While the Stein control variates generally cannot reduce
gradient variance on the toy dataset to the same level as the Taylor control variates, they show
a promising scaling pattern. They perform better on larger models—the opposite of what we
observe with the Taylor control variates. This scaling advantage is confirmed in the MNIST
results, where the Stein control variates achieve significantly lower FID scores than the Taylor
control variates throughout training. On CIFAR-10, however, the Stein control variates do not
outperform the Taylor control variates.
We experimented with three regression coefficient approaches but focused mainly on two: the
analytically optimal coefficient computed on the loss and a learned scalar parameter, as the
gradient-based approach is too memory-intensive for larger datasets. The optimal coefficient
exhibits large variance, while the learned coefficient is inflexible. A promising future direction
would be training a small neural network that takes in contextual information and outputs the
regression coefficient, potentially providing a middle ground between these approaches.
Overall, the most promising control variate approach for score model training appears to be the
Stein control variates, especially the Stein-NN method. This method offers greater flexibility
as it can be easily configured through standard neural network hyperparameters, whereas the
Stein-Taylor method is limited to very few parameters since computing high-order derivatives
quickly becomes infeasible. Future research should explore two key directions. First, modeling
the regression coefficient using a neural network could provide a middle ground between the
high-variance analytically optimal coefficient and the restrictive learned scalar parameter.
Second, it would be interesting to investigate whether larger Stein-NN networks could enable
