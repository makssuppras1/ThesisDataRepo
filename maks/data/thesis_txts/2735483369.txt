Abstract
Transwell inserts are advanced microfluidic systems meticulously designed to mimic bio­
logical barriers serving as invaluable in vitro models for investigating drug transport across
cellular barriers. While injection molding has recently emerged as the preferred manufac­
turing method for these inserts, its high investment cost and consequent high product
prices have motivated the exploration of alternative techniques. This project has intro­
duced an innovative manufacturing approach that combines 3D printing and vacuum form­
ing to create customized inserts for 96­well plates. This process has involved the creation
of custom 3D printed molds, followed by vacuum forming of the inserts, laser cutting of
apertures in the bottom, and the formation of a hydrogel layer within the aperture. Ad­
ditionally, these custom inserts have been seamlessly integrated with the OT­2 pipetting
robot, showcasing compatibility with automated procedures for pipetting, gelatin casting,
and cell seeding. Caco­2 cells were cultured within the gelatin layer for a 21­day cycle
to establish an intestinal barrier, followed by validation of its integrity through a transport
experiment and TEER measurement. Overall, these custom inserts have demonstrated
significant promise as an alternative to the standardized injection molded ones currently
prevalent in the market.

1
Introduction
1.1
In vitro and transwell inserts
Pharmaceutical companies are confronted with the stark reality that only roughly 10%
of compounds undergoing costly clinical trials ultimately progress into viable new drugs.
These trials represent a significant portion, approximately 80%, of the total associated
costs [1]. This harsh statistic underscores the considerable investment and risk involved
in drug development processes. Regarding oral administred drugs, the growing concern
over low bioavailability has prompted the escalation of drug doses, leading to heightened
risks of toxicity, complications, and also economic inefficiencies [2]. As a result, the signif­
icance of in vitro modeling in scientific research has witnessed a notable uptick in recent
years, primarily owing to its ability to accurately replicate biological environments in a
cost­effective and relatively straightforward manner, especially when compared to in vivo
studies [3]. This capability holds immense potential in mitigating the probability of drug
rejection during later stages of the drug development process.
For orally administered drugs, preclinical in vitro testing of absorption, distribution, metabolism,
and excretion (ADME) emerges as a pivotal stage in curbing drug failure rates [4]. Ab­
sorption assays specifically, serve as crucial early­stage evaluations in understanding
the absorption mechanisms of orally administered compounds across the intestinal lin­
ing and into the bloodstream. Additionally, recent advancements in automated synthesis
and combinatorial chemistry have enabled the production of vast numbers of new chem­
ical entities (NCEs) for screening as potential drug candidates. This has accelerated the
identification of lead compounds in the early stages of drug discovery [5, 6].
A commonly employed approach for conducting such studies involves the use of tran­
swell systems featuring single cell lines. These transwell inserts are meticulously engi­
neered microfluidic systems designed to replicate biological barriers like the small intes­
tine, providing an excellent platform for studying drug transport across cellular barriers [7,
8]. Comprising a porous layer that separates the apical and basolateral sides, transwell
inserts offer a controlled environment for cellular growth (fig. 1.1). When cultured on a
softer matrix, epithelial cells display organization and polarity akin to their in vivo counter­
parts [9]. The integration of hydrogel matrices enables a more accurate reproduction of
physiological characteristics, thereby enhancing the precision of in vitro study outcomes.

1.2
Transwell insert manufacturing
In recent years, 3D printing has become a pivotal manufacturing process within the biomed­
ical industry [10], and also for transwell inserts [11, 12]. This approach offers numerous
benefits, including the ability to produce highly customized and intricate designs quickly
and cost­effectively. It enables rapid prototyping and iteration, allowing for faster product
development cycles. The availability of biocompatible materials has further bolstered its
utilization in this context [13]. However, a notable limitation of 3D printing lies in its suit­
ability for large­scale production [14], specially in the case when the 96 well plate comes
into play due to the high number of wells and the difficulty to support fluidic operations
[15]. Consequently, with the increasing interest in transwell inserts, an exploration into
alternative manufacturing techniques has been initiated.
One such technique is injection molding, a longstanding method utilized in industries such
as food packaging [16] and automotive [17] but also in medical [18] and pharmaceuti­
cal [19]. Noteworthy for its capacity to yield high volumes of products with precision,
injection molding also offers a diverse range of material options. Among these materi­
als are several biocompatible plastics, including Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene (ABS)
and Polystyrene (PS), among others. Nevertheless, processes like injection molding and
other high­volume manufacturing methods often require large and costly molds to pro­
duce a significant quantity of products efficiently. This necessity for substantial molds not
only drives up production costs but also demands substantial initial investment. There­
fore, businesses must carefully consider the financial implications and scalability of such
manufacturing processes before implementation. Some injection molded transwell inserts
have reach the market in the recent years but they are very expensive, reaching prices of
around 1.500 DKK for a single 96 well plate insert [20]. This is attributed to the sequential
nature of their creation, involving a two­step process: initially, injection molding is em­
ployed to craft the inserts, followed by the subsequent attachment of a bottom porous
membrane through adhesive bonding [21]. Although it has proven effective, additional
manufacturing techniques have been studied to reduce the cost of the process.
In this particular field of transwell insert production, an innovative method for this field

that could be introduced is vacuum forming. This manufacturing approach not only facili­
tates high­volume production but also ensures a reasonable level of accuracy in the final
product. Like many other high­throughput manufacturing processes, vacuum forming ne­
cessitates the use of molds. However, these molds are notably less intricate and costly
compared to injection molding counterparts, albeit at the expense of some precision in
the finished product [22].
An investigation of this technique has been introduced in this project, tackling the early
disadvantage of purchasing functional expensive mold by the design of a 3D printed mold
that can replicate the same results for a fairly decent amount of inserts. While vacuum
forming does not excel for creating intricate designs, it offers the advantage of achieving
thinner thicknesses compared to injection molding. This is attributed to its reliance on
the stretching capacity of the material rather than the precision of the mold [22]. Conse­
quently, this capability enhances the feasibility of producing thinner hydrogel layers within
the bottom section of transwell inserts, which is the most important feature for our needs.
Despite the advantages of achieving thinner layers, there exists a necessity for balance
when creating hydrogel layers. These layers must maintain a stable grip for attachment,
and excessively thin plastic may struggle to provide the requisite stability. Therefore, care­
ful consideration is essential in striking the right balance between thickness and stability
to ensure optimal performance.
1.3
Automation for transwell inserts
When discussions turn to ”high throughput applications”, automation inevitably assumes
a central role. Over the years, robotics and automated processes have captured con­
siderable attention spanning diverse industries, from automotive [23] to more recent en­
trants like pharmaceuticals and biomedicine [24]. Robots are predominantly engineered
to tackle repetitive tasks, with pipetting being one of them within biomedical research cir­
cles.
Automated pipetting has witnessed a notable surge within major pharmaceutical com­
panies in recent times [25, 26]. However, it’s widely acknowledged that automated pro­
cesses thrive on standardized procedures and protocols, owing to the inherent lack of
adaptability exhibited by robots. The custom nature of these transwell inserts presents a
hurdle in the context of automation. Given their unique designs tailored to specific experi­
mental needs, these inserts don’t fit neatly into the standardized protocols that automation
thrives on. As a result, attempts to incorporate custom transwell inserts into automated
processes can encounter roadblocks, necessitating innovative solutions to bridge the gap
between customization and automation.
But in this regard, the ambitious idea of obtaining custom 96 well plate inserts that can be
fully automated for cell culturing could bring a novel way for pushing this technology into
the next step.

Materials and methods
2.1
3D printed molds and vacuum formed inserts
2.1.1
3D printing
The mold has been designed for vacuum forming inserts for a Nunc™MicroWell™96­
Well, Flat­Bottom Microplate. It has the size of a quarter of a 96 well plate (24 wells).
The software used for the impression has been Preform. The mold has been printed in
a FormLabs 3 printer using Black v4 resin and a layer thickness of 0.1mm. The supports
have been settled as minibases without inner supports to ensure a smooth surface in the
critical part (inner part of the wells). At the bottom of the mold, there is a 3mm gap in
contact with the wells, featuring two screw holes designed to secure a PMMA base onto
the mold. This PMMA base includes air holes that do not directly connect with the insert
wells, ensuring adequate airflow during the vacuum forming process while maintaining a
flat bottom surface for the inserts. Each hole in the PMMA base has been deburred to
ensure a smooth surface for the subsequent vacuum forming process. Attachment of the
base to the mold is facilitated by two M3 6mm screws. The sides of the mold have a 10º
angle to enhance its separation from the PS sheet after the vacuum forming. They have
been also sanded to smooth the surface for the same purpose. After the mold has been
printed, it has been washed in the Form Wash washing station (Formlabs) for 20 min and
12 washes to remove any resin leftovers. After washing, it has been introduced in a UV
Light station (Form UV, Formlabs) for 30 minutes at room temperature for fully curing the
remaining resin.

2.1.2
Vacuum froming
Following printing, the molds were placed in the Vaquform DT2 (Formlabs) vacuum form­
ing machine (fig. 2.2). A 2mm thick Polystyrene (PS) sheet, preheated at 60°C overnight,
was loaded and heated to 180°C. Once the desired temperature was reached, the sheet
was pressed onto the mold while the machine drew air from the bottom, effectively vacuum
forming the insert. Separation of the molds from the PS sheet was achieved by drilling
holes on top and hammering them out. Subsequently, the PS sheet was processed in the
laser­cutting machine to exclusively separate the inserts. Once separated, each insert
was inverted, and a 1mm diameter hole was laser­cut (BEAMO FLUX laser cutter) at the
bottom of all inserts, where the gelatin layer would subsequently be formed.

Robot setup
The robot automation has been performed using the OT­2 (Opentrons) pipetting robot.

2.2.2
Hydrogel layer casting automation
A solution comprising 15% gelatin in Phosphate­buffered saline (PBS) was utilized to gen­
erate the hydrogel layer, followed by a solution containing 15% microbial transglutaminase
(mTG) in PBS for biocrosslinking for 30 min at 37ºC in an incubator. To measure the layer

solution at a concentration of 1% of the gelatin volume, and analysis was conducted using
a Zeiss Primovert fluorescent microscope (Carl Zeiss MicroImaging GmbH). The analysis

2.3
Cell culture
Caco­2 cells (passage, 70­80, ECACC) were cultured in complete epithelial growth medium
(Dulbecco’s Modified Eagle’s Medium with high­glucose; Sigma­Aldrich with 10% (v/v) fe­
tal bovine serum (FBS, Hyclone, CA), 1% P/S (penicillin (100 U/mL) and streptomycin
(100µg/mL);Sigma­Aldrich) and 1% (v/v) NEAA (nonessential amino acids; Gibco, Fis­
cher Scientific) in T­75 cell culture flasks (Fischer Scientific). Cells were cultured in an
incubator at 37ºC, 100% humidity and 5% CO2. Complete growth medium was replaced
every 2 days. Subculturing was performed upon 90% confluency using
trypsin­ethylenediamuntetraacetic acid (EDTA) for 5 minutes.
2.3.1
Staining

(PFA) in PBS for 2min and 4% (v/v) PFA in PBS for 13min at room temperature. PFA was
removed from the samples, and samples were washed in PBS three times. The fixed
cells were permeabilized with 0.1% (v/v) Triton X­100 for 5min, blocked with 0.1% (v/v)
Triton X­100+3% (w/v) BSA for 1h at room temperature (RT). Cells were then incubated

2.3.2
Transport study
The permeability coefficient (Pc) of 0.44 kDa lucifer yellow (LY; Sigma­Aldrich) was mea­
sured by adding 30μL of 100 μM concentration of Lucifer Yellow in Hanks’ Balanced Salt
Solution (HBSS; Thermo Fisher) in the apical compartment and 200μL of only HBSS in
the basolateral compartment. The transport was performed at 37°C with slow shaking for
2h. 100μL samples from basoalteral compartment and 25μL of apical compartment with
the addition of 75μL of HBSS were transferred to a 96­well plate and the Relative Fluo­
rescence Units (RFU) were measured using a plate reader (Spark multimode microplate
reader, TECAN, Männedorf, CH). The excitation and emission (Ex/Em) wavelengths were
428nm/536nm. The concentration of LY in each sample was determined using standard
curves disposed in the same well plate using the same initial concentration of Lucifer
Yellow (100μM). The permeability coefficients (Pc, nm/s) were calculated by using the
equation:
Pc = (V r ∗Cf)/(Ci ∗A ∗t)
(2.1)
where, Pc is the permeability coefficient (nm/s), Vr is the receiver volume in mL, Cf is
the final receiver concentration (µM), Ci is the initial apical concentration (µM), A is the
membrane growth area in cm2, and t is the assay time in seconds [27].
2.3.3
TEER measurement
The EVOM2 volt­ohm meter (World Precision Instruments) was employed to obtain TEER
values of epithelial monolayers. This was achieved by positioning an Ag/AgCI chopstick
electrode pair (STX3, World Precision Instruments) at the apical and basolateral compart­
ments(fig. 2.4) [27]. The impedance values (Ω*cm2) are obtained by extracting the cell
barrier resistance values using the software PS Trace 5.9.

Results and Discussion
3.1
Optimization of the design
To achieve a functional insert, multiple parameters were optimized throughout the pro­
cess. While maintaining the top well diameter and the distance between wells constant,
the primary adjustments focused on altering the height of the well and the bottom insert
diameter. The flat bottom Nunc 96 well plate has a height of 11.3mm, with the well’s bot­
tom diameter measuring 6.1mm and gradually increasing to 7mm at the top. The aim of
the insert bottom is to be as close to the well plate bottom as possible. This adjustment
proves advantageous for imaging from beneath the plate and also for minimizing the vol­
ume of medium in each well. Taking that into account, the first mold designs proposed had
a well height of 10mm and different bottom well diameters (2,3 and 4mm respectively).
This design failed due to the very thin insert bottom, which fractured during the vacuum
forming process (fig. 3.1a).
(a) Failure in the vacuum forming process.
(b) Wobbly bottom surface of 8mm height inserts.
Figure 3.1: Failed designs.
A design alteration was implemented, reducing the height by 2mm to a total of 8mm and
the inserts were successfully vacuum formed. Nevertheless, despite these adjustments,
the bottom thickness remained excessively thin and varied significantly among the wells,
being unstable and unsuitable for laser cutting holes (fig. 3.1b).
After reducing the height again by 2mm to a total of 6mm, the insert bottom surface re­
mained plain. The 2mm diameter insert was disregarded because the bottom was not flat
enough to drill holes on it (fig. 3.2) and therefore only the 3mm and 4mm were used for
in the next steps of the project. A sum up of the different values of bottom thickness and
bottom diameters can be observed in fig. 3.3.

3.2
Robot automation
Regarding the automation and the pipetting robot, there have been two different aspects to
develop: the implementation of custom labware and the programming of robot protocols.
The flat bottom Nunc 96­well plate used in this project was not available in the Opentrons
labware library. Its creation was required from scratch as custom labware, similar to the
inserts. Consequently, numerous parameters had to be inputted for both components.
While achieving the well plate was relatively straightforward, several adjustments were
necessary for the inserts until they were perfectly compatible with the robot.
The robot protocols had to be designed for different experiments including pipetting into
the well plate, pipetting into the inserts, and casting the gelatin onto the bottom of the
inserts. The corresponding scripts for these protocols are attached as JSON files and are
referenced within the report when mentioned. The text files have not been included in the
appendix due to their large size.
3.2.1
Hydrogel casting
Hydrogel casting accounted for a considerable part of the optimization of the project,
hence various strategies were proposed to achieve a homogeneous layer in every well.
In order to pipette the gelatin directly into the hole, the inserts were inverted, positioning
their bottom part facing upwards (can be appreciated in fig. 3.8).
Figure 3.8: Initial gelatin procedure.
The initial approach involved two steps. First, a gelatin drop was deposited into the top of
each well. Then, all drops were aspirated one by one (fig. 3.8). While this method proved
unsuccessful, it showed significant opportunities for optimization in subsequent attempts.
The main one was that the drop was not stable enough to be maintained in every singlet,
either because it slid out of the surface or it was sucked inside the insert trough the hole
due to the surface tension (fig. 3.9).

==PAGE:22==
(a) Gelatin drawn inside the insert.
(b) Gelatin sliding off the insert.
Figure 3.9: Gelatin casting failures.
Following several trials adjusting multiple parameters, a promising outcome was achieved
by modifying the positioning of the pipette during drop release. As mentioned before,
releasing the drop directly onto the laser­cut hole proved problematic as the gelatin would
be immediately drawn inside. Instead, the solution was to release it slightly off­center but
not too close to the edge of the bottom. By adopting this method, the gelatin had a stable
surface to be released on. This ensured that it could smoothly slide into the hole while
being retained on top of the insert, thereby, facilitating the formation of the desired layer
exclusively within the hole.
The second, and most important optimization was to release and aspirate the gelatin
drop in the same step while ensuring it was in constant contact with both the pipette tip
and the inserts throughout the process (fig. 3.10). In this way, the gelatin drop could not
escape the surface until it was aspirated back. Combining both optimizations, a gelatin
layer could be achieved in every hole. This automated procedure with the pipetting robot
was achieved using the script 1. The process was programmed with alternating steps
of dispensing and aspirating the gelatin drop individually in every hole. The dispensing
volume was set at 20µL, while the aspirating volume was set at 40µL. This adjustment was
necessary because during dispensing, some gelatin tended to slide off the outer surface
of the pipette tip, resulting in a larger deposited volume compared to the volume within
the pipette tip. The insert with a bottom diameter of 3mm was disregarded in this step
since the surface area was not sufficient to ensure the stability of the gelatin drop on top
of it after any of the purposed optimizations, and therefore only the 4mm bottom diameter
insert was used in the next steps of the project.

To crosslink the gelatin layer, a solution containing 15% microbial transglutaminase (mTG)
in PBS was employed. This procedure was automated with the pipetting robot. The
inserts were turned and placed on top of a 96 well plate when the apical site was needed
to be filled. Initially, the proposal involved adding mTG to both the apical and basolateral
sites. This resulted in crosslinking occurring indiscriminately throughout the gelatin layer,
rather than specifically within the insert hole. After numerous trials, it was determined
that the most effective approach was to apply mTG solely to the apical site and PBS to
the basolateral site. To achieve this, scripts 2 and 3 were employed respectively. The
addition of mTG is shown in figure fig. 3.11.
Figure 3.11: mTG addition to the apical site of the inserts.
The well plate and the insert were placed in the incubator for 30 minutes at 37ºC for
biocrosslinking. Then, the mTG was removed and washed with PBS manually. The insert
with the crosslinked hydrogel layer was kept in the fridge with PBS to avoid the gelatin
drying and breaking.
3.2.2
Gelatin thickness measurement
For measuring the gelatin thickness, FluoSpheres™carboxylate­modyfied were added
to the initial solution (1% of the gelatin volume). The procedure followed for the gelatin
casting replicated the one mentioned in section 3.2.1. Once the gelatin was crosslinked,
it was visualize in the fluorescent microscope. fig. 3.12 illustrates the presence of fluo­
rescent beads within the gelatin layer. Notably, these beads are confined solely to the
insert hole and are not dispersed across the entire bottom surface. This indicates that the
gelatin layer has been successfully formed exclusively within the insert hole.

In fig. 3.13 the bottom thickness of the insert and the gelatin thickness are shown for dif­
ferent insert heights. They were measured using a Z­stack processing in the fluorescence
microscope, following a protocol developed by FAST group. This technique allows for the
identification of fluorescent beads layer by layer, enabling a quantitative measurement of
thickness by stacking all the layers containing the beads. It is noteworthy that the bottom
thickness increases after laser cutting the hole. This occurs due to the formation of plastic
burrs when plastic melts, which results in an augmented thickness. After the initial trials
with the 6mm height insert, it was anticipated that there would be a direct relationship be­

several factors that could have influenced the process, the results did not meet these ex­

3.3.1
Cell barrier integrity study
To evaluate the integrity of the cell barrier, two distinct experiments were conducted. The
initial experiment involved a transport study employing Lucyfer Yellow (LY) as a fluores­
cent compound to assess its passage through the barrier. Specifically, a volume of 30µL
of 100μM in HBSS was introduced to the apical site of the inserts (script 2), while PBS
was added to the basolateral site (script 3), ensuring continuous contact of the two liquids
with the gelatin layer. The objective of this experiment was to investigate the transport of
LY through the cell barrier and compare it with that across a gelatin layer alone.

The findings of this experiment unveiled a notable contrast in intensity at the apical site
between inserts possessing a cell barrier and those lacking it. This discrepancy led to a
significantly lower Permeability Coefficient (Pc) for the inserts with the cell barrier com­
pared to those without, indicating the formation of a tight barrier (fig. 3.16). A Permeability
Coefficient value of ≤12 nm/s is recognized as indicative of a well­established intestinal
barrier function of Caco­2 cells[15].
Figure 3.16: Intensity values of LY after the transport experiment for inserts with cell barrier
and without it, for both apical and basolateral sites.
The second experiment carried out to further strengthen this conclusion was the measure­
ment of transepithelial electrical resistance (TEER). An electric signal was applied across
the barrier using an electrode, allowing the impedance measurement, which reflects the
integrity and tightness of the barrier. The experimental set up and protocol was performed
by Hakan Gürbüz (FAST group Postdoc).
fig. 3.17 shows the teer results and the equivalent circuit used in this set up for fitting
the impedance curves. The circuit shown in fig. 3.17B represents a resistance from the
capacitance of the cell membrane (Cc), resistance of the paracellular route (RTEER), re­
sistance of the medium (Rmedium), and electrode capacitance (CE). By extracting the
value of RTEER and dividing it by the cell barrier area (0.01 cm2) the impedance of the
barrier is obtained (Ω*cm2). Despite the significant deviation in the results, the differ­
ence in the impedance confirms the presence of a well­established tight barrier within the
hydrogel layer.

4
Conclusion
In this project, a combination of vacuum forming and 3D printing techniques has been
meticulously explored for the fabrication of transwell inserts. Moreover, a concerted effort
has been made towards implementing an automated procedure adapting bespoke inserts
to the OT­2 Opentrons pipetting robot, achieving automated gelatin casting and automated
cell transferring tasks.
The efficacy of the vacuum forming process has been substantiated in achieving con­
sistent bottom thicknesses conducive for laser­cutting apertures and automating gelatin
casting, as depicted in fig. 3.13. Although the present investigation has primarily focused
on 6mm height inserts for the 96­well plate configuration, fig. 3.13 also underscores the
potential for further optimization, suggesting that alternative heights may yield thinner
gelatin layers.
Furthermore, these inserts have demonstrated their efficacy in facilitating the develop­
ment of a uniform hydrogel layer, crucial for establishing a tight and robust cell barrier,
as evidenced by the transport experimental results (fig. 3.16) and TEER measurements
(fig. 3.17). The full automation of every stage of the process underscores the potential
of integrating both manufacturing techniques to craft bespoke devices tailored to specific
experimental requirements. This approach offers a notable advantage over the recently
seen injection molding methods, as it requires a significantly lower investment while pro­
viding enhanced flexibility and customization options.
