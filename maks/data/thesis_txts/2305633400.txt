Abstract 
An extensive world population growth is creating a bigger need for food technology in-
novation. This thesis will investigate existing and potential new applications of brewers’ 
spent grain (BSG) as food source. BSG constitutes 85% of total by-products from indus-
trial beer brewing, and is widely available, as the growing beer industry produce ap-
proximately 40million tonnes BSG/year. Although BSG is considered as having high nutri-
tional value, it is also considered as a low value by-product, commonly used for animal 
feed. The low market value is mainly due to the high moisture content of BSG and there-
fore high risk of contamination. Since the 1970’s several studies have investigated poten-
tial uses of BSG, with the major focus on human food products, animal feed and as feed-
stock for fermentation processes. Economic case studies have shown that because of the 
variations between breweries (location, size etc.), using BSG as animal feed, isn’t always 
profitable. As an attempt to increase the value of BSG, this project introduces a filter-
machine that fractionises the BSG into a solid fraction (filter cake) with 11% lower water 
content than BSG, and a liquid fraction (filtrate) with a higher water content, but only par-
ticle sizes small enough to enter the 300µm filter. The two fractions were analyzed for 
protein, fibre, sugar and polyphenol (PP) content, in order to estimate their potential 
uses. Especially the filtrate fraction is expected to have potential as food product or in-
gredient in food formulations, in its raw form, but may also be a great subject for further 
refinement into purer products. The filter cake fraction was treated thermochemically 
(acid and alkaline) and enzymatic with celluclast (Novozymes) in order to produce a sub-
strate for fermentation. The yield of fermentable sugars, estimated to approx. 30%, was 
much lower than for other studies claiming to have converted up to 97% of the available 
cellulose. Despite the lack of process optimization for hydrolysis of filter cake, and pro-
duction of α-lactalbumin the project shows how BSG can be used for a wide range of 
both human and animal food products, and production of valuable proteins such as α-
lactalbumin with the implementation of a simple filtermachine technology. The project 
successfully proves the concept of how interdisciplinary is necessary in the field of biore-
fining, and how just one innovative technology solution is not sufficient in order to start 
utilising BSG in a more sustainable way.

1. Introduction 
According to United Nations (UN), the world population (counting 7.3 billion by mid 
2015) is expected to grow to 9.7 billion in the year 2050 (United Nations, 2015). This will 
require a rise in overall food production by 70% in total between 2005/07-2050. This 
production rise will not extend the use of arable land with more than 5%. Instead the in-
creased production is expected to come from higher yield, increased cropping intensity 
and technology/infrastructural improvements. At the same time, the annual growth in 
crop yields is slowly decreasing (FAO, 2009), and for this reason it is important to utilise 
by-products from the food industry, with high nutritional value. In this study Brewers’ 
spent grain (BSG), with focus on the protein fraction, will be evaluated as potential feed-
stock for biorefining, with several valuable outputs. 
Protein is one of the five macronutrients (protein, carbohydrates, fat, fibre and water) 
humans must consume in relatively large quantities to provide energy for metabolism, 
body maintenance, growth and general body functions (Joanne R. Lupton et. al, 2005). In 
the human body, proteins functional properties includes: being a major structural com-
ponent of all cells, functioning as enzymes, transport carriers, hormones etc. The rec-
ommended dietary level is 0.8g good quality protein/kg bodyweight/day (10-15% of total 
energy intake). The composition of the 20 standard amino acids (AA) varies in different 
foodstuffs of which 8 are categorized as essential AA to adults and 9 to children, because 
of the human body’s inability to synthesize these. A diet combined of food derived from 
both animals and plants provides an adequate intake of essential AA. People who avoid 
meat (vegetarians) or all animal products (vegans) must balance their diet carefully in or-
der have an adequate intake of all essential AA (WHO/FAO, 2003). 
In UN’s prediction, the highest expected population growth (by percentage) will be in Af-
rica and Oceania. In most of the countries classified as “developed economies” such as 
USA, Japan, Germany etc. the meat intake per capita per year was in 2009 in the range: 
40-120kg/capita/year. Only 5 out of 54 African countries represented a meat intake of 
more than 30kg/capita/year. The “Atlas of Economic complexity”, Center for International 
Development at Havard University (McKenney, 2015) has predicted 101 countries’ (more 
than 40 African countries included) annual economic growth rate until year 2024 with 
Uganda, Kenya, Tanzania and Egypt in the top 5, only surpassed by India. The African,

Asian, Oceania and other countries, that has been in development and are expected to

Utilising proteins in plant material by creating a human dietary supplement is basically a 
100% utilisation. If the plant material is fed to a dairy cow, the nitrogen (used as indicator 
of protein) is excreted in milk, urine and faeces. The amount of nitrogen excreted as milk 
is in examples of good dairy production efficiency around 30% (Crump, Arnd, Aguerre, 
Wattiaux, & Powell, 2015). A supplementary protein diet made from barley results in more 
nitrogen excreted in the milk, but only with a conversion efficiency of 13% (Steinshamn, 
Hoglind, Garmo, Thuen, & Brenøe, 2006). To put this in perspective (hypothetically), if a 
plant protein composition is of high quality, and it is possible to refine it with a recovery 
of just 60%, it is still twice the amount of protein an efficient milk production would yield. 
The importance of both protein amount and AA composition could make it attractive to 
refine plant protein for ease of combining them in the right amounts, e.g. baked into 
bread. The most obvious contradictory argument would be that the milk protein AA com-
position would overrule the loss of protein by low nitrogen conversion. Using protein 
concentrates as food ingredient is a well established market, mainly for whey protein (the 
major by-product of cheese production). The company “Danmark Protein” owned by Arla 
Foods is currently producing 86 different whey protein products and is still expanding 
towards new markets, and the overall market for functional foods is expected to grow 
rapidly over the next years (IndustryARC, October 2014).

1.1 Biorefining 
A biorefinery is a facility that converts biomass into valuable products. The general con-
cept of a biorefinery is shown in Figure 2. The main product groups can be described as: 
1. biofuels derived from chemical treatment of lipid fractions; 2. high protein or carbohy-
drate fractions for use as food ingredients and 3. as substrate for white biotechnology, 
with an endless range of opportunities of products (biofuels, bioplastics, fine chemicals, 
enzymes, protein) (Research Institute Biopos, 2008). A newly published market research 
report estimates that industrial biotechnology will account for more than 49% of the total 
biorefinery market share by 2019 (TechNavio, 2015). 
The main source for biorefining is lignocellulosic plant material (IEA Bioenergy Task42, 
2014). Lignocellulosic material is composed of cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin, and is 
the most abundant organic material on earth, representing 50% of total biomass 
(Chandel, Chandrasekhar, Radhika, Ravinder, & Ravindra, 2011). The energy required to 
break down this plant material is dependent on the lignin, as lignin is the “glue” that 
binds the cellulose and hemicellulose together. Lignocellulosic biomass is one of the 
largest resources and also one of the greatest challenges in biorefining. A big part of bio-
refining is the degradation of the cellulose into glucose. Historically enzymes used for 
cellulose degradation has been ineffective and inhibited by the glucose formed during 
hydrolysis. As enzyme producers, such as Novozymes, continue their research in improv-
ing cellulase products and at the same time develop new hydrolysis methods, lignocellu-
losic material becomes more interesting for hydrolysis (Novozymes, 2015). 
Rødsrud, Lersch, & Sjöde (2012) described “The most advanced biorefinery in operation”, 
named Borregaard, which is located in Norway and has been in business for more than 40 
years. The company is specialised in using timber as feedstock, for production of: Bark 
chips (thermal energy), Purified cellulose fibres, Bioethanol, Biogas and chemical com-
pounds sold for further processing to production of Lignin and Vanillin. Borregaard is a 
successful company and a prime example of being loyal to the biorefinery strategy by 
creating as much value as possible from biomass. However, this “total use” of biomass by 
integrating different technologies (and here by an impressive product portfolio), is not a 
matter of choice in terms of having a certain company appearance, it is simply some of 
the critical success factors of a biorefinery, and the only way they can compete with pe-
troleum refineries (Soetaert, 2006).

1.2 Beer production  
In order to understand the by-product BSG, a brief introduction to the beer brewing proc-
ess is given in this section.  
Beer has been a commercial product as far back as year 1200. Today it is the fifth most 
consumed beverage in the world and accounts for 36% of the worldwide total alcohol 
consumption (Bala & Aliyu, 2011). In 2014 the worldwide annual beer production was es-
timated to 1.96 billion hectolitres (Statista, 2014) expanding from just 1.3 billion hectoli-
tres in 1998 (almost 50% increase in 16 years). The beer industry consumes more than 
90% of the global malt production and around 15% of the world’s total barley production 
(Black, Bewley, & Halmer, 2006).  
The basic steps in beer brewing are: malting, milling, mashing, lautering, boiling, fer-
mentation, conditioning, filtering and bottling (see illustration in Figure 3). In the malting 
process, the barley is soaked in water, germinated and then dried to stop germination. 
When the barley grains germinate, enzymes and other important components are pro-
duced, with the purpose of degrading larger molecules. The malt is ground at different 
scales dependent on the brewery equipment. It is possible to use coarse malt and still 
obtain a relatively high brewing yield in the mashing step. Sugars are extracted from the 
malt with a temperature profile starting around 45oC and ending at 78oC (mash off), 
where the enzymes are inactivated. The grains are then “sparged” with additional water in 
order to drain/extract more sugars that are still bound to the grains. Lautering is the 
separation of the liquid sugar extract, known as wort, from the solid fractions known as 
BSG. The wort is then boiled with hops, for sterilisation, adding flavour (from the hops) 
and precipitation of hot trub (protein precipitate during boiling). The wort is transferred 
to the fermentor (sterile conditions), cooled down and inoculated with yeast for fermen-
tation into beer. Conditioning is the common word used for beer maturation (different 
processes are used for maturation). The beer is in most cases filtered before finally keg-

ging/bottling/storage. Beer brewing can basically be done with any source of cheap 
starch (that is approved for food) and grains other than malt, which are widely used, such

partly substituted, the nine process steps mentioned are the most common, but not all of

1.3.1 Animal consumption 
BSG has been successfully utilised as animal feed in e.g. cattle, fish and mink farming 
(Musatto, Dragone, & Roberto, 2006). In Denmark, one of the major breweries dispose 
their BSG as feedstock for cattle. Harboe brewery produces in average 80 ton BSG pr. day. 
Along with other by-products, both from breweries and other food and ingredient pro-
ducers, the BSG is distributed by a middleman to a network with a total of 20.000 cows, 
of which at least 8.000 cows must be ready to consume 7kg of BSG every day. This re-
quires good logistics, as the storage silo at the brewery must be emptied on a daily basis 
with no temporary storage in between the brewery and the cattle farms, due to high risk 
of microbial spoilage (Olesen, 2014). 
Nutritionally BSG is very useful as animal feedstock, but a study by Ben-Hamed (2011) 
conclude that it isn’t always profitable to use BSG for animal feed, and the economic re-
turn is very dependent on brewery size, distance to animal herd etc. Therefore, there is 
an urgent need to develop alternative uses for nutrient rich BSG. Instead of categorising 
BSG as low value that should be disposed in a convenient matter (e.g. picked up by 
farmer), BSG should instead be categorised as having a great value, inviting brewers to 
implement an on-site processing, which makes it suitable for other uses.  
Price agreements between breweries and “customers” are many and exist for both small 
and large-scale breweries. Prices between 0 and 40 USD per ton wet BSG have been re-
ported (Higgins, 2014) (Bingham, Radloff, & Dhiman, 2003) (Buffington, 2014) and for 
this reason it is not easy to estimate, what the exact value creation will be in other utili-
sation methods, to match this method.

1.3.2 Human consumption 
BSG is in general considered as having a high nutritional value. USDA dietary guidelines 
uses the following macronutrient guideline for humans 18 years and older: Carbohydrate 
(45-65%), Protein (10-35%), Fats (20-35%). Half of total grain consumption should be 
whole grain, especially in order to have a good source of dietary fibre (USDA, 2010). BSG 
used in wheat bread baking will increase the fibre and protein content and lower the 
starch content, even in whole grain wheat bread (Prentice & D'Appolonia, High-Fiber 
Bread Containing Brewers' Spent Grain, 1977). The fact that BSG is a by-product from 
human food production, has an attractive nutritive composition and does not need de-
toxification process, makes it a very interesting subject. Not only for replacing main food 
ingredients such as flour in its raw form, but also for further refinement and hereby iso-
lation of purer products of higher value.  
Prentice et al. (1978) studied the use of substituting flour with dried and milled BSG. By 
replacing 15% flour with BSG in sugar cookies, the nitrogen content was increased with 
27%, and the fibre content was increased fourfold. The organoleptic properties were low-
ered, but the product was still acceptable. The study suggested that a suitable milling 
and fractionation could improve acceptability in cookies, as it would be possible to select 
flour fineness, fiber and nitrogen content.  
Many studies focus on baking with BSG, but other uses such as replacing pork back fat in 
chicken sausages has also been investigated (Choi, et al., 2014). Fat is difficult to substi-
tute as it contributes to product characteristics in several ways, that are complicated to 
mimic (Goldberg, et al., 1991). According to Min-Sung Choi et al. (2014), 25% of pork 
back fat were successfully replaced with a BSG pre-emulsion. However, it should be noted 
that this pre-emulsion required several processing steps with chemicals and high tem-
perature levels, which must be assumed to add costs to the process. 
 
1.3.3 Building bricks 
Non-food related studies for utilisation of BSG includes using it to replace 3.5% of the 
original Dry Matter (DM) in a brick clay mixture. The bricks produced with BSG possessed 
similar or higher strength, lower density and higher porosity, resulting in better thermal 
isolation properties.  Large scale tests showed that the use of BSG in building bricks are

1.3.4 Brewers’ spent grain as media for fermentation 
Many studies have investigated BSG as a source of fermentative sugars, pre-processing 
the BSG via physical, chemical, thermal and/or enzymatic treatments. Some of the first 
publications that investigated the use of BSG as substrate for fermentation includes: Pro-
duction of xylanase by Aspergillus awamori (Bhumibhamon, 1978), production of cepha-
losporin (antibiotic) in medium with spent grains and protein sediment as nitrogen source 
(Zdunvkjak, Tamburasev, & Prester, 1980), and research in producing hydrolysates

Hordeins (35-45% of total barley protein) are storage proteins, preserving AA and metal

high proline and low lysine content. Hordein passes partly into both wort and BSG. It is 
the main reason why people suffering from celiac disease have trouble drinking beer 
(Tanner & Howitt, 2014). Prolamines are in general only soluble in alcohol solutions. She-
wry (1993) divided Hordeins into four groups (A-D) by their size and characteristics. A-
hordeins (15-25kDa) constitutes less than 5% of total Hordein content and are not likely 
to work as storage protein. B-hordeins (32-45kDa) are rich in sulfur and constitutes

malting process. Malt contains less than half of the original Hordein content in Barley.

dosperm. It remains unchanged into the BSG. Glutelin dissolves only in diluted alkali and 
is therefore the least well-studied barley protein, as the strong solvents and/or extreme 
extraction conditions needed will denature or even degrade it, and therefore complicate 
the possibility of a complete protein analysis (Steiner, Gastl, & Becker, 2011).

interest when trying to find solutions to utilize the trub produced in the wort boiling step. 
(Steiner, Gastl, & Becker, 2011).

1.5.1 Nutritional value of polyphenols  
Polyphenols (PP) as functional food ingredient has been a topic of interest for the last 
decade, and market analyzes show that there is reason to expect this market to grow sig-
nificantly in the next years (Saha, 2015) (grandviewresearch.com, 2014). The growing 
==PAGE:25==

Daniel Stender Nørgaard

interest is based on the assumption that PP has a protective effect against Cancer, Car-
diovascular disease and neurodegenerative diseases. European Food Safety Authority 
(EFSA) has so far rejected all health claims on PP except for olive oil. The claims specifica-
tion is that in order to bear the claim an amount of 20g of olive oil should be consumed 
daily (NDA, 2011). It is rather difficult to put in perspective to the PP content in Barley and 
BSG.  
Several studies assess the correlation between total daily intake of PP and health benefits. 
One of these studies investigated the correlation between PP intake and mortality in older 
adults (Zamora-Ros, et al., 2013). The study is not able to show any significant mortality 
decrease. However, it shows a correlation between excreted PP amount (in urin) and mor-
tality. In this study, subjects with high total urinary PP (>173 GAE/day) had a significantly 
decreased mortality compared to subjects with low (123-173mg GAE/day) total urinary 
PP. An average PP intake in Europe is around 1200mg/kg (Overvad, et al., 2015). A rough 
estimation according to the Zamora-Ros study, a PP intake increased with 20% 
(240mg/day) could potentially decrease mortality. Even though only one health claim has 
been approved so far, and the study of correlation between total urinary PP and mortality 
is inconclusive, PP should still be of great interest if any BSG product could contribute to 
an increased intake of 240mg/day. The PP content in Barley ranges from 190-750mg/kg 
DM (Fujita, Takeda, Kohyama, Doi, & Matsunaka, 2002). 
 
1.5.2 Bovine α-lactalbumin 
Bovine α-lactalbumin (bαLA) is used in this project as an example of a valuable protein 
that can be produced by fermentation based on BSG media.  
Lactose synthase, which synthesize lactose (2glucose → lactose), is the enzymatic com-
plex, formed by β-1,4 galactosyltransferase (catalytic component) and α-lactalbumin 
(regulatory component). α-lactalbumin is a small milk protein that is present in all lactat-
ing animals in varying forms (Permyakova & Berliner, 2000). bαLA is the common food 
protein with highest tryptophan content known (6%). Tryptophan is an essential AA and 
responsible for synthesizing the neurotransmitter serotonin which is known to play an 
important role in “chronic stress” syndrome among humans. By increasing the ratio of 
tryptophan to other AA, the tryptophan availability to the brain is increased, resulting in

tryptophan proteins are necessary (Markus, Olivier, & Haan, 2002).

2.1 Fractionation 
In order to fractionize BSG, a new technology filter system was used from the Danish 
company Dacofi. The Dacofi Filter machine is a patented system with two drum filters and 
two drum rolls with a rubber surface, driven by four separate engines (Hansen, 2014)(see 
Figure 5). The filtering is initiated by an overpressure inside the feed chamber. The fil-
trate is collected inside the drum filter, and the filter cake is scraped of the drum filter on 
the outside. The Dacofi filter machine has a potential of filtering 1ton/hour, at different 
pore sizes from 50 to 300µm. The system is suitable for different foodstuffs (and other 
organic material) and is currently on license by the Swedish company Larsson Starch 
Technology AB for potato pulp (fibre) production. The BSG used for fractionation was ob-
tained from a pilsner brew conducted at the DTU brewery (see brewlog in appendix 1).

2.4.1 Media preparation 
The BSG filter cake was used for media preparation and would undergo three (acid-, alka-
line- and enzymatic treatment) processing steps (illustrated in Figure 7): 
1. Acid pretreatment. 
150g filtercake (38%DM), stored in -40oC freezer, was autoclaved at 121oC for 15 min 
with deionised water 420g (liquid-solid-ratio 8g H2O/g DM) and Sulfuric acid (100mg 
H2SO4/g DM).  
2. Alkaline treatment 
After the acid-treatment (1) autoclaving, the pH should be approximately 1.2. pH was 
adjusted to 10 by adding 8N NaOH, followed by autoclavation at 121oC for 15 minutes.  
3. Enzymatic treatment 
After the alkaline treatment (2) autoclaving, the pH was be approximately 7.5. pH was 
adjusted to 5.5 with 2M H2SO4 and a magnet was added for stirring. If the slurry was too 
viscous to stir, sterile deionised water had to be added until rotation started.  
Water bath temperature was set to 45oC and stirring at 350rpm. Celluclast aqueous solu-
tion (Novozymes) was added in a concentration of 6% (g Enzyme/g DM) equivalent to 
2.85mL Celluclast for 150g filter cake.  
After 96 hours, the BSG slurry was centrifuged at 10.000rpm for 30 minutes, and the su-
pernatant (BSG media) was filtered by vacuum filtering with 47mm filters by gradually 
lowering the filter porosity, in the following order for a liquid volume of 250mL: 
5 x 70µm, 6 x 20µm, 1 x (10, 5, 3, 1 and 0.8µm), 2 x 0.45µm and 2 x 0.22µm.  
The pH was adjusted to 7.0 with 8N NaOH, which caused a precipitation. A blue cap top 
filter (0.22µm) was used as the final filtration before storage of the media at -20oC.

3.1 Mass balance for fractionising brewers’ spent 
grain  
As an attempt to use fractionation of the low value by-product BSG, for separation into 
two more valuable products, a simple and energy efficient filtering process was carried 
out on one batch of BSG from a pilsner brew conducted at the DTU brewhouse.  
This section will discuss the mass balance of BSG press filtering and further separation by 
centrifugation (See Figure 9). The BSG was pressed immediately after mashing and within 
approximately 1.5 hours. The temperature was 75oC degrees in the final step of mashing, 
and the temperature was gradually lowering towards RT during the pressing. The total 
amount of filter cake and filtrate was respectively, 25.9kg and 29.3kg. Based on the as-
sumption that there is a 1% loss when processing the BSG through the filter machine, the 
total amount of BSG was estimated to 55.0kg. After this experiment was conducted, the 
filter machine has been equipped with a vertical feed screw, creating an overpressure in 
the chamber that makes it possible to process 1ton/hour. Increasing the capacity from 
0.04ton/hour to 1ton/hour will result in much better consistency in future experiments, 
as the BSG won’t be gradually cooled towards RT.

3.1.1 Filter cake 
The water content of the solid fraction decreases from 73% to 62%, when processed 
through the filter machine. The machine was not optimized for filtering of BSG, when this 
experiment was conducted, and it cannot be rejected that it will be possible to reach a 
much lower DM percentage by adjusting filter pore size or other process conditions. As 
the machine is able to run without any addition of water, experiments of running the fil-
ter cake a second time, both with and without added water will be of interest, to see what 
the outcomes will be in quantity, DM content and nutritional content.  
3.1.2 Filtrate 
The filtrate was separated in a centrifuge at different times and velocities (see Table 9). 
Even though the filtrate appeared highly viscous, it was possible to separate more than 
half as supernatant (C3). At significantly lower velocity and speed (C1), a lower amount of 
supernatant was separated from the sediment, but still the fractions separated easily. The 
greater amount that can be separated as supernatant, the more concentrated will the in-
solubles be in the sediment. If the sediment is intended for drying afterwards, it is of 
greatest interest to remove as much water as possible in the centrifugation process. The 
soluble material is slightly more concentrated the higher velocity and time of centrifuga-
tion. Depending on the intended use of the two fractions, the separation ratio can be ad-
justed from 0 to at least 1.386. While there is no advantage in terms of total DM content 
of supernatant, C3 appeared very clear, in comparison to C1 and C2 that appeared very

3.2.3 Polyphenols in brewers’ spent grain fractions 
It is assumed that the natural PP content in Barley is in the range from 190-750mg/kg 
DM. Some of these PP’s are solubilised into the wort during mashing. According to Eu-
rofins analysis the PP content in the filtrate and filter cake was more concentrated than in 
the raw barley: respectively 1200mg/kg DM and 1130mg/kg DM. Based on PP health ef-
fect studies and dietary guidelines, a rough estimation gave an indication that 240mg PP 
per day could have beneficial health effects. 
The dry filtrate product has a great potential of being incorporated into food formulations 
and will require 200g dry filtrate powder to provide 240mg of total PP. Whereas the same 
serving of a high PP Barley variant would provide only 150mg of PP’s (Lasztity, 1996). The 
potential for the BSG filtrate to be used as dietary supplement is increased by the PP con-
tent, under the assumption that future research will form basis for more non-specific or 
Barley grain specific health claims. Although as dietary supplement it is not realistic to 
consume 200g/day. However, the filtrate could be considered as contributor to PP intake 
rather than a singular functional food product. It must be prioritised to analyse the con-
centration of PP in the filtrate supernatant product, and what it might contribute to a soft 
drink product in the end.

This section will discuss the HPLC analysis of simple sugars for acid- and alkaline-treated

slurry hydrolysates used for fermentation. As reference, the enzymatic treatment was also 
applied to chemically untreated BSG, described as H1+H2 in Method section 2.4 Table 7. 
Enzymatic treatment of not chemically pretreated BSG showed no sugar concentrations

3.3.1 Enzymatic treatment of acid-treated brewers’ spent grain 
The acid-treated BSG refers to H3+H4 described in section 2.4 Table 7.  
Figure 12 show the glucose, xylose, cellobiose and total sugar yield for acid-treated BSG 
and ground acid-treated BSG. Cellobiose concentration increased until approximately 75 
and 85 hours and then readily degraded to glucose. The cellobiose peak could be used as 
indicator for future trials, as optimized process conditions for the enzyme could poten-
tially result in an earlier peak, or no peak at all.  
Grinding of the BSG material does not appear to enhance the effectiveness of the enzy-

137 hours for acid-treated and ground acid-treated BSG respectively. This is important 
knowledge to gain, in order to understand how cellulase enzymes work, and a mechanical 
pretreatment of BSG should not be in focus in future experiments.

The GMO strain was tested for growth in media based on BSG, with a wild type E. coli as

media. 
The E. coli was tested for growth in liquid media prepared with 25% and 50% BSG media

3.5 Utilisation of brewers’ spent grain 
During this project several ideas for utilising BSG were investigated, both theoretical and 
practical. The filtrate and filter cake fractions can be used for some of the same purposes 
as BSG, but is also introducing several new potential applications (see Figure 18). For 
both fractions it is still relevant to consider the short storage time. The filter cake water 
content is lower than for BSG, giving opportunities for increasing storage time. The fil-
trate water content is higher than in BSG and increases risk of contamination. The lowest 
risk of wasting the by-products, due to bad transportation logistics or other factors re-
sulting in contamination, would be to have an in-house processing of the fractions. 
Breweries cannot introduce an organism different from yeast into the production, but all 
other suggestions have potential as a secondary production line in the brewery. Another 
option is to process the fractions with a certain aim (e.g. used to substitute a small DM 
percentage in human food with intensive processing requirements), but only processing 
in-house until the product has obtained shelf life.  
It is important to emphasize that this project does not evaluate the different possibilities 
to choose the ultimately most potential one. Breweries beer production, raw materials 
quality, production volume, location, tax rules, energy costs etc. vary and each brewery is 
an individual case study. By introducing the fractionation of BSG, there is now a broader 
variety of potential outcomes. Another consideration could be storing the BSG or BSG 
fractions at the brewery site, under conditions that would lower the contamination risk 
remarkably (high or low temperature, controlling with starter culture etc.). This incentive 
would give an increased interest and marked price of BSG, while large volumes would still 
limit storage time, as the brewery cannot have unlimited amounts of BSG at their site, 
neither at these conditions. 
As mentioned the price range of BSG is not well defined, and these economic factors can-
not be ignored. But sustainability is another important perspective in this context, and 
BSG as feedstock for animals does not seem to be a sustainable solution compared to the 
alternatives. Functional foods/high protein food products, low alcohol beer and health 
beneficial beverages have been chosen as potential end products, as they were consid-
ered being part of food markets that will continue to experience growth. As food trends 
are changing and not always predictable, new food groups will become of interest.

4. Conclusion 
According to UN, the world population (counting 7.3billion by mid 2015) is expected to 
grow to 9.7 billion in the year 2050. This will require full utilization of all resources in-
cluding by-products from the food industry, with high nutritional value. In this study, BSG 
with focus on the protein fraction was evaluated as potential feedstock for biorefining 
with several valuable output products. BSG accounts for 85% of the total amount of by 
products generated in beer brewing and is estimated to be available in a quantity (re-
phrase) of 40 million tonnes a year. A popular way of disposing BSG is as animal feed, but 
this is not always profitable, and the economic return is very dependent on brewery size, 
distance to animal herd etc. Therefore, there is an urgent need to develop alternative 
uses for nutrient rich BSG. Other studies, including implementation of BSG into human 
food, used homogenization processes such as milling, concluded that it could be benefi-
cial to improve acceptability into food products. The key technology that enabled this 
project was the Dacofi filter machine, creating two valuable fractions at a relatively low 
processing cost compared to other cost estimates of biomass drying. According to the 
compositional analysis of the two fractions obtained from the filtering, and in particular 
the filtrate with small particle sizes, there is a great potential for use of these in human 
food products such as bread, beer, soft drinks, protein snacks etc. The solid fraction ob-
tained from the filtration process (filter cake) was used for production of fermentation 
media by hydrolysing long chained polysaccharides into monomeric sugars arabinose, 
xylose, glucose and dimeric sugar cellobiose. The filter cake hydrolysate was successfully 
used for production of the high value protein bαLA in a GMO E. coli. The yields of fer-
mentable sugars obtained are remarkably lower than for other published research using 
BSG as substrate (not filtered). However, the hydrolysation and fermentation processes 
has not been subject to optimization, and as a proof of concept both parts are considered 
successfully, confirming that there is a potential for using the filter cake fraction of fil-
tered BSG for white biotechnology in a future perspective.

 5. Future Perspective 
In this project the conversion rate of polysaccharides, from the BSG filter cake fraction, 
into di- and monomeric sugars was very low compared to other similar research. A con-
version of approx. 30% of polysaccharides into simple sugars is not satisfying, but the 
aim for future research should not be to achieve an exceptionally high conversion rate, 
but rather do it as simple as possible in order to limit the expenses of the process. The 
process used in this project was neither very simple nor very cheap. In order to keep the 
process simple and realistic it will be relevant to consult a brewery production manager, 
also to make sure that the process that will be developed over time, will have potential to 
be tested in a brewery at some point.  
An extensive BSG composition study should include all nutrient data that could be rele-
vant, in order to substantiate the fact that BSG is of high nutritional value, and is worth 
using for other purposes than animal feed. From this point a decision should be made in 
regards to which nutrients that is present in high enough concentrations to have rele-
vance for an industrial refining/production. When this decision has been made, different 
batches of pilsner brews from larger breweries should be analysed in order to estimate 
the nutrient differences between batches. This nutrient data should be compared to hu-
man nutritional guidelines, or in best case, health claims. If a person is supposed to have 
a portion size of a BSG food product, that is unrealistic to consume, in order to achieve 
health beneficial effects of e.g. a certain vitamin, the project should not focus on this 
specific vitamin. The initial protein and PP analysis from this project is not sufficient to 
justify what the potentials of these food components are, but from this early point they 
still have potential and should be studied more intensively. In the future analysis of BSG 
protein, the focus must be just as much on the amino acid composition as on the total 
protein content. 
Future work with BSG as biomass source should always relate to the latest biorefinery 
technology and at the same time maintain the interest of breweries, middlemen, end cus-
tomers and consumers. Many studies focus on a certain endpoint (e.g. ethanol production 
or baked bread and cookies) and try to implement BSG into this product or process. By 
using the Dacofi filter machine in order to produce two fractions from BSG, and analyse 
these two fractions for what their potential outcomes might be, the limitations of frac-

tionation technology won’t backfire, because of industrial complicated fractionation proc-
esses.  
It is not sufficient only to have research groups representing great innovation in the field 
of BSG biorefining. The best way to maintain all internal stakeholders’ interests is to have 
an interdisciplinary work approach with inclusion and commitment of all collaborators. 
The collaboration between the Research group for Microbial Biotechnology and Biorefin-
ing, Skands Brewery, Dacofi and Powerbrands is a great example of exactly this. Having a 
research group linking these partners together and ensuring the appropriate funding is 
ideal. External stakeholders will alsoplay an important role, especially in regards to tech-
nology subsidies (governmental), health claims (European Food and Safety Agency) and 
competition (food producers, oil refineries etc.).  
Given the world population growth predictions, the future will call for new technology 
solutions that can ensure sustainability for the coming generations, and still let investors 
benefit economically. Besides better utilisation, such as biorefining, of widely available 
resources as BSG, a need for acceptance of GMO food products will come at some point if 
the population growth trend continuous. The fear of negative side effects with the use of 
GMO, must be measured against the positive outcomes of GMO. Products like bαLA, hav-
ing potential use in infants’ milk, may be unavoidable in the future.

Appendix 2 – Kjeldahl protocol 
Samples of BSG, BSG filter cake and filtrate were stored at -20oC prior to analysis. Liquid 
samples were thawed the day before analysis and stored at 5oC. 20g of BSG and pressed 
BSG samples were dried in a drying oven at 5-6hours at 60oC or until the sample seemed 
completely dried. Samples were then dried at 105oC for one hour and weighed at 0, 30 
and 60 min, to make sure no further weight loss was observed between 30 and 60 min. 
otherwise the sample would be dried for additional 30 min. until no weight loss would be 
observed. Samples were blended in a mini blender for 5 minutes and stored in a sealed 
tube prior to analysis. Liquid and solid sample were gently mixed in the tube before 
weighing approximately 1g of the solid samples and 3g of liquid samples. Solid samples 
were weighed on an electronic balance weight on nitrogen free weighing paper and 
placed in the bottom of a Kjeldahl tube. Liquid samples were pipetted directly into a 
Kjeldahl tube on a technical weight, carefully so that there would be no sample residue on 
the sides of the tube. Two Kjeldahl tabs were added to each tube and covered with 20mL 
sulfuric acid (96%). Two blind samples with weighing paper, Kjeldahl tabs and sulfuric 
acid and two tubes with only sulfuric acid and Kjeldahl tabs was used as references to 
test the weighing papers effect on the analytical results, and the molar concentration of 
the NaOH solution used for titration.  
 
The tubes were placed in the Foss Tecator 2020 digestor set to 440oC for 1.5 hours or 
until the liquid is clear green. When all samples are clear and green, the samples are 
heated for additionally half an hour. After cooling for 30 min. at RT, 20mL H2O were 
carefully added to each tube and mixed until homogenized. The Foss 2100 Kjeltec Distil-
lation Unit and the cooling water valve, was turned on 10 minutes before the first analy-
sis. For each sample, an Erlenmeyer flask was prepared with 40mL 0.1M HCl solution and 
5 drops of A.C. Andersen indicator. The Erlenmeyer receiving flask is placed with the out-
let from the distillation unit inside without touching the HCl solution. The Kjeldahl tube is 
placed with condensation hose. The distillation is started and runs for 5 minutes. One 
drop from the outlet was put on a piece of red litmus paper, to test if the distillation is 
complete. if the paper goes blue, the distillation must continue. The remaining HCl is ti-
trated with 0.1M NaOH until the color turns clear green. The volume of NaOH used for 
titration is used to calculate the amount of protein in the sample. The analysis result is 
actually the Nitrogen content of the sample. For foodstuffs a general N factor of 6.25 
were used.
