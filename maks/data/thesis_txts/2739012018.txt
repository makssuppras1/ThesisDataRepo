Summary
Recent research has effectively modelled human behaviour with transformer-based
models, which treat life-trajectories as sequences of events similar to sentences in
an artificial language.
This approach successfully captures complex relationships
between life-events, facilitating the exploration of socioeconomic and health-related
factors regarding life outcomes for Danish individuals.
In this thesis, we explore a new direction by incorporating relational information
from the Danish Population Network and leveraging advancements in Graph Repre-
sentation Learning.
Our primary contribution is a framework that allows modelling relational and sequen-
tial aspects of nationwide registries as one. We construct a nation-scale heterogeneous
social network with nodes representing Danish individuals, workplaces, educational
institutions and diagnoses. Individuals connect through family relationships or shared
neighbors, such as workplaces.
Inspired by Natural Language Processing advances, we develop the Heterogeneous
Graph Transformer Masked Auto-Encoder (HGTMAE), which uses a self-attention
mechanism to learn the importance of neighboring nodes, including family, occupa-
tional, educational and healthcare-oriented connections. The encoder learns struc-
tured life-embeddings of individuals, and to asses the meaningfulness of these embed-
dings, we introduce an early mortality prediction task.
We show that the life-embeddings created by HGTMAE beat state-of-the-art life-
embeddings in terms of early mortality prediction. We determine that the predictions
can be significantly improved by finetuning the encoder of HGTMAE, and here we
learn that age- and healthcare-related information are large predictors in terms of
early mortality prediction. Relations formed with family members do not seem to
significantly improve predictions.
In future work, we propose investigating how social relationships influence other life
outcomes, such as fertility, to understand the broader implications of our findings.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Psychohistory was the quintessence of sociology; it was the science of human behavior
reduced to mathematical equations. The individual human being is unpredictable, but the
reactions of human mobs, Seldon found, could be treated statistically.
- Isaac Asimov
There is a distinction between the abstract concept of human life and the complex
reality of human lives. We generally use the concept of human life to describe the
general notion of the human experience. In comparison, human lives describe a far
more complex collection of individual trajectories. Despite the perceived uniqueness
of each person, common patterns emerge globally, such as going to school, maintain-
ing employment, and creating families. Hence, we propose to further consider the
question, ”Can we leverage these similarities to derive meaningful representations of
individual lives and gain insights into the factors influencing life outcomes?”. This is
the ultimate objective of this thesis, to learn insightful representations of individuals
summarising life-trajectories as a collection of vector representations.
Achieving this objective is challenging and requires detailed digitized data reflect-
ing people and their experiences. Luckily, Denmark has anticipated the usefulness
of such records back in 1850 where Statistics Denmark can be traced back to [43].
It may have started as simple statistics, but it has gradually grown into one of the
most sophisticated data centers in the world, recording digital traces of an entire
population. It now records almost all footprints of Danes, from job developments to
family trees. They have agreed to give us rare access to most of their data.
Learning generic vector embeddings representing human lives does not originate
from us. It has previously been researched if such embeddings would aid in various
prediction tasks [34]. Savcisens et al. developed the model life2vec with this purpose.
Their idea was to represent individual lives as life-sequences containing life-events
similar to how sentences consist of a sequence of words [34].
Indeed, this was a
successful attempt at creating generic vector embeddings of individual lives.
One of the most ambitious psychological studies of our time, Harvard’s Study
of Adult Development, has shown that relationships matter in the development of
lives [65, 61, 30]. Life2vec is an extraordinarily clever way of solving the problem of
representing individual lives as vector embeddings. However, the model in its current
state does not allow for explicit modeling of relationships between people.
==PAGE:14==
2
1 Introduction
We present an alternate model formulation, another perspective of the problem,
which will allow for modeling individual life-sequences in addition to personal rela-
tionships. Imagine a nationwide social network of people connected through family
relations. In such a network, a person would correspond to a node, and a relation
would correspond to an edge between two nodes. In this thesis, we prefer the term
”graph” to ”network” and will primarily use the former.
This graph models the
relations between people. In graph theory, you would refer to this structure as a
”unipartite graph”.
We can extend this graph into what is referred to as a ”heterogeneous graph”
in literature. Here, a place of work or a school will also be represented as nodes,
and people connect to these nodes with timestamps of when the connection was
created, such as the date of hiring.
Consequently, this representation introduces
distinct node types, such as persons, workplaces, and schools. Furthermore, edges
between nodes represent different types of relations, such as child, parent, works at,
studies at, etc., with timestamps to preserve the chronological order of connections.
Thus, this approach maintains the life-sequences introduced by Savcisens et al. in
life2vec while incorporating social relationships between individuals.
The model presented in this thesis will include a large number of node and edge
types in order to explain and model the complexity which is human life. Chapter 2
will go into more detail about the graph constructs as well as the data sources used
in this thesis.
We refer to this final model formulation as ”Heterogeneous Graph Transformer
Masked Auto-Encoder” or using the acronym HGTMAE. We also present a static
version to prove that our temporal aspect works. The static version is called ”Static
Heterogeneous Graph Transformer Masked Auto-Encoder” or S-HGTMAE. Due to
the long names, we use the acronyms in the rest of the thesis.
Although this modeling approach appears sound, it remains uncertain whether
Harvard’s study can generalize to our population, the residents of Denmark, and
whether these results can be demonstrated quantitatively using a nationwide dataset.
To assess our proposed method, we propose several baseline models to investigate
whether social relations significantly influence individual life-trajectories. However,
quantitatively evaluating generic vector embeddings of human life is tricky. Therefore,
we introduce the prediction task, early mortality prediction, to assess the meaningful-
ness of our learned representations. In early mortality prediction, the problem is to
predict whether or not an individual dies prematurely within a given time frame. In
section 3, the modeling aspect of all baselines as well as HGTMAE and S-HGTMAE
will be explained in detail.

Motivation
In later years, artificial intelligence (AI) has advanced rapidly in many industries,
and it influences human life on a daily basis. Thus far, natural language processing

(NLP) and computer vision (CV) has arguably received the greatest focus in the
general public, partly due to positive reinforcement, where researchers flow toward
popular fields, and partly due to the ease of use caused by the minimal amount of
feature engineering required for deep learning models within these fields [14].
Last year, Matthias Fey et al. published a paper defining a new field of machine
learning, Relational Deep Learning [14]. They argue that deep learning on tabular
data falls behind deep learning on other data types because of the requirement of ex-
tensive feature engineering. Once the feature-engineering has been conducted, a flexi-
ble tree-based model can outperform most neural networks as one of the strengths in
neural networks is their ability to conduct end-to-end training with learnable feature
engineering. They further argue that they can remove feature engineering entirely for
tabular machine learning by viewing rows in data tables as nodes in a heterogeneous
graph. The edges are then the primary/foreign keys that relate rows across tables.
Each table will have a distinct node type and edges may also have different edge types
depending on which tables they connect. They fit a Graph Neural Network (GNN)
to the data with a specific prediction task in mind and receive performance on par
with or greater than models that have had extensive manual feature engineering by
domain experts.
This feat is not to be taken lightly. Further development in this field could result
in graphs being the data type of choice when predicting on tabular data. We could
also begin to see a shift toward heterogeneous graph representation learning in other
settings, and this is where this thesis begins to justify its existence.
As previously mentioned, we have been granted access to one of the finest datasets
on human development in the world by Statistics Denmark. Savcisens et al. have
already explored how this data can be used to learn representations through text em-
bedding techniques. We intend to limit the amount of required feature engineering
out of curiosity about the model’s performance with minimal manual adjustments.
We are particularly interested in how such a model compares to life2vec on early
mortality prediction and the creation of generic life-embeddings that effectively sum-
marize individual human lives. Lastly, the relational deep learning framework facili-
tates easy incorporation of social relationships due to the data being represented as
a heterogeneous graph, aligning with our objectives.
We hope that this thesis can provide the initial step towards using large hetero-
geneous graph models at Statistics Denmark for several modeling purposes.
As the baseline study will demonstrate, early mortality prediction might be an
unconventional prediction task, when the objective is to assess the significance of
social relationships in the learned representations. One might intuitively think that
predicting events such as childbirth would be more dependent on personal relations
than predicting mortality, and this intuition is likely accurate. However, the early
mortality prediction is chosen not because, we expect to get a higher performance
than life2vec, but because it eases model comparison. The framework that we use is
relatively new, and we need to verify its functionality. Moreover, due to the limited
research involving predictive models on this specific dataset, we selected one of the
two existing predictive tasks. Therefore, if we get comparable predictive power to that

1.2.1
A Brief History of Graphs
Graphs, or networks, are some of the most natural types of data in the world. It is
diﬀicult to think of anything within human behavior that cannot be modelled as a
graph. Although, we might have had an abstract concept of graphs back when the
first trading networks began arising in the world, the mathematical concept of graph
theory was first introduced a mere 300 years ago by Leonard Euler [2, 3, 27]. There
had, for many years, been a puzzle as to whether or not a path crossing all seven
bridges in Prussia’s capital, Köenigsberg, without crossing the same bridge twice
existed. Euler proposed viewing the islands as nodes and the bridges as connecting
edges. He then gave a mathematical proof as to why such a path could not exist, all
nodes had an odd number of edges [3].
Since then, graph theory has spread to a variety of domains, chemistry in 1878,
psychology in 1933, and many more [31, 57].
Now graphs are a well-known data type within research in many fields, and it
is especially useful when modeling data thought to be dependent on its relations to
other data points [3].
In order to avoid confusion, the term ”graph” refers to the mathematical construct
itself. It may take on many forms and be used in many settings. The term ”network”
refers to a specific problem [3]. In this thesis, we use the term graph as our models
may indeed be used for a large variety of networks - not just the ones examined in
this thesis.

1.2.2
Machine Learning on Graphs
Machine learning on graphs distinguishes itself from traditional machine learning by
allowing observations to be interdependent. Therefore, machine learning on graphs
often blur the boundaries of traditional machine learning [19]. Oftentimes, the term
”supervised learning” is exchanged with ”semi-supervised learning” as graph models
often will see parts of the test set during training due to the interdependency of
observations. Likewise, the term ”self-supervised” when dealing with graphs refers to
a model learning the graph structure without labeled data, almost like ”unsupervised
learning” in traditional machine learning [19].
This thesis will focus mainly on Graph Representation Learning (GRL), and as
we will see, the purpose of GRL is to learn vector representations of graph nodes or
subgraphs. This thesis will deal with the former. GRL can be divided into two areas;
shallow- and deep-embedding models.
While the shallow embedding models have
their merits, they simply are not viable for data of nationscale sizes [19]. Therefore,
moving forward we will explore deep-embedding models.
The first formulation of the now popular graph neural network (GNN) was made
by Scarselli and Gori in 2005 [17]. It was later generalised and published by the same
authors in 2008 [35]. They introduced a new way of aggregating nodes’ neighbourhood
information into a node’s embedding with the use of a, although later named, message
passing network. Through multiple layers, nodes would get information from other
relevant nodes in the graph. This also allowed these new GNNs to be inductive by
nature. You were, in other words, now able to simply add new nodes to the graph
and infer the node embeddings of these new nodes instead of retraining on the entire
graph. This idea is still the core in deep-embedding GRL models today.
From the first GNN, multiple other variations have derived.
By 2017, GNNs
were beginning to become very popular across machine learning applications. Bench-
marking on networks such as Cora, Citeseer, and Pubmed citation networks were
popularised by Kipf and Welling in 2016 and by 2019 GNNs had improved in both
performance on node classification tasks as well as performance on imbalanced data
[19, 26]. GNNs are now being developed to be more and more self-supervised in
contrast to early semi-supervised models. This change is important as labeled data
can be diﬀicult to acquire, and lessening the need for it can increase the number of
applications on which you can use GNNs.
With the rise of attention-based models in especially natural language modeling,
the ideas were quick to catch on to the GRL field. A notable contribution is Graph
Attention Network by Veličković et al. in 2017 [63]. This graph attention network
(GAT) has been further developed to account for heterogeneous graphs by Wang et
al. in 2019 [67]. The attention-based GNNs relied on the assumption that not all
connecting nodes are equally informative in a graph which is an assumption that
holds true in many domains including ours.

1 Introduction
1.2.3
GNNs on Heterogeneous Graphs
Since Heterogeneous Graph Attention Network (HAN) by Wang et al. in 2019, a
multitude of models on heterogeneous graphs have been proposed.
HAN uses hi-
erarchical attention to embed node-level as well as semantic-level information from
heterogeneous graphs into node-embeddings [67]. Metapath Aggregated Graph Neural
Network (MAGNN) by Fu et al. in 2020 introduced a model that encapsulates node
features as well as metapaths1 into the final node-embeddings [15].
The previously mentioned models share one key aspect:
they are both semi-
supervised.
Although, the data that we use in this thesis is well structured and
mostly labeled, it is important to us that this thesis allows for explorative research
where prediction of specific tasks might not be the main interest.
Therefore, we
primarily consider models that are self-supervised. A recent model that has shown
promising capabilities is HeCo by Wang et al. 2021 [68]. However, this model depends
on co-contrastive learning which requires the definition and sampling of negative re-
lations, thereby, demanding significant data manipulation [58]. This leads us to our
model of choice: HGMAE by Tian et al. from 2023 [58]. HGMAE, or Heterogeneous
Graph Masked Auto Encoder, uses masking of edges, node features, and positional
node information to learn generic embeddings of nodes in a self-supervised manner.
This model handles the self-supervised and heterogeneous aspect that we are looking
for, but it lacks a temporal understanding, relies on metapaths that have to be pre-
defined prior to modeling, and excludes the possibility of including direct relations
between people.
Among other things, this has lead us to use a Heterogeneous Graph Transformer
(HGT) as an encoder for our proposed modified HGMAE. HGT was proposed by Hu
et al. in 2020 and allows for temporal modeling without relying on metapaths [21].
Lastly, their proposed batching technique enables their model to scale up to large
graphs, such as the nation-scale network, which is the subject of this thesis [21].
1.2.4
Embeddings of Residents of Denmark
Creating embeddings of the residents of Denmark has previously been successfully
done by Savcisens et al. with their paper introducing life2vec; a BERT-like trans-
former trained on individual life-event-sequences [34]. The life2vec model employs a
sophisticated approach to interpreting individual life-sequences. It interprets these
sequences as sentences in a document and attempts to learn a representation that
effectively summarizes a person’s life-trajectory, and use these representations to pre-
dict future life-events.
This work demonstrates that the life2vec embeddings suﬀiciently capture informa-
tion reflecting individual trajectories that can be used to conduct accurate predictions.
However, it does not explicitly model the information that flows between individual
1Metapaths are paths that connect nodes through multiple hops.

life-sequences. Indeed, it has long been believed that relationships with friends, col-
leagues, and especially family substantially impact individuals’ lives [65, 61, 30]. By
modeling the problem from a graph representation learning perspective, one should
be able to capture how social relationships influence individuals.
1.2.5
Generality Versus Specificity
With the progress of compute allowing for manipulations of larger datasets than ever
before, a debate has arisen in academia. Can large and general models be finetuned
to perform better on prediction tasks compared to small but specific models? There
is prominent research showing that either case is true.
Andreas Bjerre-Nielsen et
al. show that when predicting academic performance, designing a small but specific
model outperforms a larger more general model [7]. In contrast, Savcisens et al. show
an increase in performance on multiple prediction tasks when finetuning a larger,
more general model with life2vec compared to smaller more specific models [34].
As artificial intelligence is beginning to find its place in society, this debate is in-
creasingly relevant, and we wish to acknowledge that there are alternatives to building
large models and model them on every data source that comes to mind. Therefore,
we will include a smaller auto-regressive model built specifically to predict early mor-
tality. We will compare the performances between this model and our final, finetuned
model. However, this will not overshadow the primary purpose of creating generic
life-embeddings.
1.3
Novelty of This Thesis
This thesis introduces, for the first time, deep learning on the relational registry
data in Denmark. By viewing the data as a large knowledge graph, we provide a
novel framework that integrates social relationships with individual life-sequences to
generate informative life-embeddings.
We model the framework with a Heteroge-
neous Graph Transformer Masked Auto-Encoder (HGTMAE) and are, to the best
of our knowledge, thereby the first ever to generate self-supervised HGMAE embed-
dings using a HGT encoder structure. We show that the generic life-embeddings,
produced by HGTMAE, capture early mortality better than previous state-of-the-art
life-embeddings. These contributions mark a significant advancement in life-sequence
modeling on Danish registry data and lay the groundwork for future refinement and
application of the HGTMAE framework.

2.1
Labour Market Account (AMRUN)
This section contains a description of all variables that we include from the AMRUN-
dataset [38]. The headings includes Statistics Denmark’s variable name as well as the
name that we give the same variable to better suit an English audience. After this
explanatory section, the variables are referenced by the names in parentheses. For a
compact summary of most of the variables, please see table 2.2.
2.1.1 DISCO-08 (jobtype)
Each job type is encoded with a DISCO-08 code which is the oﬀicial Danish extension
of the International Standard Classification of Occupants, ISCO-08 [24]. The Danish
code has been extended from ISCO-08’s original four layers to five layers to better
accommodate the Danish job market [45].
Since a person’s occupation may change over time, jobtypes are modelled as
nodes that people will connect to with different timestamps. The node features of
this nodetype will contain the higher levels of the DISCO-08 hierarchy as well as a
TF-IDF document embedding of the names to which the fifth layer DISCO-08 code
refers [55].
2.1.2 BREDT_LOEN_BELOEB (income_level)
This variable is the income of a person during a specific month, including all benefits
and ATP contribution [42].
We have chosen to sum the incomes of every month
in a year resulting in the accumulated yearly income per person. The income of a
person may change with time which is quite diﬀicult to represent using static node
features. Therefore, we have decided to keep the income from the last year (2015)
as person node features, while including the income level quantiles as nodes that a
person may connect to at different times during the period of modeling. A person
can then be connected to different quantiles with different timestamps. The features
of this nodetype will include an average of the bin that spans the quantile as well as
a length of the range; the minimum value subtracted from the maximum value of the
time span.
2.1.3 SOC_STATUS_KODE (soc_status)
Every person in Denmark falls under a socioeconomic category that intends to shed
light on how each person gets their income - be it by public welfare, student aid, or
other factors [54]. The soc_status is collected according to the ILO-rules which are a
set of rules that dictate how to prioritise aggregating data for individuals’ connections
to the job-market when ambiguities arise. For instance, the ILO-rules prescribe that
employment has a higher priority than unemployment while unemployment has a
==PAGE:23==
2.1 Labour Market Account (AMRUN)
11
higher priority than conditions outside of the working force. The code is 3 digits
long and it is built in a hierarchy where the first digit corresponds to a certain
higher level category of status. For example, if the first digit of a code is 1, most of
the subcategories will be of employment, whereas if the code starts with a 3-5, the
subcategories are different instances of people outside of the working force, such as
pensioners, cash assistance (kontanthjælp), and so forth [53].
We expect that socioeconomic status varies with time.
Hence, to account the
anticipated temporal aspect of soc_status, we represent it as nodes in the heteroge-
nous graph. However, in order to preserve feature information for the GNNs’ message
passing schemes, we include the soc_status from the final year (2015) as a person
node feature as well.
The soc_status nodes have the first digit of their code as node features to preserve
some sense of hierarchy between them.
2.1.4 TILSTAND_KODE_AMR (amr_status)
This code is another way of categorising the socioeconomic status of a person. In
some cases the amr_status will be more descriptive than that of the soc_status
while in other cases it will be less descriptive. There is no one-to-one correspondence
between the amr_status and the soc_status [51]. This is why we have decided to
include this variable in the data, given that it might provide different information
than soc_status will and vice versa.
Again, this variable is modelled as nodes due to the temporal dependency. And
again, the final year’s measurement is also kept as a person node feature.
2.1.5 ATP_BIDRAG_SATS_KODE (atp_bracket)
In Denmark, everyone above the age of 16 who is part of the working force will pay
a part of their salary to Labour Market Pension and Insurance (ATP). Normally,
employers will also pay into the pension according to the bracket and the salary of
the worker. The atp_bracket consists of 6 levels; A, B, C, D, E, and F [41]. Most
people will lie in the A bracket as this is customary but some people working in the
public sector will lie in other brackets and pay their contribution accordingly [64].
As this bracket is dependent on the type of job as well as the place of work, the
atp_bracket is modelled as nodes due to its temporal dependency.
2.1.6 ATP_BELOEB (atp_level)
The atp_level is the amount of money a person and their employer pays to ATP
on a yearly basis. Ordinarily, the employee will pay 1/3 of the atp_level while the
employer pays the rest. This is often measured as part of a person’s salary and it
is indeed included in the income_level variable [37]. See subsection 2.1.5 for more
information about ATP.

12

A person may change atp_level throughout the period of study.
Therefore,
atp_level is quantized and modelled as node types in the graphical data representa-
tions. The mean of each bin as well as the length of them are included as atp_level
node features. See figure 2.2 for a compact view of the features and their sources.
Additionally, the final year’s measured atp_level is also kept as a person node

2.1.7 TILSTAND_LAENGDE_AAR (labour_force_interval)
The labour_force_interval is a proportion of how many days a year all other sta-
tuses are true [52]. It should be 1 if people do not change soc_status or amr_status
throughout the year.
However, a lower number tells us that a person frequently
changes their labour status within the given year. As this proportion is expected to
change with time, we model labour_force_interval as nodes. We also model the
final year’s measurement of this variable as person node features.
2.1.8 KOEN (sex)
The sex of a person is recorded according to that person’s CPR-number’s 10th digit.
If it is an even number, the person is considered a female while an odd number will
result in a person being considered male [47]. Given the criteria when selecting the
cohort to move forward with, the sex of a person will be constant throughout the
period of survey. Therefore, there is no need to model sex as nodes; they are included
as person node features.
2.1.9 FOED_DAG (birthday)
The birthday of a person is given as the number of days since January 1st 1960. A
person’s birthday remains the same throughout the survey period. Therefore, the
variable is modelled as a person node feature. Birthdays are transformed to being
the number of days since January 1st 1940.
2.1.10 OPR_LAND (country_of_origin)
The country_of_origin of a Danish resident is determined by a set of rules. When
none of the parents are known, the country_of_origin is defined by a person’s other
data; if the person is a migrant, it is assumed that the country_of_origin is equal
to the country of birth. If the person is a descendant, the country_of_origin is
assumed to be the country of the person’s citizenship.
When only one parent is
known, the country_of_origin is defined by the country of birth. If the country of
birth is Denmark, the country of citizenship is used. When both parents are known,
the country_of_origin is defined by the mother’s country of birth. If this country

that no longer exist may appear on older residents. For instance, it is possible to have
a country_of_origin which is the USSR. Furthermore, some country_of_origin
codes refer to areas not formally recognised as independent countries by the Danish
state such as Palestine, Gaza, and East Jerusalem [49]. The country_of_origin
remains constant and is therefore represented as a person node feature.
2.1.11 ARB_HOVED_BRA_DB07 (business_type)
The business_type stems from the business registry at Statistics Denmark. It de-

workplace nodes will be introduced later.
2.1.12 ARB_SEKTORKODE (work_sector)
Like business_type, this variable describes what sector of work, an individual is in

to a place of work, we model this variable as a feature for workplace nodes.

2.2.1 C_ADIAG (diagnosis)
ADIAG is an acronym for ”Action Diagnosis” which is the diagnosis that a person gets
when they interact with the Danish healthcare system. The Danish doctors record
patient interactions using action diagnoses in the ”Hospital System’s Classification
System” [1]. Hospitals will then report medical data to LPR [36]. It is encoded with
ICD-10 codes, the international disease encoding maintained by the World Health
Organization [72].
A person can have multiple diagnoses during their life, thus each diagnosis is
modelled as a node. A diagnosis has a name according to WHO’s ICD-10 codes [72],
and the TF-IDF document embedding of their name is included as features for the
diagnosis nodes. Furthermore, the higher levels of the hierarchy are also included
as node features. Please note that the ICD-10 codes include many versions made
throughout the period of study, all of which can be found at their website. However,

Finally, a piece of research regarding the heritability of diseases have been made

2.3
Danish Population Network (DPN)
The Danish Population Network (DPN) is a collection of networks with different layers
that connects people through different types of links. There is a yearly granularity for

2.3 Danish Population Network (DPN)

all layers and every layer consists of people who have been present in the population
for at least one day in the period from January 1st 2008 to December 31st 2021. Links
may disappear if people die or move abroad and are no longer recorded in the registry
data [6].
In the following, the definition of each layer will be explained as well as how the
layer has been derived.
2.3.1
Family Layer
The family layer consists of first, second and third degree family relations. First de-
gree family relations are your core family - parents, siblings and children. The second
degree family relations are your grandparents, aunts and uncles. Lastly, the third
degree relations are cousins. It includes both genetic and adoptive family, however,
relations via only partnership or marriage are excluded. If, however, they have chil-
dren together, a co-parent relation will appear. The family relations are constructed
from the Danish Population Registry which includes all individuals alive and living in
Denmark from 1968 onward. This has some limitations regarding the completeness of
the graph for older individuals [6]. However, due to the nature of our chosen cohort,
we expect that these will be of little concern.
The network is constructed by having a large graph containing all relations from
2008 to 2021. Subsequently, the yearly network is built by allowing only relations
between people who are alive during that specific year. This unfortunately means that
it is possible for people to have co-parent relations with each other prior to receiving
their first child. This is not ideal for our graphs, as we view this as future information
leaking into the graphs.
To accommodate for this issue, Statistics Denmark has
updated the graphs with relationship start dates based on the birth date of individuals.
This allowed us to filter the relations and ensure we only get those who start before
or during 2015. Furthermore, these relational start dates also allow us to get accurate
timestamp information for our temporal graph, which can be crucial for the modeling
of temporal dependencies.
2.3.2
Bipartite Household Layer
The household layer is a bipartite graph with one partition being people and the
other being households. The graph is constructed by recording all Danish residents’
addresses on the last day of the year [6].
Because of the yearly granularity, it is impossible to record if a person moves more
than once within a year, which implies that the network may lack some household
relations [6].
However, we do not believe that this is a substantial problem.
We
have decided to represent households as nodes in the heterogeneous graph, because
people may change addresses during the survey period (2008-2015). This will enable
our model to catch the temporality in people’s addresses. As node features for the
households, we include the EPSG:23032 coordinates of the entrance to the house -

16

the front door - and the corresponding municipality code [10]. The coordinates and
the municipality code will often convey the same information. However, the code
could provide additional information for municipalities such as Copenhagen where
Bornholm is part of the municipality.
2.3.3
Bipartite Work Layer
The work layer is a bipartite graph with one partition being people and the other
being workplace IDs.
As people may change jobs throughout the period of sur-
vey, the workplace is modelled as nodes.
We include ARB_HOVED_BRA_DB07 and

One flaw in the work layer is that the workplace IDs may change over time. There-

during one year are colleagues [6]. This may introduce random noise into the models,
depending on how many workplaces change IDs throughout our survey period.
2.3.4
Multipartite School Layer
The bipartite school layer includes three columns of interest; a person ID, an institu-
tion number and an education program ID. We interpret a scholarly institution and
an educational program as two distinct node types and we model them thereafter.
As the specific institution and the education do not have any accessible features
within the considered registries, we draw an array with 64 dimensions from a normal
distribution and include these as features for the nodes.

3.1
Graph Representation Learning
Graph Representation Learning is the process of learning a vector representation of
nodes in a graph based on node- and neighborhood node features. The vector repre-
sentation can then be used in downstream tasks such as classification or clustering.
3.1.1
Defining Graphs
A graph is the term that some mathematicians use to refer to networks of any type
- for instance social networks, biochemical networks, etc. A network will often refer
to the specific real world problem formulations while the term ”graph” is generally
used as a mathematical construct [3]. The term ”graph” is mostly used in Graph
Representation Learning, probably to avoid confusion with networks as in Neural
Networks [19]. Hence, this is how we reference our graphical data representations.
However, in reality we talk about specific network constellations and not arbitrary
graphs, when we progress through this thesis. Yet this specific section (3.1) introduces
Graph Representation Learning as a field of study, and it deals with arbitrary graph
constructs that may be applied to a wide array of networks.

3.1.2
Heterogeneous Graphs
Now consider F, the simple family graph introduced in section 2.5.1. In this graph,

3.1.3
Graph Neural Networks
There exist a lot of different models that aim to learn a vector representation of
each node while keeping information about the graph structure as well as node and
neighborhood features - if there are any. Most models fit into either of two categories,
shallow or deep embedding models [19].
In this thesis, we will deal only with the latter, as the former introduces some
severe constraints in terms of production capabilities as well as some scaling issues
[19].
A Graph Neural Network is, as the name suggests, a deep embedding model. The
model takes as input a graph, denoted by G, along with node features, denoted by X,
and outputs vector representations, denoted by Z, which are often of low dimension.
The resulting vector is then represented in R|V|×n and subsequently decoded from Z
to a graph G∗. If G ≈G∗, it implies that a node’s neighborhood can be reconstructed
from the vector embeddings, Z.
Unlike other Neural Networks, the GNN uses a message passing scheme to allow
dependent observations to influence the embeddings of each other.
The message
passing consists of two functions; an aggregation function and an update function
[19].

3.3
Neural Encoding and Decoding Architecture
This section will present the encoder-decoder structure of our model. The encoder
and decoder are essentially Heterogeneous Graph Transformers (HGTs) with a few
modifications to account for the graph structure that we have used [21]. HGMAE
uses a Heterogeneous Graph Attention Network (HAN), however, this encoder does
not suit the dynamic nature of our graph [67, 58].
Our encoder contains n layers where n is a hyperparameter to be tuned. However,
the decoder follows the procedure of HGMAE and GraphMAE by containing only
one layer [58, 20]. Each layer contains three key mechanisms, which are a Heteroge-
neous Attention Mechanism, a Heterogeneous Message Passing and a Heterogeneous
Aggregation. For the temporal graph, see data-section 2.5.6, each layer in the en-
coder also includes a Relative Temporal Encoding. The static model is referred to as
S-HGTMAE and the temporal model is named HGTMAE. The subsequent sections
will explain in detail how each mechanism works and why it is applied.

3.4
Batching Procedure used for HGTMAE and
S-HGTMAE
The largest graphs in this thesis contain 6 million nodes and 163 million edges (see
tables 2.3 and 2.4). It is unreasonable to suggest that the parameters of the models
as well as the nodes and edges can be held in the 32 GB memory allowed by our
accessible GPU. Therefore, we have to batch the graph by sampling it.
Contrary to batching other data types, batching graphs can be quite tricky as
almost everything is well connected and dependent on everything else [16]. Whenever
we subset the graph, it is almost certain that we will remove relations between nodes
that are in our subset and nodes that are not. Furthermore, because of heterogeneity
in the graph, the difference in degree distributions of distinct node type can make the
batches unrepresentative of the population. We adapt the same walk-based batching
strategy as was introduced by Z. Hu et al., however, with our own modifications
[21].
Please note that this batching strategy is only relevant for HGTMAE and
S-HGTMAE as they allow high-order relations. The baseline models only rely on
first-order relations where no sampling is needed.

sample nτ nodes as initial nodes - see table 3.1.

3.5
Ablation Study
The ablation study builds the proposed model step by step. This study includes eight
baseline models, each serving a specific purpose in building the model incrementally.
Furthermore, to get the best performing models, we include a hyperparameter tuning
scheme for our model propositions. In the following subsections, we will explain how
each baseline model in the ablation study is built.
3.5.1
RF: Raw Feature-Based Prediction
The first baseline, RF, focuses on predicting outcomes using the raw aggregated
features from ARMRUN and LPR registers without employing sophisticated embedding
techniques to assess the predictive power of traditional tabular machine learning
approaches.
We refer to section 2.4.1 for more detailed information on the data
preparation. Initially, we employ logistic regression as the classification model on the
aggregated raw features. Subsequently, we extend the baseline model by including
a message passing scheme incorporating family relations turning the model into a
simple GNN referred to as Family Raw Features (FRF). We describe the message
passing scheme of the later prediction model in further detail in section 3.5.3.
3.5.2
L2V: Life2vec Embeddings
To compare our model with existing methods, we use the embeddings from life2vec as
the input features for several baseline models. Savcisens et al. propose to represent
individual lives as sequences of events based on the Danish national registry data
explained in section 2, and encode the sequences as natural language using transformer
architectures.
In life2vec, each discrete and discretized continuous feature constitute the vocabu-
lary, and with temporal information, they represent individual life-events as sentences,
e.g., ”In March 2008, Anakin earned 10 thousand Danish Kroner as part of his Jedi
training on Coruscant” or ”In February 2010, Anakin was hospitalized as an in-patient
due to an amputated arm” as shown in figure 3.4. Each life-event or sentence is repre-
sented by several concept tokens, with the number of tokens depending on the event
type. Health-related life-events use two to three tokens, while labour-related events
use three to seven. Hence, Savcisens et al. represent a life-trajectory as a sequence of
these concept tokens. Furthermore, they position a background sentence describing
consistent features such as origin, birth year, and gender at the beginning of each se-
quence. The maximum sequence length is 2560 concept tokens. If a sequence exceeds
this limit, they remove the earliest events while keeping the background sentence. If
the sequence is below this limit, they add padding until it reaches the limit [34].
The life2vec embeddings are learned through two stages, pre-training and fine-
tuning. Within this work, we only use the results from the pre-training stage. Specif-
ically, the initial stage consists of a Masked Language modeling (MLM) and Sequence

3.6.2
Positive Unlabeled Learning
Formulating the mortality prediction task as a binary classification problem with
a fixed window size introduces a specific challenge that should be addressed. One
significant issue is that Statistics Denmark might not have accurate data on whether
an individual is alive if that person has emigrated to a different country before dying.
Consequently, it is a possibility that the negative set contains positive observations.
This challenge is similar to a common issue in survival analysis within clinical trials
known as censoring, where trial subjects drop out of the study before the follow-up
appointment independent of the survival outcome [12]. More specifically, this scenario
can be described as right-censoring, given that we only know the true survival time
will be greater than or equal to the time of emigration [29].
To address this issue, we adopt the Asymmetric Cross-Entropy loss (ACE) recently
proposed in the Positive Unlabeled (PU) learning research [66]. PU learning deals
with binary classification where the objective is to discriminate between positive and
unlabeled samples, where the unlabeled set is assumed mixed with positive and neg-
ative samples, which closely resembles the nature of our situation, where emigrants’
survival status is unknown. The ACE loss is designed to treat the uncertainty with
respect to the negative samples by treating the prediction errors of the positive and
negative samples asymmetrically:

3.6.5
Performance Metrics
Previous work has demonstrated that traditional performance metrics can be mis-
leading and imprecise when applied to a PU-learning problem [32]. This finding is
relevant for the mortality prediction task because the negative set includes unknown
positive observations, e.g., caused by emigration.
To accommodate for this issue,
we adopt the same performance metrics as presented in the work by Savcisens et al.
Specifically, we use the Area-Under-the-Lift (AUL), Corrected Matthews Correlation
Coeﬀicient (C-MCC), corrected balanced accuracy and corrected F1-score [34]. The
performance metrics will be explained in the following subsections.
3.6.5.1
Optimization Metrics
The lift curve used to estimate the AUL deviates from the Receiver Operating Char-
acteristic (ROC) curve by representing the True Positive Rate (TPR) as a function
of the proportion of observations with a normalized score g(.) larger than threshold
s rather than the False Negative Rate (FNR) for varying thresholds.
Hence, the
lift curve illustrates the relationship between the TPR and the proportion of all ob-
servations with a score greater than the threshold, including negative and positive
observations [60]. Furthermore, the relationship between Area-Under the ROC Curve
(AUC) and the AUL is given by:
AUC −AUL = p(AUC −0.5),
(3.61)
where p is the probability of a positive outcome in the population. Thus, when p
is small, as in our mortality prediction task, the difference between AUC and AUL
==PAGE:73==
3.6 Early Mortality Prediction
61
is expected to be small [60]. Furthermore, the relationship between AUC and AUL
shown in equation 3.61 implies that AUL can be used for model comparison. However,
an issue with the AUL is that it relies on the ground truth TPR, which is typically
unknown in a PU setting. Although, Jiang et al. show that the observed TPR in a
PU-setting is an asymptotic unbiased estimate of the true TPR, which implies that
when the number of observations n increases, the difference between the two values
will become negligible [25]. Consequently, one can reasonably approximate the AUL
without prior knowledge regarding the proportion of unknown positive labels in the
unlabeled set when n is large, making it a suitable choice in PU-learning problems
for model comparison and selection [34].
In this thesis, the AUL serves multiple purposes. Specifically, it is used for early
stopping during model training. Furthermore, it is used to select the value of the
constant c in the asymmetric cross entropy loss, explained in section 3.6.2, during
hyperparameter optimization.

Specifically, the bootstrapping procedure

3.6.6
Performance Evaluation
For the early mortality prediction task, we define the problem as a node classification
setting. Within the graph representation learning (GRL) community, the standard
training procedure for this setting is commonly referred to as semi-supervised learn-
ing. However, adopting standard machine learning terminology on graphs can be
challenging. Notably, the semi-supervised learning for node classification differs from
the conventional formulation of semi-supervised learning in fields such as NLP.
Our approach involves pre-defining the splits of training, validation and test node
indices stratified according to early mortality:
Vtrain,
Vval,
Vtest,
(3.67)
In our approach, Vval and Vtest consist of transductive nodes. Hence, the validation
and test nodes are included in the training phase of the message passing scheme but
remain unlabeled concerning early mortality, thereby not contributing to the loss
computation. The usage of transductive nodes allows for the exchange of messages
between nodes in Vtrain, Vval and Vtest. Because only the labels of the training nodes
are used in the computation of the loss function, but the ability to exchange messages
between sets exists, the GRL community typically refers to this approach as semi-
supervised learning [19].

3.7.1
Early Stopping
To prevent overfitting our models, we implement an early stopping mechanism to
determine the most promising end of training. The general approach adopts a pre-
defined warm-up period, where early stopping is not triggered. After the warm-up
period, the best validation performance is continuously monitored. If the model fails
to improve the best-monitored validation performance by a certain margin within a
specified patience period, training is ended. The following are the three parameters
for the early stopping mechanism:
warm-up ⇒ewarm
(3.68a)
minimum validation improvement ⇒δmin
(3.68b)
patience ⇒epat
(3.68c)
We adopt this early stopping scheme for all our baseline models, HGTMAE, and
S-HGTMAE model to determine the duration of training. For the baseline models,
the monitored validation metric is the AUL, and due to the precision required for
this metric, we define the margin for validation improvement to δmin = 0.0001. The
warm-up period is set to 25 epochs, ewarm = 25, and the patience period to 5 epochs,
epat = 5. Hence, all baseline models train for an initial 25 epochs. Subsequently, if the
AUL does not increase by more than δmin in a consecutive 5 epochs, the training ends.
Consequently, training will proceed as long as the observed metric Mobs satisfies:
Mobs > M ∗+ δmin,
(3.69)
where M ∗is the currently best-observed validation metric. During early mortality
prediction with Frozen-S-HGTMAE, Frozen-HGTMAE, and HGTMAE we use the
same early stopping parameters as for the baselines to create a fair comparison.
For pretraining the HGTMAE and S-HGTMAE models, we have decided to in-
crease the warm-up period to ewarm = 30 due to the significant increase in the
number of parameters. Furthermore, we set the patience period to epat = 10. We
now monitor the total validation loss as the validation metric, as defined in equation
3.25. The condition for early stopping to terminate the training procedure is that the
loss should not decrease by more than δmin = 0.001 within the patience period epat.
Thus, the model continues training as long as the observed loss Lobs satisfies:
Lobs < Lbest −δmin,
(3.70)
where Lbest is the best observed validation loss.
3.7.2
Hyperparameter Tuning
Before model training, we use Optuna, a standard hyperparameter optimization frame-
work, to determine a promising hyperparameter configuration for our models.
==PAGE:77==

For the early mortality task, we use the asymmetric cross-entropy loss function
as explained in section 3.6.2. This loss function includes the constant c ≥0, which
shifts the log loss for the unlabeled samples with a fixed bias. We determine the
value of c by optimizing the validation AUL. Specifically, each model is trained for
five epochs, and the last obtained validation AUL is used as the objective function.
We use Optuna with the Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) algorithm to deter-
mine the best-performing value of c with respect to the AUL. The search space for c
is defined as c ∈[0, 1], and we use ten trials to search the hyperparameter space.
TPE samples the hyperparameters independently by constructing two Gaussian
Mixture Models (GMM) per hyperparemeter, l(x) and g(x).
l(x) is fitted to the
hyperparameters values associated with the best observed objective values, while
g(x) is fitted to the remaining hyperparameter values. Subsequently, TPE selects the
candidate value x by maximising the ratio between the two GMMs:
l(x)
g(x),
(3.71)
This biases the models towards selecting values in regions that have shown promis-
ing results [4]. The intuitive explanation is that maximizing equation 3.71 can be
achieved by choosing x with a high probability density under l(x) or a low probabil-
ity density under g(x).
For the HGTMAE model, the following hyperparameters have been selected for
optimization:
• Number of layers in the encoder block.
• Number of attention heads in the attention mechanism.
• Hidden dimension of linear layers.
Due to the extensive time required to run the large HGTMAE model for 8 epochs,
we limit the initial hyperparameter optimization process to 5 trials. The models are
trained for 8 epochs, 5 with an active dynamic TAR masking rate, and 3 with a fixed
TAR masking rate. The last observed total validation loss is used as the objective
value. As for the asymmetric cross-entropy loss, we use the Optuna framework with
the TPE algorithm for the optimization procedure.

features. This procedure is repeated 10 times with different seeds to produce 95%
confidence intervals.

(NMI) and Adjusted Rand Index (ARI), which are used to compare the cluster allo-

and S-HGTMAE embeddings with respect to the ground truth categories [28, 33, 59].

in the embedding plots.
It is important to emphasise that applying K-Means clustering might not be the
optimal evaluation method for these embeddings due to the multi-dimensional nature
of our data. Individuals who are similar with respect to one attribute might differ
notably on another attribute causing the individuals to be positioned differently in
the embedding space. For instance, as demonstrated in figure 5.6, we observe several
sex based clusters that are separated by other attributes, e.g. SOC status, even
though the local proximity still reflects sex. While an agglomerative clustering algo-
rithm could have been a more appropriate choice, time and computational memory
constraints limited our choice to K-means.

6.1
Preliminary Analysis
The preliminary analysis provides a window into the process of this thesis. We show
that the person embeddings of HGTMAE on a random municipality illustrate well-
defined structures concerning demographic and socioeconomic attributes before we
scale the network nationwide. Furthermore, during the training phase, we encoun-
tered generalization issues related to the Positional Feature Prediction (PFP) loss
function. Notably, the model never learns to reconstruct the positional encodings
produced by node2vec on our validation set.
Our analysis shows that while the
node2vec embeddings exhibit positional structure within individual batches, this struc-
ture vanishes across different batches. The primary cause of this issue is our proposed
sampling strategy. Specifically, nodes can appear in multiple batches, leading to vari-
ations in a node’s role in the network across different batches, depending on the
sampled relations in each subgraph. As a result, the same node can receive different
positional encodings. While the model can learn this batch-specific information, as
shown by the training loss, it can not generalise due to the lack of consistent patterns.
Although this limitation does not seem to degrade the quality of the embeddings
substantially, we ultimately decided not to include the PFP loss in the model formu-
lation for the nation-scale network. The primary motivation behind this decision was
time constraints. We did not have the time to improve the formulation of the PFP
learning strategy, and there was no time to run the model multiple times on the large
dataset.

Relational Data in Early Mortality Prediction
The ablation study shows that the task of early mortality prediction heavily relies

own life sequence representation is the most important component for predicting
your death. With the FL2V model, we show that when the model cannot distinguish
between information originating from the individual itself and neighbor information,
the performance in terms of early mortality prediction decreases. However, we observe
that information coming from workplace nodes has a notable contribution towards

a higher likelihood of dying. This contribution is likely significant because a subset
of individuals with a high predicted probability of dying are either retired or on leave

performance gain between L2V and FL2VA may not be significant, we see that grad-

of parameters remains constant for the graph-based baseline models, we are confident
that the improvement is due to the availability of more information for the model to

HGTMAE is our proposed method for incorporating relational data into generic life-

The embeddings of individuals in our cohort produced by the HGTMAE encoder
primarily exhibit well-defined structures concerning the AMRUN variables, except for
one cluster in the top-right corner of the projected embedding space. As previously
described in our results section, this cluster mainly consists of younger individuals
with variability in salary (Figure 5.6d) and that have the work status students/y-
ouths, employed, or individuals on parental or sick leave (Figure 5.7b). One potential
explanation for this lack of structure could be the server outage that ended our train-
ing prematurely before the early stopping mechanism could terminate the training
procedure. This specific cluster might require additional training, because it is more
challenging to represent due to larger inconsistencies in the AMRUN registry for younger
individuals in our cohort. Figure A.15 illustrates the average node in-degree for per-
son nodes as a function of age throughout our survey period. The illustration shows
that the average node degree consistently decreases from age 25 to 45 and remains
approximately constant afterwards. This result suggests two possible explanations
for the lack of a well-defined structure.
The younger part of the cohort possesses a higher node degree because they more
rapidly change their labour and residential status. Hence, it could be that the younger
part of our cohort possesses a higher node degree, because they experience more fre-
quent and drastic life changes than the older part. The difference in temporal reso-
lution or frequency of life changes between distinct age groups could be the primary
issue because the rapid changes in the lives of younger people challenge the learning
process of a single well-structured embedding. Another possible explanation could be
that the high number of neighbours among younger individuals causes the updated
node embeddings to regress towards a homogeneous representation, also known as
over-smoothing [19]. This phenomenon might occur because we initially decided to
weight the residual connection with a parameter, (1 −α), shared across all age inter-
vals, which might have been too restrictive to account for the degree heterogeneity
which correlates with age, as seen in figure A.15.
Despite this specific cluster, the embeddings demonstrate a clear separation be-
tween people regarding variables included as person node features, such as income
level, birthday, AMR, and SOC status. The embeddings also demonstrate separa-
tion, though not to the same extent, for variables obtained through connections to
other nodes such as workplace or jobtype nodes. The TAR loss component possibly
causes this notable difference in separation because it is easier to minimize than the
MER loss, as shown in figure 5.4.
Consequently, the model learns to reconstruct
masked node features more effectively than masked relations, which implies that the

processes generating health information in LPR might depend on age. For newborns
and older adults, the variety of possible health-oriented events may be smaller than
the variance in our cohort spanning the ages 25 to 65. Consequently, it might be less
challenging to model the structure of health patterns at the early and later stages of
life. This potential significant variability of health compared to labour-related life-

6.4
Using Relational Life-Embeddings to Predict Early

To further investigate the usefulness of the HGTMAE model and the embeddings
for person nodes, we apply the same logistic regression formulation used with the
life2vec embeddings for the early mortality prediction task. Our results show that the
predictive power of the HGTMAE embeddings for early mortality surpasses that of
life2vec, as illustrated by the significantly higher performance across most evaluation
metrics. Furthermore, we demonstrate that the simple logistic regression on these
embeddings perform on par with RF and FRFA, which we have feature-engineered
for predicting early mortality. Moreover, the early mortality finetuned HGTMAE signif-
icantly outperforms all proposed baseline models. However, we want to emphasize
that the substantial addition of parameters complicates a fair direct performance
comparison with the baseline models.
In the previous work of life2vec, significantly higher performance was achieved
concerning the MCC and F1-score. However, it is important to mention that they
used a different cohort of individuals between the ages of 35 and 65, whereas our
cohort includes individuals between 25 and 65. By inspecting the behavior of our
final proposed model HGTMAE, we observe that age is a major contributing factor
to our model’s predicted probabilities. This dependency is expected given that the
number of death occurrences consistently increases with age in Denmark, as shown
in appendix A.7. However, our results demonstrate that the model tends to overly
rely on this attribute, leading to a severe over-classification of death in the older seg-
ment of our cohort. This indicates that including a broader age span in our cohort
has forced the model to depend on the general pattern of age evolution to some ex-
tent naively. It would be informative to follow the approach of Savcisens et al. and
narrow the age span of our cohort to condition on age, thereby removing this factor
to some extent and observing how it might change our model’s behavior [34]. We
choose the broader age range to increase the number of relations between individuals
in our graph, as both individuals need to be within the cohort for the relation to be
present in the graph. However, a different approach could include all the individuals
as we have done but exclude individuals outside the 35-65 age span from contribut-
ing to the early mortality finetuning loss function. This would allow the model to
incorporate information from individuals outside the narrow age span by passing mes-
sages, without classifying their life outcome. Thus, this approach would correspond
to conditioning on age, specifically for early mortality classification, while enabling
information sharing between relatives and other connected individuals.

6.5
Connecting Individuals in Danish Registries
In the literature review, we mention the ongoing debate regarding whether small, spe-
cific models outperform large, general models on specific prediction tasks. Our study
contributes to this debate by demonstrating that incorporating attention-weighted
relational information between person nodes in models such as FRFA, thereby in-
creasing the model size, performs as well as, if not better than, the smaller, specific
RF model.
Furthermore, our findings show that a massively fine-tuned HGTMAE signifi-
cantly outperforms the small, specific RF model on early mortality prediction. While
the authors behind life2vec, Savcisens et al., do not explicitly address the debate be-
tween small specific or large and general models, their work illustrates that fine-tuning
a large pre-trained transformer model can surpass state-of-the-art results in multiple
downstream tasks [34]. Furthermore, Fey et al. argue that progress in deep learn-
ing for typical tabular data prediction tasks, such as survival analysis, has stagnated
due to the lack of complete end-to-end training approaches similar to those seen in
computer vision or NLP. Specifically, Fey et al. draw an analogy to the recent sig-
nificant advancements in computer vision and NLP. Computer vision experienced a
performance shift when approaches moved from using hand-engineered convolutional
filters to learnable filters, and NLP experienced a similar change when moving from
feature-engineered word representations to context-learned representations. Fey et al.
propose that formulating tabular prediction tasks as relational problems can address
this issue by leveraging recent GNN architectures, similar to our proposed HGTMAE,
to learn aggregations or feature-engineering effectively. They argue that database
schemas inherently use primary and foreign keys to connect rows across different
==PAGE:120==
108
6 Discussion
schemas, and can therefore naturally be represented as networks, where each row in
a schema corresponds to nodes in a graph while columns correspond to node features.
Taking this one step further, Savcisens et al. proposes to represent registry data from
Statistics Denmark as life-event sequences, where each row in a schema corresponds to
a life-event represented by tokens in a sequence, thus creating artificial sentences that
can be processed by standard transformer architectures. The transformer attention
mechanism can be interpreted as a fully connected graph where a node or token sends
a query, every other token sends a key and value, and an attention/importance weight
is learned based on the product of the query and key vector. The representation is
then updated based on an attention-weighted sum of the received values. As previ-
ously mentioned, the documents created in life2vec consist of rows from two primary
registry datasets, AMRUN and LPR. Hence, the weighted sum essentially corresponds
to a nearly end-to-end learnable aggregation of rows from different schemas, aligning
closely with the relational deep learning approach proposed by Fey et al. The key
difference is that Savcisens et al. orchestrates documents using a person identifier as
the relation between rows and Fey et al. propose modeling the schemas directly as a
heterogeneous graph, replacing the initial document construction step with a graph
construction.
Our approach with HGTMAE is inspired by the latter methodology because it
allowed for the intuitive incorporation of relations between individuals in the reg-
istries, when the data already is represented as a heterogeneous graph. However, it
is important to note that formulating a small, specific model capable of predicting
early mortality with extremely high accuracy was never the primary goal of our thesis.
Given more time, it is possible that a better model formulation than our simple auto-
regressive RF could have been developed. Similarly, achieving high performance in
early mortality prediction was not the original intent of HGTMAE either.
This debate also encompasses a broader discussion. It must be evaluated whether
the increase in performance justifies the associated increase in power usage, training
time, and the amount of personal information required. In the case of our prediction
task, which will not be deployed in production due to ethical concerns, the perfor-
mance gains may not be justified unless there is a scientific purpose necessitating high
performance in early mortality prediction. In conclusion, while our results support
the notion that large, general models can be effectively fine-tuned for specific tasks,
it is essential to consider the broader implications of model complexity, resource
consumption, and data privacy in the context of their intended application.
6.6
Limitations and Future Work
As mentioned in our method section, we subsample the graph into smaller batches
due to the large scale of our data and memory constraints on the available GPU.
However, subsampling graphs is nontrivial and can introduce severe biases for several
reasons, such as removal of relations. To mitigate this issue, we have constructed
==PAGE:121==
6.6 Limitations and Future Work
109
each subgraph to maximize the GPU memory usage by ensuring that the sampled
graphs are as large as the GPU can handle.
However, in our formulation of the
MER reconstruction task for HGTMAE and S-HGTMAE, we reconstruct a dense
N × N subsampled adjacency matrix, where N is the total number of sampled nodes.
This operation has substantial memory requirements, significantly limiting the num-
ber of nodes in the input graph that the model can process without exceeding the
GPU memory capacity. Thus, our proposed approach is constrained to use relatively
small batches which can induce unintended biases in the model input during the self-
supervised training. The sampling strategy also implies that nodes appear in multiple
batches, which can generate slightly different embeddings because their role in the
network might vary across batches. To avoid having several representations per indi-
vidual, we use the embedding originating from the first occurrence of an individual.
However, we want to emphasize that the N × N adjacency matrix is not constructed
during inference, which implies that the number of nodes can increase significantly
after training, potentially alleviating this issue to some extent.
Furthermore, constructing our batches also requires a substantial amount of time,
and due to the stochastic nature of our random walk-inspired sampling procedure, it
is challenging to ensure that we sample all individuals in our cohort in the batches.
Consequently, after our graph sampling, we have approximately 60%, or 2 million,
of the individuals in our training batches used for the self-supervised training phase.
However, to ensure that we evaluate the final models on the same test set as the
baseline models, we conducted a separate batch construction where we sampled the
initial nodes without replacements from the set of individuals in the test set. Fur-
thermore, we continue sampling until we have all test individuals in our test batches.
Hence, HGTMAE and S-HGTMAE have not been pre-trained on a set the same size
as the baselines. However, since the performance of HGTMAE does not seem weak
by comparison, it does not indicate that the batching strategy is inadequate. Thus,
we believe investigating how the specific batching strategy influences model perfor-
mance will merit the model and its future use. Furthermore, if we could propose
a different reconstruction learning strategy reflecting network connectivity than the
MER component, we would be able to scale the model to significantly larger graphs,
potentially removing the dependency on sampling strategies to some degree.
We want to address a few potential concerns regarding the loss function formula-
tion in the mortality prediction task, the optimisation procedure and our corrected
evaluation metrics.
As mentioned, we formulate the early mortality prediction task as a positive un-
labeled (PU) learning problem because Statistics Denmark might not have accurate
data on whether or not an individual is alive when individuals emigrate. Thus, the
data contains some right-censored observations, causing the negative set to have pos-
itive observations.
To accommodate this issue, we adopt the Asymmetric Cross-
Entropy (ACE) loss previously used by Savcisens et al. on the same data and pre-
diction task [34]. Specifically, the ACE loss introduces a parameter c that lessens
the punishment of making false positive predictions because we expect that a few of
==PAGE:122==
110
6 Discussion
them are truly positive. However, the results shown in the confusion matrices for the
baseline models indicate a severe number of false positive classifications. Hence, the
effect of formulating the problem as a PU problem and including the parameter c has
overemphasised the importance of the right-censored observations. The overclassifi-
cation suggests that additional work should have focused on determining the optimal
value of c.
We correct the evaluation metrics to account for the right-censored observations,
which might lead to artificially high metrics compared to their uncorrected counter-
parts. We adjust the metrics by assuming that α = 2.5% of individuals classified
as alive in the registry data within the 4-year prediction window are deceased. This
assumption is based on the prior work conducted by Savcisens et al. and originates
from the domain experts from Statistics Denmark. However, our cohort includes a
larger age span than in the prior works by Savcisens et al. where the observed propor-
tion of people dying between 2015 and 2020 is only approximately 1.7%. Hence, the
observed proportion indicates that the previously used correction proportion α can
not be applied to a different cohort. However, the correction factor is constant across
models. Hence, this issue will not affect the conclusions based on model comparisons.
Furthermore, the number of parameters in HGTMAE is notably larger than that of
life2vec. They have 180 million and 8 million parameters. In the future, it is worth
investigating how to minimize the parameters in HGTMAE to a more reasonable
amount.

They propose sharing
attention coeﬀicients across network layers. Hence, reducing the amount of parame-
ters substantially while maintaining the same performance [74]. This idea could be
extended to also use parameter sharing for similar edge and node types. Reducing
the number of parameters is not crucial to the model performance. However, it is
important to keep the number of parameters in mind when building deep learning
models, as larger models require more time and power to train than smaller models.
We live in an era where this should be a high priority.
Finally, we want the reader to recall the problems with our PFP loss discussed in
section 6.1. This limitation should be addressed in future work with HGTMAE.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This is not the end. It is not even the beginning of the end. But it is, perhaps, the end of
the beginning.
- Winston Churchill
This thesis explores the integration of relational and sequential data within a unified
framework, enabling a single model to learn generic vector representations of human
lives. We construct a heterogeneous graph capable of representing both the relational
and sequential aspect of nationwide registry data. To successfully learn generic life-
embeddings, we develop a self-supervised learning strategy for an attention-based
graph neural network that accounts for the relational and sequential aspects of our
graph. The resulting life-embeddings show promising results in early mortality pre-
diction, surpassing the state-of-the-art life-embeddings. Furthermore, we show the
model’s ability to be finetuned for improved predictive power.
In the introduction of this thesis, we asked three research questions. In the fol-
lowing, we attempt to answer these questions.
First, we asked how we can represent social relationships when modeling individ-
ual life-trajectories. To address this, we propose two frameworks. The first, FL2VA,
was introduced as a baseline, representing family relations as edges in a homoge-
neous graph, with life-trajectories represented by precomputed life2vec embeddings.
The second, more comprehensive framework uses a heterogeneous graph with tem-
poral information embedded in the relations. We incorporate social relationships as
edges between nodes. Furthermore, we represent individual life-trajectories as edges
to life-events, with timestamps capturing the chronological temporal aspects. Thus,
we generated life-embeddings that integrate social relationships and individual life-
trajectories by modeling this heterogeneous graph with HGTMAE.
Second, we asked what the impact of combining social relationships and individual
life-trajectories was on the quality of the life-embeddings that summarize individual
behavior. Our findings demonstrate that HGTMAE life-embeddings successfully cap-
ture demographic and socioeconomic structure, summarizing life-trajectories for most
individuals in the dataset. HGTMAE can be adapted to reflect social relationships
==PAGE:124==
112
7 Conclusion
more or less than life-trajectories, depending on the application, by adjusting the
weight between its two loss formulations. However, younger individuals introduce
challenges for HGTMAE due to various reasons discussed previously.
Third, we asked how incorporating social relationships in individual life-trajectories
can affect predictive models on early mortality. We try to answer this question by
comparing models that include social relationships with models that do not. In the
context of early mortality prediction, our findings suggest that incorporating social
relationships do not substantially improve the performance of the predictive models.
We show that the models primarily rely on individual life-trajectories when predicting
early mortality.
This thesis has been a valuable learning experience and has contributed to a foun-
dation that opens up several promising directions for future research. One potential
direction is to explore the influence of social relationships in other domains, such as
fertility, mental health, etc. While we have made progress in exploring our three re-
search questions, several aspects remain that require further investigation, and even
more questions arise.
Thus, we are committed to continuing the development of
HGTMAE to fully explore and understand its strengths and potential applications
across various contexts.
