Abstract
Fifteen percent of all strokes are caused by emboli formed in the left atrium (LA) in
case of atrial brillation (AF). The most common site of thrombus formation is inside the
left atrial appendage (LAA). The LAA is accounting for 90% of the thrombi formed in the
LA in patients with non-valvular AF. Studies have shown there is a correlation between
the LAA morphology and risk of ischemic stroke. The popular named LAA Chicken Wing
morphology is associated lower risk, while the Cauliower morphology with higher risk.
In order to verify the hypothesis of a correlation between LAA shape clusters and
thrombus risk, we build a point distribution model to quantitatively describe the LAA
shape variation. The model is based on 103 LAA surfaces segmented and reconstructed
from multidetector computed tomography volumes. We are successfully able to determine
point correspondence between LAA surfaces, by non-rigid volumetric registration of signed
distance elds. To validate, if LAA shapes are clustered, we represent the surfaces by their
rst few PCA loadings and use the loadings to identify shape clusters in the data set.
Furthermore, an unsupervised method is used to estimate the natural number of clusters
in our training set, where the number of shape clusters is estimated by validating test log-
likelihood of several Gaussian mixture models using two level cross-validation. We found
that the LAAs surfaces formed two shape clusters, broadly corresponding to the Chicken
wing and Cauliower morphology.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Heart-related diseases are the leading cause of death in the United States as well as other
high-income nations. In these countries heart-related diseases accounts for approximately
40% of deaths, and it is predicated that it will become the leading cause of death worldwide
in 2020 [1].
Atrial brillation (AF) accounts for a substantial proportion of the heart
diseases with a prevalence at 3 million people in United States, and the prevalence is
expected to increase to 7.56 million by 2050, mainly due to rising age of the population [2].
AF causes a 5-fold increase in risk of ischemic stroke, and it is the cause for approximately
15% of all strokes in the United States [3]. The most common site of thrombus formation
is inside the left atrial appendage (LAA), accounting for 90% of the thrombi formed in
patients with non-valvular AF [4]. The LAA is a complex tubular structure with a high
inter patient variability originating from the left atrium (LA). A more in-depth description
of LAA can be seen in Section 2.4
Established methods to preventing thromboembolism in patients with AF are antico-
agulation therapies, which have been shown to reduce the risk of stroke by 60% [5]. The
downsides with anticoagulation treatments are high risk for bleeding complications and
varying eectiveness, due to interactions with some foods and medications [6]. LAA occlu-
sion/Exclusion is a novel procedure that has shown great progress to prevent strokes over
the past few years. The main concepts of the LAA occlusion method is to implant a device
that close o the LAA from the systemic blood circulation. An example of a LAA occlu-
sion procedure using the WATCHMANTM closure device is illustrated in Fig. 1.1. The
WATCHMAN TM closure procedure is conducted using a catheter to deliver the device.
The catheter gets access to the LAA through the right femoral vein, and by a transseptal
puncture. The closure device is implanted through the catheter into the orice/neck of the
LAA [2]. The dierent closure devises are produced in several predened sizes in order to
cover the large shape variance of the LAA, and a suitably sized device must be selected
for each patient during preoperative planning.
To improve the success rate and reduce the complications of the occlusion procedure, is it
necessary with detailed knowledge about the morphology of the LAA. Several studies have
focused on describing the morphology. The authors of [7] have studied the LAA length,
width, orice size, number of lobes in 500 normal autopsy hearts. The LAA volume and

Background and Theory
2.1
The Heart
The heart is a pump about the size of a st located between the lungs in the mediastinal
space of the intrathoracic cavity, posterior to the sternum and anterior to the vertebral
column. The heart pumps blood through the pulmonary and systemic circulation with an
average pump-rate of 70 beats per minute.
The heart composes of four heart chambers: Two atria and two ventricles (See Fig. 2.1).
The two atria are called the right and the left atrium and they are marked with 1 and 6 in
Fig. 2.1 respectively. The LA receives oxygenated blood from the pulmonary circulation
through the four pulmonary veins. The right atrium has three major openings. Two of
openings receiving deoxygenated blood from the superior and the inferior vena cava and
the third major opening receiving blood from the heart itself. Under normal conditions,
the two atria function primarily as blood reservoirs for the ventricles. However, during
exercise the heart pumps 300-400% more blood, than during rest, resulting in signicantly
reduced lling time of the ventricles, due to increased heart rate. Passive lling of the
ventricles is no longer sucient with reduced lling time and atrial contractions become a
crucial factor for the pumping capacity of the heart.
Likewise, as the atria the heart has a left and a right ventricle. The left ventricle re-
ceives blood from the left atrium through the atrioventricular canal and pumps it out to
the systemic circulation by the aorta artery. The right ventricle receives blood from the
right atrium through the atrioventricular canal and pumps it out to the pulmonary circu-
lation by the pulmonary trunk. Four valves ensure that the blood always ows from the
atria to the ventricles and further into the arteries.
The wall of the heart consists of three dierent layers: The outer epicardium, the
myocardium and the smooth endocardium. The myocardium forms the basis for the con-
tractile properties of the heart champers. The layer is composed of cardiac muscle cells
separated from each other by intercalated disk. These disks are unique for the myocardium

and contain gap junctions that allows electrical current to propagate between cells. Thus,
the myocardium acts as a single unit and when one myocardial cell becomes excited, the
current propagates rapidly to the other cells. All these cells will then contract at the same
time, as a heartbeat.
The synchronized contraction of the heart is controlled by the electrical conducting
system. This system consists of modied cardiac muscle cells, whose purpose is to generate
and distribute action potentials. The action potentials originate in the Sinoatrial (SA) node
and propagate across the wall of the right atrium to the atrioventricular (AV) node (See
Fig. 2.2). The action potentials pass through the AV node, and further to the AV bundle,
which subsequently divides into right and left bundle branches. Here, the action potentials
descend to the apex of the ventricles, and further through the ventricles walls along the
Purkinje bers [1, 13].

2.2
Atrial Fibrilation
AF belongs to the supraventricular category of cardiac arrhythmias. The disease is
characterized by fast and irregular contraction of the atria, due to vortex like current,
in the electrical conducting system, between the SA node and the AV bundle. The two
structures are illustrated in Fig. 2.2.
A direct consequence of AF is a reduction in cardiac output, as a result to desynchron-
ization between atria and ventricles. The rapid and reduced contractile ability of the atria
leads to decreased blood ow, and thus increasing the risk of thrombus formation in the
atria. These thrombi can than lead to thromboembolism in the brain as an ischemic stroke.
Studies have shown a 5-6-fold increased risk for ischemic stroke for people with AF [3].
The prevalence of AF is estimated to about 3 million people in the United States. The
prevalence is associated with a high age dependency, where the prevalence for people <60
years is <1% and 8% for people >80 years. The prevalence is expected to increase to 7.56
million people by 2050, due to the rising age of the population [1, 14].
2.3
Stroke
Stroke is classied as acute focal neurologic symptoms, due to a disturbance in the
arteries supplying the brain. The disturbance can be due to thrombi/emboli in arteries, or
non-traumatic ruptures of arteries with bleeding into the brain tissue. Based on the two

2.3.1
Ischemic Stroke
Ischemic stroke is the most common type, which accounts for 85% of the cases. The
causes of ischemic stroke can either due to thromboses or thromboembolism. With throm-
boses, the aected artery is occluded by a thrombus formed locally in the artery, where
atherosclerosis often is the underlying cause of the thrombus formation. With thromboem-
bolism, the aected artery is occluded by a thrombus, formed in another location of the
artery system and carried by the bloodstream. Frequently, the thromboembolism is formed
in an arteriosclerotic carotid artery or inside the LA in case of AF. It is estimated that
15% of the strokes are caused by emboli, as a consequence of AF [3].
In the event of a thrombus or an embolus occluding an artery, the brain area supplied
by the artery gets a reduction in blood supply. The reduction is not evenly divided across
the aected brain areas. The blood supply to the central parts is signicantly reduced
and the brain cells will after few minutes be irreversibly damaged and later die. In the
peripheral parts of the concerned area, the blood supply is partially interrupted, and the
aected cells are only reversibly injured. The cells in these peripheral areas can be saved if
the blood supply is re-established within a short period of time. The severity of the disease
depends of the area size, aected tissue, and the location of the damaged tissue.
Mortality and Morbidity of the disease are 20% and 50% respectively, and 15% of the
aected persons have so large degree of neurologic impairment, that nursing homes are
necessary. The yearly incidence of ischemic stroke is about 795,000 in United States with
high age dependency [1, 14].
2.4
Left Atrial Appendage
The LAA is a complex tubular structure projection from the LA. The protruding LAA
from LA is seen in gure Fig. 2.3. The projection starts with an elliptically orice, which
leads to the neck region, and further to the main body of the LAA. The size of orice
varies substantially, with the major diameter ranging from 10 to 40 mm, and the minor
diameter ranging from 5 to 27 mm. Likewise, the variation in the total length of the LAA
is great, which ranging from 16 to 51 mm. Additionally, the LAA has a varying number of
lobes protruding from the main body, where two lobes is the most common [2, 15, 16]. The
LAA can be classied into the following four classes based on their dierent morphological
characteristics [10]:
1. The Chicken Wing morphology, has an obvious bend in the proximal or middle section
of the main body. The Chicken Wing may has one or more lobes protruding from
the main body.
2. The Wind Sock morphology, has a main body with sucient length and with varying

body (Fig. 2.4b).
3. The Cactus morphology, has a main body with lobes protruding in both the superior

4. The Cauliower morphology, has a limited total length and a more complex internal
structure compared to other three classes. The orice has a more irregular shape
while the rest of the LAA consist of varying number of lobes with lack of a main

The prevalence of The Chicken Wing, the Wind Sock, the Cactus, and the Cauliower
LAA classes have been shown to be 48%, 19%, 30% and 3% respectively, based on a study

3.1.1.2
Surface Segmentation and Reconstruction
The LAAs are segmented in CT-volumes and LAA surfaces are subsequently reconstruc-
ted from the segmented LAAs volumes. The segmentation and reconstruction process is
described in the following four steps:
1. The raw CT-volumes are manually cropped to only contain the tracer enhanced
regions with the LAA and some parts of the LA (See Fig. 3.2). This procedure is
conducted using the Osirix DICOM viewer.
2. The cropped CT-volumes are centered to a common origin and blurred by a Gaussian
lter kernel with standard divination at 0.5 mm (See Fig. 3.3).
3. The LAA surfaces are reconstructed from the cropped and blurred volumes using
the Marching Cubes algorithm [19], with a manually set iso-surface level in the range
[150HU;250HU]. The selected iso-surface level varies, due to variations of the amount
of tracer in the LAA. The surface reconstruction is performed using 3DSlicer's [20]
implementation of the Marching Cubes algorithm.
4. Cutting o the LAA from the LA in the reconstructed surfaces and removed discon-
nected vertices (See Fig. 3.4).

3.3
Point Correspondence
The point correspondence between LAA surfaces are determined by a non-rigid trans-
formation that map every vertex in a source surface S to every vertex in a target surface
T . In this project the non-rigid transformation that maps S →T is estimated in the
following three steps.
1. Initial similarity aliment of S to roughly align the surface to T .
2. Non-rigid volumetric registration of the roughly align S and T .
3. Propagation of vertices in the registered S directly onto vertices in T .
The surface registration is performed using a state-of-the-art non-rigid volumetric registra-
tion algorithm described in Section 3.3.3. To be able to use the registration algorithm the
LAA surfaces must be represented as volumes. In this work two volumetric representation
of the LAA surfaces are applied. The rst representation is the Raw CT-volume, where
LAA surface is extracted from. The second approach is to represent the surfaces as a
signed distance elds.

signed distance eld on the surfaces. A Signed distance eld is a voxel volume that covers

3.3.6
Markov Random Field Point Correspondences
Since the volumetric registration is conducted on the signed distance eld representa-
tion on the LAA surface, it is not guaranteed that the zero level iso-surfaces ts perfect
after the registration. Hence, it is not guaranteed that vertices in ST fall exactly on top of
T too. This problem is solved by using an approach originally described in [28], where ver-
tices in ST are propagated directly on top of T using Markov Random Field regularization.
Initially each vertex in ST is projected to the closest position on T , and thus there
is correspondence for every vertex in ST . The correspondence between the two surfaces
is now forming a correspondence vector eld (CVF). A Markov Random Field (MRF)
regularization is applied on the CVF, where each correspondence vector is penalized by
deviations from its neighbor vectors. The algorithm performs multiple iteration, where the
CVF is regularized by the MRF and every vector is reprojected to position on T . The
result of vertex propagation is a point correspondence surface SCOR. Each vertex in SCOR
is thereby placed exactly on top of its anatomical correspond vertex in T .
3.4
Template Construction
Until now, the point correspondence representation of the LAA surfaces are determined
by choosing a random surface from the training set and use it as source surface. This
procedure, however gives rise to potential high source/template bias in the point corres-
pondence. Since the selected source, can by chance, be a surface that easily is deformed to
t the majority of the target surfaces. Likewise, the opposite can be the case, where the
source has diculty to t the target surfaces, and thus the point correspondence repres-
entations are dependent on the selected source.
In this project we are reducing the template bias by creating model surface based on
all the surfaces in the data set. The model surface is created by:
1. Compute all N SCOR using an arbitrary surface as source.
2. Compute the template surface by taking the mean of N SCOR after they have been
procrustes align to a common position.
3. Use the template surface as source to determine a new set of SCOR.
By iterating between these three steps the template bias is reduced in each iteration.

The examination of LAA shape clusters is conducted by tting J GMMs to the PCA

The training
LLH is calculated from each of the trained GMM and number of shape clusters in the
data is equal to the number of mixture clusters in the GMM with the highest LLH. In
order to avoid over-tting, the number of shape clusters is determined using two level
cross-validation. The second cross-validation level is similar to the method just described,
but the number of shape cluster is determined based on the test LLH. The test LHH is
calculated as the mean of the LLHs computed from every shape in the rst cross-valuation
level. The rst level performs leave-one-out cross-validation. Here the data are divided
into N −1 training shapes and one test shape. The training set is used to train a GMM
with K mixture clusters, while the test shape is used to validate the trained GMM, using
the LLH as quality metric. This procedure is repeated until all N shapes have been used
as test shape. By looking at the test LLH, we ensure that the optimal number of mixture
clusters is not just equal to the number of training shapes. This will have been the case if
we have looked at the training LLH. Since the best training LLH is obtained, when we t
a GMM with mixture clusters for every shape.
In order to identify the shape appearance of the natural formed clusters, a new GMM
is trained on the entire data set, where the number of mixture clusters is equal to the
number of estimated shape clusters.

5.1
Data
Data set one are the mainly used data set in this project, and therefore we are only
discussing this data set. CT volumes in data set one are taken from a large data base of
MDCT volumes acquired as part of the Copenhagen General Population Study, cf. Sec-
tion 3.1. As previously mentioned are the MDCT volumes acquired on participants with
none of the listed conditions. Condition eight (ECG abnormalities), is particularly import-
ant for this project, since it excludes participants with AF from the study. This group of
participants/patients is important, since we want to get better knowledge about the cor-
relation between LAA morphology and risk of stroke, when AF is present. But also getting
knowledge about the shape of the LAA orice and neck, in order to improve success rate
of LAA occlusion procedures, which only are performed on patients with AF.
The reason we in this project use CT data acquired on people without AF, is due to
image contrast and signal to noise ratio in the acquired volumes. A reduction in image
contrast and signal to noise ratio are often seen when the images are acquired on people
with AF. AF causes a reduction in concentration of the injected contrast agent in the LA
and LAA, and thus a reduction in image contrast in these regions. The lower contrast
makes the segmentations and surface reconstructions of the LAA more dicult, due to
several factors. One of the major factor is to separate the LAA boundary from the sur-
rounding tissues. Another major factor is that the LAA has a noisy iso-surface, causing
and reduction in the surface quality.
The next question is, can our implemented model be used to quantify the shape variation
in patients with AF, when our model is trained on data without AF? In order for us to
generalize the model to patients with AF, there must not be a correlation between the shape
of the LAA and present of AF. However, this seems to be the case. A study of LAAs shapes
and structers for patients with and without AF, provided in [32], shows that patients with
AF have signicantly larger LAA volume. Which may be an indication that LAA shapes,
from patients with AF, derive from another manifold in the LAA shape space. This makes it

dicult to describe shape variation of LAA in case of AF with our model. However, we have
implemented methods that successfully can determined point correspondences between
LAA shapes with large variation. We have use the point correspondences representation
to train a statistical shape mode. Our implemented pipeline can later be used to train
statistical shape models on data acquired on patients with AF.
5.1.1
Surface Segmentation and Reconstruction
Our biggest concern in the segmentation and reconstruction process is to determine the
intersection between LA and LAA. Since variance in the intersection will be presented as
PCA components in the shape model, and potential give rise to new shape clusters. An
inspection of the major PCA components reveals no signicant variation due to variance
in the intersection between LA and LAA. However, we have developed a method to make
the intersection determination more quantitative. The developed method works by looking
at the LA in two dierent scenarios. The rst scenario is the normal situation, where we
have a surface of the LA with a LAA attached. In the second scenario, the method looks
at a surface of the LA if there is no LAA attached to it. A description of how the method
determines the LA surface without LAA can be seen in Section 7.1. By computing the
distance between the surfaces from the two cases, the LAA is dened as surface, where the
distance is larger than zero. The downside and the reasons why the method not was used
in the surface segmentation, is due to that the method adds substantial amount of time to
the segmentation work ow.
5.2
Point Correspondence
A point distribution model dependents on an accurate point correspondence repres-
entations of the training shapes, where each correspondence point is located at the same
anatomical structure in every shape. In this section we discuss the overall quality of the
point correspondences. We also discuss our optimization of registration hyper-parameters
and our method to construct template surfaces.
5.2.1
Quality of Point Correspondence
The shape variation between the individual LAA surfaces is huge and it set high require-
ments to the point correspondence approach. We have evaluated the point correspondence
quality with the two metrics described in Section 3.5.
The number of ST with negative Jacobian determinants is small, with only 6 out of
105 surfaces. As shown in the result section, only two of the six surfaces have signicant
signs of mesh inversion and are therefore excluded from the data set. We have conducted
a deeper inspection of the remaining four surfaces in order to see how the mesh inversions
inuence the shapes. The inspection reviles that the four ST have minor visible inversions
in their meshes and these minor mesh inversions have no impact on the shapes.

When it comes to distances between target and SCOR surface, then the overall distance
is low. The median RMS is around 0.6mm and by comparing median RMS to the total
length of the LAA is it many times smaller.
The inspections of SCOR with RMS above the 75th percentile, show that point corres-
pondence framework struggling to determine point correspondence in distal lobes. Sup-
porting the registration with additional landmarks on all the target surfaces, could solve
this problem. Right now, we have only add additional landmarks in cases, where the SCOR
has a RMS > 2mm after applying a registration using the default four landmarks. The
results of these changeling cases indicate that the RMS can be reduced signicantly by
adding additional landmarks to all targets. However, the placement of the four additional
landmarks can be a challenging task, since corresponding anatomical locations is hard to
determine between LAAs. If the additional landmarks are placed at wrong locations the
RMS can even get worse. Sometimes the placement of landmarks must be done multiple
times in order to nd the correct locations. All this means that support the registration
with more landmarks in every case will increase the manual work time signicantly.
Until now, we have seen that the point correspondence approach is capable of nding
correspondence in majority of the LAA shapes. The next thing we are going to discuss is
how well does the method handles the trabeculation of LAA shapes. The trabeculation
in LAA shapes vary a lot, where some of LAAs are very trabeculated, while other LAAs
have nothing. Figure 5.1 shows two target surface in blue with a lot trabeculation. A
typical type of trabeculations in data set one can be seen in 5.1a, where the LAA has a tail
like lobe protruding from the distal part of the main body. The black wire frame in the
gure represent the computed SCOR in both cases. As briey mentioned in Section 4.4 the
point correspondence framework handles trabeculation by propagate vertices away from
the empty areas. This is specially clear in Fig. 5.1b. When the method nds point corres-
pondence around a trabeculated tube/hole in the surface, the method handles it correct
by propagate vertices away from the tube. It is the correct way to handle holes, since it
is the only way, when the source surface is solid. However, in a case like the surface in
Fig. 5.1a, there are two ways to determine point correspondence in a long tail like lobe.
The rst way is to look at the gap between main body and the lobe, as hole in the sur-
face and propagate vertices away from the empty areas, as done in Fig. 5.1a. The second
way is look at it as long lobe protruding from the upper part of the main body. In order
to determine point correspondence in the second case, the upper part of the main body
in SICP must be heavily deformed by the registration, which is challenging. When the
point correspondences are determined using the second way, it is possible to model the tail
without large polygons covering the gab. Besides that, there is the no signication down
-or upside between the two ways of determine point correspondences.

5.2.2
Template Construction
In Section 3.4 we describe a method to reduce the amount of source/template bias that
occurs, when a set of SCOR are determined using a source surface, random selected from
the pool of training surfaces. The results visualized in Fig. 4.13 show that the method
is capable of reducing the template bias, since it are able to construct similar templates
from dierent sources. However, this method is not capable of reducing the template bias
associated with the number of vertices in the template. Because the initial number of
vertices from the source is constant throughout the process, and the constructed template
has the same number of vertices. The severity of this bias dependents on the variation in
vertex number of the surfaces in the training set.
The quality of all ST and SCOR are evaluated with templates constructed after 1-3
iterations of the method. Here it is seen that using a template surface signicantly increase
the point correspondence quality. In addition, we can see a small reduction in quality,
when a template is constructed after three iterations is used as source. An explanation to
the quality reduction can be found in the bending energy penalty in the registration. The
reason why bending energy penalty cause a reduction in quality is due to the used template
is smooth without any lops. In order to transform the template to a target with many and
large lobes, the transformation must have large deformations, but these large deformations
are penalized by the bending energy term. Hence resulting in an overall reduction in point
correspondence quality.

5.3
Statistical Shape Model
In this section we mainly discuss exclusion criteria for excluding SCOR with low quality
and what impact it has on the shape model. Furthermore, are we going to discuss the
remaining template bias there is the in trained shape model.
In this project we evaluate the point correspondence quality using two metrics: The
FNJD and the RMS. For both quality metrics the most accurate point correspondence
is obtained, when they are equal to zero. As seen from Fig. 4.14 this is not the case,
and we exclude SCOR with poor quality from the training set. We exclude SCOR with
RMS ≥2mm or with FNJD ≥3%. The reason why we have to exclude poor SCOR is that
they introduce unnatural shape variation into the shape model. But on the other hand, if
we are excluded to many, the shape model will only describe a small fraction of the shape
variance in the data set. We nd exclusion of SCOR with more than 3% FNJD necessary,
since these SCOR have signicant mesh inversions, that result in unnatural LAA shapes.
Including unnatural shapes in the shape model does not give rise to any improvement of
its ability to describing shapes.
As mentioned in Section 4.4, SCOR must has RMS < 0.4mm in order to has corres-
pondences between every lobes. By excluding all SCOR with RMS > 0.4mm, the shape
model can describe the varying lobes of the LAA. However, with the used data set, the
shape model will only be trained on a quarter of the data. A more detailed review of SCOR
with RMS < 0.4mm shows that they belong to one type of shape class. Meaning that a

or close to that type of shape class. In order to have a statistical shape model capable of

5.4
Cluster Analysis
As previously mentioned it is common in the literature to group LAAs into four classes
according to the morphology of the LAAs. The shape classication is based on several
shape characteristics listed in Section 2.4. In this section we are going to discuss how LAA
shapes naturally are cluster by their PCA loadings, and how the natural clustering can be
compared with the classication from the literature.
5.4.1
Visual Inspection
Visual inspection of the PCA loadings, calculated from the set of SCOR in data set
two, shows that the shapes are forming clusters in 2-D. Especially when looking at PCA
loadings corresponding to mode ve and six, where we see clusters corresponding to: The
Chicken Wing, Wind Sock and Cactus morphology. But the formed shape clusters are
not well separated, as seen from the same example, where the Chicken wing and Cactus
cluster are overlapping. We do not expect the clusters to be well separated, since the LAA
surfaces are reconstructed from biological data. However, can loadings corresponding two
other combination of PCA modes give rise to better cluster formation in 2-D.
5.4.2
Gaussian Mixture Model
In order to nd the natural number of shape clusters in the two data sets, we looked
at the test LLH computed from multiple GMM with dierent number of mixture clusters.
The test LHHs are computed with a two level cross-validation (cf. 3.6.2).

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The main aim of this project is to quantitatively describe the varying shape of the LAA
using a statistical shape model. In order to build a valid statistical shape model, we have
segmented and reconstructed 105 LAA surfaces. The LAA surface are reconstructed from
MDCT volumes, through a developed pipeline, where the MDCT volumes are anonymized
and the LAA surfaces segmented and later reconstructed.
In order to use our LAA surfaces in the point distribution model, we have implemen-
ted methods to determine point correspondence between all 105 LAA surfaces. We can
successfully determine point correspondence between 103 of surfaces. The method used to
determine point correspondence are: Non-rigid volumetric registration of signed distance
elds, MRF regularized vertex propagation, and template surface construction.
From the trained point distribution model, we are able to see that 25% of total variation
is due to variation in the total size and the shape of the LAA main body.
The second aim of this project is to use the statistical shape model to investigate how
LAA surface are grouped into shape clusters. We conducted the cluster examination by
representing the LAA surfaces by their rst ve PCA loadings. By tting several GMMs
to the PCA loadings using two level cross-validation, we found that the surfaces in data set
one, are grouped into two clusters. Inspections of the clusters reveals that rst shape cluster
correspond to the Chicken Wing morphology. While the second shape cluster correspond
to a combination of the Cactus, Wind Sock and Cauliower morphology.

Intersection Between LA and LAA
In order to use LAA surfaces for building statistical shape models, the intersection
between the LA and LAA must be well dened. A well dened intersection ensures that
non intentional variation, due to determination of the intersection, is included in the shape
model. We have developed a method to quantitatively describe the intersection between
the LA and LAA. The method works by looking at the LA in two dierent scenarios. The
rst scenario is the normal case, where we have a surface of the LA with a LAA attached, as
seen in Fig. 7.1a. In the second scenario, the approach looking on how the LA surface had
looked if there is no LAA attached. By computing the distance between the LA surfaces
from the two scenarios, is the LAA surface dened as the surface, where the distance is
higher than zero.
The LA surface from the second scenario is determined by using the MRF surface re-
construction method desribed in [24]. The initial step is to remove the LAA at a level,
where only the LA surface is maintained, but still enough LA surface for the MRF surface
reconstruction to compute a valid LA surface. An example of a LA surface, where the LAA
has been removed is shown in Fig. 7.1b. The LA surface without LAA is reconstructed
using the MRF surface reconstruction method. The reconstructed LA surface is shown in
Fig. 7.1c. The LAA surface is determined by computing the distance between the surfaces
shown in Fig. 7.1a and 7.1c. The computed distance is shown in 7.1d, where the color scale
indicate the distance.

Abstract. Fifteen percent of all strokes are caused by emboli formed in
the left atrium (LA) in case of atrial ﬁbrillation (AF). The most common
site of thrombus formation is inside the left atrial appendage (LAA). The
LAA is accounting for 90% of the thrombi formed in the LA in patients
with non-valvular AF. Studies have shown there is a correlation between
the LAA morphology and risk of ischemic stroke. The popular named
LAA Chicken Wing morphology is associated with lower risk, while the
Cauliﬂower morphology with higher risk.
In this paper, we describe a full framework for shape analysis and clus-
tering of the LAA. Initially, we build a point distribution model to quan-
titatively describe the LAA shape variation based on 103 LAA surfaces
segmented and reconstructed from multidetector computed tomography
volumes. We are successfully able to determine point correspondence
between LAA surfaces, by non-rigid volumetric registration of signed
distance ﬁelds. To validate, if LAA shapes are clustered, we employ an
unsupervised clustering on the shape models parameters to estimate the
natural number of clusters in our training set, where the number of shape
clusters is estimated by validating the test log-likelihood of several Gaus-
sian mixture models using two level cross-validation. We found that the
LAAs surfaces formed two shape clusters, broadly corresponding to the
Chicken wing and Cauliﬂower morphology.
Keywords: Left Atrial Appendage· Point Distribution Models· Clus-
tering· Gaussian Mixture Models
1
Introduction
Atrial ﬁbrillation (AF) causes a 5-fold increase in risk of ischemic stroke and
it is the cause for approximately 15% all strokes in the United States [6]. In
90% of the cases, thrombi are formed inside the left atrial appendage (LAA) in
patients with non-valvular AF. The LAA is a complex tubular structure, with
a high inter-patient variability, originating from the left atrium (LA). Studies
have shown there is a correlation between LAA morphology and risk of ischemic
==PAGE:80==
2
J.M. Slipsager et al.
stroke [3,7]. The authors of [3] reported that the popular named Chicken Wing
morphology is associated with lower risk of stroke compared to non-Chicken
Wing morphology. Several studies have focused on describing the varying LAA
morphology, where the morphology is described by the LAA length, width, or-
ﬁce/ostium size, and number of lobes. The authors of [4] reported variation:
In the LAA volume ranging from 770-19,270mm3, minor oriﬁce diameter rang-
ing from 5-27mm, major oriﬁce diameter between 10-40mm, and LAA length
ranging between 16-51mm. Their ﬁndings were based on 220 LAAs
The aim of this work is to quantitatively describe the LAA shape variation
and clustering using a statistical shape model. We are training a point distribu-
tion model based on LAA surfaces reconstructed from multidetector computed
tomography (CT) images and later use the trained model together with unsuper-
vised clustering methods to examine the natural clustering of the LAA shapes.
2
Data and Preprocessing
The LAA surfaces are reconstructed from CT images, provided by the Depart-
ment of Radiology, Rigshospitalet, University of Copenhagen. The data are ac-
quired as part of the Copenhagen General Population Study [11], where partici-
pants are oﬀered a research cardiac computed tomography angiography (CCTA)
examination [5]. Participants are excluded from the examination if they, among
other things suﬀer from AF. The CCTA examinations are performed on a 320
detector CT scanner (Aquilion One, Toshiba, Medical Systems), with the scan-
ner settings: Gantry rotation time 350ms, detector collimation 0.5 x 320, X-ray
tube voltage 100 - 120 kV, and X-ray tube current 280 - 500 mA. The acquired
CT images have a matrix size 512 x 512 x 560 and a voxel size 0.5 x 0.5 x
0.25mm.
One hundred and ﬁve CT images, with high contrast are randomly selected
from the database, where a slice from an example CT image is show in Fig. 1a.
The raw CT-volumes are manually cropped to only contain the tracer-enhanced
regions with the LAA. After cropping, CT-volumes are blurred with a Gaussian
ﬁlter kernel with standard deviation at 0.5 mm and the iso-surfaces of the inner
part of the LAA is computed using the Marching Cubes algorithm [10] with a
manually set iso-level in the range [150HU;250HU]. The selected iso-surface level
varies, due to variations of the amount of tracer in the LAA. A reconstructed
LAA surface from the example CT image, is shown in Fig. 1b.

Methods
The ﬁrst goal of this work is to build a statistical shape model [2] to quanti-
tatively describe the shape variation of the LAA. This model is created from
a training set containing N Procrustes aligned shapes, where the shapes in the
training set are represented as a series of corresponding points.
The point correspondence between LAA surfaces are determined by register-
ing a source surface S to each target surface T in the training set, such that each

S is aligned to T with a similarity transform by registration of four manually

use the volumetric registration algorithm, SICP and T must be represented as
volumes. We represent SICP and T , as signed distance ﬁelds (SDF), where each
voxel value in the SDF is equal to the signed Euclidean distance to the surface
[12,14].
The non-rigid volumetric registration is conducted by solving the optimiza-
tion given by:

Here C is a cost-function, IF is the ﬁxed volume and IM is the moving vol-

Tµ is the non-rigid volumetric transformation that transform IM to IF . The
transformation is parameterised by a parameter-vector µ. In this work, we use a
multi-level B-Spline transformation with ﬁve resolution levels. The cost function
we are going to minimize is described by:

energy penalty term. The weights: ω1 = 1, ω2 = 0.15 and ω3 = 2 are opti-
mized using a grid search. The optimal transformation parameters are found
using adaptive stochastic gradient descent [8] as optimizer, with 2048 random
samples per iteration for a maximum of 500 iterations as implemented in the

to a vertex in T .

distribution model using Procrustes alignment and principal component analysis
(PCA) as described in [2].

The point correspondence framework is applied to our 105 reconstructed LAA

the average shape of N Procrustes aligned SCOR. The set of SCOR is computed
as an initial registration of S and T , where S is selected randomly from the pool
of LAA surfaces.
We are able to determine point correspondence of the majority of the 105
target surfaces, with a median root mean square distance (RMS) between T
and SCOR at 0.6mm and a 75th percentile at 0.9mm. The surfaces with RMS
equal to the median and 75th percentile are shown in Fig. 2b and 2c respectively.

in the most distal lobes of the LAA. A review of all SCOR shows that two of the
surfaces have poor point correspondence and are therefore excluded from the
training set, leaving 103 surfaces for the rest of the analysis.

and we choose to represent the shapes using their ﬁrst ﬁve PCA loadings. The
ﬁrst ﬁve PCA loadings are used, since they all describe more than 5% of the
total shape variation in the data set. Ten GMMs, with K = 1 . . . 10 mixture
components, are trained on the PCA loadings and the test LLH is computed
from each GMM using cross-validation. The mean test LLH and mean train LLH

Conclusion
In this work, we have presented a full framework for the extraction and quantiﬁ-
cation of shape clusters of left atrial appendage and demonstrated that the two
primary shape clusters is broadly similar to previously reported shape descrip-
tions. The framework enables future statistical inference on the relation between
LAA shape characteristics and stroke risk.
