Feasibility Study on Energy Recovery from Brewers’ Spent Grains
by Joan Miquel Faubel Vidagany
Brewers’ spent grain (BSG) is the main by-product generated by a brewery. Due to its high
content of protein and ﬁbre, the difﬁculties to store it and the high and constant production, the
fastest way to dispose it is as animal feed. However, since brewers’ spent grains have a high en-
ergy content (LHV = 20 MJ/kgdb), some alternatives have been proposed to exploit its valuable
energy potential. In this project four different treatments (combustion, pyrolysis, gasiﬁcation
and anaerobic digestion) have been studied to recover the energy present in the spent grains
in the form of heat and power to integrate in the brewery. Theoretically, it is possible to cover
100 % of a brewery thermal energy demand with just spent grains. An economic assessment
has also been carried out for each of the scenarios studied at ﬁxed electricity and heat gener-
ation prices. Results show that, on the one hand, neither the pyrolysis none the gasiﬁcation
processes are economically feasible. The reasons are either high capital investment in the gasi-
ﬁcation scenario or low energetic performance in the pyrolysis process. On the other hand, the
most promising scenario from an economic point of view is the anaerobic digestion of brewers’
spent grains and combustion of the biogas in a gas engine for cogeneration of electricity and
heat. However, none of the scenarios results economically feasible if the price of the wet spent
grain at which it is sold to farmers is over e17 per tonne.

1 Introduction
1.1
Beer Manufacturing Process
Beer is the most consumed alcoholic drink worldwide and the most popular beverage after
water and tea [1, 2]. In fact, almost 200000 million litres of beer are produced in the world
every year [3, 4]. Water, malted barley, yeast and hops are the four basic ingredients to produce
this drink [5].
According to Russell et al. [5], a typical beer-making process from the reception of the raw
materials to the distribution of the ﬁnal product consists of ﬁfteen processes. As it is shown in
Figure 1.1, the brewing process starts with the malting of raw barley, which is the starch source
able to be fermented. Once the barley is malted, it is milled (frequently with other cereals) to
form the grist and thus, facilitate the wet during the mashing stage and the extraction of soluble
components during the enzymatic conversion. The next stage, mashing, consists of mixing this
grist with hot water, allowing the mixture to homogenise while sugars are produced by the
enzymatic degradation of the grist proteins and starch. This sweet and soluble malt extract is
called wort. Afterwards, the mash is sent to a lauter tun or mash ﬁlter where it is separated into
the clear liquid (wort) rich in sugars and the residual solids, called brewers’ spent grains (BSG).

The next step of the beer-making process consists of boiling the wort and adding hops. The
objectives of this process are to eliminate bacteria, extract of the bittering from hops, coagulate
the excess of proteins, give colour and aroma, remove volatiles such us dimethyl sulphide
and concentrate the sugars. Particles that have coagulated during the wort boiling and also

hop debris are removed during the trub removal stage, so that beer stability does not suffer.
Then, the wort is cooled down either to 8 - 15 ◦C (fermentation temperature for lagers) or to
18 - 20 ◦C (fermentation temperature for ales) through a heat exchanger and introduced to a
fermentation vessel with yeast and air. During the fermentation stage, the yeast grows and
alcohol and carbon dioxide are produced. Flavours compounds are also produced at this stage.
After the immature beer has been produced, part of the yeast is removed by centrifugation and
the aging stage allows the green beer to mature and stabilise by storing it at low temperatures
(under 0 ◦C). Finally, beer is clariﬁed by means of ﬁltration and carbonation in order to remove
the rest of particles suspended and adjust the carbonation level. This is the last chance to make
corrections to the ﬁnal product. Then, the ready-to-consume beer is sent to the bright beer tank
where it is stored until it is packed in bottles, cans or kegs to ﬁnally be distributed.
The brewing industry generates huge amounts of by-products, mainly spent grains, spent
hops, spent yeast and CO2 [6, 7]. Out of these, the brewers’ spent grains (BSG) represent around
the 85 % of the total by-products generated and according to the literature, it is estimated that
around 20 kg of spent grains are produced per 100 L of beer manufactured [6, 8].
1.2
Brewers’ Spent Grains (BSG)
As stated above, brewers’ spent grains is the main by-product from the beer manufacturing
process. It is mainly composed by the husks of the malted barley and it contains cellulose, non-
cellulosic polysaccharides and lignin [6]. Its exact composition depends on the barley variety,
time of harvest, characteristics of hops and other adjuncts added, and brewery technology [9].
Due to its high content of protein, minerals and ﬁbre, the difﬁculties to store it and the high
and constant production, the fastest way to dispose it is as animal feed [10].
However, as BSG has a high energy content (LHV = 20 MJ/kgdb), some alternatives have
been proposed to exploit its valuable energy potential, either by means of thermochemical con-
version (combustion, pyrolysis and gasiﬁcation) or production of biogas by anaerobic digestion
[10]. For considering thermochemical conversion of brewers’ spent grains, the moisture content
must be lower than 15 % [11]. As original BSG moisture content is around 81 %, it is completely
necessary to dry them before any thermochemical treatment.
The chemical composition of brewers’ spent grain, its large availability and its low market
price make this raw material suitable for energy valorisation. Additionally, this alternative
promotes circular economy in the brewing industry, since by-products would be used as raw
materials to produce energy within the brewing industry and thus, close the circle. It would
also contribute to reduce the amount of fossil fuels required in breweries to satisfy their energy
demand, with a subsequent reduction of their environmental impact.
Even though brewers’ spent grains might be potentially used for energy purposes, they are
also a huge source of ﬁbre, minerals and proteins which could be introduced in the human’s
diet by manufacturing ﬂakes, wholewheat bread and biscuits [6, 10]. It is proved that the
addition of BSG to bakery products improve their nutritional value [6]. In fact, the protein and
essential amino acids content are increased by 50 % and 10 %, respectively, when 10 % of BSG is

for using BSG in human nutrition.
1.3
Drying of Brewers’ Spent Grains
Brewers’ spent grains come from a lauter tun or mash ﬁlter in the beer manufacturing process
(Figure 1.1) with a water content up to 81%wt. In order to use BSG as a feed material for ther-
mochemical conversion processes, its moisture content must be reduced as much as possible
and always below 15 % [11]. According to Weger et al. [4], conventional air thermal drying
of brewers’ spent grains makes no sense, since the energy demand for drying one kilogram of
BSG, considering energy loses due to exhaust gas heat and heating up BSG, is almost 2 MJ. For
this reason, other alternatives such us mechanical drying by a screw press [8] or superheated
steam drying [13], have been studied.
Weger states that a combination of mechanical, biological and thermal processes for brew-
ers’ spent grains is a good alternative for an optimised energy utilisation [8]. Firstly, he suggests
a screw press to remove water from crude BSG to get press water and press cake. Proteins and
fats are transferred to the press water, whereas the press cake consists of cellulose, lignine and
hemicellulose. Compared to a simple thermal dried BSG, an advantage of this method is that
part of nitrogen from the solid phase is removed and thus, it can be directly co-ﬁred with other
biomass fuels without further thermal drying. Regarding to the liquid phase, this might be
fermented together with brewery waste water.
The most promising and efﬁcient drying method seems to be superheated steam drying
(SSD) due to its energy efﬁciency, high drying rate, safety and high quality of ﬁnal product [14].
This technology uses superheated steam in a convective dryer instead of hot air as the drying
medium to supply heat for drying and to detract the evaporated water [15], as it is shown in

1.4.1
Combustion
The most common and mature technology for converting biomass into heat is direct combus-
tion [17]. Combustion is deﬁned as a complete exothermic oxidation of a carbonaceous fuel
that occurs at a relatively high temperature [18, 19] and it can be applied for biomass feed-
stocks with a moisture content up to 60 % [20]. For typical biomass, the combustion reaction
can be described in a simpliﬁed way as it is shown in reaction (1.1):
CH1.44O0.66 + λ1.03 (O2 + 3.76 N2 ) −→CO2 + 0.72 H2O + (λ −1)O2 + λ3.87 N
(1.1)
Where λ is the excess air ratio that describes the ratio between the air supplied and the
stoichiometric amount of air required for combustion and CH1.44O0.66 describes the average
composition of typical biomass. There are other combustion products such as CO, CxHy, PAH,
tar, soot, unburned carbon, H2, HCN, NH3 and N2O that are not shown in reaction (1.1), but
that might be present. It also applies for pollutants such as NOx, CO2 and H2O; and for ash
and contaminants such as ash particles, SO2, HCl, Cu, Pb, Zn, Cd, etc. For this reason, ﬂue gas
cleaning measures might be necessary to meet current environmental regulations.
Long residence time, adequate temperature and turbulence are key aspects that must be
considered for complete burnout. Whereas high temperature (850 ◦C) and enough residence
time (0.5 s) can easily be achieved, the limiting factor in the burnout quality is the mixing be-
tween combustible gasses and air [18]. Biomass combustion consists of consecutive heteroge-
neous and homogeneous reactions, which can be divided in the following steps [19–22]:
• Drying. Water from biomass is vaporised before combustion can take place. Radiation
from ﬂames and from the stored heat in the body of the combustion unit provide the heat
for drying.
==PAGE:17==
1.4. Energy Recovery from Biomass
5
• Devolatilisation and volatile combustion. Once the dry biomass reaches temperatures be-
tween 200 and 350 ◦C, volatile gases are released, react with oxygen from the supplied air
and burn producing a yellow ﬂame. In terms of energy demands this is a self-supported
process, since heat from the burning gases is used to dry the fresh biomass and release
further volatile gases. After this step, there is a remaining solid material called char.
• Oxidation. The char formed in the previous stage is burned at around 800 ◦C. Oxygen is
needed at both the ﬁre bed for the oxidation of carbon, and above the ﬁre bed to oxidise
carbon monoxide and form carbon dioxide. A long enough residence time is required to
consume the fuel completely.
The combustion process is completed once all the energy in the fuel has been extracted.
To ensure a complete combustion, the amount of air must be greater than the stoichiometric
requirement, as it lowers the presence of carbon monoxide in the ﬂue gas. For continuous
combustion systems, the consecutive reactions occur simultaneously at different zones in the
furnace. The time spent for each reaction depends on combustion conditions, temperature and
fuel size, and it is common that these phases overlap for large particles.
If there are no diffusion limitations, the third step is the slowest one and hence, kinetic
processes control the reaction rate. In this case, the burning rate is exponentially related to
temperature.
Depending on the relative velocities of fuel particles and gas, and the presence of an inter-
vening heat transfer medium, three main types of combustion reactors can be used for heat and
power generation [23–25]:
• Fixed bed combustors. Biomass fuel is placed on a grate, forming a ﬁxed bed, where pri-
mary air passes through holes in the grate. Drying, gasiﬁcation and charcoal combustion
take place at this zone of the reactor. Then, combustible gasses produced are burned after
secondary air addition has taken place. These kind of reactors are used for coarse and
uneven particle sizes.
• Fluidised bed combustors. Biomass and an inert medium (normally sand) are introduced
from a lateral of the combustor and air is supplied from the bottom. A self-mixing sus-
pension of air and solid-bed is formed and combustion takes place. Compared to pul-
verised fuel technologies, a relatively coarse and wet material can be used. Depending
on the ﬂuidization velocity, bubbling ﬂuidised bed (BFB) and circulating ﬂuidised bed
(CFB) combustion can be distinguished.
• Pulverised fuel combustors. Biomass is grinded to small particles of diameter smaller than
2 mm. Fuel and primary air are mixed before entering in the combustion chamber. Then,
combustion takes place while the fuel is in suspension and secondary air addition makes
gas burnout.
Biomass combustion can generate power by using either closed thermal systems or open
processes [26]. In closed thermal processes, a heat exchanger separates physically the com-
bustion area and the power generation cycle. Due to the separation between fuel and engine,

the engine is just in contact with a clean process medium without undesired elements that can
cause damage to it. Hence, closed cycles are a good option for solid fuels.
On the other hand, open cycles are normally applied to liquid or gaseous fuels used in
internal combustion engines and gas turbines. The fuel is burned either directly inside an
internal combustion engine or in a continuous way in an external combustion chamber for
then be expanded by an open gas turbine. Since separation of particles and metals from the hot
ﬂue gases is required, the use of solid fuels in internal combustion engines is technically not
feasible.
Due to elements that may damage engines, power production from biomass combustion
are based on the following closed thermal cycles: steam turbines and steam engines used as
expansion engines in the Rankine cycle; steam turbines used in an organic Rankine Cycle;
stirling engines; closed gas turbines using compressed air; and closed gas turbines using a heat
transfer to compress air.
Among the technologies stated above, steam turbines are the most developed and used
technology for applications in thermal power station and for combined heat and power (CHP)
generation. The working principle is to use the heat generated in a combustion process to
produce high-pressure steam in a boiler (20-250 bar) which is then expanded through the ex-
pansion engine to provide mechanical power and drive and electricity generator. A combined
heat and power plant (CHP) can achieve an overall plant efﬁciency around 90 % [22].
1.4.2
Pyrolysis
Pyrolysis is the thermochemical decomposition of large hydrocarbon molecules of biomass
into smaller molecules of gas, liquid and char which occurs in the total absence of oxidising
agents, at temperatures between 300 and 650 ◦C [27]. Biomass pyrolysis yields a liquid fraction,
known as tar, non-condensable gases such as carbon monoxide, carbon dioxide, hydrogen and
methane, and char as products. Generally, the pyrolysis process reaction can be described as in
(1.2):

Depending on the heating rate, pyrolysis can be either slow or fast. If the time required to
heat the fuel to the pyrolysis temperature is longer than the characteristic pyrolysis reaction
time, it is considered slow pyrolysis. The opposite applies for fast pyrolysis. Whereas slow py-
rolysis is used for char production, fast pyrolysis is mainly used for the production of pyrolysis
oil and gas.
To maximise the yield of liquid products, low temperature, high heating rate and short
gas residence time process are required. When gas is the target product, high temperature,
moderate heating rate and long gas residence time are preferred [28].
According to Basu [27], the biomass pyrolysis process can be divided by temperature into
four different steps, although with some overlaps:

• Drying (∼100 ◦C). This is the ﬁrst step of the pyrolysis process and here free moisture and
loosely bound water is evaporated for allowing the heat to be conducted into the biomass
interior.
• Initial stage (100-300 ◦C). This stage is also known as torrefaction, which is a kind of mild
pyrolysis. Biomass is dehydrated in an exothermic way, with the release of water and
low-molecular-weight gases such as CO and CO2.
• Intermediate stage (>200 ◦C). This is the primary pyrolysis and it occurs at temperatures
between 200 and 600 ◦C. Char (primary char), condensable gases (precursors of pyrolysis
oil) and non-condensable gases are produced from the decomposition of large molecules.
• Final stage (∼300-900 ◦C). At this ﬁnal step, there is a secondary cracking of volatiles into
char and non-condensable gases. Additional char (secondary char) and gases are formed
if large-molecular-weight condensable gases reside in the biomass long enough to crack.
If condensable gases are removed quickly from the reaction site, they are condensed in
the downstream reactor as tar.
Different kind of pyrolysers, which are based on the gas-solid contacting mode, have been
developed in order to maximise the yield of gas and liquid products in a continuous process:
• Moving Bed Pyrolysers. These pyrolysers operate in batch mode and their main product is
char. They usually have a slow heating rate and a long residence time of the product in
the pyrolisis area.
• Bubbling Bed Pyrolysers. Milled biomass is introduced into a bubbling bed of inert solids
like hot sand. An inert gas such as recycled ﬂue gas ﬂuidise the bed while allowing an
intense mixing between inert solids and biomass, with a uniform temperature control.
The required heat for the process can be provided either by burning part of the product
gas or by burning the solid char in a separate chamber. The residence time of the solids is
much higher than that of the gas in the pyrolyser. Entrained char particles are separated
from the product gas using cyclones.
• Circulating Fluidised Bed Pyrolysers. The operating principle of this kind of pyrolisers is the
same than that of bubbling bed, except that the bed is expanded and solids are continu-
ously recycled around an external loop comprising a cyclone and loop seal. A uniform
mixing along the entire height of the unit and a good temperature control is provided by
the riser, which operates in a special hydrodynamic regime known as fast bed. Thus, the
superﬁcial gas velocity is higher than that in bubbling bed. Gas and solids move up the
reactor with some degree of internal reﬂuxing, so the residence time of biomass particles
is longer than that of the gas and a large biomass throughput is achieved.
Depending on the design conﬁguration, the pyrolysers metinoned above can be further
subdivided in ablative pyrolysers, rotating-cone pyrolysers and vacuum pyrolysers.
The pyrolysis products that are the most convenient for heat and power generation are
liquid and gas products by using furnaces and boilers. Although they are less efﬁcient than

1.4.3
Gasiﬁcation
Gasiﬁcation is a thermochemical process which converts a solid carbonaceous material such as
biomass, coal or petroleum coke into gaseous products, char, ash and condensable compounds
[30–32]. This conversion process is carried out in the presence of a gasifying agent (air, pure
oxygen, CO2 or steam) and it takes place by means of partial oxidation of the carbon present in
the feeding material [30, 33, 34]. Gasiﬁcation is a key process to recover energy from biomass.
The combustible product gas is usually fed to a gas engine, boiler or turbine to produce electric-
ity and heat [35]. The gasiﬁcation reaction could be represented in a simpliﬁed way as shown
in (1.3) [36]:
Biomass + Gasifying agent −→H2 + CO + CO2 + HC(g) + H2O(g) + Tar(l) + Char(s)
(1.3)
The quality and properties of the product gas depend on the feedstock properties, gasifying
agent, temperature, pressure inside the reactor and the reactor design [37]. When air is used as
a gasifying agent, nitrogen is present in the product gas and it lowers its heating value [30]. A
low quality product gas with a poor energy content (HHV < 7 MJ/m3) is produced and thus,
used in boilers, gas turbines or internal combustion engines. However, a better quality product
gas (HHV ≈10 - 18 MJ/m3) is obtained when oxygen is used as a gasifying agent, but it is more
expensive due to high oxygen production costs [30, 32].
The main gasiﬁcation process reactions are endothermic, but some exothermic reactions
also take place. The required energy in allothermal reactors is supplied externally, whereas
in autothermal reactors the energy comes from partial combustion of the biomass [33]. Even
though the chemistry of biomass gasiﬁcation is quite complex, the process can be divided in
four different stages [30, 31, 33, 38]:
• Drying. Fresh biomass has normally a moisture content between 30 and 60 % and may
exceed 90 % in some types of biomass. This step takes place at temperatures from 100 ◦C
to 200 ◦C and it is crucial to guarantee a high energy efﬁciency in the process. Every
kilogram of water in biomass requires 2260 kJ to be vaporised and that energy is not
recovered [31]. According to P. Basu [38], the moisture content in biomass for gasiﬁcation
should range between 10 and 15 %.

• Devolatilisation (pyrolysis). Once the biomass is dried, it starts to decompose thermally in
the absence of oxygen or air, so the volatile matter of biomass is reduced. This process
occurs at temperatures between 150 and 400 ◦C and it results in the formation of a solid
carbonaceous material known as "char" and gases (condensable and non-condensable).
At a sufﬁciently low temperatures some gases condense to yield liquid tars.
• Oxidation (partial combustion). Part of the biomass, gases and char are oxidised in con-
ditions of lack of oxygen with respect to the stoichiometric ratio. This stage gives the
necessary thermal energy to carry out the endothermic processes and to keep the opera-
tive temperature at the required value.
• Reduction. All the products produced in the previous pyrolysis and oxidation stages react
between them to form the ﬁnal gas. These reactions are mostly endothermic and they
occur in a temperature range between 800 and 1200 ◦C.

In up-draft gasiﬁers the biomass is fed from the top, the gasifying agent is fed from the

• Fluidised Bed Gasiﬁers. In this case the fuel is introduced from the top of the reactor with

together in the reactor chamber. The speed of the gas in the reactor is high enough to
entrain all the particles of fuel.
• Twin Fluidised Bed Gasiﬁers. In order to produce a gas with a higher caloriﬁc power, two
reactors are used. The ﬁrst reactor acts as a pyrolyser, which is heated with sand or hot
inert material from the second reactor. The second one obtains its heat by burning char
from the ﬁrst reactor.
One of the easiest processes to obtain energy from product gas is by burning it in a conven-
tional steam cycle [33]. Steam cycles are used for electricity production in conventional thermal
power plants, and the overall electricity energy efﬁciency is limited by the theoretical limit of
the steam turbine [39]. It is possible to reach higher energy efﬁciency by using co-generation
units for heat re-use. Co-combustion of the gasiﬁcation gas with conventional fuels is another
possibility to produce electricity, with an overall electrical efﬁciency of 35 % [40] with a 5-15 %
addition of biomass primary products [41, 42].
Fuel combustion in gas turbines could reach up to 40 % electrical conversion efﬁciency,
higher than that provided by steam cycles [43–45].
However, the gas must be rigorously
cleaned and it must have a caloriﬁc value higher than 4 MJ/m3 to avoid the deterioration of

in a topping cycle. Then, steam is generated through a heat recovery steam generator, using
the hot ﬂue gas from the turbine. Finally, additional electricity is generated in a steam turbine.
If the gas produced is fed to an internal combustion engine, an overall electricity efﬁciency
of 35-45 % might be obtained. Likewise in gas turbines, the gas must be cleaned and it must
have a caloriﬁc value grater than 4 MJ/m3. However, gas used for gas turbines needs a higher
purity in terms of tar and particulate contents than the one used for internal combustion en-
gines [33]. The main challenge is to integrate a biomass gasiﬁcation plant with a CHP system,
since tar must be removed from the product gas. The energetic output is normally one-third
electricity and two-thirds heat [35].

1.4.4
Anaerobic Digestion
Anaerobic digestion is a biochemical conversion process in which organic material is decom-
posed by microorganisms in the absence of oxygen into an energy-rich gas (biogas), composed
of 50 % methane (the energy-providing component) and 50 % carbon dioxide [48]. Some liq-
uid and solids residues, called digestate, are also formed due to the presence of some inerts
compounds in the organic matter. The overall process can be described by the chemical reac-
tion (1.4), where simple molecules of organic material like glucose are biochemically decom-
posed into methane and carbon dioxide by the anaerobic microorganisms:
C6H12O6 −→3 CO2 + 3 CH4
(1.4)
Three different groups of bacteria -fermentative, acetogenic and methanogenic, degrade
organic matter during the anaerobic digestion process. The ﬁrst two bacteria families are in
charge of converting large molecules such as cellulose, proteins and lipids into simpler inter-
mediates. Then, these products are further degraded to methane and carbon dioxide by the
methanogenic bacteria. Equilibrium among the three bacteria groups is crucial for the anaer-
obic digestion, and it is highly inﬂuenced by temperature and pH. The majority of digesters
operate at around 35 ◦C, which is the mesophilic temperature regime, whereas the rest operate
in the thermophilic regime, at around 55 ◦C. As long as the temperature of peak microbial ac-
tivity is not reached, higher operating temperatures produce greater metabolic activity within
each regime. Regarding to the pH, optimal values are between 6.8 and 7.2 [48]. The anaerobic
digestion process can be divided in four metabolic steps [49]:
• Hydrolysis. Large and complex molecules such as carbohydrates, proteins and lipids can-
not be taken up into microbial cells and have to be hydrolised ﬁrst. Hydrolytic bacteria
excrete hydrolases that convert these large molecules into oligomeric, di- and monomeric
compounds. For brewers’ spent grains, where cellulose is the major constituent of the
feedstock, its hydrolysis is the rate-limiting step in overall anaerobic digestion process [50].
• Acidogenesis. Water-soluble products from the hydrolysis step are fermented and a mix-
ture of different organic acids, alcohols, carbon dioxide and hydrogen is produced.
• Acetogenesis. Products from the previous step are further transformed into acetate. Hy-
drogen, carbon dioxide and C1 compounds are also formed from the oxidation of alcohols
and organic acids. These reactions are thermodynamically favoured by a low concentra-
tion of hydrogen
• Methanogenesis. Finally, methane is formed by anaerobic microorganisms that belong to
Euryarchaeota. This is normally the rate-limiting step when easily degradable organic
matter (i.e. dairy wastewater) is used. Methyl compounds like methanol are the sub-
strate for this step. Additionally, acetate can be directly cleaved in methane and carbon
dioxide (aceticlastic methanogenesis) or ﬁrst converted to hydrogen and carbon dioxide
by syntrophic acetate oxidisers and then to methane by hydrogenotrophic methanogens.

Brewers’ spent grains have a high content in fermentable sugar, which makes them deteri-
orate rapidly due to microbial activity [51], and a high ﬁbre content, that makes digestion more
difﬁcult. In the literature it was found that anaerobic digestion of brewers’ spent grains is just
efﬁcient when it is separated into a hydrolytic and a methanogenic step [6].
The growth rate of the bacteria carrying out the decomposition of the organic material un-
der anaerobic conditions is low because the bacteria, for their own survival, just need a small
amount of energy compared to the amount of energy present within the biomass feedstock. In
addition to this, these bacteria produce little heat and that is why the substrate used in biogas
plants need to be heated. The typical biomass composition is of about one half to maximum
two third methane and one third to one half carbon dioxide, depending on the biomass com-
position and process conditions. The energy content of the biogas is given by the amount of
methane and it ranges from 14 to 29 MJ/m3.
Compared to liquid-state biomass, the digestion of solid biomass can improve the process
performance because it allows a longer retention time. Two different types of reactors can be
distinguished: plug ﬂow reactors and batch operated single stage and two stage solid-state
reactors [52]. A disadvantage of continuously operated high solids reactors is that for mixing
and conveying their content, they have a relatively high energy demand. In batch operated
solid-state digesters, there is no agitation and solid-liquid separation occurs spontaneously.
Whereas the solid phase forms a ﬁxed bed, the liquid phase transfers metabolites and anaerobic
inoculum. If a single-stage reactor is used, this liquid phase (leachate) is recirculated to the
same reactor where it is produced. A two stage system is characterised when in the solid-state
reactor just hydrolysis and acidiﬁcation occur, and the volatile fatty acids enriched liquor is
sent to separate methanogenic reactors [53]. The main avantages of batch operated solid-state
digesters are that they demand less energy than continuous systems and their simplicity and
robustness, which might reduce investment costs. Nevertheless, there are some drawbacks:
ﬂuctuations of gas production and its quality, channelling of the leachate, biogas losses when
emptying the reactors and restricted reactor design such as heights.
There are different routes to produce heat and power from biogas [54–56]: combined heat
and power (CHP) generation, fuel cell technology, stirling engines, micro gas turbines and
organic Rankine cycle process. Among them, combined heat and power generation is one of
the most common utilisation of biogas and efﬁciencies can reach up to 40 % electrical and 50 %
heat [55].
Stirling engines do not required a speciﬁc type of fuel and they need very little maintenance
operations [57]. Electrical and thermal efﬁciencies of stirling engines are 24 % and 72 %, respec-
tively, for units up to 100 kWel [54]. Due to its heat efﬁciency and the fact that biogas does not
need to be puriﬁed, this technology is attractive for heat utilisation.
If the aim is to produce just heat, direct combustion of biogas in a boiler reaches efﬁciencies
up to 82 %. Biogas can be directly used in boilers without upgrading, since many large boilers
are provided with a ﬂue gas desulphurisation unit [58]. On the other hand, the level of H2S
must be below 250 ppm in combined heat and power plants to avoid corrosion of boilers and
deterioration of lubricating oil [59].

2.2
Ultimate Analysis
Elemental analysis is carried out to determine the composition of the hydrocarbon fuel in terms
of its basic elements, except for its moisture content, M. Biomass is mainly composed out of
four main elements: carbon (C), hydrogen (H), oxygen (O) and nitrogen (N), although it can
also contain small amounts of sulphur (S) and chlorine (Cl) [60].
Carbon, hydrogen and nitrogen contents of the samples were analysed by ﬂash combus-
tion using a Thermo Fisher Scientiﬁc FlashEA R⃝1112 organic elemental analyser. Then, the
oxygen content was calculated by difference. The measurement was repeated three times and
the average value was used for calculation. It was followed the procedure established by the
international standard test ISO 16948:2015(E).
2.3
Energy Content
The energy content of the biomass was determined in terms of its higher heating value (HHV),
which is the amount of heat released by the unit mass (initially at 25 ◦C) once it is burned and
the products have returned to a temperature of 25 ◦C. It is important to remark that in the
higher heating value it is included the latent heat of vaporisation of water.
A Parr R⃝Instrument Company 6300 oxygen bomb calorimeter was used to determine the
higher heating value of the dry brewers’ spent grains. Initially, the bomb calorimeter was cali-
brated using benzoic acid tablets. The measurement was repeated three times and the average
value was used for calculation. It was followed the procedure established by the international
standard test ISO 18125:2017.
After determining the higher heating value, it is possible to calculate the lower heating
value (LHV). This last value, also known as the net caloriﬁc value (NCV), is deﬁned as the
amount of heat released by the full combustion of a speciﬁed quantity of fuel less the heat of
vaporisation of the water in the combustion product.

3 Modeling and Simulations
As it has been explained in Chapter 1, four potential routes were analysed to convert the en-
ergy present in brewers’ spent grains into thermal and electric energy: combustion, pyrolysis,
gasiﬁcation and anaerobic digestion. Simulations for the different thermochemical processes
were carried out using the software DNA, whereas the biochemical process (anaerobic diges-
tion), was not simulated but instead, data from a previous study made by Carlsberg Group was
used.
3.1
DNA Software
The software used for simulating the different scenarios for the thermochemical processes was
DNA, acronym of Dynamics Network Analysis. DNA is an energy system and process plant
simulation tool developed by the Department of Mechanical Engineering at Technical Univer-
sity of Denmark [62]. DNA has been developed since 1989 and it is able to handle models of
any kind of energy system based on the control volume approach, normally systems of lumped
parameter components.
It was chosen to make the simulations using this software because it has some important
characteristics such as features for modeling solid fuels of arbitrary composition, medium com-
position can be variables, and no causality is implied on the model input -there is no restriction
of the choice of inputs and outputs. Apart from that, DNA has an extended component library
that includes models of dryers, boilers, pyrolysers, and gasiﬁers, among others, which are cru-
cial to the development of this project. Another advantage is that DNA is a portable software,
since it is written in FORTRAN77, and it is an open source alternative to other simulation tools
such as Aspen Plus.
3.2
Description of the DNA Components
Each of the different scenarios has its own process ﬂow diagram, with its own process units and
its own auxiliary process units. However, some of them are common for different processes,
such as the dryer used for the thermochemical treatments. In this section it is described how
each component is modeled and and how it works in the DNA simulation tool.
As all DNA components are modeled according to mass and energy balances, it is impor-
tant to notice that mass and energy streams in the DNA simulation tool are given following the
sign criteria for mass and energy ﬂows established by the International Union of Pure and Applied

Chemistry (IUPAC). According to this criteria, a positive sign indicates that such stream is en-
tering in the system (or component in this case), whilst a negative sign means that the stream
is leaving the system or component.
3.2.1
Dryer
A dryer is a unit used to remove moisture present in a solid material. Although there are
different kind of drying processes, the most common is the convective drying. In this drying
process, a hot convective medium (i.e. air, steam) is used to absorb the moisture present in a
solid material. As it is shown in Figure 3.1, this component in the DNA simulation tool has
three inputs and two outputs.
FIGURE 3.1: Dryer in DNA.
Inputs and Outputs
The inputs are the solid material to be dried, the ﬂuid convective medium and the total energy
required for the drying process. It is necessary to have completely deﬁned the composition,
mass ﬂow, temperature, pressure, moisture content and heat capacity at constant pressure of
the wet solid material as well as the mass ﬂow, composition and pressure of the ﬂuid convective
medium. In regards to the necessary energy, it is calculated by the DNA software once all the
required variables and parameters are deﬁned.
The outputs are the dried solid material and the stream containing the ﬂuid convective
medium plus the evaporated water from the solid material. It is enough to just specify the
temperature at which these two streams leave the drying chamber.
Parameters
This component requires two parameters: on the one hand, it is necessary to specify the mois-
ture content at which the dried solid material leaves the dryer. On the other hand, it is also
necessary to deﬁne the pressure drop between the entrance and the exit of the dryer.

In the DNA simulation tool, the pyrolysis reactor is modeled according to kinetic models,

gas, tar and char [63]. In this case, the biomass reaction products are gathered in two groups,
volatiles and char (Figure 3.4). Tars are included in the volatiles group and they are formed if
the volatiles are cooled down and thus, tars condense.

parameters, it is important to notice that there is not any drying medium because the pyrolysis
occurs without any oxidising agent.

low pressure stream which generates mechanical energy. Thus, the outputs of the turbine are
the low pressure stream and the work generated. It is required to specify the pressure at which
the outlet stream leaves the turbine.
The parameter that describes the turbine model is the isentropic efﬁciency. The isentropic
efﬁciency of a turbine is deﬁned as the ratio of the actual turbine work to its isentropic turbine
work. In other words, the isentropic efﬁciency evaluates how efﬁciently a real turbine ap-
proximates a corresponding idealised turbine which operates under adiabatic and reversible
conditions. In this idealised process, the work transfer of the system is frictionless, and there is
no transfer of heat or matter.
3.2.11
Electric Generator
A power generator is a device that converts mechanical energy into electric power. In DNA the
generator component is completely described with one input, two outputs and one parameter.
The input is the mechanical energy, for instance, coming from a turbine. The outputs are the

electric power produced and the heat released when the thermal efﬁciency of the generator is
not 100 %. Thus, the required parameter to completely deﬁne this component is the thermal
efﬁciency of the electric generator.
3.2.12
Pump
A pump is a mechanical device that transports a ﬂuid by mechanical work. The component, in
the DNA simulation tool, consists of one input, two outputs and one parameter. The input is
the inlet stream, which is completely deﬁned by means of composition, mass ﬂow, temperature
and pressure. The output is the outlet stream, which is the same ﬂuid as the inlet stream but
with different pressure and temperature. The pressure of the outlet stream must be speciﬁed.
Finally, the pump efﬁciency is the required parameter to be speciﬁed in the model. Pump
efﬁciency is deﬁned as the ratio of the power imparted on the ﬂuid by the pump in relation to
the power supplied to drive the pump.
3.2.13
Splitter
In some processes it is necessary to split a stream into n streams. In such cases a splitter is
used. There is one input, which is the stream to be split, and n outputs, corresponding to the
n streams in which the original one has been split. It is necessary to deﬁne the mass ﬂow,
temperature and pressure of the inlet stream and at least (n-1) mass ﬂows of the outlet streams.
3.2.14
Valve
A valve is a device used to regulate and control the pressure and ﬂow of a ﬂuid. In this project,
valves are used for regulating pressure. In the DNA software, the valve component has one
input, corresponding to the stream entering in the valve, and one output, which is the stream
leaving the valve. It is necessary to deﬁne the mass ﬂow, pressure and either temperature or
enthalpy of the inlet stream, as well as the pressure of the outlet stream.
3.2.15
Heat Node
A heat node is a component that does not exist in the reality, but just in the DNA software. It is
used to link n heat streams in one. It is necessary to know and specify the value of n-1 and the
value of the heat stream at which the other streams are linked. It has one input, the n stream,
and one output, the heat stream at which the other streams converge.
3.2.16

input, one output and three parameters. The input is the non-ideal ﬂuid stream, whose mass
ﬂow, composition, temperature and pressure are given. The output is the ideal gas stream and
just its temperature is deﬁned. Regarding to the parameters, there are seven in total and just

3.4.1
Drying of Brewers’ Spent Grains
In Section 1.3 it has been discussed that the moisture content in biomass must be as low as
possible and always below 15 % for thermochemical conversion routes. It was also discussed
that the most efﬁcient and effective way for drying brewers’ spent grains is the superheated
steam drying, in which superheated steam is used as a drying medium. The superheated steam
drying process is carried out under pressure, which depends on the steam pressure at which
the plant operates. For instance, if a plant requires process steam at 3.5 bar, the drying process
will take place at such pressure, as it is the case of this project.
It was decided to follow the technology developed by the company EnerDry A/S [16], in
which wet solid material is fed continuously into a pressurised vessel (the drying chamber)
by using a rotary valve, that brings the material to a screw conveyor, for then introduce it
into the pressurised vessel. In the vessel, superheated steam (ﬂuidising medium) is blown at
sufﬁcient velocity to form a ﬂuid bed of solid material. Thus, both the ﬂuidising medium and
the heating surfaces provide the energy required for the evaporation of water from the solid
material. Once the moisture has been transferred from the solid to the ﬂuidising medium, the
dried solid particles fall down onto inclined plates and slide down, passing through a gap
between the plates and the conical vessel wall. Then, they arrive to a discharge screw and pass
a rotary valve. On the other hand, the circulating steam is split between the dryer exhaust
-which either can be used as a steam process in the plant or condensed to recover the heat, and
ﬂuidising medium, which is reheated in a heat exchanger supplied with steam. A simpliﬁed
scheme of the superheated steam drying process is shown in Figure 3.10.
It is important to notice that the dryer component in DNA is a black box of what in reality
occurs in a superheating steam dryer. In reality, there is a ﬁx amount of circulating superheated
steam which works in a closed loop. Hence, the mass ﬂow of superheated steam in the DNA
simulation is zero and just the dryer exhaust is the only water stream considered. Additionally,
in the DNA simulation there is just one input which refers to the required energy for the drying
process, unlike the real process. Thus, the input Energy in Figure 3.1 includes the necessary heat
for the heat exchanger, QSuperheater, and the power required by the blower, PShaft, which depends
on the size of the dryer.
After the drying process, the steam in the dryer includes both, the circulating steam and the
steam extracted from the solid material (dryer exhaust). Then, this stream is separated between
the evaporated water and the circulating steam, which passes through a heat exchanger to be
heated up to superheated steam and then it is blown inside the dryer.

Parameters and Conditions
It was decided to dry the spent grains until a 10 % moisture content. This value was chosen
for security reasons, since there is a self ignition risk of the dried material when it leaves the
drying vessel at high temperature. Apart from that, if it is desired to pelletise the dried spent
grains and sell or use them as a solid fuel, a certain amount of moisture is necessary. Thus, to
obtain dry spent grains with 10 % moisture content, it is necessary to remove 2.95 kg of water
per second.
Different sizes of superheated steam dryers have been developed by the Danish company
EnerDry A/S [16]. On the one hand, the size of the dryer depends on the amount of water that
must be removed (evaporation capacity) from the wet solid material. On the other hand, the
supply steam pressure in the superheater depends, at the same time, on the evaporation capac-
ity and size of the dryer. The higher the supply pressure steam is, the higher the temperature
of the circulating steam is achieved and the higher is the drying potential of the steam. Taking
these two factors into account, it was decided to choose the smallest standard dryer that it is
available, the size E, so that it would be possible to obtain a higher supply steam pressure.
Hence, for that size and that evaporation capacity, the supply steam pressure should be 13 bar.
Some breweries use steam at 3.5 bar as supply process steam. Thus, the drying process
is carried out at such pressure and the water evaporated from the spent grains can be either
used as supply steam process or condensed to recover its thermal energy. The spent grains
are pressurised at 3.5 bar by a screw conveyor, whose energy requirement is considered to be
negligible in comparison to the drying process itself.
Finally, the temperature of the spent grains leaving the drying chamber is 100 ◦C and the
temperature of the dryer exhaust is 150 ◦C. These temperatures were calculated by the dryer
company after making an energy and mass balance. It was assumed that the dryer losses 50 kW
of heat.

3.4.2
Combustion of Brewers’ Spent Grains
A very simple solution to obtain thermal energy from brewers’ spent grains is to burn them
directly after they have been dried. The spent grains can be burned in a boiler and obtaine
process steam for the brewery. The process ﬂow diagram is shown in Figure 3.11.
FIGURE 3.11: Flow diagram of the combustion process.
The dried spent grains enter in the combustion chamber at a temperature of 100 ◦C, where
they are mixed with air at 25 ◦C and the combustion reaction takes place. The temperature
of the ﬂue gas is set at 90 ◦C, which is a normal temperature for a commercial boiler. This
temperature is so low compare to the combustion temperature (around 800 ◦C) because the
combustion heat is transferred through the walls of the boiler.
Parameters and Conditions
The parameters of the boiler that must be speciﬁed are the pressure ratio between the ﬂue gas
leaving the boiler and the air entering in it, and the amount of air to stoichiometric amount
of air ratio (lambda). For solid boilers it is common to work from 10 % to 40 % of excess air if
compared to the stoichiometric amount of air. Then, the lambda value is set between 1.1 and
1.4. In this case, it was chosen a lambda value of 1.1. Regarding to the pressure ratio between
the outlet and the inlet streams, it was speciﬁed to be 1, since the boiler works at atmospheric
pressure and both the air and the solid fuel are entering into the boiler at 1 bar.
Not only from the combustion process itself it is possible to recover thermal energy, but also
from cooling down the ﬂue gas. When the ﬂue gas leaves the boiler, it passes through a heat
exchanger in order to cool it down and make water condense. It enters in the cooler at 90 ◦C

3.4.3
Combustion of Brewers’ Spent Grains and Cogeneration
The combustion process explained in the previous section can be linked to a steam cycle and,
apart from producing just thermal energy, it is also possible to produce electric power. This is
a common and simple case of a cogeneration plant. The working principle is to use the heat
of combustion to produce high pressure steam, which passes through a turbine to produce
mechanical energy (work). This turbine is attached to a generator in charge of transforming
mechanical energy into electricity. The low pressure steam is then condensed and pumped
into the boiler. In this process, the circulating steam follows a Rankine cycle, as shown in Fig-
ure 3.12. It is important to notice that two turbines are depicted for DNA simulation purposes,
but in reality just one turbine is used.
FIGURE 3.12: Flow diagram of the combustion and steam cycle process.
Since high pressure steam is produced, it is possible to take advantage of it and use some
of the steam as a supply steam in the drying process. This is made by expanding the high
pressure steam in two stages. First, it is expanded to 13 bar and part of the steam is used as a
supply steam in the heat exchanger of the dryer to produce superheated steam. Afterwards, the
condensate is pumped at 180 bar and goes back to the boiler. On the other hand, the rest of the
steam which also leaves the ﬁrst turbine at 13 bar passes through another turbine to be further
expanded to atmospheric pressure. A generator is attached to both turbines and the mechanical
energy from both expansions is transformed into power. Then, this steam is condensed at 1 bar
and pumped into the boiler at 180 bar. As observed in Figure 3.12, both condensates are mixed
before going back into the boiler.

Parameters and Conditions
The parameters and conditions for the combustion process are exactly the same than in the
previous combustion process. Nevertheless, parameters and conditions for the steam cycle
plant must be speciﬁed.
As it has been mentioned before, the heat of combustion is used to produce high pressure
steam at high temperature. From the combustion process, the amount of thermal energy re-
leased is known and this is the available thermal energy for the steam cycle process. In modern
steam plants, it is possible to work a high pressure, between 150 and 200 bar. According to that,
it was chosen to produce steam at 180 bar and 540 ◦C. Then, this steam is expanded in a ﬁrst
turbine to 13 bar and the leaving steam is split between the steam going to the heat exchanger
in the dryer and the steam going to a second turbine to be expanded to 1 bar. The parameter
required to deﬁne how a turbine works is its isentropic efﬁciency, which in both turbines was
set to 85 %. This last stream passes through a condenser and it is condensed under atmospheric
conditions.
Regarding to the heat exchanger in the dryer, its thermal energy demand is known. How-
ever, it is necessary to set the temperature at which the steam leaves the heat exchanger. From
an energy balance carried out by the company EnerDry A/S, it is known that the condensate
leaves the heat exchanger ﬁve degrees Celsius below its saturation temperature at 13 bar, it
means that the condensate is at 186 ◦C. Since all the necessary heat for the drying process must
be provided by the steam coming from the boiler, and all the temperatures and pressures have
been deﬁned, the software can now calculate the mass ﬂow of steam required for the plant.
3.4.4
Pyrolysis of Brewers’ Spent Grains and Combustion of Volatiles
The pyrolysis plant was designed with the aim of obtaining two substances out of the pyrolysis
of spent grains: char and tar. On the one hand, the pyrolysis process was not optimised to
obtain oil and gas with the maximum amount of carbon, but instead it was designed to obtain
char with a high amount of carbon. On the other hand, the gas obtained was not cooled down
to make tar condense, but instead, it was directly burned to produce thermal energy, as shown
in Figure 3.13. The energy required for the pyrolysis was obtained from the thermal energy
present in the ﬂue gas from the combustion of volatiles.
The reason for maximising the carbon yield in char is to capture carbon in the pyrolysis pro-
cess, store it as a solid biochar and produce a carbon-negative fuel (pyrolysis gas). This concept
was developed by the Danish company Stiesdal A/S and its principle is based on the carbon
cycle. Carbon dioxide from the atmosphere is absorbed by plants during the photosynthesis
process. Then, these plants are used as biomass feed for the pyrolysis process in which pyroly-
sis gases are produced. The residue from the process is a biochar rich in carbon and nutrients
that can be used for enrichment of agricultural soils. The pyrolysis gas is burned and the car-
bon present in them, goes back to the atmosphere as carbon dioxide. Nevertheless, the carbon
which is present in the char is not released and it remains stable in the soil for millennia. Thus,
not only is this biofuel process carbon-neutral but it also is "carbon-negative" and the more fuel

The simulation of the pyrolysis process of the spent grains was carried out at 600 ◦C and at-

speciﬁed to be 18 150 kJ/kg and its speciﬁc heat at constant pressure to be 1.851 kJ/(kg K). Re-
garding to the char, its hydrogen and nitrogen contents were set to zero because it was speciﬁed
that all the char in a dry and ash free basis was totally made out of carbon. Its speciﬁc heat at
constant pressure was established at 1.276 kJ/(kg K). Then, the char to fuel production ratio
was set to 0.2628, which is the sum of the ash content and the ﬁxed carbon content (Table 2.1)
of the spent grains from Carlsberg 1. The energy efﬁciency of the process was assumed to be
100 %, which means that all the energy present in the spent grains is transformed. Finally, the
heat losses were set to 1 % of the energy input.
After the pyrolysis process, the combustion of volatiles takes place. It was deﬁned the
temperature of the combustion ﬂue gas to be 90 ◦C and the temperature of the inlet air was set
to be 25 ◦C, like in the combustion of solid spent grains explained in Section 3.4.2. Then, the ﬂue
gas passes through a cooler to recover its thermal energy and use it in the pyrolysis process,
but also to remove condensate. The temperature at which the ﬂue gas leaves the cooler was set
to 45 ◦C, which is under the water dew point, 63.65 ◦C, so water condenses and it is removed.

3.4.6
Anaerobic Digestion of Brewers’ Spent Grains
Anaerobic digestion of brewers’ spent grains is the only route that was not simulated using the
DNA software. Instead, numbers were determined using data from a previous study carried
out by Carlsberg Group. Anaerobic digestion is commonly used in the brewing sector to process
waste water and produce biomethane. However, this can only supply a fraction of the ther-
mal energy requirement of a brewery. Supplementing the digestion of waste water with the
digestion of the brewers’ spent grains was reviewed.
The spent grains are ﬁrst added to a hydrolysis tank where they are mixed and biodegrada-
tion of long chain compounds into short chain compounds takes place. The material is then fed
to a digester which mixes the substrate with appropriate microorganisms, to facilitate diges-
tion. A post-digestion tank ensures maximum yield, at the same time that it acts as a storage
tank. The biogas is then fed to a desulphurisation unit to remove hydrogen sulphide, before
condensing out water vapour. A typical scheme of the anaerobic digestion plant is shown
in Figure 3.16. An important advantage of this route is that the by-product generated in the
anaerobic digestion process, the digestate, can be applied to agricultural land.
FIGURE 3.16: Typical components in a biogas plant.
According to the study carried out by Carlsberg Group, just with the digestion of brewers’
spent grains it is possible to obtain a volume of biogas of 0.154 Nm3 per kilogram of wet spent
grains, with an energy content of 0.922 kW h per kilogram of wet spent grains.
Two cases were considered for the biogas usage. On the one hand, it is very common that
heat in breweries is generated by burning natural gas in gas boilers, so the biogas produced by
the anaerobic digestors could be mixed with natural gas and be burned in those gas boilers to
produce heat. On the the other hand, the biogas can be used to feed a gas engine to produce
both heat and electric power, with a 42 % of electrical efﬁciency.

Combustion of Brewers’ Spent Grains
Biomass combustion is a very common and easy way to obtain thermal energy from it. In this
case, the dried spent grains are directly ﬁred in a boiler and just thermal energy is produced.
The combustion heat can be used in the brewery to heat water and/or produce steam for being
used in the beer production process.
Since this process does not need any steam, it was assumed that all the dryer exhaust is
condensed. An important parameter to take into consideration is the amount of air to amount
of stoichiometric air ratio. In combustion processes the amount of air is normally in excess if it
is compared to its stoichiometric value. This is made to assure that all the fuel is burned and
also to reduce the emissions of carbon monoxide, since this compound is oxidised to carbon
dioxide. In this case, the combustion reaction takes place with a 10 % excess of air, so the air to
stoichiometric air ratio is set to 1.1. The mass ﬂow of air was calculated by the DNA software
according to the composition and mass ﬂow of the solid fuel and the composition of the air. To
burn 1.01 kg/s of spent grains with 10 % of moisture, it was necessary to use 6.44 kg/s of air at
25 ◦C.
A combustion process normally reaches temperatures around 800 ◦C, but in this case it was
set that both the combustion ﬂue gas and the ash leave the reactor at 90 ◦C. This is in such
way because the reactor is not adiabatic and combustion heat is transferred through the reactor
walls and exchanged with a cold ﬂuid stream which is heated up. Thus, in commercial boilers
it is very common that the ﬂue gas leaves the combustion chamber below 100 ◦C and above the
water dew point, to avoid condensation in the reactor. In this scenario, the combustion ﬂue gas

Comparison Between the Different Scenarios
In the previous section all the results from the different scenarios have been presented. In this
section those results are represented in a bar chart, as shown in Figure 3.32, compared and
discussed.
The reference brewery requires 18.18 MW of heat and the only process that would be able to
fulﬁl the demand is the direct combustion of dried spent grains. In fact, if all the spent grains
were burned, a surplus of 0.32 MW of heat would be completely lost. In this case, it would
be convenient to burn just the necessary amount of spent grains to fulﬁl completely the heat
demand of the reference brewery and destine the left dried spent grains to other purposes. The
main advantage of this scenario is that the technology used is completely developed and all
the reference brewery heat demand would be covered.
In the steam cycle process, less heat is available for the brewery but instead, power is gen-
erated by a steam cycle. The main advantage of this scenario is that the technology is also well
developed and electricity is generated, whose price is almost three times higher than the price
for generating heat.
The pyrolysis treatment, gasiﬁcation and anaerobic digestion (boiler) scenarios produce all
of them almost the same amount of thermal energy. In the pyrolysis process, just the volatiles

are burned in a gas boiler, which represents barely half of the carbon present in the spent grains.

boilers are currently in use for for heat production, it would not be necessary to purchase them.

4 Economic Assessment
After determining the technical aspects of the different scenarios, it is crucial to make an eco-
nomic evaluation of each of them to determine which one is the optimal in terms of capital and
production costs, and proﬁtability.
To carry out the economic assessment, it was followed the International Recommended
Practice No. 18R-97 by the Association for the Advancement of Cost Engineering (AACE) [70],
a cost estimate practice to evaluate, approve and/or fund projects. According to the cost esti-
mate classiﬁcation matrix by AACE, this project belongs to the Class 4 because it is a study of
feasibility and the deﬁnition of the overall project is below 15 %. Thus, a typical cost estimat-
ing method for this kind of projects is equipment factored, with typical accuracy ranges from
−15 % to −30 % on the low side and from 20 % to 50 % on the high side.

A very important section in a complete economic evaluation is the estimation of revenue, which

Product prices are established by a market study and they cannot be chosen randomly. In

grains and use it in the plant as heat and power, no sales are made. The only scenario in which
it could be possible to sell a product, would be the scenario in which the spent grains are just

4.3
Estimation of Total Product Cost
The total product cost (TPC) is the third major component of an economic analysis and it in-
cludes the total of all the costs of operating the plant, selling the products, recovering the capi-
tal investment and contributing to corporate functions such as management and research and
development. The total product cost can be divided into two categories: manufacturing or
operating costs and general expenses.
==PAGE:75==
4.3. Estimation of Total Product Cost
63
Likewise in the estimation of capital investment costs, accuracy is also important in esti-
mating the total product cost and all the costs associated with making and selling the product
must be included. Total product costs were calculated on annual basis to avoid any effect of
seasonal variation.
4.3.1
Operating Costs
The manufacturing costs include all the expenses directly related to the manufacturing opera-
tion or the physical equipment of a process plant itself. These expenses, at the same time, are
subdivided into three categories: variable production costs, ﬁxed charges, and plant overhead
costs.
Variable Production Costs
These costs include expenses directly associated with the manufacturing operation costs, such
as raw materials, operating labour, operating supervision and clerical assistance, utilities, main-
tenance and repairs, operating supplies, laboratory charges, and patents and royalties.
• Raw materials. Raw materials are those materials that are directly consumed in making
the ﬁnal product. In this case the raw material is the brewers’ spent grains. Even though
the spent grains are a by-product in the beer manufacturing process, it is currently sold.
If this by-product is used as a raw material for energy conversion, there are no revenues
from its selling. Thus, the price of the raw material was set to be the price at which the
reference brewery could sell the spent grains.
• Operating labour. It includes the quantity of human resources required to run the plant.
For chemical processes, operating labour usually amounts from about 10 % to 20 % of the
total product cost. Typical labour requirements for the drying process where found in
[74] and used for this project.
• Operating supervision and clerical assistance. For a manufacturing operation, a certain amount
of direct supervisory assistance is always required. The amount of this kind of labour de-
pends on the complexity of the operation, product quality standard and total amount of
operating labour. The cost for direct supervision averages 15 % of the cost for operating
labour.
• Utilities. In utilities it is included the amount of steam, electricity, heat, compressed air,
etc. that are required to carry out the processes. The utility requirements were determined
from material and energy balances calculated for the process.
• Maintenance and repairs. Annual costs for equipment maintenance and repairs normally
ranges from 2 % to 10 % of the ﬁxed capital investment, with 6 % being a reasonable value.
• Operating supplies. Costs of consumable items such as charts, lubricants, test chemicals,
etc are included in the operating supplies costs. The annual cost for these kinds of sup-
plies is around 10 % of the total cost for maintenance and repairs.

• Laboratory charges. In this manufacturing cost it is covered the the cost of laboratory tests
for control of operations and for product quality control. This cost can be taken as 10 %
to 20 % of the operating labour costs.
• Patents and royalties. This cost covers the payment for patent rights or a royalty based on
the amount of material produced. A rough approximation of patent and royalty costs for
patented processes is 0 % to 6 % of the total product cost.
Fixed Charges
The ﬁxed charges include the costs that do not change at all with the mount of production.
These costs include local property taxes, ﬁnancing arrangements (loan interest), insurance, rent
and depreciation.
• Local property taxes. These taxes depend on the particular locality of the plant and the re-
gional laws. It was assumed that annual property taxes are already paid by the reference
brewery and thus, this charge was neglected for this project.
• Financing. The ﬁnancing cost includes the compensation to be paid for the use of bor-
rowed capital, it means, the interest. In this project it was assumed that enough capital is
available to carry out the construction of the plant and no capital was borrowed.,
• Property insurance. This cost depends on the kind of process being carried out and on the
extent of available protection facilities. It was rated 1 % of the ﬁxed capital investment
per year.
• Rent. The amount of money to be paid for rented land and buildings is include in the rent
cost. It was assumed that enough own land was available.
• Depreciation. It is necessary to consider the time value of money for the life time of the
plant. The equipment, buildings, and other material objects comprising a manufacturing
plant require an initial investment that must be paid back, and this is done by charging
depreciation and manufacturing expenses. It was used the Modiﬁed Accelerated Cost Re-
covery System (MACRS) methodology for depreciation calculation, in which the amount
of depreciation changes year by year. The recovery period was assumed to be 7 years.
Plant Overhead Costs
Plant overhead costs include the expenditures required for routine plant services. Nonman-
ufacturing machinery, equipment, and buildings are necessary for many of the general plant
services, and the ﬁxed charges and direct costs for these items are part of the plant overhead
costs. It was assumed that no plant overhead costs were present, since these costs are already
covered by the brewing process.

4.3.2
General Expenses
In the operations of a company, apart from the manufacturing costs, other general expenses
are involved. These general expenses can be classiﬁed in administrative expenses, distribution
and marketing expenses, and research and development expenses.
Administrative Costs
The administrative costs include salaries and wages for administrators, secretaries, accoun-
tants, computer support staff, engineering and legal personnel, etc. These costs vary from
plant to plant and depend whether the plant under consideration is a new one or an addition
to an existing plant. For this project, it was assumed that these costs were 10 % of the operating
labour costs.
Distribution and Marketing Costs
These types of general expenses are incurred in the process of selling and distributing the var-
ious products. The distribution and marketing expenses were assumed to be 5 % of the total
product cost for the case in which the spent grains are dried and sold, and zero for the rest of
the scenarios because no other products are being sold in comparison to the current situation.
Research and Development Costs
In all industries new methods and products are constantly being developed as a result of re-
search and development. Research and development costs include salaries and wages for all
personnel directly connected with this type of work, ﬁxed and operating expenses for all ma-
chinery and equipment involved, costs for materials and supplies, and consultants’ fees. This
cost was assumed to be 2 % of the total product cost.
4.3.3
Operation and Maintenance (O&M) costs found in literature
Almost all the data used for calculating the total product cost was obtained from the catalogues

the scheduled lifetime are also included, whereas reinvestments to extend the life beyond the

The variable O&M costs (e/MWh) include consumption of auxiliary materials (water, lu-

4.4.4
Discounted Cash Flow Rate of Return
The discounted cash ﬂow rate of return (DCFR), is the return obtained from an investment
in which all investments and cash ﬂows are discounted. It is determined by setting the NPV
given by Equation (4.6) equal to zero and solving for the discount rate that satisﬁes the resulting
relation.
The DCFR is only of concern when the project rates favourably compared to the value of
mar used in calculating the net present value. If the NPV that is calculated equals zero, then
the mar used is the DCFR. However, if the NPV is greater than zero, then the DCFR must be
calculated. When the NPV is favourable, the DCFR will necessarily be favourable and will be
the actual earning rate of the investment. A project that has a higher discount rate of return
(DCFR) will have a higher earnings potential compared to a project with lower DCFR.

4.6.2
Proﬁtability Analysis
After presenting the results of the capital and operational expenditures for each scenario, the
proﬁtability analysis results are presented in this section. This analysis was carried out in func-
tion of the price of wet spent grains. As it was explained at the beginning of this report, spent
grains might be sold to farmers to be used as feed for the cattle. Thus, the sale of spent grains
would generate additional incomes to the brewery. Sometimes, it is so difﬁcult for a brewery
to handle the amount of spent grains generated that they are given for free and farmers just
accept to pay for their transport [11]. For this reason, the proﬁtability analysis was carried out
for a range of prices from zero to sixty euros per tonne of wet spent grains.

5 Conclusions
In this Master’s Thesis project it has been analysed, simulated and discussed different routes
(combustion, pyrolysis, gasiﬁcation and anaerobic digestion) to use the main by-product gener-
ated by the brewing process, the spent grains, as energy resource. This by-product is generated
in huge amounts and fast disposal is necessary to avoid bacterial degradation. Brewers’ spent
grains were characterised and it was found that they present a high energy content. In fact,
with the production rate of this by-product it is possible to cover, theoretically, all the reference
brewery thermal energy demands. Nevertheless, its moisture content is so high (77 %) than it
is necessary to dry them before any thermochemical treatment.
The drying technology chosen for this project was a superheated steam drying. This kind of
drying can be considered neutral in terms of energy, since all the energy supplied to the dryer
can be recovered in form of heat by the dryer exhaust.
The thermochemical routes were simulated using the DNA software, a simulation tool de-
veloped by the Department of Mechanical Engineering of Technical University of Denmark.
By simulating the different scenarios it was found that all the thermal energy required by the
brewery (18.18 MW) can be covered by the combustion of spent grains. The steam cycle sce-
nario can generate 13.97 MW of thermal energy and 4.10 MW of electricity, which are able to
cover 76.85 % and 42.01 % of the thermal and electric brewery energy demands, respectively.
With the pyrolysis of spent grains and the combustion of volatiles it is possible to generate
11.05 MW of thermal energy, corresponding to 60.77 % of the brewery heat demand. The last
thermochemical treatment, gasiﬁcation of spent grains and cogeneration plant, can generate
10.57 MW and 6.37 MW of heat and electric power, respectively. This is the scenario that gen-
erates the largest amount of electricity, being able to cover over 65 % of the brewery electricity
requirements and 58.16 % of the brewery heat demands. Regarding to the biochemical treat-
ment of spent grains, 10.20 MW of heat (56 % of the brewery heat demands) can be provided
to the brewery when the biogas is burned in a gas boiler. If the biogas is fed into a gas engine
both heat and electric power are generated. Hence, the gas engine generates 5.91 MW of heat
and 4.28 MW of electric power, which cover 32.53 % and 44 % of the reference brewery thermal
and electric demands, respectively.
Regarding to the economic feasibility for each scenario, the direct combustion of dried spent
grains is just feasible as long as the price at which the wet spent grains are currently sold to
farmers is of is under e10. For the steam cycle, this scenario is feasible as long as the price
of the spent grains is under 11 e. Both the gasiﬁcation and the pyrolysis scenarios were found
not to be feasible at all as long as the wet spent grains have an economic value in the market.
Even though in the gasiﬁcation process electricity is produced, its capital investment cost is so
large that it is not possible to recover it. For large investments, it was found that the processes
==PAGE:92==
80
Chapter 5. Conclusions
with electricity generation are more feasible that the ones in which just heat is produced. Both
biochemical treatments were found to be the most favourable from the economic point of view,
although the energetic performance was not as efﬁcient as in the other scenarios. In terms of
energy recovery for self-consumption, none of the scenarios studied is favourable for invest-
ment as long as the price of the wet spent grains is greater than e17 per tonne. For low prices,
the most favourable scenario is the anaerobic digestion of spent grains and the combustion of
the biogas in a gas engine.
The most sustainable scenario could be considered to be the one in which the pyrolysis
of brewers’ spent grains occurs as long as all the brewery thermal energy requirements were
covered. The reason is that carbon is captured in form of char and it cannot be released to the
atmosphere in form of carbon dioxide. The issue of this process is that it cannot cover all the
reference brewery heat demands by itself, so additional fuels like natural gas are needed. A
sustainability analysis for the production of pyrolysis fuels applying the concept developed by
Stiesdal A/S would be necessary to know its real impact and to assess how much sustainable
and feasible is this novel concept.
It is possible to add value to the brewers’ spent grains by just drying them on-site. Bacterial
degradation occurs rapidly due to the high moisture content, so if the spent grains are dried
immediately after the lauter stage, it is possible to enlarge their lifetime and avoid storage prob-
lems. By drying them, the transport of spent grains is cheap because there is a density reduction
and consequently, it contributes to reduce the environmental impact in their transport.
Finally, this feasibility study was analysed for ﬁxed electricity and heat generation prices,
which vary signiﬁcantly depending on the country in which the brewery operates in, and have
a strong inﬂuence on the economics of the scenarios. Thus, looking towards future works, it
would be useful to carry out both a sensitivity and uncertainty analysis for the economic assess-
ment. Additionally, through a sustainability study (i.e. life cycle assessment) for each scenario
would evaluate in a more exhaustive way the environmental impact of each considered tech-
nology.
