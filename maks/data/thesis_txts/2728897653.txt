Abstract
This research explores the development of a parametric design process, specifically tai-
lored for modular studio apartments, with a keen focus on optimizing energy performance
and Indoor Environment Quality (IEQ). As modular construction gains traction, charac-
terized by its replicated volume modules, there is a pressing need to ensure consistent
indoor climate across diverse orientations and positions.
Central to this study is the innovative parameter setup in IDA ICE, which not only stream-
lines the simulation process, but also ensures remarkable efficiency. A single modular
studio apartment simulation is executed in a fraction of the time traditionally required,
underscoring the setup’s potential.
However, the true transformative power of this research lies in the seamless integration
of Python. By harnessing Python’s vast data processing and visualization capabilities,
the research elevates the insights derived from IDA ICE simulations, making them more
accessible and actionable. Interactive plots and in-depth analyzes provide a nuanced
understanding of the data, bridging the gap between raw simulation results and actionable
design insights.
A significant portion of the research delved into the potential of a custom AHU system,
specifically Demand-Controlled Ventilation (DCV). The exploration of DCV highlighted its
profound impact on energy performance, offering insights into its advantages and chal-
lenges in the context of modular studio apartments.
The flexibility inherent in the developed design process ensures its adaptability, making
it a valuable tool across a spectrum of modular construction projects. In essence, this
research not only showcases the potential of a parametric design process but also sets
a new benchmark for modular construction design, promising a future where design de-
cisions are informed, optimized, and revolutionized by the synergy between simulation
tools and programming languages.

Introduction
In the contemporary era, the global community is grappling with the multifaceted chal-
lenges of climate change, urbanization, and housing shortages. The built environment,
particularly residential and commercial buildings, plays an important role in this narra-
tive. According to the European Parliamentary Research Service (EPRS)[1] and corrobo-
rated by other studies(State of Green[2], International Energy Agency (IEA)[3] and Ener-
gistyrelsen (ENS)[4]), buildings are responsible for up to 40% of total energy consumption
in regions such as Europe. The IEA energy statistics state that the overall energy con-
sumption of buildings has increased by 1% since 2010. Furthermore, building operations
contribute to 30% of global final energy consumption and 26% of global energy-related
emissions[3]. An Near Zero Emissions (NZE)[5] scenario suggests a potential drop in
building energy consumption by approximately 25% and a decrease in fossil fuel use by
more than 40% by 2030. To achieve the energy savings milestones set in the NZE sce-
nario, the efficiency of residential buildings will need to improve by almost 40%. This
implies that the gains, which took about two decades in the past, will now need to be re-
alised in just eight years, underscoring the urgency for technological innovation and rapid
deployment[6]. These statistics and future goals emphasise the urgent need to reimagine
how we design, construct, and operate our living spaces.
The construction industry’s response to these challenges has been twofold. On one hand,
there’s a push towards Nearly Zero Energy Buildings (NZEB) to mitigate environmental
impacts. On the other hand, innovative construction methodologies, such as modular
construction, are emerging to address pressing housing shortages and the demand for
rapid deployment of structures. The modular construction, characterised by its use of
replicated volume modules, offers both speed and efficiency. However, it also presents
unique challenges, especially in terms of energy efficiency and indoor environment quality
(IEQ).
Studio apartments, a popular choice in modular constructions, incorporate this challenge.
These compact living spaces, which merge various functional areas into a single module,
are particularly sensitive to internal and external heat gains because of their high insu-
lation levels. This sensitivity can significantly influence the indoor climate, making living
conditions uncomfortable and potentially energy-inefficient. As Ozarisoy & Elsharkawy[7]
pointed out, the lower thermal mass of modular construction materials, such as wood
and light steel, when compared to traditional concrete and masonry constructions, can
intensify these challenges.
Given these challenges, there is a growing need to explore innovative methods that can
help in evaluating and enhancing the energy efficiency and IEQ of modular constructions.
Here, the role of parametric design comes into play.
This thesis delves into the exploration of parametric design as a tool specifically tailored for
studio apartments built using modular construction. As the construction industry increas-
ingly adopts modular techniques, ensuring energy-efficient and comfortable living spaces
within these structures becomes paramount. The primary objective of this research is to
investigate whether parametric design can be used to evaluate and potentially enhance
the energy performance and thermal comfort in these studio apartment modules.
The project employs IDA-ICE for data generation and simulation, with Python serving as
the platform for data analysis and visualization. This integrated approach aims to stream-

1.1
Background
Building on the introduction, this thesis is part of a larger collaboration between DTU
Construct, ISTA, Boligfonden DTU, and PensionDanmark. This partnership aims to ad-
dress the growing need for affordable student housing, leading to the creation of modular
wooden units[8, PendionDanmark]. With the expected growth in sustainable modular con-
struction, the collaboration with PensionDanmark and ScandiByg since 2020 has devel-
oped sustainable modular construction projects such as: 300 and 478 new studio houses
placed in Ballerup and Lyngby[9].
DTU Construct has a strong background in improving heating systems. Their recent work
highlights the importance of digitalisation in reducing heating system temperatures[10],
where the research was accelerated between 2019-2022 with the help of an IFD-funded
project: Heat 4.0[11]. In 2022, DTU Construct partnered with ISTA and Boligfonden DTU
to monitor utilities in 478 student apartments on the DTU Lyngby Campus, in collaboration
of the project: ’Heating Optimisation In Studio Apartments Based On Modular Construc-
tion’[12]. These top-rated DGNB-gold buildings use wireless meters, showing the project’s
emphasis on data.
Modular construction, known for its automated production of uniform modules, offers a
potential solution to housing challenges. However, it’s not without its issues. Buildings
made this way can have high energy use and may not always provide the best thermal
comfort[13]. The high insulation in these modules can make them sensitive to heat gains,
especially affecting the indoor climate. This challenge is what this project aims to address
by using digital simulation to study and improve the energy and comfort levels of these
apartments.
While other projects, such as the HEAT 4.0: ’digitalisation of the district heating’, project[14],
focus on data-driven methods, this thesis will focus on the parametric approach to digital
simulation and design of modular studio housing. The aim is to use the IDA ICE param-
eter setup along with a Python script to create a digital tool to study the energy efficiency
and thermal comfort performance of modular apartment modules.

Modular Construction
Modular construction (MC) is a transformative approach with the potential to revolutionise
the construction industry. It promises faster, more cost-effective, and sustainable build-
ing solutions[16]. The method can accelerate construction schedules by 20-50% and
reduce material waste, leading to cost savings of up to 20%[17]. A critical review of mod-

Parametric Approach
The parametric approach, especially in the context of digital simulation and methodology

For residential buildings, internal heat gain is an essential factor to consider for energy

an estimated 3.5 W/m2 of internal heat gain, ranging from 210 to 840 W for households

3.2.4
Ventilation requirement
The ventilation system is designed according to the requirements of BR18 §443.5, which
indicates that the exhaust rate must be able to perform up to at least 15 L/s for the bath-
room / toilet, the laundry room at least 10 L/s and the kitchen must have at least 20 L/s.
This accumulates to a boost ventilation of 35 L/s, since the AHU is currently assumed to
be a Constant Air Volume (CAV) system. The airflow rate is set to 35 L/s at all times.
3.2.5
Demand-Controlled Ventilation requirements
During the development of the IDA ICE setup and the Python script, it was found through
the early prototype data analysis that the simulated Studio Apartment module, with a
Constant Air Volume (CAV) setting of 35L/s, consumed more than 58% of the total Primary
Energy Demand (PED) due to the AHU fan (HVAC aux) alone. To address this, an active
solution was evaluated by implementing a Demand-Controlled Ventilation (DCV) AHU
system. To compare the CAV and DCV systems, the requirements of BR18 were used
since the specific energy consumption of the AHU in the case study was not available.
The BR18 requirements set specific electricity consumption limits for air transport: [34]:
CAV: 1,500 J/m3 outside air for ventilation systems with constant air output.
VAV: 1,800 J/m3 outside air at maximum pressure loss for systems with variable air out-
put.
The DCV AHU system, following the guidelines of DS/EN 16798-2:2019, takes into ac-
count dynamic demand and pollutant loads, in order to optimise the ventilation rate and
significantly reduce the energy consumption associated with the AHU Fan.
The standard presents three different design methods based on recommended values
according to Category II with low polluting buildings:
Method 1: Based on perceived air quality.
Method 2: Using limit values of gas concentration.
Method 3: Relying on predefined ventilation flow rates.

IDA ICE is a recognized and widely used tool in the field of sustainable building design
and analysis. The firm is recognised for its comprehensive set of tools and capabilities
that enable professionals to evaluate the thermal performance and energy efficiency of
buildings.
IDA ICE excels in providing detailed and accurate results considering various factors,

energy losses, thermal mass effects, solar radiation, and HVAC system performance, en-

parameter setup were used to develop a design process focused on modular construc-

performance of a single modular volume, specifically the case study student apartment

Explanation of the custom DCV AHU System in steps:
1. Additional Static Sensors:
The custom DCV AHU introduces three more static sensors: Mode=3: Temperature
[◦C], Mode=4: CO2 [mg/kgdryair], and Mode=5: Rel. Humidity [%].
2. Sensor Signal Processing:
Sensor signals are fed into ’PSMOOTH’ objects to approximate real behavior using
a sine function between ’LO’ and ’HI’ inputs from the script: ’DCV_Macro’. The
’MAX’ object selects the highest sensor signal based on demand. The ’LO’ and ’HI’
inputs are defined in the graphical script ’DCV_Macro’ (see Figure 3.11).
3. Window Opening Control:
A Window opening signal (Opnctrl_1) is connected by ’LinkRef’, which emits a signal
greater than 0 when the window is scheduled to open. A ’LessEqual’ with a threshold
of 0.1 is used alongside a ’Product’ object to ensure that the MAX signals from the
sensors are set to 0 when the Opnctrl_1 exceeds 0.1, thereby preventing Demand
Control from exceeding the minimum air flow. Conversely, if Opnctrl_1 is below 0.1,
the MAX sensor signal is allowed, enabling Demand Control to surpass the minimum
air flow.
4. Minimum Fan Power Setting:
’Min. FAN Power’ is connected via ’LinkRef’ from the script: ’AHU_Airflow’, and is
combined with the ’product’ operator from ’Fan operation’ to establish the minimum
air flow rate.
5. DCV Activation Control:
An option to toggle the Demand Controlled Ventilation ON or OFF is incorporated
into the final ’MAX’ object, sourced from the parameter setup input ’DCV_ONOFF’.
6. Final DCV Output:
The highest values from the Max Sensor signals, Min. FAN Power, and the ON/OFF
signal for DCV control are consolidated into a ’MAX’ object, which then forwards the
peak value of the Demand Controlled Ventilation setup.
Summary & function:
Custom DCV AHU controls the FAN power of AirSupply and AirExhaust based on demand
from the maximum sensor output from the PSMOOTH bands, whose inputs are defined
in the script ’DCV_Macro’ and minimum airflow calculated in the script ’AHU_Airflow’ set
by Min. ACH, described by Method 3, ch. section 3.2.5. The DCV cannot increase more
than the Min. airflow if the window opening signal ’Opnctrl_1’ is higher than 0.1. The DCV
can be turned off by changing the value of ’DCV_ONOFF’ in the parameter setup. The
Fan SFP is changed based on the specific electricity consumption defined by BR18 for
CAV or VAV systems in ’DCV_Macro’.

3.3.7
Alternative methods
This section delves into two proposed techniques that aim to automate the parameter
simulation for multiple orientations. As mentioned in the parameter setup of IDA ICE
Ch. section 3.3.4, the setup does require some manual input from the user to be able
to change orientation and export data to use the Python script. Despite the potential
of these methods to streamline the parameter setup process, they were unable to be
implemented within the time frame of this thesis. The primary challenges faced included
limited understanding of the LISP programming language and the presence of bugs in the
logic blocks of graphical scripts in IDA ICE.
Nevertheless, these techniques are described in detail in the hope of enlightening future
users. Their eventual implementation could offer valuable advancements in the domain.
Although the current thesis could implement them, they have potential for future research
and development.
Method 1: Dynamic shading objects using a custom script
This method proposes a custom LISP script to automate orientation changes in the IDA
ICE model. The current IDA ICE setup requires manual input for each orientation due to
a lack of support for dynamic shading objects. The IDA ICE parameter setup used in the
thesis uses a workaround for this problem, as mentioned in ch. section 3.3.5, with a guide
on how to change the orientation for simulations in Appendix A.5.
The proposed custom script, which will be executed during simulations, aims to detect and
adapt to the prevailing orientation. This would allow for automatic transitions between
shading objects, such as from south to west. However, this approach met challenges
due to the author’s limited expertise with LISP. For reference, the associated scripts are
provided in Appendix A.8 to A.10.
Integration complications arose, particularly when trying to implement the ’run script’ fea-
ture within the graphical script. The inspiration for this method was drawn from an Equa
forum post, which discussed a related mechanism to modify floor / heating capacities
through a ’run script’ triggered by a button [41].

Python, chosen as the programming software, is an open source and versatile program-

Data import
Building upon the outlined requirements, the following are the detailed steps implemented

Environment setup:
The Python libraries os, pandas, and numpy are imported, enabling file manipulation,

Defining Directory Path:
The directory of the data folder (ex. ’DATA_From_IDAICE’) is constructed using the

Storing DataFrame: Finally, the processed DataFrame, along with the associated

4.2.3
Investigation of the DCV ON Thermal Comfort Scenario
The observed superior thermal comfort in the DCV OFF scenario is hypothesized to stem
from the specific operative temperature requirements defined by the ’Thermal comfort (EN
16798-1, without cooling)’ module. This assumption is based on the module’s distinct MIN
and MAX operative temperature criteria (see Appendix A.1) mentioned in section 3.2.2.
Thus, this section investigates this hypothesis.
The heating setpoint of 20 ◦C aligns with the MIN temperature for CAT II. If the operative
temperature falls below this, it changes to CAT III for that hour. This drop in temperature
is attributed to the ’Ideal heater’ in IDA ICE, which is operating using a ’PI controller’ with a
temperature sensor using the air temperature. The key difference to note here is that the
heating unit used in the IDA ICE model uses air temperature to define the controls of the
heater, while the thermal comfort categories are determined by the operative temperature.
The operative temperature is calculated based on air temperature (dry bulb), mean radiant
temperature (MRT), and air velocity.
Upon this information, a further examination of the thermal comfort was investigated, start-
ing by increasing the Heating setpoint. The impact on the occupancy hours are clearly
indicated by the No. of Occupancy hours shown on Figure 4.13 and Figure 4.14, where
the No. of occupancy hours within CAT III was significantly decreased.
A detailed examination of the zone operative temperature revealed its minimum value
over a year to be 19.86 ◦C. A test simulation adjusting the heating setpoint from 20 to
20.14 ◦C was conducted. Figures 4.17 and 4.18 compare these setpoints, showing a
notable increase from 4715 hours to 6915 hours within CAT II. Thus, the hypothesis that
the heater being operated by the air temperature set at 20 ◦C, instead of the operative
temperature was enough to offset the operative temperature to dip below 20 ◦C.

4.2.4
Summary of energy performance and thermal comfort
The modular studio apartment’s energy performance was assessed based on a range
of key outputs. The Demand-Controlled Ventilation (DCV) system, when activated (DCV
ON), demonstrated a remarkable 71.0% average reduction in Primary Energy Demand
(PED), highlighting its efficiency in modulating airflow and optimising energy use. This
was particularly evident with an 82.7% reduction in HVAC auxiliary consumption and a
97.9% reduction in zone heating. However, despite the minimal district heating energy
consumption in the DCV ON scenario, there was significant overheating, especially in
the south orientation. This overheating underscores the high sensitivity of these highly
insulated studio apartment modules to internal heat gains, such as those from equipment,
which contributes an additional 30.6 kWh/m2 to the total energy consumption.
In terms of thermal comfort, the DCV OFF scenario provided superior results with a heat-
ing setpoint of 20 ◦C, capturing all occupancy hours within the CAT I and II comfort cate-
gories. In contrast, the DCV ON scenario saw a drop in these categories to around 84.2-
88.9% annually. Overheating hours were also fewer in the DCV OFF scenario, especially
notable in the South orientation which failed to meet the thermal comfort standards set
by BR 18 when DCV was ON. The reduced thermal comfort in the DCV ON scenario was
attributed to the specific operative temperature requirements of the ’Thermal comfort (EN
16798-1, without cooling)’ module. Adjusting the heating setpoint from 20 to 20.14 ◦C in
the DCV ON scenario showed a significant increase in CAT II occupancy hours, support-
ing the hypothesis regarding the heater’s operation. However, the DCV OFF scenario’s
decreased CAT I occupancy hours, compared to DCV ON, is assumed to be caused by
the excessive heat removal from the constant high airflow of the CAV system.
In essence, while the DCV system’s energy performance showcased a sustainable and
efficient approach to ventilation in modern residential settings, there were trade-offs in
terms of thermal comfort, especially when considering the specific operative temperature
requirements and the system’s sensitivity to internal heat gains.

Performance of the modular Studio Apartment
The global push towards sustainability has placed energy efficiency at the forefront of the
building industry. This section delves into the energy performance of the studio apartment
module and contextualizes it within the broader industry trends and standards.
During the assessment of the studio apartment module’s energy performance, it was ev-
ident that the initial CAV setting of 35L/s accounted for a significant 58% of the total Pri-
mary Energy Demand. In the early stages of the parametric design process, transitioning
to a Demand-Controlled Ventilation (DCV) system emerged as a pivotal strategy to meet
and exceed the Low Energy Class 2020 BR18 requirements, which specify a maximum
PED of 27 kWh/m2. The DCV system’s adaptive ventilation approach offers a promising
solution, especially for highly insulated modules like the studio apartment. However, this
insulation, while beneficial in reducing heating demand, introduces challenges in manag-
ing internal heat gains. Notably, there’s an increased risk of overheating when the DCV is
active. The evaluations revealed that the demand for zone and district heating decreased
significantly, averaging reductions of 97.9% and 94.3% across all studio apartment po-
sitions. Given the module’s sensitivity to internal heat gains effectively managing these
gains becomes paramount.

trend by illustrating the progressive tightening of energy demand limits for new 150 m2

and innovation. Echoing this sentiment, the European Commission highlighted in 2020

The Parametric Design Process
The primary objective of this paper was to establish a parametric design process that
evaluates the influence of orientation and position on the energy performance and indoor
air quality (IAQ) of studio apartments within modular construction.
The design and evolution of the parameter setup in IDA ICE centered on empowering
users to simulate various orientations and positions of a modular zone, specifically a studio
apartment. In this context, the parameter setup in IDA ICE demonstrates its capabilities
as a potential tool, facilitating simulations across multiple orientations and positions while
concurrently evaluating the effects of other input parameters. Additionally, the creation
of a Python script further enhances this process, enabling efficient data processing and
visualization within an interactive and user-friendly framework.

Challenges and Limitations
However, there are certain challenges and limitations to consider:
• Model-to-Reality:
• Dynamic Shading Challenges:
• Output Selection Complexity:
• Daylight Simulation:
Model-to-Reality Limitations:
The IDA ICE parameter setup model assumes symmetry across all facades, which can
pose challenges when assessing non-symmetrical modular construction projects. Multi-
ple assumptions are made to simplify the model, but these can lead to deviations from the
actual geometry of the building. For instance, while all facades in the model are identical
except for window width and building shading objects, the real case study building has
distinct differences. The west facade has only three modules in length compared to four
modules on the other facades. Additionally, the south facade features just one module
on the top floor, with the remaining space allocated to the AHU aggregate, as depicted in
the case study section 3.1 (refer to Figure 3.5). Another significant modeling simplifica-
tion is the merging of zones. The output results combine the living space and bathroom,
although these areas have different functionalities and requirements. This merging was
necessary to facilitate custom module placement based on the X and Y coordinates within
the parameter setup. Such modeling choices, while practical, introduce discrepancies that
necessitate shortcuts or alternative approaches to approximate the case study as closely
as possible without adding undue complexity to the model. Such modeling decisions,
while streamlining the simulation process, will introduce discrepancies.
Dynamic Shading Challenges:
Accurate modeling of dynamic adjacent shading, represented by the IDA ICE ’Building
shading’ objects, is crucial for the precision of simulation results, especially in modular
constructions with varying orientations. The highly insulated modular volumes are partic-
ularly sensitive to potential heat gains, with solar heat gain being a significant contributor.
In the case study, the shading from adjacent structures on the south facade played a
significant role in modulating the internal temperatures. This shading was particularly ev-
ident when comparing the variance in results between position A and D, refer to Figure
4.15. For instance, due to the effects of shading, the top floor experienced a reduction
in overheating hours (above 27°C) from 140 to 120. This shading-induced cooling effect
was even more pronounced on the ground floor, where there was an average reduction
in overheating hours across all orientations by roughly 40% (e.g., South, Pos A = 130,
South, Pos D = 71). Without accurate representation of shading, especially on the south
facade which experiences significant solar heat gain, the results would be skewed and
not reflective of the actual building performance. Therefore, even if achieving perfect
dynamic shading representation is challenging, it is essential to incorporate shading as
close to reality as possible, especially when assessing energy performance and thermal
comfort of the studio apartment.
Output Selection Complexity:
While the parameter setup in IDA ICE allows for a high degree of customization in output
results, it also introduces challenges. Initially, the parameter setup is devoid of any pre-
defined outputs, necessitating the user or developer to manually add all desired outputs.
This requires a deep understanding of the potential impacts and influences of the analysis
to be conducted. For instance, in the DCV ON/OFF scenario, the output ’EB_MechSupAir’
51

represents the sum of multiple components such as ’During heating’, ’During cooling’, and
’Rest of time’. Relying solely on the total ’EB_MechSupAir’ can lead to a skewed under-
standing of the AHU’s performance. Analyzing the individual components, such as ’During
cooling’ and ’During heating’, would have provided a clearer and more accurate depiction
of the system’s behavior across different scenarios. This underscores the importance of
careful output selection and the potential pitfalls of misrepresentation or suboptimal data
selection.
Daylight Simulation Limitations:
The current parameter setup in IDA ICE does not support daylight simulations, even
though the feature is available within the main IDA ICE software. It was observed during
the course of this thesis that the version of IDA ICE being used encountered issues with
daylight simulations, failing to produce accurate outputs. This limitation restricts the com-
prehensive assessment of the building’s performance, especially in areas where daylight
plays a significant role in occupant comfort.

eters or properties that are ’unmappable’. While some values can be directly entered or
changed through the parameter setup, others are locked.

Conclusion
The evolution of modular construction projects demands innovative methodologies that
not only streamline the design process but also ensure optimal energy performance and
Indoor Environment Quality. This research embarked on a journey to explore the po-
tential of a parametric design process, specifically tailored for the unique challenges and
opportunities presented by modular studio apartments.
The parameter setup developed showcases promising time saving potentials. A single
modular studio apartment simulation, representing an individual zone, is executed in 9
seconds, a stark contrast to the 6,600 seconds required for a full building model. This
efficiency is further amplified when considering the setup’s capability to handle up to 5,000
simulations without encountering too many errors. Combined with the flexibility of input
simulation parameters, enables thorough and comprehensive evaluations in a fraction of
the traditional time.
The integration of IDA ICE with Python’s vast post-processing capabilities offers a trans-
formative approach to data visualization. The interactive plots, developed using Python,
not only make the data more accessible but also provide insights that might be obscured in
traditional static plots. This synergy between a robust simulation tool and a versatile pro-
gramming language provides a large toolset, enhancing the evaluation and assessment
process.
The parameter design process crafted in this research offers a flexible framework adapt-
able to various study needs, from sensitivity analyses to specific parameter evaluations.
This versatility ensures its applicability across diverse modular construction projects. As
an illustration, the thesis delved into the potential of a custom AHU system, specifically
Demand-Controlled Ventilation (DCV). When DCV was activated (DCV ON), there was
a notable 71.0% reduction in Primary Energy Demand (PED). This efficiency was high-
lighted by an 82.7% drop in HVAC auxiliary consumption and a 97.9% decrease in zone
heating. However, the DCV ON scenario revealed a challenge: significant overheating in
the south orientation, emphasizing the modules’ sensitivity to internal heat gains, which
added an extra 30.6 kWh/m2 to total energy consumption. The DCV OFF scenario ex-
celled in thermal comfort, capturing all occupancy hours within the CAT I and II comfort
categories. The DCV ON scenario, however, saw a decline to 84.2-88.9% annually in
these categories. The south orientation, in particular, didn’t meet the BR 18 thermal com-
fort standards with DCV ON. This dip in comfort with DCV ON was found to be linked
to the specific operative temperature requirements of the ’Thermal comfort (EN 16798-1,
without cooling)’ module and the IDA ICE models heating system ’ideal heater’ using an
air temperature sensor with a heating setpoint to 20 ◦C. A further simulation was per-
formed with an increased heating setpoint to 20.14 ◦C, where the thermal comfort was
improved significantly according to the ’Thermal comfort (EN 16798-1, without cooling)’
module requirements.
In essence, while the DCV system’s energy performance showcased a sustainable and
efficient approach to ventilation in modern residential settings, there were trade-offs in
terms of thermal comfort. This was especially evident when considering the specific op-
erative temperature requirements and the system’s sensitivity to internal heat gains.
While the current parametric design approach is in its early prototype stages, its potential
is undeniable. As an early prototype, it offers a glimpse into the future of modular con-

struction design methodologies. With further refinement and development, it promises to
revolutionize the way modular studio apartments, and by extension, modular construc-

In essence, the parametric design process developed in this thesis stands as a testament

It offers a promising avenue for the modular construction industry, ensuring that as the

The process, while general in its current form, provides the foundation upon which more
