Preface
This MSc’s thesis is part of the requirements from the Technical University of Denmark (DTU)
for the candidature of a master’s degree in Aquatic Science and Technology. The project consisted
of 1) ﬁeld work, 2) laboratory work and 3) writing. The ﬁeldwork involved sampling in Central
Jutland, Denmark, during the summer-fall 2020. The ﬁeldwork consisted of water sampling for
eDNA analysis and cage-trapping surveillance of crayﬁsh and crayﬁsh plague in 15 rivers. All the
tissue extractions, DNA extractions and DNA analysis were conducted in the genetic laboratory
at DTU Aqua, in Silkeborg. The goals for the thesis were to 1) provide knowledge about crayﬁsh
and crayﬁsh plague distribution in Central Jutland, Denmark, by using 2 methods: the eDNA
method (water eDNA analysis) and cage-trapping surveillance (visual identiﬁcation and tissue
analysis), and 2) compare both method’s ability to detect crayﬁsh and crayﬁsh plague.

Summary
Crayﬁsh are considered keystone species due to their ecological role. Since the introduction of the
algae responsible for the crayﬁsh plague disease (Aphaenomyces astaci) in the 19th century and
signal crayﬁsh (Pacifastacus leniusculus) in the 20th century, European native species have suf-
fered important losses. Only one of the ﬁve European species, Noble crayﬁsh (Astacus astacus),
is native in Denmark, but it is threatened by two invasive species, signal crayﬁsh (Pacifastacus le-
niusculus) and Galician/narrow-clawed crayﬁsh (Astacus leptodactylus). The main threat comes
from signal crayﬁsh and its resistance to crayﬁsh plague, which is deadly for noble crayﬁsh. Due
to the ecological threats faced by these new invasive species, the Danish Environmental Protec-
tion Agency is conducting a nationwide mapping of both invasive and native crayﬁsh species,
as well as the crayﬁsh plague, using environmental DNA (eDNA). Among crayﬁsh diseases, the
crayﬁsh plague is the most concerning one at the moment. The crayﬁsh plague aﬀects crayﬁsh
and it spreads through a water body with motile or encysted zoospores. Monitoring of cray-
ﬁsh is traditionally done by trapping, or more recently eDNA. Likewise, monitoring of crayﬁsh
plague have traditionally been done by obtaining and analyzing tissue (tail fan and abdomen
cuticle), but recently it was found that the crayﬁsh plague could be monitored directly from
water samples, which has provided a new approach for mapping the disease. These novel DNA-
based methods can even be further improved to reduce logistical and economical constraints
with new technologies such as drones, portable qPCR instruments, or with fully autonomous
monitoring technologies. Once introduced, methods to combat or remove invasive species need
to be implemented, however the majority of the current methods utilized rarely remove 100% of
the invasive crayﬁsh, but they can keep the population in low densities, decreasing their impact
on the ecosystem.
In Denmark, there is limited knowledge about the distribution of crayﬁsh, both native and inva-
sive species. The highly sensitive eDNA method is a cost-eﬃcient and rapid tool for monitoring
macro and microorganisms in freshwater samples. In chapter II, I compare conventional cage-
trapping and eDNA monitoring based on samples taken from 46 locations taken from 15 streams
in Jutland, Denmark. Some of the chosen streams had historic pressence of crayﬁsh and crayﬁsh
plague, while others had unknown presence crayﬁsh. In each location (2-5 per stream) water
samples were taken and a funnel trap was left over-night. Additionally, I compared the detec-
tion of crayﬁsh plague in tissues from 40 caught individuals (telson and abdomen cuticle). The
trapping method have shown presence of crayﬁsh plague in 6 streams (10 locations), while the

eDNA from crayﬁsh and DNA from crayﬁsh plague in water samples would aid the detection of
infected crayﬁsh populations in future monitoring programs.

Crayﬁsh
Crayﬁsh are the largest mobile invertebrates occupying lakes, streams, and rivers (Reynolds J. &
Souty-Grosset., 2012). In Europe, four out of the ﬁve native species are listed as “Endangered”,
“Vulnerable” or “Data Deﬁcient” in the IUCN Red List of Threatened Species (2012).
The
introduction of alien species of crayﬁsh in Europe such as the introduction of signal crayﬁsh
(Pacifastacus leniusculus) in the mid-twentieth century (Alderman, 1996) has been threatening
native species of crayﬁsh due to: competition, harming ecological eﬀects for indigenous species,
and acting as a vector of the oomycete deadly parasite (Aphaenomyces astaci), agent of the
commonly known as crayﬁsh plague, to which crayﬁsh of American origin is resistant (Holdich,
2009). The introduction of signal crayﬁsh in Europe was later an attempt to replace populations
of noble crayﬁsh destroyed by crayﬁsh plague and thus is not related with the initial introduction
of crayﬁsh plague in the nineteenth century (Alderman, 1996).
The crayﬁsh have a crucial
ecological role due to their transfer of energy through the food chain and their ability to physically
modify their physical habitat digging galleries in banks and consuming macrophytes (Nyström,
2002). Therefore, there is a growing concern in Europe, including Denmark, to understand and
properly manage both native and invasive crayﬁsh.
4.1
Taxonomy
Crayﬁsh can be divided into two superfamilies: Astacoidea and Parastacoidea, which distribution
is restricted to the Northern and Southern hemispheres, respectively (Hobbs, 1988). Astacoidea
is split into two families, the Astacidae and the Cambaridae. Parastacoidea only has one fam-
ily, the Parastacidae. There are 644 species of crayﬁsh described, where 605 species belong to
the superfamily Astacoidea and only 39 species belong to Parastacoidea (Crandall and Buhay,
2007). Out of these 640 species worldwide, only 5 are native in Europe: Noble crayﬁsh (Asta-
cus astacus), Galician/narrow-clawed crayﬁsh (Astacus leptodactylus), Caspian crayﬁsh (Astacus
pachypus), White-clawn crayﬁsh (Austropotamobius pallipes), and Stone crayﬁsh (Austropotamo-
bius torrentium) (Edgerton et al., 2004, Crandall and Buhay, 2007). Only noble crayﬁsh is native
to Scandinavian countries (Souty-Grosset et al., 2006), whereas the majority of crayﬁsh biodi-
versity is found in Australia (∼150 species) and North America (∼380 species) (Crandall and
Buhay, 2007).

4.2
Biology
Most of the freshwater crayﬁsh are omnivores, feeding on a wide range of detritus, macrophytes,
invertebrates (including other crayﬁsh), ﬁsh, and ﬁsh eggs (Nyström, 2002). Therefore, coloniza-
tion of areas naturally free of crayﬁsh, or replacements of ecologically contrasting crayﬁsh species
can cause an imbalance in the ecosystem, aﬀecting biodiversity and possibly ecosystem services
as well (Kouba et al., 2014).
Due to their protective exoskeleton, they can only grow by molting, which frequency declines
once they approach sexual maturity. In the ﬁrst year of life, crayﬁsh can molt up to 10 times
before reaching sexual maturity, and they can spend from 24h (small specimens) to 5 days (big
specimens) molting. They have sexual reproduction and female crayﬁsh carry and brood the
progeny for a certain period (4-6 weeks) (Reynolds et al., 2012), in contrast to other species of
decapod crustaceans, such as lobster and crabs, which have a planktonic stage.
Crayﬁsh, like other invertebrates, rely on innate immune responses when it comes to infections,
where melanized spots in the exoskeleton surface are an important component (Strand, 2013).
Crayﬁsh species resistant to crayﬁsh plague will encapsulate the growing tip of the hyphae in
melanin, which will expose the zoospores to the defence system, preventing hyphae growth into
the organs (Strand, D.A., 2013). Susceptible species cannot produce enough melanin and other
immune factors to stop the hyphae growth (Cerenius et al., 2003) and the internal defences will
be overwhelmed since the hyphae will penetrate deeper into the tissue and organs (Alderman and
Polglase, 1988; Unestam, 1973). In terms of immunity against the crayﬁsh plague, melanization
involves the activation of prophenoloxidase cascade, which is considered crucial for immunity
(Cerenius et al., 2008). If the levels of the prophenoloxidase transcript are high enough, the
crayﬁsh will be able to combat the infection and will be considered immune to crayﬁsh plague
(Cerenius et al., 2003). However, signal crayﬁsh can still be vulnerable to some A.astaci isolates,
specially during the molting (Aydin et al., 2014).
4.3
Distribution of crayﬁsh in Europe
Many factors such as water pollution, habitat modiﬁcation, the human introduction of non-
indigenous species from other continents have aﬀected the modern crayﬁsh distribution in Europe
(Holdich, 2002; Holdich et al., 2009). As a direct consequence of disease outbreaks caused by
human-mediated introductions (Souty. Grosset et al., 2006), many regions are losing their native
populations of freshwater crayﬁsh (e.g, Bohman et al., 2006; Kozubícová et a.l, 2009). In 2006, a

4.4
Freshwater crayﬁsh diseases
Freshwater crayﬁsh disease agents can be classiﬁed into six main categories: viruses, bacteria,
fungi, protists, and metazoans. The introduction of Aphaenomyces astaci (causative agent of
crayﬁsh plague) in Europe in the mid-1800s (Alderman. 1996; Edgerton et al., 2004) led to an
early interest in freshwater crayﬁsh diseases. Crayﬁsh diseases can be both economical and eco-
logical threats due to the role commercial value of crayﬁsh and key role in freshwater ecosystems,
as observed with the mortality caused by crayﬁsh plague among European species of freshwater
crayﬁsh.
The relationship between infection and disease is unknown for the majority of the crayﬁsh viruses
in wild populations in Europe. It is known that crayﬁsh are susceptible to a virus causing what
is known as White Spot Syndrome Virus (WSSV) which is listed as a non-exotic disease in EC
Directive 2006/88. Despite the WSSV has been proved to aﬀect noble crayﬁsh (Jiravanichpaisal
et al., 2004), this disease is typical from warm regions with an optimal water temperature of
18-30 °C (e.g South East Asia, Mediterranean, Middle East, India) and consequently will rarely
aﬀect Denmark. In addition, freshwater crayﬁsh can also act as vectors of viruses such as the
Infectious Pancreatic Necrosis (IPN) virus, which is well known for the pathogenic eﬀects in
salmonid species (Halder & Ahne., 1988); causing economic eﬀects in traditional rainbow trout
farms in Denmark, which are connected to the natural rivers.
Bacterial infections in crayﬁsh can be caused by diﬀerent genera (Hereunder Acinetobacter,
Aeromonas, Bacillus, Citrobacter, Corynebacterium, Flavobacterium, Micrococcus, Pseudomonas
Staphylococcus, and Vibrio) (Alderman and Polglase, 1988, Edgerton et al., 2002); they are com-
mon and widespread, and many cause opportunistic infections (Matt Longshaw, 2011). Apart
from the crayﬁsh plague disease, some other oomycetes and fungi such as Saprolegnia sp. can
aﬀect crayﬁsh populations, invading crayﬁsh eggs and moribund crayﬁsh larvae, and causing
moralities up to 100% (Herbert, 1987, Royo et al., 2002). Porcelain disease or cotton-tail disease
is a fungi disease caused not by an oomycete but by a microsporadia (Theolania spp), a sister
group of fungi (Fischer and Palmer, 2005). This parasite caused mass mortalities in Europe,
infecting native species (Noble, Galician and White-clawed crayﬁsh) and the invasive species
signal crayﬁsh (Edgerton et al., 2002; Dunn et al., 2009).
Crayﬁsh plague is, however, the main concern in Danish inland waters. This is due to the spread
by the resistant carrier, Signal crayﬁsh, which is presently threatening Noble crayﬁsh populations
in Denmark.

4.4.1
Crayﬁsh plague (Aphaenomyces astaci)
Aphaenomyces astaci is an oomycete belonging to the order Saprolegniales, a family of Leptoleg-
niaceae. Initially, oomycetes were considered a fungi due to their similarity with fungi species;
however, several traits demonstrate they are more closely related with heterokont algae (Beakes
et al., 2012; Levesque, 2011) even that oomycetes lost their photosynthetic capacity. There are
35-40 species of the genus Aphaenomyces. Species within the genus have a wide range of diﬀerent
hosts and survival strategies, including parasites on animals and plants, as well as saprotrophs
(opportunistic parasites) (Diéguez-Uribeondo et al., 2009). A. astaci is an animal parasite that
is only known to infect crayﬁsh. Several studies have, unsuccessfully, tested crayﬁsh plague abil-
ity to infect other potential hosts (e.g Unestam, 1969b; Unestam, 1972). The Crayﬁsh plague
agent is lethal in native European freshwater crayﬁsh, while North American crayﬁsh, e.g signal
crayﬁsh, is known to be resistant and can act as a vector for the crayﬁsh plague (Söderhall &
Cerenius, 1999).

the white area represents the external environment (water). Sporangia ball (2) releases motile spores

The crayﬁsh plague has an asexual life cycle (Söderhall & Cerenius, 1999) (Figure 4.2.d), initially
spreading in the water with ﬂagellated zoospores with a size of 8-12 µm; which are motile for

sporulation (Söderhäll and Cerenius, 1999), which could cause melanized spots to form on the
exoskeleton (Figure 4.3). However, melanized spots are not necessarily a sign of A. astaci, as
they can be caused by physical wounds or other infections.

The occurrence of crayﬁsh plague in diﬀerent streams can be due to the introduction of infected
crayﬁsh, but also be due to movement of contaminated items (traps, ﬁshing gear, boots, etc.) or
water with spores (OIE, 2019). In addition, any animal in contact with an infected water body
can be a vector for the disease.
The crayﬁsh plague agent is an obligated crayﬁsh parasite that cannot survive for long periods
or reproduce without a crayﬁsh host organism (Söderhall & Cerenius, 1999). In the absence
of a crayﬁsh host, the zoospores of A. astaci can survive 1-3 days in its motile form and 2 or

Crayﬁsh monitoring methods
Despite the importance of crayﬁsh in freshwater ecosystems, large-scale monitoring programs of
crayﬁsh distribution are still lacking for many European countries. Crayﬁsh can be monitored
by diﬀerent active and passive methods focusing on capturing whole organisms, as well as DNA-
based monitoring methods broadly known as Environmental DNA (eDNA) (Policar and Kozák,
2005; Kozák et al., 2015). Depending on the survey type it can reveal information about pres-
ence/absence as well as biological information such as total biomass, gender, size, or infection
(Rusche et al., 2020; Vrålstad et al., 2016). Choosing the best monitoring method depends on
both the objective and budget of the survey; all methods have advantages and disadvantages.
Hence, careful consideration of which method to choose is essential. Here, only the two most
common monitoring methods will be described.
5.1
Trapping
For decades, trapping has been the common method to monitor crayﬁsh. The use of traps often
requires less eﬀort than other methods where organisms are extracted from the environment or
visually observed, such as electroﬁshing or snorkeling surveys. Trapping is an eﬀective conven-
tional method that can catch diﬀerent species of crayﬁsh present in the area (eg: Skov et al.,
2009). There are two main trap designs: upright traps with the entrance on the top and cylindri-
cal traps with a side entrance (Holdich, 2002). Essentially, trapping in rivers consists of placing
a funnel trap parallel to the water ﬂow with bait inside, to lure the crayﬁsh into the trap. The
crayﬁsh will easily enter the trap through the conical entrance and will remain inside until they
are satiated or all the bait consumed. The trap is normally left over-night and recovered the day
after (e.g. Hein et al., 2007; Wittwer et al., 2018; Strand et al., 2019). Crayﬁsh trapping is more
eﬀective in mid-summer (June-August) when water temperatures are higher and the crayﬁsh
are more active (Lorman, 1980; Somers & Green, 1993; Richards et al., 1996). The success on
trapping is highly dependable of the chosen location, being the habitats that provide shelter such
as cobble, macrophytes or logs, which are preferable habitats for crayﬁsh to congregate (Hein et

5.2
Environmental DNA
Within the last decade, environmental DNA (eDNA) has been found to be highly eﬀective
for monitoring crayﬁsh (Strand, 2013, Wittwer et al., 2018). All organisms continuously shed
DNA to the surrounding environment.
eDNA is deﬁned as the genetic material present in
an environmental sample, such as water, containing no distinguishing signs of macro-organisms
(Hansen et al., 2018). eDNA relies on capturing and analyzing this free-ﬂoating DNA, in order to
determine if a species is present or not, based on the species-speciﬁc DNA signature. The method
uses species speciﬁc primers and a ﬂuorescencely labeled probe for targeted DNA ampliﬁcation
using a quantitative PCR (qPCR) instrument.
As opposed to other DNA-based sequencing
methods, qPCR is a cost-eﬃcient method for single-species monitoring and can additionally
enumerate the number of DNA molecules within samples.
The eDNA method has been applied for the detection of a wide range of macro-organisms
(Thomsen et al., 2012) and DNA-based methods have demonstrated a dramatically improvement
in the monitoring of invasive species (Darling and Blum, 2007), since it is non-invasive, fast and
cost-eﬃcient comparred to many conventional methods. Addtionally, eDNA has proven to be
better or equal in terms of species detection eﬃciency when comparred to other conventional
methods in both marine (Thomsen et al., 2012) and freshwater (Jerde et al., 2011; Smart et al.,
2015) environments. Over the past years, an increasing number of studies have emerged, which
speciﬁcally used eDNA monitoring to assess the presence of native and invasive crayﬁsh (e.g.
Tréguier et al., 2014; Ikeda et al., 2016, 2019; Agersnap et al., 2017; Mauvisseau et al., 2019). In
context of crayﬁsh monitoring, the eDNA method have limitations, compared with conventional
trapping. For example low concentration of eDNA from the crayﬁsh can lead to false negative
detection. In contrast, dead individuals can lead to false positive detection (Tréguier et al., 2014;
Merkes et al., 2014).
5.3
Crayﬁsh plague monitoring
The eDNA monitoring of crayﬁsh is often complemented with monitoring of the crayﬁsh plague,
due to the detrimental eﬀect it has on the native crayﬁsh populations (Robinson et al. 2018;
Mauvisseau et al., 2019; Strand et al., 2019; Wittwer et al., 2019).
Historically, diagnosing
crayﬁsh rivers populations for crayﬁsh plague has been done by collecting and analyzing tissue
from trapped individuals (Vrålstad et al., 2009). Before qPCR, the organization of animal health
(OIE) required a conﬁrmatory diagnosis based on the isolation of the crayﬁsh plague in pure

the detection using a crayﬁsh plague speciﬁc qPCR assay (Vrålstad et al., 2009) that together
with conﬁrmatory DNA sequencing is the new recommended method for diagnosing crayﬁsh
plague in tissue samples from trapped individuals (OIE, 2019, chapter 2.2.2). In resistant North
American crayﬁsh such as signal crayﬁsh, infection is usually restricted to tail fan and abdomen
cuticle (Oidtmann et al., 2006; Vrålstad et al., 2011). Therefore these tissues are recommended
from the OIE for crayﬁsh plague analysis in signal crayﬁsh (Figure 5.1). However, Vrålstad and

legs and abdomen cuticle (84.1%, 59.1% and 38.6%, respectively). Nevertheless, the crayﬁsh
plague can spread and aﬀect all organs in susceptible organisms. Unfortunately, crayﬁsh plague
detection by catching crayﬁsh, collecting and subsequent analyzes of tissue samples is time-
consuming. More recently, it has been demonstrated that crayﬁsh plague can be detected in
water samples and thereby improve crayﬁsh plague detection (Wittwer et al., 2018; Rusch et al.,
2020; Chapter II). This greatly reduces the cost and time to assess infection in rivers and can
potentially work as an indirect indicator of the presence of resistant crayﬁsh, as crayﬁsh plague
cannot exist without a suitable host, and vulnerable species do not live in waters contaminated
with crayﬁsh plague (Rusch et al., 2020; chapter II).

5.4
Novel instrumentation in DNA-based monitoring
In contrast to trapping, DNA-based monitoring is a rapidly developing ﬁeld. This is both in
terms of methods and novel instrumentation being developed to simplify logistics and analysis.
Speciﬁcally, the new instruments could be highly valuable for monitoring crayﬁsh and cray-
ﬁsh plague. One way to advance monitoring of crayﬁsh and crayﬁsh plague is to minimize the
economical and logistical constraints of collecting samples from large geographical areas (Ge-
oghegan et al., 2018). It has recently been shown that the use of drones (Unmanned Aerial
Vehicles) can conduct water sampling in remote and inaccessible locations. For example, Ge-
oghegan and colleagues (2018) used drones to collect snot samples from whale blows to assess
virus communities. Drone water sampling could automate water collection and considerably re-
duce the costs of water sampling for eDNA analysis; moreover, the eDNA sampling in freshwater
systems wouldn’t be limited by its accessibility, which is currently one of the main issues. Time
is another important factor, especially in terms of management of early invasions of crayﬁsh
and crayﬁsh plague. Currently, there is a considerable time-lag, usually, days to weeks, from
taking samples in the ﬁeld to the molecular results are ﬁnalized. Instead, the use of novel mo-
bile qPCR thermocyclers, such as the Franklin thermocycler from Biomeme, can give on-site
results of presence/absence indication of species in less than one hour. These portable qPCR
instruments do not need highly specialized laboratory equipment and require limited training
to use (https://info.biomeme.com/mobile-qpcr-thermocyclers). Implementation of such an
instrument in monitoring would be highly valuable for management applications.

6
Control methods for invasive crayﬁsh
While it is important to prevent the introduction of signal crayﬁsh in the environment, it is also
crucial to combat or remove them once they have been established (Hein et al., 2007). Many
methods have been used both experimentally and in practice and several reviews have been made
on this subject (eg: Gheradi et al., 2011; Stebbing et al., 2014, Bajer et al., 2019; Manfrin et al.,
2019); while trapping is the most common removal technique, new methods are being developed
(Manfrin et al., 2019). The methods that can be used to combat or remove signal crayﬁsh can
generally be divided into 1) mechanical removal, 2) biological control and 3) chemical control
(Gheradi et al., 2011).
6.1
Mechanical control methods
With the right eﬀort and technology, any species can be overexploited and risk extinction (Steb-
bing et al., 2014). Crayﬁsh can be a valuable product for human consumption. Thus, special
traps have been developed for years to attract and catch the crayﬁsh. Using crayﬁsh traps, one
can considerably reduce the crayﬁsh density (Stebbing et al., 2014). However, an intensive eﬀort
control can only keep the density of the stock down, but it will never achieve actual extinction
(Stebbing et al., 2014). To get signiﬁcant results, trapping should be conducted for an extending
period and sometimes in combination with increased ﬁsh (e.g. trout) predation. This could for
example be archived by enhancing the population of crayﬁsh predators by ensuring more strict
regulations for the crayﬁsh predators such as minimum length or daily bag limit (e.g. Hein, 2006).
Another method is the use of rackets or similar nets to increase the eﬀort. Rackets have often
been used in combination with funnel traps, especially for targeting individuals small enough to
escape the trap (Peay, 2001; Iversen et al., 2011). Manual removal with rackets has been used in
control experiments (Peay, 2001; Peters et al., 2008) and it was found to be an extensively time-
consuming and labor-intensive method. In the same category is electroﬁshing which consists of
attracting the target with an electric current. This method has been used as a control method
independently or as a supplement to other techniques (Iversen et al., 2011). The eﬃciency of
electroﬁshing depends on the size, depth and clarity of the water, being predominately eﬀective
in small, shallow and clear waters (Gheradi et al., 2011). However, it is still labor-intensive and
has not proven to be more eﬃcient than trapping (Stebbing et al., 2014). Furthermore, crayﬁsh
behavior/movement in an electric ﬁeld is less predictable than ﬁsh, which decreases eﬃciency
(personal communication with Søren Berg). Electroﬁshing is also non-selective and thus a de-

absence of trapping, electroﬁshing, or manual removal, crayﬁsh only need a couple of breeding
seasons to return to the former level of population density (Gherardi & Holdich et al., 1999).
Another way to combat crayﬁsh is to modify the physical habitat by making it unsuitable for
them to thrive and survive (Stebbing et al., 2014). Modiﬁcations can be done by drying out
the invaded area, destruction of habitat or creating impassible barriers.
Drying out can be
challenging to remove signal crayﬁsh since they can survive dehydration by hiding in moist soil,
even at very low (-20°C) temperatures (Kozak & Policar, 2003). Furthermore, rivers cannot
be dried out as easily as lakes or ponds and their drainage have immense negative eﬀects on
the ecosystem. Destructing the habitat can be done either by covering their hiding spots with
concrete or excavating banks with hiding caves (eg; Skov et al., 2009). This can prevent crayﬁsh
from hiding from predators during the daytime hours and during molting, increasing mortality
through predation.
6.2
Chemical control methods
The use of biocides, together with mechanical methods such as drying out water bodies, are
the only method that have achieved complete eradication of the population of invasive crayﬁsh
in practice (Peay et al., 2019). Sandodden and Johnsen (2010) were able to successfully com-
bat signal crayﬁsh in a small river system in southern Norway, using the synthetic pyrethroid
BETAMAX VET® which is based on the active substance cypermethrin. Synthetic or natural
pesticides such as pyrethroids are described as less problematic for the environment since they
degrade quickly when comparing with other pesticides like carbamates or organophosphates (Li-
dova et al., 2019). However, these pesticides have serious detrimental side eﬀects on invertebrates
and amphibians. Moreover, the physical complexity of sites plays an important role and hence
the chemicals are best administered in small and closed lakes. Control using these biocides can
also be considered in small streams, but it needs to be evaluated on case-by-case basis. The
exposure time depends on the dose (24-96 hours), and synthetic pyrethroids can remain longer
in the watercourse, which provides them with greater certainty of the desired eﬀect (Livoda et
al., 2019).

6.3
Biological control methods
Biological control is the area where most of the current experimental work is done. There are
many common and novel biological control methods, but the most common ones are predation
manipulation, bottom-up control with pathogenic microorganisms and reproductive manipula-
tion.
Crayﬁsh have many natural enemies (Kozak et al., 2015). Hence, enhancing a healthy crayﬁsh
predator community until the predatory eﬀort is higher than the productivity of crayﬁsh, will
mitigate the increasing population size. While complete extinction of the invasive crayﬁsh is not
possible (Hein et al., 2007), it can reduce the environmental impact of the invasive crayﬁsh. In
contrast, bottom-up control using pathogenic microorganisms (viruses, bacteria, or parasites) is
another potential mitigation method, but it has yet to be developed for the control of invasive
crayﬁsh (Stebbing et al., 2014).
In order to use pathogenic microorganisms to eradicate an
invasive crayﬁsh species, the pathogen needs to be species or genus-speciﬁc and safe for human
consumption. Over time some individuals can develop resistance to pathogens, which will prevent
complete extinction. Apart from crayﬁsh plague, there are presently no other fungi that are
species-speciﬁc enough or have such a clear pathogenic proﬁle to be used as a control tool.
Reducing reproductive success can be done through diﬀerent methods.
One of them can be
sterilizing, using X-ray irradiation, a large number of males, and releasing them, which will
lead to unfertilized eggs (Hänﬂing et al., 2011). X-ray irradiation has been used in combination
with intensive trapping and has reduced Lousiana crayﬁsh density by approximately 87% in a
small lake in Northern Italy (Manfrin et al., 2019). In combination with sterilization, females
can be removed in the catch to decrease reproductive success even further. Another way to alter
ﬁtness is to manipulate the gender ratio, which can prevent or decrease reproduction (Simberloﬀ,
2020). This can be done by inactivating genes of female crayﬁsh, so they only can produce male
oﬀspring. This method has been used with success in shrimp aquaculture (Hänﬂing et al., 2011).
However, this technique is still in development and has not yet been tested on crayﬁsh (Manfrin
et al., 2019). Nevertheless, these methods can be very expensive to implement on a largescale
(Simberloﬀ, 2020). Additionally, pheromones can also be used to confuse the crayﬁsh and have
been widely used in the control of insect pests (Reddy and Guerrero, 2004). For example, males
can be confused to enter a trap before or they can be confused with pheromones so they cannot
ﬁnd the females. As a result, in both cases, reproduction is minimized or prevented. Some of
the crayﬁsh pheromones have been isolated, so this technique is in development (Stebbing et al.,

7
Conclusion
Both eDNA and cage-trapping monitoring have advantages and disadvantages for crayﬁsh and
crayﬁsh plague monitoring. The eDNA method can lead to over/underestimation of crayﬁsh
and crayﬁsh plague, and the trapping surveillance success is highly dependent on the season
and chosen location.
Moreover, the trapping can lead to underestimation of small crayﬁsh
individuals. The disadvantages of both monitoring methods can be overlapped when they are
used in combination. Once introduced or established, there is no ideal method to remove signal
crayﬁsh; however, their population density can be controlled by a combination of intense trapping
and increased predation.

Abstract
The introduction of signal crayﬁsh and crayﬁsh plague is causing a rapid decline in Europe,
which is also suspected in noble crayﬁsh populations in Denmark. Due to the limited knowledge
of the actual distribution of signal crayﬁsh, crayﬁsh plague and noble crayﬁsh in Danish fresh
water systems, this project pretends to 1) provide updated information of 15 streams in Central
Jutland, Denmark, and 2) compare two methods: cage-trapping surveillance and environmental
DNA (eDNA) method on the detection of both crayﬁsh and crayﬁsh plague. Additionally, crayﬁsh
plague detection was tested in two diﬀerent tissues (telson and abdomen) in 40 individuals. Cage-
trapping surveillance detected signal crayﬁsh in 8 streams (15 locations), while the eDNA method
in 7 streams (11 locations). Crayﬁsh plague was detected in 6 streams (10 locations) with the
cage-trapping surveillance, and 9 streams (18 locations) with the eDNA method.
No noble
crayﬁsh was trapped in any of the locations (46 locations), but it was detected with the eDNA
method in one location (river Jordbro). Telson tissue showed presence of crayﬁsh plague in 14
individuals while abdomen cuticle in 9 individuals. Even that eDNA detection rate of signal
crayﬁsh has been lower than the trapping detection rate, the eDNA method had better detection
rates of crayﬁsh plague. It has been proven in this study that crayﬁsh plague eDNA is a better
predictor of signal crayﬁsh presence compared to both trapping or merely monitoring eDNA from
signal crayﬁsh. Therefore I purpose a combination of both crayﬁsh and crayﬁsh plague eDNA
monitoring to improve the overall detection of infected crayﬁsh populations in future monitoring
programs.

Introduction
The introduction of non-native crayﬁsh species is a major threat to freshwater biodiversity,
altering ecosystem processes and potentially outcompeting native crayﬁsh species (Holdich et
al., 2009). In Europe, crayﬁsh of North American origin are amongst the most prominent high-
impact invasive invertebrates. The most widespread in Northern European countries is the Signal
crayﬁsh (Pacifastacus leniusculus).
After its ﬁrst introduction into Europe in the twentieth
century (Alderman, 1996), signal crayﬁsh have continuously been reintroduced and spread by
numerous subsequent introductions for stocking and aquaculture purposes (Holdich et al., 2009).
The signal crayﬁsh have severe ecological eﬀects through competition on native European noble
crayﬁsh (Astacus astacus) populations, but in addition, signal crayﬁsh can be infected with the
pathogenic oomycete (Aphanomyces astaci) causing the disease crayﬁsh plague, which imposes
additional challenges for the native noble crayﬁsh (Rusch et al., 2020; Strand et al., 2019).
Crayﬁsh plague is an obligate parasite, which is non-lethal to the signal crayﬁsh. However, the
native noble crayﬁsh lack eﬃcient defense mechanisms to resist this pathogen and thus whole
populations tend to be eliminated as a result introduction of signal crayﬁsh carrying crayﬁsh
plague (Holdich et al., 2009).
Hence, disease-carrying signal crayﬁsh is responsible for both
spreading and maintaining the disease in the environment, which has caused a rapid decline
in native crayﬁsh populations around Europe, a decline that is also suspected in Denmark.
Currently, crayﬁsh plague is listed among the world’s 100 worst invasive species (“100 of the
world’s worst invasive alien species: a selection from the Global Invasive Species Database |
IUCN”, n.d.).
Molecular detection and quantiﬁcation of pathogenic microorganisms through
qPCR from water samples has been widely utilized for decades (Ramírez-Castillo et al., 2015)
and recently it has also been utilized to monitor crayﬁsh plague in both tissue (Vrålstad et al.,
2011) and water (Rusch et al., 2020) samples.
In Denmark, there is presently limited knowledge about the distribution of native and invasive
crayﬁsh. Only a few surveys speciﬁcally targeting crayﬁsh with very limited geographical scope
have been conducted (Skov et al., 2011; Agersnap et al., 2017; Berg et al., 2020). Albeit, there are
numerous sightings from recreational ﬁshers, environmental consultants and others, suggesting a
patchy but nationwide distribution of the invasive signal crayﬁsh (Søren Berg, unpublished data).
Signal crayﬁsh, as well as other invasive crayﬁsh, pose a great threat and are therefore subject
to the EU Regulation on the prevention and management of the introduction and spread of
invasive alien species (Regulation (EU) No 1143/2014), which require member states to monitor

Traditionally monitoring of invasive crayﬁsh and crayﬁsh plague has been conducted with traps,
by ﬁrst identifying caught individuals to species level and, if needed, conducting molecular anal-
ysis of tissue to assess if the individual is infected with crayﬁsh plague (Bolat, Mazlum, Demirci,
& Koca, 2011; Vrålstad et al., 2011). However, a new tool for monitoring and managing aquatic
macro-organisms is by utilizing Environmental DNA (eDNA). The eDNA monitoring of aquatic
systems is a rapidly advancing research ﬁeld that promises particular improvement to the early
detection of invasive species at low densities, across seasons and in any life stage (Brown et al.,
2016; Goldberg et al., 2013). eDNA is commonly deﬁned as the genetic material obtained directly
from environmental samples (e.g. soil, sediment, and water) without any obvious visual signs of
the source macro-organism (Hansen et al., 2018). eDNA studies have been applied for a wide
range of aquatic macro-organisms, here-under crayﬁsh (Agersnap et al., 2017; Strand et al., 2019;
Tréguier et al., 2014). While many studies ﬁnd promising results using eDNA for monitoring
crayﬁsh (Agersnap et al., 2017; Rusch et al., 2020; Strand et al., 2019), a study from Tréguier
and colleagues (2014) revealed that eDNA and trapping can obtain divergent results regarding
the detection of the crayﬁsh. Although the eDNA methodology had overall better detection ef-
ﬁciency than trapping, this methodological discrepancy between the two methods is concerning
for the applicability of an eDNA based approach and hence ways to optimize detection should
be investigated. Recently, it was also found that crayﬁsh plague could be assessed from water
samples using the same methodology as eDNA relies on, improving the traditional method of
assessing crayﬁsh plague greatly (Wittwer et al., 2019). However as the crayﬁsh plague cannot
be visually identiﬁed in a water sample it does not constitute true eDNA. However, for simplicity,
monitoring crayﬁsh eDNA and monitoring DNA from crayﬁsh plague will be mentioned as eDNA
throughout the manuscript.
In the present study, I compare conventional cage-trapping surveillance with eDNA monitoring
using species-speciﬁc assays targeted for noble crayﬁsh (Agersnap et al., 2017; Knudsen & Den-
mark, 2019), signal crayﬁsh (Knudsen & Denmark, 2019) and crayﬁsh plague (Vrålstad et al.,
2009) (Appendix 21 Table F.1) from the same water sample. In addition, I assess crayﬁsh plague
infection proportion using tissue samples from individuals caught in the traps. Using samples
collected from multiple rivers in central Jutland, Denmark, with both known and unknown his-
toric presence of crayﬁsh and crayﬁsh plague, I evaluate the diﬀerent monitoring approaches for
detection of crayﬁsh and crayﬁsh plaque. Finally, I also consider the implications of our results
for large scale eDNA monitoring in relation to management of invasive species.

Material and methods
11.1
Study sites
The study was conducted in central Jutland, Denmark, where both the noble crayﬁsh, signal
crayﬁsh and crayﬁsh plague have been documented (Skov et al., 2010; Vrålstad & Rolén, 2016).
15 streams were selected for the study (Figure 11.1). The selected streams comprised 6 streams
with known presence of signal crayﬁsh from previous surveys (river Klostemolle, river Karup,
river Gudena, river Alling, river Varde, and river Kongea)(Skov et al., 2010) and 9 streams
with no oﬃcial reports of crayﬁsh (river Jordbro, river Fiskbaek, river Skals, river Lemming,
river Storå, river Funder, river Gasten, and river Gudum). River Gudüm was selected based on
personal communication with residents who had observed or caught crayﬁsh at speciﬁc locations,
the rest of the streams were randomly selected. The speciﬁc sampling locations within streams
were selected using satellite images to ensure good accessibility and presence of an elevated spot
for eDNA sampling.
Two to six locations within the same stream were chosen with 1-5 km
between sampling sites. The streams were located in various surroundings including pastures,
arable land, and residential areas, oﬀering highly contrasting habitat features.

The sampling was conducted from July to the beginning of October 2020 and was composed of
ﬁrst taking a water sample for eDNA analysis (ﬁltering and preserving) and hereafter setting a
single baited trap. The sampling order, water sampling followed by setting the funnel trap, was
chosen to mitigate contamination issues. The funnel traps potentially contained residual DNA
from previous catches, thereby the traps could contaminate subsequent eDNA water samples if
the order of sampling was reversed. Likewise, sampling always started at the station furthest
downstream and subsequently moving upstream to prevent potential contamination of samples
taken downstream.
11.2
Crayﬁsh trapping, tissue sampling and DNA extraction
Trapping for sampling crayﬁsh (Berg et al, 2020; Bolat et al., 2011; Tréguier et al., 2014), was
done using semi-cylindrical plastic funnel traps with two side entrances. The trap measured 57
cm in length and 33.5 cm on the cross-diagonal section. The inner opening was a half-circle with
a width of 95 mm and a height of 50 mm. The mesh size in the trap varied, with the smallest
mesh sizes being closest to the end of the cylinder. The largest exterior mesh size was 11 mm by
55 mm (605 mm2) and was located on the ladders leading to the entrances. The largest mesh
sizes on the side measured 11 mm by 55 mm (495 mm2) (Figure 11.2). The traps are primarily
designed to catch crayﬁsh above 7 cm in total length (Berg et al., 2020).

One trap was set at each sampling location and left overnight (∼18 hours). Each trap was baited
with pork meat and dry dog food (50% of each). The traps were, when possible, placed at the
inside of a stream bend to avoid strong currents, as the unintentional movement of the trap
potentially can reduce trap accessibility and potentially reduce the capture per unit eﬀort (one
night) (CPUE). To minimize unintentional movement, the traps were attached with rope to a
metal pole, which was ﬁxated in the ground in the stream bank, thus both securing the trap
and helping to retrieve the trap after completion of the survey. Crayﬁsh tend to enter the trap
opposite to the ﬂow direction, thus the trap was placed parallel to the ﬂow direction to facilitate
the entrance. A total of 46 traps were deployed over the sampling period. To mitigate spread
of crayﬁsh plague spores and other diseases the funnel traps were disinfected between sampling
locations and streams with a Virkon X 10 % solution. After retrieval of the trap, individuals
belonging to the same location were transported separately to the laboratory, without direct
contact with crayﬁsh from other locations. Hereafter, the crayﬁsh from each location were put
in individual plastic bags, to avoid contamination of crayﬁsh plague between individuals from
diﬀerent locations and euthanized by putting them in a freezer (-20°C). Subsequently, the crayﬁsh
were thawed and identiﬁed to species level; the weight, length, and gender of each specimen
were determined and logged. Additionally, tissue samples were collected to assess infection of
crayﬁsh plague. Previous studies have illustrated that tail fan (uropods and telson) allows for
the highest probability of detection of crayﬁsh plague in crayﬁsh compared with walking legs and
abdomen soft cuticle (Vrålstad et al., 2011). Therefore, small pieces of tissue from the telson
were taken from all caught crayﬁsh using a scalpel and weighed (< 30 mg). New sterile scalpels
and tweezers used to collect tissue samples were used between sampling locations to avoid cross-
contamination between sites. After collection, tissue samples were preserved in 96% ethanol at
-20°C. Additionally, for 40 crayﬁsh < 30 mg of abdomen cuticle were taken for comparison using
the same collection method (Appendix 17 Table 17.1). For both the telson and abdomen samples,
the DNA was extracted using the E.Z.N.A Tissue DNA Kit (Omega Biotek, USA) following the
Tissue DNA protocol. Final elution was done with 80 µL of elution buﬀer. After the extraction,
samples were stored at -20°C. For each extraction batch, a negative control was implemented to
monitor contamination in the DNA extraction process.

11.4
Validation of assay speciﬁcity
Validation of assays is crucial to ensure speciﬁcity of the targeted species and minimize false
positives (Wilcox et al., 2013). For this study, we used previously developed assays targeting
signal crayﬁsh (Agersnap et al., 2017; Knudsen & Denmark, 2019), noble crayﬁsh (Knudsen &
Denmark, 2019), and crayﬁsh plague (Vrålstad et al., 2009). (Appendix 21 Table F.1). The
probes for noble crayﬁsh and signal crayﬁsh were labeled with a FAM reporter dye and non-
ﬂuorescent BHQ quencher, while the probe for crayﬁsh plague was a MGB probe. The assay
for noble and signal crayﬁsh and crayﬁsh plague has previously been utilized in other eDNA
projects (Rusch et al., 2020; Strand et al., 2019). However, to ensure the speciﬁcity was intact,
cross-validation was performed in this study as well. To validate the assays targeting signal
and noble crayﬁsh, triplicate qPCR reactions were set up with DNA from both target and
non-target species.
Besides signal and noble crayﬁsh, assays were tested against DNA from
Louisiana crayﬁsh (Procambarus clarkii), marbled crayﬁsh (Procambarus fallax) and Galician
crayﬁsh (Astacus leptodactylus).
For the validation of the assay targeting crayﬁsh plague, a
cross-validation was performed against 104 copies of synthetic DNA (gblocks gene fragments,
Integrated DNA Technologies, USA) based on the internal transcribed spacer 1 region of a
sequence from Aphanomyces laevis (Accession AY283648.1), the only sister species of A.astaci
with known presence in Denmark.
11.5
Quantitative PCR
Prior to the analysis of the samples, qPCR standards were produced. Reactions for the standards
were set up in a 20 µL reaction, consisting of 1 µL of DNA from the targeted species, 1 µL(10
µM) of forward and reverse primer, 8 µL of AccuPol DNA polymerase (Ampliqon, Denmark),
2 µL of dNTP (2 µM each), 2 µL 10X key buﬀer and 5 µL ddH2O. The reactions were run
using the following thermocycling conditions: 95 °C for 2 min followed by 35 cycles of 95 °C for
30 s, 60 °C for 30 s and 72 °C for 1 min and ending with one ﬁnal cycle of 72 °C for 5 min.

Discussion
The present study examines the spread of noble crayﬁsh, signal crayﬁsh and crayﬁsh plague in
rivers of central Jutland, Denmark, using both conventional cage-trapping and eDNA surveil-
lance. Additionally, local crayﬁsh plague infection was assessed by both DNA analyses of tissue
from trapped individuals and eDNA from water samples. Detection eﬃciency was slightly bet-
ter with trapping than eDNA (35% vs. 22%), while only 7 locations tested positive for both
methods. Nonetheless, if locations were compiled to rivers as a whole there was only one river
(river Kongeå) where none of the three sampling locations were positive for eDNA, but where at
least one individual was trapped (Appendix 16 Table 16.1). It is likely that low populations of
target species challenge detection probability of the eDNA methodology as previously reported
in other eDNA crayﬁsh studies (eg: Dougherty et al., 2016; Tréguier et al., 2014). In general,
the estimated concentration of signal crayﬁsh eDNA was always below the limit of quantiﬁcation
for the target DNA sequence, even in locations where many crayﬁsh were trapped. In contrast,
eDNA analysis of crayﬁsh plague revealed both higher detection probability and an overall higher
concentration than eDNA from signal crayﬁsh. In comparison, eDNA from crayﬁsh plague were
positive in 80% (N = 10) of the locations that tested positive for crayﬁsh plague in the tissue
samples. It has been recently found that native species of crayﬁsh do not live in waters infected
with crayﬁsh plague, and both native and non-native species are rarely detected in the same
location (based on eDNA results) (Rusch et al., 2020). Interestingly, positive detection of cray-
ﬁsh plague does not only reveal the presence of the parasite but also provides information on
the presence of its host, as crayﬁsh plague can only survive and thrive in presence of its host
(Söderhall & Cerenius, 1999). Hence, positive detection of crayﬁsh plague is in reality also likely
indicative detection of invasive crayﬁsh species, as native noble crayﬁsh is eradicated in presence
of the plague (Unestam 1972, Söderhall & Cerenius 1999). Taking this into consideration, the
detection probability of crayﬁsh plague was slightly better than trapping (39% vs. 35%, respec-
tively). One location (River Klostermølle) did ﬁnd positive detection of crayﬁsh plague while no
signal crayﬁsh or noble crayﬁsh eDNA was found and no individuals were trapped. However,
this speciﬁc river has a historic presence of signal crayﬁsh suggesting the result is likely valid
(Appendix 16 Table 16.1).). In addition, a neighboring river is known to contain infected signal
crayﬁsh (Vrålstad & Rolén, 2016). Moreover, except for one location (NO2), all the eDNA sam-
ples which was positive for crayﬁsh plague at the location was validated by detecting infection
in tissue samples and by historic presence of signal crayﬁsh (Skov et al., 2010). Further, by

species found in Finland could potentially cause speciﬁcity issues with the crayﬁsh plague assay
used in the present study. We currently have no data documenting the presence of such species
in Denmark. However, undescribed species continue to pose a general issue for eDNA analysis
to ensure robust conclusions.
Surprisingly, both the present study as well as previous studies ﬁnd highly stochastic and contra-
dictory results when analysing tissue from tail fan and abdomen cuticle from the same individual
(Oidtmann et al, 2006; Vrålstad et al. 2011). Vrålstad et al. (2011) analyzed diﬀerent sampling
techniques to improve detection of crayﬁsh plague and found that 84.1% of the individuals was
positive when analysing infection based on tail fan tissue, but only 38.6% were positives when
analysing abdomen cuticle tissue. However, Vrålstad et al. (2011) did not standardized weight of
the tissue analyzed and hence sampling could potentially be biased towards tail fan. This 45.5%
diﬀerence in detection of crayﬁsh plague between the two tissues can potentially be explained by
the lack of correlation with weight. In this study, we standardized the tissue samples by weight
(∼20 mg) but still saw a large discrepancy in the analysis between positive detection in fan tissue
(35%) and abdomen cuticle (20 %) (17. Table 17.1). The reason this discrepancy is observed
when analysis diﬀerent tissue from the same individual is due to the diﬀerence in exposed surface
area, determined by character of the tissue; the telson is rough and hairy while the abdomen
cuticle is soft. Moreover, the telson is exposed from both sides, and the abdomen cuticle only
from the outer surface.
No obvious correlation was found between eDNA concentration and infection rates in the trapped
individuals (Figure 12.3). I hypothesized prior to the study, based on previous studies (Wittwer
et al, 2019), that if many crayﬁsh was infected with crayﬁsh plague at any sampling locality this
will likely correlate with an equally high concentration of crayﬁsh plague eDNA in the water from
the locality. However, we did not see any strong correlation, which can partially be explained
by the small sampling size as well as other factors. Further, a correlation assumes 1) infected
crayﬁsh are homogeneously distributed within rivers, 2) individuals found in the trap are a good

may aﬀect the amount of zoospores in the water such as temperature, UV exposure, molting or
crayﬁsh behaviour, among others, can vary between streams, increasing the complexity of this
comparison.
The only river where the occurrence of crayﬁsh plague has been thoroughly evaluated in Denmark
is river Alling. More than 10 years ago, a total of 44 signal crayﬁsh and 14 noble crayﬁsh were
examined for crayﬁsh plague with qPCR (Vrålstad et al, 2009), but there was no detection of
crayﬁsh plague in any of them (Skov et al, 2009). Since then, an eﬀort to eradicate or reduce
the population of signal crayﬁsh in river Alling, by mainly conventional trapping, has been done
(Berg et al., 2020).
Another survey was conducted in 2018 with traditional traps do detect
signal crayﬁsh and despite the control methods, the % of the area infested with signal crayﬁsh
has increased from 11% (2008) to 35.5% (2018) (Berg et al., 2020). In the present study, no noble
crayﬁsh were identiﬁed, but instead, 59 individuals of signal crayﬁsh were found in River Alling
(3 locations) (Appendix 16 Table 16.1), of which 8 signal crayﬁsh were infected with crayﬁsh
plague. Skov et al (2011) stated that, at the time of that study, the signal crayﬁsh population
in River Alling was most likely not exposed to the disease due to the presence of noble crayﬁsh
and the absence of crayﬁsh plague in tissue samples. According to the results obtained in this
study, river Alling is now infected with crayﬁsh plague, which has likely eliminated the native
noble crayﬁsh. Other rivers with no historic presence of signal crayﬁsh and/or crayﬁsh plague
(river Nørreå, river Gudum, and river Gasten) have shown the presence of signal crayﬁsh as well
as infection with crayﬁsh plague. These results illustrate the importance of monitoring to enable
appropriate management and mitigate ecological eﬀects in Denmark.
There is an increasing focus on monitoring invasive species and EU member states are now
required to monitor invasive crayﬁsh (EU regulation 1143/2014 on invasive alien species). While
this study illustrates that eDNA monitoring is a powerful tool to screen rivers for the presence of
crayﬁsh and crayﬁsh plague, there is still much manual labor involved and a considerable lag, days
to weeks, from sampling to completion of the analysis. This is problematic since the eradication
of invasive species is almost impossible if mitigation measures are not implemented at the early
stages of introduction (Hulme P.E., 2006). In recent years socalled ecogenomic sensors have
demonstrated that the entire process, from sampling to analysis, can be conducted completely
autonomous (Scholin et al., 2017; Hansen et al., 2020), which can provide direct information

Conclusion
Here I successfully monitored the distribution of noble crayﬁsh, signal crayﬁsh and crayﬁsh plague
in rivers of central Jutland, Denmark, using both conventional cage-trapping surveillance and
eDNA. The study found a discrepancy between eDNA and the trapping methodology at speciﬁc
locations, but when samples from whole rivers are pooled, the methods yield a similar result.
We also demonstrate that monitoring crayﬁsh plague is an overall better predictor of signal
crayﬁsh presence compared to both trapping or merely monitoring eDNA from signal crayﬁsh.
Consequently, I propose that monitoring for both crayﬁsh plague and crayﬁsh eDNA improves the
overall detection of infected crayﬁsh populations. Overall, our results show promising potential
for improving the monitoring and management of freshwater crayﬁsh.

Appendix D
GFF extraction protocol
Day 1
Thaw proteinase K if in the freezer.
Turn the oven to 56°C.
1. Carefully wipe the outer surfaces of all the collection tubes and ﬁlter capsules with 5%
bleach (e.g. 6-14% diluted as a 10%) using clean tissue paper. Dry and wipe with 70%
Ethanol using tissue paper.
2. To each sample, add 340 µL proteinase K per sample and vortex for 15 seconds.
3. Incubate, while rotating, at 56°C for 24 hours (overnight).
Day 2
Preheat AE-buﬀer in the oven at 70°C.
4. Vortex for 15 s.
5. Transfer ALL liquid and the ﬁlter to a NucleoSpin ﬁlter midi.
6. Spin in centrifuge for 1 minute at the highest speed (4500*g).
7. Discard the ﬁlter part.
8. Add 3400 µL of AL buﬀer and 3400 µL of ice-cold molecular grade 99% ethanol. Note: AL
and ethanol can be premixed.
9. Vortex vigorously.
10. Make a quick spin to avoid liquid in the cap.
11. Pour the mixture into a Nucleospin Plant II midi column.
12. Spin in centrifuge for 2 minutes at the highest speed (4500*g).
13. Discard ﬂowthrough.
14. Repeat steps 11-13 until all the sample is ﬁltered through the spin column.
15. Add 1 mL Buﬀer AW1, and centrifuge for 2 min at highest speed. (No need to discard
ﬂowthough, we have lots of room in the tube).
16. Add 3 mL Buﬀer AW2, and centrifuge for 10 min at the highest speed to dry the membrane.
Discard ﬂow-through and collection tube.
17. Transfer spin column to a 15 mL collection tube.
18. Add 200 µL buﬀer AE, heated to 70°C, to the membrane.

20. Centrifuge 2 minutes at the highest speed (4500*g).
21. Re-elute DNA from DNA tube. (Apply eluate back on spin column).
22. Incubate at RT for 10 min.
23. Centrifuge 2 minutes at the highest speed (4500*g)

25. Transfer DNA to pre-marked DNA LoBind tube.
