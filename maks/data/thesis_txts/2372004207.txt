Abstract
This thesis presents an implementation of the anytime motion planning algorithm for mobile robot with
Dubins’ vehicle dynamics. The planning problem is solved by using incremental sampling-based algorithms
RRT* and Informed RRT*. The initial feasible solution is found by RRT* algorithm. During plan execution
Informed RRT* improves the quality of the plan toward optimal solution.
Over the last decade, incremental sampling-based motion planning algorithms, have been shown effective
in solving single-query motion planning problems in high-dimensional conﬁguration spaces. They forgo the
exact representation of obstacles in conﬁguration space which often reduces complexity of implementing
them and making them convenient for practical applications.
The algorithm is implemented in C++ and integrated as a plugin on MobotWare framework. The
performance of the algorithm is tested in both simulation and on real robots. The algorithm was run in
different environments with static obstacles and analysis of the results, based on 50 runs per environment
setup, is carried out.
Test cases have shown that the algorithm performs well in spaces with large number of polygonal
obstacles and that is able to ﬁnd a path trough narrow passage trough sampling of 3-dimensional state space.
The optimization by Informed RRT* algorithm proved to yield signiﬁcantly better solutions, in terms of path
cost, in all environment setups.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Over the last three decades, autonomous robots became a vital part of modern industry. They became
an essential part in a wide variety of application domains, including warehouse management, space and
underwater exploration, health care and military. One of the major problems in the creation of autonomous
robots is motion planning [15], which addresses the problem of developing the algorithms that convert
high-level speciﬁcations of tasks, given by users, into low-level descriptions of how to move [17]. Apart from
being of fundamental importance for the ﬁeld of robotics, this problem is also relevant to other disciplines
such as computer animation, biology, pharmaceutical drug design and veriﬁcation [9], [11].
In order to solve the motion planning problem different classes of algorithms have been developed.
Traditional planning methods, such as roadmaps and cell decomposition, rely on an explicit representation
of the obstacles in conﬁguration space, resulting in a high computational burden for high-dimensional
conﬁguration spaces and environments with a large number of obstacles [11]. On the other hand, sampling-
based algorithms avoid explicit mapping of obstacles in state space by random sampling of the points from
obstacle-free space and building the graph of feasible trajectories between them. Due to the computational
savings that sampling-based methods provide and their effectiveness for high-dimensional spaces, these
methods have drawn a signiﬁcant amount of attention over the last decade.
Incremental sampling-based algorithms, such as Rapidly-exploring Random Trees (RRTs) [16], have been
developed for single-query, real-time applications. RRT algorithms have been shown to be probabilistically
complete, i.e. the probability that algorithms return a solution, if one exists, converges to one as the number
of samples approaches inﬁnity [3], [17]. Although RRT algorithm is able to produce feasible solutions
quickly, there are no guarantees on the quality of such solution in terms of a given cost function [10]. In order
to address this issue new algorithms, including RRT*, were proposed and proven to be both probabilistically
complete and asymptotically optimal, i.e. the solution returned by the algorithm converges to an optimal
solution almost surely as the number of samples approaches inﬁnity [11].
In this thesis anytime motion planning by the RRT* algorithm in environment with polygonal obstacles
is going to be investigated. The Anytime RRT* algorithm ﬁnds an initial feasible solution quickly. During
plan execution available computational time is used to improve the quality of the current plan towards an
optimal solution. Two extensions that enable enable this are committed trajectories and Informed RRT*.
Once the initial plan is found, a certain portion of it is committed to robot for execution while the rest of the
plan is being optimized by Informed RRT*. Informed RRT* focuses on direct-sampling of a hyperellipsoidal
subset of states that can improve the quality of current solution. The algorithm is tested on rectangular
mobile robot model with Dubins’ vehicle dynamics and 3-dimensional state space. The state variables are
Cartesian coordinates of the vehicle, x and y, and the heading angle, θ. Tests have been performed for
different environment setups and and performance was analyzed based on 50 runs per test case.

CHAPTER 2
Motion Planning
This chapter gives a brief introduction to motion planning problem. It outlines traditional methods
developed for solving basic motion planning problem and presents the concept of sampling-based algorithms.
2.1
Motion planning problem
Given a description of the robot, an initial conﬁguration, a set of goal conﬁgurations, and a set of
obstacles, the robot motion planning problem is to ﬁnd a dynamically feasible trajectory that takes the robot
from initial state to the goal state while avoiding obstacles. This problem of navigating through a complex
environment has been widely studied in the robotics literature for at least three decades and has several
applications outside the domain of robotics [1].
Unfortunately, motion planning proved to be a complex problem to solve, consisting of diverse aspects,
the simplest of which raise difﬁcult computational issues. “The operative intelligence that people use
unconsciously to interact with their environment, which is needed for perception and motion planning, turns
out to be extremely difﬁcult to duplicate in a computer program” [15]. Producing effective motion planning
algorithms requires combining state of the art knowledge from ﬁelds other than robotics, including Artiﬁcial
Intelligence, theoretical Computer Science, Mathematics and Mechanical Engineering. The collaborative
work in these ﬁelds is essential for improving the mathematical structure of the problem and reducing its
inherent computational complexity. In general, motion planning problem for robots operating in real life
environments involves computing collision-free path between dynamic obstacles, motion coordination with
other robots, dealing with uncertainties from sensor inputs and physical properties of object in robot’s
environment. Hence, it requires robot to consider geometrical, physical and temporal constraints [15].
The basic version of the motion planning problem, known as generalized piano mover’s problem, deals
with geometrical issues only. It serves as a simpliﬁed starting point for solving more complex motion
problems. It assumes that the robot is only moving object in the workspace ignoring its dynamic properties.
Further, assuming that the robot is a single rigid object its motion is only constrained by the obstacles.
Although, these assumptions oversimplify the motion planning problem, some mobile robot navigation
problems can be realistically expressed as instances of the basic problem.
The large number of methods for solving the basic motion planning problem have been proposed over
the years. The traditional motion planning methods can be divided into three main groups: roadmaps, cell
decomposition and potential ﬁelds.
Roadmaps approach captures connectivity of robots’s free space in a network of curves. This network is
used as a set of standardized paths for ﬁnding feasible paths between two conﬁgurations. The most notable
algorithms based on this approach are: Visibility graphs, ﬁg. 2.1(a), and Voronoi diagrams, ﬁg. 2.1(b).

Cell Decomposition approach consists of decomposing robot’s free space into simple regions, called
cells, such that a path between any two conﬁgurations in a cell can be easily generated. A non-directed

decomposition divides free space into trapezoidal and triangular cells whose union is exactly equal to the
free space. Approximate cell decomposition divides free space into rectangular cells with coarse resolution
at the beginning and reﬁnes it until the solution is found or limit resolution is attained [15].

Given appropriate search techniques, exact cell decomposition methods are complete, i.e. they are
guaranteed to ﬁnd a solution if one exists and return failure otherwise. Approximate methods are resolution
complete, i.e. they are able to ﬁnd a solution, if one exists, if the resolution parameter is ﬁne enough.
These planners showed remarkable performance in complex environments within reasonable time bounds.
However, their practical implementations are limited to low-dimensional state spaces due to the large number
of cells.

number of cells that need to be checked is usually enormous. In Potential ﬁeld method, robot is represented
as a particle moving under the inﬂuence of artiﬁcial potential ﬁeld produced by goals and obstacles. As
a convention goal generates attractive and obstacles generate repulsive potential. The negated gradient of

The described methods rely on an explicit mapping of obstacles in conﬁguration space, resulting in
excessive computational complexity in high-dimensional conﬁguration spaces and environments with a large
number of obstacles. In order to avoid this problem, sampling-based algorithms were developed. Instead of
using an explicit representation of the environment, sampling-based algorithms probe conﬁguration space
with sampling scheme [17]. The algorithms sample the states from obstacle-free space and build a graph of
feasible trajectories between them based on information provided by a collision-checking module. Although
these algorithms are not complete they provide probabilistic completeness.
2.2
Sampling Based Algorithms
The most inﬂuential sampling based algorithms include Probabilistic RaodMaps (PRMs) [14] and
Rapidly-exploring Random Trees (RRTs) [16]. Both algorithms randomly sample obstacle-free space, but
differ in the way that they build a graph of feasible trajectories connecting these samples.
The PRM is a multiple-query algorithm that constructs a graph (the roadmap) whose vertices correspond
to the conﬁgurations sampled from obstacle-free space and whose edges correspond to feasible trajectories
between these conﬁgurations. Initial and goal conﬁgurations are connected to two nodes of the roadmap
and the algorithm searches roadmap for shortest path that connects these two nodes. This method proved to
be successful in practice and performs well in high-dimensional spaces [13]. The PRM algorithm received
much attention in the last two decades and many improvements were suggested by different authors.
Although multiple-query methods are valuable in structured environments, most online planning problems
do not require multiple queries since the environment is prone to changes or not know a priori [11]. For these
applications, incremental sampling-based algorithms were created as an online single-query counterparts to
PRMs. The RRTs build a tree of feasible trajectories, rooted in the initial state, by incrementally extending
towards randomly sampled conﬁgurations from nearest tree nodes, ﬁg. 2.3. Once the tree reaches the goal
region, a simple backtracking of the parent nodes from goal conﬁguration to initial conﬁguration yields a
solution.
Figure 2.3: Extend procedure of RRT [9]

widely used in solving motion planning problems. Many extensions to the original versions have been
provided, including RRT-Connect [9], MP-RRT [22] and Poission-RRT [19]. Over the last couple of years,

CHAPTER 3
The Algorithm
In this chapter theoretical background related to the algorithm is introduced. First, the motion planning
problem is stated and the RRT* algorithm is described. Then, extensions for anytime motion planning are
presented, namely, committing of trajectory and Informed RRT* algorithm. Afterwards, system dynamics
and steering procedure are outlined.
3.1
Problem Statement
In this section, the feasible and optimal motion planning problems are stated.
Let X ⊂Rd and u ⊂Rm denote state space and input space of a system with dynamics of the following
form:
˙x(t) = f(x(t), u(t)),
x(0) = xinit
(3.1)
where x(t) ∈X and u(t) ∈U.
Let Xobs denote obstacle region, and Xfree = X \ Xobs deﬁne the obstacle-free space. Finally, let
Xgoal ⊂X denote the goal region. The motion planning problem is to ﬁnd a control input u : [0, T] →U
that yields a feasible path x(t) ∈Xfree for t ∈[0, T] from an initial state x(0) = xinit to goal region
x(T) ∈Xgoal that obeys the system dynamics. Given a cost function c(x) that maps each non-trivial
admissible trajectory x : [0, T] →X to a non-negative real number, the optimal motion planning problem
imposes the additional requirement that the resulting feasible path minimizes cost function [12].
3.2
The RRT* Algorithm
The RRT*, ﬁg. 3.1, algorithm solves the optimal motion planning problem by incremental construction
of a tree G = (V, E) through random sampling of state space, where V is the set of vertices and E is the set
of edges. In this section the RRT* algorithm is described and primitive procedures are deﬁned similar to
[12], [10], [3].
3.2.1
Primitive Procedures
The primitive procedures that algorithm relies on are listed below.
Sampling: The Sample function returns a state zrand sampled uniformly at random from state space.
Cost: The function Cost(z), where z ∈V , returns the cost of the current trajectory that connects vertex
z to root vertex zinit.
Distance: dist: X × X →R≥0 function returns the cost of the optimal trajectory between two states
assuming no obstacles.

Steering: Given two states z1, z2 ∈X, the function (x, u, G) = Steer(z1, z2) solves for the control
input u : [0, T] that drives the system from x(0) = z1 to x(T) = z2 along the path x : [0, T] →X.
Collision Check: Given a path x : [0, T] →X, the ObstacleFree(x) function returns true iff x lies
entirely in the obstacle-free space, i.e., x(t) ∈Xfree holds for all t ∈[0, T].

3.3
Extensions for Anytime Motion Planning
In this section two key extensions added to RRT* algorithm, resulting in anytime motion planning
algorithm, are presented. The anytime property enables the algorithm to incrementally improve path quality
during path execution.
3.3.1
Committed Trajectory
Upon receiving the goal region algorithm starts an initial planning phase, in which it ﬁnds a feasible
solution quickly. Once the initial planning phase is completed, the algorithm goes into iterative planning
phase, in which robot executes the initial portion of the current plan. In parallel to execution the RRT*
continues to sample the space and improve the quality of pending portion of the current plan. Once the robot
reaches the end of the portion that is executing, the iterative phase is restarted.
More precisely, given a motion plan x : [0, T] →Xfree generated by RRT* algorithm, the robot
starts to execute the initial portion of x : [0, tcom] until a given time tcom. This path portion is referred as
committed trajectory. Once the robot starts executing the committed trajectory the RRT* algorithm deletes
branches connected to it and sets the end of committed trajectory x(tcom) to be the new tree root. In this

3.4
System Dynamics
This section describes system dynamics and steering procedure used by the algorithms.
The steering function used by RRT* algorithm assumes a Dubins’ vehicle model [5]. The dynamics of
the Dubins vehicle is described by the following set of nonlinear differential equations:
˙xD = vD cos θD
˙yD = vD sin θD
˙θD = uD,
|uD| ≤vD
ρ
(3.8)
where xD and yD are Cartesian coordinates of the vehicle, θD is the heading angle, vD is the velocity
and ρ is the minimum turning radius. It was shown in [5] that the optimal path between two states, for
Dubins’ vehicle, belongs to one of different six types of paths. This class of paths consists of either turning
(L or R) or straight (S) movement. The six different types are denoted as RSL, LSR, RSR, LSL, RLR and
LRL. If the steering procedure is designed to choose the minimum length path among these six paths, the
steering procedure is optimal globally. In this project, the simpliﬁed steering procedure, that limits the
movement to RSL, LSR, RSR and LSL paths, is chosen. This steering procedure is locally optimal [10].
The shortest path can be found by explicit computation of all paths and comparison of their length.
Due to the computational cost, this approach is not suitable for real-time motion planning. In order to save
computational time, the logical classiﬁcation scheme presented in [21] is implemented.
This scheme allows extraction of shortest path based on knowledge of initial and ﬁnal conﬁguration.
In order to calculate shortest path between initial conﬁguration (Pi, θi) and ﬁnal conﬁguration (Pf, θf),
according to the scheme, a new coordinate system is introduced, ﬁg. 3.6.
Let d be the Euclidean distance between points Pi and Pf. Given the initial Pi and ﬁnal point Pf, a
rectangular coordinate system with origin in Pi = (0, 0) and the positive direction of the x-axis toward
Pf(d, 0) is chosen. The initial and ﬁnal orientation angles, α and β, are measured counter-clockwise with
respect to the positive x-axis of the new coordinate system, ﬁg. 3.6. Without loss of generality, the minimum
turning radius ρ is assumed to be equal to 1. Any other radius can be reduced to 1 by scaling d = D/ρ
where D is the actual distance between the points Pi and Pf [21].

4.1
C++ Implementation
The algorithms presented in chapter 3 were implemented in C++ programming language using Eclipse
IDE and MinGW GCC toolchain. The ﬁnal version of the algorithm was developed in stages. Starting
from RRT* algorithm, round robot model and axis-aligned rectangular obstacles, the implementation was
extended to committed trajectories and Informed RRT*. Later, polygonal obstacles and a rectangular robot
model were implemented. Finally, Dubins’ vehicle dynamics was included together with classiﬁcation of the
Dubins’ set for selecting the shortest path.
In the following sections a brief overview of classes, robot model, collision checking procedure and
distance functions is given.
4.1.1
Classes
The implementation consists of classes listed below.
Vertex: Implements vertex in 3D. It contains information about Cartesian coordinates of vertex, parent
node’s index, index of edge connecting vertex to its parent node and cost of that edge.
Edge: Implements edge with properties required for characterizing Dubins’ path between the two nodes.
It contains information about centers of start and ﬁnish circles, tangent points of straight line segment and
circles, lengths of three path segments (turn-straight-turn) and edge cost in terms of length and time.
Graph: Implements graph structure with sets of vertices and edges. It handles functions for adding/removing
of vertices and edges as well as trimming of the branches of committed trajectory.
RRTstar: This class implements the core of algorithms from chapter 3. It provides several functions
that were created in order to be able to run the algorithm in C++ compiler and integrate it into MobotWare.
Short description of the functions is listed below.
Initialization: Initializes parameters of the algorithm and sets the maximum number of samples.
Set obstacles: Sets obstacles given as a vector of consecutive vertices.
Plan: Main function that executes the algorithm. It also checks periodically if the goal conﬁguration has
been reached.
Save data: Saves vertices, edges, path and obstacles data into .txt ﬁles for easier exchange between
different platforms and plotting in MATLAB.

4.1.2
Collision checking
The discrete collision checking has been implemented. Generated Dubins paths were discretized and
edges deﬁning the robot, at this discrete points, were tested for collision with edges of the obstacles. Hence,
the collision checking can be broken down into series of intersection checking for straight-line segments,
that can be solved by linear algebra. Simple functions for checking intersection of two segments were
implemented according to equations given in appendix A.
4.1.3
Distance functions
In theory the distance function is deﬁned as the cost of the optimal trajectory between two states assuming
no obstacles. That would require computing of shortest Dubins path between signiﬁcant number of states
every time the procedures Nearest and Near are being called. To reduce computational burden distance
function is replaced by Euclidean 3D distance in ﬁnding nearest node in the tree. In [20], lower and upper
bounds on optimal point-to-point Dubins paths is given in terms of Euclidean 2D distance between the
points and minimum turning radius. These bounds are used in ﬁnding the near-by vertices in the tree by ﬁrst
searching for the nodes whose upper bound for Dubins path lies within the ball of volume γ(log(n)/n)1/d.
Then, only this subset of vertices is searched in Near procedure with distance function deﬁned as the cost
of optimal trajectory.

4.2
MobotWare Integration
The implemented C++ algorithm has been integrated as a laser server plugin on MobotWare framework,
installed in VirtualBox with Xubuntu 14.04. OS. The C++ implementation was compiled with MRC code
and a user function class that handles the commands and replies of the server was implemented. By sending

optimization. The MRC and laser server are running in parallel threads. Therefore, after an initial portion of
the plan is committed by the algorithm, MRC is able to execute the robot movement, while laser server is
running optimization procedure.
The examples with different environment stages were setup in the simulator with one SMR robot. The
startup script for simulator, server and MRC has been implemented as well as SMR-CL scripts for running
the examples.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The purpose of this thesis has been implemented and performance analysis of anytime motion planner
for mobile robots based on RRT* algorithm. The Informed RRT* algorithm and concept of committed
trajectories were combined together in order to attain anytime motion planning. The rectangular model of
robots with Dubins vehicle dynamics was implemented and used for testing the algorithm in simulation and
real world.
The algorithm’s performance was evaluated in a maze environment and the algorithm was able to ﬁnd
a feasible solution in a relatively short time and remarkably improve the quality of initial solution during
execution. Moreover algorithm was tested in environments with narrow passages where it was required
to sample a speciﬁc heading angle in order to reach the goal. It showed good performance in terms of
computational time and quality of solution for the case of single narrow passage Although the algorithm
is able to ﬁnd a solution even in the environment setups whit strict requirements for sampling of speciﬁc
conﬁguration, the computational time required in more complex environments was shown to be a major
drawback. The algorithm preformed well in environments with large number of polygonal obstacles and was
able to explore the space very quickly in a considerable number of simulations. For all test cases, sampling
of informed subset proved to induce remarkable improvements in quality of solution.

CHAPTER 7
Future Work
During the period of conducting this thesis different concepts were considered for avoiding dynamic
obstacles and reducing the computational complexity of algorithm. Unfortunately, due to the time limitations
these ideas were not fully implemented or tested in the simulation.
In the real world applications information about environment is usually incomplete or the environment
contains dynamic obstacles. In such cases, the initial solution returned by RRTs might become invalid as the
new information about environment becomes available trough sensing. Therefore, replanning algorithms that
are capable of repairing affected portions of RRTs efﬁciently are of great interest for practical applications.
Examples of such algorithms can be found in [22] and [6].
Collision checking is considered to be the primary computational bottleneck of sampling-based motion
planning algorithms. A novel collision checking implementation, with amortized complexity, has been
introduced in [4]. Traditional collision checking returns either true or false depending on collision status
of a point or a trajectory in question. The new collision checking method returns a lower bound on the
minimum distance to the obstacle set as well. Using this additional information it is possible to conclude if
point is in collision without performing explicit collision check. More precisely, if the distance between
the collision-free point, that has already been explicitly tested for collision, and a new point is less than its
minimum distance to the obstacle it can be concluded that the new point is collision-free. More information
about this effective collision checking and other optimization for sampling-based algorithms can be found in
[4].
