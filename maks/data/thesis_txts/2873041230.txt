ABSTRACT 
This study investigated the possibility of using subcritical water hydrolysis (SWH) as a green 
processing method to turn defatted starfish meal (Asterias rubens), an underutilised marine by-
product, into antioxidative protein hydrolysates. The protein content, ash, dry matter, oil 
content, carbohydrate, protein solubility, molecular weight distribution, and amino acid 
composition (total and free) of two forms of defatted starfish meals, freeze-dried (FD) and spin 
flash-dried (SFD), were first characterized. Based on processing feasibility and industrial 
applicability, and comparable biochemical properties, SFD was chosen for hydrolysis even 
though FD had a higher protein and amino acid concentration. Three reaction times (5, 10, and 
15 minutes) and three temperatures (125, 150, and 175 °C) were used for SWH. 
Following hydrolysis, the protein content, degree of hydrolysis (DH), molecular weight 
distribution (SDS-PAGE), and antioxidant activity (DPPH radical scavenging assay) of the 
freeze-dried hydrolysates were evaluated. The protein content of the hydrolysates increased 
significantly with temperature and time (p < 0.001 and p = 0.0044, respectively), ranging from 
37.01 ± 1.33% (125°C, 5 min) to 53.41 ± 0.53% (175°C, 15 min). Also, the degree of 
hydrolysis was found highest at 150°C, 5 minutes (35.42 ± 0.96%), whereas 10 minutes at 
175°C generated the lowest (12.86 ± 0.19%). According to SDS-PAGE examination, the 
majority of the peptides were below 14 kDa, and the band intensity increased with increasing 
temperature and time. Also following this trend, the hydrolysates generated at 175 °C for 10 
and 15 minutes showed strong antioxidant potential; they exhibited the highest radical 
scavenging activity (65–67%) and the lowest EC50 values (0.362–0.509 mg/mL). 
These results support the hypothesis that SWH can convert starfish meal into low molecular 
weight peptides with strong antioxidant activity and that process parameters have a major 
impact on the functional properties of the hydrolysate.

1. Introduction 
The purpose of this study is to use subcritical water hydrolysis (SWH) to develop, produce, 
and characterize protein hydrolysates from defatted starfish meal. Investigating whether these 
hydrolysates have antioxidative properties that might be exploited for possible usage in food, 
feed, or nutraceutical applications is the main goal of this study. 
Producing bioactive peptides that may exhibit antioxidant properties is the primary aim of this 
hydrolysis, which enhances the raw material's functional value. Additionally, the study aims to 
show that SWH is a viable green processing approach that does not require the use of harsh 
chemicals or enzymes. Determining the effects of temperature and time variations on the 
degree of hydrolysis, molecular weight distribution, and antioxidative efficacy of the final 
hydrolysates is an additional objective. Another objective is to assess the defatted starfish 
meal's appropriateness as a substrate to produce high-value hydrolysates and to valorize this 
processing by-product. By doing this, the project turns marine waste into valuable ingredients, 
supporting initiatives for a circular bioeconomy. 
The project also involves a preliminary characterization of two raw material types: spin flash-
dried starfish meal (SFD) and defatted freeze-dried starfish meals (FD). Only one defatted 
starfish meal will be chosen for subcritical water hydrolysis based on the results of the 
characterization, technological, and economic feasibility. 
1.1. Hypotheses 
To fulfil the project's aim and objectives, the following hypothesis was put forward:  
1) Low-molecular-weight peptides are produced when defatted starfish meal is hydrolyzed 
with subcritical water. 
 
2) The resulting hydrolysates will exhibit radical scavenging activity in the DPPH assay. 
 
3) The degree of hydrolysis and antioxidant potential will be significantly influenced by 
the processing parameters (temperature and reaction time).

1.2. Background 
1.2.1. Starfish meal as a functional substrate 
Starfish are among many underutilized marine creatures even though they are abundant in 
coastal environments and have a high protein content, especially collagen-rich fractions (Islam 
& Mis Solval, 2025). Starfish are regarded as a pest by the commercial mussel farmers as they 
have 
no 
predators 
and 
prey 
on 
mussels, 
which 
are 
otherwise 
harvested for 
commercial purposes (Afrose et al., 2016). 
In Denmark, Asterias rubens is regarded as invasive because of its expanding population, lack 
of natural predators, and negative impact on biodiversity and mussel fisheries. Although this 
figure is highly dependent on starfish growth and mortality rates, research conducted in 
Western Limfjorden (2013–2015) assessed its maximum sustainable yield (MSY) at 10,000 
tons/year to evaluate control methods. The MSY is highly dependent on biological parameters 
such as growth and natural mortality, making it subject to variability. Through the 
transformation of low-value or discarded biomass into useful, value-added products, the 
valorization of such resources promotes the circular bioeconomy principles (Petersen et al., 
2016). 
Typically, from mussel fisheries, by-catch starfish are cleaned, powdered, and dried to make 
starfish meal, particularly from Asterias rubens. The starfish meal are used as a protein source 
in feed formulation for pigs and poultry. To reduce lipid oxidation, starfish meal is defatted to 
extract omega-3 polyunsaturated fatty acids that are particularly vulnerable to oxidative 
rancidity, such as Docosahexaenoic acid (DHA) and Eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA). As a result, 
the meal's shelf life and oxidative stability are enhanced. The method concentrates protein and 
peptide fractions, making the meal more suitable for use in future processes. This increases the 
meal's potential as a functional component in animal feed or as a substrate for the extraction of 
bioactive substances (Sørensen et al., 2022). 
Because of the numerous industrial uses and broad range of health advantages, there is 
increasing interest in creating bioactive peptides, which are small protein fragments that 
normally contain two to twenty amino acids. These peptides are being utilised increasingly in 
pharmaceuticals, nutraceuticals, and functional foods because of their biological properties, 
which include anti-inflammatory, anti-hypertensive, antimicrobial, immunomodulatory, anti-
cancer, and opioid actions (Korhonen & Pihlanto, 2006). Peptides remain inactive within their 
==PAGE:9==
3 
 
parent proteins until they are released by fermentation or hydrolysis, which further makes it 
possible to target particular functional sequences (Besharati & Lackner, 2023). 
1.2.2. Protein hydrolysis 
When the peptide bonds in proteins are broken, smaller peptides and free amino acids are 
produced. This process is known as protein hydrolysis. This process enhances metabolism and 
bioavailability and can release bioactive peptides with a range of physiological advantages, 
like anti-inflammatory, anti-hypertensive, anti-microbial, and antioxidant properties 
(Fernández-Tomé et al., 2019; Hou et al., 2017).  
It is possible to achieve hydrolysis by thermal, microbiological, enzymatic, or chemical 
methods. Although enzymatic hydrolysis is often constrained by its high enzyme cost and 
sensitivity to processing factors, it is widely utilized because of its selectivity and mild reaction 
conditions. Acidic or alkaline chemical hydrolysis is less selective and can produce hazardous 
byproducts or the breakdown of amino acids, which makes it less appropriate for use in food 
applications (Hou et al., 2017; Mirmoghtadaie et al., 2016). Therefore, subcritical water 
hydrolysis (SWH), on the other hand, has drawn interest as an effective and environmentally 
friendly hydrolysis technique.  
1.2.3. Subcritical water hydrolysis (SWH) 
The term "subcritical water" refers to water that maintains its liquid state at temperatures 
between 100°C and 374°C while under high pressure (1-22.1 MPa). Under these conditions, 
water's physicochemical characteristics such as its ionic product and dielectric constant 
undergo substantial changes, allowing it to function as a weak acid or basic catalyst as well as 
a polar and non-polar solvent (Álvarez-Viñas et al., 2021; King, 2006). Protein hydrolysis is 
made easier by these special qualities, which eliminate the need for additional acids, bases, or 
enzymes. As the temperature rises, water's dielectric constant falls, making hydrophobic 
substances more soluble and encouraging the breakage of peptide bonds through water 
ionization (Álvarez-Viñas et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2018). The different states of water under 
varied pressure and temperature circumstances are shown in the water's phase diagram (Figure 
1).

1.2.4. Applications of SWH in protein hydrolysis 
SWH's ability to hydrolyze proteins into useful peptides and amino acids has led to its increased 
use in the valorization of agro-industrial waste, algae, and marine biomasses such as starfish 
meal.  It has been used to achieve high yields of antioxidant peptides from materials like soy 
meal, fish by-products, and microalgae (Montesano et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2014; Zhu et al., 
2015). Additionally, SWH has been included in biorefinery techniques, enabling the extraction 
of several bioactive fractions from a single biomass, such as proteins, polysaccharides, and 
polyphenols (Álvarez-Viñas et al., 2021).  
The implementation of SWH to defatted starfish meal in this project is anticipated to 
demonstrate a sustainable method of converting a marine by-product into antioxidative 
hydrolysates that could find use in food, feed, and nutraceuticals. 
1.2.5. Advantages and limitations of SWH 
Comparing subcritical water hydrolysis (SWH) to traditional protein hydrolysis techniques 
reveals several noteworthy benefits. It is a chemical-free, environmentally friendly method that 
doesn't require hazardous solvents or outside enzymes, making it safer for use in food and feed 
applications. This approach is very effective and enables quick processing by extracting and 
hydrolyzing proteins simultaneously in one step. The recovery of various bioactive components 
is also made easier by the broad solubility because subcritical water's special thermophysical 
characteristics allow it to dissolve both polar and non-polar substances(Barea et al., 2024).  
==PAGE:11==
5 
 
Additionally, integrated biorefinery technologies are compatible with SWH. It can increase the 
total yield and functionality of extracted compounds by recovering proteins, phenolics, 
carbohydrates, and other bioactive from biomass in a single step or in a series of steps (Álvarez-
Viñas et al., 2021; Marcet et al., 2016). 
SWH has certain drawbacks in addition to its benefits. Thermal degradation is one of the 
primary issues; high temperatures or prolonged exposure can cause sensitive amino acids to 
break down or produce undesirable Maillard reaction products, which may impact on the 
hydrolysates' quality and safety (Ma et al., 2018). For the process to achieve the required 
selectivity and prevent product deterioration, precise control over key variables including 
temperature, pressure, and residence time is crucial ( Zhang et al., 2020). SWH systems also 
have a comparatively high initial investment cost because the equipment needs to be corrosion-
resistant and able to tolerate high temperatures and pressures. This may make it difficult to 
adopt on an industrial scale, especially in small and medium-sized enterprises (Di Domenico 
Ziero et al., 2020). 
1.2.6. Lipid oxidation and Antioxidant activity 
In both food systems and biological tissues, lipid oxidation is a primary factor in quality 
degradation. It eventually affects the safety, shelf life, and nutritional quality of foods by 
producing rancid flavours, off-odours, discolouration, nutrient losses, and harmful secondary 
products such aldehydes and ketones (Pokorný, 2007). Excessive production of reactive 
oxygen species (ROS) in living organisms results in oxidative stress, which promotes chronic 
illnesses like cancer, cardiovascular disease, and neurodegeneration (Pham-Huy et al., 2008). 
By neutralising ROS through processes such free radical scavenging, metal ion chelation, or 
oxidative enzyme inhibition, antioxidants reduce this damage. Despite the effectiveness of 
synthetic antioxidants (such as BHA and BHT), there is an increasing need for natural 
alternatives because of regulatory and health concerns. Natural sources, such as peptides from 
food proteins, have gained attention as safer antioxidants as a result of this change. 
1.2.7. Antioxidant Peptides and DPPH Radical Scavenging 
Antioxidant peptides are short amino acid sequences that can reduce oxidative damage in 
biological systems by scavenging ROS. Through electron donation, metal ion chelation, or 
hydrogen atom transfer, these peptides' hydrophobic residues and particular amino acids such 
==PAGE:12==
6 
 
as histidine, tyrosine, methionine, or cysteine contribute to their radical-quenching activity 
(Udenigwe & Aluko, 2012). 
The efficiency of SWH in converting protein-rich marine byproducts into antioxidant peptides 
has been shown through several studies. Studies by Barea et al. (Barea et al., 2024) and 
Alvarez-Viñas et al.(Álvarez-Viñas et al., 2021) revealed that hydrolysates of fish meal and 
marine algae produced by SWH demonstrated strong DPPH radical scavenging activity, 
particularly at elevated temperatures (160-180 °C). The creation of small molecular weight 
peptides that are rich in amino acids like histidine and tyrosine was mainly responsible for these 
bioactivities. The use of SWH to turn underutilized marine byproducts, such as defatted starfish 
meal, into antioxidative components is supported by these findings. 
Subcritical water hydrolysis yields antioxidant peptides with potential uses in the food, feed, 
and pharmaceutical sectors. They prolong the shelf life in food systems by delaying lipid 
oxidation, which serves as a natural preservative. Their capacity to scavenge ROS is associated 
with potential advantages in preventing diseases linked to oxidative stress in medicines and 
nutraceuticals. They boost immunological response and digestive health in animal feed, which 
helps animals grow more effectively (Álvarez-Viñas et al., 2021; Udenigwe & Aluko, 2012). 
These uses encourage the use of green processing technology to increase the value of marine 
byproducts such as starfish meals. 
1.3. Experimental approach 
The study was conducted to explore the possibility of employing SWH technique to produce 
antioxidative protein hydrolysates from defatted starfish meal. The two raw materials were 
defatted starfish meal that had been freeze-dried and spin-flash-dried, respectively. These were 
the biomass of starfish meals that remained after oil extraction and were stored at -40°C. The 
starfish used to make those meals were caught during the different months of 2019 and 2020.  
To begin the project, both raw materials; Spin flash dried (SFD) and Freeze dried (FD) defatted 
starfish meals were initially subjected to detailed characterization which included tests for 
different analytical parameters; protein content, total amino acid (TAA), free amino acid 
(FAA), dry matter, ash, oil content, carbohydrate content, protein solubility, and molecular 
weight determination of protein. 
==PAGE:13==
7 
 
Only one defatted starfish meal was chosen for additional processing based on the 
characterization results, technological efficiency, and economic viability. Next, using water as 
the only solvent under high-pressure circumstances that kept the liquid state of water below its 
critical point, subcritical water hydrolysis was carried out at three temperatures (125°C, 150°C, 
and 175°C) separately for three time periods (5, 10, and 15 minutes).  
After hydrolysis, liquid hydrolysate extracts from each treatment condition were collected, 
frozen at -20°C, and then freeze-dried to produce solid hydrolysates. Protein content, molecular 
weight determination of protein peptides using, degree of hydrolysis (DH), and antioxidant 
activity using the DPPH radical scavenging assay were all analyzed for the solid hydrolysates.

2.  Materials and methods 
2.1. Sample preparation 
Two defatted starfish meal samples, freeze-dried (FD) and spin flash-dried (SFD), were the 
main raw materials used in this project. Those raw materials were the meals obtained after oil 
extraction, which were stored at -40°C at DTU. The samples were then transferred in plastic 
bags, sealed, and stored in a refrigerator at 4°C until further analysis. The FD sample contained 
some flaky and gritty particles, while the SFD sample contained a homogenous mixture. 
Therefore, to appropriately homogenize the FD sample, it was ground using a mechanical 
grinder. 
2.2. Characterization of starfish meal and protein hydrolysates 
The raw materials before subcritical water hydrolysis and the freeze-dried protein 
hydrolysates(extracts) obtained after subcritical water hydrolysis were characterized by 
analyzing different parameters.   
The protein content and molecular weight analysis using SDS-PAGE were analyzed for both 
raw materials before SWH and protein hydrolysates obtained after SWH. Whereas, TAA, FAA, 
dry matter & ash, oil content, carbohydrate, and protein solubility content were only analyzed 
for the raw materials.  
All the analyses for characterization were carried out in triplicate, except for protein solubility 
and molecular weight analysis using SDS-PAGE, which were carried out in duplicate. 
2.2.1. Protein Content  
Using a Vario EL cube CHNS elemental analyser (Elementar Analysensysteme GmbH, 
Langenselbold, Germany), the protein content was determined using the DUMAS principle in 
accordance with Bioactive manual FF 202.03, which is based on the DUMAS method. 
==PAGE:15==
9 
 
Samples are at around 1800°C at the time of combustion and are completely digested at 
temperatures as high as 1200°C. Thermal conductivity or infrared detectors are used to separate 
and quantify combustion gases to determine the elemental composition of the sample.  
Sulfanilamide (200005129, Elementar GmbH, Langenselbold, Germany) was used in place of 
the standard aspartic acid. A casein and wheat standard (Weizenmehlstandard- 200007751, 
Elementar, Lan genselbold, Germany) were also used. All samples and the standards were 
weighed at approximately 10 mg each for raw materials and 5 mg each for hydrolysate and 
subjected to method 10 mg120s and 5mg 90s respectively. To check for deviance and 
consistency throughout a run, the wheat standard was used in conjunction with the casein 
standard and sulfanilamide in the following order: four Sulfanilamide standards, two wheat 
standards, two casein standards, test samples, and finally one wheat standard. By multiplying 
the sample's nitrogen percentage by a nitrogen-to-protein conversion factor, the protein content 
(%) was calculated. A nitrogen-to-protein conversion factor of 6.25 is normally used, however, 
this is often an overestimation for many decades(Mariotti et al., 2008). Since no specific 
suggested nitrogen-to-protein conversion factor for starfish was found, a conversion factor for 
fish, 5.58, was used to determine the protein content of starfish meal (Mariotti et al., 2008). 
The results are presented as average +/- standard deviations. 
2.2.2. Total amino acid 
The total amino acid composition was determined by Liquid Chromatography-Mass 
Spectrometry (LC-MS) in accordance with Bioactive manual FF 223.01 The samples were first 
weighed at approximately 30 mg each in an ordinary 4 mL glass. Then, 1 mL of 6 M HCl was 
added to each sample. The sample were then covered with nitrogen and closed with Teflon 
inserts and screw caps. The samples were then hydrolyzed in a heating oven at 110°C for 18 
hours. After 18 hours, the samples were taken out of the oven and allowed them cool to room 
temperature, and the hydrolysates obtained were filtered through 0.22μm cellulose acetate 
syringe filters into 4 mL vials using a 1 mL syringe. From the filtered hydrolysates, 100μL of 
hydrolysate was then pipetted into a 4 mL vial and pH was adjusted by adding 1.5 mL 0.2 M 
KOH and 1.6 mL buffer (100 mM Ammonium formate pH 3.1). Then, 1mL of pH-adjusted 
hydrolysate samples were transferred to vials and analyzed using LC-MS (Agilent 1260 Infnity 
II, Glostrup, Denmark) with column: bioZen 2.6μm Glycan, 100 x 2.1 mm (00D-4773 AN). 
Guard ULTRA Cartridges, bioZen Glycan 2.1 mm (AJO-9800) and ULTRA Holder (AJO-
9000 (5.11.5), and ion source ESI (Agilent).  
==PAGE:16==
10 
 
In this method, the mobile phases were mobile phase A: 10 mM ammonium formate in 
acetonitrile (100 mL buffer + 900 mL Acetonitrile) and mobile phase B: 10 mM ammonium 
formate in water (100 mL buffer + 800 mL water). Injection volume was 1 µL, flow rate was 
0.5 mL/min, and column temperature was 40 °C. To verify for deviation throughout the 
analysis, the 15 mg casein house standard was also used. Samples were analyzed the same day 
as they were prepared.  
External standard calibration curves made from a commercial amino acid standard mixture 
(Supelco 79248, 2.5 mM), diluted to 500 µM, were used for quantification. Calibration curves 
were created for each amino acid based on peak area versus concentration using multiple 
injections (0.1–1 µL) of this standard. After taking into consideration sample weight and the 
applied dilution factor (usually 1:32), the peak areas of the samples were compared to those of 
the standards to determine the amounts of amino acids in the samples. The software 
MassHunter was used to acquire and quantify the data. The total amino acid content is 
expressed as milligrams of amino acid per gram of sample.  
 
2.2.3. Free Amino Acids 
The free amino acid composition was also determined by Liquid Chromatography-Mass 
Spectrometry (LC-MS) in accordance with Bioactive manual FF 224.01.  The samples were 
first weighed at approximately 300 mg each. They are then mixed with 3 mL 0.1 M HCl and 
mixed for 30 seconds. Samples were centrifuged for 5 minutes at 5000 g. Then, 250 µL 
supernatant and 1 mL 0.1 M Ammonium formate (buffer) was pipetted in HPLC vials. Samples 
were then analyzed using LC-MS with the same method as described in section 2.2.2 above, 
where casein house standard was also used to verify for deviation throughout the analysis.  
Using external standard calibration curves made from a 500 μM amino acid mixture standard 
(Supelco 79248, 2.5 mM), quantification was done. For each amino acid, calibration curves 
correlating peak area with concentration were generated by injecting known volumes (0.1–1.0 
μL) of the standard. Then, using the standard curves and the sample weight and dilution, the 
concentrations in the unknown samples were determined by comparing their peak regions. The 
software MassHunter was used to acquire and quantify the data. The free amino acid content 
is expressed as milligrams of amino acid per gram of sample.

2.2.5. Oil Content 
The oil content of the samples was determined using the Bligh & Dyer method with the reduced 
use of solvent in accordance with Bioactive manual FF Analysis Instruction no. 109.09, based 
on studies by Bligh & Dyer (Bligh & Dyer, 1959) 
5 g sample was weighed in a centrifuge tube, 10 mL ion-exchange water, and 30 mL methanol 
were added. The tube was then tightly closed with screw cap and cooled on ice. The centrifuge 
tube was fixed to a holder on the Ultra Turrax mixing setup. Then, 15 mL of chloroform was 
added and mixed for 30 seconds at 15,000 rpm. Another 15 mL of chloroform was added and

mixed for 30 seconds. Finally, 15 mL ion-exchanged water was added and mixed for 30 
seconds. The tubes were then centrifuged in a centrifuge (Sigma 4-16KS, Osterode am Harz 
Germany) at 2800 rpm/ 307 g, 18°C for 10 minutes. After centrifugation, the methanol/water 
phase (upper phase) was sucked by vacuum suction. Then, the extract (the chloroform phase) 
was filtered into a 50 ml Pyrex bottle.  
8-9 g of extract was weighed in a dried (30 min at 105°C) pre-weighed beaker, which was 
placed in a fume hood, and immediately, the weight of the extract was noted. The beaker was 
then left overnight in a fume hood to allow the chloroform to evaporate. Thereafter, the beaker 
was dried in the oven at 105°C for 1-2 hours, then cooled in the desiccator and weighed.

of the experiment to ensure homogeneity.

was used as a positive control.

3.  Results and Discussion 
3.1.  Characterization of raw materials 
3.1.1.  Protein Content: 
The protein content of two types of defatted starfish meal samples, freeze-dried (FD) and spin 
flash dried (SFD), was measured. The results are presented in Figure 3. The protein content in 
the FD sample was 42.97 ± 0.58%, which was significantly higher than the protein content of 
the SFD sample, which was 36.01 ± 0.76%. The two samples dried using two different drying 
techniques differed significantly (p < 0.05) as indicated by the p-value of 0.000330 resulted 
from a two-tailed unpaired t-test assuming unequal variances (Welch’s t-test).  
 
Figure 3: Protein Content of Raw Materials. 
The basic variations in the drying techniques and thermal exposure of the FD and SFD may be 
the main reason for the significant difference in protein content between them. Freeze-drying, 
a low-temperature dehydration method that sublimates water under vacuum, reduces thermal 
stress and preserves the nutritional and structural integrity of delicate biomolecules like 
proteins (Ratti, 2001a). On the other side, spin flash-drying, which rapidly removes moisture 
at high temperatures (usually above 100°C), may result in Maillard reactions, protein 
denaturation, or aggregation.  
High-temperature drying methods, such as spin flash drying, are known to cause several 
physicochemical alterations that make precise protein measurement difficult. In protein-
==PAGE:26==
20 
 
determining tests, heat-induced unfolding and cross-linking of proteins can produce heat-stable 
aggregates that are either incompletely hydrolyzed or inadequately extracted. Additionally, 
Maillard reactions involving reducing sugars and amino acids can chemically bond nitrogen in 
ways that are undetectable using standard protein-determining methods (Rahman, 2007). Also, 
small nitrogenous compounds, such as free amino acids and short peptides, may volatilize, 
further lowering the overall nitrogen recovery from SFD samples (Kristinsson & Rasco, 2000). 
These activities typically do not take place during freeze-drying because no heat is involved, 
which indicates that FD samples retain more protein. 
The structure of the dried product might be another crucial element. The amorphous, porous 
structure of samples makes proteins more soluble in freeze-dried samples. However, materials 
that have been thermally dried often show altered or more compact surface structures, which 
reduces their solubility (Ratti, 2001b; Toyomizu et al., 2001). 
 
3.1.2. Total amino acids 
The two defatted starfish meal samples, FD and SFD, were tested for their total amino acid 
composition, assessed using triplicate readings. The results are shown in Figure 4. Rich amino 
acid profiles were found in both samples, with significant concentrations of aspartic acid 
(ASP), glutamic acid (GLU), glycine (GLY), leucine (LEU), and arginine (ARG), all of which 
are typical of marine-derived protein sources (Echave et al., 2022).  
In terms of quantitative analysis, FD samples typically showed greater mean values for several 
amino acids than SFD. For instance, the measurements of glutamic acid were 
43.44 ± 0.91 mg/g in FD and 41.50 ± 0.38 mg/g in SFD; glycine was 56.79 ± 5.60 mg/g and 
50.31 ± 0.20 mg/g; and lysine was 23.94 ± 1.76 mg/g and 22.95 ± 0.74 mg/g, respectively. 
These trends were consistent across most amino acids. Heat-sensitive amino acids like lysine 
(LYS), methionine (MET), and threonine (THR) were consistently reported to be reduced in 
the SFD sample, even though not all of the alterations were statistically significant (p > 0.05). 
==PAGE:27==
21 
 
 
Figure 4: Total Amino Acid Content of Raw Materials 
The effect of high-temperature processing during spin flash-drying could be specifically 
responsible for the minor but steady decline in amino acid contents in the SFD samples when 
compared to FD. It is well known that amino acids such as lysine, methionine, and threonine 
are thermolabile, meaning they can degrade or transform at high temperatures (Kristinsson & 
Rasco, 2000). Specifically, lysine's strong reactivity and potential to engage in Maillard 
reactions after drying reduce its availability in standard acid hydrolysis assays (Rahman, 2007; 
Ratti, 2001). In contrast, freeze-drying is a low-temperature dehydration technique that 
prevents chemical modifications and thermal stress. By sublimating water in a vacuum, it 
maintains the primary and secondary structures of proteins and amino acids. This process 
successfully prevents oxidative alterations, denaturation, and cross-linking that may otherwise 
compromise the integrity of amino acids. As a result, freeze-dried starfish meals maintain a 
more comprehensive and accessible amino acid profile, which is especially important for high-
value applications including aquaculture feed additives, peptide hydrolysates, and functional 
meals (Ratti, 2001). 
The results could have been affected by the physical condition of the dried material in addition 
to chemical deterioration. The formation of more compact, less porous structures in thermally 
dried samples (SFD) may interfere with the full hydrolysis and recovery of amino acids during 
analysis (Mujumdar, 2014a). However, the matrix of freeze-dried material is usually brittle and 
porous, which improves the effectiveness of enzymatic or acid hydrolysis and solvent 
penetration (Ratti, 2001a). 
==PAGE:28==
22 
 
The general amino acid pattern found in both FD and SFD samples aligns with profiles of 
marine invertebrates that are known to have significant amounts of glutamic acid and glycine, 
which contribute to their umami flavor and functional qualities (Venugopal, 2008). Similar 
studies have shown that, in comparison to samples that have been oven or hot air dried, freeze-
dried fishmeal and crustacean protein retain noticeably more amino acids and have a higher 
solubility (Brooks MS, 2013; Toyomizu et al., 2001). 
3.1.3. Free amino acids 
Defatted starfish meal samples FD and SFD were analyzed for free amino acid (FAA) content, 
which is shown in Figure 5. In both FD and SFD samples, glycine (GLY) was the most 
abundant free amino acid, followed by phenylalanine (PHE), alanine (ALA), and arginine

glycine concentration in the FD sample was substantially greater (10.76 ± 0.26 mg/g). Alanine 
and phenylalanine levels in the FD sample were also greater than those in the SFD sample, 
measuring 0.76 ± 0.01 mg/g and 1.34 ± 0.04 mg/g, respectively, compared to 0.49 ± 0.03 mg/g 
and 0.39 ± 0.05 mg/g, respectively. Most of the other amino acids were found in comparatively 
small amounts (<1 mg/g), while some, such cysteine-cysteine (C-C) and threonine (THR), were

Spin flash drying is clearly more effective at removing moisture than freeze-drying, as seen by

The oil content of the two defatted starfish meal samples, SFD and FD, was analyzed. The 
average oil concentration of the FD samples was 5.61 ± 0.34%, whereas the SFD samples had

Both samples' oil contents show that a significant portion of the lipid component was still

the material's structural integrity but may be less successful in eliminating residual oil. On the

oil separation and evaporation and reduce the amount of residual oil. A more constant and 
regulated drying process in SFD is further suggested by the reduced standard deviation shown

extraction or antioxidant evaluations, consistency in oil content is crucial because residual

3.1.8. Molecular weight distribution of proteins 
Using SDS-PAGE, the molecular weight distribution of proteins in FD and SFD starfish meal 
was assessed. Two concentrations of protein samples (10 mg/mL and 20 mg/mL) were added, 
and each experiment was duplicated to ensure reproducibility. As illustrated in figure 14 in 
Appendix B, a NuPAGE® molecular weight standard was added to wells 1 and 12 and utilized 
for molecular weight estimate based on the MOPS SDS buffer system. 
Both FD and SFD samples showed a wide variety of protein bands, with the largest bands 
showing up at 64 kDa, 51 kDa, 39 kDa, and 28 kDa. These bands ranged roughly from 14 kDa 
to >97 kDa. These match the normal molecular weights of several water-soluble proteins, 
including dehydrogenases, serum albumin-like proteins, and other globular proteins that can 
be found in marine invertebrates. Consistent sample loading and concentration-dependent band 
intensity were confirmed by the 20 mg/mL lanes' consistently stronger bands compared to the 
10 mg/mL lanes. 
Interestingly, a comparable pattern of protein molecular weight distribution was maintained by 
both drying techniques. The dependability of protein extraction and SDS-PAGE processing 
was confirmed by duplicate lanes' remarkably comparable banding patterns. 
Strong bands of around 64, 51, and 28 kDa are probably related to structural proteins, cytosolic 
enzymes, and muscle proteins, all of which are highly expressed in echinoderms like starfish 
(Asterias rubens) (Lee, 2009). The presence of proteins in the 28–64 kDa range is in line with 
previous studies on marine invertebrates, which found that common soluble proteins involved 
in energy metabolism and ionic regulation include glutamate dehydrogenase (~51 kDa), 
alcohol dehydrogenase (~39 kDa), and carbonic anhydrase (~28 kDa) (Lee, 2009; Zhou et al., 
==PAGE:35==
29 
 
2011). The band of about 64 kDa might alternatively be a representation of cytoplasmic actins 
or BSA-like proteins, which have been found in echinoderm tissues. 
The band patterns of the FD and SFD samples are similar, suggesting that the protein's 
structural integrity was mainly maintained during the two drying processes. This demonstrates 
that both FD and SFD samples are appropriate for further hydrolysis and functional tests. Subtle 
fluctuations in band intensity, however, might be the result of protein denaturation or 
aggregation during drying, which are known to impact solubility and the effectiveness of 
downstream hydrolysis. 
 
3.2. Subcritical Water Hydrolysis  
3.2.1. Selection of raw materials for SWH 
The SFD material was ultimately chosen for subcritical water hydrolysis (SWH) because of its 
practical benefits and biochemical sufficiency, even though the FD starfish meal had higher 
total and free amino acid concentrations, better protein content, and better protein solubility 
than the SFD samples. SDS-PAGE examination indicated that both FD and SFD samples 
maintained similar molecular weight distributions, suggesting that the primary protein 
structures were maintained in both situations, despite SFD exhibiting somewhat lower protein 
concentration and solubility. This suggests that the SFD sample still included structurally intact 
proteins appropriate for hydrothermal hydrolysis despite mild heat-induced denaturation. 
Furthermore, the total and free amino acid profiles, while slightly lower in SFD due to thermal 
degradation of thermolabile amino acids like lysine and methionine, remained qualitatively 
comparable to FD, suggesting that bioactive peptide precursors were still sufficiently available. 
Furthermore, the SFD sample showed less residual oil than the FD sample, which is helpful for 
hydrolysis and antioxidant activity assays because lipids can affect the rate of oxidation and 
misrepresent analytical findings (Sørensen et al., 2022).  
From a techno-economical perspective also, spin flash drying is a high-throughput, cost-
effective, and energy-efficient process that is frequently used in large-scale manufacturing 
because of its quick operation, reduced labor and energy requirements, and compatibility with 
continuous processing systems (Ratti, 2001). On the other hand, although freeze-drying 
maintains compositional integrity, it is costly, time-consuming, and unsuitable for the large-
==PAGE:36==
30 
 
scale valorization of marine side-streams. Thus, even though the SFD meal had a slightly 
reduced nutrient profile, it provided a practical and scalable solution that matched the industrial 
goal of creating a subcritical water hydrolysis process that is both sustainable and profitable.

hydrolysates from subcritical water hydrolysis of starfish meal varied from 1.56 ± 0.33% to

1.73 ± 0.15%, 1.56 ± 0.33%, and 2.88 ± 0.14% for 5, 10, and 15 minutes, respectively, the

increased to 6.93 ± 1.46%, 8.76 ± 0.74%, and 8.51 ± 1.28%. The yield increased most 
noticeably at 175°C, increasing to 10.15 ± 1.91%, 17.89 ± 0.45%, and peaking at 
21.23 ± 5.80% after 15 minutes. The results are shown in Table 1.

substantially disturb the starfish protein matrix at lower temperatures (125 °C), which leads to

thorough breakdown of protein structures, is responsible for the yield increase observed with 
longer reaction periods. The ANOVA did confirm that the effect of temperature was greater 
than the effect of time. 
The results are consistent with previous studies on SWH-based protein extraction. Protein 
yields of up to 57% were reported by (Melgosa et al., 2021) from cod frame hydrolysis at 
subcritical temperatures of 190–250 °C. (Ng et al., 2024) also found that peptide recovery from 
seafood by-products was considerably enhanced by temperatures exceeding 170°C. 
Comparatively, considering the more moderate processing conditions (maximum 175°C, 15 
min) and the complex biological structure of starfish, which contains non-protein components 
such as calcium-rich dermal layers, the yield obtained in this work (up to 21.23%) is reasonable. 
It's also important to note that the standard deviation at 15 minutes and 175°C was 
comparatively large (±5.80%), indicating duplicate variability. Reproducibility may be 
impacted by variations in heat transfer efficiency or partial thermal degradation at high energy 
input levels. 
Due to the low yield of extract from 125 °C set design, the extract from this set up was not used 
in further analysis except for protein content determination.

3.3.2. Molecular weight distribution of hydrolysates 
Using SDS-PAGE with NuPAGE® 4-12% Bis-Tris gels and MOPS running buffer, the 
molecular weight distribution of starfish protein hydrolysates produced through subcritical 
water hydrolysis (SWH) was evaluated. For calibration, molecular weight markers with a range 
of 191 kDa to 19 kDa were added to both ends of each gel. Lane annotations indicate that 
hydrolysates from two temperatures (150°C and 175°C) and three reaction durations (5, 10, 
and 15 minutes) were examined in duplicate as shown in figure 15 in Appendix B. 
The SDS-PAGE profiles showed distinct patterns of protein degradation that were impacted by 
time and temperature. Bands with high molecular weights (97–191 kDa) and medium 
molecular weights (~28–39 kDa) were visible after 5 and 10 minutes at 150°C, suggesting 
partial hydrolysis with insufficient degradation of bigger protein fragments. But after 15 
minutes, the strength of the lower molecular weight bands increased while the high molecular 
weight bands reduced indicating that the peptide bonds were gradually being broken. 
All time points showed a more pronounced change at 175 °C, where intense bands primarily 
occurred in the 19–28 kDa range and high molecular weight bands were greatly reduced or 
missing. This change indicates that temperature affects hydrolysis more than time, with higher 
temperatures promoting the creation of smaller peptides and speeding up peptide bond 
cleavage. This behaviour is in line with the physicochemical characteristics of subcritical 
water, which at high temperatures promotes the hydrolysis of peptide bonds in an acidic or 
basic manner without the need for chemical catalysts by exhibiting an enhanced ion product 
(Kw) and decreased dielectric constant (Majeed et al., 2024). Furthermore, (Matsui et al., 
1999)found that protein hydrolysates with high hydrolysis levels typically exhibit prominent 
bands on SDS-PAGE below 30 kDa, which is consistent with our observation. Additionally, 
==PAGE:40==
34 
 
(Liu et al., 2010)highlighted that bioactive and antioxidant peptides obtained from marine 
sources frequently have molecular weights of less than 30 kDa, and many are even smaller. 
The substantial presence of peptides between 19 and 28 kDa in the stronger treatment 
conditions suggests possible bioactivity, even though the 14 kDa band from the standard was 
not evident in the gels possibly because of staining limitations or lack in the protein ladder.

hydrolysis at either temperature. These results are consistent with those of (Nikoo et al., 2023)

release of peptides in hydrolysates obtained from fish.

4.  Conclusions and Recommendations 
 
4.1. Conclusion 
The study showed that the production of antioxidative protein hydrolysates from defatted 
Asterias rubens meal can be accomplished effectively using subcritical water hydrolysis. Initial 
analysis of the SFD and FD samples showed that SFD was the preferred hydrolysis substrate 
because it provided better protein solubility, adequate protein quality, and advantages for 
industrial processing, whereas FD had a higher protein  and amino acid content. 
The hydrolysis conditions had a big impact on the outcome. The maximum yield was obtained 
at 175°C for 15 minutes, and the protein concentration in hydrolysates increased with 
temperature and time. Similarly, the degree of hydrolysis showed effective peptide bond 
cleavage at high temperatures. The creation of low molecular weight peptides was validated by 
SDS-PAGE analysis, which showed that greater processing conditions produced clearer bands. 
Higher temperatures and longer durations resulted in a considerable increase in antioxidant 
activity as determined by DPPH radical scavenging. At 175°C, 15 minutes, the highest 
inhibition and lowest EC₅₀ were noted, suggesting that the produced peptides had a robust 
capacity to scavenge radicals. These results support the idea that temperature and time are 
important variables in maximizing bioactivity and that subcritical water hydrolysis encourages 
the production of antioxidative peptides. 
With possible uses in functional foods or nutraceuticals, the study generally supports the use 
of SWH as a sustainable valorization technique for marine by-products. 
4.2. Recommendations 
In light of the study's findings, several approaches are recommended to build on ideas and 
improve the use of subcritical water hydrolysates produced from defatted starfish meal in 
research and industry. To begin with, advanced peptide identification should be carried out in 
order to identify the specific low-molecular-weight peptides that are in charge of the observed 
antioxidant activity. Designing focused applications in food, feed, or nutraceutical 
formulations and establishing structure–function correlations will be simplified with an 
understanding of peptide sequences. 
==PAGE:45==
39 
 
Determining the hydrolysates' functional efficacy in the real world will also require evaluating 
their bioavailability, absorption, and stability in simulated in vivo conditions. To verify the 
health advantages of these hydrolysates, more biological validation utilising cell culture and 
animal models is required, even though the DPPH assay showed encouraging in vitro 
antioxidant activity. Endpoints, including oxidative stress markers, responses to inflammation, 
or lipid oxidation in biological systems may be the focus of these investigations. 
For optimal yield and functionality, temperature, time, and solid-liquid ratios could be adjusted 
by the use of machine learning models or response surface methodology to optimize hydrolysis 
parameters. Subcritical water hydrolysis combined with membrane fractionation or enzymatic 
hydrolysis may also improve selectivity towards functional fractions or increase the recovery 
of bioactive peptides. 
Techno-economic feasibility studies and life cycle assessments should be carried out to 
determine the economic and environmental sustainability of scaling up this process. 
Translating laboratory-scale success into commercial solutions that support marine resource 
valorization methods and the circular bioeconomy would require such comprehensive 
examination.
