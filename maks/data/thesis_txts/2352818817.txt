Abstract 
The intension of the study is to find heavy metal sources and focuses mainly on finding potential copper 
sources. This study has measured Cr, Ni, Cu, Zn, As, Cd, and Pb in 28 soil samples taken from urban and 
residential areas within and around Copenhagen. The soil samples have been taken in the 5 and 30 cm 
depths on sites, which has nearby potential heavy metal sources such as copper roofs and traffic intensive 
roads. Furthermore rainwater before and runoff rainwater after it hits the copper roofs has been measured 
for heavy metal content. 24 out of 28 tested soil samples have heavy metal concentrations, which exceeds 
the Danish Soil Quality Criteria. 8 of the 24 exceeding values are found in residential soil samples and are 
only for lead. 4 urban soil samples have heavy metal concentrations which exceeds the Danish Cut Of 
Criteria whereas 3 of them are from Bispebjerg Cemetery site which have exceeding Pb and Zn 
concentrations. It is mainly Pb which exceeds the Soil Quality Criteria and has exceeding residential values 
that ranges from 81-212 mg/kg Dry Weight (DW) while for urban samples it ranges from 64-758 mg/kg DW. 
All samples from Helsingør highway exceeds the Pb Soil Quality Criteria of (65-151 mg/kg DW) and have 3 
out of 5 samples, which exceeds the Cd Soil Quality Criteria ranging from 0.56-0.63 mg/kg DW. U-test 
revealed that Cu, Zn and Pb median concentration in soil are significantly higher in samples that are less 
than 10 m away from copper roofs compared to samples that are more than 10 m away from copper roofs. 
The Cu concentration was significantly higher in the runoff rainwater when compared to the rainwater 
gathered before it hits the roofs. This is indicated with t-tests that has p-values ranging 0.07 to 0.001. The 
Cu runoff rates for Kronborg Castle, Dance Chapel, Art Chamber Building and Christiansborg Palace are 0.6, 
0.65, 0.75, and 0.93 g/m2y respectively. All of these rates are however only based upon 3 rain events with 
Christiansborg Palace only being based upon 2 rain events.

Introduction 
It is estimated that about 56,000 t and 1,200,000 t of copper diffuses annually into the atmosphere and into 
the oceans respectively via natural causes and anthropogenic processes (Aurubis, 2013). Natural causes can 
be volcanic activity and wind erosion of topsoil (Aurubis, 2013). Anthropogenic processes can be burning of 
fossil fuels, mining and metal production, fertilization and use of pesticides on crop soils, wood 
preservation, copper water pipes and copper roofing (Rauch and Graedel, 2007; Rauch, 2012; Nielsen E. et 
al., 1995; Jensen J. et al., 1996; Falkenberg and Riis, 2002). This study is mainly in search of the origins of 
pollution of copper (Cu), but also for other heavy metals such as chrome (Cr), nickel (Ni), zinc (Zn), arsenic 
(As), cadmium (Cd) and lead (Pb). This study has investigated copper roofs and roads with high Annual 
Average Daily Traffic (AADT) as potential heavy metal sources.  Five different sites are used and are mainly 
in the center of Copenhagen, but are also in residential areas such as Kongens Lyngby and Helsingør. In 
cooperation with Copenhagen municipality the author has gained access to collect soil and rain samples 
from royal places such as Christiansborg Palace, The Royal Library Garden (Det Kongelige Biblioteks Have), 
The Art Chamber Building (Kunstkammerbygningen), Kronborg Palace and Hermitage Hunting Lodge 
(Eremitageslottet). Furthermore, The Dance Chapel (Dansekapellet) and Bispebjerg cemetery was also used 
as sites. As for a site with a high AADT, Helsingør highway near the Technical University of Denmark has 
been used. 
An investigation of previous studies and known heavy metal sources in Copenhagen has revealed that 
Copenhagen municipal has heavy metal pollution on one fifth of its total area of mainly lead (Bondam, et al. 
2008). 1,000,000 tons of contaminated soil is on a yearly average moved from Copenhagen for either 
cleaning or for being stored as landfill (Bondam, et al. 2008). Large sources such as lead in gas and 
incineration emissions are the main contributors to top soil pollution. A study from Falkenberg et al. (2002) 
has made a mapping of heavy metal soil contamination in Copenhagen and is continuously used in this 
study for results comparison. 
Top soil samples have been taken from the 5 and 30 cm depths on the different sites and three main rain 
events have been used to sample rainwater and runoff rainwater from the different copper roofs. The 
Method used to determine the heavy metal content in the soil samples have been by US EPA 3051A 
extraction method and ICP MS. Before extraction, the soil samples went through drying and crushing. The 
copper concentration in the rainwater and runoff rainwater samples where determined by using 
Spectroquant copper test kit, ICP OES and ICP MS. Some rainwater and runoff rainwater samples have had 
their Cr, Ni, Zn, As, Cd, and Pb concentration measured by ICP MS. 
The soil sample results have had their level of contamination evaluated by comparing their concentration 
to Soil Quality Criteria’s (SQC) and Cut Of Criteria’s (COC) given by the Danish ministry of environment 
(Nielsen E. et al., 1995; Miljøstyrelsen, 2015). Furthermore the Contamination Factor (CF) in relation to the 
Background Concentration (BC) have been calculated as well as a Pollution Load Index to evaluate the 
significance of the heavy metal pollution. Statistical tools such Mann-Whitney U-test, t-test and Pearson 
correlation have been used on soil and rain samples in order to highlight potential pollution sources.

Heavy metal pollution models 
The occurrence of diffusive pollution is typically due to a pollution, which has occurred over a long period 
and then either been mixed, diluted or spread out to its surroundings. This means that heavy metals that 
are no-biodegradable, immobile and persistent in the environment are able to accumulate in the soil 
environment and create a diffusive soil pollution (Falkenberg and Riis, 2002; Singh et al., 2011). Heavy 
metals are found naturally in the earth’s crust and Jensen et al., (1996) have done a Danish environmental 
study that has determined the natural distribution of heavy metals in Denmark. The study does not cover 
large cities areas such as Århus, Odense and Copenhagen since soil concentrations are usually higher and 
diverse due to anthropogenic processes that emit heavy metals. It is hard to assess the origins and specific 
sources of heavy metal pollution in an urban area such as Copenhagen because of how there often can be 
many sources located in one place. A study done by Falkenberg et al., (2002) have made a mapping of 
diffusive soil pollution in Copenhagen and have defined five conceptual pollution models called, downfall, 
line, surface, contribution and filling the earth model (seen in appendix 1). These models can help identify 
heavy metal sources by considering how the pollution is distributed throughout the depth and area of the 
soil.  
The downfall model is defined as a load, which originates from the airborne emissions like dust and gases 
from one or multiple point sources (Falkenberg and Riis, 2002). A source like this can for example be a 
chimney from an incineration facility that emits smoke. The diffusive pollution in the top soil will then have 
a tendency to become lower further away it is from the original point source. The downfall area varies 
depending on the wind, topographical, physical conditions (Falkenberg and Riis, 2002). 
The line model is a load that has its origin from an elongated element such as roads or railroads. The 
diffusive pollution will than decrease perpendicularly from the line source as the distance increases 
(Falkenberg and Riis, 2002).  
The surface model is used to describe loads which originates from evenly spreading of media such as the 
usages of slag, bog iron ore, sewage sludge etc. The diffusive soil pollution represents a relatively uniform 
distribution in the top soil layer over the entire affected area (Falkenberg and Riis, 2002). 
Contribution model describes the diffusive pollution that is varying and random load on the top layer of the 
entire area. It is derived from small random contributions to the soil through centuries of different cultural 
layers in relation to how the city grows (Falkenberg and Riis, 2002). 
Filling the earth model is used on sites that has systematically filling of soil, waste, or materials with 
unknown origins. Filling areas have usually no clear description of its boundaries and are typically low-lying 
areas, harbors, coastal areas and land development. This results in a random and varying pollution in depth 
over the entire area (Falkenberg and Riis, 2002).

Heavy metal disposition from the atmosphere 
Heavy metal deposition from the atmosphere has been measured since 1980 on Danish stations. The 
deposition affects the Danish land area in the upper soil layer (0-20 cm) and is the sum of dry and wet 
deposition. Heavy metals that are bound to particles in the atmosphere are measured and used to calculate 
the dry desposition. The number of station that measures this has however changed from 5 to 2 stations 
from the second half of 2010 (Ellermann et al., 2015b). Wet deposition is measured from the rain and have 
also undergone station alterations throughout the years but is usually measured at either eight, seven or 
==PAGE:10==
Page 9 of 80 
 
six stations. All of these changes makes the measuring program thinner than previous years and can cause 
larger uncertainties in the total deposition estimate (Ellermann et al., 2015b). The 2014 locations of the 
different measuring stations in Denmark can be seen in appendix 2, while total deposition estimates from 
different years can be seen in table 3. 
The variation of the total deposition varies a lot from year to year and depends on different factors. 
Significant factors are the actual emissions of the various sources in the nearby area, the direction and 
intensity of the wind, the amount and intensities of rain and the natural contribution in form of dust. 
Changing to better analysis method has also influenced measurement especially for Cd (Ellermann et al., 
2015b). Large decreasing changes to Pb are observed in table 3 and are mainly due to the removal of lead 
in gas and improvements of smoke cleaning (Ellermann et al., 2015b). Lead in gas was regulated since 1978 
and was since then removed in 1994 (Falkenberg and Riis, 2002).

Danmark are made of metals which corresponds to about 32,825,000 m2. In relation to how much is made 
from either copper, zinc and lead is not known. Copper roofs in Denmark are often found on older buildings 
in cities or on castles. The color of copper roofs varies a lot but is usually known for their corrosion green 
patinated color, which comes with time due to atmospheric corrosion from wet and dry deposition. The 
presence of pollutants such as SO2, NOX, O3, HCL, and NaCl determine the formation rate and the 
composition of corrosion products (pantina) (He W., 2002). An example of a copper roofs weathering cycle 
color change can be found in appendix 4. 
He W. (2002) concluded that corrosion rates are initially high followed by a continuous decrease to a lower 
rate during steady state conditions of both copper and zinc roofs. Runoff rates are however relatively 
constant on a yearly perspective for both metals and field exposures in the urban city of Stockholm showed 
annual runoff rates of 1.3 g Cu/(m2y) and 3.1 g Zn/(m2y) (He W., 2002). The protective patina layer 
formation rate on copper roofs where always dominating the dissolution rate while with zinc periods with 
higher runoff rate than formation rate would occur and would result in patina loss (He W., 2002). 
The runoff quantity and concentration of copper and zinc are affected by several precipitation parameters 
such as rain volume, pH, rain intensity and length of dry periods in between rain events (He W., 2002). The 
concentration increases with rain acidity and during a rain event the metal concentration is higher during 
the initial flush when compared to steady-state runoff (He W., 2002). The runoff rates from naturally aged 
copper roofs showed a slight enhancement with copper patina age according to He W., (2002). The 
opposite is however suggested according to Michels et al., (2003), which found that older copper roof 
released half the amount of copper in comparison to new copper roofs. No intrinsic effect of panel age on 
the runoff rate was found for naturally patinated zinc roofs (He W., 2002). 
Copenhagen municipality is interested to decouple the runoff rainwater from Christiansborg Palace into the 
harbor in order to prevent flooding during heavy rain events (pers. comm. Kristoffersen, 2016). However 
since the Palace mainly consists of copper roof, it is a question whether it would be environmentally 
responsible to discharge it directly into the harbor without any precautions. A copper roof study has shown 
that the ionic Cu concentration is sufficient to be acutely toxic to Daphnia pulex at the roof downspout and 
drain, but toxicity is not observed in the balance of the watershed (Michels et al., 2003). According to 
Michels et al., (2003) by simply allowing the storm water to flow through the drainage system will lower its 
ionic Cu concentration to the level where environmental harm is not observed. The runoff results from He 
W., (2002) and Michels et al., (2003) are shown in table 25 in the discussion section and used for 
comparison.

Methods 
Surface soil sampling 
The standard operating procedure of collection surface soil samples has been done by following instruction 
from Stefan Trapp (pers. comm. Trapp S., 2016) and Ecology and Environment, Inc. (E & E, Inc., 1997). 
Firstly the top soil layer was removed on to a plastic bag by the use of a spade in a depth of 5 cm. Then by 
using a potting trowel nine small scopes where picked randomly on the removed slab of soil and stored in a 
250 mL plastic bottle for a total volume of about 50 – 150 ml. The spade was then used to dig further down 
to 30 cm where the removed soil was placed on another plastic bag. With the potting trowel nine small 
scopes where then picked in the bottom of the sample holes side walls and stored in another 250 mL plastic 
bottle. Both the spade and potting trowel was cleaned with paper towels before each sample extraction. 
pH of soil 
The pH in the soil samples was determined by suspending the samples in 0.01 M CaCl2 solution as described 
by Borggaard et al., (2003) and the International Organization for Standardization (ISO 10390, 2005). 10 g of 
each soil samples were placed in a 50 mL centrifuge tube with 25 mL of 0.01 M CaCl2 solution. The tubes 
were then stirred by placing them on a shake table for 50 min. After stirring the tubes were centrifuged so 
that the pH could be measured in the water and soil phase. The pH was firstly measured in the water phase 
until the value was stable and then likewise in the soil phase by carefully pushing the electrode into the soil 
phase. Calibration of the pH meter (MeterLab PHM210) was done with three buffer solutions with pH of 4, 
7 and 10. The electrode was flushed with distilled water and dried with delicate task wipes in between each 
sample. 
Water content of soils 
The water content of the soil samples were measured by calculating the weight difference between the wet 
and dry soil. The wet soil weight was calculated by weighing the empty 250 mL plastic bottle before the soil 
sample was added then weighing it again afterwards and subtracting the empty plastic bottle weight. The 
dry weight was weighed after drying in an oven for at least 48 hours at 50°C. 
Vibratory disc mill (with 2mm sieving) 
Before the soil samples could be reduced to powder (0.05 mm) with a vibratory disc mill each sample had 
to undergo 2 mm sieving. This was done manually by hand sieving with a sieving set made out of stainless 
steel. Whatever sample that remained in the 2 mm sieve got discarded. Before each sieving the whole 
sieving set had to be thoroughly cleaned with compressed air by blowing it with an air blow gun. A 
vibratory disc mill with agate discs was used to reduce the samples into powder. Before the first milling run 
the grinding set was cleaned by milling 50 mL of sand from Dansand. Each sample underwent 3 to 4 
minutes of milling at 710 rpm. The reduced sample was then transferred by emptying the grinding set on a 
clean piece of paper and then into its cleaned 250 mL plastic bottle. In between each milling run the entire 
grinding set was cleaned thoroughly with compressed air under air extraction from a vacuum cleaner. 
Furthermore the insides of the vibratory disc mill and the worktable where vacuumed in between each run. 
==PAGE:16==
Page 15 of 80 
 
US EPA 3051A 
The method used for extracting Cr, Ni, Cu, Zn, As, Cd and Pb from the soil samples are US EPA 3051A. All of 
the soil samples which have been reduced to powder have been shaken thoroughly in their 250 mL 
containers. Afterwards 10-13 mL of each sample is then transferred to a new 20 mL plastic vial and then 
shaken thoroughly. These steps are done in order to ensure a homogeneous mixture. Now from each of the 
20 mL plastic vials 0,25 g is weighed and transferred to a fluorocarbon polymer vessel. The 0,25 g was 
weighed with four decimals and noted using an analytical balance (Sartorius CPA224S). 9 mL HNO3 65 % 
and 3 mL HCL 37 % where then added to each vessel and then shaken before it was sent to digestion with 
an Anton Paar Multiwave 3000. After digestion each vessel cap was filled with ultrapure water from a Holm 
& Halby water system and flushed down into the vessel. The content of each vessel was then transferred to 
a 150 mL volumetric flask. The vessel was then flushed with ultrapure water and transferred to the same 
150 mL volumetric flask. Each 150 mL volumetric flask was then filled with ultrapure water up to the 150 
mL mark. The volumetric flasks where then shaken and 45 ml of their content was transferred to 50 mL 
conical centrifuge tubes, the leftover solution was discarded. The 50 mL tubes where centrifuged at 2500 
rpm for 10 min. Lastly right after centrifugation 20 mL from each tube was transferred to new 20 mL plastic 
vials while remaining solution was discarded. At this point, the samples were ready for ICP MS. 
ICP MS, Agilent 7700x (Cu and Zi) 
The inductively coupled plasma source converts the atoms of the elements in the samples to its basics ions. 
The ions are then separated and detected by mass spectroscopy (Wolf R. E., 2005). A standard curve is 
made from a stock solution called DTU-3. DTU-3 is a custom ICP-MS standard that DTU Environment metal 
lab orders from Inorganic Ventures. The solution contains all of the seven metals in 100 mg/L. 1 mL of DTU-
3 is mixed with 9 mL of ultrapure water to make a 10.000 µg/L stock solution which is then used to make 
the other dilutions seen in table 1. All of the blank samples are mixed and used in the standard curve to 
determine the detection limit.

Rain sampling 
The rain samples which have been gathered were either taken before or after the water has run off the 
different roofs. The basic method used to collect rain before it hits the roofs have either been by placing 
rain gauges on top of the roof or on a balcony which is close by and are about the same height as the roof. 
The rain gauges are made out of plastic and are mounted with a clamp on to a metal stand. The runoff rain 
from the roofs has been collected from the downspouts by holding a 20 mL plastic vials at the opening 
while it rains. On each rain event three 20 mL plastic vails would be used to test for metal content and a 
fourth for pH measurement. In most cases, the downspouts are closed and have a cover in the ground close 
by where the downspout opening is located. The specific vials are called “Scintillation vial 20 mL” and are 
made of high-density polyethylene equipped with a screw cap. 
==PAGE:17==
Page 16 of 80 
 
Each rain sample which were intended to be tested for copper content had its pH lowered to 4 with HNO3 
0.1 M and stored in a cooler. A full 20 mL rain sample usually needed 2-4 drops in order to lower the pH. At 
one location (Kronborg) 250 mL blue cap bottles were used to collect runoff rain water because of limited 
access to the site. All of the rain gauges and blue caps used for collecting rain samples have undergone acid 
washing before sampling. 
pH of rain samples 
The pH of the rain samples was mostly measured on site with a field pH meter by submerging the electrode 
into a separate and full 20 mL vial. The pH was noted until the value was stable and the electrode was 
wiped clean in between each measurement. All of the rain gauge samples did not have their pH measured 
right after sampling which is due to the fact that enough water had to be collected to submerge the 
electrode properly. This means that all rain gauge samples have at least been exposed to the atmosphere 
for 2 days without measuring the pH. 
Copper test spectroscopy 
Copper concentration in the rain samples was measured by using a Spectroquant copper test kit with a 
Nova 60 photometer from Merck. The test kit detection limit is 0.02 – 6.00 mg/L Cu and has a Cat. #: 
1.14767.0001 at Merck. Before testing the spectrophotometer was reset by using distilled water in a 10 
mm quartz cuvette. The copper kit was tested by measuring a CuCl solution with 3 mg/L which gave results 
in the range of 2.98 – 3.1 mg/L. The copper kit consists of reagent Cu-1 and Cu-2 where the first is a powder 
and the second is a liquid. A disposable test tube with lid and 5 mL rain sample was used for each 
measurement. After adding one spoon full of the first reagent with the included dosing spoon a lid was 
applied and shaken vigorously until the reagent was completely dissolved in the rain sample. Then five 
drops of the second reagent was added which was left to react for five minutes. The quartz cuvette was 
then filled with the solution and wiped clean with delicate task wipes and measured in the 
spectrophotometer. The rain that had three 20 mL vials were all tested once while the rain samples which 
were collected with 250 mL blue caps had their content tested three times each. The quartz cuvette was 
flushed with distilled water before each test. 
ICP OES (control of photometric copper test) 
ICP atomic emission spectroscopy breaks up the molecules in the sample into its respective atoms which 
then lose their electrons which gives off characteristic wavelengths of the elements involved (Hou & Jones, 
2000). The method has been used to measure the copper concentration in some of the rain runoff samples 
and in order to compare and validate the results from the photometric copper test kit. Furthermore all of 
the rain samples which were below the detection limit of the photometric copper kit have also undergone 
[Cu] testing. The standard curve was made from the same DTU3 solution as used in the ICP MS analysis and 
was diluted to eight different standards as seen in appendix 17. The detection limit was defined as the 
smallest used standard divided by 3.85 as seen in appendix 17. “Drinking Water - HIGH” was used as 
reference Cat.#140-025-032 (SCP SCIENCE, 2016).

Level of soil contamination 
Three different methods are used to characterize the degree of contamination in the soil samples. The first 
is by comparing the results with SQC and COC values that are published by the Danish ministry of 
environment (Nielsen E. et al., 1995; Miljøstyrelsen, 2015). The SQC values are used to ensure sensitive 
usage of the soil is without risk to human health, in context of gardens, nurseries or playgrounds. Special 
care is taken to direct exposure to babies and small children (Nielsen E. et al, 1995; Miljøstyrelsen, 2015). 
The SQCI (Soil Quality Criteria Index) will be calculated by dividing the measured concentrations with 
corresponding SQC values. The COC values are used to indicate whereby areas with sensitive usage such as 
housing and childcare areas should make complete cutoff (Miljøstyrelsen, 2015). 
Second method for characterizing degree of contamination is by calculating the Contamination Factor (CF) 
for each element. This is done by dividing the soil sample concentrations with Danish background 
concentrations in relation to its specific soil type. An environmental study done by Jensen J., et al., (1996) 
have compiled background concentrations given as medians for sandy and clay soils by testing 226 and 167 
test sites respectively (seen in table 7). Since loamy soil is a mixture of sand and clay the author has chosen 
to define the background concentration of loamy soils as an average of the sand and clay values. CF values 
that are less than 1 will refer to low contamination while CF > 10 indicates high contamination (Mmolawa 
et al., 2011).

Pearson correlation analysis has been done by the use of Microsoft excel. The chosen significant threshold

Soil Quality Criteria Index 
SQCI values that are larger than 1 will have a concentration that exceeds the SQC given by the Danish 
ministry of environment (Nielsen E. et al., 1995; Miljøstyrelsen, 2015). It is observed in figure 10 that Cd has 
eight SQCI values that are larger than 1. Three of them are measured in soil samples east from Helsingør 
highway (S3A, S3B and S4A). This site is in general not accessible to the public and should therefore not 
generate any concerns towards human health. The other five Cd SQCI that are larger than 1 are measured 
behind the Dance Chapel building in Bispebjerg cemetery which is a site that is accessible to the public. 
Furthermore three Zn indexes from the same site has also shown to be large than 1 with the one being 2.64 
actually exceeding the COC given by the Danish ministry of environment (Nielsen E. et al., 1995; 
Miljøstyrelsen, 2015). In figure 10 an index of Ni is measured from the Christiansborg courtyard of 1.49 
which also exceeds the COC.

The median element abundance for soils samples that are more or less than 10 m away from a copper roof 
is the same and follows the order of: Zn > Pb > Cu > Cr > Ni > As > Cd as seen in table 13. The sequence is 
the same for urban soil samples, but not for residential samples were, Zn and Pb are transposed. The 
Mann-Whitney U-test indicates that concentration of Cu, Zn and Pb were significantly higher in samples 
that are less than 10 m from a copper roof. For Cr, Ni, As and Cd no significant difference is shown. The 
urban and residential comparison has all but for Pb significantly higher concentrations in urban soil samples 
as seen in table 13. The reason for Pb not being significantly different is due to the soil samples at 
Hermitage H. L. and Kronborg Castle, which have high concentrations that all exceeds the SQC with a 100 to 
400 %. This supports the fact that the leaded materials on the buildings such as the flashings, paint and 
window frames may contribute to soil pollution.  
Heavy metals in relation to soil depth 
Sample holes with higher metal concentration in the upper soil layer compared to the lower soil layer 
indicates that that the pollution was not done by filling the earth since that would cause a mixing of the 
pollution. A one sided paired t-test has been done on the sample holes which have had their 5 and 30 cm 
depth analyzed in table 12. It is observed that sample hole S1, S8, S10, S13 and S14 have p-values below 
0.05 %, which means that the heavy metal concentration in the upper layer are significantly higher than the 
lower layer or vice versa. For the background sample DTU (S1A and S1B) and for Kronborg C. east (S8A and 
S8B) it is observed that all of the 7 metals are higher in the 5 cm layer in the 30 cm (table 8). It is also 
observed that in both sampling holes from Kronborg C. S8 and S9 have 77 % and 110 % more Cu in the 5 cm 
depth than in the 30 cm depth respectively, which indicates that the nearby roof is polluting the soil (table 
8).

For Dance Chapel sample (S10A and S10B) and Christiansborg courtyard sample (S14A and S14B) 7 out of 7 
metals have found to be higher in the 30 cm layer than in the 5 cm layer. The S10A Dance Chapel soil whole 
is 50 m east from the Chapels copper roof and have assumingly no nearby pollution source other than 
atmospheric deposition. The wet and dry deposition on S10 has slowly worked its way through the loam 
layer and down into the clay layer by rain into the 30 cm depth where the metals can accumulate. In 
contrast Dance Chapel soil hole S12 and S13 which are 20 cm away from the copper roof have 187% and 
193% higher Cu concentration in the 5 cm layer than in the 30 cm layer respectively. The frequent watering 
of Christiansborgs courtyard during dry periods is likely the reason why the concentrations are highest in 
the 30 cm layer compared to its 5 cm layer. 
Chromium 
None of the Cr concentrations in the soil sample exceeds the SQC (figure 10), but where significantly higher 
in urban soil samples with a median of 20.2 mg/kg compared with residential soil samples median of 12.7 
mg/kg as seen in table 1. Compared to other Copenhagen urban studies the 20.2 mg/kg median is within 
the same range as seen in table 17. The urban median is only 18 % higher than the background 
concentration, which corresponds well with it having a low deposition values from the atmosphere 
compared to the other six metals. The highest Cr concentrations were measured in the Chrstiansborg 
Courtyard samples (S4A and S4B) with 40 mg/kg and 80 mg/kg in the 5 and 30 cm depth respectively. The 
==PAGE:32==
Page 31 of 80 
 
difference from this site and the other urban sites is that it is more open to traffic in a sense that cars can 
drive in the courtyard and that it is not shielded by buildings. Furthermore, the third highest Cr 
concentration is from S4A at Helsingør highway, which indicates that traffic is a source to Cr pollution. 
However since it is stated that new stone dust has been added to the courtyard 3-5 years ago (pers. comm.  
Kristoffersen, 2016) the pollution is likely to have been brought in by the new dust material. There is also 
the chance that since only one sample whole was tested on the site an unknown point source could be the 
source. 
Nickel  
The SQC and COC is the same for Ni and one soil sample (S14B) of 45 mg/kg have exceeded it. Significantly 
higher concentration where found in urban soil samples than in the residential soil samples with medians of 
17.4 mg/kg and 7.5 mg/kg respectively seen in table 13. Ni has about twice as higher atmospheric 
deposition than Cr, which corresponds to Ni has a 78 % higher median than the BC in clay soils compared to 
Cr only being 18 % higher than the BC. A strong positive correlation is found between Ni and Cr as seen in 
table 14. The highest concentrations of the two metals have been measured in the same sample hole S14 at 
Christiansborg courtyard while the lowest for the two metals are at Kronborg C. The correlation may be 
linked to chrome and nickel plating on transport vehicle’s, which is used on metal components for 
protective and esthetic values. As seen in figure 12 on Ni has higher contamination factors on the H. 
highway site compared to the other residential sites which indicates that traffic is a pollution source. 
Copper 
No Cu concentrations exceeded the high SQC of 500 mg/kg. The medians where however found to be 
significantly larger for the two U-test with urban and “<10m from roof” samples being 317% and 327 % 
respectively larger than their U-test counterparts. The atmospheric deposition of Cu is usually more than 2 
times higher than Ni. This corresponds well when comparing the medians of Cu and Ni of residential and 
“>10m from roof” samples which have 130 % and 92 % larger Cu medians than Ni medians respectively. The 
same tendency is found for urban and “<10m from roof” medians only that the differences are now more 
than 6 and 3 times higher between Cu and Ni respectively. This indicates that pollution has to come from 
other sources than atmospheric deposition such as higher traffic intensities and presence of nearby copper 
roofs. The highest Cu concentrations were measured at the Dance Chapel site in 5 cm soil samples 20 cm 
away from the roof ranging from 108 to 370 mg/kg. The samples where close to, the roofs gutter and it was 
observed that the top of the downspout was usually partially blocked which may cause the water to flow 
over the gutter during heavy rain events and thus directly on to the nearby soil. Pictures of the gutter can 
be seen in appendix 12. Another nearby source to Cu could be the cremation emissions from the chimney, 
which is 2 m south from the copper roof of the Dance Chapel. The Chimney is no longer used for 
cremations today according to pers. comm. Ranvig N. (2016), but since when is unknown. 
The concentration of Cu in The Royal Library Garden and Chrstiansborg courtyard ranged from 85-23 mg/kg 
and where 47 % and 13 % higher in the 30 cm depth than the 5 cm depth respectively. This indicates that 
the main pollution source is atmospheric deposition.

Zinc 
Zn concentration medians are significantly higher for both urban and “<10m from roof” samples when 
compared to their U-test counterparts as seen in table 13. Three zinc concentrations exceeded the SCQ 
while one also exceeded to COC as seen in table 13. These high concentrations were all measured at the 
Dance Chapel site, which were 20 cm south, and east from the copper roof. Between the three soil holes 
(S11, S12 and S13) that are 20 cm away from the Dance Chapel copper roof have an average of 521 and 692 
==PAGE:34==
Page 33 of 80 
 
mg/kg Zn in the 5 and 30 cm layer respectively. These average are more than 3 times larger than the 
concentrations measured in the S10 sample hole which is 50 m west from the roof. This indicates that the 
zinc/lead roof, which is in between the two copper roofs, is a source. Among the tested the seven tested 
metals Zn is the one that has the highest atmospheric deposition since 1985 as seen in table 3 on. 
Arsenic 
As showed a significant larger median in urbans samples compared to residential samples seen in table 13. 
The urban and residential median is however only 24 % higher and lower respectively than the background 
concentration found in clay, which suggests there is no significant pollution in neither environments. No 
concentrations exceeded the SQC. Among the 7 tested metals it is the one with second lowest atmospheric 
deposition which corresponds to the low contamination factors calculated in the two background 
concentration samples seen in table 15. A one sided paired t-test between [As] in the 5 cm layer and [As] in 
the 30 cm layer gave a p-value of 0.04, which suggest that As has a tendency to accumulate in the lower soil 
layers. 
Cadmium 
Cd concentration medians are significantly higher for both urban and “<10m from roof” samples when 
compared to their U-test counterparts as seen in table 13. The medians for urban and “<10m from roof” 
sample are 122 % and 72 % higher than the background concentrations in clay. Five concentrations from 
the Dance Chapel site have exceeded the SQC for Cd. This may be because of Cd is geologically closely 
related to zinc (Miljøstyrelsen, 2011) and that why the highest concentrations of both Zn and Cd are found 
on the same site. As seen in table 14 there is a positive correlation between Zn and Cd. Cd is the metal 
among the seven tested metals that has the lowest atmospheric deposition, which suggest that pollution 
found behind the Dance Chapel is due to nearby anthropogenic sources. Another source of Cd could be 
fertilizer (Jensen J. et al., 1996) however according to Bispebjerg cemetery gardener no fertilizer is used on 
the tested areas (pers. comm. Ranvig N., 2016). 
Lead 
The atmospheric deposition of Pb has been high since the 1970 (Jensen J. et al., 1996) and is today still one 
of the largest among the seven metals as seen in table 3. Even with the reduction of lead in gas and 
improvements of smoke cleaning there is still emitted large amounts of Pb into the atmosphere from waste 
incineration and foreign countries. The urban background concentration has a twice as high contamination 
factor than the residential background sample seen in table 14. A significant higher mean was calculated in 
“<10m from roof” samples and has a median that is 88 times larger than the background concentration as 
seen in table 13. The urban and residential U-test did not reveal any significant p-value, which is properly 
due to the Kronborg C. concentrations which acts like strong outliers. This indicates that the lead material 
used on the roof and building facades is a source to soil Pb pollution. The Pb concentrations was also less 
variable in the residential sample compared to the urban, which may indicate that atmospheric deposition 
is the dominant source at the residential sites. 
Correlation 
The strength of linear association between the heavy metal concentrations in the soil samples have been 
investigated in table 6 using all 28 soil samples. It is assumed that statistically related metals may reflect 
similar sources of contamination. Cu is significantly correlated with Zn, Cd and Pb, which suggest that a 
common contamination source like traffic i.e. combustion emissions, metal component wear or road dust.

Distance from road edge 
Table 16 shows a comparison between the Helsingør highway Cr, Zn and Pb results with two other roads 
named Vejenbrød and Rud which have AADT of 29,000 and 22,000 respectively. This means that Helsingør 
highway has a ADD which is at least 2 times larger. It is observed Helsingør 1 m concentrations are lower 
than Vejenbrød 1 m concentrations for all three metals. All of Helsingør highway concentrations are 
significantly higher than Rud concentrations and both roads seem to have same tendency to increase in 
concentration with increasing distance. It is explained by Falkenberg and Riis (2002) that Rud was four years 
old during sampling, which explains that the traffic has not yet had time to pollute the nearby soil. Same 
reasoning cannot be used for Helsingør highway since it has been used since 1956. Another difference 
between Helsingør highway and the other two roads is that the speed limit is significantly higher on 
Helsingør highway. This may be the reason why the concentration profile is slower to increase in relation to 
distance from the edge because of how metal particles are able to reach further distances.

Conclusion 
24 out of 28 tested soil sample have heavy metal concentrations which exceeds the Danish SQC . 8 of the 
24 exceeding values are found in residential soil samples and are only for Pb. 4 urban soil samples have 
heavy metal concentrations which exceeds the Danish COC whereas 3 of them are from Bispebjerg 
Cemetery site which have exceeding Pb and Zn concentrations. Furthermore for Bispebjerg Cemetery is 
that it has five samples which exceeds the Cd SQC and 3 in total that exceeds the Zn SQC. The fourth 
exceeds COC is for Ni and was sampled from Christiansborg Palace Courtyard. It is majorly Pb which 
exceeds the SQC and has exceeding residential values that ranges from 81-212 mg/kg DW while for urban 
samples it ranges from 64-758 mg/kg DW.  
All samples from Helsingør highway exceeds the Pb SQC (65-151 mg/kg DW) and have 3 out of 5 samples 
which exceeds the Cd SQC ranging from 0.56-0.63 mg/kg DW. The metal concentration along Helsingør 
highway had a common tendency to increase with distance away from the road edge which is the opposite 
compared to Vejenbrød results. The contamination factor for Pb was calculated to be 14 and 13 20 m away 
from Helsingør road edge and 30 m away from Hermitage Hunting Lodge respectively. 
Soil samples along Kronborg Castles copper roof have contamination factors which are 15-23 for Cu and 14-
17 for Pb. 5 cm depth soil sample that are 20 cm away from The Dance Chapel copper roof have measured 
108 to 370 mg/kg, which is 4-14 times higher compared to the urban background concentration of 26 
mg/kg. 
The urban contamination factor (CFUrban) for Cu, Zn, Cd and Pb are significantly higher than the residential 
contamination factors (CFresidential). Based upon one background sample hole from a residential and one 
from a urban environment the CFUrban values were found to be 256% > CFCu-Residential, 273% > CFZn-Residential 
115%> CFCd-Residential 103%> CFPb-Residential. 
U-test revealed that Cu, Zn and Pb median concentration in soil are significantly higher in samples that are 
less than 10 m away from copper roofs compared to samples that are more than 10 m away from copper 
roofs. Pearson’s correlation analysis also showed significant correlation between the three metals Cu, Zn 
and Pb. U-test revealed that Cr, Ni, Cu, Zn, As and Cd median concentration in soil are significantly higher in 
urban samples compared to sample that are from residential sites. 
The two highest metal concentration found in the background soil samples (S1 and S10) are both Zn and Pb 
which are the two metals with the highest atmospheric deposition values throughout the years. The third 
highest in the urban background soil sample (S10) is Cu which is also the third highest atmospheric 
deposition metal. 
The four highest heavy metals found in the rain gauge results are the same metals that have the highest 
atmospheric deposition for 2014. Zn was consistently the highest just like the atmospheric deposition and 
was followed with Cu which is also in the top three for atmospheric deposition. 
The Cu concentration was significantly higher in the runoff rainwater when compared to the rain gauge 
rainwater for all of the tested copper roofs and even for the brick roof with only a copper downspout. The 
Pb concentration was also found to be significantly higher for the Dance Chapel runoff rainwater. Runoff 
concentrations for As and Cd are significantly identical with the rain gauge concentration for Kronborg 
Castle and Arth Chamber Building respectively. The Cu runoff rates for Kronborg Castle, Dance Chapel, Art 
Chamber Building and Christiansborg Palace are 0.6, 0.65, 0.75, and 0.93 g/m2y. All of these rates are 
however only based upon 3 rain events with Christiansborg Palace only being based upon 2 rain events. 
==PAGE:48==
Page 47 of 80 
 
No control setup with none copper roof and none copper downspout was tested in this study, but for 
comparison a none metal roofs runoff rainwater was tested by Larsen et al. (2012) and showed significantly 
lower Cu concentration of 19µg/L. The Cu runoff concentrations from this study’s copper roofs ranged from 
1.05 to 1.63 mg/L which exceeds the LV of 0.8 mg/L given by Michels et al. (2003). However since the LV is 
for ionic Cu it cannot be concluded weather the runoffs are toxic for Daphnia pulex. 
In comparison to other studies is seems that Copenhagen have several sites that exceeds the SQC. This 
study’s limited number of soil samples is not enough to map the soil contamination on any of the tested 
sites. The pollution impact of copper roofs seems to be significantly smaller when compared to other 
anthropogenic processes such as traffic and incineration emissions especially on distances that more than 
10 m away. A significant impact from copper roofs is however likely if the runoff rainwater is directly 
discharged on to the nearby soil. This is observed on the Dance Chapel soil samples where it is likely that 
runoff water has directly discharged on to the nearby soil when the downspout is clogged with leaves. It is 
however agued by Michels et al. (2003) that the sewer system itself is sufficient enough to remove the ionic 
Cu in the runoff water and thus making it none toxic to aquatic organisms. More study of how the Cu 
concentration changes throughout the sewer system is needed in order to determine if a decoupling of 
Christiansborg Palace runoff water is sustainable for the harbor. The use of sand filters could be used to 
clean to runoff water before emitting it into the harbor.

20. Top soil heavy metal study comparison 
Following appendix is a comparison of soil sample results from this study with other similar top soil studies. Amager (NKT-rolling mill) results are from 
Falkenberg et al. (2004). Østerbro, Brønshøj-Husum and Valby results are from Falkenberg and Riis (2002). Lastly, Cd, Cu and Pb results from Li et al. 
(2014) are also used for comparison. From this study, results are shown as Helsingør highway, Hermitage Hunting Lodge, Kronborg Castle, The Dance 
Chapel and Christiansborg Palace. The figures shows the average concentration from each site with errors bars showing the highest and lowest 
concentration. The depth of the soil samples are noted on the x-axis and ranges from 0.05 to 0.5 meters. The number of measured samples for each 
calculated averages are shown in parenthesis. The number of measured samples is not known from the Falkenberg and Riis (2002) study and are 
indicated with (NA). The red line in the figures is the Soil Quality Criteria (SQC) given by Nielsen E. et al. (1995) and Miljøstyrelsen (2015).
