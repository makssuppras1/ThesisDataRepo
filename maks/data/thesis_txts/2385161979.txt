ABSTRACT  
 
In this day and age, it is essential to understand that containers ports are inherent part of the 
global supply chain. In the age of globalization and the ever changing distribution patterns, 
container ports must seek solutions to attract business from not only shipping lines but also 
from shippers and freight forwarders. Henceforth, this study evaluates changes in liner shipping 
network dynamics through the use of Annual Slot Capacity analysis. The method study calling 
patterns and capacity deployment of major shipping lines on major trade routes. It also shads 
light on modifications in ports hierarchy. Moreover, the study discovers that changes to the 
geography of liner shipping trade are determined by ports ability to capitalize on the arising 
opportunities provided by the relocation of global manufacture and distribution centers rather 
than solely relaying on container port investment. The study uncovers the complex nature of 
inter-port connectivity which is not necessarily restricted to a complementary or competitive 
nature.  
 
Keywords: Liner shipping, container port, port connectivity, port competitiveness, 
complementarity, port choice, hinterland, supply chain

CHAPTER  1  Introduction    
The global maritime container shipping network connects different countries from all over the 
world. The introduction of a container over sixty years ago, made container shipping the 
backbone of global trade. The solution of easily moveable boxes between various modes of 
transport are largely responsible for the growth of global trade. Since the first container, local 
factories transformed into international manufactures with branches in different parts of the 
world. Currently, over ninety percent of total goods movement are transported by sea. Major 
ports all over the world handle thousands of containers every day. Containers relocation 
between trucks, trains and container ships enable the once restricted regional factories to 
expand to a global network. Hence, access to maritime transport became a fundamental 
necessity to perform economic activities. 
Ports play a critical role in the international logistics and supply chain management. The 
container shipping network has experienced crucial changes in its dynamics. In 1980, East 
Asian ports handled nearly twenty percent of the global throughput. By 2014 ports in the far 
east were accountable for thirty-two percent of the global throughput (IAPH, 2015) along with 
southeast Asian ports which almost tripled its market share in approximately forty years. 
Today, the dominance of these ports is evident in the official port rankings. Nine out of the ten 
busiest container ports in the world are located in Asia. Moreover, eight of them are located in 
China. The growth rate experienced by these ports is astonishing. The third busiest container 
port in 2016 was ranked at the 117th position in 1981 (Yap, 2009). Before the establishment of 
Shenzhen Special Economic Zone which boosted export-oriented production, there were no 
direct calls at any of the Mainland China ports. Additionally, none of these Chinese ports were 
equipped to handle containers, consequently giving the monopoly to Hong Kong as a hub port 
(Wang and Slack, 2000). The first direct service to Chinese port of Shenzhen was made by 
Maersk in 1994. This regular direct service linked China with North America. Ever since the 
first direct service was established, more direct calls were placed to other Chinese ports taking 
into advantage of their natural deep-sea waters.  
Structural changes of production line and changes in distribution channels causes instability in 
container shipping market.  Moreover, for the last ten years the shipping industry has been 
weathering through rough seas. The after effects of an oversupply of ships, deployment of mega 
vessel sizes, the volatile commodity prices, and the market concentration let shippers to start 
pooling numerous resources to stay afloat. The aforementioned aspects directly impact liner 
shipping network dynamics, resulting in constant alteration of network concentration and 
polarization. Henceforth, the purpose of this paper is to present the evolution of the dynamics 
of the liner shipping industry and port connectivity, which has not been fully documented in 
previous literatures. Moreover, the study focuses on recognition of major forces driving the 
change with the notion of ports as an integrated part of supply chain. The map in Figure 1-1 
presents location of the ports included in the study.

CHAPTER  2  Liner  shipping  network  
Liner shipping is defined as a way to transport cargo being stored in containers on a regular 
base schedule on fixed shipping route. The route is decided upon strategies of shipping lines 
and the demand of shippers for specific service. It is assumed that vessels operate in cycles 
trailing demarcated port rotation established for the certain planning horizon, also referred to 
as service routes (Agarwal and Ergun, 2010). The number of vessels engaged in the service 
line depends on the accepted sailing frequency on that service route.  
2.1   Container  port  characteristics,  roles,  function  and  operations  
A port has expanded from being simply inter or a superstructure that is fully dedicated to an 
exchange of cargo between vessels to other modes of transport to the logistics and intermodal 
centers carrying various value-adding activities (Paixao et al., 2003). Ports have been primarily 
categorized into four generation-types (UNCTAD, 1999) until researchers proposed the fifth 
generation (Flynn et al., 2011). The first generation ports, as expected played traditional cargo 
handling function. The second-generation ports grew to a status of transport, industrial and 
commercial service centers. The third generation incorporated logistics functions, which has 
led to the development of logistics and intermodal centres. Moreover, the fourth-generation 
ports are a result of vertical and horizontal integration of port strategies into international 
transport logistics chains that allows for collaboration with other ports. The fifth generation 
port is said to be community and customer orientated. Yet, this outright categorization of ports 
into five strict types does not depict the real life situation when ports intersect across 
generations of activities (Bichou & Grey, 2005).   
Port facility has been also defined as “a logistics and industrial centre of an outspokenly 
maritime nature that plays an active role in the global transport system and that is 
characterized by a spatial and functional clustering of activities that are directly and indirectly  
involved in seamless transportation and information processes in production chains” 
(Notteboom 2001). The definition also matches later discovery that ports play a crucial function 
of links between various transportation modes and are accountable to provide connectivity 
between hinterland and international markets (Lee & Cullinane, 2016). Ports can be further 
distinguished by its function. Gateway ports are the interface between hinterland and deep-sea 
container trade routes whereas transshipment ports act as an interface for interchange between 
deep-sea routing of containers. Container ports, which build key container trade corridors 
enhance connectivity, thus becoming an essential part of the entire logistics chain (Notteboom, 
2009). Additionally, studies suggest that ports may improve their shipping connectivity 
through better establishment of hinterland logistic services and in turn become a part of the 
logistics corridors (Notteboom and Rodrigue, 2005). Likewise, improvement in country’s 
connectivity index might occur through increase of cargo volume in port hinterlands, expansion 
of the market for port’s service, or through facilitation of international trade and transit, along 
with the improvement of intermodal inland transport and trade connectivity (UNCTAD, 2016). 
==PAGE:13==
 
13 
 
This process of integration has been portrayed as the sixth phase of a port’s evolution under 
port regionalization. The previous five phases are defined as follow:  
Phase 1: Shipping service is one-to-one with local cargo. High engagement of the government 
in the port sector (Wilmsmeier and Notteboom, 2011); 
Phase 2: Development of a first hub-and-spoke structure, resulting in better connectivity with 
overseas markets (Wilmsmeier and Notteboom, 2011); 
Phase 3: More cargo consolidation happens in hub-and-spoke structure including traffic 
coming from other ports (Wilmsmeier and Notteboom, 2011); 
Phase 4: Carriers offer services from ports to the overseas. Moreover, the hub port looks for 
extra connectivity to port not necessarily with access to overseas markets (Wilmsmeier and 
Notteboom, 2011); 
Phase 5: Relocation of the cargo traffic to smaller ports (Hayuth, 1981). 
2.1.1   Port  competition  and  competitiveness  
Defining the port’s competition highly depends on the type of the port in question, whether it 
is a gateway, local or transshipment port, and conjointly the type of commodities it handles. 
However, the literature mainly refers to port competition as competition for trades (Notteboom 
and Yap, 2012). It develops at three levels (Voorde and Winkelmans, 2002). First level of 
competition arises within a port and arises between different terminal operators. At the second 
level, competition is between terminal operators and their rivals located in the other ports, 
referred to as inter-port competition. It can be measured on regional as well as national scale. 
National level competition happens between terminal operators located in different port ranges, 
which possess overlapping hinterlands, and thus serve the same customer. The third level is 
competition between different port authorities. Similarly, the competitiveness of a container 
port is determined by the range of competitive advantages that the port retains (Haezendonck 
and Notteboom, 2002). A measurement of competitive factors varies with different parties 
involved in the process due to their various goal. It is further explained in detail in section 2.5.1.  
2.2   Hinterland  
A hinterland is the region from which the port attracts its business. Studies suggest two 
categories of hinterlands: main hinterland and competition margin hinterland (Rodrigue and 
Notteboom, 2006). In main hinterland, port has exclusivity to provide services whereas in the 
competition margin type of hinterland, a few ports tend to interfere and compete in terms of 
services. Consequently, marking hinterland’s vital role in the port’s market position 
(Notteboom, 2002). 
2.3   Liner  Shipping  Network  design  and  structure  
Liner shipping network are created to meet the growing demand in global supply chains. 
Compound networks have replaced primarily organized routes from port-to-port. Different 
networks are mostly interconnected and intersect at the transshipment (UNTCAD, 1992).

2.3.1   Transshipment  forms    
The study will mainly focus on hub-and-spoke transshipment network configuration because 
it accounts for about 85 percent of all transshipment activities. The other two form of 
transshipment are interlining transshipment and relay transshipment. In the interlining 
transshipment, hubs play the role of an interchange point between a few long distance shipping 
routes. In relay transshipment, hub role is to connect the same shipping routes within the same 
region, while serving different port calls. In all the three forms of transshipment, deep-sea 
vessels are discharged at the transshipment hub to be later transferred to feeders or in case of 
intersection and relay transshipment, to be transferred to another large deep-sea vessel 
(Notteboom et al., 2014). Clear examples of all three instances are illustrated in  Appendix G. 
In a hub-and-spoke network configuration, feeder ports are directly connected to hub ports but 
have no direct connection between two feeder ports (Almur & Kara, 2008; Gelareh et al, 2010). 
This assumption however has been later relaxed since there can be instances where feeder ships 
calls upon a few feeder ports in parallel and also with the fact that transshipment incidences 
are not required in between these ports being called by the same feeder vessel (Alumur et al, 
2009). The liner-shipping network of this type is formed by hubs that are major ports 
responsible for the consolidation of cargo coming from spokes that are secondary feeder ports 
whose role is to serve hubs (Crainic & Kim, 2007, Hayuth, 1981, Notteboom, 2005). This 
system enables better overall efficiency through the application of the economy of scale, which 
then enhances geographical the coverage with current fleet assets as well as allow for 
significant cost savings (Meng & Wang, 2011, Racunicam & Wynter, 2005). It has been 
examined that hubs compete with other ports in terms of location of shipping lines port calls. 
Hence, the aim of previous studies was to increase hubs market share through the increase of 
the number of connections and at the same time maximizing hubs’ attractiveness through 
shorter transit times and decreased transport costs imposed on shipping lines (Gelareh et al., 
2010). Another study points out that the competitive strategic location of a hub is one with 
minimum distance to the secondary feeder ports (Zohil and Prijon 1999). The geographical 
location of transshipment port and its characteristics prove to place high importance in port 
selection decision-making process. Consequently, creating the competitive environment of the 
maritime industry. All transshipment hubs have been said to share similar characteristics in 
terms of accessibility and connection to major shipping lines. A good example is the port of 
Singapore, which is considered a pure transshipment hub where the transshipment incidence 
rate is above 75 percent.  
Nevertheless, the role and relevance of transshipment hubs slowly sifts towards gateway 
function of hub ports (Ducruet & Notteboom, 2012), which attract direct services and direct 
calls from shipping lines. In this case transshipping does not take place. Moreover, third type 
of ports exists, such which combines transshipment flow with gateway cargo flows. From the 
studies conducted this is a common occurrence in East Asia. Gateway ports such as Shanghai, 
Ningbo, Hong Kong and Busan are good example of gateway ports holding also strong 
transshipment share (Notteboom, 2014).

2.4   Role   of   Container   shipping   lines   and   shippers   on   a   network  
structure  
Shipping lines design their liner service in a way to elevate vessel utilization and benefit the 
most from employment of ever-larger vessels.  They concentrate on optimization of maritime 
network by justifying port coverage, shipping routes and transit time to achieve the largest cost 
minimization. It has been studied that shipping lines are more important players in shaping a 
maritime network than port authorities. Port authorities will do everything it takes to attract to 
its port the biggest players on the market (Verhetsel, 2009). Moreover, shippers demand direct 
services putting pressure on schedules, port rotation and feeder links (Notteboom et al., 2014). 
Furthermore, shipping lines may tailor their calling patterns also based on region’s hinterland 
structure realizing that door-to-door service improves competitiveness of services 
(Wilmsmeier and Notteboom, 2009) which is also a part of objective of this study.  
2.5   Port  choice  and  Hierarchy  in  port  networks  
Hierarchy is defined as “structure in which different parts are linked by predominance 
relations” (Medda et al., 1999). Ports network, particularly the hub-and-spoke configuration is 
a great example of hierarchical structure based on dominance and dependence of hubs on 
mainline and feeder services. Following the hierarchical concept of global network, main hubs 
are associated with great geographical coverage yet with limited number of connections among 
them compered to ports lower in hierarchical order. However, these ports have more 
connections but service will be limited to smaller geographical area (Notteboom, 2009). 
Important for this study is an exception from that rule in hierarchical order of ports in Asia 
(Robinson, 1998). Port’s position is determined by its role. Port can whether accommodates 
transshipment traffic as a hub port or generate cargo traffic other than transshipment traffic. 
Figure 2-1 demonstrates development of network structure taking into consideration a fast 
growth of Asian ports.  In these instance connections to lower hierarchical degree ports, have 
as many connections as hub ports.

2.5.1   Port  selection  criteria    
The literature points out that port choice is not an easy task. It has already been a massive 
challenge to distinguish who are the real decision-makers (Fleming and Baird, 1999; Moya et 
al., 2017). All the decision-makers such as shippers, freight forwarders and shipping lines are 
interrelated and highly involved in a process of port choice. Yet, all of them tend to have 
different objective and their decisions are driven by different determinants (Magala and 
Sammons, 2008).  The aggregate outcome of different demand, supply and market selection 
factors are presented below (Notteboom, 2009; Ducruet and Notteboom, 2012):  
 
a)   Physical and technical port infrastructure, including nautical accessibility;  
b)   Terminal infrastructure and equipment, hinterland accessibility and intermodal offer;   
c)   Geographical location vis-à-vis the main shipping lanes and the hinterland;   
d)   Port efficiency expressed as port turnaround time, terminal productivity and cost 
efficiency;  
e)   Interconnectivity of the port (sailing frequency of deep-sea and feeder shipping 
services);  
f)   Reliability, capacity, frequency and cost of inland transport services;   
g)   Quality and cost of auxiliary services such as pilotage, towage and customs;   
==PAGE:17==
 
17 
 
h)   Efficiency and cost of port management and administration  
i)   Availability, quality and cost of logistic value-added activities and port community 
systems;  
j)   Port security/safety and environmental profile;   
k)   Port reputation. 
   
Henceforward, the performance of port will have a direct impact on port selection and at the 
same port choice will be a result of port competition dynamics (Lam and Yap, 2011; Yap and 
Notteboom, 2011). Moreover, ports are under the pressure of shippers as well as shipping lines 
for its range of competitive offerings in regards to specific trade route, geographical region and 
other rival ports to which the container port is connected (Notteboom, 2009; Haezendonck & 
Notteboom, 2002). 
Factors, which shape port competitiveness, has been allocated into three categories: hinterland-
related, maritime-related, and endogenous (Notteboom, 2008). Study of maritime literature 
shows that hinterland proximity and its good connectivity are among top factors for port 
competitiveness (Parola et al.,2016). The study also complements the theory of a shift in 
supremacy of hinterland-related over maritime- related factors, which contribute, to greater 
competitive advantage (Lam & Tang, 2009; Ng et al., 2014). Endogenous drivers which are 
operational efficiency, cost, and infrastructure, are considered remarkably important 
particularly among Asian ports. For example, in case of China’s export oriented industry 
concentrated along the coast, these ports have been said that do not require a connection with 
faraway hinterlands due to their coastal export oriented production. However, hinterland-
related factors such as inland transportation were verified more important in African and 
European ports (Parola et al., 2017) rather than Asia. Both categories hinterland-related and 
endogenous are equally important in North America (Malchow & Kanafani, 2001). It has been 
studied that competitive port will also influence the economic potential of hinterland (Haynes 
et al., 1997, Notteboom, 2009).

The chapter reviews previous literature that has been published in regards to the Liner Shipping

3.1   Port  dynamics    
In literature ports are perceived as a dynamic business networks (van der Lugt et al., 2007) in 
which the triumph of each business is connected to the whole system’s competitiveness 
(Bichou & Gray, 2005; Notteboom & Winkelmans, 2002; Robinson, 2003). Academic 
literature has been argued that port especially transshipment hubs are constantly exposed to 
changes in network dynamics. Sufficient flow of cargo at the gateway ports results in bypassing 
transshipment hubs (Wilmsmeier & Notteboom, 2010). Moreover, forces such as effortless 
cargo transport shift to other hub (Notteboom et al., 2014), or a presence of bundling 
(Notteboom, 2004) constantly influence network dynamics. Recently, it has been studied that 
the ports network is governed by the dynamics of four main variables: consolidation of 
shipping and port industry, economies of scale through upgrading a vessel size, evolution of 
shipping network, and port regionalization. Hence, the dynamics of the port and shipping 
container industry are presented in Figure 3-1 as self-reinforcing loops (Martín-Alcalde et al., 
2016). To be explained, more demands will result in better competitiveness of the shipping 
sector through improved efficiency and consolidations. Moreover, ports can improve shipping 
connectivity being delivered sufficient amount of cargo flow from hinterland consequently 
ports become more attractive for its users. In turn, developing hinterlands with better logistics 
service are able to generate more cargo at the same time making possible a better use of 
economy of scale.

As in previous literature was suggested that drivers of inert-port dynamics in region of Pearl

Hong Kong. According to the author, relocation of manufacturing services to Pearl River Delta,

suggests that nature of inter-port dynamics between port of Hong Kong and Shenzhen has 
changed from being a largely competitive into a complementary nature, owing this change to 
many parallel calls at both the ports. More explanation is provided in later chapters during the 
analysis.   
3.3   Annual  Slot  Capacity  analysis  in  literature    
Annual slot capacity is not broadly presented in the literature, only few publications were 
found.  Just recently, it has found application to study changes in container shipping services. 
Annual Slot Capacity analysis has been proved useful to determine port competition on sample 
of East Asian ports (Yap et al., 2006). Case study of Maersk Line identifies configuration of 
containerized maritime network through ASC analysis (Fremont, 2007). Another study 
explores the annual slot capacity deployed by all container-shipping lines associated with three 
Southeast Asian ports, Singapore, Port Klang and Tanjung Palepas, in 1999-2004 (Lam and 
Yap, 2008). The paper exposes competition dynamics among transshipment containers.

3.4   Port  and  Supply  chain  integration  
Role of a port keeps being more important than ever visible through increasing amount of 
publications in regards to that matter (Notteboom et al., 2013).  Researches are using different 
methods and perspective to define a role of ports in supply chain. One of the earliest studies 
points out that ‘port growth is a function of all the production outcomes of firms in the port 
adjacent space-or of that space to which it is linked, either in landward space or in areas linked 
across Water Ocean’ (Robinson, 1998 & 2002). The study supports the theory that available 
efficient infrastructure and inland connections as well as value creation have significant effect 
on ports overall performance (Chen, 2001). Later, ports’ vertical and horizontal integration was 
important to gain competitive advantage (Notteboom and Wilkenmans, 2002). Different study 
suggests a set of new port performance indicators considering qualitative issues in order to 
enrich better integration of all supply chain elements (Marlow and Paixao 2003). A year later, 
another research concentrated to measure port efficiency through value-added logistics 
activities (Bichou and Gray 2004). The authors highlight relevance of landside connectivity in 
port performance. The results of that study point out nearly nonexistence of ports interest in 
logistics operations and management. It also underlines that shipping lines and freight 
forwarders practices are of the highest concern of ports. While shippers and inland connectivity 
has received less attention. In this, research some ports surveyed claim to be in conflict with 
their major customers and suppliers. Another research develops model to evaluate the extent 
of port integration in supply chain. However, it puts more emphasizes towards factors 
influencing port efficiency within a port area (Song and Photis 2008). Inland part of 
transporting goods from maritime logistics is considered to be one of many ways to cut overall 
costs however did not receive much of attention (Notteboom, 2004). Hence, researchers 
combine liner survives and hinterland services, developing a network model presented in the 
Appendix F. It proves high interrelation of sea-leg and land-leg of supply chain. Moreover, it 
emphasizes hinterland’s role expansion into entire logistics system. Supported also in author’s 
later publication (Notteboom et al., 2005). Once again, literature incorporates container 
logistics into inland freight distribution systems in order to provide greater benefit to ports 
(Notteboom, 2008). Another study investigates the performance of container port highly 
dependent on supply side, namely shipping lines perspective (Yap, 2009). The significant 
growth of vessel size and at the same time a capacity call size have forced especially gateway 
ports to reached higher degree of synchronization with their hinterlands through specialized 
high-capacity transport corridors serviced by rail or barges, often including dry ports (Roso & 
Lumsden, 2010). Ports better integration into logistics chain with consequences in smoother 
cargo flow and lower inventory costs achieved through integration of ocean carriers with other 
transport players in a network (Fanam et al., 2016). Better management and greater flows of 
cargo from hinterland makes ports more appealing for shipping companies (Martín-Alcalde et 
al.2016).

4.3   Data  collection  
Information regarding shipping line contribution to the total container throughput of a port is

Alphaliner and crosschecked with information indicated on shipping lines’ webpages.  Annual 
port’s container throughput, ports rankings, statistics and all information are access via

CHAPTER  5  East  Asian  Container  Port  landscape  
The escalation of Chinese ports has had significant implications for the major ports in East 
Asia (Tongzon & Yang, 2016). The study observes a long-run shift in the center of port and 
shipping activity away from the ports of Singapore, Hong Kong and Busan towards the major 
ports in mainland China. In late 90s, ports such as Ningbo and Xiamen were irrelevant in the 
maritime industry particularly in terms of their connectivity to the major trade routes, but in 
2001 they have already been accounted for a noteworthy share of these market and at present 
Ningbo holds for a substantial market portion. In the matter of annual container throughput 
growth rate, the compilation of data Figure 5-2 shows that all the selected ports follow the 
same pattern of ups and downs, with the exception of Port of Hong Kong which like any other 
port from year to year basis, experiences a continuous drop in number of handled containers. 
Port of Singapore also faces decline in container throughput since 2015 as presented in Figure 
5-1. Both of the ports, Hong Kong and Singapore, have operated in a large scale of connectivity. 
Referred to as a transshipment and gateway hub port respectively. This distinction is essential 
for this study purpose to recognize association of hinterland connectivity into ports 
competitiveness. Seaward connectivity is vital for any of transshipment hubs, whereas gateway 
port should offer intermodality advantage to stay ahead of competition. Port of Hong Kong, as 
a transaction centre (Hayness et al., 1997), held the title of the world’s busiest container port 
for 15 years until 2004. Overtaken by Singapore year later, in 2007 by Shanghai, 2013 by 
Shenzhen and Ningbo-Zhoushan in 2015. At that, moment Hong Kong accounted for a load 
centre for its several of its hinterlands: Guangdong’s Pearl River Delta, coastal regions from 
South to Central China, and the northern region of Southeast Asia.  
Over time Singapore proved its own intercontinental network better than the one of Hong 
Kong, on top of that Chinese regions established their own fast emerging ports as well. The 
economic growth of China is determined by export-oriented industrialization (Morrison, 2014), 
it has generated sufficient local cargo to attract direct calls. Regions such as Pearl River Delta 
PRD produces nearly 5 percent of the world’s goods accounting for 26.8 percent of China’s 
total export (HKTDC Research, 2016). In the year 1997, when Hong Kong was returned to 
China, the size of its economy was 18 percent of the size of Mainland’s and it produced one 
sixth of the size of China’s whole goods and services production.  
Currently, Shenzhen became a truly hi-tech hub with forty per cent contribution to country’s 
GDP (SCMP, 2016) and produces thirty times the goods and services as Hong Kong.  China’s 
box volumes have been growing ever since, collectively in the first quarter of 2017 marking a 
6 percent year-over-year rise. The biggest gains were recorded at Qingdao, Shanghai and 
Ningbo of 12, 10 and 9 percent respectively (Drewry, 2017). The Yangtze River Delta facility 
benefited from the intensifying demand in domestic consumption and advanced manufacturing 
industry. Surprisingly, in 2016 Port of Shanghai held on to its lead position as the world’s 
largest container port. Facing plunging pressure in foreign trade, Port of Shanghai’s container

from 2001. Furthermore, Hong Kong established its dominant position through the highest

CHAPTER  7  Practical  implication  from  ASC  analysis  
This study chose the ports of Shanghai, Singapore, Ningbo – Zhoushan, Hong Kong, Busan, 
Guangzhou/ Nansha, Qingdao, Tianjin/Xingang, Xiamen, Shenzhen to demonstrate how slot 
capacity can be useful to evaluate maritime supply chains.  
7.1   Ports  
The case of Hong Kong and Shenzhen discloses a complex nature of competitiveness and 
complementariness between the ports. The evidence in 2017 shows that the fast enlargement 
of Port of Shenzhen had a negative effect on Hong Kong’s container throughput. In general, 
Hong Kong attracts more port calls, and also wins in terms of total number of exclusive calls 
over Shenzhen but accumulates lower capacity deployment. However, members of three major 
groupings favor to call Shenzhen on the major trade routes such as Asia – Europe and 
Transpacific. On these both trade lines, a drop in Hong Kong annual slot capacity share was 
nearly equal to a rise of a value of a slot capacity share in Shenzhen over the years 2001 - 2017. 
The numbers advocate for a shift in cargo movement dynamics. Containers being handled 
almost entirely at the port of Hong Kong of 94.3 % market share in 2001, now in 2017 are 
correspondingly handled by port of Shenzhen, including additional unique calling at the port. 
It significantly boosted the market share particularly through augmentation of these extra 
exclusive calls. Nevertheless, Hong Kong continues to receive the same capacity that was used 
to call at the region. Yet on all services lines at port of Hong Kong, shipping operators are 
making parallel calls at Shenzhen in the same schedule on the trade line with Europe. Join 
calling at the both ports suggest two different characteristics of above ports. Emerging since 
2001 as a gateway hub port of Shenzhen has become a major port serving PRD’s export 
activities where port of Hong Kong has remained its role as a transshipment hub. Leading the 
position among ports, Hong Kong largely benefited from transshipments incidents between 
2001 and 2007. Transshipment traffic nearly doubled over these time period (Hong Kong 
maritime Department, 2008). 
Given port of Hong Kong container throughput in previous years as presented in Appendix B 
Figure B-1, and Far East – Europe ASC share, the data shows that slot capacity deployment in 
2017 is equivalent to its actual container throughput in its best times on trade line with Europe. 
Similar situation on transpacific route empowers the position of port of Shenzhen on the 
expense of Hong Kong with a gain in market share in 2017. The decline in Hong Kong’s market 
share was a direct result of Port Shenzhen emerging as a favorable alternative for direct 
container handling operations. Now, facing the intense competition from other PRD ports, Port 
of Hong Kong changes its orientation to one that combines transshipment flows and gateway 
cargo flow to the hinterlands to stay ahead of its competition. Gateway port requires or should 
ensure a great level of connectivity to import and export cargo.  Thus, Hong Kong only granted 
its seaward connectivity, does not generate any longer sufficient volumes of cargo flow. 
Henceforth it has to guarantee highly effective and accessible transportation system with 
==PAGE:37==
 
37 
 
neighboring countries, to improve its landward network. Yet as Hong Kong remain a good 
choice for transshipments due to its maritime links, the enormous growth of Chinese ports and 
its close connection with hinterland, encouraged some shipping lines to bypass Hong Kong. 
Hong Kong gained its importance through cabotage cargo of foreign-flagged ships, which 
could not be directly handled in mainland ports under Chinese rule that prohibits foreign-
flagged as well as owned ships to engage in China domestic shipping. That is how Hong Kong 
retains its dominant position on Intra-Asia trade routes, and any relaxation of this rule will 
directly influence its connectivity network as a hub (SCMP, 11.2016). It allows foreign carriers 
to load containers in one of the mainland ports, and via transit in Hong Kong call another 
Chinese port. Moreover, a dominant position of port of Shenzhen on the major trade routes 
contradicts the idea of cargo being of mainly local origin, consequently transferring Shenzhen 
into a global hub.  
On the contrary, in the case of PRD ports, Shanghai and Ningbo reveal complementary nature 
with comparable growth of ASC over the years on all four major trade lines. Both ports were 
experiencing similar share gains proving its complementarity nature. As gateway ports, both 
facilities offer broad access to vast services in the whole YRD. It can be concluded that through 
higher inter-port connectivity between these two ports, they extended to favorable port of calls.  
Moreover, the inter-container port complementarity is uncovered between another port pair 
such as Shanghai and Busan. This is the most evident on the two largest trade routes connected 
do that region, that is Transpacific and Intra Asia. Capacity deployment on these two trade 
routes is determinate to a comparable extent. As for the Asia – Europe trade, Busan sustain its 
initial ASC deployment, being overtaken by Shanghai on Far East – Southeast Asia, Middle 
East, South America and Australia with New Zealand trade routes in terms of ASC share. This 
however indicates a competitive nature of ports only in case of shanghai attracting higher 
number of exclusive calls on link connecting Far East with Middle East markets than Busan, 
11 and 1 respectively. Additionally, South America and Australia with New Zealand attract 
more calls made in parallel to both the port, 12 and 5 distinctly, than made individually to these 
ports.  On South America trade route both ports receive 2 exclusive calls, while only port of 
shanghai attracts exclusive calls on Oceania trade route.  
While Port of Hong Kong struggles to attract some of the world’s largest carries as a 
transshipment hub, port of Singapore continues to expand. Since both these ports are hub port 
cities and depend upon the success of transshipment capabilities, they also position themselves 
as competitors (McKinnon, 2011). Because, both the ports are characterized by its high 
seaward connectivity they had to seek a way out to increase competitiveness. Persistently 
increasing vessel movements and capacity requires an effective cargo transportation system 
(Rodrigue, 2010). In case of hub port city of Hong Kong its constrained logistics solutions with 
connection to hinterland and problems to transport cargo to port encouraged shipping lines to 
direct the goods flow to port of Singapore (McKinsey Quarterly, 2004)

7.2   Shipping  lines    
Alliances, consolidations, mergers, cross-alliance cooperation and vessel sharing agreements 
display market driven nature of shipping. It helped lower-ranked carriers grow as a target for 
bigger carriers.  The comparison of the shipping lines rankings indicates a rise of regional 
operators formerly placed beyond the top 25 in 2000 due to recent consolidations. See 
Appendix C. At that time shipping lines such as: Hamburg Sud Group, Hapag-Lloyd and 
UASC as well as China Shg Group were not global but it did not stop them from cooperating 
with truly global lines such as: Maersk, Cosco Container Lines and CMA CGM. Currently 
most shipping lines are already or can easily expand globally through slot buying agreements. 
Such arrangements put emphasis on the real foundation of their actions since they are all 
interacting with the same customer. In 2017 the purpose of business expansion for large carriers 
lost its significance due to the fact that they all share vessels as well as networks and ports calls. 
The impact and pressure put on smaller shipping lines has been extensive (Lloydloadinglist, 
2017 & containerization international, 03.2017 p.11). Then again, it can be concluded from the 
analysis that accurate recognition of the market provides opportunities for smaller carriers with 
smaller TEU capacity vessels to venture into bigger ports. With alliances and the largest ships 
mostly concentrated on East – West main trade routes, smaller shipping lines are still able to 
utilize their advantage on niche trades.   
7.3   Comparison  throughout  the  years  
To better picture constantly changing nature of port dynamics, results of this ASC analysis are 
compared to the results of conducted ASC analysis by Yap in her PhD thesis paper in 2009. 
The study evaluates inter-port relation of two pearl River Delta ports, throughout the period of 
1995 to 2006. The outlook resembles the results of ASC analysis conducted for purpose of this

9.1   Competition  and  polarization  at  East  Asian  ports  
The results obtained from graphs of nodal flows does not entirely match previous results that 
of ASC analysis. The general structure puts greater emphasis on the vastly complex nature of 
ports. A more detailed observation reveals high interdependence of neighboring ports such as 
Hong Kong – Shenzhen, and Shanghai – Ningbo-Zhoushan in all circumstances. It sheds light 
towards the possibility if the behavior of these ports are more of competitors or complementary. 
The process of spatializing a network shows close proximity of both pairs of ports. Both ports 
share the same cluster with accordance to Newman theory. Ports with closer proximity exhibits 
a higher value of community as mentioned in Newman’s theory (Newman, 2006), thus 
establishing more interdependence and relations among ports part of that community then 
towards ports outside of it. Table E-12 in the Appendix E, shows that both pairs of ports deliver 
the highest number of direct calls to each other. Thus, the analysis complements the previous 
analytical steps by uncovering the complementary nature of YRD ports. It points to a similar 
function of Ningbo-Zhoushan and Shanghai ports since both interact with other ports in similar 
manner. However, Shanghai is a gateway to Busan, while Ningbo – Zhoushan is better 
connected with southeast china’s ports.  
Furthermore, the study supports the ASC analysis results regarding complementary nature of 
Shanghai and Busan. In addition to the previous analysis, the graphs bring out interesting 
evidence that port of Hong Kong and Shenzhen share a complementary nature. A primary 
reason being, both the ports deliver the highest number of vessel calls to each other. Compiling 
various East –West routes into one Inter-Asia trade proves that Hong Kong enhanced 
Shenzhen’s growth in view of the fact that their ships started to call Shenzhen in parallel, 
contributing to almost half of its total number of calls. Yet, Shenzhen is the biggest contributor 
to Hong Kong’s throughput helping Hong Kong to sustain its position as a transshipment hub 
which is confirmed by its highest value of Authority. The outcome supports the supposition of 
ASC analysis. The visualization illuminates an important role of Hong Kong being a way into 
East-Asian market including Shenzhen for cargo coming directly from other continents through 
Singapore. This also contradicts the theory of the competitive nature of the port of Hong Kong 
and Singapore. Hong Kong receives the most of its calls from ports being considered its 
competitor (Cullinane et al. 2004). Port of Shenzhen is the way out from the East-Asian ports 
for most of containers via Singapore to reach other continents. Hence, its strong position as a 
hub according to the Hub distribution factor.  The higher throughput of Shenzhen is caused by 
notable better connections with the northeastern region of China. It attracts more services to 
call from ports such as Xiamen and Ningbo-Zhoushan. Port of Xiamen placed in the middle of 
PRD and YRD is served to nearly same degree from both clusters. It receives more direct calls 
==PAGE:58==
 
58 
 
from vessels coming from YRD than it sends vessels there. Hence, it suggests Xiamen as a 
collective seaport for cargo directed to PRD.  
Last but not least, the data sheds light on the difficulty each port has to handle, specifically 
different ship size range from as much as 19000 TEUs to as little as 1000 TEUs. At this point, 
it is important to maintain effective logistic service to ensure high port calling frequency. 
Different ship sizes require different services such as adjusted supervision or extra buffer 
operational capacity in case of smaller vessels. It has been studied that larger vessel may 
improve rail utilization at the container terminal, whereas smaller vessels cause 
underutilization of trains and keeps them waiting (World Shipping Council, 2015). Higher 
concentration of cargo on larger vessels allows for higher crane density and increases moves 
per hatch. Yet, smaller vessels cause time lost as it loses speed moving between hatches. 
 
9.2   Comparison  throughout  the  years  
To have a better picture of the constantly changing nature of port dynamics, results of this study 
are compared to the results obtained by researchers César Ducruet, Sung-Woo Lee and Adolf 
Koi Yu Ng (2011). They focused on the analysis of port competition and network polarization 
in the East Asian maritime corridor, thus it is a good instance for this study to evaluate 
distribution patterns. Authors obtained their results based on data purchased from Lloyd’s 
Marine Intelligence Unit. Figure 9-1 recognizes the strong position of three main ports: Busan, 
Hong Kong and Singapore, with Hong Kong’s strong dominance in 1996. The graph shows the 
main cargo flow corridor between port of Singapore and Hong Kong in both years. In 1996 
Hong Kong is clearly a central port followed by Singapore and Busan. Hong Kong was 
considered to be the link between China and other ports combining hub functions with a 
commercial gateway function for South China. In 2006, Singapore suppressed Hong Kong as 
the most central port. Furthermore, the study observes significant development of port of 
Shanghai while the level of development of other Chinese local ports are not of significance. 
Shanghai empowered its position with its new pattern and rose to the rank higher than of Busan. 
Hence, most researchers over emphasized the competitive nature of both the ports rather than 
their complementariness. In 2017 the Figure 9-2 shows a similar pattern to the previous years 
where Singapore maintains its central position followed by Hong Kong. Shanghai’s strong 
position is still visible along with Busan throughout the years. The extreme development of 
port of Shenzhen does go unnoticed. The port came out from a shadow of Hong Kong to 
marking its position with a very high weighted degree, almost equal to of Hong Kong. In 
addition, port of Ningbo – Zhoushan is closely following behind.

9.3   Summarize    
Clearly, the data and visualizations of network attributes confirms alterations in port network 
dynamics where radical modifications have occurred during the given analyzed timeframe. 
Moreover, port functions have been rapidly changing overtime alongside with the development 
of seaward as well as landward connectivity. This study does not endorse a competitive 
environment of maritime industry but rather the collaborative environment for the better 
utilization of each of the ports resources and properties. Resources in this study relate to a 
broader spectrum of supply chain rather than directly to port facilities. Thus, the study analyzes 
all the trade routes individually to uncover the source of ports development and improvement. 
The analyzed ports found position for growth in the areas where they possess better prowess. 
Hence, ranking of container ports throughput does not resemble the rank of these ports on the 
final graph. It is therefore apparent that the rise of Chinese ports does not necessarily result at 
the expense of other ports such as Singapore, Busan or Hong Kong. This study illustrates that 
the fast raising ports like Shenzhen, Ningbo – Zhoushan and other local Chinese ports are not 
substitute ports for previously established hub ports. All the ports have different level of 
involvement in various trade routes and through effective adoption to new challenges are able 
to better utilize their resources. The graphs show that different pathways employ diverse vessel 
capacities, which subsequently yields various level of contributions to the ports throughput.  It 
suggests that even ports within the same region are able to reinforce their position through 
adequate response to those challenges, yet not triumphing in throughput volumes. Of these 
aforementioned aspects, the ports collaborative movements result in better optimization of 
coastline resources through the process of integration with hinterland. Therefore, the 
authenticity of the changes in dynamics of liner shipping network is about responding to new 
opportunities arising from global manufacturing and distribution centers.

10.1  China  dominance  
The economic growth of China and its effects on the rest of the world are evident. China is the 
world’s second largest economy by nominal GDP and the largest by purchasing power parity. 
Moreover, it is projected to become the largest economy in a few years’ time (Yao Yang, 
Peking University).  
The study notices China’s two key vulnerabilities. First of all, the country’s economic growth 
is highly concentrated on coastal areas and secondly it fundamentally depends on international 
trade (Lee at al., 2008) The international distribution systems in China is very much focused 
on the large export flows centered around major gateways along the coastline, mainly in the 
Pearl River Delta, such as port of Shenzhen, Hong Kong and Guangzhou, and the Yangtze 
River Delta in the area of Port of Shanghai and Ningbo - Zhoushan. Bohai Rim served through 
the port of Tianjin and Fujian Province served by the port of Xiamen and few other Chinese 
ports mentioned above are primarily responsible for the strong growth of the mainland’s 
container sector as ports contributes to country's competitiveness (Cullinane et al., 1998; Thai, 
2008; Rodrigue et al., 2009). The position of neighboring manufacturing provinces of 
Guangdong and Fujian supports the area’s future development as the core of 21st century 
maritime Silk Road. China’s effects on the world economy is the boosted traffic of container 
movement and its ever-increasing impact on international trade. In addition, large 
transportation network (Wang, 2014) constantly supports the ever-growing manufacturing 
sector in the country.  Coastal region of China is characterized with a sophisticated and agile 
supply chain that is proven arduous to compete with, while the infrastructure is shoddy and 
transportation by rivers add an extra week (The Economist, 2012). Yet, the vastly growing 
inland factories slowly supersede the coastal ones. Such as Chongqing province located not far 
away from Guangdong is responsible for production of one-third of ever sold in the world 
laptops. The city positioned as a crucial logistics hub with good railway, waterway and road 
networks connectivity. Whereas Guangdong province referred to as Factory of the World 
attracts the biggest auto and phone makers. Furthermore, the e-commerce and the access to 
internet connects to global markets and even larger number of potential customers than they 
can find in their home market. 
 
10.2  Inter-­port  relation  in  broader  spectrum  of  supply  chain  system  
It is important to remember that cargo has its origin and destination beyond that of the port 
network. The knowledge that the service does not come to an end at the port of delivery but 
rather when ultimately it reaches the customer opens up new opportunities not only for ports 
==PAGE:63==
 
63 
 
but also for shipping lines as they are an inseparable part of supply chain. The fast growing 
local Mainland ports did not interfere with established port power structure. However, these 
ports became integrated into the port system as jump-off points from hinterland towards 
international markets. Some of the ports demonstrated fast growth mostly through 
collaboration with better-positioned ports to rise to the rank where they can support each other.  
 
10.2.1  
Ports  and  their  major  markets  
It is noticed from the visualizations that port of Busan dominates on trade route to North 
America especially on the East Coast. West Coast is also closely connected with North China 
ports through most of containers coming through Busan. The port has a strong presence in trade 
with South America. Additionally, due to its dense links with port of Shanghai, Shanghai’s 
operative area has diversified from only Chinese ports in 1996 to a mixture of Japanese, Yellow 
Sea Korean, and Russian ports in later years, transferring its function from a gateway port for 
the Yangtze River corridor to a hub port for transshipments. Singapore is highly polarized with 
West ports, Australia and New Zealand whereas port of Hong Kong maintains its essential 
position on Intra Asia trade. In the case of the fast growing port of Shenzhen, it is visible from 
the graphs that its strong position is established through forwarding cargo out of the East Asia 
region. Moreover, port of Ningbo – Zhoushan is the link between South East Asian ports to 
that of more northerly located ports. It is evident from the empowerment of local ports into 
global hubs that they embrace the bi-directional nature of its logistics systems. For many years, 
Port of Hong Kong relied on its proximity to Mainland and Far East trade. The exceptional port 
infrastructure, facilities and business friendly regulatory policies has proven Hong Kong’s 
position as a global hub port. Port of Shenzhen experiences a strong growth along with its 
supply chain capability ever since China was flourishing as a major powerhouse. Migration of 
manufacturing activities to PRD resulted in increased number of supply chains of port of 
Shenzhen, this in turn attracted more port calls as it was in case of Hong Kong. Due to the 
undeniable fact that Shenzhen is closer to the manufacturing sector, it necessitates the 
betterment of its facilities. The success of Shenzhen’s port complements Hong Kong through 
parallel calls and support the strategic location of Pearl River Delta. This development helps to 
explain the complementary nature of both the ports. Specifically, Shenzhen focuses on 
handling cargo originating from the vicinity of PRD. The same services that called Shenzhen 
will place a parallel call at Hong Kong in order to capitalize on Hong Kong’s transshipment 
activities, since Hong Kong still relies on short distance container traffic. 
Different forces shape the complementary nature of Ningbo-Zhoushan and Shanghai compared 
to the example of Hong Kong and Shenzhen. However, the fast rise of these ports’s influence 
comes from the development of the whole Yangtze River Delta. This example supports and 
further explains the theory behind Ducruet discovery that less central ports growth faster. This 
is due to the fast developing hinterland rather than the upgrading of the ports facilities itself. 
The abundant cargo from the developing hinterlands necessitates more calls from shipping 
lines.

10.2.2  
Shipping  lines  
The operational alliances between shipping lines comes to an end once the boxes are unloaded. 
On land, all the lines have their own terminal, dispatching, railroad arrangements, trucking 
contract and operations management (McKinsey Global Institute, April 2015).  The alliances 
also resulted in the lowered costs of shipping on the sea-leg. However, intermodal operations 
are an inherent part of getting goods to the end customer consequently contributing a massive 
share of the total cost (Notteboom & Rodrigue, 2005; Notteboom 2004) this is because it is in 
direct connection with shipping lines (Marlow et al., 2003). Nonetheless, shipping lines opt for 
ports, which can provide effective hinterland connectivity thus becoming more attractive for 
shipping lines to focus its liner services there. 
 
10.3  Maritime  Industry  complicated  nature  
Key players in maritime sector as defined by (Nam et al. 2011) are: shipping companies, ports 
operators and freight forwarders. The Containerization International Shipper Sentiment Survey 
2015 that was conducted by asking shippers and freight forwarders, underlines that freight rates 
and reputations are among the key criteria in choosing a container line. To support the 
constantly growing cargo volume of a country it requires a suitable shipping carrier which is 
chosen by shippers to specifically cater to their needs. Cargo has to be first transported to the 
one of the available ports, then shipped between the origin and destination port thus finally 
being delivered to its end customer. Shippers will choose the most fitting port among the vast 
number of available ports by fulfilling specific requirements. Firstly, the distance between the 
shipper from the port and as well as the distance to and from the port to the cargo’s final 
destination is evaluated. Next, the overall transit time, port congestion, shipping line’s vessels 
size and overall price (Tiwari, 2003) are taken into consideration. Notwithstanding, oftentimes 
the ports which offer the lowest overall cost for the required service level will be chosen as the 
port of call (Yap, 2009). Having chosen the best option/port to deliver the cargo, shippers then 
select an appropriate shipping line between the available lines that conduct a regular service to 
port (Robinson, 2002). Thus, shipping lines have to meet shipper’s requirements regarding 
frequency, reliability and geographical coverage (Slack et al, 1996) Hence, shippers are an 
essential element in the supply chain that benefits from improved connectivity with ports and 
a larger choice of shipping lines to choose from. Containers delivered to its destination port 
require further means of transportation to meet its end customer, which again will require 
access to and from its hinterland. As a general rule of thumb, ports with better connectivity are 
preferred compared to ports with better facilities as it attracts more calls thus giving higher 
importance to the ports connectivity to its hinterlands rather than the advancement that the port 
has undergone. Yet, as ports play the role of a middleman between land and sea it is important 
that it offers cost-efficient, effective and reliable service for all port users. 
Shippers have the choice and final say in any cargo routing because they hold control over the 
volume of cargo delivered. They should always be consulted and taken into consideration in 
any occurrences at the port, as they are a vital part of the supply chain. The same survey also 
==PAGE:65==
 
65 
 
suggests that actions undertaken by shippers are driven by short-term planning as they are more 
focused on the present rather than planning ahead leaving them narrow minded and vulnerable 
to future complications. Hence, close collaboration between port authorities, shippers and 
freight forwarders is imperative for the ports to be able to gain a competitive advantage to 
attract more calls.  
Nevertheless, the main problem that plagues the maritime industry is that they are divided into 
unintegrated and self-destructive sectors, although together they are key elements that make up 
the broad supply chain system as proved in the analysis. These sectors consist of three main 
divisions which are shipping lines, port logistic systems, and the rest – shippers and hinterlands. 
Container lines operate without consideration of their direct partners which are ports or 
suppliers of cargo. The whole system takes an unintegrated nature whereby their tasks becomes 
divided. With carriers being focused only on their network operations and ports dedicating their 
attention to adjust to changes arising from shipping lines operations while leaving only the 
shippers to look at the whole logistics chain. Shipping lines continue to upgrade vessel size and 
leaving the responsibility to the ports to catch up by making constant facility adjustments and 
improvements. As the survey points out the issue is not the size of the vessel but rather the 
unprepared ports, the lack of investment in port infrastructure and the lack of back room service 
of the carriers itself. It is hard to be instantaneously able to adjust to varying sizes of vessels. 
Large vessels are employed not necessarily, where they are required. Not to mention, the ability 
of shippers to deliver sufficient volume for the vessels proficient utilization. Smaller vessel 
size delivers to shippers with higher frequency and lower transit time, while larger units benefit 
only the operators through economy of scale (Notteboom, 2004; Brooks, 2000). On top of that, 
when shippers were asked in the survey if ultra-large vessels provide any benefit, the response 
was almost equally split between 44 percent saying yes and 43 percent saying no while 13 
percent were unsure. Shippers do not see any benefits in neither the higher availability of space 
nor the lower rates, on the contrary it gives them lesser options in case of manufacturing 
disruption.  Container shipping carriers do not consider that their inflexibility has an impact on 
the shippers and freight forwarders agility. Industry huge fragmentation and isolation of sectors 
does not allow for essential supply demand balance.

CHAPTER  11  
Conclusion,  Limitations  and  further  
research  
The research presented a direct approach to evaluate the changes in liner shipping network 
dynamics as well as uncovered the dynamic nature of inter-port relation. The results disclosed, 
elucidate that the judgment of a port’s performance should not be restricted to the total volume 
of the port’s container throughput nor to its position in the global ranking. The ports with the 
highest container throughput are not necessarily associated with the highest number of calls. A 
fitting example is port of Shenzhen and Hong Kong where the Mainland port attracts less calls 
from shipping lines yet leads the global container throughput ranking. Moreover, merely 
looking at the position of port of Shenzhen in comparison to Hong Kong does not directly 
suggest for an occurrence of a competitive nature between these two ports. The study shows 
that both these ports are complementary in nature whereby, the both of them receive parallel 
service calls and are primary contributors to each other’s throughput. Likewise, port of 
Shanghai and Ningbo – Zhoushan experience the same percentage of growth on major trade 
routes by receiving service calls in parallel. The analysis further discovers that ports pursuing 
a global role should not oppose the existing network structure but rather need to integrate into 
the system to enhance better seaward and landward connectivity. It can be deduced from the 
established port hierarchy, which has not undergone a significant change over the period of last 
ten years. Nonetheless, ports have gained their relevant position through better integration into 
the entire supply chain system.   
However, this deduction was arrived at by only using this particular study, which is limited to 
a sample of ten ports within East Asia and Southeast Asia region. Further research can be 
extended to all Far East region ports, to have a better picture of the spatial reaches of port 
polarization in the network. The study does not take into account the influence and regulations 
set by the government, which has an effect on the overall tone of port operations.
