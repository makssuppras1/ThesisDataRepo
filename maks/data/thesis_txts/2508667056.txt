of  Aqueous Ammonia Soaking as a method 
to enhancing the methane potential

Abstract
Biogas production from lignocellulosic biomass are a promising alternative to energy
production from renewable materials.
However, the structural and chemical composition
of lignocellulosic biomass limits the conversion into biogas, and pretreatment is therefore
needed to increase the yield and feasibility of the process. Aqueous Ammonia Soaking (AAS)
is a low-cost pretreatment, which has shown promising improvements on methane yield
from lignocellulosic biomass, due to ammonias e↵ect on breaking covanlent bonds between
lignin and hemicellulose.
The purpose of this study is to identify if certain components
in the chemical composition of the biomass is correlated to the increase in methane yield
or hydrolysis rate constant (KHyd), due to AAS pretreatment.
To study this are eight
biomasses chosen: wheat-, barley-, rye- and rape straw, grass, seaweed, chicken- and horse
manure. The biomasses are pretreated with 15% ammonia in a solid-to-liquid ratio of 83.3
g/L for 4 days at ambient temperature, prior to biochemical methane potential tests.
A BMP test is used to evaluate the e↵ect of AAS pretreatment by measuring the methane
production of raw and AAS pretreated biomasses, and to determinate the ultimate yield
and KHyd. The AAS pretreatment e↵ects the biomasses very di↵erently, where seaweed has
the highest ultimate yield increase of 46%, and grass the most negative e↵ect of -14%. The
biodegradability of AAS pretreated biomasses indicates that most of them are utilizing a
large part of the theoretical potential. The high methane yield increase of seaweed due to
AAS pretreatment, are relatively low when considering the biodegradability of 57%, which
indicates that seaweed has an even higher potential for optimizing the AAS pretreatment.
The BMP tests shows that the AAS pretreatment has the largest e↵ect in the beginning of
the methane production. The hydrolysis constant increases signiﬁcantly due to AAS for all
biomasses, except seaweed, ranging from 19 to 94%. The solubilization e↵ect due to AAS
indicates that large amounts of organic materials are solubilized, with 2-3 folds increase in
soluble COD for all biomasses, except chicken manure which has a slightly negative e↵ect,
and seaweed which has an astonishing 13-fold increase.
The analysis of the chemical composition of the biomass includes: cellulose, hemicellulose,
lignin, extractive and ash content.
Partial least squares regression is used to reveal the
most inﬂuential components of the composition.
Generally, seaweed is dominating the
PLS analysis, due to its prominent composition and has to be excluded to ﬁnd an reliable
correlation between the components and the increase in methane yields and KHyd. The results
of the PLS found that lignin content are signiﬁcantly positively correlated with the increase
of KHyd, due to AAS pretreatment. It is also found that high hemicellulose:lignin ratio are
negatively correlated with the KHyd increase. The PLS results of the increase in methane
yields, can only obtain a reliable correlation when seaweed and horse manure are excluded
from the analysis. No component has a very good correlation with the methane yield increase.
However, the cellulose and hemicellulose content have a relatively positive correlation with
the increase of methane yield, due to AAS. The lignin and hemicellulose:lignin ratio are found
to be positively correlated with the response, however, these are not signiﬁcant.

Introduction
Biomass is classiﬁed as any type of renewable materials from a biological origin and is a
highly abundant and easily available energy source (McKendry, 2002). Biomass has been
used by mankind since the discovery of the energy release from burning wood, a million
years ago. The discovery changed the path of humanity, and has been applied to fulﬁlled
the basic human need for heating, cooking and protection. The application of biomass for
energy purposes, has led to the human admiration and dependency on combustion ever since
(Strezov et al., 2014).
The industrial revolution changed human living conditions and introduced new technologies
to society, as power plants and the internal combustion engine, which required a change
in fuel sources due to increased energy demands (Rosillo-Calle and Woods, 2012). During
the revolution, the usage of fossil fuels increased drastically, resulting in a decrease in the
usage of biomass as an energy source, to the extent where biomass is no longer the primary
fuel source. The huge dependency of fossil fuels for energy generation and the fact that the
energy demand is rapidly increasing in our society, creates a number of challenges. Some
of these are greenhouse gas emissions, which is the primary reason for the ongoing climate
crises, water pollution from coal mine activities, depletion of fossil fuel and the unequal
distribution of oil, which are generating fear of energy insecurity for many societies. These
growing challenges once again makes biomass an attractive resources for energy production,
as well as its many essential advantages that can contribute to solving the challenges caused
by fossil fuels (Strezov et al., 2014).
In consequence, the scientiﬁc community is in the process of developing and implementing
technologies that can be the foundation of a new bio-based economy, including relevant
products for the energy and chemical sectors. Ligocellulososic biomass is an enormous source
of renewable material to be implemented into this bio-based economy, because of its huge
availability, low cost and environmental friendly production, and it is not compromising food
production. The production of Lignocellulosic biomass is estimated to be above 1010 tons per
year (Sanchez and Cardona, 2008). A major obstacle that limits the usage of lignocellulosic
biomass in energy and chemical production, is the lack of low-cost pretreatments for
overcoming the recalcitrance of these materials. A pretreatment is a crucial process when
using lignocellulosic biomass in energy production, since it has a huge impact on the yields,
but also a large contribution to the total costs of the process (Moreno and Olsson, 2017).

1.1
Problem Delimitation and Research Objectives
Aqueous Ammonia Soaking (AAS) is a highly eﬃcient pretreatment for increasing the
methane yield of lignocellulosic biomasses, and it constitutes a promising e↵ect for
improving the feasibility of manure based biogas plants.
Up until now, AAS has been
optimized for swine manure ﬁbers and wheat straw and it is observed that the optimal
conditions and the degree of methane yield increase, di↵er considerably between the two
biomasses. This raise the hypothesis that the chemical composition of the biomass is an
essential parameter a↵ecting the eﬃciency of AAS, which is the focus of this thesis: to
correlate the biomass chemical composition with the eﬃciency of AAS, in terms of increasing
methane yields and hydrolysis rate constant. For this purpose, eight types of biomasses are
selected and will undergo AAS pretreatment, prior to biochemical methane potential tests.
Furthermore, a characterization of the biomasses will be conducted in terms of structural
polymers (cellulose, hemicellulose, lignin), total extractives and ash content. The solubilizing
e↵ect of the AAS pretreatment is also investigated in regards to soluble sugar, soluble
total nitrogen and soluble organic materials.
The data obtained after characterization
and methane potential tests, will be subjected to partial least squares regression in order
to reveal the most inﬂuential component(s) of the biomass matrix to the improvement of
methane yield and KHyd, due to AAS pretreatment.
This study is designed to investigate the following research objectives:
• Identify if certain component(s) in the chemical composition of the biomass is correlated
to the increase in methane yield or increase in hydrolysis rate constant, due to AAS
pretreatment.
• Identify the chemical composition of the eight biomasses.
• Identify the biochemical methane potential of the AAS pretreated and raw biomasses.
• Quantify the performance of the pretreatment in regards to the biodegradability.
• Investigate the e↵ect of the AAS pretreatment on the solubilization of the biomass.

Background
2.1
Lignocellulosic Biomass as Feedstock
The major obstacle that limits the application of lignocellulosic biomass in the energy and
chemical sectors, is the lack of low-cost pretreatements to overcome the recalcitrance of these
biomasses. Recalcitrance is the plant cell walls resistance to deconstruct because of a matrix
of lignin, hemicellulose and the highly crystalline structure of cellulose, see Figure 1 (Brodeur
et al., 2011).
Figure 1: Illustration of the structure of the plant cell wall of lignocellulosic biomass. Cellulose
spirals are packed into microﬁbrils and glued together by the branching hemicellulose with
lignin tightly bound to the matrix Alonso et al. (2012).
Cellulose consist of chains of D-glucose units bonded together by β-1,4-glucosidic bonds.
Each cellulose chain are forming a spiral and the spirals are packed together into microﬁbrils
which are stabilized by inter- and intra-connected hydrogen bonds. These microﬁbrils are
connected to one another by hemicellulose and proteins such as pectin, and are covered by
lignin. Hemicellulose are composed of a 6 carbon sugar (glucose, mannose, galactose) and a 5
carbon sugar (xylose, arabinose) or uronic acids, linked together forming a branching polymer
(Hendriks and Zeeman, 2009). Uronic acids is a minor component in lignocellulosic land
plants. However, algaes and oceanic plants can contain large portions due to the structural
polymer alginate composed of chains of uronic acids (Draget, 2009). Hemicellulose are linked
to cellulose by hydrogen bonds, and covalent bonds to lignin, where it acts as the ”glue”
in the matrix, and strengthen the rigidity of the plant cell wall. The strong bonds between
lignin and hemicellulose have been heavily studied, and it has been shown that xylan plays an
important role with ester bonds to lignin. However, several other types of linkages have been
reported (Tarasov et al., 2018). Lignin is an amorphous polymer made of phenylpropane
units (p-coumaryl, coniferyl and sinapyl alcohols) and provides rigidity to the plant cell
wall and resistance against microbial degradation. Therefore, lignin is also considered non-
digestible and impedes enzymatic hydrolysis, as the enzyme is immobilized on the lignin
surface (Palonen et al., 2004), (Brodeur et al., 2011).

2.2
Anaerobic Digestion Process
Anaerobic digestion (AD) is a biochemical process that describes the metabolic reaction of
organic material in the absence of oxygen, by a mixed culture of synergistic microorganisms.
The microorganisms in the AD converts organic material into mainly methane, carbon
dioxide (biogas), and liquid and solid residue (Melville et al., 2014).
Other gases are
produced in small quantities such as: H2S, NH3, H2, N2, O2, water vapor and possible
other contaminates e.g siloxanes (Persson et al., 2006) (Appels et al., 2011).
AD is an
essential process used to recover the energy from organic waste, instead of sending it to
the landﬁll, where it would release greenhouse gases into the atmosphere. AD is a natural
process that occurs e.g in anaerobic sediments of swamps and lakes. By mimicking these
environments, the microorganism found in these places can be cultivated and controlled
in closed reactors called digesters.
The microorganism in the AD can be categories into
two groups according to how they react to oxygen.
Facultative anaerobes can survive
in the absence and presence of oxygen.
If oxygen is present is it utilized in metabolic
reactions.
If oxygen is absent will they utilize molecules such as nitrate to convert
other molecules by denitriﬁcation. The second group is obligate anaerobes which can be
subdivided into strict and aerotolorant. Strict cannot grow and can lose viability if oxygen is
present and aerotolerant do not use oxygen to grow, but can tolerate it (Melville et al., 2014).
The AD process occurs over four sequential phases, namely hydrolysis, acidogenesis,
acetogenesis and methanogenesis. In each phase, a speciﬁc community of microorganisms
metabolize the organic material and transforming it into simpler and smaller compounds,
which can be utilized by the group of microorganisms in the subsequent phase. An illustration
of the AD process can be seen in Figure 2.

As AD works in sequential phases with di↵erent communities of microorganisms living in
synergy, then the slowest phase will determine the overall rate of the process. Furthermore,
if the growth rates of a speciﬁc community are inhibited or the balance of the microorganism
shift within the process, it can a↵ect the eﬃciency and the overall rate of the digestion (Evans
et al., 2015).
2.2.1
Hydrolysis
Hydrolysis is the ﬁrst phase of AD where insoluble polymerized organic compounds such as
carbohydrates, fat and proteins are hydrolyzed into smaller, soluble compounds. That is,
oligo- or monosaccharides, long-chain fatty acids and amino acids, respectively (Ali Shah
et al., 2014).
The mechanism by which solubilization occurs is either by secretion of
extracellular enzymes such as cellulases, proteases and lipases into the bulk solution, or
microorganisms attaching the enzymes to the cell surface and adhering to the solid particles
in the solution (Vavilin et al., 2008). Generally, in feedstocks containing high lignin and
cellulose content, the hydrolysis phase is the rate-limiting step of the AD process, because
lignin protects the structural carbohydrates for microbial degradation (Srirangan et al., 2012),
(Melville et al., 2014). Other parameters a↵ecting the rate are the size of the particles, pH,
production of enzymes, di↵usion and adsorption of enzymes on the particles. Streptococcus
sp. and Enterobacterium sp. are the group of facultative anaerobes bacteria responsible for
the hydrolysis phase (Amani et al., 2010).
2.2.2
Acidogenesis
During the acidogenesis, microorganisms further degrade the products of the hydrolysis phase
through fermentation into volatile fatty acids (VFA) (acetic, propionic, butyric, formic and
pentanoic), alcohols (methanol and ethanol), carbon dioxide and hydrogen (Melville et al.,
2014). Proteolytic bacteria ferments the amino acids and generates carboxylic acids, ammonia
and carbon dioxide (Ali Shah et al., 2014). The acidogenesis phase consists of a various
populations of microorganisms, some groups of the bacteria produce acetates, carbon dioxide
and hydrogen which can be directly digested by methanogens, see Figure 2. However, a high
production rate of VFA can lead to a reduction of pH in the solution, which can inhibit
the activity of the microorganisms in the following phases, and can not be consumed by the
methanogens (Cesaro et al., 2012). These products must be converted by obligatory bacteria
into hydrogen and acetic acids in the process named acetogenesis.
2.2.3
Acetogenesis
The acetogenesis is the phase that links acidogenesis and methanogenesis. In this phase the
VFA and alcohols are oxidized into acetic acid, carbon dioxide, water and hydrogen (Evans
et al., 2015). The reaction and the thermodynamic feasibility of breaking down butyrate and
propionate into acetate under standard conditions, are shown in Table 1 (Melville et al., 2014).
As seen from the equation, the reaction is endergonic under standard conditions. However,
at low hydrogen partial pressures, the reaction is exergonic. The hydrogen reduction occurs
with a symbiosis relationship between the acetogenic bacteria and the methanogenic archaea.

Methanogens utilize hydrogen and carbon dioxide to form methane. This interdependence
between the hydrogen-producing species and the hydrogen-consuming methanogens is critical
for the stability of the overall process. On one side, the acetogenic thrives at low hydrogen
partial pressures. At the same time, the methanogenic is depending on high hydrogen partial
pressure to gain an energy win when producing methane. This symbiotic relationship leaves a
narrow thermodynamic window where the hydrogen partial pressure suits both populations,
and the whole process is stable (Melville et al., 2014), (Ali Shah et al., 2014).

2.2.4
Methanogenesis
The fourth and ﬁnal phase is where methanogenic, a community of microorganisms from the
archaea domain, convert the acetic acid, hydrogen and carbon dioxide into methane, carbon
dioxide and water.
There are two essential groups of methanogenic involved in the two
pathways, see Figure 2. The Acetoclatic methanogens that transform acetate into methane
and carbon dioxide, and hydrogenotrophic methanogens that transform carbon dioxide and
hydrogen into methane and water. The reactions can be seen in Table 1 (Melville et al., 2014).
From the reactions, it is shown that the energy gain is higher with the hydrogenotrophic
pathway. However, about 70% of the produced methane results from the conversion of acetic
acid (Liu and Whitman, 2008). The methanogenic archaea are sensitive to environmental
conditions, especially low pH can inhibit this group of microorganisms. Additionally, the
methanogens have a signiﬁcantly lower growth rate compared to the other microorganisms
in the AD (Appels et al., 2008),(Melville et al., 2014), (Ostrem et al., 2004).
2.3
Beneﬁts of Anearobic Digestion and Applications of Biogas
The AD can convert most biomasses into biogas without any pretreatment, even if the biomass
has very low dry matter content (<20%) (Milledge and Harvey, 2018).
For most other
conversion technologies, this is not applied. Combustion can only provide a positive energy
balance if the dry matter content is higher then 40%, and even at these conditions most of
the stored energy is used to evaporate the water contained in the biomass. The eﬃciency of
11
==PAGE:13==
gasiﬁcation and pyrolysis also decreases signiﬁcantly when the water content is high, and the
presence of water is undesirable in the produced bio-oil (Van de Velden et al., 2010). The
usage of these technologies requires a drying step, hence an energy input, for wet types of
biomass and waste. The biogas from AD is both energy eﬃcient and environmentally friendly
since there is a low emission of hazards pollutants. The primary application of biogas is by
sending it directly to a combined heat and power installation for the generation of electricity
and heat. The emission of volatile organic compounds in these engines is limited since 99% of
the volatile compounds are fully oxidized during combustion (Appels et al., 2011). This is an
essential advantage compared to incineration that emits hazardous compounds like dioxins,
and therefore requires an extensive gas puriﬁcation (Appels et al., 2008). Alternatively, the
biogas can be puriﬁed and upgraded for more novel applications such as vehicle fuel and fuel
cells. Also, if properly upgraded, the biogas can be injected in the natural gas grid, which in all
cases would increase the value of the biogas (Appels et al., 2008). An important puriﬁcation
step is to remove the H2S and siloxanes that give corrosive and operational problems in the
equipment. Biogas can also be converted into high-value organic compounds, by converting
the methane into syngas (a mixture of H2 and CO). This syngas is then used as a feedstock
for the production of valuable organic compounds. Furthermore, the solid fraction of the AD
is rich in nitrogen and can be used as a fertilizer in agriculture (Tambone et al., 2009). AD
is also feasible in small scale and can be applied in developing countries, where agricultural
residues can be converted in AD and provide cooking gas for a household in rural areas
(Singh and Gu, 2010). The essential drawbacks of the AD are the long retention times which
is around 20-30 days, and in some cases the low degradation yield of the organic matter, which
are mainly caused by the inaccessibility of the carbohydrate due to the lignin carbohydrate
complex. To overcome that problem, pretreatment can be used. The main encouragement
to apply pretreatment methods is to change the structural composition of the biomass, so
that it is more suitable for digestion. For an optimal operation of lignocellulosic biomass as
an energy source, a pretreatment is essential to improve its digestibility (Moreno and Olsson,
2017).
2.4
Pretreatments
Several pretreatment technologies have been suggested to overcome the low digestibility of
lignocellulosic biomass.
However, it is not a one size ﬁts all solution and the adequate
pretreatment is depending on factors as the type of biomass, the ﬁnal product, economics
and environmental impact, which have to be considered.
The pretreatment technologies
can be categorized into three groups: physical, biological and chemical, and a combination
of di↵erent pretreatment methods have been suggested (Moreno and Olsson, 2017). Many
pretreament methods have shown to be highly e↵ective for improving the digestibility of
lignocellulosic biomasses. However, the high energy input or large consumption of chemicals
increase the cost of the implementation and often restrict the applications in the industry
(Lymperatou et al., 2019). Aqueous Ammonia Soaking (AAS) is a pretreatment with high
potential to improve the digestibility of lignocellulosic biomass to increase biogas production
at industrial scale. AAS is a simple alkaline pretreatment, where the biomass is soaking in
12
==PAGE:14==
an aqueous solution of ammonia (1-32% w/w) in closed containers at mild conditions (from
room temperature up until 90°C, at 1 atm) for a desired duration (hours to days). The
ammonia reagent is removed from the biomass by evaporation or by washing the biomass
with water until an acceptable ammonia concentration is achieved for AD (Lymperatou et al.,
2017),(Oliwit et al., 2019). AAS pretreatments has multiple advantages, e.g. that ammonia
is one of the most applied chemicals in the industry, and with that comes low cost and
a lot of knowledge of processing ammonia. Furthermore, ammonia has the properties to
swell the cellulosic biomass, a high selectivity to lignin, low interaction with the structural
carbohydrates, and a volatile nature, which makes it possible to recover and reuse. The
recovering of ammonia will lower the expenses, as well as chemicals used to adjust the
pH (Kim et al., 2016). The sensitivity of ammonia to lignin has been intensively studied
and ammonia is found to break the intermolecular covalent ester bonds between lignin and
hemicellulose. The de-esteriﬁcation leads to a lignin removal of up to 75% in lignocellulosic
biomass, and keep both the cellulose ﬁbers (100%) and hemicellulose (85%) fractions in the
solid residue, and more accessible for enzymatic hydrolysis (Moreno and Olsson, 2017). The
optimal operation conditions for AAS such as ammonia concentration, solid to liquid ratio
and pretreatment duration, are highly dependant on the biomass and has to be optimized
individually (Kim and Lee, 2005). AAS’s low energy input and recovery of ammonia, gives
it potential as pretreatment for biogas production on lignocellulosic biomasss, at industrial
scale.

The correlation of ln(B −B0) and ⌧is plotted and Khyd is determined as the slope of

3.7
Partial Least Square Regression
Partial least square regression (PLS) is used to identify important variables in the composition
that correlates to a certain response, and is performed using Unscrambler Version 11.0
software (CAMO software, Oslo, Norway).
PLS is particularity good for data with very
correlated variables. The PLS builds on cross validation, where it iteratively are removing
one sample and making a new calibration model. The removed sample is then predicted
using the newly made model. The process continues to remove one sample at the time, until
every sample is left out once. Two matrixes investigates the components of the composition
(X-variables) (total-, water- and ethanol extractives, lignin, ash, cellulose, hemicellulose and
hemicellulose:lignin ratio) and the responses (Y-variables) of interest. In this case several
responses are used to see if there is a reliable correlation, such as: ultimate methane increase
(%), Khyd increase (%) and methane increase after 18 days (%). The PLS is only made
with one response at a time for the same X-variables. However, sometimes a biomass or
X-variable is left out if it dominates the analysis. The weight of the values are normalized
by dividing the mean center with the standard deviation. The reliability and performance of
the correlations are evaluated by the R2 value and the root mean square error (RMSE) of the
calibration (Monlau et al., 2012), (Esbensen et al., 2002). Low RMSE and high R2 values
indicates a good ﬁt of the model. The purpose of PLS used in this thesis is as a descriptive
statistical tool.

The result section summarizes the ﬁndings from the experiments made in this thesis.
The results are subdivided into ﬁve parts; [1] the BMP tests of the raw and AAS-
pretreated biomass, [2] the compositional analysis, [3] the e↵ect of the AAS pretreatment
on solubilization, [4] the elemental analysis and [5] the partial least squares regression. The
results are presented as a basis of gram pr 100 g TS of received biomass (%TS) to be able to
compare the results between di↵erent biomasses.
4.1
BMP Tests
The BMP results for the raw and pretreated straws are presented in Figure 8, and for raw
and pretreated grass, horse manure, chicken manure and seaweed are shown in Figure 9.
The BMP tests are monitored until the production of methane settles, which is 49 days for
the straws and 42 days for the remaining biomasses.
In Figure 8, the AAS barley straw and AAS rye straw are seen to have the exact same
pattern in cumulative methane yield, and settles the production at respectively 364 and 362
mL CH4/g TS. The same trend occurs for the raw wheat- and raw barley straw, where the
yield settles at 343 and 347 mL CH4/g TS, respectively. Rape straw has a lower production
for both raw and AAS, compared to the other straws. It is observed that all AAS straws
have a highly uneven settlement, with several drops in the cumulative yield after 21 days,
which must be due to error in the measurements.

4.3
E↵ect on Solubilization due to AAS Pretreatment
From the soluble COD, is it generally seen that a larger amount of the biomasses organic
material is solubilized due to the AAS treatment, compared to the raw biomass.
The
exception is chicken manure where the COD is marginally larger for the raw sample, see Table
6. Seaweed is signiﬁcantly a↵ected by the AAS pretreatment and has a 13-fold increase,
whereas the wheat straw has a 3-fold increase and the rest, except chicken manure, have
around a 2-fold increase in soluble COD. The soluble total nitrogen increases signiﬁcantly for
all biomasses, as an e↵ect of the AAS treatment. Chicken manure and grass have the highest
soluble nitrogen and highest soluble ammonium-N, in both the raw and the AAS treated
samples. The straws have low soluble total nitrogen content in the raw samples, but have an
increase of approximately 7-10 fold, due to AAS pretreatment. Seaweed has practically no
soluble total nitrogen in the raw sample, while the AAS pretreatment increases the solubility
to 2.0 gTN/100g TS. The ammonium-N concentration of all the pretreated biomasses is
lower than of the inoculum to ensure no inhibition could occur due to high ammonium-N
concentration in the biomasses, see section 3.1. The AAS treatment also a↵ects the biomass
by making more alkaline conditions as seen from the increase in pH in all biomasses, see
Table 7. Chicken manure is the only biomass with acidic conditions for both the raw and
AAS treated samples.

4.5
Partial Least Square Regression
Previous results showed no obvious correlation between the biomasses composition and the
increase in methane yields or Khyd, due to AAS pretreatment. In this section, partial least
square (PLS) regression is used to investigate if there is a statistical correlation. This section
will only include the ﬁndings with high enough R2 value.

4.5.1
PLS of KHyd Increase
The PLS results shows that the KHyd increase (%) is the response with the highest correlation
coeﬃcient, with a R2 of 0.91, and a root mean square error (RMSE) of 8.4 (see validation
in Appendix III). In two factors, are the predictors explaining 91% of the variance in the
response, where 60% is explained in factor 1 and 31% is explained in factor 2. From the score
plot in Figure 12a, is seaweed seen to be strongly negatively expressed in factor 1. Horse
manure has the highest positive expression in both factor 1 and 2. The remaining biomasses
are located closely together, near the center.

Discussion
5.1
BMP test
The BMP test is conducted to investigate the increase in the methane yield and KHyd, due
to the AAS pretreatment. The increase in ultimate methane yield is furthermore evaluated
in regards to the biodegradability. The e↵ects of the AAS pretreatment seen in the BMP
tests, are compared to the solubilization e↵ect of the AAS pretreatment, to try to explain
the methane production process of the biomasses. Grass and seaweed are compared to other
studies because of the abnormal e↵ect AAS pretreatment have on these biomasses.
5.1.1
Interpretation of the BMP tests
The results obtained in the BMP tests indicates that the AAS pretreatment have a much
higher e↵ect in the beginning of the methane production than towards the end.
Over
time, the e↵ect of AAS is reduced relative to the cumulative methane production of the
raw biomass. The increased methane production in the beginning indicates that the AAS
pretreatment is opening up the structural matrix in some of the biomasses, which makes the
carbohydrates more accessible for microorganisms to digest. This is also indicated from the
increase in soluble carbohydrates, soluble COD and soluble total nitrogen, seen in the results.
The results of the soluble structural carbohydrates indicates that the pretreatment has a
great impact on hemicellulose, see Figure 11. The soluble sugars derived from hemicellulose
have increased in all eight biomasses, due to AAS, even though the magnitude di↵ers
among them, from 45% for rape straw to 700% for horse manure. The impact on soluble
sugars derived from cellulose is marginal among the biomasses, except for seaweed. This
corresponds to the theory that the ammonia is breaking the ester bonds between lignin and
hemicellulose, and solubilize lignin and parts of hemicellulose (Moreno and Olsson, 2017).
The soluble COD is an indirect measurement of the soluble organic material. The soluble
organics are higher for all AAS pretreated biomasses, except for chicken manure, see Table 6.
Chicken manure has the largest absolute increase in soluble structural carbohydrates of all
biomasses, and it is therefore unexpected that its soluble COD is found to be the negative,
for the AAS pretreated sample.
Furthermore, chicken manure are also found to have a
slightly negative e↵ect in ultimate methane yield, due to AAS pretreatment. The rest of
the biomasses have an increase in soluble carbohydrates, soluble total nitrogen and soluble
COD, indicating that the AAS pretreatment are solubilizing much more organic material.
This support the experimental design, to include the liquid fraction of the pretreatment in
the BMP test. Otherwise would much organic material be lost through washing, as done in
other studies using AAS as a pretreatment (Oliwit et al., 2019) (Li et al., 2015).
The accessible structural carbohydrates and the soluble organic material increase the rate of
hydrolysis, and thereby the overall rate of the process. Similar for all BMP tests, is that the
AAS pretreated biomass has a higher methane production in the beginning, see Figure 8 and
9. However, the di↵erence in methane yield are seen to decrease over time, between the raw
and AAS pretreated biomass. This shows that most of the structural carbohydrates that
40
==PAGE:42==
are made accessible due to AAS pretreatment, are also digested in the raw biomass, but the
AAS pretreated biomass obtain ultimate yield over a shorter time period. This especially
applies for horse manure, which has an signiﬁcant increase of soluble sugars derived from
hemicellulose and soluble COD of 7- and 2-fold, respectively. The AAS pretreatment also
resulted in signiﬁcant increase of 95% in KHyd and an yield increase of 28.5% after 18 days.
After the raw horse manure has settled methane production at 28 days, has the increase
in yield due to AAS pretreatment, reduced to 8.3%. These aspects are also shown in the
increase of KHyd. The high R2 value of the correlated experimental data conﬁrms that the
hydrolysis is the rate-limiting step in the process, see Appendix II. It is seen that the AAS
pretreatment make the hydrolysis rate constant increase, and the ultimate yield is thereby
obtained in a shorter time, compared to the raw biomass. From the processed BMP data
shown in Table 3, it is seen that the highest KHyd does not necessarily result in the highest
yield after 18 days. The signiﬁcant increase in KHyd are interesting ﬁndings, as it is of huge
importance for the industry.
An ultimate yield obtained after 49 days, are not relevant in regards of practical usage in
the industry.
In the industry is it desirable to have a hydraulic retention time between
15-20 days in AD (Siddique and Wahid, 2018). The KHyd is an essential parameter for AD
in the industry, where the AD process is often made in a continuous stirred tank reactor.
If a biomass has a high KHyd value will this result in a lower retention time. This further
leads to more biomass to be processed, and an overall higher biogas productivity. The high
e↵ect of AAS on methane yield in the beginning, are also in focus in the study made by
Lymperatou et al. (2019). The study chose optimal conditions of AAS for wheat straw based
on the methane yield after 17 days of digestion, as opposed to the ultimate yield. The same
e↵ect is observed in this study, where KHyd increased up to 2-fold due to AAS pretreatment,
and the e↵ect of the AAS pretreatment are signiﬁcantly higher after 17 days compared to
the ultimate yield. An increase in KHyd is not necessarily beneﬁcial. This is seen for grass,
where KHyd is increased with 58% due to a lower ultimate yield for AAS pretreated biomass,
compared to raw. The raw and AAS pretreated methane production curves are similar until
the production for AAS pretreated grass reach ultimate production, while the production
for raw grass increases further, see Figure 9. This indicates that a higher KHyd does not
necessarily result in a higher methane production. Therefore, it is important to evaluate the
short-term methane yield increase and the KHyd together to obtain a broader perspective
on the e↵ect of AAS (Lymperatou et al., 2019).
5.1.2
The E↵ect of AAS Pretreatment on Grass
The BMP test for grass shows that the AAS pretreatment results in a substantial drop in
ultimate yield of -14%, compared to raw grass. The raw grass already has a higher yield after
11 days of digestion, and the di↵erence in yield between the raw and AAS pretreated grass
continues to increase as the digestion continues. A negative e↵ect of AAS pretreatment has
been observed in previous studies, when using both the liquid and solid fraction in the BMP
experimental setup by Lymperatou et al. (2019). In regards to solubilization did the COD
41
==PAGE:43==
increase by 100%, due to the e↵ect of AAS. The AAS pretreatment has therefore solubilized
more organic material. However, from the soluble sugars in Figure 11, it is seen that grass
has a 33% decrease in soluble sugars derived from cellulose and an increase in soluble sugars
derived from hemicellulose of 50%. The increase in soluble sugars derived from hemicellulose
is the lowest among the biomasses, and the decrease in soluble sugars derived from cellulose
is the highest. These ﬁnding of signiﬁcant increase in COD and minimal increase in soluble
sugars are somewhat contradictory. The results of the decrease in soluble sugars derived
from cellulose indicates that the ammonia conditions gives a negative e↵ect on the biomass.
When investigating the performance of the microorganisms, is the biodegradability relatively
high for raw grass, with an utilization of 75%.
In a study made by Antonopoulou et al. (2015), grass from Greece are pretreated with AAS.
The conditions for the pretreatment are 32% ammonia (w/w), with a S:L of 100 g/L and 3
days duration at ambient pressure and temperature. The ammonia is removed by evaporation
in an evaporation tower, using similar procedure as in this thesis. The ultimate yield for raw
and AAS pretreated grass are 223 and 282 mL CH4/g TS, respectively, which corresponds to
a 26% increase due to AAS pretreatment, see Table 10. In this thesis, the ultimate yield of
grass are 271 and 234 mL CH4/g TS for raw and AAS pretreated, respectively. An interesting
observation is that the methane yield of the raw grass is signiﬁcantly higher than the yield
obtained in the study. The large di↵erence in the raw grass may indicate that there is a
variation between grass in Greece and in Denmark. This is also seen from the compositional
analysis, presented in Table 10, where the results from this thesis and Antonopoulou et al.
(2015) are compared. The main di↵erence is the lignin and hemicellulose content. The lignin
and hemicellulose content in grass from this thesis are 17.2 and 14.5% TS, respectively, and
12.3 and 24.0% TS, respectively in Antonopoulou et al. (2015). The grass from this thesis
has a higher lignin and lower hemicellulose content, but a higher methane yield. This is
contradictory to the theory, where higher lignin content should protect the carbohydrates
from degradation, and lower hemicellulose content indicates that there is less carbohydrates
to digest and to produce methane. Monlau et al. (2012) have made a predictive model of the
composition of a biomass and its BMP. From the model, lignin is found to be highly negatively
correlated with the BMP, and hemicellulose is positively correlated with the BMP.

The comparison indicates
that ammonia concentration of 15% will have a negative e↵ect on the methane yield, but

lower in this thesis, the yield obtained from raw barley straw are 343 mL CH4/g TS, which
is much higher than 216 mL CH4/g TS obtained in Dinuccio et al. (2010). This is again an
example of a biomass used in other studies, with a higher carbohydrate content and lower
lignin content than found in this thesis, but resulting in a lower methane yield.
A high uncertainty is related to the comparison of BMP results with other research,
and even within the same laboratory, where high variations are observed for the same
biomass (Str¨omberg et al., 2014). This has to be taken into account when comparing the
yields obtained in this thesis, with other studies. Holliger et al. (2016) tried to create a
standardization of the BMP test, where the inoculum is considered a central parameter. The
study suggest that the inoculum has to be taken from an active AD with complex organic
materials, to obtain a diverse and highly active microorganism community for the BMP test.
This could be an important factor that explain why the di↵erence in ultimate yield of the
raw biomass obtained in this thesis, is higher than the one from Dinuccio et al. (2010) and
Antonopoulou et al. (2015).

5.1.4
AAS Pretreatment Conditions
The rationale behind the AAS pretreatment conditions are from previous studies made by
Lymperatou et al. (2017) and Lymperatou et al. (2019), where optimal conditions for AAS
pretreatment on wheat straw and swine manure ﬁbers are found. The optimal conditions for
wheat straw is 18% NH3 (w/w), 7 days duration and 50 g/L S:L resulting in a 43% methane
yield increase after 17 days. The optimal conditions for swine manure is 7% NH3, 4 days
duration and 160 g/L S:L, resulting in a 244% increase in methane yield after 17 days. It
is assumed that wheat straw is a good estimate for all the straws and swine manure ﬁbers
is a reasonable estimate for chicken- and horse manure. The duration of 4 days is chosen
because it is the optimal conditions for swine manure ﬁbers and 4 days of AAS pretreatment
on wheat straw gave only a minor decrease in methane yield compared to 7 days. The S:L
is chosen to be in between the two optimum found. The ammonia concentration is a trade
o↵, since the optimums are signiﬁcantly di↵erent. A mild concentration of 7% ammonia
is optimal for swine manure ﬁbers, but not harsh enough for wheat straw, which is why
a 15% ammonia concentration is chosen. Furthermore, the objective is not about ﬁnding
the optimal conditions for AAS of each biomass, but to investigate if there is a correlation
between some critical components in the composition that correlates to the enhancement due
to pretreatment. It is therefore desirable to ﬁnd a signiﬁcant di↵erence between the AAS
pretreated and the raw biomasses. To limit the amount of change in the experimental setup,
and be able to compare each enhancement per biomass, are the same pretreatment conditions
needed. However, optimal conditions for all biomasses are impossible when applying the
same conditions for AAS pretreatment.
The conditions are therefore chosen to generate
an signiﬁcant e↵ect in all eight biomasses. However, three of the eight biomasses have a
negative e↵ect on the ultimate yields due to the AAS pretreatment. This is not desirable
and it illustrates how diverse the e↵ect of AAS are between di↵erent biomasses.
5.2
Uncertainties in the Results
The mass balance from the compositional analysis for rye straw and horse manure, are
beyond 100% with 111 and 106%, respectively. Mass balance above 100% have also been
found in other studies, where a mass balance of 104% for wheat straw (Lymperatou et al.,
2019) and 102% for sunﬂower straw Antonopoulou et al. (2015) are obtained, evaluating
the same compositional components as in this thesis. The mass balance for horse manure
is therefore considered valid, since it is common to extend the mass balance with a few
percentages. However, a mass balance of 111% for rye straw is considered too high. One
can observe a relatively low standard deviation for all components of rye straw, except total
45
==PAGE:47==
extractives with 16.17 ± 2.56%, as seen in Table 4. The value of nonvolatile extractives of
14.66 ± 0.37% should be similar to the total extractives. This indicates that total extractives
could be overestimated with a few percent points, but does not explain the extra 11%.
The structural carbohydrates in rye straw is 74.59%, which is signiﬁcantly higher than the
closely related wheat- and barley straw, with respectively 63.09 and 62.21%. However, the
higher carbohydrate content of rye straw is conﬁrmed by the TBMP, where rye straw has
460 mL CH4/g TS, and wheat- and barley straw only have 410 and 427 mL CH4/g TS,
respectively. The 111% mass balance could therefore be caused by a minor overestimation
in several components. This is opposite for seaweed which has the lowest mass balance of 89%.
Seaweed varies from the other biomasses used in this thesis.
It has a much higher ash
content, which has a large e↵ect on the PLS results. The ash content in seaweed is high
due to the lack of washing before any of the experiments, where sand was still attached
to the biomass and contributed to high ash content. Furthermore, seaweed is expected to
have the lowest carbohydrate content of all tested biomasses, and this is also found in the
results. Seaweeds hemicellulose content originates from the detected xylan, since arabinan
is not part of its structural carbohydrates (see Figure 10). Uronic acids are known to be
a big part of seaweeds structural composition (Draget, 2009). However, the HPLC system
could not detect uronic acids in the analysis. The uronic acid will therefore be part of the
residual, which could explain the high residual found in seaweed. The hemicellulose content
of seaweed could therefore be underestimated in this thesis. Another interesting observation
regarding seaweed, is that there is no detection of sulfur. Seaweed is known to have a high
sulfur content, which is what creates the bad odor on the beach on a summer day. Some
studies have shown that certain types of seaweed contain 10% sulfur as a weight of elements
in the composition Reka et al. (2017). It is therefore a high uncertainty related to this result.
Further investigations could include compositional analysis of all biomass after AAS
pretreatment, to determine the deligniﬁcation and see the actual changes in the chemical
composition of the biomass, due to AAS. Many pretreatment methods discuss how much
deligniﬁcation a pretreatment can perform, and it would therefore be interesting to gain a
further understanding of the pretreatments e↵ect on di↵erent biomasses.
5.2.1
High Biodegradability of AAS Pretreated Wheat Straw
An interesting ﬁnding is the high biodegradability of AAS pretreated wheat straw, of
97%. AAS pretreated barley straw are also seen to have a high utilization, of 84%. High
biodegradability has also been reported by Oliwit et al. (2019), where maize bran is found to
have a biodegradability of 83% after AAS pretreatment. A biodegradability around 80% are
therefore considered plausible. However, the high biodegradability of AAS pretreated wheat
straw is further investigated. One reason could be that the methane yield obtained in the
BMP test is too high, or that the TBMP is too low. The TBMP for wheat straw obtained
in this thesis corresponds to 436 mL CH4/g VS. This is not signiﬁcantly di↵erent from the
values found in other studies: 426 (Kaparaju et al., 2009), 432 (Moller et al., 2004), 436
(Bauer et al., 2010), 444 (Ferreira et al., 2013) and 468 mL CH4/g VS (Lymperatou et al.,
46
==PAGE:48==
2019). The ultimate yield of AAS pretreated wheat straw obtained in this thesis is 398 mL
CH4/g TS. Lymperatou et al. (2019) made a study, where optimal AAS conditions for wheat
straw are found. The results showed that four of the BMP tests had yields ranging from
400-443 mL CH4/g TS, which is higher than the ultimate yield found in this thesis. Based
on these studies, can the 97% biodegradability not be rejected, but is rather regarded as a
high performing AAS pretreatment on wheat straw.
5.3
Partial Least Square Regression
The PLS is conducted to identify if certain components in the chemical composition of the
biomass, are correlated to the increase in methane yield and KHyd, due to AAS pretreatment.
The experimental set-up is also evaluated in regards to the diversity among the composition
of the biomasses, and alternative options for new biomasses are suggested.
5.3.1
Increase of KHyd
The correlation between KHyd and all the biomasses have a high R2 value of 0.92, explained
in two factors.
Seaweed has a highly negative inﬂuence on the analysis.
When a single
biomass has such high inﬂuence, is it potentially masking the correlation of the other
biomasses.
When the analysis are repeated, excluding seaweed, the results show that
seaweed is masking the correlation. Firstly, in the score plot, the remaining seven biomasses
are well spread with an acceptable R2 value of 0.78, indicating that all biomasses are
contributing to the response. The correlation loading plot changes radically when seaweed
is excluded, see Figure 12b and 13b. When many of the components are changing their
correlation, it indicates that seaweed has dominated the analysis.
Seaweed are the only
biomass with a negative e↵ect on KHyd, and has the lowest lignin content, lowest structural
carbohydrates and highest ash content of -4, 12, 15 and 51%, respectively. Seaweed has a
signiﬁcantly di↵erent response than the other biomasses, and a very di↵erent composition,
which means that the components in seaweeds composition are found to have a too high
correlation with the KHyd response. This is also seen when comparing the two correlation
loading plots, Figure 12b and 13b. Therefore, the most reliable correlation is found when
seaweed is excluded.
The lignin content is found to be positively correlated with the increased KHyd, even though
the lignin only varies from 12-23%. This correlation could be driven by horse manure and
grass, which have the highest increase in KHyd, and the highest lignin content. It is also
seen that horse manure and grass are the most positively expressed biomasses in factor 1,
see Figure 13a. The positive correlation of lignin and KHyd corresponds well with theory.
The more lignin a biomass has, the better the AAS pretreatment can perform. Ammonia
breaks the ester bond between lignin and hemicellulose, making the carbohydrates more
accessible for the microorganism.
This will lead to a faster hydrolysis, where methane
production will increase rapidly in the beginning, obtaining ultimate yield in a shorter time
period (Moreno and Olsson, 2017). Ash is positively correlated with the KHyd response.
This correlation could be driven by the high ash content of 17 and 18% in grass and horse
47
==PAGE:49==
manure, respectively. However, this correlation is not considered reliable because ash should
not inﬂuence the KHyd increase, and it is therefore considered a random correlation. There
is no clear correlation between hemicellulose and KHyd as a response, because hemicellulose
is found to be negatively correlated in factor 1 and positive in factor 2. Furthermore, the
extractives could be considered irrelevant for the Khyd increase since they are only expressed
in factor 2.
The extractives are so easily removed from the biomass, and most of the
organic matter in the extractives would already be digested without the AAS pretreatment.
Therefore, is it plausible that the extractives are not inﬂuencing the increase in KHyd, when
using AAS pretreatment.
The hemicellulose:lignin ratio is signiﬁcantly negatively correlated to the response. A biomass
with a high hemicellulose:lignin ratio is not as well protected as a biomass with low ratio,
due to lignins protective ability.
Therefore, if the ratio is high can the microorganisms
easily get access to the structural carbohydrates, and the e↵ect of the pretreatment will
be minimal.
The chicken manure and rye straw have the highest ratio, whereas chicken
manure is an indicator of this possible explanation. The BMP production curves for raw
and AAS pretreated chicken manure are more or less similar, illustrating that the structural
carbohydrates are easily accessible, see Figure 9.
This is opposite to a low ratio, where
there will be more lignin per hemicellulose, and the pretreatment can break down the bonds
between hemicellulose and lignin, and thereby increase the rate of the hydrolysis. This is
observed from the composition and BMP test of horse manure. Horse manure has the lowest
hemicellulose:lignin ratio in the PLS analysis, when seaweed is excluded. In Figure 9, it is
seen that AAS pretreated horse manure has a much faster hydrolysis rate, where methane
production increases more in the beginning compared to the raw BMP test. AAS pretreated
horse manure obtain ultimate yield after 18 days, while raw horse manure ﬁrst obtain ultimate
yield at day 28. This shows that the low hemicellulose:lignin has a positive e↵ect on the
KHyd, when the biomass is pretreated. The relationship between a low hemicelullose:lignin
ratio and a less accessible structure are also indicated by the soluble sugars experiments,
shown in Figure 11. It is seen that the soluble sugars are very low in raw horse manure
compared to the other raw biomasses, except seaweed. This indicates that it is diﬃcult to
deconstruct the structural matrix in horse manure. However, after the AAS pretreatment
has the soluble sugars derived from hemicullose increased by 7 fold for horse manure, which
is the highest obtained increase, in percentage. This indicates that AAS is breaking many
ester bonds in the structural matrix, resulting in a solubilization of hemicellulose, as it is
no longer tightly bound in the matrix. Further, when comparing the biodegradability of
chicken- and horse manure, see Table 6, it is seen that they have similar TBMP. However,
the biodegradability of raw chicken manure is 50% higher than raw horse manure. This also
conﬁrms that horse manures structural carbohydrates are less accessible, which could be due
to the low hemicellulose:lignin ratio.
5.3.2
Increase in Methane Yields
From the score plots of increased ultimate yield and methane yield after 18 days, is it
seen that seaweed has a signiﬁcant positive inﬂuence on the analysis, see Figure 14 and

==PAGE:50==
16. Seaweed is found to be masking the correlation, which is also seen in the correlation
loadings plots, where ash is the only component having a positive correlation with the
increased methane yield.
Seaweed has a much higher positive increase in methane yield
and contain a very di↵erent component composition, compared to the other biomasses.
The large variation in component composition between seaweed and the other biomasses, is
regarded to have a high inﬂuence on the results. Therefore, to ﬁnd an acceptable correlation
coeﬃcient between the components and the increase in ultimate methane yield and methane
yield after 18 days, are both seaweed and horse manure excluded from the two analysis.
Minimal di↵erence is seen between the two results, see Figure 15 and 17.
However, the
correlation with the yield increase after 18 days has a R2 of 0.92, which is much higher than
the ultimate yield increase with a R2 of 0.75. This is because the e↵ect of the pretreatment
is much higher after 18 days, than at the ultimate yield. A higher e↵ect of pretreatment
will lead to a more reliable correlation prediction, and therefore give a higher correlation
coeﬃcient. The remaining six biomasses are well spread in the score plots of the methane
yields, see Figure 15a and 17a. However, rape straw is the only biomass positively expressed
in factor 2, which contributes to a large part of the overall explanation of 43 and 46%, in
the two analysis. From the correlation loading plots, are the compositional components only
correlated to factor 1. However, the increase of methane yields are well explained in both
factors. This indicates that they are all relatively correlated with the response, but are not
explaining the response in both factors. The structural carbohydrates are the substrate for
methane production and it is plausible that a higher content of hemicellulose and cellulose
would give an increased methane yield, due to AAS pretreatment. The positive correlation
between lignin and increased methane yield is also considered valid, because, when a biomass
contain more lignin can the AAS pretreament interact with more lignin and deconstruct the
structural matrix. However, the correlation is not signiﬁcant. The hemicellulose:lignin ratio
also indicates a positive correlation with the response, but the correlation is not signiﬁcant
either. The extractives and ash are negatively correlated with the response in factor 1, which
is regarded plausible. This is because a biomass with high content of ash and extractives
will contain less carbohydrates. Ash does not contain any organic material, and the organic
material in the extractives will be digested regardsless of the AAS pretreatment.
Horse manure and seaweed constitutes a large fraction of the biomasses used to predict the
correlation, which is now excluded from the results when the two biomasses are removed
from the analysis. Excluding horse manure and seaweed drastically reduces the variation
in composition among the remaining biomasses, since the straws composition are relatively
similar. If the compositions of the biomasses are alike, is it much more diﬃcult to estimate
a correlation with the response. Lignin is a component which is interesting to investigate,
however, in this PLS analysis does the lignin content in the biomasses only range between
12 - 18%, see Table 4 and 5. With this low range is it diﬃcult to correlate lignin with the
response. This can explain why lignin has a positive correlation with the increased methane
yields, but is not signiﬁcant. The low variation in the compositions constitutes a problem
in the experimental design. When the majority of the biomasses have similar composition,
while one biomass is signiﬁcantly di↵erent, can this have high inﬂuence on the PLS analysis,

as is seen for seaweed. If the analysis was performed with other biomasses which also had
a signiﬁcantly di↵erent composition, is it likely that seaweed and horse manure would not
be excluded. Analysing more diverse biomasses with larger variations in composition could
prevent one biomass from dominating the analysis. Furthermore, a larger diversity would
also provide a better picture of the correlation between the compositional components and
the increase in methane yields for lignocellulosic biomass, due to AAS pretreatment. Other
biomasses that could have been included are some type of softwood with lignin content up
to 40% (Novaes et al., 2010), other species of algae or oceanic plants with low lignin content
(L´opez et al., 2013).

Conclusion
The PLS regression, excluding seaweed, found a reliable correlation between the components
in the chemical composition of the biomasses and the increase of KHyd, due to AAS
pretreatment. The lignin content was found to be signiﬁcantly positively correlated with the
increase in KHyd, due to AAS pretreatment. It was also found that high hemicellulose:lignin
ratio seems to be negatively correlated with the KHyd increase. Regarding the increase in
methane yields, the PLS regression could only obtain a reliable correlation when seaweed
and horse manure were excluded from the analysis. The results were similar for the increase
in methane yield after 18 days and the increase of ultimate methane yield, however, the
R2 value were 0.92 and 0.75, respectively. The regression found that no component has an
extremely good correlation with the yield increase. However, the cellulose and hemicellulose
content have a relatively positive correlation with the increase of methane yield, due to
AAS. The lignin and hemicellulose:lignin ratio were also found to be positively correlated
with the response, however, these were not signiﬁcant. The extractives and the ash content
is found to have a relatively negative correlation with the methane yield increase, due to
AAS pretreatment.
The BMP test illustrates that the pretreatment e↵ects the biomasses very di↵erently.
Seaweed had the highest percentage increase in ultimate yield with 46%, and grass had a
negative e↵ect of -14%. Another study made with grass used a harsher AAS pretreatment of
32% ammonia (w/w), which resulted in a 26% increase. This illustrates that the conditions
of AAS is highly a↵ecting the methane potential of a biomass.
However, one must also
be aware of the uncertainty present when comparing BMP tests across laboratories, where
especially the inoculum has a huge inﬂuence. The biodegradability of the AAS pretreated
biomasses indicates that most of them are utilizing a large part of their maximum potential,
where AAS pretreated wheat straw has the highest biodegradability of 97%. Furthermore,
the high yield increase of seaweed, due to AAS pretreatment, were actually relatively low
when considering the biodegradability of 57%.
This indicates that seaweed has an even
higher potential for optimizing the AAS pretreatment. When comparing seaweed with BMP
tests of other brown algaes, it was found that the seaweed used in this thesis had much lower
methane yield and theoretical yield than observed in other studies. These ﬁndings makes
other types of algae interesting to test with AAS pretreatment, if the yield increase found in
seaweed also applies for other algaes. Furthermore, the AAS pretreatment has the highest
e↵ect in the beginning of the methane production, and as the digestion proceeds the e↵ect
of AAS decreases signiﬁcantly. This is seen by the decrease of up to 20 percentage point in
yield from 18 days until the ultimate yield, for horse manure. The KHyd had a signiﬁcant
increase due to AAS for all biomasses, except seaweed, ranging from 19 to 94%.
The AAS are found to solubilize high amounts of organic material.
The soluble COD
increased for all biomasses with 2-3 folds, except for seaweed which has a 13 fold increase,
and chicken manure which had a minor decrease. Furthermore, the soluble total nitrogen
and soluble carbohydrates increases for all biomasses. The solubilization of soluble sugars
51
==PAGE:53==
derived from hemicellulose has a particular high increase, ranging from 45%-700%.
The identiﬁcation of the composition of the eight biomasses, illustrates that there are little
variation among them, except for seaweed. Seaweed has a signiﬁcantly di↵erent composition
and are therefore dominating the results of the PLS analysis.
The lignin content are
expected to have high inﬂuence on the increase in methane yield and KHyd, due to AAS
pretreatment. However, the lignin content in the biomasses only range from 12-23%, and
by excluding horse manure in the PLS analysis for the methane yield increase, the variation
of lignin was lowered to 12-18%. The experimental design should include a higher diversity
of biomasses to obtain a better understanding between the compositional components and
increase in methane yield and KHyd, due to AAS pretreatment.
Analysing more diverse
biomasses with larger variations in composition could prevent one biomass from dominating
the analysis. Such biomasses could be types of soft wood with up to 40% lignin, or other
types of algae or oceanic plants with low lignin content.
