Temperature dependent elasticity of Fontainebleau sandstone
by Petur SJÚRDABERG (s100277)
Determining the elastic parameter of sandstone and how an increase in
temperature effects these elastic parameters, is essential when storing ex-
cess heat energy in sandstone aquifer. Oedometer test is performed on a
sandstone sample from the Fontainebleau formation in France. To deter-
mine what inﬂuence increasing the temperature of the sample has on the
elastic parameters, the test subject has to be heated. Heating the test sub-
ject will also heat the test setup and inﬂuence the measurements. Therefore
tests are performed to determine the system’s deformation as a function of
temperature and axial stress. During these tests acoustic wave measure-
ments are made and the dead time as a function of temperature and stress
is determined.
The oedometer test setup uses a steel ring to ensure its boundaries and as
the diameter of the sample is a bit smaller than the inner diameter of the
steel ring, a method to negate this is tried.
At last putting the calibration results to use on the sandstone results. This
is to determine the sample’s static M-modulus and the dynmic elastic pa-
rameters and how they are inﬂuenced by temperature.
Two expressions for the system’s deformation as a function of temperature
and axial stress are obtained. One while loading the sample and one while
unloading the sample. An expression for the dead time as a function of
temperature has been obtained and is deemed valid even though there is
no axial stress part. A method with a steel foil was tried to negate the wig-
gle room but did not work. Dynamic and static M-modulus increases as
temperature increases. P-wave velocities increase as the temperature in-
creases. As a side note it is observed that the thermal expansion coefﬁcient
is dependent on porosity.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Our waste is burned at incineration stations, and the heat that is produced
is used to heat water which is pumped to the district as heating. The energy
demand is greater in the winter time than it is in the summer time. There-
fore there is a surplus of heat energy produced during the summer. As it
is now this energy goes to waste either in cooling tower or into the ocean.
Therefore it is relevant to store the excess energy for later use. This storage
is a geothermal reservoir within the Gassum formation. This geothermal
reservoir is already in use and gives district heating in Thisted. Currently
hot water is pumped up with a production well and used as district heat-
ing. Then it is pumped down again to maintain pressure in the formation.
The idea is to use the heath energy from the incineration station to heat the
water that is pumped down into the formation again.
FIGURE 1.1: Heat storage in geothermal aquifer.
Figure taken from http://www.staff.dtu.dk/ilfa/HeHo
This will raise the temperature in the formation from around 75◦C to
150◦C. This is illustrated in ﬁgure 1.1.

1.2
Problem Formulation
How raising the temperature from 75◦C to 150◦C in the geothermal aquifer
effects the formation, is to be investigated. If it does effect the strength of
the formation and if it does, to what extent. Samples are taken from the for-
mation to be tested. While testing the samples are heated to 150◦C or more.
This heating will inﬂuence the test equipment as well. The inﬂuence on the
equipment has to be determined. The goals in this assignment is to deter-
mine the system’s deformation as a function temperature and stress, and
the dead time as a function of temperature and stress. An oedometer test
setup is used. Additionally to investigate the elasticity of sandstone sam-
ples from the Fontainebleau formation, more speciﬁc the static M-modulus
and from the acoustic wave measurements the dynamic elastic parameters.
As the sample is obtained with a core drill, the diameter will not be exactly
the same as for the steel ring that ensures the oedometer test boundaries.
Therefore the sample will not ﬁt the into the oedometer setup perfectly but
leave a wiggle room. An additionally goal is to determine a method to load
a sample in to the oedometer setup such that there is no wiggle room or a
method to counteract the wiggle room.

test. This could explain why the deformation is larger at low stresses. As
the stress increases the sample deforms radially. When the stress is at about
10 MPa the sample has deformed so much that it cannot deform radially
any more, so in fact this test is not completely an oedometer test. It could
be described as a uniaxial test up to 10 MPa and as oedometer test from 10
MPa upwards.
The calibrated deformation at 2 MPa reveals a thermal expansion co-
efﬁcient of −8 · 10−6[ m

Ballato, 1991) depending on the crystallographic axis. As the Axial stress
increases so does the thermal expansion coefﬁcient. This is because as the
that sample deforms, the grains get squeezed closer together and this closes
the micro cracks and decreases the porosity. (Hassanzadegan et al., 2012)
uses an expression where the thermal expansion is determined by the solid
and by the porosity. So that the thermal expansion is dependent on porosity
is consistent with the literature.
The P-wave velocities increase as the temperature increases. The veloc-

Chapter 5
Conclusion
One goal of this project has been to determine the system’s inﬂuence on the
strain measurement and the acoustic wave measurements, then put it to use
on two sandstone samples from the Fontainebleau formation. The system’s
inﬂuence on the strain measurements has been determined by extrapolation
from tests on three steel plugs and revealed two expressions that describe
the system’s deformation as a function of temperature and axial stress. As
there is loading/unloading hysteresis one expression describes the system’s
deformation while loading and the other expression describes the system’s
deformation while while unloading. The system’s inﬂuence on the acoustic
wave measurements has been more difﬁcult to determine, as the acoustic
wave measurements were scrapped due to poor quality. Therefore an ex-
pression for the dead time as a function of temperature only has been ob-
tained. This expression is deemed to describe the dead time satisfactorily
as the calculated error from neglecting the axial stress component from the
expression is 1/10, maybe even smaller, than the estimated measurement
error.
Another goal has been to determine what to do with the wiggle room
between the sandstone sample and the steel ring, when the sample is loaded.
This to ensure the boundary condition for an oedometer test. Here a steel
foil, with the thickness of 0.1 mm, has been put all around the side of the
sample. This did not work as the sample expanded radially up to an axial
stress of about 10 MPa.
The last goal was to use the calibration on tests on sandstone sample from
the Fontainebleau formation and these revealed that the the P-wave ve-
locities increase as the temperature increases. The static and dynamic M-
modulus increases as temperature increases and the thermal expansion co-
efﬁcient is dependent on porosity. Due to technical issues it was not pos-
sible to determine the S-waves and therefore the M-modulus is the only
elastic parameter that is examined.
