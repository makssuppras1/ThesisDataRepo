Abstract
An increased focus on sustainability over the last decades has resulted in an increased
pressure on the building industry, which is accountable for a wast amount of the combined
energy and recourse consumption. The production of cement emits large amounts of
carbon dioxide (CO2) to the atmosphere, accounting for 8% of the global emissions yearly.
Furthermore, an increase in urbanization has lead to depletion of natural resources, such
as sand. Further exploitation of sand can have a negative effect on natural water systems
and disrupt the habitat of aquatic animals and microorganisms. It is therefore vital to
explore alternative construction materials and unconventional constituents, to reduce the
environmental impact of the building industry.
A way to reduce these emissions, is by exploiting sustainable building materials. GeopolyôÙ
mers are a sustainable material, synthesised by alkaliôÙactivation of an aluminosilicate preôÙ
cursor, which have attracted much attention for their ability to utilize industrial byôÙproducts,
while immobilising heavy metals. However, even though there a various benefits in using
geopolymers, it is still a novel material.
The aim of this thesis was to investigate the mechanical strength of metakaolin (MK) based
geopolymer mortar containing wood fly ash (WFA) originating from AmagervûÎrket, proôÙ
duced by circulating fluidised bed (CFB) combustion. The experimental setup was based
on a factorial design containing eight different mix designs. To investigate the optimum
use for WFA in geopolymer mortar, with regards to compressive strength, multiple test
series were conducted, utilizing WFA as either sand replacement, MK supplement or for
reduction of the amount of MK. The specimens were cured at either ambient (14 days at
20ãÎC) or elevated (80ãÎfor 24 hours, and subsequently 13 days at 20ãÎC) conditions, prior
to testing.
Furthermore, the characteristics of the utilized WFA, MK and 0/2 mm sand were deterôÙ
mined, including particle size distribution, oxide content, metal content, loss on ignition
(LOI), pH and conductivity, water solubility, metal and anion leaching, grain morpholôÙ
ogy using Scanning Electron Microscope (SEM), phase contents using XôÙray diffraction
(XRD) and thermogravimetric analysis (TGA). The results showed, that the WFA was alôÙ
kaline and predominantly contained CaO and SiO2. The XRD revealed mainly crystalline
calcite, quartz and lime, which was confirmed by the TGA analysis. The leaching test
revealed, that the WFA had a high content of leachable Cr, which may pose as an enviôÙ
ronmental issue. The characteristics of the WFA were found to be similar to those found
in previous studies of WFA originating from CFB combustion, however very different to
MK and 0/2 mm sand.
The results showed, that the inclusion of WFA increased the compressive strength for
the majority of the samples. The highest compressive strengths were found when the
WFA was utilized as 30% wt. MK supplement. The results of thermal curing showed
increased strength for reference samples, but decreased strength for samples containing
WFA, which suggested that the WFA could contain hydraulic properties (set, harden and
develop strength when mixed with water).
It was concluded that even though the addition of ash resulted in an increased compresôÙ
sive strength, the WFA did not undergo geopolymerization, and was therefore not suitable
in geopolymer mortar, neither as sand or MK supplement/replacement.

Introduction
Worldwide, 30 billion tonnes of concrete are used each year, which is three times as much
as 40 years ago, and with the increase in urbanisation, the demand for concrete is growing
steeply [1]. The key constituents of concrete are cement, sand, gravel and water, all of
which are associated with environmental issues [1ã3]. Ordinary Portland Cement (OPC)
has traditionally been used as binder in concrete, due to its low cost and widespread
availability [4]. However, the chemical and thermal combustion processes involved in the
production of OPC results in a significant amount of carbon dioxide (CO2) emitted into
the atmosphere, with the cement production accounting for around 8 % of global CO2
emissions each year [5].
Furthermore, an increase in urbanization has lead to depletion of natural resources, such
as sand. Further exploitation of sand can have a negative effect on natural water systems
and disrupt the habitat of aquatic animals and microorganisms [3]. It is therefore vital to
explore alternative construction materials and unconventional constituents, to reduce the
environmental impact of the building industry.
To reduce the extensive carbon footprint from the building industry, alternatives to conôÙ
ventional concrete and OPC have been investigated in recent years. AlkaliôÙactivated maôÙ
terials, also named geopolymers or inorganic polymers are materials that are produced
when alkaline solutions react with precursors containing aluminosilicate phases [6] which
have attracted attention as sustainable construction materials, due to the possibility of inôÙ
corporating industrial byôÙproducts and wastes as raw materials [6, 7]. Geopolymers can,
like cement, be mixed with aggregates to form geopolymer mortar and concrete which
can be tailored for a given application, depending on the raw material and processing
conditions [6, 8]. Geopolymers have previously been found to exhibit a variety of properôÙ
ties and characteristics, including high compressive strength, fire resistance, low thermal
conductivity, fast or slow setting, low shrinkage and acid resistance [8]. Furthermore,
geopolymers can possess the ability to encapsulate toxic waste in itãs matrix, thus imôÙ
mobilising heavy metals, preventing it from leaching out in the surrounding environment
[9].
The benefits of valorizing industrial byôÙproducts in geopolymers are threefoldëƒ 1. the conôÙ
siderable savings of virgin raw materials results in a substantial reduction of the environôÙ
mental footprint of the construction sector [6], 2. the inclusion of wastes have shown to
improve the geopolymer with regards to strength and durability [6, 9] and 3. the waste
which would otherwise have ended up as landôÙfilling is recycled [7]. Although the reducôÙ
tion of CO2 emissions is perhaps the most important of these benefits, there are many
issues associated with waste disposal, contamination, and land use [7], which is why it is
of great importance to investigate the feasibility of reusing these byôÙproducts for various
possible utilizations.
In an attempt to be the first carbon neutral capital in 2025, the Municipality of Copenhagen
specified a collection of goals and initiatives in the CPH 2025 Climate Plan. In turns, by
2025 all power and heat generation in the Danish capital will primarily be based on wind,
biomass, geothermal energy and waste [10]. The Danish utility company, HOFOR is
already utilizing biomass for energy production. The combustion of biomass inevitably
leads to the production of biomass ash, which is currently underutilized [11].

2.1
Wood Ash and Possibilities for Utilization
Biomass is a term for all organic matter that stem from plants e.g. wood, aglea and
crops [14]. The interest in bioenergy as an alternative to fossil energy has increased
throughout the years, mainly because it is widely accepted, that biofuel combustion does
not contribute to the greenhouse effect, as biomass emits roughly the same amount of
CO2 during conversion as is absorbed during plant growth [14, 15].
When biomass is burned for the production of energy, it produces a great amount of ash
categorised as biomass ash (BMA). If said biomass consists of mainly woody material
(i.e. chips, saw dust, bark) the ash is subcategorised as wood ash (WA).
2.1.1
Production of Wood Fly Ash from Energy Plants
When valorizing biomass for energy production, there are various different combustion
methods. Fluidized bed combustion (FBC) is recognized as the most appropriate techôÙ
nology for biomass combustion, due to the inherent advantages of low processing temôÙ
peratures, isothermal operating conditions and ability to burn a variety of fuels and immeôÙ
diately switch fuels [16]. To improve the heat transfer and reduce temperature gradients,
a sand bed is floating together with fuel on a forced high velocity air flow, inside the FBC
boiler. Reduced temperature gradients ensures a balanced combustion at a relatively
low operating temperature at around 700ôÙ900ãÎC [17]. The principle of FBC is illustrated
in fig. 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Illustration of combustion process using FBC.
During combustion, two main types of ashes are produced: bottom ash (BA) and fly ash
(FA). BAs consists of the total or partially burnt material and the sand from the suspension
bed, and therefore constitute the coarse fraction of the ashes. FAs are the particles sepaôÙ
rated from the stream of gases outside the combustion chamber, and is the finest fraction
of the ashes. FA is separated from the flue gas by specially designed systems (filters)

2.1.2
Possibilities for Utilization
Lamers et al. [11] described various ways to utilize the WA, and the disadvantages of
these. Several of these utilizations have already been implemented in practice: forestry,
fertiliser, addition to compost and landfilling. The disadvantages include:
ãÂ Forestry: The high solubility of WA leading to rapid release of nutrients, can have
an adverse effect on vegetation, making it necessary to pelletize the WA before it is
used in forestry.
ãÂ Fertiliser: The high pH and content of heavy metals limits the application for use of
WA as fertiliser.
ãÂ Addition to compost: Due to stringent requirements in the EU Decree 2003ôÙ2003,
compost regulations can often not be met by biomass ash.
ãÂ Landfilling: May pose a potential health risk, as the fine particles can be rendered
airbourne [19]. Furhtermore, landfilling is associated with leaching of hazardous

2.2
Alkali Activated Materials (Geopolymers)
Alkali activated material is a binder produced when alkaline solutions react with precurôÙ
sors containing aluminosilicate phases and form longôÙrange covalently bonded inorganic
materials [6]. Because the chemical reaction that takes place is a polymerisation, the
material is often referred to as geopolymer, which will be used henceforth in this thesis.
2.2.1
Constituents of Geopolymer Mortar
A commonly used precursor in geopolymers is the amorphous aluminosilicate emanated
by the calcination of kaolin, metakaolin. Metakaolin is a highly reactive pozzolanic maôÙ
terial, whose major oxides generally include SiO2 (40ã70%) and Al2O3 (20ã35%), with
lower contents of Fe2O3, TiO2 (<1%) and other alkaline oxides (Na2O, K2O) [27]. Due to
itãs high rate of dissolution in the reactant solution, the use of metakaolin makes it is easy
to control the Si/Al ratios, and is therefore often preferred as main precursor [28]. HowôÙ
ever, a substitution of metakaolin is sought for, as metakaolin is costly, both economically
and environmentally [27].
A material that contains mostly aluminium and silicon in amorphous form, is a possible
source material for alkaliôÙactivation, which is why industrial byôÙproducts have been invesôÙ
tigated as potential precursors, such as steel slag [29], rice husk ash [30] and ground
granulated blast furnace slag [31].
To activate the precursor, the most common alkaline liquid used, is a combination of NaOH
or KOH and Na2SiO3. NaOH is the most commonly used hydroxide activator in geopolyôÙ
mer synthesis, being both the cheapest and most widely available of the alkali hydroxides
[9]. The composition of the alkali activator has shown to have an impact on the final
strength of the cured geopolymer [32, 33]. The effect of the activator is defined in terms
of the molarity of NaOH and the SiO2/Na2O ratio.
In addition to the binder consisting of the aforementioned precursor and activator, an
important constituent of geopolymer mortar, is the sand. The inert material can have an
effect on the final product, but as 40 billion tonnes of sand are being utilized in construction
materials each year, thus endangering the ecosystem [3], a material to replace sand is
sought for. Once again, industrial byôÙproducts have been investigated for their ability to
replace sand [16].
2.2.2
Reaction Mechanisms and Products
The reaction mechanism related to alkaliôÙactivation of metakaolin or fly ash varies, but the
polymerisation in general can be described, as proposed by Glukhovsky [34] and Palomo
et al. [35]:
1. Dissolution of Al and Si in alkaline environment: OHãions break the SiôÙOôÙSi and SiôÙ
OôÙAl bonds. Dissolved Si and Al form complex species, primarily silica and alumina
monomers.
2. Polycondensation begins: The ionic species favours the contact between the disôÙ
solved procuts. The monomers react to form dimers (identical molecyles joined by
bonds), which reacts with other monomers to form trimers, tetramers and so forth.
3. Once saturation is reached, an aluminosilicate gel precipitates: NôÙAôÙSôÙH gel. The
gel is initially AlôÙrich as AlôÙO bonds are weaker than SiôÙO bonds. As the reaction
progress, more SiôÙO groups from the precursor dissolve, raising the concentration
of Si in the NôÙAôÙSôÙH gel.

2.2.3
Insights on Mixture Proportions
Currently, no standard exist on how to formulate and cast a geopolymer mix design. As
geopolymers can be produced from a wide range of aluminosilicate precursors, with varyôÙ
ing chemical composition, reactivity and availability around the world, geopolymers are far
from a oneôÙsizeôÙfitsôÙall solution, in the likes of Portland cement. However, this diversity
means that that the geopolymer is very versitile and locally adaptable [37]. A potential
solution, to address variability in the chemical composition, is the molar ratios, which can
serve as a nonôÙdimensional index to standardise raw materials.
In addition to the molar ratios, studies have found the molarity of NaOH in the activation
solution to have a great impact on the resulting compressive strength of the geopolymer
[27].
2.2.3.1
NaOH Molarity
NaOH is added to the activator to provide sufficient alkalinity. Various studies have inôÙ
vestigated the effect of varying the molarity of NaOH in the activator solution, ranging
from 6ôÙ18 M, in coal fly ash based geopolymers, where it was generally found, that 12ôÙ
16 M provided the optimum rate for the geopolymerization process, resulting in a higher
compressive strength [32, 33]. Studies concerning metakaolin based geopolymers, used
NaOH molarities ranging between 5ôÙ12 M [24, 38ã41]. The disadvantage of a high moôÙ
larity is that it results in high alkalinity, which requires skilled personnel to work with [42].
Furthermore, a high molarity can result in the production of efflorescence (formation of
white sodium carbonate or bicarbonate crystals) [9].
2.2.3.2
SiO2/Na2O Molar Ratio
Soluble silicate is often added, when activating aluminosilicates, due to the effect it has
on workability, setting and mechanical strength [27]. However, as an alkaline liquid is
essential for alkaliôÙactivation, NaOH is often added to raise the pH value. The pH of the
solution therefore depends on the SiO2/Na2O ratio, often referred to as silica modulus.
Davidovits [43] found that the optimum ratio for Na based activators, was in the range
of 1.7 to 1.9. However, various studies concerning both fly ash and metakaolin based
geopolymers have reported SiO2/Na2O molar ratios ranging from 0.34 to 3.4 [32, 38]. For

2.2.3.3
SiO2/Al2O Molar Ratio
As the composition of the NôÙAôÙSôÙH gel, and consequently the strength of the end product,
is closely related to the silica and alumina content, an important factor in characterising
geopolymers is the SiO2/Al2O3 molar ratio. [44]
The systems mechanical strength is driven by the evolution of highôÙAl gel into highôÙSi
gel. The absence of Al in the mix will retard the conversion between the phase rich in
Al and phase rich in Si, and thus result in a lower mechanical strength. However, an
excess amount of material will speed up the process, resulting in a faster set time, higher
degree of crystallisation and a lower amount of NôÙAôÙSôÙH gel, which will also result in lower
mechanical strength of the end product [27].
Rossi et al. [24] found that an increase in the SiO2/Al2O3 molar ratio, resulted in higher
compressive strength. For mixtures with low SiO2/Al2O3, condensation predominantly
occurs between aluminate and silicate species, forming poly(sialate) polymer structures.
By increasing the SiO2/Al2O3 ratio, condensation occurs between oligomeric silicates (silôÙ
icate species condensed among themselves) and aluminate species, forming a rigid 3D
network of poly(sialateôÙsiloxo) and poly(sialateôÙdisiloxo) polymer structures [8, 45].
Multiple studies, regarding metakaolin based geopolymers found, that the SiO2/Al2O3 was
the most influential parameter, with regards to compressive strength [46, 47]. However,
the optimum reported SiO2/Al2O3 ratio varies between SiO2/Al2O3=0.6ôÙ7.0 for metakaolin
based geopolymers [24, 38, 39, 47, 48].
2.2.3.4
H2O/Na2O Molar Ratio
Water takes part in the dissolution and polycondensation reaction during geopolymersaôÙ
tion, as it provides the medium for dissolution of aluminosilicates, and is therefore an
indispensable part of the mix design. However, water must be added with caution, as
additional water will lower the alkalinity of the activator, and thus lower the geopolymerôÙ
ization rate [49]. To address the balance between alkalinity and water, the H2O/Na2O
molar ratio proofs useful. The ratio for metakaolin based geopolymers is usually in the
range of H2O/Na2O = 10ôÙ20, and usually only includes the water from the NaOH solution.
The workability of the fresh paste can be altered by adding H2O, and studies have shown
that a high H2O/Na2O results in a lower viscosity. Furthermore a high H2O/Na2O ratio
can result in material not undergoing polycondensation, leading to an unset material [44].
2.2.3.5
Na2O/Al2O3 Molar Ratio
The influence of the Na2O/Al2O3 molar ratio affect the microstructure and compressive
strength of geopolymer binders [44]. Various studies have found, that regardless of preôÙ
cursor, the optimum ratio in regards to compressive strength was Na2O/Al2O3=1, due to
the fact that Na+ ions compensate the negative charge of Al3+ [44, 45]. A high Na2O/Al2O3
ratio resulting in excess Na+ ions could weaken the bond strength in sialate networks,
causing deduction of strength in the resulting geopolymer [44].
2.2.4
Thermal Curing
Different curing conditions during the initial period of geopolymerization reaction is found
to be an important factor affecting the rate of formation and quality of the final geopolyôÙ
mer [50]. Elevated curing temperature favours dissolution of silica and alumina species,
resulting in a geopolymer with higher strength. However, the optimum curing temperaôÙ
ture depends on the reactivity of the source material and the NaOH concentration, where
lower optimum temperature is found for high reactivity [51].

2.4
Design of Experiments (DOE)
Experimenting is a tool for investigating the performance of processes and systems, leadôÙ
ing to new knowledge within a certain field of study, and is applied every day, all over the
world.
Design of experiments (DOE) is a method to study multi input variables (factors) and
key output variables (responses), in a structured manner to determine whether a factor,
or a collection of factors, has an effect on the response [54]. Within the field of civil
engineering, DOE is a valuable tool to investigate how individual design parameters can
affect properties of a given material.
In DOE, a certain number of factors which are of interest are deterimned. These factors
are then run on a number of levels, meaning the number of different values a factor can
assume. Both the amount of factors and amount of levels are determined based on either
available time, material, money or all of the above [54].
When experiments are carried out at all possible combination of factors, the design style
is called a full factorial design. For k factors each with n factor levels there are nk unique
combinations of experiments. A full factorial design allows for estimation of main effects
and interactions, which is very useful in providing a straightfoward interpretation of the
effects of each variable, but requires a large number of test points [55].
DOE has previously been used to investigate how different factors such as mixing time,
curing temperature and NaOH concentration would affect properties of geopolymers, inôÙ
cluding compressive strength and leachability of heavy metals [42, 56, 57].
Lin et al. [42] used design of experiments to investigate heavy metal leachability from
coal and wood ash blend geopolymers. Three factors run at two levels, resulting in a 23
experimental setup, were utilized, which resulted in a graph displaying which parameters
(or combined effect of parameters) had the biggest effect on the measured result. All of
these studies had clearly defined boundaries, levels and targets.

Mixing Procedure
The needed NaOH solutions were produced by mixing pure NaOH pellets with water,
to obtain the solution of the required concentrations, and left overnight. The activator
was produced by mixing the given NaOH solution with comercially produced waterglass,
Na2SiO3. The aqueous NaOH and Na2SiO3 solution was stirred for 3 minutes with a
spoon. Afterwards, the MK was added gradually while stirring, to obtain a homogeneous
binder. The WFA was then added gradually while thoroughly mixing by hand, minimising
the formation of lumps. Once all WFA was added, the 0/2 mm sand was added. Lastly,
additional water was added, if needed, 3 ml at a time, to achieve a kneadable mix. After
mixing, the mortar was knead into 3.5 û 3.5 û 3.5 cm silicone moulds by hand. Figure
3.3 shows the procedure for casting sand replacement geopolymer mortar.
The samples were subsequently cured for 24 hours at ambient temperature or 80 ãÎC,
before demoulding, and were afterwards kept in a closed container, stored in a climate
chamber (20ãÎC, 85 % RH) for 13 days, resulting in a total curing time of 14 days prior to
testing.

Results and Discussion
In the following sections, the results of the experimental tests are presented and disôÙ

4.1
Characterisation of Materials
The first section concerns the characterisation of the WFA, MK and 0/2 mm sand, with
primary focus on the WFA. WFA is not a standardised material for use in geopolymers.
No guidelines or regulations on whether a particular WFA can be used as a replacemenôÙ
t/supplement for either sand or metakaolin exists. In turns, the results in the following
section will be discussed based on the requirements for the use of coal fly ash (CFA)
in geopolymers, stated in GarciaôÙLodeiro et al. [53], as well as findings in other studies

4.1.8
Summary of WFA Characterisations
It is evident from these analyses, that the WFA in general doesnãt resemble neither the
0/2 mm sand nor MK.
Studies regarding WA or BMA as sand supplement or replacement mainly utilizes the botôÙ
tom ash fraction of the ash (sieved to particle size distribution of the commercial aggregate
normally used in mortar formulations) [16], due to the similarity in particle size distribution,
high content of sand from the suspension bed (when using fluidised bed combustion) and
low content of hazardous metals. However, bottom ash and fly ash have entirely different
physical and chemical properties. The finer particles, higher reactivity and higher water
demand which is related to fly ashes may prevent the fly ash from being suitable as sand
substitute or replacement.
Comparing the the analyses results with the CFA requirements stated in [53], only 3 of
the 7 requirements were fulfilled. However, the composition of WFA differs significantly
from that of CFA, especially in regards to CaO content, and is therefore a not the most
accurate tool in estimating whether a highôÙcalcium ash will be suitable in geopolymer. This
emphasises the novelty of geopolymer, and the lack of standardisation. The literature
regarding WFA and BMFA in geopolymers showed promising results, even though very
few of the requirements were met (table 2.2).
The negative water content suggested that the WFA was self hardening. A majority of the
studies concerning WFA (presented in table 2.1) studied the feasibility of utilizing WFA
as partial cement replacement. The studies found that the WFAs to some extent conôÙ
tained either pozzolanic (ability to react with calcium hydroxide in moist environment) or
hydraulic (ability to set, harden and retain strength when mixed with water) properties.
A pozzolanic material is defined by a high content of SiO2, Fe2O3 and Al2O3 amounting
to more than 70% of the total oxides, whereas a hydraulic material is mostly controlled
by the ratio of CaO/SiO2. Table 4.5 shows pozzolanic activity and hydraulic index for the
WFA (assuming everything reacts), compared to the limits defined by DS/EN 197ôÙ1:2012.
As seen, none of the requirements are met, but is close in regards to hydraulic index. As
previously discussed, the reactivity of a certain oxide is difficult to measure, but the reacôÙ
tive SiO2 is expected to be lowered, which in turns, could results in the WFA upholding the
requirement for a hydraulic material. However, whether a material conatins pozzzolanic
or hydraulic properties cannot solely be based off of the pozzolanic activity and hydraulic
index measurement, as i.e. sand would conform to the pozzolanic activity although being
completely inert.

4.5.1
SiO2/Na2O Ratio and NaOH Molarity
Figure 4.21 shows the relation between compressive strength and the SiO2/Na2O ratio
(silica ratio) for both ambient and 80ãÎC cured samples. As seen, for both curing condiôÙ
tions, the highest strengths were found for the lower SiO2/Na2O ratio at around 1.4. This
indicates that higher alkalinity in the activator results in a higher compressive strength.
Rossi et al. [38] investigated metakaolin based geopolymers with the addition of BMA,
where a silica ratio of 0.34 was used, which achieved a compressive strength of around
10 MPa. For fly ash based geopolymers, Reddy et al. [32] found an optimum silica ratio
at 2.0, which resulted in a compressive strength of 31 MPa, using a 16M NaOH.
It is difficult to compare these studies, as both chemical composition, mix design, curing
conditions and curing time differed between each study. It is, however, evident for the
mix design in the current study that of the two levels of silica modulus, the lower modulus
favours higher compressive strength. Juengsuwattananon et al. [44] found that a lower
silica ratio was related to the formation of sodium bicarbonate resulting in lower strength.
Furthermore, a low silica modulus is related to a higher alkalinity of the activator, making
it more hazadours to work with [8, 42].
Reddy et al. [32] suggests, that the compressive strength is affected both by silica modulus
as well as the molarity of the NaOH in the activator. The study found an optimum at 16M
NaOH, which is quite higher than the molarities used in the current study (2.6M, 4.2M,
4.3M and 7.0M). The highest compressive strength in this study was found for mix MSôÙ
WFAôÙ6ôÙ30ôÙA, for which a molarity of 7.0M was used. This could indicate, that using a
higher molarity of NaOH could increase the compressive strength, howbeit this could also
increase the risk of efflorescence.

4.5.5
Main Effects of Ratios
As previously mentioned, it is difficult to asses the relation between the molar ratios and
the compressive strengths, as the strength of the geopolymer can be attributed to many
things: the oneôÙway interaction of one factor, twoôÙway interactions between many factors,
the casting of the geopolymer, the curing condition and so forth.
Furthermore, the ratios are based on the assumption, that all the oxides in the starting
material (i.e. the MK and WFA) were reactive, although that was likely not the case.
In reality, it is difficult to measure the reactivity of a given component. This was also
addressed by Juengsuwattananon et al. [44] where it was found, that only highly reactive
starting materials could provide the final geopolymeric structure with the same molar ratios
as the initial starting mix.
To provide an overview of the influence on the compressive strength for each ratio, the
magnitude of effects plots for one and twoôÙway interactions were generated using the JMP
software, for both ambient and 80ãÎC cured samples. The oneôÙway interaction plots are
seen in figure 4.26.
For ambient cured samples, the plot indicates that the SiO2/Na2O ratio had the largest inôÙ
fluence on the compressive strength, and the Na2O/Al2O3 ratio had the smallest influence.

4.7
WFA as a Hydraulic Binder
The various test results showed that for the geopolymers containing WFA, curing at 80 ãÎC
had a negative effect on the compressive strength. Moreover, the heavy metal leaching
of the geopolymers could not be measured due to high amounts of unreacted Na.
All of this suggests that a reaction mechanism, other than geopolymerization occurred
for the WFA in the samples. As discussed in section 4.1.8 the WFA likely possessed
hydraulic properties, which could be an explanation for the reaction mechanism which
occurred in the samples. Furhtermore, the negative water content of the WFA found by
the characteristic analyses suggests that the WFA was selfôÙhardening (i.e. hydraulic). A
hydraulic binder, is a substance that chemically react with water at ambient temperature,
by converting the waterôÙbinder system into a solid matrix, which is stable in water [68].
Portland cement is a wellôÙknown hydraulic binder.
Sigvardsen [65] found, that a similar WFA displayed properties of a hydraulic binder. To
test the ability of 100% wood ash (WA) mortar to develop strength, 100% WA mortar samôÙ
ples were cast with a water/binder (w/b) ratio of 0.75. The high w/b ratio was utilized for
both the WA and Portland cement samples (REF) in order to maintain workability without
using superplasticisers. The development of the compressive strength is seen in figure
4.29. As seen, a rapid increase in compressive strength was reported for 100% WA2
increasing from 10% of the compressive strength reported for REF after 7 days to 85%
after 90 days, however both WAs showed hydraulic properties.

4.8
Working with Design of Experiments
Previous studies using design of experiments (DOE) found that a factorial design was
efficient when studying the effect of two or more factors, and was more efficient than
studying one factor at a time, which could lead to misleading conclusions [42, 56, 57].
In this study, the mixture design matrix containing the 8 mix designs was based on a
factorial design setup. The matrix was based on an initial wish to replace the aggregate
part of the geopolymer mortar with wood ash, and in turns, based on the assumption that
the WFA was inert. In the trial run and SR test series it was found that the WFA was
reactive and unsuitable for the replacement of sand.
This revelation compromised the 24 factorial experiments. As factor A concerned the
amount of sand replacement, this factor was removed in the following test series. FurôÙ
thermore, factor B,C and D concerned the initial molar ratios, which had to be altered
due to the contribution from the reactive WFA. Instead of two levels, these factors were
presented as a range, which was also seen when discussing the effect of the molar ratios
in section 4.5.
This emphasises that when working with DOE and factorial designs, the experimental
setup has to be planned meticulously, with well defined and well tested boundaries, esôÙ
pecially when working with a unknown material and/or constituents.
The various advantages and disadvantages of working with DOE, and factorial designs
in particular are:
Advantages
ãÂ Provides a structured experimental plan.
ãÂ More efficient than one factor at a time testing.
ãÂ Possible to find the effect of every factor (including secondary factors) and their
interactions.
Disadvantages
ãÂ Requires wellôÙdefined boundaries.
ãÂ Material behaviour has to be known in order to carry out the experiment without
deviations from the initial design.
ãÂ Difficult to interpret the results when using more than two factors.
ãÂ Difficult to interpret the results when reaction mechanism is not certain.

Conclusion
Based on 8 different mix designs, four test series utilizing wood fly ash as either sand
replacement, metakaolin supplement or for metakaolin reduction were carried out. A total
of 238 samples were cast and cured at either ambient or elevated (24 hours at 80 ãÎC)
temperatures for a total curing time of 14 days. Additionally, investigative analyses of the
characteristics of the raw WFA were carried out.
From the experimental work conducted in this study, the following can be concluded:
ãÂ The characteristic analyses of the WFA revealed small particles, albeit larger than
metakaolin. The WFA was alkaline and contained oxides in order of decreasing
value CaO < SiO2 < SO3 < K2O < Al2O3 < MgO. The WFA contained high amounts
of soluble salts, and was found to have a high concentration of Cr which might
be problematic when utilizing the ash in building materials. Furthermore, the WFA
displayed self hardening properties.
ãÂ The characteristic results found that the WFA did not meet the majority of the reôÙ
quirements for a suitable coal fly ash in geopolymers, defined in [53], which was
also found for other biomass ashes in the literature, successfully used in geopolyôÙ
mers. This stresses the issue in using standards for coal fly ash when assessing
new types of mineral additions.
ãÂ The particle size and grain morphology varied greatly from 0/2 mm sand and conôÙ
trary to sand, the WFA was found to be reactive in the alkaline activator solution.
The WFA is therefore an unsuitable replacement for sand in geopolymers.
ãÂ The compressive strength of the metakaolin based geopolymers increased with the
addition of WFA, compared to reference samples. Insufficient amounts of metakaolin
was found to have a detrimental effect on the geopolymer which was likely caused
by a low degree of geopolymeric gel formation.
ãÂ Thermal curing resulted in higher strengths for reference samples, but decreased
the strength in samples containing WFA, which suggested that another reaction ocôÙ
curred for the WFA
ãÂ The highest compressive strength of 22.8 MPa was reached when utilizing WFA
as 30% wt. metakaolin supplement (MSôÙWFAôÙ6ôÙ30ôÙA), cured at ambient temperaôÙ
tures. The molar ratios for this sample were SiO2/Na2O = 1.4, Na2O/Al2O3 = 0.6,
H2O/Na2O = 8 and SiO2/Al2O3 = 2.4.
ãÂ The mixture design which provided the highest compressive strength regardless of
specimen type is mix 6.
Although the inclusion of WFA resulted in increased compressive strengths, it is conôÙ
cluded, that it was not likely that the WFA underwent geopolymerization, and is therefore
not suitable in geopolymers. Based on the obtained results, it is concluded that the WFA
could show potential when utilized in building materials, although a further investigation
is needed to determine the reaction mechanism related to the WFA.
