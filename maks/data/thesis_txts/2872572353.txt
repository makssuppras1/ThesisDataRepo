Abstract
This thesis presents the design and characterization of ionic liquid grafted (IL)­g silicones as ad­
vanced soft materials for dielectric elastomer actuator (DEA) applications. DEAs are lightweight,
compliant actuators that convert electrical energy into mechanical motion, offering great poten­
tial for soft robotics, biomedical devices, and next generation human­machine interfaces. Silicone
elastomers are widely used in DEAs due to their softness, thermal stability, and processability,
but their inherently low dielectric permittivity necessitates high operating voltages, limiting effi­
ciency and device integration. To address this drawback, this work explores covalent incorporation
of polar ionic liquid moieties into the silicone backbone to enhance dielectric properties without
compromising mechanical performance.
A series of imidazolium (Im)­functionalized silicone polymers were synthesized via anion ex­
change, introducing TFSI−, DCA−, TFS−, and BF−
4 anions to tune thermal and dielectric behav­
ior. Structural integrity was confirmed by NMR and FTIR spectroscopy. Thermal analysis re­
vealed that glass transition temperature and thermal stability strongly depended on anion identity,
influencing chain mobility and ionic interactions. Broadband dielectric spectroscopy identified
[Im][TFSI]­g silicone as the most promising material for DEAs, exhibiting high permittivity and
low dielectric losses. Incorporation of this IL­grafted polymer into a chemically crosslinked PDMS
network yielded soft elastomer films with enhanced dielectric constant (𝜀′ ∼9), excellent stretch­
ability (𝑠b > 300%), low Young’s modulus (𝑌∼0.4 MPa), and high electrical breakdown strength
(𝐸BD up to 63.6 V/µm) relative to other high permittivity elastomers.
The results demonstrate that covalent integration of IL moieties offers a robust strategy to develop
high performance dielectric elastomers for the next generation of actuator technologies.

Background
1.1
Artificial Muscles (Actuators)
Artificial muscles are synthetic actuators designed to replicate the functionality and motion of
natural muscles. Natural muscles generate movement through contraction and relaxation of mus­
cle fibers powered by biochemical energy, producing linear motion, adaptable force, and effi­
cient, responsive actuation. Artificial muscles aim to emulate these capabilities, offering flexi­
ble, lightweight, and energy efficient actuation solutions for applications in robotics, prosthetics,
biomedical devices, and wearable technologies. [1]
In contrast to traditional mechanical actuators, artificial muscles offer enhanced degrees of free­
dom, adaptability, and biocompatibility. These attributes make them particularly well suited for
human­machine interfaces and soft robotic systems, where safe and responsive interaction with
humans is essential. As such, artificial muscles are ideal for applications that seek to emulate
or augment human muscular function, especially in biologically inspired robotics and biomedical
engineering. [2]
The range of applications for artificial muscles is both diverse and impactful. One prominent exam­
ple is the soft robotic glove developed by the Wyss Institute for Biologically Inspired Engineering
at Harvard University (fig. 1.1). This glove integrates soft pneumatic actuators that mimic the
motion of human fingers, assisting individuals with limited hand mobility due to conditions such
as stroke or muscular dystrophy. The lightweight, flexible design allows for natural movement,
enabling users to perform daily tasks more independently while undergoing rehabilitation. [3]
Figure 1.1: Soft Robotic Glove for Neuromuscular Rehabilitation [3].
1.2
Dielectric Elastomer Actuators
Dielectric elastomer actuators (DEAs) have emerged as one of the most promising technologies
for artificial muscles, offering a unique combination of high energy density, large strain capability,
and mechanical flexibility. [4] In contrast to traditional actuators such as motors or pneumatic sys­
tems, DEAs more closely mimic the performance of natural muscle tissue in terms of compliance,
responsiveness, and adaptability.
The fundamental mechanism of DEAs involves converting electrostatic energy into mechanical
deformation. A DEA typically comprises a thin elastomeric film placed between two compliant
electrodes (fig. 1.2).
When a voltage is applied, opposite charges accumulate on the electrodes, creating an electric field
across the dielectric layer. This field induces an electrostatic pressure, known as Maxwell pressure,
described by the equation:

1.3
Silicone Elastomers
Silicone elastomers, particularly those based on polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS), are widely used in
DEAs due to their highly flexible and thermally stable molecular structure. The PDMS backbone
consists of Si­O bonds, which are longer and more flexible than C­C or C­O bonds (fig. 1.3).
Although the Si𝜎+­O𝜎−bond is strongly polarized, the non­polar methyl side groups shield these
dipoles and suppress intermolecular interactions [6]. This results in high chain mobility, large free
volume, and an extremely low glass transition temperature (𝑇𝑔≈−127 °C).
Figure 1.3: Bond angles and lengths of carbon (top) and silicon (below) based polymers.
Silicone elastomers are formed typically by cross­linking PDMS chains via condensation or addi­
tion reactions (fig. 1.4). Their flexible networks allow for large, reversible deformations (above
300%) with minimal energy loss, critical for DEA function [7]. Compared to acrylic and polyurethane
elastomers, silicones exhibit faster mechanical response, and reduced mechanical hysteresis, mak­
ing them also ideal for high frequency or long life actuation [5].
Figure 1.4: Crosslinking chemistries for silicone elastomers. (a) Pt catalyzed addition reaction.
(b) Sn catalyzed condensation reaction.
The high bond dissociation energy of Si­O (452 kJ/mol) gives silicones excellent thermal and ox­

idative stability [6]. Combined with their low 𝑇𝑔, they retain mechanical and dielectric performance
across a wide range of operating temperatures (−100 °C to 250 °C) [5]. Silicones also show strong
resistance to UV, moisture, and aging, and can sustain millions of actuation cycles with minimal
degradation [8].
While the Si­O backbone introduces dipoles, the symmetrical, non­polar methyl groups limit dipole
interaction and result in a lowered dielectric permittivity (𝜀r ≈2−3) [5]. This is significantly lower
than that of pure acrylics (𝜀r ≈5) or polyurethane elastomers (𝜀r ≈5−7) and represents one of the
main limitations for DEA applications [9, 10]. As discussed previously, actuation strain depends
on 𝜀r and the square of the electric field. Consequently, silicones require high operating voltages
(typically over 3 kV for thin films), which poses challenges for low­power or compact devices [11,
12].
In conclusion, the Si­O­based network gives silicone elastomers excellent softness, thermal range,
and operational stability, making them well­suited for DEA use. However, their inherently low
permittivity limits their actuation efficiency, prompting ongoing research into dielectric enhance­
ment strategies.
1.4
High Permittivity Fillers
Incorporation of high permittivity fillers has been one of the most studied approaches to improve
the dielectric properties of silicone elastomers. Ceramic nanoparticles such as titanium dioxide
(TiO2) [13, 14], barium titanate (BaTiO3) [15], and other metal oxides [16], are commonly used
due to their dielectric constants (𝜀r ≈6000 for BaTiO3) being several orders of magnitude higher
than that of the elastomer matrix. Their effect is further amplified by interfacial (Maxwell­Wagner)
polarization arising from the introduced inhomogeneities [16, 17].
However, the stark contrast in mechanical and chemical properties between the fillers and the
matrix presents major drawbacks. High filler loading increases stiffness, raises the Young’s mod­
ulus and compromising the elasticity. Interfaces between filler and matrix also act as defect sites,
promoting premature breakdown and lowering fracture strain. Moreover, poor interfacial compat­
ibility can lead to aging effects, as inorganic fillers tend to migrate and agglomerate within the
organic matrix over time. All these are unwanted for DEA applications where large and reversible
deformation is required. [18]
The study by Namitha et al. is a representative example of ceramic filler incorporation into silicone
elastomers [19]. They investigated composites prepared by dispersing BaTiO3 (BT), SrTiO3(ST),
and Ca(1−x)Nd(2x/3)TiO3 (CNT) into silicone rubber (SR). With increasing filler volume fraction
(up to ~43 % for CNT), they observed a clear increase in dielectric permittivity, achieving rel­
ative permittivities of 13.4 (BT), 14.2 (ST), and 11.9 (CNT). Despite these promising dielectric
enhancements, all composite systems exhibited substantial increases in stiffness (from ~1 MPa to
~3 MPa) and pronounced reductions in elongation at break (from > 400% to < 100%), as evidenced
by their stress­strain behavior (fig. 1.5). While scanning electron microscopy confirmed uniform
filler dispersion at low concentrations, agglomeration became evident at higher loadings.

1.5
Ionic Liquids
Compared to rigid ceramic fillers, liquid additives offer a more effective route to enhance the di­
electric performance of silicone elastomers while maintaining or even improving their mechanical
properties for DEA applications. Liquids are more compatible with soft elastomers and can act as
lubricants or plasticizers, reducing stiffness and enabling larger strains under the same Maxwell
pressure. This bioinspired approach mirrors natural tissues like muscle, which consist of solid
matrices (myofibrils) embedded in liquid phases (intermolecular fluid) [20].
Ionic liquids (IL) have emerged as promising liquid fillers due to their favorable physical and
electrical properties. They are salts that remain liquid at or near room temperature, composed
entirely of ions—typically a bulky organic cation (e.g. imidazolium or pyrrolidinium) and a weakly
coordinating anion such as bis(trifluoromethylsulfonyl)imide (TFSI−) (fig. 1.6).
Figure 1.6: Common cations (top) and anions (bottom) of ionic liquids.
ILs exhibit permanent dipole moments and can be readily polarized due to their ionic character and
low symmetry in the ions molecular structure. This makes ILs share dielectric features of dipolar

==PAGE:20==
solvents and simple molten salts. Their dielectric constants typically range from 12 to 35 depending
on the ion pair [21]. Although this permittivity is lower when compared to ceramic fillers, the ILs’
superior compatibility with soft silicone matrices facilitates more effective polarization and higher
filler loadings, enabling composites to achieve dielectric properties comparable to ceramic­filled
systems. Another unique property of ILs is their negligible vapor pressure, arising from strong
Coulombic interactions and bulky, asymmetric structures. This imparts superior long­term stability
compared to conventional liquid fillers like water or glycerol. In addition, ILs offer exceptional
tunability. With a nearly infinite possible ion pair combinations, their properties can be precisely
tailored [22]. For example, ILs can achieve thermal stability exceeding 400 °C, and viscosities
ranging from less than 0.5 to over 100 Pa ⋅s [23]. Hydrophilicity and other attributes can also
be finely adjusted through cation­anion selection. These tunable characteristics, combined with
other properties mentioned above have made ILs highly attractive for use in energy storage, drug
delivery, and increasingly, in polymeric materials [24, 25].
Several studies have demonstrated the effectiveness of incorporating ILs into silicone elastomers
to enhance dielectric and mechanical performance. Ankit et al. developed a self­enclosed IL­
elastomer composite using [EMIm] [TFSI]. With 20 vol% filler loading, the dielectric constant
nearly doubled (from 3.7 to 7 at 1 kHz), and Young’s modulus dropped markedly (from 1 to
0.01 MPa), resulting in a highly stretchable material (elongation up to 435%). A reduced break­
down strength of 15 V/µm was observed, consistent with trends in high IL­content systems. [20]
In another study, Liu et al. reported that blending butyl­3­methylimidazolium hexafluoroanti­
monate ([BMIm] [SbF6]) into a PDMS­based elastomer increased the dielectric permittivity from
2.3 to 7.6 at 0.1 Hz—more than a threefold enhancement. At a loading of 90 parts per hundred rub­
ber, the composite also exhibited a substantial reduction in Young’s modulus (from 0.49 to 0.15
MPa), indicating improved actuation capability. However, this enhancement came at the cost of
a sharp decline in breakdown strength, dropping from 55 to 7.5 V/µm, suggesting a trade­off in
operating voltage due to increased risk of dielectric failure. [26]
Shi et al. further advanced the concept by incorporating liquid electrolyte: propyl­methyl­imidazolium
bis(trifluoromethanesulfonyl)imide ([PrIm] [TFSI]) as dispersed liquid inclusions within silicone
matrices. At 40 vol% loading, the dielectric constant reached values as high as 20, while main­
taining excellent mechanical softness and thermal stability across a broad temperature range (−20
to 60 °C). These composites also showed promising performance in functional applications such
as strain sensors and electroluminescent devices. [27]
As discussed in the silicone elastomer section, PDMS polymers are inherently non­polar and char­
acterized by weak intermolecular forces—fundamentally different from the polar, ionic nature of
ILs. This polarity mismatch hinders the formation of strong interactions between ILs and the sili­
cone matrix, leading to several problems.
First, unbound or poorly integrated ILs, which are inherently ionically conductive, can migrate
under high electric fields, forming conductive pathways that increase dielectric losses, thereby
reducing energy conversion efficiency. At elevated loadings, ILs may also agglomerate or phase­
separate from the matrix, creating inhomogeneities that compromise dielectric breakdown strength,
as evidenced by the aforementioned studies. This imposes a trade­off between enhanced permit­
tivity and operating voltage, ultimately limiting the actuation strain achievable. Over time, unan­
chored ILs can gradually migrate to the surface under mechanical or electrical stress, resulting
in leakage, performance degradation, electrode delamination, and possible contamination of sur­
rounding components, seriously damaging the DEA devices.

1.6
Grafting of Ionic Liquids
To overcome the limitations of physically blended ILs, chemical grafting offers a more robust and
integrated approach. Covalently attaching IL moieties to the silicone backbone incorporates them
as a stable component of the polymer network, enhancing dielectric properties while maintaining
mechanical integrity and eliminating issues related to phase separation or IL migration.
Although chemical modification are considered more complex and labor intensive than physi­
cal blending, advancements in synthetic techniques and the availability of functionalized silicone
precursors, such as those commercialized by Gelest, have made tailored silicone polymers more
accessible [28].
Common grafting strategies include covalent linkage of ionic side chains, such as imidazolium
groups, via thiol­ene click chemistry or nucleophilic substitution. Several studies have been done
on synthesizing and characterizing IL grafted silicones.
For example, Reiter et al. synthesized a series of imidazolium­grafted silicones ([Im]­g silicone)
(fig. 1.7) with tunable glass transition temperatures, achieved by varying the anions. Their op­
timized formulation reached a 𝑇𝑔as low as −73 °C and demonstrated ionic conductivities up to
4.8 × 10−5 S/cm at 300 K. A low 𝑇𝑔is beneficial for ionic conductivity because it enhances seg­
mental mobility, facilitating ion transport through the polymer matrix. While the study effectively
demonstrated the molecular design strategy for reducing 𝑇𝑔and boosting conductivity, it did not
assess the dielectric performance of the PILs. [29]

Studies by Kang et al. offered a broader investigation into IL­functionalized silicones for enhanced
dielectric properties. In one case, IL­grafted silicone oils were synthesized by nucleophilic substi­
tution of chloropropyl functionalized silicone with 1­ethylimidazole. The grafted PDMS reached
high dielectric permittivity (up to 𝜀r ≈22 at 0.1 Hz). The polymer was incorporated into silicone
elastomers to fabricate pressure sensors rather than actuators, as this approach is more suitable
when low dielectric breakdown strength and high conductivity are present. [30]
In another study by Kang et al., a bis­imidazolium­based ionic liquid was employed as a crosslinker
for imidazole­functionalized PDMS (fig. 1.8), producing dielectric elastomers with enhanced me­
chanical (Young’s modulus ≈0.04 MPa) and dielectric properties (𝜀r = 7.5 at 0.1 Hz). However,
for such a system, increasing IL content means increased crosslink density and stiffness. Hence, the
best actuation strain was achieved at the lowest IL content, with the breakdown strength remaining
low (15 V/µm). [31]

3.1.2
Synthesis of IL­g silicone with varied anions
Precursor Purification
The [Im][Br]­g silicone was dissolved in deionized (DI) water and washed with pentane three times.
The bulk water was then removed by distillation under reduced pressure, followed by complete
drying in a vacuum oven at 120 °C for 24 hours.
Metathesis Reactions
In a typical procedure, 1.0 equivalent of [Im][Br]­g silicone was dissolved in deionized (DI) water.
While stirring, a slight excess (1.5 equivalents) of the desired sodium (Na) or lithium (Li) salt was
added in one portion. The polymer product precipitated immediately, and the mixture was stirred
at 40 °C for 12 hours. The aqueous phase was then decanted, and the precipitate was collected,
redissolved in ethyl acetate (EtOAc), and washed with DI water three times. The organic layer was
then separated and concentrated by rotary evaporation. The final product was obtained by drying
under vacuum at 120 °C for 24 hours.

3.2.1
Structural Analysis
This section verifies the successful synthesis and purity of the ionic liquid­grafted silicones through
detailed structural characterization. 1H and 13C NMR spectroscopy, along with FT­IR analysis, are
employed to confirm the chemical structure of the precursor and to detect changes resulting from
anion exchange. These techniques allow us to ensure that the grafted imidazolium moieties are
intact, the polymer backbone is preserved, and that the anion metathesis reactions have proceeded
cleanly without introducing unwanted side products. This structural confirmation is critical before
evaluating thermal or dielectric properties, as compositional accuracy directly impacts material
performance.
[Im][Br]­g silicone Precursor
The 1H NMR spectrum of the precursor [Im][Br]­g silicone (fig. 3.1) shows all expected proton
signals corresponding to the grafted side chains and imidazolium moiety. Multiple resonances
appear in the aliphatic region (0.5–4.5 ppm), confirming the presence of the alkyl linker and side
chain: specifically, peaks 1–3 and 11–13 are assigned to methylene and terminal protons along
the grafted alkyl chain. Resonances at 2.54 and 2.98 ppm (peaks 4 and 5) are shifted downfield,
consistent with methylene groups adjacent to a sulfur atom, indicating the presence of thio­ether
linkage. Additional downfield­shifted resonances at 4.21 and 4.35 ppm (peaks 6 and 10) corre­
spond to protons adjacent to the nitrogen atoms of the imidazolium ring, reflecting deshielding
due to their electronic environment.
The aromatic region (7.75–9.25 ppm) contains three distinct signals: peaks 7 to 9 corresponding
to the imidazolium ring protons, with the most downfield signal (peak 9 at 9.25 ppm) indicating
the proton affected by the ring’s positive charge and counter­ion interaction. Small resonances at
2.08 ppm (a), 3.15 ppm (m), and 3.33 ppm (w) are attributed to residual acetone, methanol, and
water, respectively [33]. These impurities originate from the synthesis process.

prominent when hydrophobic, dielectric polymers such as silicone are used for network formation

conductivity in the free polymer state, [Im][TFSI]­g silicone remains the most suitable candidate

3.3
Conclusion
The successful synthesis of four anion­exchanged PILs was confirmed through structural charac­
terization using NMR and FT­IR spectroscopy. These analyses verified that the anion exchange
proceeded cleanly, without detectable side products and preserved the integrity of the imidazolium­
functionalized silicone backbone.
Thermal analysis confirmed that all PILs possess high thermal stability, with decomposition tem­
peratures exceeding 200 °C. The glass transition temerature were tunable via anion selection, with
TFSI−and TFS−yielding the lowest 𝑇g values (−39 °C and −21 °C respectively) due to the plas­
ticizing effect of their bulky, weakly coordinating structures. The low 𝑇g of [Im][TFSI]­g silicone
ensures it remains liquid at room temperature, enabling solvent­free processing, straightforward
film fabrication, and stable material properties. Its enhanced chain dynamics also contribute to
favorable dielectric behavior.
Broadband dielectric spectroscopy demonstrated that anion identity critically influences both di­
electric permittivity and ionic conductivity. [Im][TFSI]­g silicone exhibited the most promising
dielectric profile for actuator applications, combining high real permittivity with low loss tangent
in the relevant frequency range. Temperature dependent BDS confirmed that the high 𝜀′ arises
from thermally activated polarization mechanisms, which are significantly diminished below 𝑇g.
In contrast, [Im][DCA]­g silicone showed the highest ionic conductivity, indicating its potential
for applications in soft sensors where rapid ionic response is beneficial.
The findings of this chapter provide a clear rationale for selecting [Im][TFSI]­g silicone as the
lead candidate for further development and crosslinking into functional elastomeric materials for
high­performance DEAs.

4.1.2
Preparation of Elastomers
The elastomer system (fig. 4.1) is based on a two­component silicone network.
Figure 4.1: Chemical structures of the reagents used (left) and schematic illustration of the resulting
crosslinked elastomer network (right).
A long chain silanol­terminated PDMS (AB116663, 𝑀.𝑊. ∼77000 g/mol) served as the primary
backbone, providing flexibility and processability. The short­chain IL­grafted silicone ([Im][TFSI]­
g silicone, synthesized in the previous chapter, with a degree of polymerization: 30­50 ) was in­
corporated to enhance the dielectric properties. Direct cross­linking of the [Im][TFSI]­g silicone
with itself led to a brittle crystalline material due to limited chain flexibility orginating from its
bulky side group and short chain length. Instead, it was co­crosslinked with the long chain silanol
via a tin­catalyzed condensation reaction between hydroxyl and methoxy groups, forming a chem­
ically bonded 3D network. Hydrosilylation chemistry, a common alternative crosslinking route,
was deliberately avoided because the anion can poison the platinum catalysts typically used in
such systems [31]. Instead, a condensation curing approach was adopted using a methoxy func­
tional crosslinker (SIB1834.5, structure shown in fig. 4.1) bearing R­O­Si functional groups. This
Ionic Liquid Grafted Silicones for Dielectric Elastomer Actuators
27
==PAGE:42==
crosslinker features six reactive sites, a flexible backbone, and small terminal groups, increasing
its likelihood of reacting with the silanol terminated PDMS chains. This is particularly important
because the bulky ionic side chains on the IL­grafted PDMS can sterically hinder access to the
chain ends. Furthermore, the crosslinker contains ester and asymmetric ether groups, imparting
slight polarity that promotes better miscibility with the IL­grafted component, ensuring a more
homogeneous cure, hence a better material with less defects. To further enhance the mechanical
integrity of the elastomer, fumed silica filler (silazane treated) was introduced. This reinforcement
significantly enhanced the stretchability and durability of the resulting elastomer film.
In a typical formulation, [Im][TFSI]­g silicone (37.5 wt.%, 41.2 wt.%, or 44.4 wt.%) and fumed
silica (5 wt.%) were added to AB11663 (5.00 g) and premixed using a SpeedMixer (FlackTek DAC
150.1 FVZ­K) at 3500 rpm for 2 minutes. The silane crosslinker SIB1834.5 was then introduced
in excess at a stoichiometric ratio of 𝑅= 𝑛OMe/𝑛OH = 1.15, followed by an additional 1 minute
mixing at the same speed. If the viscosity­induced shear heating caused a noticeable temperature
rise, the mixture was cooled at 4°C for 5 minutes. Finally, 0.5 wt.% tin catalyst was added and the
mixture was homogenized at 3500 rpm for 30 seconds.
The resulting uniform mixture was blade cast onto a polyethylene terephthalate (PET) substrate
coated with a sacrificial layer to produce thin films with a thickness of approximately 50 µm. The
sacrificial layer, prepared according to Rosset [42], is water soluble, enabling gentle release of the
cured film by submersion in water and avoiding mechanical damage. A separate portion of the
mixture was poured into a metal mold (30 × 20 × 1 mm, width × length × thickness) fixed to a
polytetrafluoroethylene (PTFE) substrate to prepare thick films. All samples were cured overnight
at 80 °C under 20% air flow.
4.1.3
Characterization
Tensile Tests
Tensile stress­strain behavior of the silicone materials was characterized at ambient conditions
using a universal testing machine (Instron 3340, INSTRON, US). Thick films (𝑑∼1 mm) were cut
into dog bone specimens with a gauge length of 30 mm and mounted between two clamps with an
initial separation of approximately 25 mm. Uniaxial extension was performed at a constant rate of
500 mm/min. Young’s modulus 𝑌was determined from the slope of the stress­strain curve within
the 1­10 % strain range. For each sample, three independent measurements were conducted, and
the average values of Young’s modulus, tensile strength, and elongation at break were reported.
Microscopic Thickness Measurement
The thickness of the thin films (𝑑<100 µm) was measured using an optical microscope (VHX,
KEYENCE, US). Three regions of each film were cut into small rectangular pieces and held ver­
tically such that the cross sectional edge faced the microscope. Digital images were captured, and
the film thickness was measured directly from the images using VHX software.
BDS
Synthesized thick films were cut into circular samples with a diameter of 20 mm and subjected
to BDS measurements at room temperature. An AC voltage of 𝑉rms =1 V was applied across the
dielectric samples with a thickness around 1 mm. Measurements were conducted over a frequency
range of 10−1 to 106 Hz.
Electrical Breakdown Strength (𝐸BD)
The 𝐸BD of the samples (thin films) was measured using a custom built setup in accordance with
international standards IEC 60243­1 (1998) and IEC 60243­2 (2001). The test was conducted by
increasing the applied voltage from 0.1 kV to 6.0 kV in 50 V increments, with each step applied
over 2.0 s and a dwell time of 1.0 s. The distance between the spherical electrodes was set to 95% of
the measured film thickness to ensure consistent contact with the sample. For each material, twelve
independent breakdown measurements were performed at room temperature, and the average value
was reported as the 𝐸BD.

Results and Discussion
4.2.1
Mechanical Properties
According to the actuation strain equation (eq. (1.2)), Young’s modulus (𝑌) is a critical mechanical
parameter, as lower 𝑌enables greater actuation under a given electric field. In addition, strain at
break 𝑠b is important for DEA applications as it defines the material’s stretchability and mechanical
limits during operation. These parameters were evaluated through tensile testing of elastomer
films containing varying amounts of [Im][TFSI]­g silicone. The measured values of 𝑌, and 𝑠b are
summarized in fig. 4.2.
All stress–strain curves (fig. 4.2 (a) and fig. B.1 to fig. B.3) exhibited stress softening behavior,
reflected by a reduction in slope with increasing strain. This Mullins effect, typical of silica filled
elastomers, likely arises from the disruption of weak physical interactions within the network. In
the present system, it does not signify mechanical failure but rather structural rearrangement under
deformation. Importantly, it suggests that pre­straining can be used to lower the effective modulus,
enabling larger actuation strains under a given Maxwell pressure.
Tensile test results for Young’s modulus (𝑌) and strain at break (𝑠b) at varying [Im][TFSI]­g sili­
cone content are shown in fig. 4.2 (b) and (c), respectively. As seen in (b), 𝑌remains around 0.4
MPa across the tested PIL loading range, indicating no significant stiffening. This compares well
to commercial soft PDMS elastomers, which typically exhibit 𝑌between 0.3 and 1.0 MPa [43].
It was observed that the Young’s modulus (𝑌) remained independent of the [Im][TFSI]­g silicone
content within the tested range. This was unexpected, as higher loadings were anticipated to in­
crease 𝑌, given that [Im][TFSI]­g silicone possesses shorter chain lengths and lower flexibility
compared to the base PDMS matrix. Its incorporation was thus expected to raise crosslink density
and dilute the soft, elastic network with the introduction of stiffer segments, leading to a more rigid
and potentially brittle material. The absence of this trend remains unexplained and warrants further
investigation. One possible explanation is that the bulky ionic side chains on the [Im][TFSI]­g sil­
icone introduce steric hindrance at crosslinking sites, limiting access to the reactive groups on the
silane crosslinker. As a result, higher [Im][TFSI]­g content may increase the number of unreacted
sites, thereby offsetting the stiffening effect typically associated with the addition of short, less
flexible chains.

4.2.2
Electrical Properties
Dielectric Properties
BDS results (fig. 4.3) revealed a clear enhancement in dielectric performance with increasing con­
tent of [Im][TFSI]­g silicone. In (a) the 𝜀′ increases steadily with PIL loading, reaching ∼9 at
1 Hz for the 44.4 wt.% sample ­ over three times higher than the typical value of pure PDMS
networks (𝜀′ = 3) [31]. This improvement, as expected, is attributed to the introduction of the
covalently bonded ionic moieties, which enhance dipolar polarization within the elastomer matrix.
Maxwell–Wagner–Sillars interfacial polarization is not expected to be a major contributor here,
as the grafted silicone shows good compatibility and dispersion within the PDMS matrix. This is
supported by the maintained mechanical integrity in tensile tests and the absence of visible phase
separation under optical microscopy (fig. B.4 to fig. B.6). As a result, permittivity contrast and
interfacial charge accumulation are expected to be minimal. To further investigate this, dielectric
dispersion could be analyzed using Havriliak–Negami modeling to extract relaxation parameters
and quantify potential interfacial contributions. However, this lies beyond the scope of this work.
The frequency dependence of 𝜀′ remains relatively weak across the measured range (1 Hz to 105
Hz), with no pronounced dielectric relaxation observed, even at high IL­g silicone concentra­
tions. This indicates that all formulations possess a similarly homogeneous network, with minimal
changes in the underlying polarization mechanisms.
Figure 4.3: BDS results of cured films with varying [Im][TFSI]­g silicone content: (a) dielectric
constant 𝜀′, (b) log (tan 𝛿), and (c) log (𝜎′) as functions of log (frequency).
The loss factor tan 𝛿, remains low (< 1) across the entire frequency range for all formulations.
Low dielectric losses are critical for dielectric elastomer actuators, as they reduce heat generation
from dipole relaxation during operation, enhancing energy efficiency and mitigating the risk of
mechanical or electrical failure under cyclic loading. At 1 Hz, tan 𝛿is approximately 0.1, which is
comparable to that of commercial silicone elastomers (around 0.05 [[5]]), considering the presence
of ionic moieties. A slight increase in tan 𝛿with higher IL­g silicone content is observed in the
intermediate frequency range (10 Hz–10 kHz) , the values remain sufficiently low (< 0.01) for
actuator applications. At both low and high frequencies, tan 𝛿values show minimal dependence on
IL­g silicone content, indicating that the dominant polarization mechanisms are largely unaffected
by IL incorporation under these conditions.
The real conductivity 𝜎′ remains below 10−12 S/cm up to 100 Hz for all samples, confirming the
insulating nature of the elastomer films. For comparison, commercial silicone elastomers typically
exhibit conductivities on the order of 10−15 S/cm [5]. At higher frequencies, 𝜎′ increases due to
localized ionic hopping.

The electrical breakdown strength of thin films with different [Im][TFSI]­g silicone loading was

exhibits a breakdown strength of around 100 V/µm under similar testing conditions [44].
The lack of a clear relationship between [Im][TFSI]­g silicone content and breakdown strength
may be explained by the influence of film thickness. The sample with the lowest 𝐸BD (44.4 wt.%)
also had the greatest thickness (86.8 µm), while the thinnest sample (41.2 wt.%, 50.6 µm) showed
the highest breakdown strength. This is consistent with well­established behavior in dielectric
materials, where thicker films tend to exhibit lower breakdown strength due to the increased like­
lihood of containing defects, voids, or inhomogeneities introduced during processing. As the film
becomes thicker, the volume of material increases, raising the statistical probability that a weak
point, such as micro voids, or regions of incomplete crosslinking, exists. These imperfections can
locally amplify the electric field and serve as breakdown initiation sites.

4.3
Conclusion
This chapter demonstrated that [Im][TFSI]­g silicones can be successfully incorporated into a
chemically crosslinked PDMS network to form soft dielectric elastomers with improved elec­
troactive functionality. The covalent integration of ionic moieties led to a significant increase
in dielectric permittivity (𝜀′ ∼9), up to threefold compared to pure PDMS, without compromis­
ing the mechanical softness (𝑌∼0.4 MPa) or elasticity (𝑠b > 300%), both critical for actuator
performance.
Despite the ionic functionality, loss factor and conductivity remained low across the tested fre­
quency range, confirming the insulating character of the films. The weak frequency dependence
of permittivity and the absence of dielectric relaxation suggest a homogeneous network structure
with well­dispersed ionic segments. This is further supported by the absence of phase separation
and stress­softening behavior consistent with a compatible, filled elastomer system. The Mullins
effect observed under strain was non­destructive and beneficial for actuation, allowing modulus
reduction through pre­straining.
Breakdown strength 𝐸BD was reduced compared to pure PDMS due to the presence of polar groups
but remained significantly higher than systems with physically blended free ILs. The variation in
𝐸BD was strongly influenced by film thickness, masking the expected trend of decreasing break­
down strength with increasing IL content. Standardizing film dimensions will be essential for more
accurate property comparisons in future work.

against commercial PDMS, is provided in table 4.3. Overall, the IL­grafted silicone network
achieves a favorable balance between enhanced dielectric properties and preserved mechanical

5
Conclusions and Outlook
5.1
Conclusions
This thesis demonstrated a successful strategy for enhancing the dielectric performance of silicone­
based elastomers through the covalent grafting of ionic liquid (IL) moieties onto polydimethyl­
siloxane (PDMS) chains. By systematically varying the anion species in imidazolium­functionalized
silicones, a series of polymeric ionic liquids (PILs) were synthesized and thoroughly characterized
to assess their suitability for dielectric elastomer actuator (DEA) applications.
Structural verification by 1H and 13C NMR and FTIR spectroscopy confirmed the clean and com­
plete anion exchange reactions, while thermal analysis revealed that all PILs exhibited high de­
composition temperatures (𝑇d > 200 °C), indicating thermal robustness. The glass transition tem­
perature (𝑇g) was highly anion­dependent, with TFSI−­functionalized PILs exhibiting the lowest
𝑇g (−39 °C) and highest segmental mobility.
Broadband dielectric spectroscopy (BDS) showed that [Im][TFSI]­g silicone had the highest real
permittivity at low frequency range among the tested PILs, combined with low dielectric losses
. Although ionic conductivity in the PIL state was relatively high, this was effectively mitigated
through chemical crosslinking within a PDMS matrix.
The resulting elastomer films maintained mechanical softness (𝑌∼0.4 MPa), high stretchability
(𝑠b > 300%), and achieved more than a threefold increase in permittivity (𝜀′ from 3 to 9) without
compromising dielectric loss or network homogeneity. The [Im][TFSI]­g silicone filled network
showed stable mechanical and dielectric properties, with minimal phase separation and favorable
electrical breakdown strength (𝐸BD = 63.6 V/µm). This demonstrates the effectiveness of cova­
lent IL grafting in balancing high dielectric response with soft, actuator compatible mechanical
properties.
5.2
Outlook
Building on the promising results of this study, several future directions can be pursued to further
advance the development of IL­g silicones for actuator applications:
• Actuation testing: Direct evaluation of actuation performance under electric fields will be
essential to assess the practical utility of these materials. Given the observed combination
of high permittivity and low modulus, the elastomers developed in this study are expected
to achieve significant strains at lower voltages. This could enable safer and more energy
efficient operation, particularly in wearable devices and biomedical actuators.
• Optimization of network architecture: Several strategies may be explored to further im­
prove the material’s mechanical and dielectric performance. First, increasing the proportion
of IL­g silicone may yield even higher permittivity, up to the limit where network integrity
is compromised or percolation of ionic pathways occur. Adjusting the chain length of the
IL­g silicone, would lead to an increase in the permittivty and may have the added benefit of
creating a more uniform dispersion of ionic domains. The networks mechanical properties
could be improved by reducing the crosslinker content. This would lower the crosslinking
density resulting in a softer elastomer. Additionally, using a crosslinker with enhanced ac­
cessibility, (with a more flexible and polar structure) may promote better integration and
mechanical uniformity across the network.
• Improved understanding of structure–property relationships: To guide future polymer
and elastomer structure design, a more detailed investigation of dielectric and mechanical
Ionic Liquid Grafted Silicones for Dielectric Elastomer Actuators

==PAGE:48==
behavior is essential. Dielectric relaxation models, such as Havriliak–Negami fitting, can
provide quantitative insights into dipolar dynamics and polarization mechanisms. When
combined with dynamic mechanical analysis, this can offer a deeper understanding of how
ionic moieties behavior and segmental dynamics contribute to dielectric performance. [45]
Such knowledge can help identify the structural features most responsible for permittivity
enhancement and mechanical softness. Additionally, complementary techniques such as
scanning electron microscopy and contact angle measurements can be used to assess phase
compatibility between IL­g silicone and PDMS, ensuring uniform morphology and stable
performance in the resulting elastomer networks.
• Device­level integration: Integrating these materials into functioning actuators, such as
multilayer stacks or soft robotic components, would provide valuable insights into their real­

encapsulation strategies to improve device stability and reproducibility.
The results of this work proposed a promising path for designing dielectric elastomers with im­
proved functionality through chemical modifications. Continued development in this direction
could unlock new possibilities in soft robotics, adaptive systems, and human­machine interfacing
technologies.
