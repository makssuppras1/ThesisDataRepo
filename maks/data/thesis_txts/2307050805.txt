Abstract
Quay walls are a type of marine structures which are continuous along the coastal area of
a port. Challenging geotechnical conditions can be met due to the marine environment
and it is quite common that a conservative design is adopted due to soft soil deposits.
The aim of this study is to examine a special cofferdam type of quay solution for soft
soil conditions; namely the plate anchored quay wall system.
The plate anchored quay wall structural concept consists of two walls: a front wall (e.g.:
combi-wall) and a back wall (e.g.: secant pile wall), which are connected with a contin-
uous plate anchor system. The purpose of the plate anchors is to provide a continuous
support over the height of the combi-wall in order to reduce the maximum bending mo-
ments and deformations on the wall.
Within the study finite element numerical models were developed to analyse the per-
formance of this structural concept in soft ground conditions. The geometry and the
structural details were based on a conceptual case study and various verification models.
Meanwhile, to model the normally consolidated soft soil conditions the Modified Cam-
Clay (MCC), the Mohr-Coulomb (MC) and the Soft Soil Creep (SSC) soil models were
applied.
As an initial step an appropriate, idealized plate anchored quay wall 3D model was devel-
oped. There were two main aspects of the analysis. First of all, the structural behaviour
was examined with regards to its overall deformation characteristic, the displacements
of the crane rails, the performance of the plate anchor system, the bending moments of
the front wall and the longitudinal act of the whole dilation part. Secondly, a number
of analyses were made to examine the effect of the constitutive soil models in details
with regards to the different loads and model assumptions of the soft ground conditions.
A major part was to compare the effect of the advanced soft soil models (MCC, SSC)
and the widely applied linear elastic-perfectly plastic soil model (MC). Furthermore, the
consequences of creep was also detailed and examined through the analyses.
Based on these numerical 3D model analyses a detailed assessment was conducted and a
deep academic insight was gathered about the adopted structural solution in soft ground
conditions. The obtained results can support quay wall projects in soft soil, either on
the level of feasibility study, or at the design phase.
Keywords: quay wall, numerical modelling, soft ground condition, creep, plate anchor.

1.3
Quay walls
In this chapter the following points are described:
The challenges, which are relating to the quay structures in the present engineering
practice and in the future with regards to the loads, tolerances and operational
differences.
The different tools and design approaches used to predict the characteristic be-
haviour and performance of the quay structure.
To describe these complex tasks, traditional quay walls are briefly described with their
main structural elements. In addition, the advantages and disadvantages of the specific
structural solutions are also assessed in order to make them more comparable.
Moreover, the most relevant load cases and tolerances are also detailed mentioning the
different design standards and guidelines. Special attention was made to summarize the
different calculation methods and approaches in case of quay wall design.
The demand for new ports and increasing capacity of the harbours was high through the
past decades [1]. The functionalities and requirements of ports were also changed and
modified along the history of ports and harbours. Thus, quay structures had to follow
these demands and various types of quay walls were developed to satisfy the wide range
of demands and unique circumstances of the different shorelines and ground conditions.
To detail all these developments would greatly expand the limits of this chapter, there-
fore only a summary of the different quay walls and quay wall functions are presented
below in order to get an insight about the main structural options and aspects used in
engineering practice. However, further examples and descriptions are available about
the history of quay walls in the following references [1] and [2].
The quay is the main part of the port for mooring and berthing. It also contains the
loading and unloading equipments and area (transhipment area in Figure 1.1). Quay
structure includes also the berthed ship and a small, usually 10 to 15 m wide back-
reach area after the apron with the crane. However, the storage area with the unloaded
containers is not part of the quay (dashed line in Figure 1.1).
The quay wall is the retaining structure of the whole quay (marked area in Figure 1.1).
However, it is not just necessarily the front wall, which separates the dock and land from
the water. Structural elements of the quay wall, like anchorages, back wall, etc. within
the transhipment area are also parts of the quay wall.

1.3.1
Main quay wall types
There are four basic quay wall types mainly used in engineering practice:
gravity walls;
sheet pile walls;
structures with relieving platform;
and open berth quays.
1.3.1.1
Gravity type quay walls
This type of structure ensures the retaining function with its own weight. Generally it
is a quite robust structure, with large self-weight. Moreover, sometimes the weight of
the soil above is also a part of the retaining system.
Gravity foundations are mainly attractive solutions in case of a subsoil with suitable
bearing capacity in relatively shallow depth [3].
In addition the presence of stones,
dense sand layers which makes the ground conditions insufficient for piling, or sheet
piling encourages the application of these kinds of structures.
Extended soil investigation is necessary, otherwise the additional soil improvement un-
der the gravity wall foundation can increase the expenses dramatically. The resistance
of the wall comes from the shearing between the subsoil under the wall and the structure
itself generated by its self-weight. Thus the wall can not slide or tilt [4].
The main types of gravity quay walls include (Figure 1.2d):
the block wall;
the L-wall;
the caisson wall;
and the reinforced earth wall.

Free-standing sheet pile wall: This type of structure behaves as an embedded beam to
transfer soil pressure to the the subsoil. This cantilevered beam ensured an elastically
fixed support with the usual embedded depth of about twice as much as the retaining
height (Figure 1.3a).
Free-standing walls are mainly made with only small retaining heights from interlocked
steel elements. In case of greater depth and retaining height some kind of anchorage
system is always necessary [1], [11].
Anchored sheet pile wall: It is usual, that in case of greater retaining height and su-
perstructure the horizontal forces become too large for the free-standing wall to be an
economical solution. Moreover, the strict deformation requirements for quay walls [1],
[7] demand greater control on the horizontal displacements of the sheet pile walls, which
usually can not be fulfilled without one or multiple anchors.
Statically all surface loads are directly transferred to the wall by the earth pressure.
In case of the anchored sheet pile wall, the quay wall itself is supported by the soil
resistance, passive earth pressure on the bottom, and by the anchors close to the top.
The bending moments and the deformations of the wall very much depend on the vertical
position of the anchor and stiffness of the embedded part [8]. The bending moments
can be reduced by placing the anchor deeper, or using multiple anchors on various levels.
However, the position of the anchorage on the sheet piles of the front wall is often
restricted with respect to the water level, when the wall is constructed in open sea [9].

can be to use tension piles.

rod connected to the sheet pile wall with an anchor head. This anchor can be fixed with
a vertical anchor wall, which is positioned at the rear side of the wall (Figure 1.4).

1.3.1.3
Relieving platform quay wall structures
In case of heavy loads and strict deformation requirements (cranes and deformations of
the railways), large retaining heights and the reduction of horizontal loads on the quay
wall structure are crucial needs. A possible solution is the relieving platform quay walls,
which transfer the vertical loads to a greater depth, without generating earth pressure
on the front wall.
This structure consists of a relieving platform, generally made from concrete, which is
supported by the front wall and piles (a combination of bearing, and tension piles). The
foundation system supports the superstructure, but also ensures stability of the whole
quay wall. The earth retaining function is provided by the sheet pile wall, or combi-wall.

pile trestle and a combi-wall at the waterside. This relieving platform is mainly used

1.3.2.1
Container loads
The geometry of the shipping containers is highly standardized and accurately defined.
Also, the maximum weight of a full container is maximized in 350 kN. Statistically
17% of the containers is unloaded, thus nowadays the generally used weight of a loaded
contained is 270 kN, which means approximately 10% of the total number of containers
[1]. This individual container load is applied on the load of the apron and taken into
account in the additional live loads.
However, at the storage area the layers of containers give a uniform load, which are
summarised below:
1 layer = 17.5 kPa;
2 layer = 35 kPa;
3 layer = 45 kPa;
4 layer = 55 kPa (empty 15 kPa);
5 layer = 70 kPa.
Higher the stacks means smaller the chance that containers with maximum weight are
stored on top of each other. In case of a new container quay structure 4 or 5 layers are
used at the storage area [1].

1.3.2.4
Berthing loads
In case of a berthed ships the main loads are the mooring force on the bollards and the
fender forces. In order to determine these forces a mooring plan and berthing simulation
is usually made. The main input parameters for these simulations are the distribution
of fenders and bollards, the wind speed with its direction, and the resulting waves gen-
erated by the ships with allowed maximum speed.
A typical mooring configuration is shown in Figure 1.11. It is general, that large vessels
are fixed at two points; at the stem line and at the bow line as well. In that case the
mooring system keeps the ship in position and the fenders protect the quay wall from
the ship movements, which is restricted by them.
At this situation the fender forces are mainly perpendicular to the structure, meanwhile
the bollard forces act as a pulling with a specified angle respect to the quay wall. This
angle between the mooring line and the berth line should be between 20° to 30° [1].

1.3.2.5
Additional variables and special loads
Apart from the above detailed and generally described load cases, there are additional
load cases, like:
seismic load;
negative skin friction (NSF);
temperature load, wave load, etc.
In case of earthquake sensitive area the design for a possible seismic event is indispensable.
The seismic load and the design consideration is based on the area’s reference PGA-
value3.
In general, at earthquake endangered areas the additional horizontal loads by the seismic
event and the risk of liquefaction are significant. In addition, for quay wall structural
point of view, the more slender quay wall types without sensitive connections are pre-
ferred, like open berth quay walls, than gravity walls or anchored sheet pile walls. Based
on recent study [21] the usual PGA limit for quay walls are summarised in Table 1.7.

The negative skin friction is active until the the so-called neutral plane [22], when the rel-
ative settlement differences between the structure and the soil become zero (Figure 1.13).
From this plane, the displacements of the structure are greater and the shaft resistance
can be considered. However, there is a transit zone where the shaft resistance can not
be fully mobilized [24].
In case of quay wall structures, negative skin friction caused several problems, especially
for elder quay wall structures, when it was not taken into account at the design phase
[25].
Therefore, the negative skin friction along the piles and retaining structures shall be
considered in an early phase of the the design [26]. There are several analytical design
approaches [27], [28], but the most suitable is the numerical simulation to define the
depth of the neutral plane and the extension of the transition zone [29].
Wave loads also act on quay walls, however these loads are usually not significant due
to the fact that generally quay walls are situated at relatively wave protected harbour
areas. The wave load is based on the significant wave height acting on the structure. In
addition operating and extreme wave height shall be also calculated [30].
In case of areas, where large temperature differences can appear, the consideration of the
temperature load on quay wall structures is necessary. This load case is highly important
in case of large dilation distances with thick concrete deck plates.

1.3.3
Main design criteria and tolerances
For the final structure the main criteria is to reach the appropriate structural safety and
to ensure the continuous operation and berthing function over its lifetime within the
structural and operational tolerances.
In terms of proper berthing the horizontal wall movement has to be limited. These limits
are relating to the retaining height of the quay wall and to the operating crane class as
well. With regards to the maximum horizontal operational deflection of the wall shall
be less than Height/300 [7] . In case of a 20 m deep quay structure it means 67 mm as
maximum.
However, it should be highlighted that for quay structures with portal cranes the gov-
erning and most important design criteria and operational limit tolerance belong to the
crane and its railway [1].
These tolerances are set by the crane supplier and at most of cases extremely strict
displacement and rotation limits are defined. The operational tolerance is the summary
of dimensional tolerances, constructional deviations and the deformation of the crane
track in the service phase. Therefore, in most standards and design guidelines these
tolerance are the main design criteria.
It is not unusual, that these operational limits define not just the foundation and the
construction sequence of the structure, but the capping beam dimensions as well [1]. For
instance, in case of open berth quay walls usually a large capping beam and the close
placing of the foundation piles are necessary in order to keep the longitudinal deforma-
tion criteria of the crane rails.
Crane tolerances below are defined depending on crane’s construction method [19]:
Difference in height of rails longitudinally: +/- 3 to 9 mm per meter;
Difference between the rails in the transverse direction: depends on the span,
usually +/- 10 mm is the max.;
Difference in inclination of rails in relation to each other: +/- 3 to 6 mm per meter.
Further tolerances and suggested values with regards to the different crane types can be
found in the following standards [31] and [32].
1.3.4
Design approaches and calculation methods
Safety is one of the most important items and parametrized requirements in the de-
sign process. There are three main approaches in order to calculate and ensure the
appropriate safety for quay wall structures:
the deterministic safety design approach;
the probabilistic approach to safety;
and the semi probabilistic method to safety.

mainly used to determine the necessary sheet pile length with the assumption that there
is an equilibrium between the active and passive earth pressures in a way that the
displacements at the anchor are zero [34].

1.3.4.3
Finite element method
In order to take into account the soil-structure interaction of the quay wall structure,
the finite element calculation method is the best option [1], [44].
Generally speaking, this method is based on the stress equilibrium, deformation of soils
and the bending behaviour of constructional elements, which are coupled and calculated
by partial and ordinary differential equations [1].
Several geotechnical software offer numerical solutions both in 2D and 3D, like Plaxis,
Diana, Mitas, etc. Within these programmes, both the soil and the structure are divided
into a mesh of finite elements on which the deformations and stresses are calculated.
There are plenty of advantages, which encouraged in the past decades the wide range of
application of finite element methods in geotechnical engineering and quay wall design
[1], [44], [45]:
in general less simplifications need to be applied in the model;
the soil-structure interaction is covered for every structural element;
calculations can be made in 2D and 3D as well;
the undrained and drained effects are fully implemented in the calculation options;
the effect of construction phases can be followed;
the estimation of deformations is reliable;
the consolidation and the time effect can be modelled;
through the different constitutive soil models several soil behaviour can be cap-
tured;
dynamic calculation can be made;
reliable sensitivity analyses can be performed;
it ensure reliable information about forces and bending moments on structural
elements.
Even though, the list of different advantages is long, some concerns have to be noted.
Namely, that because it is a complex method every applied simplification of the model
requires extended experience and knowledge about the real structural behaviour. More
sophisticated input parameters and soil properties are necessary for the advanced soil
models. Calculation wise, numerical issues and stiffness matrix problems can appear due
to the inappropriate mesh. To avoid them extended modelling experience is beneficial.
All in all, it can be concluded that for a detailed and more reliable quay wall design
finite element method is one of the best option. The complex soil-structure interaction
effects, to take into consideration the construction phases and the time effect encourage
the application of this calculation method.

a new quay structure.
For new quay wall structures the main objective is to achieve a quay wall structure
with extended retaining height, increased stiffness and strength economically. There are
innovative examples, like the containerland, the universal moveable quay, the suction
anchor and the prefabricated L-wall concepts [1], [2], which give an insight about the
future possibilities.

As conclusion it is seen that the loads and requirements are increasing continuously.
Therefore for quay wall structures several innovative new designs were introduced lately.
In general, if with a new concept a slender, more economical structure can be achieved
with the sufficient robustness the design is on the right track with regards to the future
trends.
These new structural concepts, demands integrated design approaches with the applica-
tion of finite element calculation methods in order to consider the complex soil-structure
interactions, time effect, difficult geotechnical conditions and deformations over the life

The below represented case is a conceptual design idea, which was presented with re-

The conceptual design case was provided by COWI. Within this study, only the general

2.1
Plate anchored quay wall structure
In case of soft ground conditions and large retaining heights it is necessary to limit the
deformations of the front wall and to keep the strict requirements and criteria mentioned
in Section 1.3.3.
In order to achieve this goal the quay wall must be anchored (with single, or multiple
anchor rows), or with a relieving platform where the loads are transferred to the deeper
soil strata and the quay wall is prevented to be directly loaded by the surcharge loads.
A more slender structure with robust overall behaviour means more economical and suf-
ficient solution in soft ground conditions.
In this matter the plate anchored quay wall can be a possible solution. In terms of over-
all behaviour it is a cofferdam like structure (Section 1.3.1.2), which ensures stiffness
and robust retaining wall together with the flanked soil in between.
==PAGE:44==
2.1 Plate anchored quay wall structure
26
As it is shown in Figure 2.1 the system consists of two walls; a front wall (Figure 2.1(1)),
which is a combi-wall (with the retaining height of 22 m) and at the rear side a back
wall (Figure 2.1(2)), which is a secant pile wall (long bored piles (-65 m) and shorter
(-18.5 m) CFA piles in between).
Figure 2.1: Overview of the plate anchored quay wall structure
On the top of the walls stiff and strong capping beams are implemented (Figure 2.1(3)),
in order to ensure the strict criteria for the cranes on top of them.
The walls are connected in two ways:
with plate anchors similar to a cofferdam;
and with transverse cross beams, which are connected on the level of the capping
beams.
The plate anchors (Figure 2.1(4)) are situated in between of the fenders at every 7.2 m
in order to be prevented from compression (Figure 2.2). Meanwhile, transverse beams
(Figure 2.1(5)) are implemented in between of the plate anchors and relating to the
fenders (Figure 2.2). In addition, because of the soft subsoil, the transverse beams are
supported by bored pile (Figure 2.1(6)) in between the the front and the back walls.
The main purpose of the plate anchor system apart from the fact, that they ensure the
connection between the walls, is to limit the bending moments and deformations on the
front wall.
Because these plate anchors create continuous connections between the walls (from -2.4
m to -18.4 m depth) they can restrain the deformations of the front wall along its height.
It theoretically also means reduced bending moments on it as well.

of the interlocked connection in between is closed by a steel pile, which functions as a
slider of a zipper. As this pile is driven down, the gap between the plate anchor halves
is gradually reduced along their heights, which results in pre-tension within the plates.
Moreover, due to this procedure the front and the back wall move towards each other
and passive earth pressure is mobilized in between them (Figure 2.3). This results in
relatively stiff ”soil cells” between the plate anchors and the walls. The interlocking
procedure happens before finishing the superstructure and the transverse beams on top
are not yet constructed.
In this way, the final structure consist of:
a stiff cofferdam like part, in which the front and the back walls are continuously
connected along their height by the plate anchors at every 7.2 m;
a strong and stiff upper frame, made by the longitudinal capping beams, which
are connected by the cross beams and the concrete deck on top of it;
and a deep foundation system, which includes the primary piles of the combi-wall,
the bored piles of the back wall and the middle piles of the transverse beams.
Therefore, all the significant dead and service loads are carried by these elements and
transferred to the deeper soil strata with adequate bearing capacity.

The area of the new port structure is situated in a large plain of a river delta. Thus, the
plain has been generally covered by a thick layer of alluvial clayey sediments from the

its level. In addition, this area has a continuous subsiding nature over time, which has
a rate of 0.5 cm/year.
2.2.1
Idealized soil profile and soil properties
The area of interest was investigated with great details and several CPT tests and deep
drillings were performed. In addition, also a large amount of different laboratory tests
(triaxial-, oedometer-, direct shear tests, etc.) were made on soil samples from various
depths.
Based on these data it was concluded that close to the surface a highly varying loose
and very loose silty sand and clay is situated, which has the thickness of around 7-10 m
with a thin sand layer.
Under that, a thick clay and silty clay soil was layered, with the average depth of -45 to
-55 m. A typical CPT test result is shown in Appendix A.1.
Furthermore, the deep drillings showed, that under this thick and soft clay layer a dense
sand is situated with good load bearing capacity and with the average thickness of more
than 20 m.
In order to reduce the complexity of the soil layers for the modelling an idealized soil
profile was used. In case of this simplified profile, the varying upper parts were neglected
and a 55 m deep, uniform clay layer was used, with the dense sand layer under it
(Figure 2.4). The depth of the port excavation was designed as -22 m.

2.3
Motivation and main objectives
As it was detailed above, this quay wall concept can be a promising and economical
option in case of large retaining heights and soft ground conditions. However, to get an
academic insight about its performance and effectiveness with regards to different load
cases and geotechnical circumstances a number of numerical tests and analyses were
performed.
Some of the main tasks of the analyses are listed below:
To define the most sufficient way of the plate anchored quay wall mod-
elling in Plaxis 3D;
To identify the possible effect of the different constitutive soil models;
To examine the quay wall performance in soft ground conditions (defor-
mation of the crane rails, etc.);
To investigate the effectiveness of the plate anchors in terms of the re-
duction of the front wall moments and deformations;
To examine the effect of creep in case of this quay wall system;
To assess the longitudinal behaviour of the system.
There were two main aspects, which governed the modelling and analysis. Obviously,
the structural solution of the plate anchored quay wall system was the main one. The
idealization of the geometrical reality and the modelling of the plate anchors were in
major focus.
Furthermore, the soft ground condition with creep was another key factor. It was as-
sumed, that in case of a normally consolidated clay with large creep rate the deformations
in time are rather governed by the creep. Thus, creep and the deformations relating to
it were also taken into account within the soil-structure interaction analysis.
2.3.1
Limitations of the scope
Even though, the analysis covered a wide range of geotechnical and structural aspects,
the scope of the study had to be limited. These limitations were the following:
The constructional steps and aspects were concerned with restrictions;
The driving of the flat sheet and plate anchors, the effect of buckling, etc. were
not considered;
The effect of scour due to ships were not examined within the study;
The performance in case of a seismic event and temperature load case were not
analysed;
Just a selected number of significant load cases and load combinations were applied;
Apart from the permeability differences, there were no anisotropic soil character-
istic taken into account during the analysis.
These limitations were necessary in order to make a clear scope for the present study.
However, some of the above mentioned aspects can be a basis for future work and further
development of the presented analysis.

3.1
General remarks of the geotechnical modelling
procedure
Numerical modelling and the application of geotechnical software became common in
the geotechnical engineering practice. However, numerical modelling is just a part of
the appropriate geotechnical design and analysis.
John Burland from Imperial College was the one who introduced his theory [47], which
represents how the different geotechnical practices are linked with each other (Figure 3.1).
As you may see in the figure, the understanding of the soil behaviour and the particular
ground conditions support directly the modelling process. Meanwhile, all these three

Step 1: Observe - Developing the conceptual model;
Step 2: Measure - Define the theoretical model;
Step 3: Explain - Developing and verifying the numerical model;
Step 4: Verify - Interpreting, calibrating the model against the physical reality.

3.2.1
Modelling of sheet pile wall component
For the intermediate sheet piles, the usage of plate elements as a modelling option, look
like as an appropriate and obvious solution.
However, because these sheet piles are
orthotropic structures in reality, there are some special recommendation to achieve the
right plate properties.
The geometrical orthotropic manner of the sheet pile plate means significant differences
in stiffnesses of the horizontal and vertical direction (E1 > E2).
In order to set the plate element with equivalent thickness, stiffness and inertia pa-
rameters similar to the real sheet pile, an anisotropic plate is necessary. The general
calculation steps are based on tests and empirical assumptions, which are detailed in
the material manual of Plaxis [53c].
Furthermore, there are several U and Z sheet pile profiles, which are available in Plaxis.
These conventional profiles have been already calculated and verified. Thus, without
further details the parameters of the applied pile sheet profile (AZ-28 700) were not
validated here. The applied properties can be found in Table 5.4 in Section 5.3.
Even though, most of these parameters were pre-calculated, an additional modelling
property, namely the applied wall friction ratio had to be defined. This can be done
with the Rint interface strength ratio in Plaxis (Appendix A.2).
In case of U and Z-type profile the actual surface (sheet pile surface - ltot) per meter of
the wall is larger than its stretch (dashed line - lpl). That also implies that the actual
shear surface must follow a straight line at the back of the wall (Figure 3.4) for a larger
part. Thus any reduction in the wall friction needs to follow only the part of the stretch,
which is a significantly small part. Therefore, it is usual that no reduction takes place
in the interface strength.
Figure 3.4: Shear surface (dashed line) between the sheet pile and the soil
However, for empirical reasons it is suggested to use Rint = 0.8−0.9 [1], as ltot/lpl ≈1.3,
which can be multiplied by the empirical Rint=0.67 interface reduction factor to achieve
the recommended interface strength. Therefore, an Rint = 0.9 value was applied in the
model for every sheet pile plate elements (Table 5.4).

Modelling approach of the
soft ground condition
The geotechnical challenges with regards to soft soil or ultra soft soil conditions are
numerous, especially when the structure is closer to the coast line [69] or river deltas. In
the past years it was stressed in several research papers [70], that the increased caution
and the more advanced modelling methods are indispensable for the appropriate design
[71].
Within this chapter the corresponding soft soil behaviour and its modelling challenges
are discussed. The main questions were:
What is the general behaviour of the normally consolidated soft clay?
What is the most appropriate modelling approach with regards to the soft soil
behaviour and creep?
In order to be able to answer those complex question the basics of the behaviour of
soft soils (including its time-dependence) and the different constitutive soil models are
discussed. Special attention was made with regards to the effect of creep, which in case
of normally consolidated clays can govern the deformations in time [70] [72].
Please note, that under the normally consolidated clay a dense sand is situated (Fig-
ure 2.4). The detailed discussion of the behaviour and modelling of this layer would
expand the frame of this chapter. Therefore, in the following sections the normally con-
solidated clay is covered only (Section 4.1).
Additionally, the theoretical background of the applied constitutive soil models are de-
scribed as well (Section 4.3).
4.1
Introduction of soft clay behaviour
Usually, normally consolidated clays, clayey silts and peat are considered as soft soils [70].
One of the most important property of these materials is the extreme compressibility.
For instance, according to an oedometer test report by Janbu [73] in case of a tangent
stiffness modulus at a reference oedometer pressure of 100 kPa for normally consolidated
==PAGE:71==
4.1 Introduction of soft clay behaviour
53
clays a range of Eoed=1-4 MPa was reached. Meanwhile in case of normally consolidated
sands, the observed stiffness was approximately one order of magnitude larger.
This special compressibility coupled with usually low natural permeability creates several
geotechnical difficulties. For instance, the calculation and prediction of the settlements
and consolidation in time, and the consideration of the effect of creep can be challenging
to calculate or model.
It is a usual approach, that in case of soft, normally consolidated clays undrained cal-
culation is performed, with regards to the fact, that because of the low permeability
excess pore water pressure generates due to the different construction phases and load-
ing conditions. This excess pore water pressure afterwards dissipates with time during
the consolidation, governed by the permanent loads and the permeability of the soft soil
as it is well-known in soft soil engineering cases [70] [71].
During the consolidation as the pore pressure diminishes significant deformations take
place (Figure 2.5) as a result of the volumetric strains over time. Moreover, in case of
soft soils creep also develops and can have significant effect on the deformations.
A wide range of theoretical background is available about the time-dependent behaviour
of soft soils, which are briefly described in the following section relating to consolidation
and the creep.
4.1.1
Time dependent behaviour of soft soils: consolidation and
creep
With regards to the consolidation process Terzaghi’s theory was the first one, however
it was valid just for one-dimensional cases [68]. Later on it was extended into 3D con-
solidation cases, based on the 3D-consolidation theory by Biot [75]. Even though, the
3D-consolidation models suggested by Terzaghi and Biot show good agreement in many
cases, concerns are still exist. Comparative studies like Cryer [76], Lambe & Whitman
[77] and Debbarh [78] showed that in some cases neither Terzaghi’s theory nor Biot’s
theory ensures the complete description of the consolidation process.
Originally, the consolidation process was always distinguished between the primary and
the secondary compression in the different theories and models. In this way, the basic
hypothesis is that the primary consolidation occurs during the decrement of the excess
pore pressure and volume, when the effective stresses are increasing (described with one
of the theories mentioned above).
The secondary process starts afterwards and it means further deformation and volumet-
ric decrement under constant effective stress [79].
Probably the first creep law was proposed by Buisman [74], who suggested continuous or
transient loading process to consider the secondary compression and the effect of creep.
In this concept the secondary compression started after the dissipation of the excess

settlements a linear logarithm relation was assumed in time (Figure 4.1).

avoid in case of soft soils, where the softening and post peak (shear strength) behaviour

most appropriate choice. Although, for hardening dominant cases it can provide proper
results.

4.3.1

As one of the most straight forward and widely used constitutive soil model, the elastic-

Even though, soils behave in a rather non-linear way as a first approximation the linear
elastic-perfectly plastic Mohr-Coulomb soil model can be used. The main parameters of
the Mohr-Coulomb soil model are the effective internal friction angle (ϕ′) and cohesion

4.3.3.1
Ageing of the SSC model
There are several important remarks in case of modelling with SSC model. For instance,
in SSC if a higher load is applied the cap will follow the new stress state, but not im-
mediately, though with an arbitrary chosen 1 day value [101]. In addition, after this
one day, the cap will not stop expanding, but continues with a decreased rate as it was
mentioned above as well. That means that any change in the stress state, increment or
decrement (loading or unloading) will change the velocity of the cap expanding, however
it always keeps expanding [101].
This behaviour means, that the stress history of the soil has a major role in the defor-
mations. For example, the short term deformations can significantly defer in case of an
embankment which was just built, or another mature one with the same soil conditions,
which was constructed a long time ago. Thus, in case of a newly applied, normally
consolidated material (OCR = 1) large creep deformations will occur. Because of that,
two different ageing procedure shall be applied:
1. Assign initially an OCR value, which can be estimated based on the following
equation:
OCR ≃e(
µ∗
λ∗−κ∗)ln(∆t)
(4.18)
Where ∆t is in days and in case of clay or peat layer a hundreds of years could be
defined. However, for normally consolidated soils OCR value in the order of ≃1.5
is appropriate [102].
2. Add a plastic nil step after the initial calculation phase in which ∆t is used as
the time increment. As it was mentioned before, due to creep it would result in a
certain amount of OCR [80].
Both solutions can be appropriate, however, usually there are uncertainties in the OCR
value. Therefore, in modelling practice the ageing procedure by a plastic nil step is
commonly used [72] [103] [104].
In that case, after the ageing calculation step the occurred settlement due to the creep
history has to be disregarded before the next calculation step. In addition, in order to
avoid large, unrealistic excess pore pressures due to the creep history modelling drained,
plastic calculation shall be selected.
Therefore within this study in case of the SSC model calculation, a ∆t = 1000 years
long creep history plastic nil step was applied (Section 5.5). The effect of this ageing
phase is detailed in Section 7.3.1.

4.3.3.2
Undrained behaviour of the SSC model
As it was aforementioned, those soils which exhibit creep behaviour have low permeabil-
ity. Therefore, soft soils almost always show undrained behaviour for short term loading.
However, the combination of undrained behaviour and creep in SSC model raises some
additional issues, which has to be discussed.
A simple modelling example was made in Plaxis, when an undrained and undisturbed
block of soft soil was modelled over a 10 year period with SSC model. As a conclusion
significant excess pore water pressure was generated even though the model itself was
almost undeformed [101]. The reason behind this behaviour, that creep causes plastic
deformations over time as it was discussed previously. These deformations result in a
decrease in the volume. However, the model is undrained, thus no volume strains are
allowed (δϵv = 0). Therefore, the plastic volume strain, which is caused by creep has
to be compensated by the elastic volume strain with an equal amount, but opposite
direction (Eq. 4.19).
ϵv = −ϵe
v + ϵcr
v = 0
(4.19)
In terms of stresses it means, that elastic expansion (−ϵe
v) results in decreased effective
stresses, but since no additional load was applied the total stresses have to remain the
same, which means increasing excess pore pressure to counterbalance the effective stress
decrements.
In case of consolidation however, the excess pore pressure dissipates, which leads to a
situation when the excess pore pressure would increase due to creep and decrease due
to consolidation. The exact behaviour depends on very much, whether the creep or the
consolidation is the dominant (Figure 4.2). During the consolidation procedure as the
excess pore water pressure decrease the creep rate tends to increase. If the velocity of
the creep increase and starts to generate excess pore pressure it will cause later on a
decrease in the creep velocity and acceleration in the consolidation velocity.
It has to be noted, that in case of 3D consolidation in Plaxis 3D AE, the Biot’s con-
solidation theory [75] is applied and the effect of creep in SSC model as a secondary
compression happens simultaneously with the consolidation [53c].
Modelling wise, this procedure results in a really slow consolidation process in case of
SSC undrained soils with low permeability. For creep sensitive soils at failure state, the
effect of creep can not be counterbalanced by the elastic volumetric strains. At this
situation a numerically unstable situation can appear, where it is challenging to find a
converging solution.

After performing a convergence analysis, the results obtained with the above detailed

below. For further details, please see Appendix A.10.
Constructional phases and consolidations;
– Front wall;
– Back wall;
– Apron section structural parts (Figure 2.1);
– Interlocking (Figure 2.3).
Excavation phases and consolidation;
– Excavation in 4 steps;
– Consolidation with 30 days after each excavation phase.
Loading cases and consolidation;
– SL1-SL3, CL1 and Comb1 load cases (Figure 5.3) with consolidation periods

– Comb2-Comb4 berthing load cases (Figure 5.4) with consolidation periods of

As you may see, no safety calculation was applied. The main reason behind this is that
in case of the Modified Cam-Clay model a ϕ-c reduction based safety calculation can
not be performed [53a], because the strength of the soil is defined by the principles of
the critical state soil mechanics (Section 4.3.2).
In addition, in case of the Mohr-Coulomb and Soft Soil Creep models local failures
occurred during the safety calculation.
It was concluded, that in case of the plate
anchored quay wall structure model in soft soil any safety calculation is numerically
challenging. In addition, the overall stability of the structure was not an issue at the
first place, thus this calculation type can be neglected.

5.5 Calculation phases

5.5.1
Time duration of the phases
As the permeability was low, undrained model analysis was made for every case. Thus,
in any plastic calculation phase the time duration has just a small impact on the defor-
mations and the generated excess pore pressure. With other words, it has no significant
impact if a plastic calculation step had the duration of 0 days or 100 days. A chosen 5
days value was used to be able to see stress developments in the output curves.
Therefore, in order to see any deformations, consolidation phases were implemented
after construction phases (chosen) and after every excavation and load cases. These
consolidation steps had the duration of 30 days, which was an arbitrary chosen time
range.
This time duration was allowed to be use, because the real construction time would
result in extremely long calculation time for the whole model, without significant effect
on deformations, etc. In addition, to reach 10 % consolidation state several years would
be necessary, which would again increase the modelling time tremendously. All in all,
this compromise with the time durations ensured comparable and approximate results
within an acceptable calculation time.
5.5.2
Additional settings for SSC soil material
In Section 4.3.3 several special behaviour of the SSC model were described. The imple-
mentation of these aspects are defined below.
As an important calculation phase, an ageing step was used in case of SSC soil model
after the initial calculation and before the first constructional phase. Basically this was
a 1000 years long plastic nil step in order to model the creep history and to mature the
soft soil modelled by SSC (Section 4.3.3.1). To disregard the resulted large deformation
(approx.: 4.5 m) the ”reset displacements” option was used at the first phase after ageing.
Furthermore, because of the large creep rate a drained SSC soil model was used at every
consolidation phases. This soil model was identical with the one defined in Table 5.12,
except, that it was set as ”drained” material, which means no excess pore water pres-
sure was generated due to creep. As it was described in Section 4.3.3.2 it results in a
numerically more stable situation with no effect on the primary consolidation, because
it is governed just by the permeability [53a].

5.6
Optimization of the modelling process and
numerical challenges
Because the current paper was about a numerical performance and sensitivity modelling
of a geotechnical structure several issues were solved, which were relating to convergence
and solver issues.
As it was well described by Barbour and Krahn [52] numerical modelling in the field
of geotechnics is more of a process, than a straight forward prediction of geotechnical
behaviour. Meaning that several critical idealisation, optimisation, verification and as-
sessment step should be involved until the final model is developed. These processes are
detailed in Chapter 3. Therefore, here some special settings and remarks are summarised,
which helped to develop a numerically stable and optimised model.
5.6.1
Effect of the numerical control parameters
In the newest Plaxis 3D AE release it was possible to set different numerical control
parameters. One of the most important was the solver type. For the present model the
two multi-core type solvers were used [53a]:
Picos - Plaxis Iterative Concurrent Solver (multicore iterative);
Pardiso - Parallel Sparse Direct Solver (multicore direct).
In general, Picos solver is suggested as the fastest solver type, especially in case of those
models which have ≈100.000 nodes. Meanwhile, Pardiso is a good solution for slow
convergence problems (e.g.: offshore models), where 20-40 % of calculation time can be
saved. This solver is the most robust way to solve equations and it means a better solu-
tion when large stiffness differences are involved. Consequently, this procedure requires
a large amount of memory (additional savings of the phases during calculation may be
necessary).
Because of the fact, that both models (performance model and the smaller comparative
models) contain around or more than 100.000 nodes (Section 5.2) Picos solver was used
in case of the plastic type calculation phases.
However, in case of the consolidation phases this solver gave the error message due to
slow convergence. Thus, Pardiso solver was applied with the following settings, which
are based on the experiences gained during this study:
Max. steps: increased to 1500;
Max. unloading steps: increased to 10;
Max. load fraction per step: decreased to 0.01;
Use gradual error reduction:
.
==PAGE:100==
5.6 Optimization of the modelling process and numerical challenges
82
Where, the max. calculation steps were increased in order to be able to follow the slow
convergence calculation. The unloading gives the number of steps allowed before the
soil body is defined as collapsed [53a]. In case of uplift/ swelling the default value of 5
can lead to failure in the calculation. The max. load fraction per step was reduced in
order to ensure a more gradual load development. The smaller the value, the slower the
convergence.
As a last step gradual error reduction was also used. Without all the details [53a], this
option gives a numerical trick that can speed up the slow convergence problems. In
general it calculates with a higher tolerated error during the equation solving. The error
is forced to be reduced at the initial tolerated error’s level just at the end of the calcu-
lation. Because of this special procedure, the tolerated error should not exceed 2 % and
the above written small load fraction per step is suggested.
The rest of the numerical control parameters were kept as default.

6.3
Performance of the plate anchor system
As it was previously mentioned the plate anchor system is not just a continuous support
of the front combi-wall, but with the interlocking process (Figure 2.3) it ensures rela-
tively stiff soil cells between the front and back walls. Then, the result of this anchor
system is an appropriate, stiff cofferdam like (Figure 1.7) quay structure.
It was initially assumed that, a great reduction can be achieved in the deformations
and bending moments of the front wall, because of the continuously connected plate
anchors. In addition, it was also presumed, that through the interlocking process the
front and the back wall displacement would be coupled and the whole quay system can
work together as an effective retaining structure.

When the interlocking process is finalized an active, coupled soil-structure inter-

tensioned zones would ”follow” them within the plates. In this way, the restriction
of the wall deformations and bending moments would be ensured.
But instead of seeing deformations on the front wall, which are partly ”followed” and
restrained by the back wall through the plate anchors an opposite phenomenon became
dominant. As you see in Figure 6.15a the back wall moved towards the rear side com-

the combi-wall was deformed by them through the plate anchor connection.

settle towards the rear side. The arrows show the direction of the tension forces, which
had the peak at the left upper corner of the anchors and in the middle at the back wall.
Furthermore, almost the whole plate anchor is in compression, which is a fairly unlikely
behaviour for an anchor system.
The possible effects, which have contributed to the resulted behaviour were the following:
As the quay structure was loaded at Comb1 the effect of the interlocking was
cancelled out, thus there was no active, stiff soil cells in between the walls.
Moreover, due to the larger loads on the storage area additional loads were
subjected on the back wall (partly from negative skin friction and from the
storage area’s container load). That resulted larger settlements for the back wall
(Figure 6.16a).

Consequently, it seems there is no significant reduction of the front wall’s deformations
and bending moments, when there are vertical loads on the apron section. Although,
this situation when there was no interlocking at all was also examined in Section 7.2 to
get the full insight of its effect.

The longitudinal behaviour of the structure was also examined relating to the different

more, the combination of this two (Comb4 - Figure 5.4c), which simulates the fender

It is easy to see how the different out of phase load cases have effect on the entire dilation
piece of the quay system. In case of the fender forces an extended local effect is observed.
At this load case, the upper part of the apron section has a fairly uniform displacement
towards the storage area. Thus, the passive earth pressure were evenly mobilized along
the rear side of back wall (Figure 6.20a). For the bollard force a way different deforma-
tion pattern occurred. The out of phase load towards the water side generated a local
deformation maximum, which developed mainly towards the -x direction. Furthermore,
as the stiff apron section was moved towards the water side relatively larger deformations
appeared on the rear side of the back wall, as mainly the cross beams transferred the
forces. In this way, the active earth pressure emerged at the back wall, which resulted a
more extended overall deformation area (Figure 6.20b). In case of Comb4 an extensive
rotation was observed on the whole dilation piece (Figure 6.20c). Larger passive defor-
mations were observed due to the fender forces, which had an effect on the rear side at
the storage area as well.
However, it has to be noted that, these displacements are small. The order of magnitude
is m−3. That also means 1e−5 [−] in terms of shear strains (γs), which after Atkinson and
Sallfors [111] indicates that the performance of the structure falls into the small-strain
characteristic stiffness-strain behaviour.
The main reason for these small deformations is related to the generally stiff structure
and to the strong upper beam frame, which distributes the loads (and deformations) on

the whole apron part. In addition, the transferred longitudinal displacements from one
dilation piece to the adjacent ones are rather small without significant impact.
Furthermore, as it was mentioned above the effect of the interlocked plate anchors is
minor, due to the fact that vertical operation loads (SL1, CL1) counteract the interlocked
plates and cancel out its effect.
Because of that, the stiff beam frame governs the
deformations as the overall apron section follows its displacement.
With other words the overall stiffness of the apron section in case of the longitudinal
loading phases is effected by the structural components (mainly the beams) and not
influenced by the interlocking or the plate anchors.
Thus, it can be concluded, that in case of the berthing load cases the longitudinal
behaviour of the plate anchored quay structure can be simulated with the presented
numerical 3D model. The resulted displacements were rather small, due to the stiff
beam frame, which transferred and distributed its deformation from the berthing loads
to the whole apron section.
The transferred longitudinal deformations from one dilation part to the adjacent ones
were negligible and no significant forces were imposed on them.
Due to the small-strain characteristic stiffness-strain behaviour of the enclosed soil the
application of the HSsmall model can be considered.
6.6
Conclusion of the structural behaviour based on
the performance model
Based on the above detailed analysis, the following conclusions can be drawn from the
numerical modelling:
The analysed quay system is a stiff cofferdam like structure with appropriate bear-
ing capacity;
The resulted displacements with regards to the quay wall system are relatively
small within the tolerated deformation requirements;
Under the apron section, due to the robust quay structure, there are no significant
loads subjected to the soft soil;
Under the port area significant swelling could occur, which can lead to failure;
Due to the loaded storage area larger settlements can be observed in case of the
back wall;
Different soil investigation and tests should be used at the port area or at the
storage area depending on the future stress path of the specimen. For instance,
in case of the port area, where the extensive matter causes failure swelling tests,
extensive triaxial test should be performed. Whereas, for the storage/ quay areas
unconsolidated undrained (UU) and consolidated undrained (CU) triaxial tests
would be useful to define the short and long-term undrained behaviour of the soil.
==PAGE:121==
6.6 Conclusion of the structural behaviour based on the performance model
103
The vertical plate anchor system is not effective in terms of interlocking, or as a
continuous anchor system;
There is no significant decrement in the front wall’s deformation or bending mo-
ments due to the plate anchor system;
The stiff beam frame (capping and transverse beams) govern the load distribution
and the deformation characteristic of the apron section;
There are no significant transferred effects between the different dilation pieces
due to longitudinal loads along the quay wall.
Consequently, the application of the vertical plate anchors is not recommended. More-
over, significant compression can appear within the plate anchors, due to the settlement
and deformation differences between the front and the back wall. This phenomenon can
lead to local interlocking failure, buckling of the anchors and additional horizontal loads
on the walls.

7.3.1
The effect of creep
As it was mentioned above the effect of creep was limited, as in most of the cases the

the soil. In order to understand these effects the following issues are discussed:
The effect of ageing with creep;
The effect of creep during swelling.
As it was mentioned previously (Section 4.3.3.1) an ageing plastic nil phase was applied
for the SSC model with the time duration of 1000 years. Without this calculation phase
(or application of OCR>1.0 - Eq. 4.18) the SSC soil would be considered as a new de-
posit. In that case, overpredicted displacements would take place during the loading
and consolidation phases.

7.4
Conclusion of the comparative modelling
As you may see from this chapter an extended analysis was made regarding different
structural assumptions and constitutive soil models with respect the soft soil conditions.
It was found and detailed in Section 7.1, that the connection type (hinged or fixed)
between the apron part’s deck and the storage area’s deck part can influence the over-
all behaviour of the structure. The order of displacements was changed also with the
deformation characteristic of the apron section. However, the plate anchor system was
remained insufficient.
In Section 7.2 the effect of the interlocking process was examined with more details. As
a comparison was made with and without interlocking, it was found, that a significant
positive effect can achieved in terms of deformation and bending moment reduction of
the front wall, but just after the excavation phases.
As soon as some vertical loads appeared on the apron section it cancelled out the effect of
interlocking and there was no more difference between the with or without interlocking
cases.
In Section 7.3 the normally consolidated soft clay (with high creep rate) was modelled
with the three different soil models. The purpose was to show the effect of the different
approaches, for instance:
when a dedicated soft soil model is used, like the Modified Cam-Clay and in this
way the creep is neglected;
when the effect of creep is taken into account with the Soft Soil Creep model;
when the parameters are aligned and the simple linear-perfectly plastic Mohr-
Coulomb soil model is used.
It was found that the overall behaviour of the plate anchored system with the same
structural assumption was not changed by the constitutive soil models (Figure 7.10-
7.12). However, significant differences were observed in case of the settlements under the
storage area and regarding the swelling behaviour at the port area, under the excavation.
It was concluded, that in case of the Modified Cam-Clay model almost the whole soft
soil strata reached its yield criteria, thus way larger plastic deformations occurred during
the consolidation phase after loading. Contrary to the SSC and MC soil models where
the elastic state was dominant. In addition, due to the special behaviour of the MC
soil model in case of undrained consolidation (Section 4.2.1) way smaller displacements
were reached. Thus, the MCC model overpredicts, the MC soil model underestimates
the possible displacements.
The effect of creep was also examined, because of the significant creep rate of the par-
ticular normally consolidated clay. It was found, that because of the ageing procedure
(Section 4.3.3.1 and Section 7.3.1) in which creep causes changes in the stress state,
the yield surface was extended. Thus, during the examined loading cases the NC clay
==PAGE:139==
7.4 Conclusion of the comparative modelling
121
(modelled with SSC soil model) was mostly in elastic state. Because of the absence of
the plastic deformations the effect of creep was not prominent at the apron section and
the storage area (area which were subjected to compression).
However, pronounced plastic deformations occurred due to the excavation (unloading)
at the port area. Due to swelling and the high creep rate the expansive deformations
resulted in failure in case of the SSC model. This phenomenon was just partly observed
with the MCC soil model and just locally found with the MC soil model.
Therefore, it can be concluded that the in case of normally consolidated soft clay with
high creep rate the Soft Soil Creep can ensure better estimation for the settlements where
compression is the main issue, if the ageing of the soil was taken into account. Meanwhile
the MCC model overpredicts, the MC soil model underestimated it (Figure 7.14b).
Furthermore, the unloading characteristic, which can be critical in case of the excava-
tion part, can be properly modelled by the SSC. This swelling phenomenon partly can
be captured by the MCC model as well, but it is not appropriate with the MC soil model.
Consequently, the effect of creep is important and the neglect of it should be carefully
considered. As it was shown, in case of undrained consolidation under significant vertical
loads more prominent plastic deformations can appear in case of the MCC model, than
with a properly matured SSC soil model (by ageing or OCR).
In order to reach similar response with the MCC model a slightly larger OCR shall be
used (OCR = 1.3 −1.6) for NC clay as it was also suggested by Brinkgreve [102]. Fur-
thermore, pre-overburden pressure (POP) can be applied as well instead of larger OCR
value. In that case, similar elastic response can be reached, which can confirm the usage
of the MCC model.
Furthermore, in case of the port area, where failure can occur due to swelling after
excavation (unloading). The effect of creep can be even more influential. It was shown,
that similar heave was observed in case of the MCC and SSC model. Meanwhile the MC
soil model underestimates it with almost 50 %. It means that instead of MC soil model,
the MCC with its initial properties (OCR = 1) can be a suitable estimation with failure
close yield state.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion and
recommendations
Within this study the concept of the plate anchored quay wall system was examined by
numerical models. As it was show in Table 2.4 these models were the performance model,
in which the Modified Cam-Clay model was employed and the entire dilation piece of
the structure was modelled. In addition, comparative models were also used, which were
developed to simulate various constitutive soil models and different structural assump-
tions. Based on the defined objectives (Section 2.3 ) and the results of the numerical
analysis the following conclusion can be drawn.
As a nil step the idealization of the original, complex structure was necessary, in order to
develop an accurate model, with sufficient modelling parameters (calculation time, nu-
merical stability, etc.). It was found during the model development, that the Modified
Cam-Clay model is the most numerically stable, thus it was used for the performance
model to asses the overall structural behaviour and soil-structure interaction.
With regards to the overall performance of the structure it was found that the apron
section was extremely robust, because of the stiff beam frame (capping and transverse
beams) on top. The quay system itself acted like a cofferdam and showed small defor-
mations and settlements, because of the appropriate deep pile foundation of the front
and back walls in the dense sand layer.
Because of the nature of the structure, the vertical loads were effectively distributed and
transferred to the sand layer, without significant effect on the normally consolidated soft
clay. Furthermore, as the structure was highly robust with the aforementioned upper
beam frame, the horizontal and longitudinal displacements were negligible with regards
to the whole structure. However, it was observed, that in case of the assumption of fixed
deck connections, larger settlements appeared at the back wall.
As a key aspect of the structural concept, the performance of the plate anchor system
was analysed with details. The efficiency was assessed with regards to the interlocking
process and the plate anchors as well. It was found that the interlocking and continuous
support by the plates could reduce significantly the deformations and bending moments
of the front wall after the excavation phase. However, once vertical loads were applied
on the apron section, it cancelled out the effect of the interlocking process and the pre-
==PAGE:141==
8 Conclusion and recommendations
123
tension of the plates. Furthermore, because of the modest horizontal displacements and
the settlement differences of the walls, the plate anchors were mainly compressed. At
the end it was found that the plate anchors were not efficient as an anchor system. Thus,
the application of them is not suggested.
With regards to the soft ground condition a number of model analyses were performed
through the comparative models. With these simulations the effect of the Modified Cam-
Clay (MCC), the Soft Soil Creep (SSC) with high creep rate and the Mohr-Coulomb
(MC) soil model were evaluated.
With respect to the overall behaviour (apron sections deformation, crane rails displace-
ments, horizontal movements, etc.) of the structure, there was no difference between the
various soil models because of the robust nature of the quay system. However, promi-
nent differences appeared at the stress state analysis and in case of settlements during
the undrained consolidation phase after loading.
It was found, that in case of the MCC with OCR = 1.0 almost the whole soft soil strata
became plastic and reached its yield criteria. Meanwhile, in case of an appropriately
matured SSC (ageing calculation step was applied) and MC soil models the normally
consolidated soft clay was in an elastic state. As a consequence, there was no significant
effect of the creep (as there were no plastic deformations in case of SSC). Moreover, the
settlements under the storage area were way larger with the MCC model.
Significant difference was found at the port area under the excavation, where swelling
took place. It was found that in case of the SSC model, due to the influence of the high
creep rate on the unloading stress-strain characteristic failure was reached. Meanwhile,
with MCC model a failure-close yield state was achieved.
All in all, it was concluded, that in case of a properly matured SSC soft soil with high
creep rate the MCC soil model can ensure a good alternative modelling solution for the
plate anchored quay wall system, if proper OCR values are used. In addition, it was
found that the MC soil model is not appropriate, as it underestimates the swelling and
overestimates the settlements at the consolidation phase.
Through the detailed numerical model analysis it was found that the plate anchored
quay wall structure has several issues, which make the feasibility and usability quite
questionable. However, the main criteria and tolerance requirements of the quay wall
were fulfilled.
But, because of the inefficient anchor system and the difficult construction procedure
the traditional anchorage systems for such cofferdam type structural design is rather
recommended.
==PAGE:142==
8 Conclusion and recommendations
124
Recommendations for future work
With regards to the main conclusion that the plate anchored system is not suggested,
further analysis is recommended based on the current study, but applying different struc-
tural concept.
As a future work, instead of the plate anchorage system, the modelling of a combination
of the current structure with a multi-anchored solution (horizontal anchors at different
levels, instead of the plate anchors) would be an interesting option.
As soft ground conditions combined with a marine environment can be highly sensitive
for seismic events a dynamic analysis of the above mentioned quay structure would en-
sure extremely valuable results. Especially, because the number of these kinds of quay
wall analyses is very limited.
It is also recommended to use different constitutive soil models, which can handle not
just the time dependent behaviour (like Soft Soil Creep), but also the anisotropic char-
acteristic of the normally consolidated soft soils. Possible options can be the S-CLAY1,
NGI-ADP, or Creep-SCLAY1 soil models. In this way, a more realistic analysis can
be made with valuable benchmark results with respect to the various constitutive soil
models.
Finally, as the modelling procedure is more a process, than an exact prediction it is
highly recommended to verify the numerical modelling results through back analysis
based on measurements from case studies.

A.2 Description of the structural element types in Plaxis 3D

A.2
Description of the structural element types in Plaxis
3D
Within Plaxis 2D (Plaxis 2D 2016) and Plaxis 3D (Plaxis 3D AE.02) newest releases
there are several element types, which can be used to simulate the various structural
parts of a quay structure.
In this chapter these elements are detailed briefly in order to ensure an overview of the
different modelling options in Plaxis.
Modelling of structures with volumetric elements, which option can be used in case
of piles, deck and back wall. This is the most realistic, but the less effective mode for
modelling. At this case a pile can be modelled with its real volume applying a linear
elastic material model and non-porous drainage type. The main properties are the unit
weight (γ), the Young-modulus (E) and the Poisson’s ratio (ν).
Plate elements can be also an appropriate solution for different structural parts like
the deck, back wall as these elements are used to model thin, two-dimensional structures
with significant bending stiffness [53a]. However, it has to be mentioned, that with the
appropriate parametric settings it is possible to model piles with plate elements. This
was a usual situation in case of pile rows in 2D plain strain models, nowadays embedded
pile row can be applied (see below).
The plate itself is a surface element, which is composed with 6-node triangular plate
elements with 6 degree of freedom per nodes after meshing. It is important to note,
that the plate elements are based on the Mindlin’s plate theory [55], which allows plate
deformations and deflection with regards to shearing as well as bending. The possible
changes in the length due to axial forces can be also captured with plate elements [53a].
The main parameters are the equivalent plate thickness (d), the unit weight (γ), the
Poisson’s ratio (ν), and in case of isotropic plate the Young-modulus (E1).
However, the option to set anisotropic plate element is also available. This was indis-
pensable in case of sheet pile walls modelling, where the stiffness and inertia parameters
are way different with regards to the horizontal and vertical directions. The meaning
of all plate parameters and forces, bending moments, which are relating to them are
summarised in Figure A.2.
Beam element is also available in Plaxis, which is basically a line element usually used
to model slender structures with flexural, or bending rigidity. It is build up after mesh-
ing from 3-node line elements with 6 degree of freedom. These element are based on the
Mindlin’s beam theory [55], thus deformations are possible due to both shearing and
bending. Moreover, the axial deformations and element’s length change is also included
when axial forces are applied. Although, it has to be noted that beam element can
==PAGE:156==

Figure A.2: Plate parameters with the relating forces and bending moments [53c]
not sustain torsion [53a]. The main input parameters in case of beam elements are the
cross-sectional area (A), the unit weight (γ), the Young-modulus (E) and the second
moment of inertia with regards to the local ’y’ and ’z’ axis (I2, I3).
An additional special element type is the embedded pile element in order to model
piles, rock bolts, etc. This type of structural object is made from beam elements based
on the embedded beam approach [56] with special interface elements in order interacts
with the sub-soil. In this way the interaction at the pile skin and its tip can be modelled.
Briefly, the embedded beam is considered as a beam crossing a 10-node tetrahedral el-
ement with an arbitrary direction at any place. Due to the beam element three extra
nodes are taken place within the 10-node tetrahedral. For further details and theoretical
background please see [53b] and [56].
With this element a pile can be simulated. Around the embedded pile element the shaft
region and the foot region under it the soil stresses are forced to remain elastic. In
Figure A.3 the schematic embedded pile (a) is shown with the actual finite element dis-
play as well (b). Moreover, the different resistances; shaft and tip resistance, are input
properties [53a], which ensure great control on the bearing capacity of the piles.
This element type has plenty advantages in 2D and 3D application as well:
in 2D the embedded pile row option can simulate piles in a more realistic manner
(pile and soil can move independently);
it ensure efficient finite element modelling option for pile foundations;

Additionally the interface elements has to be mentioned, because with these mod-
elling components the appropriate soil-structure interaction can be captured. This is
usually the case for soft soil ground conditions or for those type of structures, where the
effect of shaft friction is significant.
Interfaces ensure that relative displacements can appear between the structure and the
soil, like slipping and gaping. Without them the soil and the structure are tied together
[53a]. When interfaces are applied node pairs are created between the structure and the
soil. The interaction between the structural element and the soil is modelled through
these node pairs and with elastic-perfectly plastic springs in between them. One of these
springs is responsible to model the gaping, the other models the slip displacements [53c].

The interface can be rigid, which means that Rint=1.0. This Rint represents the ratio
between the interface strength and the surrounding soil strength.
Usually, this value is less than one, because the interface is weaker and more flexible,
than the soil around. For appropriate value, there are several recommendation in the
literature [63] [64] and standards [38] [65]. Some typical ranges are listed below with
regards to the interaction of different soil types and structural materials:
clay-steel structure: Rint=0.5-0.67;
sand-steel structure: Rint=0.6-0.67;
sand/ clay-concrete structure: Rint=0.7-0.8.
However, these Rint parameters are just recommended values. With regards to the differ-
ent constructional methods and specific soil conditions Rint shall be based on engineering
judgement.
