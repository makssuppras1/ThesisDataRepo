ABSTRACT
Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) are critical for integrating renewable energy and provid-
ing grid support, but their operation poses safety challenges, notably the risk of thermal runaway.
This thesis proposes a data driven methodology to assess the safety of an operating utility scale
lithium ion BESS. The first objective is to process routine operational cell level data (currents,
voltages, temperatures) into a safety indicator that quantifies how safely the battery cells are
operating. This metric is designed to be stored, analyzed, and interpreted by non technical end
users, enabling broader accessibility. The second objective is to develop a live monitoring model
that processes real time cell data to detect early signs of thermal runaway, allowing for timely
intervention and risk mitigation.
The State of Safety (SoS) function is defined based on a foundational study , yielding a score be-
tween 0 and 1 (or 0% to 100%) where 1 indicates normal safe conditions, values between 1 and
0.70 indicate a mild abuse state, and values below 0.7 indicate a critical abuse state requiring in-
tervention. The SoS model was implemented and applied to historical data from an operational
30 kW/79 kWh BESS, and its outputs were validated by checking known threshold events and
performing sensitivity analysis.
The Local Outlier Factor (LOF) is a data driven unsupervised machine learning model that was
used to flag statistical outliers in the cell level data, capturing some subtle anomalies that could
trigger possible thermal runaway events. In the lack of suitable databases to test and do the
parametrization of the model, artificial anomalies where introduced to historical cell level databases,
in the form of voltage drops and abnormal increase of cells’ temperature.
In summary, the thesis demonstrates that SoS can extract behavioral patterns of battery cells’ op-
erational data, promoting predictive maintenance, while LOF model can enhance battery safety
management by continuously monitoring for abuse conditions and informing operators in real
time. The combined insights from the SoS indicator and LOF anomaly detection suggest a poten-
tial for improved early warning systems. The work meets its objectives of developing a quanti-
tative safety assessment tool for BESS operations and opens avenues for future research, such as
refining the SoS model with inter variable dependencies and integrating machine learning tech-
niques for robust anomaly detection. Ultimately, this approach can help the PowerLabDK extend
their setup to a larger scale, in the island of Bornholm, ensuring less equipment stress, avoiding
hazardous events, and improving the overall safety and reliability of BESS.

INTRODUCTION
The last years we have seen a large expansion on Renewable Energy assets being build and op-
erated throughout the world. According to International Energy Agency’s report for renewables
in 2024 [1], more than 2,100 GW of renewable capacity was added globally, and 2.7 times higher
is expected to be added until the end of 2030, accumulating up to 9,760 GW.
Figure 1.1: Power generation by energy source in Petawatts-hours per year [2]
1.1
Electrical system green transition
Renewable energy sources (Solar, Wind, Hydropower, Geothermal, Bioenergy, Tide, Wave) are
already representing a big amount of the energy to the electricity mix and their contribution
both to electricity and total energy mix is expected to grow significantly as it can be observed in
Figure 1.1. According to IEA [3] fossil fuels represented 80% of the energy mix for 2030, but it
is expected that clean energy (renewables plus nuclear) will meet 40% of the global demand by
2030. If the energy mix is deconstructed and a more specific look is taken into electricity mix,
then it can be observed already in 2024 renewable energy sources covered more than 15% of the
demand [4], while in specific days and countries, like Spain, there where hours that renewables
covered the total consumption demand.
The clean energy transition should be done quickly, but also carefully and well organized at the
same time. The development and construction of renewable energy assets is progressing faster
than the electricity grid expansion can handle. A lot of phenomena of electrical energy being
curtailed are already observed because transmission lines can’t handle the load of photovoltaic
plants (PV) production that are all geographically closely located. This has multiple negative
consequences as not only is the energy curtailed which forces other, not clean, energy assets

1.2
Flexibility and BESS
1.2.1
Flexibility on the electricity market
The answer to the problem of unsynchronized expansion of renewables assets and electricity grid
expansion is either the faster construction of new power lines, or enhancement of the current
power lines, or the addition of extra flexibility on the grid through flexible assets which can alter
their consumption production/consumption profiles. These flexible assets, like industrial/domes-
tic demand response, heat pumps and electric vehicles charging can change their production/-
consumption profiles to help with the congestion, but they are still affected by their geographical
location, meaning they could also be impacted by grid congestion, also they can make slight ad-
justments to their profiles but might not be able to cover the whole imbalance in the electricity
market schedule, and most importantly they can’t store energy to utilize in a different hour of the
day. The last point can be succeeded by assets that can store energy and utilize it in another time
in order to help the system balance the grid imbalances caused by generation not matching con-
sumption every hour, thus help the system deal frequency deviations, and from the perspective
of the asset developer it can have economic benefits coming from providing ancillary services or
from arbitrage trading.
1.2.2
Energy storage assets
They are multiple utility scale energy storage assets reviewed, used, developed right now in the
world which can be categorized based on the type on the state of energy they store electricity,
such as thermal, electrochemical, electrostatic, electromagnetic, mechanical, chemical and ther-
mal. Utility scale describes the storage assets that are used as stationary storage to directly charge
or discharge in the electricity grid and participate in electricity markets.
Pumped hydro storage is currently the cornerstone of the global storage mix, but due to the
outstanding expansion of battery energy storage systems (BESS) the balance is expected to change
in the coming years. As it is depicted in Figure 1.2, BESS will overtake the market by 2023. The
most prominent, developed and market ready technology among BESS is lithium ion batteries (Li
ion), other researched BESS technologies are Sodium Ion, Lead Acid, Lithium Sulfur, Metal air,
Redox flow, Metal Ion, Solid state and many more[5].

1.3
Thesis Objective
The thesis focuses on the operational side of Li ion BESS and to provide an analysis on low level
data, for the purpose of this investigation the setup at PowerLabDK, at Lyngby (DTU), will be
utilized. This is because the setup at PowerLabDK is a small scale, one single battery rack of the

Low level data describes the data arising from the operational condition of cells inside the battery,
these are going to be used for preventing maintenance of the battery and prevention of failures
and thermal runaways. To cover these objectives the thesis will try to answer these questions:
• What factors contribute to cell underperformance, and what potential consequences can
arise as a result?
• How can faulty cells be identified and be replaced before failing?
• Can these results be reported in a simplified format that allows non technical users to
understand and assess them?
• What is the best monitoring method, and can machine learning help for live monitoring of
BESS?
• Can the methods be copied for the Bornhold setup?

BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK
2.1
BESS capabilities and limitations
Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) store and release electrical energy by converting it into
chemical energy and then back into electricity. In lithium ion batteries specifically, this process
involves lithium cations Li+ moving through an electrolyte between the two electrodes, while
electrons flow through an external circuit to generate electric current. This electrochemical de-
sign enables high efficiency and rapid response to grid demands.
One of the main advantages of lithium ion batteries is their fast dynamic performance. They
can react to frequency deviations within milliseconds, making them ideal for ancillary services
like frequency regulation and spinning reserve. Additionally, they offer one of the best tradeoffs
between energy and power density. Batteries can discharge large amounts of energy quickly, all
while maintaining a relatively compact size and low weight. Recent developments in pouch cell
technology are reported to achieve energy capacities exceeding 500 Ah [6], further pushing the
performance boundaries.
BESS units are also modular and scalable, meaning they can be deployed across a wide range
of applications, from residential and commercial backup to utility scale operations. They can be
charged using excess renewable energy, helping to balance intermittent sources like wind and
solar and improve overall grid reliability.
Despite their strengths, BESS still face several critical limitations. One of the most significant is
their lifetime, which averages around 15 years [7], depending heavily on usage patterns and the
type of services they provide [8]. Frequent cycling, high charge/discharge rates, and exposure
to extreme temperatures can all accelerate battery degradation. Over time, this leads to a loss of
usable capacity and reduced efficiency.
Another major concern is safety. The inherent chemistry of Li ion cells, combined with compact,
high energy density packaging, introduces the risk of thermal runaway. This occurs when inter-
nal faults or external abuse trigger uncontrollable heat generation, potentially leading to fire or
explosion[9]. What makes this even more problematic is that lithium fires are extremely difficult
to extinguish. Traditional firefighting methods, such as water or oxygen suppression, are largely
ineffective. In many documented incidents, fire authorities have opted to let battery fires burn
out rather than attempt suppression, due to the high risk and limited benefit of intervention.

2.2
BESS Architecture
Figure 2.1: BESS component diagram [10]
Nowadays, BESS is containerized to be more easily transported and controlled, thus offering a so-
lution that incorporates the essential electrical, mechanical and chemical parts inside a single con-
tainer, eventually making logistics easier and driving the cost down. If the BESS is broken down,
following the hierarchy from system to cell, then the part that only refers to storing the energy
can be described by the following parts, the System, followed by the Racks, the Modules and
in the lower level the Cells. Cells can be manufactured in a cylindrical, prismatic or pouch shape
and include the chemical and electronic components necessary for energy output/input[11]. In
addition to the storage components, a BESS also requires essential electronic systems such as a
Battery Management System (BMS), power conversion systems (PCS), and protection circuits to
monitor cell performance, regulate charging and discharging, ensure safe operation, and com-
municate with external control systems.
As mentioned before all the parts of the battery include a level of electronic monitoring, called
"Battery Management System" or "BMS" for short, and its role is to manage the performance
and the safety of the battery. BMS on system level monitors power inputs and outputs, external
conditions and it’s responsible for cooling, ventilation and fire suppression. In the lower levels of
the battery, BMS is monitoring for voltage, local temperatures and is trying to balance the State
of Charge of the cells. Also, future container models try to make enclosures fire resistant down
to module level, so if a fire erupts in a cell, then it won’t spread beyond the specific module, only
destroying the cells within this specific module and not the whole battery. Same goes for racks
and the system/container, since there is no other effective way of extinguishing a lithium fire
rather than letting it burn down, so it would be better to be contained inside the enclosure[12].

2.3
Battery faults & safety
2.3.1
Thermal runaway
Battery failures can lead to thermal runaway, where rising temperature and internal pressure
cause gas release, fire, or explosion. Thermal runaway, according to Huang et al. [13], can be
divided into 5 stages:
→First stage
Voltage gradually rises as lithium ions move from cathode to anode without major side
reactions. Temperature increase and gas generation are minimal.
→Second stage
Voltage rises sharply due to lithium plating on the anode. Lithium deposition thickens
the Solid Electrolyte Interphase (SEI) layer, raising internal resistance and temperature.
Electrolyte is oxidized, gases are generated, and cells begin to bulge slightly.
→Third stage
The voltage peaks as cathode potential increases, accelerating electrolyte oxidation and
gas production, leading to significant cell swelling. Elevated temperatures cause SEI film
decomposition, triggering exothermic reactions and further heat buildup.
→Fourth stage
Voltage drops sharply due to cathode material changes and intense side reactions consum-
ing lithium. Heat rises rapidly, leading to separator dissolving. Pressure buildup triggers
safety valves (in prismatic cells) or film rupture (in pouch cells), releasing hot gases and
temporarily suppressing temperature spikes.
→Fifth stage
The separator ruptures completely, causing numerous internal short circuits. The cell loses
its electrochemical function and behaves like a high-resistance resistor. Heat and gas gen-
eration surge, leading to violent rupture, fire, or explosion. Pouch cells, lacking a safety
valve, tend to fail more violently than prismatic cells. The process ends once combustible
materials are consumed and temperatures drop.
2.3.2
System level faults
According to EPRI report [14], the incidents regarding the BESS can be divided into 3 categories,
Balance Of System (BOS) failures, faults arising from Controls and faults from Cells & Mod-
ules. An important observation is that in many thermal runaway incidents resulting in BESS
fires, the root cause of ignition was never conclusively identified. This underscores the critical
need for comprehensive data acquisition and, more importantly, for analyzing and preserving
the most relevant operational data. Collecting high resolution measurements, such as voltage
and temperature for every cell at 1 second resolution, would indeed offer detailed insight, but
over a 15 year operational lifetime, the volume of data would become extremely large and im-
practical to store or review in its entirety. Therefore, effective data selection, compression, and
interpretation are essential for enabling meaningful diagnostics and long term safety analysis.

European & Danish standards
Standards define the safety, performance, interoperability, fire prevention, and compliance crite-
ria for Li Ion based BESS installations. Elaborating on some important standards from Table 2.1,
UL 9540A governs fire propagation testing, NFPA 855 addresses fire protection of stationary

plete BESS, the coordinated application of these standards helps ensure safe, robust, and certifi-
able deployment in both grid connected and behind the meter scenarios. Many of these standards
are constantly being updated, always being in a state of development by following the science
trends [15], and cross reference each other, highlighting their interconnection.

Denmark
A comprehensive report by DBI and Brand og Sikring (2025) [17] investigates the state of regula-
tory preparedness and fire safety guidance for battery energy storage systems (BESS) in Denmark,
with particular attention to the country’s two main electricity zones, DK1 (Western Denmark)
and DK2 (Eastern Denmark). The study, based on the reports from Danish Emergency Manage-
ment Agency (DEMA), reveals a significant regulatory gap, as Denmark currently lacks a unified
national framework tailored to the risks and operational characteristics of lithium ion BESS. In-
stead, developers and operators must rely on an inconsistent mix of local fire code interpretations,
building regulations, and references to international standards, such as NFPA 855, UL 9540A, and
IEC 62933, which are not yet formally adopted into Danish practice.
Another critical gap in Denmark’s legislation on BESS installations is the absence of clear rules
and guidelines for monitoring battery systems. Proper monitoring is essential to ensure timely
maintenance and to prevent thermal runaway events. Current legislation not only gives vague
and insufficient instructions on how to manage fires or prevent their spread, but it also lacks any
meaningful guidance on how to avoid reaching the point of thermal runaway in the first place.
2.4
Cell chemistry & faults
Batteries incorporate carbon as their negative electrode, or anode, and commonly LFP, NMC
as their positive electrode, or cathode. Lithium ions move around the electrodes, through the
electrolyte, leaving the positive electrode, passing through the separator, and inserting the car-
bon structure (commonly graphite) when charging, the opposite procedure is happening while
discharging. The role of the separator is to allow only lithium ions to pass through it, prevent-
ing electrons from moving through the electrolyte, thus forcing them to go through the external
circuit.
Most of the safety issues arise from the deposition of lithium ions in the graphite/electrolyte
interface, forming so called dendrites. Nevertheless, this interface, called Solid Electrolyte Inter-
face (SEI), is the reason that are working, since it supports the anode structure. The dendrites can
cause puncturing of the sealants, called mechanical deformation, and short circuit phenomena,
electrons running through the electrolyte rather than the external circuit, eventually increasing
the temperature inside the cell and potentially leading to thermal runaway.
2.4.1
Short circuit
Short circuit can be divided into 2 categories based if it’s happening inside the cell, Internal Short
Circuit (ISC), or outside of the cell, External Short Circuit (ESC).
ISC occurs when the anode and the cathode of the cell have direct contact due to separator fail-
ure, electrically, thermally or mechanically induced. Other reasons for ISC include mistakes on

manufacturing and insertion of Cu particles which dissolve on the cathode and precipitate on
the anode[18]. ISC can lead to thermal runaway and subsequently to fire, 52% and 26% of fire
incidents among batteries are caused by ISC and ESC, respectively[19].
ESC is simpler in principle, caused by direct contact of the cell’s anode and cathode with a con-
ductor, yet has the same serious down effects as ISC, leading to increased temperatures than can
trigger ISC and thermal runaway[20].
2.4.2
Voltage
Voltage related sources of failures can be divided into overcharge, and overdischarge, caused by
exceeding the cut off upper and lower voltage, thus they can be referred as overvoltage and un-
dervoltage, respectively.
Overvoltage can severally damage and degrade the separator through multiple mechanisms, as
depicted in Figure 2.3, and lead to ISC and thermal runaway. Firstly, overvoltage can raise anode
potential, forcing excessive lithium deposit on the anode, increasing the SEI thickness and lead-
ing dendrite formation, that may penetrate the separator and trigger ISC[21]. Another method
of ISC triggering is caused by increased internal resistance, due to extreme de-lithiation of the
positive electrode and thicker SEI, may increase the temperature and eventually lead to separator
melting[22].
Figure 2.3: Overvoltage leading to ISC [23]
Undervoltage not only favors copper dissolution, but it can even reach negative values and reverse
cell polarity and cause an increase in temperature. The main failure reason is the penetration of
the separator, triggering ISC, invoked by the copper dendrites created by the dissolved copper
[24].

2.4.3
Temperature
The internal components of batteries begin to decompose when temperatures approach 130–
150 ◦C. The SEI layer on the anode starts to degrade around 80 ◦C, followed by electrolyte break-
down between 100 and 120 ◦C. Common separator materials like polyethylene and polypropylene
collapse around 135 ◦C. Once the electrolyte decomposes, flammable gases such as H2, CH4, C2H6,
and C2H4 are released, which can ignite at high temperatures. If the separator fails and internal
short circuits occur, the stored electrical energy is released rapidly, producing additional heat and
gases. These reactions accelerate the temperature rise and trigger the chain reaction that leads
to thermal runaway[25].
Low temperatures significantly increase cell resistance and polarization in Li ion batteries, lead-
ing to capacity loss and faster degradation during cycling. This is mainly due to hindered Li+
migration in the electrolyte, electrodes, and their interphases. At low temperatures, electrolytes
become more viscous, lowering ionic conductivity and increasing interfacial resistance. While
specially designed low temperature electrolytes can help, Li+ diffusion through thick electrodes
remains a limiting factor. Anodes are more affected than cathodes, often contributing more to
total cell resistance. Challenges like lithium plating, dendrite formation, and even separator pore
blockage from metal deposition may also arise, highlighting the importance of both electrode and
separator design in improving low-temperature performance[26].
2.4.4
State of Charge
A Li ion battery at a high state of charge (SoC) not only holds more stored energy but also exists in
a chemically more reactive condition. This elevated energy and reactivity significantly increases
the risk associated with failure modes such as thermal runaway. Several studies demonstrate that
cells at 100% SoC exhibit more severe thermal runaway characteristics than those at lower SoC
levels [27, 28]. Specifically, high SoC cells release more heat, reach higher peak temperatures,
and generate larger quantities of flammable gases during failure events.
Page 11 of 60
==PAGE:18==
MSc. Thesis Fekas
Furthermore, Xu et al. (2022)[29] reports that cells charged to 100% can self-heat more readily and
propagate failure to neighboring cells with greater ease compared to cells maintained at approx-
imately 50% SoC. A fully charged cell can eject a greater volume of high temperature, flammable
electrolyte during a fault event. This elevates the combustion energy released and increases the
likelihood of cascading thermal runaway within battery cells, and subsequently battery racks.
Additionally, the previous studies have also shown that maintaining a low state of charge can
help reduce the likelihood of thermal runaway or even eliminate it. As a result, maintaining
batteries close to 100% SoC under normal operating conditions increases the vulnerability of
BESS installations to thermal incidents triggered by localized overheating or faults. To enhance
safety, batteries should ideally be kept at a low SoC when idle.

METHODOLOGY
Fault diagnosis for lithium ion batteries primarily rely on threshold based methods. However, sur-
passing these thresholds often signals that a significant fault has already occurred. Importantly,
severe faults such as thermal runaway are usually preceded by extended periods of abnormal
electrochemical and thermal behavior. Early identification of these precursors could enable pre-
ventive interventions to enhance battery system safety. Even though, the highly nonlinear and
complex interactions between electrical, thermal, and aging processes in lithium ion batteries
complicate precise characterization of these abnormal states, still such states frequently manifest
as variations in voltage and temperature measurements [31]. To capture, analyze and document
such states 2 different methods as utilized in this thesis, "State of Safety" function which works
as an analysis and documentation method for abnormal states, and "Local Outlier Factor" unsu-
pervised monitoring which serves as a predictive algorithm for thermal runaway phenomena.

4.2
LOF online monitoring
Large differences between internal and external temperatures, along with delayed heat transfer,
can slow down safety responses like early warnings, cooling, and fire suppression. Therefore,
internal temperature should be used instead of external temperature as a more accurate early
warning indicator for thermal runaway.
For this part of the thesis, a 2 level monitoring model was developed to monitor the battery out-
side of the BMS function. This was deemed helpful for 2 reasons, first to ensure and validate that
the BMS integrated monitoring system is working as it is supposed to be, giving warnings and
shutting of the BESS if a value out of the limits is detected.
The second and most important reason is trying to predict anomalies in cell’s voltage and tem-
perature deviating from the nominal behavior and thus catching any thermal runaway incidents
before happening. According to [46] temperature gradients larger than 3◦C can cause perfor-
mance issued leading to unsafe operational conditions. Of course, the second step is hard to
accomplish and especially to verify since there are no publicly available data sets in literature
regarding timeseries cell level data before a thermal runaway incident.
So, in this thesis the goal is try to introduce some anomalies based on literature revue on past data
sets, then use the anomaly monitoring code on the modified set and try to capture the anomaly,
identifying in the procedure the best amount of data to do so, how quickly the anomaly should be
detected and the gradient that should be considered as anomalies that lead to thermal runaway.
For the purpose of predicting possible failures and thermal runaway incidents before happening,
different methods were evaluated based on machine learning. These can be categorized as follows
[47]:
1. Unsupervised Learning
Unsupervised learning is used to identify unusual battery behavior without needing la-
beled data. The model learns the normal operating patterns of the system and flags any
deviations, which may indicate faults or potential failures. This approach is particularly
valuable when there are no available training data sets, and for early detection of unknown
or rare faults that weren’t presented during training, if there was any.
2. Supervised Learning
Supervised learning is widely used in battery diagnostics to estimate the state of charge
(SOC) and state of health (SOH). These models are trained using labeled data, that means
that both the input (like voltage, current, and temperature) and the output (SOC or SOH)
data are known beforehand. If the training models and the behavior of the BESS is con-
sistent, they can accurately predict SOC or SOH based on new input data, but this is quite
difficult in practice since the BESS behavior is quite unpredictable due to its chemistry.
3. Deep Learning
Deep learning methods are believed to be useful for time series databases, since they are
like supervised learning with the difference that the model is trained again during the oper-
ation of the battery. They are effective in capturing long term patterns in battery behavior,
including charge and discharge cycles, thermal dynamics, and degradation. These models
can recognize complex and long-range dependencies in battery operation data.
Page 20 of 60
==PAGE:27==
MSc. Thesis Fekas
4. Reinforcement Learning
Reinforcement learning is a dynamic approach where the battery management system
learns optimal strategies by interacting with the different BESS components. Instead of
relying on labeled data, the reinforcement learning model makes decisions such as adjust-
ing charging rates or enabling cooling and learning from the results. This method aims to
extend battery life while maintaining safety during operation.
In this thesis, Unsupervised Learning is selected as the most suitable approach for the current
setup in PowerLabDK and potentially for the future deployment in Bornholm. This choice is
primarily driven by the lack of available operational cell level data, both from the PowerLabDK
system and from other comparable BESS installations, as the specific model under study is not
yet widely adopted in commercial applications. The possibility is that until the Bornholm setup is
online there won’t be enough operational cell level data to feed other types of machine learning.
There is also the situation that even a large amount of data was available, still the chance that
it would showcase any thermal runaway incident is quite low, since thermal runaway incidents
can’t be predicted and scheduled.
In general, Unsupervised Learning is deemed to be a good solution because each BESS is quite
different from each other, even if it concerns the same model. That is because BESS can consist of
thousands of cells, which can’t be identical due to very fine chemistry and components. Never-
theless, even if the cells were identical, it is quite difficult to have a lot of data of thermal runaway
incidents, because they happen only once and then the BESS in most of the cases is totally burned
down, so no more data can be extracted from the same model.

Upon detection the LOF is triggered and goes through the following procedures:
1. The affected pack is identified.
2. The system determines whether the pack is charging or discharging, based on current sign.
3. All voltage or temperature traces from that pack and mode are collected.
4. If fewer data, than the predetermined data sample size, are available, the system waits for
additional data.
5. LOF analysis is executed and continues until the mode changes, or an anomaly is found.
Inspired by Sun et al. (2022)[48], the LOF analysis quantifies deviations between a cell’s behavior
and that of its neighbors in the same pack.
1. Data smoothing: A Savitzky-Golay filter is applied to reduce noise in the time series.
Other data filters could be applied as well, like Moving Average, Median Filter or Kalman
Filter. In fact, the Kalman Filter was tasted alongside the Savitzky-Golay filter, and the
former resulted in weaker LOF scores.
2. Calibration: At each timestamp, the mean voltage/ temperature of all cells in the pack
under investigator is subtracted from each individual value.
3. Feature Extraction:
• Simplified Distance Metric (SDM) is computed for each cell, the calibrated voltage
or temperature curve is compared directly to the pack’s median curve by summing
the absolute differences between their respective values at each time step.
• Standard Deviation (STD) is computed from the original (uncalibrated) trace over
time.
4. LOF Calculation: The features [SDM, STD] are passed into the Local Outlier Factor
algorithm, which computes a score for each cell based on its deviation from the local neigh-
borhood. As the names implies the algorithm uses a local density based clustering, rather
than a global, which is more prone to
5. Parameterization:
• The number of neighbors (MinPts) is varied between 1 and 24 for voltage (the total
number of cells per pack) and between 1 and 12 for temperature (the temperature
measurements are involving 2 cells at the same time), and the optimal parameter is
defined.
• The LOF threshold is experimentally determined based on the sample size required to
catch the anomaly as early as possible.
A cell is considered anomalous if its LOF score exceeds the defined threshold.
The monitoring algorithm performs lightweight checks by reading only the last few lines of the
CSV during each cycle, avoiding memory overload as the file grows. If LOF is triggered:
• The analysis is applied across the individual charge/discharge cycle.

Limitations
All the scientific details about the characteristics of NMC cells are based on experiments and
research on low energy capacity cells, less than 50Ah, while the cells used in the specific setup
are around 111Ah, as explained in chapter 3. Also, all the modern containerized setups are using
lithium ion cells, mostly LFP, that have more than 200Ah capacity, newest development report
cells at 500Ah levels.
Regarding the location and the ambient temperature of the battery rack, since the battery is lo-
cated inside the PowerLabDK, it is protected from environmental elements, fluctuations in the
temperature as well as extreme temperatures that are more frequent the latest years. This consist
of a severe limitation on the temperature side, since there is difficult to observe abnormal tem-
peratures on the cells when the ambient temperature is controlled. On the other side if a high
temperature is observed, in a sterilized environment, then it’s a clear sign that should be taken
into mind and further investigated. Also, no seasonal patterns can be extracted from the analy-
sis of the data due to 2 reasons, firstly as explained before the battery is located in a controlled
environment. Secondly both the past data available and the ones generated and acquired during
the actuation of this master thesis, concern the period between April to June.
Concerning validation of the LOF monitoring model, there were no datasets available that com-

and cycling. Another problem with the available data sets is the age of the data, meaning the
data are about cells that are commercially outdated, most of the cells recorded in this sets have a

This specific setup doesn’t provide data down to cell level state of charge. What can be done is
for very high or very low state of charge for the battery, it can be assumed that the cells are also
in a very high or low state of charge respectively.

useful insights into emerging thermal trends. It is important to note that temperature measure-

21 and 22 of Pack 4 reached a temperature of 49,◦C. This was an isolated event, during which the
elevated temperature persisted for 3 minutes and 57 seconds.

5.1.4
Low temperature
During the analysis of low temperature data, a large number of cells experienced low SoS values.
All SoS warnings indicating that the battery was approaching the low temperature safety thresh-
old can be classified as false positives with a high degree of confidence, based on the following
observations:
1. All events that triggered low SoS values reported the exact same temperature, 0◦C.
2. These events occurred between May and July, a period during which no ambient tempera-
tures near 0◦C were recorded.
3. The battery system is installed indoors at PowerLabDK, where it is well shielded from
environmental temperature fluctuations. Even if ambient temperatures were close to 0◦C,
they would be unlikely to affect the battery cells directly.
The only plausible explanation for such readings would be an experimental setup deliberately
attempting to cool specific cells. However, this scenario can be confidently ruled out because
there are no records of such a test being conducted, and it would be practically infeasible to ap-
ply targeted subzero cooling to individual cells within the battery rack. Based on these factors,
all low temperature SoS events in this dataset are considered false.

5.2
LOF Analysis
For the Local Outlier Factor analysis, a code was created with the purpose of continuously run-
ning during the operation of the battery, monitoring deviations in temperature and voltage to
preemptively locate anomalies that could lead to thermal runaway. The final LOF code, and the
simple threshold code, can be found in Listing 1 and Listing 2 of the appendix. In their current
form they are able to run constantly during the operation of the BESS, but they need to be acti-
vated each time the battery is going online. This is happening because for the current setup the
battery was shut down when it wasn’t used.
With the code running as expected, the next step was to determine suitable parameters for ac-
curately identifying anomalous or faulty cells. This was done through offline testing using arti-
ficially injected anomalies. The parameters explored included the sample size used by the LOF
model, the number of neighbors used for the SDM, the type of filter applied to smooth raw data,
and alternative similarity measures beyond SDM. Each of these four parameters influenced the
resulting LOF scores and was evaluated based on how well it identified the correct anomalous
cell and how distinctly it differed from the cell with the second highest LOF score.
The results in the following sections represent the results for all the packs, where cells were scored
using LOF among the cells of the pack they belong to. This representation method was chosen to
avoid any false detection of an anomaly that could lead to shutting down of the battery and loss
of potential revenue. If only the cells inside the pack, that the artificial anomaly was inserted,
were studied, it would eventually backlash when another pack exhibited some very short change
that would trigger the LOF model, and subsequently scoring high LOF in the algorithm.
5.2.1

Sun et al. (2022)[48] for their LOF model chose Discrete Frechet Distance (DFD) as their sim-
ilarity measure. The DFD measure is using a dynamic programming [49] and thus it can be
computationally heavy. Even if this drawback is set aside, the Simplified Distance Metric (SDM)
is yielding better LOF scores, for the target cell, than DFD. As it can be observed from Figure 5.7,
SDM measure is scoring higher than DFD for most cases of neighbors.

5.2.3
Voltage anomalies
According to literature [51], when a cell is experiencing an event that could lead to thermal run-
away, the voltage will experience a voltage drop in the range of 50 to 150 mV for several seconds.
The earliest prediction of a thermal runaway incident captured with a voltage based method is
reported to be around 90 minutes, while other methods can predict it close to 15 minutes before
happening[52].
The goal is to try and capture the anomaly as early as possible, but still there should be enough
data to confirm that it is an actual anomaly, not a false signal. For this reason, it was chosen to
introduce an artificial anomaly, in a single cell, by reducing 50 mV for 300 seconds, meaning all
the values in a sample size of 300 consecutive seconds were reduced equally by 50 mV. It could
have been the case that is was chosen to reduce the voltage gradually on the target cell, by re-
ducing 0.16 mV per second, but there is not enough documentation reporting the exact voltage
behavior and given the BMS drawback of renewing data every 24 seconds, it was deemed as a
better choice just to reduce all the data by the same value.
The LOF model will be evaluated for voltage anomalies both at charging and discharging mode.
That is deemed necessary since a voltage drop is applied as an artificial anomaly, then it would
be expected that during discharging, when voltage values drop naturally because of the chemical
reactions inside the cell, the LOF scores would be lower. On the contrary, since the voltage
increases naturally during charging and then suddenly a voltage drop is introduced the LOF
scores would be higher.

5.2.4
Temperature anomalies
Introducing an artificial temperature anomaly, in the form of increasing temperature, was even
more difficult than introducing a voltage drop because of the difficulty to find relevant studies.
Most of the experimental research is focused on how the cell and the battery behave after a failure
triggers a thermal runaway event, rather than trying to quantify temperature behavior before an
incident. Research suggests an increase of 0.5 ◦C per second [52], but it is not clear how close is
this behavior before the thermal runaway incident.
Approaching the matter with a more conservative view and taking in mind the BMS data renewal
window of 24-25 seconds, it was decided to apply a 6◦C gradual increase every 24-25 seconds,
following the behavior of the BMS. The same charging dataset was used as in the voltage drop
tests.
The LOF scores, for an introduced temperature anomaly, within a sample size of 400 seconds,
experience abnormally high values across a lot of different cells which could not be attributed to
temperature anomalies, rather to model capability to analyze the data. For sample sizes of 600 and
800, the LOF scores of the target cell appeared more stable, as shown in Figure 5.15, suggesting
that a LOF threshold of 20 might be suitable for capturing the anomaly. However, this assumption
does not fully hold upon closer inspection.

5.3
Discussion
Through SoS function, recurring patterns were identified, including clusters of cells experiencing
similar stress levels. For instance, cells 13, 21, 22, and 24 of Pack 8 displayed isolated undervoltage
events, suggesting possible early stage faults. Similarly, the last 12 cells of Packs 4 and 7 consis-
tently showed negative deviations in overvoltage evaluations, even though they did not exceed
the 50◦C high temperature threshold. Notably, cells 21 and 22 of Pack 4 recorded the highest
number of high temperature events, while cells 1-12 of Pack 3 showed stable behavior with SoS
values consistently at 1.
Although the SoS framework evaluates parameters individually, its diagnostic value could be ex-
tended by considering the combined effects of multiple conditions occurring simultaneously. For
example, the coexistence of high voltage and high temperature at the same time,each lowering
the SoS independently, would pose a significantly greater risk if evaluated together (e.g., through
multiplication of SoS values). However, in this dataset, no such simultaneous events were ob-
served. Additionally, incorporating SoC as a third dimension in SoS calculations may enhance
predictive power, given the well documented relationship between SoC and the severity of ther-
mal runaway.
Regarding LOF, based on both the literature and this thesis’ results, voltage based monitoring gen-
erally offers faster anomaly detection than temperature based methods [52]. This is supported
by the clearer results observed in voltage anomalies throughout the experiments. Consequently,
it is recommended that the LOF model be tuned more closely to voltage monitoring behavior.
Considering the target cell LOF scores, baseline scores of surrounding cells, and the temporal
characteristics of the anomalies, the following configuration is proposed for LOF based monitor-
ing:
• LOF threshold: 15-20
• Number of neighbors: 6-8
This parameter range yielded satisfactory results for both voltage and temperature analysis. For
applications focused on anomaly detection within 5 minute intervals, a sample size of 400-600
seconds is recommended. Smaller sample sizes showed potential for earlier detection, however,
this approach is not without limitations, smaller windows reduce the standard deviation and the
SDM (Simplified Distance Metric), unless the change is substantial. In such extreme cases, simpler
threshold based methods would likely detect the anomaly anyway. Therefore, careful parameter
Page 43 of 60
==PAGE:50==

CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
This thesis began by analyzing the foundational aspects of lithium ion battery architecture and
electrochemistry, along with relevant safety standards. It also examined how battery cells behave
under extreme voltage, temperature and state of charge conditions, how such events contribute
to degradation, and how they can potentially lead to thermal runaway. These efforts address the
first two objectives of this thesis.
Then it introduced and evaluated two complementary methods for monitoring the operational
health of Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS), the State of Safety (SoS) function and the Local
Outlier Factor (LOF) model. Each method targets different aspects of system performance and
together they form a comprehensive approach to early fault detection, preventive maintenance,
and operational insight.
The State of Safety (SoS) is an empirical, rule based metric designed to evaluate the safety of op-
erational parameters such as voltage and temperature. It provides clear, interpretable feedback
by flagging cell behaviors that approach or exceed predefined safety thresholds. The function
can be further developed by introducing weights between the different SoS parameters tested, or
by adding new parameters such as state of charge and electrical current, if such data is available
at the cell level.
On the other hand, the Local Outlier Factor (LOF) model serves as a data driven, unsupervised
anomaly detection model, capable of identifying deviations without prior labeling. It proved par-
ticularly effective in detecting rapid and localized changes in voltage or temperature. However,
its main limitation is its reliance on signal variations if a faulty cell exhibits no significant spikes
or dynamic behavior (as in cases of steady voltage drops), the anomaly may be smoothed out
during preprocessing. This risk highlights the trade off between noise reduction and sensitivity
in real time monitoring.
Together, the two methods explored in this thesis directly address two key questions, whether the
results of battery cell analysis can be reported in a simplified format accessible to non technical
users, and whether a lightweight machine learning based monitoring method can be effectively
applied for real time diagnostics. Both the State of Safety (SoS) function and the Local Outlier
Factor (LOF) model demonstrate that this is indeed feasible. Due to their simplicity, low computa-
tional demands, and minimal integration requirements, these methods could be readily deployed
in the Bornholm setup without major hardware or software modifications. Additionally, their
adaptability makes them suitable for remote monitoring applications, where data bandwidth and
on site technical support may be limited.
