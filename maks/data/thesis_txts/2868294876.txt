Abstract
Achieving sustainability through design requires a focus on the early phases of design, where approx-
imately 80% of a product’s environmental proﬁle is determined. While some attention has been given
to prevent rebound effects (RE) (negative behavioural responses offsetting potential environmental
gains), limited focus has been given to the occurance and promotion of secondary beneﬁts (SB) (pos-
itive behavioural responses increasing potential environmental gains). This thesis aims to ﬁll the gap
in literature by exploring the ﬁeld of SB within sustainable design, how SB and RE interrelate, as well
as a relation to the behavioural design strategy; nudging to promote SB.
Following the Design Research Methodology, key behavioural mechanisms underlying SB were identi-
ﬁed through a systematic literature review alongside a synthesised relation to nudges. These insights,
together with existing knowledge of nudges to prevent RE, shape the development of a tool for design
engineers. The tool, as an interactive Excel, enables designers to identify relevant SB and RE mech-
anisms in their speciﬁc design cases and apply targeted nudges to promote sustainable behaviours.
The tool was tested with design engineers, RE and behavioural experts, and companies representa-
tives. The results demonstrated the tool’s usability and usefulness, although challenges remain of
robustness and guidance on mechanism identiﬁcation.
This work contributes to the ﬁelds of behavioral and sustainable design by providing practical strate-
gies (i.e. nudges) for preventing RE and promoting SB of sustainable design. Additionally, it highlights
the need for further research to build on this knowledge to understand, test, and validate the mech-
anisms and relations.
By bridging the gap between SB promotion, RE prevention, and nudging, this thesis lays the founda-
tion for more effective, robust, and sustainable design practises.

Deﬁnitions
Sustainable design: Sustainable design, also known as Design for Sustainability, focuses on creating
solutions that promote the well-being of people and the environment by minimising negative impacts
through their life cycle (Faludi et al., 2020).
Behaviural design: Behavioral design (design for behaviour change) focuses on understanding be-
havioural patterns and designing interventions aimed to achieve behaviour change. It builds on the-
oretical foundations of cognitive psychology, social psychology, and design (Nielsen et al., 2021).
Nudging: Nudges are simple, yet low-cost interventions that can alter individual’s behavior without
applying a force of hand or signiﬁcantly changing monetary incentives (Thaler & Sunstein, 2009).
Intervention: the intervention deﬁned by a systematic attempt to encourage change in a targeted
PEB (PEB1) (Truelove et al., 2014)
Rebound effects: Rebound effects are systemic or behavioural responses triggered by a sustainable
intervention, offsetting ca. 50% of the potential environmental gain (Brockway et al., 2021).
Behavioural mechanism underlying rebound effects: Behavioural mechanisms underlying rebound
effects are structures of causal processes that explain the occurance of rebound effects (Hedström &
Ylikoski, 2010).
Secondary beneﬁts: Secondary beneﬁts (Kjær et al., 2018) are behavioural responses triggered by a
sustainable intervention, increasing the potential environmental gains by PEBs not directly targeted
by an intervention (Truelove et al., 2014).
Secondary beneﬁt mechanism: A secondary beneﬁt mechanism, named after its mediator, is the
chain from intervention potentially inﬂuenced by a nudge →(targeted pro-environmental behaviour) →
mediator potentially inﬂuenced by a moderator →non-targeted pro-environmental behaviour →out-
come/secondary beneﬁts.
Mediator/trigger underlying secondary beneﬁt: Element that cause/mediates the feedback struc-
ture of a mechanism, determining its existence (Guzzo et al., 2024).
Moderator/driver underlying secondary beneﬁt: Element that inﬂuences/moderates the feedback
structure of the mechanism, determining its strength and direction (Guzzo et al., 2024), either by in-
directly affecting the non-targeted pro-environmental behaviour through the mediator or directly af-
fecting the non-targeted pro-environmental behaviour.
Product: A physical or non-physical good provided to the customer (Kjær et al., 2017)
PSS: Both product(s) and service(s) combined in a system (Kjær et al., 2017)
STS: Systems with high technical complexity, social intricacy, and elaborate processes serving societal
functions (De Weck et al., 2011)

Introduction
To achieve sustainability, focus on the early phases of design is important as approximately 80% of
a product’s environmental proﬁle is determined in this phase (McAloone & Bey, 2009). Nevertheless,
most of the potential environmental gains of sustainable design are not achieved due to rebound
effects (RE). RE are systemic or behavioural responses triggered by a sustainable intervention, off-
setting ca. 50% of the potential environmental gain (Brockway et al., 2021) or, in some cases, even
causing a net-negative effect (i.e. backﬁre) (Andrew & Pigosso, 2024). Many RE occur due to under-
lying behavioural mechanisms (described in Appendix A adapted from Loo and Pigosso (2024)). For
instance ‘moral licensing’, where an initial pro-environmental behavior (PEB) creates a perception of
moral entitlement and the collection of moral credits to perform a subsequent harmful behaviour,
e.g. “I drive an electric car, so it feels justiﬁed to go by plane to my holiday destination” (Loo & Pigosso,
2024).
Research thus far has mainly focused on determining the effect sizes of RE and therefore knowledge
on preventing them is limited. Although, recent studies have proposed that a suitable approach for
preventing the behavioural mechanisms underlying RE of sustainable design. This approach involves
the application of nudges (described in Appendix B) in the early phases of design, as both rely on be-
havioural theories. Nudges are simple, yet low-cost interventions that can alter individual’s behavior
without applying a force of hand or signiﬁcantly changing monetary incentives (Thaler & Sunstein,
2009). Within nudges, there are three predominant categories: Decision information (i.e. nudges that
target decision-relevant information without altering options), Decision structure (i.e. nudges that alter
the arrangement of options and the decision-making format), and Decision assistance (i.e. nudges that
provide decision assistance to achieve given intentions) (Appendix A). For instance, a nudge within De-
cision information is ‘loss framing’ (i.e. framing info as losses). This could be applied to an intervention
to prevent ‘moral licensing’ by adding messages to a user’s online shopping basket: “If you choose this
product, you will increase your environmental footprint by 10%”. As individuals prefer to avoid losses,
these messages could prevent the collection of moral credits and motivate the user to keep making
environmental choices.
While research exists to attempt to prevent RE to achieve greater potential sustainable gains (e.g. The
Circular Rebound Tool of Das et al. (2023) and RE/Nudge prevention Tool in Appendix B), limited focus
has been given to the occurrence of secondary beneﬁts (SB) (Kjær et al., 2018) of sustainable design.
SB are behavioural responses triggered by a sustainable intervention, increasing the potential envi-
ronmental gains by PEBs not directly targeted by an intervention (PEB2) (Truelove et al., 2014).
In the ﬁeld of psychology, these behaviours are referred to as ’positive spillovers’. Spillovers generally
relate to the transfer of behavioural changes from intervention to non-targeted PEBs (PEB2) (Truelove
et al., 2014), while SB extends that deﬁnition to encompass the outcome of PEB2 as well. As well as for
RE, SB can occur due to underlying behavioural structures, for example, ’preference for consistency’.
Individuals with a high preference for consistency strive to align their behaviours with past behaviours
and avoid mental conﬂicts of cognitive dissonance. This can lead to further PEBs and thereby SB, e.g.
“I compost and recycle at home, so now I feel compelled to do it at work as well to remain consistent in my
behaviours”. This behavioural connection reveals a possibility of applying behavioural design strate-
gies (e.g. nudges) to SB. For example, the ‘pre-commitment’ nudge (i.e. setting environmental goals)
can encourage ‘preference for consistency’ by allowing users to commit long- or short-term to keep-
ing their CO2 footprint below a set threshold. As individuals are more likely to behave in line with a
goal if committed beforehand, this can facilitate consistency and motivate the user to keep making
environmental choices to fulﬁl said goal (Appendix B).

As of today, there is limited consolidated knowledge of SB mechanisms and how they occur inde-
pendently, in relation to RE, and in relation to behavioural design. Therefore, a stronger focus on
exploring these relations is needed to achieve greater sustainability gains by both preventing RE and
promoting SB.
Building upon the knowledge and tool proposed in the paper ‘Nudging as a strategy to prevent be-
havioural RE in the early phases of design’ (2025) (see full paper in Appendix B), this thesis aims to
ﬁll the current gap in literature by exploring the ﬁeld of SB within sustainable design, how SB and RE
interrelate, as well as a potential relation to behavioural design strategies (e.g. nudges) to promote
SB. This knowledge of SB is to be merged with the existing understanding of RE and the RE/nudge
prevention tool to create design support(s) (i.e. tools for designers).

Explaining the higher order of impact(s) of intervention; since the student is saving water while
showering, they justify that it is okay to use the bathtub more often due to moral licensing, which
means the negative impact increases due to rebound effects. However, the student might have

of the RE (concurrent RE + SB). If no RE occurred beforehand, the negative environmental impact will
be smaller since only the effect of the secondary beneﬁt is present in this scenario. If a user already
has a strong environmental self-identity this can act as a moderator and strengthen the effect of the
SB, further decreasing the negative environmental impact.
It should be noted that although this ﬁgure visualises RE and SB as equal in size, research indicates
that the environmental gains of SB are relatively small (Geiger et al., 2021; Maki et al., 2019), and it is
known that RE can have an impact of offsetting ca. 50% of potential environmental gains (Brockway
et al., 2021). Concurrently, this stresses the need to uncover the potential of SB.
Secondary beneﬁts mechanism
In order to reinforce SB, it is important to understand how the mechanisms (i.e., underlying causal
structures) function. SB mechanisms differ from RE mechanisms as they are opposite in nature. In-
stead of an intervention leading to a negative behaviour and outcome (RE), an intervention leads to
a positive behaviour and outcome (SB). The chain of events for SB, including the effect of the nudge
on the intervention, is displayed in ﬁgure 2.
A SB mechanism, named after its mediator, consists of three parts:
1. The intervention deﬁned by a systematic attempt to encourage change in a targeted PEB (PEB1)
(Truelove et al., 2014), potentially inﬂuenced by a nudge.
2. The spillover pathway of the mediator(s) (i.e. element that cause/mediates the feedback struc-
ture of a mechanism, determining its existence (Guzzo et al., 2024), also known as a trigger) and
moderator(s) (i.e. element that inﬂuences/moderates the feedback structure of the mecha-
nism, determining its strength and direction (Guzzo et al., 2024), either by indirectly affecting
PEB2 though the mediator (A) or directly affecting PEB2 (B)).
3. The non-targeted PEB (PEB2) and related outcome of PEB2 in the form of measurable environ-
mental gains (i.e. SB) (Truelove et al., 2014).

Explaining with an example, an intervention of a SMART energy eﬃcient light bulb displaying mes-

General methodology
This thesis project applies the Design Research Methodology (DRM) (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009)
in an iterative process, going through four stages; Research Clariﬁcation (RC), Descriptive Study I
(DSI), Prescriptive Study and Descriptive Study II (DSII). This methodology provides a structured
approach for conducting design research. It is designed to not only produce academically valuable
results but turn these into a practically applicable design support – especially suited for this type of
project as the main research question (MRQ) of the thesis is "How can designers use behavioural design
strategies (e.g., nudging) in the early phases of design to promote secondary beneﬁts and prevent rebound
effects?". Using the DRM provides robust groundwork based on research before starting a design pro-
cess of developing a design support.
The ﬁrst stage, RC, aims to deﬁne the scope and goal of the project to formulate research questions
(RQs) in fulﬁllment of these, to be answered in the following stages and shape the output of the thesis.
This provides the groundwork for the second stage; DSI. This stage aims to elaborate the existing sit-
uation through a systematic literature review (SLR). During the SLR, the ﬁrst three RQs were answered:
RQ1: What are the behavioural mechanisms, mediators, and moderators underlying secondary beneﬁts of
sustainability-oriented design?
RQ2: What are the connections between secondary beneﬁts and rebound effects in existing literature?
RQ3: How can behavioural design promote secondary beneﬁts?
The knowledge and understanding of the existing situation from the SLR is analysed and synthesised
to answer the RQs. The insights from DSI accompanied by a comprehensive SLR on behavioural de-
sign (Appendix B) lead to the PS stage, with the aim of developing a design support to answer RQ4:
RQ4: How does behavioural design, secondary beneﬁts and rebound effects interrelate and how can this
be optimised in a tool?
To develop the design support, the Double Diamond process is applied within the PS, going through
the last three phases; (i) Deﬁne, (ii) Develop, and (iii) Deliver (TheDesignCouncilUK, 2023). Proceeding
to DSII, the Deliver phase continued by evaluating the tool to answer RQ5:
RQ5: How useful and usable is the combined tool and how can it be improved?
The tool is tested and improved in an iterative process with design engineers and various experts
within the research ﬁeld and industry. The success criterion is deﬁned as: preventing rebound effects
and promoting secondary beneﬁts in sustainable design. To answer this criterion, the measurable suc-
cess criterion is: ease of usability and usefulness of the tool.
Finally, the complete tool is presented in a new section. The overall process of the thesis can be seen
in ﬁgure 3.

3.1.3
Creating the mediator and moderator lists
Creating the mediator and moderator lists consisted of three parts; (1) sorting, (2) clustering, and (3)
categorising.
The relevant 59 papers led to the identiﬁcation of 86 mediators and 75 moderators. Sorting these
was an iterative process with continuous group discussions between the authors and expert on RE
and behavioural economics, Imke Van der Loo. Taking a starting point in the name and description
from the papers, the mediators and moderators were sorted and assigned umbrella terms in order
to create consistency and merge duplicates. This was done with their underlying theory in mind as
well as similarities between them.
Clustering the sorted mediators and moderators based on the umbrella terms provided a list of 19
mediators and 17 moderators. The mediator list consisted of information on the type of mediator (be-
havioural, contextual or temporal), name, underlying theory, description, example, related modera-
tor, sector, design level, and papers. The moderator list contained information of name, description,
example, sector, design level, and references. Reﬁning these lists, the descriptions were collected
from the papers and summarised for each with the assistance of Copilot (Microsoft, 2024) and SciS-
pace (SciSpace, 2024). Relevant examples were taken directly from the papers and reﬁned with AI as
well (Appendix AA).
Getting an overview of the mediators and their relation to one another, the 19 mediators were fur-
ther categorised. Using the Aﬃnity Diagramming method (Martin & Hanington, 2012), the mediators
were distributed based on similarities in the underlying theory and description. With the help from an
expert on RE and behavioural economics, Imke Van der Loo, the mediators and categorisation were
further reevaluated. From this iterative process, a ﬁnalised aﬃnity map of ﬁve categories and their
overlaps in underlying theory was visualised. The moderators were categorised based on similarities
in their descriptions, resulting in 17 moderators divided into four categories.

3.1.5
SB trade-offs and strengtheners
Exploring the trade-offs and strengthening relationships between the SB mediators took a starting
point in the SLR data extraction. The data was examined to identify relevant information on me-
diators, looking at the data points ’Name of mediator’, ’Description of mediator’, and ’Strength of
evidence’. The focus was to determine whether experiments had reported instances in which two
mediators either complimented or diminished one another’s effect. Undertaking the synthesis, the
mediator descriptions and underlying theories were reviewed to identify or hypothesise potential re-
lations.
3.1.6
SB and RE relation
To gain an understanding of the intricate mediators underlying SB and RE, they were analysed in re-
lation to each other.
Taking a starting point in data extraction, the ’Possible Relation to RE’ data point in the SLR was revis-
ited to identify connections. From this, a list of relations was created with accompanying reasoning
of SB and RE that ’counteracts’ (i.e. a SB mediator that counteracts the effects of a RE mediator and
vice versa) or ’leads to’ (i.e. a SB mediator that leads to a RE mediator).
Building on the RE and SB relations found in the data extraction, a need to supplement these rela-
tionships with synthesis was identiﬁed. Doing so, a workshop was organised between the authors
and RE and behavioural economics expert, Imke Van der Loo. The process of the workshop was to
ﬁrstly familiarize oneself with the identiﬁed SB mediators and RE mechanims. Next, the participants
were asked to ﬁnd relations between mediators of SB and RE based on similarities in the underlying
theory. See the full overview and template of the workshop in Appendix D. The outputs from each
of the three participants were merged into one list. Adding onto the initial list from the workshop, a
second synthesis was performed individually both by the authors and the expert. This especially took
focus in counteracting relations with the descriptions and examples in mind.
Combining the data extraction, workshop, and second synthesis, a ﬁnal list of the relations between
SB mediators and RE mediators was created with accompanying reasoning based on descriptions and
examples. The relations were divided into two categories; ’counteracts’ (visualied in a Venn diagram)
or ’leads to’ (visualised in a relational diagram).
3.1.7
Nudge and SB relation
To gain insight into the relationship between nudging and SB, their interactions were analysed rela-
tive to each other.
Taking a starting point in the data extraction from the data point ’Possible relation to nudging?’, all
studies with a possible relation were compared to the deﬁnitions from the nudge framework (Ap-
pendix B). The nudges were usually found in the study intervention. However, some interventions
were not explicitly stated as a ’nudge’, but if their description aligned with the framework, it was
applied to the mediator. For papers mentioning multiple nudges per intervention, all nudges were
assumed to partially work independently as well. The initial relations were visualised in a ﬁgure and
provided an overview of the most prevalent nudges to SB mediators.
Building on the relation from the data extraction, a workshop was organised between the authors
and an expert on RE and behavioural economics, Imke Van der Loo. The process of the workshop

3.2.2
The mediator list
The ﬁnal list of mediators for SB contained 19 mediators within ﬁve categories, seen in ﬁgure 11:
• Shaping the identity: Mediators that emphasise forming an environmental identity through pro-
environmental behaviours.
• Maintaining consistency: Mediators that help avoid cognitive dissonance and stay consistent with
established pro-environmental behaviours.
• Believing in your own abilities and contribution: Mediators that enhance conﬁdence in the ability
to contribute meaningfully to environmental efforts.
• Shift of priorities/goals: Mediators that guide a shift in priorities to align with environmental
norms, goals, and motivations.
• Balancing beneﬁts of environmental behaviour: Mediators that address the emotional and practi-
cal beneﬁts of engaging in pro-environmental behaviours.
’Shaping the identity’ was by far the most researched category and within this category the mediator
’Environmental self-identity’ was prevalent, being mentioned in 23 out of 59 papers, and ’Environ-
mental identity’ mentioned in 8. The next most mentioned were ’Preference for consistency’ and
’Conﬁdence in your own abilities’, which were also the ones with most relations to moderators, along-
side ’Environmental self-identity’. The two least researched categories was ’Shift of priorities/goals’
and ’Balancing beneﬁts of environmental behaviour’. Many of the mediators in these categories were
found in only one or few papers, and all except for two found only contextual spillovers. For the
three remaining categories, there was a more even split between contextual and behavioral spillover.
Temporal spillover, on the other hand, was not very frequent. Furthermore, this overview shows the
underlying theories behind the mediator as they were explained in the papers. The ones in parenthe-
ses were the authors’ synthesis of theories assumed applicable. Within these, ’Cognitive Dissonance
Theory’ and ’Self-perception Theory’ were widely covered in many categories.

The moderator list
The list of moderators consisted of 17 moderators divided in four categories (ﬁgure 14):
• User personality: Moderators that are focused on the users’ personality regarding personal val-
ues, attitudes, and norms, inﬂuencing the likelihood and strength of PEB2, and subsequently
SB.
• Difference between PEB1 and PEB2: Moderators that are focused on the PEBs and the relation
between PEB1 and PEB2, inﬂuencing the likelihood and strenght of PEB2, and subsequently SB.
• Interpersonal relations: Moderators that are focused on who the messenger and user are in
relation to one another interpersonally, inﬂuencing the likelihood and strength of PEB2, and
subsequently SB.
• User demographics: Moderators that are focused on the users’ demographics, i.e. distinct char-
acteristics, inﬂuencing the likelihood and strength of PEB2, and subsequently SB.
It was clear that the most covered category was ’User personality’ with 12 related moderators, sug-
gesting that the predominant way to inﬂuence the likelihood or strength of PEB2, and subsequently
SB, is by a user’s personal values, attitudes, and norms. The moderators with the highest amount of
papers in literature were ’User has a strong environmental self-identity’, ’Recognising behaviour as
normative’, ’Similarity between PEB1 and PEB2’, and ’PEB1 is more diﬃcult than PEB2’. All except these
are only mentioned by one paper.

’Positive emotion’, ’Environmental guilt’, ’Environmental motivation’, and ’Personal norm’ all have ex-
perimental evidence >=11% and presence of effect >=50% deeming them most effective and likely to

’Motivational crowding’ (RE), where the same underlying theory is applied but with opposite effects.

instance, if an individual holds ’Negative associations’ or ’Negative stereotypes’ (RE) for environmental
actions or groups, it could undermine the effectiveness of other SB mediators. However, not all SB
(e.g. ’Environmental social identity’) have a counteracting RE and vice versa (e.g. ’Time discounting’).

Summary of stage
This section highlights key insights and observations from the DSI stage.
Looking at the study characteristics, a signiﬁcant portion of the papers in the SLR utilised experimental
methodologies, indicating robust research practices. However, this predominance could also reﬂect
the relative novelty of the ﬁeld. The limited amount of comprehensive literature reviews and meta-
analyses contributes to the lack of cohesion within SB literature and demonstrates the need to con-
solidate ﬁndings. Within these experimental studies, the predominant type of behavioural data used
was ’self-reporting’ and ’intentions’ behaviours (ﬁgure 9) and in that a gap in ’measured’ behaviour.
With this knowledge, there are both advantages and disadvantages. Self-reported data often includes
high sample sizes (Salters, 2023). However, on the negative side, there are often biases when individ-
uals report their own behaviours, as humans are prone to report what is preferred of how they see

themselves or socially acceptable, without actually acting on them (Salters, 2023).
Analysing the keywords (ﬁgure 7) highlighted the widespread use of terms like ’behavioural spillover’,
and within this ’positive spillover’. These terms, rooted in psychology, generally relate to the trans-
fer of behavioural changes from intervention to non-targeted pro-environmental behaviours (PEB2).
However, there is inconsistency in the terminology and deﬁnitions used across papers. To address
this and contribute to clarity within the literature, the term ’secondary beneﬁt’ is used in this report
to speciﬁcally refer to the environmental gains from PEB2 within the context of sustainable design.
While this addition enhances speciﬁcity in the design ﬁeld, it may risk introducing confusion within the
psychology ﬁeld. Additionally, determining the environmental context and sustainability type proved
challenging in the papers, leading to a high number of NAs. However, this information could prove
valuable and requires further research to better distinguish.
In relation to the mediator and moderator lists, of the 19 mediators identiﬁed in the SLR 10 are cov-

are addressed by just one paper, further indicating an underexplored area. Additionally, the ability to
identify and distinguish between moderators and mediators by the authors in the SLR improved sig-
niﬁcantly over time. This learning curve introduces the possibility that earlier papers analysed might
contain misclassiﬁcation. Such uncertainty could inﬂuence the reliability of results, indicating that the
lists would beneﬁt from further iteration.
It is noticeable that the mediators exhibit intricate interconnections and overlaps in underlying theo-
ries (ﬁgure 12), with some potentially serving as preconditions for others. For example, ’Environmental
contribution’ may act as a prerequisite for developing ’Conﬁdence in your own abilities’. Additionally,
certain mediators appear to function as subcategories of broader constructs; for instance, ’Environ-
mental guilt’ could underlie ’Environmental concern’, as it reﬂects negative emotions related to en-
vironmental issues in general. This complexity in the relationships between mediators introduces
uncertainty to their clustering and categorisation, highlighting the need for further investigation. Re-
garding the moderators, during the extraction of the ’Name + description of moderator’ data point in
the SLR, it was decided not to include demographic factors (e.g., culture, age, gender) from the exper-
imental studies as potential moderators. This decision was based on their typically more generalised
nature. However, these demographic factors could represent an addition to the user demographics
category, which might be worth exploring further.
Regarding the evaluation of mediators and moderators based on experimental evidence, types of
behavioral data, and the presence of effects:
• Experimental Evidence: There is generally low experimental evidence for many mediators and
moderators, creating uncertainty in explaining how SB occur and whether moderators amplify
these effects.
• Behavioral Data Types: The data predominantly relies on self-reporting and intentions rather
than actual observed and measured behavior. This raises doubts about the validity of the ﬁnd-
ings and whether SB occur as claimed in the studies. These doubts are supported by meta-
analyses as well (Geiger et al., 2021; Maki et al., 2019).
• Presence of Effect: In the SLR, mediators and moderators were included even if the studies did
not ﬁnd mediating/moderating effects, as the goal was to ﬁnd factors that could explain why SB
occur with an exploratory approach. Consequently, the evaluation results were skewed, with
some cases showing a 0% presence of effect. While many mediators and moderators does
show a high presence of effect, this does not reﬂect real-life scenarios due to the limited num-

understanding their actual impact. Further, since SB are hard to predict, the effect sizes are
very small and inconsistent (Geiger et al., 2021; Maki et al., 2019). Therefore, the actual size was
not evaluated for this purpose. Expanding on this, effect size could provide valuable insights for

The evaluation of trade-offs and strengthening effects between mediators remains highly uncertain

as inconsistent and diﬃcult to pinpoint comprehensively (Geiger et al., 2021; Maki et al., 2019). Inter-

the need for further exploration of trade-offs. This gap highlights a critical area for future research to
better understand the dynamics between mediators and their role in behavioral outcomes.
This research has focused solely on moderators as strengtheners of mediators, leaving a gap in un-

inﬂuences an identity-based SB mediator, targeting users with low environmental identity through
such an intervention might instead trigger RE, such as reinforcing negative stereotypes (RE). Further
investigation is needed to predict the initial level of environmental identity required for an identity-
strengthening intervention to have a positive impact without triggering RE.
Considering the counteracting relationship between SB and RE, it illustrates the intricate mechanisms
at play and whether they turn positive or negative is highly dependable on the individual and their
social context. Moreover, it reveals a potential for a consolidated one-to-one mapping, where each SB
corresponds to a single RE and vice versa. This highlights a need for further exploration and a uniﬁed
understanding of the relationships to cover the current gap in relations.
Reﬂecting on the SB leading to RE; despite the uncertainty of assumed relations between them, it pro-
vides a good foundation for further analysis as many of the mechanisms are covered. This highlights
the importance of these dynamics and suggests that designers must carefully consider these when
designing for SB.
Of the nudge and SB relation, a signiﬁcant number of nudges emerged during data extraction, which
strengthens and validates the proposed connection between them. This ﬁnding suggests that nudg-

the occurrence of SB. Building on data extraction and synthesis, further exploration and testing of the
nudges to promote SB are needed to further validate their effect individually.

’positive framing’ (i.e. framing information positively, beneﬁcial, or humorously to positively motivate
users to certain choices) might prove a valuable addition. This reveals a limitation in the existing
framework and emphasises the need for future research to ensure a comprehensive coverage of all
relevant nudges.

4.1
Methods

4.1.1
Deﬁnining the solution space
After extensive analysis and synthesis establishing the foundation for a design support, the tool de-
velopment phase began, building on the existing knowledge of RE (Appendix A) and nudges (Appendix
B). Before going into idea generation, it was necessary to empathise with the user the tool aims to
support. Doing so, a persona (Martin & Hanington, 2012) was created as The Design Engineer. This
persona was developed on the basis of the paper in Appendix B, as well as the knowledge already
possessed by the authors being a part of the target group. With this user in mind and building on
the RE/Nudge tool, a tool requirement speciﬁcation (TRS) (DTUSkylab, 2024c) could be developed for
a tool that encompasses RE, SB, and nudging.
4.1.2
Develop
Starting idea generation, potential platforms that could accommodate the tool were explored with
desk research and reaching out to experts. It was decided to take a starting point in Excel, and more
speciﬁcally in the tool described in Appendix B. A brainstorming session was conducted to explore
the design and composition of the new tool, starting with a silent brainstorm (adaption of KJ Tech-
nique (Martin & Hanington, 2012)). To further expand the design space and challenge assumptions, a
negative brainstorming (DTUSkylab, 2024b) session was carried out. This broader exercise explored
unconventional approaches to the tool’s design and considered additional features it might include.
Next, spiderweb evaluation (DTUSkylab, 2024d) was used to rate the composition ideas, using both
speciﬁc criteria as well as an interpretation of criteria from the TRS;
A: Ease of use
• how easy would it be to navigate in the tool?
• how easy would it be to use the functions?
• would the ﬂow of the tool be easy to follow?
B: Does it require a lot of cognitive load from the user?
C: Is the idea exciting and does it inspire further iteration?
D: It is easy to adapt the RE tool to include SB?
E: Does it properly connect Nudging, SB and RE?
To further develop the chosen ideas from the spiderweb evaluation, Brainwalking (DTUSkylab, 2024a)
was utilised to collectively build on the them. Finally, an aﬃnity diagram (Martin & Hanington, 2012)
was made to structure the ideas from brainwalking.

Throughout the tool, all colors, shapes, and content are streamlined and stay in line with Gestalt prin-
ciples (UserTesting, 2019):
• Similarity: When things appear similar to each other, people group them together and think of
them as if they have the same function. Therefore, streamlining the visualisation of the content
of each tab, buttons, and arrows eases the user process.
• Proximity/Common region: As users perceive objects close together to be more related and in a
group than if they were further apart, all content that should be perceived in one group on each
tab is placed following this principle.
• Continuity: As elements arranged on a line or curve are perceived more related, the user process
in each tab follows a line from left to right to show the relation between the steps.
• Focal point: If an object stands out visually, it will capture the users’ attention ﬁrst. With this prin-
ciple, certain things throughout the tool are highlighted to ensure they understand the process.
As of this stage, the tool was prototyped to address the core contribution of helping designers to pre-
vent RE and promote SB of sustainable design in the early phases of design. Knowing the limitations
of working in Excel, only the essential and feasible functionalities necessary for testing and evaluating
were prototyped, such as the ﬁltering of the nudges. Some functions did not work as intended, but
were deemed infeasible to fulﬁll as a part of the core functionalities before moving into DSII.

4.3
Summary of stage
Before moving on to the next stage, it is useful to reﬂect on certain aspects identiﬁed during this
phase. Several limitations were noted, including speciﬁc functionalities in the tool that did not per-
form as intended due to the constraint imposed by choosing Excel as a platform. The decision to use
Excel was inﬂuenced by the need to ensure greater accessibility for users outside of DTU. The other
platforms considered would require a license to use, and the creation of an app was not within the
scope of the project, however, these could prove valuable for future work.
The value added by addressing the design levels (Product, PSS and STS) remains uncertain, as many
of the nudges in the tool are connected to all three. This uncertainty, coupled with diﬃculties in pre-
cisely connecting STS to the papers in the SLR, makes the connection given in the tool uncertain and
may require further reﬁnement in future work. The effectiveness of promoting SB should also be
addressed. The effect sizes were small and uncertain, which motivates a stronger focus on RE pre-
vention where the environmental gains appear more signiﬁcant. Additionally, one could argue that
preventing RE might inadvertently result in SB as well.
It is important to mention that the discussion about whether or not nudging is an ethical way of induc-
ing behavioural change still holds controversy. However, being transparent about the use of nudging,
as suggested by Schubert (2017), might help mitigate the fear that nudging is used in an unethical way.
Yet, making people aware of the fact that they are being nudged might backﬁre if they are not mo-
tivated to perform the behaviour they are nudged towards (Soman, 2019). Soman (2019) argues that
implementing a nudge without ensuring the proper motivation is in place will not produce the desired
result. Soman (2019) also mentions ’sludging’ as a potential addition, which differs from a nudge by
introducing friction rather than removing it: "So these are the kinds of things we need to start asking
ﬁrst: where the motivation is going to come from and how do we make sure it exists. Then we think about
whether, to get the appropriate outcome, we need people to become more mindful, or less. Do I need to
add friction or subtract friction?". When discussing motivation it is also important to note that inducing
motivation is not a ’one size ﬁts all’ and different groups of people might react very different to the
same nudge or sludge (Soman, 2019).
It should also be noted that generally, ’sludging’ has a negative connotation as it adds friction when
there should not be: "...sludge can take two forms. It can discourage behavior that is in a person’s best
interest such as claiming a rebate or tax credit, and it can encourage self-defeating behavior such as invest-
ing in a deal that is too good to be true." (Thaler, 2018). All in all, caution should be taken if the goal is
to intentionally introduce sludge. Furthermore, questions emerged about the overall effectiveness of
using nudges in promoting SB and preventing RE, raising the possibility that other strategies might be
more suitable. However, the option of using monetary incentives, is known from literature to rarely
lead to SB (Geiger et al., 2021) and as mentioned, ’sludging’ is a controversial approach.
From a methodological perspective, it is interesting to discuss how the use of a standard design pro-
cess (i.e. Double Diamond), impacted the design of the support tool in comparison to the methodol-
ogy of the DRM. When looking at the proposed process of PS, it follows the Double Diamond well as it
goes through the phases 1) Task clariﬁcation, 2) Conceptualisation, 3) Elaboration, 4) Realisation and
5) Support Evaluation. Task Clariﬁcation corresponds with the Deﬁne phase, where the persona and
requirement speciﬁcation were constructed. Next, Conceptualisation and Elaboration corresponds to
Idea generation and Conceptualisation within the Develop phase of the Double Diamand. Finally, Re-
alisation and Support Evaluation covers the Deliver phase. In this project, the Deliver phase extends
into DSII, incorporating continuous testing and iterative reﬁnement of the tool, in principle moving
back and forth between PS and DSII after each test round. This approach differs from the traditional

deﬁnition of DSII in the DRM framework, which typically focuses solely on evaluation (without itera-
tion). Especially for a project of this type that aimed to incorporate the feedback from evaluation, the
mixture of DRM and Double Diamond proved valuable.
It remains uncertain whether the tool should focus on reinforcing SB mediators already promoted by
the design or on introducing new SB mediators. This raises an important question: Is it more effective
to strengthen an existing mediator, which may be easier to impact as the current design is already
triggering it, or to establish a new one, potentially achieving a greater overall impact? For the time
being, the decision has been to concentrate on SB mediators that are least likely to be inﬂuenced by
the design. As mentioned, this is to ensure greater impact due to the small effect sizes as well as
increasing the likelihood of SB occurring given their unpredictability. However, one could also argue
that since SB are diﬃcult to predict, the approach of going for the most likely SB mediators could be
considered more robust, as it builds on existing behaviors rather than attempting to introduce new
mechanisms that might conﬂict with established ones. It is also easier to build on existing design el-
ements, rather than introducing new strategies that could completely alter the design and negatively
impact the most likely SB. However, to fully resolve this issue, more research would be required to
determine the implications for the overall effectiveness of the tool.
Another key aspect of the tool’s functionality is its focus on helping users establish a meaningful con-
nection between RE/SB and nudging. While the tool also includes features to support the accurate
identiﬁcation of RE and SB, this has not been its primary focus. This limitation could represent a
potential weakness to address in future iterations. Alternatively, the tool might be more effective if
integrated with another tool speciﬁcally designed to identify RE/SB mediators as its core function.

Descriptive Study II
This section will answer RQ5: How useful and usable is the combined tool and how can it be improved?
The section will go through, evaluation, testing, and iteration of the tool through 1) Methods, 2) Out-
put, and ﬁnally 3) Summary of stage.
The evaluation takes a starting point in the measurable success criterion: ease of usability and use-
fulness of the tool. The evaluation of this criterion creates the basis for discussing the actual success
criterion: preventing rebound effects and promoting secondary beneﬁts in sustainable design.
5.1
Methods
This section will introduce the methods used when planning and conducting the tests, as well as the
data handling.
5.1.1
Reaching out to stakeholders
Beginning DSII, the exploration of relevant stakeholders to test the tool considered potential compa-
nies, organisations, experts in relevant ﬁelds, and target users. This process identiﬁed the key groups
necessary for testing of company representatives, experts (within RE and/or behaviour), and target
users (i.e. the design engineer).
An overview of stakeholders and their respective ﬁelds of expertise was created, illustrating how
stakeholders covered design engineers, RE experts, and behaviour experts. All stakeholders were
contacted by email or through contacts, resulting in 11 individual participants planned for.
5.1.2
Planning and conducting the workshops
The testing of the tool sought to cover the following aspects. First, to assess on a general level the
overall functionality and understanding of the content in the tool. Second, to test how well a designer
with limited to no prior knowledge of RE and SB mediators was able to understand the concepts and
apply a design case. Third, whether the designer was able to connect the identiﬁed mediators with
nudges in a meaningful way making them feel equipped to implement them in their design case.
These aspects set out to cover both how easy the tool is to use and also evaluate the usefulness of
the tool for the designer.
The testing was carried out as a workshop of approximately 1-1.5 hours. The workshops always com-
prised of three main blocks: 1) introduction to project and case with minimal guidance providing a
’real’ as possible experience (ca. 15 min), 2) testing the tool with the design case while taking notes,
using a ﬂy-on-the-wall observation (Martin & Hanington, 2012) technique to limit the obstruction (ca.
40 min), and 3) survey (Martin & Hanington, 2012) and group discussion, prompted by the notes and
survey answers (ca. 30 min).
To conduct the tests, three documents were used:
• Test plan and case descriptions (Appendix R)
• Mid-test note sheet/Observation note sheet (Appendix S)
• Post-test survey (Appendix T)

The test plan and case descriptions entailed an introduction to the project, the test, a timeline of the
given workshop, the overall goal, and design case of that particular workshop. Since the participants
were from different stakeholder groups, the test case changed slightly to ﬁt the individual. The par-
ticipants were encouraged to use real-life design cases from their respective or individual projects. If
this was not feasible, a suitable design case was provided and selected based on the professional or
educational background of the participants. The design cases (product, PSS, and STS) were created
and continuously reﬁned using Copilot (Microsoft, 2024) and ChapGPT (OpenAI, 2023) making them
easier to understand (Appendix AA). Overall, the test plan acted as a guide for the authors (as facili-
tators) to conduct the tests.
The mid-test note sheet consisted of blank boxes for participants to take notes during the test. Each
box corresponded to an Excel tool tab, guiding them to provide targeted feedback. The facilitators
used the note sheet to make observations of how the tool was used and issues they seemed to en-
counter.
The post-test survey was created taking a starting point in the survey from the paper in Appendix B,
where the main focus was to test usability (ease of use) and usefulness of the tool. The survey was
adapted with inspiration from the framework in Vernadat (1996), further iterated by Pigosso (2012), to
assess a user’s perception of a tool.
The survey was split into four sections;
1. General questions, which aimed to assess the tool’s usability and relevance. They cover ease
of use, navigation, clarity of instructions, simplicity in presenting features, overall consistency,
and applicability to the participants’ industry.
2. Rebound Effect and Secondary Beneﬁt identiﬁcation which aim to assess how effectively
the tool identiﬁes relevant RE and SB, as well as the clarity, comprehensiveness, and depth of
information provided. They focus on the helpfulness and thoroughness of the tool in providing
useful content tailored to the speciﬁc case of the participant.
3. Choosing and understanding the nudges which focus on the tool’s usefulness in choosing
suitable nudges, both to prevent RE and promote SB. Further, they consider the clarity, detail,
and comparability of the provided nudge information.
4. Implementation of nudges seeks to evaluate the output of the tool provided and the perceived
ability it gives participants to implement the chosen nudges.
The tool was tested with participants representing various professional backgrounds, including:
• Design/mechanical engineers: These participants directly corresponded with the primary user of
the tool and their tests focused on the functionality and usefulness of the tool as a whole.
• RE experts (some with knowledge in design): Similarly to the primary target user, these participants
also tested the tool in its entirety while some had speciﬁc focus on the content revolving around
RE and SB.
• Behavioural design experts: Although not aligned with the tool’s primary target group and less
familiar with design or engineering-oriented problem-solving, the participants role served as a
means of eliciting initial reactions and broader insights regarding use of the tool outside of the
intended scope. In this case, the tool acted as a boundary object, prompting discussions about
how behavioural design is currently used by companies.

After implementating the ﬁnal improvements from round 3, the measurable success criterion: ease
of usability and usefulness of the tool is evaluated. Based on the surveys and discussions with partici-
pants, the measurable success criterion was satisfactorily fulﬁlled at this stage. The participants suc-

they could envision being beneﬁcial in a design process. Evaluating the success criterion: preventing
rebound effects and promoting secondary beneﬁts in sustainable design was not feasible within the scope

success criterion, the success criterion is approximated.
The main issues of the tool proved to be proper identiﬁcation of RE/SB mechanisms and the duplicates
of nudges, recurring for several participants through all rounds. The identiﬁcation of mechanisms
was not the primary focus of the tool but rather establishing a connection between mechanisms and
nudges. To reach its full potential and meeting the success criterion in every aspect, the link between
the design task and identifying mechanisms must be strengthened. This could be achieved in three
ways:

The ﬁnal tool
The ﬁnal tool is called Behaviour Transfer for Sustainability Tool. This stage will go through 1) Value
and area of use, 2) The target user and use-process, 3) Design, contents, and features, 4) Value and
limitations and 5) Conceptualising tools for other applications.
6.1
Value and area of use
The tool is developed and iterated to answer RQs 4 and 5:
• Q4 How does behavioural design, secondary beneﬁts and rebound effects interrelate and how can
this be optimised in a tool? (PS)
• Q5: How useful and usable is the combined tool and how can it be improved? (DSII)
As is well known, many sustainable designs do not reach their full potential due to RE. Therefore, the
goal with the tool is to aid designers in achieving greater sustainable gains by providing nudges to
preventing RE and promoting SB in any given design case. This will guide designers to critically assess
how consumers interact with their designs to ensure sustainable and correct use.
The area of application is the development of sustainable solutions in the early phases of design pro-
cesses. The tool encompasses products, PSS and STS, supporting both original design and redesign,
and can be applied in various sectors. The tool is mainly designed to be used individually due to its
comprehensiveness. However, the output can be used as a boundary object for group discussion in
the further implementation of the nudges. It is suitable for university courses in the ﬁeld of sustain-
able design or in companies that design sustainable solutions or have an interest in the ﬁeld.
Being one of the ﬁrst tools to address RE and SB simultaneously while providing this level of compre-
hensiveness, it is expected to relieve the pain of not knowing why and how to inﬂuence and transfer
behaviours with tangible designs. It has been validated through 11 test participants performing design
cases in ﬂy-on-the-wall observation workshops with a post-test survey discussion. In conclusion, the
results indicate applicability to its purpose as both easy to use and useful.
6.2
The target user and use-process
The target user is the Design Engineer (see ﬁgure 25), educated within the ﬁeld of design/and or engi-
neering, mainly with experience in developing products and PSS. Sustainability is important to them
and they prefer to design for sustainability wherever possible. For most designers, RE and SB are rel-
atively new terms they have yet to learn about. Targeting the Design Engineer, the tool was developed
to fulﬁll their needs of navigating easily around the topics of RE and SB in a design process.
The task given to the designers is to understand their design task and its solution space, analyse it
in relation to RE and SB, and creatively apply and implement nudges to prevent RE and promote SB.
This is delivered as an interactive Excel tool, easily accessible. The Excel consists of 8 tabs, supporting
4 main steps.

6.3
Design, content, and features
A series of design decisions, content adjustments, and feature implementations have been made to
alleviate user challenges and ensure the tool’s usability and usefulness. Especially taking the Gestalt
principles (UserTesting, 2019) into consideration.
Providing guidance with clear instructions and navigation, the steps are described in the introduc-
tion and repeated in each step (tab), maintaining a linear ﬂow following clickable arrows, to move
between the them. This linear ﬂow is kept in the individual tabs as well with a straight line from left to
right with the Gestalt principle of continuity. Further, objects belonging to the same group of content,
are placed visually near each other to follow the principle of proximity/common region, separating the
activities in an RE group and an SB group. Both of these principles highlight the relatedness of the
content and the sub-steps. The static box in the top of each tab allows for keeping constant track of
choices. Alerting the user of important disclaimers, speciﬁc highlights and comment boxes are used
throughout the tool in line with the focal point principle. As an example, the comment boxes are bright
orange with red edges capturing the users’ attention ﬁrst.
Maintaining consistency and coherence, there is a clear streamlining of colors, shapes, style, and
wording following the Gestalt principles of similarity and continuity. Objects with the same function
maintain the same design throughout as for the arrow buttons. Teal colors are used for RE and purple
for SB related content, while beige is the neutral for both. This makes it easier for the user to navigate
quicker and more conﬁdently around the tool.
Achieving simplicity, all irrelevant information is removed or hidden, and all not strictly necessary
kept as pop-up notes when the user hovers over a cell. This prevents the user from being over-
whelmed while still applying a great level of detail and comprehensiveness. In line with maintaining
simplicity, the RE and SB mediators are cut down by removing or merging, ending with only 11 out
of the original 17 behavioural RE mechanism (Appendix A) and 8 out of the original 19 SB mediators.
In identifying the mechanism, the user is allowed to choose only 3 of each type, and the amount of

Helping the user in making right decisions from the beginning, the compare graph is provided while

not overwhelm the user at ﬁrst glance. Within the tables, the identiﬁed mechanisms and nudges are
highlighted with the principle of focal point, so the user is not required to read irrelevant information.
The same features are used in the output in the examples of existing designs.
To ensure understanding, the language is written in an informal tone without using expert terms to
limit the prerequisite knowledge requirements. This makes it easier for the user to relate to the con-
tent of the tool. Simplifying the academic terms in the introduction, a thorough and relatable example

with clear questions, descriptions, and examples. This also applies to the nudges.
Aiding the user in getting a meaningful output, note boxes to write reﬂections are added throughout,

7
Discussion
7.1
Discussion of methodology
It is crucial to look back and reﬂect on how the methodology was applied and the way it affected
the results. Integrating the Double Diamond (TheDesignCouncilUK, 2023) into the DRM (Blessing &
Chakrabarti, 2009) strengthened the process (ﬁgure 3), as:
• DRM provided a structured research approach ensuring robust and reliable results and guid-
ance on how to turn these into a practically applicable design support (i.e. tool).
• Double Diamond provided a creative and iterative approach when designing and testing the
actual support.
The two methodologies were ideal to mix, as the PS and DSII stages of DRM align well with the Deﬁne,
Develop, and Deliver phases of the Double Diamond. Where the Double Diamond especially added
value was the Deliver phase within DSII, as it incorporated an iterative process of testing and improving.
Additionally, given the authors’ expertise and familiarity with the Double Diamond as designers, its
application within the DRM was well-suited for the project.
7.2
Summary and interpretation of ﬁndings
Looking back, the main objective and goal of this thesis was to ﬁll the gap in literature by exploring
the ﬁeld of SB within sustainable design, how SB and RE interrelate, as well as a potential relation to
behavioural design strategies (i.e. nudges) to promote SB. Those insights were used to create a tool
for designers to apply nudges for RE prevention and SB promotion in sustainable design. The tool was
tested with various experts within the ﬁeld and in compa nies. This objective was narrowed down to
the following main research question:
MRQ: How can designers use behavioural design strategies (e.g. nudging) in the early phases of
design to promote secondary beneﬁts and prevent rebound effects?
To answer the MRQ, ﬁve additional research questions were proposed. During the DSI stage, the ﬁrst
three RQs were answered.
RQ1: What are the behavioural mechanisms, mediators, and moderators underlying secondary beneﬁts of
sustainability-oriented design?
RQ1 was answered by the SLR and consequent overview presented in the identiﬁed SB mediators and
moderators, which together comprise the SB mechanisms. The lists comprise 19 individual mediators
and 17 moderators, each with an accompanying description, explanatory quote (not for moderators),
example, and within which sectors and design level they were found. The most prevalent mediators
focused on strengthening an individuals identity, preference for consistency and self-eﬃcacy. Con-
sistent with other ﬁndings, the effect sizes of the SB were generally small and hard to predict. The
vast majority of the evidence that does ﬁnd an effect size is based on self-reported behaviour which
is prone to bias, compared with measured behaviour (Salters, 2023). In general, the insights from RQ1
enabled the achievement of LO2: Explain how behavioural design can support Design for Sustainability.

==PAGE:114==

RQ2: What are the connections between secondary beneﬁts and rebound effects in existing literature?
RQ2 was answered partly by the SLR, however mainly by an assumption-based approach of hypoth-
esising potential relations based on the descriptions of the mediators and underlying theory. Four
types of relations between SB and RE are described as a potential framework (see ﬁgure 19 to under-
stand their interrelations, i.e. ’leads to’ and ’counteracting’). Three out of 37 identiﬁed relations were
found in the SLR. The ability to synthesise 34 potential relations between the 19 SB and 17 RE suggests
that the two concepts are closely related in nature. This might be explained by the fact that underlying
behavioural theories behind SB and RE often are the same or very similar, but able to lead to different
outcomes depending on the mechanism. This is the case with e.g. ’Preference for consistency’ (SB)
and ’Cognitive dissonance’ (RE) where both mediators build on theories of cognitive dissonance, but
have completely different outcomes. RQ1 and RQ2 together fulﬁll the learning objectives, LO3: Deﬁne
key concepts within rebound effects and secondary beneﬁts, in the context of behavioural design and LO6:
Analyse how rebound effects and secondary beneﬁts might interact with each other.
RQ3: How can behavioural design promote secondary beneﬁts?
The last research question within DSI, RQ3, was answered both by insights from the SLR as well as
synthesising relations based on knowledge about nudges and SB mediators. There was a clear pat-
tern in the most researched SB mediators presenting the highest amount of applicable nudges. For
the least researched SB mediators, the synthesis provided applicable nudges. The apparent connec-
tion between SB mediators and applied nudges seemed to be quite strong as it was relatively easy to
identify nudges for most SB mediators directly from the SLR. RQ3, alongside insights from the paper
in Appendix B, answers LO5: Analyse how behavioural design might be used to prevent rebound effects
and promote secondary beneﬁts.
Wrapping up, it was evident that the sectors where most research has been performed was ’Waste
management’, ’Retail’, ’Energy’ and ’Water management’. Looking at the intervention vs. outcome sec-
tors, it is interesting to note that the behavioural spillover is quite prevalent within the most mentioned
sectors. Contextual spillover, where the behaviour is carried from one context to another (i.e. one sec-
tor to another), is especially noticeable from ’Waste management’ to ’Retail’ and ’Energy’. Speciﬁcally
the contextual spillover from ’Waste management’ to ’Retail’ is signiﬁcant, which is to be expected as
both sectors can be heavily inﬂuenced by interventions targeting behaviour. It should be noted that in
many cases it was not possible to determine intervention and especially outcome sector, which pairs
well with the insight that SB are hard to predict and measure. Thereby determining the accompany-
ing outcome sector fell under the same limitations, and therefore these numbers are relatively small
compared to the no. of papers analysed in the SLR. Previous literature reviews within the ﬁeld do not
attempt this type of summary of sectors, however Geiger et al. (2021) does ﬁnd the most prominent
spillover effects within energy saving, recycling, water consumption and dieting, which is consistent
with the identiﬁed sectors (see ﬁgure 8). The presented overview provides a tentative indication of the
sectors where SB might be most prevalent, since the relations shown does not measure the strength
of the SB. It also points to a gap in the literature within the less researched sectors (i.e. Agriculture
and Construction). Interestingly, instead of deﬁning speciﬁc sectors, the distinction between ’private’
and ’public’ sphere behaviour were used often in the SLR.
RQ4: How does behavioural design, secondary beneﬁts and rebound effects interrelate and how can this
be optimised in a tool?
Answering the ﬁrst three research questions enabled the PS stage to answer RQ4. During this stage,
the tool was developed in Excel to encompass SB, RE, and nudges. The tool allows the designer to

Ultimately, the thesis fulﬁls LO1: Structure and manage a research project based on the Design Research

DRM was adapted as it was accompanied by the Double Diamond.
7.3
Thesis contributions and implications
This thesis contributes scientiﬁcally to the ﬁeld of behavioural science by advancing the understanding
of behavioural mechanisms underlying SB and in which way they relate to behavioural mechanisms
underlying RE. In research today, there remains few attempts to consolidate the experimental studies
conducted in the topic of SB, as few literature reviews and meta-analysis (i.e. seven) were found in
the SLR. Within these, researchers such as Maki et al. (2019), Geiger et al. (2021), Truelove et al. (2014),
Thøgersen and Crompton (2009), and Nilsson et al. (2017) stand out as main contributors. They argue
SB are hard to predict, their effect sizes are relatively small, and there is lacking cohesion in experi-
mental practices and to what actually constitutes a spillover. The same conclusions were drawn in this
thesis and an attempt was made to consolidate the deﬁnition of a SB mechanism (ﬁgure 2) including
lists of underlying mediators and moderators (ﬁgure 11 and 14). Additionally, limited research exists
on RE and SB in relation to each other, where a mainly assumption-based approach in this thesis to
create hypotheses could pave the way for future research.
The thesis also contributes to the ﬁelds of behavioural design and sustainable design by bridging
the gap between RE prevention, SB promotion, and nudging. It does so by providing an applicable

tool for designers to use in real-life cases. The Excel tool offers a practical approach to integrate be-
havioural strategies (i.e. nudges) into designs to prevent RE and promote SB of sustainable design,
and can be employed in iterative design processes. Thereby, the tool implicates design practise by
making RE prevention and SB promotion more accessible, and thus sustainable design more accessi-
ble. Reﬂecting on the two additional versions, the Behaviour Transfer for Sustainability Tool - Lite

7.4
Limitations
7.4.1
Methods
The research faced several limitations that should be acknowledged. In general, the SLR adopted an
exploratory approach by including mediators and moderators even if their mediating/moderating ef-
fects were not found in the experimental studies. While this broadened the scope of understanding,
it could induce questions whether the mediators or moderators with experimental evidence yet 0%
presence of effect even exist, e.g. the mediator ’Collective environmental eﬃcacy’. This highlights
the preliminary nature of the ﬁndings. Furthermore, a signiﬁcant challenge was the learning curve
in identifying and distinguishing between mediators and moderators due to their often ambiguous
nature and the lack of cohesive deﬁnitions in papers today. This ability improved signiﬁcantly over
the course of the data extraction, but it raises the possibility that earlier analyses may contain mis-
classiﬁcations, potentially impacting the reliability of the results.
Another limitation lies in the reliance on assumptions for many of the identiﬁed relationships be-
tween RE-SB and SB-SB. While these relationships were carefully considered within the framework of
established behavioural theories and mechanism deﬁnitions, they remain highly uncertain. Due to
time and scope constraints, a deeper investigation to substantiate these connections with empirical
evidence was not possible. Throughout the thesis, there has been a stronger focus on mediators, as
these were essential to the SB mechanism occurring. Thereby, moderators have been less analysed
and synthesised upon. This constitutes a limitation in the project.
Throughout the testing, both real-time and retrospective methods were used. The real-time methods
could induce participants with a sense of pressure or bias due to the simulated test environment. As
the facilitators were present, participants naturally seeked help from them rather than relying on the
guidance of the tool. Furthermore, the workshops could beneﬁt from being more customised to the
speciﬁc participants. These factors could have skewed the test results.
7.4.2
Tool
The tool exhibits several limitations that should be addressed in future iterations. While it is designed
to help users establish connections between RE, SB, and nudging, its functionality for accurately iden-
tifying the RE and SB mechanisms was not the primary focus. This proved an issue throughout the
testing due to the level of abstraction and the counterintuitive nature of identifying RE and SB in the

The reliance on Excel as the platform introduces challenges in functionality, automation, and robust-
ness, where dependencies on macros and VBA code make the tool susceptible to errors when used
on different systems. Time and competency constraints led to a limited focus on streamlining the

7.5
Future work
7.5.1
The SLR results
The thesis highlights several areas for further research. The data heavily relies on self-reported past
behaviour and self-reported intention to perform a behaviour to subsequently lead to SB. This indi-
cates a need for further experimental research of actual measured behaviour. Additionally, many of
the mediators and moderators are only supported by a single paper, further highlighting a need for
future research to evaluate the lists presented. Comparing the presented mediators and moderators,
many seem to be quite similar and highly related, especially moderators in the category ’User person-
alities’, underlining the need for more research to establish the difference between a mediator and
moderator consistently.
To build a uniﬁed understanding of the dynamics between RE-SB and SB-SB relationships, future
work should explore and evaluate the hypothesised links (i.e. counteracting, leads to, trade-offs, and
strengtheners) to consolidate and map the relations. The authors hope the proposed framework can
guide future research.
Moreover, evaluation of nudges and their individual effects on both SB and RE mediators through
testing and experimentation is suggested to improve the relations. Expanding on this, a link between
SB moderators and nudges reveals a new direction for research on promoting SB. Within both of
these, building on the existing framework with new nudges for sustainability might be necessary to
ensure a comprehensive coverage, keeping in mind the controversy surrounding the use of nudging
and the need to be transparent about its usage. This opens another road of potentially expanding to
other behavioural design strategies as well, such as sludging (Soman, 2019).
7.5.2
Tool
As the implementation guide was an addition after round three it would require further testing to
reﬁne the content to ensure it provides value to the user. Speciﬁcally delving into the Excel tool, a
further reﬁnement of functions, including the backend of macros and VBA, could greatly improve the
tool and warrants further work. A potential new road also arises with this, as shifting to other plat-
forms could ensure a more robust tool, for example, in an app or website. With the knowledge of
the connection between nudges and design level from the paper in Appendix B being quite tentative,
it was decided to remove it from the ﬁnal tool. If to be included again, the connection would need
further research to deliver the expected value together with a more robust backend.

8
Conclusion
This project successfully combined the Double Diamond and DRM to address gaps in literature by ex-
ploring the ﬁeld of SB within sustainable design, how SB and RE interrelate, as well as establishing a
relation to behavioural design strategies (i.e. nudges) to promote SB. By analysing insights from a SLR
and hypothesis-based syntheses, the research clariﬁed key behavioural mechanisms underlying SB
of their mediators and moderators, while exploring their complex relationships with RE. This resulted
in the development of a practical tool for preventing RE and promoting SB with nudges. Adressing
design with nudges provided a structured approach to tackle sustainability challenges in the early
phases of design.
The Behaviour Transfer for Sustainability Tool enables designers to identify relevant SB and RE
mechanisms in their speciﬁc design cases (product, PSS, STS) and apply targeted nudges to promote
sustainable behaviours and outcomes. Testing with target users, experts, and company represen-
tatives demonstrated the tool’s usability and usefulness, while highlighting areas for reﬁnement, i.e.
including more robust functionality, clearer guidance for mechanism identiﬁcation, and potential plat-
form migration to improve scalability and user experience. Two additional concepts of the tool with
different levels of complexity, independent prompt cards ’Lite’ and Excel dashboards with accompa-
nying cards ’Flex’, were proposed to ensure applicability for users in university and company sector
contexts, catering to both individual and group work. Further reﬁnement and iteration of the tools, its
processes, and its functions are essential to developing a robust backend and expanding to a wider
target group.
Despite certain limitations, such as the reliance on self-reported data and exploratory assumptions
regarding RE/SB relations, the project contributes signiﬁcantly to the ﬁelds of behavioural and sus-
tainable design. It bridges theoretical gaps, offers practical solutions, and provides a foundation for
future research into improved understanding of the underlying behavioural mechanisms of SB and
RE. The authors urge researchers and designers to test and validate the mediator and moderator lists
explaining the occurance of SB, as well as their relations to nudges and RE.
By making RE prevention and SB promotion more accessible to designers, a step has been taken in
the direction of mitigating the ca. 50% of environmental gains that are lost due to RE and improve
design for sustainability by promoting SB. Researchers, designers, and companies are encouraged
to build on the knowledge obtained in this thesis and pave the way for more effective, robust, and
sustainable design practices.
