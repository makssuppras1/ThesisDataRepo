Abstract
In this master project the design and construction of a lab-scaled MMC converter is described. At ï¬پrst,
a general review of the mathematical modelling of the multilevel converter is carried out. Subsequently,
the various control strategies for the converter are reviewed, and an outline of the various modulation and
internal submodule balancing strategies is given.
A detailed description of the submodules that will be used for the project converter is given, in order to
understand how they work and how they can be used in the implementation of the whole converter.
The arm inductor and submodule capacitors are designed by using the recommendations found in literature.
Once the requirements for the converter and the components values have been chosen, the control parameters
of the converter are calculated in order to guarantee that the resulting system is stable.
A set of simulations is carried out in order to verify the correct functioning of the converter and further
tune the control parameters and dimensioning of the components. After this stage, the construction of the
converter is possible and its correct functioning can be veriï¬پed. Due to time constraints and delays in the
ordering and delivery of the components, the construction of the converter has not been completed.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The constant increase in electrical energy production from stochastic renewable energy sources, like wind
and sun, as well as the deeper integration of energy markets, are making efï¬پcient transmission of high amount
of power over long distances a fundamental requirement of present and future electrical power systems.
Moreover, offshore Wind Power Plants (WPP) are increasingly being commissioned and they require an
efï¬پcient connection to the main grid onshore. High Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) systems are able to
provide efï¬پcient power transmission over long distances and the rapid evolution of power semiconductors is
making them a more and more attractive solution.
Line Commutated Current (LCC) HVDC systems have been historically used for this purpose. However,
they present some drawbacks that do not permit their application in all cases; when they are connected to
weak grids, commutation failures can happen and cause DC side faults. Moreover, the reactive power ï¬‚ow
into the AC grid cannot be controlled, so the converter cannot help the grid to sustain the voltage through
reactive power injection. Finally, LCC-HVDC links are not suitable to be interconnected into a multi-point
HVDC transmission system.
These problems can be solved by using Voltage Source Converters (VSC), where the Multilevel Modular
Converter (MMC) is the most promising technology. VSC-HVDC systems are not subject to commutation
failure, due to the fact that they are self-commutated. The injection of positive and negative sequence reactive
currents into the grid (these currents are used to sustain the voltage level and not to send active power into
the grid, therefore they are deï¬پned as "reactive") can be controlled. This makes it suitable to fulï¬پll the Low
Voltage Ride Through (LVRT) grid code requirements for offshore WPPs, for example. Multi-terminal
VSC-HVDC transmission systems can be built and, in this regard, there is intensive research investigating
the creation of a future european supergrid based on this technology [25].
In conclusion, VSC-HVDC systems will deï¬پnitely play a fundamental role in the power systems of tomorrow
and therefore research and development of this technology are well justiï¬پed.
In the following sections the most used Voltage Source Inverters topologies will be brieï¬‚y reviewed.
The most basic and common topology is the 2-level converter, which is extensively used in low power
applications. For medium and high power application the 2-level converter becomes less convenient to use,
because of the increased switching losses of semiconductors with high voltage ratings and the high stress on
insulation deriving from the steep slope of the voltage transition. For these reasons multilevel topologies are
introduced. The analysis of multilevel converters will end with the Multilevel Modular Converter (MMC)
based on half-bridge submodules, which is the subject of this master project.

1.1
The 2-level Converter
The 2-level converter topology is represented in ï¬پgure 1.1. In this topology, the output voltage of each
phase is changed between Vd/2 and âˆ’Vd/2, with the neutral point as reference. PWM modulation is used in
order to control the magnitude of the AC output voltage. The harmonic content of the output voltage of the
2-level converter can be reduced by increasing the switching frequency. This is normally the case for low
power, low voltage applications.
Figure 1.1: 3-phase 2-level Inverter
When the power and voltage level increase, also the ratings of the power semiconductors must be
increased. For high power semiconductors the switching losses become very high, so the switching frequency
should be kept low. For 2-level HVDC converters the typical switching frequency lies between 1 and 2 kHz
and a LCL ï¬پlter is usually used to reduce the harmonic content of the generated voltage. By increasing the
number of voltage levels, the harmonic content can be improved and therefore the switching frequency can
also be decreased, which allows the reduction of switching losses and the removal of the output ï¬پlter.
Another reason to use multilevel converters is the reduced amount of power semiconductors that must be
connected in series. In fact, with a 2-level converter the only way to withstand the high rail-to -rail voltage is
to directly connect the devices in series. This poses problems both on static and dynamic voltage sharing of
the power semiconductors and it is not technologically convenient, because very sophisticated gate drivers
must be used. By using multilevel topologies with a sufï¬پcient number of levels, the same voltage stress on the
devices can be experienced without the need of series-connected elements. In particular, in a MMC converter
the individual switches of the submodules only have to withstand the voltage across the subcapacitor. In
high voltage applications, many of these modules are interconnected (usually a few hundreds), therefore the
individual capacitor voltages become low and well within the rating of single power semiconductor modules
commercially available.

drawback of this topology is that the power losses are not evenly distributed among the switches. A solution
to this problem is provided by the Active Neutral Point Clamped topology [33], which allows to choose
among different paths to the neutral point. This gives the possibility to evenly distribute the switching

1.2.3
Modular Multilevel Converter (MMC)
The MMC is suitable for high voltage applications because it allows to overcome the limitation posed by
NPC and Flying capacitor converter.
It is built upon individual submodules (also called cells), which are stacked in cascaded strings (for this
reason it is also called cascaded converter). This way, high voltages can be reached while maintaining the
voltage stress on the elements of the single submodule low.

presence of the second harmonic circulating current is also analyzed, because it constitutes one of the main
sources of losses in the converter if not properly controlled.
2.1

A schematic representation of a phase leg of the MMC converter is given in ï¬پgure 2.1. The submodules
are assumed to be half-bridge.
The phase leg consists of two phase arms. A phase arm is given by a submodule string and a reactor.
The number of submodules per arm is N. The AC terminals are located in the middle of a phase leg, at
the connection between the two phase arms. The phase reactors L are used in order to avoid high current
transients that would derive from the direct parallel connection between the voltage sources represented
by the capacitors of the submodules. Moreover, they provide ï¬پltering for the high harmonics of the arm
currents and reduce the current surge during faults.
It is interesting to notice that in 2-level converters the phase reactors must be put on the AC side of the
terminal, otherwise they would slow down the reversal of the current in the phase legs during commutation.
For an MMC converter this is not a problem because the current commutation happens at submodule level.
The resistance R is not physically present, but it represents the power losses happening in the converter. Cd
represents the lumped capacitance value of the DC connection. The resistance Rs is used at start-up in order
to limit the inrush currents. It is bypassed by a mechanical switch during normal operation.
The converter is usually connected to the grid through a transformer in order to adapt it to the grid voltage,
but also direct grid connection is possible (normally up to 400 kV). An ungrounded transformer can be used
in order to eliminate zero-sequence currents which are generated by the converter due to modulation or third
harmonic injection [22], for example.

Control strategies for the MMC
The control of the MMC takes place at many different levels, due to the complexity of the system and the
various variables that must be controlled.
The control of the output current of a 3-phase system can be conveniently performed by using space vectors
in the خ±خ² or dq frame. The transformation into خ±خ² and dq components will be brieï¬‚y reviewed at the
beginning of the chapter. The main advantages of using this technique are:
â€¢ In the dq frame, output current components are directly proportional to the active and reactive power
injected into the AC bus, which means that the output current reference can be directly derived.
â€¢ Saturation of the space vector does not lead to distortion of the output waveform, as it would happen
with per-phase based control.
â€¢ The fundamental positive and negative sequence output currents can be controlled separately and this
allows the converter to fulï¬پll the LVRT requirements from the grid code.
On the other hand, circulating current control and arm balancing control will be performed on a
per-phase basis, because each each individual leg and arm must be balanced.
The synchronization of the MMC with the grid will be performed by a Phase Locked Loop (PLL).
The DC-link voltage control and active/reactive power control will set the reference current for the output
current control loop. It is important to notice that DC-link voltage and active power control cannot be
performed at the same time. In an HVDC connection, the converter on the one side will perform DC-link
voltage control, while the converter on the other side will control the active power ï¬‚ow.
The circulating current control will be performed in connection with the energy balancing control, due to
their direct relationship.
From the output and circulating current control the insertion indices for the upper and lower arms are
calculated. They will form the reference which will be passed to the modulation and submodule balancing
stage.
At this point, the gate signals for the individual transistors are determined and sent to the individual devices.
At the end of the chapter, the most common strategies used to control the current injection under
unbalanced grid conditions are reviewed.

CHAPTER 4
Modulation and submodule energy
balancing
The modulation stage is a very important part of MMC converter, because it constitutes the bridge
between the output signals of the control system (the insertion indices) and the actual insertion and bypass
of the submodules. The insertion indices should ideally be sinusoidal signals, but due to the ï¬پnite amount of
voltage levels that can be generated by the converter, it is not possible to obtain a pure sinusoidal waveform
without harmonics. However, due to the ï¬پltering performed by the inductance, high frequency voltage
harmonics would generate very low harmonic currents, which is one of the main requirements of the grid
code. Therefore, modulation aims at reducing and pushing the harmonic content of the generated waveform
to high frequencies. However, the switching frequency should also be kept low in order to minimize switching
losses.
Due to the fact that modulation deals with the actual switching of the submodules, it is intimately
connected to the balancing of the individual submodule capacitor voltages (and consequently energy).
Therefore, for each modulation strategy there must be also an energy balancing strategy.
In this chapter, three modulation strategies are analyzed: Nearest Level Control (NLC), Level Shifted
Carrier Pulse Width Modulation (LSC-PWM) and Phase Shifted Carrier Pulse Width Modulation (PSC-
PWM). NLC is the simplest to implement and it is carrier-less, while LSC-PWM and PSC-PWM are more
complex and carrier-based.
Sorting-based balancing strategies will be reviewed for NLC and LSC-PWM, while for PSC-PWM a closed-
loop strategy will be reviewed.
4.1
Pulse Width Modulation
Pulse Width Modulation is based on the comparison of the reference signal with a carrier signal, whose
frequency is higher than the one of the reference signal. The main principle of operation of PWM is described
for a 2-level voltage source, which in this case would be equal to the single submodule.
When the reference signal is higher than the carrier signal, the output voltage of the submodule will be set to
the high level V i
c . On the other hand, when the carrier is higher than the reference the output voltage will be
set to the low level 0. This allows the average of the output voltage to be equal to the average of the reference
voltage taken over a period of the carrier wave. This means that by increasing the frequency of the carrier the
replica of the reference voltage at the output becomes more accurate and thus the harmonic content smaller.
However, the carrier frequency also corresponds to the switching frequency fsw, which is proportional to the
switching losses. This means that there must be a tradeoff between harmonic quality and switching losses.

4.3
Sorting-based methods for submodule energy balancing
If no balancing scheme were implemented, NLC and LSC-PWM would cause uneven conduction of the
different submodules which would lead to unbalanced losses and capacitor voltages.
The energy balancing methods are devised by considering that a capacitor charges or discharges according
to the direction of the current. This means that when the current is positive the capacitors with the lowest
voltage should be inserted and the opposite should happen when the current is negative. The individual
capacitors voltages can be sampled, ordered in a list and inserted in ascending/descending order according to
the sign of the current. The pulse generation has to be performed by the central controlling unit in order to
insert the submodules according the the list and the current direction.
The list can be refreshed and reordered at every sampling cycle and the submodules inserted accordingly.
This strategy would achieve excellent voltage balance between the submodule capacitor and a very fast
response to transients. However, the switching losses are noticeably increased because switching events

The half-bridge submodule is the fundamental component of a MMC converter and it has to be equipped
with different functionalities that allow control and safe operation of the converter. The submodules must
also be tested in order to make sure that they operate as expected.
In this chapter, the structure of the submodules used for the construction of the converter is thoroughly

â€¢ Communication with the central controlling unit: the submodule must be able to send and receive

â€¢ Measurement of the capacitor voltage: in order to perform energy balancing, the submodule must
be able to communicate the voltage across the capacitor to the central controlling unit.
â€¢ Overvoltage protection: the submodule must be capable of detecting and communicating when the
capacitor is experiencing a potentially dangerous overvoltage.
â€¢ Overcurrent protection: the submodule must be able to detect overcurrent in order to protect the
switches and communicate it to the central controller.
â€¢ Overtemperature protection: the temperature of the submodule must be monitored and communi-

â€¢ Shoot-through avoidance: there must be a sufï¬پcient dead-time between the turn-on signals for the
two transistor in order to avoid a very dangerous short-circuit of the capacitor.
â€¢ Visual aid: LEDs must visually communicate whether the submodule is operating correctly or not.

enabling signal ENA comes from the central controller and it corresponds to v_signal in the schematics of

When a protection mechanism is activated, the relative fault signal becomes low, which means that
ENABLE becomes low because now at least one of the signlas at the AND4B3 port input is low. As a
consequence, both transistors are turned off and the submodule is blocked. The Q bit of the FDP connected
to the fault signal is set to 1 by the preset. This signal is then sent to the OR port of the main schematic,

the fault is cleared, a transition of ENA from low to high makes Q equal to 1. However, the second input of
AND2B1 is still low because of inversion. Therefore, ENA is set to low again, which causes a low-to-high
transition in C of the FDP. This sets all the Q bits of the FDP to 0. The last step is to bring ENA to the high
state again, so that ENABLE becomes high.
The ï¬پrst FDP has Q initialized to 1, which means that the submodule is in a fault state when it is turned on.
The reset sequence must be therefore applied in order to start the switching operation.

6.3
Submodule Capacitor Design
The submodule capacitor constitutes a fundamental component of the converter. In a real scale system,
its choice is fundamental because it is one of the main cost drivers of the system and therefore it must
be selected with care in order to ï¬پnd the best compromise between cost and performance. The preferred
technology choice for high voltage converters is metallized polypropylene ï¬پlm capacitor [34]. This type of
capacitors provide a very high reliability due to the self-healing property of propylene and segmentation of
the metallized electrodes, which can isolate the faulty dielectric areas when self-healing is not enough. The
capacitance slightly decreases everytime a fault in the dielectric happens, which is very useful to determine
whether the capacitor is reaching the end of its lifetime (usually when the capacitance is around 5% less
than its original value) and replace it before it undergoes a total failure. Metallized polypropylene ï¬پlm
capacitors achieve low values of ESR and ESL, which is very desirable for high power converters. For the
project converter, however, aluminium electrolytic capacitors will be used. This choice is motivated by the
wide availability and low cost of electrolytic capacitors in the mF range. The capacitors used are from the
B43508 series from EPCOS/TDK.

CHAPTER 7
The project converter
In this chapter the implementation and control of the project converter will be discussed. The control of
the converter is performed via the real-time simulator OP5600 from OPAL-RT. This simulator is designed for
performing real-time simulations based on models created with Simulink. Morevoer, it supports hardware-
in-the-loop (HIL) and therefore it can be used as central controller for the converter, by implementing the
simulink control models seen in the previous chapter in real-time. In order to allow the communication
between OPAL-RT and the submodules via optic-ï¬پbers, a communication board to interface the input/output
cards of OPAL-RT with the transmitters/receivers has been designed, built and tested. The measurement of the
electrical quantities required for controlling the converter and monitor its operation has been implemented
via LEM transducers, whose output signals are sent to the central controller via coaxial cables.
7.1
The system structure
The structure of the whole converter system is depicted in ï¬پgure 7.1. The submodules communicate via
optic ï¬پbers to the central controller OP5600, whose operation is explained in the following section. The
interaction with the user happens via the host computer. The LEM transducers measure the currents and
voltages which are required to control the converter and monitor its operation (the current transducers are
depicted in yellow, while the voltage transducers are in orange). A 5V power supply is required for the
submodules, communication boards and current transducers, while a 15V supply is used for the voltage
transducers. The AC source is isolated form the converter via a delta-wye transformer and switches are
included at the output of the converter for protection.
7.1.1
OPAL-RT as central controller
The control of the converter is performed by the real-time simulator OP5600 from OPAL-RT. The main
purpose of this device is to perform real-time simulations based on Simulink models, but the capability of
exchanging input and output signals with the external environment via expansion cards makes it also suitable
to be a central controller for the converter. The submodule capacitor voltages, arm currents, DC link voltage,
DC link current and AC bus voltages are taken as inputs by the control loops, which generate the output
signals for the insertion of the submodules. The power reference can be adjusted in real-time and all the
quantities of interest are made visible on the scopes in the host computer.
The architecture of the OPAL-RT real-time simulation environment consists of two main parts: the host
computer, which constitutes the interface with the user, and the target (in this case OP5600), where real-time
calculation of the model and I/0 operations take place. The host computer has RT-LAB, which is the software
that translates the Simulink model into C code and builds it into the target. Moreover, it allows the user to

CHAPTER 8
Conclusions
A review of the MMC converter and its control schemes has been performed. A model of the project
converter has been built and the control schemes extensively tested through the simulations, which have
provided a good basis for the implementation on the actual converter. The dimensioning of the main
components (submodule capacitors and arm inductors) has been done by ï¬پrst using the methods proposed in
the literature and then reï¬پned by exploiting the simulation results. The hardware design of the submodules has
been explained in detail and the main functionalities have been successfully tested. A complete architecture
for the project converter has been designed and the control structure implemented through OPAL-RT. The
components necessary for the operation of the converter, such as communication boards and LEM boxes,
have been designed and implemented.
Limitations
One of the original aims of the project was to build and test the operation of a lab-scaled MMC converter.
The hardware implementation has not been completed in time due to big delays in the delivery of the
components necessary for the completion of the converter. Moreover, the communication with some
suppliers has been very slow, which brought to a delayed conï¬پrmation and shipping of the orders. These
factors have been the main limitation to the project.
Future work
As of today, the main application of MMC converters is HVDC links for efï¬پcient bulk transmission
of electric power over long distances and different synchronous areas. Moreover, the high degree of
compactness achieved by modern MMC converters, as well as the decreasing costs and the ability to support
the stability of the AC grid, makes them very suitable for offshore applications, especially wind power plants.
The realization of a multi-terminal HVDC network based on MMC converters will be a fundamental step
towards a stable and efï¬پcient integration of renewable energy sources into the grid and it is presently a topic
of intensive research.
In light of these considerations, the main points for future work are:
â€¢ Construction of a second converter to implement a lab-scaled HVDC link
â€¢ Construction of other converters to implement a lab-scaled multi-terminal DC network
â€¢ Experimental investigation of different control schemes under unbalanced conditions
â€¢ Experimental investigation of grid harmonics mitigation strategies

Background 
The constant increase in electrical energy production from stochastic renewable energy sources, like wind and sun, as 
well as the deeper integration of energy markets, are making efficient transmission of high amount of power over long 
distances a fundamental requirement of present and future electrical power systems. HVDC power transmission systems 
based on Voltage Sourced Multilevel Modular Converters (MMC-VSC) are increasingly being deployed to implement 
efficient power transmission, due to their excellent harmonic performance, flexibility, modularity and reliability.  
Project description 
The objective of this master project is to design and construct a lab-scaled MMC-VSC converter based on half-bridge 
submodules. This converter will then be used by the department to carry out experiments and research its capabilities in 
HVDC connections.  
First, the converter specification will be defined and the hardware requirements chosen accordingly. Then, theoretical 
calculations and simulations will be performed to design the control structure of the converter and the communication 
system. Finally, the converter will be assembled and tested to verify its simulated operations.
