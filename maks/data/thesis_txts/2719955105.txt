Abstract 
For decades, pathologists preferred action of preservation and archival of tissue, have been 
Formalin-Fixed, Paraffin-Embedded (FFPE). This is backed up by more than an estimated 
billion FFPE samples held within biobanks worldwide as of 2012 and has only grown since. 
These biobanks hold enormous potential for retrospective studies. Yet, FPPE, while excellent 
for preserving tissue for extended periods of time, limits most samples to immunostaining and 
structural analysis, as the extraction of biomolecules like proteins has proven exceptionally 
difficult. However, with the last decade’s advances in Liquid Chromatography Mass 
Spectrometry (LC-MS) based proteomics, the sensitivity of proteomic workflows has increased 
massively, allowing for deeper proteome coverages at smaller sample sizes and under less 
optimal conditions. Combining this with other recent technologies, namely Laser Capture 
Microdissection (LCM) and an in-house developed method for collecting FFPE tissue and 
extracting high levels of proteins from said tissue, this study proves that FFPE is a valuable 
resource for retrospective protein analysis. 
This study presents a protocol capable of extracting up to 3,004 unique protein groups from 
around 100 FFPE-fixated and EpCAM-stained acinar exocrine pancreatic cells. Similar results 
were achieved from H&E-stained tissue, where yields demonstrated a sensitivity of 844 protein 
groups from no less than a cluster of around seven cells.

Project Aim 
This thesis aims to optimize the process of extracting proteins from FFPE tissue for improved 
proteome analysis using LC-MS. Recent publications have presented strategies using the 
newest instruments and technologies for extracting up to 1,500 protein groups from FFPE 
tissue.[14] Others have shown up to 2,500 protein groups from frozen tissue[8], both from around 
400,000 – 500,000 μm3 of tissue (around 178 – 200 cells). This thesis sets a minimum goal of 
2,000 protein groups from an equal amount of sample. The optimized protocol will attempt to 
combine the strengths of the Single-Pot, Solid-Phase-enhanced Sample Preparation (SP3) 
techniques with the extreme precision of LCM while also evaluating intermediate steps like 
de-paraffinization or other FFPE disruptive techniques. The raw MS data will be acquired using 
Data Independent Acquisition (DIA) and analyzed with Spectronaut. With a short-term goal of 
increasing proteome coverage, the long-term goal, if the optimization is successful, will be to 
look at the actual proteome differences between healthy pancreatic tissue and tissue with 
Pancreatic Ductal Adenocarcinoma (PDAC). Therefore, as the protocol and proteome coverage 
improves, the tissue used for pilot optimizations will be healthy pancreatic tissue, with a 
graduate transition to carcinogenic tissue.

1 Introduction 
Since its introduction to proteomics, LC-MS-based proteomics has increased massively in both 
throughput and sensitivity thanks to better instruments, smarter data acquisition approaches, 
analytical software, and experimental processing of samples for protein extraction. Because of 
these new technologies, LC-MS has gained a broader and more versatile usage and has become 
an important analytical instrument in fields where other tools may either be too imprecise, too 
slow, or unfeasible for comprehensive data collection and analysis. One such field is the study 
of tissue, and more precisely, FFPE tissue[1] (Appendix 6A contains a list of all abbreviations 
used throughout). 
For more than 100 years, FFPE tissue has been the go-to preservation method, leaving hospitals 
and medical institutions worldwide with compiled banks totaling more than a billion FFPE 
samples and still counting.[2] These FFPE banks hold enormous potential for retrospective 
studies, and while it was previously believed that FFPE could not match the quality of frozen 
tissue[1],[3], studies have shown that while FFPE-bound tissue is challenging to extract, the 
protein quality is comparable to that of frozen tissue[2]; and it does not significantly degrade 
over a period of at least ten years. This, however, is only partially true for RNA, where an 
extensive decrease in quality has been observed, leaving protein as a stronger candidate for the 
analysis of cell expressions. [4] Moreover, the correlation between mRNA and protein 
expressions is known for having a less-than-perfect correlation, and though transcriptomics 
provides partial insight into the regulatory mechanisms of the cell, it is still the proteins that 
act as the functional units of the cell (excluding non-coding miRNA, etc.), only further 
supporting the need for spatial proteomics.[5],[6] While proteomic retrospective studies on FFPE 
tissue have previously been conducted, the amount of sample needed to reach an extensive 
proteome coverage usually ranges on the scale of tens of mm2[1],[7], which may seem like a 
minuscule amount when considering more than a billion samples are available worldwide. Still, 
human tissue samples, especially those that originate from patients with rare diseases, are, in 
many cases, too precious or simply not abundant enough to allow for a comprehensive 
retrospective proteomics analysis. Another major issue with using bulk samples is that it 
obscures the lines between heterogeneous cell types and proximal tissue, harming the pursuit 
of true understanding of the expressions in, e.g., malignant cells, which in turn will limit the 
capabilities of diagnosis and treatment[8]. It is these technological obstacles that this study 
aimed to tackle. 
This study will attempt to implement an optimized protein extraction and processing protocol 
for FFPE-bound tissue for a more sensitive MS analysis without compromising proteome 
coverage. The protocol is helped partially by new technologies such as LCM, an essential tool 
for extracting tissue samples on the scales of 0.1 mm2 or less, and Single-Pot, Solid-Phase-
enhanced Sample Preparation (SP3), which improves both quality and quantity when extracting 
==PAGE:7==
6 
 
proteins from the tissue. With retrospective studies as the focus point, the strategy for MS 
analysis is to use Data Independent Acquisition (DIA), as it allows for future re-mining as the 
technology advances and acquires a broader set of data compared to Data Dependent 
Acquisition (DDA). Recent advances also point to DIA having better protein coverages 
compared to DDA[9].  
A recent article presented a method for protein extraction from frozen tissue, yielding a total 
of 2,521 different protein groups from a sample size of 0.04 mm2[8]; this study will aim to reach 
a similar quality of extraction, which, if achieved, will definitively prove that FFPE is a more 
viable method of preserving tissue than flash freezing, both in terms of preservation, but also 
in regards to retrospective studying. For the optimization steps performed throughout the 
project, pancreatic tissue is used, and while the main focus is on the optimization itself, if a 
robust, reproducible, and high-throughput protocol is successfully achieved and time allows 
for it, attempts at running samples of premalignant and malignant lesions will be conducted to 
get insights into the tumorigenesis and trajectory of pancreatic cancer. With the developed 
protocol, follow-up studies could potentially discover distinct biomarkers or even treatments 
for prior mentioned conditions and hopefully other maladies if the protocol proves to be 
directly transferable to different cell populations. 
 
1.1 Laser Capture Microdissection 
LCM has gained increased popularity in both genomics[10], transcriptomics[11], and especially 
proteomics[8],[12]-[14] over the last decade, as it allows for the ultra-precise acquisition of selected 
heterogeneous cell populations. Prior to LCM, manual extraction of, e.g., carcinogenic cells 
would require dissection of large areas of tissue, which most likely would contain both the 
carcinogenic cells of interest and other cell populations within proximity. This gives a false or 
distorted view of the actual genomes, transcriptomes, and proteomes present in the 
carcinogenic cells. LCM, however, allows the user to collect areas down to the size of 
individual cells, thereby ensuring that only the populations of interest are used for downstream 
analysis.[15] Such an ability is highly advantageous for especially pancreatic cancer, as most 
tumors consist mainly of stroma, with only low percentages actually being malignant cells. 
 
1.1.1 PALM LCM 
There are many variants of LCM instruments and collection methods; most use either ultra-
violet (UV) laser beams, infrared (IR), or a combination of the two.[15] The two main 
competitors at the high end of LCM technologies are ZEISS and Leica. While both use similar 
UV methods for cutting tissue, they mainly have one key difference in their collection 
techniques, each with its own strengths and weaknesses. The PALM LCM microscope 
produced by ZEISS (which is the microscope used throughout the project) uses a defocused 
==PAGE:8==
7 
 
laser that strikes a large area of the cut tissue providing it with enough propulsion to eject it 
from the glass slide. The catapulted tissue can then be captured in the adhesive-filled lids of 
LCM tubes. This technique allows for a clean and complete cut before the collection of the 
sample. However, for the Leica model, the collection is performed using gravity, meaning that 
there is a possible chance that the sample releases from the glass slide before it is completely 
released from the remaining tissue, but while this could cause trouble, it does also have the 
benefits of not compromising the tissue as it is collected, which is a potential concern with the 
PALM LCM. Using catapulting poses the risk of the sample simply missing the adhesive cap, 
resulting in the complete loss of the potentially very precious tissue, which would be far less 
of a concern when relying on gravity. Furthermore, there is also the chance that the high energy 
defocused pulse could burn the pixel, decreasing the quality of the proteome.[15]-[17] 
The PALM LCM cuts tissue using a UV-A laser with a wavelength of 337 nm. From the source 
of the laser, the beam is navigated by mirrors to and up through the lenses (which are located 
below the sample) of the microscope. The user is therefore observing the tissue from the laser’s 
point of view, meaning that while catapulting, the tissue “flies away from the observer”, so to 
speak (see Figure 1). Utilization of the microscope lenses means that the width of the beam 
scales with the magnification level used; a higher level of magnification equals a smaller beam. 
This application ensures that for bulk collections, the quantity will far outweigh the margin of 
error, while the collection of a small cluster of cells at a magnification level of 40x or higher 
will still deliver an exact cut, which enables the high quality of collection, required for analysis 
of the desired heterogeneous populations.[15],[18]

desired cell populations. It operates by cutting designated areas of tissue using a UV laser. The 
cut tissue is then collected by de-focusing the laser beam, which catapults the tissue into the lid 
of the attached tube, when struck with the laser beam.{3}

1.1.2 Sample collection 
This project will be optimizing the protein extraction from pancreatic tissue with sizes of 
50,000 and 100,000 μm2 using 5 μm thick samples (250,000 μm3 and 500,000 μm3). Using the 
average mammalian cell volume of 2,250 μm3 (Bio Number ID 100434[19]) sums the cell count 
up to around 111 and 222 cells, respectively. This is, of course, assuming that there is complete 
cell coverage, with optimal cell density, of the collected tissue pixel, which in most cases would 
be highly unlikely. It is, therefore, not unreasonable to round the numbers down to an average 
of 100 and 200 cells. These inconsistencies in cell density also pose the problem of irregular 
results, making it harder to distinguish small increases in protein coverages from different 
optimization steps. So, to mitigate this, samples were always collected in minimum triplicates, 
trusting that the average would flatline the differences between samples; this way, sudden 
outliers would also not give a wrong impression of the results. 
 
1.2 Formalin-Fixed, Paraffin-Embedded 
FFPE has been the gold standard for preservation of biological tissue and material, practically 
since formalin fixation was first described in the 1890s, as it has proven highly efficient at 
conserving structures of both tissue and cells while still allowing for molecular permeability, 
and thereby, staining[2],[20]. A solution of 100% formalin is an aqueous mixture of around 40% 
solubilized formaldehyde, as formaldehyde is natively a gas. Fixation is usually performed at 
approximately 10% formalin, which is 3-4% formaldehyde. This mixture can then be 
complemented with 1% methanol[20],[21]. When working with or preparing formalin, one should 
always take extra precautions, as it has been shown to have several detrimental side effects like 
pneumonia, bronchitis, focal necrosis in both liver and kidney[22], and cancer[23], while also 
being a strong allergen, to name a few. Nonetheless, when appropriate precautions like fume 
hoods and masks are ensured, the risk of side effects becomes mostly neglectable. [21] After the 
tissue is fixated in formalin, it is then embedded in paraffin wax, which is done to ease the 
handling and cutting of the tissue and make storage easier.[24]  
 
1.2.1 Preservative properties 
When formaldehyde is mixed with water, it begins to cycle between three forms (see Figure 
2). First, water hydrates formaldehyde, creating formaldehyde monohydrate (also known as 
methanediol or methylene glycol). This reaction happens rapidly as the equilibrium strongly 
favors formaldehyde monohydrate, meaning that only small amounts of formalin are native 
formaldehyde. Over time, if left undisturbed, formaldehyde monohydrate begins to 
polymerize; a dehydration process creating long chains of polyformaldehyde (also known as 
polyoxymethylene glycol (POM) or polyacetal). However, polyformaldehyde does not impact 
formalin’s ability to fix tissue, as it rehydrates and depolymerizes back into formaldehyde 
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
monohydrate when it meets the minor alkaline environment in tissue. Moreover, the 
polymerization process is also somewhat inhibited by the addition of methanol.[21],[25] 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
As tissue is introduced to formalin, formaldehyde monohydrate and formaldehyde quickly 
penetrate it. The small amount of present formaldehyde then begins to bind covalently to 
biomolecules, cross-linking and locking them together. As the crosslinking begins to use the 
small supply of formaldehyde, the ratio between formaldehyde monohydrate and formaldehyde 
increases, shifting the equilibrium to dehydrate more formaldehyde monohydrate into 
formaldehyde, thereby keeping a constant supply of formaldehyde for fixation of the 
tissue.[21],[25]-[27] 
Protein crosslinking mainly occurs between formaldehyde and primary amines, namely those 
on the side chains of lysine, arginine, asparagine, and glutamine, but formaldehyde will also 
bind to the purine of tryptophan, the thiol of cysteine, or tyrosine and histidine. The entire 
process requires two molecules of formaldehyde and occurs over three major steps. First, 
formaldehyde binds to the primary amine of the amino acid and dehydrates it, creating an 
aminomethylol group. This methylol group then reacts with another molecule of formaldehyde, 
which creates the beginning of a methylene bridge with an exposed reactive ketone. The 
reactive oxygen is then free to close the methylene bridge with another amino acid or another

biomolecule like DNA or RNA (see Figure 3). However, these reactions are not 100% efficient

1.2.2 Reversing the crosslinks 
While FFPE has proven to be a strong fixative that not only presents the challenge of 
completely intertwining every biomolecule but at the same time locks everything in a paraffin 
barrier, it has become a general consensus that treating FFPE to high temperatures in 
combination with an alkaline environment, will reverse the biomolecular crosslinking, while 
at the same time melting the paraffin barrier[29]-[31], thereby theoretically releasing the cells to 
the surrounding solution. This protocol attempts to adopt these strategic norms in combination 
with strong lysis buffers and sonication. Though heating in itself should lyse the cells, it is 
possible that the fixation has rendered them too robust, and extended measures are therefore 
ensued to ensure sufficient lyses. Following these steps, the idea is then to isolate and extract 
the proteins using paramagnetic beads and the SP3 preparation. 
 
1.3 Single-Pot, Solid-Phase-enhanced Sample Preparation 
SP3 is a powerful multipurpose technique introduced in 2014.[33] It has since become a robust 
platform for protein purification and buffer exchange for downstream LC-MS analysis. Most 
lysis buffers are not compatible with LC-MS instruments, meaning that buffers like Sodium 
Dodecyl Sulfate and Guanidinium Chloride, which do not easily vaporize, will suppress 
peptide signal intensity and can clog the column resulting in constant maintenance and 
increased costs for replaceable parts. This problem only becomes more prominent when FFPE 
tissue is introduced to the equation, as both paraffin and most cell debris only add to the 
clogging of the column. This is the problem to which SP3 provides a solution, as it allows for 
the isolation and washing of proteins, from which the proteins can be eluted and digested in an 
LC-MS compatible buffer. 
 
1.3.1 Properties and application 
 The SP3 protocol uses superparamagnetic beads, typically made from a polystyrene core 
coated in either single or multiple layers of magnetite, which are covered with layers of 
polymers. The outer layer of the beads is then modified with carboxylate, which forms 
carboxylic groups bound to the outside of the beads (see Figure 4).[32] The carboxylic coating 
is proposed to function similarly to hydrophilic interaction liquid chromatography (HILIC). 
The beads create a hydrophilic environment in which protein attraction and binding can occur. 
The magnetic SP3 beads can then be isolated through to use of magnets, at which washing of 
the beads to remove buffers and other contaminants is easily performed without significant 
loss. After sufficient washing, the proteins can then be eluted from the SP3 beads by 
introducing them to an aqueous solution.[33]-[36] 
 
 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
While the SP3 protocol in itself does not introduce significant loss, it is a long and tedious 
procedure, especially as the number of samples increases, and is, therefore, often prone to user 
errors. When working with samples on the scale of 100 to 200 cells, any loss during pipetting 
could lead to a reduced signal intensity, rendering the peptide undetected as the software cannot 
distinguish it from background noise. Therefore, during protocol optimization, this project 
likewise attempted to improve the ease of use for the users by changing the workflow and by 
implementing a new 3D printed adapter for improved contact with the magnet used for the 
isolation of the beads.

1.3.2 Trypsin digestion 
Trypsin is a common serine protease found in the digestive system of most animals. It was 
evolutionarily selected as an aid in the digestion of proteins. The enzyme cleaves proteins into 
shorter peptides, thereby easing the process of uptake through the lumen.[37] However, this 
efficient cleaving of proteins has proven to be highly useful in the MS analysis of proteins. The 
inherent size and nature of proteins in their natural form previously made them exceptionally 
hard to analyze using MS, as they do not easily vaporize. But by cutting the proteins into small 
peptides, the vaporizing suddenly becomes a lot more elementary. Trypsin only cleaves at the 
carboxylic end of either lysine or arginine unless they are immediately followed by a proline 
(see Figure 5).[38] This selectivity of trypsin also significantly helps in the detection of present 
proteins, as it creates a well-defined set of rules, e.g., that the C-terminal of every peptide will 
always contain either a lysine or an arginine unless the peptide originates from the C-terminal 
of the protein. In cases of arginine- and lysine-dense areas, the resulting peptides will usually 
consist of only a few amino acids. Peptides with a length of less than 5 amino acids (or longer 
for some devices) are usually discarded from the MS analysis. This is because peptides of only 
a few amino acids have a significantly higher chance of originating from a more diverse set of 
proteins, making the identification of the peptide’s origin too ambiguous.[39]-[41] 
 
Figure 5 – The proteolytic cleaves of the protease trypsin. The top panel shows the cleaves of trypsin at the 
carboxylic end of lysine or arginine unless they are followed by proline. The bottom panel shows an example of 
peptides from trypsin cleaves. The green letters represent allowed cleaving sites of trypsin, while the red letters 
are prohibited sites due to the following prolines. It is common for MS instruments only to include peptides with 
a length of 5-50 amino acids. The crossed-out peptides are examples of peptides that would be too small and, 
therefore, too ambiguous for protein building.{7}

1.4 Liquid Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry 
LC-MS combines the strength of the precise elution control from LC with the ultra-sensitive 
mass-analyzing capabilities of MS. The first example of modern chromatographic methods 
coupled to MS was achieved in the 1950s using gas chromatography (GC). While at that time 
efficient, it was still held back by the need for the vaporization of molecules, rendering larger 
biomolecules, like proteins, impractical for GC-MS, as the analyte is required to be volatile. It 
was not until the 1980s, when John Bennett Fenn introduced the electrospray ionization (ESI) 
source[42], that MS became a sturdy and viable method for the analysis of peptides and, in 
extension, proteins. Forty years later, far advancements in MS have improved ultra-sensitive 
mass analyzers like the orbitrap, linear ion trap (LIT), and the time of flight (TOF), which in 
combination with the ESI source and improved high throughput LC, has created a robust 
platform for proteomics.[43] 
 
1.4.1 Liquid Chromatography 
LC works by the introduction of a liquid analyte to a column with a tightly packed material. 
This packed material is referred to as the stationary phase, as it is fixed, and the analyte moves 
through it. Once the analyte has been introduced to the column, an elution gradient, called the 
mobile phase, is then initiated, and the different compounds of the analyte will begin to migrate 
through the column at rates correlated to their chemical properties (see below). Most LC 
methods work by this mechanism, but there are also exceptions, such as size exclusion/gel 
filtration LC, where the analyte moves through a porous gel and is separated by its physical 
properties, namely physical size.[44] 
For many years the most commonly used method for MS analysis was normal phase LC (NP-
LC), which acquired its name after the introduction of reverse phase LC (RP-LC). They both 
work very similarly, with the only significant difference being their reversed elution profiles, 
which is where the name “reverse phase” originates. NP- and RP-LC both work on the premise 
that molecules of similar chemical properties attract. In NP-LC, the stationary phase consists 
of polar silica particles, and the analyte is introduced to the column through a mobile phase 
with low polarity. The higher polarity of the stationary phase promotes stronger polar 
interaction (hydrogen bonding and dipole-dipole interaction), and the analyte will therefore 
bind to the column. As the mobile phase is gradually changed to a more polar substance, the 
less polar compounds of the analytes will begin to “prefer” the mobile phase and elute from 
the column. This continues until the mobile phase has reached a satisfactory level of polarity 
and all the analyte has eluted.[45] 
RP-LC works in a very similar fashion, but in this case, the silica has been alkylated with 18-
carbon-long chains of alkanes (known as C18); these chains create a highly hydrophobic 
stationary phase. The analyte is then introduced through a mobile phase with very high polarity, 
such as water. Here the analyte binds through hydrophobic interactions such as London 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
dispersion forces and Van der Waals interactions and is eluted by decreasing the polarity of the 
mobile phase, usually by either increasing the concentration of acetonitrile or methanol (see 
Figure 6). Since the introduction of RP-LC, it has almost completely replaced NP-LC for MS 
analysis, as it has proven to have stronger peptide binding and provides a sharper elution 
profile.[46],[47] 
 
Figure 6 – An overview of reverse phase LC. Illustration A shows the innerworkings of a reverse phase column. 
The column is tightly packed with silica beads that have been alkylated with 18-carbon-long alkanes (C18). Due 
to the nature of the alkanes, the beads are highly nonpolar, creating strong hydrophobic interactions with less polar 
compounds and weaker interactions with more polar ones. Illustration B shows the elution profile of a reverse-
phase chromatogram (the red line is the acetonitrile concentration, and the blue line is the analyte). As the 
concentration of acetonitrile increases, the less-and-less polar compounds will begin to elute from the column.{8}

1.4.2 Mass Spectrometry 
Since its debut, MS has become one of the strongest tools for identifying and quantifying the 
molecular content of samples. It allows for extremely accurate measurements of mass-to-
charge ratios (m/z), with errors on the scale of ppm’s [48]. Because of these extreme precisions, 
analytical software can utilize a set of fragmented spectra, in combination with the known 
distribution of isotopes, to estimate analyte content. However, with proteins as the analyte of 
interest, highly advanced algorithms using robust statistical pipelines that model probabilities 
to control false discovery rates (FDR) are required to estimate which proteins are present from 
the measured peptides.[49],[50] 
The MS analytical method used in this study starts with the LC-eluting peptides traveling 
through the column to the narrow end of the ESI source. At the end of the ESI source, the 
analyte is exposed to a current of a few kV, creating charged droplets. This both ionizes the 
analyte and allows it to begin the transition from a liquid into a gaseous state. The charged 
aerosol droplets are then guided by a stream of nitrogen. During travel, the droplets quickly 
begin to vaporize, creating an increasing surface charge density, which eventually becomes 
strong enough to transition the analyte to the gaseous phase (see Figure 7A).[51] The ion gas is 
then guided into the first in a series of ion filters, the High Field Asymmetric Waveform Ion 
Mobility Spectrometry (FAIMS). In the added-on FAIMS device, the ions are exposed to an 
oscillating electric field that only allows ions with a stable flight path to traverse around the 
cylindrical inner electrode and exit into the MS (see Figure 7B); this both increases sensitivity 
and reduces noise, but also has the trade-off of also reducing signal intensity.[52],[53] Ions 
entering the MS are captured and focused through the ion funnel (previously called S-lens), 
from which they are guided to and through the bent flatapole. The bent flatapole, also called 
active beam guide, acts as the second filter by guiding only ions through a 90° curve. Any 
molecules with a neutral charge will follow a straight path and will not continue further into 
the instrument. The bent ions are guided to the final filter, the quadrupole. The quadrupole 
consists of four parallel rods, each with both a static potential and an alternating potential. By 
carefully adjusting the alternating potential, the quadrupole is capable of isolating and guiding 
ions of desired m/z ranges to the C-trap.[54] The C-trap functions as a hub for accumulating 
ions. It can guide them to either the orbitrap mass analyzer or to the higher-energy collisional 
dissociation (HCD) collision cell. For the initial MS spectra, the ions are first guided to the 
orbitrap (see Figure 7C). In the orbitrap, the ions circle the inner electrode while at the same 
time oscillating along the “wide” axis. The frequency of the oscillations is directly related to 
the m/z of the ions. The alternating currents produced by the oscillating ions are amplified, and 
the overlapping sinusoidal waves are logged. The final MS spectra are then generated by 
isolating the individual waves using the Fourier transform (see Figure 7D). [49],[50] 
These MS-generated spectra show the m/z of the individual peptides, but without further data, 
sequencing the peptides is practically impossible. The m/z values of the MS1 scan could have 
been derived from a vast number of different amino acid sequences and lengths. Therefore, a 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
subsequent set of peptides are fragmented, from which the generation of second MS spectra 
can begin, and the amino acid sequences can be determined.

1.4.3 MS/MS (MS2) 
MS/MS or MS2 refers to a second MS analysis performed after the initial one. Ions of selected 
m/z values are isolated by the quadrupole and accumulated in the C-trap, from which they 
continue into the collision cell. Inside the collision cell, ions are exposed to an inert high-energy 
collision gas, usually nitrogen. Collision gases with very high energies are capable of splitting 
the peptides at arbitrary places creating new ions, while gases with lower energy can fragment 
functional groups of other regions of the ions, creating neutral losses like water or ammonia. 
After fragmentation, persisting ions travel back through the C-trap and into the orbitrap for a 
subsequent MS spectra (see Figure 8A).[55],[56] 
The generated MS2 spectra contain a variety of all the possible fragmentations of the original 
peptide; these fragment ions are designated b- and y-ions. The b-ion fragments all contain the 
N-termini-located amino acid, while the y-ions contain the C-termini-located amino acid. It is 
possible to determine the “orientation” of precursor peptides due to the cleaving nature of 
trypsin. Because trypsin cleaves at the carboxyl of lysine and arginine, the C-termini of the 
peptides will always contain either lysine or arginine, while the N-termini can contain any 
arbitrary amino acid, except for lysine, arginine, or proline. With the known orientation, 
intelligent computer programs can begin building peptides based on the differences in ion 
masses. Because the precise mass of all amino acids is known, the amino acids can be 
determined by observing the differences between m/z peaks.[57]-[59] The example shown in 
Figure 8B illustrates two peaks with a mass difference of 129 Daltons, and since glutamic acid 
is the only amino acid with a mass of 129 Daltons, the heavier ion is determined to contain an 
additional glutamic acid. Some amino acids, e.g., glutamine and lysine, only have a mass 
difference of a few hundredths of a Dalton, which has become a neglectable problem with the 
modern sensitivity of MS. However, leucine and isoleucine are, as the name suggests, isomers 
with the exact same monoisotopic mass of 113.08406 Daltons, [60] that is known to have caused 
problems in the past. Highly intelligent algorithms are generally capable of traversing this 
problem, but other suggestions, like using MS3 data, have also been proposed as a solution for 
the leucine-isoleucine problem.[61] 
Described this far have been examples of single peptides, which is usually the approach of 
DDA. However, using DDA severely cripples the number of detections, as peptides of lower 
relative abundance will not be selected for fragmentation, and therefore, not detected. Thus, a 
broader approach is desired, namely DIA, yet DIA-analyzed spectra contain multiple peptides 
with overlapping spectra. To solve the sequences of all present peptides requires extremely 
developed algorithms, which have previously limited the depth to which MS could reach. 
However, with the horsepower of modern computers combined with more sophisticated 
algorithms, DIA-MS is now capable of far greater proteome coverages.

1.4.4 Data Dependent- and Data Independent Acquisition 
DDA and DIA refer to two distinct methods of acquiring MS2 data, each with its own pros and 
cons. DDA was, for many years, the preferred and, in many cases, the only feasible method of 
acquiring MS2 data in a non-targeted manner. DDA’s method of action is to isolate only the 
most intense peaks from each MS1 spectra for MS2 analysis (see Figure 9A). This is highly 
efficient when searching for specific compounds in the analyte, as it is both cheaper, faster to 
analyze, and easier to set up, but it lacks the required protein coverage needed for extensive 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
spatial proteomics.[62],[63] DIA, on the other hand, has no bias when choosing ions for 
fragmentation. Instead, it simply selects windows of m/z ranges across the entire MS1 spectra 
for fragmentation and MS2 analysis (see Figure 9B). This, in theory, means that with 
comprehensive data collection, MS2 spectra of all peptides within the analyte are acquired and 
can be mined. DIA is very computationally demanding and requires highly intelligent 
algorithms to extract individual peptide spectra from numerous overlapping spectra. But DIA 
goes hand-in-hand with the general theme of retrospective studies throughout this project, as it 
allows for present data collection and can be re-mined in the future as the algorithms 
mature.[62],[64],[65] As an example, this was experienced during this project, where the DIA 
analyzing software Spectronaut was able to detect 10,160 peptides using version 16, but 14,379 
peptides with version 17; that is more than a 41% increase in peptide detection from the same 
MS acquired sample, which could potentially reveal undiscovered biomarkers, or unlock 
signaling cascades previously not understood.

1.5 Pancreatic Ductal Adenocarcinoma 
Pancreatic Ductal Adenocarcinoma, or PDAC for short, is projected to become one of the major 
causes of cancer-related death by 2030, with an average survival rate of less than 10% within 
the first five years.[66],[67] A reason for this high mortality rate is partially owed to its low 
detectability and its very high therapy resistance. Most patients do not develop symptoms until 
late stages of disease development when the cancer cells have spread to other regions. 
Treatment is usually extremely difficult, as PDAC is known to be chemo-resistant due to 
complex interactions between the tumor cells and the microenvironment, dominated by dense 
fibrotic tissue. Successful treatments using surgical resection in combination with 
chemotherapy are mostly limited to patients of early diagnosis, as the metastases perplex the 
action of treatment, and even patients of assumed efficacious treatments have a high chance of 
relapse.[68] 
PDAC develops from either microscopic and undetectable mucinous lesions or by malignant 
transformation of pancreatic cysts. The latter is interesting, as clinicians have a high use of 
diagnostic imaging, and pancreatic cysts are often an incidental finding when monitoring 
disease unrelated to the pancreas[69]. Thus, pancreatic cysts represent an opportunity for early 
detection and treatment of PDAC, from which a solid understanding of the protein signatures 
could allow physicians to determine the risk of malignant progression, wherefrom a treatment 
plan can be developed and initiated. Understanding tumorigenesis could give the tools to detect 
early signs of invasive disease before it develops into full-blown PDAC, possibly preventing 
future surgeries and significantly increasing both the treatment efficacy and the patient survival 
rate. This project is therefore intended as a step in this direction by presenting a method of 
efficiently extracting and analyzing small cell populations or potentially individual cells 
through a combination of LCM and DIA-LC-MS/MS. With the deep proteome coverages 
presented here, it is hoped that common and easily detectable biomarkers of potential adopters 
of PDAC will become within reach. The use of LCM also allows for a previously neglected 
spatial understanding of heterogeneous cell populations, further guiding researchers toward a 
better understanding of PDAC and more efficient methods of tackling its high therapy 
resistance (an example of PDAC is shown in Figure 10).

2.1 Tissue fixation 
Acquired human acinar and ductal pancreatic tissue, with approval from the Ethics Committee 
prior to the initiation of the project (J. no: VD-2018-446, I-suite no. 6701), was fixated in 10% 
non-buffered formalin at a volume of around 10 - 20 times the volume of tissue, for at least 24 
hours. After fixation, the tissue was dehydrated with an incremental increase from 70% ethanol 
to 100%. The dried tissue was cleared using xylene, and once cleared, the tissue was embedded 
in paraffin. Before sectioning of the tissue, a PEN (Polyethylene naphtalate) membrane glass 
slide was UV treated using a UVP XX-15 Series for 1 hour, followed by O/N drying at 37 °C 
in a Binder drying and heating chamber (model FD 23). Then the tissue was sectioned into 5 
μm thick slices, using an HM355S rotation microtome, then placed in a water bath at a 
temperature just below the melting point of the paraffin and “scooped” up with the dried glass 
slide. The glass slide, now containing the tissue, was then dried at 60 °C for at least 1 hour to 
ensure sufficient adhesion between the tissue and the PEN membrane. 
 
2.2 Tissue staining 
2.2.1 EpCAM 
EpCAM staining was performed similarly to what is presented by Vyberg et al., 2016[70] and 
Taube et al., 2020[71]. 
 
2.2.2 H&E 
After adhesion, the slide was deparaffinized by submerging it in Tissue-Tek® Tissue-Clear® 
Xylene Substitute for 10 minutes, which was followed by 10 dips in 99% ethanol, another 10 
dips in fresh 99% ethanol, 10 dips in 96% ethanol, and finally 10 dips in 70 ethanol. After 
deparaffinization, the ethanol was removed by submerging the slide in a vessel with running 
demineralized water for 5 minutes. Following the washing, the slide was submerged in Mayers 
hematoxylin for 4 minutes, running water for 5 minutes, 0.1% eosin in 0.1 M Walpoles acetate 
buffer for 1 minute, and finally, running water for 3 minutes. The slide was then left in the 
fume hood to dry O/N, after which it was stored in a closed container at RT.

2.3 Sample collection 
40 μL of either SDS, GdmCl, TEAB, TEAB+, PBS, PBS+, or H2O2 buffer was added to the 
lid of a 0.5 mL Protein LoBind® tube. 40 μL was found to be the smallest amount of volume 
capable of covering the entire bottom of the lid, which is essential when catapulting the tissue, 
as there is a high chance of losing the samples if the tissue misses the buffer. However, if 
buffers with low surface tension are used (or a surfactant is added), it is possible to lower the 
amount needed, which is advantageous as it reduces the chance of protein loss. Note that it is 
absolutely critical to NOT use regular 0.5 mL Eppendorf tubes, and especially not LCM tubes 
where the lid is filled with an adhesive, as it will significantly diminish any yields. The tubes 
were then individually loaded on the ZEISS PALM Microbeam Laser Microdissection 
microscope using the SingleTube 500 CM II tube holder. Cell dense areas between 781.25 μm2 
and 800,000 μm2 with a thickness of 5 μm were collected in the buffer-filled lids, using the 
LCM settings provided in Appendix 6C (exocrine acinar tissue was used for all pilots, while 
the demonstration in Figure 29 used ductal cancer cells and proximal stroma). Following the 
LCM collection, the tubes were spun down at 21,300x G using an Eppendorf microcentrifuge 
5425 (software version 1.1) with inserted 0.5 mL plastic adapters for 5 minutes to ensure that 
the tissue had released from the lid of the tube. 
 
2.4 Protein extraction and digestion 
2.4.1 De-paraffinization 
2.4.1.1 First strategy 
This procedure was inspired by a protocol presented by Mansour et al., 2014.[72] However, the 
protocol is meant for tissue samples large enough to move with tweezers, so modifications 
were made to compensate for the size differences. The samples were boiled at 95 °C for 3 
minutes in the MS-grade water they were collected in, using a Techne DRI-BLOCK® DB2D 
with a 0.5 mL tube block. They were then spun down at 10,000x G for 1 minute, the supernatant 
was carefully removed, and the samples were resuspended in MS-grade water. This step was 
performed three times, and the samples were finally resuspended in either of the previously 
mentioned buffers, after which they followed the protocol in 2.4.2. 
 
2.4.1.2 Second strategy 
The samples were boiled at 95 °C for 3 minutes in the MS-grade water they were collected. 
Since paraffin is less dense than water, it was assumed that it would float to the surface, from 
which it could then be removed. Half the volume was pipetted away, aiming for the surface, 
and then the rest was speed vacuumed away at 45 °C using an Eppendorf Concentrator Plus 
5305 (software Version 4.1). Finally, the samples were resuspended in either of the previously 
mentioned buffers, after which they followed the protocol in 2.4.2.

2.4.2 Sample preparation 
The samples were boiled at 95 °C for 5 minutes (note that if using larger amounts of sample, 
or more volume, longer periods of boiling could be required), after which they were sonicated 
for ten cycles of 30 seconds of sonication, followed by 30 seconds of cooling using a 
Bioruptor® Pico sonicator. Water was pre-cooled to 4.0 °C and kept at this temperature during 
sonication by a Huber Minichiller 300. After sonication, the samples were spun down at

2.4.3 SP3 protein extraction and digestion 
Sera-Mag™ Carboxylate-Modified Magnetic Beads & SpeedBeads GE45152105050250 
(Hydrophilic) and GE65152105050250 (Hydrophobic) was vigorously vortexed, and 40 μL of 
each (both with a stock concentration of 50 μg/μL) were mixed with 120 μL of MS-grade water. 
Note that after vortexing, the stock beads will quickly begin to settle, which is why one should 
extract the 40 μL immediately after vortexing to ensure a sufficient concentration. The tube 
was then attached to a DynaMag-2™ magnet, the supernatant was removed after one minute, 
and the beads were resuspended in 400 μL of water. This washing step was performed three 
times, after which the beads were resuspended in 200 μL of water. 2 μL of washed beads were 
added to the 40 μL of sample, followed by 60 μL of 100 % ethanol to a concentration of at least 
70 % ethanol (this is critical, as the proteins will otherwise not bind to the beads). The solution 
was then mixed gently by pipetting up and down and afterward incubated at 25 °C for 15 
minutes with 800 rpm shaking, using an Eppendorf 5382 ThermoMixer C (software version 
2.1.1) with inserted 0.5 mL tube adapters to allow for protein binding to the beads. Following 
incubation, the samples were reattached to the magnet, and the supernatant was removed after 
one minute, after which the beads were resuspended in 50 μL 80 % ethanol. This step was 
performed three times, and the supernatant was subsequently removed. Note that the 
DynaMag-2™ is designed for 1.5- and 2.0-mL Eppendorf tubes which makes it quite 
challenging to extract the supernatant from the 0.5 mL tubes without accidentally removing 
some of the beads as well since the tubes have less optimal contact with the magnet.

rpm shaking. The following day, the samples were spun down at 21,300x G for 30 seconds.

2.5 Sample analysis 
2.5.1 EvoTip loading 
First, the C18 column in the EvoTips was activated by adding 20 µL of 100 % ACN to each 
tip and spinning it through at 700x G for 1 minute using an SL 16 Centrifuge (all centrifugation 
steps are conducted at 700x G for 1 minute, except for the very last step). Then the tips were 
submerged in 100 % isopropanol for 1 minute, after which 20 µL of buffer A was added and 
spun through. The tips are now activated, and the 10 μL (50 μL for the improved protocol) of 
sample was added to the tips and spun through. The tips were then spun through with 20 µL of 
buffer A, and then finally, 250 µL of buffer A was added to the tips, followed by 10 seconds 
of centrifugation at 700x G to ensure the liquid was wetting the C18-bound samples. Moreover, 
around 1/3 of the EvoTip box was filled with buffer A to further ensure that the samples would 
not dry out.  
 
2.5.2 LC-MS/MS 
For all proteome analyses, an EvoSep One liquid chromatography[73] system was used, and all 
samples were analyzed using a 31 min (40 samples per day) stepped preformed gradient (except 
samples of pilot 2 and 3, which used 58 min instead (20 samples per day)) eluting the peptides 
at a 100 nL/min flow rate. For pilots 1 through 9, a 15 cm × 75 μm ID column (PepSep) with 
1.9 μm C18 beads (Dr. Maisch, Germany) using a 10 μm ID silica electrospray emitter 
(PepSep), while the rest was analyzed with a similar sized Aurora column with 1.6 μm C18 
beads. Mobile phases A and B consisted of buffers A and B, respectively. The LC system was 
coupled online to an orbitrap Eclipse Tribrid Mass Spectrometer (ThermoFisher Scientific) via 
an EasySpray ion source connected to a FAIMSPro device.[74] 
The MS1 scans were carried out at a resolution of 120,000 with an automatic gain control 
(AGC) of 300% and maximum injection time set to auto. The m/z range from 400-1000 was 
set, and precursor ions were sequentially isolated by 10 m/z increments for the PepSep column 
and increased to 16.7 m/z isolation windows for the Aurora column; in both cases, a resolution 
of 60,000 with AGC set to 1000% and maximum injection set to auto. An MS1 scan was carried 
out after each 200 m/z segment.[75]

3 Results and Discussion 
3.1 Improving protein yields  
The starting strategy was to collect EpCAM-stained exocrine acinar pancreatic tissue in LCM 
tubes containing an adhesive lid and rely on strong buffers like GdmCl or SDS in combination 
with boiling and sonication, to disrupt the integrity of the FFPE and lyse the cells, thereby 
releasing the proteins to the solution (Appendix 6B contains a timeline and overview of all the 
pilots conducted). The proteins would then be extracted, and the buffer exchanged for an MS-
compatible one using the SP3 protocol. With no prior experience with FFPE tissue, the method, 
while theoretically robust, proved to be highly inefficient. After an hour of boiling the tubes 
upside down in their respective lysis buffers, followed by 10 minutes of sonication, the first 
sign of complications appeared. A closer examination of the cap showed that the LCM cut 
tissue was still visibly intact and stuck in the adhesive. Nevertheless, trusting that the observed 
tissue was simply residual cell debris and that the proteins were successfully suspended in the 
buffer, the SP3 beads were added to the samples. However, this only presented further 
complications. For some unexplained reason, the sample containing 4% SDS somehow 
“dissolved” the SP3 beads. This could be compensated for by increasing the amounts of beads, 
but at that point, it was decided to discard the sample and only continue with the samples 
containing GdmCl and TEAB. Sadly, they not only resulted in yields (note that yields, protein 
groups, and IDs are used interchangeably throughout) of very few protein groups (see Figure 
12) but also showed no relation between sample size and yield, as seen with the TEAB samples, 
were the “high-yield” sample contained more than twice as much tissue as the low-yield.

From the initial results, it was clear that FFPE-MS sample preparation is not compatible with

strong lysis buffers and rough disruptive methods from liberating the cells and proteins. A new

regular 0.5 mL Eppendorf tubes would not only eliminate the adhesive problem but would also 
lower extraction and processing costs, as adhesive LCM tubes are notoriously expensive, with 
a market price of around 6 € per tube.[76]

compared to the 200 nL used in the nanowell chips. This comes with the inherent risk of 
breaking the surface tension, especially during catapulting of the tissue, resulting in spillage of 
strong lysis buffer onto the glass slide, potentially destroying the entire slide. To ensure this 
would not occur, a test using mild PBS buffer was conducted prior to the lysis buffers. All 
samples contained equal cuts of 100,000 μm2. Curiously, while the new approach greatly 
increased the yields for all buffers, the single sample of PBS showed the highest output by a 
not insignificant amount (see Figure 13). This was at first regarded as a fluke but definitely 
required further testing. Comparing the highest-yielding samples from pilots 1 and 2, the 
hanging-drop approach increased the protein yields by more than 519%.

3.2 Other findings and developments 
3.2.1 PEN membrane 
During the process of optimizing protein extraction from FFPE tissue, a distinct concern was 
kept unexplored; the influence of the PEN membrane itself. While results were steadily 
improving with each pilot, it was still unclear whether the process was limited by the binding 
of the tissue to the membrane. Unsure of exactly how to examine the impact of the membrane, 
it was left under the assumption that once the pixel was treated to heating and sonication, it 
would be sufficient to release the tissue from the membrane; yet even after heating, sonication, 
and digestion high load samples still had visible pixels stuck at the bottom of the tube. 
However, this concern was then later dismissed by the discovery of Postdoc Carolin Sailer. 
While testing the LCM capturing method using droplets, she noticed during the examination 
of the collected pixels that three pixels were present in the droplet, though she had only 
performed two collections (see Figure 25). A closer inspection of the collected pixels showed 
two “typically” looking pixels clearly containing the desired cells, while the third pixel showed 
a noticeable transparent characteristic, similar to what is observed if one was to collect only 
the PEN membrane. 
While this does not definitively prove that the membrane is always easily removable, as the 
strength of the adhesion between the tissue and the membrane is highly influenced by the 
incubation time and how they are otherwise treated, it does show that the membrane might not 
be as inhibiting of the extraction process as could have been feared. Yet, it is possible that the 
sudden drops in yields observed throughout the different pilots, which is especially prominent 
during the titration experiment, are caused because the tissue did not properly release from the 
membrane. An extensive examination of different incubation times for the PEN membrane 
could prove extremely valuable, as it could increase the robustness of the extraction. This 
would, of course, require a visual examination of each collected pixel to ensure that the tissue 
was collected properly, which is not currently possible when collecting in 0.5 ml tubes. The 
caps of these tubes extend too far out, making it impossible to focus the microscope. The 
experiment would therefore have to be conducted using the same 0.2 ml PCR tubes used in 
Figure 25. This would restrain the ability to conduct an apples-to-apples comparison, but it also 
presents the question of whether all future collections should be done using 0.2 ml PCR tubes. 
The results of pilot 3 clearly showed that protein LoBind® tubes are mandatory for efficient 
protein extraction, yet protein LoBind® are not readily available in 0.2 ml, causing a bit of a 
predicament. However, using the 0.2 ml tubes would require less buffer to fill the cap, 
potentially lowering protein losses, which could, in turn, compensate for the lack of LoBind® 
coating in the tubes.

3.2.2 Deparaffinization 
Paraffin acts as a physical barrier encapsulating the tissue/proteins, and while the tissue is 
deparaffinized during staining, small amounts of paraffin are still left behind, presenting a 
possible opportunity for improving yields. Deparaffinization is usually performed using 
xylene, a highly toxic compound (because of this, laboratories are beginning to transition to 
other less toxic alternatives, like tissue clear). Introducing toxic compounds to the developing 
protocol is obviously undesirable, so other alternatives were searched for, preferably something 
that could be expected to interact nicely with the buffers. A potential match was found in the 
article by Mansour et al., 2014[72], who presented a method for deparaffinizing FFPE tissue 
using nothing but boiling water. However, the protocol was designed for samples large enough 
to transfer between tubes using tweezers, which is not viable for the samples used here, so 
modifications had to be made from which two strategies emerged. Both strategies relied on the 
properties of paraffin. As paraffin is about 10% less dense than water, the standing theory was 
that if the tissue were simply boiled in water, then the paraffin would melt and settle at the top 
from which it could be removed, after which the remaining tissue could then be resuspended 
in their respective lysis buffers. The only difference between the two approaches was the 
methods of extracting the supernatant. During the first strategy, it was suspected that pipetting 
==PAGE:47==
46 
 
away all the liquid introduced a high risk of pipetting away the tissue/proteins, so for the second 
strategy, only about half of the liquid was pipetted away, while the rest was speed vacuumed, 
i.e., dried in a centrifuge under vacuum. 
The results were less than impressive. Conducted in parallel with pilot 4, which produced 
fantastic results, the first deparaffinization strategy yielded less than half of the top-yielding 
sample from pilot 4, hardly justifying any reason to continue, but as previously mentioned, it 
was suspected that pipetting away the entire supernatant could remove the tissue as well. 
Therefore, the second strategy was attempted and done so in parallel with pilot 5. 
Unfortunately, as this pilot performed poorly due to the suspected insufficient cooling of the 
sonication bath, the data becomes unreliable. Yet, if the relative abundance is still preserved 
across the samples, then the deparaffinized TEAB samples outperform the reference (see 
Figure 26). While further investigation is of interest, the experiment with deparaffinization was 
put on hold. As the protocol transitioned from SP3, the ongoing theory was that increasing the 
number of pipetting steps needed would only decrease the yields. However, if the sample is 
sufficiently spun down, removal of only the top half should not interact with the sample, 
implying that the addition of deparaffinization to the SP3-less protocol could increase yields, 
at least if the results of pilot 5 are to be believed. But then again, it is also entirely possible that 
the extra boiling step has no meaningful effect, that the 5 minutes of boiling is more than 
sufficient enough to release the tissue/proteins, and after which the paraffin has no negative 
effect on the yields.

3.2.3 SP3 optimization and 3D modeling 
While under the assumption that SP3 was a critical factor for high-quality protein extraction, 
different approaches for optimizing the SP3 performance, robustness, and throughput were 
attempted. Focusing on the beads themselves, the three major parameters to account for are the 
concentration of beads, the volume that the beads are suspended in, and the amount of sample. 
While one could assume that the volume plays no role if the concentration is kept constant, it 
is possible that, e.g., using high-load samples in larger volumes could require a higher 
concentration of beads to ensure sufficient contact between the proteins and the beads. To 
evaluate the three parameters, 27 samples with 2, 20, and 200 ng of HeLa lysate suspended in 
10, 20, and 40 µL of TEAB, using either 0.5x, 1x, or 2x the standard concentration of used 
beads, were made. As the SP3 method is extremely laborious (30 samples require around four 
hours of labor) with a nonlinear increase in labor per sample, creating replicates for all 
conditions would require multiple sessions, which was not accomplished simply because the 
SP3 protocol was terminated. 
Without replicates, drawing a conclusion from Figure 27 becomes somewhat onerous, but 
while there seems to be no discernible impact between the amount of sample and the 
concentration of beads used, it is possible that the volume plays an important role. Though it 
is not as apparent at 2 and 200 ng of sample, at 20 ng in 40 µL, there is a distinct drop in 
detected IDs across all samples at these conditions (the three far-right orange bars), even the 
addition of twice the amount of beads did not compensation for the increased volume. While 
requiring extensive testing to truly confirm the presented results, there seems to be a hint of 
“less is more”, meaning that if prospective LCM experiments were to adopt an SP3 approach, 
measurements should be taken to ensure that the collection volume is lowered from the current 
40 µL. Moreover, it could also be a sign that even without the SP3 protocol, subsequent 
optimizations should aspire to use less volume for a potential increase in yields. 
Though SP3 was stored away for the developed protocol, and it was proven better to drop the 
hard non-MS compatible lysis buffers for the pancreatic acinar cells used through the 
optimization, it is entirely possible that this is not an option for other types of tissue. Tissue 
requiring hard lysis buffers would still be reliant on an SP3 approach, leaving all SP3-acquired 
data potentially applicable for prospective experiments.

3.3 Outlook 
With the findings in this study, future plans have become plentiful. The first step would be to 
reattempt the conditions from pilot 9; while this is not expected to dramatically increase yields, 
knowing the impact of the sonication, just as boiling was found to increase yields by around 
25%, could prove useful if later adjustments of the protocol are to be made. Likewise, 
reattempting the digestion conditions could yield valuable data and might even allow for 
efficient digestions at shorter durations. Moreover, though without premise, conjectures of 
improved yields by increasing the digestion time have been toyed with. Without knowledge of 
the cruciality of the sonication procedure, it is not illogical to assume that digestion in itself is 
the main protein-liberating factor. During EvoTip loading of high-load samples, some tubes 
still contained visible pixels, and while it is entirely possible that this is nothing but the 
remaining PEN membrane, it is conceivable that this could be tissue remnants, still containing 
bound proteins. It is imaginable that boiling and sonication are mostly responsible for the 
removal of any remaining paraffin, while digestion is responsible for breaking the actual 
formaldehyde crosslinks. 
Next to the reattempted pilots would be to look at the tissue fixation and the synergy between 
the tissue and the PEN membrane. During fixation, many factors influence the quality of the 
process, such as the type of tissue, the size of the cut tissue, and the fixation time, all of which 
could lead to under-fixation. Under-fixation is a considerate concern, as any free proteins will 
be exposed to high concentrations of ethanol, potentially altering or impairing the structure of 
the proteins. As all samples used throughout the project originate from the same FPPE block, 
obtained yields throughout do seem to suggest that no significant impairment of the protein is 
present. Still, a deeper analysis of the fixation procedure could prove to boost yields; likewise, 
extensive measurement of the PEN membrane’s impact could prove highly useful. As 
discussed in 3.2.1, there is a precarious problem with outliers in most pilots, supposedly caused 
by the interplay between the PEN membrane and the bound tissue. Since a significant decrease 
in robustness was observed with H&E compared to EpCAM-staining, one could argue that 
staining is the leading cause, yet, this decrease is not as abundant at low- and high-load samples, 
which evidently only advocates for the membrane as the matter of cause. Unless other 
explanations are proposed, testing different adhesion times and temperatures on both EpCAM 
and H&E-stained tissue should be of high priority to ensure a more robust protein extraction. 
Though the PEN membrane is suspected of causing major outliers, it is likely not the reason 
for any minor deviation. These are likely caused by differences in pipetting and the cell 
densities of collected areas. Ideas for solutions to both problems rely on a more automated 
method of collection using intelligent AI. Allowing AI to map out and collect individual cells 
(seen as proof of concept by Mund et al., 2022[83]) designated as relevant by a trained 
pathologist would ensure that all samples are directly comparable and that only cells of interest 
are examined. Connecting this setup to 96- or 384-wellplates would then allow for extensive 
automatic collection. Furthermore, using wellplates would also require less collection buffer, 
==PAGE:52==
51 
 
which could improve yields, as discussed in 3.2.3. Having samples in wellplates also presents 
the possibility of removing the single pipetting step required to transfer samples from the 
collecting tubes to the EvoTips. By creating an adapter between the wellplates and the EvoTips, 
samples could simply be spun into the tips, which would both ensure a more efficient transfer, 
and would also greatly increase throughput. Lastly, while the PALM microbeam from ZEISS 
is highly efficient at extracting designated areas, it does potentially decrease the quality of the 
extract. Observing the lower left collected pixel in Figure 25, a black spot left by the catapulting 
laser can be visibly seen. This burnt area is likely deprived of any proteomic information, 
suggesting that consideration for switching to the Leica LMD (see section 1.1.1 for a 
mechanistic explanation of the microscopes) should be considered for improved yields. Next 
to all the optimizations, testing DNA and RNA extractions could begin, and while it was 
mentioned in the introduction that RNA was found to degrade significantly over time, it is 
possible that the poor results documented in the article by Kokkat et al., 2013[4], is caused by 
the extraction process, and not from RNA degradation, opening the potential for extensive 
spatial omics.  
As the consistency and robustness between samples increase, a more biological examination 
can begin to take place, using more sparse and valuable material. Combining the high 
sensitivity of the developed extraction protocol with the precise LCM acquisitions of only 
desired cell populations has placed a deeper understanding of spatial proteomics within arm’s 
reach. Allowing researchers in the near future to better understand how malignant cells develop 
and proliferate throughout the tissue and the body. It could enable the discovery of general 
biomarkers, easily detectable for clinicians through ELISA, immunofluorescence, qPCR, etc., 
improving diagnosis rates, especially for early adopters, which in turn improves treatments and 
survival rates. Moreover, it could even be used to understand differences in patients of the same 
diagnosis but with different phenotypes and why patients respond differently to the same 
treatments. As a demonstration of the examination between heterogenous cell populations, the 
proteome of malignant tumor cells was compared with the proteome of the neighboring stroma. 
The second stroma sample in Figure 29A shows a significantly lower number of detected IDs, 
which is expected for this sample, as it contained stroma highly distanced from the malignant 
cells, while the first sample contained stroma just bordering the mentioned cells. This was done 
in the hopes of seeing a change in protein expression as the cells gradually are located further 
away from the tumor. However, Spectronaut requires at least two samples of each condition 
for it to calculate the p-values, which meant that for any comparison to be made, the two stroma 
samples had to be pooled under the same condition, yet it still serves as an acceptable 
demonstration. To verify that the collected samples are indeed of pancreatic origin, 
confirmations can be made by searching for prominently expressed proteins of the pancreas; 
some examples are shown in Figure 29B. Relative expressions can then be visually examined 
through a volcano plot (see the examples in Figure 29C). One should be sure to normalize the 
data beforehand, as it will otherwise give a wrong impression of the relative expressions. While 
not necessary, it could also be advantageous to remove all excessively expressed keratins.

4 Conclusion 
This study has developed a method capable of exceptional yields from highly sparse material 
while at the same time immensely improving throughput. Though consistency dropped during 
the few attempts at using H&E-stained tissue, strong robustness is observed throughout most 
of the pilots using EpCAM-staining. Considering that the experimental part only accounts for 
roughly two months of the project, as the progress was completely interrupted while 
troubleshooting the LCM and the EvoSep, these dubious results observed from H&E-staining 
are expected to be a minor obstacle as the protocol matures further. With the prosed methods 
of action for further improving both yields and throughput, future plans are plentiful. Pushing 
the limits ever so close to single-cell spatial proteomics, along with other omics, giving 
clinicians and other healthcare personnel a robust platform for understanding, diagnosing, and 
treating the terrorizing diseases and malignancies of the world.
