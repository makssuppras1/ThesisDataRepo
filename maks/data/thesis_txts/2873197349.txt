Abstract
This project explores the design of an autonomous painting robot with a an emphasis on
safety and user-friendliness.
The goal of this thesis was to investigate how a wall-climbing robot for interior painting
could be developed in a way that prioritizes safe operation and ease of use for non-expert
users. Due to the complexity of wall adhesion systems the scope were limited, the pro-
totype developed for this project therefore operates on the ground, with fully autonomous
operations, to enable testing of autonomous functions, safety features and control inter-
face.
The robot was built using the Sphero RVR robotic platform extended with a Raspberry Pi
4b and equipped with various sensors to support autonomous navigation, object detec-
tion and manual control. A pump and spray module was included to simulate the painting
function. Two modes of control were developed: a manual interface using a PS4 con-
troller or Android application, and an autonomous mode with obstacle avoidance and
path planning.
The prototype was evaluated through two testing methods: a quantitative safety-feature
test, and a qualitative user study involving 14 participants. The safety tests measured re-
sponse accuracy and fault tolerance under predefined conditions, while the user study ap-
plied standardized usability, workload effort and trust assessments, including the System
Usability Scale[1], NASA Task Load Index[2] and Scale of Trust in Automated Systems[3].
Results showed that the robot operated reliably under test conditions and was generally
perceived as intuitive and safe by users. The findings highlighted a need for better com-
munication feedback and hardware integration. Although full vertical adhesion was not
implemented, the project successfully established the groundwork for a safe, consumer-
friendly autonomous painting robot. Future development should include adhesion mech-
anisms, system feedback through the application, and improved paint application.

Introduction
Automation in household environments is an increasingly explored domain in both aca-
demic and commercial contexts [4]. Among the expanding category of home-service
robotics, tasks involving maintenance and renovation, such as painting, is an opportu-
nity for innovation. One such area is the development of a wall-climbing robot capable of
autonomously painting flat interior walls. Unlike traditional floor-bound cleaning robots, a
wall-climbing robot introduces new design complexities including vertical mobility, adhe-
sion mechanics, and safety concerns [5].
This project focuses on the design and implementation of an autonomous painting robot,
emphasizing safety-related functions and user-friendliness. The desired outcome is a
system that can eventually adhere to and traverse vertical surfaces while delivering con-
sistent paint coverage. The challenges in adhesion mechanics requires extensive testing
and implementation, therefore the scope of this project limits itself to implementation of
the autonomous behavior and support features. The principles and safety mechanisms
developed are transferable to future iterations.
When developing such a system, several areas must be addressed: the integration of re-
liable sensor systems for navigation, safety protocols to minimize failures, and an intuitive
control interface accessible to users with limited technical expertise. Verification and val-
idation of the prototype requires both functional and user-centric evaluation. This project
uses multiple testing methods combining quantitative reliability tests and qualitative user
studies to assess system safety and usability. A robot platform, was extended using a
Raspberry Pi and sensor suite to prototype and evaluate the functionalities.
To structure the research in the following chapter, some research questions will serve as
a guideline to gather information that provides important details of the implementation and
design for the autonomous wall-painting robot that this project seeks to develop.
1.1
Research Questions
The following questions will be investigated further, and relevant solutions will be pre-
sented and summarized throughout the chapters.
• What factors are important to consider when designing a wall-climbing painting
robot? What limitations and challenges are commonly faced for such robots, and
what factors should be taken into account when painting flat interior walls?
• When designing for safety and consumer-friendliness; what design requirements
should be considered during development, to increase accessibility and simplicity
for users with limited knowledge of robotics?
• How can the prototype be tested, and evaluated based on the safety of the robot,
its environment and during human interaction, which requires both quantitative re-
producible data and qualitative user input?

2.1
Autonomous painting robot
This study investigates a method for automating the painting of interior walls using a
robotic arm. To determine the performance of the robotic arm, two experimental setups
were used for testing: one at full scale and another at a 1:4 reduced scale, allowing for
iterative testing with minimal investment both economically and time requirements, en-
suring scalability of its operation. The system aims to refine parameters impacting the
painted surface quality. Key parameters include the choice of spraying technique, the
spraying pattern, the robot arm’s motion speed, the paint flow rate, the wall distance, and
the air pressure, all applicable considerations for this projects prototype [6].
2.1.1
Paint application variable
This paper chooses aerography, or airbrush painting, as its painting method over tra-
ditional roller or brush’s, due to variable paint saturation, which makes even coverage
difficult to achieve. Specific pathing was designed, designated for horizontal and vertical
motions. In full-scale applications, standards recommend spacing between spray lines
from 0.15 to 0.25 meters, with an average 0.32 meters used in trials. In the scaled-down
version, these distances and speeds are proportionately reduced to the speed ranging
from 0.036 to 0.11 meters per second, and the step between spray lines between 0.04
and 0.05 meters. Paint flow is controlled by nozzle size, achieving a rate of 0.3 grams
per second in scaled tests, preventing excessive flow and droplet formation, as shown in
Figure 2.1 [6].

2.1.2
Quality assurance
Quality of the painted wall is evaluated using two primary metrics: saturation and thick-
ness uniformity. Saturation reflects the color purity and intensity, while thickness unifor-
mity evaluates visible stripes or uneven surfaces of the finished product. Image process-
ing were used to quantify saturation by comparing the painted surface to a theoretical
100% saturation level. Statistical experiments are conducted by painting sample strips
at different speeds and distances, generating saturation curves that are statistically ana-
lyzed using normality tests. The results demonstrated very small residuals, confirming its
reliability in predicting painting quality [6].
2.1.3
Project relevance
Although the project in question is not a wall-climbing robot, it offers insight into multi-
ple variables to consider when automatizing paint application. Exploration of the most
optimal pathing algorithm for paint flow, trajectory, and surface distance is important to
achieve uniform coverage. Since the prototype focus is on safety features, the incorpo-
ration of failure protocols should be included, and the design should be easily interpreted
and understood by the user. Methods for modeling and validating painting performance
contributes to solving challenges such as consistent quality and overspray prevention dur-
ing the painting process [6]. The paper also directly answers the question of application
method, excluding the traditional roller or brush because of the inconsistent paint satura-
tion, making even coverage difficult to achieve. So even though the projects are distinct
in design, the challenges faced will be similar in many aspects, aligning with the research
goals of this project. This is especially true when considering the use-case on interior
walls, performed on flat surfaces, similar to that of this study’s prototype.
2.2
Adhesion techniques
This section will explore potential adhesion techniques to evaluate and determine which
would be the most fitting for this project.
The following review papers provide an extensive analysis of wall-climbing robot adhe-
sion techniques, detailing their advantages, limitations, and recent advancements [7, 8].
The primary adhesion methods used in these robots include magnetic adhesion, nega-
tive pressure adhesion, electrostatic adhesion, and bio-inspired adhesion. Each method
presents unique advantages tailored to specific applications and environments, while also
facing inherent challenges that research is attempting to overcome [7, 8].
2.2.1
Magnetic adhesion
Magnetic adhesion is widely used in wall-climbing robots that operate on ferromagnetic
surfaces, such as industrial storage tanks, ship hulls, and steel bridges. This method

==PAGE:12==
employs magnets or electromagnets to attach to the surface, ensuring strong and reli-
able contact with the surface. Robots using this method typically showcase high stability
and load capacity, making them suitable for heavy-duty applications. The limitation of
the adhesion technique is its magnetic surface requirement, restricting its versatility and
use-case scenario, while rough or corroded surfaces reduces the magnetic properties,
affecting the adhesion strength. Addressing these limitations includes the integration of
magnetic suspensions, allowing for better conformity to curved and uneven surfaces [7,
8].
2.2.2
Vacuum adhesion
Negative pressure adhesion, or vacuum suction, is another widely utilized method that
allows robots to adhere to smooth surfaces. This technique generates a pressure differ-
ential between the robot and the climbing surface, enabling adhesion through suction cups
or continuous airflow. Traditional vacuum-based robots suffer from air leakage, which re-
duce their effectiveness on rough or uneven surfaces. With the introduction of flexible
skirts and vacuum seals the air leakage has been reduced, allowing the seal to conform
to surface irregularities, thereby improving adhesion [7, 8].
2.2.3
Electrostatic adhesion
Electrostatic adhesion utilizes the attraction between charged electrodes and the surface
it climbs. This is done to secure adhesion for the robot allowing it to traverse a variety
of surfaces. The approach is lightweight and power efficient, making it suitable for small,
energy-conscious robots. The adhesion efficiency depends on environmental conditions,
including humidity, surface conductivity, and contaminants [7, 8].
2.2.4
Bio-inspired adhesion
Bio-inspired adhesion draws inspiration from natural climbing organisms, such as geckos
and insects, which utilize microscopic fibrillar structures to adhere to surfaces. Robots
employing bio-inspired adhesion rely on Van der Waals forces, generated by specialized
polymer pads that mimic the setae found on gecko feet. This method offers advantages,
including non-destructive adhesion, reusability, and effectiveness on a variety of surfaces.
Static adhesion is often limited by its relatively low payload capacity and the tendency for
adhesive degradation over time. Development of self-cleaning adhesives and hierarchical
adhesive structures would be highly beneficial for the versatility, durability and reusability
of bio-inspired adhesion [7, 8].
2.2.5
Hybrid systems
Integration of multiple adhesion techniques to utilize the strengths of different methods.
This would mitigate their individual weaknesses which could prove useful, under the right
circumstances. An example could be by combining negative pressure adhesion with pro-
pellers for downward force, allowing robots to operate efficiently on both smooth and rough
surfaces. Integrating electrostatic adhesion with bio-inspired adhesives enables robots to
maintain stable adhesion and low power consumption. These hybrid approaches have
proven valuable in applications such as high-rise building maintenance, industrial inspec-
tion, and search-and-rescue operations [7, 8].
These adhesion techniques provides insight into their potential application and its limita-
tions in regards to surface, power consumption, efficiency and reusability. These con-
siderations are important when determining the solution for this project. All the adhesion
techniques, their advantages, limitations and research directions are shown in Table 2.1.

Hardware & sensor technology
As established in the previous section, the adhesion technique most fitting for this project
is a negative pressure. This increases versatility while being cost-effective with most chal-
lenges being solvable through electricity supply. Important aspects from the application
of paint has also been established. Avoidance of droplet formation, spraying instead of

There are a large amount of potential sensors, all with unique purposes and use-case
scenarios. A brief analysis of the sensors will be conducted, to determine which would be
most beneficial for the project. Fusion of sensor readings is important to minimize critical
failures during operation, by avoiding the reliance on individual sensor readings [9, 10].
2.3.1
Object detection & Obstacle avoidance
The construction of an autonomous robot requires a high level of independence, for it
to operate successfully. Object detection and obstacle avoidance are required to ensure
smooth navigation and decrease the likelihood of task failure. This challenge is often
addressed using sensors that employ time-of-flight (ToF) calculations or image detection,
which assess object size and estimate distances accordingly. Various sensors such as

6
==PAGE:14==
ultrasonic, infrared and image processing tools are capable of determining the distance
to objects [9].
Ultrasonic sensors provide an efficient solution to measure distances, determine velocity,
and detect and avoid obstacles. These sensors function by emitting sound waves that are
outside the human hearing range to perceive the surrounding area. The process involves
emitting high-frequency sound waves, typically above 20 kHz, through a piezoelectric
transducer. This transducer converts electrical signals into mechanical vibrations, creat-
ing ultrasonic pulses that travel through the air until they reach an obstacle or reflective
surface [9]. When these waves encounter an object, they bounce back and are captured
by the sensor’s receiver. The sensor calculates the time it takes for the ultrasonic waves
to make the round trip (ToF), utilizing the speed of sound in the medium. This calculation
provides the distance to the object, and the velocity can be estimated if multiple readings
are processed. This data is then interpreted as a digital input to the system, returning
a high or low depending on the reading [11, 9]. Ultrasonic sensors provide consistent
performance under various environmental conditions, making them a reliable, affordable
and popular option for obstacle avoidance [11, 9].
Infrared (IR) sensors, provide a different approach to object detection and distance mea-
surement. These sensors operate by emitting infrared light, which reflects off surfaces
and is captured by a receiver. To determine the proximity to an object, the intensity of
the reflected signal is analyzed, or by calculating the ToF, using the speed of light instead
of sound [9]. IR sensors are often fast, affordable, and energy-efficient, making them
suitable for applications with high speed or constantly changing distance.
There are notable limitations when it comes to infrared sensors. They are highly sensi-
tive to ambient lighting conditions, and their performance can be hindered by reflective
or absorbent surfaces. For instance, dark or matte surfaces absorb infrared light, reduc-
ing detection accuracy, while highly reflective materials can distort distance calculations.
Environmental factors such as dust, smoke, or fog can scatter IR signals, leading to in-
consistent readings [11, 9].
IR sensors is popular in robotic applications due to their compact size and fast response
time. They are particularly useful in short-range obstacle detection and proximity sens-
ing, where precise distance measurement is not the primary concern. When combined
with other sensors, such as ultrasonic, they enhance a robot’s environmental awareness,
contributing to more effective navigation and object avoidance [11].
2.3.2
Feedback & communication
To provide feedback about the operation and status, bidirectional communication is nec-
essary. Bluetooth is a common component in modern technology, enabling wireless com-
munication for both control and feedback. Bluetooth provides a low-power, cost-effective
solution for real-time data transmission, making it particularly useful for user interaction,
system diagnostics, and sensor feedback. Operating on the IEEE 802.15.1 standard [12],
Bluetooth is widely used due to its simplicity, low power consumption, and user-friendly
connectivity. It is well suited for applications requiring short-range communication, with
typical ranges of up to 100 meters. One of its key advantages is its ability to establish a
stable connection between the devices, such as a smartphone or a dedicated controller,
without having to connect either device to an external source. Bluetooth is a widely known
and used communication protocol, allowing for a user-friendly design to control the robot
during operation with minimal latency [11].
In the context of a wall-climbing robot, Bluetooth enables the operator to send movement

commands, and monitor real-time sensor data. Feedback mechanisms can include alerts
regarding battery level, paint supply, and obstacle detection. Implementing Bluetooth
Low Energy (BLE) further enhances efficiency, extending battery life while maintaining

The primary benefits of Bluetooth includes the widespread availability, low implementa-

2.3.3
Orientation handling
Maintaining stability on any surfaces requires locational feedback achievable through an
Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU). This component consists of an accelerometer, gyro-
scope, and a magnetometer, tracking movement and orientation.
The accelerometer
measures acceleration in different axes, while the gyroscope captures angular velocity,
providing data on tilt and rotational motion. Both suffers from accumulative drift, offset by
a calibrated magnetometer to minimize error accumulation [11].
As mentioned, errors accumulate over time due to drift. This is primarily caused by the
need for double integration of acceleration data, leading to increasing inaccuracies in
long-term navigation. To mitigate these issues, multi-sensor fusion is commonly imple-
mented. For instance, fusing IMU data with external references such as wheel encoders,
a device to measure the distance traveled based on the amount of motor rotations, helps
correct drift and improve accuracy. Extended Kalman Filters (EKF) or other sensor fusion
techniques are often used to refine IMU readings, compensating for cumulative errors[11].
When maneuvering around obstacles or transitioning between surfaces it is important that
the robot remains stable. Continuously monitoring pitch, roll, and yaw allows the robot to
adjust its motion to maintain balance and prevent critical failure. It is also an important
sensor to initiate the painting procedure, as its orientation should be perpendicular to
the ground before activating the painting sequence. Similar for the safety features, the
readings from the sensor should be a guideline for sequence of executions in the design
[11].
Orientation handling via an IMU offers high-frequency, real-time feedback in a compact,
inexpensive solution. The IMU requires additional corrections to counteract drift, it pro-
vides sufficiently accurate data once filtering algorithms have been applied. This allows
for reliable movement control during short operations in environments where other track-
ing measures perform poorly [11].
2.3.4
Paint application
The process of automating wall painting via a robotic system, requires control over paint
delivery, spray consistency, and monitoring. This is achieved through the combination
of a non-contact liquid sensor for detecting paint and a spray nozzle with a pump that
regulates paint delivery to the nozzle. Integration of this system helps maintain a proper
paint flow, detecting irregularities in the system, and ensuring uniform application on the
surface.

2.4
Sensor fusion
Sensor fusion is the process of combining data from multiple sensors to achieve a more
accurate, reliable, and comprehensive understanding of the environment. By integrat-
ing various sensing modalities, sensor fusion improves the accuracy of individual sensor
readings, compensates for the limitations, and backups in case of sensor failure [10].
Sensor fusion ensures the system is reliable and safe by integrating data from different
sensors, improving stability and reducing critical failure events. Redundancy from multiple
sensors enhances fault tolerance, allowing the system to compensate for sensor failures
or inaccuracies. A well-designed fusion algorithm, such as a Kalman filter or complemen-
tary filtering techniques, processes the raw sensor inputs. This filters out the noise and
discrepancies to generate a clear representation of the robot’s position and surroundings
[10].
Sensor fusion introduces new challenges in automation. One of the primary difficulties lies
in managing the discrepancies between different sensor readings. Factors such as signal
interference, environmental noise, and sensor drift can lead to inconsistent data, requiring
complex filtering and calibration techniques. IMUs, for example, suffer from cumulative
errors over time, necessitating corrections through additional sensor inputs. Ultrasonic
and IR sensors can struggle with reflective surfaces or environmental disturbances, lead-
ing to occasional misreadings [14].
The computational complexity associated with real-time sensor fusion is yet another chal-
lenge. Processing large amounts of sensor data requires efficient algorithms capable of
extracting meaningful insights while maintaining low-latency responses. The selection
of appropriate fusion methods, such as competitive, complementary, or cooperative fu-
sion, depends on the application’s requirements and the trade-offs between accuracy,
response time, and processing power. Achieving robust localization and navigation re-
mains a challenge, as fusing multiple sensor inputs to track precise movements over time
is demanding [14].

Adsorption-based robot
The wall-climbing robot described in the article by Qiang Zhou and Xin Li[30], showcases
an advancement in climbing robot technology, primarily due to its innovative use of an
electrically activated rotational-flow adsorption unit. Traditional vacuum-based robots rely
on mechanical seals and direct wall contact to generate adhesion, this adhesion technique
faces challenges as seen in Table 2.1 including: vacuum leakage on rough or grooved
surfaces, frictional resistance, abrasion from continuous contact, and limited obstacle-
surmounting ability when encountering small bulges or irregularities.
In contrast, the
rotational-flow adsorption unit generates and maintains negative pressure by harnessing
the air’s rotational inertia effect. Rapidly rotating blades generates a high-speed rota-
tional airflow within a shell, resulting in a negative pressure that produces a substantial
adsorption force without direct wall contact. This noncontact approach eliminates fric-
tion and abrasion but also permits a controlled clearance between the unit and the wall,

==PAGE:21==
enhancing obstacle-surmounting capability [30].
Experimental investigation detailed in the article shows that the square-shaped adsorption
unit fully utilizes the available area, boosting the effective negative pressure region and
increasing the adsorption force by approximately 18% compared to a circular design. The
research further examines the effect of clearance height on performance; it was found that
while the adsorption force remains almost constant when the obstacle is less than 2 mm in
height, it gradually decreases as the clearance height is increased. The larger clearance
improves the robot’s ability to climb obstacles, but reduces adhesion and slightly increases
power consumption; even at a high rotation speed of 2500 rpm and a clearance of 16 mm,
power consumption remains relatively low (around 40 W) because the design minimizes
air exchange in the shell [30].
To mitigate the decline in adsorption force and the associated increase in power consump-
tion at higher clearances, the design incorporates a soft skirt made of deformable rubber
beneath the adsorption unit. This skirt effectively restrains the free space below the unit,
thereby maintaining the negative pressure field and allowing the robot to pass over ob-
stacles, such as bulges up to 15 mm high, without compromising adhesion. Experimental
results demonstrate that at an obstacle surmounting height of 20 mm, increasing the soft
skirt height to 18 mm nearly doubles the adsorption force from approximately 10.76 N to
19.89 N (an increase of nearly 85%), while also reducing power consumption [30].
The robot integrates this advanced adsorption unit with a robust locomotion and control
system. Four independently driven wheels, each powered by DC motors with gear re-
ducers and monitored via wheel encoders. An HC-06 Bluetooth module enables wireless
control, while a brushless DC motor, drives the adsorption unit’s blades to maintain the
optimal rotational speed for adhesion and obstacle surmounting. Two prototype robots
were developed and tested on various challenging surfaces: one with 85-mm-diameter
wheels and an 8-mm clearance successfully climbed an extremely coarse building wall
featuring grooves 6 mm deep and 20 mm wide, while a second prototype equipped with
larger 130-mm wheels and a 25-mm-high soft skirt navigated a fabricated wall with three
large bulges, 15 mm high, 45 mm wide, and 180 mm long [30].
Utilizing rotational airflow to generate negative pressure without direct contact, the robot
overcomes the limitations of traditional vacuum suction methods. The combination of a
square-shaped adsorption unit and a soft skirt enhances the load capacity, allowing the
robot to carry more equipment and perform more demanding tasks, but also improves
its obstacle-surmounting ability and energy efficiency. The experimental validation of the
robot’s performance on coarse and irregular surfaces showcases the versatility and reli-
ability of the robot [30].

Educational robot repurposed
The paper by Venkat Nagarajan and Pavan Singh[32] presents a robust obstacle detec-
tion and avoidance algorithm for mobile robots. This robot relies on monocular vision,
offering an alternative to more complex stereo or multi-sensor setups. Traditional single-
camera systems struggle with depth perception and often depend on pixel gradients or
keypoint extraction, which can fail under conditions of strong shadows, sharp illumination
changes, or low-texture environments. To address these challenges, the authors propose
a method that uses a modified over-relaxed Chambolle-Pock algorithm for total variation
(TV) denoising. This iterative denoising process preserves sharp edges while smoothing
out noise in low-gradient regions, isolating areas where significant differences between
the original and denoised images indicate the presence of obstacles [32].
The approach begins with preprocessing, where the algorithm applies TV denoising to the
input image to generate a difference map that highlights regions with high pixel gradients—
these regions serve as reliable candidates for obstacle detection. By running the denois-
ing process iteratively on both the original and an inverted image, the system creates
a comprehensive obstacle map that adapts dynamically to varying lighting conditions,
whether under bright sunlight, deep shadows, or low-light infrared scenarios. This itera-
tive process refines the map, as lower iterations capture minute texture differences while
higher iterations reveal broader object boundaries [32].
The entire method is implemented and tested on a Sphero RVR platform equipped with a
Raspberry Pi and a color camera with infrared capabilities. Experimental results demon-
strate that the algorithm successfully identifies safe, traversable paths even in challenging
environments with complex lighting and shadow patterns. The method not only effectively
segments obstacles and free space but also adapts to dynamic settings by adjusting user-
defined threshold parameters, ensuring reliable performance for both indoor and outdoor
applications [32].

To evaluate the safety features of the autonomous painting robot, elements from existing

automobiles and industrial robots, the core principles can be adapted to the autonomous

These frameworks helped define the boundaries and safety metrics for the controlled
experiments.

Usability instruments

physical workload during interaction with the robot. Two versions of the System Usability

4.4.2
Statistical data
To evaluate the statistical data collected, the following questions adapted from Field and
Hole [38] were considered:
1. What kind of data will I collect?
The data collected are primarily continuous numerical data derived from question-
naire scores. These include SUS scores ranging from 0 to 100, NASA TLX workload
ratings scaled from 0 to 100, and trust ratings on a 7-point Likert scale. The Likert
items in the Scale of Trust in Automated Systems are originally ordinal data, but the
composite scores are treated as continuous for the analysis.
2. How many independent variables will I use?
The test involves one main independent variable per comparison. For example,
the interface type (controller vs. application) serves as the independent variable
when comparing SUS scores. For one-sample tests, the independent variable is
the difference from a benchmark value or the mean of the Likert-scale.
3. What kind of design will I use (experimental or correlational)?
The design is primarily experimental, with participants experiencing different condi-
tions and errors, influencing their responses to the questionnaire.
4. Will my study use a repeated-measures or independent-measures design?
A repeated-measures design is used for the SUS comparison, as the same par-
ticipants provided usability ratings for both the controller and application interfaces.
The NASA TLX and trust scales uses independent one-sample comparisons against
established benchmarks or mean score.
5. Will my data be parametric or non-parametric?
Given the scale properties and sample size, the data are assumed to meet para-
metric test assumptions. Therefore, parametric tests, including paired t-tests and
one-sample t-tests, were utilized for statistical analysis.
Following the decision process outlined in the data analysis flowchart (Figure A.1) found
in Appendix A.1, parametric statistical tests were selected for analyzing the data due to

5.2.3
Painting module
The method of painting is an important consideration for this project. A typical roller would
be ideal for reducing weight and making application contained to a controlled area. It
comes with a lot of difficult challenges such as even paint distribution on the roller, and
maintaining a consistent saturation of paint on the roller. Therefore the aerography or
spray painting will be utilized instead. This method supplies consistent and measurable
paint to the spray nozzle, it does however require a pump to draw and dispense the paint
through the nozzle, which might prove challenging to solve.
The attempted solution will consists of a spray nozzle, the paint will be supplied to the
nozzle through tubing. This tubing will be connected to a diaphragm pump, which is used
in spray paint guns. This module should be able to draw the paint from the bucket to the
nozzle, and supply enough pressure to disperse the paint onto the surface.
The supply of paint can be handled in two ways, either by drawing it straight from a paint
bucket, or by having an onboard container for the paint. Both options offers benefits and
drawbacks; the first option requires the paint to be drawn upwards, introducing gravity into
consideration, requiring a stronger pump than the latter. It will also reduce the consistency
with paint delivery, as the tubing will bend depending on position, which will affect the
operation and algorithm control necessary to determine when to resume movement after
halting. This can be somewhat circumvented with the liquid sensor positioning closer to
the pump, registering when paint has entered the pump, which can then be translated into
delay in movement on the robot.
The second option, having an onboard container, makes the implementation simple, but
limits the amount of paint drastically. It will still require every aspect of the first, but the
gravitational pull and draw time will be negligible, making the implementation simpler.
It does however make the design more difficult, as the robot must be designed with a
container onboard.

5.3
Risk management strategy
Risk management addresses potential failures during the system life cycle. According
to the INCOSE Systems Engineering Handbook, the risk management process involves
identifying, analyzing, and mitigating system risks to ensure operational safety and relia-
bility [34]. For this project, the strategy combines two complementary techniques: Hazard
Identification and Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (FMEA).
The use of both methods helps avoid confusing the probability of occurrence of a failure
mode with the likelihood of a hazardous situation. Hazard Identification, outlined in Table
5.4, focuses on identifying hazardous scenarios throughout the robot’s use phases, while
the FMEA, shown in Table 5.5, provides a prioritization of technical failure modes based
on severity (S), occurrence (O), and detectability (D).
The Hazard Identification table outlines accident scenarios tied to tasks such as position-
ing the robot, navigating surfaces, spraying paint, and maintaining or handling the unit.
Each scenario defines a distinct hazard and its associated event, such as falling during
setup, collisions due to failed obstacle detection, or paint issues. These scenarios are
categorized by life cycle phase and hazard zone, ensuring complete coverage across
operational and support tasks.
To supplement this, the FMEA examines the underlying causes of these hazardous events
from a technical perspective. It quantifies the risk priority number (RPN) for each failure
mode, enabling prioritization of efforts. Linking hazardous scenarios with their failure
modes, ensures that both the environmental and technical perspectives of risk are ad-

5.4.1
Verification approach
Verification will be performed in a controlled environment constructed to simulate a blank,
flat vertical wall. The test setup consists of a physical box, made from wooden planks with

conditions for system testing. The robot will be spraying water instead of paint, to minimize
the cleanup process, while still providing a proof-of-concept.

of the painting module, particularly whether it is properly calibrated to reach designated
areas and maintains even coverage. Sensor systems, including IMU, ultrasonic, and ToF,

subsystem will be verified independently and then as part of integrated system trials to
ensure full functional compliance under realistic but controlled circumstances.
5.4.2
Validation method
The validation of the robot will include a qualitative user test focused on usability and task
execution from a user’s perspective. The environment will change, as the test requires
participants, and accessibility to these participants might require repositioning, but with
an effort to mimic the setup as accurately as possible. This will also evaluate the robot in
slightly, but similar, household scenarios.
The test will assess the intuitiveness of the control method, responsiveness during manual
steering, and the ease of transitioning in and out of autonomous mode. The participant
will be asked to complete a predefined set of tasks.
The outcome of the validation method will provide insight into whether the system meets
practical requirements from the user’s perspective and identify areas for improving hu-

documented to improve future iterations of the robot’s design and interface.

6.3.2
Sensor systems
The robot uses external sensors, ultrasonic and laser time-of-flight (ToF), to measure
distances and perceive its surroundings. It also needs to detect when liquid is available
and trigger a pump to dispense it. Since the pump requires a higher voltage than what
the Raspberry Pi or Sphero RVR can supply, an external power source is used. A relay
handles switching the pump on and off during operation.
Ultrasonic distance sensors (HC-SR04)
The robot has three HC-SR04 ultrasonic distance sensors to provide comprehensive ob-
stacle detection coverage. These sensors are positioned to monitor the front, left, and
right directions relative to the robot’s forward travel direction. The sensors utilize ultra-
sonic echolocation principles, emitting high-frequency sound pulses and measuring the
time-of-flight to determine distance to obstacles.
Each HC-SR04 sensor requires four connections: VCC (5V power), GND (ground), Trig
(trigger input), and Echo (echo output). The sensors operate at 5V logic levels, which
Design in safety related functions and development of an autonomous painting robot
43
==PAGE:51==
presents a compatibility challenge with the Raspberry Pi’s 3.3V GPIO system. The trig-
ger inputs can be driven directly from the Raspberry Pi’s 3.3V outputs, as the HC-SR04
sensors recognize 3.3V as a valid logic high signal.
The echo outputs from the HC-SR04 sensors produce 5V signals that exceed the Rasp-
berry Pi’s maximum input voltage. The wiring diagram in Figure 6.5 illustrates the solution
implemented to address this voltage incompatibility: three identical voltage divider circuits
constructed on the mini breadboard using 1kΩ and 2kΩ resistors.
Each voltage divider circuit follows a simple voltage division principle, where the output
voltage is determined by the ratio of the resistors. With a 1kΩ resistor connected between
the HC-SR04 echo output and the measurement point, and a 2kΩ resistor connected from
the measurement point to ground, the circuit creates a 2:3 voltage division ratio. This
reduces the 5V echo signal to exactly 3.3V (5V × 2kΩ/(1kΩ + 2kΩ) = 3.3V), safely within
the Raspberry Pi’s input specifications.
Time-of-flight sensor (VL53L0X)
To provide redundancy for the object detection, a VL53L0X laser Time-of-Flight sensor
is integrated into the system. Currently only the front-facing sensor has this redundancy
integrated, as a proof-of-concept, but in future iterations, these would be applied to every
distance sensor. The VL53L0X accommodates some of the disadvantages of ultrasonic
sensors, including faster response times and resistance to acoustic interference.
The VL53L0X sensor appears in the wiring diagram, it highlights the smaller footprint com-
pared to the HC-SR04 sensors. This sensor communicates via I2C protocol using only
four connections: VCC (3.3V power), GND (ground), SDA (Serial Data), and SCL (Serial
Clock). The SDA and SCL lines are visible as green and blue wires connecting directly
to the Raspberry Pi. The direct 3.3V voltage compatibility eliminates the need for level
conversion circuits, simplifying the integration considerably compared to the ultrasonic
sensors.
The VL53L0X sensor provides distance measurements in millimeters with a range from
approximately 30mm to 2000mm, depending on the target surface reflectivity. For the
autonomous painting application, the sensor output is converted from millimeters to cen-
timeters to maintain consistency with the ultrasonic sensor measurements and simplify
the sensor fusion.
Pump and relay control
The liquid dispensing system centers around a 12V R385 diaphragm pump, exceeding the
voltage capabilities of both the Raspberry Pi and Sphero RVR systems. To address this
power requirement, a dedicated power source is implemented using a GP M2 powerbank
with 10,000mAh, visible in the wiring diagram, Figure 6.5, as the large rectangular compo-
nent. This powerbank provides stable 12V output sufficient for painting operations while
being electrically isolated from the control systems, avoiding short circuiting or overvolting
other components.
The wiring diagram shows the pump control implementation, with the relay module which
acts as an electrical switch, controlling the on/off state of the pump. The relay module
uses two terminals: the Common (COM) terminal connects to the positive terminal of the
12V powerbank, and the Normally Open (NO) terminal connects to the pump’s positive
input. The relay is connected to the Raspberry Pi’s 5V, GND for power, and the signal to
any regular GPIO.
When the signal is driven high (3.3V), the relay connected the contact between COM and
NO terminals, which completes the circuit from the powerbank to the pump, activating the
Design in safety related functions and development of an autonomous painting robot
44
==PAGE:52==
pump. When the signal is driven low, the relay deactivates, disonnecting the terminals,
halting the pump. This enables the Raspberry Pi to control the 12V pump indirectly, by
allowing access to the power supply, or restricting it.
Liquid level monitoring
To prevent dry operation, the liquid reservoir is monitored using an XKC-Y25-PNP non-
contact liquid level sensor, which detects liquid through the wall of the container without
direct contact. The sensor operates using a positive output configuration, meaning it
supplies voltage when liquid is detected and pulls the output low when the level drops
below the threshold. This allows the Raspberry Pi to detect when the liquid level falls
below the threshold and automatically pause painting operations.

Sensor integration and data fusion
The robot employs a multi-sensor approach for obstacle detection, combining three HC-
SR04 ultrasonic sensors positioned at the front, left, and right sides of the robot with
a VL53L0X Time-of-Flight sensor providing redundancy for front-facing detection. The
sensor integration strategy implements a data fusion algorithm for reliability and safety.
The ultrasonic sensor implementation follows the standard HC-SR04 protocol, where a
trigger pulse initiates the measurement cycle, and the echo pulse duration determines the
distance to the nearest obstacle. The distance function implements timeout protection
to prevent the system from hanging if a sensor fails to receive an echo:
def distance(trigger, echo):
GPIO.output(trigger, True)
time.sleep(0.00001)
GPIO.output(trigger, False)
start_time = time.time()
stop_time = time.time()
while GPIO.input(echo) == 0:
start_time = time.time()
if start_time - stop_time > 0.02:
return None
while GPIO.input(echo) == 1:
stop_time = time.time()
return (stop_time - start_time) * 34300 / 2
For front-facing obstacle detection, the system implements a conservative fusion ap-
proach in the read_all_distances function. Both the ultrasonic sensor and the ToF sen-
sor measurements are collected simultaneously, and the minimum distance reading is
selected:
def read_all_distances():
front_ultrasonic = distance(TRIG_FRONT, ECHO_FRONT)
left = distance(TRIG_LEFT, ECHO_LEFT) or 0
right = distance(TRIG_RIGHT, ECHO_RIGHT) or 0
front_tof = max(tof_sensor.range / 10, 0)
front_distances = []
if front_ultrasonic is not None:
front_distances.append(front_ultrasonic)
if front_tof is not None:
front_distances.append(front_tof)
front = min(front_distances) if front_distances else 0
return {"front": front, "left": left, "right": right}
This approach ensures that if either sensor detects an obstacle, the robot will respond ap-
propriately, preventing collisions even if one sensor fails or provides inaccurate readings.

verts raw distance measurements into boolean decision variables (can_move_forward,
can_turn_left, can_turn_right). These variables are used throughout the system to
limit the robot’s movement, ensuring that control commands and autonomous navigation
respect the sensor readings.

Autonomous navigation algorithm
The autonomous painting algorithm, implemented in the autonomous_navigation func-
tion, follows a wall-following strategy that ensures comprehensive coverage of the paint-
ing area. The algorithm is designed to create a systematic painting pattern that minimizes
missed areas while avoiding obstacles and maintaining liquid application efficiency.
The navigation algorithm begins with a forward movement phase, during which the robot
advances until it encounters an obstacle detected by the front sensors. Upon obstacle
detection, the robot executes a turning sequence that alternates between right and left
turns to ensure consistent downwards movement.
The autonomous algorithm implements an obstacle avoidance maneuver, which follows
a multi-step process to navigate around obstacles while maintaining the painting pattern.
When the robot encounters a front obstacle, it performs a 90-degree turn, acting as if
it has reached a wall, then advances laterally while monitoring the side sensor, if the
side sensors detects a change of <3 cm, it initiates the obstacle avoidance maneuver,
continuing forward for the length of the robot, then turns towards the object.

Discussion
This chapter will discuss the current prototype, including its limitations, challenges and
improvement points. This outlines the future work that is required for the robot to function
as intended in a household environment, with fully functional wall painting capabilities. It
will discuss the problems and errors that occurred during development, and explain the
changes that could resolve these issues or provide more reliable functionalities.
8.1
Design choices and limitations
The design and implementation of the autonomous painting robot presented limitations,
one of which was the use of Sphero RVR. While its availability and ease of integration with
the Raspberry Pi made implementation simpler, it also introduced several constraints.
The RVR’s internal system often presented unstable behavior without producing error
messages, complicating debugging. This instability extended to sensor readings, which
would at times report correct values, yet failed to translate into appropriate actions.
These debugging challenges were enhanced by the composition of hardware connec-
tions. A recurring issue was failures caused by faulty or loose wiring, especially common
on the breadboard, with its small size and resistor circuit, leading to incorrect or intertwined
sensor inputs, difficult to isolate. Future iterations would benefit from PCB integration to
reduce the risk of electrical interference and improve signal integrity.
The Robot Operating System (ROS) was not used in this project because the prototype’s
tasks, wall detection, linear motion, and pump control, could be efficiently implemented
using direct hardware access via Python. ROS excels in larger, distributed systems re-
quiring modularity, real-time inter-node communication, or complex sensor integration, its
benefits would not be utilized in this compact prototype, but might prove useful in future
iterations.
8.2
Evaluation of safety and usability testing
The two-phased evaluation approach, quantitative safety testing and qualitative user eval-
uation, provided an useful feedback on the robot’s operation. The safety-feature tests
validated the reliability of critical systems under repeated conditions, but the reliability of
these functions might differ when verticality and gravity is introduced. The tests were
sufficient for this prototype, testing the object avoidance, emergency stop protocols, and
sensor responses, but it might not perform to the same standard with the integration of
wall adhesion, where falling hazard becomes a critical concern.
The usability testing, using a convenience sample of 14 users, provided meaningful in-
sights into user trust, system workload, and interface preference. The NASA TLX and
SUS scores confirmed that while the control interface was generally well received, some
users found the system to lack intuition. This could be attributed to unfamiliarity with
robotic systems, emphasizing the need for intuitive design, clear feedback, and error re-
covery.

is. Utilization of Kalman Filters or similar techniques to combine IMU, ToF, and ultrasonic
readings into a fail-safe feature would be beneficial for the robots operational safety. The
current state of the application is bare-bones, requiring implementation of every feedback
feature. This would be a top priority, as the system already contains the elements, but
are currently static where they should be dynamic depending on the robots status. While
implementing these feedback features into the application, the control interface should be
changed to that of the PS4 controller. This is due to the significant difference in prefer-
ence seen in the user test. The analog joystick far outcompeted the simple directional
keys the application currently uses. Implementation of these features combined would
allow the user more accurate and precise control of the robot, while being supplied infor-
mation about the robots status through their device and notifications. Further additions
such as a troubleshooting center with FAQ or similar would also be a large contributor to
improved user-friendliness, but requires more in-depth knowledge of potential issues or
questions.
The robot currently fulfills its task of providing a platform from which a wall-climbing au-
tonomous painting robot could be built. Some observations, that could prove useful in
future development, would include the classification of larger objects as walls. This is not
inherently a problem, an does not present an issue with the robots wall painting tasks,
but it does create the issue of uneven coverage, as the robot will not navigate around the
object, and the opposing side will therefore have unpainted areas. This could be resolved
with a line sensor on each side of the robot, which would detect the color change, turn
towards it, and drive until it observed the color changing back. This would allow the robot
to navigate larger obstacles or static wall-mounted objects such as radiators or similar.
The adhesion mechanism was not implemented in this iteration but was researched. The
chosen method, negative pressure suction with a skirted vacuum system, remains viable,
but must be physically integrated and tested in future prototypes. Attention to weight
distribution, surmounting ability, and energy consumption, should be observed, as these
directly impact usability and safety.
• Wall-Climbing Mechanism: The integration and testing of a functional negative
pressure adhesion system is a priority. Future work should focus on research, cal-
culation and implementation of such a system, while maintaining the functionalities
and safety-features of the current prototype.
• ROS Integration: Migrating to ROS would facilitate modular software design and
support for SLAM (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping) opening the option for
more sophisticated navigation and safety.
• Application changes: The feedback from the usability testing provided clear indi-
cation that the PS4 control interface was preferred over the application. An attempt
to mimic the control, primarily the analog joystick, should be a priority as it allows for
more accurate and precise movement. The application should also provide more
significant and detailed feedback on the operation of the robot.
• Hardware Redundancy and Enclosure Design: Future hardware iterations should
include custom PCBs, and integrate the sensors into the system, to protect the sen-
sors, making the system less prone to outside interference and disconnection.
The project successfully demonstrated that a safe and user-friendly painting robot is
achievable using accessible components. There are several points requiring improve-
ments to achieve the desired wall-painting functionalities.

Conclusion
This chapter will conclude the project and summarize the findings to answer whether the
final problem statement from chapter 3 was fulfilled. The final problem statement investi-
gated the potential for designing a wall-climbing painting robot for use in households:
How can a wall-climbing robot, with the purpose of painting a flat interior
wall, be designed with safety-features and user-friendliness as the foundation?
What functionalities are required to ensure safety protocols, consistency, and
convenience for users with little knowledge of robotics?
To address this, a ground-based prototype was developed, tested, and evaluated, focus-
ing on autonomous behavior, sensor integration, and human-centered design. The robot
utilized redundant sensors, an emergency shutdown protocol, and intuitive control inter-
faces to prioritize user safety and accessibility. Quantitative testing confirmed functional
reliability across critical safety mechanisms and core operational systems, while the qual-
itative user study demonstrated an overall positive perception and usability, despite some
preferences with the interface of choice. The user’s had a general trust to the device,
observing and understanding most of the robots features during their brief testing period,
making the experience result in the user feeling safe and in control of the robot and its
operation.
The focus for this project was to develop the autonomous behavior and safety-features of
the robot, and did not include the implementation of the adhesion technology required for
wall-climbing. The project explored the options of adhesion techniques through literature
review, and the design of the robot and hardware planning, allows for the integration
of this system, by removing the liquid container, primarily used as a proof-of-concept for
payload evaluation. The most reliable adhesion technique for this robot were the negative
pressure, supported by soft skirts to improve the obstacle surmounting opportunities.
Key takeaways include the importance of redundancy, the challenge of debugging hard-
ware faults, and the need for transparency in control feedback.
Future development
should prioritize physical wall-climbing capabilities, improve interface design, and explore
the potential for PCB development or enclosure of the system, preventing interference
with the connections.
This project has shown that it is possible to design an autonomous painting robot that bal-
ances safety and user-friendliness. It explored the options of designing safety-features
that encapsulates the system, protecting itself, the surroundings and the people, by notify-
ing the user via visual feedback, and allows the user to regain control in case of connection
loss. The robot also features an emergency shutdown, which terminates the robots pro-
cedure, in case of system failure. Together with the feedback from the quantitative and
qualitative test, it can be determined that the robot was a success, fulfilling the problem
statements requirement of safety protocols, consistency and convenience for users with
little knowledge of robotics. The project was therefore a success, with room for improve-
ment and further iterations.
