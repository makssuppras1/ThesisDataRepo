Abstract
Groundwater, a critical component of the Earth’s water cycle, serves as a vital resource for drink-
ing water and ecosystem support. Pesticide contamination, especially in a nation heavily reliant
on groundwater like Denmark, poses a significant threat to water quality. The protective seem-
ingly impermeable clayey till, harbors complexities such as sand lenses and fractures, acting as
potential pathways for contaminants. There is limited knowledge regarding the significance of
the influence of these heterogeneities on the transport of pesticides in the saturated zone and
further down into groundwater aquifers. This study investigates the dynamics of contaminant
transport in heterogeneous soils, with a particular focus on the influence of sand lenses within
Denmark’s prevalent clayey till. The study employs numerical models in COMSOL Multiphysics
(version 6.1) to analyze contaminant fate and transport, considering heterogeneous factors such
as 2D and 3D geometry, hydraulic parameters, and chemical properties. Findings highlight
the crucial role of the rotation angle of sand lenses, while also revealing limited impact from
certain sand lens configurations. An increase in the geometry of sand lenses and layers increases
contaminant transport. Additionally, the rotation of sand lenses serves as an important factor for
contaminant fate and transport. Chemical properties, specifically sorption to soil, emerge as cru-
cial determinants influencing contaminant transport especially along with the implementation
of heterogeneities. The comparison of 2D and 3D modeling results underscores the importance
of three-dimensional aspects for accurate representations of contaminant spreading especially
in the transversal direction. The study suggests further research, including exploring additional
soil properties, and degradation processes, conducting field validations, and understanding
contaminant behavior in the unsaturated zone.

Sammenfatning
Grundvandet er en vigtig resurse for jordens vandcyklus og benyttes både som drikkevand-
sresurse og støtte til økosystemer. Pesticidforurening er et stort fokuspunkt i et land som
Danmark, der i høj grad er afhængig af grundvand som resurse, og pesticider udgør en betydelig
trussel mod grundvandskvaliteten. Det beskyttende lavpermeable moræneler rummer komplek-
siteter som sandlinser og sprækker, der fungerer som potentielle og hurtige veje for forurenende
stoffer. Der er begrænset viden omhandlende den specifikke betydning af disse heterogeniteters
indflydelse på stoftransporten af pesticider i den umættede zone og videre til grundvandsma-
gasiner. Dette studie undersøger dynamikken i forureningstransporten i heterogent moræneler,
med særlig fokus på indflydelsen af sandlinser og -lag i moræneler. Studiet anvender numeriske
modeller i COMSOL Multiphysics (version 6.1) til at analysere transporten af forurenende stoffer,
idet der tages hensyn til heterogeniteter og faktorer som 2D og 3D geometri, hydrauliske parame-
tre og kemiske egenskaber. Resultaterne fremhæver den afgørende rolle, som rotationsvinklen
af sandlinser spiller, samtidig med at visse konfigurationer af sandlinser viser begrænset ind-
flydelse. En stigning i geometrien af sandlinser og lag øger transporten af forurenende stoffer.
Derudover fungerer rotationen af sandlinser som en vigtig faktor for transporten af forurenende
stoffer. Kemiske egenskaber, især sorption til jordpartikler, viser sig at være afgørende faktorer,
der påvirker transporten af forurenende stoffer, især sammen med implementeringen af hetero-
geniteter. Sammenligningen af 2D- og 3D-modelleringsresultater understreger betydningen af
tredimensionelle aspekter for nøjagtige repræsentationer af forureningsudbredelser, især i den
tværgående retning. Studiet antyder yderligere behov for undersøgelser, herunder udforskning
af yderligere jordegenskaber og nedbrydningsprocesser, gennemførelse af feltundersøgelser og
forståelse af stoffers adfærd i den umættede zone.

1 Introduction
Groundwater, a vital component of the Earth’s water cycle, is stored in aquifers, serving as
subsurface reservoirs for drinking water supply and ecosystem support [1], [2]. Its prevalence
for domestic use is driven by its availability and reduced susceptibility to pollution compared to
surface water due to natural purification through the subsurface [3], making treatment more
cost-effective [3], [4]. In Denmark, groundwater accounts for nearly all fresh water supply [5],
while across 27 European Union Member States, 65% of drinking water and 25% of agricultural
water originate from groundwater [6]. Globally, approximately 50% of drinking water and 70% of
agricultural water are sourced from groundwater abstraction [7], underscoring its significance.
Consequently, safeguarding against overdraft and contamination is imperative [8], [9], aligning
with various of the UN’s Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), notably SDG 6, 12, and 13 [10],
[11]:
• SDG 6, "Clean Water and Sanitation", emphasizes protecting water-related ecosystems.
• SDG 12, "Responsible Production and Consumption", emphasizes safeguarding ground-
water by preventing pollution and over-extraction
• SDG 13, "Climate Action", significance remains underrepresented in the current frame-
work, necessitating increased recognition and integration.
As societies attempt to fulfill these goals, understanding the dynamics of groundwater pollution
becomes paramount. In the context of Denmark, a nation heavily reliant on groundwater, the
escalating trend of contamination, particularly by pesticides, poses a significant challenge to
water quality [12]–[14]. The extensive protection of Danish aquifers by low permeable soil
layers like clayey till is a natural barrier. However, within this seemingly impermeable shield
lies a network of complex heterogeneities, including sand lenses and fractures, which can
act as swift pathways for contaminant transport. Thus, creating vulnerable points for the
aquifer, contributing to the potential spread of contaminants [15], [16]. Clayey till, constituting
a substantial portion of Denmark’s geological composition, is characterized by these intricate
heterogeneities presenting challenges in their accurate identification and localization [17].
Understanding the impact of these geological features on pollution transport in heterogeneous
clayey till is a critical aspect of ensuring the protection of groundwater.
1.1
Aim and Objectives
This report aims to enhance the understanding of contaminant fate and transport in aquifers and
aquitards by analyzing the influence of complex heterogeneous geologies on transport processes
by model-based investigations. The study focuses on the role of sand lenses in the formation
of distinct flow patterns within clayey till influencing the migration of pesticides and their
degradation products in the saturated zone. The model-based investigation employs numerical
models that simulate contaminant transport across a spectrum of geological conditions and
scenarios, thereby identifying and elucidating the crucial pathways.The specific objectives are
as follows:
• Geometry of sand lenses: Investigate the influence of sand lens characteristics, includ-
ing size, placement, and orientation within a clay matrix of clayey till, on contaminant
transport.

• Hydraulic parameters: Examine the impact of sand lenses with varying hydraulic conduc-
tivity on contaminant transport in clayey till. Explore the role of sand layers as secondary
aquifers with horizontal flow, impacting the fate and transport of subsurface contami-
nants.
• Chemical properties: Analyze the behavior of different pesticides and degradation prod-
ucts of pesticides concerning adsorption within the clay matrix of clayey till along with
incorporating the presence of sand lenses.
• Difference in contaminant flux from base scenarios: Evaluate how the relative flux is

• Use and comparison of 2D and 3D modeling results: Use 2D and 3D model setups to
describe contaminant fate and transport and comparison between these model setups to
provide further knowledge on contaminant fate and transport for the scenarios.

2 Background
2.1
Pesticide Contamination in Danish Groundwater
Groundwater faces contamination threats from anthropogenic activities like industry, agricul-
ture, and waste disposal, which may introduce a range of contaminants, including chlorinated
solvents, heavy metals, pesticides, and organic compounds into aquifers [18], [19]. A group of
compounds that are of great concern for contamination of groundwater are pesticides which are
chemical compounds employed for the eradication of various pests, encompassing rodents, in-
sects, plants, and fungi [20]. The historical practice of using various pesticides to eliminate pests
dates back to ancient times [21], [22], while the introduction of synthetic organic compounds
as pesticides occurred in the 1930s [23]. These synthetic organic compounds proved to be
highly problematic for the environment. After the 1970s till the present time, the use lower-risk
synthetic organic compounds are in use [24], e.g. in agriculture to increase crop yield [25], to
eliminate pests, or to prevent diseases from spreading [26]. These modern pesticides are a great
concern due to their toxicity and can be persistent, bioaccumulative, and mobile in soil and
groundwater [27]–[29]. Thus, the detection of these substances in groundwater can pose health
and environmental risks. The properties depend on the compound-specific characteristics and
also the soil composition [30], [31].
Pesticides entering groundwater may originate from point sources, characterized by concen-
trated contamination over small areas (e.g., pesticide storage facilities), and area sources, where
lower concentrations spread across larger areas (e.g., agricultural pesticide application). Vari-
ations and overlaps can occur, with some area sources resembling point sources in terms of
concentration [32]. In Danish agriculture, many pesticides like dichlobenil, chloridazon, and
atrazin have been used from the 1960s to the 1990s where these were phased out [12], [33]. These
pesticides pose a problem as they still exceed the established groundwater quality criteria in the
present time. In addition, the degradation products of pesticides too have proven problematic.
Many degradation products are measured in the Danish groundwater where the degradation
products desphenyl-chloridazon (DPC) and N,N-dimethylsulfide (DMS) [34] have been reported

2.2
Geological Influence on Contaminant Fate
The subsurface location of groundwater makes it challenging to study and manage [36]. Con-
taminant transport in groundwater is affected by advection, dispersion, diffusion, retardation,
degradation [37], and flow patterns created by geological features [38]. This complicates con-
taminant fate in aquifers and aquitards, where aquifers store water efficiently and aquitards
serve as hydraulic barriers with low permeability (e.g. clay layers) [39], [40]. In Denmark, glacial
processes have created subsurface heterogeneities, forming clayey till covering great parts of
Denmark as shown in Figure 2.3, where clayey till is depicted with a dark brown color. Many
of the drinking water wells from Figure 2.1b are located in areas with clayey till, which makes

it important to understand the transport processes within this type of aquitard. The thickness
of clayey till in Denmark exhibits considerable site-specific variability, ranging from less than
10 meters in certain locations to exceeding 50 meters in others [41], [42]. Sand lenses, frac-
tures, and macropores (e.g. created by worms) in clayey till can enhance the effective hydraulic
conductivity, further complicating contaminant transport [43]–[47].
Fractures in clayey till can appear as vertical, horizontal, diagonal, and a combination of these.
Vertical fractures are often found to extend around 5-10 meters into the clayey till in both
Denmark and other locations in the world [41], [46], [49], [50]. The fracture aperture in clayey
till is typically measured to be 1-20 µm and decreasing with depth due to increasing pressure
[45], [51]. Advection dominates contaminant transport within fractures, but diffusion into the
surrounding clay matrix may occur. As contamination in the fractures diminishes, there can be
subsequent diffusion of contaminants from the clay matrix back into the fractures [45], [52].
A study by Kessler et al. [44] analyzed variations of sand lenses in clayey till at different locations

in Denmark. The variations of the sand lenses are categorized into different classes depending

3 Model domain setup
The investigation of contaminant fate and transport of pesticides and their degradation products
within heterogeneous clayey till with sand lenses and sand layers is conducted via a series of
distinct computational models. These models encompass a 2D vertical and horizontal flow
model and a 3D horizontal flow model. The 2D horizontal flow model is utilized to examine
the vertical transport mechanisms within specific locations of interest, such as point sources
like pesticide storing facilities where higher concentrations are found. The 2D horizontal flow
model is utilized to analyze the influence of horizontal groundwater flow on the transport
processes occurring over extended distances downstream. Additionally, a 3D model is applied
to investigate transport processes influenced by variations in spatial dimensions, offering a
comprehensive understanding of pesticide migration in diverse geological settings of clayey till.
The models investigate geometrical parameters for sand lenses and layers along with hydraulic
parameters and adsorption to soil. In all three model setups, the simulation time is set to 100
years, except for scenarios investigating adsorption where the simulation time is prolonged. A
time step of 0.5 years for the 2D horizontal flow model and a time step of 1 year for the 2D and
3D horizontal flow models are used in the model. The point source is represented as a pulse
source persisting for the first 50 years of the simulation.

2D Vertical flow model

occurs when investigating rotated sand lenses to prevent impact on the breakthrough curve

Model Parameters
This section lists and explains the parameters used in the model setup. All the parameters used

The hydraulic conductivity, K, is generally greater horizontal than vertical for sand layers and
intact clay matrices. The ratio between the vertical hydraulic conductivity, Kz and horizontal
hydraulic conductivity, Kx and Ky, is often shown to be between 0.1 and 0.5 [55], [56]. Thus, Kz is
determined as one-fourth of Kx in the model. On the other hand, the addition of vertical fractures
can affect the effective hydraulic conductivity to become greater in the vertical direction. Various
studies show a range of 10−7 - 10−11 m/s for the hydraulic conductivity in clayey till with the
majority of the cases within a range of 10−7 - 10−9 m/s and for sand a range of 10−3 - 10−6 m/s
[57]–[59]. The hydraulic gradient in the aquifer is selected based on those observed values in
Denmark [60], while infiltration rates are derived from values in the DK-model [61], [62].

4 Model equations
In the investigation of various geological scenarios, the model builder software COMSOL Multi-
physics (version 6.1) serves as a vital tool. This software use the Finite Element Method (FEM)
to discretize the model domain into smaller elements, facilitating a comprehensive analysis.
Within this framework, complex geological phenomena are simulated and modeled through the
application of problem-dependent mathematical equations. These equations are customized
to account for the specific attributes and interactions present across diverse domains. The
modeling approach plays a crucial role in gaining valuable insights into the intricate geological
processes under examination, making it an essential component of the research methodology
[68], [69]. The models are solved using MUltifrontal Massively Parallel direct Solver (MUMPS).
For modeling the flow and contaminant transport in the geological scenarios, the physics Darcy’s
law and Transport of Diluted Species in Porous Media are used in COMSOL Multiphysics.

4.5
Mesh
In the context of simulation, a mesh is a discretized representation of the model’s geometry,
breaking it down into interconnected elements for numerical analysis. The mesh in COMSOL
Multiphysics is systematically tailored to optimize simulation accuracy and efficiency. Notably, it
attains high refinement at the source pathways, at layer transitions, and in areas with significant
hydraulic gradients, e.g. at fractures. In specific layers, such as the clay matrix, a finer mesh is
implemented compared to the aquifer due to a greater hydraulic gradient within the clay matrix.
For 2D models, a finer mesh is generated than in 3D models to avoid exceeding the computer
capacity. The 2D vertical flow base model comprises around 500,000 mesh elements, the 2D
horizontal flow base model around 1,500,000 elements, and the 3D horizontal flow base model
incorporates approximately 6,000,000 mesh elements.

10 Discussion
Varying sand lens and layer geometries reveal accelerated contaminant breakthroughs with
increased size, particularly in the vertical dimension within the clay matrix beneath the source
zone. Higher rotation angles of sand lenses also demonstrate significant contaminant break-
throughs, aligning with expectations of increased effective hydraulic conductivity for vertical
transport [92]. This hydraulic conductivity influences flow velocity, crucial for contaminant
transport in porous media, as detailed in Section 4. Rotated sand lenses with substantial rotation
angles create columns of elevated hydraulic conductivities, establishing a more direct pathway
for contaminant transport through the clay matrix, as supported by other studies [93], [94]. In
the model setup for this study, a 90° rotated sand lens shows approximately four times greater
contaminant flux compared to the base scenario and other rotations. Conversely, investigations
into the vertical placement and spacing of sand lenses yield no discernible effects on contami-
nant transport. The effective hydraulic conductivity for vertical flow remains consistent across
these scenarios [92], suggesting an absence of differences in vertical contaminant transport
within the clay matrix, although a significant difference from the base scenario is observed.
Several studies [44], [45], [93], [94] highlight the significant impact of heterogeneous soils on
contaminant transport. Additionally, numerous studies emphasize the importance of imple-
menting fractures in numerical models for contaminant transport within the subsurface [44],
[46], [51]. The most critical geometrical parameter for contaminant transport in this study has
been the rotation angle of the sand lens creating a direct flow path through the clay matrix and
enhancing the contaminant flux into the aquifer significantly.
Some implementations of sand lens layers within the clay matrix in this study show little signifi-
cant enhancement for contaminant transport compared to the base scenario. Sand lenses and
layers around 0.5 m thick and less exhibit very similar results of contaminant flux and concentra-
tion to the base scenario in the 2D horizontal flow model setup. This will only constitute 5% of
the thickness of the 10 m thick clay matrix. Kessler et al. [44] conducted an investigation of sand
lenses within clayey till at various locations in Denmark and categorized sand heterogeneities
into different classes, as shown in Table 10.1, where thicknesses below 0.5 m are applicable for
sand stringers, sand pockets, sand sheets, and very thin sand bodies. Thus sand layers with an
added horizontal flow will be unlikely and also difficult to locate.

Changing the hydraulic parameters for sand lenses embedded within the clay matrix shows
unexpectedly little impact on the contaminant transport, but this is supported by a study
showing that relatively small sand lenses (making up 15% or less of the deposit) have a limited
impact on contaminant migration [95]. Thus the magnitude of the hydraulic conductivity of the
sand lens does not have an effect. The study on the sand lens in Section 7 is 1 m thick and will
therefore constitute 10% of the surrounding clay matrix. Conversely, the study on the magnitude
of hydraulic conductivity in a sand layer with an added horizontal flow shows a difference in
both contaminant flux and concentration.
This study, along with others [31], [45], [79], [93], [96], underscores the significance of chemical

49
properties, specifically sorption to soil, for a conservative tracer, DMS, and DPC, in influencing
contaminant fate and transport. The role becomes crucial, especially when compounds exhibit
different sorption to soil coefficients in clay and sand, emphasizing the importance of incorpo-
rating heterogeneous soil. Compounds having high sorption within clayey till and low sorption
in sand (e.g. DPC) can show great concern in contaminant transport for rotated sand lenses
and layers. The contaminant transport of the compound will be greatly enhanced compared
to the expected contaminant fate and transportation. It’s worth noting that the sorption to
soil properties for the compounds only affects the breakthrough time and not the maximum
contaminant flux or concentration in this study, as shown in Section 7. Additionally, the scarcity
of information on sorption to soil coefficients, which can vary across the soil matrix [31], [96],
further highlights the need for continued research in this area.
In the 2D horizontal flow model, employing a uniform infiltration rate across the entire domain,
as detailed in Section 6, reveals negligible effects stemming from heterogeneous implementa-
tions in the clay matrix. Conversely, when employing a specified hydraulic head as a boundary
condition, variations in contaminant transport become evident due to diverse implementations
of heterogeneities within the clay matrix. The uniform infiltration rate maintains a consistent
influx at the model domain’s top, where parameters q and K (representing infiltration and
hydraulic conductivity, respectively) remain constant, as defined in Equation 4.3. Given the
constancy of q and the material-dependent K, an increase in K within the sand implementation
results in a reduced hydraulic gradient, ∇h. Consequently, under uniform infiltration, a thicker
sand lens correlates with a diminished overall ∇h and, subsequently, a lower hydraulic head at
the model domain’s top, as shown on contaminated transport plots in Appendix B. Conversely,
when maintaining a specified hydraulic head at the top boundary instead of a uniform infiltra-
tion rate, the overall ∇h within the clay matrix remains constant, leading to increased velocities
vertically.
This study presented 2D and 3D horizontal flow models for various scenarios involving sand
lenses and layers within a clayey matrix. Results indicate comparable relative flux values between
the 2D and 3D models, suggesting similar outputs for contaminant flux into and downstream
within the aquifer. However, the 3D model exhibits higher CMD values due to a larger source
area, including the width of the zone. Moreover, the 3D model provides a more accurate
representation of contaminant spreading, considering transversal dimensions, as evidenced
by relative concentration variations at different locations. The 2D model’s limitation lies in its
inability to account for the rotation of sand lenses around the longitudinal direction, a feature
addressed by the 3D model setup. In general, the use of 3D models offers a broader perspective
compared to 2D models, as discussed in other studies [97], [98]. Although these studies focus on
flow patterns in the subsurface rather than contaminant transport in groundwater, they provide
valuable insights comparing 2D and 3D model setups. Specifically, 3D models allow for more
detailed simulations, revealing advantages over 2D models when addressing heterogeneous
complexities. This perspective is supported by Fuchsluger and Gregor [99], who highlighted the
increased computational capacity and longer simulation times associated with 3D models at
the European Geosciences Union General Assembly.
While this study provides valuable insights into contaminant transport in heterogeneous soils,
acknowledging limitations is essential. The numerical models in this study involve simplifica-
tions and assumptions that may not fully capture real-world complexity. Focusing on specific
geological conditions and the exclusion of site-specific factors may act as a limitation. Unex-
plored soil and contaminant variations may affect outcomes. These limitations emphasize the
need for caution when applying results to diverse environmental settings. Addressing these
constraints by employing more nuanced models and considering a broader range of influencing
factors can enhance the understanding of contaminant transport in heterogeneous soils.

11 Conclusion
The findings underscore the critical importance of sand lens geometry, hydraulic parameters,
and chemical properties in shaping contaminant transport patterns.
Notable geometric findings included an increase in the size of sand lenses and layers showing
increasing contaminant transport, flux, and concentration. Notably, the rotation angle of sand
lenses emerges as a pivotal factor, revealing a substantial impact on contaminant flux. This
insight aligns with existing literature but also quantifies the effect, demonstrating a fourfold
increase in contaminant flux for 90° rotations in a 2D horizontal flow model. Conversely, the
vertical placement and spacing of sand lenses exhibit negligible effects on contaminant trans-
port. The study also sheds light on the limited impact of specific sand lens configurations,
particularly those with thicknesses below 0.5 m, suggesting that certain real-world scenarios
may not significantly enhance contaminant transport.
Hydraulic parameters, specifically variations in hydraulic conductivity within sand lenses em-
bedded in the clay matrix, unexpectedly showed little impact on contaminant transport. This
finding aligns with studies suggesting that relatively small sand lenses with varying hydraulic
conductivity may have a limited effect on contaminant migration. However, the study on
the magnitude of hydraulic conductivity in a sand layer with added horizontal flow revealed
differences in both contaminant flux and concentration.
Chemical properties, specifically sorption to soil, are identified as crucial determinants, espe-
cially for compounds with varying sorption to soil coefficients in clay and sand. The imple-
mentation of sand lenses is shown to induce significantly faster contaminant transport, with
pronounced differences for compounds with higher sorption to soil coefficients in clayey till
compared to sand.
A comparison between model setups, using a uniform infiltration rate and a specified hydraulic
head, reveals the sensitivity of contaminant transport to boundary conditions. The study
demonstrates that the choice of boundary conditions can influence contaminant transport
patterns, with implications for understanding the impact of heterogeneities within the clay
matrix.
Furthermore, the comparison between 2D and 3D modeling results highlights the importance
of considering three-dimensional aspects for a more accurate representation of contaminant
spreading, especially when dealing with features like the rotation of sand lenses around the
longitudinal direction.
11.1
Further Investigations
While the study presented contributes to the understanding of contaminant transport in het-
erogeneous soils, it is essential to acknowledge its limitations. The inherent simplifications
and assumptions in numerical models, coupled with the focus on specific geological condi-
tions, necessitate caution in generalizing the results to diverse environmental settings. Further
investigations should address these limitations by incorporating more nuanced models and
considering a broader range of influencing factors.
In the pursuit of advancing the understanding of contaminant transport in heterogeneous soil,
several avenues for further investigations emerge:
50
==PAGE:61==
Conclusion
51
• Integration of additional soil properties: Consideration of a broader range of soil proper-
ties, beyond hydraulic conductivity and sorption to soil coefficients, would contribute to a
more comprehensive understanding of contaminant transport processes.
• Degradation processes: Explore the degradation processes of contaminants within differ-
ent soil matrices, considering the influence of microbial activity, chemical reactions, and
other factors on the fate of pollutants.
• Field validation studies: Conducting field validation studies to compare model predic-
tions with real-world observations would strengthen the applicability of the findings and
improve the reliability of numerical models.
• Contaminant behavior in the unsaturated zone: Investigate the behavior of contami-
nants in the unsaturated zone, exploring how factors such as soil moisture, root uptake,
and vadose zone characteristics influence the transport and fate of pollutants.
These suggested investigations aim to build upon the current research, addressing specific
aspects that remain open for exploration and contributing to advancing the knowledge in the
field of contaminant transport in heterogeneous soils.
