Abstract
Vibration-based structural health monitoring is under transition to become applicable in the indus-
try. If designed and implemented appropriately, the application would possibly provide a human
and economic beneﬁt. Thus, reliable and highly sensitive structural health monitoring systems,
which perform consistently each time being used, are of great importance. From this perspective,
the reproducibility of structural health monitoring campaigns is investigated in the present work,
with the aim to reproduce a previous study, involving a structural health monitoring campaign of
a scaled offshore platform model.
The monitoring is conducted over approximately one month, using three geophones mounted
on the platform. Various signal processing, including data quality assessment, is applied in order
to obtain satisfactory data. The time domain poly reference identiﬁcation technique is used and
provides the modal parameters, based on the measured structural responses. Due to an observed
daily ﬂuctuation in the natural frequencies, an environmental model based on multivariate linear
regression is applied, in order to correct for the effect of environmental conditions on the natural
frequencies. During the last part of the monitoring, damage is gradually induced by 11 cuts in six
depth steps.
The structural health monitoring system is considered reliable, since it provides as high qual-
ity of data and as consistent an identiﬁcation as the previous study. The environmental model is
seen to remove some of the daily ﬂuctuation in the natural frequencies, yet not completely. The
model is, however, slightly less precise compared to the preceding study. Nonetheless, the envi-
ronmental model is seen to improve the damage detection. The detection of damage is based on
natural frequencies only, and a successful detection is speciﬁed as continuous outliers, deviating
more than three times the median absolute deviation. The environmental corrected natural fre-
quencies indicates damage in the second damage step for the ﬁrst two modes and eventually for
all modes except mode four. The damage detection was, however, more sensitive in the previous
study, where damage was already indicated in the ﬁrst step and eventually in all modes. Still,
the investigation conclusively shows that reproducing a structural health monitoring campaign is
possible with a well-designed measurement system.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The implementation of a damage identiﬁcation scheme in a structure, is referred to as ’structural
health monitoring’ (SHM). The ﬁeld has been around for more than three decades and one of the
early deﬁnitions by Housner [1] states that: "SHM refers to the use of in-situ, nondestructive sens-
ing and analysis of structural characteristics, including the structural response, for the purpose of
detecting change that may indicate damage or degradation". The premise is that damage change
the stiffness, mass and energy dissipation of the structure, which will alter its dynamic properties
(natural frequencies, mode shapes and damping ratios). Thus, the vibration-based method oper-
ational modal analysis (OMA) has gained interest as a SHM tool, since dynamic properties are
fairly easily obtained. Since vibration-based SHM is under the transition to becoming applicable
in the industry, a reliable SHM system is of great importance. This should be understood as a
measurement system that is able to detect damage successfully each time being used.
Nowadays, with increasing population and energy demand, the use of SHM are motivated by two
main facts; we build increasingly larger structures and try to minimise material use. As a result,
the safety margins are reduced and the demand on inspections and maintenance are correspond-
ingly increased. At the same time, the designed life-time of structures are based on experience
and statistics, wherefore one can not know the exact state of health of a given structure at a given
time. With that in mind, SHM potentially provide a human and economic beneﬁt. For instance, in
the offshore industry, the timely and risky manual inspections of an submerged substructure may
be partly omitted by utilising SHM.
Damage identiﬁcation schemes can be based on different approaches. A widely agreed categorisa-
tion by Rytter [2] include both damage- detection, localisation, assessment and consequences. The
damage detection involves a qualitatively indication that damage might be present in the structure
and will be the approach adopted in the present work. An example of a damage detection on a
full-scale bridge, based on alternation in natural frequencies has been made by Brincker et al [3].
Moreover, several studies show that modal parameters are highly dependent on environmental
conditions e.g. [4, 5], and to distinguish the changing modal parameters due to environmental
effects from the changes due to damage, is important in order to successfully apply SHM. This

The hitherto studies implies that damage can be successfully detected by use of OMA even under
inﬂuential environmental conditions. However, the reliability and reproducibility of the SHM
systems used, as mentioned above, has not been fully investigated. Additionally, based on the
fact that measurements were made over a period of only one month during winter and an arbitrary
and imperfect wooden mast was used, the sensitivity of the damage detection by Andersen and
Nielsen [6] is theoretically not deﬁnitive. Especially not in a research perspective, where different
operators should apply the same SHM system. In light of this, the main objective of the present
work is to answer the following main- and sub questions
■Is it possible to reproduce the ﬁndings by Andersen and Nielsen [6], using the same SHM
campaign?
□Is the environmental model as precise with new environmental conditions?
□Is the damage detection as sensitive with new environmental condition and with a new
mast?
It should be clear, that the main objective of the present work is to investigate the reproducibility
and not improvements and use of new methods. In order to obtain results as similar as possible, the
same experimental set-up, calculation- and identiﬁcation methods, and type of linear regression
model has been used, and the SHM campaign is executed as similarly as possible. However, the
frequency domain poly reference identiﬁcation technique has been omitted, due to poor perfor-
mance. The structural response and environmental conditions are monitored during 29 ’healthy’
days, with an uncompromised structure, before ﬁve days of monitoring are made while damage
are gradually induced by cuts with a circular saw. Damage is eventually considered identiﬁed,
manifested as continuous outliers. According to the ﬁndings by Andersen and Nielsen [6], only
the natural frequencies was suitable as damage indicator. The thesis is structured to present the
main areas of the development and execution of the SHM campaign. Initially, the theory behind
OMA is presented as background. Then the properties of the present scaled offshore platform
model is introduced and the SHM campaign is speciﬁed. This is followed by the signal processing

CHAPTER 2
Operational modal analysis
In recent years OMA has become a more widely used SHM tool, since it facilitates the opportunity
to identify the dynamic characteristics of a structure during its lifetime, which can be used to detect
structural damage. In this chapter, the basic idea of OMA, including the underlying concepts of
correlation functions and spectral density functions, will be introduced. Additionally, the two
central identiﬁcation methods; time domain identiﬁcation using AR models and poly reference
(TDPR) and frequency domain identiﬁcation using frequency-domain decomposition (FDD) will
be covered.
2.1
The concept of OMA
The fundamental idea of OMA is the process of characterising the natural modes of a given struc-
ture under operational or ambient vibrations. Each of these natural modes contain a natural fre-
quency, damping factor and mode shape [7]. In principle, in OMA the ambient vibration on the
structure should be approximately white noise, i.e. an energy distribution over a frequency range,
that covers the modal characteristics of interest. However, an exact white noise load is not crucial,
since it is merely important to excite the modes of interest properly, in order to be identiﬁed in the
measurements [7].
When applying OMA, the structural response under ambient vibration can be processed in both
the time- and frequency domain in order to identify the natural modes. In the time domain correla-
tion functions are used as free decays, which contains all the physical information if the response
is Gaussian distributed [7]. In the frequency domain, the corresponding spectral density functions
are used, to identify the natural modes. Both the correlation- and spectral density functions will
be elaborated upon in the following sections.
An important issue is to extract physically meaningful modes from noise and environmental ef-
fects, which calls for reliable and precise data acquisition and identiﬁcation algorithms.

2.2
Correlation functions
In practical cases of operational modal analysis, we only know the measured random signal and
not the inherent probability characteristics. Thus, the key ﬁrst step in OMA, is to estimate the
correlation functions, since all physical information is contained in the random signal in the case
of a Gaussian distribution [7]. This is in fact the case, as nearly all random signals are assumed
to have zero mean and are Gaussian, according to the central limit theorem - stating that a sum of
many independent and identically distributed random variables will become Gaussian distributed
as the number of variables goes towards inﬁnity [7].

Practical application
The TDPR technique are implemented by the MATLAB function id_tdpr_cx, which is developed
and provided by Rune Brincker. The implementation uses the preceding methodology based on
CF. However, the CF are calculated from the CF matrix at time lag zero, and the CF matrix
are determined by inverse fast Fourier transformation (FFT) of the SD matrix. The SD matrix
is estimated using the Welch averaging technique, with a Hanning window and 50 % overlap.
In practice, the method requires six different input parameters; F1, F2, F3, F4, F5 and F6. As
regards the Hanning window, the ﬁrst parameter (F1) deﬁnes the centre frequency of the modal
peaks inside the ﬂat bandwidth of the bandpass ﬁlter (F2). The third parameter (F3) deﬁnes the
width of the band tapering on each side of the ﬂat bandwidth. Concerning the CF, the number of
initial points to skip and the noise tail cut-off level deﬁnes the fourth and ﬁfth parameter (F4 and
F5). To conclude the method, the sixth parameter (F6), deﬁnes the number singular values to be
used in the identiﬁcation. An illustration of the parameters can be seen in Figure 2.4.

ﬁrst singular vector at a frequency line, where the corresponding singular value is dominating
[7]. The simplest way to perform FDD, is by peak picking, where the modes are estimated at
frequency lines, where peaks occur in the plot of the singular values of the SD matrix. However,
an enhanced FDD method has been developed by deﬁning a frequency band around the peaks,
wherein all singular values can be used to estimate the frequency-domain function - the spectral
density. This can then be taken back to the time domain by inverse FFT, and the poles can be
estimated by the corresponding correlation function, using the Ibrahim Time Domain (ITD) [7].
Practical application
The FDD technique is implemented by the MATLAB function id_fdd_AUTO2, which is developed
and provided by Rune Brincker. Again, the SD matrix is estimated by using the Welch averaging
technique with a Hanning window and 50 % overlap. In practice, the technique uses the preceding
methodology and FDD requires four input parameters. The ﬁrst and second parameter (F1 and F2)
deﬁnes the initial frequency of each mode of interest and the width of the frequency band around
the initial frequencies. The second parameter should be larger than ﬁve times the bandwidth
Bf = 2 ζf, deﬁned by Bendat and Piersol [8]. The last two parameters (F3 and F4), deﬁnes the
number of initial points to skip and the noise tail cut-off level. An illustration of the parameters
can be seen in Figure 2.5

Before going into the OMA identiﬁcation process and determine the physical information about

CHAPTER 7
Environmental model
When performing damage detection, the natural frequencies are expected to change by damage
induction, related to the change in stiffness. As described and observed previously, the natural
frequencies are furthermore inﬂuenced by environmental conditions, which may be difﬁcult to
distinguish from damage. This could essentially result in an insensitive and poor damage detec-
tion. Thus, an environmental model is elaborated in this chapter, with the aim to correct the natural
frequencies for the inﬂuence of environmental conditions. The environmental model is based on
temperature (T), relative humidity (RH), wind speed (WS) and wind direction (WD).
7.1
Weather monitoring
The Solar Thermal Research Group at the Technical University of Denmark provided the weather
data used for the environmental monitoring, which is available from their website [18]. Their cli-
mate station is located approximately 100 m from the structural model on the roof of the nearby
building 119. This introduce some inconsistencies in the weather data compared to the actual
conditions around the structural model, especially regarding wind speed and wind direction. Ad-
ditionally, the temperature and relative humidity are measured from the air and not within the mast
itself. Since wood is hygroscopic, the moisture content and thus material properties of the wood,
changes with temperature and relative humidity over time. This relative delay is not taken into
account, and thus, these issues may lead to an imperfect model. The weather data were obtained
with one-minute intervals and the experimental measurements were performed over 20 minutes.
Therefore, the weather data were averaged over the corresponding 20 minutes of each measure-
ment and can be seen in Figure 7.1 for both one-minute intervals and 20-minute averages.
As expected, the temperature and relative humidity display daily ﬂuctuations. This is to some
extent also the case for the wind speed, while not for the wind direction.

CHAPTER 8
Damage detection
In this chapter, the damaged state of the SHM campaign will be covered. In reality the time
of damage occurrence is unknown. However, damage induction was carefully planned and per-
formed in the present work, in order to evaluate the possibility and sensitivity of modal parameters
as damage indicators with the present measurement system. Since both the consistency in the iden-
tiﬁcation of the modal parameters and the precision of the environmental model were best for the
TDPR technique, this technique is chosen for damage detection in the present SHM campaign. As
mentioned, only the natural frequencies have been used as damage indicator in the following.
8.1
Description of damage detection
Damage criteria
The detection of damage is perceived as the occurrence of changes in modal parameters, indepen-
dent of the inconsistency of the identiﬁcation method and the inﬂuence of environmental condi-
tions. Thus, the damage criteria are formulated as; continuous outliers with a deviation of more
than three times the MAD value, taken from the median of the natural frequencies in the healthy
state.
Damage induction
The damage was made with a circular saw as eleven cuts in the mast with a stepwise depth in-
crease. Six damage steps were conducted over three days, and the damage induction scheme can
be seen in Table 8.1. An additional day of monitoring was conducted one day after the last damage
step, in order to capture the continuity of the potential outliers in the natural frequencies. The ﬁrst
cut was located 231 mm from the top of the angular mounting and the remaining cuts were located
with a continuous offset of 20 mm, as illustrated in Figure 8.1. The ﬁgure also shows a picture of
the actual damage induced in the structure.

CHAPTER 9
Discussion and conclusion
9.1
Discussion
The reproducibility of SHM campaigns has been investigated in the present work, by assessment
of the ability to reproduce the ﬁndings of Andersen and Nielsen [6]. Ideally, the application of
SHM should be consistent and highly sensitive each time being used. This is considered vital if
reliable SHM are to be implemented in the industry. However, the study is limited by only being
enabled to relate to the single previous study mentioned above, and thus, will only provide an in-
dication of the reproducibility of SHM campaigns. The reproducibility of this study is discussed
in the following.
The overall reproducibility of the SHM campaign was found to be satisfactory. Even though recur-
ring non-solved errors still were present in the measurement system, the SHM system provided a
99.4 % success rate of good quality measurements based on the data assessment criterion, which is
comparable to the previously found 99.1 %. Additionally, the two identiﬁcation techniques, once
more, performed well, which resulted in a 100 % succes rate in the identiﬁcation of all ﬁve mode.
Furthermore, the TDPR technique was used for damage detection, since its performed best, as
measured by consistency in the identiﬁcation of natural frequencies and the environmental model.
The ﬁndings related to the signiﬁcant errors also suggests that more research and development
efforts should be applied into improving the robustness of the measurement system and the auto-
mated monitoring.
The environmental model used in the present work was considered precise according to the evalua-
tion parameter RMSE and the model did reduce the overall ﬂuctuations in the natural frequencies,
related to the environmental condition. However, ﬂuctuations were still present and the model
appeared to be less precise, compared to the previous study. The difference in precision may
originate in the fact that the SHM campaigns are conducted during two different seasons. In fact,
larger variations in the temperature, relative humidity and wind speed were observed in the present
work. Moreover, contradictory results were observed as regards the precision of the model based
on RMSE, and the evaluation of the ﬂuctuation based on MAD values. The MAD value for the
45
==PAGE:60==
46
CHAPTER 9. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
ﬁrst mode displayed a slightly larger reduction, compared to the previous study, even though less
precision was evident. This may originate in the lack of training of the model, due to the in-
sufﬁcient length of the monitoring period. Overall, the environmental model suggests a general
improvement of the ability to effectively eliminate the environmentally originated ﬂuctuations in
natural frequencies. Obviously, the model would perform better if the monitoring spanned a full
calendar year. Additionally, due to the location of the climate station, the weather data was fairly
unrepresentative and since temperature and relative humidity was measured from the air, the rela-
tive delay of the effect in the wooden material was not taken into account. In conclusion, different
and more complex models could be used in order to more effectively distinguish effects from en-
vironmental conditions from the actual physical properties of the structure.
During the SHM campaign damage was effectively indicated by the environmental corrected natu-
ral frequencies, even for a small reduction of the cross section. Yet, a slightly less sensitive damage
detection was observed compared to the previous study. This may be a result of the less precise
environmental model and the inﬂuence of the torsion of the mast compared to the straighter mast
used previously. The sensitive indication of damage may also be related to the simplicity of the
structure and the critical location of the damage. The dynamic properties of the simple structural
system is simply determined by the stiffness of the mast and the geometry and mass of the plat-
form. Additionally, the location of damage is in the most critical location of the structure with
regard to structural integrity, which could result in a more sensitive detection, compared to other
exposed locations. Thus, the success of the damage detection in the present work and the general
reproducibility of the damage detection may not be scalable to full-scale and more complex struc-
tures. Within the objective of the present work, however, the reproducibility of the precision in
the damage detection demonstrates that a reliable SHM campaign has been developed, being able
to consistently identify damage and this may indicate that SHM can be implemented in real- and
simple structures.

9.2. CONCLUSION
47
9.2
Conclusion
This thesis aims to investigate the reproducibility of a previously designed and applied SHM
campaign under new environmental circumstances and with a new mast. This involves both the
assessment of the ability to reproduce the SHM campaign and the results in terms of the preci-
sion of the environmental model and the sensitivity of the damage detection. Consequently, a
previously designed scaled offshore platform model was monitored with a previously designed
measurement system under normal operation conditions for 29 days and subsequently monitored
while damaged sequentially over four days.
The SHM campaign performed well, with a 99.4 % success rate of good quality measurements
in accordance with the data quality criterion. Thus, the SHM campaign is considered satisfactory
reproduced compared to the previously achieved 99.1 %. Additionally, the used identiﬁcation
methods performed well, with a 100 % success rate in identifying all modes in each measurement.
The results were also considered valid, based on the comparison with an analysis made in the
commercial software ARTeMIS modal.
Considered vital for the sensitivity of the damage detection, an environmental model, based on
multivariate linear regression, was constructed, in order to correct for the environmental condi-
tions’ ﬂuctuating effect on the natural frequencies. The environmental correction of the natural
frequencies, showed an overall decrease in the daily ﬂuctuations. However, a less precise environ-
mental model was obtained with the new environmental circumstances, compared to previously.
The damage detection of the SHM campaign was performed with the environmental corrected
natural frequencies as damage indicator. Damage was applied with a circular saw in the form of
11 cuts made in six steps, where depth was gradually increased. The damage criteria were speci-
ﬁed as continuous outliers with a deviation of more than three times the MAD value, taken from
the median of the natural frequencies in the healthy state. Damage was eventually identiﬁed for
all modes at damage step six, except for mode four. Damage was furthermore observed in damage
step two for the ﬁrst two modes. Thus, the damage detection is considered less sensitive compared
to the precious study, since those results showed damage for all modes and damage was detected
as early as in the ﬁrst damage steps for the two ﬁrst modes.
Conclusively, the present work showed that the reproduction of a previously conducted SHM
campaign under different environmental condition is indeed possible, even though a slight vari-
ation in the precision of the environmental model and in the sensitivity of the damage detection
was observed. This may also indicate a satisfactory reliability of the present SHM system, which
is considered of signiﬁcant importance for the method’s possible relevance as a reliable tool in the
future industrial applications.

b) Place a cap on the last socket
c) Connect the chain to the base station
d) Connect the base station to the computer with a Ethernet cable

a) The software WinSSHfs is used

a) A task are created, which runs the MATLAB script MainScript_AutoMeasurement
through a cmd
b) The conditions for triggering the task are set to match the measurement speciﬁcations
