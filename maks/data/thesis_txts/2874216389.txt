Abstract
This thesis investigates the predictive power of injection molding simulations done with Moldex3D,
with a particular focus on shrinkage in a semi-crystalline polymer.
A pre-study was performed ahead of the metrology of the main part of the thesis. It confirmed
that there was a significant difference in measurement results between operators, both in terms of
systematic differences and measurement variance, meaning that the operators showed both different
average results, and a statistical difference in the "scatter" of measurement data obtained from the
same parts.
Short shot experiments and fully-filled samples were molded in a geometry of two thin plates,
connected by a runner system, the samples were very precisely measured, and the results directly
compared to simulations. The experiments revealed that the thicker cavity was consistently filled
before the thinner one, a phenomenon that the simulations were able to capture qualitatively but
underestimated the degree.
Part weight predictions showed a close agreement, supporting the validity of mass balance
in the simulations. Shrinkage, however, revealed some significant differences. While simulations
predicted uniform contraction along and across the flow of the molten polymer, experiments showed
an anisotropic behaviour with thicknesses increasing in size compared to the mold cavity dimensions,
arising from skin-core structures and post-ejection elastic recovery.
A full-factorial 25 virtual Design of Experiments with two materials (polypropylene and polystyrene)
was carried out, and demonstrated that both materials responded systematically to changes in
melt temperature, mold temperature, flow rate, packing pressure and packing time, though with
polypropylene being more sensitive to variations due to its semi-crystalline nature.
The results highlighted that while simulations are a valuable tool and provide a useful frame-
work for process optimization and shrinkage minimization, they can be less reliable for very precise
shrinkage prediction, particularly in semi-crystalline polymers, where crystallization morphology
and elastic recovery play an important role.

Introduction
1.1
Project and background
Injection molding is one of the most commonly used manufacturing processes for producing
polymer components. It offers high production rates and the ability to form more advanced, complex
shapes, compared to other processing methods. It is also valued for its ability to create precise
geometries with tight tolerances, excellent surface finishes, and minimal post-processing. Today,
precision injection molding can achieve dimensional tolerances in the range of 10-50µm and cycle–
to–cycle mass repeatability in the order of 10-20mg. This makes it ideal for mass production in
industries such as automotives and medical devices.
However, despite these advantages, getting the optimal results requires careful control of nu-
merous process parameters as well as other factors such as cooling system design and mold charac-
teristics. The final part’s quality can be affected by even minor adjustments due to the interaction
of thermal, mechanical and material factors. In the past, these complexities used to mean that
tooling times and re-design costs were high, but with the widespread adoption of simulation tools,
these parameters could be tested and optimized in a virtual environment before being implemented
in physical production.
Simulation tools for injection molding are advanced software systems that model the flow,
cooling, and solidification of the polymer within a mold. This allows process engineers to predict
part behaviour and identify potential problems and part defects before the manufacturing is begun.
By simulating the injection process, these tools can provide valuable insight into factors such as
temperature distribution, pressure distribution, cycle times, cooling times etc.
This enables a
significantly faster optimization of gate placement, cooling channel design, and process parameters,
thereby reducing the need for expensive physical trials and iterative tooling adjustments. As a
result, simulation has become an integral part of modern injection molding development. As this
has happened and simulation has become a standard element of the process, it is now considered a
distinct cost factor and an essential part of the overall process optimization.
However, the simulation software are not flawless, as they still mostly model the shrinkages
of plastic components directly from the pvT data. The consequence of this is that the shrinkage
calculation of the software assumes isotropic contraction, which does not accurately reflect the
anisotropic behaviour of shrinkage observed in real polymer parts. It is therefore imperative to
have a thorough understanding of process parameters and their interactions to achieve effective
optimization.
1.2
Problem statement
Injection molding is a cyclic manufacturing process that is typically divided into 3 main phases:
Injection, packing, and cooling. First, the material is melted and injected into the mold cavity. As
it nears the end of the filling, a packing pressure is exerted to force more material in, to compensate

for shrinkage. After packing is finished, the part is cooled down inside the mold, and eventually
ejected, and the cycle starts again. Each of these phases plays a critical role in determining final
part quality and dimensional stability.
One of the most important challenges to address when it comes to optimizing part quality is
managing material shrinkage. As the material cools down during the cooling phase, it experiences
thermal contraction, meaning that the part’s dimensions may shrink. This phenomenon is known
as shrinkage, and can adversely affect the accuracy and quality of the final product.
Shrinkage refers to the reduction in part volume and linear dimensions due to thermal contrac-
tion, as the polymer cools from the molten to solid state. In semi-crystalline polymers, this is also
attributed to crystallization during cooling. Shrinkage is commonly quantified with a percentage
using the formula:
Shrinkage(%) =

where Dp is the dimension of the part after molding and Dm is the dimension of the mold cavity.
Note that a positive shrinkage value indicates that the molded part is smaller than the mold cavity.
As stated before, in conventional injection molding, shrinkage is compensated for by packing
more material into the cavity during the packing. However, accurately predicting and controlling the
final part dimensions remains difficult, especially for components requiring high-precision finishes.
This difficulty arises because shrinkage primarily occurs during the cooling phase but is strongly
influenced by the packing phase and the complex interactions of mold constraints up until ejection, as
well as polymer behaviours such as thermal expansion/contraction, pvT properties, flow orientation,
and crystallization.
The result of the combination of these factors is that shrinkage is an inherently anisotropic,
three-dimensional phenomenon. This may be due to molecular orientation (resulting from the flow
orientation), differential cooling rates or pressure distributions, or other factors. Molded parts rarely
shrink uniformly, so it is important to examine the shrinkage along the three orthogonal directions.
For example, a recent micro-injection molding study of a cyclic olefin copolymer (COC) part showed
that shrinkage was about 0.62% in the flow direction, versus 0.69-0.79% in the transverse direction
[1]. For reference, COC materials typically have very low shrinkage levels, around 0.1-0.7%, but
even there the directional difference was obvious.
Uneven shrinkage will lead to warpage – an undesired deformation or distortion of a part that
results in a final shape that deviates from the intended geometry. Warpage always results from
differing degrees of shrinkage[2].
To sum it up, understanding how process parameters influence the shrinkage in each principal
direction is crucial to predict the three-dimensional shrinkage of an injection molded part.
Addressing these challenges relies more and more on data-driven process optimization and
advanced metrology.
Injection molding machines today are equipped with sensors and controls
that gather a great amount of process data, e.g. injection pressure profiles, screw position, cavity

pressure, temperatures etc. Analyzing this data can reveal important correlations between process
parameters and product outcomes. A relatively recent study by Wang et al. [3] has shown that it
is feasible to train artificial neural networks (ANNs) to predict part shrinkage and warpage based
on process settings. Another study by Finkeldey et al. [4] utilized machine learning methods to
accurately predict the weight and thickness of a manufactured part, showing how machine learning
can improve process repeatability. These approaches can help engineers optimize process parameters
and conditions for minimal variation in part dimensions.
Equally critical is dimensional metrology, which ensures that part dimensions are measured
with a high degree of accuracy and confidence. High-resolution measurements are taken of the mold
cavity and molded parts. By comparing these values, one can calculate the shrinkage rates in the
three principal directions. Advanced metrology ensures that the true three-dimensional shrinkage is
obtained and that any improvement efforts can be assessed in a meaningful manner. It also provides
a basis for quantifying uncertainty, a very important element to consider when differences are in
the order of microns.
The material that was chosen is SABIC PP 579S®, a polypropylene (PP) homopolymer, de-
signed for low warpage, thin-walled molding. It has a high stiffness and outstanding flow properties
according to the manufacturer [5]. The material has a nominal mold shrinkage of 1.2-2.5% depend-
ing on wall thickness and processing conditions, according to the data sheet. This is significantly
higher than optical polymers such as COC[6] or polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA) [7], which have
shrinkage rates of 0.4-0.7% and 0.2-0.8%, respectively. This difference is mostly due to the semi-
crystalline nature of PP, while COC and PMMA are amorphous polymers. The crystallization that
occurs during the cooling phase in PP will lead to increased shrinkage compared to amorphous
materials. This relatively high shrinkage rate makes PP an ideal candidate for studying anisotropic
shrinkage effects, combined with this particular grade’s suitability for thin-walled molding.
In summary, while simulation software has reduced the long tooling and implementation times
of the past, these software still rely on simplified models that cannot fully capture the complexities
of injection molding processes. Therefore, there exists a need for a greater understanding about the
injection molding process as a whole.
1.3
Research questions
The main purpose of this thesis is to investigate and characterize the three-dimensional shrink-
age behaviour of a precision-molded SABIC PP 579S® thermoplastic component with high-precision
metrology methods, and compare it with simulations done in Moldex3D, a powerful injection mold-
ing simulation software.
It is expected that the software will differ slightly due to it modeling
the shrinkage on isotropic pvT data behaviour.
This thesis will therefore combine dimensional
metrology, experimental molding trials, and process simulations to achieve the following objectives:
• Produce multiple part samples with injection molding. Process parameters (e.g. melt temper-
ature, mold temperature, packing pressure, injection speed, and holding time) will be recorded

1.4
Limitations of the study
While the aim of this thesis is to provide experimental and simulation-based analysis of the
three-dimensional shrinkage behaviour of SABIC PP 579S®, several limitations must be acknowl-
edged to properly contextualize the results.
Key limitations include:
• Material specificity. The experimental part of this project focuses exclusively on SABIC PP
579S®, a semi-crystalline homopolymer. Shrinkage behaviour varies significantly between
materials, so results may not be entirely applicable to other polymers.
• Single part geometry. Only one part geometry and gating configuration was studied. Studying
additional geometries could provide further insight into shrinkage patterns.
• The part dimensions that were used in the simulation were adjusted based on the mold mea-
surement. Lengths of the mold measurement were averaged and used to elongate the cavities,
but width were kept unchanged. Thickness variations were calculated by calculating group
averages and implementing a linear thickness variation in the model. These approximations
introduce some uncertainty that may influence simulation accuracy.
• Limited process parameter range. Time constraints limited the experimental investigation to
a single set of process parameters (with minor variations). Exploring a wider range could have
yielded valuable insight.

• Metrology uncertainty. All measurements are dependent on the resolution, calibration, and
repeatability limits of the metrology equipment, as well as operator influence in some measure-

• Process variability. Cycle-to-cycle variations in molding (fluctuations in melt temperature,

• Environmental factors. Post-molding changes due to ambient temperature or humidity were

These limitations do not diminish the findings of the project, but rather define the boundaries
within which the results should be interpreted. They should therefore be interpreted within the con-
text of this project’s conditions and handled with caution when extending them to other materials,
geometries, or extreme processing conditions.

1.6
Thesis structure
The chapters following the introduction in this thesis are organized into five main chapters,
each addressing an important aspect of the study. The chapters are:
• Chapter 2: State of the art. This chapter will review the currently existing knowledge and
recent advancements related to injection molding, polymer shrinkage, and process simulation.
It will lay out the theoretical foundation of the project and attempt to identify gaps that this
research can address.
• Chapter 3: Materials and methods. This chapter will describe the materials, experimental
procedures, and simulation methodologies used, in detail. It will outline how the data was
collected, the equipment and software that was employed, and the approach utilized to analyze
the shrinkage behaviour.
• Chapter 4: Results. Data on dimensional measurements, shrinkage calculations, and simula-
tion predictions are presented. The outcomes of the experimental molding and simulation are
put forth and compared.
• Chapter 5: Discussion: The interpretation of the results in the context of the existing literature
is presented, looking at the implications of the findings. The accuracy of the simulations is
discussed, the influence of the process parameters, and the observed anisotropic shrinkage
behaviour.

2.1
Injection molding fundamentals and shrinkage phenomena
Injection molding is a repetitive manufacturing process that consists of different phases. The
machine begins by clamping together the two mold halves. The phases are as follows:
• Injection phase: Plastic pellets are are fed from a hopper into the injection molding machine.
These pellets are moved toward the mold by the injection unit, where the heat around the
barrel and pressure inside melts the pellets. The volume of the material injected is called the
"shot". The injection time is finished when the mold is 90-95% filled.
• Packing phase: A holding pressure is applied to compensate for plastic shrinkage as the
material begins to cool and solidify. This pressure assists further material flow into the cavity,
resulting in the densification of the plastic, helping to eliminate voids and sink marks. This
helps ensure that the final part has the correct dimensions. During this phase, the pressure in
the cavity is still relatively high because it’s almost completely filled. It is applied for a few
seconds until the gate solidifies.
• Cooling phase: The cooling phase involves transferring the heat away from the molten plastic
for it to solidify inside the mold. This is accomplished with the cooling system, which is
typically water channels running through the mold. This phase continues until the part has
solidified enough to be ejected without deformation. This stage typically takes up the largest
part of the overall cycle time.
After the phases, the mold is opened to allow for the ejection of the plastic component. As the
mold separates, an ejection system is activated to push the part out of the cavity. Ejector pins are
often used in this phase, they are strategically positioned inside the mold to push the part evenly
out of the cavity, making sure that the part is not damaged in the process. Below is a simple
diagram of a typical injection molding machine.

Shrinkage mainly occurs as the material cools from the processing temperature to room tem-
perature. The volume of the material decreases as it cools, causing the part to contract away from
the walls of the cavity.
Several factors will influence this. This can be broken down between amorphous and semi-
crystalline polymers. Amorphous polymers typically have a lower shrinkage rate, as they solidify
without a phase change, meaning that their shrinkage is primarily due to thermal contraction.
However, when semi-crystalline polymers thermally contract, they also go through crystallization
which can increase the density quite significantly, leading to a higher shrinkage. Some examples
to compare between amorphous and semi-crystalline polymers can be seen in Table 2.1. Also, in
this way, compared to amorphous polymers, semi-crystalline polymers exhibit a more anisotropic

To quantify shrinkage accurately, the mold cavity dimensions are measured and compared to
the measured molded part dimensions (after cooling for 24-48 hours to allow the full shrinkage to
take place). This was described in Equation 1.1. It’s important to measure the dimensions along the
principal directions (length, width and thickness), or even at multiple points on complex geometries
because shrinkage can be direction-dependent, or anisotropic, due to the orientation of the flow and
thermal gradients (changes in temperature due to distance from the gate).
As stated before, shrinkage depends on the direction of the plastic flow during molding [1],
meaning that along one principal direction the polymer may shrink less than along another. This
differs between amorphous and semi-crystalline materials.
Amorphous materials have a random, entangled molecular orientation when in a state of equi-
librium. As they melt, the forces acting between molecules weaken and move away from each other.
During the injection phase, the shear forces cause the individual molecules to uncoil and align to the
flow direction. When the flow stops, the molecules relax and return to a state of random orientation.
Until the temperature drops, the intermolecular forces will continue to pull them closer, enough to
freeze them in place. These forces will cause uniform shrinkage, but the relaxation effect will result
in much more contraction in the direction of the flow [9].

Semi-crystalline materials, unlike amorphous ones, have regions of tightly bundled and highly
ordered molecular structures. When they melt, the crystalline structures loosen up and align them-
selves to the direction of the flow. However, when the material cools, it does not relax. It maintains
its orientation in the flow direction and the molecules begin to recrystallize, causing considerably
higher shrinkage rates. This effect is greater in the direction perpendicular to the flow [9].
A part’s thickness also greatly affects the cooling rate and therefore the crystallization and
shrinkage. As the thicker sections cool more slowly, crystals have more time to grow in a semi-
crystalline polymer, increasing the shrinkage in those sections. Thinner sections however, cool very
fast (sometimes freezing the polymer before it can fully crystallize) resulting in lower crystallinity
and thus, lower shrinkage. This difference can cause internal stresses and warpage if a part has a
non-uniform wall thickness. In uniform flat sections, most of the total volumetric shrinkage is seen
as a reduction in the part’s thickness, because the large free surfaces in the thickness direction allow
the material to contract there. In-plane shrinkage may however be partially restrained by the mold
or by differential cooling. If all sections of a part cool uniformly and the polymer had no orientation,
the shrinkage would be isotropic, shrinking evenly in all directions. In reality, differences in cooling
and molecular orientation make this very unlikely.
In conclusion, achieving minimal shrinkage depends on geometry, material selection and the op-
timization of processing conditions. This might include using materials with low inherent shrinkage,
applying adequate packing pressure to compensate for contraction and ensuring uniform cooling.
These principles form the baseline for precision injection molding.
2.2
Precision molding and dimensional metrology
Precision injection molding typically requires tolerances in the tens of microns. Standard in-
jection molding practices are not generally capable of this, and thus require some improvements.
Precision molding involves a tighter control of the process parameters, but sometimes also spe-
cialized process variants and equipment. One technique often used for high-precision components
is injection-compression molding (ICM). The difference between ICM and conventional injection
molding, is that in ICM, the mold is not fully closed during injection, leaving a small gap. After the
melted polymer is injected, the mold then closes (or a moving core compresses the cavity) for the
packing phase. This compression helps to push the melt into all parts of the cavity and compensate
for shrinkage in a more uniform way. For example, Loaldi, D. [10] and others have studied ICM
for optical applications, and shown that a properly controlled compression stroke can improve the
replication fidelity, along with reducing shrinkage compared to conventional injection molding. As
shown by Loaldi, D., a study on the effect of utilizing precision injection-compression molding in the
production of polymer Fresnel lenses, applying the correct compression stroke eliminated a large lens
defect and improved feature replication. This was due to trapped air being expelled more efficiently.
Similar to the results of Wang et al. [3], the packing phase had the biggest and most positive effect
on replication quality. Thus, even in ICM, a packing phase is necessary to compensate for shrinkage

do not substitute the necessity of proper packing. Instead, they allow precision molding of parts
that would otherwise be difficult to fill or keep within the tolerances using conventional injection
molding.
Another method in precision molding is the use of dynamic mold temperature control - heating
the mold during injection and then cooling to solidify the polymer, achieving a more rapid thermal
cycle. A study by Guerrier et. al. showed that installing an integrated multi-turn induction heating

a frozen layer that would have otherwise blocked thin sections or inaccurately recreated microfea-
tures. This served to ensure a complete cavity fill and an improved surface quality. At the same
time, this helped to reduce residual stresses and weld-line weaknesses, due to the polymer flowing
more uniformly and bonding more effectively before solidifying. The resulting parts exhibited bet-
ter mechanical strength, a better surface finish, and a better dimensional accuracy compared to
conventional injection molding.
Tosello et al. [11] highlighted the challenges associated with trying to achieve a high accuracy
surface replication for micro-optical and micro-fluidic components. This was due to a combination
of material, process, and tool related factors. It showed that polymer shrinkage and warpage was
strongly influenced by flow-induced stresses and uneven cooling. It was noted that tool wear and
limitations in mold fabrication at the micro-scale introduced geometrical errors that are magnified
when replicating microstructures. Process instabilities such as pressure and temperature variations
further contribute to these dimensional inaccuracies, thus highlighting the necessity of robust process
optimization and advanced mold design to improve replication.
Modern injection molding machines may include pressure and temperature sensors inside the
mold cavity. An injection molding methodology called Scientific Injection Molding [12] utilizes these
to:
• Determine optimum fill speed
• Determine optimum packing pressure
• Determine optimum packing time

becomes stable, therefore improving repeatability and achieving better product quality.

made up of long chain molecules, that can be completely entangled (amorphous) or form crystalline
regions (semi-crystalline). When a polymer is heated enough to melt, the crystalline regions break

Determining an optimum packing pressure is arguably more important than injection velocity.
A well-packed part will experience minimal shrinkage and warpage, but beyond a certain point,

make the effective cavity dimensions a bit larger during the moment that it experiences the pressure
peak. Once the pressure is removed and the mold returns to its original position, the part retains
this extra size. When molten polymer enters a mold cavity, it has a lower density than when it

Determining the optimum packing pressure involves first finding the lowest amount of pressure
to sufficiently fill the remainder of the mold cavity that was not filled during the injection phase.
This minimum pressure should also be able to reduce shrinkage related issues to an acceptable level.
After that, the highest tolerable packing pressure is found by finding the pressure at which flashing
occurs, or other issues happen, like the part sticking inside the mold upon ejection. Flash is the
phenomenon when the polymer is so pressurized that it leaks out of the mold. These low and high
points determine the acceptable packing pressure window. The optimum pressure will then have to
be determined through experimentation or simulation, but generally a good place to start would be
two thirds of the way between the lowest acceptable and highest acceptable points.
Determining the optimum packing time involves setting a duration that ensures that the packing
pressure is transferred effectively from the machine nozzle to the cavity. Keeping a positive pressure
on the cavity as long as the gate is open ensures optimal packing of the part and minimizes volumetric
shrinkage. Applying packing pressure until the gate freezes also prevents material backflow into the
feed system.
Since plastic can’t transmit pressure once solid, effective packing time is dependent on a part’s
geometry. Thin sections will freeze faster than thick ones, potentially isolating some areas from
pressure.
Gates are most often positioned to let material flow from thick to thin sections, but
because gates are usually thinner than the part wall, they freeze before the rest of the part, at
which point packing no longer has any effect. Continuing to apply pressure after the gate freeze will
only affect the feed system, not improving the part anymore, which makes determining the freeze

Measuring produced parts accurately to ensure they meet the set tolerances is equally impor-

Beyond just quality assurance, precise metrology also allows for a deeper insight into how

2.3
Effects of process parameters on shrinkage
Because of the complexity of shrinkage, many researchers have utilized Designs of Experiments
(DOE) and statistical analysis to understand which process factors have the most effect on dimen-
sional outcomes. This subchapter will highlight some examples.
Altan [13] used a Taguchi DOE method combined with experimental design and ANOVA to
determine the optimal injection molding conditions for minimum shrinkage. The Taguchi method
is used to reduce the variation in a process using a robust design of experiments, aiming to produce
a high quality product at low cost [14].
It was developed to avoid having to test all possible
combinations like in a factorial design, by testing pairs of combinations. This lets the observers
determine which factors most affect product quality with a lower amount of experimentation, thus
saving time and resources.
Altan analyzed a variety of process parameters such as melt temperature, injection pressure,
packing pressure and packing time. He used two materials, polypropylene (semi-crystalline) and
polystyrene (amorphous) and found that the most significant parameters were packing pressure for
the semi-crystalline material and melt temperature for the amorphous one. Injection pressure had
the least effect on the shrinkage in both cases. He found that his ANN could predict shrinkage with
reasonable accuracy, therefore demonstrating the effectiveness of combining experimental DOE with

Chen et al. [15] similarly carried out the development of a parameter optimization system.
It used mold a number of analysis methods: Mold flow analysis, the Taguchi method, ANOVA,

the best geometrical dimensions and surface roughness characteristics.

2.4
Materials for high-precision molding
In the matter of shrinkage, the choice of material will strongly affect the outcome and therefore
the realistically achievable tolerances. For optical and high-precision components, for example like
in the case of Loaldi [10], common materials are cyclic olefin polymers/copolymers (COP/COC),
polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA), and polycarbonate (PC), amongst others. These materials are
all amorphous and show a relatively low rate of shrinkage. PMMA is a favourite for the production of
optical lenses and medical applications that require high dimensional stability. COP/COC materials
are even more stable and can have a shrinkage as low as 0.1% in some cases [17]. These polymers
share the fact that they have high optical clarity and low birefringence.
The material that was used in this project was polypropylene, the specific grade was SABIC®
PP 579S, and it is a type of polyolefin which is semi-crystalline. PP generally has a higher shrinkage
than the materials discussed above, but this particular grade is advertised as being suitable for the
production of thin-walled injection molded products as it gives a low warpage, high stiffness and
outstanding flow properties [5]. When PP cools inside the mold, crystal nuclei form and grow into
spherulites in regions of relatively low flow/shear, while high-shear areas (e.g. near mold walls or
in high-flow regions) can form oriented fibrous crystals. Under a large shear stress, as can occur in
the mold, particularly at the mold walls, PP can develop a skin of highly-oriented molecular chains.
These stretched chains can act as nuclei for crystallization, resulting in long, fibrillar crystalline
cores with lamellar crystals growing off them, perpendicular to the fiber axis [18]. This skin layer,
while thin, is very rigid and oriented in the flow direction. Beneath the skin the polymer may have

a less oriented, more spherulitic core, particularly in thicker sections where the polymer in the core
undergoes slower cooling and less shear. The result is a combination, a skin-core structure through
the thickness of the PP part, with different microstructures and orientation levels in each layer.
This microstructure has a significant effect on shrinkage. The oriented skin, with molecules aligned
along the flow, will tend to shrink less in the flow direction, but can shrink (or even stretch) in the
transverse direction, while the core may shrink in a more isotropic manner. Increasing the thickness
of these oriented skin layers (e.g. by increasing the injection speed, thus increasing shear) generally
increases overall shrinkage anisotropy and can also increase the total shrinkage.
In PP, the order/architecture of how the macromolecules arrange themselves is known as tac-
ticity, and describes the spacial arrangement of the pendant methyl groups along the polymer chain.
This phenomenon leads to three primary types of stereoregularity in PP: Isotactic, where all methyl
groups align on the same side, syndiotactic, where the methyl groups alternate sides, and atactic PP,
with random methyl group orientation. The isotactic orientation, due to the methyl group align-
ment, makes up a highly regular, repeating arrangement which allows the chains to align closely
and pack efficiently into a crystal structure, giving a high crystallinity (typically 50-60% with nor-
mal processing), resulting in a high density. The syndiotactic orientation is still ordered, but the
packing is slightly less dense because the alternating orientation of can result in a less tightly packed
crystalline lattice. This will still result in significant crystallinity, but usually a bit lower than iso-
tactic (typically 40-50%). Atactic PP’s completely random arrangement of methyl groups prevents
any long-range order or efficient packing. It is thus entirely amorphous, lacking any measurable
crystallinity.
If PP is cooled very fast (e.g. in thin sections or the mold itself is very cold) some crystallization
is suppressed, resulting in a lower crystalline fraction and therefore lower shrinkage. Slow cooling
or high mold temperatures however, promote a higher degree of crystallinity, and larger spherulites,
which translates to greater shrinkage. But even at typical processing cooling rates, PP will crys-
tallize about 50-60% by volume into crystals, so significant shrinkage is unavoidable. The kinetics
of crystallization can also be affected by pressure, applying high pressure raises the polymer’s crys-
tallization temperature slightly and can produce a somewhat finer crystalline morphology [19]. In
practice though, the total crystallinity of PP at ejection does not change drastically with moderate
pressure changes, PP most often ends up at ∼50-60% crystalline by the time it solidifies, but
pressure can affect when and how the crystals form. The key takeaway is that PP’s semi-crystalline
nature leads to substantial shrinkage and anisotropy.
SABIC®is formulated with antistatic and nucleating agents. The nucleating agents promote
smaller, more uniform crystallization during cooling, which leads to reduced and more uniform
shrinkage, therefore giving it low warpage. The low warpage still does not mean that the absolute
shrinkage is not meaningful. As such, SABIC® PP 579S offers a more isotropic shrinkage, making
it viable for applications that require tight dimensional control, even if it remains less precise than
amorphous polymers.

2.5
Simulation of shrinkage in injection molding
Computer-aided engineering (CAE) tools for injection molding, such as Autodesk Moldflow,
Moldex3D, SIGMASOFT, and others, have become a crucial step in the injection molding industry
to analyze the filling, packing, cooling, and warpage of molded parts. Simulation software have
advanced to the point where they are more often than not employed to predict shrinkage and
compensate for it.
However, predicting shrinkage and warpage is challenging and not perfectly
accurate, especially for complex part geometries or microfeatures. Researchers are actively working
on improving these simulations by developing better material models.
Conventional injection molding simulations use a combination of flow analysis and thermal
analysis to predict how a polymer fills a mold cavity and how it cools and solidifies. The princi-
pal equations solved are conservation of mass, momentum, and energy [20]. These equations are
combined with polymer-specific models, notably, the pvT (pressure-volume temperature) relation-
ship of materials and a viscosity model. The pvT relationship is often described by a two-domain
Tait equation of state, and describes how much a polymer material will shrink when going from
being molten to solid, at different pressures. Simulations use this to calculate volumetric shrinkage.
When a part is ejected and fully cooled, the predicted shrinkage in each direction is derived from
how the material was packed and how it froze in the simulation. The warpage is then predicted by
considering differential shrinkage and residual stresses.
One historical limitation is that simulation tools use the assumption that the mold cavity is
rigid and of a constant volume. They fill this rigid volume with material and apply a packing profile.
But using this assumption means that the simulation will not capture effects like cavity expansion
or machine platen deflection, where the platens attached to the mold do not provide enough support
to stabilize it against cavity injection pressure and clamp tonnage. Tosello and Costa [21] noted
that standard simulation software do indeed consider the mold a rigid, fixed volume, which leads to
discrepancies in predicting part dimensions. In real injection molding, molds do deform slightly. This
can for example be because a high packing pressure slightly pushes the cavity walls apart, resulting
in a larger part. Some advanced simulations allow coupling with structural finite-element analysis
(FEA) of the mold to include mold deformation, but this is not common. The trend in current
research for high-precision injection molding is to include these effects. For example, Carpenter
et al. [22] incorporated measured mold compliance into simulation to improve the accuracy of a
multi-cavity part’s variation predictions.
Another area of progress is three-dimensional simulation and micro-scale effects. Conventional

For shrinkage in particular, a critical input is the material’s pvT and shrinkage-related prop-

For this reason, it is often of great interest to calibrate simulations by molding a simple geometry
and adjusting the pvT or shrinkage model until the simulation matches the measured shrinkage

allow specifying different shrinkage rates in the flow-direction vs. transverse direction, to enhance
warpage predictions. The accuracy of simulation in predicting absolute part dimensions is perhaps
within 5-10% of the actual shrinkage, but it can vary. Tosello and Costa [21] found that simula-
tions need careful setup, and emphasized the need to minimize inconsistencies in experiments (e.g.
shot-to-shot variation) to have valid data to compare the simulation to.
Advanced simulation also assess residual stresses as a cause warpage, so, warpage prediction
requires that the simulation knows the frozen internal stresses of the part. Simulations compute the
stress developed during the packing and cooling phases (coming from temperature gradients and
constrained contraction). For example, if one side of a part cools faster than the other, it shrinks
while the other is still hot and pliable, thus producing a bending moment. Simulation can capture
this with layered temperature profiles and differing shrinkage at each layer. These residual stress
profiles then cause the part to deform when ejected from the mold
Currently, one trend is to create a "digital twin" of the injection molding process. This means
not simulating only, but pairing a simulation with real machine data, process parameters and
outputs. For example, a cavity pressure sensor’s readings might be used to determine simulation
profiles, to ensure that the simulated injection/packing pressure profiles match reality. Tseng et al.
[24] exemplified this by developing a method to use nozzle pressure and screw position data to set
up more realistic simulation boundary conditions. This aligned the simulation filling and packing
timing with the real case, thus improving shrinkage predictions. Therefore, the more the simulation
knows about the real process dynamics, the better it will be able to predict the outcomes.
However, despite all of these advances, challenges still remain. Predicting shrinkage for highly
crystalline materials can be difficult because crystallization kinetics (how rapidly and to what extent
a polymer crystallizes) are hard to model.
Some simulations employ simplistic models, which,
for example, are unable to capture differences in crystallinity between the core and the "skin"
(outermost layer), which affect shrinkage. The current state of the art in simulation can qualitatively
predict warpage behaviours and give an estimate of how much warpage will be seen, but exact
20
==PAGE:27==
Master’s thesis
2
State of the art
magnitudes might differ from reality unless careful calibrations are carried out.
In conclusion, simulation of shrinkage in injection molding is a powerful tool, but must be
used with a deep understanding of its limitations. The state of the art is moving toward improving
simulation reliability by including more real-world factors such as mold compliance, wall slip and
advanced material models. In practice simulation is used for shrinkage compensation, to decide
the mold dimensions.
A simulation is run, the shrinkage is predicted, and the cavity is scaled
accordingly to achieve closer dimensional agreeability. This can get the produced part very close to
the desired dimensions, at the very least requiring fewer adjustments. For warpage, simulation can
suggest changing the gate location or modifying some aspects of the geometry. However, when high
precision is needed, simulations are often supported with actual trial runs for final adjustments.

3.1.1
SABIC® PP 579S
The material that was used, both in the experimental injection-molding and simulations, was
Polypropylene (PP), a semi-crystalline thermoplastic [25], known for its light weight, high stiffness,
high impact and chemical resistance, high heat resistance, and dimensional stability. The polymer
belongs to the polyolefin family. It is produced through the polymerization of propylene (C3H6),
which is a monomer produced through petroleum refining. The polymerization is typically done
with a Ziegler-Natta catalysis [26], which allows for control over molecular weight distribution and
three-dimensional molecular structure. The resulting polymer chains consist of repeating units of
−CH2 −CH(CH3)−, making up a hydrocarbon backbone with methyl groups attached to every
other carbon atom.
Figure 3.1: Molecular structure of PP.
The chemical structure is characterized by saturated carbon-carbon single bonds, resulting
in a highly stable structure. PP can crystallize when its macromolecules have a regular, ordered
architecture (tacticity).
PP’s semi-crystalline nature contributes to its toughness, stiffness and chemical resistance. Its
crystallinity typically ranges from 30-60% [27]. The strong van der Waals forces between chains in
crystalline regions provide rigidity, while the amorphous regions contribute to impact resistance. The
chemical bonding consists entirely of covalent bonds within the polymer backbone, with secondary
van der Waals interactions between polymer chains.
PP is non-polar and hydrophobic, which
contributes to its chemical resistance against acids, bases, and organic solvents. Common processing
methods of PP include injection molding, extrusion, blow molding, and thermoforming. In summary,
PP is a highly engineered material with a well-understood chemical structure and a wide range of
processing applications. Its balance of mechanical strength, chemical resistance and processability
makes it a popular option in the packing, automotive, and medical industries.
SABIC PP 579S[5] is a specially formulated PP that has been optimized for thin-walled injection

the likelihood of incomplete fills or weld lines. Coupled with the polymer’s ability to replicated
fine mold details and produce high-gloss surfaces, these characteristics make SABIC® PP 579S

An amorphous polymer, BASF Polystyrol® 456M[30], was used solely in the Moldex3D sim-

aromatic thermoplastic made up of repeating styrene monomers. The polymer chains are made up
of repeating units of −CH2 −CH(C6H5)−. The molecular structure can be seen in Figure 3.5.
Figure 3.5: Molecular structure of PS.
It is characterized by its phenyl-group (C6H5). PP, in its commercial form, is primarily atac-
tic, which means that the phenyl-groups orientate themselves randomly, thus resulting in a fully
amorphous polymer.
General-purpose PS is stiff and easy to mold, but naturally brittle. BASF Polystyrol® 456M,
however, is an impact-grade variant of PS, enhanced for toughness.
This is typically achieved
through rubber modification, which is done by dispersing small particles of elastomer (rubber)

shear yielding, localized plastic deformations spread through the matrix, thereby further dissipating
the energy. This transforms the otherwise brittle PS into a much tougher material with much higher
impact resistance, while still retaining good flow in injection molding. The viscosity and pvT plots
of the PS-grade can be seen in Figures 3.6 and 3.7. They are taken from the Moldex3D database.

the material to cool more slowly and uniformly, thereby reducing internal stresses and differential
shrinkage. This helped to ensure good dimensional stability. The cooling time was set to eject the
sample once it reached an ejection temperature of 90°C, this turned out to be about 10 seconds.

solidification. This speed allowed the mold to fill completely without resulting in excessive shear
stress in the polymer, producing a controlled flow front throughout the cavity.

A pre-study was to be performed, to evaluate the differences in measurements between two

Testing for normality is an essential step, as many parametric tests, like ANOVA or t-tests, rely on
the assumption that the data are normally distributed. Verifying this assumption with normality
tests ensures that parametric tests are valid and statistically reliable. The following data will be

3.9
Pre-study: Measurement differences of operators
Human factors often play an important role in introducing variability in metrological tasks,
which affects the reliability of measurements in fields such as injection molding that require a high
degree of precision. Operator techniques or errors, such as inconsistent handling of equipment or a
lack of attention when measuring, can lead to deviations in recorded data. Differences in experience
or training can cause variations in the interpretation of measurement results. Fatigue or negligence
during repetitive measurements can introduce errors. Non-standardized protocols between opera-
tors can increase inconsistencies. This emphasizes the need for standardized procedures, operator
training, and, if possible, automation to minimize variability and ensure reliable metrological results.
A pre-study was conducted for this thesis, with the aim of gauging the measurement consistency
of two operators (Halli and Juan) and to compare the human factor between them. The study aimed
to investigate whether the measurement precision differs significantly between the two operators
when measuring the same samples. The samples to be measured were the fully molded samples made
from SABIC® PP 579S. Establishing a reliability of measurements would consequently strengthen
the credibility of the experimental data of the thesis. The pre-study employed blinding during the
data collection phase to minimize bias, ensuring that the operators were not aware of each other’s
measurements until after they were completed.
The chosen method to gauge this inter-operator difference was to compare measurement aver-
ages and measurement variability. The first sample would assess systematic bias, as in, checking
if the operators get different average measurements. This difference could also be influenced by
environmental factors (different times of measurement, temperature, humidity), so the better de-
terminant of the difference between operators is the second method, to compare the measurement
variance. Comparing the variance captures the measurement consistency of each operator.
3.9.1
Systematic bias
The typical statistical test to assess systematic bias would be a two-tailed paired t-test. How-
ever, since the distribution of the data is not known and cannot safely be assumed to be normally
distributed, a non-parametric test must be chosen instead. A non-parametric test is generally less
powerful than a parametric one, but since a significant portion of our data does not pass normality
tests, it must be chosen. The selected statistical test for this case is the Wilcoxon signed-rank test.
This test, similar to the t-test, determines whether two dependent groups differ significantly from
each other, but instead of using the mean values of the groups, the test uses the ranks.
The required assumptions for the Wilcoxon signed-rank test[40] are:
• Repeated measurements. The test is used for dependent samples, which means that the
same subjects or units are being measured under different conditions (i.e. different operators).
• Metric or ordinal data. The data should be ordinal or metric (numerical data), and not
categorical (e.g. eye colour).
51
==PAGE:58==
Master’s thesis
3
Materials and methods
• Symmetric distribution of differences. The differences of the paired observations should
be symmetrically distributed around the median. Unlike the parametric t-test, the Wilcoxon
signed-rank test does not require normality, but works best when the distribution is approxi-
mately symmetric.
• No significant outliers. Extreme outliers may influence the ranking process, reducing the
test’s reliability.
• Random sampling. The sample should be randomly selected from the population to ensure
unbiased results.
All of the assumptions are met. Although the test assumes that the sample is randomly selected
from a population, this condition is mostly relevant if the goal is to generalize findings of a broader
population of samples. The purpose of this analysis is not to do that, but to do a direct comparison
between two operators measuring the same set of samples under similar conditions.
The hypotheses of the Wilcoxon test are very similar to that of the paired t-test, but differ
in the way that the Wilcoxon test assesses whether there is a difference in the central tendency
whereas the t-test assesses whether there is a difference in the mean. The hypotheses are then:
• H0: There is no significant difference between the groups in terms of central tendency.
• H1: There is a significant difference between the groups in terms of central tendency.
Typically, the Wilcoxon test is used to evaluate two dependent samples from measurements of
a population. In this case, the mean of repeated measurements for each sample was used in order to
isolate potential systematic differences between operators. While this reduces the statistical power
by negating within-sample variability, it provides a clearer picture of central tendency differences
under matched conditions.

Flow-front
An investigation of the flow-front was performed to evaluate how accurately the software was
able to reproduce the filling progression. The short shot samples used for this validation were those
with injection volumes of 7, 9, 11, 13, 15cm3. These short shot’s weights were treated as target
conditions for the simulation. In Moldex3D, the virtual short shots were recreated by running the
filling analysis until the simulated part weight matched the experimental values. This approach was
meant to ensure that comparisons between experiment and simulation were based on equal amounts
of material injected into the cavity.
The experimental and simulated short shots were then compared in terms of flow-front ad-
vancement in the plates of the part geometry. By evaluating the degree of agreement at each stage
of the filling process, the accuracy of the simulation’s flow direction could be estimated in a clear
way.

not very accurately. In Moldex3D, Cavity 1 filled more slowly than in the experiments, while the
thinner plate filled more rapidly. This difference may be attributed to how the simulation model
handles shear-rate dependent viscosity and pressure distribution. In practice, the higher shear rates
in the thinner plate led to significant shear thinning, lowering the effective viscosity and enabling a
faster flow than could be expected solely based on thickness. At the same time, the thicker plate has
a lower pressure drop, which supports the melt’s tendency to go in that direction. If the simulation
is underestimating the amount of shear thinning in the thinner cavity, or overestimating pressure
losses in the thicker cavity, the predicted flow will fill the cavities more uniformly than the actual
molding process. The simplified model of the mold cavity used in the simulation must also be
considered, but its effect in the difference of the cavity-filling should not be too large though, as the
widths of both cavities were reduced compared to the actual mold cavity.
Another contributing factor could be oversimplified assumptions in the simulation’s flow model.
Variations in melt compressibility, localized temperature gradients, and wall-slip occurrences that
take place in the experiments may not be fully captured by the simulation. These factors combined,
could explain why the simulation underestimates the tendency of the thicker cavity to fill so much
faster.
In summary, the short shot comparison demonstrated that the simulation was capable of of
capturing the qualitative filling trend, but underestimates the asymmetry between the cavities.

The potential shrinkage of the parts is closely related to the amount of material forced into

to lower dimensional shrinkage, this relationship is consistent across both PP and PS. This was
also supported by a preliminary assessment of the simulated shrinkage and its close correlation.
With that in mind, it can be seen for both PP and PS, the packing time had the most significant
effect on the part weight, meaning that increasing the packing time led to the biggest reduction
in potential shrinkage. Following it is the packing pressure, which, according to Wang et. al. [3],
should have been the biggest contributor. This is most likely due to the difference in increase of
the parameters, since the lower packing time was set at 2 seconds, the higher at 16 seconds, and
the lower packing pressure set at 200 bar, the higher at 300 bar. An increased melt temperature,
contrary to the findings of Wang et. al. resulted in a lower part weight. This can be explained
though, since a hotter melt is slightly less dense. If a fixed volume (cavity volume) is injected, the
mass of the resulting part will be lower since mass = density × viscosity. Increasing the flow rate
had a negative, albeit very small, effect on the part weight, for both materials. A faster flow means
a more pronounced shearing effect in the material, which reduces the viscosity, and leads to less
resistance to pressure loss along the flow, translating to less packing efficiency, and therefore, a very

The increase in thickness of the plates is most likely not due to mold deflection, mold breathing
or other mechanical failures, as the increase is quite large, while mold breathing is typically on
the order of a few microns. It can, most likely, be instead attributed to local densification from
packing (a higher pressure at the end of the packing phase will mean a higher density of the melt
inside the cavity), freeze timing (once a solid skin-layer forms, it can transmit pressure but if it
78
==PAGE:85==
Master’s thesis
4
Results
forms too late, pressure is less effectively transmitted), elastic recovery at ejection (regions that are
hotter/less packed at ejection rebound more, regions that were well packed near a rigid skin-layer
rebound less), and in-plane vs. through the thickness strain-coupling (a large in-plane shrinkage
can translate to a small increase in the out-of-plane dimensions after release).
For Plate 1, at the gate, the lower injection volumes show a shrinkage through the thickness,
but as the volume is increased, the shrinkage decreases until there is a thickness increase. At these
low injection volumes, the gate region in Plate 1 sees a brief pressure and cools fast enough that the
skin forms and there is no packing. As the injection volume increases and packing is performed, the
gate region stays hotter for longer and the machine maintains pressure longer in front of the gate.
This raises the local density at the end of the packing phase, and leaves there a hot, compressible
core at the point of ejection. When released, this core recovers elastically, so the measurement flips
to a thickness increase.
For Plate 2, at the gate, the thin section causes high shear rates at the gate, which leads to
strong shear heating and a delay in the freeze of the skin in that region. The lower injection volumes
also are not packed at all. This should lead to a hotter and less packed core that will exhibit a
greater elastic recovery. It can be seen that the fully-filled samples start to reverse this effect, though
still increase slightly in thickness.
The far ends of both plates are the lowest-pressure regions during short shot filling, and without
a packing phase, remain underpacked, thus resulting in a net shrinkage. When packing pressure
is applied however, more material is driven into the far end before the freeze, and even though
the pressure drops the further the flow is from the gate, the long packing time allows the melt at
the ends to compress before freezing. This still leaves a warm core that rebounds upon injection,
resulting in a thickness increase.

Discussion
5
Discussion
A pre-study was performed ahead of the metrology of the main part of the thesis. It confirmed
that there was a significant difference in measurement results between operators, both in terms of
systematic differences and measurement variance, meaning that the operators showed both different
average results, and a statistical difference in the "scatter" of measurement data obtained from the
same parts.
Results showed that while Moldex3D is capable of qualitatively reproducing key experimen-
tal trends in filling and packing behaviour and shrinkage development, notable deviations persist
in quantitative accuracy. For the filling studies, simulations consistently underestimated the domi-
nance of the thicker cavity to fill first during short shot molding, primarily due to an underprediction
of shear thinning and overestimation of pressure drops in the thicker regions.
In terms of part weight, simulation and experiments showed good agreement, indicating that
the material flow rate into the cavity was quite accurately captured. Shrinkage predictions, however,
revealed some differences. The simulations predicted more uniform shrinkage across the flow and
through the thickness, where the experiments showed a thickness increase, most likely driven by
elastic recovery of underpacked regions and skin-core crystallization effects. The simulation also
consistently predicted greater shrinkage near the gate compared to the flow end, reflecting the
effect of premature skin freeze and the packing being cut off, which was not always the case in the
experiments.
The full factorial 25 virtual Design of Experiments highlighted that the melt temperature,
packing pressure and packing time had the most significant effect when trying to minimize shrinkage,
represented by the amount of material being packed into the cavity, while showing that the mold
temperature and flow rate had an almost negligible effect. It also showed that the melt temperature
and flow rate had the most significant impacts with regards to maximum injection pressure reached,
while the mold temperature had only a very slight effect. Overall, PP exhibited a higher sensitivity
to the varying parameters than PS. Overall, the results serve to confirm that the Moldex3D software
aligns with general understanding that packing, melt temperature, and mold temperature will have
the largest effects on shrinkage predictions.

Future work
6
Future work
While it is the hope that this thesis has provided some insights into the filling behaviour, shrink-
age predictions, and capabilities and limitations of injection molding simulations, several other lim-
itations remain. These limitations should show the opportunities for further research or refinement,
both in the experimental methods employed, and the approaches taken in the simulations.
From the author of this thesis, the key recommendations to be made for similar research would
be:
• Characterize the mold and cavities in a more robust way, ensuring that the simulation setups
follow as closely as possible.
• Before each measurement process, perform a calibration of the measurement equipment, to
better evaluate the uncertainty and systematic biases inherent in the measurement procedure.
• Similarly, capture the inputs and outputs of injection molding trials more precisely, to better
capture them in the software.
• When trying to compare experimental and simulated short shots, complementary comparisons
of the volumetric filling and/or injection time would provide a better insight into flow-front
prediction, rather than just matching short shots based on weight.
• Investigate mold deflection in a meaningful way, perhaps through a clamp-force experiment.
• Test more than one material experimentally, preferably a semi-crystalline polymer versus an
amorphous polymer.
• Perform an experimental DOE followed by a virtual one, to truly test a simulation software’s
accuracy in the effect of process parameters.
• Better model the molten material in the screw region in the simulation software to better
capture the melt behaviour.
Lastly and most generally, it is important to go into a project such as this one with a better
understanding of the basics of injection molding, and injection molding simulations. There is a
learning curve that should not be underestimated.
