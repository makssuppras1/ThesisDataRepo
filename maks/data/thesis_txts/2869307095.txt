This thesis was elaborated at the DTU Sustain, Department of Environmental and

Engineering degree, study line Civil Engineering. This thesis aims to assess the

Master’s Thesis
Abstract
This study explores how Bayesian optimization and multivariate distributions can
improve soil parameter estimation for a deep excavation at a metro station in Copen-
hagen. The goal is to increase numerical modeling accuracy by using back-analysis
from real-world measurements. After defining the initial soil properties, surface loads
and the groundwater table (GWT) were adjusted using the back-analysis approach,
generating posterior distributions of soil parameters that fit within the observed
displacements.
Two analyses were carried out. The first used a soil set with overly stiff limestone
layers, which caused unexpected compensations in surrounding soil parameters and
resulted in unrealistic behavior.
To correct this, a new set of soil samples was
generated using multivariate distributions, leading to more expected results.
The findings show that surface loads had little impact on excavation, with the op-
timized values being close to the assumed uniform design load of 20 kPa. However,
the groundwater table showed much greater variation. The GWT values maxed out
the Bayesian optimization bounds, indicating that a wider parameter range should
have been considered from the start.
Overall, this study shows that Bayesian optimization can significantly improve ac-
cepted soil parameters, thereby increasing model predictions to align with real-world
measurements. The findings shows the importance of accurately predicting ground-
water tables, as uncertainties in water pressure can have a major impact on the
response of deep excavations.

2.4
The left chart shows the GP prior, it is the model’s belief about the
function f before any data is observed, and is determined only by
the mean function µ(x) and the covariance function K(x, x′). The
colored lines are random samples from the prior distribution, repre-
senting possible functions that could describe the objective function
f.
Additionally, the shaded blue area shows the variance around
the mean function µ(x). The right chart illustrates the posterior of
the GP, where the updated belief about the function after observing
four data points from f are seen as is the black dots. Using Bayes’
theorem, the GP is incorporating these observations to update its
predictions of f.
This results in variance is reduced significantly
near the data points, as the GP are more confident about the func-
tion in these areas, as the mean prediction is passing through the
observed points. The colored lines represents any random samples
about the posterior distributions, that is constrained by the observed
data. These are the updated beliefs of f guided by the observed data
(Rasmussen and Williams 2006).

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
Over the past decades, rapid urbanization and the increasing demand for eﬀicient
transportation have led to a growing need for underground space. In dense urban en-
vironments, where surface space is limited, subsurface infrastructure such as metro
systems has become essential for commuting. The construction of deep excavations
for such infrastructure is governed by the behavior of retaining walls and the result-
ing ground movements. Excessive soil deformations can pose significant risks, and
can potentially cause damage to adjacent structures, leading to severe economic and
human consequences (Zhang 2023).
Understanding and accurately predicting the response of retaining walls in deep ex-
cavations is therefore critical in ensuring the safety and stability of underground
construction (Panagiotis et al. 2024a). Therefore, accurate predictions of lateral
wall deflections are critical in the design of excavation support systems. Excessive
conservatism due to uncertainties in underground conditions often results in over
predictions of the wall deformations, increasing economical and environmental costs
(Jin et al. 2021).
Unfortunately, predicting the geotechnical response on structures is diﬀicult due to
the inherent variability of soil, which stems from natural geologic processes, measure-
ment uncertainties and transformation of the models for the design. Soil properties
can vary significantly due to deposition history. Additionally, measurement errors
from sparse data, equipment precision, operator influence, and random testing ef-
fects introduce additional uncertainties. This is compounded when transforming the
measurements into the assessment of the soil properties, which also may introduce
biases and uncertainties. Moreover, samples tested in a laboratory may not be rep-
resentative of soil in the field. (Phoon and Kulhawy 1999). This process is shown
below in Figure 1.1.

As it is critical infrastructure, the structures are usually equipped with monitoring
systems to track the performance during construction. This can provide essential
data, that enables back analysis, which identifies the discrepancies between predicted
and observed responses. This process not only assesses the conservatism, but also
allows engineers to calibrate the geotechnical properties to improve future designs
or subsequent construction stages. Often the conservatism observed in geotechnical
designs stems from uncertainties in soil properties. By analyzing monitored data,
designers can back analyze and infer the soil parameters to make the predicted re-
sponse align more with the observed data, and not only evaluate the properties
based on laboratory testing.
In addition to soil variability, other sources of uncertainty have a significant im-
pact on the response of the structures. These include uncertainties in the structural
system, the performance of anchors, surface loads on the construction site, and
groundwater table fluctuations. Each of these factors contributes to the overall un-
certainty in the response of retaining walls and must be addressed to get reliable
designs, where the monitoring systems play a key role in evaluating these uncertain-
ties.
1.2
Thesis Goals
To address the uncertainty, engineers have traditionally considered soil parameters
as independent variables, modeling them using probability density functions (PDFs)
(Phoon and Kulhawy 1999). However, these univariate distributions overlook the
fact that geotechnical parameters are often interdependent. To account for these
interdependencies, the degree of association between variables can be quantified us-
ing correlation coeﬀicients (Panagiotis et al. 2024b). Therefore, this study uses soil
samples generated from constructed multivariate distributions that aim to capture
the actual variability of soil parameters. These samples are applied in a back analy-
sis, simulating a metro excavation thousands of times to identify the most probable
parameter values.

The soil samples used in this study were generated using a probabilistic framework

of the clay tills and limestone compared to traditional univariate distributions.

the model. Unless stated otherwise, all information is sourced from (Panagiotis et

Bayesian Optimization
Bayesian optimization (BO) is a sequential design strategy aimed at the global
optimization of black-box functions without assuming any specific functional forms.
This approach is particularly useful for optimizing functions that are expensive to
evaluate (Garnett 2023). In general, BO is a powerful tool for optimizing black-box
systems of the form:
x∗= arg min
x∈X f(x),
(2.2)
Where f : X →R represents the domain or design space of the objective function
f, whereas no assumptions are made, and x denote the set of input parameters.
As shown in Equation 2.2, the optimization goal is to identify the point at which
the global minimum of occurs. BO is particularly suited for problems where the
following conditions hold:
• The objective function lacks a closed-form expression.
• Analytical derivatives of are not available.
• The function can only be evaluated at discrete points.
Therefore, the evaluation is restricted to point-wise queries, where the value of f
at any query point x ∈X must be determined through computational simulations
or physical experiments (Zhang et al. 2021). Algorithm 1 outlines a systematic
approach to implementing Bayesian Optimization:

2.3.1
The Bayesian Philosophy
Having outlined the model and algorithm, it is important to understand the broader
philosophical underpinnings of Bayesian optimization, which guide its design and
application. Compared to classical mathematical optimization, a known, directly
computable objective function is not required in Bayesian optimization. Instead, it
relies on some information about identified points, and by accumulating a suﬀicient
amount of information, the minimum is inferred. This approach avoids the need to
explicitly express the objective function, which is the major advantage of Bayesian
optimization. This characteristic is referred to as ”black-box” optimization (Garnett
2023).
Bayesian optimization does not refer to a single specific algorithm but rather to a
philosophical approach to optimization from in Bayesian inference. Optimization at
its core, is a sequence of decisions, in each iteration, one must decide where to make
the next observation and whether to terminate based on the outcomes. Since the
outcomes of the decisions are determined by the system, the success of optimization
relies entirely on effective decision-making (Garnett 2023).
2.3.2
Gaussian Processes (GPs)
The first step in Bayesian Optimization is to construct a surrogate model, which
approximates the unknown objective function at all feasible points. For this study,
a Gaussian Process (GP) is applied as the surrogate model, due to the flexibility and
the ability to make predictions for the data by applying prior knowledge. Several
other surrogate models are also available such as Bayesian Neural Networks and
Deep Kernel Learning (Li et al. 2024).
GPs (Rasmussen and Williams 2006) are probabilistic models that define distri-
butions over any type of functions.
The goal of GPs is to infer the underlying
distribution of the objective function f from observed data, consisting of input pa-
rameters x and the corrosponding values f(x). The concept of GPs is to model the
relationship between x and f(x) as a multivariate normal distribution. This implies
that the joint probability distribution P(x, f(x)) holds the range of possible func-
tion values for f, given the observed data (Görtler et al. 2019). Before proceeding
into the mathematical formulations of GPs Figure 2.3 is visualizing the behavior of
GPs.

Bayesian Inference
Bayesian inference, often described as a form of “educated guessing,” is a probabilis-
tic approach for inferring uncertain aspects of a system based on observed data. It
applies a principle of all unknown quantities can be represented as random variables,
which are modeled with probability distributions over their possible values.
It begins with a prior distribution P(f), representing the initial assumptions about
the objective function f before any data is observed. The prior holds existing knowl-
edge about the function f. And when new data D is observed, the prior is updated
using the likelihood function P(D | f), which quantifies how well f explains the
observed data. Combining the prior and likelihood through Bayes’ theorem yields
the posterior distribution P(f | D), which represents updated beliefs about f given
the observed data:
P(f | D) = P(D | f)P(f)
P(D)
(2.3)
This approach is foundational for Bayesian Optimization, where the posterior dis-
tribution guides the selection of query points. With this foundation established,
the focus now shifts to applying Bayesian inference within the optimization process
(Garnett 2023).

µ(x) and a covariance function K(x, x′):
p(f) = GP(f; µ, K)

3.2
Stratigraphy and Soil Conditions
3.2.1
Geology
The geology of the Copenhagen area is made up of several sequences of deposits
resulting from its geological history. During long periods of submersion and warm
climate, marine sedimentation was highly present, leading to the formation of large
amounts of marine deposits. Over geological time, these deposits were subjected
to compaction, forming dense and consolidated limestone layers, that can reach
thicknesses of several kilometers (Sørensen 2019).
In more recent geological history, during the Quaternary period, the area experi-
enced multiple glacial events associated with the Ice Ages. These glaciers deposited
extensive, unsorted sediments, including clay-rich glacial till and sands. The glacial
activity also exerted significant pressure on the underlying soils, resulting in highly
consolidated layers. The topsoil comprises a combination of natural deposits and
anthropogenic fill, accumulated since the last glaciation. This fill includes mate-
rial deposited from human activities, while the natural deposits reflect post-glacial
processes(Frederiksen et al. 2003).
3.2.2
Geological Investigation and Derivation of
Geotechnical Values
The Geological Investigation Report (GIR) (JV 2018) outlines how input parameters
are derived and used for modeling the response on the retaining walls, through site
investigations and laboratory testing such as Field Vane Tests (FVT) or Standard
Penetration Tests (SPT). This section will provide the overveiw of deriviation of
parameters for each soil. It’ll be subdivided into the fill, sands, clays, and limestone.
Fill
The fill layer is the top most layer in the Copenhagen soils. This layer is characterized
by its anthropogenicity and highly heterogeneity (Frederiksen et al. 2003). And
specifiacally, the Cityringen - branch off to Sydhavnen, where ØVK is, is more or
less built on reclaimed land. Meaning significant fill layers are deposited by human
(COWI SYSTRA Joint Venture 2017). The thickness of the fill is measured to be
5.2 m. No SPT or triaxial tests were carried out on the fill layers at EBR/ØVK,
which are mainly cohesionless.
When considering the inherent variability of the
fill, and grading of layers, the effective strength parameters were estimated using
engineering judgment and using line wide data. The results are shown in Table 3.2.
Clay Tills
Below the fill, the clay tills are subdivided into Upper and Lower Clay Till. These
layers differ in strength and elasticity, with the Lower Clay Till having higher
strength.
The undrained shear strength (Cu) for the clay tills was derived using SPT, FVT,
and triaxial tests, with the SHANSEP framework applied. Characteristic values for
Cu were determined as 200 kPa for the Upper Clay Till and 260 kPa for the Lower
Clay Till, based on available test results and engineering judgment.
The clay tills display high preconsolidation pressures, with values of 600 kPa for Up-
per Clay Till and 2400 kPa for Lower Clay Till, due to their glacial loading history.
The overconsolidation ratio (OCR) indicates the degree of consolidation, with higher

Rock Mass Strength and Compressibility for Parameters for
Copenhagen Limestone
Below the soils, there are three layers of limestone the SPW is embedded into.
The limestone units at the site exhibit variability due to glacial disturbance and
stratigraphic differences. Key characteristics of the different limestone types are
summarized below.
Glacially Disturbed Limestone (CL)
The glacially disturbed zone shows evidence of deformation, with original stratig-
raphy preserved but significantly fractured. In some areas, a ”crushed” limestone
variant appears, characterized by a blocky, interlocked mass with angular blocks
formed by multiple joint sets. (COWI SYSTRA Joint Venture 2017)

==PAGE:32==

Master’s Thesis
Upper Copenhagen Limestone (UCL)
The UCL is a horizontally bedded unit with layers of varying hardness and thickness
(from a few centimeters to 1 m). Flint is common, occurring as dark nodules (50–100
mm thick, 100–200 mm long) or layers (0.2–0.4 m thick, occasionally exceeding 1
m). Strongly indurated limestone and flint layers are associated with high resistivity
and low porosity in geophysical logs. The transition to the Middle Copenhagen
Limestone (MCL) is marked by log marker H1, characterized by changes in porosity,
conductivity, and resistivity, along with a gamma log peak at GM3.(COWI SYSTRA
Joint Venture 2017)
Middle Copenhagen Limestone (MCL)
The (MCL) has fewer indurated layers and flint bands compared to the UCL, with
higher porosity and conductivity. Its flint occurs as burrows or precipitates with
smooth transitions to the UCL(COWI SYSTRA Joint Venture 2017). Unconfined
Compressive Strength (UCS) tests and Point Load Tests (Is,50) indicate that the
MCL’s strength correlates with bulk density and moisture content. Higher bulk
density (above 2.2 g/cm3) increases strength, while higher moisture content re-
duces it. These properties were incorporated into the design using regional datasets.
Rock mass strength and compressibility parameters were derived using the Gener-
alised Hoek-Brown criterion, and the values are consistent with those used in similar
projects, making them suitable for the ØVK excavation.
In generel, the three distinct layers of limestone used for the modelling, is mostly
the same, having the CL layer significantly more fractured, hence lower in strength,
and for the UCL and MCL there is a slight difference in the flint and water content,
and the parameters are derived through Brazil tests, UCS, and comparisons with
line wide data and the Øresund bridge project.

Methodology
4.1
Numerical Modeling
The numerical model was developed in Python using PLAXIS 2D as an API, via
the plxscripting library (PLAXIS Development Team 2025).
This integration
enabled fully automated model setup, calculations, and result extraction, providing
a robust framework. The model incorporates the seven geological layers: Fill (FY),
Upper Clay (ML1), Lower Clay (ML2), Glacial Sand (MS), Glacially Disturbed
Limestone (CL), Upper Copenhagen Limestone (UCL), and Middle Copenhagen
Limestone (MCL). The properties of the layers were modeled using the multivariate
distributions from previous papers as outlined in section 2.2. The retaining wall
were modeled using a plate, with corresponding interfaces and the anchors were
considered as node-to-node anchors.
Where one retaining wall is shown due to
symmetry and computation eﬀiciency. Additionally, grout body for anchors was
embedded in the CL and UCL, with its bonded length and resistance adjusted
based on multilayer-dependent properties. Below in Figure 4.1 an example model is
visualized.

this study, presented in chronological order.

Skin Friction on Anchors
The anchors help minimize wall deflections by transferring loads into the limestone.
The skin friction along the bonded length of the anchor is a key parameter for this.
This subsection explains how the shaft resistance is derived.
CL
The anchors are embedded 2.8 m in the CL, where the shaft resistance for the
glacially disturbed limestone was selected due to significant core loss observed in
geotechnical investigations. Since the core loss was mostly due to fracturing caused
by glacial activity, the standard H1 assumption was considered conservative for this
material. The high degree of fracturing results in reduced rock quality, which must
be taken into account to avoid underestimating the capacity of the material (JV
2019). To address this, the method proposed by (Bustamante and Doix 1985) for
fractured chalk was adopted as it is better suited in evaluating heavily fractured
rock masses.
This method uses SPT data to determine shaft resistance in such
conditions. By referencing their graph (MC.2 line) and applying a median SPT value
of 117 blows, including extrapolated values, the shaft resistance was determined to
be qs = 400 kPa. The results are shown in Figure 4.5.
Figure 4.5: Derivation of shaft resistance on CL of 400kPa.

4.1.5
Staged Construction
In the Construction tab, the sequence of construction is organized. For these analy-
ses, only the initial excavation and anchor activation stages are considered, as they
deviate the most from simulations and more phases are computationally costly. In
PLAXIS, the process is divided into five phases:
1. Initial Phase: Represents the default loading conditions with no construction
or loads activated.
2. Installation of SPW [Phase 1]: Activates the secant pile wall (SPW) and
respective interfaces.
3. GWT Lowering [Phase 2]: Lowers the groundwater table (GWT) +1.2
m DVR90 or 1 m below ground level (BGL). This phase incorporates hydro-
static water pressure based on piezometer measurements during construction,
achieving a GWT of -0.94 m DVR90 outside the pit and -11.1 m DVR90 inside.
Additionally, the UDL of 20 kPa is activated in this phase
4. Excavation to -2.5 m [Phase 3:] Excavating 4.7 m BGL to a depth of -2.5
m DVR90.
5. Activation of first Anchor [Phase 4]: The node-to-node anchor and its
embedded anchor were activated and prestressed to 664 kN, as observed in the
field data.
When the setup is complete, the command g_i.calculate() is executed to initiate
the PLAXIS calculation process.

4.1.6
Post Calculation
As the model finishes the computationally costly calculation, PLAXIS Output is
opened to extract results. For this study, displacements, shear forces, and bending
moments were extracted and stored in CSV files for subsequent data processing.
Additionally, the UDL, GWT, and the soil input parameters for each simulation
run are saved in a JSON file to ensure organized and structured formatting across
all runs. The csv and json files are saved common folder. With the results linked to
their corresponding inputs and soil parameters, the Bayesian Optimization process
is then applied to get the UDL and GWT distributions.
4.2
Implementation of Bayesian Optimization
Bayesian Optimization (BO) is applied for the anchor activation phase to optimize
uniformly distributed surface loads and groundwater levels both inside and outside
the excavation pit, as shown in Figure 4.7b. More specifically, the BO minimizes
the error between measured and simulated wall deflections during this phase, with
the inputs and error passed to the BO algorithm. Whereas the inputs are the UDL
and GWT levels, this will now be referenced to as the external loads. To ensure
consistency, the same soil configurations are kept across all BO runs while optimizing
the displacement predictions, by changing the external loads. The optimization is
performed for 10 iterations pr soil sample, for balancing between computational
costs and near-optimal errors.
4.2.1
Calculation of Error Between Simulations and
Measured Deflections
The Bayesian Optimization (BO) framework aims to minimize the error between
simulated and measured deflections of the retaining wall. For this case the process
involves nine measurement points along the wall as seen in Figure 2.1a, where the
simulated displacements from PLAXIS are interpolated to match the exact depths of
the measured values. The corresponding deflections at these locations are compared
by subtracting the measured values from the simulated ones.
To penalize large discrepancies, the squared differences are calculated, to ensure
the BO algorithm avoids input parameters in regions with large deviations. This
improves convergence and refines the search of optimal parameters. The applied
formulation for calculating the error is seen in Equation 4.8 below:
e =
∑
(S −M)2
(4.8)
Where S represents simulated interpolated displacements, and M represents mea-
sured values on the retaining wall. The error calculation is integrated into a matrix
that corresponds with the input parameters for optimization. Here, x represents
the UDL, y and z correspond to the GWT levels in and outside the excavation pit,
respectively. By structuring the inputs in this way, the optimization process effec-
tively correlates external loads with the error, as used for the sequential updating.

4.2.3
Sequential Updating
The inputs, along with their corresponding error, are provided to the BO algorithm,
which then proposes the next set of parameters to minimize the error. This itera-
tive process is carried out 10 times. Although ideally, it would continue until full
convergence, significant error reduction is typically observed after approximately 8
iterations, with the results showing signs of convergence.
In this way, the BO algorithm sequentially updates the parameters while minimizing
the error. Only parameters within the measured bounds are considered valid, and
the samples inside the measured bounds form the basis for generating an updated
posterior distribution of the external loads, along with the soil parameters.
The first iteration always starts with the initial conditions suggested by the design
reports (JV 2019) namely a surface load of -20 kPa and groundwater levels of -11.1
m and -0.94 m DVR90 inside and outside the excavation pit, respectively. These
initial conditions as input for the first iteration of the BO. Equation 4.9 shows how

6.2
Load Uncertainty
For the design reports, the GWT was determined using historical data, with an ex-
pected lowering of GWT inside the construction pit. However, during construction,
the GWT was lowered by more than 5 meters in the pit compared to the design
expectations. A Uniformly Distributed Load UDL of 20 kPa was used in the design.
The results shows that optimizing external loads significantly improved the number
of accepted soil samples. Specifically, in the first sample set, the number of accepted
soil samples in the anchor activation phase increased from 14 to 81 out of 14,615
samples, while in the second sample set, it increased from 9 to 87 out of 312 samples.
Which shows the impact of optimizing external forces in increasing the accepted
soils.
6.2.1
Surface Loads
For the first set of soil samples, the most probable UDL was 18.7 kPa, which closely
aligns with the assumed design value of 20 kPa. In this case, GWT was the domi-
nant governing factor. However, in the second sample set, the most probable UDL
increased to 24 kPa, resulting in a 20% increase.
As previously mentioned, the retaining wall exhibited pronounced bending in the
soils in the first sample set. Consequently, increasing UDL led to further bending,
making many soil samples unacceptable. If a single simulated point exceeded the

distribution of UDL’s between 15–20 kPa.
6.2.2
Groundwater Table
In the first sample set, the modeled GWT values were -0.48 m outside the pit and
-11.6 m inside. However, the most frequent GWT values were observed between
-0.25 m and -0.1 m outside the pit, with a strong concentration at -13.1 m inside.
This suggests that a large number of cases required a uniform ‘push’ on the wall to
fit within acceptable bounds. Given the high stiffness of the limestone, this adjust-
ment led to additional bending of the upper part of the wall, which in turn required
lowering the UDL to compensate.
For the second sample set, the GWT distribution remained similar, with -0.1 m
outside the pit and -13.1 m inside being the most frequent values. Since upper and
lower GWT bounds were maximized, the parameter space for Bayesian Optimization
(BO) was likely constrained, possibly limiting further refinements.
Notably, the
measured GWT inside the pit aligns well with expectations, given that piezometers
were placed close to the wall. However, outside the pit, the GWT measurement
area was located approximately 50 meters from the retaining wall, which introduces
some uncertainty, as shown in Figure 4.9.
6.3
Load Influence on Soil Behavior
For this analysis, the Uniformly Distributed Load (UDL) was set to 20 kPa. The
back-analysis produced load values closely matching this assumption (18.7 and 24
kPa), confirming that the originally assumed 20 kPa was appropriate. The primary
objective of this study was to assess whether load uncertainty significantly impacted
the system’s behavior. The results suggest that, for the selected set of soil samples,
variations in load did not introduce significant additional uncertainty. Instead, the
key uncertainties in the model appear to be more closely related to soil property
variability, GWT, or the structural system rather than the applied UDL. The back-
analysis validates the UDL of approximately 20 kPa.
In contrast to the UDL, which remained consistent with design values, the ground-
water table exhibited significant deviations between initial assumptions and back-
analysis results. Originally, the GWT was expected at elevations of -11 m inside the
pit and -.94 m outside. However, the analysis found that the most common values
were -0.25 m outside the pit and -13 m inside.
This discrepancy indicates that, while load uncertainty had low influence, ground-
water conditions were far more variable than anticipated. The results suggest that
overly restrictive artificial bounds on the GWT during the Bayesian optmization
may have constrained exploration.

6.5
Model Limitations
Although a sophisticated approach was used to define the soil parameters, and lead-
ing to accurate distributions using BO, other parameters contribute to the overall
model uncertainty and require attention.
6.5.1
Hydrolic conditions
The model assumes groundwater follows a hydrostatic distribution along the retain-
ing wall, resulting in water pressures are considered uniform and constant. In reality,
107
==PAGE:121==
6
Discussion
Master’s Thesis
groundwater is highly variable, influenced by seasonal changes, and variations in soil
permeability. These factors can cause localized pressure differences that affect soil
strength and wall stability. A more detailed, groundwater model could improve the
analyses.
6.5.2
Structural System
The secant pile wall and anchors are modeled with idealized material properties, as-
suming the concrete, steel, and anchor prestressing all behave exactly as anticipated.
However, in construction, material properties can vary due to manufacturing differ-
ences and errors upon installation. The assumption of linear elasticity also means
that potential cracking, plastic deformations, or non-uniform load distribution in
the structure aren’t fully captured.
6.5.3
Soil Stratification and Homogeneity
The model assumes the soil layers are perfectly uniform, with clear distinct bound-
aries between different materials. In reality, natural soil deposits are often much
more irregular, with gradual transitions, local variations in stiffness, and small het-
erogeneities that can influence how forces are distributed.
These details aren’t
present in the model, which could impact the accuracy of the results.
6.5.4
Bayesian Optimization
Although Bayesian optimization is a powerful tool for finding the most optimal ex-
ternal loads, limitations are inevitable. The predefined parameter ranges restrict the
search space, which means that some physically valid solutions might be excluded,
as might have been the case for the GWT. The optimization process also relies on
a finite number of simulations, meaning that certain parameter interactions might
not have been fully explored.
6.5.5
Future Work
Future analyses could be improved by incorporating more advanced groundwater
modeling, refining the structural representation, and expanding the Bayesian opti-
mization process. In particular, a more detailed shaft resistance model for anchors
could improve accuracy, while allowing Bayesian optimization to explore a wider
range of groundwater table (GWT) values could lead to better parameter estima-
tions. Additionally, more parameters, such as soil layer thickness, could be included
in the optimization process to better capture soil variability. However, increasing
the number of input parameters would also increase computational costs,

Conclusion
This study applied soil parameters generated through multivariate distributions to
a real-world metro station in Copenhagen to obtain more accurate soil parameters
for numerical modeling. Once the initial soil properties were defined, the surface
loads and groundwater table (GWT) were optimized using Bayesian optimization,
with a back-analysis approach to ensure that the simulated wall behavior aligned
with observed deformations. Thousands of simulations were conducted to generate
a posterior distributions of soil parameters that fit within the accepted displacement
bounds, along with distributions for surface loads and groundwater conditions.
Two analyses were carried out. The first used a soil set with excessive strength in
the limestone layers, which make up the bottom half of the retaining wall. This
rigid rock behavior led to unexpected distributions for the soil parameters, as they
compensated in unexpected ways. To address this issue, a new soil sample set was
generated, based on the multivariate distributions, which produced results that were
closer to expectations.
The distribution of surface loads showed that they have limited impact on deep
excavations. Engineers assumed a uniform surface load of 20 kPa, which closely
aligned with the Bayesian optimization results, where the most probable values were
19 kPa and 24 kPa for the first and second soil sample sets, respectively. However,
the groundwater table showed much greater deviations, with the most common
values found at -13 m inside the pit and -0.1 m outside. The GWT maxed out the
Bayesian optimization bounds, suggesting that a wider parameter range should have
been considered from the start.
Overall, this study yielded significant improvements in the identification of accepted
soil samples, that made the displacements fit within measured bounds by adjust-
ing surface loads and groundwater conditions through a Bayesian framework. The
findings shows the importance of accurately predicting groundwater tables, as un-
certainties in water pressure can have a major impact on the response of deep exca-
vations.
