Abstract
The anticipated surge in wind power generation in the North Sea over the coming decades has
prompted the development of extensive offshore networks. This development raises concerns about
energy dissipation during cables faults, which was previously mainly addressing onshore faults for
point-to-point connections. The interconnection of multiple points makes the positioning, sizing,
and optimization of energy dissipation methods more complex. In this study, we employ a multi
terminal HVDC network model based on the one that connects Belgium and Denmark via the
Danish North Sea Energy Island (NSEI), implemented in the DIgSILENT Power Factory program.
Three dissipation methods were tested: onshore HVDC chopper, offshore AC chopper, and wind
turbine DC link choppers and results were evaluated for three types of faults: onshore AC, offshore
DC and offshore AC. The different options are then compared on the choppers’ capacity in terms
of power and amount of energy dissipated, as well as on the voltages behavior within the network.
The results strongly recommend the use of wind turbine DC link choppers as main energy dissipa-
tion units. For onshore faults, these are activated automatically as soon as the offshore converter
deceases its power setpoint. This requires the wind turbines to be in grid forming control. HVDC
chopper units remain useful to limit the initial peak due to the activation time delay. Then, for
permanent DC faults, wind turbine choppers dissipate power until the turbines ramp down their
aerodynamic power to a lower steady state value. Finally, they are also used as fault ride through
strategies for offshore AC faults.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The world is facing an energy transition towards more sustainable and renewable sources of en-
ergy. In this context, the European Union (EU) has set ambitious goals to reduce greenhouse gas
emissions by 55% by 2030 and reach carbon neutrality by 2050 [1]. Among the multiple options
to produce renewable energy, wind power, and particularly offshore wind, represents a significant
energy source to exploit. It is becoming the main driver of the European strategy towards carbon
neutrality [8]. The offshore wind power installed in the North sea currently stands at 12 GW, but
the EU aims to increase it to 60 GW by 2030 and 300 GW by 2050 [9]. The four neighbouring
countries, Belgium, the Netherlands, Germany and Denmark have signed an agreement in Ejsberg
(The Esbjerg Offshore Wind Declaration) to contribute by 150GW by 2050 [33]. This significant
increase brings with it many technological challenges in order to manage this large decentralized
power generation.
To achieve this, energy islands have become a possible solution for the future, showing promising
results in centralizing, converting, and exporting large amounts of power to national grids. Den-
mark, with its North Sea Energy Island (DNSEI), remains the world leader in this regard. The
construction of its first artificial energy island is planned for 2032, located 100 km off the west
coast [24]. Current plans also involve the installation of a hydrogen station with Power-to-X tech-
nology next to the island, enabling the transport of hydrogen over and above the DC connection to
shore. This is done to achieve greater efficiency, considering the large power capacities involved
[12]. Additionally, this platform will serve as a gateway for interconnection between countries,
ensuring a secure supply for the future network.
Several international connections are already planned, including the construction of a 2 GW DC
cable spanning over 600 km, connecting Belgium and Denmark, known as the "Triton Link" [30]
[20]. This hybrid interconnector project not only establishes an electrical connection between the

When the transmission capacity of the interconnector is not fully utilized for offshore wind en-

Furthermore, the management of faults when transporting significant power over long distances

oughly investigated. To ensure a stable and reliable grid for customers, offshore wind farms need
to exhibit a certain level of resilience to potential faults in the supply chain. The management of
this excess energy during the milliseconds following a fault is the main subject addressed in this
report.
1.2
Scope and Limitations
This study encompasses the investigation of energy dissipation strategies during temporary AC
faults and DC faults. To model the entire MT-HVDC (multi-terminal high voltage direct current)
grid connecting Denmark, Belgium, and the DNSEI, and to analyze its behavior during transient
periods, certain assumptions have been established.
Firstly, the wind farms have been modeled as one aggregated wind turbine (WT) in this study.
While the parameters and elements have been scaled up, this simplification does not take into ac-
count the individual behavior of each wind turbine. Additionally, the aerodynamic behavior of the
turbines has not been considered, assuming a constant input power without any disturbances. It
is important to note that a more comprehensive study is required to verify that the simplifications
employed in the model accurately reflect the performances of the complete system.
Then, it was assumed that the offshore converter has full knowledge of the wind farm’s power pro-
duction, especially when the wind farms operate in grid-forming mode and the offshore converter
operates in grid-following mode. This aspect will be further discussed in Section 3.2. In relation
to this, it was assumed that the different grid-forming wind farms can be interconnected on the AC
side, disregarding the synchronization steps.
Additionally, the converter controls have been designed to handle the considered faults. However,
certain transients, reconnections, re-energization processes, and other similar events have not been
included in the study as they fall outside the scope of the research. Nevertheless, it is assumed that
these aspects are functioning properly.
Furthermore, this report specifically focuses on three types of faults: onshore AC faults, offshore
DC faults, and offshore AC faults on the collector grid, all occurring under full-power scenario.

1.3
Research goals
1.3.1
Main goal
The objective of the study is to determine the optimal strategies for dissipating energy during fault-
ride-through events in a multi-terminal high voltage DC (MT-HVDC) system. The specific focus
of the study is on the North Sea DC grid, which connects Denmark, Belgium, and the DNSEI.
The research aims to identify the most efficient and effective methods to manage and dissipate
energy during fault occurrences in this interconnected system. By investigating fault-ride-through
strategies, the study seeks to enhance the overall performance and resilience of the MT-HVDC
system in the North Sea region.

1.4
Problem description
In the context of the MTDC network, the need for an energy dissipation strategy arises from the
limitations of offshore wind turbine generators (OWTG). These generators are unable to rapidly
reduce their power output during a fault, and their down-regulation capability is limited to a rate
of around 0.1 to 0.25 pu/s. Consequently, there is an accumulation of excess energy during the
ramp-down period that must be dissipated effectively. In cases of temporary faults with durations
of approximately 2 seconds or less, such as onshore AC faults, the OWTG do not need to down-
regulate their power output. Instead, the focus is on dissipating the excess energy during the fault
ride-through (FRT) period.
Many power dissipation strategies have been conducted for pole-to-pole connections ([36], [23],
[32], ...). But the recent fast increase of MTDC offshore grids calls for further studies on power
dissipation strategies to optimize their performances. In the well-known reference book "HVDC
GRIDS, For Offshore and Supergrid of the Future" by Dirk Van Hertem, Oriol Gomis-Bellmunt,
and Jun Liang [36], it is mentioned without further comments that "In a large DC grid such as a
supergrid, the rating and the distribution of the DC choppers is not trivial." This statement summa-
rizes the limited research conducted on this topic. With the large power considered, determining
the optimal rating and position of the choppers must take into account the interconnection, provid-
ing another potential approach to power evacuation and cost reduction.
After that, one could solve the dissipation problem by dissipating the energy in the WT DC link
choppers, already implemented in the the turbines, and trigger them using communication means.
But taking into account the fault detection time and the sending and receiving time of the signal
especially for the long distance covered by the Triton link, the order of magnitude of the signal
transmission becomes too large for the temporary defects considered.
Then, Energinet, the Danish transmission system operator (TSO), has made the decision to con-
struct the North Sea grid using a partially-selective protection system. This means that DC circuit
breakers (DCCBs) will only be installed on the DC bus coupler between the two offshore HVDC
bi-pole converters, on the DNSEI. Figure 1.4.1 depicts this for a schematic 4 terminals grid. CB
are represented by the dark squares on the DC bus coupler lines and on the AC connections.
This approach divides the MT-HVDC system into two protection zones. It is assumed that the
selection of DCCB technology, the sizing of current-limiting inductors, and the overcurrent rating
of the converters are coordinated to ensure that any faults occurring in one protection zone allow
for the coordinated operation of all converters within the unaffected protection zone. It is worth
mentioning that achieving such efficiency may require the use of extremely rapid DCCBs, with
opening duration of 2-3 milliseconds, combined with inductors of several Henrys [4]. The detailed
operation of these devices is beyond the scope of this study, and interested readers are encouraged
to refer to [4] for more information.

Background
This chapter covers the different theoretical aspects, in a global way, used in the rest of this report.
First, the concept of grid code and Fault Ride Through are developed. Then the chapter conducts a
theoretical analysis of the different possibilities to dissipate energy, i.e. AC and DC choppers, and
develops on their sizing. And finally, the operation and control of AC/DC converters are studied.
The latter are indeed an integral part of the study of energy dissipation in the MT-HVDC network.
2.1
Low Voltage Fault Ride Through
Electrical networks are susceptible to experiencing faults. The Danish grid, as indicated by the
Nordic and Baltic Grid Disturbance Statistics [34], encountered an average of 7.3 faults per year
with consequences for end-users between 2012 and 2021. However, faults can also occur in the
DC network, affecting the DC lines or converters.
In many cases, faults are temporary and cleared within a few milliseconds. During this critical
period, it is crucial to maintain the network’s operation and prevent a loss of voltage across the
entire system. The installed equipment must remain connected under specified conditions, which
is known as Low Voltage Fault Ride Through (LVFRT).
The grid code, initially introduced in Denmark in 1978, mandated that power plants, and later wind
plants in the late 1990s, must be capable of withstanding deep voltage transients. This requirement
was implemented due to the substantial growth of wind energy and the increasing contribution of
wind power to the Danish energy mix [28] [3].
The grid code specifies that power plants, including wind turbines, must provide low voltage ride
through. This requirement ensures that the power plants continue to support the network during
fault conditions, which are often temporary in nature.
Implementing Fault Ride Through capability is crucial because without it, wind turbines would
be disconnected from the grid during faults, leading to lengthy resynchronization processes and
potentially causing significant transients in the grid [22].

During a fault, the onshore converter’s power flow is limited due to its current limit and the voltage
drop on the AC side. However, the offshore wind farms continue to maintain a constant power
output. As a result, the excess energy cannot be transferred to the network and must either be
consumed locally or redirected within a MT-HVDC network until the fault is cleared.
This temporary mismatch between production and consumption leads to an accumulation of power
in the DC connection, resulting in an increase in DC voltage. This can create issues with power
electronic elements such as capacitors, converters, cables, etc. To address this, various solutions
have been proposed. Some literature suggests reducing the individual power output of the wind
turbines [37][5], while others recommend the use of choppers. These power dissipation methods
enable the wind turbines to remain connected to the grid, as required by the grid code.
For minor voltage drops on the grid, where the grid voltage remains above 0.8 per unit (pu) [38],
the power reduction can be managed through generator control. However, for more significant
faults, the power reduction achieved through generator control alone may not be fast enough to
ensure a safe transition. In such cases, choppers, both AC and/or DC, need to be activated.
The main focus of this report is the technical and feasible solutions optimization of power dissipa-
tion methods during faults in order to ensure the stability and reliability of the grid.

2.2
Power dissipation strategies
This section specifically addresses power dissipation strategies downstream of the wind turbine’s
generator during faults. There are three potential power dissipation strategies: using the DC link
chopper of the back-to-back converter inside the WT, using an AC chopper located at the offshore
AC collector grid, and DC choppers located at the onshore HVDC converter. It is important to note
that for FRT, the power output from the wind turbine’s generator remains constant.
A chopper is an effective method for dissipating energy for a short period by dissipating energy
in a resistor. Choppers can be categorized into two types: DC choppers and AC choppers. While
the operation of these technologies differs, the underlying principle remains the same, which is
to circulate current through a resistance to dissipate energy. In both cases, the goal is to safely
manage the excess power during a fault.
Proper sizing of the choppers is crucial. If the chopper is too small, it may fail and cause damage to
the entire system. On the other hand, an oversized chopper is expensive and requires a larger area,
which can be particularly costly for offshore stations. Therefore, careful consideration must be
given to selecting the appropriate size of choppers to ensure efficient and reliable operation while
optimizing costs.
In the following sections, the operation and characteristics of both DC choppers and AC choppers
will be discussed in detail, providing insights into their respective advantages, limitations, and
considerations for sizing.
2.2.1
AC choppers
Figure 2.2.1 illustrates the schematic of an AC chopper unit where each branch comprises anti-
parallel thyristors and a resistor in series to dissipate the power. The AC chopper is then connected
via a step-down transformer to the high voltage AC offshore network. It is arranged in a delta
configuration to prevent triplen (3n) harmonics from appearing on the AC grid. Afterwards, in
both half-cycles, the Firing Angle Controller (FAC) regulates current (and hence power dissipated)
in the AC chopper resistor for thyristor valve control [32].

The limitations of currently available thyristor devices in the semiconductor industry can make it
difficult to design and operate an AC chopper at the required power level for a wind farm. To
address this, multiple AC chopper units with smaller ratings can be connected in parallel to handle
device current within its rated capacity (usually 4 units [29]). Additionally, each chopper unit or
group is connected to the Point of Common Coupling (PCC) through a step-down transformer to
avoid having too many series-connected thyristor devices in the AC chopper valve. This approach
can result in an optimal design based on performance requirements, equipment cost, and device
ratings [32].
For optimal performances, the AC chopper’s total power absorption is managed by controlling the
number of activated units and fine-tuning with the firing angle of a specific unit [32]. In this report,
it assumed that the power dissipated by the AC chopper is linear and does not have any steps.
2.2.2
DC choppers
While wind turbines are typically equipped with a DC chopper in their DC-DC converter to handle
system failures, the HVDC system itself must also be capable of dissipating the entire power output
of the wind farm for a brief period.
The operation of the DC chopper is based on a simple principle. It involves triggering the switch
"T" using a Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) signal, referred to as "Tc". When the switch, which
actually consists of a number of series connected IGBTs, is turned ON, the voltage across the
DC capacitor is discharged through resistor Rc, facilitating a decrease in voltage. The rate of
discharge and the current flow are determined by the value of resistor Rc. Typically, DC choppers
are installed onshore to minimize the additional expenses associated with the offshore platform
[36].
In an HVDC grid, two HVDC choppers are typically used. The first chopper is placed between the

2.3
Converters
Converters play a crucial role in high-power electronics by facilitating the conversion of electrical
energy between different forms. Two important types of converters in this field are rectifiers and
inverters.
Rectifiers are used to convert AC into DC. In contrast, inverters perform the opposite conversion,
converting DC into AC. Depending on the specific application, different types of converters can be
employed. Lower-cost and less controllable converters may utilize simple diodes, while more ad-
vanced and higher-performance converters incorporate insulated-gate bipolar transistors (IGBTs)
with anti-parallel diodes [35].
In order to establish a complete DC connection between two AC grids, both rectifiers and inverters
are employed, with one on each side.
2.3.1
Converter types
In HVDC systems, different converter topologies are used, including half-bridge Modular Multi-
level Converters (MMC), full-bridge MMC, and two-level converters. Half-bridge MMCs utilize
a series of half-bridge cells connected in series, where each cell consists of a capacitor and two
switches. During normal operation, one of the switches is conducting while the other is non-
conducting. In contrast, full-bridge MMCs employ a full-bridge configuration, with each arm of
the bridge containing multiple cells. This allows bidirectional power flow and provides increased
fault tolerance compared to half-bridge MMCs. On the other hand, two-level converters, such as

2.3.2
Converter control
By adjusting the Pulse Width Modulation (PWM) signals sent to the various IGBTs, converters
offer a degree of flexibility in their control. Typically, control is implemented on the d and q axes
using a Phase-Locked Loop (PLL), which provides the reference angle on the AC side. These
control components enable the manipulation of two independent variables on the AC and/or DC
side. In this project, the following control strategies are employed [3]:
• VAC −f also known as grid forming. It regulates the voltage on the AC side, both the
magnitude and the frequency.
• VDC −Q changes the d-axis current to control the DC voltage and the q-axis current to
modify the reactive power flow on the AC side
• P −Q control governs the reactive and active power by manipulating the q and d-axis cur-
rents.
Numerous other control modes exist for different applications. Examples of alternative combina-
tions include VDC −VAC, VDC −f, and more.

2.3.3
Converter blocking mode
The so-called "blocking mode", or "diode rectifier mode," is primarily used for safety reasons to
protect the IGBTs from excessive current or voltage stresses. In such cases, the control system can
deactivate the IGBTs and operate the rectifier circuit in a diode-like mode. This means that the
IGBTs are effectively turned off and do not actively control the current flow. Instead, they behave
as simple diodes, allowing the rectifier to continue functioning with limited functionality.
For half bridge MMCs or two level converters, due to the direction of the diodes, no further current
can flow from the DC to the AC side. This can be better understood by examining the internal
structure of an IGBT rectifier, as shown in Figure 2.3.2 below. If the voltage on the AC side drops
to zero and the IGBTs are switched off, the upper anti-parallel diodes will block the current and
isolate the AC fault. This is mainly useful for offshore AC fault, to avoid having large current from
the DC side feeding the fault.
However, HB-MMC and two-levels converters lack the ability to prevent the AC grid from con-
tributing to fault currents on the DC side. To address this limitation, circuit breakers (CBs) are
necessary to protect HB-MMCs. DC CBs are often used for this purpose, but they can be expen-
sive. Alternatively, AC CBs can be used, but their longer response times may not be ideal for
ensuring effective protection in HVDC systems [21]. Figure 2.3.2 below explains the path taken
by the current during a DC fault, and highlights the converter’s inability to avoid the fault current.
[15]. The addition of two extra switches in the sub-modules to the HB-MMCs make it possible to

2.4
DC protection system
To ensure safe operations on the DC network, several DC protection strategies are currently used
and categorized according to three protection philosophies [2]. These are supposed to ensure safe
operations when a DC fault occurs on the grid.
• Non-selective protection (N-S) with only converter’s AC circuit breakers
• partially-selective protection (P-S) equipped with DC circuit breakers at the interconnecting
line (Shown on Figure 1.4.1)
• fully-selective protection (F-S) with DC circuit breakers at all offshore DC line ends
The choice of the protection system is indirectly influenced by the type of converters used in
the network. FBMMC converters were designed for applications without DC circuit breakers, or
non-selective operation, as they have the capability to block the current flowing into a DC fault.
However, for multi-terminal HVDC systems, this approach is not very practical. In the event
of a DC line fault, it would be necessary to block all the converters and then clear the fault by
disconnecting the faulty line.
With a full-selectivity approach, no converters need to be blocked. Instead, the faulted line can sim-
ply be tripped, allowing all the converters to continue supplying power to the remaining sections
of the DC grid. This ensures uninterrupted operation of the system.

3.2
Control schemes
3.2.1
Control strategies
When incorporating DC connections into a model, the converters effectively segregate the AC
network into independent AC networks in terms of frequency and reference phase angle. In the case
of isolated AC networks, such as offshore collector grids, a grid-forming converter is essential for
maintaining stable grid conditions. Without it, the network lacks the ability to regulate frequency
and voltage, leading to potential fluctuations. The grid-forming converter acts as a reference,
providing control signals to synchronize and stabilize the AC system, ensuring reliable operation.
Similarly, in isolated DC link systems, effective DC voltage control is crucial for reliable opera-
tion. Fluctuations or deviations in the DC voltage can result in inefficient power transfer, increased
losses, and potential system instability. To mitigate these issues, it is necessary to dedicate one
of the converters to specifically control the DC voltage. This dedicated converter continuously
monitors and adjusts its output to maintain the desired voltage level, enabling stable and efficient
power transmission throughout the DC link.
In the studied grid, which includes one isolated AC and two DC connections, it becomes crucial to
have one of the converters, either from the wind farms or the offshore converter, implemented as
a grid-forming converter to maintain AC voltage magnitude and frequency control in the offshore
AC network. Furthermore, in the DC link connections, one of the converters in each connection

must be implemented as a Vdc-Q converter to ensure proper DC voltage regulation and balance
within the system.
Accordingly, two models have been created, featuring similar connections but different control
schemes. Figure 3.2.1 below illustrates a simplified model of the HVDC connection, from the
wind turbine generator to the national grid, while Table 3.2.1 outlines the two distinct control
strategies considered. Three-phases connections are drawn as one line representation, while the

The primary difference between the two models lies in the converter responsible for regulating
both the magnitude and frequency of the AC voltage on the offshore collector grid. In the first con-
trol strategy, the offshore converter adjusts its power transfer to maintain the AC voltage within the
desired range for both magnitude and frequency. In contrast, the second control strategy delegates
this responsibility to the wind farms themselves. To ensure better clarity, we will refer to the first
control strategy as the Offshore Converter Grid Forming (OC-GF) strategy and the second as the
Wind Farms Grid Forming (WF-GF) strategy throughout the remainder of this study.
The WF-GF strategy was implemented to investigate the system’s behavior when reducing the
power transmitted from the offshore converter. This approach serves as a means to activate the WT
DC link choppers without requiring communications between the offshore stations and the WTs.
In contrast, the OC-GF strategy, which employs grid-forming offshore converters, does not have
the capability to accomplish this.
However, the WF-GF strategy encounters another problem. Since the power of the aerodynamic
part can be considered constant over short time intervals, it is almost impossible for the generator
side converter to control the voltage on the DC link. The voltage control is therefore done indirectly
by the amount of energy that the offshore converter transfers. As stated in the assumptions, it is
assumed that it knows exactly the power produced by the generators.
One option to ensure this could be to implement a droop control in the WT grid-side converters,
which establishes a relationship between the DC voltage of the wind turbines and the AC voltage
of the grid collector. With droop control, the offshore converter can adjust its power setpoint based
on this AC voltage. Droop control is a technique commonly used in power systems to regulate
the power sharing between different sources or inverters. It consists of adjusting the reference of
a controlled variable, here the AC voltage magnitude reference, according to another variable, the
DC voltage [3]. The inclusion of droop control in this context is not covered in detail in this report
and requires further investigation in future studies.

AC chopper

3.4
Simulation and result analysis
RMS simulation in Power Factory refers to the simulation of electrical power systems using the
RMS (Root Mean Square) method. The RMS simulation technique takes into account the steady-
state behavior of the system, considering factors such as voltage magnitudes, phase angles, and
current flows. It uses a time-domain approach, where the simulation models the behavior of power
system components over a specific time period, typically in small time steps (0.0001s). By ana-
lyzing the RMS values of electrical quantities, such as voltages and currents, the simulation can
accurately represent the dynamic behavior and stability of the power system under normal and
abnormal operating conditions.
Further improvements should confirm the results using EMT simulations. The latest takes into
account the electromagnetic nature of the system, modeling the high-frequency components, elec-
tromagnetic interactions, and device-specific characteristics. This level of detail allows for a more
precise representation of system dynamics, including voltage and current waveforms, voltage sags
and swells, electromagnetic transients, and device switching transients. While EMT simulation
requires more computational resources and longer simulation times compared to RMS simula-
tion, its higher accuracy makes it essential for in-depth analysis and optimization of power system
performance [14].
The results from the RMS simulations were then uploaded to the MATLAB program in the form of
time series. This program allows a better data handling and the creation of more precise and clear
graphs. As an example, the instantaneous energy dissipation data of the choppers were filtered
using a low pass filter, in order to obtain a more readable graph of the average power dissipated
over time.

Basic scenario
In case of an offshore fault upstream of the AC choppers, neither the AC choppers nor the HVDC

current from the DC side to the collector network. This faulty current will damage the converter
components.

diode rectifier. No more current can flow from the DC to the AC side, and the fault is isolated until

4.4
Summary
Chapter 4 commenced by examining the behavior of the network during an onshore AC fault,
specifically analyzing the DC voltage magnitude and chopper size for three dissipation possibil-
ities. Optimal dimensions were determined through simulations with varying chopper sizes, and
these optimal solutions will be utilized for subsequent model comparisons throughout the report.
Additionally, it was emphasized that a multi-terminal network allows for the distribution of dissipa-
tion capacities across multiple chopper units, reducing individual sizes compared to point-to-point
networks.
Subsequently, the section delved into the network’s response to an offshore AC fault. During such
an event, the offshore converter connected to the faulty wind farm enters a blocking mode, pre-
venting the DC grid current from feeding the AC fault and potentially damaging the converters.
The control strategy employing wind farms to control the offshore grid exhibited superior perfor-
mances by maintaining control even when the offshore converter is blocked.
Lastly, the study explored an offshore DC fault, highlighting two scenarios based on existing lit-
erature [10]: a coupled or decoupled offshore AC network. The results elucidated the HVDC
system’s reaction to the fault and underscored the utilization of WT DC link choppers in coupled
AC structure, as a means of energy dissipation while the wind turbine gradually reduces its power
to achieve a new steady state.

Chapter 5
Discussion
The "Discussion" chapter critically examines the previously presented results. For each fault, a
comprehensive comparison is conducted, primarily focusing on the size of the choppers and the
network’s response. This comparative analysis facilitates the drawing of conclusions regarding the
optimal utilization of energy dissipation strategies.
Furthermore, the chapter ends by proposing an optimal plan based on the findings and suggest-
ing the implementation of a new grid code. The proposed grid code aims to enhance economic
efficiency by leveraging the insights gained from the study.
5.1
AC Onshore Fault
As a recall, when a fault occurs on the onshore grid, the power unbalance leads to an increase of the
voltage on the HVDC grid. It is therefore important to dissipate the power during the fault period.
Four possibilities are studied here: dissipation in onshore DC choppers (HVDC), in offshore AC
choppers with either the offshore converter in grid forming (OC-GF) or grid following (WF-GF),
and in wind turbine DC choppers (WT). The comparison is based on three factor: the HVDC
voltage increase, the chopper power capability and the chopper energy capacity.
5.1.1
HVDC Voltage
A first aspect is the HVDC voltage magnitude. The latest shall remain in a range, defined arbitrary
to 1.05pu. This value is a key parameter in the chopper control. It represents the voltage at which
the duty cycle is maximal and the chopper dissipates continuously. Chosen here to be 1.05, this
value can be changed easily for others requirements. One shall however keep in mind the voltage
difference between the busses, due to the losses in the DC lines.

5.2
DC Offshore fault
In this section, the focus will put on the differences between an AC coupled or not on the offshore
substation.
The main advantage, explained in [10], appears in lower power conditions, not simulated in this
study. A full utilisation of the healthy converter is possible with a coupled AC, while the loss of
a converter in a decoupled AC configuration leads automatically to a loss of 25% (one unit out
of four connected) of the pre-fault power. Moreover, when using a coupled AC, the wind farms
remain connected to the converters. This could potentially make the re-energisation process easier
than in the other configuration. And finally, simulations have shown that the power curtailment to
50% could in this case be handled by the DC link chopper, meaning no extra expenses.
Coupled AC offshore grid therefore appears as the best option.
5.3
Technologies comparison
Based on the previous comparisons, offshore AC choppers appear to have the less relevant perfor-
mances. This for different reasons developed here.
Firstly, these are located on the offshore platform. Although the study does not address the eco-
nomic point, it seems evident that the installation of such equipment requires a very expensive
infrastructure and space on an offshore platform [36]. But also, they remain depend on the rate of
change of the converter power setpoint when considering the time before activation. This leads to
a non-significant increase of the voltage on the DC side that could potentially damage the equip-
ments. And finally, they do not contribute to offshore AC and DC faults. Although the use of AC
chopper for power dissipation in DC fault with AC coupled was not investigate (this is done in
[17]), DC link WT choppers show really good performances on this point.
On the other side, the wind turbines have to be equipped with choppers in their DC link to with-
stand an offshore AC fault. This remains the only option, as the WTs can not ramp down fast
enough. Although the activation time of the DC link WT choppers leads to the same peak as the
AC technology, they can be considered as "free" in this case. Moreover, as required by the grid
code, they must have chopper with an energy capacity of 2puRPs, whereas, as shown in the previ-
ous graphs, this limit is only reached for AC offshore faults. Indeed, during onshore AC fault, only
0.25puRPs is dissipated and 1puRPs for DC fault with current ramping down rate (0.1puP/s).
Finally, power dissipation in the wind turbines does not lead to extra power flow in the DC cables.
No oversizing is therefore required in this case.

5.6
Summary
In conclusion, this chapter systematically examined the various faults individually and explored
different options for each case. The study revealed that a combination of HVDC choppers and
WT DC link choppers yields the most favorable performance during an onshore AC fault. The
presence of HVDC choppers helps mitigate the initial surge in HVDC voltage, while the choppers
in the turbines effectively dissipate the remaining energy. Importantly, the use of these choppers
does not represent any extra cost, as their presence is mandatory to ride through offshore AC fault
on the collector grid.
Moreover, in the event of an offshore DC fault, a topology employing a coupled AC network
demonstrated greater optimization, particularly when the turbines are operating below their rated
power. By redistributing power to the healthy converters, a higher power transfer capacity can be
maintained.
Furthermore, the optimal model selected was subjected to simulations during an onshore AC fault
to validate the employed theoretical approach. The results affirmed the model’s optimal perfor-
mance and allowed for a reduction in the size of the HVDC choppers. Power and energy balances
have been carried out on the simulations results to ensure the validity of the model used.
Finally, given that an offshore AC fault imposes limitations on the chopper sizes within the tur-
bines, a suggestion is made for a new grid code specific to offshore AC networks. This proposed
code, with more relaxed requirements, enables a reduction in the size of the turbines’ choppers,
albeit at the cost of more frequent disconnections during temporary faults.

6.1
Conclusions
This report has investigated different energy dissipation strategies for multi-terminal HVDC sys-
tems. This was achieved by simulations on a 4GW HVDC-MT network model, built on the DIgSI-
LENT Power Factory program. Although simplified, the model bases its values on the network
supposed to connect Belgium, Denmark and the DNSEI by 2032. The parameters and values of
the model can however be modified if the reader wishes to adapt it to another network. The net-
work is a bipole, connected in the North Sea with the Danish energy island, producing 4GW of
wind power. This value is expected to grow considerably in the coming years and with this, the
challenge represented by the dissipation of energy is likely to become even more complex and
important.
In this context, three main energy dissipation technologies have been analysed: onshore HVDC
choppers, offshore AC choppers and WT DC link choppers. And this, on three quite common
types of faults, namely AC onshore, DC offshore and AC offshore, on the collector grid. The three
energy dissipation possibilities were individually subject to simulations, including the mentioned
defects. Based on the results, the technologies could be compared, mainly on the voltage increase
on the different networks and on the required choppers capacity.
Based on these results, it appears that the combination of the use of choppers included in the
turbines and HVDC choppers shows the best performances. Indeed, since the turbines must be
equipped with choppers in order to ride through offshore fault, their presence is mandatory. There-
fore, the installation of AC choppers becomes redundant, as their performances are similar to that
of the turbine choppers. Finally, due to the activation time of the choppers included in the turbines,

As far as DC faults are concerned, an AC coupled presents the best results. Once the fault is

by their ability to ride through an offshore fault. Based on this, a new grid code for offshore net-
works has been suggested. The latter reduces the capacity of the choppers by half, but increases
the risk of disconnection of the turbines. Further statistical analysis on offshore faults should be
conducted to confirm this approach.

of the choppers on the HVDC network is 150MJ. The final distribution of power dissipation be-
tween the turbines and the HVDC choppers can be fine-tuned, by a more complete and economic
analysis of the problem.
6.2
Recommendations for further work
The model made in power factory can be improved to make it more realistic. In particular the
modeling of the turbines and the control of Vdc within the DC link connection. This could also be
controlled by the grid side converter, with a droop control VDC →VAC. In addition, it is necessary
to ensure the reaction of the wind farm to the increase in its AC network. Modeled here as a single
aggregated turbine, the dissipation of energy within the turbines must be well synchronized in or-
der to obtain an equitable distribution of energy between the different choppers.
After that, the connection to the Belgian Island and other countries around the north sea should be
included in the model. The study showed here the relevance of the use of WTs chopper in energy
dissipation. Theoretically, an identical conclusion will be drawn for a larger and more powerful
network, but this remains to be demonstrated.
Finally, it would be interesting to establish a study on the installation of optical fiber or other means
of communication with the turbines. This installation seems relatively inexpensive compared to a
wind farm project. This would trigger the choppers in the turbines more quickly, without going
