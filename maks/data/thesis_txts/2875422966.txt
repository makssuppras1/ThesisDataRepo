Abstract
This thesis investigates the potential of having Visual-Language Models (VLMs) serve
as foundation for general-purpose agents capable of complex, sequential interaction
with digital interfaces. VLM-based agents have historically been struggling with the ac-
tion gap - not being able to successfully convert high-level semantic understanding into
low-level planning (and execution) of correct action sequences.
Although more of a trend than a true class of models, the recent emergence of Large
Action Models (LAMs) presents a promising direction for conquering both levels of rea-
soning. These models focus on more direct, lighter pipelines that includes as much of
the low-level action interpretation responsibilities in the learnable model where the
reasoning and planning is happening (compared to in a external API).
We explore CAPTCHA-solving as a benchmark for the development of LAMs. CAPTCHAs,
designed as human verification mechanisms exploiting AI hard problems, offer a rich
spectrum of tasks that require both high-level semantic understanding and low-level
interaction capabilities. Instead of focusing solely on bypassing CAPTCHA defenses, this
work uses them to probe the limits of current VLM systems in a more general sense,
particularly in their ability to reason, plan, and act coherently in visually grounded en-
vironments.
Unfortunately, there is a lack of viable open source CAPTCHA data.
This is natural
given that neither commercial CAPTCHA developers nor independent researchers wish
to share any sort of information that could aid the wrong people in bypassing their sys-
tems. A major contribution of the thesis is the development of a CAPTCHA-centric data
framework that allow generation of high- and low-level labels for offline supervised
learning as well as environment for interaction in online reinforcement learning.
First experiments are conducted to determine the degree of high-level understand-
ing that can be expected from state-of-the-art (SOTA) pre-trained VLMs (Molmo and
Qwen2.5VL) with different decoding strategies (soft guidance and forced decoding). Next,
a untrained LAM baseline is developed using the same VLMs as backbone models to
examine VLM ability to convert high-level planning into sequential low-level planning
of actions using dynamic visual information about the CAPTCHA during the encounter
with the agent. At last, we propose a Large Action Model (LAM) composed of a rea-
soning module for high-level task planning (VLM) and an action adapter (transformer)
for translating plans into executable interface actions (either from a single image or
a dynamic environment using frame memory). The model can be perceived as a type
of encoder-decoder transformer model. Attempts are made to train the model using
both behavior cloning (BC) and proximal policy optimization (PPO) with additional ex-
periments into techniques such as curriculum learning and bucket sampling to improve
training efficiency.
We find that while SOTA VLM have enough high-level understanding to identify the over-
all idea of how any of our fourteen custom CAPTCHA designs should be solved, they still
struggle when it comes to describing exact high-level steps (e.g. which images to click or
which letters to write) for some designs. These designs are typically either semantically
hard (e.g. a segmentation task where high-level resolution is needed to discern objects)
or require many varied action steps. When used for dynamic prediction of low-level ac-

Terminology overview
These are some important custom terms that will be introduced and later discussed
multiple times throughout the thesis. The following small overview of custom defini-
tions can be used as a quick reference.
Action CAPTCHA (A-CAP)
CAPTCHA similar to those found in the wild and provided commercially (sometimes out-
right clones).
Understanding CAPTCHA (U-CAP)
A dumbed-down version of A-CAPs that require less high-level semantic understanding
than many of the commercial counter parts using the same type of design or interaction
mechanisms.
CAPTCHA solver
A program consisting of a pipeline that takes sensory input from the browser (in the form
of text and/or images) sends them through a AI that predicts some level of solution which
can be interpreted by an (custom) API that executes the low-level solution the AI output
represents.
Universal CAPTCHA solver
A CAPTCHA solver capable of handling both seen and unseen CAPTCHA formats by pri-
marily relying solely on perceptual input (e.g., images) and general reasoning, without
requiring domain-specific rules or APIs.
High-level CAPTCHA solution
Some representation of a correct final puzzle state for the CAPTCHA in question.
Action trajectory
A sequence of actions commands that can be directly mapped to one or more browser
API function calls.
Low-level CAPTCHA solution
A trajectory that executed in the graphical user interface leads to a correct final puzzle
state for the CAPTCHA in question without being detected as a bot.
Optimal trajectory
The low-level CAPTCHA solution trajectory that represents the fewest hardware-level in-
teractions (physical mouse movements, key presses, etc. for a human).

Introduction
The report will examine the subject of general CAPTCHA solvers with Visual Language
Models (VLMs).
1.1
Motivation
To motivate the exploration of the subject, we must first discuss the current and his-
torical role of CAPTCHA as cybersecurity defense mechanism in relation to automated
CAPTCHA solvers. By this, the value of exploring VLMs for building a ‘universal CAPTCHA
solver’ will become evident. We will show that the solver is a case study well suited
for exploring more general frameworks for letting general artificial intelligence interact
with the physical or digital world.
1.1.1
Bad bots, bad bots - what you gonna do?
To counter the threat of bad bots, websites rely on CAPTCHAs as the primary defense
mechanism [1]. The oxford dictionary [2] defines a bot as an autonomous program on
a network (e.g. the internet) that can interact with systems or users. Simultaneously, a
‘bad bot’ is a popular term used to refer to bots that are programmed to perform their
tasks with malicious intent [3]. Examples include but are not limited to: (1) illegal web
scrapping, (2) distributed denial-of-service attacks, (3) covert manipulation of public
opinion on social media and (4) credential stuffing. According to [3], the traffic con-
ducted by bad bots, as well as bots in general, is increasing rapidly compared to traffic
conducted by humans. In 2023, a third if all internet traffic was estimated to be con-
ducted by bad bots. Consequently, defense mechanisms against bots are as important
as ever. Trends in bot and specifically bad bot traffic are illustrated in fig. 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Trends in bot traffic from 2013-2023 (from [3])

hard AI problems first introduced by [4][5] back in year 2000. An AI hard problem is a

1.1.3
A game of AI and mouse...
CAPTCHA design has always been a game of cat and mouse with the field of AI (especially
ML) being the cat. It has arguably even been more so in the last one and a half decade
where the complexity of AI hard problems has increased even faster due to the rise
of deep learning. Present day, one cannot blame CAPTCHA providers if they struggle
to design new CAPTCHA that lie in the sweet spot between low human cognitive load
requirements and high AI difficulty. It is important to note that CAPTCHA providers have
over the last 10 years mainly tried to deal with the rise of the complexity in AI hard
problems through integration of behavior analysis into ‘existing’ CAPTCHA puzzles. The
behavioral analytics side of CAPTCHA will not be a central part of this report for the
sake of limiting scope. This will be discussed further throughout the report. A rough
overview of the typical modern CAPTCHA verification protocol (incl. behavioral analysis)

1.1.4
Universal CAPTCHA solvers based on VLMs
To motivate the role of VLMs in the context of CAPTCHA solvers, we start by wondering
what it might require to build a CAPTCHA solver that would be able to generate solu-
tions (action sequences) for both in- and out-of-distribution CAPTCHA designs. Such a
solver is what we have earlier alluded to using the nomenclature ‘universal CAPTCHA
solver´. A universal solver would have to be powered by a AI engine with extraordinary
capabilities in complex reasoning for general sequential problem solving. In the text
domain, this level of expressiveness was achieved with the introduction of large lan-
guage models (LLMs) [6].
In recent years, progress have been made to extend these sequential capabilities to
the image domain with VLMs [7][8]. A universal CAPTCHA solver would optimally rely
primarily on visual input. Modern CAPTCHA designs can include adversarial techniques
to obfuscate the content of images embedded into the design. However, it typically
isn’t implemented to have an effect on the UI components. The text representation of
the CAPTCHA in the document object model (DOM) of the browser could give additional
information about the location of interactive objects in the CAPTCHA. However, to limit
the scope of the project the text representation in DOM is not considered. This will
allow us to build our own CAPTCHA without the same level of security concerns for ad-
hering to the standard CAPTCHA protocol and in web scraping we do not have to deal

with the use of cross-origin restrictions that the providers use to deter people from
inspecting the CAPTCHA DOM. The VLM is a natural place to start exploring given its
previously discussed capabilities. Note, that doing this, we also adopt the assumption
that the AI model in our solver program pipeline could potentially be able to deal with
unseen CAPTCHA. However, we still need to be able to execute the solution to any type
of CAPTCHA design in the browser (non-specialized API).
The amount of literature available on the subject of a universal CAPTCHA solver is ex-
tremely limited (almost non-existent). The only example we were able to find in the
context of CAPTCHA solvers was the recent program Oedipus by [9]. However, arguments
can be made against the future potential of the resource heavy framework in more de-
manding digital tasks (not only CAPTCHA). Ignoring the inference cost, the model per-
formance also show plenty room of improvement indicating that the so called ‘action
gap’ is yet far from being closed in the CAPTCHA domain. The action gap refer to the
observed tendency of LLM/VLM-based agentic programs to fail translating correct high
level problem solutions (e.g. ”click the square, then the triangle”) into executable ac-
tions (low level solutions).
1.1.5
Closing the action gap with VLAMs
The unification of task understanding and action execution have always been acknowl-
edged to be a very difficult challenge in computer science. This was already observed
by Hans Moravec back in 1988, where he formulated what was to become known as
Moravec’s paradox [10].
”It is comparatively easy to make computers exhibit adult level
performance on intelligence tests or playing checkers, and difficult or
impossible to give them the skills of a one-year-old when it comes to
perception and mobility.”
– Hans Moravec
The paradox loosely states that contrary to traditional assumptions, reasoning requires
very little computation, but sensorimotor and perception skills require enormous com-
putational resources. Now that the reasoning capabilities of AI are rapidly growing,
the action execution phase is rapidly becoming the remaining bottle neck in the jour-
ney toward artificial general intelligence (AGI) [11]. Unsurprising, the subject is as such
currently heavily researched in both robotics planning and automated GUI (digital) nav-
igation.
Vision language action models (VLAMs) are a group of VLMs that aim to close the action
gap. Another increasingly common moniker used to describe these models is large ac-
tion models (LAMs). We will use the term LAM as a umbrella term for VLAM that also
includes purely text based models. There exist no strict definition of these models,
which makes it hard to discuss them in a more general sense. That being said, they do
represent a broader movement in agentic LLM/VLM research that aim to make agentic
models both more efficient and effective by making a simpler and more direct pipeline
for translating understanding into action with as little responsibility as possible on the
API to decode intention.
In LAMs there seem to be a trend of more low-level modeling of interactions through
tool calls to action functions (although this is not true in all cases) in a common browser
API (such as seen in [12][13][14]). The lowest level of modeling interactions is in this con-

text user level where a dragging action consists of calls to atomic actions functions for
mouse key down, mouse movement and mouse key up. One of the arguments for this
approach is that the low-level action representation at the API (minimal API interpre-
tation) forces the model to understand actions more in depth as it needs to internalize
more of the pipeline from sensory input to actual execution of the generated plan in
the environment. Another argument is that low-level interactions provides increased
flexibility for the model to naturally fit any training environment without the require-
ment of a new specialized system.
In addition to this, the trend is that LAMs are often designed with less overhead in terms
of space and time complexity than previous attempts to agentic AI with LLM/VLM core
has been [15]. This allows them to be scaled down and used for specialized domains in-
stead of having a single behemoth architecture of different RAG modules for tool calling
and memory that need to be able to handle all domains (e.g. single application vs. full
cross-application OS navigation). All this contribute to a more efficient framework that
can combat the latter of current state-of-the-art (SOTA) digital agent programs (such as
[16]): (1) Inability to utilize the full understanding prowess of the LLM-like core due to
the action gap and (2) limited practical usability due to slow inference.
The ‘universal CAPTCHA solver’ is a perfect challenge for exploring agentic AI with VLAMs.
The project have potential to both raise awareness of the current threat of AI in bot de-
terrence and highlight the need for further research in areas liked VLAMs to progress
towards achieving AGI.
1.1.6
Data Acquisition Challenges
To train and validate our models a good quality dataset with a good variation of CAPTCHA
designs is required. Access to online data (interactive environment) would be ideal.
That being said, offline data (as labeled images) would likely be more realistic. How-
ever, quality open source dataset of CAPTCHA puzzles (not just sub-components without
layout) is hard to come by (except for easy text-based CAPTCHA).
Academic papers with few exceptions almost never release their datasets. As example
a study of a novel CAPTCHA solver would always require a dataset at least for validating
performance. In our experience, some papers settle on web scraping of offline exam-
ples (labeled images or videos) of commercial providers’ CAPTCHA designs. An example
is Oediupus [9] that paid a relatively small sum of money for API access to extract ob-
servations (without labels).
We assume that these existing datasets are not shared due to legal and (already du-
bious) ethical concerns. Scraping image assets or DOM snapshots at scale risks viola-
tion of usage agreements and potentially exposes the offender to legal liability. Even if
such legal liability could be avoided it would still be highly debatable in regard to ethics.
As another example a novel CAPTCHA solver design may be introduced in a study. Our
experience shows, new designs almost never get shared (neither as demos without rate-
limit or as open source). Here we hypothesize that researchers want to protect the value
of their invention by ensuring people have limited access to study how to break it. More
or less the same reason is most likely what fuels the CAPTCHA industry of commercial
providers. In the commercial provider case we are however always guaranteed initial
access to examples of their design (e.g. by paying for an API ourselves). However, again
legal and ethical issues could be considerable.

In addition to legal and ethical issues with web scrapping commercial CAPTCHA, there
would likely also carry with it a wide range of practical issues. First of all, web scraping
would require some level of manual annotation (or build of a annotation system that
uses specialized models when applicable). Rate limits imposed by providers would also
slow down scraping - especially if IP blocking is a possibility.
In addition, the behavioral analysis layer would likely make the degree of independence
between scrapped episodes hard to judge. In other words, without complete blocking
of all meta data analysis the humanness-scores predicted by the CAPTCHA would im-
pact each other. Even eliminating part of the meta data through virtual machines and
residential proxies would require a lot of resources.

A separate script from the backend server script is used to generate datasets of ran-

a sequence of action tokens from a custom action vocabulary. At each time step a sin-

stored. The optimal trajectory sample and the optimal actions at each step can be used
as supervised training data. The action vocabulary in itself represents a contribution
as the full version allow human level control of the browser UI through atomic actions
and custom operator tokens.
Most VLAM utilize reinforcement learning as part of their training scheme [15]. Coliseum
includes a Gymnasium environment to support online reinforcement learning [17]. To
support the interaction with the browser required by the Gymnasium environment as
well as the simulation script, a interaction scheduler has been built that handle both
high-level token execution and potential requirements for action execution at equidis-
tant discrete time points (time discretization). Atomic action tokens are (when the next
discrete time point is reached) supplied to a custom browser controller that translates
each action token in the vocabulary into a browser API (Playwright) function call [18].
To the best of our knowledge, this environment specialized for CAPTCHA is the first
of its kind to be made open source. While more general environments surely exist that
can accomplish the same, this environment combines everything into one in a transpar-
ent and compact format which can quickly be reused and expanded with new CAPTCHA
designs using standardized template functions. It is also the only larger collection of
easily accessible open source non-legacy interactive CAPTCHA designs.
1.2.2
Analysis of VLM and VLAM models
The report builds somewhat on the assumption that there exists an action gap in con-
ventional VLMs. The natural starting point is to try and reproduce it. This requires us
to first show that VLMs are able to understand all the CAPTCHA on a high-level (founda-
General CAPTCHA Solver
7
==PAGE:20==
tion). A model (V0) is implemented as a VLM that takes a image of the initial CAPTCHA
state and a prompt as input and converts it into a high-level solution in a design specific
prespecified format. This is done as pure inference (no training) by engineering design
specific prompts that guides the VLM to output natural text in the earlier mentioned de-
sign specific formats. This of course require the addition of a translation module. It is
expected that a pre-trained state-of-the-art VLM with (mostly) soft guidance should be
able to generalize well enough to support that the VLM has understanding capabilities
to solve the CAPTCHA collection used in the experiments. Experiments with structured
output are also conducted.
Now we are ready to examine whether we can reproduce the action gap. A model (V1) is
implemented similarly to V0 to convert a initial image and an engineered prompt into
a specific prespecified format. The only difference is that the format now consists of a
sequence of action and operator tokens that have been prespecified. Note that there
is still no training involved. It is expected that there will be a clear difference in the
full trajectory accuracy of V1 compared to the GT solution accuracy of V0 on the same
CAPTCHA designs. This would be a clear indication of a action-understanding gap. In
addition, the per timestep accuracy of actions are computed which is of course expected
to be higher than the full trajectory accuracy but might also provide useful insight into
what type of interactions are especially affected by the action gap.
Most VLAM models are built using standard VLM architectures. The type of VLM archi-
tecture used is typically single-path VLM. These architectures are chosen due to their
state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in tasks that require sequential planning and rea-
soning (such as most agentic tasks) [19]. In our case, Molmo and Qwen2.5VL are used as
backbone VLMs in all of the models in the report [8][7].
The V2 architecture builds on a frozen vision‐language backbone by replacing its orig-
inal inference head with a compact, trainable decoder that classifies discrete action
and operator tokens in place of generating free‐form text. By constraining outputs to
a fixed vocabulary of API calls V2 dramatically reduces the search space and eliminates
the need to discard out‐of‐format responses. When presented with a static CAPTCHA
- whose visual state fully determines the solution under the Markov assumption - the
decoder attends autoregressively to the backbone’s frozen embeddings and emits an
entire action trajectory from a single screenshot, internally caching key–value pairs
to track its own predicted state.
In environments that violate the Markov property
(“dynamic CAPTCHAs”), the same decoder falls back to a step‐wise prediction mode,
optionally ingesting prior screenshots through cross‑attention to accommodate mild
non‐Markovian dependencies without triggering a full replanning loop.
Training proceeds in two distinct phases. First, a supervised pre‐training stage uses
simulated “optimal” action sequences, drawn from a curriculum that introduces mod-
eling of progressively more complex mechanics into the vocabulary. This phase not
only instills basic action vocabularies but also validates the robustness of the schedul-
ing infrastructure that orchestrates token‐to‐API mappings. Second, we finetune the
decoder with proximal policy optimization: the learned critic signal serves both to re-
fine token selection and to provide a dynamic replanning trigger when environment
feedback suggests the Markov assumption has been breached. By gradually increasing
CAPTCHA complexity - splitting tasks into action‐only (A-CAPS) and understandingac-
tion (U‑CAPS) categories - we ensure that exploration both improves policy robustness

Background
This section give a short overview of the history of CAPTCHA and cover some of the deep
learning techniques that are foundational for the report.
2.1
Historical Evolution of CAPTCHA
Since their invention, CAPTCHAs have undergone multiple stages of innovation driven
by the dual pressure of easy human usability and adversarial resilience. A part of this
project has been to get a overview of the different types of CAPTCHA in existence and use
them as inspiration for building a curriculum of CAPTCHA designs as well as the types of
requirements they impose on modeling interaction. This section briefly chronicles the
major milestones and shifting security–usability trade‐offs of CAPTCHA during the time
of their existence. Many different types of CAPTCHA designs have been categorized by
works such as [1]. The report includes a overview of these in appendix A.1.
2.1.1
Text‐Based CAPTCHAs (iteration 1)
The idea of using AI hard problems to detect bots was as mentioned in the introduc-
tion section facilitated by [4][5]. The first versions of CAPTCHA were completely static,
text-based ones. These designs typically require to recognize a distorted sequence of
alphanumeric characters displayed in an image. They are surprisingly still prevalent
on the internet even though they have all been broken by specialized optical character
recognition (OCR) solver programs. In 2014 Google officially announced that AI could
solve even the most difficult variant of distorted text at 99.8% accuracy [20]. The fact
that so many remain in use is likely due partly to historical reason and partly due to their
simplicity from a developer perspective (open source). Examples of some text-based
designs historically adopted on a large scale can be seen in fig. 2.1.

2.1.2
Audio CAPTCHAs (iteration 2)
Audio CAPTCHAs were initially introduced to accommodate visually impaired users [1].
These designs typically require users to input what is said in a audio clip of distorted
speech overlaid with noise (e.g. a sequence of spoken digits). As late as 2018, Google
introduced a variant of this exact design. That being said, the upper limit on the amount
of audio distortion that can be applied to speech in a audio CAPTCHA before it becomes
ineligible is rather low (small room to operate in audio CAPTCHA design). In addition, to
this audio CAPTCHA are impractical (draws attention). The security of these simple audio
based CAPTCHA are also under serious strain given the fatly increasing performance of
automatic speech recognition (ASR) models in the age of deep learning [1].
2.1.3

Image‐selection challenges emerged to exploit humans’ superior visual‐semantic rea-
soning. Early schemes used fixed icon sets and simple label matching; later ones em-

2.1.4
Interactive CAPTCHAs (iteration 4)
To outpace solvers, CAPTCHA began to impose more complex UI motor planning require-
ments on the user such as cursor dragging. The gap between solution understanding
and action execution was thereby widened. CAPTCHA also began to increase the require-
ments on action memory by requiring longer ordered sequences of ordered actions (not
just unordered sets of actions). That being said, CAPTCHA were still static in the sense
that the visual CAPTCHA state mostly obeyed the Markov property. Most modern solvers
can break all the common interactive CAPTCHA if trained on similar examples first [1].
The interactive CAPTCHA are very prevalent at other providers than Google. As example,
Geetest [29], the CAPTCHA provider for sites such as AliExpress, exclusively uses behav-
ioral analysis wrapped versions of interactive CAPTCHAs. Examples of some interactive
designs can be seen in fig. 2.3.

2.1.5
Behavioral Analytics CAPTCHAs (present)
Google reCaptcha’s noCaptcha was one of the first widely popular CAPTCHA to utilize
behavioral analytics for bot detection [1]. This design is illustrated in fig. 2.4. Today,
all major CAPTCHA providers most likely use at least some level of behavioral analytics
in their CAPTCHA puzzles. The behavioral analytics can as earlier mentioned be divided
into two categories: (1) tracking of UI interaction during the current CAPTCHA encounter
and (2) analysis of meta data largely independent of the current CAPTCHA encounter.
The exact data used by commercial CAPTCHA providers is of course not openly shared.

However, it is safe to assume the latter category often includes data about the cursor
trajectory, scrolling behavior and timing of key strokes. Similarly, it is assume that data
from the former category often include device fingerprints such as hardware IDs, win-
dow rendering dimensions (strongly correlated to the size of the hardware display as
most people browse the internet primarily in full screen mode), IP address, browser ses-
sion ID, cookie history (including history of past encounters with any of the provider’s
CAPTCHA) and much more.
In other words, this paradigm shift moved the arms race into modeling human dynam-
ics. At the same time is also raised privacy concerns. While successful attempts have
been made in bypassing meta-data-based detection (or even exploit it to bypass de-
tection) it still adds another layer of cost to a solver setup (e.g. building fake user
‘identities’ on virtual machines by letting them randomly surf the web enough to build
a ‘cover story’ as well as paying for residential proxy servers) [1][3]. Consequently, the
meta layer can be circumvented but at a cost which have to be compared to the price of
getting CAPTCHA solved using cheap labor with a human-in-the-loop strategy or attacks
such as the one outlined by [33].
A strategy used by some is to have the bot transfer control over the GUI to a hired
human who can solve the CAPTCHA and then surrender control back to the bot to let
it continue its business. This is also known as a human solver relay attack [1]. On the
human dynamics modeling side of behavioral analytics CAPTCHA there are plenty of
methods to build specialized APIs to convert CAPTCHA solutions into e.g. human-like
cursor trajectories [34]. The conclusion by [1] is that the common behavioral analytics
CAPTCHA have all been broken as well.

2.2
Large Language Models (LLMs)
Large language models (LLMs) depart from recurrence-based models by replacing re-
current neural networks (RNN) layers or long-short-term-memory (LSTM)/gated recur-
rent unit (GRU) modules with transformer modules. LLM architectures have generally
diverged into three main structural families: (1) encoder-decoder, (2) encoder-only and
(3) decoder-only.
2.2.1
Encoder LLM
The encoder only LLM has a architecture fully comparable to the encoder of the origi-
nal transformer model [6]. The encoder consists of three phases: (1) input processing,
(2) main processing and (3) inference head. Main processing alludes to a sequence of

The QA model is trained to find the answer to a prompted question in a prompted input

large fully bidirectional forward pass. The role of the inference head is to for each out-
put context from the processing phase predict the probability that this token position

2.2.2
Decoder LLM
The decoder only LLM has a architecture comparable to the decoder of the original
transformer model - though without the cross-attention mechanism in the transformer
block. The structure of the section will be similar to that of the encoder LLM: First some
new mechanisms required to understand decoder LLM are introduced. This is followed
by an example of the inference pipeline for a decoder LLM - specifically a GPT-style QA
model [38].
Causal self-attention
In the decoder LLM causal self-attention is the primary type of self-attention used
(along with some basic masked self-attention for PAD tokens in the training pipeline).
Causal self-attention is itself a type of masked self-attention. The difference is that
each token position gets its own mask that disregards all input token positions after the
current position. This is used to avoid a model can look at future data during the au-
toregressive prediction process (see next subsection). An example of causal-attention
is illustrated in fig. 2.12.
Figure 2.12: Causal attention masking example [39].
Autoregressive process

2.2.3
Encoder-decoder LLM
The encoder-decoder LLM combines the encoder from the encoder LLM and a modified
version of the decoder from the decoder LLM.
Cross-attention
The decoder transformers in the encoder-decoder LLM includes an additional sub-block
of attention, residual and normalization after the masked multi-head self-attention.
The additional attention layer is a cross-attention layer. A cross-attention layer is al-
most identical to the self-attention in the basic transformer. The only difference is that
the keys and values are not computed in the layer itself but supplied from outside.
The intuition is that cross-attention layers allow the model to request information from
elsewhere (attending to another collection of vectors of the same dimension). It can be
used for many different purposes such as building a memory system for a model.

2.3
Vision language models (VLMs)
While LLMs excel at pure textual reasoning, vision–language models (VLMs) extend the
transformer paradigm to jointly process images and text. In other words, the core ob-
jective is to learn unified representations and cross‐modal. Apart from how the visual
tokens are produced and later fused, the core machinery remains largely identical to
that of an LLM.
2.3.1
Image Encoding Backbones
Early VLMs inherited convolutional feature extractors directly from computer‑vision pipelines,
but more recent designs typically employ a Vision Transformer (ViT) backbone. In a ViT,
an image is partitioned into a grid of fixed‑size patches, each of which is flattened, lin-
early mapped to a d‑dimensional token, and augmented with a learned 2D positional
encoding. Fig. 2.16 illustrates the original ViT from [40]. Many vision backbones are also
made as hybrids convNets and ViTs. An simple example is the use of convolutional lay-
ers in front of the ViT input processing to bring down input resolution of the ViT (lower
computational cost).
2.3.2
Visual grounding
Visual grounding is the ability to link textual references to specific image regions. Of-
ten, when literature describes a VLM as ‘supporting’ visual grounding, it refers to the fact

that the VLM has been explicitly trained with architecture and/or loss mechanics that
encourage visual grounding performance to a point where it can be practically used.
This typically includes the ability to output normalized point or bounding box coordi-
nates or segmentation masks in a text‑friendly format. While some model such as [41]
obtain this through the inclusion of dedicated inference head (e.g. regression head for
coordinates), decoder VLM typically learn to model it directly through its natural text
output using some kind of guidance to make the text adhere to an interpretable format.
This is also the case for the VLMs used in the experiments in this report.
2.3.3
Structural families
VLMs broadly fall into two structural families: (1) single-stream models and (2) dual-
stream models.
Dual-stream models
The dual‐stream architecture was first popularized by [42]. This model like all most
other popular dual-stream models are dual-encoder models. In this architecture the
model independently encode image and text inputs using separate backbones (e.g.
transformer encoder for text and ViT for images). The resulting embeddings for match-
ing text and image are aligned by using methods such as contrastive loss optimization.

In contrastive loss a batch of text embeddings are individually compared in pair-wise
fashion with all image embeddings from the same batch. Unormalized cosine similarity
(aka. dot product) is used as similarity measure. The trick is to maximize the similar-
ity between the matching text and image embedding pairs in the batch and minimize
the similarity with the rest. If the batch of image and text embeddings both occupy
the same index in the batch this can be formulated as a matrix-matrix product the the
objective is to maximize the sum of the diagonal elements added to the sum of the
negative off-diagonal elements of the resulting matrix.
The dual encoder model architecture works well for many tasks such as image cap-
tioning and have fantastic zero-shot capabilities (sometimes outperforming previous
specialized models without training). A bit similar to our discussion about encoder-
only LLMs the architecture is not really suited for sequential reasoning tasks. This is
can arguably be attributed to the fact that that modalities are fusioned too late (on the
”final embedding”-level).
Single-stream models
Single-stream models (aka. fusion-encoders) are models that processes and fusion the
image and text modality together from more or less right after the input processing.
The single-stream approach was really first brought to the forefront by VisualBERT [43].
This can be seen as a rather trivial extension of BERT [37] (encoder LLM) where a vi-
sion backbone is used to extract input embeddings for the LLM core model (in this case
BERT) that are appended to the input tokens with a SEP token between the modalities.
One can split single-stream models into all the same sub-structural families as dis-
cussed for LLMs (encoder VLM, decoder VLM and encoder-decoder VLM). However, they
all build on the same machinery for integrating the vision modality into a LLM core. In
the methodology section we will consider decoder only LLMs as these unsurprisingly
(given our similar discussions in the LLM section) are the best subcategory for sequen-
tial reasoning.

2.4.3
Structured Decoding for Tool Calling
Structured decoding refers to techniques that constrain the output of LLMs and VLMs to
follow predefined formats or syntactic rules. This could be pushing the output to com-
ply with specific schemas such as JSON objects, HTML tags, function calls, or domain-
specific grammars. This approach is valuable in embodied agent scenarios where model
outputs must interface with downstream systems requiring strict input formats such as
APIs for interacting with the physical or digital environment. The implementation of
structured decoding can be based on pure soft guidance or include strict enforcement
of hard constraints.
Soft guidance
Soft guidance relies on in-context learning and prompt engineering. In-context learning
simply describes how to train (or finetune) a model with a specific output format in the
training data instills a prior over this structuring of the output in inference. In addition,
one can engineer prompts with explicit instructions and exemplars demonstrating the
desired output structure within their input context. It is very computationally efficient
(no added inference cost) but only encourages format compliance (no guarantees). In
the case of an invalid output structure one cannot do much else than re-run inference
with some degree of stochastic sampling (as to potentially get a different output).

2.5
Prompting Paradigms in Agentic Reasoning
In the attempt of extending LLMs/VLMs for controlling embodied agents, multiple ex-
tensions have been made to the Chain-of-Thought (COT) prompting technique to turn it
into a more powerful agentic paradigm able to handle dynamic environments.
2.5.1
Chain‐of‐Thought Prompting: Exposing Latent Reasoning
Chain‑of‑Thought (CoT) prompting [46] is a mechanism for encouraging stronger se-
quential reasoning for multi-step tasks by making internal deliberations explicit and
exposing these (normally) latent reasoning steps to the user. This allow the user to fol-
low the thought process of the model. CoT works by including a prompt instruction such
as “Let us think step by step,” which causes the model to generate a sequence of inter-
mediate “thought” tokens before a final output. The model can be trained (or in other
ways guided) to segment its token output into intermediate thoughts and conclusion
using e.g. special tokens. Using a OpenAI model or DeepSeek in ‘think longer’ or ‘deep-
think’ mode uses CoT to display the models intermediate reasoning while highlighting
the conclusion. While increasing performance on multi-step tasks, the approach does
come at increased computational cost and inference time. CoT does not incorporate any
form of feedback from an external environment or any memory state across prompts in
a dynamic setting.
2.5.2
ReAct: Interleaving Reasoning with Tool Invocation
ReAct (Reasoning and Acting) [47] is a paradigm that extends CoT prompting by embed-
ding tool calls into the reasoning loop. In each autoregressive iteration, the agent emits
a “thought” segment explaining its current inference, followed by an “action” segment
invoking an external API (e.g. search the web or use calculator). The returned observa-
tion is assimilated into the next autoregressive step’s prompt thought-segment (text or
visual). This introduces state across repeated prompts to the agent with the ability to
obtain feedback on and make use of external help through tool calling. This tight in-
tegration of perception, action, and reasoning opens the door to dynamic interaction,
but still operates in a sequential chain and lacks any higher‑level self‑evaluation.

2.5.3
Reflexion: Critique-Driven Revision and Episodic Memory
Reflexion [48] extends the ReAct loop with proactive self‐evaluation and iterative refine-
ment. After an initial sequence of CoT prompting yields a candidate action (solution),
the agent enters a “Critique” phase to identify inconsistencies, unsupported assertions,
or hallucinations. It then executes a “Revision” phase, generating an improved answer
informed by its own critique. Some implementations such as incorporate an episodic
memory buffer (e.g. retrieval augmented [RAG] system or memory graph interpreted
with graph neural network) to record past successes and failures, guiding future rea-
soning.
2.6
Large Action Models (LAMs)
Large Action Models (LAMs) constitute a specialized subclass of LLM/VLM systems that
are designed to close the persistent “action gap” between high‑level task planning and
low‑level command execution. LAMs are a broader movement of LLMs/VLMs that aim
to close the action gap through the prioritization of streamlined action interfaces and
end‑to‑end execution. More simply put, the movement aim to make agentic models
both more efficient and effective through simpler and more direct pipelines that di-
rectly integrates action prediction into the learned model as to decrease the distance
from input to solution.
The lowest level of modeling interactions is in the context of GUI navigation, the level
where a dragging action consists of calls to atomic actions functions for mouse key
down, mouse movement and mouse key up. LAMs are not in anyway constrained to
use such a low-level interaction model as there are arguments both for and against it.
One of the arguments for this approach is that the low-level action representation at
the API (minimal API interpretation) forces the model to understand actions more in
depth as it needs to internalize more of the pipeline from sensory input to actual ex-
ecution of the generated plan in the environment. Another argument is that low-level
interactions provides increased flexibility for the model to naturally fit any training en-
vironment without the requirement of a new specialized system. In other words, if the
core LLM/VLM is able to internalize interaction model low enough to be a super-set of
the way humans can interact with a GUI while solving CAPTCHA (maybe all the way down
to hardware level), the model would be assumed scalable to any other GUI navigation
problem with the correct fine-tuning.
LAMs are often (but again - not always) designed with less overhead in terms of space
and time complexity than previous attempts to agentic AI with LLM/VLM core has been
[15]. This also allow LAMs to be scaled down and used for specialized domains instead
of having a single behemoth architecture of different RAG modules for tool calling and
memory that need to be able to handle all domains (e.g. single application vs. full
cross-application OS navigation).
All this contribute to a more efficient framework that can combat the latter of current
state-of-the-art (SOTA) digital agent programs (such as [16]): (1) Inability to utilize the
full understanding prowess of the LLM-like core due to the action gap and (2) limited
practical usability due to slow inference. It is based on this interpretation of the LAM
philosophy that this report will base its model development.

that, at each discrete time step t, the agent observes a environment state st. It then
samples an action from its policy at ∼π(s) and receives a receives a corresponding
reward r(st, at). The agent lastly transitions to a new state st+1 according to the envi-

all states such that the (expected) reward total (aka. total return) is maximized. The

ronment further from the underlying goal (e.g. penalty for wasting time plus a reward
for getting closer to a goal state). The reward function can - depending on the RL tech-
nique used - be modeled either implicitly or explicitly as well as learned or engineered.

Agent-E: Hierarchical Planning for Web Navigation
Agent-E [12] uses a two-level LLM-based agent stack for goal-oriented web browsing. A
initial LLM decomposes user goals into a high-level plan of abstract steps. The first of
these steps are then send to a second LLM that autoregressively predicts a sequence
of corresponding browser API function calls (such as ”input text ”...” in <field>” where
<field> is a identifier from the DOM). These are the function calls the model assumes
are required to execute the the current high-level step. After every action, the agent
emits a natural-language summary of the resulting UI change (e.g., ”login button is now
visible”) through observation of changes in the DOM. The action feedback from all au-
toregressive steps are concatenated into a single feedback message that the high-level
planner then uses to first verify whether the entire task has been successfully com-
pleted (the full task might have required less high-level steps than initially planned).
If not, it will evaluate whether the feedback indicates that the plan is moving along as
expected. This will determine whether it progresses to delegating the next high-level
step or decides to rep-plan (using the current state of the DOM and the original task
description as input).
Notice Agent‑E thus does include failed‑step information during replanning. Instead
the high-level planner can also decide to inquire further into the current state of the
DOM before throwing out the old plan. This is done by appending one or more new
high-level steps dynamically as feedback is received that asks the low-level planner to
use action function calls and sensor function calls to inform it further of the current
DOM state. This mechanism with that uses the addition of sensor function calls to al-
low the model to extend the reflexion mechanism is what makes Agent-E innovative.
Fig. 3.6 and fig. 3.7 illustrates the workflow of the Agent-E model just covered as well as
the different sensor and action tool calls available to the model.

OpenVLA: LAM for embodied Control
OpenVLA is a step toward accessible, general-purpose vision-language-action models
for robotic manipulation [53]. The architecture of OpenVLA is illustrated in fig. 3.8.
The model attaches a dual-stream visual encoder - one branch for precise spatial un-
derstanding and another for semantic-linguistic grounding - to a LLM reasoning core
(based on Llama2-7B). The output of the LLM isn’t free-form natural language from
Llama2’s original vocabulary, nor is it completely retrained to use a brand-new action-
specific vocabulary. Instead, it adapts the existing Llama2 tokenizer by augmenting it
with special action tokens mapped to discrete, quantized robot commands. This is done

3.2.1
Molmo
Molmo [8] (September 2024) is a open source VLM released by the Allen Institute for AI,
comprising four configurations: MolmoE-1B (a mixture‐of‐experts model with 1B active
and 7 B total parameters), Molmo-7B-O (the principal 7 B variant), Molmo-7B-D (a demo
checkpoint), and Molmo-72B (the largest scale). Only the MolmoE-1B and Molmo-7B-
O versions (excluding the demo version) were small enough to be considered for this
project.
Model overview
Molmo is based on Qwen2 (or its MoE extension in MolmoE-1B) LLM (reasoning core).
CLIP ViT-L/14 is used as pre-trained vision encoder. The open source community have
struggled to keep up with the performance of proprietary VLM models. This has es-
pecially been evident in the finetuning phase that all foundational VLMs typically go
through. To squeeze out the last few percentages of accuracy it requires very high
quality data. While open source models have recently gravitated towards relying on
datasets made available by proprietary model providers (in the small scope these are
made available) as well as synthetic data generated by proprietary model’s (through
API calls). Consequently, such open source models effectively become distillations of
proprietary VLMs.
The Molmo model is in itself not especially innovative; the architecture and training
scheme (initial pre-training of already pre-trained models followed by finetuning) is
similar to other open source VLM models. The difference mainly lie in the introduction
of a new dataset, PixMo, used to train Molmo. The unique thing about this dataset is that
all its data has been collected/synthesized and labeled without the use of any exter-
nal VLMs. About half of PixMo consists of data collected and labeled by humans. Most
of this is pointing data (for visual grounding). Molmo’s default behavior is to predict
natural text (unconstrained) possibly interlaced with points denoted using an HTML-
like format. (x,y) coordinates are scaled to 0-100 (to get scale independent predictions
that generalize better). For a single point, the format is ”<point x=”10.0” y=”10.0” alt=”alt
text”>Inline text</point>”. For multiple points the format is ” <points x1=”10.0” y1=”10.0”
x2=”20.0” y1=”20.0” ... alt=”alt text”>Inline text</points>”. The use of a specific notation
format like this makes it easy to extract the points. The second half of PixMo consists
of classically simulated data and labels (similar to how this report creates its CAPTCHA
pre-training dataset) all centered around different specific tasks for enhancing visual
grounding (e.g. interpreting clocks, counting or generating code for visualizing data).
In addition to PixMo, a number of other open source high quality academic datasets
are used for training. One example of this is a simplified version of the AndroidControl
dataset used for training tool calling and sequential reasoning in GUI navigation for
digital agents (although the exact setup is a bit unclear from the paper).
Note that Molmo uses soft-guidance through how it is trained (label format) to push
predictions toward the correct output format for points. The model does not natively
enforce structured output in anyway so there is in principle no guarantee that out-
put format will not deviate from expectation. Note also that Molmo support not only
single-image but multi-image input. The multi-image scenario is simply dealt with by
concatenating the embedding streams of the patches of the two images (with special

3.2.2
Qwen2.5VL
Qwen2.5‑VL [55] (Februrary 2025) is a open source single-stream decoder VLM model
made by Alibaba Group available in sizes 3B, 7B, and 72B parameters. Only the 3B and
7B versions are small enough to be considered for the project.
Model overview
Qwen2.5‑VL is based on the Qwen2.5 LLM [56] (reasoning core). A custom ViT architec-
ture is used as vision processor for converting images to the multi-modal embedding
space used as input for the reasoning core.
Without going too much into detail the model firstly makes a innovation in spatial mod-
eling by introducing native dynamic resolution processing. Instead of resizing inputs to
a fixed size, the ViT ingests images at their native spatial resolution and applies win-
dowed attention to reduce computational cost without sacrificing perceptual fidelity.
Secondly, the model makes a couple of innovations in temporal modeling by introduc-
ing temporal dynamics through dynamic FPS sampling and absolute time encoding via
multi-modal rotary position embeddings (MRoPE).
In addition the model is trained to support both bounding‑box and point‑based local-
ization (visual grounding), enabling precise object detection, counting, and semantic
joint reasoning. This include successful training of grounding of GUI elements on desk-
top and mobile. Some of this training (e.g. Android Control benchmark) not only require
perception and reasoning but structured step-by-step tool command sequences (tool
calling). In other words, QWEN2.5-VL is also trained for digital agentic tasks such as
logging into an app or navigating menus. When predicting points and bounding boxes
a specific JSON format is used but unlike Molmo coordinates are predicted using the
original pixel scale (not normalized) as the authors believe the model actually benefits
from learning to model the scale.
The model is trained to default to output natural text. To allow tool calling (etc.) we
want to be able to map generated output to API tool calls with as little uncertainty
as possible. That being said, Qwen2.5-VL natively is not build explicitly to do forced
structured output. The result is that without the use of an external library (or other
modifications) the output is freely predicted natural text tokens (not action tokens or
any truly guaranteed format). The model is natively able to do well with soft guidance
accomplished through prompt engineering. A argument for the generator even exists as
to specify we want conditioning on the output being JSON format (in short - it supports
JSON). It can, however, still diverge from this format which would for example require
the model to be rerun again assuming non-greedy decoding is used.
As of writing, it is the highest ranking model on the huggingface open source VLM leader-
board [19]. [55] does only two comparisons with Molmo (which is a bit suspicious) where
it slightly outperforms Molmo on the CountBench benchmark (counting) while the op-
posite is true for the PointGrounding benchmark (pointing). However, one should not
forget to highlight that Qwen2.5-VL distinguishes itself from Molmo by being able to
process videos as chronological sequences of large numbers of frames (not just multi-
image).

Coliseum: Data framework
In the absence of freely available, high‐quality CAPTCHA datasets, COLISEUM provides
an end‐to‐end platform that synthesizes CAPTCHA puzzles with matching labels of high-
and low-level solutions and exposes them through interfaces for both supervised and
reinforcement learning. By unifying template specification, content randomization, la-
bel simulation, and data management, COLISEUM addresses the key obstacles impeding
fast reproducible research in agentic CAPTCHA solving.
4.1
Flask Backend
At the heart of COLISEUM lies a Flask server that hosts interactive CAPTCHA templates
in a standard web browser. The server can operate in standalone mode for local ex-
periments (such as debugging) or be deployed to a public domain to support concur-
rent users. Incoming HTTP requests trigger template rendering and content generation,
while server‐side logic enforces a handshake protocol mimicking real‐world CAPTCHA
behavior: client interactions are sent back for validation before new puzzles are issued.
4.2
Template Library
Each CAPTCHA design is implemented as a parameterized HTML, CSS, and JavaScript tem-
plate. Templates define spatial arrangements of images, text fields, buttons, or drag-
gable elements, together with rules governing valid responses and server-side commu-
nication.
4.2.1
Easily extendable
A new designs can be added by authoring an additional template file for the client-side.
A number of methods are expected in the JavaScript to allow the template to interface
with the backend. Here the most important is the Jinja template data receivers (a few
lines responsible receiving template content from the backend) and the submit (vali-
dation) HTTP request sender (e.g. linked to a button). On the server-side a procedural
content generator function and user response validation function needs be added. The
server-side methods are highly standardized and easy to extend to new designs.
4.2.2
Default library
The default library of CAPTCHA design templates implemented can roughly be split into
the categories of pure action CAPTCHA (A-CAPs) and understanding-action CAPTCHA (U-
CAPs). The reader is encouraged to run the Flask backend script to experience live
demos of each.

4.3
Content Generator
Although tables 4.1 and 4.2 show examples of the different design templates populated
with content, the templates themselves only describes the layout and client-side me-
chanics of the CAPTCHA. Instead it is the role of a dedicated content generator instan-
tiates each template with randomized assets drawn from curated pools.
In the example of a Google ReCaptcha-style image classification CAPTCHA, a HTTP re-
quest from the client requesting a new CAPTCHA would cause the generator of the afore-
mentioned CAPTCHA to sample a image class in a underlying dataset of images. The im-
ages would be processed (if necessary) and converted to b64-encodings for each grid
position. This would all be packed into a payload along with other puzzle parameters
as well as a unique episode ID (UUID).
The generator would also return the high-level solution on a design-specific format.
In the case of our example this would be a binary mask indicating which images needs
to be selected by the client.
All of this is stored in a makeshift database based around dictionaries with thread locks
to avoid any data races caused by concurrent access. The unique episode ID is used for
indexation. In the beginning of the project a SQLite database was used but was deemed
a bit overkill for the relatively small scale of maximally a few concurrent users.
The payload with the CAPTCHA content and episode ID is then passed to the Jinja tem-
plate engine which renders the template with its content. When the client-side submits
a solution through a HTTP request a payload is received with the client’s predicted so-
lution state (high level solution) and the episode ID (unaltered). The client’s prediction
can then be compared with the stored ground truth solution state. When a CAPTCHA
has been evaluated or have been stored in the server over some time limit (e.g. due to
client leaving the web page), it is deleted from the database.
This whole process yields an effectively infinite distribution of CAPTCHA instances, miti-
gating overfitting by ensuring agent exposure to diverse visual challenges. It also mim-
ics the real CAPTCHA protocol with its handshake that always keeps the GT solution
server-side. This also means that even the few fully open source CAPTCHA designs that
exist can be incorporated into the framework without excessive changes to their work-
flow.
Note that content generation can be done on the fly as described above but also be
run standalone to generate a content dataset that can be used for academic purposes
where reproducibility is key (such as our experiments) and we want to avoid block-
ing threads slowing down training by requiring greater CPU resources. Generation of a
dataset using this method is quick with the use of CPU multi-threading.

4.4.2
Solution
A high‑level solution encodes the solved CAPTCHA state without reference to any GUI
interactions. For a classical 3×3 image‑grid CAPTCHA, one might represent the solution
as a binary vector s ∈{0, 1}9, where si = 1 exactly when the ith image is selected. This

The format of the high-level solution(s) stored on the server and the prediction returned
by the client often does not match. This is mainly due to the fact that the server-side
solution must describe all possible solutions (with tolerance). An example is the 3D
CAPTCHA where the server-side solution(s) stored is a bounding box where the pre-
dicted solution is a single point which is evaluated as correct if being located inside the
server-side bounding box.

description of the low-level plan to how we have implemented for simplicity.
4.5
Action vocabulary, scheduler and executioner
We will now discuss our vocabulary of interaction commands (actions and operations)
and how they are tied to the CAPTCHA interaction mechanisms discussed and imple-
mented using an interaction scheduler and browser controller (browser API wrapper).
A script has been made to generate and cache action vocabularies including the re-
quired tokens for specific CAPTCHA on the fly.
4.5.1
Action tokens and the browser API
The action tokens all start with the prefix ‘A_’. These are handled by the action execution
method in the browser controller class instance. The browser controller partly acts as a
44
General CAPTCHA Solver
==PAGE:57==
wrapper for a Playwright instance of a chromium browser. An action token can be con-
verted into a Playwright API call and asynchronously executed using the execute action
method. The browser controller also allows for screenshots with optional overlays that
indicate the cursor position as well as rerouting the browser to new URLs.
Button interface (UP, DN, TR)
All button actions needed to solve the CAPTCHA - both key presses and left mouse click
- is encoded by tokens on the format ‘A_<mode>_<button>’ where <mode> describes a
press action, release action or trigger action (quick press-release).
Cursor interface (CT)
Playwright is a stateless API where the cursor simply teleport to a specified location.
The browser controller wrapper keeps track of cursor position as a state. This is partly
as to have the option of using visual feedback about the previous cursor position by
drawing a overlay over the screenshot. It is also to allow future research to examine
human-like smooth cursor movement using small displacements of the mouse from
previous position instead of teleport. To limit the size of action space we implement
cursor movement as commands of the format ‘A_CT_X000Y000’ where the 000s are
placeholders for the index of a x and y grid tile in the screenshot state. The movement
action for a grid tile moves the cursor to its center position. Translation between pixel
coordinates and grid coordinates is handled by the browser controller as Playwright
expects pixel coordinates.
4.5.2
Operation tokens and the interaction scheduler
The interaction scheduler wraps the browser controller to separate low-level actua-
tor/sensory operations from high-level structural commands. It manages timed inter-
actions and concurrency through special operation tokens prefixed with O_, enabling
structured control flow during browser interactions.
Start & end episode (O_BE, O_EE)
O_BE (Begin Episode) initializes the interaction episode. Until this token is processed,
any action commands (A_*) are ignored and treated as O_WT (Wait). O_EE (End Episode)
terminates the episode by capturing a final screenshot of the browser state, storing it
in the state queue, and setting an episode-ended flag that stops the scheduler’s pro-
cessing loop. This token triggers the transition to post-episode cleanup.
Start & end concurrency (O_BC, O_EC)
O_BC (Begin Concurrency) activates concurrent action handling where subsequent ac-
tion commands are queued rather than executed immediately. O_EC (End Concurrency)
disables concurrency mode and flushes all pending actions from the queue, executing
them sequentially. This allows batched execution of actions when atomic state changes
are required, ensuring intermediate states don’t interfere with dependent operations.
Delay (O_WT)
O_WT (Wait) serves as a no-operation token that maintains interaction pacing with-
out executing browser actions. In timed mode, it is automatically inserted when the
command queue times out (controlled by pre-defined interval time and custom timer
class). Before episode start (pre-O_BE), it replaces invalid action commands. During
episodes, it ensures regular state capture and timer synchronization without altering
browser state, enforcing consistent observation intervals.

Label Simulator
To produce low-level solution labels COLISEUM includes a simulation module that gen-
erates optimal trajectories (episodes) for CAPTCHA in a pre-generated dataset of CAPTCHA

In essence, each simulator function receives a logger, browser controller as well the
high-level solution as input. An optimal low-level solution is planned from the high-

The action sequence is rolled out as a loop of state-action pairs logged in that order.

4.7
Gymnasium Environment
A custom Gymnasium environment integrates the COLISEUM Flask backend and asyn-
chronous interaction scheduler to allow for online reinforcement learning. The envi-
ronment conforms to the standard Gymnasium API methods.
4.7.1
Action and Observation Spaces
The action space is a discrete set A = {0, 1, . . . , |V | −1}. Observations consist of raw
RGB screenshots of the rendered CAPTCHA in the headless browser. Formally, the ob-
servation space is defined as O = Box(0, 255, (H, W, 3), uint8), where H and W match
the viewport dimensions.
4.7.2
Episode Lifecycle
When reset() is called the environment starts by deleting its intra-episode memory.
Next, a CAPTCHA design from the active curriculum is sampled. The chosen design’s
content and corresponding ground-truth solution are then either generated on the fly
or loaded from a precomputed cache (for the sake of reproducibility). In either cache
the CAPTCHA data is loaded into the Flask backend and the headless browser owned
by the browser controller navigates to display the puzzle. Depending on whether the
design requires timed interactions, the scheduler is restarted in timed or untimed mode
(to allow as fast interaction as possible). The first screenshot is then retrieved from the
schedule’s state queue and returned along with an initial reward of zero. This data can
be used in a typical RL loop to make the model predict an action command. This is
passed to the interaction scheduler with the step function which waits to process the
command until the next quantized time step is reached. After the processing of the
46
General CAPTCHA Solver
==PAGE:59==
command is done a new observation (screenshot) is made and the reward for the step
is calculated. Both of these are returned for the continued iteration of the loop one of
the criteria for ending the episode is met.
4.7.3
Curriculum Sampling
Curriculum progression is governed by two thresholds: a minimum encounter count
ni ≥min_encounters and a minimum success rate ¯si ≥min_success. After every
check_interval episodes, any design satisfying both criteria is promoted into the ac-
tive curriculum, and sampling probabilities pi are recomputed according to pi ∝1 −¯si.
When the design of new episodes is sampled by reset() selection probabilities are pro-
portional to the inverse of each design’s running mean success rate. This is done to
ensure that harder puzzles receive more focus. It should in turn result in a increased
success rate for that design - making requirements for adoption of new designs into the
current design pool faster.
4.7.4
Reward Structure
The immediate reward rt can be split into 3 parts: (1) Constant time penalty rstep, (2)
design specific reward for step transitions rdist and (3) potential end of episode reward.
The constant time penalty is simply a small negative reward that is added to encour-
age the model to select optimal trajectories (as few commands as possible to reach a
goal state). Internal puzzle-state logs are queried to compute the reward signal for step
transitions which are proportional to the reduction in a heuristic distance metric. The
heuristic is design-specific but roughly measures the number of state changes required
to get to any goal state.
As an example, the distance for the image classification CAPTCHA (aka. CLASS) is the
number of selections required to reach a goal state (ignoring pressing the submission
button). Thus the reward becomes positive when the agent selects a correct image and
negative when it selects a wrong image (or de-selects one of the correct images previ-
ously selected). The potential end of episode rewards include: (1) a submission bonus
rsubmit applied when the submit action is issued), (2) a success bonus rsuccess upon cor-
rect solution and (3) a truncation penalty rtrunc if the episode ends without submission
or upon reaching the maximum step limit.
4.7.5
Synchronization and Vectorization
To allow training/evaluation using multiple copies of environments to simulate multi-
ple predicted trajectories simultaneously a couple of environment wrappers are con-
structed using the Python third party libraries Gymnasium and Stable Baselines 3. The
first wrapper transforms the asynchronous ‘web’ environment described above into a
synchronous version (needed for third party RL training libraries). The second wrap-
per (applied to the sync wrapper) allow parallel processing of an arbitrary number of
parallel environments in a efficient fashion.

Methodology
A lot of the topics that would conventionally be considered methodology has already
been covered in chapters 2 and 4. In this chapter, we instead limit ourselves to detail-
ing the experiment with the concrete model and training technique implementations
used/developed (incl. the design of the models V0, V1 and V2).
5.1
VLM backbones
The section does not match any section in chapter 7 but simply introduces the details
of the customizations of the two VLM backbones Molmo and Qwen2.5VL introduced in
chapter 3.
5.1.1
Core Wrappers: Processor and Model
The backbone VLMs and input processors are wrapped with custom classes. This is done
partly to create a simplified and shared interface and partly to allow quick integration
of additional required functionality that is not directly supported by the native classes
from huggingface. One example of this batched processing. The native Molmo input
processor is not a standard huggingface class (but ‘remote code’) and does not support
batched input (even though the model class does). Meanwhile the Qwen2.5VL proces-
sor does support batched processing but still require the batched ‘user prompts‘ to be
manually constructed into chat-style message format referencing the path to any im-
ages or video also used as separate input for the vision encoder. Our wrapper classes
introduces greatly simplified batch processing for both backbones. Another example
of the simplified interface is the automatic extraction of only newly generated tokens
(not tokenized vision-text input) and the last hidden layer state of each autoregressive
processing step (needed for the adapter in the V2 model).
Note that Molmo has required some work to get working right due to the addition
of batch support revealing a bug (discussed in huggingface model hub) in the native
code. The native bug caused a CUDA memory access error that in a transformer-based
model is typically caused by some level of incorrect token ID indexation. This seem
to have been fixed during the project period but initially required the use of a non-
official branch of the model on huggingface (provided by a fellow user on the model
hub forum). The code supports the use of Molmo 7B, MolmoE 1B, Qwen2.5VL 7B and
Qwen2.5VL 3B (see chapter 3) but only Molmo 7B and Qwen2.5VL 7B are used in any of
the experiments as these strike the best balance between performance and required
computational resources as well as being the most comparable version pair from each
backbone category. Qwen2.5VL 7B or Molmo 7B will as a result in the experiments often
be referred to simply as ‘Qwen’ or ‘Molmo’ for brevity.
To support fast prototyping all model weights were converted to safetensors (MolmoE
1B weights are natively the old pickled format) with a high number of shards to allow
parallel loading using multiple Python processes (Molmo 7B weights are safe tensors
but natively uses too few shards for fast loading).
5.1.2
Pipeline Wrappers: Soft guidance and forced decoding
To simplify the model interface even further a full pipeline class that utilizes the above
mentioned wrappers (processor and model) is implemented for soft guidance experi-
ments. In addition, a second pipeline class is implemented for forced structured decod-

ing with Qwen2.5VL utilizing the third party Python library Outlines. It attempts to force
the VLM output of the backbone to adhere to a JSON format on a specific format (includ-
ing specific entries with specific types of values). The desired information is in our case
described through a set of engineered schema (class objects) using the model output
evaluation third party library Pydantic. Outlines compiles the class objects for each de-
sign and caches them to increase efficiency. Outlines decoding works more or less as
described in chapter 2. It guarantees JSON format this way and we also use it guarantee
other things such as the type of the data entries or its length. Pydantic schema does
not support features for enforcing uniqueness (e.g. that a data entry should consist of

It seems (although this might be wrong) that the most common way to deal with this

can use the validation method of the Pydantic schema to make a post-hoc check that

trigger either a new random sampling of the decoder distribution for the autoregres-
sive step whose decoding violated the requirement or a full re-run of the autoregressive
process. Given uniqueness is a constraint that would only be valuable in the two easi-

5.1.3
Introduction prompt and message roles
All uses of the VLM backbones for CAPTCHA solving include a layer of soft guidance
through prompt engineering (also when using forced structured decoding). This initial
prompt sets the stage by shortly describing the role (something similar to ”you are
a highly accurate CAPTCHA solving agent”) and overall goal of the agent (something
similar to ”your job is to provide a solution to the CAPTCHA on the format provided
below”). While Molmo is simply a raw decoder (what is see is essentially what you
get), Qwen2.5VL is as mentioned a chat-based decoder that uses a message format of
the input text prompt to be able to re-use it for later autoregressive processes. In our
experiments we do use this feature of history across ‘encounters’ but we do use both
the ‘user’ and ‘system’ role (the model itself uses the ‘assistant’ role). In an encounter,
the ’system’ role is used for providing the initial prompt (see above) as a first message
while the ‘user’ role is used for a second message.
5.1.4

The first ‘experiment’ is simply to get a few people to try and solve the different CAPTCHA
designs to act as a simple baseline for the models. While a large sample size would be
ideal, a weak baseline (due to uncertainty) from a few samples is deemed okay as we will
not strongly rely on the accuracies obtained. The human ‘baseline’ is not used as much

A dataset is first generated of ten different 450x450 resolution single color images. The

white, orange, purple. The aim of the VLM is to predict a descriptive color code for each
image. In this context, a ‘descriptive’ color code refers to a color code that represents
a color that in human perception is fairly close to the color represented by the target
color code. In other words, we need to utilize that two colors might that are perceptu-

(e.g. ”red”). To do this the each image colors RGB code is transformed to perceptual
LAB space (matches human perception better) and used as a cluster centroid. Decision
boundaries for the label space is then obtained by assigning each discrete color code
to the closest neighbor using euclidean distance.

5.2.1
E1: Exploring Backbone Understanding (V0 model)
First main experiment evaluates the performance of the model we will refer to as ‘V0’.
This experiment is done to evaluate the degree of high-level understanding the VLM
backbones has of CAPTCHA. The inference is based solely on a single screenshot of the
initial state of the CAPTCHA. The V0 model is a simple wrapper for one of the backbone
pipelines (either soft guidance or forced decoding version) with the addition of an final
‘interpreter’ which (if possible) converts the backbone output string into a pre-defined
format required for auto-evaluation.
The solution text format is simply a formal description or summary of any solution state
of the given CAPTCHA. The format extraction is rather trivial, design dependent and rely
mostly on standard string formatting, regex filtering and uniqueness checks for sets.
In cases where the backbone output does not follow the required text format within
a certain limit (where interpretation is possible) the data point is registered as wrong
formatting (distinct from the conventional correct and incorrect prediction categories).
To guide the backbone towards correctly formatted output, the initial introduction prompt
(see section 5.1.3) is followed by a design-specific prompt. This design-specific prompt
contains a very rigorous explanation of the format using named variables to describe
values mathematically (with type and range). In the soft-guidance configuration of the
V0 model, this design specific prompt is also followed by 3 concrete examples of the
format’s correct use. The interpreter then converts the data extracted from the well-
formatted backbone output into the data type used by the backend server CAPTCHA
end-state evaluator function (re-used in this context) for the specific design.
Note that the experiment is conducted with all designs from the custom dataset ex-
cept the designs that require either timed action (STOP & HOLD) or dragging (DRAG
& ROT). Here the solution is either not meaningfully described by an initial state (e.g.
STOP CAPTCHA solution is defined by a time between button presses) or require some
interaction to derive (e.g. ROT CAPTCHA solution requires a known relation between the
linear velocity of the slider and angular velocity of the rotating circular image crop). In
addition, also note that the although the quantitative part of this experiment does not
make use of CoT prompting, the outcome of initial exploration using CoT and Reflexion
for improving is covered. The inclusion of a quantitative setup for this would be ideal
but is not included as to limit the scope of the experiment (due to time constraints).

E2: Exploring backbone execution (V1 model)
The next main experiment evaluates the performance of the model we will refer to as

The V1 model structure is similar to the V0 model (wrapper of backbone pipeline and
final interpreter). The backbone VLM still uses the same token vocabulary for predict-
ing natural text (no fine-tuning or transfer learning). The shift to defining the CAPTCHA
solution as a sequence of commands makes the V1 model (unlike the V0 model) more
general. How general of course depends on how we prompt the backbone VLM. In the
experiment the V0 model is run in one of two prompt configurations: (1) with a full list
of the full available command vocabulary in the prompt or (2) with only the commands
in the prompt that are required to solve the given CAPTCHA. Each prompt of course in-
cludes a description of what it does.
It is the role of the interpreter to extract the command(s) from the backbone natural
text output. Similar text processing techniques are used here for extraction as in the
V0 model (just adopted to command output). There are basically two ways to run the
model: (1) static mode and (2) dynamic mode. In static mode only the initial screenshot
is used to predict the full solution trajectory (as in model V0). In dynamic mode the
model is re-run each step to update the vision input with the new timestep screenshot
of the CAPTCHA environment. The static mode is fast but challenging in the sense that it
can’t rely on any visual feedback throughout the trajectory. The dynamic mode can uti-
lize visual feedback but also does not have as rich a command history built into it as the
static mode version inherently has through its cached keys and values from the previ-
ous iterations in the autoregressive process. To remedy this the prompt is updated with
a descriptions of previous commands predicted in earlier iterations. Under ideal cir-
cumstances both these setups should be tested (for comparison) but to limit the scope
of the report we will focus on the dynamic mode version. The reason dynamic mode
is chosen above static mode is because each step of dynamic mode puts less effort on
the command memory of the model.
In conclusion, the prompt format used (to match dynamic mode) is the always included
introduction prompt, the action history, the description of the expected format and the
vocabulary (full or limited depending on configuration). The expected format in dy-
namic mode is simply natural text that reasons about what command to choose to get
closer to solving the encountered CAPTCHA (CoT-style) followed by a command from the
command vocabulary enclosed in ”<..>” (and not use ”<..>” anywhere else). This also al-
lows the model to use commands as part of its reasoning process (e.g. Molmo has a
tendency to list available options as it considers which to choose) without them being
considered as a final command by the extractor. If multiple commands marked with
”<..>” is found in the natural text output, the first one is used, as the model might just
have jumped ahead to plan its next step action as well. If no command marked with
”<..>” is found or the command marked with ”<..>” does not match the format, the pre-
diction is added to the count of wrongly formatted output (similar to how three metrics
are used in the V0 model). No forced decoding is used - only soft guidance.
The next consideration is how to evaluate the performance of the model. To get the
per step accuracy for each design, we similar to the training of a diffusion model sim-

ply sample a batch of design transitions (time step screenshot and optimal actions)
along with all the actions that has come before it (trajectory from start to current time
step) from the test set. When the accuracy for a design stabilizes (after a number of
epochs over the test set encounters) it is removed from the pool of designs sampled
(like reverse curriculum). This is continued to none remain.
5.2.3
Custom LAM: VLM with Trained Action Adapter (V2 Model)
The last main experiment examine if the results of the V1 model can be surpassed by
training a custom LAM (V2 model).
The custom LAM switches the final interpreter module of the V0 and V1 models with
an decoder network we will refer to as an ‘action adapter’. The action adapter is a
small decoder only LLM (with caching etc.) following the exact same design principles
as described in chapter 2. Instead of translating a natural text output (decoded from
a sequence of token ids representing tokens from backbone token vocabulary) into a
command vocabulary (as done in model V0 and V1) the action adapter directly predicts
tokens from the command vocabulary. This guarantees the model to always output in-
terpretable sequences of actions.
The backbone is linked to the action adapter in a encoder-decoder constellation. The
backbone VLM is given a generic prompt that consists of the initial introduction (see
section 5.2.1) followed by a request for the model to describe in high-level steps how
to solve the CAPTCHA (e.g. what to click on). Then the last hidden layer state at each
autoregressive step is generated through the usual autoregressive process of the back-
bone (the predicted token ids/output text is discarded). We will refer to this sequence
of hidden layer states as the high-level context. These are used to compute keys and
values similar to how it is done at the end of the encoder in the original transformer
model (as described in 5). The action adapter acts as the decoder and attends to these
through cross-attention.
The above model matches the static configuration of the V1 model (full trajectory pre-
diction from a single initial screenshot). If the V1 model’s dynamic mode was to be
extended to the V2 model we would similarly re-run the full encoder-decoder architec-
ture at each time step. As discussed when introduced, the static mode is by far the most
efficient of the two modes with O(1) time complexity compared to the O(n) time com-
plexity of the dynamic mode where n is the number of tokens of the longest trajectory
in the batch. On the other hand we expect the dynamic mode to be the most effective
of the two modes (again as discussed when the two modes were introduced for the V1
model).
Inspired by [12] (see 3.1.2), we utilize that the high-level planning is now decoupled from
low-level planning, to create a new mode which we denote as ‘semi-dynamic’. In this
mode the high-level planning module (backbone VLM) is run once using a single image
while the command autoregressive process of the action adapter is run dynamically
(updated each time step) with visual input. Each time step the new screenshot is pro-
cessed into a embedding using a convolutional neural network and added to a buffer
that can hold a pre-specified number K of screenshot embeddings. The buffer thus
not only adds access to current visual input but also K −1 previous observations. A
circular buffer is used to avoid expensive re-allocation of tensor memory (in-place).
The adapter is can attend the frame embeddings through cross-attention. The new
cross attention and normalization is specifically added after the cross-attention and

the transformer blocks. However, before the adapter can attend to the frame embed-

The training of the model is split into behavior cloning and fine tuning using PPO. In
both cases the backbone has frozen weights (not updated). The behavior cloning train-
ing setup is more or less just a basic transformer decoder training setup where the
forward pass is done simultaneously for all time steps. Typically the data loader re-
turns a sequence which you can shift 1 step to the left or right (masking first or last
element) to obtain the input and label sequence (a result of the autoregressive nature
of the model). However, to take into account the fact that CAPTCHA can have multiple
optimal trajectories (and thus multiple optimal actions per trajectory step) it would be
possible - but inefficient - to simply shift the single optimal trajectory used as input.
Consequently, for each trajectory we also pass all optimal actions as a multi-one-hot-
encoding (binary vector where 1’s describe an optimal action). If we normalize this
vector we obtain a label distribution pt which can be used in a soft-target cross entropy

Discussion
The results are discussed below in the order they where presented in chapter 7 followed by a
brief discussions of possible directions for future research.
8.1
Human Baseline and Data Collection
The human baseline indicated 100% accuracy for all A-CAPs with accuracies for U-CAPs ranging
from 68% to 100%. This is inline with the motivation for the categorization of said CAPTCHA. The
A-CAPs serve no problem for humans as they mostly pose a challenge in terms of input dexterity
and planning length. The U-CAP accuracies signify the greater difficulty imposed by additional
requirements on semantic reasoning skills.
The most difficult CAPTCHA for humans is apparently the ‘MATH’ design. While it was expected
that the text distortion applied in this CAPTCHA would pose somewhat of a challenge (unlike
the simple algebraic equations), it was not expected to be to this extent. As the number of en-
counters made with the design increased, the test people slightly improved as the test people
described they started to ‘figure out’ typical distortion patterns (specifically for the operator).
That being said, the extension of this mechanism was minimal. We kept the design as is to have
a range of different difficulties.
Note that the results are based on a low amount of samples of each design (60 in total) spread
over a low number of test people (3 in total) and presented consecutively (not in random order).
No statistical tests (p-test or confidence intervals) have been done as to limit the scope of the
report given the low significance of this specific data in the greater scheme of the report (mostly
acts as a unit test).
In addition I think it is important to discuss the limitations of using our own designed data frame-
work where one can interact with custom CAPTCHA designs. The custom approach is ethics-wise
preferable to scraping observations against terms of service of a commercial CAPTCHA provider.
However, it also means that the our CAPTCHA are not really comparable with real world data
(used by most other papers - although often for a very limited number of designs and samples).
The ideal setup would require a collaboration with a CAPTCHA provider (or similar) as unlikely
as it is. Also remember that we do use some scrapped data so we are not perfect either. This
was done to lend more credibility to some of our custom CAPTCHA designs to make them closer
to what would be observed in the real world.
8.2
VLM VRAM and Wall-Clock Time
The investigation of inference time and peak VRAM was done to ensure that the planned back-
bone configurations for later experiments would not cause trouble with regard to both of these
constraints. The choice of the 7 billion parameter versions for each backbone model was a natu-
ral choice as it was the biggest versions that could fit within a single GPU on DTU HPC. It was the
easiest setup to get something to work and simultaneously be able to compare across models.
One thing we needed to make ourselves familiar with was whether saving hidden layer outputs
of the VLMs during inference would cause a spike in peak VRAM use (and how much). How-
ever, the results show that it has virtually no impact likely due to PyTorch avoiding memory
de-allocation when favorable. The whole VLM exploration (except video experiments) was done
using V100 GPUs. While A100 GPUs are available at DTU HPC, they are seldom available as in-
teractive nodes, and have potentially very long queues for batch jobs. To make certain VRAM
wouldn’t be an issue, the VRAM limit was therefore initially set to the V100 GPU 32GB limit. After
tinkering around it was found that Molmo would be too large to run in FP32 with mixed precision
(used in original paper) with a meaningful batch size if limited to a V100 GPU. On huggingface
hub, the authors described the model could be run in bfloat16 with minor drop in performance
General CAPTCHA Solver
73
==PAGE:86==
(exactly how much was not specified). Instead float16 was used as bfloat16 is not supported
on the Volta GPU architecture (of the V100 GPU). Qwen2.5VL is designed specifically to integrate
well with autocast which in our case converts most of the weights to float16 (if run on a V100
GPU). Forcing the model to run fully in float16 or bfloat16 led to garbage output likely due to
some of the weights being highly sensitive.
With these configurations it was possible to run both backbones without gradient tracking using
a batch size of 8 which was deemed efficient enough in terms of VRAM. The wall-clock token limit
for dynamic prediction in model 1 was also deemed alright - especially since the A100 would
always be a viable backup option even if it could not be relied on for the generation of entirety
of results during the project period.
During summertime the A100 GPUs became more available. This was a very welcome oppor-
tunity - partly due to the more VRAM headroom for bigger batches, the increased speed - but
especially for another reason. During main experiment prototyping, we at some point started
to observe Qwen2.5VL generating strange artifacts to varying degree (depending on the case).
These artifacts had not been present in the initial VLM exploration and mainly consisted of the
model (randomly) switching to Chinese, breaking new lines and substituting key words with the
string ‘addCriterion’. The former of these artifacts was most common, and in the beginning we
believed this to simply be a result of the model struggling with structured decoding, causing it
to regress to mimic the training data. No mention of the string was made in the original white
paper or the huggingface hub discussion space (or at least not directly). Later we attributed it
to being a result of quantization. However, in the end enough users had experienced the same
problems as us for a partly Chinese discussion post (originally made in March with a rather vague
description) about it to gain enough traction on huggingface hub for us to stumble upon it when
again searching for a probable cause. Today the bug has been reproduced by many with various
model configurations and GPUs but is still ignored by the owner. The exact cause for the bug
has not been determined but it never seem to occur with a low batch size (which explains why
we did not encounter it during most of the development process). In our case, we found that the
best results were obtained changing the default padding to left-padding (which was for some
reason not used by huggingface) and most importantly only use Qwen2.5VL with flash attention
bfloat16 for bigger batch sizes (all main experiments). The speed up this change delivers result
in an average time per RL environment iteration when evaluating the dynamic step configura-
tion of the V1 model (model setup most likely to be constrained by 1 second time resolution) of
0.43 seconds. This leaves a very comfortable margin considering some of the processing time
might also be attributed to the environment (loading next step).
8.3
VLM Expressive Power and Decoding Strategies
The image caption generation examples showed promising free format semantic reasoning skill
for both image and video. Some experimentation was done with beam search and different
decoding strategies (and hyperparameters). Note that changing from greedy to stochastic sam-
pling should in theory lead to better free format generation (which unbeknownst to us at the
time would be relevant later). Beam search was also experimented with but was mostly kept
out of the report for brevity as it had negligible effect and would only potentially cause greater
resource use (which could potentially become a liability when using the V100 GPU).
The weird bias observed for Qwen in the experiment with color identification with different
prompt phrasings is a bit of a mystery. It might be a training bias or something caused by how
the model pre-processes images before inference (e.g. caused by normalization or something).
I was not able to replicate it with other colors but also invested next to no time in doing so
given the small importance of the discovery. The structured decoding experiment with color
classification, however, was a interesting indicator of the potential of using forced decoding to
overcome the soft guidance (or bias) induced by the training data. In essence the experiment
indicate that even if we tell a LLM/VLM to strictly output a certain format (known to it), it may
be nearly impossible to get it to follow if it is heavily biased toward another representation
through its training. In the experiment this is assumed with RGB code relative to hex color code

for representing colors. Based on soft guidance output alone an normal observer could not be
blamed for doubting whether Qwen had ever seen examples of hex color code in its training
data. However, the results of the forced decoding setup shows that it can reason about hex
color code if forced. This is relevant for our CAPTCHA solver models which require structured
decoding in some format to interact with the digital world as an agent. That being said, the

8.4
CAPTCHA Understanding and the V0 Model
It is now time to discuss whether VLM backbones have the high-level reasoning power to solve
CAPTCHA. Let us first state that this part of the project took considerably longer than expected.
This was not only because of the idiosyncrasies of the specific models chosen (such as both
having major bugs). The process of developing prompts (and corresponding solution state for-
mats) was long with many iterations. It early became clear that the Molmo model’s performance
in structured decoding was subpar compared to Qwen2.5VL. Besides acknowledging the basic
difference in performance that is likely to exist between the two backbones, we also attribute
the difference to the non-optimized quantization of Molmo compared to Qwen (using autocast).
In general Molmo is also just more talkative providing text output that (if allowed) can be mul-
tiples in length of the output of Qwen2.5VL. Molmo is more prone to automatically include CoT
style pondering over the prompt it got (e.g. when asked to identify colors in the initial VLM
exploration, its default behavior was to list common colors first to see if it could match one of
them to what it saw). This often clashes with the short and concise formats expected in our
structured decoding experiments in the report (incl. the V0 model).
Simultaneously, it became clear that it was impossible to optimize the same prompt scheme
to work for both backbones. Consequently, Qwen2.5VL was chosen as the primary VLM to match
prompt engineering to. This is important to consider when comparing backbone performance
as this of course favor Qwen2.5VL. The aim was to obtain prompts for each design that the VLM
backbone would follow even with soft guidance. In other words, the design prompts (and cor-
responding solution formats) should not be too far away from the training bias of both models.
For the reason explained above, this was only possible to achieve for one of the backbones at
a time - in our case Qwen2.5VL (our chosen optimization target).
Refining of the prompt engineering/solution format was done through a number of repeated
tests similar to those presented in chapter 7 as well as through qualitative assessment of addi-
tional CoT output. The CoT output would often reveal misunderstandings of the model indirectly
or directly point out confusion about how to link the solution format to a CAPTCHA screenshot.
The results indicate that the Qwen model is best with soft guidance and outperforms Molmo
(taking into account our discussion above). The result that soft guidance in this case outper-
forms forced decoding is the opposite of what was seen in the (uncertain) initial experiment
with color prediction. This could either be a result of the initial experiment’s low sample size
leading a non representative result or it could be because the format chosen in the V0 experi-
ment soft guidance version is already so close to the training bias format that forced decoding
to a new JSON format actually hurts performance. The reason for the wrong predictions vary
from design to design. In general it is clear that the models struggle with the pointing CAPTCHA.
Molmo is specifically trained to do pointing so it is surprising that it does not perform better.
This might be influenced by a few things such as: (1) 3D CAPTCHA has a semantic layer that might
cause trouble and (2) the pointing format used here does not match the format used for pointing
General CAPTCHA Solver

by Molmo (training bias). We were also rather surprised to see the relatively low performance
on the CLASS CAPTCHA. The higher performance on the MOVE CAPTCHA (structurally similar), as
well as qualitative assessment of CoT output inquiring how to solve the CAPTCHA design, clearly
show that the model does understand the overall concept of how to solve it. We attribute the
problem to poor visual embedding of small objects (does not handle high resolution well). This
hypothesis is supported by the SEG CAPTCHA (also structurally equivalent) and MOVE having
a significantly higher accuracy than the class CAPTCHA. MOVE does not require embedding of
small scale information to solve (the letters displayed in each grid tile is large). Similarly, SEG
shows one large image split into tiles with objects often spreading across multiple tiles (less
frequent to search for small scale objects). Problems with resolution and small scale objects in
VLM agentic reasoning in a digital environment has also been observed by another influential
VLM paper which used it to motivate the development of their model CogAgent [13].
Although, the results look a bit bleak, one should also remember the results with MOVE, CLASS
and SEG as examples for average per tile accuracy across the test set. These (as expected) show
that an overall accuracy for solving the full CAPTCHA is of course not a indicator of performance
on what we in sequential solution of problems would consider as sub-tasks (in this case con-
sidering image tiles in a grid one by one to determine the occurrence of an object). That being
said, if we ended the report on these results, the future for a general CAPTCHA solver powered
by a VLM would look bleak.
Incredulous to the unexpected bad reasoning results above, we attempted to generate solu-
tions in natural text for MOVE, CLASS and SEG. The reason these specific designs were chosen
was only for simplicity as a subset had to be selected due to the time expense required for
manually evaluating natural text solutions against specific formatted solutions. To our disap-
pointment, the result was only a very small increase in performance for CLASS and SEG. The
increase is so small that a p-test/confidence interval calculation shows that we cannot reject
the null hypothesis that the performance is in fact identical. The performance of MOVE increased
a significant amount which might point to the fact that VLM performance on this design is not
limited by vision resolution but core reasoning (specifically being able to link the final state
representation format and a higher level internally known solution). To test this hypothesis
further the CoT follow-up experiment was conducted (with the format interpretation having its
own separate forward pass). Here a significant increase in performance is seen for multiple of
the CAPTCHA types. However, note that it (as mentioned in the results section) does come at a
relatively sharp increase in inference time (at least double as initial state image is used in both
passes while only the prompt is updated). A few informal experiments were done to examine
the potential of a Reflex mechanism. Reflex was considered, as one of the most common errors
made by the backbone in the CoT two pass experiment, was to forget a part of its reasoning (e.g.
when CoT iterated over grid tiles in CLASS) in the second translation pass. The Reflex mecha-
nism was loosely tested with a setup where the backbone was prompted with the chat history
and a new prompt asking if the new solution satisfied the prompt format. However, from all
the examples observed, the Reflex pass would fail to identify any errors. However, with further
developing the Reflex mechanism would still likely be able to aid to some degree - of course at
the cost of the addition of another layer of inference time to the model pipeline.
In conclusion, while SOTA VLM like our backbone models are able to solve some CAPTCHA on
a high-level, they are still far from being the Swiss army knife that the scientific articles docu-
menting them sometimes allude to. The main challenges is processing different scales of image
content, CoT coherence (especially when doing natural language sequential planning and rea-
soning about vision input) and structured decoding. Of course a lot of the designs (if not all) are
zero-shot or few-shot prediction. Finetuning a VLM to a number of designs and evaluating it on
unseen designs would be a natural next step. However, finetuning one of the VLM backbones in
its entirety would require more resources than we have available for this project. Techniques
such as LoRA were considered as an approach to deal with this problem. However, it was deter-
mined (in a discussion with the project supervisor) that it would be better to not prioritize this.
We agree as finetuning would not be especially interesting from a technical point of view (little
engineering required - just a lot of resources and tinkering with hyperparameters). In addition

8.5
The Action Gap and V1 Model
The first challenge in building a model for prediction of trajectories is the necessity of building
a command vocabulary suited for inference. In many ways this vocabulary is the foundation of
the project as its creation induced the need for understanding both the different type of inter-
actions that could be encountered in CAPTCHA (and the digital world in general) as well as the
framework needed to schedule and execute said actions in a live environment. The commands
(and specifically the set of action tokens denoted with prefix ‘A’) are more or less as low level as
they can get. In discussions my supervisor he argued that a simplification could be to to remove
the click action command ‘A_TR_CK’ and instead make the cursor movement action command
include a follow-up click automatically. The decision not to follow this suggestion was primarily
based on our belief that a LAM (which was our main aim to implement - not just any VLM agent)
is to a high degree defined by its action dexterity. In addition, the automatic clicking action
could in principle also be a problem in a number of interactions such as those requiring drag-
ging (even more so when at some point behavioral analysis comes into play in future research).
While we are tempted to state that in hindsight it would have been nice with a less complex
vocabulary, we truly believe that the current complexity of the vocabulary is for the better. All
cursor movements required for the fourteen custom CAPTCHA designs in Coliseum have clicks
following cursor movement. It should be easy (at least in supervised learning) to recognize this
mechanism. In a RL environment where a model is not evaluated on whether a command at
a current step is one of the optimal ones, the boundary between cursor movement and click
action also allow the model to explore. An example could be that the model wants to click on
an specific image tile in the CLASS CAPTCHA. If a cursor movement automatically was followed
by a click, a wrongly placed cursor movement could cause interaction with UI elements which
the agent did not intend to interact with (e.g. clicking the wrong image tile). With the separate
cursor movement and click actions the model can - if run in a dynamic step-by-step configura-
tion in the RL environment - correct its previous action (and in model 2 maybe even use frame
memory to learn from the discrepancy to inform selection of future cursor movement coordi-
nates).
In the end the model 1 was only evaluated in a dynamic configuration - partly to limit scope
of the report and partly because the V0 experiments (as well as previous works documenting
the existing of an action gap) really signaled that the model was likely to struggle (so we chose
the easiest setup of the two). The results show that the V1 model (dynamic version) is actually
able to generate correct trajectories for a large proportion of the trajectory step of each CAPTCHA
(at least when considering the non-universal limited vocabulary ‘V’ configuration). However, the
RL environment based full trajectory accuracies show very low performance. At first we were
concerned that this was due to a bug in our implementation of either the model or RL envi-
ronment. We were prepared that the full trajectory accuracy was likely to be lower for than
the average single step accuracy due to compounding errors. However, the observed results
are a bit extreme and one can also argue that the RL environment could potentially allow the
flexibility of the model to correct its course when a wrong command is issued (as discussed
earlier). Upon closer inspection we found that the dramatic difference is (as expected) caused
by compounding errors - but also (somewhat unexpected) the fact that the V1 model in many of
the CAPTCHA improves its step performance the closer it is to the CAPTCHA goal state. We can
conclude that the action gap exist if using our V0 model benchmark for comparison with the
trajectory accuracy of model V1 (which are the most comparable metrics across the V0 and V1
model).
8.6
Attempting to Build a Custom LAM
The final part of the project did not get to the state we aimed at. The V2 model architecture, BC
training setup and PPO algorithm (with the distance-based reward function) has been imple-
General CAPTCHA Solver
77
==PAGE:90==
mented exactly as described in chapter 4 and 5. All of these facets of the V2 model experiments
have been unit tested and work ‘as intended’. However, getting to a stable training setup was
more challenging than expected. It was hard to reach a model worth including in the report.
This was of course also influenced by the fact that the previous experiments took longer time
than anticipated as well Qwen2.5VL bug being a time waster.
The BC training was for some reason really hard to stabilize. We started with the SEQ CAPTCHA
and never reached the requirements for progressing to the next state in our curriculum learning
scheme. We attempted to train with a bunch of different losses but never found a configuration
that reached more than approx. 50% accuracy before starting to collapse (loss decreased but
so does training accuracy). The collapse would always cause the model to predict the same
token for all time steps within an episode (meaning that predicted tokens does vary but only
across the batch dimension). During initial experiments we had started the curriculum with the
SEQ CAPTCHA, and the same was observed (but faster collapse). It dawned on us that our ‘trick’
where we evaluate the loss as a multi-classification task on the optimal tokens was somewhat
flawed in the context of CAPTCHA with multiple optimal paths (which is a large part of them).
The optimal action ‘list’ used as label describes the optimal actions at a time step. In extreme
cases, such as the SEQ CAPTCHA, there are nine different options for optimal actions (one for
each shown key) in the first autoregressive step. In the second autoregressive step there are 8
optimal actions and so on. This means that if the model can guess a late key in the random order
they are pressed in the trajectory sample, it can through the prediction of that token get a very
low loss (as this token is optimal in many steps). However, the ‘mode collapse’ persisted when
switching to a fully sequential CAPTCHA like SEQ (only one optimal action per step) - although
it occurred at a lower rate. A number of different losses were implemented to encourage varied
token output but none were able to eliminate the problem. Higher scaling was also applied to
the learned position token embeddings in attempt to encourage the model to learn time step
progression as we feared the time embeddings from the VLM encoder drowned out the position
signal from the decoder. As an additional attempt to deal with this we implemented a learnable
global gate for the encoder signal. These changes helped turn the variance of the time dimen-
sion of the logits to get a ‘healthy’ size (before it also collapsed). However, the collapse with
vanishing gradients could still not be avoided.
In regard to RL the Gymnasium environment was successfully implemented and used for evalu-
ation of the V1 model. In training the synchronous vectorized wrapper class was used to attempt
to do PPO with Stable Baselines 3. We had no prior experience framework and it ended up being
extremely rigid to work with - both in regard to the model being a encoder-decoder model and in
regard to decoder being used as both value and policy with different network heads. While the
latter was fixed with some ‘hacks’, the former would require us to split up the model to include
the encoder as part of the reset() function in the RL environment. This would be possible due to
the encoder (in this special case) having frozen weights. However, at this point I found the PPO
implementation to rigid and did not fully trust it even making the mentioned changes. This was
unfortunate and instead we moved to a manual implementation of PPO. Our single environment
PPO implementation was built (and functioning) but a vectorized extension was not finished in
time. Note that many of the new VLM agents (such as [63] and [64]) use direct preference op-
timization (DPO) for RL fine tuning. We simulate the CAPTCHA as a time discrete environment
and have full knowledge of the underlying state. This of course makes it possible to auto-label
the simulated trajectories for DPO. We opted for the more conventional PPO for the sake of
simplicity although it comes with the responsibility of engineering a meaningful reward signal.
Unfortunately we did not end up with a stable trained model (due to time constraints). It would
have been interesting to examine if the value head could be used as an indicator for when it
is time for re-triggering the high-level planning encoder (when things go south). In general we
would also point to our lack of experience with design of servers and websites. We went for the
asynchronous version of playwright but this caused a lot of headaches down the road which
were hard to predict given our limited experience with RL frameworks and building server APIs.

8.7
Final thoughts
It is unfortunate that I did not end up with trained models worth showing at the end. It would
arguably have been better to focus on fewer CAPTCHA designs to have more time for experimen-
tation with the BC and PPO setups. On the other hand I saw the creation of a unique CAPTCHA
data framework with a varied action interface as a central part of the project. In addition, em-
bracing action dexterity is in my opinion central to the new wave of LAM. I would also like to
highlight the amount of research required to write the project beyond studying learning tech-
niques, etc. as the new LAM movement is still quite new (and does not follow a strict definition).

Conclusion
In this report we have shown that while VLM have come far, they still have way to go before
being able to truly generalize to the varied tasks (here in the form of different CAPTCHA de-
signs) that a digital agent encounters. This is at least true when using the models untrained. A
universal CAPTCHA solver might be possible with proper training but we in general struggled to
train a custom LAM without it collapsing. The environment designed for CAPTCHA training and
evaluation can serve as a jumping off point for future research.
