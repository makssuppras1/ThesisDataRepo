Abstract
This thesis is concerned with modelling and optimization of periodic materials which
are strong against buckling instability in the microstructure. A simpliﬁed stability
model based on homogenization theory, linear stability theory and Floquet-Bloch the-
ory is established to predict failure in the microstructure for arbitrary macroscopic load
conditions. Initially, the model is employed to perform stability investigations on the
commonly used square, triangular and hexagonal honeycomb. Results show that they
have non-optimal strength characteristics, and that a large potential for optimization
exists.
A topology optimization problem is then formulated to ﬁnd material microstructures
which exploit the unused potential and facilitate increased strength against buckling
instability in the microstructure. The optimization framework is utilized to design ma-
terials under various load conditions and requirements to isotropic or square-symmetric
material behaviour. For all optimizations, hierarchical type microstructures are ob-
tained which oﬀer large improvements in strength compared to the pre-existing designs
at the cost of minor stiﬀness reductions. In one of the optimization cases, a strength
improvement of 196% is achieved at the cost of only 15% stiﬀness.
Subsequently, it is investigated how the material density inﬂuences the strength poten-
tial associated with using the optimized over the non-optimized microstructures. Re-
sults show that the largest strength potential exist for low material densities, whereas
the potential gradually reduces for increasing material density. The optimization tool
is therefore highly attractive in lightweight material design.
Finally, an overall performance comparison of the pre-existing and optimized mate-
rial microstructures is conducted by mapping their strength characteristics with failure
surfaces in macroscopic principal stress space. Results show that the optimized materi-
als oﬀer consistent and large strength improvements over the pre-existing honeycombs
throughout the investigated load regions.

1 Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In past centuries, strive for improvement has pushed the boundary of technology to an
extend where highly optimized designs utilize the full potential of natural materials.
The continuous search for better solutions has led the rise of engineered metamaterials,
which are materials with artiﬁcially constructed microstructures that defy the natu-
ral limits. Common examples include lightweight metallic foams, micro-lattices and
honeycomb structures which oﬀer superior properties in terms of strength, stiﬀness,
shock absorption and thermal insulation. A characteristic of these materials is that
their eﬀective material properties are highly inﬂuenced by the geometrical layout of
the microstructure. As this parameter is controllable, it can be exploited to craft ma-
terials for speciﬁed needs. This possibility is very lucrative to industries where demand
to performance and material capabilities are high, e.g. in the aeronautic and space
industry. In the past, the limiting factor of metamaterials has been the inability to
fabricate the microstructural patterns with available production methods. However,
recent developments within additive manufacturing and the 3D-printing technology
have destroyed this barrier. Accordingly, these materials have a bright potential and
will inevitably become standard in the future.
Due to the immense potential of metamaterials, it has attracted much interest within
the ﬁeld of research in the last decades. Initial studies were focused on establishing ho-
mogenization models to describe their eﬀective mechanical properties [2], while recent
work has been focused on more complex matters such as scale eﬀects [3] and material
non-linearities [12]. The design aspect has also been a hot topic. Here the method
of inverse homogenization [19] has gained popularity, which uses the mathematical
optimization approach called topology optimization [5] to synthesise the microstruc-
ture such that desired eﬀective mechanical properties are obtained. In speciﬁc, the
method is concerned with ﬁnding the optimal distribution of material and void inside
the microstructure under speciﬁed restrictions of allowable design. Until now it has
been used to create many interesting material microstructures with eﬀective properties
such as negative Poisson’s ratio, extremal bulk modulus, and extremal shear modulus
[20], see Figure 1.1. Although these designs are optimal in the sense of their targeted
elastic behaviour, they have been designed without any restrictions on their strength
capacity. As a consequence, many designs suﬀer from low structural strength which
limits their practical application. Under compressive loading, the limiting factor of
strength is often the occurrence of localized buckling instability, which is a phenom-
ena where the structure experiences loss of stiﬀness and fails. Especially in slender

Some work has gone into characterizing this type of instability, eg. [3, 11, 22], but

in periodic materials, and subsequently make use of topology optimization to design
buckling resistant materials which are stronger than the current state of art.
1.2
Structure of thesis
The thesis is organized as follows. In Chapter 2, a model is presented for predicting lin-
earized buckling instability in periodic media, and then employed to study pre-existing
microstructures. In Chapter 3, the topology optimization problem of the linearized
buckling model is formulated, and the general procedure for numerical implementation
is outlined. Chapter 4 is devoted to material design, and presents the results obtained
from exploring various optimization formulations and design requirements. Finally
Chapter 5 ends the thesis with conclusions and suggestions for future work.

This chapter presents a simpliﬁed model based on homogenization to predict buckling
instability within a periodic material. The model is ﬁrst presented in continuous form,
whereafter it is discretized into a ﬁnite element model for numerical implementation.
Subsequently, it is employed on three pre-existing microstructures to investigate the
fundamental aspects of buckling instability in periodic media.

2.2
Linearised stability model based on homogenization
Modelling of structures composed of periodic media is a complex task due to the
high level of heterogeneity in the material microstructure. A complete description of
the structure requires an immense amount of information, as it must include all the
details of the periodic variations in the microstructure in addition to the macroscopic
material layout. It is apparent that a full analysis including every detail is a very
comprehensive if not impossible task. Therefore, the problem will be reduced by use
of the homogenization method, which is based on the principle of separation of length
scales. In speciﬁc, it assumes that the problem can be separated into two independent
length scales; the microscale to account for small variations over the microstructure,
and a macroscale to account for larger variations occurring over the macrostructure.
By this separation, the modelling is reduced to a problem on the microscale from which
homogenized quantities are derived, and a problem on the macroscale which is based
on the homogenized quantities. The validity of the homogenization approach is based
on the assumptions that the scale of the microstructure is inﬁnitely small compared to
the scale of the macrostructure, and that the microstructure has periodicity of inﬁnite
extend (i.e. no boundary eﬀects). Although these assumptions may be hard to justify
in practice, the model is still expected to provide reasonable approximations, and its
simplicity outweighs other alternatives.
The phenomena of buckling instability in periodic media is inherently a highly com-
plex subject matter due to its non-linear nature. Hence, a simpliﬁed model based on
linearized bifurcation analysis will be employed for predicting instability. The model
will be based on the assumption of small displacements with a linearized strain mea-
sure, where stress stiﬀening is the only geometrically non-linear eﬀect retained. This
simpliﬁcation will disregard the non-linear stiﬀness reducing eﬀects of geometric imper-
fections, which means that the strength predictions of the model will be conservative.
Furthermore, instability modes may develop both on the microscale and macroscale. A
global stability analysis on the macroscale could be carried out using the homogenized
stiﬀness properties, but the present study will be focused only on modelling instability
on the microstructural level. On this level, the buckling modes may develop with many
diﬀerent wavelengths depending on the base geometry and load conditions, spanning
from highly localized cell-periodic modes to non-local long wavelength modes stretch-
ing over several cells. In order to eﬃciently capture all forms of failure in the model,
the Floquet-Bloch method will be used. The method conﬁnes the study to the base cell
whilst imposing a constraint on the spatial periodicity and hence avoids computation-

The Floquet-Bloch method
To model instability spanning over several base cells, the instability equation is sup-
plemented with the Floquet-Bloch condition on the localized modes:
φ(y + Rj) = φ(y)eikT Rj,
j = 1, 2
(2.11)
where k = (k1, k2)T is the wavevector and Rj are primitive lattice vectors which
deﬁne the spatial repetition of the base cell within the microstructure, see Figure 2.2a.
This thesis considers quadratic and rhombic base cells, which have lattice vectors
R1 = (d, 0)T , R2 = (0, d)T and R1 = (d, 0)T , R2 = (d/2,
√
3d/2)T , respectively. The
Bloch-condition links periodicity between the base cells with a spatial harmonic shift
through the complex exponential function with the wavevector k as the free parameter.
Every possible wavelength of the instability modes can then be described by letting k
vary within the non-repeated zone in k-space. This region is deﬁned as the Brillouin-
zone, and is visualized in Figures 2.2b-c for the square and rhombic base cells. A
complete buckling analysis thus entails solving the instability problem for all points
within the unique domain of the Brillouin-zone, and the most critical wavevector will
deﬁne the wavelength of the microstructural failure mode.

2.2.6
Strength characterization by failure surfaces
The macroscopic stress state imposed by external loading may vary depending on
structural application. It is thus evident to employ a universally valid tool for charac-
terising strength against microstructural buckling instability under arbitrary loading.
Such a tool can be used as a performance measure for material designs, but it may also
facilitate the ability to create materials engineered for highly speciﬁc load cases, or to
create robust materials with universal strength. Here the method of failure surfaces
as proposed in [7] will be used. By this method, the onset of failure by bifurcation
is represented with a surface in principal macroscopic stress/strain space for various
orientations of principal loads w.r.t. the initial coordinate system θ, see Figure 2.3.
Load states which are purely bi-axial in the initial coordinate system will thus have
θ = 0, whereas rotation of the material w.r.t. the initial frame or shear loading will
cause θ to become non-zero. Using this principal load approach for load represen-
tation is favourable, as it allows for direct comparison to other failure models like
the von Mises yield criterion for isotropic materials or the Tsai Wu criterion for com-
posites. To identify the failure surface, the principal domains are parametrized by

This chapter outlines the general theory of topology optimization applied to material
design with special emphasis on treating buckling instability. This includes the for-
mulation of the optimization problem, a sensitivity analysis, a ﬁltering technique for
regularization, and a description of the computational model for numerical implemen-
tation.
3.1
Concept of topology optimization
Topology optimization is a mathematical method used for systematically designing
mechanical structures for optimal performance. In speciﬁc, the method is concerned
with ﬁnding the layout of material within a speciﬁed design domain which minimizes
or maximizes an objective function whilst satisfying imposed constraints. This can be
formulated by the following generic design optimization model:

the material layout of the structure can be parametrized into a set of design variables,
including sizing, shape and topology methods. Here the topology method is used, as it
provides complete freedom in the structural topology. This is favourable in the early
design phases where the basic understanding of optimality is still unclear. The method
represents the material distribution using an element-based domain discretization, and
hence constitutes a discrete optimization problem in its basic form. To enable more
eﬃcient gradient-based optimization, the problem must be relaxed. A popular choice is
the density based SIMP-approach [5], which uses element densities as design variables
to interpolate material stiﬀness between the void and solid phase, whilst enforcing a
discrete design through penalization of intermediate densities. Other methods such
as the evolutionary or level-set approach exist, but they will not be considered here.
Solving the optimization problem is an iterative procedure. With oﬀset in an initial
design, gradient information is used in conjunction with an optimization algorithm to
ﬁnd incremental design updates which improves the objective. This process continues

The method of topology optimization has since its introduction by Bensøe and Kikuchi
in 1988 become increasingly popular and developed into its own research ﬁeld. This is
partly attributed to its ability to perform systematic optimization of highly complex
systems, which eliminates the painful approach of trial and error used in conventional
design processes. Furthermore, its general versatility has allowed for widespread appli-
cations within multiple ﬁelds of physics, spanning from its original intend in mechanical
structures to ﬂuid problems and photonics, see [5] for a detailed overview. Common
examples of application include maximizing structural stiﬀness or dynamic properties,
mechanism synthesis, ﬂuid ﬂows and material design. The latter will be elaborated in
detail in the next section.
3.2
Topology optimization in material design
Optimization in material design has been an ongoing ﬁeld of research in the last cen-
tury. Early work was focused on ﬁnding theoretical bounds of the eﬀective elastic
properties, and investigations led to the discovery of multiple microstructures close
to the theoretical bounds of the bulk and shear modulus, see [20] for details. After
the introduction of homogenization theory by Benssousan et al. [2], the topology op-
timization method was developed with oﬀset in numerical homogenization by Bensøe
and Kikuchi [4]. Here, the method was intended as a tool for systematic generation of
optimal composite materials in compliance and dynamic problems. Later Sigmund [19]
used topology optimization formulated as an inverse homogenization problem to ﬁnd
truss-based microstructures with prescribed constitutive properties. The problem was
extended to a continuum type problem in [20], and isotropic microstructures yielding
extremal bulk and shear moduli were found. Subsequently, topology optimization has
been applied successfully to multiple problems in material design, eg. phononic mate-
rials with wave-propagating properties [18], materials with ﬂuid permeability [10], and
more recently materials with prescribed elastic properties under large deformations
[23]. The aspect of stability optimization has, however, received little attention de-
spite its critical importance in lightweight material design. This may be attributed to
the complexity of the subject-matter and the challenges associated with eigenvalue op-
timization, including spurious modes, eigenvalues with multiplicity and computational
expense. Initial studies by Bensøe [3] used shape optimization on a square microstruc-
ture modelled with a beam theory and Bloch-wave analysis to optimize the critical
buckling load. Extension into a continuum type topology optimization formulation
was done by Neves et al. [13], but they only considered macroscopic instability based
on the homogenized stiﬀness. Instability in the microstructure was later addressed in
[15], but the study was retained to highly localized instability modes, thus rendering
the applicability of the designs limited. Accordingly, a full optimization model which
accounts for all types of failure in the microstructure has yet to be developed, and this
constitutes the objective of the thesis.

3.3
Design parametrization
Using the density based approach, the problem is parametrized by assigning a contin-
uous variable ρe ∈[0, 1] to all ﬁnite elements. The variables, which can be regarded as
relative element densities, are stored in the vector ρ, and will be used to interpolate
material properties between a void phase (ρe = 0) and solid phase (ρe = 1) during
optimization. If geometric symmetry conditions are imposed, the material distribution
in the base cell will be determined from a mapping of the fundamental symmetry do-
main as seen in Figure 3.1. In this case, the element densities within the fundamental
domain will be designated as design variables µ, and the remaining elements ρ will be
obtained through the mapping:

the simple relation µ = ρ occurs.
The material properties will be interpolated using the SIMP-scheme, as it has been
successful in many other buckling optimization problems.
However, in its regular
form, the scheme suﬀers from the notoriously known problem of artiﬁcial modes which
pollute the analysis. These are non-physical modes and numerical artefacts of the
interpolation scheme which occur in low density regions, see Figure 3.2. To eliminate
the modes, the linear stiﬀness must remain large compared to the stress stiﬀness with
vanishing density.
Various methods of diﬀerent complexity have been proposed to

3.4
Problem formulation
Instability can be incorporated into an optimization formulation in multiple ways. As
the focus of this thesis is to explore the strength potential of periodic materials, it will
constitute the main objective of the optimization. In more practical applications, other
characteristics such as stiﬀness or dynamic behaviour may have primary importance.
Here, buckling instability would then typically enter as an inequality constraint, where
the critical bifurcation load is required to be above a speciﬁed threshold.

For the case of prescribed strain,

optimization problem has the trivial solution λi →0 for µe →0, i.e. the strain bifur-
cation load is maximized by vanishing the structure. The problem was dealt with in
[15] by considering it as a reinforcement problem where material is added to a ﬁxed
geometry, but this approach restricts the design-space and potentially eliminates supe-
rior solutions. Alternatively, one could impose a minimum stiﬀness requirement with
the homogenized moduli, or introduce a minimum mass constraint. This would eﬀec-
tively eliminate the problem of vanishing structures, while it is also has the advantage
of being an evident constraint in practical applications. A second problem is that
the eigenvalues under strain-loading are not aﬀected by penalization of the current
SIMP-scheme, as matrices K, G are interpolated nearly identically. This implies that
convergence towards discrete designs relies on penalization through active constraints
where penalization is eﬀective, e.g. through a minimum stiﬀness requirement. For
prescribed stress-loading, the above mentioned complications are not present. This is
a consequence of the stress bifurcation load being dependent on the eﬀective stiﬀness,
thus making the SIMP-penalization active directly through the eigenvalues. Accord-
ingly, utilization of stress-loading simpliﬁes the optimization by requiring no additional
constraints to ensure convergence.

3.5

and constraint functions w.r.t. the design-variables to ﬁnd extrema. In the present

a sensitivity analysis of both distinct and multiple eigenvalues which require diﬀerent
methods of evaluation. The sensitivity of the homogenized material properties and
volume constraint is covered extensively in literature, eg. [14], and will thus not be
included. Furthermore, it is noted that indices on λi(kj) are omitted in preceding
derivation to retain clarity.

3.5.3
Multiple eigenvalues
Eigenvalues with multiplicity are commonly encountered in buckling problems with
structural symmetry or in optimization problems where adjacent eigenvalues coalesce.
When an eigenvalue is multiple, i.e. ˜λ = λi = ... = λi+M−1 where M is the multiplicity,
the eigenmode will be a non-unique linear combination of the independent modes φi.
This introduces lack of diﬀerentiability, which complicates the sensitivity analysis.
The problem is dealt with using a perturbation method as presented in [17], and
involves solving a sub-eigenvalue problem for ﬁnding the M physical sensitivities of
the M-tuple eigenvalue for a given design variable perturbation. The method utilizes
directional derivatives in conjunction with a sub-optimization problem for ﬁnding the
best design-perturbation (direction of steepest descent).
Here, a simpliﬁed version
without a sub-optimization is used, where sensitivities are found by individual design
variable perturbations. This approach is only approximative and may result in reduced
convergence rates of the optimization, but it has reduced computational cost and is
easier to implement.

3.7
Computational model
A schematic of the computational procedure which is used to solve the optimization
problem is shown in Figure 3.3. The procedure can be grouped into two parts: 1)
Optimization, which includes the iterative procedure to ﬁnd optimum by incremental
design updates, and 2) Finite element analysis, where the homogenization and sta-
bility problem are solved to obtain objective functions and sensitivities used in the
optimization.
In the optimization procedure, the ﬁrst step is problem initialization. Here, the op-
timization and material parameters are deﬁned, whereafter the ﬁnite element mesh,
ﬁltering matrices and the mapping matrix are constructed. Entering into an iterative
procedure, the design variables are mapped to the full structure whereafter ﬁltering
is applied. Next, the ﬁnite element model is employed to obtain the objective and
constraint functions and their sensitivities. This includes ﬁnding the K most critical
eigenvalues λi(kj), {i, j} ∈S and associated generalized gradients dΛij
km/d ¯ρ.

unﬁltered sensitivities are then obtained by use of the chain rule, and subsequently
mapped to the design domain to get sensitivities with respect to the design variables.
Using the MMA-algorithm, an incremental design update is computed, and the process
is repeated until convergence has been reached, i.e. where the design increment has
reduced below a speciﬁed threshold.

5 Conclusion
The objective of the thesis has been to design periodic materials with improved
strength against microstructural buckling instability by use of topology optimization.
The foundation of the work has been a simpliﬁed model based on homogenization the-
ory, linearized elastic stability analysis and Floquet-Bloch theory in order to predict
instability in the microscale. This analysis proved to be a comprehensive task, as modes
of instability may formate with many diﬀerent wavelengths in the microstructure, and
as each mode requires an individual stability problem to be solved. In speciﬁc, the
process involved ﬁnding the most critical mode of all possible wavelengths spanned in
the Brillouin-zone of the wavevector space.
The model has been employed to investigate buckling instability in three basic mi-
crostructures, including the square, triangular and hexagonal honeycombs. This anal-
ysis served to highlight the fundamental aspects of the stability problem, but also to
establish a benchmark for the optimized designs. These microstructures were found to
be far from optimal, as indicated by the large variation in the critical bifurcation load
over the diﬀerent wavelengths.
Subsequently, the method of topology optimization has been utilized to design mi-
crostructures with improved strength. Here, an optimization problem was formulated
based on prescribed load states of either macroscopic strain or stress. Through a case
study, it was found that both formulations could be used to achieve the same optimiza-
tion goal, but that the stress-based was superior in the cases of maximizing strength.
The developed optimization framework was then employed to optimize materials sub-
ject to various macroscopic load cases and symmetry conditions. With oﬀset in the 3
basic microstructures, topologies were found which yielded signiﬁcant improvements in
strength at the cost of minor stiﬀness reductions. Their optimality was conﬁrmed by a
full stability analysis, which showed large reductions in the variation of the bifurcation
load over the wavevector space. The topologies of the 3 optimized designs were all
found to be hierarchical type structures of the basic counterparts. This result is very
strong, as it may serve as a basis for the development of an analytic model describing
conditions of optimality.
Furthermore, the application of geometric symmetries in the base cell was found to be
very useful. The symmetries facilitate model reduction in the optimization problem in
terms of reduced design space, but also in the stability analysis with a reduction in the
unique domain of the Brillouin-zone. Additionally, they can be used to impose materi-
als symmetries such as isotropy or square symmetry in the homogenized macroscopic
properties.
Investigations were also conducted on the strength-density relations in the basic and

5.1
Future work
Strength optimization of periodic materials by use of topology optimization is a rather
untouched subject matter despite its importance in the advancement of lightweight
materials. Accordingly, a multitude of directions lie ready to be explored in future
work. Some of the most evident extensions are presented below:
• Without additional development of the framework, it would be interesting to tai-
lor highly specialized failure curves. This would involve inclusion of multiple load
cases in the bound formulation, and eventually removal of symmetry constraints
to grant more freedom within the design space.
• Extension into 3D. Increasing the number of dimensions allows for more freedom
and potentially stronger microstructures, and it would be possible to investigate
the eﬀects of open and closed walled cells. However, this extension calls for a fully
parallel implementation as the number of elements in the domain discretization
increase extensively.
• Ensure manufacturability by use of a robust ﬁltering technique.
• Include non-linear eﬀects into the model. As buckling inherently is a non-linear
phenomena, an evident extension would be to extend the model with geometrical
non-linearity. This would enable sensitivity analysis towards imperfections and
determination of post-bifurcation behaviour. Other non-linear phenomena such
as scale eﬀects in the microstructure and non-linear material models would also
be relevant topics to consider.
• Multiscale optimization where both the microscale and macroscale are included
in the design space. This approach allows for the study of true hierarchical type
structures which seemingly are optimal.

P(k)-ﬁelds of optimized topologies for symmetric loading
The optimized topologies have been designed with 45 and 60 symmetries in square
and rhombic base cell, respectively. With symmetric loading, these symmetries are
expected to manifest themselves in the P(k) ﬁelds. This is true for the square cell,
but the triangular and hexagonal appears to be skewed, see Figure B.3. The cause
is related to mesh-dependency in the beams of the stabilizing web. With a thickness
of only 1-2 elements, the stiﬀness becomes dependent on the orientation of the beam,
see Figure B.4. This causes variation in eﬀective stiﬀness of the larger beams they
comprise, which also inﬂuences the strength and thus causes asymmetry in the P(k)
ﬁeld. The eﬀect on the optimization is that the critical value is not found along reduced
search route, and that the actual strength is slightly smaller than predicted (7%). A
workaround would be to optimize along an extended route, or simply to eradicate the
eﬀect completely by increasing the ﬁlter radius such that the minimum length scale
covers more than 1 element. The latter would however require the number of DOF to
be increased to retain the same level of detail in the structure.
