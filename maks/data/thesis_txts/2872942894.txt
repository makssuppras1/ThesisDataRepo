Abstract
In contemporary data science workflows, scripts play a pivotal role in tasks such as data pre-
processing, model training, and evaluation. These scripts, often authored in Python, tend to
evolve rapidly as models are tweaked, techniques are refined, and hypotheses are tested. Despite
their utility, such scripts are typically non-interactive, offering limited support for exploratory
development and iteration.
Interactive computational notebooks have emerged as a partial solution, enabling greater flexi-
bility. However, they introduce significant drawbacks, including out-of-order execution, hidden
program state, and fragmented logic, all of which hinder reproducibility and maintainability.
This thesis investigates whether it is possible to combine the rapid, incremental feedback char-
acteristic of notebooks with the robustness and reproducibility of traditional script-based work-
flows. In particular, it examines the feasibility of incorporating intelligent caching mechanisms to
reduce redundant computation while ensuring semantic correctness—thereby enabling workflows
that are reproducible by design.
To this end, a comprehensive review of existing tools was conducted, alongside the development
of multiple prototype systems to evaluate alternative approaches. From this investigation, four
core requirements emerged as essential to an effective solution: Partitioning, Input Analysis,
Output Tracking, and Foreign Function Interface (FFI) Handling.
Our findings indicate that while several existing tools partially address these requirements, none
satisfy all of them comprehensively. Noteworthy progress has been made in the areas of input
analysis and output tracking, whereas FFI handling continues to pose challenges for which no
definitive solution currently exists.

Introduction
In data science workflows, scripts serve as the backbone of many tasks—powering data prepro-
cessing, model training, and evaluation. These scripts, often written in Python, tend to change
frequently as models are tweaked and evolve, new ideas are tried, and hypotheses are tested.
Despite their central role, these scripts are usually non-interactive, offering little to no support
for exploratory development and experimentation.
To address this, computational notebooks emerged, providing a more exploratory environment.
Now, they have become indispensable tools in data science, education, and scientific research.
They enable users to weave together code, visualizations, and explanatory text into a single,
interactive document. This blend of narrative and computation empowers users to think, ex-
periment, and share more intuitively than traditional script-based workflows. Their dynamic,
self-documenting nature has made them an essential medium for research, prototyping, and
communication. They have, however, also introduced a new class of challenges, particularly
around reproducibility. Imagine rerunning a complex data analysis script and getting entirely
different results—despite nothing seemingly having changed. For many data scientists working
in computational notebooks, this scenario is far too familiar.
Beneath their appealing interface lie well-known limitations. Notebooks encourage out-of-order
execution, leading to hidden state and tangled dependencies. They often lack robust tooling
for version control, testing, and modularization. Dependency management is mostly manual,
making reproducibility brittle at best. A large-scale study analyzed over one million Jupyter
notebooks from GitHub, attempting to execute them to assess reproducibility [1]. Of the valid
notebooks, only 24.11% ran without error, and just 4.03% produced reproducible results—figures
that starkly highlight the scale of the problem.
These issues are compounded by how notebooks are typically used. Another study found that
nearly a quarter of GitHub notebooks contained no explanatory text at all—only code or visu-
alizations. In a closer look at over 200 academic notebooks, they observed that while most de-
scribed their methods, few articulated reasoning or interpreted results. Interviews with notebook
users echoed this theme: notebooks are often regarded as personal, exploratory, and messy [2].
In contrast, Python scripts, when written carefully, are reproducible by design. They enforce a
linear execution order, encourage modularity, and integrate naturally with testing and version
control tools. But as said, they offer little in terms of interactivity or responsiveness. When
working with large datasets or computationally expensive models, rerunning the full script after
a minor change is both inefficient and tedious.
This thesis explores the feasibility of enhancing the traditional script-based workflows with in-
telligent caching mechanisms to avoid redundant computation and improve iteration speed—
without giving up reproducibility. The goal is to answer the question: Can we make scripts just
as responsive as notebooks, while keeping their deterministic, reproducible nature?
While various caching techniques exist in Python—ranging from memoization libraries to reactive
notebook systems—none fully address the complexity of real-world workflows. Simple approaches
often fail to account for mutable state, global variables, external files, or subtle dependencies.
What’s missing is a practical and robust solution that enables script-based development to enjoy
the incremental responsiveness of notebooks without sacrificing reproducibility. This thesis aims
to identify and understand the limitations of current solutions, evaluate promising techniques,
and ultimately prototype a system that addresses these challenges.
To this end, the thesis is guided by the following research questions:
1) What are the current issues of a trivial caching solution?
2) Can any existing techniques be used to fix the problem?
3) What has to be done to make this possible?
Through the design and evaluation of a prototype system, this thesis aims to contribute both
conceptual insight and practical tools toward building more reproducible and efficient Python-
based workflows—whether in notebooks, scripts, or something in between.

Background and Problem Context
As discussed in the introduction, computational notebooks have become a cornerstone in data
science workflows due to their support for interactive, incremental development. Unfortunately,
one of computational notebooks greatest strengths is also its biggest weakness—as they often
suffer from reproducibility and maintainability issues. In contrast, Python scripts—when written
with care—offer better compatibility with testing, version control, and reproducibility, but lack
the immediacy and responsiveness of notebooks.
This trade-off raises a natural question: can we augment script-based workflows with mechanisms
that retain the incremental speed of notebooks, without inheriting their drawbacks? The most
intuitive answer is to cache the results of previously run code and reuse them when inputs remain
unchanged—a technique known broadly as memoization.
At first glance, the idea of caching appears straightforward: hash the input, store the output,
skip recomputation when possible. Python even provides built-in tools like functools.lru_cache ,
and many third-party libraries generalize this pattern. However, in practice, applying caching
to general-purpose Python scripts—particularly in the context of data science and exploratory
programming—turns out to be surprisingly fragile.
Several subtle but critical challenges arise. Scripts may rely on global state, mutate objects, or
depend on the side effects of earlier computations. The order of execution, which is implicit and
enforced in scripts, can also be broken by caching if careless. Additionally, even trivial changes
in a script may invalidate, or even worse, incorrectly not invalidate, the cache or cause silent
inconsistencies if not accounted for.
In this thesis, we refer to these naive or minimally instrumented caching techniques as triv-
ial caching solutions. While they may accelerate isolated or pure-function computations, they
quickly break down when applied to real-world scripts involving dynamic data, file I/O, external
libraries, or impure state.
These limitations suggest that a more robust approach is needed—one that can reason about
dependencies, side effects, and incremental state in a principled way. Before attempting to design
such a system, we must first understand what the current landscape of tools offers, and where
those tools fall short.
To illustrate the fragility of trivial caching, consider the following simplified example using a
global variable and a common data science pattern:
In [1]:
import
pandas as pd
CONFIG = {’drop_na ’: True}
In [2]:
def
analyze_data (df):
...
if CONFIG[’drop_na ’]:
df.dropna ()
...
return
df
In [3]:
df = pd.read_csv (...)
result = analyze_data (df)
Later, the user experiments with different cleaning strategies by updating the configuration:
In [4]:
import
pandas as pd
CONFIG = {’drop_na ’: False}
A trivial caching solution might hash the analyze_data function’s code and its explicit arguments
( df ), but it likely won’t track the mutable global variable CONFIG . As a result, even though the
function’s behavior has changed (via
CONFIG[’drop_na’] = False ), the cache would incorrectly
return the old result where dropna() was applied.

This illustrates a broader problem: real-world Python code, particularly in notebooks, often rely
on global, mutable configuration or state that lives outside function scopes. Without tracking
such dependencies, a caching system risks producing stale or incorrect outputs.
The next section surveys a variety of existing technologies related to incremental computation,
reactive execution, and function-level caching in Python. Our goal is to identify the core mech-
anisms these tools provide, the assumptions they make, and the specific limitations that prevent
them from fully addressing the problem at hand.

Literature Review
In this section we first cover our methodology which outlines how we selected and reviewed prior
research and then we go over our findings. We then examine the current landscape of existing
technologies relevant to our problem space, analyzing their core functionalities and intended use
cases, highlighting the limitations that prevent them from effectively achieving our desired goal.
Finally, synthesize these insights into a list of requirements which are necessary in order to create
a robust solution capable of efficient, incremental execution.
3.1
Methodology
The literature review was a process of both spontaneous discovery and structured searching.
Some of the initial materials were identified by Bolun through exploratory reading and informal
browsing, which served as a valuable starting point. These early sources provided foundational
insights and led us to additional relevant literature by following their citation trails.
To conduct a more systematic search, we used academic databases and search engines such
as DTU Findit, Google Scholar, IEEE Xplore, and ACM Digital Library, focusing on a set
of keywords. These included: caching, memoization, function caching, dependency tracking,
incremental execution, dependency management, computational notebooks, and reproducibility.
These terms reflect the core concepts related to our research focus and allowed us to locate a
broad and diverse set of relevant works.
By combining both exploratory and targeted strategies, we ensured a comprehensive under-
standing of the current research landscape and identified key themes, challenges, and gaps in
the existing literature.
3.2
Findings
Now, we present our findings, categorized by their approaches and goals—acknowledging that
some works span multiple categories—while also highlighting their key strengths and weaknesses.
3.2.1
Reactive Notebooks
Reactive notebooks are interactive environments that automatically track and propagate data
and control dependencies between cells.
Inspired by principles from reactive programming,
these systems ensure that changes to variables or functions automatically trigger re-execution of
dependent cells, maintaining consistency throughout the notebook.
reactivepy
reactivepy is a reactive Python kernel for Jupyter notebooks that automatically tracks variable
dependencies and re-executes affected cells when values change [3]. Each cell is limited to a
single definition (variable, function, or class), and the kernel performs static analysis to infer
dependencies. When a value is updated, any dependent cells are re-executed in the correct order,
enabling a spreadsheet-like programming model without requiring manual re-runs. reactivepy
also supports asynchronous functions, making it suitable for interactive and dynamic workflows.
marimo
marimo is a reactive Python notebook that ensures consistency between code and outputs
through automatic dependency tracking. When you run a cell or interact with a UI element, ma-
rimo determines which other cells depend on the changed values and automatically re-executes
them, or marks them as stale [4].
marimo statically analyzes each cell to determine which global variables it reads and defines.
It then constructs a directed acyclic graph (DAG) where edges represent dependencies: if one
cell defines a variable that another references, an edge connects them. When a cell runs, its
dependent cells are marked for re-execution.
However, it’s important to note marimo does not track object mutations. That means operations
which mutate existing objects–such as my_list.append(42) or my_object.value = 42 –are not treated
4
==PAGE:11==
as changes that would trigger the re-execution of dependent cells. Additionally, marimo requires
that every global variable is defined only once in order to keep code and outputs consistent.
Nodebook
Nodebook is a Jupyter Notebook plugin that enforces strict top-to-bottom cell execution [5],
treating the notebook more like a script. By ensuring each cell depends on all those above it,
Nodebook reduces errors from out-of-order execution and makes notebooks more maintainable
and predictable. It also offers practical benefits: output can be serialized and saved to disk,
allowing notebooks to resume from their last state without re-running all cells. Nodebook’s
output change-tracking makes it easy to modify a variable at the top of the notebook (e.g., a
hyperparameter) and re-run downstream cells, without manually identifying which ones need to
be re-executed.
akernel
akernel is a flexible Python kernel for Jupyter that supports multiple execution modes, including
reactive programming, concurrent execution, and cell caching [6]. In reactive mode, it tracks
variable dependencies to automatically update dependent cells, enabling out-of-order execution
and dynamic dataflows. With execution caching, cells are re-evaluated only when their inputs
change, as inputs and outputs are inferred directly from the cell code.
Some of its limitations are:
stdout / stderr redirection to cell output works only via the print
function, and rich output representations are not yet supported—only the standard __repr__ is
used. Consequently, native Matplotlib figures do not render, though ipywidgets are compatible,
allowing use of tools like ipympl for interactive plots.
IPyflow
IPyflow enhances Jupyter’s default Python kernel with bolt-on reactivity, enabling smarter and
more reliable execution without requiring changes to existing notebooks. Designed as a drop-
in replacement, it maintains full backward compatibility while introducing precise, fine-grained
dependency tracking. IPyflow can infer nuanced relationships between cells—even for operations
like attribute access or subscripting—allowing it to automatically re-execute dependent cells
when upstream values change [7, 8].
A key feature of IPyflow is its ability to persist cell dependency information directly into note-
book metadata. This enables seamless recovery and reproducibility: after restarting the kernel,
users can jump to and execute any cell, and IPyflow will ensure that all necessary upstream
computations are executed first. This supports a more flexible and robust interaction model,
where users are no longer constrained to linear or manual cell execution order.
Despite its strengths, IPyflow has limitations. It does not currently track side effects that occur
outside the Python runtime, such as system calls or file I/O. As a result, changes to the external
environment may not trigger re-execution of affected cells.
3.2.2
Checkpointing Notebooks
Checkpointing notebooks represent a broad class of interactive computing environments that
support restoring the computational state to a previous point in the notebook’s execution history.
This capability enables powerful exploratory and iterative workflows by allowing users to “rewind”
the state of the notebook, replay earlier computations, and explore alternative execution paths.
Multiverse Notebook
Multiverse Notebook is a computational notebook engine designed for time-traveling explo-
ration [9]. It lets users navigate to any prior execution state and branch off with new code—while
preserving strict state isolation—enabling safe, intuitive experimentation without affecting past
or parallel states.
To support efficient branching, Multiverse uses a copy-on-write mechanism that creates lightweight
snapshots, deferring memory duplication until modifications occur. It also employs an aggressive
5
==PAGE:12==
tenuring strategy, promoting objects in the garbage collector to prevent premature collection and
maintain snapshot stability across branches.
Multiverse adopts a pragmatic stance on file I/O, treating it as outside the scope of checkpointing.
Capturing side effects like file writes would require costly OS-level sandboxing, and ruling them
out entirely is impractical due to common use cases like logging. Instead, Multiverse assumes
that users manage file I/O explicitly.
Fork It
Fork It enhances computational notebooks by enabling users to create and manage independent
execution states, allowing exploration of alternative analyses without overwriting prior work [10].
It supports both proactive forking and retroactive backtracking to earlier states by serializing
the interpreter state using dill1.
The prototype integrates into Jupyter with a side-by-side UI for forks but is currently limited
to one fork at a time. It also suffers from performance bottlenecks due to saving full execution
state after each cell, which can be costly for large datasets.
ElasticNotebook
ElasticNotebook is a plugin which enables live migration through a novel checkpointing and
restoration mechanism [11]. By monitoring cell executions, ElasticNotebook analyzes the run-
time behavior, variable sizes, and dependencies between variables and cells. This enables it to
generate a precise replication plan which makes it possible to reconstruct the full session state
on another machine or environment.
Despite its robustness and efficiency, ElasticNotebook has several limitations. It does not handle
non-deterministic operations (e.g., random number generation or time-based functions) though
they do comment that extending ElasticNotebook with annotations such that variables or cells
are enforced to copy instead of recomputing is possible. The system also lacks automatic detec-
tion of side effects—cells that interact with external resources (e.g., file systems, APIs) may be
re-executed unsafely during restoration. Additionally, deserialization failures may occur due to
incomplete or incorrect implementations in third-party libraries; while ElasticNotebook falls back
to recomputation when possible, restoration fails if neither deserialization nor recomputation is
possible.
Kishu & Kishuboard
Kishu enables time-traveling between notebook states using a fault-tolerant, efficient system
of incremental checkpointing and checkouts [12]. By capturing changes at a novel Co-variable
granularity, Kishu preserves complex inter-variable dependencies with minimal overhead. When
checking out to another state, it calculates the precise difference between the current and tar-
get states to perform an incremental checkout. Complementing Kishu, Kishuboard allows users
to intuitively manage both code and data state of computational notebooks in a flexible way,
achieving both execution rollbacks and checkouts across complex multi-branch exploration his-
tory [13].
Kishu utilizes fallback recomputation when the state-delta transformation is not possible. When
a co-variable cannot be stored in a checkpoint, Kishu is able to efficiently reconstruct the target
state using fallback computation by finding the shortest path according to the checkpoint graph.
Kishu guarantees exact restoration for all serializable Co-variables with respect to bytestring
representation if there are no hidden serialization errors. Kishu uses cloudpickle2 with dill as a
fallback for object serialization, so similarly to ElasticNotebook, if a Python object’s __reduce__()
or serialization logic is incorrect or silently lossy, restoration may be flawed.
While Kishu’s
fallback recomputation restores some problematic Co-variables, exactly restoring Co-variables
that both fail to store/load and are created non-deterministically (e.g., random generators) is
currently unsupported.

3.2.3
Function Memoization
Function memoization refers to the automatic caching of function results to avoid redundant
computation. When a memoized function is called with the same inputs, the system returns a
previously computed result instead of re-executing the function. This technique is particularly
well-suited to interactive and exploratory workflows, where functions are often invoked repeatedly
with identical or similar inputs.
mandala
mandala is a Python library that simplifies scientific data management and incremental comput-
ing by shifting away from traditional logging methods. Instead, it uses memoization of function
calls as the core mechanism for saving, loading, querying, and deleting computational artifacts.
Through compositional memoization, mandala tracks how memoized functions interact, effec-
tively memoizing complex computations end-to-end and creating rich computational graphs [14].
In addition to supporting standard function memoization, mandala enables transparent memo-
ization of Python’s native collections—such as lists, dictionaries, and sets—via customized type
annotations (e.g., MList[int] , inheriting from List[int] ).
However, mandala currently has some limitations. It does not handle external I/O operations
(e.g., file or network access), and it lacks fine-grained invalidation for mutable objects: if one
member of a dictionary or list changes, the entire structure is treated as modified, potentially
triggering full recomputation of dependent functions.
IncPy
IncPy is an enhanced Python interpreter that automatically memoizes pure function calls to
support incremental recomputation [15, 16]. It does this by tracking a rich set of dependencies—
including function arguments, return values, global variables, file reads and writes, and the
bytecode of the function and all transitively called functions. Additionally, it captures stdout
and stderr separately during execution and replays them when a function is skipped, preserving
observable behavior.
When a function is called, IncPy first checks for a matching cache entry based on the function’s
full name (including file and class context), its arguments, and global dependencies. If no match
is found, the function is executed normally. If a matching entry exists, IncPy validates that none
of the relevant files or code dependencies have changed. If any dependency has been updated or
deleted—for example, if a transitive callee’s bytecode has changed—the cache entry is invalidated
to avoid incorrect reuse. If all dependencies are unchanged, IncPy skips the function call, replays
the captured terminal output, and returns the cached result, effectively emulating the original
execution much more quickly.
To improve robustness, IncPy atomically saves each cache entry to disk immediately after a
function returns, rather than waiting until program termination. This design allows developers
to recover gracefully from crashes: upon re-execution, IncPy can skip all previously memoized
calls up to the point of failure.
The main limitation of IncPy is its inability to track impurity, dependencies, or non-determinism
in non-Python code such as C/C++ extensions or external executables.
memo
memo is a Python library that is targeted towards speeding up financial computations by caching
the results of function calls—both entire computations and reusable subproblems—to disk [17,
18].
By avoiding redundant recalculations, especially in iterative workflows, it significantly
reduces runtime and improves productivity. It offers fine-grained control over hashing, equality
checks, I/O, and storage, and supports divide-and-conquer memoization in certain workloads,
making it particularly effective for large, repetitive tasks.
joblib
Joblib is provides a set of tools which enables lightweight pipelining in Python. In particular:
transparent disk-caching of functions and lazy re-evaluation (memoize pattern), and easy simple
7
==PAGE:14==
parallel computing [19]. It is commonly used in data science and machine learning workflows
to speed up repeated computations and manage large data. joblib can persist objects to disk
using an optimized pickling mechanism, which is faster for large numpy arrays compared to the
standard pickle module.
DiskCache
DiskCache is a Python library which provides persistent (on-disk) storage for caching results of
function calls or storing data between runs [20]. It’s designed to be fast, simple, and reliable,
making it particularly useful when your application deals with expensive computations or data
retrieval operations that you don’t want to repeat unnecessarily.
3.3
Gaps and Limitations
Despite the diversity of approaches in the tools we reviewed—from reactive notebooks to function-
level memoization—none fully satisfy the needs of reproducible, efficient, incremental script
execution. Each falls short in one or more of the following key areas:
• Global State Awareness: Many tools fail in (or simply do not consider) tracking or
responding to changes in global variables or configuration which can lead to incorrect
results either immediately or at a later point.
• Filesystem Awareness: Changes to input files or external resources often go undetected
unless explicitly modeled.
• Fine-Grained Dependency Tracking: While not strictly incorrect, adding granularity
to track dependencies at the attribute or nested level could significantly improve accuracy,
enabling more precise invalidation and reducing unnecessary recomputations.
• Side-Effect Modeling: Some tools assume pure functions (or leave it up to the user to
know), but real-world code has side effects—e.g., writing to disk, modifying globals—that
are invisible to trivial caching.
• Serialization Fragility: Results are often cached using serialization mechanisms (e.g.,
pickle), which can break across code changes or fail for non-trivial objects.
These limitations highlight the absence of a practical, robust solution that unifies responsiveness,
correctness, and reproducibility. Addressing these shortcomings informs the system requirements
we outline next.
3.4
System Requirements
Through our study of existing tools and techniques, we distill four core requirements for building
a system capable of efficient, incremental execution in Python:
1. Partitioning: The system must partition a program into chunks—units of execution that
can be reasoned about independently. In notebooks, the obvious choice is code cells; in
scripts, the equivalent might be function bodies, top-level statements, or syntactic blocks.
2. Input Analysis: To determine whether a chunk needs to be re-executed, the system
must analyze its inputs. This includes function arguments, variable dependencies, and any
external state read by the chunk. Without accurate input tracking, the system risks stale
or incorrect results.
3. Output Tracking: The system must identify and/or store the outputs of each chunk.
This might involve capturing return values, tracking mutated global state, or persisting
file outputs. Output tracking is essential both for avoiding redundant computation and for
restoring the correct program state on re-execution.
4. FFI Handling: Many Python programs rely on libraries written in C or C++, such as
NumPy, pandas, or PyTorch. These often include foreign function interfaces (FFIs) that
bypass Python’s runtime, making it difficult to observe state changes from the outside. A
robust system must either account for or safely isolate such effects.

4
A Framework over Existing Work
In this section, we propose a framework for categorizing the previously examined technologies
based on the system requirements. We extract their design approaches, with the aim of identi-
fying how they relate to our overarching goal: achieving the fast, incremental responsiveness of
notebooks without sacrificing reproducibility.
4.1
Defining the Framework
To meaningfully categorize and compare existing solutions, we need a shared lens—a framework
grounded in the core requirements. This framework is structured around our four key require-
ments. In the following, we look each requirement and examine—at a high level—the range of
design choices available.
Partitioning
The first requirement, partitioning, is relatively straightforward: by dividing a program into
discrete chunks and associating cached information with each, we enable selective reuse when
the program changes. This partitioning can be approached either statically or dynamically
(Static) Partition : P →p0, p1, ..., pn
(Dynamic) Partition : S × P →S′ × p × P ′
where:
• P is the original program,
• pi denotes a chunk of the program,
• S is the current state,
• S′ is the updated state after extracting a chunk p,
• and P ′ is the remaining program after extracting p.
Input Analysis
The second requirement is input analysis which is the key to making the subsequent script
executions run faster.
By analyzing the chunk’s input and cross-checking with the cache it
should determine how to execute the chunk.
We define the input analysis function as:
AI : p × S →I
where I is the extracted input slice relevant to chunk p in the current state S.
Here are some examples of the shape of I:
• Dirty bit(s): A flag or assertion that indicates whether the state relevant to the chunk is
valid or has been modified since the last execution, based on some bookkeeping data.
• Full state: A complete state comparison to determine if the chunk has previously executed
with this exact state.
• Slice of state: A partial state comparison that checks whether the chunk has run with a
specific subset of the current state.
• All-accepting: An unconditional approach where the execution strategy is selected regard-
less of the current state.
Input comparison techniques vary in complexity and precision, with the most common including:
• Value comparisons: Directly comparing the input values.
9
==PAGE:16==
• Hashing: Comparing hashes of the input values.
• Indexing: Utilizing values produced by a chunk as indices to reference cache entries.
• Bit: Marking changes in the state with bits that denote whether particular aspects of the
state are valid or invalid.
Given a chunk p and current state S, input analysis yields an input slice I = AI(p, S). The
cache is then consulted to select an execution strategy:
selector : I × C →ES
A simple sketch of this process might look like:
I ←AI(p, S)
if I ∈C then
return C[I]
else
return Normal
end if
Execution Strategy
An execution strategy describes how to obtain a chunk’s output. It can be modeled as:
p × S × C × ES →O
where O is the resulting output.
These are the execution strategies that we have identified:
• Nothing: Check nothing, do nothing.
• Replace: If we saved the entire state after running the chunk, replace it.
• Update: If we saved the effects of running a chunk, apply them.
• Pure: If we saved the chunks return value, return it.
• Trace: If we saved any trace-operations, execute them.
• Compile: If we compiled or transformed a version of the chunk, execute it.
During the initial run, no cached strategies exist yet, so execution must default to a "normal"
execution which could then populate the cache and enable one or more of the defined strategies.
There exists a loose hierarchical ordering among the execution strategies in terms of control over
the state i.e. expressive power:
Nothing < Pure < Replace < Update < Trace < Compile
The hierarchy can be understood as follows:
• The Nothing strategy has control over nothing, since it neither reads nor modifies the
state.
• The Pure strategy allows control over a single value, returning a cached result without
modifying the broader state.
• The Replace strategy operates at a coarser granularity by replacing the entire state with
a previously saved snapshot.
• The Update strategy subsumes both Pure and Replace, as it enables selective modification
of values in the state, effectively mimicking the capabilities of both preceding strategies.
10
==PAGE:17==
• The Trace strategy extends beyond Update by not only applying state modifications but
also by replaying arbitrary trace operations, which may include control flow and side-effect
management.
• Finally, the Compile strategy encompasses the functionality of Trace while introducing
the ability to execute a specialized, potentially optimized “smaller” program representation.
This can be viewed as a form of partial evaluation, where the chunk is precompiled to
improve execution efficiency.
Additionally, one must pay careful attention to the preconditions before applying an execu-
tion strategy. These preconditions ensure that the execution remains valid and yields results
equivalent to those of a normal program run. The preconditions are as follows:
• Nothing: Neither the input nor the output slice of the program state has changed since
the last execution.
• Replace: The entire state must be identical to its last execution.
• Update: The input slice must be identical to its last execution.
• Pure: The input slice must be identical to its last execution and the chunk must not have
side-effects.
• Trace: If the trace depends on control-flow values, then they must be identical to its last
execution.
• Compile: The compilation-dependencies must be identical to its last execution.

4.3
Granularity
Granularity concerns the size and scope of the code segments (chunks) when partitioning the
script.
Each level of granularity comes with its own benefits and trade-offs.
The finer the
granularity, the more nuanced the control and potential reuse—but often at the cost of complexity
in both tracking dependencies and effects.
12
==PAGE:19==
4.3.1
File
At the highest level of granularity, the file is treated as a single chunk. This is the simplest
model to reason about since the entire program is processed in one pass.
4.3.2
Checkpoints
Next, we have checkpoints which divide the file into two parts at each checkpoint: what can be
assumed to have already run, and the remaining part of the file which has not run yet. The
benefit of checkpoints is that we can assume that the state at the checkpoint is the same as
having run the file up until that point.
4.3.3
Cells
In notebooks, code is naturally segmented into cells, making them a convenient unit for execution.
The main problem with cells in notebooks is that execution is not required to be linear. A cell
can reference variables or definitions from later cells or rely on state mutated in a non-linear
order, which complicates things. Despite this, cells remain a practical level of granularity due
to being directly visible and interactive to the user.
4.3.4
Functions
It is reasonable to chunk based on functions since they are well-defined and straightforward
to work with. Functions encapsulate logic, have a clear interface via parameters and return
values, and often represent meaningful units of computation. However, side-effects and reliance
on external state can make their boundaries fuzzy.
4.3.5
Blocks
The block level describes some form of arbitrary chunking of code beyond just functions, such as
basic blocks or sequences of statements within a function. While the approach may offer more
fine-grained control than functions, it also requires defining these chunks in a meaningful way
which and it would probably also introduce new complexities, especially in regards to sensible
tracking of input and output.
4.4
Input
Input analysis determines whether a given chunk of code should be re-executed by evaluating
whether its dependencies (i.e., inputs) have changed.
The granularity and strategy of this
analysis plays a central role. In general, the more precise the input tracking, the more effectively
a system can avoid redundant computation.
4.4.1
Dirty Bit(s)
The dirty bit(s) are essentially a way to mark the state/input—in its entirety or specific parts
of it—valid/invalid. Depending on if the bit(s) mark the whole state as invalid or only parts of
the state, it can be seen as both more course-grained or fine-grained than Full State.
4.4.2
Full State
A more precise but computationally heavier strategy is comparing the entire input state. This is
typically implemented by serializing the relevant data and computing a hash. It requires that the
input is deterministically serializable and may introduce overhead for large or complex inputs.
4.4.3
Slice of State
Next, we have state slicing which aims to identify only the relevant subset of the state that a
chunk depends on. Slices can be derived using a variety of techniques, including static, dynamic,
or hybrid approaches that combine both.
13
==PAGE:20==
4.4.3.1
AST Analysis
One approach is to statically analyze the program’s AST to determine which variables are
referenced but not defined locally. This enables the system to conservatively estimate the input
dependencies of a chunk without requiring runtime information.
4.4.3.2
AST Transformations
This technique extends static AST analysis by transforming the code to embed additional in-
strumentation. Each variable or expression of interest can be decorated or wrapped to carry
metadata about usage, enabling symbolic tracking of non-local dependencies. This allows the
system to resolve values that are otherwise ambiguous or dynamically scoped.
4.4.3.3
Lineage and Liveness Tracing
A dynamic approach involves tracing the execution of the program to capture when and how
variables are defined and used. This includes capturing line-by-line execution, function calls and
returns, and exception flows. The system can then assign timestamps or version tags to symbols
and maintain a dependency graph, ensuring that any chunk is only considered valid if all its
dependencies are up-to-date.
4.4.3.4
Lazy Reachability Detection
Another technique is to track the transitive reachability of values through global references.
Since global variables often serve as entry points to wider structures (e.g., objects or lists), it is
possible to infer dependencies by observing which values become accessible through attribute or
item access. By maintaining a set of reachable addresses and their provenance, the system can
approximate a chunk’s dependency footprint without explicit instrumentation.
4.4.3.5
Dynamic Tracing
In systems that treat functions as first-class computational units, input dependencies can be
tracked dynamically at the call level. This typically involves hashing function arguments and
recursively tracing calls to other functions, thereby constructing a call graph or trace tree.
4.4.4
All-accepting
At the final level we can run the chunk regardless of state. There is no analysis on the input
which in turn means no restrictions on the input i.e. an all-accepting input.
4.5
Output
Output tracking refers to identifying and potentially storing the effects of running a chunk.
Understanding the output is critical for re-use—if the whole output is not captured, then we
cannot guarantee that re-using it is the same as executing the chunk normally.
4.5.1
State Replacement
One approach treats each chunk as a full transformer of program state. After execution, the
entire state is saved and replaced upon reuse. This is a coarse-grained but conceptually simple
approach, as long as every value in the state can be reliably stored and restored.
4.5.1.1
Process Snapshotting
A specific form of state replacement involves process-level snapshotting, such as using operating
system features like process forking. Each chunk creates a new process, and state is preserved by
maintaining the forked process tree. This allows complete restoration of memory, environment,
and runtime context.
4.5.2
Partial State
A more efficient and targeted strategy involves tracking only the parts of the state modified by
a chunk. This requires identifying which variables or objects were written to, allowing selective
restoration.
14
==PAGE:21==
4.5.2.1
Incremental Checkpointing
Instead of saving the full or partial program state, this technique captures only the minimal
information needed to reconstruct the state after a chunk executes. This includes storing which
variables changed and what steps are needed to re-derive them, often using dependency graphs
and execution logs.
4.5.2.1.1
State Reconstruction
One approach builds a graph structure mapping code chunks to the variables they define or use.
Checkpoints consist of only the relevant variables, associated code, and optional cost metrics
(e.g., execution time, memory usage). The system can then efficiently plan a state restoration
by selecting which chunks to rerun and which variables to reload.
4.5.2.1.2
Delta-Efficient Checkpointing
Delta-efficient checkpointing saves only the differences (deltas) between states. This involves
grouping interdependent variables (e.g., those sharing references) and versioning them together.
The result is a compact checkpoint graph that supports fast and incremental state restoration.
4.5.3
Pure
Result of running the chunk is returning some value and determining if the run changed the state
in any way i.e. if the execution was pure or had side-effects. If a function is pure, its output
depends only on its inputs, making it ideal for memoization.
4.5.4
Trace Replay
This approach involves recording a trace of the operations performed during chunk execution.
On re-execution, the system replays the trace and optionally asserts that the behavior (including
side effects and control flow) matches the original. This offers a balance between correctness and
performance but can be sensitive to changes in the runtime environment.
4.5.5
Compilation
Compilation-based strategies convert a chunk into a specialized executable representation that
can be run independently.
This is conceptually similar to partial evaluation: known inputs
are embedded at compile-time, while dynamic inputs are provided at runtime. Compilation can
encapsulate the behavior of several other strategies and is useful for optimizing frequently reused
computations.
4.5.6
Normal Execution
No special output tracking is performed; the chunk is just executed. This is the default and
simplest mode where no caching or memoization is attempted. While it avoids complexity in
verifying correctness, it also forfeits potential performance benefits.
4.6
Foreign Function Interface
Interfacing with external code—particularly through FFIs like C/C++ extensions, system calls,
or third-party libraries—poses a significant challenge for incremental execution and caching sys-
tems. These interactions often fall outside the purview of Python-level instrumentation, making
it difficult to observe their behavior, track their side effects, or determine whether their outputs
remain valid. Broadly speaking, existing solutions tend to fall into the following strategies.
4.6.1
Disabling Instrumentation or Caching
In many cases, systems simply turn off instrumentation or caching around known opaque or
non-deterministic boundaries. This avoids incorrect reuse of stale results but sacrifices potential
performance gains in affected areas. An example of this could be a memoization system which
allowed decorators or context managers to explicitly mark a chunk (or even a part of a chunk)
as non-cacheable or side-effect-prone.
15
==PAGE:22==
4.6.2
DSLs or Annotations
Some more advanced systems provide a domain-specific language or annotation syntax that gives
users manual control over how specific edge-cases are treated. These might include hooks for
describing purity or invalidation logic in case cached values are missing or inconsistent. This
approach shifts the burden to the developer but allows fine-tuned control when necessary.
4.6.3
Fallback Re-execution
A common safety net is to fall back on full or partial recomputation when a cache lookup fails,
or when the system detects an inconsistency it cannot resolve automatically. For instance, if an
external call relies on native state that cannot be serialized or replayed, the system may opt to
rerun the original computation path.

Evaluation
To assess how close we are to our goal—reducing runtime while re-executing a script while
preserving reproducibility—we evaluate both performance and correctness. Performance is rel-
atively straightforward to measure: we record the execution time of each script version across
runs.
Correctness, however, is more challenging. It requires ensuring that the output produced by
an incremental run is identical to that of a full, normal execution.
This includes all effects
such as console output, generated plots, and any produced artifacts.
Because verifying this
exhaustively is quite tricky, we did not initially implement automated correctness checking in our
benchmark runner. Instead, we focused on manual inspection: we observed whether executions
completed without errors and whether the console output matched. Our primary focus remained
on measuring performance gains.

its successors by using its state replication algorithm.

mandala

IncPy

our core goal—incremental execution baked directly into Python’s interpreter. This approach
promised an efficient, “hands-off” way to re-execute scripts incrementally. Unfortunately, there

Python 2. Given the vast differences between Python 2 and the modern Python 3 environment
we used for our benchmark suite, adapting IncPy was effectively infeasible. As a result, despite
its promising design, there was no practical way to test it with our benchmarks.

Solution Prototyping
As we have seen regarding all the existing work—none of it aligns perfectly with our goal and our
attempts to massage the solutions did not bear fruit the way we had hoped. In this section we
cover the work we have tried ourselves. The approach has been heavily guided by our benchmark
suite—I guess you could call it benchmark driven development.
6.1
Granularity
When the project began initially, we quickly set our focus on the granularity of functions since
this was something that was very familiar and it would be easy to get started. While we were
confident that this choice was solid, we also considered alternatives, especially because so much
of the related work we found was based upon notebook code cells.
This made us question
whether a solution with arbitrary chunks could make sense, but we ultimately decided against
exploring this idea further—It seemed like it would require a lot of thought to come up with
an algorithm that could partition a program into arbitrary chunks in a way which would have
greater potential regarding performance in re-execution. Thus, we settled upon continuing our
work with functions as our granularity.
6.2

object wrapping, and AST instrumentation. It is important to note that all three approaches are
still in progress and remain incomplete. As such, the results presented should be interpreted as
preliminary and may not fully represent the performance or behavior of their eventual, finalized
implementations. I would also like to emphasize that my primary contributions have been focused
on the bytecode strategy and to a lesser extent the object wrapper.

regards to reads and writes. The dependency analysis in combination with the input-checker
have to be precisely coordinated in order to guarantee that the execution strategy is selected

track the stack state at every step, simulating the effects of each instruction as precisely as
possible.
With that in mind, we began developing the bytecode interpreter. This required building a
custom virtual machine—an execution engine that could simulate Python bytecode while also
incorporating the additional logic needed to trace global variable interactions. We implemented
as many instructions as possible, prioritizing those most relevant to our analysis goals—especially
those that manipulate the stack, control flow, or access global state.
One of the major sources of complexity was supporting analysis of nested function calls, which
introduced significant challenges due to Python’s flexible argument binding semantics. When a
function is called, Python handles argument matching using a sophisticated set of rules involving
positional arguments, keyword arguments, default values, and even variadic parameters like
*args and
**kwargs .
Accurately replicating this behavior in our interpreter was non-trivial.
This was further complicated by the fact that we had to implement a custom Frame class to
represent execution contexts within the virtual machine. Mapping arguments to local variables
in this frame–especially in a way that respects Python’s own binding logic–turned out to be
surprisingly difficult.
Handling these variations turned into a long series of special cases and edge-case logic, much of
which had to be discovered empirically. Through trial and error, we gradually built up a set of
handling rules that allowed the interpreter to deal with most function calls.
Later, we discovered a subtle but critical gap in our analysis: functions that modified the ‘self’
object or other mutable arguments were not being tracked correctly. What was missing was an
additional mechanism in our interpreter’s context to record changes to mutable objects which
came from the frames local variables.
By integrating this tracking of local mutations alongside our existing global read/write analysis,
we significantly increased the fidelity of both our input and output analyses. That said, our
analysis is still a work in progress. There remain functions whose internal/external changes we
cannot fully track. To maintain overall robustness, we have implemented a blacklist that excludes
these problematic functions. This pragmatic approach allows us to focus on the majority of
functions where our interpreter can provide confident and precise insights.

6.2.2
Object Wrapper
We employed the wrapt5 library as the foundation for implementing our object-wrapping mech-
anism. wrapt offers a robust ObjectProxy class, which served as the base for our custom proxy
wrapper. This design allows us to create a transparent wrapper around an object, such that
any interactions with the proxy—whether method calls, attribute accesses, or mutations—are
seamlessly forwarded to the underlying wrapped object.
The primary motivation behind adopting an object wrapper was to enable fine-grained intercep-
tion of attribute access and mutation operations. Specifically, we aimed to monitor and record
all attribute reads and writes performed on the target object. To achieve this, we extended
the behavior of the proxy by overriding the special methods __getattribute__ and __setattr__ .
These method hooks allowed us to capture every instance in which an attribute was accessed or
modified, providing the necessary insight into the data flow within the wrapped object.
By ensuring that all inputs to a function were wrapped with our proxy, we were able to build
a pretty complete picture of when data was read or written. At this stage, our focus shifted
toward resolving nuanced edge cases and ensuring the robustness of the system across diverse
scenarios.
One of the challenges we encountered was the issue of inadvertently double-wrapping objects.
Re-wrapping an already proxied object could lead to confusing behavior, redundant tracking, and
performance degradation. To address this, the __new__ method of our proxy checks whether the
target object is already wrapped—either by type or via a cached entry in a WeakValueDictionary .
This ensures that each object is wrapped at most once, and that previously wrapped instances
are reused.
Additionally, we attempted to extend our tracking mechanisms across modules, including third-
party and foreign modules.
This, however, introduces significant complexity.
Passing in a
wrapped object to a C function that expects the unwrapped object will almost certainly result
in a crash. Furthermore, some in-python modules rely on strict identity checks—using is or
id() —which is a problem since we cannot instrument ‘is’-checks through the wrapper.
6.2.3
AST Instrumentation
As our object-proxy approach became increasingly more difficult to improve—particularly due to
its considerable runtime overhead—we began exploring an alternative based on AST instrumen-
tation. For this, we leveraged Pyccolo6, a library for declarative source-level instrumentation
in Python. Pyccolo allows users to specify what to instrument—such as variable accesses or
function calls—while abstracting away the how. Because it operates at the source-code level
rather than bytecode, it offers greater portability across Python versions and provides a cleaner
integration path for source-based transformations.
The instrumentation was done on a per-function basis, but it would also be possible to instrument
the entire program prior to execution. That would allow us turn on instrumentation selectively
for any function being called. Although this approach introduces some pre-execution overhead,
it also provides a more principled way to handle code fragments where source information is
otherwise unavailable, such as lambdas, constructors, or functions decorated with C-based built-
ins.
We quickly got the AST-based dependency tracking to a functioning level, but there are yet
challenges that we need to overcome. Notably, changes to external module-level state are not
tracked, and modifications to C-level state—such as triggering the garbage collector—remain
invisible to our instrumentation. In addition, state changes from C-builtins that invoke Python
callbacks (like constructors or special methods) are not captured, instead we attempt to detect
changes indirectly by diffing the arguments.

6.3
Caching Mechanism
Now that we have outlined our approaches to input analysis and output tracking, we turn our
attention to the caching mechanism. In this section, we describe our strategies for serialization
and hashing, as well as our choice of storage for our cache.
6.3.1
Serialization & Hashing
To efficiently convert arbitrary Python objects into a binary representation suitable for indexing
the cache, we implemented a custom converter function. This function uses a cascading strategy
which tries a series of conversion methods. The goal was to maximize robustness while minimizing
performance overhead.
The function begins by attempting to deterministically extract the object’s buffer interface
where applicable—this is the most direct method for simple data structures like byte arrays
or memory views. If that fails, it sequentially falls back to a set of increasingly general (and
increasingly expensive) serialization methods: marshal , pickle , and finally cloudpickle. In worst-
case scenarios where none of these methods are applicable, it resorts to encoding the object’s
repr or falling back to Python’s built-in hash . While these final steps allow serialization to
proceed in extreme cases, they are clearly suboptimal, as they can introduce non-determinism
(e.g., if repr or hash relies on the object’s memory address).
We experimented with several other serialization libraries in search of better trade-offs. However,
joblib proved to be too fragile across a diverse set of object types, and while dill7 offered broader
coverage, its performance was prohibitively slow for our use case. We also benchmarked orjson8,
which, despite its performance reputation, was approximately four times slower than pickle in
our specific workloads. As of writing, we have not yet evaluated msgspec9—a high-performance
serialization library that shows promise—but plan to explore it in the future.
To hash the converted binary representations of objects, we used xxHash10, a family of extremely
fast, non-cryptographic hash algorithms designed for high-performance use cases. Unlike crypto-
graphic hash functions such as SHA-256, xxHash does not prioritize security guarantees, but for
the purpose of content-based caching it offers far superior performance with negligible collision
risk in practice—we specifically used the xxh128 variant, which is blazingly fast.
6.3.2
Storage
To accelerate repeated executions across runs, we needed persistent, disk-based storage—unlike
in-memory caching strategies commonly used in notebook-oriented solutions, which vanish be-
tween sessions.
Our initial approach was simple: we serialized and saved objects directly to disk using pickle ,
storing each cache entry in a separate file. While this method was easy to implement, it quickly
became unwieldy as the number of cache entries grew.
To improve manageability, we switched to a key-value store—initially using sqlitedict11, a lightweight
wrapper around SQLite that provides a dictionary-like interface backed by an embedded SQL
database. This gave us a more structured and scalable way to store cache entries, with the
added benefit of atomic operations and a single backing file. Eventually, we discovered LMDB12
(Lightning Memory-Mapped Database), which proved to be significantly faster for our use case.

7
Discussion
7.1
Addressing RQ3: What has to be done?
When considering the reviewed technologies holistically, it becomes clear that while every re-
quirement for robust, incremental execution is addressed somewhere, no single system satisfies
all of them. Solutions tend to specialize: some offer fine-grained input/output slicing, others
provide strong persistence models. What remains elusive is a unified system that balances all
core aspects: accuracy, performance, compatibility, and ease of use.
Among the most promising designs are those with fine-grained dependency tracking—especially
those capable of slicing state at a variable or expression level. These systems, such as IPyflow,
combine static and dynamic instrumentation to track how specific slices of the global state
influence computation. This level of granularity provides not only more flexibility in determin-
ing when recomputation is necessary, but should also improve caching efficiency and trust in
correctness. In contrast, coarser-grained systems either over-invalidate or risk missing subtle
dependencies.
One requirement that remains debatable is how to deal with foreign function interfaces (FFIs)
and third-party modules, particularly those written in C or C++. There is no silver bullet. Some
systems opt to disable instrumentation/caching around such calls entirely, while others lean on
fallback recomputation when cache restoration fails. As we have seen, a promising direction
may lie in offering an embedded DSL (Domain-Specific Language) that allows users to manually
annotate or wrap sensitive code regions. This strategy provides a balance: it “just works” in the
common case, while still giving users control over edge cases.
7.2
Navigating Trade-offs
A key tension is the trade-off between precision and performance. While speed is desirable,
precision is essential to uphold the core promise of reproducibility. A system that silently re-
computes incorrect outputs is worse than one that takes slightly longer to execute but guarantees
correctness. Therefore, precision must take precedence, even if it comes at the cost of runtime
or additional instrumentation overhead.
Another important consideration is the developer experience. While a completely automatic
system is ideal, Python’s dynamic nature makes this especially difficult. A practical solution
may need to strike a balance between automation and explicitness. For example, adding minimal
user annotations (via decorators or a lightweight DSL) may be an acceptable trade-off if it allows
the system to handle complex or ambiguous code reliably.
7.3
Evaluating Existing Work
Based on the exploration of existing work, IPyflow emerges as one of the most promising
candidates.
Its hybrid tracking model—combining static AST analysis with dynamic AST
instrumentation—allows it to build accurate program slices. However, it currently lacks per-
sistence to disk, limiting its ability to restore state across sessions or after kernel restarts. If
persistence were added—e.g., by serializing cell outputs after each execution or memoizing cells
by default—it could bring IPyflow over the line in terms of a complete solution. Although it
does not handle external state (such as files mutated by other processes) in a sound way, they
found that it still produced useful slices that successfully reproduced the desired outputs [8].
mandala is another strong contender, especially due to its declarative, functional approach. By
treating computation as a composition of pure functions, it makes tracking and caching easier
by design. However, it requires the user to write code in a very specific way, which may not align
with typical data science workflows. For new projects, mandala offers a clean and elegant model,
but for existing notebooks or users unfamiliar with this style of programming, the refactoring
cost can be a significant barrier.
We also revisited IncPy, a decade-old tool that integrated caching directly into a modified Python
interpreter. In theory, it seemed to achieve many things modern tools still struggle with: tracking
file reads/writes, call dependencies, and even fine-grained global variable usage. However, IncPy
29
==PAGE:36==
was built for Python 2, and its development has long ceased. We speculate that its failure to
gain traction is largely due to the reluctance of developers to rely on custom Python interpreters.
In practice, users probably prioritize compatibility, support, and future-proofing, and may be
wary of straying too far from mainstream Python.
Another tool that warrants attention is akernel, a more recent development that aims to improve
ipykernel in multiple aspects. What makes akernel intriguing is its aim to support not only
reactive execution, but also cell execution caching, potentially allowing for real-time feedback and
efficient recomputation. Although we were unable to investigate it in depth—having discovered
it late in the process—its presence signals ongoing interest in bridging the gap between flexibility
and reproducibility in notebook environments.
7.4
Limitations and Future Work
Despite the promising direction of our own work, several issues remain that constrain the current
implementation and evaluation.
We have not yet implemented a robust strategy for handling file I/O. While one might consider
caching file contents, in practice, the overhead of doing so can outweigh the benefits—especially
for common operations like reading datasets with
pandas.read_csv() . Reading these types of
datasets directly from disk is often fast enough. However, this assumes that file contents remain
unchanged, which is not always the case. A more nuanced approach, such as file fingerprinting
or timestamp/version checking, may be necessary to detect meaningful changes while avoiding
unnecessary recomputation.
Our benchmark runner is still under development and currently lacks comprehensive output
validation. As such, we cannot yet fully verify that any existing work nor our own prototype
consistently produces correct or identical results to uncached executions. This is critical for
establishing the soundness of our caching mechanism.
Regarding the bytecode approach, our virtual machine is still a work in progress. For instance,
the block stack has yet to be implemented, which limits our ability to analyze constructs like try-
except statements—these currently have to be skipped. Additionally, our algorithm for mapping
arguments to frame locals is not yet robust, requiring us to skip some functions during analysis.
Finally, due to Python’s highly dynamic nature, there are still obscure edge cases that cause the
virtual machine to crash, resulting in incomplete analyses—an obviously undesirable outcome.
Our earlier efforts using object-wrapping to track data flow introduced significant performance
overhead. Moreover, this method proved difficult to scale due to complexities in handling third-
party C extensions and identity-sensitive operations (e.g., use of is or id() for comparison).
Some Python internals—and even standard library components—depend on these identity guar-
antees, making seamless wrapping extremely difficult. Due to these concerns, we have decided
to move away from this approach.
Our most promising avenue appears to be AST instrumentation—though this is by no means
the obvious or straightforward choice. The path forward remains challenging, with a substantial
amount of work still required. Edge cases continue to surface in what feels like an endless stream,
and it remains to be seen whether the approach will ultimately prove practical and robust enough
for widespread use. That said, we do have evidence that it can be done—IPyflow stands as a
compelling example of that.
7.5
Related Work
An influential line of work comes from the field of self-adjusting computation (SAC), as developed
by Umut A. Acar and collaborators [21, 22]. SAC provides a formal foundation for programs
that can automatically respond to changes in their inputs by selectively re-executing only the
computations affected by those changes. Rather than recomputing everything from scratch, SAC
systems maintain dynamic dependency graphs that allow them to identify and propagate changes
efficiently. This approach has demonstrated near-optimal update performance in many cases and
aims to offload what would otherwise be an algorithmically complex and error-prone task from
the developer. While SAC has primarily been applied in functional languages with stronger type
30
==PAGE:37==
systems and purity guarantees, its core principles—tracking control and data dependencies to
support selective recomputation—align closely with the goals of this thesis. However, applying
these ideas directly to Python is challenging due to its dynamic nature, mutable state, and lack
of built-in purity guarantees.
Another recent and interesting direction is exemplified by HiPy [23], which tackles the challenge
of optimizing Python code by extracting high-level semantics and compiling it to efficient C++
code. Unlike most tools in our survey, HiPy does not merely trace or analyze a limited subset of
operations; instead, it transforms general Python functions into program generators that produce
statically-typed intermediate representations (IR). This design enables aggressive optimization,
such as logical operator fusion and partial evaluation, while still supporting dynamic Python
features through fine-grained fallback to the interpreter.
Although HiPy is not designed to
support incremental execution or caching per se, its methodology for isolating semantics and
enabling reproducibility through a compiled representation aligns conceptually with our goals.

Conclusion
This thesis set out to explore whether it is possible to combine the fast, incremental responsive-
ness of computational notebooks with the reproducibility and robustness of script-based work-
flows. We investigated this question with the intent of exploring intelligent caching mechanisms,
aiming to reduce redundant computation while preserving correctness—creating something that
is reproducible by design.
To this end, we formulated three research questions, examined a wide range of existing solutions,
and implemented multiple prototype systems to test different approaches. Through our analysis,
we established a set of four core requirements for building an effective solution: Partitioning, to
divide programs into meaningful chunks of execution that can be reasoned about independently;
Input Analysis, to determine whether a chunk’s inputs have changed and thus choose the correct
execution strategy; Output Tracking, to capture the effects of running a chunk and effectively
apply them; and FFI Handling, to account for the behavior of native libraries and safely deal
with external code.
Our findings show that while several existing tools address parts of this problem, none fully meet
all four requirements. Moreover, our own prototypes—while covering more ground collectively—
remain incomplete.
Most notably, the overhead introduced by our analysis techniques often
outweighs the benefits of caching. In their current form, these systems are not yet practically
useful: analysis is too expensive, results are sometimes invalidated prematurely, and in some
cases, execution fails or crashes altogether. These are clear signs that the system is not yet robust
enough to handle the wide variety of patterns encountered in real-world Python workflows.
Despite these limitations, this work reinforces the idea that reproducibility and interactivity
need not be mutually exclusive. The concepts explored here offer promising tactics for future
development. We have identified several opportunities to improve performance, correctness, and
adaptability.
In conclusion, while our current prototypes are not yet production-ready, they validate the
feasibility of the broader goal. We believe it is possible to build a system that makes script-
based development as flexible as notebooks while remaining reproducible by default. This thesis
provides both a roadmap and a foundation for continued progress toward that vision.
