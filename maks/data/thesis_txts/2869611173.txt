Abstract
This thesis explores the mathematical aspects of machine learning and applica-
tions.
Specifically, it examines the approximation theoretic properties of neural
networks through the lens of Riesz bases. The thesis is based on the work published
by Daubechies, DeVore, Foucart, Hanin, and Petrova and the work published by
Schneider and Vybiral.
The thesis first gives a short presentation of relevant theory and results concerning
Riesz sequences and Riesz bases in a Hilbert space H. Specifically, the Gram matrix
is introduced, and it is shown how the Riesz sequence condition can be stated in
terms of this matrix.
In the second part of the thesis, a concrete system of piecewise linear functions is
presented, and it is shown that this system forms a Riesz basis for the Real Hilbert
space L2([0, 1]).
This system is then extended to a multivariate Riesz basis for
L2([0, 1]d).
In the last part of the thesis, the feed-forward ReLU neural networks are intro-
duced, and their properties are examined. Specifically, it is shown how these net-
works can be constructed to output sums and compositions of functions. An explicit
construction of the networks used to implement the functions from the univariate
and multivariate Riesz bases is given. It is also shown how arbitrary finite linear
combinations of the basis elements can be implemented using the ReLU networks.

Introduction
The application of neural networks in machine learning tasks has been increasingly
successful during the last decade. As a result, their success is often empirical, and
the mathematical foundations of the networks, despite the networks being stud-
ied and utilized since their introduction in the 1940s [1, 2], remain poorly under-
stood. Therefore, developing a more rigorous theoretical framework for these neural
networks could enhance our understanding of their behavior and potentially drive
significant practical advancements.
One of the early and often cited theoretical results is the universal approximation
theorem for neural networks [3, 4], which states that neural networks can approxi-
mate any continuous function on a compact domain to any desired degree of accu-
racy. Yet a powerful result, it is limited to the existence and does not provide any
additional insight into, e.g., the complexity of the networks or the approximation
eﬀiciency, which would aid a better understanding of the networks. Indeed, the
modern success of artificial neural networks is not just a consequence of this result
but in part due to the compositional structure of deep networks leading to more
effective approximations [5, 6, 7].
Today, much research is going into the theoretical approximation properties of
neural networks, and many interesting results in this direction have been published.
Many of the results investigate neural networks that use the Rectified Linear Unit
(ReLU) activation function, as this is one of the most used activation functions in
applications, and the resulting networks are the simplest and easiest to understand
in a theoretical setting. Examples of results in this direction are [8], where the au-
thors show that deep ReLU networks approximate functions of d variables with an
accuracy comparable to that of linear approximations using algebraic polynomials
while maintaining a similar number of parameters, and [5] where it is shown that
deep ReLU networks can approximate certain classes of functions at rates that sur-
pass those achievable by classical methods such as free-knot splines.
In this thesis, we study the approximation properties of feed-forward neural networks
with the ReLU activation function. The thesis investigates the work published by I.
Daubechies et al. in [5] and by C. Schneider et al. in [9] and is structured as follows:
In Section 2, we present relevant theory and definitions regarding Riesz sequences
and Riesz bases. We define the Gram matrix and show how the Riesz sequence
condition for a finite sequence in a Hilbert space H can be reformulated and stated
in terms of the corresponding Gram matrix.
Section 3 presents the system of piecewise linear functions from [5] and, by follow-
ing the approach of [9] and results from section 2, proves that this system constitutes
a univariate Riesz basis for the real Hilbert space L2([0, 1]).
In Section 4, we follow the approach of [9] and extend this system of functions
to higher dimensions d ≥1 by a construction that avoids using tensor products and
instead relies on inner products with fixed vectors in Rd. We then show, using the
same approach as for the univariate case, that this system constitutes a multivariate
Riesz basis for the real Hilbert space L2([0, 1]d)

Finally, in Section 5, we turn to approximation properties of neural networks.
Specifically, we first define and present the relevant theory regarding the feed-
forward ReLU neural networks. Here, we show how to construct ReLU networks
to represent sums and compositions of functions. We then show that these ReLU
networks can implement the elements from the univariate and multivariate Riesz
bases, and we explicitly state the dimensions and complexity of the corresponding
networks. Finally, we show that arbitrary finite linear combinations of elements
from these bases can also be implemented using the ReLU networks.
Thus, this thesis aims to bridge the gap between the impressive empirical per-
formance of neural networks and a more rigorous mathematical understanding of
their approximation properties. Through a detailed presentation of the works by
Daubechies et al.
and Schneider et al., this thesis not only offers insights into
the expressive power of feed-forward networks but also highlights their complexity
and practical implementation capabilities. The results obtained are expected to con-
tribute to the broader understanding of machine learning, specifically deep learning,
and inspire further research into their theoretical underpinnings and applications.

4
A Multivariate Riesz Basis
In this chapter, we extend Theorem 3.1.6 to higher dimensions d ≥1 and construct
a Riesz basis for L2([0, 1]d) that can be eﬀiciently represented using ReLU neural
networks, in line with [9]. A natural approach is to use tensor products of functions
from R1, but this leads to an exponential growth in Riesz constants and is poorly
suited for neural networks as the multiplication function (x, y) 4→x · y can only
be approximated, as shown in [13] [14]. Instead, we construct a multivariate Riesz
basis of piecewise aﬀine functions that avoids tensor products, relying instead on
inner products with fixed vectors in Rd to ensure compatibility with neural network
architectures, as done in [9].
