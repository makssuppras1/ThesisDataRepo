I. Abstract 
This thesis is based on FLSmidths goal and ongoing effort to improve the capability to model combustion of 
alternative fuels including Refuse Derived Fuel (RDF). This is done in order to give a better process 
understanding, thereby facilitating better equipment design and improving the quality of customer support 
with regards to increasing alternative fuel substitution for economic and environmental benefit. 
A literature survey has been conduction describing the use of alternative fuels, including RDF in cement 
production and the general composition and properties of RDF. The combustion phenomena related to 
combustion of solid fuels have been described and modeling methods and principles for single particle 
combustion have been described and discussed including methods for modeling non-spherical particles. 
Based on methods from combustion literature, a single particle combustion model for biomass derived 
materials was developed capable of handling non-spherical geometries, by approximating them with simple 
1-dimensional geometries such as infinite cylinders and infinite slabs. The combustion model consists of two 
separate models: one handling heating, drying and devolatilization processes and another one handling the 
char oxidation process. 
Based on the sensitivity analysis it was suggested to deal with the heterogeneous nature of RDF when 
modeling the biomass derived material should by subdividing the material into subgroups based on the 
materials thermal diffusivity and the char density as these parameters had the highest model sensitivity. 
The model has been validated for both spherical and cylindrical particles for two different biomass materials 
using experimental data from single particle combustion measurements at different reactor conditions. For 
both geometries, the deviations are within the spread of the experimental data. 
For spherical particles at 1231 °C, gas oxygen concentration 3 vol%, water concentration 27 vol% and slip 
velocity of 2.76 m/s the maximum deviations was found to be 8 % for the devolatilization time, 21 % for the 
char oxidation time and 17 % for the full combustion time. While the average deviations was ±3.8 %, ±13 % 
and ±11 % for the devolatilization time, char oxidation time and full combustion time respectively.  
For cylindrical particles at 1200 °C, gas oxygen concentration 20 vol%, water concentration 20 vol% and 
slip velocity of 3.4 m/s the maximum deviations was found to be 40 % for the devolatilization time, 21 % for 
the char oxidation time and 31% for the full combustion time. While the average deviations was ±30 %, ±12 
% and ±21 % for the devolatilization time, char oxidation time and full combustion time respectively.  
The developed model was used to evaluate the combustion time of a model RDF particle chosen to be a 0.65 
mm thick disc shaped cardboard particle with a diameter of 13 mm. The combustion time was evaluated 
under different conditions relevant for cement processes, specifically the calciner. The base conditions being 
bulk gas properties of 1373 K and 10 vol% oxygen and water concentration, with a particle slip velocity of 
8.2 m/s. The devolatilization time and char oxidation time was found to be 1.3 and 7 seconds respectively, 
showing that under these conditions, the char oxidation is the most significant part of the combustion time.  
From the simulation of the combustion of a cardboard RDF particle under different conditions it was 
concluded that the combustion time is most sensitive to the oxygen concentration, followed by the bulk 
temperature and the slip velocity. The water concentration in the gas phase has a slight influence as well. 
Furthermore, the simulation of the combustion of cardboard particles with different sizes and moisture 
content, showed that, the smallest dimension of a fuel particle, in this case the thickness, is the most 
influential.

1 Introduction 
The main constituent of Portland cement is clinker, made from a mixture of lime, clay and sand. The 
production of clinker is achieved via the calcination process in which limestone (CaCO3) is decomposed to 
calcium oxide (CaO), followed by a series of clinker reactions where the different metal oxides present in the 
raw material, form minerals such as alite, belite, ferrite and alumineate [1]. The process is highly energy 
intensive, due to the high temperatures needed and the strongly endothermic calcination reaction. The energy 
for the process is supplied by direct combustion of fuels inside the reactors [2]. The high energy requirement 
of the pyroprocessing means that ~30-40 % of the  production cost in a cement plant comes from the fuel 
cost [3].  
The high cost of fuels and the large CO2 emissions in the industry provide both an economical and an 
environmental incentive to reduce consumption of expensive fossil fuels. Previously a high degree of energy 
optimization has been performed within the industry to reduce production costs and increase 
competitiveness.  Recently efforts in many developed countries have been focused on finding alternative 
fuels sources. One of the possibilities is substituting with cheaper and partly/fully CO2 neutral alternative 
fuels. One group of the alternative fuels utilized are treated waste in the form of refuse derived fuels (RDF) 
[3]. The heterogeneous composition of RDF is a challenge for running stable processes. In order to increase 
the efficiency and operability of RDF combustion processes, traditional modeling tools for particle 
combustion should be developed to handling the heterogeneous nature of RDF. The focus of this work will 
be to develop a single particle model for predicting the conversion behavior and combustion time for the 
different non-plastic combustible materials in RDF.

2 Project description 
 
Background 
Due to the demand of reducing the fuel price and the CO2 emission in cement production, there is growing 
interest in utilizing refuse derived fuels (RDF) in cement production, both in kiln burners and in calciners. In 
order to facilitate the combustion of RDF while achieving high efficiency and low emissions, it is desirable 
to develop modeling tools that can describe the combustion of RDF particles in cement processes. 
Objective 
This project aims to develop and validate a single particle model to describe the combustion of non-plastic 
RDF particles including non-spherical geometries. The model will describe the external and internal 
transport phenomena for the particle and the related reactions (e.g. pyrolysis and char oxidation). The 
developed model will be validated using experimental single particle combustion data from literature. 
Finally, the model will be applied to evaluate the conversion RDF particles in cement processes, and an 
analysis of the influence of fuel properties (e.g. moisture content, size) and operation conditions (e.g. 
temperature, gas composition) on the combustion time will be carried out. Suggestions for material 
groupings to handle the heterogeneous composition of RDF should be made. 
Content 
 
Literature survey on RDF composition and properties and modeling of single particle combustion, 
including non-spherical particles. 
 
Develop a single particle combustion model for non-plastic RDF particles capable of handling non-
spherical particles. 
 
Validate the model based on experiments in a single particle combustor. 
 
Perform a sensitivity analysis of the model. 
 
Based on the model, evaluate the conversion of RDF particles in cement processes and the influence 
of fuel properties and operation conditions. 
 
Based on the modeling result suggest strategies for handling the heterogeneous nature of RDF when 
modeling

3 Literature survey 
This section is a literature survey on refuse derived fuel (RDF) and how its combustion can be modeled. First 
a short motivation for using alternative fuels, such as RDF, in the cement industry is presented. This is 
followed by a section describing what RDF is and which fuel properties it possess. Then the phenomena 
related to solid combustion are described followed by an extensive section about modeling single particle 
combustion. 
3.1 Alternative fuels for cement production 
Traditionally the cement industry has been using fossil fuels such as coal, oil and gas. The prices of these 
have generally risen across the years while oil and gas prices for example have been subject to large periodic 
fluctuations [4]. Furthermore, there is increased pressure from both governments and society in general to 
reduce the amount of greenhouse gases emitted by the industry. In order to do this extensive energy 
optimization have been implemented, but to go further co-firing with a mixture of fossil fuels, usually 
pulverized coal, and alternative fuels can be implemented [4,5].  
Due to the increased focus to reduce cost and carbon footprint in the cement industry by using alternative 
fuels, the degree of substitution of conventional fuels at cement plants increases. In some highly 
industrialized countries, more than 25 years of experience implementing the use of alternative fuels in 
cement production has helped facilitate a continued increase in substitution. In 2006 the share of thermal 
energy from alternative fuels for CEMBUREAU members [6] was 18 % [7] up from 10 % in 1995 [8]. The 
achievement of a high use of alternative fuels is dependent on the price of conventional fuels, the availability 
of alternative fuel sources and environmental legislation in the region/country. As an example of the big 
differences between regions/countries, Figure 3-1 shows the thermal substitution rate in 2010 for the EU, 
worldwide and selected countries.

3.2 Refuse derived fuel 
This section gives a short description and definition on what RDF is and how it is generally produced. 
Refuse derived fuels are combustible solid fractions recovered from different waste streams. RDF is 
primarily produced from municipal solid waste (MSW) via a process where non-combustible fractions are 
removed through various mechanical processes. Usually the raw MSW is shredded after which it is passed 
through a series of sieving, ballistic and magnetic separators. Further size reduction can happen between 
separation steps to facilitate better separation [14]. Depending on the source and treatment process the 
composition and quality of the different fractions can vary widely. Some facilities use near infrared detection 
to remove high chlorine materials [15], perishable biomass such as food waste is sometimes removed as, 
although combustible, it can have a high moisture content and therefore reduce the overall lower heating 
value (LHV). Some facilities use a biodrying step, in which moisture is evaporated from the RDF by heat 
released from biological decomposition of biomass present after the sorting [16]. The final product has a 
high content of combustible materials such as paper, plastics and textiles while most of the non-combustibles 
and low calorific materials have been removed [15]. Apart from mixed municipal waste, RDF can be 
produced from/contain dehydrated sewage sludge, mixed industrial waste and other types of waste [16].

3.3 Solid particle combustion 
In this section, the general principles of combustion of solid particles are described. This is followed by an 
investigation of the experimental study and modeling of solid combustion with a focus on refuse derived 
fuels.  
Combustion of solid fuels can be divided in five general steps: Heating, drying, devolatilization, volatile 
oxidation and char oxidation. Drying, devolatilization and volatile oxidation, or char oxidation are not 
==PAGE:27==
Page 24 of 134 
 
necessarily all applicable for all solid fuels [29]. Particles, such as the plastics PE and PP, can convert fully 
into volatile gases as seen in Table 3-4, leaving no char for char oxidation. While char from a pyrolysis 
process has already completed the devolatilization process and will burn almost purely through char 
oxidation. The steps are not exclusively consecutive and the different steps will often overlap. In the 
following sections, the principles of each step are described. Note that each step can be different for various 
solid fuels, due to the difference in chemical composition and physical structure [30].  
3.3.1 Heating 
In order for a solid to burn, it must reach sufficiently high temperatures for the devolatilization and/or char 
oxidation reactions to occur at significant rates. All three modes of heat transport, conduction, convection 
and radiation can play a role in the particle heating depending on the combustion method [31,32]. 
External heat transport to the solid surface is facilitated either by convection from the flow of hot gas, 
radiation from the surfaces of the combustion chamber and/or other burning particles or through conduction 
through a fuel bed [29,32,33]. For small particles and/or at low temperatures the radiation can be negligible, 
while for larger particles and at higher temperatures, typical for combustion, the radiation plays an 
increasingly important role in the external heat transport [34].  
Internal heat transport in a solid particle generally happens through conduction through the solid material and 
the fluid present in the pores. If the particle is porous heat is additionally transferred through radiation from 
pore wall to pore wall [31,33,35]. In some cases where there is a net flux of gaseous products for example 
volatiles and water vapor through the porous structure, convection can play a role. Due to gas released inside 
a particle during combustion a net flow can occur in the outwards direction. This results in a convective heat 
transfer that counteracts conduction and radiation by cooling the outer layers which will be hotter than where 
the gas comes from [31].  
As the particle is heated, drying and pyrolysis start to take place at significant rates, energy might be released 
or consumed in reactions and in the phase transformations occurring. This will have an effect on the heat 
transfer throughout the particles. Furthermore, these processes will change the properties of the particle, such 
as the density, heat capacity and thermal conductivity [28,32]. 
3.3.2 Drying 
Most solid fuels contain water (in one way or another), RDF especially can have a very high water content 
compared to conventional fuels, making the drying step more dominating in the total combustion time and 
the ignition delay [3]. Generally the water in fuel can exist in three forms; bound water, free liquid water and 
water vapor in the pores. Bound water is adsorbed by for example cellulose, through hydrogen bonding with 
the hydroxyl groups. Free water is held within the pores by capillary forces. The water vapor exists within 
the unfilled pores in the gas phase [36–38]. 
The amount of water that can exist as bound water is defined as the fiber saturation point (FSP). The fiber 
saturation point varies with the macromolecular composition of the fuel. For lignocellulosic biomass such as 
wood it can exceed 30 %. Free liquid water will only be present in the fuel if the moisture content of the fuel 
exceeds the FSP [28]. 
The main driving force for drying is the difference between the partial pressure of water vapor and the 
equilibrium partial pressure of free and bound water [39]. 
As the fuel particles are heated water will start to evaporate at the surface where the temperature will rise 
first. The free water will start to move outwards through diffusion and capillary forces. As the temperature 
==PAGE:28==
DTU

inside the particle rise free liquid water will start to evaporate, followed by desorption of bound water [37]. 
The evaporation and desorption of free liquid and bound water is highly endothermic and thereby have a 
potentially significant impact on the heating of the particles [28].  Through convection and diffusion the 
water vapor will primarily be transported outwards towards the particle surface and the gas phase. However, 
because of temperature gradients some water might diffuse into colder parts of the particle where 
recondensation can occur [37]. 
3.3.3 Devolatilization 
The devolatilization of solid fuels is a complex heterogeneous reaction process. It involves numerous 
chemical species as reactants, intermediates and products, interacting through a large network of reactions 
[40,41]. The devolatilization behavior and the products are dependent on the type of fuel, due to the 
difference in the original chemical composition. Solid fuel devolatilization is often divided into 2 steps, a 
primary pyrolysis and secondary reactions [36,41,42]. 
Primary pyrolysis starts at around 500 K for biomass [36] and paper while it start at around 700 K for 
polyethylene [43]. Here the solid structure decomposes to volatile gases, tar and char for solid fuels such as 
coal, biomass and paper [31,33,35–37,41,43–47], while some plastics as discussed earlier will not leave a 
char [43]. The volatile gases consist of primarily CO2, CO, CH4, H2 and H2O [44] depending on the fuel.  
The tar consists of a mixture of phenols, ethers, polyaromatic hydrocarbons and other heavy compounds 
[40,41,45]. The tar compounds are volatile at the pyrolysis temperature but condensable near room 
temperature. Depending on the thermal conditions some of the tar will undergo secondary reactions, both 
homogeneously in the gas phase and heterogeneously on the particle/char surface. These secondary reactions 
include cracking into non-condensable gas products (volatiles) or polymerization and reattachment to the 
solid (char) [30,41,42]. The secondary reactions leading to char formation are more dominant when the 
particles are bigger and the temperature is lower, due to increased residence time inside the particle and 
lower internal heating rates, and due to the temperature dependency of the kinetics. This leads to a difference 
in the char yield at low heating rates, such as most thermograviometric analysis (TGA) experiments and high 
heating rates obtained in industrial burners [28].  
During the devolatilization process the fuel particles are subject to physical changes [47]. These can include 
shrinkage, swelling [42] and/or fragmentation. This leads to changes in the particle size, porosity and pore 
size distribution, sometimes even the overall geometric shape of the particle can be subject to change 
[35,48]. 
3.3.4 Volatile oxidation 
In the presence of oxygen in the gas phase, the combustible volatiles  such as CO, CH4 and H2, released 
during devolatilization will burn in the gas phase [3].  
This is generally observed by either a flame surrounding the particle at a small distance from the surface or 
more homogenously in the bulk gas phase, depending on the mixing. This is like other gas phase combustion 
phenomena, controlled by; mixing of the combustibles and the oxidizer in this case oxygen, temperature and 
possibly quenching phenomena [49]. 
3.3.5 Char oxidation and gasification 
Another of the key steps in particle combustion is char oxidation. The solid char left by the devolatilization, 
consists primarily of carbon and inorganic ash compounds. The combustion of char occurs by a combination 
of partial and complete oxidation followed by combustion of the formed gaseous species. The process

3.4.1 Transport phenomena 
Combustion processes are largely controlled by transport phenomena, and a sufficient description of the 
different transport mechanism is a key aspect in modeling combustion. In this section models for selected 
transport processes involved in solid fuel combustion are presented. 
3.4.1.1 
External transport  
The external heat and mass transport to and from the particle surface is some of the key limiting factors in 
the combustion time of solid fuels [3,29,51].  
In the modeling of heat and mass transfer modern computational fluid dynamics (CFD) methods are the most 
advanced and can achieve the most precise results for quite complex systems. Here the Navier-Stokes 
equation is solved together with the flux equations, Fick’s and Fourier’s laws, through different 
discretization schemes and numerical solution procedures. Phenomena like radiation and reactions are built 
into most commercial CFD programs such as COMSOL, ANSYS Fluent and SOLIDWORKS. The necessary 
simplicity of the models and the long calculation times are the major disadvantages of CFD [54–56]. 
The details of CFD methods are beyond the scope of this work as it is the modeling of the conversion 
behavior of the single particles that is of interest. Therefore, the external transport phenomena will be 
described through the use of laminar film theory.

In the 2 film model the gasification reaction, R 3, between carbon dioxide and char is included together and

gasification reaction and reaction R 6. The homogenuous reaction is again assumed instantaneous  and 
happens in the same infinitely thin flame sheet as the CO oxidation. 
In the continuous film model, the only assumption is the quasi-stationarity of the film layer. All reactions can 
occur throughout the film layer and is modeled using either global kinetics or by implementing elementary 
reactions [47,51]. 
3.4.6 Non-spherical particles 
Dynamic modeling of particle combustion and particles in general is often based on simplified geometric 
assumptions in order to make the solving of the PDEs manageable. By assuming a perfectly spherical 
particle with uniform boundary conditions the problem can be reduced to a one dimensional model where all 
variables are only dependent on the radial coordinate and the time. This leads to PDE models which can be 
discretized in the radial direction and implemented in a numerical solver in software like Matlab etc. 
Using spherical particle approximation work well for fuels such as pulverized coal and some pulverized 
biomasses. However, for other fuel like woodchips, logs and especially MSW and non-pelletized RDF 
assuming spherical particles is generally less suitable. In the following section different methods for 
modeling non-spherical particles will be discussed. 
3.4.6.1 
Equivalent diameter 
Particles that are non-spherical can be modeled as spheres, by using an equivalent diameter. The equivalent 
diameter for a non-spherical particle is defined as the diameter of a sphere, that would have the same mass or 
volume as the non-spherical particle [52]. This can be calculated by equation 3-77.  
 
               
 
  
 
   
         
         
 
  
 
 
3-77 
The combustion model is solved as normal for a spherical particle but using the equivalent diameter [52]. 
This model works well for small particles and particles that are near spherical with low aspect ratios [60]. 
However, it becomes problematic for cylinder type particles with high aspect ratios and for flat particles like 
foils and flake, where the real thickness of the particle, being it the thickness of a foil or the diameter of a 
cylinder is far from the equivalent diameter. Gera et al. [59] investigated the consequences of the equivalent 
diameter assumption on the calculated conversion of switchgrass particles with an aspect ratio of 4 in an 
industrial boiler. CFD modeling was used for the external transport processes and a non-isothermal transport 
model for the internal transport with and without the equivalent diameter assumption. They found that at an 
equivalent diameter of 1 mm the predictions was the same, but for all equivalent diameters above 2 mm, the 
predictions using the assumptions were critically different [59].  A similar investigation was performed by 
Momeni et al. [90] but with experimental work instead of modeling. This investigation showed significant 
differences in burnout time for biomass particles of equivalent diameters of 3 mm. with ~8 s for an actual

sphere and a decreasing time for cylinders with increasing aspect ratios with a burnout time of ~4.5 s at 
aspect ratio 8 [90]. 
The same behavior can be observed for plate like particles. The assumed shape also have a large effect on the 
predicted flight behavior in a reactor, with spherical assumptions causing large deviations [20,91,92]. 
However, the prediction of flight path is not part of this work. For a more thorough investigation of this, see

3.4.6.3 
2 or higher dimensional models 
If equivalent diameter or 1 dimensional models are insufficient a 2 dimensional model can be implemented. 
This can be the case for spheres and cylinders with non-uniform boundary conditions on the angular 
coordinate or for cylinders with non-negligible gradients in the longitude direction. In the most extreme 
extent a 3-d model of a particle can be implemented, however the gained accuracy in regards to the physical 
description might be drowned in all the other uncertainties built in to the model and the effort might be 
wasted. 
3.5 Discussion 
The discussion of the literature study is divided into subsections to give a better overview of the different 
subjects and to have an easier reference point for the modeling decisions. 
3.5.1 Material groups 
One of the main problems with modeling RDF combustion is handling the high in-homogeneity of the fuel. 
This is a consequence of the nature of the fuel itself consisting primarily of treated municipal or industrial 
waste. Because of the wide variety of materials, developing a single particle model valid for all RDF 
materials, seems infeasible. However, it is also infeasible to include different models for every kind of 
material and all particle shapes and sizes. One possible solution to compromise both of the above mentioned 
issues could be to group materials of similar physical and chemical properties 
The material typically found in RDF can be separated into different categories for how they qualitatively act 
during combustion. The following 3 categories can be made; biomass derived materials, plastics/polymeric 
materials and non-combustibles. 
The biomass derived materials naturally include all kind of plant based biomass commonly found in waste, 
both woods and perishable biomass. However, biomass derived materials like paper, cardboard and non-
artificial textiles are also included. Common to these are that they are all fibrous and porous materials, many 
==PAGE:53==
Page 50 of 134 
 
made up of cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin and water can therefore be bound via hydrogen bonds. The 
volatile matter and fixed carbon content in wood and the biomass based RDF components are somewhat 
similar as seen in Table 3-4 in section 3.2.1.1. Furthermore, the pyrolysis kinetics [78,102] and char 
oxidation kinetics [103] for these materials are in the same range. 
The plastics and polymeric materials are made up of polymers like PE, PS, PVC etc. but also artificial 
textiles made from polymer fibers. Common to this category is extensive melting behavior and lack of char 
formation [30]. 
The non-combustibles include inorganic materials like ceramics, metals and natural minerals like sand and 
stone. These can react and form oxides under combustion conditions, this is not of interest in the RDF 
combustion modeling. However, in a cement plant calciner the endothermic calcination reaction does have a 
large effect on the reactor temperature. Plenty of models have been developed for this in the cement 
literature.

corresponds to the biomass derived materials and the plastics respectively.  
The focus of this work will only be the biomass like materials, as the combustion behavior of plastics are 
significantly different, due to the melting behavior and lack of char formation for most polymers. 
3.5.2 Models structure 
The combustion of solid particles can be split up into 5 general steps: Heating, drying, devolatilization, 
volatile oxidation and char oxidation. However, including all 5 steps in a single model will make the model 
unnecessarily complicated. Instead the combustion process can be divided into two models; one model that 
accounts the heating, drying and devolatilization processes as these are closely connected. The oxidation of 
the volatiles in the gas phase can be ignored as only the conversion time of the particle is needed in this 
project. The second model can account for the char oxidation, assuming that the onset of char oxidation 
happens after full devolatilization of the particle. This is a commonly used assumption justified by the large 
outwards convective flow of volatiles during devolatilization counteracting any oxygen transport to the 
charred surface.

3.5.3 External transport 
Although CFD technology is available and could be used for modeling the external transport processes, this 
will not be done in this work as it is beyond the scope of the project. 
The simple film theory using mass and heat transfer correlations using Sherwood and Nusselt number 
correlations has been implemented with great success in numerous models, not just for combustion but in 
general. They have stood the test of time, even as more modern and complex methods have been developed. 
The results from a single correlation cannot be extrapolated to all conditions and situations. However, many 
different correlations using the general form of the two dimensionless numbers             
  & 
            
  have been developed for various conditions [22]. Care should be taken to choose a 
correlation valid for the system investigated.  
One phenomena not taken into account using the film theory is the release of gases during drying and 
devolatilization. This outwards flow of gases would cause a convective transport away from the particle 
counteracting the conductive heat transport to the surface. The release of gases could also be in the form of 
jets escaping via a few larger pores disrupting the boundary layer and creating a more complicated flow 
pattern [28].  
3.5.4 Internal transport 
The internal transport is primarily of interest for the drying and devolatilization model, where the heat up of 
the particle controls the drying and the reaction rate of the devolatilization reactions. 
The heat transfer is the critical intra-particular transport part. However, mass transfer can have an influence 
on the heat transfer through convection and if recondensation of moisture is included. By assuming no 
recondensation and negligible heat capacity of the escaping gas, the need for solving the gas phase mass 
balance is avoided. This greatly simplifies the model and many authors have done this without major impact 
on the model predictions [13]. Applying these assumptions implies that the only intra-particular transport 
phenomena modeled is the conductive and radiative heat transfer [28]. 
Key parameters for the heat transfer inside the particle are the thermal conductivity and the heat capacities. 
For these there are correlations the temperature dependencies for some materials. However, RDF consists of 
many materials of which most do not have correlations for such properties. But values can be found for room 
temperatures for a wide variety of materials likely to be found in RDF as seen in Table 3-6. Therefore 
constant values for the virgin fuel would be easy to implement. This should not lead to a too high impact, as 
the devolatilization starts at around 500 K after which the char properties becomes important. Hence, the 
importance of modeling the virgin fuels heat capacity and thermal conductivities, at elevated temperatures is 
low. The temperature dependency of the char properties are more important due to the elevated temperatures 
at which it is present. For the heat capacity the simple linear expression suggested by Koufopanos et al. [33] 
gives unrealistic values at high temperatures, while the Einstein model adapted for biomass by Lu et al. [52] 
flattens out at higher temperatures.  
The use of the same expression for the char thermal conductivity for different chars might be problematic 
due to the difference in morphologies likely to occur, as the porosity will influence the effective 
conductivity. The problem is less severe with the heat capacity as the char is assumed to have more similar 
properties for the dense solid, although inorganic ash compounds can differ from material to material. 
As stated in section 3.4.1.2.2 the effective thermal conductivity of char exceeds that of the virgin fuel at high 
temperature, due to the radiative heat transfer. This is not included in the temperature dependency 
==PAGE:55==
Page 52 of 134 
 
correlations suggested by Koufopanos et al. [33]. This can be dealt with by including the radiative heat 
transfer coefficient. This will also include influence of the porosity and account for some of the temperature 
dependency of the heat transfer.  
The influence of bound water on the heat capacity is not dealt with a lot in the combustion modeling 
literature, however it is commonly assumed to be equal to the heat capacity of liquid water [105]. This will 
give it the same weight as in all the models not accounting for the difference between bound and free water. 
The influence on the thermal conductivity is unknown as well and many models not accounting for bound 
water assumes that the free water in the pores is not a continuous phase and that the influence is therefore 
negligible 
As char oxidation during combustion is mostly limited by external mass transport, the diffusion into the char 
particle is often neglected by assuming an impermeable char surface [51]. This will also be the case in this 
work in order to keep the model simple. 
3.5.5 Drying 
Three different types of drying models have been presented in section 3.4.2 each with their strengths and 
weaknesses. The heat flux model is the simplest model, but is not able to reproduce the actual temperature 
behavior of drying particles [68].  
The chemical reaction model is able to reproduce the actual temperature curves measured in drying particles 
and the wider temperature range at which evaporation occurs. The weakness of the model is that it lumps 
many different phenomena into a simple Arrhenius expression [36,68,74]. 
The equilibrium models are the most rigorous drying model and directly describe the actual process. 
However, it requires the solving of the gas phase mass balance and modeling of the intra-particle flow and 
pressure gradients. 
3.5.6 Devolatilization kinetics 
Many types of devolatilization mechanisms exists each with their own strengths and weaknesses. In order to 
achieve good result for a combustion model the right weight of simplicity and precision must be found. 
The simplest models are the global single-component mechanisms using a fixed volatile and char distribution 
as a parameter set. However, a single set of kinetic parameters cannot describe the devolatilization of 
biomass when larger temperature intervals are modeled [75]. At high temperatures and high heating rate a 
single set of parameters is sufficient, but does not correlate to the low temperature behavior [77]. 
For low temperature low heating rate kinetics a multi-step reaction mechanism achieves higher better 
correlation to the conversion behavior, at the price of increased complexity as mass balances for intermediate 
components must be solved. This can be simplified by using a fixed volatile and char distribution and 
summing the different reaction rate constants leading to weight loss. This removes the predictive capability 
for the char yield, but gives good correlations to combustion of particles [60]. 
By combining the simplified multi-step reactions for low temperature kinetics with the global single-
component mechanism for high temperature kinetics gives a kinetic model useful for a wide temperature 
range [28]. 
3.5.7 Char oxidation kinetics 
For the char oxidation kinetics there is a significant influence from the origin of the char, due to catalytic 
activiation from ash component, and the surface structure influence the actual surface area. However, as the

3.5.8 Non-sphericity 
The non-spherical particles must be handled in order to properly model the combustion time of the wide 
variety of materials found in RDF. As described in 3.4.6.1 the use of equivalent diameter are unsuited for 
RDF due to the many large particles with large aspect ratios, which would result in overestimation of the 
combustion times. 
However, the use of simplified 1-dimensional geometries allows for the use of the same transport equations 
for different particles shapes by using a geometric shape factor. In this way particles far from sphericity can 
be modeled as either infinite cylinders or slabs [59]. 
3.6 Conclusions 
The composition and properties of RDF vary widely due to the in-homogeneity of the source materials and 
the difference in manufacturing. RDF can be split in 3 material categories; biomass derived materials, 
plastics/polymeric materials and non-combustibles. 
Based on the qualitative combustion behavior, the proximate analysis and the likeness of the kinetics, the 
biomass derived materials can be modeled using methods developed for wood combustion modeling. 
The modeling of the particle combustion can be split into a heat transfer and kinetically controlled drying 
and devolatilization model and a mass transfer and kinetically controlled char oxidation model. 
The external heat and mass transfer can be described sufficiently using film theory including radiation for the 
heat transfer. 
Neglecting intra-particular heat transfer by convection simplifies the model, as the need for solving the gas 
phase mass balance during drying and devolatilization is avoided, due to lack of mass transfer dependency.  
The drying can be modeled using a chemical reaction model, as this can give good agreement with 
experimental results for burning wet fuel particles.  
For the devolatilization a kinetic scheme using a combination of low and high temperature mechanisms can 
give a model useful for a wide range of temperatures and heating rates 
The char oxidation will be modeled using 2 different 1 film models one only accounting for the oxygen-char 
reactions and one also accounting for the water gasification reaction. 
Non-sphericity can be deal with using simplified one-dimension geometries, as using equivalent diameters is 
problematic for larger non-spherical particles with high aspect ratios.

In this section a particle combustion model will be developed using principles described and discussed in the

The particle combustion is modeled in two separate phases, a drying and devolatilization phase and a char

full devolatilization of the particle, as the flow of combustible volatiles will counteract mass transfer to the 
particle and consume the oxygen in the boundary layer of the particle. Therefore two different and 
independent models are derived.

As seen from Figure 4-6 the devolatilization time scales linearly dependency with the fuels density and heat 
capacity. Together these make up the thermal mass of the fuel and give the amount of energy needed to heat 
up the particle. In the model the density during conversion is directly proportional to the original density as it 
is given by           , hence the linear dependency would be expected. The modeling of the density as 
linearly dependent on the conversion is based on the assumption of no swelling or shrinkage of the particle 
during devolatilization. Shrinkage is well known to happen during drying and devolatilization of wood, 
however the total mass of the particle and thereby the amount of material need to be heated would still be 
linearly dependent on the conversion. The main influence would be on the external heat transfer as described 
in [60].  
For the heat capacity this is not the case, as the heat capacity of the char is independent heat capacity of the 
fuel and dependent on temperature. This suggest that the biggest contribution comes from the heat up of the 
virgin fuel and not the char. This can also be observed from the simulation example in Figure 4-2, as after 
full devolatilization of the surface a major increase in the heating rate is observed. The sensitivity of the fuel 
heat capacity is slightly lower than for the density, hence the char heat capacity still has some effect. As 
changes to the density and heat capacity has the same weight in the differential equations. 
For the fuels thermal conductivity there is a non-linear dependency, with the sensitivity decreasing as the 
thermal conductivity gets higher. This can be caused by the independence between the fuels thermal 
conductivity and the chars thermal conductivity in the model and the effect of the radiative heat transfer. The 
thermal conductivity of the fuel is only relevant at low temperatures, as all fuel is converted to char 
immediately after 800 K is reached. As the fuel thermal conductivity is increased the resistance to heat 
transfer in the char layer surrounding the unconverted core becomes the dominating factor, while decreasing 
the fuel thermal conductivity will result in the resistance for heat transfer into the unconverted core 
becoming increasingly dominating. This could be explained by the analogue of the shrinking core model 
with both mass transfer resistance in the ash layer and kinetic resistance in the un-reacted core. The changes

in the thermal conductivity of the fuel are comparable to increases in the kinetics or the mass transfer, 
shifting the importance of the controlling mechanism [108].

high temperature, to gain too much influence. As conductivity has no influence on the first derivative at the 
surface. This leads to a cool down and a plateau for the surface temperature at around 800 K, giving a lower 
temperature gradient through the fuel leading to a delay in the full devolatilization time. An example of this 
is given in the figures in Appendix 2. When increased the values for the pore size and porosity of the char 
beyond the starting point makes them unrealistic as the porosity goes to ~99 % and the pore size goes to the 
mm size, the same range as the particle. Hence this should not cause problems in modelling using realistic 
parameters.

4.4.2 Reactor conditions 
For the reactor conditions, the calciner of a cement plant is of high interest, as it is frequently used for RDF 
combustion. 
In modern calciners, the temperatures can range between 800 to 1200 °C, with the highest temperature in the 
lower part, where the most of the combustion happens. The temperature reduces up through the calciner due 
to the endothermal calcination reaction of the raw meal [1]. The oxygen concentration varies between the 
riser duct 0.6-3.8 vol% to the inlet of the tertiary air supply of around 10 vol% [110,111], while the tertiary 
air itself is heated atmospheric air with an oxygen concentration of ~21%[1]. The water concentration in the 
gas phase varies between 7- 12 vol% through the calciner [111], larger variations can occur depending on the 
cement plant and the fuel used in both calciner and main burner[1]. A temperature of 1100 °C is used for the 
base conditions as it can then be varied in both directions and it is still in the end of the primary combustion 
temperature. As the tertiary air is added to ensure complete combustion of volatiles and char, the oxygen

5 Discussion 
Overall, the models developed for drying and devolatilization and the char oxidation performs quantitatively 
well in the validation simulations using experimental results from wood combustion. The largest deviation 
from a single datapoint is 30 % for both the devolatilization time and the full combustion time, however the 
overall deviation is significantly lower. This includes both spherical and cylindrical particles of different 
wood types, different slip velocities and oxygen contents for the gas phase. It is unknown how the models 
will perform in regards to simulating the combustion of the other non-plastic RDF components like paper 
and cardboard, as the availability of such validation material is poor. However, paper and cardboard have 
very similar combustion behavior to wood as it is based on cellulose. This can be seen from the proximate 
analysis presented in Table 3-4 and the pyrolysis kinetic data presented in Grammelis et al. [112] and Sørum 
et al. [102]. Currently unpublished experimental data from DTU CHEC also shows similar char oxidation 
kinetics for wood, cardboard and paper [103]. Furthermore, as seen in the sensitivity analysis made in this 
work, the most dominant factors is not kinetics, but the internal heat transfer during pyrolysis and external 
mass transfer during char oxidation. The performance for plate like geometries, which is interesting with 
regards to the paper, cardboard and textile fractions of RDF has been validated using experimental data 
either. 
The qualitative behavior of the models during conversion is in good agreement with what could be expected. 
This can be seen in the sudden increase up in heating rate, at the surface and core after full devolatilization in 
the temperature-time profiles in Figure 4-2 and the speedup of char conversion as mass transfer resistance is 
reduced in Figure 4-10.The exception for the lack of the drying plateau for the temperature at around 100 °C 
reported for fuel with high moisture content [73]. This can be seen in the simulation of the model particle in 
Figure 4-21 in section 4.4.3, where there is still moisture in the fuel after the temperature passes 400 K. This 
is either caused by the small particle sizes simulated, not allowing for the effect to manifest or by too low 
kinetic parameters for drying, as a faster rate of drying might lead to sufficient cooling effect by the 
endothermal process.  
As seen from the sensitivity analysis in sections 4.1.7.2 and 4.2.6 and the simulation of different conditions 
and fuel properties 4.4.3 the influence of the different parameters are all in good agreement with what would 
be expected including the effect of the moisture content increasing the devolatilization time. The exception 
being the problematic effects with the radiative heat transfer when increasing the porosities and pore sizes. 
However, increasing these would give unrealistic high values with porosities above 0.9 and pore sizes at 
around a tenth of the particle size. 
For the devolatilization model the primary assumption used to develop the model are listed below 
1. There is local thermal equilibrium between the solid, liquid and gas phase inside the particle. 
2. The heat capacity of the gas phase inside the particle is negligible 
3. The heat of devolatilization is zero or negligible 
4. The drying can be described by a chemical reaction rate accounting for all resistances to evaporation 
5. The distribution of char and volatiles after conversion is known fixed parameters 
The implications of the first assumption are removed by the second assumption, as the thermal equilibrium 
between the phases would be used to model the internal convective heat transfer which is assumed 
negligible. As shown in Cozzani et al. [13] the influence of the convective heat transfer by the volatiles are 
negligible, especially at higher temperatures. Furthermore, it greatly simplifies the model.

Assumption 3 is generally considered valid for mass loss models such as this one [60]. Furthermore, the heat 
of devolatilization is considered hard to measure due to the low values caused by the endothermic volatile 
formation and exothermic char formation [113].  
As discussed above the use of the chemical reaction rate model for water evaporation could not replicate the 
drying plateau at ~373 K, therefore using assumption 4 might not be good for predicting the temperature 
profiles in the particle. However, quantitative the effect on the combustion time might still be valid, as seen 
from the simulation results for different fuel moisture contents in Figure 4-28, the devolatilization time is 
doubled between dry fuel and fuel with a moisture content of 30 %. This is similar to the values obtained by 
Bryden et al. [105]. Other parameters for this model exists and could be investigated if the drying influence 
should be improved. Furthermore, the model does not describe the presence of free water, thus if fuel with 
moisture contents above the fiber saturation points need to be simulated, this should be implemented in the 
model. 
Assumption 5 is required in order to use the kinetic implemented in the devolatilization model, however it is 
well known that the char yield is dependent on the temperature and heating rate at which the devolatilization 
takes place. As the heating rate is different throughout the particle the char density might vary within the 
particle, however as seen on the simulation example for pine wood and the simulation of cardboard, the 
majority of the conversion happens through high temperature kinetics due to the high heating rate. Due to 
this the char yield should correspond to high heating rate char yield. The difference in char yield would be 
most problematic for large thick particles where low temperature kinetics are more important. 
Due to practical reasons constant value for the heat capacity and solid thermal conductivity of the virgin fuel 
was used. This made it possible to implement literature values of these parameters for different RDF 
materials. This could be problematic as these properties are known to be temperature dependent. However, 
due to opposite effects of the temperature, and the poor results from the available correlations when 
compared to tabular values, using constant values for the material modeled remain the best solution at the 
moment. 
The assumption of no shrinkage during conversion has the biggest impact on the char oxidation model as 
particle size highly influences the external mass transfer. If the shrinkage is known this can be corrected by 
adjusting the particle size before calculating the char oxidation time, as the two models are independent. 
Supposing the fixed carbon content is the true value, the no shrinkage assumption, will give a over estimate 
of the char particle size and an under estimate of the char density, as the char mass must be constant.  
The ultimate goal of combustion modeling is to be used to make predictions of combustion behavior in real 
processes where large quantities of fuel is used. 
As discussed in section 3.5.1, RDF can be grouped into 3 main groups: charring biomass material, non-
charring plastics and non-combustibles. The groups are based on the qualitative combustion behavior and the 
model types needed to describe it. The biomass materials have been investigated in this work and in order to 
model the combustion processes on a unit operation scale the particles have to be subdivided further, due to 
the heterogeneous nature of RDF even within the material categories.  
The first obvious subdivision is to group particles in one of the 3 different geometries: sphere, cylinder and 
plate, depending on which describes its shape the best. A fuel analysis would then have to be developed to 
differ between the different geometries, especially the plate geometry, this could be a combination of sieve 
analysis and particle image analysis/photoscale measurement as suggest by Liedmann et al. [104]. This 
==PAGE:105==
Page 102 of 134 
 
would characterize the fuel particles sizes and geometries, with a key importance in finding the smallest 
dimension. 
From the sensitivity analysis and simulation of different particle properties, suggestions for further particle 
groupings can be made. Particles should be grouped based on the properties that have the highest influence 
on the combustion time. Besides from the critical temperature for devolatilization kinetics shift, the 
devolatilization time have the highest sensitivity to the virgin fuel properties such as density, thermal 
conductivity and heat capacity. However, subdividing the fuel based each on each of these parameters would 
lead to 9 more groupings for each geometry. Instead the grouping could be made based on the thermal 
diffusivity of the fuel material, 
  
       . For the char oxidation time, besides from the stoichiometric 
coefficient, the fuel property with the highest influence is the char density, hence this should be taken into 
account for the grouping. Using the no shrinking assumption the char density is equal to the fuel density 
multiplied by the char yield, hence the proximate analysis of the different materials is required. 
For each material category and sub category a combustion time distribution could be calculated based on the 
particle size distributions as the particle size/thickness determines the actual combustion time and behavior. 
This can be based on either the whole fuel samples particle size distribution or the 3 different geometries 
particle size distribution, or assumptions about the correlations between these. 
The material groupings suggested above does not take into account the aerodynamic properties of the 
particles, which decide the path and speed travelled by each particle and hence the conditions it 
experiences and for how long. This is important to known, as these have major influence on the 
conversion speed during the different combustion phases. As seen from the simulation results in 4.4.3 
the oxygen concentration have the largest effect, while the slip velocity and temperature have a medium 
effect and the water vapor concentration has the smallest effect.  
Another way to group the materials could be by using the method suggested in Johansen et al. [60] to 
convert the temperatures profiles and conversion behavior to sets of isothermal kinetic parameters and 
group the particles based on their mass and isothermal kinetic parameters. This also makes the models 
easier to implement in CFD modeling as the computational load of the system is significantly reduced 
[60].

6 Conclusion 
The literature survey showed that the typical compositions of RDF can be distribution into 3 primary groups: 
Biomass derived materials, plastics and non-combustibles. For the biomass derived material, the literature 
survey showed that the combustion related properties such as the proximate analysis and the pyrolysis 
kinetics are similar for the most common materials and woody biomass. Due to this it is concluded that 
models derived for woody biomass can be used for the biomass derived materials. Furthermore, from the 
literature study it could be concluded that the non-sphericity of the RDF materials can be dealt with by using 
simplified 1-dimensional geometries like infinite cylinder and slabs. 
The combustion model developed consists of two separate models: one handling the heating, drying and 
devolatilization processes and another one handling the char oxidation process. 
From the sensitivity analysis for the drying and devolatilization model it can be concluded that this model is 
most sensitive to the parameters related to thermal diffusivity and to the critical temperature separating the 
kinetic mechanisms. This shows that this process is primarily controlled by internal heat transfer. 
Furthermore, it can be concluded that the drying and devolatilization model experiences numerical problems 
when the porosity goes towards unity and when the pore size are in the same order of magnitude as the 
particle size.  
From the sensitivity analysis for char oxidation model it can be concluded that this model is most sensitive to 
the parameters related to the char yield and the stoichiometric coefficient for the oxygen-char reactions. The 
sensitivity to the kinetic rate constants was low showing that the process under the simulated conditions is 
primarily controlled by external mass transfer. 
The model has been validated for both spherical and cylindrical particle for two different biomass materials 
using experimental data from single particle combustion measurements at different reactor conditions. The 
average deviation between the model and the experimental values for the devolatilization time was ±3.8 %, 
and ±30 % for the experimental data from Lu et al. [109] and Momeni et al. [90] respectively. The validation 
material showed that the char oxidation model using a stoichiometric coefficient of 2 for the oxygen-char 
reaction and including water gasification should be used. For the char oxidation times the average deviation 
between the model and the experimental data was ±13 % and ±12 % from the same sources. All the 
validation model results were within the experimental deviation for both sources. 
It can be concluded that the model can predict the combustion time and the time for both distinct phases of 
combustion within the experimental accuracy of single particle combustion measurements. 
The combustion of a disc shaped RDF particle of cardboard, with a of diameter 13 mm, a thickness of 0.65 
mm and a moisture content of 15 wt%, with a slip velocity of 8.2 m/s in a cement calciner bulk gas 
properties of 1373 K and 10 vol% oxygen and water concentration has been simulated. The devolatilization 
time and char oxidation time was found to be 1.3 and 7 seconds respectively, giving a total combustion time 
of 8.3 seconds. 
From the simulation of the combustion of a cardboard RDF particle under different conditions it can be 
concluded that the combustion time is most sensitive to the bulk oxygen concentration, followed by the bulk 
temperature. The water concentration in the bulk gas phase has a slight influence as well. Furthermore, it can 
be concluded that due to the significant influence of the slip velocity on the combustion time, the precision of 
the estimate of this value is important. 
==PAGE:107==
Page 104 of 134 
 
From the simulation of the combustion of cardboard particles with different sizes and moisture contents, it 
can be concluded that, the smallest dimension of a fuel particle, in this case the thickness, is the most 
influential on the combustion time. Furthermore, it can be concluded that the moisture content have a large 
influence in the initial phase of combustion. 
It is suggested that in order to deal with the heterogeneous nature of RDF when modeling, the biomass 
derived material should be grouped with respect to one of the three simplified geometries and if necessary 
subgroups based on the materials thermal diffusivity and the char density.  
7 Future work 
Validation data for different materials common in RDF should be acquired through single particle 
combustion of different materials, geometries and particle size. This should include non-perfect geometries 
and particles larger than 10 mm as this is common for RDF. The model should then be tested against this 
material to validate its applicability and find its limitations. 
The effect of different simplifying assumption such as; no internal convective heat transfer and isothermal 
char oxidation should be tested against models that include these phenomena.  
The effect of the volatile release on the laminar film layer should be investigated. This could be done by 
using a model including the internal convective heat transfer and assuming the volatile leaves the particle 
uniformly distributed at the surface. Furthermore, the possibility of the volatiles leaving through only a few 
larger pores creating a jet effect disrupting the laminar film layer should be considered. 
The long term goal of modeling RDF is to facilitate better implementation of RDF in existing cement plants 
and to help improve future equipment design for RDF combustion. As such future, work with the single 
particle models developed in this work should include efforts on improving the models relevant for CFD 
software, as this is an important tool for in depth computational investigations of combustion processes. In 
order to do this, the model can be used to convert the devolatilization kinetics to isothermal kinetics for 0-
dimensional models as done for biomass in Johansen et al. [60]

Thermogravimetric analysis 
Thermogravimetric analysis (TGA) is widely used to investigate thermal degradation of materials. In relation 
to solid combustion, it is used to study the kinetics of both pyrolysis and char oxidation, by looking at the 
mass loss rates dependency on temperature and gas phase composition.  
The sample placed in a well-controlled furnace, on a microscale recording the change in mass. The sample is 
then heated at either a constant heating rate or constant mass loss. The experiments are performed in a 
controlled atmosphere. An inert atmosphere can be used in order to study only drying and pyrolysis, while a 
controlled oxygen concentration can then be used to study char oxidation. Furthermore water and CO2 can be 
used for studying gasification. The sample can be milled to a fine powder increasing homogeneity and 
reducing mass and heat transfer limitations, in order to study the intrinsic kinetics.

Single particle combustion 
Singe particle combustion can be studied in various ways. In the data used in the validation of the model in 
this work the CHEC groups Single Particle Combustor located at the Department of Chemical and 
Biochemical engineering has been used. 
This consists of a vertical ceramic tube with a hydrogen burner in the bottom. The temperature and gas phase 
composition can be measured via various ports in the side. The samples are inserted on a platinum holder 
inside a ceramic heat-shield with is withdrawn to start the heat-up. The combustion process is then captured 
by a high speed camera, from which the length of the different combustion phases can be analyzed. The 
devolatilization time is recorded as the time a clear visible flame surround the particle. The char oxidation 
time is from the moment the flame is extinguished until complete oxidation of the char particle visible due to 
the glow of the char surface.

function dcdt = model_py(t,c0,p,RC,SFOR,sel) 
%MODEL_PY.m function script solving the coupled heat balance and 
conversion 
%of volatiles and bound water 
%   The model_py.m script is a FV spatial discretization of the PDE 
system 
%   coupling the heat balance of the single particle to the drying and 
%   devolatilization processes. 
%% Initial values 
% Initial values are restored from c0 in the call script 
  
%% Initial conditions 
T     = c0(1:p.len_r);              % [K] Temperature 
X     = c0(p.len_r+1:2*p.len_r);          % [-] Release of volatiles 
==PAGE:131==
Page 128 of 134 
 
Y     = c0(2*p.len_r+1:end); 
  
%% Constants 
sigma   = 5.6703*10^(-8);           % Stefan-Boltzmann konstant 
Rg      = 8.31447;                  % Gas constant 
  
%% Vectors 
r     = linspace(0,p.r0,p.len_r)'; 
dr    = p.dr; 
len_r = length(r); 
dTdt  = ones(len_r,1)*298; 
  
% In- or exclude swelling 
if sel.swell == 1 
    swell = swell_f(p,X); 
    r_swell = p.r0*(1-swell); 
else 
    r_swell = p.r0; 
end 
  
epsilon = 4*pi*r_swell^(2)/(4*pi*p.r0^(2))*p.epsilon;  % [-] net epsilon 
hg = Gas_h(RC.v,RC.Tg,2*r_swell); % calculates the external heat transfer 
%coefficient  
char    = p_char(T,X,p); %calculates the char properties 
kt      = char(:,1); 
dens    =char(:,2); 
cp      =char(:,3); 
  
%% Particle drying calculations 
% Enthalpy of evaporation and desorption of bound water 
  
% [J/kg] enthalpy of evap 
Hlw     = 1000.*(3179-2.5.*T); 
% [J/kg] Enthalpy of desorption 
Hsorp   = 0.4*Hlw.*(1-Y./(p.Mfsp+0.298-0.001.*T)).^(2); 
  
% [J/kg] Net enthalpy of particle drying (desorption and evaporation) 
Hvap    = Hlw+Hsorp; 
  
% Net equation for particle drying 
kH2O    = SFOR.H2OA.*T.^(1/2).*exp(-SFOR.H2OEa./(Rg.*T)); 
dYdt    = kH2O.*(p.proximate(1)-Y); 
  
%% Particle temperature calculations 
K         = kt./(dens.*cp);                    % [m^2/s] 
  
%% Boundary conditions 
% Heat transport, surface 
qk        = hg*(RC.Tg-T(end));                     % [J/(m^2 s)] 
%convection 
qs        = epsilon*sigma*(RC.Tv^(4)-T(end)^(4));  % [J/(m^2 s)] 
%Radiation

% Center, adiabatic boundary 
dTdt(1)   = kt(1)/(dens(1)*cp(1))*(T(2)-T(1))/(dr^2)... 
    -dYdt(1)*Hvap(1)*p.m0*p.proximate(1); 
  
% Surface, external flux boundary 
% Calculates T_N+1 
Tend = T(end-1)+(qs+qk)*dr/kt(end); 
% Calculates dTdt(end) 
dTdr      = (Tend-T(end-1)) /(2*dr); 
d2Tdr2    = (Tend-2*T(end)+T(end-1)) /dr^2; 
dTdt(end)   = K(end)*1/r(end)^(2)*(2*r(end)*dTdr+r(end)^(2)*d2Tdr2)... 
    -dYdt(end)*Hvap(end)*p.m0*p.proximate(1); 
  
%% Main domain calculations 
i     = 2:len_r-1;                              % 1 => r=0, end => r=R 
% Temperature gradients 
dTdr      = (T(i+1)-T(i-1)) /(2*dr); 
d2Tdr2    = (T(i+1)-2*T(i)+T(i-1)) /dr^2; 
  
% Net ODE 
dTdt(i)   = K(i)*1./r(i).^(2).*(2*r(i).*dTdr+r(i).^(2).*d2Tdr2)... 
    -dYdt(i).*Hvap(i).*p.m0.*p.proximate(1); 
  
%% Devolatilization calculations 
% Calculating the blending function for high and low HR kinetics 
k = zeros(length(T),1); 
  
for i=1:length(T) 
if T(i) <SFOR.t_crit 
        k(i) = SFOR.A1.*exp(-(SFOR.Ea1)./(Rg.*T(i))).^(SFOR.n1)... 
            + min((SFOR.A2.*exp(-(SFOR.Ea2)./(Rg.*T(i))).^(SFOR.n2)),... 
            (SFOR.A3.*exp(-(SFOR.Ea3)./(Rg.*T(i))).^(SFOR.n3))); 
else 
        k(i) = SFOR.AHigh.*exp(-(SFOR.EaHigh)./(Rg.*T(i))).^(SFOR.nHigh); 
end 
% Net devolatilization equation 
dXdt = k.*(p.proximate(2)-X); 
  
%% Summary 
dcdt  = [dTdt; dXdt; dYdt]; 
  
end

function h = Gas_h(v,Tg,Ts,d,alpha,p) 
%Gas_h.m Calculates the heat transfer coefficient 
%   Function script Gas_h.m calulates the heat transfer coefficient for a 
%   the particle. All correlations rely on dry air. 
if alpha==0 
    d=p.L; 
else 
    d=d; 
end 
%% Heat Transfer coefficient [J/(m^2 K s)]

% v = [m/s] bulk flow velocity  
% Tg = [K] gas phase temperature at infinity r 
% Ts= [K] surface temperature of particle 
% d = [m] particle diameter/characteristic length 
% alpha= shape factor 0 = slab, 1 = cylinder and 2 = sphere 
%% Constants 
Mw   = 0.02896;                   % [kg/mol]    Molar weight, dry air 
%% Correlations  
T=(Ts+Tg)/2; 
% calculates avarage values 
cP  = 31.2-1.46e-02*T+3.29e-05*T^2-2.09e-08*T^3+4.36e-12*T^4;       % 
[J/(mol K)] specific heat capacity 
kt   = -1.72e-04+1.03e-04*T-5.67e-08*T^2+2.68e-11*T^3-4.92e-15*T^4; % 
[J/(m K s)] specific heat conductivity 
mu   = 3.98e-07+7.24e-08*T-4.81e-11*T^2+2.28e-14*T^3-4.20e-18*T^4;  % [Pa 
%s] Viscosity 
rho  = 362.65*T^-1.006;                                             %  
% [kg/m^3] density 
  
%% Dimensionless numbers 
Re   = v.*rho.*d./mu;               % [-] Reynolds number 
Pr   = cP.*mu./(Mw*kt);             % [-] Prandtls number 
if alpha == 1 %Nusselt number for cylinders 
    if Re <= 4 
    Nu   = 0.989.*Re.^(0.33).*Pr.^(1/3); 
    elseif Re <= 40 
    Nu   = 0.911.*Re.^(0.385).*Pr.^(1/3);   
    else 
    Nu   = 0.683.*Re.^(0.466).*Pr.^(1/3);  
    end 
elseif alpha == 2 
    Nu   = 2+0.6.*Re.^(1/2).*Pr.^(1/3); % [-] Nusselt number, sphere 
elseif alpha==0 
    Nu   = 0.648*Re.^(1/2).*Pr.^(1/3); % [-] Nusselt number, plate   
end 
%% heat transfer coefficient 
h    = Nu.*kt./d;                   % [J/(m^2 K s)]

Laminar mass transfer film layer 
function [hm l_film] = film( v,T,R,alpha,p ) 
%film.m Calculates the thickness of the laminar film for mass transfer 
%   Function script film.m calulates the thickness of the mass transfer 
boundary layer for a 
%   the particle. All correlations rely on dry air. 
  
%% Film thickness [m] 
% v = [m/s] bulk flow velocity  
% Tg = [K] gas phase temperature at infinity r 
% Ts= [K] surface temperature of particle 
% d = [m] particle diameter/characteristic length 
% alpha= shape factor 0 = slab, 1 = cylinder and 2 = sphere 
if alpha==0 
    d=p.L ; 
else 
    d=R*2; 
end

mu   = 3.98e-07+7.24e-08*T-4.81e-11*T^2+2.28e-14*T^3-4.20e-18*T^4 ; % [Pa

rho  = 362.65*T^-1.006   ;                          % [kg/m^3] density
