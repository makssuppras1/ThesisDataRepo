ABSTRACT
Vision impairment affects over 2.2 billion people worldwide, with conditions such as retinitis
pigmentosa and age-related macular degeneration leading to retinal degeneration. Retinal im-
plants offer a potential solution by electrically stimulating retinal neurons, bypassing damaged
photoreceptors.
While existing devices, such as the Argus II, have demonstrated functional
improvements, they remain limited by low resolution and poor integration with natural eye
movements. This project focuses on detecting neural activity, in the form of spikes, in electri-
cal signals recorded from porcine neuroretinal tissue, utilizing photovoltaic, 3D carbon-based
electrodes. These biocompatible electrodes require no external power source, making them a
promising advancement for retinal prostheses.
The study analyses three data types: spontaneous (unstimulated baseline activity), stimulated
(electrically evoked activity), and TTX (recordings after tetrodotoxin application to block neural
activity, confirming the biological origin of spikes). Since real ground-truth data is unavailable,
experiments are also conducted on synthetic datasets. Given the presence of noise and artefacts
in recordings, a preprocessing pipeline based on independent component analysis is implemented
before detecting neural activity
For detection, a non-data-driven approach using the continuous wavelet transform and canonical
polyadic decomposition is explored, as certain mother wavelets closely resemble action potential
waveforms. Additionally, a supervised approach is introduced using a support vector regression-
based model. Two simple methods are implemented to establish baselines: a threshold-based
detector, which relies on baseline noise levels, and a mean predictor, which assumes a constant
average number of spikes per segment.
The preprocessing pipeline showed promising results but was not able to completely remove
all artefacts. Among detection methods, support vector regression achieved the lowest errors,
with half of the predictions deviating by less than 25% from the true number of spikes. The
wavelet-based approach required careful parameter tuning and tended to overestimate when
not optimally configured. Three out of four methods detected a decrease in spike counts from
stimulated to TTX and spontaneous data, supporting the hypothesis that the device effectively
evokes neural activity.
These findings underscore the potential of machine learning-based approaches for neural activity
detection while highlighting the challenges of designing robust, generalizable models for noisy
electrophysiological data.

CHAPTER1
INTRODUCTION
The World Health Organization (WHO) has estimated that more than 2.2 billion people world-
wide suffer from near or distance vision impairment (WHO 2023). Although leading causes
include refractive errors and cataracts, a significant proportion is due to conditions such as re-
tinitis pigmentosa (RP) and age-related macular degeneration (AMD). They are both caused
by the deterioration of the photoreceptors, with AMD primarily affecting central vision and RP
affecting peripheral vision. The prevalence of RP is 1 in 4000 (Zhang 2016), and AMD affects
one in eight people 60 years of age or older (Vyawahare and Shinde 2022). Loss of vision can
significantly impact quality of life, and a study has shown a link between vision impairment and
an increased risk of dementia (Shang et al. 2021). Clinicians and scientists have been looking for
a solution for centuries. Benjamin Franklin claimed to the Royal Society of London that sight
and hearing could be restored with electricity (Mills, Jalil, and Stanga 2017). Later in 1929,
the German neurosurgeon Otfrid Foerster had a patient who experienced phosphenes - seeing
spots of light without light entering the eye - when he electrically stimulated the visual cortex
(Billingsley and Bradbeer 2008). However, it was not until the 20th century that there was a
significant breakthrough in artificial vision.
Andrew Huxley was a student of physiology at Cambridge and was invited by research scholar
Alan Hodgkin to conduct experiments on squid giant axons1 (Schwiening 2012). After some
unsuccessful experiments to determine the viscosity of axoplasm, the duo inserted an electrode
inside the nerve fibre. It was thus the first-ever recording of an intracellular action potential
(AP) took place. They demonstrated that the movement of sodium and potassium ions caused
the electrical impulses along nerve fibres. This knowledge of how neurons send electrical signals
has enabled the development of devices that can take over functionalities usually performed by
neurons.
The above-mentioned conditions, RP and AMD, target the photoreceptors in the retina. Pho-
toreceptors are neurons responsible for converting light to electrical impulses. Retinal implants
are thus devices that aim to replace this functionality (Ayton et al. 2020). Several factors com-

in degenerative diseases. Phosphenes can be evoked by electrical stimulation along any point
of the visual pathways, however retinal stimulation has been the most successful (Luo and Da
Cruz 2016). The reasons for this include better access during surgery than intracranial sites
and potentially predictable and reproducible retinal mapping by stimulation at a pre-processing
site.
There are two overall methods for retinal implants to mimic the behaviour of photoreceptors;
direct electrical stimulation and photodiodes. In direct electrical stimulation, there is both an
internal and external system. Usually camera-based, the external part sends an image of the
outside world to a processing unit that further sends it to electrodes implanted in the eye.
Photovoltaic implants differ slightly by using incident light to project an image onto the retina.
Photodiodes then transduce the signal into electrical stimulation.
As of 2019, there are three regulatory-approved retinal prostheses, and approximately 500 pa-
tients have been implanted globally (Ayton et al. 2020). The Argus II was the first prosthetics
vision device to obtain regulatory approval in both Europe and the USA (Luo and Da Cruz
2016). It is a wireless, epiretinal system meaning it is embedded on top of the retina directly
stimulating the retinal ganglion cells, and thereby bypassing all remaining retinal layers. Evoked
responses are sent to the brain via the optic nerve for interpretation as usual. Patients implanted
with the device have shown improvements in visual functions with some even being able to iden-
tify letters. However, visual information is not dependent on eye movements and with only 60
electrodes the resolution is limited (Wang et al. 2012). Ideally, an implant would devote one
electrode to each retinal ganglion cell to match the resolution of the visual system. This requires
electrodes to have a size in the order of a few micro-metres. Reducing the electrode area, how-
ever, increases the current and charge density which can cause tissue damage (Sekirnjak et al.
2006).
This project aims to detect neural activity in electrical signals recorded from porcine neuroretinal
tissue.
Three types of measurements are analysed: spontaneous activity, which provides a
baseline of neural activity without stimulation; evoked activity, where neural responses are
elicited through electrical stimulation; and evoked activity following the application of a neural
agent designed to inhibit nerve function, serving as a control to validate the biological origin of
detected spikes. Such recordings are usually dominated by noise stemming from the recording
hardware, the ambient recording environment, and the spatially averaged activity of distant
cells (Nenadic and Burdick 2005). Furthermore, unlike their intracellular counterparts, there
is a great variability in the shape and amplitude of the potentials. The retinal implants used
in this project do not need an external power source, and thus run completely photovoltaically
(Kusumanchi et al. 2025). Furthermore, the electrodes are 3D minimizing proximity to target
neurons as well as being made of carbon that are bio-compatible.
1.1
Related Work
For a retinal implant to function effectively, it must be capable of evoking neural activity. Over
time, various prosthetic designs have been proposed.
At the time of Sekirnjak et al. 2006,

retinal implants used a small number of electrodes measuring several hundred micrometres in
diameter. They aimed to address this limitation by designing and testing a microfabricated
multielectrode array capable of stimulating individual retinal ganglion cells. Their approach
made use of electrodes with diameters ranging from 6 to 25 µm, to increase spatial and temporal
precision. Unlike earlier designs, which simultaneously stimulated large groups of neurons, this
study demonstrated the potential for direct activation of individual cells. Using small-diameter
electrodes also reduced the current and charge densities required for stimulation, maintaining
safety thresholds while achieving precise targeting.
In contrast to the epiretinal Argus II system, Wang et al. 2012 introduced a photodiode array
designed for subretinal stimulation. They argue that stimulating the inner retinal neurons while
utilizing the remaining functional retinal layers results in more natural spiking patterns. The
device was surgically implanted in rats and activated using near-infrared light projected onto
the implant. A notable advantage of this system is the simplified surgical procedure due to the
absence of an extraocular power supply; the implants operate entirely photovoltaically. The
study also highlights that even small electrodes can successfully stimulate the retina. Addition-
ally, it suggests that using pillar electrodes could reduce the stimulation threshold due to the
decreased proximity to target neurons.
Common to these studies is the electrical recording of neural activity. APs show up as spikes
in the data, hence, there is a need for peak detection algorithms, with numerous having been
proposed over time. A widely used technique is amplitude thresholding, where the threshold can
be set statically or be dependent of the estimated noise level. The method is easy to implement
and understand but fails quickly for low signal-to-noise (SNR) ratios. Power detection offers a
slight improvement by computing the power of the signal in a sliding window and identifying
spikes where the signal exceeds a threshold derived from the standard deviation and the mean
of the noise level (Harris et al. 2000). Matched filtering is another widespread method for peak
detection. The technique maximises the SNR when the signal of interest is embedded in white
noise. It can only be applied in a supervised manner, unfortunately, as the method is based on
template matching (Nenadic and Burdick 2005).
Another approach is to employ wavelet transforms.
They can be used in a wide range of
applications, such as data compression, noise removal, and, most importantly in this context,
pattern recognition (Milani, Abas, and De Silva 2022). An example of such an application is
Du, Kibbe, and Lin 2006, which aimed to detect spikes in mass spectra. Rather than detecting
peaks directly in the raw data, they incorporated a continuous wavelet transform (CWT) and
detected peaks by looking for local maxima in the wavelet space.
The results showed that
for mass spectra, they could accurately detect spikes even at low amplitudes whilst keeping
the number of false positives down.
The wavelet transform is not only applicable for peak
detection in mass spectra, as it has also been heavily used in electrocardiograms (ECG) (Merah,
Abdelmalik, and Larbi 2015; Zou et al. 2015; D’Aloia, Longo, and Rizzi 2019).
Wavelet transforms have been widely employed for detecting APs in extracellular recordings.
Nenadic and Burdick 2005 utilised wavelet decomposition across multiple scales to separate noise
from signal at each level. Spike detection was performed using Bayesian hypothesis testing,
where the arrival times of candidate APs were analysed to determine if sequential spikes should
be merged into a single event. Building on this, Shalchyan, Jensen, and Farina 2012 introduced

The wavelet transform is a self-contained method looking at the problem on a micro-scale, i.e.
on a peak-to-peak basis. One can also adopt a supervised technique and consider it a regression
problem predicting the number of spikes along an entire time series. There is a great number
of strategies for solving regression problems. Linear regression is a popular method due to its
simplicity and ability to analyse relationships between variables. The linearity makes it easy to
understand and interpret. For cases with correlated independent variables, ridge regression can
address multicollinearity and make the problem less ill-posed. However, these methods assume a
linear relationship between dependent and independent variables and typically require normally
distributed noise, limiting their applicability to more complex datasets.
Logistic regression,
while powerful for binary classification problems, is not suitable for continuous outcomes.
Support Vector Machines (SVM) provide a robust alternative for regression tasks, as they are
able to model non-linear relationships. Kernel functions map data to a higher-dimensional space
where the data is linearly separable. Support Vector Regression (SVR), an extension of SVM, is
based on the same principles but adapted to predict continuous values. Applications of SVR in
time series analysis have been promising. For example, Lu, Lee, and Chiu 2009 proposed a two-
step approach combining Independent Component Analysis (ICA) and SVR for financial time
series forecasting. The ICA stage aimed to mitigate the challenges of noisy and non-stationary
data, inherent in financial time series, by isolating and removing noisy components. When the
denoised data was fed into the SVR model, the framework outperformed traditional SVR and
random walk models in terms of reduced prediction errors.
Nonetheless, a potential drawback of SVR in this context is its focus on the global character-
istics of the data, potentially overlooking local trends that may be crucial for spike detection.
Khorrami and Moavenian 2010 performed a classification of ECG signals using SVM. However,
rather than performing the classification in the time domain, features were extracted before-
hand using both the discrete and continuous wavelet transform. For their specific purpose, the
discrete transform outperformed the continuous counterpart.
1.2
Aim of Thesis
For this device to function as a retinal prosthesis in the future, it must reliably mimic the
role of photoreceptors by stimulating the remaining retinal nerves using electrical signals. This
thesis focuses on detecting compound action potentials (CAPs) in electrical recordings from the
device to assess whether it effectively activates the retinal nerves. To validate this functionality,
comparative experiments will be conducted between stimulated data and both spontaneous
activity and recordings obtained after applying a neural agent.
Since signal data is often noisy and dominated by artefacts, a preprocessing pipeline is ap-
plied before any detection algorithm. The detection is initially based on the method proposed

by Kusumanchi et al. 2025, serving as a baseline. To improve performance, a self-contained
approach is introduced, combining the widely used wavelet transform, (Nenadic and Burdick
2005), and Canonical Polyadic Decomposition (CPD) (Diniz 2019). Additionally, Support Vec-
tor Regression (SVR) is employed for comparison with a supervised, data-driven approach.
A synthetic data generation procedure is implemented to facilitate validation and supervised
learning techniques. The following research questions can sum this up:
1
To what extent can linear unmixing methods, such as ICA, enhance the separation of
CAPs from background noise and artefacts in electrical signals?
2
How can prior knowledge of the shape and duration of CAPs be used to design wavelet
transforms that maximize correlation with the signal of interest?
3
How do the counting precision and reliability of CAP detection compare across the wavelet-
based, SVR, and thresholding methods, and what are the strengths and limitations of
each?
4
Does the number of detected CAPs decrease from stimulated to spontaneous and neural
agent recordings in a manner consistent with the findings of Kusumanchi et al. 2025?
Several factors motivate the choice of using more traditional machine learning methods rather
than large deep learning models. Wavelet transforms are a well-established technique for fea-
ture extraction in time series, allowing prior knowledge of the signal of interest to be easily
incorporated. The transform that not only works on a time scale but also a frequency scale is
easy to interpret. Additionally, their unsupervised nature makes them suitable for tasks where
labelled data is unavailable. Although data-driven, SVMs require significantly fewer training
samples than artificial neural networks (ANNs), making them a faster alternative (Khorrami
and Moavenian 2010). Good representations can be challenging to obtain in the time domain,
which is why regression is performed in the frequency domain, where essential features have
already been extracted.

CHAPTER2
BIOLOGICAL BACKGROUND
The human senses define our relationship with the surrounding environment. Among the many
senses possessed by humans, the sense of vision is especially important; we perceive up to 80%
of our impressions through sight (zeiss 2021) and are able to distinguish between thousands
of different colours. In the brain, more space is allocated for the visual system than for all
other senses combined. As such, vision is a critical sensory input, and studies have linked vision
impairment with increased risk of dementia, as discussed by Shang et al. 2021.
The eye is a complex organ, with significant processing occurring before signals are transmitted
to the brain. To understand its mechanism, we must first understand the anatomy of the eye
and the role of photoreceptors. Photoreceptors, a specialized type of neuron, play a central role,
and this chapter will also dive into their structure and functions.
2.1
Anatomy of the Eye
The eye, though small, is an intricate organ. Figure 2.1 provides an overview of its structural
components. From the outermost layer inward, the first encountered is the conjunctiva, a mucous
membrane. Beneath it lies the sclera, a white membrane about 0.67 mm thick (Norman et al.
2010), which helps maintain the shape of the eye. Next is the choroid, a thin, pigmented layer
(Reece and Campbell 2011).
In addition to the conjunctiva, the anterior segment of the eye includes the cornea, anterior
chamber, lens, and iris. The cornea is a transparent layer that covers the iris and pupil, serving
two primary functions: protecting the eye from germs and transmitting light to the retina and
lens. The iris, the coloured part of the eye, contains muscles that regulate the size of the pupil,
the central black dot, thereby controlling the amount of light reaching the retina. The lens
adjusts its shape to focus on objects, contributing 30% of the eye’s focusing power, while the
cornea provides the remaining 70% (Courville, Smolek, and Klyce 2004). Positioned between
the iris and the cornea, the anterior chamber is filled with a fluid called aqueous humour.

Moving further in the eye, we reach the posterior segment. While several structural components

vitreous body, also known as the vitreous humour, comprises 80% of the eye’s volume. This
clear gel-like substance fills the vitreous chamber between the lens and the retina. In addition
to maintaining the eye’s shape, it plays a vital role in nourishing the surrounding structures
At the back of the eye, light reaches the retina, a complex structure made up of millions of
photoreceptors and other specialized cells.
Its central region, the macula, is responsible for
detailed central vision. Within the macula lies the fovea, a tiny area barely half a millimetre in
diameter, responsible for sharp focus. To understand the intricate processes of the retina, we
will first explore the functions of photoreceptors and the mechanisms through which neurons
propagate signals. A more detailed discussion of the retina will follow in Section 2.3.
The axons of retinal ganglion cells (RGCs) converge to form the optic nerve, which exits the
eye at the optic disk, creating a hole in the retina. This area, incapable of detecting light,
is commonly referred to as the ”blind spot.” RP and AMD can damage the photoreceptors,
rendering them non-functional. As a result, the retina loses its ability to detect light, leading
to vision impairment. With this context, we now dive deeper into the structure and function of
photoreceptors.

acuity, we rely on just 100.000 cones or ∼0.1% of our total amount of photoreceptors.

2.2.2
Transport across the Cell Membrane
The simplest form of transport across the cell membrane is passive diffusion (Mordecai P.
Blaustein, Joseph P.Y. Kao, and Donal R. Matteson 2012). In this process, a molecule dissolves
into the phospholipid bilayer, diffuses across it, and re-enters the aqueous environment on the
opposite side of the membrane. This mechanism does not require assistance from membrane-
embedded proteins or energy input; it is purely driven by the relative concentrations of the
molecule inside and outside the cell. Due to the lack of energy expenditure, this process is also
known as passive transport. The ease with which molecules pass through the membrane depends
on its permeability, but only a select group of molecules—such as O2 and C2O—can diffuse pas-
sively. Water-soluble molecules get repelled by the hydrophobic tails of the phospholipids and
can therefore not passively diffuse as well as substances that are fat-soluble (Biga et al. 2019)
This is depicted on the left side of Figure 2.5, where a carbon dioxide and an oxygen molecule
are shown diffusing across the membrane.
Another type of passive transport is facilitated diffusion, as illustrated in the middle of Figure
2.5. Like simple diffusion, it is considered passive because it does not require energy; movement
occurs along the electrochemical gradient. However, this process is facilitated by specific proteins
embedded in the membrane, which assist in transporting molecules that cannot freely diffuse
through the lipid bilayer.
The cell membrane contains various types of transport proteins,
including channels, carriers, and pumps, with more than a third of the human genome dedicated
to encoding these membrane proteins (Mordecai P. Blaustein, Joseph P.Y. Kao, and Donal R.
Matteson 2012). Facilitated diffusion specifically involves channels and carriers, enabling the
transport of charged or larger molecules across the membrane that would otherwise be unable
to pass (Geoffrey M. Cooper 2000).
Channel proteins are open pores within the cell membrane that allow molecules of the appro-
priate size and charge to pass through. Both ends of the pore are simultaneously open, enabling
rapid transport. For instance, a single channel can facilitate the movement of approximately
30 million K+ ions per second. A specific type of channel, known as a voltage-gated channel,
opens when the membrane potential reaches a certain threshold, allowing ion flow in response to
changes in electrical charge. The two blue membrane proteins depicted in Figure 2.5 illustrate
examples of an ion channel in its closed and open states.
Carrier proteins are responsible for transporting molecules such as sugars and amino acids
across the membrane, with glucose carriers being an example. Unlike channels, carrier proteins
function by undergoing conformational changes to facilitate the transport of a molecule. The
process begins when the molecule binds to the carrier protein, triggering a gate on one side of the
membrane to close, effectively locking the molecule within the carrier. Subsequently, another
conformational change causes a gate on the opposite side to open, allowing the molecule to pass
through. This mechanism is illustrated on the right side of Figure 2.5. Because both the binding
of molecules to the carrier and the associated conformational changes require time, transport
via carrier proteins is significantly slower than channel-mediated transport. However, carrier
proteins accelerate the movement of specific molecules compared to passive diffusion, enhancing
transport efficiency.
The final mode of transport across the cell membrane is active transport, which relies on energy

Neurons and Action Potentials
A schematic of a typical neuron is shown in Figure 2.8. Key components include the cell body,
which contains the nucleus, and dendrites that receive inputs from other neurons. The axon,
which originates at the axon hillock, varies in length depending on the neuron type, ranging from
a few millimetres to over a metre. It is through the axon that the neuron propagates signals.
Voltage-gated sodium channels are distributed along the axon, with a high concentration at the
axon hillock and nodes of Ranvier. At its terminal end, the axon branches into multiple terminal
branches, forming synaptic connections with target cells, which could be other neurons or tissues
such as muscle fibres (Mordecai P. Blaustein, Joseph P.Y. Kao, and Donal R. Matteson 2012).

resting membrane potential is approximately -70 mV. If the membrane potential reaches a

This
sudden change in membrane potential is known as an action potential (AP), as illustrated

The phase during which Na+ ions flow into the neuron is referred to as the
depolarisation phase, and it peaks at around +35 to +40 mV. This rise in voltage opens voltage-
gated potassium channels, allowing K+ ions to flow out of the cell along their electrochemical
gradient, initiating the repolarisation phase.
However, the potassium channels close slowly,
which causes the membrane potential to briefly drop below the resting level, a phenomenon
known as ”hyperpolarisation.” The duration of an AP varies across neuron types but typically
lasts between 1 and 2 milliseconds (Reece and Campbell 2011).
This mechanism is referred to as an ”all-or-none” response: once the threshold value is reached,
the same response is triggered every time, regardless of the strength of the stimulus. High-
intensity stimuli do not increase the magnitude of the APs, but they do increase the frequency at
which APs are generated (Reece and Campbell 2011). After each AP, there is a refractory period,
meaning that rapid, successive stimulation will not lead to additional APs.
This refractory
period arises from the inactivation of voltage-gated sodium channels during the repolarisation
phase, which prevents the influx of Na+ ions. The refractory period can be divided into the
absolute refractory period, during which no APs can be generated, and the relative refractory
period, when a stronger stimulus is required to trigger a response.

dendrites, opening ion channels that allow specific ions to flow into the cell. These ions can have
either excitatory or inhibitory effects. Excitatory ions increase the membrane potential, bringing
it closer to the threshold value, while inhibitory ions drive the membrane potential further away

Until now, we have focused on the generation of a single AP. However, for an organism to sense
anything, this signal must be transmitted all the way to the brain. An AP propagates along
the axon, with each AP serving as a stimulus to open successive voltage-gated sodium channels.
This process can be compared to knocking over the first domino in a row; as each sodium
channel opens, it triggers the depolarisation phase further along the axon. Meanwhile, behind
the advancing AP, the repolarisation phase occurs, rendering the sodium channels inactive. As
a result, the AP can only propagate in one direction, away from the cell body.
Eventually, the AP reaches the end of the axon, where it encounters another neuron or type of
tissue at a junction. In the case of another neuron, this junction is referred to as a synapse. Each
terminal branch of the axon forms a synaptic terminal (Reece and Campbell 2011). The neuron
transmitting the AP is referred to as the pre-synaptic neuron, while the receiving neuron is called
the post-synaptic neuron (see Figure 2.10). The pre-synaptic neuron contains vesicles filled with

The Retina
With an understanding of neurons, propagation of signals and the functionalities of photore-
ceptors, we are ready to explore the retina as a whole. A cross-section can be seen in Figure
2.12. The retina is comprised of five different types of neurons: photoreceptors, ganglion cells,
amacrine cells, bipolar cells, and horizontal cells. These are arranged into three nuclear and two
synaptic layers (Baden, Euler, and Berens 2020). The first nuclear layer, the outer nuclear layer
(ONL) contains the photoreceptors, and the first synaptic layer, the outer plexiform layer (OPL),
refers to the junction where these meet the bipolar and horizontal cells. The inner nuclear layer
(INL) is connected with amacrine and retinal ganglion cells in the inner plexiform layer (IPL).
The axons of ganglion cells gather in the nerve fiber layer to form the optic nerve. Despite the
large number of neurons, the mammalian retina only measures 100-200 µm in thickness (Gregg,
McCall, and Massey 2013).
Currently, the mouse retina serves as the benchmark in the field. In this thesis, data is recorded
from pigs as the structure and size match the human retina better than a rodent’s. A detailed
understanding of specific retinal circuits in humans and other mammals is still an active research

question (Baden, Euler, and Berens 2020). Below is a brief explanation of how light is processed
in the different layers of the retina.
When light enters the eye through the pupil, it has to pass through the entire retina first to reach
the photoreceptors in the back. When in darkness, photoreceptors are in a state of depolarization
(Reece and Campbell 2011). This might seem counterintuitive as we think of this phase as the
first part of an activation. However, this is the case for photoreceptors. There is a continuous
release of the neurotransmitter glutamate, referred to as the dark current. When light strikes the
photoreceptor, the sodium channels close off, causing the cell to hyperpolarize and the stream
of glutamate stops. This affects the next cells in line, namely the bipolar cells, as seen in Figure
2.12. The bipolar cells can have one of two reactions to the ceasing of the glutamate stream;
they either hyperpolarise or depolarise. A bipolar cell is said to be an OFF-center cell if it
hyperpolarises and an ON-center if it depolarises. This is determined by the synaptic contacts
the bipolar cells have with the respective rods and cones. ON-bipolar cells react to increments
in light, and OFF-bipolar cells react to decrements in light.
Another type of cell in the INL is the horizontal cell, seen as a purple cell in Figure 2.12.
Horizontal cells are lateral, inhibitive neurons that are coupled extensively, giving them a large
receptive field. By adapting to the ambient light level, the mean illumination can be subtracted.
This enables the photoreceptors to react to local changes in illumination that carry the most
information (Gregg, McCall, and Massey 2013). When the ambient light level increases, there
is a decrease in the electrical coupling of the horizontal cells. The local inhibition thus becomes
more local which enhances contrast (Bertalm´ıo 2020).
Retinal ganglion cells (RGCs), located in the ganglion cell layer (GCL), are divided into ON-
==PAGE:28==

19
center and OFF-center types, each responding differently to changes in light. ON-center cells
activate when light hits the center of their receptive field, while OFF-center cells respond to
darkness in the center. Importantly, ON-center cells are suppressed when light also illuminates
the surrounding area, a phenomenon called surround suppression. This mechanism ensures that
RGCs are sensitive to changes in light rather than constant illumination, efficiently encoding
changes in the visual scene. This reduces the number of signals transmitted from millions of
photoreceptors to approximately one million RGCs, highlighting the retina’s significant role in
visual processing. As the optic nerve is comprised of the axons of the RGCs, the signals are sent
to the brain for further processing.
Amacrine cells, found in the INL and depicted in pink in Figure 2.12, play a key role in lateral
inhibition within the retina. With roughly 30 distinct types, these cells contribute to various
functions, including signal averaging, noise reduction, and contrast enhancement (Gregg, Mc-
Call, and Massey 2013). Despite their importance, many details about their specific connections
and functional anatomy remain unknown, leaving opportunities for further research.
This entire workflow of processing light signals relies on photoreceptors transducing light into
electrical signals. In diseases such as RP and AMD, which directly affect photoreceptors, this
critical process breaks down, leading to vision loss. Retinal implants attempt to address this
deficiency by replicating or replacing the photoreceptors’ functionality.

good balance between time and frequency resolution, making it well-suited for detecting transient
waveforms. The Morlet wavelet, due to its oscillatory nature, offers better frequency localization
but at the expense of poorer time localization. This is reflected in its scalogram, where the
frequency components are well-defined, but the signal appears more spread in time. The Mexican
Hat wavelet is particularly effective for edge detection, which is clearly visible in its scalogram.
It provides sharp time localization but at the cost of lower frequency resolution, meaning the
detected features are more localized in time but less precise in frequency.

The Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT) selects a subset of scales from the CWT. In the context
of wavelet transforms, the terms ”continuous” and ”discrete” therefore do not refer to the nature
of the data, but rather the nature of the applied wavelets. The chosen scales are dyadic (aj = 2j)

In addition to limiting the number of scales, the DWT iteratively downsamples the signal as
the scale increases. Since scale and frequency are inversely related, the highest frequencies are
captured at the lowest scale. As the scale doubles, the highest frequency present is halved, be-
cause the highest frequency corresponds to the fastest oscillations of the wavelet. Stretching the
wavelet by increasing the scale extends these oscillations, causing every other data point to be-
come redundant. Consequently, downsampling retains only the essential points while preserving
all relevant information.
While the DWT is well-suited for data compression, it is less effective for detailed time-frequency
analysis (Arts and Van Den Broek 2022). This limitation is illustrated in Figure 3.4, where the
top panel shows a time-varying signal, and the middle and bottom panels display the cor-
responding DWT and CWT coefficients, respectively (Arts and Van Den Broek 2022). The
DWT’s coarse, logarithmic discretisation is evident, making it challenging to capture complex
time-frequency relationships. The downsampling is also noticeable, with more bins present at
higher frequencies.
In contrast, the CWT provides a more detailed representation of these
relationships, albeit at the cost of higher computational complexity.

DATA
Understanding the characteristics and structure of the recorded data is essential for analysing
neural activity in the retinal tissue. This chapter presents the experimental setup used for data
acquisition, followed by a detailed description of the collected data.
4.1
Experimental Setup
Six porcine eyes were collected at a Danish slaughterhouse immediately after euthanization. The
tissue was prepared by removing both the anterior segment and the vitreous body, whereafter a
segment of approximately 3 mm ×10 mm was dissected from the neuroretinal tissue. The time
between collection and dissection never exceeded one hour to ensure the nerves were still active
(Kusumanchi et al. 2025). The tissue was placed between the electrode chip with carbon pillars,
and a 32-channel multi-electrode array (MEA) was placed on the retinal ganglion side. This
setup, as well as the layout of the array, can be seen in Figure 4.1. There are more channels
in the array than the ones marked with numbers, however, they are the ones that are used
in the project. The black dot marks where a hole has been drilled. More information on the
preparation of the tissue and the fabrication of the MEA can be found in (Kusumanchi et al.
2025).
Several experiments were carried out on the different eyes. To get an indication of spontaneous
neural activity, a recording without stimulation was performed. This was followed by 10 Hz
stimulation of 1 ms duration and 1 V amplitude for 10 seconds. As the sampling frequency
was 30 kHz, this resulted in 100 stimulations and 300.000 data points.
The stimulus was
performed by first applying a -500 mV offset, such that the net amplitude became 500 mV
during stimulation to avoid frying the eyes. One final test was conducted with the nerve poison
TTX to prevent the nerves from generating action potentials.
This is to validate that the
spikes detected in the stimulated data are biological in origin. The 500 mV is derived from
a threshold voltage experiment; the minimum stimulation voltage where the device reliably
induced evoked potentials in the tissue above the spontaneous activity (Kusumanchi et al. 2025).
Tested stimulation intensities ranged from 100 mV to 1500 mV.

At the start of the modelling process, the goal is to remove the SA since the focus is on the
segments between them. Given that SAs have significantly higher magnitudes than the rest of
the signal and occur every 0.1 seconds, they can be easily identified using peak detection, at least
in the case of clean synthetic data. To isolate the relevant time segments, the middle 2400 data
points—corresponding to 80 milliseconds—are extracted between consecutive SAs, following the
approach of Kusumanchi et al. 2025. This ensures that no residual artefact contamination is
present when searching for CAPs. When we refer to ”removing the SA” in the next following
sections, this is what is meant.

Preprocessing
As seen in the previous chapter, the data contains several sources of noise. Unlike in (Lu, Lee,
and Chiu 2009) we have stationary time series, however, we hypothesise that models will perform
better if we implement some preprocessing. Just as in the mentioned study, we will use ICA to

43
remove noisy components. The preprocessing steps are outlined below.
First, the data is centred by applying a high-pass filter to remove frequencies below 1 Hz.
Secondly, to combat the high frequency noise a low-pass filter is applied to remove frequencies
above 5000 Hz, as we do not expect anything of interest to happen at these frequencies. A
Wiener filter with a window size of 5 is applied to smooth the signal and combat white noise.
After these steps, ICA will be applied to identify components in the data that behave like the
PLI. This is done by decomposing the 32 channels into 32 independent sources using ICA and
computing the autocorrelation function (ACF) for each source. As the stimulation artefacts
dominate the signals with their large magnitude, they are removed before the ACF is computed.
The signal is then reconstructed after removal of the source with the largest absolute mean ACF
for lag 10 ms, 20 ms and 30 ms, respectively. This procedure is repeated until the reconstructed
signal has an ACF (without the stimulation artefacts) of 65% of the original signal or after a
maximum of 10 iterations. This procedure is illustrated in Figure 5.6.

5.4.1
Support Vector Machines
Figure 5.14 shows the PSD for two channels of synthetic data after preprocessing. The low-
pass filtering is evident as frequencies above 5000 Hz have very low magnitudes. The welch
method, from scipy.signal, that estimates the PSD by dividing the signal into segments has
been applied. Here 256 points in each segment have been used. The SVR is then performed on
the frequency domain rather than the time domain. Moreover, as frequencies above 5000 Hz
have been removed, the regression is only performed for frequencies below 5000 Hz. This greatly
reduces memory usage as the number of data points is considerably decreased. As SVR can be
sensitive to outliers, the data is normalized to the range [0, 1].
SVR will be performed using a linear kernel for three main reasons. First, the linear kernel
requires no additional hyperparameters to tune, making it both efficient to train and test.
Second, regression is performed on a feature vector rather than the raw data, reducing the
need for complex kernel transformations. Finally, Gaussian kernels require careful tuning and
tend to overfit (Cheng et al. 2024; Han and Jiang 2014), whereas a linear kernel may generalise
better when extrapolating from synthetic to real data—provided that the assumption of a linear
relationship holds.
The training data for the SVR model is further used to ”train” the Mean predictor, which
comprises of computing the average number of CAPs per channel.

DISCUSSION
7.1
Reflections on Data
The data used in this project posed several challenges. Beyond being highly noisy, many chan-
nels contained frequent malfunctions. An additional difficulty in the modelling process was the
absence of an input signal, making it impossible to determine whether observed irregularities
stemmed from the recording electrodes, the input itself, or another unknown factor. Further-
more, detected spikes could not be definitively confirmed as biological in origin, which motivated
the use of synthetic data. However, synthetic data is not without its limitations. While it closely
resembled real data, as illustrated in Figure 6.1, this comparison was based on a channel, which
may not fully capture the variability present in the real dataset. The repetitive behaviour of
the PLI signal in the synthetic data, modelled from the TTX data using ICA, was less appar-
ent in the specific channel of stimulated data shown in Figure 4.2. This discrepancy could be
due to ICA extracting a specific representation of PLI from the TTX data, greater variability
between eyes than initially assumed, or slight differences in stimulation and recording electrode
behaviour across recordings.
The preprocessing algorithm effectively reduced noise levels in some channels but occasionally
also altered relative magnitudes, which is a notable drawback.
This is particularly relevant
given that Kusumanchi et al. 2025 focused on the area under each spike. Consequently, this
type of preprocessing may be best suited for counting activations rather than analysing their
amplitudes. To prevent the removal of valuable signal components, an upper limit was set on
the number of ICA components that could be discarded. Removing too many could allow other
non-relevant components to dominate. The preprocessing pipeline in this project specifically
targeted three types of noise: high-frequency noise, white noise, and PLI. However, as shown
in Figure 4.2, PLI was less pronounced in certain channels, which may explain why its removal
was less effective in those cases. Alternative preprocessing strategies might have yielded better
results, particularly for channels where PLI was less prominent.
The approach for binning the data between stimulations was inspired by Kusumanchi et al. 2025

identifies only their highest point. However, this method risks mistakenly removing activations,

and Wavelet models, as they relied strictly on the available data, whereas SVR could learn and
compensate for systematic biases.
7.2
Performance of Proposed Methods
First of all, it is worth noting that the nature of the synthetic dataset also influenced model
performance. The number of CAPs per channel typically fell within the range [40, 160], which
inherently constrained the Mean predictor’s error. In the most extreme case—where half of the
channels contain 40 CAPs and the other half contain 160—the Mean predictor would consistently
estimate 100 CAPs per channel, leading to a uniform absolute error of 60. This bounded error
explains the model’s relatively strong performance. In contrast, models like the Wavelet method,
which do not assume a fixed mean, can yield more varied predictions. This suggests that the
effectiveness of the Mean predictor was a consequence of the dataset’s structure rather than the
model’s suitability for the task.
A key limitation of the supervised methods, SVR and the Mean predictor, was their inability
to generalise beyond the training distribution. This limitation makes SVR less suitable when
real data differs significantly from the synthetic data used for training—an essential challenge
given that the true number of activations in real data is unknown.
The training data was
generated based on assumptions and design choices to provide SVR with the best possible
learning conditions. Specifically, spontaneous spiking was modelled with a baseline frequency of
50 Hz, varying between 40 and 60 Hz, which naturally imposed a lower bound on the number of
occurring CAPs. The number of evoked CAPs per channel was varied to the extent permitted by
the setup, effectively creating an upper limit. This variation was introduced to prevent SVR from
merely learning the mean and instead capturing underlying structures in the data. However,
if these assumptions were incorrect and the synthetic data failed to adequately represent real
conditions, the method will struggle to generalise to unseen distributions.
Another drawback of data-driven methods is their lack of explainability. In real data, where
conclusions are drawn at a broader scale rather than on an individual peak-to-peak basis, this
may be less critical. However, in synthetic data, where the true signal is known, it becomes
difficult to verify whether the learned representation aligns with expectations. In contrast, the
Wavelet and Threshold methods can provide direct comparability between detected spikes and
ground-truth synthetic CAPs. For real data, experts can manually assess detected spikes and
provide a second evaluation.
SVR was ultimately applied in the spectral domain rather than the time domain. Preliminary
experiments revealed that SVR struggled to generalise in the time domain, as it failed to recog-
nise similar CAPs unless they occurred at the same time. This finding aligns with Khorrami
and Moavenian 2010, which demonstrated that feature extraction using wavelets improved SVR
performance. It also highlights fundamental challenges in working with time series data. One
==PAGE:70==

61
major difficulty is that time series data contains strong temporal dependencies, meaning models
must account for correlations across time points rather than treating each observation as inde-
pendent (Tan et al. 2021). While these dependencies often occur between consecutive points,
electrical signals, as observed in this study, introduce correlations at specific frequencies, further
complicating the analysis. Additionally, high sampling rates lead to a large number of data
points, making both feature extraction and computational efficiency more challenging. These
challenges motivated transformation to the frequency domain that greatly reduced the number
of data points as well as removing the temporal dependencies.
In the Wavelet model, the penalty for false positives was set to λ = 0.99, following Nenadic and
Burdick 2005, who also used a high penalty to prevent overestimation. Similarly, Du, Kibbe,
and Lin 2006 demonstrated that peak detection in mass spectra using CWT could reliably
identify both small and large peaks while minimising false positives.
The results from this
project showed that the median error was consistently negative, indicating an underestimation
of spike counts, while the upper quartile of the error distribution was positive. Reducing the
penalty might shift the median error closer to zero but could also introduce more false positives,
making this parameter challenging to tune. Overestimation likely arose because noise patterns
resembled CAPs. Although the selected mother wavelet closely matched the CAP waveform, it
also demonstrated a high similarity to the noise, motivating the use of CPD to remove residual
noise before counting. However, this was not always sufficient, as seen in Figure 6.10, where
PLI residue spikes still were misclassified as CAPs by the Wavelet method.
Unlike the synthetic stimulated data, the Wavelet method failed to detect any activations in
the synthetic spontaneous data. As shown in Figures 6.11–6.12, the CAPs were not clearly
visible in the time series, even after preprocessing. However, the same applied to the activation
shown in Figure 6.8, which was nonetheless detectable in the scalogram at lower frequencies.
The preprocessing steps and model parameters were primarily optimized for stimulated data,
suggesting that the assumption of no additional tuning for spontaneous data may not hold.
Given that spontaneous data has a different order of magnitude, the chosen scales may not be
optimal. Additionally, since false negatives posed a greater challenge in this context, lowering
the penalty parameter λ might have improved detection.
The comparison between the Wavelet and Threshold methods implemented in this project and
the results reported by Kusumanchi et al. 2025 (Figure 6.7) yields interesting insights. The two
unsupervised methods in this study showed strong agreement with each other, yet their patterns
did not align with those reported by Kusumanchi et al. 2025. The only methodological difference
in the Threshold approach was the inclusion of preprocessing prior to spike counting. Possible
explanations could be: preprocessing may have revealed previously hidden spikes, making them
easier to count; it may have introduced artefacts that were misclassified as spikes; or it may
have had some other unintended effect.
To evaluate whether the spike count decreased when analysing spontaneous activity and after
applying TTX to the eyes, we can examine Table 6.3. No decrease was detected for the Mean
predictor on the TTX data. This is to be expected as it relies solely on the mean as its learnable
parameter and therefore lacks the ability to generalise across datasets. Instead, it simply uses the
training labels as a reference, making it ineffective for detecting variations in spike count across
different conditions. In contrast, the goal was that SVR learned an underlying representation

of the data, allowing it to adapt to different distributions more effectively. As the training data
for the SVR was identical whether it was tested on stimulated or TTX data, the decrease in the
number of spikes we observe for the TTX data might reflect that it has generalised and learned
a good underlying representation.
Both methods also reported a decrease in detected activations for spontaneous data. However,
it is difficult to determine whether this reflects a genuine reduction in activity or differences
in the training setup. The Mean predictor detected roughly half as many activations in the
spontaneous data as in the stimulated data, which aligns with the synthetic dataset’s design,
where the mean spike count per channel is 100 for stimulated data and 50 for spontaneous data.
In contrast, SVR detected 64% of that of the stimulated, meaning it identifies more activations
than the expected five per second. This could suggest that spontaneous activity exceeds the
assumed frequency or that SVR counted artefacts or other non-neural events.
Examining the non-data-driven methods, Threshold and Wavelet, we observed a decrease in de-
tected spikes. Since these methods do not incorporate learned biases, this result is encouraging.
However, the Wavelet model’s performance on spontaneous data should be interpreted with cau-
tion. As seen in Figure 6.5, the Wavelet model failed to detect spikes in synthetic spontaneous
data. Therefore, the absence of detected spikes in real spontaneous data likely reflects this limi-
tation rather than an actual absence of activity. The Threshold method, on the other hand, was
able to detect synthetic spontaneous activity, though with a tendency to underestimate. The
substantial decrease in detected spikes could be attributed to this underestimation, combined
with the method’s tendency to sometimes overestimate the number of spikes in stimulated data,
as suggested by Figure 6.4. Despite these factors, the observed reduction in spike count remains
substantial, indicating a genuine decrease in activity.
We also observed a decrease in detected activations for the TTX data with both methods,
though it was less pronounced. Since the experimental setup for the stimulated and TTX data
was identical, with the only difference being the presence of the neural agent, any reduction
in detected spikes was likely due to decreased neural activity. This suggests that the observed
decrease is genuine.
7.3
Future Considerations
The preprocessing performed remarkably well for some channels, as seen in Figure 6.3. If further
algorithm development ensures consistently stable results across all channels, it could eliminate
the need for complex CAP detection methods. In such cases, the Threshold method would be
sufficient, provided CAPs remain as clearly distinguishable as in the aforementioned figure. In
the construction of the synthetic data, a small offset was introduced in the locations of the SA.
Preprocessing might improve by first aligning these offsets. As discussed in Appendix C, this
adjustment helps further suppress PLI but at the cost of increased leakage between channels,
which may lead to a higher false positive rate.
If the current preprocessing approach is maintained, the characteristics of the channels will
continue to vary notably, making it challenging to design a single Wavelet method that performs
well across all cases. One possible approach would be to adapt the tuning parameters based

on the specific characteristics of each time series to improve performance. This also includes
considerations regarding the differences between stimulated and spontaneous data.
Gaining more insight into the data – such as arrival times after stimulation, arrival frequency,
and duration – would enable the construction of more accurate synthetic datasets. This, in turn,
could enhance supervised methods by allowing them to learn representations that better reflect
real data.

CONCLUSION
This study demonstrates that advanced signal processing and supervised machine learning meth-
ods, particularly Support Vector Regression (SVR), modestly outperform simpler methods in
reliably detecting compound action potentials (CAP) in electrical signals recorded from retinal
implants, although effective preprocessing remains critical.
This study aimed to assess the effectiveness of different signal processing and machine learning
methods for detecting CAP in electrical recordings from retinal implants. Specifically, we in-
vestigated whether linear unmixing methods, such as independent component analysis (ICA),
could improve CAP separation, how prior knowledge of CAP shapes could inform wavelet-based
detection, and how various detection approaches compared in terms of reliability and precision.
The first step in the investigation was data acquisition, which included both real recordings from
biocompatible 3D pillar electrodes and synthetically generated data. Three types of real data
were collected: stimulated, spontaneous, and TTX. Both stimulated and spontaneous datasets
were also synthetically generated.
A preprocessing pipeline was developed to address noise
and artefacts, incorporating low- and high-pass filtering and the removal of ICA components
displaying artifactual patterns.
Two baseline models were introduced: the Mean predictor,
serving as a sanity check, and the Threshold method, enabling comparison with Kusumanchi
et al. 2025. Additionally, a non-data-driven approach based on Wavelet transforms and CPD
was evaluated, alongside a supervised method using SVR.
Preprocessing played a key role in noise removal and artefact reduction, particularly in mitigating
power line interference (PLI). However, its effectiveness varied across recordings, with some
residual artefacts persisting. Synthetic data confirmed that preprocessing could reveal CAP-like
structures but occasionally distorted relative magnitudes, potentially impacting amplitude-based
analyses.
By employing prior knowledge of the structure of CAPs, we selected the cgau1 wavelet, as it
closely resembled the expected CAP shape. However, while this wavelet had a high correlation
with CAPs – especially in synthetic data – it also correlated strongly with noise, limiting its
ability to distinguish between true activations and artefactual patterns.

CHAPTER 8. CONCLUSION
65
The Mean predictor served as a useful baseline, consistently yielding median errors close to zero.
The Threshold method tended to underestimate spike counts in spontaneous data but performed
slightly better for stimulated recordings.
While its accuracy improved in experiments with
stronger true signals, it remained the method with the most varied and extreme predictions.
Similarly, the Wavelet model generally underestimated spike counts and struggled to detect
spikes in spontaneous data. Careful parameter tuning was required to prevent overestimation,
leading to median predictions below the true values with occasional large overestimates. Among
all methods, SVR performed the best, consistently achieving lower errors. For stimulated data,
the interquartile range of predictions remained within 25% of the true values. While SVR also
performed well on spontaneous data, it occasionally produced extreme predictions for this data
type.
Importantly, all methods, except the Mean predictor, detected a decrease in CAPs when tran-
sitioning from stimulated data to TTX-treated and spontaneous recordings. This aligns with
the expectation that neural activity should decrease in the absence of stimulation or under neu-
ral agents, providing indirect validation of the device’s ability to evoke CAPs. The observed
trends in terms of a decrease in spike rates were largely consistent with the previous findings
of Kusumanchi et al. 2025. Conversely, there was a discrepancy when replicating the threshold
voltage experiment, which can be attributed to the addition of the preprocessing.
In summary, while the more advanced methods, Wavelet and SVR, outperformed the Thresh-
old approach, the improvement was not substantial. The relatively strong performance of the
Threshold method may be partly attributed to the preprocessing step. Ultimately, developing
a reliable CAP detector for this dataset requires a combination of robust preprocessing and
carefully chosen detection models.

APPENDIXC
EFFECT OF ADDING OFFSET IN SA
LOCATIONS
Introducing a small offset in the locations of stimulation artefacts (SAs), allowing their positions
to vary by 20 data points (approximately 2/3 ms) in either direction, has an affect on the
preprocessing algorithm.
The top panel in Figure C.1 shows a filtered signal without an offset, while the bottom panel
includes an offset. In the bottom panel, a channel where the residual PLI is visible is deliber-
ately chosen to illustrate the difference. The characteristic repetitive pattern of PLI is far less
pronounced in the top panel than in the bottom. This effect is also evident when computing the
ACF before and after removing ICA components. In the top panel, the ACF at 50 Hz (excluding
SAs) was initially 97%, but after removing just one component, it dropped to 5%. In contrast,
the bottom panel initially had an ACF of 58%, and even after removing 10 components, it only
decreased to 40%.
The large difference in the initial ACF values is due to how the time series is binned when
removing SAs. When SAs occur at the exact same locations, and the same template is used
for all instances within a channel, the binning process remains temporally consistent. Since
600 data points (corresponding to 20 ms) are removed per segment—a multiple of 20 ms—the
