Abstract
In the transition towards a low┬şcarbon energy system, literature has found Hybrid Power
Plants to be a key potential player, given the benefits they can bring in combining renew┬ş
able energy resources with or without battery energy storage systems.
This project aims at solving the sizing problem of a hybrid power plant (HPP) with an
objective to maximize its profitability when participating in various energy markets, within
the framework of the Nordic electricity markets. The HPP sizing tool HyDesign, developed
by DTU Wind, is reviewed, extended and applied to fit this objective.
An Energy Management System is modelled to provide optimal operational strategies for
plant assets with the objective to maximize revenues. This operational strategies are
evaluated under a case study and the impact of relevant design parameters as well as
market price volatility on the plantÔÇÖs revenues is assessed through a sensitivity analysis.
The new extended model is integrated into the HyDesign sizing tool and used to determine
an optimal HPP configuration to maximize plant profitability. Results are again subject to
a sensitivity analysis on relevant design variables, component costs and market prices.
Results indicate a significant potential for increased plant profitability when participating
in balancing markets. In the context of sizing a HPP power plant, profitability in configu┬ş
rations with participation in these additional electricity markets displays more robustness
to variation in component sizing and market price volatility.

Introduction
1.1
Background
The transition towards a low┬şcarbon energy system is a pressing global issue. The Euro┬ş
pean Union (EU), has set ambitious targets for increasing the share of renewable energy
sources (RES) in its energy mix, as outlined in the Clean Energy Package and the Re┬ş
newable Energy Directive [1]. RES play a pivotal role in achieving decarbonization goals,
but the expected increase in grid penetration does not come without its challenges, as
they lead to a significant decrease in power system inertia. In such scenarios, electricity
power systems become more sensitive to frequency deviations, and therefore stability [2].
In face of this, hybrid power plants combining multiple RES with or without conventional
generation sources are emerging as promising solutions.
These systems aid not only in solving the variability in the supply side, but also in the
demand side. European power demand is expected to increase by 40% by 2050, making
Europe only but more dependent on electric power [3]. The often mismatch between
demand peak times and generation peak periods from RES can be partially or totally
covered with the help of battery energy storage systems, which can also contribute in
decreasing undesirable renewable energy curtailment and increasing plant revenue [4].
In line with the EUÔÇÖs roadmap towards a less carbon┬şintensive power energy system,
European electricity markets stand at the forefront of this transformation [5]. Leveraging
the opportunities provided by these markets is essential to increasing revenues for hybrid
power plants. By participating in different electricity markets, such as balancing markets
and spot market, and offering grid┬şservices and other ancillary services, hybrid power
plants can earn additional revenue, thereby increasing their profitability and improving
their viability.
This project explores the intricate task of sizing hybrid power plants to optimize revenue
generation within the framework of Nordic electricity market, with focus on these plantsÔÇÖ
Energy Management Systems (EMS). The findings and recommendations derived from
this study can assist stakeholders like project developers, policymakers and market par┬ş
ticipants in formulating robust strategies, optimizing investment decisions, and fostering
the development of sustainable and economically viable hybrid power projects that drive
Europe towards its clean energy future.

1.2
Problem formulation
Energy management systems (EMS) aim to develop production and storage operational
strategies for assets while maximizing the value of hybrid power plants (HPP) during oper┬ş
ation and minimizing the impact of forecast uncertainties. They optimize revenue streams
through energy bidding for different electricity markets based on power and energy market
forecasts [6].
HyDesign is an optimization tool developed by DTU for sizing hybrid power plants in which
an EMS is configured. This EMS model currently only contemplates the participation of
HPP in the spot market. Given the potential for increased revenue that additional market
participation could offer, its integration into the tool is the natural next step for its devel┬ş
opment.
In developing an EMS that integrates both spot and balancing market mechanisms, this
revenue potential could be evaluated, thus responding to the research questions proposed
below.
1.3
Research questions
The research questions proposed for the presented project are the following:
1. How to solve the sizing problem of a Hybrid Power Plant (HPP) to maximize the
HPPÔÇÖs profitability when participating in various electricity markets and design its
operational strategy.
2. How the operational strategy of a HPP obtained through the HyDesign sizing tool is
affected by changes in various relevant parameters, such as uncertainty in market
prices.
3. What is the current electricity market landscape in the Nordics, and what role does
HPP play in it.
1.4
Methodology and outline of the project
In order to answer said research questions, the methodology applied for the development
of this paper is the following:
ÔÇó In Chapter 2, a literature review is conducted on EMS, HPP and the different elec┬ş
tricity markets in the Nordics, in particular the spot market, balancing market and
ancillary services.
ÔÇó In Chapter 3, the optimization sizing tool used, HyDesign, is introduced.
ÔÇó In Chapter 4, the optimization problem of maximizing HPP revenues under the spot
market mechanism is extended to include balancing markets.
Results for both
single┬şmarket and multiple┬şmarket models are assessed under a case study, and
a sensitivity analysis is conducted on relevant variables and parameters.
ÔÇó In Chapter 5, the EMS model, including spot and balancing market mechanisms, is
then integrated into HyDesign sizing optimization tool.
The tool is run according to the site selection and all corresponding parameters of
the case study in order to obtain the HPPÔÇÖs optimal plant configuration. Results
obtained are subject to sensitivity analysis on relevant parameters.
Finally, results and relevant findings are presented. Potential areas for future research
are further discussed.

1.5
Assumptions and limitations
This Master Thesis presents some limitations, mainly due to the limited time available for
completion. In the project, the following assumptions have been made:
ÔÇó The project observes market structure and pricing mechanisms for the Nordic elec┬ş
tricity markets and does not necessarily apply to other locationsÔÇÖ market mecha┬ş
nisms.
ÔÇó The technologies used in the studied HPP include onshore wind power and battery
energy storage systems (BESS). Other technologies like solar power or even power┬ş
to┬şX could also be included in future studies but are dismissed for the present project.
ÔÇó The resulting EMS model from this project is a Mixed Integer Linear Programming
(MILP) model with the objective to maximize revenues for a Hybrid Power Plant with
onshore wind and a battery energy storage system (BESS).
The solution to this model, given wind power and market price forecasts, defines the
operational strategy of the BESS, and spot and balancing market bidding strategy
of the HPP.
For simplification, balancing markets and ancillary services are assumed to be rep┬ş
resented as an hour┬şahead market in which surplus of power in respects to the
agreed spot market volume can be sold to the grid in the form of up┬şregulation, and
deficit of power is penalized in the form of down┬şregulation.
When discussing results derived from the formulated model, all references to bal┬ş
ancing markets and their potential benefits are done in terms of this simplified market
design.
ÔÇó The extended EMS model formulated to participate in balancing markets considers a
market mechanism in which volumes are traded an hour before real┬ştime dispatch.
At this time, the EMS receives wind generation forecasts that are assumed to be
perfect and, therefore, the exact wind power available in real┬ştime. The clearing
market price forecast for this hour┬şahead market is also assumed to be perfect.
ÔÇó The Energy Management System model is specifically designed to serve sizing pur┬ş
poses and be integrated into the sizing optimization tool. A model designed for an
operational EMS would need to consider different or additional assumptions and is
out of this projectÔÇÖs scope.
ÔÇó Uncertainties are dealt with through sensitivity analysis of the obtained results for
both the EMS optimization model and the results from the HPP sizing optimization.
ÔÇó Additional assumptions taken throughout the completion of this project are described
in the dedicated chapters and sections in the report.

Literature Review
2.1
Hybrid Power Plants (HPP)
A Hybrid Power Plant (HPP) is a power generation system that utilizes at least one re┬ş
newable energy source and one conventional energy source or more than one renewable
energy source with or without conventional sources. It can operate as a standalone or
grid┬şconnected system [7]. While the focus of standalone solutions, or off┬şgrid HPPs, is
exclusively set on the satisfaction of local consumption needs, grid┬şconnected HPPs op┬ş
erate as grid┬şintegrated power plant units to serve the needs of the bulk power system
and energy system environment [8].
In the context of renewable energy sources, an HPP consists of multiple generation tech┬ş
nologies, such as wind and solar with or without storage technologies to generate elec┬ş
tricity and other energy vectors like hydrogen [9].
As it is widely known, one of the main features that characterize renewable energy sources
like wind and solar is their variable and intermittent nature, which brings significant com┬ş
plexity to their penetration in the grid at high shares. Studies, however, have shown a
negative correlation between wind and solar resources, subject to site┬şspecific charac┬ş
teristics and proportion of installed capacity [10]. This fact makes the two technologies
favourable to be combined, and therefore for a hybrid power set up to be an optimal so┬ş
lution to efficiently utilize RES, with potentially reduced curtailment [10].
In line with these findings, a study conducted by Petersen et al. in the case of a Vestas
Power Plant solution integrating wind, solar PV and energy storage found that, by merging
wind and PV power, their individual power fluctuations could be attenuated, and thereby,
the overall power plant output gradient could be reduced [8].
The same study goes on to suggest that by combining technologies with variable gener┬ş
ation patterns, the overall capacity factor of the HPP could be improved in comparison
to when such technologies are used separately, and could potentially improve the plantÔÇÖs
overall energy output. The paper states that, ÔÇŁin particular, in regions with high diurnal
and/or seasonal complementarity of wind and solar power, a high degree of capacity is
achievable, relative to the grid connection limitationsÔÇŁ [8]. The paper also suggests that,
by combining the two technologies in a hybrid power plant setting with battery energy
storage systems (BESS), battery use is significantly reduced, thus allowing to extend the
lifetime of said storage system [8]. In the context of profit maximization in hybrid power
plants, the suggested reduced power fluctuations, increased power output and extended
battery life that are implied in this study could significantly benefit the business case of
hybrid power plants.
Even when wind and solar resources are not significantly correlated, HPP setups can
become advantageous when dispatching power in the electricity markets, since the plant
owner can benefit from different correlation patterns of price with wind or solar generation
at particular points in time [11].
Co┬şlocation is also an advantageous design characteristic of hybrid power plants, if used.
It is found when all generating assets have individual Points of Connection (PoCs) but are
connected to a single substation that acts as the interconnection point between HPP and
external grid [8]. A single point of connection implies reduced infrastructure investment

costs and thus reduced overall investment costs for the project and the grid in particular
[11].
Overplanting, that is, installing more capacity than that of the point of connection, to a
certain extent, can also aid in maximizing the use of existing network infrastructure and

Overall, renewable┬şbased hybrid power plants can benefit society, power systems, de┬ş
velopers and plant owners. For the latter, hybridization can aid in capturing the future

2.2
Energy Management System (EMS)
As discussed in the previous section, combining one or more renewable energy sources
with or without storage systems can be key to overcoming RESÔÇÖ intermittency implications
in terms of grid stability and plant revenues, among others. The design of hybrid power
plants, however, can significantly increase the projectÔÇÖs overall cost, especially when in┬ş
cluding battery storage systems. To ensure this cost is kept at the lowest level, optimal
asset sizing and an efficient energy management system integration are essential [4].
Optimal sizing of the various components of a hybrid power plant aims to maximize the
value of this HPP under given constraints, in hand with the physical design of the plantÔÇÖs
layout, to reduce the cost of elements such as by┬şshared land use and investment in
network infrastructure. Optimal operation and control of the plant, in its turn, is ensured
through a Power Management System (PMS) and an Energy Management System (EMS)
[9].
Though often mentioned together, it is worth clarifying that EMS and PMS are quite dif┬ş
ferent in terms of control objectives: EMS is generally associated with energy economic
objectives, while PMS is instead associated with technical aims [13].
As stated in Jamal et al.ÔÇÖs review of both systems, EMS ÔÇŁmonitors, controls, optimizes
and regulates the load, transmission, distribution, and generation┬şrelated activitiesÔÇŁ. Its
primary role is to ensure an effective balance between demand and supply despite finan┬ş
cial uncertainties and limitations. These uncertainties may arise from fluctuations in RES
generation, market prices or load activities [13].
EMS models vary widely depending on the plantÔÇÖs requirements and decided control and
optimization strategies. They can take on trading functionalities when dispatching in elec┬ş
tricity markets, energy scheduling capabilities and real┬ştime data accounting in combi┬ş
nation with SCADA systems, as well as transmission security management to improve
system reliability, safety and performance. [13].
Ultimately, the purpose of an EMS is ÔÇŁto find the best compromise among the operation
objectives which optimizes the performance of the generation facilityÔÇŁ [14]. This facilityÔÇÖs
design characteristics, operational requirements and the desired optimization strategy will
define the most suitable management approach for each power plant case [14].
In the case of the HyDesign tool developed by DTU, the purpose of the Energy Manage┬ş
ment System modelled for hybrid power plants is to develop production and storage plans
for assets while maximizing the value of said HPP during operation [9], that is, maximizing
its revenue when participating in the various electricity markets.

2.3
Nordic electricity markets
2.3.1
Power markets
Conceptually, energy markets are a central marketplace for exchanging energy, seeking to
match energy supply and demand. It is mainly this balance between supply and demand
that determines the ultimate power price in the markets. Nonetheless, weather conditions
or availability of plants producing at full capacity, amongst other factors, can impact this
price [15].
Power markets, which have historically been a state┬şcontrolled commodity, have, in the
last 30 years, undergone a process of deregulation and introduced free competition. The
purpose of deregulation has been to establish a more efficient power market, which al┬ş
lows for the exchange of power between countries and provides increased security of
supply. By integrating power markets across larger regions, available power capacity can
be used more effectively than in smaller regions, resulting in improved productivity and
greater efficiency [15]. Several publications have dwelt on the characteristics, benefits
and drawbacks of open competition and deregulated energy markets, but said discus┬ş
sions are out of this projectÔÇÖs scope.
2.3.2
Electricity markets: Case of the Nordics
In the early 1990s, the Nordic countries deregulated their power markets and merged their
markets into a unified Nordic market [15]. This unified Nordic electricity market consists of
different marketplaces that serve as ÔÇŁtime windowsÔÇŁ for the physical trading of electricity.
These include the day┬şahead market, the intra┬şday market, and the balancing market.
Figure 2.1 illustrates the timeline of operation of the different electricity markets. Nord Pool
Spot is responsible for the day┬şahead and intra┬şday markets, while each Nordic countryÔÇÖs
TSO is responsible for balancing markets ┬ş Energinet in the case of Denmark [16].
Figure 2.1: Operation timeline of different Nordic electricity markets.
Spot market
In the Nordics, the main trading volume occurs on the day┬şahead market, also referred to
as the ÔÇŁspot marketÔÇŁ. It is designed as an auction┬şbased power market with zonal pricing,
meaning all nodes in each bidding zone adopt a uniform electricity price. Denmark, for
example, consists of two zones: DK1 and DK2.
The spot market is a pay┬şas┬şcleared bidding mechanism that matches demand and sup┬ş
ply bids. Its resulting ÔÇŁsystem priceÔÇŁ ┬ş the price for each hour of the next following 24┬şhour
period, is key for price formation in the following time windows, namely intra┬şday and bal┬ş

==PAGE:20==
ancing markets, as well as the financial markets for long┬şterm contracts [16].
Intra┬şday market
The intra┬şday market acts primarily as a correction market, still managed by the market
operator. It opens after the hourly day┬şahead prices are published and closes 1 hour be┬ş
fore the operational time. The market serves as a continuous trading mechanism through
pay┬şas┬şbid, offering the chance to adjust day┬şahead schedules in response to updated
forecasts or asset failures. It incentivizes generators and consumers to reduce their im┬ş
balances and utilize flexibility [17]. Unlike the spot market, no clearing mechanism is
involved, and electricity is traded through bilateral financial contracts [18].
Once day┬şahead and intra┬şday markets are settled, the market operatorÔÇÖs job ends, and
the transmission system operator (TSO) takes responsibility for balancing markets and
ancillary services.
Balancing markets and ancillary services
When entering the hour before real┬ştime dispatch, regulation power comes into the picture
if either the load or the generation units deviate from the volume cleared in the spot market.
Since regulating power needs often appear suddenly and very close to physical delivery
time, transmission system operators (TSOs) must have enough available capacity at any
time [19].
In the Danish balancing electricity markets, when the system operator, Energinet, antic┬ş
ipates a power shortage, it engages in up┬şregulation by either incentivizing power pro┬ş
ducers to increase their electricity generation or encouraging consumers to reduce their
electricity consumption. This is achieved by offering financial compensation to producers
and consumers. Conversely, when a surplus of power is anticipated, the system operator
employs down┬şregulation by attempting to sell electricity at lower prices to power produc┬ş
ers, encouraging them to reduce their generation. In this scenario, Energinet facilitates
adjusting the market conditions to maintain the balance between supply and demand [20].
From the supplying side, especially for power plants with energy storage systems, these
imbalances could become new sources of revenue. When real┬ştime generation does not
match forecasts, excess power could be sold to the system operator at up┬şregulation
prices, expected to be higher than the cleared spot market prices. However, when there
is a power deficit, the non┬şaccomplished power volume is penalized at a down┬şregulation
price, accounting for the system operator having to find an alternative source to supply
the planned power that was undelivered [21].
Competition between suppliers of regulating power capacities ensures competitive up┬ş
regulation and down┬şregulation prices [19].
Balancing markets, therefore, ensure power supply┬şdemand balance and safe operation
of the system within the hour of operation by trading in automatic and manual reserves
used by the Nordic TSOs. [16].
From the SO point of view, ancillary services are ÔÇŁall grid support services required by
the transmission or distribution system operator to maintain the integrity and stability of
the transmission or distribution system as well as the power qualityÔÇŁ [22]. These can
be divided into three main categories: Frequency┬şsupporting, voltage┬şsupporting, and
black┬şstart services. In the context of electricity markets and balancing services, only the
frequency┬şsupporting services are looked at under the scope of this paper.
In DK1, the services delivered are Frequency Containment Reserve (FCR), Automatic

[23].
Manual reserves (mFRR) have a response time of up to 15 minutes before delivery time,
whereas automatic reserves (aFRR) have up to 5 minutes. FCR, in turn, is the fastest┬ş
response frequency supporting ancillary service, with up to 30 seconds of response time.
It is also known as ÔÇŁprimary reserveÔÇŁ or ÔÇŁregulating reserveÔÇŁ and is the closest balancing

Pricing of balancing power markets
Since understanding balancing markets is essential to this projectÔÇÖs completion, the mar┬ş
ketÔÇÖs pricing mechanism is worth mentioning.
As previously mentioned, the balancing power market plays a crucial role in maintaining
the equilibrium between electricity supply and demand in the Nordic electricity markets.
When there is a need for balancing power, bids in the balancing power market (BM) are
activated sequentially until the desired balance is achieved. The clearing price in the BM
is determined based on the last bid that was activated for a specific hour.
If additional power generation, known as up┬şregulation, is required, the clearing price is
set at the value of the highest activated bid. Conversely, for down┬şregulation, when a
reduction in power generation is required, the price is equal to the lowest activated bid
[21].
In that regard, market prices provide signals of the grid balancing requirements: typically,
prices for up┬şregulation in the balancing power market are equal to or higher than spot
market prices in the respective price area, and down┬şregulation prices in the BM are equal
to or lower than the spot price. In cases where no regulation is needed, the balancing
power price remains equal to the spot price [21].

The extended version of the Energy Management System (EMS) model aims at integrat┬ş

The assumptions considered in the initial spot market model continue to apply to the new
model, and the following are now considered as well:
1. The model is written so that it provides a day┬şahead and balancing market strategy
for the HPP power production and battery schedule:
For each hour, the power output of the HPP to be bid to the spot market is calculated
from the day┬şahead wind forecasts and the battery capacity available for charging
and discharging.
It is assumed that when the wind forecasts and up┬şand┬şdown┬şregulation prices are
updated, the model will define how much it can bid to the balancing markets accord┬ş
ing to the hour┬şahead wind forecasts and remaining battery capacity available.
The value of participating in these markets sequentially is maximized simultaneously
in the same objective function, accounting for spot and balancing market clearing.
2. As described under the Assumptions and Limitations section 1.5, the wind forecast
for the hour ahead is assumed to be perfect. That is to say that the wind power
forecasted in the hour ahead is assumed to be the actual wind production in real┬ş
time for each hour.
3. Similarly, price forecasts are also assumed to be perfect: price forecasts used for
spot market revenues are equal to the spot market clearing prices for each hour,
and up┬şand┬şdown┬şregulation prices are assumed to be known in the hour ahead.
These assumptions are strong for an operational accurate EMS model. However,
when designed for sizing purposes, they allow exploring the full potential of partici┬ş
pating in multiple electricity markets.
4. The model is designed to return a strategy to maximize revenues from both spot
and balancing markets while avoiding non┬şdelivery penalties. Therefore, the model
aims to define bids for the spot market that will always be fulfilled when hour┬şahead
forecasts are updated.
5. When regulation prices are equal to spot market prices, meaning that no regulation
in the grid is needed, participation in the spot market will be prioritized. That is to
say that, when up┬şregulation prices are the same as the spot market clearing price,
under the assumption that these prices are perfect forecasts, the available power
will be bid in the spot market rather than offered as up┬şregulation.
6. Down┬şregulation is often assumed as a penalty for being unable to deliver the planned
power. In this model, it is defined as such in that it is indicated as a negative revenue
in the objective function. Because the model is designed to maximize the objective
value, down┬şregulation is expected to be avoided. However, cases in which down┬ş

also contemplated and are discussed in the sensitivity analysis section.

Model (4.2) shows the optimization model used for this EMS case.
Given the updated wind generation and price forecasts, the objective function aims to
maximize the revenue from the HPPÔÇÖs power bid to the spot market at the cleared price
and the revenues from regulation. As previously described, down┬şregulation is modelled
as a negative revenue, although the possibility of down┬şregulation prices being negative
is contemplated.
Constraint (1) represents the HPPÔÇÖs output power to be bid in the spot market, given the
wind power generation day┬şahead forecasts, the power used from discharging the battery
or deducted from charging it, and the wind power curtailment, if any. The wind production
variable P w
DA(t) represents the wind generation forecast for the DA.
When the HA forecast, P w
HA(t), comes in, the real┬ştime power to be finally delivered to the
spot market in real┬ştime is determined. Constraint (2) describes this information update,
indicating the updated hour┬şahead wind generation forecast (assumed perfect) and the
updated battery charging and discharging schedule. This constraint also accounts for the
power sent to up┬şregulation and the curtailment and down┬şregulation volumes, if any.
Constraints (4) and (5) limit the power that the HPP can deliver to the value of grid capacity
at the connection point, and constraint (6) makes sure that the HPP, after updating all
forecasts and potentially participating in the balancing markets, still delivers what was
agreed to be dispatched in the spot market. As described before, the realization of SM
bids has been prioritized in the definition of this model.
Constraints (7) to (17) describe the battery behaviour:
Constraint (7), just like in the spot market┬şonly model, defines the energy state of charge
of the battery at a given time as the state of charge in the previous hour with the addition
or deduction of battery power from charging or discharging the battery throughout that
particular hour. This constraint indicates what the battery would be scheduled to do with
the day┬şahead forecast information and is not the final battery strategy.
Constraints (8) and (9) limit the batteryÔÇÖs SOC according to battery characteristics, and
constraints (13) and (14) limit the planned charging and discharging power to the maxi┬ş
mum battery power.
In constraints (10), (11), (12), (15) and (16), the exact formulation is being used to define

Constraint (17) then describes the binary variable used in constraints (13) to (16), defining

When considered interesting to explore, this variations have also been conducted in the
HyDesign configuration with only spot market participation, allowing for a better under┬ş
standing of the implications of having extended the EMS model in the tool.

Conclusion and future works
6.1
Conclusions
This master thesis has successfully addressed the proposed research questions by pro┬ş
viding insights into solving the sizing problem of a Hybrid Power Plant (HPP) to maximize
profitability through participation in various electricity markets and the design of its oper┬ş
ational strategy. DTUÔÇÖs HyDesign sizing optimization tool has been reviewed, extended,
and employed to address the studyÔÇÖs research objectives.
The first research question focused on solving the sizing problem of the HPP and for┬ş
mulating an operational strategy. The developed Energy Management System (EMS)
model has demonstrated its efficacy in maximizing HPPÔÇÖs revenue by incorporating par┬ş
ticipation in multiple electricity markets. The case study conducted on an onshore wind
and battery energy storage HPP in West Denmark has showcased a significant revenue
increase of 66% when including balancing market participation, validating the hypothesis
of this marketÔÇÖs potential to provide additional revenue.
The next step to further evaluate plant profitability has been to successfully integrate the
EMS model with multiple┬şmarket strategy into the HyDesign tool. Under the location and
market framework of the case study explored, the extended sizing optimization has been
run. The derived optimal plant configuration has demonstrated a substantial improvement
in profitability compared to when sizing with a spot market┬şonly EMS approach.
The second research question explored the impact of various parameters on the opera┬ş
tional strategy derived from the extended EMS model and the sizing solution from inte┬ş
grating it into HyDesign.
The sensitivity analysis conducted on the EMS resulting operational strategies for the
case study has revealed that changes in wind installed capacity have the most substan┬ş
tial effect on plant revenues, highlighting the importance of accurately considering this
parameter in the sizing optimization process. Moreover, the analysis has showcased the
EMS optimization strategyÔÇÖs ability to handle changes in other design parameters and
pricing scenarios.
As mentioned, the optimal plant configuration derived from integrating the new EMS model
in HyDesign has demonstrated a substantial improvement in profitability compared to the
spot market┬şonly approach. A comprehensive sensitivity analysis of plant design vari┬ş
ables and market prices has validated the optimal design choices. The analysis has high┬ş
lighted how, by participating in additional markets, the profitability of HPPs is more robust
to changes in design variables and when facing market price volatility. Moreover, ana┬ş
lyzing component costs has shed light on the future revenue potential that technological
advancements in the hybrid power plant sector could bring.
The third research question aimed at understanding the current electricity market land┬ş
scape in the Nordics and the role that Hybrid Power Plants play in it. The project pro┬ş
vides insights into the market framework in the Nordic and demonstrates the potential for
increased profitability by participating in its balancing markets. By integrating these mar┬ş
kets into the HyDesign tool, the project has revealed the significant financial benefits and
improved revenue streams for designing HPPs to operate in the region.

In summary, the findings in this project highlight the proposed EMS modelÔÇÖs effective┬ş
ness and when integrated into the HyDesign tool, the significant potential for increased
profitability in hybrid power plants. This research provides a valuable contribution to the
field by offering insights and guidance for designing and operating hybrid power plants in
dynamic electricity markets.
6.2
Future works
This project presents an assessment of profitability of hybrid power plants when partic┬ş
ipating in balancing markets from a sizing perspective, while considering numerous of
assumptions.
As described in section 1.5, the project presents a number of limitation, mainly due to
the time available for its completion. Therefore, several lines of future research fall as
possible continuations of this project. Among them:
ÔÇó The methodology used in this research could be expanded to include a span of other
renewable technologies like solar PV and Power┬şto┬şX.
ÔÇó Modelling of electricity markets closer to real┬ştime operation would be provide a
more precise evaluation of profitability potential. Integration of ancillary services
lies among the possible options.
ÔÇó More precise wind generation and market price forecasting could be considered,
allowing for more accurate results.
ÔÇó Investigation of the current regulatory framework of hybrid power plants and their
participation in balancing markets and ancillary services would aid in further as┬ş
sessing the viability of using HyDesign tool to size new power plants.
