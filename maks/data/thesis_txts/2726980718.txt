Abstract
Nowadays, wind turbines’ control mostly relies on feedback methods, where the controller
reacts to the state of the turbine and acts upon it. It implies that the turbine needs to
be out of equilibrium for the controller to change. That is why feed-forward techniques
are being explored, where the controller would now react to information about the flow
happening ahead of the turbine to anticipate the changes. To explore the possibility of
implementing such methods, this project aims at reconstructing the full incoming inflow
using simulated measurements from a hub-mounted LIDAR.
The estimation is achieved using physics-informed machine learning (PIML), which relies
on both the simulated measurements and partial differential equations (PDEs) to recon-
struct the velocities.
Two sets of data are used for the wind measurements.
First, a
Mann-generated flow is tested, relying on regularly spaced points across the domain to
train the network on. Then, a large eddy simulation (LES)-generated turbulence field is
used, with two separate sampling schemes for the training. Furthermore, simulations of
the NREL 5MW [1] turbine are performed in the aeroelastic tool HAWC2 [2] to evaluate
the effect of the errors in estimation.
Although the method exhibits promising results in estimating larger turbulent structures,
the study reveals limitations in capturing higher-frequency variations. Furthermore, the
investigation does not demonstrate the effectiveness of physics in the training, mainly due
to the challenge of determining the appropriate governing equations for the training set.
Finally, the loads’ analysis emphasizes the significance of accurately modeling larger-scale
structures to predict the turbine loads with a reasonable error, while the accuracy of the
smaller-scale variations has a lower impact.

1.
Introduction
With 255 GW of installed wind capacity across Europe at the end of 2022 and sustained
growth in installation campaigns, finding ways to mitigate costs and improve the perfor-
mance of current wind turbines is a focus point in the wind energy sector. The control
strategy chosen for these structures dictates their behavior depending on wind conditions
by acting on a set of parameters, such as the pitch angle of the blades. A given control
scheme can improve the performance and mitigate loads for the same turbine. Choosing
an appropriate strategy is key to the optimal operation of a wind turbine through its entire
lifetime.
Most current methods rely on feedback, where the turbine reacts to the disturbance and
acts upon it. However, newer feed-forward strategies relying on measurements of the flow
ahead of the turbine have been developed and show great potential in reducing the loads,
thus extending the turbine’s lifetime [3], [4]. The input to these controllers is generally the
incoming wind speed, which needs to be accurately measured. Traditional measurement
tools, such as anemometers mounted on masts, are not fit for such a task: they provide
a single measurement in space that does not represent the flow across the entire rotor
plane. Furthermore, such an approximation would worsen as the turbine grows, hindering
the controller’s performance. Meteorological masts are also increasingly expensive as they
grow taller [5]. Thus, light detection and ranging (LIDAR) technology has been emerging
as an appropriate measurement tool for such strategies, as it scans the inflow ahead of
the wind turbine and provides several simultaneous measurements from different points
in space.
However, relying solely on LIDAR measurements does not provide high-resolution informa-
tion on the turbulence in the entire plane, which could be used to improve the turbine’s
control. This thesis thus aims at estimating the full turbulent inflow ahead of a simu-
lated turbine based on sparse measurements. The turbulence estimation is done through
a physics-informed neural network, a machine-learning method that relies both on data
points (provided by the LIDAR) and knowledge of the physical laws governing the flow
(the Navier-Stokes equations).
After showing how the literature review highlighted three main research questions for the
scope of this work, a general background on neural networks and PINN is given to provide
the reader with the terminology that is later used. Then, the first part of this project,
dedicated to estimating Mann-generated turbulence boxes, is explained. The findings of
this method led to a change in the input data and a restructuring of the methodology.
The results of this new method are then discussed; the reconstructed flows are then used
in an aeroelastic simulation using HAWC2 to compare the influence of estimation errors
on the structure of an NREL 5MW turbine. Then, conclusions are drawn from the results,
and future work is suggested.

2.
State of the Art
This literature review focuses on inflow estimation methods using LIDAR measurements.
First, the current state of the LIDAR technology is studied. Then, recent inflow estimation
methods are compared. Finally, an estimation based on physics-informed machine learning
is presented.
2.1
Wind measurement - LIDAR technology
2.1.1
LIDAR measurements
LIDAR systems are employed to measure the wind speed remotely in the direction of
their beams. The measurement relies on the signal received from back-scattered light: the
fraction of the beam reflected from the moving particles in the air will have a change in
frequency from the Doppler effect. The particle’s speed can be derived by measuring the
frequency difference in the resulting signal. Assuming the particles in the air are carried
at the same speed as the wind, the wind speed in the line of sight is thus acquired [6].
Two main technologies exist to compute the speed at a specific point in space: continuous
wave (CW) systems and pulsed LIDAR. CW LIDARs will focus the beam through a lens
to receive data at a certain distance, while pulsed devices emit laser pulses and rely on
the time of flight to separate the different distances of measurements. In this project, a
pulsed LIDAR is considered, a technology that can measure several distances in a quasi-
instantaneous way, but consequently with a slower sampling frequency from one beam to
the next [7].
For inflow estimation, the devices can be ground-based, as seen in [8], where the measure-
ments were used to reconstruct the wind field. However,to determine indicators at hub
height, where the longitudinal component is the most critical, it is more practical to mount
the LIDAR on the turbine itself. Two positions can be examined: on the nacelle behind
the blades, pointing forward (nacelle-mounted), or rotating with the hub in front of the
blades (hub mounted). The main difference between both will be the potential blockage
with the blades passing through the line of sight.
[9] reconstructs a wind field using a simulated LIDAR. The authors model the blockage
of the blades by filtering out some of the results to mask them. They estimate that the
availability would drop as low as 60% for the lower beams. [10] presents an algorithm to
improve data reliability and, among other improvements, detect when the LIDAR hits the
blades to exclude these points. With this method, it is claimed that for the V52 Vestas
turbine, where a Spinner LIDAR was mounted, data availability from field measurements
ranged from 57% to 89%, depending on the beam’s position. It was found that the lower
part of the pattern, as expected with increased blade thickness, experienced on average
lower availability. Mounting the LIDAR on the hub is expected to raise the availability,
as one clear and constant source of data falsification will be removed. However, it will be
harder to define regularly spaced patterns, as the beams for the project are expected to
be fixed at a given angle to the hub and spinning at this fixed angle.

2.1.2
Challenges and shortcomings
Though LIDARs offer clear new use cases compared to a cup anemometer, some short-
comings, mainly coming from the spatial averaging and line of sight (LoS) are important
to underline to best understand the challenges to overcome when using LIDAR data.
A LIDAR will only measure the LoS wind velocity, meaning the velocity perceived along
the beam direction. This is often referred to in the literature as the Cyclops Dilemma,
which can be circumvented using several assumptions [11]:
• the wind is purely one-directional, and this direction is known: the wind can be
calculated based on the LoS wind and the beam angle.
• the wind has no vertical component, and the flow is considered homogeneous at a
given height. The wind can then be deduced from two separate measurements of the
same height.
Figure 2.1: The Cyclops Dilemma: how to derive the actual wind speed using only LoS
wind speeds, from [11]
In Figure 2.1, the dash-dotted lines represent the wind values derived from the assumption
that v = w = 0, the vertical and cross-horizontal components of the wind. The dashed
lines represent the wind value derived from the assumption of a homogeneous flow and no
vertical component, which is thus obtained by using both LoS wind speeds to derive u and
v. It can be assumed that adding a third beam at a different angle could allow removing
the assumption w = 0, if the homogeneous simplification is kept.
Furthermore, as the pulse used to measure the wind velocity cannot be infinitely small,
the resulting wind speed will be an average of the LoS velocity within a cylinder the size
of the pulse. This will result in the loss of higher frequency content due to the spatial
averaging.
However, as can be seen in Figure 2.1, the configuration of the beams has a major role in
improving the results’ reliability, as a singular beam with a fixed angle would likely result
in partial information on the wind only.

2.2
LIDAR-based inflow estimation methods
A substantial effort is currently put forward to use LIDAR measurements in the wind
energy sector, with goals varying from extracting wind characteristics to recreating the
entire inflow.
2.2.1
Partial inflow reconstruction
A first approach to the use of sparse LIDAR measurements lies in determining spatially-
averaged values, or characteristics from the flow that used to be estimated on a singu-
lar measurement from a cup anemometer, such as the free stream velocity or the rotor-
averaged wind speed (RAWS).
An example of this approach can be found in [9], where the RAWS and the rotor-averaged
wind direction (RAWD) are determined from simulated LIDAR data. The fictitious wind
field is generated based on the Kaimal spectrum with added vertical sheer following a
power law.
To simulate the actual measurements, the authors added Gaussian white
noise, and a Gaussian weighting function was used to account for the spatial averaging of
the device. The reconstruction of the wind flow is treated as an optimization problem,
where a discrete-time Kalman filter is implemented, and several simplification assumptions

assumptions lead to having full knowledge of the global correlation matrix of the wind
components at specific locations. This matrix is then used in the Kalman filter to estimate
the flow. However, only averaged quantities are displayed in the article, so it is unknown if
such a method would apply to the full 3D reconstruction. The method’s performance was
evaluated by calculating the root mean squared error (RMSE) of the selected indicators,
which varied from 0.405m s−1 to 0.818m s−1 for the RAWS.
In [14], the free-stream velocity is the target of the estimation, using experimental LIDAR
measurements, despite the measurements being taken in the induction zone. The authors
use the Levenberg-Macquardt algorithm to minimize the error between the measurement
and the wind model from which they deduce the free stream velocity. This wind model
assumes horizontal homogeneity, a known vertical shear profile and a homogeneous wind
direction. Furthermore, a one-dimensional induction model is added to account for the
effect of the rotor on the stream. This method revealed that the modeled wind speed was
within 1-1.5% of the mast-measured results.

Alternatively, full reconstruction of the wind field without focusing on perfect replication
has been explored in [12]. The emphasis is not on accurately simulating a flow field here

The method used to produce the turbulent field relies on constrained Gaussian turbulence,
where only the longitudinal component of the wind u is constrained for simplicity. Mann
turbulence boxes are created, and their constrained versions are derived from it by select-

2.3
Physics-informed machine learning
As the previous section showed, achieving full flow reconstruction based only on the sparse
data LIDAR measurements provide is a complex task that results in heavy assumptions
(flow homogeneity, for example) or settles to compute aggregated variables.
Machine-
learning-based methods could potentially bridge the gap, as they are known to be more
flexible with noisy data with a smaller computational cost in some instances [16]. However,
such methods generally require a lot of data to train the neural network before outputting
results using the trained network. Such data sets can be prohibitively expensive to get to
apply this method. However, physics-informed machine learning (PIML) methods offer the
alternative to both rely on data and known equations that define the state to estimate.
Thus, less data would be needed, as the solutions are already more constrained using
partial differential equations (PDEs).
2.3.1
Method and motivation
For data-driven neural networks, training means adjusting the parameters of the network
for every data point fed to the network during the learning phase, so that the input
matches the desired output, minimizing a defined loss function [17].

2.3.2
Application to inflow estimation
The estimation of an incoming flow has been carried out using PINN in two consecutive
papers by the same main author.
In [20], the method is first tested using only two-

scope is then extended in [21], where the estimated flow is now three-dimensional, and
the effective viscosity from the equations is no longer assumed but becomes an estimated
parameter.
The general architecture of the chosen neural network is shown in Figure 2.4. The structure
allows for the network to train on two types of losses by updating the shared set of training
variables: the loss from the data and the loss generated from the PDEs.

The scope of the work presented in [21] relies on simulated LIDAR data consisting of 5

beams are spread to form a square pattern, with one central beam. Thus a plane parallel
to the rotor plane would be similar to Figure 2.2a. It is mentioned that all beams also
rotate at a speed of 15 °/s to cover more space of the domain. Four cases are considered
with wind speeds of 8, 13, 18, and 23 m s−1 and a turbulence intensity of 6%.
Although the results are promising, with the RMSE for all three wind components being
consistently below 13% of the mean wind speed, only a single LIDAR configuration is
explored. Furthermore, this configuration is idealistic: most commercial LIDARs have a
sampling frequency of 1 Hz but cannot sample from multiple beams simultaneously. The
sampling frequency of this configuration would then drop to 0.2 Hz, and the amount of
data would be divided by five if a comparable time frame is chosen. Another drawback
of the method is how the trained network is tailored to a wind speed: the cases studied
involve separate training, which challenges the possibility of making it a real-time solution
for inflow estimation. Additionally, no explanation or hypothesis is given for the increasing
error away from the rotor center, which could become challenging for bigger scopes: their
rotor diameter was only about 60 m, while the NREL 5MW considered for this thesis
has a diameter of 126 m.
Finally, no comparison is provided to a purely data-driven
methodology, that would justify the use of the Navier Stokes equations in this case.
2.4
Summary
Based on the literature review, using LIDAR measurements for inflow estimation provides
data ahead of the wind turbine in many different locations, which a more traditional mast
cannot do. However, two main issues must be addressed when using these measurements:
only having the LoS velocity and the spatial averaging of the LIDARs.
The current shortcomings of LIDARs could explain the sparse existing methodology to
reconstruct the full turbulence field using such measurements: the papers studied here
that did not rely on a machine-learning method either had strong assumptions and/or
were not focused on solving the full turbulent field. The method developed in [20] and [21],
where a PINN is used to resolve the full flow, is the most promising result concerning this
objective, as all three turbulence components were estimated, relying only on simulated
LIDAR measurements.
However, as mentioned previously, the method was used on a smaller rotor, and predictions
were gradually worse away from the center: adapting the methodology on a larger scale
will be a focus point in this thesis. Furthermore, no comparison is provided with a purely
data-driven case; it is not certain then that including the Navier-Stokes equation had a
significant positive impact. A second focus point in this thesis will then be to evaluate the
performance of this method compared to a purely data-driven one. Finally, no assessment
method is provided in these articles to estimate the impact of the estimation error on the
structure. This thesis will use an aeroelastic simulation with the reconstructed flow to
quantify the impact the loss in accuracy can have on the wind turbine.

3.1.2
Training and predicting
The learning process for this network would then be to update all the weights and biases
of the network to minimize a cost function based on some known data. This cost function
could be the RMSE of the network output compared to the desired value. Assuming a
training dataset of Nd elements, where the i-th element of the dataset would have input
values Xi = [x1,i, x2,i] and the corresponding output values Yi = [y1,i, y2,i], then the
training process would have the following steps:
Defining the training hyperparameters of the network
• Number of epochs E, corresponding to the number of sweeps through the entire
dataset the training process will proceed with.
• Batch size Nb: in a given epoch, the dataset is divided into batches for computational
cost purposes. Nb then defines the number of batches B per epoch, with B = Nd/Nb.
If the batch size is not a divisor of Nd, some batches may have a different size.
• Loss function L: the training variables will be updated based on the loss calculated
for each batch, as the algorithm will try to minimize it. A typical loss chosen can
be the RMSE.
• Learning rate lr: defines how big of a change with the training variables is desired
for each iteration. The value needs to be tuned to compromise between speed and
stability while also trying to avoid falling into local minima.
Training the dataset
An iteration corresponds to the algorithm calculating the generated values for each sample
of a given batch, calculating the corresponding loss, and updating the training variables
by using the gradient of the loss with respect to the parameters of the network (weights
and biases). Equation (3.2), adapted from [22], reflects how all the parameters of the
network Θi at the i-th step of training are updated, based on the values X and Y from
the selected batch of data B, and the gradient of the loss with regards to the parameters:
Θi+1 = Θi −lr · ∇ΘL (Θi; Xk∈B; Yk∈B)
(3.2)
This gradient descent method is called mini-batch gradient descent. However, many more
methods exist, among which the Adam optimizer [23], which has proven to be robust to
changes in parametrization and delivers adequate results. This work will further refer to
the gradient computation as loss back-propagation.

per epoch, so fewer changes to the training variables, but changes that optimize the loss
over more points. However, this setup has not been tested, as the training time revealed
to be very sensitive to the size of the batches, and a sensitivity analysis on it would have
been time-consuming.

4.2.3
Discussion on the first run
The main takeaway from this first run is that although the estimated flow tracks the
original data’s main variations, no true learning from the physics seems to have taken
place, which could explain the better performance of the interpolation method. This can
stem from several issues that need to be addressed:
• The weighing of the physics loss needs to be reevaluated to make better use of the
knowledge of the PDEs provided to the network.
• The PDEs themselves could be inappropriate to the model used: the Navier-Stokes
equations assume a 4-dimensional flow (in space and time). The equations were here
adapted to fit our 2D plane varying in time, but the data from the Mann box itself
may not fit this adaptation.
Furthermore, some concerns were raised regarding the eﬀiciency of the current architecture.
As training this specific network was rather long (around 40 minutes per epoch), it was
suggested to reduce its depth and breadth. The following sections thus try to address
both concerns by first performing a sensitivity analysis on the network’s architecture and
then addressing the learning of the PDEs.

Two main aspects are evaluated to improve the learning of the network with the PDEs:

4.4.1
Changing the collocation points
In the first run, the collocation points were randomly selected at each step across the full
domain. This could lead to a sub-optimal solving of the PDEs, as there are no boundaries

4.5
Summary
The results from this first run show that the true added value of physics-informed neural
networks could not be established. Some of the issues originate from the network setup
and are listed below.
First, the three turbulence components u, v, and w were not scaled in the same manner
before the training: ¯u was treated as the longitudinal wind speed (including U0, so not
zero-centered) while ¯v and ¯w had a zero-mean. This choice can impact the optimization
of the result, as not all inputs to the network vary across similar ranges, which is regarded
as more optimal for the training. As this issue was not the only obstacle to using Mann-
generated data, there was no attempt made to fix it for this case. However, the turbulence
components are all scaled identically in the next chapter, with LES data.
Furthermore, the PDEs implemented do not fit the data used: the momentum equations
used for the residual originally rely on 4-dimensional data, and the assumption of frozen
turbulence complicates their use. The conservation of mass could have been added as it
does not rely on time. However, [28] suggests in Section 2.3 that the conservation of mass
is not assured in a Mann-generated turbulence field. Then, the data used for the training
does not follow the equations from the model: the network thus faces the impossible task
of trying to match ill-fitted equations to the data. A solution could have been to derive the
full Navier-Stokes equations to adapt it to our use case of a 2-dimensional plane with u, v,
and w varying in time. However, this solution would tailor the methodology to the data
input and thus not be applicable in a real-life scenario with actual LIDAR measurements.
To still establish the relevance of using PINN to estimate an inflow, it was then decided
to use large eddy simulation-generated data. This method relies directly on the numerical
solving of the Navier-Stokes equations: the conservation of mass is insured.

Grid
The same evenly-spaced 8 × 8 grid is reused from the previous work. However, as the
new data has a finer resolution and is now a 55 × 55 × 28 000 box, this training dataset
accounts for 2.11% of the total turbulence field instead of the previous 6.25% with the

if the relative performance is improved.
LIDAR-like scheme
The LIDAR-like sampling scheme is inspired by the simulated LIDAR used in [21]. It
consists of a central beam that points horizontally ahead of the turbine and four evenly
spaced beams around it with a cone angle of 15 ◦. Each beam has 11 ranges spaced each
by 19 m.
Furthermore, all beams rotate simultaneously with a fixed rotational speed
ωb = 15 ◦s−1. This configuration allows all the sample points to lay within the rotor area,
with a higher density towards the center. For this thesis, both shortcomings of LIDARs
(spatial-averaging and LoS) are ignored to do a first trial with uneven sparse data. This
means that when a point in space is said to be sampled for the training dataset, all three
turbulence components u, v, and w are known.
The aforementioned LIDAR configuration would result in 4-dimensional sample points:
in time and space. Thus, further processing is needed to comply with the 3-dimensional
nature of the data handled (the LES data provided varies with y, z, and t). Taylor’s
frozen turbulence hypothesis is assumed in this thesis, which states that a turbulent eddy
travels with the mean wind speed while keeping its characteristics. Figure 5.1 details how
this hypothesis is used to build the learning dataset containing the LIDAR-like data. The
scheme in this figure is simplified for visualization purposes as follows:
• Only four beams are kept, with five ranges each.
• It is assumed that each range is separated horizontally by ∆x. As the beams have
a cone angle here, this means that the ranges are not at equal distances from each
other.
• The free wind speed is assumed to be U0.
• The LIDAR shoots the four beams twice 2∆t apart, where ∆t = ∆x/U0.
The samples are differentiated by color in Figure 5.1: the ones shot at t = t0 in red
and the ones shot at t = t0 + 2∆t in blue. The estimated plane is represented in light
red. As Taylor’s frozen turbulence hypothesis is assumed, having beams shot 2∆t later is
equivalent to shifting them by 2∆x upstream.
The final result of this sampling is a training dataset where each row contains the coor-
dinate (y, z, t) of each sampled point and the turbulence components (u, v, w). The
training dataset has 28 454 points across the full box, meaning 0.0336% of the total data
available. This reduces the training dataset by a factor of almost 63. In an ideal case
where all sampled data is obtained, the total number of points should be 55 × Nt where
Nt is the duration of the flow available (in seconds, truncated to the full second), as this
ideal LIDAR samples all five beams with 11 ranges each every second. However, the last
ranges cannot all be used, as the furthest ones lay outside of the box’s boundaries.
Furthermore, this sampling scheme means that the first few seconds of the simulation have
no data points. The wind speed at U0 = 8 m s−1 implies that the first sampled points (the
centerline beam, for the first range) will only reach the plane after 2.37 s of the inflow,

5.1.3
Neural network
Based on the sensitivity analysis carried out in Section 4.3, the same 20-layers, 128-nodes
architecture is kept through the different cases studied in this chapter. Furthermore, the
initial weight for the physics loss is set to wp = 10−2 based on Section 4.4, as it corresponds
to the biggest weight tested that would still reach satisfying variability. This value will
be validated by checking that the physics loss follows the expected trend (increasing and
then decreasing with the epochs).
The implementation of the Navier-Stokes equations was not successful in the first itera-
tion of the project. This finding led to three main changes in the methodology of this
iteration. First, the network does not predict the full non-dimensional longitudinal wind
speed anymore, but the zero-mean turbulence component ¯u = u/U0. This is achieved by
having the training dataset containing solely the zero-mean turbulence components. Then,
the equations used to calculate the physics loss are changed to simplify their evaluation.
Finally, a network purely data-driven is trained with the same parameters to provide a
better understanding of the role of the PDEs.
The Navier-Stokes equations derived in [21] cannot be implemented in the training of this
network using the data at hand, as the flow is purely 3-dimensional but still contains all
three turbulence components. It is thus chosen to simplify the PDEs used only to keep
the conservation of mass equation. This simplification allows for better network control,
as the pressure no longer needs to be calculated and neither does 1/Re. This change can
be perceived as restraining the degrees of freedom of the network, as fewer parameters can
be adjusted to decrease the loss. The conservation of mass for an incompressible flow state
that ∇· u = 0 where u is the velocity vector. However, the dimension x does not exist in
our data set, but the original OpenFOAM simulation was run in all four dimensions. To
account for the longitudinal variation of the wind, the equation used in the training of the
network is set to:

7.
Conclusion
This project aimed at estimating an inflow using LIDAR-like measurements. The first
iteration relied on turbulence data generated using the Mann model. This phase built the
general methodology that was later reused and helped underline the importance of the
choice of hyperparameters in the structure of the network. For example, the network’s
depth and breadth greatly influenced the accuracy of the results, and so did the weight
given to the physics loss. It was concluded from this part that the learning of the Navier
Stokes equations was challenging on such a dataset: doubts were raised on the compliance
of the data with the equations. The machine learning tool developed was then applied
to a LES-generated dataset with reduced equations in the physical model. Two different
sampling schemes were used as training data. The first relied on regularly spaced points,
while the other mimicked LIDAR measurements. All cases generated by these training
sets were tested both on a purely data-driven network and a physics-informed network.
An interpolation method was used as a baseline case for comparison purposes. Then, all
reconstructed flows were used in an aeroelastic simulation using HAWC2 to compare the
resulting loads onto the NREL 5MW turbine [1].
7.1
Key takeaways
The first goal of this thesis was to adapt the method presented in [21] to a bigger rotor.
While the method proved to be robust to a wider area than just a 60-meter diameter rotor,
the flow reconstruction broke down further away from the center in the LIDAR-sampling
scheme. However, this result was observed for all three methods discussed: data-driven
and physics-informed machine learning, as well as a simple interpolation. Thus, this loss of
accuracy is linked to the much sparser data availability on the outer part of the rotor that
could be bridged with a different sampling scheme. Moreover, the method’s accuracy was
also linked to how rapidly varying the original flow would get. It was found that despite
a training of the network having converged, it was impossible to get the higher-frequency
variations of the flow. This is linked to the training of the network itself, whose goal is
to minimize the loss across the full dataset, with a fixed number of weights and biases to
tune. It will thus ultimately have an averaging effect on the reconstructed flow.
Further, the effectiveness of physics in the training could not be highlighted in this project.
This finding stems first from the challenge of finding the appropriate set of equations that
rule the training set, as it was highlighted in Chapter 4. Using the full set of Navier-Stokes
equations would require a proper dataset that was not available for this work. Relying
solely on the conservation of mass proved not to be enough to gain an advantage over the
purely data-driven training.
Finally, the loads’ analysis underlined the importance of solving larger-scale structures
(such as shear) to accurately recreate the loads onto the turbine. In-plane loads are gener-
ally less affected by the resemblance of the flow. In contrast, out-of-plane loads will have
a higher fidelity with more turbulence, as these loads are highly driven by aerodynamic
forcing on the turbine.

7.2
Future work
The work presented in this thesis could be extended in several directions.
First, the
training dataset could be changed to better match the current state of technology of
LIDARs and test the performance of the estimation under degraded conditions. Then,
the physical model used could be improved to better capture the Navier Stokes equations.
Furthermore, the robustness of the network and all its parameters could be explored.
Finally, the real-time estimation and its implementation in an actual scenario could be
assessed.
7.2.1
Realistic LIDAR measurements
The configuration used here for the LIDAR measurements does not reflect the current
state of this technology. Hence, it is necessary to determine whether such results can
be achieved with a more realistic setup or gauge the type of specification needed from a
LIDAR to get accurate results.
First, the LoS was ignored in this work.
To include the unique direction of LIDAR
measurements, a solution would be to modify the training dataset and adapt the data loss
as follows. The training dataset would now only include the LoS velocity vLoS along with
the corresponding azimuthal angle ψ and cone angle θ of the LIDAR. While the network
would still estimate all turbulence components of the wind ¯up, ¯vp, and ¯wp, the data loss
initially calculated in Equation (4.9) for a given batch of data can be adapted to:

one beam at a time, at a frequency of 1 Hz. This sampling frequency would equate to

training needs to first be checked. Otherwise, a minimum sampling requirement could be
established, providing a base LIDAR required for such a method to be implemented.
Finally, a sensitivity analysis of the configuration of the beams should be explored. As
explained previously, the reconstruction performs best with more data density.

targeting certain areas of the rotor could be beneficial. For example, removing density
from the very center of the rotor and translating it outwards could improve the flow
reconstruction where it matters most for the turbine’s operation.

Furthermore, this work assumed the flow had no induction effect from the turbine. It

expected in the case of free flow. To address this effect, the effect of the induction either
needs to be included in the physical model during the training itself or applied onto the
reconstructed flow by using a known induction model.
7.2.3
Robustness assessment of the training
The hyperparameters of the network could be validated through a more structured frame-
work, where each is independently analyzed through a sensitivity study.
[29] offers a
rigorous framework to assess the robustness of machine-learning algorithms in power sys-
tems that could be adapted to the present use. Specifically, the hyperparameter tuning of
the current work could benefit from their methods, where all combinations of parameters
are tested on different random seeds. The seed impacts the initialization of the network
and, thus, impacts the overall result.
7.2.4
Adjusting the method to fit a controller scheme
Achieving real-time flow prediction using LIDAR measurements is necessary to eventually
use this method in a controller strategy.
This implies that both the training and the
predicting need to be done in reasonable time.
It was found that in this work that the training time was dependent on the size of the
training dataset: when using the full grid-sampling data in the LES case (amounting to
1 792 000 samples), each training step would take on average 27 s. By contrast, the sparser
dataset relying on the LIDAR sampling scheme (with less than 30 000 samples) could be
trained in under 0.2 s per step. A real-life training could thus be performed: the network
could be pre-trained on a large dataset of known data, then continuously updated with
incoming LIDAR measurements. The prediction of the network used here was found to
be linearly dependent on time with the number of points fed into the network. For the
entire domain of 55 × 55 × 28000 points, the prediction took 95 minutes. Thus, predicting
the entire plane for a single time step would take 0.2 s. Depending on what knowledge of

the flow is expected for the optimal operation of the LIDAR-assisted turbine, the real-life
training of the network and prediction of the flow ahead can be considered.
Although the reconstruction could be achieved with regards to the rapidity of the method,
some limitations first need to be addressed to realistically implement the estimation in a
practical scenario. The mean wind speed U0 was a known variable in this project, which
helped scale the inputs to the network and rely on the non-dimensional PDEs in the
training. With actual LIDAR measurements, only the line of sight velocity will be known,
and the mean wind speed is ever-changing. A new strategy will be needed to optimally
process the data for the training of the network.
Although the interpolation of the training datasets performed better in this project, the
method explored here still shows promise. First, through the previously stated future
avenues possible, the current state of the methodology can be greatly improved, while the
interpolation method has already reached its maximal potential. A more advanced physics
model could establish the advantage of the physics-informed over the data-driven training,
and better robustness of the parameters tuning could optimize the resulting flow.
Furthermore, the load analysis showed that in a lot of respect, capturing the larger eddies
provided satisfying results with recreating the moments on the structure. However, it
remains to be determined how much knowledge of the flow will be needed to appropriately
control of the turbine. It is possible that only estimating the larger turbulent structures
is suﬀicient to either reduce the loads on the turbine using this new controller compared
to a traditional feedback method, or increase power production.
