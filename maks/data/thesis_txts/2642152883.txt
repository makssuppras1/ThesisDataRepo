Summary
During anaerobic digestion (AD), organic matter is converted through a multi step process into
biogas. The biogas is mostly made up of methane (CH4) and carbon dioxide (CO2), which is pro-
duced in the ﬁnal step of AD by methanogens. However, these methanogens are highly sensitive to
disturbances. One of these disturbances is sulphate (SO2−
4 ) which can be present in relatively high
concentrations in e.g. industrial waste water or in manure from animals that have a protein rich
diet such as pigs. High concentrations of SO2−
4
promote the growth of sulphur reducing bacteria
(SRB) which are able to out compete methanogens for substrate and reduce SO2−
4
into sulphides
(S2−, HS−, H2S), thereby inhibiting the CH4 production. In order to ensure operational stability dur-
ing AD, monitoring and control strategies can be used to ensure the disturbances are kept within
reasonable ranges.
The eﬀect of SO2−
4
and sulphides on AD was investigated by adding four increasing concentration
levels (0.2 g S/L, 0.4 g S/L, 0.8 g S/L, and 1.6 g S/L) of Na2SO4 and Na2S to batch reactors with
cattle manure. A gas phase analysis, as well as a liquid phase analysis of the batch reactors was
carried out, and the results showed that 0.2 g Na2SO4-S/L had decreased the CH4 yield by 71%
compared to control. The inhibition increased with increasing levels of Na2SO4 in the batch reac-
tors. Furthermore, the competition for substrate was the main factor for decreased CH4 yield. The
addition of Na2S to the batch reactors showed that for 0.2 g Na2S-S/L, the methane yield increased
by 18%, while 0.4 g Na2S-S/L had the same CH4 yield as control. The other two concentration levels
(0.8 g Na2S-S/L and 1.6 g Na2S-S/L) had decreased CH4 yield by 15% and 25%, respectively. The
Na2S results showed that an initial high concentration of sulphides could cause a longer lag period
for the methanogens. Based on these results, a possible solution for SO42−and sulphide control
would be a ratio controller that would ensure the C/SO2−
4 remains optimal for methanogens.

Introduction
While the energy production in the developed world has varied little the last 30 years due to more
eﬃcient consumption, developing countries have increased their energy production considerably
[1]. Currently, the majority of the energy generated today is from fossil fuels. Coal, oil and natu-
ral gas all have a relatively high energy density. Furthermore, they are relatively easy to transport
making them very reliable sources of energy across the world. And whilst there still is a relatively
high abundance of fossil fuel deposits left in the world, these deposits are often located in deeper
wells and mines which can be too costly to extract. Furthermore, as climate change is becoming a
growing concern for many people, there has been an increased focus on the development of renew-
able energy technologies. Recently, there has also been a push towards a more circular economy
as it presents additional economic opportunities. In the European Union it has been estimated that
by utilizing principles of circular economy, the gross domestic product of the European Union could
potentially be increased by 0.5% in 2030 with the creation of 700000 additional jobs [2]. This
could prove vital, as it is believed that by the year 2050, the global consumption of biomass, fossil
fuels, minerals, and metals will be doubled, and the annual waste generation will be increased by
70% [3; 4].
Anaerobic digestion (AD) could play a crucial role to recycle some of our waste. It is a process which
can utilize waste from a myriad of diﬀerent sources such as agriculture waste, manure, municipal
waste, sewage, and food waste. The product from AD is biogas and digestate which are a clean
source of energy and fertilizer, respectively. Although AD is not a novel concept and has been used
for several decades now, improvements can be made to optimize the process [5]. Low biogas yield
speciﬁcally, and to a lesser extant high maintenance costs, corrosion problems from hydrogen sul-
phide (H2S), and inability to maintain optimum pH have all been attributed to situations where AD
has not been economically viable [6]. To avoid these problems, automatic process control systems
can be used to improve operational stability, and allow the AD to operate at maximum capacity.
These systems are designed to monitor parameters that are sensitive to process disturbances, as
well as using controllers to ensure the parameters are kept within acceptable ranges.
The primary objective of this thesis is to analyze how sulphate (SO2−
4 ) and sulphides (S2−, HS−,
H2S) aﬀect methane (CH4) production from AD. This will be achieved using batch reactors with
cattle manure as substrate and Na2SO4 or Na2S salts. Additionally, the results of the analysis will be
used to suggest possible implementations for SO2−
4 and sulphide control in a rule based controller.

1.1
Anaerobic Digestion
Anaerobic digestion is a complex multi step process in which microorganisms breakdown biodegrad-
able material in absence of oxygen (O2). It is a natural process which can be found in many diﬀerent
environments such as swamps, sediments, paddy ﬁelds, inside digestive system of ruminants, and
landﬁlls. Utilization of the process allows us to degrade bio-wastes and convert it into biogas which
is a green and renewable energy source. The biogas generated from AD is typically a mixture of
∼60% CH4 and ∼40% CO2 [7; 8]. The ratio between the two gasses varies depending on the overall
AD process. The biogas can be combusted in a combined heat and power system directly or it can
be upgraded to ∼97% CH4 which enables it to be injected into the natural gas grid [8]. In addition
to the biogas, a digestate is also produced from the remaining organic matter which is rich in nutri-
ents, and can be used as fertilizer. Although AD is often divided into four intermediate steps, each
of these steps has individual processes that interact with diﬀerent processes from other steps. An
overview of some of the major interactions between the diﬀerent processes can be seen in Figure

Hydrolysis of the substrate in AD is necessary to break down complex organic molecules into simpler
soluble organic molecules. Bacteria excrete diﬀerent types of enzymes which catalyze hydrolysis

carbohydrates into saccharides. There are diﬀerent groups of hydrolytic enzymes for proteins, lipids

Depending on which substrate is used, the duration of hydrolysis can vary. If the carbohydrates
have an abundance of lignocellulosic material, the rate of hydrolysis can be greatly reduced [9].
Hydrolysis is therefore often considered the rate limiting step for degradation of particulate or solid
organic matters such as manure, sewage sludge and crop residues [10].
1.1.2
Acidogenesis
The next step in AD is acidogenesis. During this step, fermentative bacteria further breakdown sol-
uble organic products that were created during hydrolysis. This results in acidic conditions and the
production of alcohols, CO2, hydrogen (H2) and volatile fatty acids (VFA) [11]. The production of
these products is governed by which reaction is the most energetically favourable for the metabolism
of the fermentative bacteria [12]. An important external factor here is the partial pressure of the
H2. During fermentation, the co-enzyme acetyl-CoA acts as an electron acceptor and is reduced
to acids and alcohols. However, during low H2 pressure conditions (<10−4 atm), the fermentative
bacteria are able to use H+ as electron acceptor and produce H2, CO2, and acetate instead of acids
and alcohols [13].
1.1.3
Acetogenesis
Acetogenic bacteria convert the VFA, alchohols and other fermentation products from the acido-
genesis into mainly acetate, H2, and CO2. These reactions are enabled by two diﬀerent groups of
acetogenic bacteria. The ﬁrst group is known as proton reducing bacteria which can produce ac-
etate, CO2, and H2O from VFA see Table 1. However, due to these reactions being endothermic
at standard condition, the concentration of H2 must be <10−4 atm which is low enough to make
the reactions possible [14]. The proton reducing bacteria are able to achieve this by forming a syn-
trophic relationship with H2 consuming methanogens, as well as SO2−
4
reducers. Once the partial
pressure is below this threshold, the reaction becomes exothermic and the energy yield increases
with decreasing H2 partial pressure [12]. However, if the partial pressure becomes too low, the
energy yield from the H2 oxidation is not suﬃcient to achieve oxidation of VFA. Therefore, there is
a small range which allows for both methanogenesis or SO2−
4 reduction and VFA oxidation to occur

1.2
Key Inﬂuencing Factors
1.2.1
Temperature
AD can be performed at diﬀerent temperatures. The two most conventional setups are mesophilic
digestion ∼(30-43◦C) and thermophilic digestion ∼(48-60◦C) [20]. Both provide their own advan-
tages as well as drawbacks. The mesophilic digestion is the most widely used as it is more tolerant
to changing environmental factors, however, the retention time is longer which results in a lower
biogas production over time. Thermophilic digestion on the other hand, has lower retention time
thereby digests the feed stock quicker and produces more biogas over time. Additionally, due to
high temperature, most pathogens will be eliminated. However, thermophilic microorganisms are
generally more sensitive to environmental changes. Furthermore, the economic cost of additional
input energy required to maintain thermophilic temperatures results in mesophilic digestion being
easier for large scale bioreactors.

1.2.2
pH
There are a wide variety of diﬀerent microbial species involved in AD, each with an optimal pH
range. Due to the individual processes and reactions taking place during AD, the pH can vary greatly.
Fermentative bacteria have a pH optimum of ∼(5-7), while methanogens have a pH optimum of
∼(6.5-8) [21]. Buildup of VFA is generally the main reason behind lower pH levels. In addition,
other compounds typically found in bioreactor such as ammonia, H2S, and H2 [21]. Furthermore,
the pH is related to the dissociation characteristics of some inhibitors which is explained in section
1.3. It is therefore very important to maintain the pH for operational stability. This is typically
achieved in manure digestors by buﬀering compounds such as bicarbonate which is found in cattle
manure.
1.2.3
Nutrients
In order to achieve adequate degradation of organic matter in bioreactors, the microorganism need
to receive the necessary nutrients required for growth. Most of these nutrients are usually available
in the necessary amounts when treating animal manure [13]. The nutrients can be divided into
macro- and micro nutrients. The primary macro nutrient required for AD is nitrogen as it is essential
for protein synthesis. Phosphorus is required for ATP and other nucleic acids, potassium is required
for cellular transport and cation balance, and sulphur is required for certain amino acids. However
these three are not required in the same amounts as nitrogen. Carbon is a building block required
for all organic molecules, and functions as the primary energy source for AD. However, since there
is always an abundance of carbon available it is not considered a limiting nutrient. Instead, the ratio
between it and other nutrients is used to deﬁne the nutritional requirements. Examples of these
ratios have been reported to be (25:1 for C:N), (180:1 for C:P) and (65:1 for C:K) for thermophilic
AD of municipal solid waste [22].
Micro nutrients required for AD are generally cobalt, copper, iron, molybdenum, nickel, selenium,
tungsten, and zink. These are used in lesser amounts and are often required for very speciﬁc en-
zymes [21].
1.3
Inhibition
There can be several diﬀerent sources of inhibition during AD. Usually these are ammonia, VFA, and
sulphide and are formed when the substrate is degraded. However, other inhibitory compounds such
as LCFA, heavy metals, and antibiotics can sometimes be present in the substrate from the start.
1.3.1
Ammonia
During AD ammonia is more often than not one of the main sources of nitrogen which is essential
for microbial growth. Despite this, high concentrations of free ammonia (NH3) will typically have
an inhibiting eﬀect. Depending on the substrate, the amounts of ammonia present can diﬀer. Cattle
manure will usually have an ammonia concentration of 2-4 g-N/L, while pig or chicken often have
ammonia concentration greater than 4g-N/L [13].
The inhibition is dependent on diﬀerent factors such as temperature, pH, substrate, and adaptation
of microorganisms. In general it can be said that higher temperature results in higher metabolic
growth and digestion rate. However, high operating temperatures and pH, often result in a higher

1.3.2
Long chain fatty acids
LCFA are mainly formed during hydrolysis of lipids when the ester or ether bond between the head
and the tail of the lipid is severed. Once severed, the head can be a glycerol or alcohol, while the tail
will be a LCFA. The mechanism behind LCFA inhibition is from adsorption to the surface of microbial
cells. It induces lysis of protoplasts and damages the transport channel of microbial cells [29]. The
amount of inhibition diﬀers depending on which LCFA is causing inhibition. Previous research has
shown that palmitic acid causes 50% inhibition at 3 g/L, stearic acid causes 50% inhibition at 1.5
g/L, and oleaic acid causes 50% inhibition at 1.5 g/L [30].
1.3.3
Volatile fatty acids
The accumulation of VFA during AD is an indicator of a kinetic uncoupling between acid producers
and consumers [31]. The VFA are produced during acidogenesis when soluble organic products are
fermented. While high concentration of VFA may result in inhibition, it is not the concentration of
VFA itself which inhibits. The inhibition is associated with the undissociated form of the VFA as it
is able to permeate cellular membranes thereby decreasing internal pH. The concentration of the
undissociated form of VFA is highest at low levels of pH [31; 32].

1.4
Control Strategies
Process instability is one of the most important challenges during the AD process. The use of control
strategies to improve the biogas yield is not a novel concept. Some of the key inﬂuencing factors
discussed in section 1.2, have to be monitored to ensure operational stability. Substrate composi-
tion, pH, temperature, biogas composition, and production are important fundamental operating
parameters. However, parameters indicating process disturbances such as VFA, alkalinity, ammonia
and H2 are of greater interest [6].
Industrial scale biogas plants primarily use biogas production as the main indicator for overall pro-
cess performance. This is due to being the main objective of the AD process, as well as being relative
easy to measure. However, biogas production does not reﬂect on the diﬀerent processes which are
responsible for CH4 production. Other indicators are therefore needed, as CH4 production will only
show process imbalance when the process is already damaged [44]. pH is commonly the only online
liquid indicator, however, it works poorly for process imbalance in systems with high buﬀer capac-
ity such as when digesting animal manure. Furthermore, industrial scale biogas plants often deal
with a variety of diﬀerent substrates where the buﬀering capacity diﬀers which can make pH an
unreliable indicator of process imbalance [25]. The concentration of VFA has been identiﬁed to be
one of the most important parameters for process control [32]. Accumulation of VFA during AD is
dependent on several diﬀerent factors such as hydraulic retention time, loading rate, temperature,
and disturbance [13]. VFA measurements during AD can be carried out using oﬄine auto titrators,
however, these are not able to read individual VFA. Therefore, there has been increasing focus on
online measuring methods which are also able to read individual VFA such as gas chromatography,
liquid chromatography, and spectroscopy [45].

2.5
Control Strategy
The control strategy which will be discussed in this thesis is based on work from literature [50].
Since the core of this thesis is not focused on the control part, this thesis will not go into great
details regarding the structure and various rules and conditions that are used, see Appendix 6.4
for an overview. In short, the control strategy is designed for a CSTR and is based on supervisory
control structure. The outer loop is a rule based controller with 12 diﬀerent rules. Each rule is
active under certain conditions, and applies new set point adjustments, as well as a new feed ﬂow
strategy to maintain and keep reactor stable, see Figure 7. The inner loop is a feedback control
loop which consists of a proportional controller. The conditions in the outer loop are based on the
current gas production and VFA concentration during AD. Outside the inner- and outer loops is an
inter-lock system which prevents acidiﬁcation by checking the pH value. When the pH is below 6,
the feed ﬂow rate is set to zero and gas set point will be kept constant.

H2S is considered to be very toxic as it is able to readily permeate the cell membrane and denatures
native proteins [27]. The inhibitory eﬀect H2S has on diﬀerent anaerobic species varies, however,
in general SRB are less aﬀected compared to methanogens [54]. H2S has been reported to cause
50% inhibition at about 53 mg HSS-S/L and complete inhibition at 212 mg H2S-S/L [55], 50%
inhibition in potentially acclimated mesophilic upﬂow reactors at about 1.4 g H2S-S/L [56]. Other
reported values are 53-132 mg H2S-S/L (pH 7-8 suspended sludge), 95 mg H2S-S/L (pH 6.4-7.2),
and 265 mg H2S-S/L (pH 7.8-8.0) [27].
Overall, the decreased formation of CH4 in the batch reactors with Na2SO4 seems to primarily stem
from competition between SRB and methanogens for substrate. Secondly, the formation of H2S from
SO2−
4
reduction is also likely to have caused inhibition, however, the concentration of H2S reached
inhibition very late and by that time the system was already inhibited from competition between
SRB and methanogens. These issues are reported to occur frequently when AD is subjected to high
SO2−
4 concentrations [41].

3.3
Implementing Future Control Conditions for SO2−
4
and Sul-
phides
Applying new conditions regarding SO2−
4 concentrations for a control strategy would be problematic.
SRB are able to out compete methanogens in anaerobic conditions when the substrate is limited.
The control strategy proposed in section 2.5 is designed to monitor the VFA concentrations and
either increase or decrease the feed ﬂow of substrate to maintain optimal CH4 production. In order
to promote methanogens when the concentration of SO2−
4 is high, additional substrate with a lower
sulphur content would have to be added to increase the C/SO2−
4 ratio. One solution to this could be
a ratio control strategy which would monitor the current C/SO2−
4 ratio and enable a secondary feed
ﬂow with a diﬀerent substrate that had a lower sulphur content. However, since inhibition from VFA
is considered more important than SO2−
4 inhibition, the secondary feed ﬂow would only be enabled
during conditions when the VFA concentration was low to avoid accumulation of VFA.
The results from the total sulphur content in the solid and liquid phase analysis in section 3.2
showed, that HS−in the liquid phase would be consumed quickly by microorganisms. Therefore, if
the control strategy is able to maintain optimal conditions for methanogens, it could possibly not be
necessary to have a speciﬁc controller for sulphide concentration. In a scenario where the concen-
tration of sulphides was very high, there could be other options such as implementing microaeration
which promotes the growth SOB. This is a methods used by some industrial scale biogas plants to
get rid of H2S [59]. Furthermore, by maintaining pH levels above ≈8.0, the fraction of HS−that

would dissociate into H2S could be kept to a minimum [54]. However, this would only reduce the
fraction of H2S temporarily. If there was a buildup of VFA, the pH would decrease again and the
fraction of H2S would increase.

Conclusion
The addition of SO2−
4
proved to cause severe inhibition of the CH4 production from AD. The in-
hibition was observed to be 71% compared to the control in the lowest concentration level (0.2 g
Na2SO4-S/L), and increased with increasing Na2SO4 concentration levels. This was primarily caused
by SRB out competing the methanogens for substrate. Furthermore, the concentration of total sul-
phides was also well within reported inhibition ranges for the batch reactors with a concentration
≥0.4 g Na2SO4-S/L. On the other hand, when Na2S was used for the batch reactors, level 1 (0.2 g
Na2S-S/L) had increased the yield of CH4 by 18% compared to the control, while level 2 had similar
yield as the control, and the CH4 yield in level 3 and 4 was lower than the control. This was likely
caused by a longer lag phase caused by high initial concentrations of sulphide from the Na2S.
Based on the analysis of the eﬀect of Na2SO4 and Na2S on AD, a possible control and monitoring
system for scenarios with high concentrations of SO2−
4 or sulphide would be a combination of online
monitoring and ratio controller. The online monitor would observe whether the C/SO2−
4
ratio or
the sulphide concentration was within acceptable ranges for operational stability. If they were
outside these ranges, a ratio controller would enable a secondary feed ﬂow which would introduce
a diﬀerent substrate with a higher C/SO2−
4
ratio. However, the secondary feed ﬂow would only be
enabled during conditions when VFA levels were within acceptable ranges.
5
Future Perspective
It is unclear as to why the the CH4 yield increased by 18% when the concentration of Na2S was 0.2
g Na2S-S/L. This could be further investigated by analysing the composition of the microorganic
community in the Na2S batch reactors. This could shed some light on whether there were speciﬁc
microorganisms that promoted the increased CH4 yield or some other factor. Furthermore, a similar
experiment should be attempted with diﬀerent concentration levels to see if the results can be
replicated. These levels could be 0.1 g Na2S-S/L, 0.2 g Na2S-S/L, and 0.4 g Na2S-S/L which could
tell whether lower concentrations of Na2S would increase the CH4 yield even further.
