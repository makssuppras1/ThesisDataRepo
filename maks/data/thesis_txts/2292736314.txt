Summary (English)
Investigation of dierent blind source separation methods for automatically arte-
fact suppression has been conducted, namely that of individual component anal-
ysis (ICA) and principal component analysis (PCA). The suppression method
is based on the variance of these sources. They have been tested on simulated
data and semi simulated data. Both ICA and PCA has been successfully used in
automatically suppression of artefact signals in electroencephalography. Accord-
ing to the simulations conducted, it seems that ICA has a better performance.
Furthermore incomplete separation of cerebral activity from the artefact source,
and suppression of cerebral activity in artefact sources, with wavelet denoising
has also been investigated. It has been found that a global threshold method
called Visu shrinkage, with a modied threshold for the lowest frequency band
in the approximation coecients using Sure shrinkage, performed the best.

Introduction
In order to investigate and trying to understand the human brain, several
methods has been invented, e.g functional magnetic resonance imaging(fMIR),
positron emission tomography imaging (PET), electroencephalography (EEG),
Magnetoencephalography (MEG) and Near infrared spectroscopy (NIRS). There
are several reasons in to focus on EEG measurement one is recent years develop-
ments of commercial 16 and 32 channels EEG headset/cap has been introduced
to a semi commercial market. Making EEG measurement really relevant for
scientic and commercial purposes. Historically EEG measurements has been
help in laboratories and hospitals settings. Where the subject was "xated"
(told to sit still and not blink to much) to avoid artefact contamination of the
recording. EEG record potential dierent in the electro physiology, which occur
in the brain and various other sources, such has muscle movement eye move-
ment and eye blinks. This pose a problem because the amplitude of the artefact
sources can be of much high of those of the brain. The artefact contamination
gets worse when the laboratory xation constrains are eased, like in working
with kids, more real life like setting which allows more movement or potential
commercial uses. There exist already several blind source separation schemes
for removing artefact form EEG recording such as adjust, BCIlab, faster and
eyecatch. Mainly individual components analysis and principal component anal-
ysis are popular choices. There are one problem with these methods which is
that they are not capable in completely isolate the artefact signal from the brain
signal, resulting in unintentional removal for brain signal. There has been pro-

Chapter 2
Brain structure
The purpose of this chapter is to give a brief overview of the brain and its
structure. For more details please consult [Rei10]. The human brain consist of
approximately a trillion nerve cells, each of which is connected to around 10 000
other nerve cells. The brain is then divided into 3 compartments: the hindbrain,
the middlebrain and the forebrain. The hindbrain is placed on top of the spinal
cord. The hindbrain's primary function is to take care of the life supporting
functions of the body, such as breathing and heartbeat rhythm. Furthermore,
it deals with balance, and sets the overall alertness of the brain. The largest
area of the hindbrain is the cerebellum, which take care of last mentioned func-
tions. It has been suggested that it is also involved in spatial reasoning and
sound discrimination [BL03]. The middlebrain is associated with coordination
of movements which includes movements of the eyes. It also relays auditory in-
formation from the ear to the brain. The forebrain consists of several important
brain compartments. The cortex and sub-cortical parts thalamus , hypothala-
mus, amygdala and hippocampus. Thalamus functions as a relay station for the
sensory information being delivered to the cortex. Hypothalamus plays a central
role in control of motivated actions, such as eating, sleeping and sexual desire.
Amygdala and hippocampus form, together the limbic system, whose functions
are in memory, learning and processing of emotions. Most of the compartments
comes in pairs, which is also the case for the sub cortical ones. That means that
there are a right and left side amydala for instance.

2.1
Theory behind EEG signals
The potential dierences originating from the activities of a single neuron are
far too small to be measured with EEG equipment. What is picked up is the
signal from large continuities (in the magnitude of millions) of neurons activating
more or less synchronized. The signals resulting from this synchronized activity
fall into a very broad range of frequencies. Historically they have been divided
into certain frequency bands with characteristic features. Further, as discussed,
the signals have also characteristic spatial patterns. The spatial patterns are,
as discussed earlier, linked to the areas of the brain required for handling the
current task undertaken by the brain. The energy distribution of the EEG signal
is concentrated mainly at low frequencies below 30 Hz, and has a characteristic
peak around 7-14 Hz also called the alpha band. The actual potential dierences
measured, are often measured as the potential dierence between a reference
electrode, often placed on the ear, and the scalp electrodes.
The measured
potential is in the magnitude of 10 - 100 µV. In EEG signals it is common
to distinguish between two types of signals spontaneous potentials and evoked
potentials. Evoked potentials occurs when external stimuli are present, hence
the ones used in determining brain functionality in dierent areas.
2.2
Placement of EEG electrodes
In order to secure standardization and reproducibility of EEG experiments, the
10-20 system for electrode placement has been developed. For a detailed descrip-
tion please refer to [CLN85] and [Soc94]. It describes labelling and positioning
of the electrode on the scalp. The "10" and "20" indicate that the electrodes
are placed at 10% and 20% of the front-back and right-left scalp distances. Each
electrode has a letter code associated with it: F for frontal, T for temporal, C
for central, P for parietal and O for occipital. The positions of these landmarks
have a slight ambiguity attached to them. Therefore a certain care should be
taken when comparing results. For example dierent positions of the reference
channel can be found to be the anterior root of the tragus, the peak region
of the tragus or the upper limit of the external ear canal. There are practical
problems associated with placing the electrodes, related to the precision with
which they can be placed on the scalp. A common tool for maintaining the
electrodes relative positions to each other is EEG electrode cap, where the elec-
trodes are part of a cap which is placed on the scalp. In the case of this report,
a headset with the same properties has been used. The cap can be slightly tilted
between experiments which results in a slight translation of the coordinates of
the cap/headset. Another problem is that the electrode needs to be able to con-

Principal component analysis (PCA) is a method of reducing the dimensionality
of a given problem, by choosing the dimension there best describes the variability
in the data in the best possible manner. It is done by creating a orthogonal basis

its properties are based on [TSK06] and [EC12]. A nice property is that in most
cases the majority of the variability can be described by few basis vectors. So

In order to evaluate the performance of the artefact removal and reconstruction
algorithm, two methods are used. The rst method is compare the cleansed and

6.1
Simulated EEG data
In order to simulate the EEG data the approach of Mäkinen et al [MTM05]
has been adopted with modications, but his entire method will be discussed
here. This approach has successfully been used in [YBHC04] and [YBH+07].
The authors of these articles have made their code for the simulated data pub-
licly available at http://www.cs.bris.ac.uk/ rafal/phasereset/. The dataset to
be simulated consists of 32 electrodes and a sampling frequency of 250 Hz. The
energy of the signal has been set to be around 20 µV. The length of the simu-
lation was 973 epochs each containing 200 frames.
In the method a single dipole model was used for simplicity, to simulate the
==PAGE:56==
40
Simulation of EEG signal
topography of the error-related negativity (Ne/ERN) peak. Briey explained
this is the part of the ERP, that is measurable with an EEG device, for details
[FHHB90] and [GGC+93]. The position dipole was x = 0 the middle line, y =
2.5 cm anterior to the T7-T8 axis and z = 6.5 cm superior to FPz-Oz axis. The
orientation was x = 0, y = 0.1, z = 0.99. The scalp voltage distribution for
the dipole was computed using a standard forward model algorithm from besa
[SHB10] and the software can be found at www.besa.de. A second dipole was
added at a frequency at 1 Hz and with a positive peak at 200 ms. The position
of the second dipole was x = 0 cm, y = 0.5 cm and z = 0.45 cm, the orientation
of the dipole was x = 0, y = -0.25 and z = 0.97. In order to simulate noise there
was added uncorrelated background "noise" to the two signals from the dipoles.
The way the noise was simulated using 50 dierent sinusoids of randomly vary-
ing frequency and phase. The phase and frequencies are randomly selected for
each epoch. The span of frequencies was 0.1 to 125 Hz, the phase was chosen
between 0 to 2π. The maximal allowed power of a single sinusoid was set to
20 µV. All the amplitudes on other frequencies were then scaled to match the
power distribution of EEG signals. This process is similar to an inverse-Fourier
transform with random phase of the spectra analysis of real EEG data. Two
assumptions have been made that are not entirely true. First the frequency dis-
tribution changes with the electrode position on the scalp. It has been assumed
that the frequency distribution do not change on electrode position and hence
equal for all electrodes. A further problem is that in the method of the example,
the "noise" is added to the dipole signal in a way which makes it uncorrelated.
The last one is of real concern, because ICA assumes that the signals are mixed
together. In order to obtain this, the "noise" is mixed together with a random
mixing matrix, and then renormalized such that the highest amplitude is 20µV.
This mixing matrix will not match that of the dipole signal, so the dipole has
been omitted. This should ensure that each channel has a power spectra which
closely approximates that of an average EEG signal. Yet the correlation between
channels do not have any reassembles to that of a real EEG signal. Further the
eect of having the Ne/ERN peak is lost as well.
In order to add artefacts to the signal, the following approach was chosen. The
shape of the added artefacts was obtained by using a FIR bandpass ler (3-11
Hz) on a Poisson distribution, with an amplitude three times higher than that
of the brain signal, such that the amplitude of the artefacts becomes 10 µV.
This has been done in a similar way by [DSM07], [NV06] and [AMJ12]. The
artefact can be seen in 6.2. The artefacts were added to a random channel and
then mixed together with the other channels using the mixing matrix. The data
set was then divided into intervals of 4 seconds and in each interval at a random
position an artefact signal occurs. An example of a simulated signal can be seen
in gure 6.3 and the spectrogram of the simulated signal can be seen in gure 6.1.

age performs really well, slight improvements can come from a higher threshold
level in the lowest frequency part of the approximation coecients. Both Bayes
and Visu shrinkage benets from this with regards to the NMSE. Some small
increase in error may come to some of the frequency bands as a consequence
of this increased threshold level. Furthermore is the soft thresholding scheme
clearly performing better than the hard thresholding scheme. The main reason
that wavelet denoising works is that it assumes the signal is a smooth function,
which also can be seen from the plots of the residuals, which after denoising
the residual are more smooth. This is furthermore conrmed by the better per-
formance using soft threshold scheme, which has the property to generate very
smooth functions.
This might pose a limit to this method concerning other
types of artefacts than eye artefact such as muscle artefact which are not neces-
sarily very smooth. Another challenge for wavelet denoising is that the wavelet
coecients are very sparse and consist of few large values. This is especially the
case for the ICA. The only method which is designed to be robust in this case
are Visu shrinkage, properly giving the reason for its good performance. The
sparsity poses problem for the statistically based method because a lot of the
coecients do not contain information on the distribution of the signal nor the
noise, and therefore forces a decision to be made with very little information.

7.3
conclusion
There has been some trouble with the simulated data, especially with regards
to the ICA. So for the conclusion more empathy will be placed on the semi
simulated data, which also should be closer to that of real EEG data. It seems
possible to automatically nd and remove artefacts based on the variance, when
the amplitude of the artefacts is much higher than those of the cerebral activity.
It seems that the ICA approach is better at nding pure artefact sources than
PCA, and is therefore to be preferred. Based on the semi simulated data it seems
that soft threshold is clearly to prefer. In order to determine the best scheme
for wavelet denoising, some problems was encountered due to the sparsity of
the wavelet coecients. So the robust scheme of Visu shrinkage seems to be
preferred. Another trend seems to be present that more aggressive thresholding
is preferred in the low frequency bands. This explains the good performance of
the hybrid approach of Sure shrinkage. The last scheme was the best performing
threshold scheme.
