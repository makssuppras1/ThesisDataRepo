ABSTRACT 
This thesis aims at identifying the differences or similarities between men and women and how they 
experience and perceive road anger in Denmark. The data used for the thesis was a representative 
online survey among 3,986 inhabitants from Denmark, done by DTU. To enlighten differences and 
similarities between men and women, Thematic coding - and Principal component analysis were used. 
The methods were used to investigate, what makes people angry while driving, how long do people 
think about road anger episodes, and the involvement and expression in road anger episodes. 
Thematic coding was used on the open-ended questions regarding what makes people angry while 
driving and how long do people think about a road anger episode. From the Thematic coding analysis, 
categories and themes were identified on both questions. However, no significant differences were 
found between men and women, from the open-ended questions. As a further investigation, a 
Principal component analysis was performed on the standardized question regarding, what makes 
people angry while driving, and the involvement and expression in road anger episodes. The results 
from the Principal component analysis regarding, what makes people angry while driving, a 2-factor 
solution was confirmed. The 2 factors obtained, were categorized as; Personal problems and 
Externalities. The results from this showed a significant difference between men and women, 
indicating that woman is more likely to get angry if they either have personal problems or if 
externalities are interfering with their driving. Furthermore, the results regarding the involvement in 
road anger episodes both as aggressor and victim showed a significant difference between men and 
women in some of the categories. Additionally, a Principal component analysis was performed on the 
standardized question from Driving anger expression (DAX). The results, confirmed a 4-factor 
solution, with the factors; Constructive behavior, Physical aggression, Verbal gestures, and Use of 
vehicle, where previous studies have found identical factors (Møller & Haustein, 2017). Significant 
differences were found in Constructive behavior and Use of vehicle.  
From the thesis, it was concluded that women were more likely to get angry when they either have 
personal problems or if externalities are interfering with their driving. Additionally, differences were 
found in men's and women’s expression and involvement in road anger episodes. Moreover, 
similarities between men and women were found regarding how long people think about a road anger 
episode, the behavior of other road users, that makes them angry, and that they impose responsibility 
on other road users and not themselves.

1 INTRODUCTION 
Experiencing road anger is unpleasant for all road users. However, as there is seen an increase in the 
road network in Denmark. Since 2010, the increase in traffic has been 14%, where the number of 
kilometers driven has increased by over 35% (Vejdirektoratet, 2020). This increase in cars on the 
road network does not come without implications. Road anger is found to be one of the larger factors 
that pose a traffic safety risk (Wickens, et al., 2016). Recent research suggests that the level of road 
anger expression in Denmark is increasing in the high-density populated Capital region (Møller & 
Haustein, 2017). To prepare actions to mitigate road anger, a better understanding of the factors that 
affect feelings and expressions of road anger is needed. 
1.1 PROBLEM STATEMENT 
This project focuses on the identification and examination of differences between men and women 
and how they perceive and experience road anger in Denmark. This is done with the use of a survey 
completed by DTU. The project aims at finding both differences and similarities between men's and 
women’s behavior regarding road anger. To investigate gender differences three aspects regarding 
road anger are investigated: 1. What makes people angry while driving, 2. How long do people think 
about an episode and 3. Involvement and expression of road anger episodes. Both qualitative and 
quantitative methods are used to assess if differences or similarities are found between men and 
women in different scenarios.

2 BACKGROUND 
Before investigating an in-depth analysis, it is important to understand what road anger is and the 
current situation of the subject. Therefore, this section will introduce road anger and how this 
phenomenon is in Denmark and what it can lead to. Differences between men and women’s 
involvement in accidents are also further presented and finally, differences found from other countries 
are described.    
2.1 ROAD ANGER 
All over the world road users such as car drivers, cyclists and pedestrians drive or walk every day. 
While driving, road users can experience different scenarios, good or bad. Almost all road users have 
experienced a good scenario, but on most occasions, the users have experienced a bad or unpleasant 
scenario (Møller & Haustein, 2018). The bad or unpleasant scenario is often related to being 
endangered by the turbulent behavior of another driver that shows anger or is aggressive towards 
them. Research indicates that there has been an increase in road anger as both victims and one who 
engages in road anger in Denmark from 2008 – 2016 (Møller & Haustein, 2017), see Table 1. Here 
it is shown that there has been a significant increase in road anger on the Danish road network.

When either experiencing or engaging in a turbulent behavior on the road, there are two different 
expressions. The first is aggressive driving and this is not to be confused with the more familiar term 
“road anger” since road anger is a more extreme form of aggressive driving where the person has an 
intent to commit a criminal act of physical violence. Aggressive driving can be categorized as unsafe 
driving behavior, with a disregard for safety. Research suggests that the difference in aggressive 
driving and road anger is as follows (Weinberger & Sreenivasan, 2017).

traffic. The same is seen with blocking cars, as it can happen sometimes, that another car is blocking 
your way, but not intentionally.     
Anger is a well-known emotion by all people. This emotion can have positive or negative 
consequences, depending on how it is expressed. If it is expressed in a moderate, non-hostile 
constructive way it can be taken positively, e.g. “paying closer attention to being a safe driver” or 
“paying closer attention to others driving to avoid getting in an accident” (Deffenbacher, et al., 
2002).  Even though there can be a positive consequence, the negative consequences are often more 
dangerous if the anger is expressed in traffic. Here it can contribute to a traffic safety risk. Research 
indicates that there is an increasing problem in Denmark with road anger (Møller & Haustein, 2017), 
(Møller & Haustein, 2018). Road anger is as mentioned earlier a term that is used to a wide range of 
different anger outbursts, ranging from violence in the form of physical fights, cursing, or making 
obscene gestures. These outbursts happen when two road users have a confrontation in a way where 
either one or both people’s goals are obstructed e.g. a person drives too slowly or if the person does 
not pull in too the right lane.  However, it is mentioned that road anger is not always caused by the 
actual behavior perceived in traffic, it can also be how the act or behavior is interpreted (Haustein & 
Møller, 2019). When people experience a situation where road anger is involved, the perception of

the situation, how they react, and how they perceive the other person's behavior can be crucial to how 
their aggression is expressed. Research shows that people who react more aggressively when driving 
are often blaming their reactions on other road users, saying they are driving badly (Lennon &

2.2 ROAD ANGER AND ROAD SAFETY 
Accidents on the road are always a danger and cause a considerable loss to individuals, their families, 
and to the nations in which the accidents occur. Fatalities or injuries in traffic lead to a cost in 
treatment and a loss in productivity for the people who either died or were seriously injured. 
According to the World Health Organization, (Organization, 2020), road traffic accidents are costing 
most countries 3% of their gross domestic product. It can be concluded that road traffic accidents 
have a significant impact on the country and specifically to the people involved, but what the causes 
for many of the accidents are yet uncertain.  
According to the Danish Road Directorate (Vejdirektoratet, 2019), the number of fatalities in 2018 
was lower than the years before. Nevertheless, the number of injured people on the Danish road 
network increased in the same year. 
The number of fatalities registered by the police on the Danish road network in 2018 was 171 people, 
where the number of fatalities of car drivers and younger people in the age group from 18-24 years 
was lower than the previous years. However, the registered number of people with smaller injuries 
was 1,425 and the people with serious injuries were registered to be 1,862. This was the highest 
number of seriously injured people on the Danish road network since 2013 (Vejdirektoratet, 2019).  
From all registered accidents in Denmark, there are no figures on how many of these accidents are 
caused or related to road anger. Road anger is in general not something that there are figures on in 
other countries, partly because as described earlier, it is difficult to unbiased define road anger across

countries, ages, gender, etc. as it is partly determined by the situation or reaction. However, in Canada, 
a study by Wickens, et al. (2016), have analyzed if road anger is an accident factor.  
Wickens, et al. (2016) collected data through many years with a connection to an ongoing cross-
sectional survey of randomly selected adults, from 18 years and over in Ontario, Canada. In the period 
from 2002-2009, the data included a total of 12,830 adults who had driven within the past years. From 
the participants, they found that 1/3 had engaged in a form of aggression while driving. The results 
showed that 30.9% had exhibited in “less severe” aggression, 0.3% had exhibited in “severe 
aggression” and 1.5% had exhibited both. The study further found those road users that exhibit 
aggression while driving is more likely to be involved in traffic accidents than road users that do not 
exhibit aggression while driving. They also found that road users that exhibit less severe forms of 
aggression, e.g. making gestures and shouting, are more likely to be involved in traffic accidents. 
Therefor road anger may be an important research area, as it can be expensive not to, and because 
knowledge in this area is not well known in Denmark.

2.3 ACCIDENTS AND GENDER 
Of the 171 registered fatalities from 2018, 122 were men and 49 were women which correspond to 
approximately 2.5 times more men lost their lives on the Danish road network. The Danish Road 
Directorate, (Vejdirektoratet, 2016) has investigated the involvement of men and women in road 
traffic accidents per driven kilometer. From this, it is found that men contribute to 62% of the fatalities 
and injuries on the Danish road network. Moreover, the number of fatalities in the age group from 
18-24 years were lower than the previous years, the group is still the one with the highest number of 
fatalities and injuries of all the age groups. This can be seen in Figure 1, where the number of fatalities 
and injuries is shown distributed by age. From Figure 1, a peak can be seen for both men and women 
in the age group 18-24, where men have a peak of 60 persons and women have a peak of around 42 
persons.  To understand why this tendency might be, the difference in gender travel behavior is to be 
clarified.

2.4 GENDER DIFFERENCES FROM OTHER RESEARCH 
As road anger and how it can relate to accidents and the differences between genders in accidents are 
described, a further investigation of what other research has found on gender differences between 
men and women is to be sought out. Three studies have been found, where gender differences have 
been analyzed regarding road anger in the countries Spain, New Zealand, and a combined analysis 
from Finland, UK, and Holland. Findings from these studies and their results are then to be described 
and further used in the discussion to see if similarities are found between results from this thesis and 
their studies. A study by González-Iglesias, et al. (2012) investigated gender differences in both 
driving anger and traffic violations. Their study consisted of survey data collected in Spain, with a 
sample of 541 drivers. The results from the analysis showed that women had significantly higher 
scores on traffic obstructions, where men had a significantly higher score on police presence. The 
study also looked at answers regarding hostile gestures, illegal driving situations, slow driving, and 
discourtesy and found that there was a similarity between men and women. Furthermore, a study 
Sullman (2015) investigated driving anger expression among drivers of New Zealand, with the use 
of Factor analysis. From the Factor analysis, the study found a 3-factor solution containing, verbal 
aggressive expression, use of vehicle, and adaptive constructive behaviors. The results showed that 
men were more likely to exhibit verbal aggressive expression and to use a vehicle to express their 
anger while driving. The results furthermore found that women were more likely to be constructive 
or adaptive in road anger episodes. Another research investigated anger and aggression among drivers 
in three European countries (Finland, UK, and Holland), (Parker, et al., 2002). The data was collected 
through a survey. The researchers made a Factor analysis to reduce the data related to driving 
behaviors and driving anger, where a 5-factor solution was obtained. The factors included (progress 
impeded, reckless driving, direct hostility, inconsiderate driving, and impatient driving). The study’s 
focus was not on gender differences, however, analyzed the differences between men and women 
from the whole dataset. This showed that men would react more to impatient driving, impeded 
progress, and inconsiderate driving.   
The highlights from the background information are therefore as follows; That road anger is 
increasing in Denmark and contributes to a safety risk. Moreover, it is found that people who engage 
in road anger are more likely to be involved in a road traffic accident. Additionally, it is highlighted 
that men are on more occasions involved in road traffic accidents than women. Differences are also 
seen between men's and women’s travel pattern, mode of transport, and their driven kilometer. 
Moreover, differences regarding road anger are found from previous studies, from other countries.

3 METHODS 
The methods of the data collection, where it was conducted, and whom it was distributed to are

3.1 PROCEDURE AND PARTICIPANTS 
This section includes a short description of how the data was collected and a demographic description 
of the participants.  
The collection of the data was done by the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), through an 
online survey-based website in connection to the RELAX-project (REduce the Level of road Anger 
eXpression). The survey was made to assess the respondent's self-reported measures which were 
related to driving anger expression, driving anger situations, driving behavior, and personality. The 
survey was conducted in the years from 2018 – 2020 and was distributed through relevant 
stakeholders as The Road Safety Council, The Federation of Danish Motorists, and The Danish 
Instructor organization. From the survey, the total number of 3,986 respondents aged between 18 and 
89 years (mean age: 49 years and SD: 16.17) from Denmark answered the survey.   
The project's objective was to develop a cognitive-behavioral intervention to obtain a lesser hostile 
interpretation of other road users’ behavior while driving on the Danish road network.  
The student did not participate in the data collection.  
3.2 MEASURES/MATERIAL 
The survey consists of 108 questions to gather information about demographics, driving behavior, 
and anger expression and experience. Four of the 108 questions are the foundation of the data used 
in this project.    
To understand what makes people angry while driving, the respondents were asked 10 closed-ended 
questions: (1) I am tired, in a bad mood, (2) in a hurry, (3) when there is congestion, (4) roadwork, 
(5) passengers in the car, (6) I am stressed, (7) bad weather (rain/snow), (8) it’s very warm, (9) I am 
hungry/thirsty, (10) other. The 10 questions were assessed on a five-point Likert scale (“Strongly 
disagree” – “Strongly agree”). Furthermore, an open-ended question was asked, so the respondents 
could elaborate in the written text on what makes them angry while driving.  
==PAGE:18==
 
Master thesis 
 
17 
 
To understand how long people, think about episodes regarding road anger while driving, the 
respondents were asked “How long do you typically think of the episode, when other road users show 
aggression towards you (e.g. verbally, with gestures or with their vehicle)?”. From the survey the 
respondents had the opportunity to answer one of the following: (1) Not at all, (2) Shortly after the 
episode, (3) Until the current trip is over, (4) Part of the day, (5) The rest of the day, (6) For several 
days, (7) Have not experienced/don’t know. If the respondents, would want to elaborate on their 
answer, situation, or if they would give another answer than the standardized options, they could do 
so in the next question through an open-ended question.  
To further understand how people, experience and perceive road anger, the respondents were asked 
the question regarding driving anger expression (DAX) “Everyone feels angry or furious from time 
to time when driving a car, but there is a difference in how people react. In the following, several 
statements that others have used to describe their reaction are presented”. The respondents were 
further asked to mark the answer that best indicates how they react in a situation where road anger is 
involved. In the mentioned question, 16 underlying statements were represented to the respondents, 
these questions can be seen in section 4.4.1, Table 27. The 16 questions were assessed on a five-point 
Likert scale (“never” – “always”).         
The difference between men and women’s involvement in road anger episodes were to be further 
investigated. To understand how people were involved in road anger episodes as aggressors and 
victims. The respondents were asked to answer 10 questions. 5 questions regarding being a victim 
and 5 questions regarding being an aggressor in road anger. The respondents had the opportunity to 
either answer “Yes” or “No”. The questions can be seen in section 4.4 in Table 25 and Table 26.

3.3.1 Phenomenology  
Through qualitative research, phenomenology is used to understand social phenomena from the 
respondent’s perspective and how they describe the world as it’s experienced. From this assumption, 
the correct and real reality is as the people perceive it (Norlyk & Martinsen, 2008). Phenomenology 
can then be used to get a more in-depth understanding of people's experience, by investigating 
different perceptions and ideas, which usually is attributed to a phenomenon in the lifeworld, in which 
we live in.  The lifeworld is to be understood as the everyday world in which we live our lives without 
further reflections. In the lifeworld, people share their surroundings, language, and meaning, and this 
is where experiences are made (Martinsen & Norlyk, 2011).  This world is filled with meaning and 
significance which is silent, and this meaning can be found with the use of phenomenology 
(Martinsen & Norlyk, 2011). By using phenomenology as an approach to the investigation of the 
phenomenon of road anger, it can be further described. Phenomenology is used to better understand 
the answers of the respondent’s open-ended answers from the survey. In the analysis, what is right or 
wrong is not to be decided but a nuanced picture of reality perceived and experienced by drivers, and 
what makes them angry in traffic is to be sought out.  
3.3.2 Hermeneutics  
Hermeneutics is the science of text interpretation, where the purpose is to obtain a valid and common 
understanding of texts. Throughout the last many years, it has not only been text but also 
experience/actions, meaning the interpretation of an experience/action can be done. The starting point 
in hermeneutics is that human actions can be described, by finding and understanding why the 
person/persons did these actions. To do the interpretation, one uses his/her interpretation, and this can 
go on forever. When one has read a text and made interpretations, the same person can start over, to 
get more insight from the text/action. This is called the hermeneutic spiral, see Figure 4 (Hegner, 
2020). The student did not use this approach directly on the respondent’s answers. However, it is 
mentioned to understand, if another person would analyze the data, he/she could have a different 
interpretation.

3.3.3 Qualitative research  
Qualitative research aims to understand individuals and how they see the world and what kind of 
relations means something to the individual (Aspers & Corte, 2019), (Hoffmann, 2013).  To obtain 
this information, methods such as participant observations, semi-structured interviews, interpretive 
textual analyzes, and open-ended questionnaires are often used. Through these approaches, the 
researcher is to be open-minded to the subject that is being investigated. By using these methods, the 
respondents can give a nuanced description of their perception and experience of the phenomenon. 
The qualitative research then describes phenomenon’s with words and not with numerical values as 
quantitative research does (Robson & McCartan, 2017).   
In research such as this project where the open-ended questions are represented to the respondents in 
the survey, the student has chosen to use qualitative research to obtain knowledge about why and how 
the respondent’s perception and experience any form of road anger.

3.3.4 Inductive approach  
In Qualitative research the method most often used is inductive and interpretive. When researchers 
use the qualitative method it’s possible to get a strong internal validity, but generalizability is often 
lower than seen in quantitative research (AU, 2020). In this research, the inductive approach implies 
a search for different patterns from open-ended questions and a development of the explanation from 
the respondents, to give a nuanced picture of the experience of the phenomenon road anger. In open-
ended questions, the respondents are given the possibility to elaborate on a question with their own 
words and not given the opportunity to give closed answers e.g. “yes” or “no”.

Thematic coding is described by Gibbs (2007) and Robson & McCartan (2017) as “how you define 
what the data you are analyzing is about”. The coding entails that one or more sections from the text 
(answer) are identified and thereby given a code to obtain the same general idea. These sections are 
known as codes/categories and when two sections from different answers are about the same, they 
are given an identical code. When all answers have been given a code, they are then grouped to a 
theme, where the themes should express something important to the research question.  
The qualitative data analysis, thematic coding was used since the open-ended questions from the

3.3.6 Quantitative research  
Quantitative research is a method often used in surveys, where the aim is to get information about the 
population through different standardized questions. By using the quantitative method in a survey 
where the respondents fill out the questions themselves, it’s possible to quantify and measure their 
answers. Different from qualitative research where the data is collected by using interviews, focus 
groups, and open-ended questions. The meaning and content of answers from qualitative research are 
investigated and not quantified (Robson & McCartan, 2017).    
3.3.7 Deductive approach  
In quantitative research the method most often used is deductive and testing hypothesis. By doing so 
the researcher has a hypothesis he/she wants to test, and this is usually the case in survey studies 
where the researcher would like to test e.g. x leads to y.  This method can give the researcher strong 
extern validity and is often better than the qualitative method in getting generalizability (AU, 2020). 
This is possible as in quantitative research a larger target group can be met and thereby more 
responses than in qualitative research. As the questions are often standardized, elaboration, and 
nuance of the respondent’s answers are missing, and thereby the causality can be difficult to 
determine. Where causality is the generation and determination of one phenomenon by another 
(Spirkin, 1983). 
3.3.8 Frequency analysis  
Frequency analysis is used to analyze the frequency or number of times an event occurs. In this thesis, 
this is used to make the descriptive statistics and furthermore to investigate the frequency of answers 
in questions related to road anger experience and perception.  
3.3.9 Cross-table analysis  
To investigate the difference between men and women’s answers in the survey, a cross-table analysis 
was used. This is a quantitative method often used to analyze the relationship between two or more 
variables. Cross-table makes it easy to analyze and compare the result of several variables, with other 
variables. In this thesis, the students, investigated the difference between men and women 
respondents on several questions, such as, what makes people angry while driving and how long they 
think about an episode after it happened. The cross-table analysis gave a clear overview of the 
respondent’s answers, and by doing so, the student would be able to see if there was any difference 
in the way men and women perceive and experience road anger.

3.3.10 Principal component analysis 
The principal component analysis (PCA) with Oblim rotation and Kaiser normalization is used as a 
dimensionality reduction method in this thesis. As the dataset is large, the method is used to reduce 
the dimensionality of this dataset. To do so, the PCA transforms a set of variables into fewer variables, 
were these would still include a large amount of the information from the transformed variables. 
While reducing the number of variables from the data, the accuracy of the new variables, would not 
be as precise as all the variables, but making the data set smaller it is in most occasions easier to 
analyze (Jaadi, 2019).   
To do the calculation of the abovementioned, the components of the PCA are the directions of the 
data where there is most variance, or where the data is most spread out. To investigate where the data 
has the most variance, eigenvalues and eigenvectors were calculated. Eigenvalues are the total amount 
of variance that the principal component can explain. In this thesis, eigenvalues above 1 are used to 
find the principal components of the data. Eigenvectors are the weight of each eigenvalue and are 
used to calculate the component loading by the following formula;  
(𝑒𝑖𝑔𝑒𝑛𝑣𝑒𝑐𝑡𝑜𝑟) ∗√𝑒𝑖𝑔𝑒𝑛𝑣𝑎𝑙𝑢𝑒= 𝑐𝑜𝑚𝑝𝑜𝑛𝑒𝑛𝑡 𝑙𝑜𝑎𝑑𝑖𝑛𝑔 
The component loading can then be interpreted as the correlation of each item with a principal 
component and from this can be used to calculate the variance for each principal component (UCLA, 
2020). 
As mentioned, the eigenvalue is the total amount of variance that the principal component can explain. 
The first principal component will explain most of the variance, where the variance of the next 
components is obtained by dividing the first component, and so on. By doing so, the first will explain 
the most and the last component will explain the least of the variance. From the total variance 
explained and from a scree plot, it was then possible to determine how many components were needed 
to explain the data.  After the number of components was determined, a pattern matrix was calculated, 
to see which questions could be connected into new variables (UCLA, 2020).    
As a further investigation of the internal consistency in the newly obtained principal components or 
factors, the Cronbach’s alpha was calculated. So, to determine how closely the questions are as a

4.3 HOW LONG DO PEOPLE THINK ABOUT AN EPISODE? 
4.3.1 Standardized questions 
To investigate how long people, think about an episode, a cross-table analysis was carried out. This 
was done to see what the difference between men and women’s answers was in the standardized 
categories from the question “How long do you typically think of when other road users show 
aggression towards you (e.g. verbally, with gestures or with their vehicle)?” (q8a_effect). 
The results from the cross-table analysis can be seen in Table 22. From the results it is shown that a 
small percentage of both (6.1%) men and women (3.0%) don’t think about the episode at all, however, 
a difference of (3.1%) indicates that men could be more likely not to think about the episode. The 
largest proportion of answers are in the category “shortly after the episode”. The largest percent of 
men (56.2%) have answered that they think about the episode shortly after it happened. The same 
tendency is shown of women’s answers (44.6%), nevertheless, this category also shows the largest 
difference between men and women (11.6%), which could also indicate that a large proportion of 
men only shortly think about an episode, where women may think about an episode for a longer 
period of time. From the category “until the trip is over”, the result from men (13.7%) and women 
(14.2%) only have a small difference of 0.4%, so no large difference is found in this category. The 
category “part of the day” shows that 14% of men answered that they think about an episode part of 
the day, where 19.9% of women answered the same. This shows a difference of 5.9%, which could

5 DISCUSSION 
In the following section, results related to the hypotheses will be discussed. In addition, the methods, 
quality of data, and limitations of the thesis will be discussed  
 
The purpose of this thesis was to examine the differences between men's and women’s experiences 
and perceptions of road anger in Denmark. For this, a thematic coding analysis was conducted with 
the use of phenomenology and hermeneutics to further understand the phenomenon of road anger and 
what makes people angry while driving and how long people think about an episode. To undergo the 
analysis the respondent’s own words were used from their answers in open-ended questions. Cross-
tables were used to investigate differences in the answers between men and women on the 
standardized questions, related to what makes people angry while driving and to find out how long 
people think about an episode. Driving anger expression and the standardized questions related to 
what makes people angry while driving were both examined in a Principal component analysis, to 
see if differences between men and women were significant in the new obtained factors.  
 
The first hypothesis was as follows; women are more likely to experience road anger when 
undergoing psychological interference. The result from the study related to the standardized questions 
on what makes people angry while driving showed a significant difference between men and women 
in all questions. The PCA on the questions showed that the questions could be grouped into two 
components, personal problems, and externalities, where a significant difference between the two 
new variables was found. This showed that women are more likely to experience road anger when 
they either have personal problems or if externalities are interfering with their driving. So, the 
hypothesis is confirmed.  
This might lead to that women are more insecure while driving or do not have the same confidence 
in themselves when it comes to driving or when they are experiencing road anger scenarios and 
therefore get angry at other drivers. However, this could also be because women are more aware of 
when they are more likely to get angry while driving. Moreover, as mentioned in section 2.1, research 
from Haustein & Møller (2019) mentions that the perception of another road user's behavior can also 
be a cause of road anger. So, if women have personal problems or are interfered by externalities, their 
focus while driving may be elsewhere and because of this, they may interpret other road user’s 
behavior differently, leading to road anger in more situations.     
==PAGE:60==
 
Master thesis 
 
59 
 
Men on the other hand may not say they are more likely to get angry in situations because of personal 
problems as it is harder for men to accept some sort of phycological aspect, as this is not seen as 
masculine (Sunhedsstyrelsen, 2020).  
The second hypothesis was as follows; women are more likely to think longer about a road anger 
episode than men. The results from the standardized questions related to how long people think about 
an episode involving road anger showed a significant difference from the U-test. The results showed 
that a larger proportion of men were likely to think about the episode for a shorter period. A larger 
proportion of women on the other hand showed that they were more likely to think about the episode 
for a longer period.  
As further investigation with Thematic coding was done to figure out how long people think about 
an episode. The results showed that a large percentage of both men and women would say it depends 
on the episode. Moreover, the results showed a small difference between men and women that said 
they think about an episode after the trip has ended. These obtained results showed no significant 
difference between men and women in all the obtained themes. However, the results from the 
Thematic coding analysis are somewhat different from the results from the standardized questions. 
The differences in the results might be due to different understandings of the possible questions, as 
the respondents might answer different in the elaboration than in the standardized questions and vice 
versa. Meaning that in the standardized question, it could be that the male respondents look at more 
scenarios, thinking that in general, they do not think about road anger episodes for a long time. Where 
women on the other hand might think of a specific situation, and how long they thought about that 
specific episode.  
So, the hypothesis cannot be confirmed, as the results are different from the two methods.  
 
The third hypothesis was as follows; inattentive driving is a problem in Denmark and is related to 
aggressiveness from both men and women drivers. To investigate this problem, a thematic coding 
analysis was done on the question related to what makes people angry while driving. The results 
showed that 9% of both men and women thought this was one of the main triggers for them to get 
angry while driving. So, this category showed that it is something to be aware of in the future. As 
more and more technology enters our lives in every way, there is also seen a larger increase in what 
cars can do with this technology. The increased technology may have a larger influence on our 
concentration or the way we need to stay focused while driving. As mentioned in the introduction, 
according to (Underwood, 2005), (Rothengatter, 1991) and (Green & Senders, 2013), human factors 
==PAGE:61==
 
Master thesis 
 
60 
 
are a sole contributory factor in up to 95% of all road accidents. Therefore, even though inattentive 
driving is not one of the mentioned subjects from the respondents, it might be good to further 
investigate this in the future. The hypothesis can be confirmed, as a respectable percentage of both 
men and women say that they get angered at inattentive drivers.     
Previous studies also indicate that inattentive driving has a large influence on provoking road anger 
(Parker, et al., 2002) 
 
The fourth hypothesis was as follows; reckless driving from other road users is related to a large 
proportion of aggressiveness. To investigate the problem, the results from the thematic coding 
analysis on the question related to what makes people angry while driving was used. The results 
showed that this was the second most mentioned situation were men and women would get angry 
while driving. A total of 25% of the respondents mentioned this as a trigger to them. The results when 
comparing men and women in this theme, showed a total of 27% men and 22% women, where no 
significant differences were found between gender. Reckless and careless driving is therefore 
something that makes both men and women angry while driving. This result was expected since when 
driving on the road there is a form of flow that is needed to make it fluent and safe. When this flow 
is changed by other people who are driving reckless or careless, they are likely doing it because they 
do not think about anyone else than themselves. By doing so, other people's flow is compromised and 
therefore a large proportion of both men and women might get angry in these situations. The 
hypothesis can therefore be confirmed.  
A previous study from Parker, et al. (2002) has also found that reckless driving is a factor that induces 
road anger in general. Furthermore, a study by Holgaard, et al. (2018) found that road users on more 
occasions get angry when they experience reckless and careless driving than when another road user 
makes a mistake while driving.  
Furthermore, it is mentioned that human factors are a sole contributory factor in up to 95% of all road 
accidents (Underwood, 2005), (Green & Senders, 2013), (Rothengatter, 1991). Reckless and careless 
driving is seen as some of these human factors, and they may be a large cause of people getting angry. 
It has additionally been found that people who engage in road anger are more likely to be involved in 
a road traffic accident (Wickens, et al., 2016).

The fifth hypothesis was as follows; most people impose responsibility on other road users. To figure 
out if this was the situation, the results from the thematic coding analysis regarding how long people 
think about an episode were used. From the thematic coding analysis, it was found that a larger 
number of people either mentioned that others were to blame for the incident, or that they themselves 
were to blame. The results showed that 78% of men and 79% of women said it was the other's fault 
in the episode. So, no significant difference between genders was found, which was expected. This 
was expected, as most people are somewhat aware of what they are doing, even though inattentive 
might be present. However, when people get scared or surprised by another road user’s behavior, they 
are more likely to say that it was the other person's behavior that was a sole factor to the episode. 
Thereby, the hypothesis can be confirmed. 
Previous research has also shown that people who react more aggressively when driving are often 
blaming other road users, saying they are bad drivers (Lennon & Watson, 2011).  
 
The sixth and seven hypotheses were as follows; men are likely to act more aggressively than females 
with the intention of physical confrontation and women are more likely to experience road anger by 
verbal gestures, such as swearing and making insults. - To investigate the problem, the involvement 
and expression in road anger episodes were analyzed with the use of questions related to what people 
have experienced both as aggressor/victim and DAX. The results from involvement in road anger 
episodes as an aggressor showed a significant difference between men and women’s behavior from 
the questions “I myself have given the ´finger´ to another road user and “I myself have threatened 
another road user”. The results indicate that male drivers are more likely to act with one of the 
abovementioned behaviors than women. It was furthermore found that men were more likely to 
experience the following behaviors from another road user as a victim as these results showed a 
significant difference: “I got the given the ´finger´ from another road user”, “I have been threatened 
by another road user” and “My vehicle has been hit or kicked on”. From this, the hypothesis can be 
partially confirmed.  
The results from the Principal component analysis on DAX showed that a 4-factor solution was 
suitable to explain these questions. The factors obtained were (Constructive behavior, Physical 
confrontation, verbal gestures, and use of vehicle) to express anger. The found factors have been 
found suitable to explain DAX by previous studies from (González-Iglesias, et al., 2012) and (Møller 
& Haustein, 2017). The results from the principal component analysis showed significant differences 
between genders in constructive behavior and use of vehicle. So, women were more likely to express

themselves constructively when experiencing road anger where men were more likely to express 
themselves with the use of a vehicle. A previous study from (Sullman, 2015) has also found this to 
be the case. The results furthermore showed no significant difference between men and women when 
analyzing physical confrontation and verbal gestures from the Principal component analysis. Another 
previous study has found different results (Møller & Haustein, 2017). The results from this study 
indicate that men are more prone to physical aggression than women. However, the results 
furthermore showed that women were more likely to express road anger in a constructive way. 
Moreover, from verbal gestures, no significant differences were found between men and women. So, 
from this, the hypothesis can not be confirmed, as there is seen no difference between men and women 
in physical confrontation and verbal gestures.  
5.1 DISCUSSION OF METHODS 
In the following section, the methods used for the thesis will be discussed as well as the pros and cons 
of each method. Verification of the thesis is to be done to determine the validity, reliability of the 
project. 
Validity refers to whether a method examines what it aims to examine and obtains valid data that can 
answer the project's objectives and hypotheses. The credibility of the students is crucial for validity. 
To determine the validity, possible sources of errors are investigated. Here, the student will always 
be a valid source of error. The validity can be increased by performing the examination procedure 
with high transparency throughout the process (Robson & McCartan, 2017). The hypothesizes have 
been useful to enlighten road anger and the differences between men and women. From the Thematic 
coding analysis, some of the respondent’s answers were somewhat hard to interpret. This may be due 
to the students’ understanding of what they were trying to say, or that the respondents did not fully 
understand the questions or if the question could be understood in different ways. As mentioned, the 
student can be a valid source of error. This may be due to the student having a different 
preunderstanding of the respondent’s answers than they themselves will have. The student has also 
made the categories and themes for the Thematic coding analysis, by reading all answers from the 
respondents. Some answers may have been easier to understand if the respondents were interviewed 
instead of them given written answers in the survey. However, the number of respondents would not 
have been as good if this was to be done. So, this could be something that could be done in future 
research, to see if the same answers would be obtained.  
==PAGE:64==
 
Master thesis 
 
63 
 
Reliability is about how consistent and reliable the results are. High reliability is achieved if another 
person can make the same study and find the same tendencies and results (Robson & McCartan, 
2017). The reliability of the project is expected to be medium as the qualitative data is more reliable 
than the qualitative data obtained. However, the elaboration of the respondent's answers from 
Appendix 10.1 and 10.2, would make a better understanding of what lies behind every answer from 
the students' interpretation in the Thematic coding analysis. The process of the Thematic coding 
process is also further described in section 3.3.5, to make it more interpretable from another 
researcher to undergo the same process if needed again. Additionally, the reliability could be 
increased if two persons have coded the data independently of each other. However, this was not a 
possibility in this project.   
5.1.1 Pros and cons for Thematic coding analysis  
The qualitative research method Thematic coding analysis is flexible and allows the researcher to 
engage in this type of analysis. Moreover, the method is good in analyzing a large dataset, with more 
ease, by being able to sort into themes (Robson & McCartan, 2017). As mentioned in section 5.1, 
reliability way be a concern due to the understanding and interpretation of the data (answers), where 
the student can be a source of error.  Moreover, in the analysis, there is some risk of missing nuances 
of the dataset (Robson & McCartan, 2017). So, the analysis is somewhat subjective and depends on 
the student’s decision, meaning that reflections throughout the process with interpretation and choices 
made, needs to be reflected carefully.  
5.1.2 Pros and cons for Principal component analysis 
The Principal component analysis is a good method when the researcher has many variables, that can 
be hard to visualize (i2tutorials, 2020). The analysis helps to find a correlation between variables in 
a more efficient way than doing it by hand. Additionally, after the analysis, the obtained components 
are independent (no correlation) of each other (i2tutorials, 2020). Nevertheless, by using the analysis, 
new components are obtained. By doing so, the new components may become less interpretable than 
the first variables. Furthermore, information can be lost during the analysis even though the 
components try to describe the maximum variance of the data. So, the obtained components do not 
describe as much variance as the first variables.

6 CONCLUSION AND FURTHER WORK 
The purpose of the thesis was to examine the difference between men's and women’s experience and 
perception of road anger on the Danish road network. By using both qualitative and quantitative 
methods to investigate the differences in road anger differences between men and women were found. 
It was found that there was a difference between men and women when it comes to being interfered 
by either personal problems or externalities. From this, it was found that women were more likely to 
be affected by these scenarios and therefore experience road anger when some of these scenarios are 
present. Furthermore, it was found that women were more likely to think longer about an episode 
involving road anger. Inattentive driving was not found to be a large problem for both men and 
women, but this could be a good thing to implement in a survey in the future. Reckless driving is one 
of the factors that cause road anger between men and women, however, if other road users infringe a 
traffic law, most people experience road anger. Additionally, it was found that from men and women 
there was no significant difference in whom they impose the responsibility. However, most men and 
women impose responsibility on other road users and blaming them for the episode. It was 
furthermore found that men were more likely to give the ´finger´ or threaten another road user and on 
more occasions experience the same behavior from another road user. Nonetheless, it was additionally 
found that there was no significant difference between men and women when it comes to physical 
confrontation and the use of verbal gestures. Significant differences from constructive behavior and 
use of vehicle were found from the Principal component analysis. This would indicate that women 
are more likely to act in a constructive way than men, where men on more occasions will use their 
vehicle to express road anger towards another road user.  
From the thesis, it was then concluded that women were more likely to get angry when they either 
have personal problems or if externalities are interfering with their driving. Additionally, differences 
were found in men's and women’s expression and involvement in road anger episodes. Moreover, 
similarities between men and women were found regarding how long people think about a road anger 
episode, the behavior of other road users, that makes them angry, and that they impose responsibility 
on other road users and not themselves.  
As shown, differences between men and women have been found in some scenarios, however, it 
seems that both men and women get angered at the same behaviors from other road users in Denmark. 
However, similarities in scenarios are found, which would indicate that the difference between men 
and women and how they experience and perceive is not totally different. Therefor campaigns or 
movements to mitigate road anger, can be focused on the population and not on gender. Campaigns 
==PAGE:67==
 
Master thesis 
 
66 
 
such as “Brug 2 sek. mere” translated “Use 2 sec. more”, which are focused on both men and women 
in intersections, could give inspiration to another campaign regarding road anger. The campaign could 
be something as “Giv plads og respekter andre” translated “Make way and respect others”.   
Additionally, it is recommended in a future survey, that more standardized questions related to what 
behaviors make people angry from other road users. Moreover, this could be supplemented with 
observations from real-life scenarios while either driving with participants or if there is an opportunity 
to observe the participants through video or record of sound. Even though there might be some bias 
from these kinds of observations, this could give more insight into how men and women experience 
or perceive road anger.

10.2 ELABORATION OF CATEGORIES (HOW LONG DO PEOPLE THINK ABOUT AN 
EPISODE?) 
1. Depends on the situation:  
a. This is chosen when the respondents answer that it can depend on the situation or 
mention two different scenarios, where one can be a minor episode and the other can 
be a scary episode.  
b. E.g. “Det kommer an på situationen - fagter rører mig ikke så meget, men da en bilist 
hang med hoved og halvdelen af overkroppen ude af vinduet og råbte og fagtede over 
en strækning på 200 m, tog det et par dage” Translated ” It depends on the situation - 
gestures don't touch me that much, but when a motorist hung his head and with half 
the upper body out of the window and shouted and fenced over a distance of 200 m, it 
took a few days” 
2. Shortly after the episode: 
a. This is chosen when the respondents answer that it, they only think about the episode 
for a short or shorter period of time after.  
b. E.g.” Meget kortvarigt, men det er langt siden noen har vist agressivitet” translated 
“Very short-lived, but it's been a long time since anyone has shown aggressiveness” 
3. Longer time after the episode: 
a. This is chosen when the respondents answer that they can think about the episode for 
years, or they get reminded of an episode when they drive on the same road as where 
the episode happened.  
b. E.g. “Kan sagtens tænke over situationen flere gange i løbet af dagen, men mest i 
forhold til om jeg gjorde noget forkert” translated” Can easily think about the 
situation several times during the day, but mostly in relation to whether I did 
something wrong” 
4. When the trip ends: 
a. This is chosen when the respondents answer that they think about the episode until 
they arrive at their destination.

b. E.g. “Som regel til turen er slut eller noget andet fanger min opmærksomhed,

hours each way” 
5. Thinks about the episode for years 
a. This is chosen when the respondents answer that they are thinking about an episode
