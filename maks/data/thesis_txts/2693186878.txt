Abstract
The present report aims at comparing ’¼éoating wind turbines related softwares : AQWA and Orcawave for the
hydrodynamic database of the ’¼éoater, Orca’¼éex 11.1.a and a coupling between Orca’¼éex 11.1a and OpenFAST
for the aerodyanmic and the structural dynamics. When these ’¼ürsts calculate the hydrostatic coef’¼ücients,
damping, added mass coef’¼ücients and other hydrodynamic characteristics for various wave periods and wave
directions, the seconds solve the equations of motion of the whole system, including aero-hydro-servo-elastic
response.
In the assessment of the hydrodynamic databases, input parameters such as damping lid coef’¼ücient were
changed according to different de’¼ünitions, while results have been translated so they were outputted at the
same geometrical point. Good matching was found between AQWA and Orcawave results, with noticeable
difference in the drift loads comparison.
A Floating wind turbine model has been built in order to perform the comparison. It was composed of the
University of Maine wind turbineŌĆÖs RNA and its controller, and in-house mooring lines / ’¼éoater / transition
piece and tower, which required modi’¼ücations to ’¼üt to the UMaine RNA.
A simpler model, disregarding the ’¼éoating aspect through an onshore con’¼üguration, was ’¼ürst studied, with a
comparison of steady wind simulation from cut-in wind speed to 50 msŌłÆ1 with production state from cut-in
wind speed to cut-out wind speed and idling state onward. The comparison between softwares of steady state
mean loads and wind turbine related channels was conclusive. Simulations involving turbulent wind speed
with the same range were also run on the same model, with extrema and standard deviation also compared. It
again led to similar results between Orca’¼éex 11.1.a and OpenFAST.
Finally, a complete model with the ’¼éoater was used to perform the comparison through simulations with
environment on waves and / or wind turbine conditions. For the simulations of the global system under wind
only environment (steady and turbulent), the comparison was good for the mean of the physical quantities
related to the wind turbine blades and tower, but with some difference on the minimum and maximum of those
quantities. The environmental conditions accounting for waves only (regular / irregular ) with a very good
match. Finally, the coupled wind waves cases were studied, with non matching wind turbine behavior due to
turbulent wind ’¼üles that were not prepared correctly, but the comparison of the ’¼éoater motion between FOF
and OF was satisfying, as the wind is thought not to be mostly driving the ’¼éoater motion with an extreme
production load case.
Consequently, for the ’¼ürst step of the BW-ideol validation of the wind turbine model in Orca’¼éex,the results
are positive.

Introduction
The present report covers topics from structural dynamics, hydrodynamics and servo-aeroelasticity applied on
’¼éoating wind turbine through a validation of the speci’¼üc features available from Orca’¼éex 10 release and a
recent software developed by Orcina as well named Orcawave.
The ’¼ürst part of this reports deals with the hydrodynamic database of a ’¼éoater created from a mesh ’¼üle and other
inputs. The hydrodynamic database is composed of primary hydrodynamic parameters required to undertake
complex motions and response analysis when implemented in a software simulating equations of motions for
a ’¼éoating wind turbine. On this ’¼üeld of the ’¼éoating wind turbine will be compared the software currently used
by Ideol, Ansys AQWA (called AQWA for simplicity purpose in the report), to Orcawave.
Secondly, time domain simulations of ’¼éoating wind turbines will be studied, on one side with the current
IdeolŌĆÖs methodology, using the coupling between Fast and Orca’¼éex, with Orca’¼éex modeling the hydrodynamics
and forces applied to the ’¼éoater and mooring lines and Fast modeling the behavior of the wind turbine
and acting as the master of the simulation. Orca’¼éex is based on the ’¼ünite elements method and forms an
integrated software solution to perform in-place and installation analyses of a wide range of offshore structures
including ’¼éexible and steel risers, umbilical risers, pipelines, ’¼éoating hoses and mooring lines. FAST (Fatigue,
Aerodynamics, Structures and Turbulence) is a Code developed by National Renewable Energy Laboratory,
which is a comprehensive aeroelastic simulator capable of predicting both the extreme and fatigue loads
of two or three-bladed horizontale-axis wind turbines. It employs a combined modal multibody dynamics
formulation, accounting for 24 degrees of freedom, for blade, rotor and nacelle motion, tower motion and
plateform motion. The software that was compared to this coupled softwares is Orca’¼éex from release 10 it
is now possible to model a wind turbine, presumably solving the similar equations as in FAST. In the release
11.1.a of Orca’¼éex, the whole ’¼éoating wind turbine can be modelled.
allowing to calculate a hydrodynamic data base, with diffraction radiation calculations, on offshore structures
; and the possibility to model a wind turbine directly in Orca’¼éex. The tools subjected to evaluation will
be compared against the current IdeolŌĆÖs methodology in the modeling of the wind turbine, namely AQWA
and Fast Orca’¼éex 9.7. AQWA is the software used for the calculation of the hydrodynamic database of the
’¼éoater, whilst the coupling of FAST and Orca’¼éex (FOF) (old release : v9.7) is used in solving the coupled
aero-hydro-servo-elastic equations of motion of the ’¼éoating wind turbine. The way Fast-Orca’¼éex works is that
OpenFAST is the master of the simulations launched, solving equations of motion, taking into account Orca’¼éex
output at each time step. The method based on coupled softwares has been validated against measurements in
the case of the Floatgen demonstrator.
As wind turbines are becoming bigger and bigger, the subject of the comparison has been chosen to be a 15

OpenFAST
The FAST (Fatigue, Aerodynamics, Structures and Turbulence) Code developed by National Renewable

In this coupling, OpenFAST models the wind turbine aerodynamic loads and structural dynamics, the turbine

ŌĆō OpenFAST transmits the ’¼éoater position and velocity to Orca’¼éex;
ŌĆō Orca’¼éex calculates line tensions, hydrodynamic loads (excluding added mass forces) and added mass

ŌĆō Orca’¼éex transmits to OpenFAST the resulting loads and added mass matrix;

7.2.1
Unsteady aerodynamics
One feature that is not currently available in Orca’¼éex and is usually used by OpenFAST is the unsteady
aerodynamics (UA), also called dynamic stall. This effect represents the unsteady ’¼éow passing around an
blade section whose in’¼éow angle is not constant. Essentially, the aerodynamics coef’¼ücient : lift Cl and drag
Cd, instead of being strictly dependant on the angle of attack, are also dependant on the ’¼éow around the blade
section. Lift and drag coef’¼ücient are in hysteresis state, when the angle of attack changes.

Conclusion
The ’¼ürst part of benchmark has been done between the softwares Orcawave with AQWA used for the calculation
of the hydrodynamic coef’¼ücients of the ’¼éoating foundation (hydrodynamic database). The results obtained
with the two software are very similar, in terms of the added mass, the radiation damping and the load
RAOs (’¼ürst order wave induced loads), with some differences around the critical period of the ’¼éoater on
the last physical quantity. Some more evident difference has been found in terms of second order wave
loads (drift forces) essentially due to the presence of large peak in Orcawave results, that seems to have no
physical explanation (probably a numerical irregular frequency). Further investigations would be necessary
to understand these differences on drift force results and in particular the presence of this peak in Orcawave
results. A preliminary step for the comparison of hydrodynamic databases was the calibration of the damping
lid coef’¼ücient, which de’¼ünition between AQWA and Orcawave is different.
Concerning the wind turbine response analysis, the ’¼ürst step was running simulations, in OpenFAST and
Orca’¼éex, necessary to create a generic model, the BW-Ideol 15 MW, by adjusting the tower structure to
comply with offshore constraints, de’¼üning a prebend shape for the blades in Orca’¼éex and ensure that the tower
mode frequency of the ’¼éoating wind turbine would lay far from the 3P region of the rotor. The generic model
was ’¼ürst used to compare OpenFAST with Orca’¼éex directly on an onshore version of the BW-Ideol 15 MW
wind turbine. Steady wind and turbulent wind cases were simulated, from which statistics of the Orca’¼éex
and OpenFAST simulations were calculated. The comparison between those was very good, except on blade
structural mode, which are accounted differently in OpenFAST and in Orca’¼éex. It is thought that controller
transition difference between wind speed region is the cause of different behavior between Orca’¼éex and
OpenFAST. However further investigations are needed to ’¼ünd reasons for differences seen around rated wind
speed. Despite those differences, the wind turbine model in Orca’¼éex was validated, based on the OpenFASTŌĆÖs.
In the last part of this work the behavior of the wind turbine in ’¼éoating condition has been analyzed throughout
numerical aero-hydro-servo-elastic coupled analysis. First static and decay tests have been performed. It was
found that the hydrostatic behavior of the ’¼éoater were very similar in FOF and in OF. However, some difference
on the damping for the heave, roll and pitch degrees of freedoms have been observed from the decay tests. Then
the steady and turbulent wind load cases were simulated without waves. Results were still matching between
FOF and Orca’¼éex, even though differences were slightly increased compared to the onshore model case. Then
the regular and irregular waves load cases were simulated without wind. The ’¼éoater behavior between FOF
and OF was very similar with differences on motion of the ’¼éoater not larger then 2% for all tested load cases,
leading to validation of the ’¼éoating wind model for non-concomitant wind/waves environmental conditions.
Finally, extreme (in terms of wave height) production load cases were run and compared between FOF and
OF, but due to different turbulent wind ’¼üle (time history) between the softwares, only the ’¼éoater motion was
examined based on the assumption that their statistical values are weekly in’¼éuenced by the difference on the
wind time history .
This work has been the ’¼ürst important step for the validation of FWT Orca’¼éex fully coupled model, that
allows to replace Fast-Orca’¼éex coupled model with a simpler model with clear advantages in terms of model
development and postprocessing of results. Additional investigations are still necessary to fully understand
some differences between the results obtained by using the two coupling analysis tools.
