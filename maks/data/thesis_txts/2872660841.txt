Abstract
The thesis investigates wind loads on a solar tracker array at the Hørsholm solar field of
Heliac A/S using Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD), comparing Reynolds-Averaged
Navier-Stokes (RANS) and transient Large Eddy Simulation (LES) turbulence models.
The methodology employed involves a detailed geometry and mesh generation for con-
figurations specified by Heliac, numerical investigations using the realizable k −" RANS
model, a transient LES approach, and validation against experimental wind data that were
collected from the Hørsholm solar field.
Results demonstrated that LES simulations provided higher accuracy in capturing com-
plex turbulent wakes but at an increased computational cost when compared with the
RANS simulations which achieved sufficient accuracy while requiring less computational
resources. Finally, a hybrid CFD approach is suggested as optimal for a more resilient,
cost-effective and efficient solar field design, ensuring structural and operational reliability.

Introduction
In the face of the rapidly changing ecosystem caused by climate change, there is an urgent
call to develop sustainable energy solutions that minimize the use of fossil fuels and carbon
footprint while also reducing production costs in order to increase accessibility and align
with the United Nations’ Sustainable Development Goals. According to Ember’s European
Electricity Review which aims to track the progress towards clean energy, in 2022 wind
and solar power generated a record high of 22% of the EU’s electricity, surpassing both
coal power (16%) and, for the first time, fossil gas-generated electricity (20%) [1].

1.1
Literature Review
M.Mier-Torrecilla et al. emphasize that Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) has emerged
as a highly effective tool for determining wind load distributions over solar collectors with
great accuracy. The ability to accurately assess these loads enables further structural
optimization, leading to increased reliability and decreased production costs. The study
utilized CFD analysis to establish a virtual wind tunnel and calculate the three-dimensional
flow around a scaled module, along with the resultant load coefficients. The numerical
results were validated with experimental data in a physical wind tunnel. Based on their
findings, the study concludes that CFD is a suitable method for computing wind loads
around solar collectors. The relative mean errors of the numerical results were found to
be within 10% with respect to the reference experimental data, which is of the same order
as experimental uncertainty [2].
Ghanadi et al.in ”Numerical Investigation of Wind Loads on an Operating Heliostat”[3],
investigate the static and dynamic forces from the upstream buffeting flow and downstream
vortex shedding that solar collectors are subjected to due to the fact that they operate in
the atmospheric boundary layer (ABL) by using transient turbulence models.The analysis
focuses on simulating a single square shape flat-plate solar collector. They conclude
that the accuracy of the resolved drag coefficients with respect to experimental data (by
Peterka et al. [4]) presented in the study proves that using transient turbulent models
(LES) can be a good and accurate practice in order to estimate loads that occur under
high turbulence levels within the ABL.
In ”Aerodynamic Analysis of Wind Loading on Parabolic Trough Collectors Using High-
Fidelity CFD Modeling”, Yellapantula et al. [5] focus on investigating the effects of wind
loads acting on single and six-row configurations of parabolic through collectors by using
Large Eddy Simulations (LES) whose results are then compared with experimental wind
tunnel data. The research concludes that these simulations highlight the need for high-
fidelity unsteady calculations in order to provide key design considerations for parabolic
troughs to improve their reliability.
Joachim Paetzold, Steve Cochard, David F. Fletcher, and Anthony Vassallo have con-
ducted a series of studies that aim to simulate wind loads and their impact on parabolic

effect of wind loads on such arrays could offer valuable insights both academically and
industrially, further advancing our understanding of wind load dynamics and facilitating the
optimization of solar plant design.

2.1
Basic Conservation Principles of Fluid Mechanics
The conservation law for an extensive property relates the rate of change of the amount of
that property in a given control mass to externally determined effects [8]. Fluid flow gov-
erning equations represent mathematical statements of the conservation laws of physics.
These could be simplistically described as:
• A fluid’s mass is conserved
• The sum of forces in a fluid particle are equal to the momentum rate of change
(Newton’s second law)
• The rate of energy change in a fluid particle is equal to the sum of the heat and work
rates done to the specific particle (Thermodynamics’ first law)
It is important to highlight that the fluid is regarded as a continuum and the fluid’s behaviour
is being examined on a macroscopic level where the molecular structure and the molecular
motions are ignored. The properties examined include velocity, pressure, density and
temperature as space and time functions. These properties can be considered as averages
in terms of larger molecule quantities. Additionally, one can define as a fluid particle or point
in a fluid the smallest possible element whose macroscopic properties are not influenced
by individual molecules [9].

2.3
Turbulence
Understanding the fluid flow behaviour becomes critical when analyzing its effect in the
design and simulation of fluid-dependent systems. As for an accurate fluid modeling in
computational fluid dynamics (CFD), a deeper understanding of flow paths and velocities
within the systems is required. Fluid flow within a system can be either laminar or turbulent.
The attributes of each flow type are different, and therefore understanding of each is
essential.
Figure 2.1: Laminar and turbulent flow in a pipe [10]
Laminar flows are characterized by a smooth and streamlined nature of fluid motion as
well as by low Reynolds numbers. In a laminar flow, the properties of the fluid- including
velocity and pressure, remain constant at each point of the flow.
Turbulent flows occur at higher Reynolds numbers and are characterised by chaotic and
random flow behaviours where velocity and pressure vary with time and within different
regions of the flow. Since turbulence enhances heat and mass-transfer rates, the majority
of the flows in engineering problems are turbulent flows. Therefore turbulence modelling
is a key aspect in CFD.
Given the difficulty to precisely define the turbulence phenomenon, Tennekes and Lumley
[12] proposed certain characteristics that govern turbulence. The most important ones are
presented below:
• Irregularity
• Diffusivity
• Instability at large Reynolds numbers
• Dissipation of turbulent kinetic energy
• Continuum

The Reynolds number is defined as the ratio of inertia forces to viscous forces:

2.4
Turbulence Modeling
Due to the irregular nature of the turbulence flows and the random fluctuations of various
flow properties that were further analysed in the previous sections of this chapter, there is
a vast amount of information that needs to be analysed if one were to describe a given
turbulent flow in a complete manner. Such a detailed level of approximation most of the
time proves to be very computationally expensive and almost impossible to be utilised on
a practical engineering level.
Turbulence modeling aims to solve this problem. The governing equations are being
manipulated so that the small-scale high-frequency fluctuations are removed, which results
in a modified set of equations that is computationally less expensive to solve. This
modification in the governing equations results in additional unknown variables that require
to be determined. In summary, turbulence modeling can be described as the process of
closing the modified Navier-Stokes equations by providing required turbulence models
[13].

2.4.1.3
Large Eddy Simulation (LES) Model
The large eddy simulation (LES) model is a transient model which explicitly resolves the
large motion scales, while a sub-grid scale model is used to handle the small scales of
motion. This makes LES particularly useful for wind-related applications, where the large
motion scales are usually responsible for the forces of interest and are more problem-
dependent as they are determined by the boundary conditions and the geometries of each
respective flow. This advantage sets LES apart from RANS-based models, which tend to
struggle with complex flows featuring pronounced vortex shedding or special influences of
curvature, rotation, buoyancy or compression. [14, 15, 16].
Although LES have proven to be advantageous, they have some inherent limitations that
need to be considered. The primary constraint is the computational cost, which can be
prohibitively high due to the necessity for a finely resolved grid to accurately capture
Simulation of wind loads on a Heliac solar collector array

==PAGE:28==
the large-scale structures of the flow. Furthermore, to model the effects of small-scale
structures that are not resolved by the grid, precise subgrid-scale models are required,
adding to the complexity of the computation. The accuracy of LES also depends on the
quality of the initial and boundary conditions, which can be challenging to obtain in practical
applications. Moreover, LES may not be appropriate for certain types of flows, such as
those that contain strong shocks or highly separated flows. [17, 18]. Nevertheless, it
proves to be a useful tool for the current project and is expected to provide further insight

3.1
Geometry Creation
Creating an appropriate geometry for the computational domain is imperative for achieving
greater precision in CFD analysis. This entails taking several design considerations into
account, especially when a three dimensional geometry is to be created. Firstly, any
unnecessary design details should be disregarded from the geometry since they increase
computational cost without contributing to accuracy. As a result, only the flat plates of the
solar trackers will be considered, the thickness of which (0.005m in y-axis) will also be
neglected as it is proportionally unimportant when compared with the dimensions of the
domain in the same axis (106.31m).
Furthermore, regarding the geometry of the 3x3 array configuration,the in-between distance
of the trackers, as well as the distance of the flat plate from the ground needs to be
considered. More specifically, the distance of the flat plate from the ground contributes to
the total height of the array which is defined as the Hmax value in the z-axis.
Lastly, in order to minimize the undesirable channel effect, the space around the solar
tracker arrays should be sufficient enough to satisfy certain blockage and directional block-
age ratios. For that reason the domain height is set to 7Hmax, while the domain’s upwind
boundaries as well as the leeward boundary are set to 6Hmax and 16Hmax respectively.

4.1.1.4
RANS Models Evaluation Summary
Overall, the RANS simulations of the three array configurations provide valuable insight
regarding the impact of wind loads and their distribution on the structural integrity of the
solar trackers.
In Case A, there is a presence of distinct high and low pressure areas in the front and
back of the trackers, respectively, which produces pressure differentials and potentially
higher drag forces on the structures.
Case B emerges as the most critical from a structural standpoint. Due to its high tilt angle
(75 °) of the trackers, it produces the most widespread turbulent wake, which subjects the
solar tracker structures to more severe and extensive turbulent forces. This can lead to
increased vibrational stresses and potential fatigue issues for the supporting structures.
For Case C, it is clear from the evaluation of the velocity, pressure, and turbulence contours
that it presents the least challenging conditions for the structures. This configuration exhibits
the smoothest velocity and pressure distributions of all three cases, resulting in more stable
and predictable loads on the solar trackers.
To summarize, it is evident that the simulations for the three aforementioned configurations
highlight the importance of a well-designed supporting frame that can be able to withstand
drag forces and dynamic loading due to high wind.

4.2.1.4
LES Models Evaluation Summary
To summarize, the LES simulations effectively capture the turbulence effects that develop
during the transient simulations and their effect on the array configurations.
In case A, throughout all the contours created, the presence of contrasting velocities as
well as contrasting pressure zones around the trackers indicates a turbulent flow around
the array, which is being confirmed by the contours of the mean turbulent kinetic energy
(TKE). These contours indicate that the trackers are exposed to strong aerodynamic forces
and turbulence which can ultimately impose significant dynamic loads on the structures -
especially around the lower edges of the trackers where the is flow separation and wake
formation.
Case B presents a less turbulent environment compared to case A. Nevertheless, there
are still present regions of high TKE in the lower edge of the trackers. These regions
are more dispersed and lead to reduced turbulence and less pressure differentials being
developed. As a result, the dynamic loading on the trackers is expected to be less severe
compared to case A leading to a more stable flow which is yet turbulent and which still
poses challenges for the structural integrity of the array.
In case C, the LES evaluation revealed that the configuration generates higher levels of
TKE in the edges of the trackers which is likely due to the high azimuthal rotation and low
tilt angle which lead to a more extensive wake formation and turbulence that extend further
downstream the x-axis compared to cases A and B.
Overall, based on the evaluation, it is evident that the LES simulations are able to capture
in more detail the turbulence phenomena that occur on the solar tracker array compared to
the the RANS simulations. More specifically, LES simulations provide more detailed results
in terms of turbulence along all three rows of the array compared to the RANS simulations
which seem to focus mostly on the first row of trackers which is directly exposed to the
wind. In addition, LES simulations seem to also capture more details on the top edge of
each solar collector compared to the RANS simulations.

5.3
Validation of the LES Model
Similar to the previous section, the first step in validating the transient LES model is
to accurately define the inlet boundary conditions in order to ensure the robustness of
the validation. To achieve this, it is essential to analyze and adapt the on-site inlet
measurements so that they align with the initial LES model while also closely reflecting the
actual the on-site conditions.
5.3.1
Specifying the Reference Velocity at the Inlet
For specifying the wind reference velocity at the inlet of the validation model there are two
important considerations: the transient nature of the LES validation model and the on-site
inlet wind measurements of table E.1, Appendix E.
Regarding the transient nature of the validation model, one might initially consider intro-
ducing the time factor to the inlet reference velocity to capture the chaotic and non-linear
tendencies of wind more realistically. However, a critical challenge arises due to the signif-
icant misalignment in the time scales between the measured data and the LES model’s
flow time. More specifically, the weather station was collecting data every 1 minute while
the total flow time of the initial model was approximately 0.20 seconds.
Given this discrepancy, using the maximum recorded high wind speed as a steady inlet
velocity is more appropriate. This approach ensures a robust validation by testing the
model under the most extreme observed conditions, aligning with the LES model’s ability to
resolve small-scale turbulence without introducing artificial fluctuations that the simulation’s
short time frame cannot accurately reflect.
Therefore, as in the previous section 5.2, the adjusted reference velocity that will be used
at Href = 10m is Uref10m = 9.76m/s.

LES Model Validation Summary
The validation process of the LES model has provided important insights into the impact of
varying inlet boundary conditions on flow predictions in LES models, as well as the critical
importance of consistent and accurate data collection in CFD simulations.
Comparing the initial and validation LES models demonstrated a generally strong agree-
ment in terms of the mean velocity magnitude and mean turbulent kinetic energy. While
the Bland-Altman plots focused on highlighting any possible disagreement in these pa-
rameters. However, notable differences arose in the mean pressure coefficient contours,
where the validation model significantly under-predicted the mean pressure coefficient
across the domain. Apart from the varying inlet conditions, this disagreement might be
also attributed to the non-linear relationship between the velocity and pressure in fluid
dynamics according to the Bernoulli equation. Additionally, the sensitivity of LES modelling
to inlet conditions should be taken into account. Since LES models resolve large-scale
turbulence while modelling the smaller scales, even small velocity deviations at the inlet
might be responsible for significant prediction disagreements.
Furthermore, the comparison of the validation model against the measured data and
the RSME value it produced, highlighted the importance of establishing an accurate data
collection process. Several aspects such as the choice of equipment and the measurement
time frame can significantly impact the accuracy of the validation process.

Conclusion
This chapter concludes the thesis by providing a discussion on the application of CFD on
the Heliac case study and its findings. Additionally, it addresses the project’s limitations
and offers proposals for future work.
6.1
Discussion
It is without doubt that the thesis highlights the importance of CFD analysis in industrial
applications. Wind simulations for structural integrity can prove very crucial when designing
solar fields, as the simulations can identify structural vulnerabilities and allow refinements
for enhancing resilience against extreme wind conditions. When assessing wind loads
timely and accurately, any possible structural issues are being addressed and solved
during the design process, preventing any possible obstructions during the operational
period of the solar field, maximizing benefit and energy production as well as minimizing
maintenance-related downtime costs.
In the Heliac case study, all three configuration cases (A, B and C) that were specified by
Heliac as critical for simulation analysis, did indeed provide crucial insight in regards to the
impact of wind loads on each configuration. Since having defined the critical configurations
in terms of azimuthal and tilt rotations, allows for and in-depth analysis of the wind impact
on them. Having the three critical configurations Case A, B and C respectively allowed for
a structured numerical set-up for each case. First each geometry was created followed by
meshes for both RANS and LES simulations.
Undoubtedly, during the numerical investigations, both RANS and LES simulations pro-
vided insight as to how high wind speeds affect each configuration. RANS simulations
proved to be a computationally efficient approach that effectively identified areas of concern
in regards to turbulence developing within the array structures. However, LES simulations
provided a more detailed representation of the flow dynamics, capturing additional effects
that the RANS simulations did not fully resolve- particularly at the top edges of the solar
collector plates and across the rows of each array.
Even though performing LES simulations is computationally more expensive, it allows
for a more precise and targeted design of solar collector structures. This can particularly
prove very beneficial when targeting the outermost collectors of an array which usually
are the most affected from wind loads as ”Wind effects in solar fields with various collector
designs” [6] suggests, already highlighted in section 1.1.
Based on this, it is suggested that a hybrid simulation approach should be adopted when
designing solar collector fields. RANS simulations can provide a general assessment
of wind effects across the entire solar collector field, identifying critical areas of concern.
While, LES simulations can be then applied selectively in specific areas of the domain or
solar panels to allow for a more detailed analysis. This approach ensures that structural
reinforcements are focused where they are needed, balancing computational cost with
structural reliability, leading to a cost-effective yet accurate solution.
To validate the numerical simulations, experimental investigations were conducted at
Heliac’s solar field in Hørsholm, where two wind stations collected data at specific locations
within the array. These measured data points were then used to rerun the simulations,

allowing for a direct comparison between the initial simulations and observed results. The
validation process highlighted the importance of a thorough approach, including the careful
selection of appropriate measuring equipment and consideration of external influencing
factors. Ultimately, the validation process enhances the reliability of CFD simulations,
which is essential for their application in commercial and industrial settings.
6.2
Limitations
Despite the valuable insights provided by the project, certain limitations must be acknowl-
edged. The research was constrained by several factors which are further analyzed
below.
First and foremost, the analysis only covered a small 3x3 array instead of the total 144
solar trackers of the Hørsholm solar field. This was due to the complexity and scale of
the full system, which presented challenges in computational feasibility and resources.
As a result, the findings are based on a limited section of the field rather than a full-scale
analysis of its behavior towards wind loads.
Additionally, both geometry and mesh generation were constrained to some degree.
Regarding the geometry, all solar trackers were simplified by including only their flat
plates and excluding the rotating arms and supporting frames in an effort to minimize the
computational cost. A more detailed geometry could provide insights into the specific areas
where the rotating arm and supporting frame are most affected during the simulations.
While for meshing, the Mesh II generation that was used in the LES simulations had
limitations in the total cell count that could be created in ANSYS Workbench, as discussed
in section 3.2.2. Without this limitation, the solution could have been more detailed and
may have offered further insights
Finally, it is important to highlight the constraints imposed during the experimental investiga-
tions, specifically during the on-site measurements. These constraints can be summarized
into two major aspects: the measuring equipment and the measuring duration. Regarding
the measuring equipment, while utilizing a weather station at the inlet is a logical approach,
using weather stations to capture wind speeds at specific areas around the trackers of
the array is not efficient and limits the accuracy of the validation. This setup prevents
simultaneous data collection at the designated points, restricting both the number of data
points that can be leveraged as well as the accuracy of the comparisons between the initial
and validation simulations. Additionally, the weather station must be manually moved
around the designated points and can only reach the tracker plate at a certain distance and
height, making it difficult to precisely specify each location afterwards, during the validation
simulations. As for the measuring duration, it should be long enough to capture a variety
of wind conditions, including fluctuating wind speeds and directions, in order to ensure
that the collected data accurately reflect real-world variability providing a more accurate
representation of the wind loads acting on the area.
6.3
Proposals for Future Work
The limitations identified in the project highlight the importance of future work that can
enhance the accuracy and scalability of CFD analysis in structural assessment and overall
design of solar fields. Some key directions for further research and development are
presented below.
Future research could focus on simulating a larger portion of solar collectors in the solar field.

Such scaling of the simulation can aid in providing a more accurate and comprehensive
understanding of the wind’s behavior across the entire solar field, as well as valuable
insights into the interactions between multiple rows of trackers.
Moreover, additional configurations with different azimuthal and tilt rotations could be
examined in order to gain a deeper understanding of how varying tracker orientations
influence the wind loads that are being developed in the solar field. Future studies could
identify possible additional critical configurations that result in increased aerodynamic
forces and/or turbulence effects. Such analysis would be particularly valuable for opti-
mizing tracker positioning to minimize structural loads while maximizing energy efficiency
generation.
Further geometry refinement of the solar trackers to include additional components be-
yond the flat plates could also help in identifying vulnerable areas within the supporting
structures. This could provide a more accurate assessment of any potential structural
weaknesses caused by wind loads and turbulence along the array, allowing for a more
targeted improvement approach.
Another aspect that could be addressed in future work is the mesh refinement to address
the limitations encountered in this project. Enhancing the resolution of the LES simulation
meshes could increase the accuracy and provide a more detailed representation of the
flow phenomena occurring within the solar field.
Regarding validation through experimental investigations, future work should initially focus
on establishing a procedure framework that can help streamline the data collection process
and minimize any possible errors. Moreover, future work could focus on exploring and
integrating more advanced measuring equipment that would allow for a continuous and a
more efficient data collection. An ideal example would be to implement pressure sensors
attached to designated positions in the solar trackers that will continuously gather wind

more precise measurements.

to refine CFD methodologies for wind load assessment in solar fields.
