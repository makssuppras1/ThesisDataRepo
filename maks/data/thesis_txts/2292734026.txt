Abstract
In two-stroke diesel engines the lubrication between the piston ring and cylinder liner has high
impact on the mechanical loss. The harsh environment results in poor condition for the lubricant
ﬂuid. This paper researches the lubrication between the piston ring and the cylinder liner to
shed light on the physical behavior.
Theoretical results are calculated from the aspects and assumptions of tribology. Calculation
of the physics along the piston ring is determined with theory from literature and a step wise
ﬁnite diﬀerence method analysis.
Experimental results are measured from a test rig consisting of a small ring specimen upon a
land segment acting as the cylinder liner. The ring and land is separated with an oil ﬁlm and a
motor simulates the motion of an engine. A LabVIEW VI is developed to capture measurements
as experiments are running.
The experiments indicates a hydrodynamic state (surfaces separated with a ﬂuid ﬁlm) between
the ring and land segment. Also the interaction between the two surfaces increases in friction as
the ﬂuid ﬁlm becomes thinner and the eﬀect of dry friction occurs.

Introduction
Large marine ships carry billions of tonnes of cargo every year 1. The cost of shipping cargo
economically and environmentally relies on the eﬃciency of the engine that drives the ship. The
power loss in the engine corresponds to the input energy in the form of diesel combustion to
the power accessible at the output. Engineers working with large marine ship engines strive
to decrease the power loss. A lot of the power loss comes from friction between moving parts.
Graphical illustration of the combustion chamber is shown in Figure 12.
Figure 1: Illustration of the combustion chamber and surrounding components of a MAN B& W engine (known
as MAN Diesel & Turbo today).
This project concerns the power loss caused by friction between the piston ring and cylinder
liner in large marine two-stroke diesel engines. Mechanical loss in large two-stroke engines
corresponds to approximately 4-15% of the total loss. From this 26% is located at the piston
rings [Voelund]. The piston rings are lubricated with oil which creates an oil ﬁlm between the
piston ring and cylinder liner. From tribology (science of rheology) the oil ﬁlm behavior highly
depends on the load, the viscosity of the lubricating oil and the velocity of the ring upon the
liner. Tribology is the study of surfaces’ motion in relation to each other and the main matters

Technically the injection of the oil happens as the piston leaves the top dead centre (TDC). A
groove with a zigzag pattern is milled into the liner with holes for the oil to enter the zigzag
pattern. The oil is dragged down by the stroke of the piston along the cylinder liner. The
cylinder liners surface has a crosshatch pattern which is machined by a honing process. This
enables the oil to accumulate in the crosshatches and to be dragged with the piston ring as it
passes. It is necessary to know that no oil is recovered when ﬁrst injected. The amount of oil
injected is lost after the cycle of the two-strokes and new oil is injected.
Fundamentals of tribology are the main theoretical approach used to describe the behavior of
the lubricating oil ﬁlm. From ﬂuid mechanics, Reynolds equation describes the pressure along
the piston ring. The pressure and the friction force depend signiﬁcantly on the velocity and
viscosity of the oil. Analytical and numerical results are described and compared to experimental
test measurements. All content in this report is drafted from a mechanical engineering study
and assumes that the reader has a basic understanding of mechanics, especially ﬂuid mechanics
and tribology.
For experimental testing, a simple test rig consisting of a piston ring specimen and a cylinder
liner (referred to as the land segment of the test rig), is considered. The land segment is mounted
to a moving table moving back and forth by a motor with a crank mechanism. The motion is
simulating the motion of the engine. Various measurements are captured in order to generate
suﬃcient data to compare with analytical and numerical results.
Testing reveals the behavior of the oil ﬁlm. From testing it is also possible to validate the impact
of the theoretical assumptions. The friction force of a cycle can determine the physical behavior
and display issues. Furthermore, an attempt is made to determine the time frame for the oil to
ﬂoat away as the ring travels back and forth. As the oil in the engine is injected for each cycle,
a question of the oil waste becomes a reality.

Basic Theory and Concepts
The main subject of this thesis is tribology and concerns oil viscosity behavior and surface
texture theory related to ﬂuids mechanics. Basic ﬂuid mechanic and tribology terms used in
this thesis are brieﬂy described in this section.
Tribology is the interaction of surfaces in motion. In this thesis the application of suﬃcient,
but not excessive lubrication is researched to avoid excessive high friction forces and wear. The
theory describing the physics derives mainly from ﬂuid dynamics and theory of surface texture.
Resistance when rubbing two surfaces against each other is called friction. Friction comes from
the materials texture on a micro scale. The interaction between the two surfaces causes peaks
and valleys on the surface to crack and has an adhesion eﬀect (one can think of the material
being welded together). The smoother the surface texture the lower the friction, as the peaks
and valleys are smaller and lesser interactions occurs. For a graphical illustration see Figure 4(a).
2.1
Surface texture
No surface is perfectly smooth and will always have peaks and valleys. It is necessary to
be aware of the surface roughness in tribology interactions. Various parameters are used for
describing the surface texture, one of the most common is the arithmetical mean deviation, the
Ra value. The Ra value is given from the area that diﬀers from the mean value line divided by
the length of the sample. Mathematically given from equation (1) and illustrated with Figure 4(c).

2.3
Viscosity
Viscosity comes from the study of rheologity and concerns liquid ﬂow behavior and their defor-
mation. Viscosity is one of the most important lubricant parameters in tribology. Conditions of
lubrication impacts the shear stress of the ﬂuid ﬁlm and the viscosity highly determines the
ﬂuid ﬁlm thickness. Three diﬀerent viscosity terms are explained below:
Kinematic viscosity νrm2{ss is the viscosity explained with earth conditions (eg. atmospheric
pressure, gravity). Atmospheric pressure and gravity contributes to this parameter.
Dynamic viscosity ηrPa  ss is determined from the shear stress and shear rate. Couette
ﬂow (explained in section 2.5) application determines the dynamic viscosity and is the
dominant factor of the shear stress acting in a ﬂuid.
Apparent viscosity is the dynamic viscosity depending on the shear rate. Shear rate is the
relationship between the velocity and ﬁlm height, as seen in equation (4). It is worth

the pressure from the combustion chamber and along the liner. The values has been made
up but notice that the pressure declines for each ring. The pressure could for example be
10MPa in the combustion chamber, 8MPa on the other side of the ﬁrst piston ring and
6MPa on the other side of the second piston ring. Considering the top ring when the piston
travels along the cylinder liner. The pressure on the front side would be 10MPa meaning the
pressure distribution along the ring would start at 10MPa. On the backside the pressure is
8MPa. With a backside pressure of 8MPa cavitation cant be present inside the lubrication.
The backside pressure distribution along the last half of the ring, is considered to be at least zero.
To include this eﬀect in the FDM solution, a manipulation is added to describe the back side
pressure. First zeroes are added at the back side to see the pressure distribution become
asymmetric and deviate from zero at the middle point of the ring (at x  0). Next is to add an
inlet and outlet pressure to show a change in the pressure distribution and avoids a cavitational
area (ppxq   0).

7.3.1
Sampling and Aliasing
Aliasing is the eﬀect of a signal becoming indistinguishable, meaning a reconstruction of the
signal from the captured samples does not corresponds to the original continuous signal. For
example a camera ﬁlming a rotating wheel. If the rate of the rotating wheel approaches the
rate of the cameras frame per second the wheel could look like going backwards or standing
still. This explains problems with the sample rate that need to be addressed. Nyquist theorem
states that one should sample two times the signal cycles 5. If for example the wheel has 1
spoke (awkward design of a wheel, but eases the understanding of the example), the sample
rate should then be at least 2 times the angular velocity of the wheel. So the spokes location
would at least be present at two positions in the pictures corresponding to a revolution.
The NI PCI-6221 is capable of sampling at a maximum rate of 250rkS{ss. LabVIEW captures
measurements from 9 channels from this the maximum sample rate is calculated from equation
50. It seems suﬃcient to run experiments at a angular velocity of around 60rrev{mins. The

7.3.2
Producer and Consumer
The producer and consumer principal (based on a Master/slave principal) is a fast way of
capturing measurements6. One while loop, "the producer" does the sampling of measurements
as fast as possible. Only action corresponding to sampling measurements are present in this
loop. One loop corresponds to the one sample. The loop execution rate is speciﬁed with a
"Wait until next ms multiple" function, set to 0.001rss. The sampling rate is then stated as
1rkHzs (if the software can sample that fast). Another while loop, "the consumer" works out
the second priority actions at a slower rate and writes the data to a ﬁle. The actions in the
consumer does not necessarily has to be executed in an exact period of time or at a certain fast
rate. The two loops are connected with a queue. The queue contains an "enqueue" element,
that adds data to the queue and a "dequeue" elements that releases the queue data. The mea-
surements captured in the producer loop puts the data in the enqueue element. The consumer
loop releases the data as fast as stated with a "Wait until next ms multiple" function set to
0.002rss (corresponding to 0.5rkHzs). This means running a experiment of 10rss takes 20rss.

sumer being the "write to ﬁle". LabVIEW Front panel and block diagram is found in appendix A.

7.4
Procedure steps for experiments
Small mistakes and variations in the experimental procedure could have signiﬁcant impact on
the measurements. Therefore the setup and the steps has to be carefully and thorough executed.
Executing force transducer calibration:
1. Disassembling the bridge and piston rod to clear space for the wire.
2. Connecting the wire to the force transducer frame assembly, over the pulley and down to
a hook.
3. Executing the LabVIEW calibration setup settings.
4. Adding a weight to the hook and speciﬁed the load in LabVIEW.
5. Adding another weight to the hook and speciﬁed the load in LabVIEW.
6. This is repeated for diﬀerent weights for a more precise calibration.
7. Again this is repeated for tension as well.
8. At last the calibration is tested for veriﬁcation.
Distance probes are calibrated as followed. A special micrometer without the anvil face is
mounted at the end of the bridge with the spindle face touching the land segment. It is important
that the micrometer exactly lifts the bridge as this acts as the reference point. The voltage from
the sensors are captured. The thimble is turned for a distance of 0.01mm from the reference

Concerns of the test rigs duability
Working practically with a test rig for experimental results always leads to various practical
issues and problems that need to be addressed and solved, which are listed below.
7.5.1
Sampling
Varies editing in the LabVIEW VI to sample fast enough was a problem until an adjustment of
the sample rate with the Producer and Consumer setup succeeded with a sample rate of
5rkS{ss (without the 2 counters from the encoder). As calculated in section 7.3.1 this is lower
than the max but suﬃcient and works reliable. The analog signal reader reads 5 values for each
and the ﬁrst value is written into the data ﬁle.
Improvements in the LabVIEW VI
Two days before handing in the report a small improvement came into my mind. First the
distance sensors might be able to show smoother measurements if the 5 read values per channel
(from the analog task channels) of the sample loop was averaged before written to ﬁle. This
might take some power but thought to be insigniﬁcant. Furthermore a calibration factor is
added to the 5 read values from the sample loop for the force transducers, sensors and contact
measurement. This is done before the data is extracted to 1 measurement with a "index array"
function. If this was done after the data has been extracted to 1 measurement fewer calculations
is executed. The eﬀect should not be crucial, but mentioned for the sake of good order.
7.5.2
Grinding the land section leads to ﬁsh scales
The land section were grinded as part of preparation to the experiments. However after the
grinding, the surface resembled ﬁsh scale. This is a normal problem for workshops doing plane
44
==PAGE:53==
Hans Niels-Christiansen s093521
grinding. When the machine stands still for a period of time, the grinding liquid travels down
to the bottom of the wheel and causes the grindwheel to become non-circularly. As the machine
is started again the grindwheel is slightly eccentric and jumps on the surface of the part and
leads to a concave wave surface.
7.5.3
Scratch at the one end because of a tilted specimen
During the preparation of the LabVIEW program a problem mounting the ring specimen with
the bracket was revealed. Two blocks are bolted together to form a loose "perfect" ﬁt pocket for
the specimen (able to rotate around the x-direction). The pocket were too narrow for a loose ﬁt
so the specimen were fastened to a locked position, unable to align parallel to the land segment.
This resulted in a scratch along the movement area of the specimen on the land section. The
scratch is not deep and impact is thought to be negligent. The loose ﬁt pocket were solved with
a piece of tin foil between the brackets.

ﬁrst couple of cycles. It is diﬃcult to determine as there is an almost fully oil ﬁlm at TDC and
BDC as the velocity equals zero within the ﬁrst cycles. Furthermore the ﬁrst couple of cycle the
the ring specimen still distributes the oil on the land segment.

Discussion
This paper includes theoretical and experimental results and strives to connect the two. The
theoretical results are based on the assumption of a hydrodynamic state. The experimental
results are measurements from tests that reveals the characteristics of the hydrodynamic state.
In this section the results are discussed.
Discussion about the Theory
The analytical results follow the theory of tribology. Inputting a minimum ﬁlm thickness h0
corresponds to a pressure distribution from the assumption; that Poiseuille ﬂow is equal to
Couette ﬂow (equation 11)). The FDM analysis is done for a numerical solution to same
equation. Comparison of the two theoretical results shows exact similarities as expected. This
is true as long as the FDM is represented with suﬃcient points.
The load carry capacity is found from the pressure distribution over the ring. As examined
this corresponds to zero from a fully theoretical perspective. However by manipulating the
pressure distribution of the cavitational area on the back side, it is possible to express a load
carry capacity. From a physical perspective this is represented by cavitational ﬁngers where
the oil breaks up into partitions, separated by air gaps. With this the friction force can be
described. From experimental results the magnitude of cavitational ﬁngers can be estimated.
Experimental Results
Force Calibration
The force transducers were calibrated. The test run veriﬁes the signal within the range of
20rNs. This is more than plenty as the friction force should not exceed the half of this.
Oscillations
The oscillations indicate bad lubricated conditions as the FFT analysis showed no signs of struc-
tural vibrations. The ring specimen of radius 40rmms tends to oscillate as the ﬁrst. In longer
57
==PAGE:66==
Hans Niels-Christiansen s093521
experiments (500+ cycles) the oscillations started as the lubricating ﬁlm began to disappear
and dry friction occurred. The structure most have some weak connections one might want to
take a look at. Maybe the structure should be made of steel instead of aluminum. The surface
of the land segment could use a new and perhaps better surface grind. The oscillations could
come from the ring jumping on the surface of the land segment.
Low Viscosity Lubrication
Experiments with the oil of kinematic viscosity of ν40  22rmm2{ss, showed, that it could not
establish a full ﬂuid ﬁlm. At least not under the condition in the test rig. Perhaps it could have
been done with a minimum load of wz  5rNs (the bridge itself) and maximum velocity, but
this test was not executed as it seems to be far from the physics inside the engine.
Velocity plot
The velocity plot shows a hydrodynamic behavior. The higher the velocity the higher the friction
force. At TDC and BDC the ring specimen tends to contact the land segment at lower velocities
but not at higher velocities. With the higher velocities the ring is position at TDC and BDC
for shorter amount of time and the ring does not squeeze through the oil. The measurements
are similar to those performed in article [Voelund]. It is a good indication that the test rig can
reproduce measurements done 5 years ago.
Comparison of Radius
The comparison plot in section 8.5 illustrates the expected behavior. The ring specimen of
radius 40rmms oscillates because of diﬃculties to establishing a full oil ﬁlm. The friction force,
ﬁlm thickness and contact measurements follows the velocity. At TDC and BDC the friction
force and ﬁlm thickness is approximately zero and the contact measurement indicates contact
between the ring and land. The specimen with radius of 80rmms contributes to the lowest
friction force and highest ﬁlm thickness. Except for the ring specimen of radius 40rmms, the
measurements was as expected.
58
==PAGE:67==
Hans Niels-Christiansen s093521
Comparison of Measured and Calculated Friction Force
When the friction force is calculated from the minimum ﬁlm thickness the eﬀect backside should
not contribute. However calculations match the measurement well with the eﬀect of negative
backside pressure. This means that almost no cavitational ﬁnger are present and the impact
of Couette ﬂow is signiﬁcantly. From a tribological point of view the circular arc is very large
compared to the rest of the geometry.
Groove Experiments
Experiments with a groove at TDC and BDC showed, that the friction force increased as the
number of cycles increased. The distance sensors measurements was unable to produce suﬃ-
ciently precise data. They indicated a very thin ﬁlm thickness. It was not possible to precisely
indicate when the hydrodynamic behavior failed. But it easily showed a hydrodynamic behavior
up too 750 cycles. The ring specimens of 160rmms and 240rmms even showed tendencies of no
contact at TDC and BDC for up to more than 150 cycles. The specimen with radius 160rmms
seemed to act hydrodynamic after 1000 cycles smoothly. Although the contact measurement did
indicate contact. There were some problems with the encoder at the long experiments which
might need to be addressed. Nothing that seems to impact the results of the experiments but
inconvenient for the data processing. The coeﬃcient of friction calculated from the experiments
is larger than some of the referenced literature states. Remember the calculation of the coeﬃcient
of friction is at a high velocity ( 0.2rm{ss) in the middle. It is lower at lower velocities close
to TDC and BDC. It is easily to provoke the hydrodynamic state. Since the ﬁlm thickness
is around 10rµms, particles of this size could be present on the land segment, causing higher
friction. The test hall is not completely sterile and the environment around the test rig picks up
dust and such. Doing the experiments oil ﬂoated away as soon as it was pushed near the edge of
the groove. The experiments used a few cycles to spread the oil all over the contact area. With
the presence of these factors it is nearly impossible to set up the "perfect" hydrodynamic state.
Lubricated state
A Stribeck curve was calculated from the friction force of the 250th cycle. At TDC the friction
force is large due to the contact. Then the friction force decreases due to ring starting to climb
upon the oil. As the ﬂuid ﬁlm is established the lowest friction force is present and the friction
force increases with the velocity. This is the known physical behavior but the lowest point of
59
==PAGE:68==
Hans Niels-Christiansen s093521
the imitated Stribeck curve is µ  0.035, which again is larger than expected. It is probably
possible to imitate a Stribeck curve if an experiment was directly setup up to do so. Most likely
with a low velocity and high sampling frequency as the lowest friction force probably occurs in
a short time period.
Round up
Various experiments has been executed that indicates hydrodynamic interaction between the
ring specimen and land segment. Ring radiuses of 80rmms and 160rmms corresponds to the
best geometry of the ring. The impact of Couette ﬂow is signiﬁcant as the cavitational back side
of the ring inﬂuenced the friction force. From friction force measurements the hydrodynamic
state was noticed to be more than 1000 cycles with the oil ﬂoating away through grooves. But
the friction force approximately doubled with that amount of cycles.
Conclusion
The behavior of the ring on the liner can be described with the theory of tribology. Indications
of hydrodynamic state is conﬁrmed, Even though environment has a large impact.
Translating the results from the test rig to a big scale is diﬃcult. The ring easily pushed and
dragged the oil back and forth. The liner surface is smooth and this returns steady hydrodynamic
behavior. The liner in the engines has a crosshatch pattern making it more diﬃcult to determine
the hydrodynamic behavior.
As it is now, the oil is injected occurs just beneath TDC, from a zigzag pattern in the cylinder
liner. Meaning that the injection point is exposed to the combustion as the piston moves
downwards. The eﬀect of this is unknown but I could not see a hindrance for the oil to be
dragged from BDC and upwards. This leaves the injection point more concealed under operation.
Even from a gravity point of view there seems to be no issues. The amount of power and
pressure in the ﬁlm has high impact.
The backside pressure has little to no impact on the hydrodynamic state. Cavitational ﬁngers
were not highly represented. Probably has more eﬀect when lesser oil is available, as lesser oil
60
==PAGE:69==
Hans Niels-Christiansen s093521
are being dragged with the ring.
From testing the piston ring shows no indication not to act hydrodynamic. Eﬀects of the
surrounding have high impact. As the ﬂuid ﬁlm is thin it tends to contact with little disturbance.
Low viscosity oil has trouble establishing a ﬂuid ﬁlm and would probably have further trouble
when the cylinder liner is positioned vertical.

Mobil Velocite™ Oil Numbered Series
Spindle and Hydraulic Oils
Product Description
The Mobil Velocite™ Oil Numbered Series oils are premium performance products primarily designed for the lubrication of high­speed spindles in machine tools. They are also
used in some critical hydraulic, circulation systems and air line oilers where the appropriate viscosity grade is selected. They are formulated from select high­quality, low viscosity
base oils and additives that impart good resistance to oxidation and protection from rust and corrosion. They possess very good resistance to foaming and separate readily from
water.
 
Features and Benefits
The Mobil Velocite Oil Numbered Series provide exceptional lubrication of close­tolerance bearings which helps keep the bearings running cool and helps maintain the precision
required by many of today's critical machine tools. Although the Mobil Velocite Oil Numbered Series oils were designed for spindle bearings, they exhibit the required properties to
function as low pressure hydraulic and circulating oils as long as the proper viscosity is selected. This feature can help minimise inventory costs and reduce the potential for
product misapplication.

STATOIL LOADWAY EP 220 
Industrial Gear Oil 
 
 
APPLICATIONS 
LoadWay EP 220 is recommended for all types of industrial 
gearboxes for operating temperatures up to +80 °C. Below 
this temperature, no harmful oxidation products or deposits 
are formed. 
ADVANTAGES 
LoadWay EP 220 handles heavy loads without allowing 
gearbox components to wear down. The product provides 
rapid water separation and air release, and is highly filterable. 
This translates into high levels of operational safety and 
reliability. 
CHARACTERISTICS 
LoadWay EP 220 is a mineral-oil-based oil for gearboxes used 
in heavy-duty applications. It is intended for extreme heavy-
duty use at normal temperatures. The oil contains additives to 
inhibit wear, corrosion, oxidation and foaming. Other 
important features of LoadWay EP 220 are rapid water 
separation and air release, and its excellent filterability.
