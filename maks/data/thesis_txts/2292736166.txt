Abstract 
 
Freight transportation contributes largely to nuisances in urban areas such as congestion, noise or 
air pollution, which impacts significantly the quality of life inside the cities, and therefore their 
attractiveness. Moreover, the need for freight transportation is expected to increase in the future. 
In order to tackle this issue, the aim of city logistics is to improve the distribution of goods within 
cities by considering the entire freight transportation flow holistically and coordinating the 
deliveries, instead of seeing each trip as being independent from the others. 
This report aims at introducing an algorithm which uses a Large Neighbourhood Search heuristic 
within a simulated annealing framework to solve city logistics instances using real-life data. To 
this extent, a literature review covering those subjects is conducted and its outcomes are 
presented. In this report, the city logistics problems are modelled as pickup and delivery problems 
with time windows. When tested on benchmark instances widely studied in the literature, the 
algorithm performs well and provides good solutions. Furthermore, the algorithm is used to solve 
newly-generated instances based on real-life data from the city of Copenhagen.

1. Introduction 
It is commonly acknowledged that freight transportation contributes largely to nuisances in urban 
areas such as congestion, noise or air pollution. It impacts significantly the quality of life inside 
cities and therefore their attractiveness. Besides, recent studies on freight transportation within 
the cities have shown that the average vehicle load is quite low and the number of empty trips is 
significant, due to the lack of freight consolidation between carriers and the imbalance between 
inbound and outbound flows of goods in urban areas. On the other hand, freight transportation is 
essential for the majority of urban activities. Given the expected growth of the urban population 
in the future and the development of direct business-to-customer deliveries, the need for freight 
transportation in the cities will increase accordingly [1]. Therefore, not only there is room for 
improvement regarding urban freight transportation management, but this improvement is greatly 
needed in order to overcome the challenges that cities will face in the future. 
In this perspective, city logistics aims at improving the distribution of goods inside cities with 
regard to both companies’ competitiveness and environment preservation. Instead of seeing the 
deliveries from supplier to customer as individual trips, they should be considered as part of an 
integrated logistics system [2]. By consolidating the goods and coordinating their delivery, the 
traffic can be reduced in the inner city, saving expenses for the company and reducing both 
congestion and gas emissions. This service can be further improved by using environmentally-
friendly vehicles, whose size can be adapted to the cities’ particularities such as narrow streets or 
restricted areas. As far as business is concerned, this service can be interesting, both for the 
companies selling goods who can spare the inconvenience of travelling in the inner city to deliver 
them, and the end customers who receive their order at once and can therefore better organise 
themselves. 
The rest of the report is constructed as follows: section 2 introduces the city logistics problems 
and the large neighbourhood search heuristic through a summary of the literature on the 
respective topics, chapter 3 presents the project along with the modelling of the problem at hand, 
while section 4 deals with the presentation of the software and its various components. Finally, 
section 5 reports and analyses the results of the program’s experiments on benchmark and newly-
generated instances, while section 6 concludes the report.

2. Literature review 
2.1. City logistics 
2.1.1. The context 
Because they concern both individuals and companies, city logistics systems involve two main 
groups of stakeholders: public authorities and private companies. While the former group is 
mainly interested in the well-being of city dwellers and therefore sees in this system a way of 
ensuring a better quality of life by reducing both gas emissions and noise within the city, the 
latter aims at increasing its revenues, and its interest in the project lies in the reduction of the 
transportation costs [3]. Several examples of coordination of freight transportation have been 
implemented and are reported in the literature. It includes not only city logistics systems but also 
cooperation of concurrent freight carriers with the aim of lowering the delivery costs. Instances of 
this cooperation in Germany and Japan in the 1990’s have led to a reduction of both travel time 
for vehicles and transportation costs for the companies [4]. 
Over the past few years, public authorities have begun intervening through policies such as 
access time window regulations or vehicle restrictions in city centres, in order to lessen the 
impact of freight transportation on people’s lives. However, in their study on the influence of 
these regulations in several cities in the Netherlands, Quak and De Koster [5] concluded that 
imposing such restrictions without significantly lowering the number of kilometres travelled 
resulted in a deterioration of the pollution level and an increase of the delivery costs. Indeed, 
typical time windows impose vehicle access to the city centre to be restricted to the morning, 
which coincides with rush hours, hence increasing congestion and environmental impacts as well 
as costs for the freight carriers. Muñuzuri et al. [6] arrived to a similar conclusion for the situation 
in Spain as they showed that the imposed restrictions, instead of leading to more collaborative 
deliveries, resulted in an increase of the delivery costs with a higher number of vehicles needed 
for their operations.  
Other initiatives, aiming at increasing the average vehicle load by restricting the access to some 
public facilities based on their load factor, were implemented in Copenhagen and Amsterdam [4]. 
More drastic measures, such as congestion charging or the creation of low-emission zones which 
also applies to private transportation, are emerging in some areas to reduce both road traffic and 
gas emissions in the city centre, by encouraging the shift to more ecologically-friendly vehicles. 
However, they require stronger will-power and determination from the authorities.  
==PAGE:9==
 
Page 8 of 54 
 
 
 
Because city logistics systems aim at optimising the entire freight transportation flow inside the 
cities, they require an important management structure. Public policies alone have suffered a lack 
of efficiency to tackle this issue. As Dablanc points it out [7], local authorities do not necessarily 
have the required knowledge to better organise freight transportation, and many focus on 
regulating it or banning it instead of organising it more efficiently. In order to succeed, this type 
of initiative needs a stronger relation between the different stakeholders involved rather than 
imposing regulations to freight carriers.  
One example of a successful city logistics system is the one implemented in Monaco, where large 
trucks are banned and have to deliver to a consolidation centre located just outside the city. The 
delivery of goods within the city limits is performed by a private carrier which receives subsidies 
from the government as the urban freight delivery is regarded as a public service [2].  
  
2.1.2. Vehicle routing for city logistics 
The Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP) is one of the most known combinatorial optimisation 
problems in the transportation sectors, and has been widely studied in the literature for more than 
50 years. As stated in the introduction, the goal of city logistics is to better organise the freight 
transportation and therefore to optimise the vehicles’ routes within the cities. The problem can 
thus be modelled as a capacitated vehicle routing problem (CVRP), which aims at minimising the 
transportation costs while obeying to various constraints such as the vehicle capacity or the 
service of all customers. The problem can be further tightened with additional constraints specific 
to urban areas like accessibility restrictions to the delivery zone. The consolidation takes place in 
the depots: vehicles coming from outside unload their goods, which are then transferred there in 
smaller vehicles and delivered in the city. Since those depots are the interface between the outer 
and inner parts of the city, they should ideally be placed at easily-accessible locations, close to 
the entrance of the delivery area. Additionally, they may involve intermodal platforms to increase 
the consolidation possibilities [1].  
In order to further optimise the freight transportation inside the cities and encourage more 
customers to use this service, it might be beneficial to perform various additional tasks instead of 
focusing only on delivering merchandise from companies located outside the city. These include 
the collection of packaging materials once the customers have finished unwrapping their parcels, 
the pickup of goods to be brought to the depot for deliveries outside the zone in question, and the 
delivery of commodities between customers of the area. This leads to considering other variants 
==PAGE:10==
 
Page 9 of 54 
 
 
 
of the CVRP such as the VRP with backhauls or the VRP with simultaneous deliveries and 
pickups. Those problems appear for instance in the context of reverse logistics when dealing with 
reusable packaging like returnable bottles [8].  
In the case of large urban areas, it might be beneficial to use several layers called echelons for the 
routing of vehicles since the delivery zones to be covered are vast. The most common of those 
problems is the two-tier system which can be modelled as a two-echelon capacitated vehicle 
routing problem (2E-CVRP), and involves the planning of vehicles at both echelons [9]. In this 
problem, a depot is located outside the city at an easily-accessible place, where companies can 
drop off goods, which are then put together in vehicles and delivered to smaller sites close to the 
city centre called satellites, building up the first echelon. From those satellites acting as depots, 
the goods are transferred into smaller vehicles, which make the delivery to customers inside the 
city centre, thus constituting the second echelon [10]. This problem has additional time 
constraints with the synchronisation of the two echelons at the satellites, acting both as delivery 
nodes for the first echelon and depots for the second one. An instance of this problem has been 
implemented in Amsterdam, where trams transported the goods from warehouses located outside 
the city to several locations inside, from which goods were transferred into electric vehicles 
operating the final stage of the distribution [2]. 
 
2.2. Large neighbourhood search 
2.2.1. General presentation 
One of the particularities of the Vehicle Routing Problem is that it is NP-complete, meaning that 
even though any given solution to the problem can be checked efficiently, there exists currently 
no algorithm that is able to find the optimal solution in a polynomial time. Therefore, in order to 
ensure a-priori a good solution within a reasonable amount of time, one has to use techniques 
such as heuristics. This section will focus on a technique that has been developed recently and 
provides promising results in the literature: the Large Neighbourhood Search (LNS) heuristic.  
Similarly to other local search heuristics, the LNS moves in the solution space by investigating 
the neighbours of the current solution in search of a candidate improving the objective function. 
The difference lies in the definition of the neighbourhood. For traditional local search algorithms, 
the neighbourhood is quite small, being defined by simple changes in the solution such as 
swapping two customers or relocating a customer to another place. On the other hand, the LNS 
==PAGE:11==
 
Page 10 of 54 
 
 
 
neighbourhoods are significantly bigger, and are obtained by destroying and repairing a 
substantial portion of the solution [11]. By having more complex neighbour operators and 
therefore investigating a larger neighbourhood than other heuristics, it aims at avoiding one of the 
most common problems found in local search algorithms: being stuck in a local minimum.  
The LNS heuristic works as follows: a removal operator destroys a part of the solution, which is 
then rebuilt by a repair method. At each iteration of the algorithm, the optimisation process 
focuses only on this part of the solution, the rest being fixed. This means that a neighbourhood is 
defined as the set of solutions which contain at the same position the elements that have not been 
removed during this iteration. Additionally, the size of the part removed from the solution can 
vary in order to diversify the search. However, this size should be controlled in order to avoid a 
too small neighbourhood, which could lead to a hard navigation in the solution space (as for 
standard local search heuristics) or a too big neighbourhood which would take too long to 
explore, thus increasing the running time of each step [11].  
When choosing how to repair the solution, one is faced with the common insertion dilemma: 
inserting the requests optimally at the expense of the running time or heuristically, that is quickly 
but with no guarantee of optimality. Regarding the optimal reinsertion, model-based heuristics or 
matheuristics, which combine mathematical modelling and metaheuristics in order to find a 
balance between solution quality and computational time, have been recently used to enhance 
local searches. One idea is to move in the search space by solving exactly a problem given a 
partial solution [12]. In the context of LNS, this partial solution corresponds to what is left from 
the destruction step and cannot be modified, while the visits which have been removed are 
reinserted optimally. The use of exact methods for exploring a large neighbourhood is at the 
origin of the LNS heuristic and has been successfully applied to the VRP [13]. 
 
2.2.2. The adaptive version 
The LNS heuristic can be supplemented by an adaptive layer, where the destruction and repair 
operators are selected in a pool of methods. At each iteration, a pair of destruction-repair methods 
is stochastically picked according to their past performances, forming therefore different 
neighbourhoods to investigate. This means that a well-performing method is more likely to be 
chosen than an ill-performing one, but the choice of the latter one is not totally prevented in order 
to diversify the search by forming structurally different neighbourhoods [14].  
==PAGE:12==
 
Page 11 of 54 
 
 
 
When using this adaptive LNS (or ALNS), one has to choose a framework on which to work in 
order to decide whether to accept or reject a new solution. This can be a simple descent approach, 
meaning that only improving solutions are accepted or a more complex framework such as the 
simulated annealing metaheuristic, which can accept worse solutions than the current one with a 
certain probability [15]. When working on a small neighbourhood, the latter is preferred in order 
not to stay stuck in a local minimum by exploring worse neighbours, which might lead after some 
iterations to a better solution. But, since the LNS works on a large neighbourhood and thus 
investigates a significant number of potential solutions, the use of the first framework is also 
justified. Both have provided high quality solutions in the literature, even though the most recent 
papers tend to use metaheuristics to guide the search to provide a greater diversification in the 
search space exploration.  
 
2.2.3. The LNS heuristic applied to VRP instances 
The LNS heuristic is particularly well suited for complex problems such as the Vehicle Routing 
Problems (VRPs) and in particular those with many side constraints [16]. Indeed, the presence of 
side constraints is a major issue as it reduces the neighbourhood size as some moves become 
infeasible. This significantly increases the risk of staying stuck in a local minimum for local 
search working in a small neighbourhood, but this risk becomes less of a problem when working 
with a large neighbourhood with considerably more move possibilities. Both the original and the 
adaptive versions of the heuristic have been widely used on various instances of the vehicle 
routing and the pickup-and-delivery problems and have showed promising results.  
Ropke and Pisinger [14] have tested both the original and the adaptive version of the heuristic on 
the pickup and delivery problem with time windows. They have compared them with other 
known heuristics for the problem and both versions of the large neighbourhood search have 
produced better results than the common heuristics when applied on benchmark instances. They 
have also set them against each other with comparable results for small to medium-size instances 
and significantly better ones for the ALNS on larger instances.  
It has also been applied in the city logistics context to two-echelon systems. In [17], 
Hemmelmayr et al. tested their adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search on benchmark instances 
and their algorithm outperformed the other methods for the 2E-VRP found in the literature, with 
several new best solutions found.

3. Project definition 
3.1. Project presentation 
3.1.1. Scope of the project 
The goal of the project is to solve city logistics instances modelling real-life problems by creating 
a program based on a Large Neighbourhood Search heuristic. The use of this heuristic is justified 
by the size of the instances, making it impossible to ensure that an optimal solution can be 
reached in a short time with exact methods, as well as by the good results it has provided in the 
literature. The destruction part of the heuristic is performed by several removal operators while 
two options are tried for the repair step: an exact repair based on a mathematical model and 
heuristic repair methods. The local search is furthermore supplemented by an adaptive layer to 
select the methods based on their performances. The functioning of the software is described 
further in details in Section 4. Finally, several versions of the heuristic are tested with one or 
more repair methods and compared to each other in the last part of the report.  
In order to represent city logistics systems, a pickup and delivery problem with time windows 
(PDPTW) has been chosen. The reason for this choice is that it deals with a more general 
problem than the VRPTW since it can handle both pickups and deliveries and is therefore better 
suited to match the diversity of the requests coming from the users of this service. In this 
problem, each vehicle services requests, made up of a pickup at a customer or the depot and a 
delivery to another customer or the depot. Since a pickup and its corresponding delivery are part 
of the same request, both actions should be done by the same vehicle. In addition to deliveries, a 
service aiming at relieving customers from the packaging of the goods they have just received 
can be provided upon demand. Since the packaging collection has to be performed at a later time 
than the delivery and as they are not part of the same pickup-and-delivery request, the second 
visit to the customer might be done by a different vehicle than the one used to fulfil the initial 
delivery, with an obvious precedence constraint. 
There is only one depot and one type of vehicle; hence the capacity constraint to be obeyed in 
each route is the same. Since the vehicle occupancy is fluctuating along the route, with both 
pickup and delivery customers, the capacity should be checked at all times. Besides, each 
customer has a time window which should always be satisfied. Both time window limits are 
considered hard constraints since they are likely to correspond to shop opening hours, personnel 
availability or access restrictions, which make deliveries outside the time windows inconvenient.

3.1.2. Problem modelling 
Upon acceptance of their order, each customer can ask for one of the following services: delivery 
only, delivery and later collection of packaging material (also called back visit), pickup only, 
simultaneous delivery and pickup with or without collection of material. Each of these services is 
then broken up into pickups and deliveries, which are then linked together to form requests.  
In order to have the same number of pickups and deliveries and to simplify the model, every time 
a node (customer or depot) implies the completion of several tasks, it is virtually decomposed 
into several nodes, one for each task, located at the same place. For example, a vehicle which 
would deliver n customers from the depot would have to visit n pickups located at the depot to 
load the goods. Therefore, a delivery to a customer from the depot would be treated as a request 
whose pickup node is a copy of the depot and delivery node a copy of the customer. Those copies 
are referred to in the following report as visits. This separation of services gives more flexibility 
to the model as multiple trips per vehicle are allowed: a visit to the depot to pick up goods is not 
considered as a return to the depot but as the service of a node. 
A model of the problem is presented in Figure 1, where the big blue circle represent the service 
zone, the square at the top corner models the depot located on the outskirts of the city, the black 
dots symbolize the nodes to be served, each of them having a time window. The green arrows 
show an instance of a route, which starts and ends at the depot and serves several customers along 
the way, two of them being visits to the depot at the beginning of the route to load some goods.

3.1.3.Cost representation 
The objective of vehicle routing problems is to minimise the total routing expenses. To this end, 
several aspects of the transportation costs have to be considered such as: purchase and 
maintenance of the vehicles, salary of the drivers, fuel consumption, and delay on the service of 
requests. To model those aspects in the project, the cost has been broken down into three 
components: 
- 
A fixed cost for each vehicle used 
- 
A cost on the distance travelled 
- 
A cost on the time spent out from the depot 
 
According to the situation, several combinations of these components with different unit prices 
can be used. For instance, one may want to model the driver wage as a fixed cost while other may 
prefer modelling it as a time-dependent cost or as a combination of the two to separate full-time 
and temporary employees.

The objective function (A) aims at minimising the routing cost. For this problem, the cost 
function has been decomposed into a distance-dependent cost to model the travelling costs (fuel, 
vehicle maintenance) and a pull-out cost for each vehicle used to represent other parameters such 
as drivers’ salaries. Those costs are stored in a cost matrix where all costs correspond to distances 
with an extra fixed cost for all arcs leaving the start depot. 
Constraint (1) makes sure that all pickup locations are served exactly once. Constraints (2) are the 
flow conservation constraints, forcing all the vehicles arriving at a customer to leave as well. 
Constraints (3) and (4) ensure that all routes respectively start and end at the depot. The capacity 
constraints (5) impose a maximum capacity on each visit while constraints (6) make sure that the 
vehicles leave the start depot and arrive at the end depot empty. Constraints (7) update the usage 
of the vehicle between two visits. Constraints (8) ensure that the service of all customers begins 
within their time windows: both time window limits are hard constraint for the start of service. 
Constraints (9) make sure that vehicles can serve two customers consecutively, taking into 
account both service time at customers and travelling time between them. Constraints (10) force 
the pickup and the delivery corresponding to the same request to be served by the same vehicle. 
Combined with constraints (2), they ensure the service of all deliveries. Constraints (11) impose 
the precedence of the pickup over its corresponding delivery in the same request while constraints 
(12) make sure that a return visit to a customer for collection of material is always performed 
after the delivery and some buffer time, needed for the customers to unwrap the parcels they just 
received. Finally, constraints (13), (14) and (15) define the decision variables. 
Since this model is meant to be used by complex instances, that are hard to solve in practice, it 
will not be used directly but is developed to optimise part of the solution. The input of this model 
is therefore made up of a set of partially-built routes and a list of unassigned requests, building up 
the set of feasible arcs.

3.3.1. Representation of the problem 
The problem to solve is represented in Java by the abstract class AbstractProblemInstance and 
its subclasses which represent different versions of the problem. The classes contain the main 
information about the problem such as the list of requests to serve, the number and capacity of the 
available vehicles or the distance matrix for all the nodes. In order to have all the data necessary 
for the good functioning of the software at hand, the default constructor of the subclasses requires 
a file containing the information needed.  
Two additional classes are needed for the representation of the problem: Node and Request. To 
model the depot and the customers, the objects of the Node class have a unique ID, a location and 
time window limits while Request is designed to represent the pickup-and-delivery requests with 
the following parameters: request ID, pickup node, delivery node, demand, service duration at 
both nodes, time limit on the delivery, previous ID and following ID. The last two parameters are 
precedence indications created to link a delivery and a subsequent back visit for a given 
customer, and therefore indicate whether another request should be performed before starting this 
one. The time limit on the delivery is set in order to let enough time to return to the customer 
when a back visit is planned. All those parameters should be accessible from the file reader and 
created at the same time as the problem.  
It is important to note that the classes Node, Request, AbstractProblemInstance and its 
subclasses are data of the problem and as such shouldn’t be overwritten once they are created. 
For pre-processing purposes, AbstractProblemInstance has an abstract method ShrinkTW() 
which cuts the infeasible parts of the time window. Such reductions occur when the node is 
located in an area with an access time restriction or to match the time windows of a pickup and its 
delivery. For example, if the time window of a delivery starts at 8:00 while the time window of 
its corresponding pickup starts only at 9:00 and the minimum driving time between the two nodes 
is 30 minutes, the start of the delivery time window can be postponed to 9:30. The class also has 
methods to create a distance matrix from coordinates if necessary and to save the problem data to 
a file to be used by the MIP solver CPLEX. Additionally, all the classes have default constructors 
and methods to access their parameters from external classes.

4. Presentation of the algorithm 
4.1. The adaptive search 
As mentioned in paragraph 2.2.3, an adaptive version of the LNS heuristic has been successfully 
applied to several versions of the VRP and PDP, and has been chosen to be implemented in this 
project. The framework of the heuristic used in the project and that will be presented in this 
section (4.1) is similar to the one Røpke and Pisinger have used in their projects (in [14] or [8] for 
example). The framework has been provided by DTU Transport at the beginning of the project, 
and adjustments needed to adapt it to the present project were performed. 
 
4.1.1. Exploration of the search space 
The core components of the LNS heuristic, and therefore of the adaptive version, are the 
destruction and repair methods which will be described in later sections. These methods represent 
the actual movement from neighbour to neighbour in the search space by modifying the solution 
at each iteration: first a destruction operator removes requests from the solution, which generates 
a neighbourhood to explore, and then a repair method reinsert them, thus choosing the most 
promising neighbour to visit or alternatively to stay put. 
Since several methods exist in the ALNS, a method is necessary to select the destroy-and-repair 
couple at each iteration. The method implemented in this project is a roulette wheel selection, 
which selects stochastically one operator of each kind in the list of available methods. At the 
beginning, each couple of destroy-repair methods has an equal weight, which is adjusted after 
each time it is used. The weight is increased if the pair has led to an improved or accepted 
solution in the last iteration and decreased otherwise. The method works as follows: first, a 
random number is selected between 0 and the sum of weights of all the pairs, and then the 
weights are cumulated one by one, always in the same order, until the random number is reached. 
The couple whose weight has been used last to reach this number is selected by the method. For 
example, assuming that the weight of each method is 1, if the random number is included 
between 0 and 1, the first method is selected, if it is between 1 and 2, the second is selected and 
so on. Therefore, a higher weight leads to a greater chance for the method to be chosen by the 
roulette wheel since it has a broader range of success. This means that after some iterations the 
selection process is biased to select with a higher probability the most successful methods without 
totally rejecting the less successful ones. The pseudo-code for this method is shown in Table 3.

4.2. Route construction 
In the context of Vehicle Routing Problems, a route is defined as a cycle starting at and returning 
to the depot. Therefore in the default constructor of a route, visits to the depot are added at the 
start and end of the route. Any other visit is restricted to be inserted between these two depots. 
 
4.2.1. Request insertion 
When inserting new requests to existing routes, the main idea is to ensure that the route stays 
feasible. Therefore the pickup and delivery visits building up the request are added during the 
same invocation of the insertion method and the route is evaluated every time the list of visits is 
modified. The insertion process occurs as follows: the pickup visit is first added, the route is then

4.3. Destruction operators 
In this section, the various destruction methods that were implemented in this project are 
presented. The destruction step is the first half of the neighbour operator in a LNS heuristic. As 
its name suggests, its goal is to destroy parts of the solution by removing some requests from the 
route plan in order to reinsert them later at a different place with the aim of improving the 
==PAGE:29==
 
Page 28 of 54 
 
 
 
solution cost. Since the reinsertion has to be feasible and given that removing only a pickup or a 
delivery without the complementary visit would lead to a reinsertion in the same route, the two 
visits composing a request are removed at the same time by the destruction operators.  
The number N of requests to remove is randomly selected between a lower and an upper limit at 
the beginning of the destruction step. Randomly choosing the number of requests to remove 
contributes to increasing the diversification of the local search since removing a different number 
of requests from a given solution implies that different neighbourhoods are explored even for a 
same destruction method. On the other hand, imposing bounds on this choice permits to control 
the size of the neighbourhood so as to find a balance between smooth navigation and fast 
exploration of the solution space. Indeed, removing too few requests constrains the search in a 
small neighbourhood which means that the next candidate solution has to be in close proximity of 
the current one whereas removing too many of them is likely to make the reinsertion more 
difficult and thus longer. After experimentation, the bounds have been set to 5 and 15 requests, 
which represent respectively 10% and 30% of the total for 50-request instances.

The algorithm starts with the selection of a reference customer, who is removed from the solution

4.4. The repair step 
After removing some requests from the candidate solution, the route plan becomes infeasible 
since those requests are no longer served. Therefore, in order to obtain the next solution, they 
have to be reinserted. Unlike the construction heuristic presented above, this step focuses on the 
solution quality, so the reinsertion methods should seek to minimise the total cost.  
Two structurally different approaches have been investigated and will be presented in this 
section: optimal and heuristic reinsertion of the requests. On one hand, the MIP solver CPLEX 
reinserts the requests optimally after the destruction step of the local search algorithm. The 
matheuristic, which combines heuristic search and exact solving, is used in order to improve on 
one hand the running time of the exact solver, and on the other hand the solution quality of the 
local search. On the other hand, the two heuristic repair methods are used with the advantages of 
providing faster solution and the potential for a greater diversification. Because the advantage of 
one method over the others is not certified a-priori, several versions of the software will be tested, 
using alternatively exact and heuristic repair methods. 
 
4.4.1. Exact repair 
The first approach that has been implemented is inspired from matheuristics. The idea is to 
reinsert optimally the removed requests by calling the CPLEX solver at each iteration in order to 
find the best neighbour given a partial solution based on the model introduced in 3.2. Because a 
call to the exact solver is often time-consuming, several techniques are used to speed up the 
reinsertion process.  
First of all, a warm start is given to the exact solver in order to reduce the running time of the 
repair step. The rationale behind this idea comes from the following realisation: the solution 
obtained at the end of a full iteration (destruction + reinsertion) is at least as good as the previous 
one since it is still in the repair neighbourhood of the partial solution. Therefore, giving this 
==PAGE:33==
 
Page 32 of 54 
 
 
 
previous solution as a starting point for the reinsertion process permits to speed up the search for 
a feasible solution and rejects straightaway worse solutions by allowing the cutting of branches of 
the search tree.  
Besides, the set of arcs that can be used in the solution is considerably reduced both for reasons 
of time efficiency and in accordance with the input from java. Indeed, the OPL model is used 
with partially-built routes and the assignment of some customers to vehicles as well as the order 
of those customers in the route are fixed. Therefore, the feasible arcs that are given to the solver 
can be sorted into two categories: the arcs already used in the partial solution and the arcs linking 
an unassigned customer to any customer or depot in the existing routes or another unassigned 
customer in any route. The arcs that are not feasible time-wise, meaning that the visits cannot be 
performed consecutively, are removed from the input set. For instance, if route k is composed of 
the following sequence of customers: {0, i, j, (n+1)}, then, the arc leaving from customer i 
imposed by constraint (1) in the model using vehicle k can only lead to either j or an unassigned 
customer with an appropriate time window. Additionally, the set of unassigned arcs is completed 
by arcs linking the two depots in unused routes. 
Both those factors contribute largely to reducing the running time of the reinsertion step. 
However, in some occasions, the reinsertion is very difficult and would still take a considerable 
amount of time. In order to cut the average time spent on reinserting the requests, an upper time 
limit of five minutes is put on the call to the solver. If the search is too long and reaches the time 
limit without finding an optimal reinsertion, the best solution found thus far is returned. Since the 
reinsertion process is warm started with the last candidate solution, the repair process will return 
in any case a feasible solution with an objective value at least as good as the one found at the 
previous iteration. It should be noted that the time limit has only been reached a negligible 
number of times and is thus not considered to have altered the matheuristic purpose.

4.4.3. Regret heuristic 
Although relatively fast, the previous method has a major flaw: the insertion of the hardest 
requests is postponed to the end of the process, and thus the last requests to be inserted might 
increase significantly the total cost since less insertion possibilities exist. To overcome this 
problem, a second heuristic method for reinsertion has been implemented called regret repair, 
which stores information about the two best insertion possibilities of each request. Once all the 
information is gathered, the request with the highest difference between the insertion cost of its 
best and second best insertion position is inserted. This tends to spread the cost better over the 
repair process in order to reduce the solution total cost after reinsertion. The method works 
similarly as the previous one, with the main difference that several arrays are necessary to store 
all the information needed instead of only a few parameters in the simple repair method. 
 
4.5. Solution feasibility verification 
An important point of the heuristic that has already been mentioned is the feasibility of the final 
solution. Obviously, the final state of the routing plan should be feasible according to the 
specified constraints in order to be an acceptable solution. One way of ensuring that and adding 
robustness to the heuristic is to check the feasibility of the solution at all times because a punctual 
infeasibility (especially an unplanned one) could propagate and finally lead to an infeasible 
output. The particularity of the LNS heuristic is to alternate between feasible and infeasible 
solutions when removing and then re-inserting the requests. Therefore, the feasibility of the 
current solution should only be tested at the end of the insertion phases of the heuristic.  
Two levels of evaluation have been implemented. The first, at the route level, happens every time 
a visit is added or removed. The route is checked for the following constraint violations: 
exceeding the vehicle capacity, making a service outside the time window, not planning enough 
time between the services of two consecutive customers, and not having a delivery after a pickup.

5.1. Instance generation 
In order to analyse the algorithm, five instances of 50 requests have been generated by randomly 
selecting a number of stores in the city of Copenhagen. Then, each instance has been adapted to 
match the parameters of the tests. An example of an instance generated is shown in Figure 4, 
where each blue circle represents a customer to serve and the depot is located on the outskirts of 
the city, at the bottom left of the picture. 
 
Figure 4: Example of a random instance generation 
The default parameters for the time window width have been set to 4h in the city centre where 
most of the nodes are located and 8h outside, all starting at time 0. Tighter time windows are a 
common practice in city centres where access time restrictions exist and allow deliveries only at 
specific times, usually in the morning until 11 or 12. On the other hand, time windows outside the 
city centre correspond to constraints imposed by shop opening times. It should be noted that for 
all scenarios, the depot has the same time windows as the customers outside the centre and 
therefore the routes are technically limited to 8 hours including loading and unloading at the 
depot, which corresponds to a normal working day. 
Moreover, since small vehicles are used in the city centre, the vehicle capacity has been set to 60 
units, while the demand is randomly distributed between 10 and 20 units, and the service time at 
customers is set to 10 minutes. The influence of the number of vehicles is not studied as part of

5.2. Parameter tuning and preliminary tests 
As already stated in the previous sections, the parameter tuning is important because it will have 
an impact on the performance of the program and the quality of the final solution. Indeed, ill-
chosen parameters might make the search harder or prevent some promising directions in the 
space exploration.  
For the tuning phase, a set of three representative instances of 50 requests have been used with

As highlighted by the graphs, the width of the time windows has an influence on the cost. This is 
mainly due to the fact that tighter time windows force the use of more vehicles in parallel since 
the same number of customers has to be serviced in a smaller amount of time. Indeed, in the 
scenario with large time windows, four vehicles are used on average in the final solution as 
opposed to six or seven in the scenario with tight time windows. On the other hand, the 
contribution of distance to the cost is similar in all scenarios. The vehicles being quite small, a 
vehicle round can serve only a limited amount of requests and therefore, even in the scenario with 
large time windows, frequent returns to the depot are necessary. The main difference between the 
scenarios is thus that a larger time horizon enables drivers to make multiple rounds with the same 
vehicle, which lowers the total number of vehicles used and therefore the objective function.  
However, the algorithm’s running time for 15,000 iterations is reduced by 63% on average when 
using tight time windows compared to the scenario with large time windows. This can be 
explained by the fact that tighter time windows reduce both the solution space and the 
neighbourhood size, and therefore makes the reinsertion of the requests easier. Nevertheless, even 
in the scenario with large time windows, the running time takes less than a half-hour, which is an 
acceptable duration for the generation of a routing plan.

As expected, adding back visits increases the total cost of the solution as well as the number of 
vehicles needed since more requests have to be served compared to the base scenario. However, 
when looking more closely, it can be noticed that the increase is not linear. Indeed, the average 
slope is steep in the beginning and tends to flatten out as the percentage of requests with back 
visit grows up to a certain point before exploding. This seems to exhibit the presence of a portion 
around 40% where the progression of cost is lower and above which the back visits become more 
expensive and therefore less attractive. This slow progression of the cost while adding back visits 
to the problem might reveal the existence of an ideal percentage of requests with back visits 
around 50%. However, the benefits of adding this back visits should be investigated more broadly 
and further testing should be performed in order to verify this fact.   
Likewise, the average running time is increasing with the number of back visits even though no 
clear behaviour can be predicted as it seems very specific to each instance. For example, the 
difference in running time between the 10% and the 30% scenario is significant but in reversed 
order for instances 2 and 4, while it is very low for instance 1. It could depend for example on the 
location of the customers who need a back visit.

6. Conclusion 
This report aimed at presenting city logistics concepts, along with an algorithm using the large 
neighbourhood search to solve instances using real-life data. In that regards, a literature review 
was conducted to gather knowledge on the various topics. Then, the problem was modelled as a 
pickup and delivery problem with time windows. Consequently, a program was developed to 
solve instances of this problem. Finally, the algorithm was tested both on benchmark instances 
widely studied in the literature and on newly-generated ones based on data representative of the 
city of Copenhagen. 
The algorithm developed as part of this report applied a Large Neighbourhood Search heuristic 
based on a simulated annealing framework. This metaheuristic navigates the solution space by 
subsequently removing and reinserting some requests in the solution. Different destruction and 
repair methods were considered. The destruction methods included random, critical and related 
request selection, whereas the repair methods were comprised of two heuristic and an exact 
reinsertion methods. 
This algorithm provided strong results on benchmark instances, achieving good robust solutions 
within a short time. Furthermore, the studies conducted on some parameters showed how these 
parameters influence the cost, which could help to determine the most beneficial scenario, and to 
approximate the cost of additional services. For instance, the relation between the percentage of 
requests with back visits and the cost does not seem to be proportional as the most beneficial 
scenario appears to be the one with a percentage of approximately 50%. 
The work of this report could be supplemented by other studies that were not performed due to 
time constraints. For instance, the addition of a second echelon in the city centre or the use of 
additional types of vehicles such as refrigerated trucks could be investigated. Moreover, the 
results observed in this report should be confirmed by additional experiments and investigated 
more broadly. In particular, all the tests have been performed on instances of 50 customers, and 
included only deliveries from the depot and back visits to customers. The study could be 
extended by generating instances with a variable number of customers or by including other types 
of request such as pickups and deliveries between customers.
