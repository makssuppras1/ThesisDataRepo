Summary
Generative adversarial networks (GANs) are probably one the most relevant
machine learning breakthroughs in the recent years.
The initial goal of this
thesis is to analyse GANs in detail, aiming for them to become interpretable
models.
In the context of image generation, a preliminary study on the combination
of GANs and active learning as means to improve the performance of existing
models is conducted. Following the uncertainty region principle, a new model
introducing an oracle in the training loop is proposed.
This innovative framework is tested on a variety of sets, ranging from a simple
Gaussian toy example, to a complex human faces image collection. The perfor-
mance of the suggested GAN variation is assessed with a variety of evaluation
metrics, including the most widespread and commonly used. From these results,
the pros and cons of such quality measures are critically reviewed.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Machines capable of understanding and modelling the world we live in, that has
been the promise of deep learning for ages. Until not so long ago, discrimina-
tive models had all the attention to steer in that direction. In the recent years
though, the resurgence of generative models has fostered an unprecedented in-
terest in the topic within the research community.
Ever since its introduction in 2014, the generative adversarial network (GAN)
framework [1] presented by Goodfellow has marked a milestone in machine learn-
ing. To put it simply, a GAN is a game between a generator and a discriminator.
The former aims at perfectly replicating some given data, while the latter tries
not to be fooled by the generated model. Both players are then improved driven
by competition. The striking success of GANs has allowed to represent prob-
ability distributions over any data kind, such as images, text or even audio,
opening the door to countless artiﬁcial applications. Some of the most promis-
ing results tackle extremely challenging tasks, such as realistic image generation
[2] [3], image-to-image translation [4], music generation [5] or even medical data
generation [6] [7].
As any other brilliant idea, generative adversarial nets do not come without
ﬂaws. To quote some, widely reported weak points are instability, loosely deﬁned
architecture or demanding synchronism between generator and discriminator.
A plethora of GAN variations have been proposed trying to solve these incon-
veniences. Likewise, a great deal of modiﬁcations have been made aiming at
improving the current models, as it still remains a diﬃcult task to synthesise
realistic samples on high dimensional datasets.
==PAGE:18==
2
Introduction
In spite of the myriad of ways to upgrade the models, there is still one ﬁeld
that is still unexplored: active learning. The ﬁrst contribution of this work is a
preliminary study of the inclusion of a human in the GAN training loop. From
a theoretical approach, the potential beneﬁts of a GAN active learning proposal
are assessed. Since it is so far the most popular application, the focus is set on
image generation.
With many available models, a valid and accurate metric for fair comparison is
indispensable. Nevertheless, there is almost a complete lack of consensus in this
respect. It is, to this day, notoriously diﬃcult to ﬁnd a measure which provides
meaningful results in distinct scenarios. The most immediate proof of evalu-
ation metrics lagging behind theoretical progress is that the most widespread
techniques are also the most criticised. The second contribution of this project
is then to study the existing quality measures, apply them to the proposed
methods and attempt to shed a light upon their real pros and cons.
The ultimate goal of deep learning is to mimic human intelligence, and gen-
erative models have the potential to do so.
Research directions are not yet
exhausted, as there is always room for improvement. However, a new machine
learning breakthrough can only be achieved through a parallel research on eval-
uation methods.
1.1
State of the Art
Generative adversarial networks were ﬁrst introduced by Ian Goodfellow in 2014,
[1]. The aim behind this framework was for deep generative models to match
the striking success of deep learning in artiﬁcial intelligence applications. In
the process, two models are trained simultaneously: a generative model G and
a discriminative model D. Together, they play a minimax two-player game, in
which G aims to replicate real data while D learns to determine whether a sam-
ple comes from the real or generated distribution. The promised advantages
of this framework relate primarily to the needed computational requirements.
Moreover, they are also an alternative to maximum likelihood estimation strate-
gies and avoid the use of Markow chains. However, instability is a well known
problem of GANs. Indeed, there is still little explanation about this unstable be-
haviour. The need of precise synchronism between generator and discriminator
training, together with a careful choice of architecture, parameter initialization
and selection of hyper-parameters, are the main reasons why this framework is
so fragile [8]. Several approaches to tackle this problem have been proposed,
including regularization [8], feature matching, minibatch discrimination or his-
torical averaging [9], or even a new class of architectures, robust to most settings,

such as Deep Convolutional GANs [10].
To overcome the diﬃculties that GANs and deep learning itself present, one
could think of introducing human in the loop (HITL) models in their frame-
works. So far, the literature describing such solution is quite limited. A re-
markable approach concerning deep learning can be found in [11]. In a process
similar to active learning, [11] proposes a new framework to interpret large-scale
image datasets. This procedure integrates human eﬀort into a sequential image
classiﬁcation pipeline. First, images are randomly drawn from a set. Next, these
images are labelled through crowdsourcing, using a custom Amazon Mechanical
Turk Interface (AMT). The labelled subset then trains a binary classiﬁer, which
is afterwards run on both unlabelled and labelled samples. The classiﬁer out-
puts scores for the images, for which they can be classiﬁed as positive, negative
or unknown, the latter for the ambiguous samples. These unknown images are
given back to the set and the pipeline runs again iteratively until the number
of ambiguous samples is low enough to be manually labelled.
Delving once again into GANs, the existing research on HITL applied to such
framework is even scarcer. One particularly interesting example for the purposes
of this work is found in [12]. In the context of image generation, it is especially
important that the output of the generator is not only semantically meaning-
ful, but also aesthetically pleasing. Being said so, [12] proposes including an
objective function in the generative model which optimises the data generated.
This function is a generic Positive Interaction Rate (PIR). In order to do so,
a ‘PIR estimation model’ is introduced in the generative model loop. Then,
the generated images and human interaction upon themselves train such model.
The output of the estimator is fed to the generator, integrating the expectation
of the estimated PIR to the loss function of the generator. The overall process
was moderately successful at targeting diﬀerent visual features for the generated
images. Moreover, the amount of training data used was signiﬁcantly lower to
previous experiments with other settings. These results suggest that the ap-
proach presented in [12] may be also applicable to other ﬁelds, such as sound
generation.
Another closely related approach can be found in [13]. Stated by the authors as
the ﬁrst alike contribution, this work presents a new approach combining GANs
and active learning, which has been given the name of Generative Adversarial
Active Learning (GAAL). In particular, their setting combines query sampling
with uncertainty principle.
The active learning part of the procedure is in
this case included in the generator model.
With a trained generator on all
unlabelled data, the labelled data is randomly initialised by drawing a small
fraction of the data. Next, a optimisation problem to ﬁnd the uncertainty region
is solved. The solutions of such problem are the new inputs to the generator,
whose output is labelled by a human oracle. This labelled data can then be

Dubbed categorical generative adversarial networks (CatGAN), the framework
presented in [14] aims to generalise the original work in [1] to multiple class
generation and discrimination. The most remarkable particularity of this work
is the inclusion of a semi-supervised scheme. By adapting the original Goodfel-
low’s formulation of the problem, the multiclass adaptation is achieved, being
later extended to the aforementioned semi-supervised setting. Interestingly, the
evaluation of the framework shows that the multiple class extension yields a
slightly inferior performance than state of the art results when implemented in
an unsupervised setting. However, switching to a semi-supervised framework
reports competitive results and, in the context of images, produces visually
pleasing pictures.
The increasing interest in generative models has lead to a substantial theoret-
ical progress. While there are countless contributions to improving the GAN
framework, there has been little research on the evaluation metrics of such mod-
els. Moreover, the large number of GAN applications favours lack of consensus
regarding which evaluation measure is most suitable. Ideally, evaluation met-
rics should be able to account for the classiﬁable properties and diversity of
the generated samples, as well as detecting overﬁtting.
A diverse generated
ensemble means that the new overall class probability distribution is evenly dis-
tributed, while highly classiﬁable samples are sharp, quality images which can
be conﬁdently interpreted as real [15].
Being an implicit approach, GANs generate data drawing samples from the true
probability distribution, rather than explicitly constructing the density itself.
Taking that into consideration, it is no surprise that the average log-likelihood
is still the most commonly used criteria to assess the performance of GANs [16].
The signiﬁcance of this metric is such that it has been widely chosen in diﬀerent
GAN frameworks with distinct applications [1][17][18]. Computed by ﬁtting a
Parzen window to the generated samples [19], this method was described in [1]
as the best available technique, in spite of its restricted performance in high
dimensional spaces.
Furthermore, log-likelihood favours models which store
training samples, being unable to detect overﬁtting.
Along the same lines, two statistical similarity measures, the Kullback-Leibler
divergence [20] and the Jensen-Shannon divergence [21], are also frequently
found as evaluation metrics. In this case, both measures share the same draw-
back as average log-likelihood, as they need to be obtained with density estima-

Another widespread technique to assess the performance of unsupervised repre-
sentation algorithm such as generative models is to use the generated samples
as feature extractors on supervised datasets. In other words, the classiﬁcation
performance of GANs is regarded as an evaluation metric.
A notable usage
of this indirect technique can be found in [10], where their DCGAN model is
trained on Imagenet-1k, using later the discriminator’s convolutional features
from all layers to train a regularized linear L2-SVM classiﬁer. The classiﬁcation
performance obtained for the CIFAR-10 dataset is superior to that of various
K-means implementations, being surpassed only by that of Exemplar CNNs
[25]. According to the authors, since their DCGAN model was actually never
trained on CIFAR-10, these results also prove the robustness of their imple-
mentation. In the same work, a similar procedure is also applied to the SVHN
dataset, achieving once more state of the art performance. Another example
of this same procedure is found in [26], in which the authors extend the GAN
framework to tackle the problem of 3D object generation.
Generating realistic images is an obvious goal for GAN implementations. Clas-
siﬁers can then be used not only indirectly as was just discussed, but also as a
direct measure of visual quality: the more true-to-life a generated image is, the
higher its classiﬁcation score. This evaluation technique can be seen, for instance
in [27], where the authors make use of Places-AlexNet [28] to assess the quality
of the generated images. In particular, it is the norm of the softmax output,
representing the maximum probability, which accounts for the accuracy of the
produced representations. Two last representative cases of this strategy can be
found in [29] and [4]. In both cases, a score yielded by two diﬀerent networks,
VGG [30] and FCN [31], measures the quality of the generated images.
A closely related approach in [27] suggests an image retrieval measure to eval-
uate the performance of GANs. In this case, the metric is obtained from the
nearest neighbour distance between a validation set and their generated im-
ages. Here, the proposed framework consists of an ensemble of GANs. Same
as average log-likelihood methods, evaluation on nearest neighbours has been
discouraged in [16]. A small experiment performed by the authors of this last
work shows that minor pixel shifts can radically change the output of nearest
neighbours based on Euclidean distance. As a consequence, the method is not
completely reliable to assess visual quality. This deﬀect can be easily overcome
by using perceptual distance metrics instead. Moreover, overﬁtting does not
always necessarily mean that the model is replicating images from the available
test dataset. Therefore, a distance based, whether Euclidean or perceptual, will
not be helpful in identifying overﬁtting.

If there is any other technique as widely spread as average log-likelihood, that
is the Inception Score (IS), proposed by [9]. Based on the deep convolutional
network Inception [32], pre-trained on ImageNet [33], combines two diﬀerent
criteria to evaluate samples. In the ﬁrst place, the conditional label distribution
p(y|x) is expected to present low entropy as an indicator of high quality samples.
On the other hand, the marginal distribution p(y) should have high entropy,
account for large diversity.
The KL divergence of the two measures is then
computed, obtaining the IS which, according to the authors, correlates with
human judgement. As any of the other evaluation metrics presented, the IS
is not exempt from ﬂaws. To begin with, it shares log-likelihood drawbacks,
such as inability to detect overﬁtting. In addition, using a pre-trained network
on ImageNet, this score favours object generation rather than realistic images.
Finally and as shown in [34], IS is quite sensitive to image resolution and an
asymmetric measure. As discussed, since its presentation in [9], IS has been
extensively used to assess the performance of diﬀerent GAN models as, for
instance, [35] and [36].
In relation to images, there is a number of quality measures coming from other
ﬁelds, such as image coding and compression, that can also be useful in the
task of performance evaluation.
The most commonly found metrics are the
structural similarity (SSIM) [37], peak signal-to-noise ratio (PSNR) and the
sharpness diﬀerence (SD) [34]. As these metrics represent direct comparisons
between ground-truth and generated images, they provide a straightforward
visual examination of the performance of a given GAN model. However, the
actual ground-truth is an inﬁnite set of images. Consequently, these measures
are restricted to the extent of the available training datasets. These metrics have
also a prominent presence in the existing literature, examples can be found in
[7], [38] and [39]
The last, and perhaps most intuitive, way of analysing the quality of GAN
generated images is direct human evaluation. There are as many evaluation
approaches as diﬀerent GAN applications.
For example, some works follow
the Visual Turing Test [40] to design their interfaces [9], [36]. An important
distinction in these methods comes from how long the images are shown to
the annotators for. Based on prior studies proving that humans can rapidly
retain scene characteristics ([41], [42]), some evaluation techniques restrict the
exposure time ([17], [43]), as some authors claim that accuracy is a function of
viewing time [17]. Finally, while some studies such as 52 rely on a limited number
of volunteers, most of them automate this process with Amazon Mechanical
Turk [9] [36] [43] [44] [45]. The principal advantage of this approach is that
the obtained accuracy is, of course, perfectly correlated with the human visual
system. However, it is certainly time-consuming and can be unreliable, since it
does not speak for the quality of the generated model in terms of, for example,
overﬁtting.

2.1.1
Theoretical background
Discriminative models have become increasingly important in the context of
deep learning. Backpropagation and dropout algorithms, using piecewise linear
units, have favoured this success. On the contrary, generative models fail to
leverage these procedures. The term generative describes any model that, given
a training set with pdata distribution, is able to estimate such distribution, usu-
ally referred to as pmodel. From this result, generative models may be able to
replicate the true distribution explicitly, generate samples from pmodel or both.
The adversarial framework, introduced by Ian Goodfellow et al. in 2014 [1], is a
minimax two-player game, in which two models are trained in order to estimate
generative models. In the context of generative adversarial networks, GANs,
these two models are known as generative and discriminative. The generative
model, G, aims to learn the data distribution, while the discriminative model, D,
intends to accurately determine whether a sample came from the true data dis-
tribution or G. The two adversaries are driven to improve their methods until G
replicates the true model and D cannot distinguish between both distributions.
The aforementioned models work via the principle of maximum likelihood esti-
mation (MLE). Speciﬁcally, the framework indirectly interacts with probability

The two gradient descent optimisation algorithms shown in this section have
exhibited particular behaviours. It has been widely suggested in the literature
that iteratively decreasing the step size improves the performance of the algo-
rithm by avoiding overshooting in the vicinity of the minimum [46] [47]. Indeed,
the update of the step size here presented was inspired by the Adagrad algo-
rithm. Adagrad modiﬁes the step size in each iteration, dividing its value by the
square root of the sum of the squares of the gradients w.r.t the parameters to
be optimised. Instead, the step size is here decreased by the square root of the
current iteration plus a ﬁxed, tunable, term. It is interesting to note that both
versions perform much worse without the square root operation [46]. A review
of diﬀerent gradient descent algorithm also hints that a diﬀerent step size value
for the diﬀerent parameters to be optimised yields an increased performance, as
was implemented in this section [46].
Regarding the use of momentum, it has also been reported in the literature
that a higher momentum term may lead to oscillations and instability [10].
This result was seen in this section, proving that although accelerating training
somehow, higher momentum values might produce poorer results than those
obtained with SGD.

From the wide variety of gradient descent algorithms described in the literature,
Adam, [46][48], is arguably one of the most extensively used [10][49][50]. This
method, similarly to momentum, adapts the learning rate as an exponentially
decaying average of past gradients. Once again, this reﬂects the need for decreas-
ing the step size with the number of iterations. Moreover, the fact that Adam
relies on an exponential rule to reduce the learning rate further explains why a
non-linear operation such as the aforementioned square root operation is useful
and necessary in this context. Being less sensitive to initialisation and able to
adapt the learning rate, Adam is usually chosen over SGD with momentum.
The original GAN scheme described in [1], recommends running more steps of
optimisation in one of the players than the other. However, in practice one step
for each player has been found to work best [49]. This result is also supported
by the example presented in this section, in which alternating one optimisation
iteration of each model was found to be suﬃcient to achieve the optimal result.
2.1.2
Neural networks in GANs
Beyond the extremely simple example presented in the previous section, the
generator and discriminator functions in a GAN framework are typically repre-
sented by deep neural networks. Indeed, the models are presented as multilayer
perceptrons in [1]. The wide variety of networks used in the literature is ex-
plained by the loose requirements of GANs. The only conditions that need to
be met are that G must be diﬀerentiable and the dimension of z has to be at
least as large as that of x in order to have full support in the x space [49].
Mostly motivated by image representation and generation, the architectures
used in GANs have been constantly evolving.
Also, the well-know training
instability of early GANs inspired research on new architectures able to overcome
this signiﬁcant ﬂaw. The work of Radford et al., presented in [10], proposes a
class of convolutional networks which allow stable training, suitable for deeper
generative models. This architecture is known as DCGAN, ‘deep, convolution
GAN’, and it constitutes the grounds on which most GANs are currently based
on.
DCGAN adopts the all-convolutional network presented in [51]. Prior to this
work, most CNNs alternated convolution and max-pooling layers. The necessity
of such complicated schemes was questioned in [51], reaching the conclusion that
a much simpler solution, replacing pooling with strided convolution, was able
to achieve the same state of the art performance. Strided convolutions were
proved to be as useful when seeking spatial dimensionality reduction, aimed in
order to cover larger parts of the input in higher layers.

The next guideline in DCGAN is to eliminate fully connected layers. In such a
layer, the neurons are fully connected to every activation in the previous layer.
Following the trend of global average pooling as a regulizer to avoid overﬁtting,
[52], DCGAN ﬁnds an intermediate solution in which the highest convolutional
features are connected to the input and output of generator and discriminator,
respectively.
The problems related to initialisation and gradient ﬂow already seen in the pre-
vious section are solved by batch normalization [53]. In this way, learning is
stabilised by standardising the input to each input. However, it is important
to note that the ﬁrst and last layers are not normalised. This prevents insta-
bility and also allows the model to learn the mean and scale of the true data
distribution.
Regarding activation functions, every layer in the generator except the last
one, which employs the hyperbolic tangent (tanh), uses ReLU activation [54].
Another important diﬀerence between the original GAN work [1] and DCGAN,
is that maxout activations in the discriminator are replaced by leaky rectiﬁed
activations [55].
As an example of the DCGAN architecture, the layout of the generator used for
the LSUN dataset [11] is depicted in Fig. 2.18.

needing preprocessing. The only step followed prior to training is a rescaling of

2.2
Active Learning
Learning algorithms often need to be trained on thousands of labelled examples.
In practice, these instances are sometimes diﬃcult, time-consuming and costly
to obtain.
Active learning is a subﬁeld of machine learning which proposes
querying unlabelled samples to be labelled by a human annotator, often known
as oracle. In this way, the algorithm is able to learn from chosen instances,
reducing the amount of sample data required and increasing its accuracy.
Three diﬀerent active learning settings are described in the literature, namely
membership query synthesis, stream-based selective sampling and pool-based
active learning [56]
In the ﬁrst scenario, membership query synthesis [57], an oracle is asked whether
an unlabelled instance is a member of an unknown set. The unlabelled sample
can be either part of the input space, or a newly generated instance [56]. This
setting may initially seem appropriate in the context of GANs, in which one of
the player’s aim is precisely to generate distribution-alike samples. However, in
the early steps of the algorithm, when the samples generated are far from the
ground truth, the oracle rejects generated images too easily. In other words,
the requested queries turn out to be not informative enough, not helping the
algorithm improve and also adding the computational and time eﬀort of querying
the oracle.
Stream-based selective sampling [58] represents a scenario which aims to over-
come the limitations of the previous one. The name of this approach arises from
the fact that, in this case, samples are individually drawn and queried. The key
point of this approach is that this queried samples are selectively chosen accord-
ing to some predeﬁned measure, i.e. the learner sequentially draws samples from
the input distribution and decides whether they are suﬃciently informative to be
==PAGE:45==
2.2 Active Learning
29
queried. To do so, the crucial step if of course to set the needed informativeness
measures. There are numerous strategies to tackle this issue. Examples of such
measures are Fisher information ratio or estimated error reduction. Another
approach is to establish an uncertainty region in which falls every instance the
learner is not sure about. Those ambiguous samples within the region are the
only ones queried. By doing so, the region of uncertainty is sequentially reduced
and thus the learner model can be further improved.
The approach of pool-based active learning [59] is similar to that of stream-
based selective sampling. In this case, instead of sequentially sampling instances,
queries are drawn from a pool of unlabelled samples. In this setting, the infor-
mativeness of ambiguous instances is computed in a bulk. Then, the most obvi-
ous limitation of this scenario is the computational requirements of computing
that measure of uncertainty, which might me excessive for certain applications.
From this entire set, pool-based active learning selects the best query. Con-
trarily, stream-based decides individually whether a query is ﬁt to use or not.
Although not strictly necessary, the pool of queries is assumed to be static [56].
The procedure to be followed in this work is inspired by active learning. This
brief study of the three main scenarios provides a useful starting point to the
inclusion of an oracle in the GAN model.

2.3
GANs and human in the loop
Active learning has inspired many other processes in the context of deep learn-
ing.
Human in the loop is the name given to models which require human
interaction, or beneﬁt from it. As discussed in section 1.1, this procedure is still
scarcely described in the literature, specially regarding GANs. The big question
here is if GANs can leverage human interaction and, if so, how can it be done.
Before delving into more sophisticated applications, the usefulness of introduc-
ing an oracle in the GAN framework will be assessed in the previously reported
Gaussian example. In this case, the oracle cannot be though as a human for ob-
vious reasons. In the context of images or audio, human assistance can actually
help improve the quality of generated samples, as we humans are the best possi-
ble judges. Nonetheless, a human eye can hardly distinguish between a true and
a generated Gaussian sample. Relating this statement to the aforementioned
example, in which the aim was to model the mean of a Gaussian distribution,
when the discriminator itself is in doubt due to the generated mean being close
to the true one, a human oracle will not be more accurate than the discriminator
and thus, will be of no use. Consequently, in this scenario the oracle must be
the algorithm itself.
The discriminator output represents a probability, an indicator of how accu-
rately the generator is synthesising true data samples. Whenever this output
approaches the value 0.5, then the generator can be said to be achieving its
purpose. However, can it further improve its performance? If in doubt, the dis-
criminator can be informed that the distribution it is looking at actually comes
from the generator. Consequently, it could be expected that the generator will
try harder to fool its counterpart. This “hint” means nothing more than a man-
ual modiﬁcation of the output of the discriminator, i.e. substituting 0.5 by 0.
In this way, the discriminator is informed that, although precise, the data it is
looking at that does not correspond to the true distribution.
This approach can be easily related to the previously described active learning
scenarios. First and foremost, the strategy does not correspond to a membership
query synthesis, as the decision of which instances are to be queried is far from
arbitrary.
Indeed, by taking an interest in samples which are nearly fooling
the discriminator, the oracle model is eﬀectively selecting the most informative
samples. With regard to images, which are the focus of the presented algorithms,
any representation managing to produce a discriminator output close to 0.5
ought to be thought as the most representative example of generated image.
In that case, the discriminator is learning from the trickiest images, forcing
the generator to produce even better instances. On the contrary, poor quality
images which clearly amount to gibberish to human eye are uninformative, as the
==PAGE:47==

discriminator already knows that those are, in fact, generated and not ground
truth.

tive sampling or pool-based active learning. With reference to GANs, and more
speciﬁcally to the DCGAN architecture, as the model is trained in mini-batches,
the proposed approach can be though of as pool-based active learning.

As a ﬁnal remark about this approach, there are some questions to be resolved
about the method. First and probably most importantly, it is not mentioned how

six human annotators so it is safe to assume that they did not resort to any crowd

have been conducted. Of course, human labelling is a time consuming process
and such eﬀort, even in the case of only six participants, must be taken into
account. Also, it is not completely clear whether the generator is trained only
with the labelled samples or with the combination of labelled and unlabelled
samples after the ﬁrst iteration. Lastly, it would have been interesting to see
the performance of their method over the complete multilabel MNIST dataset.
All in all, GAAL is an innovative work which has served as inspiration for the
previously suggested GAN+oracle procedure. The main similarity between both
is the use of a region of uncertainty. Whereas GAAL makes use of a SVMactive
algorithm, GAN+oracle simpliﬁes that step by considering the ambiguous in-
stances those which fall within a ﬁxed range of the discriminator output. Also,
the de novo generated samples are the ones used to query the oracle in both
algorithms. The main and obvious distinction between both methods is that,
while GAAL does include a human annotator in the training process, the oracle
previously discussed is just a ’hint’ given by the model itself.
The extension of DCGAN+oracle to an actual human in the loop model is
pretty straightforward. Instead of having the training process setting to zero
the probability of a generated image being considered real by the discriminator,
a human observer inspects the generated image and tells the algorithm whether
it is real or synthesised. In this way, the algorithm aims at generating images
that are accurate according to the HVS.

2.4
Evaluation
Despite the countless GAN variations found all over the literature and the great
excitement within the research community that they cause, there is still lack of
consensus as to which is the most appropriate evaluation metric.
Although this disagreement is widely acknowledged, it can be said that there
is little research trying to solve the problem. Indeed, literature criticising the
existing methods is much more spread than works proposing new or improved
techniques. As a result, it remains a complicated task to fairly compare models.
Evaluation metrics can be divided in two main groups: quantitative and quali-
tative measures. Quantitative metrics are considered to be objective, as they do
not involve human direct assessment in any way. Although saving the time eﬀort
that human evaluation requires, this has the obvious counter eﬀect of lacking
perceptual meaning. On the other hand, user studies might miss critical model
ﬂaws such as overﬁtting, mode dropping or low diversity. Moreover, ﬁnding ev-
idence of a model outperforming others via qualitative assessment would imply
having the same experiment, under the same conditions, run at large scale with
an overly high number of human observers.
This section describes a collection of the most prominent existing evaluation
metrics. Their description is followed by a review of their main advantages and
disadvantages. Also and since these are the metrics used for the subsequent
experiments, their application is shortly explained.
2.4.1
Quantitative
2.4.1.1
Indirect Sampling Likelihood
Indirect sampling likelihood (ISL) is the method used by Goodfellow in [1] to
compare the performance of his GAN framework to that of other deep generative
models. To the author’s knowledge, ISL is the best available method which
manages to describe how accurate the generated distribution is compared to the
ground truth. Introduced by Breuleux et al. in [61], ISL was originaly meant
as a framework in which two diﬀerent sampling methods, Herding [62] and Fast

method was proposed as a means to estimate the quality of a generative’s model
distribution, aiming to avoid the subjectivity of visual assessment, which may
also be biased by overﬁtting. The ISL method is summarised in the original
work as follows:
1. Generate samples from the trained generative model
2. Use either generated samples or training plus generated samples to train
a density model, P, for which the likelihood can be computed.
3. Compute the likelihood of the test set under P.

kernel density estimator, namely a Gaussian Parzen window. Measuring the
likelihood of the test set under P provides an idea of how "far’ the generated

erage’ of the test set. The approximation of the density function given a set of

An important observation, not explicitly mentioned in any of the works which
use ISL as a quality measure, is that before computing it, it is necessary to
rescale the images to [0,1] if x ∈[0, 255]. It is also pointed out in [61] that
it is a good practice to add random noise, u ∈[0, 255], to the image before
estimating ISL. However, there is no allusion to this procedure in the studied
literature.Then, random noise has not been added in this work when computing
ISL.
The drawbacks of this estimation technique are well-know. First of all, the num-
ber of samples used to compute ISL is critical, as a higher number of instances,
due to the central limit theorem, gives a more accurate estimation. This prob-
lem, which is already evident in low dimensional spaces, is further worsened
because of the curse of dimensionality.
Also and in relation to the computation of ISL, the original proposal in [61]
argues about the inclusion of the training set while obtaining the density model.
With generated samples only, the evolution of the obtained ISL curve with
respect to the number of generated samples is an indicator of the quality of
the mixing process. Indeed, poor mixing and a smaller amount of generated
samples yields a slower slope, as the test set cannot be expected to be any
similar to the generated samples. Regarding the inclusion of training samples
in the density estimation, it is an important step in order to avoid favouring
overﬁtted models. A small number of generated samples of an overﬁtted model
will obtain an excessively high ISL. However, including training samples in the
computation of the density models prevents this unwanted eﬀect from occurring.
In spite of this recommendation, as it has not been followed in any work in the
studied literature, ISL is computed in this work using exclusively generated
images.
The greatest detractor of the use of average loglikelihood as a measure of quality
is undoubtedly [16]. In order to provide a deeper insight as to why this evaluation
metric could actually be inappropriate, two of the toy examples presented in [16]
will be here reproduced. There are two diﬀerent scenarios that show that ISL
can fail to assess the quality of a generated distribution.

the scale of the Gaussian kernel is chosen overly small, the obtained loglikelihood
will be very poor irrespective of how plausible the generated images are. The
second example is precisely the contrary scenario: high loglikelihood for low
quality samples. Considering a mixture model, composed of a good model with

2.4.1.2
Inception Score
Inception score (IS) is a quality measure proposed in [9] and widely accepted as
one of the most suitable evaluation metrics for generative models. In comparison
to the previously discussed approach, ISL, IS is a rather indirect method to
assess image quality, as it does not approximate the likelihood straight from the
generated samples. On the contrary, the statistics of the generated batch are
obtained from the output of a pre-trained neural network. In this case, such
model is the deep convolutional network Inception [32], hence the name of the
metric.
The Inception network was designed to achieve state of the art classiﬁcation
results in the ImageNet Large-Scale Visual Recognition Challenge 2014. The
main motivations between this network are eﬃciency and practicality.
The
result of such design was a network with signiﬁcantly reduced computational
costs, with respect to other notable networks such as VGGNet [30]. Taking into
account only the layers with parameters, Inception consists of 22 layers. To that
number, 5 pooling layers must be added.

Same as average log-likelihood or any other evaluation metric, IS is not exempt
from limitations. The ﬁrst weak point of this evaluation metric is that, being
trained for the ImageNet dataset, Inception may not perform satisfactorily for
other sets. In this sense, the IS for images depicting objects rather than more
realistic scenarios are favoured since those are similar to the original ImageNet
samples. Moreover, ImageNet contains far more classes than some of the most
commonly used datasets, such as CIFAR. In that case, the conditional distri-
bution has a higher entropy than it actually should have. In general, using a
trained network on a completely diﬀerent dataset is regarded as a poor choice
[65].
Another inconvenience of this technique is that there is a great deal of diﬀerent
Inception implementations. A slight alteration of the network weights typically
does not aﬀect the classiﬁcation accuracy. However, this does have an impact
on the obtained IS. This interesting eﬀect has been reported in [65] and it is
also worth noting that some examples of usage in the literature do not specify
which network was used, as for example in [35].
A remarkable downside of IS is that it fails to report overﬁtting and a GAN
replicating training samples can easily fool the score. The IS is usually given in
the literature in comparison to other state of the art results. A better idea, in
combination with such study, is to use the IS obtained from the validation set
[65].
An unwanted event directly related to diversity in the GAN framework is mode
collapse, meaning that the generator output is restricted to limited classes, even
repeatedly replicating the same sample. While [34] claims that IS is not able

1, since in that case p(y) = p(y | x).

Structural similarity index (SSIM)
An ever-present concern with objective visual quality assessment methods is
their ability to match the human visual perception. Some of the most well-
known and widely used measures, such as MSE and PSNR, reportedly fail to
replicate the human vision system (HVS) [68].
Mainly two ideas inspired the authors of [37] to deﬁne SSIM. First of all, images
are known for its strong spatial dependencies. Secondly, it has been assumed
that the HVS is highly suited to extract structural features from images.
For the purpose of extracting the structural information, three (relatively) inde-
pendent features are combined. The illumination of an scene is independent of
its structure. Also, how an image is perceived depends on relative, rather than
absolute, luminance change. Therefore, the ﬁrst component of SSIM is a lumi-
nance comparison between the two images that are being considered, deﬁned
as:
l(x, y) = 2µxµy + C1
µ2x + µ2y + C1
(2.61)
where x and y are the two scenes, µx and µy are their mean intensities, and C1
is a constant included in order to prevent the algorithm from crashing when the
sum of the mean intensities is close to zero.

Given the local dependencies of pixels with respect to their neighbours, it is no
surprise that image quality is best described locally rather than globally. Indeed,
all the previously presented expressions are meant to be used in neighbourhood
of pixels, and not the whole of the scene. The implementation chosen in this
work, available in the Image Processing Toolbox of Matlab, is computed using
local statistics.
One last important remark regarding this metric, is the set of conditions which
are to be met.
1. SSIM is symmetric, SSIM(x, y) = SSIM(y, x).
2. SSIM is bounded from above, SSIM(x, y) ≤1, but it can also take on
negative values.
3. SSIM is a unique representation, and only equals 1 if and only if the two
images in consideration are exactly the same (x = y).
Regarding the experiments carried out in this work, some of the involved datasets
contain several and highly diﬀerentiated classes. The generated images to be
compared are not always verbatim copies of the ground truth, which does not
mean that they are poor representations. In fact, acquiring literal copies of the
original data could potentially hint an overﬁtted model. Further, within classes,
ground truth images also diﬀer. Indeed, even in the case of the MNIST dataset,
a great deal of diﬀerent depictions of the same ﬁgure exist. Therefore, measures
such as SSIM and PSNR, which directly measure the error between images,
should be applied carefully. As a matter of fact, SSIM and PSNR are only used
to assess the quality of images belonging to datasets with diﬀerentiated and
ﬁxed classes.
For this measures to be representative of the overall quality of a certain method,
they should only be computed between images representing equal classes. For
example, it would not make much sense to compute how diﬀerent a represen-
tation of a ‘9’ is from a ‘2’, as they are naturally disparate. Since the volume
of data being dealt with is quite large, it would be even more computationally
extensive to take into account data labels when computing SSIM or PSNR. For
that reason, SSIM and PSNR are calculated over the entire test and generated
==PAGE:71==
2.4 Evaluation

datasets. It is assumed that the highest scores are obtained when comparing
equal classes. Then, the SSIM and PSNR for a given model is computed as the
mean of the maximum scores over the complete dataset. Speciﬁcally, the SSIM
and PSNR results are sorted in ascending order. A percentage depending on
the extension of the dataset and the number of diﬀerent classes is taken from
such array of measures and then this fraction of scores is averaged, standard
deviation also being computed. In order to evaluate SSIM and PSNR in this
way, it is essential that all image classes are evenly represented both in the test
and generated sets.
2.4.2
Qualitative
Image quality assessment, as it has been shown, is not a trivial task. More-
over, the large disagreement regarding objective measures further hinders the
process. Besides, distortion in images can be found in inﬁnite diﬀerent forms
and then, a single objective measure is unlikely to account for them all.
In
this scenario, resorting to qualitative evaluation metrics might be regarded as
the least problematic solution. Subjective as they are, the number of diﬀerent
quality assessment approaches is endless.
Perhaps the most obvious procedure is a ‘Turing-like’ test, in which a human
annotator is simply presented the images that need to evaluate. In practice,
the interface shown and conditions of the experiment greatly diﬀer from imple-
mentations, and with that the concerns about the validity of the tests arise.
In the matter at hand, comparison of images generated from diﬀerent models
can be though as being presented as single or double stimulus. With respect to
the evaluation scheme, it can be either categorical, comparison or a similarity
judgement. In addition, it ought to be noted that literature commonly considers
showing scenes for a limited amount of time. The reason why the viewing time
is ﬁxed is that, in this way, it is ensured that the viewer pays equal attention to
all the examples that need to be categorised [71] [72] [73].
The evident distinction between single and double stimulus is that the former
presents only one scene to the observer while the latter shows two. A forced
comparison between two images has been reported to be the easiest experiment
for observers [72]. Moreover, intuitively it can be though that an observer might
change the criterion with which chooses a categorical score as that shown in Fig.
2.31 as the experiment runs. For instance, if the overall quality of the generated
instances is poor but the annotator is shown ﬁrst the best image of the set, the
observer might select ‘bad’ for this one, discovering later that the remaining
images are even poorer.

Since the aim of this work is to directly compare diﬀerent GAN models, the
selected setup is double stimulus. Regarding the viewing time, according to
[71] and [72], 3 seconds are enough to evaluate images. Therefore, the images
are shown at the same time and for 3 seconds, allowing the observer as long as
desired to make a decision after the scenes are gone. With respect to the order
of apparition, it has been considered that the images should be shown either on
the left or right side randomly, hoping not to favour any model for its viewing
location. Lastly, both scenes appear on the screen at the same time.
The proposed subjective assessment is extremely simple, as it solely involves a
GUI presenting two diﬀerent images. A series of volunteer observers are then
recruited to choose the image which looks more realistic.
This scheme allows not only to compare real and generated images, but also
pictures produced from diﬀerent algorithms, i.e. vanilla and oracle GANs.
The statistics derived from this subjective assessment consist of a success rate,
deﬁned as the number of times a model was preferred over the other, divided
by the total number of images in the stack.
As a subjective measure, it is of course the most accurate in terms of visual
perception.
Considering that the ﬁnal aim of this whole work is to obtain
perceptually ﬁner images, the result of this measure should be decisive when
choosing the algorithm to go with.

functionality is exactly the same in both cases: the previously deﬁned numeric

contrary it is thought to be generated) and the window is closed.
Similarly to human qualitative assessment, the generated image is shown for a
limited amount of time. Speciﬁcally, and following the guidelines in [71] and
[72], the viewing time is set to 3 seconds.

CelebA
CelebA is a large-scale data collection of face images built in [82] so as to test a

loglikelihood (NLL) is the chosen metric. Two last representative examples of

in the other two previous datasets. The size of celebA images is 64×64, having
also 3 RGB colour channels. In comparison, each MNIST and fashion-MNIST
sample accounts for 28 × 28 = 784 pixels, while every celebA instance adds up

MNIST and fashion-MNIST come with 50K training samples, whereas celebA
contains more than 200K. Consequently, the training process is much more
computationally expensive and only allowed for a limited amount of experiments
due to time restrictions.

4.1
Oracle in the loop
Since the ultimate goal of this project is to assess whether a human in the loop
can improve GAN performance, it might initially seem irrelevant to test the
inclusion of a ’virtual’ oracle on real datasets. However, it can actually serve as
a precedent for human performance. Depending on the dataset under considera-
tion, it would be extremely easy for a human to discard generated samples. For
example, the MNIST set is fairly simple to learn and, as a consequence, early
training iterations already output decently accurate images. On the contrary,
datasets such as CelebA are so complex that even the best quality synthesised
images cannot always fool the human eye. In the former case, the discriminator
might not actually beneﬁt from human opinion, as an observer can be as dubi-
ous as the network is. However, generating a human face is far from trivial and,
in most cases, a 0.5 output of the discriminator does not necessarily mean that
the synthesised face does not appear as gibberish. In a way, an analogy between
complex datasets and the Gaussian toy set is obvious. Same as a human cannot
tell a genuine gaussian sample apart from a generated one if merely looking
at its numerical value, an observer might not be able to diﬀerentiate between
an authentic and a generated MNIST digit or fashion item. For these reasons,
experiments with an oracle have been included both in the Gaussian toy set,
and the realistic datasets.
==PAGE:87==
4.1 Oracle in the loop
71
In a nutshell, the keypoint of this algorithm is that an observer can give a
hint to the discriminator whenever it is about to be fooled by the generator.
Nevertheless, if the annotator is also tricked, the given hint might have a counter
eﬀect.
With respect to this oracle, two diﬀerent experiments have been designed. First,
the inﬂuence of the width of the conﬁdence interval will be inspected. Next, the
eﬀect of introducing noise in the oracle query will be evaluated.
4.1.1
Inﬂuence of the decision interval
The trials within this section experiment with diﬀerent conﬁdence intervals. The
sole fact of this parameter existing already justiﬁes trying out the performance
given by tuning it. Moreover, the most prominent example of active learning in
GANs found in the literature showed the most interesting result that a higher
number of queries actually decreased the classiﬁcation accuracy [13]. A wider
decision interval not only alters the quality of the generated images, but also
alters the execution time.
The chosen intervals, taken the possible decrease in accuracy into account, allow
the algorithm to query the oracle only when extremely in doubt. That is to
say, the conﬁdence range stays within the closest vicinity of 0.5. For clarity,
the experiments with respect to this interval are gathered in Table 4.1 for the
Gaussian toy set and Table 4.2 for the realistic datasets.

The potential of a human in the loop implementation as such described depends
on the dataset under consideration.

with a virtual oracle would be not changing the discriminator loss function at
all. Since human labelling is time consuming, this second experiment is meant
as a model for human behaviour.

CelebA
CelebA is a completely diﬀerent set than the ones previously seen. Apart from
its completely diﬀerent nature, no validation or test sets were considered. The
reason why is that the borrowed DCGAN implementation trains the model with
all the +200K images. According to [82], the whole set is indeed partitioned
in training, validation and test sets. Nevertheless, by the time this work was
implemented, the links to download both the dataset and the information about
this partition were disabled 1. For this reason, it was decided to proceed as in
the DCGAN implementation chosen, and use the whole set for training.
Since the faces in the used set are not labelled, nor they are selectively synthe-
sised by entering a desired class in the generator, a KNN classiﬁer cannot be
used to assess the performance. As a consequence, the only results for this set
originate from the inception score and average loglikelihood.
With respect to IS, it was computed with a test set of 50K images in order to
reduce the computational requirements, keeping the number of batches to 1. As
discussed, there is no validation test to compare with. Instead, the ﬁrst 50K
training images are used to calculate an upper bound for the score. Indeed, IS for
the training set is higher than that of both implementations, meaning that the
trained models are very much likely not suﬀering from overﬁtting. Comparing
the vanilla and oracle versions, the performance of the latter is superior.

6.1
Inﬂuence of the decision interval
Starting with the Gaussian toy set, it is no surprise that the results with re-
spect the number of samples follow the same trend irrespective of the conﬁdence
interval. Being based on MLE, the GAN model could not be expected to be-
have diﬀerently: a higher sample budget results in both lower error and smaller
variance. Also, if the number of samples is suﬃcient, the bigger the decision
interval, the better. Results were improved both in terms of MSE and stan-
dard variation, meaning that the generated Gaussian distribution is not only
more accurate but also more stable. Also, the number of queries shows that
even for this simple set, GAN takes quite some time to learn the ground truth
distribution.
Moving on to the DCGAN implementation, the ﬁrst interesting result to look at
is the number of queries with respect to the width of the interval. Incidentally,
this value serves as measure of how simple a dataset is for the network to learn.
A discriminator score over the generated samples, D(G(z)), close to 0.5 means
that the discriminator is dubious about the nature of z, i.e. the generator is
fooling it. Indeed, recalling Eq. 2.2, when the true and generated distributions
are equal, the output of the optimal discriminator is 0.5. Therefore, the higher
the number of queries with respect to the total number of iteration, the more
often the generator has managed to trick the discrimininator. In other words,
the network easily learns to replicate the ground truth. In the case of MNIST,
increasing the interval from 0.01 to 0.025 provokes an increment of queries from
==PAGE:116==

5894 to 19525, which accounts for the totality of iterations. It was noted that,
while training, the discriminator score over the synthesised images reaches a
value around 0.5 already in the ﬁrst iterations. Comparing this evolution with
the one obtained in the GAN approach speaks for the striking performance of
DCGAN. The same trend, although not as pronounced, was also found for the
fashion-MNIST set.
According to the IS, the most accurate and diverse distribution is the one gen-
erated out of a network using an interval of 0.5, Table 5.6. This result is quite
interesting since tells that the best IS is obtained by completely changing the
original discriminator loss function.
Motivated by [65], IS was computed using three diﬀerent number of splits: 1, 10
and 100. According to [65], this is a parameter that can aﬀect the metric. Their
argument is backed up by empirical results using the Inception v3 network,
apparently on ImageNet. The results obtained for the datasets considered do
show such variation, although not as signiﬁcantly as in [65]. Typically, IS is
computed on 50K samples and 10 splits, meaning on chunks of 5K instances.
For the case of ImageNet, which has 1000 diﬀerent classes, 5K might not be
enough to accurately estimate the marginal distribution ˆp(y).
Nevertheless,
MNIST and fashion-MNIST only have 10 classes, and celebA does not have
predeﬁned classes. Therefore, a plausible explanation would be that the limited
number of classes allows for a better estimation.
Oddly enough, while proposed as a suitable evaluation metrics in [9], IS is not
reported for MNIST (set which is used in such work). Nevertheless, [87] provides
the scores for their method (SGAN) and a number of other implementations,
which have been gathered in Table 6.1. This paper does not provide a ﬁgure for
IS but rather a curve depicting the evolution of the score with respect to the
number of training iterations. These ﬁgures have been analysed and the score
for approximately 19525 iterations (corresponding to the training process here
followed) has been retrieved. These results are indicated in Table 6.1 by a ≈
sign. DCGAN obtains one of the lowest scores and the oracle implementation
achieves a slight improvement. In any case and excluding the result given by
5-S-GAN, both of the models studied provide average results.
One ﬁnal remark regarding IS on MNIST is that, in [83], an experiment is
conducted in which they show a histogram of the IS achieved by their model
and the standard GAN (replicated in Fig. 6.1 for convenience). Thanks to this
result, the IS obtained for the models proposed in this work can be said to be
superior to that given by a vanilla GAN implementation.

As a last comment regarding IS on the MNIST-like sets, the confusion matrices
shown in the previous chapter provide further evidence of the diversity of both
DCGAN and DCGAN+oracle.
No examples on IS were found in the literature to assess the quality of syn-
thesised celebA images. Indeed, the only found work which makes use of it in
this dataset is [89], but as a part of a empirical study on evaluation techniques.
What they report is actually a measure named relative inverse inception score
RIS = (1−IS/IS0), where IS0 stands for the upper bound of the training set IS
(as was also reported in this work). As the aim of this paper is to test whether
IS is a valid measure, they compute RIS under a number of diﬀerent scenarios
(mode dropping, rotation of images, etc.) and they do not provide the IS value
for plain synthesised images. Therefore, it is impossible to compare the results
here presented. Still, such work provides an interesting insight about the IS.
Their conclusion is that IS does correlate with human perception, as suggested
in [9], and that it does a good job evaluating diversity and quality. Nonetheless,
the authors also stated that IS cannot report overﬁtting. Yet, as discussed here
and in [65], providing an upper bound can hint whether the model is overﬁtted.
Indeed, this threshold is only surpassed in the case of MNIST, which is a strong
suggestion that the model is overﬁtted.
As for the image quality measures, they are considerably inconclusive as to
which model performs best. However and in relation to overﬁtting, it might
be relevant that the PSNR is higher for MNIST than fashion-MNIST. Whereas
SSIM measures the overall structural similarity of images, and is built so as to
provide further correlation with the HVS, PSNR speaks for direct diﬀerences
between image pixel. To put it simply, the top PSNR scores are obtained from
1:1 image copies. Then, acquiring a higher PSNR score, together with the fact
that the IS for MNIST is superior in synthesised than real images, might be the
deﬁnite proof of an overﬁtted model.
Speaking of the diﬀerences between distances used in the KNN classiﬁer, the
most suited for the MNIST set is Minkowski (p = 3). As explained, this metric
assigns a higher weight to further neighbours. It was also seen that Euclidean
distance is very sensitive to pixel shift. Manhattan distance, while being less
sensitive to large diﬀerences, suﬀers from small scale changes. The digits in
MNIST are depicted in a variety of ways but still (Fig 6.2), same ﬁgures must
be classiﬁed into the same group. The pixel shift between two instances repre-
senting the same number can then be considerable. This is further aggravated
by the fact that the colour range of these images is limited to black and white,
without intermediate grey shades. Consequently, the Minkowski distance, which
takes into account distant neighbours, performs better in this dataset.

average loglikelihood is a measure of the coverage of the test set. This means that

slightly modifying the discriminator and generator losses would achieve a better

architecture of the network. Up next is the oracle model, which does change the
optimisation function. The lowest performance in terms of loglikelihood is given
by conditional adversarial nets [18], which modiﬁes the loss function to include
conditional auxiliary information. Having said so, it is safe to say that, for a
model which refashions the vanilla formulation, the loglikelihood performance
is quite up to standards.

6.2
Introduction of noise in the decision
If a human annotator was to decide whether a numerical value belongs to a
real or synthesised Gaussian distribution, intuition says that the decision would
be completely random at all times.
This is precisely the eﬀect aimed when
introducing noise in the decision for the Gaussian toy example. Although the
results might initially seem quite random, careful analysis actually allows to see
that they make quite a lot of sense.
In the case of 1K samples, it was seen that the introduction of an oracle slowed
the convergence since it repeatedly makes D(G(z)) fall back to zero. It was
already reported in [1] that the early iterations of the GAN algorithm might be
slow due to the discriminator being more powerful that the generator and indeed,
that is the output seen. Nevertheless, by introducing noise, the number of times
D(G(z)) goes back to the beginning is reduced, as proved by the number of oracle
queries which are at least halved for 1K and a noisy oracle. By increasing the
number of available samples to 10K and 100K, this little convergence problem is
solved and, consequently, there is no further diﬀerence between having a noisy
or regular oracle.
The same result could be observed in the more complex case of a noisy DC-
GAN+oracle trained on MNIST. 25 epochs are more than enough to train the
network for such case and, as a consequence, the performance is comparable
to the noise-free scenario. In relation to the values obtained for the diﬀerent
evaluation metrics, they are as inconclusive as in the regular setup.
Since the noise experiment was aimed at modelling human behaviour, it can be
said that in the case of the MNIST sets, perhaps the whole idea of a human oracle
is irrelevant. As shown, in the early iterations the network already reproduces
almost to perfection the real images. Therefore, one could think that a human
might not be beneﬁcial at all. However, even if the annotator was detrimental
for the algorithm if not able to tell apart real and synthesised images, in the
long run it would not matter, as the set is so simple that it does not represent
a challenge for the network at all.
The situation for complex datasets as celebA could be completely diﬀerent, and
it remains a task for the future to conduct further experiments both including
this artiﬁcial noise and actual human judges. Intuitively and backed up by the
IS, the use of a human oracle in the case of celebA (and other complex sets), is
potentially useful.
==PAGE:125==
6.3 Human in the loop
109
6.3
Human in the loop
Unfortunately and due to time restrictions, only one experiment involving a
human in the loop could be run.
The chosen set to test the human in the
loop model was celebA. As it is the most complex set, it is considered that this
scenario from which the network could beneﬁt the most.
Introducing a human in the training process for just one more epoch managed
to improve the inception score and almost level it to the value obtained by
the oracle implementation. Regarding loglikelihood and following the reasons
discussed above, modifying slightly the loss function of the model has the eﬀect
of reducing this metric.
Also, the attained value is higher than that of the
oracle version, most likely because in this last scenario the human annotator
was queried only 9 times.
Then, the behaviour with respect to the inception score follows the trend seen
in the theoretical oracle example. Moreover, the loglikelihood decreases once
again when modifying the original GAN formulation.
This means that the
oracle experiments could actually serve to model human behaviour.
Although these results are promising, it must not be forgotten that the extent
of this last experiment was shorter than desirable because of time limitations.
Thus, to have a full picture of the pros and cons of the proposed approaches,
further and largest experiments should be conducted.

Discussion
==PAGE:127==
Chapter 7
Conclusion
In relation to generative adversarial networks, the production of extensions and
variations of the original algorithm has been anything but scarce since its in-
troduction back in 2014.
As an overly simpliﬁed explanation, GANs are a
two-player game, in which a generator aims at replicating genuine data, and a
discriminator tries not to be fooled by the former. Competition between these
two players drives the algorithm to, eventually, generate data indistinguishable
from the ground truth. The ﬁrst goal to be fulﬁlled in this project was for GANs
to become interpretable models. Any new framework proposal based on a pre-
vious one should be preceded by a thorough understanding of the theoretical
background, and that is something many works lack.
As countless other contributions, the initial aim of this research was to improve
an already state of the art framework, more speciﬁcally for image generation.
With such a number of precedents, it is challenging to ﬁnd a direction which can
actually outperform existing models. Due to the lack of research in the area,
it was decided to settle the study line on a combination of GANs and active
learning. The loosely deﬁned GAN architecture allowed to choose a network
which solves the well-known stability ﬂaws of the original formulation, namely
DCGAN.
More in detail, the ultimate objective was to introduce a human in the training
loop, and assess whether GANs can leverage this intervention. The main idea
is that an annotator could, in principle, increase the performance by labelling
uncertain instances. Before that, a theoretical study of the potential beneﬁts
of such modiﬁcation had to be conducted. To this end, a ‘virtual’ oracle was
introduced in the framework for a Gaussian toy set. Once seen that this proposal
==PAGE:128==
112
Conclusion
could actually improve GANs performance, the oracle setup was moved to the
aforementioned DCGAN network, and implemented over a set of increasingly
complex sets: MNIST, fashion-MNIST and celebA. The introduction of this
type of oracle is justiﬁed by the undeniable fact that involving humans in any
way requires a considerable time eﬀort. Thus, the suggested alteration of the
vanilla implementation had to be ﬁrst tried out in a theoretical approach.
As the aforementioned oracle is queried whenever generated samples come close
to the uncertainty boundary, an evident experiment to conduct consists of test-
ing a variety of diﬀerent conﬁdence intervals. This oracle, when queried, informs
the discriminator that the instance it is dubious about is actually synthesised
and not real. As a consequence, the generator is expected to try harder and cre-
ate more accurate samples. While the oracle knows which samples are generated
and which are not, a human annotator cannot be expected to be as precise under
general circumstances. Then and aiming to model human behaviour, another
experiment involved randomly changing the oracle decision towards generated
or real.
A full description of a newly proposed algorithm needs to go hand in hand with
a set of results that describe its performance. For that purpose, a collection of
evaluation metrics was thoroughly analysed and implemented. Speciﬁcally and
in the context of image generation, the studied methods comprise loglikelihood,
inception score, image quality measures, classiﬁcation accuracy and qualitative
assessment.
Although the absence of consensus concerning quality evaluation has been long
discussed in the literature, the extent of disagreement between metrics was found
to be even more critical than expected. Moreover, the techniques reportedly
considered most widespread, loglikelihood and IS, have actually little presence.
In spite of being mentioned and even explained in a not insigniﬁcant number
of works, only a handful of results are delivered. Therefore, the performance
obtained with the proposed model could be compared to only a few other exam-
ples. Still, the small number of performance references in the literature showed
that the proposed models perform reasonably well.
Even if more comparisons could have been established, it would have been an
almost impossible task to decide on which model performs best. Apart from an
obvious disagreement among the research community as to which is the most
adequate evaluation technique, a measure itself does not usually agree with the
remaining set of metrics. In addition, every single metric has important disad-
vantages which cannot be overlooked. To say the least, an optimal evaluation
measure should have clearly deﬁned bounds, detect overﬁtting and mode col-
lapse, favour high ﬁdelity and diverse samples, be able to distinguish between
real and generated images and, ﬁnally, agree with human perception. Loglike-
==PAGE:129==
113
lihood can be diﬃcult to estimate and can be misleading for high dimensional
spaces. Moreover, it is not necessarily associated with visually pleasing results
and favours overﬁtted models. The inception score is a measure of diversity and
image quality that, although said to be correlated with human perception, fails
to report mode collapse. Also, there has been signiﬁcant criticism about the
inception score not being able to report overﬁtting. Nevertheless, it was found
that using the validation inception score as an upper bound could possibly de-
tect overﬁtted. With respect to image quality measure, they do not provide
conclusive information about a speciﬁc model performing better, but combined
with the inception score could be valuable to assess overﬁtting. Regarding clas-
siﬁcation accuracy, though it is true that the sole accuracy percentage is not an
indicator of image diversity, looking at the confusion matrix obtain can actu-
ally hint whether every class is equally represented within the synthesised test.
Finally, visual inspection is highly subjective and therefore, unless conducted
in large scale, is not a reliable metric for fair comparison. Focusing again on
the models proposed, the obtained results for inception score and classiﬁcation
accuracy are comparable to state of the art performances. On the contrary,
every modiﬁcation of the original GAN formulation decreases its loglikelihood
score.
All in all, the proposed oracle method cannot be said to be superior or infe-
rior to any other model. The major hindrance found during this project was
undoubtedly the uncertainty regarding evaluation metrics.
Nevertheless, the
results both quantitative and qualitative show the potential applications of the
presented framework.
Can humans help improve the performance of cutting edge machine learning
algorithms? The answer given by this project is that it depends. And that
is actually the answer to many data science questions such as “what kind of
problem solves this model?” or “what type of data is it suited for?”. In the stud-
ied scenarios, the determining factor was found to be the nature of the dataset
at hand. MNIST and fashion-MNIST did not experience a great improvement
thanks to the inclusion of the oracle. In such cases, even if a human annotator
was asked to tell apart a generated and real sample, the response would be ran-
dom as the network is simply very good at replicating these sets. However and
taking celebA as an example, there is still a long road to go until deep learning
models can fool a human eye when synthesising faces. Then, it is possible that a
human judge labelling uncertain samples could upgrade the model, as was seen
in the last human in the loop experiment.
This project is a preliminary study of what an active learning GAN approach
could look like. To the extent possible, its performance has been assessed and
compared to state of the art algorithms. However, many experiments to further
improve the performance of a human in the loop GAN implementation remain
==PAGE:130==
114
Conclusion
for future work. First and foremost, a large scale crowdsourcing model training
should be conducted in order to fully understand and evaluate this novel pro-
posal. Also, a more extensive qualitative evaluation test should be carried out.
With respect to evaluation metrics, new methods or reﬁnement of old ones is
absolutely required so as to steer the direction of progress. In comparison, the
majority of research is focused on producing newer, more sophisticated models
while seeking appropriate metrics should be a priority. Hopefully, this work will
serve as an inspiration to forthcoming algorithms and as a guideline for quality
evaluation.
