Abstract(English)
Background:
Throughout the world, millions of people suﬀer from food allergies. Some allergies
can result in more severe conditions such as anaphylaxis, which in worst cases can
have fatal outcomes. One of the deadliest food allergens is peanut, and a method to
prevent the development of peanut allergy, could save lives and unnecessary suﬀering.
Methods:
An animal experiment exploiting Brown Norway Rats was applied with the aim
of inducing tolerance for peanuts via either intragastric (i.g.), sublingual, oral, or
subcutaneous route of administration. The rats received one of three doses of peanut
protein extract. A pilot study optimised a dose to be used for intraperitoneal (i.p.)
sensitisation with peanut as allergen. This method would be utilised in an attempt
to sensitise the rats, to evaluate the induced tolerance. Finally, an ear swelling test,
i.p.
challenge, and i.g. challenge was performed, with the intent of eliciting an
immune response that could be used to assess if the tolerance induced, have been
sustained throughout the sensitisation period.
Blood samples taken throughout
the experiment would be tested for allergen speciﬁc immunoglobulin G1 (IgG1)
with indirect Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA) and allergen speciﬁc
immunoglobulin E (IgE) with antibody capture ELISA.
Results:
All of the dosage groups in subcutaneous injections showed an IgE response, already
after the initial prevention phase, indicating that the method had sensitised the
rats instead of preventing the sensitisation.
The i.g., sublingual and oral route
of administration all showed excellent potential in preventing sensitisation, with a
greatly declined IgE antibody production, and ear swelling. For these three routes
of administration the greatest decline were observed with the highest dosage group.
Conclusion:
I.g., sublingual, and oral administration of high doses of peanut protein extract
shows great potential of preventing peanut allergy in Brown Norway Rats, whereas
the subcutaneous seems to sensitise the rats at the dosage groups used in this ex-
periment.

1.1

Food allergies
Throughout the world, millions of people suﬀer from food allergy (FA). The per-
centage of people who suﬀers from FA varies by country, so it not known exactly
how many people suﬀer from it. In the United States (US) and in large parts of
Europe, the amount of people suﬀering from FA varies from less than 3% to over
10%1. The large variation in the number of FA cases around the world has partly
been attributed to local diet2. Of the countries where suﬃcient data have been
collected throughout the last decades, the amount of FA cases has increased over
time1. It is however not known how much of this rise is attributed to more cases, or
better and wider diagnostics.
FAs result in clinical symptoms varying in seriousness from slight discomfort (e.g.
redness and itchiness caused by histamines triggering inﬂammation), to more severe
conditions, such as anaphylaxis, which clinical symptoms include constrictions of
airways, where can have fatal outcomes3.
Studies in the US based on local data estimates that annually over 120.000 visits
to emergency departments are caused by FAs, over 50.000 of these are thought to
be caused by anaphylaxis4,5. FA can be induced by various food sources, but over
90% of FA cases are derived from allergens in “The big eight”-list of FAs. This list
includes milk, egg, wheat, soy, ﬁsh, shellﬁsh, tree nut, and peanut6. FAs are usually
developed in childhood and is most times outgrown before adulthood, but certain
FAs are rarely outgrown and may follow the individual throughout their life7.
1.1.1
Peanut allergy
One of the most serious allergies in “the big eight”-list is peanut allergy(PA). It
is approximated that around 1% of children and around 0.6% of adults in the US
suﬀer from PA7. The amount of people aﬀected by PA seems to rise yearly, but it
is unclear what causes this increase, but a study has found a link between non-oral
exposure and development of PA8.

1.1.2
Underlying mechanisms of food allergy
Most cases of food ingestion will lead to tolerance of the proteins. Tolerance is a
state where oral ingestion of the food antigens will not lead to adverse eﬀects in the
individual. In such cases proteins or peptides will cross the epithelial barrier of the
gut. The antigen presenting cell (APC) associated with inducing tolerance is cluster
of diﬀerentiation (CD)103+ dendritic cells that produces transforming growth factor
(TGF)-β9. The APCs will migrate to the Mesenteric lymph node, where they can
promote T-cell diﬀerentiation to regulatory T-cells10. The regulatory T-cells can
trigger B-cell proliferation to produce non-inﬂammatory immunoglobulins.
When an individual is exposed to an allergen, and the immune system cannot develop
tolerance, or loses oral tolerance, the epithelial cells will produce cytokines that
promote a T helper cell 2 (Th2) immune response that drives diﬀerentiation of CD4+
T cells to Th2 cells. The Th2 cells will promote B-cells to produce immunoglobulin
E (IgE)11, which is an important mediator in many allergic reactions. This process
is known as sensitisation.

The cytokines attributed to the mechanisms of anaphylaxis includes histamine,
tryptase, leukotrienes, prostaglandins, TNF-α, and platelet-activating factor (PAF).
Circulating leukotrienes and histamines can stimulate the synthesis of nitros oxide,
which triggers the production of cyclic guanosine monophos-phate (cGMP). cGMP
generation results in relaxation of vascular smooth muscle and TNF-α, and PAF
results in anaphylaxic hypotension3.
Even though the immune system is sensitised to the speciﬁc allergen and produces
allergen-speciﬁc IgE antibodies, it may not result in an allergic response. This can
be caused by the allergen not having a high enough binding aﬃnity to the antibodies,
preventing them to produce a suitable bond to trigger the mast cell degranulation.
For oral allergic reactions caused by ingestion, a lack of gut permeability may pre-
vent enough protein from crossing the blood-gut barrier to activate basophils. The
binding aﬃnity and gut permeability may be aﬀected by stomach acid deforming the
proteins prior to uptake, or digestion of the proteins by mechanisms of the gut such
as metabolism. The permeability may be a negligible factor for reactions caused
by mast cells which are in the tissue.
A lack of clinical symptoms in sensitised
individuals may also be caused by blocking antigens negating the eﬀects of allergen-
speciﬁc IgE. Some individuals simply show no clinical symptoms even after being
sensitised13,14.
1.1.3
Diagnosis and management of peanut allergy
If a person is believed to have developed PA, the diagnosis often occurs in one of
two ways. A skin prick test (SPT) can be performed where a drop of peanut extract
is introduced to the cutaneous region by a needle, along with histamine as a positive
control and a saline solution as a negative control. If mast cells with allergen speciﬁc
IgE antibodies are present in the cutaneous region, a hypersensitivity reaction will
ensue and a wheal will develop on the skin, showing a positive SPT. Although the
SPT can be used as an indicator as to what food may cause an allergic reaction, a
positive SPT should not be used as an ultimate diagnosis of PA15.
The preferred way to establish if an individual has a certain FA, is to perform an oral
food challenge where the individual is put on a restrictive diet avoiding the allergen
in question. The allergen is then gradually introduced in increasing amounts until
an adverse eﬀect is measured. If no adverse eﬀects occur in this phase, the cause
of allergy may be linked to another allergen. Even though this method has a low
amount of false positives compared to SPT and is fairly easy to perform if the

When a person has been diagnosed with PA, the only suitable management may be
complete avoidance since the manifestations of the allergy are too severe to manage
with normal allergy medicine. Avoidance of the allergen is possible if enough care is
given, since any traces of peanuts must be labelled on the packaging of food products.
Trace amounts of peanuts can be found in foods not related to peanuts, if any of
the product is in close contact of peanuts during either production or transport.
Informing the surroundings may also be important, since it might require personnel
to change their workﬂow, if the person suﬀering from PA is dining out or using air
travel17.
If the person suﬀering from PA is in contact with peanut it could introduce ana-
phylaxis, a reaction too severe to treat with normal allergy symptom mitigating
medications such as antihistamines. For these instances, the person is to carry an
epinephrine auto-injector to use promptly after symptoms arise. After epinephrine
has been administered, the patient should be rushed to a medical facility, since
around one third of anaphylaxis cases have a bi-phase reaction that might require
further medical attention18. This bi-phase reaction might start as late as eight hours
after the initial allergic reaction. The epinephrine auto-injector is the single most
life saving measure for people suﬀering from PA, but not all aﬀected individuals are
carrying an auto-injector at all times, and of those who carry an auto-injector, some
are carrying them after their expiration, decreasing their reliability. Some carry an
auto-injector, not knowing how to use it correctly19, which further puts the life of
the allergic person at risk.
Because of the restrictions on everyday life, suﬀerers from PA, and the friends and
family of those people, experience increased stress in their everyday life. The con-
stant burden of reading labels and worrying about accidentally ingestion of peanut
and ﬁnding themselves restricted in social situations, leads to a reduced quality of
life (QoL) of the aﬀected individuals.

Therapeutic strategies for treating or preventing PA
Because of the massive impact and lowered QoL for people suﬀering from PA, and
because there is currently no approved cure, therapies are under development for PA.

There are mainly two diﬀerent strategies for allergy therapy. Primary prevention
is a strategy where an unaﬀected person in high risk of developing PA is given
a therapy to lower the risk of developing sensitisation.
When therapies aim to
desensitise patients, the goal is to reduce or eliminate the production of IgE and/or
eliminate clinical symptoms. Several allergy studies have accomplished a reduced
IgE production over the long term, and while the allergen speciﬁc IgE have declined
in these studies, a consistent pattern of increasing allergen speciﬁc immunoglobulin
G4 (IgG4) have emerged. The underlying mechanics for desensitisation is closely
linked to a class switching in the antibody production towards IgG420.
1.2.1
Learning early about peanut (LEAP)
Until the mid 00’s, it was the oﬃcial recommendation that infants in the high-risk
group for developing PA should avoid peanuts in their early life, but a human study
in 2015 found that introducing peanut using early oral introduction in the ﬁrst
months of infancy resulted in a drastic reduction of developed PA cases compared
to a control group who avoided ingestion of peanuts21. This study became known
as LEAP, and since oral administration is easy to administer, it has become the
recommended method for preventing allergy.
After the LEAP-study, a follow-up study was made, concluding that the tolerance
developed in the LEAP study persisted in the children after 12 months of peanut
avoidance22. This makes a strong case for oral administration being a suitable can-
didate for primary prevention of PA, but the study cannot conclude if the tolerance
will persist into adulthood.
1.2.2
Routes of administration
Besides oral administration, another route of administration for primary prevention
of PA could be sublingual immunotherapy (SLIT) where peanut proteins are placed
under the patient’s tongue and left for a period of time for the proteins to be absorbed
via the mucous gland. The subject may swallow the proteins afterwards, so the gut
mucosa is also introduced to the allergen. This method is fairly easy to administer.
SLIT has shown some excellent results for desensitisation of people suﬀering from
PA23. It is however not currently known if the tolerance induced by SLIT would is
sustained after the experiments24.

Subcutaneous immunotherapy (SCIT) is a method where an allergen is introduced
to the immune system via injections under the skin. Studies on SCIT as a primary
prevention method are lacking, but studies on using SCIT in desensitisation have
been done, and shown that long-term immunotherapy using SCIT may be eﬀective
in treating individuals with PA25, but can be associated with severe reactions, and
since at least two fatalities have been caused by subcutaneous injections to treat
PA26, and it is therefore necessary to proceed with extreme caution when studying
SCIT on humans.
Studies in sensitised mice have shown that SCIT have potential as a method for
desensitisation. In these studies SCIT induced an immune response similar to that of
OIT, which both resulted in decreased IgE and increased amounts of immunoglobulin
G2 (IgG2) sub-type a. Since mice and rats lack the IgG4 known to be correlated with
desensitisation in humans, the subtypes of IgG2 (a, b and c) may need studying, as
to determine their importance in allergy27.
I.g., sublingual, oral and subcutaneous is just some routes of administration that is
being screened for their potential for treating and preventing PA.

Using animals in food allergy models
For studying FA, utilising a human model would ensure that the result correspond
with that of a human immune system. However, there are some risks involved in
clinical trials for studying primary prevention. Allergy could be induced in otherwise
non-allergic individuals, and when treating allergic individuals, allergic reactions
such as anaphylaxis could be induced.
Therefore, it may be preferable to use an animal model for the studies, since alterca-
tions would be easier to manage, and there are more ways to challenge the induced
tolerance since the rats can be sacriﬁced after the experiments.
When using animal models for FA studies, a lot of factors have to be considered.
Animals used for testing vary from humans in a lot of ways. Dogs have similar
gut anatomy and nutritional requirements to that of humans, and pigs have sim-
ilar anatomy and immunology of the skin and gastrointestinal tract compared to
humans28. Rodents may not be the most similar to humans in immunology and
anatomy, however they have some key advantages, since they are cheaper to gain

When using rodents for FA models, guinea pigs are rarely used because of strong

Fi-

BN rats can be sensitised for allergens using several routes, and some of the widest
utilised are i.g. route using an adjuvant, and intraperitoneal (i.p.) injections that
may not require adjuvants. BN rats can also be used to assess clinical symptoms of
food allergy, such as anaphylaxis, where a couple of endpoints can be used, such as
body temperature, concentration of mast cell proteins and intestinal permeability.
With anaphylaxis, a decrease in body temperature and motor activity is observed,
while intestinal permeability and mast cell protein concentration such as rat mast-
cell protease 2 (rMCP-II) is increased28.
1.4

Hypothesis and aim
Even though all the routes of administration described above have ultimately achieved
the same goal of decreasing allergen speciﬁc IgE, the underlying immune mecha-
nisms responsible of this may vary, and therefor vary in their safety and eﬃcacy.
This Master’s Thesis “Primary prevention of peanut allergy - Impact of dose and
route of administration” directly compare all these routes of administration at three
diﬀerent doses, to quantify an optimal strategy for primary prevention of PA. This
has been done by using an animal model utilising BN rats. BN rats were initially
given their respective dose via their respective method, and then any tolerance was
challenged by i.p. post-immunisation. Finally, the induced tolerance was tested
during a series of challenges, to provoke an allergic response, to test if any tolerance
induced in the ﬁrst phase was still maintained in the rats. Blood samples during
the animal experiment was collected for measuring the IgG1 and IgE responses in
the rats.
The overall aims for this master thesis include:
Quantiﬁcation of immunoglobulin G1 (IgG1) and IgE during i.p. post-immunisation
and challenges after initial treatment for primary prevention.

2 | Methods
2.1
|
Animal model
To evaluate the impact of route of administration for primary prevention of PA,
an animal model exploiting BN rats was applied because of their ability to pro-
duce a similar immunoglobulin response to that of humans, and to have similar
epitope-speciﬁcity as humans.
The experiment was carried out by the National
Food Institute at Technical University of Denmark (DTU). All experimental condi-
tions were approved by the Danish Animal Experiments Inspectorate and overseen
by the Animal Welfare Committee at the National Food Institute at DTU.
During the animal experiment, the animals were given doses of Peanut protein ex-
tract (PPE) in Phosphate-buﬀered saline (PBS). The PBS was produced in house
by using the recipes described in appendix A. This PBS-Stock was also used during
antibody response evaluation.
2.1.1
Animal care
All the rats were bred in-house by the National Food Institute at DTU and were
14th generation of BN rats fed on rice ﬂour based diet without milk and soy.
The rats were kept in 800cm3 macrolon cages with two rats per cage for males, and
four rats per cage for females.
The rats were kept at 22 ± 1 °C. The air was at 55% ± 5% relative humidity and
changed 50-55 times per hour with over pressure. The rats were in light for 12 hours
per day from 09:00 to 21:00, and in the dark from 21:00 to 09:00.
The rats were given feed and water ad libitum and were not exposed to other food
sources. They were observed daily and weighed weekly.

2.1.2
Animal model design
The animal study was divided into three phases. Since the study evaluated the
primary prevention of PA, the animals were introduced to PPE for three weeks
from day 0 to day 20. Afterwards, an attempt to break the tolerance was initiated
and lasted for three weeks from day 28 to 49. Finally, to test whether the animals
showed clinical symptoms of PA, several tests and challenges were made.
For each route of administration, 32 rats were applied. For each of the route of
administrations, the rats were split into four groups with eight rats in each, three
distinct groups with varying doses of PPE and one control group.
An overview of the animal experimental design is included in ﬁgure 1.
Figure 1: Timeline of the animal experiments three phases spanning over 58 days. From day 0-20
the subcutaneous groups received PPE three times a week, while the other routes received PPE
daily.
2.1.2.1
First phase: Tolerance induction
The tolerance induction phase was designed to present the allergen and allow the
immune system to recognise the allergen and build a tolerance. During this phase
the rats were given a dose of PPE in PBS solution either daily or three times a week.
The dose and frequency varied depending on the group and route of administration.

2.1.2.2
Second phase: i.p. post-immunisation
In a pre-study prior to this experiment, rats were sensitised with either 50 µg, 125
µg or 500 µg of PPE once a week for ﬁve weeks. The results from this study showed
that the group given 50µg PPE showed the highest IgE response and no signiﬁcant
change were observed after the third week. From this pre-study, the optimal method
for sensitisation was found to be 50µg in three to four weeks. This method was used
when trying to induce sensitisation in the rats.
From day 28 and until day 49, all the rats were given a weekly dose of 50 µg PPE in
0.5 ml PBS via i.p. injections for four weeks. Prior to all the i.p. injections, a blood
sample was taken from the tongue vein of the animal and about 0.5 ml of blood was
collected.
2.1.2.3
Third phase: Challenging the tolerance
In the third and last phase, diﬀerent in vivo tests were used to elicit provocation
to determine whether the induced tolerance had been maintained during the second
phase. To simulate a human SPT an ear swelling test was performed at day 53.
Each rat was sedated with injections containing hypnorm rhidazolan and each of
their ears normal thickness were measured. Afterwards they were injected with 20
µl of PBS containing 10µg of PPE into each ear. After 30 minutes their ears were
measured again and the new thickness was noted. While the animals were sedated
on day 53, a temperature chip was inserted into the neck.

2.1.3
Preparation of blood samples
Blood samples taken from the rats during the experiment were left at room tem-
perature to coagulate for one hour and left refrigerated at 4°C overnight. The clot
was removed carefully using a wooden applicator stick. The remaining blood was
centrifuged at 3000 rotations per minute for 20 minutes, and the serum was moved
onto new 1.5 ml tubes, labelled, and stored at -20 °C.

2.1.4
Evaluation of antibody response
To test the antibody response during the experiments, the prepared sera from day
28, 35, 42, 49, 56 and 58 were analysed for allergen speciﬁc IgG1 and IgE. This
was done using indirect and antibody capture Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay
(ELISA). Coating buﬀer was made in bulk in-house and stored at room tempera-
ture after autoclaving. All incubation steps took place in the dark, and between
each step, the plates were washed ﬁve times using a microplate washer from Biotek
(model 405TS) loaded with PBS-Tween (PBS-t) as wash buﬀer. 0.2 M sulphuric
acid used as stop acid were diluted in-house. The reagents made in-house was made
using the instructions described in appendix A. The plates used were 96 well Max-
isorp microtitre plates (96-well, Nunc, Roskilde, Denmark), and the substrate used
was an aqueous formulation of 3,3’,5,5’-Tetramethylbenzidine (TMB-One) (4380A
Kementec Diagnostics, Taastrup, Denmark).
2.1.4.1
IgG1 - Indirect ELISA
Indirect ELISA was used to testing the IgG1 response. The plates were coated with
100 µl/well of 0.5 mg PPE/ml of coating buﬀer and stored at 4°C overnight in a re-
frigerator. After the plates were washed, the top row of the plates were loaded with a
positive control in well 2, negative control in row 3 (1:8 Naïve BN rat serum) and of
samples in well 4-12, leaving well 1 with pure PBS-T to measure background. The
plates were then diluted two-fold downwards using a Biotek Precision microplate
pipetting system controlled by a computer with Bioteks Precision software, leaving
50 µl in all wells while the plates were incubated on a shaking table for 60 minutes.
After the plates were washed, 50 µl/well of secondary antibody diluted 1:20,000
horseradish peroxidase (HRP) labelled Mouse-Anti-Rat IgG1 (3060-05 from South-
ern Biotech, Birmingham, AL, USA) in PBS-T were added to all the wells and left
to incubate for 60 minutes on a shaking table. Afterwards, the plates were washed
in the washer and manually washed in the sink twice.
100 µl/well of substrate
TMB-One were pipetted to the plate, and it was left for 12 minutes in the dark on a
shaking table. Exactly 12 minutes after applying TMB-One, 100µl/well of stop acid
were pipetted to the plate to stop the enzymatic reaction. The plates were kept in
the dark until the absorbance could be measured by the microplate reader (Gen5,
BioTek, EL800 Instrument, Winooski, VT, USA) at OD450 and OD630 as reference
wavelength and the software Gen5 from BioTek were used to communicate to the

2.1.4.2
IgE - Antibody Capture ELISA
To evaluate the prevention of sensitisation, antibody capture ELISA was used.
Plates were coated with 100 µl/well of 1:2,000 dilution of mouse-anti-rat IgE (HDMAB-
123, Hybri-Domus, Nottingham, UK) and stored overnight at 4°C. The plates were
washed and blocked using 200 µl/well of 3% v/v rabbit serum in PBS-T, and in-
cubated at 37°C in a dark heating cabinet for 60 minutes. After the blocking step,
samples were loaded using a similar technique as indirect ELISA. The plates were
diluted two-fold using the robot, and the plates were left on a shaking table in the
dark at room temperature for 60 minutes. After washing, 50 µl/well of digoxigenin
(DIG) coupled PPE were pipetted to the plates, and they were again left in the dark
on a shaking table at room temperature for 60 minutes. Afterwards 50 µl/well of
HRP labelled Sheep-anti-DIG-POD (Ref. 11633716001, ROCHE, Mannheim, Ger-
many) were pipetted to the plates, and they were once again left to incubate for
60 minutes in the dark on a shaking table at room temperature. The plates were
washed ﬁve times in the washer and two times manually, and 100 µl/well of TMB-
One were pipetted to the plates and they were left in the dark on a shaking table.
Exactly 12 minutes after applying TMB-One, 100 µl/well stop acid was pipetted to
stop the enzymatic reaction. Just as with the indirect ELISA, the absorbance was
measured at 450nm and 630nm as a reference wavelength and the Gen5 software
was used to calculate the dilution at the cutoﬀat ∆OD=0.1.

the non-parametric alternative to ANOVA, Kruskal-Wallis test was used followed

4 | Discussion
During this experiment, rats were given various doses of PPE with one of three routes
of administration with the aim of evaluating the impact of these two factors in the
eﬃcacy of inducing a tolerance that would provide the specimens with tolerance
even after attempting to sensitise the rats with an model optimised from a pilot
study.
During the second phase of the animal experiments, the animals in the control
group did not present allergen speciﬁc antibodies during the day before the third
week during the sensitisation. The sensitisation period in BN rats of three weeks
was also found during the pilot study, where all the animals were sensitised after
the third week, and have also been documented in other studies28.
For the subcutaneous routes of administration, no signiﬁcant change were observed
among the treated routes.
The results gathered from in vivo and the antibody
response points towards subcutaneous method having sensitised the animals instead
of inducing any tolerance.
For the doses from 1 µg to 100 µg we can support
the studies that claim that subcutaneous injections is a poor choice of route of
administration for inducing tolerance for allergens25, but we cannot predict if a dose
higher than 100µg or lower than 1µg would have had greater tolerance inducing
capabilities in the rats.
The i.g., sublingual, and oral routes of administration all showed a signiﬁcant decline
in the IgE produced after the second phase. It has been speculated that non-oral
introduction of the allergen is correlated with FA development8 and the IgE data
from these experiments seem to support this claim, since the subcutaneous method
resulted in highly sensitised rats.
The i.g., sublingual, and oral routes showed the greatest decline of IgE response in
from the groups given the highest dose. Some mechanisms of desensitisation are
believed to be dependent on repeated high-dose exposure of the allergen29, and this
may explain in part why the high doses seems to have provided better protection
against later allergy development.
Of the in vivo endpoints chosen to represent clinical manifestations of allergy (ear
swelling, symptom score, temperature, and faecal water loss), ear swelling was the
only endpoint to give a signiﬁcant change that mirrored any pattern that supported
the IgE antibody data.

Temperature have previously been used as an endpoint to measure anaphylaxis
where it showed a signiﬁcant where a signiﬁcant drop was observed over 120 minutes28,
but during this experiment many rats did not display a temperature drop, which
makes it impossible to conclude that any group experienced a signiﬁcant tempera-
ture drop. The ‘gold standard’ of temperature measurement is via a digital thermal
thermometer, but in this experiment the temperature was measured using subcu-
taneous temperature measurement microchip. This however, should not alone be
responsible for the data gathered, since the temperature microchips have been found
to closely resemble the results found with a digital rectal thermometer, within the
speciﬁcation of the equipment30. The lack of a temperature drop, and actual rise in
temperature after i.g. challenge initiation may be due to increased activity after a
change in the rats environment, even though rats usually experience a lowered motor
activity under anaphylaxis28, but the motor activity of the rats were not measured
during this experiment. It is possible that the rats did not experience an IgE medi-
ated reaction during the i.g. and oral challenges, and they, like humans sometimes
do13, produce allergen speciﬁc IgE without experiencing any allergic reaction.
For the ear swelling data there seems to be a severe diﬀerence in the ear swelling
data gathered from the male and female groups receiving treatment with i.g. and
sublingual. The dissimilarity on ear swelling data based on sex does not seem to
be correlated to diﬀerent eﬃcacy of the treatment, since other clinical data and the
IgE response does not show such patterns. Ear swelling test have been thoroughly
studied in various rodents. The development of the ear swelling test in mice found
no discernible diﬀerences between the response based on gender31.
The diﬀerence between the three routes that received 1mg of PPE shows that there
might be a diﬀerence in immune response between the three routes, since all the
animals that received 1mg of PPE via oral produced a fair amount of IgG1, but
only one animal in the remaining groups produced any IgG1. More data such as
gene expression and a larger variety of antibodies (i.e. subtypes of IgG) might need
to be analysed before any mechanisms can be identiﬁed.

5 | Conclusion
The i.g., sublingual and oral routes of administration shows great potential in pre-
venting development of peanut allergy in BN rats. The results in this study shows
that a high dose have greater potential, but cannot conclude if there is an upper
limit where the eﬃcacy drops or if it will simply stagnate. Most rats receiving 100
mg of PPE during this experiment, showed little to no production of allergen speciﬁc
IgE. The Sublingual doses could also be raised to provide more points of compar-
ison between these routes of administration. For subcutaneous administration the
doses or frequency should be revised if the preventative potential should be assessed
correctly. Furthermore, the clinical endpoints might need revision or addition, to
further assess if the rats is sensitised without displaying symptoms.
