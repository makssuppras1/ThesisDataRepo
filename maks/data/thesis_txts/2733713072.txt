Abstract
Abstract
Offshore Energy Hubs (OEHs) are a new concept in power systems. The purpose of an OEH
is to harvest the vast wind energy potential at sea and establish a solid energy sector coupling
and electricity generation to fight climate change. OEHs are considered multipurpose multi-
terminal high-voltage direct current (HVDC) systems, which interconnect multiple areas by
a hub that collects power generation from connected offshore wind farms (OWFs) and hosts
power-to-X (PtX) facilities for hydrogen production.
All connected components are converter-interfaced, i.e., the offshore AC grid can be consid-
ered inertia-less and with minimal passive damping. Some of the proposed configurations
require the parallel HVDC modular multilevel converters (MMCs) of the bipole HVDC inter-
connectors, used for power transmission to shore, to operate in grid-forming (GFM) mode.
The findings are valid for HVDC voltage source converters in general and are here referred
to as converters.
Stability concerns have been raised due to this design of the OEHs. One of these concerns
is the unintended active power sub-synchronous oscillations (SSOs) measured between the
GFM converters in time-domain simulations. The main objective of this master thesis is
thus to analyze the nature of these sub-synchronous active power oscillations and propose
ways to mitigate them.
The converters are controlled by a GFM power synchronization control (PSC). The instabil-
ity is studied by decreasing the impedance between the parallel converters. It is found that
the converters cannot properly synchronize their internal frequencies when the impedance
separating the converters becomes too small, which gives rise to instability by exponentially
increasing SSOs. The frequency deviation is imposed by the parallel GFM control of the
converters.
A small-signal model of the converter with the GFM PSC architecture is developed by the
component connection method to analyze the nature of the stability problem.
The thesis has found that a cross-coupling virtual impedance (CCVI) can stabilize the sys-
tem, even when the separating impedance approaches zero, without adding any new dy-
namics and perplexing the tuning process. This property of the stability-enhancing CCVI
supports the OEH functional requirement of expandability.
Impedance-based and eigenvalue-based stability frameworks are applied by bode plots, input
passivity index analysis, and root locus plots to confirm and describe how the CCVI provides
stability where the basic PSC does not.

generation. Its popularity might be explained by its intuitively control design, the various
stability-enhancing features, and its modularity. The thesis is thus motivated to study how
the VI can provide sufficient damping of the SSO by a cross-coupling virtual impedance

When the thesis title starts with the words "Stability analysis of ... ", it is obvious to ask
what kind of stability is of interest, as there are many domains of stability in power systems.
Classic stability topics count rotor angle stability, voltage stability, and frequency stability.

by IEEE PES [11]. Some of these domains are overlapping and indistinguishable in some

Hence, the thesis will investigate stability in the time domain via simulation and further prove

2.1.1
Stability Concerns
The offshore AC grid at the OEH is by virtue inertia-less, and each 1 GW converter unit
of each of the 2 GW bipole HVDC connection systems is only separated by the transformer
and arm impedance of the MMC on the AC side. By knowing the generic hub design, it
should be possible for the reader to relate the identified stability concerns of the OEH in
general. The concerns are briefly stated for an overview.
In [4, 5, 25], the following challenges are identified by NSWPH:
1. Uncontrollable power output of the OWFs (determined by their power plant controller),
2. The power sink mode of the HSCs limits flexibility of their grid-forming control,
3. Damping from loads is negligible as the PtX plants are converter-based.
4. Circulating power between the HSCs due to frequency controllers in parallel.
5. Active and reactive power oscillations between the HSCs,
6. Coordinated active power management during fault recovery from pole fault,
7. Trapped energy challenges with offshore AC faults,
8. Current limitation during asymmetrical faults of parallel GFM MMCs.
The OEH consortium furthermore addresses small-signal stability challenges on the AC hub
and in the HVDC grid [8]. In other literature [18], high-frequency resonance (HRF) between
MMCs from different vendors poses a stability concern in multi-infeed HVDC systems. In
[26], an HRF incident between an MMC and a weak grid is reported in China. The defini-
tion of a weak grid is disputed [27], but the point is clear; many different stability concerns
are raised when regarding the modular and scalable design of OEHs in general due to its
many interfaces and its multipurpose design. Any solution to the challenges thus begs for
simplicity and universalism.
It is recalled that this thesis only studies the 5th item in the abovementioned list; namely,
the active power SSOs between parallel GFM HSCs.

2.1.2
Remarks on the Operation Philosophy of the OEH
The multi-vendor design of the OEH promotes that the stability challenges are solved in a fa-
vored manner. This means that, even though the scope of the thesis is limited, some general
requirements for the solution design must be concluded by the assumed operation of the HSC.
Distributed vs. Centralized Control Architecture
Typically, power system components, such as wind turbines and HVDC interconnections, do
not have any communication between them. Only the transmission system operator (TSO)
requires in some grid codes capability to request the setpoints of the plant [28]. This then
promotes solutions to the stability challenges that utilize distributed control (known from
distributed generation and micro-grids) rather than centralized control [29].
Power-sharing Schemes
Because of the power sink mode of the GFM HSCs and the lack of controllability of the
active power generation, any power imbalance between active power reference and infeed
must be handled by a power-sharing scheme.
The proposed droop control in [8] serves a primary frequency regulation by its proportional
control action. If the active power imbalance persists for any reason, e.g., lack of communica-
tion because of the distributed control architecture, the power-sharing scheme by the droop
control implies that the HVDC spokes are non-dispatchable. This might conflict with the
requirements for generators (RfG), as the HSC cannot transmit exactly the power demanded.
In [30], it is suggested to implement a secondary regulation via an integral control action
on the power imbalance вҲҶP. The integral action would allow the individual HSC to ad-
just its power reference over time to neglect the power imbalance. This would not restore
the system to nominal frequency, as the other converters would also handle the additional
power, which the HSC, with secondary control, does neglect after some time. However, this
solution depends on whether the specific connected areas are considered dispatchable or non-
dispatchable. There is also a minimum requirement of one spoke not having the secondary
regulation activated to make the power-sharing scheme feasible.

HVDC Converters and HVDC System Topologies
The HVDC technology and system topology are fundamental for understanding the purpose,
operation, and design of the OEH. This is because the development over the last 20 years has
changed the operational constraints of HVDC systems in general, as these highly depend on
the specific topology and converter technology. As this is not the main topic of the thesis, the
different HVDC converter topologies and HVDC connection layout designs are only covered
briefly.
2.2.1
HVDC Converters
Among the conventional HVDC converters are the line commutated converters (LCC), the
two-level voltage source converter (VSC), and the MMC [31]. It is emphasized that the
MMC is a type of VSC. The corresponding traits and drawbacks of the HVDC converter
technologies are highlighted.
Line-Commutated Converters
The LCC technology made the commercial breakthrough of larger HVDC systems. It is
based on thyristors for inverting DC/AC and a 12-pulse rectifier-bridge for rectifying AC/DC.
The thyristors cause large harmonic content due to the firing angle, and power quality is
only assured when using bulky and expensive filters. Furthermore, smoothing reactors are
required on the DC side and reactive power compensation is also inevitable [19, chp. 1.].
An operational constraint is that the entire HVDC link must de-energize to change polarity
and re-energize to start transmission again.
Voltage Source Converters and Modular Multi-level Converters
The VSC-HVDC topology has the advantage over LCC technology on the following points:
VSC enables active and reactive power to be controlled independently and bidirectional.
This increases the operability and can improve the business case via ancillary services. Also,
VSC HVDC systems can change power flow direction without reversing the voltage polarity.
This enables continuous operation during a change of power flow direction [32].
Furthermore, MMCs with Half-bridge (HB) submodules (SM) have dominated since 2010
[33] and offer several advantages over conventional VSC-based converters, as they allow in-
dividual gating of switches, enhancing modulation capabilities, but with the drawback of
increased control complexity. The MMCs can thus synthesize a more sinusoidal modulation
voltage with reduced dv/dt, significantly lowering harmonic content (the total harmonic dis-
tortion (THD) is below 0.1%), and there is typically no filter required. Additionally, MMCs
facilitate lower switching frequencies due to the high number of SMs per arm with a minimal
rate of change in arm currents (di/dt) [32]. In [34], it is stated there is no need for an AC-
filter when the number of submodules of an arm exceeds 200. As an example, the INELFE
onshore HVDC connection (1 GW, Вұ320 kV) from France to Spain has 400 submodules in

Mostly for learning purposes, other important topics for understanding the MMC operation

Submodules
In [46], the conducting losses of 29 different SM topologies have been analyzed. It was con-
cluded that the HBSM is close to being the most efficient module while being very simple in
design, which is why it is favored in HVDC applications.

Switching Operation
The switching operation of an HB MMC can be found [19, chp. 16.]. There are 6 feasible
operational conditions of the HB SM. In normal operation, only one of the two IGBTs in
the HB SM is activated at the time. The current direction then dictates the current path
inside the SM. In blocked operation, the two IGBTs are turned off and the converter does
not convert power, as the current paths are simply conducted by the snubber diode in the
HB SM [51]. The blocked operation is used in protection schemes in case of faults [52], and
during energization [53].

Capacitor Balancing Algorithms (CBA)
During normal operation, as current goes into the capacitors, then the capacitor voltage
increases. When the modulation scheme requires more or fewer SMs to be activated, an
imbalance between the capacitor voltages is achieved over time. To reduce the negative
effect of the unbalanced capacitor voltages, the imbalance can be minimized by a capacitor
balancing algorithm or a proptional control action [39].
Circulating Current Suppression Control (CCSC)
The circulating current suppression control (CCSC)2 is caused by the inevitable voltage
unbalance of the arm phases. The circulating current has a second harmonic component,
which impacts the arm currents and increases the ripple effect on the SM capacitor voltage.
It is possible to eliminate these circulating currents by a capacitor in parallel to the arm
inductances (resonant filter), or by active control over the AC voltage reference [39, 48].
All of these details of the MMC are neglected in the thesis, but the mentioning is valueable
in understanding the difference between the assumptions of the investigated model and the
real dynamics of the MMC.

3.2.4
Virtual Impedance
The virtual impedance Zv serves many purposes, such as active stabilization, power flow con-
trol, harmonic reduction, unbalance reduction, and fault-ride-through operation [9]. Only
topics of active stabilization are in the scope of the thesis, especially damping of SSOs grid-
connected VSCs.
The virtual impedance can be realized by inner or outer virtual impedance controllers in
general, but not all functions of the abovementioned properties are associated with this de-
gree of freedom. The mitigation of harmonic instability, as an example, is only eligible at the
inner control loop because the outer virtual impedance loop is influenced by the bandwidth
of the current and voltage control loop [64].
As mentioned before, the PSC strategy decouples active and reactive power control actions.
This can cause a loss of controllability in coupled networks given certain network configura-
tions, as the current and voltage dynamics are not related in the controls but in the network
by the impedance. The control can thus become unstable when it is incapable of providing a
stabilizing voltage change by the inner voltage (modulated voltage) Edq for any given angle
perturbation because of the decoupled control architecture.

Phase-locked Loop
The synchronization of the grid-following converter often happens through a phase-locked
loop, where the conventional synchronized reference frame phase-locked loop (SRF-PLL)
model [66] is shown in Figure 3.10.
Figure 3.10: Conventional synchronized reference frame phase-locked loop (SRF-PLL)
The SRF-PLL transforms the phase voltages to dq-domain. By considering the direct-axis
to be related to voltage magnitude, and making a PI control action on the quadrature-axis,

The derived angle is then fed back to the Clarke-Park transformation.
Optimizing the transient performance of the SRF-PLL involves different filtering techniques,
but the implemented SRF-PLL will only be assumed to be based on a low-pass filter from
the physical measurement unit, also known as the LPF-PLL [66].

Stability Analysis
In this section, the stability properties of the OEH are analyzed via an impedance-based sta-
bility framework (by bode plots and input passivity index) and an eigenvalue-based stability
framework (by root locus plots, participation matrices, and mode shapes). The impedance-
based stability is only able to elaborate the individual converter-stability properties by the
open-loop frequency responses, as the closed-loop form is not derived. Instead, the system
dynamics are investigated by the eigenvalues of the system and the associated state matrix
[77].
To ease the flow of the stability analyses, the different theoretical background is outlined
for the impedance-based and eigenvalue-based stability framework before the analyses are
carried out. However, an exception is how the input passivity index is obtained, as it makes
more sense after evaluating the bode plots.
Note that the state space system of the OEH was defined in Chapter 5 and is fundamental
for the following section.

Impedance-based stability methods impose analyzing the admittance or impedance transfer
function of the converter by connecting it to another transfer function, representing the grid
dynamics [78]. The grid model can vary from a simple Thevenin equivalent, Zg = Rg + sLg,
or expressing unbalanced and frequency-dependent characteristics with a wideband model
[79]. The stability criteria from the impedance-based stability framework are based on the
general Nyquist criteria (GNC), and stability margins from gain and phase margins (derived
from bode plots and positive jПү) [80, 75].
However, concluding on stability from the open-loop frequency response of the HSC in a
multi-converter system is not sufficient, as the stability conclusion can vary on where in
the system the impedance is considered [80]. Therefore, any stability conclusions from an
impedance-based stability framework are considered outside the scope of the thesis. The
open-loop frequency response will thus only provide insights into the stability-enhancement
features of the virtual impedance, and establish a formulation for assessing the passivity
index of the converter at an evaluated frequency.

The differences between the PSCAD and the SSM are listed in the following and are due to
various reasons:
1. The OWF is modeled as a power source in PSCAD and a constant negative impedance
in the SSM, which might alter the damping of the system,
2. The power is filtered by a low-pass filter in the SSM instead of a smoothing time
constant as in the PSCAD model,
3. The high-reject filter of the measured currents and voltages are ignored in the SSM,
4. The SSM network impedance is per-unitized based on power flow, whereas the PSCAD
network impedance is based on ohmic values,
5. The SSM of the network is composed of algebraic equations, which might alter some
interdependent controller couplings at higher frequencies, which would also affect the
critical mode О»9,10.
It is thus acknowledged that the implemented SSM is not able to identify the critical
impedance and critical mode frequency exactly but is fairly close, due to modeling dif-
ferences. These differences contribute to the list of proposed future work presented later on.

Conclusion
The main goal of the thesis has been to study the active power sub-synchronous oscillations
(SSOs) between the parallel high-voltage direct current (HVDC) modular multilevel con-
verters with grid-forming (GFM) control in offshore energy hubs (OEHs). Hereby, it was an
objective to answer why these occur and how these can be mitigated while avoiding intro-
ducing new dynamics and maintaining the functional requirements on the expandability of
OEHs. The findings of the thesis are valid for HVDC voltage source converters in general
and are here referred to as converters.
The cause of the active power SSOs between the parallel converters relates to the individually
imposed frequency by the GFM power synchronization control (PSC). Because the resulting
frequency deviation from the GFM control provokes power gradients in time, the magnitude
of the impedance between the converters is crucial for stability conditions. However, due to
the OEH design, it is implied that the separating impedance is small and unknown.
The thesis showed by an expected operational point and dynamic equations that the reac-
tive power gradients could be neglected given the grid conditions. Time-domain simulations
further showed that the active power oscillations are observed as a classical hunting problem
between the converters. This was also verified by the mode shapes from the small-signal
model.
The mitigation of the active power oscillations was achieved by implementing the widely
researched and intuitive cross-coupling virtual impedance (CCVI) in the outer loop of the
GFM PSC. The basic PSC, without the CCVI, has decoupled the power synchronization
loop and the voltage regulation loop, which is sensitive to angle perturbations, and stability
is conditioned on external and unknown network damping.
Because the CCVI couples the loops via the current and voltage, and the current is already
a state in the system, the CCVI, and its stability-enhancing feature, do not complicate the
system further or require advanced tuning, as it is composed of algebraic equations. This
promotes a simplistic and universal solution design to the identified problem of active power
oscillations in inertia-less systems with minimal damping, and the solution can hopefully
benefit ongoing research activities in OEHs. It is considered that the CCVI should help any
GFM control strategy with decoupled control actions in general.

7. Conclusion
The verification of the stability-enhancing properties of the CCVI has been studied by the
impedance-based and eigenvalue-based stability framework. Bode plots of the open-loop fre-
quency response of the converter showed that the CCVI provided symmetry in the converter
admittance and additional damping for sub-synchronous frequencies. An input passivity in-
dex evaluation further proved that the converter is much less active in the sub-synchronous
frequency region, and that virtual inductance, enabling the coupling effect of the CCVI,
drives this property the best.
The input passivity index further showed that the virtual inductance should not be chosen
without care, as it would make the resonance point more prominent and shift it towards
higher frequencies related to the switching frequencies. The thesis thus recommends a vir-
tual inductance of 0.01 вүӨLv вүӨ0.1 pu, but emphasizes that the lower bound depends on
system configuration.
Root locus plots from the eigenvalues obtained from the complete SSM confirmed that the
CCVI achieves stability, even if the separating impedance is negligible, by shifting the un-
stable mode, related to the SSOs, far enough into the left-hand plane.
Future Work
It is desired to further mature the stability analysis of the HVDC converter from active
power oscillations by implementing the internal dynamics related to the MMC technology.
By upgrading the model to an average arm model, the stability of higher resonances, which
might be affected by the CCVI, can be studied properly. The model upgrade would impact
both the PSCAD model and the small-signal model.
Studying stability at higher frequencies further implies that the network model must be
evaluated as ordinary differential equations to better capture the dynamics above nomi-
nal frequency. Since the CCM is already applied, not much effort is required to finalize
this step. Utilizing CCM for capturing the network SSM would also obtain all state-space
system-related matrices, which would allow proper time-domain validation and other control
system checks and investigations, such as controllability and observability of the system.
It is of high interest to further conclude on the stability from the impedance-based stability
framework, and not much work is required to formulate a closed-loop formulation of a grid-
connected HVDC converter. Studies would imply assessing the Nyquist criteria and gain
margins and phase margins.
Developing an analytical framework for defining stability boundaries regarding power gradi-
ents provoked by frequency deviations across impedances would also be a novel continuation
of the presented work and provide better confidence in a recommended virtual inductance
functioning as a coupling factor.
