Project formulation 
Society is dependent on plastic materials in order to be able to support the growing population. If we consider 
the most recent projection of the world annual plastics production, it indicates that in 2050, 20% of the an-
nual production of oil should be used for preparation of plastic materials. This underlines the need to find 
alternative sources of raw materials that can be used for preparation of plastics to reduce this strain on 
depleting resources. This can either be done by improved recycling, by introduction of new bio-based poly-
mers or as is intended in this project, by using biopolymers originating from natural sources. Biopolymers do 
not have sufficiently good properties in their pure form and will therefore have to be formulated together 
with different types of plasticizers, additives and reinforcing agents in order to bring them to a property level, 
where we could expect that they could substitute some of the currently used plastics [1]. 
The objective of this project is therefore to investigate possibilities of preparation of biopolymer systems that 
can be formulated into classical plastic materials, with a focus on cellulosic and alginate-based systems. The 
project is intended to take part in research projects that are ongoing with the school of architects in Copen-
hagen as well as with some collaboration to a small company interested in Alginate based biopolymer sys-
tems. In this context a key performance parameter will be to make materials extrudable, to facilitate a simple 
and direct incorporation into currently used equipment. In the project, this will be attempted through blend-
ing with different types of plasticizers such as e.g. various polyols (glycerol, sorbitol, etc) but many other 
additives could also be considered. The project should be initiated with a literature study covering the current 
state of the art in biopolymer extrudates, with the purpose to identify particularly promising combinations. 
The experimental part of the project will focus on preparation of systems in a lab scale (possibly by simple 
solvent casting) and testing of materials parameters on these systems, before specific formulations should 
be tested in extrusion. If time permits, tests should be conducted in 3D printing of filaments or liquid formu-
lations.  
All the prepared materials should be fully characterized using classical analytical techniques such as FT-IR, 
DSC, tensile testing, whereas rheology could be employed as a technique to characterize the different for-
mulations and their flow behaviour.

Abstract 
The scope of this project was to enable extrusion of the naturally occurring biopolymer alginate through 
plasticization where glycerol was chosen as plasticizer. 
First, initial formulations based on dissolution of alginate were prepared with solvent casting. Then, water 
was attempted minimised for alginate-plasticizer solutions by freeze-drying, but drying was found to be in-
sufficient as wet, possibly glycerol-rich parts were still observed after drying.  
Secondly, alginate-water-glycerol formulations were prepared through speed mixing or manual mixing and 
tested with hot-pressing and extrusion. High glycerol contents of 80, 106, and 160 pph with no addition of 
water  and 75 or 150 pph glycerol with 30-90 pph water were tested for hot-pressing and lab-scale extrusion 
at 90°C. Water was found to help ensure glycerol incorporation in the alginate. Due to possible glycerol mi-
gration at high contents, 25 pph glycerol was tested based on promising formulations from literature with 
100 pph water. Extrudability was tested with water contents of 100, 125, and 150 pph, and 100 pph was 
found to be sufficient. Extrusion was optimised for the formulation with 25 pph glycerol and 100 pph water, 
AW100G25, by testing extrusion time, mixing method, and temperature. Manual mixing of the premix and 
5 min heating of the premix in the feeding chute with extrusion at 90°C was found to be sufficient to produce 
smooth, yellow, and transparent cylindrical extrudates. As reference, extrusion was also performed on a for-
mulation with 100 pph water and no glycerol, AW100. These formulations were also tested in a pilot-scale 
extruder to produce yellow, transparent sheet extrudates. The sheets were relatively wet and drying resulted 
in deformation of the sample shape to produce hard and smooth samples. This made tensile testing difficult 
due to slipping and insufficient load of the equipment. For the pilot-scale extrudates, only estimations of 
Young’s modulus could be obtained from dog bone specimens which were of poor quality due to many de-
fects after complications during injection moulding. Tensile testing was possible to perform on the cylindrical 
extrudates from the lab-scale extrusion and resulted in significantly lower and more reasonable Young’s mod-
uli and tensile strengths.  
Lastly, prepared extrudates were analysed by DSC which produced ambiguous results, hindering evaluation 
of plasticizer incorporation based on glass transition temperature. A possible melting or glass transition at 
- 56°C was observed for several of the produced extrudates but no trend was identified. Water and glycerol 
contents were attempted estimated by TGA but was found not to be an ideal method as water and glycerol 
evaporation overlaps.  
Future work should ideally include further minimisation of water within limits of processability to avoid large 
deformation when drying, and processing temperatures should also be optimised.

1 Introduction 
The world faces a great challenge of transitioning into a sustainable future with a more responsible use of 
resources. Mishandled fossil-based plastic waste is used as a tangible expression of the need for this transi-
tion. Plastic materials are necessary in many applications that provide value to society, such as food packag-
ing to reduce food waste [2]. Currently used plastic materials are primarily fossil-based thus contributing to 
the depletion of this resource. Alternatives to fossil-based plastics are therefore of great interest. 
One option is to find alternative polymers, and ongoing development of new plastic materials based on nat-
urally occurring polymers such as alginate, cellulose, and chitin has gained increasing attention in recent 
years [1]. These natural polymers are also interesting from an industry point of view due to non-toxicity, low 
cost and large availability of raw materials [3].  
In development of technical applications with these natural polymers, a key challenge is that the majority of 
naturally occurring polymers are not thermoplastic and thus unsuitable for existing thermoplastic processing 
methods such as extrusion. This is due to strong intermolecular forces such as hydrogen bonding between 
the polymer chains resulting in thermal decomposition occurring before melting. In order to make these 
natural polymers thermoplastic the hydrogen bonding must be weakened by chemical modification and/or 
physical modification using plasticizers. [1] Polyols such as glycerol are commonly used to plasticize natural 
polymers and plasticizers should ideally not be fossil-based.  
Additionally, the chemical and physical modification of the polymer pose challenges in relation to the prop-
erties of the finished material due to migration of additives, sticky surface, shrinkage of final shape due to 
water evaporation, colour, and possessing sufficient mechanical properties [1]. A balance between the plas-
ticization and performance of the material is therefore needed and depends on the application.   
In this project, the focus will be on plasticizing the natural polymer alginate for thermoplastic processing with 
extrusion. However, the literature study will also include the natural polymers chitin and its derivative chi-
tosan, as well as cellulose derivatives to give a broader overview of the general advancement in the field of 
processing naturally occurring biopolymers.

2 Literature study 
2.1 Material components 
Like conventional plastic materials based on petrol polymers, plastic materials based on biopolymers also 
need to be formulated to achieve the right properties for the selected application. In this section, the com-
ponents of such plastic materials will be introduced. Firstly, the relevant biopolymers and the theory behind 
plasticization will be briefly described. Additionally, blending of biopolymers will be discussed as an alterna-
tive and/or supplement to plasticization.  
2.1.1 Biopolymers 
The experimental part of this thesis considers the naturally occurring biopolymer alginate. However, a 
broader selection of biopolymers is investigated in this literature study to give an overview of the general 
advancement in the field of processing naturally occurring biopolymers. Biopolymers from natural origin can 
be proteins, polynucleotides, polysaccharides, but the focus will be the polysaccharides cellulose and rele-
vant derivatives, chitin and chitosan, alginate, and starch.  
Cellulose and chitin are two of the most important natural polymers commercially due to their abundance, 
accessibility, low cost, non-toxicity, biodegradability, and biocompatibility [4].  
In recent years, alginate has attracted similar attention due to large accessibility and non-toxicity. Alginate 
has been used as a safe ingredient in food systems as an additive controlling consistency, and promising 
properties for producing films. These properties in relation to the price also makes it an attractive biopolymer 
for development of new food and non-food applications. [5], [6] 
Starch is also an abundant low-cost biopolymer and has already been commercialised in packaging materials 
which are competing with petroleum-based packaging materials especially within the food industry [7]. 
Blends of starch and the above-mentioned biopolymers have been studied to great extent and are therefore 
included within the scope of this literature study.  
In the following section, cellulose and derivatives, chitin and chitosan, alginate, and starch will be briefly 
introduced.

Cellulose and derivatives

polymeric chains of cellulose are linear and strongly hydrogen bonded. This results in sheets of a highly crys-
talline matrix. Therefore cellulose is highly insoluble in water and degrades before melting. [4]

Sources of chitin are shells of crustacea, exoskeletons of insects, specific types of fungi, and marine organisms 
such as plankton and it is the second most abundant polysaccharide after cellulose [8]. The most common

Chitin has two structural forms – an α- and β-form. The α-form has the strongest intermolecular interactions 
and an antiparallel arrangement, whereas the β-form has the weakest interactions and parallel arrangement. 
The α-form is the most abundant. [4] 
Chitin has limited solubility due to the strong intermolecular interactions, which makes modification difficult. 
It is insoluble in water, alkali, and common organic solvents, but soluble in most solutions of organic acids 
with pH > 6. [10] The modified Chitosan is soluble in aqueous solution of acetic acid [11].

2.1.2 Plasticizers  
Biopolymers cannot be thermoplastic processed similar to conventional petrol-based plastics due to strong 
intermolecular hydrogen bonding between the polymer chains. To make the biopolymers more commercially 
attractive, thermoplastic processing of biopolymers must be enabled by weakening the strong intermolecular 
hydrogen bonds. This can be done by plasticization.  
Plasticization can be classified as external by physical modification and internal by chemical modification. 
External plasticizers interact with the polymer between the chains by intermolecular forces (hydrogen bond-
ing and van der Waals interactions). In internal plasticization covalently bound groups are introduced in the 
polymer backbone during polymerization or processing. The internal plasticizers are non-extractable as op-
posed to external plasticizers and will therefore not result in migration of plasticizer. However, internal plas-
ticized polymers often still require external plasticizers in order to ensure a suitable processing window. Fur-
thermore, chemical modification has problems with scaling up and high production cost, which make external 
plasticization more interesting from an industrial point of view. [1] 
A plasticizer reduces the glass transition temperature (Tg) of the polymer and as a result lowers the modulus. 
Decrease in Tg of the plasticized polymer results in increased flexibility and decreased melt viscosity thus 
enabling extrusion or moulding. This is hypothesised to be the result of increased free volume of the polymer 
which increases long-range segmental movement of the polymer molecules due to the motion of the low 
molecular weight plasticizer [8]. A typical external plasticizer is a low molecular weight organic compound 
with a low Tg, but can in some cases also be a miscible high molecular weight polymers with low Tg [4]. A 
problem with low molecular weight plasticizers is their tendency to migrate or evaporate from the polymer 
matrix, thus reducing mechanical and barrier properties over time [1]. 
The overall criteria of plasticizer selection are cost, compatibility with the polymer, stability, the effect on 
processability, and the permanence, which is the ability of the plasticizer to stay mixed with the polymer. 
Permanence is thus dependent on compatibility [8]. Therefore, it is not only relevant to look at the thermal 
properties, but also miscibility parameters for the polymer and plasticizer [1]. Relatively few of the reviewed 
studies measures Tg. 
Another term concerning plasticization is the efficiency, which is the Tg reduction per unit weight of plasti-
cizer. High efficiency is seen by a plasticizer’s ability to broaden the range of the glassy-to-rubbery transition, 
but is often victim of low permanence. [4]

reported as a weight percentage (wt%) of the polymer or the total mass. Therefore, when comparing for 
example glycerol with sorbitol, the same wt% will result in a larger amount of glycerol molecules than sorbitol 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
9 
 
molecules due to sorbitol’s higher molecular mass. This should be taken into account when different plasti-
cizers are evaluated. 
The properties of the processed plasticized polymers need to meet the requirements set by the intended 
application. Therefore, balance between plasticization and thereby the processability and the performance 
of the material is often needed. In the reviewed studies, the general trend for the effect of plasticizer on the 
mechanical properties is increase of elongation-at-break (EB) with increasing plasticizer content and decrease 
of the tensile strength (TS) and modulus. Also, inconveniences as additive migration and shrinkage of the 
final shape due to water evaporation are often unacceptable but can possibly be accepted to some degree 
in certain applications. Appearances such as colour and sticky surface can also limit the application.  
2.1.3 Blending of polymers 
Blending of polymers can also be an option in making biopolymers commercially attractive.  
Firstly, the cost of the material is an important factor. As an example, chitosan has a higher price than poly-
ethylene (PE), so a blend of chitosan and metallocene PE have been prepared to create a more affordable 
material with chitosans good antibacterial properties [11].  
Secondly, processability of the biopolymers can also be achieved through blending with already thermo-
plastic polymers such as PE and PLA [3], [9]. Alginate has also been used as a plasticizer for starch together 
with water to enable processability as well as better mechanical properties [39].  
Additionally, a thermoplastic starch (TPS), which is brittle and has high water sensitivity due to its hydro-
philicity, was added chitosan to increase the application possibilities by reducing the water affinity, as chi-
tosan is insoluble in water, and additionally to enhance the mechanical properties [10], [19], [20].  
Lastly, blending can also result in improved compatibility. Alginate has been used to improve homogeneity 
of a starch film, together with enhancing the mechanical properties [18].  
Blending requires miscibility, however in composites reinforcing agents are by definition completely immis-
cible [8].  
 
2.2 Solvent casting 
Alginate and chitosan films have primarily been produced by solvent casting. Solvent casting is a simple 
method to produce films but is not suitable for scale-up to industrial levels thus preventing commercial use 
[21]. Therefore, solvent casting is mostly useful for initial assessment of a biopolymer-plasticizer system.

2.2.1 Alginate 
Alginate films has primarily been plasticized with water and glycerol but also with sorbitol, xylitol, mannitol, 
polyethylene glycol (PEG), and fructose. The effect of Ca2+ as a crosslinker to improve the plasticized alginate 
has also been investigated.  
Firstly, the studies which does not use a crosslinker will be reviewed (Table 2.3).  
Ashikin et al. [13], plasticized M- or G-rich alginates only with water. It was concluded based on rheology that 
films prepared with M-rich alginates (G/M= 39/61) showed higher plasticity from G-rich alginates (G/M= 
63/37).  Only 2 and 4wt% alginate solutions were used which resulted in a high water-content in the films. 
However, this was not measured in the final films. Different drying temperatures were tested, and plasticity 
decreased from 40 to 60°C, which could be due to larger evaporation of water during drying and thus lower 
amount of plasticizing water in the tested films. Opposite of this, an increase in plasticity was observed at 
80°C and the authors explained this due to higher air-bubble formation in the film during drying. The highest 
film plasticity was thus concluded by the authors to be with M-rich alginate and drying at either 40 or 80°C.  
Bagheri et al. [5] studied M-rich (G/M= 40/60) alginate plasticized with water and 50wt% glycerol at different 
drying temperatures from 25-90°C. They found that the glycerol content of the films was affected by the 
drying temperature. They observed a decrease in the glycerol content with increasing drying temperature to 
approx. 26wt% glycerol at 90°C. This is contrary to expectations, as glycerol is considered a non-volatile plas-
ticizer with a boiling point at 290°C [5]. Additionally, as water has a boiling point of 100°C, much lower than 
for glycerol, the water content is also affected by the drying conditions, showing that even though Ashikin et 
al. [13] achieved plasticized films with a high water content, the water content and thus the film properties 
would not be stable as water evaporates.  
Avella et al. [15] also tested G-rich alginate with high molecular weight (G/M=61/39, 𝑀̅𝑤=1080kDa) and M-
rich alginate with low molecular weight (G/M=45/55, 𝑀̅𝑤=586kDa). M-rich alginate resulted in higher 
Young’s modulus and tensile strength (TS), but lower elongation-at-break (EB), with the opposite observed 
for the G-rich alginate. The low EB for M-rich alginate appears contrary to the findings from Ashikin et al. [13] 
that saw highest plasticization with the M-rich alginate. However, this  could be due to different 𝑀̅𝑤 of the 
alginates used, as Ashikin et al. [13] does not state the 𝑀̅𝑤 and is only using water as plasticizer. It also 
appears contrary to the higher stiffness for the G-block in alginate due to the rigid conformation but can 
perhaps be attributed to the plasticization of the alginate. 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
11 
 
Additionally, Avella et al. [15] observed better glycerol incorporation with the polymer chain for G-rich and 
high  𝑀̅𝑤 alginate. For  M-rich and low the 𝑀̅𝑤 alginate only slight interactions between glycerol and alginate 
were seen at high glycerol content resulting in low incorporation. 
Sorbitol has also been tested as a plasticizer of alginate together with glycerol by both Jost et al. [16] and Gao 
et al. [6]. Jost et al. reported that fewer molecules and higher mass of sorbitol were required compared to 
glycerol to obtain same properties, exemplified by 30wt% glycerol and 50wt% sorbitol. Furthermore, glycerol 
was observed to increase the water permeability of the material compared to the unplasticized alginate, 
where no change in water permeability was seen with sorbitol. Highest TS was observed for glycerol plasti-
cized films compared to sorbitol plasticized films but similar magnitude of EB were reported. Oppositely, Gao 
et al. [6] observed the highest Young’s modulus and TS for sorbitol plasticized alginate films, but lowest EB 
compared to glycerol. Mixtures of glycerol (gly) and sorbitol (sor) was tested in different compositions and a 
25wt% plasticizer mixture with 70% sorbitol resulted in a good compromise between TS/modulus and EB. 
The difference between mechanical property magnitudes for sorbitol and glycerol in the two studies could 
be due the difference in processing methods and/or differences in alginate polymer. However, mixtures of 
sorbitol and glycerol as plasticizers for alginate appear to be a promising solution.  
As mentioned, plasticized alginate films have also been prepared with a Ca2+ crosslinker to improve e.g. me-
chanical properties and water vapour permeability (WVP). (Table 2.2). However, this method does not appear 
to be suitable for thermo-mechanical processing as CaCl2 needs to be added slowly to avoid gelation as de-
scribed by Silva et al. [22] and Olivas et al. [7].  
Silva et al. [22] tested the drying rates at different temperatures for films solvent casted from an alginate-
glycerol-CaCl2-mixture to obtain the same moisture contents in the films, so higher drying temperature al-
lowed for reduced drying time. The highest temperatures, 50 and 60°C, resulted in decrease of mechanical 
properties due to considerable glycerol loss. Therefore, it was suggested that the temperature should not be 
above 40°C. Similarly, Olivas et al. [7] tested the conditioning of films after preparation by storing the films 8 
days at different relative humidities (RH). When comparing 59 and 98% RH, decrease in TS and increase in EB 
was seen for films plasticized by glycerol and sorbitol, showing these films absorb moisture from the air, 
which has a plasticizing effect.  
Olivas et al. [7] and Santana et al. [23] improved the water vapour permeability (WVP) by immersing the films 
casted with different plasticizers in dilute CaCl2 solutions thus crosslinking the surface. The lowest WVP was 
observed for fructose and sorbitol when compared with glycerol and PEG [7]. When glycerol, xylitol, and 
mannitol were compared, mannitol resulted in the lowest WVP and glycerol lowered the Tg of the alginate 
both before and after crosslinking of the surface [23].

2.2.2 Chitosan 
Like for plasticized alginate films, the effect of drying conditions on glycerol-plasticized chitosan films has also 
been investigated by Fernández-Pan et al. [24]. Drying condition combinations of 40°C, 80°C, 20% RH, and 
40% RH were used. No significant effect of RH was seen and could be due to low RH compared to the study 
by Olivas et al. [7]. This could also be due to a difference between alginate and chitosan. However, the tem-
perature affected drying kinetics and mechanical properties in the same way. The higher temperature re-
sulted in decrease of TS and EB as observed by Silva et al. [22]. Different molecular weights of chitosan were 
tested and did not affect the drying kinetics, but TS was seen to be larger for 780 kDa chitosan compared to 
the 430 kDa chitosan.   
Chitosan films with chitin nano-whiskers (CNW) has also been prepared by Kelnar et al. [33] and tested with 
diglycerol, tri-glycerol, tetra-glycerol and PEG600 as plasticizers. The CNW were incorporated to improve TS 
and modulus of the plasticized chitosan. Tetra-glycerol resulted in the highest modulus at all plasticizing con-
tents, and second highest EB after tri-glycerol. Vlacha et al. [25] tested the effect of nano-clay on chitosan-
based material to minimize phase separation, which was observed with dynamic mechanical analysis (DMA) 
above 10wt% of both of the tested plasticizers – glycerol and oleic acid. Glycerol resulted in better plastici-
zation due to higher EB and lower Tg but increased the WVP compared to unplasticized chitosan. Oleic acid 
reduced WVP and improved antibacterial properties. Phase separation has not been discussed or quantified 
in the reviewed studies above other than this by Vlacha et al., which is a shortcoming of the material prop-
erties.  
2.2.3 Cellulose derivatives 
Cellulose based materials have also been prepared by solvent casting. Methyl cellulose (MC) has been casted 
by Khan et al. [37] with malic acid and sorbitol as plasticizers (Table 2.4). The moisture uptake was investi-
gated, and a higher uptake was observed for the plasticized samples with stabilisation occurring after 7 days 
for both pure MC and plasticized films. The study focused on edible films and thus high WVP is desirable and 
observed for sorbitol together with higher thermal resistance and EB. Depending on the application different 
properties might be advantageous and low water permeability would for example not be wanted for pack-
aging. Additionally, has deep eutectic solvent (DES) consisting of choline chloride and urea also been used to 
plasticize regenerated cellulose by solvent casting [38]. 
Even though solvent casting is a frequently used method, solvent casting is not an ideal method to process 
polysaccharide-based films due to limited film thickness, low productivity in an industrial production, and the 
lack of possibility to customise the properties to different applications by blending with fillers or other ther-
moplastic polymers. [21]

2.3 Thermo-mechanical processing 
As previously mentioned, solvent casting is not suitable for scale-up to industrial levels which prevents com-
mercial use. Therefore, formulations suitable for thermo-mechanical processing such as mixing or kneading 
with subsequent pressing, extrusion, and injection moulding are necessary to be developed and optimised 
[21]. Alginate and chitosan have been formulated for mixing and kneading, whereas cellulose derivatives 
formulations have been prepared for extrusion. Starch-blends and -composites has been formulated for pro-
cessing by both mixing/kneading and extrusion.  
2.3.1 Mixing and kneading 
2.3.1.1 
Alginate films 
Alginate films has primarily been prepared by solvent casting, but also through thermo-mechanical pro-
cessing. The reviewed studies are summarised in Table 2.5. 
Two studies by Gao et al. [6], [21] investigates preparation of plasticized alginate films (0.5mm) by thermo-
mechanical mixing at 80°C, 15 min, and 100 rpm in an internal batch mixer with subsequent compression 
moulding by a hydraulic press for 15 min., 160 bar and at 110°C and 120°C, respectively. The prepared films 
were stored 14 days at 57% RH and room temperature (23-25°C) to stabilise properties. Both studies inves-
tigate the effect of plasticizer content.  
In the first study [21], glycerol is tested as the plasticizer together with water to ensure granular destruction 
of the alginate. The sodium alginate had a G/M-ratio of 50/50. Water content was kept constant at 35wt% 
and the glycerol content was varied at 0, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 wt% based on alginate. The plasticized films were 
a transparent brown/yellow colour due to the colour of the alginate powder. Increasing glycerol content 
resulted in decrease of TS and Young’s modulus and increase of EB until 30 wt% glycerol, where major seg-
regation phenomenon was observed by the surface becoming sticky due to glycerol migration towards the 
surface. From 10 to 20 wt% glycerol, the TS decreased from 20.8 ± 2.3 to 8.0 ± 0.2 MPa and the Young’s mod-
ulus decreased drastically from 934 ± 97 to 77 ± 20 MPa. The opposite was seen for EB which increases from 
12 ± 3 to 22 ± 6%, however the change is of smaller magnitude compared to TS and the modulus. According 
to the author, the moisture content of the films was “quite-high” compared to starch and chitosan films due 
to alginate being more hydrophilic and an increase was observed with increasing glycerol content due to 
increasing amount of hydrogen bonding of glycerol OH-groups and water.   
In the second study [6], sorbitol, glycerol, and mixtures of the two polyols were investigated. The single plas-
ticizers contents were 20, 25, 30, 40, 50 wt% with a water content of 50 wt% based on alginate. Additionally, 
three mixtures with sorbitol-glycerol ratios of 30:70, 50:50, and 70:30 of 25 wt% total plasticizer content 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
17 
 
were tested. These films were also transparent and brown for sorbitol or yellow for glycerol plasticized films, 
respectively.  
Based on scanning electron microscopy (SEM), Fourier-transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR), and X-ray dif-
fraction (XRD), sorbitol or glycerol insertion into the alginate network was confirmed. The smoothest, most 
homogenous surface was seen by SEM for 50 wt% sorbitol. For an increase in plasticizer content, more ho-
mogeneity of the films and decreasing thermal and mechanical properties were observed. Compared to sor-
bitol, glycerol was observed to plasticize better as shown due to the smaller size and higher hydrophilicity, 
and as a result these films had lower TS and Young’s modulus, higher sensitivity to water, and increased 
transparency.  
Water content was measured for the prepared films, except for 25 wt%. For 30 w% glycerol, 21.4±0.2% was 
measured and the water content increased with increasing glycerol content. For the 30 wt% sorbitol film, 
9.5 ± 0.1% water was measured, and the water content of the films was observed to depend very little on 
sorbitol content. For the gly/sor-mix with 70% sorbitol (25 wt% total), 14.0 ± 0.1% water was measured, and 
the water content decreased with increasing sorbitol content and decreasing glycerol content. The water 
content is thus seen to be affected to a great extent by the glycerol content. Furthermore, the water sensi-
tivity is shown in Figure 2.8, where films plasticized with 30 wt% sorbitol or glycerol, respectively, have been 
immersed in water for 5 min. at 23°C. The glycerol-plasticized film on the right (yellow) was reported to be 
largely dissolved and to have lost its shape integrity, whereas the sorbitol-plasticized film on the left (brown) 
was reported to be swollen and only partially dissolved with almost intact film shape. Similarly as for the 
other study by Gao et al. [21], segregation phenomenon with a plasticizer-rich microphase is observed for 
glycerol above 30 wt%. This is clearly seen by a large decrease in mechanical properties (TS and modulus) for 
films plasticized with plasticizer content above 30 wt%.

2.3.1.2 
Chitosan films 
Chitosan films has been processed by thermo-mechanical kneading by Matet et al. [26] and Epure et al. [27], 
and are summarised in Table 2.5. 
Epure et al. [27] studied the drying condition’s effect on the properties of chitosan films prepared with glyc-
erol as plasticizer. Glycerol and chitosan powder were manually mixed with 2% acetic acid solution to a paste 
with chitosan content of 20 wt% and then processed by blending an internal batch mixer (80°C, 15 min., 
100rpm) followed by hot-pressing (110°C, 250 bar, 15 min., 3 min. cooling, 10 min.) to obtain films of 1 mm. 
The chitosan had 97% DA, and the glycerol content was kept constant at 25 wt% of the glycerol and chitosan 
amount. The drying conditions influence the water content in the tested film. The mechanical properties 
were tested at 23°C with 33, 57, and 75% RH, and at 57% RH the highest EB (approx. 35-40%) and TS (approx. 
15 MPa) were seen. EB decreased with increasing exposure time at 33 and 57% RH, and the materials reach 
stable properties after 25-35 days. This is slightly different to when drying conditions were tested on a solvent 
casted glycerol-plasticized chitosan film, where no significant effect of RH on mechanical properties was seen 
[24]. However, this could be due to drying at higher temperatures of 40°C and 80°C, as well as with lower 
relative humidities of 20% and 40% .  
Matet et al. [26] studied the effect of the polyol plasticizers glycerol (gly), xylitol (xyl), sorbitol (sor), or mix-
tures on a 96% deacetylated chitosan. The tested concentrations were 10, 25, and 50 wt% glycerol, whereas 
sorbitol or xylitol was tested at 10 and 50 wt%. The mixtures gly/xyl, gly/sor, and xyl/sor had 25wt% of each 
component in the mixture. Before processing, the plasticizer was added dropwise to the chitosan powder, 
and then a 3% acetic acid solution was added in a 75:25 ratio and manually mixed. The different formulations 
were melt-blended in an internal mixer (80°C, 15 min., 100rpm) and subsequently hot-press moulded (110°C, 
15 min., 270 bar) to obtain films of 1 mm and 80µm. The effect of crystallinity on mechanical properties was 
seen. With increasing polyol content, the crystallinity and elongation-at-break (EB) decreased, and the tensile 
strength (TS) increased. The authors suggest this is due to phase separation between plasticizer and chitosan 
in the range between 10 and 25/50wt% plasticizer. This is in agreement with the observation by Gao et al.

Thermomechanical mixing or kneading has also been employed for starch blend formulations. The reviewed

Starch-blends with alginate and with or without water has been tested by López et. al [28] and Córdoba et 
al. [12], respectively.  
Córdoba et al. [12] studied the effect of the alginate composition and water content on film properties. Corn 
starch containing approx. 28% amylose blended  with sodium alginate (Mw = 210 kDa) at 5, 10, and 15 wt% 
was plasticized with 35 wt% glycerol and water was not added. The processing was done using a batch coun-
ter-rotating twin screw mixer at 140°C, 32 rpm, 8 min followed by compression moulding at 140°C, 15 min 
and 21.07 MPa (200.7 bar). Torque was monitored through the processing, and plasticization energy and 
steady state torque decreased with increasing alginate content. Mechanical properties decreased, except EB 
which increased together with impact resistance. Tg was determined for all samples. The unplasticized sam-
ple had a Tg of -69.48°C and at 5% alginate an increase to -66.61 was seen followed by a decrease from - 79.34 
to -86.98°C from 10 to 15wt% alginate. This shows alginates plasticizing properties on starch. Granular struc-
ture of starch is argued to be reduced based on SEM. However, López et al [39] argues that the disruption of 
starch granulates is not sufficiently efficient in the study by Córdoba et al. [12] and therefore questions the 
usability of films processed without water. López et al. [39] does however stress the advantage of alginates 
plasticizing effect in minimizing water content.  
López et al. [28] have also tested starch-blends with chitosan or chitin with glycerol and absence of acetic 
acid as used in solvent casting to solvate chitosan. The formulations with varying chitosan (82.5% DA) or 
chitin contents were processed by mixing at 140°C and 50 rpm for 15min, stored for 7 days at 60%RH and 
25°C, followed by thermo-compression at 140°C for 6 min with pressure increase from 80 to 180 kg/cm2 to 
obtain 1 mm thick films. Corn starch (24% amylose) was plasticized with 30 wt% glycerol and 45 wt% water 
and blended with 5 and 10 wt% chitosan and 10wt% chitin, respectively. From SEM a homogenous and 
smooth surface was observed with no glycerol migration visible. The mechanical properties and water vapour 
permeability (WVP) data was evaluated by statistical ANOVA analysis. The elastic modulus increased from 
approx. 1050 MPa to approx. 1150 MPa for the two chitosan-blends with no concentration dependence and 
no significant effect from chitin. TS was increased by 10wt% chitosan and chitin from approx. 11 MPa (no 
chitosan/chitin) to 12 MPa. The chitosan samples showed decreased in EB from approx. 2.4 to 1.6% with no 
effect from chitin.  The WVP was decreased for 10 wt% chitosan and chitin from 1.33 ∙ 109  g/(s m Pa) to 
approx. 0.87 and 0.59 respectively. Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) showed that incorporation of

were also evaluated and showed reduction of S. aureus and E. coli.  
Furthermore, a starch composite with microcrystalline cellulose (MCC) plasticized by sorbitol, glycerol or sor-
bitol-glycerol mixtures have been studied to obtain suitable processing conditions in relation to plasticizer 
and MCC content. Wheat starch with 87% and 25% amylose, respectively, were melt processed with a plas-

2.3.2.1 
Starch-blends and -composites 
Starch blended with alginate and chitosan, respectively, has also been prepared by extrusion. The reviewed 
starch-blends and -composites are summarised in Table 2.7. 
Souza et al. [18] prepared a starch-alginate blend plasticized by glycerol and water by either single-screw 
extrusion or twin-screw extrusion. Alginate content of 1-10 wt% was tested with 15 wt% glycerol. The starch 
was based on corn and had an amylose content of 20%. Processing by single-screw extrusion was done at 
temperature profile of 70-115°C from feeder to die and 20-40 rpm. The blends were pelletised and pressed 
at 110°C and 0.92 bar for 15 min. to obtain 1 mm film. Extrusion with a twin-screw with a slit die 
(25.0 × 1.0 mm) was done at a profile of 70-95°C and 40-50 rpm. The alginate-starch blend was extruded at 
lower temperature than starch alone as the temperature profile of thermoplastic starch was 90-130°C. To 
condition the samples, drying at 50°C followed by 96 h storage at 25°C and different RH was done to obtain 
5, 9, and 15% water in the prepared films. Tensile tests, DMA and SEM was performed on the samples. SEM 
showed a homogenous surface of the blend. Mechanical properties were affected by water and alginate 
content.  For increasing water content, the Young’s modulus and TS decreased and EB increased. 3% alginate 
(approx. 40-50% EB for 9-15% water) resulted in higher EB compared to 6% alginate (20-25% EB for 9-15% 
water). TS was in the range from 5 to 20 MPa and the Young’s modulus in the range of 1200 to 100 MPa for 
5% and 9% water for the different alginate contents. 
Dang et al. [10] and Mendes et al. [19] prepared starch-chitosan films.  
Dang et al. [10] first prepared pellets using a twin-screw extruder at 90-125°C and 170 rpm followed by film 
preparation by a single-screw extruder at temperature profile of 130-140°C  and 35-45 rpm with a ring shaped 
die as film blowing attachment at 150°C. Cassava starch was processed with chitosan (85% DA, Mw=500 kDa) 
in concentrations of 0.37, 0.73, 1.09, and 1.45% in a 1% (v/v) acetic acid solutions with 25 wt% glycerol. The 
prepared films were tested at 42 and 62% RH. TGA showed decrease in weight reduction with addition of 
chitosan and increase of content, so the starch-chitosan blends have higher thermal stability than thermo-
plastic starch (TPS). Melt flow index (MFI) was also measured and showed increase in melt viscosity with 
incorporation of chitosan and with increasing content of chitosan. Tg was estimated and showed an increase 
from 16°C for TPS to 19.2-22.1°C for the TPS-chitosan blends. These trends were explained to be  due to 
interactions between starch and chitosan. Mechanical properties followed this trend and showed increase 
of TS and Young’s modulus and decrease of EB with incorporation of chitosan. It was found that the RH has 
a larger effect on the mechanical properties than the chitosan content as a result of water’s plasticizing effect. 
RH of 62% resulted in higher EB, very small and little effect of chitosan content and 42% RH showed higher

Cellulose acetate and cellulose blends 
Thermoplastic processing by extrusion of the investigated biopolymers has primarily been done for cellulose 
acetate (CA). Triethyl citrate (TEC) has been used as plasticizer and triacetin (TA) has been used as plasticizer

Zepnik et al. [35] investigated TEC contents of 15, 20 and 25 wt% with a CA (DS = 2.5) and without water. 
Extrusion of strands was done in a co-rotating twin-screw extruder at constant throughput of 10 kg/h and 
screw speed of 320 rpm with injection of the liquid plasticizer into a water bath and subsequently pelletised. 
Specific mechanical energy input (SME) as well as melt flow rate (MFR) and shear viscosity at 220°C was 
evaluated during extrusion. The die pressure, SME, and shear rate are observed to decrease with increasing 
TEC content. Films were prepared by compression moulding at 197°C with a thickness of 0.2mm. Foam ex-
trusion test were also performed with a single-screw extruder and a strand die at temperature profile of 200-
195°C, die temperature of 185°C, and a screw-speed of 12 rpm with 1 wt% of chemical blowing agent Unicell 
TS (p-toluene-sulfonyl-semicarbazide). The foaming ability was investigated with SEM and increase in pore 
size was seen with increasing TEC content. Too high plasticizer content results in the foam collapsing. The 
authors deemed the optimal TEC content to be 20 wt% based on good balance between foam forming prop-
erties and rheology.  
Wang et al. [34] compared PEG200 (Mw = 200) and TEC as plasticizers of CA (DS = 2.45). The extrusion was 
done by 3-zone twin-screw extruder with a temperature profile of 190-220°C and 200 rpm followed by injec-
tion moulding at 190-220°C to obtain standard specimens for mechanical testing. Before extrusion the plas-
ticizer was mixed with CA in a high-speed mixer with plasticizer content of 15, 20, 25 wt%. The injection 
moulded specimens were evaluated by rheology, solubility, mechanical and thermal properties. MFI was ob-
served to increase with increasing plasticizer content and PEG200 resulted in higher values than TEC, i.e. at 
25 wt% 5.9 and 10.4 respectively. The mechanical testing showed larger TS and lower EB for TEC than 
PEG200. PEG200 resulted in higher izod-notched impact strength. The properties for 20 wt% TEC are 
52.6 MPa TS, 22.9% EB, and 4.8 kJ/m2 impact strength and 20 wt% PEG200 resulted in 37.5 MPa TS, 32.9% EB, 
and 10.8 kJ/m2 impact strength. Thermal properties determined by DMA and DSC generally showed a lower 
storage modulus and Tg for PEG200-CA than TEC-CA. A large variation was seen for Tg measured by DMA and 
DSC and the measured Tg-values were in the range of 99-156°C including different plasticizers, content, and 
method. The solubility was evaluated by Hansen solubility parameters. The parameters of PEG200 (δ=24.3) 
and CA (δ=25.1) are much closer than for TEC (δ=17.0), which indicate better compatibility of PEG200 and 
CA.

2.4 Comparison of processing methods 
Several studies have been done to investigate the effect of different processing methods on the biopolymers. 
Solvent casting and thermo-mechanical mixing or extrusion have been compared for alginate. Chitosan-

2.5 Formulation without water 
Alginate and chitosan have primarily been processed with water by solvent casting or in thermo-mechanically 
processed blends. However, Córdoba et al. [12] attempted to prepare starch-alginate blends processed by 
thermo-mechanical blending without water as described in section 2.3.1.3. As mentioned, López et al. [39] 
commented that full destruction of starch granulates had not occurred as result of no water in the formula-
tion even though the plasticizing effect of alginate on starch was an advantage in minimizing water content. 
Gao et al. [21] used water in their processing of alginate and glycerol by thermo-mechanical blending, but 
full destruction of granulates was not achieved as small amount of alginate was still observed in the SEM 
image. As a solution, they suggested increasing the temperature, shear force, and water content based on 
the findings of Ashikin et al. [13] and Avella et al. [15]. This aligns with the comment from López et al. [39]. 
Thus, water appear to have a destructive effect of polymer granulates in the prepared films and can therefore 
not necessarily be completely avoided in formulations. Furthermore, a starch-cellulose fibre composite was 
prepared without water by Battegazzore et al. [30] but it was not discussed if full destruction of starch gran-
ulates was achieved, so this could also be a disadvantage of this processing method.  
Lastly, none of the reviewed cellulose acetates were extruded with the use of water. This could be attributed 
to the modification of cellulose to CA has introduced thermoplastic behaviour for the polymer, so the plasti-
cizing effect of water might not have been necessary. The glass transition temperatures of unplasticized CA 
are in the range of 190-202°C and depend on the degree of substitution [1], hence the need to decrease Tg 
of CA further with external plasticizers for better processability as seen by the reviewed articles. 
The reviewed plasticized films absorb moisture from the air after preparation as seen by the long storing 
times of the films after preparation to achieve stable properties. Therefore, it is not only the water in the 
formulation that should be the focus but the water content in the prepared films in general. For plasticized 
alginate films, the moisture uptake could be hindered as the water vapour permeability was shown to de-
crease by  immersing the films in dilute CaCl2 solutions to crosslinking the surface [7], [23]. 
As mentioned, water evaporation poses a challenge in relation to property stability of prepared materials 
due to the large plasticizing effect of water on the natural polymers and derivatives. Further studies should 
therefore be done to minimize the water content both by formulation and conditioning of the prepared films 
also keeping the destruction of the polymer granulates in mind.

3 Experimental plan 
The scope of the project is to prepare thermoplastic alginate through plasticization to enable processability 
by extrusion.   
In the first phase, a preparation method to sufficiently incorporate the plasticizer in the alginate polymer will 
be investigated with preparation by solvent casting (part A) and by extrusion (part B).  
Part A will be conducted to enable selection of suitable plasticizers and content ranges for further testing. 
Films/sheets will be prepared from an aqueous solution of alginate with different plasticizers and plasticizers 
contents. Glycerol, sorbitol, glycerol/sorbitol-mixtures, and possibly xylitol and mannitol will be tested as 
plasticizers. The alginate-plasticizer solutions will be solvent casted, dried, and hot pressed.  
In part B, water content will be attempted minimised within the limits  of processability and if possible, in a 
water free version. A mixture of alginate, water, and promising plasticizers and plasticizer contents from 
part A will be extruded, dried, and hot pressed with subsequent conditioning to stabilize the water content. 
Minimising the water content will be done by adding a range of different water contents in the mixture or 
testing removing the water pre-extrusion in the drying step, possibly by freeze drying.  
Incorporation of the plasticizer will also be compared for the samples prepared by solvent casting and extru-
sion. 
The sufficiency of the plasticizer incorporation will be evaluated on the glass transition temperature based 
on DSC, as well as microscopy and FT-IR. Furthermore, water content in the conditioned films and mechanical 
testing will be performed in part B.   
The second phase will focus on the processing of the plasticized alginate and will largely depend on the results 
from the first phase. Processing condition such as temperature and the scale of the extruder will be investi-
gated and evaluated with mechanical tests and possibly rheology.  
If time permits, a suitable formulation could be tested in the 3D-printer at the Architect School.

4 Results and discussion 
In order to achieve the ideal level of alginate plasticization for thermo-processing by extrusion, the research 
focused on investigating different formulations with hot-pressing as initial tests and then directly in the ex-
truder as the solvent casting procedure is less representative of thermo-processing. 
Firstly, prepared solvent casted films will be discussed in relation to selection of plasticizer and general back-
ground for plasticizer content and method of incorporating the plasticizer. Then, formulations for extrusion 
and minimisation of the water content will be discussed. Premixes have been prepared with high and low 
glycerol contents and varying amounts of water. To evaluate if the premixes are suitable for extrusion, hot-
pressing and testing directly in the extruder have been employed. Subsequently, the optimisation of the ex-
trusion process with a lab-scale extruder and following extrusion in a pilot-scale extruder will be discussed. 
Lastly, collective evaluation of the prepared extrudates will be done based on DSC analysis, tensile testing, 
and water content determined from TGA analysis. The clear division in phases described in the experimental 
plan was not possible to follow completely due to overlapping of the phases in the practical execution of 
experiments.  
The analytical data of the DSC, TGA and FT-IR analysis as well as tensile testing performed to evaluate the 
samples can be seen in appendix B, C, E, and D, respectively. 
 
4.1 Nomenclature 
The prepared samples are named after the composition of the prepared premixes, such as AW100G25. A 
refers to alginate, W refers to the water in the premix, and G and S refer to glycerol and sorbitol, respectively. 
The numbers following W, G or S refer to the parts  per hundred (pph) of the pure plasticizer or total water 
amount used in the premix based on pure alginate without the residual water content of alginate or plasti-
cizer. When no number is listed after W either no water has been added during the sample preparation and 
only the residual water from the alginate powder or the plasticizer is present in the sample, or the alginate 
has been dissolved in a large amount of water which has since evaporated thus indicating the water amount 
in the liquid premix is not useful to state in the sample name. Lastly, repetitions of a sample are denoted with 
# and the number of the repetition, such as AW125G25#2. The water and plasticizer content has been stated 
in pph based on weight to indicate that it is based on the alginate amount only, and to not confuse it with 
wt%. In appendix  A.1 the conversion between wt% and pph is reported.

4.3 Formulation for extrusion and minimisation of water 
4.3.1 Freeze drying 
Initially, water minimisation in formulations were thought to be done by preparing dissolutions of alginate 
with plasticizer and then removing the water to avoid deformation of the extruded material as a result of 
water evaporation. A method of removing the water is to freeze dry the liquid samples to form foams which 
could ideally be fed directly into the extruder. Therefore as mentioned in section 4.2.1, samples based on 
Gao et al. [6] were prepared with glycerol, sorbitol, or a gly/sor-mixture by dissolution of alginate and plasti-
cizer by either speed mixing or mixing by magnetic stirring with heating. This was prior to the decision only 
to focus on glycerol as the plasticizer.   
The freeze-drying method resulted in complications as it was very difficult to remove the sample from the 
round-bottomed flasks and the samples were not dried homogenously as liquid and possibly plasticizer-rich 
regions were found after freeze-drying. This is illustrated in Figure 4.5a,b for AWG27 after freeze-drying 16 h 
and AWG8S19 after hot pressing the freeze-dried foam. This could be due to insufficient incorporation of 
plasticizer and/or the sample not being frozen sufficiently before freeze-drying due to the lower freezing 
point of the plasticizers compared to water.  
Furthermore, DSC was performed on the speed mixed samples as they appeared the driest due having dried 
overnight after freeze-drying before being inspected. The second cycles of the DSC curved are shown in Fig-
ure 4.4, where several smaller transitions can be observed for all samples. This could indicate several phases 
of the material with individual glass transitions and therefore meaning the plasticizers have not been incor-
porated in the alginate sufficiently. Similar, step transitions around +80°C was observed for all samples. 
By closer inspection a glass transition like step-change at approx. -31°C can be observed for the sample pre-
pared with the gly/sor-mixture. Gao et al. [6] stated this mixture as the most promising in terms of mechanical 
properties of their prepared samples. The use of sorbitol is therefore not uninteresting, the focus was just 
selected to be on glycerol to narrow down combinations that were to be optimised.

glass transition temperature. However, the large step transitions could also be an indication that not all of 
the glycerol has been incorporated in the alginate as the samples were also observed to be different degrees 
of sticky, which could indicate migration or insufficient incorporation of the plasticizer as stated by Gao et al.

be hot pressed at lower temperatures than 90°C, such as r.t. and 40°C. As seen in Figure 4.6, the produced 
sheets were more opaque compared to the sheets hot pressed at 90°C. Additionally, was 120°C tested as it 
was the hot pressing temperature used by Gao et al. [6] after thermo-mechanically mixing their samples. This 
resulted in degradation of the alginate as seen by the darker colour in Figure 4.6. The suitable temperature 
for further development of thermo-processing the alginate samples was therefore selected to be 90°C.

4.4 Optimisation of AW100G25 extrusion 
4.4.1 Lab-scale extrusion 
Different variations of extruding the AW100G25 formulation were performed to optimise the extrusion pro-
cedure with seven repetitions of the premix. In Figure 4.16, the produced extrudates are shown for each 
AW100G25 repetition and in Figure 4.17, the premix and inside the extruder after end production of extru-
date are shown.  
For premix #1 and #2, respectively, approx. 11-13 g of the chopped premix was fed through the chute in two 
rounds. The previously mentioned problem of the sample being compacted and getting stuck in the chute by 
lowering the piston immediately after feeding the sample, was solved by leaving the sample to heat 15 min 
with the piston up and the motor on. This enabled lowing the piston and pressing the sample into the ex-
truder barrel. The sample was let cycle 10 min before feeding the second half of the sample, repeating the 
heating and cycle, then flushing the extrudate. This resulted in a total of 40-50 min in the extruder before 
flushing. This was done to enable cycling of the material as done for the standard polystyrene extrusion de-
scribed in the manual for the MiniLab extruder. The prepared formulas have higher viscosity than melted 
polystyrene and thus need longer time to move through the extruder. Additionally, the hot-pressed samples 
described in section 4.3.5 (Figure 4.14), were very soft and gel-like even after 30 min in the hot press at 90°C 
and no degradation of alginate was observed. Thus, a long extrusion time at the same temperature was not 
expected to degrade the sample as the additional effect of the shear force from the screws was not

considered initially. However, the discolouring of the sections of the extrudate seen in Figure 4.16 (e.g. for 
#1 and #3) witness to the degradation of the alginate. Furthermore, the sample in the cycle chamber after 
extruding AW100G25#2 50 min was black compared to the AW100G25#1 as seen in Figure 4.17. The majority 
of the AW100G25#2 extrudate was discoloured matching the sample in the cycle chamber.

 Extrusion of AW100G25#4 was performed similarly. Feeding 13 g sample over two arounds with 15 min

amount of extrudate produced. However, the waiting time during the extrusion after feeding each portion 
of sample to ensure the sample not getting stuck in the chute is as mentioned a problem.

predominantly smooth extrudate. Parts of the extrudate surface were slightly frayed.

4.5 Evaluation of extrudates 
4.5.1 DSC 
4.5.1.1 
Extrudates with high glycerol content 
To investigate the effect of plasticizer content and processing method on the plasticization of the alginate, 
DSC has been performed on the extrudates and premixes of selected samples described in the sections 
above. The second cycle of the DSC analysis from -90°C to +180°C is used as volatiles such as unincorporated 
glycerol and water will have largely evaporated in the first cycle.  
Firstly, the samples prepared with high glycerol content and varying water contents will be evaluated. DSC 
curves of these premixes and extrudates are presented in Figure 4.19 with the DSC curve of the alginate 
powder and glycerol as reference. Compared to the alginate powder, a stabilising effect is seen for all plasti-
cized samples. Large step changes indicating a glass transition are seen for the AWG75#2 premix, as well as 
the AW30G151 premix and extrudate. These step changes are similar to the ones seen for the hot-pressed 
samples AWG80, AWG106, and AW160 in section 4.3.2, Figure 4.7. The transitions for AWG75#2 and 
AW30G151 are between -45°C to -55°C with the 150 pph glycerol content resulting in the lowest transition 
temperature as expected for increase in plasticizer.  
However as previously discussed, these clear transitions might not be evidence of efficient plasticisation but 
could be the result of a glycerol-rich phase not incorporated sufficiently in the alginate. As observed for the 
paste premix AWG150 and the solid AW30G151 premix in section 4.3.2., no water results in less incorpora-
tion of glycerol at high contents as seen by 30 pph water changing the premix from a paste to a solid mass. 
Therefore, is it more likely that the large transitions of AWG75#2 (blue curves) compared to the less distinct 
transitions of AW61G75 and AW90G75 (grey and green curves) are expressions of glycerol being in a separate 
phase. This questions if the large transitions of AW30G151 is simply due to more glycerol resulting in better 
plasticization or resulting in a larger separate glycerol-rich phase. 
When comparing the respective premixes and extrudates, a difference in DSC curves is seen for all formula-
tions except AW91G76 (green curves). For sample AW61G75 (grey curves) the curve below 36°C is shifted 
from premix to extrudate to show a slightly more defined step change. For AWG75#2 (blue curves) the clear 
step change is flattened to show the possibly separate glycerol-rich phase has been decreased. This could 
==PAGE:70==

indicate better incorporation of glycerol or that the glycerol content of the tested sample has decreased from 
premix to extrudate.  
Lastly, peaks are seen at 61°C and 75°C for the AWG75#2 premix and AW90G75 extrudate, which could indi-
cate a crystallisation temperature resulting from glycerol, however such peak is not seen for the pure glycerol 
(purple curve). It could also possibly be an error or jump in the analysis.

to approx. -83°C [40].

Furthermore, the manufacture of the DSC instrument, TA Instruments, describe in a technical document [41] 
that molecular relaxation can cause glass transitions to appear such that it is misinterpreted as a melting

also possibly be glass transitions and thus giving an explanation for these peaks seen for the extrudates.  
The interpretation of the DSC depends on the peak being a melting  or glass transition. A melting peak would 
indicate the glycerol either results in a melting transition of the plasticized alginate or insufficiently incorpo-
rated glycerol melts at this temperature. On the other hand, a glass transition would indicate plasticization 
of the alginate.

peaks, it is difficult to make a clear-cut interpretation of the DSC curves.

The attempted tensile testing is described under the experimental section in 7.7.4. The extrudate sheets of

flattened as the drying made the sample edges bend upwards as previously described. Rectangular speci-

On the ElectroForce tensile testing equipment, the problem was not the specimen slipping from the clamp, 
but the load of 50 lbf – approx. 222 N – being insufficient to break the sample. The dog bones produced by 
injection moulding the extrudate just after extrusion, was then the only dry samples available for testing.

5 Conclusion 
The development of a suitable formulation of alginate for thermo-processing by extrusion was investigated.  
Glycerol and sorbitol were initially tested as plasticizer, but glycerol was selected for further development. 
Minimisation of water in the sample was attempted by freeze-drying aqueous alginate-plasticizer solutions, 
but the drying was found not to be sufficient due to remaining liquid and possibly glycerol-rich regions of the 
dried foams.  
Solvent casting was used to test incorporation of plasticizer and mixing procedure for dissolved alginate and 
plasticizer. Speed mixing proved the most useful method to ensure incorporation of glycerol and the order 
of mixing in glycerol could not be significantly distinguished.  
Speed mixing alginate with high glycerol contents of 80, 106, and 160 pph with no addition of water were 
prepared and tested by hot-pressing at r.t., 40°C, 90°C, and 120°C to find 90°C as the most suitable temper-
ature for processing. Subsequently, addition of water in the premixes was found to be important for incor-
poration of glycerol as speed mixing AW30G151 resulted in a solid premix whereas the AWG150 premix was 
a paste.  
Samples with high glycerol contents of 75 and 150 pph and varying water contents were then tested in a lab-
scale extruder. AW30G150 and AW61G75 were possible to extrude, but samples AWG75 and AW30G75 
proved too viscous to extrude. Due to the possibility of plasticizer migration at high glycerol contents and 
observation of possible glycerol evaporation after hot-pressing, 25 pph glycerol was decided upon based on 
promising formulations from the literature. 
To ensure proper flow through the extruder, water contents of 100, 125, 150 pph were tested for 25 pph 
glycerol. All water contents enabled extrusion, thus 100 pph was found to be a sufficient water content. A 
water content of 100 pph was also sufficient to produce an extruded reference sample without glycerol, 
AW100. 
The extrusion process was optimised for the promising AW100G25 formulation with 100 pph water and 
25 pph glycerol. Extrusion time, speed mixing or manual mixing, and temperatures of 80, 90, or 100°C were 
tested. It was found that short extrusion times were needed to avoid degradation of the alginate. Addition-
ally, both mixing methods and extrusion at both 90 and 100°C resulted in visually promising extrudates. The 
optimised procedure consists of feeding the manually mixed AW100G25 through the feeding chute and leav-
ing it to heat 5 min in the chute to avoid blockage of the entrance to the extruder barrel. The sample could 
==PAGE:82==
 
 
73 
 
then be moved into the extruder barrel with a piston and extruded to produce a smooth, yellow, and trans-
parent cylindrical extrudate.  
In continuation hereof, manually mixed AW100G25 and AW100 was tested with a sample size of 100 g in a 
pilot-scale extruder to producer sheet extrudates. Bubbles were observed in the extruded sheets and could 
be a result of water evaporation during extrusion. Furthermore, drying the samples openly in the fume hood 
resulted in deformation of the sheets for both samples due to the high water-contents and produced hard 
and smooth samples which made tensile testing difficult due to slipping and insufficient load. Tensile testing 
of injection moulded dog bone specimens was also attempted, but due to poor quality of the specimens, only 
Young’s modulus could be estimated from the dog bones. Tensile testing was possible to perform on the 
cylindrical extrudates from the lab-scale extrusion and resulted in significantly lower and more reasonable 
Young’s moduli and tensile strengths. 
DSC analysis were in many cases ambiguous and clear evaluation of plasticizer incorporation could therefore 
not be done based on Tg as expected. Furthermore, a transition at -56°C was observed for the majority of the 
produced extrudates, which could be a melting transition or a glass transition.  
Lastly, TGA was found to be a less ideal method to determine water and glycerol contents in the samples as 
the glycerol and water evaporation overlaps, making it difficult to determine the isolated glycerol content.  
 
 
==PAGE:83==

6 Future work 
Future work should include further optimisation of alginate-glycerol-water formulations and processing con-
ditions. As the prepared extrudates were relatively wet resulting in deformation during drying, a balance of 
the processability and lowering the water content should be further investigated. Furthermore, investigation 
of an ideal extrusion temperature should be done as promising extrudates were produced at both 90°C and 
100°C. Additional plasticizers could also be investigated as an alternative to or in combination with glycerol. 
In addition, the problems with tensile testing of the dried extrudates should also be attempted solved in 
order to evaluate the material properties to determine suitable applications for the material and thus which 
further development is need.  
Furthermore, the prepared alginate materials are still water soluble, which limits the possible applications. 
Immersing in or spraying with a dilute Ca2+ solution to crosslink the surface could be tested to increase water 
resistance.  
Lastly, despite the natural origin of alginate, using natural polymers are not necessarily an environmental 
quick fix as environmental burdens can be shifted to other areas during the full life cycle of the material. 
Therefore, evaluations of those shifts and hot spots of environmental impacts should ideally be included in 
the further developments of these new materials. This could be done by employing the life cycle assessment 
(LCA) method to evaluate not only the effect on climate change and resource depletion, but a broader selec-
tion of environmental impact areas in all stages of the life cycle.

by TGA. All water mentioned is demineralised water.

Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC) 
The DSC analysis were performed on a Discovery DSC from TA Instruments. The analysis was performed with 
20°C/min or 10°C/min and two overall cycles/runs. In the first run, the samples were heated from room tem-
perature to +180°C with subsequent cooling to -90°C. In the second DSC run, the sample was heated from -
90°C back to +180°C. The max. temperature +180°C for the DSC analysis was determined by TGA of the so-
dium alginate powder, freeze-dried AWG25 and AWS25, and was based on degradation of the samples ob-
served around 200°C. Unless otherwise stated a hole was punched in the lid for the DSC pan to allow volatiles 
to be removed from the sample. Analysis data can be found in appendix B.

The analysis was performed using a Thermo Scientific Nicolet iS50 attenuated total reflection Fourier trans-
formed infrared spectrometer (ATR-FT-IR) with a build-in diamond. The sample spectra were prepared using 
32 scans and a resolution of 4 cm-1. Analysis data can be found in appendix E.

Speed mixing of samples were performed on either a SpeedMixer German Engineering by Hauschild DAC

Freeze drying 
Freeze drying was performed on a Christ Alpha 1-2 LDplus freeze dryer with an ice condenser at -60°C. Freeze

7.7.4 Tensile testing 
The produced extrudates were stored in closed LDPE zip-lock bags in the fume hood for 2 days. Injection 
moulded specimens were stored in open bags to enable drying. The soft extrudates were removed from the 
bags and placed on a plastic sheet in the fume hood to evaporate the water. The samples started bending 
after drying 15 min, so the ends of the sheet pieces were taped to the surface to avoid further deformation. 
After drying 4.5 hours, the tape was observed not to hold the AW100 samples which had become very hard 
and bended. This was attempted prevented further by placing tape across the middle of all samples and by 
placing approx. 1 cm glass plates and additional weight on the AW100 samples to weigh them down. The 
samples were dried overnight and the AW100G25 samples had become hard but still relatively flexible. 
Dog bone specimens 
Tensile testing was attempted on an Instron 3345 with a Bluehill universal software and a load of 500 N and 
5 mm/min. Weight and dimensions of injection moulded dog bones described in Table 7.29 were measured 
and the specimens were observed to have many defects, especially very deformed surface and shape were 
present at the ends. Thus, the specimens were not ideal for testing. The area to be placed in the clamps were 
sandpapered and cut criss-cross in the surface to increase the grip of the clamps. The dislocation between 
the clamps were approx. 2.5 cm. The samples either broke in the clamp or the maximum load was reached 
(Table 7.30). Dog bone 5.3 broke in the test area but had a defect in this area prior to testing. Thus, tensile 
strength and elongation-at-break could not be obtained. However, the initial slope was measured for modu-
lus determination.

Rectangular specimens 
Dried sheets prepared from AW100 were too hard to cut manually and could possibly have been prepared 
with a laser cutter, but this was not attempted due to time constraints. Thus, only the extruded sheets 5 from 
AW100G25 were cut into rectangular specimens of approx. 1 × 3 cm. Six samples were prepared from a 
smooth sheet with no or very few bubbles visible, and six additional samples were prepared from a sheet 
with many visible bubbles. 
Approx. 5 mm was marked at each end of the specimen to be placed in the clamps resulting in dislocation 
between clamps of approx. 2 cm. Specimen 1 with and without bubbles were tested but resulted in the sam-
ples slipping from the clamps. Making the sample less smooth by use of sandpaper and cutting criss-cross in 
the surface or use of tape did not improve the slipping. Thus, the rectangular specimens could also not be 
tested.   
Additionally, the rectangular specimen 1 without bubbles were attempted tested with an TA Electro Force 
with a load of 50 lbf which is equivalent of approx. 222 N. The specimen was tightly fastened in the clamps 
which have spikes, so slipping was not observed. However, the sample could not be tested as the load maxi-
mum was reached. To be able to test the samples, a 500 kN machine would have been needed.
