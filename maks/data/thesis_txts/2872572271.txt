Abstract 
The complexity of fermentation processes poses a challenge for their understanding, development 
and industrial implementation. Important process parameters such as substrate and product con-
centrations are often only available with a considerable delay. A transition to real-time measurements 
is warranted. 1H nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy is a promising analytical method 
to obtain real-time insight into fermentation processes because of its non-destructive and quantita-
tive nature. Therefore, its applicability was investigated by monitoring Escherichia coli fermentation 
processes using both off-line and on-line measurement approaches. To realize the latter, a setup 
was developed to circulate bacterial suspension from the cultivation vessel to the NMR spectrometer 
in a continuous manner. The experiments were conducted with a low-field (80 MHz) benchtop NMR 
spectrometer and more conventional (400 and 500 MHz) NMR spectrometers to address the aspect 
of sensitivity. Results from off-line 1H NMR measurements were largely consistent with those ob-
tained from high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC). On-line 400 MHz 1H NMR spectros-
copy allowed the real-time monitoring of 15 molecules including substrate (glucose), product (tryp-
tophan) and byproducts (mainly organic acids) with a temporal resolution of 10 min. With the 80 MHz 
NMR spectrometer (on-line measurement), 5 molecules were detected during the fermentation pro-
cess. However, the 80 MHz NMR spectrometer, being considerably smaller, was found to be partic-
ularly suitable for on-line measurements, as it can be more easily positioned besides bioreactors. 
These results demonstrate that 1H NMR spectroscopy is a suitable on-line analytical method for 
fermentation processes.

1 Introduction 
Fermentation processes are inherently complex and dynamic because the microorganisms carrying 
out the bioconversion of substrates into products are themselves complex biological systems. Un-
derstanding and controlling the process is vital, independent from the application, and requires suit-
able analytical methods and sensors. In the case of fermentation processes, the following parame-
ters are commonly measured in real-time: temperature, pH, dissolved oxygen and off-gas composi-
tion (Simon et al., 2015). For these parameters, well-established methods or sensors exist that are 
commercially available. However, these parameters only scratch the surface of a fermentation pro-
cess, with important parameters not being covered, such as biomass, substrates, product, byprod-
ucts and viability of the culture. Analytical methods exist for these parameters but in most instances, 
they do not provide information in real-time and involve manual procedures. However, in the last few 
decades there has been a growing effort to overcome the challenges associated with real-time ana-
lytical methods for fermentation processes (Gerzon et al., 2022; Simon et al., 2015; Veloso & Fer-
reira, 2017). 
1.1 Process Analytical Technology and Measurement Modes 
The term process analytical technology (PAT) was coined by the Food and Drug Administration 
(FDA) in the 2000s as a framework promoting innovative pharmaceutical development, manufactur-
ing and quality assurance (FDA, 2004). Since then, PAT has become widely recognized and spurred 
development in the field of on-line fermentation monitoring (Gargalo et al., 2022; Gerzon et al., 2022; 
Veloso & Ferreira, 2017). The classification of measurement modes (i.e. how a measurement 
method is integrated into a process) was also formalized by the PAT framework. The following four 
measurement modes are generally recognized (Bakeev, 2010; Lundsberg-Nielsen et al., 2018): 
 
In-line: The measurement occurs inside the process equipment, such as a temperature probe 
inside of a stirred tank bioreactor.  
 
On-line: The sample is diverted from the process where it is measured, such as a bypass 
from a stirred tank bioreactor combined with a spectroscopy method that is placed on the 
bypass.  
 
At-line: The sample is removed from the process and measured in close proximity, such as 
an optical density (OD) measurement of a sampled bacterial suspension. 
 
Off-line: The sample is removed from the process and measured at a later point, such as 
high-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC) measurements of supernatant from a fer-
mentation that that has been sampled and stored until the end of the experiment.  
Another important term that is used in this context is real-time, which is used to describe an analytical 
method or sensor where the obtained measurement is available within seconds or minutes, instead 
of hours or days (Bakeev, 2010). There does not exist a defined limit until which a measurement is 
==PAGE:7==
7 
 
considered real-time. The term near real-time is also used in this context (FDA, 2004). In general, 
in-, on- and at-line analytical methods or sensors fall under the real-time and near real-time terms. 
1.2 On-Line Methods for Fermentation Processes 
Besides nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy, which is the focus of this study and is 
explained in detail in the following chapters, several promising on-line methods and sensors exist for 
the application in fermentation processes besides traditional sensors (e.g. pH, temperature and dis-
solved oxygen). Many of these methods are also based on spectroscopy. Examples are near-infra-
red, Raman, dielectric, Fourier transform infrared and fluorescence spectroscopy (Veloso & Ferreira, 
2017). Main benefits of spectroscopic methods are their non-destructive nature, fast measurement 
and lack of extensive sample preparation (Gargalo et al., 2022; Veloso & Ferreira, 2017). Biosensors 
are a different type of measurement but can also be used in an on-line or in-line mode. They are 
based on biological recognition elements that are specific for a molecule, such as a sugar, limiting 
the number of molecules that can be measured by a single biosensor (Veloso & Ferreira, 2017). Soft 
sensors are not an analytical method in the traditional sense but have nonetheless proven them-
selves useful. The underlying principle is to obtain process parameters that are not directly measur-
able by using commonly available signals such as pH and temperature, to estimate the desired pro-
cess parameters (Gargalo et al., 2022). Mass spectrometry is a method that is commonly used as 
an off-line method but can also be used as an on-line/at-line method for fermentation processes 
(Cortada-Garcia et al., 2022). 
1.3 Nuclear Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy 
Atoms are made up of electrons, protons and neutrons with the latter two forming the nucleus. 
Hence, protons and neutrons are known as nucleons. Each of these subatomic particles has a mag-
netic moment and spin, the latter being a quantum phenomenon. Both are inherent properties, as is 
mass, for example. The spin and magnetic moment are heavily intertwined, and they are always 
oriented in the same or opposite direction, depending on the gyromagnetic ratio γ. For nuclei with a 
positive γ (γ > 0) the spin and magnetic moment are oriented in the same direction, while they are 
oriented in the opposite direction for nuclei with a negative γ (γ < 0) (Levitt, 2008). The gyromagnetic 
ratio γ and its importance for nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy will be explained in a later 
section of this chapter.  
With the exception of 1H (protium), nuclei contain both protons and neutrons with each of the nucle-
ons having its own spin and magnetic moment. However, NMR spectroscopy operates on the level 
of nuclei, as the name implies. When looking at a nucleus as an entity, the combined nucleons define 
the spin and magnetic moment of the whole nucleus. This is described with the nuclear-spin quantum 
number Ⅰ. The value of Ⅰ for a nucleus is a multiple of ½ (0, ½, 1, 1½, …) and defines the number

For reasons of simplicity, the nuclear-spin quantum numbers Ⅰ mentioned in this study, are always 
the ground state nuclear spin Ⅰ of the isotope. The difference between the two terms is not of 
importance in this study and will thus not be explain in more detail; see (Keeler, 2010) for further

The following rules apply for the nuclear-spin quantum number Ⅰ of an isotope (Keeler, 2010): 
 
If both protons and neutrons are present in even numbers, then Ⅰ is 0.  
 
If both protons and neutrons are present in odd numbers, then Ⅰ is 1 or a higher integer.  
 
If the combined number of neutrons and protons is odd, then Ⅰ is ½ or a higher half integer. 
Mainly nuclei with an Ⅰ of ½, such as 1H (protium), 13C, 15N and 31P, are used for NMR of biomole-
cules (Klostermeier & Rudolph, 2017; Markley et al., 2017). In this study the focus lays on 1H NMR. 
Therefore, NMR will be described in this chapter only in the context of nuclei with an Ⅰ of ½. Before 
looking further into the NMR phenomenon of ½ nuclei, it is worth noting that nuclei with an Ⅰ of 0, 
such as 12C, 16O and 32S, are NMR silent. This is because the nucleons form pairs, proton with proton 
and neutron with neutron, resulting in the cancellation of their spins (Levitt, 2008). Based on Equation 
1, nuclei with an Ⅰ of ½ have two eigenfunctions. Each of the two eigenfunctions have an associated 
eigenvalue. The exact meaning of eigenfunctions and eigenvalues is outside of the scope of this 
study. Of importance is, that the two eigenvalues of a ½ spin nucleus are different in the presence 
of an external magnetic field, as is the case in NMR spectroscopy.  
When looking at the two eigenvalues with an energy level model in mind, they can be understood as 
two different energy levels (Figure 1). This phenomenon of two different energy levels appearing in 
the presence of a magnetic field is called the nuclear Zeeman splitting. In the absence of a magnetic 
field the eigenvalues/energy levels are degenerate (identical). The two eigenvalues, or energy levels, 
are also referred to as eigenstates or spin states, which can be further divided into spin up (α) and 
spin down (β) (Levitt, 2008).

Importantly, it has to be noted that a spin cannot only be in one of the two energy levels, which is often 
wrongly stated. In fact, a spin exists in a quantum superposition state. From an energy model perspec-
tive, the two energy levels can be understood as the upper and lower limit with infinite possible energy 
level between in which the spin and magnetic moment can also exist (Keeler, 2010; Levitt, 2008). 
Having briefly looked into the nuclear aspect, let us now proceed with the magnetic aspect of NMR. 
There are two magnetic elements at play during an NMR experiment. Firstly, the above-described 
magnetic moment of the nuclei. Visually speaking, this magnetic moment can be understood as a small 
magnetic bar. Secondly, the static magnetic field B0 which is generated by the NMR instrument (Levitt, 
2008). Both of them are required for NMR spectroscopy. If an isotope has no magnetic moment, like 
12C, or if no magnetic field is present, then NMR spectroscopy is not possible.  
To understand what happens during NMR spectroscopy one can think about it in terms of geometry. 
Even though, the geometric approach, also known as the vector model, cannot explain the NMR phe-
nomenon in its entirety, it comes much more natural to us than quantum mechanics (Keeler, 2010; 
Levitt, 2008).  
In the case of 1H NMR spectroscopy, the magnetic moments of all the 1H nuclei can be viewed as 
small vectors (Figure 2). To further simplify, they can be combined to one vector called bulk magneti-
zation or net magnetization. In the absence of an external magnetic field the vectors are orientated 
randomly in all three dimensions (Figure 2, a)). The resulting bulk magnetization will thus be close to 
zero with no distinct direction. If a magnetic field B0 is applied, as is the case in NMR spectroscopy, 
the magnetic moments will orientate them self slightly in the direction of the magnetic field B0 (Figure 
2, b)).  
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
 
Figure 2: NMR vector model: a) Nuclei (grey dots) and their magnetic moments (small arrows) in the ab-
sence of a magnetic field B0, no bulk magnetization is being built up. b) In the presence of a magnetic field 
B0, the magnetic moments orientate themselves slightly parallel to B0, resulting in a bulk magnetization.  
This alignment process is not instantaneous but occurs over a span of milliseconds to seconds and 
is referred to as T1 (Levitt, 2008). The alignment with the magnetic field B0 occurs due to the energy 
difference between the parallel (spin up, α) and antiparallel (spin down, β) orientation of the magnetic 
moment to the magnetic field B0 (Figure 1). As mentioned, the preference for the aligned orientation 
is very small. This is due to the thermal motion, which constantly disturbs the individual magnetic 
moments. Only in the bulk magnetization is the alignment observable (Keeler, 2010). By utilizing a 
stronger magnetic field, the energy difference and thus the bulk magnetization can be increased 
(Figure 1). 
The aligned bulk magnetization is almost undetectable against the strong magnetic field B0 (Levitt, 
2008). Therefore, during NMR spectroscopy, the bulk magnetization is rotated away from B0. This 
results in the bulk magnetization to precess around the magnetic field B0, which allows it to be meas-
ured by a detector in the orthogonal plane (Figure 3). As stated above, the generation of a bulk 
magnetization requires a strong magnetic field. It is thus not convenient to introduce another strong 
magnetic field to achieve the rotation of the bulk magnetization (Keeler, 2010).  
To overcome this issue, the last part of the NMR term comes into play: resonance. This phenomenon 
can be compared to a swing for children which swings back and forth with a specific frequency. 
When pushing the swing with the same frequency, the swinging motion will be stronger than if push-
ing with a deviating frequency. For the resonance in NMR, the bulk magnetization processes around 
the magnetic field B0 with a specific frequency, known as the Larmor frequency ω0. If a radio-fre-
quency pulse at the Larmor frequency is applied, the bulk magnetization rotates towards the anti-
parallel orientation of the magnetic field B0 (Figure 3, b)). After the radio-frequency pulse is stopped,

for example at 90°, the bulk magnetization will gradually realign again with the magnetic field B0. The 
resonance phenomenon is the reason why the much weaker radio-frequency pulse can rotate the 
bulk magnetization in the presents of stronger magnetic field B0 (Levitt, 2008).

The above mentioned Larmor frequency is the product of the magnetic field B0 and the gyromagnetic 
ratio γ, which is an isotope specific property (Equation 2). 1H has for example a gyromagnetic ratio 
γ of 2.68 · 108 rad · s−1 (Levitt, 2008). The spin and magnetic moment of a 1H nucleus will thus pre-
cess with a Larmor frequency of -400 MHz in the presence of a 9.39 T strong magnetic field B0. NMR 
spectrometers are commonly categorized based on the 1H Larmor frequency.

As described above, the bulk magnetization can be rotated away from the magnetic field B0 with a 
radio-frequency pulse, which then results in the precession of the bulk magnetization around the 
magnetic field B0. This precessing bulk magnetization can be detected with a wire coil that is placed 
closely around the sample in the perpendicular plane to the magnetic field B0. A current will be cre-
ated in the coil due to the precessing bulk magnetization. This current and the signal resulting from 
it are the strongest when the bulk magnetization has been rotated 90° away from the magnetic field 
B0. The signal obtained from this current is known as the free induction decay, and, as the name 
suggests, it decays over time. This time is referred to as T2. The free induction decay (FID) signal is 
then converted via Fourier transform into a spectrum that is easier to comprehend then the FID signal 
(Figure 4) (Keeler, 2010).

Based on the information provided so far about NMR spectroscopy, all 1H nuclei in a sample would 
precess with the same Larmor frequency, for example -400 MHz in the presence of a 9.39 T strong 
magnetic field B0. This would result in a single peak on a 1H NMR spectrum. Fortunately, this is not 
the case. On a microscopic scale, the local environment of a 1H nucleus varies and influences the 
magnetic field B0 that the specific nucleus experiences. This implies that 1H nuclei in different mole-
cules and positions within molecules precess with different Larmor frequencies, resulting in a 1H 
NMR spectrum with information about the composition of the sample and structural information about 
the molecules. The effect that the local environment has on the Larmor frequency of a nucleus is 
called the chemical shift and is proportional to the magnetic field B0. The proportionality of the chem-
ical shift makes it possible to compare spectra from NMR spectrometers with different magnetic 
fields (Levitt, 2008).  
1.4 NMR Spectroscopy for On-Line Fermentation Monitoring 
An analytical method that is to be used for real-time on-line fermentation monitoring, benefits greatly 
from the following two characteristics. Firstly, if a diverted sample stream should be returned to the 
process, then the method should be non-destructive and ideally non-altering. Secondly, the method 
should not require any preparation of the sample. In addition to the on-line specific characteristics, 
an analytical method should be robust, reproducible, quantitative and applicable to a wide range of 
conditions. NMR spectroscopy combines all of these characteristics, to varying degrees, making it a 
promising method for on-line applications in fermentation monitoring (Bharti & Roy, 2012; Bouillaud 
et al., 2019; Giraudeau, 2023; Markley et al., 2017). Furthermore, NMR spectroscopy provides struc-
tural information on the measured molecules (Keeler, 2010). Another characteristic that is particu-
larly relevant for measuring microbial suspensions is that NMR spectroscopy measures all the nuclei 
present in a sample (Bouillaud et al., 2019). In the case of quantifying glucose in a microbial 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
suspension with NMR, both intracellular and extracellular glucose are being measured. Most off-line 
methods, for example HPLC, can only measure the extracellular (supernatant) or intracellular glu-
cose with a quenching and extraction procedure. This is especially relevant for high cell density 
processes where the biomass can make up an substantial amount of the total volume (Smolke, 
2009). It is thus not surprising that NMR spectroscopy has been investigated as an on-line method 
for fermentation processes for many decades (Chen & Bailey, 1993; Kreyenschulte et al., 2015; 
Neves et al., 1999; Phuong et al., 2025).  
As described in the chapter above (1.3), NMR spectroscopy is possible with any isotope with a nu-
clear-spin quantum number above 0. The individual Isotopes differ in their NMR spectroscopy prop-
erties. Regarding their sensitivity, the gyromagnetic ratio and their natural abundance is of im-
portance. For example, 1H has an abundance close to 100% and a high gyromagnetic ratio, while 
13C has a low abundance and gyromagnetic ratio, making 13C NMR spectroscopy less sensitive 
(Levitt, 2008). However, 13C has a wider chemical shift range, making separation of peaks easier 
(Keeler, 2010). For on-line NMR spectroscopy in the field of fermentation, mainly 1H (Bouillaud et 
al., 2020; Kreyenschulte et al., 2015; Phuong et al., 2025), 13C (Neves et al., 1999) and 31P (Chen & 
Bailey, 1993; Noguchi et al., 2002) have been used. The scientific questions that have been investi-
gated with the isotopes of the different elements vary because of their NMR properties and their role 
in microorganisms. 1H has primarily been used to quantify substrates (e.g. glucose and glycerol), 
products (e.g. ethanol, lipids) and byproducts (e.g. organic acids) (Bouillaud et al., 2020; 
Kreyenschulte et al., 2015; Phuong et al., 2025). Furthermore, the alkyl protons (1H) have been 
correlated to dry cell weight to estimate the biomass during a fermentation process (Kreyenschulte 
et al., 2015). 31P on the other hand has been used for quantifying intracellular metabolites (e.g. 
nucleoside triphosphate and sugar-phosphate) and intracellular pH via inorganic phosphates (Chen 
& Bailey, 1993; Noguchi et al., 2002). 13C was used to investigate glucose metabolism under aerobic 
and anaerobic conditions by quantifying intracellular metabolites. Since 13C has a low natural abun-
dance, 13C labelled glucose was used in this study (Neves et al., 1999).  
The main impediment of NMR spectroscopy is its low sensitivity (Giraudeau, 2020). To illustrate this 
in the context for fermentation, a study utilizing off-line mass spectrometry detected 419 molecules 
(Link et al., 2015), while another study utilizing off-line 600 MHz 1H NMR spectroscopy detected 33 
molecules (Forsberg et al., 2024). Both studies were done with Escherichia coli (E. coli), with the 
former using a chemically defined medium and the latter a complex medium. It is noteworthy that 
the NMR-based study employed 1H NMR spectroscopy, which uses the 1H nucleus with its high 
gyromagnetic ratio, making it more sensitive than other nuclei. To overcome this limitation, increas-
ingly stronger magnets are being developed for NMR spectroscopy, although at the cost of increas-
ing prices and spatial footprints (Bouillaud et al., 2019; Moser et al., 2017).

1.5 Benchtop NMR Spectrometers 
The increasing prices and footprints made high-field NMR spectrometers less accessible. Further-
more, the super conducting magnets that are used for high-field NMR spectrometers require cryo-
gens (liquid nitrogen and liquid helium) to be operational. Hence, high-field NMR spectrometers are 
mainly found in laboratories specialized for these instruments (Singh & Blümich, 2016).  
In addition to high-field NMR spectrometers, more compact low-field NMR spectrometers are avail-
able which use permanent magnets. They are also known as benchtop NMR spectrometers because 
they are small enough to be placed on a regular laboratory workbench, which allows for more inte-
grated experimental use cases. For example, a benchtop NMR spectrometer can be placed besides 
a chemical reaction or a fermentation process, where it can be used as an on-line or at-line analytical 
method. The small footprint is made possible by the permanent magnets which do not require insu-
lation jackets filled with cryogens. But the permanent magnets produce a weaker magnetic field, 
reducing the resolution and sensitivity of NMR spectroscopy. Thus, benchtop NMR spectrometers 
used for measuring complex mixtures such as microbial suspensions are limited to the more abun-
dant molecules. Similar to high-field, benchtop NMR spectrometers have undergone developments 
to increase their magnetic field strength (Bouillaud et al., 2019).  
Despite their reduced resolution and sensitivity, benchtop NMR spectrometers have become a focal 
point for on-line applications in recent years because of their accessibility and size (Singh & Blümich, 
2016). This is also the case for their application on fermentation monitoring (Bouillaud et al., 2019) 
with the main focus being on-line 1H NMR spectroscopy (Bouillaud et al., 2020; Kreyenschulte et al., 
2015; Phuong et al., 2025).  
1.6 Aim of This Study 
The aim of this study is to investigate the potential of 1H NMR spectroscopy for real-time monitoring 
of fermentation processes. Both off-line and on-line measurement approaches are being explored. 
To enable the latter, particular emphasis was given to the implementation of a setup that allows 
continuous circulation of bacterial suspension from the cultivation vessel to the NMR spectrometer. 
Since accessibility and sensitivity are major challenges of NMR spectroscopy, a low-field (80 MHz) 
benchtop NMR spectrometer is tested in addition to more conventional (400 and 500 MHz) NMR 
spectrometers. The model microorganism E. coli is utilized in batch fermentation processes for this 
study.

2 Materials and Methods 
2.1 Escherichia coli Strain DDB35 
The E. coli strain DDB35, a derivative of the BW25113 strain, was used for all fermentation experi-
ments in this study, with the exception of the tryptophan fermentation experiment (Chapter 3.6) 
(Datsenko & Wanner, 2000). This strain produces no recombinant product and has a wild type (WT) 
phenotype. The E. coli strain DDB35 was obtained from Lei Yang, Scientific Lead at DTU Biosustain.  
2.2 Escherichia coli Strain HMP3071 
The E. coli strain HMP3071, which was used for the on-line tryptophan fermentation experiment 
(Chapter 3.6), was developed at DTU Biosustain to produce tryptophan. This strain is also BW25113 
derived and has the following genotype: folE(T198I), ynbB(V197A), ΔtnaA, ΔtrpR(P2–ckDDC), 
obgE(E350A), ΔyddG::PJ23101–sgAANAT(D63G), ΔpfolE::PJ23100, ΔldhA::PJ23101-sgAANAT 
(D63G), trpE(S40F), ΔfhuA (Datsenko & Wanner, 2000; Yang et al., 2022). The E. coli strain 
HMP3071 was obtained from Lei Yang, Scientific Lead at DTU Biosustain. 
2.3 Cryo Stocks of DDB35 and HMP3071 
The cryo stocks of both strains used in this study were started from cryo stocks of Lei Yang’s group. 
They were streaked onto LB agar plates and incubated over night at 37 °C. The DDB35 colonies had 
a light brown color and slight shine (Appendix 1). The agar plates were stored in the fridge until the 
shake flask cultivation was started. A single colony was picked with an inoculation loop and transferred 
into a shake flask containing 20 mL of DDB_SM_004 medium. The shake flask was then incubated at 
37 °C and 200 rpm (∅ = 5.08 cm) for about 24 h. Subsequently, 50 μL of the bacterial suspension was 
used to inoculate another shake flask containing 20 mL of DDB_SM_004 medium. This second shake 
flask was then incubated at the same conditions for another 24 h. A third shake flask containing 50 mL 
(in the case of DDB35) or 35 mL (in the case of HMP3071) of DDB_SM_004 medium was then inoc-
ulated from the second shake flask. The incubation conditions were kept the same as before. During 
the cultivation, the OD was measured every few hours to determine the exponential phase (Appen-

2.5 Light Microscopy 
For the microscope images an Axiolab 5 microscope with an Axiocam 208 color camera (Zeiss, 
Germany) were used. The sample (E. coli suspension) was diluted with 9 g/L NaCl saline to reach a 
concentration where most bacterial cells on the focal plane are separated from each other without 
overlapping. 50 μL of the sample was pipetted onto a glass microscope slide and covered with a 
cover slip to hold the sample. An objective lens with a 100-fold magnification in combination with 
immersion oil was used for taking the images.  
2.6 Optical Density Measurement and Dry Cell Weight Calculation 
From manual samples, which were taken during all fermentation experiments, the OD was measured 
via spectroscopy. A wavelength of 600 nm was used to measure the OD of the sampled bacterial 
suspensions. The Jenway spectrophotometer 7205 (Cole-Parmer, USA) was used for all experi-
ments besides the on-line 400 MHz 1H NMR experiment with E. coli DDB35 (Chapter 3.5) and 
HMP3071 (Chapter 3.6). During these two experiments the MicroSpek™ DSM-Cuvette Cell Density 
Meter (LAXCO, Inc, USA) was used. Due to the inconsistency of OD measurements between spec-
trometers, the OD values obtained from the MicroSpek™ were converted with a conversion factor of 
2.114 to values equivalent to the Jenway spectrophotometer. The conversion factor was determined 
by measuring the same sample with both spectrophotometers. The OD was measured only below 
0.8. In the case of higher OD, the sample was diluted with 9 g/L NaCl solution and remeasured.  
Dry cell weight (DCW) was not directly measured during this experiment but calculated from the OD 
measurements, which were done from the manual samples taken about every 2 hours. A DCW/OD 
conversion factor of 0.34 was used for this conversion. This factor has been established in the Inte-
grated Fermentation Platform group at DTU Biosustain for E. coli.  
2.7 Growth Rate Calculation 
The growth rates were either calculated from the OD or the integral carbon dioxide transfer rate 
(CTR) values. When calculating the growth rate for a whole phase, for example the exponential 
phase of the shake flask cultivation (Appendix 5), all available OD values within this phase were 
used for fitting a natural exponential function to those values. In instances where the growth rate 
was calculated for individual time points, for example during the on-line 400 MHz 1H NMR experiment 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
with E. coli DDB35 (Figure 16), the OD value at that time point in addition to one value before and 
after were used. For each of these 3 OD values the natural logarithm was calculated and subse-
quently a linear regression was done. The first derivative of the resulting linear function was calcu-
lated, represents the growth rate. The identical calculation was done for the growth rate based on 
the integral CTR values.  
2.8 Shake Flask Fermentation with the TOM Device 
The E. coli strain DDB35 was used for the shake flasks fermentation experiment. A preculture of 
25 mL with DDB_SM_004 was inoculated from the prepared cryo stocks. A 250 mL shake flask was 
used as the cultivation vessel and the preculture was incubated at 37 °C, 200 rpm (∅ = 5.08 cm) 
over night. The batch fermentation was done inside of a shake incubator equipped with a trans-
fer-rate online measurement (TOM) device (Kuhner shaker, Switzerland) for oxygen transfer rate 
(OTR) and CTR measurement during the experiment. Two 500 mL shake flasks for biological dupli-
cates were used that were equipped with FlexCaps (Kuhner shaker, Switzerland). Besides the two 
tubes connecting the flasks to the TOM device, a sampling port was incorporated (Appendix 4). The 
sampling port included a metal needle with a length longer than the height of the flask and a silicon 
tube ending at a Luer Lock connection. The flasks were autoclaved with the FlexCap and sampling 
port. 100 mL of DDB_BM_006 was pipetted into both flasks and inoculated from the preculture to 
reach a starting OD of 0.1. Subsequently, the two flasks were incubated at 37 °C, 225 rpm (∅ = 5 cm) 
and 70% humidity for a duration of 12 h. During the fermentation manual samples were taken with a 
syringe for OD, HPLC and off-line 1H NMR spectroscopy. 
2.9 High-Performance Liquid Chromatography 
From the manually sampled bacterial suspension, 1 mL was pipetted into a 2 mL Eppendorf tube 
and subsequently centrifuged at 15000 g for 5 min. The supernatant was separated from the cell 
pellet via decanting and frozen at -20 °C. After the fermentation experiment was concluded, the su-
pernatant samples were thawed, and per sample, 3 times 50 μL (technical triplicates) were diluted 
with 200 μL of 0.005 M sulfuric acid (1:5 dilution). Subsequently, the samples were filtered with a 
0.45 μm hydrophilic filter plate (96 well). The HPLC analysis for the quantification of glucose, ethanol 
and organic acids was done with a Rezex ROA-Organic Acid H+ (8%) column (Phenomenex, USA). 
As mobile phase 0.005 M sulfuric acid was used with an isocratic elution mode. The column was 
kept at 60 °C and the volumetric flow at 0.7 mL/min. The HPLC was equipped with an ultraviolet light 
and refractive index detector. The latter was used for quantification. For each quantified component 
a 6-point calibration curve was done in the same run as the samples.

2.10 Off-Line 1H NMR Spectroscopy of Supernatant 
Off-line 1H NMR spectroscopy was done from the same frozen supernatant samples as the HPLC 
measurements (Chapter 2.9). For each sample, 600 μL of the supernatant was sterile filtered 
(0.22 μm) into a 2 mL Eppendorf tube. 500 μL of the filtrated supernatant was mixed with 100 μL of 
NMR buffer in another 2 mL Eppendorf tube. The buffer contained D2O for frequency locking, a 
phosphate buffer and two internal standards (sodium 3-(trimethylsilyl)-1-propanesulfonate (DSS) for 
chemical shift referencing and maleic acid (MA) for quantification). 1660 mg of NaH2PO4 was dis-
solved in 20 mL of 60% D2O and 40% H2O (12 mL D2O and 8 mL H2O). The pH was adjusted to 7.4 
with 10 M NaOH. Subsequently, 2 mg of DSS and 4.8 mg of MA were dissolved in the phosphate 
buffer. The 600 μL of filtrated supernatant and NMR buffer was then pipetted into 5 mm NMR tubes. 
2.10.1 Off-Line 500 MHz 1H NMR Spectroscopy 
The NMR measurements were performed at 298 K on a 500 MHz Avance Neo spectrometer (Bruker 
Biospins, Germany) operating at a 1H Larmor frequency of 499.83 MHz and equipped with a 5 mm 
DCH CryoProbe. The spectrometer was equilibrated to 298 K and the 1D NOESY experiment was 
acquired (pulse program, noesygppr1d). Each FID was sampled for 24 min by acquiring 256 scans 
using an acquisition time of 1.7 s and an inter scan delay of 4.0 s.  
2.10.2 Off-Line 80 MHz 1H NMR Spectroscopy 
The NMR measurements were performed at 298 K on an 80 MHz SpinSolve benchtop spectrometer 
(Magritek, New Zealand) operating at a 1H frequency of 80.41 MHz and equipped with a 5 mm room 
temperature probe. The spectrometer was equilibrated to 298 K and the 1D WET experiment was 
acquired (pulse program, WET). Each FID was sampled for 17 min by acquiring 256 scans using an 
acquisition time of 3.2 s and an inter scan delay of 0.8 s. 
2.11 Cultivation Setup for On-Line 1H NMR Spectroscopy 
The 500 mL shake flask and FlexCap (Kuhner shaker, Switzerland), which have been used for the 
shake flask fermentation with the TOM device, were used for the cultivation vessel in this setup. 
Inside of the shake flask a magnetic stirrer bar was placed that was driven by the magnetic stirrer 
RCT basic (IKA, Germany). The flask was placed into a 2 L glass beaker and metallic thermal beads 
were filled around the flask for improved heat transfer (Appendix 11). The cultivation temperature 
was controlled by a temperature sensor which was connected to the magnetic stirrer.  
All liquid lines going into the flask consisted of a metal needle connected to either silicon or polytet-
rafluoroethylene (PTFE) tubing. The off-gas and aeration lines (silicon) were equipped with a 
0.22 μm sterile filter. A simple aeration pump (AM-TOP, CR10) was connected to the latter. For 
inoculation an additional line (silicon) was included, which was closed off with a Luer Lock connec-
tion, that could be used in combination with a syringe. The needle of line 1 (PTFE), going from the

overview). Line 4 (silicon), going from the bubble trap to the flask, and line 3 (PTFE), going from the

openings of which two were used for the aeration and off-gas lines. Lines 3, 4 and the inoculation 
line were located in one opening, while line 1 was located in the fourth opening. The latter two open-

Between the flask and the NMR tube, a bubble trap was incorporated into the loop. A glass vial

The capillary was completely filled with the buffer and sealed with capillary sealer (Critoseal MS5000 
E4010).

parameters themself were kept unchanged.  
2.13.1 On-Line 400 MHz 1H NMR Spectroscopy

acquiring 128 scans using acquisition time of 2.0 s and an inter scan delay of 2.0 s. A delay (d30)

keeping the same acquisition parameters.

2.15 1H NMR Data Analysis 
1H NMR spectra were analyzed using MestReNova (Mestrelab, Spain) software. Phase and baseline 
correction was performed manual. For the spectra from the off-line measurements, the DSS peak 
was used for chemical shift referencing, while MA was used for the on-line measurements. The 
peaks of the detected molecules were manually integrated and normalized to the MA signal of the 
corresponding measurement. Afterwards, the values of the individual molecules were normalized to 
the time series of that molecule.

The batch cultivation was started with an initial glucose concentration of 22.51 ± 0.58 g/L (HPLC 
measurement of the batch medium).

3.3 Development of a Cultivation Setup for On-Line 1H NMR 
NMR spectrometers are commonly built to analyze samples in an off-line manner. Individual liquid 
samples in NMR tubes can either be placed manually into the NMR or with an autosampler. To take 
advantage of the non-destructive nature of NMR spectroscopy, a setup for on-line measurement of 
bacterial suspension (fermentation broth) was developed (Figure 13). For this, a loop going from the 
cultivation vessel (stainless steel stirred tank bioreactor or shake flask) to the NMR was envisioned. 
The setup was designed with an aerated stirred tank bioreactor in mind. To prevent gas bubbles 
from entering the NMR tube, which can disturb the magnetic field, a bubble trap was included in the 
setup.  
 
Figure 13: Schematic overview of the final setup for on-line 1H NMR fermentation monitoring: A 
shake/cultivation flask with a magnetic stirrer connected via multiple lines to a bubble trap and an NMR. Line 
(1) transports the bacterial suspension into the bubble trap, from which line (2) transports it into the NMR 
tube. To maintain the liquid level in the NMR tube, line (3) transports the bacterial suspension back into the 
shake/cultivation flask. Line (4) maintains the liquid level in the bubble trap. Lines 1, 2 and 4 are operated 
with a single peristaltic pump that has 4 pump heads. Line 3 has is operated by a separate peristaltic pump. 
An enveloping tube safeguards the NMR spectrometer from liquid overflow. The volumetric flow rates of the 
peristaltic pumps represent only the liquid flow and excludes any air drawn into the tubing.  
For the proof of concept, a shake flask was used as the cultivation vessel. The circulation of the 
bacterial suspension from the cultivation vessel to the NMR was designed as a loop and includes a 
bubble trap (Appendix 9). In this bubble trap, drawn in gas bubbles will rise and escape the bacterial 
suspension. In the bubble trap the needle of line 2 is placed at the farthest point of separation from 
the line 1 needle to prevent any rising gas bubbles passing through the bubble trap into line 2. The 
tube of line 2 was designed to reach the bottom of the 5 mm NMR tube, while the tube of line 3 was 
placed above to set the volume within the NMR tube (Appendix 10). In addition, a capillary with the 
==PAGE:35==
35 
 
internal standard maleic acid was placed into the NMR tube. To maintain the liquid level in both the 
bubble trap and the NMR tube, lines 3 and 4 were operated with higher pump speed, resulting in air 
being pumped into those two lines. To maintain the atmospheric pressure, air inlets equipped with 
0.22 μm sterile filters were added in the cultivation vessel, bubble trap and NMR tube. To ensure 
gas exchange of the cultivation/shake flask head space, ambient air was pumped into the flask 
through another 0.22 μm sterile filter. The enveloping tube, in which line 2 and 3 were enclosed 
started from the NMR tube and reached the outside of the NMR. This safety feature was included in 
the setup to prevent any damage to the NMR spectrometer from liquid overflow. Inside of the NMR 
tube a capillary for the NMR standard was placed. To maintain the cultivation temperature the shake 
flask was placed into a glass beaker filled with thermal beads and the beaker was placed onto a 
laboratory stirrer with integrated heating (Figure 14). The residence time for the whole loop, exclud-
ing line 4, is 134 s and the residence time in the NMR tube is 21 s.

3.7 Comparison of On-Line and Off-Line 1H NMR Spectra Quality  
During on-line 1H NMR spectroscopy the required flow from the bioreactor to the NMR and the tubing 
of the loop affected the homogeneity of the magnetic field. To assess the loss of resolution due to 
the on-line mode of measurement an off-line sample (i.e. without flow and tubing) was measured 
directly after the tryptophan experiment.  
The resolution of the last measured on-line 1H NMR spectrum was lower than the resolution of the 
off-line spectrum (Figure 20). The peaks of the on-line spectrum were generally broader and also 
had a wider base. This can best be seen at the acetic acid peak. Nonetheless, the strong peaks 
such as glucose, citric acid, pyruvic acid, acetic acid were well identifiable and separatable (Figure 
20). Pyruvic acid is not visible in the on-line spectrum (Figure 20, top) because at this time point it 
had already decreased again. Formic acid, which is outside of the selected section of the spectrum 
(Figure 20), had also a strong peak. Weaker peaks, such as tryptophan, alanine, ethanol and propi-
onic acid were still well identifiable; and the integrals showed coherent trends from the on-line 1H 
==PAGE:41==
41 
 
NMR measurements (Figure 19). The weak peaks of fumaric acid, succinic acid, valine, isoleucine, 
leucine and unidentified molecule (0.13 ppm), made their identification challenging and their integrals 
less coherent. This was especially true for this experiment. The integrals of the weak peaks of the 
previous experiment (Chapter 3.5) were more coherent.  
The bacteria were still active in the sample that was taken at the end of the fermentation, which was 
then used for acquiring the off-line spectrum (Figure 20, bottom). Furthermore, there was still glucose 
left for the bacteria to metabolize. The difference in composition of the two spectra, especially the 
pyruvic acid and ethanol peaks, most likely originates from the active bacteria.

4 Discussion 
The goal of this study was to implement and evaluate 1H NMR spectroscopy for real-time monitor-
ing of fermentation processes. Since microorganisms are at the center of every fermentation pro-
cess, a basic assessment of the growth physiology of the E. coli strain DDB35 is important. During 
the shake flask experiment with off-line 1H NMR and HPLC (Chapter 3.1) the biological duplicates 
had comparable growth rates of about 0.5 h-1 during the exponential phase. Similar growth rates 
were reproduced during the on-line 1H NMR fermentation experiments with E. coli DDB35 (Chapter 
3.5 and 3.8), even though the mixing method had changed. During the first experiment the flasks 
were shaken in an orbital motion inside of an incubator, while in the latter two experiments a mag-
netic stirrer bar was used. The same Erlenmeyer flask type, as well as cultivation temperature, 
volume, medium and cryo stocks were used for all three experiments. The growth rates reported 
in this study are comparable to the reported growth rate of 0.65 h-1 for E. coli BW25113, which is 
the parental strain of DDB35 (Perrenoud & Sauer, 2005). Another study looking into E. coli strains 
commonly used for industrial applications reported growth rate between 0.61 h-1 and 0.97 h-1 
(Monk et al., 2016). Both studied cultivated E. coli under aerobic conditions, 37 °C and used M9 
medium, which is similar to the media used in this study. During the on-line 1H NMR fermentation 
experiments with E. coli HMP3071 (tryptophan producing strain), a lower growth rate between 0.3 
and 0.4 h-1 was measured during the exponential phase (Chapter 3.6). This might be due to the 
metabolic burden of producing tryptophan. Besides the growth rate, during all three on-line 1H 
NMR fermentation experiments with E. coli DDB35 and HMP3071 (Chapter 3.5, 3.6 and 3.8), sim-
ilar final OD values between 12.26 and 12.90 were reached. 
In addition to the OD values and derived growth rate, CTR and OTR were obtained via the TOM 
device during the shake flask experiment (Chapter 3.1). Both of these measured parameters 
started to stagnate after about 6 h of fermentation. This indicates that the cultures had reached an 
oxygen limitation, i.e. the cultures required more oxygen than the cultivation system could provide. 
This is further supported by the off-line 1H NMR and HPLC data which shows increased concen-
trations of organic acids and ethanol after 6 h. While production of acetic acid in E. coli also occurs 
under sufficient oxygen availability via overflow metabolism (Bernal et al., 2016), ethanol, succinic 
acid and formic acid are related to anaerobic conditions (Gonzalez et al., 2017). Especially, formic 
acid indicates oxygen limitation (Potter et al., 2000; Sawers et al., 2004). The RQ calculated from 
CTR and OTR and the growth rates calculated from integral CTR, deviated from expected values. 
The growth rates from the integral CTR values are throughout the fermentation considerably higher 
than the growth rates from the OD values. Based on group internal experience and literature, the 
growth rates from the two methods should be more similar (Wechselberger et al., 2013). Further-
more, the RQ stayed below 1 during the whole cultivation, except for the value at 2.5 h, even 
==PAGE:45==
45 
 
though the theoretical RQ for glucose to CO2 and H2O and for glucose to acetic acid is 1. It has to 
be noted that manual samples were taken during the process, likely interfering with the OTR and 
CTR measurements. The glucose uptake rate of 8.35 ± 0.04 mmol ∙ gDCW-1 ∙ h-1 during the oxygen 
rich phase is in line with values reported in literature for aerobic conditions which range from 6.65 
till 12.47 mmol ∙ gDCW-1 ∙ h-1, depending on the strain (Boecker et al., 2021; Monk et al., 2016). 
Since the glucose uptake rate depends on the growth rate, it is important to compare this param-
eter at similar growth rates. A study looking at the glucose uptake rate at different growth rates by 
cultivating a WT E. coli strain in a chemostat, reported a glucose uptake rate of about 
7.5 mmol ∙ gDCW-1 ∙ h-1 at a growth rate of 0.5 h-1  (Varma & Palsson, 1994). This glucose uptake 
rate of 7.5 mmol ∙ gDCW-1 ∙ h-1 is a bit lower than what has been measured in the shake flask exper-
iment of this study (8.35 ± 0.04 mmol ∙ gDCW-1 ∙ h-1) at the same growth rate. Regarding the yield of 
biomass (DCW) from glucose, the shake flask experiment during the oxygen rich phase resulted 
in a yield of 0.342 ± 0.005 g/g. When assuming a DCW composition of C1, H1.8, O0.5, N0.2 the 
c-mol/c-mol yield is 0.41, which is slightly lower than the yields of 0.5-0.6 reported in literature 
(Monk et al., 2016). 
The off-line 1H NMR spectroscopy results from the shake flask experiment with the TOM device 
show a comparable level of precision, similarly standard deviation, as the HPLC results (Figure 9). 
Only the lactic acid quantification resulted in broad standard deviations in the chase of both ana-
lytical methods. This might be explained by the low concentrations of lactic acid. Regarding the 
comparability of off-line 1H NMR and off-line HPLC results from the same supernatant samples, 
the normalized NMR results show a high consistency with the HPLC concentrations. This is in line 
with reported results from glycerol quantification with 1H NMR and HPLC during cultivation of yeast 
(Hansenula polymorpha) (Kreyenschulte et al., 2015). Only the formic acid results differ between 
the methods with the HPLC showing higher concentrations as the 1H NMR. An observed ad-
vantage of the off-line 1H NMR spectroscopy during this experiment is the additional 9 molecules 
(total of 16) that were detected (Figure 10 and Figure 11). In a study that also utilized off-line 1H 
NMR for monitoring E. coli fermentation a total of 33 metabolites were identified, with the additional 
metabolites being primarily amino acids (Forsberg et al., 2024).  
NMR spectrometers are available with magnets of different strength. Generally, NMR spectrome-
ters with stronger magnets are more expensive and larger but have a higher sensitivity and reso-
lution (Moser et al., 2017). The difference of resolution can be seen by eye when comparing a 
500 MHz and an 80 MHz 1H NMR spectrum (Figure 12). The lower resolution makes the separation 
of peaks with similar chemical shifts more challenging. This especially affects 1H NMR due to the 
narrow Larmor frequency range of 1H nuclei (Giraudeau, 2020). On the other hand, 1H nuclei have 
the highest gyromagnetic ratio, making 1H NMR spectroscopy the most sensitive (Levitt, 2008). 
==PAGE:46==
46 
 
Nonetheless, NMR spectrometers with weaker magnets will have lower sensitivity and thus in-
creased detection limits. In recent years, there have been promising approaches to overcome the 
lower resolution of low-field benchtop NMR spectrometers with sophisticated data processing ap-
proaches (Giraudeau, 2023).  
So far off-line 1H NMR has been the focus. However, NMR is well-suited for on-line measurement 
with its non-destructive and intrinsic quantitative nature. To implement this mode of measurement, 
a technical solution is required to transport the sample, in this case the bacterial suspension, to 
the NMR spectrometer in a continuous manner. For this, a setup has been developed in this study 
(Chapter 3.3). A number of key details of the developed setup are noteworthy. Firstly, sterility is 
crucial for fermentations and therefore the loop/bypass should be easily sterilizable and should be 
designed in such a way that the risk of contamination is reduced. The developed setup can be 
autoclaved with the cultivation vessel or separately. If a capillary with an internal standard is re-
quired for the experiment, it needs to be sterilized separately and added in a sterile manner. In the 
scientific literature of on-line NMR spectroscopy for fermentation, the aspect of sterilization is rarely 
covered (Bouillaud et al., 2020; Chen & Bailey, 1993; Kreyenschulte et al., 2015; Neves et al., 
1999; Noguchi et al., 2002; Phuong et al., 2025). Only Kreyenschulte and colleagues reported on 
the sterilization aspect, flushing 70% ethanol through the bypass before cultivation. Secondly, dur-
ing aerobic fermentations air is supplied into the cultivation vessel, commonly via a sparger. In 
addition, mixing of the suspension results in the formation of small air bubbles. If such air bubbles 
reach the NMR spectrometer they can reduce the homogeneity of the magnetic field, thus reducing 
the quality of the acquired spectra (Baumann et al., 1997; Keifer, 2007). For this reason, a bubble 
trap has been integrated into the developed setup (Figure 13). Bubble traps have also been utilized 
by other groups in their setups (Kreyenschulte et al., 2015; Phuong et al., 2025), while other groups 
went the opposite way by sparging air into the NMR tube (Chen & Bailey, 1993; Kramer & Bailey, 
1991). This was done to ensure aerobic conditions within the NMR tube at the cost of reduced 
resolution. Thirdly, either a flow cell (Bouillaud et al., 2020; Kreyenschulte et al., 2015; Phuong et 
al., 2025) or a NMR tube, equipped with tubes to the cultivation vessel, (Chen & Bailey, 1993; 
Neves et al., 1999; Noguchi et al., 2002) can be used for on-line NMR. The former is mainly used 
for low-field benchtop NMRs, while the latter is used for conventional high-field NMRs. In the case 
of this study, where the goal was to utilize both types of NMRs, a setup was developed which 
allowed the usage of different NMRs (80 MHz and 400 MHz spectrometer). For this, a 5 mm NMR 
tube was used that was compatible with both NMRs (NMR probes) in combination with tubing 
connecting it to the cultivation vessel. Lastly, NMR spectrometers are expensive instruments, and 
when using a setup with an NMR tube, there is a risk of bacterial suspension overflowing into the 
NMR probe. Due to the thin diameter of the NMR tube used for making the setup compatible to 
==PAGE:47==
47 
 
different NMRs, it was unfeasible to add an additional safeguard tube as has been done in other 
setups (Chen & Bailey, 1993; Neves et al., 1999). Instead, a novel approach was taken by using 
an enveloping silicon tube reaching from the NMR tube to the outside of the NMR (Figure 13). This 
solution has the advantage that even in the case of a pump failure or blockage, the overflowing 
bacterial suspension will be contained inside of the enveloping tube, thereby reducing the risk of 
damaging the NMR instrument. To retain the compatibility aspect of the setup, the enveloping tube 
was implemented to suit the dimensions of both NMRs. 
The first fermentation experiment with the developed on-line 1H NMR setup, in combination with 
the 400 MHz spectrometer (Chapter 3.5), demonstrated that an identical number of molecules can 
be identified, similar to the off-line experiment with the 500 MHz NMR spectrometer. In contrast to 
the off-line measurements which relied on samples taken manually, the on-line mode allowed the 
acquisition of 1H NMR spectra throughout the experiment. NMR spectroscopy allows for measure-
ments to be taken at nearly any desired time interval. In this study 128 scans, each lasting 4 s, 
were done for one measurement/spectrum. This resulted in a temporal resolution of 10 min. How-
ever, it would also be possible to reduce the number of scans and thus the temporal resolution. 
For example, in two studies utilizing on-line 1H NMR for fermentation monitoring, temporal resolu-
tions of 3 min and 1 h were used (Bouillaud et al., 2020; Kreyenschulte et al., 2015). Even though 
a temporal resolution of 10 min might be on the limit of being considered real-time, near real-time 
might be better fitting, the individual scans are acquired in real-time (few seconds) and can be 
surveyed on the go. For example, during the above-mentioned experiment (Chapter 3.5), it was 
possible to gain an overview of the metabolites already in the first minute of a measurement (cou-
ple of scans), allowing real-time monitoring of the fermentation. The reason behind the accumula-
tion of multiple scans into one measurement/spectrum is the improvement of the signal-to-noise 
ratio (Keeler, 2010).  
The higher temporal resolution of 10 min (first 12 h) during the on-line 1H NMR experiment (Figure 
17) then the 2 h during the off-line experiment (Figure 10), allowed for a more comprehensive 
overview of the fermentation process. Furthermore, the data was already available, in a less pol-
ished form, during the fermentation experiment. With the off-line mode, the data was only available 
a few days later. In the fermentation experiments of this study, the obtained information from the 
on-line 1H NMR about the fermentation was not used to alter the experiment. However, the possi-
ble high temporal resolution and real-time nature of on-line 1H NMR, opens up promising possibil-
ities to not only monitor but control fermentation processes. In line with that, Bouillaud and colleges 
suggested that this technology could be used for real-time optimization of fermentation processes 
(Bouillaud et al., 2020).  
==PAGE:48==
48 
 
An additional advantage of on-line over off-line NMR spectroscopy was that no sample preparation 
was required. Besides the time saving from the omitted sample preparation, it also did not require 
any consumables. When comparing the on-line NMR spectroscopy to off-line HPLC during the shake 
flask experiment (Chapter 3.1), this is even more eminent since no solvents were required and there-
fore less chemical waste was produced. However, it has to be noted that high-field NMR spectrom-
eters with super conducting magnets require liquid helium and nitrogen (Keeler, 2010). This is not 
the case for low-field benchtop NMR spectrometers with permanent magnets (Bouillaud et al., 2019).  
The fermentation experiment with the tryptophan producing E. coli strain HMP3071 (Chapter 3.6) 
demonstrated that on-line 1H NMR spectroscopy not only allows the monitoring of fermentations in 
regards of substrate uptake and byproducts such as organic acids but also biotechnological prod-
ucts, in this case tryptophan (Figure 19). Being able to monitor the product formation in real-time 
should prove itself valuable for bioprocess development and optimization. For a product to be de-
tectable by 1H NMR spectroscopy, it needs to have at least one peak that does not overlap with any 
other components of the bacterial suspension, such as byproducts or medium components.  
As mentioned above, regarding air bubbles, the homogeneity of the magnetic field affects the reso-
lution of the spectra. A similar negative effect may arise from the turbulent flow, tubing and capillary 
inside of the NMR tube. This is indicated by the lower resolution observed when comparing an on-line 
with an off-line 1H spectra done with the same 400 MHz NMR spectrometer (Figure 20). Nonethe-
less, the resolution was still high enough with the on-line mode to identify 15 molecules in the 
HMP3071 and 16 in the DDB35 fermentation experiment, compared to the 16 during the off-line 
fermentation experiment.  
When comparing the normalized quantities of the detected molecules during the off-line and on-line 
1H NMR fermentation experiments (Figure 10, Figure 17 and Figure 19), the following observations 
can be made: the molecules with high concentrations and thus strong signals, for example glucose 
and acetic acid, show less variability between measurements than molecules with presumable low 
concentrations and weak signals, such as fumaric acid. This variability was only observed from the 
on-line experiments. This might be because of the higher temporal resolution during those experi-
ments and/or the reduced resolution. In regard to the main trends, the three experiments show sim-
ilar results. Glucose is consumed and due to the overflow metabolism acetic acid is produced (Bernal 
et al., 2016). Succinic, pyruvic, formic, lactic acid and alanine are mainly produced after about 10 h 
(Figure 10 Figure 17) or 14 h (Figure 19). This indicates a change from an aerobic to a partially 
anaerobic metabolism (Gonzalez et al., 2017; Potter et al., 2000; Sawers et al., 2004). The subse-
quent decrease might come from the transition into a stationary phase with heavily reduced growth 
and metabolism. This might have reduced the oxygen uptake from the culture below the OTR of the 
==PAGE:49==
49 
 
system. Supporting this line of thought is the OTR data from the off-line 1H NMR fermentation ex-
periment (Figure 5), where the OTR decreases after reaching a stationary phase. The increase in 
oxygen availability at this point might allow the bacteria to fully oxidize the organic acids. To further 
investigate this phenomenon, a dissolved oxygen probe in the on-line setup would be of great value.  
The on-line 1H NMR fermentation experiment with the 80 MHz SpinSolve spectrometer resulted in 
acquired spectra with the lowest resolution during this study (Figure 22). This is both due to the lower 
magnetic field of this NMR spectrometer and the effects of the on-line setup (flow, tubing capillary). 
In contrast to the 16 detected molecules detected with the 400 MHz NMR, only 5 molecules were 
detected. It is plausible that some of the organic acids that likely reached their maximum earlier in 
the fermentation, as it was the case during the other experiments (Figure 10, Figure 17 and Figure 
19), would be detectable with the 80 MHz. Nonetheless, it allows monitoring of the main two mole-
cules glucose and acetic acid. It is important to keep in mind that the 80 MHz SpinSolve spectrometer 
has reduced cost, a smaller footprint and does not require cryogens (Bouillaud et al., 2019). This is 
reflected by multiple fermentation studies with low-field benchtop 1H NMR spectrometers (Bouillaud 
et al., 2020; Kreyenschulte et al., 2015; Phuong et al., 2025), while, to the best of my knowledge, no 
high-field on-line 1H NMR fermentation experiment has been reported in previous studies.  
To conclude, on-line 1H NMR spectroscopy is a promising method for fermentation monitoring, ben-
efiting from its inherent quantitative and non-destructive nature. In the case of low-field benchtop 
NMR spectrometers with permanent magnets, these advantages are further complemented by more 
affordable pricing, maintenance and smaller size at the cost of reduced resolution and sensitivity. 
With sensitivity and resolution being the main limitations of NMR spectroscopy, they also represent 
its greatest potential for future advancements, whether through more sophisticated methods or 
stronger magnets (Giraudeau, 2020; Silva Elipe et al., 2024).

5 Outlook 
The cultivation vessel used for the developed setup for on-line 1H NMR spectroscopy is a simple 
shake flask without any integrated sensors or controllers such as pH and dissolved oxygen, which 
are normally present in laboratory-scale bioreactors. Simplicity was an advantage for this study be-
cause it allowed easier implementation, transportation and more flexibility. The next logical step 
would be to utilize this technology with a controlled bioreactor. This would not only improve the fer-
mentation side of the experiment but also the 1H NMR spectroscopy. For example, during the fer-
mentation experiments in this study no pH control was in place resulting in a changing pH during the 
fermentation experiment. This resulted in challenges for monitoring certain molecules during the 
experiments with on-line 1H NMR because chemical shifts can be pH sensitive (Appendix 14). An-
other important benefit of a more sophisticated bioreactor is that it would allow for a more complex 
and better controlled fermentation experiment such as a fed-batch or chemostat. These operation 
modes would also allow for high cell density experiments, which are common in industrial processes.  
Such an improved setup in combination with a chemostat would allow for an interesting process 
development approach. If for example a biotechnology product can be identified and quantified with 
on-line 1H NMR, then a chemostat experiment could be run where the production rate would be 
available in real-time. One cultivation parameter such as growth rate, temperature or pH could be 
varied and the obtained data utilized without delays, caused by off-line measurements, to optimize 
the production rate within the same experiment. In essence, such an approach would allow for 
real-time bioprocess optimization.
