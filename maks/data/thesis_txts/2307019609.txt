Abstract
Mulitlevel Flow Modelling (MFM) is a functional modelling language used to model industrial
plants with respect to material and energy ows and the purpose of these ows in the overall
system. The functional decomposition provided by a MFM model allows reasoning about causes
and consequences of specic failures throughout an automated plant. However, automated systems
do not always run in a single conguration, but may be operated in dierent modes. Each mode has
a dierent overarching purpose and relies on a specic conguration of the system. Awareness of the
current mode is essential to draw the right conclusions about the state of the system. While MFM
has been proven to work well as a diagnostics and operations support tool, the notion of operational
modes is not included directly in MFM. Previous research suggested dening distinct models for
each mode.This approach implies high modelling eort and diculties in relating corresponding
functions of dierent modes to each other. This project proposes a concept to consistently represent
operational modes in MFM and interpret the information included in MFM models to identify
plausible modes and required mode transitions.
The start-up procedure of a simplied power plant is analysed with regards to the objectives and
conguration of distinct modes by established MFM approaches. Following previous approaches,
the intention of the modes is reected by the MFM control concepts. The constraints introduced
by properly working control can be interpreted as boundaries of an operational mode. By including
conguration changes as control actuations each operational mode can be represented by a set of
control functions and control relations. This set distinguishes the mode from a reference model.
The proposed solution is implemented in a new reasoning system and compared to the pre-
viously established approaches. Applying the developed concepts to the start-up procedure of a
power plant yields one common model that can be derived from a structural representation. Pro-
cedural information required to describe the operational modes is included in the mode description
after the common model has been developed. The separate consideration of dierent information
levels eases the modelling process and further results in directly related models for the modes.
While the fundamental principles of the proposed concepts have been implemented and evalu-
ated, further work is suggested to facilitate real-time reasoning about the current mode and select
the correct mode based on the constraints identied in this work.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Multilevel Flow Modelling (MFM) is a Functional Modelling (FM) language that has been demon-
strated as a valuable tool for representing operational knowledge about processing plants in several
technological areas. MFM has originally been developed to represent complex systems for Human
Machine Interface (HMI) problems related to supervisory control. However, with technological ad-
vances in computer systems, MFM has been developed further to allow diagnostics and operation
support based on the MFM representation. These models enable cause and consequence reasoning
about failures in a system, as well as automated failure analysis. Among others MFM is used
in operator support scenarios in research projects such as the OECD Halden Reactor Project [1],
but also nds application in commercial solutions like Eldor's AlarmTracker1 as a diagnostics and
decision support tool for industrial plants. [2], [3]
1.1
Motivation and Goals
Modern industrial systems are growing increasingly complex and usually do not operate in just
one conguration. These congurations are often related to operation procedures such as start-up,
production and shut-down as well as emergency procedures. To make sensible decisions about the
system it is paramount to base any reasoning on the correct current status of the system. These
dierent possible states of a system are referred to as operational modes. [4]
While operational modes have long been a subject of research in relation to MFM, e.g. by
Lind [5] and Lind et al. [6], there is not yet a consistent way of modelling modes in MFM. Especially
Lind et al. [6] cover the modelling of modes of a nuclear power plant and show that the function
and objectives of distinct modes can be reected suciently by MFM. However, the models derived
for the start-up procedure do not include the operational knowledge on how the modes are related
to each other or when one model is no longer a valid representation of the current status of the
plant. Furthermore, specic functions are not consistent throughout the models, even though they
are linked to the same physical components.
The goal of this project is to extend the existing MFM modelling language and reasoning
system to consistently reect operational modes. The extension shall provide means to determine
when to change between modes and to identify corresponding system components and states. The
start-up procedure of a simplied power plant serves as case study to analyse the requirements for
these new concepts.
The cause-consequence reasoning based on MFM has been implemented using a discontinued
Java-based rule engine. To overcome this hurdle and tentatively nd a long term solution, the
reasoning system is to be ported to work with another rule engine.

Chapter 2
State of the Art
This chapter describes the theoretic foundation and research related to the present work. The
rst part focuses on the concept and applications of FM in general and relates those concepts to
MFM. In the second part the syntax and patterns of MFM will be outlined. Finally, the concept of
Operational Modes will be claried and related research in the context of MFM will be presented.
2.1
Functional Modelling
FM is a way of describing a complex system or product by considering the function of the system
as a whole or as a combination of the functions of system components.
Most FM research is
connected to engineering design and cross-disciplinary communication with a focus on mechanical
engineering and product design. Erden et al. [7] review a number of known FM frameworks and
their application. They identify function as the intermediary between possible object behaviours
and the needs or objectives a system is meant to full. Dierent approaches to FM either regard the
structure of a system as the dening element for the resulting behaviour, or disregard structure
completely by focusing solely on the function as a purpose.
Most FM approaches are closely
connected to the specic domain they have been initially developed for and are limited by the
paradigms of the specic problem domain. Based on the history of FM as a tool for communication,
the majority of approaches dene some schematic representation of the derived model, however,
only few of them have actually been implemented as a design verication tool. Erden et al. [7] reveal
two fundamental benets of applying FM to engineering problems. Firstly, FM allows a general
overview of the entire system in tangible terms of function on dierent abstraction levels, linking
interconnected knowledge domains. Secondly, FM facilitates computer reasoning by combining
dierent knowledge domains into a common model. This model can be used as the basis for model
based reasoning and thus become widely applicable in the industry as support tool for design,
diagnosis and maintenance. [7]
Even though MFM has not been widely recognised in the context of FM, it shares the common
concepts of abstraction decomposition and functional representation. However, MFM originated
as a language for model-based work analysis to assist human operators in identifying and handling
unknown operation situations [8] and HMI design [2]. Therefore, MFM has been applied more
in the elds of process and automation engineering than in the context of mechanical engineering
design. Like most FM approaches MFM regards the purpose, rather than the physical behaviour
of a system as the dening element.
MFM represents function by means-end and whole-part
decomposing the system as a hierarchy of dierent functional levels (means-end), with regards
to intended actions. Function is syntactically modelled by the relations of fundamental functions
contributing as a part of a subsystem or functional aspect. Each subsystem or aspect is put into
the overall systems context by describing the purpose (end) of its function (means) in the system.
Using only a few fundamental functions as the basic building block allows qualitative reasoning
about success or failure of these actions. MFM uses a graphic modelling language to facilitate the
communication through functional models. [9]

2.2
Multilevel Flow Modelling
As previously described, MFM decomposes a system as a hierarchy of means and ends. Each level
of this hierarchy can be described by objectives being realized by functions which can be linked to
physical components. Thus, a MFM model consists of interconnected structures of syntactically
connected functions that are the means to realize objectives. While the objectives at the highest
level represent the purpose of the overall system, objectives linked to more decomposed levels reect
functions that are necessary for the proper functioning of other connected parts of the system. At
the core of the functional description are ow functions, representing the ow of material or energy.
Figure 2.1 shows the fundamental functions and symbols used in MFM. [2]
The following sections will introduce an example of MFM modelling and will give an overview
of the modelling process. Furthermoremore, the underlying denitions of the ow and control
functions used in MFM are described and related to the established inference rules for MFM.

2.2.1
Modelling example and process
The example shown in Figure 2.2 was introduced by Lind [10]. The MFM diagram represents the
overarching objective (cob2) of maintaining the energy level on the warm side. While the energy
ow structure efs2 shows the overall function of the system from the most prevalent (energetic)
perspective the model is decomposed to show more detail by including the mass ow of coolant
(mfs1) as the means for the desired energy transport. Decomposing the system further, efs1
represents the energy needed for the pump as means to produce a part of the mass ow. Besides
the energy and material ows MFM also models operational constraints introduced by control
systems such as a water ow controller represented by cfs1 and a temperature controller cfs2.
Heussen and Lind [3] analysed the process of developing a MFM model. They identied that a
model is usually generated in an incremental process, gradually specifying more detail of the process
by including more in depth knowledge about the technology domain. The domain knowledge can
be gathered from dierent forms of technical documentation, such as Piping and Instrumentation
Diagram (P&ID)s and procedure handbooks. They identied four formal stages to the modelling
process [3]:
1. Process Analysis identies mass and energy ows and connects the functions of the system,
compiling them in relevant ow-structures.

2.2.2
Function concepts
As MFM composes the function as means for a specic purpose by describing the mass or energy
ow, the respective ow is the dening element for the underlying function primitives. Table 2.1
shows the denition of the ow functions and the resulting constraints on their nominal operation,
together with the dened abnormal states if the constraints are violated.
The two ow-constrained functions transport and barrier play the most important role, as they
connect pairs of the other function types, thus directly reecting the physical ow. Sink and source
functions denote the boundary of the system as an end or a starting point of a ow, respectively.
Finally, storage and balance can both be collection or splitting points for multiple ow paths inside
the system. Following these denitions, valid MFM syntax always contains a transport or a barrier
linking two ow functions of the other four types. [1]

The MFM control relations enable and disable resemble the two complementary states that
have been described for conguration valves as open or closed. Considering the behaviour of a
valve as something that can functionally provide a connection between two reservoirs it can be
resembled as a transport. Arguably, a valve may also be considered as something that provides
separation between reservoirs and should hence be reected as a barrier. However, for the sake
of uniformity, the former representation is chosen. Accordingly, an enabled transport function
represents an open valve, while a disabled transport function is equivalent to a closed valve. A
disabled transport should hence be interpreted to be functionally the same as a barrier.
A closed conguration valve does not permit any ow and is thus suciently represented by
the disable relation. However, when opened, a conguration valve may be represented by the same
ow function as a subsequent control valve. It is hence possible that the ow function associated
with a conguration change is controlled with an actuate, rather than an enable relation. Since
the actuate relation has not yet been explored in terms of MFM based reasoning [1], the enable
and actuate relations are both interpreted as indicating the nominal behaviour of a transport. The
model should indicated where control actuation is dened in the context of the application by
using the actuate relation.

Chapter 5
Implementation
MFM-based diagnostic reasoning had previously been implemented by Lind and other contributors
based on the Java-based JESS rule engine. However, the JESS project has not been maintained
in the past years and only a small user community is available for support. A far more popular
alternative for a rule engine is the open-source Java-based project JBoss Drools, which is supported
and still developed by Red Hat1.
Drools oers a wider range of functionality, such as truth
maintenance and rule execution control. Zhang [1] describes that it is necessary to reason about
temporal information to facilitate online reasoning. The Fusion package, which is part of Drools,
is specically designed for timely events and provides the necessary basis for such reasoning.
Given these advantages, the core of the MFM reasoning system has been implemented in Drools
as part of this project. In the following, the underlying information structure of the previous
implementation is presented and related to the respective parts of the new framework. The rule
base for the Drools inference engine is outlined and the representation of the new concepts is
described.
5.1
Information structures
To implement the reasoning system both JESS and Drools require a structured representation of
specic concepts of the model as facts. The JESS implementation relied very little on Java classes
and implemented these representations as fact templates. However, the models have to be read
from a le, which is a sequential and straightforward task in Java but more dicult to implement
in the rule system.
Furthermore, it is possible to equip Java classes with some inherent logic and, for example,
restrict properties to predened values. Consequently, the fact structures to represent the model
and reasoning elements are implemented as Java classes for the Drools framework.
The analysis of the JESS implementation revealed a rather complex hierarchy of fact templates,
an extract of which is shown in Figure 5.1. The complete analysis of the JESS templates is shown
in Appendix C The rst implementation in Drools focuses only on the set of rules described by
Zhang [1]. Therefore, only a reduced set of the relevant MFM concepts has been implemented, as
shown in Figure 5.2.
For the new implementation, specic MFM functions have been dened as types of a more
general representation of syntactically similar functions, such as barriers and transports or the
inuence and participate relations. The types are dened as integer values, which are identical to
the types used in the MFMSuite model les. The specialized classes have constructors limiting the
permitted types of properties. Another signicant dierence is that no state property has been
included in the concept classes, since the model itself does not represent a state. The reasoning
information is superimposed on the model instead, rather than included by modifying instances of
the model concepts.

The structures used to represent inferred information are widely adopted from the JESS imple-

are then further divided into postdictions and predictions for the cause and consequence reasoning
respectively. In contrast to the MFM modelling concepts postdictions and predictions rarely occur
in the same rule. Having separate classes, eases the creation of rule patterns exclusively related to
one or the other.
The JESS implementation uses a JESS script function to avoid contradicting assumptions in the

functionality has been included in the MFMAssumption class. Based on the justifying assumptions

Chapter 7
Discussion
This chapter discusses the results of applying the proposed concepts. First, the Drools imple-
mentation is evaluated. Second, the models generated using these concepts are compared to the
conventionally developed mode models. Furthermore, the two approaches are compared with re-
gards to the design process and conformity with the established MFM interpretation. Finally, the
limitations of this work and perspectives for future work are discussed.
7.1
Implementation
During this project the MFM-based cause-consequence reasoning system has been newly imple-
mented in the Java-based Drools framework. To verify that the implementation is fundamentally
correct, cause and consequence reasoning have been run in both frameworks. The established ex-
ample of a simple heat transfer system has been used for this verication. As shown in Chapter 6,
the resulting cause and consequence trees provide identical information.
The fundamental inference rules are implemented using Drools decision tables, which proved to
be an ecient way of handling the recurring structure of most of the rules. However, the extended
functionality incorporated in the JESS framework, such as truth maintenance and interfaces for
real-time reasoning about an actual processing plant, has not been implemented completely. Given
the theoretical scope of this work, the extended functionality was not required.
Drools oers a wide range of functions that are commonly used in rule-based systems, e.g.
truth maintenance and rule-ow control. Aspects of truth maintenance have only been supercially
explored since no live interface for the reasoning system has been established. Drools oers the
possibility of using the built-in truth maintenance rather than having to develop a complete set
of rules to ensure consistent reasoning results in a dynamic scenario, e.g. real-time diagnostics.
The rule-ow mechanism, on the other hand, has an important role in the implementation of
the proposed modelling approach. Besides ensuring the correct execution order of the respective
rule sets, this facility can be used for more complex, branched execution ows. Considering an
operating plant that goes through a sequence of modes, the rule-ow can be used to execute mode
changes when necessary and ensure that the reasoning system is using the correct model.

which can be provided by a P&ID. The procedural knowledge necessary for each mode is imposed

Hence, the new approach allows the separation of the dierent aspects of domain knowledge to an

The MFM model for each mode is an interpretation of the common model, that represents the

same structural components across modes, such as the transport and barrier functions fullled by

throughout all modes without additional manual eort during the modelling.
As described in Chapter 2, control functions in MFM are used to model intent and operational
knowledge. However, the interpretation of these in the MFM reasoning system has not yet been
explored.
The proposed solution follows the rational of understanding control functions as a
representation of intended intervention. By changing the model, additional constraints can be
explicitly included in the reasoning. While changing the model based on the interpretation of parts
of the model itself might seem unintuitive, this approach allows to rely on the established underlying
reasoning about MFM ow functions and their behaviour.
Interpreting control functions and
relations to generate the model used for reasoning does not interfere with other implementations,
since control functions and relations have previously not been considered in the context of MFM-
based reasoning.
7.4
Limitations
The concepts introduced in this thesis facilitate the modelling of dierent conguration modes
in MFM and provide the foundation for identifying nominal end points of a mode within MFM
reasoning. As pointed out in Chapter 4, the constraints that dene the end points of a mode are
quantitative values specic to a physical system. Given the theoretic nature of the analysed power
plant, a meaningful quantitative scenario could not be established.
The proposed and implemented concepts for mode change indicators, however, can serve as a
foundation to further investigate operational modes and represent a mode ow as a state diagram
similar to the sketch in Figure 4.1(b). The conditions for a mode change can therein be dened
with varying granularity, e.g. by including breached constrains of multiple controlled functions.
The results of modelling the start-up sequence of the power plant show that subsequent modes
only dier by small conguration changes. Relying on a mode ow that links subsequent modes,
the modelling eort could be further improved by representing only the changes with regards to
the previous mode.
In the models described in this thesis, the means-end relations have only been considered as
connectors. By the denitions outlined in Chapter 2, there is distinct meaning in the means-end
relations and only certain combinations with control functions are valid. However, the distinct
meaning of each relation has not been established in terms of inference rules. Further research
should specify the interpretation of means-end relation in the reasoning context, by considering
their meaning beyond the preprocessing introduced in this project.
This interpretation could
provide means of identifying failure of the control in the cause-consequence reasoning.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The goal of this project was to extent the MFM modelling language and reasoning system to
consistently reect operational modes and determine when mode transitions are required. This
project further aimed at implementing a new version of the reasoning system.
Based on the analysis of distinct MFM models for the start-up modes of a simplied power
plant, common indicators for required mode transitions have been identied and the representation
of corresponding elements has been compared. Operational intention has been included as the
purpose of control actions represented by MFM control functions and relations.
A concept for interpreting controlled storages in MFM has been proposed to include nominal
behaviour in the MFM-based reasoning and thus identify necessary mode transitions. Exploring
the existing control relations in MFM, the disable relation has been suggested as a way of indicating
parts of the system that are disconnected in a specic conguration. Combining these two concepts,
a mode can be represented as a set of control functions and connected control relations that are
changed with respect to a reference model.
The concept has been evaluated using a new implementation of the MFM reasoning system,
which is meant as a starting point for using the more comprehensive Drools reasoning engine
instead of the now deprecated JESS engine. The fundamental reasoning system of the new imple-
mentation has been shown to yield the same results as the previous implementation. Using the
newly developed concepts, valid models for each mode can be generated by the reasoning system.
The Drools implementation has proven to be rather straightforward with regards to the im-
plementation of rules and the decision tables are a valuable tool for dening similar rules in bulk.
Further work is required concerning truth maintenance and completely porting the existing JESS
implementation to provide compatible interfaces with other systems.
The present work provides a starting point for further research regarding operational modes in
MFM. The contributions to the research eld of MFM include, among others:
• Analysis of the JESS implementation of the reasoning system;
• New implementation of the fundamental reasoning system in Drools;
• Conceptual denition of mode shift indicators linked to storages;
• Modelling approach for consistently representing operational congurations;
• Rule base to generate MFM models based on the newly introduced approach and interpre-
tation concept.
Based on the ndings and implementations produced during this project open questions in
the general MFM denitions can be identied. Suggestions for future work based on this thesis
include:
• Application of the proposed concepts to an actual plant, including shut-down and emergency
procedures;

• Facilitation of real-time reasoning by incorporating function attributes and timely events,
which can partially be ported from the existing JESS implementation;
• Extension of the reasoning to enable mode detection and automatic model transitions, pos-

• Denition of failure states of control functions, tentatively including the meaning of means-
end relations and specifying objective-control relations with dierent interpretations.
