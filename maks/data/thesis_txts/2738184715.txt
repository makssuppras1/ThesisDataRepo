Abstract
This thesis aims to investigate the capabilities of the Segment Anything Model (SAM) and
augment them for Video Object Segmentation (VOS). The main objective is to incorporate
and test various techniques such as advanced point tracking and object tracking.
The project begins with an overview of relevant approaches and implementations in the
current state of VOS. It then details the methodology and rationale behind the conducted
experiments, followed by a presentation of these experiments and their results.
Initially, benchmark tests based on ground truth masks provided a reference point. Subse­
quently, in the context of semi­supervised learning, various modifications were explored.
These modifications included: 1) passing previous frames through the convolutional layer,
2) developing custom embeddings of previous frames instead of utilizing prompt embed­
dings, 3) implementing point tracking, and 4) combining point tracking with object tracking
techniques.
Despite the modest results of the experiments, the study shows promise for future de­
velopment. Future work could explore improvements in selecting starting tracked points,
frequently reinitializing the points and the bounding boxes. Additionally, these enhance­
ments could be combined with the employment of the convolutional layer for processing
previously predicted masks.

Introduction
Video Object Segmentation (VOS) is a Computer Vision (CV) classification problem that
can be described as the task of separating foreground regions from backgrounds in video
sequences. VOS has numerous applications (e.g., robotics [1], video editing [2], au­
tonomous driving, surveillance, etc.) as it establishes correspondence of identical objects
across frames, but achieves more detailed object representation than object and pattern
tracking algorithms (pixel­level masks rather than bounding boxes). The multi­faced prob­
lem in VOS comes from the fact that objects can change in size, shape, and appearance
over time which makes it difficult to maintain a consistent segmentation. Significant break­
throughs have been made possible through the use of benchmark datasets which have
led to advancements in VOS research. These are large­scale annotated video sequences
that are important for training and evaluating VOS models.
Researchers in their effort to tackle the various VOS challenges, are deploying various
advanced methodologies. Memory­based approaches are prevalent, utilizing temporal
information of the objects across different frames to handle appearance changes and
occlusions effectively. This strategy is evident in the deployment of Space­Time Mem­
ory networks (STM) [3], which utilize past video frames as a reference to aid current
frame segmentation, highlighting the importance of memory mechanisms in maintaining
temporal consistency across video segments. Furthermore, the integration of attention
mechanisms ­such as transformers­ has become more widespread [4, 5]. These mod­
els prioritize the most relevant areas of an image by using self­attention to assess the
significance of different pixels within the global context of the video [6]. Another modern
approach is the employment of point trackers. Point tracking refers to the task of man­
aging the dynamic nature of video objects, where key points or features of an object are
tracked across frames. These methods, while innovative, are not devoid of limitations.
Memory­based techniques, for instance, are prone to accumulating errors over time. In
point tracking, inaccuracies, due to rapid appearance changes or fast­moving objects,
etc., can propagate errors throughout the video sequence.
Given these developments, this thesis aims to explore and combine these state­of­the­art
methodologies to address existing gaps in VOS. More specifically, in this thesis, we are
tackling the VOS problem via the Segment Anything Model (SAM) [7], which represents a
novel approach in CV, particularly inspired by advances in Natural Language Processing
(NLP) [8] and foundational models that excel in zero­shot and few­shot learning through
prompting techniques. SAM introduces the concept of promptable segmentation, which
requires the model to generate a valid segmentation mask based on diverse prompts.
While SAM excels in segmenting objects from static images, the focus is to extend its
capabilities to consecutive frames [9, 10]. We conduct a series of fine­tuning experiments
using the DAVIS 2017 dataset, achieving the highest result of 61.17% by combining SAM
with a point tracker and an object tracker, however, the result is modest compared to re­
cent implementations. Hopefully, the insights gained from this research could significantly
contribute to the field, providing a foundation for future innovations in video analysis and
segmentation.
This thesis is divided into four main sections. The first section, related work, provides a
general overview of the most relevant and modern implementations. The second section
explains the methods used and details how the models are implemented. The third section

Literature Review/Related Work
In this chapter, we present and review the most relevant related work without delving too
deeply into technical details. We begin with an overview of VOS and highlight several con­
temporary, widely used models, followed by an exploration of point tracking techniques.
2.1
Video Object Segmentation
VOS seeks to achieve high­quality segmentation of a target object instance throughout
an input video sequence. This procedure entails precisely outlining the borders of the
requested item in each frame of the video, ensuring that the segmentation mask continu­
ally tracks the object as it travels and potentially changes its appearance over time. The
goal is to preserve precision in the object’s contours despite obstacles like occlusions,
illumination and viewpoint shifts, and the existence of other dynamic objects in the scene.
High­quality VOS allows for accurate tracking and isolation of the object, supporting addi­
tional analysis and applications that demand precise and dependable object information
throughout the video stream.
The most common strategy in recent and effective implementations introduces some form
of memory to enhance the segmentation model’s capability. This memory is used to store
previously processed frames and/or previously generated segmentations, which are then
utilized in future iterations. This approach allows the model to reference past information,
improving its accuracy and consistency in segmenting the target object across the video
sequence [3].
We are focused on presenting some of the most significant networks in this category rather
than covering many of them.
2.1.1
STCN: Space­Time Correspondence Networks
Space­Time Correspondence Networks (STCNs) [11] represent an advancement over the
existing STMs [3]. Therefore, we begin by presenting an overview of STMs.
STMs are architectures designed to retain information from previously processed frames.
These frames, along with their annotations (or segmentation probability maps, as depicted
in Figure 2.1), undergo encoding via a memory encoder and are stored in a memory bank.
The memory encoder generates Key embeddings and Value embeddings: Keys encode
visual semantics robust to appearance variations, while Values store detailed information
for mask estimation, such as object boundaries and target object characteristics.
The current frame, containing only the RGB channels, undergoes encoding via a Query
encoder, which produces similar output but with slightly different Value embeddings. Here,
Values capture detailed appearance information crucial for accurate mask decoding. In
contrast, Values from the memory encoder also include foreground/background classifi­
cation information.
Within the Space­Time Memory Read module, similarity is computed between Key feature
maps of memory frames and those of the query frame. The Value feature maps of the
memory frames with the highest similarity scores are retrieved and passed to the query
decoder, along with the Value feature map of the query frame. Finally, the query decoder
generates the segmentation mask.
STCNs have a similar structure but include a significant difference that enhances the
model’s performance. They employ separate encoders for the Key and Value feature

2.1.2
Segment Anything
SAM is an advanced CV model developed to perform high­quality segmentation tasks on
images. Created by researchers at Meta AI, SAM is designed to enhance the accessibility
and efficiency of image segmentation, irrespective of the complexity or variability of ob­
jects within the image. Image segmentation, a fundamental task in CV, involves dividing
an image into segments that represent meaningful parts or objects.
The innovation of SAM lies in its remarkable zero­shot generalization capability, allowing
it to provide precise segmentations across diverse image datasets without extensive task­
specific training. This ability enables SAM to perform accurate segmentation on previously
unseen images, significantly enhancing its utility across various applications and datasets,
and reducing the need for extensive, time­consuming model retraining and adaptation.
SAM employs a unique architecture that integrates a powerful backbone with a novel seg­
mentation head, enabling the model to handle a wide range of segmentation scenarios.
The backbone, typically based on a deep Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) or a trans­
former model, extracts rich feature representations from the input image. The segmen­
tation head processes these features to produce detailed and accurate segmentations.
This architecture allows SAM to excel in various applications, from medical imaging to
autonomous driving, where precise segmentation is critical.
A notable feature of SAM is its ability to segment objects based on diverse inputs, includ­
ing point clicks, bounding boxes, and free­form text prompts. This versatility enhances
its adaptability to different user needs and interaction modes. For example, a user can
click on an object within an image, and SAM can accurately segment the clicked object.
Alternatively, providing a bounding box around an object or a textual description can also
guide the model to perform the desired segmentation. This flexibility significantly improves
the usability of SAM, allowing it to be seamlessly integrated into various workflows with
minimal adjustments.
The performance of SAM is underpinned by extensive training on large­scale, diverse
datasets. This robust training regimen enables the model to generalize effectively across
different domains and image types, making it a reliable tool for segmentation tasks in
real­world applications. Furthermore, the open­source availability of SAM encourages its
adoption and adaptation by the broader research and development community. By pro­
viding the model and its codebase, Meta AI fosters collaboration and innovation, enabling
developers and researchers to build upon SAM and tailor it to their specific requirements
[7].
As illustrated in Figure 2.3, SAM comprises three fundamental components. The first and
principal component is the image encoder. This encoder is based on a minimally adapted
Vision Transformer (ViT) [12], pretrained using a Masked Autoencoder. The image en­
coder processes the input image to generate image embeddings. The second component
is the prompt encoder, which transforms user inputs—such as points, bounding boxes,
or text, as previously described—into embeddings. The third component is the image
decoder, which employs a modified Transformer decoder block utilizing both prompt self­
attention and cross­attention mechanisms. This decoder receives embeddings from both
the image and prompt encoders and outputs multiple segmentation masks along with their

2.2
Point Tracking
Point tracking is a fundamental technique in CV that involves identifying and following
specific points of interest across consecutive frames in a video sequence [14]. These
points, often referred to as feature points, can correspond to various elements in the
scene, such as corners, edges, or other distinctive regions that can be either in the fore­
ground or background. The primary goal of point tracking is to maintain a consistent and
accurate trajectory of these points throughout the sequence, enabling a variety of ap­
plications, including motion analysis, object tracking, video stabilization, and augmented
reality, among others.
Figure 2.4: Example of point tracking. The initial points are propagated to the later frames

point trackers that is relevant to this project.

2.2.1
Co­Tracker
The co­tracker [18] is an advanced mechanism designed to enhance the tracking of ob­
jects across sequential frames in video processing applications. Unlike traditional single­
frame object detection methods, the co­tracker operates by maintaining a consistent un­
derstanding of object positions and movements over time. This is achieved through the
simultaneous processing of multiple frames, ensuring that the temporal coherence of
tracked objects is preserved.
One of the key features of the co­tracker is its ability to align reference frames with query
frames. This alignment process is essential for maintaining object continuity, especially
in dynamic environments where objects may undergo significant changes in appearance,
position, or scale. By utilizing reference frames, the co­tracker provides the network with
historical context, allowing it to make more informed predictions about the current state
of objects in the query frames.
The co­tracker architecture typically involves a combination of CNNs, Recurrent Neural
Networks (RNNs) [19], and transformer­based models. The CNNs are responsible for
extracting spatial features from the frames, while the RNNs or transformers handle the
temporal dependencies, effectively capturing the motion and evolution of objects over
time. This hybrid approach enables the co­tracker to leverage both spatial and temporal
information, leading to more accurate and reliable object tracking.
Additionally, the co­tracker incorporates a feedback loop mechanism, where the predic­
tions from the current frame are used to refine and update the tracking model for sub­
sequent frames. This iterative process allows the co­tracker to adapt to changes in the
environment and improve its tracking performance continuously. The feedback loop also
helps in mitigating the impact of occlusions and other challenges commonly encountered
in video tracking tasks.

2.2.2
Segment Anything Meets Point Tracking
This paper [20] combines the strengths of SAM with point tracking techniques to advance
the field of video segmentation. The implementation begins with the selection of query
points, which include foreground points (positive points) and background points (negative
points). These query points can be selected by the user or, in semi­supervised VOS,
derived from the mask of the first frame. The point tracker is utilized to propagate these
points to subsequent frames of the video. The propagated points are then used to prompt
SAM, obtaining the segmentation masks. In each iteration, the predicted segmentation
mask from the previous frame is also provided to SAM, except for the first frame, where
no previous predictions exist. Additionally, optional reinitialization of the tracked points
is performed. Specifically, after a certain number of predictions, the query points are
reinitialized using the latest predicted masks. All previous points are discarded, and the
new query points are propagated by the point tracker to the subsequent frames.
Figure 2.6: SAM­PT: Segment Anything Meets Point Tracking. Points are initialized based
on the first frame of the video and propagated to subsequent frames by the point tracker.
Consequently, these points serve as prompts for SAM.

Methodology
This chapter provides a comprehensive examination of the essential components and
techniques employed in this research. It introduces the models and implementations de­
veloped, detailing their design, structure, and theoretical foundations. The approaches
adopted are analyzed, with emphasis on the rationale behind their selection and the steps
involved in their application. This thorough overview aims to offer a clear and detailed
understanding of the methodologies and strategies underpinning this research, thereby
setting the stage for the subsequent analysis.
All the developed and experimental methods aim to fine­tune SAM. Numerous training
programs and codes are executed, but these details are discussed in the next chapter.
This chapter focuses on how we use the data, models, and tools in general. Moreover,
all the methods fall under the semi­supervised learning category, which means that for
every video, we have access to the RGB frames and the ground truth mask of only the
first frame, while the masks of the following frames are hidden.
Firstly, in our initial two implementations, we attempt to introduce a memory component
to our systems. Specifically, in Chapter 3.1, we utilize the CNN of SAM to input the first
frame as a reference frame fr, while the query frame fq, or fi, is processed as usual
through the image encoder. This method involves a simple modification to the default ar­
chitecture of SAM. Due to underwhelming results, we move to a more complex approach
in Chapter 3.2. Here, we take advantage of the valuable image encoder of SAM, pro­
cessing the reference frame fr through it and passing that information as embeddings
to the mask decoder. Since both of these models produce unexpectedly low results, the
subsequent implementations take a different direction. In Chapter 3.3, we combine SAM
with a modern and highly effective point tracker. In Chapter 3.4, we further enhance the
model’s capabilities by combining two trackers with SAM: one point tracker and one object
tracker.
3.1
SAM 4 Channels CNN
The rationale behind this architecture, as already stated, is to leverage the capabilities of
the CNN integrated within SAM, as depicted in Figure 3.1. Our objective is to introduce
a form of memory to the network by incorporating a previous frame as a reference point
fr. This is achieved by combining one of the previous frames with its corresponding
annotation and subsequently passing this composite input to the convolutional layer. To
accommodate this approach, the convolutional layer must be modified to accept an input
channel configuration set to 4, allowing it to process both RGB reference frame and its
segmentation mask simultaneously. Since we have access only to the first frame’s mask,
the first frame f0 is used as the reference frame.
The integration of memory into the network is critical for enhancing its predictive accuracy
and contextual understanding, particularly in scenarios where temporal dynamics are es­
sential. By providing the network with a historical frame of reference, we enable it to make
more informed predictions about the current frame.
Furthermore, it is crucial that the prompt data refers to the reference frame, f0, as our
goal is to predict the object present in the query frame fq, which is processed through
the image encoder. In this context, fq is devoid of additional information, and we rely
solely on the reference frame to inform our predictions. This methodology underscores the

3.2
SAM Custom Embeddings
This implementation aims to enhance the model by altering the embeddings produced by
the prompt encoder and replacing them with image embeddings from a previous frame,
f0, to serve as a reference. This approach seeks to introduce a form of memory to the
model but with different information compared to the previous experiment. We leveraged
the expectation of the mask decoder that the prompt encoder provides an output of N
tokens, each with a dimension of 256. Our strategy involves passing the reference frame
through the image encoder to obtain image embeddings with dimensions of 256x64x64.
Consequently, the image encoder produces 256 features, each with spatial dimensions
of 64x64
Next, we select points of interest from the reference frame, f0, with half of these points
being part of the objects (referred to as positive points) and the other half not being part
of the objects (referred to as negative points). These points are then mapped onto the
64x64 resized encoded images. For each point, we obtain 256 features. Therefore, the
number N of tokens corresponds to the total number of selected points, which are then

3.3
SAM and Co­Tracker
In this methodology, we aim to leverage the capabilities of the co­tracker in conjunction
with the SAM model to enhance object segmentation in video sequences. The co­tracker
is employed to track specific points throughout the video frames during offline processing.
This tracking process yields the coordinates of the selected points in each frame, which
is going to serve as valuable input data. The first frame, along with its mask, is used to
initialize these points, which are then propagated to the subsequent frames.
The output of the co­tracker, consisting of these coordinates, is fed into the prompt en­
coder of SAM. By integrating this tracked information, SAM can utilize the temporal con­
sistency provided by the co­tracker to segment the object across video frames accurately.
This approach leverages the co­tracker’s ability to maintain continuity of object tracking
over time, thereby improving the segmentation performance of SAM.
The object tracker also provides information about the visibility of the propagated points.
Points that may be occluded in subsequent frames, either due to the rotation of the object
or obstruction, are omitted.
Moreover, this combined method offers several advantages. First, the use of co­tracker
outputs as input to the SAM model ensures that the segmentation process benefits from
a robust temporal context, reducing the likelihood of segmentation errors that might occur
due to frame­by­frame variations. Second, by processing the videos offline, we can afford
to utilize more computationally intensive tracking algorithms, which may not be feasible in
real­time applications but significantly enhance the accuracy of the initial point tracking.

3.4
SAM combined with Object Tracking and Point Tracking
Building upon the previous method, this implementation seeks to enhance the prompting
of SAM by integrating an object tracker with a point tracker. Points alone can be unreliable
in certain scenarios, such as when the object rotates, the camera angle changes or the
object is occluded by another object, resulting in the invisibility of most or all initial points.
In such cases, specifying the object solely with points can be challenging. To address
this issue, we incorporated a lightweight ViT object tracker. The tracker is initialized with
the bounding boxes derived from the ground truth mask of the first frame of each video.
It then propagates these bounding boxes to subsequent frames. Consequently, both the
bounding box data and the point data are utilized to prompt SAM.
However, the object tracker also encounters challenges, particularly when the object is
obstructed for several frames and then reappears. During these periods of occlusion,
the tracker may erroneously focus on other objects and misidentify another object as
the target. When the object reappears, the tracker might either resume correct track­
ing or continue to struggle, erroneously following the wrong object. Additionally, issues
arise when similar objects are in close proximity. In such cases, the tracker might exhibit
anomalous behavior, such as tracking the incorrect object or producing a large bounding
box that encompasses multiple objects. To address these challenges, we implement a
validation mechanism: a bounding box is considered valid only if it contains at least one
positive tracked point, as provided by the Co­tracker. This ensures greater accuracy in
maintaining the correct object within the bounding box.

4.1
Dataset
The dataset utilized for this project is the DAVIS 2017 dataset [21], which is widely recog­
nized in the field of VOS. DAVIS 2017 comprises a total of 90 video sequences, specifically
curated to challenge and benchmark segmentation algorithms. These videos are divided
into a training set and a validation set, adhering to a 2:1 ratio. This results in 60 videos
designated for training purposes and 30 videos reserved for validation. Each video in the
dataset is annotated with pixel­level accuracy, providing detailed ground truth masks for
evaluating segmentation performance. The diverse and complex scenarios represented
in DAVIS 2017 make it an ideal choice for advancing and assessing the robustness of
VOS models.

4.2
Strategies
All experiments are conducted multiple times to ensure the selection of appropriate hyper­
parameters. We experimented with different optimizers, learning rates, epoch numbers,
Learning to Segment and Track any Object for Video Object Segmentation
13
==PAGE:24==
and loss functions to identify the most effective combination for training the model. This
iterative approach allows us to fine­tune the model’s performance and achieve optimal
results.
The loss function we employed throughout the experiments is Binary Cross Entropy (BCE)
with logits loss [22]. This loss combines the traditional BCE function with a sigmoid layer,
making it particularly suitable for handling binary masks with values in the range of [0,1].
We considered various loss functions during exploratory experimentation; however, they
did not demonstrate any particular efficacy for this specific project.
Initially, we focused on training only the parameters of the mask decoder, a widely adopted
approach for fine­tuning SAM. This method allows the model to adjust the mask gener­
ation process while keeping the rest of the network unchanged. But, we also explored
training both the mask decoder and the prompt encoder. This method aims to refine not
only mask generation but also prompt processing, thereby potentially enhancing overall
performance.
Importantly, we do not attempt to train the image encoder, as this would be counterproduc­
tive. The image encoder is designed to extract robust and general features from the input
images and retraining it could degrade these features and negatively impact the model’s
performance. Moreover, the image encoder has been pretrained on a large database,
endowing SAM with strong zero­shot generalization capabilities. Retraining it would un­
dermine these capabilities and diminish the benefits of extensive pretraining.
Most of the videos depict multiple objects. When a video contains more than one object
in its frames, we separate and isolate each object, treating them as distinct videos, each
focused on one of the individual objects. Consequently, the model might iterate through
the same frame multiple times, but each time the focus is on a different object.
Lastly, we utilize the base model of SAM. There are two additional versions: the large
and the huge models. The differences between these versions lie in the size of the image
encoder (specifically the ViT) and the number of parameters. To expedite the process,
we select the base model and safely assume that our findings would apply to the larger
versions as well, given their superior capabilities.
4.3
Metric
The primary metric used to validate the accuracy of the model is the Jaccard Index, also
referred to as the J­score or Intersection over Union (IoU) score. This metric is pivotal
in evaluating the performance of segmentation models as it quantitatively measures the
similarity between the predicted segmentation and the ground truth annotations. The
Jaccard Index is calculated as the ratio of the intersection of the predicted and ground
truth regions to their union, formally defined as:
IoU = |A ∩B|
|A ∪B|
(4.1)
where A represents the set of predicted pixels and B represents the set of ground truth
pixels. A higher IoU score indicates a greater degree of overlap between the predicted
segmentation and the actual ground truth, thus signifying better model performance. This
metric is particularly useful in the context of VOS due to its robustness in handling vary­
ing object sizes and complex background scenarios. By utilizing the Jaccard Index, we
can effectively assess and compare the precision and accuracy of different segmentation
models within the dataset.

Summary of results
In the first round, a benchmark experiment is set using information from the ground truth
masks of every frame, achieving a high accuracy of 79.59% when a combination of bound­
ing boxes and points is employed and 87.2% when incorporating boxes and masks. This
round serves as a reference and does not classify as semi­supervised learning, making
it incomparable with subsequent results. However, it suggests that the combination of
prompting methods is the best approach, especially when utilizing the low­scale masks.
The second round involves experimental modifications to SAM’s architecture without in­
troducing robust techniques, attempting to equip it with a simple kind of memory before
advancing to more complex ones. This resulted in a significantly lower accuracy of 5%, so
we deemed it unnecessary to proceed with other implementations that involve the CNN
of SAM as a memory component.
In the third round, image features from the image encoder are used as embeddings, which
are created from the reference frame, bypassing the prompt encoder as another attempt to
introduce some kind of memory. This approach was unsuccessful, yielding 0% accuracy.
The fourth round introduced the Co­Tracker, a point tracker initialized with random points
from the ground truth mask of the first frame of each video. The propagated points were
used to prompt SAM, achieving an accuracy of 58.32%.
The fifth and most successful round combines the Co­Tracker with a single object tracker
initialized with bounding boxes derived from the ground truth mask of the first frame for

Although the final model performed below expectations, our study reveals critical insights
into the integration of SAM with advanced tracking techniques for VOS, leaving hope for
future improvements. Suboptimal results indicate that there are numerous unexamined
concepts that offer significant potential for improvement.
An initial improvement would be to enhance point selection for the point tracker, ensuring
the propagated points provide greater clarity to the model in subsequent frames. One
option is to employ dense point tracking, where a large number of points are tracked
throughout the entire video. Alternatively, a k­medoid [23] approach could be used to
establish a uniform point distribution within the object’s area.
Moreover, another improvement would be periodically reinitializing the points and propa­
gating them again after a certain number of predicted segmentation masks. This approach
tries to minimize the errors of the point tracker caused by rapid movements of the object
or rotation. Similarly, we can do the same about the bounding box, reinitializing it, and
making the object tracker propagate it again in the following frames.
Furthermore, we can employ the CNN of SAM as it is intended, which is to provide it
with predicted segmentation masks (in logit form) from previous iterations. This has been
shown improvements in other implementations.
Finally, it is important to reitarate that the backbone of SAM that is used for this project
is ViT­B, while most modern implementations use the ViT­H, which on its own can bring
improvement to our results.
In conclusion, while the current study’s results are modest, and there is always the pos­
sibility of undetected bugs existing in the code, several untapped ideas hold promise for
future exploration. By addressing these areas, we can potentially achieve more robust
and effective solutions for VOS tasks.
