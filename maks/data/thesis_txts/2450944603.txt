Abstract
As the power system moves towards a more renewable future, fewer thermal power plants
are available for frequency regulation. A possible solution to this problem is to utilize the
increasing number of electric vehicles (EVs). While these vehicles are connected to the
grid, they can act as ﬂexible loads, reacting quickly to frequency changes, using a droop
controller. The batteries also allow them to deliver power to the grid (V2G). However,
while this solution might solve the frequency problem for the Transmission System Oper-
ator (TSO), it could generate new problems for the Distribution System Operator (DSO),
as large loads are coordinated and changed synchronously.
The aim of this thesis is to investigate how EV ﬂexibility for frequnecy balancing aﬀects the
distribution system. This is done by simulating a low voltage (LV) grid in Bergen, Norway.
BKK, the power company responsible for the distribution system Bergen as well as a large
part of western Norway, has provided information and parameters about the grid to ensure
accurate scenarios. About 30 diﬀerent scenarios were run in DIgSILENT PowerFactory
using Quasi-Dynamic simulations.
The analysis found that the main challenges were
overloaded lines, under/over voltage and rapid voltage changes (RVCs). Overloaded lines
and under voltage are caused by maximum loading, and not the fast changing loads. EV
ﬂexibility for frequency regulation could cause an increased simultaneity, but problems
would mainly occur due to the presence of EV independently of the service. On the other
hand, the sudden increasing and decreasing loads could cause RVCs, if the penetration
and charging rate is high enough, and the droop characteristic is steep. However, even
with 100% EVs, 3.6kW chargers and a droop characteristics with 0.2% droop gain, the
grid codes would only be broken 4 times throughout a week, for one of the loads.
After locating the potential challenges, the thesis set out to investigate possible solutions.
Earlier research has shown that EVs are able to provide reactive power for voltage regu-
lation. This concept was therefore utilized as a countermeasure to decrease the voltage
changes created by the frequency regulation service, thereby decreasing the likelihood of
under/over voltage and RVC. This proved to be successful and opens up the possibility for
EV to deliver both frequency regulation and voltage regulation simultaneously. The study
found that a QV-regulation with a dead band ﬁtted for the individual loads, based on the
normal/acceptable voltage levels, provided the best results. Furthermore, the location of
contributing EVs shown to be central to avoiding problems were EV located closer to the
medium voltage/low voltage (MV/LV) transformer provided the best solution.

1 | Introduction
1.1
Motivation
As the power system undergoes a transition towards decarbonization, discontinuation of
nuclear power, and increased centralized and distributed renewable energy generation, the
amount of variable energy production is reduced. This creates a need for other sources
of ﬂexibility for frequency-, voltage- and power ﬂow regulation. One potential source is
through consumer ﬂexibility, either though electric vehicles or other electric appliances.
During the last decade, there has been a drastic increase in the number of electric vehicles
(EV). This is especially prominent Norway, where the number of EVs and plug-in hybrid
vehicles (PHEV) doubled between 2016 and 2018. The share of new cars sold being EV
or PHEV is, was 2018, 50%[1]. The charging of these cars creates extra load on the grid
and a need for grid reinforcement and/or new solutions. At the same time as EVs are
becoming popular, distributed energy resources (DER), i.e. small scale energy generation
on a residential level, are appearing in the distribution grid. As the prices for photo voltaics
(PVs) are decreasing, the penetration of these DER units are increasing [2]. While DER
is positive for generating energy in a green and renewable way, new challenges occur as
consumers are now becoming prosumers with bi-directional power ﬂow. However, these
new technologies might be used for ﬂexibility purposes, thus increasing its positive impact
on the system.
To cope with these new challenges, a company called Tibber is working on a new energy
marked called "Energimarked 2.0". This is supposed to be a market place where consumers
can get economic incentives for delivering ﬂexibility. BKK, largest power distributor in
western Norway and external advisor for the thesis, has taken interest in this and is
investigating how to best implement the technology into the distribution system. This
thesis will look at how the distribution grid is aﬀected by EV providing ﬂexibility for
frequency regulation.
This service can potentially cause problems for the distribution
grid and solving frequency problems for the Transmission System Operator (TSO) might
therefore cause congestion and voltage problems for the Distribution System Operator
(DSO). The thesis is structured in the following way:

2.1
Power ﬂow
Power ﬂow is central in understanding how EV ﬂexibility eﬀects the distribution grid.
This section provides a fundamental understanding on the concepts most relevant for this
thesis.
When discussing how balancing ﬂexibility inﬂuences the power ﬂow and stability of a distri-
bution network, the conductor plays a signiﬁcant role. In the transmission grid, overhead
lines are the main type of conductors and they cover large distances in areas that have low
population density. Overhead lines are cheaper to install, however, distribution systems
appear in areas of high population density and cables are therefore preferable. Due to
the fact that cables lay underground, the weight of the cables is not as important as for
overhead lines. Thus, cables can be made of heavier, but less resistive materials. Further-
more, the resistance (R) in transmission lines is signiﬁcantly lower than the reactance (X),
resulting in a high X/R ration, between 5 and 10. However, the conductors in distribution
grid have a higher resistance and have therefore a low X/R ratio, between 0.2 and 2[5].

2.2
Stake holders
The power system is divided into a transmission and distribution system. The transmission
system transports high voltage (HV) power over longer distances, for example between
cities. The TSO, Statnett in Norway, is responsible for the transmission system. The
distribution system consists of medium voltage (MV) and/or LV lines and distributes
power to the consumers. The distribution grid is operated by the DSO, which in western
Norway is BKK. Furthermore, an aggregator is important for implementing ﬂexibility to
the system. This stakeholder aggregates loads in order to provide a reserve large enough
to participate in the reserve market. In addition to these, several other units participate
with diﬀerent services to the power system, however, not all are relevant for this thesis.
2.2.1
Transmission System Operator
The TSO is responsible for frequency- and voltage control, bottlenecks and other ancillary
services, as well as overall system stability in the transmission grid[12]. Relating to the
challenges illuminated in this study, the frequency control is the service of interest relating
to the TSO. Figure 3 shows the diﬀerent reserves for frequency regulation, with the ﬁrst
graph showing frequency, and the second graph showing how the power reserves respond
to the frequency.
Figure 3: Reserve products [13]. The top part shows the frequency, while the section graph shows
how the power output from diﬀerent reserves responds to the frequency.
Inertia is the ﬁrst service to respond when the frequency changes. The inertia help sustain
the frequency and decrease the severity of the frequency change, by releasing or absorbing
the energy stored in the rotating masses [14]. With less inertia in the system, the frequency
will be more susceptible to changes in load and production [15]. Figure 4 shows how the

The primary reserve consists of frequency containment reserve for both normal operation
and in case of disturbance, called FCR-N and FCR-D respectively. FCR-N is activated

within 30 seconds. FCR is, as previously mentioned, mainly provided by thermal and
hydropower plants, as they are able to quickly change their power production. Norway is

2.2.2
Distribution System Operator
The DSO is deﬁned, by the Norwegian government, as a company that has concessions in
regional- and/or distribution system, and more than 100 000 customers[21]. This entails
that the company is responsible for the maintenance and operation of the distribution
grid, and to ensure power distribution to the consumers [12]. To ensure this, the DSO
needs to keep the voltage within acceptable limits (presented later in the section), avoid
bottlenecks and overloads. As the penetration and capacity of DER in the distribution
system is increasing, new problems arise. While the customers have only been consuming
power, they are now producing and becoming prosumers[22]. However, if used correctly,
DER could be of great beneﬁt to the DSOs, both in terms of voltage regulation and
congestion management.
As the consumption peek occurs at the afternoon, this is when the likelihood of overloaded
lines and under voltages is highest. This is elaborate further in Section 5.3.1. The solution
for these problems have traditionally been to reinforce the grid or use tap changers on the
transformer stations[23]. Such solutions are not ideal, as grid reinforcement is expensive
and unpractical, and tap changers are can not always react fast enough is the voltage
changes quickly[6]

Aggregator
In a presentation from 2017, Energinet deﬁnes an aggregator as: "Aggregator: Has entered
into an agreement with an electricity customer on access to disposing of the electricity
customer’s ﬂexible consumption and/or generation in the electricity market. The aggre-
gator pools ﬂexibility from customers and converts it into electricity market services, for
example for use by the TSO, DSO and/or BRP."[27]. Where BRP is Balancing Responsi-
ble Party. This type of stakeholder enables thousands of consumers, that would not have
been able to participate in the energy market by themselves, to contribute to the energy
system. The aggregator thereby acts as an intermediate party between the EV owner and
the market[28]. As there are many ways of implementing an aggregate into the market,
its impact on the system is the same: To aggregate consumer ﬂexibility and to regulate
their consumption in a synchronous way. Tibber, established in 2016, is an example of an
aggregator[29]. The company delivers diﬀerent services to the consumers, therein moving
their EV charging to the times where the power prices are lowest. They have cooperated
with BKK on researching EV ﬂexibility in the distribution system and with Statnett on a
pilot project on consumer ﬂexibility (presented in Section 3.3).

2.3
Consumer ﬂexibility
There are many diﬀerent deﬁnitions for what ﬂexibility in power systems is, but the Danish
Energy Agency deﬁnes it as: “The term ﬂexibility describes the ability of a power system
to cope with variability and uncertainty in both generation and demand, while maintaining
a satisfactory level of reliability at a reasonable cost, over diﬀerent time horizons.” [30]
Throughout the coming years, the power system will, according to government plans, go
through a transition towards less fossil-based energy sources and more renewable energy
sources. This results in reduced control over the power production in order for it to match
the consumption. The traditional way of regulating the frequency has been to change the
production to match consumption. However, as less variable energy sources are available,
this becomes diﬃcult.
Therefore, a solution could be to regulate the consumption to
match production, instead of the other way around. This can be done using any electrical
appliances, but as EVs are large loads and temporarily reducing the charging rate does
not impact the customer more than a small increase in charging time, EVs seem to be the
ideal solution.
A list of ﬂexibility products arranged in a prioritized order is presented in Ref.
[12].
These can be seen in Table 2. The stakeholder of interest is written in parenthesis. To
prioritize the services, they are sorted into 4 categories where (1) is most important and
(4) is least important: (1) Emergency situations: ﬂexibility is required to avoid instability
or blackout. (2) Local requirements: only a limited number of ﬂexibility resources are
available. (3) Global requirements: Solving non-emergency problems in the transmission
grid. (4) Planning and ﬁnancial aspects: Non-technical limitations set by the accuracy
of forecasts and ﬁnancial penalties for not matching contracts.
Such prioritization is
important as the services can come into conﬂict with each other.

2.3.2
Balancing ﬂexibility at the distribution grid
When utilizing consumers for regulating their loads to match the production, i.e. frequency
regulation, the TSO creates large load changes in the distribution grid. Because of this,
TSO-DSO coordination is highly important in order for the service to generate additional
problems for the DSO[32]. As seen in Figure 2, there are several services to cover, and
solving one might make the EV unavailable for the others. As presented in Section 2.2.1
there are diﬀerent frequency regulation reserves and choosing the most ﬁtting for EV
ﬂexibility is important. The response time can be a signiﬁcant factor in deciding this and
will be investigated further in Section 3.
Figure 1 and Equation 1 in Section 2.1 describes how the voltage is impacted by the load.
As the EV loads are changing to provide frequency regulation, it will cause the voltage
level in the distribution grid to change. As mentioned earlier, Figure 6 shows that the
need for up and down regulation is about the same.
Therefore, the voltage would be
both increased and decrease because of the EV ﬂexibility. Furthermore, as the loads are
changing synchronously, the total load on the grid might increase, thereby increasing the
possibility of overloaded lines.
2.3.3
Flexibility for the distribution grid
Using ﬂexibility to tackle voltage and congestion problems has not been as common in
the distribution grid as in the transmission grid, due to the lack of variable generation
in the distribution grid. However, consumer ﬂexibility could be beneﬁcial for the services
presented in Table 2.
Because of the low X/R ratio that is typical for a low voltage distribution grid, using
reactive power is not as eﬀective for voltage regulation as it is in MV/HV systems where
the ratio is higher. [33, 11]. Because of this, smart charging of EVs might be an ideal
solution. However, while utilizing active power regulation for frequency control, reactive
power regulation for voltage control might be used to counterbalance the voltage problems
generated. By doing this, the service can be provided the TSO, while minimizing the eﬀect
on the distribution grid. According to the prioritization of ﬂexibility products presented
in Table 2, local voltage control should be prioritized over frequency regulation. However,
ﬁnding a common ground where frequency regulation can be delivered in while still keeping
the voltage stable, is of interest for both stakeholders.

3 | State of the art
The recent increase of electric vehicles, coupled with the increase in renewable generation
has sparked an interest among scientists for researching EV ﬂexibility. A literature review
from 2018, showed that most of the research is published after 2015[32]. Several TSOs
and research centers have shown interest in the technology, running pilot projects and
comprehensive test projects. Statnett, just ﬁnished a pilot project with Tibber, the ag-
gregator presented in Section 2.2.3, on balancing ﬂexibility. Several diﬀerent sources were
tried, while only some of them were able to fulﬁll the regulations. The project is thor-
oughly presented in Section 23. Furthermore, several projects have been done in Denmark
looking into EV ﬂexibility for voltage regulation, congestion management and frequency
regulation. The research papers from two of these projects are presented in the following
sections.
3.1
Parker project
The Parker project lasted from 2016 to 2018 and aimed at validating that EVs could be
used as a source of ﬂexibility both on a system-wide scale, but also locally[4]. It is based
on two previous projects, NIKOLA [37] and EDISON[38], which both showed that the
technology has great potential. The Parker project was the ﬁrst commercial project in
the world to provide system-wide ﬂexibility using V2G. 10EV with bi-directional chargers
were utilized in the project.
The authors in Ref. [28] set out to identify the barriers for implementing EV ﬂexibility
into the power system.
The paper presents both technical and market barriers.
The
technical barriers range from grid observability/smart metering, EV supply equipment
and communication. The main focus points of these were related to creating standardized
equipment, standardized EV tests and creating grid codes to ensure that every contributor
meets the expectations.
The study deﬁnes ﬁve diﬀerent attributes that characterize a
ﬂexibility source. These are depicted in Figure 8; direction, power capacity, starting time,
duration, location. These are theoretical attributes that need to be controlled in order in
order for EV ﬂexibility to have a positive impact on the system as a whole. Furthermore,
the paper presents 4 phases the technology needs to get through before implementing it
in the market. The ﬁrst phase is "initial preparation and viability" and is where they see
the technology today.
In Ref. [39], the authors present an experimental study on the role of EV in ﬂexibility. The
study looks at EVs capability to provide congestion management, local voltage support
and FCR. Through ﬁeld testing on one Nissan Leaf, the researchers found that even

to 2.5 seconds.

Ref. [5] studied voltage regulation using reactive power from electric vehicle supply equip-

low, and therefore reactive power is less eﬃcient for voltage regulation than active power.

Furthermore, the study found that the X/R ratio is not the most important parameter,
but rather the X and R values by themselves, and therefore the length of the feeder.
3.3
Statnett - Fast Frequency Reserves 2018
In 2018, the Norwegian TSO, Statnett, ran a pilot project that was intended to look at
a new type of reserve that would cope with the challenges that appear due to lack of
variable generation [44]. The fast frequency reserve (FFR) in the study should be acti-
vated at a frequency of 49.6Hz, respond within 2 seconds and have a possible duration
of 30 seconds. In addition to this, the reserve should be ready for a new activation after
15 minutes. 5 diﬀerent technologies were tested for these criteria; hydropower, pumped
storage hydropower, industrial thyristor-controlled electrolysis, aggregated EVs and unin-
terruptible power supply systems (UPS). The hydropower plant was not able to respond
quickly enough. The pumped storage hydropower had troubles with waiting for 30 seconds
before ramping back to normal operation. Thus, these two technologies did not pass the
qualiﬁcation test.
18th of July 2018, the Finish nuclear plant Olkiluoto was disconnected, causing a frequency
drop. It was considered highly unlikely that an event causing the frequency to drop to a
level below 49.6Hz would occur during the time of the pilot project. However, this drop
was so signiﬁcant it triggered the reserves. 2 out of 4 technologies activated its ﬂexibility
reserves. The 2 technologies that failed to respond, were both caused by issues related
to the measurement devices. In the end, the project was a success, proving that several
technologies are able to deliver ﬂexibility that meets the requirements from Statnett. A
rough estimate shows that the Nordic system needs 200-400MW of FFR. Statnett predicts,
in the report, that they will be able to acquire its share of this within 2020. However, to
make this work, IT and measuring technology need to be developed and veriﬁed.

4 | Problem Statement
As stated in Section 3, State of the art, several projects have been able to deliver both
voltage- and frequency regulation from EV ﬂexibility. The research has shown that the
concept has great potential, however, to what extent one service can have a negative
eﬀect on other aspects of the power quality, requires further research. The purpose of this
study is to look at how the use of ﬂexibility aﬀects the distribution grid. Based on the
stakeholders’ needs presented in Section 2.2, previous research presented in Section 3 and
discussions with BKK this thesis will look at three questions:
1. What, if any, problems arise when consumer ﬂexibility is used in the LV system?
2. What are the critical levels?
3. How can these problems be avoided/solved?
These questions are to be answered through simulation of a distribution grid in Bergen.
The simulation program used for this purpose is DIgSILENT PowerFactory, which provides
the possibility of gathering information on the power ﬂow in a distribution system over
time. To ensure that the results are generalizable, the chosen LV grid is compared to
benchmark grids used for similar purposes.

5 | Method
The purpose of this research was to look at how a distribution system is aﬀected by EVs
providing ﬂexibility as a service for frequency regulation. This was done by simulating
a distribution system in Bergen through 1 hour, with EV consumption connected to the
frequency via a droop controller. The cooperation with BKK opened up valuable possibil-
ities in terms of simulating scenarios that were as close to reality as possible. This chapter
presents the method and aspects taken into consideration.
5.1
DIgSILENT PowerFactory
To run power ﬂow simulations of the grid, DIgSILENT (Digital simulation of electrical
networks) PowerFactory 2018 was used. PowerFactory can be used for analyzing genera-
tion, transmission, distribution and industrial system[46], and for this case the distribution
system was the one of interest. The program is designed as an interactive software tool for
analysis of power system to optimize planning and operation. The accuracy and validity of
the results achieved in PowerFactory have been conﬁrmed a number of times by companies
that have used the program for planning and analysis of real power systems.
The program allows for several diﬀerent simulation functions, however, in this study only
Quasi-Dynamic Simulation was used. Through such a simulation, a distribution grid can
be simulated over a longer period of time with varying consumption. Thereafter, values
for voltage, loading, active and reactive power ﬂow, and more, can be found and graphed.
Furthermore, the programs can also create heat maps of the system, presenting potential
problems in a clear and visual way.

desirable to ﬁnd a topology that allowed the simulations to provide as much knowledge
as possible. Not only for the exact grid that was simulated, but also knowledge that can
be used in other areas. This section will present the diﬀerent aspects that were taken into
consideration when deciding on the grid model.

5.2.4
Transformers
For simplicity, the transformer was not considered when choosing which of the 8 LV grids
to simulate. This was done as the main problems were thought to be overloaded lines
and voltage drops in the conductors. However, when the grid was chosen, the respective
parameters were used.
As described in Section 2.4, there are both 230V and 400V distribution grids in Bergen,
depending on location. The MV grid, transporting the power between the LV grid have
either 11kV or 22kV. The transformers are equipped with tap changers, which are useful
if ∆V is too large. The tap changers can then be used to increase the voltage at the load
side of the transformer to e.g. 1.05 p.u., to avoid breaking grid codes of maximum 10%
voltage deviation from the nominal value. However, in the simulations, the tap changer
was not used.
As the position of the tap changer was not known, it was considered
the best solution to avoid unjustiﬁed assumptions. Furthermore, some distribution grids
used step-up transformers along the LV lines to raise the voltage a few percents to avoid
under voltage. As none of the grids in Table 4, nor the benchmark grids, have step-up
transformers, it will not be utilized in this study.
5.2.5
Interconnection
The reference bus in the PowerFactory simulation is to represent the rest of the Nordic
grid. As this is a large grid, the changes within the distribution grid are neglectable and
the reference grid can be seen as inﬁnitely large. This means that neither the system
frequency nor the voltage in the MV grid will be aﬀected by the changing loads. This does
set limitations on the analysis in terms of it deviating from reality.
5.2.6
One or three phases
When studying a distribution system, it can be simulated both as a one and three phase
system. In reality, there are three phases, and to provide 230V to the consumers, they are
connected between two phases. This means that the consumers are not loading the lines
evenly. There is also no oﬃcial system that makes sure that if there are 10 consumers,
they are evenly distributed across the three phases. This might result in an uneven load,
and an unbalanced grid. In the simulations done in this study, the grid is assumed to be a
balanced three-phase grid. This divides the load, i.e. 3kW, across the three phases, thus
loading them with 1kW each.

When constructing a simulation grid at the distribution level, the load sizes are important.

In Ref. [5], a distribution grid with residential loads were simulated with 12.5kW EVSEs.
A paper looking at power quality issues in LV grids due to EVs, provided a table, showing
diﬀerent charging power rates. These values are shown in Table 8. From this table it can be
seen that the charging rate varies from 2.3kW to 22kW, depending on the current and the
number of phases connected. Independent of these grid connection possibilities, there are
limitations on the EVs themselves. EVs have onboard chargers that are used to invert AC
current from the grid to DC current provided to the battery. A Nissan Leaf has a build in
charger of 6.6 kW, meaning that even with a 32A and 400V grid connection, the charging

5.3.2
Power Direction
As seen in Section 3, researchers have successfully utilized V2G technology and there is a
clear potential for applying it as virtual inertia or primary frequency control. Therefore,
a droop characteristic providing V2G at lower frequencies was used. Further elaboration
on that can be seen in Section 5.3.6.
5.3.3
Starting time and duration
Starting time could have great impact on how the distribution system is aﬀected. As shown
in Figure 18 in Section 5.3.1, the consumption is highest in the morning and afternoon.
Because of this, the lines are more likely to be overloaded in case of over frequency and
a need for increased consumption. The starting point was, in this case, connected to the
frequency. This entails that the starting point of the service is directly connected to the
times when the frequency deviates from its nominal value.
In Statnett´s pilot project, the requirements for the duration of the ﬂexibility sources
was set to be 30 seconds. In this thesis, the power consumption/injection was directly
connected to the frequency, and thus the duration was not relevant as an input.
5.3.4
Location
The exact locations of the EVs are diﬃcult to determine, as there is no knowledge about
the actual consumers in the area. Therefore, an even distribution of EVs was assumed.
This entails that for a scenario with 50% EV penetration, every load had its household
consumption, plus 50% of an EV load. As it does create a simpliﬁed version of reality, a
set of scenarios with non-even load distribution were done to make sure that all aspects
were investigated. As seen in Equation 1, the active and reactive power transmitted, have
impact on the voltage drop. Therefore, having heavier loads at the end to feeders will
cause larger voltage drops. Because of that, two separate scenarios with all EV located at
the ﬁrst half of each feeder, i.e. closest to the transformer, and one at the last half was
done in order to investigate this eﬀect. A third scenario was investigated where all EV
were located at the leftmost branch. In these three scenarios the EV located in the grid
charged at full capacity, i.e. as if the penetration was 100%.
One concern when implementing V2G is voltage increase as the load acts as a generator
instead of a load. To investigate scenarios where some consumers have full V2G EV loads,
were done. This is because with an even distribution, a household consumption of 1.6kW
and an EV charge rate of 2.3kW, the penetration would need to be 70% for the EV load to
exceed the household load. Therefore, a scenario where 4 loads around and including load

Droop Characteristic
Section 2.2.1 presents the need for both inertia and FCR. The pilot project done by
Statnett delivered a service, FFR, similar to FCR, but with a response time of 2 seconds,
compared to 5-30 seconds for FCR. Virtual inertia responds to the rate of change of
frequency, and because of the natural inertia that is to be replaced, the response time needs
to be even faster that FFR. Based on the relevancy of the pilot project from Statnett, the
ACES and Parker project, and the short response time needed to deliver virtual inertia,
the service chosen to be studied in this thesis is FCR and/or FFR. Both of these services
respond to the frequency at each given point by using a droop characteristic.
The droop characteristic is the correlation between frequency (f) and activ power consump-
tion/output (P). This correlation can be read from Equation 6, where rp is the droop gain,
∆f is the change in frequency, fnom is the nominal frequency, ∆P is the change in Power
and Pnom is the nominal power. This means that if the droop gain is 5%, a 5% change
in frequency causes a 100% change in power. This characteristic determines how much
frequency regulation the EVs can provide, and has great impact on how the distribution
system is aﬀected. Using a droop characteristic that represents a realistic scenario was
therefore of high importance.

Reactive power
According to a test done by Idaho National Laboratory on a Nissan Leaf, the power
factor when charging is between 0.98 inductive and 1.0, depending on the charging rate
[59]. Furthermore, BKK reports that the power factory in the households around the area
of interest is 0.98 ind., i.e. consuming reactive power. Because of this, the loads were
simulated with a power factor of 0.98 ind.
Figure 25: Correlation between P and Q giver power factor of 0.98 ind. and 0.9 cap.
In one of the articles [5] relating to the ACES study presented in Section 3.2, it was
demonstrated that reactive power could be used to increase the voltage in the distribution
grid.
The authors found that by setting a ﬁxed power factor on 0.9 capacitive would
increase the voltage by 0.012p.u.. Based on this research, this approach was applied ﬁrst
to SimpleGrid and then to BergenGrid, when delivering EV ﬂexibility with 0.4% droop
gain, 50% EV penetration and household consumption of 1.6kW. In Figure 25, the phasor
diagram with pf = 0.9 cap. shows that the apparent power is 1.11 p.u. As the reactance
in the lines is so low, the losses due to reactive power will also be low. A phasor diagram
is shown in Figure 25, illustrating the relation between active and reactive power given
the power factor of 0.98 ind. and 0.9 cap.
Setting a ﬁxed power factor provides a PQ-characteristic, increase the capacitive reactive
power when the active power consumption increase. Another solution would be a QV-
characteristic. In that case, the reactive power would be dependent on the voltage level.
By setting a dead band at normal voltage level of each individual load, the reactive power
compensation would only be activated when needed. However, to simplify the simulations
and avoid creating individual droop characteristics for each load, this analysis is done in

Analysis
After creating the grid model, and setting up the scenarios, PowerFactory was used to
analyze how the grid responded. Each scenario was run individually, by changing the load-
and power factor characteristic of each consumer. In case of the QV droop characteristic,
a static generator was placed next to each load to provide the reactive power. This was
done as LV loads in PowerFactory does not allow for such characteristics. As the voltage
and loading were the main variables of interest, these were graphed in order to see how
the system responded to each individual scenario. Furthermore, the voltage and loading
levels were analyzed in Excel to compare the results of each other. This was important
as it showed the correlation that allowed for a better understanding of the system. The
simulation results were studied and compared to the stakeholder needs presented in Section

7.2.1
Reactive power
Section 6.4 presents the simulation results where reactive power was used to counterbalance
the voltage drop. The power factor was initially set to 0.98 inductive. This was based
on parameters for household consumers from BKK, and for EVs from researchers and
developers. As an inductive power factor causes the load to consume reactive power, more
reactive power is conducted though the lines. This results in a larger voltage drop and
higher line loading, than with a unity power factor. Research shows the possibility of EV
chargers providing power in the fourth quadrant, i.e. providing capacitive reactive power,
which can be beneﬁcial for the power quality. The simulation shows that a capacitive power
factor of 0.9 contributes signiﬁcantly to counterbalancing the voltage problems cause by
EV ﬂexibility, as the research also showed.
The problem with a ﬁxed power factor is that the EV provides capacitive reactive power,
even at times where voltage control is not needed. This increases the charging time, and/or
increases the consumption of apparent power.
The EVSE provides reactive power by
changing the phase angle of the current, causing it to lead the voltage. This decreases the
DC provide to the battery. This is not an ideal solution, as eﬃcient energy consumption is
one of the central aspects on the road to a greener society. Additionally, providing reactive
power at all times also results in a higher voltage diﬀerence than necessary. Therefore a
QV-controller was proposed as an alternative solution. By creating a droop characteristic
that provides reactive power at times when the voltage is low, but with a dead band at a
point where the voltage is at a satisfactory level. This results in the voltage being lifted,
but not unnecessarily high, and therefore decreasing the likelihood for RVC.
The simulations showed that reactive power can counterbalance the voltage problems
caused by EV ﬂexibility for frequency regulation, to a signiﬁcant degree. This would also
open up for EVs providing voltage control at times where voltage problems arise unrelated
to frequency regulation. However, as the conductors in the grid have a larger resistance
than reactance, voltage regulation using active power would be more eﬃcient than using
reactive power. The beneﬁt of using reactive power for voltage regulation is that the active
power can then be used for frequency regulation. This would provide services for both
TSO and DSO. The prioritization of ﬂexibility products should, however, be kept in mind.
Additionally, the most important stakeholder to satisfy when implementing the service is
the owner of the EV. Without his/hers satisfaction, the EV would not be available.

in Section 5.3.1, that the load increase in the afternoon, as people come home from work.
Based on this, it is likely that the same pattern can be seen for EV charging. The charging
time is dependent on the charging rate, the initial SOC and the size of the battery. With
EV being connected at diﬀerent times, and EV being suﬃciently charged throughout the
evening/night, it is likely that not all EV connected to the grid are charging simultaneously.
Through a service provided by Tibber, the aggregator mentioned in Section 2.2.3, the EV
charging can be automatically moved to the time of day where the power prices are lowest.
If all EVs were connected to this service, the EVs would charge synchronously and result in
high simultaneity. If by implementing EV ﬂexibility, all EV, not only the ones connected
to the grid but not yet fully charged, participated, a similar scenario would occur. All
EV would increase their charging level synchronously if the frequency dropped, causing
increased line loading. How large these load charges would need to be in order to create
problems are discussed in Section 7.1.2 and 7.1.1. It is important to note that if an EV
is fully charged and using droop characteristics that do not allow for V2G, the EV would

8 | Conclusion
The aim of the study was to look at how EV ﬂexibility for frequency regulation aﬀects the
distribution grid. Understanding this is important, as providing frequency regulation to
the TSO can be a potential solution for the lack of variable energy generation resources
that might occur when moving towards a more renewable energy system. However, it can
also generate additional problems for the DSO, due to the large load changes. Consumer
ﬂexibility can be utilized for solving several diﬀerent problems relating to grid operation,
and it is important that solving one problem does not generate new ones. Both the Parker
and ACES project successfully utilized EVs for both voltage and frequency regulation.
Furthermore, a pilot project from Statnett was able to provide frequency regulation from
EVs, as well as other sources of consumer ﬂexibility. This suggests that the technology
has potential.
In the problem statement, three questions where proposed: (1) What problems arise when
consumer ﬂexibility is used in the LV system? (2) What are the critical levels? (3) How
can these problems be avoided/solved? These questions where studied by simulating an
LV distribution grid in Bergen, Norway. Simulations using the frequency throughout an
hour, the EV consumption with diﬀerent droop characteristics, penetrations and charging
rates, were done.
Question 1
The simulation showed that the potential problems most likely to occur where over/under
voltage, rapid voltage changes and overloaded lines. Which of these problems that arise
ﬁrst, depends on several factors, therein EV penetration, droop characteristic, charging
rate and household consumption.
Question 2
While every system is diﬀerent in terms of loading capacity, load distribution and impedance,
the simulations provide a good understanding of how the particular grid of interest re-
sponds to the service. Additionally, as the grid is chosen due to its resemblance to the
benchmark grids as well as LV grids in the area, the results are to a large extent gener-
alizable. The simulations found that a load change of 3.6kW per consumer would result
in a voltage drop of 10% at the loads furthest away from the transformer. Depending on
the initial voltage level, such voltage changes could break the grid codes and cause loads
to disconnect. Furthermore, the time it takes for this load change to occur depends on
the droop characteristics. The simulation with a three-step characteristic (fully charging,
idle and fully discharging) showed instantaneous voltage changes, which can break with
grid codes set for RVC. Using a 100% penetration, 3.6kW charger and 0.4% droop gain,
RVC breaking the grid codes at load A would only happen 4 times a week. Both RVC

8. CONCLUSION
and voltage changes are dependent on not only load change, but also of the initial voltage.
However, the results showed that this had only a small impact on the resulting changes.
The line loading can also be aﬀected by EV ﬂexibility. The grid of interest would have its
ﬁrst overloaded line at 5.6kW per consumer.
Question 3
Providing the service at night, when the household consumption is lower, decreases the
likelihood that it causes problems in the distribution grid. The likelihood that under-
voltage and overloaded lines occur are more dependent on the number of EVs than whether
or not they are providing ﬂexibility. However, ﬂexibility can increase the simultaneity as
the loads are now synchronized. Therefore, the ability of EVs to provide ﬂexibility should
be dependent on the time of day, to decrease the probability of voltage and loading issues.
Furthermore, several papers presented in the report suggested using reactive power to
provide voltage regulation. Setting the power factor to 0.9 conductive, instead of 0.98
inductive, both increased the voltage and decreased the ∆V between times of high and
low loading. Regulating capacitive reactive power depending on the voltage proved to be
a better solution compared to a ﬁxed power factor. Using a dead band, the charger can
limit the capacitive reactive power to only when it is needed, decreases ∆V compared to
a ﬁxed pf = 0.9 cap. Finally, the location of consumer ﬂexibility can have large eﬀect on
the result. Having the consumers further away from the transformer causes the power to
be conducted over longer distances, and thus experience greater voltage drops.
While pilot projects and previous research have shown the potential of the technology, this
thesis provides a thorough analysis of how a an LV distribution grid in Bergen responds
to the presence of EV ﬂexibility. It has shown that a signiﬁcant EV penetration and a
charging rate needs to be present before the service causes problems for the distribution
system. It also showed that the droop characteristic has a large impact and it is therefore
suggested that when implementing the service, the droop characteristic is ﬂexible. By
being able to change the droop depending on the penetration and charging rate, the
contribution of each EV can be optimized while still ensure grid safety.
8.1
Future research
While this thesis has looked at how diﬀerent EV penetration, charging rate and droop
characteristics aﬀect the LV system, further research should look at what penetration and
charging rate is expected in the future. The technology is developing at a rapid pace and
the market structure has great inﬂuence in the future of EVs. Furthermore, the thesis has
only looked at residential distribution grids and has not looked at the use of centralized EV
charging for ﬂexibility and its eﬀect of the system. Finally, before letting the technology
out into the market, a set of grid codes needs to be present in order to prevent problems.
