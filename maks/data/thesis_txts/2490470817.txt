Abstract
The bridge construction industry is a very competitive market and the winning bid often belongs to the
contractor who can oﬀer the most slender and longest bridge. The introduction of numerical calculations
in the last years has enabled the construction of stay cable bridges, and they keep increasing in size and
slenderness. Which is the basis for the increasing interest in the subject of supplemental damping. This
is supported by the case of the large structural amplitudes that was observed at the Oresund Bridge due
to wind/rain induced vibrations.
The scope of the thesis is to investigate the supplemental damping of large scale stay cables, with
the electromagnetic resonant shunt damper ERSD. The ERSD is constructed such that it imitates the
properties of the tuned mass damper or TMD, thus achieving better performance. The goal is to derive
the formulation of the ERSD with the shunt, for both in series- and in parallel connection. The ERSD
consists of electrical components which enables multiple shunts and hereby damping of multiple modes.
The electrical system is described in its correlating mechanical system, for which is used to calibrate the
ERSD with the ﬁxed point method. Modal analysis is applied to uncouple the MDOF system by the
modal representation. The calibration includes background ﬂexibility correction for multiple modes and
is re-calibrated with the equal modal damping method. The root locus analysis is applied to validate the
obtained modal damping of the system. The performance of the ERSD is compared to the performance of
the viscous dampers currently implemented at the Oresund Bridge. The mechanical damping parameters
for the ERSD is then transformed back into its electrical properties, and a proposal of the design of the
components is done.
The supplied data of the Oresund Bridge is derived directly from the source, as it is supplied by COWI
A/S who was the contractor on the project. The structural responses are obtained by harmonic loads
which triggers the resonance of the structure. The loads does not resemble the real cases as they are not
stochastic loads measured from the Oresund Bridge.
The project revealed that the equal modal damping for the ERSD is achievable for the MDOF system by
applying the background ﬂexibility correction. The ERSD in series connection signiﬁcantly outperforms
the viscous damping system in terms of the modal damping. The ERSD is proven sensitive to alternating
structural parameters and alternating damper parameters, it requires very accurate calibration.
It is concluded that it is not possible to design the electrical components for the ERSD implemented
near the anchorage point of the cable. To obtain more realistic values for the electrical components, the
ERSD has to be moved much closer to the displacements of the targeted modes. It is recommended that
the ERSD in parallel connection is investigated further as it maybe has the properties to enable more
realistic values for the design of the electrical components.

1 Literature study
The Tacoma Highway bridge in 1940 illustrated the phenomenon of structural resonance and it created
a demand for the supplemental damping. The classical theory for the tuned mass damper, TMD was
formulated by Den Hartog [20] and the performance was enhanced by the ﬁxed point method, Krenk
[28]. The TMD properties could be imitated by other resonant vibration absorbers by the balanced
calibration demonstrated by Krenk and Høgsberg [30]. Smith [26] then imitated the TMD properties
with the inerter, which lead to the ’J-damper’ scandal in Formula 1 [9].
Wu [31] demonstrated that the piezoelectric shunt dissipated vibrations, if it was calibrated by the ﬁxed
point method. Later Lossouarn [25] derived a design proposal for the piezoelectric shunt inductors. The
balanced calibration of the electromagnetic- and piezoelectric shunt damper was formulated by Høgsberg
[21]. According to Behrens [8] the EMSD can be used for mitigating vibrations for large scale structures,
and Zhu [16][17] demonstrated that they could harvest the energy at the same time.
Passive and damping for multiple modes are the topics investigated today and Cheng. et al[32] presented
the damping of multiple modes with the EMSD connected to a feedback controller and a semi-active
damping can be achieved as demonstrated by Wang [14] as the self powering damper system could power
the feedback controller, it seems that the holy grail is to derive a passive damping system for multiple
modes.

2 Introduction
The scope of the thesis is to examine the supplemental damping of large structural stay cables, in the
form of an electromagnetic resonant shunt damper or ERSD. The ERSD consists of a magnetic core and
a coil, connected to a shunt. The coil windings generates an electric ﬁeld and leads to an alternating
voltage Ve, by the relative motion between the magnet and the coil. The windings introduces inherent
inductance Le and resistance Re, the shunt attributes with increasing resistance Rs, inductance Ls and
capacitance Cs. The aim is to apply the link between the electrical system to that of the mechanical
system, as the force from mechanics f is proportional to the electrical current I by the coupling given
from the motor constant Km.
The performance of mitigating structural vibrations can be enhanced by imitating the properties of the
tuned mass damper or TMD. The TMD consists of a spring, damper and an auxiliary mass. The ERSD
does not include an auxiliary mass, but is instead activated by the relative motion between the magnet
and the coil. The target of the thesis is to design an ERSD to imitate the TMD, by a shunt in series
and parallel connection and compare it to the conventional viscous damping system.
The taut cable properties are described by Krenk [27] and the analytic supplemental viscous damping
applied to the cable is also formulated by Krenk [29]. The analytic solution is applied to verify the
numerical simulations derived by the ﬁnite element formulation. The theory of the ERSD circuit is
based on the work of Høgsberg [21] and the ﬁxed point calibration method by Krenk [28]. The results
of the ERSD series connection are compared to the results of the parallel piezoelectric shunt damper by
Wu [31]. The correcting of background ﬂexibility will follow the method described by Høgsberg [23] and
re-calibrate by the equal modal damping method given from Krenk [30] with correction included. The
performance of the ERSD for parallel/series are compared to the viscous dampers analytic results from
Krenk [29], numerical results by the discretized method by Krenk [27]. Subsequently comparing it to
stay cable 10ESSN on the Oresund bridge, Larsen [4]. The design of the ERSD’s electrical components
are determined from the applied method by Lossouarn et al. [25]. The examples given in the project
regarding the Oresund Bridge are supplied by Larsen [2], [3] and [4]. The dimensions of the stay cables
of the Oresund Bridge are attached in appendix F and technical drawings in appendix I. The report
structure is composed by the following topics illustrated at ﬁgure 2.1.

2.1
Stay Cables
When designing stay cables, the supplemental damping is not included before the cable has been di-
mensioned, the eﬀect of structural vibrations are dealt with afterwards. The Oresund bridge is a good
example described by Larsen [3], stay cable 10 ESSN had insuﬃcient supplemental damping supplied by
the viscous damping system. The solution was to implement both a viscous damper and a TMD, the
viscous damper IRD achieves δ = 4.5% with inherent damping and δ = 9.8% is required and the added
TMD δ = 5.5%. But two damper systems would complicate the continues maintenance and is not as
aesthetically, hence the aim is to investigate if the ERSD can provide δ = 9.8%.
The resonating loads acting on stay cables with diﬀerent frequencies are described by the U.S Department
of Transportation [5] and the variety of loads are listed below.
• Vortex excitation of an isolated cable.
• Vortex excitation of groups of cables.
• Wake galloping for groups of cables.
• Galloping of single cables inclined to the wind.
• Rain/wind-induced vibrations of cables.
• Galloping of cables with ice accumulations.
The Rain/wind-induced vibrations and galloping of cables due to ice accumulations is investigated in this
study. Ice accretion has the consequence of alternating the modal mass mr of the cables, demonstrated
by Koss [11] and Demartino [10]. The Oresund Bridge is investigated as the primary example, thus
acting as a benchmark between the viscous- and ERSD system. The Oresund Bridge stay cables are
illustrated at ﬁgure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Photos by Steen Brogaard [18]: The Oresund Bridges stay cables, illustrating a possibility of interference
galloping due to closely spaced cables.
The photos at ﬁgure 2.2 illustrates long and slender cables but also closely spaced in groups of two, thus
the possibility of inducing galloping, this was investigated by Larsen [3][4] and the necessary damping
for mitigating cable galloping is given as

If the wind speed and galloping instability are known for the cable, then the modal damping can be

2.2
Electromagnetic Resonant Shunt Damper
The aim is to construct the ERSD for a shunt in series and parallel circuit. The damper is calibrated
to imitate the TMD to enhance the performance, done by calibrating it by its mechanical parameters,
mass md, stiﬀness kd and damping cd. Note that the ERSD is an electrical system, thus the electrical
parameters has to be correlated to the coherent mechanical properties. The correlation between the
electrical and mechanical parameters are described by Chen et al. [9] and illustrated at ﬁgure 2.3.

9 Conclusion
The goal was to implement a numerical model based on the formulation of the taut cable. The model
has been successfully validated by the convergence analysis between the numerical and analytic natural
frequency, illustrated at ﬁgure 4.3.
The numerical viscous damping model based on the discretized

The formulation of the ERSD in series connection is veriﬁed by comparison to the results from Høgsberg
[21], appendix C. The ﬁxed point calibration for the ERSD has been validated by Wu [31], D. The equal
modal damping method with balanced calibration for the ERSD is veriﬁed for both ﬁxed point and
balanced calibration by Høgsberg and Krenk [24], appendix E.
The desired root locus analysis for the ERSD validates that it imitates the TMD properties by ﬁgure
6.6. The equal modal damping, ζA ≈ζB, of the ERSD in both series and parallel connection given at
the ﬁgures 6.11 and 6.12 for the 2 DOF system.
By implementing the MDOF system it has been revealed that the ERSD yields good results with back-
ground ﬂexibility correction. The ERSD approximates the results for the 2 DOF system. The ERSD
performs much better at resonance than for the viscous damper. Analysis of the sensitivities revealed
that the ERSD was much more sensitive to alternating structural properties and alternating damper
properties, then the viscous damper. The viscous damper also dampens better outside of resonance,
veriﬁed by the ﬁgures 7.1 and 7.2.
It has been validated that the ERSD is capable of dampening modes 1 and 2, for changing circuits and
that it can obtain equal modal damping for both modes, veriﬁed by the ﬁgures ??, 6.15 and 7.7.
The design of the ERSD electrical components leads to negative shunt resistance and inductance. The
sizes of the electrical components are unrealistic given from the tables 8.3,8.4 and 8.5. By moving the
ERSD much closer to the displacements of the speciﬁc mode, it yields much better results as listed in
the tables 8.3 and 8.6.
It is recommended that the design of the electrical components investigated much further, especially for
the ERSD in parallel connection.

10 Further Work
Section 8.1 concludes that a negative impedance of Ls and Re was required in order to design the shunt
inductor and resistor. The negative inductance for an electromagnetic shunt damper, EMSD has been
demonstrated to work by Zhou [15] and the negative by Stabile [13]. The ERSD with both a negative
inductance Ls and resistance Rs would be interesting to analyse, design and test.
The viscous damper IHD, implemented at stay cable 10ESSN on the Oresund Bridge broke. This was
only discovered by observations of large amplitudes, Larsen [4]. Hence the life cycles of a constructed
ERSD could be exciting to analyse and observe if it outperforms the diﬀerent types of viscous dampers.
It is demonstrated by Stabile [12][13] that the temperature would inﬂict the ERSD’s electrical compo-
nents, and subsequently alternate the ERSD properties. It would be interesting to analyse the ERSD
properties by changing temperature and compare them to the ﬁgures 7.12, 7.10 and 7.11 that represents
alternating properties for cd, md and kd.
A study of the magnetic inﬂuence on the ERSD electrical components, by experiments of diﬀerent support
constructions. Further an analyse on implementing ERSD on already built constructions, if they have
inherent magnetic properties which precautions should be taken.
For the structural dynamic problems, a passive damper is always preferred over the active and semi-active
dampers. A master thesis was proposed at DTU, dealing with a passive damping of multiple structural
modes by a friction damper that imitates TMD properties. It could be worth designing the equivalent
electrical components for the design.
Other applications such as supplemental damping of wind turbines. Oﬀshore wind turbines are subjected
to wind and wave loads and the frequency of the loads are often below 5Hz, Høgsberg [22]. A TMD
would could be connected just below the Nacelle, the TMD would be restricted to weight and damper
displacement. Hence it could be interesting to analyse the results with an ERSD implemented compared
to the TMD. This also implies to the viscous dampers implemented in the bottom of the OWT.

• Add all sub folders to the path.
• Open the ﬁle Smart_Calibration_Program
• Choose damper type

– ERSD parallel connection = 2
– TMD = 3
– Viscous damper = 4
• Choose if correction or not, only correction for ERSD
– Correction = 1
– No correction = 0
• Load the examples in
• Choose number of elements
• Choose damper location by connectivity
• Choose desired damping for ERSD and TMD
• For the 2 DOF system choose damping by mu under ”—-Calibrating the smart damper”—-
• Choose the range under ”—-FRF for the full system—-”
• Alter the applied force under ”—- Response function—-”
For calculations of the Root Locus
• For the MDOF system, it is calculated in the folder ”7 Root Locus”
