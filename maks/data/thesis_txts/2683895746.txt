Abstract 
The objectives of the study 
The objectives of the study were to investigate the effect of a novel hot air-drying (52 oC) 
method on the physiochemical properties of food. For this purpose, fresh and frozen Ulva spp. 
and Fucus vesiculosus were dried and compared with freeze dried samples of the same species. 
The physiochemical properties analyzed were color profile, water activity, water holding 
capacity, oil holding capacity, moisture content, ash content, protein content, amino acid 
profile, free amino acids, total phenolic content, pigment profile and antioxidant activity.  
The results of the study 
The analyses reveal that heat sensitive compounds may decrease due to hot air-drying. Water 
soluble compounds may also decrease in frozen seaweed which is hot air-dried due to thawing 
but it is not always the case. Difference in properties were found mostly when the hot air-dried 
samples were compared to freeze dried samples.  
The Ulva species could not be identified as its genome sequencing was in process when this 
report was submitted.

1. Introduction 
 
Figure 1: Types of algae. 1 is microalgae and 2, 3 and 4 are macroalgae. 
Seaweeds consist of several thousand species (Meadows & Campbell, 1988) of marine 
macroscopic photosynthetic algae in nature and have a variable chemical composition depending 
on the season and habitat. There are three different categories of seaweed: Phaeophyceae 
(brown seaweeds), Rhodophyta (red seaweeds) and Chlorophyta (green seaweed) (figure 1). The 
nutritional composition of the three categories differs as well as their properties and applications.  
Seaweeds have been used in Southeast and East Asian cuisine for more than a millennium and is 
being used by Western people in the past 3 centuries for consumption and commercial use (Atack 
et al., n.d.). The largest consumers of seaweeds are Chinese, Japanese and Korean people and 
the 3 main seaweeds used as food are Undaria pinnatifida, Laminariaceae spp., Pyropia spp. and 
Porphyra spp., the highest value being of the latter which is pressed into nori sheets to wrap rolls 
of sushi or onigiri (Mouritsen et al., 2018). 
Seaweeds are widely used in Asian cuisine due to its umami taste, ‘umami’ being a word first 
coined by Japanese chemist Kikunae Ikeda in 1908. It was the discovery of glutamate receptor, 
located in of taste buds that confirmed the taste of umami flavour (Chaudhari et al., 2000). 
==PAGE:8==
P a g e  | 8 
 
 
 
Moreover, certain compounds, like inosinate in katsuobushi (bonito flakes) and guanylate in 
dried shiitake mushrooms are known to work in synergistic fashion with glutamate to provide an 
umami taste and this has been known by Japanese for centuries (Mouritsen et al., 2012).The 
umami taste of seaweeds is mainly due to high content of glutamic acid and aspartic acid 
(Bocanegra et al., 2009). Seaweeds also have medicinal properties and have a salty, fishy and/or 
umami taste depending on the seaweed and where they grow. Seaweed has recently been used 
as a filling in meat products (Gullón et al., 2020) and to provide a fishy flavour in plant-based fish 
products. Dry seaweed is also used as a flavour enhancer and thickener in foods (Roohinejad et 
al., 2017). 
 
Most species have high levels of different bioactive compounds such as polyphenols and 
carotenoids (Holdt & Kraan, 2011; Khalid et al., 2018) and have nutritional benefits (MacArtain 
et al., 2007; Matanjun et al., 2009)  and are the main reasons for consumer acceptance of 
seaweeds in the EU, the other being the gastronomic potential of seaweeds (van den Burg et al., 
2019). The nutritional composition of seaweeds is affected by geography, seasons, and water 
quality (Kumar et al., 2015; Schiener et al., 2014; Susanto et al., 2016). 
In 2005, the global production of seaweed was 14.7 million tonnes while in 2015, production had 
nearly doubled to 30.4 million tonnes, 29.4 million tonnes of which was cultivated. Annually, a 
wide variety of products are made from the seaweed industry which have an estimated value of 
5.5-6 billion USD which amounts to about 12 million tonnes of seaweed and 85% of this is used 
in food products for human consumption. Due to demand outstripping supply of naturally 
growing seaweed, cultivation industries now produce more than 90% of the markets demand. 
In Denmark, there are approximately 350 species of seaweeds found naturally but only 10 are 
used for eating purposes (Hendriksen & Lundsteen, 2014). Commercial seaweed cultivation is 
growing in Denmark with a focus on Saccharina latissima, Ulva spp. and Palmaria palmata. 
Industrial cultivation of Fucus vesiculosus has been attempted, but with no success, and are still

harvested from natural populations. There are a few seaweed cultivation plants in Denmark, for 
both research and commercial purposes. In the years 2011-2015, the Coastal Directorate issued 
permits for 7 seaweed facilities (Jens et al., 2016). 
Studies show that seaweed consumption is related with lower breast cancer rates in Japan and 
China as compared to North America and Europe (Pisani et al., 1999; Yuan & Walsh, 2006). 
Neurodegenerative diseases and cancer were also lower in East Asian countries (Barbalace et al., 
2019). Seaweeds constituted more than 10% of Japanese diet with average consumption 1.4kg 
per person per year (Burtin, 2003) 
Fresh seaweeds have a high moisture content (75-90%) and are highly perishable due to 
enzymatic activity, lipid oxidation and microbial activity. So, a preservation method, such as 
drying, is applied right after harvesting because there is a risk of microbial spoilage, particularly 
in the moist weather of the northern hemisphere. Drying also decreases the water activity (aw), 
weight, and volume, making it easy for logistics. Once dried, the seaweed can be stored in a cool 
dry place for a few years without loss of gel content (Naylor, 1976). Furthermore, seaweeds need 
to be properly dehydrated to make it viable for industrial processing. 
It has been demonstrated that drying in the natural environment leads to uneven drying. This 
reduction in drying quality has led to conventional drying methods like vacuum drying and hot 
air drying. However, there is difference in bioactive and volatile compounds, antioxidant capacity 
and general quality when using these drying methods (Neoh et al., 2016; Sappati et al., 2019; 
Uribe et al., 2019) so the process method with least amount of change to the seaweed is 
preferred.   
1.1 Theoretical background 
Certain parameters are important to consider regarding food processing, like colour and 
nutritional composition. The colour of food has a very strong influence in consumer choice. 
Colour and taste are equally considered by consumers. The colour of food may change with the 
number and method of processing steps needed before the product is ready. In one study, the 
==PAGE:10==
P a g e  | 10 
 
 
 
colour of sea moss (Kappaphycus alvarezii) was different when the seaweed was freeze dried, 
dried in the sun or dried in the shade (Vairappan et al., 2014). Water is important in food 
processing as it is a solvent for many reactions. When the water in food has been evaporated to 
a certain percentage and microbial growth is controlled, the dehydrated food is still susceptible 
to undesirable chemical and physical changes. This is because the water content predicts little 
about the properties of the water present in food. In many instances, the water content in the 
food changes over time as the water in the food may or may not be in equilibrium with the water 
vapor in the environment. This influences the stability of the food (Troller & Christian, 1978). 
Water activity (aw) determines availability of water for chemical reactions, enzyme activity and 
microbial growth. Low aw is associated with a longer shelf life. The limiting aw for life forms, like 
bacteria and fungi, to grow in foodstuffs is ≤ 0.6 (Sappati et al., 2019). 
Water holding capacity (WHC) is the ability of food nutrients to hold or release water from their 
three-dimensional structure. WHC has an important role in developing food texture and 
emulsions, especially in beverages and semi solid foods. Various intrinsic and extrinsic factors like 
pH and temperature affect the WHC capacity of food (Haque et al., 2016). High oil holding 
capacity (OHC) in certain food ingredients is indicative of stable emulsification and flavour 
enhancement in food (Brewer et al., 2016). Salt and pH can influence emulsification properties 
(Sarkar et al., 2016).  
Amino acids, pigments and polyphenols are important compounds determining the nutritional 
composition of foods. Protein quality is as important as protein quantity in seaweeds (Stévant et 
al., 2018), particularly as seaweed is a possible ingredient in plant-based foods. The nutritional 
quality of proteins can be determined by the amino acid profile. There are 22 amino acids of 
which nine (THR, MET, VAL, HIS, LYS, LEU, PHE and ILE) are essential amino acids (EAAs) because 
the body cannot synthesize them. LYS, ILE, and VAL are branched chain amino acids (BCAAs) and 
are vital because they comprise 35 – 40% of EAA in human muscle tissue (Shimomura et al., 
2006), and are therefore of good quality to build muscles. Free amino acids are single amino acids 
that need no digestion in the body.  
==PAGE:11==
P a g e  | 11 
 
 
 
Plant pigments are molecules which give plants their characteristic color and are important for 
photosynthesis (Jaleel et al., 2009). Pigments in photosynthetic organisms are chlorophylls, 
carotenoids, and anthocyanins (Sudhakar et al., 2016). Chlorophylls are natural green pigments 
which are vital for photosynthesis while carotenoids are lipid soluble pigments found in fruits, 
vegetables, and algae. Different carotenoids exhibit numerous chemical and functional 
properties (Young & Lowe, 2001). Carotenoids are produced by many fungi, bacteria and 
microalgae, which are currently the main source of industrial carotenoid production (Zhao et al., 
2019). Lutein, lycopene, zeaxanthin, fucoxanthin and astaxanthin are some common carotenoids. 
One of the most important carotenoids is fucoxanthin, which is an orange-colored pigment and 
is one of the most abundant carotenoids. They are found in brown seaweeds and diatoms and 
have notable applications in human health and nutrition. Fucoxanthin was first isolated from 
Fucus spp. in 1914 (J. Peng et al., 2011). 
Polyphenols are a class of compounds comprised of multiple phenol structural units. They are 
micronutrients and have numerous therapeutic and nutritional properties. The most common 
phenolic compounds in brown seaweeds are phlorotannins (Agregán et al., 2017). Polyphenols 
are present in numerous fruits, vegetables, spices and even nuts (Vinson & Cai, 2012). Brown 
seaweeds are particularly known to exhibit antioxidation properties due to polyphenols (Dang et 
al., 2017; Hahn et al., 2012). Polyphenolics show antioxidation activity (Bors & Michel, 2002). 
They are known to be good reducing agents, free radical scavengers and metal chelators. 
Different studies signify nutraceutical properties of phlorotannins as well as their application in 
food, pharmaceutical and cosmetic industries (Mekinić et al., 2019).  
Some common methods to test antioxidant activity are DPPH scavenging activity, iron chelation 
and iron reduction. 1,1-diphenyl 1-2-picrylhydrazyl (DPPH) is a stable radical that is used to 
evaluate the capacity of antioxidants to capture and disable free radicals. The ability of 
antioxidants to reduce radicals is important to determine if the food ingredient can be used as 
an antioxidant. Iron chelation is the antioxidants ability to bind iron ferrous (Fe+2) ions. Ferrozine 
is added to the assay which forms a colored complex with excess iron. Higher iron binding ability 
==PAGE:12==
P a g e  | 12 
 
 
 
of the antioxidant with iron will lead to a lighter color of the complex. The absorbance is observed 
spectrophotometrically. Iron reduction determine antioxidants ability to reduce ferric (Fe+3) to 
ferrous (Fe+2) ion. An increased reduction will lead to an increased absorbance when observed 
spectrophotometrically. Ferrous supplements have higher bioavailability than ferric supplements 
to treat iron deficiency (Santiago, 2012) so this assay is important to understand iron 
bioavailability by the antioxidants in the sample.  
1.1.1 General chemical composition of seaweeds 
Composition of seaweeds vary considerably among species and due to season, geography, and 
population. Proximate composition of air-dried seaweeds is 3-47% carbohydrates, 1.5-4% lipids 
and 10-35% ash (Y. Peng et al., 2015). Protein content is higher in red and green seaweeds (10-
47% dry weight (d.w.)) than in brown seaweeds (3-16% d.w.) (Rohani-Ghadikolaei et al., 2012). 
Some seaweeds, like Pyropia spp. and P. palmata have a higher protein content than high protein 
legumes such as soybeans (Fleurence, 1999). Considering protein quantity, it is possible to 
develop a product or meal with a nutritional claim of high protein, either for humans or animals 
(Rohani-Ghadikolaei et al., 2012). Likewise, it is also possible seaweeds can be used as a cheap 
plant-based protein source (Černá, 2011). There are also differences in the amino acid profiles of 
seaweeds, some with high quality profiles. Seaweeds have essential amino acids (EAA), such as 
valine and isoleucine, indicating an above average protein quality. The main AA in Undaria 
pinnatifida is leucine while Pyropia spp. contains leucine, phenylalanine, and threonine. Leucine, 
valine, and methionine are abundant EAAs in P. palmata. Considering the EAAs in Ulva spp., they 
are considered as complete proteins (J. Ortiz et al., 2006).  
Seaweeds contain 1.5-4% of lipids (d.w.) due to which macroalgal fat cannot be considered a 
relevant source of energy.  Seaweeds contain 3-47% (d.w.) of carbohydrates depending on the 
species and season. Structural components, like aalginate and cellulose undergoes little annual 
variation, however, storage carbohydrates like, laminarin and mannitol, are higher in summers 
(Sharma et al., 2018). The lipid and carbohydrate content does not change due to drying (Badmus 
==PAGE:13==
P a g e  | 13 
 
 
 
et al., 2019; Sappati et al., 2019; Stévant et al., 2018) which is why lipid and carbohydrate analyses 
were not considered for current study.  
One of the main utilities of seaweed is the extraction of hydrocolloids which are used in the 
ingredients and biotechnology industry. The industry uses about 8 million tonnes of seaweed 
annually (Ferdouse et al., 2018). Algal hydrocolloids are also mainly polysaccharides. Seaweeds 
are used extensively in the hydrocolloid industry since the 1980s, when only a few countries were 
processing seaweeds for hydrocolloid extraction. Due to economic potential in hydrocolloid 
extraction, several countries have realized the potential and value of seaweed compounds and is 
now a profitable business (Bixler & Porse, 2011).  
1.1.2 Effect of drying on the chemical composition of seaweeds 
 
Figure 2: A schematic representation of a sorption isotherm with a hysteresis between the 
adsorption and desorption isotherms. An isotherm can be divided into three regions 
(Airaksinen, 2005). 
A general desorption isotherm follows a type II sigmoid curve (figure 2) during drying as reported 
by (Uribe et al., 2019) while numerous other seaweeds also follow this pattern. Seaweeds are 
dried because after harvesting there is a risk of spoilage, particularly in the moist weather of the 
==PAGE:14==
P a g e  | 14 
 
 
 
northern hemisphere. Drying also decreases the water activity (aw), moisture content, weight and 
volume and reduces microbial growth. Once dried, the seaweed can be stored in a cool dry place 
for a few years without loss of gel content (Naylor, 1976). Also, hydrocolloid extraction is only 
possible with dried seaweeds. 
The moisture content and aw of seaweeds are highly dependent on the drying methodology used. 
All drying methods, like sun drying and vacuum drying, reduce the aw thus extending the shelf life 
when compared to fresh samples. A higher moisture content was reported for sun drying as 
compared to conventional drying, vacuum drying and freeze drying (Uribe et al., 2019). 
Antioxidant activity and phenolic compounds decrease with drying and storage (Jiménez-Escrig 
et al., 2001). Ash content is higher in conventional drying methods as compared to freeze drying 
or vacuum drying (Sappati et al., 2019). In addition, temperature and drying time are often 
reported to lead to the degradation of proteins in food and longer drying times lead to lower 
proteins. The surface colour of seaweeds shifts from lighter to darker tone after drying, which is 
partly due to non-enzymatic Maillard reactions between reducing sugars and amino acids. 
Moreover, chlorophylls and carotenoids undergo isomerization and/or oxidation, leading to 
darker colours (Silva et al., 2019). 
Conventional drying shortens the drying time as compared to vacuum or sun drying. This is due 
to the higher temperatures employed in conventional oven drying. Vacuum drying gives a lower 
drying rate, due to low temperatures, and due to the inability of vacuum to overcome the bonds 
between water and other molecules.

1.1.3 Ulva spp. 
 
Figure 3: Different strains of U. lactuca. A, B and D are from Andøya and C from Bodø, northern 
Norway. 
Ulva spp. are edible green seaweeds, several of which are categorized as novel food in the EU 
(European Commission, n.d.). Ulva lactuca is the most common Ulva species. Its fronds are thin 
and flat, appear somewhat ruffled and their average length is 18 cm. The seaweed has color 
variations from green to dark green depending on whether it is underwater or has washed 
ashore. It is, however, difficult to identify Ulva spp. from morphology alone so gene sequencing 
techniques are used to identify them (Kawai et al., 2007). Examples of Ulva spp. is given in figure 
3.  
Ulva lactuca grows in on both sides of the Pacific and Atlantic coasts. It is also found in the Indian 
Ocean, South East Asian seas, and Australia. It is used to add flavor to soups and salads. When 
dried, the blades darken and become brittle, so it is easy to powder it. In Japan, U. petrosa, known 
as aosa nori, is sometimes used to wrap sushi as the texture closely resembles nori which are 
made from Porphyra spp. Aosa nori is used as it has a milder taste and are less expensive. Ulva 
==PAGE:16==
P a g e  | 16 
 
 
 
spp. powder is used as a savory seasoning in processed Japanese foods like pancakes and noodles 
or to give flavor to potato chips and rice crackers (S. K. Kim et al., 2011). 
Ulva spp. have unique polysaccharides, called “ulvan” that have been researched extensively. 
Ulva spp. have high water holding capacity. An analysis of polysaccharides from U. lactuca show 
that 1 g of fiber could absorb 1.1 g of oil and 11 g of water (Yaich et al., 2015). Moreover, ulvans 
from U. petrusa modified to have greater number of sulfate radicals have exhibited stronger 
scavenging, reducing and chelating abilities than native ulvans, indicating potential antioxidant 
properties (Qi et al., 2005). Ulva spp. are a good source of proteins and minerals (Roleda et al., 
2020; Tabarsa et al., 2012). Ulva spp. have numerous bioactive properties due to carotenoids, 
polyphenols and certain peptides (Déléris et al., 2016). Chlorophylls and carotenoids in Ulva spp. 
significantly decrease after several drying methods when compared to freeze drying (Uribe et al., 
2019).  
Minerals are the main component of dried Ulva spp. with levels between 22 – 36% (d.w.) as 
reported for U. lactuca (Peña-Rodríguez et al., 2011) and U. intestinalis (Rohani-Ghadikolaei et 
al., 2012). Air drying leads to increased greyness in Ulva spp. (Pinheiro et al., 2019). Low 
temperature drying like freeze drying and vacuum drying show little difference in the essential 
amino acid composition of Ulva spp. (Uribe et al., 2019).

1.1.4 Fucus vesiculosus 
 
Figure 4: Fucus vesiculosus. It is characterized by swollen areas on the fronds which are the 
reproductive receptacles, ©(Guiry, 2021)  
Fucus vesiculosus is a brown seaweed, known commonly as “bladderwrack”, and is found in the 
intertidal zones along the coastlines of the Baltic Sea and North Atlantic Ocean (Nygård & Dring, 
2008). It grows to about 90 cm tall and is characterized by spherical air bubbles placed pairwise 
on its fronds (figure 4). These air bubbles are absent in younger plants. Other important species 
in the genus are Fucus serratus and Fucus spiralis. 
Fucus vesiculosus has numerous nutritional and phytochemical properties (Balina et al., 2016; 
Honold et al., 2016). Iodine was first discovered in F. vesiculosus by a chemist in 1811 and was 
used in traditional medicine to treat goitre and other thyroid related problems, along with 
arthritis, obesity and digestive problems (Catarino et al., 2018). Fucus species are also an 
excellent source of bioactive compounds such as phlorotannin and fucoxanthin which have 
therapeutic properties against cellulite and skin aging, blood clotting, asthma, psoriasis, cancer 
and inflammation (Ale et al., 2011; Catarino et al., 2019). Due to these functional properties, 
Fucus spp. have seen great use in the cosmetic, pharmaceutical and nutraceutical industries 
(Díaz-Rubio et al., 2009). Fucus vesiculosus is also high in fibre, particularly alginic acid and 
fucoidans, alginic acid being as high as 59% (dry weight (d.w.)) in some cases (Radwan et al., 
==PAGE:18==
P a g e  | 18 
 
 
 
1996). Several studies show antioxidant and antitumor behaviour of fucoidans while alginic acid 
gels have pharmaceutical properties (Afonso et al., 2019). 
Due to the presence of higher bioactive dietary fibre and antioxidants in F. vesiculosus as 
compared to other seaweeds, F. vesiculosus extracts and fucoidan powders are used as functional 
ingredients and supplements (Dürig et al., 1997; K. T. Kim et al., 2014). F. vesiculosus extracts can 
be used to reduce lipid oxidation in fish-oil enriched mayonnaise (Honold et al., 2016), granola 
bars (Hermund et al., 2016), milk (O’Sullivan et al., 2014) and yogurt (O’Sullivan et al., 2016). 
Fucus vesiculosus powder can also increase the rheological properties of bread in bakery (Arufe 
et al., 2018). Fucus spp. extracts have utility in the field of microbiology as alginates from 
seaweeds are considered good prebiotics for several GI tract bacteria (Cherry, Yadav, et al., 2019) 
and the addition of fucoidans from F. vesiculosus in beverages have demonstrated strong 
bactericidal effects against S. typhimuium and L. monocytogenes (Poveda-Castillo et al., 2018). 
Fucus vesiculosus may also contain arsenic (Cherry, O’hara, et al., 2019) and inorganic arsenic is 
a potential risk due to being carcinogenic.  
Like all seaweeds, nutrients in F. vesiculosus also varies with seasonal changes (Lehvo et al., 2001; 
Riget et al., 1995). On a wet basis, the moisture content of fresh F. vesiculosus fluctuates from 
71-84% and ash from 23-36%. Fibre content fluctuates the most from 4 – 59% (Catarino et al., 
2018).   
Drying leads to uneven colour changes in Fucus vesiculosus with the seaweed becoming more 
darker and yellow (Moreira et al., 2016). Fucoxanthin is mainly responsible for the colour of 
brown seaweeds.  
Polyphenols, and as a result antioxidant activity, in Fucus vesiculosus is lower in freeze dried 
samples when compared to oven dried samples (Silva et al., 2019). This is in contrast with other 
studies as a drying temperature of 70 oC and 90 oC resulted in higher fucoxanthin yield from 
Saccharina latissima (Stévant et al., 2018) and Sargassum spp. (Norra et al., 2016) and these 
results could possibly be utilized by the hydrocolloid industry for higher extraction.

1.2.1 Freeze drying

figure 6. 
==PAGE:20==
P a g e  | 20 
 
 
 
 
Figure 6: Product temperature profile during freeze drying (Cristina Ratti, 2013) 
Freeze drying (FD) is a low temperature dehydration process which utilizes sublimation. It is done 
by freezing the product, decreasing the chamber pressure, and then removing the ice by 
sublimation at the triple point of the material (Figure 5). This ensures that ice will sublimate 
rather than melt. During the freezing step, larger ice crystals are preferred, as this will facilitate 
the faster removal of water. Larger ice crystals are produced by freezing the product slowly. In 
contrast smaller ice crystals are formed by instant freezing but will lead to a higher resistance to 
water transfer in the food product (Nowak & Jakubczyk, 2020). 
FD results in a higher quality product which includes better retention aromas, original flavors and 
heat sensitive nutrients are retained and less shrinkage of the product (Franks, 1998). However, 
volatile compounds like alcohol and acetic acid can also sublimate, if they are present in the food 
product, which may lead to undesirable changes.   
==PAGE:21==
P a g e  | 21 
 
 
 
During freeze drying microscopic pores are created in the product which enables fast 
rehydration, but also leaves the biomass more porous and brittle. Depending on the product, the 
freeze drier and duration of cycle, the cost of FD is about 4-8 times more expensive than air drying 
(C. Ratti, 2001) so the process is not preferable if the value of the product remains unchanged 
after freeze drying. Since biomass samples are needed in dried form for chemical analyses, FD is 
currently the only method resulting in maximum retention of compounds when compared to 
fresh samples (Franks, 1998). Desired physical properties such as crispiness, low moisture and 
water activity are also achieved with FD. However, FD is not feasible to scale up and other 
methods are utilized, with compromise in product quality. The current study is a comparison of 
freeze-dried seaweeds with a novel hot air drying to understand the quality of air-dried seaweeds 
so that the air drier can be used for other food products. 
1.2.2 Novel hot air blast drying 
The novel hot air drying (AD) method utilized by Hansen and Lindstrøm (H&L) uses a temperature 
of 52 oC. The product to be dried is laid on a perforated surface and the hot air is blown through 
the perforations directly on the product. After drying, the product is detached from the surface 
with a scrapper. The samples provided by H&L were dried for 50 (±5) minutes. The company gets 
a dried crispy seaweed product, but they do not know what compounds are affected by this. 
1.3 The aims, objectives, and hypotheses of this study 
The aim of this study is to investigate the effects of a novel hot air-drying method on the quality 
of seaweed for foods. For this purpose, the physical and chemical quality of Ulva spp. and Fucus 
vesiculosus will be analysed. The seaweeds will be dried either fresh, or after freezing and 
thawing. The dried samples will be provided by Hansen and Lindstrøm and will be compared with 
freeze dried Ulva spp. and Fucus vesiculosus. The chemical parameters to be analysed are 
moisture, ash, and protein content, amino acids profile, pigment composition, total polyphenolic 
content, and antioxidant potential, which will be analysed by three different assays. Physical 
parameters to be analysed are the drying over time, water and oil holding capacity, colour 
changes and water activity (aw).  
==PAGE:22==
P a g e  | 22 
 
 
 
• H1: It is hypothesised that the drying curve of both seaweeds will follow a sigmoid drying 
curve. 
As the air-drying temperature is 52 oC and it is being compared to freeze dried samples, the 
following hypotheses are drawn based on the facts: 
• H2: After air-drying fresh samples, its water and oil holding capacity, protein content, 
amino acid profile and ash composition should be similar to freeze dried samples, as these 
compounds remain stable at 52 oC.  
• H3: It is expected that heat sensitive compounds like phenolics and pigments will 
decrease after air-drying and air-dried fresh samples will have a lower amount of these 
compounds when compared to freeze dried samples.  
• H4: Due to lower phenolic and pigment composition, the antioxidant activity is expected 
to decrease in hot air-dried fresh samples when compared to freeze dried samples.  
After freezing the samples, it is assumed that intracellular water released during the thawing 
process will carry hydrophilic compounds, and these compounds will be lower, when these 
samples will be air-dried. Based on this assumption, it is hypothesized that: 
• H5: The pigment composition, total phenolic content, amino acids profile and antioxidant 
activity of frozen hot air-dried samples will be significantly decreased from fresh air-dried 
and freeze-dried samples.  
• H6: Due to the same time and temperature during drying, fresh and frozen air-dried 
samples will have the same water holding capacity, oil holding capacity, moisture content 
and aw.

2. Experimental methods 
2.1 Sample preparation  
 
Figure 7: Sample preparation method for Ulva spp. and F. vesiculosus 
The brown seaweed Fucus vesiculosus was harvested in Juelsminde, Denmark (N55°70' E10°03’) 
by the company Hansen and Lindstrøm and the green seaweed Ulva spp. was harvested in Nivå, 
Denmark (N55°92' E12°52’) in early October. After harvesting, the seaweeds were divided into 3 
batches of 3 kg each. 9 kg seaweed was transported to Hansen and Lindstrøm for hot air drying.  
2.1.1 Air drying 
24 hours after harvesting Ulva spp., 3 kg of the seaweed was air dried while 3 kg was frozen to -
40oC, thawed and then dried in hot air for 50 mins. 3 kg of Ulva spp. was stored at 2oC for 24 
hours after harvesting, vacuum sealed and then stored in -40oC for 72 hours until freeze drying.  
3 kg of F. vesiculosus was directly dried for 50 mins while 3 kg was frozen, thawed for 24 hours 
and then dried for 50 mins.  3 kg of frozen F. vesiculosus was transported to DTU for freeze drying 
and was stored in -40oC until freeze drying.  
2.1.2 Freeze-drying  
After 72 hours of storage in -40oC, the seaweed was placed on aluminium trays and the weight 
was noted. Freeze drying was carried out for 24-30 hours. After approximately every hour the 
freeze drier was turned off and the seaweed was weighed to determine water evaporation.

P a g e  | 24 
 
 
 
However, no weight measurements were carried out at night and weekends. The mass loss by 
the evaporation is calculated from the measured mass before and after drying. 
All dryings were performed in triplicates to obtain statistically different samples. The three 
batches of seaweeds obtained after the drying process are: 
• Freeze dried Ulva spp. and F. vesiculosus (FDU and FDF, respectively) 
• Fresh Ulva spp. and fresh F. vesiculosus air-dried (FRU and FRF, respectively) 
• Ulva spp. and F. vesiculosus air-dried after freezing (FZU and FZF, respectively) 
In total there were 18 samples, 9 samples per seaweed.  
10 g of seaweed was stored whole for microstructure analysis while the rest was grinded to a fine 
powder. For chemical analysis, the powdered seaweed was stored in -20oC while for physical 
analysis the seaweed was stored in a cool and dark place at ambient temperature. 
2.2 Colour profile 
Colour of the dried seaweed was determined using a chroma-meter (Minolta CR-200, Minolta 
Camera Co. Ltd, Japan) and expressed in the CIELAB colour space. The chroma-meter was based 
in the Illuminant D65 standard illumination in which L* values are on a scale of black (0) to white 
(100), a* values are on a scale of green (-163) and red (+126.6) and b* values are on a scale of 
blue (-127.7) to yellow (+145.3) (Buckley & Giorgianni, 2016) 
2 g of dry sample powder was placed in a petri dish on a dark surface and the colour profile was 
generated using the chroma-meter. Each sample was analysed 3 times. As the colour profile of 
seaweeds before drying was not analysed, change in the colour profile (ΔE) could not be 
calculated.  
2.3 Water activity  
The water activity (aw) meter was first calibrated with solutions having aw of 0.45 and 0.9. Then 2 
g of powdered sample (n=3) was taken in a plastic container and placed in the water activity 
meter for a reading. Aw is a dimensionless value and has no unit.

was carried out at 3000 g for 20 minutes. The water/oil was decanted out and the tubes were 
weighed again. The difference of the weight of the tubes before and after the procedure was 
taken as the amount of wet sample in the tubes. Results were calculated using the formula for 
WHC.

a vortex mixer for 30 seconds (as above) and heated by pacing in a water bath for 1 hour at 80oC.

2.6 Total amino acid analysis 
To determine total AAs acid hydrolysis was carried out followed by derivatization and

2.6.1 Acid hydrolysis:

2.9 Total phenolic content  
Phenolics in plant extracts is detected Folin-Ciocalteu reagent (FC reagent) which initiates redox 
reactions of the phenolic compounds. The reaction forms a blue complex and can be detected by 
colorimetry, like ultraviolet-visible (UV-VIS) spectrophotometry which determines the total 
concentration of phenolic hydroxyl groups between 725 nm and 765 nm. The result is expressed 
in μg of gallic acid equivalents (GAE) per mL or mg of extract. The FC reagent is rapidly 
decomposed under alkaline conditions, which is why acid is added to the solution to lower the 
pH. Excess reagent precipitates when sodium carbonate is added which gives high turbidity and 
makes spectrophotometry possible.

evaporation rate was obtained by turning off the freeze drier and measuring the mass of

3.3.1 Moisture and ash content 
Moisture content and ash was determined for Ulva spp. and F. vesiculosus and presented in 
figures 13 and 14, respectively. The drying process of foodstuffs occur in the falling rate period 
(Moreira et al., 2017) when there is an internal mass transfer of moisture to the surface. This can 
be seen in section 3.1. The droplet temperature then increases until the heat and mass transfer 
rates are in equilibrium (Amador & Martin de Juan, 2016). The age, size and environmental 
conditions affect the morphology of seaweeds and influence the moisture and ash content 
(Clendenning, 1962).  
The results show that there is no difference in the moisture content of FDU, FRU and FZU (One-
way ANOVA, df = 7, p > 0.05) but there is a significant difference in the ash content of Ulva spp. 
(One-way ANOVA, df = 7, p < 0.05, Tukey’s HSD) and F. vesiculosus (One-way ANOVA, df = 8, p < 
0.05, Tukey’s HSD). The decrease was found between freeze-dried and air-dried samples but not 
between the air-dried samples. This implies that air-drying fresh or frozen Ulva spp. and F. 
vesiculosus had no effect on the ash content. In addition, air-drying fresh and frozen samples 
resulted in the statistically same moisture content, indicating storage method had no effect on 
the final moisture content by air-drying.

A moisture content of ≤ 20% in dried foodstuffs is below the moisture content necessary for food 
spoilage micro-organisms to form colonies (Sappati et al., 2019) suggesting that the drying 
method employed by Hansen and Lindstrøm would result in a safe shelf-life of dried foodstuffs. 
Generally, freeze-drying results in a lower moisture content than conventional drying methods. 
Freeze-drying of pre-dried squash achieved a moisture content of 19% (d.w.) (Donsì et al., 1998) 
while freeze-drying raisins, currents and figs resulted in a moisture content of 14, 17.3 and 9.7% 
(d.w.), respectively (L. T. Lim et al., 1995). This implies that freeze dried Ulva spp. and F. 
vesiculosus in this study resulted in a moisture content not different from other studies.  
Gracilaria gracilis and Osmundea pinnatifida oven dried at 60oC had a moisture content of 8% 
and 11.8% (d.w.), respectively (Rodrigues et al., 2015). Several seaweeds collected from the 
northwestern Spanish coast and air-dried at 50oC had a moisture content between 6.64% and 
9.86% (d.w.) (Gómez-Ordóñez et al., 2010). However, the moisture content of air-dried Ulva spp. 
and F. vesiculosus in this study was approximately 11% (d.w.) indicating the air-drying method 
employed for this study resulted in a slightly higher moisture content.  
Seaweeds generally have high mineral content. Wild samples of U. rigida collected from Tunisia 
contained 21 - 26% ash (d.w.) (Frikha, 2011) while Ulva spp. collected from Guayacan beach, Chile 
in April had an ash content of 22% (d.w.) (Uribe et al., 2019). Furthermore, F. vesiculosus collected 
from the Atlantic Ocean in August and September had an ash content of approximately 20% 
(d.w.) (Lorenzo et al., 2017) while the same species collected from the Baltic Sea fluctuates in ash 
content from 14% (d.w.) to 21% (d.w.) throughout the year (Truus et al., 2001). These studies 
have a slightly different harvesting time (early Oct) of Ulva spp. and F. vesiculosus in this study.  
The ash content in this study indicates that these seaweeds harvested in early autumn do not 
differ in their ash content than the same seaweeds harvested in late summers. A literature 
comparison also indicates that F. vesiculosus and Ulva spp. generally have a lower ash content 
that other seaweed species like Ascophyllum nodosum (30% d.w.) and Bifurcaria bifurcate (31% 
d.w.) (Lorenzo et al., 2017).

polyphenol levels in both Ulva spp. and F. vesiculosus are the same after freeze drying and air 
drying. It is possible that phloroglucinol equivalent (PGE) as a standard will yield different results.  
Fucus vesiculosus is known for their high TPC and antioxidant content and much research has 
been done to replace artificial antioxidants in foods (Honold et al., 2016; Jacobsen et al., 2019). 
Based on the TPC, antioxidant analyses, to test the antioxidant quality, were carried out on F. 
vesiculosus and not on Ulva spp., as the latter had low TPC and was not considered enough for 
antioxidant assays.

3.3.5 Antioxidant assays 
Due to low quantity of TPC in Ulva spp. (15 ± 4 mg GAE/g d.w.) as compared to F. vesiculosus (160 
± 25 mg GAE/g d.w.), antioxidant (AO) assays were only performed on F. vesiculosus.  
DPPH radical scavenging activity 
DPPH radical scavenging activity (RSA) assays work on the principle of transfer of electrons, as 
explained by figure 26. DPPH scavenging activity is measured in the concentration of antioxidant 
required to cause 50% loss in DPPH activity and is expressed as InC50 value. Higher phenolic 
content leads to lower µg / mL needed to achieve 50% inhibition (Wang et al., 2012). Fucoxanthin 
is the primary compound in F. vesiculosus responsible for DPPH activity, along with phloroglucinol 
(Fariman et al., 2016).

4 Conclusion 
Higher temperatures during drying often results in depleted antioxidant content (Badmus et al., 
2019), however, due to no statistical difference in any antioxidant assay, no conclusion can be 
drawn about which F. vesiculosus sample had the highest antioxidant activity and content. 
Nevertheless, it can be inferred that air-drying F. vesiculosus did not change the antioxidant 
content in the seaweed when compared to freeze drying.  
4.1 The robustness of the hypothesis 
Based on the analyses conducted the hypotheses are revisited to check their robustness: 
• 
 H1: It hypothesised that the drying curve of both seaweeds will follow a sigmoid drying 
curve. 
• The falling rate graph (figure 9) is the ratio of mass during drying to initial mass, and is 
based on water evaporation from the samples, which follows a sigmoid curve as shown 
in figure 2.  
• H2: After air-drying fresh samples, its water and oil holding capacity, protein content, 
amino acid profile and ash composition should be similar to freeze dried samples, as these 
compounds remain stable at 52 oC. 
==PAGE:61==
P a g e  | 61 
 
 
 
➔ The total protein content, amino acid profile and ash decreases in F. vesiculosus. The 
total protein content and amino acid profile is similar while ash decreases in Ulva spp.  
➔ Water holding capacity shows no difference in both seaweeds while oil holding 
capacity decreases in F. vesiculosus but not in Ulva spp.  
• H3: It is expected that heat sensitive compounds like phenolics and pigments will decrease 
after air-drying and that air-dried fresh samples will have a lower amount of these 
compounds when compared to freeze dried samples.  
➔ Pigments and phenolic compounds remain same for both seaweeds.  
• H4: Due to lower phenolic and pigment composition, the antioxidant activity is expected 
to decrease in hot air-dried fresh samples when compared to freeze dried samples.  
➔ The antioxidation capacity is same between all F. vesiculosus samples.   
• H5: The pigment composition, phenolic compounds, amino acids profile, and antioxidant 
activity of frozen hot air-dried samples will significantly decrease compared to fresh air-
dried and freeze-dried samples.  
➔ Pigments decrease for frozen Ulva spp. Phenolics decrease in frozen air-dried samples 
compared to fresh air-dried F. vesiculosus.  
➔ Total proteins and amino acid profile decrease for frozen air-dried F. vesiculosus but 
not for frozen air-dried Ulva spp.  
➔ The antioxidant activity is same between all F. vesiculosus samples.  
• H6: Due to the same time and temperature during drying, fresh and frozen air-dried 
samples will have the same water holding capacity, oil holding capacity, moisture content 
and aw.  
➔ Water holding capacity, oil holding capacity, moisture content and ash is similar in F. 
vesiculosus air-dried samples while aw is lower for frozen air-dried samples.  
➔ Oil holding capacity, moisture, and aw is similar in Ulva spp. while water holding 
capacity decreases in frozen air-dried samples.

4.2 Future perspectives 
The addition of seaweeds in processed meats has shown to improve taste, texture, emulsion 
stability, water, and oil holding capacity and increase mineral and dietary fiber content. Promising 
examples of Himanthalia elongate powder and Lamina japonica has shown to increase 
nutritional and organoleptic qualities of sausages (H. W. Kim et al., 2010; López-López et al., 
2009). Seaweed has been used to extend the shelf life of food products (Roohinejad et al., 2017). 
Ulva spp. can be added in burgers (Kumarathunga et al., 2016) or can be used in feed for a wide 
range of fish species (Abdel-Warith et al., 2016). Aqueous extracts of Ulva lactuca can be used 
for chemical preservation of canned salmon (Jaime Ortiz et al., 2014).  
Fucus vesiculosus has high antioxidant potential and its extracts have shown to inhibit lipid 
oxidation in minced cod (Jónsdóttir et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2010). Synthetic additives and 
preservatives in food products can be replaced by phlorotannins present in F. vesiculosus. 
Fucoidans in F. vesiculosus have shown strong inhibition towards α-glycosidase and F. vesiculosus 
extracts can be instrumental to reduce the assimilable caloric content of foods without changing 
the recipe (K. T. Kim et al., 2014).
