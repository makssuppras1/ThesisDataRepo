Abstract
In order to improve solar photovoltaic (PV) diagnosis and reduce maintenance costs, mod-
elling and understanding the failures of PV modules is crucial. Solar cell cracks is one of
the main failures a PV module can undergo. Solar cell cracks have a strong effect on the
performance of modules on the field since they reduce the power output.
This project aims to investigate the effects solar cell cracks have on the electrical per-
formance of PV modules. The evolution of cracks and how they affect the electrical and
diode model parameters of cells and modules was studied. By understanding and mod-
elling the cell cracks, power loss models, aiming to simulate the power loss of cracked cells
and modules from electroluminescence (EL) images, can be derived.
For characterizing solar cell cracks, a combination of 9-cell and single-cell mini-modules
of monocrystalline and multicrystalline silicon were manufactured, where all cells can be
accessed, allowing a full characterization on both cell and module level.
These mini-
modules were characterized over several degradation stages showing different types, sizes
and positions of cracks.
Electrical characterization was done using light and dark I-V curves. Diode model param-
eters of the cells were studied by applying several methods, including curve fitting to the
equivalent diode model. Using EL images, cracks were characterized by calculating the
inactive cell area and differentiating between B and C type cracks.
Electrical characterization of solar cell cracks showed a consistent decrease in maximum
power point current where B cracks were present. C cracks caused a significant decrease
in short circuit current, which increases even more when both B and C cracks appear
combined. Curve fitting light and dark I-V curves to the equivalent circuit of the cells
showed limitations when modelling cracked cells. The evolution of the diode model pa-
rameters was not consistent as the models ceased to work for cells showing cracks. A
final qualitative analysis using Suns-Voc curves of the cells proved to be very useful for
the understanding of the evolution of both electrical and diode model parameters over
degradation stages with increase in cell cracks.
By using EL image characterization, state-of-the-art series resistance mapping models have
been applied with the objective to use them in a power loss simulation model for cracked
solar cells. The series resistance mapping model and power simulation proposed by Kropp
proved to be very accurate identifying cracks and simulating power loss. A final power
prediction using a data driven approach has been proposed. Using the characterization
of the manufactured mini-modules, a very promising model was built which only requires
EL image parameters as input.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
1.1
Context
The world energy sector is improving year after year.
Installed capacity of renewable
energies is increasing at a high rate, for which solar photvoltaic (PV) energy is one of
the main contributors [1]. Solar PV is an important power generator in sectors such as
transport, buildings and electricity industry.
Solar PV has been increasing its installed capacity year after year, reaching 700GW in
2020, and it is expected to raise six times the current capacity by 2030 [2]. Furthermore,
costs reduction of the most well know photovoltaic silicon technologies suggest that there
are still possibilities of improvement on the global stage of solar PV energy.
Over the last decade, commercial PV costs have been decreasing greatly, specially the price
trend of crystalline Silicon wafers [3]. Still, operational and maintenance costs (O&M),
installation and the modules themselves add up to a significant cost. Figure 1.1 shows the
projected evolution of PV systems costs in the next decade. In 2021, the modules and
labour costs add up to 51% of the total systems costs in installations below 10MW. This
accounts for a high percentage of the total costs, and even though future perspectives
show trends in cost reduction, modules, installation and labour costs will still hold an
important share of the PV systems cost breakdown.
Figure 1.1: Cost elements of PV systems future perspective. Source [3]

In order to avoid extra costs and extend the lifetime of modules, installation and mainte-
nance and investing in PV reliability plays a very important role. Frequent inspections of
PV installations can help locating failures in the installation preventing future problems.
1.2
Background
Solar cells and modules can undergo different types of failures during their lifetime. Figure
1.2 lists the frequency of the main degradation types observed in modules working on the
field. Over the whole study time and in the most recent study sample of 10 years, cell
cracks appeared to be the 5th most common degradation [4].
Figure 1.2: Percentage of failures affecting crystalline-Si modules analyzed in [4], with
permission to reproduce.
Failures on PV modules, where cell cracks are included, can sometimes remain unnoticed.
Weather conditions such as strong winds, hail and snow, as well as mechanical stress and
hits sustained during the transport and installation of PV modules are the main drivers
of PV cells cracks [5].
Electroluminescence (EL) was introduced by Fuyuki back in 2005 [6], since then, it has
become the main asset for detecting and characterizing cell cracks. Since its introduction,
EL imaging has helped to extend research on characterization methods for cell cracks.
Using EL imaging, cell cracks are easy to visualize and analyze qualitatively, however,
using images alone, it is diﬀicult to assess the impact they have on cells and modules
power [7]. This fact is a big limitation for the inspection of modules in PV installations.
General PV diagnostic can be performed by means of electrical characterization of the
modules, using current-voltage (I-V) curves to detect failures problems like shading in the
field and failures like potential induced degradation (PID) and series resistance increase
[8, 9, 10]. Furthermore, image diagnostic using Infrared PV imaging (IR) is widely applied
nowadays. EL imaging though, allows to detect cell cracks much more accurately than
IR, being able to identify crack position, location and severity.
Prior to installation, it is important to detect cell cracks because they can evolve and cause
power loss, cell reverse biasing and hotspots over time [11], [12]. So even if the power loss
is not critical initially, their evolution over time can have serious consequences causing
more severe failures and power loss mechanisms that would reduce the power generation
and end the module’s lifetime prematurely. For that reason, it is important to investigate
how much solar cell crack area, extracted from EL images, correlates with increase in series

resistance, so that the severity and future energy loss on the system can be estimated and
the modules can be replaced if necessary [11, 13].
Figure 1.3 shows the degradation of the nominal power drop caused by cell cracks and
other PV failures and how they evolve over the module lifetime.
Figure 1.3: Failure effect on nominal power of PV modules [14]
Recent research on the topics addressed in this work start with cell crack characterization,
where the effect cell cracks have on electrical and diode model parameters of the cells
is of special importance. Understanding what happens to the cells when cracks appear,
combined with automatic detection and classification of cracks and failures algorithms,
can lead towards good power prediction models.
Solar cell cracks have been studied and classified in different types, mainly being micro-
cracks (type A), which do not cause major reductions in EL intensity but can be identified
in high current bias EL images; partial disconnections (type B) which at low current bias
do not cause a complete loss in EL intensity; and total electrical disconnections (type C),
which can be differentiated from type B cracks in low bias EL images, where they appear
as a dark region [11, 15]. The different sizes, orientations and severity of cell cracks have
proven to cause degradation of electrical and diode model parameters eventually leading to
power loss [16, 17]. Also, cell cracks cause series resistance increase based on the distance
of the crack from the busbars, and full cell disconnections have proved to decrease the
current output of the cell [18, 19]. Electrical characterization and diode model parameters
of cracked cells have been studied in [20], by modelling the change in the shape of I-V
curves. Furthermore, the evolution of these cracks over degradation stages was approached
using light and dark I-V measurements over several stages of degradation [21], and in depth
analysis on cell level of cracked PERC and Al-BSF cells in mini-modules was analyzed by
Whitaker [22]. Very recently, the effect of crack distribution and uniformity of cracks in
cell performance parameters was analyzed by Dhimish [5, 23].
Cell crack quantification was investigated recently [24] proposing a way to calculate a per-
centage of cracked area differentiating and quantifying B and C crack percentage. Classi-
fication and detection of crack has been attempted using EL images statistical parameters
[25, 26]. Moreover, recent research focuses on the application of advanced machine learn-
ing algorithms in combination with EL images and EL images statistical parameters to
detect and quantify cracks of modules [27, 28, 29].
Research on voltage distributions of cells [30, 31] using EL images has allowed to create se-
ries resistance (Rs) maps making possible to quantify the state of the cell interconnections,

cracks and finger failures [32, 33]. These maps opened a possibility to qualitatively analyze
the regions with increased series resistance aiming towards making power simulations.
Power prediction of modules has been investigated using EL images and electrical cell pa-

in an attempt to diagnose PV modules in a contactless manner, deep learning algorithms
are being applied showing exceptional results in power prediction using EL measurements

1.3
Project objectives
The objective of this work is to investigate the effect solar cell cracks have on electrical
and diode model parameters of crystalline silicon cells and how they affect the cells and
modules performance. Crack features such as cracked area will be analyzed and used to
find relationships with the power loss and performance parameters on both a module and
cell level.
A correct understanding and modelling of the evolution of electrical and diode parameters
of cells over mechanical degradation is crucial for understanding how power loss correlates
to parameters directly extracted from EL images alone, therefore, improving the diagnosis
of PV installations. That is why, state-of-the-art methods used to create series resistance
maps from EL images will be tested and evaluated. Using these maps, a power loss predic-
tion using EL images will be attempted. Furthermore, a data driven power loss prediction
approach combining all electrical, diode and EL image parameters of the analyzed cells in
the project will be developed.
This thesis objectives can be summarized in the next four points:
• Investigate the effect of solar cell cracks on the electrical and diode model parameters
of PV cells and modules.
• Test the state-of-the-art series resistance mapping models. Find and compare their
limitations and find which one is the most adequate to simulate power.
• Apply an analytical power simulation of the cells by using the series resistance maps.
• Investigate and develop a novel data driven approach that can predict power output
by just using EL image characterization.

2.1
Experimental methodology and characterization process
In order to research the project objectives, an experimental methodology has been devel-
oped.
Firstly, the mini-modules required for the work were manufactured. A total of two custom
9-cell mini-modules (one monocrystalline Silicon and one multicrystalline Silicon) and 12
single-cell mini-modules (7 monocrystalline Silicon and 5 multicrystalline Silicon) were
prepared for the analysis.
In the field, EL image characterization allows to visualize cracks at module and cell level,
however, cell level electrical characterization, such as light and dark I-V curves is not
possible. In this work, mini-modules were manufactured in order to access the individual
cells, allowing to investigate how each cell affects the final module parameters and how
the electrical parameters of both cells and module change due to cell crack degradation.
The characterization procedure consisted on light I-V recording by flash testing at Stan-
dard Test Conditions (STC, 1000 W/m2, 25 ℃, AM 1.5), as well as 600 and 200 W/m2,
using a class AAA Sun simulator EcoSun 10L REV E. Indoor EL imaging was done using
a Nikon D3500 24.2 MP DSLR camera, at different current bias levels (from 0% to 100%
Isc in 5% Isc steps), furthermore, background images without illumination were taken.
For the different series resistance mapping models, different EL images were required (dif-
ferent exposure times, current bias or terminal voltage), the specific types of recorded EL
images will be discussed in the corresponding sections. Moreover, dark I-V curves of all
cells were acquired.
After an initial characterization of the cells and modules, the mini-modules were subject
to one round of mechanical stress until cell cracks appeared, then the power loss was
measured and a new round of characterization was done. This procedure was repeated
two times, finishing with an initial stage and 2 degradation stages fully characterized.
9-cell mini-modules were mechanically stressed by applying weight on top while checking
live, using EL with an InGaAs camera, that cell cracks were appearing. Singe-cell mini-
modules cracks were induced hitting the cell with a ruler using a device developed in a
guillotine style (see Figure A.1 in Appendix) and then, applying stress and vibrations on
the edges of the cell until the cracks opened and B and C cracks appeared.
It is important to note that, in this project, the same degradation stage is not comparable
among different cells. The force and hits applied between degradation stages is different for
all cells, therefore, cracks are generated with different sizes, shapes and their effect on the
cells performance will be different. The intention was to create a pool of different cracks

2.7
EL images processing
Raw EL images taken at the lab, generally require some post processing before working
with them. The different series resistance mapping methods applied in Chapter 4, require
each one their own methodology and equations, however, in this section, the main image
processing applied to all images will be described.
The main steps followed when taking EL images were image subtraction, cropping, and
conversion to grayscale and normalization. For this process, Matlab’s image processing
toolbox was used.
For each exposure time, a background EL image with no illumination was also recorded.
Image subtraction was applied using the background images in order to eliminate the
noise in the images. Also, in order to work only with the required cell, a crop was done
manually. This is the reason why the EL setup needed to be as stable as possible, in
order to minimize errors in this cell cropping process. Since the design of the EL image
acquisition setup allowed for stable, repeatable EL images, there was no need to correct
the images perspective.
Images are often represented by pixel values in the range 0 to 255, then, the EL images
had to be converted to grayscale and normalized to a matrix of values ranging from 0 to
1 representing the normalized EL intensity level. This normalization was done by diving
the pixel value by the maximum pixel value in the image.
For all cells analyzed in this work, EL images were used to obtain relevant histogram
parameters and processed to quantify the cell cracks observed over degradation stages,
these two processes will be further explained in Chapter 3.

3.1
Characterization approach
Cell cracks will be characterized in this chapter using the manufactured mini-modules.
The characterization procedure followed in this chapter, as explained previously will consist
of cracking the mini-modules over several stages of mechanical stress, where cells showing
similar types and sizes of cracks can be compared. Changes in the electrical parameters of
the module and cells will be studied using light and dark I-V characterization. Solar cell
cracks properties, such as area, type of crack and severity [24, 43] will be investigated and
used to characterize cracks from EL images alone. Histogram parameters of the EL images
will be analyzed in the characterization process in order to find what relevant information
EL images alone can give. Furthermore, diode model curve fitting and analysis of the light
and dark I-V characteristics will be performed to investigate the cell diode parameters by
using a model based on light I-V comparison and curve fitting models.
The main analysis has been done on the 9-cell mini-modules due to the fact that module
parameters can also be investigated.
A single cell mini-module will be fully analyzed and used as validation of the main find-
ings. This mini-module has been stressed mechanically three times, aiming to analyze
independently each type of crack after each degradation. Firstly, micro-cracks, secondly,
B cracks and finally C cracks. Furthermore, this cell was also characterized on all degrada-
tion stages at multiple irradiance levels, allowing to analyze the evolution of the Suns-Voc
curve over the degradation stages.

3.2
Cracked area and crack type quantification
Using the mini-modules EL images, and applying the method introduced in [24], cracks
in solar cells can be quantified by using thresholding of the grayscale EL image.
This quantification method has been applied to both 9-cell mini-modules and single-cell
mini-modules. Using the EL images recorded at different Isc bias levels, by applying a
threshold above the noise level on a low bias EL image, B and C cracks can also be
quantified and differentiated from the whole cell area loss.
The process followed was firstly, using a EL image of the cell in its initial state, to find a
threshold in order to calculate the baseline low EL intensity (LELbaseline). In the initial
state, low EL intensity regions are the background and busbars, as shown in Figure 3.3a.
Using Otsu’s method [44] an initial threshold is found, in the example given in the Figure,
a threshold of 0.25 was used.
For quantifying the whole area loss of the cell in a damaged stage, using a high bias EL
image, and applying the initial threshold, the image can be segmented finding a new low
EL level (LEL) intensity region. In Figure 3.3b a damaged state EL image of the same cell
is segmented. The percentage of inactive area, or combination between B and C cracks is
given by equation (3.1):

3.8
Suns-Voc and qualitative curve analysis
Results from curve fitting showed unusual development of Rs on cracked cells, that is
why, in order to further investigate the evolution of this parameters, Suns-Voc curves were
measured.
Suns-Voc is a characteristic curve of PV cells where the influence of Rs is not present [49].
This curve is built by taking several irradiance light I-V measurements of a cell, then,
the application of the diode model equations at Voc eliminates the current and Rs term,
leaving an expression where plotting together the measured irradiance in Suns and Voc
gives a curve similar to the I-V characteristic but without series resistance effects.
By comparing this curve to the measured I-V curves one can evaluate whether the power
loss effects due to cracks in a cell are due to series resistance or another power loss mech-
anism.
This characteristic curve could also be fitted to the diode model equations. This was tested
and similar issues as in the two previous curve fits were found, unnatural development of

==PAGE:53==
certain model parameters made this fitting of little use. Instead, a qualitative analysis by
curve comparison is proposed. This way, Bowden’s and curve fitting parameters can be
validated.
In this section, Suns-Voc curve was only measured in stage s2 of the 9-cell mini-modules
cells.

3.26b shows the difference between the light I-V and Suns-Voc of cell A1 of the
mono-Si mini-module. Both curves were shifted to current density units (A/cm2) for a
proper comparison. The gap between both curves represents the effect of Rs on the cell
performance.
Suns-Voc analysis contradicts the result from curve fitting to the single diode model, where
a decrease in Rs was observed, since there is a clear effect of series resistance in this cell
power loss.
Single diode model fitting indicated a decrease in Rsh and a big increase in J0. Between
stages s0 and s1, dark I-V (Figure3.26a) shift indicates a variation in shunt resistance,
moreover, the light I-V (Figure 3.13) evolution in the low voltage region supports this
behaviour.
Stages s1 and s2 on the other hand, do not show a big change in dark I-V even though C
cracks increase significantly, the light I-V characteristic evolution shows a decrease in Isc,
this indicates that some series and shunting effects are present but not reflected by the
curve fitting methods.
Cell B3 of the multi-Si mini-module shows similar results as cell A1. Figure 3.27b shows
the Suns-Voc curve, which indicates again series resistance losses. Shifting on the dark
I-V (Figure 3.25) and changes in the low voltage region light I-V (Figure 3.27a) denote
shunting losses too.
Bowden method estimated a large Rsh decrease over degradation in this cell due to the
decrease in Isc because of the C cracks. Between stage s1 and s2 there were only more B
cracks induced in this cell, which had an effect on Rs, reducing not only Impp, but also
Isc. The combination of a large amount of B and C cracks can have this mixed effect on
the cell parameters. This is modelled by Bowden as a big shunting loss even when dark
I-V shape did not change.
In stage s2, cells A1 and B3 show a similar B and C crack percentage with 28.15 and 32.34%
respectively. The Suns-Voc curves of both cells show a similar effect when compared to the
light I-V characteristic, however, Bowden analysis indicated that one cell was affected by
Rs and the other one by Rsh. Between stage s1 and s2, either cell presented a big change
in the dark I-V shape, even when, cell A1 showed a increase in C cracks and cell B3 on B
cracks mainly [35].

Discussion
In this chapter a full process of characterization was applied to all the mini-modules under
study.
Cracks were characterized by calculating the inactive area percentage differentiating be-
tween B and C cracks using EL images. This procedure proved to be very valuable for
evaluating the electrical and diode model parameters, since a strong relationship between
crack percentage and power loss was observed.
Histogram parameters of the EL images also proved to be very valuable when charac-
terizing cracks.
From the point of view of fault detection and power prediction these
parameters propose a very interesting approach, because no electrical characterization
would be required.
On the electrical characterization of cracked cells, very interesting results were found.
Cracks characterized with C type caused mainly changes on the I-V curve on Isc as well
as Impp when combined with B cracks. B cracks alone were identified as losses related to
Impp mainly. These results were found and validated in multiple cells analyzed throughout
the project.
Characterization of solar cell cracks and methods for power loss estimation

==PAGE:59==
Regarding diode model parameters, out of the investigated methods, Bowden’s method
proved to be the most robust in terms of quantifying Rs and Rsh over several crack stages.
Curve fitting to either single or double diode model found a limitation calculating the
series resistance over degradation. It has been proved that the models are not valid once
the cells are not at initial state any more. When the whole dataset was analyzed, series
and shunt resistance showed very clear trends of evolution over B and C cracks, however,
power loss mechanisms do not seem to be related to a specific type of crack, since cells with
similar crack percentage showed different influence of series and shunt resistance. Since
this analysis was completed using only crack percentage differentiation, further research
could investigate if crack orientation has an influence on the evolution of these parameters.
The proposed Suns-Voc, light and dark I-V curve qualitative analysis showed consistent re-
sults, although this analysis can be inconvenient due to the large amount of measurements
required to build the Suns-Voc curve. This analysis proved to be helpful to validate Bow-
den’s calculations. Suns-Voc taken at all degradation stages can very easily corroborate
which power loss mechanism is the main driver in a cell.
The attempt to induce cracks progressively, in order to analyze the isolated effect of each
type of crack failed due to inability to consistently and effectively create the desired cracks.
The methodology followed to induced cracks does not allow for such precise evolution of
cracks, therefore, this analysis could be improved an validated if a more accurate method
of inducing cracks was achieved.

4.1
Introduction to Rs mapping and power loss models
Over the years, multiple analytical models have been developed in order to estimate the
series resistance of the PV cells from EL images. In the following sections, some of these
models will be applied and evaluated.
The approach has been to follow a chronological order of the methods, starting with Hinken
model for series resistance mapping, Haunshild model and finally, the most recent Kropp
model which apart from the Rs mapping model proposes a way of simulating the power
of a module from the series resistance map. A method for evaluating the severity of cell
cracks from EL images at different exposure times was also tested. After analyzing the
results of Kropp’s power simulation for all mini-modules, a data driven approach will be
developed using linear regression to a dataset created with all the power measurements
of the mini-modules of the project. The intention of this model is to evaluate if a power
prediction using only EL images would be feasible. Finally, both Kropp simulation and
the linear regression model results on the mini-modules will be compared.
For the following sections, single-cell mini-modules were used to test and validate the
models. Hinken and Haunschild model for Rs mapping as well as the severity of cracks
algorithm were tested on single cell mini-modules, while Kropp model was applied to all the
single-cell mini-modules and also the 9-cell mini-modules due to the outstanding results.
Two single-cell mini-modules have been selected for the visualization of results in the
following sections, one mono-Si and one multi-Si. Both mini-modules were mechanically
stressed two times, showing a combination of B and C cracks, which made them perfect
for evaluating the models. Figure 4.1 shows the EL images of stages s0, s1 and s2 of both
mini-modules imaged at 100% Isc bias.

4.5.2
Power simulation
Once the series resistance map is obtained, the module power is simulated. Figure 4.13
presents the methodology followed to simulate cell power using Kropp’s method.
Firstly, the Rs map is segmented into ten Rs parts for each cell.
For each segment,
its corresponding relative area share of the cell is calculated (Asegment/Atotal). With each
segmented section of the cell Rs, a single diode model is simulated using the corresponding
Rs value. The rest of the diode parameters (Rsh, J0, nid and Jph) are estimated from curve
fitting to the single diode model the light I-V at STC of the cell at its initial stage, without
any cracks. The final cell power comes from the parallel connection of all simulated single
diode models simulated at their correspondent Rs segment value and the rest of the diode
parameters. Note that each segment simulation needs to be scaled relative to the area
contribution of each segment to the cell.

parameters estimated initially did not represent properly the shape of the I-V curve.

Recent research has shown great progress on fault detection and power loss prediction for

Summary and Conclusions
5.1
Conclusions
Throughout this project, solar cell cracks have been studied and characterized. An analysis
of how the evolution of cracks in PV cells affect their electrical and diode parameters of
cells and modules has been carried out. After understanding the effect of solar cell cracks,
series resistance mapping models have been applied and compared, with the objective
of using them to simulate the power loss. Finally, a data driven approach, using linear
regression has been proposed.
Firstly, 9-cell mini-modules and single-cell mini-modules were manufactured.
In these
mini-modules, individual cells can be accessed. These mini-modules were cracked over
several degradation stages by applying mechanical force.
This procedure was used to
induce cracks and power loss in a controlled manner.
By means of light and dark I-V characterization, the electrical parameters of the mini-
modules over mechanical degradation stages were analyzed. Diode model parameters were
studied using Bowden’s method and curve fitting the light and dark I-V curves to the single
and double equivalent diode model.
EL image characterization was done to all the mini-modules under study in order to quan-
tify the inactive cell area. Furthermore, B and C cracked area percentage was calculated
for all cells. The evolution of B and C crack percentage over degradation stages proved to
be linearly related to the cell power.
Histogram parameter of EL images, such as Mean, STD, Skewness and Kurtosis were ob-
tained and analyzed. These parameters also showed the expected patterns of development
with power loss and proved to be very representative of cell cracks. Mean and Kurtosis
decrease over cell cracking while STD and Skewness increase.
On the electrical parameters, B cracks were found to affect mainly Impp, while C cracks
and the combination between B and C cracks decrease strongly Isc. These results were
found and validated on multiple cells.
Results proved that Bowden’s method is the most robust for characterizing diode model
parameters over degradation stages. Fitting to either the single or double diode model
resulted in contradictory evolution of parameters, where the series resistance decreases
over degradation. Curve fitting fails when cracks appear, therefore, it is only applicable
with cells at initial uncracked state. On the module level, diode model parameters evolved
affected by the influence of the cells. It has been observed that the main power loss drivers
of the cell were also the main power loss drivers of the modules. The magnitude of the
series and shunt resistance estimated for the modules was lower than for the cells.

==PAGE:82==
Final validation using comparison of Suns-Voc, light and dark I-V curves of the cells at
all degradation stages proved to be very valuable. Suns-Voc evolution over degradation
revealed information about the effect of Rs on the cells, which combined with the observa-
tion of light and dark I-V curves helped identify the major power loss mechanisms of the
cells. This qualitative validation was performed in a single-cell mini-module where three
stages of degradation were done, aiming to analyze the effect of micro-cracks, B cracks
and cracks independently on each degradation stage. The desired evolution of cracks was
not achieved, however, the qualitative curve comparison served to validate the results
presented.
By means of EL characterization, several Rs mapping models were tested and compared
in the mini-modules.
Firstly, Hinken model was tested. This model, resulted in good Rs images for the initial
state of the cells, where no cracks or bad contacted regions of the cell were present. As
soon as cracks were observed, the model started to fail since the linearity between UT ϕ′/ϕ
and ϕ′, from which the model is derived, is not maintained. This model also fails to map
multi-Si cell, limiting its performance. Because of the model limitations and the fact that
multiple EL images were required and the computational time of the model was long, this
model was not tested any further.
Haunschild model, proposed a faster way of calculating a series resistance map.
This
model only used two EL images and some cell electrical characterization. Results showed
a good Rs mapping of the cells at initial stage and it was able to recognize bad contacted
regions such as B cracks and finger disconnections as regions with increased Rs. The main
weakness found on this model was that it failed to map total disconnections such as C
cracks.
A crack severity algorithm based on EL images recorded at different exposure times was
tested. This model, showed very good result in terms of crack modelling and recognition,
since all types of cracks were modelled as high severity regions. The main drawback of this
model was the fact that it is not a series resistance map, therefore, it lacks the physical
relationship with cell power.
Finally, the series resistance mapping proposed by Kropp was tested. This model resulted
in very good crack identification. Furthermore, it only required two EL images and initial
cell parameters obtained from diode model curve fitting. Due to the good mapping of
Kropp’s model, all the mini-modules were mapped using this model. After mapping all
the mini-modules, Kropp’s power simulation model was tested.
Kropp’s power simulation of the 9-cell mini-modules proved to be very accurate, showing
small deviations from the measured power. The initial state of the multi-Si mini-module
showed a larger deviation of 19.49%. This deviation was analyzed and found to be due
to a bad estimation of the initial diode model parameters. The rest of the degradation
stages of the 9-cell mini-modules showed a deviation between 0.65% and 6.86%.
Power simulation using Kropp was also applied to the rest of the single-cell mini-modules.
The overall power simulation showed an RMSE error of 0.155. Larger errors were found
for smaller powers, specially for multi-Si mini-modules. No clear relationship was found
between the calculated inactive area and the simulation error. That is why, the increase
in error at lower powers is a consequence of using the diode model parameters from the
undamaged cells. The most important limitation found in this simulation model was the
need of good diode parameters estimation.

==PAGE:83==
Ultimately a data driven power prediction approach was developed using linear regression.
A dataset of all the measured points of power of the mini-modules at all degradation
stages was created. This dataset includes all electrical and diode model characterization
parameters, as well as EL histogram parameters, B and C crack percentage. The dataset
was split in to mono and multi-Si cells and the mono-Si dataset was used to test the
linear regression models. A first model was run using all the dataset parameters as inputs.
This model resulted in a RMSE of 0.007, proving that the characterization methodology
followed in the project uses parameters very well correlated with the power output of
the cells. In an attempt to reduce the number of inputs of the model and simplify the
characterization process, a PCA was run. A second model using the components from the
PCA that were more explicative of the dataset variance was tested. This model showed
worse results in terms of RMSE, with 0.072, also the model still required electrical and
diode characterization parameters, which is inconvenient if the model was to be tested on
the field.
Finally, the linear regression model was tested using as inputs only EL characterization
parameters, with the intention to evaluate a model that in the field would require no
electrical characterization at all. This model showed a RMSE of 0.049. A very promising
result considering the few inputs necessary. Kropp’s model power simulation of the mono-
Si dataset resulted in a RMSE of 0.11, worse than the linear regression. The comparison
between both models however is unfair, since Kropp’s model RMSE is the result of simu-
lating all the mono-Si cells power stages, while the linear regression model only uses 25%
of the data available to test the model.
In conclusion, electrical characterization of solar cell cracks over degradation stages proved
to show consistent results when B and C cracks were identified. The same behaviours were
observed, and the trends were similar for all the mini-modules under study. Bowden’s
method showed the best results in terms of diode parameters evolution, and its validation
by comparison of Suns-Voc, dark and light I-V curves was very robust. B and C crack
quantification proved to be a strong metric to characterize cell cracks. Power simulation
using Rs map and power simulation proposed by Kropp showed outstanding results in
cell and module simulation, where the most important factor to account for is the proper
diode parameter estimation. The final data driven approach, using a linear regression
model trained with just information from EL images is very promising. Even though it
was not tested to predict a full module power, the results showed in cell simulation with
an RMSE of 0.049 is very positive. The good results and easy characterization makes it a
good candidate to be tested on the field, although a more complete dataset and validation
is still required.
5.2
Future work
The methodology followed to induce cracks on single-cell mini-modules can be improved. A
more complete investigation of the electrical and diode parameters could be accomplished
if a controlled crack evolution is achieved. Being able to induce exactly the same crack
on two cells and make them evolve in parallel could reveal clearer trends between cracked
area and electrical parameters.
The direction and position micro-cracks, B and C cracks have not been taken into account
in this work. Further investigation could reveal different trends in electrical and diode
parameters evolution with cracks.
Further research is required on the severity map of cracks.
This model showed very

promising results, being able to identify bad contacted cell regions.
If a link between
severity and series resistance was found, a new power simulation could be done using
Kropp’s method.
Future applications would be to test Kropp model with field EL images and evaluate
its performance outside the lab.
Kropp power simulation model was simplified on its
application for the mini-modules under analysis; however, for commercial modules with
bypass diodes, the full model proposed in the paper would need to be applied. Kropp
also proposed a shunt resistance mapping for modules affected by PID. The combination
of both models and their testing on the field could be interesting for developing a fault
detection and power loss diagnostic tool.
The linear regression model proposed using only EL image parameters as inputs was able
to predict well the cell power, however, the model was trained and tested just with the
cells analyzed in this project. The dataset must be expanded and evaluated again. After
that a final validation using real field data would be of interest. On top of the linear
regression model evaluated in this project, the dataset could also be trained and tested
using more advanced machine learning algorithms.
Further research could also go int the direction of finding extra parameters that could be
extracted from EL images, for example the severity map or other histogram parameters
no discussed in this project.
