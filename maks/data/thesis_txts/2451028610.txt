Abstract 
The City of Knowledge partnership in Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality has evolved tremendously since 
its foundation, however attracting and retaining students is still a big challenge specifically for Lyngby. 
This study aims to carry out a sustainability assessment of the city centre area and explore solution 
scenarios to bring students into the city. Both quantitative and qualitative methods were used for data 
collection: one survey with 153 students, 2 interviews with municipality stakeholders, 4 with students 
along with numerous email correspondences with other stakeholders. An indicator framework was 
created, which helped in comparing and assessing the sustainability of the different solution scenarios. 
The result of this study is the implementation of a mixed solution scenario. First of all, the solution 
scenarios include improved communication in the form of reaching out to students on their preferred 
communication channels and adding English as a secondary language due to the highly international 
nature of DTU students, teachers and researchers. Secondly, a large focus needs to be on establishing 
student/youth housing in the city centre of Lyngby or close by to ensure students’ participation in the 
city’s everyday life. The methods and goals of the City of Knowledge partnership are already on the 
right track regarding communication improvements. However, providing student/youth housing in 
Lyngby can pivot student interest towards the city and improve the integration process significantly 
for it to become a vibrant university town.

Introduction 
1. Introduction 
Globalisation and urbanisation are an ever-growing pervasive phenomenon that poses several 
challenges to the world. Connecting people, neighbourhoods, cities and countries more closely 
together creates both opportunities for and barriers to sustainable development. These goals have 
also been addressed in number 11 of the UN Sustainability Goals as ‘Sustainable cities and 
communities’ to make cities and human settlements safe, resilient and sustainable (United Nations, 
2015). The interconnectedness between humans lead to an increase of internationalisation, shifting 
the cities’ focus from exclusively local initiatives to a larger, worldly context (Fien, 2010). The best 
companies have several offices in several countries and attract employees from all over the world, the 
best universities attract students from all over the world and the best cities attract citizens from all 
over the world. All the cities want to be appealing to citizens who can give the city special status among 
the other cities. Knowledge and innovation have become one of the most important aspects of 
development and branding of cities, and with this trend the universities have gained an increased role 
in urban planning and development.   
“The changing relationship between the campus – both academic and corporate – and the city 
is transforming urban realities. Worldwide, universities and their host cities are evolving into 
‘knowledge cities’. University and corporate campuses thereby not only take on a central role 
for the cultural, economic and social development of the city, they are also establishing 
themselves as laboratories for a new Denkkultur1.” (Hoeger, 2007, p. 13) 
The relationship between the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) and Lyngby-Taarbæk 
municipality is no exception to this trend. The long-term municipality strategy, called the ‘Vidensby 
2020’  (in English: City of Knowledge Strategy) was created to facilitate the sustainable growth and 
development of the municipality (Lyngby-Taarbæk Kommune, 2019a). The City of Knowledge initiative 
represents an international profile and partnership between the private businesses, education and 
the public authorities, aiming to make the area more attractive for businesses, citizens and students 
and establish a closer relationship between Lyngby and the university (DTU). Their vision is to create 
a vibrant university town where these stakeholders can cooperate to create “the best conditions for 
research, study, business and entrepreneurship” (Lyngby Taarbæk City of Knowledge, 2018a). 
DTU is located north of Copenhagen, in the Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality, outside of Lyngby city as an 
island with a unique relation to the surrounding towns. It is a leading university in the Nordic Region

2.1 Technical University of Denmark 
The institution, which is today known as the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), was founded by 
H.C Ørsted in 1829 as the College of Advanced Technology. Over time, the university had locations in 
several different places around Copenhagen. At the year of the foundation, its home was in the centre 
of Copenhagen, Studiestræde and Skt. Pedersstræde in the professor’s manor. It was the first 
institution in Denmark with a master’s programme in engineering, namely Chemistry and Mechanics 
(Just, 2019). 
In the following years, more independent study programmes were created, such as in 1857: Building 
Engineering and in 1903: Electronics. With the continuous growth of the institution, the university had 
to move to a new building at Sølvtorvet (Sørensen, 2014).  
However, the student numbers continued to grow and with extensive need for more space (for - 
among others - laboratory facilities) another extension of the institution was inevitable. On the 100th 
anniversary of DTU, the foundation stone was laid down for a new building at Østervoldgade. The 
development of the new building was undertaken in 8 stages from 1929 to 1954 (Sørensen, 2014). In 
the meantime the institution changed its name to the Technical College of Denmark in 1933 (Just, 
2019). 
Within a few years, the university had to move again, due to the large and continuous growth of study 
programmes and students. After a couple years of construction from 1962 to 1974, the institution 
moved to its current location in Lundtofte, near the town Lyngby and in 1994 became the Technical 
University of Denmark (DTU) (Sørensen, 2014).  
In 2007, DTU merged together with several stakeholders such as the Risø Research Center and the 
Danish Space Center, which strengthened the university, but also doubled the number of employees 
and broadened its research areas and capacity (Just, 2019).

Today, DTU has three campuses and several research and teaching facilities around Denmark. The 
Lyngby Campus is the main one with a 106-hectare area divided into the four quadrants, north of 
Copenhagen near Lyngby. The Ballerup Campus in mainly home to the BEng students and is located 
10 kms from downtown Copenhagen. Risø Campus, on the other hand, is home to research facilities 
and can be found close to Roskilde, 40 km west of Copenhagen (Campuses - DTU, 2018).  
DTU is currently under constant development and expansion: more than 20 construction projects have 
been planned for the period 2010-2021, most of which have already been completed. The university 
is investing more than 4 billion DKK in the development of Lyngby campus (Greisen, 2016). However, 
these development projects are not just related to construction work. DTU has been testing pop-up 
environments all around campus between the buildings to boost campus life. Just to mention a few 
elements: hammocks, street food and benches were placed all around the campus and were well-
received by both students and employees, creating more life on campus (Trolle, S. ‘KGS. Lyngby 
FUTURE’, 2016, p. 14). 
The number of students, employees and researchers is growing: every year, it increases by at least 
400 people compared to the year before (see Figure 2) and this tendency is expected to continue in 
the foreseeable future (Arvedlund, 2019).

2.2 Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality 
Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality (LTK) is located to the north of Copenhagen and it is a part of the Greater 
Copenhagen region of Denmark. The municipality consists of seven smaller towns: Kgs. Lyngby, 
Ulrikkenborg, Sorgenfri, Virum, Lundtofte, Hjortekær and Taarbæk, see Figure 4 (Lyngby-Taarbæk 
Kommune, 2017d). Two hundred and fifty years ago, it was a fishing and hunting area. However, in

university city, with several companies and DTU in the centre (Lyngby-Taarbæk Kommune, 2017e).  
 
Figure 4: Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality (Lyngby-Taarbæk Kommune, 2017d) 
From the country houses and royal castles, the municipality developed into a sprawling city with many 
jobs. In the 1930s new residential areas were built. From the 1960s onwards, when DTU moved to the 
municipality, the city’s development improved and especially Kgs. Lyngby and its Main Street evolved 
into a lively knowledge city (Lyngby-Taarbæk Kommune, 2019b). 
In 2012, the Lyngby-Taarbæk City of Knowledge and Urban Development association was founded, 
with the vision “to develop Lyngby-Taarbæk into one of Europe’s leading university towns with the 
best conditions for research, study, business and entrepreneurship” (Lyngby Taarbæk City of 
Knowledge, 2018a). In the last six years, several large companies and organisations have joined the 
initiative, such as Microsoft, Maersk Drilling & Maersk Supply Service, COWI and DTU. By the end of 
2017, the City of Knowledge reached 80 members. Several projects were developed during the years 
in regard to several areas such as urban development, network, welfare innovation, 
internationalisation, housing and so on (Lyngby Taarbæk City of Knowledge, 2018a). 
Throughout history, since DTU moved to Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality, the university has been an 
island within the area (Jørgensen and Jespersen, 2019). With the City of Knowledge initiative, DTU 
works in close collaboration with the municipality and the other members to create a vibrant 
university town. Several new projects have been developed in different fields such as ‘Smart City’, 
‘Science Festival’ and the foundation of ‘Boligfonden’, the housing foundation (Lyngby Taarbæk City 
of Knowledge, 2018a).

As of January 1st, the population of the municipality is 55,240 people. In the 1960s, the population was 
around 65,000. Since then, the population has steadily declined or fluctuated. To increase the 
numbers, the municipality adopted a housing program in 2009, which should ideally result in about 
62,000 inhabitants by 2028 (Lyngby-Taarbæk Kommune, 2017f). Based on the statistics of Lyngby-
Taarbæk municipality (calculated on 1 January 2017), there are 26,000 homes in the municipality, of 
which approximately 1,400 are dormitories. According to the future plans, 1.500 more homes are in 
the pipeline, of which approximately 850 will be specifically built for young people (Lyngby-Taarbæk 
Kommune, 2017f).

Lyngby has a population of 12,214 citizens (as of 1 January 2017), and it is the second largest town of 
the municipality (Lyngby-Taarbæk Kommune, 2018a). The border of Lyngby to the west is the lake 
Mølleådalen and Sorgenfri Slotspark, to the east the Helsingør Motorway. Lyngby consists of several 
neighbourhoods and numerous parks and green areas. Kgs. Lyngby represents Lyngby’s central

The second area is characterised by a relatively close town with a town square and a shopping street.

Background 
Since the Knowledge City association started in 2012, it has been a great interest to create a closer 
collaboration with DTU and additionally attract more students to Lyngby city centre. In 2015, a 
partnership was established called the ‘City Life in Lyngby’ with the purpose of “creating a number of 
cultural events and high-quality experiences in Lyngby” (City Life in Lyngby - Vidensby, 2016). 
There have been several initiatives directed at students, such as the ‘smart city hubs’ (Smart City Hub 
- Vidensby, 2016), the ‘biketown showdown’ (Biketown Showdown - Vidensby, 2016), the ‘housing 
exchange program’ (Vidensby Boligbørs - Vidensby, 2018) or the ‘Celebrating the new academic year’ 
and ‘International Day’ (City Life in Lyngby - Vidensby, 2016).  
To gain a better understanding and get an overview of what has been done until now by the 
Knowledge City and the City Life in Lyngby partnership, some of the initiatives are described in more 
detail below. 
Smart City Hub 
The Smart City Hub program was started in 2017 and since then, thirteen Hubs have been held in 
several places (DTU, Malmö, Lyngby), with different participants, ideas, concepts (Smart City Hub: 
Ideas and solutions - Knowledge city, 2019). The Smart City Hub is a physical place for cooperation, 
meetings and solution ideation across students, businesses, researchers and the Lyngby-Taarbæk 
municipality (Smart City Hub - Vidensby, 2016). 
The purpose of the hubs is to support networking between the different stakeholders and collaborate 
on finding solutions for specific issues and cases related to sustainable urban development. These 
ideas can be further developed by Lyngby-Taarbæk or other cities to find a feasible solution for the 
issues (Smart City Hub - Vidensby, 2016). The hub is facilitated by experts, the specific cases are pitched 
by Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality, DTU, or other companies such as Niras, Egedal Municipality or 
Microsoft (Smart City Hub: Ideas and solutions - Knowledge city, 2019). Each topic has a relevant Smart 
City-theme like planning, buildings, supply or mobility (Smart City Hub - Vidensby, 2016). 
Biketown Showdown 
The City of Knowledge strongly focuses on representing Lyngby as a sustainable, social and attractive 
university town for students and young adults (Biketown Showdown - Vidensby, 2016). Every year 
since 2016, as a mark of the beginning of the new academic year, the City of Knowledge and several 
collaborators organise the Biketown Showdown event, where old bikes are collected, renovated by 
the TEC School students and sold for students, paired up with several activities and concerts (Biketown 
Showdown marks the new academic year - Vidensby, 2018).

3. Theoretical Background 
3.1 Knowledge Cities 
The knowledge city project has been a highly favoured research field in recent years, as a sub-field of 
knowledge-based development (Ergazakis et al., 2009). Knowledge-based development and 
knowledge management have been the focus of discussions in the last twenty years, as it is applicable 
to several areas such as business, education, government and healthcare (Carrillo, 2006). 
There are several views, opinions and definitions of knowledge cities, as the concept is very broad and 
complex. One of the most known definition is the following by Ergazakis et al.:  
“A knowledge city is a city that aims at a knowledge‐based development, by encouraging the 
continuous creation, sharing, evaluation, renewal and update of knowledge. This can be 
achieved through the continuous interaction between its citizens themselves and at the same 
time between them and other cities’ citizens. The citizens’ knowledge‐sharing culture as well 
as the city’s appropriate design, IT networks and infrastructures support these interactions.” 
(Ergazakis, Metaxiotis and Psarras, 2004, p. 7)  
To transform a modern well-developed city into a knowledge city, it requires active support and a 
coherent strategy from all stakeholders. There are six main categories defining the success factor of a 
knowledge city development: political, strategic, financial, technological, societal and environmental 
(Ergazakis, Metaxiotis and Psarras, 2004).  
Regardless of the several definitions and approaches, one thing is clear: a knowledge city has many 
benefits. Just to mention a few: a more sustainable economy, citizens are actively involved in the city 
development, better educational services and strong innovation dynamics (Carrillo, 2006). 
In conclusion, according to Ergazakis et al., a knowledge city should be able to achieve the following 
points. It should provide access to the new technologies for all citizens. Additionally, it should excel in 
research which provides the platform for new knowledge-based goods and services. Providing 
instruments to make knowledge accessible to citizens in a systematic, efficient and effective way is 
another crucial aspect. Last but not least, it should generate, attract and retain highly skilled citizens 
and have civic centres to be open to diversity and foster face-to-face relations (Ergazakis, Metaxiotis 
and Psarras, 2004). 
In the last decade, more and more cities have transformed into knowledge cities. Barcelona, Spain has 
one of the largest university communities in Europe, with eight universities in the city and several 
==PAGE:24==

technology centres and a scientific park network. The city started transformation of the city in 2001 
(Trullén, 2001) (Carrillo, 2006). As of 2009, the knowledge city concept was and still is a dominant 
characteristic in the strategic choices of the city. (Ergazakis et al., 2009). 
A slightly different example is Delft from the Netherlands, where they turned the unemployment 
problem into a thriving knowledge city initiative, by recognising the importance of the local University 
of Technology (Carrillo, 2006). The ‘Delft Knowledge City’ cooperation is built around four pillars: 
education, research institute, entrepreneurs and government (Ergazakis et al., 2009). 
Lyngby-Taarbæk started the knowledge city initiative in 2012, with the goal to become one of Europe’s 
leading university towns (Lyngby Taarbæk City of Knowledge, 2018a). In the last seven years, the

The Lyngby-Taarbæk City of Knowledge and Urban Development defines their vision as the following

•

The City of Knowledge partnership is based on the Triple Helix innovation model. The Triple Helix 
model is a methodological tool, which uses collaboration and communication between the scientific 
institutions, businesses and public authorities as the path to innovation (Leydesdorff and Etzkowitz,

According to the City of Knowledge, “Lyngby is one of the most attractive development areas in the 
Capital Region” (Lyngby Taarbæk City of Knowledge, 2018b). Therefore, one of the main focuses of 
the project is to develop the area into a unique ‘Innovation District’ (Lyngby Taarbæk City of 
Knowledge, 2018b). 
 
3.2 Innovation districts 
Innovation districts are defined as follows: “geographic areas where leading-edge anchor institutions 
and companies cluster and connect with start-ups, business incubators, and accelerators.” (Katz and 
Wagner, 2014a, p. 1). 
==PAGE:25==
 
14 
 
 
Theoretical Background 
The modern idea of innovation is that innovation happens when people come together and work 
together and not in isolation (Katz and Wagner, 2014b). In recent years, the innovation district concept 
has been steadily rising in popularity as a result of the changing trends all around the world. To 
mention a few examples where this mindset has taken roots: Baltimore, Barcelona, London, Berlin 
and Stockholm are all on the edge of change (Katz and Wagner, 2014b). The main idea is that with 
creating and empowering these innovation districts, it can help to address the main challenges of our 
time: “sluggish growth, national austerity and local fiscal challenges, rising social inequality, and 
extensive sprawl and continued environmental degradation” (Wagner, 2019, p. 12). 
The innovation districts are originally from one of the following models: the “anchor plus” model, the 
“re-imagined urban areas” model or the “urbanized science park” model. The “anchor plus” model 
can be primarily found near large universities, where they attract related firms, entrepreneurs and 
companies, such as: Kendall Square in Cambridge and MIT, or the Lyngby City of Knowledge initiative 
and DTU (Katz and Wagner, 2014a) (Katz, 2017). The “re-imagined urban areas” model is usually found 
in formal industrial areas close to the city centre and easily accessible, for example Boston’s South 
Boston waterfront. Finally, the “urbanized science park” can generally be found in isolated suburban 
or exurban areas with increasing density or at sprawling research universities. To mention an example: 
North Carolina’s Research Triangle Park (Katz and Wagner, 2014b).  
The innovation ecosystem is driven by three main assets: economic assets, physical assets and 
networking assets. To summarise the essence of it: economic assets are the firms, institutions and 
organisations present in a certain area. The physical assets are the private and public spaces (buildings, 
open spaces, streets, parks, plazas…), the privately-owned places (shared labs, hub…) or even the 
infrastructure. The networking assets, on the other hand, are the relationships between these 
aforementioned stakeholders (individuals, firms and institutions) (Katz and Wagner, 2014a).  
However, to achieve a unique and vibrant environment, where innovation can thrive, the connectivity, 
diversity and quality of the place need to be ensured (Katz, S. Vey and Wagner, 2015). In order to 
achieve this, as mentioned by Julie Wagner (urban researcher and co-author of the research paper 
“The Rise of Innovation Districts”) in an interview: “students can play a crucial role in the development 
and growth of innovation districts” (Wagner, 2019, p. 13). This is an interesting and important view, 
as the Knowledge City initiative shares the characteristics found in other instances of innovation 
districts and, as a consequence, the student perspective must also be taken into account.

3.3 The University and the City 
Historically, cities and universities have been strongly connected, as most of the oldest universities 
were even named after their parent cities (Hebbert, 2018), such as DTU’s parent university, the 
University of Copenhagen (KU) (Sørensen, 2014). 
Throughout history, two major university trends have emerged. Michael Hebbert categorised it as the 
American suburban or rural settings campus universities and the European model with more city-
centre locations (Hebbert, 2018). Having said that, according to Claes Caldenby in a report published 
by The Danish University and Property Agency, there are two categorisations: the internalist 
perspective and the externalist perspective. The internalist perspective considers development driven 
by inner driving forces, in a closed, undisturbed environment. Therefore, it is associated with the 
previously mentioned American model. The external perspective claims that reality raises issues which 
are the preconditions of development. Accordingly, this concept requires a strong involvement with 
society, open meeting places and a university integrated in the city, similar to the European model. 
(Claes Caldenby, Universitets- og Bygningsstyrelsen, 2013) 
Both the internalist and the externalist perspective contain several subcategories, which have been 
established throughout the last 800 years. The internalist concept contains the college, the American 
campus and the so-called external university. The externalist concept includes the “universitas”, the 
institutional universities and the city campus (Universitets- og Bygningsstyrelsen, 2013). These six 
concepts are explained in more detail in the following sections. 
The college (internalist) 
The college is mimicking the structure of a monastery. It belongs to the internalist category and usually 
consist of closed walls encircling a building with a courtyard where students and teachers study, eat 
and sleep in a separated world. These types of universities can traditionally be found in England, for 
instance in Oxford and Cambridge (Universitets- og Bygningsstyrelsen, 2013).  
The campus university (internalist) 
The campus university is originated from the American university model. However, the first American 
universities were created based on the English college model, therefore it has several similar aspects. 
The campus concept adopted the idea of taking responsibility for the student’s entire life, including 
accommodation, study and meals, as well as leisure activities, which resulted in isolated communities 
(Universitets- og Bygningsstyrelsen, 2013). 
==PAGE:27==
 
16 
 
 
Theoretical Background 
The external universities (internalist) 
The external universities closely resemble the campus university type and often even share the same 
name, but the former does not include accommodation for the students or sport facilities. These 
universities were developed as a solution to the sudden growth of universities in the 1960s, when 
institutions were placed outside of the city to grant space for future expansion (Universitets- og 
Bygningsstyrelsen, 2013). 
Universitas (externalist) 
The name universitas means ‘guild’, which refers to the guild of university teachers. The early 
universities did not have a dedicated educational building, only rented rooms in everyday houses and 
moved around from one city to another. However, one area of the city belonged to one guild, while 
other streets belonged to others. The street of the universities were usually named after the 
institution, such as the first location of DTU, which was called ‘Studiestræde’ – Study Alley. 
(Universitets- og Bygningsstyrelsen, 2013) 
The institutional university (externalist) 
The institutional university originates from the universitas model. From the 19th century, when 
universities started to vastly expand in most of the European countries, it became impossible to 
accommodate the whole university in one place. As a solution, the universities spread out and became 
well integrated with the city by taking smaller pieces of land around the city (Universitets- og 
Bygningsstyrelsen, 2013). A good example of the institutional university is the University of 
Copenhagen, which has four campus areas all around Copenhagen, integrated into the city (University 
of Copenhagen, 2013).  
The city university (externalist) 
The city universities were a reaction to the external universities in the 1990s, a pursuit to move the 
universities back to the city. To solve the space issue, often these universities were moved into old 
industrial or military buildings in the city (University of Copenhagen, 2013).  
Looking at these categories, it is challenging to place DTU in just one category. With the large Lyngby 
campus outside of the city, DTU is definitely more of an internalist concept, somewhere between the 
‘campus university’ model, as the campus caters for all possible student needs (dormitories, canteens, 
stores, education…) and the ‘external university’ model, since DTU moved out of the city as a response 
to the vast expansion of the institution. However, with the new City of Knowledge initiative and 
Innovation district action plans, the university attempts to move in the direction of the externalist

concept. Despite that, it poses a great challenge for DTU, that they do not want to change the location 
of the institution, only connect it and integrate it better with Lyngby-Taarbæk. 
 
3.4 Students in the City 
Students and academic communities could be the driving force for urban development, as students 
keep the cities diverse and lively. They are the main consumers of cultural and recreational facilities 
and the working class of tomorrow (Russo, van den Berg and Lavanga, 2003). However, achieving and 
maintaining a sustainable relationship between city and university is a complex and challenging task. 
As it is phrased by Antonio P. Russo et al.: 
“Universities may be ‘good’ to cities, and cities may be ‘good’ to universities, but such a 
virtuous relationship, and the benefits expected from the triple helix development model, is far 
from automatically achievable.” (Russo, van den Berg and Lavanga, 2007, p. 214) 
Russo, van den Berg and Lavanga (2003) studied nine European cities with a large education sector 
and investigated the impact of student communities on cities, and the relationship between students 
and their host communities. They classified the students’ influence on their cities into four categories: 
economic development, housing market, infrastructure and internationalisation (Russo, van den Berg 
and Lavanga, 2003).

As illustrated by Table 1, there are several impacts of the student communities on the cities. The 
presence of a university, education and research communities in a city helps to create more jobs, 
greater financial turnover and more services (Russo, van den Berg and Lavanga, 2003).  
Students also have a crucial impact on the housing market, as they have different spending behaviours 
compared with the locals. As tenants, they have a faster lifecycle and usually enjoy a lower protection, 
which can push up the price levels of the market. Therefore, the housing preferences of the students 
usually are not satisfactory in the “normal” market, as they require lower price categories and a 
closeness to the university areas (Russo, van den Berg and Lavanga, 2003). DTU deals with this 
phenomenon by building more dormitories on campus. These accommodations are fulfilling both the 
low price and the closeness to the university criteria, yet separate the students from the city, losing 
direct economic benefits as a result. 
As mentioned above, students are major consumers of cultural and recreational products and often 
are producers themselves. The so-called ‘cultural quarters’ are an up-and-coming type of urban 
development, as it opens up an area of the city for the student communities (Russo, van den Berg and 
Lavanga, 2003). 
Students also bring social innovation and cultural change to local cultures, as their rhythm of life differs 
from the general working-class residents’. However, it is highly important to find a balanced symbiosis 
between students and locals. (Russo, van den Berg and Lavanga, 2003) 
Based on the research of the nine European university cities, a strategic planning analysis was 
conducted focusing on the sustainable relationship between city and university, by Antonio P. Russo 
et al. (Russo, van den Berg and Lavanga, 2003). In the development of higher education in a city, the 
different stakeholders are affected in different ways. However, as a key takeaway, a balanced 
relationship must be established between the three key stakeholders: higher education, local 
community and the private sector. The main aspects of a strategic planning are knowledge exchange 
(science parks, incubators, networking…), economic development (offices, scholarships, widening 
participation, continuing education…) and urban planning (housing, spatial planning, transport and 
mobility, events…) (Russo, van den Berg and Lavanga, 2007). 
In summary, an extensive urban strategy for a student-friendly city should include the following points 
(Russo, van den Berg and Lavanga, 2007, p. 215):

• 
“Attracting the students: marketing the city as a student-friendly community, to be on the 
map of the global competition for talent, which is emerging as one of the key challenges in 
the restructuring of the world’s economy. 
• 
Assisting the students: offering high-quality services for welcoming and assisting the students, 
to achieve a better integration with the host community. 
•

In order to qualify as a student-friendly city, Lyngby needs to satisfy these criteria. All aspects had a 
direct influence on the selection, analysis and evaluation of the scenarios and indicators.

4.1 Research question 
How could Lyngby improve its knowledge city efforts regarding student friendliness and achieve its 
goal of becoming a truly vibrant university town? 
This research question highlights the importance of the efforts that Lyngby has taken in recent years 
with its knowledge city aspirations and acknowledges its goal of creating a close collaboration and 
sustainable habitat for companies, DTU and its population. Having said that, the initial attempts of 
attracting students to the city and retaining them there has been met with mixed results. This thesis 
thus proposes three hypothetical solution scenarios, which would improve the intention of Lyngby 
becoming a place for students, locals and companies to thrive in with an elevated student presence. 
The six points for a comprehensive urban strategy for a student-friendly city, as discussed previously 
in the theoretical section, provided guidance for the setup of the solution scenarios. It is, however, 
worth mentioning that some aspects like “attracting students” or “increasing the opportunities for 
contact with other stakeholders” with events and activities such as the Science Festival or the Smart

projects in progress in the municipality, of which some apartments are dedicated to student housing. 
Lastly, aspects such as “empowering the students” or “keeping the students linked to the city” have 
not been extensively worked on due to the special circumstance of DTU being essentially an isolated 
island within the municipality (as discussed in the University and the City chapter in the theoretical 
section) and these efforts are relatively newly founded. The three solution scenarios that have been 
hypothesised, analysed and evaluated for this project aim to improve as many of the previously 
mentioned aspects as possible and answer the proposed research question.

5. Methodology 
5.1 Urban Sustainability Reporting 
“Urban Sustainability Reporting is a tool for informing local government, as well as individuals, 
businesses, and other organizations, about the progress that they are making towards achieving urban 
sustainability” (McLaren, 1996, p. 184). The reporting process can be done in a myriad of ways and 
McLaren highlights several process steps necessary for the success of urban sustainability endeavours. 
The definition of sustainability goals at the start of projects and the continuous scoping of these goals 
throughout the process are two crucial first steps. Having said that, the most essential step is to choose 
an appropriate indicator framework, which if applied properly, defines the criteria to meet the 
sustainability goals. These indicators naturally evolve over time within the framework, as new data is 
brought into the process and new insights are gained. (McLaren, 1996) 
In this report, a combination of goal- and domain-based framework approaches will be used. As 
McLaren describes, using a combined framework can overcome many of the disadvantages of the 
individual frameworks by complementing each other’s strengths and weaknesses. The goal-based 
approach will be used to create a direct link between the indicators and the sustainability goals. The 
domain-based framework approach will also be used as they start with key dimensions of 
sustainability defined early on, which are the environment, the economy and society. Within these 
dimensions, appropriate and necessary indicators can be created. These indicators should be carefully 
selected and re-evaluated during the process as the data collection might alter these to varying 
extents and thus, they must be modular and flexible. To ensure a more holistic coverage of the 
investigated topics, within each indicator several sub-indicators will be created. (McLaren, 1996) 
After having collected data and solidified and analysed indicators, the last steps in the Urban 
Sustainability Reporting process is to prepare and present the report to relevant stakeholders and 
assess the performance of the indicators in relation to the predefined sustainability goals. This 
monitoring phase should be periodically carried out after the process has been completed to maintain 
current levels of sustainability and to improve in other areas if new opportunities arise. (McLaren, 
1996)

The data collection process from two other stakeholders – PKS and IAESTE – had been identical in 
terms of the purpose they fulfilled and the way in which they were carried out. The purpose was to 
gain insight into the inner workings of student-led organisations and how they complemented the 
official associations with similarly professional and useful help. 
With Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality, as one of the major stakeholders, two in-depth interviews were 
conducted about the student housing situation in the municipality, as well as email correspondence 
with several members of the municipality. 
The main objective of the interview was to gain an overview of the municipality’s side of the topic and 
to understand the ongoing and future plans regarding student housing and knowledge city initiatives. 
Furthermore, the interview aimed to map out the current housing situation and to see whether the 
plans are fulfilling the needs.

Methodology 
Students 
The aim of the survey was to get an overview of the students’ housing situation and preferences 
through an exploratory and confirmatory research study and set the stage for possible future 
interviews.  
The students were asked to participate in a survey, to ensure the possibility of reaching out to a bigger 
amount of people. However, given the short time frame, only a sample of students were interviewed. 
To ensure a natural diversity of the replies, 2-2 bachelor and master classes were the interview 
subjects in the first round. After the first replies, based on the feedback, the survey was further 
developed and sent out to one more class with a mixture of bachelor and master students. 
The survey was created based on the principles of questionnaire construction, using a mix of 
qualitative and quantitative questions. To ensure that the survey is simple, easy and relatively fast to 
fill out, the majority of the questions were quantitative, with some qualitative ones to strengthen and 
complement the rest of the questions (Johnson and A. Turner, 2014). 
In total, the survey was sent out to approximately 200 students, both at bachelor and master level as 
well as both to Danish and international students. In the first stage, 119 students replied, followed by 
another 34 students in the second round, resulting in a total of 153 replies out of the approximate 
200, or a response ratio of around 76%. 
The survey was split up into four main parts. The first part was about mapping out the current situation 
and the student preferences with the interest of knowing where DTU students live currently, where 
they would prefer to live and so on. The second part was about how easy or hard it was for the 
students to find accommodation and how long it took. The third part was about Lyngby as a place to 
live and the elements students miss compared with the big city offerings of Copenhagen, while the 
last part was about demographic questions such as age and citizenship among others. 
In product design, an often-used conceptual design method is to create personas and user 
characteristics for a group of people, who are mostly representing the broad user base. Each persona 
is a potential consumer type and it should be described with their personalities, which are used during 
the development of the product. This helps the designers understand and relate to the target group 
and consequently come up with the best suitable solution. Additionally, this method ensures that the 
design is based on the personas – for example, “What would XY say?” – and not driven by the design 
team’s preferences and beliefs (Hansen, 2015).

Methodology 
As one of the main stakeholders in this report are the students from DTU, it was crucial to properly 
understand the different student types and their preferences. In order to achieve that, several 
personas (student types) were developed based on the result of the student survey. 
To gain a more extensive view and to help validate the scoring of the indicators, four in-depth 
interviews were conducted with students. The aim was to carry out at least one interview from each 
group of student types to get a holistic picture.

6. Analysis 
6.1 Current student housing situation 
The housing situation has been a sensitive topic throughout Denmark in the last 10-15 years with the 
increasing numbers of university students. According to the Danish Construction Association, there is 
a need for 22,000 student accommodations in Denmark, and out of those 8,400 are needed in 
Copenhagen alone2 (Balslev, 2018). As of last year, based on the Danish Statistics, 150,000 students 
(and 10,000 PhD students) live in the metropolitan area (The City of Copenhagen, 2017) (StatBank 
Denmark, 2019). Since the population of Copenhagen and the metropolitan area equals 1,99 million, 
that means that more than 7,5% of the whole population consists of students (World Population 
Review, 2019). 
General overview 
The student housing situation is especially extremely complex in the capital region of Denmark. There 
are several organisations and administrative offices handling the day-to-day processes, but the two 
largest are the Central Institution Committee (CIU) and the College’s Office in Copenhagen (KKIK) in 
the metropolitan area, which together provide approximately 11,500 student and youth 
accommodations (Københavns Kommune, 2016) (Kollegiernes Kontor I København, 2019).  
Nonetheless, there are some other options to register for halls of residences, such as directly through 
the waiting list of dormitories or, in some cases, through educational institutions.  
Traditionally, the accommodation is not provided by the institution in Denmark. However, as a result 
of the current difficult housing situation, most of the universities offer at least guidance to their 
students. The Technical University of Denmark (DTU) works together with Boligfonden DTU – a non-
profit housing foundation – to provide housing for international students and employees at the 
university (Home - Boligfonden DTU, 2019). The Accommodation Office at DTU (the point of contact 
for Boligfonden DTU) provides a list of halls of residence and student housings in the capital region of 
Denmark, that DTU students and employees can apply for through them. However, the 
Accommodation Office can only provide accommodation for a maximum of two semesters and they 
prioritise bachelor and first-year master students (Viborg, 2019). Due to the ‘two semester’ rule, 
usually they can accommodate almost all the applicants quickly, but this is only a starting base for 
students to gain time to find a new accommodation (Viborg, 2019).

DTU is also collaborates with the Polyteknisk KollegieSelskabamba (PKS) – a non-profit management 
company – which, unlike Boligfonden DTU, provides accommodation for all students, not only from 
DTU (Home | Polyteknisk KollegieSelskab amba, 2019). PKS has around 1,850 rooms and they provide 
rooms for students who have more than six months left of their studies (Traberg, 2019). According to 
the regulations, they need to prioritise Danish students. There are constantly around 800-1,000 
students on the waiting list – which can be years long, due to the prioritisation rules – but the numbers 
jump up in the peak period (before semester start) (Traberg, 2019). 
If someone is coming to DTU for a shorter study period through The International Association for the 
Exchange of Students for Technical Experience (IAESTE), the organisation provides accommodation 
for them. At IAESTE Denmark, usually they provide accommodation by renting rooms from landlords, 
so the students can rent the room through the organisation. It is, however, a difficult and time-
consuming process for the organisation (Støle, 2019). 
Last but not least, the private housing market is always an option for finding accommodation. 
However, due to the rising housing demand in Copenhagen and its metropolitan area, it is hard to find 
an accommodation (Næss-Schmidt and Kilsdal, 2019a). Not to mention that, as a result of the 
demographic pressure in recent years and the housing market not being able to keep up with the pace 
for the demand, the housing prices have rapidly increased, presenting a challenge to students with a 
limited budget (Næss-Schmidt and Kilsdal, 2019b). 
If someone is not under education, but still between 18 and 30 years old, they are still eligible for 
youth housing across many apartments in Denmark. The general youth housing in Lyngby-Taarbæk 
municipality is managed and registered by the housing association Dansk almennyttigt Boligselskab 
(DAB). Since the rent of a youth housing is considerably lower, it can be a great life start support for 
young people after finishing their education, but you are only entitled to stay a maximum of five years 
in a youth home (Lyngby-Taarbæk Kommune, 2019c).

According to figures from Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality population projections (Befolkningsprognoser

7. Solution Scenarios 
There are several options and perspectives to address the problem at hand, therefore three different 
scenarios were developed. The three scenarios focus on the research question: how could Lyngby 
improve its knowledge city efforts regarding student friendliness and achieve its goal of becoming a 
truly vibrant university town? 
The solution scenarios are developed and build on having the future development plants of Lyngby 
Taarbæk municipality in mind. All three scenarios are focusing on Lyngby and its’ surroundings, 
however the city centre is under constant development. The city centre map below (Figure 23) 
illustrates the planned urban development projects. (Lyngby-Taarbæk Kommune, 2017e)

7.1 Scenario 1 – Improve current situation 
Scenario 1 is built on the ideas and information from the interviews, meetings and research of the 
work of Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality, the city coordinator and the City of Knowledge homepage. 
The core idea is to create better and more successful communication channels for the students, so 
they can realise and explore the wide range of events and possibilities the city can offer. The 
communication can be improved on several levels. First of all, as there are a large number of 
international students, researchers and employees at DTU, the communication preferably should be 
in English, or at least to some extent. Furthermore, 10% of the residents of Lyngby are international 
citizens (Barklund, 2019). Certainly, some of the events can be suitable for foreigners as well as for 
Danes, even if the event is not in English, such as markets or festivals.  
Secondly, adequate communication channels should be used, as students are probably not reading 
for example the local newspaper. As discussed with Line Barklund, city coordinator at the interview: 
“We need to communicate more in English, and we need to get out to their (students) channels. 
I mean we have a very great local newspaper, and lot of things comes into the local newspaper, 
but of course the students wouldn’t read the newspaper at all. So, we need to like get out of 
course to social media and like that, but also to know exactly what media are they on.” 
(Interview: Barklund, 2019) 
Underlining that, even the students who live close to the city do not know a lot about what is going 
on in Lyngby.  
“I don’t know anything about what happens in Lyngby actually, no. It’s really hard, because I 
think, then you have to read the local paper. And I think we only get one maybe, and then it’s 
gone at the dormitory. So, when you don’t go their website, because why…. I think Facebook 
would actually be the best to do it, like events in Lyngby, because if it’s nearby you it pops up. 
[...]But usually no, I have no idea what’s going on in Lyngby.” (Interview: Student. 24 years old. 
Nybrogård, 2019)

7.2 Scenario 2 – More student housing in Lyngby city centre 
The second scenario is based on the idea that raising the student and youth population of the city 
would help the involvement of the students in the city’s life. As it was visible from the survey results, 
a lot of students prefer Copenhagen over Lyngby, since most (if not all) of their friends are in 
Copenhagen. Increasing the student population would not only influence the young residents of the 
city but would potentially also attract their friends and encourage more students to live in the city. 
One might say that it is not sufficient to only build new student or youth housing if the students do 
not want to live in the area. However, given the current challenging housing situation in the 
metropolitan area of Denmark, and the years-long waiting lists for dormitories and youth housings, 
the concern seems insignificant. 
Creating more student housing in Lyngby would be a more drastic transformation of the city, however, 
it could lead to a long-term solution. After all, the city centre is already facing a big change with the 
Light Rail route going through Klampenborgvej. (Lyngby-Taarbæk Kommune, 2017e) 
It was also pointed out by Claus Nielsen, Chairman of the Board of Lyngby-Taarbæk City of Knowledge 
and Urban Development, that:

young people. Today, only a few thousand people live in the centre, and you can tell. So many

FUTURE’, 2016, p. 11) 
As a first step, the number of planned housing projects for students in Lyngby could be increased from

than 5% of the DTU student population (DTU, 2019). Looking at the results of the survey, 14% of the 
students marked Lyngby as their preferred living place and around 30% of the students would consider 
moving to Lyngby, even if they can get an apartment for a similar price in Copenhagen. Even with a 
relatively small sample size, Lyngby does seem to catch the interest of students and this change could 
persuade these students to actually move to Lyngby. 
Nielsen also added: 
“If we get a lot of young people settling in the centre, it will vitalise the city and give it a boost

8. Development of Indicators 
8.1 Indicator framework 
An indicator framework was developed in order to assess the three solution scenarios from a 
sustainable perspective. As mentioned earlier in the Methodology chapter, the framework is a mix of 
domain and goal-based, and it also strives to achieve the Sustainability Goal 11 from the UN 
Sustainability Goals, which is about creating sustainable cities and communities. Accordingly, it is 
essential to develop indicators in all three pillars of sustainability: social, economic and environmental. 
These three pillars of sustainability and their interdependency are illustrated in Figure 26. 
 
Figure 26: Interdependency of the three pillars 
The indicators are developed with the goal of evaluating the scenarios and deciding whether the 
indicator results are showing the progress towards or away from sustainability (McLaren, 1996). 
Several of the steps in ‘Developing an Urban Sustainability Report’ defined by McLaren were followed 
in the upcoming section (McLaren, 1996).  
In the scoping stage, the number of necessary indicators were determined to be around 10 - 20 to set 
a spatial and temporal boundary to the report. Through several iterations, 9 indicators were chosen, 
with several sub-indicators. However, working with only 9 indicators makes it challenging to cover 
every aspect of the topic and sustainability. The indicators must be specific and measurable, but 
should still answer the research question in the end. 
In this case, 3-3 indicators were chosen in each category (social, economic, environmental), to ensure 
sufficient coverage of the dimensions of sustainability and the problem statement. Some indicators 
==PAGE:62==
 
51 
 
 
Development of Indicators 
potentially could be placed in more than one category, as the sub-indicators can touch on different 
topics to get a more holistic picture. As the most important weakness of the domain-based framework 
is that: “it does not directly link indicators with sustainability goals” (McLaren, 1996, p. 190), a mix of 
domain- and goal-based framework was used. To define the sustainability goals clearly in the case of 
Lyngby and DTU, an ideal case of outcome was drawn up. 
The ultimate goal is to make Lyngby more attractive for students, but still a desired place to live for 
the non-students that currently live there. To establish a satisfying connection between DTU and 
Lyngby and in that way create an accessible and vibrant city centre by ensuring an easy, fast and 
convenient transportation to and from DTU. To create and keep a balanced demographic, where the 
majority of the young generation do not move out, but do not take over the city either. Living 
“together” should be beneficial for all stakeholders (students, locals, companies), and it should be a 
beneficial investment for the city in the long run. To be able to interest students in living in Lyngby, 
the housing and living expenses have to be affordable for a student. Last but not least, it is highly 
important to preserve the city’s historical, cultural and environmental identity, but still create a 
liveable, modern and sustainable city for everyone.

8.2 Scale of evaluation 
A scale was defined in order to be able to evaluate and compare the scenarios with each other. For 
each indicator and sub-indicator, a grade will be assigned on a scale ranging from -3 to 3, where the 
scores are going from worse outcome, unchanged or not applicable to better outcome as follows: 
• 
Score -3 represents that the change will lead to much worse circumstances. 
• 
Score -2 represents that the change will lead to worse circumstances. 
• 
Score -1 represents that the change will lead to slightly worse circumstances. 
• 
Score 0 represents that the circumstances will not change, or the indicator is not applicable. 
• 
Score 1 represents that the change will lead to slightly better circumstances. 
• 
Score 2 represents that the change will lead to better circumstances. 
• 
Score 3 represents that the change will lead to much better circumstances. 
The scores compare the change caused by the solution scenarios. Due to having mainly intangible 
indicators, the scores are not based on actual numbers but compare the scenario-driven changes to 
each other.

Scenario 1 does not have any direct influence on the apartment prices of Lyngby, therefore it is

not included (EDC, 2019).

11. Discussion and Conclusion 
Up until this point, the report had an analysis of the collected data, which allowed the sustainability 
assessment to be carried out. In the previous section, the three different solution scenarios and their 
evaluation were showcased to pinpoint areas of improvement for the Knowledge City project. These 
scenarios aimed to contribute to achieve the Lyngby-Taarbæk municipality’s and the City of 
Knowledge’s partnership vision of becoming a vibrant student-friendly university town. The report 
was created with a heavy student focus. 
Major findings of the study 
There are several major findings that can be derived from the different sections of the report. Based 
on the historical and theoretical background, DTU is in a special situation. DTU does not directly belong 
to any of the categories describing university and city relationships. This is mainly due to its long 
history with many relocations because of its continuous exponential growth in student numbers. DTU 
could be considered in the internalist perspective throughout its history, however with the advent of 
the City of Knowledge initiative, it definitely is moving towards an externalist perspective. The move 
towards this externalist trend is to be more involved with the city and for this reason, the solution 
scenarios need to be mindful of this trend. 
Internalist universities are made up of three major pillars provided in the same location: education, 
accommodation and free time activities. The City of Knowledge partnership and the Lyngby-Taarbæk 
municipality had several initiatives running in recent years with mixed results. All of these initiatives 
(Smart City Hub, Biketown Showdown) aimed to approach the students’ free time or educational 
activities. However, based on the primary data collection, the students’ perspectives and perception 
of Lyngby did not change significantly in recent years. A general consensus was that Lyngby is seen as 
a family friendly and elderly town, which does not spark interest for students. In order to solve this 
problem, the accommodation pillar would need to be changed, which are addressed by the three 
solution scenarios. 
Based on the sustainability assessment, the most beneficial next step would be a mix of the three 
solution scenarios. This means that in order to improve the current situation, the first solution scenario 
would mean improved communication via student preferred channels and in English next to Danish 
to account for DTUs’ international nature. The second solution scenario aims to create more 
student/youth apartments in the city centre to include students in the everyday life of the city. The 
youth accommodation would also mean that there is a long-term integration of the students into the 
area and that they do not have to leave their homes after graduation, which would be a life starter for 
==PAGE:89==

them. The last scenario, while not directly bringing more students into the city, can be thought of as 
an expansion, since the second scenario is limited by the relatively limited available space in Lyngby. 
Meaning of the findings and why the findings are important 
From the above-mentioned results and the analysis of the current situation of Lyngby, it can be safely 
stated that the area has the potential to become a student-friendly university town. Having said that, 
there are some challenges Lyngby has been facing in recent years and these include, but are not 
limited to, the price and availability of housing, especially for the arguably tight student budgets. 
Additionally, its perception of being a university town among the surveyed and interviewed students 
could be improved. While the students’ viewpoints were generally positive, critiques of housing, 
transportation, prices, events and social life are justified, even though they are being worked on 
continuously. The location of Lyngby being essentially in between DTU and Copenhagen also poses a 
challenge, as many students prefer the opportunities afforded by the capital. 
Taking the mixed-scenario approach would address most of these challenges or at least it would 
definitely be a step in the right direction for both the city and students. This would be highly important 
for all stakeholders in this area. Many resources have already been invested in this project, and while 
the DTU and the private companies have seen a resurgence, the local community is relatively left 
behind, but is no less important in order for Lyngby to achieve its long-term goals. This would also 
mean that the 6 pillars of a student-friendly city described by (Russo, van den Berg and Lavanga, 2007) 
would be directly addressed by the solution scenarios. Improving communication would definitely 
attract more students and increase the opportunities for contact between all stakeholders. Housing 
students and assisting them by keeping them linked to the city close to the university would be served 
by either solution scenarios 2 or 3, albeit to slightly different extents. 
To further highlight the importance of the three solution scenarios, it is worth taking another look at 
the impacts of student communities on cities as discussed by Russo et. al. in their paper “The Student 
City. Strategic Planning for Student Communities in EU Cities” (Russo, van den Berg and Lavanga, 
2003). This is important, because it bears implications for all relevant stakeholders in this area. In the 
current situation, the housing projects that are being built in Lyngby have limited number of student 
apartments. Increasing the number of student/youth apartments would bring many advantages to the 
city, which would outweigh the initial investment required. 
The advantages of involving students in the everyday life of the city revolve around four major aspects 
(Russo, van den Berg and Lavanga, 2003): economic development, housing market, infrastructure and 
internationalisation.

First and foremost, the City of Knowledge partnership already boosted the areas’ economic 
development to be one of the best in the country (Lyngby Taarbæk City of Knowledge, 2018b). 
Companies already exist and more are brought in to Lyngby every year. Students represent the future 
of citizens and workers, which means a direct economic benefit, as they are also one of the most 
potent human capital resources. If students live in Lyngby while they are studying, get a job in Lyngby 
after they graduate, and stay living, consuming and spending money in Lyngby. However, students are 
concentrated around DTU, so the city cannot gain the maximum benefits for its local shops and 
businesses. Providing the students with accommodation opportunities would further bolster 
economic development in the area.  
As discussed by Russo et. al. (2003), the accommodation of students has been solved mainly by 
campus facilities in the past. With the move towards an externalist perspective for DTU, dedicated 
low-cost structures need to be established in the city with potentially mixed building functions. This 
should not be seen as a disadvantage, but as the Executive Vice President of DTU Claus Nielsen said: 
“Housing for young people should not be seen as a cost. It should be seen as an investment.” (Claus 
Nielsen ‘KGS. Lyngby FUTURE’, 2016, p. 11).  
Students are also essential in this area, because they can be consumers of cultural and recreational 
products, while also being producers themselves. For instance, the area could see the inauguration of 
more local sport clubs and voluntary student events. Students also bring about cultural richness and 
increase the attractiveness of the urban environment by actively utilizing cultural facilities and 
services. This would elevate the cultural status of Lyngby and also diversify Lyngby’s demographic 
composition. These developments would put Lyngby on the map for students looking for an attractive 
and vibrant university town between DTU and Copenhagen. 
Limitations and considerations 
Some limitations of the project were initially defined in the problem description section. However, 
these focused on the limitations of the analysis of the gathered data or the sustainability assessment. 
Limitations of the solution provided are worth mentioning here, to provide the relevant stakeholders 
with the necessary understanding to improve their aspirations.  
Considering that the area is under constant development, the current assessment can only provide a 
snapshot of one possible future outcome. For instance, the new Light Rail project is a core element of 
the solution proposal, assuming it will solve most of the issues regarding transportation, availability 
and accessibility of Lyngby from DTU. Nonetheless, the Light Rail project is a relatively unknown factor 
in the project, as it is still in progress with several challenges and its predicted impact is purely 
==PAGE:91==
 
80 
 
 
Discussion and Conclusion 
hypothetical. While the connection that the Light Rail will establish is extremely beneficial to the area, 
it also means that other non-Lyngby areas will also benefit from this positive change in transportation 
opportunities. This could potentially mean that students will likely choose a living space in the 
surrounding areas due to the availability and because housing prices in these areas (e.g. Søborg) are 
currently much more student-friendly than in Lyngby. 
Future steps 
The six-point urban strategy as discussed by Russo et. al., (2003) needs to be fulfilled for creating a 
student-friendly city. Out of these six points, the aforementioned solution satisfies three: ‘attracting 
the students’, ‘housing the students’ and ‘increasing opportunities for contract’. The solution 
scenarios do not fulfil the other three points (‘assisting the students’, ‘empowering the students’ and 
‘keeping the students linked to the city’) as the first three points are prerequisites for these to happen. 
There is no empowering students and involving them in local issues, if they do not live there. In order 
to make Lyngby a truly sustainable university town on the long run, the remaining three points will 
also need to be addressed in the future. 
While this study provides a comprehensive analysis of the current situation, other further research 
could explore similar topics with, for instance a larger sample size of students. Additionally, as 
mentioned previously, the project being constantly in flux means that the situation can change within 
small timeframes. Moreover, exploring and comparing case studies of similar situations of university 
towns could prove beneficial by analysing how similar issues were solved in other cases. Having said 
that, this is an extremely difficult issue due to the complex nature of DTU and Lyngby’s situation and 
that is the main reason why these have been omitted from this report. From a more practical 
perspective, building on top of this analysis, an elaborate budgeting plan would need to be carried out 
along with the establishment of a timeframe, which could be integrated with the current Knowledge 
City plans. These steps would be required if these solution scenarios were to become reality.
