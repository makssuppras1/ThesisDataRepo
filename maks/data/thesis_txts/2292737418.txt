Abstract
The increasing impact of methane emissions from landﬁlls calls for action.
Where other
solutions are not economically or technically feasible, using biomitigation systems (e.g. bio-
covers) is almost maintenance free, very cheap and eﬃcient provided the temperature is
optimal. Therefore, especially in colder climates, it is important to ensure at the design
stage that the biocover will work. For this purpose modelling is an excellent tool.
The aim of this thesis is to present, compare and discuss main features characterising dif-
ferent modelling approaches. Analysis of the results include a discussion on the applicability
of the models for a biocover design, as well as the accuracy of the models in predicting the
observed data in a particular case study in Denmark.
Five one dimensional models with heat equation as the governing equation are included.
The comparison starts with the simplest analytic steady state model (1), followed by an an-
alytic time dependent model with constant energy term (2), with energy term varying with
time (3), and with energy term varying with depth (4). Finally, a numerical model done in
COMSOL Multiphysics v.4.4 is considered (5). Models (2) and (3) assume periodic ﬂuctu-
ation of both temperature and the energy term. The solution to model (4) is derived from
eigenfunctions and Fourier series for heat equation with homogeneous boundary conditions.
Model (5) couples three equations: temperature (via heat equation), with methane and oxy-
gen concentrations (via Monod kinetics and Fick’s law). This model assumes diﬀusion as the
only transport process.
All models are validated using observation data collected at a built-for-purpose biocover
system at AV Miljø landﬁll in Copenhagen, Denmark. The biocover was fed by dilute landﬁll
gas pumped out of the adjacent leachate wells. The total pump rate and gas composition
were measured to control the gas load to the biocover. The fact that atmospheric air was
part of the gas mixture is also discussed in the paper.
The steady state solution indicated that the initial value assumed for thermal conductivity
was too conservative, this was adjusted with a correction factor. Models (2) and (3) required
==PAGE:8==
iv
no other correction factors and they were insensitive to changes in uncertain parameters (heat
capacity and density). Eigenfunctions in model (4) had to be scaled appropriately. Finally,
model (5) required correction factor on the terms related to Monod kinetics.
The increasing complexity of the analytic models resulted in an increasingly good ﬁt: the
R2 factor increased from 0.72 for model (1), through 0.90 for model (2), 0.92 for model (3)
to 0.95 for model (4). For model (5) the goodness of the ﬁt was slightly lower, 0.92. At the
same time, model (5) was superior in predicting the lower temperatures, which especially for
colder climates is the main criterion of success.
Our analysis conﬁrms that the models are applicable for biocover design, as they can
predict the correct trends. In theory model (5) can also be used to model speciﬁc scenarios,
such as: increased methane ﬂux from the landﬁll; inﬂuence of moisture content; contribution
of compost respiration to the total energy balance; density of the microbial population;
the consequences of surface temperature on the ﬁrst day of operation on the temperature
development inside the biocover.
The results indicate the installation time has profound inﬂuence on the future operation;
if the biocover is installed during the winter, it can take months before it is fully operating,
and it is probable that the microbial community will die in the latent phase. Hence this
aspect should be designed with care in colder climates. When modelling higher ﬂuxes the
model failed to illustrate the correct trends: no methane was oxidised in the biocover, and it
was only transported to the surface via diﬀusion. This suggests there is a bug in the model.
Contribution of soil respiration and methane oxidation could not be quantiﬁed, but it is clear
that both processes are necessary to maintain optimal temperature throughout the year.
As exempliﬁed by the case study, analytic and numerical models have their strengths
which should be utilised at the design stage. Moreover, the performance of the system can
be aﬀected by many factors, where temperature is among the most important ones.
Key words
biocover design, landﬁll, methane emissions, analytic/numerical modelling,
heat equation

Chapter 1
Introduction
A somewhat unforeseen consequence of development and globalisation is waste; rich countries
produce more waste per capita than poor countries, and the socioeconomic status correlates
well with the amount of waste generated. By lifting whole regions of the world out of poverty,
we witness the beginning of a hitherto unseen era of consumerism and waste generation —
by some estimates the amount of waste that is generated globally will triple before 2100
[Hoornweg et al., 2013].
Coping with this growing problem is not only a question of how to reduce the generation
rate, or how to handle the stream of waste, but also how to mitigate the environmental impact.
The waste sector accounts today for 23±4% of total emissions of methane from anthropogenic
sources [Stocker et al., 2013]. Locally, high methane concentrations can increase explosion
risks. Globally, they contribute to climate changes; considering it is 25 times more potent
than carbon dioxide, the forecasts for the next century do not look very optimistic.
In that respect methane emissions from landﬁlls are of major concern. It is unrealistic to
expect that all waste can be diverted elsewhere in the waste handling system; this requires a
major behavioural change for consumers, but also investment capital many countries simply
do not have. Likewise, collecting and utilising methane emitted from landﬁlls is a tempting
idea, but for many reasons it is not an ideal solution. Firstly, collection systems are diﬃcult

Biomitigation technologies, on the other hand, have shown very promising results where

Experience from three studies at Danish

with active population of bacteria which can oxidise methane to carbon dioxide — are almost
maintenance-free, cheap and eﬃcient [Cassini, 2013; Pedersen et al., 2011; Scheutz et al.,

emissions. Therefore, it is important to ensure at the design stage that the biocover will
work. For this purpose modelling is an excellent tool.
The aim of this thesis is to develop suitable models which can be used at the design
stage of biocovers, but also to predict future emissions from landﬁlls with biocovers already
installed. The models are both analytic and numerical and increase in the level of complexity;
common to all of them is their focus on temperature development inside the biocover.
All models are validated using observation data collected at a built-for-purpose biocover
system at AV Miljø landﬁll in Copenhagen, Denmark. The biocover was fed by dilute landﬁll
gas pumped out of the adjacent leachate wells. The total pump rate and gas composition
were measured to control the gas load to the biocover. The fact that atmospheric air was
part of the gas mixture is also discussed in the thesis.
Chapter 2 describes all elements of the landﬁll and of the biocover system. It ﬁnishes with
a brief review of the modelling approaches to date. Chapter 3 provides the mathematical
formulation of the analytic model, Chapter 4 describes the numerical solution. Both chap-

simpliﬁcations and assumptions used. In Chapters 5 and 6 results of the diﬀerent simulations
are discussed. The discussion include comments on applicability of the models for biocover

Gas distribution layer
Usually, the gas distribution layer, or GDL, would be placed directly over the waste mass.
It is made of various materials, such as glass cullet, gravel or ﬂuorescent light bulbs [Abichou
et al., 2009; Scheutz et al., 2009]. The common feature to all GDL’s is the coarse material of
which they are made, which enables easy gas transport and, at least in theory, a homogeneous
gas distribution across the entire surface. The GDL system can be passive or active. In the
former case the gas moves naturally upwards due to advection, in the latter case it is being
transported using mechanical pumps.
The sole purpose of the GDL is to distribute the gas evenly to the biocover and thus to
avoid emission hotspots; if a part of a biocover is overloaded with a high methane ﬂux, the
microbes will not have enough time to transform the gas to carbon dioxide. However, it is
not uncommon to ﬁnd information in the literature about the methane oxidation happening
already in the GDL [Huber-Humer, 2004]. This could happen particularly on the boundary

4
Biocover
The purpose of this layer is to facilitate chemical and biological reactions which transform
unwanted compounds into some less polluting compounds. Most importantly, the methan-
otrophs living in soil or compost can convert methane to carbon dioxide and thus limit the
extent of the global warming potential of the landﬁlls, and decrease local explosion risks.
Other advantages include conversion of volatile organic compounds or retention of water,
[Christensen, 2011, Chapter 10.9] and [Molins et al., 2008]. Typically, the biocover (or the
soil layer in the top cover) will have no more than 1.5 m in thickness; the dimensions are
dependant on several factors, where temperature, methane inﬂux and oxygen penetration
are the most important [Lechner et al., 2002].
Some studies have shown that at low methane ﬂuxes normal top cover is suﬃcient to
oxidise methane [Abichou et al., 2009]. The eﬃciency of the top cover — even at low ﬂuxes
— varies during a year, for it depends on water content, ambient air temperature and other
factors. Therefore, on many sites a mature compost is used instead.
The reason to use mature compost instead of natural subsoil is the more active micro-
bial community, which is furthermore specialised in converting methane to carbon dioxide.
Moreover, the microbes need very particular conditions to thrive — this includes the amount
of nutrients such as nitrogen, potassium and phosphorus; moisture content; oxygen content;
temperature; salt content; concentration of heavy metals; pH — and by using mature compost
one ensures that all those preliminary conditions are satisﬁed and optimised. Additionally,
Gebert et al. [2011] have shown that compaction plays an important role in the oxidation
process (high compaction implies smaller pore size and higher degree of discontinuity of pores,
which both impede gas transport). In this study it was estimated that the air-ﬁlled porosity
should be at least 0.14 of the total volume, for lower values air-ﬁlled porosity can be one of
the limiting factors of methane oxidation.

Microbial community
The methanotrophs present in compost are strict aerobes, meaning that oxygen is the limiting
factor for their activity; the reaction rates are probably most sensitive to oxygen supply, and
for that reason it is usually the top 20–30 cm of the compost layer which experiences the
highest microbial activity [Scheutz et al., 2009]. Oxygen concentration falls drastically below
this depth, and so does the microbial activity. For that particular reason it is also preferred to
use mature compost instead of fresh organic material, as the decomposition process in fresh
compost can take up all the oxygen which should have been used for methane oxidation.
Although the decomposition process in mature compost is much slower than in fresh
compost, it is not absent altogether; compost respiration will cause temperature increase in
the biocover. Stoichiometry and the energy generated will depend on a particular type of
biomass, but in general the reaction will follow the scheme proposed by Christensen [2011,
Equation (9.1.1)]:
biomass + O2 −−→H2O + CO2 + new biomass + heat,
(2.3)
The type of biomass and general conditions in compost will also determine the character
of microbial community.
Broadly speaking, there are two main types of methanotrophs
present in compost, type I and type II. There are naturally some exceptions, but in general
the division comes from the enzymes the bacteria use in methane oxidation. All bacteria
are capable of producing the pMMO enzyme (particulate monooxygenases), which can only
oxidise methane under copper-rich conditions. In case of copper-stress the oxidation potential
of type I bacteria decreases, whereas type II synthesises another enzyme (sMMO, soluble
MMO) which enables methane oxidation. The cells containing the sMMO enzyme usually
grow slower than the ones containing the pMMO enzyme.
Moreover, type II bacteria is
capable of N2-ﬁxing (i.e. using nitrogen from atmospheric air in the metabolic processes),
whereas type I is not. Finally, type II thrives in conditions characterised by high methane
concentrations and low oxygen concentrations, whereas the opposite holds for type I [Scheutz
et al., 2009]. As a consequence, under nitrogen and copper-rich conditions type I bacteria tend
to dominate, whereas in case of either copper or nitrogen-stress type II bacteria dominate.

Temperature
Each community has an optimal temperature interval in which the reaction rates peak.
Some studies estimate the optimal temperature to lie between 25 and 35 ◦C [Scheutz et al.,
2009], other studies suggest that the peak activity is reached for much higher temperatures,
around 50 ◦C [De Schoenmaeker, 2013]. The activity proﬁle is asymmetric with respect to
temperature (it decreases very fast past the optimum). Some methanotrophs, but only of
type I, can adapt to lower temperatures [Scheutz et al., 2009]. This also implies that type I
will dominate in places where the average annual temperature is lower.

A sub-optimal design of the biocover can result in local rapid increase of temperature.
When methane is warmed up, it will rise to the surface, and if the temperature gradient is
high enough, the gas will advance at a faster pace than the microbes can convert it to carbon
dioxide. This in turn creates a pressure gradient which further reinforces the eﬀect (more gas
is sucked in to the warm area, warmed up, and elevated before it can be converted). This
eﬀect is known as “chimney eﬀect” and is mostly associated with composting windrows.
Transport processes
Yet another factor aﬀecting methane oxidation is a homogeneous gas distribution. In dis-
tributing gas three physical processes play a role: advection, diﬀusion and dispersion. Ad-

vection is a movement of particles induced by a total pressure gradient; diﬀusion is a particle
movement induced by diﬀerences in concentrations (partial pressures) of the particular gas;
and ﬁnally, dispersion is a random movement of particles in all directions which is caused by
turbulence of the medium in which the gas is immersed (usually another gas).
The order of magnitude of these diﬀerent transport processes depends on diﬀerent factors:
• For small pore size Knudsen diﬀusion can be important (Knudsen diﬀusion occurs when
the free molecule path is longer than the pore diameter, which causes the molecules to
collide with the pore walls, rather than with other molecules).
• For high total pressure gradients advection can be important.
• Considering that CO2 is much more soluble in water than CH4, it is likely that all, or
a lot of carbon dioxide generated from methane oxidation, will diﬀuse in water-ﬁlled
pores causing a higher pressure gradient compared to the surface.
• From the stoichiometry of the reaction, 3 moles of gas (one mole of methane and two
moles of oxygen) are transformed to less than one mole of gas (assuming that part of
the CO2 will dissolve in water), which creates a void in the air-ﬁlled pores and increases
the rate of advection.
Inhibitors
Diﬀerent phenomena can inhibit the microbial growth: water content, salt, trace metals, or
the exopolymeric substances, also known as EPS. EPS are sugars used by bacteria to anchor
inside the pores, and it has been observed that in case of carbon excess (e.g. if the methane
ﬂux is too high), the EPS are produced in high amounts and can as a consequence clog the
pores [Scheutz et al., 2009]. Hence it is important to maintain the methane ﬂux at a suitable
level.

Landﬁll
At AV Miljø the waste is characterised by its low organic content. It consists mainly of

it will work until the end of 2017 [AV Miljø, 2013].
The landﬁll is divided into 22 cells and 10 sectors (see Appendix B, Figure B.1 for details).
The thickness of the waste layer in each cell is around 5.5 m. There is no synthetic bottom

2.3
Aim and objectives
Despite the general consensus that biocovers can be an attractive alternative to other mit-
igation technologies, there is still very few mathematical models which could facilitate new
designs and deepen our understanding of the processes involved. Some of the early works,
for instance by De Visscher and Van Cleemput [2003], were only validated by laboratory
experiments. Other used a combination of ﬁeld data and constant environmental conditions
[Abichou et al., 2011]. Finally, in some of the latest models, [Abichou et al., 2011] in par-
ticular, the authors developed correction factors for the reaction rates to account for the
non-homogeneous conditions in the ﬁeld. And yet, considering the importance of tempera-
ture on the oxidation eﬃciency, it is surprising that to the best of our knowledge, no model
has been developed that would couple equations for methane and temperature and at the
same time used ﬁeld data rather than laboratory data to adjust the parameters and validate
the model.
Our models are hoped to ﬁll this obvious gap; all models presented in this thesis have
temperature-dependence and hence can be used to model seasonal changes. Furthermore, the
most complex numerical model described in Chapter 4 couples both temperature, methane
and oxygen concentrations. And ﬁnally, the models were validated with ﬁeld observations
from AV Miljø. As such the models presented here are probably the most complex models
of methane oxidation in the biocover to date.

Analytic solution
Analytic solutions to PDE’s give exact values, rather than estimates, yet not all PDE’s have
an analytic solution, and this is particularly true for variable initial and boundary conditions.
In such cases numerical solution is the only viable alternative.
Even if an exact solution to a particular equation does not exist, solving a simpliﬁed
analytic model can still give an important insight into the processes involved. The model
is fast and easy to understand and as such it can describe general trends and identify the
important parameters.
The rest of this chapter describes how the analytic solution is obtained; it also describes
the boundary conditions. The section ﬁnishes with a complete list of all parameters used
(Table 3.3). Results and discussion of the model are included in Chapter 5.

Transport processes
Advection
Advection is a movement of particles due to pressure gradient. In physical
systems advection is responsible only for a fraction of the total transport; Binning et al.
[2007] conclude that about 25% of total oxygen in pyrite oxidation layers is transported
by advection. On the other hand, Pokhrel et al. [2011] argue that high pressure gradients,
which are primary drivers for advection, are unlikely to occur in landﬁll biocovers. This
result correlates well with the previous estimates from Molins et al. [2008], where simulations
conducted after laboratory experiments suggest that 99% of gas transport is by diﬀusion.
Hence advection is excluded from our model. For mathematical description of advection, as
well as tentative results from models where advection is included, refer to Appendix E.
Diﬀusion and dispersion
The general term dispersion refers to the sum of two diﬀerent
processes: mechanical dispersion (or mixing) and molecular diﬀusion, they will be called
dispersion and diﬀusion in the rest of the document. These two processes, although gov-
erned by diﬀerent physical phenomena (see also Section 2.1, 4 Biocover), can be described
collectively by one mathematical equation, namely:

Dispersion coeﬃcient
When the gas moves through porous medium, the grain size and
distribution of pores forces the gas to diverge from the main direction of ﬂow. The change
in gas concentration due to this movement is mechanical dispersion.
Dispersion can dominate over diﬀusion in some velocity regimes; if the gas velocity is very
low, diﬀusion dominates, whereas the opposite holds true for high velocities. At the micro-
scopic scale the velocity is so low that the dispersion term tends to be neglected altogether.
De Visscher and Van Cleemput [2003] quote the threshold velocity to be 1 m/day (dispersion
can become important for velocities above this value). This, however, is unlikely to happen
in landﬁll cover soils. Moreover, Binning et al. [2007] estimated that gas transport in an un-
saturated zone was mainly governed by diﬀusion, whereas dispersion was at times 100 times
smaller. Similar approach is assumed in our model, and thus Ddisp = 0 and Dtot = Ddiﬀ. If,
despite our predictions, dispersion happened to be important in the biocover, Appendix E
describes the appropriate modelling approach.

and how the solution looks. Hence it is interesting to examine how the model is aﬀected by
changing the conditions.
Current boundary conditions assume known temperature on the surface (Dirichlet BC
at x = 0) and known ﬂux at the bottom (Neumann BC at x = L). The fact that it is
generally challenging to measure the temperature at the bottom of the biocover justiﬁes the
use of Neumann BC at that point. Furthermore, ﬁeld measurements undertaken by Bonany
et al. [2013] prove that waste in a landﬁll can keep constant negative temperature for up to
18 months, which only reinforces the assumption of zero ﬂux (i.e very good insulation) at
the bottom of the biocover. Hence there is little freedom regarding the choice of BC at the
bottom.
On the surface of the biocover the question remains as for which surface temperature one
should choose; there is otherwise no doubt that it has to be a Dirichlet BC, otherwise the
problem is ill-posed. Recall Figure 3.2 which compares the best ﬁt (i.e. the BC used in the
model) to the observed temperatures. Measured temperature can ﬂuctuate for up to 10 K
almost overnight and therefore, depending on the applications, best ﬁt may not necessarily be
the best choice; if the main interest lies in modelling the average case, best ﬁt is appropriate.

5.2.2

There are two interpretations of a time dependant energy term: ﬁrstly, it can correspond to
the energy from compost respiration and its variation in time. Secondly, it can correspond
to variable methane inﬂux at the bottom of the biocover; if we assume Em from Equation
(3.5) is dependant on t and ﬂuctuates periodically, the same will hold for the energy term
S(t).

then pumped into the GDL. This gives a lot of freedom for high and irregular ﬂuctuations.
For example, Cassini [2013] points out that the amount of methane released from the landﬁll
is well correlated with the atmospheric pressure, as the gas transport is driven by pressure
gradients. Moreover, there is always a risk of leaking pumps and valves in this setup.

5.3
Final remarks
Recall that the strength of analytic models lies in their transparency. From a perspective
of a non-modeller, or even a layman, working with analytic models can help understand
the system in question. Moreover, having seen the exact solution, it is easier to predict the
behaviour of the model given diﬀerent design scenarios or diﬀerent values of the parameters
used. Finally, analytic models can provide a fast way of verifying whether the parameters
used are correct or whether a model is sensitive to changes in key parameters. In all these
respects, analytic models are, or can be, superior to numerical models.
Our analytic models include a number of simpliﬁcations; most notably, eﬀect of moisture
content on various parameters such as thermal conductivity and speciﬁc heat capacity is
disregarded. Yet despite this limitation they provide a good ﬁt to observed data and the ﬁt
improves with higher degree of complexity (steady state, time dependant case with constant
S, with S(t) and ﬁnally with S(x)). Table 5.2 lists the diﬀerent models and their R2–values
including the two cases with varying boundary conditions denoted as area. Hence we can
conclude that the models represent well the processes in the biocover even despite their
relative simplicity as compared to the numerical model.

Assessing the biocover performance was hitherto only possible in a very indirect way; by
looking at the temperature proﬁles obtained in the analytic model we could see whether
the temperature remained above a certain threshold (which we assumed to be 25 ◦C). We
claimed that since the temperature condition was satisﬁed, the biocover was probably working
properly.
At the same time, we had no information about how much methane is being
oxidised, or whether oxidation happens in the ﬁrst place (recall that the energy term was
dependant on neither methane nor oxygen concentrations).
The numerical model in Multiphysics provides the missing elements of the picture: on
top of calculating the temperature proﬁles, it gives concentration proﬁles of the two gases
in question, as well as it can calculate the methane emissions to the atmosphere. In the
remaining part of this chapter we will analyse the results of the numerical model and discuss
their implications for the biocover performance.
Similarly to the analytic model, some correction factors were necessary for the model to
work, these are described in Section 6.1. Section 6.2 discusses the consequences of the surface
temperature on the ﬁrst day of operation for the future performance. Sections 6.3 and 6.4

6.2
Start of operation
One of the main motivations behind the numerical model is the possibility of assessing dif-
ferent design scenarios, whether they include diﬀerent depths, climate patterns, or methane
inﬂux. As we will show in this section, the ﬁrst day of operation of a biocover is equally
important as all the other design parameters.
Figure 6.2 illustrates temperature proﬁles for the ﬁrst year of operation of the biocover
for two starting days: January 1 and June 1. If the biocover is laid on the landﬁll at the
beginning of the year, it can take even ﬁve months before it “warms up”. If, on the other
hand, the biocover is set during the summer, it immediately has the right temperature.
The consequences of the initial temperature are profound. Although it may seem that the
eﬀectiveness of the biocover is the same in both cases — as soon as the biocover “warms up”
the temperature ranges are comparable — this is only due to the simpliﬁed version of our
model. Recall we assumed the microbial population to be constant throughout the biocover,
and the density did not depend on temperature. In reality those several months it takes for
the biocover to reach suitable temperature is most likely going to be deadly to most of the
methanotrophs.
The simplest way to avoid killing the microbial ﬂora in compost before the operation
starts, is to wait until the summer months. If, for any reasons, the biocover needs to be

1. Bugs
At the moment the primary issue with the model is the problem with reaction
rates and methane oxidation. Finding the reason for this (possibly a bug) should have an
outermost priority.
2. Moisture and pressure
In our model we assume that both atmospheric pressure and
moisture content remain constant. Cassini [2013] points out that both of these parameters
will have a direct inﬂuence on methane oxidation. As Figure 6.7 illustrates, there is a clear
correlation between the moisture content at diﬀerent depths and precipitation. There is al-
most no lag time between high rain events and increased moisture content. Moreover, we can
see that moisture remains almost constant closer to the surface, whereas the ﬂuctuations are
much higher at greater depths. As moisture aﬀects many diﬀerent parameters — permeabil-
ity, porosity, thermal conductivity — it seems reasonable to adjust the model with the values
of those parameters as a function of water content (which furthermore will vary throughout
the biocover).
Similar observations were also made by Cassini [2013] with respect to pressure. The author
noticed that methane emissions increased whenever the atmospheric pressure dropped. This
would suggest that changing pressure can induce advection and thus aﬀect transport processes
inside the biocover.
3. Transport processes
In the current model advection and dispersion are not included at
all, whereas diﬀusion assumes temperature independent diﬀusion coeﬃcient. Depending on
the pressure gradients, adding advection can potentially become the most inﬂuential process,
closely followed by temperature dependant diﬀusion.
More information about transport
processes can be found in Appendix E. The section about advection includes both theory
and tentative results from Multiphysics.

4. Microbial community
There are many diﬀerent aspects related to the methanotrophs
in the biocover. Most notably, adding equation for B — i.e. the microbes — would imme-
diately improve the model. But there are also more subtle changes that can be done. For
instance, we have not considered a possible oxidation in the gas distribution layer. Neither
did we discuss a preferential oxidation; that is, how is oxygen divided between the two heat
generating processes, or whether there are other processes or organisms in the biocover which
use oxygen in their metabolism without oxidising methane at the same time.

Conclusions
The main objective of this work was to develop and analyse modelling tools which could
satisfy needs of future biocover designers and engineers, modellers and non-modellers alike.
The innovative element in this modelling approach included coupling multiple equations into
one numerical model, as well as focus on temperature, rather than methane concentration.
We have discussed diﬀerent analytic and numerical models of heat and gas transfer in
biocovers. The analytic models included solution to steady state, as well as solutions to
time dependant case with three diﬀerent energy terms: constant, time dependant and depth
dependant. In all models heat equation was the governing equation. In numerical model in
Multiphysics three equations were coupled: temperature via heat equation with methane and
oxygen concentrations via Monod kinetics and Fick’s law. Diﬀusion was the only transport
process included. All models were validated with ﬁeld data from a built-for-purpose biocover
system at AV Miljø landﬁll in Copenhagen.
The main motivation behind using both analytic and numerical models lies in their com-
plementary features; analytic models allow for a fast and easy to understand analysis of
the processes included. Because the models are usually very simpliﬁed, they help focus on
particular parameters or problems. Numerical models, on the other hand, although they are
much more diﬃcult to implement, once established allow for a multivariable analysis where
complicated boundary and initial conditions can be considered.
Analytic models showed increasingly good ﬁt with increasing complexity, the ﬁt to numeri-
80

cal model was not the highest, yet the model was superior at predicting the lower temperature
ranges which is particularly important in colder climates. Despite their simpliﬁcations the
models predicted the observed data well which proves their usability. Having said that, nu-

behave as expected for methane ﬂuxes higher than what was observed at AV Miljø. Once
these problems are solved, the models can hopefully be used in a variety of contexts.

Appendix A
Summary of the physcial, chemical and
biological phenomena in a
landﬁll–biocover system
The list included in this appendix summarises physical, chemical and biological phenomena
which can be considered and included in the mathematical model, and which were described
in Chapter 2.
1. Landﬁll
1. Waste type: it will determine the methane production potential. It can also be used
to predict when the emission peak is going to occur (easily degradable waste will reach
the emission peak much faster).
2. Amount of waste: as in the previous point, this parameter is necessary to estimate the
methane emission potential.
3. Possible escapes paths of methane: the amount of methane escaping through lateral
cracks in bottom liners, or through the leachate collection system will determine how
much methane can be transformed in the biocover.

Appendix A. Summary of the physcial, chemical and biological . . .
4. The temperature in the landﬁll: this can potentially be important when deﬁning the
boundary conditions for the biocover. If the temperature diﬀerence between the landﬁll
and the biocover is big, a heat ﬂux through the bottom boundary of the biocover can
occur.
5. Landﬁll gas concentration: in particular, the concentration of methane in the landﬁll
gas.
6. Time scale: the choice of parameters (and as a consequence, the results of the model)
will depend on the time scale considered. For instance, if we assume the biocover is put
simultaneously with the waste, the temperature of the waste mass will vary with time.
Furthermore, if we only consider the waste in the methanogenic phase, it will aﬀect
the emission potential (as some methane would have already been emitted before this
phase).
2. Top cover
1. Possible escapes paths of methane: the amount of methane that reaches the biocover
directly from the top cover (and thus circumventing the pumping system in the GDL),
as well as the amount of methane escaping through lateral paths and not reaching the
biocover at all.
2. Heat conduction: the amount of heat transferred from the landﬁll to the biocover
through the top cover needs to be assessed. Moreover, for the purpose of the modelling
top cover has to be characterised either as a heat insulator or heat conductor, depending
on its properties.
3. The thickness of the top cover: heat conduction — if any — will depend on this
parameter. And so will the methane ﬂux towards the biocover.
4. Water content: if moisture content in this layer is aﬀected by the precipitation, this will
imply that the leachate collection system in the gas distribution layer is not eﬀective
enough to prevent any water from percolating to the lower parts of the landﬁll. If the
==PAGE:104==
89
water content in the top cover is high, it can aﬀect the amount of gas migrating from
the landﬁll (if there is any).
3. Gas distribution layer
1. Moisture content: it is important to control the water content in the GDL to ensure
a smooth (i.e. clog-free) path for the gas to the biocover. If the water builds up on
the boundary, or deeper in the GDL, this can hinder the gas, make the distribution
in-homogeneous, or force the gas to ﬁnd other escape paths.
2. Porosity (air-ﬁlled and water-ﬁlled): porosity is the free space available for gas or water
transport. It is therefore important to estimate how much of the void is ﬁlled with
water, and whether the air-ﬁlled spaces are distributed evenly (otherwise this may
cause problems in the gas distribution).
3. Flow rate and methane concentration (in the inlet, and in the upper parts of the GDL).
4. Pressure: the pressure gradient will determine the advection rates, it is therefore im-
portant to know how the pressure is distributed within the GDL.
5. Oxygen concentration in the inlet: oxygen is known to be the limiting factor in methane
oxidation, and hence the oxidation will surely be enhanced by the fact that the oxygen
is also pumped from below. How much it helps will depend obviously on how much is
pumped in.
6. Methane transported from the top cover: most of the methane entering the GDL comes
from the leachate wells from the waste mass underneath the biocover, but some gas
could have also escaped through the top cover. This amount can play a role in the gas
distribution within the GDL.
7. Microbial activity in the GDL: if a bioﬁlm is present in the GDL, it is important to
assess how active the microbes are. That is, how much methane can be transformed
before the gas even reaches the biocover.

4. Biocover
1. The amount of inhibitors, especially heavy metals in the biocover.
2. The production of EPS with time and how it can aﬀect the microbial growth.
3. The magnitude of compost respiration and how it develops with time.
4. The magnitude of methane oxidation and how it develops with time.
5. The water content and how it develops with time. Or inversely, the magnitude of air-
ﬁlled porosity versus time. Water-ﬁlled pores slow down diﬀusivity, block the oxygen
pathways, and increase fugitive emissions. On the other hand, water stress inhibits the
microbial growth. Therefore, water content has to be monitored at all times.
6. Multiple gas model: the relationship between methane, oxygen and carbon dioxide is
obvious, as they are all included in the methane oxidation process. But there can also
be other gases, such as nitrogen, which will aﬀect the results (e.g. by forcing advective
ﬂuxes towards the surface). The role of other gases should be investigated.
7. If chimney eﬀect is possible in the biocover, its extend and duration should be evaluated.
8. Moving oxidation front: knowing that the microbial activity is dependant on tempera-
ture, it could happen, that the oxidation front will move deeper into the soil when the
ambient air temperature drops. If the change is considerable, moving front boundary
should be used in the modelling.
9. Transport processes: advection, dispersion and diﬀusion will have diﬀerent role and
magnitude depending on the particular conditions. Their role in this particular model
needs to be analysed.
10. Methanotrophs: the growth rate of the microbial community and how it develops with
time.
11. The dependency of the methanotrophic activity on methane and oxygen concentrations.

12. CO2-solubility: how much it dissolves in water, and how important this eﬀect is for the
overall reaction kinetics.
5. Atmosphere
1. Prevailing winds (strength, frequency and direction).
2. Barometric pressure.
3. Precipitation (type, amount).
4. Vegetation cover.
5. Temperature.
6. Air humidity (can aﬀect heat transfer).
7. The magnitude of the diﬀerent heat transfer processes between the surface and the
biocover (convection, conduction, radiation).
8. Albedo of the surface (will determine how much of the solar radiation is absorbed).

E.2

In broad terms, dispersion is divided into two components: longitudinal dispersion, which de-
scribes the mixing along the direction of the path, and transverse dispersion, which describes
the mixing in directions normal to the ﬂow path. Bear and Cheng [2010] mention that the

transverse dispersion is 8 to 24 times smaller than the longitudinal dispersion.
The porous medium aﬀects the velocity, as well as the magnitude of the two dispersive
components in three diﬀerent ways:
Longitudi-

Transverse:
turbulent
1. Pore size: velocity is higher when the gas moves through bigger pores.
2. Path length: pore distribution in not uniform, meaning that some paths are longer than
others.
3. Friction: the middle of the pores is characterised by lower friction compared to the
sides. This stretches the gas front in a u–shaped form.
One of the methods of modelling dispersion requires exact information about the change of
velocity in space and time. This, however, is both impossible and impractical in complex
systems due to the three factors mentioned above.

Appendix E. Transport processes
Permeability κ is likely to vary depending on the direction (x, y, z), the geological structure
of the material etc. In such cases κ is a tensor, yet here — for simplicity reasons — it is
assumed that the permeability is homogeneous in all directions (the medium is isotropic),
and hence κ is constant.
Velocity in the advective transport is dependant on the total pressure of the gas, and not
on partial pressures of the individual components — we need information about all species
in order to calculate the pressure gradient. In our simpliﬁed model we only keep track of
oxygen and methane concentrations, whereas we have no information about carbon dioxide
and nitrogen. Therefore, we estimate the pressure gradient from the bottom ﬂux.
Firstly, let us assume that all bottom ﬂux is due to advection. We can see in Figures 4.5
(right panel) and 4.7 that bottom ﬂuxes for methane and oxygen (Qm and Qo) are within the
same ranges, and as there are four gases in the mixture we let Q = 4 · Qm = 140 g/(m2 · day)
(here we take the maximum of Qm). Hence the equation for ∇P becomes:
