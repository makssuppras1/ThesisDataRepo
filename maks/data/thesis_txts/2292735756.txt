Abstract
From the recent extreme cloudbursts striking Denmark and causing major flood damage, the
necessity to improve urban drainage system design arose. Climate change projection, together with
population support have made the resilience of cities to climate change one of the main priorities for
urban planning. These changes, inducing larger extreme rain events, are expected to cause more
frequent and possibly more costly floods, especially in densely developed urban area such as
Copenhagen. To cope with those upcoming challenges, Copenhagen Municipality has developed a
comprehensive climate change adaptation plan, based on extension of underground pipe network,
infiltration and local storage of runoff. 
In this study, the efficiency of the adaptation plan in relation to pluvial flooding for the catchment of
Nørrebro is analysed. A socio-economic flood risk assessment is performed to evaluate the expected
reduced flood risk considering climate change adaptations. Flood hazard is simulated by a 1D/2D
coupled hydraulic model and flood vulnerability is estimated from insurance data. Flood risk is
obtained by combining hazard and vulnerability assessments, and it is expressed in monetary terms
as Expected Annual Damage (EAD). Investment costs of adaptation measures are compared to
induced benefits. They are integrated into the Net Present Value (NPV) over an observation period
going from 2015 to 2110.
It is found that, in the presence of climate change and under the assumptions of the analysis,
Nørrebro adaptation plan becomes beneficial after approximately 50 years, which corresponds to
half of the observation period. However, the assessment is subject to several uncertainties such as
those regarding climate forecast, damage estimation, construction costs evaluation and discounting
of future benefits. A sensitivity analysis  is performed to quantify the relative importance of
uncertainties  underlying  some  of  the  parameters.  Discount  rate  and  private-public  share  in
investment costs appear to be the most sensitive and uncertain parameters. Moreover, damage
reduction  induced  by adaptation  measures  was  not  as  large  as  expected,  maybe  due  to  the
dominance of green solutions in the adaptation plan.

1. Introduction
Precipitation pattern is expected to be significantly impacted by climate change in a 50-100 years
horizon. It will induce serious consequences on sewer system load, leading to possible high flood
damage cost especially in densely populated cities  (Freni et al. 2010). Therefore anticipation of
change should be integrated in urban drainage planning. Climate change will come gradually over a
long time horizon, but their extent and how quick they will occur are subject to uncertainties.
Mishandled assessment could lead to under-scaled and insufficient adaptation measures or on the
opposite over-scaled and unnecessary ones  (Danish Energy Agency 2008). Comprehensive and
continuously adjusted analysis, accounting for consequences of climate change, likelihood of
occurrence and costs of prevention, are needed to optimize investments in adaptation initiatives.
Studies have shown that a risk-based approach is a good method to address increased flood risk
(Messner et al. 2007). Flood risk is thus assessed as exposure of vulnerability to hazard. This has
been facilitated by technological progress in modelling and rising awareness amongst public
opinion. Indeed, the recent extreme precipitation events in Denmark have enhanced the public
recognition of the necessity to adapt urban planning to future climate (Olsen et al. 2015).
Moreover,  flood protection measures  are partly financed by public funds, therefore they are
competing  with other  society's  needs  such as  health,  security or transportation  (Plate 2002).
Therefore, efficiency and synergy effects with urban planning should be maximized. For instance, it
is important to coordinate drainage adaptation with other renovation projects (Zhou 2012) or to take
into account the long lifetime of sewer systems, 50 to 100 years (Copenhagen Municipality 2011),
and make them suitable for future climate (Olsen et al. 2015).
In literature, climate change impacts on flood risk in Danish cities have been assessed, together with
possible adaptations, regarding both sea level rise (Hallegatte, Ranger, et al. 2011) and urban pluvial
flood  (Zhou, Halsnæs, et al. 2012). Similar studies have also been conducted in other countries
(Ranger et al. 2011). They are based on different frameworks  (Hallegatte, Henriet, et al. 2011;
Zhou, Mikkelsen, et al. 2012), but follow overall the same steps. First hazard assessment to identify
the probability and extent of flooding, then vulnerability assessment to describe potential adverse
effects caused by exposure of susceptible areas to flooding. Combination of these two assessments
gives a characterization of flood risk expressed as Expected Annual Damage.
In this project, the method developed in Zhou, Mikkelsen, et al. (2012) is applied. This framework
has previously been used and extended as part of scientific papers and Master Thesis such as Hede
& Kolby (2013), Olsen (2013) and Kashani & Andersen (2012). For instance it has been extended
to account for stepwise investment strategies. 
Implementation of a closed sewer system in Copenhagen was first decided in 1857 when the fast
population growth makes the sanitary condition poor. It was designed as a combined sewer system
in which waste water and rainwater were collected and discharged into the harbour or the canal. The
system was then extended including intercepting sewer along the harbour, two treatment plants
(Damhusåen and Lynetten) and retention basins in case of heavy rainfall. Today the network
consists of 90% of combined sewer system with 1100 km of main sewer and the two treatment
plants receive water from Copenhagen and the neighbouring municipalities (Sørensen et al. 2006).
6
==PAGE:7==
Nowadays, the efficiency of traditional closed underground combined sewer systems is questioned
and  sustainable  solutions  relying  on  infiltration  and  local  management  of  rainwater  are
implemented.
Preliminary analysis have already been conducted to identify and qualify possible adaptation
strategies. The purpose of this Master Thesis is to assess whether the latest planned adaptation
measures, developed by Copenhagen Municipality in cooperation with consultancy companies, are
economically beneficial regarding future climate. Flood damage in the present and future climate
with and without adaptations are modelled and compared in order to evaluate the expected reduced
risk. A Cost-Benefit Analysis is performed to compare investment costs with socio-economic
benefits induced by the adaptations. The emphasis is put on pluvial flooding caused by extreme
precipitation leading to sewer surcharge. Adaptation measures have been developed regarding sea
surge, but they will not be investigated in the following. Moreover the study area is limited to the
hydrological catchment of Nørrebro, which is not directly in contact with the coast.
Projected climate change and adaptation measures in response to them are first described with a
special focus on the study area. Then the different steps of the assessment are detailed in the
methodology. Finally, results are presented and discussed in the last parts.

2. Climate Change and Adaptation
2.1. Climate Change
In Copenhagen, climate change effects will be seen as increased heavy rainfall events during
summer, increased precipitation during winter, increased number of days with high wind and rising
sea level (Copenhagen Municipality 2012). For instance, by 2100, the intensity of design rain event
with return period of 10 and 100 years are expected to increase by 30% and 40% respectively and
the mean sea level around Copenhagen will rise by 1m (Copenhagen Municipality 2011). Therefore,
drainage systems will have to deal with more frequent high volumes of water in a short period
(Zhou et al. 2013) leading to a potentially greater flood risk.
Several studies have already shown an increasing trend in extreme precipitation events over the last
century in different parts of the world including Denmark (Madsen et al. 2009). The observation of
more extreme climate in the 20th century was indeed one of the conclusion of the fourth IPCC
(Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change) report (IPCC 2007), which also added that this trend
is very likely to continue in the 21st century. 
In Europe, a report from the European Environmental Agency (EEA) showed that flood is the first
cause of natural hazard in terms of occurrence and overall losses with 213 events for EUR 52 173
billion of overall losses recorded between 1998 and 2009. It is also the third in terms of affected
people with 3 145 million people affected by floods during the same period (EEA 2010)
In Copenhagen, during summer 2010 and 2011, the city suffered from a serious flood two years in a
row (Copenhagen Municipality 2011). Damages caused by the 2011 downpour in Copenhagen was
estimated to cost DKK 5 to 6 billion (Copenhagen Municipality 2012).
These recent severe cloudbursts in Denmark have raised the population awareness on the necessity
to adapt cities to climate change (Olsen et al. 2015). Despite mitigation strategies adopted on global
scale to limit climate change, adaptation measures are needed locally to cope with unavoidable
changes (European Commission 2009).
According to DMI (Danish Meteorological Institute), an extreme rain event is characterized by
more than 15mm of precipitation in 30min (Copenhagen Municipality 2012). Extreme events cause
flooding in urban area when the runoff generated exceeds the conveyance capacity of the drainage
system (Zhou 2012). In many cases, pipes in Copenhagen sewer system were constructed 150 years
ago with a different design objective (Copenhagen Municipality 2011). Therefore, extreme events
can cause significant damage even under present climate conditions. Cities are especially vulnerable
to extreme climate events because of high concentration of people, activities and wealth (Hallegatte,
Henriet, et al. 2011). Moreover, urban development along with impermeability of urban surfaces
reduce  the  potential  infiltration,  which  accentuates  the  burden  on  urban  drainage  systems
(Hammond et al. 2013). Additionally, urbanization raises the issue of water quality due to polluted
runoff (Zhou 2014). 
There are two possibilities to tackle the increasing flood risk, either adapt the city to maintain the
current  risk  level,  or  change  the  accepted  risk  level  (Arnbjerg-Nielsen  et  al.  2008).  Since
8
==PAGE:9==
Copenhagen municipality has chosen to keep the current risk level (water at ground level once
every 10 years), adaptation strategies are needed to handle the additional volume of water.
To cope with this expected increase in pluvial flood risk, the municipality of Copenhagen has
developed  a  comprehensive  adaptation  plan  (Copenhagen  Municipality  2011;  Copenhagen
Municipality 2012). Since 2010, it is recommended to use the A1B scenario from the IPCC for
climate  change  adaptation  planning  in  Denmark  (Danish  Nature  Agency  2012).  However,
Copenhagen Adaptation plan is based on the A2 scenario following previous recommendations
(Danish Energy Agency 2008). Those two scenarios make similar projections for 2050 horizon and
the uncertainty ranges of the 2100 horizon projections overlap. Therefore this will not affect
significantly the results. The projections for Denmark show a warmer and wetter climate, with more
rain in winter and both more drought periods and more heavy downpours in summer. Taking 1961-
1990 as reference period, by 2100, the annual mean temperature is expected to increase by 2.9 to
3.2°C and the changes in precipitation are expected to be 5% and 26% in summer and winter,
respectively (Danish Nature Agency 2012).
2.2. Adaptation plan
In 2011, after the two severe downpours that struck Copenhagen, the Municipality developed a
comprehensive  adaptation  plan  with  recommended  measures  to  prevent  future  flooding
(Copenhagen Municipality 2011). This adaptation plan contained recommendation to cope with all
challenges risen by climate change: heavier and more frequent downpours, higher sea level, higher
temperature, urban heat island, groundwater issues. Only adaptations regarding pluvial flooding are
investigated here, even though some adaptation measures can be used to tackle several issues by
synergy effects. This plan was then extended in 2012 in the Cloudburst Management Plan with
more details on recommended measures and funding  (Copenhagen Municipality 2012). Finally
Master plans with complete description of the measures to be implemented were achieved for each
sub-catchment in the Municipality during the following years. It should be notice that those
adaptation plans were designed to protect facilities from runoff from the road. Basements were
assumed  to  be  protected  from  back  flow  by backwater  valves  installed  as  private  initiative
(Copenhagen Municipality 2011).
The three main objectives of urban drainage are public hygiene, flood protection and environmental
protection  (Chocat et al. 2007). Climate change issues apart, the first two objectives are rather
fulfilled  by conventional  drainage  systems  in  Denmark,  as  part  of  the  developed  countries.
However, criticisms of these traditional drainage systems have risen. As they are designed with the
single objective of controlling water quantity, limited concerns are shown for water quality and
recreational values (Zhou 2014). 
The  adaptation  plan  developed  by Copenhagen  Municipality does  not  deal  exclusively  with
increasing traditional drainage system capacity to manage stormwater, but also with implementing
more  sustainable  solutions  with  additional  benefits  in  mind  (called  Plan  B)  (Copenhagen
Municipality  2011).  Sustainable  solutions  are  characterized  by  decentralized  stormwater
management to deal with it at a local scale. Indeed, both traditional and sustainable solutions used
separately can not cope efficiently (economically and socially)  with the excess water, but a
9
==PAGE:10==
combination can handle extreme rainfall and reduce the burden on sewer system on a normal day.
Furthermore, intensification of extreme events is not the only expected consequence of climate
change. An increase in heat island effect leading to increase in temperature in the city is also
expected. Thus, green and blue solutions offer the advantage of both managing locally stormwater
and cooling the city. Unlike underground pipes, most of the sustainable solutions are in sight and for
instance, developing blue and green infrastructures need to concern quite a large area to have a
really significant impact on rainfall. That is why, emphasis is put on the necessity to plan adaptation
strategies  in  conjunction  with  local  urban  planning  and  include  it  in  a  wider  context.  This
willingness to integrate sustainable and traditional drainage systems together with urban planning is
described as synergy effects in the Master plan. 
Three  different  methods  are  combined  for  efficient  stormwater  management  and  flood  risk
reduction.  First,  the  underground  drainage  system  is  expanded  with  larger  pipes,  additional
underground basins and pumping stations. Then, when it's possible, the runoff is disconnected from
the sewer system to be managed locally in Sustainable Urban Drainage Systems (SUDS) (using for
instance infiltration). And finally,  part of the runoff is diverted to area where flooding would make
the least damage such as car parks, playing fields or green parks. Thus, stormwater is temporarily
stored and the discharge to the sewer system is delayed.
The implementation of all adaptation measures in the entire municipality will last 20 years with
priority given to areas with high risk and where adaptation is easier.
Copenhagen Municipality has thus chosen not to focus on traditional solutions as it used, but to
implement sustainable solutions with the secondary objective of making the city greener. On the
other  hand,  the  additional  benefits  of  sustainable  solutions  are  more  difficult  to  estimate  in
monetary terms, therefore, they tend to be underestimated or unaccounted for in risk assessment.
Moreover, reserves about green solutions arise because urban runoff is generally polluted and
natural infiltration might not be able to deal properly with it, leading to accumulation of persistent
pollutants in the environment (Chocat et al. 2007).
2.3. Study area
In Copenhagen's adaptation plan, the Municipality is divided into sub-catchments according to
hydrological characteristics. The study area chosen for this study is the catchment of Nørrebro.
Despite its name, it does not fully correspond to the administrative district of Nørrebro. Therefore,
in the following, Nørrebro refers to the hydrological catchment instead of the administrative district.
Nørrebro catchment is located in the North East part of Copenhagen. It overlaps part of the
administrative districts of Nørrebro, Østerbro and Indre By (Figure 1). It consists of Rigshospital,
Fælledparken and other parks, residential areas, the north campus of Copenhagen University and
two of Copenhagen inner lakes (Indre Søer). Therefore, there is a large variety of land use including
recreational areas (green and blue), densely populated areas and main roads. The residential areas,
the hospital and the campus are important values to be protected. On the other hand, the many
parks, especially Fælledparken, and the lakes offer great opportunities to implement green and blue
adaptation measures in this dense urban context. Therefore, in this sub-catchment, adaptation
10
==PAGE:11==
measures described in the Master plan consist mainly of alternative solutions, the traditional sewer
network is very little expanded.
The catchment is located downstream from northern catchments of the Great Copenhagen. It is
characterized by low terrain elevation with a natural slope toward the lakes. Therefore, it receives a
large part of upstream runoff that ends up in the lakes.
In Nørrebro Master plan (Copenhagen Municipality 2015), 20 projects were selected and described.
They are designed in order to be able to handle cloudbursts up to 100 years rain events in 100 years
when implemented together. Therefore generally, the efficiency of an upstream project depends on
the fact that the downstream projects are well established. The overall solution chosen is to delay
and store the rain water upstream for later discharge into the inner lakes. However the respect of
green protected areas and current good water quality in the lakes were kept in mind during
planning. Three main flow ways toward the lakes are identified in the catchment (Figure 2). In each
part of the catchment, it is investigated whether cloudburst projects can be planned in coordination
with other local projects, such as road renovation, cycle path, prevention of heat island effect, metro
construction sites, green connection through the city or urban development, to enhance synergy
effects and reduce costs.

3. Methodology
The method described in the framework developed by Zhou, Mikkelsen, et al. (2012) is followed to
assess economic benefits of the adaptation plan in relation to climate change and pluvial flooding.
In this method, hazard and vulnerability are analysed to quantify flood risk in monetary terms.
Expected Annual Damage, in the study area in the situation with and without adaptation measures,
are used as risk indicator. The benefit of adaptation measures is defined as reduced risk. Then the
reduced damage thus obtained is compared to the investment cost of adaptation in order to calculate
the net benefit of the adaptation plan. The following part describes more in detail the required steps
based on the quoted framework.
3.1. Flood Risk
In this study, flood risk assessment is conducted to appraise the efficiency of adaptation measures
planned by Copenhagen Municipality for Nørrebro catchment regarding pluvial flooding. To do so,
investment costs and benefits of adaptations are evaluated accounting for expected climate change
impacts. Benefits are measured as reduction of Expected Annual Damage (EAD), from the situation
without adaptations to the situation with adaptations. Investment costs are provided by HOFOR.

Risk is commonly defined as the product of hazard and vulnerability (Apel et al. 2009). Where
hazard represents the physical and statistical aspects of the flood and vulnerability is the adverse
effects on people or assets due to exposure to flood. Three types of data are needed to evaluate the
risk as  EAD  (Freni et al. 2010). Hydrological and hydraulic data are needed in the hazard
assessment part to quantify the severity and the distribution of the flood. Physical data representing
land  use  and  building  characteristics  together  with  economic  data  are  then  needed  in  the
vulnerability assessment part to appraise the potential consequences of a flood.
Given an extreme external loading (characterized by its duration, return period and climate factor),
hazard  is  determined  by  simulating  flood  characteristics  (water  depth  and  extent).  While
vulnerability is assessed from assets classification for which unit damage costs are defined as
function of flood depth. Those two assessments are combined in a GIS-based model to calculate the
actual flood risk in monetary terms. This procedure is conducted for each return period and climate
condition in both models (with and without adaptation). Those results are used to build functional
relationships between damage cost and return period, which are then integrated into EAD. A
schematic overview of the framework is presented in Figure 4.
3.1.1. Hazard Assessment
Hazard assessment focuses on analysing flood characteristics. Given a climate condition and an
adaptation situation, probability distribution of floods, overland flow path and hazard indicators are
investigated (Zhou 2012).
3.1.1.1. Modelling tools
Maximum flood depth and extent are chosen as hazard indicators. They are simulated using a
coupled 1D-2D hydrological model with the software MIKE URBAN developed by DHI. 1D-2D
model enables a more accurate modelling of overland flow especially under extreme events where
the flow is not confined to streets  (Freni et al. 2010). On the other hand, it requires  more
computational efforts. MOUSE computational tool simulates the 1D pipe flow solving the complete
Saint-Venant equations, based on an implicit, finite-difference numerical solution. The model is
based on the assumptions of incompressible and homogeneous water, small bottom-slope with the
horizontal, wavelengths larger than water depth and sub-critical flow. Therefore the continuity and
momentum equations can be written as follows (MIKE by DHI 2014b):

These equations are valid for free surface flow only, but they can be generalized for pressurized

The 2D overland flow is simulated by MIKE 21 model from the digital elevation layer. The two
models are coupled by MIKE FLOOD inside MIKE URBAN to simulate the exchange between the
pipe network and the overland flow (MIKE by DHI 2014a). In the model, the runoff is directly
connected to the drainage network, therefore, flooding occurs only due to a surcharge of the sewer
capacity.
The Time/Area method (Model A) is used to simulate runoff from rainfall time series. The use of
Time/Area Method enables to take into account the non-constant contribution of the catchment area.
The surface closest to the connected manhole will contribute first to the runoff (Butler & Davies
2011). The contribution of each catchment is calculated according to the following parameters
(MIKE by DHI 2014c). The initial loss is the rainfall depth required to start the surface runoff, it
corresponds to the wetting and filling of terrain depression. The impervious area is the fraction of
the catchment considered to contribute to the runoff. It depends on the land cover and the return
period of the precipitation. The hydrological reduction factor represents the water losses from
evapo-transpiration or imperfect imperviousness. These  two factors  are  used to calculate the
reduced  area  which  is  the  part  of  the  catchment  actually  contributing  to  the  runoff.  The
concentration time is the time required for the most distant point of the catchment to reach the point
of outflow. The Time/Area curve accounts for the shape of the catchment layout. There is three pre-
defined curves for rectangular, divergent and convergent catchment. The last two parameters control
the shape of the hydrograph. The hydrographs thus generated are then loaded to the MOUSE Pipe
Flow model as input.
In each simulation, all catchments are loaded with the same extreme rain event in the form of a
Chicago Design Storm (CDS). It is a synthetic rain event commonly used in urban drainage system
flow simulation. It is estimated based on Intensity-Duration-Frequency relationships knowing the
mean annual precipitation, the return period of the event and its location and duration. It is suitable
to use CDS to calculate the extent of the floods for extreme precipitation because the antecedent
conditions  play  a  minor  role  (Zhou  et  al.  2013).  In  hazard  assessment,  CDS  rain  events
characterized by an annual mean precipitation of 700mm and a duration of 24h are defined for the
return periods 2, 5, 10, 50 and 100 years with different climate factors. The return period indicates
the occurrence probability, it is a statistic measure of the average recurrence interval of an extreme
climatic loading (Zhou et al. 2013). An example of such a rain time series can seen be in Figure 5.
All  the  CDS  rain  used  in  simulation  are  generated  from  the  Excel  file
“regional_cds_ver_3_2_1.xls” (Spildevandskomiteen 2006). 
Additionally, the network is loaded locally with the domestic wastewater of the whole catchment
area and constant discharge from the upstream catchment of Lyngby. Small constant discharge
named Gentofte Rende and Soborghus Rende are also added to account for the contribution of the
channelling of these natural watercourses.

3.1.2. Vulnerability Assessment
A vulnerability assessment is then required to evaluate the adverse effects related to a flood. There
are different categories of flood damages. They can be direct or indirect. Direct damages occur from
immediate contact with the flood, whereas indirect damages occur further in space or time and are
caused by disruption of physical or economic linkages. They can also be tangible or intangible.
Tangible  damages  are  damages  to  market  goods,  whereas  intangible  damages  concern  no-
marketable goods such has ecosystems or health. (Messner et al. 2007). While difficulties in the
evaluation of tangible damage are only technical, for intangible damage, ethical issues rise because
they can't always be quantified in monetary terms. Examples of damages for the different categories
are presented in Table 5.

In most of the studies, damage evaluation is limited to direct tangible damages because they are
easier to define and quantify (Apel et al. 2009). Indeed they can be estimated as monetary costs
based on hydraulic parameters  (Freni et al. 2010). Moreover, in industrialized countries direct
damage on buildings and their contents dominate the overall damage cost (Messner et al. 2007).
However, traditional frameworks have been extended to include also direct intangible damages
related to health or environment (Hallegatte, Henriet, et al. 2011). Studies based on multi-criteria
analysis also allow to account more easily for direct intangible damages (Meyer et al. 2009). As for
indirect damages, they are highly dependent on the spatial and temporal boundaries of the study and
fewer models are available (Merz et al. 2010).
A common method to determine direct tangible damages is to use stage-damage functions (Messner
et al. 2007). They establish a relationship between the flood characteristics and the absolute or
relative damage of a specific asset category. Flood characteristics can be flood depth, duration,
velocity, area, time of occurrence... However, unlike hydraulic models which are nowadays quite
accurate, damage curves can be considered as the bottleneck in flood damage estimation. Indeed,
they are highly dependent on available data, local conditions and chosen model (Freni et al. 2010).
So they represent a main source of uncertainty in the assessment process. Nevertheless, in this case,
due to too few available data, a simple binary method using threshold value is used  (Zhou,
Mikkelsen, et al. 2012). For each asset category, a critical flood depth is defined with an associated
uniform unit cost. Flood depths and unit costs are defined mainly according to Olsen et al. (2015),
Zhou, Mikkelsen, et al. (2012) and  COWI (2014). Given a valuable asset, damage costs are
considered whenever the water depth exceeds the critical threshold defined for this type of asset.
The different unit costs together with water thresholds are listed in Table  6 for the different
vulnerability classes. Asset categories are basements, buildings, roads, manholes, water pollution
and  health.  Building  category is  divided  into  residential,  small  business, commercial,  public
institution and hospital. Buildings are classified according to the main use of their basement and
ground floor. Small business category represents properties where the basement and/or the ground
floor is occupied by a small shop. Commercial stands for bigger business in individual properties.
Public institution is used for properties such as school, university, museum, administrative office…
A separate category is used for the hospital since it is more valuable. The main use of each building
present in Nørrebro catchment is presented in Figure 10. A basement is considered as flooded if the
water depth is above 0.03m in the status situation and 0.1m in the planning situation. A different
threshold water depth is used since an aim of the adaptation plan is to ensure no damage to

buildings when the water depth on streets is below 10 cm (Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al. n.d.). A building
is considered as flooded if the water depth is above 0.1m in a buffer zone of 3m around the
building. Unit damage costs for residential properties and small business are directly taken from
COWI (2014). They are based on insurance claims after 2011 flood in Copenhagen. Unit costs for
commercial properties and public institutions are extrapolated from these report. Indeed, public

3.1.3. Expected Annual Damage
Results from the hazard assessment, presented as flood map, and the vulnerability assessment
describing land use are combined in a GIS-based model, described in Kashani & Andersen (2012).
The GIS model is described in detail in 8.4. Appendix 4. Flooded assets are extracted based on the
stage-damage functions in order to calculate the total damage cost in each situation for the different
external loads. They are then used to identify one functional relationship between damage and
return period for each of the four situations (status and planning models under both present and
future climate conditions). A log-linear model is assumed in all cases to extrapolate damage cost of
non simulated probabilities (Arnbjerg-Nielsen & Fleischer 2009). Flood risk can be represented as a
function of the return period in the flood risk density curve (RDC). It combined probability and
damage cost to show the relative contribution of events to the average annual damage. Indeed, an
event with high return period may have a high damage cost, but it will only contribute to a minor
extent to the average cost because of its low probability.
The Expected Annual Damage (EAD) is used as risk indicator to appraise the vulnerability of the
study area to flood hazard in monetary terms. EAD is define as integration of flood damage (D)
over all occurrence probabilities (p) for the study area (A) (Zhou 2012).

3.2. Cost Benefit Analysis
Socio-economic assessment of adaptation initiatives in response to climate change is carried out
using Cost Benefit Analysis (CBA) (Damgaard et al. 2006). It is a commonly used decision making
tool based on welfare economics theory. It has been recognized as the major appraisal technique for
public investments and public policy (Pearce et al. 2006). Benefits and costs, defined as increase
and reduction in human well-being respectively, are compared to appraise the efficiency of an
adaptation policy. When applied to the present case, benefits are counted as reduced risk between
the  situation  with  adaptation  and  the  situation  without  adaptations.  Costs  are  counted  as
construction costs of adaptation. For the tested option to be efficient, the social benefits must
exceed the social costs. 
The aggregation over time requires to use discounting to express future costs and benefits in present
values. When using discounting, present costs and benefits are assumed to have more importance in
the decision making process than future ones. According to the Danish EPA guidelines a discount
rate of 3 % should be used in climate change projects for long-term investments (Damgaard et al.
2006). Net Present Value (NPV) is the preferable criterion used to aggregate all costs and benefits
into a unique value  (Pearce et al. 2006). It is calculated according to the following equation. A
project can be considered as economically attractive when the NPV is positive.

4.3. Flood risk
From the previous  estimation of flood damage under different conditions, flood risk can be
evaluated by the mean of EAD in both situations with and without adaptation to climate change.
The method used to assess flood risk is based on the assumptions of no change in the catchment
(such as urban development) apart from adaptation to climate change. This is justified since the
catchment is already densely urbanized and the few parks are protected areas. Moreover,  adaptation
measures are assumed to be implemented at the beginning of the period in a one-step lumped
investment. In reality, the immediate implementation of all adaptation measures is not fulfilled.
However, despite the possibility to take step-wise investment strategy into account, the assumption
was kept due to time constraints and relatively short construction period compared to the total study
period. A linear climate change over time is also assumed since there is no reason to use another
function (Zhou, Mikkelsen, et al. 2012). Furthermore, given the large uncertainties associated with
the estimation of climate change impacts, assuming a linear relationship is reasonable (Zhou 2012).
Therefore, estimation of EAD is needed only at four points: with and without adaptation at the
beginning and at the end of the study period.

4.5. Sensitivity Analysis
Hitherto, NPV has been calculated to assess the efficiency of climate adaptation plan in Nørrebro.
Due to  the assumptions  and  simplifications  made,  results  are subject to  uncertainties.  Three
categories of uncertainties arise from the analysis (Chow et al. 1988). Inherent uncertainties, which
stem from the random variability of phenomena such as climate change. Model uncertainties, which
come from using equation to model the reality, such as stage damage functions. And parameter
uncertainties, which result from approximated parameters in the equations, such as discount rate.
Therefore in the following, a sensitivity analysis is performed on some of the parameters to identify
their relative importance.
4.5.1. Climate factor
Prediction of climate change is subject to large inherent uncertainties. Moreover, future climate
conditions are estimated by climate factors defined for each return period. Model and parameter
uncertainties are thus also expected. The important uncertainties underlying climate change impacts
make hazard assessment uncertain despite the high level of confidence associated with hydraulic
modelling. In order to assess whether those uncertainties have a large impact on damage estimation,
flood  simulations  using  different  climate  factors  are  conducted.  Flood  depth  and  extent  are
simulated with CDS of 10 years return period as external load using climate factors of 1.1 and 1.5.
Impervious area in hydrological parameters are calculated following the method described in
3.1.1.4. Equivalent return periods of 16 and 78 years are used, respectively. The total damage cost
associated with 10 years event for climate factors 1, 1.1, 1.3, and 1.5 are presented in Figure 23. It
appears that there is a quadratic relationship between damage costs and climate factors. Climate
factor 1.3 is taken as reference scenario. Decreasing climate factor by 15% leads to a reduction of
damage cost by 55% and increasing climate factor by 15% leads to a 33% rise in damage cost.

4.5.4. Discount rate
Previously, a discount rate of 3% was used to calculate NPV according to Danish recommendations
for projects related to the environment. However, it implies that only 6% of the costs and benefits in
95 years are accounted for in the NPV. This can raise moral issues such as inter-generational equity
(Pearce et al. 2006).
Using the implementation costs of all adaptation measures under the assumption of climate change,
other discount rates, constant or linearly declining from 3% to 1%, are applied to assess the
sensibility of this parameter. The NPV over the study period are presented in  Figure 25 for the
different discount rates. With no discounting (0%), NPV increase is exponential, while with higher
discount rates (2%, 3% and 5%) the rise is more logarithmic. It can be explained by the fact that
when no discounting is applied, the calculated benefits are directly summed up. This benefit
increases over time since, as climate change impacts increase, adaptation measures have more
effects on flood risk reduction. While, applying discount rate results in weighting benefits with a
decreasing rate. The declining discount rate from 3% to 1% gives results very similar to 2%
discount rate, except that NPV rise is almost linear.
The break-even point, corresponding to the time when NPV becomes positive, and the NPV at the
end of the study period in million DKK are listed in Table 13 for several values of discount rate. No
discounting results in a final NPV 12 times higher and a break-even point occurring 23 years sooner
compared to the reference situation (3% discount rate). Declining and 2% discount rate give a final
NPV around three times higher and a break-even point occurring 10 years sooner than in the
reference situation. With 5% discounting, a positive NPV is not reached within the study period.

5. Discussions
The results obtained as part of this Master Thesis and their limitations are discussed in the following
section. Uncertainties underlying the analysis  are pointed out, the sensitivity of the different
parameters are discussed and suggestions for further improvements are given.
Cost-Benefit analysis results show that, under the selected assumptions, the planned adaptation
measures become beneficial in good time, as the NPV is positive after 35 to 50 years. The
adaptation plan for Nørrebro is cost-effective after approximately half the observation period.
However uncertainties of different kinds arise from the assessment. They come from assumptions
made in the hydrological modelling and the economic analysis, and from projection of future
climate impacts and adaptation measures.
First, it is not possible to know for sure what would be the climate conditions in 100 years. In this
study climate factors are used to model the expected increase in rainfall intensity. They are
estimated based on scenarios using, among other things, projection of greenhouse gas emissions and
downscaling methods. Therefore, unavoidable uncertainties arise from forecast of future climate.
They can be observed in the figure showing NPV with and without assuming climate change
(Figure  22).  While,  when  the  climate  is  expected  to  move  toward  more  frequent  extreme
precipitation, implementing adaptation measures appears to be a good investment. In the other case
NPV is hardly positive at the end of the study period. However, this last scenario is not realistic.
NPV presented in dotted line, assuming no climate change, should be interpreted only as the
extreme lower boundary of the confidence interval regarding climate change uncertainty. The
sensitivity analysis regarding the impacts of climate factor on damage costs was performed for 10
years event in status model. From this analysis, it seems to be a quadratic relationship between
climate factor and damage cost, but further analysis are needed to confirm or disconfirm this trend.
Moreover,  research  efforts  regarding  climate  in  Denmark  have  first  mainly  focussed  on
understanding and describing climate change impacts (Danish Energy Agency 2008). So climate
forecast  can  be deemed  as  reliable.  Additionally,  a  study  of  uncertainties  in  the  economic
assessment  of  adaptation  to  climate  change  performed  in  Odense,  Denmark,  has  found  that
uncertainties regarding climate change impacts have the least influence on NPV (Arnbjerg-Nielsen
& Zhou 2014).
Secondly, despite the relatively high level of confidence resulting from hydraulic modelling, flood
risk assessment remains uncertain due to the estimation of damage costs. The difficulty of finding a
general method to assess flood damage arises from the large variety of damage and assets, and from
the fact that they are highly dependent on the place and time of occurrence. Indeed, the relative
concentration of wealth, together with prevention and awareness of the population, have a large
influence. The simplification, resulting from using binary stage damage function, leads to a larger
level of uncertainty, as this all-or-nothing way of doing might not be always applicable. A more
complex function including multiple stages could help to have a more accurate estimate of damage
costs with gradual increase. But such a function is not available yet for the study area. Another
factor of uncertainties in the stage damage function is the fact that only the maximum water depth is
41
==PAGE:42==
accounted for as hazard indicator. Other parameters such as the time of occurrence of the flood and
its duration as well as the flow velocity and population warming (Apel et al. 2009) could impact
resulting flood damage, especially when considering social consequences. 
The recent heavy cloudbursts in Copenhagen have enabled a more realistic estimate of material
damage from insurance claims. However, non-insured assets and intangible damages, such as traffic
disruption and spreading pathogens, are not accounted for in insurance database. So the resulting
total damage cost might underestimate the actual cost. Moreover, information on the exact cause of
damage and the extent of the flood are not available in every case (COWI 2014), which makes it
more difficult to extrapolate unit costs. The unit costs used in this study are based on a combination
of several studies, all from Denmark. Tangible damage on properties and urban facilities used
average costs based on insurance claims. Regarding housing, the same importance is given to all
properties  regardless  of their  actual  value. It is  in agreement  with Danish legislation which
guaranties a uniform service level. Health and water pollution categories are the only intangible
damages accounted for, it could be extended with other categories if more information were
available. 
According to the sensitivity analysis, damages to buildings and basements are the most sensitive.
These categories represents also the largest share in the total flood damage. Estimation of damage to
buildings, which is directly based on insurance claims, is the most advanced one, thus the less
uncertain.
Nevertheless, uncertainties regarding damage cost appraisal need to be put into perspective. The
most extreme events, that represent the largest damage cost, contribute only to a minor extent to the
total damage cost, since they have low probability of occurrence.
Then,  the  assumption  that  all  adaptations  are  implemented  in  one  step  might  lead  to  an
overestimation of the overall investment cost. Studies  have shown that step-wise investment
strategy enables a better allocation of resources, resulting in a larger net benefit (Zhou 2012). It is
possible to account for a more complex investment strategy in the CBA. However the expected
refinement of the results does not seem to worth the effort given the long simulation time.
Moreover, the additional intermediate time steps will be concentrated in the first twenty years of the
observation period, which is the planned implementation time for the whole municipality.
In the CBA, when the total construction costs is used, it might cause a bias in the results. Since
some adaptations are considered in the implementation costs, but not in the hydraulic model, their
potential benefits are not accounted for. That's why the adaptation measures non-included in MIKE
URBAN model were subsequently removed from the construction cost. Moreover, within the
intended adaptation not implemented in the model, there are measures, such as green paths all over
Nørrebro catchment (NO20 and NO19), that are not directly focused on cloudburst, but more on
daily rain. In addition, they make urban hydrology more complex, and are not accurately modelled
under  the  chosen  modelling  assumption.  Therefore,  it  is  reasonable  not  to  consider  those
adaptations when simulating flood hazards under extreme rainfall. On the other hand, adaptation
42
==PAGE:43==
measures NO05 and NO12 should be included in the model for further assessment.
It should also be kept in mind that eventually adaptation measures will be implemented in the whole
municipality.  Meaning  that  runoff water  would  be managed  locally in  upstream catchments.
Therefore, less burden would be put on Nørrebro catchment. However, simulating adaptation
measures  in  the  whole  municipality  with  the  necessary  level  of  detail  requires  too  much
computation time to be performed as part of this Master Thesis.
Furthermore, the choice of discount rate has an important impact on the results. The 3 % rate used
in this study is based on Danish recommendation. Nevertheless, it implies that today's benefits and
costs appear more important than future ones. This is common practice and in agreement with
welfare economics, but it is questionable since future generations will suffer the effects of climate
change  more  acutely.  This  “tyranny  of  discounting”  seems  to  be  contrary  to  sustainable
development (Pearce et al. 2006). Applying a time declining discount rate could help to face this
controversy. This solution presents additional advantages. First empirical researches have proved
that it better reproduces the actual people behaviour (Frederick et al. 2002). Secondly, it is justified
by the uncertainties in future interest rates and future economic growth (Pearce et al. 2006). On the
other hand time declining discount rate might create time inconsistency, which is when present
decisions are contradicted by latter behaviour. But consistency turns out not to be always a realistic
assumption regarding society behaviour. 
According to the sensitivity analysis, discount rate appears to be a very sensitive parameter
especially when it is reduced. A discount rate higher than 3%, makes the adaptation plan no longer
beneficial within the observation period, whereas a lower discount rate makes it beneficial 12 to 23
years sooner, with a final NPV up to 12 times larger. The declining discount rate gives results very
similar to a constant 2% discount rate due to the relatively short observation period.
Finally, the accuracy of EAD estimation could be improved by simulating flood hazard for more
return periods. The five different rain events used for each situation in this study give a reasonable
estimate of the relationship between damage cost and probability of occurrence. According to Olsen
(2013) increasing the number of designed storms used to seven would enable a better description of
the log-linear function. However, a compromise should be made between level of accuracy wanted
and the available simulation time.
The purpose of the adaptation strategy is not to eliminate all possible risk, but to maintain an
acceptable flood risk characterized by design criteria. So far, the design criteria for sewer system
was to cope with 10 years return period rain events. With the development of Copenhagen climate
adaptation plan, the selected design criteria is now to ensure discharge from sewer at ground level at
most every 10 years, and 10 cm of water on the streets no more than once every 100 years
(Copenhagen Municipality 2012). From the results of hazard assessment, it appears that the selected
criteria regarding 10 years return period is fulfilled under both present and future climate condition
43
==PAGE:44==
when adaptation measures are implemented. However, regarding the criteria for 100 years return
period,  under  present  climate  conditions,  around  300  basements  and  buildings  are  flooded,
including the part of the hospital, because water is above 10cm on the street. And under future
climate conditions, the water level on the street exceeds 10 cm up to 20 cm for instance at
Kartoffelrækkerne, between Guldbergs Plads and Sjællandsgade, at the north of the University, and
at Ryesgade. Therefore, damage reduction from status to planning model, even under present
climate is not as large as expected. When climate adaptations are implemented, the EAD is expected
to decrease by 90% under present climate conditions. According to the results showed previously,
the actual reduction is only by 40%. One explanation can be the fact that the hydraulic model does
not  include  all  planned  adaptation  measures.  For  instance,  green  paths,  are  supposed  to  be
implemented at the above mentioned places and all over the catchment, but they are not considered
in MIKE URBAN. They are designed to retain approximately the equivalent of a 10 years rain
event  (Copenhagen Municipality 2013). Therefore, water level on the streets could be actually
lower than estimated. Cloudburst and retention roads in Frederik V's vej and Tagensvej are not
included in the model neither. Moreover, in Nørrebro catchment the choice was made to focus on
alternative solutions based on infiltration and temporary storage of runoff. Pipe network is hardly
expanded. Those green solutions are more difficult to model accurately in MIKE URBAN (DHI
2015). Modelling runoff from green areas comes up with technical challenges such as realistically
computing hydrological losses and handling of overloads. When including runoff from pervious
areas, initial soil moisture, and the duration and the asymmetry of the CDS affect the simulation
results. It could lead to underestimate or overestimate the load of the pipe network. MIKE URBAN
can handle surface runoff in two different ways. It can either be computed using a hydrological
model and directly loaded into the drainage system, or precipitation is directly loaded onto the 2D
surface model and hydrological losses are computed at a raster cell level. Because of the large
extent of the network model and the restricted available 2D surface model, the first method was
used in this analysis. In this case, runoff from both impervious and pervious area participates in the
overland flow only when the network is overloaded. This method has the drawback that during
extreme events, green areas generate unrealistically high loading of the drainage network. In reality,
instead of accumulating on the 2D surface around the connection location, the runoff would flow
toward the lower terrain. To minimize this issue, the number of sub-catchments and connection
nodes were increased in Nørrebro. It should be noticed that the artificial drainage network added in
Fælledparken enables a reduction of the error associated with modelling the runoff of this large
green area. On the other hand, as urban catchment, the area is dominated by impervious surface and
it is not recommended to apply the second method in this case. Furthermore, in the Time/Area
model (A) used as hydrological model, no specific method for computation of infiltration is
included. Infiltration is roughly approximated by a fixed initial loss and a reduction factor. More
complex methods, such as Horton's equation and Soil Conservation Service method are available
with other surface runoff models. For instance, the Kinematic wave model (B) is deemed the most
suitable for simulation of runoff from previous area because it considers infiltration with Horton's
equation in a conceptually simple way.
However, sustainable solutions may not be as cost effective as traditional solutions, but they present
the advantage of providing additional benefits, resulting from more and better recreational area and
44
==PAGE:45==
an overall greener city. These new amenity values are one of the objective of Copenhagen climate
adaptation plan, but they are difficult to quantify and hence to include in the Cost-Benefit Analysis.
To overcome this problem hedonic valuation methods can be used to appraise the impacts of green
and blue area (Zhou et al. 2013). Moreover, in the adaptation plan, it is sought to make the most of
each adaptation measure by multiplying the possible benefits. A single adaptation can be used for
both reducing flood risk and contributing to cooling the city. Therefore, in the decision making
process, climate change impacts should also be considered as a whole to assess adaptation. On the
other  hand, alternative solutions  are partly based on infiltration  of urban runoff. This  poses
environmental concerns such as pollution of water bodies and especially groundwater. Indeed,
emphasis is put on groundwater protection by Danish legislation, which would tend to promote
more traditional drainage systems (Zhou 2012).
6. Conclusion
The purpose of this study was to assess whether the latest climate adaptation plan developed by
Copenhagen Municipality for the hydrological catchment of Nørrebro is cost efficient. A socio
economic risk based assessment was performed including flood risk identification and cost-benefit
analysis. The flood risk assessment was used to quantify flood risk in monetary terms expressed as
Expected Annual Damage (EAD). It included hazard and vulnerability analysis for rain events of
different return periods under present or future climate conditions and accounting or not for
adaptation measures. It resulted in EAD ranging from 18 million to 75 million DKK, depending on
climate  and  adaptation  conditions.  The  cost-benefit  analysis,  comparing  reduced  risk  due  to
adaptation and their construction cost, gave a positive Net Present Value after approximately half
the study period. Therefore the studied adaptation plan for Nørrebro can be deemed to be cost
efficient. 
However, due to the fact that the adaptation plan used mainly alternative solutions, the risk
reduction induced by adaptation measures was not as high as expected. Further analysis, using
modelling tools more adapted to green and blue solutions and hedonic valuation to quantify
additional benefits, could be conducted in order to refine the results.
Nevertheless, due to simplifications and assumptions made throughout the study, results were
expected to be subject to large uncertainties. A sensitivity analysis and appraisal of parameters
uncertainties was performed to assess the accuracy of the results. It was found that climate factor
was a relatively sensitive parameter but not very uncertain. On the other hand, discount rate was
both sensitive and uncertain. The most sensitive vulnerability classes regarding unit costs were also
the ones that were estimated with the least uncertainties. Estimation of public and private share in
investment costs was however subject to uncertainties and could impact to a large extent NPV.

8.1. Appendix 1: Master Project description
Modelling of the efficiency of Copenhagen Climate Adaptation Plan in relation to
climate flooding
Due to climate change, Copenhagen will face increased frequency of extreme precipitation, rising
sea level and extended period of drought in the coming years. This will lead to a higher flood risk
amongst other things. In order to limit future costs of damage, the city has developed an adaptation
plan for the next 100 years including extension of existing sewer system, dikes, new green areas for
infiltration and cooling purpose, etc. Those investments are designed to reduce the Expected Annual
Damage from flooding. 
The objective of this project is to model those climate adaptation measures in order to assess
whether they can be recommended. Their investment costs will be compared to the reduced flood
damages they allow. The method described in Zhou et al. (2012) will be followed. The description
of adaptation measures will be provided by Copenhagen and DHI will give the support for the
model to simulate flood damages. The work will be divided into the following steps:

Selection of scenarios with and without adaptation measures and climate change

Study of the coupled 1D-2D hydrological model designed by DHI

Hazards maps from hydrological modelling

Vulnerabilities analysis

Estimation of damage costs in the different scenarios for rain events with different return
periods

Cost-benefit analysis: comparison of investments (cost) and reduced flood damage together
with additional benefits (benefit)
This project is a Master Thesis with a workload of 30 ECTS conducted from 16/02/15 to 16/07/15.

shapefiles containing flooded items in each vulnerability class as output. In the diagram model
inputs are presented in blue, tools in yellow and intermediate and final output in green. An overview

Processing of flood map
The flood map resulting from MIKE FLOOD simulation showing the depth and the extent of the
flood in the catchment is first processed to extract layer with water depth over a certain threshold
depending on the vulnerability class.
In the chosen method, it is not necessary to know the exact water depth, but only whether it is above
a certain threshold. Therefore, the raster layer flood is reclassified with the toll “reclassify” to give a
unique value to the cells with water depth within a certain interval defined by the different
thresholds stated above. After conversion of the flood_class layer to polygon shapefile with the tool
“Raster to Polygon”, the resulting layer flood_poly.shp is reduced to Nørrebro catchment with the
tool “Clip”. From this layer, three layers are extracted, with the tools “Select”, “Select (2)”,
“Select(3)”,  “Select(6)”  and  “Select(7)”  respectively,  to  account  for  the  different  thresholds,
flood_map_1.shp contains water depth above 0.03m, flood_map.shp_2 contains water depth above
0.1m, flood_map_3.shp contains water depth above 0.15m, flood_map_4.shp contains water depth
above 0.2m and flood_map_5.shp contains water depth above 0.3m. 
Basements and Buildings
The layer building_norrebro_improved.shp contains buildings located in the study area with an
attribute corresponding to their use. A building is considered as flooded when a water depth above
0.1m is observed around the building in a buffer zone of 3m. The tool “Buffer” is used to create a
buffer zone around each building in the layer building_buffer.shp. The overlay tool “Spatial Join
(4)” is used with building_buffer.shp as target input and flood_map_2.shp as join feature. The join
condition is set to “intersect”. The tool “Select (8)” is then used to extract flooded buildings. A
similar method is used to assess flooded basements. flood_map_1.shp and flood_map_2.shp are
used for status and planning situation respectively as join feature in “Spatial Join”. And “Select (5)”
is then used to extract flooded basements.
Damage cost are then calculated. A field named “damage” of float type is added in the attribute
tables (tool “Add Field” for basements and “Add Field (2)” for buildings). The hospital basements

LAR (Lokal Afledning af Regnvand) solutions are drainage elements to handle locally stormwater
and disconnect it from the underground pipe network. They can be implemented as green roofs, and
infiltration and elements in private gardens and public parks. They are modelled in MIKE URBAN
by increasing initial loss parameter up to 3cm in the corresponding catchments. LAR solutions are
not represented in the latest adaptation plan (apart from NO16), but they are included in the
hydraulic model, at De Gamle By, Righospitalet, Panum institute, and Niels Bohr Science Park,
according to a previous version of Nørrebro Master plan. 
Green paths planned all over Nørrebro are not implemented in MIKE URBAN since they do not
directly address cloudburst challenges and are not easily modelled under the chosen modelling
assumptions. They consist of planting trees and bushes along roads and aim at making the city
greener.  They  are  designed  to  retain  around  60mm  of  precipitation,  with  is  approximately
equivalent to a 10 years rain event. They enable to reduce the amount of water borne by the
drainage system during daily rain events  and small cloudburst.  So that, the  need for sewer

Cloudburst roads (skybrudsveje) are redesigned streets with V-shape, higher curbs or additional
trenches to enable stormwater to flow within the limits of the road. They can also be implemented
as hollow pavements so that water can flow in underneath channels or pipes decoupled from the
sewer system. They are represented as red lines in Figure 31 and blue arrows in Figure 32. All
cloudburst roads are included in MIKE URBAN model apart from NO05 and NO12. They are

Delay roads (forsinkelsesveje) are established on major axis to prevent vulnerable assets with lower
terrain elevation located downstream to be flooded. They are made of green elements on the edge of
the road that temporarily retain stormwater. They are represented as blue lines in Figure 31 and
green lines in Figure 32. All delay roads are included in MIKE URBAN model apart from NO08.
They are modelled by increasing initial loss parameter up to 1cm in the catchment corresponding to
the road.
Delay spaces (forsinkespladser) are established in public parks, squares and car park. They can be
open-air elements or underground. Their purpose is to retain stormwater to reduce the amount of
water  flowing  toward  downstream  roads. They are  connected  to  cloudburst  roads. They are
represented as striped polygons in Figure 32. They are not modelled in MIKE URBAN.
