Abstract
The retinoic acid receptor (RAR) is part of the retinoid system. Together with the
retinoid X receptor (RXR) these act as ligand dependent transcription factors that tar-
get specific sequences, which are known as retinoic acid response elements (RAREs).
Retinoic acids (RA) are the ligands which bind to a RAR/RXR heterodimer and
hereby activates trancription. RA is synthesized in target cells from retinol (vita-
min A). This retinoid system is considered to be an endocrine system where RAs
act as hormones and since RA is synthesized within the cells and thereby is not
dependent on secretion and transportation they are considered to have intracrine
activity.
The retinoid system is regulating a number of biological processes such
as reproduction, embryonic development and immune responses, etc. and it seems
reasonable to believe that a disruption of this system could cause adverse effects
on multiple organs. Endocrine systems being analyzed by regulatory agencies have
largely been focusing on the estrogen, androgen, thyroid and steroidogenesis (EATS)
pathways. In 2020, OECD and the Nordic Council of Ministers published reports,
emphasizing the need for regulatory insights regarding consequences of perturbations
in the retinoid system. Through a non-testing approach, namely by developing a
Quantitative Structure-Activity (QSAR) model this thesis aimed at predicting RAR
antagonists based on qHTS data collected from the Tox21 program. 9,667 structures
had been screened for RAR antagonism and after QSAR-targeted data processing
and multiple structure curation steps the data was divided into training and test set.
By using the LeadScope Predictive Data Miner (LPDM) software a ”cocktail” model
was built from the training set. The cocktail model that was based on a ”single”
model and a composite model (offered by LPDM) was internally validated by using
DTU Foods’s ten-times five-fold cross-validation (10x5-fold-CV) and externally val-
idated by the test set. The predictive performance of the model was evaluated by
using Cooper’s statistics. Hereafter, the training set and test set were combined to
create an extended model based on all of the available data. This extended model
was validated by 10x5-fold-CV. Both models showed a high predictive performance
based on their balanced accuracy and even with an imbalanced data set both models
turned out to be rather sensitive and specific. The top structural alerts for RAR
antagonist actives and RAR antagonist inactives are presented in this thesis.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Over time human lives have become dependent upon man-made chemicals. Espe-
cially, factors such as the growing human population and the higher life expectancy
have influenced this [2]. More people will naturally consume more and as this growth
has not been seen in the past we have to adapt to this new reality and try new solu-
tions. In order to feed everyone it has been necessary to make an effort for yielding
as much as possible from the agriculture [3]. This has resulted in the production of
plant protection agents which will kill off any unwanted species in the crops making
the output larger. Another example where chemicals are used in the food supply
chain are in the packaging. Here, it is common to use plastic as it protects the food
from contamination or damage, it prevents waste by preserving the products for a
longer time and it allows the food to travel longer distances [4].
Chemicals introduced in the industry are expanding yearly and testing these in vivo
by using test animals are time-consuming and costly [5–7]. This has been the ba-
sis for the development of alternative methods, such as non-testing approaches like
quantitative structure-activity relationship (QSAR) models since these are lower in
monetary costs and can be executed faster [7]. Instead of in vivo data QSARs are
developed from cell-based (in vitro) data.
1.1
Purpose
The purpose of this thesis is to develop a QSAR model related to endocrine disrup-
tion. Endocrine disruption can happen by the interference of endocrine disrupting
chemicals 3. Broadly these can be identified as exogenous agents that interfere with
the synthesis, release, transport, distribution, binding, actions, metabolism or elimi-
nation of endogenous hormones which are necessary for homeostasis [8]. Currently,
the endocrine systems being investigated in regulatory toxicology are largely limited
to estrogen, androgen, thyroid and steroidogenesis (EATS) pathways [9]. However,
”nonreproductive” effects need to be taken into account when describing endocrine dis-
ruption associated with exposure of chemicals. Therefore, the QSAR developed in this
study will be focusing on the retinoid system which regulates a number of processes

sponse. Current knowledge classifies the retinoid system as being an endocrine system
with retinoic acids being the receptor ligands having intracrine activity. Specifically,

antagonism.

CHAPTER 2
The Retinoid System
In this first theory chapter, out of a total of five, the retinoid system will be described,
including uptake and transport of retinol, the metabolism of retinol into biologically
active retinoic acid along with a description of the retinoid receptors of which RAR
is the one being modeled.
2.1
Retinoids
The retinoid system is regulating a number of biological processes including repro-
duction, embryonic development, cell growth, vision and immune response [10–15].
Retinoids are recognized as vitamin A (retinol) together with its natural and synthetic
analogs (Figure 2.1) and have to be added through diet [16,17]. The all-trans-retinol
is by definition vitamin A and will here only be referred to as retinol.
Figure 2.1. Chemical structures of a selected subset of retinoids.
==PAGE:20==
4
2 The Retinoid System
Generally, vitamins are a group of molecules with important functions that are nec-
essary in small doses for sustaining life [18]. Apart from being vitamins, retinoids are
considered to be hormones as well, thus the retinoid system is recognized as an en-
docrine system [19,20]. They have hormonal-like signalling of retinoid-specific nuclear
receptors which affect transcription of a broad number of genes [17]. Retinoic acids
are the receptor ligands having intracrine activity that are synthesized from retinol
within target cells [16,19] . Before going into further detail with these receptors, the
uptake, transport, metabolism and storage of retinoids will be described.
2.1.1
Uptake and Transport
In the body the most abundant forms of retinoid are retinol (ROH) and retinyl esters
(RE) [21]. These micronutrients have to be derived from the diet either in the form of
carotenoids (which are orange, yellow and red pigments) obtained from plant-based
sources or ROH and RE obtained from an animal-based diet [17]. In the intestinal
lumen, RE will be hydrolyzed into ROH by retinyl ester hydrolase (REH) before
uptake into enterocytes (the absorptive cells found in the intestine) while carotenoids
and ROH are absorbed without alterations (Figure 2.2).
Figure 2.2. Uptake of retinoids from the intestinal lumen into enterocytes as well as the
transport to target cells. When ROH reaches the target cell, the metabolism can begin.
Details are found in the text.
==PAGE:21==
2.1 Retinoids
5
Here, ROH is re-esterified into RE by transferases such as the lecithin:retinyl acyl-
transferase (LRAT) [17]. Normally, ROH is dependent on chaperone proteins and
intracellularly ROH is bound to cellular retinol-binding proteins (CRBP1 or CRBP2)
which is believed to manage transport [16]. Additionally, the re-esterification of ROH
into RE by LRAT is believed to be due to CRBP binding. Other transferases are con-
sidered to catalyse this reaction as well, such as the acylCoA:retinol acyltransferase
(ARAT).
From the enterocytes, the carotenoids and the newly synthesized RE enter the cir-
culation incoorporated in chylomicrons [17]. Chylomicrons are lipoprotein particles
that transport lipids from the intestine to other targets within the body [22]. Even-
tually, the chylomicrons are taken up by the liver and RE is re-hydrolyzed into ROH,
again by REH [21]. Hereafter, ROH is either re-esterified (catalyzed by LRAT) to
RE for hepatic storage or bound to retinol binding protein 4 (RBP4) for release into
the bloodstream. Here, the ROH-RBP4 complex associates with transthyretin (TTR)
which is believed to stabilize the complex and reduce renal filtration of retinoids [16].
Transport of the ROH-RBP4 complex across target cell membranes may be facilitated
in both directions by STRA6 (stimulated by retinoic acid gene 6) [17].
2.1.2
Metabolism
Within target cells, there is a number of conversions that need to occur for the
transformation of ROH into biologically active retinoic acid (RA) [17].

enables transcriptional activation. The grey triangles surrounding the receptor heterodimer
and RARE represent co-activators/repressors.
ROH is reversibly oxidized to retinaldehyde (all-trans-retinal, Figure 2.1) by retinyl
dehydrogenase 10 (RDH10) [16]. Retinaldehyde is then either irreversibly oxidized
to RA (catalyzed by retinal dehydrogenases (RALDHs)) or reduced back to ROH by

retinaldehyde reductase 3 (DHRS3). However not fully understood, it seems that the
ratio of holo-CRBP to apo-CRBP is determining if retinaldehyde should be further
oxidized to RA or reduced to ROH [17].
Holo-CRBP is CRBP bound to either
ROH or retinaldehyde (CRBP-ROH or CRBP-retinaldehyde complexes) and apo-
CRBP is unbound CRBP [23]. Furthermore, the conversion of ROH to retinaldehyde
is determined by a negative feedback response to available RA. Next, RA can be
degraded to non-active polar metabolites by cytochrome P450 hydroxylase type 26
(CYP26) or be transported to the nucleus by a retinoic acid-binding protein (CRABP).
In the nucleus, RA mainly acts as a ligand to the retinoic acid receptor (RAR) which
form heterodimers with the retinoic X receptor (RXR) for transcriptional activation
[17].
2.2
Retinoid Receptors
RARs and RXRs constitute the retinoid receptor family. Each type has three sub-
types resulting in RARα, RARβ, RARγ and RXRα, RXRβ, RXRγ where RARα,
RXRα and RXRβ are expressed extensively throughout the body, while the remain-
ing (RARβ, RARγ and RXRγ) show tissue-specific expression [24].
They belong
to the same nuclear receptor family as vitamin D, steroid hormone and thyroid hor-
mone receptors [17]. Nuclear receptors are found within cells and function as tran-
scription factors which are activated when a ligand is bound [25,26]. They regulate
the expression of genes affecting processes such as reproduction, metabolism and de-
velopment [27].
Even though the ligand sensitivity varies a lot depending on the
receptor, their structural organization among most of them are similar [24]. RAR
and RXR have similar organization containing an unstructured N-terminal region, a
DNA-binding domain (DBD) with two highly conserved zinc finger motifs that tar-
get the receptor to specific sequences which are known as the retinoic acid response
elements (RAREs) [21]. After DBD is a hinge region, which allows the protein to
be flexible upon simultaneous receptor dimerization and DNA binding [27]. Lastly,
the ligand binding domain (LDB) and dimerization interface can be found in the C-
terminal region. Within the RAR domains there are regions which mediate specific
interactions with RXR forming the aforementioned heterodimer required for activa-
tion of RA target genes.
During this description, RA has been used to define all retinoic acid isomers, but
the binding aﬀinity to retinoid receptors are dependent on the structure.
Hence,
RAR can be activated both by all-trans- and 9-cis-retinoic acid stereoisomers, as well
as synthetic ligands, but the all-trans-retinoic acid is considered as the only endoge-
nous RAR ligand [17, 28]. 9-cis-retinoic acid has been found to be available in very
small doses in the cell and does not bind CRABP which is believed to be necessary
for entering the nucleus [28]. All-trans-retinoic acid will be referred to as RA in this
study. Initially it was suggested that 9-cis-retinoic acid was the endogenous ligand

for RXR, but the physiological relevance remains unclear [17]. In the absence of RA,
there is a recruitment of a co-repressor complex that suppresses transcription. When
RA binds RAR the receptors undergo conformational change which dissociates these
co-repressors and promotes recruitment of co-activators to enable transcriptional acti-
vation [17]. The dissocating of the co-repressor complex is dependent on RA, but RA
is also suﬀicient. Hereby RXR can be considered as a ”silent partner” in RAR-RXR
heterodimers.
2.2.1
RXRs ”Cross-Talk”
Retinoid receptors are dependent on the formation of dimers and these can either be
as the aforementioned RAR/RXR heterodimers or as RXR/RXR homodimers [19].
Additionally, RXR can form heterodimers with other nuclear receptors and hereby
cross-talk with a broad range of signalling pathways. These include the vitamin D re-
ceptor (VDR), the thyroid stimulating hormone receptor (TSHR) forming RXR/VDR
and RXR/TSHR heterodimers, respectively [17,19]. Other receptors have been found
to dimerize with RXR as well [17]. Furthermore, RXR is essential for steroidogenesis
and affect the amount of circulating sex steroid hormones [19]. As a consequence,
there may be competition for RXR for activation of DNA. Thus, retinoids binding
RXR may influence other genes than RXR responsive ones such as vitamin D and
thyroid hormone responsive genes. Similarly, vitamin D and thyroid hormone may
regulate the RXR responsive genes as well.

In this second theory chapter the endocrine disruption is explained along with the

3.1
Endocrine System
The endocrine system is one of the major control systems within the body [29]. It
consists of glands found throughout the body, hormones made up by these glands and
receptors located in target organs and tissues that recognize and respond to hormones.
Hormones are identified as chemical messengers that are synthesized by endocrine
cells in response to specific stimuli [29]. Hereafter, they enter the bloodstream where
they are transported from the site of secretion to target cells. Since the hormones
reach all parts of the body it is important that the receptors responding to these
signals are highly specific. Hormones are responsible for controlling and regulating
processes from conception into old age including the development of the brain and
nervous system, the growth and function of the reproductive system along with the
metabolism and blood sugar levels [30]. When a hormone binds to a receptor the
receptor carries out the hormone’s instructions which could be to alter the cell’s
protein composition.
3.2
Retinoic Acids Act as Hormones
The current knowledge about the retinoid system evidently reveals that this is indeed
an endocrine system where retinoic acids are the synthesized receptor ligands with
hormonal activity [9]. Due to the intracellular transformation of retinols, retinoic
acids do not need to be secreted nor do they require receptors located on cell surfaces
to mediate their activity [31].
Instead they stay within the cell and act directly
as intracellular messengers that regulate cellular function. This type of hormonal
activity is called intracrine activity.

3.3
Endocrine Disruption
Broadly, endocrine disruption can be identified as effects of natural or synthetic ex-
ogenous agents that affect the endocrine system of the individuals who are exposed [8].
Thus, the disruption of the usually normal hormonal functions causes physiological
alterations. By the World Health Organization (WHO) these endocrine disrupting
chemicals (EDCs) have been defined as exogenous substances or mixtures that al-
ter function(s) of the endocrine system and cause adverse health effects in an intact
organism, or its progeny, or (sub)populations [32]. Over time, it has been shown
that EDCs act at multiple sites and use multiple mechanisms of action. EDCs can
influence any step in an endocrine system such as the synthesis, release, transport,
receptor binding and metabolism [32]. Of these, the receptor-mediated mechanisms
have received the most attention even though it has been found that the remaining
mechanisms are equally important.
As mentioned in the beginning of this chapter, hormones are responsible for con-
trolling and regulating vital physiological systems that are critical and necessary.
Timing of EDC onset is significant for the physiological consequence following the
exposure [30]. The sensitivity to EDCs is at its highest during tissue development,
thus the developmental effects will be occurring at lower doses than what is required
for effects in adults. Furthermore, the consequence of exposure during development
will remain through life and the tissues have a different predisposition for disease
in adulthood compared to that of a non-exposed tissue [30]. In adults, an EDC is
believed to have short-term effects meaning that when it is present the effect can be
found, but it will diminish when the EDC is withdrawn.
3.4
Retinoid System Perturbations
Since the retinoid system are essential for many crucial functions within the body (e.g.
reproduction, embryonic development, adult growth and development, etc.) it seems
reasonable to believe that a perturbation of this system could cause adverse effects
on multiple organs. It has been shown that cleft palate can be induced by triazole
fungicides by CYP26-mediated RAR activation while others suggest that retinoids
are may play a role in Alzheimer’s disease [33,34]. Furthermore, a publication from
the Nordic Council of Ministers called ”Retinoids in Mammalian Reproduction, with
an Initial Scoping Effort to Identify Regulatory Methods” and a Draft Detailed Re-
view Paper (DRP) by The Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Develop-
ment (OECD) which reviews the: ”role of retinoids in female and male reproduction,
craniofacial/skeletal development, and the nervous system” both from 2020 clearly
emphasizes the need for regulatory insights [17,21]. Therefore, the study conducted
in this thesis is highly relevant.

4.1
Traditional Risk Assessment
Toxicology is the investigation of the adverse effects chemicals can have on living
organisms. It is important to design strategies to preclude environmental contami-
nation and through testing, it can be determined whether the chemical is damaging
or harmless for the organism in focus. Regulatory toxicology is when governmental
agencies use these scientific insights to develop regulations and other strategies for
reducing and controlling exposure to alarming chemicals.
Generally, the term ”risk” is used in situations where there is a chance, but not
a certainty, that something undesirable may happen. Hence, risk can be considered
as a concept covering two aspects; (1) the possibility of an unwanted outcome and
(2) the uncertainty over the occurrence and degree of that outcome [35]. Since it is
known that there is a possibility for a substance to be harmful for human health it
is necessary to make an assessment to further determine whether or not it should be
regulated. Figure 4.1 shows the steps in traditional toxicology testing for both the
risk assessment and management [1, 35, 36]. As this project is centered around the
assessment of chemicals the steps in risk management will only be presented, but not
further described.
Hazard Identification
Here, the goal is to identify or develop information that could suggest or confirm
whether the chemical composes a hazard or not [1, 37]. This has traditionally been
identified as the observable effects of exposure to a toxic chemical in a test animal.

4.2.1
High- to Low-Dose Extrapolation
By providing the test animals with a high dose of substance it has been possible to
identify the adverse apical endpoints [40]. This strategy has been defended by the
need for statistical power to observe a high number of occurrences of harmful and
toxic responses as the test population is small and only experiences short exposures
[40]. Often, people will not be in danger of high dose exposures and this low-dose
extrapolation from high-dose data has shown to present results which are unrealistic
in real life. Lower dose responses are as important as the high-dose responses and
should not be ignored. Hormones and EDCs produce non-linear responses and in some
cases the curves are more complex than a sigmoid-shaped curve [30]. Furthermore,
they can be non-monotonic meaning that the slope of the curve changes sign at some
point within the range of the examined doses [43]. Therefore, the largest response
might not be shown at a high dose and these non-monotonic dose-responses have been
reported for more than 60 EDCs [30]. Hence, the extrapolation from the high dose
used experimentally to the lower dose expected in the environment can give incorrect
results [43].
4.2.2
Animal to Human Extrapolation
Differences in the physiologic systems of test animals and humans makes it diﬀicult
to extrapolate the experimental findings [40].
The pharmaceutical thalidomide is
a classic, yet catastrophic, example of when this extrapolation failed. It was used
to treat morning sickness in pregnant women in the late 1950’s and early 1960’s and
toxic responses, such as severe malformations of the fetus, were undetected in the rats
used for testing [44]. Later, it was shown that humans are 100 times more sensitive
to this response than rats due to the biological differences [45]. Even though this

animal-to-human extrapolation is still a challenging task.
4.3
Toxicity Testing in The 21st Century
These limitations have been known by the regulatory toxicology community for years,
but there has been a lack of feasible testing alternatives and other technologies have
been requested due to the growing need for testing chemicals used in commerce [7,40].
Furthermore, it is considered beneficial to make testing less time-consuming, low-cost

4.3.1

In 2007, the U.S. National Academy of Sciences (NAS) released a report entitled

increase the eﬀiciency of testing by replacing the challenging animal-based in vivo
experiments with human-based in vitro toxicity pathway assays.
Here, testing is
performed on human cells or cell lines [40]. The growing knowledge about complex
cellular response networks (i.e. the interactions between genes, proteins and other
molecules that establish cellular processes) and how environmental factors can cause

5.1
Machine Learning Approach
Computer algorithms being able to emulate the human intelligence and learn from
experience have been explored in various fields including statistics (e.g. multivariate
regression and classification), computer science (e.g. artificial intelligence and ma-
chine learning) and engineering (e.g. pattern recognition) [58]. These approaches
are all reliant on data they can be trained upon. Here, the learning methods are
algorithms, which are typically implemented in a software, that estimate unknown
dependencies between the system’s inputs and outputs from available data consisting
both of input and output samples [59].
The techniques used in the development of a QSAR model are based upon two fun-
damental principles: (1) the hypothesis that, under similar environmental conditions,
structurally similar compounds behave alike and (2) that behavioral dissimilarities
found in compounds are correlated with variations in structure and composition [60].
5.2
Similar Property Principle
The similar property principle was presented in the book Concepts and Applications
of Molecular Similarity by chemists Mark A. Johnson and Gerald M. Maggiora in
1990 [61].
The similar property principle states that similar chemical compounds
have similar properties [62]. It can be diﬀicult to make a formal definition of how sim-
ilarity should be understood and measured, since this can be perceived and described
==PAGE:34==
18
5 Quantitative Structure-Activity Relationship Models
differently according to the profession asked. However, a chemist would probably
describe similar compounds as chemicals having approximately similar backbone and
almost the same functional groups. Other would regard two chemicals as being simi-
lar if they match each other topologically and if their connecting bonds had a large
number of common features [62]. Hence, the similarity between chemicals can be
understood as either structural or functional.
5.3
Definition
QSARs are built upon machine learning algorithms, usually implemented in soft-
ware, and describes the quantitative relationship between chemical structures and
their properties [51,63,64]. The modeled endpoint may be categorical or continuous.
The chemical characteristics of a molecule in numerical form are encoded within ’de-
scriptors’ and are the predictor variables [51]. These are commonly described as the
independent variables or structural/compositional components as well. The response
variables, e.g. the reactivity, toxicity or bioactivity of a chemical, are described as
’activities’ and are the dependent variables [60].
Thus, the goal is to establish a
mathematical correlation between the descriptors and the activity regarding a spe-
cific endpoint [63]. The selection of descriptors is a crucial step in QSAR modeling
since these are used to define the structures. The model is trained on data generated
from chemicals with known structures and is used to predict the activity of untested
chemicals [51].
5.4
Model Development
Figure 5.1 illustrates an overview of the workflow required in the development of
a QSAR model. The first step in developing a QSAR model is the collection and
processing of data [63, 65]. The next step is to prepare the data set used for the
training of the model (i.e. training set) and the set used for external validation of
the model (i.e. test set) [63]. Based on the training set the descriptors have to be
selected which is done during the development of the model. Hereafter the model is
externally validated. The workflow is presented in Figure 5.1 and in the following
sections each part will be explained.
5.4.1

mation, i.e. information about the chemical’s characteristics and the activity (either
continuous or categorical) of the chemical [63]. The descriptors used for investigating
RAR antagonism are structural and physico-chemical. The structural descriptors are
most often represented in a machine-readable two dimensional (2D-QSAR) format,

5.4.1.1
Collection
The data can be acquired in three ways and depends not on either endpoint or descrip-
tors. [60]: 1) the data can be obtained through new laboratory experiments; 2) it can
found in the literature from reports, academic journals, dissertations, etc; and 3) it
can be obtained from computer programs with already existing, embedded models or
from electronic databases containing experimental endpoint data and/or descriptor
values gathered from academic publications. Each of these versions of data collections
has its benefits and limitations [60]. Some view option 1) with obtaining new experi-
mental data as having the greatest reliability, especially if it has been gathered from
the same laboratory, produced by the same scientists. Generally, it is challenging to
obtain consistent reliable data from the literature as this often require multiple re-
sources [60]. Consistency when generating data is a key factor in producing a reliable
model, but some view data found in the literature as being more diverse. Hereby it
can be argued that these models are reflecting the ’real world’ more realistically [60].
If data from computer programs is produced by an embedded model there is a risk
of circular reasoning and therefore these are often believed to be unsuitable for the
development of a new QSAR model.
5.4.1.2
Data Processing and Structure Curation
As mentioned, the performance of a model is highly correlated with the data used for
development when applying machine learning techniques. Thus, checking the data
for errors is a crucial step [63,65,69]. Even in situations where the experimental data
has been obtained from the same laboratory, generated by the same team it is very
important to investigate the data in detail [65]. In this study, the OASIS Database
Manager has been used for processing of data. This software stores chemical struc-
tures along with process and manages information about these compounds [70].
Most software used to compute the molecular descriptors are unable to model in-
organics and therefore these structures should be identified and filtered out [63, 65,
69]. Similarly, compound mixtures should be identified and removed. Removal of
organometallics is likewise recommended before computation of molecular descrip-
tors [63,71]. Salts can be present in the data set and need to be handled since their
presence can generate various errors when calculating descriptors [65,69]. Exclusion
of salts are preferred, but removal of metal counterions followed by neutralization of
the remaining organic compound is a common procedure for maintaining data [65].
Additionally, standardization of tautomeric forms, ring aromatization and normaliza-
tion of selected chemotypes could similarly be considered here [63,65].
To achieve correct model development and validation it is important to ensure that
the data set does not include duplicates, i.e. each structure should only be represented
once [50, 65, 71]. For external validation, the data present in the test set should be
unquestionably excluded from the data used for training [71]. If there are duplicates

present in the data set there is a possibility that the same structures are present
both in training and test sets. If cross-validation is performed, duplicates can sim-
ilarly occur in both the training subsets and their respective validation subsets. In

which is not actually the case [50].
Duplicates can arise for a number of reasons

due to inconsistency.
5.4.1.3
Preparation of Training and Test Sets
Not all, but most QSAR models are reliant on statistical machine learning algorithms
which, as mentioned, are dependent on training from data. Thus, through software
the machine should automatically learn a function that can be used to predict the
endpoint value of untested compounds. For this study the data is divided into two
separate subsets, namely the larger training set and the smaller test set. The training
set is the subset of data which will be used for model establishment and the test set
is the remaining data that will be used for the external validation of the model. It is
extremely important that the test set is completely left out of model development [72].
There are several ways to split the data into these subsets which are all built on
either two fundamental ideas, random or rational splitting [63, 71]. The latter can
either focus on the endpoint activity or the descriptor space.
Each having their
own benefits and limitations. Studies have shown that rational splitting (e.g. by
clustering techniques or artificial neural networks (ANNs)) generate more predictive
QSARs, but at the same time many suggest that since the impact in performance is
insignificant it is acceptable to use random splitting [73]. By rational splitting it is
easier to take uncommon active structures into account and making sure that they
are represented in the training set [73]. If not focused on, these structures might
only be represented in the test data and hereby they are not considered in the model.
Generally, it is diﬀicult to make a robust model if the data set consists of similar
structures and it should be stressed that the model performs better with a diverse
set of structures. The model produced in this study will be based on all data in an
extended model and thereby all structures will be included in the model.
Next, the sizes of the training and test sets should be determined. It is not pos-
sible to formulate a general rule when it comes to the impact of training set size
on the quality of prediction [74]. However, the amount of data used in a test set is
usually between 10% and 30% [72]. Again, the preferred training set size should be
based on the particular data set along with the type of descriptors used for model

22

development. The training set size should be big enough so as much information as
possible is retained in the model, but at the same time overfitting (i.e. the model is
too specific) should be avoided [71,74].
Lastly, there needs to be a balance in the response variables of the training and
test sets [63,71]. When using HT data, such as Tox21 and ToxCast data, it is com-
mon that the chemicals having an active response on the endpoint are belonging to a
smaller group compared to the group of compounds having an inactive responses [63].
If the data sets used for training is imbalanced then there is a tendency for the model
to predict structures for the larger class (i.e. as being inactive). Thus, the model
will have a high specificity and a low sensitivity performance. Therefore it is recom-
mended that the training is balanced which can be done by either oversampling of
the smaller class or undersampling of the bigger class [63].
5.4.2
Descriptors
Descriptors are the chemical features that characterize a molecule and they are a
prerequisite for being able to establish the correlation between structures and prop-
erties in QSAR modeling. Different types of descriptor values include: topological,
geometric or electronic [49]. Then there are the constitutional descriptors which are
unaffected to conformational change since these include the number of atoms and
bonds, atom type, ring count and the molecular weight [49]. Lastly, there are the
structural or fragment based descriptors which represent properties related to sub-
structural motifs [49,51].
Structural keys or molecular fingerprints are sets of descriptors representing the ab-
sence or presence of a structural fragment [75,76]. The information is encoded in an
array of binary values, i.e. 0 if the feature is absent and 1 if the feature is present. The
keys are reliant on a pre-defined fragment dictionary being a list of fragments selected
to be significant [76]. Molecular holograms are similar to fingerprints, but molecular
features are not pre-defined. Furthermore, holograms use counts of features instead of
registering presence or absence [76]. Since descriptors usually cover different numer-
ical ranges it can be necessary to perform transformation on these, and perhaps on
the response variable, too [71]. If they are not transformed the descriptors with larger
values would dominate others which could mean that the validity of the statistical
analysis is weakened.
It is not uncommon that some of the generated descriptors are redundant and it
is therefore crucial for the predictive performance that these are removed [71]. Re-
dundant descriptors are descriptors which are either common in almost all of the
structures or descriptors which are only present for a single structure [52, 77]. In
both situations the extra information does not contribute to the overall trend found
in the training set. Similarly, some of the descriptors can be found to be correlated
==PAGE:39==
5.4 Model Development
23
which will have to be taken into account by reduction techniques such as principal
component analysis (PCA) [75]. PCA creates uncorrelated hidden variables from the
descriptors.
For a QSAR model to be predictive and reliable it is important to select a small subset
from the large pool of descriptors generated from the training set. Thus, a pitfall in
QSAR development is that selecting too many descriptors (i.e. overfitting, where not
only the activity itself but also random effects/’noise’ is being modeled) will make the
model too complex, too specific and diﬀicult to interpret [71]. There is a great range
of different selection techniques assigned to one of the following categories: filtering,
wrapper or embedded (hybrid) [49, 60]. Filters arrange the descriptors into subsets
as a pre-processing step and a feature relevance score is calculated resulting in the
removal of low-scoring features. The biggest advantage of using a filtering approach is
that they require little computational time due to their simplicity [49]. Furthermore,
they are independent of the classifier used. Examples include Euclidean distance, Mu-
tual information (MI), and correlation methods [49]. Next, are the wrapper methods
which have better performances than the filter approaches. However, they are more
costly in computational time and complexity due to the dependence on classifier [49].
Strategies such as heuristics (e.g. forward selection and backward elimination) and
step-wise regression can here be used to add or remove features. The embedded meth-
ods are a combination of the filter and wrapper methods where algorithms from both
categories are used. This is often advisable since the advantages from the approaches
are preserved while some of the disadvantages are being reduced [49].

5.4.4

After the QSAR model is built it is necessary to make performance measures, mean-
ing that it should be validated statistically through goodness-of-fit, robustness and
predictivity [38]. Many statistical performance approaches can be applied in this step
and in the report, ”Guidance Document On the Validation of (Quantitative) Structure-

more common algorithms [38]. In this study, the internal validation of the model is

5.4.4.2
Cross-Validation
Cross-validation (CV) can be applied in many versions and is a popular technique for
validating machine learning algorithms [72]. The version used for validation of the
developed QSAR model is the k-fold-cross-validation. Here, the training data is split
into k equally sized parts [72]. Common numbers for k are 5 and 10, i.e. 5-fold-cross-
validation and 10-fold-cross-validation. Now, k-1 is used for training and the k-th
part is used for testing [72]. If possible, this is repeated a number of time making
sure there is no bias from the first random split. These k ”external validations” are
averaged to give an overall statistics with the validations’ variance expressed as the
standard deviation (SD) [81].
5.4.4.3
External Validation
External validation is dependent on a test set, which is data left out after data
processing, before model development. It is extremely important that the data in the
test set has not been used in training of the model. The predictivity estimate of the
model found after k-fold-cross-validation is the performance of the model applied to
the test set [72].
5.4.5
Leadscope Predictive Data Miner
The software used in this study is the commercial QSAR modeling software Lead-
Scope Predictive Data Miner (LPDM) which is part of the LeadScope Enterprise
Server [82]. This is used by pharmaceutical researchers (as well as academia and
regulators) to draw knowledge from the large amounts of data being produced by
e.g. HTS [83]. Furthermore, it provides tools to find correlations between biological
activity and structural features, which is the fundamentals of QSAR modeling [83].
When importing training data into LPDM it calculates nine continuous descriptors
==PAGE:42==
26
5 Quantitative Structure-Activity Relationship Models
for all compounds, namely the AlogP, hydrogen bond acceptors and donors, Lipinski
score, molecular weight, parent atom number, parent molecular weight, polar surface
area and rotateable bonds [84, 85]. Furthermore, the structural similarities between
compounds are assessed by comparing compound fingerprints represented as a vector
of binary values indicating if a feature is present or absent [81, 86]. These features
were selected from a library of 27,000 2D structural keys [86].
After import, there is the option to include an assembly of scaffolds, also called
macrostructures (MSA), which are dynamically built creating larger fragments. They
are assembled from the aforementioned keys gathered from the training set [86]. Ad-
ditionally, scaffolds can be added from previously generated data set scaffolds or by
importing descriptors from external sources [84]. The next step involves removal of
features that are present in one or all compound(s) [86]. Hereafter, simple significance
tests (t-test for continuous responses and χ-test for categorical responses) are used to
evaluate the influence of each descriptor [81,86]. Top 30% of the descriptors found in
the χ-test are selected for categorical responses and the top and bottom 15% found
in t-test are selected for continuous responses [85].
Now for the model development. For continuous responses LPDM uses the partial
least squares (PLS) method for model development. The model built upon categorical
responses is based upon partial logistic regression (PLR) [86]. The data obtained from
the RAR antagonism assays contain categorical response variables and the model will
therefore be developed using PLR. Further descriptor selection occurs through step-
wise extraction of features (linear combinations of predictor values) that are highly
correlated with a dependent response variable [86]. During model development, k-fold
cross-validation is used gaining statistical measures such as a predicted residual sum
of square (PRESS) [84]. Descriptors having low loading, low weight and are high in
residuals are identified and between 5 and 25 are removed. This reduced descriptor
set is used again for another round of model development and cross-validation. This
is repeated until up to 15 models have been developed and the one having the lowest
PRESS is selected as the final model [84].
Since toxicological data sets often are imbalanced LPDM offers to develop composite
models [85]. Here, a modeler can set a ratio between the two activity classes and
make up to ten sub-models, where the smaller (often the active) class is resampled.
Then a composite model is generated by aggregating these sub-models [85]. This step
is done to increase the sensitivity of the model.

This fifth and last chapter presents guidelines that should be followed in order to use
QSARs in regulations.
6.1
OECD QSAR Guidelines
In 2004, the OECD agreed on five principles for the validation of QSAR in models for
scientific or regulatory use [87,88]. The ”The OECD Principles of (Q)SAR Validation”
report had been requested to provide a unified structure for QSAR validation as
the regulatory use of these varies significantly between agencies [55]. Additionally,
these principles should promote a shared acceptance of QSAR models by enhancing
transparency and consistency [87]. If a QSAR should be accepted for regulatory use
the model, their validation as well as these five principles have to be documented in the
QSAR Model Reporting Format (QMRF) [55]. Furthermore, the predictions should
be documented in the QSAR Prediction Reporting Format (QPRF). Authorities can
use QMRF and QPRF to assess the model and conclude whether or not the prediction
should be included in a chemical risk assessment [55].
The OECD Principles for Validation of QSAR Models
1. A Defined Endpoint
The QSAR modeled should have a clearly defined endpoint. An endpoint refers to
any biological, physico-chemical or environmental effect that can be modeled, i.e. it
has to be measurable [38]. It is preferred that the data used for training a QSAR
model is homogeneous and has been produced in the same laboratory with the same
protocols. However, this is not always possible and therefore data produced in dif-
ferent laboratories using different protocols are often combined which can affect the
performance of the model. By ensuring transparency and clarity in the endpoint def-
inition and presentation of training data it will be easier for a user to determine the
quality of the model [38].
==PAGE:44==
28
6 OECD QSAR Guidelines
2. An Unambiguous Algorithm
One of the most important presumption in scientific integrity is the ability to explain
the results very thoroughly so the experiments and results can be reproduced. There-
fore the algorithm used to relate the endpoint data to the descriptors of the chemical
structure should be explained in such detail that the findings can be produced or
tested by others in the scientific community [38]. If the QSAR model has been de-
veloped through a commercial software, and in these cases it is important that the
software and modeling process is described, if possible. A lack of transparency with
respect to the algorithms used will negatively influence the regulatory acceptability.
3. A Defined Domain of Applicability
QSAR predictions are limited generally to the chemicals that are structurally similar
to the ones used for model development. This principle requires the developers to
define the limitations with respect to the models structural domain [38]. Chemicals
that satisfy the scope of the model are considered to be within the applicability
domain (AD) and are classified as interpolated whereas the ones outside AD are
classified as extrapolated [89]. The given model is more reliable for predictions of
interpolated compared to extrapolated and this applicability domain should be clearly
stated. The following principle is therefore highly dependent on the model’s AD.
4. Appropriate Measures of Goodness-of-fit, Robustness and Predictivity
To fulfill this principle both the internal performance (i.e. goodness-of-fit and robust-
ness) and the predictive performance (by using an appropriate test set) should be
determined [38]. These are covered in 5.4.4. Determining a measure of performance
is usually dependent on the methods and parameters used. For categorical models
Cooper statistics can be used, as being the case in this study, and for continuous
models R2 and Q2 are usually used.
5. A Mechanistic Interpretation
The intention is to ensure that if any mechanistic association between descriptors and
endpoint is identified this is documented [38]. However, it is acknowledged that it
is not always possible to provide this association, but some consideration should be
given to the possibility of a mechanistic association between the descriptors and the
endpoint [87].

sensitive and more specific. Thus, the cocktail approach is aiming at increasing the

and their degradation products.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
In this thesis, two QSAR models for RAR antagonism have been developed from
Tox21 in vitro data based on a concentration threshold of 50µM. First, an initial
model was developed which was validated internally by 10x 5-fold-cross-validation
and externally by a test set. By internal validation, the model was shown to have a
high predictive performance with a balanced accuracy of 89.0% and a low SD of 3.5%.
Data sets originating from toxicological experiments are often imbalanced, which has
been the case for the RAR data here as well. This means, that the sets contain more
inactive structures compared to active ones, which can be seen as the models often
have high specificity and a lower sensitivity. However, through a controlled splitting
of data into the training and test sets ensuring that it does not occur completely
random this was taken into account. Additionally, software used to model toxicolog-
ical data is often aware of this and has techniques to minimize this imbalance. The
software used here, LeadScope, can handle a ratio of actives to inacitves of 1:10 and
for the data used here the ratio was 1:5. Hereby, the overall sensitivity and specificity
are not that dissimilar and the model has been shown to be high in sensitivity and
specificity. The external validation showed similar results with a balanced accuracy
of 90.2 %.
Hereafter the extended model was developed which was built using all training and
test data. This model could naturally only be validated internally and here it was
again by 10x 5-fold-cross-validation. The performance for the extended model showed
similar results as the initial model and the biggest difference between these models
were a lower SD in the overall sensitivity. This shows a high degree of robustness of
the model as the variance in predictive performances are low. The balanced accuracy
was lower (88.8%) for the extended model, but again the SD was lower and hereby the
model will more often predict chemicals as expected. Lastly, the top structural alerts
for RAR antagonist actives and RAR antagonist inactives were identified showing a
trend in both classes.
