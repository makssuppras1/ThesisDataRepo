ABSTRACT 
 
Snakebite at first glance does not seem to be a problem, which should concern a 
modern human. It is, however, one of the most neglected diseases of our era as declared by 
the WHO. It is estimated that each year, 5 million bites occur, leading to 150,000 deaths and 
a striking 400,000 amputations. The lucky ones who manage to avoid a deadly outcome of 
snakebite envenoming are often mentally and physically scarred for life. Current treatment 
against snakebite is based on the administration of animal-derived antivenom. Even though 
currently available antivenoms save lives, they are of a non-human origin, which can inflict 
severe adverse reactions when administered to human patients. This highlights the need for 
a new generation of antivenoms, using modern biotechnological techniques. 
A novel approach of developing recombinant monoclonal fully human antivenoms, 
has been guiding the research of this thesis. The main focus was to discover and test in vivo 
the neutralization potential of fully human recombinant antibodies against medically relevant 
toxins from the venom of black mamba (Dendroaspis polylepis), green mamba (Dendroaspis 
angusticeps), and monocled cobra (Naja kaouthia). Toxins of focus were selected by 
employing the toxicity score, previously established through the toxicovenomic approach. 
Using a unique antibody library and employing the phage display technique, a suite of 
scFv antibodies, expressed in E. coli has been discovered. Screening with ELISA was used to 
identify the most promising clones. Based on their sequences, 18 of the scFvs were converted 
into IgG format and expressed in mammalian Expi-293 cells. A mouse model was used for 
preclinical evaluation of recombinant human IgGs in neutralizing the effects of toxins, present 
in the venom of black mamba or monocled cobra. 
IgGs were incubated with their target toxins or whole venom in different molar ratios 
and administered either by intracerebroventricular or intravenous route.  
All 3 monoclonal human IgGs against a-cobratoxin from monocled cobra significantly 
prolonged survival of mice administered with lethal doses (4 μg) of the a-cobratoxin. One of 
the discovered human IgGs targeting a-cobratoxin provided prolonged survival when tested 
both against the toxin itself and when tested against the whole venom from N. kaouthia. This 
underlines the importance of a-cobratoxin in N. kaouthia venom, and the applicability of the 
Toxicity Score in toxicovenomics for target selection. 
In total, 15 monoclonal human IgGs were tested against black mamba toxins, all 
providing prolonged survival in mice. Remarkably, 9 out of 15 human IgGs provided full 
protection in mice administered with lethal doses (0.5 µg) of the black mamba toxins. 
Moreover, some IgGs showed a cross-reactive neutralization potential when tested with 
various different toxins from the venom of black mamba. Additionally, a mixture of selected 
IgG antibodies targeting several different toxins successfully neutralized whole venom from 
black mamba, providing a strong proof of concept. 
For the first time, discovery of monoclonal fully human IgGs that are capable of 
neutralizing snake toxins in vivo has been reported. Snake toxin neutralizing antibodies could 
be the first step in the development of recombinant snake antivenoms and contribute to 
safer, more affordable, and more effective treatment options against snakebite envenoming 
in the future.

1 INTRODUCTION  
1.1 Snakebite is one of the most neglected tropical diseases of our era 
It is difficult to fully comprehend the magnitude and complexity of the snakebite problem if 
you do not live in an area in which you are at risk for an encounter with a venomous snake. 
Nonetheless, snakebite each year occurs in more than 5 million cases and results in over 2.5 
million envenomings, causing 150,000 deaths, and a devastating 400,000 amputations, 
putting at risk mainly farmers and their children who live in snake infested areas (Figure 1) 
(1). Even when victims manage to survive, they are often left with mutilated bodies and sky-
high medical bills, which leave them affected for life. The health statistics are mostly based 
on the number of reported hospitalized cases to the authorities, which does not reflect the 
extent of the problem. In low income countries, victims often do not seek medical help or fail 
to reach the hospital (2). The lack of reliable information leaves the epidemiological impact 
of snakebite grossly underestimated and seemingly unimportant to health authorities, 
pharmaceutical companies or research funding agencies around the world (3). Because most 
snakebites occur in sub-Saharan Africa, Asia, Latin America and Papua-New Guinea, almost 
exclusively affecting poor and politically unempowered people living in rural parts of generally 
low-income, tropical countries (4), the World Health Organization (WHO) recently re-
incorporated snakebite in its list of neglected tropical diseases, which will hopefully bring 
more interest in tackling the problem in the future (5).

1.2 Present: Traditional animal-derived antivenoms 
The currently available medical treatment against snake envenoming consists of 
administration of animal-derived antivenom, containing toxin-targeting antibodies. The 
described method was developed by Calmette in the late 19th century and has not changed 
much since (Figure 2) (6).  Antivenom production starts with the collection of selected snake 
venom which is used for repetitive injections of large animals (typically horse or sheep). 
Immunization is either performed using whole venom from an individual species, resulting in 
monospecific antivenom, or a mixture of venom from multiple species, giving rise to a 
polyspecific antivenom. After a prolonged immunization period, which can take up to 1.5 
years, animals build up an antibody response against the different components from the

snake venom. Next, blood is drawn and the serum is isolated by removal of blood cells. 
Antibodies present in the serum are then purified by precipitating other serum proteins after 
which the antibody solution is formulated to ensure better stability of the antiserum for long-
term storage (7) (8) .

One of the challenges associated with current antivenoms is the lack of adequate supply to 
remote areas where the most snakebites occur, (9) and even though current antivenoms 
succeed to neutralize snake venoms and save lives, they come with number of drawbacks. 
The production scheme relies on the use of animals, which leads to high batch to batch 
variation (10). Another problem is variation in the quality of the antivenom among different 
manufacturers due to the differences in technological resources, making it difficult to design 
a standardized treatment (11). Moreover, currently produced antivenoms often contain 
impurities and contaminants (12), which can be hazardous to the recipients as there is a lack 
of strict quality control and both preclinical and clinical assessment of antivenom efficacy and 
safety (13). The production process itself is time consuming and expensive, placing the price 
in the range of $60-600 per treatment (14). Additionally, antivenoms contain a mixture of 
different antibodies, where only a small subset recognizes specific toxins from the snake 
venom, leading to decreased therapeutic efficacy. Current antivenoms are also not very 
efficient in neutralizing the toxins responsible for the local pathological effects in 
envenomings by viperid and some elapid snake species (13). Due to their heterologous 
nature, they are often incompatible with the human immune system, resulting in acute 
anaphylaxis and delayed serum sickness adverse reactions, which in worst case scenarios may 
result in death of the patient (15)(16).  
 
1.3 Current advances in antivenom development 
Numerous limitations of existing antivenoms call for improvements, which could be 
implemented through the use of modern biotechnological techniques. So far, several 
==PAGE:11==

10
approaches have been tested, however, none of the alternative therapeutics mentioned 
below are currently being used in clinic for snake envenomation treatment. Novel strategies 
include the improved antisera production by next generation immunization technologies, use 
of small organic compounds, metal ion chelators, carbohydrates, peptides, nanobodies, and 
recombinantly produced antibodies. The challenges that present themselves with use of 
alternative therapeutics are often related to their stability, size and its effect on 
pharmacokinetics, affecting their ability to reach the target, production costs, safety and 
regulatory reservations on their way to the market. 
 
1.3.1 Novel immunization techniques 
One of the challenges in current antivenom production is unbalanced response against the 
medically relevant toxins from the snake venom, due to composition variation of the injected 
venom, amplified by the immunogenic response variation among different specimens used 
for repetitive immunizations (17). Variation in response could be controlled using 
standardized immunogens, designed to contain only the medically relevant ones. So far, a 
successful use of DNA strings (18) (19), synthetic peptides (20) and recombinantly produced 
toxins (21) was reported as an alternative to immunization with whole venom or mixture of 
several different venoms. This could provide uniform immunization mixtures, leading to 
targeted antibody development. Additionally, an alternative administration route of 
immunizing mice in the epidermal layer of their abdominal region with a multi-epitope DNA 
immunogen using a GeneGun has been reported (22). However, the antibody mixture 
produced in such fashion still originates from a non-human organism and therefore may be 
immunogenic, leading to adverse reactions and expensive production (16). 
 
1.3.2 Small molecule inhibitors against animal toxins 
Toxins have been vastly studied as drug leads, yet significantly less has been done towards 
the development of the molecules, where toxins themselves are the target. Small inhibitors, 
targeting key venom toxins have been proposed to diminish the risk for adverse reactions, 
related to immunogenic reactions of animal-derived antivenoms. Molecules that have been 
isolated from plants, microorganisms and animals, or synthetically prepared have been 
reported to have an ability to neutralize active components from the snake venom. Small 
molecule inhibitors typically target enzymatically active toxins, by mimicking the natural 
substrate or scavenging an important cofactor for the apoenzyme (23). Synthetic compounds, 
varespladib (24), suramin (25) and compounds isolated from plants, such as rosmarinic acid 
(26) have shown the ability to neutralize phospholipase A2 (PLA2) activity. Batimastat and 
EDTA exert an inhibitory effect by mimicking the cleavage site of metalloproteinase 
substrates, causing a decrease in local haemorrhage and dermonecrosis when tested in vivo 
(27). Moreover, heparin (28) has been shown to inhibit hyaluronidase activity. Some of the 
molecules proposed above, such as metalloproteinase inhibitors are already being used in 
cancer therapy, indicating clinical safety and efficacy (29). Recently, the use of functional 
oligonucleotides such as DNA aptamers has been proposed for neutralization of lethal peptide 
toxins (30). DNA aptamers are small in size and are relatively easy to produce, they can be 
stored at ambient temperature and are not very immunogenic or toxic. Furthermore, such 
molecules could be produced cost effectively and have a fast biodistribution in the patients, 
which in turn, however leads to a very short half-life, limiting their use. Although none of the 
molecules could be used as a single treatment, due to the complexity of snake venom and its 
multiple components which need to be neutralized to successfully lessen the devastating 
symptoms of envenoming, they could supplement current therapies, by targeting 
components of the venom for which no immune response can be triggered naturally.

1.3.3 Naturally occurring animal toxin neutralizing proteins 
It has been proven that some animals, such as the South American opossum (31), ground 
squirrels (32), and snakes themselves (33) have naturally occurring protective proteins against 
snake venom components, which have shown 4-6 times greater toxin neutralizing capacity 
than commercially available antivenoms, however, one of the limitations of such molecules 
could be expression of non-standard protein formats. They are highly variable in molecular 
structure, which could lead to high production costs. Additionally, the combined heterologous 
origin and often large size of protein type molecules are very likely to provoke immunogenic 
reactions in human recipients. 
 
1.3.4 Toxin targeting antibodies 
Different antibody formats from both animal and recombinant expression systems have been 
suggested as potential snake envenomation therapy. The discovery of monoclonal antibodies 
targeting toxins from snakes (34), scorpions (35), spiders (36), and bees (37) has been 
reported, first introduced through hybridoma technology in 1982, where they developed 
antibodies in mice targeting a toxin from the black necked spitting cobra (Naja nigricollis) (38), 
showing neutralizing activity of the toxin both in vitro and in vivo . Several murine monoclonal 
antibodies targeting toxins have been developed since (39) (40). Regrettably, non-human 
antibodies do not solve the problems of immunogenicity due to their heterologous nature. 
The use of transgenic humanized animals, capable of producing human IgGs against snake 
toxins has been suggested to limit immunogenic adverse reactions in humans, which would 
however, not solve the problem of high production costs and regulative reservations. 
 
Current antivenoms used in clinic are manufactured in IgG, F(ab)2 or Fab antibody format 
(Figure 3). Horses which are most commonly used for the production, generate antibodies in 
the IgG format, which can be treated with papain or pepsin in order to remove Fc region and 
obtain F(ab)2 or Fab antibody formats, leading to reduced immunogenicity and improved 
safety (8). However, modifications in antibody format cause significant changes in 
pharmacokinetic properties (41) (42). Fab antibodies have been shown to better reach and 
neutralize the toxins in deep tissue, but have a reduced half-life, compared to IgG antibodies, 
whereas F(ab)2 pharmacokinetic profiles being somewhere between IgG and Fab. The IgG 
format may be a good solution for targeting systemically acting toxins, whereas the Fab 
format may potentially be more ideal against locally acting toxins, which means future 
antivenoms could potentially combine different formats of antibodies to tackle a spectrum of 
different toxins following their individual biodistribution profiles (14).

Recently, a synthetic biology approach for plant-made antivenom (plantivenom) was 
formulated using an in vitro selected pluribody against Bothrops asper snake venom toxins

12
and has been shown to neutralize a wide range of toxin activities and provide protection 
against lethal venom doses in mice (43). Although production of antibodies in plants seems 
like a cheaper alternative to production in animals at first glance, it results in expensive 
downstream processes, and poses a risk of impurities in the final antibody mixtures. 
Additionally, plant produced antibodies aimed to be used as pharmaceuticals in humans are 
less likely to succeed when going through regulatory requirements and therefore entering the 
market and reaching the patients. 
 
Progress in phage display technique allowed the discovery of human antibodies, most often 
in single-chain variable fragment (scFv) format (44). Other antibody formats have been 
suggested (Figure 3), such as single domain antibodies (VHH), also known as nanobodies 
obtained from camelids.  They bring a number of advantages, such as improved targeting, 
and increased stability under extreme pH and temperature, which could reduce the costs of 
cold chain logistics (45). Moreover, use of bispecific nanobodies has been reported (46), 
however VHH antibodies have a poor half-life . To tackle this problem, researches coupled VHH 
with a human Fc fragment  or suggested coupling to several molecules, such as carboxy-
terminal peptide (CTP) (47) or albumin (48).  
 
1.3.5 Antivenomics and high density peptide array technology 
It is not completely clear to which extent non-toxic components in the immunization mixture 
affect the immune response against medically relevant toxins during the traditional 
production process. Important venom components could upon immunization go unnoticed 
by the animal immune system if the toxins had low immunogenicity, whereas less important, 
but immunogenic, venom components may give rise to a strong antibody response, resulting 
in a suite of antibodies that are not medically relevant. The ability of antibodies to cross-
neutralize similar toxins is a highly desirable feature as it could limit the number of antibodies 
used in the mixtures of monoclonal antibodies. ELISA and immune-affinity chromatography 
are currently established methods for assessing cross-reactivity, which however does not 
always successfully predict cross-neutralization in vivo (49) (50). Therefore, high-density 
peptide microarrays (51) have been introduced to map epitopes and explain the molecular 
background of why some similar toxins succeed in evoking cross-reactive antibodies and 
others do not. This could reduce expenses related to research and development by allowing 
high throughput screening and epitope mapping, guide the development of novel antivenom, 
and reduce the need for in vivo studies in model animals for both traditional and novel 
antivenoms. 
 
1.4 Future: Recombinant antivenoms 
There are a number of advances that have emerged in the field of biotechnology, since when 
traditional antivenom production was first described, which could be implemented in the 
research of novel snake envenomation treatments. The problems with safety and regulation, 
efficacy, half-life and costs of production could be solved by employing a variety of advances 
in biotechnological techniques, such as toxicovenomics, phage display, and recombinant 
expression for the development of fully human recombinant antibodies against snake toxins. 
There are several benefits advocating the use of human IgG-based recombinant snake 
antivenoms. 
 
Since snakebite is a disease affecting the poorest parts of society, pricing is essential and it 
determines the availability of treatment and the likelihood of maintaining a normal life post 
envenomation. Current antivenoms are very expensive, costing $60-600 per dose, whereas

13
calculations based on the production data for monoclonal antibodies and for oligoclonal 
antibody mixtures predict recombinant antivenoms to cost $55-65 per gram, which could 
offer a treatment for $30-150, presenting a significantly more affordable option (14) (52). 
 
Secondly, immunogenic adverse reactions are some of the most devastating complications 
related to current therapies and can undermine the success of snakebite treatment. 
Recombinant antivenom could solve the problem by using fully human antibodies as key 
therapeutic components. Homologous origin of antibodies would eradicate the recognition 
by the human immune system and decrease the disturbance of the patient’s homeostasis, 
which is already violated by the presence of the snake venom in the body. Additionally, to 
tackle the problem of poor half-life of current F(ab)2 and Fab fragments, whole human IgG 
antibodies could be used. 
 
Moreover, currently available antivenoms contain antibodies against several different 
antigens, which have triggered the immune response of animals during their whole lifetime, 
whereas novel antivenoms could be designed to target only medically relevant snake venom 
toxins, bringing the therapeutic content from the current 5-36 % up to 100 %. This would 
provide better efficacy and allow the administration of significantly lower doses per 
treatment. 
 
Current manufacture design relies on the availability of snake venom and use of animals. 
Recombinant antivenoms could be produced in mammalian cell cultures, which would enable 
standardization of the production process and reduce batch to batch variation by using stable 
expression cell lines.

2 OBJECTIVES AND AIM OF THE RESEARCH 
This thesis is a part of a much larger project which aims to develop the world’s first 
recombinant snake antivenoms, based on toxin targeting mixtures of human antibodies.  
 
Due to the abundance of venomous snakes and the excessive lack of highly effective therapies 
against snakebite, this thesis focuses on a limited number of snakes. There is a certain level 
of conservation in the venom composition among species belonging to the same snake family, 
therefore three elapids are selected to be a focus. Black mamba (Dendroaspis polylepis), 
green mamba (Dendroaspis angusticeps), and monocled cobra (Naja kaouthia). The ultimate 
goal is to discover antibodies against medically relevant toxins from the selected snakes, 
produce them in mammalian cell cultures, test them in preclinical studies and identify the 
best IgG antibody candidates which could in the future be used in recombinant snake 
antivenom mixtures. Antibodies are developed in IgG format to extend half-life of the 
therapeutic and prevent recurrence. They are selected from a human source, aiming to 
reduce immunogenicity in human patients. 
 
There are two different type of binders that are in our focus. First, antibodies which 
specifically recognize toxins from selected snakes only and could be used in mixtures of 
monoclonal antibodies. Secondly, antibodies with cross-reactive potential, able to recognize 
similar toxins from different snakes, belonging to the same family and could be used in 
polyspecific mixtures. This allows creating two different repertoires of antibodies, allowing 
the possibility of using distinct strategies to tackle treating snake envenoming. Cross-reactive 
antibodies are highly desirable, because they could reduce the number of antibodies in a 
mixture required to neutralize venoms of multiple snake species. 
 
Antibodies are expressed in mammalian cell cultures and tested in a rodent animal model to 
prove the state-of-the-art concept that toxicovenomics and phage display combined can be 
successfully applied in research and development of recombinant antivenoms, capable of 
neutralizing snake toxins in vitro and in vivo, without compromising the safety in live systems.  
A step-by-step approach is used, where antibodies are first being tested against the toxins 
they have been raised against through phage display. Several doses of antibodies are then 
assessed to identify the lowest amount of antibodies which is needed for a full toxin 
neutralization in vivo. The best antibodies targeting different medically important toxins from 
the venom are then selected to prepare a mixture of antibodies which is tested against whole 
venom. 
 
It is the ambition to contribute to the development of more specific, safer, cheaper 
generation of novel snakebite antivenoms, by employing novel biotechnological techniques 
and move a step forward to an era of recombinant antivenoms being accessible to snakebite 
victims.

To achieve the proposed goal, the following questions need to be taken into consideration 
and used to guide the development of the project. 
 
1. Which snake of focus should be chosen? 
2. Which snake venom components need to be neutralized?

3.1 Key factors in selecting snakes of focus 
There are more than 3000 of known snake species in the world from which around 600 are 
venomous. From that more than 200 are recognized as medically important by the WHO (8) 
(53). This highlights the need to consider various aspects when deciding on which snakes to 
take into focus through the development of recombinant antivenoms, especially because new 
species are still being discovered every year.  
 
One way to roughly narrow the number of species to choose from is by dividing them into 
two categories, based on their medical importance. Category one covers snakes of the highest 
medical importance, by definition venomous snakes that are common or widespread and 
cause numerous snakebites, resulting in high levels of morbidity, disability or mortality. 
Category two includes snakes of secondary medical importance, those being highly venomous 
snakes capable of causing morbidity, disability or death, for which exact epidemiological or 
clinical data may be lacking and are less frequently implicated, due to their activity cycles, 
behaviour, habitat preferences or occurrence in areas remote to large human populations (8).  
 
While the research focus was narrowed down solely to category one snakes, the number of 
species belonging to this category is still overwhelming, highlighting the need to implement 
other criteria which include, medical need for snake antivenom, accessibility to snake venom 
for research purposes, severity of envenoming, complexity of venom, clinical manifestation 
of envenomings by species, distribution of venom after envenomation, site of action for 
components from the snake venom, perception of threat from a given species and therefore 
likelihood to attract attention of both the general and scientific audience, which indirectly 
affects the probability of obtaining the financial support for initiating and completing the 
research. Moreover, previously performed work within the field and acknowledging the limits 
of current state-of-art technology have been taken into account when selecting the snakes of 
focus.  
 
They were primarily selected based on the availability of toxicovenomic data (54) (55) (56), 
which enabled the selection of medically relevant toxins from the snake venom, and 
previously performed antibody discovery against toxins from selected snakes has contributed 
to easing the launch of this research project. 
 
Snakes have been selected to belong to the same taxonomic rank, enabling to therapeutically 
target similar toxins using similar strategies for different species. Viperidae, Elapidae, 
Atractaspididae, and some of the Colubridae are the four snake families that cover venomous 
snake species. Snakes belonging to Viperidae and Elapidae are responsible for the most 
serious envenomings, but have some distinct differences in the biodistribution and

3.2 Medically important toxins are identified by employing toxicovenomics 
Snake venom is a complex product of evolution. Different snake species employ a variety of 
hunting strategies, leading to a large diversity in venom compositions (59). Variability is also 
observed among individual specimens because of varying environmental factors in the 
habitats where the specific specimens are collected (57). In addition to that, changes over the 
lifetime of the snakes (60), and differences in venom between male and female animals have 
been reported (61), making it difficult to determine which components are responsible for 
the observed symptoms in human victims, therefore making identification of the medically 
relevant toxins a troublesome task. 
 
Therapeutic targets can be identified by employing toxicovenomics (62), which combines 
venomics (63) and toxicity score (64) (Figure 6). Venomics studies venoms towards a 
comprehensive characterization of the whole toxin profile of venomous snakes, using 
proteomics, transcriptomics, genomics, and bioinformatics tools. Venomics approach starts 
out with snake venom collection and fractionation by RP-HPLC (reverse phase high-
performance liquid chromatography). Fractions are identified by combination of N-terminal 
sequencing, sodium dodecyl sulfate polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) and mass 
spectrometric determination of the molecular masses. Moreover, quantitative proteome 
estimation of venoms is conducted, based on the analysis of the chromatographic data and      
SDS-PAGE densitometry. At the same time, toxicity of the collected fraction is assessed by in 
vivo toxicity studies.  
 
Toxicity score is used to identify the most pathophysiologically relevant toxins by combining 
the information about toxin identity, toxicity and abundance obtained from venomics 
analysis. Toxicity score takes into account intrinsic toxicity and abundance of the toxin in the 
whole venom. It is calculated by dividing a percentage of relative toxin abundance in the 
venom by the LD50 in a mouse model. 
 
 
 
For each venom individually, a toxicity score threshold value has to be defined, taking into 
account the total amount of venom that is usually injected by a specific species. For snakes 
that inject higher amounts, even toxins with relatively low toxicity score may be important, 
whereas for snakes that inject low amounts of venom, only predominant components might 
play a key role. 
 
Although, toxicity score can be useful for ranking the toxins based on their medical 
importance, it should be implemented with caution when venom exerts the features of 
synergism. Synergism arises when a venom displays a higher toxicity score than the sum of 
the toxicity scores of individual venom components (65). A toxicovenomic approach has been 
applied to the venoms of all three selected snakes in this thesis. Synergism can be observed 
in both mambas, Dendroaspis angusticeps, and Dendroaspis polylepis, whereas Naja kaouthia 
relies on two complementary strategies for acute toxicity.

3.2.1 Black mamba 
Black mamba is one of the deadliest snakes, inhabiting open bush country sides of Eastern 
and Southern Africa, feeding on small mammals. Specimens are typically coloured in olive 
green with a black mouth, reaching up to 4.3 m in length. It is known to strike repeatedly, 
using 3-6 mm long fangs and injecting 4-8 ml of venom, which is attributed with strong 
neurotoxicity and is not associated with local tissue damage. Early onset of symptoms can 
occur within 15 min post envenomation leading to respiratory muscle paralysis, and 
involuntary skeletal muscle contractions (66). 
 
The most abundant components in D. polylepis venoms are Kunitz-type proteinase inhibitors 
family (61.1 %), which includes dendrotoxins, and α-neurotoxins belonging to the three-finger 
toxin family (31.0 %) (Figure 7). In addition, the venom contains lower percentages of proteins 
from other families, including metalloproteinase, hyaluronidase, prokineticin, nerve growth 
factor, vascular endothelial growth factor, phospholipase A2, 5ʹ-nucleotidase, and 
phosphodiesterase. The most lethal components are α-neurotoxins, which induce post-
synaptic blockade of the neuromuscular junctions, resulting in flaccid paralysis, and to a lower 
extent dendrotoxins, which inhibit the voltage-dependent potassium channels at the 
presynaptic nerve terminal, leading to enhanced release of acetylcholine, inducing 
involuntary muscle contractions (55) (67).

3.2.2 Green mamba 
Green mamba is a relatively small and shy mamba species, on average measuring 1.4 m in 
length, and coloured in green, providing perfect camouflage for their arboreal habitats of 
Southeastern Africa. It mostly feeds on warm-blooded animals, such as rodents, bats, and 
birds. It is known to be less aggressive compared to its relative D. polylepis. Envenoming 
however, results in similar neurotoxic effects including the swelling of the bitten area, 
dizziness, nausea, difficult breathing, irregular heartbeat, and respiratory paralysis, which 
can, if not treated, lead to rapid death within 30 min (68) (69). 
 
Even though, both D. angusticeps and D. polylepis belong to the same genus, their venoms 
show remarkably different compositions. D. angusticeps is dominated by toxins belonging to 
three-finger toxin family (69.2 %), followed by Kunitz-type proteinase inhibitors family (16.3 
%) (Figure 8). The most lethal components and therefore the medically important targets are 
difficult to determine as the toxicity seems to arise from synergistic actions of different toxin 
families, possibly including dendrotoxins, fasciculins, and synergistically-acting toxins, causing 
neurotoxic effects. Venom of D. polylepis causes similar symptoms, however they are 
predominantly attributed to α-neurotoxins which were completely absent in the venom of D. 
angusticeps (54).

Specimens have elongated nuchal ribs forming a hood with a characteristic O-shaped mark.

The N. kaouthia venom is dominated by the family of three-finger toxins (77.5 %) including

3.3 Antibody discovery through a phage display technique 
Once medically important components from the selected snake venom have been identified, 
phage display selection is implemented to discover antibodies that are able to recognize, 
bind, and neutralize the toxins accountable for the devastating symptoms of a snakebite.  
 
Phage display is a high-throughput method which employs several selection rounds, allowing 
to screen through large libraries consisting of phages which both display unique scFv 
antibodies on their surface, and contain the DNA encoding of each individual antibody variant 
in their inside (73) . A typical selection round consists of 5 key steps as shown in Figure 10. 
First, biotinylated antigen is indirectly attached to the solid surface of a plastic well coated 
with neutravidin or streptavidin. The excess antigen is washed off. To reduce non-specific 
binding of phage to the well, skimmed milk is added to block the plastic surface. The selection 
is then performed by adding the page antibody library. A selection of antibodies displayed on 
the phage surface will recognize their targets and bind to the toxins. After sufficient 
incubation time, unbound phages are washed away, leaving behind only the phages 
displaying high affinity antibodies. The remaining strongly bound phages can be eluted either 
by changing pH or applying digestive enzymes, such as trypsin. Eluted phages are then 
amplified in E. coli, which gives rise to an enriched pool of phages containing high affinity 
antibodies towards toxins of interest, which can be used in next rounds of selection, 
increasing the number of phage binders. The selection outputs of phages are screened with 
ELISA to estimate the success of the method, and sub-cloned into E. coli cells to obtain 
monoclones which are used for further screening through a series of additional ELISA and 
DNA sequencing. Once the antibody gene is isolated, it can be conveniently shuttled into a 
variety of expression formats, providing a renewable resource of antibodies (74).

3.3.1 Factors influencing the efficiency of phage display selection 
A variety of factors that affect the efficiency of a phage display selection need to be taken 
into consideration when designing the experiments and analysing the obtained results. 
Differences in the success of phage display may arise due to clonal variation in amplification 
of phages, level of antibody display on the phage surface, affinity of displayed antibodies, and 
antigen presentation, which can all lead to bias in selection of antibodies that are not in fact 
good binders for selected antigens (75). 
 
Clonal variation of a displayed antibody sequence can affect the translation, transport, 
folding, and the stability of the fusion of phage particles leading to differences in amplification 
of phages (Figure 11) (76). This may result in a bias where phages with fast and stable 
assembly, but low affinity outcompete phages with high affinity, leading to a population of 
low affinity antibodies. For that reason, E. coli can be grown at suboptimal temperature 
during amplification to reduce this effect.

antibodies are outcompeted by phages displaying low affinity antibodies because they are 
predominant in the mixture, due to their favorable amplification properties.

Level of antibody display is another factor which can lead to selection of undesired antibodies 
(Figure 12). Antibody displaying phages should ideally display only one copy of antibody 
fragment. However, phages occasionally carry more than one antibody, which can lead to a 
selection of phages displaying multiple low affinity antibodies over phages carrying only one 
high affinity antibody due to avidity. To reduce this effect, antibody sequence is packed into 
phagemid plasmid. In this case, helper phages are needed to generate functional phage 
particles that are correctly assembled and display both phagemid G3P fusion protein with 
antibody and the wild type G3P protein of the helper phage. The phagemid replicates in E. 
coli as a double-stranded plasmid, but co-infection with a helper phage results in the 
production of single-stranded phagemid DNA, which is packaged into phage particles. The 
helper phage provides all the proteins necessary for phage assembly, including wild-type 
copies of all the coat proteins. For phage display, the phagemid contains a gene that encodes 
an additional copy of one of the coat proteins fused to the polypeptide that is to be displayed.

Antigen presentation is another essential factor for optimal selection of antibodies with 
desired qualities. It highly depends on the quality, purity and immobilization of the antigen 
(Figure 13). It is important that antigen mixtures do not contain impurities to ensure selection 
of antibodies against desired antigens and not against the impurities instead. Antigen 
immobilization can be done directly on the plastic well surface or through a biotin linker. 
Direct immobilization on the well surface is the easiest and fastest way to attach antigens 
prior to addition of the phage library. This, however sometimes results in a poor display of an 
antigen towards the solution. Additionally, it can lead to conformational changes and 
potentially to denaturation of the antigen, making the selection bias towards a denaturated 
version of the toxin favouring a selection of antibodies which do not recognize native antigen 
with high affinity (76).

3.3.2 Design of antibody display library 
In this thesis, a unique naïve antibody library, developed by IONTAS is used for antibody 
discovery. The library provides a rich source of antibody diversity, potentially providing 
hundreds of unique antibodies per target. PCR amplification of human lymphocytes from 43 
donors, mainly collected from peripheral blood was used to create repertoires of light chain 
variable regions (VL) and heavy chain variable regions (VH), where VH genes are grouped into

peptide (Gly4 Ser Gly4 Ser Gly3 Ala Ser) (Figure 3), covering 1010 unique antibody variants (82)

3.4 Approaches to assessing the neutralization potential of discovered antibodies 
All therapeutic agents intended for human use have a fundamental and ethical requirement 
of proven safety and efficacy, established initially by preclinical in vitro and in vivo laboratory 
tests in laboratory animals and if the results of these prove satisfactory, by clinical trials in 
human patients (8). Taking into consideration the limited physiological relevance of animal 
models to human envenoming, the tests for determining venom lethality and antivenom 
neutralizing capacity are currently the only validated means of assessing venom toxicity and 
antivenom neutralizing potency for both manufacturers and regulatory authorities worldwide 
(84) (85). Guidelines for assessing the neutralization potential of currently existing 
antivenoms have been directing the design of preclinical studies in this thesis. The golden 
standard for preclinical assessment of antivenom efficacy against elapid toxins is 
neutralization of lethality (86). Median lethal dose of the venom or toxin is determined and 
used for median effective dose assay, which indicates venom or toxin neutralizing potency of 
the therapeutic. 
 
3.4.1 Determining median lethal dose (LD50) 
Median lethal dose is defined as the minimal amount of venom or its toxins causing death in 
50 % of the mice injected. Initially, the LD50 of venom or isolated toxins is determined by 
administering different doses to groups of mice of defined weight range, with solutions of 
varying doses of venom or isolated toxins, dissolved in saline solutions. The number of dead 
animals is recorded over the time of 24-48 hours. LD50 is estimated using Probit analysis (87), 
Spearman-Karber (88) or alternative procedures (such as non-parametric methods) (8) (11).

3.4.2 Determining median effective dose (ED50) 
Next, to assess the neutralizing potency of the toxin targeting antibodies, a median effective 
dose is determined. Median effective dose is defined as the volume of antivenom that 
protects 50 % of the mice injected. Initially, a fixed amount of venom or toxins with various 
doses of antibodies adjusted to a final volume with saline solution is incubated. The amount 
of venom or toxins which is to be neutralized in the assay is usually referred to as the 
challenge dose and it normally represents two fold of LD50. The mixtures are incubated for 30 
min at 37 °C and injected into groups of mice of defined weight range. A control group 
injected with a mixture of the venom or the toxin challenge dose with saline solution alone is 
typically included to confirm that the venom challenge dose induces 100 % lethality. The 
number of dead animals is recorded over the time of 24-48 hours. ED50 is estimated using 
Probit analysis (87), Spearman-Karber (88) or alternative procedures (such as non-parametric 
methods) (8) (11). 
 
3.4.3 Routes of administration 
Two different routes of administration, shown in Figure 15 have been used to assess the 
neutralization potential of snake toxin targeting antibodies in preclinical studies. In the case 
of monocled cobra, 3 toxin-neutralizing IgGs have been tested through intravenous injections, 
whereas in the case of black mamba, 18 toxin-neutralizing IgGs have been tested through 
intracerebroventricular injection. Intravenous administration is more representative of real 
envenoming, it is easier to perform, requiring less skilled personnel, and is less invasive for 
the model animal. The route was selected for efficacy evaluation of highly concentrated 
antibody mixtures, available in larger quantities. Intracerebroventricular injection is less 
representative of envenoming, requires more experience to perform and can be fatal for the 
animal if not executed properly. It is, however more sensitive and requires significantly less 
material. Even though antibodies can be produced cost effectively on a large scale, they are 
quite expensive to produce in the phase of research and development, highlighting the 
importance of having different screening approaches available in order to minimize the 
expenses of in vivo neutralization studies and increasing the number of potential therapeutic 
antibody candidates screened.

4 EXPERIMENTAL STRATEGY AND OVERVIEW 
The strategy behind this project is to fractionate snake venom, using RP-HPLC to isolate 
individual toxins (Figure 16A) and employ toxicovenomics to select fractions of medical 
importance. Human antibody library and phage display are used to discover unique scFv 
binders against selected toxins from the snake venom (Figure 16B). Toxins used for antibody 
discovery are biotinylated. The level of biotinylation is assessed using MALDI-TOF-TOF 
analysis. Success of phage display is assessed by polyclonal ELISA. Polyclonal mixtures of 
antibody expressing clones are sub-cloned to obtain monoclonal E. coli cultures (Figure 16C).

In vivo assays were conducted in CD-1 mice of both sexes in the weight range 18-20 g, supplied

5.2 Methods 
5.2.1 RP-HPLC venom fractionation 
Venom was fractionated using RP-HPLC. 50 mg of venom was dissolved in 5 mL of water 
containing 0.1 % trifluoroacetic acid (TFA), mixed and transferred to Eppendorf tubes in 
aliquots of 215 µL, which were stored at -20° C. Prior to RP-HPLC, aliquots were thawed, 
mixed, centrifuged to remove the cell debris. Per each run, 200 µL of venom was fractionated 
by RP-HPLC on a C18 column (4.6 × 250 mm, 5 μm particle, Teknokroma). Elution was 
performed at 1 mL/min by applying a gradient towards solution B (acetonitrile, containing 0.1 
% TFA). The program contained the following steps: 0% B for 5 min, 0–15% B over 10 min, 15–
45 % B over 60 min, 45–70% B over 10 min, and 70% B over 9 min. Fractions were collected 
manually, dried in a vacuum centrifuge (Savant) or by a combination of nitrogen evaporation 
and freeze-drying. Dried fractions were dissolved in 1x PBS. Protein concentrations were 
determined using a spectrophotometer (Cat #NDONEW, Thermo Fisher) using PBS as blank 
measurement. The concentrations were corrected using the average value of the extinction 
coefficients of the proteins in each fraction. 
 
5.2.2 Biotinylation of fractions 
Fractions obtained with RP-HPLC were biotinylated at 1:1 toxin to biotin molar ratio. Biotin 
was dissolved in 170 µL of milliQ water and added to the fractions. The mixtures were 
vortexed and incubated at room temperature for 30 min. Biotinylated fractions were 
transferred on the membrane in purification tubes and centrifuged at 14,000 g, 30 min to 
remove residual biotin. Flow-through was discarded. Columns were washed three times by 
addition of 300 µL of PBS and centrifugation at 14,000 g, 30 min. Samples from the membrane 
were recovered by reverse spinning. This was done by removing the filtrate tube, inverting 
the membrane and inserting it into a new filtrate tube, followed by centrifugation at 2,000 g, 
2 min. Concentrations of purified biotinylated fractions were measured using 
spectrophotometer (Cat #NDONEW, Thermo Fisher). The fractions were stored at 4° C. 
 
5.2.3 MALDI-TOF-TOF 
The level of biotinylation and IgG target determination were conducted using MALDI-TOF-
TOF. Analysis was performed on a MALDI-TOF-TOF 4800 Plus mass spectrometer (AB Sciex) in 
linear positive mode, using 500 shots at a laser intensity of 4200 by a mass spectra comparison 
of native fractions and biotinylated fractions.

5.2.8 In vivo neutralization studies 
Recombinant human IgG antibodies produced in mammalian Expi-293 cells were used to

supplied by Instituto Clodomiro Picado, following protocols approved by the Institutional 
Committee for the Use and Care of Animals (CICUA), University of Costa Rica. Mice were 
housed in cages for groups of 3-6, and were provided food and water ad libitum. 
 
5.2.8.1 Determining median lethal dose (LD50) 
The lethality of the whole venom and fractions or isolated toxins was tested by intravenous 
(i.v.) injection in groups of four mice (18–20 g body weight). Various amounts of venom or 
fractions/toxins were dissolved in PBS (0.12 M NaCl, 0.04 M sodium phosphate buffer, pH 
7.2), and injected in the caudal vein, using an injection volume of 100 μL. Then, deaths 
occurring within 24 h were recorded. LD50 was calculated by Probits, using the BioStat® 
software (AnalySoft).

6 RESULTS AND DISCUSSION 
6.1 Overview 
The aim of this project was to contribute to the development of a more specific, safer, 
cheaper generation of novel recombinant snakebite antivenoms by employing modern 
technological techniques. Because the work described in this thesis is a part of a much larger 
project, which involves combined efforts from researchers coming from several different 
laboratories around the globe, a short overview of the current status of the research and 
development is provided (Figure 17). 
 
Snake species black mamba (Dendroaspis polylepis), green mamba (Dendroaspis 
angusticeps), and monocled cobra (Naja kaouthia) have been selected from the Elapidae 
family, exerting similar toxic effects and therefore enabling therapeutical targeting of similar 
toxins. Moreover, the availability of pre-existing toxicovenomic data and previously 
performed antibody discovery work influenced the choice of which toxins to take into focus. 
Originally, several other toxins from the selected snakes were proposed to be of medical 
importance and therefore targets for antibody discovery (Table 1). However, only a few toxins 
from the original list have been included in this thesis. Nonetheless, all stages of the 
experimental plan for research and development of toxin targeting antibodies have been 
covered through the experimental work.

6.2 RP-HPLC snake venom fractionation 
In order to be able to selectively discover antibodies against individual components from the 
snake venom and perform in vivo neutralization studies, antigen preparation is the essential 
stage of recombinant antivenom research and development. RP-HPLC was used to separate 
individual fractions (Figure 18) from the venom of selected snakes. Firstly, an analytical 
fractionation was performed to distinguish the identity of the peaks, through a comparison 
with previously reported toxicovenomic studies. Since the crude venom used in this thesis 
was traced back to originate from the same batch as the one mentioned in the 
aforementioned toxicovenomic studies, chromatograms show a high degree of similarity. 
Furthermore, several preparative fractionations have been performed to obtain a sufficient 
amount of antigens used in antibody discovery through phage display and for in vivo 
neutralization studies. 
From the venom of black mamba, 27 fractions were identified, out of which fractions Dp5, 
Dp6, Dp7, Dp8 containing toxins belonging to families of three-finger toxins and bovine 
pancreatic trypsin inhibitors/Kunitz inhibitors (Figure 18A) have been collected for screening 
of previously discovered scFv antibodies, and for in vivo neutralization studies of IgG 
antibodies converted from the best scFv clones. Green mamba venom fractionation revealed 
29 (and in some cases 30) fractions, out of which fractions Da5, Da7, Da9, Da11, Da12 
containing toxins belonging to families of three-finger toxins, bovine pancreatic trypsin 
inhibitors/Kunitz inhibitors, and synergistic like proteins (Figure 18B) have been collected for 
toxin antibody discovery through a phage display. In some cases, an additional peak Da9.1 
appeared in between Da8 and Da9, but has not been previously reported in the literature. 
From the venom of monocled cobra, a vast majority of toxins belong to the three-finger toxin 
family. It was postulated that previously discovered IgGs against fraction Nk6 will recognize 
other fractions. Therefore, venom fractionation was originally intended to be used for 
preparation of antigens used in IgG monoclonal ELISA to identify cross-reactivity potential 
ofrecombinantly produced IgG antibodies against all 28 fractions (Figure 18C). However, due 
to limited time availability and other experimental priorities, fractionated venom of this snake 
species was never used. Instead, purchased isolated fraction Nk6, containing a-cobratoxin 
was used for specificity IgG ELISA and in vivo neutralization studies. Nonetheless, all of the 
selected fractions are among the most abundant components from the venom and reportedly 
have relatively low LD50 values, therefore having a high toxicity score and are thus the most 
medically important components from the selected venoms. In the case of toxins used for in 
vivo neutralization studies, LD50 values were confirmed prior to the execution of the 
experiments. 
Collected fractions from the RP-HPLC used for phage display and screening of scFv or IgG 
clones were biotinylated and assessed with MALDI-TOF-TOF prior to their use. On the 
contrary, fractions intended to be used for the assessment of neutralization potential of IgGs 
in vivo were used in their native form.

6.3 MALDI-TOF-TOF analysis of the biotinylation levels 
Fractions collected from RP-HPLC were biotinylated for use in phage display and for screening 
of both scFv and IgG antibodies with ELISA. Because direct immobilization has the tendency 
to affect antigen conformation and cause its denaturation, indirect immobilization through 
biotin linker was used.  
 
Nonetheless, the level of biotinylation can differ greatly and cause significant deviations in 
the success rate of phage display selections. Optimal level of biotinylation prevents antigen 
interacting with the surface of the well where phage display is performed. Moreover, it 
increases the availability of the antigen to the antibody displaying phages. On the contrary, 
excessive biotinylation results in a decrease of its binding sites availability for interactions 
with phages. Additionally, antigens used in this thesis are relatively small (6000-8000 Da) 
which decreases the likelihood of antibodies recognizing the antigens if not properly 
presented to the solution. It is therefore essential to perform analysis of the prepared 
biotinylated antigens prior to execution of phage display. Screening with ELISA also requires 
the use of biotinylated antigens. The level of biotinylation is not as essential as for phage 
display, as long as it is within the same range for all antigens. ELISA assay is based on a 
comparison of signals in arbitrary units from several clones and ranking them from the best 
to the worst. Nonetheless, consistent level of biotinylation is preferred. 
 
Fractions obtained from green mamba venom fractionation were already biotinylated and 
analysed by Queralt Serra Camprubí. Fractions from monocled cobra venom fractionation 
were not biotinylated. Only fractions from black mamba venom (Dp5, Dp6, Dp7, Dp8) were 
biotinylated as part of this thesis. Biotinylation was carried out at a 1:1 toxin to biotin molar 
ratio and analysed using MALDI-TOF-TOF (Figure 19). The level of biotinylation was 
determined by comparing the spectrum of un-biotinylated and biotinylated fractions. Since 
fractions contain several toxins, their identity has first been determined based on their 
molecular weights using previously reported data from toxiovenomic studies. Molecular 
weights from this analysis do not completely match reported values, due to the limited 
resolution of the MALDI-TOF-TOF machine. Fractions 5, 6, and 7 (Figure 19A-C) contain 
Dendrotoxin-d and a-elapitoxin, whereas, fraction 7 (Figure 19D) only contains Dendrotoxin-
1.  
 
Since a ratio of 1:1 was used for the biotinylation, spectral analysis should ideally show one 
biotin molecule per each toxin in the fractions. However, fractions contain a mixture of 
unsuccessfully biotinylated toxins and toxins with one, two, or three biotin molecules. An 
excess of un-biotinylated toxin combined with smaller share belonging to the toxin coupled 
to one biotin is less problematic than over-biotinylated fractions. Underbiotinylation is not an 
issue for solid phase phage display selections as any antigen that is not captured by 
streptavidin will be washed away prior to selection. However, it does represent a problem in 
solution phase phage display selections (e.g. for affinity maturation) as only less than half of 
the bound phage will be captured, or the other half will act as a deselecting antigen. 
 
In fraction 5 (Figure 19A), both Dendrotoxin-d and a-elapitoxin contain one or two biotins per 
toxin. In fractions 6 (Figure 19B) and 7 (Figure 19C), dendrotoxin-d contains one or two

6.4 Green mamba (Dendroaspis angusticeps) 
6.4.1 Polyclonal phage-scFv ELISA and monoclonal scFV ELISA are used for screening 
of phage display outputs and subcloned populations 
Biotinylated fractions Da5, Da9, Da9.1, Da11, and Da12 from the venom of green mamba 
were used for antibody discovery through phage display selections. In total two rounds of 
soluble state selections and two rounds of solid state selections were performed.   
 
Solid phase selections were carried out by indirect immobilizing of antigen through pre-
coated streptavidin or neutravidin to a plastic surface of a well. Skimmed milk was used to 
block the wells and prevent unspecific phage interactions with the plastic. After washing, the 
phage library was added and left to bind to the antigen. The initial wash removed unbound 
phages, whereas trypsin eluted the phages which have recognized their antigens and were 
later used together with a helper phage to infect TG1 cells, which were incubated on plates 
overnight and gave an enriched sub-population of the phagemid library. In soluble state 
selections, the antibody displaying library and antigen were mixed in the solution. Magnetic 
beads, coated with streptavidin were used to capture the biotinylated antigen and bound 
antibody displaying phages. The beads were then washed to remove the unspecific binders. 
Antigen binding phages were eluted with trypsin and together with helper phages used to 
infect TG1 cells, which were incubated on plates overnight in the same way as in solid state 
phage display selections.  
 
Success of antibody discovery was assessed in several different ways. First, selection output 
yields were determined by counting the colony forming units (CFU) from overnight incubation 
(Table 10). During phage display, 1010 of unique phage displaying antibody variants are added 
to the solution. The number of colonies is expected to be in the range of 104-106 in the first 
round, and 106-108 in the second round, indicating a successful phage display. However, when 
solid state selections were performed, CFU values were very low (data not shown), stipulating 
failed phage display. Hence, soluble state selections have been carried out, as they present a 
better chance for a discovery of specific antibodies when working with tricky antigens. In this 
case, CFU counts were in the range of success indicative of antibody discovery.

Through polyclonal ELISA, scFv antibody is expressed as a fusion protein attached to G3P 
protein on the coat of the phage, which binds to the target antigen and is detected with triple 
FLAG labelled antibody. Even though, this assay is highly sensitive, it gives rise to a number of 
false positives. Firstly, because it only screens heterologous populations of antibodies. 
Secondly, different antibodies display differing affinities towards the antigens. Thirdly, 
antibody displaying phages are amplified at different rates, due to the clonal variation. To 
reduce some of those effects, selected scFv antibody populations were sub-cloned into 
efficient expression vector which enables isolation of individual clones. Monoclonal ELISA was 
performed to screen large numbers of clones for antigen binding activity (Figure 20B-25B).

threshold value of 2000 TRF was chosen to be a selective factor for clones which were sent

therefore not enclosed as part of the results. Unfortunately, only a small percentage of scFv 
clones showed a strong binding potential against their antigens, indicating low efficiency of 
phage display selections (Figure 20C-25C).

6.5 Monocled cobra (Naja kaouthia) 
Extreme potency of monocled cobra venom is mostly due to the presence of three-finger 
toxins, which represent an astonishing 77.5 % of the total protein content (Figure 9), out of 
which 32.2 % are taken by alpha-elapitoxin-Nk2a, also known as a-cobratoxin. Toxicity score 
of this toxin almost reaches the same level as the the whole venom. Its abundance and high 
toxicity make it a perfect pharmacological target. Moreover, the majority of other highly toxic 
components from the venom of monocled cobra belong to the same protein family. It was 
postulated that antibodies, designed to target a-cobratoxin should be able to recognize, bind, 
and neutralize other similar components from the venom. Moreover, the arsenal of three-
finger toxins in the venom of monocled cobra is so abundant that cross-reactive antibodies 
recognizing toxins from the same family may have a potential to neutralize the whole venom. 
 
As a result, a series of phage display selections against fraction Nk6 was conducted by Andreas 
H. Laustsen, resulting in a discovery of high affinity scFv clones, which were transformed into 
IgG format and expressed in mammalian cells. A first batch of in vivo neutralization studies 
has been carried out by Saioa Oscoz (unpublished data). Due to promising results, a second 
batch of recombinant IgG antibodies was produced in mammalian cells and tested in a mouse 
model as part of this thesis.

6.5.3 Evaluation in a mouse model reveals neutralization potential of IgG antibodies 
in vivo 
In vivo neutralization studies, were conducted in CD-1 mice of both sexes in the weight range 
18-20 g. Elapid venoms cause death mainly through neurotoxic effect resulting in death. 
Reduction in lethality was therefore used as measure of efficacy of the therapeutic. Toxin or 
whole venom were mixed with antibodies in 1X PBS and incubated for 30 min at 37 °C, as 
recommended by WHO Guidelines for Production Control and Regulation of Snake Antivenom 
Immunoglobulins. Administration was performed through intravenous injection. Due to high 
biological variability, control animals were always injected first. Only when the challenge dose 
was confirmed and all animals died, preparation of mixtures took place. Challenge doses for 
both, a-cobratoxin and whole venom were chosen based on reported LD50 values. Animals 
receiving both, a-cobratoxin or whole venom of monocled cobra died in a similar fashion. 
Symptoms were observed within the first few minutes post-injection and included respiratory 
paralysis which eventually lead to death. 
 
Firstly, a series of experiments was performed to assess the ability of IgG antibodies to 
neutralize isolated a-cobratoxin (Figure 27). For this purpose, toxin with a challenge dose of 
4 µg was mixed with antibodies in different molar ratios (1:3, 1:4, 1:6, 1:8). As a control, 
animals received an injection containing a-cobratoxin and unspecific antibodies against 
lysozyme. In all cases, control animals died as expected. All three IgG clones tested against a-
cobratoxin from monocled cobra prolonged the survival of mice administered with lethal 
doses of the a-cobratoxin. Moreover, a dose dependant response was observed. Higher 
doses of antibodies caused prolonged delay of death (Figure 27). It is postulated that a higher 
dose of antibodies could provide a full protection against a-cobratoxin. Clones 368_01_C05 
(Figure 27A) and 368_01_C01 (Figure27B), prolonged the death of animals for several hours, 
whereas clone 368_01_H01 (Figure 27C-D) only prolonged the survival by minutes. Because 
this clone previously showed the most promising results, the experiment was repeated, 
however, similar results were obtained. After investigation, it seems that clones were mixed 
during the preparation of DNA used for transient transfection of mammalian cells.

Selection outputs were sub-cloned in bacterial cells. The best scFv clones against fraction Dp8 
were also converted into IgG format, expressed in mammalian cells and tested in preclinical 
in vivo neutralization studies by Saioa Oscoz (unpublished data). Fractions Dp5, Dp6, and Dp7 
contain mixtures of dendrotoxins and three-finger toxins, whereas Dp8 only consists of 
dendrotoxins. For that reason, it was hypothesized that phage display selections against Dp8 
were more likely to result in a discovery of highly specific antibodies, targeting dendrotoxins. 
Additionally, secondary screening of monoclonal populations, performed after sub-cloning 
for scFv antibodies raised against Dp5, Dp6, Dp7 did not give conclusive results. Therefore, 
those antibody populations were not considered as good candidates for IgG format 
conversion at the time. It was postulated that results could originally not be replicated due to 
a poor quality of antigens used for secondary screening.  
 
To inspect this assumption, antibody displaying phage display outputs were first screened 
with scFv polyclonal ELISA to confirm the success of previously performed antibody discovery 
work. Due to a high abundance of several phage display outputs containing scFv candidates, 
only outputs from two experiments showing the best results in primary screening were 
selected to be evaluated once more as part of this thesis (Figure 29). Antibody displaying 
mixtures of phages were tested in duplicates and in two different dilutions. In all cases, a dose 
dependant response can be observed, where lower dilution (1:2) resulted in higher signal, 
compared to higher dilution (1:20). As a negative control, antibodies selected against b-gal 
were tested against individual antigens, which were used for selections. Negative control 
showed no signal. To check the experimental setup, a positive control with b-gal specific 
antibodies in combination with b-gal as an antigen was used. Positive control showed a strong 
signal and confirmed a successful ELISA analysis. Screening for Fc, neutravidin, and 
streptavidin binding antibodies was performed to exclude unspecific binding signal from the 
selected population of antibodies. All selection outputs indicated negligible binding to Fc. No 
neutravidin binding signal was observed, whereas unspecific binders against streptavidin 
were detected in the selection outputs for antibodies discovered against fraction Dp7. 
Nonetheless, it was estimated that all phage display selections were performed successfully. 
Selection outputs against all fractions indicated the presence of scFv antibodies with the 
ability to recognize their original antigens, used in the phage display selections. Moreover, 
antibodies raised against fractions Dp5, Dp6, and Dp7 show a high-degree of cross-reactivity. 
This effect is highly desirable and is most likely owed to the presence of toxins from the same 
protein families in all fractions.

Polyclonal ELISA of phage display outputs was successfully reproduced matching the results 
of primary screening performed by Andreas H. Laustsen. Even though outputs from two

To ensure the quality of selected clones, cryostocks of selected sub-cloned homologous scFv 
antibody populations were screened with scFv monoclonal and scFv capture ELISA (Figure 30). 
Initial scFv monoclonal ELISA was used to confirm antigen binding activity of scFv expressed 
in E.coli. However, the binding signal recorded by this method is a combined function of 
affinity and expression. Since antibody expression can vary significantly from clone to clone, 
ranking with scFv capture ELISA was applied. In this case, an antigen-specific antibody was 
used to capture limited amounts of antigens which were then available for interactions with 
the scFv antibodies expressed by sub-cloned E. coli from stock cultures. This standardizes scFv 
concentrations irrespective of expression levels and allows screening based solely on affinity, 
allowing to rank clones based on their ability to bind their respective antigens.

originally used for respective phage display selections. However, in capture ELISA, scFv 
antibodies were tested for cross-reactive potential with other fractions from the venom, due 
to a limited availability of antigens for screening purposes. All experiments were conducted 
in duplicates. Screening against neutravidin and streptavidin antibodies was performed to 
exclude unspecific binding of selected monoclonal scFv antibody mixtures. None of the 
selected clones showed binding to neutravidin or streptavidin. Moreover, strong binding 
against their original antigens was observed, indicating the ability to recognize their original 
antigens, used in the phage display selections. Moreover, antibodies raised against fractions 
Dp5, Dp6, and Dp7 show a high-degree of cross-reactivity. Clones raised against Dp7 show 
binding activity against Dp5 and Dp6. Similarly, clones raised against Dp5 and Dp7 both show 
binding activity against Dp7. However, those signals were relatively low, compared to signals 
indicating specificity. For capture scFv ELISA two conditions using 25 nM and 2.5 nM of 
antigen were assessed. A dose dependant response was observed. It is important to note that 
some signals may seem relatively low. Nonetheless, all values are well above 2000 TRF, which 
is usually used as a cut-off value, when selecting the best clones. Overall, screening of selected 
scFv clones with monoclonal ELISA and capture ELISA confirmed selection of clones to be 
suitable candidates for conversion in IgG format.

6.6.3 Success of recombinant IgG expression in mammalian cell cultures 
All 16 scFv antibodies were converted into IgG format and expressed in mammalian Expi-293 
cells in smaller volumes (Table 12), compared to IgG antibodies against Nk6 from monocled 
cobra. Smaller scale of antibody production was selected due to a larger number of antibodies 
which needed to be assessed in vivo and since no preliminary preclinical testing has been 
performed. A possibility that some of the produced antibodies will not be of high quality and 
high costs of production in a laboratory scale were taken into consideration. Antibodies were 
removed from the cell broth by centrifugation, purified by protein A chromatography, 
dialysed in 2x PBS to ensure optimal physiological environment, and concentrated.  
 
IgG antibodies were obtained through a format conversion of scFv clones. Since phage display 
selections can be carried out with either kappa or lambda libraries, clones are prepared in a 
different way, depending on where they originate from. Originally, all clones from phage 
display selections using kappa libraries were cloned in two different vectors, pINT3 and 
pINT54. Alternatively, clones from phage display selections using lambda libraries were 
cloned in pINT3 only. However, preparation of expression plasmids involves a four-part 
ligation, which is accompanied by a high probability of failure. Newly formed expression 
vectors were DNA sequenced to confirm correct VH/VL pairing. In some cases, the inserts were 
not correct, so at the end a combination of both expression vectors was used to transfect 
mammalian cells. Expression vector pINT3 gives a higher rate of success when preparing the 
constructs. Expression vector pINT54 is associated with much lower rates of success. 
However, it sometimes results in increased expression of the antibodies in the mammalian 
cells, leading to high yields. pINT3 combines two different promotors, EF-1 alpha for light 
chain and CMV for heavy chain, whereas pINT54 contains CMV promotor for both light and 
heavy chain.  
 
Antibodies against fractions from the venom of black mamba were produced in the period 
when optimization of mammalian expression was taking place. Some production parameters 
might have been sub-optimal which resulted in a precipitation of the control antibodies. 
Owing to a concern of causing the precipitation of test antibodies, they were prepared in a 
less concentrated form, compared to antibodies targeting fraction Nk6 from monocled cobra.

6.6.6 MALDI-TOF-TOF is used for a pilot study of IgG target identification 
Since fractions Dp5, Dp6, and Dp7 contain both three-finger toxins and dendrotoxins, 
antibodies discovered against those fractions could target either of the components. For a 
precise design of antibody mixtures, antibody targets have to be determined. A pilot study

the mixture, the peak corresponding to three-finger toxins decreased, indicating the antibody

Summary 
Phage display was used to discover a suite of scFv antibodies targeting fraction Dp5, Dp6, Dp7 
from the venom of black mamba, which were converted to IgG format and produced by 
mammalian cell expression in a small scale. Success of phage display selections was confirmed 
by a repeated screening with scFv polyclonal ELISA. Functionality of scFv clones after sub-
cloning was confirmed with scFv monoclonal ELISA and scFv capture ELISA. Binding activity of 
IgG antibodies was confirmed with IgG monoclonal ELISA and IgG capture ELISA. 
Neutralization potential of antibodies was assessed through intracerebroventricular route of 
administration, using a mouse model. In total, 15 monoclonal human IgGs were tested against 
black mamba toxins, all providing prolonged survival in mice. Remarkably, 9 out of these 
human IgGs provided full protection in mice administered with lethal doses (0.5 µg) of the 
black mamba toxins. Moreover, some IgGs showed a cross-reactive neutralization potential, 
when tested with various different toxins from the venom of black mamba. Additionally, a 
mixture of selected IgG antibodies targeting several different toxins successfully neutralized 
whole venom from black mamba (1 µg), providing a strong preliminary proof of concept.

7 CONCLUSIONS 
Snakebite is one of the most neglected diseases, as declared by the WHO. Currently available 
treatment is based on animal-derived antivenom. Although it might save lives, and neutralize 
snake venom, serum based therapy poses a hazard towards its human recipients. High batch 
to batch variation, low therapeutic content, and adverse immunogenic reactions present a 
challenge which could be tackled by developing novel therapeutics using modern 
biotechnological techniques. 
  
This thesis was conducted as a part of a much larger project which aims to develop the world’s 
first recombinant snakebite antivenoms, based on mixtures of human antibodies. Research 
and development of antibodies targeting toxins from the venoms of three different snakes 
were included in this thesis. Overall, previously published data on toxicovenomics was used 
to identify toxins of medical importance in the venom of green mamba, black mamba, and 
monocled cobra. RP-HPLC was employed to fractionate the venom. Isolated fractions were 
used for toxin targeting antibody discovery using phage display. Success of phage display was 
evaluated by polyclonal scFv ELISA. Polyclonal mixtures of antibody expressing clones were 
sub-cloned to obtain monoclonal E. coli cultures, which underwent a thorough screening with 
scFv monoclonal ELISA and scFv capture ELISA to confirm binding activity and estimate affinity 
respectively. Based on the obtained ELISA results, scFv clones were converted into IgG format 
and expressed in mammalian cell cultures. Functionality of recombinantly produced IgG 
antibodies was confirmed by IgG monoclonal ELISA and IgG capture ELISA. Mouse model was 
used for in vivo neutralization studies with IgG antibodies targeting toxins from the snake 
venoms.  
 
Toxicovenomics and phage display combined were successfully employed to discover a suite 
of antibodies which are capable to recognize, bind, and neutralize toxins from snake venoms 
of black mamba and monocled cobra. Antibodies showed binding with neutralization 
potential against their original antigens used for phage display antibody discovery and were 
additionally able to cross-react with other fractions present in the venom. This was 
demonstrated on several levels, through scFv polyclonal ELISA, scFv monoclonal ELISA, scFv 
capture ELISA, IgG monoclonal ELISA, IgG capture ELISA and in vivo neutralization studies.  
 
For pre-clinical evaluation, two different routes of administration were used. Standard 
intravenous injection was employed for assessment of antibodies that have already gone 
through a series of preliminary tests and were available in larger amounts (IgGs against Nk6 
from monocled cobra). Additionally, a novel approach using intracerebroventricular injection 
was applied for neutralization potential analysis of antibodies available in smaller quantities, 
with no pre-existent tests (IgGs against Dp5, Dp6, Dp7 from black mamba).  
 
For the first time, discovery of monoclonal fully human IgGs that are capable of neutralizing 
snake toxins in vivo has been reported. A monoclonal population of antibodies targeting 
fraction Nk6 from the venom of monocled cobra tested against crude venom, showed a 
prolonged survival and indicated neutralization potential. More remarkably, a mixture of 
antibodies targeting medically important fractions Dp5, Dp6, Dp7, and Dp8 from the venom 
of black mamba showed a full neutralization and potent protection against crude black 
mamba venom. Results confirmed the suitability of the experimental design for discovery and 
production of recombinant antibodies targeting snake toxins.

8 PERSPECTIVES AND FUTURE EXPERIMENTS 
For the first time, fully functional human recombinant IgGs have been reported to provide full 
protection against elapid toxins and whole venoms in vivo. A collaborative approach including 
multiple experts from various fields working together in research and development of novel 
therapeutics helps provide a great capacity to overcome the inevitable challenges 
encountered during the experimental work. Therefore, I would like to passionately encourage 
those interested in the field to exchange innovative ideas towards a common goal of 
improving snakebite treatment. 
 
Phage display used for toxin targeting antibody discovery is a versatile and robust technique, 
allowing to screen libraries of unique antibody variants. Several conditions could be optimized 
in the future. Selections of high quality antibodies could be improved by affinity maturation 
through gene shuffling in CDR regions from the selection outputs, followed by additional 
rounds of phage display with newly introduced variants from selected populations of 
antibodies. Moreover, in this thesis, a uniform amount of antigens was used for panning, 
which does not allow differentiation between low affinity and high affinity binders until later 
screening with ELISA. As an improvement, a decreasing amount of antigens could be used in 
each selection round to select only the best clones, exerting the highest affinity towards 
selected antigen. Additionally, phage display experiments were performed against the same 
toxin in each selection round. This is a good approach for highly specific toxin targeting 
antibody discovery. However, one of the original goals was to discover a suite of antibodies, 
capable to recognize, bind, and neutralize similar toxins from different snake venoms. The 
current approach is based on identifying cross-reactive clones through ELISA screening. 
Alternatively, a more proactive approach could be implemented by introducing alternating 
panning against several toxins belonging to the same family, but originating from a different 
snake. For example, fraction Dp8 from green mamba contains dendrotoxins and is similar to 
fraction Da5 from black mamba. Using both fractions in different selection rounds could give 
rise to antibodies, specific for dendrotoxins, yet versatile enough to be applicable for 
treatment of envenomings caused by different species. Since antigen presentation plays an 
important role in successful phage display selections, preparation of antigen should be 
optimized. Only one batch of biotinylated toxins was used for the discovery of antibodies. 
However, several different ratios of toxin to biotinylation reagent could be used in parallel to 
determine the optimal biotinylation rate, specific for individual toxins.  
 
Antigen preparation by RP-HPLC venom fractionation is a time consuming step and therefore 
a limiting factor. Since antigens are used excessively in several steps, such as phage display, 
screening with ELISA, and in vivo neutralization studies, alternatives should be explored. 
Toxins could in the future be produced by synthetic or recombinant technology, which would 
reduce the variation in the quality of antigen preparation and allow to prepare toxins which 
cannot currently be isolated from the venom by fractionation due to their instability.  
 
Suggested route of administration for assessing the neutralization potential provided by the 
WHO is intravenous injection. Since small scale production of antibodies is expensive, 
intracerebroventricular route of administration has been introduced, as it requires less 
material. However, lethal doses have previously only been determined for intravenous 
injection, highlighting the need to perform LD50 determination studies of isolated fractions 
for intracerebroventricular injection. Additionally, because antibody selections have been 
performed with fractions which often contain several different toxins, antibodies with good 
affinity and observed neutralization potential may only bind to one of the fractions. It is 
==PAGE:79==

important to perform the experiments where targets of produced antibodies are determined, 
which could be done by MALDI-TOF-TOF.

Results presented in this thesis are a product of work performed by Urska Pus. However, 
several researchers from all around the world have contributed to the execution of the 
experimental plan. Toxicovenomics studies of black mamba, green mamba, and monocled 
cobra have been performed by Andreas H. Laustsen, Bruno Lomonte, Brian Lohse, Julián 
Fernández, José María Gutiérrez, Line P. Lauridsen, Arne R. Rasmussen, and Christina Milbo. 
Inceptive phage display selections for binders against toxins from the venom of black mamba 
and monocled cobra have been performed by Andreas H. Laustsen and analyzed under 
supervision of Aneesh Karatt Vellatt. Antibody discovery against toxins from the venom of 
green mamba, subcloning, and screening was performed by Urska Pus. Initial green mamba 
venom fractionation and biotinylation for phage display was conducted by Queralt Serra 
Camprubí, whereas black mamba venom fractionation and biotinylation for screening 
purposes of antibodies against black mamba venom has been carried out by Cecilie Knudsen. 
Screening of scFv and IgG antibody populations against black mamba toxins was performed 
by Urska Pus. Analysis by MALDI-TOF-TOF was performed by Bruno Lomonte. Expression of 
IgG antibodies in mammalian cell cultures was completed by Rachael Leah. Antibodies against 
black mamba and monocled cobra were tested through a series of in vivo neutralization 
studies, designed by Urska Pus, and assisted by José María Gutierrez, Ana-Silvia Arias, and 
Cecilie Knudsen.
