Abstract
The use of refrigeration is of great importance for the world of today as it is often directly linked
to the living standard. In a future perspective, the role of refrigeration only becomes more sig-
niﬁcant as living standards throughout the world increases. The ﬁeld of refrigeration spans wide
across many applications but also has a great environmental impact due to the refrigerant used and
the energy consumption of these systems. This impact is even greater due to the faulty operation
which is often undetected for numerous system. The use of fault detection and diagnostics has
been known for decades but with limited commercially use within the ﬁeld of refrigeration sys-
tems. Currently, the use of data-driven fault detection and diagnostics methods is most common.
However, the use of physics-based digital twins do have several advantages. Furthermore, current
research is quite limited regarding the investigation of fault within refrigeration system including
a liquid line receiver.
In this thesis a physics-based digital twin for fault detection in heat pumps and refrigeration includ-
ing a liquid line receiver which is able to simulate fouling and leakage has been developed. The
digital twin modelled in this thesis is modelled following the ideology of the simulation process
which strives towards the lowest level of detailing needed. The digital twin has been successfully
validated against fault free and faulty operation of an experimental setup. The digital twin has been
used to investigate the inﬂuence of fouling and leakage. The fouling proves to have great negative
impact on the performance of the system due to change in operational conditions but small inﬂu-
ences on the mass distribution within the components. Whereas, leakage results in no impact on
system performance due to the receiver in the system acting as refrigerant buffer allowing other
components to operate at optimal conditions.
Furthermore, the digital twin is used for data generation of fault free and faulty operational data
used for training and testing of a fault detection and diagnostics tool. The results of this study
proves that a digital twin may be used to generate data. The fault detection and diagnostics tool
is able to predict single simultaneous occurring faults with great accuracy and illustrates that the
best parameters for fault detection and diagnostics is the UA-value and the ﬁlling level of receiver.
The study also highlights the sensitivity of a fault detection and diagnostics tool with regard to
fault severity.

Introduction
In this chapter the motivation and background regarding the studying of Fault Detection and Di-
anostics in refrigeration system and heat pumps is presented. A present situation overview of
refrigeration will be given along with a future perspective. Second, the idea and principles of
Fault Detection and Dianostics are described. Third, an overview of state of the art within this
ﬁeld is summarized. Fourth, the simulation process which ideology the work in the project con-
forms to is described. Lastly, the scope of this thesis is presented along with an overview of the
structure of the thesis.

highest electricity consumers [8]. Supermarkets has an approximate annual energy consumption
of 540 kW h/m2 [9], which is more than 2 million kW h annually for a typically supermarket.

1.1.2
Fault Detection and Dianostics
Fault detection and diagnostics (FDD) is a practice which has been used for several decades cover-
ing many different ﬁelds. The research and use of the FDD process have since the 60’s been know
within ﬁelds including nuclear, aerospace, process control and military. FDD is together with
prognostics the two methods that is essential for condition-based maintenance [20]. The main
objective of FDD is to investigate and automate the detection of faults within physical systems
and diagnose the cause of these. FDD may also be used as a precautionary method to predict fu-
ture failures and the life-span of component. The main objective of the FDD system is the ability
to early detect fault, address each fault by diagnosing the cause of the fault/faults and then take
necessary actions in order to limit or prevent loss of performance, damage to the system, loss in
service ect.
The typical operation and maintenance process for a engineered system including a FDD process
has been described by [21] and the process is presented in Figure 1.3. This representation of the
FDD process has been widely used in many ﬁelds to describe FDD for both critical and non-
critical systems. Overall the process consist of four major processes where the ﬁrst two step are
considered as the FDD process. First process consist of monitoring the physical system from
measurements. Based on these measurements the FDD process must determine whether a fault is
present or not. Followed by a diagnosis determining the cause of the fault. Afterwards the fault
is evaluated based on certain criteria. The last process uses the information obtain in the fault
evaluation to determine what decision must be taken. Though the overall idea behind the FDD
process is quite simple and may be boiled down to three key point: a) Fault Detection, b) Fault
Isolation and c) Fault Identiﬁcation, the methods behind it are numerous and their approaches

1.1.2.1
FDD Methods
The main objective of any FDD method is to ﬁrst detect a faults and afterwards diagnose their
causes. The major difference in the nummerous approaches is their knowledge used to determine
diagnose and isolate faults. However, all is based on the same principle of comparing operational
data to fault-free data. At one of the spectre the methods use a physical understanding of the
speciﬁc system to formulate the diagnostics also known as knowledge-based methods and at the
other end spectre the methods are driven solely by data also known as data-driven methods. A
classiﬁcation seperating the different methods into sub categories has been developed by [20].
This categorization is presented in Figure 1.4. Here the methods of fault dianogstics are split in to
three main categories.
Quantitative Model-Based
Quantitative methods consist of methods that is based on the actual physics of a system. Model-
based methods uses model of a process, plant or system to analytically results with sensor meas-
urements to determine whether or not a fault has occurred. The models under this category may
further be split into: a) detailed physical models and b) simpliﬁed physical models. The detailed
models rely on a high level of knowledge regarding the physical relationship and characteristics
of the components within the system. Here a set of detailed differentiel equation must be set for
quatities such as mass, energy ect. Different faults also need different level of model detailing in
terms of fault detection. Faults occuring under transient requires a dynamic model to detect this

ferential and algebaric equation. Simpliﬁed physical models may also be steady-state models, as
many fault may be detected during steady-state operation. Simpliﬁed models are also of a much
lower complexity which results in them being computational easy to solve and hereby, much faster
compared to the detailed physical models.
Qualitative Model-Based
The second category of methods are Qualitative Model-Based. Similar to the ﬁrst group, the
methods in this category are model based, but here detection and diagnostics of faults are based
on qualitative relationships or knowledge. Qualitative Model-Based is divided into to two sub-
categories: a) Rule-Based and b) Qulitative physical models. Some of these method are able to
take a quantitative input are return a qulitative output and other have qulitative in-/outputs. Meth-
ods utilizes fuzzy logic to convert a quantitative input to a qualitative output. Method under the
rule-based category use in essence a knowledge based approach in developing a system of if-
then-else statements in order to draw conclusions. The rule-based method may be further divided
into three sub-categories: a) Expert systems, b) First-principle Models and c) Limits/Alarms. All
three method are quite similar as all uses rules but the deﬁnition of rules are different. The ex-
pert systems uses the knowledge and expertise of experts to formulate the system of statements
which should be evaluated. The second category uses rules constructed based on ﬁrst principle.
Lastly, a very simply method concerning limits and alarm may be used. This method relies on the
comparison between raw data which is directly measured and expected values.
The second sub-category from the main category is Qualitative Physics-Based Models.
This
method may be used for systems even when one has incomplete or uncertain knowledge of the
speciﬁc process occurring in the system. The two approaches is either derive qualitative equations
based on the governing equations describing or derive qualitative behaviour from the governing
equations.
Process History Based
The last main category consist of Process History Based methods also commonly known as data
driven methods. For data driven method every input and output are known and measured quantit-
ies. Data driven rely on data to mathematically ﬁnd patterns in order to relate measured input with
measured outputs. Data driven methods can be divided into Grey-Box modelling and Black-Box
modelling.
In Grey-box modelling the system is formulated in a way where any esitmate of investigated
parameters may be traced back to a actual physical principle or parameter governing the system.
In Black-box modelling the approach is similar to that of the Grey-box models. However, paramet-
ers can not be traced back to any physical parameter or principle. Black-box models uses statistical
and non-statistical methods to deduct the relations between inputs and outputs. Statistical models
may use method such as polynomial regression and non-statistical methods are based on machine-
learning and may use artiﬁcial network or other pattern-recognition. Black-box method may be
easier to set up, but the estimation of parameters tends to be less robust compared to Grey-box
models. Furthermore, Black-box models can not extrapolate outside their data range.

1.1.3
Sate of the Art
As stated, the ﬁeld of FDD has been investigated for several decades. An extensive review of
state-of-the-art FDD methods for refrigeration of commercial and residential buildings has been
carried out by [20, 22, 23]. The reviews illustrates a ﬁeld with regards to FDD for refrigeration
as a quite broad ﬁeld with numerous different model approaches and with limited commercially
implemented solutions. A trend in FDD methods is observed as in recent years a large majority of
FDD systems developed are data-driven.
Numerous physical models have been developed over the year, but only few has been used in
FDD implementations. The authors of [20, 22] highlights that physics-based models are often
the most accurate, reliable and is well suited to use in data-poor environments as data can be
obtained from the models. However, they are also often very complex, hard to formulate and may
need substantial computational power. This may render physic-based model unsuited for real-time
FDD [14, 19, 23].
The use of qualitative model have proven to have the advantages of being simple to formulate
and apply. They are well suited for data-rich and may present the most expedient way to meet
analytical needs [20, 22]. The authors of [6] and [24] both illustrated that the use of FDD based
on qualitative model was able to predict faults occurring with in a supermarket refrigeration unit.
The method proved to able identify faults within the system. Although the method proved to be
a sufﬁcient and robust method for identifying single faults, the method struggled to detect and
diagnose simultaneous occurring faults. The ability to detect multiple simultaneously occurring
faults are show through literature to be difﬁcult. As concluded by [6] rule-based methods requires
an effective set of rules and typically require several measurement inputs, but may not require
extensive training data for a speciﬁc system, as a data-driven method would require.
The data-driven methods are simple to develop and use, therefore, suitable for almost any applic-
ations as long as a signiﬁcant amount of measured data are available [20, 22]. The authors of [6]
also studied a purely data-driven black-box method. Compared to the rule-based model investig-
ated, the data driven model did not have the same accuracy but it was able to detect changes in the
energy consumption. In [7] the authors investigate the use of a black-box data driven model based
a neural network for fault detection. The model was able to distinguish between fault-free and
faulty operation both in transient and steady-state operation. Several of the methods used in FDD
are grey-box modelling. The aurthors of [25] investigated several gray-box component models
regarding the impacts of faults on performance for vapour compression systems. The model was
trained for eight different cooling systems. Overall, the models ensured accurate predictions for
a wide range of conditions both with and without faults. Proven the versatility of the grey box
approach. Another study using grey-box modelling were performed by [26]. This study provides
the ﬁrst published results of successful detection of multiple simultaneous occurring faults. The
model used a virtual sensor approach which was able to detect and isolated faults, even when mul-
tiple faults was imposed. Systems including a liquid line receiver are not often investigated with
regards to FDD. However, a grey-box model was also developed by [19] for FDD of a refrigeration
systems with a liquid line reciver. The receiver was be a particular challenge with regards to the
charge estimation. However, the overall model performed quite well with the ability to predict the
effects of faults on most operating variables with reasonable accuracy.

1.1.4
Simulation Process
The simulation process is method designed for modelling and simulation of engineered systems
[28]. The method is developed within the section of thermal energy under the department of
mechanical engineering at DTU. The method consist of ten steps devised by [29]. The ten steps
are presented below:
1) Research Question:
Here the main research question of the project is formulated and the question must be con-
crete.
2) Mind Model:
This step consist of constructing a mind model of the system one is investigating. Here one
must understand the state-of-art of the physical system i.e. how does the system work, how
is it constructed, what physical phenomena describe the system, etc. One may look at the
system on system, component and/or sub-process level.
3) Physical Model:
Here the Mind Model is evolved by applying the physical principles that model must be
able to simulate to answer the Research Question. Assumptions and boundary conditions
applied to the system along with governing equations for all components and sub-processes
are described.

4) Mathematical Model:
In this step the physical model is transformed into a mathematical model by describe the
system and its components in terms of equations directly from the physical model.
5) Numerical Model:
The mathematical model is translated into a numerical model by implementing it into a
simulation software.
6) Evaluation of the Numerical Model:
Once the numerical model is constructed, the solution must be evaulated. One must check
whether the solution provided by the model is mathematical and numerically credible. If
not satisﬁed with the numerical solution, one should return to step 5 of the method.
7) Veriﬁcation:
If the solution is credible, it must be veriﬁed by comparing the results with expectations
which may come from the Mind Model or by comparing model to theoretical results or
theory. Different scenarios must be checked in order to verify the model. If the model can
not be veriﬁed one should return to one of the previous steps. This could be back to step 5
but may even be back to step 2 as a general misconception could have been made there.
8) Validation:
The last quality check of the model is the validation. Here solution of the model is compared
to experimental obtain data. If the model can not be validated one may return to one of the
previous steps, once again it might be even back to step 2. However, a model might not be
validated due to other factors such lack of experimental data or poor quality of experimental
data. If this is the case one may use the model unvalidated and assume that physics used to
construct the veriﬁed model represent real-life system correctly.
9) Simulation:
The model is now validated and is a good approximation of the real system. Results produce
by the model will be a good representation of the operational behavior of the system. Here
one can perform various simulation in order to cover and answer the research question from
step 1.
10) Conclusion:
The main ﬁndings of the model can be presented.
The motivator for using the simulation process for this project is that the project strives towards
creating a model of the lowest detailing level which adequately simulates and predict leakage and
fouling occuring on a heat pump or refrigeration system.

receiver at state point 4 and enters the valve. The refrigerant is expanded through the valve and

a two phase state at state point 5. In the evaporator energy is transferred to the refrigerant from
the air in the storage room causing the refrigerant to evaporate and be superheated. The superheat
after the evaporator is controlled by an thermal expansion valve (TXV).

2.1.1 Steady-State Limitations
2.1.1
Steady-State Limitations
As the cycle of this project is modelled in a steady state situation, there are scenarios which
simply can not occur. As stated at the outlet of the condenser the refrigerant is saturated liquid.
The possibility of the refrigerant being sub-cooled as it exits would imply that the entire receiver
would be ﬁlled with sub-cooled refrigerant. The state of the refrigerant entering and exiting the
receiver must also be the same in order to obey the energy balance of the component. As it is
highly unlikely that the receiver would be completely ﬁlled with refrigerant, the scenario will not
be modelled.
Furthermore, if the refrigerant content within the cycle becomes low enough, the liquid level or
ﬁlling level in receiver may cease to exist. If the ﬁlling level becomes low enough one might
encounter a scenario where gas may be sucked through to the valve. However, valves are usually
designed for liquid to pass through. Due to the much lower density of gas, the mass ﬂow through
the valve in this situation would be signiﬁcantly lower than if liquid passed through. Even if the
valve was fully open, it would appear closed to the system as almost no mass would pass through.
Subsequently leading the conclusion that the system would not be able to operate. Lastly, it would
be highly unlikely of this scenario to occur without being detect earlier in the process. As a result,
the scenario where the ﬁlling level within receiver reaches zero will not be modelled.
This concludes that there must at all time exist two regions within the evaporator and condenser,
one with superheated gas and a two-phase region.

Heat Exchanger Modelling
In this section the methodology for determining of the heat transfer area and coefﬁcient for both
the evaporator and condenser is presented. In order to reduce repetition, both heat exchangers are
described as they are both modelled as ﬁnned-tubed heat exchangers (FTHX). A ﬁnned-tube heat
exchanger is presented in Figure 2.5. The refrigerant ﬂows within the tubes of the FTHX while
air passes between the ﬁns and over the tubes. The tubes of a FTHX may be placed in one of two
conﬁgurations: 1) Aligned or 2) Staggered. The difference between the two conﬁgurations is that
the tubes in direction of air ﬂow are either places right behind the tube in front or with a slight
displacement perpendicular to the air ﬂow, as illustrated in Figure 2.5. The FTHX is modelled
with regards to the following assumptions:
• Pressure loss on refrigerant side is neglected
• Spatial variations in temperature are neglected
• Dehumidiﬁcation of the air is neglected
• Inlet and outlet temperatures of air are assumed to be uniform
• The heat transfer coefﬁcient of the UA-value only accounts for the air side resistance, as
heat transfer coefﬁcient of the refrigerant is much greater (little resistance).
• FTHX is modelled as a counter-ﬂow heat exchanger

2.2.3
Condenser
This section describes the principles of the condenser and the modelling of the component. For
the condenser the following additional assumption is used.
1. Inlet of condenser is always superheated gas due to compressor.
2. A sub-cooled region can not occur due to assumptions and energy balance of the receiver.
In the condenser, the main objective is to dissipate heat by transferring energy from the refrigerant
to another media which in this case is the ambient air. This energy transfer is possible as long
as a temperature difference between the refrigerant and the air exits. Typically, there are two
refrigerant regions within the condenser: 1) A region with superheated gas and 2) a region with
two-phase ﬂow. Due to the assumption regarding the sub-cooling, there cannot exist a region with
sub-cooled refrigerant. The condenser is modelled using the moving boundary approach between
the two region. The heat transfer process is illustrated using a T,Q-diagram, as seen in Figure 2.8.

the refrigerant condensates in the second region. The regions with the condenser is inﬂuenced
by several parameters. The inlet is depend on the discharge state after the compressor, meaning
that greater superheat would result in a greater desuperheat region and a smaller two-phase region,
and vice versa. Second, the overall heat transfer coefﬁcient of the condenser (UAcond) sets the
pressure which subsequently set the density of refrigerant in different states. This coupled with
the mass of refrigerant (Mcond) in the condenser, also affect the regions.

2.2.6
Thermostatic Expansion Valve
The main purpose of the valve is to maintain the pressure difference between the high and low
pressure side. Secondly, the valve also ensure that the mass ﬂow of refrigerant match the heat ﬂux
of the evaporator resulting in a speciﬁc superheat. This also ensures that superheated gas enters
the compressor and hereby, averting liquid entering the compressor which may cause damage.
The valve is modelled as a thermostatic expansion valve (TXV). A TXV is the most common
typ of valve used in refrigeration systems and heat pump. A schematic of a TXV is presented in
Figure 2.14. Key components of a TXV e is 1) inlet and outlet, 2) Diaphram, 3) Superheat bulb, 4)
valve mechanism and 5) spring. The superheat bulb is place right after the outlet of the evaporator.
Depending on the type of valve used, the bulb can be ﬁlled with the same refrigerant used within
the system (parallel charged) or with a different ﬂuid (cross charged). The ﬂuid within the bulb
will be in gas and/or liquid depending on the operating conditions and/or type of TXV. As the bulb
heats up, the pressure inside increases. Once the pressure inside the bulb which is also pushing
down on the diaphram is greater than the pressure beneath the diaphram and the force acting upon
the diaphram cause by the spring, the valve starts to open. As seen from Figure 2.14, the valve
illustrated is externally equalized meaning that the pressure on the underside of the diphram is not
the same as the pressure leaving the valve, but rather the pressure at the outlet of the evaporator.
This commonly used to account for the pressure drop occuring through the evaporator, which if
not accounted for could lead to high superheat. However, as no pressure is assumed over the
evaporator in this study, the pressure at outlet and the pressure on the underside of diaphram is
equal. Once the force on both side of the diaphram is equal, the valve settles into a ﬁxed position.

The ﬁrst concept used is categorization. Categorization is the ability to recognize similarities
or be able to differ between certain features within a pool of data. Meaning that one is able
to based on speciﬁc values to classify object. For instance, it could be to distinguish between
ﬂower species or in this case fault occurring in a refrigeration system. Therefore, one needs a set
of classes/categories. For this study eight different categories are needed. These categories are
deﬁned in Table 2.13. This is all the different states the system can operate in.
In pricinple one feeds data to the FDD tool and it returns one of the eight categories. In order
for the FDD tool to distinguish between the categories a Decision Tree is used. A schematic of a
decision tree is illustrated in Figure 2.21. Each box in the decision tree contains a parameter. This
parameter is evaluated based on a certain criteria. Criteria are located on the branches between the
boxes as seen from the ﬁgure. The parameter is passed to each criteria and where the criteria is
true, one follows that branch to the next box where a new parameter or the same parameter is once
again evaluated. This is repeated until one reaches the bottom where the last parameter leads to
category.
The entire decesion tree is developed by training the FDD tool with data. The data set will be given
in a table form where rows represent operational points and columns represent parameters. The
FDD tool is then trained by given this data set along with a columns vector where is rows holds the
correct category for each operational point. In other words, the FDD tool develops criteria based
on the values of the parameters given and the known result. Once the FDD is train, one may test
it using data not used in the training data, so called test data. The test data will given to the FDD
tool without the solution vector. The accuracy of the FDD tool may be measured by comparing
the prediction made by the FDD tool on the test data against the solution vector of the test data.
This also means that one alwyas has the known solution for both the training and test data when
developing and testing a FDD tool.
A k-fold cross validation method is used to avoid selection bias. When giving data to a machine
learning algorithm (FDD tool), one could end up given the algorithm training data and test data
that aligned quite well, meaning that the algorithm produces a false sense of accuracy. One should
ﬁrst of all randomize the data, so that not pattern are present. And then applying the k-fold cross

It was found that the digital twin constructed in this study was able to simulate the effect of fouling
applied to the heat exchangers. The fouling modelled and experimentally applied where plates are
places on the frontal area of the heat exchangers which lead to a greater pressure drop needed
to be overcome by the fans. This then leads to a drop in volume ﬂow which leads to a decrease
in UA-value. This proved to be a great solution for applying and simulating fouling, especially
on the experimental setup where this is the only option. As described, fouling is the growth of
material on the heat transfers surfaces of a heat exchangers. This could be, as used in this study,
something stuck to the heat exchanger causing a greater pressure drop. However, it could also be
the formation of material ,such as ice, on the ﬁns of the exchangers which has a low conductivity
and therefore, contributes more to the low UA-value than the effects of the greater pressure drop.

conductive material on the ﬁns which would result in a UA-value equivalent of that fouling case
using the method of this study. The materials used are presented in Table 4.1. The UA-value

The receiver modelled in this thesis obeys both mass and energy balance. However, for it to obey
the energy balance the enthalpy at inlet and outlet must be equal, as modelled in this thesis. As the
receiver also models the hydrostatic pressure generated by ﬁlling level and the enthalpy at outlet
is equal that of the inlet, a isenthalpic pressurisation of the refrigerant occurs. The process must
be isenthalpic in order to obey the energy balance of the receiver but this also implies that must be
a temperature difference within the liquid refrigerant. As the inﬂuence of the hydrostatic pressure
is quite small, the simple modelling of the receiver does not affect the results and the purpose
of the model as a digital twin for fault detection. One might neglect the hydrostatic pressure as
effects are small. However, the phenomenons occurring regarding with the receiver may not be
precisely described with this level of modelling detail. Here one might consider investigating
a more detailed model of a receiver where energy transfer between liquid layers might occur.
However, as concluded from the validation, this level of detailing is not needed for the purpose of
the model.
The fan characteristics are quite rough approximation of a real life characteristic, since non was
obtainable. However as seen, the approximations did produced good results. The fan charac-
teristics not being precisely equal to that of the actual fans might be the reason that the greatest
deviations occur between the experimentally obtained and model at high fouling settings as the
volume ﬂow of air is more affect at higher fouling which subsequently affects the heat transfer
coefﬁcient.

CHAPTER5
Conclusion
In this thesis, a steady state digital twin for fault detection in heat pumps and refrigeration systems
with a liquid line receiver has been developed. The digital twin has been developed based on the
ideology of the simulation process by striving towards the lowest level of modelling detail needed.
A successful validation of the digital twin was carried out comparing model obtained results to
results obtained from a experimental refrigeration setup at different fouling conﬁgurations. From
the validation it was found that the greatest deviations are observed at high fouling conﬁgura-
tions. The ﬁndings of the validation proved that the level of detail needed for a digital twin to
sufﬁciently simulate the affects of fouling for a refrigeration system was to model the compressor
using polynomials, the heat transfer coefﬁcient of the heat exchangers only accounting for the
heat transfer resistance associated with the air side and a void fraction model combined with a
discretisation of 75 volume of the two phase region within the heat exchangers proved necessary
to obtained convergence regarding refrigerant mass within. Furthermore, the model needed the
fans to be modelled using fan characteristics, a pressure drop model for the air side pressure of
the heat exchangers, the fouling plates modelled as oriﬁce plates, the valve modelled as a theoret-
ical thermostatic expansion valve. The heeat transfer model approach proposed by Granryd [31]
proved to be the best model for estimation of heat transfer coefﬁcients on the air side.
The effect of fouling on the system operation was investigated. It was found that applying any kind
of fouling results in a decrease in system performance. System performance regarding COP was
affected more negatively by condenser fouling compared to fouling of the evaporator. However,
the cooling capacity of the system showed greater decrease when applying evaporator fouling
compared to condenser fouling due to the decrease in mass ﬂow. The effects of fouling was
observed to have greatest impact on the pressure side that the fouling occured, meaning condenser
fouling had great effects on the condenser side of the system compared the effects observed on the
evaporator side. Relative change in fouling showed to be more inﬂuential on system performance
at higher level of fouling. The mass distribution with heat exchangers and receiver was investigated
when applying fouling. It was found that fouling on either or both of the heat exchangers led to an
increase of refrigerant mass within the receiver and hereby an increase in the ﬁlling level which
was caused by the change in mass within the heat exchangers. Here mass within the evaporator
decreased when fouling was applied due to either a decrease gas density or increase in percentage
of gas occupying the volume. The mass within the condenser was affected by the change in gas
density caused by the affects of fouling. However, the change in mass within the receiver was
found to be quite small due to the effects of fouling. The same was observed for the ﬁlling level.
The air inlet temperature for both heat exchangers was observed to have greater impact on the
mass distribution and ﬁlling compared to the effects of fouling.
The effects of leakage proved to be insigniﬁcant with regards to system performance as no change
in performance was observed when leakage was applied to the system. This also highlighted
the beneﬁts of having a receiver in the system as the receiver acts as a refrigerant buffer storing
excess refrigerant allowing the other components to ﬁnd their optimal operational condition at any
given scenario. The only factor of the system affected was the ﬁlling level of the receiver which
decreased due to the leakage. However, this also implied that leakage could only be detected by
investigating the ﬁlling level of the receiver.

5.1 Future Work
Page 74
A case study was performed using the digital twin for data generation of fault free and faulty data.
The data was used to train a fault detection and diagnostics tool and furthermore, used to test the
tool. The case study proved to be capable of generating data that could be used for training of a
fault detection and diagnostic tool. The study further illustrate that the most essential evaluation
parameters were the ﬁlling level within the receiver and the UA-value of the heat exchangers. It
was found that even a simple estimated UA-value showed great results for both the single and
multiple faults occurring with a accuracy of 90 % and 75 %, respectively. Furthermore, the case
study highlighted the sensitivity fault detection and diagnostics tool as the tool were better at
correctly predicting fault of greater severity than the once trained with.
5.1
Future Work
As discussed in this thesis, the effects of material developing on the ﬁn area of the heat exchanger
could be of interest to investigate especially regarding the formation of bio ﬁlm. Here one could
investigated what contributes mostly to the decrease in UA-value caused by fouling of bio ﬁlm
growth or blocking of ﬂow area leading to lower volume ﬂow due to bio ﬁlm growth. However,
this might prove hard to experimentally validate as growth of bio ﬁlm might be difﬁcult to experi-
mentally replicate.
As presented in this thesis, the experimental setup did not allow for simulation of leakage. There-
fore, it might be of interest to show that leakage of the experimental setup with a receiver does
not affect the performance of the system as shown by the digital twin in this thesis. Furthermore,
it would be interesting to investigate a experimental setup where implementation of ﬁlling level
sensor has been carried out as this would allow one to evaluate the void fraction model used in the
digital twin.
Furthermore, it would be of great interest to implement the fault detection and diagnostics tool
developed using data generated by the digital twin on a experimental setup to evaluate the per-
formance of the tool in a real life situation and test the level of fault severity. Further, one could
also investigate the use of other machine learning method that may result in higher accuracy.
