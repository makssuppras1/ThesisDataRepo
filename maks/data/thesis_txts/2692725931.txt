Abstract 
 
While ready-to-eat (RTE) meals have become very popular, the same have also caused concerns 
among food producers and authorities. As these products are not processed by consumers after 
purchasing them, microbial behaviour is important to be investigated before a product is put on the 
market. Most important microorganism that has caused outbreaks from RTE meals is Listeria 
monocytogenes. Thus, the aim of this thesis was to investigate the growth of L. monocytgenes 
between a piece of cheese and tomato, a combination often found in RTE sandwiches. The growth 
was investigated using challenge tests with storage conditions of 10 ± 0.5°C for 10 days. Further on, 
the intrinsic factors, pH and water activity (aw) of the ingredients, were also studied.  
The experimental work was divided into three microbial and two chemical studies.  
It was found that L. monocytogenes did not grow in samples with two pieces of cheese or with two 
pieces of tomato on top of each other but did grow in a sample with cheese and tomato together in 
two out of three experiments. The concentrations increased from 4.3 log CFU/pc to 5.5 log CFU/pc 
and from 5.0 log CFU/pc to 9.0 log CFU/pc. Furthermore, pH of cheese decreased from 5.5 (SD = 
0.11) to 5 (SD = 0.08) and in contrast, pH of tomatoes increased from 4.4 (SD = 0.1) to 4.9 (SD = 
0.1). Aw measurement of both cheese and tomato were very similar with no significant changes 
through the incubation period where the average was 0.97 (SD = 0.004). Thus, equilibrium was 
reached in both pH and aw between the two ingredients. Aw equilibrium was reached on the first day 
of measurements while pH equilibrium was reached between day 3 and 7.  
Results will contribute to data base for predictive models that can be used for predicting growth of 
L. moncoytogenes in multi-component food systems.

1. INTRODUCTION 
Today, people are chasing money, power, success and a wilder, faster pace of life. Thus, one 
of the basic human needs has fallen into a second plan; feeding; making people seek convenient, fast 
foods. This has forced food industry to provide consumers with a solution – ready-to-eat (RTE) meals. 
One of the most famous ones was invented very long time ago, for the same reasons. In 1762, a 
gambling addict called John Montagu did not find it convenient to leave the gambling table, asking 
his butler to bring him meat tucked between two pieces of bread. As John was the Fourth Earl of 
Sandwich, this specialty was called after him.  Sandwiches are very convenient and today can come 
in a variety of forms, served cold or hot (“Grain of truth about sandwiches”, 2005). However, the 
storage temperatures of RTE meals in general should be taken seriously. Since RTE meals are 
products that are already precooked and packaged, requiring no further handling by the consumer, 
RTE can pose a great risk from food-borne pathogens. One of the most prevalent ones in these 
products is a bacterium called Listeria monocytogenes (Huang & Hwang, 2012). Beside being one of 
the most common pathogens in RTE foods, it also belongs to the most dangerous and deadly ones, 
causing the highest number of deaths compared to other food-borne pathogens (Paudyal & Karatzas, 
2016). 
 
Listeria monocytogenes is ubiquitous. The reason is that it can be found in soil and water, 
from where it contaminates various food stuff such as vegetables and meat (Haase et al., 2013). When 
it once enters the processing plant, if the right hygienic procedures are not implemented, it can persist 
on the surfaces creating biofilms on utensils and equipment, exposing food to cross-contamination 
even after it has been processed (Camargo et al., 2017). Thus, RTE meals are the most common 
products with its contamination, as customers do not process food any further, after it has been 
potentially contaminated. Even if the product is refrigerated at consumer’s home, L. monocytogenes 
will still be able to survive and grow to concerning concentrations (Walker et al., 1990). 
 
In sandwiches, two products that are commonly used, sometimes can be a vehicle for L. 
monocytogenes to get into this RTE meal. These are cheddar cheese and tomato. Tomato is a 
vegetable and, thus, can be contaminated while still growing on the wine, from the soil and water. 
Cheddar cheese can be contaminated from the milk used in processing, being an end-product of milk 
fermentation, or by cross-contamination in the processing plant. However, when these products are 
on their own, they are not perfect substrates for the growth of L. monocytogenes. On contrast, when 
the two are put together, L. monocytogenes shows growth (Bingöl, 2020). Cheese and tomato together 
can be called by definition a composite food. Multicomponent composite products present more 
complex situations, especially at the interface of the dissimilar components, where there will be an 
==PAGE:7==

equilibrium established in properties that affect microbial growth, which may alter the expected 
behaviour of pathogens during storage in either the food components or their interface. This 
phenomenon is not yet thoroughly investigated and thus raises questions.  
 
A possibility of predicting this growth would allow food producing companies to save a lot 
of money and time on investigating their products and the potential they have for supporting growth 
of the microorganisms. These kinds of models exist and are used widely by food producing 
companies. However, data sets for development of these predictive models need to be extensive. As 
heterogenous food systems and their potential to support growth of L. monocytogenes does not 
contain enough data, additional studies should be conducted in order to increase understanding of the 
topic.  
 
1.1 THESIS OBJECTIVE 
 
Objective of this master thesis is to investigate potential of growth of L. monocytogenes and 
characterise the intrinsic factors that support the growth of L. monocytogenes in the interface between 
slice of cheese and a tomato, the combination often found in sandwiches. 
 
 
The hypothesis was that L. monocytogenes will not grow between two pieces of tomatoes and 
two pieces of cheese, due to their intrinsic factors that could limit growth. Furthermore, it was 
hypothesized that when two pieces of tomato and cheese come together, equilibriums of pH and water 
activity (wa) would be achieved and thus allow L. monocytogenes to grow. 
 
 
This was done through challenge studies with L. monocytogenes. Three types of samples were 
tested: 
- 2 slices of cheese together 
- 2 slices of tomato together 
- a slice of cheese and a slice of tomato together 
 
 
The testing included inoculation of the mentioned and incubation for 10 days. Temperature the 
samples were kept in was 10 ± 0.5°C. The sampling and microbial counting was done on days 0, 3, 7 
and 10. 
 
 
Chemical tests were also executed, though on uninoculated samples. AW and pH were measured 
on the same days as microbial counting was done.

2. LITERATURE REVIEW 
 
2.1 LISTERIA CHARACTERISTICS 
Listeria monocytogenes was discovered in 1926 by E.G.D. Murray and J. Pirie. The two were 
working independently on outbreaks among laboratory rabbits and guinea pigs (Lecuit, 2020). 
The bacteria first got the species name “monocytogenes” by Murray to describe a new bacillus with 
potential monocytosis - producing activity in both the rabbits and guinea pigs. Some years after, in 
1940, Pirie named Listeria in honour of Lord Joseph Lister, British pioneer of sterile surgery (Lundén 
et al., 2004). 
 
It is one of the 17 members of the Listeria genus (Orsi & Wiedmann, 2016). L. monocytogenes 
is the only one recognised as a human pathogen while L. ivanovii can cause diseases only in animals 
(EFSA, 2019). L. monocytogenes is a rod shaped bacteria (0.5–4 μm in diameter and 0.5–2 μm in 
length), catalase-positive, and oxidase-negative, gram-positive facultative anaerobe that does not 
form spores (Jamshidi & Zeinali, 2019). It expresses β-hemolysin which can be seen as zones of 
clearing on blood agar (Figure 1). The organism has a peritrichous flagella, which is why it shows a 
characteristic tumbling motility. (Farber & Peterkin, 1991) Flagella cannot be produced at and above 
37 °C. Thus, it is not motile above 37°C. It shows most motility 20-25°C (Batt & Tortorello, 2000). 
 
L. monocytogenes can ferment a number of carbohydrates including hexoses and pentoses where the 
fermentation patterns can be used to distinguish L. monocytogenes from other Listeria species (Batt 
& Tortorello, 2000).

2.2 OPTIMAL FACTORS 
L. monocytogenes is one of the most ubiquitous bacteria in natural environment, living 
naturally in soil where it can survive up to several months by multiplying in decaying vegetation 
(Lyautey et al., 2007). It can grow in the temperature range of 1.5 °C to approximately 45 °C, with 
an optimum temperature of 30 – 37 °C (Farber & Peterkin, 1991). The organism can withstand 
freezing and is inactivated by heating at 60 °C for 30 min (Batt & Tortorello, 2000). Optimum pH is 
7.0 with a full range growth conditions of 4.3 - 9.6 (Table 1) (Farber & Peterkin, 1991).

As this bacterium is one of the most rigid ones when found under stressful conditions, it is important 
to investigate its behaviour under the mentioned. The following section thus describes mechanisms

2.3 BEHAVIOUR UNDER ADVERSE ENVIRONMENTAL CONDITIONS 
 
2.3.1 Low temperatures 
The ability that L. monocytogenes have to survive and grow at low temperatures, like 
refrigeration  temperatures, makes even low level of contaminated product potentially dangerous for 
consuming. Furthermore, cross-contamination in domestic refrigeration can also lead to more 
contaminated products (Tabit, 2018). Although behaviour of L. monocytogenes is not fully 
understood when exposed to stressful environment, such as low temperatures, studies have shown 
that there is a variety of mechanisms involved in the adaptation of L. monocytogenes to low 
temperatures (Gandhi & Chikindas, 2007).

2.3.2 Low pH 
With a lower limit of pH for growth being 4.3 - 4.6, L. monocytogenes is in a group of bacteria that 
is not acid tolerant. However, the organism has the ability to alter its cellular physiology so it becomes 
highly resistant to environments with low pH (Gandhi & Chikindas, 2007; Koutsoumanis et al., 
2003). When L. monocytogenes is exposed to mild acidic environment (pH = 5.5), acid tolerance 
response (ATR) is activated. Adaptations that L. monocytogenes has to low pH include: 
- inducing proteins, some of which are the same ones induced as a response to low temperature, 
- pH homeostasis which is accomplished by proton transport across the cell membrane, (Gandhi & 
Chikindas, 2007) 
- implementation of the glutamate decarboxylase system where increased cytoplasmic pH is a result 
of intracellular proton being consumed in a reaction 
- mutation of general stress sigma factor (σB) and redirection of transcription (Ryan et al., 2008) 
- two-component regulatory system in which the two components sense the changes outside the cell 
after which the response regulator enhances altered gene expression 
Morever, as a consequence of activated the above mentioned ATR, L. monocytogenes can become 
acid-adapted with a higher resistance to osmotic shock (25 - 30% NaCl), alcohol stress, and heat 
shock (52oC) (Gandhi & Chikindas, 2007). 
 
2.3.3 High salt concentration / low aw 
Historically, salt started out as being a preservative in food. The reasoning behind this is that 
salt reduced aw which is the amount of unbound water that is available to support microbial growth 
and react in chemical reactions. It is believed that chloride and sodium ions ability to associate with 
water molecules is why salt can successfully lower aw (Fennema, 2007; Potter & Hotchkiss, 1996). 
==PAGE:12==
 
11 
Salt can also directly impact the growth of bacteria by causing osmotic shock that results in loss of 
water from the cell. By loosing water from the cell, microbial organism will die or have retarded 
growth (Davidson et al., 2005). Beside the physiological changes, gene expression patterns can also 
be altered, resulting in osmoadaptation. Responses include: 
- protein induction 
- accumulation of compatible solutes in high concentration in the cell without affecting the cell 
functions. As a result, the cell can take osmolytes from the environment to regain the osmotic 
balance. It has been shown that this mechanism can result in an up to 2.6-fold increase in growth 
rate of L. monocytogenes (Gandhi & Chikindas, 2007), 
- mutation of general stress sigma factor (σB) and redirection of transcription (Ryan et al., 2008) 
- two-component regulatory system in which the two components sense the changes outside the cell 
after which the response regulator enhances altered gene expression (Gandhi & Chikindas, 2007) 
 
L. monocytogenes shows high tendency of cross-protection to any kind of environmental stress, 
resulting in its resistance. By exposing it to the environment that this organism is sensitive to, it will 
become an even stronger organism (Gandhi & Chikindas, 2007). 
 
As this microorganisms has been shown to adapt to various environmental conditions, it has 
consequently also caused a lot of cases of infections called “listeriosis”. Following, the infection is 
further explained.  
 
2.4 LISTERIOSIS 
L. monocytogenes ubiquitousness and survival properties are why it can be easily transmitted 
from food to humans, especially if hygiene and regulations are not respected (Haase et al., 2014). As 
L. monocytogenes has possibility to cross blood-brain, placental and gastrointestinal protective 
barriers in human organism, the symptoms and outcomes that occur as the sign of the infection can 
range from minor aches to death (Table 3) (Caballero et al., 2003).

Listeriosis in human organisms can cause invasive and noninvasive infections. Invasive 
listeriosis can cause sepsis, abortions, encephalitis and meningoencephalitis. It has shown to have a

2.5 OUTBREAKS 
This intracellular pathogen has been causing several outbreaks of food-borne diseases within the 
past decade. It has thus been recognised as one of the most significant food-borne pathogens by the 
World Health Organization. As a result of process and technology innovations, the way that food is 
produced, distributed and stored have changed during years. This has created a potential for 
widespread outbreaks that involves a lot of countries worldwide (de Valk et al., 2005).  
 
==PAGE:14==
 
13 
Most common foods that have been linked to L. monocytogenes reported outbreaks include:  
- Milk and dairy products, 
- RTE foods (deli meats), 
- Seafood, smoked seafood, 
- and Pâtés (Desai et al., 2019). 
 
Products that have served as vehicles for over half of the total listeriosis outbreaks over years 
are milk and dairy products. High percentage of these outbreaks have been linked to products 
produced from raw milk. 
In the summer of 2001, 48 people had contracted febrile gastroenteritis from consuming dairy 
products that were manufactured at a local farm in Sweden. The consumed fresh cheese was produced 
from raw milk. The L. monocytogenes isolates from all the infected patients were identical to the ones 
isolated from the dairy product. Another example in dairy product induced outbrek includes listeriosis 
outbreak in 1995 with 37 cases, in France. This outbreak was associated with the consumption of Brie 
cheese produced from raw milk. L. monocytogenes isolates were identical to isolates from four types 
of Brie cheese samples. Epidemiological study results confirmed that all the patients have consumed 
these four types of Brie cheeses (Lundén et al., 2004). 
Furthermore, RTE foods have also caused many L. monocytogenes outbreaks. Multi-country outbreak 
in 2017 caused 21 cases of listeriosis as an outcome of consuming contaminated RTE meat product. 
Nineteen cases occurred in the Netherlands and remaining two occurred in Belgium. The patients had 
onset of symptoms between 2017 and 2019. Most of the isolates from meat samples matched the 
outbreak strain. Contamination point was found to be the production site (EFSA, 2019). In 2014, 
”rullepølse “, a Danish cold cut specialty made from pork, caused an outbreak affecting in total 41 
patients with 17 deaths. As this outbreak had a large impact in Denmark, the Danish Ministry of Food, 
Agriculture and Fisheries implemented new control of Listeria in Denmark (Agasan et al., 2014). 
Moreover, in UK, there has been 8 detected outbreaks since 1999. From these, in two outbreaks it has 
been proven that cheddar cheese and tomatoes were vehicles for L. monocytogenes (Little et al., 
2012). 
 
A seasonal pattern has been observed with the occurrence of outbreaks in period of 2008-2017 
in EU. It was concluded that the peaks are in the summer seasons with less high peeks in the winter 
seasons (EFSA, 2018). Number of listeriosis cases has been reported to grow over years, as seen in 
Table 4. The increase throughout the years can be justified by the fact that more various foods are 
being recognised as vehicles for L. monocytogenes transmission (EFSA, 2018).

2.6 MONITORING AND REGULATION 
 
As it poses great risk and a high mortality rate, prevention of listeriosis is considered to be of 
major importance. Thus, great amount of work had to be done to decrease number of listeriosis cases. 
Preventive measures were implemented in all along the food chain and the consumers were educated 
(Lundén et al., 2004). Monitoring along the food chain starts at preharvest (farm), continues in 
processing, and ends at post-harvest (retail). L. monocytogenes is also controlled by implementation 
of HACCP programme, Good Agricultural Practices (GAP), Good Hygiene Practices (GHP), Good 
Manufacturing Practices (GMP), both at the processing stage and retail. Moreover, sampling 
procedures at the production site are mandatory to check for compliance with the food safety criteria 
(EFSA, 2018). This can be found in Commission Regulation (EC) No 2073/2005 on microbiological

2.7 USE OF PREDICTIVE MICROBIOLOGY 
In recent years, public has been more informed about the crises in the food industry, thus 
raising awareness of the safety of the food they eat. They have also started to asses food more 
critically, demanding healthy, nutritious and fresh products (Saygi & Shipman, n.d.). Beside concerns 
of chemicals in food, microbial safety has also became a discussed topic amongst consumers, as 
hazards such as L. monocytogenes has caused some concerning outbreaks and the outcomes 
(Devlieghere et al., 2009). 
 
To ensure the microbial safety, one of the ways is to do challenge studies with food products. 
A microbial challenge study includes contaminating tested food products with bacteria of concern 
and observing their behaviour during production and distribution under different conditions (Spanu 
et al., 2014). However, microbial challenge study has its downfalls as it is usually time-consuming 
and expensive. Thus, companies with raising numbers of new and different products were seeking 
for an alternative method to ensure the microbial safety of all of their products (Devlieghere et al., 
2009). As a response to this demand, in the last 20 years, predictive models in foods have been 
developed due to the appearance of statistical packages and computational resources. 
 
Predictive microbiology, an area within food microbiology, studies behaviour of micro-
organisms in food products in response to environmental conditions through mathematical functions 
(Pérez-Rodríguez & Valero, 2013). It is a combination of mathematics, information system, 
technology, statistics and microbiology knowledge. Microbial behaviour is determined by storage 
conditions (e.g. temperature) and properties of the observed food product (e.g. pH and aw).  The 
mathematical models can give an estimation of growth rate, death response, production of toxins or 
sporulation, relationship between inputs and outputs (Stavropoulou & Bezirtzoglou, 2019), all based 
on the premises that the populations of microorganisms will have consistent responses to 
environmental factors, and that by characterising foods, it would be possible to predict their response, 
from past observations (Batt & Tortorello, 2000). Therefore, quantitative studies on microbial 
populations are needed to provide data for database. These databases contain a great amount of 
information about the effects of different conditions on microbial growth, survival and death 
(Fakruddin et al., 2012; Feiner, 2006). The information in databases can provide knowledge of the

changes (e.g. pH, aw, salt concentration) in the product itself, from which, by inputting environmental 
conditions (e.g. temperature, humidity, atmosphere), behaviour of bacteria can be predicted (Fox & 
McSweeney, 2017). Predictive microbiology model has a wide range of practical applications, shown

2.8 FOOD INGREDIENTS AND LISTERIA 
 
2.8.1 Cheese 
Generic name for the group of products that are made from fermented milk is cheese. This 
product, that has originated from an objective of preventing milk spoilage, today has a role of one of 
the most valued cuisine products. It is produced all around the globe, in a wide range of shapes and 
tastes, resulting in over 1000 types of cheeses (Ferrão et al., 2019). As it is produced from milk, it is 
the main ingredient that contributes and dictates the safety and quality including biochemical, 
microbiological and sensory aspect of the finished product. Thus, milk can serve as a vector for 
bacteria to come into cheese but also provide an excellent medium for the further growth of bacteria 
and other microorganisms (Fox & McSweeney, 2017). 
 
As already mentioned in chapter 2.5, several outbreaks of L. monocytogenes have been linked 
to RTE  foods and dairy products, cheese particularly, belonging to both the categories (Swaminathan 
& Gerner-Smidt, 2007). One of the biggest and the first ones dates back to 1985, when a major 
outbreak of L. monocytogenes was detected after consumption of Mexican cheese in southern 
California, after which it started to be more noticed in other outbreaks around the world. Both non-
invasive and invasive listeriosis were linked to these outbreaks. Half of the listeriosis outbreaks in 
EU were related to L. monocytogenes outbreaks from dairy products (Swaminathan & Gerner-Smidt, 
2007). 
 
Suitable environment from cheese poses great opportunity for growth of L. monocytogenes 
and its tolerance responses that can result in both presence in cheese and persistence on surfaces in 
processing plants, creating unremitting biofilms.  Even though in EU extensive food safety 
regulations have been implemented, the control over it has remained a challenge (Melo et al., 2014). 
Usually, pasteurisation is accepted as an effective treatment against any pathogen in food, including 
dairy products. However, as already mentioned, cheese belongs to both the RTE and dairy products 
category, meaning that it is not subjected to heating treatment before consumption but is rather stored 
at refrigeration temperatures before consumption. These temperatures are usually not allowing 
growth to other bacteria but L. monocytogenes can survive and sometimes even grow at these 
(McAuley et al., 2014). 
 
 
Beside being contaminated from milk used in the production of cheese, some other vectors 
are also a possible way for L. monocytogenes to get onto cheese. Multiple sources include brine, 
drains, floor, starter cultures, packaging material, shelves, clothes, knifes, coolers and brushes 
(Chambel et al., 2007). Along with milk, some other elements also dictate composition of cheese and 
==PAGE:19==
 
18 
thus growth of L. monocytogenes. These are manufacturing conditions, starter cultures (type and 
activity), acidification, salting and ripening (Gnanou Basse et al., 2000). Because there is over 1000 
types of cheese, compositions can vary greatly between them. It is why, for the purpose of this work, 
only cheddar cheese will be discussed.  
 
This cheese is a very popular and loved type of cheese due to its distinctive flavour, aroma 
and taste. It is classified as a hard cheese that is ripened and has a long shelf life. It does not have any 
surface flora. Its production process include a lot of various processing steps, making this process 
rather complicated. Thus, quality, yield and composition are influenced greatly by the processing 
steps and conditions. Main cultures used in the production, for initiating acidification, are 
Lactococcus lactic subsp. cremoris and Lactococcus lactis subsp. lactis., while sometimes  other 
strains of Lactobacilli are also used for accelerating ripening and improving or changing flavours and 
aroma (Nassar et al., 2015).

acids are also very often added to food with a function of preservation, flavoring, acidification and

- lactic acid: 11.53–13.37mg g−1,  
- formic acid: 0.28–0.50mg −1g, 
- citric acid: 2.10–2.13mg g−1, 
- acetic acid: 0.14–0.15mg g−1 (Mullin & Emmons, 1997)  
 
2.8.1.2 Jameson effect 
RTE food, cheese including, usually have L. monocytogenes present in low numbers, in 
contrast to natural background microbiota and spoilage microbiota, which can be present in very high 
numbers (Mejlholm & Dalgaard, 2013). Interactions between these background microorganisms and 
L. monocytogenes may occur, thus mathematical models were developed to predict these interactions 
(Giménez & Dalgaard, 2004). 
This interaction can be described as growth suppression of L. monocytogenes by a single dominant 
strain, referred to as a “Jameson Effect” in literature (Culliney & Schmalenberger, 2020).  Production 
of specific inhibitors of growth (e.g. bacteriocines, siderophores) by one strain, and competition for 
substrates between the strains, are believed to be the reasonings behind this phenomenon (Mellefotn 
et al., 2008; Dalgaard, 2002). 
 
Basis of the mathematical models that predict the Jameson effect is that there is an upper 
concentration limit of the growth of L. monocytogenes (and other bacteria). Usually, when L. 
monocytogenes grows in food, this upper limit is around 109 CFU/g or CFU/ml. But, when found 
with dominant strain, this concentration will become lower (FAO/WHO, 2004). In cheese, this 
dominant strain belongs to Lactic acid bacteria (LAB). Many studies have proven that LAB will 
stop the growth of L. monocytogenes when it reaches its maximum concentration. This 
phenomenom is depicted in Figure 2.

2.8.2 Tomato 
This vegetable is one of the most important vegetables in the whole world and its production 
went up to 105 million tons in 2001, confirming its importance. It is also economically attractive as 
the crop duration is short and it gives a high yield. Beside this aspect, tomato is also very nutritious 
(rich in vitamins, essential amino acids, dietary fibres, minerals and sugars), healthy and contributes 
to a well-balanced diet. Raw, fresh tomatoes can be used in salads and sandwiches while when 
cooked, it can be used in soups, meat and fish dishes, and sauces (Balemi et al., 2005). 
 
Tomato has caused a lot of outbreaks in the history. Some of the microorganisms credited for 
them are Salmonella spp., hepatitis A, norovirus and L. monocytogenes (Krug et al., 2020). There is 
a lot of steps in the farm-to-table sequence where L. monocytogenes can enter and contaminate 
tomatoes. Starting with cultivation at farm, it can be contaminated from irrigation water, domestic 
animals, improper handling and soil. Further on, during production, it can also be contaminated by 
improper handling and from the production surfaces (biofilms) (Honjoh et al. 2016).  
 
Intrinsic factors of raw, fresh tomatoes can be seen in Table 7. Aw and salt concentration do 
not pose an obstacle for growth of L. monocytogenes in tomatoes, but pH can. As the lower limit of 
pH that L. monocytogenes can withstand (Table 1) is 4.3 - 4.5, pH in tomato is on a borderline of 
allowing growth. Thus, by EU definition, tomatoes are on the borderline of supporting growth of L. 
monocytogenes. Studies have shown that, when stored at temperature from 5 to 15°C, tomatoes do 
not usually pose as a great substrate for the growth of the organism. However, some studies have 
proven that L. monocytogenes can grow at refrigeration temperatures (Nørregaard Sarr, 2020; 
Beuchat & Brackett, 1991).

3.5 ANALYSIS OF SAMPLES 
 
3.5.1 Microbial test 
 
After incubation, each sample was transferred with a sterile tip into a sterile stomacher filter-
bag (400 ml, interscience) with 50 ml of 0.9% saline. Then, these were stomached in a stomacher 
(Stomacher 400) two times for 60 s at high speed. After this, 0.1ml of liquid part from the stomacher 
bag was used for 10-fold dilution and 0.1 ml of that same liquid was plated on each of the selected 
plates (Table 8) separately.  
Ten-fold dilutions were done accordingly with the expected counts, each time the sampling was done. 
The dilutions were then plated.  
==PAGE:28==
 
27 
Plates were incubated on days 0, 3, 7 and 10 and were counted 3 days after each sampling day.  
Tryptone Soy Agar (TSA) (SSI diagnostica) plates were used in all microbial experiments as they are 
for general-purpose that can provide enough nutrients for a wide range of microorganisms. Thus, 
TSA plates are non-selective and require usage of other selective plates to get a better understanding 
of the whole microflora of the tested samples. TSA plates were incubated for 3 d at 21 ± 1 °C, 
aerobically.  
 
Palcam agar plates (E & O Laboratories Ltd) were also used in all microbial experiments as they 
are selective and indicative towards L. monocytogenes. This is possible because of the double 
indicator system that Palcam medium possess. 
1) Aesculin and ferrous iron 
2) Mannitol and phenol red 
 
 
Firstly, L. monocytogenes hydrolyses aesculin which results in the black halo circles around the 
colonies. Secondly, it does not ferment mannitol which differentiates it from other contaminants that 
can ferment mannitol easily (enterococci and staphylococci). This results in change from red to 
yellow in the pH indicator phenol red. (“Dehydrated Culture Media”, n.d.) Thus, Palcam plates were 
used for identifying and enumerating concentration of L. monocytogenes in the tested samples. 
Palcam plates were incubated for 3 days at 30 ± 1 °C, aerobically.  
 
 
De man, Rogosa, Sharpe agar (M.R.S.) (Oxoid) plates were used in microbial experiment 3 as 
a selective medium towards growth of lactobacilli. By containing sodium acetate, it suppresses 
growth of other microbiota present. M.R.S. plates were incubated for 3 days at 37 °C, anaerobically. 
These plates were placed into anaerobic chamber with bags generating anaerobic conditions (Thermo 
SCIENTIFIC) by releasing CO2. Samples containing cheese in them were plated on M.R.S. plates (C 
+ C and T + C).  
 
DG18 agar plates (SSI diagnostica) were also used in microbial experiment 3. DG18 agar is 
a selective medium towards moulds and yeasts by containing Dichloran which can disable spreading 
of mucoracous fungi and in general restrict colony sizes, thus allowing formation of only small 
colonies of yeasts and moulds (“Dichloran-Glycerol (DG18) Agar Base“, n.d.). They were incubated 
for 3 days at 21°C, aerobically. Samples that had tomato in them were plated on DG18 plates (T + T 
and T + C). 
 
 
==PAGE:29==
 
28 
3.5.2 Chemical test 
 
Chemical test was divided into two parts. These two were measuring pH and aw of the 
samples.   
 
 
pH measurements were conducted using a digital pH meter (PHM 92 LAB pH METER). 
Samples were diluted with distilled water (1:4) and homogenised for approximately 30 min at a room 
temperature. The results were given by inserting a pH probe into the liquid part of the sample, after 
the homogenisation.   
 
 
Aw was measured with Aqua Lab model CX-2 (Decagon Devices, Inc.). The samples were not 
treated in any way before measurements.  
 
3.6 DATA ANALYSIS  
 
All of the samples were analysed in duplicates. For each sample, agar plates with bacteria were 
counted and the weighted average was calculated and converted to log CFU/pc. Average and standard 
deviation of duplicate samples were calculated in Microsoft Excel.  
 
 
Samples that were subjected to pH and aw measurements also had duplicates where average and 
standard deviation were calculated in Microsoft Excel. To confirm statistical significance of the data 
from the study, t-tests were conducted in Microsoft Excel. Significance level of 5% (p<0.05) was 
used to evaluate the differences.

As the pH in tomatoes in general ranges from 4.3 to 4.5 (Table 7), and in connection to growth

depending on its maturity. Anthon et al. (2011) has shown that, if left on wine after ripening, pH will

aerobic microorganisms. As high concentrations of LAB were observed, the Jameson effect could be

profiles. As sandwiches are usually stored at temperature of refrigeration, it would be very useful to 
have studies with temperatures that would resemble those of temperatures in consumers refrigerators. 
A study done on domestic refrigerators showed that highest percentage of mean ranges belonged to 
a temperature range of 6 to 6.9°C (James et al., 2002). As Ziegler et al. (2019) studied that there is a 
significant difference between the rates of growth of L. monocytogenes at 5 and 8°C, it is also 
expected that there would be significant differences in rates of growth between 6 and 10 ± 0.5°C. 
Thus, the concentrations of L. monocytogenes between a slice of cheese and tomato would change at 
a slower pace at 6°C than measured in this study, however, worthy of investigating.

5. CONCLUSION 
The purpose of this master thesis was to study the growth potential of L. monocytogenes to 
grow in between slices of cheese and tomato, a combination used daily in RTE sandwiches.  
It can be concluded that, in contrast to two slices of cheese together or two slices of tomato together, 
there was growth. In two out of three microbial experiments, in cheese and tomato samples, L. 
monocytogenes grew from the inoculum levels to 5.5 and 8.0 log CFU/pc.  
Different trends of changes of aw and pH were also noticed between the types of samples. Due to 
differences in intrinsic factors that act as a driving force, there was a mass transfer and, thus, 
equilibrium at the interface between cheese and tomato, allowing growth of L. monocytogenes.  
 
As RTE foods are becoming more and more popular, special attention needs to be dedicated 
to heterogenous food systems where products have different intrinsic factors. Furthermore, this kind 
of knowledge and data from studies will contribute to development of predictive models for multi-
component food systems, and food producing companies in the future. It was important to get a better 
understanding whether measuring each ingredient, that is a part of a multi-component food system, 
as a whole non-homogenized samples, can give an overview of the conditions at the interface. As the 
mass transfer is occurring at the interface between the two ingredients, and the equilibrium of pH and 
aw were confirmed in this study, this contributed to the hypothesis. Thus, when collecting data for the 
new predictive models for multi-component food systems and growth of L. monocytogenes, it will be 
enough to measure each ingredient as a whole non-homogenized samples, after being stored in the 
given multi-component system. These data can be then used as inputs for the predictive models.

Listeriosis is a foodborne disease caused by the listeria bacterium Listeria monocytogenes. 
The infection is most likely to sicken pregnant women and their newborns, adults aged 65 or older, 
and people with weakened immune systems. Pregnant women are 10 times more likely than other 
people to get a Listeria infection. 
Symptoms; fever, muscle pain, headache, fatigue and neurological symptoms and diarrhea. 
The work with Listeria monocytogenes takes place in dedicated lab-areas. When working with 
Listeria monocytogenes, coat and gloves have to be worn to protect clothes or any open wounds. 
All sample materials containing Listeria monocytogenes must be marked with LISTERIA.  
The work environment has to be disinfected with ethanol and waste has to be thrown into right 
place. 
 
 
Corona virus is a potential danger in this work as well, even though it is not an agent used in 
experiments. COVID-19 is a newly contagious disease caused by severe acute respiratory syndrome 
coronavirus (SARS-CoV-2). Symptoms of COVID-19 often include fever, cough, fatigue, breathing 
difficulties, and loss of smell and taste. The virus that causes COVID-19, spreads mainly when an 
infected person is in close contact with another person. Small droplets and aerosols containing the 
virus can spread from an infected person's nose and mouth as they breathe, cough, sneeze, sing, or 
speak. Other people are infected if the virus gets into their mouth, nose or eyes. The virus may also 
spread via contaminated surfaces. 
The work environment has to be disinfected with ethanol before and after the work is done and 
waste has to be thrown into right place. In the building, the mask has to be worn if in close contact 
with other personel. More than 5 persons should not be allowed to be in closed spaces at the same 
time.
