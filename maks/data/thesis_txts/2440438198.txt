Abstract 
Stimuli responsive supramolecular polymers are interesting, adaptive materials which find 
application in several technologies. The weak non-covalent interactions that connect the building 
blocks of stimuli responsive supramolecular polymers are prone to external stimuli, and thus can be 
utilized to create controllable materials. As many stimuli responsive supramolecular polymers are 
created on basis to mimic nature, they often possess characteristics of significant interest from a 
biomedical perspective. 
In this project the synthesis of building blocks for the assembly of an anion responsive carbohydrate-
based supramolecular polymer is explored. The building blocks investigated are based on a 
ferrocene core moiety connected to different amino acids linked to α(1,4)-glucan chains, by one of 
two types of expected anion binding nitrogen-based moieties as shown in figure A. In the planned 
three-step synthesis for the building blocks with an ethylenediamine-based linker, it was discovered 
that a neat synthesis for the direct amide coupling of the ferrocene dimethyl ester with ethylene 
diamine gave rapidly formation of desired product with minimal by-product creation. In the planned 
six step synthesis of the building blocks supposed to utilize a triamine moiety as a linker, five of the 
synthetic step were investigated. It was observed that HATU was the best coupling reagent for the 
formation of the amide between the triamine linker and the ferrocene-peptide core in the fourth 
step.

Objective of the project 
This project fits within a greater project with the overall goal of synthesizing and investigating 
ferrocene-peptide conjugates as possible building blocks, for the creation of anion responsive 
supramolecular polymers in an aqueous environment. The synthesis of the building blocks of 
interest is explored in this project and provides the foundation for eventual creation of stimuli 
responsive supramolecular polymers. 
1. Introduction 
 
1.1 Stimuli responsive polymers 
Polymers are macromolecules composed of smaller, often repeating, monomeric units and have a 
significant impact in the everyday day lives of humans. Many of the materials utilized in our 
everyday living are composed of polymers, and even the polymeric structure of DNA and proteins 
indicates the importance of polymers to humans. As a natural consequence, polymers have gained 
increasing attention through the years, leading to diverse applications in a wide area of 
technologies.1 
Polymers that alter their physical or chemical behaviour in a response to environmental changes, 
are often categorized as stimuli responsive polymers. The stimuli can occur in a variety of different 
forms, e.g., changes in pH2 or temperature3, presence of ions, or biomolecules4, exposure to 
mechanical force5 or magnetic fields6.  
As many of the stimuli responsive polymers that have been synthesized tend to be created on a 
basis to mimic nature, they often find application in the area of medicine, especially that of drug 
delivery.1 
 
1.1.1 Stimuli responsive polymers in drug delivery systems 
When designing biomedically relevant systems for drug delivery based on stimuli responsive 
polymers, there are several important aspects that need to be taken into consideration. As the 
delivery system is administered within the body, the system is required to work in an aqueous 
environment and consist of non-toxic biodegradable components. Furthermore, the drug must 
reach its desired place in the body as stimuli is applied to the system, and the administration of 
==PAGE:9==
 
9 
 
the delivery system has to be affordable.1 Two types of stimuli responsive polymers that have 
been developed for drug delivery or drug loading, are liposomes and hydrogels. 
Liposomes 
The stimuli responsive polymers designed to imitate nature and facilitate drug delivery are often 
referred to as smart polymers. Often these polymers are designed to create vehicles incapsulating 
a drug, that upon external stimulus will be released from the vehicle, thus controlling the drug 
delivery.1 Liposomes are small particles built of amphiphilic monomers forming a lipid bilayer, that 
organizes around an aqueous interior. The hydrophilic parts of the amphiphilic monomers interact 
with each other through non-covalent supramolecular interactions, as well as the hydrophobic parts 
of the amphiphilic chains interacts with each other, thus building up the structure, illustrated in 
figure 1. Liposomes have been studied for the purpose of drug delivery, as water soluble drugs can 
be trapped within the liposomes and realised by certain stimuli. This type of controlled drug delivery 
using stimuli responsive polymers was presented by Robert Blumenthal and co-workers in 1978.7 
They reported the in vitro use of liposomes to release the enclosed antibiotic drug neomycin upon 
heating the liposomes. As the liposomes was heated, disturbance at the edges between the lipid 
and aqueous compartments of the liposomes, allowed increased diffusion of neomycin from the 
liposomes, which inhibits certain protein synthesis by Escherichia coli thereby killing the se 
microbes.7 Following the work of Robert Blumenthal and co-workers, the field of designing stimuli 
responsive polymers for drug delivery have been largely expanded, and a variety of stimuli 
responsive polymers in the area have been reported.1

Hydrogels 
A significant group of stimuli responsive polymers utilized in drug delivery is that of hydrogels. These 
porous water-swelling gels are composed of a polymeric crosslinked network and have proven 
useful in drug delivery technologies. This often works by storing drugs into the pores of the hydrogel, 
which then will be released from the gel depending on the rate of diffusion of the drug. 
Furthermore, hydrogels will often respond to external stimuli by changing the rate of swelling, which 
will affect the rate of drug release. As a result of the character of hydrogels, they have found 
appliance in transdermal drug delivery, especially in combination with microneedles to increase skin 
permability.1 This type of drug delivery system was reported by Colin P. McCoy and co-workers, who

Another example of drug loaded into a hydrogel in purpose of biomedical application, is an anion-
responsive cobalt-based metallopolymer hydrogel reported by Chuanbing Tang and co-workers,

also been develop, as the supramolecular based systems have proven efficient in producing 
controllable materials.11 
1.1.2 Stimuli responsive linear supramolecular polymers 
If the forces that connect the single units of a polymer are of non-covalent character, the polymer 
can be categorized as a supramolecular polymer. The supramolecular interactions binding the 
building blocks of a supramolecular polymer together, can be that of van der Waals interactions, 
electrostatic interactions, hydrogen bonding, hydrophobic effects, metal-ligand coordination and so 
on.12 As these interactions are weaker compared to covalent bonding interactions they are typically 
also reversible. The equilibrium between assembling and disassembling a supramolecular polymer, 
can be affected by external stimuli (figure 4). If a linear supramolecular polymer is to be created, 
there must be specific and directional interactions between the monomers directing the assembling 
of the polymer, so prolongation of the polymer can occur. 
 
Figure 4: Reversible assembling of a linear supramolecular polymer 
If a supramolecular polymer alters its physical or chemical behaviour in response to stimuli, it can 
be defined as a stimuli responsive supramolecular polymer. As supramolecular polymers in many 
instances are created with the purpose of imitating nature, they are often studied in aqueous 
environments. Various stimuli responsive supramolecular polymers, responding to different stimuli 
in aqueous solutions, have been reported.12 An example of this is the formation and degradation of 
an electrochemical responsive supramolecular polymer reported by Yu Liu and co-workers. The 
polymer assembled by electrostatic interactions between a positively charged viologen-based dimer 
guest molecule, and a negatively charged ditopic p-sulfonatocalix[4]arene molecule as host. The 
resulting supramolecular polymer could be disassembled and reversibly controlled by

molecule, from the four-charged state to the neutral state, the electrostatic interactions between 
the two molecules was weakened and the polymer disassembled. Likewise would oxidation from 
the neutral state of the viologen dimer molecule back to the fully charged state, reassemble the 
polymer as strong electrostatic interaction between the two building block again were possible.13 
 
Figure 5: Assembly/disassembly of a redox responsive supramolecular polymer developed by Yu Liu 
and co-workers.13 
He Tian and co-workers developed a photoswitchable supramolecular polymer build of a monomer 
using the viologen molecule to link two terminal coumarin moieties, which would interact with 
gamma cyclodextrin to build up a polymer in an aqueous environment. Cyclodextrins are cyclic 
linked α-D-1,4 glucans which have been shown to create a hydrophobic interior in aqueous 
environments, in which hydrophobic guests can interact. As the coumarin moieties of the monomer 
prefer a non-aqueous environment, and there is a potential entropic advantage of releasing the 
water molecules inside the hydrophobic cavity of gamma cyclodextrin, the monomer and gamma 
cyclodextrin interact by fitting the coumarin moieties in the hydrophobic cavity. As the gamma

1.2 Anion responsive supramolecular polymers and receptors 
Supramolecular polymers that respond to the presence of anions, thus altering their physical or 
chemical behaviour, are defined as anion responsive supramolecular polymers. An example of an 
anion responsive supramolecular polymer developed by Min Xue and co-workers, is a pillar[5]arene-
based polymer created from hydrophobic C-H∙∙∙π and N-H∙∙∙π interactions between the macrocyclic 
components of the pillararene, and an attached alkyl chain containing a dialkylammonium salt. At 
low concentrations in chloroform the aliphatic protons of the alkyl chain were significantly shifted 
upfield in the 1H NMR spectrum, compared to what would be expected from unshielded aliphatic 
protons. This was especially seen for the protons of H8, H9, H10 and H11 (figure 7), suggesting that 
these protons were shielded by the pillararene cavity, indicating folding of the molecule at low 
concentrations. As the concentration were increased, all proton peaks became wider indicating 
polymerization. NOESY spectra confirmed correlation of H15 with H8 and H9 at high concentrations, 
suggesting the structure of the polymer as shown in figure 7. By adding chloride anions, it was 
possible to switch the supramolecular polymer back to the monomer building blocks, shown in 
figure 7. This probably occurs as chloride anions form strong ionic pairs with the positively charged 
amine group, thus disturbing the interaction between the alkyl chain and the pillararene cavity.15 
 
Figure 7: Formation and disassembly of the different conformations of the anion responsive 
pillar[5]arene reported by Min Xue and co-workers.15

Anions are interesting binding targets in several different areas. From a biological perspective, about 
70% of known enzyme substrates possess a negative charge. At physiological pH, the source of free 
energy in biological systems, adenosine triphosphate, contains four negative charges, and 
furthermore is it a requirement for all cells to selective recognize the similar anions of MoO42-, SeO42- 
and SO42-. In a biomedical context, the generic disease of cystic fibrosis is a consequence of failure 
in the regulation system for chloride anions and is to this day still a difficult disease to treat 
efficiently. Environmentally speaking, anions gives rise to pollution problems, both as causes of 
overgrowth of plants and algal in water supplies, and toxic by-products from nuclear power plants. 
As such, anions remains important binding targets.16 
1.2.1 Anion binding 
When binding anions, there are several features that influence to which degree the anion will be 
bound. The core element of an anion is its negative charge, which makes electrostatic interactions 
a common way of binding anions. The cavity in which an anion is bound needs to be sizeable to fit 
the ion, as anions tends to be larger than their positively charged counter ions. In contrast to cations, 
anions will sometimes be protonated at lower pH, thus being in a non-ionic form. Solvation effects 
can also influence anion binding. In a hydrophobic cavity inaccessible to solvent, the less hydrated 
anions will typically be bound with higher affinity. In contrast, more hydrated anions will typically 
be bound with high affinity in cavities prone to polar solvents, as here a solvophobic effect may play 
an important role. Furthermore, anions will prefer to be coordinated in certain geometries, which 
require the structure of the binding site, to place the interacting groups according to the preferred 
structure of the anion being bound.16 
1.2.2 Anion-receptor design 
In nature there exist many anion receptors as part of anion transport systems. In these systems 
there is a requirement for anion receptors to not only have high affinity towards specific anions, but 
also for immediate release of the anions at their target destination. A typical feature in the anion 
receptor binding site in proteins and enzymes, is the use of groups bearing nitrogen atoms to 
coordinate anions. This is underlined by the common presence of the amino acid residue of arginine 
in natural anion binding sites. Arginine contains a guanidine group in its side chain, which is 
particularly efficient in binding anions. The guanidine moiety stays protonated throughout an 
extensive pH range, which makes the arginine residue a reliable source of hydrogens able to partake 
==PAGE:17==
 
17 
 
in hydrogen bonding with anions. Furthermore, the guanidine moiety can partake in hydrogen 
bonding with two anions simultaneously, creating an interaction motif called the arginine fork 
(figure 8).17 
 
Figure 8: The guanidine moiety of arginine and binding of two phosphate anions through the arginine 
fork motif.17 
In proteins, the backbone is made up of amides arising from the polymerization that take place 
when amino acids couple together. As amides are excellent hydrogen bond donors, they are able to 
partake in strong interactions with anions and other partially negatively charged atoms. A common 
motif in many proteins is the nest motif which consists of three amide groups, not participating in 
backbone hydrogen bonding, creating a pocket in which the amide groups have optimal 
circumstances for binding to anions, illustrated in figure 9.17 
 
Figure 9: The nest motif. 
When designing an artificial receptor supposed to have high affinity towards anions in a binding site, 
a variety of different molecular moieties, that have been reported to effectively bind anions can be 
considered. Most of these motifs are originally inspired from Nature’s anion receptors, and the 
trend of have nitrogen atoms in the binding site persist in many synthetic anion receptors.17 
==PAGE:18==
 
18 
 
Examples of a synthetic phosphate receptor based on guanidine18, a synthetic receptor utilizing 
amide groups in its binding site19, and synthetic receptor based on amines are illustrated in figure 
10. The amine-based receptor is able to bind biologically important phosphate anion targets such 
as ATP4- at low pH. 
 
Figure 10: Synthetic receptors utilizing nitrogen-based moieties in the binding site. From left to right: 
A guanidium- based receptor18 binding a phosphate anion, an amide-based receptor19 binding a 
chloride anion, and a amine-based receptor able to bind ATP4- at low pH. 
David K. Smith and co-workers reported the design of synthetically accessible anion receptors with 
a core molecular moiety containing amide and multiple amine groups. The receptors designs were 
inspired by the natural occurring anion receptor spermine. Two of the designed receptors, one cyclic 
and one pseudo cyclic (figure 11), were shown to have high affinity for phosphate anions in an 
aqueous environment even with competing chloride anions present.20 
 
Figure 11: Two receptors with exceptional high affinity for phosphate anions developed by David K. 
Smith and co-workers, and the natural anion receptor spermine.20 
Besides having molecular moieties that can bind anions with high affinity in an anion receptor, it can 
also be beneficial to have structural motifs assisting in pre-organizing the anion receptor. 
==PAGE:19==
 
19 
 
Preorganisation aids the receptors binding groups in being at optimal position ready for anion 
binding and assures, that the enthalpic favoured interactions between anion and binding moieties 
gets magnified. Additionally, preorganisation minimizes the entropic penalty for the receptor to fold 
in a conformation preferable for binding.17 The conformations of the cyclic and pseudocyclic 
receptors shown in figure 11, are quite preorganized, which makes the receptors prepared for anion 
binding contributing to their binding affinity. As such, the preorganisation of anion receptors should 
be taken into consideration in the design. The pseudocyclic receptor has inspired the design of anion 
responsive building blocks for supramolecular polymers described in this thesis. 
1.3 Ferrocene-dipeptide conjugates 
Ferrocene is organometallic cyclopentadienyl complex, consisting of Fe(II) coordinated by two 
cyclopentadienyl ligands. Many ferrocene analogues have been studied in various areas of 
chemistry21, especially have ferrocene-dipeptide conjugates been studied as scaffolds for the 
investigation of protein secondary structure. The distance between the centres of the two 
cyclopentadienyl ligands of ferrocene is approximately 3.3 Å, which is a suitable distance for 
hydrogen bonding between peptides in 1,n’-dipetide conjugates of ferrocene. A peptide attached 
to one of the cyclopentadienyl rings, will be in a suitable position to partake in hydrogen bonding 
with a peptide attached to the other ring, as shown in figure 12.22 
 
Figure 12: Hydrogen bonding in a ferrocene-dipeptide conjugate.23 
The hydrogen bonding in ferrocene-dipeptide conjugates introduces helical chirality, which typically 
will be determined by the absolute configuration of the amino acids adjacent to the ferrocene core 
in the specific molecule. The helical chirality can be defined as either P- or M-helical chirality, 
depending on the direction in which the folding occurs. P-helical chirality can be defined as a left-
handed turn around an axis, whereas a right-handed turn can be defined as M-helical (figure 13). P-
==PAGE:20==
 
20 
 
helical chirality is typically introduced by having L-amino acids adjacent to the ferrocene core 
moiety, likewise is M-helical chirality often a consequence of having D-amino acids as the first 
peptides next to the ferrocene core.22 
 
Figure 13: Illustration of P- and M-helical chirality arising in two 1,n'-dipeptide conjugates of 
ferrocene reported by Toshikazu Hirao and co-workers.22+24 
1.4 Ferrocene carbohydrate conjugates 
Besides peptides, ferrocene analogues have also been synthesized as carbohydrate conjugates. The 
first ferrocene carbohydrate derivative developed, was a β-glucopyranoside of hydroxyethyl 
ferrocene reported in 1961.21+25 Since then, several ferrocene carbohydrate conjugates have been 
reported, amongst them are a number of carbohydrate derivatives of 1,1’-Ferrocenedicarboxylic 
acid reported by Margherita Venturi and co-workers, two is shown in figure 14. The molecules were 
synthesized in good yields, from chlorocarbonyl ferrocene reacted with an amine group attached to 
the respective protected sugars.26 
 
Figure 14: Ferrocene carbohydrate conjugates reported by Margherita Venturi and co-workers.26

Most of the ferrocene carbohydrate conjugates that have been synthesised, consist of short 
carbohydrate chains and utilizes protection group chemistry in their synthesis. The conjugation of 
ferrocene with longer oligosaccharides has not previously been explored.  
1.5 Project Aim 
Stimuli responsive supramolecular polymers are important constructs in many drug delivery 
systems, and their potential to improve therapeutics treatments have granted them a lot of interest 
in recent years. The ability to construct supramolecular polymers from specific and reversible 
interactions, makes them important targets for controlled drug delivery. As supramolecular 
polymers are held together by relatively weak forces, mild external stimuli are often enough to 
cause the polymer to change its physical or chemical properties in a desired direction.11 
α(1,4)-Glucans are linear polymers composed of glucose molecules linked together through α(1,4)- 
glycosidic linkages similar to cyclodextrins. These polymers tend to form helices in aqueous 
environments by orientating their hydrophilic groups on the outside of the helix to interact with 
water molecules, leaving a hydrophobic inside of the helix. It is known that long malto-
oligosaccharides can wrap around and bind or solubilize hydrophobic guests, such as polyTHF,27 
carbon nanotubes,28 molecular wires,29+30 lipids and surfactants,31 in left handed helices, illustrated 
in figure 15. 
==PAGE:22==
 
22 
 
 
Figure 15: Binding of a hydrophobic guest inside a polymer of α(1,4)-glucans creating a polymer-
polymer inclusion complex. 
This project aims at exploring building blocks for eventual creating of anion responsive 
supramolecular polymers in water, that exploit the interactions between carbohydrate side chains 
of a two-armed building block, and hydrophobic chains of a second two-armed building block, 
shown in figure 16. 
 
Figure 16: Illustration of a carbohydrate-based building block interacting with a hydrophobic building 
block creating a self-assembled supramolecular polymer. 
==PAGE:23==
 
23 
 
The carbohydrate-based building blocks will consist of a 1,1’-substituted ferrocene core moiety 
coupled to amino acids which will be linked to a carbohydrate chain by an anion binding amine-
based moiety (figure 17). In the group we have investigated the binding of α(1,4)-glucans to 
surfactants, and found binding interactions as strong as 16.000 M-1 for a twelve unit long α(1,4)-
glucan and an amphiphile with sixteen carbons.32+33 We have also found that the energy binding 
increases linearly with the increasing length of the α(1,4)-glucan. On this basis we hypothesise that 
it could be possible to exploit the interaction between α(1,4)-glucans and alkane moieties to build 
supramolecular polymers.32 
 
Figure 17: Core structure of the carbohydrate-based building blocks. 
The ferrocene core of the building blocks is rotationally flexible, creating the possibility for the 
attached side chains to rotate and partake in the most optimal conformations available for the 
monomer building block. As 1,1’-ferrocene dipeptide conjugates is known to possess specific helical 
chirality, the folding of the building blocks might be affected by the absolute configuration of the 
amino acids that are adjacent to the ferrocene core.22 To investigate this, three different amino 
acids, that of L-valine D-valine and glycine, will be used to create three different types of building 
==PAGE:24==
 
24 
 
blocks. Both ethylenediamine and the triamine moiety shown in figure 18, will be used as amine-
based linkers, to explore anion binding to the building blocks. As certain nitrogen-based groups are 
known to be good at binding anions, and the triamine moiety has been used to build synthetic anion 
receptors with high affinity, these groups are incorporated in order to be able to bind anions.17 Its 
envisaged that anion binding could lead to the disassembly of the polymer, as this would orientate 
the two arms of the carbohydrate-based monomers in the same direction rather than in opposite 
directions, which is required to assemble the polymer. If this change can be induced in the polymer, 
it would make it anion-responsive. 
 
Figure 18: Amine-based linkers intended for use in the synthesis of the carbohydrate-based building 
blocks. 
The α(1,4)-glucan chains that are intended to be coupled to the building blocks via the amine-based 
linkers, will ideally be long α(1,4)-glucan chains, to induce the helical structure these carbohydrate 
chains adopt to in aqueous environment,27 and provide a binding site for the arms of 
complementary hydrophobic monomers. However, as a starting point in this project, a short α(1,4)-
glucan chain of maltotriose will be employed in order to first develop the synthesis.

2. Results & Discussion 
 
2.1 Retrosynthetic analysis 
In a retrosynthetic perspective, it was envisioned that the building blocks utilizing ethylenediamine 
as a linker, could be prepared in a three-step synthesis as shown in figure 18. The bond between the 
ethylenediamine linker and the α(1,4)-glucan chains, was thought to be made by reductive 
amination between the unprotected carbohydrate chains and the free amines. A simple amide 
formation reaction between ethylenediamine and an ester, could connect the linker to the building 
molecule. Finally, disconnecting of the peptide bonds reveals simple staring materials of the methyl 
ester protected amino acids and 1,1’-ferrocenediarboxylic acid, capable of reacting together in a 
peptide coupling reaction. 
 
Figure 18: Retrosynthetic analysis of building blocks using ethylenediamine as a linker.

The ferrocene-peptide conjugates were analysed by 1H NMR. It was noticed that the chemical shift

proton next to the R-moiety with a 4J coupling constant of 1.3 Hz, splitting the triplets further into 
doublets, thus creating a triplet of doublets splitting pattern as illustrated in figure 21.

2.4 Coupling of maltotriose to the ethylenediamine linker 
The coupling of maltotriose to the ethylenediamine linker moiety was thought to happen in an acid 
catalyzed reductive amination, utilizing sodium cyanoborohydride as a reducing agent. In solution 
α(1,4)-glucan chains exist in equilibrium between a cyclic hemiacetal formation, and an open chain 
aldehyde formation (figure 30). In the open chain formation reductive amination can occur, as an 
amine group can react as a nucleophile attacking the electrophilic carbonyl carbon of the aldehyde. 
Sodium cyanoborohydride was chosen as a reducing agent, as it only reduces the imine to the amide 
in the last step in the synthesis, and not the aldehyde to the alcohol, which other reducing agents 
might. A possible mechanism for the reaction is illustrated in figure 30. 
 
Figure 30: Possible reaction mechanism of the reductive amination reaction attaching a α(1,4)-
glucanto the ethylenediamine-linker moiety. 
A reaction was performed using a small amount of Fc-[CO-L-Val-NHCH2CH2NH2]2 as starting material, 
to investigate optimal reaction conditions before a synthetic scale up. The reaction conditions are 
shown in scheme 4.

atmosphere. CH3COOH and a solution of NaBH3CN in MeOH was added and the reaction was stirred 
at r.t. for three days.

The reaction was frequently monitored by LCMS over three days, but no formation of product or 
decrease in starting material were observed. It was speculated that the acidic conditions in the 
reaction might have protonated the terminal amine groups of the ethylenediamine-linker, thus 
making it a less good nucleophile, and unable to react. While mildly acidic conditions generally 
favoured for reductive aminations, in these conditions the primary amine tends to be protonated 
to a significant degree. Conjugation of glycans via reductive amination at their reducing end is 
frequently carried out using anilines or using primary amines with a neighbouring amine, as these 
have a lower pKa. It was hoped that the diamine starting material (3a) might anyway react as 
protonation of an amine group on one of the arms of 3a would decrease the pKa of the amine on 
the other arm of the same molecule. However, this appeared not to be the case, as little or no 
reaction took place.

It was hypothesised that the coupling of maltotriose to the building blocks with the triamine linker 
5 might be more successful, as the triamine linking moiety contains two amine groups capable of 
being protonated. When the tertiary amine of 7a, 7b or 7c protonates it is close to the primary 
amine and so might more effectively reduce the pKa of the primary amine nucleophile. This could 
enhance the possibility of one of the terminal amine group to be unprotonated, and thus able to act 
as a nucleophile in the reductive amination. The following sections, (2.5, 2.6 and 2.7) describe the 
development of the synthetic route towards the alternative building blocks 7, for which the

3. Conclusion & Future Work 
In this project the synthesis of possible building blocks for a carbohydrate-based anion responsive 
supramolecular polymer has been explored. The synthesis of two different sets of building blocks, 
differentiating by the type of anion binding moiety envisioned to connect the core of the building 
blocks to α(1,4)-glucan chains, has been investigated.  
The synthesis of the first set of building blocks 3a, 3b and 3c required the coupling of 
ethylenediamine to the methyl esters Fc-[CO-AA-OMe]2 (2a, 2b and 2c). Improved reaction 
conditions for this synthetic step were developed, by preforming a neat reaction. These molecules 
proved to be difficult to couple to α(1,4)-glucan chains in the following synthetic step, as they are 
relatively poor nucleophiles under acidic conditions.  
The second set of building blocks incorporating a triamine moiety as the linker involves a planned 
six step synthesis. The L-valine analogue of the ferrocene-peptide-triamine Fc-[CO-L-Val-
NHCH2CH2NH(CH3)CH2NH-Boc]2 7a was successfully synthesised, albeit in low yield. In the fourth 
step of the synthesis (the coupling of the triamine 6 to ferrocene peptide conjugate dicarboxylic acid 
4) it was observed that using HATU as coupling reagent along an excess of boc-protected triamine 
starting material gave the most rapid conversion, with the fewest side products. Acid-catalyzed boc-
deprotection to give the diamine ready for reaction with the oligosaccharide appeared to give 
complete conversion, but a means to isolate the pure product still needs to be identified. 
Future work based on information acquired in this project, should focus on optimizing the synthesis 
of the building blocks utilizing the triamine-linker moiety and the coupling of these building blocks 
to α(1,4)-glucans. If successful synthesis of the final building blocks is reached, the building blocks 
should be analysed by CD-spectroscopy for their possible helical chirality. Their affinity towards 
binding anion should also be investigated, this information could be given by UV-spectroscopy, NMR 
titrations or CD-spectroscopy. It should also be explored weather the α(1,4)-glucan arms of the final 
building blocks do in fact bind hydrophobic molecules, and if this change the conformation of the 
molecules. In a longer perspective, complementary building blocks containing hydrophobic side 
chains connected to a ferrocene core should be synthesised as counterparts to the carbohydrate-
based building blocks, with the intention of creating anion responsive supramolecular polymers.

4. Experiments 
 
All solvents used in the synthesis or during any type of chromatography have been of technical 
quality. The solvents have been used in the form they were delivered in by commercial chemical 
suppliers. Reagents and starting compounds have been used in the form they were delivered in by 
commercial chemical suppliers, except for ethylenediamine which was distilled over CaH2 before 
use. Dry solvents were obtained from a Pure Solv MD-7 Solvent Purification system. NMR 
experiments were conducted on a 400MHz Bruker Ascend NMR spectrometer. LCMS analysis was 
performed on a a Waters AQUITY UPLC system utilizing a PDA and SQD electrospray MS detector. 
Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC) was carried out using Merck aluminium sheets covered with silica 
gel C-60. For Dry Column Vacuum Chromatography (DCVC) Merck Geduran silica gel 60 (0.015 nm – 
0.040 nm particle size) was used as stationary phase and for flash chromatography Merck Geduran 
silica gel 60 (40-64 μm particles) was utilized. For column chromatography using alumnia, 
Brockmann 1 aluminium oxide activated, neutral was used.
