Abstract
This thesis provides a quantitative assessment of the extent to which companies are
achieving Absolute Environmental Sustainability Decoupling (AESD), where economic
growth is uncoupled from environmental impact. The study analyzes a large, longitudinal
dataset of self-reported corporate data from the Carbon Disclosure Project (CDP) span-
ning 2013 to 2023. Methodologically, it combines quantitative analysis in Python with the
novel application of a Large Language Model (LLM) for the efficient classification of qual-
itative corporate disclosures. The findings reveal a divergence between aggregate and
individual performance: while sector averages show a clear trend towards absolute de-
coupling, analysis of individual companies identifies more varied outcomes, with distinct
categories of strong, moderate, and limited decoupling performance. The research also
highlights significant practical challenges inherent in using corporate self-reported data,
including structural inconsistencies and human error, underscoring the need for improved
data governance. Ultimately, this work delivers an evidence-based assessment of corpo-
rate decarbonization progress and demonstrates the potential of combining quantitative
indicators with LLM-based analysis for sustainability research.

Introduction
This chapter establishes the foundation for the thesis. It begins by outlining the broad
context of climate change and the rise of corporate sustainability efforts, then narrows the
focus to the significant environmental impact of the built environment sector. The chapter
concludes by presenting the specific objectives and the structure of this study.
1.1
Background: Climate Change and Corporate Action
By the end of the 19th century, scientists began to recognize that the warming of the
globe and rapid climatic shifts are likely driven by human activity. The primary catalyst
was the industrialisation of the West, a period marked by a significant increase in the
burning of fossil fuels (Arrhenius 1896; IPCC 2022; United Nations 2025). More recently,
the substantial growth in manufacturing and economic activity in East Asia, particularly in
China, often referred to as the ”world’s factory”, has become a major contributor to global
emissions (Erbach and Jochheim 2022; Myllyvirta 2024; Evans 2024).
Through the persistent work of scientists and the support of environmentally conscious
funding bodies, this understanding has solidified into a broad consensus, though chal-
lenges in public perception remain (Tohidi et al. 2025). As the scientific evidence mounted,
it became increasingly possible to persuade government officials to establish environmen-
tally focused targets for reducing global emissions, with the Paris Agreement standing as
a landmark example (United Nations 2015). In response to these international efforts and
national directives, individual companies have started to implement their own strategies
to cut emissions, either through regulatory compliance or voluntary initiatives (UNFCCC
Authors 2022; UNFCCC Authors 2024; Pont et al. 2024).
The proliferation of such corporate strategies has led to the creation of extensive datasets
that allow for analysis of these emission reduction efforts. This master’s thesis is based on
one such dataset provided by the Carbon Disclosure Project (CDP), a global non-profit
that runs the world’s largest environmental disclosure system (CDP Worldwide 2025).
The dataset comprises annual surveys completed by companies since 2014, and the re-
sponses illuminate the types of emission reduction goals being set, the methodologies
used, and the progress made toward achieving them. The dataset also includes compre-
hensive company descriptions, enabling vital sector-specific analysis.
Analysing the effectiveness of these governmental policies and corporate decisions is an
inherently complex undertaking (Hinkel and Bisaro 2015; Roberts et al. 2018; O’Brien
2012). It draws on a multitude of research fields, including life cycle assessment (LCA),
game theory, and system modelling, as well as the fundamental sciences of chemistry
and physics. Due to this complexity, and as exemplified by the structure of major global
assessments, research in this area is often segmented by sector or topic to ensure depth
and feasibility (Rawat, Kumar, and Khati 2023; IPCC 2022). This thesis will, therefore,
adopt this focused approach by defining its scope to companies operating within the built
environment sector.
1.2
Focus Area: The Built Environment Sector
The built environment sector is a primary driver of global greenhouse gas (greenhouse
gases (GHG)) emissions, responsible for approximately 37% of the world’s total (United
Nations Environment Programme 2023). This impact is set to intensify due to powerful
2

global urbanization trends; by 2050, it is projected that 68% of the world’s population will
reside in urban areas, necessitating a significant expansion of housing and infrastructure
(UN-Habitat Core Team 2022). Consequently, the manufacturing industries that supply
materials to the construction sector have a critical role to play in sustainability. Their
ability to decouple business growth from embodied carbon emissions is pivotal, making
this sector a high-impact area for research.
Historically, efforts to decarbonize the built environment have focused on reducing oper-
ational carbon. This refers to the emissions generated during a building’s in-use phase,
primarily from energy consumed for heating, cooling, and lighting, which has tradition-
ally accounted for the largest share of a building’s lifecycle emissions (Ibn-Mohammed
et al. 2013). However, as buildings become more energy-efficient and grid electricity
gets cleaner, the relative importance of operational carbon is decreasing. As a result,
embodied carbon—the emissions associated with the manufacturing, transportation, and
installation of building materials—is becoming an increasingly significant portion of the
total lifecycle emissions (World Green Building Council 2019). Addressing these upfront
emissions from the manufacturing sector is therefore a critical and growing priority for the
industry (Bygningsreglementet 2018; ECOS 2024; European Commission 2024).
1.3
Conceptual Framework: From Relative to Positive,
Absolute Decoupling
The climate reports, such as those from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change
(IPCC 2022) and the Global Center on Adaptation (UNFCCC Authors 2024), underscore
that achieving meaningful reductions in GHG emissions is a complex and urgent task.
That requires deep collaboration across governments, industries, and civil society. Within
this broader effort, the role of the private sector is critical, not only as a major source of
emissions, but also as a potential driver of rapid decarbonisation and innovation.
For businesses to navigate this transition effectively, a structured approach is needed to
align corporate strategy with global climate goals. A key concept in this context is envi-
ronmental decoupling, which describes the process of breaking the historical link between
economic growth and its associated environmental pressures, such as GHG emissions.
The pursuit of decoupling provides a pathway for a business to maintain or increase its
economic output while simultaneously reducing its environmental impact. However, not
all forms of decoupling are sufficient to meet the scale of the climate challenge.
To provide a clear and scientifically grounded pathway, the Science-Based Target ini-
tiative (SBTi) offers a framework for companies to set emission reduction targets in line
with the Paris Agreement’s goal of limiting global warming to 1.5°C (SBTi_FAQ_2024). A
Science-Based Target (SBT) defines how much and how quickly a business must reduce
its emissions to be considered aligned with climate science. It transforms the abstract
global goal into a concrete, time-bound objective for an individual company, thereby pro-
viding a benchmark against which its decoupling efforts can be measured.
The relationship between economic growth, emissions, and a SBT gives rise to different
categories of decoupling, which are illustrated conceptually in Figure 1.1. In this figure,
a single, constant trajectory for positive economic growth is represented by the blue line,
while the required emission reduction pathway to meet a specific Science-Based Target
is shown as the red line. These two lines act as key benchmarks, dividing the graph into
four distinct, coloured areas. Each of these areas represents one of the categories of
decoupling. A company’s decoupling status is determined by where its actual emissions
trajectory would lie on this plane.

Figure 1.1: Absolute vs Relative decoupling. Values normalised by base year index.
The categories are defined as follows:
• Negative decoupling: A state in which emissions increase faster than economic
growth. Here, the environmental impact intensifies with economic activity, moving
directly against climate objectives.
• Relative decoupling: An intermediate state where emissions continue to increase,
but at a slower pace than economic growth. While this signals an improvement in
emission intensity, it is insufficient as total emissions are still rising.
• Absolute decoupling: This marks a critical turning point where emissions decrease
in absolute terms while the economy continues to grow. This is the minimum stan-
dard for a company to be contributing to climate change mitigation.
• Positive, Absolute decoupling: This is the most ambitious and desirable state. It
is achieved when absolute decoupling occurs at a rate that meets or exceeds the
company’s SBT. This signifies that the company is not only reducing its emissions
but is doing so fast enough to align with the global 1.5°C trajectory, representing a
true synergy between economic success and environmental stewardship.
• Recessive decoupling: The least desirable state, where both economic output and
emissions decline, but emissions decrease at a faster rate. This typically reflects a
shrinking economy rather than proactive climate mitigation, and is not shown in the
figure as it assumes negative economic growth.
The strategic imperative for businesses is to transition towards positive, absolute decou-
pling. To steer and control this complex transition, companies must employ tools that
translate strategic goals into measurable action and objective feedback. This is the func-
tion of performance indicators: quantifiable measures used to track and assess the status
of a specific process or outcome against a defined objective (Rahdari and Anvary Rostamy
2015). By simplifying complex phenomena into a manageable set of metrics, indicators
provide the evidence base for structured performance management, enabling companies
to understand their current state, set clear targets, and make informed decisions to close
the gap between performance and ambition (Blessing and Chakrabarti 2009).
Within performance management, indicators are often categorized as either lagging or
leading (Pojasek 2009). Lagging indicators measure past outcomes, such as annual GHG
emissions, providing a verdict on historical success but offering little insight for future ac-
tion. In contrast, leading indicators track the drivers and activities that are expected to

influence future results, such as investment in green R&D or the adoption rate of circular
economy practices (Cohen et al. 2012; Mariia Kravchenko 2019). While leading indicators
are essential for proactive, forward-looking strategy, robust lagging indicators are indis-
pensable for accountability and for verifying whether strategic goals have actually been
met.
In the context of corporate climate strategy, a key lagging indicator must therefore as-
sess whether a company’s performance aligns with the reduction trajectory defined by
its SBT. This thesis specifically employs the CAPRO indicator as such an assessment
tool, based on the framework developed by Lux et al. (Lux, Günther, and Kloepffer 2023).
The CAPRO indicator serves to quantitatively evaluate if a company’s change in carbon
productivity meets its science-based target for a given period. It thus provides a clear,
evidence-based verdict on the company’s decoupling success, transforming sustainabil-
ity from a peripheral concern into a measurable component of strategic performance.
While decoupling economic growth from environmental impacts has been widely adopted
as a sustainability strategy, evidence shows this does not necessarily ensure that environ-
mental pressures remain within absolute limits. For instance, analysis of even the most
successful national decoupling efforts demonstrates that the rate of emissions reduction
is insufficient to meet the Paris Agreement’s targets (Le Quéré et al. 2019; Vogel and
Hickel 2023). This gap underscores the need for robust corporate tools to measure and
manage progress toward true sustainability.
The foundation of such corporate environmental management lies in the use of sustain-
ability indicators - quantifiable metrics that allow companies to track performance, inform
strategic decisions, and enhance transparency (Mariia Kravchenko 2019; Rahdari and
Anvary Rostamy 2015). Research highlights the value of leading indicators, which are
designed to proactively drive future performance improvements rather than just report on
past results (Pojasek 2009; Cohen et al. 2012).
However, merely tracking indicators is insufficient; the targets they measure against must
be externally validated and ambitious. This has led to the development of frameworks
like SBTs, which provide a clear methodology for companies to set emission reduction
targets that are aligned with the decarbonization pathways required by climate science
(Bjørn et al. 2022).
While SBTs represent a significant step in aligning corporate goals with a key planetary
boundary, the ultimate objective is to achieve absolute environmental sustainability de-
coupling (AESD). This concept requires a company to ensure its total environmental im-
pact — across multiple domains beyond just carbon—remains within its allocated ”fair
share” of the Earth’s carrying capacity, thereby contextualizing its performance within ab-
solute ecological limits (Haushild 2015; Hjalsted et al. 2020). To this end, this thesis
leverages corporate disclosure data to analyse performance against science-based tar-
gets, offering a method to more clearly visualize a company’s progress towards the goal
of absolute sustainability (Grand 2016; M. Villers et al. 2024).
”Science-based targets give companies a clearly-defined path to reduce greenhouse gas
emissions in line with limiting global warming to 1.5°C. They define how much and how
quickly a business must reduce its emissions to be in line with the Paris Agreement goals.”
”Reducing greenhouse gas emissions in line with climate science is good for the planet
and for businesses. In a recent We Mean Business survey, more than three quarters
(79%) of business leaders agreed that science-based targets (SBTs) keep companies on
track to specific timelines. This finding demonstrates the value of SBTs to businesses

By answering these questions, this thesis provides several key contributions. Method-
ologically, it documents a transparent workflow for processing complex, real-world sus-
tainability data, offering a practical guide for handling large Excel-based datasets in Python.
Empirically, it presents a novel analysis that quantifies how effectively different companies
are able to follow their own stated emission targets. Finally, it makes a practical contribu-
tion by offering insights into the broader experience of working with such data, providing
valuable context for both corporate practitioners and researchers utilizing self-reported
environmental information.
1.4
Thesis Structure
Building on the challenge of tracking corporate progress towards true sustainability, this
thesis analyses the performance of manufacturing companies within the built environ-
ment. It leverages the extensive CDP dataset to examine self-reported environmental
data in conjunction with established SBTs. The primary goal is to apply a method for
analysing and visualizing this complex information, thereby offering a clearer understand-
ing of a company’s trajectory toward AESD.
To ensure a rigorous and structured investigation, this research is guided by the Design
Research Methodology (DRM) (Blessing and Chakrabarti 2009). The DRM framework
organises the thesis into the following distinct stages:
• The initial Research Clarification stage establishes the theoretical foundation. This
includes the comprehensive literature review presented in the preceding sections
and an introduction to the CDP dataset that forms the basis of the analysis.
• Descriptive Study explores the current landscape of corporate climate action. This
involves a detailed examination of the theory behind SBTs and the concept of AESD,
establishing them as the key benchmarks for this study.
• The core contribution of this thesis is presented in the Prescriptive Study. This sec-
tion details the practical methodology developed for extracting relevant indicators
from the dataset, matching them with corporate SBTs, and creating a contextual-
ized visualization of the results.
• Conclusion and Discussion evaluates the outcomes. This concluding part presents
the key insights derived from the data analysis and discusses the effectiveness of
the developed visualization method in providing a more holistic picture of corporate
sustainability performance.

2.1
Design Research Methodology
The master project is structured after the framework defined in the DRM, with four main
stages as presented in Figure 2.1 (Lucienne T. M. Blessing 2009). Each stage has a set
of basic means and main outcomes, which are described in more detail below.
The typical DRM method includes four stages with possible reiterations of stages 2-4
improving the analysis and results form initial to comprehensive (Lucienne T. M. Blessing
2009). In many cases, fulfilling all four of the stages to a comprehensive level is not
feasible; in that case, the DRM still allows for a thesis structure with fewer stages. This
work presented in this thesis in particular is presented according to the three stage model
including Research Clarification (RC), Descriptive Study 1 (DS 1) and Prescriptive Study
(PS).
Figure 2.1: Thesis process flow based on the DRM framework (Lucienne T. M. Blessing
2009).
The first stage is RC, where a preliminary understanding is developed through an ini-
tial literature review. This review establishes a foundation for the research by exploring
key terminology from existing studies on AESD, and the role of indicators in corporate
decision-making towards absolute sustainability. The main outcome of the RC stage is
the project plan with defined activities, milestones, and research goals.
The following stage, DS 1, continues with a more in-depth literature review assessing
how indicators can be used to support strategic decision-making for sustainable business
development. In addition to processing the available dataset to get a clear systematic
overview.
The understanding gathered from the empirical data analysis in DS-1 is fundamental for
the development of the support tool in the following stage, PS. The tool to support strategic
decision making is made by processing the data in Python and employing the CAPRO
indicator.

2.2
Research Questions
The main research question for the thesis is: How to inform companies toward strategic
implementation of AESD through indicators?
Three secondary questions are defined in order to answer this research question.
Q1: How can performance indicators be used to quantify and visualise the alignment of
companies in the built environment’s manufacturing sector with AESD through their
self-reported emission database?
Q2: How to structure and operationalise large-scale self-reported corporate environmen-
tal data from the CDP platform in order to assess AESD?
Q3: What practical insights can be drawn from the process of analysing these datasets
regarding the current state of corporate sustainability reporting and performance
tracking to enable AESD?
2.3
AESD Indicator
This study employs the CAPRO indicator (DCAP,t) to assess Absolute Environmental Sus-
tainability Decoupling (AESD) performance at the company level. The CAPRO indicator
was originally developed by (Lux, Günther, and Kloepffer 2023) in the context of corpo-
rate environmental performance metrics. Its application in this study follows its selection
and further conceptual development in an unpublished article by (Manon Villers et al.
2024), which presented a set of indicators designed to quantify the decoupling of value
added from greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions in alignment with absolute environmental
sustainability principles.
According to (Manon Villers et al. 2024) the CAPRO indicator—along with its bidimen-
sional decomposition and incorporation of an AESD threshold—emerged as one of the
most promising tools for corporate-level decoupling analysis. The article emphasized
that defining such thresholds at the company level should be guided by alignment with
SBTs, thereby enabling an assessment of whether the company’s performance meets
the required decoupling consistent with planetary boundaries. The methodology outlined
in (Manon Villers et al. 2024) also offers a systematic approach to evaluate the ability
of indicators to meaningfully represent AESD in real-company contexts and conceptual
scenarios.
To quantify the degree of decoupling between economic growth and environmental im-
pact, the analysis relies on several key performance metrics. The primary variables are
the absolute emissions in a given year t, denoted as Et (metric tonnes CO2e), and a
measure of economic output, such as Total Revenue, TRt (EURm).

2.3.1
CDP and SBTi dataset
This study uses a complex dataset from the CDP, an international non-profit organisa-
tion that administers a global disclosure system for environmental information (Carbon
Disclosure Project 2024). CDP gathers standardised, self-reported data from companies
through an annual questionnaire that covers quantitative and qualitative aspects such as
greenhouse gas emissions, energy use, climate risks, and sustainability strategies. Par-
ticipation is voluntary, so the coverage of the dataset varies from year to year.
Each dataset is structured in a set of modules, the newest dataset has a total of 13 mod-
ules.
The assessment of AESD focuses on three types of quantitative data. First, direct and
indirect greenhouse gas emissions, covering scope 1, 2, and 3, are used to evaluate en-
vironmental impact. Second, economic growth is assessed using either disclosed value
added or estimates derived from production intensity data. Finally, the analysis includes
data on SBT, which provides insights into company-level climate targets aligned with
science-based thresholds.

3.1
Research Question
The main goal of DS 1 is to obtain an understanding of the research context through
empirical data analysis. At the core of this study is the first and second research question:
Q1: How can performance indicators be used to quantify and visualise the alignment of
companies in the built environment’s manufacturing sector with AESD through their
self-reported emission database?
Q2: How to structure and operationalise large-scale self-reported corporate environmen-
tal data from the CDP platform in order to assess AESD?
To answer these question, the study applies two complementary methods. A structured
literature review is used to identify key theoretical perspectives, frameworks, and debates
surrounding sustainability indicators. In parallel, a complex dataset is processed to ex-
tract empirical patterns and practical insights (regarding the structure? ie. not neces-
sarily the content.). Together, these methods provide a grounded understanding of both
the conceptual and operational dimensions of indicator use, forming a foundation for the
subsequent research stages.
3.2
Method
3.2.1
Structured Literature Review
To identify relevant theoretical perspectives and existing approaches to sustainability indi-
cators in a corporate context, a structured literature review is conducted. The review uses
the Scopus database and focuses on publications that address sustainability-related met-
rics with a forward-looking or prescriptive orientation, particularly in relation to business
performance.
The development of the search strategy is informed by a set of control papers that illus-
trate how leading indicators are applied in sustainability and business contexts. Mariia
Kravchenko (2019) present a framework for ex-ante sustainability screening of circular
economy initiatives in manufacturing firms. Pojasek (2009) discusses the integration of
leading indicators to drive sustainability performance at the corporate level, while Cohen
et al. (2012) examines the reporting of non-financial leading indicators linked to economic
and sustainability outcomes. These papers provide early thematic anchors and help clar-
ify the conceptual boundaries of interest in this study.

3.2.3
Literature Review Insights
This section presents the results of the literature review, focusing on how sustainability
indicators are developed and applied to support strategic decision-making in corporate
and industrial contexts. The review draws on 15 selected publications that meet criteria
for relevance to sustainability, indicator-based assessment, strategic or decision-support
functions, and business or manufacturing context. Together, these sources offer insights
into how indicators can be used as tools to support performance management systems
and strategic sustainability integration within companies.
The central purpose of this literature review is to understand the conceptual, methodolog-
ical, and practical roles that indicator-based tools play in enabling organizations to as-
sess, guide, and improve their sustainability performance. In particular, the review seeks
to identify how existing approaches can inform the development of an indicator-driven
tool—based on the CAPRO decoupling indicator, for aligning company-level action with
the principles of AESD.
The reviewed literature reveals a growing emphasis on the use of metrics and indicators
in business sustainability reporting and strategy. However, it also highlights key limita-
tions in how indicators are typically designed and used: many focus on relative improve-
ments, lack connection to absolute environmental thresholds, or are not well integrated
into strategic decision-making processes. These gaps suggest a need for more effective
tools that can bridge sustainability assessment with corporate planning and transforma-
tion.
The results are structured into five thematic areas that emerged from the literature: (1)
conceptual foundations of sustainability and indicator use, (2) roles of indicators in strate-
gic decision-making, (3) methodological frameworks for indicator development, (4) appli-
cations in manufacturing and material sectors, and (5) challenges related to corporate
data and implementation. These themes serve to ground the subsequent development
of an AESD-oriented tool for companies in the built environment sector.

Method: Data Processing and Analysis
This section details the computational methodology, tools, and techniques used to pro-
cess, analyze, and visualize the CDP data set. It outlines the rationale for the choice of
programming language, the specific libraries used for each stage of the data pipeline, and
the approaches taken for data cleaning and classification.
4.2.1
Choice of Tools: The Python Scientific Ecosystem
The primary tool chosen for all data processing and analysis in this thesis is the Python
programming language. Originally designed with an emphasis on code readability and
clear syntax, Python has become one of the world’s most popular programming lan-
guages, consistently ranking at the top of industry indexes (TIOBE Software 2025). Its
accessibility has made it a dominant force in data science, scientific computing, and fi-
nance (McKinney 2017). For this thesis, a programming-based approach was essential,
as the data is distributed across several large files, making cross-file data retrieval and
processing prohibitively complex to perform manually in spreadsheet software like Excel.
Although the core Python language is interpreted and may exhibit slower performance
compared to compiled languages, its strength in data analysis stems from an extensive
ecosystem of specialised libraries. This ecosystem includes industry-standard tools like
the Pandas library, which provides high-performance data structures, and its foundational
NumPy library, which ensures execution speed by implementing functions in lower-level
languages like C (McKinney 2017; Harris et al. 2020). This flexibility—providing access to
both general-purpose tools and specialized high-speed alternatives—makes Python the
ideal choice for the varied data processing tasks required in this research. The different
packages and their roles are discussed in the following sections.
4.2.2
Data Loading
A key advantage of a programmatic approach is the ability to automate the loading and
consolidation of data from multiple files. Python’s ecosystem offers numerous packages
for this purpose, with Pandas being the most common. Pandas can intelligently load var-
ious file formats into a DataFrame object (McKinney 2017). The DataFrame is a highly
versatile in-memory data structure that provides a rich set of functions for data manipula-
tion, statistical analysis, and visualization.
However, the flexibility of Pandas comes at a performance cost. Its default Excel parsing
engine, openpyxl, builds a full in-memory representation of the file’s structure, which can
be slow and memory-intensive for very large files (openpyxl 2024). For this thesis, when

Linear Regression and Forecasting
Historical data can be used in combination with mathematical models to identify the under-
lying trends and make forecasts regarding future development. In this context, a model
provides a simplified description of a real-world process, separating a consistent pattern,
or ’signal’, from random, unpredictable variations, or ’noise’. Linear regression is a com-
mon and powerful technique for this purpose, where a mathematical function is fitted to a
set of observed data points.
In single variable, linear regression, the complexity of the model is governed by the order
of the polynomial, p, chosen to represent the trend. A model of order p describes the
predicted output, ˆy, with the equation:

4.2.5
The Sigmoid Curve
In this work, the social system dynamics theory presented in ’Diffusion of Innovations’ by
Everett Rogers (Rogers 2003) is used to define the expected trajectory a company will
follow when trying to achieve a company-wide goal, for example, reduction of emissions.
The assumption is that a large company behaves similarly to a social system, where the
propagation of ideas follows similar pathways. The theory related to the change dynamics
states that the adoption of a new idea or practice within a population follows a normal
distribution (a bell curve) (Rogers 2003, Fig. 7.3). More specifically, this means that
large-scale strategic transformations within a company rarely proceed at a constant, linear
pace. A more realistic and effective planning tool is the sigmoid curve (or S-curve), which
is addition to (Rogers 2003) also supported by SBT community (Bjørn et al. 2022). The
proposed S-curve models a project’s cumulative progress through three distinct phases
of the normal distribution bell curve: an initial period of slow growth as foundational work,
learning, and initial trials are conducted; a second phase of rapid acceleration where the
initiative is scaled across the organisation and the bulk of the progress is made; and a
final maturation phase where the rate of change tapers off as the project approaches its
final goal. The upper sub-figure in section 4.2.5 illustrates such a strategic plan. The S-
curve is the equivalent of the cumulative density function of the normal distribution curve
in the lower sub-figure, i.e. the integral of section 4.2.5. This derivative curve shows that
the required pace of change—and therefore the intensity of investment and activity—is
low at the start, rises to a peak in the middle of the project’s timeline, and declines as
the objectives are met. In section 4.3 these can referred to as Low Intensity and High
Intensity periods.
The specific split into high and low intensity regions is defined based on the standard de-
viations of the normal distribution curve. According to the approximation in (Rogers 2003,
Fig. 7.3) one standard deviation from the peak covers 34% of the progress towards the
goal, and by splitting the total duration into four equal length parts, the following percent
wise progress along the S-curve is achieved: 16% - 34% - 34% - 16%. The intensity, or
rate of change at these crossing points, depends on the normal distribution curve, nor-
malized by the timeline. The example in section 4.2.5 shows that the change should be
about 1%-5% and 5%-8% in the low and high intensity periods, respectively.

4.2.6
Advanced Data Analysis: Text Classification
A primary challenge within the CDP dataset is the systematic classification of unstructured
text, such as for example corporate descriptions of climate initiatives. Traditional natural
language processing (NLP) approaches to this task include statistical methods like term
frequency-inverse document frequency (TF-IDF), which evaluate the relevance of words
within documents (Ramos et al. 2003). For handling minor variations in phrasing or typo-
graphical errors, string similarity algorithms, such as those provided by the FuzzyWuzzy
library, are also effective tools (SeatGeek 2024). However, these methods primarily op-
erate on lexical similarity and often struggle to interpret deeper semantic contexts.
The advent of LLMs, based on the transformer architecture, represents a paradigm shift
in this domain (Vaswani et al. 2017). Unlike the aforementioned techniques, LLMs are ca-
pable of understanding the context and nuance of language, making them highly effective
for sophisticated classification tasks.
While powerful, leveraging the state-of-the-art LLMs typically requires sending data to
third-party cloud services via an application programming interface (API). This practice
introduces significant data governance and privacy risks, particularly when handling po-
tentially sensitive corporate information. Submitting data externally creates uncertainty
Green Growth Gap
21
==PAGE:29==
regarding its storage, access, and potential use in training future models, a risk often
unacceptable in a corporate context (Isik, Joshi, and Goutas 2024).
To mitigate these risks while still harnessing the analytical power of LLM technology, this
thesis adopted a local deployment strategy. This approach guarantees that all data re-
mains within a secure and familiar environment (the personal computer). The Ollama
framework was utilized to run Meta’s open-source Llama 3 model on a local machine,
a choice that deliberately prioritizes data security and methodological control over the
higher performance of cloud-based systems (Ollama 2025).
The classification itself was implemented using a prompt-based, few-shot learning tech-
nique. This method involves providing the LLM with a few examples of correctly classified
text within the prompt itself. This guidance helps the model produce more accurate and
consistent outputs for new, unseen data, a technique shown to be highly effective for mod-

Trelleborg AB began the period with negative decoupling, as emissions increased faster

These actions have steered Trelleborg toward a consistent decoupling trajectory aligned

Three companies – Sandvik AB, Beijer Ref AB, and Skanska AB – demonstrate limited or
recessive decoupling performance, as shown in Figure 4.9. Both Sandvik and Skanska

shown little or no growth over the same period, indicating recessive decoupling or a weak
relative decoupling. These reductions are primarily linked to operational shifts, project
portfolio changes, and increased procurement of renewable electricity. Beijer Ref, on the
other hand, experienced nearly a 50% increase in revenue alongside a rise in emissions of
comparable magnitude—partly due to acquisitions in regions with more carbon-intensive
energy profiles. Despite some improvements through energy efficiency and renewable
energy investments, the overall emission trends for these companies remain inconsistent
with the pathway required to meet science-based targets.
Four companies are classified as having anomalous or inadequate data, as shown in Fig-
ure 4.10. The first is Hempel, which lacks sufficient data for a complete assessment. The
company disclosed total revenue for only one year; however, based on estimates from
its annual report, total revenue appears to have increased by approximately 75% since
2013. This suggests that Hempel may be on track to achieve absolute decoupling, as in-
dicated by the trend in location-based Scope 2 emissions in Figure 4.10a, which displays
both location-based and market-based values. If the assessment were based solely on
market-based Scope 2 emissions, Hempel might even qualify for positive absolute de-
coupling, where emissions decrease at a rate exceeding the SBT. Nevertheless, due to
the limited and inconsistent data, no definitive conclusion can be drawn.
The remaining three companies—Billerud AB, Danfoss A/S, and YIT Oyj—exhibited emis-
sion trajectories with sharp anomalies that significantly diverge from their prior trends.
These irregularities may stem from data inconsistencies or substantial structural changes
within the companies.
In 2022, Billerud AB recorded a striking increase in emissions, reaching approximately
300% of the base year value. This surge is directly linked to the acquisition of three
production mills in North America, where reliance on fossil fuels such as oil and gas is
substantially higher than in the company’s European operations. According to Billerud,

In the same year, Danfoss A/S saw emissions rise from around 10% to 50% of the base

Corporation, which significantly altered the company’s structure and emissions reporting

inconsistencies that rendered “the figures of 2017 and 2018... not comparable and the
direction of change not measurable” (Carbon Disclosure Project 2024). Furthermore, YIT
stated that it was “unable to recalculate 2018 in accordance with the new methodology”
adopted in 2019 (Carbon Disclosure Project 2024). Subsequent structural changes—
including the sale of Nordic paving and mineral aggregates businesses, the closure of
one office in Russia, and the downsizing of emission-intensive operations such as paving
in Lithuania and Norway—further disrupted data consistency. These reductions reflect
operational changes rather than production-driven efficiency gains, making the emissions
data from 2017 to 2020 unsuitable for reliable decoupling analysis.

4.3.2
Sector Average Analysis
In the previous section, the economic and emission data of the individual companies have
been analysed. In this section, the sector-average data are presented, focusing on historic
and forecasting trends for the individual scope emissions and the total revenue figures.
As discussed in section 3.2.2, the reported data is sparse, with many missing points.
Direct computation of the averaged values based on sparse data can lead to misleading
findings. For example, if a company reports revenue data for only two years, the computed
mean for those two years will look like sudden growth. In theory, the most correct way to
deal with the sparse data is to define the largest subset for where the data is available from
all companies; however, in reality, this is often unrealistic as the results data sets become
too small to be useful. Instead, the second-best option is to leverage the information in the
sparse datasets to build prediction models that can help fill out the missing data points.
In this case, a first-order polynomial model was selected to fit the data. As discussed in
section 4.2.3 the model complexity can have an influence on the forecasting accuracy of
the model; the results are therefore verified visually before the averaging calculations are
performed.

missing points. Each model is presented with different colors, and the colors are retained
across the sub-figures.

5
Conclusion and Discussion
The thesis presents the analysis of a large survey of self-reported company data about
revenue, emissions, and emissions targets, among other information. The dataset in-
cludes annual surveys from 2013 to 2023, providing enough data to do meaningful analy-
sis of the state of the emission reduction goals and activities of the surveyed companies.
The data has been analysed with the goal of assessing the degree of achieved Abso-
lute Environmental Sustainability Decoupling, meaning how well companies are able to
achieve the desired reduction in emission without reducing the revenue. The sector av-
erage results show that companies are indeed able to achieve absolute decoupling. On
the individual level however, the results are more varying, with four identified categories.
including strong, moderate, and limited decoupling.
The CDP dataset which has been the backbone of this thesis has presented several chal-
lenges including (1) extraction of the data across several Excel files with varying structure,
such as column names, numbering and use of the Excel sheets (2) data reporting struc-
ture that includes highly similar questions, which makes querying the data tedious (3)
human errors in the input, especially pertaining to the unit of the reported values, some
type of safeguard system is highly recommended to ensure that analysis on the reported
data isn’t ruined due to inconsistent use of units.
The use of the lama 3.2 LLM has been shown to work well with this type of data, enabling
classification of companies based on the long self descriptions, which if done manually
would have taken a week. However there have been limitations due to the limited PC
hardware of the author. Upon further use of LLMs to process this type of data access to
a GPU would be recommended.

You are an expert in business and industry classification.

produces materials for the built environment** (building
materials , insulation , steel, cement, HVAC systems , etc.).
3
4
A company should be classified as "BuiltEnvironment" if:
5
- It **manufactures or produces materials** used in construction
or building (e.g., building materials , insulation , steel,
cement , HVAC systems).

7

environment , classify it as "Other".

reinforcing materials , structural strengthening systems ,
