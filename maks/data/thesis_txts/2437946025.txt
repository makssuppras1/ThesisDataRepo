Summary
The monitoring of scalar variables in aquatic environments is a task required by multiple
applications, as well as laborious and, in many cases, dangerous to be carried out by
human operators. A solution to this issue is the incorporation of technologies, usually
unmanned vehicles, in the development of sampling missions, which calls for the energy
that allows the operation of the involved devices, and, thus, limits the amount of data
that can be obtained for processing. In contrast, the amount of information must be
high enough for the reconstruction of scalar fields to be reliable. This thesis designs and
develops an adaptive sampling strategy, by means of an underwater vehicle, responsible
for reaching a trade-off between both restrictions: the energy available for the vehicles
and the precision of the estimation. In addition, for the purpose of satisfactorily tracking
the trajectories of the vehicle, a suitable control strategy is also designed and developed:
Model Predictive Control.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The maps of scalar fields, widely used in research areas, are obtained from the sampling
of the scalar variables and the estimation of those points of the map that have not been
sampled, so that a continuous field is generated. Adaptive Sampling is a technique which
aims to reconstruct a continuous scalar field in an area of interest, through the discrete
data acquisition and, generally, the interpolation of those under certain conditions or
constraints.
This technique requires sampling strategies capable of changing according to certain
variables being sampled, and, thus, the name of adaptive. In this way, despite having
scenarios or distributions of unknown fields, the estimation becomes more precise, in
order to obtain a scalar field with the highest precision (the lowest estimation error)
possible.
Mobile robots are used in a wide range of applications to deal with unstructured
environments, and develop certain tasks, such as rescue, surveillance or data analysis,
among others, where there are factors and elements whose movement can not be con-
trolled. These robots can be used to carry out adaptive sampling, so that the estimation
is performed with higher efficiency and autonomy.
Within the environments in which mobile robots could develop this environmental
sampling (land, air or water), in recent years numerous studies have been implemented
in the aquatic sector and it is in this area where this project focuses on.
Autonomous Underwater Vehicles (AUVs) are used in this sector for environmental
sampling.
These robots are able to follow predefined trajectories, without the need
of human interaction, through navigation and control. However, these vehicles have
limitations in terms of available energy, which must be taken into account when preparing
missions for it.
This project aims to solve the problem associated with adaptive sampling of variables
of interest in aquatic environments, through the use of autonomous underwater vehicles,
and taking into account the aforementioned limitation in terms of their available energy.
In the next sections of this chapter, the motivation and the problem which this project
focuses on, as well as its objectives, will be described. The structure of the memory and
the contributions that have been made during the development of this thesis will also
be shown in this chapter.

1 Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The reasons why maritime sampling is necessary are varied and do not only affect sci-
entific aspects. As an example of this, there are fisheries advisory services that carry
out an analysis of the meteorological conditions and the fishing oceanography to provide
strategic fishing forecasts, in order to increase the efficiency and the benefit in fishing
operations [1].
Another aspect in which the monitoring of variables is involved is in the analysis of
the pollution of oceans and rivers which could be exposed to waste from nearby industries
or ships that contain polluting materials (such as oil or petroleum). These materials can
be harmful to the health of both humans who may be affected by these bodies of water,
and the flora and fauna that is in the vicinity of the contaminated area [2].
Marine scientific studies are also a common example in which it is required to obtain
certain scalar fields, such as temperature or pressure. Some studies, especially those
related to marine biology, seek the thermocline, which is a thermal layer in aquatic
environments in which the variation of temperature with depth is greater than in the
upper and lower layers, due to the greater blooming of microorganisms in this area [3].
The thermocline is also related to the stratification of water, which affects underwater
acoustics and, therefore, submarine communications. Therefore, the identification of
this layer can also have military purposes [4]. Other scientific analysis are carried out
to determine changes in the landscape related to certain marine characteristics, such as
the height of the tide, which could have an economic impact on coastal areas, by eroding
and damaging the structure of these, so its monitoring could help create systems that
mitigate this impact [5].
Despite the wide range of situations in which maritime sampling has been used, this
task is complex and hazardous to be performed by human beings. Marine sampling
operated by humans is also expensive, due to both the preparation required to achieve a
certain precision, and the hardware necessary for the data acquisition and the equipment
of the operators, which must withstand the working conditions (hostile temperatures
and pressures, corrosive environments) [6]. The economic disadvantage, as well as the
meteorological conditions, mean that the amount of missions executed by operators is
limited and, thus, the frequency of monitoring and the possibility of improving the
estimates of a certain scalar field by adaptive sampling.
Under the circumstances described above, the use of autonomous vehicles in sampling
applications is very widespread. These vehicles are capable of controlling the trajectory
and of taking measurements by means of in-situ sensors. Although these robots rep-
resent an increase in speed, repeatability, efficiency and accuracy, it is also necessary
to consider their limitations, such as the energy available for propulsion and sampling,
among others. That is why environmental sampling through the use of these robots
becomes an optimization problem, in which a trade-off must be reached between the
energy available to develop the mission, and the estimation error of the scalar field that
has to be rebuild.

1.2 Project Description and Objectives
3
This thesis deals with the aforementioned optimization problem, taking place within
the context of the REMORA project (REconfigurable MOdular Robotic system for
Aquatic environment) [7] and in collaboration with DTU-Aqua, which performs scien-
tific studies which require the observation of variables in aquatic environments.
1.2
Project Description and Objectives
As indicated above, this project aims to solve the optimization problem associated with
adaptive sampling of maritime environments, reaching a compromise between the accu-
racy of the estimation of the scalar field and the energy used for navigation and data
acquisition, which is essential to build such an estimate.
The scope of the thesis described in this document includes the definition and devel-
opment of the adaptive sampling strategy that allows to solve the described problem,
as well as the design and implementation of a control strategy that allows the robot to
follow the trajectories provided by the sampling strategy.
The objectives pursued by this project are described as follows:
• Proposition and development of sampling strategies and observation of how they
affect to the accuracy of the scalar field of interest.
• Design of a control strategy capable of tracking trajectories with the highest pre-
cision possible.
• Simulation of the sampling and control strategies and evaluation of performance
in terms of accuracy and robustness.
• Implementation of the proposed strategies in the BlueROV platform, through ROS
environment.
• Validation of the results.

in the previous section (Section 1.2) requires firstly conducting a literary survey, so that
the analysis of the advantages and disadvantages of the existing methods will support
the elaboration of this thesis. This section consists in a review of the existing studies in
both fields, the adaptive sampling strategies and the control ones.

1 Introduction
1.3.1
Adaptive Sampling Strategies and Path
Generation
Adaptive sampling is an active research area, where the sampling strategies used in the
existing studies can be classified, based on the data processing and decision making
accordingly, into offline and online techniques. The offline methods are those in which
the measurements are processed after performing the mission, when new trajectories that
have to be followed must be generated until reaching the predefined objective. Online
techniques, on the other hand, carry out the processing and decision making while the
vehicle is executing the mission.
Offline Strategies
Offline strategies are based on predefined trajectories in which the vehicle has to perform
the sampling tasks, and the following decision making after the evaluation of the results.
In the existing literature, the most used patterns to develop the adaptive sampling
technique are lawnmower and spiral ones.
The analysis carried out in [8], evaluates those patterns (spiral and lawnmower pat-
terns, as illustrated in Figure 1.1), in terms of both estimation error (calculated through
a method known as Kriging [9]) and the energy used by an AUV, of different predefined
trajectories. After analysing the results obtained in this evaluation the following conclu-
sion is achieved: it is preferable to use the systematic spiral for low variance fields with
low sampling density or high variance, while the random spiral is more adequate for
high sampling density with low variance fields. This study, however, does not analyse
the possibility to find an optimal trajectory whose pattern is different from the spiral or
lawnmower ones.
As an example of studies which uses those mentioned patterns, the project described
in [10], based on learning algorithms and lawnmower patterns, the underwater vehicle
learns from the previous mission and changes the distance between the parallel lines
of the pattern, so that the reconstruction error is minimised, but without taking into
account the energy used by the vehicle to execute the missions.
Learning algorithms are also used in [11], where, through the performing of the
method Self-Organizing Map Based Adaptive Sampling (SOMBAS), in each iteration,
new candidates are generated from the training of the map and by employing certain
objective function.
Instead of focusing on specific patterns, other researches use trajectory optimization
algorithms, such as the one performed in [12], where the Centroid-tracking algorithm is
employed to optimize, in terms of energy, the trajectory between predefined way-points,
but without taking into account the estimation error.
Another example of the projects focusing on trajectory optimization is described
in [13].

CHAPTER 2
Adaptive Sampling
Strategy
Doing the sampling task efficiently requires to take into account the error of the estimated
and the energy needed, which results in the optimization problem which this project aims
to solve.
This chapter contains the the design of a sampling technique capable of optimizing
the accuracy of the estimate and the energy required for the robot to perform the task.
2.1
Overview of the Sampling Strategy
The general sampling strategy diagram is shown in the Figure 2.1, where different blocks
are interconnected, representing the loop-process which this thesis is implementing, de-
scribed in the following steps:
1. An algorithm called Harmony Search (HS) will be executed. This algorithm, given
n random points (way-points), will build, by means of an iterative method, dif-
ferent ways of join them, evaluate, through a certain objective function, different
trajectories that can be followed to pass by all of them, and return the optimal
one.
2. The trajectory selected by the HS Algorithm is optimized in terms of another given
objective function.
3. Once this path has been optimized, the sampling experiments and estimation,
carried out with a method called Kriging, take place.
4. After obtaining the continuous field, the loop starts again by evaluating through
the HS algorithm the n points having the maximum variance of the estimation
error (considering that they should not be very close to each other).
5. The steps 1-4 are repeated until the maximum estimation error variance of the
estimation is lower than a certain threshold.

Finding the global minimum may be challenging and involve high computational cost.
However, the complexity of the problem is reduced if the task becomes the search for a

Selection of the spline order
The spline of cubic order, as indicated, has 2 × (N −1) fewer coefficients to select
(variables to solve in the optimization problem) than the quartic spline, so its compu-
tation should be simpler. The reason why the optimization problem is developed in
the beginning with both orders of spline, is because the quartic one is considered more
suitable to obtain an acceleration function, and, thus, force or torque, of class C1, which
allows a feasible implementation in the physical prototype.
The implementation of both spline-based optimization is carried out in Matlab, by
using the function fmincon, which allows the inclusion of non-linear objective functions
and constraints, and requires an initial point, that is, an initial set of spline coefficients
from which the search for the optimal solution starts.
An evaluation is developed which aims to conclude with the order of the most ade-
quate spline for trading-off between computing time and satisfactory results. The initial
points (initial coefficients) are calculated by using the functions spline (cubic) and spapi
(quartic). In the Figure 2.5, the optimal solution of a problem through a cubic spline
path is represented as an example (wd = 0.1, wϵ = 0.9, ϵa = 0.6, obtaining a final
ϵ = 0.55). On the other hand, in the Figure 2.6, the results by using Quartic Spline-
based optimization can be observed (wd = 0.4, wϵ = 0.6, ϵa = 0.6, reaching a final
ϵ = 0.35).
These simulations conclude with minimal difference in computing time and feasibility
of solutions between both orders of splines. Therefore, the selected one is the Quartic
spline, in order to guarantee smoothness in the curves of forces to be used as inputs of
the system.

Curvature-based optimization
Despite improving the results by incorporating an initial spline-search, when the
radius restriction is not satisfied in this, the computation time increases highly and, in
many occasions, a feasible solution is not found. Besides, if the radius restriction is
included in the initial spline-search, no feasible solution can be found.
For the purpose of solving this situation, an extra function to be optimized is added
in the initial spline-search: it has to maximize the minimum radius, as long as there are
points where the radius of curvature is smaller than allowed.
After this new approach, the percentage of feasible solutions increases to around 90%.
However, the computing time increases (20-25 minutes for 25 points), and most of the
time is due to the optimization of distance and deviation. Besides, the initial spline and
the optimal one do not vary highly, the optimization method is simplified by following
the following strategy:
1. Execute the HS Algorithm to find paths only maximizing the minimum radius
of curvature.
2. When the best path is found, optimize the selected one, minimizing distance
and deviation as in (2.15).
With this new approach, a feasible solution of 64 points can be found. In the Fig-

required to follow this trajectory are found in the Figure 2.12. The maximum allowable
deviation ϵa is 3, resulting in a final error of 1.3.

The HS algorithm implemented in the Sampling Strategy and described in Chapter 2
defines the generation of the trajectories the robot is commanded to follow to minimize
the energy applied in the mission.
It is essential, though, to take into account the
estimation of the scalar fields of interest, for the purpose of increasing the precision of
the reconstruction.
The estimation technique is approached through a method known as Kriging, whose
design and implementation will be exposed in this chapter.

CHAPTER 4
Control Strategy
The Sampling Strategy described in Chapter 2 defines the generation of the trajectories
where the robot is commanded to take the measurements. It is a requirement, thus,
these trajectories are properly tracked.
For this purpose, it is essential to devise a
control strategy in charge of allowing the vehicle to reach the desired states so that
the computed (estimated) energy of the trajectory to follow (through the energy model
described in (2.4)) is close to the real one.
This chapter collects the design and development of the control strategy implemented

4.2
Kalman Filter
The estimates needed by the MPC are computed through an Extended Kalman Filter,
which uses some measurements given by, at the same time, the estimation of the pose
and the velocity by means of a second Kalman Filter which has as an input the position
of the robot obtained by computer vision techniques applied to the measurements of a
camera.
The position of the robot is measured by a vision-based system. The ligth of four
IR-LEDs placed at a known position of the robot [30] is detected by a camera through
a passive IR-filter and this information is processed by computer vision techniques, so
the position is calculated. Then, this information is used as an input of a Kinematical
Kalman Filter (KKF) to estimate the velocities in {n} (ˆvx and ˆvy). With the velocities
and the measures given by the IMU, the heading ( ˆψ) can be estimated through a Com-
plementary Filter (CF). The Figure 4.4 shows this architecture. This architecture was
implemented by [20].

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
This thesis has developed an approach to the reconstruction of scalar fields of interest
taking into account constraints in terms of estimation precision and available energy.
The results achieved have been tested through simulations and analysed in previous
chapters.
In this chapter, the limitations of the method implemented will be discussed.
6.1
Sampling Strategy
6.1.1
Sequence Generator
The generation of the sequence of candidate trajectories (see Chapter 2) uses an algo-
rithm whose complexity increases with the number of points to evaluate (O(n222)). For
this reason, the computation of maps divided into a high amount of cells may require
long computation times.
6.1.2
Optimization Algorithms
The algorithms implemented for the optimization of the trajectories (see Chapter 2) are
highly dependent on the initial point and on the number of variables to have into account.
Therefore, the computation time may be affected by the increment of way-points used
to perform the interpolation of the spline.
6.2
Estimation Strategy
The small dimensions of the pool used to test the results implies that the measured fields
of pressure and temperature barely vary in the plane sampled. Therefore, the operation
of the developed approach can only be proved through the simulations provided in
Chapter 3.

7.1
Conclusions
Theoretical knowledge has been applied to design a sampling strategy responsible for
reconstructing a scalar field, by reaching a trade-off between the energy required to
perform the task and the estimation error of the reconstruction.
The approach is based on the kinematic and dynamic model developed by [20], and
focused in the generation through the Harmony Search algorithm and optimization of
spline-based trajectories. The HS algorithm generates the sequence to cover an area by
visiting some points, random points in the beginning, and evaluates the energy required
to track the path which joins them.
This path is developed by optimizing a spline
trajectory which interpolate the points. After the evaluation of a certain number of
generated paths, the HS algorithm returns the optimal one.
The implementation of the sampling technique revealed that quartic order is more
suitable for the purposes of smooth curves in the acceleration, and, thus, realizable forces
and accelerations for the motors of the physical prototype.
The computation time and rate of feasibility in the solutions of the formulated prob-
lem implied the setting-up of a previous optimization problem with the aim of maximiz-
ing the minimum radius providing that points with the radius lower than the allowed one
exist. After selecting the optimal path between the evaluated ones by the HS algorithm,
a second optimization is performed to this path. This optimization holds, as objective
function to minimize, the distance of the trajectory and the position error between the
path and the way-points used to build it.
When obtaining the trajectory to follow, the sampling experiments are carried out
and the reconstruction of the temperature and pressure fields is performed through a
technique known as kriging. The estimation error is evaluated and the HS algorithm
starts again with the points where the estimation error variance is higher. The strategy
is repeated until reach a certain threshold in the variance of the estimation error.
The control architecture of a MPC has been designed and implemented to ensure the
accurate tracking of the reference trajectory. The validation of the linear model showed
that the assumption of negligible turning rate in the yaw DOF was not proper, so a non

7.2
Future Work
Despite the fact this thesis approaches a solution whose operation has been proved to
work satisfactorily, all research work can be improved. The following list describes some
lines of future research that may be of interest.
• Improvement of the Sampling Technique. An initial approach could be the
inclusion of the covering area of the map to sample as a term in the objective
function to optimize, so that the precision of the reconstruction can be higher.
• Sampling Strategy Online. To save energy and time, an interesting implemen-
tation could be an online strategy, with the decision-making during the mission.
• Experiments. Due to lacking of time, the approach has only been tested through
the Gazebo simulator. The experiments in the ocean are required to prove the real
utility of this technique.
• Implementation of the method with 4-DOF. This thesis is based on the
measurements in the XY plane. The inclusion of the heave may be interesting for
some scalar fields, or for another objectives, such as tracking the thermocline.
• Obstacle avoidance. The ocean environments are unknown and the assumption
of obstacle-free may be wrong. It could be an interesting future research to imple-
ment a technique in charge of detecting and avoiding obstacles that may appear
in the predefined path while performing the mission.
