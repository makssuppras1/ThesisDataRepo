Abstract
During the work of this project a state–of–the–art instrument, RADEN at MLF, J-PARC,
Japan, was used for imaging with polarised neutrons to collect unique data from two
magnetic alloys of Co38Ni33Al29. This study focuses on the feasibility of measuring and
reconstructing macroscopic magnetic domains within bulk materials. As an initial in-
vestigation, 1 out of 9 elements in the polarisation matrix was measured for 3 projec-
tions angles, namely the PY Y element. This is the ﬁrst time such an experiment has
been performed and therefore no prior methods for analysing the measured data ex-
ists.
From sparse data it is possible to conﬁrm that the two alloys possess a multiple mag-
netic domain conﬁguration. The mean values of the y–components of the magnetic
ﬁelds could be determined from the polarimetric data. The combined ﬁelds strength
of the x– and z–components of the magnetic ﬁelds can be determined. However, the
individual values of each component are unobtainable.
A numerical method is used to formulate a set of symmetry rules for a multiple set
of existing solutions when only measuring the PY Y element. Additionally, it is shown
that a unique solution can be found by measuring all elements in the polarisation ma-
trix.
Finally, a method for analysing data for the full polarisation matrix from two perpen-
dicular projection angles is presented. This has been included in a magnetic recon-
struction algorithm that can reconstruct simulated data from n ×n domains.

Resumé
I dette projekt blev instrumentet RADEN ved MLF, J-PARC, Japan, brugt til at udføre
imaging ved brug af polariserede neutroner til at indsamle unik data fra to magnetiske
legeringer bestående af Co38Ni33Al29. Dette studie fokuserer på muligheden for at måle
og rekonstruere de magnetiske domæner inde i faste matrialer. Indledningsvis blev 1
ud af 9 elementer i polarisationsmatricen, PY Y , målt for 3 projektionsvinkler. Dette
er første gang et sådant eksperiment er udført, og der ﬁndes derfor ingen tidligere
beskrevet metoder til analyser af de målte data.
Baseret på det målte data kan det bekræftes, at de to legeringer består af ﬂere mag-
netiske domæner. Det er muligt at bestemme middelværdier af y–komponenterne i de
magnetiske felter. Den kombinerede feltstyrke af x– og z– komponenterne i de mag-
netiske felter kan bestemmes, men det er ikke muligt at bestemme de eksakte værdier
for hver komponent.
Ud fra en numerisk metode formuleres et sæt symmetriregler der bestemmer antallet
af mulige løsninger der er gældende ved kun at måle PY Y elementet. Derudover bliver
det påvist, at der ﬁndes en unik løsning når alle elementerne i polarisationsmatricen
bliver målt.
Afslutningsvis præsenteres en metode til at analysere data fra den fulde polarisations-
matrix baseret på to vinkelrette projektionsvinkler. Dette er blevet inkluderet i en mag-
netisk rekonstruktionsalgoritme, der kan rekonstruere simulerede data fra en prøve der
indeholder n ×n domæner.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Imaging is a non–destructive technique to reveal the interior of objects and it is widely
used in several ﬁelds. E.g. in airport security to examine luggage without opening
them, in health sciences to diagnose patients and in various scientiﬁc ﬁelds to inves-
tigate fundamental characteristics of materials. There are a number of different tech-
niques within the ﬁeld of imaging, each serving as a tool to observe speciﬁc sample
properties. Despite having well developed techniques for imaging the different den-
sities in tissues and to observe the crystal lattice orientations inside bulks, imaging of
the magnetic ﬁelds in the interior of bulk materials is yet to be fully understood nor
developed.
Neutrons are the best candidate to probe magnetic ﬁelds inside bulk materials. Ex-
ploiting that neutrons have an intrinsic characteristic of carrying a magnetic moment
makes it possible to interfere with magnetic objects. Furthermore, neutrons do not
carry any charge, thus it has a large penetration depth in solid matter. Combining
these two characteristics make neutrons ideal to probe matter that possess a macro-
scopic magnetic domains.
The method of imaging with polarised neutrons has developed rapidly over the last
two decades. The number of technical and methodical improvements and innovations
have laid the foundation for a reasonable spatial and temporal resolution [1, 2]. The
theory of imaging with polarised neutrons is complex but in the essence it is to mea-
sure how much a neutron precesses in a magnetic ﬁeld and correlate this precession
back to a speciﬁc position in space. In the latest accepted paper regarding polarised
neutron imaging, a successful reconstruction of the magnetic ﬁeld strength and ori-
entations for a coil is done [3]. The current limitations for this technique are that the
reconstruction relies on the assumption that there is linearised around B=0 and that we
have neutron spin precession angles below 180◦, the so–called phase wrapping limit.
The main objective of this thesis is to fully develop a method that can probe an un-
known sample by employing polarised neutron tomography and reconstruct the mag-

netic ﬁeld strengths and orientation.
The idea is to conﬁne the area of interest and write an analytical expression describing
the precession of a neutron in the magnetic ﬁeld and ﬁt all the parameters that are in
play for multiple angles and wavelengths at the same time. In this way there are no
limitations regarding the number of domains nor is it limited to only probing low mag-
netic ﬁelds with a maximum neutron precession of 180◦. Instead of being limited to
a narrow window of a polarimetric signal with half a phase, this method exploits the
entire wavelengths dependency to its advantage.
BUT HOW TO GET THERE ONE MIGHT ASK? To answer this question the thesis is struc-
tured as followed. The background chapter 2 will provide the fundamental theory,
which includes a brief description of neutron radiography and tomography. Next, an
argumentation for using neutrons to probe magnetism inside condensed matter is pre-
sented, together with a description of how to produce polarised neutrons neutrons
and how to distinguish the neutron wavelengths. The physics of a neutron in a ho-
mogeneous magnetic ﬁeld is then presented. Including ways to control the magnetic
moment of neutrons and how to detect them. Finally, how an experiment is carried out
and how to retrieve data are presented. The experiment was performed on two poly-
crystalline samples of Co38Ni33Al29 at a the novel and state–of–the art neutron beam–
line 22 RADEN, MLF, J–PARC. This is the only beam line in the world cable of doing
full time–of–ﬂight polarimetric neutron imaging. From the experiment an investiga-
tion of the magnetic domains is performed and the symmetry rules for measuring the
same signal are formulated and discussed in chapter 3. Based on the successful anal-
ysis a magnetic reconstruction algorithm was developed and presented. Finally the
important parts are discussed in chapter 5 followed by a conclusion and an outlook in
chapter 6.
Lastly, it should be mentioned that the work presented in this thesis is seen as a proof
of principle, and the work is far from done in order to perform full reconstruction of
the magnetic ﬁeld from an unknown sample, but it is a step in the right direction to
reach the ﬁnal goal.

Chapter 2
Background
The following nine sections will provide the relevant concepts to describe imaging with
polarised neutrons. First, the basic ideas behind traditionally imaging are outlined. An
introduction to the unique characteristics of the neutrons that are exploited and how
to produce polarised neutrons will be explained. The physics of a neutron in a ho-
mogeneous magnetic ﬁeld as well as the concept of Time-of-Flight (ToF) that makes
wavelength dependency efﬁcient will be outlined. The experimental set up used to
perform polarised neutron imaging will be shown in detail, together with a descrip-
tion of how each relevant part works, followed by a description of how to retrieve the
polarised signal from an experiment. Finally, the essence of the mathematical issue
that needs to solved in order to perform a thorough analysis of the investigated sam-
ples is addressed. Fundamental university level physics and mathematics is assumed
throughout this thesis.
2.1
Imaging
The scope of imaging is based on two fundamental techniques, namely radiography
and tomography, which will be presented in the following sections. The framework is
used for neutrons but valid for X–rays as well.
2.1.1
Radiography
Generally, a neutron source emits neutrons in all directions. The neutrons are guided
and collimated in such a manner that the neutrons probing the sample can be assumed
to be a parallel beam. This is an assumption since the mathematics of a parallel beam
is much less cumbersome than for a diverging beam, a so called fan beam. The essence
of radiography is to measure how much a well–deﬁned beam is being attenuated by the
illuminated material. Neutrons can be removed from the direct beam either by scat-
tering or by nucleus absorption in the material. The scattering of neutrons is caused
by incoherent and coherent scattering. The attenuation of a parallel beam straight

2.1.2
Tomography
Tomography is based on several two–dimensional (2D) radiographic projection angles.
Based on the several attention measurements it is possible to reconstruct the investi-
gated sample in three–dimensions (3D). The mathematics behind traditional tomog-
raphy can be found in various text books [9, 6]. The principle of neutron tomography
performed on a horseﬂy is shown in Figure 2.1 to the left, for (0◦, 45◦, 90◦, 135◦, 180◦)
projection angles. By mathematically matching the coordinate system of the sample
and the coordinate system of the detector to all projection, a 3D image can be pro-
duced, shown in Figure 2.1 to the right.
Imaging magnetic ﬁelds with polarised neutrons is mathematically much more in-
volved because the observables are tensors and not scalars, as they are for traditional
attenuation imaging [10, 11]. To outline this mathematically different approach to-
wards imaging and the experimental method is the aim for the rest of this chapter.
2.2
Neutrons as a Probe for Condensed Matter Imaging
Neutron imaging is a less known technique than X-ray imaging. The main reason for
that is the fact that it is less costly to generate and manipulate high intensity X-rays
than neutrons [6]. In the ﬁeld of material science there is a rule of thumb, that if an
experiment can be conducted with X-rays, X-rays are the preferred choice. The aim of
this project is to develop a method to study macroscopic magnetic ﬁelds in condensed
matter. The reasons for choosing neutron imaging as the preferred probing tool, will
be outlined in this section.
The neutron is a spin 1/2 nuclear particle with a mass of mn = 1.675×10−27 kg. Neu-
trons possess no electrical charge, thus they will have a large penetration depth which
makes them suitable for the study of bulky samples. Using neutrons makes it possi-
ble to distinguish between isotopes due to the nature of the isotope dependant cross
section, which makes it possible to design a sample in such manner that the features
of interest are highly visible for the neutrons. Furthermore, the neutron possesses a
magnetic moment of

3.1
Polycrystalline Samples of CoNiAl Alloy
Two samples consisting of Co38Ni33Al29 alloy were chosen to be investigated at RADEN,
BL22, at J-PARC, MLF, Japan. The two samples were annealed for an hour at 1350 ◦C
and 1250 ◦C respectively. Annealing at high temperatures will cause a macroscopic
break in intra atomic symmetry between the atoms and thereby cause domain forma-
tion. Annealing at different temperatures will cause a distribution of domain sizes.
Previous work by our collaborator J. Kopeˇcek from the Institute of Physics of the Czech
academy of sciences (FZU), has shown that the majority of the domains have a diame-
ter in the range of 900 - 1400 µm. Both samples are roughly cubic with the dimensions
of 0.5 cm× 0.5 cm× 0.5 cm which suggests that there exist up to 6 domains along each
dimension of the crystals. Electron backscattered diffraction images of the two sam-
ples are shown in 3.1. The crystalline domains are indicated with separate colours. This
mean that each colour is a speciﬁc crystalline orientation. It can be seen that the top
crystal, which was annealed at the highest temperature (1350 K) posses larger crystal
domains than the bottom crystal annealed at lower temperature (1250 K).

This section is devoted to presenting the results from the analytical approach. This
method computes the analytical expressions for all the neutron paths for the 1 × 1 ×

all the paths for a single slice are then all ﬁtted at the same time. This is a non–linear
ﬁtting approach which is strong since it takes all crossing neutron paths and ﬁts all the
involved parameters for a single slice at once.

and orientations, multiple solutions based on the ﬁtted values is investigated. Finally
a set of rules regarding multiple solution when only measuring PY Y is formulated and
applied to the ﬁtted values.

The method used for investigating a multiple set of magnetic domains producing the
signal in PY Y will be outlined. To narrow the search the number of simulated domains
are reduced to two. Each magnetic ﬁeld is described by its normal vector in the three
directions nx, ny, and nz together with the precession angle, φ. By only investigating
multiplicity for two domains reduces the computation time to search a large number

vectors B1 and B2. To sum up, this investigation will try to ﬁnd the rules for a combi-
nation of B1 and B2 and a combination of B’1 and B’2 produces the same signal i PY Y .

precession angle have to be same for all wavelengths. Recalling the precession angle,

Chapter 4
Magnetic Reconstruction Algorithm
In this chapter the procedure for the magnetic reconstruction algorithm is presented
together with the results from a reconstruction from two projection angles on a ran-

4.1
Reconstruction Procedure
A method for reconstructing a magnetic ﬁeld from the measured polarimetric neutron
data with two perpendicular angles, e.i. 0◦and 90◦, will be outlined in this chapter. As
described in [10] [29], tomographic reconstruction of a magnetic ﬁeld is not as the stan-
dard attenuation tomography, due to the fact that the polarisation of a neutron beam
travelling through a region of various magnetic ﬁeld directions and strengths cannot
be calculated using a simple line integral. This is due to the nature of non-commuting
properties of the neutron spin orientation along the path [30]. By exploiting that all the
magnetic ﬁelds can be represented by non-commutative operators makes it possible
to match a polarimetric signal to a unique set of operators. These operators can then
be unfolded and a magnetic domain reconstruction can be produced. The procedure
of ﬁtting the polarisation signals from the two polycrystalline samples of Co38Ni33Al29
have framed the method for the reconstruction algorithm presented in this chapter.
The frame of this procedure is presented in the ﬂow–chart diagram in Figure 4.1.
Firstly, a n × n random magnetic ﬁeld conﬁguration is generated, this is illustrated
in the red the boxes in Figure 4.1. Secondly, the polarimetric signals are calculated.
The number of neutron paths that will probe the sample, are manually determined.

Chapter 5
Discussion
In this chapter the results obtained from the polarised neutron imaging experiment
on the two polycrystalline Co38Ni33Al29, and interpretation pitfalls from the developed
magnetic reconstruction algorithm are discussed. Besides offering a short overview of
the work done in this thesis, suggestions to improve the magnetic reconstruction algo-
rithm are outlined.
OVERVIEW OF THE WORK DONE. The polarised neutron imaging experiment performed
on the two polycrystalline samples was a success. Despite only measuring the PY Y el-
ement for three sample rotations, it was possible to distinguish the polarimetric signal
from different parts of the sample and for different sample rotations. The main chal-
lenge for the analysis was the lack of statistics. However, it was possible to observe a
tendency in the y–direction and on the overall magnitude of the magnetic domains in
the sample, and these values corresponds to the magnitudes within the same order ob-
served in similar alloys [23, 25, 26, 27].
From the ﬁtted values, suggestions towards the signals of the remaining elements in
polarisation matrix have been formulated. The suggested conﬁguration based on the
ﬁts on PY Y –data have a four fold solution possibility. Therefore, none of the suggested
structures can be deﬁnitively rejected or accepted. From observations of many fold so-
lutions, based on the sparse data set, a set of symmetry rules of the indistinguishable
polarimetric signals have been formulated.
A magnetic reconstruction algorithm have been successfully developed. The current
version of the algorithm is capable of taking a full polarisation matrix as input and re-
construct the magnetic domains. It can handle two perpendicular projections with
the same amount of wavelengths available at an experimental set–up and reconstruct
with 99.999% accuracy the magnetic magnitudes and orientations for a n × n domain
sample.

Depolarisation
From an experiment the polarisation signal is calculated from the number of neutrons
measured in each pixel of the detector. For a speciﬁc setting the number of spin–up
and spin–down neutrons were recorded which was interpreted as a precession of the
neutron spin. An intrinsic issue of performing a polarised neutron imaging experiment
is the concept of depolarisation of the beam that can lead to incorrect interpretation.
There is two types of depolarisation, a spatial and a temporial. The concept of a spatial
depolarisation is shown in Figure 5.2. If a beam of polarised neutrons probes at least
two different magnetic orientations or strengths and are detected in the same pixel, the
signal gets depolarised. The obtained polarisation signal is then an expression of the
mean precession that the neutrons undergoes. A reconstruction of a depolarised sig-
nal will be a mean value of the probed area. Since a 3×3×3 domain reconstruction was
the highest presented in this thesis, this issue was disregarded in the analysis. Tem-
poral depolarisation becomes an issue if the magnetic ﬁeld strengths are big enough
to cause a large range of the precession angles within one time–bin. This means that if
the fastest and slowest neutrons within one single time–bin have a too large precession
angle between them, the measured signal will suffer from depolarisation.
The concept of depolarisation can for the case of wavelength dependent neutron imag-
ing be treated solely as a resolution issue. The degree of depolarisation is dictated by
the detector limits regarding the temporal– and spatial resolution. The expense of hav-
ing high resolution in both space and time is the low counting statistics. Depending
on which features of a sample that is desired to probe, the right balance between the
counting statistics, temporal resolution and spatial resolution must be chosen.
Figure 5.2: Concept of depolarisation of a polarised neutron beam. If the beam of neu-
trons observed in the same pixel is exposed to at least two different magnetic ﬁelds the
beam will be depolarised.

Chapter 6
Conclusion and Outlook
Polarised neutron imaging on the two polycrystalline samples of Co38Ni33Al29 was suc-
cessfully performed at RADEN, MLF, J-PARC. It was possible to distinguish the mea-
surements from the PY Y element for three projection angles. Furthermore it was pos-
sible to distinguish the polarimetric signal from different parts of the samples. The
varying signals from different parts of the samples indicates that the samples possess
different magnetic orientations throughout the samples.
It was possible to divide data from the samples into 3×3×3 domains with a fair statisti-
cal error. By using a non–linear ﬁtting approach it was possible to ﬁt all parameters in a
complete slice from two projections angles at once. From the analysis it was possible to
determine all the y–component in each domain. The mean magnetic magnitudes from
the ﬁt did not match the magnitudes observed in similar alloys [23, 25, 26, 27]. From the
measurement it is not possible to conclude anything from the x– and z–components,
besides that these are limited to contribute to the overall magnitudes.
Neglecting the uncertainties on the ﬁtted values, the remaining elements of the po-
larisation matrix were calculated based on the ﬁts from the PY Y data. According to
the symmetry rules, the solution has a four fold symmetry. Therefore none of the sug-
gested conﬁgurations can be deﬁnitively rejected or accepted. From observations of
many fold solutions, a set of symmetry rules of the indistinguishable polarimetric sig-
nals have been formulated when only PY Y is measured. The full polarisation matrix
should be measured before a complete analysis can be done.
A magnetic reconstruction algorithm was successfully developed. The current algo-
rithm is capable of reconstructing a magnetic conﬁguration from a sample by taking
a full polarisation matrix obtained from two perpendicular projection angles. For a
random input the algorithm reconstructs with 99.999 % accuracy the magnetic magni-
tudes and orientations for an n×n domain sample. For some conﬁgurations there is a
large solution space when probing with only two projection angles. These conﬁgura-
tions can be determined with additional projection angles.
61
==PAGE:74==
The next version of the magnetic reconstruction algorithm should incorporate signals
from several projection angles in order to determine all types of magnetic conﬁgura-
tions. Increasing the number of domains in a slice to n × n will increase the compu-
tational time. Therefore a different approach for high resolution data is required. This
could be carried out by either developing a new ﬁtting algorithm or employing super-
computers.
Finally, the results presented in this thesis are used for the application of a new beam
time for the experimental set–up RADEN, MLF, J-PARC. An additional beam time will
allow to probe for several projection angles and longer espouser times. Based on the
analysis it is expected that the extra beam time will makes it possible to perform demon-
stration study of a three–dimensional (3D) magnetic ﬁeld mapping of an unknown
sample, which will be its ﬁrst of a kind.
When this technique of magnetic mapping of 3D materials is fully matured, it can be
applied in a wide range of scientiﬁc and engineering ﬁelds. This includes electrical
engineering, superconductivity, energy materials and thermoelectric materials. It is of
high importance to fully develop the method of 3D magnetic mapping of materials,
since it has applications within numerous important ﬁelds of science and technology
of the 21st century.

Learning objectives 
Throughout the M.Sc. project “Imaging with Polarised Neutrons” the overall learning objective is 
to study physical phenomena of magnetism inside bulks with state of the art techniques and 
with the purpose of generating a fundamental contribution to the current limitations within the 
research field.  
To achieve the overall objective the thesis will be based on these individual learning objectives 
• Be able to conduct an experiment at a state of the art neutron imaging instrument. 
• Employ relevant mathematical models to analyze data from sparse data set. 
• Employ thorough literature search as a basis for understanding and explaining 
experimental results, and to describe the current state of the research field. 
• Communicate experimental results and literature findings for supervisors and members of 
the team both orally and through reports. 
• Use aspects of project management to control and plan the time spend on different 
activities to optimize the final output.  
• Work individually and in collaboration with supervisors to perform research at an 
international level.
