Abstract 
Allostery is a molecular process that regulates protein activity through the binding of molecules at re-
gions distant from the protein’s active site. Mutations in these allosteric regions have been found to 
change the protein’s capacity to be regulated, which in turn leads to the emergence of diseases. 
 
While most of the bioinformatic studies performed on disease-related proteins and their druggability 
are centered on the active site due to its high evolutionary conservation, studying allosteric mutations 
and their long-range effects to protein structure and activity can help discover more options for therapies 
involving these mutated proteins. Thanks to the growth in the field of structural modelling and the 
widespread creation of computational tools, new computational pipelines for protein analysis and effect 
prediction, such as MAVISp, are being developed in this field.

1. 
Introduction  
1.1 
Protein Allostery in Disease 
 
Protein allostery is one of the many biological processes involved in regulating the activity of proteins 
in living organisms. This process occurs when a certain ligand binds to a specific region (allosteric site) 
in a protein, which causes a conformational change to said protein. This structural change, in turn, will 
affect the protein’s functional site (orthosteric site), regulating its activity. [1] 
 
This regulatory mechanism can be disrupted in certain genetic diseases. If a protein suffers a point 
mutation in an allosteric site it may lose its ability to be regulated through ligand binding, causing it to 
be in a permanently inactive or active state. This could prove more problematic if the mutated allosteric 
site is tasked with regulating protein-protein interactions or the affected protein regulates other proteins’ 
activity, since the mutation could affect entire signaling cascades and contribute to complex disease 
phenotypes. Due to this mechanism, such diseases have come to be known as “allosteric diseases”. [2] 
 
1.2 
 Challenges in Predicting Long-Range Allosteric Mutations 
 
While the study of how the protein structure affects its function is of great importance to understand 
allosteric diseases, it remains very challenging for several reasons.  
To start, the structural changes to a protein caused by allosteric regulation can be very subtle. Even if 
there’s a small change on the dynamic landscape of the protein, it could lead to the stabilization of an 
active conformation or destabilizing an inactive one without any a priori obvious structural change. 
 
Other issues are the lower degree of evolutionary conservation of allosteric sites compared to active 
sites, complicating its identification through sequence analysis. Also, the long-range nature of allosteric 
regulation, taking place through complex amino acid interactions, can be difficult to discern. [1] 
 
The final issue in this problem is the cellular environment. The enormous complexity of regulation 
processes inside cells, coupled with their interconnectivity and effects on many other processes makes 
it very difficult to predict the effect of certain changes on a bigger scale. [2] 
 
1.3 
Computational Frameworks for Allostery 
1.3.1 Summary of AlloSigMA2 
 
The main challenge in predicting long-range allosteric mutations lies in the difficulty of quantifying 
how a single residue change can influence distant sites across a protein structure. Traditional methods 
often fail to capture this effect because they focus on local stability or direct contacts. To address this, 
we use Allosigma2, a tool that it is a structure-based statistical mechanical model of allostery 
(SBSMMA) that is designed to predict changes in allosteric free energy resulting from point mutations. 
The method classifies mutations as "UP" or "DOWN" based on the volume of side chains of the amino 
acids. It uses an elastic network model of the protein to simulate how a mutation at one position can 
energetically affect distant residues, named as response sites. By applying this model across the entire 
structure, AlloSigMA2 generates an Allosteric Signaling Map (ASM), which provides a systematic,

residue-level description of potential long-range effects. However, because AlloSigMA2 relies on a 
static protein structure, it cannot fully capture conformational dynamics or the behavior of protein en-
sembles.To overcome this limitation, we integrated its ASM results into the long-range module in sim-
ple mode of MAVISp. Also, MAVISp adds additional layers of analyses, including molecular dynam-
ics, to refine and validate the predicted allosteric pathways. [3] 
 
1.3.2 The MAVISp Framework  
 
To predict long-range, allosteric effects of missense mutations, this study leverages the MAVISp 
(Multi-layered Assessment of Variants by Structure for proteins) framework. MAVISp is a modular 
framework developed to evaluate the structural and functional consequences of amino acid substitu-
tions. It consists of two modes: a simple mode, which analyzes single static protein structures, and an 
ensemble mode, which incorporates conformational changes from multiple structures, generated 
through molecular dynamics simulations. MAVISp is organized into modules, each dedicated to a spe-
cific dimension of protein variant analysis, such as stability, interaction interfaces, post-translational 
modifications, or long-range effects, as seen in Figure 1. For this study, particular emphasis is placed 
on the long- range module. [4]

ensemble mode, while those colored in white are available in simple mode. The long-range module, 
highlighted in pink, is the primary focus of this thesis.

In the Simple Mode, the module employs the AlloSigMA2 algorithm as described in Section 1.3.1. The 
inputs are the PDB file, and the Allosteric Signaling Map (ASM) generated by Allosigma2. Then, a 
systematic filtering workflow is applied, which is a ΔG > |2| kcal/mol, a minimum atomic contact dis-
tance of 5.5 Å to ensure non-local effects, and solvent accessibility above 25% (SASA). In addition, 
response sites located within functional pockets are highlighted using Fpocket. These filters ensure that 
the predictions focus on mutations with long-range consequences.  [4] 
 
In the Ensemble Mode, the predictions from the Simple Mode are validated and refined using a more 
sophisticated, all-atom approach. This process involves building atomic-contact-based Protein Structure 
Networks (PSNs) from MD simulation trajectories, applying graph theory algorithms to identify the 
shortest communication paths between the mutation site and its predicted distal response sites. Then, 
the path analysis verifies whether a route for allosteric communication exists within the protein’s dy-
namic conformational space, adding a crucial layer of validation to the initial coarse-grained prediction. 
[4] 
 
This approach makes MAVISp a powerful tool for studying how disease-related variants cause long-
range effects. 
 
1.4 
 Case studies: GRB2 and DLG4 
 
Some of the more widespread domains mediating protein-protein interactions, which are also present 
in proteins related to allosteric diseases, are Src Homology 2 and 3 (SH2 and SH3, respectively) do-
mains and PDZ domains. Some of the more experimentally studied proteins containing these domains, 
therefore making them interesting for further bioinformatic studies due to their implications in certain 
diseases, are GRB2 and DLG4. 
 
1.4.1 GRB2 
 
Growth Factor Receptor-Bound Protein 2 (GRB2) is an adaptor protein that plays an important role in 
intracellular signal transduction. GRB2 contains one single Src Homology 2 (SH2) domain, flanked by 
two Src Homology 3 (SH3) domains (N-SH3 and C-SH3). SH2 domain recognize phosphorylated ty-
rosines, while SH3 domains bind proline-rich sequences, allowing GRB2, depending on the receptor, 
to direct the signal towards different cellular responses. [5]

1.4.2 DLG4 
 
The DLG4 gene, which is located in the locus 17p13.1, encodes for a protein called postsynaptic density 
protein 95 (PSD-95). It is a member of the membrane-associated guanylate kinase (MAGUK) family, 
and its most widely studied member. 
 
Like other proteins from the same MAGUK family, PSD-95 contains a guanylate kinase-like domain, 
one Src Homology 3 (SH3) domain and three PDZ domains, all linked together by disordered linker 
regions. These domains give PSD-95 the capacity to bind to other proteins (c-termini regions through 
PDZ domains, proline-rich regions via SH3 domains and cytoskeleton components through the GUK 
domain). [7]

2.1 
 AlphaCutter Workflow  
 
Many bioinformatic tools are designed to predict on structured regions and therefore their performance 
is diminished by the presence of non-globular regions. It is one of the reasons we want to trim them out. 
 
AlphaCutter is a computational tool designed to filter predicted protein structures by removing non-
globular, unstructured regions that are commonly found in models generated by deep learning methods. 
These flexible loops, termini, and low-complexity segments can introduce significant noise, negatively 
affecting downstream structural bioinformatics applications such as molecular dynamics simulations, 
and pocket detection. A limitation is relying on pLDDT scores can cut regions that do have functional 
relevance and are not necessarily part of nonglobular regions but turns or linkers. To overcome this, 
AlphaCutter was developed with a contact-aware approach. This contact awareness is the main ad-
vantage of AlphaCutter over the simple pLDDT cutoff, as regions forming non-local contacts with dis-
tant residues are rescued as domain regions regardless of their pLDDT scores. 
 
The AlphaCutter algorithm operates through a defined sequence of steps. First, the default protocol 
calcultes secondary structure elements using DSSP (Dictionary of Protein Secondary Structure) to an-
notate each residue. Next, it identifies potential non-globular regions by locating unusually long seg-
ments, specifically, loops longer than 20 amino acids and helices longer than 30 amino acids. The core 
discriminative step involves generating a contact map of the structure. A long loop or helix is only 
classified as non-globular and marked for removal if none of its residues form stabilizing contacts 
(within ≤ 4.5 Å) with any residue at least five positions away in either of the N-terminal or C-terminal 
direction. Noteworthy, all β-sheet residues are always retained due to their inherent structural role. 
Lastly, the remaining contacting fragments are merged into coherent output domains.  
 
To evaluate the potential future use of AlphaCutter in MAVISp, we used a curated set of ten human 
proteins (GPC5, DLG4, GRB2, PELI2, BCKDK, ATG4A, BTG1, ATG5, PARK7, RB1) was selected 
from the MAVISp database. These structures were accessed via their unique UniProt accession IDs. To

evaluate AlphaCutter, we benchmarked its performance against manual curation from biocurators ac-
cordingly to MAVISp criteria. 
 
The performance of the algorithm was tested by varying two critical parameters across a range of values. 
The first parameter, fragment_min, specifies the minimum size of a contiguous fragment considered in 
the structure, with values of 5, 10, 15, 20, 35, and 50 residues examined. The second parameter, 
plddt_min, defines the the average pLDDT of the given fragment/domain, with thresholds values of 0, 
50, 60, 70, and 90.

2.3 
MAVISp Simple Mode Analysis for Long-Range Effects 
To evaluate the potential long-range, allosteric consequences of missense variants, we employed the 
Simple Mode pipeline of the MAVISp framework. This approach leverages the AlloSigMA2 algorithm, 
which uses an elastic network model to simulate how mutations disrupt communication networks across 
a protein's structure. 
 
We analyzed the AlphaFold2-predicted structures for two key scaffold proteins: GRB2 (UniProt Acc: 
P62993) and DLG4 (UniProt Acc: P78352). To ensure the reliability of the analysis, which is best suited 
for well-structured, globular domains, we manually curated these full-length models. The following 
structured domains were selected for investigation: 
 
==PAGE:14==
      
 
Allosteric Mutations in Disease Related Proteins 
 
10 
• GRB2: The C-terminal SH3 domain (residues 160–214) 
 
• DLG4: The PDZ3 domain (residues 311–394) 
 
These trimmed domains were first screened for suitability using a standard quality control protocol. 
This involved verifying their globularity [13] using globcheck and checking for the presence of coiled-
coil motifs using CoCoNat. Allosigma2 does not perform that well with elongated and non-globular 
protein domains.. Domains passing these checks were submitted to the AlloSigMA2 webserver to gen-
erate allosteric signaling maps (ASMs). These maps quantify the predicted change in free energy (ΔΔg) 
across the entire structure in response to virtual mutations at each position. 
 
The resulting allosteric signaling maps were processed and refined using a series of biophysical filters. 
This post-processing step identified high-confidence allosteric sites by requiring them to be surface-
accessible and located within binding pockets, as predicted by FPocket. The final output of this pipeline 
consists of structural files detailing the coordinates of these predicted allosteric pockets. [14] 
 
These curated allosteric pocket files, representing regions most sensitive to mutational perturbation, 
were used as the direct input for the subsequent analysis of allosteric communication pathways, de-
scribed in Section 2.4.3. 
 
2.4 
Molecular Dynamics Simulations 
 
Molecular dynamics (MD) simulations were conducted to capture the conformational dynamics of spe-
cific domains from the GRB2 and DLG4 proteins. The goal was to generate structural ensembles for 
use in the MAVISp framework's Ensemble Mode to then apply the LONG_RANGE module in the 
Ensemble Mode. This is crucial for assessing long-range and allosteric effects of variants that may be 
missed in single, static structures. 
 
2.4.1 System Preparation  
 
The initial structures for simulation were the same trimmed AlphaFold2 (AF2) models used in the MA-
VISp Simple Mode analysis, ensuring consistency across the computational workflow. 
 
• GRB2: The SH3 domain (UniProt: P62993), residues 160–214, was used. 
 
• DLG4: The PDZ2 domain (UniProt: P78352), residues 312–394, was used. A proline residue at 
position 311 was removed from the initial simple mode model to resolve a force field parame-
terization issue, resulting in a final simulated range of 312–394. 
 
After trimming, the proteins were prepared in GROMACS, with their topology and coordinates gener-
ated using the CHARMM36 force field. To neutralize the protein ends, NH₂ and COOH groups were 
added to the N- and C-termini, respectively. For any histidine residues, we verified the correct protona-
tion state using H++ software along with GROMACS tools. Based on tool consensus, the structures 
were protonated in the epsilon state.  
==PAGE:15==
      
 
Allosteric Mutations in Disease Related Proteins 
 
11 
 
Once the proteins were prepared, they were placed in a dodecahedral box and solvated with TIP3P 
water molecules, ensuring at least 1.2 nm separated the protein from the box edges. Sodium and chloride 
ions were then added to neutralize the system and achieve a physiological salt concentration of 150 
mM. 
 
To remove any steric clashes that might have arisen during setup, the system first underwent energy 
minimization. Following this, we performed a two-step equilibration with positional restraints on the 
protein’s heavy atoms. The first step was conducted under the NVT ensemble (constant number of 
particles, volume, and temperature) at 298 K using the velocity-rescale thermostat. This was followed 
by NPT equilibration (constant number of particles, pressure, and temperature) at 298 K and 1 bar, 
using the Parrinello-Rahman barostat. 
 
Bond lengths involving hydrogen atoms were constrained with the LINCS algorithm, while non-bonded 
interactions were treated with a 0.9 nm cut-off for both van der Waals and electrostatic interactions. 
Long-range electrostatics were handled using the particle-mesh Ewald (PME) method. [15] 
 
Before the productive simulation run, we carried out a short 5 ns test simulation to assess the perfor-
mance of the system and we proceeded with the full production run. The resulting trajectories were 
centered on a central atom (Atom 687, PHE-205 CA for GRB2; Atom 691, ILE-359 CA for DLG4) to 
ensure the protein remained within the simulation box. Using the Mol Analysis script [16], we examined 
trajectory movies, RMSD, RMSF, radius of gyration, and minimum distance between the protein and 
its periodic image. These analyses confirmed that the systems were stable and ready for the production 
molecular dynamics simulations. 
 
2.4.2 Productive run of the system 
 
Production simulations were performed using GROMACS 2024 together with the CHARMM36 force 
field with a time step of 2 fs. Each protein system was simulated for a total of 1 µs. Simulation param-
eters were kept consistent with the equilibration protocol as described in Section 2.4.1. The stability of 
the simulation was monitored using the Mol Analysis script at a checkpoint after 500 ns and again upon 
completion of the full 1 µs trajectory.  
 
2.4.3 MAVISp Ensemble Mode Validation of Long-Range Paths 
 
Following molecular dynamics simulations, long-range interactions were analyzed using the 
LONG_RANGE module of the MAVISp ensemble mode. We constructed a weighted atomic-contact 
Protein Structure Network (acPSN) from the molecular dynamics ensemble with PyInteraph2. In this 
network, residues are represented as nodes, and an edge was defined between two non-adjacent residues 
if any of their side-chain heavy atoms were within 4.5 Å. 
 
To isolate biologically relevant, persistent interactions from transient noise, we filtered edges based on 
their occurrence (persistence) across the trajectory. We applied a stringent persistence threshold (P_crit) 
of 50%, retaining only those interactions present in at least half of the simulation frames for subsequent 
analysis. 
==PAGE:16==
      
 
Allosteric Mutations in Disease Related Proteins 
 
12 
 
We then performed path analysis on this filtered acPSN to identify the shortest communication paths 
between the mutation site and its predicted response sites. The simulation data validated the long-range 
nature of these pairs by confirming the residues never form local contacts. The analysis prioritized paths 
connecting the mutation site to residues located within predicted functional pockets, as predicted by the 
Fpocket algorithm. 
 
Finally, we annotated genetic variants as damaging if a communication path of four or more residues 
was identified connecting them to a functional pocket site, indicating a potential mechanism for long-
range effects. [4] 
 
2.5 
Implementation and Benchmarking of the Δh 
 
2.5.1  Δh Calculation  
 
The background-normalized allosteric free energy term, Δh was implemented within the MAVISp Al-
loSigMA2 workflow to provide an alternative metric to ΔG. The Δh value for a residue i was calculated 
as follows: 
 
Δℎ# = Δ𝑔# −Δ𝑔# 
where: 
• 
Δℎ# = background-normalized allosteric free energy  
• 
Δ𝑔#= allosteric free energy difference  
• 
Δ𝑔#= mean allosteric free energy across the protein chain  
 
The calculation was integrated into the existing pipeline via the allosigma-heatmap.py script. The func-
tion calculate_dh_from_dg processes the Δg matrix by computing column-wise means, subtracting 
them to generate the Δh matrix, and replacing any resulting NaN values with 0. 
 
A -mode flag (-m) was added to the allosigma-heatmap.py script to choose the analysis metric. The flag 
accepts Δg (default, processes Δg data) or Δh (processes Δh data). The selected mode dynamically sets 
the data arrays for plotting (plot_up_ar, plot_down_ar), updates plot labels to display 'Δg' or 'Δh', and 
prefixes all output files with 'dg_' or 'dh_'. 
 
2.5.2 Benchmarking Results: ΔH vs. ΔG Performance: ROC Analysis 
 
Due to analysis comparing the two allosteric metrics, we have chosen to use Δg as default in the 
LONG_RANGE module, as will be explained in Section 3.4. A benchmarking analysis was designed 
to critically evaluate the predictive performance of the Δh metric against the established Δg metric. The 
goal of this analysis was to determine if the Δh metric offered any improvement in identifying known 
allosteric mutations from deep mutational scans in proteins DLG4 and GRB2. 
 
==PAGE:17==
      
 
Allosteric Mutations in Disease Related Proteins 
 
13 
The benchmarking protocol was applied consistently to both proteins and both metrics (Δg and Δh): 
 
1. Data Preparation: The analysis was conducted using the previously established optimal atomic 
distance cutoff of 5.5 Å to filter out local contacts, as employed in the Long_Range module in 
Section 1.3.2. 
 
2. Filtering Conditions: For each protein and metric, two filtering conditions were tested: 
a) no_sasa: No filtering based on sidechain solvent accessibility. 
b) sasa_25: Filtering retaining only response sites with a relative sidechain accessibility 
higher than 25. 
 
3. ROC Analysis Setup: To find the optimal energy threshold for each condition, the initial filtering 
step was run with the Δg threshold parameter (-t) set to 0. This ensured the full dataset was 
retained for subsequent threshold scanning. 
 
4. Performance Evaluation: For each resulting filtered dataset, a Receiver Operating Characteristic 
(ROC) analysis was performed using a custom script (do.py). The script was used to scan all 
possible energy thresholds to classify mutation sites as positive (allosteric) or negative, based 
on a comparison against a curated gold standard list of experimentally validated allosteric sites. 
Key performance metrics recorded included the Area Under the Curve (AUC), precision, recall, 
F1-score.  
 
2.6 
Validation of Allosteric Pockets 
 
2.6.1 Fpocket Analysis 
 
To validate the structural relevance of predicted allosteric pockets, we implemented an analysis pipeline 
using Fpocket. The objective was to determine if response sites identified by the AlloSigMA2 workflow 
in the simple mode were located in computationally predicted pockets and to compare these predictions 
against experimentally mapped allosteric sites in DLG4 and GRB2. 
 
The analysis was performed using the custom script pocket.py.[13] 
The generated output file contains this information: 
 
• Is_in_Pocket: Boolean indicating if the residue is part of any predicted pocket. 
• Pocket_IDs: A semicolon-separated list of all pocket IDs the residue belongs to. 
 
• Pocket_Score: The corresponding pocket score(s) for the pocket(s). 
 
• Drug_Score: The corresponding drug score(s) for the pocket(s). 
 
• Pocket_Atom_Names: The names of atoms from the residue that are part of the pocket.

4. 
Conclusions 
 
Based on the obtained results, we can say that both AlphaCutter and CoCoNat have been successfully 
implemented as preprocessing tools in our computational pipeline. While CoCoNat has proven useful 
for the prediction of coiled-coil domains which difficult the usage of AlloSigMA 2, AlphaCutter should 
only be used as a helper to manual trimming since it doesn't provide acceptable results in all cases. 
 
The downstream analysis on GRB2 and DLG4 show that the Long_Range module inside MAVISp can 
be reliably used to identify protein allosteric sites with effect in other distant regions of the same protein. 
 
Through the addition and benchmarking of the dh metric we have confirmed that dg remains a more 
reliable descriptor of the effects of mutations to protein allostery, specially when paired with a SASA 
filter. 
 
Finally, analysis made via Fpocket can help to validate the hypothesis that many validated allosteric 
sites are located in pocket domains. 
 
5. 
Future Directions 
 
In order to continue improving the computational pipeline presented here, AlphaCutter could be com-
pletely implemented as preprocessing tool in conjunction with manual trimming and PAE scores in 
order to improve the trimming strategies used. 
 
If we would like to further understand the effects of allosteric mutations on protein structure and func-
tion, we would need to vastly increase the space of analysed proteins, not only in amount but also in 
structural diversity and requiring the use of more complex trimming solutions.
