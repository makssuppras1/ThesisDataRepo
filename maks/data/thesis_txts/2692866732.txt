Abstract
Over the recent years we have seen an almost exponential increase pertaining the
use of Digital Media and the technologies enabling it. Along with that trend - sta-
tistically, it was seen a rapid use of IoT devices and spread of ”mini to macro-scaled
IoT networks”. The above ﬁgures, were further supported by the recent ﬂuctuations
of IoT device use during the pandemic. The data showed an increased use of IoT
devices at a signiﬁcant rate relative to same periods of previous years. It meant
that with more devices brought forward and connected in the IoT networks, would
result to an increased potential for vulnerabilities or security issues.
Thus, the ﬁgures have conﬁrmed the rising trends pertaining the use of IoT
devices which on the other hand requires an increase awareness and preparation
towards minimising the possible issues that could arise. To prepare and discover
some potential vulnerabilities related to the increase use of IoT devices, this work
was focused mainly with the use of Home appliances. The choice, conducting the
work and its scope with those devices was related to the variety of available IoT
devices part of our daily use, potential private data leaks, and diversity of user base
(i.e. children’s, parents, guest users etc.). Thus, we can understand the importance
of home network comprised of many smart IoT interconnected devices. So, a higher
level of security for the Domestic IoT device is a must, reinforced also by previous
cases of IoT device attacks.
The aim of this work was to identify major issues from the daily use of Home
appliances, and how to have an increased security - as its core objective.
The
observations made revealed that much of the security issues with smart-homes and
its appliances consisting of many IoT connected devices are more trivial that one
would think. Thus, I would hypothesise that often times an increased device security,
it is not always the best solution rather, its proper use and awareness of security
risks linked with the device Ecosystem.
Therefore, to simulate an Ecosystem a virtual network was created along with
the use of its devices created in a virtual machine. Then, in a real-world scenario we
tested our home devices Samsung Smart TV and a Smart Printer from HP. Both of
them showed vulnerabilities that are documented in this thesis. At a later step, we
preformed a brute-force attack on a virtual machine to emphasizing the importance
of a strong password and not only.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Over the last decades observing the growth of technology there has been many ad-
vancement from the variety of tech gadgets, up to software level. There are many
reasons that made me pick this topic, but I would like to brieﬂy mention the three
most signiﬁcant ones. The ﬁrst one was 02193 Ethical Hacking a three week course
during the June, which introduced both the theoretical part and practical part on
hacking strategies and ﬁnding vulnerabilities. The second motivation was another
course at DTU 02234 Current Topics in System Security which in its curriculum had
security in IoT devices, where among others we were provided a research paper The
Internet of Hackable Things[10] which showed examples where security on IoT de-
vices had failed badly or was not taken in consideration at all. The third reason was
a book from Aditya Gupta The IoT Hacker’s Handbook which I found very helpful
and supplementary in addition to the other reasons mentioned above. The author
covers all needed for IoT Pentesting, Attack Surface Mapping, protocols, tools and
examples as well. Provoked by the reasons mentioned above and the increase of the
number of devices used at home during the pandemic I decided to test parts of the
devices I use daily at home.
IoT devices are becoming increasingly useful in many ﬁelds including home ap-
pliances to gather data and transmit the information to the user to monitor the
device(s) or equipment either using a private network or the cloud. In this thesis,
are provided case studies from companies who have released products or services
(for which case practical penetration testing was conducted) their level of security,
IoT device issues up to companies that have suﬀered IoT device attacks with great
ﬁnancial losses. Provided the state-of-the-art of the IoT devices one can observe
still issues despite their level of sophistication. The work presented hypothesises
that the issue stems from the manufacturer taking no responsibility about the po-
tential threats, security weaknesses, helping the user keep the device up to date or
properly install/conﬁgure and operate it. Based on the above premise, there could
be potential short and long-term beneﬁts when evaluating the security aspect for
the user. It means that for a company comprising inadequately with the securing
IoT devices could weight to one or the other (i.e. advantages or disadvantages) as
in the instance below.
1. Advantages with lowering the security standards could bring lower product
cost to market, less resources.

2.1
Internet of Things
According to Merriam-Webster dictionary the deﬁnition for the Internet of Things
or shortly (IoT) is: ”a system of interconnected devices, mechanical and digital
machines, objects, animals or people that consist of actuators and sensors that
continually collect and share data over the network without requiring human-to-
human or human-to-computer interaction”.
In the realm of Computer Science it could be said that the data collected by IoT
devices can be sent to the back-end of the systems specialised for storage, processing
and cloud computing where the latter plays a critical role.
IoT devices despite being part of today’s technological trends it worth to note
that they have been around for some time. The ﬁrst IoT device was invented in
early 1980s by a group of students at Carnegie Melon University. It was integrated
in and part of a vending appliance, the Coca-Cola vending machine which was able
to check if there were drinks available in it.
Nowadays IoT devices are found in diﬀerent products of our everyday life such
as: wearable devices, smart-homes, healthcare appliances, industry, transportation
logistics, military and more as reported by Statista.com The proliferated use in
such applications could be related to the electronics packaging getting smaller, more
powerful and cheaper. Some other drivers could be even data driven in a global
scale. An illustration of a potential driver could be the situation that arose form the
pandemic. The statistics show that during the COVID-19 pandemic the number of
IoT devices connected to the internet increased almost 3 fold compared to the last
3 years.

The Jeep hack
Security researches Dr. Charlie Miller and Chris Valasek have performed one of the
most signiﬁcant hack on the history of IoT devices[5]. They exploited vulnerabilities
in Chrysler’s Uconnect system to take the control over the car. It allowed attackers
to remotely connect it over a cellular connection. When nmap-ing it showed port
6667 open and running D-Bus over IP for communication between processes, which

2.2.2
Insulin pump
Medical devices are well known to provide critical service / care to patients however,
they also can be prone to vulnerabilities if the security measures are not in place.
A case depicting a vulnerable scenario was with the Insulin Pumps. In scenario
an attacker/eavesdropper was able to take control of the device by modifying the
dosage the insulin pump injected to the patient. The insulin pumps containing the
security ﬂaws where from Animas and were know as OneTouch insulin pumps.[15]
The approach that the attacker took stemmed from the lack of data encryption.
The researcher showed an example where omitting the encryption of the data lead
to security weaknesses which led to the comprise of the device(s). This attack was
pointed by a researcher called Jay Radcliﬀe where he found out that the insulin
pumps are prone of replay attacks.
Insulin pumps communicated in plain text,
which made it very easy for everyone to intercept, modify and deliver dosages of
insulin. Thus, the lack of information encryption is an invitation to modify the
data sent by the device allowing for communication interception from the attacker.
In such case the company was quick to react and patch the security ﬂaw found in
OneTouch ping insulin pumps.
The above case shows that lack of attention to device communication (i.e. data
format, protocol) when the product is not build up to spec brings further conse-
quences to the end user. Imagine the companies that try to cut costs and com-
promise product quality which later could be manifested with security ﬂaws to the
end user. Another element resulting in poor speciﬁcations for the products could be
the rush of the producers sending the product in the market fastest possible which
builds up pressure for them hence, producing cheap devices. Therefore compromised
quality and pressure to build cheap devices can lower the security measures making
them easy to compromise and thus invite for fatal consequences to the patient or
user of the device.
2.2.3
Smart Door Locks
Locks often times draw attention from the attackers for the very purpose they are
built which in essence is to ensure security for the user. Though, that promise is
diﬃcult to sustain since security weaknesses can be found even in the ”smart locks”.
Such scenario was illustrated with many smart locks where the attackers where able
to obtain superuser/ admin privileges. Their approach was by using a network at-
tack method that allowed the attacker to modify the key value. The value of the
key was obtained after they were superuser privileges due to a ﬁrmware ﬂaw (not
checking the SSL signature) that allowed access to higher security levels.

2.2.5
Nest Thermostat
Smart homes’ uses of IoT devices has been shown to increase at a fast pace. In
that verge Googles Smart Nest Thermostat has become a dominant choice for home
owners. As with most of things smart its instruction as part of the IoT revolution
revealed some security weaknesses pointed out cyber-researchers. In a speciﬁc sce-
nario malicious ﬁrmware injected into the device by executing malicious software
while pressing a button on the nest device for 10 seconds to trigger a global reset.
During the reset state the device will search for input media (i.e. USB device).
At that state where the device was accepting input the attacker injected malicious
code/ ﬁrmware sent via its input pin ’sys-boot5’.

created a worm as proof of concept which would be able gain access to a lightning

the spread of the malware from a single bulb to many in a distance over 400 meter.

3.1.2
Security protocols
WEP
WEP or Wired Equivalent Privacy[14] is the ﬁrst security protocol and was the
standard from 1999 to 2004. During this period of time, cryptographic technol-
ogy was restricted and mos of the manufacturers could only provide devices with 64
bit encryption. Apart from providing poor security, WEP was also hard to conﬁgure.
WPA
WPA or WiFi Protected Access is the second security protocol and was created
in 2003. Compared to its predecessor, WPA brought some minor improvements in
security by using Temporal Key Integrity Protocol (TKIP). WPA was also easier to
conﬁgure compared to WEP. The weakness of WPA was that Wi-Fi Alliance had to
some keep some elements from WEP for being compatible with older devices, which
leaded to well-know vulnerabilities.
WPA2
In 2014 was released WPA2[19], with much stronger encryption using than WPA
using AES (Advanced Encryption Standard) instead of TKIP. WPA2 was based on
RSN (Robust Security Network) mechanism, which provided several features such
as: Strong encryption and authentication support for infrastructure and ad-hoc net-
works, Reduced overhead in key derivation during the wireless LAN authentication
exchange. Support for opportunistic key caching to reduce the overhead in roam-
ing between access points, pre-authentication, where a station completes the IEEE
802.1X authentication exchange before roaming and CCMP (Counter Mode with
Cipher Block Chaining Message Authentication Code Protocol) encryption mecha-
nism based on the (AES).

Shodan does is that it identiﬁes the devices connected to the internet, their location
and their users.
It grabs the system’s banner and gathers data of every service
running on the port of a system. Banner grabbing is a crucial element on the pen-
testing process for discovering systems vulnerabilities. Besides it Shodan also shows
the vulnerabilities for each scanned system. It is widely used by hackers for ﬁnding
systems in reconnaissance stage.
In Shodan we can deﬁne our search by ﬁltering based on city, country, operating
system, network, ports and more. When a more speciﬁc target is required, we can
simply combine ﬁlters in the search by simply adding them one after the other.
Referring to OWASP IoT Top 10 most commonly used attacks, weak, guessable
and hard-coded passwords lead the list. The majority of the devices found on the

5.1
Guidelines towards an increased security of
IoT Devices
Considering various IoT devices operating in diﬀerent countries that have various
regulatory body(-ies) makes the operation of such devices relative complex and un-
regulated for the most part.
Hence, the introduction of standards is often time
needed to set a base standard for the majority of the users and as such bring a
more secure framework to operating and maintaining the operation of the device.
In Europe the main standard used in the telecommunication industry is best known
with European Telecommunications Standards Institute (ETSI).
ETSI starndard for Europe speciﬁes certain provisions that are to be taken both
from the users and the manufacturer of consumer electronic devices in general and
IoT devices speciﬁcally for the case concerning this work. An instance where the
application of ETSI standard depicts security provisions for consumer devices will
be presented below. The case studied was devised by the organisation studying and
classifying the security of connected devices. The provisions taken from the manu-
facturer are listed below.
1. Change of the default settings of the IoT device(s).
2. Use of authorisation certiﬁcates to establish connection.
3. Lack of strong and true randomized passwords (i.e. unique passwords).
The above list describes some potential measures that could be taken from the
manufacturer of the device though, according to ETSi standards there are security
provisions described for the end-user that allow for increased security of IoT device
when applied correctly. From the user end some complementary security provisions
that could be the below-mentioned security mechanisms.
1. Biometric authentication.

2. PIN.
3. 2FA Authentication application.
The importance of security provisions relies to future-proof security level for the
user and the device. If those provisions are not taken, it could spell problems and
other security issues for the user. Therefore, it is obvious that both the manufac-
turers and the user can do better to minimise the security issues by adhering to
the right standard (i.e. ETSI for Europe). An instance illustrating manufactures’
actions towards security provisions would be the release of frequent updates for their

apply etc. To elaborate, below are given few approaches of good provisions from de-

3. Monitor/trace users log-in device and IP address thereby identifying suspicious
connections, etc.
5.1.1
Security policies
Devices and the hardware running in them requires certain policies to be considered
before its being released to the consumer. Also, users have some limitations in the
use of device within a certain area/territory or power level. Thus, we can understand
that in such cases is best to be aware of and ensure compliance with the security
policies to have an optimal use of the equipment. The security policies are best used
when the right provisions are taken and applied during its use life-cycle. A case
that would illustrate such application is the use of ”right security mechanisms”. By
having the correct method/mechanism it might help identify addresses aiming to
monitor the credential space/combination from the attacker who might be trying
to ﬁnd the right credential combination via a brute-force technique. Thus, having
the correct security provisions in place derived from with the security policies in
mind the ﬁrmware would be likely to identify and inhibit most common malicious
techniques (i.e. brute-force attaches). The security provisions often take time to re-
ﬂect the changes from the device manufacturer which relies from the users feedback
or device data. Thus, it shows the importance of reporting the vulnerabilities to
the manufacturer to allow them chances providing solution and/or patches towards
a security concern. The reporting process for the most part requires the user to
simply send a report to the company by allowing its software to collect user’s use
patterns or device logs. Once, the problem/issue(s) are reported from the user the
manufacturer moves on to ensuring an update/patch to all the users once the have
a solution. Though, there could be cases where a problem might be found and the
manufacturers’ solution is not ready for which case the vulnerabilities uncovered -
