Abstract
This thesis deals with the small-signal stability of power systems with wind energy penetration. Only
converter-interfaced wind turbines have been studied. The Nordic32 test system and three modiﬁcations of it
have been used to study the effect of varying wind penetration. Time domain simulations and eigenvalue
analysis have been carried out using PSS®E and NEVA® respectively. The method used for tuning of
power system stabilizers is the frequency response method. Over the course of work, major ﬂaws have been
identiﬁed in the PSS®E implementation of dynamic wind turbine models that interface with the grid using a
converter. Another major issue has been discovered in the NEVA® module which led to the release of an
update where the issue has been ﬁxed. A basic disagreement has also been identiﬁed between the common
frequency response of the generator, excitation system and power system (GEP) transfer function and the one
shown by the NEVA® module. The overall quality and utility of the NEVA® Eigenvalue Analysis module
has been demonstrated. A speed-input power system stabilizer has been proposed. The same has proven to be
effective and superior to the original power-input stabilizer. It has been concluded that converter-interfaced
wind turbines do not have a single deﬁnite effect on small-signal stability. It is suggested that wind turbine
modes of operation and control, which have not been studied in this thesis, can have a more marked effect
on small-signal stability.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The problem of power system stability is one related to the stable operation of electric generators. The
power system is dominated by synchronous generators. As the name implies, these generators have the
rotational speed of their rotors synchronized to the frequency of the induced AC voltages in the stator
windings. Once generators are connected together in a grid, the frequency of their stator quantities must be
the same and the mechanical speed of their rotors will also be synchronized to this frequency (Kundur, 1994).
The problem of power system stability largely comes down to maintaining synchronism of all electrical
machines in the system, that is their speed of rotation (frequency) should be the same and in phase. When
electrical machines are in and remain in synchronism they are in an equilibrium. A stable power system
is capable of sustaining this equilibrium under normal operating conditions and ﬁnding a new state of
equilibrium after a disturbance (Kundur, 1994). A disturbance can be an increase in load, the tripping of a
line, the disconnection of a generator or any change that forces the system to make a transition from one
steady state to another.
The ability of synchronous machines to remain in synchronism, which also means that their rotor angles
do not move relative to each other is termed rotor angle stability. The occurrence of swings in the rotor angles
of some generators relative to others will change the power outputs of the machines due to the power-angle
relationships and may eventually lead to excessive angular separation and loss of synchronism. A single
machine can lose synchronism with the rest of the system or a group of machines can lose synchronism with
another group while the generators inside these groups remain in synchronism after the separation of the
groups (Kundur, 2004).
Small-signal stability, which is the main topic of this work, is one of the two types of rotor angle stability,
with the other being transient stability. It is deﬁned as "the ability of a power system to maintain synchronism
when subjected to small disturbances" (Kundur, 1994). Further more, "a disturbance is considered to be
small if the equations describing the resulting response of the system may be linearized for the purpose of
analysis" (Kundur, 1994).
The speed of rotation of electric generators (as well as acceleration and deceleration) depends on the
power balance/imbalance of two opposing "forces" - electrical power of the loads and mechanical power
of the generator turbine. If at a given moment the mechanical power becomes larger than the electrical
power the generator will accelerate and vice versa. Provided that all generators in the system accelerate or
decelerate together there will be no power redistribution between generators. However, if a generator runs
faster than another for a given amount of time, the angular position of its rotor relative to that of the slower
generator will change and part of the load will be redistributed between the machines (Kundur, 2004). The
faster machine will become more loaded, this will reduce the speed difference and the angular separation
between the two machines. To a certain extent this mechanism will act to restore the equilibrium in a way

some generators relative to others, ﬂows of power back and forth between generators will occur as described

in a timely manner. In such a case the system is considered stable. On the contrary, if an oscillation is
insufﬁciently damped the power ﬂows between generators continue to increase until protective devices react,
e.g., by a cascade of equipment tripping, eventually leading to a blackout (Knüppel et al., 2012). Insufﬁcient
oscillation damping may be a limiting factor for power systems, disabling them from utilizing higher power
ﬂow capacities that would otherwise be possible.
A disturbance causes a change in electrical torque comprised of two components (Kundur, 2004):
• Synchronizing torque, in phase with rotor angle deviation.
• Damping torque, in phase with the speed deviation.
The stability of a power system depends on both of these components. Insufﬁcient synchronizing torque
may result in a steady increase in generator rotor angle, i.e., aperiodic instability. The aperiodic instability
is also called monotonic or monotonic mode in some of the literature (Gibbard et al., 2015) as it refers to
monotonically increasing or decreasing amplitude over time. A lack of damping torque may result in rotor
oscillations of increasing amplitude, i.e., oscillatory instability. These are the two types of small-signal
instability. In present day power systems, the stability problem is usually one of insufﬁcient damping.
The best means of damping oscillations are power system stabilizers (PSS). These are control devices
designed to produce a component of electrical torque in phase with rotor speed changes, thereby modulating
generator power to damp out oscillations. Power system stabilizers act through the generator’s excitation
system and their output signal is fed to the exciter voltage reference input. Stabilizers add a feedback loop,
usually positive. Effective damping of oscillations by means of stabilizers includes choosing their location
(i.e. particular generators) and transfer function parameters. Parameters should be tuned in such a way that
the stabilizer produces a voltage signal component that is advanced in phase, to compensate for the lag
in exciter and generator dynamics. Filters are also used in stabilizers, to prevent the feedback loop from
applying compensation outside the frequency range of oscillations. Stabilizers may utilize different input
signals, as long as these signals transmit information for the rotor speed deviations.
Power systems have seen a massive increase of wind penetration in the recent years. Inevitably, this
results in systems with new characteristics and thus has implications for power system stability. Currently,
the two wind turbine concepts which prevail are type 3 and type 4 wind turbines. This is largely because
of the presence of a power electronic converter, which allows for a more optimal, i.e, efﬁcient extraction
of wind power by means of a varying wind turbine speed. Another plus of type 3 and type 4 turbines is a
considerable amount of freedom in wind turbine control including reactive power management provided
by the converter. These wind turbines feature a decoupled control of active and reactive power thanks to
their four-quadrant converters. The "decoupled" term has another meaning as well. It is used to purport
that the wind turbine speed of rotation is not ﬁxed to the grid frequency (as already mentioned). Due to
this decoupling, the grid only sees a power converter devoid of dynamics inherent to rotating electrical
generators. Seen from the grid, the wind turbine is a static electric generator. The turbine rotor speed is not
ﬁxed to the grid frequency. It is for this reason that type 3 and type 4 wind turbines are classiﬁed as variable
speed wind turbines, i.e., the turbine speed can adjust to the wind speed independently of grid frequency.

Another aspect of the decoupling effect is that the wind turbine does not contribute to the total system inertia.
Unlike a synchronous machine, the wind turbine does not resist any changes in grid frequency, e.g., in case
of disturbances, which has implications for frequency stability and control.
In the context of small-signal stability and eigenvalue analysis (also called modal analysis) "the term
mode is used to refer to the natural or characteristic response to a disturbance of the small-signal dynamics
of the power system" (Gibbard et al., 2015). Since the dominant wind power conversion technologies today,
that is type 3 and type 4 turbines, are not synchronously connected to the grid, they do not themselves cause
electromechanical modes of oscillation (Vowles et al., 2008). However, there are several mechanisms by
which wind generators indirectly impact modes of oscillation (Gautam et al., 2011, Vowles et al., 2008):
• Displacing synchronous machines which are directly involved in oscillations, thereby affecting the
modes.
• Signiﬁcantly altering the power ﬂows in the transmission network, thereby affecting the synchronizing
forces.
• Displacing synchronous machines that have power system stabilizers.
• Interacting with synchronous machines to change the damping torques induced on their shafts.
The ﬁrst three mechanisms are independent of the wind turbine type and conversion technology. By
replacing synchronous generators, the penetration of wind power in effect reduces the system inertia and
affects stability. The fourth mechanism depends on the wind turbine type and wind farm controls (Vowles
et al., 2008).
It is possible to involve wind turbines in power system oscillations directly by supplying them with
power oscillation dampers (POD) which manipulate the active or reactive power of the converter to improve
damping and this has been an area of recent and emerging research (Gautam et al., 2011, Anaya-Lara, 2006,
Domínguez-García, 2012, Fan, 2011). It is believed that this research topic will become more and more
relevant in the near future as oscillation damping requirements are not yet imposed on wind farms but are
very likely to be as their proportion grows. Besides, engineers will be in a more favorable position if greater
progress is already made in this research, once damping becomes a requirement for wind generators.
Although there is some understanding of the principal mechanisms by which wind turbines affect
small-signal stability, questions remain open. The research efforts in this area show results which are at best
ambiguous and inconclusive. Further effort has to be made to reach more deﬁnite answers as to which are
the impacts of wind turbines on small-signal stability. The present work aims to be a part of these efforts.
Modeling of wind turbines is an important factor for good analysis. Wind turbine and wind farm models
are still undergoing a long process of development. Purpose-speciﬁc modeling is at the core of successful
studies. Depending on the focus of the study, a wind turbine might be modeled by a large number of non-
linear differential equations or by a reduced and simpliﬁed algebraic model. Studies concerned with details
about a speciﬁc wind turbine manufacturer and model, its performance during faults and other transients,
interaction between a wind turbine and the rest of the wind farm, including the collector network may require
a detailed model of a wind turbine. Usually such studies will try to resolve questions about a wind farm
or wind turbine itself. On the other hand, studies which revolve around the power system as a whole, its
stability and control (as is the case in the present work), security assessment, power ﬂows, economic dispatch,
unit commitment, etc., do not require detailed models of speciﬁc wind turbines, in-farm components and
topology. Moreover, even if such models are readily available, their use in general grid studies will increase
the computational time unnecessary without contributing with valuable results. There are two general types
of wind turbine models – vendor speciﬁc and generic. A generic model is: nonproprietary, with tunable

The mathematical relationships describing the dynamics of power systems, including all their components,
are essentially non-linear. Computing detailed models of components, taking into account these non-
linearities, including differential equations is resource- and time-consuming. The general approach of
studying power system behavior is non-linear time-domain simulation where a particular disturbance is
applied. There, the simulation will establish whether the system will recover after the perturbation. However,
time-domain simulations have to be performed for a large number of different disturbances and at many
different system operating points. Even then, characteristics of the system and important scenarios may be
missed or underappreciated.
Fortunately, for small changes around an operating point the non-linearities may be omitted and the
system equations can be linearized (Rogers, 1996). In fact, the very deﬁnition of small-signal stability, i.e.,
which disturbance is considered small, depends on the extent to which a system can be linearized while
remaining a valid model for stability studies. More information on the extent to which linearizing system
equations remains valid and acceptable for the purpose of analysis can be found in Gibbard et al., 2015. Even
when working with linearized models, practical systems are so large in size that they can only be handled
and calculated by computers. Once the system model is linearized eigenvalue analysis can readily be applied
to yield valuable and detailed information about system characteristics without heavy calculations. This
type of analysis is independent of the particular disturbance which may occur in a system and is therefore a
good tool for assessing system stability. Furthermore, eigenvalue analysis can be used for the design and
evaluation of controllers for improving system performance (Gibbard et al., 2015). It is important to note
that linear eigenvalue analysis is not meant as a substitute for time-domain simulations but as a complement.
It is still required that controls designed with the help of eigenvalue analysis are tested by a set of non-linear
simulations afterwards to verify their efﬁcacy and robustness (Rogers, 1996).
Software products are essential for conducting power system studies. Time-domain simulation software
has been widely used for decades and different products of this kind have undergone a long evolution which
has made them very efﬁcient and versatile for power system analysis. On the other hand, there has been
little development in eigenvalue analysis tools over the years, limited competition, hence there are few of
them and they are yet to be improved. It is believed, that some of the reasons for this are that signiﬁcant
issues of oscillations have emerged at a later stage of the development of power systems and also because
the mathematical background behind eigenvalue analysis is somewhat more demanding to power engineers,
which makes eigenvalue analysis software harder to use. Some of the most widely used tools for time-

State of the Art
Vowles et al., 2008 found that cases with and without wind power are comparable in terms of damping
performance, i.e., wind power does not affect small-signal in a single deﬁnite way. They also found that
introduction of wind power can alter signiﬁcantly power ﬂows in the system, which in turn may lead to
congestion and degradation of damping performance. Rueda and Erlich, 2011 found no universal effect of
wind on the power system but established that "the impact of wind generation depends on the location of the
wind power plant, the amount of the conventional generation replaced and the degree of network congestion".
Modi et al., 2011 found that wind penetration affects the power ﬂows on the network and the damping of
inter area modes. They also found that it is important whether the conventional generators replaced by
wind turbines were ﬁtted with power system stabilizers which directly impacts the damping of oscillations.
They conclude that large wind power penetration affects damping performance signiﬁcantly. Gautam and
Vittal, 2009 found that increase of DFIG (doubly-fed induction generator) wind generation can have both
beneﬁcial and negative impact on small-signal stability. In another publication, Gautam et al., 2011 found
that as the inertia of the system decreases due to increase of DFIG, eigenvalues move towards the positive
half plane, thus making the system less stable. Knüppel et al., 2012 found that inter-area modes are largely
unaffected by increased capacity of wind power plants and mode of operation but also that a park-level
voltage controller has the largest impact on the inter-area modes. Sun et al., 2013 found that as the wind
power output increases the eigenvalues move to the left of the s-plane with the damping performance being
best at maximum wind power output. Mehta, 2014 found that modes become less stable as wind generation
replaces synchronous generators and their PSS but also recognized that above a certain penetration level
the effects of wind power become ambiguous. Yang, 2011 found that wind power integration deteriorates
small-signal stability in high load conditions. It is clear that the research on the topic has provided results
which can be considered ambiguous at best.

3.1
Synchronous Generators
Synchronous generators are machines which at steady state have their rotating shaft synchronized with
the frequency of the alternating current, hence the name "synchronous". In an interconnected system all the
synchronous machines (motors and condensers) are rotating with the same speed (i.e. frequency) and in
phase so we refer to them as being "in synchronism" or being "synchronized".
Different applications of synchronous machines require different speeds of rotation. The rotational speed
of a synchronous machine is related to the number of poles by the following expression:
n = 120f
p
(3.1)
where n is the speed in revolutions per minute, f is the frequency of the supplied current and p is the
even number of poles.
Traditionally there are two types of synchronous machine rotors - cylindrical rotors and salient pole
rotors. Salient pole rotors are better suited for applications where a large number of poles are required to
result in a low rotating speed as is the case in hydraulic turbines. Cylindrical (round) rotor construction is
more appropriate for higher rotational speed realized with less poles. This construction is easily balanced
and centrifugal forces are evenly distributed. Round rotors are mechanically more stable than salient pole
rotors. Generally, round rotors are used in steam and gas turbines that rotate at high speeds, usually 1500 or
3000 rpm.
3.2
Small-Signal Stability and Power System Oscillations
The following introduction to the concepts and nature of power system oscillations has been adopted
from Rogers, 1996.
Even in very large electric power systems the frequency is almost uniform due to the operation of
synchronous generators. The grid frequency is a result of the balance between the opposing forces of
mechanical and electrical torques, respectively mechanical and electrical powers. Speed governors adjust the
mechanical power to maintain this balance and keep the frequency within tight limits. In an interconnected
power system generators produce torques that are dependent on the relative angular displacement of their
rotors. When the displacement between the rotors increases, an electrical torque trying to reduce this
displacement is generated. In effect this torque keeps the generators in synchronism. For this reason these
torques are referred to as synchronizing torques. After a small disturbance the synchronizing torques may
keep the generators in synchronism but oscillations of the relative rotor angles may appear. The goal of

power system stability and control is to ensure that such oscillations will decay in a timely manner in which
case the system will be small-signal stable. If, however, the oscillations increase in amplitude with time
the system is small-signal unstable and the problem of oscillatory instability needs to be addressed. The
problem of power system oscillations ﬁrst occurred when systems became more stressed in order to supply
increased loads. As systems needed to supply increasing loads generators needed to rely increasingly on
their excitations systems which in turn led to unstable oscillations. Thus, oscillations became a barrier to
the usage of higher power transmission capacities. The other reason for the emergence of power system
oscillations was that more systems became interconnected to increase the capability of supplying loads and
to improve the economical aspects of power system operation. Often the interconnection between systems
is realized by means of weak tie lines. Synchronizing torques are lower across these tie lines and at the
same time each of the interconnected areas has high aggregate inertia which on the whole gives rise to low
frequency inter-area oscillations. A large number of small-signal stability issues ﬁrst appeared with the
interconnection of different power system areas. One of the main problems with power system oscillations
is that they are hard to detect or notice early enough. Just a small increase of a tie line ﬂow may trigger
oscillations of increasing amplitude without any system fault or other noticeable condition manifesting
itself up to the point when oscillations amplitude causes protective devices to trip a line or disconnect a
load causing a partial or full system collapse. There are two types of synchronizing oscillation - local and
inter-area. In the context of oscillations these are also referred to as local and inter-area modes. "Mode is
the technical term for a speciﬁc oscillation pattern. It is often used, more loosely, to refer to an oscillation
at a speciﬁc frequency" (Rogers, 2012). A local mode of oscillation (also a plant mode) is an oscillation
pattern where a single generator oscillates against the rest of the power system. It is called a local mode
because the source of the oscillations is conﬁned in a small part of the system. These modes usually have
a frequency between 1 Hz and 2 Hz. Inter-area modes of oscillation are more complex. They involve the
oscillation of a group of generators from one part of the system against a group of generators in another part
of the system. In the case of inter-area modes, the machines within the groups that oscillate against each
other are closely coupled (Kundur, 1994). These modes are also called global modes and usually have lower
frequencies - in the range of 0.1 Hz to 0.7 Hz. Generally, in small systems, inter-area oscillations have higher
natural frequencies than those of large systems. For a stable operation of the power system all oscillatory
modes must decay in a timely manner after a disturbance. A proper design of a power system should be
performed in such a way that oscillations are stable under the widest possible operating conditions. However,
the design implementations which have been introduced to take care of power system oscillations should not
be the limiting ones for the system’s operation. If the system operation is constrained, it should be a result
of thermal limits or transient stability considerations (i.e., loss of synchronism). A proper analysis should
reveal the following characteristics of oscillations:
• Frequency and damping of each of the oscillations.
• Which particular generators participate in each of the oscillations.
• Which generators can have a control effect on each of the oscillations.
3.3
Eigenvalue Analysis
The following introduction to eigenvalue analysis (also known as modal analysis) has been adopted from
Rogers, 1996, Rogers, 2012 and Kundur, 1994.
A single generator and its controls can be described by about 10 ﬁrst order differential equations. If a
system is small, e.g., having 20 generators, it can be described by 200 equations. Practical electric systems

are not that small. A system may have thousands of generators, described by tens of thousands of equations.
Computing differential equations is very time- and resource- consuming so when doing bulk system studies
by time-domain simulations only a small number of the models are in the form of differential equations
while most of the models are in algebraic equations. Even then, an analysis of such systems can only be
done with the aid of computer programs. The eigenvalue analysis, which is applied on a linear system is
considerably less computationally demanding. Power system dynamics are nonlinear in nature but for a
small region around an operating point the nonlinearities can be omitted and the system can be linearized for
the purpose of small-signal stability studies. It has been conﬁrmed in practice that a linear analysis of power
systems gives reliable results that are accurate enough for most cases. Linearization of system equations
makes possible an effective and systematic calculation of the dynamic modes of oscillation. The procedure
of linearizing the system model is beyond the scope of this project but it is described in Kundur, 1994.
Eigenvalue analysis allows to:
• Check if a system is stable around an operating point.
• Identify the cause of unstable oscillations.
• Design controls to improve oscillatory stability.

3.4
Mode Shape
The right eigenvector gives the mode shape, i.e., it indicates the relative activity of the states within a
mode. However it cannot be used as an indication of which states are more important for control purposes.
Other parameters, called residues, which would be presented in the current report can be used for that. The
mode shape can show whether a mode of oscillation is local, inter-area, inter-machine or intra-station. Right
eigenvectors of local modes are dominated by a few variables from a single generator or a closely connected
group of generators. That is the vector components related to those variables have signiﬁcantly higher values
compared to all the rest. In contrast, right eigenvectors of inter-area modes have their components distributed
more equally throughout areas of the system and values corresponding to generators from different areas are
comparable. Because the mode shape gives the relative amplitude distribution of an oscillation throughout
the system it can be used to identify a mode of oscillation in a dynamic simulation. A mode shape is related
to what can be seen in a dynamic simulation but cannot be used to determine the best location for damping
controls. It is because a small generator may oscillate with a higher amplitude than a large machine which

oscillates with a lower amplitude. However, the oscillation energy of the larger machine might be much
higher and more signiﬁcant than the one of the smaller machine. In this case damping controls placed at
the large machine would be much more useful in damping the oscillation. Therefore, it is the relative phase
information provided by mode shapes that is useful while the relative amplitude of the states depends on the
units of the states.
3.5
Participation Factors
Participation vectors (or matrices) give a good indication of how important a generator can be to the
control of an electromechanical oscillation. For example, the rotor speed components of a participation vector
can show how sensitive an eigenvalue is to damping applied to a speciﬁc generator. If such a component
is 0, it means that damping placed on this generator will have no effect on the damping. If the component
is positive and high the generator can be a good location for damping controls. In eigenvalue analysis it is
common to speak of participation factors which are the elements of participation vectors. Note that the
participation factors are complex numbers but, as is often the case for the larger factors, they are almost real.
Therefore, for ease of interpretation the magnitudes of the participation factors are plotted in a bar-chart
form" (Gibbard et al., 2015, p. 460). By using participation factors it can be identiﬁed whether a mode is an
electromechanical or different kind of mode. A mode is electromechanical when the participation factors
that are highest in its participation vector are the ones for the rotor-angle and speed states. The participation
factors and more precisely their real part has yet another very useful feature. If the real part of a participation
factor is positive, adding damping to that generator will improve the damping of the corresponding mode.
If however, it is negative, adding damping to that generator will indeed decrease the damping of the mode.
This is particularly useful when analyzing multi-machine systems, where a damping controller placed at a
particular generator may be beneﬁcial for some oscillatory modes and detrimental to others. Another feature
of the participation factors which is of utmost importance is that they are valid only at time t = 0s . At a
later time, e.g. t = t1 some of the modes will have decayed and the circumstances will have changed such
that the participation factors derived by eigenvalue analysis are no longer meaningful (Gibbard et al., 2015,
p. 459). In order to damp an oscillation a component of electrical torque must be produced in accordance
with the oscillation. This is realized by means of control system feedback. To inﬂuence a mode of oscillation
by feedback, a choice of input must be made that will excite the mode and a choice of output must be made
that will allow for the effect from the feedback to observed. The measures of those two mechanisms are
called controllability and observability. In the context of modal analysis, the ith mode is considered to be
controllable by the jth input if the product viBj is not zero. The ith mode is considered to be observable if the
product Cjui is not zero. Magnitudes of those two products are deﬁned as controllability and observability
respectively. A mode must be controllable by means of the chosen input and observable by means of the
chosen output in order to inﬂuence the mode.
3.6
Residues

The terms ri in the numerator of the above partial expansion expression are called residues. It is easy to see

usefulness of the residues is that they point to the generators which can be ﬁtted with effective damping
controls and the particular signals that these controls should employ. It is therefore of no use to place
damping controls on generators the residues of which have smaller values than those of other generators.
It is noteworthy that the residues are, in general, complex numbers. As such, they have a magnitude and
an angle when considered in their polar form. The residue angle has a special signiﬁcance in one of the
damping controller design methods. However, this method is not employed in the present work and so an
additional account of the method employing residue angles is found to be unnecessary. More on this matter
can be found in Rogers, 2012, p. 145.

In a multi-machine system the frequency response of each generator is affected by the rest of the power
system, making it highly irregular with mixed contributions from the system (Figure 3.2). When designing a
power system stabilizer this inﬂuence has to be eliminated or otherwise the dynamics of other machines will
alter the frequency response signiﬁcantly and a proper stabilizer tuning would be impossible. To neglect the
dynamics of other machines the latter are represented as inﬁnite buses by increasing their inertias to very
high values. This in effect keeps their speeds and rotor angles constant. As can be seen in Figure 3.3, "the
resulting phase characteristic has a relatively simple form free of the effects of natural frequencies due to
interaction with external machines" (Kundur et al., 1989). In some of the literature, the transfer function
response, when the speeds and angles are held constant is called a response with shaft dynamics disabled
(Gibbard et al., 2015).

The phase frequency responses of a generator used for PSS tuning can be considered to have two
components (Lam and Yee, 1998). The ﬁrst component depends only on the generator under investigation has
great, dominating effect on the frequency response and is ﬁxed regardless of the different operating conditions

the external system. It has been established that this component is not affected much by the dynamics of the
rest of generators (Lam and Yee, 1998).
The PSS shown Figure 3.1 utilizes speed as input signal. There are however stabilizers which utilize
electrical power, accelerating power, terminal bus frequency or terminal voltage (Rogers, 2012, Eremia,
2013). There are dual-input stabilizers which utilize two signals to sense oscillations. These can be beneﬁcial
for damping of oscillations in a wider range of frequencies, e.g. both local and inter-area which occur at
different frequencies. Also, accurate measurements of speed might be difﬁcult to obtain due to the order of
magnitude of speed changes and noise in the signal (Boldea, 2005). In such a case power signal is a good
input. In such an implementation the speed- and power-input signals are fed through different ﬁlters or no
ﬁlter at all. When speed input is used it is fed through a washout ﬁlter (high-pass ﬁlter). This removes the
steady state value of the speed and ensures that steady changes of speed do not modify terminal voltage
(Kundur et al., 1989). Filters are also used to remove the signals at shaft torsional frequencies. Speed-input
stabilizers can interact with generator shaft torsional modes so such ﬁlters may be necessary (Rogers, 2012).
The electrical power signal, however, has a high torsional attenuation so there is no need to feed the power
signal through a torsional ﬁlter (Sreedevi and Paul, 2010).

CHAPTER 4
Nordic32 Test System
The Nordic32 test system is a ﬁctitious network having properties that can be found in the real Swedish
and Nordic power systems. It was ﬁrst proposed in CIGRE-TaskForce-32.02.08, 1995 for transient stability
and long term dynamic studies. The system includes hydroelectric and thermoelectric generators. The
system is long with power transfers between an area of concentrated hydro generation and an area mainly
dominated by loads and thermal units. The system consists of 32 nodes, as the names suggests and there is
a total of 22 generators. There are 14 load buses which are connected to the original system by means of
two-winding transformers equipped with on-load tap changers. The hydro units participate in frequency
control using speed governors (model HYGOV, PSS E 33.3 Model Library Documentation 2012, p. 7-25).
All generators are ﬁtted with a simple excitation system model and a maximum excitation limiter (models
SEXS and MAXEX2 respectively, PSS E 33.3 Model Library Documentation 2012, pp. 6-131,5-4). Some of
the lines are series compensated by ﬁxed shunt reactors. There are three voltage levels corresponding to the
following subsystems:
• The main transmission system at 400 kV consisting of 19 buses.
• A regional system at 220 kV with 2 buses.
• A regional system at 130 kV with 11 buses.
The network structure of the Nordic32 test system at 400 kV is shown in Figure 4.1. It is divided into
four parts:
• North, with hydro generation and a little load.
• Central, with lots of loads and considerable amount of thermal generation.
• Southwest, with a few thermal generators and a little load.
• External, with both hydro and thermal generation and a comparable amount of load.
The modiﬁcations made to the original Nordic32 system, mainly the replacement of thermal units
by wind turbines, are made to produce cases with different degree of wind power penetration. Different
modiﬁcations of the Nordic32 system and a variety of applied disturbances were tried out in dynamic
simulations, amounting to about 30-35 cases which were then narrowed down to eliminate the redundancy in
their features. This resulted in four distinct cases intended to demonstrate the changes that take place as
more wind generation enters in a system while conventional power generation share decreases. However, the
results, their interpretation and conclusions drawn are restricted to type 3 and type 4 wind turbines.

CHAPTER 5
Modeling
Any model is a simpliﬁcation of an actual phenomenon or device. A good model is one where the
appropriate simpliﬁcations have been made for the speciﬁc purpose served. Models for different studies call
for different simpliﬁcations. However, simpliﬁcations which would lead to the omission of features that are
important and relevant to the study should not be made.
5.1
Load Modeling
Loads in this work have been represented by static load models. A static load model is deﬁned as "a
model that expresses the active and reactive powers at any instant of time as functions of the bus voltage
magnitude and frequency at the same instant" (“Load representation for dynamic performance analysis [of
power systems]” 1993). The particular static load model used is the composite ZIP model, which stands for
constant impedance (Z), constant current (I) and constant power (P). It is a polynomial load model where
active and reactive powers are described by the following equations:
P = P0[a1(V/V0)2 +a2(V/V0)+a3]
(5.1)
Q = Q0[a4(V/V0)2 +a5(V/V0)+a6]
(5.2)
where P0, Q0 and V0 are the initial conditions’ values.
The speciﬁc composition of load modeling in this work is 50% constant current, 50% constant impedance
for active loads and 100% constant impedance for reactive loads. This particular load composition has been
adopted from Rogers, 2012.
5.2
Modeling of Wind Turbines for Load Flow
Modeling wind turbines to perform a load ﬂow is simple. They are modeled as conventional generators
connected to a PQ bus. This way the active power P and the reactive power Q have constant values. For
example if active power and power factor are set or assumed the resultant reactive power is calculated.
Provisions can be made for a more detailed modeling of wind turbines for load ﬂow studies but these are
more appropriate for type 1 and type 2 wind turbines where the wind turbine is coupled directly with the
grid (Feijdo et al., 2000).

5.3
Generic Wind Turbine Generator Models
For the purpose of this work a generic wind turbine model has been used. A generic model is a model
that is standard, public and not speciﬁc to any vendor (Pourbeik, 2013). Generic wind turbine models can be
used with different simulation software products and can meet the modeling requirements for studies where
the speciﬁc wind turbine vendor or model is of no importance. Both GE PSLF® (Positive Sequence Load
Flow) and SIEMENS PSS®E simulation softwares come with standard-library generic models of all four
types of wind turbines.
Taking into account the purpose-speciﬁc nature of the dynamic wind turbine models the following
assumptions and guidelines have been outlined by WECC (Ellis et al., 2011):
• The models are intended for the simulation of events in a time associated with typical transient stability
simulations, i.e., ten to twenty seconds.
• It is assumed that in the simulation time frame the wind speed remains constant.
• The models are not designed for use in simulations that involve severe frequency excursions.
• The models are suitable for representing individual WTGs or the equivalent of a wind power plant.
• The main model components do not include protective functions.
5.4
Wind Generator Model with Power Converter (Type 4)
The dynamic generator/converter model which interfaces with the grid is an algebraically computed
current source. A block diagram of the model is shown in Figure 5.1 (PSS E 33.3 Model Library Docu-
mentation 2012). It takes into account the terminal voltage to compute the necessary active and reactive
currents which result in the active and reactive powers commanded by the electrical control model of the
wind turbine. Miller et al., 2003 This model has provisions for observing the converter’s current limits. The
inputs to the model are active and reactive current commands and the output is a current source which is
injected to the grid.
Because wind turbines type 4 are interfacing with the grid via the full-scale converter, in bulk power
system simulations, they do not have many of the conventional physical quantities of a generator. Seen from
the grid, the wind turbine is not modeled by the equation of motion. For this reason wind turbines with a
full converter do not have speed, rotor angle, mechanical power, excitation voltage, excitation current and
etc. However, behind the power converter, the wind turbine dynamics are still described by the equation
of motion. Seen from the wind turbine, the converter is analogous to the grid, having electrical torque and
respectively electrical power which represent the load.

the eigenvalue analysis is performed. For this reason, the dynamic simulation problem does not invalidate
the eigenvalue analysis and its implications for small-signal stability.

less and less oscillatory modes. This is natural since synchronous generators can cause oscillatory modes and
participate in them while wind turbines do not cause or participate in oscillatory modes alone unless ﬁtted
with additional controls. However, even though wind turbines with full-converters do not add or change
speed and rotor angle states of the state matrix, they do introduce some changes to the matrix because of the
voltage control they usually employ.

The phase frequency responses shown by the NEVA® module (like the one in Figure 8.3) are rather

present in the signal "chain" from the exciter input to the speed (Figure 3.1). Over the course of work on this
project, all attempts to resolve this nonconformity of the phase response shown by the NEVA® module have
failed.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
In the current report, the main concepts in the problem of small-signal stability have been presented. For
the purpose of studying small-signal stability eigenvalue analysis complemented by time-domain simulations
has been performed. Modeling aspects of full-converter wind turbines for bulk power system studies have
been introduced and discussed. The major difference between conventional generators connected to the
grid and converter-interfaced generation has been recognized. Generic wind turbine models have been
used for the time-domain simulations while for eigenvalue analysis no wind turbine models were supported
by the software tool used at the time of the project. It has been recognized that this fact restricts the
conclusions drawn from the eigenvalue analysis to a certain extent. Based on the experience with wind
turbine generic models it is reasonable to consider them as being under development. Numerical instability
issues occasionally occur in different steady-state and dynamic setups. Some ﬂaws in the particular software
implementation of these models have also been identiﬁed. It was discovered after a series of troubleshooting
steps that the models for photovoltaic generation, wind turbine type 3 and type 4 in PSS®E v.33.3.0 are
ﬂawed. The root cause is related to the angle of the terminal voltage of the wind turbine (or photovoltaic)
power converter. In order to inject current correctly the power converter model is following the moving
angle of the terminal voltage. The angle moves within the limits of −π to +π radians, with the direction
depending on the particular disturbance, that is the sign of the power imbalance. When the angle reaches
one of the limits, e.g., the value of −π radians it jumps instantaneously to +π radians, causing a spike in
the terminal voltage, current and other grid quantities. This issue has been ﬁxed in later versions of PSS®E
by the introduction of an if-else statement in the generator model that ensures a smooth transition of the
quantities depending on the terminal voltage angle as it passes from −π to +π radians.
A total of four different cases have been produced so that different degrees of wind penetration can be
tested. The small-signal stability analysis of the Nordic32 system and its modiﬁcations has been possible
with the NEVA® module. Due to the lack of support of wind turbine models, the effects of wind turbine
modes of operation and control have not been possible to study. Eigenvalues have been calculated, together
with damping ratios, frequencies of oscillation, eigenvectors, participation factors and residues. Oscillatory
modes, generators involved in the oscillations and best candidates for installing PSS have been identiﬁed. It
was not possible to recognize any single, deﬁnite effect of increased wind turbine penetration on small-signal
stability. Number of modes between cases is similar and the damping ratios of insufﬁciently damped modes
are also comparable.
Power system stabilizers have been proposed and installed at generators for the purpose of improving
oscillation decay, i.e., improve damping of oscillatory modes. An attempt has been made to tune stabilizers
by the frequency response method. It was found that there is a basic disagreement between the frequency
response plots of the phase shown by the NEVA® module and what is expected according to the theory and
57
==PAGE:74==
58
CHAPTER 9. CONCLUSION
literature. First and foremost, because the signal between the exciter reference voltage summing junction
and the speed is lagging, the sign of the phase frequency response should be negative. The phase shown by
the software module however has a positive sign which depicts a phase lead. It is known with certainty that
the overall frequency response is negative, i.e. lagging. Another feature of the frequency response which is
in disagreement with the theory is the curve shape of the frequency response. On a linear scale, which is
the case with the graph in the NEVA® module, the overall phase frequency response curve of generators
should be simple, close to a second-degree curve. Instead the frequency response plots shown resemble
a third degree curve or, alternatively, a shape that would be expected on a logarithmic scale. Due to the
disagreement mentioned above, an alternative interpretation of the frequency response shown by the NEVA®
module was tested. Based on unstable PSS performance this interpretation was rejected and a speed-input
PSS with different parameters was proposed by trial and error. The effect of the second proposed stabilizer
was ﬁrst demonstrated when ﬁtted to only one of the generators. It was shown that the stabilizer improves
signiﬁcantly damping performance when ﬁtted to this particular generator which conﬁrms the previous
ﬁnding, based on residues, that this generator affects mode damping signiﬁcantly. Then it was shown that
when the same stabilizer is ﬁtted to all generators the damping performance is improved further. It was also
demonstrated that the proposed speed-input PSS is superior to the original power-input PSS. However, it is
not argued that speed-input stabilizers are more effective than power-input stabilizers. The results of the
current work only show that the proposed speed-input PSS with the selected parameters performs better
than the original power-input PSS with its original parameters. It is perfectly possible that a better-tuned
power-input PSS performs better than the proposed speed-input PSS.
It was established that the frequency response plots between different cases do not differ notably. The
differences between cases are only in operating conditions while the dynamics of the generators in the
external system are ignored. This corresponds well with the observation of Lam and Yee, 1998 that the
frequency response "is not markedly affected by change of operating conditions". For this reason, PSS of
generators in the four different cases have been supplied with the same parameters.
It was also found that frequency responses of different generators are similar in terms of transfer function
phase at the frequencies of oscillatory modes. To a great extent this is because the generators have the same
excitation systems with only slight differences in parameters. Because of this similarity different generators
have been equipped with PSS of identical parameters. The dynamic performance has then been assessed and
found to be satisfactory.
It was observed that as the wind turbine penetration increases the number of oscillatory modes decreases.
Trivially, this demonstrates that power generation technology that interfaces with the grid via power convert-
ers does not directly participate in oscillations. This holds true not only for wind turbines with converters
and photovoltaic inverters but also for any generation that is behind a power converter. However, this is not
considered a valid argument to support a claim that wind turbines have a positive effect on power system
oscillations in all possible cases and operating conditions. It is borne in mind that at present there are
limitations to wind turbine analysis conducted with the NEVA® module. Even that there is no single deﬁnite
effect of wind power on small-signal stability it is not safe to assume that introduction of wind power will
not degrade small-signal stability without extensive analysis of the speciﬁc system and case.
The current version of NEVA® Eigenvalue and Modal Analysis tool, v.12.0, suffers from a number
of limitations and ﬂaws which signiﬁcantly decrease its utility. There is a large number of software-wise
issues, unrelated to engineering or mathematics which, however, make the tool not user-friendly. The
documentation of NEVA® is rather limited and does not inform the possible user of the current limitations
of the software. There is a great contrast between the level of sophistication of PSS®E and the NEVA®
module. It is obvious that PSS®E has undergone great improvement while the NEVA® module is yet at

an early stage of development. A possible reason for this is the limited scope of the software and limited
interest in eigenvalue analysis tools by the industry.
