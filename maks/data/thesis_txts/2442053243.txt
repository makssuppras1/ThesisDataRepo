Abstract
Brown algae are photosynthetic multicellular marine organisms with a singular
cell wall. It is composed by diﬀerent carbohydrates. Some of them can be found
in plants; like cellulose, in animals; like fucose-containing sulfated polysaccharides
and some other are unique to their nature, such as the alginates(Michel et al.,
2010a,b). Even though the composition of the extracellular matrix of brown algae
remains unclear, very accurate models have been designed(Deniaud-Bou¨et et al.,
2014)
In the recent years, these organisms have started to raise an interest within
the scientiﬁc community due to their great industrial potential for production of
hydrolytic enzymes (Stiger-Pouvreau et al., 2016). Conventional methods used
to bio-reﬁne the macroalgae cell wall present often inconveniences which raise up
the need of ﬁnding alternatives to this process (Arasi et al., 2016).Due to the
complexity of the matrix, it is well known that a mixture of enzymes is needed
in order to break it down. As marine fungi are known to produce extracellular
hydrolytic enzymes, they have been chosen, in the present work, as an alternative
to conventional ﬁsico-chemical methods for the algaes breakage.
Three marine fungi, P. salina, D. gaudefroyi, and A. fuci, were used to harvest
enzymes and deﬁne their performance in speciﬁc substrates.Preliminary testswere
carried out in order to know more about these selected fungi, as well as to study
their potential to degrade diﬀerent algae-based substrates.These fungi were grown
in S. latissima. Their secreted enzymes were collectedand evaluated by screening
for enzymes in diﬀerent substrates at diﬀerent pH and temperatures.
The reducing sugars assay was used to deﬁne the enzyme performance when re-
acting with ﬁve speciﬁc substrates: galactomannan, xylan, pectin, carboxymethyl-
cellulose and fucose. This assay was also used to deﬁne enzymatic performance
==PAGE:6==
when reacting directly in S. latissima.P. salina, and A. fuci showed positive ex-
tracellular activity with galactomannases, xylanases, pectinases, carboxymethyl-
cellulases and fucoidanases. The highest activity in P. salina was found in xylan,
followed bycmc, galactomannan, fucidan and pectin. On the other hand,for A.
fuci, the highest activity was found in cmc, followed by xylan, galactomannan,
fucoidan, and pectin.
An assay with alginate lyase was perfomed at diﬀerent temperatures and pHs.
P. salina was found to have the highest positive activity at pH 6, 30 °C, followed
by pH 7 at 50 °C. On the other hand, A. fuci showed only activity for alginate
lyaseat pH 8 and a temperature of 30 °C.
The comparisonamong enzymes obtained from liquid and solid-state cultures
was performed with A. fuci. The highest enzyme activity was found when produc-
ing enzymes in solid-state fermentation.
Enzymatic synergy of our mixture of enzymes was tested in an experimental
assay with Cellic ctec2 from Sigma Aldrich. This mixture of enzymes enhaces our
produced enzymatic cocktail.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Seaweed biotechnology
The seaweed biotechnology ﬁeld has importantly developed in the last decade, to
a point where algae-derived products could potentially be cultivated, processed,
reﬁned and manufactured to supply the world population (Borowitzka, 1992).
Algae biomass exhibit several advantages when compared to plant-based biomass.
Firstly, it has the advantage of growing rapidly in diverse types of water. They can
be used as food and feed, and valuable chemical compounds can be obtained from
them (Vidal, 2012). In addition, algae are capable of using energy from light to
anabolize structural polysaccharides, which provide their characteristic strength
and ﬂexibility (Borowitzka, 1992).
These polysaccharides are found to have a
broad range of applications, thus their isolation from algae cell wall is considered
a biotechnological process (Xu et al., 2017).
Up to date, the physical and/or chemical methods are the most proﬁtable
bioreﬁnery processes involved in the breakdown of the cell wall and the extraction

of the desired compounds (Xu et al., 2017). Alternatively, this biomass can be
processed using bioreﬁnery methods, where enzymes are used as a green technology.
Accordingly, microbes have been a recurrent source for enzyme production due to
their diversity, ease of genetic manipulation, and the well-known conditions for
their cultivation in laboratories (Steinberg et al., 2017).
Hypothetically, producing enzymes from marine fungi, which share their en-
vironment with algae biomass, could increase the advantages of the bioreﬁnery
process. Moreover, by secretion of hydrolytic enzymes and the mechanical force
applied by the hyphal growth, ﬁlamentous fungi are capable of penetrating diverse
solid substrates (Money, 2001).
1.2
Algae
Based on a morphological deﬁnition of algae, they are organisms that contain
chlorophyll. Algae group a wide range of unicellular and multicellular organisms,
with 37,000 currently described (Guiry, 2012), including pond scums, snow algae,
terrestrial algae, freshwater and marine phytoplacton and seaweeds (Bladby et al.,
2017).
Seaweeds are exploited for both food and non-food applications. In partic-
ular, seaweeds are used in the food industry due to their high concentration of
essential vitamins, trace elements, lipids, polysaccharides, enzymes, and miner-
als, when compared to terrestrial foodstuﬀs (Patia et al., 2016). Their use dates
back to the fourth century in Japan and the sixth century in China (FAO, 1981).
Moreover, seaweeds have been also exploited for medicinal purposes, animal feed
or agricultural proﬁt for thousands of years (Fleurence and Levine, 2016). More
recently, they are considered as a third generation feedstock for the production
of biofuels, cultured as a low-cost, high-energetic and entirely renewable (of Ed-
inburgh, 2009). During this process, valuable co-products (bioactive lipids, agar,
alginate, pigments...), proﬁtable for pharmaceuticals, animal feed, and paper and

feasible process (Sharma and Sharma, 2017). Seaweeds are cheap to grow with the
economical investment of the process coming upon the extraction and processing
of their valuable compounds (Bleakley and Hayes, 2017). The global market is
expected to reach up to $17.59 billion in 2021 (Mac Monagail et al., 2017).
Seaweeds are multicellular marine algae classiﬁed in three diﬀerent groups:

1.3
Brown algae
Brown algae are distinguished by their greenish brown color given by the carotenoid
photosynthetic pigment fucoxanthin, chlorophyll A and C (Patia et al., 2016).
These pigments are found in chloroplasts with thylakoids in stacks of three, con-
tained in a girdle lamella (Wehr, 2014). To date, 1800 species of brown algae have
been described, most of them found in marine environments, predominately in
colder waters. The order Laminares and Fucales are the most harvested (Rioux
and Turgeon, 2015).
Their size can vary from 1 to more than 100 meters (Britannica, 2018). Their
food reserves are complex polysaccharides, laminarin being the main storage car-
bohydrate (Fleurence, 2004). They contain components shared with plants (celul-
lose) and animals (sulfated fucans), as well as unique ones such as alginates (Michel
et al., 2010a). Alginate is the main component of the cell wall, diﬀering with other

1.4
S. latissima
Saccharina latissima (Fucus saccharinus, Linnaeus, 1753) is a brown seaweed with
high value in the market due to its high range of compounds that can be found
in mono-, oligo-, and polymeric composition (Sterner and Edlund, 2016). It is the
fastest-growing brown algae by producing spores and a high-biomass cultivation
is possible using biotechnology. It is possible to produce 20-30 tons dry weight
biomass per hectare per annum (Kraan, 2016).
It is found in the northern Paciﬁc Ocean, northern Atlantic Ocean, and occa-
sionally in warmer waters of the Mediterranean Sea and Brazil. In the northern
hemisphere, it is found in rocky coastal ecosystems, ranging from temperate to
polar (Tiwari and Troy, 2015). It also grows in northern-European waters includ-
ing Denmark (Marinho et al., 2015). In accordance, this brown algae is used as
biomass for this project.
”It can grow up to 3 meters in length, with a claw-like holdfast, a small, smooth,
ﬂexible stipe, and an undivided laminate blade with parallel, ruﬄed sides and an
elongated, tongue-like appearance” (Groenendijk et al., 2016). Depressions in its
frond are quite characteristic, the stipe is relatively small, cylindrical in section,
and more ﬂexible than the other species described in the order Laminaria (Lund,
2014).

1.5.1
Carbohydrates
There is a growing interest in the carbohydrates found in brown algae when search-
ing for an alternative biomass resource (Brownand Tustin 2010; Dave et al.2013).
Carbohydrates can be classiﬁed in two major types deﬁned by its diﬀerence
in functionality. Both structural carbohydrates (such as alginates, fucoidans and
cellulose) and storage carbohydrates (such as mannitol and laminarin) compose
the cell wall (Synytsya et al., 2015).

Structural carbohydrates
Alginates
Alginates account for the 40 % dry weight (Phillips and Williams, 2009). They
are comprised by two diﬀerent monomers: guluronic acid (G) and mannuronic
acid (M) (Rhein-Knudsen et al., 2015) (Lobban and Wynne, 1981). The former
provides mechanical support due to its rigidity, while the latter provides ﬂexibility.
Based on their spatial arrangement, three diﬀerent blocks are found, G-blocks
consecutively binded to G residues, M-blocks with consecutive M residues, and
MG-blocks ﬁnding M and G residues alternatively arranged, together with mixed
salts such as sodium, magnesium, calcium, and others (Figure 1.3).
They have a high range of applications related to their physical properties.
In Laminaria species, alginate is found with high M/G ratio (Haug et al., 1974).
This is related to its low gelifying properties which, together with their colloidal
properties, makes alginates suitable for several applications in biotechnology as
well as in the food-processing, medical and pharmaceutical industries (McHugh,
2003).

varies according to the extraction process, season of harvest, and local climatic
conditions (Mabeau et al., 1990; Honya et al., 1999; Ale et al., 2011).

is found when xylose, galactose, mannose, and glucuronic acid are present (Jiao et
al., 2011). The most common composition found are the hetopolymers (fucoidans)

Storage carbohydrates are deﬁned as storage metabolites that do not have an
immediate role and are accumulated in the organism. They are only metabolized
when there is a lack of energetic resources (Stiger-Pouvreau et al., 2016).
Carbon storage appear in both monomeric and polymeric forms. In brown
algae, the C-storage source is present in polymeric form which allows each cell
to have smaller osmotic pressure compared to its equivalent in monomeric carbon
forms (Stiger-Pouvreau et al., 2016).

et al., 2015).

β-(1,6)-intrachain links (Kadam et al., 2015). The reported content of laminarin
in brown algae is up to 35 % dry weight (Mautner, 1954).
There are two types of laminarin: M-chain end is substituted by a D-mannitol,
whereas G-chains end with glucose as a reducing end (Figure 1.4).
Laminarin has antitumour, anti-apoptotic, anti-inﬂammatory, anticoagulant,
and antioxidant activity.
It can also be suitable for food applications (Kadam

Discussion and conclusions
4.1
Fungi environmental factors
The study of the growth media will lead to the optimization of the process to pro-
duce a larger amount of fungi. If the speciﬁc nutrition requirements of the organ-
isms are known, the growth media can be supplemented and therefore the division
of the fungi will be faster. This will turn out to reduce the cost in large-scale
fermentations used in biotechnological processes (Basu et al., 2015). Filamentous
fungi play a major role in biotechnology regarding its adaptations to diverse en-
vironmental conditions (Childress et al., 1998). During evolution, organisms have
developed a wide range of adaptations, either to survive to changes in their cur-
rent environment or to colonize new habitats. Plasticity can be a common feature
in a group of organisms but can also happen in each individual specie (Bate-
son, 2015)Accordingly, they have been used in biotechnology only in the recent
years, and its taxonomically and biochemically characterization is under research
(Damare et al., 2012). Thus, our selected marine fungi undergo several prelimi-
nary experiments to the screening of enzymes in order to get a better knowledge
of them as they remain to be one of the least understood group in the sea habitat.
The eﬀect of salinity was the ﬁrst aspect to study. Salinity is the main factor

4.2. ALGAE BIOREFINERY
4.2
Algae bioreﬁnery
The most commonly methods used to extract polysaccharides accountswith some
inconveniences such as high temperatures, slow extraction rates, damages to the
polysaccharides, and diﬃculties on the separation (Arasi et al., 2016). Moreover,
structural modiﬁcations can occur when extracting a polysaccharide, which can
aﬀect as a bioactive compound (Garcia-Vaquero et al., 2017).
Recent studies
have developed pretreatments in the algae biomass by using commercial enzymes
(Gupta et al., 2017).
Enzymes can be used for polysaccharides extraction from the algae cell wall.
Some studies address the beneﬁts of using this method when comparing it to
conventional ones (Horn et al., 2006).
High eﬃciency, energy, quicker process,
and targeted extraction are some of the main features that increase the interest of
using enzymes. However, it is well-known that in order to breakdown the complex
matrix of the algae cell wall, a mixture of enzymes and not a single one is needed
to treat this biomass. Enzymatic methods are expensive(Arasi et al., 2016), thus
they are not suitable for industrial proﬁtable process. Alternatively, fungi produce
wide range of extracellular enzymes under certain conditions. No previous reports
are available on fungal crude enzyme methods to treat the algae biomass, until
Sanjeev Kumar Prajapati et al. (2015) studied two fungal strains for production
of crude enzymes using sugarcane bagasse for algae biomass pretreatment. The
research showed this method as promising forbioreﬁny algae biomass (Prajapati
et al., 2015). Enzyme activity from all three organisms in algae were found. D.
gaudefroyi had the lowest quantity of enzyme activity due to their slow-growth
rate compared to the other two fungi. Activities were found at diﬀerent ranges
of temperature and pH. This translates into the presence of not only one but
a mixture of diﬀerent enzymesfrom the 3 fungi in the presence of S. latissima.
In the present work, the evaluation of extracellular activity of glycosyl hydrolase
was determinated by using reducing sugars assays in diﬀerent substrates. From

Enzymatic synergy
The complexity of the algae requires several enzymes, working on synergy, for its
hydrolysis. This complexity not only comes within the substrate components, but
also with their seasonal, environmental, and structural variations. Recent studies

4.4
Liquid-state vs solid-state fermentation
The most common way to produce fungal products is in liquid fermentation. There
is, however, a huge interest in using solid-state fermentation (SSF) as higher
enzyme concentrations are found when comparing to liquid-state fermentations
(Viniegra-Gonz´alez et al., 2003). Furthermore, SSF accounts with several beneﬁts
for industrial production for instance, low waste of water, low energy expenditure,
less expensive downstream processing and potential higher volumetric productiv-
ity, etc.. (Gowthaman et al., 2001). The results obtained during this project are in
accordance with the above statement as it is clearly observed that the production
of enzymes is higher when using solid-state fermentation.

Chapter 5
Conclusions and future work
The carbohydrate-degrading enzymes in marine fungi compromise a mixture that
could potentially be used to breakdown the cell wall of the brown algae. The fungi
studies during this project did not produce produce enough enzyme to replace
other current industrial conventional methods. However, the mixture of enzymes
collected from P. salina, and A. fuci could be used in synergy with the commercial
enzyme Cellic ctect2, to enhace enzyme activity.
Furthermore, the characterization of the composition of the enzymatic mix-
ture is highly recommended. Future discoveries using genomics, transcriptomic,
proteomic, and interatomic analyses are excellent tools to study the carbohydrate-
degrading enzymes from this poorly explored group of organisms.
Solid-state culture seem to lead to a higher enzyme production. Further studies
of this system are needed.
Techniques such as microscopy or DNA test could
overcome the handicap of having an heterogeneous biomass.
