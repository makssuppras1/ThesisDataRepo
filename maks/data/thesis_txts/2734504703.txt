Abstract
This thesis introduces an approach for reducing stresses in suspension bridge girders and
similar structures, in particular, to address the issue of critical fatigue cracks observed
in Denmark’s Lillebælt Bridge. The approach employs the Maxwell-Betti theorem to re-
veal regions of influential character towards stress reduction, implemented through Finite
Element Analysis with MATLAB and ABAQUS software. Verified through systematic de-
velopment and testing of the approach, followed by a final application to a finite element
model of a section of the Lillebælt Bridge, the approach is proven to achieve a substantial
stress-relieving effect. This is exemplified by an achieved stress reduction of 39% in a criti-
cal welded connection. While proven effective, utilizing the approach should be cautiously
considered alongside structural and practical impacts before real-world application.
The thesis contributes to the sustainability and longevity of bridges and other civil struc-
tures by suggesting directions for future research of maintenance measures and has the
potential to make a substantial contribution to the field of structural optimization.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The Lillebælt Bridge is a suspension bridge connecting the Danish islands Funen and
Jutland, completed in 1970. Since the opening of the bridge, the daily traﬀic volume has
progressively risen from approximately 15,000 to 80,000 vehicles in 2020. This is considered
close to the maximum capacity [1]. Traﬀic loads, particularly from heavy vehicles, induce
substantial fatigue stresses in the bridge deck. After more than 50 years, this has resulted
in cracks in the structure beneath the slow lane of the Lillebælt Bridge deck. Potential
solutions to the identified problem are already being explored and evaluated, to which this
thesis contributes.
Problem objective
The purpose of this study is to create an approach for reducing critical stresses in bridge
girders, specifically rooted in the issues with fatigue stresses of the Lillebælt Bridge, to
minimize the level of maintenance and to extend the lifespan of the Lillebælt Bridge and
similar structures.
Based on the Maxwell-Betti theorem, the approach draws parallels to the theory of influ-
ence lines and involves revealing zones of influential character towards stress reduction.
The approach will not only be systematically described, tested, and verified on simple
test domains employing Finite Element Method (FEM), it will also be applied to a FEM
model of a section of the Lillebælt Bridge deck to demonstrate the practical impact.
This thesis marks a novel approach within this field of study, namely, utilizing the Maxwell-
Betti theorem concerning stress reduction as an influence of design modification. A method
for direct determination of influence lines in FE software, based on the Maxwell-Betti
theorem, is described by professor in structural mechanics, PhD Lars Damkilde [2]. The
principles of Damkilde’s approach serve as the basis for this new approach to the Maxwell-
Betti theorem.
1.1
Sustainability
In recent centuries, it has become clear that there is a necessity to address climate concerns
and acknowledge our responsibility for the environmental impact on Earth. According to
World Green Building Council [3], the built sector is globally responsible for 35% of energy
consumption, 38% of energy-related carbon emissions, and 50% of resource consumption.
Agreements on the EU level as well as the United Nation’s Sustainable Development Goals
(SDGs) [4] acknowledge the significance of this matter. In recent years, there has been a
noticeable interest in taking action from industries, including the construction industry.
COWI A/S exemplifies this trend with a new strategy, striving towards a sustainable future
and becoming carbon neutral by 2050 [5]. COWI, which stands for Chr. Ostenfeld & W.
Jønson, is an international consulting group with expertise in engineering, environmental
science, and economics. Established in 1930, COWI played a crucial role as the designer

Prioritizing sustainability in the construction industry is a complex matter, as pursuing
a sustainable design may sometimes conflict with considerations of time and financial re-

1.2
Finite Element modelling
Within the field of structural analysis, internal forces and deformations as a reaction
to outer provocations can be described from differential equations.
For more complex
structures, it becomes diﬀicult to solve the systems analytically, and more comprehensive
numerical methods must be utilized. FEM serves as a numerical tool for achieving ap-
proximate solutions for structures with realistic geometries and boundary conditions. It
builds on the principle of dividing the structure into smaller elements, of which a set of
static principles and mathematical equations describes the behaviour. By evaluating each
element, the behaviour of the entire system can be described. Today, much commercial
software builds on FEM, employing Finite Element Analysis (FEA) for solving complex
structures for designing or redesigning purposes [7].
FEM serves as a tool for describing, testing, and verifying the developed approach of
this thesis. Coding with MATLAB and the commercial software ABAQUS is utilized.
ABAQUS builds on a user-friendly interface with three-dimensional model visualizations
that simplify the application of material parameters, boundary conditions, and load cases.
This facilitates the modelling of complicated structures by handling the computation of the
Finite Element (FE) equations behind the user interface. Using commercial software for
handling FEA limits the risk of human errors. However, in order to achieve a satisfactory
level of accurate results, a proper understanding of the theory behind the method is
essential [7].
A detailed model of a section of the Lillebælt Bridge will be used to exemplify the ap-

2.3
The stiffening girder and its function
In this study, the structural focus will be on the stiffening bridge girder, as structural
challenges have been identified in this region of the Lillebælt Bridge.
The bridge deck in a cable-supported bridge consists of a stiffening girder.
Vertically, the girder must be able to transfer loads to the supporting cable system, carry
loads locally between cable supports, and distribute concentrated forces to minimize the
impact of large stresses and deformations.
Horizontally, moving traﬀic and asymmetric loading, as well as wind, earthquakes, and
other environmental factors, introduce lateral and axial forces. The girder must handle
axial forces by ensuring proper buckling stiffness. Lateral forces must be taken as lateral
bending in the deck for bridges with a self-anchored cable system or as an interaction
between the deck and cable system for bridges with an earth-anchored cable system. The
last category applies to the case of the Lillebælt Bridge.
Torsional moments are introduced as loads are applied on either side of the deck. The
torsional stiffness relies heavily on the support offered by the cable system. When two
cable planes are present on each side of the deck, like in the case of the Lillebælt Bridge,
the cables support against torsional moments on each side of the deck. Some bridges are
equipped with just a single central cable plane, for which the torsional stiffness relies on
the stiffness of the deck itself [10].

2.3.2
Fatigue
As bridges are constantly exposed to various loads and environmental challenges, these
dynamic and sometimes unpredictable conditions can induce cyclic loads on the bridge
components and give rise to fatigue failure over time [10].
Cyclic loading introduces a span of stresses, also referred to as the stress range, ∆σR,
spanning from the minimum stress level to the maximum stress level applied. A load cycle
is characterized as a sequence of loading and unloading with time, outlining the stress
range as the amplitude of this cycle [14].
The fatigue strength of a component is defined by the size of the stress range, which will
cause failure of the member after a certain amount of load cycles. Conversely, fatigue life
is defined as the number of cycles that cause a component to fail with a certain level of
stress range for every cycle [14]. For evaluation of fatigue resistance, fatigue assessment,
and design, the design codes [15] recommend the nominal stress approach, which is based
on the Palmgren-Miner cumulative damage rule and the code specific S-N curves. The
S-N curves describe a relationship between the fatigue strength, S, and fatigue life, N,
accompanied by a detail-specific factor [16].
The design code provides several detail categories for orthotropic steel decks as they are
vulnerable to fatigue cracking in the welded connections [16]. This is due to small dis-
continuities from an uneven or irregular welding process [17] or because sharp changes in
the direction concentrate the stresses at the root or toe of butt or fillet welds. Figure 2.8
visualises this phenomena.

CHAPTER 3
Theory of approach
Implementing design changes in a structure, whether by adjusting member positions,
adding or removing material, or altering material strength, undoubtedly influences the
forces distribution of the structure. Structural optimization plays a central role in the de-
sign phase of a new project, implementing and simulating the effect of the changes ahead
of production. However, design modifications applied to already existing structures are
often not integrated into routine maintenance practices, even though minor adjustments
could potentially offer significant preventive benefits.
3.1
Optimization of structures
In today’s society, there is a strong emphasis on sustainability as well as cost in the de-
sign phase of any structural-related project. Structural optimization complements both
design areas, which modern technology of today makes highly accessible, employing CAD
(computer-aided design), modern FEM (Finite Element Method) tools, and AI (artificial
intelligence) [18]. At its core, optimization of structural designs generally means enhancing
its strength, reducing weight, minimizing cost, or achieving other desired criteria within
a set of boundary constraints. Within the field of finite element analysis (FEA), topol-
ogy optimization utilizes mathematical methods for distributing a domain’s material for
an optimized shape [19] [20]. More eﬀicient use of algorithms and modern interpolation
methodologies for topology optimization are being employed in computational frameworks
[21], and multiple vendors within the CAD software industry are incorporating the method
of Generative Design into their programs. The generative design method (GDM) itera-
tively generates and explores the solution space for various diverse designs within a set
of requirements. By means of parametric search and evolutionary-based algorithms, it
iteratively recognises and builds upon previous achievements to guide the search towards
an optimized solution. This method significantly reduces design time and creates solu-
tions not restrained by conventional design methods or the imagination of the designer,
often resulting in designs resembling structures observed in nature [18]. Figure 3.1 shows
an example from engineering.com of the Golden Gate Bridge in San Francisco redesigned
with the generative design method.
Figure 3.1: The Golden Gate Bridge redesigned with Autodesk’s generative design [22].

3.2
Influence lines
Influence lines can serve as a tool for identifying the most critical position of a load, as
they illustrate how a specific point is affected (influenced) by a varying load placement
throughout a structure. Visually, an influence line is a graph running along the structure,
revealing the level of effect that each load position will cause on a chosen point of interest.
Figure 3.2 shows a moment influence line for the specific point of interest, i, in a simply
supported beam of 3 spans:
Figure 3.2: Example of a moment influence line for a three-span simply supported beam.
As seen in fig. 3.2, placing a load precisely at point i will exert the most significant positive
influence at point i, resulting in the most significant positive moment at point i. Placing
a load in the mid-span will result in the greatest negative influence, leading to the most
significant negative moment in point i.
Various methods can be employed for creating influence lines. The most obvious is cal-
culating and collecting the resulting forces in a specific point i for different load cases.
However, this approach can be extensive for complicated structures, even with modern
computer- and FE technology. Alternatively, the Müller-Breslau method can be utilized.
This approach defines the influence line for a specific point from a single load case by re-
leasing a degree of freedom and displaying the displacement field of the considered virtual
system. This method was described by Cifuentes in 1990 [23] in relation to FE calcu-
lations. However, this approach requires modification of boundary conditions for each
desired influence line within an existing model, a process that can be notably ineﬀicient
and labour-intensive. A more direct determination of influence lines in relation to FE soft-
ware was argued for by Jepsen in 2018 [24], based on Damkilde’s work from 2002 [2]. In

CHAPTER 4
Influence of design changes
This chapter systematically develops, tests, and verifies a new approach for revealing
zones of influential character towards stress reduction. The new approach is based on the
Maxwell-Betti theorem, specifically Betti’s law, and will be termed the Betti theoretical
approach throughout this thesis.
In alignment with the theory of influence lines, Betti’s law is utilized, combining the
internal and external works of one system with the internal and external work of a second
system. In this study, the first system, named System 0, corresponds to the actual load
situation and its resulting displacements. The second system, System 1, is established to
gain specific knowledge. In system 1, external virtual work is applied to a particular region
of interest, reflecting the intended impact of the design modification. For example, if the
objective is to impact the stresses of region i in the x-direction, work should be applied to
region i, simulating this event. Throughout this thesis, the index i marks the position of
interest, while the index j denotes any position throughout the structure with potential
influential character.
Parallel to the direct approach described by Damkilde [2], boundary conditions remain
the same for both systems, engaging two different load cases to reveal an influence rate of
various regions throughout the entire structure.
All calculations in this chapter are conducted in SI units.
4.1

and direct access to the mechanics of the calculations, for which initial simple testing of

In ABAQUS, the following technique is generally employed in applying the Betti theoret-
ical approach for structural analysis:
The structural model is created, boundary conditions are applied, and the structure is
meshed with appropriate finite elements. Subsequently, systems 0 and 1 are individually
configured within what is termed a load ”step” in ABAQUS. During these load steps,
boundary conditions and mesh configurations are propagated, ensuring that each step
shares identical integration points for the FE calculations. After analysis of the model,
the results of each step, systems 0 and 1, are combined according to the Betti theoretical
approach applicable to the chosen type of elements. The numerical result of this combi-
nation is for each FE node displayed in what is termed a new ”output field” in ABAQUS.

5.1.4
Model accuracy
For the sake of result evaluation, it is important to recognize the accuracy of and limitations
associated with the girder model.
The decision to model just a 62 m section of the 1080 m suspension Lillebælt Bridge
introduces certain limitations in capturing the comprehensive effects and interactions as-
sociated with the global structural behaviour of the entire bridge. Critical aspects such
as deflections and accurate distribution and redistribution of loads are hereby neglected.
Reference points were used by the vertical supports to represent the cables supporting
the deck, but their stiffness may still oversimplify the flexible behaviour of the actual
global cable system in the bridge, leading to misinterpretation of the load distribution.
Transverse and axial supports were added in addition to Scotto di Mase’s boundary con-
ditions to prevent rigid body motion; however, their stiffness and constraints also lack
global representation. A possible solution to this matter could have been to model the
full bridge, having a fine mesh and detailed geometry by the trough section and limiting
the complexity and amount of elements in the remaining bridge for computational ease.
The mesh of the model already consists of a more coarse mesh in the outer region and a
progressively refined mesh closer to the trough section and critical weld. The coarse mesh
does not deliver high accuracy in terms of stresses but does serve its purpose of acting
with the right stiffness towards the critical weld. The refined mesh in the critical weld,
however, is found to be relatively coarse compared to its calculated stress distribution, see
fig. 5.11. This increases the risk of significant stress discontinuities, which compromises
the accuracy of the model. A possible solution to this matter could have been to refine
the mesh further around the area showing high discontinuities and limit the amount of
elements found in the remaining trough section.
The composed mesh through the thickness of the diaphragm plate contains elements of
high aspect ratios, meaning dimensions far from being regular on each side. See fig. 5.18.
High aspect ratios can result in interpolation errors and lower computational accuracy. A
possible solution to this matter could be to reconstruct the mesh, limiting the number of
elements through the thickness and increasing the number of elements in the plane of the
diaphragm plate.
Reduced integration has been used to limit computational resources. Full integration, es-
pecially within the elements surrounding the area of interest, could provide more accurate
results for this study.
A convergence test could be employed to determine the accuracy level of the model.
Convergence in a model is achieved when it approaches stable and accurate results verified
through multiple iterations of calculation.
Even though the model exhibits some concerns with respect to accuracy, the main ob-
jective of this study is to identify relative differences for indicative purposes, prioritizing
this rather than achieving numerical accuracy aligned with reality. The goal is not to
simulate the exact stress level within the weld but rather to assess the influence of mod-
ifications. Consequently, and due to time constraints, the proposed solutions to enhance
model accuracy have not been given priority.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
The developed Betti theoretical approach of this thesis has the possibility of being eﬀi-
ciently incorporated into proactive maintenance measures of structures. The results of
chapter 5 shows a significant impact of applying the approach within the targeted scope,
reducing the stresses by the weld root of the critical weld by 39%.
Beyond influencing the structural durability and longevity of existing structures like the
Lillebælt Bridge, the approach holds the potential to enhance current optimization tech-
niques. Other optimization methods, like generative design, build on the fitness of pre-
vious successful analyses and their effectiveness, thereby relying on multiple iterations
before being noticeably eﬀicient with respect to the specific design. This new approach
immediately reveals a direction for potential optimizing design modifications, based on
the initial design, which thereby reduces computational time substantially.
However, while the approach is based on the behaviour of the entire structure, it does
not provide holistic optimization insights for the entire domain simultaneously. At the
moment it is limited to only producing results with respect to the x, y or z-direction, one
at a time, and for the specifically selected load case. A slight modification in the design
generates a new distribution of loads and deformations, which creates a new image of the
influence rate throughout the structure. Implementing changes should thereby be done
iteratively, with small steps at a time, to minimize the risk of new complications. Two
numerical analyses for the two coupled systems must be made for every iteration, but its
indicative nature still substantially accelerates the process of optimizing a structure.
It is important to state that any proposed new designs or modifications of existing struc-
tures based on this approach should undergo thorough consideration, testing, and evalua-
tion before implementation. The applied design modification to the FEM girder model of
the Lillebælt Bridge is, therefore, far from being a final solution proposal. Not only should
the possibility of new stress concentrations or, by other means, weakening of the structure
be taken into account, but also practical aspects. The currently preferred choice by COWI
of making an additional weld on the inside of the troughs is chosen with respect to multiple
considerations: documented effect, impact on the total self-weight of the bridge, ease of
execution, influence on bridge traﬀic, and cost-effectiveness. Not only should a potential
solution derived from the Betti theoretical approach have holistically verified structural
benefits, but it should also be thoroughly considered with respect to these key factors for
the possibility of being a practical reality.
Deveoplment of the approach
A systematic description, testing, and verification form the scientific basis for developing
the new Betti theoretical approach in this thesis. Initially, applying the theory to simpler
structures allows for more analytically comprehensive results and easy troubleshooting.
The complexity of the tests gradually increases as the results of the previous test are proven
satisfactory. Not only are the various tests important for reaching an understanding of
Reduction of Stresses in Bridge Girder by application of Maxwell-Betti principle in Finite Element Model

the approach, but they also serve as continuous verification of the different techniques and
approaches employed.
Whether a result is satisfactory enough to move forward depends on the problem objec-
tive. As the scope of the approach is to detect regions of relatively influential character,
achieving numerical accuracy plays a less critical part as opposed to the indicative nature
of the results. Therefore, the consistency test plays an essential part in verifying the ap-
proach. The negativity or positivity of the influence rate, IfR, as well as the size, should
correspond relatively to the level of influence that the region is manually found to impose.
The approach is also observed to be compatible with various structural scopes. Whether
normal force relative to area or stress relative to thickness or stiffness is engaged, the IfR
plots of the different tests show significant alignment. This further verifies the theory and
shows agility within the approach.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Based on the discovered fatigue issues of the Lillebælt Bridge, a new approach rooted in
the Maxwell-Betti theorem is successfully developed. It serves as an indicative tool for
discovering potential design modifications for the reduction of critical stresses.
The approach is proven effective by being applied to a FEM model of a section of the
Lillebælt Bridge, successfully reducing the critical stresses of a welded connection by 39%.
By means of no thorough consideration of the structural and practical impact, the con-
ducted design modification solely serves as an exemplification of applying the developed
approach.
The approach not only demonstrates particular effectiveness in terms of proactive main-
tenance measures for existing structures but can also be integrated into procedures of
structural optimization in the initial design phase.
7.1
Future work
For further studies, it would be interesting to dive into the effect of increasing the com-
plexity of the test domain on the accuracy of the results. The deviations between the
Betti theoretical and manual methods are highly dependent on the size of the manual
modification, but this is, however, only in the verifying state of the approach. Too high
numerical deviations or inconsistent results due to the complexity of the structure would
be problematic, but interestingly, when increasing the complexity going from a 2D mem-
brane structure to a 3D configuration, the deviation between the methods did not seem
to increase but rather decreased. This is, however, only based on the two tests conducted
in this thesis, which is a very limited statistical foundation. The successful reduction of
stresses in the complex girder model further shows the eﬀiciency of the approach.
Due to time constraints, a proper iterative application process of the Betti theoretical
approach onto the girder model was not achieved. For further development of the works of
this thesis and in order to conduct a proper redesign suggestion, multiple iteration rounds
of applying the approach to the girder model and modifying the design thereafter could
be done. Combined with optimization tools and held against practical implementation
considerations, this could potentially deliver a better solution to the described fatigue
issues than the one investigated by COWI today. For this, it would also be interesting to
look into the modern trough designs and the impact of cope holes with respect to fatigue
on the welded connections.
The investigations in this thesis have been confined to the x-direction. As deliberated
upon in the discussion, employing all three directions is essential for creating a more
holistic image of the influence rate throughout the structure, even though one direction
is often found more critical than others.
A design modification might have a desired
impact with respect to one direction but an undesired impact with respect to another.
For future work and more accessible use of the Betti theoretical approach, a system for
revealing alignment between the influence rate of the different directions could be created.

Alternative method for plane structures

approach, and this technique was not given further attention, the errors have

A method for deriving influence rate in relation to plane structures is described. This
method utilizes force and displacement in relation to revealing changes in strain relative
to changes in thickness and area length.
This serves as an alternative to the method
described in section 4.4, which employs stress and strain in relation to revealing change in
stress relative to change in thickness.
A demonstration and verification making use of Matlab is presented, also covering a con-
sistency test for the approach.
E.1
Theory of approach
Betti’s law is employed do describe the influence of changing the design of a membrane
structure, combining the external and internal work of two systems. The external work
consists of the loads imposed on the systems, representing the influence of the design
change.
The internal work refers to the consequential deformations induced by these
external loads in their respective systems. Once again, the systems are denoted system 0
and system 1. The area of interest is denoted i, and the area subjected to a design change
is denoted j. The Betti theoretical combination of the two systems will be s follows:
