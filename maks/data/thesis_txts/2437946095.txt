Abstract
A major consequence of hearing impairment is the inability to detect low-level
sounds while high-level sounds have the same perceived loudness as for normal hearing
people, referred to as a reduced dynamic range.
Compression schemes are used in
hearing aids to compensate for the reduced dynamic range by applying a level-depended
gain function. A recent study by Hassager et al. (2017a) showed that conventional fast-
acting compression introduced distortions leading to a broadening of the sound source
as well as image splits. They introduced a new compression system, termed "direct-to-
reverberant-ratio (DRR)-aware" compression, which was shown to preserve the spatial
impression of sound sources.
The DRR-aware compressor processes the direct and
reverberant parts using diﬀerent time constants. Fast-acting compression was applied
to the direct part while slow-acting compression was applied to reverberant components
of the sound. However, the DRR-aware compression system was only evaluated in terms
of spatial perception in a single-source scenario.
In this study, the beneﬁt of the DDR-aware compression on speech intelligibility
and listening eﬀort was investigated. The goal of the present study was to investigate the
impact of DRR-aware compression on speech intelligibility and listening eﬀort compared
to conventional fast-acting compression. Pupil dilation was measured as an indicator of
listening eﬀort. The hypothesis was that the DRR-aware compression method preserves
the spatial image, such that less eﬀort is used in understanding speech compared to
a fast-acting system. The fast-acting compression system was expected to introduce
some distortions as a result of a broadening of the sound sources. It was expected that
the listeners will use more eﬀort in understanding speech.
Hearing-impaired listeners were tested in terms of speech intelligibility and listening
eﬀort using a speech-in-noise test. A two-talker scenario was tested as a function of room
reverberation and angular separation between the target and the interfering talker.
For a large spatial separation and low reverberation time, the results showed very
similar speech intelligibility scores between the two compression schemes. The DRR-
aware system showed higher intelligibility scores compared to the fast-acting system for
closely spaced sources and more reverberant environment. The pupil data showed no
signiﬁcant diﬀerences between the two methods.

Introduction
Motivation
Hearing loss is one of the most common physical disabilities in the world and is a
great burden to the public health system. Today around 360 million people worldwide
are suﬀering from disabling hearing loss according to the World Health Organisation
(2017).
Several studies have shown that hearing impaired (HI) people have diﬃculties
understanding speech in background noise (Plomp and Mimpen, 1979; Dubno et al.,
1984; Marrone et al., 2008), which is even more pronounced when separating one talker
in a multi-talker scenario.
The occurrence of room reverberation typically increases the diﬃculties of hearing-
impaired listeners to understand speech. Several studies have shown that people with
HI are performing worse in speech intelligibility tests compared to normal-hearing (HI)
people in reverberant environment (Yang and Hodgson, 2006; Harris and Reitz, 1985;
Irwin and McAuley, 1987), and a larger signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is needed to achieve
the same performance level (Yang and Hodgson, 2006).
In current hearing-aids, the gain used to compensate for the hearing loss is level-
dependent, meaning that low-level sounds will get a larger gain compared to high-level
sounds in order to compensate for loudness recruitment (Stone and Moore, 2003). This
level-dependent gain function is referred to as compression. A diﬃculty with this type
of hearing-aids processing is that reﬂections and room reverberation will typically be
categorized as low-level sounds and will therefore receive a larger gain than the direct
sound. This can cause distortion of the perceived source position since the reverberant
sound has been ampliﬁed which can cause image splits, i.e. perception of the sound
coming from diﬀerent places at once) (Hassager et al., 2017b).
To overcome this fundamental limitation, Hassager et al. (2017a) developed a com-
pression method which applies diﬀerent time constants to update the gain for the time-
frequency units (T-F) units dominated from the direct part, as compared to those T-F
units dominated by reﬂections and room reverberation. These time constants are com-
monly referred to as attack and release time.
With short attack and release times
,referred to as fast-acting compression, the direct part will quickly reach the desired
sound pressure level. Compressing the reverberant sounds with a long attack and re-
lease time (slow-acting compression), a longer time is used to reach the desired gain
(Stone and Moore, 1992) and will therefore keep a lower gain than for the direct part
of the sound. This system is referred to as "direct-to-reverberant-ratio aware" (DRR-
aware) compression. In the experiment by Hassager et al. (2017a), it was shown that
the natural spatial impression was preserved in the the DRR-aware compression system
compared to the conventional fast-acting compression system. It indicates that by com-
pressing the whole system using small time constants, more distortions are observed,
where a dual system is beneﬁcial with diﬀerent attack and release times for the direct
path of the sound and the parts dominated by reﬂections and room reverberation.
==PAGE:12==

2
Distortions introduced by the fast-acting compression system will not beneﬁt the
HI listeners. Especially in acoustically challenging conditions, such as a reverberant
environment, a fast-acting system will reduce the modulation depth of the signal (Shen
and Lentz, 2010; Verschuure et al., 1996; Stone and Moore, 1992) which may be detri-
mentalt for sound source separation and speech perception. Also for closely separated
sources, the DRR-aware compression algorithm has shown to maintain the spatial im-
pression of the scene (Hassager et al., 2017a).
In the present thesis, it was investigate if the DRR-aware compression algorithm
beneﬁts speech intelligibility and listening eﬀort in more realistic listening conditions.
A speech-in-noise test was used to investigate the eﬀect of the DRR-aware compression
system, together with the conventional fast-acting system, on speech intelligibility and
listening eﬀort for diﬀerent acoustic scenarios. To measure listening eﬀort, pupillometry
was used. Pupillometry is the measurement of pupil size over period of time and can
be used in a cognitive task to study listening eﬀort (Sirois and Brisson, 2014). Wendt
et al. (2017) showed a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in listening eﬀort between diﬀerent noise
reduction schemes although no diﬀerence in speech intelligibility was observed. It was
therefore interesting to investigate if there is a beneﬁt of the DRR-aware compression
method on listening eﬀort, reﬂected in reduced pupil size.
The hypotheses of the present study were as follow:
• The HI listeners will utilize less cognitive resources when the spatial impression of
the source is restores due to the DRR-aware compression scheme. In contrast, the
conventional fast-acting compression algorithm is expected to introduce spatial
distortions and thereby may make it more diﬃcult to separate diﬀerent sources,
particularly in conditions where the sources are spatially close.
• It is also expected that a more reverberant environment will cause even more dis-
tortion by the fast-acting compression as a result of the room reﬂections ampliﬁed
by the system. The diﬀerences in listening eﬀort is expected to be larger between
the DRR-aware and the fast-acting compression system with increasing amount
of reverberation.
• Conditions with small angular separation are expected to show larger diﬀerences
in listening eﬀort between the two methods, as a result of a broadening of the
sound sources introduced by the fast-acting compression method.
Intelligibility was controlled by using listener-speciﬁc SNR to achieve a speciﬁc
intelligibility score. For an intelligibility level of 100%, no diﬀerence in listening eﬀort
may be observed between the diﬀerent hearing-aid compression schemes. Therefore, it
is desirable to choose a lower intelligibility level, but still measure in a realistic listening
situation. Wendt et al. (2018) showed that too low levels of intelligibility will decrease
the pupil size, since the listener is using less motivation presumably due to the diﬃculty
of the task.

Background
2.1
Ear and hearing loss
The ears are a paired organ which consists of an external, middle and inner ear. The
external and middle ear have the function of capturing, transmitting and amplifying
sound, while the inner ear is transforming the sound waves into electrical stimuli which
are further sent to the auditory cortex located in the temporal area in the brain (Porth,
2011).
According to the World Health Organisation (2017), there are more than 360 mil-
lion people in the world suﬀering from severe hearing loss, which is one of the most
common physical disabilities.
Our hearing is measured in decibel (dB) with a reference of 0 dB for normal hearing
(NH). Hearing loss is deﬁned as an increase in sound level of 20 dB or more in order
to be able to detect sounds at diﬀerent frequencies (Porth, 2011). There are diﬀerent
degrees of hearing loss; mild (26 to 40 dB), moderate (41-55 dB), severe (71-90 dB),
and profound (> 91 dB) (Porth, 2011), as illustrated in Figure 1.

The most common type of hearing loss is called sensorineural hearing loss and is
caused by dysfunction of the cochlea located in the inner ear. The function of the
cochlea is to convert sound waves into electrical signals sent to the auditory cortex
in the brain. There are several causes of sensorineural hearing loss, such as genetics

and trauma. The most common cause, however, is age, and thus sensorineural hearing
loss is a common disability among elderly people (Porth, 2011).
It can be caused
by the destruction of outer hair cells (OHCs) inside the cochlea due to for example
exposure to loud noises. Sounds that are transmitted by the middle ear of a person
with sensorineural hearing loss are the same as for NH, but low-level sounds are not
ampliﬁed as much by the cochlea. These abnormalities of ampliﬁcation in the cochlea
is not linear, i.e. for low-level sounds a larger ampliﬁcation is needed than for high-level
sounds where the loudness perception is almost the same as for people with NH. This
is referred to as a reduced dynamic range, Figure 2.
Figure 2: Dynamic range of normal hearing and hearing impaired (Al-Salim et al.,
2010)
A common diﬃculty among hearing impaired (HI) people is the ability to distin-
guish between the target sound (e.g. a talker) and interfering noise. It gets even more
challenging when the interfering noise is another talker, due to both sounds having
similar spectral and temporal features. By comparing speech recognition in stationary
noise to ﬂuctuating noise (non-speech), there is a beneﬁt of the modulated noise in
speech recognition as a result of people listening in the dips (Festen and Plomp, 1990).
However, HI listeners have more problems listening in the dips and the beneﬁt is not as
strong. A higher speech-reception threshold (SRT) is needed to be able to distinguish
two talkers, i.e. a larger signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) is needed to understand a certain
(example 50%) amount of the speech presented compared to normal hearing people.
If the sound is spatially separated from interfering noise, several studies have shown
a beneﬁt in form of a higher speech intelligibility for HI people (Marrone et al., 2008;
Bronkhorst, 2000). The auditory system is using diﬀerent cues to localize and segregate
sound sources and recognize speech. Interaural time diﬀerence (ITD) is a binaural cue
==PAGE:15==
2
BACKGROUND
5
(i.e. using diﬀerence between the ears) used to locate the sound source. It is deﬁned as
the diﬀerence in time between the sound reaching the two ears. If the sound is coming
from the left, the sound will reach the left ear before the right.
Another auditory
cue used by our auditory system to distinguish between sounds is the interaural level
diﬀerence (ILD) which is the diﬀerence in sound level of the signal reaching the two
ears. If a sound comes from the left, the head shadows the right ear, making the level of
the sound reaching the right ear lower than the left. A monaural cue used in localizing
the sound source is the direct-to-reverberant ratio (DRR), and is used to estimate the
distance to the sound source. It is deﬁned the ratio between the direct part of the sound
and the parts dominated by room reﬂections and reverberation.
In people with normal hearing, the basilar membrane (BM) in the cochlea com-
presses the input sound level into a reduced range that can be translated to electrical
impulses sent further to the auditory cortex. This type of compression is reduced for
people with sensorineural hearing loss, making the system more linear. To compensate
for the reduced dynamic range and loss of compression, wide-dynamic range compres-
sion (WDRC) algorithms were introduced in hearing-aids in the 1980s (Levitt, 2007).
2.2
Wide-dynamic range compression
WDRC algorithms are used in hearing-aids to compress the wide-dynamic input
signal into a reduced dynamic range in order to restore audibility (Launer et al., 2016).
As mentioned earlier, HI people have a reduced dynamic range due to the destruction
of OHCs, among others. The compression algorithm is using level-depended gain cal-
culations in such a way that low-level sounds gets a larger gain than high-level sounds.

Figure 3 shows the steps of the WDRC algorithm. The input signal is divided into
diﬀerent frequency channels called a ﬁlterbank. The width of the diﬀerent channels
in the ﬁlterbanks are increasing with increasing frequency (Launer et al., 2016), the
same principle as our auditory system (Fig. 5). HI people can have diﬀerent degrees of
hearing loss for diﬀerent frequencies; usually with a larger loss in high frequencies, called
sloping hearing loss. The level is estimated independently for each frequency channel,
and the gain is calculated depending on the estimated level. If the sound level reaches
a very high value (> 110dB), there will be almost no increase in the output sound
level due to everything being compressed. A make-up gain is then added to the level-
depended gain, calculated from the audiogram of the individual listener. The combined
gain is now added to the signal in frequency domain, and the inverse short-time Fourier
transform (ISTFT) is computed before it is presented to the listener.
The input/output function of one channel in the compression system is illustrated
in Fig.
4.
The red dashed line refers to linear compensation, the blue line is the
compression function, and the black line is the same compression function with make-
up gain added. It can be seen that for high input levels, a linear gain will give an
uncomfortably loud output level. In a compression system, if the input sound level
reaches a certain threshold, called the compression threshold (CT), the compression
part is activated. Here, a reduces slope is applied called compression ratio (CR), and
is the change in input level needed for 1 dB change in output level (Stone and Moore,
1992). Sound levels under the CT have a ratio/slope of 1, linear ampliﬁcation. The
make-up gain is a constant gain that is added to the time-varying gain function, and
is calculated from the individual listener’s audiogram. The WDRC system operates

Stone and Moore (1992) explained how diﬀerent combinations of CT and CR can
be utilized to create diﬀerent compression systems. For example, high values of both
CTs and CRs can be used to limit the maximum output of the hearing aids. Using a
high value of the CR, the increase in input level will cause almost no increase in output
level because a big change in input level is needed for 1dB change in output level. This
type of compression system is called a compression limiter. Using smaller values for the
CT and CR will compress the large variation in level of the input signal into a reduced
dynamic range of output level, which can help the HI people restore audibility (Stone
and Moore, 1992). In this thesis only WDRC is considered, and therefore low CTs and
low to medium CRs are used.

2.2.1
Attack and release time constants
Speech is a non-stationary signal and quickly varies in level across time. An instant
calculation of the gain mapping of the input level would cause some distortions to the
signal due to sudden steps in gain. The ﬂuctuating input level is smoothed with a
low-pass ﬁler in order to get more smooth transitions in gain. The speed in which the
estimated level, used to calculate the gain, reaches a steady-state value when a change
occurs in the input level is referred to as attack time and release time. These time
constants can be seen as kind of a cut-oﬀfor the low-pass ﬁlter, where long attack and
release times gives a very smooth transition in level, and also alonger time to reach a
steady-state value. Attack time is the time it takes to reach a steady-state value after
a sudden increase in sound level, while release time is the time it takes for the system
to reach a steady-state level when a certain decrease in input level occurs.

Fast-acting compression is a common WDRC algorithm in today’s commercial
hearing aids. It uses short attack and release time (T < 200 ms (Souza, 2002)) to
quickly compensate for sudden changes in sound level of the input signal (Stone and
Moore, 1992), left panel of Fig. 6. The gain calculated for the fast system is largely
varying, reaching all the way back to 0 (no compression) for the speech pauses. The
slow acting system, right side in Fig. 6, uses a long time to reach the steady state level,
and never reaches the 0 values in the speech pauses because the attack and release time
are 2 s . With a sudden increase in input level, a short attack time will quickly decrease
the gain and will save the listener from being exposed to uncomfortably loud output
levels. A short release time will be able to rapidly increase the gain when there is a
decrease in input level, thus even the ﬁrst part of the input signal is detected by the
listener.
There are several studies discussing how short attack and release time eﬀect the
modulation depth of a signal (Shen and Lentz, 2010; Verschuure et al., 1996; Stone
and Moore, 1992). They show that the modulation depth is reduced after compressing
the signal with a fast-acting system. This is a consequence of low-level sounds being
ampliﬁed more than high-level sounds, and the diﬀerence between the peak and valleys
is decreased. These valleys, or dips, are used by the auditory system to distinguish
between sounds, referred to as listening in dips. By comparing the top left panel with
the bottom left panel in Figure 6, it can be observed that the diﬀerence between the
==PAGE:20==
2

peaks and valleys is reduced after the signal has been compressed with a fast-acting
system. As already mentioned, HI people are having a greater diﬃculty listing in dips,
and with a reduced modulation depth caused by the fast-acting compression system,
this would make it even more diﬃcult distinguishing between diﬀerent sources.
In a speech signal, speech pauses will get a larger ampliﬁcation by the fast-acting
compression system due to a lower input levels. In these speech pauses, noise and room
reﬂections will be ampliﬁed more (Stone and Moore, 1992), which can cause distortions,
such as changes in the DRR.
Hassager et al. (2017b) investigated the impact of fast-acting compression on spa-
tial perception in reverberant environments.
They showed that when applying the
fast-acting compression, a broadening of the sound source was perceived compared to a
linear reference condition for both normal hearing and hearing impaired people. Also,
more distortions were observed such as image-splits (perceiving the source as coming
from several places), and the source perceived closer to the subject. These distortions
were even larger for hearing impaired listeners compare to normal hearing. Fast-acting
compression system will enhance all low-level sounds; this includes sounds dominated
by energy from room reﬂections.
A slow-acting compression uses a long attack and release time (release time >200
ms (Souza, 2002)), i.e. a longer time is used to reach the steady state level. This long
time constants will preserve the DRR, but will also not compress the signal as much.
Hence, certain sound will not be perceived by the listener, as low-level sounds are left
underampliﬁed. Comparing the top right panel with the bottom right panel in Figure
6, it can be seen that some of the last parts of the output signal is less ampliﬁed than
the beginning of the signal as a result of the large time constants. Studies by Neuman
et al. (1998, 1995) investigated the preference from the listener on use of diﬀerent release
times. A longer release time was reported back to improve the sound quality and noise
was reduced.
2.2.2
DRR-aware compression
By using a fast-acting system, audibility is restored, but noise such as reverber-
ations and room reﬂections will be ampliﬁed more than the direct parts of signal. A
slow-acting system, on the other hand, will not compress the input levels as much, and
some sound will be left underampliﬁed. Therefore, Hassager et al. (2017a) suggested a
dual system which is using diﬀerent time constants for parts of the signal dominated
by direct parts and parts dominated by room reﬂections and reverberation, referred
to as direct-to-reverberant aware (DRR-aware) compression method. This system is
compressing the direct part with a fast-acting system and the reverberant part of the
signal with a slow acting system. It employs a blind classiﬁcation for classifying the
direct parts versus reverberant parts using the variation in the signal as a feature for
the classiﬁcation. Figure 7 shows the compression scheme with the binary classiﬁcation
represented in dotted lines.

2.3
Listening eﬀort assessments
There are several ways of measuring listening eﬀort, either objectively or sub-
jectively, i.e. the listeners own perception of the diﬃculty level, or by physiological
measurements (Tednes and Seeman, 2017). Pupillometry is the measurement of pupil
size, which is an indication of listening eﬀort. Kramer et al. (1997) showed that a higher
speech intelligibility score gives a lower pupil dilation, i.e. a decrease in SNR shows
an increase in pupil size. The pupil dilation is aﬀected by changes in the autonomic
nervous system, more speciﬁcally by changes in the activity of the parasympathetic ner-
vous system (PNS) and the sympathetic nervous system (SNS). The SNS is responsible
for the so-called "ﬁght or ﬂight reﬂex" (Sand et al., 2006). Increased SNS activity, for
==PAGE:23==
2
BACKGROUND
13
example due to an increased utilization of cognitive resources, would lead to an increase
of the pupil size. Recently, pupillometry has been applied in traditional speech-in-noise
tests to measure the listening eﬀort involved in speech recognition (Wendt et al., 2017,
2018). Listening eﬀort is deﬁned as a form of mental eﬀort that involves tasks with
listening (Pichora-Fuller et al., 2016). If a listener allocate more resources, the listening
eﬀort increases. It is hypothesized that this is reﬂected in changes of the pupil dilation.
Wendt et al. (2017) tested the eﬀect of diﬀerent noise reduction schemes on listening
eﬀort. It was demonstrated that listening eﬀort was reduced with active NR scheme
even though there was no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in word scoring. Furthermore, signiﬁcant
diﬀerences in the pupillary response, and thus in listening eﬀort, was shown between
diﬀerent NR schemes, when speech intelligibility was at ceiling, and no diﬀerences in
recognition performance between NR schemes were measured.
Wendt et al. (2018) showed that the highest eﬀort was reached for intelligibility
scores between 30% and 70%. For lower intelligibility levels, the pupil dilation decreased
indicating less eﬀort used. If the word scoring was less than 50%, the intelligibility scores
decreased indicating that the subjects had less motivation in understanding the speech.
This is indicating that speech intelligibility levels less than 50% can not be used in
studying listening eﬀort.
2.4
Speech intelligibility test
Speech intelligibility tests are used in hearing aid research to measure speech per-
ception in people with hearing-impairment. A common speech intelligibility test is the
hearing-in-noise test (HINT), originally developed by Nilsson et al. (1994). A Danish
version of a speech-in-noise test was developed by Nielsen and Dau (2011). It is most
commonly used to measure the signal-reception-threshold (SRT) in diﬀerent scenarios
such as speech in a multi-talker environment. The SRT describes the signal-to-noise ra-
tio at which listener can detect a certain amount of the speech sentence, normally 50%
(Nilsson et al., 1994). In this thesis the speech-in-noise test was used with a ﬁxed SNR,
and comparing the percentage correct words obtained for diﬀerent acoustical scenarios.

The following sections describe the experimental setup used to evaluate the impact

3.1
Experimental design
A speech-in-noise test was performed using the Danish hearing in noise test (HINT)
(Nielsen and Dau, 2011). The subjects were asked to repeat back a 5-word sentence
(target) presented in 1-talker interfering noise (competing talker).
They were then
scored on how many correct words were repeated back from 0 to 5. One trial was
referred to as the speech and noise presented as well as the response from the subject.
After every ﬁfth trial, the subjects were asked to indicate on an iPad the location and
width of the source from the the last target sentence presented. During playback of the
sound, the subject’s pupil size were recorded, indicating listening eﬀort. The output
measurements of this experiment are pupil dilation, location of the sound source, and
percentage of correct words repeated back.
The target speaker was the 5-word sentences from the HINT lists, and the com-
peting talker was a person reading the news, both male voices with the same long-term
average spectrum (LTAS). A 3-second representation of ’noise’ was started before the
target and ended 3 seconds after. This was necessary to be able to calculate a baseline
for the pupil data. The target and competing talker signals were both convolved with
diﬀerent binaural room impulse response (BRIRs) from diﬀerent speaker positions to
visualize the headphone output as located at that speaker. They were then scaled with
an ﬁxed SNR, calculated for each listener using an adaptive procedure tracking the
80% correct word scoring point. Now, the signals were added together and were set
to a RMS level of 70 dB, where both the target and competing talker were present in
the signal. The combined signal was then sent through one of the two compression
methods.
Table 1 shows the diﬀerent conditions used in this experiment. ’Low’ reverberation
indicates a normal class room (RT30 = 0.5s), and ’mid’ reverberation indicated an
auditorium (RT30 = 1.3s). It is expected that the DRR-aware compression will show
less listening eﬀort as a result of less reverberations and room reﬂections being ampliﬁed,
and that the diﬀerence in listening eﬀort is larger between the two compression methods
for more reverberant environments. Angle in table 1 referrers to the angular separation
between the target and the competing talker. A diﬀerence in listing eﬀort is expected
for two diﬀerent angular separations, and that the listening eﬀort will be larger for
spatially close separated sources. It is also expected that the fast-acting compression
method will show a larger amount of eﬀort used as a result of broadening of the sound
sources compared to the DRR-aware compression.
Before running the actual test, the listeners performed a speech-in-noise test to
estimate the individual SNR at 80% correct recognition. This was carried out using

3.3
Binaural room impulse response measurements
Individual binaural room impulse responses (BRIRs) were used in the experiment
to simulate the diﬀerent conditions virtually over headphones. Majdak et al. (2014)
showed that people are better externalizing using individual BRIRs due to people hav-
ing diﬀerent shapes of the ears and head. During measurements, the subjects were
asked to hold their head ﬁxed and focus on the speakers placed in front. The BRIRs
were measured using a 2-second sweep from 20Hz to 24kHz. The sound was presented
at 71 dB sound pressure level (SPL) at the chair, and recorded by two DPA (2017)
microphones positioned at the entrance of the ear canal at a sampling frequency of 48
kHz. This was repeated for all speaker, in total 64 times. The recorded BRIRs were
then truncated, in order to remove noise in the beginning and end of the measured
BRIRs. The trucation started at 50 samples before the ﬁrst detected peak of all 64 po-
sitions, and with a total length of 256 samples. The BRIRs for the diﬀerent loudspeaker
positions were then normalized using the maximum value of all BRIRs. A Tukey win-
dow, with a ratio of 0.2 (the ﬁrst and last 20/2 percent of the samples equal to parts
of a cosine), was then multiplied to all BRIRs to smooth out potential discontinuities
at the beginning and the end of the BRIRs. The processed anechoic BRIRs were now
convolved with diﬀerent room impulse responses, giving two sets of BRIRs at low and
mid reverberation times.

Pupillometry post-processing
All trials within a condition were time-aligned, starting from 2.2 s until 11.2 s
after sound onset, i.e. a time segment of 9 s in total. Eye blinks were removed by
calculating a threshold using 3 standard deviations lower than the mean value of the

individual trial was baseline corrected by subtracting a baseline pupil value from the
signal. The baseline pupil value was calculated using the mean value from 1.5 s to 3 s
at the beginning of the the trial (see Fig. 12). If there were less than 50% of the data
in a trial which were reliable, the trial was excluded from further analysis. Unreliable
data are the data points which are NaN.

3.8
Hit-False Alarm Rate
The hit-false alarm rate (H-FA) was calculated to analyze how accurate the binary
classiﬁcation of the direct and the reverberant sound was for the DRR-aware compres-
sion at the acoustic conditions considered in this thesis. It was calculated by using the
BRIRs measured for all subjects, using the frontal speaker positions from -90 to 90. Hit
rate (H) was deﬁned as the percentage of correctly classiﬁed direct sound time-frequency
(T-F) units, while false-alarm rate (FA) was deﬁned as the number of wrongly classiﬁed
T-F units dominated by reverberation (Hassager et al., 2017a).
The H-FA rate was also calculated looking at only those T-F units where the
power of the target was greater than the power of the competing talker. This was
computed in order to investigate the performance of the classiﬁer when the target
speaker was dominating, due to this being the most interesting sound to the listener. If
the performance of the classiﬁer is decreasing when the target is dominating, then the
performance in the experiment can be decreased. The H-FA rate was also calculated
for when the power of the competing talker was greater than the power of the target.

3.9
Statistical analysis
A simple paired t-test was carried out comparing all the diﬀerent conditions for
each of the outcome measurements, with a signiﬁcance level of 95%. This was done
in order to investigate if there is a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in speech intelligibility and
listening eﬀort between the two compression methods, diﬀerent reverberation time and
diﬀerent spatial angles. As several studies showed before (Wendt et al., 2017; Zekveld
et al., 2010), with a decrease in intelligibility there is a decrease in pupil dilation, which
is an indication of increased listening eﬀort. A linear regression model was therefore
used to estimate the relationship between word scoring and pupil dilation, in order to
investigate if there is a correlation between word scoring and pupil dilation measured
in this study.

5
Discussion
An experiment testing two diﬀerent compression methods in diﬀerent acoustic sce-
narios on speech intelligibility and listening eﬀort has been conducted. In this section
the results from section 4 are discussed in more details.
5.1
Short-term DRR classiﬁcation
The performance of the classiﬁer in the DRR-aware compression system, classifying
the direct and reverberant parts of the input signal, was investigated in the conditions
considered in this thesis. As shown in Fig. 16, conditions with medium reverberation
showed a decrease in performance compared to low values of reverberations on the
classiﬁcation for the frequency channels from 250 Hz to 4000 Hz. For example, using
more information from the last window (120 ms instead of 80 ms as used in this study)
seems to improve the performance for medium reverberation while still showing the
same performance for low reverberation (see Fig. 28 in Appendix A).
Comparing the H-FA rate in this study to the study by Hassager et al. (2017a), the
performance of the classiﬁcation was similar for the low reverberant condition. In this
study, a two-talker scenario was investigated while the study by Hassager et al. (2017a)
used a single source. This indicate that the classiﬁcation works similar for single source
compared to a two-source scenario.
It was also investigated if the performance of the classiﬁcation is diﬀerent for the
T-F units where the power of the target is greater than the power of the competing
talker. The performance of the classiﬁcation showed a slight improvement when the
target is dominating compared to the overall performance. Which is important when
this is the important sound for the listener.
It was observed that the classiﬁcation performs better when the target is domi-
nating in compared to the situation when the competing talker is dominating. The
diﬀerences in performance of the classiﬁer could be because the individual SNRs, mea-
sured in the experiment, were all positive SNRs.
5.2
Spatial perception
The spatial perception was recorded in this study in order to link Hassager et al.
(2017a) work to the current study, and investigate if there were similar patterns in
both studies.
The results from the spatial perception in this study were shown in
Figs. 18 and 19 in section 4.2. A trend of front-back confusions were observed, i.e.
perceiving the source from the back when presented from the front. By visual inspection,
a general trend was be observed where small angular separations (Fig. 18) showed a
more spatially distributed sound image compared to large angular separation (Fig. 19).
This distribution was even larger for medium reverberation (Figs. 18b and 18d) than for
low reverberation for closely spaced sources. No diﬀerences between the two diﬀerent
compression methods on spatial perception were observed.

5.3
Speech intelligibility
A speech-in-noise test was carried out where the subjects were asked to repeat a
target sentence and count the amount of correct words repeated back. An increase in
reverberation time was expected to have a decrease in speech intelligibility, which was
shown with a p-value < 0.001 (Fig. 21). It was also expected that with a larger angular
separation between the two sources, intelligibility would increase due to a release from
masking, which was also shown with a p-value < 0.001.
In general, for all conditions, there was no signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the two
compression methods on speech intelligibility (p-value = 0.22). In a more reverberant
environment and closely spaced sources (conditions 2 and 6, table 1), the DRR-aware
compression method showed a higher speech intelligibility than the fast-acting compres-
sion method, p-value = 0.02. This was the only condition with a signiﬁcant diﬀerence
in word scoring between the two compression methods.
5.4
Pupil dilation
The results for the pupil data showed that under 50% speech intelligibility, the
pupil data indicated a lower pupil dilation, which may be an indication of reduced
motivation in understanding the sentence since the task was too diﬃcult (Wendt et al.
(2018), Fig. 23). The peak pupil dilation and mean pupil dilation showed a signiﬁcant
correlation with word scoring (p-value = 0.05) while the peak latency show a correlation.
This indicates that with an increase in word scoring there is a decrease in pupil dilation.
This does not exclude that there can be changes in pupil dilation even though there is no
diﬀerence in word scoring, but it indicated that less eﬀort is used in easier tasks. It was
expected the even though there was no signiﬁcant diﬀerences in speech intelligibility,
there could still be some diﬀerences in listening eﬀort, indicated by an diﬀerence in pupil
dilation.
However, there was no signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the two compression
methods in terms of peak pupil dilation, mean pupil dilation and peak latency for any
of the conditions.
5.5
Limitation of the study
One diﬀerence between this study and the study of Hassager et al. (2017a) is
that Hassager et al. (2017a) used a single source, while in this study a two-talker
scenario was investigated. Therefore, diﬀerent patters could have been obtained in this
study compared to the one by Hassager et al. (2017a) due to some interaction with
the interfering sound source. Another diﬀerence between this study and the study by
Hassager et al. (2017a) was that Hassager et al. (2017a) used NH listeners while in this
study HI listeners were used. Even more confusions and distortions may have been
obtained using HI listeners, as discussed in another study by Hassager et al. (2017b).
A reason for the diﬀerences in the spatial perception between this study and the study
performed by Hassager et al. (2017a) could be the multi-task paradigm used in the
==PAGE:47==
5

37
current study. After completing ﬁve trials the listeners were asked to indicate on an
iPad the source location and the width of the source. The listening task seemed to
be prioritized, the subjects were not focusing as well on the location of the sound
source, and the localization was therefore not as accurate. It was also reported back
from the listeners that it was diﬃcult to focuse on the localization of the target while
also concentrating on understanding the speech. A solution could be to use one list
in the HINT framework at the end of the experiment where the diﬀerent experimental
conditions were presented randomly and the task would be to only localize the target
source. Then the listener could focus on one task at a time.
The SNRs obtained at the adaptive procedure at 80% correct word scoring showed
very diﬀerent intelligibility results in the experiment between diﬀerent subjects, for the
same condition. The reason for an adaptive tracking the SNR prior to the experiment
was to achieve similar performance levels for all subjects during the experiment. Rea-
sons for the diﬀerences in performance could be many. For example, some people are
more exposed to the HINT sentences, and performed therefore better during the test.
Nielsen and Dau (2011) shows that there is only a small memory eﬀect of -0.15 dB
SRT such that being exposed to HINT should not provide a big eﬀect in word scoring.
It could also be that in the adaptive procedure, the subjects experienced some release
from masking for the linear condition due to less distortions from compression, and
that there was less release from masking when compression is applied. A too low SNR
could then have been measured in adaptive procedure, giving all the conditions in the
experiments a lower performance. As shown in the results, subjects with similar hearing
loss have measured diﬀerent SNRs, and this is also reﬂected in the word scoring, with
a lower performance for the lower SNR with similar hearing proﬁle.
An adaptive procedure using a fast-acting compression method, low reverberation
and 60 degrees separation could be an alternative to the linear condition used in this
study. The intelligibility results obtained for low reverberation and 60 degree separation
showed very similar results between the two compression algorithms for all subjects
(average of 3.4% diﬀerence). The average diﬀerence between the highest and the lowest
word score for all subjects were 39.3%. A 95% correct word scoring point could be
a better SNR tracking point, in which all conditions operated on a point above 50%
intelligibility. This would improve the data by having the subjects more focused, and
more motivated, as shown by Wendt et al. (2018).
Since there was no computer screen located inside the experimental room, the
calibration of the eye-tracking camera was carried out by asking the subjects to focus
on diﬀerent loudspeakers. This could be a reason for the low reliability of the data for
some of the subjects. Using a screen inside the experimental room in the form of a
tablet could have improved the calibration. This could have also helped improving the
setup values by being able to see the focus of the lens, and distance from the camera to
the subject. A second person would not have been needed for the setup and calibration
of the camera, as it was in this experiment.
The eye-tracking camera was attached to the chair in the experimental room, and
was therefore not as secure as having the camera placed on a table in front of the subject.

Small movements made by the subjects during the trials could have changed the position
of the camera, which could have inﬂuenced the recordings. These movements by the
subject could also change the position of the subject to the camera. By change the
type of camera used, in the experiment, for example using a goggle based one instead
of a desktop version, this type of noise could be removed.
Movements of the eyes due to the target not being located in the center, or front,
of the subjects was observed. When the target sentence was presented, some of the
subjects tended to change the focus of the eyes towards where the target was presented.
By changing the location of the target sounds to the center, this noise in the pupil data
could have been avoided. This study was linked to Hassager et al. (2017a)’s study
where a larger eﬀect of distortions was observed for more lateral source positions. It
was therefore interesting to investigate these lateral source positions. These movements
of the eyes were mostly observed for subjects with a larger degree of hearing loss, since
they are more used to reading on the lips.
Reliability seemed to have a small decrease towards the end of the session showing
the most reliable data at the beginning of the experiment (see Fig. 35 in Appendix C.2).
This can indicate that the subjects are getting more tired at the end of the experiment.
A small increase was shown for the last two conditions, where the subjects knew that
there were only few lists left. An alternative would be to divide the experiment into two
sessions. It was also reported back from the subjects that it was tiresome to focusing
the eyes for such a long time.
A total of 8 subjects were tested in this experiment, which could also have eﬀected
the data.
Due to the low number of subjects nothing can be concluded, but some
trends can be seen. In the word scoring data, there were some clear trends for one
condition as already discussed, but this could have been clearer with more data. In
the pupil data, more data is needed to see a clear trend. In this study, only three full
measurements were obtained, while at least 10-12 full measurements of data would have
been preferable.

6
CONCLUSION
39
6
Conclusion
In this thesis, the eﬀect of hearing-aid compressing on speech intelligibility and
listening eﬀort was investigated. A compression scheme compressing parts dominated
by energy from the direct part of the signal and parts dominated by reverberation were
compressed with diﬀerent time constants were tested together with an conventional fast-
acting compression No large diﬀerence were found between the two compression meth-
ods on spatial perception. Some small diﬀerence were observed between the diﬀerent
reveberant conditions, where medium reverberation shows more spatially distributed
sound images compare to low reverberation. More closely separated sources showed
more spatially distributed sound images. A signiﬁcant diﬀerence was shown for low
reverberation and spatially closely separated sources on speech intelligibility between
the two compression methods. However, this was the only condition with a signiﬁcant
diﬀerence between the two compression methods. No diﬀerence was shown in the pupil
data between the two methods. This may have been resulting from a too little dataset
obtained with only having three subjects with data from all 8 conditions. A next step
would be to collect more data in order to have more stable pupil data. Moreover, a
diﬀerent condition used in the adaptive SNR tracking should be investigated more in
order to have a more similar speech intelligibility performance for all subjects.
