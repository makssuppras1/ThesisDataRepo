Summary
This study investigates the effect of alloying on corrosion run-off of ductile cast iron in
coastal atmospheres. The study has been done in collaboration with the company GH
Form Aps, that manufactures and sells cast iron city inventory.
The project investigates the effects of low-level alloying of ductile cast iron. The
study screens effects of different alloying elements when added to ductile cast iron.
The initial hypothesis was, that by alloying, corrosion rates and runoff rates would
decrease. Small additions of alloying elements allowed for altering of the microstructure
of the material and the properties of the oxide layer forming at the surface. Inspiration
was drawn from weathering steels that contain below 4% alloying elements and form
complex protective rust layers that decrease corrosion rates.
Samples were cast of a EN-GJS-400-15 grade cast iron, but containing low levels of
copper (0.5%), chromium (0.4%), nickel (1.2%) and silicon (4%). Alloying elements were
combined in accordance to a 23 factorial design. Samples were cast at Ferrofos Aps.
Samples were tested in a cyclic corrosion chamber to simulate atmospheric corrosion,
with alternating cycles of drying and spraying of salt containing fog (2.5% NaCl).
The alloyed samples were tested for weight loss after 130 hours of spraying. Another
set of samples were placed on concrete tiles placed in the chamber, to evaluate the runoff
visually, since the main issue addressed by the company was the visibility of the runoff.
The drying period of the cycle allowed for drying of run-off onto the concrete tile.
The alloyed samples showed extensive corrosion, as intended. Weight gain slowed
over time, indicating the formation of protective iron oxide/hydroxide layers.
Alloying as performed, showed no significant effect was seen on corrosion rates.
Weight loss of the samples ranged from 6.3% to 7%.
Severe run-off was seen from all alloyed samples.
The visual appearance of the resulting patinas have been documented and described.
Copper alloying gives the most even defect-free patina. Silicon alloying (4%) gives a
hard adherent patina but also shows defects such as flaking and blistering.
The effects of the alloying elements on the surface microstructure of the castings
have been analysed using image analysis. Effects of copper are not large enough to
be conclusive. Nickel is a mild pearlite stabilizer when combined with copper. When
nickel is combined with chromium, it counteracts the formation of ferrite seen in castings
containing 4% silicon. Chromium is shown to be a strong pearlite stabilizer. Silicon, at
4%, promotes large fractions of ferrite.
A hardening effect is seen that is linear to the fraction pearlite stabilized. This is
applicable to castings containing 2.5% silicon. Silicon, at 4%, deviates from this trend

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Purpose
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate the effects of alloying ductile cast iron, in
terms of uncovering what alloying elements slow corrosion rates and decrease so-called
run-off, specifically in chloride containing coastal environments. Since corrosion rate
and run-off severity are not necessarily correlated, they will both be tested. Thin walled
DCI samples were used in this study (thickness of 6.2 mm).
The topic was chosen in collaboration with the manufacturer of city inventory of cast
iron products GH Form Aps, located in Borup, Denmark.
Items from their current production will be analysed to better understand the mate-
rial giving rise to the issue.
Experiments will be designed according to factorial design, to quantify the effects of
single alloying elements but also their interactions. The approach is deemed suitable for
the project, since factorial design allows for investigation of a large experimental space.
The thin-walled alloyed samples will be analysed, to reveal the effects seen on the
microstructure from additions of various elements.
Possible effects on patina appearance due to alloying will be documented, since the
visual appearance of the patinated cast iron is of importance in the application.
1.2
Untreated Cast Iron: Use and
Sustainability
Untreated irons and steels have been used for decades in an outdoor environments. They
can achieve a patina of warm reddish brown rust, that is both decorative and protective.
It can be most readily seen as cladding for buildings, as large structures like bridges,
as sculpture and on smaller items such as frames for raised garden beds, benches, lamp
posts and large flower bowls. The structures are typically made of low-alloyed sheet
metal (so-called weathering steel) or of cast iron, both resistant to corrosion in different
ways.
The low environmental footprint of steel [Mur11] and cast iron [GHF], makes them
a attractive materials for the increasingly environmentally concerned manufacturers,
designers and buyers of steel and iron items. When left untreated, the environmental

Run-off as a Broader Issue
Some studies on potential contamination of alloying elements in the run-off have been
carried out, but run-off is mainly an aesthetic issue, which may be the reason why it has
not received nearly as much attention as the degenerating types of corrosion that can
be detrimental to e.g. large structures. Despite this, run-off is an issue seen by multiple
alloy types placed outdoors, such as by weathering steel [SCI] (the trademark name
being Cor-Ten steel) and by roofing, statues and other historical artifacts. Literature
on the topic mainly focuses on these two applications.

2.1
Ductile Cast Iron
2.1.1
General Characteristics
Cast iron is typically divided into one of four types: gray-, ductile-, white-, or malleable
cast iron. Other names of ductile cast iron (DCI) include spherical graphite cast irons
(SG-irons) and nodular irons.
Uses. J.R Davis [Dav96] describes applications and advantages of DCI. By volume
produced, cast iron piping is the most common application of DCI, using e.g. ASTM A
716 and 746 grade irons. The second most common application is for automotive.
DCI presents significant advantages. Taking automotive application as an ex-
ample - due to high repeatability of the castings in terms of mechanical properties, DCI
can be used for critical automotive parts, such as crankshafts, truck axels, disk brake
calipers, high temperature turbohousings and manifolds. The automotive industry re-
quires high cost efficiency, which DCI production can deliver. Other applications include
gears, parts for heavy machinery like farming-, construction- and oilfield equipment. In
other words, mainly structural applications where cost efficiency, high strength and
toughness is important, along with good machinability.
Properties Of the cast irons, DCI is characterized by high strength, elongation and
toughness due to its nodular graphite, that give it its unique properties. The microstruc-
ture consists of nodular graphite in a matrix of varying fractions of ferrite and pearlite.
Due to the spherical graphite particles, DCI has higher tensile strength (commonly 420
MPa) than e.g. gray cast iron (commonly 210 MPa). Since the nodules are discrete,
unlike the continuous flake graphite in gray cast iron, DCI can have almost steel-like
mechanical properties, without the high solidification shrinkage of steel, that makes it
difficult to cast. Other favorable properties include a 10% lower density compared to
steel, good damping properties and low coefficient of friction, making DCI suitable for
gears and other similar mechanical applications. In this study, the specific grade used
can be classified as EN-GJS-400-15.
The chemical composition of DCI differs from gray- and mottled cast irons, as
seen in Table 2.1.1. Contents of unwanted phosphorous and sulfur from prior processing
of the pig iron should be kept low, demanding materials of higher purity. The elements
and their effects can be seen in overview in Table 2.1.4.

2.1.2
Metallurgy of Ductile Cast Iron
Many factors influence the final microstructure cast irons [Dav96]:
• Cooling rates. High cooling rates will: 1) refine graphite size and the structure of
the matrix [Dav96], 2) the matrix will have an increased fraction of pearlite over
ferrite, 3) formation of carbides are at higher risk, 4) chill in the overall structure
may also be seen. Slow rates will coarsen the microstructure consisting of pearlite
and promote ferrite over pearlite.
• Liquid treatment. Treatment determines to a large degree, the graphite morphol-
ogy and thereby the mechanical properties of the iron.
For DCI it consists of
both Mg-treatment, to change shape from flake to spherical and afterwards inocu-
lation, to increase nodule count. Examples of inoculants include Ba and Al. The
mechanism for of formation spheroidal graphite is covered in Section 2.1.3.
==PAGE:20==

• Chemical composition. A multitude of elements affect microstructure and proper-
ties. These are listed in Table 2.1.4.
• Heat treatment. Post heat treatment can transform the structure of the matrix
and thereby the mechanical properties. Selected treatments are covered in Section
2.1.5.
• Silicon content.
Silicon content increases the C-diffusivity in the matrix and
thereby increases ferrite content.
Silicon content can be kept within a certain
range depending on the C content, to avoid specific defects such as graphite flota-
tion. (Fig.2.5).
• Graphite shape. The graphite shape determines the final mechanical properties.
Fig. 2.3 gives a comparison of cast irons with flake, compacted and spheroidal

• Eutectic temperature. The addition of elements affect the eutectic temperature

2.1.3
Solidification and Solid State Transformation of
Hypereutectic Thin Walled DCI
The Fe-C phase diagram (Fig. 2.1) depicts the solidification and solid state transfor-
mations upon cooling the iron melt. Above 1154◦C nucleation and growth of graphite
starts. As mentioned earlier, Mg is the cause of the spheroidal shape of the graphite.
Products of Mg reactions are found at the surface of the graphite nuclei and change the
lattice interface between the Mg-products and the carbon, in such a manner that leads
to growth of the spheroidal graphite shape [Ped06].
When reaching the eutectic point at 1154◦C, austenite (γ-Fe) and cementite nucleate
and grow. Solubility of C in austenite is not adequate to dissolve the concentrations of
C present, hence the two separate.
Due to diffusion mechanisms at the solid/liquid
front (Fig.2.6), a lamellar structure is obtained. As the two types of lamellae grow,
they contribute to each others growth, by exchanging of C - austenite rejects C causing
higher levels of C in the melt, promoting cementite growth.
At 738◦C, ferrite and
cementite nucleate and eventually no austenite is retained. Ferrite has very low solubility
of C, so it diffuses into the cementite layers or the graphite nodules.
The lamellar
structure of ferrite and cementite are known as pearlite. If given enough time at the
high temperatures, the pearlite coarsens (cementite layers thicken). If held there longer,
carbon will diffuse into the graphite, leaving the matrix fully ferritic.
Figure 2.6: Growth mechanism of eutectic lamellae. [FÅ06].

2.1.5
Heat treatment of Ductile Cast Iron
DCI is heat treatable, but often seen as-cast. Heat treatment is a method that can be
utilized to meet desired mechanical properties. The treatments are depicted as continu-
ous cooling transformation curves (CCT) in Fig. 2.7, where lines represent the cooling
rates of different treatments.
Annealing. Produces a ferritic matrix. The ferritic matrix gives improved ductility
and low strength [Dav96]. The alloy should not contain carbide stabilizing elements,
that may hinder the transformation to ferrite [Dav96]. A typical treatment consists of
heating above the critical temperature to 870-900◦C. The duration is 1 hr per inch of
thickness. Then, the casting is cooled at a rate of 55◦C/h, until 345◦C and then air
cooled [Dav96].
Normalizing. Produces a fine pearlitic matrix that improves tensile strength. Thick
castings will require alloying to ensure the pearlite matrix is obtained throughout. A
typical treatment consists of heating above the critical temperature to 870-940◦C for 1
==PAGE:25==
2.2 Atmospheric Corrosion of Ductile Cast Iron
15
hr per inch of thickness [Dav96]. The casting is then air cooled. Normalising can be
followed by tempering for stress relief from cooling.
Hardening. Produces a martensitic matrix that results in a hardness or strength in-
crease [Dav96]. A typical treatment consists of heating to 845-870◦[Dav96] and quench-
ing in oil. Hardening should be followed by tempering for stress relief from cooling,
especially in castings with varying thickness.

2.2
Atmospheric Corrosion of Ductile Cast
Iron
The corrosion mechanism of ductile cast iron in atmospheric conditions can be charac-
terised as uniform corrosion, seen as a coherent reddish brown layer of familiar rust.
The noble graphite particles will act as a site for cathodic reduction of oxygen. The
reactive iron matrix will act as an anodic site for iron dissolution. The redox reaction is
illustrated in Fig. 2.8. For iron, in atmospheric conditions characterised by abundance
of oxygen, the main reactions occurring are:
• Fe →Fe2+ + 2e (anodic/oxidation reaction)
• O2 + 2H2O + 4e →4OH−(cathodic/reduction reaction, specifically reduction of
oxygen)
The product Fe(OH)2, is unstable and therefore further reacts with water and oxygen
to produce a the hydrate Fe2O3 ·nH20 or Fe(OH)3 (iron hydroxide).

In oxygen-poor conditions Fe3O4 is more stable, as indicated by the Pourbaix diagram
for iron in Fig. 2.9. It can be recognised as black rust and is known under the name
magnetite. If water is limited, Fe3O4 will appear black in color and if water is present, it
will appear greenish [Bar04]. Many oxide and oxide-hydroxides exist, that are more or
less stable under certain conditions. A line of transformations will lead to stable types.
In the anodic reaction, iron is in dissolution and corrodes away. The Fe2O3 that is

2.2.1
Oxide Layers of Iron
Corrosion products of iron exist in the form of various polymorphs. The study of them
is often done in relation to steels that have been low-alloyed for increased atmospheric
corrosion resistance, also known as weathering steel or by the trade name Cor-Ten steel.
Common corrosion products mentioned in connection to atmospheric corrosion of
iron based alloys are lepidocrocite (γ-FeOOH), goethite (α-FeOOH) and akageneite (β-
FeOOH). In weatherings steels, the layer will have an outer region of porous γ-FeOOH
and an inner compact layer of α-FeOOH, stabilized by the alloying elements [Raf+16].
Cu has an ability to decrease the size of the FeOOH particles within the inner layer
[Mur11]. Presence of Cr can result in compact Cr-goethite. The presence of chloride
promotes formation of β-FeOOH that has a structure suspected to not have the same
ability to stall transport of chloride to the substrate below [Raf+16][CS96]. The protec-
tive inner layer has been found to show resistance to aggressive species such as chloride
[Raf+16].
2.2.2
Microstructures and Corrosion Rates
The elements of DCI are similar to those of other cast iron types, but also mild steels.
Comparing their corrosion resistance, highlights different electrochemical mechanisms
that are not related to their elements [LaQ58].
The matrix structure. For DCI (and the majority of other cast iron types) the
matrix will be ferritic, pearlitic or tempered structures. Ferritic structures will have
higher corrosion rates than pearlitic structures [LaQ58].
This is also often valid in
marine environments (Fig. 2.10).
Graphite. In the case of DCI, graphite exists as discrete particles with low area of
interface due to the spheroidal shape [LaQ58]. Since corrosion will penetrate along the
interface of the graphite and the matrix, the discontinuous graphite spheroids show an
advantage over flake graphite [LaQ58]. Flake graphite has the advantage, that flakes
can persist although the matrix corrodes away, due to their inter-connectivity.
The
phenomenon is known as graphitization.
Presence of insoluble constituents such as carbides and silicious compounds will
improve the adherence of the graphite, that will otherwise dislodge as the corrosion
attack proceeds [LaQ58].
Strength of DCI is also a influential factor in stalling erosion corrosion [Col13] and
gives DCI an advantage over weaker gray cast iron.
Alloying elements.
Alloying elements such as copper and nickel can raise the
nobility of the iron in cast irons. Copper is of high nobility (resistant by immunity) and
applicable as alloying element in reducing environments [Bar04].

2.2.3
Atmospheric Corrosion in Coastal Areas
Atmospheric corrosion is characterised by cyclic variations of moisture and dryness, tem-
perature and pollution. It is an important field of study, due to its widespread nature,
that results in damage of structures and high associated costs. In coastal or marine areas,
corrosion rates are often higher, due to higher humidity and deposition of chloride onto
the corroding metal. The main cathodic reaction will be reduction of oxygen [Bar04].
Common corrosion rates of iron in coastal environments can be seen in Table 2.10.
The determining factors of atmospheric corrosion rates are time of wetness (TOW),
composition of the electrolyte present and temperature [Bar04].
TOW. If metal surfaces are clean and smooth, corrosion will not occur below a
relative humidity of 100%. Dust, soot and other pollution along with corrosion products,
can act hygroscopically to decrease the required relative humidity before the surface
becomes moist. Rain can both act favourably by cleaning the surface, but also increases
TOW.
Chloride containing electrolyte. As mentioned earlier, chlorides have an ability
to reduce the protectiveness of the oxide layer by formation of less protective oxide
structures, that may allow transport of anions through it. It also increases conductivity
of the electrolyte that may promote galvanic effects [Col13]. In marine atmospheres, the
rate of chloride deposition and corrosion rates are related [Bar04]. Rates are typically
in the range of anywhere from 3-100 mg/m2d, (Scandinavian regions) [Col13]. Table
2.10 shows, that in all environments, corrosion rates decrease with time. Ferritic DCI
corrodes at approximately half the rate of mild steel in marine environments. Corrosion
rates for weathering steel in cities in England can be seen in Fig. 2.11.

2.2.4
Run-Off
Run-off, also called metal release in literature, is most seen studied in the context of
either preservation of historical structures or contamination due to local increase of
metal ions. In the cases of historical structures, zinc and copper are often the studied
materials. In cases of contamination, weathering steel is often studied.
For copper and zinc, it has been established, that the corrosion rates do not indicate
the concentration of run-off and that both proceed independently [FR05]. As corrosion
rates decrease with time, metal release can remain constant [Raf+16]. Some variation
occurs, but due to varying weather conditions. Here, run-off is most concentrated at the
beginning of rainfall, where the build-up of corrosion products can be released [FR05].
It is not possible to predict metal release by the study of the pure elements that the
alloy of interest consists of [FR05]. Metal release can be tested with samples placed in
field, with collection of the run-off in plastic containers for analysis. Measurements of
weight loss or thickness reduction is also seen in literature, along with documentation
of the appearance and structures of the corroded surfaces. This experimental method
requires a longer time span to get a full picture of the development of the patina.

2.3
Summary
• The microstructure of DCI is dependent on a range of factors, such as composition,
cooling rates, liquid treatments and heat treatments. In terms of alloying elements,
some may raise or lower the eutectiod start temperature, giving rise to either higher
or lower ratios of pearlite and ferrite.
• The solidification of thin walled hyper-eutectic DCI, is characterised by solidifica-
tion of spheroidal graphite due to Mg-additions. Upon reaching the eutectic line
temperature, austenite forms along with cementite, in a lamellar structure. Lastly,
at the eutectoid temperature, ferrite and pearlite form.
• Heat treatment of DCI include annealing to produce a ferritic matrix, normalizing
to produce a fine pearlitic matrix and lastly hardening to produce a martensitic
matrix.
• Corrosion of DCI in this case, can be characterised as atmospheric and uniform.
Here, graphite acts as a cathodic site for oxygen reduction and iron an anodic site
for iron dissolution.
• Oxide layers of iron vary and develop over time and in correlation with the specific
conditions. Different polymorphs exist with varying protective abilities. Protective
structures such as α-FeOOH and amorphous layers can be stabilized with low-level
alloying, in the case of steels.
• Corrosion rates of DCI are in some cases half of that of plain steels. For plain steels,
corrosion resistance can be increased by low-alloying (weathering steel), depending
on the environment.
• The presence of chloride in the environment can cause breakdown of protective
oxide layers. Chloride can promote formation of oxides that have less protective
abilities and allow for transport of anions. Chloride may also increase galvanic
effects.

were micrographed.
LOM micrographs were also taken on a Keyence VHX5000 digital microscope. Since

have varying height over the surface geometry. This allows for the entire image to be in

Salt Spray Testing Considerations
5.4.1
About Salt Spray Testing
Neutral salt spray (NSS) testing is a method for determining comparative atmospheric
corrosion rates by accelerated testing. The applicable standard, ISO 9227:2017, Corro-
sion tests in artificial atmospheres – Salt spray tests, mentions limitations to the method.
It is a comparative method, meaning results can be compared to other NSS results, but
do not indicate corrosion resistance under service conditions. Similarly, is does not in-
dicate long-term corrosion resistance, due to possible delayed stresses arising from the
corrosion product. Lastly, only coatings and substrates with a high degree of similarity
can be compared.
5.4.2
Salt Spray Test Chamber
The salt spray testing chamber (Fig. 5.7) is a Q-Fog Cyclic Corrosion Tester, model CCT
with a 600 liter chamber. Cycles consisted of 1,5 hours of wetting followed by 1 hour
of drying. The solution consisted of deionised water and 2.5 wt% NaCl. Temperature
for spraying was 35◦C. For drying temperature was 25◦C to provoke running and not

6.1
Summary
In this chapter, alloying elements are chosen along with their concentrations. The aim is
to later investigate their effects on corrosion rates and run-off. The final concentrations
were chosen:
• Cu: 0.5%
• Si: 2.5 and 4%
• Ni: 1.2%
• Cr: 0.4%
The levels chosen are based on realistic levels for implementation. Highly alloyed
cast irons are known to have high corrosion resistance, but for this specific context these
are out of scope due to cost considerations.
The alloying elements can be introduced briefly:
• Copper is uncomplicated to add since it has good solubility in iron. It has been
acknowledged in literature as effective for application where iron based alloys are
subjected to atmospheric corrosion.
• High silicon cast iron (above 3% Si) is known to be one of the most cost effective
corrosion resistant iron alloys, especially for applications where acids are present.
• Nickel has shown to improve protectiveness of the oxide layers formed in chloride
containing environments.
• Chromium is known to have a similar effect, by also improving protectiveness of
the formed oxide layers. It poses a risk of segregating and forming carbides. For
this specific case where cooling rates during casting are high, due to thin walled
specimens, the risk is estimated to be insignificant.
The following sections argue for the choice of alloys and their concentrations.

6.2
Predicted Mechanisms of Alloying for
Improving Corrosion Resistance
Alloying elements in DCI are predicted to have multiple effects on corrosion mechanisms:
• Alter microstructure of the DCI, by promoting a larger fraction of pearlite over
ferrite. Pearlite is known to have increased corrosion resistance compared to ferrite
[LaQ58].
• Alter the structures of the oxide layers forming at the surface and increase their
protective abilities.
• Alter electrochemical effects, due to presence of higher fraction of more noble
elements (compared to iron).
• Possibly alter kinetics effects due to the distribution of these elements.
6.3
Choice of Alloying Elements and
Concentrations
Some methods of alloying DCI for improved corrosion resistance, are already well estab-
lished. For cast iron, alloying elements used for this purpose are Si, Ni, Cr, Cu and Mo
[Dav96].
6.3.1
Silicon
Si can be added in ranges of 3-14% for improved corrosion resistance [Dav96]. It is one of
the most cost effective choices for applications requiring corrosion to resistance to acids.
When oxidizing, high silicon cast irons form strongly adherent surface films [Dav96].
Higher levels of silicon causes brittleness and casting difficulties.
Until 5% it is
improves strength [Ste+14] (Fig.6.1). Slightly above these levels, it first has a solution
embrittling effect and at even higher concentrations, graphite degenerates [Ste+14]. This
sets a limit to how much can be added, that depends on the strength required for the
specific application.

The sample castings are characterised to analyze the effects of the alloying elements

and Ni, that are added at lower levels, do not harden the matrix directly, but by

8.8
Uncertainties in Corrosion Testing
Many uncertainties were present in the NSS-testing method used. Since they are already
covered throughout the chapter, they will be summarized briefly.
Choice of testing method. Choice of testing method may have resulted in devel-
opment of patina that varies differently than that of outdoor conditions, both in visual
impression and in protectiveness. Less aggressive test methods may have given different
results. In order to fully investigate the issue, in field testing over a longer duration may
be needed.
Simulating atmospheric corrosion and outdoor conditions. Atmospheric cor-
rosion rates and run-off in marine areas, is not only dependent on TOW and chloride
concentrations. Sulphur, other pollutants and pH concentrations of the rainfall and sea
water will have an effect. Since the run-off issue was identified to be an issue in marine
areas, the presence of chloride was estimated to be the relevant factor to single out. The
corrosion rates and run-off severity may not be accurate to outdoor conditions, but their
relative resistance to chloride containing moisture will have validity.
Chamber conditions. Since results reflect where samples were placed within the
chamber, the chamber is suspected to give rise to uncertainties. More attention to tests
of these conditions and sample rotation could have been done.
Small sample size.
Effects of alloying were predicted to be larger.
The small
variance seen in weight loss of the alloyed samples requires a larger sample size in order
to verify whether the variations seen are random or actual effects of the alloying elements.
Varying sample sizes. Both cut and whole path guides were included in NSS test-
ing. This causes results that cannot be compared across samples of different geometries.
The effects of varying sample size/mass was not quantifiable. For the alloyed samples
set, it may be the source of the variation seen.
Surface roughness. Uncertainties include the differing surface roughness of the
individual sample and across samples. Removal of the outermost 10-20 µm is known to

9.1
Discussion
9.1.1
Effects of Alloying
Inspiration for choice of alloys and concentrations, was taken from known alloying of
cast irons, but also low-level alloying of steel (weathering steel).
In steels, alloying
elements have a significant positive effect on atmospheric corrosion resistance. The NSS
testing performed in this study indicates that this may not be applicable to ductile
cast iron. The cause of this, could be the fact that unalloyed ductile cast irons already
have a significantly higher corrosion resistance compared to steel (approximately double
according to Table 2.10). Possible positive effects of low-level alloying may dwindle in
comparison to the inherent corrosion resistance of DCI.
Since alloying has been done on such low levels, the main effects that can increase
corrosion resistance (apart from altering the oxides forming at the surface), are due to
the alloying elements ability to stabilize pearlite. Results from heat treatment indicate
that these effects are insignificant.
If the patinas developed, had variance in their protective abilities, it was presumably
seen in weight loss results. It may be, that if a un-alloyed whole path guide was included
in testing and compared to the alloyed path guides, the effect of copper would be no-
ticeable. The cut samples used to evaluate the effect of copper alloying leave a level of
uncertainty that can be prevented by using whole path guides for testing. This will be
included in future work.
9.1.2
Choice of Corrosion Testing Method and
Execution
The methods chosen may not be suited for test of the protective abilities of oxide layers.
Effects were predicted to be more apparent and therefore accelerated testing was chosen.
If methods closer to actual atmospheric corrosion rates were chosen instead, it may have
allowed for observations of any minor effects present.
The NSS testing performed is typically used for test of coatings. The method can
be used to either verify or reject the coatings protective ability by either pass/fail after
a set amount of hours. This indicates the aggressive nature of the NSS-testing method.
==PAGE:92==
82
9 Conclusion and Discussion
Oxide layer formation and development is a complex phenomenon [Col13]. It is the
authors opinion that these are best studied in field due to the time dependence that the
development of the layers required. It can also be discussed whether the patinas formed
during NSS-testing can be used as indicators of what patinas in field will look like over
time.
The execution of the NSS-testing was flawed due to varying sizes of the samples,
along with use of only one sample set of alloyed specimens, that did not include a whole
un-alloyed path guide.
9.2
Future Work
Due to the nature of the project, where casting of alloys is done once and the corrosion
testing time consuming, findings of the project have not been iterated upon. Known
uncertainties and mistakes have could not be set right.
• Due to lack of a whole un-alloyed sample in the NSS-weight experiment, this
experiment will be repeated. Three sample sets will be used and the duration
increased. The larger sample set may allow for confirmation of the lack of effects.
Samples will be rotated within the spray chamber to alleviate effects of variance
in conditions.
• It will be investigated whether an amorphous inner oxide layer is present on a
corroded un-alloyed and alloyed samples.
• Ideally, the alloyed samples would be subjected to field testing in a marine or
moderate marine environment. Run-off would be collected and analysed for ion
concentration. The final weight loss and thickness reduction would be measured
after several months. Patinas would be documented. In field corrosion testing
requires projects of long duration. Once set up, they require less attention and are
suited to run alongside other work.
• Findings that point in a direction for future work include silicon alloying, since
carbide formation can be avoided and the oxide layer adherence improved. Based
on the uneven patinas seen, lower concentrations than 4% can be considered for
future investigation. The effect of heat treatment could be investigated further by
heat treatment of an alloyed sample of choice. If effects seen are repeatable, heat
treatment in the form of premature shake out may be feasible in an automated
casting setting.
• Future work in the from field testing, could include study of the effects of heat
treatment on the visual evenness of the patina, since results indicate there may
be a correlation between fine structures like martensite and the evenness of the
patina.

9.3
Conclusion
This project investigates methods of preventing or alleviating corrosion run-off from
cast iron city inventory. The location of the observed issue is Danish coastal cities. The
run-off can result in severe staining of the area where items are mounted.
Corrosion run-off is an broader, more general issue observed from many iron and steel
structures in outdoor environments. These structures include items such as sculptures
and bridges.
The items mentioned are produced by a manufacturer of cast iron city inventory.
Before suggesting methods of preventing run-off, the items from their current production
at the company were studied, to gain understanding of the material that gives rise to
the issue.
The materials proposed for alleviation of run-off severity are alloys of ductile cast
iron. Alloying elements chosen are copper, nickel, chromium and silicon, in levels of
0.5, 1.2 and 4%, respectively. The choice of these elements and their concentrations,
are based on literature study. The chosen elements are known to increase corrosion
resistance of ductile cast iron.
Weathering steels were also a source of inspiration. They are low-alloyed with similar
elements in order to improve atmospheric corrosion resistance. The applicable method
of corrosion protection of these steels is formation of protective oxide layers, that are
stabilized by the alloying elements.
The effects of the chosen elements on corrosion rate is described in literature, but
effects on run-off were unknown and to be investigated.
Alloying elements were added to the baseline composition of ductile cast iron, that
was already in production at the mentioned company.
The samples for the study were cast, with varying alloying elements and combinations
thereof. Eight alloy types were cast in total, in order to analyze results in accordance
to a 23 level factorial experimental design. This would allow for calculation of effects on
corrosion weight loss, after corrosion testing.
Corrosion rate and run-off severity are known to be independent for copper and
zinc placed in outdoor environments. Both were therefore tested. The reasoning for
investigation corrosion rates in the form of weight loss, was due to the possible formation
of a protective oxide layer that would stall corrosion rates.
To simulate the environment in which the issue is observed, salt spray testing was
used to test alloys for run-off signs and corrosion rates.
Run-off was tested by mounting alloys onto tiles in the spraying chamber, with a
slight inclination. Since the run-off is an issue due to its visual appearance, the method
chosen was one where results could be evaluated visually.
Results show that the low-alloying, as performed in this study, had no effect on
either corrosion rates or run-off. Severe run-off was seen from all alloys after 227 hours
of spraying. The weight loss from samples after testing varied from 6.3 to 7%, which is
less than anticipated. The minor variations are not given any significance. The results
indicate that the intrinsic corrosion resistance of DCI may overshadow the minor effects

seen when low-alloying. It could also be due to the test method chosen (salt spray),
that may not simulate outdoor conditions and the way oxide layers are developed there,
resulting in oxide layers with less protective abilities.
The patina of the alloys that was developed under salt spray testing show, that the
baseline alloy with added copper gave the patina with the most even color and the least
amount of defects.
Un-alloyed samples were heat treated by annealing, normalizing and tempering. The
heat treatments showed insignificant effects in terms of weight loss.
The patina of
the tempered sample was much more even than the annealed sample. The normalized
sample can be ranked as lying between. This may indicate that fine microstructures of
the matrix may lead to a more even patina.
