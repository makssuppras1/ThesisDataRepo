Preface 
 
This thesis explores bacterial cellulose (BC) production and enhancement through innovative 
co-cultivation techniques involving Komagataeibacter xylinus and Pseudomonas putida sp. 
BC is a biomaterial of significant interest owing to its exceptional mechanical properties, 
biocompatibility, and potential applications in various industries, including biotechnology, 
medical devices, and environmental engineering. However, traditional methods of BC 
production often face limitations in yield and material properties. Recent research suggests 
that co-culturing BC-producing bacteria with other microorganisms can address some of 
these limitations by enhancing production and altering the characteristics of cellulose. 
 
This research focuses on co-culturing K. xylinus with P. putida sp., a bacterium known for 
producing bacterial cellulose and exopolysaccharides (EPS), respectively, that can integrate 
into the cellulose matrix, potentially improving the material's crystallinity and strength under 
ocean water media. Often, biobased manufacturing uses a significant amount of freshwater 
to produce the product of their interest. This work was motivated by the need to optimize BC 
production in ocean water, making the process sustainable and closer to greener production. 
The project faced significant challenges, particularly in the bioreactor part, which provides 
valuable insights and lays the groundwork for future research in optimizing co-cultivation 
systems for enhanced biomaterial production. 
 
The completion of this thesis would not have been possible without the support and guidance 
of my advisors, colleagues, and lab mates. Their expertise and encouragement were 
instrumental in navigating the complexities of this interdisciplinary project. I hope this 
research contributes meaningfully to biomaterials and inspires further innovations in 
sustainable BC production. An special mention is to be given to Divya for her incredible 
support.

Abstract 
Bacterial cellulose (BC) is a highly versatile biomaterial with wide-ranging applications in 
biotechnology, material science, and environmental engineering. Traditional methods of BC 
production primarily involve the cultivation of Komagataeibacter xylinus in various nutrient 
media. This study investigates the production of BC by co-culturing K. xylinus with 
Pseudomonas 
putida 
sp. 
in 
ocean 
water. 
Pseudomonas 
putida 
sp. 
produces 
exopolysaccharides (EPS), which integrate into the cellulose matrix, potentially improving the 
mechanical and structural properties of the resulting BC. Our results demonstrated a 
significant improvement in BC yield, with an increase of 8% compared to monoculture control. 
Additionally, the co-cultured BC revealed a crystallinity index of a 1.52-fold increase, 
representing a notable improvement over the control samples. These findings indicate that the 
incorporation of EPS from Pseudomonas putida sp. augments BC production and enhances 
the quality of the cellulose produced, making it more suitable for applications requiring higher 
strength and stability. Overall, this study highlights the potential of co-culturing K. xylinus with 
Pseudomonas putida sp. in ocean water as an effective strategy to optimize BC production 
and improve its material properties, paving the way for the development of advanced 
biomaterials with superior performance characteristics under reduced freshwater footprint.

Introduction  
a. Cellulose structure and synthesis 
Cellulose is an extracellular linear homopolysaccharide with a repeated structure of two long 
chains of β-D-glucopyranose linked by a β (1,4) glycosidic bond. Cellulose polymeric structure 
( 
) is formed by repetitions of thousands of glucose monomers linked by Van der Waals 
interactions and hydrogen bonds, which allow glucose units to stock parallelly and form a 
crystalline 3D structure 1. In nature, cellulose is mainly found in the form of microfibrils as the 
main constituent of the cell wall of plants, algae, and oomycetes, although various bacterial 
species can also synthesize it as nanofibers. 2,3The last is called bacterial cellulose (BC). 
Overall, cellulose is the most abundant biomaterial on Earth. 4 It has been used for centuries 
to make a wide range of products, from paper and textile goods to biomedical materials and 
renewable energy sources in the last decades. 3. 
 
Figure 1. Cellulose polymeric structure (n=degree of polymerization). Figure adapted from (Zhong, 2020). 
Each glucose unit is connected to the next by a glycosidic bond established between the C1 
anomeric carbon (with a β-configuration) and the C4 position of the neighbor glucose. Two 
hydrogen bonds (dashed black lines) are formed with each adjacent unit. 5.  
The elongation of the cellulose polymer takes place at its non-reducing end, the one that 
shows an unmodified C4 hydroxyl group, due to a nucleophilic substitution reaction in which 
a glucose unit from the uridine diphosphate glucose (UDP-glucose) is transferred to the free 
hydroxyl group (McNamara et al., 2015). For the reaction to take place, the acceptor hydroxyl 
group is first de-protonated. The orientation of the glucose units gives cellulose its 
characteristic amphipathic character.  The aggregation action driven by the additional Van der 
Waals interactions between the glucopyranose rings causes the molecule to self-associate, 
creating a macromolecular complex that becomes insoluble in water past a degree of 
polymerization of 6 to 8.  Depending on its origin and treatment, cellulose polymers from plant 
sources can range from 1000 up to 15,000 glucose units 3,6. 
 
==PAGE:5==
5 
 
Cellulose fibers obtained from wood and plants show similar composition and complex 
structure as chains are packed in layers as nanofibrils. They are linked to other biopolymers 
like hemicellulose and lignin or pectin that held the cellulose layers together and maintain the 
structural integrity of the tree or plant 7.  
 
In plants, the cell wall is divided into primary and secondary cell wall (PCW and SCW). The 
last is the part that presents the higher cellulose proportion. The cellulose content in plants is 
generally 30-75%, and in wood, 40-50% 3,8. Some authors estimate that around one-third of 
the total mass of many plants is cellulose. 6.  
Four types of crystalline cellulose have been described (I, II, III, and IV). The first kind exists 
in natural products and is divided into two others: triclinic (Iα) and monoclinic structure (Iβ), 
which seems to present a lower thermodynamic stability. The proportion at which these two 
allomorphs are found in natural products is characteristic of the origin of the cellulose. Bacterial 
cellulose shows Iα in around 0.6 mass fraction ratio while cellulose Iβ is dominant in the higher 
plants with a mass fraction of ∼0.8 9. 
 
Cellulose biosynthesis is a sophisticated natural process achieved by cellulose synthase 
enzymatic complex (CeS), which uses UDP-glucose as a substrate. (Saxena & Brown, 2005). 
The detailed synthesis mechanisms in different organisms are not yet known. They can vary 
considerably by kingdoms, although the catalytic subunit of the enzyme seems to be 
conserved and has been identified as bacterial cellulose synthase subunit A (BcsA) in 
prokaryotes and cellulose synthase subunit A (CeSA) in eukaryotes. 5. 
The model organism for plants is Arabidopsis thaliana, for which 10 CeSA proteins have been 
identified, although only 3 of those are needed to conform to a functional cellulose synthase 
complex. The process occurs in the plasma of plant cells, where the membrane-integrated 
enzymatic complex or rosette is found. CeSA 1, 3 and 6 are involved in the biosynthesis in the 
PCW, while in SCW, cellulose synthesis is controlled by the subunits 4, 7, and 8 8.  
 
The catalytic mechanism has been described by McNamara et al., 2015 as a classic SN2-like 
substitution reaction. As a nucleophile, the non-reducing C4- hydroxyl group of the polymer 
attacks the C1 carbon of the donor UDP-glucose molecule. Simultaneously, the nucleophile 
loses a proton, and the substrate is stabilized through the union of a divalent metal cation, 
usually Mg2+ or Mn2+, to its pyrophosphate group. In this process, the donor’s glucose 
anomeric carbon configuration is inverted from α when it is attached to UDP to β when it is 
attached to the cellulose polymer. This shift forces the UDP to exit as a leaving group. The 
polymer elongation requires an adequate location of the next glucose unit after each catalysis 
step. However, the mechanism that rules over the cellulose's translocation and the polymer's

from the biosynthetic machinery due to shearing forces or that the enzymes stall due to 
increasing polymer interactions in the extracellular space (McNamara et al., 2015).

d. Bacterial cellulose (BC) characteristics and structure 
While cellulose is a more sustainable alternative to petroleum-based plastics and polymers, 
the extraction processes to obtain its derivatives and to separate cellulose from the polymers 
that forms its natural structure (lignin, hemicellulose and pectin) are not efficient. Plant-
cellulose extraction requires the use of harsh chemicals and large quantities of water and 
energy, which, altogether, don’t provide a solution to current environmental concerns 12. In this 
regard, the use of microbial fermentation to produce cellulose has become an interesting and 
fast-growing alternative. Bacterial cellulose (BC) can be naturally synthetized by a wide variety 
of bacteria such as those from the genera Gluconoacetobacter (before known as acetobacter), 
Rhizobium, Azotobacter, Agrobacterium and Pseudomonas, among others 9.  
 
The best-known bacterial cellulose (BC) producer bacteria belong to the acetic acid family 
Acetobacteraceae, which are obligated aerobes capable of oxidizing alcohols, aldehydes and 
sugars to acetic acid in aerobic conditions. Komagataeibacter and the recently described 
Novacetimonas are highlighted as the best producers. The bacteria Komagataeibacter 
europaeus, 
Komagataeibacter 
medellinensis, 
Komagataeibacter 
rhaeticus, 
Komagataeibacter xylinus, and Novacetimonas hansenii are well recognized as the most 
stronger producers of cellulose and are commonly employed as model organisms in the 
investigation of cellulose (BC) and its various uses 1.  In fact, K. xylinus is the main producer 
organisms for kombucha and traditional Philippine dessert nata de coco 13.  
 
In these bacteria, BC forms a protective layer that although is not essential for survival, 
provides a considerable advantage 14. The exact function of this complex polysaccharide in 
bacteria remains a question. Some hypotheses suggest that the biofilm that is formed from 
the cellulose nanofibers a) helps the cells to keep close contact with the highly oxygenated 
surface of the media; b) provides a protection against ultra-violet light and other critical 
environment alterations such as pH (Buldum & Mantalaris, 2021; c) enhance nutrient 
availability by improving transmembrane transport and protecting against heavy ions and 
metals 15.  
 
The chemical structure of bacterial cellulose is almost identical to its plant-derived counterpart. 
While plant cellulose presents “impurities” in the form of residual polymers, BC produced via 
fermentation is free of these residues. Remarkably, bacterial cellulose resulting from 
fermentation processes, yield a high-purity product with traces of nutrients, metabolic by-
products and microorganisms that can be easily discarded and without the cellulosic materials 
(hemicellulose, lignin, pectin and waxes) associated with most plant-based cellulose

extraction 15. Differences on the three-dimensional structure confers bacterial cellulose 
improved properties like higher tensile strength and water holding capacity, biocompatibility, 
and degradability. In addition to its traditional use in food industry, bacterial cellulose offers a 
broad range of uses in biomedical applications thanks to its biocompatibility such as wound 
dressing 1,14,16. 
e. Bacterial cellulose biosynthesis in Komagataeibacter xylinus 
Bacterial cellulose production efficiency in K. xylinus is extremely high, being able to 
polymerize 200,000 glucose molecules into nanofibers each second. Komagataeibacter 
xylinus is an extensively described microorganism for commercial BC fermentation 9. The 
Komagataeibacter genera can synthetize two allomorphic forms of cellulose: the more 
crystalized but less stable form I, which is found in higher proportion; and the allomorph II 1.  
BC synthesis in K. xylinus is a complex mechanism that can be divided in two stages: 
Intracellular polymerization and extracellular self-assembly into nanofibers (Zhong, 2020). The 
polymerisation of cellulose takes place intracellularly and consist of four enzymatic steps. First, 
glucose is phosphorylated by a glucokinase (GK) and converted into glucose-6-phosphate 
(G6P). G6P is then isomerized to glucose-1-phosphate (G1P) by a phosphoglucomutase 
(PGM) enzyme. UDP-glucopyrophosphorilase (UDPhase) transforms G1P into UDP-Glucose 
(UDP-Glc), which is the precursor utilised by cellulose synthase (CS) in the polymerisation 
process to form β1,4-glucan chains 4.  
The crystalline nanofibers are immediately secreted to the extracellular region, where the 
molecules self-assembly into crystalline nanofibers due to the high tension produced by the 
hydrogen bonds and other inter and intra molecular forces like Van der Waals interactions 1. 
As new fibres co-crystallize and accumulate, a visible cellulose pellicle of increased density 
and volume is formed 1,17,18.

In K. xylinus, the biosynthesis and membrane transport of bacterial cellulose (BC) are mainly 
regulated by the bcsABCD operon, which encodes the four main proteins in the cellulose 
synthase complex (CS). BcsA catalyses the polymerization of UDP-Glc units and facilitates 
the allosteric regulation of BC synthesis through cyclic di-GMP. BcsA along with BcsB are 
responsible for the pore formation in the cytoplasmic membrane. BcsC is the periplasmatic 
protein that created the β-barrel pore in the outer membrane that allows for the glucan chain 
transport to the extracellular region. Finally, BcsD takes care of the correct orientation of the 
CS complex 1. 
f. Bacterial cellulose production conditions 
The standard growth media for bacterial cellulose production is the HS media, in which 
glucose is the main carbon source. Several monosaccharides, oligosaccharides, organic 
acids, alcohols, and sugar alcohols such as molasses, fructose, mannitol and even agricultural 
residues have been successfully used as alternative carbon sources to reduce production 
costs and improve sustainability 15,16,19.  While the culture media is the most important aspect 
in the BC production, cultivation conditions (pH, agitation, temperature) have a determinant 
impact on the yield and the cellulose quality. How exactly they influence the metrics of the 
process remains a contradictory discussion topic. BC is usually produced in static conditions 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
at an optimal temperature of 30ºC and a pH around 6 although it can take place in a pH range 
of 4.5 to 7.5 9,20.  
 
Similarly to most fermentations, oxygen availability is a critical factor to consider in BC 
production. K. xylinus and other usually used BC producers from the Gluconoacetobacter 
genera are obligated aerobes. Thus, they use the cellulose membrane to reside closer to the 
air-liquid interface in the culturing vessel. While agitation is the most common way guarantee 
oxygen transfer in fermentations, in the BC production case, the macromolecular structure is 
disrupted when the culture is agitated, yielding lower quantities of BC in the form of small 
irregular pellets. It has been argued that static cultivations present higher genetic stability, 
which helps maintain higher yields 15,21.  
 
g. Exopolysaccharides (EPS) characteristics, composition and structure  
Exopolysaccharides (EPS) are high molecular weight extracellular compounds produced by 
microorganisms as defensive biomacromolecules in response to stress conditions or 
environmental changes such as nutrient deficiency, heat stress and drought 22 and even high 
salt concentrations as EPS bind to cations including Na+ 23.  
The specific composition of these polymeric compounds can vary significantly according to 
the organisms’ needs, leading to an enormous molecular diversity. Four hundred structurally 
different EPS types have been identified and many order are still unknown 24.  Depending on 
its composition, EPS can be homopolymers or heteropolymers formed by sugars (from a wide 
range of monosaccharides) and non-sugar components linked by an extensive list of linkage 
types, for example: α-D glucans, β-D glucans, fructans, polygalactans, etc. The presence of 
different functional groups have the potential to change the properties and functions of 
exopolysaccharides 22. EPS composition from Pseudomonas putida grown in 2% glycerol and 
2% xylose media was described to be formed mainly of glycerol and galactose 
monosaccharides respectively 25. Other studies state that the composition of the EPS biofilm 
from Pseudomonas putida is predominantly formed by glucose and rhamnose and depending 
on the stress conditions that the strain was subjected to, other glycosylic compounds could be 
found. Under NaCl stresses, mannose becomes the second most abundant compound after 
glucose, while in the case of PEG presence, mannuronic acid is the second most relevant 26. 
EPS can be produced closely attached to the cell surface, capsular EPS, or secreted to the 
extracellular region where they can be found slightly attached or free 27. EPS present a highly 
hydrated structures due to their capacity to effectively bind a substantial quantity of water 
thanks to the presence of hydrogen bonds between hydroxyl groups, such as polysaccharides, 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
and water molecules, as well as electrostatic interactions 28. As a result of their properties, 
EPS have a large potential in fields like medicine, agriculture, bioremediation and construction 
as a replacement to popular petrol-based polymers like polyacrylate, polyvinyl alcohols 24.  
h. Biosynthesis of exopolysaccharides 
All classes of microorganisms belonging to several phylogenetic groups are able to synthesize 
exopolysaccharides 29. Some of the most relevant are Acetobacter, Pseudomonas, 
Xanthomonas, Bacilli such as Bacillus, Lactobacillus and Streptococcus 27. Several lactic acid 
bacteria (LAB) have the potential to create EPS that can be used as texturizing agents in the 
food industry 29.  
 
Among all of them, Pseudomonas putida is a versatile soil bacterium that is able to survive 
freely also in liquid environments. P. putida is known to produce alginate to create hydrated 
environments and reduce the effects of water limitation 26 by acting as an antioxidant agent to 
decrease reactive species (ROS) 30. KT2440 strain stands out as a laboratory workhorse 
widely used due to its robust metabolism 31. In addition to alginate, Pseudomonas putida 
KT2440 possess the genes for cellulose biosynthesis (bcs) 32 and two additional gene clusters 
that have been identify to encode for  exopolysaccharide a (pea) and exopolysaccharide b 
(peb) 33,34. 
 
To the best of this author’s knowledge, the complete biosynthesis pathway for the 
exopolysaccharides production in Pseudomonas putida KT2440 has not yet been described. 
Similarly to the biosynthesis of BC in K. xylinus, in general terms, EPS biosynthesis starts with 
the production of polymers’ building blocks: activated sugars such as UDP-glucose and GDP-
mannose. They are derived from the central metabolism route and the pentose phosphate 
(PP) pathway. In the next phase the polymerization of the sugars nucleotides takes place by 
the action of glycosyltransferases enzymes. Finally, the EPS is moved across the membrane 
into the extracellular matrix 35.   
i. BC-EPS interaction  
In the search to improve bacterial cellulose production, the effect of adding polysaccharides 
to cellulose cultures has been previously investigated 36,37. Chao et at., 2001 proved that when 
0.1% (w/w) of agar was added to a 50L airlift fermentation, BC production was improved by 
nearly 30%. Some studies found that the addition of purified EPS into the cultivation improved 
the BC quality, showing improved Young’s modulus of the BC pellicles 18. The specific 
interaction mechanisms between BC and EPS are particular to the organisms and 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
polysaccharide type. For example, the addition of 2% pullulan increased the BC production in 
Komagataeibacter hansenii by more than 4-fold 38. 
 
Despite the yield enhancement that the addition of pure polysaccharides can provide, it is not 
a cost-effective or sustainable option. The use of co-culture strategies arises as an alternative 
where a polysaccharides – forming strain is grown in conjunction with cellulose-forming 
organism to enhance BC production and improve the quality of the end cellulosic product  
13,18,20.

b. Glycerol stock preparation and cell propagation  
Glycerol Stock Preparation for Pseudomonas putida 
Glycerol stocks for P. putida  were prepared by adapting the protocol described by 40. A single 
colony was picked from an LB-agar plate stored at 4ºC and transferred to LB media (10 g/L 
tryptone, 5 g/L yeast extract, and 10 g/L NaCl) in a 12mL test tube, with 10-20% working 
volume. The culture was incubated at 30ºC under static conditions. Upon reaching mid-
exponential phase (OD600 = 0.6-1) as described by 41, 25 mL of fresh LB media in a 250 mL 
shake flask was inoculated with 5% of the initial culture volume. The culture was incubated 
under the same conditions until it again reached mid-exponential phase. At this point, 0.5 mL 
of the culture was mixed with 0.5 mL of 50% glycerol solution in a cryovial. The cryovials were 
stored at -70ºC for future use.

Cell propagation of Pseudomonas putida

culture into a 250 mL shake flask containing 25 mL of LB media. The culture was incubated at

g. Product extraction and purification 
➢ Exopolysaccharides extraction 
The exopolysaccharides (EPS) production ability of Pseudomonas putida was studied under 
different ocean water proportions, carbon sources and yeast extract concentrations. The 
precise composition, and therefore the properties, of the EPS obtained under each of the 
studied conditions, particularly ocean water cultivations, are unknown. However, the solubility 
of most EPS in aqueous solutions has been widely reported 22,24,43,44. The presence of two 
types of EPS, cell-bound and soluble of EPS, was described in Pseudomonas putida G7 43. 
The two fractions presented identical monosaccharides compositions and properties. Ethanol 
precipitation is a commonly used exopolysaccharides purification method that effectively 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
separates EPS from proteins without disrupting the structure of the compound 45. Ethanol 
reduces the solubility of EPS in aqueous solutions, causing the precipitation of the polymer 46.  
 
EPS was extracted day by day or after 5 days of cultivation following an adaptation from the 
protocol described  (Chug et al., 2021) as depicted in Figure 8. Briefly, bacterial cells were 
separated from the EPS-containing broth by centrifugation at 8000 rpm for 20 min at 4ºC. The 
liquid fraction underwent an overnight precipitation at 4ºC with 2 volumes of absolute ethanol 
(EtOH).  The precipitated EPS was recovered by centrifugation. The cell-attached EPS fraction 
found with the cells after centrifugation was separated from the cells by ethanol precipitation 
using one volume of absolute ethanol. The supernatant contained the EPS detached from the 
cell pellet, which was discarded. EPS from the cell-attached fraction was finally recovered by 
centrifugation. The two fractions were lyophilised for approximately 2 hours or dried at 60ºC 
for 1 hour and finally weighted separately.

➢ Phenol-sulfuric acid method 
An adaptation of the commonly used sulfuric acid method 48 was followed as described by 
Rühmann et al., 2015In this protocol, 96-well microtiter plates are used instead of cuvettes to 
measure the carbohydrates content. Purified EPS samples were resuspended in 1mL of MiliQ 
water. 50 µL of the solution was added to a well, followed by 150 µL of concentrated sulfuric 
acid and 30 µL of phenol (5% wv). The plate was then incubated for 5 min at 90ºC to develop 
the colour, and OD was measured at approximately 490nm when cooled down.  
The phenol-sulfuric acid method is a quantitative approach to indirectly determine the total 
carbohydrate content of a polysaccharides sample 49. Highly concentrated sulfuric acid breaks 
down the glycosidic bonds that held together polysaccharides’ structure, separating it in its 
monomeric constituents. Further dehydration reactions convert the monosaccharide into 
furfural derivates. Pentoses are transformed into furfural while hexoses are converted into 
hydroxymethylfurfural (HMF). The phenol condenses the furfural derivates, forming a yellow-
orange coloured complex. The colour intensity is proportional to the sugars concentration and 
can be measured through spectroscopy at approximately 490nm (Nielsen, 2017; Yue et al., 
2022). Glucose is the default standard for sugar content determination, however in 
heteropolysaccharides, the different monosaccharides resulting from the hydrolysis have 
structural and physicochemical properties differences. For those polysaccharides composed 
of 6-carbon sugars (fructose, galactose, rhamnose) and arabinose, glucose calibration can be 
used. However, when mannose, ribose, fucose and acid sugars are predominant, a correction 
factor should be applied to the glucose calibration or a new curve for the main component 
should be made 49. EPS is a complex heteropolysaccharide and its composition highly 
depends on the nutrients and cultivation conditions. Hence, no previous information regarding 
the specific monosaccharide’s composition under the proposed conditions could be found. 
Thus, calibration curves for the suspected main components (glucose, mannose, galactose, 
ribose) were made using the corresponding wavelength (490nm, 481nm, 491nm, 480nm, 
respectively (found in appendix).

Discussion 
 
1. Effect of media in EPS production and cell growth  
 
The characterization of P.  putida growth and EPS production in Figure 11 at high substrate 
concentrations (20 and 10 g/L of potassium acetate + 5g/L YE) suggested that the use of 
ocean water in the fermentations can significantly improve EPS production compared to fresh 
water and LB. EPS obtained from 100% ocean water cultures a was 5 and 3 times higher than 
their freshwater counterpart in 20g/L and 10 g/L of potassium acetate respectively.  
 
It has been reported that Pseudomonas putida KT2440 responds to certain stress conditions 
modulating its gene expression to producing exopolysaccharides (EPS), particularly alginate. 
For example, under water-limiting conditions, alginate production increases, contributing to 
biofilm architecture and stress tolerance 22,26. Other studies demonstrated the upregulation of 
alginate genes within 4 hours after being subjected to hyperosmotic stress 52 and reported 
increased EPS production in increased stress levels (1.4M salt and 50ºC) 23. Alginate in 
particular helps to maintain a hydrated microenvironment that protects cells from desiccation 
and improve survival rates (Chang et al., 2007). In addition to alginate, other EPS compounds 
can also be produced cellulose, pea, and peb 52. The P.  putida KO strain used in this study 
presents deletions in the some of the main genes involved in cellulose synthesis and secretion 
(ABC transporter and glucokinase), which might redirect the metabolic flux towards the EPS 
production. Studies reported that under stress conditions when cellulose-producing genes are 
deleted, there’s a “compensation” mechanism that upregulates those genes involved in EPS 
production to help alleviate the effects of adverse environmental conditions. The effect of this 
genetic compensation was immediate however, under persisting stress, EPS production 
returned to normal levels 53. This corresponds to the results obtained of EPS concentration for 
4 days (Figure 13) in 2g/L of potassium acetate, in which after the second day, EPS 
concentrations declined in all of the studied conditions. 
 
Overall, EPS production due to osmolarity stress response was validated in the rest of the 
studied substrate compositions. Those media with higher ocean water % yielded higher EPS 
concentrations than freshwater or 50% ocean water media when compared within the same 
carbon source concentrations. 
 
Stress levels are not the only factor that determines EPS production in P.  putida. Carbon 
sources and their concentration play a most important role 47. In 2g/L of potassium acetate

2. Co-culture effect on cellulose concentration  
In the co-culture experiments performed in 50 mL tubes, it was found that simultaneous 
inoculation of P.  putida was only successful in cellulose production in 50% OW at the lowest 
P.  putida inoculation percentage (0.5%) (figure 17). No cellulose was produced in HS media. 
This suggested that when inoculated at the same time, P.  putida’s faster metabolism is prompt 
to overgrow the media, creating disadvantageous conditions that inhibit cellulose production 
by K. xylinus. The effect was even more pronounced in HS media and at higher P.  putida 
inoculation %. Lower Xylinus/Putida inoculation ratios were tested under a new co-inoculation 
day. Inoculation of Pseudmonas at the second day of cultivation was successful to improve 
BC production at all inoculation percentages. Up to 93% improvement was observed in 50% 
OW; 0.5% P.  putida when compared against the monocultures. These positive results 
suggested that early growth stages of the K. xylinus cultures are more susceptible to be 
negatively affected by the presence of P.  putida, Overall, the higher performances were seen 
in the 50% ocean water conditions, which is consistent with the stress-response 
exopolysaccharide production discussed.  
 
Some studies propose that the improved BC production results from co-cultivations are 
consequences of positive cell-cell interactions between EPS and BC producers as well as of 
the incorporation of EPS in the cellulose matrix 1. Co-culturing K. xylinus with Bacillus cereus 
enhanced BC yield from 1.2 to 4.4 g/L, which was attributed to acetoin and 2,3-butanediol 
production by B. cereus 54. Similarly, co-culturing K. hansenii with Aureobasidium pullulans, 
resulted in a 4.5-fold increase in BC production and a 6-fold improvement in Young's modulus 
thanks to pullulan 38. However, not all co-cultivation strategies are beneficial as seen also in 
this study. Other studies have found that when co-culturing K.intermedius with Dekkera 
bruxellensis in molasses media, BC yield decreased 24% despite the growth increase 55.

3. Scalability  
 
From the 50mL falcons to the shake-flasks (250mL) cellulose production increased from 
around 1 g/L in HS and 0.2 g/L in 50% OW, to close to 3 g/L and 0.4 g/L respectively. In 
contrast, from the shake flasks to the 1L GSTR reactors concentrations of BC decreased 
significantly.  
 
Scaling up bacterial cellulose production has supposed a challenge for many due to labour-
intensive tasks, high production costs and space requirements for static cultivations 56. Thus, 
the use of bioreactors offered an alternative that could help taggle the inconvenient that come 
with these fermentations. Innovative approaches proposed a two-stages cultivation. First, K. 
xylinus is grown in static conditions to help promote the attachment of the cells to the BC. The 
second stage is initiated using fresh media with the BC pellicle from the previous stage 57. This 
strategy demonstrated to obtain improved BC yields.  
 
The cultivation strategy used in the GSTRs, consisting of an initial K. xylinus growth stage in 
agitated conditions for 24 hours, failed to demonstrate its efficiency. Thus, the opposite 
approach, starting from a long-static cultivation of K. xylinus and followed by P.  putida 
inoculation, might be interesting to investigate. 
 
Another factor that might have influenced negatively the scale-up process is the surface area 
of the cultivation vessels. A larger surface area that allows for BC formation in the liquid-gas 
interphase. Some studies have investigated on the correlation of BC yield with the surface 
area of culture vessels. It was observed that traditional vertical-walled containers limit BC 
thickness due to a "wall effect," where growing cellulose moves downwards into the nutrient 
broth 58.

4. Material testing and characterisation studies 
SEM revealed detailed information about the surface characteristics of bacterial cellulose, 
including the arrangement and diameter of the cellulose fibrils. The images provide insight into 
how different growth conditions, such as media composition or the presence of co-cultured 
organisms, influence the fibril network and overall texture of the cellulose. This was consistent 
with the findings of (Liu et al., 2021) which show the dense, interwoven nature of bacterial 
cellulose fibres, affected by the presence of extracellular polymeric substances (EPS) as 
shown in Map data1 figure 30 a. The addition of EPS alters the appearance of the fibres, 
potentially making them more irregular or less densely packed, depending on the cultivation 
conditions. From the FTIR peaks, the presence of EPS introduces shifts in existing peaks, 
reflecting the contribution of proteins, lipids, or nucleic acids. For example, peaks around 
1660-1535 cm⁻¹ could indicate the presence of proteins, while those around 1080 cm⁻¹ might 
correspond to carbohydrate structures different from those in cellulose. XRD patterns are 
widely used for cellulose crystal structure analysis and crystallinity calculations. For BC

corresponded to the (100), (010), and (110) crystallographic planes, respectively. The co-
cultured BC and the control BC all exhibited the three major peaks.  After processing

Conclusions 
 
This study demonstrates the potential of in-co-culture fermentation to enhance bacterial 
cellulose (BC) production and improve its mechanical properties compared to traditional 
monoculture setups. The findings highlight the significant influence of fermentation scale on 
co-culture outcomes, with the smaller-scale fermentations showing more success than the 
larger-scale reactor conditions. Although unexpected factors may have inhibited 
Komagataeibacter xylinus growth in larger reactors, the success at a smaller scale 
underscores the importance of optimizing co-culture conditions. 
The study also found that the extracellular polymeric substances (EPS) produced during co-
culture fermentation interact with BC microfibrils, affecting their bundling and aggregation 
behaviour. This interaction suggests that while BC production was lower, further optimization 
of the co-culture conditions could enhance both yield and material properties. 
The research supports the hypothesis that the composition of the co-culture media can 
influence BC production and its crystallinity properties. A significant decrease in BC production 
was observed in bioreactor settings, likely due to competition for nutrients and space between 
K. xylinus and Pseudomonas putida. Further investigation into the interactions between co-
cultured microorganisms at reactor scale is needed, but the co-culture method shows promise 
for producing BC with improved characteristics. This preliminary work lays a foundation for 
future optimization and scale-up studies, with potential commercial applications. 
Additionally, using considerable amount of ocean water supplemented with cost-effective 
media achieved cellulose with better properties than more complex and expensive media. This 
finding suggests that ocean water is a cost-effective and sustainable alternative for BC 
production opening avenues for diverse applications.

Future perspectives 
The co-culture of Komagataeibacter xylinus and Pseudomonas putida sp. shows significant 
promise for improving bacterial cellulose (BC) production and material properties. However, 
the initial reactor system used in ocean water conditions encountered several operational 
challenges, leading to suboptimal BC yields. This underperformance highlights the necessity 
of optimizing both cultivation conditions and reactor design to fully realize the potential of this 
co-culture system. 
1. Optimization of Reactor Design and Cultivation Conditions 
➢ Challenges with Current Reactor System: The existing reactor design failed to sustain 
the co-culture effectively under ocean water conditions. Issues such as inadequate nutrient 
diffusion, oxygen supply, and the accumulation of metabolic by-products likely contributed 
to the low BC yields. 
➢ Membrane Reactor Approach: Future research will focus on developing a membrane 
reactor system, which can offer better control over environmental parameters and 
microbial interactions. A membrane reactor would allow for more stable cultivation 
conditions by managing the diffusion of nutrients, oxygen, and metabolic by-products, 
crucial for maintaining a balanced co-culture and maximizing BC production. 
➢ Fine-Tuning Operational Parameters: Optimizing parameters such as temperature, pH, 
and nutrient concentrations within the membrane reactor is essential. By carefully 
controlling these factors, it is possible to enhance the productivity and quality of BC 
produced by the co-culture. 
2. Improved EPS Quantification and Understanding 
➢ Need for Accurate EPS Quantification: Current methods for extracellular polymeric 
substances (EPS) quantification are insufficient at low volumes, with total sugar 
quantification being affected by the monosaccharide composition of the samples. To better 
understand the EPS-BC interaction, more accurate determination methods and a detailed 
analysis of EPS composition are needed, particularly for ocean water (OW) cultivations. 
The molecular diversity of EPS is vast, with over 400 structurally different types identified, 
and many more yet to be discovered. 
 
==PAGE:58==
58 
 
➢ Variations in EPS Composition Under Stress: Studies have shown that the composition 
of EPS monosaccharides can vary depending on stress conditions, such as temperature 
and salinity. For instance, research by Sandhya & Ali (2015) demonstrated that under 
stress conditions like high temperatures and salt concentrations, the production and 
monosaccharide composition of EPS increased. Understanding how these stress-induced 
changes in EPS composition can influence BC production could be key to improving the 
incorporation of EPS into cellulose, potentially enhancing its properties.
