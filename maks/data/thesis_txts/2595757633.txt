Abstract
This thesis deals with material optimization of three-dimensional truss structures subject
to constraints representing the physical phenomena of equilibrium, yielding, global- and
local stability, which ensure that the optimized structure is safe.
In the literature, the global stability constraint is commonly implemented using a semidef-
inite formulation that is computationally expensive. This thesis seeks to reduce the com-
putational strain imposed by the global stability constraint by investigating alternative
representations of the constraint. Furthermore, the optimization schemes found in the
literature are based on either a rigid-plastic or linear elastic material model. By utiliz-
ing such formulations, it is impossible to efficiently optimize a structure in the two most
common limit states of the Eurocode, namely the ultimate- and serviceability limit state,
simultaneously. Therefore, this thesis also seeks to present an optimization scheme that
can combine both material models in separate load cases.
Initially, it is attempted to implement the global stability constraint by utilizing linear
programming. However, this approach is found to be unviable. Therefore, the common
semidefinite formulation is adopted and simplified by applying modal truncation. This
approach shows promising results for the linear elastic material model, as it was possible
to reduce the computation time by approximately 80%. However, for the rigid-plastic
material model, the approach is unusable in its current state.
An optimization scheme that can consider a mixture of elastic and plastic load cases
is developed by combining the commonly used separate formulations of each material
model in a single system matrix. This approach leads to a working formulation that,
unfortunately, becomes unstable for certain systems. It is briefly attempted to improve
the stability of the formulation, but to no avail. Therefore, the combined optimization
scheme presented in this thesis is not particularly robust. However, the formulation
works well for some systems. This is illustrated by applying the formulation on a section
of a suspension bridge in the end of the thesis.

Resumé
Dette speciale omhandler materialeoptimering af tredimensionelle gitterkonstruktioner,
underlagt betingelser, der repræsenterer de fysiske fænomener: ligevægt, flydning, global-
og lokal stabilitet, som sikrer, at den optimerede konstruktion er sikker.
I litteraturen implementeres den globale stabilitetsbetingelse almindeligvis ved hjælp
af en semidefinit formulering, der er meget beregningstung.
Dette speciale ønsker
at mindske denne beregningsbyrde, ved at udforske alternative repræsentationer af
betingelsen. Ydermere er optimeringsalgoritmerne, som er præsenteret i litteraturen,
baseret på enten en stift-plastisk eller en lineær elastisk materialeopførsel. Ved at an-
vende disse formuleringer, er det ikke muligt at optimere en konstruktion effektivt i de
to mest almindelige grænsetilstande beskrevet i Eurocode, nemlig brudgrænse- og an-
vendelsesgrænsetilstanden, samtidig. Derfor ønsker dette speciale også at præsentere en
algoritme, der kan kombinere de to materialemodeller i separate lasttilfælde.
Indledningsvist forsøges det at implementere den globale stabilitetsbetingelse ved at an-
vende lineær programmering. Det erfares dog, at denne metode ikke er brugbar. Derfor
forsøges det i stedet at simplificere den velkendte semidefinitte formulering vha. modal
trunkering. Denne metode viser lovende resultater for den lineær elastiske materiale-
model, idet det er muligt at reducere beregningstiden med 80%. Metoden er dog ikke, i
dens nuværende tilstand, brugbar for den stift-plastiske materialemodel.
En optimeringsalgoritme, der kan håndtere en blanding af elastiske og plastiske lasttil-
fælde, udvikles ved at kombinere de, fra litteraturen kendte, formuleringer for hver af
de to materialemodeller i én samlet systemmatrix. Denne fremgangsmetode fører til
en brugbar formulering, som dog, desværre, er ustabil for visse tilfælde. Det forsøges
kortvarigt at forbedre stabiliteten af formuleringen, dog uden effekt. Derfor er den kom-
binerede optimeringsalgoritme, som bliver præsenteret i dette speciale, ikke synderligt
robust. Formuleringen fungerer dog godt for nogle systemer. Dette illustreres ved at
benytte formuleringen på et udsnit af en hængebro i slutningen af afhandlingen.

Introduction
Infrastructure is an essential cornerstone of the everyday life of people in the globalized
world. Roads connecting cities, harbors connecting continents and bridges facilitating
unobstructed passage over bodies of water or canyons. Indeed, it is difficult to imagine
what the modern world would look like without all of these.
The evolution of these infrastructural elements has been drastic ever since their con-
ception. Few of these have, however, underwent a progression as substantial as that of
bridges. With bridges ranging from the Arkadiko Bridge, one of the oldest arch bridges
still in use and existence today [McAneney, 2020], with a culvert span of 1 m, to the
modern-day Akashi Kaikyō Bridge with a main span of 1991 m [JB honshi-kōsoku, 2020],
the evolution of bridge design is apparent. In recent times, however, the main span of
suspension bridges has ceased to increase.
The longest achievable main span of suspension bridges can be estimated, by realizing
that the limiting factor is the stress in the main cable [Tang, 2017]. Typically, 80%
- 90% of the total load on the main cable is dead load [Chen and Duan, 2014]. The
maximum span length is, therefore, mainly controlled by the self weight of the girder.
This indicates that, unless a stronger cable material is developed, the dead load of the
bridge girder must be reduced, e.g by material optimization, to facilitate longer spans.
Furthermore, in these times of climate change, the construction industry must do its part
to reduce carbon dioxide emissions. The production of building materials accounted for
11% of the world’s total carbon dioxide emissions in 2015 [Aberge, Dean, and Dulac,
2017]. Therefore, it is evident that there also is an environmental benefit associated
with material optimization in Civil Engineering.
In this context, material optimization means minimization of the structural volume
subject to physical constraints, which ensure that the optimized structure is mechanically
sound.
These constraints can e.g.
represent phenomena such as static equilibrium,
yielding of structural members, global stability and local stability [Poulsen, Olesen, and
Baandrup, 2020].
The global stability constraint is commonly implemented by demanding that the sum of
the elastic and geometric stiffness matrix must be positive semidefinite [Kočvara, 2002].
This formulation is, however, computationally expensive, which means that its applica-
bility to large structures is limited by the current generation of computers. Therefore,
an alternative representation of the global stability constraint, or a method to reduce
the computational complexity of the semidefinite formulation, is desirable.
The current optimization schemes are based on either a rigid-plastic framework [Poulsen,
Olesen, and Baandrup, 2020], [Mikkelsen, 2018] or a linear elastic framework [Meyer,
2019], [Vestergaard, 2019]. These optimization schemes are, by themselves, not suit-
able for designing structures in two of the most common limit states prescribed by the

Eurocodes. The rigid-plastic framework is only suitable for the Ultimate Limit State
(ULS), as it does not provide the magnitude of the deflections, which are required for
the Serviceability Limit State (SLS) verification. In contrast, the elastic framework does
provide the complete deflections, and is thus suitable for the SLS, but might be too con-
servative, and thus not environmentally and economically optimal, for use in the ULS.
For these reasons, an optimization scheme that combines the two frameworks is desired.
This thesis investigates how the current formulation of topology optimization of truss
structures can be improved and utilized to minimize the dead load of bridge girders. It is
investigated whether the computational complexity of the global stability constraint can
be reduced by applying linear programming (LP) or the principle of modal truncation.
Furthermore, an optimization scheme that can optimize truss structures subject to both
linear elastic and rigid-plastic load cases is proposed.

CHAPTER 1
Global Stability Analysis
When loading a structure with an increasing compressive loading, a sudden buckling
failure can occur. This phenomenon is known as a stability failure. A sudden stability
failure only occurs for geometrically perfect structures, which are loaded centrally such
that deflection does not occur initially. For a structure that is not geometrically perfect,
a small amount of buckling will occur as soon as the loading is applied. The magnitude
of the initial buckling is, however, magnified greatly when the loading approaches the
ideal stability load. Therefore, the ideal stability load (given for a geometrically perfect
structure) is an important parameter of a structure in compression even though real-
life structures have imperfections. For this reason, the phenomenon of global stability
failure must be incorporated into the optimization algorithm. This chapter outlines the
principles used to treat the problem of global stability of truss structures in a numerical
program.

CHAPTER 2
Plastic Material Optimization
Initially, this thesis considers material optimization of truss structures using a rigid-
plastic formulation. More specifically, a lower bound approach implemented in the FEM
is utilized. Consequently, the normal force distribution of this formulation is plastic.
The plastic optimization scheme, therefore, combines the elastic buckling problem with
plastic forces, which may seem wrong. However, as discussed in [Poulsen, Olesen, and
Baandrup, 2020], this is not a problem for the validity of the solutions. This chapter
aims to present how the lower bound approach is set up as well as why it is a lower
bound.
2.1
Rigid-plastic Material Model
Rigid-plastic modeling assumes the material is rigid until yielding and any hardening
that may occur after yielding is disregarded, as seen in Figure 2.1b. For this assumption
to be reasonable, a requirement is that the material can maintain a given stress level,
even at large strains. Furthermore, it is ideal if the linear elastic part is small and has
a great inclination. This fits well with the behavior of mild steel which has a small
linear elastic regime and then a large relatively flat plateau at large strains, as seen in
Figure 2.1a. Therefore, it is reasonable to model mild steel in a rigid-plastic framework
as it will be done in this thesis.

3.2.1
The Semidefinite Equations
This section aims to present the methodology used for defining the semidefinite con-
straint of (3.19) in a way such that MOSEK is able to interpret it. Here, the main
principles of the procedure will be outlined. However, due to the complexity of the
method, the procedure is explained more thoroughly using a practical example in Ap-
pendix B.
The purpose of the semidefinite equations is to pass the layout of the semidefinite variable
¯X to MOSEK, solely by using a, x and ¯A. The two matrices CGs and Cs, which are
a part of a, are used to relate the design variable, x, to the layout of the positive
semidefinite matrix. The matrices are created such that Cs contains the contributions
from K, while CGs contains the contributions KG. The two matrices are created by
identifying the element-wise contributions to the global stiffness matrices, and allocating
them in CGs and Cs so column and row number corresponds to element and equation
number, respectively.
The equation number is an integer given for every element in the lower triangular part
of ¯X. In this report, it is chosen to generate the equation numbers so the diagonal is
defined first. Subsequently, the off-diagonal elements are defined row-wisely, from left
to right, starting from the top of the matrix.
Finally, the ¯A matrices are defined for each equation number such that the Frobenius
inner product of (3.20) yields the element of ¯X related to the equation number. The
MOSEK formulation is easily expandable to generalized structures. The verification of
a generalized optimization script is the topic of the next section.

CHAPTER 5
Elastic Material Optimization
Up until this point, only a plastic material optimization scheme has been considered.
This is perfectly adequate, as long as the ultimate limit state (ULS) is considered and
sufficient ductility is ensured. It is, however, important to remember plastic optimiza-
tion has its limitations. For one, it does not provide the magnitude of the deflections,
which is very important in the serviceability limit state (SLS). Furthermore, it might be
impossible to provide sufficient ductility for the rigid-plastic material model in certain
situations. In these situations, as well as for SLS considerations, an elastic optimization
scheme is needed. This chapter presents the implementation and verification of such a
scheme.
5.1
Design Variables
The normal force in the bars, β, is not an explicit design variable in the elastic opti-
mization scheme. Instead, the normal forces are obtained indirectly through the nodal
displacements. Therefore, the design variables are the nodal displacements, V, and the
cross-sectional area of the bars, m, in the elastic optimization scheme.
A convex optimization problem can only have linear equality constraint functions [Boyd
and Vandenberghe, 2009]. In the present case, the equilibrium constraint is a non-linear
equality constraint.
This is due to the fact that the calculation of qe includes the
multiplication of the two design variables as seen in (5.5). Therefore, it is necessary
to solve the optimization problem in an iterative manner, using a linearization of the
equality constraint, in order to be able to use the convex solver of MOSEK. In practice,
this is done by choosing feasible initial values, m0 and V0, and subsequently updating
these values in an iterative loop:
m0 = m0 + ∆m,
V0 = V0 + ∆V
(5.1)

CHAPTER 6
Modal Representation
The semidefinite formulation of the global stability problem is computationally complex,
which forces MOSEK to use a lot of memory when solving the optimization problem.
Therefore, a method, which can reduce the computational complexity, is desired. It
is suspected it might be possible to apply the principles of modal transformation and
truncation, utilized within structural dynamics [Krenk, 2018], to the global stability
constraint as well. This section deals with the investigation of this belief.
Firstly, the semidefinite global stability requirement, shown in (3.18), is observed. The
usual modal transformation, described in e.g. [Inman, 2014], is performed. This leads
to the following constraint in the modal space:
ΦT(K + KG)Φ ⪰0
(6.1)
where Φ is a matrix that contains all the eigenvectors of the global stability problem,
governed by (1.37), concatenated column-wisely. In dynamic analysis, the concept of
modal truncation can be utilized to reduce the system size, while retaining the most
important information about the system. As mentioned earlier, it is this concept that
might apply to the semidefinite matrix as well, thereby allowing for a reduction of
memory usage.
Modal truncation is performed by reducing the number of modes included in Φ. If the
structural system consist of ndf unsupported DOFs, then the eigenvalue problem will
result in ndf modes with ndf entries each. This means the full Φ is a ndf x ndf matrix.
Therefore, when the transformation, shown in (6.1), is performed, the resulting matrix
will also be ndf x ndf. However, if only the first m modes are included, Φ becomes a ndf
x m matrix. This means the modal transformation will result in a m x m matrix, thus
reducing the size of the matrix MOSEK has to ensure is positive semidefinite. Modal
truncation is, however, a reduction of the system and therefore only an approximation.
As the eigenvectors change when the design is updated, it is necessary to solve the
eigenvalue problem and perform the modal transformation in each iteration.
The formulation of Cs and CGs, described in Section 3.2, is identical for the plastic
and elastic formulation. For this reason, the modal transformation procedure is also
identical for the two formulations. To set up the modal global stability constraint in
MOSEK, modal transformation is applied to the global partially differentiated element-
wise stiffness matrices. Next, Cs and CGs are assembled based on these. Although
the size of the stiffness matrices may change due to modal truncation, the number of
design variables remains the same. Therefore, only the number of rows associated with
global stability in the system matrix, a, is reduced. The modal procedure used for the
plastic optimization scheme can be seen in Algorithm 6.1 while the procedure of the
corresponding elastic scheme is shown in Algorithm 6.2.

6.2
Computational Complexity
The sole purpose of the modal formulation of the global stability constraint is to reduce
the computational complexity. The modal transformation does, however, entail large
amounts of additional calculations. Therefore, it is not a given that the modal formu-
lation decreases the computational complexity. This section investigates this issue. For
this part of the report, a computer running Microsoft Windows 10 Enterprise, with an
Intel Core i7-7820HQ CPU and 32 GB of memory is used.
A major part of the computational complexity is the computation time. It is noted
the computation time of an algorithm is not necessarily well defined, as it is heavily
dependent on the e.g. the system specifications and the number of background processes
running. Therefore, computation time is often described as a non-precise method of
measuring the efficiency of an algorithm. For this reason, ”Big O”-notation, denoted
O, is used to supplement the run-time measurements. This notation is used within this
thesis to describe how the number of semidefinite constraints increases with the size of
the input, i.e. the semidefinite matrix.
It is recalled that only the lower triangular part of the semidefinite matrix is defined in
MOSEK. Therefore, the number of constraints for a n x n semidefinite matrix is given
as Pn
i=1 i. This sum can also be expressed as f(n) = 1
2(n2 + n), where f is the number
of semidefinite constraints and n is the size of the semidefinite matrix which is equal to
the number of included modes. It is noted that f(n) is O(n2) which shows the number
of constraints grows quadratically with the size of the matrix. The implication of this
is a huge potential for reducing the number of semidefinite constraints using modal
truncation.
This potential is illustrated by using two examples from the previous section. It was
found that using n = 5 was equally as good as using the non-modal formulation, corre-

number of constraints. The amount of semidefinite constraints is calculated to 1275 and
15 based on f(n) for n = 50 and n = 5, respectively. This corresponds to a reduction
in the number of semidefinite constraints of 98.8%.
The drawback of the modal formulation is, as mentioned earlier, that it entails additional
calculations as it becomes necessary to obtain the eigenvectors and recalculate Cs and

The equilibrium, yielding and local stability constraints are

CHAPTER 7
Multiple Load Cases
Real structures are typically subjected to more than one load case.
Therefore, it is
necessary to produce an optimization script that can optimize a structure for multiple
loads that are not applied simultaneously. These issues are dealt with in Section 7.1 and
Section 7.2 for plastic and elastic formulation, respectively.
Additionally, a structure typically has to be verified in both the ULS and the SLS. As
mentioned earlier, the plastic optimization scheme might be used for the ULS calcula-
tions while the elastic scheme may be used for the SLS calculations. Therefore, a script,
that can simultaneously satisfy the plastic and elastic formulation subjected to different
loads, is needed. This is the topic of Section 7.3.
7.1

the inbuilt MATLAB function, cond, is utilized to calculate the condition number based
on the above formulation.
A rule of thumb regarding the meaning of the condition

when solving the system [Cheney and Kincaid, 2013].

7.3.1
Relaxation of the Elastic Global Stability Constraint
In an attempt to relax the elastic global stability constraint, different methods are uti-
lized. Firstly, it is investigated whether the procedure of modal truncation applied to
the global stability constraint of the elastic part, presented in Chapter 6, provides a more
stable solution. It is suspected that it might do so, as modal truncation is associated
with a loss of information that may make the constraint less strict.
Before conducting the condition number investigation, it was confirmed that the non-
modal solution was obtained with all modes included, which indicates that the modal
formulation is implemented correctly. When carrying out the investigation, it was, how-
ever, noted that the solutions obtained using modal truncation, would not perfectly
match the non-modal one. This is contrary to the findings of Chapter 6, where the same
solution was obtained consistently with 5 modes or more.
However, the obtained solutions would often look reasonable and the small deviations
can be explained by the loss of information stemming from the modal truncation. Ad-
ditionally, using fewer modes made the system converge slower. This allowed for the
optimizer to run more iterations, as seen in Figure 7.8, before terminating, but did not
provide a better solution. However, using fewer modes, could also lead to non-realistic
solutions in some cases and the method should, therefore, be used cautiously.
The
modal truncation approach does not significantly improve the stability compared to the
new formulation, as seen in Figure 7.8. For this reason, and because of the increased
insecurity associated with the method, it is discarded.
Secondly, a method denoted ε-perturbation, which is suggested in [Evgrafov, 2005], is
applied. This method is similar to the one used for the relaxation of stress constraints in
[Cheng and Guo, 1997]. In ε-perturbation, the relaxation of the elastic global stability
constraint, is applied by introducing a function, o(ε), for which limε→0+ o(ε)/ε = 0. Here,
ε is a sequence of positive descending numbers. The function, o(ε), and the sequence of
numbers, ε, are used to limit the cross-sectional area and relax the semi-definite matrix
as shown:
m ≥o(ε)
K + Kg + Iε ⪰0
(7.19)
As the design variables are formulated incrementally, the first constraint of (7.19) must
be rewritten before it can be implemented in MOSEK. This is done by applying the
identity m = m0 + ∆m which yields:
∆m ≥o(ε) −m0
(7.20)
This constraint sets a lower bound on the allowable cross-sectional area of the bars thus
restricting the feasible set. The second constraint of (7.19) replaces the original semi-
definite constraint of the elastic part of the formulation. The new addition, Iε, relaxes
the global stability constraint. In this thesis o(ε) = ε2 is applied, like it was done in
==PAGE:109==
7.3 Combined Elastic and Plastic Load Cases
89
[Evgrafov, 2005]. Furthermore, ε is defined as a sequence of logarithmically decaying
numbers.
After running the optimization scheme, it was concluded that the ε-perturbation did
not improve the stability of the system as the condition number remains unchanged and
the calculations terminate at the same point as seen in Figure 7.8. This is in accordance
with the findings of [Stolpe and Svanberg, 2001] and [Tugilimana, Coelho, and Thrall,
2018], which state that the ε-perturbation is not easily applicable for large structures as
the trajectory of the solution, can be discontinuous, regardless of the smoothness of the
chosen sequence of ε. For this reason, the ε-perturbation is abandoned in this project,
as it seems to only add another constraint to the design variable, without providing the
needed relaxation.

CHAPTER 9
Application: Bridge Girder
Throughout this thesis, several optimization algorithms have been presented and verified
using simple fictional structures. In this chapter, the developed optimization scheme is
applied to a more realistic structure, namely a section of a twin-box suspension bridge
girder.
For this purpose, the 75 m long bridge extract, illustrated in Figure 9.1, is utilized. The
girder consists of two outer symmetrical box girders, each with a 9 m wide carriageway
on top, corresponding to 3 traffic lanes, and a connecting girder placed in between. Each
of the girders has a height of 3 m, while the width is 7.5 m and 12 m for the outer and
middle girders, respectively. Furthermore, both outer box girders are supported by four
hangers, which are placed 25 m apart.
Figure 9.1: Section of suspension bridge
9.1
Numerical Model
When modeling the bridge section, it is necessary to make several simplifications. Firstly,
it is recalled that only bar elements are implemented in the optimization scheme. This
means the entire structure must be modeled using bars.
Secondly, the mesh of the
model must be quite coarse to ensure the computational complexity does not exceed the
computational capacity available. Lastly, the boundary conditions at the ends of the

bridge section must be simplified, as the structural influence of the adjoining girder, is
not known.
It is chosen to omit the structural influence of the carriageway. Each of the three girders
is divided into six identical boxes, of which the vertices are connected by bar elements.
The hangers are modeled by 10 m long bars, which are simply supported at the top. In
this case, some degree of horizontal fixation is necessary to prevent numerical errors and
carry the horizontal load, as the hangers only can carry vertical forces in this linear model.
Consequently, and for simplicity’s sake, the adjoining girder is assumed to fully constrain
the ends of the bridge section in the xy-plane. The final FEM model is illustrated in

To reduce the number of load cases and thus the computational complexity, some con-

the system. A yield stress of 235 MPa is used for all bars for simplicity’s sake. This
is a significant approximation for the hangers, which typically are made of very high
strength steel. The cross-sectional area of the hangers is fixed to 16240 mm2, to ensure
a constant stiffness of the supports. This value is approximately double that of a realistic
bridge. However, as the cross-sectional area of the hangers is fixed and the yield stress
is lower than usual, it is necessary to choose the value conservatively.

9.3
Discussion
The results obtained for the bridge section seem reasonable, especially when considering
the simplicity of the model. However, several limitations of the formulation are still
evident.
First and foremost, the computational complexity of the formulation greatly limits the
applicability of the optimization scheme. It took approximately five days to obtain a
solution for the three load cases combined, even for a model as simple as the one used
here. Furthermore, the memory usage increases drastically with the use of more elements
and load cases. Therefore, to optimize realistic structures, a vast amount of memory and
processing power is required. The modal truncation method can, potentially, be used to
reduce the computational complexity, but as discussed in Chapter 6, the method only
showed promising results for the elastic formulation. Therefore, the potential reduction
of the computational complexity is limited.
Another significant limitation of the formulation is that the linear FEM is used. In this
framework, the equilibrium equations are formulated based on the undeformed state.
Therefore, as the wind loading is perpendicular to the hangers, the hangers are unable
to carry any wind load. This means that the pendulum effect of the hangers is neglected
in the current formulation. In the DTU course: 11375 - Bridge Structures, it was found
that approximately 75% of the wind loading is carried by the pendulum effect, whereas
only 25% is carried by bending of the deck. Consequently, the current formulation is
not able to realistically model the behavior of a suspension bridge. The formulation
is, therefore, unreliable for transverse load cases. This limitation can be remedied by
utilizing the non-linear FEM, in which the equilibrium equations are formulated based
on the deformed state.
==PAGE:132==
112
9 Application: Bridge Girder
Furthermore, the formulation can alter the design without affecting the magnitude and
distribution of the dead load. This is a significant simplification, as the chosen design
is paramount for both parameters. Therefore, to obtain precise results, the connection
between the design and dead load must be present in the model.
Lastly, as it has been the case throughout most of the thesis, the non-convex nature
of the formulation introduces uncertainty regarding the optimality of the solution. For
the bridge section, it was observed that altering the initial guess or the allowable step
size, could double the volume of the structure. As the calculations took five days to
complete, it is infeasible to thoroughly explore the solution space by applying different
initial guesses and step sizes. Therefore, the non-convexity of the formulation introduces
a significant limitation, as the obtained solution can be far from globally optimal.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
This thesis presents an optimization scheme for three-dimensional truss structures sub-
ject to multiple load cases, and constraints representing static equilibrium, yielding,
global- and local stability. The formulation is able to consider linear elastic and rigid-
plastic material models, as well as a combination of these, in separate load cases.
Initially, it is attempted to implement the global stability constraint in the framework
of linear programming. However, this framework fails to consider linear combinations of
buckling modes and leads to alternating solutions, even for simple structures. Therefore,
the global stability constraint is implemented using semidefinite programming in the final
formulation. The semidefinite framework entails computationally complex calculations.
For this reason, it is attempted to reduce the computational complexity by applying
modal truncation.
This approach shows promising results for the linear elastic material model, as it was
possible to reduce the number of included modes by 90% for multiple structures, while
still obtaining the correct solution. Furthermore, the reliability of the method seems
to be independent of the order of the eigenvectors. By applying modal truncation, it
was possible to reduce the computation time by more than 80%, although the reduction
was limited by the additional calculations associated with modal truncation. Despite
the promising results, more evidence is required, before the method can be generalized.
In contrast, modal truncation was deemed unusable in the rigid-plastic framework, as
there seemed to be no consistency in the results.
The local stability constraint is implemented by limiting the compressive normal force
in the bars to the Euler buckling load. The expression governing the Euler buckling load
is non-convex. Therefore, the constraints are based on a linearization of the expression,
which is used to establish a solvable convex sub-problem. However, by applying this
principle, the obtained solutions are not guaranteed to be globally optimal. Therefore,
it is important to carefully investigate the solution space to ensure a reasonable opti-
mum has been reached. Furthermore, it is observed that the scaling of the system is
paramount, as it can change the obtained solution drastically. Consequently, it is also
essential to carefully investigate whether the used scaling is reasonable.
It is possible to combine the linear elastic and rigid-plastic material models in separate
load cases with reasonable results. However, in some cases, the solver states that the
system is infeasible or ill-posed after few iterations. It is concluded this problem is likely
to be related to the global stability constraint of the elastic part of the optimization
scheme. It is attempted to solve the problem by applying modal truncation to the elastic
global stability constraint as well as relaxing the constraint to no avail. Consequently,
the formulation remains unstable in some cases despite these efforts.
==PAGE:134==
114
10 Conclusion
Conclusively, the capabilities of the optimization scheme are demonstrated by optimizing
a section of a fictional suspension bridge subjected to two plastic and one elastic load
case. In this instance, the formulation is stable and yields reasonable results. However,
the computation time was five days, despite the simple model used. Combined with the
instability of the formulation and the general limitations thereof, it is difficult to imagine
that the formulation has commercial applicability, without further work.
10.1
Further Work
As the rigid-plastic material model is commonly used within the field of structural opti-
mization, it would be beneficial if modal truncation also could be applied to the plastic
part of the optimization scheme. Therefore, some additional research, uncovering why
the approach is inconsistent and whether it can be remedied, is in place. Additionally,
the promising results of modal truncation in the elastic part presented in this thesis only
constitute a small sample size. Consequently, additional research on the generalizability
of the results is needed, before a general method can be deduced. For example, it would
have been ideal to investigate whether the elastic modal truncation also could be ap-
plied to the bridge example. However, this has not been possible due to time constraints.
Furthermore, it might not be necessary to update the eigenvectors in each iteration of
the modal formulation, especially when the solution is almost converged. This should be
investigated, as it has the potential to reduce the computational complexity significantly.
As the combination of elastic and plastic load cases sometimes results in an unstable sys-
tem, more research, uncovering why the instability occurs and how it might be prevented,
is needed to bolster the robustness of the formulation.
The presented optimization scheme does not recognize the inherent connection between
the structural design and the dead load, meaning the optimization scheme is neither
awarded for choosing a light design nor punished for choosing a heavy design. This is a
significant limitation of the formulation that can be improved by linking the load vector
to the cross-sectional area and material of the bars.
Furthermore, more types of elements can be added to the formulation to improve the ac-
curacy of the bridge model. For example, the implementation of non-linear bar elements
would allow the model to consider the pendulum effect. Additionally, shell elements are
useful for modeling the closed box girders.
Throughout the report, steel structures have been considered. If one instead wishes to
optimize structures made of concrete, it may be useful to implement a material model
that, for example, takes the effects of cracks into account to ensure that the model is as
realistic as possible.
