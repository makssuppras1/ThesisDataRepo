Abstract
The potential of structural topology optimization for robust design has been investigated, and
it shows potential in three areas: System level approach, component level approach, and un-
certainty inclusion.
A crank mechanism for an internal combustion engine has been studied with kinematic analysis
and declared over-constrained. Using a 2D topology optimization model in the load-plane, the
original design of a connecting rod has been conrmed. The same 2D model has suggested
structures that adds exibility in the over-constrained plane. The results achieved cannot be
used to directly to create a new component, but the approach shows great potential.
Another topology optimization model based on geometrical non-linearity has predicted a struc-
ture that increases stiness under tensional load. This is done in an eort to design a component
from it's resulting load-displacement with a robust response. The model was unable to converge
for compressive loads and future iterations of this approach should be investigated.
Finally a discussion of the potential use for topology optimization for robust design has been
carried out and ve research topics has been proposed. The project conclude that the combined
eld needs more research before in can be directly used in product development, but if further
developed it has immense potential.

Part I:
Introduction
This thesis explores the potential uses of topology optimization for robust design. Both subjects
have become increasingly popular in the last decade, but the use of topology optimization for
robust design has yet to be investigated in depth. The motivation for this project has roots
in the theoretical background for the two subjects, and their methodologies are an important
part of the introduction of their combination and thus this thesis.
The thesis is organized in 4 parts: In part I the two subjects will be introduced with their
theoretical background, their challenges and current research - and how they potentially could
benet from each other. In part II and III two cases from robust design are considered using
topology optimization as a conceptualization tool.
Finally in part IV the potential use, implementations, limitations, and further research is
discussed. The report serves as a starting point for further research in the combined eld and
suggests some areas that should be studied further.
1.1
Robust design
Robust design is both a term describing a specic design and a design methodology. Robust
design in general is related to accounting for lifetime variance for a product or mechanism, and
limiting the impact of this variance.
A robust design refers to a design's response to variance and was introduced in (Taguchi,
1986). If variance, e.g. production, wear, operating condition or similar, is introduced to a
robust design it will have less of a impact when compared to a non-robust design. A robust
design is less sensitive to variance in design parameters and it is thus better at meeting the
functional requirements.
The method of robust design is taking variance considerations into account as early in the design
phase as possible, as discussed in (Arvidsson and Gremyr, 2008). Changes to a design are a lot
cheaper to make in the early design phases. At the same time the designer has much bigger
freedom to make changes early. This is indicated on gure 1.1, where the relation between cost

The method of robust design seeks to include variance early in the design phase, preferably
already in the conceptual design phase. A number of design principles have been outlined in
research, e.g. (Christensen and Howard, 2016). These principles are identied to reduce the
variance of functional performance of a product.
In the following a few of the design principles will be described along with some of the analytical
tools that are in the robust design toolbox.
1.1.1
Principles and methodology
The transfer function
The transfer function was rst introduced in (Taguchi, 1986) and describes the relationship
between a design parameter (DP) and the functional requirement (FR) of the system. Often
transfer function are non-linear functions depending of several design decisions. In (Christensen
and Howard, 2016) some principles to improve the robustness related to the transfer function
is described including: Parameter optimization and change of design principle. On gure 1.2a
three transfer functions are shown for single DP-FR system. It is seen that TF3 is the most
robust of the 3 transfer functions, as it is less sensitive to variance in the design parameter.
This is an example of change of design principle. On gure 1.2b a parameter optimization
has taken place, where choosing the nominal design parameter to DP3 yields the most robust
solution.

Design clarity
A design can be correctly constrained using kinematic design, but still have low robustness.
One issue could be clarity of each interface between components, (Christensen et al., 2012). The
interfaces must be without ambiguities, e.g.: Large or multiple interface surfaces, interference
of ashes and rounds, or angled interfaces.
For high load systems it is sometimes necessary to have large contact surfaces as stresses might
exceed material limits. The designer must pay attention to both the design clarity, but also
the load carrying surfaces.

Axiomatic design
Axiomatic design was introduced in (Suh.N.P., 1990). The axioms of design are related to
systems with multiple functional requirements and design parameters and states:
• Axiom 1: The independence axiom. Maintain the independence of functional require-

• Axiom 2: The information axiom. Minimize the information content of the design

Robust design optimization
Robust design optimization is the inclusion of variance in deciding on design parameter values.
This is for example done in (Roos et al., 2013). Here dierent mathematical reliability models
are applied on simple designs to minimize the deviation of a specic design. Robust design
optimization relates to the parameter optimization principle, and is a mathematical implemen-
tation of this principle. Parameter optimization comes in a late design phase, as the parameters
has to be chosen at this point.
System level vs. component level
This project distinguishes between system level and component level analysis and approaches.
System level approach is an analysis of a system using the robust design method, and then
based on the analysis determining what should be done. Examples of system level analyses
could be kinematic design and axiomatic design. Design clarity can be seen as an interface
level approach, and be included in the system level approach. The system level analysis is used
to identify if any of the components in the system can be redesigned for better robustness. It
can be described as a sort of top-down approach.
Component level approach is concerned with the robustness of a specic component, for example
related to the transfer function and parameter optimization. By improving the robustness of
a single component this can improve the overall system robustness. This can be seen as a
bottom-up approach.

Rigid and exible bodies
The analyses presented above are based on rigid bodies, and adding exibility is often a way
of dealing with over-constraints or ambiguous interfaces. Flexibility adds a new set of design
possibilities, but it also adds more complexity to the system and the model describing it. A
correctly constrained and clear design can have robustness issues if the exibility of components
haven't been thought o.
Taking exibility into account is especially important when dealing with high loads and cyclic
loads. High loads can cause unclear designs or change component behavior. For over-constrained
designs any misalignment can often lead to elastic components carrying extra load and fatigue
of these components can become a concern.
1.2
Design Optimization
Design optimization is the process of applying a mathematical model to a physical design and
then optimize for the most optimal output of the design while being constrained on other
outputs. Depending on the design requirements the mathematical model can involve dier-
ent physics, e.g. structural mechanics, uid mechanics, heat transfer, electrical conductivity,
chemical behavior and so on. Any physical problem described by a set of equations can be
optimized.
Structural design optimization can generally be divided into 3 dierent areas: Parameter, shape,
and topology optimization. Although all three methods optimize for a set of parameters they
perform quite dierently and have dierent limits and benets.
Parameter optimization is usually identied by a single or low number of parameters that di-
rectly link the physical design and the mathematical model. Often parameter optimization
involves a simple optimization that can be done analytically or with numerical methods. The
parameters are decided by the designer, and the mathematical model is often based on multiple
assumptions to simplify the model.
Shape optimization also optimizes for a set of parameters, but these parameters describe the
curvature of a shape instead of directly linking the physical design to the mathematical model.
This optimization method is especially good for optimizing designs where the general design
has been decided, but certain nal changes are to be made - for example avoiding stress con-
centrations.
Topology optimization diers from the two previous methods as it involves a high number of
parameters and does not require any initial concept to optimize. It can serve as a concept-
generating tool. However, the computation time of topology optimization is considerably higher
than the former two.
5

As mentioned in section 1.1 parameter optimization is a known method for robust design,
and nding optimal solutions can improve robustness of designs. A brief example: Finding the
best relationship between width b and height h of a beam's rectangular cross section. The cross
section needs to have a certain area moment of inertia, but have limits on how exact the width
and height can be produced. What should the values of b and h be for the area moment of
inertia to be the least sensitive to upper and lower limit of production errors?
Shape optimization has seen some applications in robust design optimization. For example in
(Brujic et al., 2010), where a gas turbine has been optimized using shape optimization and
robust design.
1.3
Topology Optimization
Topology optimization is a method to distribute material in a predened design space (domain)
in order to achieve the optimal performance of the domain (Bendsøe et al., 1988). It involves
iteratively changing the material layout and at each iteration performing a nite element anal-
ysis. In this thesis structural topology optimization is applied, but this method is applicable
for any physical problem that can be modeled with dierential equations. For a general intro-
duction to topology optimization see (Bendsøe, 1995) and (Bendsøe and Sigmund, 2002), and
for an introduction to Finite Element Analysis see (Cook and Witt, 2002).
In the following the basics of the model applied in this thesis will be laid out, explaining the
general formulation. Then, in part 2 and 3 of the report, the special applications for each
problem will be derived.
With knowledge in nite element analysis and programming experience, it should be possible
for the reader to understand and setup the topology optimization code based on the following
introduction.

In this project a gradient based optimizer is used: Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA).

Commercial software
There are several commercially available software tools that can perform topology optimization,
among them two of the large FEA systems: Comsol and Abaqus Tosca. They have quite a
large library of constraint and objective functions and can handle non-linear behavior (both
material and geometrical). They are primarily used for volume optimization in eorts to save
material in a model.
Comsol also has the great advantage that you can apply a whole set of dierent physics from
structural mechanics and uid dynamics to electromagnetism, acoustics and a lot more, while
also giving the users access to dene the objective and constraint function themselves. That
means an experienced user in these programs would be able to set up similar optimization
problems to this thesis. I choice to develop my own code to get a better understanding of all
the underlying mathematics and the dierences in implementation.
1.3.3
State of the art of academic research
The academic research in topology optimization revolves around increasing the areas of ap-
plication and increasing performance. The areas could be new physics, special behavior (e.g.
non-linear behavior), length scale (e.g. MEMS), variation and manufacturing techniques.
14
==PAGE:22==
Performance can be increased by lowering computational time and increasing the size of the
structures that can be studied. The TopOpt group at DTU is one of the leading institutions
studying topology optimization. In the following a few articles related to the academic research
in topology optimization are presented, primarily from DTU.
Even though topology optimization is being applied in industry it is not as used as nite
element analysis in general. One reason could be that engineers are not trained to use a simu-
lation tool as a conceptual design tool, but only as a validation tool. This could be dealt with by
using some of the simple topology optimization tools available. At http://www.topopt.dtu.dk/
2D and 3D applications are available for free, or the simple 88 or 99 MATLAB line code could
be used, (Sigmund, 2001) and (Andreassen et al., 2011).
Another reason has been the limited size of structures that can be analyzed due to resolu-
tion limits and computation time. Researchers at DTU has created a new internal design for
an airplane wing using topology optimization and a 8000 processor supercomputer. The opti-
mization is based on the SIMP-model and density ltering. The solver has to be an iterative
solver due to storage and memory requirements. The article is presented in Nature, Interna-
tional Journal of Science, (Aage et al., 2017).
Manufacturing designs created with topology optimization often requires additive manufac-
turing for production. Topology optimization is limited in its application due to this - any
development in additive manufacturing also paves the way for topology optimized designs to
enter the industry even more. For more on the challenges for additive manufacturing see (Gao
et al., 2015).
A topic that is very related to robust design is accounting for misplacement of materials in
the topology optimization. One method is to repeat the optimization for a number of random
small nodal pertubations using Monte Carlo simulations. This is found in (Jansen et al., 2013).
Other ways of accounting for misplacement of material are found in (Schevenels et al., 2011)
and (Lazarov and Sigmund, 2012).
1.3.4
Robust topology optimization
There are several research articles dealing with robust topology optimization. Robust topology
optimization can generally be split in to two categories: Local mesh convergence issues and
accounting for variance.
Local mesh convergence is important for the FE-analysis. If parts of the mesh only has the
thickness of one element local mesh convergence is not ensured and numerical instabilities can
15
==PAGE:23==
occur. When using the smoothed Heaviside projection the threshold η has an impact on the
length scale that is being imposed. For η = 0 a length scale is imposed on the solid phase of
the design. Similarly if η = 1 a length scale is imposed on the void phase. In order for the
design to remain robust and manufacturable minimum length scale must be imposed on both
the void and solid phase. In (Wang et al., 2011) a robust formulation is suggested ensuring a
minimum length scale on both void and solid phase. This formulation ensures no one-element
thickness of neither void or solid phase and therefor a more numerically robust design. At the
same time the robust formulation ensures length scales that can be produced using the additive
manufacturing and securing a better concept.
By introducing variance in topology optimization, the concepts generated will be less sensi-
tive to manufacturing or lifetimes variance, depending on the type introduced. One way to
introduce variance is by varying the size and direction of the applied load. This has been done
in numerous research articles including (Ben-Tal and Nemirovski, 1997), (Brittain et al., 2012)
and (Kogiso et al., 2008). Another way of dealing with variance is by introducing uncertainties
in material properties, (Asadpoure et al., 2011) and (Chen et al., 2010). And nally one can
introduce uncertainties on the geometry of the structure and thus simulating variance in pro-
duction. This is a well studied subject in the TopOpt group at DTU, as documented above.
These methods of dealing with variance directly in the topology optimization are greatly re-
lated to robust design and how to implement variance considerations as early as possible in the
conceptual design phase. They are not, however, implemented in this thesis, but the potential
will be discussed in part IV.

2.3
Load cases
The load case for the connecting rod is rather complicated, and this is one of the limitations of
the model. The system is highly dynamic and accounting only for forces due to gas pressure
is not sucient. The dynamic forces in the system occurs as the system rotates, and the mass
and center of mass of the connecting rod inuences the dynamic loads. At the same time the
nominal load is only in the (x, y)-plane, but the overconstraint is in the (x, z)-plane. That
means, to include all dynamic forces the system has to be evaluated in 3D and the topology
optimization must also be performed in 3D.
Since we are only doing 2D topology optimization, the results cannot directly be related to
an actual design of a connecting rod, as the connecting rod has to be evaluated in two separate
optimizations. First a topology optimization will be conducted in the (x, y)-plane based on the
dynamic load cases derived below, but for 3 individual static cases. The loads are based on a
academic setup, see (Shenoy and Fatemi, 2005).
Afterwards an optimization will be done for a more general setup in the (x, z)-plane.

2.5
Discussion, limitations and further investigations
The connecting rod is historically a very well studied component and expecting any major
changes to the design will require more studies. In the (x, y)-plane optimization we saw that
with this method can predict designs that have shown great use, as the compression load case
comes up with a design that looks very similar to the initial design. For the combined load
case studies the goal should not be a sum of compliances but a volume minimization, where
the compliance and stress for each load case is a constraint. Maybe there is some improvement
to nd for the weight of the connecting rod. At the same time the static load approach is not
totally true for the actual load that the component experiences.
In the (x, z)-plane optimization the rst change to the model could be adding a dierent bound-
ary condition. Instead of having a zero-displacement condition and a small bending force, there
could be a "misalignment"-boundary condition, where the boundary is rotated. That way the
misalignment is added directly in the model and not through a pseudo-bending.
The results obtained in this study cannot be directly used to redesign the connecting rod to
become more robust. The results are not accurate enough
In any case there is a trade o between any exibility added and the risk of buckling. Any
constraint on compliance in z-direction must be able to deal with the risk of buckling. How this
constraint should be dened depends on how the specic boundary condition is set at the small
end. The joint type at the small end is a cylindrical joint which is open in the x-direction. But
if the two axes are not completely aligned in the small end the friction could turn the cylindrical
joint into a revolute joint. This has a great impact on the critical load for buckling. Using
Euler column theory describes how a column xed in both ends has an column eective length
of K = 0.5. A column only xed in one end and free to translate in the other has K = 1. The
critical load is dened as:

This means that if the connecting rod follows Euler column theory the critical buckling load is
4 times lower for a connecting rod with a translational joint than revolute joint in the small end.
Even with changes to the 2D-model, a new design should be made with a 3D optimization
model, since we cannot have constant cross-section in either plane. It would be extremely in-
teresting to repeat the studies in 3D, and also apply dierent objective and constraint functions
to get the value of the two planes.

Numerical instabilities of low-stiness elements
When dealing with large deformations a numerical instability occurs when there are excessively
distorted elements in low-stiness regions. This results in indenite or negative denite element
stiness' and collapsing elements. The displacement for a node surrounded by void elements
will oscillate, and the Newton-Raphson procedure is unable to converge. There are a couple
of ways to deal with this. (Buhl et al., 2000) and (Pedersen et al., 2001) suggested relaxing
the convergence criteria, by removing nodes surrounded by void elements from the convergence
criteria. This is the method used in this project because of its rather easy implementation.
Another way is to remove the void elements from the FE-analysis and then reintroduce them
in the optimization, (Bruns and Tortorelli, 2003). Finally, an energy interpolation scheme is
introduced in (Wang et al., 2014). Here low-stiness elements are modeled with linear small
displacement theory. This method has shown great potential and would be very interesting to
implement in future studies.
On gure 3.2 an example of nodal exclusion is shown. The node indicated with red is surrounded
by elements below a threshold limit ρ∗and will therefor be removed from the convergence
criteria. In this project a density threshold of ρ∗= 0.3 is used.

Discussion, limitations and further implementations
Based on the results above it is clear that a number of things has to be improved before this
design method can be directly used for creating component with specic robust behavior. The
rst thing that comes to mind is the resolution of the domain. With higher resolution the
objective function is easier to control and the risk of large changes in objective will be smaller.
The increased resolution however demands more computer power. And it already requires quite
56
==PAGE:64==
a lot.
Load case 2 and 3 show that the normal linear constitutive material model doesn't deal very
well with compression. This is also something that is noted in literature, where hyperelastic
material models are extensively tested. It would be extremely interesting to apply these hyper-
elastic materials models for these studies and see if that can improve the results. Along with
the hyperelastic material models it would be very interesting to apply the energy interpolation
scheme suggested in (Wang et al., 2014) to ensure convergence of low density element. There
are elements that get highly distorted with the current model, and the threshold density has a
great inuence on the convergence criteria.
Along the suggested implementations above a better use of the Heaviside projection is needed.
This is a general improvement for both studies.
With the above mentioned implementations a number of test studies have to be made be-
fore this method is directly applicable to create robust designs.
The limits of the method
have to be found and properly studied before it can be used. Load case 1 does however show
some potential, as the result could be implemented in a real life application with only some
improvements, mostly controlling the Heaviside projection.

Part IV:
Discussion
In section 2.5 and 3.3 the results of the two studies were discussed, and improvements to the
individual models was suggested. In this section we will take a step back and look at the
potential of studied as well as other topology optimizations models for robust design, and what
needs to be studied further before it has potential.
General conceptualization
The rst thing to acknowledge is that topology optimization for robust design should not be the
rst method of conceptualization. As seen in the system analysis and in the introduction, the
robust design methodology works well with systems of multiple components. Often products or
mechanisms are constructed of multiple components and here topology optimization becomes
very limited. The tools developed in this thesis are rst applicable when there is a general
concept to dene boundaries. Topology optimization is not button you can press, where a
great design suddenly appears. It requires understanding of the system that the component
contribute to. But it can also work as an idea generator.
Both robust design and topology wants to apply analytical principles in the conceptualization
phase.
If the designer can make clear boundary conditions, topology optimization can be
used as an early stage idea generator, where designers can get inspiration to the topology of
their design. This requires very little understanding of the system and the nal requirements.
Topology optimization can also function as a second stage development tool, where design
requirements and the limits or constraints of the design space are known. To do the second stage
concept generation the designer is required to have a great deal of knowledge on how topology
optimization works, as choices made on FEA, optimizer, ltering, objective and constraints
all aect the nal result.
If not understood properly the solution achieved require intense
validation, and the benet of bringing FEA into the concept phase could be lost. As commercial
software becomes better this will eventually not become an issue, as the technological knowledge
barrier will decrease over time.
58
==PAGE:66==
System approach
The system analysis approach is based on the robust kinematic analysis, and is a way to improve
designs that are somewhat limited. After performing the kinematic analysis of a mechanism
of rigid components, a conceptual design phase often comes afterwards. If a single target com-
ponent in the system can be identied to deal with topology optimization can be a powerful
conceptualization tool. If multiple interfering components have to be conceptualized topology
optimization is not an excellent tool, as interference between components are very dicult to
model using nite elements and topology optimization.
For the connecting rod specically studied in this thesis, the system is very complicated. There
are many constraints to take into account, and this complicates the optimization model and
the results. Although complicated it serves as an excellent example of the system approach and
should be studied further. A simpler 2D static problem should be evaluated before continuing
with the more complex load case for the connecting rod.
Component approach
The component approach to create robust designs might have more academic value than imple-
mentation in product development at this point. The model used in this thesis needs a lot of
work before it can be implemented in real life applications. The idea of designing components
with a specic sensitivity to certain load case is really interesting and should be studied further,
as the tool could potentially conceptualize structures that hasn't been thought of before.
Validation, testing and manufacturing
All the results acquired in this study needs to be tested, just as any new concept. For a normal
concept validation this is done by consulting experienced engineers, doing simulations, physical
experiments, and usually all three.
However - any simulation for a continuous mechanical
component is normally done using FEA, which is already used for the concept generation. This
is a great advantage, as the FEA-tool can be used moving the design forward, but at the same
time self-validation can be dangerous if there is a aw in the long chain of computations or
assumptions. It is therefor important that designs created using topology optimizations are
tested thoroughly before application.
Manufacturing of topology optimized designs is a continued research area. Because of the nature
of solid-void designs, with multiple non-circular holes, it can be dicult to manufacture 3D
optimized components using conventional production method. Often additive manufacturing is
needed to achieve the desired topology. The development of additive manufacturing is therefor
closely related to the applicability of topology optimization. Along with this comes the question
59
==PAGE:67==
of setting tolerances on a part to be produced using additive manufacturing.
Variance included in simulations
As mentioned in the introduction of the thesis, researches in the TopOpt group at DTU and
elsewhere are looking into robust designs, where variance is introduced in the optimization
formulation. This could for example be done adding small variance to the position of each node
to simulate production variation and randomize the variance using Monte Carlo simulations.
This is a very interesting approach that could be applied to any optimization procedure, not
just topology optimization. The problem is that these randomized variance introductions are
expensive in computer power, and creating high-resolution topologies with this method might
be a bit down the line.
There is also a great discussion in relating any nodal variance to nal production variance
and how the nodal variance should be interpreted with tolerances and geometrical product
specications.
4.1
Proposed future studies
From the discussion above ve research topics are proposed. They have all relevant academic
research behind them. Some of them are more related to topology optimization, robust design,
or additive manufacturing.
Non-linear curve following
Implement the energy interpolation scheme and hyperelastic material models to improve the
curve following model. Find ways of improving computation time, so the resolution can be
improved. After initial optimization model improvements nd a relevant mechanism to use as
an implementation model and test using 3D printed components.
Within this topic materials with non-linear behavior should also be investigated. It can serve as
an inspirational tool where materials with negative poisson's ratio or with orthotropic stiness
can be used to design materials with specic responses.
The method is not limited to specically designing specic load-displacement responses, but
could also be designed to a points movement in the plane or similar.
Static 2D system approach
Use the system level approach to redesign a component for static loads. This means doing
the same work as with connecting rod, but choosing a simpler system, where 2D topology

==PAGE:68==
optimization can be used without limits. The solution should be compared with other concepts
based on the robust design methodology.
3D System approach
Based on the work done in this thesis, can topology optimization come up with a new design
for a 3D connecting rod? It could also be based on a similar system analysis with less complex
load behavior. The issue is improving a design on a previously ill-conditioned system that relies
on very tight tolerances. This study could include dierent objectives and constraints and a
simple test setup. A great output could be two dierent 3D-printed components: a robust and
a non-robust, and could be showed as a case of topology optimized structures for robust design.
Robust design optimization
Based on the research implementing Monte Carlo simulations and other work, look into dierent
methods to solve optimization problems under uncertainty. Are the methods general for all three
types of structural optimization (parameter, shape and topology)? How can we relate nodes'
variance in position to production related variance?
Additive manufacturing and robust design
Since additive manufacturing is becoming more and more advanced, it is important that the
designer knows the limitations of its accuracy and how any variances in this production aects
the nal product. Is additive manufacturing better in creating robust designs, and how can
this technology be exploited in the design process?

Conclusion
In this thesis the potential of structural topology optimization for robust design has been in-
vestigated. It is clear that both subjects can benet from each other, as both elds want to
apply their methods early in the design phase as conceptualization tools.
There are a number of ways where topology optimization can be used for robust design: At the
system level, component level, and uncertainty inclusion.
At the system level topology optimization can be used for designing components for over-
constrained systems. This has been studied for an internal combustion engine crank mechanism
with limited results. The crank mechanism is working under a highly dynamic load pattern
and this complicates the topology optimization model. A 2D topology optimization model has
been applied even though the load and over-constraint is in 3D. In the in-load plane, we were
able to predict a topology similar to an existing connecting rod. In the over-constrained plane
only theoretical results were produced. The trade o in opening the over-constraint and risking
buckling has not been accounted for in detail, and this is necessary for the results to be directly
useful.
However, the method of using topology optimization for designing robust components at the
system level looks promising. It is easily applied for simple components under low loads. If high
loads are acting on the system, the optimization model becomes rather complex, and requires
more additional research. The next goal should be to look into a simpler 2D model, where this
approach can be fully used.
At the component level topology optimization can help with generating ideas for completely
new structures with specic responses. In this thesis a simple geometrical non-linear model was
applied with little success. A structure that stiens while under tensional load has been devel-
oped, but no positive results were found under compression. The main problem is the linear
material models inability to handle compression of the nite elements. Before this method is
mature enough to design new components several material models have to be tested while at
the same time computational time needs to be lowered.
The components designed with this method could very well be some very interesting compliant
62
==PAGE:70==
mechanisms for low load system.
Uncertainty inclusion is something that hasn't been studied in this thesis, but current re-
search shows uncertainty being used in all types of structural optimization. The main obstacle
for uncertainty applied in topology optimization is the increased computational time. At the
same time very few (if any) actual components have been designed in this way, most of the
research have been on academic structures. So applications are still limited.
While the results obtained in this thesis doesn't show the full potential of this combined topic,
it can be concluded that there is a clear potential, but it will require more research. There are
no clear overlap in the two elds to nd in current research, so ve elds of research have been
proposed to further look into this.
