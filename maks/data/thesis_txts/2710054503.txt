Abstract 
 
General framework 
Solar energy is a green and sustainable energy source that, unlike the other renewable 
energy sources (RES), is easily available all over the planet but has low power, and it is inter-
mittent on a local scale. Hence, it needs to be exploited in vast areas, and the installment of 
the technology is expensive. Therefore, site selection for the utility-scale photovoltaic (PV) solar 
farm is a complex topic that has a significant impact on the power performance, economic, 
environmental, social factors, and existing as well as future infrastructures (Al Garni & Awasthi, 
2018). 
 
Methodology 
Given the benefits of solar energy and the level of necessity of PV deployment, this re-
search proposes an integrated framework to determine the optimal location for a successful 
solar PV project through a semi-automatic mapping methodology. To achieve this, 49 criteria 
impacting the PV project development have been evaluated by 28 experts, and then chosen 
factors were implemented as input parameters and processed in a geographical information 
system (GIS) environment. Finally, the output has been validated with a dataset of existing 
solar farms. 
 
Results 
The described strategic mapping methodology may represent a significant decision-mak-
ing tool either for private entrepreneurs or for the public sector in supporting funding and plan-
ning policies for the green transition.

1 Introduction 
Suitable areas mapping as Green Energy Transition strategic tool 
1.1 Solar Parks Land Origination & Background Problem 
The further development of solar farms is a priority for the Green Transition (European 
Commission, n.d.). Solar farm development can, however, be a long and complicated process, 
especially when targeting a new geographic area where solar power is not exploited. The ideal 
area needs to meet several requirements, e.g., the risks from flooding or other weather ex-
tremes should be low, it should be relatively flat, preferably cleared, and near existing (electric) 
infrastructure. Relevant regulations and restrictions must be considered, and the installation 
must be acceptable to the local community and the other stakeholders.  
Due to the lack of a standardized land origination procedure, generic workflows rely 
mainly on personal networking prior to a manual investigation of the above-mentioned critical 
factors for specific study areas. 
Usually, the suitability of an area is considered at a later stage in a project’s plan when 
the site has already been identified. Consequently, the outcomes of the feasibility assessment 
usually affect the work plan and compromise the success of the project itself. Hence, this thesis 
aims at researching an adequate land origination methodology that would help reduce potential 
losses in the early stages of the project planning. 
  
1.2 A Priori Limitations of the Research 
The thesis is limited to the research of a methodology for the identification of optimal 
areas for constructing grid-connected PV projects. However, the general framework of the pre-
sented methodology is equally relevant to the location of off-grid PV, wind, or combined wind 
and solar renewable energies park in-land. 
Moreover, since the success of a solar project depends on various aspects related to 
both site’s conditions and external variables (i.e., political interests and market demand), it is of 
importance to note that the combined methodology of this research handles both measurable 
and non-quantifiable data. The latter are not spatially implemented in the risk assessment of 
suitable areas.   
Indeed, many relevant criteria to develop a solar farm cannot be easily quantified into 
values since they involve political, ethical, or business decisions. Therefore, finding the optimal 
location for a project may not guarantee its success (i.e., public unacceptance can threaten the 
solar project and lead to delays or to its cancelation). 
1.3 Purpose and Research Questions 
The aim of this thesis is to design a generic methodology able to screen larger regions 
suitable for the development of potential solar parks based on available datasets and multi-
criteria decision-making analysis (MCDMA). The research is conducted from an academic per-
spective and does not involve stakeholders’ business preferences. Therefore, the following re-
search questions will be addressed: 
• 
What information (data) is needed to identify proper locations for solar farms (screen-
ing level)? 
 
• 
Is it possible to design a robust methodology for evaluating and categorizing the 
risks, synergies, and trade-offs related to the construction and operation of a solar

1.4 Method 
The aim of the study is to find a risk assessment tool to evaluate the chance of succeed-
ing with the development of a solar panel project in Denmark. The research goals and method-
ologies are the following: 
- 
Data acquisition and identification: building knowledge on the information needed 
through literature review, data collection with a questionnaire, gathering of available 
spatial data. 
 
- 
Data analysis: structuring collected information and analyzing relevance to the success 
of a project in order to detect data with the highest risks. 
 
- 
Developing a GIS-based Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) technique. 
 
- 
Creation and testing of numerical implementation of the MCDA framework; data pro-
cessing, re-evaluation, and readjustment of the techniques applied.  
 
- 
Prototyping and testing: evaluation, comparison, and validation of the results through 
the GIS database and qualitative assessment of uncertainties derived from the refined 
methodology or from structured and non-structured data. 
In this thesis, all the relevant information is collected using primary and secondary data. 
The primary data were collected through a structured online survey sent to experts in solar 
development. The secondary data have been gathered from freely available databases pub-
lished by Danish authorities and Aarhus university. 
 
The methodology covers the identification of site selection criteria of solar PV projects 
through literature review, a survey submitted to experts to support the creation of a weighted 
system of the criteria, and, finally, a project success analysis framework based on the Play 
Analysis process of the oil and gas industry.

2 Literature Review 
 
Since the estimated life span of a solar farm is 25 years, several studies have already 
been conducted regarding the optimal selection of utility-scale PV solar projects locations 
aimed at increasing energy production and decreasing initial investment and operational costs. 
Site suitability methodologies are found to be mainly based on decision criteria tools 
supported by a spatial analysis in GIS environment. Multicriteria decision-making (MCDM) ap-
proaches are widely used in many research papers concerning PV systems assessment. 
MCDM excels at deconstructing a complex problem into smaller sections with assigned distinct 
weighted factors used for the final comparison. Moreover, the overlay of a spatial tool such as 
geographical information systems (GIS) enhances the outcome of the chosen decision model, 
especially when tackling land suitability (Pereira & Duckstein, 2007). That said, the combination 
of the two methods is not in general deemed essential for determining the location of a suitable 
site for a potential solar project. 
In Chapter 2 of the “Advances in Renewable Energies and Power Technologies” book 
(Al Garni & Awasthi, 2018), 54 papers written between 2007 and 2017 have been reviewed 
with the aim of gathering relevant information regarding site selection methodologies for the 
development of PV solar farms. The studies show that MCDM techniques have been an effi-
cient tool in the implementation of many renewable energies projects. 
In particular, the analytical hierarchy process (AHP) approach, integrated with GIS and 
other decision support techniques, is found to be a robust solution to complex matters, including 
the ranking of land suitability assessments from a qualitative and quantitative point of view 
(Chandio, et al., 2013). The methodology has a pyramid structure, having the goal of the deci-
sion at the vertex, the criteria in the middle, and the alternatives at the lowest level. For instance, 
a GIS-AHP methodology was chosen to ease the feasibility assessment of a large area in 
southern England for the development of an on-shore wind or a solar PV project, indicating the 
latter as the ideal solution, given the high number of environmental constraints limiting suitability 
for renewables (Watson & D. Hudson, 2015). 
Moreover, when analyzing multiple alternatives, the AHP methodology may be combined 
with other MCDM approaches. Among others, the Weighted Liner Composition (WLC) is con-
sidered easy to incorporate through the tools of the GIS software environment (Malczewski & 
Rinner, 2015). Similar to WLC, the integration of different Outranking Methods (i.e., Elimination 
and Choice Translating Reality, ELECTRE) with AHP and GIS has been used to classify po-
tential locations for solar farms by performing a classification of each location based on its 
absolute advantages and disadvantages (Sánchez-Lozano, Henggeler Antunes, Socorro 
García-Cascales, & Dias, 2014). 
The combination of decision-making techniques and GIS-based MCDM analysis is an 
effective tool for land suitability analysis and, thus, strategic renewable energy planning, 
whether it is solar, wind, hydro energies, or bioenergy (Martínez-Martínez, Dewulf, & Casas-
Ledón, 2022).  
In most of the studies on site feasibility for renewable energy farms, uncertainties are 
evaluated through sensitivity analysis or by the comparison with existing solar farm locations 
and by changing MCDM method in the GIS environment. It is recommended to perform the 
validation in the criteria value (data) normalization stage in order to confirm the reliability and 
accuracy of the results obtained (Shao, et al., 2020) 
Up to date, no article or technical paper has been found regarding the application of the 
Play Analysis methodology for detecting sites suitable for solar farms, including potentially suit-
able areas. However, the framework is a consistent decision-making tool that combines statis-
tics and spatial analysis. The method estimates the probability of success of specific prospects

Furthermore, there are no studies within the existing literature that focused on identifying 
the best suitable locations for solar PV energy development specifically related to Denmark. 
Thus, a standard comprehensive methodological approach that includes both site conditions 
and national regulations is today still lacking. 
Hence, this study aims at developing a generic methodology based on multi-criteria anal-
ysis for identifying areas in Denmark potentially suitable for new solar farms installations, as 
well as assessing associate risks, and ultimately optimizing the success of project implemen-
tations.

3 Survey 
 
This chapter describes the methodology and results of the survey conducted to collect 
experts’ opinions on identified criteria impacting the success of a solar project. 
3.1 Survey Methodology 
An online questionnaire was prepared to determine the importance of criteria for the site 
selection of solar PV projects. Email invitations were sent to a total of 87 international experts 
from both industrial and municipal business areas. The web survey was completed anony-
mously and accessed through a Google Forms link (https://forms.gle/KSWbwStEyVsdua9M6). 
All the responses are attached among the deliverables of this thesis. 
 
3.1.1 Respondents 
The people surveyed were GreenGo Energy Group (GGE) (GreenGo Energy Group A/S, 
2022) international employees and Danish municipalities. A total of 28 experts submitted their 
responses, of whom 17 are from industry, 11 from municipality and 1 is from both industry and 
academia. The average panelists’ professional experience in solar is 11 years and their working 
countries are Denmark (20), USA (4), Mexico (2), Greece (1), Spain (1) and Sweden (3). The 
distributions indicate that the questionnaire results tend to be oriented to the GGE project de-
sign in Denmark rather than to a municipal or academic perspective.

The proximity to the power grid is an important parameter to consider while identifying technical 
challenges(Watson and Hudson, 2015). The projects where the SPV PP is located far away 
from the transmission or distribution grids will have a lighter electrical power loss since the 
amount of power loss is directly proportional to the distance to the grids. The inevitably higher 
power loss due to the distance also has an negative impact on the economic efficiency of the 
project namely, loss in the annual energy yield and revenue and (2) additional cost of capital 
expenditures (CAPEX) or investment costs associated with longer cabling costs. 
 
Economic indicators 
Economic perspectives of SPV PP investments are investigated using this main criterion. 
Initial investment cost (CAPEX):. Capital expenditures (CAPEX) is a synonym of initial invest-
ment costs. This is the cost that is derived from the expenses during the initial stage of the 
investment. It includes capital expenses such as solar panel, construction, electrical and civil 
work cost of the concerned project. 
Annual energy yield: The yearly energy yield and capital income that is produced from the SPV 
PP investment, significantly depends on the solar radiation value parameter. 
Operation and maintenance cost (OPEX): The OPEX cost category is the cumulative expense 
caused during the operational life of a project. This cost entails the cost of service related to 
maintenance, overhead cost of the company, insurance premiums etc., 
Land cost: The expenses produced due to type of the land, and characteristics of the project 
location where the SPV PP will be built will considered land cost. If the land is bought then the 
cost is considered to be CAPEX in other cases it is a recurring cost. 
 
3.2 Survey Results and Discussion 
A first overview of the responses is presented by topic. Afterwards, the outcomes of the 
questionnaire are shown according to the occupation of the participants, to reflect the stake-
holders’ different perspectives. 
The survey’s participants were asked to evaluate 49 criteria under four main categories 
in terms of the following linguistic scale: ‘‘strongly disagree’’, ‘‘disagree’’, ‘‘neutral’’, ‘‘agree’’, 
and ‘‘strongly agree’’, and, voluntarily, add notes at the end of each cluster. 
 
3.2.1 Results and Discussion by Topic 
 
In this paragraph, the results are presented for the four main categories: technical (Fig-
ure 3), environmental and sustainability (Figure 4), permitting/social/political (Figure 5) and eco-
nomic indicators (Figure 6) criteria.  
 
According to the results of the survey, the most influential cluster for the development 
of a PV project is represented by permitting/social/political criteria. The consensus on the rele-
vance of this group is not a surprising outcome, since regulatory policies must be secured for 
the success of any solar energy project. 
Environmental reviews of the land are among the permitting requirements that need to 
be fulfilled, and, therefore, sustainability parameters are also found to be core elements of a 
project development.

3.2.2 Results by Occupation 
Figure 7 shows an interesting analysis of the responses per occupation of the surveyed 
experts. Once all the responses were gathered, the blue line identifying industry, was found to 
be representing employees working at GreenGo Energy only. On the other hand, municipality 
responses reflected trends in policies agreements procedures applied in the Danish municipal-
ities. 
Overall, the two stakeholders greatly agree on social/permitting/legal criteria, followed 
by the environmental criteria cluster. The relationship between the two has previously been 
discussed but it is important to stress that these parameters may strongly impact the success 
of a solar project. 
The output comparison of economic criteria for municipality and industry is not surpris-
ing. Indeed, the cluster shows a major interested in economic indicators for the industry com-

4 GIS suitability model 
 
In this chapter, theoretical and analytical concepts previously obtained from literature 
and survey’s responses are integrated to a spatial feasibility study to identify suitable locations 
for the development of solar parks on the Danish territory. Experts’ opinions are indeed crucial 
for conducting the presented analysis. 
However, it is relevant to notice that this research site suitability model is based on a fea-
sibility study that does not consider all relevant factors influencing the success of a project. 
Favorable locations for solar PV farms are assessed only for chosen surveyed environmental 
and technical parameters, while social, political, legal, and economic variables are disregarded. 
The final map categorizes the study area according to a common risk scale. 
The spatial analysis was conducted with the use of editing and geoprocessing tools in a 
GIS environment, specifically in the ArcGIS Pro version 2.8 software (ESRI, n.d.) licensed by 
GreenGo Energy Group A/S.  
Input data of different formats (vector, raster and tabular) and spatial extent are derived 
from various official sources. The delivered output datasets are raster layers with a defined 
homogenous spatial extent. 
 
4.1 Model Methodology 
 
Suitability model framework 
The main logic behind the presented suitability model methodology is drawn from the Play-
Based Exploration (PBE) workflow used in the oil and gas industry. This approach is adopted 
mainly by oil exploration and production (E&P) companies to provide a better understanding of 
the project’s basin and petroleum systems at an early stage of the process (Geoventure, 2017). 
In the PBE methodology, the probability of a project success is assessed through a “py-
ramidal” sequence of spatial geological study, known as “play” (Figure 8). The first step focuses 
on a basic description of the basin type, and it is the starting point for the following study (Play 
Based Exploration). The play detects larger areas with similar conditions, and it is afterwards 
evaluated to assess its influence on the project success under a specific prospect. Relative 
risks values are assigned. At a prospect level, additional site-specific criteria are introduced. 
Prospective locations for future exploration are displayed and statistical methods are used to 
find volumes of yet-to-be discovered hydrocarbons (Kamila, Muhsinah, & Hartanto, 2018).

solar PV projects. As shown in Figure 9, potential sites are found as a result of the combination 
of environmental restricted areas and weighted technical criteria.

4.1.2 Second sub-model: weighted sum of technical criteria 
The spatial modelling of weighted technical criteria was carried out on the basis of the 
outcomes of the survey (Chapter 3). The first step of the process involves the identification of 
main criteria. Afterwards, the criteria are treated and reclassified to a common ranking scheme 
from 1 to 3 (from less suitable to most suitable), to guide the interpretation of the raster output 
data. Weights are assigned to the layers to place the levels of importance determined by the 
panel of surveyed experts. Finally, the weighted sum is computed. The output map of the sec-
ond model is a continuous raster dataset whose cell values represent an indicator of suitability 
levels over the Danish land.

4.2.1 Restrictions sub-model results

with values of 0 or 1, representing respectively restricted and viable cells for the development 
of a solar PV park. The layer is the result of the chosen reclassified binary environmental con-
straint data (table 2 in chapter 4.1.1). Restricted areas appear scattered and have a limited

The restriction model output guides the suitability analysis by the elimination of specific 
environmental protected areas from the overall evaluation of favorable locations. Therefore, it 
is crucial that the data considered represent an actual limit for the development of a solar PV 
project in the study area. 
Some of the environmental constraints evaluated in this research are distances from 
physical barriers, such as streams, lakes, and forests, while others identify nature protected 
zones (i.e., birds, nature types, habitat protected areas) regulated by the Nature Agency (“Na-
turstyrelsen”), under the Ministry of Environment (Naturstyrelsen, n.d.). 16,2% of total Danish 
land area is protected (OECD, 2021) and includes zones that would not compromise the design 
of a solar PV project but would affect the approval process of building permits requested from 
local authorities and stakeholders. Thus, the restriction model only processes environmental 
constraints that represent major impacts on the project’s success. 
As a result, the coastal proximity protected zone designated in the latest amendment of 
the Planning Act (Erhvervsministeriet , 2020) have been disregarded in the analysis. The 
coastal protection zone covers an area of 3 kilometers distance from the coastline and special 
planning justifications for construction are required to preserve the landscape. Hence, even 
though providing the right documentation may compromise the timeline or success of the solar 
project’s approval procedure, the protected coastal area does not severely constraint its final 
development. Being 52 kilometers the maximum distance from a point on land to the water 
(WorldAtlas, 2018), the introduction of a restricted coastal buffer zones would have significantly 
reduced the extent of suitable sites. However, special attention should be given to suitable 
parcels within coastal areas in relation to climate-related events such as flooding or coastal 
erosion.

5 Discussion 
 
This chapter replies to the research questions addressed in Chapter 1, respectively: 
• 
What information (data) is needed to identify proper locations for solar farms (screen-
ing level)? 
Available information on permitting and environmental restricted area are found to 
be essential for the approval of solar projects. Technical data representing physical 
barrier are also considered significantly impactful on the project success.  
 
• 
Is it possible to design a robust methodology for evaluating and categorizing the 
risks, synergies and trade-offs related to the construction and operation of a solar 
farm, thereby optimizing the associated chance of a successful project implementa-
tion? 
Yes, an integrated methodology of qualitative and quantitative data is essential for 
the feasibility study of potential project areas at the first stage of the project planning. 
 
5.1 Limitations 
The main limitations of the presented research are linked to data gathering and accuracy, 
interpretation of findings, time constraints and larger data processing requirements. 
 
5.1.1 Survey

Sample size: number of respondents  
The response rate (number of people who answered the survey divided by the number 
of emails sent by the author) is 32%. A higher percentage would have provided a more accurate 
overview of experts’ opinions by reducing potential “bias” due to lack of expert knowledge 
and/or of a general understanding on the criteria to be evaluated in the survey. Despite being 
a limitation for the presented study, the volume of responses is considered satisfactory since 
the quality of expressed opinions is supported by the year of experience of the audience (Figure 
X). 
 
 
Figure 28: Average work experience of the surveyed experts. 
 
Representativeness of respondents as stakeholders 
The survey respondents were limited to one company (GreenGo Energy Group) and its 
customers (employees of the Danish municipals). Hence, the evaluation of criteria from a mu-
nicipal perspective is strictly dependent on regulations and on physical parameters of the study 
area. On the other hand, GreenGo Energy Group experts work on projects located in different 
countries (Denmark, Sweden, USA, Mexico, Poland, Latvia), and their opinions may be repre-
sentative of other private companies.  
 
5.1.2 Suitability Model 
 
Accuracy loss: uncertainty of raster datasets 
All output datasets of the suitability model are raster files affected by unavoidable un-
certainty when aiming at finding exact locations. The major loss of information is introduced 
when converting input vector datasets to pixels, due to the transformation of linear or point 
features to the overlapping matrix of cells. This limitation may have significantly affected the 
restriction model output, whose data inputs are all shapefiles. Moreover, the spatial resolution 
of the suitability model was set to 100 m2, being the maximum cell size among all input datasets 
(flood raster). In addition, reducing the resolution of the output datasets would have increased 
processing requirements resulting in longer computational time and larger computer storage 
used.

Linear correlation of weighted criteria: uncertainty of ranking system 
 
The risk of a solar project success is first evaluated for each single raw or treated input 
dataset using a scale of suitability integer values ranging from 1 to 3. Once decimal weights 
are combined to the related reclassified values, the suitability scale is stretched to a wider range 
of values with similar thresholds. Hence, the final ranking system is heavily dependent on the 
weighting system, and do not consider potential multiple correlations between the evaluated 
criteria.  
 
Criteria selection 
Criteria selection has been limited to selected environmental and technical parameters 
evaluated in the questionnaire, with the purpose of conducting a standard approach valid for a 
first screening of areas with highest and lowest risk of project success, in or outside the study 
area (Denmark). Hence, the presented methodology for conducting a suitability study narrows 
down the extent of favorable parcels, but it is not an exhaustive analysis. According to the 
questionnaire’s results, equally relevant local data on zoning and regulation policies should be 
integrated in the analysis. Moreover, the criteria selection process is affected from data availa-
bility and data quality.  
 
Accuracy of the validation methodology 
 
The accuracy of the model output is discussed according to datasets showing existing 
solar parks and municipality approved local plans in chapter 4.2.3. However, the comparison 
with the accessed dataset does not ensure that the chance of success of potential solar projects 
in most suitable areas is maximized.  
 
5.2 Further work 
The below list of further work is provided according to the limitation stated in 5.1: 
- 
Improve the accuracy of the survey responses by enlarging the population of 
surveyed experts and ensuring an extended geographical coverage of the pan-
elists working countries. 
- 
Refine the suitability model by introducing specific land use types to the re-
striction model (i.e., agricultural areas would be not restricted while built areas 
restricted), and additional criteria to the weighted sum sub-model. 
- 
Improve the GIS model spatial resolution by reducing the pixel size in the envi-
ronment of the ModelBuilder application. 
- 
Refine risk assessment methodology able to handle the interdependence be-
tween weighted criteria. 
- 
Build a flooding dataset in line with solar panel related issues, and with higher 
spatial resolution. 
- 
Research methodology for a posteriori analysis.

6 Conclusion 
 
The Land Origination Suitability model provides a preliminary guide for future large-scale 
solar farm projects, by identifying areas where solar farm development may be appropriate. 
This project could be improved by altering the scoring scales and the weighting scheme to 
make it better fit a cost-benefit analysis. 
Other than reflecting potential project areas, the outcome of this research may be able 
to guide the investments of private and public sectors and be used as a strategic tool to support 
national, regional and local planning, as well as to direct European or any governmental funds 
for renewable energy projects. The methodology aims at maximizing the chance of success of 
a potential solar PV project to make it appealing for citizens, landowners and entrepreneurs 
deploying the technology. 
Never before has it been more important for each country to reach energy independency. 
Observing the ongoing project developments in PV solar panels, they are expected to hold 
a significant market in the energy sector of Denmark and expected to show substantial growth 
in the forecast period.
