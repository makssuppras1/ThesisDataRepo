A B S T R A C T
Neuromelanin-sensitive magnetic resonance imaging (NM-MRI) holds promise as a
non-invasive imaging biomarker for neurodegenerative disorders, including Parki-
son’s disease (PD). Neuromelanin (NM) is a dark pigment present in dopaminergic
(DA) neurons located in brainstem structures such as the Substantia Nigra pars Com-
pacta (SNc) and the Ventral Tegmental Area (VTA). NM-MRI enables visualization
and quantification of these structures, as the NM-MRI signal intensity is shown to cor-
relate with the NM concentration, which is significantly reduced in patients with PD.
While NM-MRI has demonstrated significant potential in visualizing NM-containing
neurons, it remains challenging to accurately distinguish between NM-containing
brainstem structures, such as the SNc and VTA due to lack of anatomical borders.
These regions are often treated as a single DA midbrain complex despite showing
significant structural and functional differences. Additionally, the DA neurons in the
SNc extend into the Substantia Nigra pars Reticulata (SNr), further complicating the
delineation of these brainstem structures. This suggests that NM-MRI alone is unable
to distinguish these regions.
The aim of this thesis was to image NM-containing brainstem structures, particu-
larly the SNc, and investigate their connectivity. The connectivity was examined using
tract density imaging (TDI) maps derived from single-shot (SS) diffusion-weighted
imaging (DWI) and multi-shot (MS) DWI, while comparing the performance of these
two DWI techniques. Data were collected using a Siemens 3 tesla (T) whole-body scan-
ner with a scanning protocol that included two optimized sequences for visualizing
the SNc. An imaging pipeline was developed to process the images for analysis.
The results revealed differences in contrast and connectivity within the SNc com-
pared to surrounding structures, such as low anisotropy and anterior-posterior fiber
orientation. While some delineation between the SNc and SNr was observed, sepa-
rating the SNc and VTA remained challenging, suggesting an overlapping spatial
distribution of NM-containing neurons.
The findings highlight the potential of combining NM-MRI with TDI and using
fractional anisotropy (FA) maps to visualize the SNc more effectively. However,
further development is required to refine the methodology improve the accuracy of
distinguishing these brainstem structures.

R E S U M É
Neuromelanin-sensitiv magnetisk resonans imaging (NM-MRI) viser stort potentiale
som en ikke-invasiv biomarkør til afbildning af neurodegenerative sygdomme, herun-
der Parkinson’s disease (PD). Neuromelanin (NM) er et mørkt pigment, der findes i
dopaminerge (DA) neuroner lokaliseret i hjernestamme strukturer som Substantia
Nigra pars Compacta (SNc) og Ventral Tegmental Area (VTA). NM-MRI muliggør
visualisering og kvantificering af disse strukturer, da NM-MRI signalets intensitet har
vist sig at korrelere med NM-koncentrationen, som er markant reduceret hos patien-
ter med PD. Selvom NM-MRI har demonstreret signifikant potentiale i visualisering
af NM-holdige neuroner, er det fortsat udfordrende af skelne præcist mellem NM-
holdige hjernestammestrukturer, såsom SNc og VTA, grundet manglende anatomiske
grænser. Disse regioner behandles derfor ofte som et samlet DA hjernestammekom-
pleks, selvom de viser signifikante strukturelle og funktionelle forskelle. Derudover
strækker DA neuronerne i SNc sig ind i Substantia Nigra pars Reticulata (SNr),
hvilket yderligere komplicerer afgrænsningen af disse hjernestammestrukturer. Dette
antyder, at NM-MRI alene ikke er tilstrækkeligt til at skelne mellem disse regioner.
Formålet med dette speciale var at afbilde NM-holdige hjernestammestrukturer,
især SNc, og undersøge deres konnektivitet. Konnektiviteten blev undersøgt ved
hjælp af tract density imaging (TDI) maps fra single-shot (SS) diffusion-weighted
imaging (DWI) or multi-shot (MS) DWI, mens performance af disse to teknikker
blev sammenlignet. Data blev indsamlet med en Siemens 3 tesla (T) scanner med en
skanningsprotokol, der inkluderede to optimerede sekvenser til at visualisere SNc.
En pipeline blev udviklet til at processere billederne til analyse.
Resultaterne viste forskelle i kontrast og konnektivitet i SNc sammenlignet med
omkringliggende strukturer, så som lav anisotropy og anterior-posterior fiberretning.
Selvom en afgrænsning mellem SNc og SNr blev observeret, forblev det udfordrende
at adskille SNc og VTA, hvilket antyder en overlappende fordeling af NM-holdige
neuroner.
Resultaterne fremhæver potentialet ved at kombinere NM-MRI med TDI og bruge
fractional anisotropy (FA) maps til en mere effektiv visualisering af SNc. Dog kræves
yderligere udvikling til at forbedre metodologien og øge præcisionen i at skelne
mellem disse hjernestammestrukturer.

I N T R O D U C T I O N
Parkinson’s disease (PD) is the second most common neurodegenerative disorder,
affecting more than 10 million people worldwide [35]. A pathological hallmark of
PD is the loss of dopaminergic (DA) neurons in the Substantia Nigra pars Compacta
(SNc) [27]. The diagnosis of PD primarily relies on motor symptoms, which typically
manifest only after 50-80% of DA neurons have been lost. For this reason, there
has been a growing interest in developing imaging biomarkers for PD to aid in
earlier diagnosis. With advancement in medical research and neuroimaging, several
magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) biomarkers have been proposed [10, 20, 30, 43].
These advanced techniques have the potential to show changes specific to PD and
have been reported to be useful for early diagnosis and follow-up [10, 20, 30].
One significant biomarker in PD is the detection of neuromelanin (NM), a dark
pigment found in several DA neurons in the brainstem [10, 20, 36]. NM can be
visualized using NM-sensitive MRI (NM-MRI), which has demonstrated to provide
valuable information on the structural changes in the SNc related to PD [20, 30]
Another promising MRI technique that has shown potential as a PD biomarker
is diffusion-weighted imaging (DWI). DWI is sensitive to water diffusion, and can
provide several diffusion indices shown to correlate with axonal damage [2, 32].
Additionally, the diffusion-weighted (DW) images can be used to reconstruct fiber
tracts through tractography, which can be used to investigate anatomical connectivity
in the brain [30].
NM-MRI and DWI have shown great potential in detecting pathological changes in
PD. NM-MRI has proven particularly useful in investigating structural abnormalities
in the SNc, while DWI offers insight into changes in the anatomical connectivity
within the brain. However, to the best of our knowledge, no prior studies have
combined NM-MRI with DWI and tract density imaging (TDI).
1.1
problem statement
The overall aim of this thesis is to image NM structures in the brainstem, particularly
the SNc, at 3 tesla (T) and investigate their connectivity. This will be achieved
1
==PAGE:23==
1.2 objectives
by: 1) Implementing and testing conventional single-shot (SS) and multi-shot (MS)
DWI techniques, as well as a NM-MRI sequence, in a small population of healthy
individuals (N=4) 2) Evaluating the quality of conventional SS vs MS DWI for high-
resolution brainstem imaging 3) Evaluating overlap between NM-rich areas from
NM-MRI with connectivity areas from DWI-based TDI.
The primary challenge related to this aim is the difficulty in accurately delineating
the SNc from other brainstem structures, particularly the Ventral Tegmental Area
(VTA) and the Substantia Nigra pars Reticulata (SNr).
Combining NM-MRI with DWI and TDI could potentially detect structural changes
associated with PD while also providing information about connectivity patterns in
these regions. Additionally, using MS DWI techniques could speed up data acquisition
and improve image resolution.
1.2
objectives
The overall objectives of this thesis are:
1. To understand NM-MRI and DWI, including how they are generated and their
underlying mechanisms.
2. To understand artifacts associated with DWI and explore potential solutions.
3. To critically assess current published scientific literature relevant to the aim of
this study.
4. To carry out scan sessions for NM-MRI and DWI, optimizing the sequences
by understanding the trade-offs between parameters to obtain high-resolution
images within a clinically acceptable time frame.
5. To perform TDI on the acquired DW images using the MRtrix3 software.

2.1
fundamentals of magnetic resonance imaging
MRI exploits the resonance phenomena that arises in nuclei with an odd number of
protons and/or neutrons. These nuclei possess an intrinsic property called angular
momentum, making them behave like rotating spheres, and they are often referred to
as spins. Hydrogen (H1 with a single proton is the most used nuclei in MRI because
of its high natural abundance (1027) [9] that is mostly found in water molecules and
its high sensitivity to MRI [23]. The magnetic resonance (MR) phenomena occurs
through the interaction of the spins with three types of magnetic fields: a strong static
magnetic field B0, a radio frequency (RF) field B1 and linear gradient fields G.
When spins are subjected to the static magnetic field, B0 two effects will occur:
1) there will be a slight tendency of the spins to align in the direction of the field
(called the z- or longitudinal direction) forming a net magnetization M and 2) they
will exhibit resonance at a well-defined frequency called the Larmor frequency. The
Larmor frequency is directly proportional to the B0-field and is expressed by the
Larmor equation:
ω0 = γB0,
(2.1)
where γ is the gyromagnetic ratio specific for each nuclei. For 1H, γ = 42.58MHz
T
[9,
23]. In the presence of only a static magnetic field, the net magnetization M reaches
an equilibrium state known as M0.
To obtain an MRI-signal, the net magnetization M needs to be rotated away from
equilibrium onto the xy-plane (also known as the transversal plane). This can be
5

achieved by applying an oscillating RF-field B1 perpendicular to B0. This field is much
weaker than the static field but if it is tuned to the Larmor frequency of the spins,
they will be excited. The B1-field induces a torque that causes the magnetization to
be rotated away from equilibrium by an angle α while it precesses about the z-axis.
The rotation frequency away from the equilibrium state is analogous to Eq. (2.1) and
is given by:
ω1 = γB1
(2.2)
The angle of rotation away from the z-axis is known as the flip angle (α) and depends
on the strength and duration of the applied B1-field. For a pulse of constant amplitude
B1 applied for a duration t, i.e., a rectangular pulse, the flip angle α is given as:
α = γB1t
(2.3)
The largest possible MR signal is obtained for a flip angle of 90◦, where all the
magnetization is rotated onto the transversal plane. The behavior of the magnetization
M for a 90◦excitation in the laboratory frame of reference is illustrated in Fig. 2.1c.

To simplify this complex motion of the magnetization, a frame rotating about the

stationary. Fig. 2.1d shows the behavior of the magnetization for a 90◦pulse in the
rotating frame of reference.
Following an excitation, the magnetization M will eventually return to its equi-
librium state M0, implying the transverse magnetization Mxy must decay away while
the longitudinal magnetization Mz must be restored. T1 is the time constant that
characterizes the magnetization returning to equilibrium in the z-direction. Relax-
ation of the longitudinal component occurs due to an energy exchange between the
nuclei and the surrounding lattice, and thus T1 is also known as the spin-lattice time

constant. Microscopically, the lattice generates randomly fluctuating fields as a result
of Brownian motion of surrounding magnetic dipoles. These fluctuations enhance
longitudinal relaxation, i.e., shorten T1, when their transverse components match the
spin resonance frequency, effectively acting as an RF-field.
T2 is the time constant that characterizes the decay of the transverse magnetization
and is also called the spin-spin time constant. The xy-component field fluctuations
that account for T1-relaxation also account for T2-relaxation as the magnetization must
eventually return to its equilibrium position along the z-axis. However, T2-relaxation
is dominated by the z-component field fluctuations, hence T2 ≤T1. The z-component
of the fluctuating field causes a broadening of the spin’s resonance frequency leading
to a loss of phase coherence (dephasing) of the transverse magnetization, resulting in
signal decay [23].
An additional loss of transverse magnetization is caused by inhomogeneities in the
static magnetic field. This is described by the relaxation time constant T∗
2 ("T2-star").
The signal loss causing T2-relaxation is irreversible, however, lost phase coherence
caused by variations in B0 can be recovered using refocusing pulses (180◦RF-pulses)
to gradually realign the nuclei.
While the magnetization M has a precessing transverse component, an MR sig-
nal can be measured via Faraday’s law of induction. The precessing magnetization
induces an electromotive force (EMF) in an RF coil that detects changes in magneti-
zation in the transverse plane. The detector coil can either be the same coil used to
produce the B1-field or a separate coil. The time signal that is generated is called a
free induction decay (FID) and is illustrated in Fig. 2.2.

2.1 fundamentals of magnetic resonance imaging
2.1.1
Image Formation
Spatial localization of the recorded MRI-signal can be achieved by applying linear
gradient fields (or gradients) in addition to B0. MRI images can be created by spatially
mapping whole volumes using 3-dimensional (3D) imaging but it is time consuming
and can result in sub-optimal image contrast or spatial resolution. Instead, it is more
advantageous to spatially map a plane using 2D acquisition schemes [23].
Gradients are magnetic fields that creates a linearly varying field. By making the
resonance frequency ω0 of the spins dependent on position, a Fourier transformation
of the frequency contribution corresponds to the spatial positions of the spins. Gradi-
ents can be applied in any arbitrary direction. The total magnetic field generated by
applying a gradient field using an arbitrary position vector, r is:
B(r) = B0 + r · G
(2.4)
As a consequence of this, the Larmor equation in Eq. (2.1) becomes:
ω(r) = γ(B0 + rG) = γB(r)
(2.5)
The spins will be frequency-encoded in the direction of the applied gradient. As an
MRI-image is 2-dimensional (2D), a second dimension needs to be spatially mapped.
This is done by applying a gradient in another direction before recording the FID.
This gradient causes the spins to have different phase, and the spins can be spatially
phase-encoded [3, 23].
When B1 is applied in the presence of B0, all spins will be excited and the excitation
is non-selective. If gradient fields are applied simultaneously with the RF-pulse, it
is possible to only excite spins within a single slice of volume, and the excitation
is selective. This is achieved by applying B1 at a temporal bandwidth that matches
the range of resonance frequencies of the spins in the wanted slice. To achieve a
rectangular slice profile, the frequency content of B1 must be a rectangular function,
i.e., ideally a sinc function. However, a perfect rectangular slice profile is impossible to
achieve as B1 is of finite duration, and hence the slice profile can only be approximated
[9, 23].
The 2D map of spatial frequencies that are collected to create an MR image is
called k-space. Each point in k-space corresponds to spatial frequency- and phase
information about the final MRI image. The sampling trajectory of k-space and speed

of which k-space is sampled, determines the field-of-view (FoV) and resolution of the
image. The lower spatial frequencies contain information about the contrast in the
image, while the higher spatial frequencies contain information about the details of
the image [3, 9, 23].
The most typical sequences used in clinical MRI samples one line of k-space at a
time following an excitation. In contrast to conventional MRI sequences, fast MRI
sequences samples all points in k-space or large parts of k-space in a single shot. One
popular fast MRI sequence is single-shot echo-planar imaging (SSEPI) that is the
primary method of collection DWI data. In SSEPI, all k-space points are collected in a
single shot [3]. SSEPI using spin-echo is illustrated in Fig. 2.3.

2.2
neuromelanin-sensitive mri
Neuromelanin (NM) is a dark complex pigment that is present in specific neurons
distributed along the brain stem. The pigment is composed of proteins, metal ions,
10
==PAGE:32==
2.2 neuromelanin-sensitive mri
and other components, and is produced by dopamine oxidation [39, 40]. The largest
concentration of NM-containing dopaminergic (DA) neurons are located in the SNc
and VTA. These structures play a key role in motor functioning and reward-related
behavior.
NM normally accumulates with age but is diminished in people with neurodegen-
erative disorders [5, 39, 40]. This has lead to a growing interest in developing MRI
techniques that can assess the integrity of the SNc and VTA.
Neuromelanin-sensitive MRI (NM-MRI) has proven effective in accurately visu-
alizing the SNc and VTA by leaving NM-signal hyperintensive. Several studies have
demonstrated significant reductions in the NM-MRI contrast and the volume in
these regions in patients with neurodegenerative disorders, highly suggesting that
NM-contrast reflects a loss of NM-containing neurons [10, 39, 40]. This is further
supported by studies, showing that signal intensity of NM-MRI in the SNc and VTA
has closely correlates with the number of NM-containing neurons, indicating a link
between hyperintensity and the presence of NM [14].
The physical mechanisms of NM-contrast still remains unclear but are thought
to involve a combination of magnetization-transfer (MT) and T1-relaxation [39, 40].
The hyperintensity observed in NM-MRI is suggested to be partially a result of
T1-shortening effects from paramagnetic iron stored in NM. Additionally, MT prepa-
ration of NM-MRI has been proven to increase the contrast between NM-containing
neurons and adjacent tissue. The MT-effects are generated by an interaction be-
tween free water molecules and protons bound to immobile macromolecules. The
MT-preparation leaves NM-containing regions hyperintense by providing a higher
saturation of the adjacent tissue.
Multiple MRI pulse sequences have been used for NM-MRI. The most commonly
used sequence in NM-MRI studies is the Turbo Spin Echo (TSE) sequence a 3 T. This
sequence has the advantage of being easy to implement on the scanner. However, as
studies have demonstrated an increased hyperintensity can be achieved by combining
T1-weighted imaging with MT-preparation, the specific absorption rates (SAR) can
become too high. This is a result of using an explicit MT-preparation pulse and a
refocusing pulse in TSE. If the scanner allows high SAR, it will modify the scan
parameters, and thereby decrease the reproducibility of the sequence and results [39].
Other sequences have also been used for NM-MRI, such as 2D and 3D gradient
echo sequences (GRE) and 3D turbo flash (TFL).

Delineation of NM-containing Structures
NM-MRI has demonstrated significant potential in visualizing NM-containing DA
neurons within the brainstem. While NM-MRI is highly effective in highlighting NM-
contrast, it remains challenging to accurately distinguishing between NM-containing
brainstem structures. This difficulty arises from a lack of clearly defined anatomical
borders separating them [39, 41].
The SNc and VTA are both key DA structures within the brainstem. However,
they show significant structural and functional differences [41]. Fig. 2.4 illustrates the
brainstem, highlighting the location of the SNc and VTA in an axial view.

The SNc is part of the nigostriatal pathway, with DA neurons projecting from the
SNc to the dorsal striatum. The SNc is part of the basal ganglia loop. Dysfunction
within the SNc is highly associated with motor deficits, such as those seen in PD [39,
41]. The SNc is characterized by a high density of DA neurons (<80% SNc) [41] and
is a well-defined anatomical and functional region.
In contrast, the VTA is part of the mesocorticolimbic pathways, with DA neu-
rons projecting to the nucleus accumbens in the ventral striatum, as well as to the
prefrontal cortex. The VTA plays a key role in cogintive functions, reward-related
behaviors and addiction. Unlike the SNc, the VTA is highly heterogeneous in its
neuronal composition, consisting of 50-80% DA-neurons intermixed with GABAer-

gic, glutamergic and combinatorial neurons [41]. This cellular heterogeneity further
complicates the delineation between SNc and VTA.
Despite the significant structural and functional differences, the SNc and VTA are
treated as a single DA midbrain complex in many studies [39, 41]. The dysfunction
in the SNc is strongly linked to motor symptoms in neurodegenerative diseases,
while the VTA is associated with cognitive functions and reward-related behaviors.
Achieving more precise delineation of these structures could significantly enhance
the understanding of their distinct role and contributions to brain function and

An additional challenge in delineating the SNc is the presence of Substantia Nigra

that extend into the caudal part of the SNr [16]. This overlap further complicates the
accurate delineation of NM-containing brainstem structures when relying solely on
NM-MRI alone.

description of the principles of DWI. Lastly, the application of DWI, along with its

2.3.1
Diffusion
Diffusion is the random and spontaneous movement of particles within a medium
due to Brownian motion. Albert Einstein was able to describe Brownian motion
theoretically in 1905 finding a relationship between the diffusion coefficient and the
mean-squared displacement of the particles:
⟨x2⟩= 2D∆,
(2.6)
where x2 is the average square distance of the particles over a the diffusion time ∆
and D is the proportionality constant called the diffusion coefficient [3].
In free diffusion, the displacement distribution is a Gaussian function as there
is an equal probability of particles of displacing a given distance from the origin,
regardless of the measured direction. However, in the human body, the diffusion of
13
==PAGE:35==
2.3 diffusion-weighted mri
particles is often restricted by barriers such as cell membranes, cytoskeleton, and
macromolecules. As a result of these restrictions, the displacement of particles only
approximately obeys Eq. (2.6) and the diffusion coefficient D is no longer a well-
defined quantity. To account for this, the Apparent Diffusion Coefficient (ADC) is
used, which represents an average diffusion coefficient that accounts for the effects of
tissue structure [3].
The basic principles of diffusion weighting were introduced in 1965 by Stejskal
and Tanner [3, 6], making it possible to quantify the diffusion of water particles in a
given direction. They introduced a Pulsed Gradient Spin-Echo (PGSE) sequence that
consists of linear gradient field pulses (diffusion-encoding gradients) that are applied
before and after a refocusing pulse. The first pulse causes the spins to dephase. If
the spins are displaced by the same amount, the second gradient pulse completely
rephases the spins and the signal would remain the same. However, if the water
particles diffuse randomly, the phase increment they gain during the first gradient
pulse does not cancel the phase dercrement leading to phase dispersion and spread
in the spin’s phases and thus, attenuation of the signal [3].
The signal equation for diffusion-weighted images is:
S = S0 · e−b·ADC,
(2.7)
with b being the b-value, dictating how sensitive the signal is to diffusion, and is
given by:

where G is the area of the diffusion gradient, δ is the duration of the diffusion

the signal S will depend on the gradient area, G and the diffusion time ∆. As the
created phase rolls depends on the gradient area, hence smaller gradient areas results
in less diffusion-weighting while longer diffusion times result in more diffusion
sensitivity because the water molecules have a longer time to diffuse.
By applying diffusion gradients in multiple directions, it is possible to estimate the
rate and direction of the diffusion. In principle, the diffusion coefficient is estimated
by collecting data with no diffusion-weighting called a b0-image and data with a
non-zero diffusion-weighted gradient in the wanted direction [3].

preference of diffusion as the particles tend to move more freely along the fiber
direction. The diffusion in white matter is anisotropic, and a single ADC is not ade-
quate to describe diffusion. Instead, the ADC is replaced with a diffusion tensor D [3].

2.3.1.1
Tract Density Imaging
After data acquisition, a parametric model can be created representing the main
diffusion direction at each voxel. The diffusion tensor can be used to reconstruct
global axonal pathways by linking each voxel with a similar eigenvector to its

neighbor. This is called tractograpy or fiber tracking and relies on the assumption
that diffusion across fibers are hindered compared to along the fibers [3].
Fiber tracts can be reconstructed using a probabilistic approach, which assumes a
distribution of orientation between voxels. This enables the recovery of more extensive
fiber bundles but might generate invalid connection by overestimating fiber tracts
[44].
A more novel post-processing technique that has been introduced is called tract
density imaging (TDI). Compared to standard DWI or diffusion tensor imaging (DTI),
TDI can produce maps with much higher spatial resolution to up to 500 ¯m isotropic
resolution [11]. TDI allows for a quantitative assessment of fiber tract density, which
reflects the relative number of streamlines that pass through a voxel. TDI provides
a more detailed visualization of the organization of fibers than TDI, particularly in
areas with crossing or bending fibers.

Single-shot (SS) methods are ultra-fast imaging sequences that are capable of acquir-
ing a full slice within a single repetition time, TR. The most commonly used pulse
sequence for DWI is single-shot echo-planar imaging (SSEPI), which enables rapid
acquisition [3]. This speed is crucial for measuring diffusion in multiple directions
and over multiple b-values. The pulse sequence and sampling trajectory is similar to

DWI uses large diffusion gradients to produce inchoherent phase shift that are
sufficient for producing signal loss in the presence of microscopic water diffusion.
However, the use of the large diffusion gradients makes bulk motion from head
movement and cardiac pulsation inevitable which can interfere with the imaging
data [3].
Head movements can be considered a rigid-body motion. Every time a person
moves head during the application of diffusion gradients, the data in k-space will
exhibit global phase change from the spins acquiring a constant phase and a shift
from head rotations. This type of artifact is easy to characterize but is completely
random for each shot.
Cardiac pulsations produce a non-linearly changing phase in the spins across the
image, resulting in a smearing effect of the data in k-space. This is more difficult to
characterize than head movements but is more easily predicted and repeatable given
a steady heartbeat.
The most pronounced source of artifact caused by motion-related phase is the
corruption of the Fourier encoded-phase. If the diffusion measurements are taken
over several shots, the motion for each shot during the diffusion weighting will

==PAGE:39==
2.3 diffusion-weighted mri
almost always be different. This leads in differences in the motion-related phase
across k-space measurements, that can corrupt the Fourier encoded phase and result
in a significant loss of image quality and more pronounced motion artifacts [3].
SS methods have the great advantage of collecting data after a single diffusion-
weighting gradient resulting in a constant motion-related phase in all k-space mea-
surements. The shot-to-shot variation can be removed and the Fourier encoding is
not corrupted.
2.3.2.1
Challenges with SSEPI DWI
Even though SS methods have several advantages, such as rapid acquisition and re-
moving disparities in motion-induced phase of the data, there are some compromises
that come with SSEPI.
MRI data is generally measured symmetrically in k-space about its origin, and
symmetric sampling is robust to both phase variations and can maximize signal-
to-noise ratio (SNR). SSEPI takes advantage of the fact that if phase variations are
slow, asymmetric sampling can be used to sample further out to one side of k-space,
thereby increasing resolution and is a valuable way to use limited sampling time.
However, head rotations can cause displacement of data in k-space. In this case, the
asymmetric sampling can lead to significant artifacts. However, this can be mitigated
by symmetric sampling. In SSEPI, this prevalence of this artifact can be reduced by
decreasing the asymmetry of the trajectory by beginning the sampling further away
from the k-space origin but comes at the expense of signal loss from T2-decay and
longer duration of the trajectory [3].
SSEPI is particularly sensitive to susceptibility-related artifacts and T∗
2-blurring.
Field inhomogeneities occurs most commonly around tissue/air boundaries, and
causes a change in the precession frequency of the spins. Thus, the spins will ex-
perience dephasing leading to accumulation of phase errors in the phase-encoding
direction. This can results in geometric distortion in the reconstructed image in that
direction [3, 6].
SSEPI has a relatively long sampling of k-space inducing T∗
2-blurring and imposes
a long echo time. The effects of T∗
2-relaxation is more pronounced in regions with
large B0-inhomogeneities, while the long echo time (TE) results in smaller signal [6].
Reduction of susceptibility-artifacts can be achieved by sampling k-space faster,
thereby decreasing the accumulation of the phase errors. This is achieved by reducing
echo spacing by increasing readout bandwidth, reducing number of phase lines by

==PAGE:40==
2.3 diffusion-weighted mri
decreasing FoV and combining several sub-sampled k-space acquisition using MS
techniques [6].
2.3.3
Multi-Shot Sequences
MS techniques consists of acquiring several sub-sampled images and combining
them to reconstruct a full k-space data set. This can be achieved by skipping lines in
the phase-encoding direction, thereby reducing the effective echo spacing and the
distortions [3].
A disadvantage of MS techniques are that the overall acquisition time is longer
than in SS techniques. As for N shots, the diffusion weighting must be repeated N
times. If averaging for better SNR is required for SS methods, then MS techniques
may result in time savings [3].
Another disadvantage is the accumulation of phase errors from motion leading
to artifacts as described in Sec. 2.3.2. This can however be overcome by measuring
the corrupt phase and removing it from the imaging data prior to combining them
using navigator echos that enables both linear and non-linear phase corrections.
The non-linear approaches are most easily applied when each shot in the MS data
acquisition samples a contiguous set of data points from a sub-region of k-space [6].
In this way the Nyquist sampling condition is fulfilled and the phase correction can be
applied as a complex multiplication. One way to do this is using readout-segmented
echo-planar imaging (EPI), where k-space is filled with a series of concatenated
segments in the readout direction [6]. Fig. 2.6 illustrates the trajectory for SSEPI and
readout-segmented EPI, respectively.

Brain Extraction
Brain extraction is the task of removing the skull in an image, so only the brain is
left. Brain extraction can potentially result in better coregistration of images as the

the section above, brain extraction was omitted for coregistration in the pipeline. The
brain extraction was instead used solely for the purpose of performing TDI on the

In this thesis, two brain extraction tools have been tested: bet from the FSL software
[33] and mri_synthstrip from the FreeSurfer software [7, 12].
BET was tested by performing brain extraction using multiple threshold between 0
and 1. The thresholds were used in steps of 0.1 to investigate the optimal threshold.
However, BET failed to extract the brain for the NM and RESOLVE images, as it
removed parts of the brainstem, thereby interfering with the ROI (see A.2).
In comparison, mri_synthstrip successed in extracting the brain. The results of
the brain extraction for slice 25 in the RESOLVE image are shown in Fig. 3.4.

3.5.1
Importing DICOM Files
The acquired data from scan sessions were imported and converted to NIfTI format
using dcm2niix [18].
3.5.2
Processing the SSEPI Images
SSEPI are prone to susceptibility artifacts as described in Sec. 2.3.2.1. To correct for
the artifacts, the SSEPI images were processed using a provided MATLAB script. The
script corrects for susceptibility artifacts, eddy currents and denoises the data. The
given script was modified to fit the the pipeline.
34
==PAGE:56==
3.5 pipeline
3.5.3
Coregisterering to the b0-image
DW images are acquired with multiple diffusion directions and are prone to motion
artifacts. To correct for movement between the applied diffusion directions, each
direction was coregistered to the subject specific b0-image. This process was done for
both the SSEPI and RESOLVE images.
First, each diffusion direction was separated using fslsplit from FSL software [34].
Then, a MATLAB function was created to perform coregistration using coregister:
estimate from SPM12 software [8]. Finally, all diffusion directions were merged using
fslmerge from FSL software [34]. For the MATLAB script, see B.2.1.1 and B.2.1.2.
3.5.4
Coregistering to the MPRAGE Image
All images were coregistered to their corresponding MPRAGE image to ensure all
images were in the same subject space, and the T2-weighted image was resliced to
match the MPRAGE image. This was accomplished by creating a MATLAB script
that uses coregister: estimate&reslice from SPM12 software [8]. The script can
be found in B.2.2.
3.5.5
Brainstem Segmentation
A whole-brain segmentation was performed using run_samseg from FreeSurfer soft-
ware [7, 12], using the MPRAGE and resliced T2-weighted images as input. The
segmentation separates the the brainstem into three distinct regions: 16 (brainstem),
28 (left ventral DC) and 60 (right ventral DC). These segmented regions were then
merged to create the brainstem mask using mri_binarize and converted to NIfTI-
format using mri_convert from FreeSurfer software [7].
3.5.6
TDI
Before performing TDI, the b-vectors were rotated to align with the coregistered
SSEPI and RESOLVE images using a created MATLAB script (see B.2.3.1 B.2.3.2).
Brain extraction was then performed using mri_synthstrip from FreeSurfer soft-
ware [12] to reduce computation time and improve biological plausibility by ensuring
the downstream analysis was performed within the mask.
35
==PAGE:57==
3.6 data analysis
The TDI of the SSEPI and RESOLVE images were performed using the MRtrix3
software [38].
The images were first converted to MIF format, and the diffusion tensor was
calculated to acquire a FA-map. A response function using constrained spherical
convolution (CSD) was used to model multiple fiber orientation. Tractography was
performed using the brainstem mask created in Sec. 3.5.5 for one million stream lines.
Finally, the TDI map was generated using a isotropic voxel size of 0.5 mm3.
3.5.7
Normalization to MNI-space
The final step in the pipeline was to normalize all images, including the TDI- and FA-
map, to a common space. The images were normalized to the MNI ICBM152 template
[21] using normalize: estimate&write from the SPM12 software [8]. Finally, all
images were resliced to the resolution of TDI of 0.5 mm. A MATLAB script was
created for this process, which can be found in B.2.4.
3.6
data analysis
After completing the pipeline, data analysis was performed on the resulting images.
The TDI maps from the SSEPI and RESOLVE images were overlayed on the NM
images using MRtrix3. The purpose of this was to examine any potential overlap
between fiber distribution and NM contrast.
Since all images were normalized to MNI space, the NM images, TDI- and FA-maps
were overlayed with a probabilistic atlas from
https://identifiers.org/neurovault.collection:3145 [26]. The goal of this was
to investigate if there is any relation between the atlas segmentations and features
observed in the images.

D I S C U S S I O N
This chapter will discuss various aspects of the results obtained in this thesis, focusing
on if a better delineation of brainstem structures can be achieved.
First, the results were analyzed with respect to segmentations across different
images and atlas segmentations. Then, a comparison between RESOLVE vs SSEPI
methods is presented and discussed.
5.1
overlaying tdi maps on nm images
An intial investigation aimed to explore the potential overlap of NM contast and TDI
maps derived from SSEPI and RESOLVE.
In Fig. 4.1, a prominent green stripe can be seen to dominate in the NM contrast at
z = −17.5, corresponding to the anatomical location of the SNc. The green stripe in
the TDI map reflects a high density of anterior-posterior oriented tracts. The green
stripe gradually transitions to blue below the NM contrast, indicated by the red
arrows. This suggest change in tract orientation from anterior-posterior to inferior-
superior. This pattern is most pronounced in the three first subjects, indicating a
consistent anatomical configuration. However, in subject 4, the green stripe is most
prominent at z = −15. This could potentially be a result of individual anatomical
variability or differences in the organization of the tracts, or a result of inaccuracies
in the coregistration.
The brightness of the green stripe varies across the four subjects. This could possibly
correspond to anatomical differences in the DA connectivity strength.
As the z-value increases, the green stripe becomes thinner and curves around a
low connectivity area (black), that aligns with the anatomical placement of the red
nucleus (RN). This suggests that the green stripe represents tracts that may be specific
to the SNc.
Differences are visible when comparing the TDI maps from SSEPI to RESOLVE.
Compared to the TDI map from SSEPI, the TDI map derived from RESOLVE appears
more smeared and less detailed. This is likely due to RESOLVE using fewer diffusion
directions (20) and b-values (2), leading to a lower angular resolution. The TDI map

from SSEPI shows a more prominent pattern of the tracts, especially in the NM-
contrast area. SSEPI uses a higher number of diffusion directions (60) and b-values
(3), improving the directional accuracy. However, RESOLVE has a higher SNR and
reduced susceptibility to artifacts which can make it more robust for tracking long
fiber pathways.
Although the overlay of the TDI map on the NM image provided valuable informa-

the SNc from the SNr and VTA.
5.2
overlaying atlas segmentations on nm images
This analysis aimed to investigate the relationship between NM-contrast and a
probabilistic atlas from https://identifiers.org/neurovault.collection:3145 in
MNI-space.
Fig. 4.2 shows the overlay of the atlas segmentations of SNr, SNc and VTA on
the NM image for all subjects. The atlas segmentations only displays voxels with a
probability ranging [0.2; 1] of being part of the brainstem structure.
For all subjects, it seems that the SNc overlaps closely with the NM contrast.
This observation is in correspondence with literature that SNc has a high NM-
concentration [39, 40]. Additionally, it can be seen that SNr appears to fall outside
the NM contrast. This could suggest a potential delineation between the SNc and
SNr, and may reflect the lower NM content in SNr compared to the SNc. This is in
accordance with anatomy, in which the SNr is located on the outer edge of the SNc.
However, this could also be a result of an coregistration error.
The VTA appears to overlap with the inner edge of the NM-contrast. This could
potentially be because VTA contains lower levels of NM compared to the SNc [41].
However, the overlap between the VTA and NM contrast is less distinct, and it
remains challenging to delineate these regions using NM MRI alone.
As these findings are consistent across four subjects, it supports literature sug-
gesting that NM contrast could aid in delineating the SNc from the SNr because
of differences in NM contrast [36]. However, the NM contrast is less effective in
differentiating the SNc from the VTA. The overlapping regions of these two areas
in both the NM contrast and the atlas suggests a shared spatial distribution of NM
containing neurons.

no clear separation of the SNc from the VTA. The overlap between these regions may
reflect the shared orientation of their DA projections.

The FA-map shows a darker region, that aligns with the SNc. This indicates an

NM-containing DA neurons. The SNr appears to located on the outer edge of the
dark region, indicating a higher anisotropy in SNr. A study in cats has shown that the
SNr exhibits a laminar organization [37], and it is possible that a similar organization

anisotropy area. However, the FA-maps did not provide clear separation between the

C O N C L U S I O N
The primary aim of this thesis was to image NM-containing brainstem structures,
particularly the SNc, and investigate their connectivity at 3 T. This was achieved by
combining NM-MRI with TDI from SSEPI and RESOLVE to enhance visualization
and understanding of DA brainstem structures.
NM-MRI, along with the RESOLVE sequence, was optimized for visualizing the
SNc, and data was acquired from 4 healthy subjects using a Siemens 3 T PRISMA
scanner. A processing pipeline was developed to perform coregistration, brain ex-
traction, whole-brain segmentation, TDI and normalization to MNI-space, based on
preliminary results from testing different software.
The NM images were overlayed with TDI maps, and probalistic atlas segmentations
were overlayed on the NM image, TDI- and FA-maps. The results revealed notable
differences in contrast and connectivity within the SNc compared to surrounding
structures, including low anisotropy, high NM-MRI signal intensity and an anterior-
posterior fiber orientation. While some delineation of SNr and SNc was observed, the
VTA remained challenging to separate, suggesting an overlapping spatial distribution
of NM-containing DA neurons.
These results highlight the potential of combining NM-MRI with TDI and FA-maps
for studying the DA system in the brainstem. Future research should focus on refining
this methodology to enhance the accuracy of delineating these brainstem structures.
