Abstract
This thesis has been prepared in collaboration with TrackMan A/S (the company) as a
project beneficiary. The company produces electronic devices with one or two optical
systems in the form of a fixed­lens camera. The thesis concerns a camera calibration
process, which is one of the production steps. The process involves a determination
of the camera’s intrinsic and extrinsic parameters, as well as non­linear lens distortion
parameters. It was found that the performance of a parameter estimation varies between
devices and needs to be improved.
The current solution, implemented by TrackMan, differs between types of calibrated de­
vices. There are several reasons for that. One is a result of the sequential product portfolio
development and the introduction of new methods over the years. The second reason is
that different device models require a calibration setup tailored to their camera’s physical
properties, such as focus and focal length.
This paper presents a proof­of­concept for an automated camera calibration process using
a collaborative robot. The new framework offers support for all TrackMan products. It
also unifies multiple procedures into one from the user’s perspective. The concept was
tested regarding camera calibration quality, robustness, and reliability. On top of that, two
supervision systems were implemented to monitor the robot online. Based on the data
from those systems, detecting a change in the process or a sensor failure is possible.
The results show that the new approach leads to more stable and reliable camera cal­
ibration results. The suggested process increases TrackMan’s production flexibility and
capacity. It allows for live monitoring against change and fault detection. Lastly, it mini­
mizes the risk of production interruption due to failure.

project beneficiary. For this reason, the following chapter starts with the company de­

1.1
TrackMan
TrackMan A/S is a company that specializes in sports technology, particularly in the field
of golf and baseball. TrackMan is known for its advanced tracking and analysis systems
that provide real­time data and insights for athletes, coaches, and enthusiasts.
For golf, TrackMan is widely recognized for its golf radar technology. The company’s
flagship product, the TrackMan Launch Monitor (Fig. 1.1), is a radar­based system that
tracks various aspects of a golf shot, including club speed, ball speed, launch angle,
spin rate, and more. This data helps golfers, instructors, and club fitters to analyze and
optimize performance, make data­driven decisions, and improve overall skills.
Figure 1.1: TrackMan’s two most recent ”Launch monitors” [1]. TrackMan 4 on the left
and TrackMan iO on the right
In addition to golf, TrackMan has expanded its technology to baseball. The TrackMan
Baseball system employs radar technology to capture and analyze data related to pitching
and hitting. This includes pitch velocity, spin rate, release point, trajectory, and other
crucial metrics. Baseball teams, coaches, and players use this information to enhance
training, performance assessment, and strategic decision­making.
When reading about the company and its products, the terms ”radar technology” or ”dual
radar” are frequently used. What is essential to notice is that each TrackMan device also
has at least one fixed­lens camera. Because the device uses radar and camera informa­
tion, both systems must be well­calibrated separately and together. This first calibration

1.2
Motivation
Camera calibration is crucial for TrackMan devices to ensure accurate and reliable data
capture and analysis. In the context of TrackMan’s golf and baseball systems, camera
calibration plays a pivotal role in aligning and synchronizing the radar measurements with
corresponding visual data. By precisely calibrating the cameras used in conjunction with
radar technology, TrackMan can accurately track the trajectory, speed, and spin of balls
as well as the movement of sports equipment. Without proper camera calibration, dis­
crepancies between radar and visual data may arise, compromising the accuracy of the
insights provided by TrackMan devices.
Figure 1.2: TrackMan’s indoor golf simulator [1]. TrackMan iO is mounted between a
screen and a projector on the ceiling.
An excellent example is the installation of the TrackMan iO in the so­called indoor golf
simulator (Fig. 1.2). After mounting the device on a ceiling, TrackMan iO’s vision system
needs to determine where the floor and the screen are. If the production calibration were
incorrect, on­site calibration would also be wrong. As a result, a customer hitting a golf ball
perfectly in the middle of the screen would see it flying off­center in the virtual environment.
It goes without saying that ball data and statistics (e.g., speed, direction, launch angle)
would not be correct either.
1.3
Scope
Given the importance of camera calibration for TrackMan applications, it is essential to
describe the criteria for successful calibration and the entire process leading to it. The
latter can be divided into the following steps:

1. Camera sensor inspection (visual, using a magnifying glass).
2. Lens inspection (visual, using a magnifying glass).
3. Assembly of the camera sensor and the lens (the lens is screwed into a holder
mounted to the camera PCB).
4. Semi­manual focusing (manual adjustment of the lens distance from the sensor

10. Image acquisition for extrinsic calibration.
11. Extrinsic calibration (camera and radar frames alignment).
12. Optimization of the extrinsic parameters.
13. Verification of the extrinsic calibration results (verification of the 3D reprojection er­

14. Decision ­ Pass/Fail.
The project scope excludes steps 1 to 5 and focuses on the rest. The goal was to find an
opportunity for improvement within image acquisition, intrinsic and extrinsic calibration,
as well as optimization and verification.
The success criteria were defined by lower reprojection error, a camera model that better
generalizes to new data, and higher process stability ­ all three will be described later. On
top of that, the desire was to unify the setup for as many TrackMan products as possible.
Finally, a new level of automatization was expected to simplify the work for the human
operators.
1.4
Literature review
The following literature review is divided into three parts. The camera calibration part
introduces the topic of camera modeling and the methods to determine the parameters
of those models. The supervision systems part provides an overview of how one might
monitor an automated system such as an industrial robot. Lastly, the third part presents
a few examples of how an algorithm can detect changes and faults in a system based on
data from the supervision system.
1.4.1
Camera calibration
One technical way to describe a camera is ”a mapping between the 3D world (object
space) and a 2D image” [3]. In other words, it is a function that, given the coordinates
of a point in 3D (world), returns the coordinates of the same point projected onto a 2D
plane (image). Many camera models exist that describe three fundamental aspects of a
camera. Those are intrinsic, lens distortion, and extrinsic parameters. Intrinsic param­
eters describe the mapping from 3D to 2D, assuming the coordinates are in a common
coordinate system. Lens distortion parameters account for non­linearities in the 3D to 2D

==PAGE:14==
mapping that intrinsic parameters can not capture. Lastly, extrinsic parameters define the
relative pose between the camera and object coordinate systems.
Probably two of the most common families of intrinsic camera models are Pinhole and
Fisheye camera models. The Pinhole camera model seems the most used for all cameras
with a field of view below 120 degrees. One reason for that is simplicity, as the Pinhole
model is linear. The second reason is readily available implementation and support in
tools like Matlab ­ ”Camera Calibration Toolbox for Matlab” [4] and libraries for C/C++ and
Python ­ OpenCV ­ to name a few.
Matlab and OpenCV implementations rely on Zhang’s method from the year 1998 [2] to
initialize the camera matrix, followed by non­linear optimization. The latter was inspired
by Heikkilä and Silvén’s work [5]. The third method, which has been used since 1987,
is Tsai’s method [6]. The main difference between Zhang’s and Tsai’s methods is their
calibration target type. Zhang utilizes a 2D (coplanar) target, while Tsai’s method relies on
a 3D (nonplanar) target. In TrackMan, camera calibration is performed using the OpenCV
library.
In [7], the authors described singularities and named the parameters that can not be
estimated while working with plane targets. Their work helps avoid singularities in practical
camera calibration, allowing for building a better dataset. In [8], the authors presented a
novel approach to camera calibration. The new method improves final accuracy with
respect to a standard method using precision planar targets. With this novel approach, it
is possible to use inaccurate, unmeasured, roughly planar targets.
Autocalibration, described in [9, 10], is an alternative to standard offline calibration rou­
tines. It determines parameters from typical camera images of a general scene. Specific
methods may make few or no assumptions about the viewed scene or camera motion be­
tween images. However, even with a reduced camera model, the autocalibration process
is unreliable and depends on the specific scene composition.
Finally, in recent years, the field of extrinsic calibration between two sensors mounted
rigidly to a moving body capable of determining egomotion has caught the attention of
researchers. The article [11] addresses the crucial role of camera­LiDAR sensor fusion
in autonomous navigation research. The proposed novel calibration method achieves
accurate six­degrees­of­freedom (6­DOFs) rigid­body transformation estimation between
the camera and LiDAR sensors. Similar work was described in the article [12]. The
difference was that the sensors were generalized to any sensor capable of estimating its
motion.
On top of that, the researchers added global optimality criteria to the extrinsic calibration
in article [13]. Lastly, the most promising in the context of this thesis was an article about
”hand­eye calibration” [14]. The authors proved that the described technique provides
a certifiably globally optimal solution to both the known­ and unknown­scale variants of
hand­eye calibration (provided that the measurement noise is bounded).
1.4.2
Supervision systems
It is clear that image acquisition in the calibration process cannot be done manually. For
one, a camera must be steady enough not to blur the image. Additionally, as it will be
shown later, each camera should take at least ten pictures for good results. Those re­
quirements demand the camera calibration framework to be automatic or at least semi­
automatic. The most obvious choice for the camera’s source of motion is an industrial
robot (preferably 6­DOFs). Even the best manipulators can break or encounter a fault.
That is why research in supervising industrial robots is presented.

When robots are produced, after mechanical assembly, they are calibrated. Usually, it is
done with an external tracker, such as a laser tracker or high­accuracy optical system. A
user might use the same method to verify that the robot is still operational. The work [15]
presents a calibration method using Denavit­Hatenberg parameters. The authors used a
laser­tracking interferometer to measure the end­effector’s position. This procedure could
be used occasionally to check if the robot’s performance does not change over time.
In the article [16], scientists overviewed the calibration technology of industrial robots.
Again, usage of the laser tracking system has been mentioned. On top of that, the paper
mentions drawstring displacement sensors, probes, and range finders.
The last paper [17] admittedly does not concern industrial robot supervision. Neverthe­
less, the inverse of the presented idea could be used to do so. In the paper, the authors
used an industrial robot as a ground truth for the visual odometry system (VO). If the cam­
era tracked the robot’s movements with a defined tolerance, it could also detect when it
deviates from the preprogrammed path. This observation will be exploited in this thesis.
1.4.3
Change and fault detection
Change and fault detection are integral aspects of control systems, ensuring dynamic
processes’ stability, reliability, and performance. In control systems, change detection
involves identifying alterations in the system’s behavior or parameters, which could result
from external influences, variations in operating conditions, or component degradation.
Fault detection, however, focuses on recognizing and isolating abnormalities or malfunc­
tions within the system.
One valuable source of information about diagnosis and fault­tolerant control is the book
by Blanke, Kinnaert, Lunze, and Staroswiecki [18] with the same title. It covers topics like
system modeling and analysis, fault diagnosis of deterministic and stochastic systems,
and more. The most interesting (from this thesis point of view) is a chapter about change
detection, especially CUSUM and GLR algorithms, which will be discussed later.
The second book that deals with detection theory was written by Steven M. Kay [19]. It
uncovers useful properties of statistical signals that allow for their analysis. Again, the
GLR test was derived for the mean change detection. Final formulas allow for an easy
implementation in discrete systems, which will be part of the discussion in this thesis.
1.5
Goals
As the title of this thesis suggests, the subject at hand was quite broad. A framework
for camera calibration must have been developed, including design, implementation, and
testing. This paper describes a proof­of­concept that aimed at addressing as many of the
TrackMan’s needs as possible. Those were:
• Accommodation of all TrackMan’s products.
• Data acquisition automatization.
• Acceptable camera calibration quality.
• Results validation.
• Online monitoring and change detection.
• Simplicity
• Scalability.
• Justifiable cost of the system.

1.6
Methods
In order to address all user needs, a framework built around an industrial robot was sug­
gested. A core idea is that the robot will ensure the camera’s movement with respect
to a calibration target. A collaborative robot with 6­DOFs was chosen to allow for maxi­
mal flexibility. The robot was additionally specified based on a payload, required reach,
repeatability, and operator’s safety.
As a next step, the robot was used for data acquisition using one of the cameras used
by TrackMan in its products. Based on the data set, a suitable calibration procedure was
addressed. Different approaches to the problem were tested, including parameters such
as relative pose to the target, training dataset size, and how TrackMan optimizes the
camera model.
Next, two supervision systems were developed and benchmarked against each other. The
first uses laser triangulation sensors to accurately measure the end­effector’s position (up
to micrometers). The system decides if there is a change based on a local measurement.
Before a camera calibration procedure starts, the robot must take a control measurement
at the so­called ”Laser Measuring Station” (LMS).
The second supervision system consists of a calibrated monocular camera mounted per­
manently on the robot. The camera can estimate its position based on a known environ­
ment. The second system works in every robot pose as long as the reference markers
are within the field of view. However, this visual localization (VL) approach is inferior in
estimation accuracy, which will be discussed in this thesis.
Lastly, the process’s stochastic nature enforced the implementation of a change detection
algorithm. The Generalized Likelihood Ratio (GLR) algorithm was chosen because it
detects unknown mean change. On top of that, parameters can be tuned to achieve
desired probabilities of false and correct detection.
Ultimately, all the system components had to be integrated into one coherent framework.
At an early stage of the project, it was decided that Python (programming language) would
be ”the glue”. The first reason was that camera calibration scripts used by TrackMan were
written in Python. Secondly, implementation was to be kept simple without introducing
unnecessary hardware ­ such as Programmable Logic Controllers (PLCs). Only real­time
requirements for the system were connected to the robot’s movement. Fortunately, the
robot comes equipped with a real­time controller, which was responsible for the safety
aspect of the project.
As a result, the whole process can be controlled from one computer with Python installed.
That is possible because interfaces were developed for each part of the system. That in­
cludes communication with the robot over TCP/IP, laser measuring station (LMS) through
RS422, and camera through USB­C.

Coordinate systems
There are three main groups of coordinate systems present in this paper (all of which are
cartesian coordinate systems):

3.1.2

The pinhole camera model is only valid in theory. In practice, a camera with an infinitely
small aperture does not exist. Therefore, an optical system (such as a lens) is usually
required to focus the incoming light onto the image sensor. In doing so, some degree of
lens distortion is introduced. It varies between lens types. Usually, it is more challenging to
manufacture a wide lens with low distortion than it is to create a narrow lens. Nevertheless,
the distortion is modeled for all cameras.

3.1.3
Non­linear optimization
Zhang’s method [22], which is implemented in toolboxes such as ”Camera Calibration
Toolbox for Matlab” [4] and OpenCV package, returns only an algebraic solution for the
intrinsic and extrinsic matrices. It starts by neglecting lens distortion. (The method also
assumes that all target points are co­planar). Then, it relates object points to the image
projections through homogenous coordinates. Lastly, by means of a closed­form solution,
it estimates camera extrinsic and intrinsic parameters.
It is important to stress that algebraic methods provide simplified solutions to the problem.
Non­linear parameters, such as lens distortions, can not be solved with those techniques
[24]. Therefore, algebraic methods are refined by non­linear optimization ­ the first sup­

3.3.4
Efficient pose selection
Number of images used for camera calibration is only one variable of the process. From
the company’s perspective, the faster the calibration step, the better. Therefore, the num­
ber of poses needed should be minimized, with a constraint on output meeting TrackMan’s
requirements. The work by P. Rojtberg and A. Kuijper [23] provided the guidelines for ef­
ficient pose selection and was the subject of the following test (Fig. 3.6).
The first five images were chosen manually following the rules described in the paper.
The selection primarily focused on the practice of maximizing the angular spread. If the
calibration output did not match previously achieved values (Fig. 3.5), the number of
pictures was increased. In Figure 3.6, blue horizontal dashed lines represent values the
previous test converged to. Green dots represent tests following efficient pose selection
rules. As shown, an image set of size 21 achieved the same values as the complete
dataset for the CL camera. That result suggests that the number of pictures needed can
be at most 21 if chosen wisely.
In the paper [23], the authors got satisfactory results based on only 10 frames used with
the OpenCV algorithm. This number is significantly lower than the suggested upper limit
of 21. Choosing one over the other means cutting the calibration time in half. The reasons
for the discrepancy are:
• In the study [23], a pattern was panned and tilted in the range of (−70o; 70o), whereas
in this paper, the movement was limited to (−20o; 20o). (The reason was the robot’s
off­center placement.)
• Most of the images were either close to being parallel to one of the target wall axes
or the plane ­ which is not recommended.
• The camera was not moving around the center of the target wall (”satellite motion”)
but around the origin of its coordinate system. (It was due to the size of the wall and
the robot’s limited range of motion.)
After placing a robot in the center of the room, it should be possible to generate poses
similar to the ones described by P. Rojtberg and A. Kuijper. Consequently, the estimated
number of images needed for intrinsic camera calibration should drop from 21 towards
10. Further study is required to determine the optimal number.
3.4
Result validation
The undeniable advantage of the robot is the possibility of creating testing datasets for
validation. All the calibration stations at TrackMan were considered stationary, hence very
similar poses were used for training and testing. That is why the company had a problem
estimating focal length (fx, fy). An additional optimization step had to be implemented
to go around that problem. It relied on explicit information about where the camera was
in space during the extrinsic calibration. (That is why all the device­specific fixtures have
reflective markers.) Conversely, the robot opened the door for more extensive result val­
idation in the future.

4
Supervision systems
From the application point of view, such as camera calibration, information about where
the camera is in space is not explicitly needed. Most of the methods (Zhang [22], Tsai [6],
DLT [25]) estimate camera pose based on the image and target dimensions. Neverthe­
less, in practice, TrackMan uses explicit information about one of the poses to optimize
for intrinsic parameters and to calculate extrinsic parameters between the camera and
the radar. So far, this approach has given results acceptable to internal customers and
valid for the company’s camera applications.
Although image capture positions were measured during setup creation, they have never
been remeasured to verify that there was no change in the system. Currently, no system
monitors pan­tilt robots or static fixtures on the floor. If the device under test (DUT) fails
the calibration procedure, there is no clear indication of which part of the system mal­
functioned. The company wanted this situation to be changed. The goal was to create a
supervision system to monitor the robot’s performance over time. That includes detecting
sudden changes in robots’ position and long­term effects like drift and wear.
This chapter describes different system considerations and introduces two supervision
systems that were implemented and tested. The first is called Laser Measuring Station
(LMS), which characterizes high accuracy and repeatability. The drawback of the system
is the local character. The second system is imaged­based and is called visual localization
(VL). It does not have location­specific constraints but suffers from worse accuracy and
repeatability than LMS.
The purpose of the chapter is to explain how those systems work and their specific at­
tributes. The capabilities of the two systems have been tested, and the results will be
presented with a commentary. It will be shown that the two systems are designed to work
together rather than replace one another.
4.0.1
Motivation
There are many reasons why the robot could change its behavior. Even though industrial
robots are known these days for their reliability, there are cases when they break or need
unplanned maintenance. The most apparent reason for the robot to break is hardware
malfunction. Both mechanical and electrical failures are possible, but the probability is
relatively low.
Arguably, the most common reason for a robot to change its behavior is a misusage by
the personnel. It includes untrained people taking manual control, modifying existing pro­
grams and settings, or even deliberately sabotaging the work of machines. Even trained
individuals might negatively influence the workflow through a lack of knowledge or imag­
ination. Those problems might seem exotic but are standard. No matter the reason, the
consequences of a robot malfunction can be severe, but even worse is not detecting them
in time.
4.0.2
Requirements
The main characteristic that makes industrial robots comparable is repeatability, as de­
fined in the ISO 9283:1998 standard. FANUC CRX20­iA/L collaborative robot character­
izes a repeatability of 0.04 mm. It means that going from point A to B, from the same
direction, a robot is guaranteed to reach a point inside a sphere, with a center at barycen­

the robot.
This thesis distinguished two types of supervision systems. The first type refers to a robot
as a machine working correctly or not. The second type validates that the machine exe­
cutes a correct program. For example, a type one supervision system requires measuring
equipment with similar or better accuracy than a robot to verify that it is within the men­
tioned ISO 9283:1998 specification. It is possible to have one system that can handle

of movement, there will be a moment when the teeth of the two gears are not in contact.

Visual odometry (VO) is the process of estimating the ego­motion of an agent (e.g., ve­
hicle, human, or robot) using only the input of a single or multiple cameras attached to
it [26]. VO has a wide range of applications and has been effectively applied in several
fields [27]. For example, in space exploration, VO is used to estimate the ego­motion of
the NASA Mars rovers [28]. The core of VO is a camera pose estimation, which comprises
rotation and translation computations based on 3D­2D point correspondence.
In the process supervision described in this chapter, the most important thing is to verify
the robot’s pose while the camera under test acquires an image. The trajectory the robot
takes between the points is secondary. Because the environment around the robot was
already designed for cameras, testing a simple visual localization (VL) application for that
purpose was pretty straightforward.
It was decided to use cameras already installed on the robot (CR, CT, and CL) and bench­
mark their pose estimation capabilities against a ground truth reference (total station mea­
surements). In order to calculate the rotation and translation, the intrinsic matrix and the
distortion coefficients must be known. Therefore, the cameras were calibrated accord­
ing to TrackMan standards, including additional parameter optimization. The 3D­2D point
correspondence was based on the ArUco markers present in the room.

5
Change detection
The last part of the presented robotic camera calibration framework is a change detec­
tion. The process will work correctly, assuming the robot’s behavior is repeatable and has
no faults. The supervision systems were introduced to provide data about the system’s
condition. Nevertheless, based on them, a decision has to be made whether the robot is
ready for testing or requires human intervention.
LMS and visual localization (VL) measurements are stochastic. Therefore, correct and
false detection probabilities must be considered when choosing decision thresholds. This
chapter discusses change detection algorithms suitable for the task. It starts with a short
introduction to CUSUM and GLR algorithms. Next, examples of change detection tuning
are presented, assuming Gaussian signals. The mean and variance change detection is
considered.
5.1
Theory
In this paper, the robot’s movement is considered deterministic on a macro scale and
stochastic on a micro­scale. The robot moves from point to point in a closed control loop.
When working correctly, the movement is within specification. Nevertheless, the distance
to a reference point is random inside the repeatability sphere. Data shown in the histogram
in Figure 4.3 give an idea about the probability density function of the process.
Consequently, the robot position measurements with LMS and VL are treated as indepen­
dent random variables z(i), i = 1, 2, … with probability density function pθ(z) depending
upon one scalar parameter θ. Before an unknown change time, k0, θ equals θ0. At time
k0, it changes to θ = θ1 ̸= θ0. The change detection problem is then threefold [18]:
• Detecting whether the condition is normal, i.e. θ = θ0, or the parameter θ has
changed to θ1.
• Estimating the change time k0 (if needed).
• Estimating the value of the change in the parameter if the change magnitude is
unknown.
The detection problem distinguishes between two hypotheses: H0 ­ the nominal case,
and H1 ­ a change has occurred. The condition under H0 is assumed to be known. For
H1, two situations are considered. First, the θ1 is known, or second, θ1 is unknown. This
distinction leads to two different classes of change detection algorithms:
• Detection of a known change – the cumulative sum (CUSUM) algorithm.
• Detection of an unknown change – generalized likelihood ratio (GLR) algorithm.
Both the CUSUM and the GLR algorithms rely on Neyman–Pearson’s approach to de­
tection. No prior knowledge is assumed on an initial condition and the change time. A
fundamental measure to investigate whether data corresponds to a probability density
function pθ0(z) or pθ1(z) is the ratio between the two probability density functions evalu­
ated at z. The probability density function of the underlying random variable evaluated
at z is, by definition, the likelihood function. Since probability distributions are often as­
sumed to be Gaussian [18], the logarithm of this probability ratio gives very convenient
calculations. The log­likelihood ratio of an observation, z, is defined as:

Conclusions
6.1
Summary
This thesis presented a complete framework for the robotic camera calibration process.
The project started with an overview of TrackMan A/S needs and current production pro­
cedures. Next, an implementation of a collaborative robot was suggested. The 6 DOFs
manipulator (FANUC CRX20­iA/L) has been chosen based on a created list of require­
ments. It was placed in the camera calibration room so as not to interfere with a running
production.
For the purpose of the thesis, dedicated hardware had to be developed. The first item
was the T­Fixture, used to mount cameras on the robot and to be a rigid body of refer­
ence during the measurements. The second piece of equipment designed was a laser
measuring station (LMS). It was used to monitor the robot and provide measurements for
the fault detection algorithms.
The presented tests and results would only be possible with software. Therefore, many
tools have been developed in Python to control each system part and connect it in one
coherent framework. Those were:
• T­Fixture pose estimation based on Leica TS13 measurements.
• FANUC robot world frame identification and new UFrame calculations.
• Image capture.
• ArUco markers detection and identification.
• Image pre­processing and filtering.
• Intrinsic camera calibration.
• FANUC RMI (Remote Motion Interface R912) API integration over TCP/IP in Python.
• Laser Measuring Station (LMS) interface (communication with sensors over RS422,
measurement triggering, and storage).
• Image­based localization (Visual Localization).
• Change detection algorithms implementation (GLR).
Individual system components were proven to work. A recommendation for an intrinsic
camera calibration process has been provided. Specific guidelines were laid down regard­
ing the type of images used and their number. Two supervision systems were proposed ­
laser measuring station (LMS) and visual localization (VL). The former was used to verify
that the robot works within the specification. Based on the data, change detection al­
gorithms were implemented and tested. It is possible to detect both the robot’s and the
sensor’s faults.
The project’s ultimate goal was to make a camera calibration process automatic for dif­
ferent TrackMan devices. Therefore, this paper would not be complete without a final
proof­of­concept. A video has been made to illustrate how the calibration of TrackMan iO
will look in the near future. It is available under the link: https://youtu.be/eSj5702j_G4.

6.2
Reflections and future work
The robotic camera calibration framework presented in this paper is a multidisciplinary
enterprise. It covers mechanical design, automation, robotics, computer vision, signal
processing, programming, and more. On top of that, the time limit has been set, where
the project development phase was limited to five months. Naturally, some aspects of it
require improvement if they are to be implemented by TrackMan in the production line.
The following is a comment section on different parts of the project, where limitations,
changes, and future work are described.
6.2.1
Proof­of­concept setup
The main limitation, except time, was the space available for testing. As mentioned, the
company’s production capacity was reaching its limits, and the impact of the robotic cam­
era calibration project had to be minimized. Consequently, the robot was placed in a
nonoptimal position in the room. As a result, the robot worked in a limited space; hence,
the poses it could achieve with respect to the target wall did not reflect its true capabilities.
6.2.2
Safety
The collaborative robot was chosen to ensure maximum safety for the people around it.
One of the unforeseen surprises was the noise that FANUC CRX20­iA/L makes. The
high­pitched sound is loud enough to irritate the human operators. Before deploying the
robot to production, one obligatory task will be to take measures to prevent hearing loss.
6.2.3
Measurements of reference
The total station (Leice TS13) measurements were used as a reference during the project
because of the equipment’s availability and ease of use. The accuracy of it (1 −2 mm)
was not sufficient most of the time to draw conclusions and make comparisons. A better
accuracy tool should be used for the final camera calibration setup ­ preferably < 0.5 mm.
6.2.4
Camera calibration methodology
The only camera calibration method tested was Zhang’s [2], implemented in the OpenCV.
Other methods could be tested, including ones using non­planar targets (Tsai [6], DLT
[25]). The focus was put on the first method because the company had no explicit interest
in changing it. All interested parties accepted the calibration results using planar targets.
The 3D target testing, as an alternative, was not possible at the moment due to a lack of
human resources.
Nevertheless, the output could be improved following the guidelines presented in this the­
sis. The change of images taken and their number would benefit the process. Additionally,
creating testing and validation sets would prevent the optimization from overfitting.
6.2.5
Supervision systems
The supervision systems were developed with change detection in mind. The concern
was that the robot’s failure might negatively impact production. Therefore, it needs to
be monitored regularly. Nevertheless, neither Laser Measuring Station (LMS) nor Visual
Localization (VL) can monitor the robot constantly and accurately enough. The comple­
mentary use of both systems is recommended. Regardless, this decision will be taken by
the company’s management, as the cost of LMS is significant (approx. 50,000 DKK).
6.2.6
Hardware
Designed and manufactured hardware (such as T­Fixture, TrakckMan iO fixture, and LMS)
was planned for the R&D phase of the project. After the proof­of­concept is approved,
additional hardware must be developed for the final robotic camera calibration. The most
critical parts will be:

6.2.7
New camera calibration room
The project was developed to implement it in a new TrackMan headquarters. The new
room needs to be specified to fit the robotic calibration concept. Figure 6.2 presents a
draft of how two robots could be placed in space to facilitate TrackMan device calibration.
The robots are placed at a different distance from individual walls, which makes it possible
to use cameras with various focus lengths just by turning towards an appropriate target
wall with markers (P1, P2, P3). The preferred number of robots for planned production
volumes is two.

6.2.8
Software
The final step in creating a camera calibration routine is structuring the code into a device­
specific calibration program. Even though all Python tools developed for the project have
been tested, they need to be tailored to specific routines. Robot paths have to be pro­
grammed and structured into complete processes. Production engineers will do this work.
6.2.9
Change detection
Methods for change detection were presented together with the tuning examples. The
final decision on what kind of faults should be detected is up to TrackMan. LMS can detect
faults below 0.1 mm, while VL can detect faults below 0.5 mm. (Assuming the camera
performance does not change over time.) In order not to introduce artificial disturbances
in the detection system, the LMS and supervision camera should not be dismounted once
installed.

B
Terminology
The Safeprocess Technical Committee of IFAC, the International Federation of Automatic
Control, has compiled a list of suggested definitions [30] in the field of fault­tolerant control.
It is generally in accordance with the terminology used throughout this thesis, when it
comes to change and fault detection and supervision systems. The definitions below are
direct citations from [30] and [18].
Constraint
The limitation imposed by nature (physical
laws) or man. It permits the variables to take
only certain values in the variable space.
Discrepancy
An abnormal behaviour of a physical value or
inconsistency between more physical values
and the relationship between them.
Error
Deviation between a measured or computed
value (of an output variable) and the true,
specified or theoretically correct value.
Fail­safe
The ability to sustain a failure and retain
the capability to make a safe close­down.
A system where the occurrence of a single
fault can be determined but not isolated and
where the fault cannot be accommodated to
continue operation.
Failure
Permanent interruption of a systems ability to
perform a required function under specified
operating conditions.
Fault
Unpermitted deviation of at least one char­
acteristic property or parameter of a sys­
tem from its acceptable/usual/standard con­
dition. A fault is the occurrence of a failure
mode.
Fault detection
Determination of faults present in a system
and time of detection.
Fault detector
An algorithm that performs fault detection
and isolation.
Fault diagnosis
Determination of kind, size, location, and
time of occurrence of a fault. Fault diagno­
sis includes fault detection, isolation and es­
timation.
Fault estimation
Determination of a model of the faulty sys­
tem.
Fault identification
Determination of the size and time­varying
behaviour of a fault. Follows fault isolation.
Used as a synonym for fault estimation.
Hardware redundancy
Use of more than one independent instru­
ment to accomplish a given function.
Residual
Fault information carrying signals, based
on deviation between measurements and
model­based computations.
