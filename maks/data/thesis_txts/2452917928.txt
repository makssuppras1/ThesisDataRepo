Abstract 
 
Gridshell structures are making a resurgence in the building industry after numerical and 
fabrication tools have become increasingly advanced. At a time when architecture modelling is 
advancing at a faster pace than its analytical counterpart, the need for designing optimised and 
structurally rational form found geometry in a fast paced and parametric environment arises. 
Due to gridshells undergoing large deformations to initially attain the form, their analysis has 
been difficult to carry in finite element programs and their prestress and behaviour has been 
often approximated using 3 degrees of freedom spline theory in the parametric interface of 
Grasshopper.  
 
The aim is to explore the design possibilities presented through form finding gridshells with 6 
degrees of freedom nonlinear beam bending mechanics by combining enhanced dynamic 
relaxation solver and real material parameters. The work is done using K2Engineering, a plugin 
in the parametric design environment of Grasshopper. The thesis relies on comparing form and 
structural response output of 18 computer simulations varying multiple rectangular, square, 
and circular cross sections organised by aspect ratio and corresponding bending stiffness. 
Finally, the research compares the structural response to that of equivalent form geometries 
without the pre-bending. 
 
The study revealed that the in-plane stiffness of the section has significant influence on 
attainable forms similar to the out-of-plane bending stiffness. Symmetrical sections showed 
lower displacements and maximum stresses experienced compared to their rectangular 
counterpart of the same out-of-plane bending stiffness in all 3 ratios and 18 cases studied. Wider 
rectangles’ in-plane stiffness led to an incremental beam-like failure as opposed to the deep 
sections’ local buckling. However, and regarding nonlinear buckle measure it was concluded 
that bending active structures experience geometrical and torsional stiffening that lowers their 
maximum displacement post loading (at the cost of higher bending stress overall), but more 
importantly improves their failure mechanism and endurance to nonlinear buckling load cycles, 
especially near supports.  Although the symmetric sections were performing better than their 
equivalent rectangles in the gridshells with bending, without the prestress, they were 
experiencing both larger deformations and reduced tolerance to buckling. 
The successful implementation of relevant mechanical theory in K2Engineering made it 
possible to study the presented case and build intuition on the rather complex gridshell 
behaviour. There is thus great potential in further using this tool to enhance collaboration in 
the design process in project specific topology and boundaries as well as expanding the practical 
use of gridshells with bending now that more benefits of its prestressing has come to light.   
 
 
 
 
 
Keywords: Bending active; gridshells; nonlinear buckling; dynamic relaxation; Kangaroo; 
K2Engineering; shell structure

1. Introduction 
 
Shell structures, a type of lightweight structures, are making a resurgence in the building 
industry after numerical and fabrication tools have become increasingly advanced. A type of 
shells, gridshells consist of discretised elements or long continuous elements bent to the 
required shape and curvature. Bending active grid shells structures present not only the benefit 
of concentrated shell mass in discretised elements, but also improving day lighting and allowing 
for further reduction of materials, in addition to the ability to manufacture and transport 
straight elements that are subsequently bent into form.  
At a time when architecture modelling is advancing at a faster pace than its analytical 
counterpart, the need for designing optimised and structurally rational form found geometry 
arises. Relying on geometry for rigidity results in light and truly form-active design which 
utilises the structure efficiently. The form finding and analysis of bending active gridshells is 
slowly becoming available in the parametric design field under different theories and methods. 
The parametric design field represents a fast response and dialogue building platform in design. 
The motivation of this thesis is to advance existing numerical tools using full 6 degrees of 
freedom (DOF) co-rotational beam theory in order to be able to explore form possibilities and 
generate form found geometries with structural rationality in a fast-paced environment. A 
consideration of the existing literature and tools shows that predominant 3DOF spline theory 
currently in implementation lacks the incorporation of anisotropic cross section properties and 
torsion, and that a tool based on more accurate element behaviour would better inform the 
design space and dialogue at early design stages. Conversely, 6DOF beam theories implemented 
lack the corresponding solver system that would provide unconditional stability of results.  
There is no lack of critical investigation of each theory; rather, it is their convergence and the 
implication of it in an explorative platform that is missing.  
The aim is then to explore the design possibilities presented through gridshells with beam 
bending 6DOF form finding that are underlined with dynamic relaxation theory. The ambition 
is to gain better insight into shell structural behaviour through comparing the behaviour of form 
found bending active structures with varying cross sections and bending stiffness (EI) from 
3DOF and 6DOF theory. Although the actual tool can be used on bending active grid elements 
prescribed to a given surface, the thesis addresses a regular quadrilateral grid simulated with 
the leading erection process. This is believed to better demonstrate the differences in attained 
form according to each theory. The objectives to meet this aim are: 
• 
Objective 1: implement co-rotational (appropriate mechanics theory to relevant 
nonlinear solver) and verify the implementation on simpler cases 
• 
Objective 2: examine form found gridshells with basic quadrilateral grid 
• 
Objective 3: compare forms of gridshell with a. opposing width depth ratio sections and 
b. same EI sections and c. based on 3dof vs 6dof 
• 
Objective 4: Evaluate structural behaviour of gridshells of the same EI and study their 
respective behaviour to permanent loads and response to buckling 
• 
Objective 5: Draw on the effect of prestressing the laths in their overall stability 
compared to non-stressed shells.

The works value lies in the incorporation of real material parameters and full beam mechanical 
theory in the design process. Thereby giving more physical meaning to the playful nature of 
form finding on Kangaroo2 - a physics plugin for Grasshopper developed by Danial Piker  -and 
building intuition on the implication of biaxial bending and torsion on the form finding process 
as well as the structural response of said gridshells, all using the more stable Kangaroo2 solver 
for live feedback. Additionally, this work sheds light on the behaviour of active bent gridshell 
structures under incremental loading up to failure, using a nonlinear buckling analysis. Finally, 
and perhaps the most vital value this work is hoped to bring is that it will bring qualitative 
insight on active gridshell behaviour in a fast-paced design environment, which is much needed 
alongside the accessible quantitative output.  
The work will rely on comparing form and structural response output of rectangular, square, 
and circular cross sections. The work will not examine the different topologies possible by 
mathematical curves nor will it address the 3DOF spline theory in detail, and where possible, 
will present the mechanical and mathematical theory behind the implementation.  
This thesis addresses the form finding of bending active gridshells and thus commences by 
studying the existing literature in order to identify the pressing questions in research and place 
the work in context accordingly. Based on the findings of the literature, the aims are restated, 
the main variables for investigation are identified, and a methodology is established.  The paper 
provides reasoning for decisions made throughout.  The subsequent chapters deal with 
presenting the results of the study and discussing their implications. Finally, conclusions are 
presented and future work is suggested where applicable.

2.1 Gridshells 
A shell structure is one that derives its strength from its form and, as the geometry governs how 
they work, their classification is both geometrical and structural. Shells are surfaces with a 
dimensional thickness much smaller than its planar spread, allowing them to resist external 
loads mainly in membrane action, which is synonymous with in-plane compression stiffness (B. 
D'Amicoa, et al., 2015); they can be single or double curved. This paper will focus on double 
curved surfaces. In spite of the associated rigidity, shell structures are identified as lightweight 
structures due to their ability to span large spaces using little material. Shells can be considered 
the simplest form of shelter in nature (Figure 10), from an egg shell, to a mollusc and others; 
therefore their use in manmade architecture is natural, especially if some of the structural 
principles behind the ones in nature can be seamlessly integrated in the design. Shells can be 
temporary structures, roofs or a building of their own.  
Gridshells – a subset of shells – are shells that have their mass concentrated in individual 
members. The individual members can be manufactured curved and discontinuous (often 
requiring multiple unique connection designs) or can be flat continuous members that are 
elastically bent into position – often referred to laths (Hernández & Gengnagel, 2015). The laths 
can be a single or multiple layers depending on the covered span, required curvature and 
required bending stiffness. Gridshells can be more favourable in projects where material 
savings, low technical labour, and indoor lighting are a priority (Malek, 2012).

2.2 Bending active gridshells 
Gridshells that are elastically strained into position are known as bending active. The materials’ 
inherent bending stiffness plus the geometrical cross section govern the attained curvature. 
Such structures are expected to exhibit rotational freedom over the nodes normal to shell surface 
to facilitate the erection process  (Lienhard & Gengnagel, July 2018). Although getting the form 
requires large inextentional deformation, an amount of out-of-plane stiffness is required post 
erection to lock the form in position and resist further deformation under permanent loads (B. 
D'Amicoa, et al., 2015). Stabilisation methods by maintaining a shear stiffness in the plane of 
the shell include triangulating the grid, turning the node connections to complete rigid or adding 
external tension cables (Naicu, et al., 2014). Alternatively, the external membrane itself can act 
as a stabiliser, but for the purpose of this research only rigidifying the node will be implemented 
and the other methods are only mentioned and not explored further (Lienhard & Gengnagel, 
July 2018). 
On the other hand, sudden inextentional deformation post form attainment is reduced with the 
reduction of in-plane stiffness, meaning that as the shell becomes less structurally efficient, its 
deformation mode becomes safer (Figure 3 and Figure 2). To fully benefit from the membrane 
action of a certain geometry means jeopardising the structure’s stability in buckling (large 
compression forces) and so reverting to carrying loads in bending may seem counter intuitive 
but ensures a safer structure (Adriaenssens, et al., 2014). Bending active gridshells are thus 
occasionally termed hybrid structures due to their use of beam and membrane load carrying 
mechanisms. Due to the nature of the erection process, actively bent grid shells are inherently 
highly nonlinear structures in their response to load and the popular linear elastic theory is 
non-applicable from the outset and will not be used herein. 
Finally, it must be noted that to attain large curvature and enough elasticity for erection, a 
smaller bending stiffness is necessary, which conversely decreases load bearing capacity and 
restrains the shell from safely spanning larger areas (Lienhard, 2014). To cover larger spans, 
the shell grid is usually built up in multiple layers and so effectively increasing the cross section 
bending stiffness over the nodes. For the benefit of this research, only single lath designs are 
investigated and therefore the analysis and connectivity of multiple lath layers are out of scope.

2.3 Gridshell Construction 
The key advantage of bending active gridshells lies in their ease of transport and construction, 
and their relatively simple connection design. The laths are transported flat before work 
commences on-site, making them relatively easy to build and affordable (Naicu, et al., 2014). 
The three leading erection methods include: 
1. Incrementally building up scaffold beneath the laths to push the grid upwards, 
2. Lifting the laths at specified points along their length and using gravity and their weight 
at the edges to bend them into shape, and 
3. Laying the grid flat at an elevation then lowering the edges into anchoring position 
(Adriaenssens, et al., 2014).

2.4 Form finding  
The surface geometry of a shell is usually subjected to multiple constraints that can often 
contradict an optimal structural shape. Form exploration or form finding is a long standing field 
explored by engineers and designers alike. Forms can typically be categorised by mathematical 
functions, form found (physical or digital) or freeform (Figure 15) (Poulsen, 2015). Of course, 
forms described mathematically like a cylinder or a hyperparaboloid can have analytical 
solutions; however these forms are often both architecturally restrictive and their analysis 
difficult and demanding. This thesis’ focus is on the form found forms whose geometry is 
motivated by their stiffness and vice versa – essentially, forms that are “found” by finding an 
equilibrium state between the external load, and the internal stresses carried by the material 
due to said loads.  
Before addressing shells, it is important to note the role of Robert Hooke’s search for 
Funicularity. Funicular in this context refers to forms that would ensure load is carried in pure 
compression or tension. In the search of a form for an arch that would carry the applied load in 
compression and minimal bending, the inverted chain cable transformed how engineers seek 
geometrical optimality. Hooke’s result showed that there was an infinite number of optimal 
forms, all of which depended on the applied load arrangement and the span to rise ratio 
(Adriaenssens, et al., 2014). Funicularity is closely related to minimal surfaces, where the 
structure is encouraged to do the least work to be stable. The work in this thesis attempts to 
capture some of the analogue principles of the hanging chain, in the digital realm. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 13 Example of a catenary cable and its inverted form, an arch (Khalili, 2008) 
==PAGE:19==
13 
 
In three dimensions, the form possibilities are even greater but considerably more complex 
because of the possible load paths (Adriaenssens, et al., 2014). Much literature exists on the 
works of Gaudi, Candela, and Frei Otto, amongst others that have pioneered structural form 
exploration and thus will not be addressed here.   
In the late 20th century, numerical tools were developed that essentially pursued the same 
funicularity sought by Robert Hooke but in a computational space. The four most important and 
widely used methods include the Force Density, Thrust Network, Particle Spring, and Dynamic 
Relaxation methods, which can be divided into stiffness based, geometrical based and dynamic 
based (Adriaenssens, et al., 2014). Although developed by the same ambition, these tools are 
not restricted to producing fully funicular forms. 
Stiffness based: Stiffness matrices formed of material elastic stiffness Ke, which is constant but 
at the introduction of geometrical stiffness Kg and where relevant initial displacement stiffness 
Kδ(directly related to the deformation of the system=, the material starts to behave differently 
under incremental loading[Leonhard]. 
Geometrical based: The force density (FD) is a ratio of force over length of a cable or bar (or 
surface) segment. FD is a geometrical stiffness problem dealing with structures in tension. The 
thrust network (TNA) method is a generalisation of the Funicularity or thrust line principles. 
Equilibrium state is reached as in a pin jointed system, and thus deals with compression 
bar/segment elements only (Figure 14). Both methods do not consider material elasticity 
(Veenendaal & Block, 2012).  
Dynamic based:  Dynamic relaxation (DR), first established by Day in 1965, and later developed 
by Barnes (Veenendaal & Block, 2012) is a numerical procedure that solves a set of nonlinear 
equations to reach static equilibrium by simulating a pseudo dynamic process in time. Barnes 
then expanded the procedure for structural mechanics. Summarized, the technique traces the 
motion of the structure through time under applied loading. The procedure entails discretising 
a grid into finite elements with intermediate springs and fictional nodal masses in order to 
calculate new nodal positions based on Newton’s law of motion. The technique is effectively the 
same as the Leapfrog and Verlet methods, which are also used to integrate Newton’s second law 
through time (Adriaenssens, et al., 2014). 
The Particle Spring (PS) method is very similar to DR with a simple damp coefficient; however, 
it deals only with springs, and thus material axial or bending stiffness cannot be incorporated 
and consequently, unlike DR – which can deal with bars as well as surfaces – the PS is restricted 
to bar elements.  
Figure 15 Zaha Hadid Nordpark station 
Figure 14 Pavilion by ETH, using TNA

DR is the most popular method for realising compression gridshells due to its ability to deal 
with nonlinearity and material properties, and thus will be the method carried forward in the 
following chapters (Naicu, et al., 2014). In brief, DR is what is done with the force to reach 
equilibrium around a node. How one finds the forces to reach equilibrium are illustrated in the 
next chapter.  The reader is referred to the paper ‘An overview and comparison of structural 
form finding methods’ (Veenendaal & Block, 2012) as well as the book Shell Structures for 
Architecture  for a comprehensive discussion on the methods and their application.  
An alternative way to seeking a structurally or construction optimal gridshell is through the 
optimisation of the lath arrangement rather than the surface itself. There are many ways to 
create a quadrilateral grid on a surface. The different grid topologies are dependent on the 
surface, and any special structural properties the laths need to fulfil (Hernández & Gengnagel, 
2015). Topologies include and not limited to, principal curvature, asymptotic lines, isocurves 
(used in this paper), geodesic or principal stress lines. When a structure is optimised as curves 
instead of surface, certain mechanical stresses (such as torsion) could be left from consideration 
(Schling, et al., 2017). 
Parametric design 
The results of the numerical methods discussed above are best visualised in a parametric design 
environment. The most widely adopted program is Grasshopper, a visual programming 
extension of Rhinoceros. Grasshopper makes it possible to create shell geometry with ease in 
manipulating its geometrical parameters and visualising the change immediately/getting 
instant feedback, thus providing a vital contribution to the dialogue between architects and 
engineers especially at the early stages of design. The parametric design platform also allows 
for the integration of work objectives from the outset with performance based design thus 
avoiding the post rationalisation processes that cost many projects time, money and a cut of the 
creative element (Harding, 2014). 
2.5 Nonlinear solvers 
Structural systems are often discretised to discrete finite elements (FE) connected at nodes, and 
in the case of gridshell structures, this is a valid decomposition. The analysis “goodness” 
becomes a matter of FE solver abilities whereas some use implicit and others use explicit 
solving. Although dynamic relaxation is developed as a form finding tool, it is equally used for 
the structural analysis of a form, the reason being that DR, an explicit solver leads the way for 
analysis of structures with large deformations. The node wise iteration of DR, based on explicit 
methods means that one can deal with information from the previous step only to find 
information regarding the position, velocity and acceleration of each node for each iteration (no 
future prediction) (Senatore, et al., 2011). 
Conversely, the majority of commercially available softwares use implicit methods. The Finite 
Element Method is a matrix-based approach, and for static and quasi-static problems it uses 
implicit integration schemes which solve a system of algebraic equations where the unknowns 
are represented by the displacements (Bauer, et al., 2018). 
The implicit method based on the Newton Raphson entails using “future” information on the 
displacements and necessitates the formation of a global stiffness matrix for the system and the 
inverse of the tangent stiffness matrix at each iteration which is computationally expensive. 
However, the Newton Raphson method is well known for its fast convergence and unconditional 
==PAGE:21==
15 
 
stability. The DR method neither needs a global stiffness matrix nor is it unconditionally stable; 
it also relies on a large number of parameters for its control. Despite the latter, the former 
suggests that DR is the far more suitable solver when dealing with large displacements 
(Senatore, et al., 2011).  
The positive attributes of implicit solvers (and the irrelevancy of large deformation analysis in 
most commercial projects) mean FEM solvers are developed as accurate, stable and rigorous 
best used for detailed design. Consequently, actively bent gridshell structural analysis in FEM 
programs is carried out on the mechanical behaviour of shells with the initial strains and 
stresses produced due to the large deformations being imposed as a “pre-stress” load case 
(Brandt-Olsen, 2016). Brandt argues that the problem with that is twofold, the first issue being 
that these pre-stress forces are not an accurate representation of the actual incremental 
increase of the stress during forming. Second, the prestress forces cannot be determined for 
arbitrary geometries. (Brandt-Olsen, 2016) Finally, although one attains a quantitative insight 
on the behaviour during the loading, there is a lack of qualitative insight on the structural 
behaviour and the consequence of the form finding process and associated stresses on the 
mechanical behaviour of the shell in the conceptual design stage (Li, 2018).

2.5.2 Position based dynamics 
A drawback quickly observed in DR implementation is the “overshooting” of the node due to 
some highly stiff element resulting in a large acceleration vector, and thus leading to instability.  
In a visual programming interface, a problem like this causes system explosion. Kangaroo, a 
physics plugin for Grasshopper developed by Danial Piker was rewritten to Kangaroo2 with a 
new solver based on position based dynamics (PBD) to deal with this issue. PBD is based on DR 
principles but improved to avoid instabilities and sensitivities to all the parameters previously 
input by the user (Senatore & Piker, 2014). 
Kangaroo2 works by giving “goals” strengths relative to each other as weighted factors acting 
on the solver. The scalar weighted factors keep the new position of each point within the convex 
hull of the preset target point (driven by static equilibrium); meaning that a goal with infinitely 
larger strength weighting causes particle to move to the exact target point. Additionally, 
Kangaroo2 works by performing all projections for one step independently and in parallel. 
(Bauer, et al., 2018). PBD has been successfully implemented with a structural calibration based 
on spline 3DOF theory in K2Engineering, a plugin extension by (Brandt-Olsen, 2016), this 
thesis starts by verifying the inclusion of 6DOF full beam theory in K2Engineering.

2.6 Nonlinear structural mechanics 
To determine the residual forces and moments that drive either of the above solvers, there are 
two popular strain measures that are integrated to give us the equivalent deformation. 
Nonlinear problems are often analysed with either a Total Lagrangian measure or a Co-
rotational one. While the former deals with deformations in an element relative to its initial 
state, the latter measures strain relative to the deformed state of the element by deducting the 
effect of rigid body motion. Co-rotational beam is widely considered a more appropriate measure 
for bending active structures (Olsson, 2012) (Naicu, et al., 2014). 
2.6.1 Spline Theory 
Spline theory here is included very briefly for completion, but the focus of the upcoming work 
will be on the beam theory. As an introduction to beam theory implementation in nonlinear 
dynamics based solvers, splines take precedent. Splines are essentially straight elements that 
are bent into shape (Adriaenssens, 2000). The elements are symmetrical on all axes and do not 
sustain torsion. Spline theory was developed by Adriaenssens and Williams as a simpler way of 
carrying out structural analysis on active bent elements as it is preferable if the spline were 
bent by pure bending without any torsion (Adriaenssens, 2000). The idea was that this would 
enable slotting discrete straight segments together to create a curve in space. Moments are 
approximated on segments from the shear force resultant at the start and end nodes, deeming 
it a 3 degrees of freedom concept that approximates bending in the presence of information on 
two consecutive chords (Figure 16).  
 
Adriaenssens argued that the rotational degrees of freedom are not only not required, but often 
their coupling with axial stiffness and translational degrees of freedom cause conditioning 
problems in explicit methods (Adriaenssens, 2000). This phenomenon could still be observed in 
certain stiffness and cross area combination, but it is not as prevalent considering the updated 
solver scheme that ensures that the node particle does not move too far from convex hull of 
solutions causing instability from bending and axial stiffness working opposite to each other in 
retrieving the particle (Senatore & Piker, 2014).

2.6.3 Buckling  
Buckling, a phenomenon of structural instability, is considered a geometrical failure (as opposed 
to material yield or crush) and is highly important to understand when studying gridshell 
structures due to their vulnerability to it (Li, 2018). Gridshell structures addressed in this paper 
are form found to be as structurally efficient as geometrical and boundary constraints allow by 
carrying loads through axial compression, and a direct consequence of such efficiency is 
susceptibility to sudden collapse. Thus, stiffness – and what increases it - and stability 
discussion are intertwined in this section 
Many academic books – as well as other papers – examine the simple case of the buckled column, 
first believed to be solved analytically by Euler (Lienhard, 2014). In the context of this research 
an evaluation of the findings on the buckling of arches and elastica is more eminent, although 
an example of a buckling column is available in Brandt’s MSc thesis using 3DOF components 
for reference.  
The first shape a buckled element attains, the Eigenmode, is related to the first Euler bucking 
and essentially describes the geometrical state of deformation at a relatively low energy state. 
The buckling mode deformation of a structure can be assumed to have a maximised output of 
deformation versus ratio input energy. The mode is one that shows the least resistance to an 
input energy to the system (Lienhard, 2014). Malek’s PhD (Malek, 2012) studied the stability of 
gridshells, although not bending active, but she did find that triangulated topology was better 
than quad, and that the shallower the shell the less the topology mattered. Additionally, 
densifying the grid had a positive structural contribution, especially so in shallower caps. A lot 
of her findings are now intuitive but at the time needed to be built and supported. As far as the 
author is aware, there is currently no equivalent study of bending active gridshell behaviour as 
specific and comprehensive as hers.  
==PAGE:27==
21 
 
Like the nonlinear static analysis discussed earlier, buckling of actively bent structures 
necessitates nonlinear measure of its buckling where an incremental loading procedure 
performs multiple equilibrium iterations in between each load step/deformed shape when the 
stiffness is changed (Lienhard, 2014). It is in this analysis that the DR proves itself most useful 
as it is naturally built to account for this iterative process with load controlled buckling 
measure. The stability of gridshell structures has been assessed by Oschendorf and although 
the findings showed that the prestress decreased the buckling capacity of gridshells, with the 
difference in capacity between stressed and unstressed diminishing with increase of prestress 
using a linear measure, it is believed to not be a comprehensive take on the case and suggested 
by the author a nonlinear buckling study needs to be conducted (Mesnil and Ochsendorf 2013). 
The study also concluded that prestressing does not alternate the failure mode or mechanism 
when compared to non-stressed sections. Finally, a recent study by Baverel (Lefevre & Baverel, 
2015) on stability of spherical cap bending active shells determined that the prestress effect has 
20% positive or negative margin on the buckling capacity and that as the curvature increased, 
providing a steeper lath near the support, attracted a local failure at the support.  
 
An arch is the simplest form of a curved line element in space designed to carry loads in plane 
versus how a straight beam element would carry the loads (bending). Elastica on the other hand, 
is the study of thin elastic lines. They are curves that generate minimal potential bending 
energy (Lienhard, 2014). The elastica is characterised by its local curvature along its length. 
Active bent structures are a hybrid of both. The stability results presented in literature on the 
former two using a nonlinear measure, as well as the latter gridshells using a linear measure 
are presented below in bullet form for clarity. Findings on arch and elastica using a nonlinear 
buckle measures show a lot of consensus between the main contributing authors (Brandt-Olsen, 
2016) (Lienhard, 2014) (Lefevre & Baverel, 2015) and include; 
• 
Geometrical stiffness largely influenced by axial forces in a section 
• 
Tensile stresses developed on the outer fibres of a section due to torsion in an arch or 
bent beam provide geometrical stiffness  
• 
Geometrical stiffness contribution increases as rotations get larger 
• 
Leinhard finds the torsion stiffening effect increases with decreasing elastic stiffness 
• 
Increased curvature in elastica better for buckling capacity (Brandt-Olsen, 2016) 
although with high curvature, there is a reduction in the positive pre-stress effect, 
Brandt argues that the bending reduces the materials ability to withstand applied load 
• 
Brandt and Leinhard also conclude that when bending action becomes more dominant 
in elasticas, the risk of buckling decreases, indirectly inferring to the same for active 
bent gridshells 
• 
Prestress introduced a more ductile behaviour, which is line with bending action, 
compared to a more sudden change in displacement which would be a quality of a 
system dominated by compressive forces 
• 
Lefevre and Baverel determined variation in elastic gridshell capacity ranging 20% 
positive or negative depending on load conditions due to the presence of the prestress

2.7 Scope of analysis in a parametric interface 
This section deals with where design and analysis of form found active gridshells is currently 
possible. Due to FE programs being outside the scope of this paper they are only mentioned 
briefly. FE programs are typically used for nonlinear analysis, but Oasys for example, having a 
DR solver option can be used for form finding, thus tracking prestresses in the sections, while, 
ANSYS, another popular FE program that deals with geometrical nonlinearity, cannot.  
Both design and preliminary analysis can be carried out in a parametric interface, and that is 
what is of relevance for the thesis’ aims and methodology. Below the most prominent plugins 
for Grasshopper are presented and discussed.  
Karamba 
Karamba is a finite element analysis tool built for Grasshopper. Karamba is based on standard 
FEM direct stiffness method which needs formulation of stiffness matrices and is based on small 
deflection theory (Preisinger, 2016) and thus the large deflection analysis is based on 
superposition. Karamba handles geometric non-linearity by an incremental approach which 
incurs unavoidable drift from the exact solution (Preisinger, 2016). 
Additionally, in the context of actively bent shells, Karamba’s beam elements do not make it 
possible to access information about the forces and moments that arise from the movement post 
the large deformation analysis nor does it include a stiffness multiplier for different section 
shapes, and this has been verified and studied in (Brandt-Olsen, 2016) on elastica. The large 
deformation analysis thus gives a qualitative output characteristically based on small 
deformation theory but all incrementally imposed.  
Finally, the Karamba plugin contains a buckling analysis component, but once again it applies 
a linear buckling approach. Linear buckling can only give an indication of critical areas in the 
geometry, but does not provide reliable information on the actual buckling loads. In fact, the 
Karamba component is unable to output buckling load factors (BLF) less than 1.0 and thus may 
ignore the first buckling mode which may be very local (Brandt-Olsen, 2016). 
Kangaroo2 
Is a physics based plugin that uses an advanced form of DR, PBD presented earlier as a solver 
to find the relaxed form of shells. DR, as mentioned earlier, is most relevant for gridshell 
structures to its nonlinear solving and material consideration. Kangaroo2, the current version, 
is based on a Projected Based Dynamics that deals with the instabilities experienced in 
Kangaroo1/DR method due to overshooting of target. Kangaroo2 weighs the target node point 
due to prescribed objectives (loads, supports, material stiffness) against one another and 
projects the node based on a scalar weighting as opposed to adding the forces on each node. 
Therefore, K2 is a more stable program and is widely adopted as a form finding tool (Bauer, et 
al., 2018). 
The detailed algorithm behind the solver - the most powerful and useful part of Kangaroo - is 
not publicly available. What is available are the goal scripts, which all invariably work through 
the solver. The release of the language of the goals has made it possible for users to custom 
script their own goal, and lead way to the precedent of this thesis project, Cecilie Brandts 
K2Engineering which is now a standalone plugin. 
==PAGE:29==
23 
 
Although forms are often found using arbitrary stiffness values, if the correct stiffnesses are 
input, then the output behaviour will reflect the real deformation of the structure. However, 
Kangaroo2 stops here where any further structural analysis would have to be carried external 
to the geometry created. A calibration of form to material properties was implemented by Cecilie 
Brandt in her MSc work using the 3DOF spline theory. The real-time feedback works ideally as 
well with a visual programming interface as the user can actually see the iteration and 
incremental change in form and associated force.  A more detailed account of the Kangaroo goal 
construction can be found in section 3.1. 
K2Engineering 
Is a plugin for Grasshopper that uses the same construction of Kangaroo goals and acts as a 
natural extension of the solver that is however, calibrated to real structural behaviour. At the 
time of this research commencement, K2Engingeering only dealt with spline theory and thus 
modelling beams as a combination of a bar and a rod element with the necessity of having two 
consecutive chords to work out the moment from the shear at the nodes. It was also not possible 
to model bi-axial bending behaviour and torsion. The reader is referred to Cecilie’s master thesis 
for a more detailed description of how the spline theory previously presented in section 2.6.1 
was implemented in K2Engineering.  
Nonetheless, K2Engineering presented the important feature of being able to 1. Trace the 
prestress in the form from forming process 2. Analyse the structure based on a simplified large 
displacement theory and calibrated element stiffnesses all within the same parametric 
interface. 
Since K2Engineering works as an extension to K2, it benefits from the nonlinear solver when 
dealing with buckling analysis. Therefore, a nonlinear buckling analysis is now possible in the 
parametric design environment without having to deal with the large number of parameters DR 
had that all influenced the result.  
SmartForm and EMU 
Due to the limitations of a 3DOF system realisation of material and its real behaviour, dynamic 
relaxation with form finding in 6DOFs has been developed and implemented by Olsson in his 
MSc thesis (Olsson, 2012) and most recently Poulson in his thesis (Poulsen, 2015). While the 
former implemented the tool within SMART form plugin developed by Buro Happold for 
Grasshopper, the latter created his own tool plugin titled EMU (Poulsen, 2015). SMARTFORM 
actually took it a step further by performing a modification to the form based on optimisation of 
the section realised by the utilisation ratio (UR). The form would adapt to find a new equilibrium 
if the UR was unacceptable. It additionally adopted a stress based approach when sizing for 
ULS. Eventually SmartForm was publicly maintained as a 3DOF tool as its intention was 
meant for intuitive and quick exploration and the 6DOF combined with DR was deemed too 
user complex (highly sensitive to input parameters). For example, if the damping or fictious 
masses of nodes too high, the movement (translational or rotational) was very small and 
convergence would take many more cycles to attain (speed problem) and conversely, if the 
former parameters set too low, the simulated damping would lead to numerical instability 
(solution problem). Neither papers presented or discussed the implication of using 6DOF of 
3DOF in the form exploration. Both papers mention issues arising from opposing axial and 
bending goals causing wrinkling in the form.  
==PAGE:30==
24 
 
These tools were the first attempt at more accurately designing active bent structures while 
incorporating the forming forces as it became possible to export the models with prestress into 
commercial FEM packages for further analysis.  
All tools above have been validated by various methods. Some against FEM where relevant and 
others against each other and analytical solutions. limitation to these tools explicitly discussed 
by the respective authors include the wrinkling of the form when taking bending into 
consideration (Olsson, 2012) as well as instability related problems related to timestepping, 
both of which are DR solver inherent issues.

3. Methodology 
 
The set of principles underlining the proposed computational simulations are presented herein. 
A mix of qualitative and quantitative analysis of the outcome of the computational simulation 
would affirm or reject the hypotheses laid in section 3.2 Hypothesis below. The methodology of 
this thesis ensures the soundness of the results by initially investigating simple and analytically 
solvable cases to verify the tools’ mechanical ability available in Appendix A. Secondly, early 
explorative cases are supported by physical models (for precision and intuition build up on 
impeding collapse). The numerical results are compared to output from other platform (EMU 
for active bent, and Karamba for no pre bending) where feasible for verification in Appendix C. 
Although employing analytical tools initially, numerical programming and computing are the 
tools used to acquire the quantitative data and while a significant part of this work has been to 
develop K2Eng into a 6DOF tool with Cecilie Brandt it is not addressed in detail here as it is 
considered a tool for the investigations rather than the subject of the thesis.  
The forms were optimised based on support movement, and a small load to cause the grid to 
buckle out of plane with no predefined surface. For gridshell structures, the most critical stage 
is the erection stage, and that’s why it was decided to mimic this process (Poulsen, 2015). The 
gridshell consists of single lath layer in each directions, and was arranged as isocurves. The 
decision to maintain the grid regular is based on the ease of construction and manufacture and 
application of an appropriate façade.  Additionally, keeping the grid non optimised is anticipated 
to impart knowledge about the form attainment itself.

intended to give the reader clarity on each aspect and how they relate to each other. Form

rectangles (of varying degree of anisotropy w:h). For each rectangle CS ratio (for ex 4:1 ratio for 
w:h) the equivalent bending stiffness (critical in load carrying plane) was used as the constant 
determining the respective CS dimensions for non-rectangular sections. Varying the EI based 
on rectangular ratios is believed to be the clearest way to provide insight into what transitioning 
into 6DOF actually signifies for in-plane stiffness and associated mechanical consequences. The 
choice of CS dimensions and grid spacing was decided intuitively from design precedents and 
was refined when needed.  
Material yield was not a constraint to the form design and will not be directly addressed, but 
commented on where visible. Although GFRP can also be used, the studies presented here all 
assumed timber sections for better load bearing abilities. Finally, the simulations were 
conducted small span (8x8m) however, it is believed that the results have a wider implication 
and the trends assumed scalable. Finally, as considering the practical boundaries for 
construction is the conclusive part of this thesis’ methodology, the methods presented below are 
in accords with simulating the real construction method and connectivity most timber gridshells 
are built with. Thus, rotation of laths at the nodes in the plane of the grid during erection, and 
incremental pushing of point support inwards in Figure 20 (diagonal to XY plane) are deemed 
the essential links between computer simulation and physicality and are implemented herein.

3.1 Methods 
In order to fulfil the methodology laid out above, the following methods were applied. Firstly, 
setting up of physical models using paper and timber strips to investigate large deformation 
and in plane stiffness of beam like laths. Secondly, developing K2Eng from a 3DOF spline theory 
calibrated tool to a 6DOF in C# Visual Studio for Grasshopper interoperability to use in 
conjunction with the Kangaroo2 solver. Thirdly, verifying the developed tool using simple cases 
that were cross verified with analytical solutions. Fourthly, stating hypothesis and devising 
appropriate case study incorporating the variables and constraints wished to be investigated in 
the methodology to answer questions stated in section 2.8.2 Restating aim. Finally, refining and 
updating the case study where improved procedures prove to bring better results.  
Physical Models 
Owing to the highly complex behaviour of actively bent gridshell structures, physical models 
were tested in this study to attain qualitative insight and build intuition that often cannot be 
attained from numerical analyses (Adriaenssens, et al., 2014). The effect of torsion, previously 
presented by (Lienhard, 2014), is re-experimented with first, then a small scale model with the 
same controlled parameters will be developed to simulate both the construction process and the 
form and consequent behaviour. 
Updating K2Engineering 
The theory needed for implementation has been previously introduced in 2.6 Nonlinear 
structural mechanics and below a demonstration of its implementation within the 
K2Engineering environment using C# programming language is proved. But first a little 
reminder on key Kangaroo2 terminology. 
Goal: An objective that drives a set of points to a current position. It constitutes a move and strength vectors that calculate 
where it would move the points to, based on the goals’ strength weighted against another’s. (Brandt-Olsen, 2016) 
Considering how Kangaroo2 works with goals it was vital to translate K2Engingeering goals to 
induce a “move” vector on the nodes, with a specific “strength” that can be resolved in the 
Kangaroo2 solver. The detachment of the element end frame from node frame was the method 
by which large deformations can be traced numerically in a beam. To translate this to 
Kangaroo2 meant that the movement traced by the solver for the node position had to be 
translated to the current beam end frame position. From there the local strains (longitudinal 
and rotational) were measured before they were translated back to global movement to the 
nodes.

Presentation of results 
Results on form will be in the form of qualitative commentary on the shape, rise and twist near 
the support. Structural response cases extend some of the form cases by applying permanent 
uniformly distributed loading on nodes first then carrying a buckling analysis second. While the 
former is for a quantitative analysis – supported by maximum stresses and displacements, the 
latter is restricted to qualitative commentary on the failure mode in this research. Each set with 
the same EI will be subjected to the same loading conditions and finally compared to a non-
stressed shell for a well-rounded perspective on the implication of prestressing the shells. 
Additionally, as nonlinear buckling does not give a single indication of the buckle factor, a 
decision has been made to consider a 1m displacement anywhere in the grid as failure. 
Validation of tool 
In order to verify the procedure itself, validations on the structural response, construction 
process simulation and pre-stress measure were carried out. Validating the tool’s structural 
response against simple two-dimensional cases is available in Appendix A. Validation of the 
node design in the simulations including an example of a hyperbolic paraboloid grid is available 
in Appendix B. Regarding three dimensional gridshell, the pushing of support and upward 
buckle form is validated against EMU (existing tool based on DR theory) and the response of a 
gridshell without prestress subjected to loading is compared to Karamba (existing tool based on 
FE) in Appendix C.  Adding the prestress in a 2D arch or elastica as an external initial prestress 
in Karamba has been presented and discussed by Brandt in her thesis and its limitations 
therefore will not be addressed here. Although extensive sensitivity studies have been carried 
out on the updated K2Engineering, their conclusions are discussed throughout where applicable 
but they are not presented due to their vast parameters and perhaps confusing output to reader.

3.2 Hypothesis 
Form – Physical and numerical 
1. With the proposed forming process, a synclastic shape is assumed to be formed 
2. Circular 6DOF and square section should have the same form 
3. Wider rectangular sections will demonstrate significant twist near the supports 
4. Due to the lack of the 3DOF’s torsional capacity, the rise around the supports will be 
less than that of the equivalent square section in 6DOF 
5. Deeper cross sections will rise less overall due to restraint in curvature 
6. Square sections will have less overall rise (compared to the rectangles) due to equal 
section stiffness 
Structure  
1. The structural response will be dependent on the attained forms, but the response as 
well as the failure mechanics will be highly dominated by the bending stiffness.  
2. Symmetric sections will be more stable under loading 
3. 3DOF and 6DOF circles would yield similar results 
4. Sections that go into axial tension due to forming process will have improved the 
bearing capacity 
5. Sections with the same EI stiffness in the loading plane will deflect similarly under the 
same load 
6. Where a symmetric section has more EA than the equivalent rectangle it will be more 
prone to sudden compressive failure 
7. Local relief upon loading will be experienced where significant curvature (and bending 
stresses) were developed during forming 
8. Enhanced stiffness due to the tensile stresses on the outer fibres of twisted elements in 
wider sections 
9. Sections with more curvature will experience higher axial stresses upon loading 
10. In regards to gridshells without prestress; 
a. Actively bent grids will experience higher maximum stress compared to non-
stressed shells upon loading 
b. Non pre-stressed predicted to always deflect less under the same load a prebent 
grid

4.2 Procedure 
1. Initial 
The very initial run of the case study was carried out using the inherent rigid joint in 
Kangaroo2, but it became quickly obvious that this sort of link would not a. represent real life 
construction b. provide the correct insight to forms found. Although briefly presented in the 
results, this approach was abandoned to include a custom scripted component attempting to 
simulate a bolt connection. Additionally, different support movement as percentages were 
examined, and 30% movement was decided to be exaggerated enough to show distinct behaviour 
while at the same time not ruining a direct axial force transfer to the ground (normal) or adding 
unneeded strain on corner points. Moreover, test 1 was run with single central point loads that 
would ensure the whole network would buckle upwards upon support movement. In subsequent 
test 2 (one further examined and discussed) the load was removed halfway through the 
simulation (at 50% of prescribed support movement). Furthermore, test 1 with single point 
supports similar to the physical model showed to be highly inefficient when it came to load 
bearing capacity and consistently failed at the support. Even with a uniformly distributed loads 
on nodes, the failure was local to the elements at the support, and thus test 2 uses two point 
supports at each corner, with 8 overall. Finally, using a uniformly distributed load to cause the 
initial buckle showed drastically different forms and stress distribution and was deemed 
unsuitable.  
2. Amended 
With the above in mind, test 2 focused on a few cases with 2 support positions in each corner 
pushed to reach the same position as the 4 point support (test 1) for direct comparison. The 
same scissoring component was used in both. Initially started by offsetting one direction of the 
grid laths and connecting it to the other direction with a bar element, but this proved to be 
inadequate in capturing the joint rotation of the laths out of plane even if releasing the rotation 
==PAGE:42==
36 
 
perpendicular to normal. The latter was implemented with a custom component written by 
Daniel Piker. Like all components, the scissor has a “strength” factor, and in the same nature 
of weighting components the strength of the scissor was given the same magnitude as that of 
the beam and support stiffness except when dealing with wide sections. Additionally, the cross 
sections were updated based on knowledge from precedents and test 1 results so that there 
small enough not to buckle during construction but still be used for load bearing. In other words, 
the failure would be governed by shell stiffness rather than individual member. Finally, the grid 
spacing was reduced from 0.8 to 0.5 and the cases brought down to 18 cases represented by 12. 
For the buckling measure, a maximum displacement of 1m is used for all tests. It is believed 
that 1m is large enough to observe a more dramatic and informative failure mechanism.

4.4 Limitation of methodology 
In accordance with the thesis objectives laid out in chapter 1  and the questions raised from the 
literature review in 2.8.2 Restating aim the case study attempts validate the hypothesis in 3.2 
Hypothesis. The anticipated limitations and short coming of the solver and methods are listed 
below, and that of the research is presented in the discussion chapter 
Solver:   
1. Due to the incremental construction procedure, the simulation is time 
consuming as it needs to reach equilibrium in the upward buckle before next 
support push  
2. No indication of yielding. So, although the shape is “true”, but where there are 
sharp changes in curvature, these are areas with potentially yielded fibres. 
3. High sensitivity to goal parameters, including strength of support stiffness 
during forming 
Methods: 
1. The offset of the laths to each other in modelling the scissor joint is not the most 
structurally representative and the strength given to this joint has noteworthy 
consequences on the form 
i. Where wide rectangles wanted to experience high level of twist, the joint 
stiffness was reduced to refrain the lath from folding 180 at the joint just 
trying to adhere to the lath below (Figure 38). 
 
 
 
 
2. Due to not relying on a predefined surface, torsion developed in the forms are 
not easily re-captured from baked geometry  
3. Lack of edge beams 
4. Choosing rigidifying joint over triangulation to provide in plane shear stiffness 
as a simpler to execute computational option -that also makes the grid easier to 
read- although a rigid moment connection is a technical construction challenge 
5. End to end connection is not more rigid than central connections

6. Discussion 
The discussion is divided, like the methodology and results, into form and structural response. 
The form section first discusses results from test 1, with 4 support points, then test 2 with 8 
support points. The structural response discussion is concerned mainly with test 2 and starts 
with discussing maximum stresses developed in sections due to forming, then directly compares 
– by displacement and maximum stress measures – structural behaviour of sections with the 
same EI in the load carrying plane when loaded with uniformly distributed load, and when 
subjected to a nonlinear buckling measure through a buckling load factor BLF determined as 
load cycles until 1m deformation is reached. Finally, the discussion addresses the contrast in 
structural response between gridshells with bending, and those of the same geometry but 
without the prestress. It is worth noting that results were symmetrical in the orthogonal grids. 
6.1 Form 
Physical model 
In examining an elastic strip under central point load and one with significant twisting near its 
support Figure 22 it was observed that there was more resistance in the latter to the applied 
load. The additional stiffness finding adhered to what was previously observed by (Lienhard, 
2014) and others in their study of elastically bent beams.  
The making of the physical models was the first indicator at the importance of getting the lath 
to lath connection right and the significance it has on the construction process. The use of stapler 
clip in the paper model and elastic wire in the timber grid elucidated the importance of having 
the laths slide over each other while forming. The most significant outcome of playing around 
with the model is that it was immediately observable that the gridshell would not entirely go in 
a double curvature form with 1 support point per corner; on the contrary, the top was quite flat 
regardless of how much the corners were pushed in.  This built an early intuition on the 
contribution of in-plane stiffness. Additionally, it became obvious that attaining double 
curvature, either positive or negative, would not occur simply with a regular grid of equal 
lengths. A large change in length is required or a grid that is not regular to begin with.  
Furthermore, the erection process, which induces high bending stresses in the laths, and tight 
curves at the edges, proved tricky to navigate and subjects the global form to sensitivities if the 
corners are not simultaneously and symmetrically moved.  
Simulations 
Test 1 
In regards to the rectangular sections, the deep rises more than wide and although this result 
was not initially anticipated it seems as the form was governed more by the sections’ in-plane 
stiffness than its out-of-plane bending stiffness, effectively allowing the deep lath to basically 
push the free edge laths out to cause the double curvature Figure 26. The attempt at attaining 
double-positive – curvature from a symmetrical square section shows that it subjects the 
outer/edge laths to much compression and the central one to much tension (Figure 51). This result 
was verified by the physical model Figure 25, but the concept clarified even more with the update 
of the methodology to consider eight point supports. It can also be seen that the wider sections 
twist more near the support, which is expected considering the smaller radius of gyration in the 
==PAGE:69==

direction favourable to the rise. For sections sharing the same EI in the load carrying plane, it

6.2 Force 
The following discussion is mainly governed by comparisons of sections based on their bending 
stiffness in the load carrying plane and will focus on the improved test 2 in accordance to 
methods of discussion in 3.1 Methods.  
6.2.1 Forming 
Owing to the difference in bending stiffness across deep and wide rectangular sections, they will 
not be compared directly, but a general consideration of the bending stress showed they were 
higher in the weaker axis exploited to attain the form for each ratio (My in wide, Mz in deep). 
All 6DOF sections experience 0 torsion and Mz stress in the central laths. In regard to sections 
with the same EI, the maximum axial stresses observed Table 8 were larger in wide sections 
over the symmetrical equivalent and that is attributed to larger CS area. However, for the 
bending stresses, wide sections experience higher maximum Mt and My than the equivalent 
symmetrical section, but less Mz which can be seen as a consequence of an available radius of 
gyration smaller in one axis than other. The deep sections experience higher Mt, Mz but lower 
My than the equivalent symmetrical sections with similar justification for the Mt and lower My 
assumed to be due to the homogenous stress distribution in symmetrical sections. 
In regards to the 3DOF and 6DOF circular sections, it is seen that the 3DOF circles consistently 
experience larger axial stress during the forming process than its counterpart Table 8. This 
could be due to the lack of multi axis bending, but it will also be discussed later that the 3DOF 
solver showed high sensitivity to segment length division, where it became less onerous as the 
length decreased, making this direct comparison not particularly accurate. 
*as mentioned in the methodology, test 1 presented several problems upon loading, but what 
can additionally be seen in the results section is that during the form finding process.  Figure 43 
show that in plane global buckling was possible during the erection process. The sides buckled 
in a second order style and it is believed it is because just getting the grid off the ground 
instantiated first order buckle.  It’s seen in sections with width to depth ratio greater than 3, 
but nothing conclusive can be said on when to expect these, just to be aware that it could occur.  
6.2.2 Loading  
The results on form carry significance on the force discussion (as should be the case), but due to 
the forms being very similar in test 2, more variables than the just the curvature are at play. 
The results show that the wide deflects 2x less than the equivalent square section Table 9 and 
it is perhaps due to the larger EA in the former.  The deep sections deflect up to 4x more than 
square which is attributed to the same as above, where the deep sections experience a reduced 
EA to the equivalent symmetrical section, which makes sense. A note on rectangular section, 
although not comparable, an unexpected finding was that deeper sections have a higher max 
displacement than a lower EI wide (across all three ratios), albeit in a different distribution 
where in Figure 40 it is seen that the deep suffers most in the outer laths. This is possibly due 
to the locked out-of-plane stresses from the forming process.  
In regards to bending and torsional stresses the Mt/My/Mz distribution is visibly seen to be the 
same across the shells, only with varying magnitude, the excerpt of which is in Figure 41. Max 
==PAGE:71==

Mt decreases post loading in wide sections indicating significant locked in tensile stresses from 
the twist, that are later relieved in loading Table 10, which agrees with (Lienhard, 2014)  
findings in his PhD. Furthermore, max My does not vary much post loading in wide, but that 
may be due to the magnitude of the chosen UDL. Conversely, the maximum My decreases post 
loading in deep sections because of high level of stress on the top side being counteracted by 
applied load. Finally, it appears that with symmetric sections, the max bending stresses value 
post load is larger than pre (wide 3:1), but this contrasts to stresses being less post loading (Table 
10) when dealing with the higher 1:2 EI’s in all deep sections. This is sort of plausible because 
of the same reason as above, higher EI sections were stressed more to reach the same boundary 
conditions, and thus acting better as “prestress”.  
Again, the axial stresses in the 3DOF sections are larger than those of the 6DOF under the 
same loading conditions. The large discrepancy in displacement was too suspicious Table 9, as 
some sections were not even capable of being loaded by the same load before failing, despite the 
identical EA and EI but a sensitivity study proved that with the reduction of segment length, 
the deformations approximate to that of the 6DOF, although always remaining higher. It is now 
understood that the actual node to node length matters significantly when distributing the 
internal stresses for how the splines are implemented. Due to the 3DOF’s output of resulting 
moments between chords instead of specific axis moments, there is no direct comparison in 
terms of bending stresses; this would also be considered unfair because the forms are not 
identical, so larger or lower stresses will not only  be attributed to difference in theory, but also 
the large difference in form.  
6.2.3 Buckling 
In regards to buckling, wide sections show symmetrical and flat top failure – arguable to call it 
global as it seems more beam bending like - with local failure at support. Conversely deep shows 
failure local snap pockets around the apex and also symmetrically Figure 45. The symmetry is 
expected and so is the beam like failure which a bending active member is dominated with as 
opposed to a section without stress which would be dominated more by compressive stresses 
when loaded, thus making it more prone to sudden failure. The failure mechanism in the former 
is justified by the weaker plane failing incrementally under the load, whilst the latter, with a 
higher EI in loading plane propagates the effect of the incremental loading to the flatter regions 
with reduced curvature.  This finding is interesting because in spite of the wider sections 
showing more sections in compression from forming Figure 51 its failure mechanism was less 
sudden than the deep sections showing a variety of areas in tension and compression Figure 52. 
The symmetrical sections show a failure mechanism similar to deep. It could be understood that 
the reduction of the in-plane stiffness (compared to wide), allows the deformation and 
redistribution of the load in a way that makes best use of the double curvature at the apex. 
Finally, symmetrical consistently show higher BLF than rectangular equivalent could be due to 
the homogeneity in the sections stress, and not just attributed to EA ratios as initially believed. 
3DOF circle sections showed a consistent onerous conduct in loading and in failure. The stressed 
buckling mode of 3DOF does not adhere to the 6DOF buckling mode. In fact, it resembles the 
beam likes incremental and global collapse of the wide sections.  
* a note on going from 4 point to 8 point is that the four point although clarified certain 
weighting of structural principles over others when it comes to shell form finding, it proved to 
consistently fail in a local manner at the supports. This is to be expected due to the high

6.2.4 Without prestress 
In considering the from baked form / no bending sections, it is worth noting that the results 
were verified compared to Karamba’s second order solver (based on small displace theory) when 
applicable. The program as it stands does not allow circular solid sections, so the validation was 
done on the rectangular and square. Owing to the small displacement theory the solver is based 
on, the results start diverging massively when the displacements exceed a few cm. The 
comparison was for reassurance and a case of which is presented in Appendix C, Structural 
response comparison with Karamba. 
Response to load  
In regard to rectangular sections and displacements, wide sections displace more w/o stress but this 
includes significant deflection at the free edges Table 11. This occurrence can be attributed to the 
lack of stress from bending curvature which further explains the deflection of the pre bent laths 
being higher than the w/o in areas where the laths are more straight (near the corners). Such finding 
is expected and adheres to the fundamental engineers equation. Deep sections maximum 
displacement is less w/o, making them more stable and dominated by their out-of-plane bending 
stiffness and this is likely due to the lack of the in-plane stresses induced during the forming process 
that cause the displacement near the free edge to be quite significant. Concerning bending stresses 
both max My post load ratio in wide is larger than Mz and max Mz post load ratio in deep is larger 
than My as expected as that means the axis with the smaller depth is stressed more upon loading. 
However, only the deep sections experience relief in their My post loading compared to sections 
without prestress which is owing to the significant bending stress they are exposed to in the forming 
stage. Regarding symmetrical sections, they consistently displaced less with pre bending and under 
same load suspected to be due to the homogeneity in the prestress. Whereas, the maximum stresses 
are higher in a loaded pre bent shape than not loaded because even with the “relief” experienced in 
the bending active, the initial forming stress leaves a higher magnitude of stress across sections 
highly stressed section. Finally, without the locked in-plane and out-of-plane prestresses, the wider 
sections displace more under an equivalent load to the deep (non-equivalent EI).  
Results of 3DOF circle sections loaded without pre bending are presented in this paper but did show 
much large displacements. This agreed with the onerous trend attributed to chord length sensitivity, 
but also adheres to the general conclusion seen in 6DOF’s where the stressed section deflected 
slightly less. 
Buckling  
Apropos to the failure in rectangular sections, w/o showed consistently slightly higher BLF than

of span in sections w/o stress supporting the proposition that a beam bending dominated shell fails 
more ductile (Brandt-Olsen, 2016) and this case w/o stress is dominated with compressive stresses 
that cause this sudden and local snap. Thereby, the section with bending maintained the roof 
stability and globality of the fail attributed to the high prestress in the central laths with high

Symmetrical sections w/o stress always demonstrated lower BLF. In symmetric section the failure

the w/o Figure 57 and the that is again likely due to the lack of torsion or tensile stresses in that

6.3 Toward practical construction 
To put the above results discussed in a wider context one should consider the original value of 
this work. Bending active structures present a simpler and arguably more economic way of 
making shelters for various purposes. The results presented here present a new added value to 
these structures which is their ability to resist persistent uniform buckling inducing loads. The 
jump from 3DOF to 6DOF although in previous literature was sometimes presented as mundane 
becomes quickly clear in this thesis that it presents new design opportunities at the cost of 
simulation time and slightly increased complexity.  The results presented here show the impact 
of not just going to 6DOF, but what with the 6DOF beam one can design, therefore making the 
application and analysis of gridshells with bending more practical in varying scenarios. The 
connection design in construction can be similar to those discussed in the literature and 
simulated in the case studies, but the introduction of in-plane shear stiffness is recommended 
to be through triangulation, and not rigidifying the nodes as demonstrated here. This is to keep 
the construction process simple.  
The abundance of work on choosing the grid topology in order to facilitate manufacturing or 
assembly process or stress flow can equally be implemented with this tool. The benefits of each 
particular curve type to a surface are out of this projects scope but the figures below show a 
basic study of geodesic and asymptotic curves on a hyperboloid surface loaded uniformly as bent 
and without bent stresses, where regarding to maximum displacement measures, seem to 
adhere to above findings.

7. Conclusions 
 
While exploring the design opportunities of form found gridshells using beam theory, this thesis 
has utilised mechanical beam theory, numerical nonlinear solving theory combined with object 
oriented programming in Brandt’s K2Engineering to explore the different design possibilities. 
A direct outcome of this is that we are now able to simulate full mechanical behaviour of actively 
bent structures and not just limited to timber rods, thereby overcoming limitations usually 
associated with the 3DOF spline theory and instability inherent in the DR theory.  By 
investigating the form and consequent structural behaviour of gridshells with varying cross 
sections, this paper sheds light on the significance of moving from 3DOF spline theory in 
parametric design space to full 6DOF beam theory and elucidating the value gridshells with 
bending bring to failure over gridshells without. Thus, the updated K2Engineering (obj. 1): 
• 
Allows designer to learn new things quicker 
• 
Reduces input complexity, but at the cost of sensitivity to parameters 
• 
Overcomes instability problems familiar to other solvers by using Kangaroo2 engine 
• 
Permits use of rectangular sections providing more architectural flexibility and curve 
optimisation possibilities for pre-set surfaces (asymptote, principal,.. etc)  
studies on the form concluded with (obj. 2-3); 
• 
Out of plane stiffness is a dominant factor in attaining double curved surfaces using 
anisotropic cross sections 
o Using a regular grid to attain a double curved gridshell is impractical in wider 
rectangles where their in-plane stiffness restricts them from curving at the apex 
o Using a regular grid to attain a double curved gridshell is possible with deep 
rectangular sections as their out of plane stiffness allows them to push outwards 
when the central laths wish to curve or elongate 
o A deep vs. wide section (with different out of plane EI) will result in the deeper 
section rising more and giving high curvature in the middle compared to the 
wide when the boundary conditions are constant between both. 
• 
As the laths approximate a discretisation of a double curved surface, the anisotropic 
section properties have less impact on the form 
o Shortening of the edge lath on all sides in the regular grid resulted in similar 
forms between orthogonal cross sections 
• 
Form finding gridshells to approximate a non-developable surface (as many double 
positive curvatures tend to be) is “easier” with a. symmetric sections or b. highly elastic 
material 
• 
Using symmetrical sections results in having in-plane and out-of-plane bending equal 
so they can elongate respective planes to compensate for increase in length required to 
attain double curvature 
• 
3DOF circles restrain the laths possible rise due to the lack of torsion allowance 
The thesis then extended to structural behaviour of the gridshells with varying CS and using 
a nonlinear buckling measure and concluded with (obj. 4);

• 
Sections of higher EI can deflect more under the same load if the out of plane stiffness

Symmetrical sections of equivalent EI out of plane deflect more than wide and less

6DOF square and circular sections produce very similar results while 3DOF circle
