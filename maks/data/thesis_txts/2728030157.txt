Abstract
The extensive use of polyurethane (PUR) products has led to increasing environmenôÙ
tal concerns, as traditional disposal methods often result in persistent waste and polluôÙ
tion. Enzymatic degradation with urethanases offers a promising and ecoôÙfriendly way
of recycling PUR without a large environmental burden. However, there are currently
very few urethanases available with industrially viable activities. In this study, two putaôÙ
tive urethanases from the lipase family I.3, LipCE and ubLipA, were purified by isolation,
solubilization, and refolding of inclusion bodies after recombinant expression in E.coli.
Qualitative studies found that LipCE was thermally stable until 51.77ô¯C and hydrolyzed
ImpranilôÛ in an aqueous solution. Both LipCE and ubLipA were successfully crystallized
in closed conformations and gave XôÙray diffraction to a resolution of 1.8 û. LipCE and
ubLipA had six and eight calcium binding sites identified, respectively, with four of them
being shared between the two lipases. In LipCE, two lid structures covering the active site
were identified, while ubLipA had a single longer lid structure performing the same funcôÙ
tion. Multiple attempts were made to crystallize LipCE and ubLipA in open conformations,
where these lid structures reveal the active site, including soaks with ibuprofen, oils, and
coôÙcrystallization with paraoxon ethyl. Unfortunately, none of the attempts yielded crysôÙ
tals with a solventôÙexposed active site. The mechanism for opening these lid structures
has yet to be fully understood, and this study indicates that the presence of calcium ions
alone was insufficient to open them.

1
Introduction
Plastics have undergone a transformative journey over the last century, progressing from
a valuable resource utilized by the military as a lightweight metal alternative to becomôÙ
ing an indispensable component of our modern lives [1]. With their durability, resistance
to degradation, and ease of production, plastics have emerged as vital elements in nuôÙ
merous aspects of everyday existence, encompassing areas such as food packaging,
clothing, construction, electrical insulation, and a multitude of other applications [2]. UnôÙ
fortunately, the beneficial properties of plastics also make them difficult to dispose of. As
of 2015, an estimated 6300 million metric tonnes of plastic waste had been produced
globally, with approximately 9% of that plastic waste recycled, and the remainder of it eiôÙ
ther ending up incinerated (12%) or in landfills (79%) [1]. To combat the plastic pollution
rate, sustainable bioplastics and enzymatic biodegradation of existing plastics are being
heavily researched [3]. Plastics with hydrolyzable bonds, such as esters or amides, have
the potential to be enzymatically depolymerized into wellôÙdefined oligomers or monomers,
enabling their reuse in the production of virgin plastics or upcycling into higherôÙvalue prodôÙ
ucts. Notably, polyethylene terephthalate (PET, see Figure 1.2B) can be enzymatically
depolymerized to yield terephthalic acid and ethylene glycol, which was shown by the
company Carbios when they managed to hydrolyze more than 90% of amorphized PET
plastic bottles within 10 hours [4]. Polyurethane (PUR) is the secondôÙlargest class of hyôÙ
drolyzable plastics, with an annual global production of more than 25 million tonnes [5],
making it an obvious next target for biocatalytic degradation.
1.1
Composition and Synthesis of Polyurethane
Polyurethane is a class of plastics widely used to create soft foams (mattresses and
sponges), hard foams (insulation and construction materials), and thermoplastics (sport
shoe soles). The plastic is characterized by its central polymerization reactions, in which
isocyanates react with alcohols, forming urethane linkages and amines to form urea linkôÙ
ages [6]. The plastic consists of alternating block coôÙpolymers with hard, crystalline segôÙ
ments and soft, rubbery segments, as shown in Figure 1.1. The hard blocks are made up
of a small isocyanate and a chain extender, namely a diol or diamine, while the soft blocks
consist of polyols or macrodiols, typically ranging from 1000 to 2000 Da in size. The hard
segments are highly polar, while the soft regions are apolar, making them thermodynamôÙ
ically incompatible. This incompatibility leads to heterogeneity in the PURãs bulk material
morphology, granting regions of relatively high and low crystallinity. The degree to which
this phase separation occurs depends highly on the individual segmentsã chemistry and
the processing history [7].
The alternating soft and hard blocks in PUR make the plastic elastomeric. This means
that the soft regions can elastically deform during strain, and subsequently, the hard segôÙ
ments ensure that the material reverts back to its original form after strain removal. These
elastomeric PURs are typically considered thermoplastics since their elasticity does not
stem from crossôÙlinkages. This means that elastomeric PURs can be reshaped by meltôÙ
ing numerous times without a significant loss of material properties. When heated, the
hard segments undergo a dissociation of their crystalline structure, enabling them to reôÙ
organize themselves. Once cooled, the phases separate again, reinstating the materialsã
elastic properties [6].

1.2
Enzymatic Degradation of Polyurethane
PURs are a staple class among biocompatible materials. They have been used in mediôÙ
cal devices like pacemakers for over 50 years. The plasticsã exceptional biocompatibility
and versatile mechanical properties make them an obvious choice for devices implanted
directly into our bodies. Specifically, PURs have excellent resistance to enzymatic degraôÙ
dation in the human body, and reducing the biodegradability of the plastic has been an
active field of research ever since it was first used in the medical field [10]. Unfortunately,
as an unintended consequence, many PURs used in industry today are exceptionally
challenging to recycle.
Due to the complex structure of PUR, partial depolymerization, targetting specific bonds
found in all polyurethanes, regardless of their building blocks, is an excellent initial apôÙ
proach towards enzymatic degradation of PUR. Hydrolases such as esterases, cutinases,
and lipases, targeting the ester bonds present in the plastic, similar to enzymes active
against PET, are actively being researched and characterized [11, 12], with hopes of
finding enzymes that are able to effectively cleave the ester bonds present in PUR. The
PlasticsôÙActive Enzymes Database, PAZy [13], contains an overview of all currently charôÙ
acterized enzymes with biocatalytic activity against PUR.
1.2.1
Family I.3 Lipases
Among the characterized PURôÙdegrading enzymes, the lipases PueA and PueB from the
subfamily I.3 are promising targets [14, 15], showing activity against waterôÙsoluble PUR
homologs, such as ImpranilôÛ [11]. Currently, there is limited information on the details of
the catalytic activity these lipases have against PUR and Impranil. Fortunately, however,
the catalytic domain of a Pseudomonas sp. MIS38 family I.3 lipase (PML) has been strucôÙ
turally characterized with a bound ligand [16]. These studies indicate that I.3 lipases are
highly calciumôÙdependent, as 11 calcium ions were bound to the crystal structure. PML
was also found to exhibit significantly higher catalytic activity in the prescence of Ca2+.
This calcium dependency has also been wellôÙdocumented in other I.3 lipases studies.
For instance, the metagenomeôÙderived family I.3 lipase LipCE was found to have strongly
increased levels of activity as well as thermal stability in the presence of calcium [17]. EnôÙ
zymes such as PML and LipCE might have similar activities to PueA or PueB, something
which is also indicated by their sequence homology (Figure 1.4).

Aims
This Masterãs thesis aims to structurally and functionally characterize putative urethanases
for the degradation of polyurethane. The results from the project are intended to contribute
towards the engineering of synthetic urethanases with increased efficiency in polyurethane
degradation, high chemical resistance, and thermal stability. Two family I.3 lipases, LipCE
and ubLipA, have been identified based on phylogenetic analysis of their amino acid seôÙ

project also aims to elucidate the activation mechanism of these enzymes.

3.1
Chromatography in protein purification
Protein purification is the process of reducing an often complex mixture of proteins to a
single or a few specific proteins. Protein purification is used for the industrial production
of enzymes, diagnostics, and research. Crucially, for this project, it is used for obtaining
highôÙquality proteins for structural and functional studies. Protein purification is typically
used for isolating recombinant proteins. When recombinant protein is produced, it is harôÙ
vested alongside proteins native to the host organism. Therefore, it is considered impure.
Through the use of chromatography, these proteins can be isolated by selectively separatôÙ
ing them based on their distinct physicochemical properties, such as their size, charge,
polarity, and affinity for specific ligands. Therefore, chromatographical techniques are
widely used in protein purification [20].
Various chromatographical techniques are available today, each presenting its own set
of benefits and limitations. Liquid chromatography (LC), which utilizes a liquid medium
to separate samples as they migrate through a column, is the most relevant technique
for isolating proteins. This is because LC can be used to separate proteins in mild conôÙ
ditions without high temperatures, organic solvents, or other strong denaturing agents.
Other chromatographical techniques, such as thinôÙlayer chromatography (TLC) and gel
electrophoresis, while not widely used for protein purification of proteins, offer methods
for analyzing the composition of protein samples.
Sodium dodecyl sulfateôÙpolyacrylamide gel electrophoresis SDSôÙPAGE is a fast and powôÙ
erful tool for both analytical and validation purposes, especially in protein purification. The
technique utilizes sodium dodecyl sulfate (SDS) to separate proteins by size. SDS serves
a dual function in SDSôÙPAGE: Firstly, SDSãs anionic sulfate group provides a netôÙnegative
charge to target proteins while also masking their intrinsic charge. Secondly, the long
hydrocarbon chain in SDS binds to target proteinsã hydrophobic regions, denaturing quaôÙ
ternary, tertiary, and secondary structures. When used in conjunction with a reducing
agent, such as dithiothreitol (DTT), to break disulfide bonds, proteins can be completely
linearized and coated in SDS with a constant massôÙtoôÙcharge ratio of 1.4 grams SDS per
gram protein [21]. When SDSôÙbound protein samples are placed in a polyacrylamide gel
and exposed to an electric field, they will migrate through the gel with a speed directly
relative to their charge, whereas less negatively charged proteins will migrate faster. The
samplesã size, concentration, and purity can be extrapolated by visually analyzing the
bands of protein migrating through the gel. While the technique holds great analytical
prowess, it is best used in conjunction with other purification techniques to validate the
purity of purified proteins.
Unlike SDSôÙPAGE, which is mostly used for analytical purposes, fast protein liquid chroôÙ
matography (FPLC) is a set of commonly applied techniques for highôÙquality protein puôÙ
rification. FPLC techniques utilize solubilized proteins with a liquid mobile phase, which
migrate through a stationary phase, commonly called a column. The type of separation
of proteins through FPLC depends on the combination of mobile and stationary phases.
Some chromatographical techniques, like ion chromatography and affinity chromatograôÙ
phy, utilize a column to fully bind the protein of interest in the initial mobile phase conôÙ
ditions. And then, by gradually changing the mobile phase toward conditions where the
proteins will dissociate from the column, the proteins bound to the column are separated

3.1.1
Size-Exclusion Chromatography
SizeôÙexclusion chromatography (SEC) is a form of FPLC used to separate molecules, in
this case, proteins, by their molecular weight. The technique is also widely known as gelôÙ
filtration and molecularôÙsieve chromatography. Itãs a technique commonly used in the fine
polishing steps of protein purification. In SEC, proteins are partitioned between a mobile
phase and a stationary phase, the latter of which consists of a porous matrix with wellôÙ
defined porosity. The stationary phase is typically composed of a matrix of beads packed
into a column with two measurable liquid volumes: The external volume, representing the
liquid between the beads, and the internal volume, which is the liquid inside the beads.
The external volume is typically referred to as the void volume V0, and the sum of the
external and internal volumes is considered the total column volume Vt or CV [25].

Following sample injection, proteins larger than the pore size of the column matrix will be
excluded from the internal volume of the beads and will, therefore, only diffuse throughout
the external volume. Consequently, these larger proteins will travel through the column at
the same speed as the mobile phase and elute with the void volume V0. In protein purifiôÙ
cations, this fraction typically contains large oligomers and aggregated protein. Smaller
proteins, with sizes small enough to fit into the pores, will diffuse throughout both the
external and the internal volumes, and therefore migrate more slowly. Since the beads
have a range of pore sizes, proteins of larger relative sizes will have a smaller internal
volume to diffuse through than their smaller counterparts. This effect causes the proteins
to be separated within the column relative to their size and elute in a sequence relative
to their molecular weight. The specific elution volume for each protein Ve will always be
greater than V0 and relies on the fraction of the internal volume available to the protein for
diffusion. This fraction is called the partition coefficient Kav, and can be experimentally
determined by:

In SEC, the molecular weight of proteins can be estimated by running a series of stanôÙ
dard calibration proteins through the SEC column and comparing a protein of interestãs
elution volume Ve with a linear regression curve of the calibration proteins. If the protein
of interest shares the same shape as the standard calibration proteins, which are typically
globular, Ve provides a reliable mass estimate. However, nonôÙglobular proteins can elute
significantly earlier than spherical proteins with the same mass, significantly overestimatôÙ
ing their molecular weight. This phenomenon is commonly observed in highly asymmetric
and elongated proteins and is caused by the proteins rapidly tumbling while in solution.
This tumbling leads them to take up more space while in solution than their actual size
suggests. The apparent volume that proteins occupy in a solution can be described as
their Stokes radius Rs, commonly called their hydrodynamic radius. Even though Ve of
a protein more closely represents their Rs, it is generally assumed that all proteins in a
mixture have the same symmetrical shape so that proteins will elute in descending order
of their molecular weight [25ã27].
When designing a SEC experiment, it is important that the column material is compatible
with the samples of interest. The column should be chemically inert toward the sample
and allow for highôÙresolution separation within the sampleãs molecular weight range. For
biological samples, Superdex, which consists of a mixture of dextran and agarose, is comôÙ
monly used for its low nonspecific interactions with proteins, in addition to a high pressure
tolerance and stability after exposure to abrasive chemicals and detergents (such as SDS,
urea and hydrochloride). Superdex columns are compatible with most mobile phases,
and therefore it is recommended to use a buffer optimized for the stability of the sample
protein as a mobile phase. However, the addition of at least 100 mM NaCl or KCl can
prevent both proteinôÙprotein and proteinôÙmatrix electrostatic interactions, granting higher
resolution [25, 28].

small volume. 1ôÙ5% of the total column volume is typically recommended. Because of this
low sampleôÙhandling capacity, SEC is usually one of the final stages in protein purification

3.2.1
Growing protein crystals
Crystals are generally formed when a substance in an aqueous solution precipitates
slowly. Most people have probably experienced this with sea waterëƒ as the water evapoôÙ
rates due to heat from, e.g., the sun, the salt concentration in the water slowly increases
until the liquid is supersaturated. At that point, salt crystals will start forming, or nucleatôÙ
ing. As the salt crystallizes, the salt concentration in the water decreases. When there
are already crystals, any additional salt in a supersaturated solution will contribute towards
growing the existing crystals rather than forming new ones as the water evaporates. With
proteins, the same principles apply, with the added layer of complexity that they also need
to remain in solution without aggregating or denaturing. A standard method for achievôÙ
ing this is to dissolve purified protein in an aqueous solution containing a precipitant at a
concentration, [precipitant], slightly lower than what will precipitate the protein. Through
controlled evaporation of the water, [protein] and [precipitant] will both rise, which results
in protein precipitation [36, 37].
One of the most frequently used crystallization techniques is vapor diffusion. This method
involves letting a small drop of protein/precipitant solution equilibrate with a larger aqueôÙ

toward the reservoir with a higher precipitant concentration than in the protein drop. This
causes water to slowly leave the protein drop until the dropãs and reservoirãs osmolarity are
equal. Figure 3.3A shows a typical vapor diffusion chamber diagram. As water leaves the

rigorous interactions, which stabilize into a crystal. Free proteins surrounding the crystal
will then align themselves with it and incorporate into the crystal surface. As this hapôÙ
pens, [protein] decreases while the precipitant concentration remains stable, leading to a
transition into the metastable zone, where the protein concentration is no longer superôÙ

slowly adsorb into the surface of existing crystals. [36ã39].

3.2.2
Crystal symmetry and X-ray Diffraction
To better comprehend the phenomenon of reflections in protein crystals, it is essential to
delve into the fundamental aspects of crystal geometry. Crystals comprise an array of
stacked unit cells. A unit cell is the smallest portion of a crystal lattice that shows the
threeôÙdimensional pattern of the entire crystal. A unit cellãs shape is characterized by
its three main axes, a, b, c (also known as lattice vectors x, y, z), and the angles öÝ, öý, ö°
between them. A unit cell can contain as little as a single protein but can also comprise
proteins in a dimeric or oligomeric conformation. Furthermore, depending on the shape
of the unit cell, it can contain two or more asymmetric units, each containing one or more
proteins. The symmetry of how unit cells and their asymmetric units are arranged within
a crystal lattice is described by the crystalãs space group. Biological molecules are able
to form in sixtyôÙfive different noncentrosymmetric space groups [36, 37].
The Braggs demonstrated that calculating diffracted beam angles in a crystal could be apôÙ
proached by treating diffraction as the reflection of XôÙrays from sets of equivalent, parallel
planes. This is why each spot in a diffraction pattern is called a reflection. These sets of
crystal lattice planes are identified by three indices hkl (Miller notation), where h, k, and l
denote the inverse intercepts of a lattice plane along the lattice vector, a, b, c, respectively
[42].

commonly known as the phase problem, and finding a solution to it involves using a similar
data set to determine the phase angles in the original. One such method is Isomorphous
replacement. It works by adding strong diffractors, such as Hg, Pt, or Au, to the same loôÙ

metals are added by soaking crystals in solutions containing them. Initial estimates of
the phases can be calculated by comparing the diffraction of these soaked crystals with
unsoaked crystals of the same protein.
Another method of solving the phase problem is molecular replacement, where the phase
information is derived from the structure factors of a known protein to serve as initial apôÙ
proximations for the phases of a new protein. When viable, this method only requires a
single native data set, saving the crystallographer large amounts of time and protein. The
known protein in this context is called a phasing model and should be highly similar to the
new protein. Traditionally, this has entailed using closely related proteins as the phasing
model [36, 43]. However, an AI system that predicts a proteinãs threeôÙdimensional strucôÙ
ture from its amino acid sequence, Alphafold, has recently become widely available [44].

crystallographers to estimate solutions to the phase problem with molecular replacement
without using a crystal structure from a different protein. As a result, the phasing problem
can be solved rapidly with few resources compared to classical methods. This has been
made especially evident by Aithani et al. [45].

electron density map in accordance with the proteinãs sequence while complying with the
rules of protein structures (backbone angles, bond lengths, rotamers, etc.). However,
the phases have most likely not been fully solved with the first estimated solution, so the
electron density map might be crude. To improve the model, the initial map can be used
to calculate new solutions to the electron densities. When performing this calculation, a
small portion of the initial data set is withheld from the calculations. If the calculated map
is more similar to the withheld data set than the previous map, i.e. if the RôÙfree value is
lower, it is seen as a closer approximation to the real protein structure. This process can
be performed iteratively until an accurate approximation of the electron density map has
been calculated [36, 37, 43].

5.4.1
Lid helices sequester the active sites in a closed conformation
The catalytic site lies inside a hydrophobic trench in both LipCE and ubLipA. At the bottom
of this trench, the primary catalytic residue, Ser208 in LipCE and Ser157 in ubLipA, is
situated, as shown in Figure 5.12. The other residues highlighted in the figure are, based
on studies on an I.3 lipase from Pseudomonas sp. MIS38 (PML), likely involved in the
catalytic activity of these lipases. Their positions in space are fully conserved across all
I.3 lipase structures investigated in this work. However, in the crystal structures obtained,
all the catalytically involved residues are entirely sequestered by closedôÙlid structures that
close up the trenches containing them. As a result, no solvents or ligands have accessed
the active site in any of the crystal structures obtained in this study.
In LipCE, two alpha helices, Lid1 and Lid2 (Figure 5.12a), act as physical barriers to open
and close access to the active site. Based on sequence alignment with PML [16] as
shown in Figure 1.4. Lid1 ranges from Pro147 to Gly168 and covers a large portion of the
active site trench. Lid2 consists of the residues between Gly49 and Ala64 and makes up
a smaller helix, which covers a smaller region of the active site trench opening.
On the other hand, the active site in ubLipA is covered by a single lid with high sequence
homology to Lid1 in LipCE and PML. This lid covers up the active site trench by bending
towards the location of Lid2 in LipCE, effectively covering the same area with what seems
like a simpler mechanism. The lid comprises residues ranging from Gly97 to Pro118,
which form an alpha helix with a highly flexible amino acid chain on either side, indicating
that the loop can be moved.

Discussion
6.1
Possible optimizations to the purification of Family I.3
Lipases
The first work performed in this study was a solubility assay of seven different family I.3
lipases, including LipCE and ubLipA. The assay explored the solubility of each lipase in
buffers containing 50ôÙ500 mM NaCl. Through this assay, we discovered that none of the
lipases showed significant bands in the soluble portion of the cell lysate. These results
indicated that the proteins of interest were likely aggregated in the inclusion bodies. We
utilized dounce homogenization with wash buffers containing EDTA to ensure the proteins
were sufficiently unstable without Ca2+ before solubilization and refolding. This method
for separating, solubilizing, and refolding the lipases worked well on most of the initially
tested lipases. We found that the most significant step to optimize for increased yield is
the refolding step since there were no significant bands containing the protein of interest
in any waste fractions before this step (see Figure 5.1). To optimize the refolding step, a
good place to start is by modifying the contents of the refolding buffer, such as choosing
between LôÙarginine or LôÙglycine (and their concentrations) or adjusting the pH or glycerol
content. Additionally, the speed at which the refolding buffer is added might impact protein
precipitation since the protein is given more time to refold correctly.
After the solubilization and refolding of LipCE and ubLipA, the lipases were already very
pure, as shown in Figure 5.1. Because of the small number of contaminants present at
this stage, SEC became a viable option as a next step. Normally, the proteins would have
been purified by nickel affinity chromatography using the hexahistidine tags present on the
proteins. However, since the affinity chromatography step was replaced by solubilization
and refolding, future expression vectors designed to work with these lipases should ideally
be designed without the NôÙterminal HisôÙtag and TEV cleavage site. This would simplify the
purification process in cases where the HisôÙtag is unwanted. To further reinforce this point,
Figure 5.3 shows that the protein purity dropped after TEVôÙcleavage to remove HisôÙtags.
Additionally, a larger fraction of the proteins aggregated after TEV cleavageëƒ however,
based on the available results, it is difficult to conclude whether this is an artifact of the
TEV cleavage or the absence of the HisôÙtag.
6.2
Urethanases as a means to recycle polyurethane
One way to tackle the prospect of recycling PUR is through the use of biological systems to
break down the plastic at a molecular level. Several studies have found microorganisms
capable of degrading PUR [58ã60]. These microorganisms have shown efficacy in breakôÙ
ing down flexible and rigid foams, adhesives, and coatings made from PUR and using
PUR as a carbon source for microbial growth[60]. The latter could prove immensely useful
to biotechnological industries if implemented properly. However, wellôÙdefined monomeric
products are required to recycle PUR into new, virgin plastics. In turn, the reaction mechaôÙ
nisms responsible for the degradation of PURs must be thoroughly characterized. ThereôÙ
fore, isolating and characterizing enzymes capable of efficiently degrading PUR is crucial
to eventually recycling PUR into virgin plastics.
Enzymatic recycling of PUR involves utilizing enzymes as catalysts to cleave the urethane
bonds present in the link between the hard and soft copolymer blocks of PUR. There are

The most important chemical components to recover from PUR degradation are the amineôÙ

a carbamic acid and a primary alcohol. According to Magnin et al. [61], such a carbamic
acid group will spontaneously degrade under most conditions, resulting in the release of
a CO2 to form an amine. Similarly, when using an amidase to cleave the amide bond
portion of the urethane group will result in an amine and a hydrogen carbonate, which
most studies have found to spontaneously split into a primary alcohol and CO2 [61].
Lipases, such as LipCE and ubLipA, are esterases that catalyze the hydrolysis of ester
bonds in triglycerides into free fatty acids and glycerol. Since lipids are the main substrate
of lipases, they typically have hydrophobic residues surrounding the catalytic site, as is
the case with family I.3 lipases. This hydrophobicity close to the active site may benefit
PUR degradation, as many PURs contain long apolar chains. However, this has yet to be
experimentally explored.

Conclusion
In this work, the family I.3 lipases, LipCE and ubLipA, were successfully purified with
high yields and crystallographyôÙgrade purity. A novel purification protocol was developed
to purify these lipases from E.coli inclusion bodies through solubilization, refolding, and
sizeôÙexclusion chromatography. The refolding step in protein purification was identified as
the major protein loss. Furthermore, this study solved LipCE and ubLipAãs crystal strucôÙ
ture models in closed conformations with resolutions of 1.8 û and 1.9 û, respectively.
Structural alignment of these structure models helped highlight structural differences beôÙ
tween the two enzymes, especially around the catalytic domain, where two lid structures
sequester the active site in closed conformations. These lidsã activation mechanisms apôÙ
pear to depend on more than just Ca2+ presence. Furthermore, six calcium binding sites
were identified in LipCE, and eight sites were found in ubLipA. Four of these sites were
shared between the two enzymes. The CôÙterminal domain of the two proteins is strucôÙ
turally nonôÙconserved. This work also proposed a route toward crystallizing LipCE and
ubLipA in open conformations. The qualitative studies of LipCE performed in this work
showed that the enzyme has high thermal stability, with a melting temperature of 51.77ô¯C,
and performs esterase activity against a polyurethane homolog, ImpranilôÛ. In summary,
this study provides insights into the purification, crystallization, and structures of the putaôÙ
tive urethanases LipCE and ubLipA. Further research is needed to investigate the open
conformations of the proteins, as well as their applicability as polyurethane degraders in
comparison with other currently available urethanases.
