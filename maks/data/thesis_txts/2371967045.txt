1
Introduction
The advancement of renewable power industry and plans to decrease usage of con-
ventional power sources lead to possibility that demand in certain regions can be fully
covered with green energy for long periods. [1] However, 100% renewable grid is vulner-
able to calm cloudy days, when insuﬃcient wind and solar power is generated. In this case
the demand is usually covered from non-renewable sources. When their usage is limited
in the future, the main backup power source will be energy storage. High load regions
would require large backup storage capacity. Not all regions are suitable for building hy-
droelectric storage, so increasing capacity may be problematic without battery technology
breakthrough or signiﬁcant advancement of alternate storing methods.
Balancing 100% hourly demand on a regular days with wind and solar power also
means, that on a windy day the same capacities produce more renewable power, than
can be used immediately. The generation can be curtailed, so the farms would underper-
form during favourable conditions. Alternatively the excess power can be either stored as
backup or transferred to another region to help it balance the local demand.
Exporting excess generated power to high consumption regions would reduce their
backup needs, reliance on conventional power sources and consequently increase share
of renewable sources. Energy exchange is already intensive between neighbouring coun-
tries, especially within Europe. [2] Taking it to a larger scale is the next logical step.
Several concepts of supergrids or global grids are already suggested [3–5], their econom-
ical advantages are discussed.
In this thesis a theoretical pan-Eurasian power network is researched. The grid con-
nects main load centers in Europe and China to areas with high renewable potential in
Arabia, Tibet etc. An outlook into 2030-2050 allows to assume uniﬁed transmission net-
work in each cluster and wider use of renewable power.
Large scale grid encounters a problem of representative nodes. In the simplest case
the power network can be deﬁned as one node per country. This approach is suﬃcient in
Europe due to countries size, but may not perform well for the large countries like China,
Russia or India, where population density (and therefore – demand) and renewable power
potential vary greatly. Additionally daily peak loads are shifted in countries occupying
several time zones. Clustering approach is applied to detect regions with similar charac-
teristics.
Choosing the clusters, that do not necessarily coincide with countries borders, sets an
obstacle on acquiring enough data for simulation. While wind and solar power can be es-
timated from available atmosphere reanalysis data, the demand time series are not always
available countrywide for long periods. Therefore demand time series are approximated

The second part describes the clustering and its parameters. Initially, spatio-temporal

The grid constructed from larger cluster set is used to examine power ﬂows and the way
they are inﬂuenced by power balance. The amount of renewable power in the grid can be
regulated by changing share of renewables globally or locally. For the most calculations
simple case of homogeneous grid is regarded, when renewable share is equal for all nodes.
The smaller grid simulations demonstrate computational heavy optimization.
Data generation and clustering performed in basic Python modules, grid simulation
uses additional Gurobi module. [6] AC/DC power conversion in the grid is not accounted.

2.1
Assumptions and simpliﬁcations
Clustering and power grid calculation is performed for Eurasia. Acquiring real weather
and load data for the large area is not possible, therefore generation and demand time
series are estimated from reanalysis data and country statistics. Several assumptions are
made to perform modelling in a reasonable time:
• Iceland (Europe) is excluded because of isolated location and strong reliance on
geothermal power and hydropower rather than wind and solar energy. Papua New
Guinea (Oceania) is added because of adjacent border with Indonesia.
• Population density is constant
• The climate does not change signiﬁcantly. This allows to assume similar yearly
variations and use 1996-2015 MERRA data instead of near future extrapolation.
• Only onshore area are considered, 19431 MERRA grid cells in total.
• Renewable power is generated by wind and PV farms. Concentrated solar power
and other types of renewables are not considered, but may be assumed as part of
backup.
• Wind farms are installed with equal capacity, PV farms have equal capacity within
the same power mix zone (Section 2.4).

3
Clustering methods
3.1
ST-DBSCAN and clustering parameters
Deﬁning all onshore points (MERRA grid cells centers) as nodes and simulating a
power grid is impossible because of their high number – 19341 in total. Grouping similar
points in clusters helps to reduce amount of potential nodes.
The clustering result should be suitable for transformation into power grid nodes,
which suggests that they should posses a special characteristics:
1. Reasonable number of clusters. While the power grid can be constructed from
any number of clusters, the computational time increases with more elements in the
grid. In this work grids up to 120 nodes/clusters are considered.
2. Low noise. The main aim of constructing power grid from clusters and simulating
it is to analyze how the demand can be covered with renewable energy. Therefore
it is important that high load points are clustered and not discarded as noise.
3. Continuity. Mathematically a cluster, that consists of several separated areas, is
acceptable. However, clustering an area for the power grid implies, that each cluster
has its own transmission network so the power could be transferred within it. This
assumption cannot be done for a cluster, consisting of several disjointed parts. An
exception is a cluster detected on closely located islands: common transmission
grid may be used there.
Deﬁnition 3.1. Cluster is a set of points that are similar to each other more, than to other
point sets.
Deﬁnition 3.2. Continuous cluster is spatially continuous area, that contains points be-
longing only to one cluster. An exception is interruption by the sea, as long as distance
between separated areas is comparable to their size.
Several methods perform clustering on raster data, so the ﬁnal result is essentially
a splitted area, which fulﬁlls requirements 2 and 3 – k-means, spectral clustering etc.
However the algorithms are only applicable to static data. Among existing clustering
methods only a few allow clustering of spatial-temporal data. ST-DBSCAN algorithm,
derived from DBSCAN operates on spatio-temporal data and is able to resolve adjacent
clusters. This makes it suitable for splitting a large dense area into clusters.
Deﬁnition 3.3. Neighbourhood is a distance function, deﬁned as D(r,ρ). [20]
Deﬁnition 3.4. Core point is a point, which neighbourhood r < Rmax, ρ > ρmin contains
at least k points. [20]

ST-DBSCAN algorithm limits point neighbourhood both by distance Rmax and correl-
ation ρmin and attempts to form a cluster if the core point’s neighbourhood contains at
least k points.
Parameters Rmax, ρmin, k are chosen depending on the problem. The recommended
heuristics method is to analyze the distances to k-th nearest neighbours. [21] Point limit
k can be estimated as k = ln(N), where N – total number of data points to be clustered.
For N = 19431 the corresponding minimum number of points in neighbourhood will be
k ≈10.
Now minimum cluster size Rmax, ρmin to contain k neighbours can be determined for
each point of the data set. Since the clustering uses two independent parameters, they
should be regarded in a pair. Total number of neighbourhoods containing at least k =
10 points are calculated depending on parameters Rmax ∈[0,1000]km, ρmin ∈[0,1] and
plotted as 3D surface (Fig. 3.1).
Figure 3.1: Number of neighbourhoods, containing at least 10 points, for Rmax > r, ρmin < ρ
The valley near Rmax = 250km, ρmin = 0.7 indicates that most points found in this
neighbourhood will have at least k = 10 spatio-temporal neighbours. Heuristics analysis
gives similar values for wind and renewable power. PV power time series are well correl-
ated because of diurnal cycles, so the correlation threshold can be relaxed up to ρ = 0.97.
The disadvantage of the original ST-DBSCAN is strong dependence on the order of
points passed to the algorithm (Fig. 3.2). If a potential core point is already in a cluster,
it is not regarded despite possibly having larger neighbourhood and better correlation to
its points. Additionally all points in neighbourhood are assigned to a newly discovered
core point, regardless if they already belong to another cluster. Noise regions are similar
as points with neighbourhoods smaller than k are not clustered.

3.2
Alternate spatio-temporal clustering method
ST-DBSCAN does not perform well on gridded data because of strong dependence
on points order and point reassignment to new clusters. Nevertheless its deﬁnition of
the neighbourhood can be used as a base for another spatio-temporal clustering method.
An algorithm, suggested by Marc Hüsch [22] is inspired by ST-DBSCAN and solves the
problem of clustering spatio-temporal gridded data. The modiﬁcation ensures, that ﬁrst
clusters are detected around core points with largest neighbourhoods and all clusters will
contain best correlated points.

3.3
Hierarchical clustering
Spatio-temporal clustering of residual load data produces many small clusters after
minimum points requirement is omitted. An eﬀective way to reduce number of clusters
is to combine similar ones using agglomerative clustering approach. The main aim is
not only merge clusters based on similarity, but do it so the ﬁnal result is suitable for
conversion to a power grid. This places a restriction on the algorithm: the clusters have to
remain continuous, therefore merging should be allowed only for adjacent clusters.
Deﬁnition 3.6. Adjacent neighbour is a cluster/grid cell, which has a common border
with other cluster/grid cell. Clusters separated by the sea are considered adjacent if the
border of the cluster is found within a certain distance from another cluster center and no
other clusters are present in between.
Comparing correlation of the clusters’ centers only would not be enough to pass as
similarity criteria. The power grid will use summed up data of the cluster to deﬁne a grid
node. Therefore it is important to ensure that total residual loads in clusters are similar
before merging. The sums correlation is used as the main merging criteria. Distance limit
is not added because of adjacency condition already present.
At each step the following actions are performed to generate upper level clusters:
1. Total residual load time series are calculated for each cluster by summing up data
from all cluster points.
2. Adjacent clusters are found.
3. Total residual load correlation is found for all pairs of adjacent neighbours.
4. The pairs are sorted from highest to lowest correlation.
5. Pair with the highest correlation is merged into a new cluster, which is assigned
order number 0.
16
==PAGE:25==
3. CLUSTERING METHODS
6. The next pair in sorted list is checked to have both clusters unmerged. If any of
them is already merged with another one, then the pair is skipped and the next one
in list is checked to satisfy the same condition. Order number remains unchanged.
If both clusters are not yet merged, they form a new one. The order number is
increased by 1 and assigned to this new cluster.
7. Step 6 is repeated until the pairs correlation list ends or the correlation decreases
below a threshold. Since sums correlation is lower than single points, the threshold
is also lowered.
8. If some clusters remain unmerged, they are copied to next level in hierarchy unmod-
iﬁed with order number adjusted accordingly. I.e. if level n+1 contains k clusters,
then level n unmerged cluster is assigned number k +1 at level n+1.
9. If total cluster number is less than a required limit at the end of the step or no
adjacent clusters are valid for merging because of low correlation, then merging is
stopped.
The sums correlation degrades fast with the clusters size, the process may slow down
at the later steps, because only a few clusters would be available for merging. To prevent
this a supplementary condition is added to 9): if total number of clusters at the end of the
step decreased less, than a pre-deﬁned number, then merging should be stopped, as total
number of clusters cannot be reduced signiﬁcantly anymore.
3.4
Preparing clusters for a power grid
A notable diﬀerence of residual load clusters is that they are often non-continuous and
less uniform in urban areas (Fig. 3.3). Points corresponding to higher population than the
surroundings tend to form a cluster despite being separated by the distance. The merging
result needs more corrections, before the clusters can be used as a base for power grid.
Three functions are used to fulﬁll cluster continuity condition.
• Dilution – the function attempts to relocate cluster points, which are badly connec-
ted to their respective clusters. ’Bad connection’ means that the point has less than
a deﬁned number of neighbours. If this number is 1, then the function detects and
tries to relocate only single points, which are either disconnected from the clusters
or represent 1-point micro-cluster.
Since the grid is rectangular, any of 8 points surrounding point in question, can be
considered a neighbour, if they are located onshore. The point with bad connection
to a cluster is relocated to the one where adjacent neighbour with the highest cor-
relation belongs to. If the point is surrounded by the sea, the closest cluster point is

• Reassignment – the function redistributes all points from the cluster which contains
less than a certain number of points. The process is similar to dilution: the point is
reassigned to the cluster where its most correlated adjacent neighbour or the closest
point belongs to. Unlike dilution, the function is applied to each point of a cluster
and always reassigns them to another one.
• Splitting – the function founds clusters that have disconnected areas, which are too
big to apply dilution, and splits them into separate clusters. Some cases do not
require splitting, for example, clusters formed on archipelagoes.
If applied to initial clusters, cleaning functions would disturb clustering result because
of high number of single points and small clusters. Therefore merging is performed ﬁrst,
then clusters are smoothed with functions until the result is suitable to transform into
power grid: no disconnected areas or small clusters are observed. Additionally several
oscillating points (points that switch between the same clusters, if dilution is applied
twice) are reassigned their cluster manually.

It is possible to estimate, how similar are the clusters from diﬀerent years (periods).

is no one ’true’ cluster distribution, that could be used as reference, all possible pairs of
years (periods) are compared. If 50% overlaps between clusters occur for at least 3/4 of

switch the cluster too often and the cluster shape is less dependent on clustered time series
length.

detected from available data. Nevertheless it should be remembered that shape variations
still occur in middle Eurasia, so the 20-year clusters are not the one and only possible

7
Results analysis and discussion
7.1
Overview
The transition from raster data to grid simulation takes several stages:
1. Time series generation, if needed.
The process is straighforward and is not
memory consuming.
The required computational time depends on time series
length. (Section 2)
2. Initial spatio-temporal clusters generation with spatio-temporal algorithm (ST-
DBSCAN or modiﬁcation). Depending on time series length the process may be
computational heavy. If the same time series are used for clustering with diﬀer-
ent parameters, the correlation and distance matrix may be stored for further use,
speeding up consequent cluster analysis. In case of short time series the correlation
can be calculated only for spatial neighbourhood of each point, thus reducing de-
pendence on correlation matrix. This method does not gain computational time for
long series (20 years). (Section 3.2, 4.2, 4.3.1)
3. Cluster merging to reduce total cluster number. The algorithm calculates time
series sums for each cluster and their correlation. As total cluster number always
smaller than number of data points, the correlations are calculated faster, but the
process has to be repeated at each step because the clusters are changing. Depend-
ing on starting cluster count and data similarity, the merging may take time com-
parable to spatio-temporal clusters generation. The process should be limited by
both maximum cluster number and cluster number reduction between two steps to
prevent prolonged merging at later stages, when few pairs of clusters satisfy correl-
ation threshold. Cluster number is not cut exactly by half at each next step. (Section
3.3, 4.3.2)
4. Cluster cleaning, if needed. When time series are not well correlated, small or
single point clusters may be detected. Some of them are not subjected to merging
because of low correlation to surroundings. Their presence increases total num-
ber of clusters, preventing grid simulation in reasonable time. The points from
micro-clusters should be reassigned to bordering bigger clusters based on best cor-
related neighbours, thus eﬀectively reducing total count without altering cluster’s
shapes. The process is semi-automatic: all reassignment functions can be scripted,
but their appliance and detection strength should be input manually depending on
the clusters. (Section 3.4, 4.3.2)
5. Conversion to power grid is performed by assigning each cluster a node. If the
link length is essential to grid simulation or results analysis, then nodes are placed in

years of ideal supergrid, where every pair of nodes is linked, with local export
scheme. (Section 5.2, 6)
7.2
Clustering
The task of deﬁning power grid through clustered data posses a big challenge. It puts
many restrictions on the ﬁnal result: cluster number, size and spatial continuity. Robust
clustering methods, that operate on spatio-temporal data, produce large number of small
clusters.
Combining spatio-temporal and agglomerative algorithms helps to overcome side ef-
fects of initial clustering. Initial spatio-temporal clusters are merged based on adjacency
and correlation between their summed time series. However, agglomerative method does
not give full control over exact number of clusters. While the maximum number can be
limited, cluster merging may stop earlier, if correlation threshold is reached. Additionally
merging can be limited by maximum distance (in this case the centers have to be recal-
culated at each step) or maximum size to prevent appearance of large clusters in the late
stages.
Combined, two algorithms allow clustering of moderately correlated data. In case of
sparse data the ﬁrst algorithm can be replaced by ST-DBSCAN. The resulting clusters do
not necessarily coincide with countries. Several clusters may loosely follow national bor-
ders, but this behavior is observed rarely. For clusters covering parts of several countries,
an assumption is required, that international power exchange is not limited within the
cluster. Considering that clustering is performed for α = 1.0 (⟨G⟩= ⟨L⟩), the simulations
provide a far future outlook, when limitations in power exchange are overcome.
Several stable formations may be detected near notable orographic features: the
clusters are most likely to follow Himalayas range and occupy territory of Taklamakan
desert (Fig. 7.1a). Presence of long high mountain ranges in West China leads to ex-
tremely diﬀerent weather conditions on the plateau and at high altitudes, that are reﬂected

7.3
Power grid simulation
Simulating power grid conﬁrms, that uniting all nodes in transmission network helps
to decrease need in backup power, be it non-renewable energy or power from the storage.
However even unlimited transmission case does not provide one perfect solution. Two
regarded schemes: local and synchronized export, have advantages and disadvantages
(Table 5). Local export scheme has a high negative mismatch in selected nodes. It can
be covered by using conventional power sources – with the large backup needs this may
not comply with aim to decrease usage of non-renewable. Or dispatching power from
hydroelectric storage – this may be problematic in regions experiencing drought. Or
using energy stored in batteries – if existing technology progress enough to fuﬁl backup
needs.
Alternative is to switch to synchronized transmission, which requires smaller backup
power dispatch in each node. However this scheme would demand stronger connections,
than are used in local export scheme. The links should be strengthen by ≈20% for α = 1.0.
The gap between total transmission of both schemes increases with α.
Renewable power time series assume equal installations of wind and PV farms. In real
case the installations may be concentrated close to Chinese load nodes because of favour-
able conditions for renewable generation in West China. Therefore the actual decrease
of backup may be higher, than simulated. If the power cables capacity would increase
faster than energy storage capacity, then synchronized export could become a preferable
transmission scheme.
The constructed power grid always reacts to changing power balance. The highest
amounts of energy are always transferred through the links connected to high load nodes
– main importers of excess power. Changing renewables share α shifts preferable sources
of renewable power, as can be seen from link usage patterns. Intensity of power exchange
depends on energy generation and used transmission scheme. In local export renewable
nodes export energy to load nodes more often than to each other, while synchronized
transmission has less variable link usage.

Small clusters sizes allow to explore two cases. First is a homogeneous one, when
all nodes generate renewable power and have the same share α. Second one is inhomo-
geneous: renewable power generation is cut-oﬀin clusters, where installations of wind
and solar farms may be economically ineﬃcient due to climate. Harsh climate zones are
sparsely populated, therefore nodes located there do not require large amounts of backup
power, if no renewable power is generated locally. The main increase of total backup
comes from high load nodes, as less renewable power is available for import.
The more direct connections between renewable and load nodes a grid has, the more
eﬀectively the power can be transferred. The number of links cannot be increased in-
ﬁnitely due to geographical and economical obstacles. Similarly to transmission scheme
choice, there is no unique conﬁguration, that optimizes all grid properties. The optimiza-
tion can be carried out for a speciﬁc parameters, that are most important at the moment.
Finding optimal grid is a time consuming process, since each tested conﬁguration has
to be simulated for a certain time period to produce representative result. A simple 1-
parameter optimization was performed on a small grid as an example. More complex grid
conﬁguration, that minimizes several parameters (total values or selected nodes), can be
carried out with genetics algorithm, but this goes outside the scope of the thesis.
The power grid simulation is generalized and can be used with grid derived from
clusters, or any other conﬁguration, provided nodes generation and load time series and
links are known. Overall method is ﬂexible and can be used to model a grid, when little
information is available on the nodes and their connections.
