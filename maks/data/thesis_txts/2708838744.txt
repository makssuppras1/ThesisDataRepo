Abstract  
Reducing bycatch in the brown shrimp (Crangon crangon) fishery through anterior 
escape panels  
The brown shrimp (Crangon crangon) beam trawl fishery is considered one of the most 
important fisheries in the North Sea. However, the operation in coastal areas and the 
use of small mesh size raise concerns about the retention of juvenile fish from important 
commercial stocks. Furthermore, the low efficiency of current sorting devices inside the 
trawl for the juvenile fish indicates that preventing their entrance into the trawl could 
be a better strategy. To understand the spatial distribution of the catch when entering 
the anterior part of the trawl, an initial trial was conducted, attaching collection bags to 
the netting distributed around the trawl mouth. The results showed that round fish were 
caught predominantly close to the top panel, behind the bobbin line, and away from the 
vessel. Flatfish were caught exclusively near the bottom panel, while brown shrimp were 
found in all collection bags, although predominantly in collection bags by the bottom 
panel. These results suggested that a large mesh escape panel installed behind the 
bobbin line could reduce bycatch of round fish, but likely at the cost of brown shrimps. 
Therefore, three escape panels were designed and compared in terms of bycatch and 
commercial catch. Unexpectedly, the panels were not able to improve the escape of 
small pelagic species, such as herring (Clupea harengus) and sprat (Sprattus sprattus), 
but the results showed a reduction in catches of round fish, especially for whiting 
(Merlangius merlangus) while maintaining brown shrimp catches in addition to a 
possible reduction of fuel consumption. Moreover, larger individuals of dab (Limanda 
limanda) could escape through the panels. Thus, a panel comprising the whole width of 
the top netting panel from 1.5 m behind the bobbin line until the fishing line may be the 
best alternative to maximize the escape of juvenile fish ( with no loss of brown shrimps 
while exploiting the potential reduction in fuel consumption.

Abstrakte 
Reducering af bifangst i hesterejefiskeriet (Crangon crangon) ved hjælp af anterior 
flugtpaneler 
Fiskeriet rettet mod hesterejer (Crangon crangon) ved brug af bomtrawl anses for at 
være et af de vigtigste fiskerier i Nordsøen. Fiskeriet foregår i kystområder og gør brug 
af små maskestørrelser, hvilket giver anledning til bekymring for bifangst af juvenile fisk 
fra vigtige kommercielle bestande. Lav effektivitet af nuværende sorteringsanordninger, 
som findes i trawlet, indikerer ydermere at det kunne være en bedre strategi at 
forhindre juvenile fisk at komme helt ind i trawlet. Derfor for at forstå den rumlige 
fordeling af fangsten, når den bevæger sig ind i trawlet, blev der udført et indledende 
forsøg, hvor opsamlingsposer blev fastgjort til nettet og fordelt rundt om mundingen af 
trawlet. Resultaterne viste, at rundfisk hovedsagelig blev fanget tæt på toppanelet, bag 
bobbin line og væk fra fartøjet. Fladfisk derimod blev udelukkende fanget nær 
bundpanelet, mens hesterejer blev fundet i alle opsamlingsposer, dog mest i 
opsamlingsposer nær bundpanelet. Disse resultater antydede, at et stormasket 
flugtpanel placeret bag bobbin line kunne reducere bifangsten af rundfisk, dog 
sandsynligvis på bekostning af fangsten af hesterejer. På baggrund af dette blev tre 
flugtpaneler designet og sammenlignet med hensyn til bifangst og kommerciel fangst. 
Uventet var panelerne ikke i stand til at forbedre fangsten af små pelagiske arter, såsom 
sild (Clupea harengus) og brisling (Sprattus sprattus), men resultaterne viste en 
reduktion i fangsten af andre rundfisk som især hvilling (Merlangius merlangus), mens 
fangsten af hesterejer forblev den samme. Følgelig blev en eventuel reduktion af 
brændstofforbrug sporbar. Desuden kunne større individer af ising (Limanda limanda) 
undslippe gennem panelerne. Således kan et panel, der omfatter hele bredden af det 
øverste netpanel fra 1,5 m bag bobbin line indtil fishing line, være det bedste alternativ 
til at minimere fangsten af juvenile fisk uden tab af hesterejer og samtidig udnytte den 
potentielle reduktion i brændstofforbrug.

1 Introduction 
The necessity of a sustainable global development attempting to decrease the fossil fuel 
emission raised worriment in some fisheries, especially for trawlers (UN, 2015; FAO 
2018). Three major concerns have been raised in the recent years regarding the 
sustainability of shrimp fisheries (McHugh et al., 2017). Most of shrimp trawlers are 
dragged to catch the target species, being considered as active fishing gears. The small 
mesh size deployed contributes to increase the drag resistance and therefore the fuel 
consumption and CO2 emissions (Hussein et al., 2021; O'Neill et al., 2018). Parker and 
Tyedmers (2014) found that crustacean-trawl fisheries can use more than 10,000 litters 
of fuel per tonne of landed catch, turning out to be one of the most fuel intensive 
fisheries. 
 
The second main concern raised is the impact in the substrate and in the sessile non-
target organisms caused by the trawl (Eigaard et al., 2017; Hiddink et al., 2019; Rijnsdorp 
et al., 2020). Bottom trawl gears are commonly used to catch shrimps and these gears 
are dragged along the bottom having otter boards, beams, chains and/or ground ropes 
to stimulate the shrimp and fish into the net and keep the gear close to the ground (De 
Borger et al., 2021). Bottom trawls penetrate in the seafloor at least 10 cm but can also 
go up to 35 cm deep, depending on the sediment type and gear specifics (Borger et al., 
2021). Besides the sediment mobilization, significant proportions of benthic fauna are 
removed (O’Neil et al., 2015; Tiano et al., 2020). In the EU, 90% of the fishing effort is 
done in shallow areas with depth ranging from 0 to 200m and particularly in the North 
sea (IV), 63% of the shallow area is trawled at least once a year (Eigaard et al., 2017).   
 
Shrimp trawlers are known to have high unwanted bycatch rates and consequently 
higher discards rates due to the small mesh size used (Kelleher 2005; Gilman et al., 
2020). The global fisheries production between 2010-2014 was on average 84.6 million 
tonnes, being on average 10.8% (9.1 million tonnes) of this amount discarded annually 
(Gilman et al., 2020). Shrimp trawlers accounts for the highest discard rates, having for 
each tonne caught an average discard of 55%, resulting in more than 500 thousand 
tonnes discarded annually. (Gilman et al., 2020). The discards are mostly composed by 
juveniles of important species targeted commercially by other fisheries, and also 
endangered and charismatic species (McHugh et al., 2017). As survivability varies with 
species, size, temperature, how the catch is handled on board etc. the juveniles of 
important commercial stocks as plaice and whiting can be affected due to low survival 
rate (Noak et al., 2020; Berghahn et.al., 1992).  
 
1.1 The brown shrimp fishery in the North Sea 
The brown shrimp (Crangon crangon) (Figure 1) supports an international fleet in the 
southern North Sea where 550 beam-trawlers vessels of six nations (The Netherlands, 
Germany, Denmark, Belgium, France and UK) exploit the stock (Addison et al., 2017). 
Considered socio-economically as one of the most important fisheries in the North Sea, 
this fishery employs more than 1,000 fishermen and thousands of people in the

2011; ICES, 2019). The fishery operates mainly inside territorial waters (12 nautical 
miles) of Belgium, Denmark, Germany, and The Netherlands, but can be extended until 
30 m depth (Figure 2) (Addison et al., 2017).

catch such a small species, small mesh sizes need to be used resulting in high bycatch 
and discard rates (Temming et al., 2017; Ulleweit et al., 2010). Cod (Gadus morhua), 
whiting (Merlangius merlangus), dab (Limanda limanda), plaice (Pleuronectes platessa), 
flounder (Platichtys flesus) and sole (Solea solea) are important commercial species

discard’s levels reported in 90s from the brown shrimp fishery was approximately 2 ,000 
tonnes of cod, 1,500 tonnes of whiting, 12,000 tonnes of plaice, and 600 tonnes of sole.

The annual landings started to be reported in the 50s, but only in the mid 90s begun to 
be considered reliable (Addison et al., 2017). Since then, they were never lower than 
25,000 tonnes and had an increasing tendency until reaching a peak in 2005, when 
38,628 tonnes were landed in total in the North Sea. After few years with landings 
fluctuating around 35,000 tonnes, a decrease started to be seen, reaching in 2017 the 
lowest reported value since 2002 of 22.077 tonnes (ICES, 2019). The Netherlands, 
Germany and Denmark have been responsible for the majority of the annual landings, 
accounting in 2017 for more than 90% (ICES, 2019). The landings are reported in cooked 
weight and referred to the commercial-sized fraction, since the catches are submitted 
to mandatory sieving processes to separate the target specie from the bycatch and 
boiled on board (Tulp et al., 2016; Addison et al., 2017). The catches pass by two sieving 
processes before landed and a last one ashore. Onboard, the unsorted total catch goes 
into a rotating sieve drum (6.5mm bar space; Figure 4A) that separates by size and body 
format the undersized shrimp and bycatch species, that will be discarded, from the 
commercial sized shrimps. The commercial sized shrimps are cooked in the boiling pot 
and transferred to a trembling sieve (Figure 4B) with 6.5 mm bar space to ensure that 
only individuals with carapace width equal to or larger than 6.5 mm (>45 – 50 mm total 
length) are retained (EU Regulation 2406/96). When on land, the last sieving process is 
done with a 6.8 mm bar space sieve to separate the different commercial sizes to be sell

accordingly to their price in auction (Addison et al., 2017). After auction, the shrimps are 
transported to Marroco in Africa, where they are manually peeled and afterwards 
brought back to Europe to supply the marked (Pascoe & Revill, 2004; Aviat et al., 2011).

Despite the importance and bycatch issues, the fishery lacks in management. There are 
no output controls, as minimum conservation reference size (MCRS) and Total Allowable 
Catch (TAC), and few input controls (Steenbergen et al., 2015; Temming et al., 2017; 
Addison et al., 2017). In national level, limiting the number of licenses emitted, 
restricting number of fishing days or defining closed areas to the fishery are practices 
adopted (Addison et al., 2017). Besides, according to the European Union (EU) 
Regulation No 2019/1241 it is mandatory to use sieve nets or selection grids to reduce 
bycatch and codends with a minimum diamond mesh size of 16 mm (Addison et al., 
2017). Sieve nets are cone shaped nets inserted into standard trawls which direct 
unwanted by-catch to an escape hole cut into the body of the trawl, allowing the target 
species, which can pass through the mesh of the sieve net, to be retained in the cod end 
(Revill & Holst, 2004; Addison et al., 2017). 
 
In 2016, the Dutch, German, and Danish producer organizations started the process to 
obtain a certificate of sustainable and well-managed fishery with the Marine 
Stewardship Council (MSC) (Addison et al., 2017). With the rising concern about the 
origin and sustainability of sea food products consumed, the industry would risk losing

2017). One of the basic criteria to obtain this standard, is to have a management system 
ensuring the sustainability of the fishery. In 2017 the fishery was granted with the MSC

stated, besides the beams dimension, gears weight and the mandatory sieve process of 
the catch, that the sieve net maximum opening should be 70mm or the grid should have

flatfish individuals with total length bigger than 10 cm and Vorberg (2015) conclude that 
the sieve net can be considered the best alternative (Polet et al., 2004; Addison et al., 
2017). In the MSC management plan was also agreed that the codend mesh size would 
be gradually increase from 20mm to 26mm over the period 2016 to 2020, since during 
the certification process was noticed that the 22mm mesh size preferably used by the 
fishermen was retaining considerable fractions of non-commercial sized shrimps 
(Addison et al., 2017; Veiga-Malta et al., 2020). While the MSC evaluation was being 
developed, Santos et al., (2018) concluded that a cod end with 26mm mesh size would, 
on the contrary, result in considerable losses of shrimps with commercial size. This 
impasse created resistance among the fisherman to increase the mesh size to 26 mm, 
because there was a risk of losing shrimps, motivating the search for new alternatives. 
In 2021, the brown shrimp fisheries was also allowed to discard 6% of the total annual 
catches of all species under catch limits (e.g. TAC), due to the de minimis exemption 
granted for this fishery (EU Regulation 2018/2035). 
 
1.2 Brown shrimp biology and behaviour in response to trawls 
The brown shrimp is distributed from Iceland in the North Atlantic to the North Sea and 
Baltic Sea, as well as the Black Sea and Mediterranean. It is found in shallow waters close 
to the coast down to 40 m depth, in soft and sandy substrates. The brown shrimp 
tolerates a wide range of salinity, from estuarine waters to up to 30psu, and 
temperature (0-30°C) (Addison et al., 2017). Tielmann et al. (2015) concluded that this 
species benefits from the tidal stream transport to migrate, providing larvae recruitment 
to all areas. Therefore, the whole North Sea population can be considered as a single 
stock. Brown shrimp is a short-lived species (1.5-2 years) with average and maximum 
total length of 79mm and 109 mm, respectively.  
 
The female individuals can be seen carrying eggs during the whole year, but the highest 
egg production is commonly seen in winter and summer (Addison et al., 2017). The 
recruitment peak that occurs during winter is followed by smaller recruitment waves 
from spring and summer spawning (Temming et al., 2017). The juveniles, migrate to 
shallow waters, where the higher temperatures, shelter, and food availability favour 
high growth rates. As the shrimps grow, they start to migrate to sub-littoral areas until 
reaching deeper regions when adults (Addison et al., 2017). Due to warmer 
temperatures, the cohort from spring and summer have a greater number of eggs and 
faster development, and together with the winter cohort form a major wave of adult 
==PAGE:14==
6 
 
shrimp resulting in a peak of biomass in August and landings in September-October in 
the same year (Temming et al., 2017). After late summer and early autumn, few shrimps 
older than one year are left. Thus, the winter and summer egg production form the basis 
for the fishery in the autumn of the same year, as well as the following winter and spring 
(Temming et al., 2017; Addison et al., 2017).  
 
Although it cannot be considered as a key low trophic level species, the brown shrimp 
plays an important role as prey species for several predators (Addison et al., 2017). 
Besides small fish, e.g. goby (Pomatichistus microps), common seasnail (Liparis liparis), 
hook-nosed bullhead (Agonus cataphractus), birds and the shore crab (Carcinus 
maenas), important commercial species as dab, whiting and cod prey upon the brown 
shrimp (Addison et al., 2017; Gunther et al., 2021). Most of the shrimp preyed are small 
(from 10 to 30mm length), being cod the only species that consumes individuals larger 
than 50 mm (Jansen, 2002). Although the fishery targets individuals bigger than 50 mm, 
suggesting little competition with the natural predation, significative catches and 
discards of undersized shrimp were registered (Jeffery & Revill, 2002; Lancaster et al., 
2002). Lancaster et al. (2002) investigated the brown shrimp survivability rates when 
discarded and found that more than 90% of the shrimps were alive after 24 hours, even 
the undersized ones.  Furthermore, recent studies concluded that natural (M) and 
fishing (F) mortality rate reversed over the last decade, going from a scenario where M 
was assumed to be higher than F to a scenario where F is twice as high as M, as a 
consequence of the decrease in predators’ abundance and continue exploitation of the 
stock (Temming & Hufnagl, 2015). 
 
The brown shrimp typically lives on or buried in soft sediments, having nocturnal feeding 
habits, and remaining buried during the day (Evans et al., 2007).  The species response 
to visual or vibrational stimulus is to perform a single or a sequence of maximum five 
tail-flips, which comprise an abdominal flexion and extension that propels the individual 
vertically (Arnott et al., 1998; Jeffery & Revill, 2002). The jump hight and number of tail-
flips done is directly related to the total length, being the larger shrimps capable of 
jumping higher and do more tail-flips mechanism to ensure escape (Arnott et al., 1998). 
Jeffery and Revill (2002) showed that in lower temperatures (<6°C) a brown shrimp with 
total length bigger than 7 cm has the vertical moviment significantly inhibited when 
compared to in higher temperatures (>10°C), decreasing 30% (from 11.5cm to 8 cm) and 
being caught predominantly in closer proximity to the bottom netting. Moveover, in 
high light conditions the shrimps first try to swim in front of the gear and only when 
there is no other option, they move vertically (Jeffery & Revill, 2002). On the contrary, 
when the light condition is reduced (e.g. night-time or sediment suspension) they 
perform the tail-flip mechanism much sooner having more chances to escape away from 
the trawl. Evans et al., (2007) observed in laboratorial experiments that brown shrimp 
have a weak tendency towards active shoaling behaviour. Contrary than in the field 
experiment that shrimps were found in clumps and size segregated, suggesting 
microhabitats preferences and limitations to environmental gradients (e.g. salinity, 
pressure, temperature etc) (Tielmann et al., 2015).

1.3 Fish behaviour in response to trawls 
For round fish, the most common behaviour observed when reaching the trawl mouth 
is to turn around to be oriented in the towing direction and keep certain distance from 
the trawl, also called as “herding”. They also tend to stay in close proximity to the 
bottom or shoaling when in good light conditions (Queirolo et al., 2012; Glass & Wardle, 
1989). When the decision of cease swimming is made and depending on the species, 
size, and level of metabolic exhaustion, the fish can rise upwards, be taken under the 
footrope or swim directly into the trawl (Ryer 2008; Winger et al., 2010; Queirolo et al., 
2012; Krag et al., 2015). Round fish also strongly rely on visual sense to react in an 
ordered manner and in low light intensities, the ordered patterns behaviour stops 
leading to little evidence of reaction (Glass & Wardle, 1989; Winger et al., 2010). 
Depending on the species, size, and level of metabolic exhaustion, the fish can rise 
upwards, be taken under the footrope or swim directly into the trawl (Ryer, 2008; 
Winger et al., 2010; Queirolo et al., 2012).  
 
On the other hand, flatfish is specialized in camouflage and shown a more passive 
behaviour, remaining buried or leaded in the sediment as an attempt to disguise, until 
directly or very close contact with the gear components (Ryer, 2008; Ryer et al., 2010; 
Winger et al., 2010; Queirolo et al., 2012;). Once they react, they try to swim 
perpendicular from the stimulus, keeping a constant distance from the threat or 
bursting to gain distance and time to camouflage in the substrate. This movement 
eventually concentrate the flatfish in trawl path (Ryer, 2008; Winger et al., 2010). When 
fatigued, flatfish commonly rise, close to the ground, and fall back into the net or keep 
swimming above the ground allowing the ground gear to pass beneath (Bublitz, 1996; 
Ryer & Barnett, 2006). In dark or low visibility conditions, flatfish tend to either hop off 
the bottom, as startle response, or swim upwards and away from the bobbin line 
disturbance (Eaton and Hackett, 1984). Furthermore, due to bigger sizes, the larger 
individuals have better swim capacity having more chances to contact the panel and 
escape (Videler & Wardle, 1991).  
 
1.4 Bycatch reduction devices 
The research done to mitigate bycatch is mainly based on improving size and species 
selectivity (McHugh et al., 2017). The types of mitigation methods can be divided in two 
main groups according to their aim: avoid the catch of unwanted individuals (anterior 
modifications) or select them after entering the trawl (posterior modifications).  
 
Anterior gear modifications may decrease the herding process efficiency, redirect fish 
out of the trawl path or facilitate the escapement in the trawl mouth region (McHugh et 
al., 2017; Melli, 2019). Fish separation is preferable in the initial phase of interaction 
between fish and the trawl as can increase survival probability (Suuronen, 2005; Ryer, 
2008; Graham, 2010). Studies have shown that modifications in the trawl mouth can 
significantly reduce by-catch (Hannah & Jones, 2000; Beutel et al., 2008). Krag et al. 
==PAGE:16==
8 
 
(2015) observed significant reduction in whiting and cod catches in the Nephrops fishery 
(Nephrops norvegicus) with a low headline (~2 m) topless trawl, in which the bobbin line 
was in front of the headline.  
 
McHugh et al. (2015) tested three different simple anterior fish excluder' (SAFE) devices 
in the Metapenaeus macleaya fishery. The first SAFE device tested was a single wire 
stretched between the beam shoes, hanging in front of the beam, and the other two 
devices had the same wire, but flat plastic strips with 0.06 or 0.2 m wide were attached 
to it. All three SAFEs showed significant reduction in bycatch species with no reduction 
of the target species and among the three SAFEs tested, the larger plastic strip (0.2m) 
showed better reductions in bycatch numbers and weight. Farias et al. (2019) proposed 
an anterior modification for shrimps fyke nets, installing a horizontal panel in the inner 
portion of the wings until the upper portion of the first funnel ring and substituted the 
regular top netting panel with a fan panel made of ropes. The modified gear tested could 
reduce by more than 65% the bycatch.  
 
Posterior gear modifications are the most studied and capable of separate and/or select 
the unwanted catches by their morphological differences, behaviour in respect to the 
vertical distribution and swim ability. Modifications in the trawl body mesh, as 
increasing the size or changing the format (i.e. from a diamond to square mesh) are 
commonly and successfully applied (Polet, 2000). Grids are mainly applied in crustacean 
fisheries, selecting individuals that can pass through the bars and diverting the larger 
ones to an escape window or specific compartment with bigger mesh size in the end of 
the trawl (codend) (Veiga-Malta et al., 2020; Silva et al., 2021; Polet, 2002). The sieve 
net, which is attached inside the trawl, directs the unwanted individuals larger than the 
mesh size to an escape window, while the target species that can pass through the sieve 
net, is retained in the codend (Polet et al., 2004; Revill & Holst, 2004). Codend 
modifications are the most extensively studied regarding selecting species out (McHugh 
et al., 2017). Besides the twine material and thickness that direct influence in the 
starching capacity, the mesh size and format can be increased or changed, respectively 
(Revill & Holst, 2004; Santos et al., 2018; Günther et al., 2021) 
 
Vieira et al. (2017) tested a kite escape device (KED), consisted of a combination of 
fisheye (elliptical opening in the codend that allows the escape of flatfish) and escape 
window (window with larger square meshes), installed in the upper cod end panel. The 
use of the KED improved the bycatch reduction with no loss of shrimps. Silva et al.(2012) 
tested the Nordmøre-grid bar for the Atlantic sea bob shrimp (Xiphopenaeus kroyeri) 
obtaining excellent bycatch reduction according to the bar space tested. The Nordmøre-
grids with 24 mm, 20 mm and 17 mm bar spaces could reduce the total weight of 
unwanted brachyurids by 75, 95 and 97%, respectively, without affecting the target 
species catch.  
 
Specific for the brown shrimp fishery, mainly posterior gear modifications were 
investigated. Boddeke (1965) tested a beam trawl with horizontal separator panels to 
==PAGE:17==
9 
 
separate brown shrimp from flatfish, as the brown shrimp would propel themselves into 
the top section of the trawl and be retained in an upper cod end and the flatfish retained 
in the lower compartment. Brabant (1973) also tested a separator panel but obtained 
minimal reduction in bycatch and problems with clogging. Berghan (1992) examined a 
separator panels and rollers instead of chains in ground gear of beam trawls having 42-
100% of bycatch reduction on cost of 2-20% of shrimp reduction due to weed clogging. 
In more recent studies, Polet (2000) tested the selectivity of a trawl with nominal mesh 
sizes of 28 mm in the front part decreasing to 22 mm in the aft part combined with a 
cod end with 22 mm mesh size. He found that the brown shrimp escape in the trawl-
body, especially in the lateral part of the trawl belly was higher than in the codend and 
selection for plaice was not significant in the regions analysed. In 2002, Polet tested 
again the same gear from Polet (2000) with the addition of a grid with bar spacing of 
14mm. Despite that 15% of commercial-sized brown shrimp was lost, a reduction of 70% 
and 65% in fish and benthos was observed. Furthermore, a cleaner catch was registered, 
requiring less sorting, and increasing selectivity in the codend. 
 
Polet et al. (2004) evaluate the use of sieve net in the brown shrimp fishery, concluding 
that the selectivity is very poor for commercial fish species as dab and plaice, withing 
and cod with length below 10 cm. However, the selectivity of the sieve net increases 
with the fish length, reaching 100% reduction in lengths around 20 cm. Santos et al. 
(2018) investigated different combinations of codend mesh size and shape in a baseline 
trawl-body with 30 mm mesh size in the front, decreasing to 20 mm in the rear with 
sieve net of 60 mm mesh size. By using either codends with ~29 mm diamond-mesh size, 
square-mesh codends with ~25 mm mesh size, or T90 codends with ~27 mm mesh size, 
is possible to reduce by half the bycatch rate at the cost of 30% of the commercial 
catches.  
 
The only anterior gear modification tested for the brown shrimp fishery until the present 
moment is the pulse trawl, that substituted the stimuli created by the bobbin line per 
electric pulses inducing brown shrimp to jump doing trail-flip mechanisms (Verschueren 
et al., 2019). Despite of the bycatch reduction of up to 76% with no loss of commercial 
shrimp, spinal injuries in Atlantic cod (Gadus morhua L.) after exposure were observed, 
being currently prohibited this gear modification in the North Sea (Verschueren et al., 
2019).  
 
2.0 Aims 
The first aim of this thesis is to understand the spatial distribution of the species in the 
catch while these are entering the trawl and identify areas with higher bycatch 
concentrations when compared with brown shrimp. 
 
Polet (2002) successfully examined in the brown shrimp fishery the selectivity of the 
target species and few flatfish species in different areas of the beam-trawl body, as well 
as in the codend. For the shrimps, the trawl body, mainly the rounded lateral area, 
==PAGE:18==
10 
 
allowed higher escapement than the codend. For plaice, the selection was poor in either 
both trawl body and codend regions. Jeffery and Revill (2002) investigated the vertical 
distribution of brown shrimp in different temperatures but did not look into bycatch 
species. Larsen et al., (2021) investigated the vertical distribution of deepwater shrimp 
(Pandalus borealis) and dominant bycatch species at the point where the Nordmore grid 
section is installed. The deepwater shrimp was mainly caught in the lower part of the 
trawl belly, cod (Gadus morhua) and American plaice (Hippoglossoides platessoides) 
preferred the mid-section and Haddock (Melanogrammus aeglefinus) principally in the 
upper section of the trawl belly. Therefore, the lack of research until the moment to 
specifically investigate the spatial distribution of bycatch species while entering the 
beam trawl in the brown shrimp fishery and the distribution patterns founded by others 
encouraged the first aim of this project. 
 
With information gained from trial I and knowing that modifications in the anterior part 
of the trawl can significantly reduce the bycatch and previously determine which 
organisms enter the codend likely increasing survivability after escaping (McHugh et al., 
2017), the second aim was to design anterior gear modifications that could reduce the 
bycatch of undersized fish that were not excluded by the sieve net.  
 
Information gathered with fishermen and also observed during previous sea trials that 
small pelagic species such as herring and sprat attempt to scape throughout the top 
panel remaining trapped when the gear is retrieved back to the boat, suggested that 
some of the bycatch species tries to scape throughout the top panel. Moreover, the 
codend is a region that stays frequently clogged because of the small mesh size and 
occurrence of hydroids and benthos, decreasing the escape chances gradually from the 
anterior part to the posterior part of the net (Polet, 2000). Therefore, developing panels 
with bigger mesh sizes in the top netting panel would allow fish with higher swim ability 
that tries to getaway swimming upwards to encounter an escape area and exit the trawl 
before reaching the codend.  
 
3. Materials and Methods 
To accomplish the aims, this study was divided into two trials. Trial I was conducted to 
understand the spatial distribution of the species in the catch while these are entering 
the gear. In the second trial (Trial II), three experiments were developed to test three 
distinct anterior escape panel designs based on the information acquired from Trial I.

3.2.3.3 Identifying similarities in the bags catch composition 
Nonmetric multidimensional scaling (nMDS) is known for identifying clusters of highly 
interconnected nodes from high dimensional connectivity data (Ben-Naim et al., 2004). 
Thus, this analysis was chosen to describe the similarities among the species 
composition of the nine collection bags. Two datasets were analysed: the first 
considering the total number of fish, by species in each collection bag of all hauls 
performed; and a second one containing the total number of fish, by species, summed 
over the hauls in each collection bag. For the second dataset, with the pooled data, one 
of the hauls were excluded because one of the samples was lost.  
 
As described in Zuur et.al (2008), nMDS analysis produce ordinations of objects from 
any distance matrix, which is made of distance estimations that aims to quantify the 
==PAGE:27==
19 
 
difference between samples. The goal of nMDS is to graphically represent, as closely as 
possible, the calculated distance matrix in the fewest number of dimensions. It is a rank-
based approach, meaning that the differences in the distance matrix are substituted by 
ranks, or orders, based on their dissimilarities (Zuur et.al, 2008). In this manner, raking 
the distances basing on their dissimilarities, also indicates which samples are considered 
more similar. For the present analysis, the distance matrix was generated using the Bray-
Curtis dissimilarity (Bray & Curtis, 1957). Despite of being one of the most widely used 
abundance-based measure, the Bray-Curtis dissimilarity shows strong relationship to 
ecological distance (Clark, 1993). It implies the relativization of species-wise differences 
by the total abundance of species, reflecting the proportion of the total species 
abundances (Ricotta & Podani, 2017).  
 
Thus, the nMDS analysis returns a graphical visualization of the similarities and 
dissimilarities of the samples in which, for our case, the horizontal axis x represents the 
real Bray-Curtis distances between the samples and the vertical axis y the distances 
obtained by the two nMDS configurations. Therefore, the axis does not suggest any 
biological meaning. nMDS calculations do not maximize the variability associated with 
the individuals’ axes of the ordination and by having arbitrary axes, the plots can be 
rotated, inverted, or centered not affecting the interpretation (Legendre & Legendre, 
1998). In an nMDS ordination diagram the similar objects are plotted closer and the 
dissimilar objects far apart, according to their ranking.  
 
Kruskal & Wish (1978) drew the attention to the fact that the determination of the 
number of dimensions of an nMDS ordination is not only a statistical question but also 
should consider the interpretability of each one of the axes and the solution stability 
(Legendre & Legendre, 1998). Since nMDS is not an eigenvalue method, the STRESS 
measurement is used to evaluate how good the configuration with m dimensions (plot 
axes) is as demonstrated by Legendre & Legendre (1998). A rule of thumb to evaluate 
the fitness is (Zuur et.al, 2008):  
 
• 
STRESS < 0.05: Excellent configuration allowing detailed inspection 
• 
0.05 < STRESS < 0.1: Good configuration; No need to increase number of 
dimensions (m) 
• 
0.1 < STRESS < 0.2: Be careful with interpretation 
• 
0.2 < STRESS < 0.3: Problems start, especially in the upper range part of the 
interval 
• 
STRESS > 0.3: Poor representation and increase of dimensions (m) should be 
considered

The catch ratio values represent the relative catch efficiency of the experimental gear 
compared to the baseline gear and can be equal to or higher than 0. 𝐶𝑅(𝑙, 𝐯) value equal 
to 1.0 indicates that the catch efficiency for both gears at length l are the same, while 
𝐶𝑅(𝑙, 𝐯) lower or higher than 1.0 means that the experimental gear is catching 
respectively less or more at length l for a given species than the baseline gear (Veiga-
Malta et al., 2018). For example, a catch ratio value equal to 0.75 means that the 
experimental gear catches 25% less fish with length l than the baseline gear, and a value 
equal to 1.75 indicates that the experimental gear catches 75% more fish with length l 
than the baseline gear. 
 
3.3.4.2.1 Estimation of confidence intervals 
A double bootstrapping method with 1000 repetitions was used to calculate the Efron 
95% (Efron, 1982) confidence limits (CIs) for the catch comparison (CC) and catch ratio 
(CR) curves (Herrmann et al., 2017). This methodology account for uncertainties due to 
within- and between-haul variation in the catching process (Herrmann et al., 2017). 
Having lower or upper CI above or below the values of equal performance of the gears 
demonstrate that there is a significant difference between the baseline and 
experimental gears’ selectivity performance. The reference values of equal performance 
for CC and CR curves are 0.5 and 1.0, respectively. 
 
3.3.4.2.2 Model goodness fit evaluation 
SELNET statistical software (Herrmann et al., 2012) allows for the use of distinct methods 
to evaluate the fitness of the model. The model´s ability to describe the experimental 
data was assessed through the p-value, residuals inspection and comparison between 
deviance and degrees of freedom (DOF). The p-value quantifies the probability of 
obtaining by coincidence at least as big a discrepancy between the experimental data 
==PAGE:36==
28 
 
and the fitted curve (Veiga-Malta et al., 2020). The p-value is calculated based on the 
deviance and degrees of freedom (DOF) and for a model to be considered as having a 
good fit, the p-value should be higher than 0.05. Deviance and DOF should have same 
orders of magnitude, otherwise the model has a poor fit and is not being able to describe 
appropriately the experimental data (Wileman et al., 1996: Melli et al., 2018). 
 
The next step to evaluate the adequacy of the model to describe the experimental data 
is looking at the residuals. By doing a scatterplot of the residuals in the ordinate (y axis) 
against the fitted values in the abscissa (x axis) no correlation or pattern distribution 
should be observed in order to accept the model as having good fitness. Any kind of 
dependency or pattern noticed in the plot suggest that the chosen model does not give 
a good representation of the experimental data and a different model should be chosen 
(Montgomery, 2012). 
 
In cases where the fit statistic is poor, but the residuals analysis does not display any 
pattern, the model can still be considered to represent well the experimental data and 
the poor fit statistic are attributed to overdispersion in the data (Wileman et al., 1996). 
 
3.3.4.3 Checking for difference in weight and number of individuals  
To check for significant differences in catch weight and number of individuals per species 
caught in each codend, a method based on testing whether the population means of 
two groups can be considered equal or not was chosen. Moreover, to decide if the 
analysis could proceed using a parametric method, which consider the normally of the 
data as one of the basic assumptions, the Shapiro-Wilk test was applied. A dataset 
containing the total number of individuals per hauls of each species from the 
experimental and baseline gears was tested.  
 
The t-statistic for the Shapiro-Wilk test is obtained by dividing the square of an 
appropriate linear combination of the sample order statistics by the usual symmetric 
estimate of variance, as described in (Shapiro & Wilk, 1965). The null hypothesis (𝐻𝑜) 
considers that the population is normally distributed and by having a p-value lower than 
the alpha level, also called significance value, the null hypothesis should be rejected, and 
it can be concluded that the population does not have a normal distribution. On the 
other hand, a p-value above the alpha level indicates that the population has a normal 
distribution. After performing the Shapiro-Wilk test per species, it was concluded that 
only the Gobius spp. sampled population for both trawl sides could be considered as 
normally distributed. Thus, it was decided that a non-parametric test should be used for 
the next analysis (Ostertagova et al., 2014).  
 
To investigate significant differences in weight and number of individuals per species 
caught in each codend the Kruskal-Wallis test (Kruskal and Wallis, 1952), described 
previously in section 2.2.3.1, was performed. Four datasets were tested, the first two 
containing for each experiment and species the total number of individuals and the total 
==PAGE:37==
29 
 
weight per haul from the experimental and baseline gears, being tested the same 
species considered for the catch-comparison analysis. The last two, the total number of 
individuals and the total weight of all fish species found during Trial II were grouped into 
four main groups according to the species spatial distribution in the pooled nMDS 
analysis from Trial I. The brown shrimp were kept as separated groups since is the target 
species of the fishery.

5. Discussion 
 
The hierarchical cluster and ANOSIN analysis support the results from the exploratory 
analysis using the average weight by group that there is a clear difference in the species 
spatial distribution while entering the trawl.  
 
Pelagic species were found in all samples from TB bags and in some hauls the catch of 
shrimp, swimming crab and gobius made the catch composition be more alike of the B 
bags. Demonstrating variability in the catch composition for this area and rising concern 
of losing some shrimp if they have the possibility to escape. The higher catches of pelagic 
species in the TB bags than in the TF bags indicate that for beam trawls the escape 
response is mainly driven by the bobbin-line. These groups stay in close proximity to the 
bottom when being herding and swim upwards is a behaviour commonly adopted as an 
escape attempt (Queirolo et al., 2012; Glass & Wardle, 1989), but since the headline is 
ahead of the bobbin line, they were caught by the TB bags. Krag et al. (2015) achieved 
significant reductions for cod and whiting when testing a low headline (~ 2m) topless 
trawl that allowed the escape when swimming upwards. 
 
==PAGE:61==
53 
 
The higher catches in the B bags than in the TB bags suggest that the shrimp vertical 
movement may have been inhibited due to low temperature (~7°C during sea trial I. 
Source: SeaTemperatures.net) or that the majority of the individuals caught moved 
vertically enough only to escape the approaching bobbin line, instead of performing 
repeated tail-flips mechanisms, as also reported by Jeffery & Revil (2002). The few 
individuals caught by the TF bags in the top netting panel could be explained by an early 
attempt to escape caused by visual detection of the beam trawl, that is ahead of the 
bobbin line (Berghahn et al 1995). Another possibility is that as consequence of a beam 
trawl configuration, the TF1 and TF3 bags located in the lateral area of the trawl mouth 
were also partially positioned on top of the bobbin line and the jumping behaviour done 
by the shrimps close to the beam shoe to avoid the ground gear may have led them into 
these bags.  
 
Previous studies reported that flatfish rely mostly in mimitization, starting escaping 
response only when in close contact to the ground gear and when fatigated rise in the 
water column or fall back into the net (Bublitz, 1996; Ryer & Barnett, 2006 Ryer et al., 
2010). This behaviour is coherent with the higher catches found in the bags located in 
the bottom netting panel (B bags) and also explain the catches in TB bags. Different than 
expected, that the flatfish species would move away from the gear or vessel stimuli (Ryer 
et al., 2010; Winger et al., 2010), the higher catches in the B bags were in B7, the one 
closer to the vessel. Previous studies observed that flatfish herding disappears under 
low light conditions, as they tend to rise off the bottom in response to the disturbance 
(Ryer & Barnett, 2006; Ryer et al., 2010). Additionally, as low light intensity is highly 
corelated with water turbidity and vision plays an important hole in the behavioural 
response to trawl gear (Glass & Wardle, 1989; Ryer & Barnett, 2006; Addison et al., 
2001), the sense of direction may have been affected. Furthermore, turbulence created 
by the drag resistance in the trawl mouth may have impaired swimming capacity, 
considering that most of the flatfish had small sizes and limited swimming capacity 
(Videler & Wardle, 1991; Queirolo et al.,2012). The higher catchers in the central TB bag 
are a consequence of accumulation in the center of the trawl path, as the flatfish tend 
to swim perpendicularly to the bobbin line slipping along it and accumulating in the 
center of the trawl path until falls into the net (Ryer 2008; Winger et al., 2010; Queirolo 
et al 2012).  
 
Queirolo et al. (2012) noted that small bentho-pelagic and demersal fishes, which 
represent most of species from the others benthonic group analyzed, had an active 
behavioural response of swimming ahead of the bobbin line, but not enough to avoid it 
due to their limited swimming abilities. This behaviour may have also happened during 
trial I, resulting in higher catches of the other benthonic group in the B bags. The clear 
pattern for this group of being caught mainly in the lateral bags, can be due to the conical 
format of the net, called tapering effect. As the trawl cross section decreases along the 
mouth and body, the individuals that enters in the trawl closer to netting sides could 
have been concentrated from a wider area of the trawl mouth.  
 
==PAGE:62==
54 
 
Higher catches of swimming crab in the B bags may be due to their reduced mobility and 
swimming capacity only for short periods as previous study reported (Hartnoll, 1971). 
Furthermore, this capture pattern indicate that the bobbin line is the main trigger for a 
reaction since swimming occur normally for predation or as escape reaction. As the 
swimming capacity is also directly related to their size, bigger swimming crabs could 
have possibly reached the TB bags as well as had hydrodynamic help to reach the top 
netting panel behind the bobbin line (Hartnoll, 1971).  
 
Distinct from previous studies that show diel effect in catches of brown shrimp 
(Berghahn et al 1995; Addison et al., 2001), flatfish (Ryer et al., 2010), herring (Adlerstein 
& Ehrich, 2002), whiting and cod (Krag et al., 2010 and 2015; Melli et al., 2018), no clear 
or evident diel patter were found in the catch vertical distribution during trial I.  
 
By analyzing the results from trial I, it was clear that modifications done in the bottom 
netting panel would imply a loss of shrimps. Thus, modifications in this are were 
excluded from the possible designs that could reduce bycatch since it could be 
unacceptable for the fisherman to loose shrimps. The area considered as most 
interesting for a panel was the top netting behind the bobbin line, where most of the 
pelagic species tried to escape despite the risk of losing some shrimp. Hence, a panel 
located behind the bobbin line could increase the scaping chances for the pelagic 
species, endorsing panel 2 was design. The pelagic species also attempted to escape 
through the top netting ahead of the bobbin line and had a trend of being caught mainly 
in the bags further away from the vessel (TF3 and TB6) possibly as an effort to move 
away from the stimuli created (i.e., noise, turbulence). This trend suggests that perhaps 
a panel only in the outer part of the trawl may reduce pelagic species while minimizing 
shrimp losses, supporting panel 1 and 3 design. As almost no shrimp was caught by the 
TF bags in the top netting ahead of the bobbin line, extending panel 1 throughout the 
whole top front of the trawl until the headline may increase the escape of pelagic species 
with few further losses of shrimps, supporting panel 3 design.  
 
Some of the expectations created for trial II were not met when testing the panels 
performance. Although possible loss of commercial shrimp could have happened, it was 
not observed in any of the panels tested. The average sea surface temperature during 
trial II were approximately 15°C, 8°C higher than in trial I (SeaTemperatures.net). 
Contrary from what was found by Jeffery & Revill (2002), that in warmer temperatures 
the average vertical movement of the shrimps is higher being caught mainly in the upper 
compartment of the codend (~40 cm off the bed), the higher temperatures encountered 
during trial II do not seem to have contributed to their escapement. The no reduction in 
shrimp catches may be explained by the fact that the bags were collecting everything 
within 10 cm from the top netting panel but to be able to escape the shrimps needs to 
contact the panel. Higher catches of undersized shrimp were registered in panel 1 and 
as its format is integrated in the design of the other two panels tested, there is no clear 
explanation for the increase in catches of undersized shrimp only in this experiment. 
However, besides having higher shrimp catches, the population structure caught during 
==PAGE:63==
55 
 
experiment I had also higher numbers of undersized individuals. Thus, if bigger 
aggregations of shrimps containing small individuals had been encountered while 
testing the two other panels, this effect could also have happened.  
 
The escape of pelagic species was not significantly affected by the panels, except from 
whiting. In general, low number of individuals belonging to the pelagic species were 
registered in trial II. As round fish enters into the trawl often in mass and more as a 
behavioural decision of the school to voluntarily go into the gear rather than a 
consequence of fatigue, the panels could have given better results if larger shoals of 
pelagic species had been found (Wardle, 1993; Ryer, 2008). Contradicting the normal 
behaviour of moving away from the stimuli, whiting with total length ranging from 8.5 
cm to 13.5 cm were reduced only when testing panel 2, suggesting that this species 
doesn’t escape away from vessel as previous studies reported (Glass & Wardle, 1989; 
Queirolo et al., 2012). Disorientation due to low light conditions and/or high turbidity 
may have interfered with the escape success, as the vision sense enables ordered 
reaction (Glass & Wardle, 1989). 
 
The use of sieve net, which is capable of exclude fish with total length bigger than 10 
cm, is mandatory in this fishery, so no expectation was made regarding improving 
escape of larger individuals. However, large individuals of dabs were encountered during 
trial II and all panels showed a significant reduction for them. Besides the rising 
behaviour when in low visibility conditions, the flatfish with bigger sizes also have higher 
swimming capacity and more chances to contact the panel to escape (Eaton and 
Hackett, 1984; Videler & Wardle, 1991). In trial I, most large dabs were encountered in 
the B bags, making this positive result from trial II a surprise. This significant reduction 
also demonstrates that the panels could be used combined with the sieve net as boost. 
The significant results from the Kruskal Wallis analysis for this species when using weight 
can be explained by the presence of few individuals with bigger sizes in the baseline 
gear, contributing more substantially to a difference in total weight than in numbers. 
 
Regarding the statistical analysis, the catch comparison analysis for herring in the third 
experiment and for plaice in all experiments demonstrate how a restrict length class 
range can influence in the analysis and results interpretation. The low variability in 
length sizes, limit the length range making it difficult to conduct catch comparison 
studies. One way to improve in the future could be to round down to half centimeter 
increasing the number of length classes available when fitting a length-based model. 
 
During the sea trial the fisherman noticed a potential reduction in drag with increase of 
the panel size. The smallest panel (panel 1) already showed a slightly reduction in drag, 
agreeing with previous studies that observed drag reduction with increase of mesh size 
(Thierry et al., 2020). Reduction of drag comes accompanied with decrease of fuel 
consumption, minimizing one of the biggest concerns about trawling, the fossil fuel 
emissions (Priour, 2009; Sala et al., 2008; McHugh et al., 2017). As no commercial sized 
shrimp was lost in the experiments, allocating a panel in the top netting panel of the 
==PAGE:64==
56 
 
trawl can be a plausible alternative for the industry to reduce CO2 emissions. Further 
investigations could be done to quantify the drag resistance and calculate the reduction 
in fuel for a beam trawl with large mesh size escape panel in the top netting panel. 
 
Besides the possible fuel reduction and no loss of shrimp, the panels be easily covered 
by a netting with regular mesh size (e.g. 22 mm) is one more reason to incentive the 
fisherman to introduce them in beam trawl gears. Thus, in situations where some loss 
of shrimps is suspected, the anterior modification tested can be covered or applied 
seasonally, especially during the colder seasons when the lower temperatures inhibit 
the tail-flip mechanism and the vertical movement done by the brown shrimp is reduced 
(Arnott et al., 1998; Jeffery & Revill, 2002). 
 
Based on the results acquired from the anterior gear modifications tested in trial II, the 
best possible configuration for a panel would be one comprising the whole width of the 
top netting panel from 1.5 m behind the bobbin line until the headline, as if panel 3, that 
comprehends only the exterior part of the top netting panel, was extended on its whole 
width.  With this arrangement, the chances of reducing whiting and other pelagic species 
may increase with no loss of brown shrimps, while maximizing the potential reduction 
in fuel consumption.  
 
6. Conclusion and future work 
In conclusion, the thesis was able to understand the spatial distributions of the species 
while entering the trawl and provided insights regarding the escapement of pelagic, 
flatfishes, brown shrimp, and benthos. Although the panels tested does not work as well 
as expected, they showed potential reduction in bycatch and fuel consumption. By 
virtue of the benefits noted, fishermen became interested in using them.  
 
Further investigations in the brown shrimp fishery could focus more on modifications in 
the anterior part of the trawl, as for example floating counter-herding devices in 
between the beam trawl sweeps or hanging from the beam trawl. McHugh et al. (2015) 
tested a wire positioned in front of the beam and a wire with plastic strips hanging from 
it for the Metapenaeus macleayi fishery and obtained positive results reducing 
considerably roundfish catches. Also, the “hydrodredge” developed by Shephard & 
Goudey (2009) could be adapted for a beam trawl. The cups used to deflect the water 
downward to lift the scallops could be installed substituting the bobbins and hopefully 
being a stimulus strong enough to excite shrimp escape behaviour while decreasing 
benthos and possibly flatfish bycatch.  
 
Additionally, in future experiments in which few length classes are encountered, would 
be recommended to round down the total length to the nearest half centimeter instead 
of to the nearest centimeter, creating more length classes to be analyzed.
