Abstract
What this project will aim to prove is how necessary it is to expand the MFM syntax
and concepts in order to include a better qualitative representation for thermodynamic
processes.
This project will ﬁrst give an overview of the state of art of MFM and exergy analysis
in thermodynamics. Then, some single system components, as a turbine and a compres-
sor, and some more complex cycles, as a refrigeration and a heat pump cycle, will be
described and analysed.
These systems will be modelled with the MFM technique and mathematically simulated
in order to show the link between thermodynamic properties and functional modelling
language.
It will be then described why and how MFM can be changed in order to include ex-
ergy transformations. A ﬁrst step on how it could be possible to achieve this syntax
expansion would be also given and the question would be left open for further consider-
ations and implementations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This project aims to investigate the role of exergy and exergy losses by means of one
functional modelling method, namely Multilevel Flow Modelling (MFM) for thermody-
namic processes. Exergy represents the usable part of energy in a system and recent
trends [14] are trying to shift the focus of chemical power plants to try and optimize for
exergy eﬃciency instead of energy, for a better understanding of a system performance.
Expanding the use of MFM to chemical processes would become a very powerful tool to
give a better overview of the consequences of variations in the process of achieving the
main goal which is converting energy or chemicals in a more eﬃcient way.
This project intends to investigate by means of Multilevel Flow Modelling (MFM), the
role of exergy and exergy losses in processes where an energy transformation takes place.
In particular, the project aims to ﬁnd a way to represent those exergy losses for energy
process by means of MFM.
In the project, a background introduction for MFM and exergy with its role in energy
transformation would be provided. After this necessary background introduction, dif-
ferent cases will be presented. The ﬁrst cases will be simple system components, as a
turbine and a compressor and then the analysis will move further to more complex cases
as a refrigerator system and a heat pump. These cases will be ﬁrst analysed thermody-
namically (thermodynamic eﬃciency, energy balance, enthalpy and entropy variations)
and later modelled in MFM. When they are modelled in MFM an analysis about exergy
losses and how to represent them in MFM will be carried on.
The MFM modelling will be done with the software available at the moment, MFM
Suite. The current version of the software enables the user to represent by means of
MFM syntax, mass and energy ﬂows in a process and objectives and goals of the entire
process. However, the software does not include a proper way to represent thermody-
namic reactions and exergy losses, so the importance of improve the above-mentioned
software will be also discussed.

1.1
Objectives
Multilevel ﬂow modelling is a modelling technique for qualitative reasoning proposed
and developed by Prof. Emeritus Morten Lind. The research on the topic is still open
and subjected to extensions and improvements. This project aims to meet the interest
of the writer in this ﬁeld with a modelling problem that came up during an attempt to
model a thermodynamic process with MFM. MFM is at the moment lacking a speciﬁc
way to represent thermodynamic interactions in systems that produce energy. Thus far
the mass ﬂows and the energy ﬂows of the system had been taken into account, but
with the increasing importance that exergy is gathering when it comes eﬃciency eval-
uation, it is important to be able to represent those changes in MFM models too. As
a matter of facts, exergy represents the maximum available work that it is possible to
obtain from a process. It can be easily understood that, studying the amount of actual
work produced, gives a much clearer overview of the actual eﬃciency of the system: for
example, a process A with a hypothetical thermal eﬃciency of 100% can waste most
of the work produced, resulting in less eﬃciency than another process B, in which the
thermal eﬃciency is much lower. Another important aspect is to be able to represent
the inﬂuence of surroundings temperature Ts, which intervenes and becomes relevant
when an exergy analysis is carried on.
The aim of this project is to set the groundwork for a future expansion of energy ﬂows
in the MFM technique, in a way that would make possible to take into account ex-
ergy generation and to ﬁnally have a more complete overview of the system and its
interactions.
1.2
Thesis structure
In this report, it will be shown that a new way of representing thermodynamics reactions,
particularly exergy, in MFM has to be implemented. The current state of MFM does
not take into account of the usable part of energy during energy ﬂows but only of the
total energy of the system. Having a qualitative overview of the amount of useful work
would, in fact, give a better understanding of the system and/or its components.
In order to do so, the notions of exergy and MFM will be explained as well as the state
of art for those theoretical supports.
In the entire project, some simple cases will be the object of study, namely a turbine,
a compressor and a refrigerator cycle (and its opposite case, the heat pump). These
systems will be ﬁrst mathematically modelled from a pure thermodynamic point of view,
and an energy balance and exergy analysis would be carried on. The above-mentioned
systems will be then modelled by means of MFM, with the software MFM Suite(current
version 1.4.5). Finally simulations of the models will be carried on.

Theoretical Background
In this section a theoretical background of the methods used for this project will be given.
This section will be crucial to understand the importance of Functional Modelling and
the basic functions of MFM as well as the current state of this method. In addition,
a theoretical explanation of exergy and its importance in system performance analysis.

2.1
Functional Modeling
Functional Modelling (FM) has a central role in engineering design and operation of
dynamic system and in the development of advanced HMI. FM is composed of concepts,
methods and tools aimed at representing purposes and functional structure of complex
dynamic system [8]. Functional concepts, as shown in [8], are useful for focusing on
speciﬁc problem solving situations in an operating complex dynamic system. In FM,
concepts of functions are belonging to a more vast group of concepts, called means-ends
concepts, which expresses purposes, goals and means. With those 3 deﬁnitions, from [8],
diﬀerent views on functions and functional modelling will be presented:
 Deﬁnition 1: Given two systems P and Q the aim of FM is to represent P by the
functions it serves in the achivements of goals and purposes of Q;
 Deﬁnition 2: The functions of P in relation to Q are the eﬀects of its behaviour
and structure on Q;
 Deﬁnition 3: The functions of P in relation to Q deﬁne P’s interface to Q.
These 3 deﬁnitions can be seen as diﬀerent understanding of FM, as shown in the ﬁgure
below ([8]):

For a better understanding of FM and its purposes, a deﬁnition of functions should
be given. In [12], the functions are described generally with 4 diﬀerent aspects:
 Functions are social facts: functions are ascribed to items or systems depending
on the interest of an user. This implies that functions are considered subjective
in an ontological sense. However functions have to be considered objective in an
epistemic sense because having a function is a fact that cannot be contested;
 Functions are relative to goals: Functions are not intrinsic properties of a system
but they are associated to a particular goal of the user.
 Functions and roles: A function is deﬁned in relation of what it does to another
physical entity; therefore a function implies a distinction between the roles served
by the agent and the object. The agent and object roles are relative to the function,
that may have more roles. The distinction between roles and functions describes
a separation of static and dynamic: functions refer to changes while roles refer to
aspects which persist or are static.
 Functions, dispositions and structure: The realization of the function is dependent
on the availability of physical entities that can realize the function. The realization
has both structural and dispositional features, so functions and roles of a system
are dependent on its dispositions.

Means and end are concepts of everyday common use: an end can be a state to obtain
or a performance requirement and a mean can be a tool or an action. Being end or mean
is not an inherent property but rather context-dependent. The relation between means

2.2
Multilevel ﬂow modeling
Multilevel Flow Modelling is a functional modelling technique for qualitative reasoning,
more precisely a technique for modelling industrial processes on several interconnected
levels of means and part-whole abstractions [11]. MFM’s primary objective is to give an
overview of the goals of the plant taken into account, with a methodological way to deal
with the complexity of the plant itself ([11]).
The idea behind MFM is to provide qualitative informations, that are not usually con-
sidered by plant and control system descriptions and modelling of the systems. MFM
aims to achieve a representation of goals and functions of processes.

2.2.1.1
Flow Functions
MFM has its own speciﬁc ontology: functions are represented by elementary ﬂow ele-
ments, expressed by means of the basic MFM symbols, and the functional structures are
linked together by means-end relations. Flow functions in MFM has to be included in a
function structure, that is a set of functions connected by causal relations.
The basic MFM functions for energy and mass ﬂows are Source, Sink, Storage, Bal-
ance, Transport, Barrier, Distribution, Conversion and Separation. They are
shown in ﬁgure 2.8 and described in detail in Appendix A.
In ﬁgure 2.8, also MFM relations, which are important to deﬁne relations between
functions, are illustrated as well as control symbols, but the latter will not be used in
this project.
Figure 2.8: Basic MFM symbols, ([11])
Two overall principles are always considered and combined iteratively when building
an MFM model:
 Deﬁnition of objectives of the modelling system
 Associations of functions with system components

Relations

Cause eﬀect relations
The MFM models are based on cause-eﬀect relations which are independent of the mod-
elling object [11]. Since cause-eﬀect relations are essential for MFM modelling, speciﬁc
rules need to be developed. In MFM cause-eﬀect relations are associated with goal-
function and function-function patterns in MFM models:
 Relations of inﬂuence interconnecting the ﬂow functions within the ﬂow structures
 the means-end relations making connections between the ﬂow structures
Inﬂuence relations
As shown in [11], ﬂow functions are interconnected within a ﬂow structure by two types
of inﬂuences: direct and indirect, which determine the cause-eﬀect relations between the
states of the ﬂow functions.
Direct inﬂuences
A direct inﬂuence is represented when, for example, when in a
certain ﬂow an increase with transport will increase a successive storage.
Indirect Inﬂuence
They are represented explicitly by the Inﬂuencer and Par-
ticipant between transport functions and ﬂow functions. They represent how state of
transport are inﬂuenced by the ﬂow functions in a ﬂow structure.
2.2.1.3
Means-end relations in MFM
The means-end relations express the cause-eﬀect relations between functions, goal and
objectives [10]. The mean-ends relations can be classiﬁed in two more subgroups, ac-
cording to what they connect:
– Objectives with ﬂow structure: the relations produce, maintain, destroy and sup-
press shown in Figure 2.8 are included in this group;
– Functions with ﬂow structures: produce-product and mediate relations.
The latter, implies that in MFM two diﬀerent types of cause-eﬀect relations can be
individuated when connecting functions, depending on if they are connected with shifts
in aspects or roles:

Roles
The current MFM ontology mainly covers the representation on the goal and functional
level. However a relation among structure-role-function can be established further down

the means-end dimension.
Roles in MFM are attributes of functions, describing how system components or subsys-
tems contribute to the function. Taking the standard example of a transport function
realized by a pump we can attribute two roles to the transport function. One role is the
agent which causes the transported ﬂuid to move and the other role is the object which
is moved i.e. the ﬂuid.
Roles are used to relate plant functions to its physical realization and oﬀer an abstract
way of representing physical structures or objects.
This speciﬁc separation of roles enables the qualitative representation and analysis of
diﬀerent parts of energy and their implications to the whole process. A more detailed
explanation on how roles can represent the diﬀerent parts of energy, will be given in
chapter 5.
2.3
Thermodynamic concepts
The central purpose of this work is to demonstrate how exergy analysis is a more ac-
curate and convenient mean to analyse a system and how important it is to ﬁnd a way
to represent exergy in MFM models, in order to get a more appropriate qualitative de-
scription of the system and its functions and objectives. In this section, an explanation
of what is exergy will be provided and its relevance in the study of a process and the
possible connection with MFM models will be discussed.
2.3.1
Basic thermodynamic concepts
In order to have a better understanding of the following exergy discussion, some of the
basic concepts of thermodynamics have to be analysed. In this paragraph, outlines for
the laws of thermodynamics and the concepts of enthalpy and entropy will be given. A
more detailed explanation of the concepts in this section, can be found in [13].
First and second laws of thermodynamics
 First Law: The total energy of any system and its surroundings, considered
together, is conserved [13]:
∆E = Q −W
(2.1)
where ∆E is the total energy change of the system, Q is the heat added to the

2.3.3
Exergy
In thermodynamics, the exergy of a system is deﬁned as the maximum useful work possi-
ble during a process that brings the system into equilibrium with a heat reservoir. ([17])
It is, in a more simple way, the maximum work one can draw from a system by a ther-
modynamic transformation. Though exergy, once called ”available energy”, is closely
linked to energy, they have a fundamental diﬀerence. Based on the ﬁrst principle of
thermodynamics, it is known that energy is always conserved and thus can neither be
created nor destroyed, just transformed and transported.
Exergy is diﬀerent: it can be destroyed, whenever there is a thermodynamic transforma-
tion. If there is a temperature diﬀerence, there are irreversibilities that leads to exergy
destruction. All in all, every non-isothermal transformation destroys a certain amount
of exergy and thus limits the output work that can be retrieved [6].
Figure 2.18: Visual representation diﬀerence between energy and exergy in a combustion
power plant, [15]
Figure 2.18 shows a visual representation of the diﬀerence between exergy and energy:
the red ﬂow, which represents the energy ﬂow, shows the total energy produced, while
the green ﬂow, the exergy ﬂow, is substantially thinner because it shows only the part
of energy that is used, i.e. exergy.
Exergy is a property dependent on both the state of the system and the state of the
==PAGE:30==
2 Theoretical Background
21
environment. The exergy of a system at equilibrium with the environment, is zero.
Thereby exergy of a stream is the part of the energy which maximally can be extracted as
useful work in the process of bringing the system into equilibrium with a heat reservoir.
Note that, as the energy of a stream is transformed during a speciﬁc production, the
exergy varies. (The part of the exergy which is destroyed, i.e. no longer available for
useful work with the selected reference conditions, is by some investigators called anergy.
This term will not be used here).
In thermodynamic systems, exergy analysis allows for better tracking of the eﬃciency
of the process on a input-output point of view, as the available energy in the start and
the end of the process allows a close monitoring of the irreversible losses as well as the
energy losses.
Consequently, Exergy may be used to qualify the part of energy of a process stream which
may be used for useful work. During an energy conversion the amount of exergy which
is lost can be clearly identiﬁed. However, the challenge in doing so requires accounting
for the entropy increase during the energy conversion [6].
Hence exergy may be used to clarify the role of the maximally useful work of a process
stream. Similarly the role of the lost exergy may be clearly identiﬁed as well, even only
qualitatively, using functional models.
2.3.4
Exergy and Entropy
Exergy and entropy are linked variables, when exergy decreases in a closed system en-
tropy increases. Both variable are related to the second law of thermodynamics: In a
closed system, any possible (non-idealised) transformation produces entropy (δS), su-
perior to the change in heat (δQ) divided by the temperature of the transformation
(T).
dS > δQ
T
(2.35)
Exergy (B) is deﬁned as the enthalpy (H) minus the entropy (S) multiplied by the
ambient temperature TR.
B = H −TRS
(2.36)
It can be seen in the mathematical relation the close link between the two. The sources
of exergy loss and the sources of entropy are the irreversibilities.

The thermal energy ﬂow structure represents the changes in the thermal energy for

Chapter 5
Representation of exergy in MFM
The simulations show how exergy and lost work are changing in the diﬀerent components
and cycles analysed. Combined with the MFM models, a quantitative and a qualitative
analysis for each system is available. It is clear though that irreversibilities and energy
availability must be modelled in MFM to have a complete overview. A possible link
between exergy and MFM model was already presented in [19]; in this paper, the authors
proposed a visual model, called EXCEM that could combine exergy, cost, energy and
mass when describing qualitatively performances of thermal systems. Cost was not taken
into account in this project, though, since it is still weak the link between economical
costs and MFM models. The method as to exergy modelling can be achieved in MFM
is still an open question.
For the achievement of this purpose, the importance of roles must been taken into
account. Roles in MFM are similar to the semantic roles (or cases) used in linguistics to
deﬁne the meaning of verbs. In MFM roles have been introduced to increase the level of
detail in modelling. The roles also enable more resolution in reasoning. The deﬁnition
of roles for MFM functions is seen as an issue for decision and further development. The
deﬁnition of roles will be important for deﬁning ontologies for diﬀerent process domains
in MFM, since roles will be domain dependent.
At the moment, agent and object roles are available and there is no distinction between
domains. These roles can be associated with any MFM function. It is expected that
roles will be deﬁned as needed for diﬀerent domains. Note that the introduction of new
roles does not aﬀect the MFM functions, since the roles are seen as attributes of the
functions. Thus a set of balance types which are distinguished by their associated roles
can be obtained. Separation and conversion functions as subtypes of balances (reﬂected
in their symbols), are deﬁned. They are distinguished by having diﬀerent roles. Note
that at the moment these roles are not fully developed. To represent exergy and their
signiﬁcance to the process, roles for a special group of functions can be identiﬁed.
What the results obtained show is that the fundamental diﬀerence between mass and
energy on one hand, and exergy on the other hand, is that exergy is not conserved which
calls for an adaptation of the MFM toolbox.
The need for distinguishing between useful energy and losses in exergy analysis can be
53
==PAGE:63==
5 Representation of exergy in MFM
54
accommodated by introducing three roles for an (energy) conversion function. These
roles are used to distinguish between three ﬂows of energy involved in the conversion:
 the input energy (convertant)
 the useful energy which can be transformed into work (maximum available work /
exergy)
 the loss which cannot be transformed into work (exergy loss)
The names to be given to the roles is presently an open question. By introducing these
roles for a conversion function, a state indicating the rate of conversion can be associated.
It has to be noted that the roles introduced for the conversion will have implications for
the syntax of MFM models since an energy loss cannot be the input energy of another
conversion. The roles will also require future extensions of reasoning rules. However,
the existing rules will still apply because they do not refer to information about roles.
The MFM symbols represent basic concepts, and can be sorted in diﬀerent categories.
Sources and sinks represent the availability of a certain substance (mass, energy) in the
process as the source makes the substance available for the process in focus and the sink
represent that the resource is not inside the boundaries of the system in focus.
For exergy, sources and sinks are useful for losses of exergy that go out of the scope of the
system. On the contrary, exergy can be plainly destroyed. This means that the exergy
that disappeared during the energy degradation in the process can not be recovered at
all. The total exergy of the universe is diminishing every time a transformation consumes
exergy.

Chapter 6
Conclusions
This project has shown the importance of MFM technique and the importance of com-
bining it with exergy analysis for the thermodynamic processes. Exergy analysis is an
useful tool to analyse the eﬃciency of a system, therefore it is necessary to implement
exergy analysis with MFM modelling in order to have complete qualitative models for
thermodynamics systems.
The thesis ﬁrst stated the importance of functional modelling, especially of MFM for a
qualitative description for design and operation of systems. It was recognized especially
that MFM is a powerful tool to describe goals and functions of processes.
Then the importance of exergy analysis in evaluating performances of thermodynamic
systems was stated. The exergy analysis was individuated to be useful, thereby providing
a better overview of actual work and lost work used in the system. The processes chosen
to present the beneﬁts of exergy analysis, were chosen to be single component systems,
as a turbine and a compressor, and more complex cycles with multiple components,
heat pump and refrigeration cycles. The cycles were ﬁrst studied from a thermodynamic
point of view, taking into account the energy balances.
The cycles were later modelled with the MFM technique. Before the modelling, a goals
decomposition for the systems was given, in order to point out the objectives of the sys-
tems and the functions of every component in the cycle. With the goals decomposition
it was more natural to produce MFM models in which energy ﬂows, mass ﬂows and
objectives were well stated. In particular, was given importance to the losses in each
system component, losses that are crucial when exergy needs to be described.
The MFM models were followed by simulations in which exergy variations and lost work
variations were shown, according to variations in characteristic parameters for the sys-
tems. The simulations were extremely helpful to relate the losses in the MFM models
with the quantitative counterpart, in order to show the need of expanding MFM syntax
with a more detailed way to describe exergy. Combining the simulations with the MFM
models, would give the means to develop new reasoning rules when considering the ex-
ergy representation in MFM models.

6 Conclusions
58
It was ﬁnally discussed the importance of expanding the MFM syntax and the MFM
concepts behind the roles, which could be useful to describe the agents involved in ex-
ergy conversion and exergy losses. A ﬁrst draft on how that could be implemented was
pointed out, as well as the need for further studies and implementations.
6.1
Future work
In the discussion, the need of expanding MFM modelling language with a syntax that
would be able to include exergy variations was pointed out. A ﬁrst draft on how this
syntax could be improved was given. However, this syntax is not complete. The roles
and the name given to them in exergy conversions are still not present an open question.
Another aspect that is believed needs to be ﬁxed, is how to represent the eﬀect that
surroundings temperature, Ts, has on the systems, especially when it comes to exergy
variations. In the present MFM syntax, environment temperature is not taken into ac-
count, but it would be extremely interesting to widen MFM borders in order to include
this consideration as well. This will require further studies that could not have been
carried on in the scope of this project.
Another aspect that proposes an open debate, is how to actually represent the conver-
sion when exergy wants to be considered. In the given approach, a conversion function
was used, but it could be discussed whether there is the need in MFM to include a
completely new function that represents the exergy conversion.
According to these improvements, it is clear to understand that the present software will
also need to be modiﬁed.
Other improvements could be carried out in the simulations too. For now exergy losses
for single component systems and lost work for more complex systems were identiﬁed. It
could be useful to understand what happens when more than one parameter is varying
and what exergy analysis states in this case is: are exergy losses increasing in every
component? How is the entire cycle reacting?
In order to expand the simulations a more appropriate knowledge of thermodynamics
systems and their behaviour is required.
A future application of this work could be used to investigate how to apply considera-
tions on exergy analysis in MFM models to large scale systems, e.g. a wind turbine.
It is believed that a more accurate analysis for the simulations, can also help in develop-
ing new reasoning rules for exergy in MFM models. This aspect can also be investigated
as future work.
