Abstract
The following thesis describes the design of a Σ∆modulator for a very low power appli-
cation in a hearing aid. Its purpose is to monitor the battery voltage, so DC accuracy is
the most important measure besides power consumption. The bandwidth requirement
of 12 kHz makes monitoring voltage fluctuations due to transient power peaks possible.
The circuit was initially designed and simulated in Matlab using the Schreier Toolbox.
Several nonidealities in the implementation were modelled and their impact on the perfor-
mance evaluated. The subsequent circuit was designed as a third order CIFF structure,
which was implemented as a discrete time system using SC integrators.
In order to achieve a low power consumption, the integrators were implemented us-
ing cascode inverters as opposed to the traditional implementation using operational
transconductance amplifiers. The high input referred offset that inverters exhibit, as
well as 1/f noise were reduced by utilizing autozeroing. All other auxiliary circuit blocks
were also designed and included in the final performance evaluation.
The final circuit achieves a SNDR of 72.3 dB for a 1.6 V input voltage, while consuming
3.6 µW. In the worst case corner the SNDR performance drops to 69.5 dB, with the
maximum power consumption of the circuit being 6.7 µW. The total capacitance of
the modulator is 3.1 pF. Charge injection and clock feedthrough cause a combined DC
offset of 5.7 mV, which can be reduced by making a single GND calibration measurement.
After calibration, the circuit shows a DC error of less than 300 µV across the entire input
voltage range.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Project Scope
The demand for portable and battery powered electronics with continuously improving
performance is increasing every year. The scaling down of complementary metal oxide
semiconductor (CMOS) technology allows fitting much more computing power in the
same die area, while the supply voltages also scale down with it. This enables low power
designs, but poses challenges for analog circuits since the threshold voltages are not
scaling accordingly. Designing traditional analog amplifier blocks thus becomes more
difficult.
Especially in hearing aids, where power consumption is an extremely important met-
ric, low power implementations are essential. Analog to digital converters (ADCs) are
the connection between the real and the digital world, where the signal processing is
done. Nyquist rate ADCs are mostly limited by the precision that components can be
manufactured in CMOS technologies. This limits their use for higher resolutions, where
their power and area consumption rises dramatically. The sigma delta (Σ∆) modula-
tor has some benefits in this regard, since it relaxes the analog circuit requirements by
requiring a higher speed in the digital domain. This is not usually a limiting factor
in CMOS technology, as very fast circuits can be implemented. The Σ∆modulator is
capable of achieving very high resolution due to its linearity, which is made possible by
an inherently linear single bit analog to digital conversion.

2.1
Analog to Digital Conversion
The purpose of analog to digital conversion is to map the real life input signal, which
is continuous in amplitude and time, into a finite representation, to be used in digital
signal processing. This includes the quantization of the continuous amplitude, as well as
sampling in time. Since the continuous values are mapped into a set of discrete values, a
loss of information cannot be avoided. In nyquist rate data converters, the sampling fre-
quency is determined by the signal bandwidth. The Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem
states, that the sampling frequency fs has to be at least twice as big as the bandwidth
BW of the signal in order to avoid aliasing
fs ≥2BW
(2.1)
In the frequency domain sampling leads to a repetition of the original spectrum with
the sampling frequency fs. This is the reason, why the original spectrum should not
have any frequency components above fs/2, since they would be aliased down into the
signal band and change the original signal spectrum.
ADCs are specified by the number of bits N, which correlates to the number of voltage
levels M they can resolve.
M = 2N
(2.2)
The smallest voltage an ADC can distinguish is called VLSB and in the case of uniform
quantization is the same as the quantization step size ∆.

2.3
Noise Shaping
The concept of noise shaping is to move the quantization noise power out of the signal
bandwidth and into the frequency range outside of the range [−fB; +fB]. Since the signal
contents outside of the signal bandwidth will be decimated and filtered subsequently, a
further improvement of SQNR or resolution can be achieved this way. Conceptually this
can be seen in figure 2.6 where the majority of the quantization noise power lies outside
of the signal bandwidth leading to a higher SQNR.

3.1.1
General Considerations
The project specifications were for a modulator that measures the battery voltage, as
well as internal nodes for debug purposes. In order to achieve this with the same modula-
tor, the inputs would have to be multiplexed. The so called incremental Σ∆modulator
does this by resetting the integrator values when switching to a different channel or
input [3]. The downside to this is that the incremental operation needs a relatively long
conversion time to achieve high SNR values. The decimation filter can be implemented
more simple than for the typical Σ∆modulator, which does not give any benefit in
this application since filter banks are already implemented and available in the hearing
aid. It was additionally decided, that the battery voltage measurement cannot be mul-
tiplexed or time interleaved but rather needs to run continuously. The measurement of
internal nodes would only be used in special situations and for debug purposes. For this
and because area usage is not the main concern, it was decided that a second copy of
the modulator could be instantiated for this purpose. The demand for the high input
impedance is only necessary for the measurement of internal nodes which cannot be
loaded. The measurement of the battery voltage does not have this restriction, since
it is a low impedance source. A standard switched capacitor implementation of a Σ∆
modulator has an equivalent input impedance that depends on the sampling capacitor
and the switching frequency and is not a high impedance input. One possible solution
to the measurement of internal nodes could be to include a buffer at the input. The
downside to this is additional errors and offset, which would have to be dealt with. The
final conclusion from this is, that for the design the focus is put on the battery voltage
measurement and thus the input impedance specification is neglected.
3.1.2
Discrete vs Continuous Time
The loop filter H(z) can be implemented in DT or continuous time (CT). DT implementa-
tions use switched capacitor (SC) based integrators while CT integrators are made with
operational amplifiers (opamps) with resistors and capacitors (active RC) or transconduc-
tors and capacitors (gmC). Both implementations have benefits and drawbacks, which
will be discussed briefly, also see chapter 5 in [1].
==PAGE:22==
12
3 The Σ∆Modulator
The main difference is the implementation of the modulator coefficients. In the case of
DT modulators the coefficients are implemented using the ratio of sampling to integrat-
ing capacitor. In typical CMOS processes, capacitor ratios can be implemented with
a precision of 1% to 0.1% [4]. In a CT modulator the coefficients are typically imple-
mented as RC time constants. This means that the coefficients are implemented with
absolute values of resistance and capacitance, which is poorly controlled. The absolute
component values can easily have ±20% variation, so additional measures have to be
taken to implement the loop filter coefficients in the CT case.
The DT modulator is a sampled system, which means that its input requires an AAF to
avoid aliasing of out of band signals down into the signal band. If the OSR is high, the
requirements on this AAF are relaxed in comparison with nyquist rate converters, where
a sharp transition band might be necessary. In the case of the DT Σ∆with a high OSR
a first order low pass filter is sufficient. The CT modulator typically does not require
an AAF because the signal first passes through the loop filter before it is sampled and
quantized. The loop filter in this case already filters high frequency components.
Another effect of the sampled nature of the DT system is that the settling behaviour
within the clock period does not have an effect on the modulator, as long as the settling
is linear. The system only needs to settle to the required accuracy within the clock
period. The CT integrator on the other hand needs to remain linear all the time.
When comparing the gain bandwidth (GBW) requirements of the two implementations,
the CT system only needs opamps with a GBW of around 2−3fs. The amplifiers in the
DT system need enough GBW to achieve very good settling and typically 5−10fs is the
design rule to minimize the settling error and not degrade the SNR performance. This
is clearly the benefit of a CT system, since a lower GBW allows for a design with lower
power consumption. In addition, this might also be important for very high frequency
systems, where designing amplifiers with such high GBW is becoming difficult.
Clock jitter and the shape of the feedback waveform are other sources of error in contin-
uous time implementations. These have very little effect on DT implementations.
In this project, a discrete time implementation is chosen because of the previously men-
tioned benefits.
3.1.3
Feedback vs feedforward structures
The first structure that will be presented is the cascade of integrators with weighted
feedforward summation (CIFF). This structure has only one feedback path to the input,
but sums all the integrator outputs in front of the quantizer. When the NTF is imple-
mented as a Butterworth high pass filter, the STF is set as well. The downside to this
structure is that the STF contains peaking at higher frequencies, so if the signal contains
energy at these frequencies the modulator might become unstable. In this DT system
an AAF filter will have to be implemented, so this peaking should not cause problems
since the input signal is filtered.

4.1
The Inverter based Amplifier
As CMOS technology is scaling down, the supply voltages are also decreasing. For low
power designs, low supply voltages are also advantageous, but this limits the possibilities
for amplifier circuit implementations. The performance of OTAs is limited by low supply
voltages. For this project the most important performance metric besides resolution is
power consumption. In order to acchieve a low power consumption inverters are used as
the amplifiers. Inverter based switched capacitor Σ∆modulators have been implemented
and reported in several publications [7][8][9]. Inverters are mostly used for digital circuits,
but when operated in the linear region can be used as analog amplifiers. Inverters are
single input amplifiers and amplify the input signal with respect to the trip voltage,
which is set by the nMOS and pMOS input pair and their relative geometries. Inverters
are in class AB operation when the supply voltage is larger than the sum of threshold
voltages VDD > Vtn + |Vtp|. Both transistors are then operating in strong inversion and
a constant cross current flows. When the supply voltage is lower or at the boundary of
the sum of threshold voltages VDD ≤Vtn + |Vtp|, the inverter operates as class C and
the cross current is drastically reduced. In this case both transistors operate in weak
inversion. The operation in weak inversion brings the benefit of a much higher gm/Id
efficiency and thus achieves a larger gain for the same current when compared to an
amplifier in strong inversion. The intrinsic speed of the transistors and thus the GBW
of the inverter amplifier is decreasing in the weak inversion region, which is a trade
off. The main challenge with inverter based amplifiers is that they are biased by the
supply voltage and not by a bias current. Supply voltage variations thus will change
the biasing, which in this project is not a main concern since the reference voltage is
a regulated supply with low variation. A challenge with the inverter is the impact of
threshold voltage variations, since they can change the operation of the amplifier from
class C to class AB, which leads to a drastic increase in power consumption. Threshold
voltage variations because of process corners are the main concern here, since they can
vary a lot. Another limitation with inverters is their maximum DC gain of around 50 dB.
To increase the gain of the amplifier, cascodes are added to the inverter to boost the
DC gain at the cost of output signal swing, as well as requiring a biasing circuit. The
output swing limitation is not that critical, as the integrator outputs are much smaller
for CIFF than for CIFB.
Another challenge with the inverter amplifier is the trip voltage, which is set by the two

4.3.4
Circuit Timing
In order to implement the CIFF structure all three state variables or integrator outputs
X1, X2 and X3 have to be summed at the quantizer input. In order to be able to do
this, they have to settle and be summed in the same clock phase. The problem with
the most commonly used autozeroing circuit in figure 4.1a is that the integrator input
samples at phase ϕ1, but the switch at the output has to be clocked with ϕ2 because of
the autozero operation in ϕ1. The switch ϕ2 at the output is the one doing the sampling
for the next integrator, which means that the inputs of the subsequent integrators in the
circuit are integrating in different phases. Integrators 1 and 3 would integrate in phase
1 and integrator 2 would integrate in phase 2. This is a problem for the feedforward
summation. The common solution for this is to use noninverting and inverting switch
configurations for the feedfoward path, which allows the summation in the same phase
but changes the polarity of half of the signals. In fully differential implementations this
is not a problem, because the inverted signals are just swapped at the quantizer input
to revert the polarity again. This is not possible in the single ended implementation
and thus the autozeroing circuit from figure 4.1b is used instead. Another possibility is
the implementation of the CIFB structure, where only the feedback signals have to be
aligned with each integrator input. The feedback signals can then be applied via D type
flip flops, where the appropriate clock signals and an inverted output make it easy to
apply the correct signal.

The first integrator is the most important block for the system performance. Any error

function of their respective inputs to the modulator output and thus require much less
stringent performance. The inverter based integrator which is the amplifier used in this
project has been chosen for the reasons named in section 4.1.
The gain of the inverter amplifier is dependent on the transconductance gm and output
impedance Rout of the inverter.

5.1.3
Process Variation
The main challenge with the inverter based amplifier, as mentioned before, is the effect
of PVT variations. The high transconductance efficiency comes from the fact that the
amplifying transistors are in weak inversion. This is only the case when the supply
voltage is smaller than the sum of the threshold voltages of nMOS and pMOS. In this
project the supply voltage is assumed to be ideal, so in this case does not affect the per-
formance. The main problem is the threshold voltage variation with respect to process
variation. For the amplifier the worst case scenarios are the slow nMOS, slow pMOS (SS)
in combination with a large capacitance at low temperature, as well as the fast nMOS,
fast pMOS (FF) in combination with a small capacitance at high temperature. In the
FF case both threshold voltages are much smaller than in the typical case, which causes
the amplifying transistors to be in the strong inversion region instead of weak inversion.
The result is a circuit with a higher GBW, since the loading capacitance is smaller than
in the typical case and also because the gm is larger than designed. Additionally the
bias current is increased a lot, so the inverter consumes more power than in TT. The
SS case is the opposite, since the transistors are biased even deeper in the subthreshold
region leading to a smaller bias current and smaller resulting gm. The load capacitance
is higher and thus the GBW is smaller than in the TT case. The circuit then has to be

between the other cases and the mismatch in threshold voltages skews the trip voltage of
the inverter. This is not a problem since it is compensated by the autozeroing operation.

system. These can be implemented in two different ways. The decision for the solution

is equal to the input voltage. If using a single nMOS transistor, the gate signal thus has

turn the mosfet on. In this system the input voltage into the switches ranges from 0 V

has to be at least Vth higher.

As mentioned before, one of the problems with the inverter based amplifier is the poor
power supply rejection ratio (PSRR). The reference used is a regulated voltage, so no
large deviations are to be expected, but noise will have an effect. The main problem with
the implemented circuit is that the reference voltage is used as the supply voltage for
the ADC and also used as the feedback signal. This means, that the feedback signal is
summed together with the input signal at the input of the first integrator and any error
signal that Vref contains appears in the output spectrum unfiltered. This also means that
improving the PSRR of the integrators will not solve this. One solution to this could be
to use a clean regulated voltage just for the feedback and seperate voltage as the supply
for the integrators, quantizer and auxiliary blocks. Since the single supply solution does
not provide any power supply rejection, the solution with separate supplies is examined.
The autozeroing operation of the inverters does not only reduce DC offset and 1/f noise
but also attenuates any low frequency noise on the power supply rail. This works the
same way as the DC offset cancellation, as all voltage deviations at the integrator output
are sampled on to the compensation capacitor. The lower the frequency content of this
noisy signal is, the more of it can be reduced. This was examined and the resulting
PSRR can be seen in figure 5.12. The result shows how the inverter integrators do
provide some power supply rejection and that the rejection is higher for low frequencies,
matching the expectations.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
6.1
Summary
The simulation results of the previous chapter show that the designed Σ∆modulator
block fulfills the given specifications, except for the SNDR degradation of 0.5 dB below
specifications in the FF corner. All essential auxiliary blocks besides the common mode
voltage rail were designed and included in the performance summary.
First, the relevant theory for the Σ∆modulator was presented, which subsequently
enabled the theoretical modelling and loop filter design in Matlab. Several nonidealities
and their effects on the system performance were then simulated and a design decision
with respect to the loop filter implementation made.
The first step to the actual circuit implementation was using VerilogA modelling of the
inverter amplifier and quantizer, as well as utilizing ideal passive components. This
simulation could then be used to verify the implementation against the Matlab results.
The VerilogA implementation revealed the first nonideal behaviour, since the amplifiers
low frequency gain is not ideal and thus leads to a degradation of the in band noise
attenuation. Apart from this, the implemented circuit with VerilogA amplifiers showed
similar performance and also enables much shorter simulation times when compared to
the transistor-level implementation.
Starting with the inverter amplifiers, parts of the circuit were then implemented as tran-
sistor circuits to verify the overall system behaviour was still fulfilling the specifications.
This showed another small degradation in performance, as the VerilogA models are lin-
earized and do not include the complex behaviour of the transistor circuits. After all
circuit blocks had been implemented, the final performance of the modulator could be
evaluated. The modulator showed a SNDR of 81 dB, which is another drop of 3 dB
when compared to the VerilogA implementation. This can be explained due to the fact
that the large signal behaviour and nonlinearities of the transistors were not modelled
in VerilogA and thus lead to an increase in distortion and an increased noise floor.
The final performance of the modulator was then evaluated with respect to SNDR as
well as INL. The modulator achieves a SNDR of 72.6 dB for a 1.6 V DC input with
transient noise in the typical process corner. Charge injection and clock feedthrough
lead to an input referred DC offset of 5.7 mV in the modulator. The integral nonlinearity
across the input range is less than 300 µV, after calibrating the system using a GND
measurement to determine and cancel the input referred DC offset error. The circuit
==PAGE:60==
50
6 Conclusion
implementation requires a total capacitance of 3.1 pF and its power consumption is
below the set specification using only 3.6 µW in the typical process corner. The power
consumption does show a large deviation in the FF (6.7 µW) and SS (2.35 µW) corners,
which should be improved.
6.2
Future Work
This section describes some of the improvements or changes that could be investigated
with more time.
The most important improvement could be made to the inverter based amplifiers. Their
susceptibility to PVT variations should be addressed. The inverters could be improved
by biasing them specifically and not having them biased directly by the supply rail. The
goal would be to reduce the power consumption variation and make their performance
more robust with respect to PVT variations. In [16] this was achieved by supplying
the inverters with the exact voltage to always bias them at the boundary of weak and
moderate inversion using an LDO voltage regulator and a replica of the inverter with
input and output shorted. This could also improve the power supply rejection ratio
of the integrators. [17] presents three different schemes to lower the PVT impact on
the inverters: semi-constant current biasing, constant current biasing and constant gm
biasing, which could also be investigated.
Another improvement could be made to the comparator, since its power consumption
is very high in comparison with the rest of the circuit. One idea could be to power
down the comparator and only turn it on for a specific time before and after the DFF is
clocked to meet its setup and hold times. This way the constant cross current could be
eliminated for the majority of the clock period. A second option could be to use a logic
gate instead of the comparator, where the second input is a clock signal which goes high
shortly before the DFF decision.
The common mode level was not generated in this project. Either this voltage rail has to
be generated and its power consumption included or the decision made, that the voltage
is generated and supplied from a separate block.
Furthermore the modulator should be examined with respect to mismatch using monte
carlo simulations. Here, especially gain errors due to the mismatch of capacitors is of
interest.

function [ out, x ] = sim_SD( order, u, a, b, c, x0, k, type, offset, Vddlim, epsilon )
%SYNTH_SD Summary of this function goes here
%
This function calculates the difference equations for the third order
%
CIFF and CIFB structure. It also includes scaling coefficients and some
%
nonidealities, like integrator output clipping, integrator incomplete
%
settling and a quantizer uncertainty bound
N = length(u);
% x = zeros(order, N);
x(:,1) = abs(x0);
v = zeros(1, N);
y = zeros(1, N);
if type=='CIFF'
for i=2:N
x(1,i) = bound((x(1,i-1) + b(1)*u(i)/k(1) - c(1)*v(i-1)/k(1))*(1-epsilon),-Vddlim,Vddlim);
x(2,i) = bound((x(2,i-1) + c(2)*x(1,i)*k(1)/k(2) + b(2)/k(2)*u(i))*(1-epsilon),-Vddlim,Vddlim);
x(3,i) = bound((x(3,i-1) + c(3)*x(2,i)*k(2)/k(3) + b(3)/k(3)*u(i))*(1-epsilon),-Vddlim,Vddlim);
y(i) = a(1)*x(1,i)*k(1) + a(2)*x(2,i)*k(2) + a(3)*x(3,i)*k(3) + b(4)*u(i);
v(i) = quant(y(i),offset);
end
out = v;
elseif type=='CIFB'
for i=2:N
x(1,i) = bound((x(1,i-1) +
b(1)*u(i) - a(1)*v(i-1))*(1-epsilon),-Vddlim,Vddlim);
x(2,i) = bound((x(2,i-1) + c(1)*x(1,i) + b(2)*u(i) - a(2)*v(i-1))*(1-epsilon),-Vddlim,Vddlim);
x(3,i) = bound((x(3,i-1) + c(2)*x(2,i) + b(3)*u(i) - a(3)*v(i-1))*(1-epsilon),-Vddlim,Vddlim);
v(i) = quant(x(3,i),offset);
end
out = v;
else
out = NaN;
end
Listing 5: quant.m
function [ Out ] = quant( In, bound)
%QUANTIZE
%
1 Bit quantizer, with uncertainty bound
pd = makedist('Normal');
if abs(In) <= bound
Out = sign(random(pd)); %random output if quantizer input is within the bound
else
if In >= 0
Out = 1;
else
Out = -1;
end
end
end

APPENDIX B
Auto-evaluation of project
The master thesis project was a very challenging and complex project, which made it
especially difficult to have a good overview of what needs to be done and how long the
separate phases will take at the beginning of the project. The time schedule was more
helpful during the second half, where it showed a good overview of what still needs to
be done and if the respective topics needed to be closed to move on and be able to finish
the whole project.
At the beginning of the thesis I was not organised enough, taking notes on loose paper.
This improved massively once I started using a notebook and trying to take notes of
design decisions made, questions that come up on every day, even if very short. This
made the work much more clear and I could plan the to do’s for the next day better as
well. In addition, it shows that progress was made in the weeks, when difficulties arose
and it otherwise seemed as if no progress was happening, which proved motivating.
Another lesson learned, was that it is sometimes better to have a short spontaneous
meeting and clear questions before spending a week trying to simulate and understand
a circuit with wrong assumptions. For this, the IC lab and being able to talk to other
students proved very helpful, as well as Ivan taking the time whenever it was possible.
The circuit was successfully implemented and fulfilled the specifications, which was very
rewarding even though I would have liked to implement a few more changes/improve-
ments.
