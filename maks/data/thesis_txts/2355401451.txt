Abstract
The work in this thesis is based on a recently introduced 3D surface-proﬁle technique,
i.e.
the two-level undercut-proﬁle substrate (2LUPS) concept [1]-[2], used for production of
multi-ﬁlamentary high-temperature coated conductor (CC) tapes. Reducing the superconductor
ﬁlament width linearly reduces the alternating current hysteretic energy losses [3], and it enables
manufacturing of stable high-temperature superconducting magnets [4]. A new process of tape
masking and Ni-based electroplating on a Ni-W metal alloy substrate to form similar 3D surface-
proﬁles as those achieved by the 2LUPS concept [5], which is based on two levels of plateaus
connected via an undercut-proﬁle, is investigated. The undercut-proﬁle should be large enough
to enable a shading eﬀect during subsequent physical vapor deposition (PVD) of layers, thereby
creating self-formed and physically separated superconductor ﬁlaments on the two plateaus, while
still utilizing the full width of the CC. This will theoretically increase the engineering current
density compared to current ﬁlament techniques utilizing e.g.
laser striation or mechanical
scribing.
Inspection of the metal substrate cross-section using focused ion beam milling and scanning
electron microscopy (FIBSEM) reveals that an undercut-proﬁle is achieved by using kapton tape
as a mask while electroplating nickel to create the upper plateaus.
The arithmetic surface
roughness of the electroplated nickel layer is determined via atomic force microscopy (AFM)
to be suitable for CC fabrication. To verify if the undercut-proﬁle is suﬃcient, an electrically
insulating layer of SiO, simulating the buﬀer layers in CCs, followed by an electrically conductive
layer of Ag, simulating the superconducting layer, is deposited using PVD, and four-point probe
measurements to create I/V characteristics are used to measure resistance across plateaus. The
plateaus are deemed electrically insulated from each other, as the resistances from each insulating
layer adds up to the total resistance through both plateaus. Accordingly, it is expected that
these new electroplated 3D surface-proﬁles will also enable ﬁlamentization of superconductors
produced by PVD processes. A small caveat to these ﬁndings, is the lack of a suitable proﬁle
for the use in CC fabrication being manufactured in this project. The adhesive in the masking
tape creates bulges or protrusions in the proﬁle, so a further study on thinner adhesive layers or
a diﬀerent masking material altogether is needed.
The possibility of texture transfer from the Ni-W metal substrates to the plated Ni layer
is also investigated, for the use in the cheaper rolling assisted bi-axially textured substrate (RA-
BiTS) fabrication process. The electrodeposited Ni would during annealing at low temperatures
experience an abnormal grain growth stage, thereby rendering it incapable of attaining the tex-
ture needed for RABiTS fabrication. Furthermore, the thermal grooving during annealing of the
pure Ni could also become a problem for the ion beam assisted deposition (IBAD) process, as a
surface roughness of < 5 nm is desired. The author of this thesis therefore strongly recommends
investigating the possibility of plating e.g. Ni-W instead.

Preface
This thesis is submitted as fulﬁllment of the requisites for obtaining the Master of Science
and Engineering degree at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The work has been
carried out at the Department of Energy Conversion and Storage at DTU Energy. The project
was supervised by Dr. Anders Christian Wulﬀ, and co-supervised by Dr. Peter Stanley Jørgensen
and Ms. Pernille Hedemark Nielsen. The 30 ECTS point thesis was carried out from August
22nd, 2016 to January 12th, 2017. Part of the work was presented at the Sustain-ATV conference
2016 at DTU, receiving a nomination for best project in the Materials category.
The work done in this thesis investigates tape masking and Ni-electroplating 3D surface
structures, with the aim of creating an undercut-proﬁle capable of shading during subsequent
physical vapor deposition of layers. This would enable self-formed ﬁlaments of the deposited lay-
ers on the two plateaus, which can be used for producing multi-ﬁlamentary high-temperature su-
perconductors. The eﬀects of annealing is thereafter considered, to determine if crystallographic
texture transfer from the underlying metal substrate to the electroplated layer is possible, with
the aim of enabling the new metal substrate to be used in the high-temperature superconductor
RABiTS production process.

Introduction
Superconductivity was a phenomenon discovered in 1911 by Dutch physicist Heike Kamer-
lingh Onnes at Leiden laboratory. He was studying the intrinsic properties of metals at very low
temperatures, and surprisingly noticed that mercury cooled below 4 K, at a speciﬁc temperature
Tc, would experience a sudden drop of several orders of magnitude in electrical resistance [3]. It
was later shown that this was in fact zero resistance below Tc, rather than some micro-resistance
as Onnes believed. Lead and tin were also soon discovered to display the same characteristic at
distinct Tc, and the Leiden group predicted the capability of creating magnetic ﬁelds in excess
of 10 T [3]. However, they soon stumbled upon a problem; namely the critical current Ic. There
was a limit to the amount of current which could ﬂow in the superconductor before switching
to a normal conducting state, and therefore a limit to the generated magnetic ﬁeld. The next
breakthrough in superconductivity came in 1933, where Meissner and Ochsenfeld discovered that
if an external magnetic ﬁeld was applied above Tc, and the material was then cooled below Tc,
the superconductor would expel the magnetic ﬁeld. This was due to shielding currents arising
on the surface canceling the magnetic ﬁeld inside the superconductor, meaning the magnetic
susceptibility becomes -1, i.e. a perfect diamagnet. This is known as the Meissner phase [3].
These two deﬁning properties of a superconductor are shown in Figure 1.
Figure 1: Graphs over the two deﬁning properties of a superconductor. Left shows the drop of electrical
resistance below Tc, right shows the drop below Tc of susceptibility to that of a perfect diamagnet [3].
This was followed by an experimental discovery of a new class of superconductors in 1936
by Lev Shubnikov. This new class of materials would, above the critical ﬁeld Hc(T) deﬁned in the
Meissner phase, allow partial penetration of magnetic ﬂux lines, now called the vortex phase, in
very speciﬁc quantized amounts of h/2e. In 1950, the theory of the new type of superconductor
was made by Lev Landau and Vitaly Ginzburg, creating the Ginzburg-Landau theory, which was
much expanded upon by Alexei A. Abrikosov [3]. They theorized that superconductivity would
be suppressed inside these vortex lines over a diameter of ξ, known as the coherence length,
and the magnetic ﬁeld will at each ﬂux tube penetrate a length of λ, known as the London
penetration depth, into the superconductor. The penetration of these magnetic ﬂux tubes into
the superconductor occurs between magnetic ﬁelds deﬁned Hc1(T) to Hc2(T), above which the
superconductor would switch to the normal conducting phase. When a superconductor exhibits
both the Meissner phase and the vortex phase, it is a type II superconductor, the types which
only exhibit the Meissner phase are called Type I, shown in Figure 2. The typing is determined
from the ratio λ/ξ [3]. It took 46 years from the discovery of superconductivity by Onnes before
a quantum theory describing the cause of superconductivity emerged. This was in 1957, known
as the BCS theory, proposed by John Bardeen, Leon N. Cooper, and J. Robert Schrieﬀer. The
theory beautifully explained that the eﬀect of superconductivity was due to attraction between
two electrons, called Cooper pairs, via phonon-mediated electron coupling.

Another breakthrough came in 1986 when J. George Bednorz and K. Alex M¨uller of the
IBM laboratory discovered high temperature superconductors (HTS) [3]. They jointly discovered
that lanthanum barium copper oxide (LBCO) had a Tc of 35 K, much higher than anything
previously discovered. This led to much study of cuprate materials of ceramics formed from
transition metal oxides, with the highly anisotropic structure similar to LBCO, making way
for superconducting compounds such as bismuth strontium calcium copper oxide with a Tc of
107 K, and yttrium barium copper oxide with Tc of 93 K. Today superconductors are used for
a wide range of applications, such as magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) machines, nuclear
magnetic resonance (NMR) spectroscopy, electromagnets, accelerator magnets, magnetic ﬁeld
coils in tokamaks, power cables, etc.

YBa2Cu3O7−δ (YBCO) is one of the type II HTS cuprates typically used in coated con-
ductor (CC) technology. Besides having a very high critical temperature where liquid nitrogen
can be used for cooling, it can withstand very high magnetic ﬁelds, and additionally has a high
current density on the order of 106 A/cm2, even in liquid nitrogen temperatures. A comparison
between YBCO and NbTi of these critical parameters is shown in Figure 4, where superconduc-
tivity takes place at values below the colored surfaces. Clearly YBCO is superior in performance
and would greatly increase the possible magnetic ﬁeld, but the reason it is not widely used is
mainly fabrication cost. NbTi costs around $1/kA m at 4.2 K. Although there would be major
savings in the cryogenic system, since cooling to liquid helium is much more expensive than
liquid nitrogen, in order for YBCO, or any other HTS, to be competitive, the price needs to be
around $10/kA m at 77 K. Current estimates of the price from SuperPower are around 60$/kA m
for a 10 mm wide YBCO tape at 77 K [10]-[11].

YBCO is a perovskite structure, which is highly anisotropic, and the actual supercon-
ductivity takes place on the CuO2 planes. Figure 6 shows the unit cell of YBCO, where the
superconducting CuO2 planes are colored in. These planes need to be well-connected from one
YBCO unit cell to the neighboring unit cells, as the superconductive properties of YBCO is de-
pendent on plane alignment between the unit cells. The current density decreases exponentially
as a function of the misalignment angle. This is seen in Figure 7 showing diﬀerent types of plane
misalignments, and the eﬀect on Jc as a function of θ. In order to limit misalignment of the
superconducting planes, texturing of the thin YBCO layer is carried out via two diﬀerent meth-
ods; rolling assisted biaxially-textured substrates (RABiTS), or ion beam assisted deposition
(IBAD).

0.5-5 µm YBCO layer is then deposited, also using PLD at 10−1 mbar, and lastly the stabilizing
Ag layer is deposited using PVD at 10−5 mbar [15].
1.2
Alternating Current Energy Losses
If the CCs are used to conduct alternating current (AC), or are subject to a time varying
magnetic ﬁeld, they will be subject to AC energy losses. The superconductor will experience
multiple heat sources, which may impact the stability and performance. These energy losses are
coming from the voltage which is induced in the superconductor, and they can be split into two
major categories; coupling losses and hysteresis losses.
Figure 10: Screening current in a super-
conductor, reproduced from [4].
Coupling Losses
When applying a magnetic ﬁeld, screening cur-
rents are induced in the superconductor, additional to
the transport current from the power supply. Because
there is no electrical resistance in the superconductor,
these currents can, in principle, persist forever, and this
is a coupling Joule loss. They generate a magnetic ﬁeld
opposite to the applied ﬁeld, to reduce the change of
the magnetic ﬁeld inside of the superconductor. These
screening currents are proportional to the supercon-
ducting ﬁlament width, since they run in the supercon-
ducting layer, so reducing the ﬁlament width reduces
the induced magnetic ﬁeld [4]. This eﬀect also aﬀects
high ﬁeld direct current (DC) superconducting mag-
nets, as screening currents are induced during the initial
ramp up to the high magnetic ﬁeld. Creating smaller
ﬁlaments would therefore enable more ﬁeld-stable HTS
magnets.
Furthermore, coupling losses, Qc, occurs in parallel ﬁlaments connected via a conductive
metal matrix. Eddy currents, much as the screening currents induced in the superconductor to

Hysteresis Losses
Hysteresis losses, Qh, are caused by magnetic properties becoming irreversible, as ﬂux lines
are prevented from free motion due to ﬂux pinning [16]. Flux pinning is simply an eﬀect of defects
purposely introduced into the lattice to pin the ﬂux tubes penetrating type II superconductors
during the vortex phase. These ﬂux tubes move due to the Lorentz force, but pinning creates
a potential quantum well which requires energy to escape. Some thermal energy is therefore
needed to move the ﬂux tube out of the pinning site, so a higher Jc and Bc2 can be achieved 1.
Hysteresis loss occurs during cycling of the magnetization curve of the superconducting ﬁlaments,
since heat is released during this cyclic operation, and it is deﬁned as:
Qh = B · Jc · w · n
(1.3)
when combined with the Bean equation, and assuming the ﬁeld amplitude is much larger than
the penetration ﬁeld. Jc is the critical current, w is the width of the ﬁlament, and n is the
demagnetization factor [3]. Reducing the ﬁlament width is therefore the best way to reduce Qh
for optimal performance in a superconductor.
To reduce w in a CC, and thereby reduce both the induced screening currents and Qh,
top-down processes like laser striation and mechanical scribing are used to ﬁlament the tapes.
These ﬁlament techniques signiﬁcantly reduce the engineering current density JE, as each groove
is 70-75 µm wide [17], and a 12 mm tape can have anywhere from 12 to 48 ﬁlaments, thereby
eﬀectively removing 6.4% to 29.4% of the superconductor. The grooves also introduce potential
defects in the remaining superconducting layers at the border where striation or scribing took
place, thereby even further reducing JE.
This is a serious limitation on the ﬁlament width of YBCO in comparison to NbTi, which is
made via warm extrusion and cold working of a billet containing the pre-fabricated NbTi rods and
Cu support. This technique allows for twisting during fabrication to reduce coupling loss, and
enables ﬁlament diameters of just a few micrometers with upwards to thousands of ﬁlaments in
each cable. For MRI purposes, the ﬁlament diameter of NbTi is around 20 µm in a copper matrix,
with 50-100 ﬁlaments in each wire [3]. Since ﬁeld stability is of high importance, the ﬁlament
width of YBCO created via traditional top-down processes is simply not competitive compared
to NbTi. Too much of the superconducting layer is lost when creating thin ﬁlaments, and the
reduction of JE increases the price of the HTS, which is already far too high in comparison to
NbTi.

The motivation for this thesis is to produce metal substrates for the fabrication of multi-
ﬁlamentary CC superconducting tapes, while retaining a theoretically full width JE. If the full
width of the CC can be used, where ∼70 µm is not simply wasted to produce each ﬁlament, and
much thinner superconducting ﬁlaments can be made, YBCO could become more competitive
with NbTi, as it would allow higher stability of the ﬁeld, as well as decreasing the overall price
as JE increases.
This goal will be attempted by producing a metal substrate with two plateaus connected
via an undercut-proﬁle, as shown in Figure 12. The undercut-proﬁle is there to shade during
subsequent deposition of layers via physical vapor deposition (PVD) techniques, which would
allow the layers on the two plateaus to be in ﬁlaments while theoretically using the full width of
the CC. The idea is based on the two-level undercut-proﬁle substrate (2LUPS) concept [1]-[2],
but the plateaus are created via electroplating and tape masking to form the 3D surface-proﬁles,
as described in the patent [5]. The 2LUPS concept of the undercut-proﬁle was proven to work
via etching, but by depositing the plateaus rather than etching, there is less wastage of material.
This new tape masking and plating concept will therefore need to be tested. Furthermore, to
extend the use of this undercut-proﬁle to include the RABiTS process, the possibility of texture
transfer from the Ni-W metal substrate to the electroplated Ni layer is also investigated.
Hypotheses
This thesis will revolve around the production of these tapes, both with and without texture
for use in respectively the RABiTS and IBAD fabrication processes. This can be summed into
two main hypotheses which need to be answered:
1. Is the undercut-proﬁle created via the tape masking and electroplating method suﬃcient for
shading during PVD deposition of layers, i.e. will the undercut-proﬁle enable self-formed
ﬁlaments of the layers deposited on the plateaus?
2. Can the texture be transferred from the cube textured metal substrate to the electroplated
layer, aimed at use in the RABiTS process?

2.1
Process of Electroplating Nickel
Electroplating is used to deposit a metal coat or thin layer onto another metallic or con-
ductive surface via the use of electrochemical processes. By deﬁnition in electrochemistry, when
a redox half-reaction is occurring at an interface, the electrode where oxidation takes place is
called the anode, and the electrode where reduction takes place is called the cathode. [18]. The
substrate to be coated is the cathode of the setup, and therefore has a negative charge. The
cathode, along with the electrolyte, make up one half-cell in an electrolysis cell. The anode,
which is positively charged, is the metal used to make the ions that are deposited on the cath-
ode to produce the coat or layer, and it, together with the same electrolyte, make up the other
half-cell in the setup. A direct current (DC) ﬂowing in an external circuit connects the half-cells,
leading electrons from the anode to the cathode. The electrolyte, also called the plating bath, is
an aqueous solution containing the plating metal in oxidized form, either as an aquated cation
or a complex ion. A voltmeter and an ammeter should also be incorporated into the system,
to measure the voltage and current of the setup, as well as a means of controlling the voltage
across the electrodes and the current between them. Furthermore, the bath should be ﬁltrated
continuously to ﬁlter out any impurities, and a pump can be used to prevent sedimentation on
the bottom of the container as well as provide agitation. Figures 13 and 14 show respectively a
schematic diagram of the setup and a picture of the setup used in this project.

For this project, Ni was plated onto a Ni-W alloy, which had either been cold-rolled and
annealed to produce a strong cube texture, or only cold-rolled in which case it had rolling texture.
During the electrolysis process of nickel plating, the nickel goes through an oxidation process at
the anode, called an anodic half-cell reaction:
Ni(s) →Ni2+
(aq) + 2e−
(2.1)
and a reduction process at the cathode, called a cathodic half-cell reaction:
Ni2+
(aq) + 2e−→Ni(s)
(2.2)
Other reactions, such as the dissociation of water and the dissolution of an acid, HA, at
the anode also occur;
H2O →H+ + OH−
(2.3)
HA →H+ + A−
(2.4)
which contribute to the decrease in eﬃciency from the oxidation reaction at the anode to the
reduction at the cathode. This decrease is due to some of the electrons combining with H+
at the cathode to make hydrogen gas, which simply bubbles out of the system. This eﬃciency
decrease leads to an increase in the concentration of nickel ions in the electrolyte, as well as an
increase in pH due to the excess of OH−ions, which will have to be compensated for in the long
run. It should be mentioned that the hydrogen ion is very chemically unstable because of its
high aﬃnity for electrons. It will therefore react with the free electron pair of the oxygen atom
in the H2O molecule, forming a hydronium ion, H3O+. Thus equations 2.3 and 2.4 are better
described by the proton transfer reactions:
2H2O →H3O+ + OH−
(2.5)
HA + H2O →H3O+ + A−
(2.6)
The hydrogen ion does not stay with a single H2O molecule, but rather moves from one H2O to
the next. This eﬀect is due to the small size and mass of the hydrogen ion, which allows it to move
very fast through the electrolyte by jumping from one H2O molecule to the next, intermittently
creating new H3O−ions when it reacts with H2O. The overall movement of the hydrogen ion
looks as if H3O−is moving. However, because the hydrogen ion can move without having to
physically move the bigger H3O−molecule through all the molecules in the electrolyte, the
movement of the hydrogen ion occurs much faster [19]. This is called the Grotthuss mechanism.
The electrolyte used is called a Watts nickel plating solution, and with the addition of
organic brighteners, carriers, and wetters from SurTec [20], it becomes a bright nickel solution.
Brighteners help increase the ductility and leveling properties of the deposited nickel layer, used
in this case to decrease the surface roughness of the plated layer.
Carriers reﬁne the grain
structure and act as a basis for the brightener. Lastly, wetters function as anti-pitting agents
by decreasing the surface tension of the solution. The wetter, along with some agitation of the
cathode, is provided because hydrogen ions in the electrolyte are attracted to the negative charge
of the cathode, where they deposit as hydrogen gas bubbles after combination with electrons,
which will create pitting in the plated layer. Agitation, in this case in the form of the solution
being pumped onto the cathode surface from the ﬁltration system, and the decrease in surface
tension from the wetter, helps dislodge the hydrogen gas bubbles.

The initial mixture is heated to 40 ◦C and kept under constant stirring for 2 hours. The
desired additives are then added, and the bath is pumped continuously through a carbon ﬁlter
at 2-10 times the total bath volume per hour with a pore size of 5-15µm to ﬁlter out any solids
and prevent sedimentation at the bottom of the bath. After 24 hours, a dummy layer is plated
to check if the bath produces the desired results, and depending on the results, further additives
can be added if needed.
The additives used in this project included SurTec 850A, which is
a carrier and therefore acts as the basis for the system. Too little of this will create a matte
surface, whereas too much does not aﬀect the plating. SurTec 850 B was used, which is a leveling
agent that has the ability to decrease surface roughness. SurTec 850 L is the wetting agent, and
therefore reduces pitting by decreasing the contact angle. Initially some problems with pitting
was experienced, so the amount of SurTec 850L was increased from the suggested 3.0 mL/L to
8.0 mL/L, until the plated layer had no visible pitting. Lastly, SurTec 856, which is a brightener,
was added due to its additional leveling eﬀect. Too little of this leads to bad leveling, but too
much can lead to poor adhesion [20].
For the plating process, the pH of the bath should be in the range 4.0-4.4, the temperature
of the bath should be in the range between 40-60 ◦C, and the cathode current density in the range
0.5-2.0 A/dm2. To get consistent results, the pH was kept at 4.2, the temperature during plating
was 48-50 ◦C, and a current density of 1.0 A/dm2 was used in all experiments. The electrodes
were kept at a constant distance of 8 cm, since changing the distance will aﬀect the electrical ﬁeld
strength. A greater distance between the electrodes would require a higher voltage to get the
same result, but the electrodes should also not be too close, as the electric ﬁeld lines will bend
more near the edges of the electrodes, thereby depositing an uneven layer. The only remaining

2.3
Electroplating Process on an Atomic Scale
This section is heavily inspired by the electroplating book by N. Kanani [22, ch. 5]. The
process of electroplating described in the previous section and shown in Figure 13 only very
brieﬂy and broadly explain the process. A closer look at what is happening at the atomic level
is therefore necessary to better understand electroplating. The full process is very complex, but
it can, a bit simplistically, be broken down into ﬁve important steps:
1) Moving the hydrated metal ions from the bulk solution to the vicinity of the cathode.
2) Partial/full removal of the hydration sheath at the electrolyte/cathode interface.
3) Formation of adsorbed atoms due to charge transfer at the cathode surface.
4) Lateral surface diﬀusion of the adsorbed atoms on the cathode surface to form crystal nuclei.
5) Formation of the metallic layer via electrocrystallization and growth of the stable crystal
nuclei.

When a potential diﬀerence is applied across the electrodes, it will

metal ions, which are liberated in the electrolyte bath. Water molecules,

thereby forming a hydration sheath around the metal ion, as shown in

These hydration sheaths can be in several layers, but when
approaching the cathode, the metal ion typically loses the outer hydration
sheaths and only retains a single layer of surrounding water molecules.
Figure 16: Depiction of the ﬁve important processes at the cathode surface, reproduced from [22].
After being liberated from the anode, the metal ions travel to the cathode via three diﬀerent
ways: migration, convection, and diﬀusion. Migration is due to the electrical ﬁeld between the
charged electrodes, which makes cations, in this case metal ions, electrostatically attracted to the
cathode. The overall velocity of the ions is rather low, on the order of micrometers per second,
due to collisions with solvent molecules and viscous drag of the liquid, which makes the overall
contribution to transport via migration rather low. Convection, or agitation, is the movement of
the metal ions with the electrolyte. Agitation of the substrate being plated, whether by stirring
the bath, or, in the case of this project, pointing the outlet from the ﬁltration pump towards the
cathode, both helps transport metal ions towards the cathode, and prevents hydrogen bubbles
from adhering to the surface of the cathode, thereby avoiding pitting.
The metal ions can
only approach the cathode surface until they reach a certain boundary, known as the diﬀusion
layer. The thickness of the diﬀusion layer, also known as the Nernst layer, is determined by
the amount of convection. Without any convection, the diﬀusion layer is around 200 µm, but

2.4
Crystal Structure, Grain Boundaries, and Annealing
Periodic arrangements of atoms determine the crystal structures of crystalline materials.
To describe a crystal plane, the Miller indices (hkl) are used, while the direction of the plane may
be given by the vector <uvw>. Thus (hkl)<uvw> can be used to describe a crystallographic
orientation [23]. A grain has the same crystallographic orientation throughout the entire volume,
and a grain boundary (GB) is deﬁned as the region of atomic mismatch between two adjacent
grains. To ﬁnd the misorientation between two grains, ﬁrst the crystal coordinate system of
each grain is transferred to the specimen coordinate system using a g-vector e.g. given in Euler
angles. The misorientation angle between two grains in the specimen coordinate system is then
deﬁned as the smallest angle by which the two grains would align to have the same orientation
[24]. Polycrystalline materials consist of many grains, and if they have preferred crystallographic
orientations, it is said that they have texture [25]. The degree of texture, ranging from weak to
moderate to strong, depends on the percentage of crystals in the material aligning according to
the preferential orientation. For superconducting applications, >98% of the grains must have
the same orientation and at least 90% of those grains must have a <10◦misorientation angle
(see Figure 7), in order for the metal to be deﬁned as having a very strong texture [23].

Slip and Twin Deformation
Deformation of a polycrystalline metal leads to a
subdivision of the individual grains relative to the ap-
plied stress, and since this change is not random, but in-
volves rotations directly related to the crystallography,
the grains acquire a preferential orientation. This pre-
ferred rolling texture only becomes stronger the more
the metal is deformed [27].
Two possible ways plastic deformation occurs in
cubic metals, is via slip and twinning, and they both
occur on speciﬁc crystalline planes and in certain crys-
tallographic directions. Slip is a permanent relative dis-
placement of a part of the crystal, and it happens by
sliding one plane of atoms an integral number of atomic
distances across from another plane. In a face-centered
cubic (fcc) structure, slip occurs in the (111)<110>
crystallographic orientation, which is the most densely packed plane and direction. For a single
crystal, the orientation of the crystal above and below the slip plane remains the same after
slip deformation. In polycrystalline materials the slip planes and directions are diﬀerent from
one grain to the next, so the critical resolved shear stress, i.e. the smallest stress at which the
material irreversibly changes dimensions via plastic deformation, also changes from one grain
to another [28]. GBs impede slipping as the slip directions of crystals oriented favorably may
change when they cross a GB. Thus the strength of polycrystalline materials is higher compared
to single-crystal materials.
Unlike slip, where atoms move an integer number of atomic spaces, twinning occurs by
atoms moving fractional atomic spaces, leading to a rearrangement of the lattice structure. For
a single crystal, the orientation of the crystal changes in such a way that the distorted part
becomes a mirror image of the non-distorted part. Twin deformation therefore causes orienta-
tion diﬀerence in the crystal across the twin plane. For an fcc structure, twinning occurs in the
(111)<112> crystallographic orientation. Coherent boundaries have one-to-one matching lattice
bonds between the grains, whereas incoherent boundaries consist of all other ratios. There are
special types of GBs called Σ boundaries, which denotes the degree of ﬁt between the structures
of the two grains. The value assigned to Σ describes the reciprocal of the ratio of coincidence
lattice sites to the total number of sites in the lattice [27]. Speciﬁcally Σ3 boundaries have a
misorientation angle of 60◦[23] and are considered twin boundaries.
Annealing Process
Most of the work used for shaping (i.e.
deforming) a metal is lost as heat, but ∼1%
remains as energy stored in the metal. The release of this stored energy during the annealing
process is the driving force for recovery and recrystallization. Cold-rolling (CR) is a process
where the shape and structure of the metal is changed to a deformed state by rolling it, thus
reducing the thickness at a temperature well below the recrystallization temperature, typically
room temperature for convenience. This changes the tensile strength and hardness of the metal,
but reduces ductility.
To get a strong cube texture, which has the ideal orientation (001)<100> in Ni, a very
high total thickness reduction of >90% is needed [23]. To recrystallize the metal, an annealing
process is needed. Figure 23 shows diﬀerent stages during the annealing process. A deformed,

ture. The dislocations rearrange themselves or annihilate with each other, but the GBs are not
signiﬁcantly aﬀected. This is followed by the recrystallization stage, during which new grains,
largely without dislocations, are formed. These new grains will grow until they consume the

GB Anchoring Mechanism
A groove may develop in a polycrystalline substrate at GBs in contact with a gas interface
during annealing at elevated temperatures [30]. Material accumulates on each side of the groove
due to surface diﬀusion, as seen in Figure 24. The depth of the groove, d, is related to the
decrease in the area of the GB surface (the grey area in Figure 24), which reduces the free
energy of that plane. The annealing time and temperature, the surface energy, and the GB
energy will aﬀect the depth and width of the thermal groove [23]. The thermal grooves can
limit GB migration at the surface, as they can act as anchoring points depending on dihedral
angle β of the groove. If a certain critical angle βc is exceeded, boundary surface migration may
occur, thereby changing the position of the GB. Thus, while a GB is anchored, it will develop
grooves during annealing, until it reaches a critical dihedral angle βc, after which the GB will
start moving and develop a groove at a new position. Doping a material, in this case Ni to
Ni-5 at.%W, will slow down the grain growth, since the tungsten atoms, which act as pinning
points in the lattice, gather at the GBs, thereby limiting their movement and growth.

on the surface and below the undercut-proﬁle, the mean free path is smaller than the height
diﬀerence of the two plateaus, as shown in Figure 28.
If a pressure of 15 mbar is used, the
corresponding temperature for the silver is around 1600 ◦C [36], and the calculated mean free
path is around 30 µm. This amount of pressure is considered to be a medium vacuum [34].
Case 2: λmfp > h
The second case, where λmfp is bigger than the height of the plateaus, but still on the same
order of magnitude, seen in Figure 29, some of the particles will still deposit on the underside of
the undercut-proﬁle, shown as path a), but most of the particles will deposit on the ﬂat surface
as shown in path b). Essentially, only a layer a few nanometers thick needs to be deposited
between the plateaus for current to be able to run through, so even a deposition percentage of
around 1% of the thickness of Ag being deposited on the plateau will ruin the purpose of the
undercut-proﬁle. If a pressure of 1.2 mbar is chosen, the corresponding temperature of the silver
is around 1300 ◦C, and the mean free path will be around 307 µm, or about 10 times larger
than the height of the plateau. This would still lead to a noticeable deposition beneath the
undercut-proﬁle, but the pressure is again only considered a medium vacuum.
Case 3: λmfp ≫h
To avoid any deposition on the underside, the mean free path should be of the order of the
chamber length, or longer, as shown in Figure 30. The chamber length of the PVD system is
22 cm in this case, so if a pressure of 1.3 · 10−3 mbar is used, which is considered the beginning
of a high vacuum, the corresponding temperature of the silver is 920 ◦C, which will give a mean
free path of 21.5 cm. Very little should deposit beneath the undercut-proﬁle for this case, but
to be on the safe side, pressures lower than 1.3 · 10−3 mbar should be used.
The Experimental System:
For the PVD process depositing Ag via thermal evaporation, the pressure measured in the
chamber was 0.6-0.7 · 10−3 mbar, corresponding to a temperature of the silver atoms leaving the
source of around 880 ◦C [36]. This corresponds to a λmfp on the order of 38.6-45.1 cm, which is
longer than the distance of 22 cm between the sample and the source. The atoms at this vapor
pressure and temperature therefore, on average, will not experience collision before deposition,
since they will travel straight from the source to the sample. However, experimental experience
with the PVD system indicates that the Ag source is in a liquid state when vaporized, meaning
the temperature and pressure is at least around 962 ◦C and 3.6 · 10−3 mbar. As mentioned, the
underestimate of the pressure stems from the pressure gradient in the chamber, which is higher
when close to the source compared to the chamber walls. Using those values, λmfp is at most
around 8.1 cm. This is not exactly ideal, since it is less than the length of the chamber, but very
little should deposit beneath the undercut-proﬁle since it is on the same order as the chamber
length, and still more than 3 orders of magnitude longer than the plateau height.

Methodology and Experimental Procedures
This section ﬁrst describes the step-by-step procedures used to fabricate plateaus. This
is followed by a more in-depth explanation of each step. To create the metal substrate with
the two-levels of plateaus, and to ensure that the undercut-proﬁle is suﬃcient for enabling self-
ﬁlamentization during deposition of the subsequent layers, the steps are as follows:
1. Start with a metal substrate, in this case recrystallized Ni-W. Cut kapton tape to have the
right proﬁle, and apply it to the Ni-W on the areas which needs to be masked. Electroplate
Ni with the desired thickness to create the undercut proﬁle.
2. Check if the electroplated Ni layer has suﬃcient adhesion to the Ni-W metal substrate.
3. Use atomic force microscopy (AFM) to determine surface roughness of the sample.
4. Use focused ion beam scanning electron microscope (FIBSEM) and light optical microscope
(LOM) to check the proﬁle of the undercut, surface uniformity, and defects of the sample.
5. PVD of electrically insulating SiO layer, followed by electrically conductive Ag layer.
6. Use a four-point probe setup to make I/V characteristics between the plateaus and for
each individual plateau, thereby determining the resistance of the insulating SiO layer.
7. Use FIBSEM again to look at the deposited layers, and visually conﬁrm that they do not
connect.
To evaluate if texture transfer during an annealing process is possible, step 2 and 3 is the
same as above, and the remaining steps are as follows:
1. Start with two samples: recrystallized Ni-W and deformed Ni-W. Cleaning, tape masking,
and electroplating is the same as above.
4. Carry out electron backscatter diﬀraction (EBSD) of sample to determine texture of the
lower Ni-W and upper electrodeposited Ni plateaus.
5. Anneal both samples to ensure grain growth of the nanocrystalline electrodeposited Ni,
ideally with texture transfer from the Ni-W metal substrate. Use LOM to check grain
sizes.
6. Determine if the texture of the plateaus after annealing have a strong cube texture ideal
for superconductor fabrication.
3.1
Plating the 3D Surface Proﬁle
To make the two-level plateau metal substrate with the undercut-proﬁle for CC tapes, a
commercial Evico GmbH Ni-5 at.%W base metal substrate tape, referred to as Ni-W from here
on, with a thickness of approximately 67-68 µm is used. The Ni-W base metal substrate used
in this project is made by melting the individual substrate sample materials together (nickel
and tungsten) in an induction oven, then isotropically forging it into a bar shape. This bar
is cold-rolled with a very high thickness reduction of ∼98% to create the strong cube texture
needed. The metal tapes can then be annealed to reach the fully recrystallized state. The Ni-W
metal substrate used in this project are received as either deformed or fully recrystallized, and
thereafter cleaned in an ultrasonication bath ﬁrst with acetone, then with ethanol, and dried
with an air drying gun. Kapton tape, a special type of polyimide with a silicone adhesive which
can withstand high temperatures up to 300 ◦C, is applied to the area where plating is not desired,
such as the backside and the future lower plateaus.
==PAGE:38==

The 3D surface proﬁle shown in Figure 12 is created using kapton tape. The proﬁle is
cut using a special blade angled 45◦, but the adhesive does not hold the shape of the cut, so
electroplating must take place to a thickness where the carrier is reached. The kapton tape was
heated to 250 ◦C, and mechanical force was applied perpendicular in a rolling motion to the
surface to ﬂatten the tape and remove any air bubbles.
The metal substrate with the masking tape is thereafter connected as the cathode in the
electroplating bath, and a suitably thick layer is plated onto the substrate. The current density
throughout all experiments in this report is kept at 1.0 A/dm2, so the layer thickness is controlled
by varying the plating time. After the plating process, the metal substrate is cleaned again using
an ultrasonication bath with acetone for 30 min. and ethanol for 5 min. The acetone dissolves
the silicone adhesive on the kapton tape, thereby making the carrier easy to peel oﬀwithout
deforming the 3D proﬁle of the electroplated Ni layer.
3.2
Adhesion Testing
Adhesion between the base metal substrate and the plated layers is very important to pre-
vent the upper plateau from simply peeling oﬀ. When the superconductor is experiencing a high
magnetic ﬁeld, the Lorentz force will act upon it, which might delaminate the layers if adhesion
is not high enough. There is no standard way of measuring adhesion of an electroplated layer
to the substrate underneath, and most of the methods used for testing adhesion are destructive
[37]. A peel test used by SuperPower determines the peel strength in N/cm from the peeling
load as a function of displacement curve. This test can be carried out at various angles, which
changes the stress state at the peeling front [38].
The method used in this project is a simpliﬁed version of the peel test by SuperPower,
but it does not give an estimate of the peel strength. It was done by applying a piece of duct
tape on top of the electrodeposited layer and pulling it oﬀby hand in a 90◦angle. The ﬁrst test
was done slowly, and if the plated layer adhered to the surface, another piece of duct tape was
applied and pulled oﬀwith a much greater speed. Additionally, the metal substrate was bent
back and forth ﬁve or six times to form circular loops with a diameter of approximately 3 cm. If
the electroplated layers could withstand both tests and not crack or peel oﬀ, then the layer was
considered to have a good enough adhesion to the metal substrate underneath.

3.4
FIBSEM and LOM for Milling and Imaging
Figure 32: Diagram of FIBSEM setup [43].
To investigate the surface proﬁle without having potential sample preparation eﬀects, a
focused ion beam (FIB) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM) is used. The FIBSEM is a
Zeiss CrossBeam 1540XB with Zeiss SE2 and Zeiss Inlens detectors. A SEM works by guiding
a beam of focused electrons towards the sample, which either interacts with the atoms on the
surface of the sample, or go deeper into the sample. If the exit energy of the electron is < 50 eV,
then they are called secondary electrons, and are typically the ones interacting with the surface
of the sample. The higher energy electrons are called back-scattered electrons, which usually
comes from deeper in the sample. There are two detectors in the SEM used, one positioned
at the same point as the beam of incoming electrons, called an in-lens detector, which mainly
==PAGE:40==

Methodology and Experimental Procedures

detect secondary electrons, and one towards the side which detect all the electrons. A SEM
typically has a resolution of around 1 nm. The SEM is used both to look at the surface of the
sample to check for uniformity and ensure that no pitting has taken place during electroplating
of Ni, and to investigate the undercut-proﬁle after milling. For imaging, an accelerating voltage
of either 5 kV or 10 kV was used, with aperture sizes of either 30 µm or 120 µm, depending on
which combination gave the clearest images.
The FIB works by sending out a beam of gallium ions which sputter away some of the
sample, thereby creating ions, electrons, and neutral atoms, of which the electrons are detected.
When the beam current used is high, e.g. 5 nA or 10 nA used in this project, then the FIB can
be used to mill the sample, actively removing the sample to a desired depth, so the internal
structure can be viewed. The material removed will need to re-deposit somewhere, and this
typically happens on the other side of the hole being dug and not in places actively milled. For
this project, the FIB is used to mill away a portion of the sample between the two plateaus,
so the undercut-proﬁle can be seen with the SEM. The beam is not always very well-deﬁned,
so a halo surrounding the beam will mill the sample around the desired place. The beams are
generally chosen with a halo that is as small as possible and behind the front side of the milling
direction, so a well-deﬁned cross-section can be seen. The tilt angle between the SEM beam and
the cross-section which appears after milling is 36◦for all SEM images.
Furthermore, for imaging the diﬀerent levels with the undercut-proﬁle, a light optical
microscope (LOM) was also used. The samples were cast in epoxy, and a ∼1 mm thick layer is
removed via sanding. This method is used since the edge has not been aﬀected by being cut
with a scissor. The proﬁle can then be viewed using a Leitz GmbH Aristomet microscope, with
a Leica MC120 HD microscope camera to take pictures.
3.5
Thermal Evaporation PVD
To check if the plateaus are divided into subsequent ﬁlaments when the buﬀer layers,
superconductive layer, silver layer, and copper layer are all deposited, an electrically insulating
layer simulating the buﬀer layers is deposited via PVD, followed by an electrically conductive
layer simulating the superconducting YBCO layer. This is done with an Alcatel PVD system.
To create the layers, a ∼350 nm SiO2 layer was deposited on the nickel via thermal evaporation
using PVD. The pressure used for PVD of SiO changed from 1.1 · 10−3 mbar to 0.7 · 10−3 mbar
from the beginning to the end of the process. The change occurs because the SiO captures some
of the particles in the chamber, thereby adding them to the deposited layer as impurities, which
lowers the pressure of the chamber over time. The SiO layer was followed by thermal evaporation
of a ∼400 nm Ag layer via PVD in an initial chamber pressure of 0.07 · 10−3 mbar, which at the
end of the deposition was lowered to 0.06 · 10−3 mbar.
For the PVD processes of depositing Ag via thermal evaporation, the temperature of the
silver atoms leaving the source is around 880 ◦C [36], determined from the vapor pressure which
changed from 0.07 to 0.06 · 10−3 mbar. This gives a λmfp of the silver atoms on the order of
38.6-45.1 cm. However, as mentioned in section 2.5, the Ag source is liquid when vaporized, so
the temperature and pressure of the source must at least be around 962 ◦C and 3.6 · 10−3 mbar,
leading to a λmfp of at most 8.1 cm. This is not the best situation as the chamber length is
22 cm, but deposition beneath the undercut-proﬁle should still be very limited. If the plateaus
are still in ﬁlaments during these conditions, they will deﬁnitely be in ﬁlaments at the lower
chamber pressures used in superconductor fabrication.

3.8
Annealing Process
For the annealing process to transfer texture from the Ni-W to the electroplated Ni layer,
an oven with a max temperature of 1200 ◦C was used. There was no speciﬁed temperature ramp,
and the samples would simply be placed in the oven when the oven had heated up to the needed
temperature. Once the duration for the annealing process was reached, the samples were taken
out and allowed to cool in room temperature.
Two diﬀerent annealing atmospheres were used in this project; forming gas and air. The
annealing process using forming gas, a mixture of nitrogen with 5 mol-% hydrogen, was used since
this combination is an inert gas, meaning it will not react with the metal. It is also typically
used in annealing processes because it decreases oxide growth on the surface of the metal for
any oxygen still in the reducing atmosphere. On the other hand, annealing in air will develop
an intentional oxide layer on the surface, and the thickness of this oxide layer can be controlled
via the annealing temperature and time [46].

4.1

linear ﬁtting of the data to estimate the slope since the resistance follows Ohm’s law. Ideally
the y-intercept of the curves should be at 0, since no current is sent through the sample, but
the voltmeter will have noise, which is in this case detected as 0.5 µV. The uncertainty of the
measurements stems from the small changes in the voltage after the current was applied.
The resistance between the plateaus is 25.9 Ω, which is the total resistance. The red and
blue curves is the resistance across a single plateau to the back of the sample, therefore only
showing the resistance of a single SiO layer. The red and blue resistances, respectively 14.4 Ω
and 10.5 Ω, should add up to the total resistance across both plateaus. With the uncertainties
included in Figure 43, the resistances do overlap with the value for the total resistance, so the
plateaus are considered to be in ﬁlaments. The resistance of the blue and red path is not the
same as would be expected from layers of equal thickness. This could possibly be because the
area of the upper plateau was smaller, since a mask was used when depositing SiO and Ag
to avoid deposition at the edge of the sample, as seen in Figure 33 from Section 3.6 which
includes the area of the deposited layers.
However, another cause could be if the thickness
varied across the surface of the top plateau, for example at the edge of the undercut-proﬁle, or
if there was some impurities on the top plateau, which would make the thickness at that point
less, thereby reducing the resistance of the top plateau. This was not further investigated, as
the results obtained from the resistance measurement indicated that the layers on the plateaus
are electrically insulated.

Section Summary
Using standard kapton tape with an adhesive layer thickness of 25 µm, sample K25 was
made and FIB milled. Another part of the sample had PVD deposition of SiO and Ag, followed
by four-point probe measurements to determine if the Ag layers were electrically insulated. The
resistance between two Ag layers on diﬀerent plateaus was found, and the resistance measure-
ments indicated that the Ag layers on the plateaus were electrically insulated, i.e. in ﬁlaments.
The answer to hypothesis 1, regarding whether tape masking and electroplating would create
a suﬃcient shading eﬀect for subsequent PVD of layers for multi-ﬁlamentary superconductor
tapes, is therefore yes. However, an optimal and ﬂow free undercut-proﬁle was not achieved in
this project. The main obstacle to creating an undercut-proﬁle with no bulge or protrusion was
identiﬁed to be related to the adhesive layer of the masking tape. In sample K25 with standard
kapton tape, the adhesive layer would contract after cutting, and a corresponding bulge would
be seen in the plated layer, as seen in Figure 44. The thickness of the adhesive layer is directly
related to the height of the bulge, as seen in Figure 49 of sample K15, where the bulge was 15 µm
corresponding to the 15 µm thick adhesive layer. A proﬁle created with standard kapton tape
will therefore have a bulge which extends beyond the tip of the undercut-proﬁle. To circumvent
this problem, a method of spray painting a thin adhesive layer onto cut kapton carrier was at-
tempted for sample KS. This adhesive did not have suﬃcient adhesion between the carrier and
the metal, so despite a sharp undercut-proﬁle, there was still deposition beneath the masking
tape seen in Figure 52. The protrusions created were 10 µm thick, and extended up to 100 µm
beyond the tip of the undercut-proﬁle. Lastly, a method of spraying a rubber coating on the
Ni-W to form a mask, cutting the rubber mask on the metal substrate, peeling away the strips
not needed, and plating Ni was investigated. This method yielded a good undercut-proﬁle, but
with a very thin layer of Ni deposited beneath the rubber mask, as seen in Figure 55 of sample
RS. This layer was only a few µm thick, and extended at most 50 µm beyond the edges of the
upper plateau. The method used in sample RS gave the best proﬁle achieved in this project,
and would have a higher JE compared to state-of-the-art ﬁlamentization techniques which use
70-75 µm per ﬁlament [17].

4.2
Nickel Plated with Texture Transfer
For the second hypothesis, to test if texture can be transferred, the metal substrates used
are (A.) recrystallized Ni-W and (B.) deformed Ni-W. The ﬁrst metal substrate, referred to as
sample R for recrystallized (because it has been deformed and annealed), is soft and extremely
malleable since it has a high ductility but a low tensile strength [42], which unfortunately makes
it diﬃcult to work with. The second metal substrate, referred to as sample D for deformed
(since it is in a deformed state), will be hard, since it has a high tensile strength but a low
ductility compared to the non-deformed state. This hardness makes it easier to work with, as
it will not easily bend, and can tolerate a higher force before it reaches plastic deformation. It
is also suggested that the deformed Ni-W will not experience signiﬁcant formation of slip lines
by the edge of the knife track which is used for cutting the masking material compared to the
recrystallized state [2].
The metal substrates were cleaned and prepared as described in Section 3.1. Kapton tape
with a total thickness of 40 µm was used as the tape mask and the Ni was plated to a total
thickness of 30 µm with no regard for cross-sectional proﬁle since only texture is of interest. It
needs to be investigated if the electroplated 30 µm Ni can gain the texture of the underlying
Ni-W metal during an annealing process, since it has previously been proven to work for very
thin Ni layers (see Section 3.8 on texture transfer). For the deformed metal substrate, which has
a rolling texture, the strong cube texture will develop during annealing at around 1050 ◦C [23].
The aim of using this deformed metal substrate will be to test if the strong cube texture which
develops during annealing can be transferred to the electroplated Ni layer in the process. For
the recrystallized metal substrate, which already has a strong cube texture, it needs to be tested
if the Ni can acquire the same strong cube texture as the metal substrate underneath during
an annealing process. To distinguish the diﬀerent layers, the Ni-W and electroplated Ni layers
for e.g. the deformed sample will respectively be called sample D:Ni-W and sample D:Ni, even
though the electroplated Ni itself is not in a deformed state.

4.2.3
Annealing Process
For the RABiTS fabrication process described in Section 1.1, to create the proper buﬀer
layers and YBCO layer, a pre-treatment temperature of 650 ◦C for 90 min. followed by the actual
annealing process at 1050 ◦C for 120 min. is needed for the deformed Ni-W to obtain a strong
cube texture. The annealing takes place in a forming gas atmosphere, as described in Section
3.8. The electroplated Ni layer needs to be stable at the temperatures used, with little grain
growth after the desired texture has been transferred. The ﬁrst annealing step was carried out
at 650 ◦C for 90 mins, and the sample was allowed to cool in room temperature. Inspection of
the Ni surface revealed huge grains on both samples R:Ni and D:Ni, with considerable thermal
grooving between the GBs. Initially it was thought that the samples had cracked, but a closer
view with LOM proved this to simply be very large grains, as seen in Figure 64.

lower plateau can also be seen. The grains of the Ni should, ideally, be the same size as those of the
Ni-W.
Clearly the Ni experienced an abnormal grain growth phase at some stage during the an-
nealing. The huge grains are elongated, dispersed with smaller grains nestled in-between. These
grains are very unusual, as the thickness of the thin ﬁlm typically limits the size of the grains.
Some of these are up to 100 times longer than the thickness of the 30 µm plated Ni layer. No
visible diﬀerence in these abnormal grains were seen between sample D:Ni compared to R:Ni,
which might be expected as D:Ni-W has rolling texture compared to the strong cube texture of
R:Ni-W. EBSD mapping was not carried out on these grains, because abnormal grains normally
do not belong to the (001)<100> cube orientation [27], and would therefore not be suitable for
CC fabrication.

Literature Comparison
Some literature [50]-[53] suggests that electrodeposited nanocrystalline Ni is very unstable,
and can in some cases experience recrystallization at room temperature. At higher temperatures,
a stage of grain growth and abnormal grain growth occurs, which then at around 500-600 ◦C
goes towards an equilibrium state. The stage of abnormal grain growth varies depending on the
temperature used and the duration of annealing. The additives used in the Watts bath will also
aﬀect the diﬀerent grain growth stages, and the electroplating conditions such as pH, tempera-
ture, and current density, will aﬀect the initial mode of growth, leading to deposits exhibiting
certain structural properties [54], as mentioned in the theory Section 2.1. This all suggests that
for electrodeposited nanocrystalline Ni, it is nearly impossible to avoid experiencing a stage of
abnormal grain growth if annealing temperatures as high as 1050 ◦C are employed. This abnor-
mal grain growth stage will undermine any attempts of transferring or creating a strong cube
texture in the annealed Ni, as grains are desired in the same order of magnitude as the grains in
the recrystallized Ni-W base metal substrate. It is interesting to note that this electrodeposited
nanocrystalline Ni layer experienced grain growth of 5-6 orders of magnitude.
Texture Transfer Conclusion
High purity Ni has been used as the base metal substrate in YBCO superconductor fabri-
cation before alloying Ni became the norm, but that required a cold-rolling step to deform the
metal, leading the strong cube texture to develop during annealing [55]. The diﬀerence in the
explosive grain growth of the nanocrystalline state opposed to the more normal grain growth
experienced by the deformed state during annealing could stem from the diﬀerences in where
the energy is stored in the metal. For the nanocrystalline state, the free energy is stored as
interfacial GB energy, whereas the energy of the deformed metal is stored mainly as dislocation
GB energy in the grains [56]. Therefore, the stored energy of the nanocrystalline state will be
much higher compared to the deformed state, as there are many more GBs in a nanocrystalline
substrate. This diﬀerence might account for why nanocrystalline Ni experiences an abnormal
growth stage at a much lower temperature than deformed Ni. In sight of the experimental results
which yielded abnormally large grains, and the literature suggesting an abnormal grain growth
stage at comparatively low annealing temperatures, the answer to hypothesis 2 is therefore that
texture transfer from the Ni-W metal substrate cannot be induced in the electroplated nanocrys-
talline Ni. The D:Ni-W layer requires very elevated temperatures to recrystallize, which would
lead to abnormal grain growth in the D:Ni layer. Furthermore, texture also cannot be transferred
from the R:Ni-W layer to the electroplated R:Ni layer, since CC RABiTS fabrication techniques,
such as the one American Superconductor Corporation Inc. uses, requires 700 ◦C for the PVD
deposition process of the initial buﬀer layers [14].
4.2.4
Surface Roughness of Annealed Ni
Unfortunately, the result of the annealing at 650 ◦C brings up another problem; thermal
grooving. As concluded, the electroplated Ni cannot be used for the RABiTS fabrication process.
However, the IBAD process also requires some high temperatures fabrication steps to deposit the
diﬀerent layers [15][57]. For the deposition of the barrier layer, seed layer, and the IBAD-MgO
layer, the PVD and PLD depositions occur at room temperature. The Ni would not change
considerably during this room temperature deposition, but these layers have a total thickness
of only 43-120 nm [15].
This is followed by epitaxial e-beam deposition of a MgO layer at
temperatures ranging from 300-600 ◦C, which will lead to an abnormal grain growth stage in
the electroplated Ni layer. Furthermore, the PLD deposition of the LaMnO3 buﬀer layer and
superconducting YBCO layer is deposited at temperatures ranging from 700 ◦C to 800 ◦C. For
the IBAD process, the texture of the metal substrate is irrelevant, but the metal substrate still
needs to have a low surface roughness. In Figure 64 the GBs seem to have experienced signiﬁcant
thermal grooving, which would immensely increase the surface roughness. To determine how
deep the gain boundaries are, AFM measurements across the GBs are investigated.

Annealing in Air
A possible solution to the increase in surface roughness due to abnormal grain growth,
is to instead anneal the Ni in air. This will create an oxide layer on the surface of the Ni. As
mentioned in Section 1.1, the buﬀer layers prevent oxygen diﬀusion, so after the buﬀer layers have
been deposited, oxygen will not be able to diﬀuse from the NiO layer into the superconducting
layer. To create the oxide layer, diﬀerent annealing temperatures and times were investigated
for diﬀerent parts of sample R. This sample was chosen because both R:Ni and R:Ni-W had a
lower surface roughness compared to D:Ni and D:Ni-W. To distinguish the annealed samples,
the diﬀerent layers will be referred to as Nitemp,time and Ni-Wtemp,time, where temp and time
refers to the annealing temperature and time.

Section Summary
To determine if texture transfer from the Ni-W to the electroplated Ni is possible, two
types of Ni-W were investigated; deformed Ni-W and recrystallized Ni-W. After Ni deposition,
EBSD mapping of the recrystallized Ni-W was done, and it was concluded that the deposition
process does not signiﬁcantly inﬂuence the texture of the Ni-W. The metal substrates were
annealed, and upon investigation of the Ni surface, signiﬁcant abnormal grain growth was seen.
Literature suggests that electrodeposited nanocrystalline Ni will experience a stage of abnormal
grain growth at low temperatures, which prevents texture transfer in the RABiTS fabrication
process, since it uses higher temperatures.
The conclusion to hypothesis 2 is therefore that
texture transfer is not possible from the Ni-W to the electroplated Ni layer for the use in the
RABiTS CC fabrication process.
Furthermore, the surface roughness of the annealed Ni might have problems for the IBAD
fabrication, which for some deposition steps require high temperatures. It is diﬃcult to conclude
if this is the case, since the barrier layer, seed layer, and IBAD-MgO are all deposited at room
temperature. These layers might prevent the abnormal grain growth of the Ni from signiﬁcantly
aﬀecting the topography of the structure during e.g. the epitaxial MgO deposition at 300-600 ◦C.
But for the sake of determining a worst case scenario, it was assumed that it did have an eﬀect,
and an attempt to reduce the surface roughness via annealing in air to grow an oxide layer on the
Ni and Ni-W was carried out. The overall surface roughnesses of all the annealed samples were
too high for CC IBAD fabrication, even for the 600 ◦C sample. However, the eﬀect of growing a
thin oxide layer, at a much lower temperature, and then annealing the sample in a forming gas at
a higher temperature, might give some sort of decrease in the surface topography of the sample.
One could also look into the eﬀect of simply growing a thin oxide layer at a lower temperature,
and then starting the IBAD fabrication process. This might reduce the overall change in surface
roughness compared to the samples annealed in air, but the extent of this eﬀect is diﬃcult to
determine without the actual deposition of buﬀer layers.

Conclusion

5
Conclusion
The goals of this project are condensed into two hypotheses; 1) whether tape masking and
electroplating can create a undercut-proﬁle that can shade during subsequent PVD deposition
of layers, thereby enabling the production of multi-ﬁlamentary CCs, and 2) whether texture can
be transferred from the base Ni-W metal substrate to the electroplated Ni layer via annealing.
Hypothesis 1
The ﬁrst hypothesis was investigated by making 2LUPS via tape masking and electroplat-
ing Ni onto a Ni-W metal substrate. This new metal substrate had an electrically insulating
layer of SiO and an electrically conductive layer of Ag deposited via thermal evaporation, to
simulate the deposition of buﬀer layers and YBCO layer in CC fabrication. By measuring the
voltage as a function of applied current via a four-point probe setup, the resistance of the lay-
ers on each individual plateau was measured and compared to the total resistance across both
plateaus. The resistance from each of the individual layers should theoretically add up to the
total resistance across both plateaus. The experimentally measured values of (14.4 ± 0.3) Ωand
(10.5 ± 0.4) Ωfor the individual plateaus corresponds well with the measured total resistance
of (25.9 ± 0.5) Ω, which indicates that the deposited layers on the two plateaus are electrically
insulated, and therefore in ﬁlaments. To visually conﬁrm the separation of layers, FIBSEM and
LOM images of the shading eﬀect from the undercut-proﬁle in the layers were also shown.
The undercut-proﬁle of sample K25 did not look as expected due to a bulge in the elec-
troplated Ni layer, which comes from the contraction of the adhesive layer in the masking tape.
The bulge would undermine the eﬀect of the undercut-proﬁle, as subsequent deposition of Ag
and Cu used in CC fabrication, with a total height of ∼25 µm, would electrically connect the
plateaus. To counteract this bulge, a masking tape with a thinner adhesive layer of 15 µm was
used to make sample K15, but this lead to a 15 µm bulge that would connect the plateaus.
Another method of spray painting adhesive onto a kapton carrier was attempted for sample
KS. This yielded an undercut-proﬁle with no bulge, but due to lift-oﬀon the edges of the car-
rier, thick protrusions of Ni up to 100 µm wide deposited beneath the masking tape. Lastly,
a method of using a rubber spray to mask the Ni-W was investigated.
Sample RS, created
via this method, had a sharp undercut-proﬁle with a thin layer deposited beneath the rubber
mask extending around 20-40 µm beyond the tip of the undercut-proﬁle.
This was the best
proﬁle achieved, and using this method would give a higher JE for CC fabrication compared to
state-of-the-art ﬁlament techniques. It is therefore strongly recommended that experimentation
with this method to reduce or remove deposition beneath the rubber mask is further investigated.
Hypothesis 2
The second hypothesis was tested by starting with a deformed Ni-W and a recrystallized
Ni-W metal substrate and plating Ni on both. EBSD mapping of the grains and GB angles
of the Ni-W after plating was taken, determining that the electrodeposition process does not
signiﬁcantly aﬀect or inﬂuence the texture of the Ni-W. EBSD mapping of the electroplated
Ni was not conducted, as the metal was either nano-crystalline and/or had organic compounds
on the surface. The metal substrate was annealed in a forming gas atmosphere for 90 min. at
650 ◦C. Abnormally large Ni grains of upwards to mm size appeared, with a large amount of
thermal grooving. Electrodeposited Ni will experience an abnormal grain growth stage at much
lower temperatures, making it unsuitable for texture transfer with the aim of use in the RABiTS
fabrication processes, as temperatures in excess of 700 ◦C are needed. The conclusion to hypoth-
esis 2 is therefore that it is impossible to transfer the texture of the Ni-W to the electroplated
nanocrystalline Ni layer with the experimental procedures used in this work. Additionally, the
thermal grooving increases the surface roughness of the plated Ni. Growing an oxide layer on the
surface to counteract thermal grooving was also investigated, but these oxide layers signiﬁcantly
increased the surface roughness.

Outlook
51
6
Outlook
Some additional measurement techniques and set-ups which could have been used to control
the bright nickel solution includes using a reference hull cell, and doing impurity measurements
on the plated Ni layer. Adding additives to the Watts nickel bath to create the bright nickel
solution could potentially create problems if too much of the additives were added, as that
can adversely aﬀect the plating process. A hull cell would qualitatively test the eﬀects of the
additives in a much smaller sample size of around 267 ml, which would replicate the plating on
a smaller scale. This would have helped prevent adding too much of the organic additives, as
they are very hard to remove again from the bath, and for testing the eﬀects of the diﬀerent
additives before adding them to the whole bath. The hull cell would also enable measuring
properties like macro-throwing power capability, which was completely ignored in this project,
and allow ﬁnding the optimal current density [59]. Furthermore, there was some diﬃculty with
EBSD measurements of the electroplated nickel, possibly due to high levels of organic materials
on the surface, which could stem from the organic additives in the bright nickel solution. It
could therefore be interesting to do impurity measurements on the plated Ni surface, e.g. via
glow discharge optical emission spectroscopy to determine the types of impurities and the depth
at which they penetrate below the surface [60].
Some things which were not considered in this project is if the technique of tape masking
and electroplating on a 10 cm sample would really be representative for a 1 km sample. Con-
sistency in a 10 cm sample was very hard to achieve, so one could easily imagine running into
consistency issues with 10 m samples, and much more so on the km scale. A consistent proﬁle
was not achieved at any point in this project, but the author strongly recommends further in-
vestigating the method of spray painting adhesive onto a kapton carrier or using a rubber spray
mask, as the sharpest undercut-proﬁle was achieved via these methods as opposed to using stan-
dard kapton tape. For the former method, an adhesive which has better adherence between
the carrier and the metal substrate needs to be used, so the edges of the masking tape do not
experience lift-oﬀ. For the latter case of using a rubber spray mask, diﬀerent methods of cutting
needs to be investigated so the edges of the mask keep adhering to the surface. Another possible
solution is to apply some wax-like adhesive to kapton carrier instead of spray adhesive. Cutting
the carrier to the desired trapezoidal shape with this wax-like adhesive would allow the adhesive
to keep the shape after cutting as opposed to the silicone adhesive which contracts.
Texture could not be transferred to the electroplated Ni due to an abnormal grain growth
stage when annealing. A possible solution to this issue, is to electroplate Ni-W [61]-[63] instead
of pure Ni, since Ni-W is currently successfully used as the metal substrate in the RABiTS
fabrication technique [14]. However, one runs into diﬀerent problems when electroplating alloys.
An immediate concern for the use in superconductor applications is that the surface of the elec-
troplated layer seems to experience cracking [64] and in some cases even anomalous microspikes
[65]. All these studies used high amounts of tungsten and applied the layers via DC electroplat-
ing. One study was able to decrease and completely eliminate the formation of cracks, thereby
getting a smooth surface, by using a novel idea of pulsed current plating with diﬀerent pulsed
current densities [66]. Furthermore, plating with Ni-W instead of Ni would not have the sig-
niﬁcant thermal grooving seen when annealing in forming gas, so for both IBAD and RABiTS,
electroplating with Ni-W instead might give better results compared to pure Ni. It should be
mentioned that all the articles referenced in this paragraph all electroplated with a much higher
amount of tungsten ranging from 18-67 wt.%W, and not much research has been carried out on
electroplating with such a low tungsten content as would be needed for Ni-5 at.%W. This process
might therefore have diﬀerent challenges than the ones mentioned above, and further research
is needed.

AFM Data Treatment with SPIP 6.6.2
Figure 70: Image of raw data.
Step 1:
When opening a .ezd ﬁle in SPIP 6.6.2,
two windows pop up;
one with a .tl ex-
tension and one with .tz, where the lat-
ter is the raw data ﬁle.
All AFM mea-
surements were taken over a 20-by-20 µm2
area.
Figure 71: Image of plane corrected data.
Step 2:
First some plane correction is needed, as
AFM microscopes typically have a non-linear
coupling between the lateral plane and the z-
axis, which causes an unwanted bend in the
image. A polynomial ﬁt uses a slope correc-
tion which is based on a Least Mean Square
(LMS) ﬁt to the entire image. Polynomial de-
grees for the LMS ﬁt can be set between 0 and
5. A degree of 3 was used in this report, as the
program did not recommend using higher ex-
cept in very special cases. Furthermore, small
surface corrugations can at times be overshad-
owed by the noise in the AFM microscope.
Typically, this creates very visible steps be-
tween the scan lines. Additionally, temporary
contamination of the probe, for example from
dust or particles on the surface, will cause the
scan lines to be leveled in a wrong way. These eﬀects can be reduced by leveling the individual
scan lines to more representative levels by using the ”Linewise leveling” and/or ”Linewise oﬀset”
options, depending on which eﬀect is needed.
==PAGE:71==
References
57
Figure 72: Image of line scan before and after correction.
Step 3:
Each scan line can be viewed using the horizontal cross section proﬁle. Due to vibrations
from external sources, such as a door slamming or a person walking by, the AFM tip cannot
properly scan the surface, and will instead display a line.
This is simply noise, and can be
corrected using the ”Correct selected cross section” option, which takes an average from the
scan line above and below the one selected. This gives a more representative scan line, although
one should be careful if more than one consecutive line has a lot of noise. An example of a scan
line correction is seen in Figure 72, where the uncorrected scan line is in the top right corner
and the same scan line after correction is below it.
Figure 73: Image of noise reduction after median
ﬁlter has been applied.
Step 4:
This is followed by a noise reduction,
in this thesis by using ”median 7x7 weak”.
The median ﬁlter utilizes stepwise moves of
a data window with speciﬁed size of 7-by-
7 across the image.
If the center value in
the data window diﬀers more than a cer-
tain value from the median value, it will
be exchanged by the median value.
The
threshold value determining whether or not
to modify a pixel value depends on the lo-
cal standard deviation within the data win-
dow.
The higher the standard deviation
is, the higher the threshold value will be
set.

Step 5:
Lastly to carry out the surface rough-

applied. These functions remove respectively
long and short wavelengths of a user speci-
