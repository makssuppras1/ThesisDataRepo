ABSTRACT
Plastic waste management is one of the major environmental concerns of the 21st century and
the problem is extended worldwide. Due to the rapid growth of the population as well as the in-
dustrial sector, it is estimated that the quantity of solid waste has reached about 300 million tons
globally each year and it is expanding rapidly. This problem is even more severe in some areas of
the world, or in countries with booming economies. Different studies have been made all over in
Indonesia and the results are shocking. Litter levels have almost doubled in the last 30 years. This
thesis aims to develop a project able to reduce the amount of plastic waste illegally disposed in
the environment in Indonesia, focusing in particular on the Bali Province. Social developments in
Indonesian lifestyles and tourist activities have resulted in an increasing amount of litter all over
in Bali and especially in beaches and rivers. Furthermore the thesis is focused on the reduction of
plastic waste in Bali because it was declared to be in ”garbage emergency” in 2018.
The use of energy resources, the health problem and the impacts on the environment from the
plastic waste disposal create several concerns because this polymer material is non-biodegradable
and it lasts in the environment for many years. One of the most efﬁcient solution against the
pollution generated from garbage is recycling, which can both save energy consumption in the
production phase and achieve a green end of life approach for plastics. With the concept of see-
ing plastic waste as a valued resource, this report is focused on the development of an innovative
way to reuse plastic. The purpose of this project is to test samples made out of recycled plastic
manufactured in Bali, in order to comprehend the mechanical properties of recycled plastic. The
results will provide useful information about the innovative material and will demonstrate the pos-
sibility of using it to design a pavilion. The pavilion will be designed with the ”brick concept”
which means that it will be structured from one singular multi-functional component. The ﬁnal
outcome of the thesis will open new opportunities in the building industry, because will establish
the feasibility of constructions, built with recycled plastic. The recycled plastic has a great po-
tential because is is brand new product in the building industry, which can replace the traditional
construction materials.
Two different types of plastic have been received from Bali, the Extruded Recycled Plastic (ERP)
and the Mould Recycled Plastic (MRP). Several mechanical tests were performed on the two mate-
rials which aimed to evaluate the mechanical properties, the stress-strain relation and other aspects
of the plastics. In this report the compressive, tensile and ﬂexural properties of the materials were
evaluated through different type of test which are thoughtfully documented. After the data from
the tests were processed, it was found that the ERP plastic is more resistant than the MRP. There-
fore, the ERP is more suitable for applications in structures and buildings. For this reason, the
sustainable pavilion designed and described at the end of the report, was calculated using the data
collected for the ERP. In conclusion the study demonstrates that it is possible to use the ERP as
structural material and it can be used in the building industry.

Motivation
The quantity of solid waste is expanding rapidly. It is estimated that the rate of expansion has been
doubling every 10 years. This is due to the rapid growth of the population as well as the industrial
sector [2]. The production of plastics has reached about 300 million tons globally each year. The
use of energy resources, the health problem and the impacts on the environment from its disposal
trigger overriding concern on plastic recycling, which can both save energy consumption in the
production phase and achieve a green end-of-life approach to plastics. With the concept of seeing
waste as a valued resource, plastic waste ended up in recycling and energy recovery has reached
57.9 % on plastic in Europe each year [3].
Nowadays, several projects around the world are focused on the disposal of plastic waste. Al-
though recycling is one of the most developed solutions to this problem, many companies are
trying to use a different method in solving the issue. The ordinary concept of recycling is chang-
ing and the modern approach consists of using plastic waste in new ways, which were not explored
before. This new way of thinking has brought the researches on plastic waste disposal, to substitute
common materials such as wood, steel or concrete with plastic polymers. In the building industry,
for instance, companies have understood the fact that purchasing recycled plastic products and
using them, is a win-win situation. For this reason today, many construction products are made
from recycled plastic, such as water drainage, pipes and ducting, wall cladding, rooﬁng materials,
ground stabilisation, damp proof membranes, kerb-stones, scaffolding planks, coarse aggregate in
concrete and many others.
Other companies such as ”Conceptos Plasticos” [1] built their business only on recycled plastic,
employing the material as a primary resource for the realization of dwellings and several types of
buildings. Among the solid-waste materials, plastics have received a lot of attention because they
are generally non-biodegradable. On a weight basis, there are about 10 billion kilograms of plastic
wastes only in the U.S. per year, which represents about 7% of the total solid wastes. However,
plastic wastes are very visible, since they constitute about 30% of the total solid waste [6]. The

Project goal and approach
The main goal of the project described in this report is to evaluate the mechanical properties

types of plastics. The mechanical properties of recycled plastic are determined through a series
of tests. The tests were performed following the guidelines of the International Organization for

• Compression test
• Tensile test
• Three point Bending test

1.4
Plastic Properties
Plastic is a material consisting of a wide range of synthetic or semi-synthetic organic compounds
that are malleable and so can be molded into solid objects. Pure polymeric materials are di-
vided into two main categories. The ﬁrst type is thermoplastic, which can be melted for recycling
and reused in the plastic industry and they include: polyethylene (PE), polypropylene (PP) and
polyethylene terephthalate (PET). Thermoplastic acquires malleability and softness when exposed
under the action of heat. After the heating process thermoplastic can be modeled or formed into
ﬁnished objects which after cooling down, return to being rigid; this process can be repeated many
3
==PAGE:18==
times. The second type is thermosetting plastic. This plastic cannot be melted by heating because
the molecular chains are bonded ﬁrmly with meshed cross-link. These plastic types are known
as silicone, epoxy resin, unsaturated polyester and polyurethane. Thermosetting plastics harden,
after an initial softening phase by heating, due to the effect of cross-linking. In the softening phase
due to the combined effect of heat and pressure thermosetting plastics are pliable. If thermoset-
ting plastics are heated after hardening, they no longer return to softening, but they decompose
and carbonize. At present, these plastic wastes are disposed of by either burning or burying in
landﬁlls. The reuse of thermosetting plastic could reduce the pollution caused by the burning pro-
cess as well as the cost of its waste management. Plastics are typically organic polymers of high
molecular mass and often contain other substances. They are usually synthetic, most commonly
derived from petrochemicals, however, an array of variants are made from renewable materials.
Due to their low cost, ease of manufacture, versatility, and imperviousness to water, plastics are
used in a multitude of products.
From a practical point of view, suitable blends are generally used, consisting of one or more
polymeric materials plus the addition of additives. For this reason, the standard classiﬁcation
of polymeric materials is accompanied by a ”commercial” classiﬁcation, according to which the
polymeric materials are divided into: - Fibers: they have considerable mechanical resistance and
have little ductility compared to other polymeric materials; this means that ﬁbers stretch a little if
subjected to traction and can withstand high breaking loads. - Resins: particular plastic materials
formulated from thermosetting materials; - Rubbers: formulated from elastomer plastic materials.
The advantageous characteristics of plastics compared to metallic and non-metallic materials are a
greater ease of processing, the cost-effectiveness, the colorability, the acoustic, thermal, electrical,
mechanical (vibration) insulation, the resistance to corrosion and the chemical inertia , as well
as water repellency and resistance to mold, fungi and bacteria. The disadvantages are the attack
by solvents (especially thermoplastics) and acids (in particular thermosetting materials) and poor
resistance to high temperatures. Another peculiarity is the low speciﬁc density - which gives a
high lightness - between a minimum of 0.04 - 1 kg/dm for polystyrene and a maximum of 2.2
kg/dm of polytetraﬂuorethane (PTFE), with a very heterogeneous physical resistance depending
on the type of plastic [10].
1.5
Plastic recycling
Recycling is the practice of recovering used materials from the waste stream and then, incorpo-
rating those same materials into the manufacturing process. Recycling is one of the prominent
development of this environmentally conscious era. There are three main arguments for recy-
cling: ﬁrst, it preserves the precious natural resources; secondly, it minimizes the transportation
and management costs; and thirdly, it avoids the environmental load caused by waste material
such as space requirements. Great strides have been made to increase recycling rates worldwide
in recent years. The major consideration to support recycling is the expansion, all over the world,
of infrastructure for recycling. Few technological and economic constraints currently limit the full
and efﬁcient recycling of plastic wastes into useful products.
Among the different types of recyclable thermoplastic, sometimes other different materials are
found, which must be identiﬁed and removed. Often, plastic waste batch, are seriously contam-
inated by dye residues, paper, adhesive residues, coatings and other substances. The separation
of such complex mixtures is often impossible even if the cost is reasonable. Cleanness, type and
grade of purity have been essential for high-grade recycling of plastics. Furthermore, mixed plas-
tic wastes can be used to a limited extent of time and only in low-grade applications. Mixing of
plastics types makes processing into high-quality products difﬁcult and expensive or sometimes
even impossible. There are also numerous technical hurdles to overcome when recycling plastic.
4
==PAGE:19==
Firstly, materials recovery facilities are responsible for sorting and processing plastics, but they
struggle to do it economically. Moreover, when different types of plastics are melted together,
they tend to phase-separate, like oil and water, and set in different layers. The phase boundaries
cause structural weakness in the resulting material, meaning that polymer blends are useful in only
limited applications. The most widely manufactured plastics behave this way, which limits their
utility for recycling. Each time plastic is recycled, additional virgin materials must be added to
help improve the integrity of the material. Most of the time, even recycled plastic has new plas-
tic material added in. The same piece of plastic can only be recycled about 2–3 times before its
quality decreases to the point where it can no longer be used [8]. The recycling of plastic is done
through different methods as listed below:
• Chemical Modiﬁcation: Plastic can be recycled by chemical modiﬁcation or depolymeriza-
tion. There are two ways to achieve the depolymerization, which are hydrolysis (a type of
chemical decomposition) and Pyrolysis (which is a thermal decomposition), Some of these
technologies are relatively easy to develop, but in general, it is complicated to chemically
modify mixed plastic to produce useful and economical chemical feed-stock.
• Mechanical Recycling: refers to processes which involve melting, shredding or granulation
of waste plastic. Garbage must be sorted before mechanical recycling. Different technology
is being introduced to sort plastics, using various techniques such as X-ray ﬂuorescence, in-
frared spectroscopy or electrostatics ﬂotation. Following sorting, the plastic is either melted
down directly and moulded into a new shape or melted and shredded into ﬂakes and then
processed into granules. The chemical and physical properties of these recycled materials
are similar to products manufactured from virgin resins.
• Thermal Processing: consists of heating a thermoplastic at very high temperatures, thus
making the plastic ﬂow. The liquid plastic is then converted into a new product as it cools.
This method does not involve the modiﬁcation of the chemical composition of the plastic.
However, this process cannot be repeated indeﬁnitely because it may adversely affect plastic
properties. Despite thermal processing cannot be applied to all plastic types because some
of them degrade at high temperatures, it becomes more efﬁcient when various thermoplastic
are mixed.
• Fillers: Plastic waste can also be used as ﬁllers with virgin resins or other materials like
concrete or ﬁll material for road constructions. In such applications, the chemical com-
position of the plastic is generally not very signiﬁcant. This is the most effective method
to recycle contaminated plastic in second grade applications. The plastic waste is melted
and co-extruded into mouldings with virgin resins having superior properties. These virgin
resins are forced into the perimeter of the mold, while the recycled plastics with inferior
properties are injected into the center of the mold [6].
Broadly, among the methods above mentioned, the two most used processes to recycle plastics
are: Chemical recycling (where the plastic is broken down into basic components) and mechan-
ical recycling (where the plastic is collected, sorted, washed, ground into powders and melted to
obtain the required shape). It is important to point out that recycled plastic is hardly used to make
identical plastic items which replicate its previous form. As a ﬁnal result of the recycling process,
the plastics are reduced to pellet form. Then, the ﬁnal product is transported to manufacturing
companies to be redesigned and be used in making other useful objects.

MATERIAL
2.1
Source and Problem framing
The recycled plastic studied in this thesis is produced by the company: ”Sustainism Lab”, a low-
tech factory located in Bali, Indonesia. The aim of the company is to manufacture samples of
recycled plastic maintaining the production process as low-tech is possible. Maintaining the pro-
cess low-tech will create for Bali a low cost solution able to reduce the dramatic plastic waste
problem in this area. The decision of keeping the production low-tech is related to the fact that
Bali, is mainly a touristic island, not well industrialized and lacking modern facilities. The garbage
disposal problem is particularly severe in Indonesia, as in many other developing Asian countries
since their economies expanding rapidly. Through abundant tourism economy opportunities, Bali
Province in Indonesia offers numerous jobs which attract many people to move to Bali, which
increased the rate of urbanisation in Bali Province.
The difﬁculty that often occurs, in developing countries, is the fragility of the waste management
system, lack of ﬁnancial resources, and limited participation of residents. Bali Province is not ex-
empt from these problems. Since proper waste management is one of the objectives of Sustainable
Development Goals (SDGs), Bali province needs to improve the facilities to sort the waste.
Waste management policy needs to be prepared to respond to speciﬁc activities in an area accord-
ing to its characteristics. In the Province of Bali, where expanding economies were driven by the
tourism sector, the management policy development needs to be prepared. The waste management
must be based on basic data that can describe the existing conditions of waste due to the activities
of residents and tourists.
Regional autonomy in Indonesia causes waste management data not to be collected properly that
cause policy making not effective. From preliminary studies, it was conﬁrmed that all cities in Bali
Province are not well coordinated so that the existing waste quantities, compositions, and waste
management chains are confusing. In 2019, there is a Province Regulation regarding the prohi-
bition of the single-use plastics, but the effectiveness of the policy cannot be evaluated because
of the unavailability of actual data on waste matters. Also for the Indonesian National Policy and
Strategy 2019, by the end of 2025, waste in Bali Province must be reduced by 30%. The lack of
analysis of the actual waste management condition makes it difﬁcult for policymakers to deter-
mine the direction of their policies. The waste management existing condition is needed to deﬁne,
evaluate, and analyse, so it can be baseline data for improving solid waste services.

mistreatment of waste.

2.3
Manufacture of Samples
The two different types of recycled plastic samples studied and tested in this report, were manufac-
tured in ”Sustainism Lab” and delivered to DTU in December 2019. Sustainism Lab is a research
and design workshop located in Bali, which has a simple and low tech set up reﬂecting its basic
and low-cost proﬁle. The machines used during the production process are built locally in Bali but
designed by a Dutch company called ”Precious Plastic” [12] which has a long experience in this
emerging sector, provides technical information and design details to assemble devices capable to
recycle plastic. The plastic sample production employed a low-tech mechanical recycling process
which was subdivided into the following phases:
1. Collection: the ﬁrst step is the collection of scrap plastic from the municipality’s disposal
dumps and polluted environments. Afterward, the waste was properly packed and trans-
ported towards the specialized plastic processing workshop and stored in a collecting yard.
2. Sorting: the actual plastic recycling process starts in a processing workshop, whit the sort-
ing of the different plastic items by their resin content and color. This process is also done to
ensure all contaminants are eliminated. Usually, there are machines speciﬁcally conceived,
which help in sorting the plastics, according to their resin content and recycling mills which
10
==PAGE:25==
sort the scrap by symbols at the bottom of the plastics. However, in Bali this process was
shorter and hand made.
3. Shredding: after the sorting phase, plastic recyclables are then shredded into tiny chunks.
The shredder used in this stage was a double axis machine able to shred up to 50 kg of plastic
every hour (depending on the desired ﬂake size ready to be recycled). Technical information
about the shredder are showed in Appendix Appendix A. The heavier and lighter plastic
ﬂakes were separated using a specially designed machine. The separation process helps in
ensuring that different types of plastic are not mixed together in the ﬁnal product.
Figure 2.5: Professional Shredder used by ”Sustainism Lab” in Bali
4. Cleaning: after a complete separation, the ﬂakes or chunks are then washed with detergents
to remove the remaining contamination. These shredded fragments then undergo processes,
to eliminate impurities like paper labels. Once the cleaning process is complete, the clean
ﬂakes are passed through specialized equipment that further separates the plastic resin types.
The plastic ﬂakes are then subjected to moderate heat to dry.
5. Melting: The dry ﬂakes are melted down and molded into a new shape, but sometimes
could be processed into granules. The melting process is done under regulated temperatures.
There is specialized equipment designed to melt down plastics without destroying them.
6. Making of pellets: after the melting process, generally in recycling factories, the plastic
material is extruded and compressed into the form of small pellets known as ”nurdles”. In
this state, the plastic pellets are ready for reuse or be redesigned into new products, starting
the manufacturing process.
About this last phase, ”Sustainism Lab” used two distinct approaches to manufacture the samples,
which result in two completely different products. The ﬁrst approach was the manufacture of
recycled plastic samples through simple moulding. This basic process consists of melting down
the recycled plastic and then moulding it in proper stamps. The outcome of this process was a
black plastic sample, which is further described in subsection 2.4.
The quality of the samples obtained from the moulding process was not satisfying. Consequently,
an evolution in the production process was developed. The second type of recycled plastic samples
were manufactured through an extrusion process. This process helps to avoid several problems
11
==PAGE:26==
that emerged in the moulding process and improved signiﬁcantly the quality of the ﬁnal product.
In fact, the improvements are immediately recognizable just by visual inspection of the extruded
samples which resulted as professionally manufactured. Further information about the recycled
plastic samples obtained through the extrusion process are provided in subsection 2.5.
2.4
Molded Recycled Plastic
Since the aim of the project is to reduce the amount of plastic waste in developing countries, the
production process of the molded recycled plastic (MRP) must remain as low-tech as possible.
For this reason, the ﬁrst batch of recycled plastic produced in Bali, was ﬁrstly molded using basic
tools.
The molding process was performed with commonly thermoplastic materials collected previously
in the environment. The plastic material was fed into a heated barrel where the temperature in-
creases allowing the material to start its ﬂow. In this phase, the individual chains of plastic are
weakened, as a result of increased space between molecules. This process reduces the plastic vis-
cosity, which enables the polymer to ﬂow with the driving force of the injection unit [13]. Then,
the material is mixed using a helical shaped screw-type plunger, and injected with high pressure
into a mould cavity. From the mould cavity, the material gets out and goes in the mould, where the
recycled plastic cools down and hardens to the conﬁguration of the cavity. The mould was hand
built in Bali according to the shape and dimensions of the samples. Thermoplastics were selected
because they have particular characteristics that make them highly suitable for injection moulding
and recycling.
The ﬁnal result of such low-tech process is a black and slightly sticky plastic, which was deliv-
ered to DTU in blocks of irregular cubic shape with average dimensions of 70x70x120mm. These
samples have an elementary aspect with plenty of irregularities such as variable colours and soft
consistency. It was even noticed the presence of foreign materials in some samples. Injection
moulding is a complex technology where possible production problems might arise. They can
be caused either by defects in the moulds, or more often by the moulding process itself. In fact,
working to shape specimens from this material, some irregularities were noticed both outside and
inside the sample. Among the various defects encountered in the material, there were:
- Blister: Raised or layered zone on the surface of the part deriving by tools or material which are
too hot, often caused by a lack of cooling around the tool or a faulty heater.
- Color streaks: Localized change of colour caused because the master-batch was not mixed prop-
erly, or the material has run out and was starting to come through as natural only. Another expla-
nation could be that previous coloured material dragged in the nozzle or the check valve.
- Contamination: The presence of unwanted or foreign material which can be easily identiﬁed in
the samples, thank to different colours or matter found in the samples. This problem is due to poor
material quality introduced by bad recycling or regrind policy which may include ﬂoor sweepings,
dust and debris. An important episode must be mentioned, which highlights the weakness of the
material. Cutting the sample in smaller specimens for a speciﬁc test, traces of paper were found
inside the material, which broke immediately. In another specimen was found the presence of
metals while in some samples there was water trapped inside the plastic.
- Splash marks: represent as small bubbles caused by trapping moisture. Moisture presence in
the material, is usually caused when hygroscopic resins are dried improperly. Trapping of gas in
external areas due to excessive injection velocity, too high material temperature, or the recycled
plastic was being shredded too much.
- Bad smell: there are several reasons why the moulded plastic samples have a strong smell. In
many plastics, residual traces of monomers can produce unpleasant odors. Some chemicals and
stabilizers used in the production phase often bring a strong odor. Furthermore, due to the lack
12
==PAGE:27==
of temperature controls, incomplete combustion processes result in the presence of a variety of
chemicals that have nasty odors. Complete combustion of most polymers/plastics, usually results
in H2O, CO2 and some mineral ash being produced. Incomplete combustion produces hydrocar-
bons with many ”free radicals” that can react with the nasal passages and cause a nose burning
feeling or in other words the strong smell produced by the MRP samples. A possible solution
for this problem is to burn plastics at a temperature higher than 500 ◦C and if possible recycle the
exhaust gases into the feed of the ﬁre. This procedure will ensure the complete combustion of any
plastic [14] and avoid some of the above mentione problems.

smelly. The strong smell coming from the samples is almost intolerable, and it could cause people
headaches even after a short term exposition. Despite a long exposition to fresh air for more than
three months, the material continues to have a strong odor. Due to its smell problem, this type
of material is not recommended to be used in the building industry, because users will suffer.
Anyway, most of these problems were solved with the introduction of the extrusion method in the
production process.

2.5
Extruded Recycled Plastic
The extruded recycled plastic (ERP) produced in Bali thorough the utilization of a professional ex-
truder machine was developed after the ﬁrst attempt to produce a low-tech molded plastic sample
from recycled products. Although this new process cannot be considered low-tech, the principle
behind it is quite simple and the cost of the machine was affordable for the producers in Bali. Fur-
thermore, the improvement in the production process resulted in a higher quality product lacking
the previously mentioned defects.
Plastics extrusion is a high-volume manufacturing process in which the raw compound material
is in the form of nurdles. This process starts by feeding plastic material (pellets, granules, ﬂakes,
or powders) that are gravity introduced from a top mounted hopper into the barrel of the extruder.
The material enters through the feed throat (an opening near the rear of the barrel) and comes into
contact with the screw. The rotating screw forces the plastic beads forward into the heated barrel.
In this phase, the desired extrusion temperature is rarely equal to the set temperature of the barrel
due to viscous heating and other effects. The heating proﬁle is set for the barrel, increasing the
temperature from the rear part (where the plastic enters) to the front. This allows the plastic beads
to melt gradually as they are pushed through the barrel and lowers the risk of overheating which
may cause degradation in the polymer [15]. Extra heat is contributed by the intense pressure and
friction taking place inside the barrel. The recycled plastic is melted and formed into a continuous
proﬁle. The material is gradually melted by the mechanical energy generated by turning screws
and by heaters arranged along the barrel. At the front of the barrel, the molten plastic leaves the
screw and travels through a screen pack to remove any contaminants in the melt. The molten
polymer is then forced into a die, which shapes the polymer into a shape that hardens during
cooling.
Figure 2.7: Extruder Machine Section
Although extruder technology has much in common with plastic injection molding used previ-
ously, the main difference is that extrusion is usually a continuous process. The post pressing
procedure was the most preferred method in Bali, because it optimizes the production process,
making it faster and more productive. Furthermore, The extruder machine directly extrudes the
recycled plastic into the designated mold and this approach makes the plastic samples more uni-
form and resistant. A key development was to combine the extruder with the injection machine. In
this way, the extrusion is a continuous process where plastic ﬂakes are inserted into the hopper and
extruded into a line of plastic, which were directly heated and injected into a mould. Therefore,
14
==PAGE:29==
the process became relatively quick and well suited for making samples. Another innovative de-
velopment in the production process was the temperature control, which was obtained connecting
the heating element directly to the extrusion barrel. The introduction of warning sensors in this
machine made also possible to understand when the material is at a set temperature and when it
is at environment temperature. Controlling the temperatures in the process, resulted in the elimi-
nation of the smelly problem in the plastic samples. The producer states the plastic utilized has a
high percentage of LDPE, which melts at 115 ◦. During the manufacturing, the temperatures were
set higher to assist the extruder in processing the plastic without compromising the quality of the
ﬁnal product. The extruder machine developed by Sustainism lab is showed in Figure 2.8 below.
Figure 2.8: Extruder Machine used by ”Sustainism Lab” in Bali
The extruded samples turn up in a sort of light blue colour and a parallelepiped shape with di-
mensions of approximately: 55x55x180 mm. The improved material from a ﬁrst inspection seems
qualitatively better and harder than the low-tech molded samples. Despite the ERP is harder than
the MRP, at the same the material is lighter. In general, the ERP material is more regular in all
its properties and it lacks visible imperfections. Furthermore, the crucial problem related to the
bad smell, noticed in the MRP, was entirely solved for the ERP using the extrusion method. The
most important improvement due to the extrusion production process was that the annoying odors
reported in the moulded samples completely disappeared in the extruded ones. This was an im-
portant step in the development of this project because allows the possibility of this material to be

used in the building industry.
The only defect of the extruded samples is that most of them present a hole in their core. In some
samples, the hole goes across the element, so it is almost possible to see through the samples. This
imperfection in the sample is due to the extrusion process because in Bali are using a low-cost
extruder which cannot be compared with the professional machinery utilized in plastic factories.
The problem emerged because there is a large difference in temperature between the hot plastic
and the cold extruder plunger. As a consequence, when the melted plastic is in contact with the
cold metal surface of the extruder plunger, it dries up instantaneously, creating a weak zone in the
center of the samples. This issue could be solved by slowing down the load applied to the plunger
and increasing the overall time of the process. Another possible solution could be to warm up the
plunger to avoid the temperature shock between plastic and metal surface. This solution will keep
the plastic melted for a longer period, which will avoid the formation of holes or any other type
of imperfections in the samples. However, a consistent amount of ERP samples did not show this
problem, neither when they were cut in smaller pieces.

Method
The specimen preparation was made during March 2020, while the density evaluation was per-
formed on 14th of May 2020. The specimens were cut according to regulations of ISO 1183-1

shearing was used by qualiﬁed personnel to obtain the correct specimen sizes, with an accuracy
of 0,2 mm in accordance with the ISO. The surface of the specimen was made smooth and free of
cavities, in order to minimize the entrapment of air and other materials. Then, the specimens were
labelled with a marker without compromising the specimen characteristics.

4
COMPRESSION TEST
The goal of using recycled plastic for building structures, could be reached only if the design
values about this innovative material are provided to architects and engineers. The data about
the compression properties of the ERP and MRP are important because they can be used in the
design phase and for the structural calculations of a project. In order to understand the behaviour
of the recycled plastic subjected to external loads, the compressive characteristics of the material
must be studied. Unfortunately, nowadays building regulations, data sheets from manufacturers or
even the Eurocode are not providing such information, because the recycled plastic is a material
which needs to be further examined, studied and developed. Therefore, the aim of this chapter is to
provide the compressive strength characteristics of the ERP and MRP materials and to comprehend
their behaviour under certain conditions, illustrating the procedures adopted during the study.
The ﬁrst batch was tested directly from the raw samples received from Bali, as they were produced
without performing almost any machining process. The samples received from Bali presented a
parallelepiped shape with rough dimensions of around 55x55x180 mm for the ERP and 80x80x130
mm for the MRP. The lateral surfaces were kept with all the irregularities, created from the low
tech production process, observed in the samples. The only operation made on the raw samples,
was to cut the top and the bottom edge, in order to have the two cross sections in contact with the
test machine, parallel and aligned with each other. In this way the reliability of the compression
test increased signiﬁcantly.
However, a second batch of specimens was tested with an adjusted length, aimed to prevent any
buckling problems under compression loads, from affecting the results. The second batch of
specimens was machined with a cubic shape and dimensions of 50x50x50 mm. The specimens
were shaped from the same rough samples above mentioned, with a band-saw for metals, ensuring
that the characteristic of the material did not change.
The specimen preparation of the extruded recycled plastic ERP, required an additional step before
the tests. As already outlined in subsection 2.5, some of the ERP samples were manufactured with
an irregular hole in their core. The compressive properties evaluation, could have been affected
from this imperfection. To avoid errors in the results, the hole was enlarged and made completely
circular through a drill press for metals. The hole was made with high precision in the center of
the specimen, with known dimensions of the diameter of ∅20 mm. This procedure allowed to
calculate the correct specimen cross section, removing the circular hole area from the square area
and deﬁne correctly the compressive characteristics.
4.1
Method
The tests aimed to investigate the compressive behaviour of the recycled plastics and to determine
the compressive strength, compressive modulus and other aspects of the compressive stress-strain
relationship of the ERP and MRP under the conditions deﬁned in the standards. The compression
tests were executed using recommendations of the international standard ISO 604 [17]. The stan-
dard speciﬁes a method for determining the compressive properties of plastics. The standard test
specimen (type A) is deﬁned in the ISO with dimensions of 50 mm × 10 mm × 4 mm for com-
pression tests. Although the ISO gives speciﬁc recommendations about the specimen dimensions,
different specimens, with different shape and dimensions, were machined in this study in order to
ﬁt the test machine ﬁxture available in DTU. Furthermore, the study of bigger specimens allows
to collect a wider range of data, more suitable for applications in the building industry. The tests
were performed in standard atmospheres with a temperature of approximately 23 ◦C and 50 % of
relative humidity, as speciﬁed in ISO 291 [18].

Compression test description
The tests on the raw samples were carried out on the 28/05/2020, while the tests on cubic speci-
mens were performed on the 14/07/2020. The test setup consisted of placing the specimen between
the surfaces of the steel plates and aligning the centre lines marked on the specimen with the center
of the plates. The ﬁrst step was to apply the extensometer on the specimens through the utiliza-
tion of four rubber bands. During the procedure was ensured that any distortion or damage was
caused on the test specimen. The instrument was free of inertia and was ensured that there was
no slippage between the extensometer and the specimen. Then, the extensometer was calibrated
and any displacement or strain recorded during the application were balanced to zero. To ensure a
high level of precision in the test, the center of the plates was signed with a marker and then with
the help of a ruler, the specimen was positioned exactly in the center of the circles drawn on the
plates. The end surfaces of the specimen were always parallel to the compression plates. After the
specimen was positioned on the bottom plate, the top plate of the machine was lowered until the
ends of the specimen were in contact with the compression plates and a slight stress was recorded
from the load cell. At this point, the test specimen was compressed along its major axis at constant
speed until the specimen fractured or the load decreased. All the above mentioned considerations
are in accordance with ISO 604. The set up at the beginning of the compression test is showed in

Discussion
All the compression tests were performed with success and any anomalies or evident errors hap-
pened during the testing phase. From the reference and the studies provided by the ”Structured
Environment” and ”DTI”, was evident that the material behaves better under compression loads.
For this reason, two different types of tests were carried out, to analyse thoroughly the compression
properties of the materials. For the same reason, the sustainable pavilion, described in section 8,
is designed with an arch structure. The arch as a design choice, will increase the probabilities for
the material, to be exploited mainly in compression.
Although the ISO 604 recommends to test small specimens of 50x10x4 mm, it was not possible
to follow the standards for the specimen dimensions, because the test machines available in DTU
are designed to test big specimens and to evaluate properties of large elements. The test on small
specimens according to ISO 604, was performed anyway with a different equipment. Despite
was possible to collect data from the small specimens, the specimen failure happened always with
clear buckling. For this reason, the test is considered just as a qualitative test with the aim of
understanding the buckling properties of the material. The qualitative buckling test is showed in
section 5.

Bali, where no additional preparation processes were performed, with cubic specimens machined
as perfect right prism. The aim of the comparison is to understand if the machining process on
samples, like the cut through a metal saw, could have affected the material properties. Regarding

5
BUCKLING
The buckling test described in this chapter is a different interpretation of the compression test indi-
cated in the ISO standard 604 [17]. The standard suggests for plastics, to perform the compression
tests on very small specimens with dimensions of 50 x 10 x 4 mm. Since the test machine available
in DTU are designed to perform tests on big elements, it was very challenging to test such small
specimens purely in compression. Therefore, the specimens were tested with equipment provided
with special grips applying a compression load. The data collected from the test, are used as qual-
itative data which provide information regarding the compressive properties and rupture due to
buckling effect, of the ERP and MRP plastics. The specimens used in the test were cut and tested
in from two different directions.
5.1
Method
ISO 604 indicates the standard dimensions of a specimen for the compression test. The specimen
was cut from large plastic samples. An additional reﬁnement was needed to shape the specimens
according to ISO 604. The reﬁnement aimed to obtain the correct specimen sizes with an accuracy
of 0,2 mm. This last reﬁning process was performed using a high precision mechanical shearing.
To investigate the buckling behaviour of ERP and MRP plastic, the specimens were prepared
following the relevant ISO deﬁnitions. All the machining operations aimed to shape the specimens
were carried carefully, so that smooth surfaces resulted. Particular care was taken in machining
the ends of the specimens, so that smooth, ﬂat, parallel surfaces resulted. The surfaces facing the
compression plates were parallel and at right angles to the longitudinal direction. Sharp, clean
edges, perpendicular to the longest axis of the specimen to within 0.025 mm, were formed. The
specimens were cut in the shape of a right prism. The ﬁrst visual inspection after the cutting
phase showed that the specimens resulted free from scratches, pits, sink marks, twists, ﬂash, or
any other visible imperfection that are likely to inﬂuence the results. A micrometer reading to
0.01 mm was used to measure the thickness, width and length. Gauge marks on the specimens
were used to identify the different batches and to visualize the change in length during the test.
The gauge marks were made approximately equidistant from the midpoint of the test specimen
and impressed with a paint marker, without causing damage or detrimental effects on the material
tested. The tests were performed in standard atmospheres with a temperature of approximately
23 ◦C and 50 % of relative humidity, as speciﬁed in ISO 291 [18].
Specimen description
The specimens (type A) used in the evaluation of the buckling properties of the materials are the
same used for the density evaluation. The specimens were used twice, to reduce the amount of
work needed for the tests and because the density evaluation did not change the material properties.
Therefore, the specimens used for the buckling test are already showed in Table 3.2 and Table 3.3
of section 3.

the grips were activated. The test specimens were compressed along their major axis at a constant
speed until the specimens fractured or the load decreased. All the above mentioned considerations
follow the ISO 604.

5.5
Discussion
The buckling test described in this chapter was used as a qualitative test, performed to check the
behaviour of the two different materials under an axial compression load which induced a buckling
rupture in the specimens. In the beginning, the buckling test was intended to be a compression
test with specimens of dimensions imposed by ISO 604 [17]. Since the test showed immediately
buckling on the specimens, the results were not used for the evaluation of compressive properties.
The results obtained in the buckling test, conﬁrm that the ERP material is in general more resistant
as already proved in the previous chapter with the compression tests. The ERP material, not only is
able to bear higher loads compared with the MRP, but it also shows an elastic rupture mechanism.
The elastic rupture of the ERP is an important characteristic for the material because if it is used in
the building industry, for example, it shows the deformations before the collapse of the material.
On the contrary, the MRP material showed a fragile behaviour under buckling effects. The MRP
specimens broke instantaneously, without showing any type of deformation. This makes the MRP
not ideal for the building industry because the material collapse suddenly without showing defor-
mations.
The results could be further investigated in order to obtain useful values for the veriﬁcation of
columns built with Extruded Recycled Plastic. In conclusion, the qualitative buckling test demon-
strates that the ERP material is more appropriate than the MRP, for applications in the building
industry.

TENSILE TEST
This chapter illustrates the methodologies used to deﬁne the tensile strength characteristic of the
two recycled plastic material studied in this thesis. The general principles for determining the
tensile properties of plastic are deﬁned and detailed in ISO 527 [24] and its subsequent parts.
6.1
Method
The methods used to investigate the tensile behaviour of the test specimens and for determining
the tensile strength, tensile modulus and other aspects of the tensile stress-strain relationship are
explained in ISO 527-1 and 527-2. The conditions deﬁned in ISO 527-1 [24] are suitable for the
study of both Extruded Recycled Plastic (ERP) and Molded Recycled Plastic (MRP). The princi-
ple of the tensile test is that the test specimen is extended along its major longitudinal axis, with
a stress application, at a constant test speed until the specimen fractures or reaches its maximum
strain. During this procedure, the load sustained by the specimen and its elongation, are measured.
The tests were carried out in a standard atmosphere as described in ISO 291 [18]. The machining
process to obtain the required specimens is explained in detail in the next subsection 6.2. The test
specimens were placed in the grips of the test machine. This operation was carried out paying
particular attention to align the longitudinal axis of the test specimen with the axis of the test ma-
chine. The grips were tightened ﬁrmly to avoid slippage of the specimens or any other movements
during the test.
For the determination of the Tensile Modulus Et the strain rate used was constant and it did not
change because the grips were mounted correctly, avoiding motion between them and the spec-
imen. The tests were performed in standard atmospheres with a temperature of approximately
23 Celsius and 50 % of relative humidity, as speciﬁed in ISO 291 [18]. To ensure a standard
atmosphere, the selected specimens were conditioned in a temperature and humidity controlled
environment until the beginning of the test.
6.2
Specimen description
The test was carried out on specimens cut from cubic samples, using a band-saw for metals, to
obtain plastic sheets with a thickness of 10 mm as speciﬁed in the standard. After this process, the
plastic sheets were machined, with a high precision mechanical shearing by specialized personnel,
from semi-ﬁnished products to the t-bone shape, having dimensions according to ISO 527-2 [25].
Since the samples were produced in Bali with low-tech methods, there is no possibility to ensure
that the production process respects the requirements illustrated in the standards. For this reason,
there is no possibility to ensure the conditions of the sample preparation. After the machining pro-
cess, the specimen conformity was checked with a visual inspection. All the specimens resulted
with surfaces lacking visible ﬂaws, scratches, sink marks, or other imperfections. The test speci-
mens had mutually perpendicular pairs of parallel surfaces free of twist or ﬂash. The edges were
straight, symmetric and free of defects. The specimen dimensions after the machining process
were measured with a micrometer caliper which ensured a precision of 0.1 mm, acceptable for the
ISO.
Both the recycled plastics were tested as isotropic materials because was not possible to machine
the test specimens in two different directions, since the samples received from Bali were too small
for the required length dimensions imposed by the ISO. Although paragraph 7.1 of the ISO speci-
ﬁes that a minimum of 5 specimens should be tested, a total of 6 specimens were tested, in order
to have greater precision in the results and to have an extra set of data in case of any anomalous
rupture of one specimen. At the end of the machining process, the specimens fulﬁlled the ISO

Tensile Tests Description
Test tensile test was carried out on ERP specimens on the 24th of June and the day after on the
MRP. The ﬁrst step was to install the grips in the test machine with the help of specialized lab
personnel.
Then the correct pair of four jaws were selected considering the characteristics of the plastic
material and the specimen dimensions. Once the jaws were founded, they were sprayed with
mechanical grease on their external part, which is the part touching the grips. This procedure
was necessary in order to ensure enough lubrication between the jaws and the grips. The mutual
movement of these two parts ensures a good grip of the specimens. After the jaws were sufﬁciently
lubricated, they were mounted on the grips and locked with the designated clips. A stainless
steel ruler was applied on the test machine trough a magnet, without compromise the machine
measurements. The ruler was applied in order to be able to measure the initial gripping distance
for every specimen.
Afterwards, the extensometer was prepared with the right gauge length and connected to the test
machine in the dedicated slot. After the initial set up was ready, a camera to record the experiment
was installed on a leveled tripod and placed in front of the grips. An external led light was placed
in the test area, in order to have a better quality of the pictures. For each specimen, a picture was
taken at the beginning of the test and at the end of the test, to document the specimen rupture. For
each specimen, the entire test was video recorded. Then, the extensometer was directly applied
to the test specimen through the utilization of two rubber bands, which were wrapped around the
specimen in two points. This operation was carried out carefully avoiding any damage to the
specimen.
At this point, the specimen was installed in the grips of the test machine. The correct positioning
of the specimen required high precision and a lot of time. The specimen was ﬁrstly installed
in the bottom grip and tightened up through the levers included in the setup. The force applied
by hand was enough to avoid any slippage of the specimen. Although the force was high, any
specimen was broken. Subsequently, the top grip was lowered and positioned in the grip section
of the specimens. This procedure was carried out carefully, by using the help of the ruler and
the gauge marks which were previously drawn on the specimen for this particular purpose. Once
the overall set up was ready for the test, the displacement and force measurement of the machine

6.7
Discussion
It is evident that the ERP is more resistant than the MRP. All the graph and tables showed in
this chapter conﬁrm that the ERP is more hard and strong. Although the tensile stress, the Ten-
sile Young’s Modulus and the elongation of the ERP material are considerably higher than the
MRP, the tensile strain values do not differ signiﬁcantly in the two materials. The explanation for
this low difference in the tensile strain values is that the ERP specimens reached an high tensile
stress at almost the same tensile strain values of the MRP, in fact as direct consequence of this
interpretation, the Young’s modulus of the ERP is signiﬁcantly higher than the MRP.
Although the tensile test was carried out identically on the ERP and the MRP, using the same pro-
cedure, the same equipment and calculating the values with the same considerations, it is important
to mention that the specimen types for the two material were different with different dimensions
in the length, but not in the cross section. The values of the ERP are directly compared with the
values of the MRP because the test executed were completely the same, but it is not possible to
know deﬁnitively if the difference in length in the two types of specimens, could have affected the
results. As a consequence of the different lengths, all the values related to the length of the spec-
imens are different, such as the initial gripping distance and the specimen shape in general. For
this reason, there is no certainty that the data on the two materials are comparable, even if both the
test were carried out following the standards. Even though in general the ERP is more resistant to
tensile loads than the MRP and the two materials were compared in this chapter, a further investi-
gation of the tensile properties on specimens with the same dimensions is recommended. For this
reason, the manufacturer in Bali should produce Recycled plastic samples with equal dimensions,
in order to guarantee a fair and reliable comparison between the ERP and the MRP.
In subsection 6.6 three different values for the tensile Young’s modulus were displayed and de-
scribed carefully. Although the evaluation of the Young’s modulus from the software of the test
machine could be very precise because it calculates automatically the linear regression in the
tensile stress/strain relationship, the values obtained are too high if compared with the values
calculated from the stress/strain data collected from the same software. In the test on ERP, the
70
==PAGE:85==
Young’s modulus calculated from the software was 845 MPa, which is signiﬁcantly different com-
pared with the values calculated manually from the data collected. The tensile Young’s Modulus
calculated manually from the force and displacement data recorded in the software is 426 MPa
using the strain from the test machine displacement and 442 MPa using the strain from the exten-
someter. Since the difference between the values is very excessive, since there is no description
of the method used by the software and since the values calculated manually are very similar, the
assessment made by the software was discarded. Therefore, the Young’s modulus was calculated
as average of the two values obtained manually. The deﬁnitive tensile Young’s Modulus of the
ERP is
Et ERP = 434 MPa
Only the ERP Young’s Modulus was discussed because is more suitable as construction material
and it is the material utilized in the pavilion design. In any case, the tensile Young’s Modulus of
the ERP is almost two times higher than the MRP.
Since both the plastics tested have a modulus of elasticity in tension between 70 MPa and 700
MPa under the set of conditions described in this chapter, they can be categorized as ”semi-rigid
plastic” as stated in paragraph 3.13 of ISO-527-1. Therefore, after the evaluation of the tensile
properties of the two plastics, it is possible to state that the ERP is more suitable as construction
material than the MRP.

THREE POINT BENDING TEST
The scope of this chapter is to determine the ﬂexural properties of recycled plastics under deﬁned
conditions. The preferred test specimen, the description of the method and other recommendations
for the ﬂexural property evaluation are deﬁned in ISO 178:2019 [26]. The ISO standard used as a
guideline is relevant both for moulded or extruded plastics. The ﬂexural properties of the recycled
plastics were determined through a standard three point bending test. The ﬂexural behaviour of the
ERP and MRP was determined through the evaluation of the ﬂexural strength, ﬂexural modulus
and other aspects of the ﬂexural strain/stress relationship under the conditions deﬁned by the
standards. The Three point bending test consist of loading in the mid-span, a freely supported
beam of recycled plastic. The tests were performed in standard atmospheres with a temperature of
approximately 23 Celsius and 50 % of relative humidity, as speciﬁed in ISO 291 [17].
7.1
Method
The method described in ISO 178 [26] is used to investigate the ﬂexural behaviour of two different
recycled plastic namely ERP (Extruded Recycled Plastic) and MRP (Molded Recycled Plastic). A
test specimen of rectangular cross section, lying on two circular supports, was deﬂected utilizing
a loading edge acting on the specimen mid-span until rupture occurred at the outer surface of the
specimen. During the procedure, the force applied to the specimen and the resulting displace-
ment of the loading piston was measured by the test machine connected with sensors to computer
equipment. The direct displacement of the specimen was not measured because the lab was not
provided with a deﬂectometer, small enough to be applied below the specimens. The ﬂexural
properties were determined using ”Method A” as speciﬁed in paragraph 8.7 of ISO 178. Method
A consists of determining the ﬂexural stress and the ﬂexural strain curve using only one test speed.
The force applied and the specimen deﬂection were measured continuously without interruption,
using the same test speed which was used for the modulus determination region.

Discussion
The ERP material revealed in general better characteristics than the MRP, which made the ERP a
more suitable material for construction purposes. Overall the ERP resisted under higher ﬂexural
loads and higher deﬂections than the MRP.
It is important to mention that the test was performed without the application of a deﬂectometer
below the mid-span of the specimens due to the equipment available in DTU lab. Therefore, all
the values related to deﬂections, ﬂexural strain and ﬂexural Young’s modulus are measured and
evaluated from the displacement of the cross head of the test machine. This procedure categorizes
the ﬂexural test performed as ”test type II” according to the standards. In annex C of ISO 178
[26] a correction method for the test machine displacement values is explained. The method is
called: ”compliance correction for type III-tests”. The compliance correction described in the
standard allows to categorize the test results from type II to type III, increasing the precision of the
ﬂexural properties evaluation. The compliance correction described is especially relevant for the
ﬂexural Young’s modulus. In section C1 and C2 of the annex, the theory behind the compliance
correction is well documented and two different methods of correction are provided. It would be
interesting for further researches to apply the second method proposed in the ISO 178 with formula
C.4 of for the compliance correction, on the data collected in this report. The method consists of
measuring precisely the deﬂection ∆sref of a reference specimen relative to the supports. Using
this parameter the machine compliance for the correction can be determined with Equation 7.8
from showed below.
CM = 1
F (s −∆sref)
(7.8)
Where:
Fmax : Flexural load applied to the reference specimen - [N]
s : Displacement indicated by the equipment during the test (e.g. cross head displacement) - [mm]
∆sref : Deﬂection of the reference specimen as determined by a calibrated reference instrument
(e.g. deﬂectometer).
Regarding the values of the ﬂexural Young’s modulus of the ERP, evaluated from the software
of the test machine in Table 7.5, it is important to highlight that these values are wrong. The Ef
test values of the ERP are overestimated because the software considered a regular square cross
section, while the ERP specimens had a hollow square cross section. The values of Ef calculated
from the displacement are correct because the correct inertia was considered in the ﬂexural stress
calculation. In conclusion, it is possible to state that the ERP is a more elastic material than the
MRP, despite the ﬂexural Young’s modulus evaluated for the two materials are not so different.

PAVILION DESIGN
The sustainable pavilion is designed for expositions regarding plastic pollution and environmental
problems with the aim of raise awareness in public opinion about the massive impact that plastic
has on the planet. Since the plastic tested was received from Bali, an additional purpose of the
pavilion is to inform people about the enormous problem that Indonesia has with plastic pollu-
tion. The exposition is supposed to be in Europe, therefore the pavilion is designed following the
building regulations outlined in the Eurocode. Since the pavilion will be exposed in exhibitions, it
was designed as a temporary structure, which will be dismantled at the end of the exposition. The
sustainable pavilion is designed using the Extruded Recycled Plastic (ERP) widely described in
the previous chapters of this report. The concept was maintained as simple as possible, in order to
facilitate the assembling and dismantling process. A single building component, which can shape
the entire structure, was developed.
8.1
Single Component
After a long design process and several iterations, the building component was developed as a
hollow plastic brick with a triangle shape as illustrated in the pictures below.
Figure 8.1: Building component (front view)
Figure 8.2: Building component (lateral view)
The building component was developed following the concept of classic bricks. The element was
designed with a triangle shape to contribute to the load distribution in the structure. Furthermore,
the basic triangle shape makes easy the interlocking system between different elements. The
building component has the upper point of the triangle cut hence it is designed with a ﬂat edge.
Having a triangular element with a ﬂat point allows to place the element both on the larger base or
on the small one. This important characteristic of the element permits to lay element on top of each
other, without the need of developing a complicated connection system. Another important feature
of the element is that it has a hollowed core. The empty core of the brick shows two principal
advantages. The ﬁrst advantage is a reduced amount of material needed for bricks production
and, as a consequence, its weight is signiﬁcantly lowered. The second important advantage is that
hollow elements contribute to increase the amount of natural daylight coming in the pavilion. The
building component and its dimensions are illustrated in Figure 8.3 below.

Since the sustainable pavilion was intended as a temporary structure, the main focus during the
design process was on keeping the structure as simple as possible. The principal concept for
the pavilion design was the development of a single building component for the entire structure.
This solution has many advantages. First of all, the production process of a single element is
easier than the production of different building components. Furthermore, the utilization of only a
single element makes the assembling and disassembling of the pavilion a lot easier, allowing the
re-usability of the building element and the repeatability of the construction process. From the
single element, an arch structure was developed using the element as a brick. The design decision
is mainly related to the fact that the material has a better resistance under compression loads as
demonstrated by all the tests described in this report.

8.4
Static system
The static system of the sustainable pavilion is an arch, which by deﬁnition, is a constructive
element that can only absorb compressive forces. Arches represent primary structural components,
which were widely used in the past and they are still a current trend in architecture due to their
strengths and architectural appeal. The ﬁrst considerations of the static system are about the
arch terminology. The arch designed is supported by concrete foundations (further described in
subsection 8.7) where the heels of the arch are connected. The heels are the lowest points and
the crown is the highest point of an arch, respectively. The horizontal distance between the two
heels is the span l and the vertical distance between the heels line and the crown is the rise f. The
extrados is the top outer surface of the arch and the intrados is the lower inner surface of the arch.
The arch scheme used as static system is illustrated in the following ﬁgure.
Figure 8.13: design diagram of three-hinged arch and terminology
The fundamental feature of an arched structure is that horizontal reactions appear even if the
structure is subjected to vertical load only. These horizontal reactions HA = HB = H are called
thrust (Figure 8.14), without the thrust, the structure cannot be treated as an arch. The presence of
thrust leads to a fundamental difference in behavior between arches and beams.
95
==PAGE:110==
Figure 8.14: Reactions of the arch
The bending moments in arches are smaller than in beams of the same span and loads. The
advantages of arches over beams increase as the length of the span increases. The Presence of
thrust demands reinforcement of the part of a structure which is subjected to horizontal force. The
thrust is usually absorbed by a tie, but the pavilion designed does not include any tie. The static
system designed for the sustainable pavilion is a two hinged arch because has more rigidity than
three hinged arches [27]. The characteristic distribution of the maximum bending moments of a
two hinged arch loaded is showed in Figure 8.15 below.
Figure 8.15: Graph of the maximum bending moment for a two hinged arch
This type of arch is sensitive to the displacements of supports, changes in temperature, and errors
of fabrication [27]. The arch designed for the pavilion is built up of a series of ERP blocks that
mainly sustain compressive forces. The arch has a thickness of 150 mm (the same thickness of the
ERP blocks), which gives to the arch a certain bending resistance. The arch structure developed
in this report is not designed with a tie, thus, there are no elements that can ”pull” the arch.

8.5
Loads deﬁnition
The loads acting on the sustainable pavilion are considered applied to the arch structure, selected as
a static system. Arches, as main structural components, are subject to a variety of loads depending
on the purpose of the arch and conditions of its operation. For the arch structure designed, the
loads considered are:
• Vertical action: Self weight
• Horizontal action: Wind Load
No live loads are considered because the pavilion is a temporary structure with a not accessible
roof. Furthermore, since no installations are provided in the pavilion, no maintenance is needed
on the roof which will be completely not accessible. The above mentioned loads act mainly in the
longitudinal plane of symmetry of the arch and lead to in-plane bending.
Self weight
For the evaluation of the vertical loads, it was assumed that only the dead load resulting from the
self weight is acting on the vault structure. The self weight was evaluated ﬁrst, on a single arch and
then extended on the overall vault structure. As already mentioned in subsection 8.1 the weight of
one brick is Wbrick = 15.385 Kg which converted in Newton gives a load for each brick of Wbrick
= 150.87 N. Since the one sector of the arch consists of two brick connected, the weight of one
sector of the arch is two times the weight of one brick, thus:
Wsector = 2 · Wbrick = 2 · 150.87 = 301.74N
(8.1)
As already illustrated in Figure 8.4 , the arch is divided in 24 sectors and built with a total of 48
bricks. Therefore, the weight of the arch is calculated as following.
Warch = 24 · Wsector = 24 · 301.74 = 7241.76N
(8.2)
In the previous subsection 8.1 was explained that the overall vault structure consist of 16 arches
juxtaposed and connect to each other, as illustrated in Figure 8.8. Thereby, since the self weight
of one arch is Warch = 7241.76 N, the total weight of the vault structure was calculated as:
Wvault = 16 · Warch = 16 · 7241.76 = 115868N
(8.3)

A signiﬁcant load for arched structures is the wind pressure. For the evaluation of the horizontal
loads, it was assumed that only the wind load is acting on the structure. The wind load was
evaluated only for the case of wind acting parallel to the arch plane as illustrated in Figure 8.16

Generally, in the case of tall arches, the in-plane wind loads lead to signiﬁcant internal forces in the
arch. Furthermore, if an arch has a small own weight, then the formation of the negative reactions
is possible; this dangerous phenomenon leads to the separation of the arch from the support or
from the foundations [27]. The wind pressure directed perpendicular to the plane of the arch leads
to the out-of-plane bending of the arch. These loads are absorbed by bracing between arches.
In the pavilion design, 16 juxtaposed arches are connected together. This design choice should
help to partially reduce the out-of-plane bending due to wind directed perpendicular to the arch.
A dangerous phenomenon is often observed in the case of an arched cover with open sides. The
wind pressure, parallel to an open aperture, ﬂows around and creates a vacuum inside. As a result,
the positive pressure onto the arch increases and suction decreases.

8.6
Structural Calculations
A comprehensive structural analysis includes strength and stability analysis. Strength analysis
(static analysis) deals with the determination of internal forces and deﬂections of the arch due to
the action of static loads only. Stability analysis deals with the determination of loads which leads
to new forms of equilibrium (the loss of stability) of the arch. The structure was designed with a
parabolic arch shape, which employs the principle that when weights are uniformly applied to the
arch, the internal compression resulting from those weights will follow a parabolic proﬁle. The
parabolic arch shape produces the most thrust at the base but can span the largest areas. For the
analysis, the following data are outlined and speciﬁed:
• Type of arch: two hinged arch
• Shape: parabolic
• Dimensions: span l = 8.48 m - rise f = 3.00 m
• Location of supports: foundations at the base on the same level
• Area of the supports: Aarch.base = tbrick · warch = 150 · 750 = 112 500 mm2
• Presence of tie: no
• Number of arches: one - single arch
The assumptions made for the structural calculations of the sustainable pavilion are listed below:
1. The ERP used as a material of the arch obeys Hooke’s law (physically linear statement).
2. Deﬂections of the arches are small compared with the span of the arch (geometrically linear
statement).
3. Supports are considered elastic, thus the relationship between deﬂection of constraint and
the corresponding reaction is linear.
4. Although the real arch is ”out-of-plane”, which means that the arch is not spanning in one
straight plane, the static system of the arch is considered anyway on one plane to simplify
the calculations.
5. The loads are applied in the longitudinal plane of symmetry of the arch.

8.8
Further developments
The sustainable pavilion design analyzed in this report, was focused more on the vault structure
built with ERP elements because the entire project aimed to evaluate the characteristics of this
new material. Although many aspects of the design were considered in this chapter, there are still
other aspects of the project which should be further discussed and developed. Some of the pavilion
components, which were not included in this report, are listed and commented in this section.
The connections between each ERP brick element were not analyzed in this report. Although
connections are a fundamental part of building design, in this report the bricks are supposed to
be ﬁrmly connected to each other. It might be interesting to further investigate a detailed type
of connection which can seal completely the brick together on every side and make the pavilion
waterproof. The connection should be strong enough to sustain loads of the overall structure and
possibly be a part itself of the brick made out of ERP. The brick with the layer of glass connected
in the center of the element, utilized on the roof of the structure needs more developments and
considerations. Regarding the foundations, a possible solution for the pavilion design would be
a concrete foundation that can sustain all the loads transferred from the ERP structure. The most
important feature of the foundation is that it should guarantee the inclination of 22.4 ◦designed
for the base of the arch, which will ensure the overall structural stability. It would be interesting
to investigate also a connection between the concrete foundations and the ERP bricks.
The next step in the design of the sustainable pavilion would be investigate more the structure.
Since the model studied in this report was calculated with some assumptions to simplify the
project, it would be interesting to calculate the vault as a real structure. Other loads like live
loads, snow loads and seismic load with the load combinations provided in the Eurocode should
be considered in order to verify the structure for every possible scenario.

CONCLUSION
The research described in this report aimed to determine the mechanical characteristics of two
different types of plastics, the Extruded recycled Plastic (ERP) and the Molded Recycled Plastic
(MRP). During the evaluation of the mechanical characteristics of the material, other aspects of
the ERP and MRP were outlined. For example, the imperfections related to the production process
were identiﬁed, described and suggestions to improve the material are provided in the report. The
material qualities were documented thoughtfully providing useful information and a complete un-
derstanding of the recycled plastics in terms of mechanical properties.
The report provides a general knowledge about the recycled plastics studied, including the me-
chanical characteristic values which are used in the building industry during the design phase.
The results provided in this research were obtained through different mechanical tests performed
following the standards. The most important results obtained from the tests are listed below:
• Compressive properties: evaluation of the compressive characteristic strength fck and the
Compressive Young’s modulus Ec
• Tensile properties: evaluation of the tensile characteristic strength ftk and the tensile Young’s
modulus Et
• Flexural properties: evaluation of the ﬂexural characteristic strength fmk and the ﬂexural
Young’s modulus Ef
The tests outline clearly that the ERP material is, in general, more resistant than the MRP re-
gardless of the applied load. Therefore, one important outcome of the research, is that the ERP
material is more suitable for applications in the building industry than the MRP.
Concerning the compression tests, for the ERP material, an average maximum load of 59.511 N
was recorded, which is more than two times higher than the compressive loads supported by the
MRP. The ERP showed a longer elastic phase in compression, compared with the MRP which
behaved more like a brittle material. The ERP deformed slowly under compression loads, without
reaching a clear breaking point and showing visible cracks, only in some of the specimens tested.
The data collected from the tensile test, show again that the ERP is signiﬁcantly more resistant
than the MRP. The results outlined in this research, indicate clearly that the ERP is more efﬁcient
in compression than in tension. Although for the ERP a tensile elastic modulus of Et = 625 MPa
and a ftk = 4.83 MPa were evaluated, the tests performed demonstrates that it is better to use the
material for applications which require high resistance to compression rather than tension.
106
==PAGE:121==
The evaluation of the ﬂexural properties of the ERP demonstrates that the material behaves differ-
ently when a bending moment is applied. Some of the ERP specimens showed an elastic behaviour
where the relation between ﬂexural stress and ﬂexural strain was linear. Other specimens of the
batch tested instead, showed a brittle behaviour reaching a clear rupture for low deformation val-
ues. In general, the results from the three point bending tests indicate that it is preferable to avoid
any application of the ERP material for elements subjected to bending moments or any other type
of ﬂexural stresses.
The main purpose of the research described in this report was to investigate the possibility of
building a sustainable pavilion through the utilization of recycled plastics as a structural material.
After several tests were carried out on the materials, it can be stated that the Extruded Recycled
Plastic has a huge potential for the application in different sectors of the building industry. The
pavilion designed in this report is a clear evidence that the material could be employed success-
fully on small projects, with the great advantage of reducing plastic pollution.
The work documented in this report can be considered as the ﬁrst stage of studies, intended to
discover possible applications of recycled plastic in new sectors. There is still a lot of research to
do to implement the material. Although the mechanical properties of the ERP were thoughtfully
evaluated, future researches must be done in this ﬁeld. For example, it would be interesting to
study the material from a chemical point of view, in order to comprehend the intrinsic properties of
recycled plastics. The report outlines that further developments are needed in the recycling process
and in the manufacture of the ERP material, and future work could be done in order to improve and
standardize the production process of ERP. In conclusion, this study is a ﬁrst achievement towards
the interesting challenge of using recycled plastic as structural material which is an innovative
solution, aimed to reduce the massive impact of plastic pollution on the environment.
