Abstract
Yoghurt is an important part of the human diet, now and throughout history. However, by now
there is well established scientific evidence supporting the fact that our food production systems are
in trouble. Global food production accounts for approximately a third of global GHG emissions, of
this, dairy is 4%. In a comparison of plant-based and conventional yoghurt, it was found that the
former lowers the GHG emissions by 69% the land use by 42%. There is therefore precedence for
selecting plant based products over dairy. However, in multiple studies on consumer perception,
plant based yoghurts generally score significantly lower than conventional dairy yoghurts. They are
perceived as more expensive, less tasty and have an overall low acceptability score.
In the production of plant based yoghurts, variety in the fermentation matrixes contribute to
the outcome of the fermentation.
Nuts like almond, with a higher fat content creates a better
mouthfeel and more dense gel structure of the final yoghurts. Soy has higher protein content and
acts as a buffer during fermentation, yielding a higher final lactic acid concentration. They also
aggregate and form a firm gel structure. In yoghurts, sugars are broken down to lactate and lactic
acid via the homofermentative Emden Meyerhof Parna pathway by LAB, to provide energy for
growth and survival.
The aim was to develop methods for determining the concentration of selected macronutrients,
in plant based dairy alternatives. For this purpose, 40 different plant-based drinks were selected
and categorised by the different fermentation matrixes: oat, soy, almond, rice and mixed. The plant
drinks were inoculated with 0.04% culture and fermented for 5 hours at 43 °C.
On each raw plant drink, 2 FT-IR measurements and 1 fat (NMR), 1 protein (DUMAS), and 1
sugar and organic acid analysis (HPLC) were taken. For each plant drink 6 samples were fermented,
on samples 1 to 5 a FT-IR measurement was taken. For samples 1 to 3 a reference analysis was
done with the same methods as for the raw samples. Sample number 6 was frozen intact for later
analysis if needed.
Out of 40 plant drinks, 29 ended with a pH below 5.0, 9 samples had a pH ≤4.5. Of these,
most were oat plant drinks with higher sugar contents. From the reference data, the highest protein
concentrations were observed in the soy samples 2.5-3-5%. For fat, the highest concentrations were
from almond samples and oats with added oils (3.5-4%). By far, the highest total carbohydrate
concentrations were from the starchy oat and rice samples.
It was also observed that, of the
measured sugars, glucose, sucrose, and maltose were the substrates preferred by the culture as
they decreased at fermentation. The highest concentrations of lactic acid were seen in soy samples
after fermentation (average concentration of 2.23 mg/g).
It is hypothesised that this is due to
the buffering capacity of the proteins. Proteins can increase acid production by maintaining pH
during fermentation and thereby allowing more time for growth and more metabolites at the end
of fermentation.
In general, it was found that models could be built on all selected variables, for both raw and
fermented data. For fat and protein the models were very similar as the content does not alter
notably during fermentation. It could therefore be argued that, the raw and fermented data could
be merged into one model. For total sugar and total carbohydrates, the model could predict the
concentrations with errors comparable to those observed in literature. However, in both the raw
and the fermented data, the concentrations of organic acids were low. Despite this, models were
built with reasonable performances, compared with literature, when additional variable selection
was performed. The overall aim of this thesis was to create tools for better understanding of plant
based dairy alternatives by creating methods for the rapid detection of fat, protein, sugar and
organic acids, and this was done.

Dansk abstrakt
Historisk og i moderne tid, er yoghurt et vigtigt element i hverdags kosten for mennesker verden
over. Generelt bidrager den globale fødevareproduktion med ∼30% af udledningen af drivhusgasser,
heraf udgører mejeriproduker 4%. I en sammenligning af plantebaseret og konventionel yoghurt blev
det observeret, at plantebaseret yoghurt reducerer udledningen ad drivhusgaser med 69% og brug
af landareal med 42%. Der er derfor klare fordele ved at vælge plantebaseret yoghurt produkter.
I flere forbruger undersøgelser, scorer plantebaserede yoghurter generelt lavere end konven-
tionelle mejeriyoghurter. De bliver opfattet som dyrere, mindre velsmagende og har en samlet lav
acceptabilitetsscore. Bedre forståelse af disse produkter fra producenterne side, kunne poteintilt
føre til bedre endelige produkter.
Ved produktion af plantebaserede yoghurter bidrager forskellige fermenteringsmatricer til forskelle
i både fermenteringsprocessen det endelige resultat. Gennerelt, i yoghurter nedbrydes sukker af kul-
turen til mælkesyre via den homofermentative Emden Meyerhof Parna metabolisme vej. Nødder,
som mandler, der har et højere fedtindhold, skaber en bedre mundfornemmelse og en mere tæt
gelstruktur i de endelige yoghurter. Soja har et højere proteinindhold og fungerer som en buffer
under fermenteringen, hvilket resulterer i en højere endelig mælkesyrekoncentration. De bidrager
også til en fast gelstruktur in det endelige produkt.
Formålet var at udvikle hurtigmetoder til at bestemmelse af sukker, syre, fedt og protein in
plantebaserede drikke og yoghurter.
Derfor blev 40 forskellige plantedrikke udvalgt og indelt i
fermenteringsmatricer: havre, soja, mandler, ris og blandet (mixed). De blev inokuleret med 0,04%
kultur og fermenteret i 5 timer ved 43 °C .
På hver rå plantedrik blev der foretaget 2 FT-IR målinger samt 1 fedt (NMR), 1 protein
(DUMAS) og 1 sukker- og organisk syreanalyse (HPLC). For hver enkel plantebaseret drik blev der
fermenteret 6 replikater; for prøve nummer 1 til 5 blev der foretaget en FT-IR-måling. For prøve
nummer 1 til 3 blev der udført en referenceanalyse med de samme metoder som for de rå prøver.
Af de 40 plantedrikke endte 29 med en pH ≤5,0, og 9 prøver havde en pH ≤4,5. De fleste
af disse var havredrikke med højere sukkerindhold. Ifølge reference-data blev de højeste protein-
koncentrationer observeret i sojaprodukterne (2,5-3,5%). For fedt, var de højeste koncentrationer
fra mandeldrikke og havredrikke med tilsat olie (3,5-4%). De absolut højeste koncentrationer af
kulhydrater blev fundet i stivelsesholdige havre- og risdrikke. Det blev også observeret, at af de
målte sukkre, var glukose, sukrose og maltose de foretrukne substrater for kulturen. De højeste
koncentrationer af mælkesyre blev observeret i sojaprodukter efter fermenteringen (gennemsnitlig
koncentration 2,23 mg/g). Generelt blev der udviklet modeller for både for rå og fermenterede data
der er sammenlignelige med dem observeret i literaturen. Hvad angår fedt og protein, er modellerne
meget ens, da indholdet ikke ændrede sig markant under fermenteringen. For fremtidige modleller
kunne det derfor overvejes at kombinere de rå og fermenterede data i en model. For modellerne
bygget på totalt sukker og totale kulhydrater er forkellen mellem rå og fermenterede data støre.
Gennerelt var koncentrationerne af organiskesyrer lave i både de rå og fermenterede data. På trods
af dette blev der bygget modeller med med errors sommenlignelige med dem observeret i literaturen,
dog efter yderligere variable selection.
Det overordnede mål med denne afhandling var at udvikle metoder til hurtig måling af sukre,
syre, fedt og protein, og dette lykkede.

Aim and hypothesis
Plant based dairy alternatives, and in particular yoghurts, currently are currently very varied
and with a great range of different matrixes. Consumers have certain expectations of both yoghurt
and ’plant drink’ type products and currently they are not, always, being met. One reason for
this is the sheer variety; raw materials like oats, soy, and almond do not behave the same as
dairy products when fermented, they require different processes and, often, additives. While great
improvements have been made over the last few years, further development is needed to meet
consumer expectations. One useful tool for this, could be rapid methods for measuring the fat,
protein, carbohydrates, sugars, and organic acid content before and after fermentation. It is known
that such models were developed with great success for conventional dairy products. Therefore, it
was hypothesized that developing such models for plant drinks and yoghurts should also be possible.
To do this, a sturdy calibration dataset with relevant reference methods is required. The experiment
was therefore designed to understand if it was possible to develop these models, and to test and
understand the model performances.
In short, the overall aim of this thesis were to develop rapid methods for measuring the sugar,
fat, protein, and organic acid content of plant based yoghurts and drinks. In order to achieve this,
several subaims were formulated:
• To understand the importance of plant based dairy alternatives, in today’s society, in terms
of impact on GHG emission.
• To understand some of the barriers and challenges against plant based dairy alternatives and
the consumer perception of them.
• Understand the production and fermentation of plant based yoghurts, in comparison to con-
ventional yoghurt. How different commercially available plant based drinks, perform during
fermentation.
• Selecting and utilising relevant reference methods for measuring protein, fat, carbohydrates
and organic acids, here included selection of relevant nitrogen factor for determination of
protein.
• Designing and compiling a calibration dataset capable of modelling of the mentioned nutrients.
• Developing chemometric models capable of predicting the fat, sugar, protein, and organic acid
concentrations in plant-based yoghurts and drinks. Here included, testing different variable
selection and preprocessing methods for the best possible results.
• To critically evaluate and discuss model performances by comparing the prediction and cross-
validation errors of different models.
2
Problem definition
Develop rapid methods for measuring the concentration of fat, protein, total carbohydrate, total
sugar and total organic acids, in plant-based drinks and yoghurts, by use of FT-IR. To critically
evaluate and discuss the model performances.

Introduction
The earliest recorded consumption of a yoghurt-like product goes all the way back to 6000bc
India, where the health promoting properties were remarked upon (Fisberg and Machado, 2015).
Since then, in both historical and modern societies, yoghurt has remained an important part of
the human diet. It served as a way of preserving nutrient rich dairy products without access to
refrigeration or modern preservation techniques, like UHT treatment (Bylund, 1995). It also makes
dairy accessible to a much broader spectrum of the population. In the world today, 57-65% of
the population is lactose intolerant (Catanzaro et al., 2021). During the fermentation process, the
majority of lactose is broken down by the culture, which make yoghurt suitable for many people,
who would otherwise be unable to consume dairy (Bylund, 1995).
While yoghurt in particular, and dairy in general, are important products, they are also known
to contribute to higher greenhouse gas emissions (GHG) (Willett et al., 2019). Given the current
climate crisis, it is of absolutely vital importance that we implement changes, urgency. Global food
production accounts for 34% of all GHG emissions (Crippa et al., 2021), dairy product account for
4% of global emissions, so there is certainly room for improvement (York et al., 2018). In a world
of rising demand for dairy, and dairy products, alternatives could help meet the rising demand and,
perhaps, replace some of the current dairy production (York et al., 2018).
At this time the one of the biggest barriers against plant based yoghurts, is consumer liking;
they have off-tastes, off-colours and unpleasant textures. Another very important factor for food
purchases, is consumer acceptability; consumers are unfamiliar with these products, and they may
not currently correspond with what is expected of a yoghurt product. Conventional yoghurt is a
nutritionally dense food, many plant based alternatives, on the other hand, contain more carbohy-
drates and more sugar with poorer nutritional profiles (Moore et al., 2018), (Craig and Brothers,
2021). In general, further development is therefore needed before plant based yoghurts becomes a
viable alternative on the market.
A better understanding of plant-based yoghurts, their composition, processing, and nutrition
could help food producers formulate products with higher sensory profiles, better nutrition, and
still keep production cost low.
Currently, many such methods and models exist for dairy products, but not plant based alter-
natives. This is mainly due to interest and time; while conventional dairy has existed for millennia,
translating all this knowledge into non-dairy yoghurt, has proved a challenge. Unlike diary, plant
based yoghurt can consist of a broad variety of bases, depending on availability and desired char-
acteristics. Each base behaves differently when fermented, the process and fermentation matrix
must therefore be adapted to each specific matrix. This also proves an interesting challenge in the
development of rapid methods. The methods must be able to accommodate a great deal of natural
variation and still perform well. To do this, models must be made with a considerable library of
different plant bases, in the calibration dataset. This also includes considerable variation within
each group, as bases may vary considerably depending on the processing.
In general, developing models for plant based yoghurts and drinks pose several interesting
challenges that must be considered when a calibration set is designed. However, in the end, help-
ing producers better understand, develop and improve plant based yoghurts, is as important step
towards a more plant based future.
Developing rapid methods for measuring fat, sugars, protein and organic acids could be a useful
step in this direction.

3.1
Nutrients in conventional- and plant based yoghurts
The nutritional values of conventional dairy yoghurts are standardised and well-defined within
the industry (Bylund, 1995). For plant based yoghurts, on the other hand, the nutrients vary greatly
between the different matrixes, processing methods and producers (TetraPak, nd).
Dairy, conventional, yoghurt is, a nutrient dense food containing, on average, 1.7% fat (0.0-
10.2%), 5.5% total sugars (3.6-9.5%), and 5.4% protein (2.2-11.0%) (Moore et al., 2018). Yoghurt
is also a good source of certain micronutrients such as calcium, phosphorus, and vitamins B1, B2
and B12. Not only is yoghurt nutritionally dense, it also has high bioavailability and the protein
contains all essential amino acids (Mckinley, 2005). However, it must also be noted that the fat
in yoghurt is, for the most part, saturated fat and that many yoghurt products have added sugar
which may offset some of the health benefits (Moore et al., 2018).
In general the picture is more mixed for plant based yoghurts, nutritional content may depend
on matrix and processing (TetraPak, nd). Therefore, when the nutritional content is examined,
doing so in products on the market creates a clearer picture. (Effect of different bases and nutrients
on fermentation and processing, from a manufacturers’ perspective, can be seen in sections, 3.4 and
3.5).
A study on 249 plant-based yoghurts in retail shops across the US found that they, on average,
had 4.6% fat of which an average of 1.6% was saturated. They had an average of 12. 7% carbo-
hydrates of which 6. 7% was sugar. For protein, an average of 4% was found, however, some soy
based Greek style yoghurts had protein contents up to 20%. They also found the enrichment of
plant-based yoghurts occurred in 47% of samples for calcium and 21.7% for vitamin B12 (fortified
to ≥10% of the recommended daily value) (Craig and Brothers, 2021).
It is noted that the percentage of carbohydrates and sugars are much higher in plant-based
yoghurt; however, they do contain more unsaturated fat. While the content in terms of protein is
similar, the bioavailability has not been as extensively studied but is, in general, lower for plant-
based products Clark et al. (2022).
In general, defining the nutritional values of conventional dairy milk is easy. However, for plant
based yoghurts, generalisation can lead to an oversimplification of a great deal of variety. Therefore,
considering individual matrixes and products in necessary for an understanding of these products.
3.2
Consumer perception of plant based yoghurts
Several studies have been made on consumer perception of plant based products, here included
yoghurt. As previously noted, there is much more variation between plant based yoghurts than
dairy yoghurt. The different matrixes all affect the outcome of the final yoghurt, which makes a
comparison more challenging.

3.3
Environmental impact of dairy- and plant based yoghurts
In a world of ever rising temperatures, sea levels, and more extreme weather phenomena,
lowering greenhouse gas emissions (GHG) is of vital importance. On average, global food production
accounts for a third of greenhouse gas emissions, of this, diary accounts for 4% Crippa et al. (2021).
In a comprehensive review of literature, Carlsson Kanyama et al. (2021) found that plant-based
yoghurts had, on average, 69% lower GHG emissions and 42% lower land use when compared
with similar conventional products. Therefore, it is clear that substituting conventional yoghurt
with plant-based alternatives could contribute to lower GHG emissions, if a broader sector of the
population consumed these products.
GHG emissions, such as methane, carbon dioxide and nitrous oxide, are produced by several
levels in the food production chain.
These emissions stem from everything from transport and
machinery to tillage and microorganism in the soil. However, from dairy, methane from ruminating
livestock is a considerable contributor to the GHG emissions of the industry (Willett et al., 2019).
In a more specific analysis, Üçtuğ (2019) found that the majority of the GHG emissions of
yoghurt was from the raw milk production. However, the energy usage was considerably higher for
yoghurt, comparable to that of cheese.

3.4
Fermentation of plant based yoghurts
Different plant bases have been fermented for centuries in different parts of the world. Tofu,
tempeh, and miso are just some fermented soy based products commonly seen in south-east Asia.
Traditionally, either spontaneous fermentation or ’back-sloping’ the use of a previous batch for
fermenting the next, is used. Depending on the product, fermentation cultures are often a mixture
of lactic acid bacteria (LAB), yeasts, and moulds.
Vegetables have been fermented to kimchi
in Korea, sauerkraut in Germany, in remarkably similar processes despite different fermentation
substrates. In both cases, sensory changes and preservation have been the main motivators for
production of these products (Hui and Evranuz, 2012).
While dairy yoghurts have been seen for a long time, plant based yoghurt is a newer concept.
This is likely due to the extra processing required to achieve a product with the desired sensory
quality. Previously, many of the LAB used for producing plant based yoghurts were isolated from
dairy. However, as differences in available nutrients affected the potential growth, dairy strains
performed unreliably in these substrates. More recently, strains isolated from plant bases or adapted
to utilise available nutrients are used (Hui and Evranuz, 2012), (Harper et al., 2022).
In dairy yoghurt, carbohydrate metabolism, lipolysis, and proteolysis activity is responsible for
the majority of sensory changes during fermentation (Harper et al., 2022).
Both for dairy- and plant based yoghurts, different nutrients impact both the process and the
outcome of the fermentations.
For proteins, the LAB cell envelope proteases break large proteins into peptides, which are then
transported into the cell. Here, intracellular endopeptidases and aminopeptidases break them down
into smaller peptides. These are the broken down to amino acids by dipeptidases and tripeptidases.
As LAB have a severely limited ability to synthesise amino acids, this mechanism is vital for cell
survival and growth (Harper et al., 2022).
In plant based products, protein structures are highly variable, depending on the matrix used.
In general, these mechanisms are much less studied than those in dairy products. Plant proteins
contain more tightly packed globular proteins with more beta-sheets, which are less accessible to
proteases. Multiple LAB strains were found to be able to break down the proteins of cereals. Primar-
ily those found in sourdough starters, here included Fructilactobacillus sanfranciscensis. However,
the application for production of plant based yoghurts are limited (Harper et al., 2022). Several
LAB species were able to produce cell envelope proteinases capable of breaking down soy proteins,
here included L. acidophilus and L. helveticus. Another strain L. delbrueckii subsp. bulgaricus was
capable of hydrolysing wheat gliadins (Pescuma et al., 2013).
For soy, the most commonly observed proteins are beta-conglycinin, trimers of alpha- and
alpha’- and beta-sub units and glycinin. While most LAB strains were capable of breaking down
the trimers and the beta-conglycinin, the ability to degrade glycinin was rarer (Aguirre et al., 2008).
In dairy products, the lactose is transported across the cell membrane and hydrolysed into
glucose and galactose. The glucose is then metabolised to lactate, and lactic acid is produced. For
many strains, galactose and other monosaccharide can also be metabolised after transformation
(Harper et al., 2022). LAB used for yoghurt production are always homofermentative. CO2 and
ethanol, the products of the heterofermentative pathway, are considered defects in conventional
yoghurts (Bylund, 1995), (Adams et al., 2016).

two ATP are spent.

Legumes, such as soy, lentils and chickpeas, are often higher in protein and lower in sugars.
In plant based yoghurt, they provide the texture by gel formation and binding of water and oils
(Dhakal et al., 2023). In dairy, proteins contribute to a higher organic acid concentration of the
final yoghurt, by acting as a buffer during the fermentation (Salaün et al., 2005). This buffering
capacity was also found for protein in plant bases. In a study on multiple plant drinks, it was found
that soy protein had the highest buffering capacity, comparable to that found in dairy. The protein
in rice had the lowest buffering capacity, and almond somewhere in-between (Shin et al., 2021). It
must also be considered that rice and oat are not naturally high in protein and, not considering
additives, this is often reflected in the plant drinks.
Nuts such as cashew, almond, coconut and hazel are often high in fat but can also be higher
in protein depending on the variety. Fat contributes to a firmer- and higher viscosity gel in plant
based dairy alternatives (Canon et al., 2022). The fat also contributes to a pleasant mouthfeel
when bound in a gel structure, and the proteins have strong emulsifying properties and can form
water-in-oil emulsions. This is likely due to the polarity/non-polarity ratio of the proteins and the
non-polar side chain composition (Dhakal et al., 2023).
Cereals like oat, rice, corn, and barley have high sugar and starch concentrations.
Oat in
particular have considerable amounts of dietary fibres like beta-glucan. While non-hydrolysed the
fibres can help with gel formation with stronger water retention, they are often inaccessible to the
starter cultures (Dhakal et al., 2023).
The strains used for conventional dairy yoghurt production are Lb. delbrueckii ssp. bulgaricus
and Streptococcus thermophilus Bylund (1995). However, for the fermentation of plant products,
several strains have been tested. In a study of 61 strains, Genet et al. (2023) found that 14 were
capable of fermenting a soy base, to a pH lower than 5.5 after in 6 hours.
In other study on multiple specific Leuconostoc strains, it was found that fermentation in soy
based medium yielded a pH drop to below 5.5 in 6 hours for two of the tested strains (Wätjen et al.,
2023).

Comparison of conventional- and plant based yoghurt production
A comparison of the production of plant based- and conventional dairy yoghurt can be seen in

In the manufacturing of plant and dairy yoghurt, both start with formulation of the fermen-
tation base. Conventional dairy yoghurt is restricted by Danish legislation to include only milk
(Ministeriet for Fødevarer, Landbrug og Fiskeri, 2023).
The fermentation matrix of a plant based yoghurt is often chosen based on multiple factors,
here included availability of raw materials, cost of production, desired texture and flavour, targeted
consumer segments and more. The most commonly used bases are those readily available such as
oats, soy, almond, rice and coconut, but other ingredients such as tiger nut and buckwheat are also
used sometimes (TetraPak, nd), (Adeyanju et al., 2022).
The preparation of the matrix for fermentation, is where the biggest differences are between
conventional and plant-based yoghurt. For many cereals, it may be necessary to cleave the starches
to dextrins with alpha-amylases to make the sugars available to the culture (TetraPak, nd).
Legume bases are often wet ground and heat treated to make protein more bioavailable. Higher
heat treatment of plant based yoghurt have been shown to positively impact the needed fermentation
time. When comparing pasteurized and ultra-high temperature (UHT) treated plant bases, the

5
Results and discussion
During fermentation all samples were observed in terms of changes in odour, colour, and tex-
ture. This was done purely qualitatively and only by the experimentor. Therefore, the scientific
reproducibility of the evaluation is highly questionable. However, as some patters were observed
that corresponded with those observed in literature, they were kept in the report. The fermenta-
tion process varied considerably between matrixes, depending on the nutritional content. For many
low-sugar varieties, a pH drop of ≤1 was observed. These samples also lacked any of the expected
aromas of a yoghurt. For the higher sugar oat and rice drinks, a pH drop was observed early in the
fermentation, with textures remaining thin and ’watery’ and, for many samples, lumpy. This was
also the findings of Wang et al. (2023) who described the texture as ’loose’ and ’coarse.’ They often
had a distinct yoghurt-like odour after fermentation. This is also consistent with the findings of
Wang et al. (2023) where it was observed that the volatile flavour compounds in oat yoghurt were
more similar to that of conventional yoghurt, than those of soy based yoghurts.
For the high protein soy drinks, a notable change texture was observed, with many of them
achieving the desirable thick texture of a set yogurt. This was also found by Grasso et al. (2020) and
Wang et al. (2023), where the firmness of the gel in soy-based yoghurt was most similar to that of
conventional yogurt. They theorised that this was caused by cluster formations due to hydrophobic
interaction of heat denaturated proteins. During the pH drop at fermentation, the charge of the
proteins was neutralised and gel formation could occur (Lucey et al., 2022), (Brückner-Gühmann
et al., 2019). However, after stirring, the structure reverted to the thin and almost ’watery’ texture
observed for many of the other samples.
These samples also had a distinct yoghurt-like odour
depending on the success of fermentation, that is, the pH drop.
For the high fat samples, like the almond and coconut drinks, the effect seemed to depend
on the other ingredients present. Several sources theorised that the combination of protein and fat
helped create a texture and mouthfeel similar to that of conventional yoghurts (Dhakal et al., 2023),
(Grasso et al., 2020), (Mehta et al., 2023). The gellated proteins captured fat globules in the matrix
creating a ’pleasant’ mouthfeel.
The colour of the yoghurts depended largely on the matrix in question. As no notable changes
were observed during the fermentation, many of the off-white or beige oat drinks remained so, while
the white coconut, rice mixture remained a greyish white.

better in the pre-experiment than in the main one. The differences might be due to differences

experiment was, on average, 41.0 - 42.3 °C, for the third pre-experiment this was lower, 40 - 41

caused the difference, despite using the same setting.
The difference in fermentation rates might also be due to oxidative stress, in pre-experiments.
During the fermentation, the lid was regularly opened to measure temperature and pH. Inevitably,
the sample was stirred slightly every time the probe was inserted. However, in theory this should
have made the performance poorer in the pre-experiment, as most LAB are aerotolerant anaerobes
Adams et al. (2016).

From appendix 6, figure 31, it is clear that there are some discrepancies, between the pH
measured at the beginning and end of the main experiment, and the data found in the pH (iCINAC)
experiments. There seem to be no observable patter, as to which is higher, sometimes the iCINAC
was higher, sometimes the main experiment was. As the same procedure was followed for both
experiments (adjusted for varying sample volume) inoculation differences are unlikely to be the
cause.
One suggestion might be that the lid of the iCINAC experiment with the addition of the pH
probes, allowed more oxygen circulation, than the closely sealed lid of the main experiment. Zourari
et al. (1992) found that in the presence of oxygen, yoghurt culture produced high concentrations of
hydrogen peroxide, which inhibited growth and acid production. This, however, does not account for
the fact that sometimes the iCINAC sample performed better, and sometimes the main experiment
did.
Another reason might be the volume difference, cultures with the same inoculation rate, but
different media volumes may act differently. Speers and Stokes (2009) found that, for yeasts, the
fermenting volume had a statistically significant effect in the pH in beer products.
In a study on bacterial morphogenesis, it was found that the surface to volume ratio strongly
affected the cell size and shape of most studied strains. This, in turn, affected the growth rates at
the surface and in the liquid. Depending, of course, on the culture in question (Harris and Theriot,
2018).
In an anaerobe culture the surface, oxygenated, cells are expected to grow more slowly
compared to cells suspended in the liquid.
One sample in particular (ID039) behaved very strangely, in the iCINAC experiment, suddenly
dropping to a pH below 3, before rising to expected levels. The sample was the Coop365, oat drink,
inoculated with exactly the same procedure and at the same time as the remaining samples. It is
therefore not likely that this is the cause of the deviation. As this phenomenon lasted about 1.5 h it
is also unlikely that it was caused by an unfortunate probe movement. This means that the primary
cause was likely a defective probe or an instrument error, however, this cannot be confirmed from
the data.
5.6.2
Modelling of pH
Multiple methods were tested for modelling of pH, both with raw and fermented data. No
combination of pre-processing or variable selection, manual or iPLS could derive a meaningful
model. These models were therefore not included. This might be due to insufficient data, as others
have succeeded in building models on pH in plant yoghurt (Greulich et al., 2023). However, as
FT-IR measures the configuration of the chemical bonds of the molecules, (Pavia et al., 2015),
changes in pH rarely results in changes large enough to be detected on its own. More commonly,
the products of the fermentation, like organic acids, are used to measure the success of the process.

culture.

pasteurized and homogenized soy protein had the highest buffering capacity, closely followed by
almond proteins. In contrast, rice proteins had a significantly lower buffering capacity.

degree. This might be due to lower protein contents, especially for almond samples, but it is likely
also due to differences in protein structures and composition. Harper et al. (2022) found that the
protein structure, in particular the quantity of β-sheet structures, affected the availability of the
proteins during fermentations. Soy in particular had a higher β-sheet content, when compared with
other plant based proteins.

Carbohydrates
For carbohydrates, one replicate per plant drink was analysed for both the fermented and the
raw data. Within the dataset several values were below the limit of quantification (LOQ), that is
the value could be any number between 0 and LOQ. When modelling, an integer is needed therefore
some assumptions had to be made. Therefore, two scenarios were tested, values calculated on ’LOQ
= 0’ and values where the ’LOQ’ was used. That is, in the calculation, when the value for a sugar
was at LOQ, either 0 or the LOQ itself was added to the sum in the calculation. To determine
which method was more accurate, both scenarios were modelled, and the results compared (later
sections).
For interest, the data was summed into total carbs, total polysaccharides and total sugar. In the
total sugar, the values of Galactose, Glucose, Tagatose, Fructose, Sucrose, allo-Lactose and Lactose
were summed. In total polysaccharides, Maltose, Maltotriose, Maltotetraose, Maltopentaose were
included. For total carbohydrates, all available data was summed. In all sums several sugars were
at LOQ for all samples, that is no sample had concentrations above LOQ, in particular Galactose,
Tagatose, allo-Lactose and Lactose. It might be argued that these should be excluded from the
calculations, but for the sake of consistency they were included. These totals were also calculated
and modelled with both scenarios, ’LOQ’ and ’LOQ=0’. However, only totals calculated with ’LOQ’
are shown in the tables. From the analysis, maltose was included in the polysaccharides as it was
run with the same method. An overview of selected sugars and carbohydrates compiled by matrix

Glucose is utilised directly via the glycolic Emden Meyerhof Parna pathway, where lactic acid
is generated. Sucrose and maltose, are enzymatically converted to glucose by isomerases before
glycolysis by the same pathway (Adams et al., 2016), (Madigan et al., 2018).

strained utilised glucose and lactose best, with poorer performance by other sugars. However, as all
tested strains in the paper were isolated from dairy, they are adapted to milk nutrients. The Vega
premium (TM) culture used in the experiment was specifically designed for plant based yoghurts.
The culture is therefore adapted to metabolise different (available) nutrients. This might account
for the improved sucrose metabolism.
In another study, the metabolism of Streptococcus thermophilus was studied during dairy fer-
mentations. Here they found that glucose, sucrose and lactose were all fermented, however, the
strains tested were unable to metabolised galactose (Iyer et al., 2010).

of sucrose in soy based yoghurts after fermentation. They also found considerable concentrations
of glucose, sucrose, and maltose, in oat based samples. This corresponds with what was found in
the experiment, however, with some variation to account for production differences and additive.
In general, Part et al. (2023) commented on ’considerably heterogeneity’ in the composition of the
samples which was also the case for this experiment.
No measure of uncertainty is known for this data, which is a huge error, as there is no way
of verifying the results. The data was analysed as it was still useful, but in principle the results
could be wildly misleading. It might be argued that the raw and the fermented data on the same
samples could be used to gain some idea of the validity of the results, if they deviate well beyond

5.9.3
Raw PLS models
All variable selection was done manually, that is, visibly noisy or insignificant areas were re-
moved. Automatic variable selection with iPLS was tested but showed no significant improvement,
except for the models on organic acids. For cross-validation, Venetian blinds were used with a thick-
ness of two samples per blind, to make sure replicates were not separated. The data was split 65%
to 35% for calibration and validation data sets. Both the root mean squared error of cross-validation
(RMSECV) and the root mean squared error of prediction (RMSEP) were used to evaluated model
fitness. For the protein models both methods for calculating the final protein content were tested.
For several of the sugars and organic acids, the detected levels were below the limit of quantifi-
cation (LOQ). In these cases, when the sums were calculated, two scenarios were tested; the values
were set to ’LOQ=0’ and ’LOQ’, and both were modelled. This is, of course, inaccurate as the level
is not necessarily zero or LOQ but somewhere in between.

Conclusion and perspectives
The rise of the global temperature and the emission of GHG gasses is a worldwide problem.
Plant-based yoghurts have considerably lower emission GHG and land used when compared to
conventional dairy alternatives, they can therefore be part of the solution towards a more sustainable
diet. However, in sensory tests, plant-based products perform very poorly in both taste, appearance
and texture. To increase consumer acceptability, further development is necessary. As a part of
this, better methods for understanding the composition of these products could be useful for food
manufacturers. Chemometric models based on a comprehensive and well-defined calibration dataset
can measure the content rapidly and without the need for further expensive chemical analysis. No
comprehensive models were found on the macronutrient concentrations of plant based yoghurts in
literature. Therefore, the main part of this project was to develop, test and discuss these models.
For this purpose, 40 different commercially available plant drinks were purchased and one
measurement of carbohydrates (HPLC), organic acids (HPLC), protein (DUMAS), and fat (NMR)
were taken, along with 2 measurements on FT-IR on the raw plant drinks.
The same 40 plant drinks were then fermented in 6 replicates, using a commercially available
starter culture for 5 hours at 43 °C. In fermentation replicate no. 1-3 the protein and fat were
measured with the same method as the raw samples. For replicates 1-5 a FT-IR was also taken.
The experiments were performed over two consecutive weeks, with fermentation of 10 different plant
drinks per experimental day. On the same day as fermentation, the spectroscopic measurement was
taken, with the next day used for reference experiments. This was done to achieve the best possible
covariance between the different variables for prediction purposes.
The protein was found to be the highest for the soy matrixes, around 2.5-3-5%, the lowest by far
were the rice and some oat samples <0.5%. The fat was found to be between 0.5-1%, mostly rice and
unenriched oat samples, and 3.5-4%, mostly almond and enriched oat samples. For carbohydrates,
both individual sugars were considered and a total sugar and total carbohydrate sum was calculated.
Here it was found that, by far, the largest total carbohydrate concentrations were seen in oat and
rice samples, ∼60-90 mg/g. For soy and almond, the concentrations were lower, ∼10-15 mg/g.
From the data, it was also apparent the glucose, sucrose, and maltose were the sugars preferred by
the culture during fermentation. The sugars were converted to lactic acid by the homofermentative
Emden Meyerhof Parna pathway. In the fermented samples, the concentration of lactic acid in soy
was 2.23 mg/g. These were the highest observed in the experiment due to the buffering capacity
of proteins. Despite this, the concentration observed were much lower than those expected from
a conventional yoghurt fermentation. However, lower concentrations were also seen in commercial
plant yoghurts, as noted in the literature. In general, the lactic acid concentrations were lowest for
rice samples.
A correlation analysis was performed for the raw and the fermented data. Here it was found
that the strong positive correlation of total organic acids and protein % could possibly interference
with the prediction. However, when the data was examined further, the correlation appeared to be
mostly due to low values in both datasets.
In the inspection of the spectral data, several noisy areas were removed; mainly the areas before
1000 cm−1, the areas after 3000 cm−1, the water band at 1700-1550 cm−1, and a flat area at 2800-
1800 cm−1 containing no useful information. For the models on organic acids, a further variable
selection with iPLS was required for model improvement.
PCA models were made on both fermented and raw data with results in both showing a
distinct difference between the bases, the sugar/starch bases, oat, and rice were grouped together,
and almond and soy were grouped apart. The mixed samples were grouped differently depending

In both the raw and fermented data, different nitrogen factors for calculation of the protein
content were tested, with the values from ISO and literature consistently performing better than
6.25. All protein models were therefore built with these reference values.
On the raw data, the protein model had a RMSEP of 0.18% R2 = 0.98. For the fermented
data with the same preprecessing, the RMSEP was 0.20%, R2 = 0.96.
For fat, a RMSEP of 0.13% (R2=0.98) was found. The fermented data had a RMSEP of 13%,
R2 = 0.98. Exactly the same as the raw data. In general, little change was observed in the fat
content between raw and fermented samples.
For total carbohydrate and total sugars two scenarios were tested ’LOQ=0’ and ’LOQ’, here
the latter, on average, performed better and was therefore selected. Here, the raw carbohydrate
model had a RMSEP of 5.81 mg/g (R2=0.97), and the fermented a RMSEP: 4.815 mg/g (R2 P:
0.979).
When modelling organic acids, it was necessary to perform further variable selection to improve
the prediction. For the raw data a model with a RMSEP of 0.098 mg/g (R2=0.96) was achieved,
for the fermented data the RMSEP was 0.22 mg/g (R2=0.97). While the error seem very low on
the raw data, it must also be noted that the concentrations observed in the samples were very low.
Despite this, both of the models performed reasonably when compared with the errors observed in
literature.
In conclusion, it was possible to predict the fat, protein, total carbohydrate and total organic
acid content of plant-based yoghurts with reasonable errors.
For further research, it might be
interesting to test the developed models with a completely independent test set to see how they
perform in a real world setting.
From the data it is clear that the differences between the fermented and the raw samples are
not large, especially for fat and protein. It would therefore make sense to combine the raw and
fermented data into one model.
In this report, models for sugars and organic acids are calculated on summed data.
The
potential issue with this is that the model description must be extremely detailed, as to which
specific sugars and organic acids are included in the calculation. If a potential client measured
different acids or sugars, the model prediction would not fit. Also, in these models maltose was
included in the carbohydrate models. Primarily due to the fact, that it was analysed with the
polysaccharides.
It was therefore kept in that data packet.
However, for an industrial model,
inclusion in the total sugar prediction would make more sense.
Arguably, the models on yoghurt do not currently reflect the plant based yoghurts already
commercially available. Those often contain thickeners, acidifying agents and many other additives.
However, the aim was to build models for use in the production of plant based dairy alternatives.
The models could therefore be a useful tool for manufacturers to understand the fermentation
process better and, hopefully, to improve it.
