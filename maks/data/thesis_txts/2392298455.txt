ABSTRACT  
The	   objective	   of	   this	   study	   was	   to	   investigate	   the	   sensory	   properties	   of	   dromedary	  
camel	  milk	  yogurt	  produced	  from	  six	  different	  thermophilic	  LAB	  yogurt	  cultures;	  STI-­‐12,	  
STI-­‐14,	  YF-­‐L904,	  YF-­‐L812,	  YC-­‐X11	  and	  YoFlex	  Express	  1.0,	  provided	  by	  Chr.	  Hansen	  A/S.,	  
to	  characterize	  their	  proteolytic	  activity	  using	  HPLC-­‐MS.	  
Sensory	  evaluation	  was	  performed	  on	  the	  samples	  by	  an	  expert	  panel,	  consisting	  of	  four	  
professionals,	   testing	   for	   eight	   different	   sensory	   attributes;	   sourish,	   sweet,	   stole,	   fat,	  
salt,	  bitter,	  astringent	  and	  fluid.	  The	  aim	  was	  to	  compare	  the	  patterns	  of	  peptides	  that	  
provide	   a	   bad	   taste	   to	   the	   patterns	   of	   peptides	   that	   provide	   a	   better	   taste,	   thereby	  
characterizing	  peptides	  which	  may	  cause	  off-­‐flavours	  in	  camel	  milk	  yogurt.	  
The	  contribution	  of	  the	  cultures	  on	  the	  fluidity	  was	  measured	  by	  sensory	  evaluation	  in	  
comparison	  with	  rheological	  measurements.	  Cow	  milk	  was	  used	  as	  a	  reference	  in	  the	  
study.	  
The	  results	  indicated	  no	  significant	  difference	  between	  the	  taste	  of	  camel	  milk	  yogurts	  
produced	   with	   the	   different	   starter	   cultures,	   except	   for	   the	   stole	   taste	   where	   a	  
statistically	   significant	   difference,	   with	   a	   p-­‐value	   below	   0.05	   significance	   level,	   was	  
detected	   between	   YF-­‐L812	   and	   STI-­‐12	   and	   between	   YF-­‐L812	   and	   YoFlex	   Express	   1.0,	  
with	   YF-­‐L812	   having	   the	   highest	   score	   for	   stole	   taste	   among	   them.	   Additionally,	   the	  
rheological	  measurement	  showed	  no	  texture	  formation	  in	  yogurt	  produced	  from	  camel	  
milk,	  in	  five	  out	  of	  six	  cultures,	  and	  only	  a	  slight	  texture	  formation	  was	  observed	  in	  YF-­‐
L812.	   It	   was	   concluded	   that	   YF-­‐L812	   showed	   the	   most	   distinguishable	   behaviour	   in	  
camel	  milk,	  and	  further	  analysis	  might	  reveal	  peptides	  responsible	  for	  the	  stole	  taste	  
and	  could	  perhaps	  discover	  how	  to	  get	  more	  texture	  formation	  in	  fermented	  camel	  milk.

1   INTRODUCTION	  
Dromedary	   camel	   milk	   has	   a	   similar	   composition	   of	   that	   of	   cow	   milk,	   it	   has	   similar	  
protein	   content,	   but	   a	   higher	   content	   of	   calcium,	   vitamin	   C,	   ash,	   sodium,	   potassium,	  
phosphorous,	   zinc,	   iron	   and	   magnesium.	   Moreover,	   it	   has	   a	   lower	   lactose	   content,	  
contains	  less	  saturated	  fatty	  acid,	  and	  a	  greater	  total	  cholesterol	  content	  (Hashim,	  Khalil,	  
and	  Habib	  2009),	  (Ladjevardi	  et	  al.	  2016).	  Furthermore,	  dromedary	  camel	  milk	  is	  also	  
said	  to	  be	  capable	  of	  countering	  diseases	  like	  hepatitis,	  Alzheimer,	  HIV	  and	  cancer,	  due	  
to	  its	  anti-­‐bacterial	  and	  anti-­‐	  virus	  properties,	  antibody	  essence,	  bioactive	  peptides	  and	  
anti-­‐	  cancer	  components	  (Salami	  et	  al.	  2011),(Meisel	  2004),	  (Fiat	  et	  al.	  1993),	  (Ladjevardi	  
et	   al.	   2016).	   Dromedary	   camel	   milk	   is	   therefor	   considered	   as	   a	   health	   promoting	  
product,	  especially	  when	  it's	  fermented.	  	  
Several	  studies	  have	  shown	  that	  fermented	  camel	  milk	  products	  don’t	  have	  appropriate	  
texture	   formation.	   Rheological	   analysis	   is	   extensively	   used	   in	   the	   food	   industry	   to	  
measure	   and	   obtain	   products	   with	   desired	   texture.	   Moreover,	   analytical	   sensory	  
evaluation,	   can	   be	   used	   to	   tell	   the	   differences	   between	   the	   products,	   regarding	  
organoleptic	  and	  texture	  properties,	  together	  with	  a	  hedonic	  analysis,	  this	  can	  be	  used	  
to	   get	   useful	   information	   for	   improving	   the	   sensory	   quality	   of	   food	   products	   and	  
reaching	  the	  target	  group.	  	  
Previous	   experiments	   made	   in	   the	   course	   23564	   at	   DTU	   (see	   Appendix	   15),	   have	  
indicated	  that	  an	  unpleasant	  taste	  is	  often	  associated	  with	  fermented	  dromedary	  camel	  
milk	   products.	   As	   such,	   there	   was	   also	   a	   noticeable	   difference	   between	   the	   taste	   of	  
different	  cultures	  used	  for	  fermentation.	  Based	  on	  these	  experiments,	  it	  is	  important	  to	  
improve	  the	  quality	  of	  fermented	  camel	  milk,	  to	  get	  fermented	  dromedary	  camel	  milk	  
products	   to	   be	   more	   desirable	   by	   consumers.	   	   The	   aim	   of	   this	   study	   is	   therefore	   to	  
investigate	  the	  peptide	  profile	  of	  dromedary	  camel	  milk	  fermented	  with	  thermophilic	  
LAB	  cultures,	  and	  to	  investigate	  if	  some	  of	  the	  peptides	  can	  be	  linked	  to	  various	  taste	  
attributes,	   including	   bitter	   and	   sourish	   tastes.	   As	   such,	   this	   will	   benefit	   companies	   in	  
improving	  products	  made	  from	  fermented	  dromedary	  camel	  milk.	  Furthermore,	  the	  aim	  
is	  to	  study	  the	  effects	  of	  different	  cultures	  on	  the	  rheological	  properties.

1.2   MILK	  PROTEINS	  	  
Milk	  consists	  of	  around	  3	  %	  protein;	  the	  two	  major	  protein	  categories	  are	  caseins	  and	  
serum	  (whey)	  proteins.	  Caseins	  coagulate	  at	  pH	  4.6	  whereas	  whey	  proteins	  remain	  in	  
the	  solution	  at	  pH	  4.6	  (Fox	  et	  al.	  2015).	  
1.2.1  
Caseins	  	  
The	   composition	   of	   caseins	   in	   cow	   and	   dromedary	   camel	   milk	   are	   listed	   in	   Table	   2	  
below.	  The	  protein	  fraction	  of	  cow	  milk	  consists	  of	  80	  percent	  caseins	  and	  20	  percent	  
whey	  proteins.	  	  The	  ratio	  of	  whey	  protein	  to	  casein	  is	  higher	  in	  camel	  milk	  than	  in	  cow	  
milk,	  but	  lower	  than	  in	  human	  milk	  (Fox	  et	  al.	  2015).	  
The	  main	  4	  caseins	  in	  milk	  are	  α-­‐s1,	  α-­‐s2,	  β-­‐	  and	  κ-­‐caseins,	  together	  they	  form	  the	  casein	  
micelles	   in	   milk	   by	   non-­‐covalent	   aggregation,	   the	   size	   of	   these	   micelles	   in	   cow	   milk	  
varies	  from	  20	  nm	  to	  500	  nm.	  The	  casein	  micelles	  control	  the	  coagulation	  stability	  in	  
milk.	   The	   size	   of	   the	   micelles	   affects	   the	   coagulation	   time.	   Small	   to	   medium	   sized	  
micelles	  gives	  the	  optimum	  coagulation	  time	  (Fox	  et	  al.	  2015).	  	  
Casein	  micelles	  in	  dromedary	  camel	  milk	  are	  different	  from	  that	  in	  cow	  milk,	  where	  the	  
distribution	  of	  caseins	  where	  shown	  to	  be	  broader	  than	  that	  of	  cow	  milk	  with	  greater	  
number	  of	  large	  particles.	  Cow	  milk	  has	  significantly	  higher	  α-­‐s1	  and	  κ-­‐casein	  compared	  
to	  dromedary	  camel	  milk,	  whereas	  dromedary	  camel	  milk	  has	  instead	  a	  greater	  amount	  
of	  β-­‐casein,	  as	  shown	  on	  Table	  2.

1.3   LACTIC	  ACID	  BACTERIA	  
Lactic	   acid	   bacteria	   (LAB)	   are	  defined	   as	   species	   that	   can	   ferment	   sugar	   and	   produce	  
lactic	  acid	  as	  an	  end	  product.	  They	  are	  widely	  applied	  in	  the	  food	  industry,	  especially	  for	  
fermentation	  purposes,	  as	  they	  are	  well-­‐known	  for	  being	  Generally	  Recognized	  As	  Safe	  
(GRAS)	   microorganisms	   (Widyastuti	   and	   Febrisiantosa	   2014).	   The	   most	   important	  
property	   of	   LAB	   during	   milk	   fermentation	   is	   their	   ability	   to	   acidify	   milk	   and	   their	  
proteolytic	  activities,	  which	  enable	  them	  to	  generate	  flavour	  and	  texture	  from	  protein	  
degradation	  (Griffiths	  and	  Tellez	  2013).	  	  	  
LAB	   can	   be	   categorized	   into	   three	   groups;	   obligatory	   homofermentative,	   facultative	  
heterofermentative	   and	   obligatory	   heterofermentative	   LAB,	   depending	   on	   their	   sugar	  
metabolism.	  The	  first	  group	  coverts	  glucose	  exclusively	  to	  lactic	  acid	  using	  the	  glycolysis	  
==PAGE:9==
	  
10	  
pathway,	  where	  glucose	  is	  converted	  to	  lactic	  acid	  in	  the	  ratio	  of	  two	  molecules	  of	  lactic	  
acid	  for	  each	  glucose	  molecule	  (Endo	  and	  Dicks	  2014).	  Homofermantative	  species	  are	  
recognized	   as	   group	   1	   lactobacilli	   and	   include	   Enterococcaceae,	   Lactobacillaceae	   and	  
Streptococcaceae	  (Endo	  and	  Dicks	  2014).	  	  
The	  second	  group	  facultative	  heterofermentative,	  metabolize	  glucose	  to	  lactic	  acid	  only	  
by	  the	  glycolysis,	  and	  pentoses	  and	  related	  sugars	  only	  by	  phophorketolase	  pathway,	  
the	   species	   that	   are	   included	   in	   this	   group	   are	   Enterococcus,	   Lactococcus,	   Lactovum,	  
Paralactobacillus,	  Pediococcus,	  Streptococcus,	  Vagococcus	  and	  group	  II	  Lactobacillus	  spp	  
(Endo	  and	  Dicks	  2014).	  	  
The	   last	   group	   are	   the	   obligatory	   heterofermentative	   LAB,	   that	   breaks	   down	   glucose,	  
pentose	   and	   related	   sugars	   to	   form	   lactic	   acid,	   acetic	   acid,	   ethanol	   and	   CO2	   by	   the	  
phosphokatolase	   pathway.	   Leuconostoc,	   Oenococcus,	   Weissella	   and	   group	   III	  
Lactobacillus	  spp.	  are	  included	  in	  this	  group	  (Endo	  and	  Dicks	  2014).	  	  
LAB	  cultures	  can	  further	  be	  categorized	  based	  on	  their	  optimum	  growth	  condition,	  there	  
are	   moderate	   thermophilic	   cultures	   that	   have	   optimum	   growth	   conditions	   for	  
temperatures	   between	   40	   –	   60	   °	   C	   and	   mesophilic	   cultures	   that	   can	   grow	   with	  
temperatures	  below	  40	  °	  C	  	  (Messner	  and	  Sleytr	  1992).	  
1.4   METABOLIC	  PATHWAYS	  OF	  FLAVOR	  COMPONENTS	  
Fermented	  products	  have	  some	  characteristics	  taste,	  made	  from	  biochemical	  reactions	  
during	  the	  fermentation	  process.	  The	  flavor	  in	  fermented	  products	  comes	  from	  3	  major	  
components;	   lactose,	   lipids	   and	   proteins	   (Smit	   2002),	   (McSweeney	   and	   Sousa	   2000),	  
(Fox	  1995).	  	  
1.4.1  
Lactose	  and	  citrate	  
Lactose	   is	   hydrolyzed	   to	   galactose	   and	   glucose	   by	   starter	   cultures.	   Galactose	   is	   then	  
converted	  to	  either	  glucose-­‐6-­‐P	  by	  galactose	  positive	  starter	  cultures	  and	  leuconoctocs	  
through	   the	   Leloir	   pathway,	   or	   through	   tagatose	   pathway	   to	   glyceraldehyde-­‐3-­‐P	  
(Widyastuti	  and	  Febrisiantosa	  2014).	  Glyceraldehyde-­‐3-­‐P	  is	  then	  converted	  to	  pyruvate,	  
which	   is	   the	   starter	   component	   for	   formation	   of	   flavor	   components	   such	   as;	  
acetaldehyde,	  diacetyl	  and	  acetate(Marilley	  and	  Casey	  2004)	  see	  Figure	  1.	  
==PAGE:10==

Thermophillic	  starter	  cultures	  are	  generally	  citrate	  negative	  (Cogan	  and	  Hill,	  1993),	  but	  
bacteria	  that	  can	  metabolize	  citrate,	  can	  produce	  diacetyl,	  acetoin	  and	  acetolactate,	  (de	  
Figueroa	  et	  al.	  2000).	  
1.4.2  
Milk	  fat	  	  
Studies	  have	  shown	  that	  milk	  fat	  have	  an	  impact	  on	  taste	  of	  fermented	  products,	  by	  the	  
replacement	  of	  whole	  milk	  with	  skim	  milk.	  Lipolysis	  of	  triglycerides	  from	  milk	  produces	  a	  
high	  concentration	  of	  short-­‐	  and	  intermediate-­‐	  chain	  fatty	  acids	  (Bills	  and	  Day	  1964).	  	  
Short-­‐	   chain	   fatty	   acids	   are	   desirable	   since	   they	   have	   a	   high	   impact	   on	   aroma	   when	  
combined	  with	  other	  flavor	  components.	  They	  act	  as	  substrates	  for	  enzymatic	  reactions	  
yielding	   methyl	   ketones	   and	   secondary	   alcohols	   and	   esterification	   of	   hydroxyacids	  
yielding	   lactones.	   Fatty	   acids	   can	   react	   with	   alcohols	   and	   form	   esters	   such	   as;	   ethyl	  
acetate,	  ethyl	  octanoate,	  and	  methyl	  butanoate	  (Cadwallader	  2009)	  see	  Figure	  1.	  
1.4.3  
Proteolysis	  of	  milk	  proteins	  
Proteolysis	  of	  milk	  proteins	  have	  a	  direct	  impact	  on	  flavor	  enhancement	  in	  fermented	  
products,	  by	  releasing	  peptides	  and	  amino	  acids	  contributing	  to	  the	  aroma.	  	  
When	   lactococci	   lactis	   and	   other	   LAB	   grows	   in	   milk	   proteinase	   and	   oligopeptide	  
transport	  systems	  plays	  a	  crucial	  role	  (Kim	  2014).	  The	  components	  that	  are	  involved	  in	  
casein	   hydrolysis	   and	   that	   forms	   the	   proteolytic	   system	   are,	   a	   cell-­‐wall	   associated	  
proteinase,	   amino	   acid	   transport	   systems,	   extracellular	   peptidase	   and	   intra	   cellular	  
peptidase.	   Moreover,	   the	   proteolytic	   system	   requires	   a	   key	   enzyme	   known	   as	   cell-­‐
envelope	  proteinase	  (CEP),	  which	  covers	  over	  40	  %	  of	  the	   cleaving	  of	  peptide	  bonds,	  
into	   more	   than	   100	   varieties	   of	   oligopeptides	   (Kim	   2014),	   (Griffiths	   and	   Tellez	   2013).	  
There	  are	  several	  types	  of	  proteinases	  sorted	  by	  their	  specificity	  to	  casein,	  there	  is	  the	  
type	  that	  mainly	  hydrolyze	  ß-­‐casein,	  called	  PI-­‐type	  proteinase	  ,	  the	  type	  that	  hydrolyze	  
both	  ß-­‐,	  k-­‐	  and	  a-­‐casein	  is	  PIII-­‐type	  and	  a	  type	  that	  can	  hydrolyze	  ß-­‐casein	  with	  a	  PI-­‐type	  
specificity	  and	  a-­‐casein	  with	  a	  PIII-­‐type	  specificity,	  is	  an	  intermediate	  type	  called	  PI/PIII-­‐
type	  proteinase	  (Vos	  et	  al.	  1991).	  	  
Lactococci	   can	   transport	   di-­‐,	   tri-­‐	   and	   oligopeptides	   for	   the	   uptake	   of	   nitrogenous	  
compounds	  by	  the	  cell,	  with	  its	  ability	  to	  produce	  three	  different	  transport	  systems.	  The	  
peptides	   can	   then	   be	   hydrolyzed	   into	   amino	   acids	   that	   are	   crucial	   for	   its	   growth.	  
Released	   amino	   acids	   undergoes	   biochemical	   reactions	   such	   as;	   decarboxylation,	  
transmission,	  reduction	  and	  dehydrogenation,	  producing	  an	  extensive	  variety	  of	  flavor

1.5   PROTEOMIC	  BY	  HPLC-­‐	  MS	  	  
	  High	  Performance	  Liquid	  Chromatography-­‐	  Mass	  Spectrometry	  (HPLC-­‐MS)	  is	  a	  method	  
used	   to	   separate	   and	   detect	   chemical	   and	   biological	   compounds	   in	   a	   mixture.	   The	  
compounds	   are	   separated	   through	   the	   liquid	   chromatography	   and	   detected	   through	  
mass	  spectrometry.	  
HPLC-­‐MS	   is	   a	   technique	   capable	   of	   analysing	   a	   wider	   range	   of	   compounds	   including	  
thermally	   labile,	   have	   a	   high	   molecular	   mass,	   high	   polarity	   compounds	   and	   even	  
proteins	  via	  proteomic	  analysis.	  	  
Proteomic	   analysis	   is	   a	   method	   used	   for	   identification	   and	   quantification	   of	   proteins	  
(proteome)	  of	  a	  biological	  system	  or	  organism,	  at	  a	  specific	  point	  of	  time.	  HPLC-­‐MS	  is	  
widely	  used	  for	  proteomic	  analysis.	  
The	  sample	  containing	  the	  compounds	  of	  interest	  (analyte)	  is	  injected	  into	  HPLC	  column,	  
the	   peptides	   are	   then	   separated	   based	   on	   their	   interaction	   with	   the	   coating	   on	   the	  
column	  (stationary	  phase)	  and	  with	  solvent	  eluting	  (mobile	  phase)	  through	  the	  column.	  
By	  the	  time	  the	  peptides	  pass	  through	  the	  column,	  the	  peptides	  are	  accelerated	  through	  
an	  electric	  field,	  where	  they	  get	  ionised.	  Then,	  the	  molecules	  are	  separated	  per	  their	  
mass-­‐charge	  ratio	  m/z,	  in	  this	  case	  with	  the	  help	  of	  the	  quadrupole	  mass	  filter,	  allowing	  
only	  the	  particular	  fragments	  to	  reach	  the	  MS	  detector	  (Harris	  2010).	  The	  fragments	  for	  
the	  particular	  peptides	  are	  assigned	  from	  database,	  and	  thus	  the	  protein	  sequences	  are	  
predicted.	  	  The	  result	  comes	  as	  a	  chromatogram	  including	  spectra	  of	  the	  ion	  fragments,	  
with	   retention	   time	  –	   the	   time	   it	   takes	   the	   analyte	   to	   be	   detected,	   starting	   from	   the	  
injection	  and	  until	  the	  compound	  is	  eluted	  –	  on	  the	  x-­‐axis	  and	  intensity	  on	  the	  y-­‐axis	  
(Harris	  2010).

2.1   FERMENTATION	  	  
For	   this	   project,	   dromedary	   camel-­‐	   and	   cow-­‐	   milk	   were	   fermented	   using	   6	   different	  
thermophilic	  yogurt	  starter	  cultures;	  YoFlex	  Express	  1.0,	  YF-­‐L904,	  YF-­‐L812	  and	  YC-­‐X11,	  
STI-­‐12	  and	  STI-­‐14,	  provided	  by	  Chr.	  Hansen	  A/S.	  The	  fermentation	  took	  place	  in	  building	  
227	   at	   DTU,	   dromedary	   camel	   milk	   was	   delivered	   freshly	   from	   Holland.	   Both	   milk	  
variants	  were	  pasteurized	  at	  85	  °	  C	  in	  water	  bath	  for	  20	  minutes,	  followed	  by	  cooling	  
step	  to	  approximately	  40	  °	  C.	  Each	  culture	  bag	  was	  dissolved	  in	  500	  mL	  salt	  water.

2.2   Sensory	  	  
An	  expert	  panel	  consisting	  of	  4	  female	  professionals	  from	  DTU,	  all	  with	  prior	  experience	  
in	   sensory	   evaluation,	   were	   selected	   for	   the	   objective	   and	   descriptive	   sensory	  
evaluation.	  The	  laboratory	  used	  for	  the	  sensory	  evaluation	  followed	  the	  requirements	  
settled	  by	  the	  International	  Organization	  for	  Standardization	  (ISO,	  1993).	  One	  evaluation	  
session	  was	  held	  with	  2	  participants	  of	  the	  expert	  panel	  and	  the	  supervisor	  Egon	  Bech	  
Hansen	  for	  selection	  of	  LAB	  cultures,	  6	  out	  of	  10	  cultures	  were	  selected	  based	  on	  their	  
sensory	  attributes.	  Two	  1-­‐hr	  training	  sessions	  were	  held	  for	  selection	  of	  attributes	  and	  
panel	  training.	  At	  the	  end	  of	  the	  second	  session,	  6	  flavour	  and	  taste	  attributes	  and	  2	  
texture	  attributes,	  were	  chosen.	  	  
The	   sensory	   assessment	   took	   place	   over	   two	   days,	   were	   the	   experts	   were	   served	   6	  
samples	  with	  a	  duplicate	  each	  day	  after	  lunch	  time.	  The	  first	  session	  was	  for	  evaluation	  
of	  fermented	  dromedary	  camel	  milk	  with	  a	  duplicate,	  and	  the	  second	  was	  for	  evaluation	  
of	  fermented	  cow	  milk	  with	  a	  duplicate.	  The	  fermentation	  of	  the	  milks	  was	  prepared	  4	  
days	  prior	  to	  each	  sensory	  evaluation.	  After	  fermentation,	  the	  samples	  were	  stored	  at	  5	  
°	   C,	   70-­‐72	   hours	   prior	   to	   the	   sensory	   evaluation.	   Before	   each	   session	   20	   mL	   of	   each	  
sample	  was	  poured	  into	  transparent	  plastic	  glasses	  allocated	  with	  a	  randomly	  selected	  
code,	  to	  randomize	  the	  samples.	  Additionally,	  water,	  spittoon	  and	  unsalted	  crispbread	  
and	   crackers	   was	   also	   provided	   free	   of	   choice.	   An	   unstructured	   line	   scale,	   where	   the	  
assessors,	  put	  a	  mark	  on	  the	  scale	  individually,	  and	  then	  the	  scores	  of	  each	  sample	  were	  
then	  agreed	  upon	  between	  assessors,	  then	  the	  mark	  was	  sat	  on	  the	  scale	  by	  a	  person	  
not	  participating	  in	  the	  sensory	  evaluation.

4   DISCUSSION	  
The	  spiderweb	  diagram	  on	  Figure	  6	  from	  the	  sensory	  analysis	  shows,	  that	  the	  fermented	  
camel	   milk	   differed	   drastically	   from	   fermented	   cow	   milk	   when	   it	   comes	   to	   the	   stole	  
attribute	  and	  the	  fluidity,	  which	  are	  some	  of	  the	  disadvantages	  camel	  milk	  is	  known	  to	  
have.	  Comparing	  the	  rheological	  measurements	  with	  the	  sensory	  analysis	  indicate	  that	  
the	   both	   agree	   that	   the	   fluidity	   of	   fermented	   camel	   milk	   is	   drastically	   lower	   than	   for	  
fermented	  cow	  milk,	  which	  is	  indicated	  on	  the	  spiderweb	  for	  the	  sensory	  analysis	  and	  
from	   the	   m	   values	   in	   Table	   13	   and	   Table	   14	   for	   the	   rheological	   powerlaw	   model.	  
Furthermore,	  Table	  7	  also	  shows	  that	  there	  are	  statistical	  significant	  differences,	  with	  a	  
significance	  level	  of	  0.05	  between	  the	  means	  of	  some	  of	  the	  independent	  variables	  for	  
fluidity	  with	  a	  p	  value	  as	  low	  as	  0.015.	  	  
The	  n	  and	  m	  values	  for	  the	  different	  cultures	  are	  like	  each	  other	  in	  fermented	  cow	  milk,	  
except	  for	  YF-­‐L812	  that	  has	  a	  higher	  m	  and	  lower	  n	  value.	  The	  fermented	  camel	  milk	  on	  
the	  other	  hand	  had	  different	  m	  and	  n	  values	  but	  they	  were	  close	  to	  each	  other,	  the	  one	  
with	  the	  highest	  m	  value	  is	  also	  YF-­‐L812	  in	  camel	  milk,	  this	  might	  indicate	  that	  YF-­‐L812

The	  differences	  in	  the	  data	  distribution	  is	  higher	  for	  sourish	  and	  bitter	  taste	  than	  for

stole	  taste,	  therefore	  the	  differences	  in	  data	  distribution	  seems	  to	  be	  bigger	  in	  sourish	  
and	   bitter	   taste,	   if	   there	   were	   more	   replicates	   and	   a	   bigger	   expert	   panel,	   significant

5   CONCLUSION	  
Sensory	  properties	  in	  camel-­‐	  and	  cow	  milk	  fermented	  with	  6	  different	  cultures;	  STI-­‐12,	  
STI-­‐14,	   YF-­‐L904,	   YF-­‐L812,	   YC-­‐X11	   and	   YoFlex	   Express	   1.0,	   were	   sensory	   examined,	  
focusing	  on	  the	  organoleptic	  attributes;	  sweet,	  sourish,	  stole,	  fat,	  salt,	  bitter,	  fluid	  and	  
astringent.	  The	  sensory	  evaluation	  was	  performed	  by	  an	  expert	  panel	  consisting	  of	  four	  
professionals.	  
Based	   on	   the	   results	   from	   the	   fermented	   cow	   milk	   sensory	   evaluation,	   no	   statically	  
significant	   difference	   between	   the	   means,	   at	   or	   below	   0.05	   significance	   level,	   were	  
found	   among	   the	   6	   different	   cultures.	   The	   stole	   taste	   in	   fermented	   camel	   milk	   was	  
found	   to	   be	   significantly	   different,	   with	   a	   p-­‐value	   below	   a	   0.05	   significance	   level,	  
between	  YF-­‐L812	  and	  STI-­‐12	  and	  between	  YF-­‐L812	  and	  YoFlex	  Express	  1.0,	  with	  YF-­‐L812	  
having	  the	  highest	  score	  for	  stole	  taste	  among	  them.	  
However,	  the	  results	  showed	  that	  the	  differences	  in	  the	  data	  distribution	  for	  bitter	  and	  
sourish	  taste	  is	  big	  for	  camel	  milk	  samples,	  but	  the	  variances	  between	  the	  replicates	  are	  
great,	   which	   made	   it	   difficult	   to	   find	   statistical	   significance.	   There	   could	   be	   statistical	  
significant	   differences,	   both	   in	   fermented	   camel-­‐	   and	   cow	   milk	   that	   could	   not	   be	  
detected	  in	  the	  present	  study	  since	  the	  resources	  were	  restricted,	  but	  an	  expert	  panel	  
with	  more	  participants	  and	  more	  training,	  could	  help	  reduce	  the	  uncertainty	  and	  the	  
variance	  between	  replicates,	  which	  could	  provide	  more	  reliable	  results.	  The	  proteolytic	  
activity	   was	   not	   characterized,	   since	   most	   of	   the	   samples	   were	   not	   statistically	  
significant	  different	  from	  one	  another.	  Results	  from	  rheological	  measurements	  showed	  
that	   all	   samples	   of	   camel	   and	   cow	   milk	   showed	   a	   shear	   thinning	   behaviour.	  
Furthermore,	  the	  results	  indicated	  that	  the	  viscosity	  in	  camel	  milk	  samples	  were	  very	  
low	  compared	  to	  cow	  milk	  samples,	  with	  YF-­‐L812	  having	  the	  highest	  viscosity	  among	  the	  
6	  different	  cultures	  in	  fermented	  camel	  milk.	  
Based	   on	   the	   findings	   of	   this	   study	   it	   can	   be	   concluded	   that	   YF-­‐L812	   was	   the	   most	  
distinguishable	  culture	  used	  to	  ferment	  camel	  milk.	  More	  studies	  in	  this	  field	  with	  better	  
circumstances	   could	   be	   used	   to	   study	   YF-­‐L812	   more	   deeply,	   which	   might	   reveal	   the	  
peptides	   responsible	   for	   the	   stole	   taste	   in	   camel	   milk,	   and	   perhaps	   discover	   how	  
fermented	  camel	  milk	  can	  get	  more	  texture.
