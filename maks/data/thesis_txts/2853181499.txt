Abstract 
In biosensor development, traditional flat 2D electrodes are commonly used for transducing signals. However, ad-
vancements in 3D cell culture systems and organoid technologies demand vertically oriented 3D electrodes to better 
mimic complex, biomimetic environments. This thesis focuses on the immobilization of biomolecules on 3D-
printed pyrolytic carbon electrodes, fabricated using stereolithography (SLA) technology. By functionalizing the 
electrode surface with ethylenediamine (EDA) and aminobenzoic acid (ABA), high densities of amino and carboxyl 
groups are introduced to enable biomolecule tethering. Electrochemical techniques such as cyclic voltammetry (CV) 
and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) were employed to assess the performance of these modified 
electrodes, while fluorescence microscopy confirmed the immobilization of biomolecules. Pyrolytic carbon was 
selected as the electrode material due to its large window of electrochemical stability, biocompatibility, and versa-
tility in terms of conductivity and structural integrity. 
The creation of vertically standing 3D pyrolytic carbon electrodes opens up new possibilities for the design of highly 
functional biosensors and optoelectrical waveguides. These structures are particularly suited for applications in 
brain organoids, where they can be integrated into complex neural environments to precisely monitor and manipu-
late neurotransmitter signaling.

1. Introduction 
1.1 
Scope and motivation of the project 
In most biosensor applications, flat 2D electrodes are commonly used as the primary transducers. However, 
advancements in 3D cell culture systems and organoid technology demand vertically oriented 3D electrodes 
to support more complex, biomimetic environments. These 3D transducers are essential for constructing bio-
sensors that operate effectively within three-dimensional cellular structures. This need is especially relevant 
in neural applications, where cells are genetically modified with light-sensitive ion transporters. Such modi-
fications enable precise stimulation of specific cell types within a 3D environment, which is crucial for stud-
ying neural activity in brain organoids or neural tissue models. For light-based stimulation to be effective, 
optical waveguides must emit light uniformly across a large volume. Thus, the design of "leaky" waveguides, 
capable of emitting light in 360° along their entire length, is essential to illuminate the target region compre-
hensively. 
Combining 3D electrodes with optical waveguides within a single structure could pave the way for innovative 
3D optoelectrical waveguides. Such structures could be achieved by fabricating hollow 3D frameworks, which 
are pyrolyzed to render them conductive and then filled with a light-curable polymer to function as optical 
waveguides. This approach not only introduces a new fabrication technique involving the 3D printing of hol-
low polymeric structures but also calls for a reimagined paradigm in biosensor design. Traditional biomole-
cule crosslinking techniques used for 2D electrodes are unsuitable for these 3D configurations. For instance, 
crosslinking with glutaraldehyde is ineffective for 3D electrodes, as it would likely result in an over-coating 
that fills and obscures the entire 3D structure, preventing selective functionalization. Additionally, in carbon 
pyrolytic structures, the natural abundance of functional groups like carboxyl (-COOH) and amino (-NH₂) is 
very low on the surface, making it challenging to rely on these alone for effective biomolecule immobilization. 
To address these limitations, surface modification techniques are necessary to increase the density of reactive 
groups and enable controlled biomolecule attachment on complex 3D geometries. 
The focus of this thesis is on the immobilization and visualization of biomolecules in 3D pyrolytic carbon 
electrodes. The goal of this is to provide a starting point to develop in the future 3D electrodes and optoelec-
trical waveguides that can operate effectively within 3D cellular environments like brain organoids. This work 
forms a crucial part of the OpenMind project,1 an initiative funded by the HORIZON-EIC-2021-PATH-
FINDEROPEN-01 program. OpenMind aims to replace animal models with advanced human brain organoid 
systems, or "connectoids," that better replicate human neural responses. Within this broader project, this thesis 
addresses the development of pyrolytic carbon electrodes and waveguides capable of penetrating these con-
nectoids. By doing so, it enables precise monitoring and manipulation of neurotransmitter signalling in 3D 
models, which is critical for understanding disease mechanisms and testing therapies. Within the larger scope 
of the OpenMind project, this thesis contributes to the development of pyrolytic carbon electrodes and wave-
guides designed to penetrate connectoids, allowing for precise monitoring and manipulation of neurotrans-
mitter signaling within 3D models.  
The design used in this thesis serves as a simplified prototype based on larger electrode structures. The find-
ings presented here are intended to inform and guide the adaptation of these electrodes for real-world

1.2 
Objectives  
This final degree project aims to optimize and characterize directed biomolecule distribution on vertical 3D 
electrodes using different test scenarios in order to provide 3D electrodes with high density functional groups 
through an electrochemical functionalization strategy. To achieve this main goal, we have established the 
following objectives: 
a. Stereolithographic 3D printing of vertically oriented hollow 3D structures that are precursors for elec-
trodes 
b. Pyrolysis of the printed structures combined with electrical interfacing 
c. Electro-functionalization of the pyrolytic carbon 2D and 3D electrodes with amine and carboxylic 
functionalities 
d. Immobilization chemistries for tethering biomolecules of 2D and 3D electrodes 
e. Electrochemical characterization of the of the fabricated 3D pyrolytic electrodes with cyclic voltam-
metry (CV), and electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) in each of the experimental steps 
f. Fluorescent and microscopic visualization of the distribution of immobilized biomolecules around the 
surface of the 3D structures

2. Experimental techniques 
2.1 
3D printing techniques and applications 
3D printing, has emerged as a revolutionary technology with a profound impact across various fields, includ-
ing engineering, healthcare, and manufacturing. It enables the creation of complex geometries and customized 
objects by adding material layer by layer, a process that contrasts with traditional subtractive manufacturing 
techniques. The flexibility and precision offered by 3D printing have led to a rapidly growing market, with 
applications expanding into biomedicine, aerospace, and even consumer products. The industry has seen tre-
mendous growth since the mid-1980s, when the first commercial 3D printing systems were introduced.2 
The origins of 3D printing date back to 1987 with the development of the first stereolithography (SLA) system 
by 3D Systems. Initially, 3D printing technologies were limited to creating prototypes, but advancements in 
materials and printing methods have expanded its capabilities. By the 1990s, new resins were commercialized 
for a wider range of 3D printers, enabling more intricate designs and applications. Since then, material devel-
opments have allowed for tougher, more durable prints, suitable for aerospace and medical industries. As seen 
in the timeline, the 2000s marked a shift towards more accessible and affordable 3D printers, fostering wide-
spread adoption across various sectors. Today, 3D printing is not only a tool for rapid prototyping but also for 
end-use production. The 3D printing market has grown rapidly, with applications expanding across industries. 
The market is projected to reach billions in value, driven by advancements in technology and material sci-
ences. The affordability and accessibility of 3D printing systems have lowered barriers to entry, making it a 
viable option for businesses of all sizes. The market for bioprinting and medical 3D printing, in particular, is 
expected to grow significantly as the technology continues to evolve. 
3D printing begins with a digital design, typically created in CAD software, which is then converted into an 
STL file format. This file format represents the object as a series of triangles, which allows for easy manipu-
lation and slicing. Slicing is a crucial step where the model is divided into thin layers, which the printer will 
build sequentially. Key parameters such as resolution, print time, and calibration influence the final output. 
Higher resolution provides finer details but increases print time. Calibration ensures accuracy, as misalign-
ments can lead to defects in the final product. These parameters require a balance based on the specific appli-
cation and desired quality of the print.

3D printing has significantly impacted biomedicine and bioengineering, especially in the fields of bioanalytics 
and biosensors. 3D printing enables precise fabrication of electrodes and other microstructures that can detect

lab-on-a-chip devices, which are essential for diagnostics and research. There are several types of 3D printing 
technologies, each with unique principles, advantages, and limitations. Key technologies include: 
2.1.1 
Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF) 
Fused Filament Fabrication (FFF), also known as Fused Deposition Modeling (FDM), is one of the most 
widely used 3D printing techniques due to its cost-effectiveness and versatility. The process begins with a 
thermoplastic filament, such as polylactic acid (PLA), acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS), or polyethylene 
terephthalate glycol (PETG), which is fed into a heated print head where it melts. The molten plastic is then 
extruded through a nozzle onto a built platform layer by layer, following a predetermined path. As each layer 
cools and solidifies, it bonds with the layer below, gradually building up the desired 3D object. 
FFF is compatible with a broad range of thermoplastics and can even accommodate composite filaments in-
fused with materials like carbon fiber or wood. While it is valued for its accessibility and material flexibility, 
FFF generally produces prints with a lower resolution compared to resin-based methods. The layer lines are

or high-detail work. However, its cost-effectiveness and scalability make it suitable for prototyping and low-

2.1.2 
PolyJet Printing 
PolyJet Printing is an advanced additive manufacturing technology that functions similarly to inkjet printing. 
It involves jetting tiny droplets of liquid photopolymer onto a build platform in precise layers. The droplets 
are instantly cured by ultraviolet (UV) light, allowing the building to progress rapidly. PolyJet can produce 
extremely fine details, with layer thicknesses as low as 16 microns, and achieve smooth surface finishes com-
parable to injection molding. 
A significant advantage of PolyJet is its ability to print with multiple materials and colors simultaneously. 
This capability allows for the creation of complex, multi-material objects with varying mechanical properties 
and textures within a single print. For instance, PolyJet can combine rigid and flexible materials to produce 
parts with variable flexibility, such as soft grips on rigid tools. This makes it valuable for prototyping func-
tional parts and producing realistic models for medical and educational purposes. 
However, PolyJet printing has certain limitations. It tends to be slower and more expensive than other tech-
niques, primarily due to the high cost of the photopolymer resins and the need for support material, which 
must be removed post-printing. Additionally, the cured photopolymer is susceptible to degradation over time 
when exposed to sunlight or high temperatures, making PolyJet less suitable for long-term applications com-
pared to thermoplastic-based methods. Despite these limitations, PolyJet’s high resolution and multi-material 
capabilities make it a unique and valuable tool in areas requiring detailed, multi-material prototypes.4

Pyrolytic carbon 
2.2.1 
Carbon and carbon structure 
Carbon (C), a fundamental element for life on Earth, has a remarkable ability to interact and form bonds with 
nearly all other elements. This unique capability enables carbon to create an extensive variety of organic 
molecules and compounds, each exhibiting diverse physical and chemical properties. The versatility of carbon 
stems from its ability to adopt different hybridization states—sp, sp², and sp³—which leads to the formation 
of various bond types and, consequently, a range of structural forms or allotropes. 
Among these allotropes, we find materials like diamond, graphite, fullerenes, graphene, carbon nanotubes, 
amorphous carbon, and glassy carbon. Each allotrope displays distinct structural and electronic characteristics 
that make them suitable for a wide range of applications. For instance, diamond, with its sp³ hybridization, is 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
7 
 
highly rigid and an excellent thermal conductor, while graphite, with sp² hybridization, has layered structures 
that provide good electrical conductivity. 
Pyrolytic carbon, an allotrope that is structurally related to graphite, is created through the pyrolysis of organic 
compounds at high temperatures in an inert environment. The sp² hybridized carbon atoms in pyrolytic carbon 
arrange in a layered, graphitic-like structure, imparting it with properties akin to glassy carbon, such as chem-
ical stability, conductivity, and biocompatibility. This combination of properties makes pyrolytic carbon par-
ticularly useful as an electrode material in electrochemical applications. Its structure can be further modified 
and tailored during the pyrolysis process, enabling it to serve as a versatile platform for various applications, 
especially within the field of biosensors and other biomedical devices.7  
The choice of specific carbon allotropes, such as pyrolytic carbon, in electrochemical systems often depends 
on the desired electrochemical behavior, which is influenced by factors like surface structure, bonding con-
figurations, and the presence of functional groups. Pyrolytic carbon's unique combination of stability, con-
ductivity, and ease of surface modification underscores its growing importance in electrochemical research 
and applications. 
2.2.2 
What is pyrolytic carbon? 
Pyrolytic carbon (PyC) is an allotropic form of carbon that consists of troostitic graphene sheets. PyC has 
emerged as a significant material in the realm of electrochemical applications, particularly in the biomedical 
field. Its unique properties—chemical inertness, high conductivity, biocompatibility, and tuneable surface 
characteristics—make it an ideal candidate for a wide range of uses, from biosensors to microbatteries and 
neural interfaces. Pyrolytic carbon is similar in structure and behavior to glassy carbon but offers greater 
versatility in terms of fabrication and application. Derived through the pyrolysis of organic precursors under 
inert conditions, PyC has become an invaluable tool in advanced electrochemical setups due to its customiza-
ble morphology and electrochemical properties.8 
2.2.3 
Pyrolysis 
Pyrolytic carbon is a form of carbon that is created through the thermal decomposition of organic materials in 
an oxygen-free environment. Unlike traditional burning, which occurs in the presence of oxygen and results 
in combustion, pyrolysis involves heating organic compounds to high temperatures (often between 800°C and 
2500°C) in an inert atmosphere, such as nitrogen or argon. This controlled process transforms the organic 
material into a solid carbon structure without oxidizing since the formation of oxygen comes from the precur-
sor material itself. The absence of oxygen prevents the material from burning, allowing it to retain a portion 
of its original structure while forming a stable, graphitic-like layer.  
Pyrolysis is a thermal degradation process conducted at high temperatures in an inert atmosphere, typically 
resulting in glass-like pyrolytic carbon when applied to organic polymer photoresists. This transformation 
involves a series of simultaneous reactions, including hydrogen transfer, isomerization, dehydrogenation, and 
condensation.9 
Generally, the pyrolysis of thermosetting resins can be broken down into three primary stages: 
• 
Pre-carbonization (T < 300°C): in this step unreacted monomer molecules and any remaining sol-
vent within the polymer precursor are removed. This step helps purify the material by eliminating 
volatile components before further heating. 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
8 
 
• 
Carbonization (300°C < T < 1200°C): In this stage, the organic material is converted into a carbon-
rich solid, typically with an amorphous or disordered structure. The polymeric precursors break down, 
and hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, and other non-carbon elements are gradually released as gases. 
• 
Annealing (T > 1200°C): At this high-temperature stage, the material undergoes further structural 
reorganization. Annealing helps to improve the crystallinity of the carbon by reducing defects and 
increasing the alignment of the graphene-like layers. 
The structure of pyrolytic carbon is determined by the specific conditions under which it is synthesized. For 
instance, the temperature, heating rate, and precursor material all influence the final properties of the carbon. 
Higher pyrolysis temperatures generally produce more graphitic structures with higher conductivity, while 
lower temperatures tend to yield more amorphous forms with increased porosity.10 
2.2.4 
Electrochemical properties 
The electrical conductivity of pyrolytic carbon is closely related to its graphitic content, which can be adjusted 
by varying the pyrolysis conditions. Highly graphitized PyC exhibits excellent electron mobility, facilitating 
rapid charge transfer at the electrode surface. This makes it an excellent choice for applications requiring high 
power densities, such as supercapacitors and lithium-ion batteries. One of the defining features of pyrolytic 
carbon is its tunable surface area and porosity. Depending on the precursor and pyrolysis conditions, PyC can 
be made with varying levels of porosity, which directly impacts its surface area. A high surface area is bene-
ficial for applications like biosensors, where it provides more active sites for interactions with biological mol-
ecules. The porosity also enhances mass transport within the electrode, which is crucial for improving the 
overall efficiency of electrochemical devices. Pyrolytic carbon is chemically inert and highly resistant to cor-
rosion, even in harsh environments. This stability is largely due to its strong covalent carbon bonds and gra-
phitic structure, which protect it from oxidation and other forms of degradation. PyC’s corrosion resistance 
makes it ideal for applications that operate in aggressive conditions, such as wastewater treatment and indus-
trial electrolysis.8 
2.2.5 
Applications in biomedical devices 
In biomedical contexts, pyrolytic carbon is frequently employed in the fabrication of electrodes for biosensors 
and cell culture platforms. Its biocompatibility, coupled with its customizable surface chemistry, enables pre-
cise interactions with biological molecules and cells. Pyrolytic carbon electrodes have been used to detect 
various analytes, including glucose, dopamine, and neurotransmitters, with high sensitivity and specificity. 
Additionally, PyC makes an excellent substrate for cell growth and differentiation, offering new avenues for 
tissue engineering and regenerative medicine.

Surface modification of carbon – functionalization methodologies 
In the context of electrode surface modification, several techniques have been developed to introduce specific 
functional groups that allow the attachment of biomolecules, such as antibodies, to pyrolytic carbon elec-
trodes. Surface modification is essential for applications in biosensing and biofuel cells, where direct inter-
facing of biomolecules with electrode surfaces facilitates targeted interactions and signal transduction. How-
ever, while surface functionalization is beneficial, not all methods are equally suitable for the delicate structure 
==PAGE:20==

of carbon electrodes. Techniques such as nitric acid oxidation, electrochemical reduction of diazonium salts, 
and electro-oxidation are among the methods considered for carbon electrode modification. 11 
Nitric acid oxidation is a common functionalization method that introduces carboxyl and hydroxyl groups 
onto the carbon surface. This process is particularly vigorous and can lead to significant degradation of the 
electrode material. Studies have shown that nitric acid oxidation damages the carbon structure due to the 
strong oxidative environment, which leads to electrode disintegration, particularly in materials like multi-
walled carbon nanotubes and pyrolytic carbon.12 
In contrast, the electrochemical reduction of diazonium salts provides a more controlled approach to func-
tionalization. By utilizing diazonium salts, specific chemical functionalities can be grafted onto the electrode 
surface. The reaction involves generating aryl radicals from the diazonium salts, which then covalently bond 
to the carbon surface. This technique is successful in introducing stable functional groups, but it is primarily 
limited to aryl compounds. Due to the instability of aliphatic diazonium salts, this method is not suitable for 
attaching more flexible, aliphatic linkers that are often preferred in bio-applications due to their ability to 
enhance molecular mobility and improve redox interactions.12 Another alternative method for carbon surface 
functionalization is electropolymerization, which involves the electrochemical polymerization of monomers 
to form a thin, conductive polymer film on the electrode surface. This technique is highly versatile, as it allows 
for the introduction of a wide range of functional groups depending on the chosen monomer. Unlike the dia-
zonium salt method, electropolymerization can incorporate both aliphatic and aromatic compounds, offering 
greater flexibility in surface modification. However, electropolymerization can lead to thick, non-uniform 
films that obstruct the electrode surface, reducing sensitivity and limiting electron transfer. 
Given the limitations of the mentioned methodologies, electro-oxidation emerges as a versatile and effective 
functionalization strategy. Electro-oxidation can be used to graft both aliphatic and aryl linkers onto carbon 
electrodes (see Figure 5). This process involves the application of an anodic potential to a carbon electrode in 
the presence of amine-containing compounds, leading to the oxidation of amine groups and their subsequent 
covalent attachment to the carbon surface. Electrooxidation is not damaging to the electrode compared to 
other oxidative treatments with acids which can degrade the surface. The tethered molecules may have differ-
ent functionalities, such as amino and carboxylic groups, which can serve as anchoring points for biomole-
cules. In the context of this study, electro-oxidation of ethylenediamine (EDA) and aminobenzoic acid (ABA) 
was utilized to create an electrode surface functionalized with amino and carboxylic groups respectively to 
support covalent immobilization, for example electron transfer mediators and biomolecules.13

2.4 
Electrochemical techniques 
Electrochemical techniques are essential tools in understanding and characterizing the behavior of materials 
and systems at the electrode-electrolyte interface. These techniques provide critical insights into redox pro-
cesses, charge transfer kinetics, diffusion rates, and impedance characteristics of materials. Among the wide 
array of electrochemical methods, Cyclic Voltammetry (CV) and Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy 
(EIS) are two of the most widely used, each offering distinct advantages for probing different aspects of an 
electrochemical system. 
Cyclic Voltammetry (CV) and Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy (EIS) serve distinct yet complemen-
tary purposes in electrochemical analysis. CV is primarily used to identify qualitative features, such as the 
types of electrochemical reactions occurring and to estimate the approximate magnitude of the currents in-
volved. In contrast, EIS is employed to obtain precise quantitative data on well-defined electrochemical prop-
erties, offering insights into reaction kinetics, charge transfer resistance, and capacitance. 
2.4.1 
Introduction to the Electrochemical cell 
An electrochemical cell is essential for conducting electrochemical measurements, and it typically involves a 
three-electrode configuration: a working electrode, a reference electrode, and a counter electrode, all sub-
merged in an electrolyte solution. The working electrode is where the primary electrochemical reaction takes 
place, while the reference electrode provides a stable potential against which the working electrode’s potential 
can be measured. The counter electrode completes the circuit by balancing the current flowing through the 
cell. There are three main electrodes on a three-electrode setup (See Figure 6): 
• 
Working Electrode (WE): The working electrode is the site of the electrochemical reaction of inter-
est. Its potential is controlled by a potentiostat, relative to the reference electrode, allowing precise 
manipulation of the redox processes occurring at its surface. A critical requirement for the working 
electrode material is that it remains chemically inert over the range of potentials used in the experi-
ment. This ensures that any observed current is attributable to the analyte and not to any side reactions 
with the electrode itself. 
• 
Reference Electrode (RE): The reference electrode provides a stable and well-defined electrochem-
ical potential, serving as a benchmark against which the working electrode's potential is measured. 
This electrode passes negligible current, minimizing voltage drop between the reference and working 
electrodes, which enhances control over the working electrode's potential.  
• 
Counter Electrode (CE): The counter electrode completes the circuit and ensures that current flows 
between itself and the working electrode. Its surface area is generally larger than that of the working 
electrode (about ten times larger), allowing it to handle the total current required without influencing 
the kinetics of the reaction at the working electrode. To balance the current at the working electrode, 
the counter electrode often swings to extreme potentials, which should not interfere with the reaction 
under study. 
The electrolyte solution, composed of a solvent and a supporting electrolyte, facilitates ion movement, which 
compensates for the electron transfer between the electrodes. The supporting electrolyte is chosen for its abil-
ity to dissolve readily in the solvent, remain chemically inert under experimental conditions, and contribute 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
11 
 
to solution conductivity. A suitable solvent is stable within the experimental potential range, dissolves both 
the analyte and the supporting electrolyte efficiently, and does not participate in unwanted side reactions. 
In an electrochemical cell, the conductivity of the solution depends on the supporting electrolyte's concentra-
tion, which helps to reduce solution resistance. Ion movement within the cell is driven by diffusion, which 
arises from concentration gradients between different points in the solution. This movement allows ions to 
balance charge and maintain the continuity of the electrical circuit.15

2.4.2 
Cyclic voltammetry 
Cyclic Voltammetry is a potent analytical method that allows the study of reversible and irreversible redox 
reactions by varying the potential of an electrode and monitoring the resulting current. It provides valuable 
information on reaction mechanisms, electron transfer rates, and the thermodynamic properties of electroac-
tive species. CV is often employed for the characterization of batteries, fuel cells, and sensors, as well as for 
studying electron transfer in biological systems. In CV, a potential is scanned at a set rate called the scan rate 
which can be expressed in V/s. When the potential reaches a value where an electrochemical reaction occurs, 
a current is generated. The forward scan typically represents the oxidation process (anodic reaction), while 
the reverse scan corresponds to the reduction process (cathodic reaction). 15 
The basic relationship governing the current in a reversible redox reaction is derived from the Randles-Sevcik 
equation, which predicts the peak current ip for a reversible reaction in an electrode, this equation describes 
the effect of scan rate () on the peak current (ip) for a cyclic voltammetry experiment. In diffusion-controlled 
processes, the peak current is directly proportional to the square root of the scan rate while for an adsorption 
process the peak current is directly proportional to the scan rate. 15

In cyclic voltammetry (CV), several key parameters play a crucial role in determining the shape and behavior 
of the resulting voltammogram. Understanding how these parameters interact can provide valuable insights 
into the electrochemical processes at the electrode surface. 
The scan rate, which controls how fast the potential is swept, significantly affects the position and height of 
the peaks in a cyclic voltammogram. At faster scan rates, the peak currents increase because Nernst diffusion 
layer does not have sufficient time to reach equilibrium. This means that electroactive species are supplied 
and consumed rapidly within the diffusion layer, leading to a higher current response. However, after reaching 
the peak, the current decreases due to diffusion limitations, where the transport of species to the electrode 
surface cannot keep up with the rate of reaction. This decline in current after the peak is governed by the 
depletion of electroactive species near the electrode, a process driven by diffusion rather than the scan rate 
itself. As the scan rate increases further, the peaks can become distorted, and the peak separation, the differ-
ence in potential between the oxidation and reduction peaks widens. For electrochemically reversible pro-
cesses involving freely diffusing redox species, the peak current is proportional to the square root of the scan 
rate, as described by the Randles-Sevcik equation. 
Another key parameter is the diffusion coefficient, which governs the transport of the electroactive species 
toward the electrode. A higher diffusion coefficient results in faster mass transport, leading to sharper peaks 
and higher currents. In contrast, slower diffusion can cause peak broadening and can also influence the peak 
separation by slowing down the reaction kinetics. The Randles-Sevcik equation not only helps to estimate the 
peak current but can also be used to confirm whether an analyte is freely diffusing in solution or has adsorbed 
onto the electrode surface. 
The concentration of the electroactive species (C₀) in solution directly affects the magnitude of the peak cur-
rent. According to the Randles–Sevcik equation, the peak current is proportional to the square root of the 
concentration. Therefore, higher concentrations result in more pronounced peaks, providing better signal-to-
noise ratios and making the redox processes easier to observe.

The potential range (min and max potentials) defines the boundaries within which the potential is cycled. This 
range determines which redox processes can be accessed during the CV experiment. For example, if the redox 
potential of the species of interest lies outside this range, the reaction will not be observed. Choosing appro-
priate min and max potential values is crucial for ensuring that all relevant electrochemical reactions are cap-
tured without overextending the range, which could lead to undesirable side reactions or electrode degrada-
tion. 
Finally, the electrode surface area impacts the overall current response. A larger electrode provides more 
surface area for electron transfer, increasing the current response. Surface effects, such as adsorption of spe-
cies onto the electrode, can also affect peak shape and separation, particularly when the reactions involve 
surface-bound intermediates. 
2.4.3 
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy 
Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy, on the other hand, is a frequency-domain technique that measures 
the impedance of an electrochemical system over a range of frequencies. EIS is particularly powerful for 
understanding complex processes such as charge transfer resistance, double-layer capacitance, and ion diffu-
sion within materials. This method is widely applied in corrosion studies, battery testing, and the development 
of electrochemical sensors, providing a detailed picture of both surface and bulk properties. 
In EIS, the total impedance Z(ω) is recorded as a function of the frequency ω of the applied signal. The 
impedance response is typically represented in a Nyquist plot, where the real part of the impedance Z′(ω) is 
plotted against the imaginary part Z′′(ω). A typical Nyquist plot for a simple electrochemical system consists 
of two different parts, a semicircle at high frequencies representing the Rct and a Cdl, and a linear region at 
low frequencies representing diffusion-controlled processes (Warbug Impedance).15 
 
Figure 8. EIS Nyquist plot illustration representing the negative of the imaginary versus the real parts of the electrochemical cells. 
Source: see reference17.  
The different parts of a Nyquist plot are shown in Figure 8. Where the electrode resistance refers to the 
inherent resistance of the electrode material itself. The electrode resistance can vary based on the material 
used. Electrolyte resistance is the resistance offered by the bulk solution (electrolyte) to ion movement be-
tween the working and counter electrodes. It is also reflected in the high-frequency intercept on the x-axis of 
the Nyquist plot. The electrolyte resistance is influenced by factors like ion concentration, ionic mobility, and 
the distance between electrodes. Internal resistance is a collective term that includes the electrolyte resistance 
and other resistances within the electrochemical system, such as the resistance within the electrode material 
itself and the wiring of the system. This value is crucial in determining the overall performance of an 
==PAGE:25==
 
 
14 
 
electrochemical cell, especially in battery and fuel cell research. Diffuse layer resistance is part of the low-
frequency region of the Nyquist plot. It refers to the resistance due to the movement of ions in the diffuse 
layer of the electrical double layer (EDL), which forms at the electrode-electrolyte interface. As ions diffuse 
through this layer, they encounter resistance that depends on the properties of the electrolyte and the surface 
charge of the electrode. 
EDL formation occurs at the electrode surface, where ions from the electrolyte arrange themselves in layers 
(Stern layer and diffuse layer) to balance the charge on the electrode. The steep slope in this region reflects 
capacitive behavior as the EDL builds up. Ion diffusion is represented in the Nyquist plot by a low-frequency 
tail, which appears as a straight line with a slope of 1. This line represents the Warburg impedance, indicating 
the diffusion of ions through the electrolyte to the electrode surface. The equilibrium differential capacitance 
is related to the capacitance of the EDL at equilibrium, reflecting how much charge can be stored in the double 
layer at a given potential. In the Nyquist plot, the equilibrium capacitance is part of the semicircle that repre-
sents the double-layer charging process. A larger semicircle typically indicates a higher differential capaci-
tance. 
In EIS several key parameters influence the measurements and help to interpret the data. The frequency range 
plays a significant role, as EIS typically involves the application of an AC potential over a wide range of 
frequencies, often from millihertz to several kilohertz. Lower frequencies are essential for examining pro-
cesses controlled by diffusion, while higher frequencies provide information about charge transfer and surface 
reactions at the electrode-electrolyte interface. The Cdl is another critical factor, which reflects the capacity 
of the electrode’s surface to store charge. This parameter is closely linked to the surface area of the electrode 
and the nature of the electrolyte solution. It is particularly important in systems where capacitive behavior 
dominates, such as in supercapacitors or batteries. The Rct is a measure of the ease with which electrons can 
transfer between the electrode and the electrolyte. A lower charge transfer resistance indicates faster electron 
transfer kinetics, which is essential for applications like fuel cells and electrochemical sensors where high 
efficiency is required. 
At low frequencies, the Warburg impedance becomes prominent. This parameter is associated with diffusion 
processes, particularly the transport of ions or molecules through the electrolyte to the electrode surface. War-
burg impedance represents the resistance to mass transport, and its presence in the Nyquist plot is typically 
seen as a sloped line at lower frequencies. Understanding this parameter is crucial in systems where diffusion 
controls the reaction rate, such as in batteries. 
The overall impedance in an electrochemical system can be modelled using Randles circuit. The Randles 
circuit is one of the most well-known equivalent circuit models in electrochemistry, especially in the context 
of electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS). It serves to represent a diffusion-controlled faradaic reac-
tion at a planar electrode. The Randles circuit, as described, models a semi-infinite diffusion process, where 
the electrode reaction is governed by the rate at which reactants diffuse to the electrode surface. 15 
At its core, the Randles circuit consists of a combination of two basic components: the ionic resistance (Ri) 
and the double layer capacitance (Cdl). In the absence of a faradaic reaction, this would be the simplest model 
for an electrode in an electrolyte. However, when a faradaic reaction occurs, an additional component comes 
into play: the charge transfer resistance (Rct), which represents the resistance associated with the electron 
transfer reaction. Rct is placed in parallel with the Cdl because the faradaic reaction occurs alongside the 
charging of the double layer at the electrode. 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
15 
 
The key feature of the Randles circuit is the assumption that the rate of the faradaic process is limited by 
diffusion. To account for this, a diffusion-related impedance component, known as the Warburg impedance 
(W), is added in series with Rct. The Warburg impedance reflects the resistance to mass transport and plays a 
critical role when the electrochemical reaction is controlled by the diffusion of species to the electrode surface. 
This basic framework can be extended to more complex systems, where multiple semicircles or overlapping 
processes may appear in the impedance response, depending on the electrochemical processes involved.

The Randles circuit produces a distinctive Nyquist plot, which is a common way to visualize impedance data. 
The plot typically features a semicircular shape in its high-frequency region, attributed to the combination of 
Ri and the Rct/Cdl parallel circuit. At lower frequencies, the plot transitions to a 45° line, representing the 
Warburg impedance, which dominates as diffusion becomes the limiting factor. Both Nyquist and Bode plots 
generated from Randles circuit data are valuable tools for analysing the behavior of electrochemical systems, 
providing clear insight into the resistive and capacitive elements, as well as the diffusion-controlled processes 
involved in faradaic reactions. 
2.4.3.1 
Understanding impedance in electrochemical systems 
Impedance measures the opposition to current flow when a potential is applied to an electrochemical cell. 
When additional layers are introduced on an electrode surface—such as in the tethering of biomolecules—the 
impedance response is expected to change significantly. For instance, when measuring impedance in a 
ferro/ferricyanide redox couple, the presence of an insulating layer would typically increase the overall im-
pedance. This increase can manifest as a larger semicircle in the Nyquist plot, reflecting a higher charge 
transfer resistance. Additionally, the Warburg element related to diffusion may become more prominent, as 
the layer can also hinder ion transport to the electrode surface. 
The extent of these changes depends on the thickness and properties of the layer. Thin, conductive layers 
might have a minimal effect, whereas thicker or insulating layers would greatly impede both charge transfer 
and mass transport, leading to a notable increase in total impedance. 
In reversible redox systems the ideal case consists of an inert metal electrode of an ideally planar surface 
immersed in a electrolyte solution of large volume. The electrolyte solution contains a redox couple in low 
concentration, dissolved in what is called a base or supporting electrolyte. This supporting electrolyte consists 
of inert ions at a high concentration, giving the solution high conductivity. 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
16 
 
The current within the bulk solution is carried by the migration of these supporting electrolyte ions, while at 
the metal/electrolyte interface, the current flows through electron transfer linked to the redox system. The 
electron transfer across the interface is assumed to occur instantaneously, meaning that the concentrations of 
redox species are always determined by the electrode’s potential at any given moment. 
Initially, the concentration of the redox species is uniform throughout the solution. Since electron transfer is 
considered extremely fast, the current is governed by the diffusion of redox species to the interface, rather 
than the charge transfer process itself. This transport is driven entirely by diffusion, with other transport mech-
anisms like convection and migration being negligible under these conditions.18 
2.4.3.2 
The relationship between pH, pKa and impedance 
In electrochemical measurements, the pH of the solution plays a crucial role in determining the behavior of 
redox-active species and the electron transfer processes at the electrode interface. The pH affects the protona-
tion or deprotonation states of molecules, which in turn influences their electrochemical properties. Protona-
tion refers to the addition of a proton (H⁺) to a molecule, whereas deprotonation is the loss of a proton. These 
changes are often dictated by the pKa of the species—essentially the pH at which half of the molecules are 
protonated. 
In solutions where the pH is controlled or buffered, the species' protonation states are stabilized, which directly 
impacts charge transfer, especially when conducting techniques such as EIS. When surface-modified elec-
trodes include molecules with ionizable groups, such as carboxyl or amine groups, the protonation state of 
these groups becomes critical. The protonation state depends on the pKa of the tethered species and the pH of 
the electrolyte solution. For example, at a pH below the pKa of a carboxyl group, the group will be protonated 
and neutral, while at a pH above the pKa, it will be deprotonated and negatively charged. 
The protonation state affects both the electron transfer kinetics and the interfacial capacitance. When the teth-
ered molecules are protonated, charge transfer might be easier due to reduced electrostatic repulsion, resulting 
in lower impedance. In contrast, when deprotonated, negatively charged species might repel redox-active an-
ions in solution, increasing impedance by reducing the efficiency of electron transfer at the surface. This 
protonation-dependent behavior can lead to significant shifts in impedance, especially at the pH values near 
the pKa of the tethered species. 
A common redox system, ferrocyanide/ferricyanide ([Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻/³⁻), often used to study electron transfer pro-
cesses, typically operates in a neutral or slightly basic pH environment when prepared in phosphate-buffered 
saline (PBS), which maintains a pH around 7. The extent of impedance change depends largely on the pKa of 
the tethered molecules, which governs their protonation states in the buffered solution.19 
Thethering species with low pka (acidic species) 
In the case of tethering molecules with a low pKa, such as carboxyl groups (which generally have pKa values 
around 4-5), the protonation state is influenced by the neutral pH of the system. At pH 7, these groups will be 
largely deprotonated, carrying a negative charge. The deprotonated acidic groups create a negatively charged 
interface, which can lead to electrostatic repulsion with the negatively charged ferrocyanide and ferricyanide 
ions in solution. This repulsion reduces the effective electron transfer at the electrode surface, leading to an 
increase in the charge transfer resistance (Rct) and, consequently, the overall impedance in the EIS measure-
ments. The presence of these deprotonated groups acts as a barrier, hindering the efficient interaction of the 
redox species with the electrode.20,21 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
17 
 
Tethering species with a high pka (basic species) 
For tethering molecules with a high pKa, such as amine groups (which typically have pKa values around 9-
10), the situation is different. At pH 7, these groups remain largely protonated, carrying a positive charge. The 
protonated amine groups do not repel the negatively charged ferrocyanide/ferricyanide ions and may even 
create a more favorable environment for electron transfer by attracting these anionic species. The presence of 
positively charged groups near the electrode surface can enhance charge transfer efficiency, lowering the 
charge transfer resistance and reducing the overall impedance in EIS measurements. These tethered molecules 
could act as electron mediators, facilitating faster electron transfer between the electrode and the redox couple. 
21 
2.4.3.3 
Relationship between impedance and cyclic voltammetry 
Electrode surface modification through tethering molecules or materials plays a crucial role in altering the 
electrochemical behavior of the electrode-electrolyte interface. When a substance is tethered to an electrode, 
it can influence the electron transfer process, affecting key electrochemical parameters such as impedance and 
current responses. This section of the thesis aims to explain how the tethering of biomolecules impacts EIS 
and CV measurements, with a focus on the relationship between increased impedance observed in EIS and 
the resulting changes in the CV profile of the modified surface or electrode compared to the bare electrode.  
When a material tethered to the electrode surface increases the impedance of the system, this indicates a 
reduction in electron transfer efficiency at the electrode-electrolyte interface. This happens when the tethered 
material acts as a physical barrier, limiting the access of redox species in the solution to the electrode surface 
and impedes electron transfer. This tethered layer may introduce additional resistance to electron flow across 
the interface which results in an increase Rct in the equivalent circuit model.19 
If impedance is increased, the expected results in CV would be a reduction in the peak current observed during 
the redox process as governed by the Randles-Sevick equation. Further, the anodic and cathodic peals in CV 
(typically sharp and well-defined in electroactive bare electrode surfaces) after tethering, the peaks may be-
come broader dure to the slower electrode transfer rates across the modified interface.  
The shift in peak potential is linked to the increase in charge transfer resistance (Rct) observed in EIS. When 
electron transfer becomes more sluggish, the system requires a greater driving force (overpotential) to achieve 
the same level of electron flow. This effect manifests as a peak shift, often toward higher potentials for oxi-
dation and lower potentials for reduction, indicating the need for increased energy to overcome the resistance 
at the interface. 
In CV, the peak separation (ΔEp) between the anodic and cathodic peaks is indicative of the electron transfer 
kinetics at the electrode. For a reversible redox reaction, ΔEp typically remains small (around 59 mV for a 
single electron transfer at room temperature). However, when tethering increases impedance and slows down 
electron transfer, the system becomes quasi-reversible or even irreversible, leading to an increase in ΔEp. The 
increase in peak separation arises from the additional resistance and slower electron transfer kinetics intro-
duced by the tethered material. This behavior aligns with the increased charge transfer resistance observed in 
EIS measurements, where the slower kinetics demand a higher potential difference to drive the redox reaction 
at a similar rate.19 
Alternatively, if a tethered material or biomolecule reduces impedance, the electrochemical behavior at the 
electrode-electrolyte interface would indicate enhanced electron transfer kinetics or improved conductivity at 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
18 
 
the interface. In this case, we can expect increased peak currents in the CV, given that electron mobility is 
enhanced). This would be visible by larger peak currents after the bare electrode and decreased peak separa-
tion. 
2.5 
Immobilization chemistries for tethering biomolecules 
2.5.1 
EDC/NHS activation 
In chemical biology, the use of carbodiimides as crosslinking agents plays a crucial role in forming covalent 
bonds between biomolecules. A particularly effective and widely used carbodiimide is 1-ethyl-3-(3-dimethyl-
aminopropyl) carbodiimide (EDC), a water-soluble reagent. EDC facilitates the direct formation of amide 
bonds by linking carboxyl (-COOH) and amine (-NH₂) functional groups in molecules without leaving any 
spacer. The process begins when EDC interacts with a carboxyl group, resulting in the formation of an O-
acylisourea intermediate. This intermediate is short-lived but highly reactive toward primary amines, promot-
ing efficient coupling between the two groups. The reaction culminates in the creation of a stable amide bond, 
with the byproduct being a water-soluble isourea compound, making the process clean and easy to manage in 
aqueous environments.22 
However, the reaction efficiency can be further enhanced by incorporating N-hydroxysuccinimide (NHS) or 
its water-soluble derivative sulfo-NHS. The presence of NHS stabilizes the reaction by transforming the O-
acylisourea into a more stable and reactive NHS-ester intermediate. This intermediate is less susceptible to 
hydrolysis and demonstrates improved selectivity toward amines, ensuring a more efficient and controlled 
reaction. The use of sulfo-NHS in particular is preferred in aqueous conditions, as it increases the overall 
solubility of the reaction components.

byproduct. 23,24 
 
EDC/NHS chemistry, in this context, has been employed to activate the carboxyl groups of ABA molecules 
tethered to the electrode. The activation of these carboxyl groups is essential for subsequent antibody conju-
gation, enabling the visualization and attachment of the desired biomolecules.

2.5.2 
Glutaraldehyde activation 
In the context of surface functionalization for biosensor applications, glutaraldehyde is a commonly used 
cross-linking reagent for activating amine groups on carbon surfaces, which then allows for the attachment of 
biomolecules such as antibodies. Glutaraldehyde is particularly valuable due to its ability to create stable 
covalent bonds between primary amines on the substrate and target molecules. This method is highly versatile 
and efficient, especially when aiming to immobilize proteins or enzymes on amine-modified surfaces like 
those of 3D pyrolytic carbon electrodes. 25 
The chemistry of glutaraldehyde involves its bifunctional aldehyde groups, which can react with primary 
amines to form Schiff bases. This reaction generally proceeds through an imine linkage, which can subse-
quently be reduced to form more stable, irreversible covalent bonds. In aqueous solutions, glutaraldehyde can 
exist in multiple forms, including linear and cyclic hemiacetal structures, and its exact behavior depends on 
pH and concentration. Glutaraldehyde can react with amines creating active aldehyde sites that can bind to 
antibodies or other molecules. This two-step activation process is robust and can be conducted under mild 
conditions, which is beneficial for maintaining the integrity of sensitive biomolecules like antibodies.26 
2.6 
Surface analysis techniques 
Fluorescence microscopy offers a direct and non-destructive approach to assess biomolecule immobilization 
on carbon electrodes. Its ability to confirm the successful attachment and distribution of biomolecules makes 
it especially relevant for this work, where verifying the presence of specific functional groups and ensuring 
uniform coverage on 3D electrode structures are key objectives. 
Fluorescence microscopy is an essential tool for the analysis of surface-functionalized electrodes, particularly 
in biosensor applications where biomolecule localization is critical. Fluorescence occurs when molecules ab-
sorb light at a specific wavelength, causing excitation to a higher electronic state, and subsequently emit light 
at a longer wavelength with lower energy. This process, illustrated by the Jablonski diagram (see Figure 11), 
requires the use of fluorescent dyes or fluorophores that specifically label the molecules of interest. This tech-
nique allows for the visualization of biomolecules attached to surfaces, enabling researchers to confirm the 
presence and distribution of immobilized species. 
In this research, fluorescently labeled antibodies were employed, specifically utilizing Alexa 546 as the fluor-
ophore. Alexa 647 absorbs light maximally at around 650 nm, corresponding to red light, and emits fluores-
cence at approximately 668 nm, producing a bright far-red signal. The choice of Alexa 546 is particularly 
advantageous due to its high photostability and strong fluorescence intensity, which is essential for clear vis-
ualization in the analysis. This selective binding of fluorescent antibodies to specific reactive groups intro-
duced during the functionalization process allows the fluorescence signal to directly correlate with the quantity 
of functional groups successfully tethered to the electrode surface. Thus, the approach provides quantitative 
insights into the efficacy of the functionalization, where a stronger fluorescence signal indicates a higher 
degree of successful attachment.27 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
20 
 
 
Figure 11. Jablonski diagram illustrating the process of fluorescence. Upon absorbing a photon, a fluorophore transitions from the 
ground state to an excited electronic state. After non-radiative relaxation, the fluorophore emits light at a longer wavelength as it 
returns to the ground state.28 
 
While fluorescence microscopy is non-destructive and provides clear, interpretable images, visualizing 3D 
structures presents unique challenges. In particular, issues such as light scattering and signal attenuation may 
complicate imaging at different depths within the structure. Confocal fluorescence microscopy can mitigate 
some of these issues by allowing optical sectioning and enhancing depth resolution, which is critical for ana-
lyzing the functionalization throughout a 3D electrode. Additionally, parameters such as excitation intensity 
and exposure time must be carefully managed to minimize photobleaching—a phenomenon where fluoro-
phores lose their ability to fluoresce due to prolonged exposure to excitation light. Quenching, another chal-
lenge, occurs when fluorophores are in close proximity, leading to reduced fluorescence efficiency. Despite 
these limitations, fluorescence microscopy remains a powerful technique for analyzing biomolecule attach-
ment on functionalized electrodes.29

3.2 
2D electrodes 
The 2D pyrolytic carbon electrodes used in this study were previously fabricated as described in previous 
studies9 using SU-8, a negative epoxy-based photoresist. These electrodes had been previously fabricated by 
depositing SU-8 onto silicon wafers with a 600 nm silicon oxide layer, followed by a two-step spin coating 
process to achieve a 17 µm thick SU-8 layer. This layer was patterned lithographically using a chromium 
mask and a mask aligner for precise exposure, followed by development in PGMEA to create the desired 
electrode structure. 
The SU-8 photoresist was then pyrolyzed at high temperatures (900°C to 1100°C) in a nitrogen environment, 
converting the polymeric material into conductive pyrolytic carbon. This pyrolysis process resulted in a car-
bon structure that closely resembles glassy carbon, characterized by its favorable electrochemical properties. 
To improve the electrode performance, an oxygen plasma treatment was applied to the surface to enhance 
wettability before further electrochemical testing. 
The final structure consists of a circular carbon working electrode (WE) with an area of 12.5 mm², a counter 
electrode (CE) of 25.2 mm², and a gold pseudo-reference electrode (RE) with an area of 0.8 mm². The contact 
leads were fabricated from a carbon layer with an additional gold and titanium or chromium adhesion layer. 
The entire structure was passivated using an SU-8 layer, except for the active sensing areas and contact pads.

3.3 
Production of 3D pyrolytic carbon electrodes 
Creating high-performance 3D electrodes is a lengthy, complex, and resource-intensive procedure that re-
quires careful optimization at each step to ensure the resulting structures have the necessary attributes. The 
process is outlined in the subsections below, while Figure 13 provides an overview. 
The first step involves designing the model using CAD software. The model is then exported in a standard 
triangulation language(.STL), after which the printer settings are specified, including layout and support struc-
ture definitions. The printing process takes place in a tank. Post-processing begins with washing the printed 
parts using the Form Wash to remove excess resin. Once rinsed and dried, the parts undergo post-curing using 
the Form Cure machine, which finalizes the structure by further strengthening the material.

3.3.1 
Structure design 
The design and fabrication of 3D electrodes in this study were achieved using stereolithography (SLA) print-
ing, a high-precision additive manufacturing technique. The process began with the 3D modeling of the elec-
trode structures, designed using SolidWorks 2023 software. The finalized 3D models were exported from 
SolidWorks as stereolithography files (.stl), a standard format for 3D printing, which translates the CAD ge-
ometry into a mesh of triangles that can be interpreted by 3D printing software.  
3.3.2 
3D printing 
The DLP printing process was carried out using the Form 3 Formlabs printer with High Temp V2 resin. The 
STL files generated from SolidWorks were uploaded to the PreForm Software V.2.18.0 (Formlabs Inc.), 
which is specifically designed to interface with Formlabs printers. PreForm allows the user to optimize the 
print layout, generate supports, and prepare the file for printing. It automatically slices the 3D model into 
layers and generates support structures necessary for the printing process. 
One of the critical things in this step of the process is the support generation and refinement. In this case, the 
support structures were carefully modified to avoid interference with the critical regions of the electrodes, 
particularly the inner areas. The aim was to minimize the use of supports while ensuring structural stability 
during the print process. The final support configuration involved tilting the structures laterally, placing sup-
ports only on the sides to reduce interference with the electrode's functional parts. This editing process is time-
consuming and can significantly affect the quality of the print. For instance, printing six electrode structures 
required approximately seven hours due to the complex geometry and precision required. 
The printing process was conducted using High Temp V2 resin, a photopolymer resin known for its high 
thermal resistance and mechanical stability. This resin is designed for applications that require parts to with-
stand elevated temperatures and maintain dimensional stability, making it suitable for electrodes intended for 
pyrolysis. The resin, with an aging of 2022, exhibits high accuracy, low shrinkage, and strong adhesion prop-
erties, which are critical for maintaining the fidelity of the 3D-printed structures. 
3.3.3 
Washing of the printed structures 
After the printing process, the 3D-printed structures were subjected to a cleaning process to remove any re-
sidual resin. This was done using a Form Wash system (Formlabs), which automates the cleaning of parts 
with isopropanol. The printed structures were immersed in isopropanol while still attached to the printing 
==PAGE:35==

platform. The washing protocol was based on the resin manufacturer's guidelines, with the High Temp V2 
resin requiring an 2-propanol wash for 6 minutes to ensure complete removal of excess uncured resin. This 
step is critical as any leftover resin can impact the mechanical properties and final finish of the structures. 
3.3.4 
Post-processing: removal of supports and drying 
Once washed, the electrode structures were removed from the printing platform using a metal spatula. The 
supports were carefully separated from the main structure by manually snapping them off. Following this, the 
structures were washed again in 2-propanol to ensure all residual support material and resin were removed 
(Form Wash machine). The structures were then dried using compressed air, ensuring that no solvent residues 
remained that could affect subsequent curing steps. 
3.3.5 
Curing of printed structures 
The final step in the post-processing of the printed electrodes was curing, which further strengthens the ma-
terial by fully polymerizing the resin. The curing process was conducted at 60ºC for 10 minutes in the Form 
Cure machine. The curing process in resin 3D printing involves solidifying photosensitive monomers by ex-
posing them to controlled ultraviolet (UV) light or other high-energy light sources. This process is crucial for 
achieving high-resolution prints with smooth surface finishes. The curing process starts during the printing 
itself but later a post-curing process is performed by exposing the finished structure to additional UV light. 
3.3.6 
Analysis and iteration of the process 
Following curing, each electrode structure was examined to ensure that it met the necessary quality require-
ments. The primary criteria were mechanical stability, structural integrity, and sufficient pad area for the at-
tachment of electrical contacts for later electrochemical analysis. Additionally, the long arm of the electrode, 
designed to separate the area of interest (the pillar) from the rest of the structure, was assessed for appropriate 
dimensions and robustness. 
If the structures passed the quality control checks, they were deemed suitable for pyrolysis. If any flaws or 
brittleness were detected, the process was repeated, optimizing the design or print settings until satisfactory 
results were achieved. This iterative process ensured the reliability and reproducibility of the electrode fabri-
cation process. 
3.3.7 
Pyrolysis process 
The pyrolysis process utilized in this study follows an established methodology optimized for the carboniza-
tion of materials, ensuring consistent and high-quality results that was optimized in previous studies.30 The 
pyrolysis was performed using a PEO-604 furnace (ATV Technology, Germany), which operates in a con-
trolled nitrogen environment. Nitrogen was supplied at a flow rate of 20 standard liters per minute (SLM), 
creating an inert atmosphere conducive to pyrolysis. 
The pyrolysis protocol involved a precise temperature ramping sequence. Initially, the temperature was in-
creased at a rate of 2°C per minute until reaching 340°C. This initial stage was followed by a slower ramping 
rate of 1°C per minute, extending up to 425°C. Lastly, the temperature was raised at a rate of 2°C per minute 
to the final target of 900°C. At this peak temperature, a dwell time of 1 hour was maintained, which allowed 
the carbonization process to stabilize. Dwell time refers to the period during which the furnace is held at a 
constant temperature, enabling thorough thermal treatment and ensuring uniform structural transformation 
across the material.

3.3.8 
Plasma treatment 
The oxygen plasma treatment of the 3D-printed pyrolytic carbon electrodes was performed using a low-pres-
sure plasma system, specifically the Diener ATTO model (Diener Electronics). The plasma treatment process 
involved placing the electrodes in the chamber of the ATTO plasma system, where they were exposed to 
oxygen plasma at a power setting of 100 W and a pressure of 0.3 mbar. The treatment duration was set to 10

During treatment, pure oxygen gas was introduced into the chamber, to maintain consistent plasma conditions. 
Post-treatment, the electrodes were carefully removed from the chamber and stored under ambient conditions

3.3.9 
Electrode preparation for electrochemical analysis 
Wire attachment using silver-filled two-component epoxy 
The preparation of pyrolyzed 3D carbon electrodes for electrochemical analysis involved both cable attach-
ment and selective insulation of non-active areas. To secure the electrodes to cables, an EPO-TEK® H20E 
epoxy kit was used. This silver-filled, two-component epoxy system, widely applied in microelectronics for 
its high conductivity, was mixed in a 1:1 ratio according to the manufacturer's instructions. A drop of the 
mixture was applied to the pad section of each electrode to attach the cable, leveraging the pad’s ample surface 
area for a secure adhesive bond and low contact resistance. The assembly was then cured at 80°C for three 
hours to ensure a reliable connection. 
Insulation using SU-8 epoxy resin 
To confine electrochemical activity to the pillar alone, the arm and pad were insulated using SU-8 epoxy resin, 
sourced from MicroChem Corp., a common supplier in the microfabrication industry. SU-8 is a durable,

chemically resistant, negative photoresist often used in microelectronics. Insulation was achieved using a 
PDMS-based immersion chamber, designed to allow controlled exposure of the electrodes to the SU-8 solu-

3.4 
Electrochemical analysis 
All electrochemical measurements were performed in a 0.01 M phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) containing a 
10 mM solution of ferrocyanide/ferricyanide, prepared by mixing equal volumes of 10 mM 
K₄[Fe(CN)₆]·3H₂O (potassium ferrocyanide) and 10 mM K₃[Fe(CN)₆] (potassium ferricyanide). This buffer 
solution was prepared to maintain a stable pH of 7. The electrochemical analysis utilized a PalmSense poten-
tiostat within a three-electrode setup. An Ag|AgCl electrode served as the reference, a platinum wire func-
tioned as the counter electrode, and the working electrode was made of pyrolytic carbon. 
For cyclic voltammetry (CV) measurements, the following parameters were set: an equilibration time of 0 
seconds, an initial potential of -0.4 V, and a reversal vertex at 0.8 V, with a return to -0.4 V. The potential was 
applied with a step size of 0.01 V at a scan rate of 0.1 V/s, and three cycles were performed for each meas-
urement. 
Electrochemical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) measurements were conducted with an AC amplitude of 10 
mV. The frequency sweep ranged from 1 MHz to 0.1 Hz, encompassing 71 points at a resolution of 10 points 
per decade. The measurements were performed at open circuit potential, and the stability criterion was set at 
0.1 mV/s. 
3.5 
EDA tethering 
Prior to the electrochemical oxidation of EDA, the electrodes were conditioned in anhydrous ethanol to re-
move any contaminants and prevent interference from water during the functionalization process. This step 
ensures that the high oxidizing potential used does not lead to water electrolysis, which could disrupt the EDA 
attachment. 
The functionalization was performed by immersing the electrodes in a solution of 50 mM EDA and 100 mM 
lithium perchlorate (LiClO₄) as the supporting electrolyte, both dissolved in anhydrous ethanol. 12 Using an 
Ag/AgCl reference electrode, the applied potential was cycled between 0.0 and 1.3 V at a scan rate of 10 mV/s 
for three cycles. The CV scan between these potentials facilitates the oxidation of EDA, allowing it to attach 
to the electrode surface.

3.7 
DHB tethering 
The conjugation of DHB onto the electrodes was achieved using reductive amination chemistry, where amine 
groups generated on the carbon electrode surface were coupled to the aldehyde (CHO) groups on 2,5-dihy-
droxybenzaldehyde (DHB). This process was facilitated by NaCNBH3 as the reducing catalyst at pH 7.4. The 
DHB solution was prepared by mixing a 10 mM solution of NaCNBH3 in PBS and then adding DHB to reach 
a final concentration of 50 mM. The electrodes were incubated in this DHB solution at pH pH 7.0, for 24 
hours at room temperature. The incubation took place on an orbital shaker set to 300 rpm, ensuring that the 
solution was consistently stirred to optimize the reaction on the electrode surface. This extended incubation 
period allowed sufficient time for the reductive amination reaction to complete, promoting effective attach-
ment of DHB to the functionalized electrode.12 
After the incubation, the electrodes were rinsed with deionized water and subsequently washed in PBS on a 
Labline Orbit Shaker for 15 minutes. This washing step was crucial to remove any DHB that was non-specif-
ically adsorbed onto the electrode surface, leaving only covalently attached DHB for further electrochemical 
analysis. The CV measurements were then performed in deoxygenated PBS, which was prepared by bubbling 
nitrogen through the solution 15 minutes prior to use. Throughout the experiments, cyclic voltammetry (CV) 
was used to evaluate the tethering process of DHB to the electrode surfaces. Several scan rates were employed 
during different experiments to analyse the effect of scan rate on the functionalization efficiency. The typical 
settings for CV included an equilibration time of 0 seconds, a starting potential of -0.6 V, and a vertex potential 
of 0.9 V. The potential was cycled between these values, returning to -0.6 V at the end of each cycle, different 
scan rates were tested from 0.01V/sec to 1 V/sec.12 
3.8 
EDA/ABA mixing molar ratios experiment 
Parent solutions of both EDA and ABA were prepared at a concentration of 10 mM each. These solutions 
were then mixed in different molar ratios to create three distinct experimental conditions: ABA:EDA, 1:10, 
1:5, and 2:5. In the first condition (1:10), 2 mL of the ABA solution and 18 mL of the EDA solution were 
mixed to obtain a final volume (Vf) of 20 mL. For the second condition (1:5), 4 mL of ABA and 16 mL of 
EDA were mixed for a total volume of 20 mL. Lastly, in the 2:5 condition, 8 mL of ABA and 12 mL of EDA 
were mixed, also producing a final volume of 20 mL. 
For the electrochemical tethering process, the mixed solution containing both ABA and EDA was used for 
simultaneous tethering. A single cyclic voltammetry (CV) scan was applied at a scan rate of 5 mV/s to initiate 
and control the electrooxidation of both molecules on the electrode surface.

3.11 Antibody conjugation protocol 
The antibody conjugation protocol for the functionalization of 3D pyrolytic carbon electrodes was adapted 
from a standard immunocytochemistry procedure. After the successful attachment of Laminin antibody on the 
electrode surface, the process began with blocking the electrode surfaces to prevent non-specific antibody 
binding. This was achieved by incubating the electrodes in 1% (v/v) goat serum for one hour at room temper-
ature using a plate shaker set to 300 rpm. 
Following the blocking step, the electrodes were incubated with a primary antibody solution consisting of 
mouse laminin antibody (Sigma-Aldrich, 2 mg/mL) at a 1:1000 dilution in PBS. The incubation was carried 
out overnight at 4°C to allow sufficient binding of the primary antibody to the target on the electrode surface. 
After incubation, the electrodes were rinsed thoroughly with PBS to remove any unbound primary antibodies. 
Next, the electrodes were incubated with a secondary antibody solution. Goat anti-Rabbit IgG (H+L) Alexa 
Fluor™ 546nm conjugate (ThermoFisher, 2 mg/mL) was used at a 1:500 dilution in PBS, which binds spe-
cifically to the primary antibody. This incubation was performed for 30 minutes at room temperature, again 
on a plate shaker set to 300 rpm to ensure uniform binding. Afterward, the electrodes were washed multiple 
times with PBS to remove any excess secondary antibodies. All rinsing steps were performed at room tem-
perature.

4. Results and discussion 
4.1 
Vertically standing 3D pyrolytic carbon electrodes 
The design and fabrication of the 3D-printed structures for electrochemical applications required multiple 
iterations to meet the necessary requirements for functionality, size, and structural integrity. Initial designs 
were preliminary and underwent significant refinements to achieve an electrode structure that was sufficiently 
large for subsequent electrochemical analysis while avoiding bulkiness. The need to avoid overly large designs 
was driven by several factors: first, the high cost of resin and printing processes minimizing material usage 
advantageous from an economic perspective; second, larger structures require more time to print and process, 
reducing overall efficiency; and finally, future applications of this research aim for miniaturized designs, 
making bulkier prototypes counterproductive. 
The designed structure incorporates three essential components: a pillar, a pad, and an arm. The pillar is the 
most critical element, as it serves as the site for biomolecule tethering, a key step in the functionalization of 
the electrode. Therefore, the pillar must be highly reproducible, with minimal defects introduced during sup-
port removal, post-printing processing and pyrolysis.  
The second component, the pad, was designed to provide a large enough surface for securely attaching a cable, 
which will later be used for electrochemical testing. The size of the pad needed to balance two conflicting 
requirements: it had to be large enough to accommodate a glued cable, but not so large that it became difficult 
to handle or contributed to unnecessary bulk in the overall design. The pad also served as the connection point 
between the functional parts of the electrode and the external circuit, further underscoring the importance of 
its size and shape. 
The arm, which connects the pillar and the pad, was included to create sufficient distance between the electri-
cal junction and the area of interest (the pillar). This separation is crucial for avoiding potential interference 
between the electrical components and the chemicals used for biomolecule tethering, which often involves 
submerging the pillar in solvents. 
Another key requirement of the structure was porosity, particularly in the pillar. High porosity increases the 
available surface area for biomolecule attachment, enhancing the electrochemical activity of the electrode. 
From a structural perspective, porosity is essential for reducing internal pressure during the pyrolysis process. 
Pyrolysis, a high-temperature process necessary for converting the printed resin structure into carbon, can 
generate high internal pressures due to the degassing of trapped gaseous products. These gases, if not ade-
quately released, can cause the structure to collapse or fracture, particularly in the pillar, leading to defects 
such as bubbles or cracks. The inclusion of pores in the design helps mitigate this risk by providing pathways 
for gas escape during pyrolysis, which reduces the likelihood of structural failure and ensures a more uniform 
==PAGE:41==
 
 
30 
 
and homogeneous carbon structure. The pyrolysis process is a necessary step for transforming the 3D-printed 
resin structures into carbon-based electrodes.

4.2 
Electro-functionalization of the pyrolytic carbon electrodes 
The functionalization strategies employed in this study were inspired by previous research conducted by Bisht 
et al. at the University of California, Irvine, which explored diffusion-free mediator-based miniature biofuel 
cell anodes fabricated on Carbon-MEMS electrodes.12 This approach involves the electrochemical oxidation 
of amine linkers onto the electrode surface. This functionalization technique is compatible with both aliphatic 
and aryl linkers, utilizing stable compounds that provide a robust and adaptable framework for subsequent 
modifications. Building upon these established methods, the current work aims to extend these principles to 
enhance the structural and functional properties of 3D pyrolytic carbon electrodes for biofunctional applica-
tions. 
Throughout the functionalization process, two types of electrodes were evaluated: the 3D electrodes described 
previously in methodology and a specific set of 2D electrodes with a “lollipop” shape. Assessing both 3D and 
2D electrodes provided a dual perspective on the robustness and effectiveness of the functionalization tech-
niques. This comparative approach was essential for two reasons. Firstly, it allowed us to validate the func-
tionalization process across different electrode geometries, thereby enhancing the reliability and versatility of 
the results. Secondly, as detailed in the Materials and Methods section, the production of 3D electrodes is both 
time- and resource-intensive. To facilitate comprehensive analysis, the readily available 2D electrodes served 
==PAGE:46==
 
 
35 
 
as practical and efficient testing models. Findings derived from these 2D models provided valuable insights 
that could subsequently be applied to the 3D electrodes. 
In the study, two electrode designs will be tested for their effectiveness in tethering biomolecules. Design A 
utilizes only EDA linkers for attaching DHB, a mediator known for enhancing electron transfer during elec-
trochemical analysis. This approach is straightforward and provides amino groups for biomolecule binding. 
Design B, however, incorporates both EDA and ABA linkers. The inclusion of ABA in Design B introduces 
carboxylic functional groups in addition to the amino groups from EDA, offering increased versatility for 
biomolecule attachment and potentially improving binding stability and orientation. DHB serves as an elec-
tron mediator, facilitating redox reactions by shuttling electrons between the electrode surface and the bio-
molecule.

4.2.2 
3D electrodes stability test 
To assess the stability and robustness of the 3D pyrolytic carbon electrodes, two electrochemical tests were 
conducted following oxygen plasma treatment and SU8 resin insulation. The stability tests are essential for 
ensuring that the electrodes maintain consistent electrochemical behavior over time, which is crucial for their 
reliability in repeated applications. The desired outcome is for the electrodes to show minimal degradation or 
alteration in performance.  
The first test involved studying the electrochemical behavior of the electrodes across a range of scan rates 
using cyclic voltammetry (CV). This experiment helps to understand how the electrodes respond to different 
rates of electron transfer. The scan rates tested were 0.1, 0.2, 0.4, 0.8, and 1 V/s. By examining how the current 
response scales with the scan rate, we can evaluate the electrode’s ability to handle various electrochemical 
conditions. The relation of the peak current with the scan rate tells us about diffusion-controlled processes and 
reaction kinetics.19 If the peak current scales with the square root of the scan rate, the process is typically 
diffusion controlled. This means the current is primarily influenced by the rate at which the reactants diffuse 
to the electrode surface. On the other hand, reversible reactions generally show consistent peak-to-peak sep-
aration regardless of scan rate. An increase in peak separation with scan rate suggests slower, quasi-reversible, 
==PAGE:51==
 
 
40 
 
or irreversible reactions due to kinetic limitations.15 Since here we are analysing the bare electrode with no 
surface modification nor mediator the results expectedly show a linear relationship between peak current and 
the square root of the scan rate, characteristic of a diffusion-controlled process. rather than to the square root 
of it, indicating a largely non-Faradaic, capacitive behavior. The results of this study can be appreciated in 
Figure 27.

anodic peak was observed, consistent with the expected non-reversible attachment process.

Over the course of the three CV cycles, a decrease in peak current was noted with each successive scan for 
both 2D and 3D electrodes. This trend is indicative of surface saturation, as the electrode becomes fully cov-
ered with EDA molecules, leaving fewer active sites available for further oxidation. The initial cycle showed 
a prominent anodic peak, confirming that the EDA had begun oxidizing and attaching to the surface. By the 
third cycle, the peak was significantly reduced, reflecting that the electrode surface had reached functionali-
zation capacity. The decrease in current across cycles supports the hypothesis that EDA molecules progres-
sively cover the electrode surface, saturating the available sites. This saturation behavior, along with the con-
sistent anodic peak in the initial cycle, provides strong evidence for successful EDA functionalization. 
The results in Figure 30 highlight distinct differences between 2D and 3D pyrolytic carbon electrodes during 
the electro-oxidation of ethylenediamine (EDA). Notably, the current response in the 3D electrodes is signif-
icantly higher than in the 2D electrodes, which becomes evident when comparing the cyclic voltammograms 
(CVs) of both electrode types on the same scale (top four charts in Figure 30). This indicates that 3D electrodes 
offer an increased surface area, thereby facilitating a higher degree of EDA oxidation. The greater surface 
area of the 3D structures likely enhances electron transfer rates, resulting in higher currents and supporting 
their suitability for applications requiring efficient electron exchange. 
Another key observation is the difference in oxidation potential between the 2D and 3D electrodes. In 3D 
electrodes, the anodic peak occurs at approximately 0.8 V, aligning closely with previously reported values 
for pyrolytic carbon electrodes in the literature12. This consistency suggests that the 3D pyrolytic carbon elec-
trodes share similar surface characteristics to those in the cited studies, allowing for comparable electrochem-
ical behavior. The oxidation peak for 2D electrodes, however, shifts to around 1.03 V. This deviation could 
stem from variations in surface morphology or intrinsic electrochemical properties between the planar 2D 
electrodes and the more complex 3D pyrolytic carbon structures. The higher potential in 2D electrodes may 
indicate that a greater energy input is required to achieve oxidation, possibly due to a less favorable surface 
environment for EDA attachment. However, the results clearly show a successful attachment of EDA on the 
surface of both electrode types.

conditions, as the pH of the ferro-ferri solution is close to its second pKa. Consequently, this partial protona-
tion would reduce the positive charge density on the electrode surface, as fewer amino groups are fully pro-

4.2.5 
Study of different tethering conditions 
As discussed earlier, the tethering process shows significant variability, indicating that the process is inher-
ently inconsistent. This variability can be attributed to several factors, including human error, the setup itself, 
and the intrinsic differences in the electrodes produced in-house. Both 2D and 3D electrodes, being non-
industrial and handmade, inherently lack the uniformity that would be expected from commercially produced 
materials. To address this, further investigations were carried out to assess whether improved tethering con-
sistency could be achieved by adjusting specific parameters. The goal is to reduce the variability in the process, 
as measured by the standard deviation of Rct after biomolecule tethering. Lower variability suggests a more 
uniform distribution of tethered molecules across the electrode surface, and consequently, a more consistent 
tethering process. Two key parameters were investigated to observe their impact on the consistency of the 
tethering process, in both 2D and 3D electrodes: 
• 
Scan rate 
• 
Concentration of the tethered molecule 
Additionally, another approach was explored where both EDA and ABA were tethered simultaneously. This 
is feasible due to the fact that both molecules can be tethered within the same potential window, enabling the 
preparation of a solution with a specific molar ratio of ABA and EDA. This method allows for the tethering 
of both amino and carboxylic groups in a single step. The simultaneous tethering strategy is advantageous for 
several reasons. Firstly, it increases the likelihood of achieving a more homogeneous distribution of both 
amino and carboxylic groups, as opposed to sequential tethering where one molecule may occupy a substantial 
portion of the electrode surface, leaving fewer available sites for the subsequent molecule. Secondly, this 
approach simplifies the surface functionalization process, potentially reducing the number of steps required 
to functionalize the electrode surface.  
The results of this analysis are presented in Table 6. One of the most prominent observations is the high 
variability in the tethering process across almost all conditions tested. This variability is reflected in the sig-
nificant standard deviations observed, particularly for the 2D electrodes. For instance, the EDA tethering on 
2D electrodes at a scan rate of 5 mV/sec showed a 60% condition error, while increasing the scan rate to 10 
mV/sec resulted in an even larger condition error of 102%. This suggests that neither a higher nor lower scan 
rate clearly improves the tethering process's consistency, highlighting the inherent variability in the tethering 
mechanism. Furthermore, increasing the concentration of the tethered molecules does not seem to provide any 
substantial improvement either, as demonstrated by the concentration-dependent results for both EDA and 
ABA. 
The significant variability might stem from several factors, including inconsistencies in the electrode surface 
itself, human error during preparation, and the complexity of the tethering mechanism. It is also important to 
note that the electrodes used in this study were fabricated in-house, meaning they might lack the uniformity 
seen in industrially manufactured electrodes. This inherent variability further complicates the optimization of 
the tethering process and suggests that achieving a more consistent surface modification might require deeper 
process control.

Mediator attachment 
In this section, the mediator attachment involving 2,5-dihydroxybenzaldehyde (DHB) is investigated. DHB is 
chosen as the mediator molecule because of its relatively low equilibrium redox potential, which minimizes 
potential losses during the electron transfer process, especially when compared to other mediators such as 
ferrocene. This makes DHB an effective molecule for facilitating electron shuttling between the enzyme and 
the electrode, crucial for improving electron transfer efficiency. The mediator molecules were covalently im-
mobilized onto the electrode surface via ethylenediamine (EDA) linkers (See Figure 42). To confirm the suc-
cessful attachment of DHB to the EDA-functionalized electrodes, cyclic voltammetry (CV) was employed, 
with the aim of observing characteristic electron transfer behaviors. The functionalized electrodes were rinsed 
with PBS to remove any loosely adsorbed DHB molecules, while an adsorption control was included, where 
untreated electrodes were incubated in the same DHB solution without undergoing the EDA functionalization 
process. 
 
Figure 42. Schematic representation of the tethering of the DHB mediator on a previously modified electrode with EDA. Source: 
own elaboration with Biorender ®. 
 
As shown in Figure 43 a comparison was made between three electrodes: an EDA-functionalized electrode 
without DHB, an EDA-DHB functionalized electrode, and the bare electrode incubated with DHB (adsorption 
control). The results clearly show that the EDA-DHB functionalized electrodes exhibit a larger redox current 
compared to both the adsorption control and the EDA-functionalized electrode without DHB. This increase 
in redox current confirms the successful covalent immobilization of DHB onto the electrode surface. The 
adsorption control, which involved only passive incubation with DHB, shows a significantly lower current, 
indicating that physical adsorption alone does not effectively immobilize DHB to the electrode surface. This 
observation strongly supports the need for covalent attachment through EDA linkers to achieve stable medi-
ator immobilization. 
This result aligns with the expected behavior of covalently tethered DHB functioning as a mediator, support-
ing more efficient electron transfer between the electrode and redox species. In addition, the comparison be-
tween 2D and 3D electrodes reveals a consistent increase in redox current for both types, although 3D elec-
trodes exhibit slightly higher currents due to their larger surface area, further reinforcing the benefits of DHB 
immobilization on varied electrode geometries.

activated electrodes (using EDC/NHS), the carboxyl groups of ABA react with the amine groups on laminin. 
For EDA-activated electrodes (using glutaraldehyde), the aldehyde groups react with amine groups on lam-
inin, forming stable covalent bonds. Once laminin is securely attached to the electrodes, the primary antibody,

more fluorescence was detected, indicating more successful tethering of the EDA molecules. Conversely, the 
1:5 ratio showed considerably less fluorescence, implying a lower degree of functionalization. While the 
source of the fluorescence (EDA or ABA) could not be definitively attributed, the results suggest that simul-
taneous functionalization with both ABA and EDA was achieved. Further studies using techniques such as 
NMR or X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS) would be beneficial for quantifying the distribution and 
density of the attached chemical groups.

Despite the positive indications of biomolecule immobilization presented in this section, there is some uncer-

fective and obtaining a deeper, more detailed analysis of the distribution and density of the tethered molecules. 
The curved, cylindrical shape of the 3D electrodes with small holes required focusing on specific points, which 
makes it challenging to claim that the images obtained fully capture the uniformity or comparative efficiency 
of EDA versus ABA functionalization across the entire electrode surface. This limitation highlights a potential 
improvement for future studies: creating a flat, 3D-printed electrode structure. A flat surface would enable 
more consistent imaging and improve the reliability of the fluorescence data, providing a more uniform field 
of focus and thus allowing for a more precise evaluation of which functionalization method achieves better 
coverage. Additionally, a flat structure would facilitate the use of other surface analysis techniques, such as 
Fourier-Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FT-IR) and X-ray Photoelectron Spectroscopy (XPS), which re-
quire smoother surfaces for optimal results. Although FT-IR and XPS are powerful techniques for character-
izing functional groups and surface modifications, they were not used in this study due to equipment limita-
tions. FT-IR, for instance, provides detailed information on specific chemical bonds (e.g., C=O, C-O, N-H), 
which would be valuable for characterizing functional groups on carbon surfaces. Through sampling modes 
like Attenuated Total Reflectance (ATR) and Specular Reflection, FT-IR could offer a comprehensive spec-
trum of functional groups without affecting sample integrity.

states on the electrode surface. By measuring the energy of ejected electrons from atoms in the outermost

for a more detailed comparison between functionalization methods, offering clearer insights into their effi-

5. Conclusions and future outlook 
The conclusions of this thesis highlight the successful development and functionalization of vertically stand-
ing 3D pyrolytic carbon electrodes, demonstrating their potential for biosensing applications. The fabrication 
process maintained structural integrity, particularly when milder curing conditions (60°C for 10 minutes) were 
applied, which preserved morphology and improved electrochemical performance. These 3D electrodes, fab-
ricated through pyrolysis, exhibited stable electrochemical characteristics, making them suitable for use in 
complex biosensing environments, as evidenced by their stability during repeated electrochemical cycling. A 
comparative analysis showed that the 3D electrodes had lower variability in biomolecule tethering than tradi-
tional 2D electrodes. Optimal tethering conditions were achieved at a scan rate of 5 mV/s, where uniform 
functionalization was most evident. While the functionalization protocol was effective, further optimization 
is recommended to enhance its robustness and consistency, which could improve reproducibility across dif-
ferent applications. Electrochemical techniques (CV and EIS) confirmed successful surface modification of 
the electrodes. EDA-functionalization led to significant improvements in charge transfer resistance and peak 
current, underscoring its superior electrochemical performance relative to ABA. The 3D electrodes demon-
strated more consistent results in functionalization compared to 2D electrodes. The functionalization of the 
electrodes using EDA and ABA successfully introduced amino and carboxyl groups, respectively this was 
verified by the successful attachment of antibodies on the functionalized surfaces. EDA-functionalized elec-
trodes exhibited a more uniform distribution and higher functionalization efficiency, as suggested by stronger 
fluorescence signals when compared to ABA-functionalized surfaces. 
Future studies should explore other 3D electrode designs that would be compatible with advanced surface 
analysis techniques, such as Fourier-Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FT-IR) and X-ray Photoelectron Spec-
troscopy (XPS), to gain deeper insights into the distribution and density of tethered chemical groups. Addi-
tionally, exhaustive testing of tethering conditions should be undertaken to establish a more stable surface 
modification protocol, enhancing biomolecule attachment consistency. Exploring flat, 3D-printed electrode 
designs would also improve visualization and facilitate the use of analytical techniques that require flat sur-
faces, thereby enabling a more comprehensive assessment of functionalization quality and biomolecule dis-
tribution. Overall, this thesis provides a foundational approach for the development of 3D electrodes and 
optoelectrical waveguides tailored for 3D cellular environments.
