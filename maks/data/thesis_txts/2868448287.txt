Abstract
This thesis deals with tribological behaviour, such as friction and the overall structural
integrity of the sealing system of rotary shaft seals from Haagensen Seals. The thesis aims to
investigate the behaviour of two types of rotary shaft seals, type R and type BSB, in pressurized
applications below 10 bar.
The experiments were carried out with an tribological test machine at Haagensen Seals.
The primary objective was to perform experimental tests on type R and BSB seals. However,
due to the experimental setup’s constraints, which included a minimum system pressure of 3
bar, testing was limited to the BSB seal as the type R seal’s pressure limit was exceeded. The
experiments analysed parameters such as pressure, temperature, and frictional torque. The BSB
seals were subjected to three different loads, 0, 5, and 10 bar, for 5 minutes at two speeds, 150
and 1000 rpm.
A numerical model was developed in COMSOL Multiphysics®, incorporating the Reynolds
equation to describe lubrication behaviour and the Bush contact model to simulate the interac-
tion between the seals and shaft surfaces. For the type R seal, the FKM demonstrated superior
performance to NBR due to its higher stiffness, reduced displacement and maintained higher
contact pressures. While both seals showed a reduction in radial force under dynamic condi-
tions, FKM exhibited smaller reductions. The BSB seal showed distinct characteristics, includ-
ing increased contact pressure due to interaction with a metal insert at pressures exceeding 1
bar. The film thickness was reduced with increasing pressure and maintained low leakage rates.

Introduction
Controlling fluid movement has been central to human innovation for over a millennium.
From the earliest aqueducts of ancient Rome to the advanced machinery of the Industrial Rev-
olution, civilisations have continually sought solutions to manage fluid flow, minimise leakage,
and reduce friction in mechanical systems. The early innovations of clay pipes for irrigation
and water mills for harnessing energy laid the foundation for developing technologies that are
still crucial to modern engineering.
The need to control fluid movement within mechanical systems became even more critical
with the beginning of rotating machinery during the Industrial Revolution. Here, they saw the
need for mechanical seals. Mechanical seals were initially designed to prevent oil leakage and
block external contaminants from entering a system. Over the years, they have evolved and
become more advanced, with improved material and design.
Today, mechanical seals are widely used in various industries, such as automotive, mar-
itime, and the rocket industry, to seal moving machine parts. The seal is crucial in ensuring this
machine’s safe and efficient operation by minimising leakage, reducing energy consumption,
and, most importantly, minimising environmental impact. This field’s continuous research and
development have created highly specialised mechanical seals that can handle the most chal-
lenging applications. These advancements have made them indispensable in modern industrial
operations [2].
A typical mechanical seal for rotating applications consists of five primary components: a
primary ring, a mating ring, an O-ring, a spring, and a shaft connection, as shown in Figure 1.

The primary ring is mounted on the rotating shaft with a set screw. The primary ring main-
tains contact with the stationary mating ring, fixed to the housing. In order to maintain proper
contact between the seal faces, an axial force applied by a spring is added. O-rings serve as sec-
ondary sealing elements and are positioned between the primary ring and the shaft and between
the mating ring and the housing to prevent leakage at the static interfaces. During operation,
pressure builds up and a thin fluid film develops between the primary and mating rings, creating
a narrow gap due to hydrodynamic effects. This fluid film reduces wear by minimising direct
contact between the sealing surfaces. However, the film must remain sufficiently thin to prevent
leakage. The thickness of the film depends on various interacting physical phenomena, such as
surface roughness, fluid viscosity, relative velocity of the sealing faces, and applied load.
While mechanical seals provide several advantages, such as effectively preventing leakage,
they have some drawbacks. They are generally more costly than other sealing solutions and

An alternative solution for the mechanical seal is the rotary shaft seal, which offers a sim-
pler and more cost-effective method for sealing rotating shafts in less demanding applications.
Using a radial shaft seal can reduce the number of components from five to as few as two or
three. The two most commonly used radial shaft seals are shown in Figure 2 with their corre-

The seal to the left, Figure 2(a), is a type R seal. It is a low-pressure seal with a metal insert
covered with rubber. It also includes a spring that helps maintain consistent contact between
the seal lip and the shaft surface. The R seal can handle up to 1 bar pressure. Conversely, the
seal on the right, Figure 2(b), is a type BSB seal. This medium-pressure seal incorporates two
metal inserts, one at the front and another at the back, without including a spring. This design
enables the BSB seal to manage up to 15 bar pressures.
The aim of this project is to investigate the behaviour of the two types of rotary shaft seals
in pressurised applications below 10 bar. This will be done by conducting experiments under
different pressure loads and rotational speeds. The obtained data will be analysed for tribologi-
cal properties such as friction, leakage, and the overall structural integrity of the sealing system.
A numerical model will also be made and the results will be compared with the experimentally
obtained data.

Theoretical Background
In this chapter the basic topics within tribology and seal mechanics will be reviewed.
2.1
Lubrication
When talking about seals, the subject of tribology will occur several times. Tribology is
the science of interacting surfaces in relative motion, including the study and application of
friction, lubrication, and wear principles [3]. Tribology is interdisciplinary and encompasses
many academic areas, including physics, chemistry, materials science, mathematics, biology,
and engineering.
Lubrication can be defined as the application of some material between two objects moving
relative to each other to allow smooth operation as much as necessary, but why use lubricant,
and what is its purpose? The purpose of a lubricant can be boiled down to four main properties.
Firstly, lubrication is used to reduce wear and premature fatigue. Secondly, lubrication is used to
enhance favourable driving characteristics such as low noise or low friction. Thirdly, lubrication
prevents seals from overheating and deteriorating lubricant by transferring the generated heat to
the surrounding environment. Lastly, lubrication is used to coat metal to protect it from foreign
elements such as water. As water is unable to penetrate the metal, the rusting process does not
start [3].
Lubrication can have different regimes when looking at the contact interface between the
shaft and seal. There are various lubrication regimes, and the type of regime depends on several
factors that influence the lubrication, including applied load, lubricant viscosity, pressure at the
interface, velocity of the films, etc. [4]. Traditionally, these regimes are denoted as boundary,
hydrodynamic, and mixed regimes and can be defined as:
• Boundary Lubrication (BL):
In this regime, the lubricant film cannot separate the surfaces in relative motion, causing
the asperities to bear most of the applied load. This results in a high coefficient of friction
and high energy loss.
• Hydrodynamic Lubrication (HL):
In the hydrodynamic lubrication regime, the lubricant film will completely separate the
two surfaces in relative motion. As a result, the load is fully supported by the lubricant.
This can occur in two forms: hydrostatic lubrication, where external pressure is applied,
and hydrodynamic lubrication, where the relative motion between surfaces generates a
hydrodynamic lift force.
• Mixed Lubrication (ML):
For the mixed lubrication regime, the lubrication film and surface asperities share the
load. This regime serves as the intermediate state between boundary lubrication and
hydrodynamic lubrication.

2.2
Friction
Friction is one of the three fundamental principles within tribology. When two bodies
attempt to slide over each other, friction will occur due to the surface roughness. At the mi-
croscopic level, surfaces have irregularities called asperities, which create resistance when the
surfaces are in contact, as shown in Figure 5. These asperities interact with one another, leading
to both elastic and plastic deformation. The microscopic contact points between these irreg-
ularities create frictional forces, which oppose the relative motion of the two surfaces. The
magnitude of this friction depends on the material properties, surface roughness, and the force
pressing the surfaces together.

This force can be divided into two components: a normal force and a tangential force. The
normal force is the contact force that is perpendicular to the surface a body comes into contact
with. The tangential force, on the other hand, is the force that acts on a body in motion where
the direction of the force is tangential to the curved path of the body or parallel to the slope on
which the body slides.
There are two primary forms of friction, static and kinetic friction (also called dynamic
friction). Static friction force is the force needed to get a body pushed into motion relative to
the other body. The kinetic friction force is the force necessary to maintain a stable sliding of a
body.
When talking about friction in a mixed lubrication regime, the normal load applied to the
contact is distributed between two forces, hydrodynamic force and asperity contact force.
• Hydrodynamic force:
Hydrodynamic force is the force applied by a fluid in motion on a solid object (FH).
• Asperities contact force:
Asperities contact force is the force that occur when microscopic roughness (asperities)
of two surfaces come into contact (FC).

2.3
The Reynolds Equation
When having a mixed lubrication regime, a pressure distribution will occur between the
seal and the shaft. In this section, the relation between the pressure distribution and the lubri-
cant film will be explained. This relation can be found by using the Reynolds equation. The
Reynolds equation is the governing equation that describes the pressure distribution in a lubri-
cant film. It is a partial differential equation that combines mass conservation and momentum
conservation into a single equation. The equation was first derived in 1886, and the classical
Reynolds equation could describe the pressure distribution in almost any fluid film bearing and
seal [11].
Figure 6 shows the interaction between the fluid film, the rotating shaft, and the seal lip.
The seal maintains contact with the rotary shaft, creating a barrier between the pressurised oil
and atmospheric air. The shaft rotates with a constant angular velocity and drags the fluid along
its surface, generating a thin lubricant film between the lip and the shaft. This film generates a
pressure distribution which is governed by the Reynolds equation.

2.3.1
The Flow Factor Method
Another assumption in the original derivation of the Reynolds equation is that the inter-
acting surfaces are considered to be ”smooth”. This assumption applies for relatively thick
lubricant films, where the film thickness is much thicker than the surface roughness (h ≫Ra).
In such cases, the effects of surface irregularities on the pressure distribution are negligible, and
the lubricant flow can be modelled as occurring between idealised, smooth surfaces.
However, as the operating film thickness decreases and approaches the order of the surface
roughness (h ≈Ra), the validity of this assumption becomes questionable. Under these condi-
tions, the lubricant flow is significantly influenced by the surface topography, and the interaction
between asperities on the two surfaces can no longer be ignored.
To account for the effect of roughness on the pressure, the Reynolds equation has been
modified by Patir et al. [14]. The modified equation can be seen in Equation 10. This equation
is also known as the Average Reynolds Equation.

2.5
Contact Analysis
In order to analyse the mixed lubrication regime and the pressure distribution, the interac-
tion between the shaft and the seal has to be studied. In order to do so, a contact model has to be
made. In this thesis, two different contact models have been used. The first was a static contact
model, which determined the pressure distribution at the interface during the assembly process.
The second is an asperity contact model, which accounts for the interaction of microscopic sur-
face roughness (asperities) to evaluate the contact mechanics and pressure distribution under
operating conditions.
2.5.1
Static Contact Model
For the static contact model, contact pair was used in COMSOL Multiphysics® (COM-
SOL). COMSOL is a simulation software that is widely used to simulate physical and engineer-
ing problems, just like ANSYS, ABAQUS, etc [16].
When modelling contact, it is important to specify the interaction between two boundaries,
especially their contact state and the forces involved. For the static contact analysis, the contact
between the shaft (source boundary) and the seal (destination boundary) is described using
two fundamental quantities, this are the gap function, gn, and the contact pressure, Tn. The
gap function can be defined as the distance between the boundaries of the source Γsrc and the
destination Γdst, as shown in Figure 10. In order to find this distance, a search is performed
normal to the destination boundary, as illustrated with the ndst vector in Figure 10. When
the boundaries come into contact, gn = 0, some reaction force will be developed. This is the
contact pressure. The contact pressure ensures that the gap function remains non-negative,
thereby preventing surface penetration or overclosure. In this context, static contact pressure is
the primary focus, and thus, friction and adhesion are neglected [17].

When using the Augmented Lagrangian method, COMSOL will first calculate the dis-
placement of the two bodies in contact and then, in a separate step, calculate the contact pressure
Tn, and the friction force Tt. After this, it will check if the solutions match the actual physical
behaviour by fulfilling a convergence criterion. Because the method splits the problem into parts

and uses iterations, it allows for better adjustments of the contact pressure and friction forces.
This makes the Augmented Lagrangian method robust to handle complex problems. However,
it is a more expensive method because it has a higher computational cost and will require more
fine-tuning to converge.
On the other hand, the penalty method is computationally cheaper, as it does not add extra
degrees of freedom. Here, the contact pressure and friction force are calculated directly from
the displacements and the so-called penalty stiffness. This penalty stiffness acts like a virtual
spring preventing the two bodies to penetrate each other. This penalty factor can be changed.
By increasing the penalty factor, the spring stiffens. The higher the penalty factor is, the more
accurate the contact solution will be because the bodies will not penetrate each other as much.
However, suppose the penalty factor is set too high, the system will become numerically un-
stable, meaning that the solver might have trouble finding a solution or producing inaccurate
results.

2.5.2
Asperity Contact Model
For the asperity part, the surface interaction of the two bodies has to be looked at. When
two bodies with rough surfaces are subjected to a force and come in contact with each other,
they start to deform. When they deform, it is difficult to identify the actual contact points and
the average separation between the two bodies, as they do not make contact uniformly due
to their roughness. This complexity has prompted the development of various theories. In
1882, Heinrich Hertz formulated the Hertz contact mechanics theory, a fundamental theory on
this topic. The Hertz contact mechanics theory, which assumes that all surface asperities are
spherical bumps of equal height, predicts a non-linear relationship between the contact area and
the applied force, as shown in Figure 11(a).
However, the estimation of the contact area, which is based on the unknown radius of the
asperities, has proven to be inaccurate. Therefore, in 1966, an alternative approach came to life,
known as the Greenwood and Williamson contact model (GW model), which offers a practical
modification to the Hertz model. This model introduces random variations in the heights of
the asperities while still treating them as spherical with equal radii. Due to its practicality,
the GW model has become one of the field’s most widely used contact models. The GW model
considers a plane and a nominally flat surface covered with many asperities. Figure 11(b) shows
a schematic of the contact of rough surfaces according to GW. The model takes the mean level
of the rough surface as a reference plane, with z representing the heights of the asperities above
this plane. The model assumes that all asperities on the rough surface share the same radius, β.
However, their heights vary randomly between z and z+dz above the reference plane, following
a particular probability distribution φ(z)dz. When the surface comes into contact, the asperities
on the rough surface begin to interact with the smooth surface, resulting in pressure, pa, caused
by the deformation of the asperities [19][20]. This pressure is defined as:

However, counting potential local maxima between the acquired measurement points is risky.
These challenges are inherent in 1D measurements. They include sampling limitations, which
arise when the sampling resolution is too low to capture fine surface features. Another issue is
misrepresentation, where insufficient resolution distorts high-frequency surface features. Ad-

length refers to the distance over which the surface roughness profile is measured and averaged

standards specifying the appropriate cut-off length.

paper, a cut-off length of 25 µm was used in their simulation, which produced reliable results.

2.6
Material Properties
To compute the behaviour of the seals, the material properties have to be determined. In
this section the material properties of the seal will be explained.
As described in the Introduction, two seal types need to be modelled: type R and type
BSB.
The type R seal, shown in Figure 14, is one of the most commonly used designs. Its
rubber outer layer ensures a secure seal within the housing bore, even when surface roughness
increases or thermal expansion occurs. This makes the type R seal highly reliable and the go-to
choice for rotating shafts with a slight pressure difference of up to 1 bar [29].
The BSB seal differs significantly from the standard oil seal due to its unique design, as
seen in Figure 14. Its metal supports are optimised for the sealing lip, resulting in excellent
sealing. In contrast to type R, the BSB can withstand higher pressure, up to 15 bar, depending
on the rotational speed and temperature. In a specific case, it can even withstand up to 80 bar
[30].
The materials used for these seals are Fluoro-Elastomer (FKM) and Nitrile Butadiene Rub-
ber (NBR).
FKM, also known as VITON™, is used in both type R and BSB seals. It is known for
its resistance to a wide range of chemicals, heat, and oil. However, it has limited resistance to
methanol, steam, and highly polar fluids. Additionally, FKM has a hardness of 75 IRHD [31].
NBR is primarily used in type R seals. It offers excellent resistance to oils, gasoline, and
chemicals due to its acrylonitrile content. However, NBR has poor weathering resistance and is
unsuitable for outdoor applications. Additionally, it has a hardness of 70 IRHD [32].

2.6.1
Nonlinear Materials Model
As described in the previous section, FKM and NBR are highly nonlinear materials due to
their rubber-like behaviour. In order to simulate their behaviour, a nonlinear material model has
to be used. Firstly, nonlinear materials can be characterised by a nonlinear relationship between
stress and strain, equivalent to the relationship between force and displacement.
There are several nonlinear material, such as nonlinear elastic, bi-linear elasto-plastic,
multi-linear plastic, hyperelastic, viscoelastic, creep etc. In this case, FKM and NBR fall into
the hyperelastic material group. Hyperelastic material differs from conventional elastic mate-
rial because it can sustain and recover from extensive elastic deformations without permanent
damage. In order to find the relation between stress and strain, a strain energy density function
is used in hyperelastic material. Various hyperelastic models, such as Neo-Hookean, Arruda-
Boyce, Ogden, Mooney-Rivlin, etc., can be used. The most commonly used models are the
Neo-Hookean and the Mooney-Rivlin models when modelling hyperelastic materials [33].
The Neo-Hookean model (NH) is a molecular-based theoretical approach that is considered
one of the fundamental hyperelastic models available. This model characterises a material’s
hyperelastic behaviour using a single independent material parameter. In contrast, the Mooney-
Rivlin model (MR) is one of the most frequently used models for analysing the nonlinear elastic
characteristics of materials. The order of the Mooney-Rivlin model can be modified according
to the complexity of the material’s stress-strain behaviour.

single, uniform sliding, which is the case for 60 rpm, small folds or buckles form at the leading
edge of the contact area and then pass across the surface. This buckling and unbuckling action
increases the energy required to maintain sliding, which contributes to the high friction force
and additionally causes the friction force to vary over time [35] [36].

case. Here, the 120 rpm curves continuously increase with time and are very stable throughout
the test, reaching a steady state of 0.91. On the other hand, the 60 rpm curves continue to
increase and show unstable behaviour, reaching a coefficient of friction of 1.15.

3.3
Test Protocol
In order to minimise the risk of errors or biases influencing the outcomes of the experi-
ments, a test protocol was made and applied across all experiments. The test protocol can be
seen below:
1. Turn on the sensors and the computer.
2. Adjust the pressure in the hydraulic system using the pressure valve.
3. Power on the test setup.
4. Increase the pressure in the system using the LOGO! interface.
5. Check if the desired pressure is achieved.
6. Readjust the pressure valve if the desired pressure is not reached.
7. Once the desired pressure is achieved, increase the speed on the shaft using LOGO!.
8. Verify that the pressure remains stable.
9. If the pressure is unstable, readjust the pressure valve again.
10. If the pressure is stable, begin the measurement in DAQami.
11. Allow the experiment to run for 5 minutes.
12. After 5 minutes, stop the experiment.
13. Turn off the test setup.
14. Release the pressure from the hydraulic system.
15. Turn off the sensors and the motor.
16. Replace the seal and check for damages.
17. The system is now ready for the next experiment.

Numerical Model
In this section, the numerical model will be explained.
5.1
Solution Process
The FEA solution process is multistage, each dedicated to solving a specific part of the
problem. A computational flow chart for the numerical model is presented in Figure 35. The
flow chart implements two types of coupled solutions algorithms: a finite element analysis of
the deformation and a statistical representation of the contact model. The flow chart follows a
segregated loading where convergence is reached in each loading step.
The process begins with input parameters, where all necessary data for the model, such
as material properties, system pressure, and other relevant information, are provided. Next is
the assembly step, where the shaft is moved in the radial direction toward the seal to obtain the
initial contact between the seal and the shaft and form the interference fit. The process then
checks whether the contact pressure has converged. If it has not stabilised, the system iterates
back to refine and recompute the pressure distribution. If it converges, the process proceeds to
the pressurising stage, where the system is at the desired pressure to simulate operational condi-
tions. However, the process also loops back through the assembly step to ensure all parameters
are properly updated and accounted for.
In the next stage, the asperity contact model and the fluid mechanics are computed simulta-
neously. The asperity contact model addresses the contact between microscopic surface peaks
and calculates the contact pressure distribution. At the same time, the fluid mechanics stage
uses the Reynolds equation to simulate the fluid flow within the model. The model then per-
forms a convergence check for the film thickness to ensure it has stabilised. If the film thickness
has not converged, the process loops back for further adjustments and iterations. Once the film
thickness has converged, the model outputs the final results, including pressure distributions,
deformations, and other performance metrics.

In order to compute the solution, it is necessary to assemble and solve a set of equations
over all of the elements of the model, where the solution to these equations approximates the
proper solution to the partial differential equation. This equation within each element is also
called shape function and can be of different order. The cubic serendipity shape function was
used for the type R seal, where the shape function has a displacement that varies quadratically
within the element. However, when using the cubic serendipity shape function for the BSB seal,
the elements would overlap each other at point D in Figure 36 when reaching high pressure,
resulting in an undefined variable. In order to get around it, the shape function was reduced to
first-order linear discretization. Lowering the discretization will lead to a model that requires
fewer computational resources but will have lower accuracy and result in a stiffer response,
thereby, it will be more difficult for the elements to overlap.

Coefficient of Friction
In the material properties table, Table 2, it was noted that the coefficient of friction ranges
from 0.93 to 1.2 for NBR and from 0.91 to 1.15 for FKM. For the numerical model, it is assumed
that the seal does not experience stick-slip motion during operation. Stick-slip is a phenomenon
where two surfaces in contact alternately stick together and then slip apart due to fluctuating
frictional forces. When stick-slip occurs, the coefficient of friction can reach as high as 1.2 in
cases involving rubber against steel. Consequently, the coefficient of friction is set at 0.93 for
NBR and 0.91 for FKM.

6.1
Limitations of Contact Model
After some trial and error, it was concluded that the GW model had several limitations,
particularly when the load was increased to a sufficiently high value, as described by Rodi˜no et
al., [27]. When increasing the load, the standardised separation value, H, approached a negative
value, leading to an unstable and incorrect solution. This instability arises because the input pa-
rameters, including Young’s modulus, are so low that the resulting contact pressure approaches
zero. Furthermore, under high load conditions, the narrowing of the contact boundary predicts
an infinite number of contacts rather than a decrease. Consequently, the model fails to provide
a realistic depiction of the contact behaviour under such conditions. Therefore, the upcoming
results are only computed with the Bush contact model.

7.1
Pressure Variations
The results show that the hydraulic system was not entirely leak-proof. By examining the
pressure drop in Figure 25, it is observed that the average pressure drop is approximately 1 bar
across all tested conditions, 5 bar, 10 bar, and for both CW and CCW directions. In order to
understand the relationship between volume change and the leakage mechanism, the system
was analysed using the following equation:

where ∆p is the pressure change, K is the bulk modulus of elasticity of the fluid, ∆V is the
volume change of the fluid, and V0 is the initial volume of the fluid. For this analysis, the
initial volume of the fluid was computed to be approximately 3.2 L, and the bulk modulus of
elasticity was assumed to be 1 GPa [41]. The results indicate that a pressure decrease of 1 bar
corresponds to a volume change of approximately 0.3 mL, equivalent to roughly six droplets
of oil [42]. Detecting such a small quantity of oil leakage is extremely challenging. This
analysis suggests that the observed pressure drop primarily comes from small undetectable
leaks or internal system compliance rather than significant oil loss. Additional factors such as
micro-seepage through seals, minor component deformities, or trapped air bubbles could also
contribute to the pressure loss. When analysing the pressure increase in Figure 31, the pressure
rise was approximately 2.5 bar and 3.3 bar for the 5 bar and 10 bar cases, respectively. This
increase could be attributed to thermal effects, such as friction between the seal and the shaft.
At high speeds, friction generates heat, potentially causing localized thermal expansion of the
fluid and contributing to a rise in pressure. Although the temperature sensor recorded only a
0.2°C increase, the temperature at the contact point may be significantly higher. By using the
Equation 42, the pressure increase due to temperature changes was estimated:

0.44°C, respectively. The increase in pressure could also result from the so-called pumping
effect, a phenomenon commonly observed in rotating systems involving fluid dynamics. The
pumping effect occurs when rotational motion induces fluid movement, generating localized

also change from boundary lubrication to hydrodynamic lubrication.
For the fluid component, either the rotational speed should be reduced or the film thickness
should be increased. However, reducing the rotational speed may not be feasible for replicating
realistic operating conditions. Alternatively, increasing the film thickness may require incor-
poration of the Couette term into the Reynolds equation to account for its role. The modified
results, which neglect the fluid component, align closely with the experimental data. This raises
questions about the necessity of accounting for the fluid contribution when the film thickness is
small.

7.4
Roughness
The steel shaft used in the block-on-wheel experiment had a surface roughness of Ra =
0.4 µm, while the shaft at HS had a roughness of Ra = 0.9 µm. This raises the question of
the significance of surface roughness on the coefficient of friction. According to Pinedo et al.,
[45], roughness can significantly impact the coefficient of friction. Their study, which tested
two types of rubber material on surfaces with varying roughness, showed that initial friction
increases due to more surface asperities interacting with the elastomer. Over time, however, the
coefficient of friction stabilises as asperities wear down, resulting in more consistent contact.
At higher sliding velocities, the influence of surface roughness on the coefficient of friction
decreases because thermal effects become more pronounced than at lower velocities. Increased
sliding speeds generate more frictional heat, which raises the contact temperature and softens
the elastomer. As the material becomes more flexible compared to its rigidity at lower tempera-
tures, the influence of surface roughness diminishes. However, in this thesis, the effect of shaft
roughness on the coefficient of friction was neglected, as its impact under high-speed conditions
is assumed to be minimal.

Conclusion
This thesis aimed to investigate the tribological properties of radial shaft seals under dif-
ferent pressure loads, especially focusing on type R and type BSB seals. The study aimed to
evaluate their performance in pressurised applications through both experimental testing and
numerical modelling. However, due to limitations within the hydraulic system, the experimen-
tal analysis was limited to the BSB seal.
A series of experiments were conducted using HS’s tribological testing machine. The BSB
seal was tested under three different pressure loads, 0, 5, and 10 bars, at 150 and 1000 rpm
rotational speeds for 5 minutes in both CW and CCW directions. At 150 rpm, the pressure
gradually decreased for both the 5 and 10 bar cases, prompting modifications to the hydraulic
system. At 1000 rpm, pressure increases due to thermal expansion (2.5 bar for a 0.3°C rise) and
the pumping effect (0.49 bar) were observed. Frictional torque increased with both rotational
speed and pressure, peaking at higher speeds with noticeable instability and wear at 10 bar.
The frictional torque was found to be significantly influenced by both pressure and rota-
tional speed. The friction torque increased with both increased pressure and rotational speed.
At 150 rpm, the torque remained stable across all pressure levels in the CCW direction but
showed instability at 10 bars in the CW direction due to wear on the seal lip. In contrast, at
1000 rpm, torque values increased significantly, especially at higher pressures, with noticeable
wear effects leading to increased instability.
For the numerical model, two seal designs were modelled with two different materials. For
the type R seal, two materials were used: FKM and NBR. The FKM demonstrated superior
performance to NBR due to its higher stiffness, which reduced displacement and maintained
higher contact pressures. While both seals showed a reduction in radial force when fluid was
introduced to the system, FKM exhibited smaller reductions, indicating a more consistent seal-
ing capability. Additionally, FKM achieved a minimum film thickness of 0.13 µm at 1.5 bar,
compared to NBR’s 0.17 µm, and had lower leakage rates, with a maximum of 0.00063 mm³/s
versus NBR’s 0.0014 mm³/s.
The BSB seal, which was designed for higher pressure, showed distinct characteristics,
including increased contact pressure due to interaction with a metal insert at pressures exceeding
1 bar. The static contact pressure for the BSB seal peaked at 1.72 MPa at 10 bar, while fluid
lift effects significantly reduced the asperity pressure. The film thickness was reduced with
increasing pressure, reaching 0.15 µm at 10 bar, which correlated with a rise in friction torque
from 0.59 Nmm/rpm at 0.5 bar to 1.71 Nmm/rpm at 10 bar. The BSB seal maintained low
leakage rates, with an average of 0.0015 mm³/s at 5 bar.
However, comparing the numerical and experimental results revealed significant overesti-
mations in frictional torque, with deviations of up to 505%. The asperity contact contributed
57% of the total torque, with fluid shear forces accounting for 43%. Reducing the coefficient
of friction from 0.91 to 0.25 lowered the asperity contact torque to 33%, aligning numerical
results more closely with experimental data. Incorporating the Couette term into the Reynolds
equation could further refine the role of film thickness in reducing fluid shear effects.

Future Work
There is always room for improvement. One limitation of this thesis was the inability to
test the type R seal due to the experimental setup minimum pressure threshold. Future work
should focus on modifying the setup to include the testing of type R seals at lower pressures
and to be able to measure the temperature at the contact zone.
The experimental work observed thermal effects, particularly at higher speeds, resulting in
higher pressure. Further studies should incorporate detailed thermal analyses to evaluate their
impact on the lubrication.
Moreover, extending the numerical model by including the shaft’s tangential velocity and
more complex variables, such as shaft misalignment, could improve the predictive accuracy of
the results.
