Abstract
The increasing integration of renewable energy sources and distributed generation presents
significant challenges for modern power systems. Ensuring the stability and reliability of
these systems requires advanced optimization techniques. This thesis develops a frame-
work for Transient Stability Constrained Optimal Power Flow (TSC-OPF) to address these
challenges. The primary contributions are twofold: first, formulating a general notation for
the TSC-OPF problem, which accommodates disturbances using stationary distributions
in dynamic constraints; second, developing modular, extendable, and reusable software
tools for simulation and further research.
The proposed TSC-OPF framework integrates dynamic constraints to ensure system sta-
bility under transient conditions, enhancing reliability and optimizing operational costs.
Numerical experiments on a modified IEEE 9-bus system demonstrate the effectiveness
of the methods, emphasizing the importance of considering dynamic constraints and
stochastic elements in power flow optimization. Future work includes implementing TSC-
OPF to incorporate constraint tightening, refining stability assessment methods, exploring
multi-stage optimization, multiple shooting methods, and enhancing parameter estimation
techniques.
This thesis successfully provides a robust TSC-OPF framework, offering significant poten-
tial for future research and practical applications in optimizing power system performance
amidst increasing complexity and uncertainty. While the development of modular and
robust software tools has been partly successful, the implementation of handling distur-
bances remains incomplete, presenting a key area for future work.

Introduction
The increasing integration of renewable energy sources, the proliferation of distributed
generation, and the evolving demands of modern energy systems have led to the emer-
gence of the Smart Grid—a highly sophisticated, responsive, and efficient electricity net-
work. Figure 1.1 provides an overview of the different components in a smart grid’s trans-
mission network.
Figure 1.1: Overview of the different components in a transmision network
Component (A) represents the connection to the wider power grid. Component (B) de-
notes a traditional power generator, such as those using gas or coal, which operate in-
dependently of control mechanisms. Component (C) illustrates a power generator that
can be fully controlled, allowing for adjustments in output as needed to balance the grid.
Component (D) signifies renewable energy sources, which are inherently stochastic due
to their dependence on natural conditions; examples include solar panels and wind tur-
bines. Component (E) depicts deterministic power consumption, such as industrial fa-
cilities with predictable energy usage patterns. Component (F) represents deterministic
power consumption that can be controlled through mechanisms like dynamic pricing, en-
couraging users to adjust their consumption based on price signals. Finally, component
(G) refers to stochastic consumers, including cities and residential areas, where energy
usage patterns are variable and less predictable.
Central to the successful operation of a Smart Grid is the ability to manage and optimize
power flow in a manner that is both reliable and economically efficient. This challenge is
significantly compounded by the inherent uncertainties associated with renewable energy
generation, load demands, and other dynamic system variables. Ensuring the stability
and reliability of such a complex system requires advanced optimization techniques that
can handle these uncertainties and dynamic behaviors effectively.

1.1
Literature Review
The classic optimal power flow is well understood. It optimizes a cost function based
on the power output of the generators subject to constraints that ensure each component
operates within some limit [10]. However, this relatively simple optimization problem does
not account for the transient stability. Transient stability in power systems refers to the
ability of the system to maintain synchronism when subjected to a transient disturbance,
such as a short circuit or a sudden loss of a generation unit. This stability is crucial for
ensuring the reliable operation of power systems, particularly as they become increasingly
complex with the integration of renewable energy sources. Transient Stability Constrained
Optimal Power Flow (TSC-OPF) is a critical tool used to optimize the operation of power
systems while ensuring their transient stability. [4]
Assessing whether a power system is transiently stable involves several methodologies,
each with its unique approach and formula. The Center of Inertia (COI) method is used to
assess the overall stability of a power system by examining the behavior of the system’s
aggregate inertia. The COI is calculated using the following formula:

on the principle that for a system to remain stable, the accelerating area must equal the
decelerating area on a power-angle curve. Classical EAC considers a single-machine
infinite-bus system, determining stability by comparing the areas under the power-angle
curve before and after a disturbance. If the areas are equal, the system is considered sta-
ble. The EEAC generalizes this principle to multi-machine systems by transforming the
problem into an equivalent single-machine problem, aggregating the effects of multiple
machines and disturbances into a single equivalent machine model, allowing for a more
comprehensive assessment of stability in complex power systems. [1]
TSC-OPF approaches can be broadly classified into conventional methods and data-
driven methods. One conventional approach involves linearizing the dynamics of the
power system to simplify the stability analysis. [3]. This method, however, does not ac-
count for stochastic variations and non-linearities in the system. Another approach using
a detailed dynamic model of the power system is proposed in [9]. It uses the EEAC for
stability analysis, which, while more accurate than linear models, still does not consider
stochastic factors. There has also been efforts to To address these limitations, a prob-
abilistic framework using Monte Carlo simulations has been proposed in [7], accounting
for uncertainties and stochastic variations in the system.
==PAGE:9==
Data-driven approaches leverage machine learning techniques to improve the efficiency
and accuracy of TSC-OPF. Machine learning models, have been developed that can han-
dle large datasets and provide real-time stability assessments. By the nature of machine
learning, these all take stochastic variables into account.
Both conventional and data-driven approaches to TSC-OPF have their advantages and
disadvantages. Conventional methods are computationally accurate and have well-established
methodologies and a well-understood theoretical basis, but they require complex mathe-
matical derivations and extensive calculations, which may not handle large-scale systems
efficiently. Data-driven methods excel at real-time computation, are capable of manag-
ing large datasets, adapt to real-time data, and can consider stochastic factors, but they
require large amounts of data for training, need proper offline training time, and may lack
transparency in decision-making processes.
The increasing penetration of renewable energy sources into the power grid necessitates
the development of efficient methods for TSC-OPF. These methods must be capable of
handling the variability and uncertainty associated with renewable generation to ensure
the stability and reliability of the power system. Both conventional and data-driven ap-
proaches offer valuable tools for ensuring transient stability, though each has its strengths
and weaknesses. In [1], It has been proposed, that future research should focus on de-
veloping methods that combine the robustness of conventional techniques with the adapt-
ability of data-driven approaches to effectively manage a complex power system with a
high influx of renewable energy sources.

Background
2.1
Overview
A power grid can be elegantly represented as a mathematical weighted graph, where
nodes represent buses (points where lines connect, including loads, generation, or both)
and edges represent transmission lines or transformers. Each node in this graph is char-
acterized by four crucial variables: Voltage Magnitude (Vm), Voltage Angle (φ), Active
Power (P), and Reactive Power (Q). Furthermore, each edge in the graph is charac-
terized by an ’Equivalent Admittance,’ derived from the line’s resistance, reactance, and
susceptance, reflecting how easily current flows through the line. The interplay of these
variables is governed by the power flow equations, a set of nonlinear algebraic equa-
tions ensuring the balance between generated and consumed power while considering
transmission losses. These equations are foundational and will be detailed further in sec-
tion 3.1. The derivation and explanation of these equations will mainly follow [10].
In addition to these static variables, the dynamic behavior of generators is crucial. Specif-
ically, the generator frequencies, ωg, must be equal for the power grid to remain stable.
The synchronization of generator frequencies is governed by the swing equations, which
describe the rotor angle changes over time due to power imbalances. This idea behind
this model will be explored in section 2.5, where as a specific implementation and general
notation of the model will be presented section 3.2. These explanations and models will
closely follow the derivation from [6].
Furthermore, some buses exhibit stochastic behavior, such as wind turbines and con-
sumer loads, introducing variability and uncertainty into the system. The approach to
modeling these uncertainties will be discussed in section 3.3.
Bringing these elements together, we can formulate an optimization problem, by allowing
some of the buses to be controllable. Initially, we consider static optimization of power
flow, followed by incorporating dynamic constraints to account for generator synchroniza-
tion. Finally, we extend the optimization to include both dynamics and stochastic ele-
ments, creating a robust model that reflects real-world complexities. The formulation this
optimization problem will be detailed in section 3.4.
This sets the stage for a deeper exploration of the theoretical foundations and practical
challenges in optimizing power systems, presented in the subsequent sections.
2.2
Buses
In the analysis of power systems, buses are fundamental components where multiple
electrical elements such as generators, loads, and transmission lines are interconnected.
In AC power systems, we are particularly interested in two main characteristics at each
bus: Voltage and Power.
The voltage at a bus can be represented as a complex number:
V = Vmejθ
where Vm is the voltage magnitude, θ is the voltage angle relative to a reference bus and j
denotes the imaginary unit. The voltage magnitude (Vm) represents the absolute value of
the voltage at a bus, crucial for maintaining the stability of the power system by ensuring

that voltage levels remain within acceptable limits. The voltage angle (θ) is important for
determining the direction and magnitude of power flows in the network.
The power at a bus is also expressed as a complex number, known as apparent power:
S = P + jQ
where P is the active (real) power and Q is the reactive power. Active power (P), also
known as real power, is the actual power consumed by loads or supplied by generators.
It is responsible for the actual work done in the system, such as lighting, heating, and run-
ning motors. Reactive power (Q) is the power that oscillates between the source and the
load. It is necessary for maintaining the voltage levels required for the efficient operation
of the power system, but it does not perform any actual work.
These complex representations provide a comprehensive view of the electrical state at
each bus, capturing both the magnitude and phase relationships as well as the interplay
between active and reactive power.

2.6
Software Design
Good software design aims to create software that is extendable, testable, and readable.
Correctness ensures the software behaves as expected, fulfilling requirements and han-
dling errors gracefully. Extendability allows the software to grow and adapt over time.
Testability makes it easier to verify functionality and detect bugs. Readability ensures the
code is easily understood by developers, facilitating maintenance and collaboration. By
adhering to principles like encapsulation, separation of concerns, and composition, we
build systems that meet these goals, providing lasting value and maintaining high-quality
standards. These principles also contribute to making the code more modular, which
enhances understandability, reusability, and parallel development.
==PAGE:16==
2.6.1
Encapsulation
Encapsulation is the practice of bundling data and methods that operate on that data
within a single unit, typically a class, while restricting direct access to some of the object’s
components to protect the integrity of the data. This principle helps in maintaining data
integrity and hiding the internal implementation details.
Consider a BankAccount class. In the wrong way, direct modification of the balance
attribute is allowed, which can lead to unintended consequences.
class BankAccount:
def __init__(self, owner, balance):
self.owner = owner
self.balance = balance
# Public attribute
account = BankAccount("Alice", 1000)
account.balance -= 100
# Directly modifying the balance
In this approach, anyone can change the balance directly, potentially causing issues like
withdrawing more money than available. This violates the principle of encapsulation, lead-
ing to data integrity problems.
To fix this, we encapsulate the balance attribute and provide public methods for controlled
access and modification.
class BankAccount:
def __init__(self, owner, balance):
self.owner = owner
self.__balance = balance
# Private attribute
def deposit(self, amount):
if amount > 0:
self.__balance += amount
def withdraw(self, amount):
if 0 < amount <= self.__balance:
self.__balance -= amount
else:
raise ValueError("Insufficient funds or invalid amount")
def get_balance(self):
return self.__balance
account = BankAccount("Alice", 1000)
account.deposit(200)
account.withdraw(100)
print(account.get_balance())
# Correct way to access the balance
This approach ensures data integrity by making the balance attribute private and providing
methods to manipulate it safely, preventing invalid operations. Encapsulation protects the
internal state and allows for better control over how data is accessed and modified.

2.6.3
Composition
Composition is the principle of building complex systems by combining simple objects
or functions rather than inheriting from classes. This approach promotes flexibility and
reusability by allowing different components to be composed together to form new behav-
iors.
Consider a Car class that might need to have different capabilities like Engine and Transmission.
The wrong way is to use inheritance for adding these capabilities, which can lead to a rigid

3.1
Power Flow Equations
In this section, we formulate the power flow equations described in section 2.4 within
the context of an optimal control problem. From eq. (2.9), we have N complex, non-
linear equations ensuring the power balance in the grid. By splitting these equations into
their real and imaginary parts, we obtain 2N real equations. However, as explained in
section 2.1, each bus has four associated variables. Therefore, the set of equations is
under-determined with 2N degrees of freedom. To resolve this, we specify two variables
at each bus and determine the other two using the power flow equations. Typically, this
is done by introducing three types of buses:
• Generator Buses (PV Buses): At these buses, the voltage magnitude (Vm) and
active power (P) are specified. These are used to model ordinary synchronous
generators such as coal or gas power plants.
• Load Buses (PQ Buses): At load buses, the active power (P) and reactive power
(Q) are specified based on the load demand. These typically model consumers
drawing power from the grid.
• Reference Bus (Slack Bus): Also known as the swing bus, the reference bus
serves as the reference point for voltage angles in the system. At this bus, the
voltage magnitude (Vm) and voltage angle (φ) are specified. This typically models
the connection to the greater power grid.
With these specifications, we obtain a fully determined system of equations. Note that it is
in these specified variables that we can model uncertainty and introduce control variables.
We can model this more generally by introducing a column vector z ∈hbbR4N, containing
all the associated variables at each bus. We call this the algebraic variables. With this
vector, the power flow equations can be formulated as:
g(z, p) = 0,
(3.1)
where g is the real and imaginary parts of eq. (2.9) stacked on top of each other, and p
is the parameter vector including known parameters of the grid, such as line admittance.

(3.2)

vector function of u and d containing the specified values for each bus. u is a vector repre-

3.4.3
Constraint tightening using covariance
Up until now, the optimization algorithms have not accounted for stochastic variables. We
are primarily interested in how the stochastic variables affect the stability measure, s. In
fig. 3.1 a simple way of doing this is illustrated. The idea is to construct a confidence
interval using the covariance matrix of its distribution, and making sure the whole confi-
dence interval is within the tolerance instead of just a single realization. In section 3.3 we
derive exactly how to approximate the covariance matrix of s. By choosing appropriate
linearization points, we can calculate the stationary distribution of s beforehand. This is
both a very simple and computationally cheap way of incorporating stochastic.

Future Work
The most important future work is to implement the TSC-OPF to incorporate constraint
tightening as explained in Section section 3.4.3, allowing for the effective handling of
stochastic variables. Performing numeric experiments to validate this approach will be
crucial for demonstrating the practical applicability and effectiveness of incorporating re-
newables as stochastic variables using the stationary distribution of constraints.
Future work could also investigate refining the methods for assessing system stability.
The current approach, while effective, is somewhat crude. Future research could involve
a thorough analysis comparing this stability metric with other advanced methods, such as
probabilistic approaches, Lyapunov-based methods, and energy function analysis. De-
veloping new metrics and indices tailored to the specific characteristics of modern power
systems with high renewable penetration will provide deeper insights and more reliable
criteria for ensuring stability.
Another promising direction is exploring the implementation of the multiple shooting method
for handling dynamic constraints. This advanced approach involves dividing the time
horizon into smaller intervals and solving them simultaneously, which can significantly
enhance the handling of dynamic constraints in Stochastic Optimal Power Flow (SOPF)
models. Developing and implementing multiple shooting algorithms could provide a more
accurate and computationally efficient solution to the SOPF problem.
Future work could also investigate multi-stage optimization, where the decision-making
process is broken into multiple stages representing different time horizons or levels of
detail. This approach allows for more refined and adaptive optimization strategies, con-
sidering the evolving nature of power systems and incorporating feedback mechanisms
to improve robustness and efficiency. Implementing multi-stage optimization can help
manage uncertainties and dynamic changes more effectively, ensuring better long-term
performance and resilience of power systems.
Expanding the scope of research to include a variety of dynamic models will further sup-
port these efforts by providing a more comprehensive understanding of power system
behavior under different conditions. Future studies could explore the integration of ad-
ditional dynamic models, such as detailed models of renewable energy sources, load
dynamics, and advanced generator models. This integration will allow for a more holistic
analysis and optimization, considering a wider range of factors and interactions within the
power system.
To support these optimization techniques, improving parameter estimation is crucial for
the reliable operation of power systems. Future research could focus on enhancing es-
timation techniques for key parameters such as inertia constant (H), damping coefficient
(D), and reactance (xd). Additionally, refining methods for estimating other system pa-
rameters (p) will enhance the model’s accuracy. Employing advanced statistical methods,
machine learning algorithms, and real-time data integration can achieve more precise and
adaptive parameter estimation, leading to better system performance and stability.
By addressing these areas in future research, we can develop more sophisticated, reli-
able, and efficient methods for managing and optimizing power systems, ultimately con-
tributing to the advancement of smart grid technologies and sustainable energy solutions.

Conclusion
This thesis has developed a comprehensive framework for Transient Stability Constrained
Optimal Power Flow (TSC-OPF) to address the challenges of modern power systems with
integrated renewable energy sources. The main contributions are twofold: first, formu-
lating a simple method to incorporate renewables as stochastic variables by using the
stationary distribution of constraints; second, developing modular software tools for fur-
ther research.
By incorporating dynamic constraints, the proposed framework ensures system stability
under transient conditions, enhancing reliability and optimizing operational costs. Numeric
experiments on a modified IEEE 9-bus system demonstrate the effectiveness of these
methods, emphasizing the importance of dynamic constraints and stochastic elements in
power flow optimization.
Future work should focus on implementing the stochastic variables into the provided soft-
ware solution and conducting numeric experiments to validate this approach. Addition-
ally, exploring multi-stage optimization, multiple shooting methods, and diverse dynamic
models will further enhance TSC-OPF capabilities and contribute to advancing smart grid
technologies.
In summary, this thesis provides a robust framework for TSC-OPF, offering significant
potential for future research and practical applications in optimizing power system perfor-
mance amidst increasing complexity and uncertainty.

1. Bus: The bus number (an identifier for each bus in the system).
2. Type: The type of bus:
• 1 = PQ bus (load bus)
• 2 = PV bus (generator bus)
• 3 = Slack bus (reference bus)
3. Pd: Real power demand (load) at the bus (in MW).
4. Qd: Reactive power demand (load) at the bus (in MVar).
5. Gs: Shunt conductance (in MW demanded at the bus).
6. Bs: Shunt susceptance (in MVar injected at the bus).
7. Area: Area number (used for dividing the system into areas).
8. Vm: Voltage magnitude (in per unit, p.u.).
9. Va: Voltage angle (in degrees).
10. BaseKV: Base voltage level (in kV).
11. Zone: Zone number (used for dividing the system into zones).
12. Vmax: Maximum voltage magnitude (in p.u.).
13. Vmin: Minimum voltage magnitude (in p.u.).
The generator data table includes the following variables:
1. Bus: The bus number where the generator is connected.
2. Pg: Real power output of the generator (in MW).
3. Qg: Reactive power output of the generator (in MVar).

The branch data table includes the following variables:
1. From Bus: The bus number at the ”from” end of the branch.
2. To Bus: The bus number at the ”to” end of the branch.
3. r: Resistance of the branch (in per unit, p.u.).
4. x: Reactance of the branch (in per unit, p.u.).
5. b: Total line charging susceptance (in per unit, p.u.).
6. RateA: Long-term rating (continuous thermal rating) of the branch (in MVA).
7. RateB: Short-term rating of the branch (in MVA).
8. RateC: Long-term emergency rating of the branch (in MVA).
9. Ratio: Transformer off-nominal turns ratio (usually 1 for transmission lines).
10. Angle: Transformer phase shift angle (in degrees, usually 0 for transmission lines).
11. Status: Branch status (1 = in service, 0 = out of service).
