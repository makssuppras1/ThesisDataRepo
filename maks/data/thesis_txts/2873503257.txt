Abstract  
This Master’s thesis investigates aspects of human exposure to pesticides, pesticide-related 
compounds and other chemical contaminants, through the analysis of human urine using liquid 
chromatography coupled with mass spectrometry (LC-MS). The study integrates both targeted and 
non-targeted analytical approaches, to develop suitable methodologies and assess exposure. In the 
first part of the work, the focus was on the development of a method for the analysis of four dialkyl 
phosphate (DAP) metabolites -common biomarkers of organophosphate pesticide exposure. A 
chromatographic method for the analysis of DAP metabolites, under HILIC (Hydrophilic 
Interaction Chromatography) and WAX (Weak Anion Exchange) conditions was optimized and 
among several tested protocols for the extraction of DAP metabolites from urine, a liquid-liquid 
extraction with acetonitrile and QuEChERS salts offered the most promising results, though full 
validation was not achieved. In the second part, a non-targeted high-resolution mass spectrometry 
(HRMS) workflow was applied to urine samples collected during a dietary intervention study. The 
aim was to examine changes in chemical exposure, particularly pesticides, resulting from a shift 
towards a plant-based diet. While the abundance of some pesticide-related compounds increased 
post-intervention, no consistent trend could be observed. Furthermore, a wide range of other 
exogenous compounds, including pharmaceuticals, industrial chemicals, and personal care product 
ingredients, were annotated. This thesis demonstrates the value of targeted and non-targeted LC-
MS approaches for comprehensive exposure assessment and highlights both limitations and 
potential for future research.

1. Introduction 
Pesticides are an integral part of modern agriculture, as they prevent, eliminate, or manage harmful 
organisms-pests and diseases while protecting plants and plant products throughout the production, 
storage, and transportation steps [1]. This leads to higher product yields and better-quality products 
[2], covering the needs of the growing global population and contributing to food security [3]. In 
Europe, more than 400 pesticide compounds have been approved and are already on the market  
[4]. They are used not only in agriculture, for crop protection, but also in residential settings for 
household purposes (on plants and against common insects) [5].  
Despite the benefits, the increasing exposure to pesticide residues is a global environmental and 
public health concern due to their potentially harmful effects on ecosystems [6] and human health 
[7]. This is the reason why the European Union has established a comprehensive legislative 
framework -including Regulation (EC) No 1107/2009 - to regulate the approval and use of active 
substances in pesticides. In addition, the control of pesticide residues in consumables [8], is 
covered under Regulation (EC) No 396/2005, where maximum residue levels are established 
(MRLs). 
Different types of pesticides are categorized based on the type of pest they target and their chemical 
structure. These include insecticides, herbicides, fungicides, rodenticides [1], and plant growth 
regulators, whereas among the most common modern pesticides are those belonging to chemical 
classes such as organophosphates, neonicotinoids [9], phenoxyacetic acids, chloroacetanilides, 
triazines, synthetic pyrethroids, and carbamates [10]. Organophosphates (OPs) are the most widely 
used insecticides globally, both in terms of agricultural and residential contexts, because of their 
effectiveness and their relatively short persistence in the environment [11]. They became especially 
popular after the restriction of using persistent organochlorine pesticides (OCs) [12], and are 
synthetic compounds, usually esters, amides, or thiol derivatives of phosphoric, phosphonic, 
phosphorothioic, or phosphonothioic acids [13].  
However, the implications of OPs’ use on the environment and human health are a worldwide 
problem, depending on the duration, frequency, and level of exposure to the compounds [14]. Both 
acute and chronic exposure to such pesticides can lead to harmful health effects, which can occur 
during this period or afterwards [15].  OPs are characterized by a short half-life, both in the fields 
==PAGE:14==
6 
 
and in the human body, often being eliminated within 24 and 72 h after exposure [16]. Although 
OPs do not accumulate in the body, their exposure has been linked with carcinogenic, neurological, 
reproductive, immunological, and genotoxic effects [17]. These insecticides are neurotoxins that 
can potentially deteriorate the function of the nervous system by inhibiting the activity of the 
enzyme acetylcholinesterase in humans [14]. The frequent use of OPs in combination with their 
severe implications makes them an object of interest in exposure studies.  
The general population is exposed daily to a mixture of different pesticides, including OPs, by 
several routes [18]. These can be the ingestion of pesticide-containing food [19],[20], water [21], 
and other drinks, usage of household products with pesticide ingredients, or residing close to 
agricultural areas, by inhaling outdoor and indoor contaminated air [22] or house dust [23]. 
Individuals can also be exposed to OPs during the handling and application of such chemicals, via 
dermal [13] exposure. The dominant route of exposure varies for different individuals [11], with 
diet intake (mainly fruit and vegetables [24]) and water consumption being the most significant 
sources of pesticide exposure for the general population [25], and also for higher-risk groups [26], 
such as children [11] and mothers. Different studies [27],[28],[29] have found an association 
between the consumption of organic diets [30] (food produced through methods that avoid 
synthetic fertilizers and pesticides) and reduced concentrations of pesticide residues and 
metabolites in urine samples, compared to conventional diets.  
Nowadays, to assess the integrated exposure to pesticides and other chemicals, human 
biomonitoring (HBM) is widely employed [26]. This is defined as the direct measurement of the 
concentration of chemical pollutants or their metabolites (evaluation of biomarkers of exposure) 
in human fluids and tissues [31]. HBM is valuable since it provides data on integrated exposure 
from multiple routes and pathways. This can be done by examining biological samples such as 
serum, lipids, urine, blood, and breast milk [14]. Among them, urine is the most typical matrix to 
assess pesticide exposure, since most pesticides are promptly metabolized into more polar 
compounds and excreted via urine [32], and furthermore, because it is easily accessible and non-
invasive. 
Following exposure, organophosphates do not accumulate in the human body, as they are rapidly 
metabolized to specific and non-specific metabolites, and primarily into dialkyl phosphate 
metabolites (DAPs), which are then excreted in urine [13], due to their high polarity. These DAPs 
==PAGE:15==
7 
 
are non-specific biomarkers [13] of population exposure to OP pesticides, because they result from 
the metabolism of multiple OP compounds. While urine as a matrix offers many advantages for 
assessing exposure, it also introduces disadvantages that might affect the determination of 
pesticide metabolites. Biomarkers in urine are short-lived and have high variations in 
concentration, which makes it hard to relate biomarker levels to specific health effects or exposures 
[33]. Additionally, human urine samples demonstrate high variability [34] among individuals 
(different diets, metabolism, or environmental conditions), causing various matrix effects that 
influence the determination of metabolite levels.  
Biomonitoring exposures and developing methods for determining different pesticide biomarkers 
are essential for understanding health risks and developing relevant regulations. Pesticide exposure 
biomarkers are found in the general population at very low concentrations, often in the order of 
μg/L. Thus, detecting them requires highly sensitive and selective analytical methods [10]. Most 
of these methods, for the analysis of pesticides and their metabolites in urine, utilize some type of 
chromatography, either gas (GC) or liquid chromatography (LC), whereas the detection techniques 
vary, from basic UV absorbance detection to advanced mass spectrometry analysis [35], leading 
to a broad range of limits of detection (LODs).   
Among these methods, liquid chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) seems to 
be the most used for general pesticide biomonitoring in urine [36]. Specifically for DAP 
metabolites, most of the exposure assessments utilize GC coupled to different detectors, due to the 
low limits of quantification (LOQs) of the former, yet including complex sample treatment 
protocols that require derivatization steps, leading to increased analysis time [5]. On the other 
hand, LC-MS/MS is the method of many of the recent studies on DAPs, offering high sensitivity 
and selectivity and requiring less extensive sample preparation procedures, by applying simple 
treatments [14] such as dilution, liquid-liquid extraction (LLE), QuEChERS (Quick, Easy, Cheap, 
Effective, Rugged and Safe), and Solid-Phase Extraction (SPE).  
The abovementioned are designed as targeted methods for analyzing DAP metabolites (and others, 
depending on application), focusing on a limited number of analytes (usually 2-6) [14]. However, 
non-targeted approaches, employing high-resolution mass spectrometry (HRMS), have become 
more common for pesticide exposure assessment. HRMS offers advantages over traditional 
==PAGE:16==
8 
 
tandem mass spectrometry, since it collects full-scan spectra, giving a more comprehensive view 
of a sample’s composition [37], and enabling the detection of a broader range of compounds.  
The aim of the present Master’s thesis is to evaluate human exposure to pesticides and other 
chemical contaminants through the analysis of human urine, using liquid chromatography coupled 
with mass spectrometry. The project combines both targeted and non-targeted analytical 
approaches and is structured in two main chapters. In Chapter I, the focus is on the development 
and optimization of a method for the determination of four dialkyl phosphate (DAP) metabolites 
of OP pesticides in human urine. This process involved testing of various chromatographic 
conditions, optimization of mass spectrometry parameters, and evaluation of numerous sample 
preparation protocols. In Chapter II, HRMS is employed to screen for a broad range of chemical 
features in urine samples collected during a dietary intervention study. The aim was to explore how 
short-term dietary changes could affect the chemical profile of urine, including the presence of 
pesticide-related compounds. By combining these two complementary approaches, this thesis aims 
to provide a comprehensive understanding of human exposure to known and unknown chemical 
contaminants.

2. Research Questions and Hypotheses 
This Master’s thesis investigates human exposure to pesticides and other chemical contaminants 
through both targeted and non-targeted approaches, using human urine samples collected from a 
previous pilot study [38] conducted by Sahur Faysal Farah. The present work is structured into 
two main parts, each of which states distinct research questions and hypotheses.  
In Chapter I, the focus is on the development and optimization of a targeted method for the 
determination of four dialkyl phosphate (DAP) metabolites. In early stages of method 
development, the scope also included glyphosate and its main metabolite, aminomethylphosphonic 
acid (AMPA), due to their importance in human exposure studies (see Theoretical section 3.2 for 
detailed description of the analytes). Based on these, the following research questions were 
formulated: 
Q1: Can a single LC-MS method be developed to simultaneously determine DAP metabolites 
and glyphosate/AMPA, given their similar physicochemical properties? 
Q2: Which chromatographic conditions are the most suitable for DAP metabolites analysis, 
and which sample preparation and extraction method results in optimal recoveries and 
repeatability for the quantification of these metabolites in human urine? 
From these research questions, the following hypotheses were stated: 
H1: It is possible to develop a unified LC-MS method for the analysis of both DAP metabolites 
and glyphosate/AMPA, due to their common physicochemical characteristics, such as high 
polarity and anionic nature. 
H2: Optimizing sample preparation protocols and testing various chromatographic conditions 
will lead to acceptable recoveries and repeatability of DAP metabolite detection in human 
urine.  
In Chapter II, an HRMS non-targeted analysis workflow was applied to the human urine samples 
collected from the previous pilot study, in which participants followed a short-term plant-rich diet. 
The analysis involved different extraction methods, and two distinct types of chromatographic 
columns (details in the Experimental section 7). Based on these, the following research questions 
were formulated:

3. Theoretical Background 
3.1 Metabolism, Excretion, and Pesticide Metabolites in Urine 
As mentioned in the Introduction, the assessment of human exposure to pesticides and other 
chemical pollutants can be conducted through human biomonitoring studies, with urine being one 
of the most valuable matrices for these purposes. Hence, it is crucial to understand how pesticides 
and their metabolites end up in this biological fluid. 
The term xenobiotic refers to any chemical (such as pesticides, occupational chemicals, or drugs) 
to which an organism is exposed and is extrinsic to the organism's normal metabolism [39]. When 
pesticides and other xenobiotics enter the body through different exposure routes (ingestion, 
inhalation, or dermal exposure), they are recognized as foreign and are attacked by a range of 
metabolic enzymes. These enzymes cause a series of reactions to modify the structure of the 
xenobiotic, so it can be easily eliminated [40].  
If the xenobiotic is polar, it can be easily excreted from the body, whereas non-polar compounds 
are typically not easily eliminated.  The body often transforms these non-polar compounds into 
more water-soluble structures, known as metabolites, enabling the easy excretion of the 
compounds from the body. Typically, these modifications are divided into Phase I and Phase II 
reactions [40].  
Phase I metabolism generally involves functionalization reactions such as oxidation, reduction, 
and hydrolysis, most of which take place in the liver, whereas some can also occur in the gut wall, 
blood plasma, and other tissues. These reactions are mainly catalyzed by non-specific enzymes, 
and particularly by the cytochrome P450 enzyme system (CYPs). The mode of action of these 
enzymes is that they add or reveal masked polar functional groups (e.g., -OH, -COOH, etc.) that 
might already be present in the compounds. Once these polar functional groups have been 
introduced, the overall compound is more water-soluble and is more easily eliminated when going 
through the kidneys. 
The Phase II metabolic pathway includes mostly conjugation reactions, meaning that a polar 
molecule is attached to a polar group that is already part of the compound or has been introduced 
during Phase I reactions. Transferase enzymes catalyze such reactions, with glucuronic acid and 
==PAGE:20==
12 
 
sulphates being the most common polar compounds introduced. The result of Phase II is a 
conjugate with significantly higher polarity, and thus an increased excretion rate in urine.  
Kidneys are the most important organs of excretion of the body's metabolites [41]. They filter the 
blood of xenobiotic compounds, like pesticide residues, and remove these waste chemicals in the 
urine. This process involves three main steps: glomerular filtration, tubular secretion, and/or 
tubular reabsorption [41]. In the glomerulus, passive filtration of the blood takes place, allowing 
small molecules - including xenobiotics or their metabolites - to pass through the glomerular pores 
into the nephron, forming the initial filtrate. Polar compounds typically remain in the nephron and 
get excreted in the urine, whereas hydrophobic compounds are more likely to be reabsorbed back 
into the blood [40]. 
The ability of different individuals to metabolize pesticides and other foreign compounds can be 
affected by various parameters [42] such as age, sex, diet, immune system, and overall health. 
People with an inefficient metabolic system might demonstrate high susceptibility to pesticide 
toxicity. 
Interpreting data from biomonitoring studies of human exposure to pesticides, using biological 
samples such as urine, can be challenging, since the concentration of pesticides in urine is affected 
by many different factors. Diet is one of these factors, since it has been shown that organic diets 
are associated with significantly lower levels of urinary pesticide analytes, compared to 
conventional diets [43]. Furthermore, as mentioned before, individual metabolism can play an 
important role, and exposure can also be affected by environmental conditions (e.g., pesticide 
applications in residential areas). 
Finally, urine dilution is another important variable, as the concentration of the pesticides and other 
chemical metabolites in urine can be influenced by the individual’s hydration state at the time of 
sample collection. To address this, correction for urine dilution is often necessary. This can be done 
through creatinine correction or specific gravity correction, with the former being the most 
common. Creatinine is a metabolic byproduct of muscle catabolism and is generally excreted by 
the kidneys at a constant rate within a healthy individual [44]. This makes creatinine a suitable 
marker to estimate urine concentration, especially in biomonitoring studies. In this work, creatinine 
levels were also monitored across participants of the study to evaluate urine dilution during non-
targeted analysis (section 8.2).

As explained in this section, pesticide metabolites found in urine are highly polar, variable, and 
present in low concentrations. This is the reason why it is important to select appropriate 
chromatographic and mass spectrometric methods for metabolite determination, as will be 
explained later (sections 3.4 and 3.5).  
3.2 Studied Pesticides and Metabolites, focusing on Dialkyl Phosphates 
Following the explanation of how pesticides are metabolized and excreted via urine, the next step 
is to focus on the specific analytes targeted in the first part of this thesis.  
Quantification of dialkyl phosphate (DAP) metabolites is the most commonly used approach to 
assess exposure to organophosphate (OP) pesticides. When entering the body, OP pesticides tend 
to be metabolized quickly through Phase I and Phase II reactions [45], resulting in highly polar 
metabolites excreted in urine. Nearly all OPs metabolize to a DAP moiety, which consists of a 
phosphate and two methyl (dimethylphosphates - DMPs) or two ethyl groups (diethylphosphates 
- DEPs), since most of the OPs are either O,O-dialkyl substituted, where the alkyl groups are 
usually methyl or ethyl. To better illustrate how different OP pesticides give rise to DAP 
metabolites, depending on their chemical structure, Figure 1 [13] presents the general metabolic 
pathways of two OP pesticides -methyl parathion and diazinon- into their corresponding DAP 
metabolites. Both pathways involve a hydrolysis step, representing Phase I metabolism.

These DAP metabolites are common to about 75% of the United States Environmental Protection 
Agency registered OP pesticides [46], and are the following six: dimethyl phosphate (DMP), 
dimethyl thiophosphate (DMTP), dimethyl dithiophosphate (DMDTP), diethyl phosphate (DEP), 
diethyl thiophosphate (DETP), and diethyl dithiophosphate (DEDTP).  
DAP metabolites are non-specific, as each can originate from the metabolism of multiple OP 
pesticides. DAPs do not retain any of the structure unique to the pesticides that they come from, 
thus making it hard to identify individual pesticides from these metabolites. However, since they 
are common metabolites of most insecticides, they provide useful information about cumulative 
exposure to such pesticides [11]. For instance, detection of DMP in a urine sample may indicate 
exposure to malathion, dichlorvos, or methyl parathion, among others - all of which metabolize 
into DMP. To demonstrate this overlap, Table 1 [13] lists examples of OP pesticides grouped

3.3 Sample Preparation and Extraction Methods for Urinary Pesticides 
and their Metabolites 
The typical experimental workflow for quantifying biomarkers of exposure to pesticides and other 
organic pollutants involves a sample pretreatment step, followed by an extraction and clean-up 
process, and finally by a separation and detection method [26]. Among these, sample preparation 
and extraction play a critical role in the identification, confirmation, and quantification of the 
==PAGE:26==
18 
 
targeted compounds [54].  During these processes, the analytes of interest are isolated and/or 
concentrated from the matrix to ensure they are suitable for separation and detection [54]. 
Furthermore, proper sample preparation is important because it directly influences the accuracy, 
reproducibility, and sensitivity of the method [55]. 
Urine is a complex biological matrix that contains many endogenous compounds, including 
inorganic salts, proteins, urea, creatinine, pigments, and a wide range of metabolites [56]. All these 
can interfere with the detection of analytes, especially in mass spectrometry, where matrix effects 
like ion suppression or enhancement can affect quantification. Additionally, due to the analytical 
challenges associated with the nature of the analytes of this study (polarity, ionization, and low 
concentrations), efficient sample preparation is essential to reduce matrix effects, concentrate 
analytes, and improve chromatographic separation.  
The main theoretical principles behind the sample preparation methods applied in this study, as 
well as other techniques commonly reported in literature, are presented in this section. In the 
context of this work, several sample preparation and extraction approaches were explored to 
achieve good recoveries of DAPs from urine. For the non-targeted analysis, two distinct sample 
preparation techniques were applied. A detailed description of the specific protocols used can be 
found in the Experimental sections 5.2 and 7.2 of this report.  
Solid-Phase Extraction (SPE) was a widely explored technique within this study. It is commonly 
used for sample cleanup and analyte concentration by isolating and separating the wanted 
compounds from liquid matrices. In SPE [57], the analytes are retained on a preconditioned and 
activated solid-phase material (sorbent) as the sample passes through the cartridge. Unwanted 
matrix components are washed away, while the analytes remain bound to the sorbent. 
Subsequently, the analytes are eluted using a suitable organic solvent, resulting in cleaner extracts, 
since interferences have been removed. Factors affecting the recovery of the target compounds 
include the selection of the right sorbent, which must match the polarity and physicochemical 
properties of the compounds, as well as the choice of the elution solvent, which must effectively 
release the retained compounds from the sorbent [58]. In this work, two types of SPE cartridges 
were used: strong anion exchange (SAX) and Hydrophilic-Lipophilic Balance (HLB).  
SAX cartridges [59] contain quaternary amine groups bound on silica that remain positively 
charged under a wide range of conditions. This makes them highly selective for retaining anionic 
==PAGE:27==
19 
 
compounds, such as phosphate metabolites. Elution is achieved by disrupting the analyte-
stationary phase interactions, typically by modifying the pH of the elution solvent.  
Regarding HLB cartridges [60], they contain a universal polymeric reversed-phase sorbent that 
includes a neutral polar “hook” and remains stable from pH 0-14. This makes it suitable for the 
extraction of a wide range of acidic, basic, and neutral compounds from various matrices. The 
sorbent is made from a specific ratio of two monomers - the hydrophilic N-vinylpyrrolidone and 
the lipophilic divinylbenzene, allowing it to retain a wide variety of analytes, from highly polar to 
non-polar. HLB cartridges are compatible with a range of solvents for conditioning, loading, 
washing, and eluting analytes. The chemical structures of the SPE-SAX and HLB sorbents are 
shown in Figure 2 to highlight the differences in their functional groups and retention mechanisms.

Another well-established extraction and clean-up process for pesticides and metabolites applied in 
this work is liquid-liquid extraction (LLE). LLE is based on partitioning the analytes between two 
immiscible solvents, meaning that typically the compounds are extracted from an aqueous 
biological sample (in this case, urine) to an organic solvent. Changing the organic solvent or 
adjusting the pH of the buffer, which can be present in the aqueous phase, can lead to the effective 
transfer of the wanted compounds to the organic phase, while most matrix components remain in 
the aqueous phase [61],[62]. Polarity is usually the most important factor in the selection of a 
suitable organic solvent or mixture of solvents.  
A widely adopted method for multiresidue analysis of pesticides that involves an LLE step, and 
can extract both polar and non-polar analytes, is the QuEChERS method [62]. In this method, 
acetonitrile is the extraction solvent, and a salt and buffer mixture is added to promote phase 
==PAGE:28==
20 
 
separation and salting out, and to stabilize the pH. The salts added to facilitate phase separation 
are anhydrous magnesium sulphate (MgSO4) and sodium chloride (NaCl). In the typical 
QuEChERS method, clean-up steps follow extraction [63], by using, e.g., dispersive SPE (d-SPE). 
The QuEChERS method was employed in this study both for the extraction of DAP metabolites 
and during the sample preparation steps for the non-targeted screening approach.   
LLE is a low-cost technique with good repeatability and compatibility with many instruments, 
making it useful for many applications [57],[61]. However, it requires high amounts of solvent, 
and developing a method for extracting multiple analytes can be challenging [61]. On the other 
hand, SPE typically requires less solvent, results in cleaner extracts, and often in higher recoveries, 
making it the preferred method [57].  
One final method of sample pretreatment utilized herein is the clean-up of urine samples, achieved 
by adding a high volume of organic solvent to the sample. It has been reported [64] that mixing a 
biological fluid with a water-miscible solvent, like acetonitrile or methanol, induces protein 
precipitation, as evidenced in this study by the formation of a white precipitate following 
acetonitrile addition and centrifugation. The addition of the solvent disrupts the hydration layer 
surrounding protein molecules and reduces repulsive interactions between them, thereby 
decreasing their solubility and promoting precipitation. Subsequently, centrifugation and filtration 
can remove the precipitate, and the clean supernatant can be used for the analysis.  
In addition to the methods applied in this study, several other sample preparation techniques have 
been reported in the literature [26],[65] for analyzing polar pesticides and metabolites, such as 
DAPs, in urine. The selection of sample preparation and extraction techniques largely depends on 
the analytical instrumentation used, as analytes must be compatible with the requirements of the 
analytical method. 
For instance, when gas chromatography coupled with mass spectrometry is employed, a 
derivatization step is typically required. Particularly for the analysis of DAPs in urine, many 
researchers have reported derivatization with pentafluorobenzyl bromide (PFB-Br [46],[66],[67]) 
or N-Methyl-N-(tert-butyldimethylsilyl)trifluoroacetamide (MTBSTFA) [68],[69],[70],[71]. 
These agents improve the thermal stability and volatility of compounds with polar functional 
groups (such as DAPs), and the derivatives produce characteristic fragmentation patterns in the 
MS, depending on the agent [72],[73].  
==PAGE:29==
21 
 
In contrast, derivatization does not seem to be a common technique for liquid chromatography-
mass spectrometry analysis of DAPs, compared to the commonly used SPE and LLE methods. 
Additionally, other approaches have been reported for DAPs, such as lyophilization [11],[74] or 
simple dilution [74] followed by direct injection of the sample into the instrument. 
Another common pre-treatment step of biological samples is the hydrolysis of the conjugated 
forms of the target biomarkers. Many of the metabolites are excreted in urine as glucuronide and 
sulfate conjugates [26], after Phase II metabolic reactions that involve conjugation with glucuronic 
acid and sulfates. To quantify the total amount of metabolite, deconjugation is necessary to release 
the free form of the analyte. This can be achieved by enzymatic hydrolysis, using β-glucuronidase 
enzyme [39], or acid hydrolysis [26].  
Although these methods were not used in the present study, they are important to consider. Overall, 
selecting and optimizing appropriate sample preparation and extraction methods remains essential 
for the successful determination of polar pesticide metabolites in biological matrices, like urine.  
In non-targeted screening (NTS) approaches, where the aim is to detect a wide range of unknown 
compounds, the sample preparation and extraction methods are also very critical. In such analyses, 
methods that are as non-selective and broad-spectrum as possible are preferred to retain the 
majority of compounds present in the sample. However, one of the main challenges in non-targeted 
workflows is the matrix effect, which can suppress or enhance ionization of analytes. Therefore, 
selecting a balance between removing the matrix and maintaining chemical diversity is one of the 
key issues in NTS. As reported in the literature [75], different extraction methods can be employed 
in untargeted analyses, including, among others, broad-spectrum SPE (with HLB being the 
preferred sorbent), conventional LLE with QuEChERS salts, and protein precipitation with various 
solvents.  
3.4 Liquid Chromatography 
In this work, liquid chromatography is the analytical technique employed to separate the 
components of the prepared urine sample and isolate the desired analytes, prior to detection by 
mass spectrometry. In LC, separation is based on the different interactions of compounds between 
a liquid mobile phase and a solid stationary phase as they move through a chromatographic 
column. To perform this separation, the method employed was high-performance liquid 
==PAGE:30==
22 
 
chromatography (HPLC) in which the mobile phase is driven through a packed column under high 
pressure [76]. 
The fundamental principle behind every chromatographic separation, including HPLC, is that each 
compound interacts with other chemical species in a specific way. This means that during 
chromatography, a sample is divided into individual components due to the differences in the 
relative affinities of the various molecules for the mobile and stationary phases. When components 
with different polarities pass through a column with a specific mobile phase, the ones with the 
strongest affinity for the mobile phase move faster and elute first. Since each component has a 
different polarity, they elute at different times and can be analyzed individually.  For example, in 
the analysis of urinary pesticide metabolites, highly polar compounds, such as DAPs, interact 
differently from less polar compounds, leading to variation in their retention times and allowing 
for their individual detection. 
Elution can be done under isocratic conditions, where the mobile phase composition remains 
constant throughout the run. However, gradient elution is also an option, with the mobile phase 
composition progressively changing during the run. In gradient elution programs, two or more 
solvents of different polarities and in various ratios are used, resulting in increased separation [77] 
efficiency, which is extremely useful when analyzing a wide range of compounds, as in the case 
of pesticide analysis.  
Different stationary phases can be employed, depending on the chemical properties of the target 
analytes. For instance, in RP chromatography, the stationary phase is non-polar while the mobile 
phase is relatively polar. The polar solutes being separated will have a higher affinity to the mobile 
phase, thus eluting faster, compared to less polar compounds, which are highly retained by the 
column [77]. Common columns for reverse phase separation are normally composed of silica 
particles chemically modified with hydrophobic alkyl chains [78] (e.g., C18, C8).  RP 
chromatography is a versatile technique, especially for analyzing non-polar or weakly polar 
compounds, and is very popular for pesticide and metabolite analysis.  Although not used in this 
study, normal phase LC, where the stationary phase is polar and the mobile phase is non-polar, is 
an alternative chromatographic method.  
For small, highly polar, or ionic compounds, such as dialkyl phosphate metabolites, studied herein, 
conventional RP chromatography might exhibit poor retention and separation [79]. To address this, 
==PAGE:31==
23 
 
various stationary-phase chemistries and types of chromatography have been explored, with 
Hydrophilic Interaction Liquid Chromatography (HILIC) and ion-exchange chromatography 
being common, valuable options, in the analysis of pesticide metabolites in urine [5],[80],[81]. In 
this study, the selection of columns combining HILIC and weak anion exchange (WAX) 
mechanisms was guided by the chemical properties of the analytes of interest, meaning high 
polarity and anionic character. 
HILIC is an alternative HPLC mode, which employs typical polar stationary phases, such as silica, 
amino, or cyano [82]. The mobile phase is similar to that used in RP [82] chromatography, meaning 
that it contains high amounts of organic solvents (typically acetonitrile), but also aqueous 
solvent/buffer or other polar solvents [83]. The polar surface of the stationary phase retains a layer 
of water from the mobile phase, forming an aqueous-rich layer, resulting in enhanced retention of 
polar analytes. The mechanisms of this retention can be hydrogen bonding between the polar 
functional groups of the compounds and the stationary phase, hydrophilic partitioning of the 
compounds' aqueous-rich layer and the bulk of the mobile phase, and electrostatic interactions.  
Ion-exchange chromatography is usually employed for separating ionizable molecules, based on 
variations in charge properties [84]. In this mode, analytes with a specific charge are retained 
weakly or strongly on an oppositely charged stationary phase, and the elution of the compounds is 
controlled by modifying the ionic strength or pH of the mobile phase. The mobile phase is normally 
an aqueous buffer, whereas the stationary phase is an inert organic matrix, chemically derivatized 
with ionizable functional groups. 
The basic setup of an HPLC system typically includes several components, as demonstrated in 
Figure 3. The mobile phases are stored in solvent reservoirs (1) and are delivered into the system 
by the pumps (2). The sample is introduced into the system using a sample injection device (3), 
and then it enters the chromatographic column (4), where separation occurs. When analytes elute 
from the column, they pass through a detector (5), and the final data is processed by a data 
acquisition and output system (6).  
==PAGE:32==
24 
 
As previously noted, HPLC is an advanced form of LC that utilizes high-pressure pumps to push 
the mobile phase through the column at faster rates. This enhances both the separation efficiency 
and the speed of analysis. The application of high-pressure results in well-defined peaks for each 
component, facilitating the easy identification and quantification of the target compound. As a 
result, HPLC has become an essential tool across various fields, such as pharmaceuticals, 
environmental monitoring, chemicals, and food safety [85].  
Ultra-High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (UHPLC), used in certain experiments in this 
study depending on the column type, is an advanced form of HPLC that enables faster and more 
efficient separations [86]. It operates at much higher pressures, enabling the use of smaller 
stationary phase particles (around 2 μm compared to the 5 μm typically used in HPLC [86]). The 
smaller particle size increases the surface area, resulting in improved resolution and quicker 
analysis of complex samples. These enhanced properties make UHPLC very effective in isolating 
individual compounds from complex matrices, such as urine.

Some of the most common detectors that follow chromatographic separation are: Ultraviolet or 
Visible Detector (UV/Vis), Diode Array Detector (DAD), Fluorescence Detector (FLD), and Mass 
Spectrometry Detector (MS). Among these, MS is widely employed for qualitative and 
quantitative purposes, due to its high sensitivity and selectivity for the target analyte. It can detect 
a broad spectrum of compounds, including those that lack UV chromophores or are thermally 
labile (limitations for other detectors). In addition, MS can detect components in co-eluting 
chromatographic peaks, minimizing the need for perfect chromatographic separation, and provides 
structural information for the compounds [89]. 
The characterization and identification of the compounds with mass spectrometry are based on 
their mass-to-charge ratio (m/z). The working principle of MS [76] involves three main steps: 
ionization, mass analysis, and detection. In the first step, the molecules are converted into charged 
species by the ionization source. Then on, these species are led to a mass analyzer to be separated 
according to their mass-to-charge ratio. Finally, a detector measures the abundance of each ion 
exiting the analyzer, producing a signal proportional to intensity. When combined with appropriate 
calibration, this signal can be used for quantification purposes.  The resulting output of the analysis, 
known as a mass spectrum, represents the number of ions detected at each mass-to-charge ratio.  
As mentioned above, ionization is the first crucial step in mass spectrometry. MS requires ions to 
be in the gaseous phase, highlighting the importance of having an interface to connect the outlet 
of the LC with the MS inlet. At this interface, the LC mobile phase is efficiently transferred to gas, 
while simultaneously, analytes are ionized [90].  
Electrospray ionization (ESI) is considered the most valuable ionization technique for coupling 
between LC and MS [86], and is particularly effective for analytes with ionizable basic or acidic 
polar functional groups [91], as in the case of pesticide metabolites in urine. ESI is a soft ionization 
technique [76], meaning that little fragmentation of analytes occurs; however, it is still possible to 
intentionally increase fragmentation. A schematic representation of the ESI mechanism is provided 
in Figure 4 [76], to visually illustrate the process by which the liquid sample is transformed into 
charged droplets and finally ionized species. In ESI, a small amount of sample from the LC is 
pumped through a fine metal capillary. At the tip of the metal capillary, a high voltage is applied, 
==PAGE:34==
26 
 
creating a strong electric field that turns the liquid into an aerosol of charged droplets. A flow of 
sheath gas (dry N2) helps to form the droplets and leads this spray to the mass spectrometer. While 
the droplets move to the mass analyzer, they shrink as the drying gas aids the solvent evaporation. 
Finally, the charged analytes are released from the droplets into the analyzer. ESI can operate in 
positive and negative modes, with charging of the analytes generally being done via protonation 
or deprotonation, respectively, depending on the potential of the spraying nozzle [91].  
After ionization, the charged species are separated in the mass analyzer based on their m/z ratio. 
There are different types of mass analyzers, categorized based on the mechanism of ion separation 
according to m/z. These mass analyzers can be magnetic sector, ion trap, quadrupole (Q), time-of-
flight (TOF), orbitrap, and ion cyclotron resonance (ICR) analyzers, and they can be further 
coupled in series to form tandem MS analyzers [86]. In this work, the focus will be on Quadrupole 
and specifically on Triple Quadrupole MS, and high-resolution Orbitrap MS, since these are the 
mass analyzing systems coupled to LC, throughout the experiments. 
Quadrupoles (Q) consist of four parallel, equally distant metal rods to which both a constant 
voltage and a radiofrequency (RF) oscillating voltage are applied. The main principle of ion 
separation in MS [76],[92] is that when ions travel through a magnetic or electrical field, their 
movement is strongly dependent on their m/z ratio. In a quadrupole, the applied electric field 
causes ions to follow complex oscillating trajectories as they move from the ionization chamber 
to the detector, allowing only ions with a specific m/z ratio to reach the detector. The ions with 
these specific m/z ratios have ‘stable’ amplitudes of oscillation and reach the detector without

sprayed through a metal capillary, forming charged droplets that undergo solvent evaporation, resulting in the
release of ionized analytes in the mass spectrometer.  [76] 
==PAGE:35==

hitting the quadrupole rods. On the other hand, ions with ‘unstable’ oscillations collide with the 
rods and are lost before they reach the detector. The amplitude of oscillation can be controlled by 
adjusting the constant and RF voltages, thus enabling the selective filtering of the ions based on 
their m/z ratios.  
While a single quadrupole can filter ions by their m/z ratios, linearly using three quadrupoles 
allows for more detailed analysis of ions, and this is the basis of Triple Quadrupole MS (Tandem 
MS). The triple quadrupole (QqQ) is probably the most used MS/MS instrument [92]. In the first 
quadrupole (Q1), the analyte of interest is selected. This precursor ion collides with a collision gas 
in a second quadrupole collision cell (Q2) and undergoes fragmentation, a process known as 
collision-induced dissociation (CID). The fragments of the precursor ion are led to a quadrupole 
mass analyzer (Q3), where structural information can be obtained. Normally, Q1 is set to select 
only one m/z ratio, and this is where ions with different m/z ratios are filtered out [92]. A schematic 
representation of a triple quadrupole MS system, showing these three functional quadrupoles and 
their roles in ion selection, fragmentation, and analysis, is shown in Figure 5 [93].  
 
As previously mentioned, a high-resolution, accurate-mass Quadrupole-Orbitrap Mass 
Spectrometer coupled with LC was also employed for some experiments throughout this study. As 
a matter of course, such an instrument has a different operating principle compared to the triple 
quadrupole system.  
High-resolution mass spectrometers are designed to distinguish analytes of interest from potential 
interferences [94]. Orbitrap mass spectrometers use high-resolution accurate-mass (HRAM) 
technology and enhance the abovementioned ability by distinguishing ions with very small mass

differences, even at low m/z values [94]. Its HRAM capabilities make Orbitrap a commonly 
preferred mass analyzer for identifying unknown or untargeted compounds at very low 
concentrations (sub-ppb) and/or with very small mass differences [95] (sub-ppm), having 
applications in the analysis of low-abundance, highly complex samples, such as samples in the

[95]. Furthermore, non-targeted analysis (NTA) approaches -used in this study- benefit from the 
Orbitrap’s HRAM capabilities, as it enables broad chemical screening and facilitates the 
identification of potential environmental or metabolic exposures in human urine, based on accurate 
masses [96].  
For a better understanding, the basic working principle [97] of Orbitrap MS is explained herein. 
The Orbitrap is an ion trap analyzer composed of a central electrode and two outer electrodes, 
allowing it to function as both the analyzer and the detector. When ions enter the Orbitrap, they 
are captured by a process called “electrodynamic squeezing”, after which they oscillate around the 
central electrode and between the outer electrodes. Each ion type oscillates at a specific frequency, 
based on its m/z ratio, enabling their separation. The oscillating frequencies of the ions are detected 
on the outer electrodes, generating the mass spectrum. The Orbitrap operates as a Fourier-
Transform mass analyzer.  
Mass spectrometers can collect and record data in different ways by applying different acquisition 
modes, depending on the required sensitivity and selectivity, and the aim of the analysis performed. 
Mass spectrometry methods can be broadly categorized into targeted and non-targeted approaches. 
A targeted analysis deals with the detection or quantification of one or more specific, pre-defined 
compounds that are already well-characterized chemically or biologically [98], and their 
fragmentation patterns are known. In such methods, a calibration curve is typically constructed, 
using standards, allowing for quantification of the analytes in the samples. On the contrary, non-
targeted or untargeted analysis involves the simultaneous detection of a large number of 
unspecified compounds, providing mostly qualitative results [98].  
One of the most common acquisition techniques in targeted analysis is multiple reaction 
monitoring (MRM), also referred to in the literature as selective reaction monitoring (SRM). The 
conventional implementation of MRM MS experiments uses triple quadrupole mass 
spectrometers, where the ion of the target compound is first isolated and then fragmented to 
==PAGE:37==
29 
 
produce fragments or daughter ions [99]. One or more of these ions are selected afterwards, 
typically one for quantification and at least one for identification, as recommended by the 
SANTE/11312/2021v2 [100] guidelines. Only ions that match both the precursor ion and the 
specific daughter ion masses are detected [99]. By ignoring all the other ions that flow into the 
mass spectrometer, high sensitivity and specificity for the simultaneous analysis of targeted 
compounds in a single experiment [101] are achieved, however, optimization of the instrument is 
necessary. Another acquisition approach for targeted analysis is selected ion monitoring (SIM). In 
this technique, the mass spectrometer is set to monitor the intensity of specific m/z values of 
interest, in an expected retention time window, introducing high sensitivity into the analysis [102]. 
During SIM, the quadrupole is set to pass only ions of interest to the detector, whereas in MRM, 
the transition from a selected precursor ion to a specific product is monitored [103].  
Targeted analyses can also be achieved using high-resolution systems, such as Orbitrap MS. 
Targeted SIM (tSIM) is the high-resolution counterpart to SIM, and with this acquisition method 
target precursor ions are isolated and transferred to the mass analyzer for detection [104]. tSIM 
does not employ fragmentation of the parent ions, but it confirms the identity of the target ions by 
accurate-mass measurements in combination with information regarding the elution time. Hence, 
tSIM is a simple, sensitive, and selective method [104]. Another acquisition method used for 
targeted analysis in Orbitrap MS is Parallel Reaction Monitoring (PRM) or t-MS/MS, or t-MS2. 
In t-MS2, the target precursor ions are accurately selected and then fragmented before being led to 
the mass analyzer, where all target products are analyzed with high accuracy, offering quantitative 
and confirmatory information. This method is also characterized by high sensitivity and selectivity 
[105], whereas these parameters can be further enhanced by combining tSIM with t-MS2. 
Regarding non-targeted workflows, one of the fundamental acquisition modes is the Full MS scan. 
In this mode, the mass spectrometer measures the intensity of all ions within a broad range of 
mass-to-charge ratios [106], providing comprehensive qualitative information about the sample 
composition. Another common mode used in non-targeted analysis is the combination of a Full 
MS scan with data-dependent MS2 (ddMS2). In this method, the instrument first performs a full 
scan (FS) to identify the most abundant precursor ions and then selects a number of abundant ions, 
or ions from an inclusion list, for fragmentation and MS/MS analysis [107]. This enables the 
==PAGE:38==
30 
 
identification of unknown compounds by providing information about their fragmentation 
patterns.  
In contrast, data-independent acquisition (DIA) is a mode in which all precursor ions identified in 
a specific mass range get fragmented [107], rather than only selected ones. One widely used DIA 
technique is All-Ion Fragmentation (AIF), in which no precursor ion selection is performed. All 
ions entering the mass spectrometer are fragmented at the same time, producing a complex MS/MS 
spectrum that includes fragments from all co-eluting compounds. For all these non-targeted 
approaches, high-resolution mass spectrometry systems are required.  
Most of the acquisition modes mentioned above were applied throughout the experiments in this 
study, depending on the specific mass spectrometer used and the objective of each analysis.

4.1 Sample Collection and Background of the Previous Pilot Study 
The urine samples used in this study were collected as part of a previous pilot study conducted by 
Sahur Faysal Farah (MSc thesis, 2024 [38]), at the DTU National Food Institute, under the 
framework of the OnePlate project. The OnePlate project [108] is part of the Organic RDD 8 
program, coordinated by ICROFS (International Center for Research in Organic Agriculture and 
Food Systems) in collaboration with the Green Development and Demonstration Program (GUDP) 
under the Danish Ministry of Food, Agriculture, and Fisheries. The project aims to investigate how 
people can transition to a more plant-based and organic diet following the official Dietary 
Guidelines, and to monitor the effects of switching to such a diet, including potential changes in 
pesticide exposure.  
This previous pilot study involved nine healthy adult participants (five males and four females, 
aged 25-77) who were monitored over two different weeks: a baseline week when participants 
followed a regular meat-rich Danish diet, and an intervention week when a plant-rich diet, 
according to official Danish dietary guidelines, was followed.  Both diets were based on 
conventionally grown (non-organic) food commodities, as participants were specifically instructed 
to increase their intake of conventionally grown fruits and vegetables. Different participants are 
referred to as PPx from now on (e.g., PP1 corresponds to participant 1).  
Urine samples were collected as follows: first-morning urine samples were obtained on days 3, 5, 
and 7 of each week, while 24-hour urine collections were also performed on day 7. The food types 
and quantities consumed by the different individuals were monitored in online food diaries, and 
questionnaires were completed to better frame the study.  
On average, participants increased their intake of fruits and vegetables and reduced their 
consumption of meat during the intervention week. However, adherence to the dietary guidelines 
varied, as some participants decreased their intake of fruits and vegetables during the intervention 
week, rather than increasing it. Results of the pilot study indicated that the primary route of 
exposure to pesticides was dietary intake, with a small influence from other environmental 
exposures. Details on the specific urine samples analyzed herein can be found in the respective 
Experimental sections 5.1 and 7.1 of Chapters I and II, respectively.

5. Experimental  
5.1 Urine Samples and Sample Preparation 
As described in section 4.1, urine samples were collected during a previous pilot study. In the 
present work, a subset of these samples was pooled and used during the development and testing 
of extraction methods for the analysis of DAP metabolites.  
Before extraction methods, all samples were centrifuged at 4°C and 5000 rpm for 10 minutes to 
remove solid particles and precipitate proteins. Throughout the experiments, only plastic 
containers and labware were used to minimize compounds’ adsorption on glass, since previous 
studies [109] have reported that highly polar pesticides tend to bind the active sites of glass 
surfaces. 
5.2 Extraction Methods for DAP Metabolites 
Pooled urine samples were spiked with a mixture of non-isotopically labeled DAP standards 
(DMTP, DMDTP, DEP, and DETP) to achieve final concentrations in the extracts between 25 and 
100 ng/mL, depending on the method tested. Each extraction method was evaluated in at least two 
replicates, along with a blank urine sample.  
The goal was to develop a method that would give high and reproducible recoveries in LC-MS 
analysis. Due to challenges introduced by the physicochemical characteristics of DAPs and the 
complexity of the urine matrix, multiple approaches needed to be tested. The detailed protocols 
for each method are presented below. Further discussion on the reason for the selection of each 
method, along with the results and interpretation, can be found in the Results and Discussion 
section 6.2. For clarity, abbreviations were created for each method, describing its type and key 
components (Table 4). The methods are grouped below by the sample preparation technique used.

samples were prepared.

Two replicates of spiked samples were prepared.

5.3 Instrumental Methods 
The initial part of the method development for DAPs was done using a Thermo Ultimate 3000 
UHPLC coupled with a Bruker EVOQ triple quadrupole mass spectrometer. This part involved 
MS and LC optimization, including the selection of compound-specific transitions and testing of 
various chromatographic conditions to achieve separation of the DAPs. 
Due to technical issues with the EVOQ instrument, the analysis was transferred to a Thermo 
Vanquish UHPLC coupled with a Thermo Scientific Orbitrap Exploris 120 mass spectrometer, 
used for the evaluation of the different extraction methods performed. The chromatographic 
method developed on the EVOQ was retained, with only a minor modification to the total run time. 
However, the mass spectrometry parameters had to be adjusted for the Orbitrap, since the two

5.3.1 Chromatographic Separation  
Chromatographic separation of DAPs was performed using a Torus DEA column (2.1×100 mm, 
1.7 μm), at 50°C. A VanGuard Cartridge (2.1 mm I.D. x 5 mm) packed with the same stationary 
phase was used as a guard column. The mobile phases were (A) an aqueous solution containing 
1.2% formic acid and 10 mM ammonium formate, and (B) acetonitrile containing 0.5% formic 
acid. The flow rate used was 0.5 mL/min, and the injection volume was 10 μL.  
The gradient elution program on the EVOQ was as follows: 0 min 10% A, 0.5 min 10% A, 1.5 min 
80% A, 4.5 min 90% A, 17.5 min 90% A, 17.6 min 10% A, and 23 min 10% A.  On the Orbitrap 
system, the same solvent ratios were used, but the total run time was shorter by reducing the 
column equilibration time between injections. The gradient elution program was as follows: 0 min 
10% A, 0.5 min 10% A, 1.5 min 80% A, 4.5 min 90% A, 13.5 min 90% A, 14.0 min 10% A, and 
17.0 min 10% A. Figures showing the elution gradients for both instruments can be found in 
Appendix 1.  Based on the experiments and the technical specifications of the column, the sorbent 
requires initial mobile phase conditioning and equilibration until stable pressure to achieve 
reproducible results.

6. Results and Discussion 
In this section, key findings from the development of the chromatographic separation method and 
the evaluation of the various extraction and cleanup procedures are presented. The experiments 
were part of an attempt to develop an analytical method to determine DAP metabolites in human 
urine, with the general goal of applying it to real samples, collected within the framework of the 
OnePlate project, in subsequent studies.  While optimization of MS parameters was an important 
part of this work, relevant results (mainly optimal CEs) are presented in the Experimental section 
5.3.2, to facilitate potential method replication. 
6.1 Chromatographic Separation 
At the beginning of the project, the aim was to develop a single liquid chromatographic method 
capable of simultaneously analyzing both glyphosate/AMPA and DAP metabolites. These two 
compound groups demonstrate similar physicochemical characteristics -high polarity, strong 
anionic character, and low molecular weight- which suggest that a unified method would lead to 
faster analyses and reduced solvent consumption.  Due to these specific characteristics, RP 
chromatography is less suitable for the separation of such compounds, compared to other types of 
chromatography, as highlighted in Theoretical section 3.4.  
Therefore, two columns suitable for analyzing highly polar and anionic compounds were tested: 
the Waters Anionic Polar Pesticide Column (APPC) and the Waters TorusTM DEA Column. These 
columns were also suitable for analyzing glyphosate/AMPA and DAPs, as reported in the literature 
[110]. Both employ ethylene-bridged hybrid (BEH) spherical particles with tri-functionally boned 
diethylamine (DEA) ligands, providing a combination of HILIC and WAX interactions. According 
to the manufacturer [111], the main differences between the two columns are their particle sizes 
(1.7 and 5 μm, respectively) and the intended use: Torus DEA is primarily packed for supercritical 
fluid chromatography (SFC) but also has been used in LC applications, while the APPC is QC 
tested for UHPLC systems.  
Two methods previously described by the EU Reference Laboratory (EURL) Quick Polar 
Pesticides (QuPPe) guidelines [110] were tested and evaluated:  
• Method A: Based on the reference, it can be applied to both columns and is originally 
optimized for the analysis of glyphosate/AMPA, among others. The mobile phases were an 
==PAGE:54==
46 
 
aqueous solution of 1.2% formic acid (eluent A) and acetonitrile containing 0.5% formic 
acid (eluent B). The gradient elution program and flow suggested were the same for both 
columns. 
• Method B: Based on the guidelines, it can be applied to both columns with slight 
modifications and is designed mainly for the analysis of DAPs. The mobile phase differed 
from that in Method A by the addition of ammonium formate in the aqueous solution. For 
the Torus DEA, 10 mM ammonium formate with 1.2% formic acid was used, whereas for 
the APPC, 15 mM ammonium formate was required. The elution program and flow were 
the same as in Method A, with a slight change in the re-equilibration step on the APPC. 
A full description of Methods A and B can be found in Appendix 2. 
6.1.1 Method A 
It was decided to first test Method A as a potential approach for the simultaneous determination of 
glyphosate/AMPA and dialkyl phosphate metabolites. The main characteristic of the method is that 
it employs acidified mobile phases with formic acid, without the addition of a buffer. Method A 
was applied to both the Torus DEA column and the APP column, and the results were qualitatively 
evaluated based on signal intensity and peak shape. For this purpose, individual standards of 
glyphosate, AMPA, and four DAPs were injected at a concentration of 100 ng/mL. 
Glyphosate and AMPA were successfully detected under Method A. When comparing the Torus 
DEA and APPC columns, for the separation of these analytes, the APPC yielded sharper peaks and 
significantly higher signal intensities. Although Torus DEA also retained these analytes, it 
demonstrated less intense signals. This difference is illustrated in Figure 7, which clearly shows 
the enhanced signal intensity and improved peak shape obtained with the APPC column. These 
observations are consistent with previous findings of Zhang et al. [112], who reported similar 
results when comparing the same columns for glyphosate and AMPA. The improved performance 
of the APPC may be attributed to specific interactions between the analytes and the particle 
structure of the column [112]. 
==PAGE:55==
47 
 
 
On the contrary, Method A did not demonstrate satisfactory results for DAP metabolites. 
Specifically, none of the four compounds (DMTP, DMDTP, DEP, DETP) were detected under 
Method A, in any of the columns, despite the relatively high concentrations of the standards. This 
suggests that under the acidic, non-buffered conditions of Method A, the DAPs were not retained 
on the stationary phase and, therefore, eluted with the void volume. Given that these columns are 
generally suitable for retaining highly polar compounds (such as DAPs), the lack of retention 
herein can probably be attributed to the absence of a buffering agent, as will be further discussed 
in subsequent sections.  
6.1.2 Method B 
After evaluating Method A and finding out that it is suitable only for glyphosate/AMPA and not 
for DAPs, it was decided to try Method B to check if, with these parameters, the simultaneous 
analysis of the two groups of compounds is possible. Method B differs from A in the use of 
ammonium formate as a buffering agent, enhancing ionic and retention stability. Method B was 
also tested in both columns.

The choice of injection solvent plays a crucial role in achieving good peak shape, resolution, and 
high sensitivity for the wanted compounds [113]. The analytes must be soluble in the injection 
solvent, and it should be compatible with the mobile phase used in the experiment. In this study, 
the effect of injection solvent on signal intensity was moderate and varied across the compounds, 
with no consistent trend, making interpretation complex. Milli-Q water and H₂O–ACN (90:10, 
v/v) gave the highest responses for most analytes—especially for DMDTP, as visualized in the bar 
plots (Figure 10), when comparing intensities. In contrast, among the worst-performing solvents 
were the ones with high organic content: mobile phase B and 10% formic acid in MeOH. 
(especially for DMDTP). These organic solvents likely did not match the highly aqueous 
composition of the mobile phase and resulted in small peak intensities, unlike injection solvents 
with higher water content. Mobile phase A, despite being aqueous, resulted in relatively low peak 
areas for all DAPs, likely due to the presence of both formic acid and ammonium formate, which 
may have affected ionization of DAPs. 
The RSD values (calculated based on the formulas shown in Appendix 3) were low across all 
solvents and analytes (typically below 1%, as seen in Table 8), indicating good repeatability of the 
==PAGE:60==

injections. According to the SANTE 11312/2021 v2 [100] guidelines for pesticide analysis in food 
and feed, an RSD value of less than 20% is considered acceptable. Based on these findings, H2O-
ACN (90:10, v/v) was selected as the injection solvent for subsequent experiments. 
6.1.4 Summary of Chromatographic Method Evaluation 
At the beginning of the project, the aim was to develop a single chromatographic method for the 
simultaneous analysis of both glyphosate/AMPA and DAP metabolites. Despite their similar 
physicochemical characteristics, the method development process showed that they exhibit 
different chromatographic behavior under the HILIC/WAX conditions offered by the available 
columns.  
Method A, which employed an acidic, non-buffered mobile phase, performed well for the analysis 
of glyphosate and AMPA, especially with the APPC column. These compounds exist in multiple 
charged forms, demonstrated by their different pKa values, and likely interact with the column 
mainly through HILIC mechanisms, such as hydrogen bonding and partitioning into the water-rich 
layer formed on the hydrophilic surface [83]. The absence of a buffering agent may have allowed 
the polar interactions to dominate, instead of the ion-exchange ones. Other studies [114],[115] have 
also reported good chromatographic separation of glyphosate and AMPA under HILIC conditions, 
without the employment of a buffering agent in the mobile phase.  
In contrast, none of the DAP metabolites were detected under Method A in either column, probably 
due to their strong negative charge and their dependence on the WAX mechanism for retention. 
This assumption was further supported during Method B testing, when ammonium formate was 
introduced. The buffer provided a stable ionic environment that potentially enabled WAX 
interactions with the diethylamine groups of the column, leading to the separation of DAPs. 
However, under these buffered conditions, glyphosate and AMPA were not sufficiently retained, 
likely due to changes in pH that affected their ionization or competition with buffer ions. These 
findings are consistent with the QuPPe [110] method guidelines, which recommend non-buffered 
mobile phases for the analysis of glyphosate and AMPA, and buffered conditions (e.g., with 
ammonium formate) for DAP metabolites, when using such columns.  
Additionally, various injection solvents were tested to assess their suitability with chromatographic 
conditions. Among them, H2O-ACN (90:10, v/v), which matched the high-water content of the 
mobile phase, was selected for further analyses of DAPs.  
==PAGE:61==
53 
 
After selecting the chromatographic method and the suitable injection solvent for the analysis of 
DAP metabolites, DAP standard mixtures were injected at three concentration levels: 1, 10, and 
100 ng/mL. These results demonstrated a linear instrumental response, with peak areas increasing 
proportionally with concentration. Preliminary calibration curves of the four DAP compounds 
shown in Appendix 4 exhibited excellent linearity, with coefficients of determination (R2) greater 
than 0.99 for all analytes.  
In conclusion, the chromatographic evaluation showed that a single unified method for both 
glyphosate/AMPA and DAPs was not achievable under the tested conditions. While Method A 
with the APPC column remains suitable for glyphosate and AMPA (pending further optimization), 
Method B with the Torus DEA column and H2O-ACN (90:10, v/v) as the injection solvent was 
selected as the chromatographic method for DAP analysis.  
6.2 Evaluation of Extraction Methods for DAP Metabolites 
An essential part of this work was the evaluation of various sample preparation and extraction 
protocols to achieve high and reproducible recoveries of DAP metabolites (DMTP, DMDTP, DEP, 
DETP) from human urine. A wide range of sample preparation methods were assessed for their 
extraction efficiency, including SPE and LLE approaches, and a protein precipitation-based 
method. An overview of all the tested methods is presented in Table 4, and they are described 
thoroughly in Experimental section 5.2. The extraction methods were selected and tested 
progressively, based either on relevant literature or by adapting protocols commonly used for 
pesticide analysis.  
Since all protocols included a filtration step before LC analysis, potential analyte loss was 
evaluated. Standard DAP solutions (50 ng/mL) were analyzed with and without filtration, showing 
no retention of analytes. Peak area comparisons are presented in Appendix 5.  
The performance of each method was assessed based on absolute recoveries (%) and repeatability, 
calculated as RSD (%). Recoveries were calculated by comparing the concentration of the analyte 
measured in the final extract to the theoretical spiking level [100]. For the methods that yielded 
non-zero recoveries for at least some of the compounds of interest, RSD was calculated based on 
the formulas provided in Appendix 3. The analytes’ concentrations in the sample were determined 
using matrix-matched calibration curves, prepared individually for each method.

Τhe first methods performed were SPE-SAX-1 and SPE-SAX-2, which were adapted from 
protocols developed for glyphosate analysis (due to similar physicochemical characteristics) by 
Connolly et al. [116] and Ruiz et al. [51], respectively. These relied on SAX cartridges, potentially 
suitable for the anionic character of DAPs. Both methods yielded moderate recoveries of DEP, 
with SPE-SAX-2 (elution solvent: 1M HCl:CH3OH (90:10, v/v)) giving a slightly better result 
(58.7 > 35.1%) compared to SPE-SAX-1 (10% formic acid in methanol). None of the other DAPs 
were recovered, meaning that these compounds were either not retained by the sorbent or were not 
eluted with these solvents. According to the manufacturer's guidelines [117], the SAX cartridges 
are designed for the extraction of weakly acidic compounds, with pKa > 2. This means that the 
compounds of interest with pKa values between 1.11 and 1.55 (see section 3.2), being mostly in 
their fully ionized forms under the experimental conditions, are too acidic to be retained by the 
quaternary amine groups of the sorbent. It is therefore concluded that these SPE-SAX methods 
might be suitable for glyphosate analysis, as found in the literature, but are unsuitable for extracting 
DAP metabolites. 
It should be noted that for the calculation of RSD (%) for the DEP analyte in methods SPE-SAX-
1 and SPE-SAX-2, only two replicates were taken into consideration (due to sample volume 
limitation). As a result, the RSD (%) values reported for these methods are considered less 
statistically reliable. However, for all the other calculated RSD (%), three replicates were taken 
into consideration.  
The SPE-HLB method, based on previous in-house work [38], showed no recoveries for any 
analyte. This might be related to the high polarity and anionic character of the DAPs, 
characteristics that might not allow for their retention on the hydrophilic-lipophilic balanced 
sorbent, despite its ability to retain a wide range of compounds, with varying polarities. 
None of the LLE methods using ethyl acetate as the extraction solvent yielded meaningful 
recoveries for any of the compounds. This suggests that ethyl acetate is not a suitable solvent for 
DAP extraction, likely due to its relatively low polarity, which does not allow partitioning of highly 
polar and ionic species, such as DAP metabolites, from aqueous matrices like urine. These results 
come in disagreement with a previously published study from Kumar et al. [14], reporting good 
recoveries of DAPs with ethyl acetate LLE.

strong matrix effects that affected the retention of this compound. Chromatograms of the four

In summary, the development of a sample preparation method for extracting DAP metabolites from 
human urine proved to be challenging. This can be attributed to the strong anionic and highly polar 
character of the analytes, combined with the complexity of the urine matrix. Most of the SPE and 
LLE methods tested had inadequate recoveries for DAPs. Although none of the methods fulfilled 
the recovery and repeatability criteria mentioned in SANTE 11312/2021 v2 [100] guidelines, LLE-
ACN-QuEChERS had the most promising results, since moderate recoveries were achieved for 
three of the four analytes (DMTP, DMDTP, and DETP), with acceptable RSD values. This LLE-
ANN-QuEChERS protocol could be further improved. Some suggestions could be the slight 
acidification of the matrix to adjust the charge of DAPs, or the optimization of the salt mixture 
used in the extraction.  
Other approaches can be employed to overcome difficulties associated with matrix-matched 
calibrations when calculating recoveries from the various extraction methods. One approach is the 
==PAGE:66==
58 
 
incorporation of an internal standard (IS) compound. This is a substance added in a known, 
constant amount to all samples, blanks, and standards. The IS should be chemically similar to the 
analytes of interest, but different enough so that it can be distinguished by the detector [77]. In this 
case, isotopically labeled standards of the DAPs would be a good choice, as they would exhibit 
similar chromatographic behavior to the analytes but different m/z ratios. The addition of IS would 
compensate for matrix effect, variabilities in the sample preparation process, and instrumental 
fluctuations. Since compounds of interest and IS would have similar chemical behavior, the 
abovementioned variabilities would affect both in the same way, and the results can be corrected 
based on the response of the instrument to the IS. Unfortunately, suitable internal standards were 
not available in this work. 
An alternative approach is the application of the standard addition method. This is a widely 
employed method in cases where strong matrix effects are present [77], such as in urine analysis. 
In this method, known amounts of the DAP standard mix would be added to equal volumes of the 
same urine sample, at different levels. The LLE-ACN-QuEChERS method would then be applied 
to all spiked samples in the same way. The peak areas would be plotted against the added 
concentrations of the standard, and the concentration of the analyte in the original (unspiked) 
sample can be calculated by extrapolating the calibration curve to the x-axis. In this way, matrix 
effects can be compensated, as they affect both the analyte in the unspiked sample and the spiked 
samples equally.  
Finally, it needs to be noted that full method validation was not conducted, since time constraints 
did not allow for further exploration of sample preparation methods to achieve acceptable 
recoveries. All recoveries were assessed at a single concentration level, with only two or three 
replicates per method. For future validation, multiple concentration levels must be included to 
assess linearity, recovery experiments must be performed with more spiking levels and more 
replicates, and other validation parameters such as matrix/effects and LOD/LOQ must be assessed. 
However, the plethora of methods assessed provided information on the challenges of such 
analyses of polar pesticide metabolites in urine and created a background for future work.

Based on the overall aims outlined in the Introduction and the study’s research questions and 
hypotheses, Chapter II presents a non-targeted analytical approach to evaluate broader chemical 
and pesticide exposure through the analysis of human urine. While Chapter I focused on the 
development of a targeted method to analyze DAP metabolites -common biomarkers of 
organophosphate pesticide exposure- in human urine, this chapter expands the scope to assess a 
wider range of exogenous compounds. A summary of the complete non-targeted analytical 
workflow used in this study is provided in section 7.4.  
7. Experimental 
7.1 Sampling Design  
For the non-targeted analysis, a subset of urine samples from the pilot study described in section 
4.1 was selected. Specifically, samples from participants PP1, PP2, PP3, PP4, PP5, PP6, PP7, and 
PP9 were analyzed. Participant PP8 was excluded due to insufficient sample volume.  
To investigate potential differences in the urine samples between the two dietary periods -baseline 
and intervention weeks- samples from two specific time points were chosen: day 3 of the baseline 
week and day 7 of the intervention week. These samples were chosen to maximize the difference 
between the two diets: day 3 of the baseline week represents the participant’s regular dietary 
patterns, before the dietary shift, while day 7 of the intervention week reflects the longest exposure 
to the plant-based diet possible. This sampling design aligns with the research question and 
hypothesis of this non-targeted study, focusing on potential changes in urinary chemical profiles 
as a result of dietary modifications. 
7.2 Extraction and Clean-up Methods 
The choice of sample preparation method plays a crucial role in non-targeted analysis. The goal is 
to extract as many compounds as possible, combined with sufficient clean-up, to avoid column 
clogging or contamination. In this work, two distinct sample preparation strategies were employed 
for the non-targeted analysis of human urine, aligning with the aim of evaluating how different 
chromatographic and extraction methods may broaden the range of chemical features detected, as 
outlined in Research Question 4 and Hypothesis 4 (section 2).  
==PAGE:70==
62 
 
One of the methods applied to the samples was the LLE-ACN-QuEChERS, as detailed in section 
5.2 (without DAPs spiking). This method was selected since it was intended to detect polar 
compounds in urine and yielded the most promising results for the analysis of polar dialkyl 
phosphate metabolites, as discussed in the Results and Discussion of Chapter I (section 6.2).  
The second extraction method applied to the same samples was the generic SPE-HLB method, 
also described in section 5.2. The only difference was the injection solvent: H2O-ACN (50:50, v/v) 
was used instead of 90:10, v/v, for better compatibility with the mobile phases used. HLB is the 
preferred solid phase for non-targeted screening because of its extensive cleaning capacity [75]. 
The method aimed to extract a wider variety of compounds, ranging from non-polar to slightly 
polar.  
Before extraction, all samples were centrifuged at 4°C and 5000 rpm for 10 minutes to remove 
solid particles and precipitate proteins.  
All individual samples, for both extraction methods, were prepared in duplicate to enable statistical 
analysis. Due to limited time and instrument availability, more extractions per sample could not 
be performed within the scope of this study. One of the replicates was injected twice, resulting in 
three replicate measurements per sample. No standards were spiked into the samples. Furthermore, 
pooled quality control (QC) samples were prepared separately for each of the methods by 
combining equal volumes (10 μL) of the final extracts from all participants (from both sampling 
days). These pooled samples were analyzed periodically through the LC-HRMS runs. Solvent 
blank samples were also included throughout the sequence to evaluate contamination in the 
system.  
7.3 Instrumental Methods 
Following sample preparation using the LLE-ACN-QuEChERS and SPE-HLB methods, aiming 
to extract highly polar and a range of non-polar to slightly polar compounds, respectively, the 
samples under examination were subjected to non-targeted analysis. This was performed using a 
Thermo Vanquish UHPLC coupled with a Thermo Scientific Orbitrap Exploris 120 mass 
spectrometer. 
==PAGE:71==
63 
 
7.3.1 Chromatographic Separation 
For samples prepared with the LLE-ACN-QuEChERS method, chromatographic separation was 
carried out using the HILIC method for DAP metabolites, described in section 5.3.1, focusing on 
highly polar analytes. This included the use of the Torus DEA column (2.1×100 mm, 1.7 μm), with 
the mobile phases being (A) an aqueous solution containing 1.2% formic acid and 10 mM 
ammonium formate, and (B) acetonitrile containing 0.5% formic acid. The same elution gradient 
program as described in section 5.3.1 for the Orbitrap system was applied.  
For the SPE-HLB prepared samples, RP chromatography was used to analyze a broader range of 
analytes, including non-polar compounds. Separation was achieved on an Acquity UPLC BEH 
C18 column (2.1 x 50 mm, 1.7 μm particle size), maintained at 30°C. The mobile phases were (A) 
Milli-Q water containing 0.1% formic acid and 5 mM ammonium formate solution and (B) 
methanol with 0.1% formic acid and 5 mM ammonium formate. The injection volume was 2 μL, 
and a flow rate of 0.3 mL/min was applied. The gradient elution program was as follows: initially, 
the column was equilibrated at 0% B eluent before injection. Upon injection, B rapidly increased 
to 40% within 0.1 min, then gradually increased to 70% over to 6 min. This was further increased 
to 100% B by 8 min, and held constant until 13 min. The proportion of B eluent was then lowered 
to 0% within 0.1 min and maintained at this level until a total run of 15 min. Figures showing the 
elution gradients for both HILIC and RP methods are presented in Appendix 6. 
7.3.2 Mass Spectrometry 
The Orbitrap mass spectrometer used for the non-targeted analysis of human urine was equipped 
with a HESI source, operating in both positive and negative ionization modes, for each 
chromatographic method (HILIC and RP), separately. For both HILIC and RP methods, samples 
were analyzed using data-dependent acquisition, with full scan followed by ddMS2 mode. This 
means that full scans were collected, and the most intense precursor ions above a specific threshold 
were selected for MS2 fragmentation.  
For the HILIC method, the general source parameters (spray voltage, sheath gas, auxiliary gas, 
sweep gas, ion transfer temperature, vaporizer temperature) were the same as those used in the 
targeted analysis of DAPs, as described in section 5.3.2. For the RP method, the spray voltage was 
set to 3.85 kV in positive mode and to 3.0 kV in negative mode, the ion transfer temperature was 
==PAGE:72==
64 
 
350°C, and the vaporizer temperature was 300°C. Sheath gas, auxiliary gas, and sweep gas flow 
rates were 35, 10, and arb. units, respectively.  
The Orbitrap operated in full-scan mode with a resolution of 60,000 over a mass range of m/z 100-
1,000. An inclusion list was applied to trigger MS/MS scans for selected precursor ions in both 
chromatographic methods. For ddMS2 acquisition, the main parameters were: resolution of 
15,000, isolation window of 2 m/z, for both methods, and HCD collision energies of 20, 40, 60 
(%) for HILIC, and 25, 50, 75 (%) for RP. Dynamic exclusion was set to 3 s after one occurrence 
(mass tolerance ± 10 ppm) for HILIC, and to 15 s after two occurrences (mass tolerance ± 5 ppm) 
for RP. 
The inclusion list employed for both HILIC and RP acquisitions contained hundreds of compounds 
of interest, mainly pesticides, described by their exact m/z values. Inclusion lists can improve the 
acquisition of MS2 spectra and are commonly used in non-targeted analyses to ensure that 
compounds relevant to the study are selected for MS2 fragmentation [96]. If no targets of the 
inclusion list were found, the instrument was set to perform dependent scans on the most intense 
ions, to preserve non-target sensitivity. In the HILIC method, the inclusion list was scheduled by 
retention time (0-17 min), whereas in RP, the inclusion list was unscheduled -though in practice, 
this had the same effect.  
7.3.3 Data Processing 
In this work, the Compound Discoverer 3.3.2.31 (CD) Software (ThermoFischer Scientific, 
Waltham, MA, United States) was used for non-targeted data analysis to extract molecular features 
and identify compounds. CD is a qualitative data-processing program that employs accurate mass 
data, isotope pattern matching, and fragment matching, combined with mass spectral library 
searches, to identify the structure of molecules present in the samples [120]. This is achieved by 
processing accurate-mass spectra from high-resolution mass spectrometers such as the Orbitrap 
MS.  
The CD program uses a method based on nodes to create processing workflows (processing 
methods) to analyze raw data files. Herein, the same workflow was used for the analysis of the 
different batches of samples, which included samples analyzed with the HILIC method in both 
negative and positive polarities, as well as with the RP method in both negative and positive 
polarities (four batches in total). Each batch of data included not only the human urine samples 
==PAGE:73==
65 
 
(after sample preparation with LLE-ACN-QuEChERS and SPE-HLB, for HILIC and RP, 
respectively) but also pooled QC samples and solvent blanks. 
The processing workflow employed was customized in-house, meaning that some parameters of a 
workflow that existed by default in the program were adjusted. A short description of it, as given 
in CD, can be found in Appendix 7. The workflow tree applied in this study is presented in Figure 
12, illustrating the nodes used for the present non-targeted analysis, as well as their connectivity. 
The different nodes shown in Figure 12 are described below, based on the CD User Guide for LC.  
A processing workflow always starts with the “Input Files” node, where raw data is inserted. 
Workflows that process MS data require the “Select Spectra” node afterwards, to read and filter 
the MS scan data in the raw files. The “Align Retention Times” node is used when multiple input 
files are present.   
Next, the “Detect Compounds” node is used to extract peaks, representing individual features, and 
for this purpose, it includes peak quality thresholds to filter out low-quality chromatographic 
peaks. In this case, the mass tolerance was set to 3 ppm, with a minimum peak intensity of 106, 
and a minimum of 5 scans per peak. Only the most intense isotope was used for each feature, and 
many ions were taken into consideration, including common and less common adducts. The 
detected unknown features across the input files were combined based on their molecular weight

==PAGE:74==

and retention times (“Group Compounds” node), and the tolerances used for matching mass peaks 
were 3 ppm and 0.1 min, respectively.  
The “Fill Gaps” node is used to find peaks that were detected in one of the input files but were 
missing from other input files in the file set, and it fills in missing data points. Furthermore, the 
“Mark Background Compounds” node marks features present in the sample blanks and filters them 
out if the sample/blank ratio is less than 5. 
Once features were detected and grouped, they were screened across various databases and 
libraries to annotate compounds.  The “Search ChemSpider” and “Search mzCloud” nodes refer 
to online databases, while the “Search Mass Lists” node matches features against in-house 
compound libraries. All searches were performed with a mass tolerance of 3 ppm. The "Search 
ChemSpider” node included searches in structure-based chemical databases, such as PubChem, 
FDA, and Thermo Fischer. The “Search mzCloud” node compared MS/MS spectra of detected 
features with those in the mzCloud database. In parallel, compound features were matched to 
public mass lists (e.g., EFS HRAM, Norman SUSDAT, FCCDB), as well as local mass lists 
focused on the detection of pesticides and their metabolites, including a list of accurate mass and 
formula information for the dialkyl phosphate metabolites. It is important to note that Mass Lists 
and ChemSpider searches rely on molecular ions and isotope distributions from full-scan MS data, 
whereas mzCloud searches depend on precursor ions and their corresponding fragment ions from 
MS/MS data.  
The “Apply Spectral Distance” node was used to assess spectral similarity between the 
experimental spectra and potential annotations.  The “Predict Compositions” node estimated the 
molecular formulas of unknown compounds. Finally, the “Assign Compound Annotations” node 
was employed to export information about compound names, formulas, and structures from the 
various data sources.  
All the abovementioned search processes and the execution of the workflow required at least half 
a day to be complete, even for one of the four batches (HILIC and RP, negative and positive 
modes). The result of the CD software search was a large file that contained thousands of potential 
candidate compounds present in the samples. Thus, the application of different customized filters 
was necessary to limit the number of suspect compounds before moving on to manual inspection 
of the peaks.  
==PAGE:75==
67 
 
The set of filters applied in all four studies is presented in Figure 13, as shown in the CD software. 
Since the filters used herein are connected by an ‘AND’ condition, only the features that meet all 
five criteria remain after filtering for further analysis.  
The filters applied were chosen to optimize data quality and confidence in compound annotation. 
Features detected in the background (in the solvent blank samples) were eliminated. Moreover, 
only features with MS/MS fragmentation data were chosen for further assessment. To ensure that 
the variability among the same sample groups was low, features with a group coefficient of 
variation (CV) higher than 30% were excluded. Furthermore, to make sure that only peaks with 
good shape are considered, features with peak ratings less than 4 (out of 10) were eliminated. 
Finally, only features that fully matched the mzCloud results were taken into consideration to 
ensure reliable identification. The final output of the processing, after the application of the filters, 
was then evaluated, as described in Results and Discussion.  
Regarding the confidence level of feature identification, there are several methods 
[121],[122],[123] to define it, with most of them being derived from Schymanski’s et al. [121] 
classification with some modifications. In this study, feature identification followed Schymanski’s 
guidelines by using high-resolution accurate mass and MS/MS fragmentation pattern matching 
against spectral libraries and databases, primarily mzCloud. Based on this, all reported compounds 
were identified at Level 2a, which corresponds to probable structure identification confirmed by 
library spectrum match. This level indicates a high degree of confidence; however, no features 
were assigned to Level 1 confidence, corresponding to a confirmed structure.

8. Results and Discussion 
In this section, the results of the non-targeted analysis of urine samples collected from eight 
individuals, on day 3 of the baseline week and day 7 of the intervention week, are presented. 
Following data processing and the application of relevant filters in the CD software (Experimental 
section 7.3.3), quality control (QC) assessments were performed. Statistical analysis and manual 
inspection of features were then conducted to identify compounds showing significant differences 
between the two sampling days. Particular attention was paid to pesticides and other chemicals, 
not naturally present in human urine. Before proceeding with the results, readers are advised to 
revisit the study flowchart in Figure 14 of section 7.4. 
8.1 Quality Control Evaluation  
As a first step in assessing data quality and reliability, pooled quality control (QC) urine samples 
were evaluated. A rapid, systematic check of data quality was performed by applying principal 
component analysis (PCA) to the complete dataset [124]. PCA is a widely used statistical technique 
for multivariate analysis that reduces data to a few characteristic dimensions for visualization of 
sample clustering and further analysis [125]. This is achieved by calculating a new set of variables 
(principal components), which are chosen in a way that the important information and variation in 
the data are retained.  
In this case, PCA was performed in the CD software by including specific postprocessing nodes 
in the data analysis workflow (Descriptive Analysis and Statistical Analysis). These nodes enabled 
the creation of scores plots based on principal component analysis. At first, the software generated 
a plot, showing the proportion of variance explained by each principal component, along with the 
cumulative proportion of variance, which helps assess how much of the total variability of the 
dataset is represented by the main components.  
The first two principal components (PC1 and PC2) were selected since they accounted for the 
largest variance in the data. Based on these components, PCA plots were generated, and the data 
points were labelled as QC (for the pooled QC samples) and as test samples (for the studied 
samples).  Figure 15 shows the PCA scores plot for data acquired in RP negative ionization mode. 
==PAGE:78==
70 
 
In Figure 15, the QC data points -highlighted with a red circle- cluster tightly and relatively close 
to the origin of the PCA plot, in comparison to the total variance of the projection. This indicates 
low variance, high analytical reproducibility [124], and stable instrument performance across the 
LC-HRMS runs. It ensures that data acquisition is reliable and that the data obtained can be used 
for subsequent statistical analysis and compound identification.  
Ideally, the QCs should cluster at the origin of the PCA plot [124], and any deviation from the 
origin can usually be attributed to unavoidable variability, due to pipetting errors or sample weight 
discrepancies during the preparation of the pooled samples. As long as the QCs remain tightly 
grouped, relative to the observed dispersion of the urine samples tested, then the data can be 
considered of high analytical quality [124].

8.2 Evaluation of Creatinine across Sampling Days 
As mentioned in the Theoretical part (section 3.1), the hydration status of the individuals at the 
time of urine collection can affect the measured concentrations of metabolites and other 
compounds. Creatinine is a suitable marker for urine dilution, since it is typically excreted at a 
relatively constant rate in healthy individuals. 
In this study, and prior to compound identification, creatinine levels were examined to determine 
whether urine dilution was different across the sampling days, and whether creatinine correction 
was needed. This was done by evaluating the marker’s level across the urine samples collected on 
day 3 of the baseline week and day 7 of the intervention week, from each individual. The feature 
corresponding to creatinine was detected in all samples analyzed with RP chromatography in 
positive ionization mode, and it was identified using both accurate mass (identified at m/z 
114.0659) and retention time (identified at 0.51 min).  
Chromatographic peak quality was manually inspected among the different samples. 
Subsequently, statistical analysis for creatinine was performed, using the same parameters as for 
the differential compound analysis that follows -namely, a log₂ fold change threshold of 1 and a p-
value < 0.05. No features corresponding to creatinine were within these thresholds. Visual 
inspection of the volcano plots was also performed, in which creatinine was not among the 
significantly changing features. Therefore, the results showed no significant differences in 
creatinine levels between the two sampling days for any of the individuals. 
Since no statistically significant difference of creatinine was observed and urine concentration was 
consistent across sampling days, no creatinine correction was applied. These findings support that 
the differences in chemical features observed among the sampling days can be attributed to actual 
changes in the compounds' concentration in the sample and not to variabilities due to physiological 
dilution of the compounds, since urine dilution status seems to be uniform across both days. 
8.3 Differential Analysis and Feature Selection  
Since the present study aims to identify differences in urinary chemical features between two 
sampling days for different individuals, differential statistical analysis was performed on the 
processed LC-HRMS data. For this purpose, volcano plots were generated separately for each 
==PAGE:80==
72 
 
participant, across all analytical conditions (HILIC and RP, in both positive and negative ionization 
modes), using the CD software. 
Volcano plots are scatter plots commonly used in statistical analyses and metabolomics to visualize 
the relationship between the magnitude of change (log2 fold change) and the statistical significance 
(- log10 p-value) of each feature between two conditions. In this case, each point of the plot 
represents a molecular feature, and those with significant differences are likely compounds 
affected by dietary changes and can therefore be considered potential candidates for further 
investigation.  
In the current volcano plots, features from urine samples collected on day 3 of the baseline week 
and on day 7 of the intervention week were compared. Features were considered significantly 
different if they met both of the following criteria: a p-value < 0.05 and an absolute log2 fold 
change > 1, which corresponds to at least a twofold change between the intensities of the feature 
among the two days.

8.4 Compound Identification 
Herein, preliminary experiments were conducted using full-scan (FS) acquisition combined with 
all-ion fragmentation (AIF) for non-targeted analysis. This approach provided a large amount of 
fragmentation data; however, the lack of precursor ion selection led to highly complex MS/MS 
spectra. These spectra contained many overlapping fragment ions from co-eluting compounds, 
making it hard to confirm the presence of features with high confidence.  As a result, AIF was not 
used for final compound identification in this work. All compound annotations presented in the 
following section were based on DDA and were confirmed by accurate mass and MS/MS library 
matching. This allowed for probable structure identification at Level 2a confidence, following 
Schymanski’s classification, as defined in the Experimental section (7.3.3). The challenges of AIF 
in non-targeted analyses have also been documented in other studies [126],[127], where DDA or 
other strategies were preferred for compound annotation, instead.

Chlorpyrifos (that give 1-Naphtol) are considered commonly applied pesticides [129], and this 
likely explains their frequent presence in the urine samples.

presence in the samples collected at the end of the intervention week (green arrows), particularly 
N-(2,6-Dimethylphenyl)-N-(methoxyacetyl) alanine, Didemethylisoproturon, and Isoquinoline.

carry pesticide residues when conventionally grown, as herein.

On the contrary, some features exhibited decreased abundance at the end of the intervention week 
(red arrows), results that cannot be connected to the dietary change. This highlights the variability 
in exposure patterns across individuals.  
Notably, although a targeted mass list for dialkyl phosphate (DAP) metabolites was included, none 
were detected in any of the studied samples. This may be due to their low concentrations in urine 
or due to insufficient ionization.  
On average, the presence of pesticide-related compounds was increased in the samples collected 
on day 7 of the intervention week, as indicated by the greater number of increases than decreases 
(31 green vs. 24 red arrows). While this might suggest a potential correlation between a higher 
content of plant-based commodities in the diet and higher pesticide exposure, the presence of many 
pesticide-related compounds in lower amounts at the end of the intervention week indicates that 
no clear trend can be established between diet and exposure within the scope of this study.  
This trend may become more apparent with a larger sample size from more individuals. 
Additionally, the extent to which participants adhere to the guidelines should be considered. As 
shown in the previous pilot study [38], not all participants increased their intake of fruit and 
vegetables during week two, despite an overall increase in consumption of such commodities. 
Ensuring compliance with the guidelines and ideally standardizing diets across participants would 
probably lead to less variability in the results. 
In conclusion, while this study did not establish a clear trend between a more plant-based diet and 
pesticide exposure, it demonstrated the presence of pesticide-related compounds in human urine 
samples, confirming that exposure to pesticides is occurring. These results emphasize the 
importance of non-targeted HRMS for monitoring food or environmental exposures and highlight 
the need for large, well-designed studies to understand the impact of diet on pesticide exposure, in 
a better way.

As demonstrated in Figure 17, endogenous metabolites constituted the largest category of 
compounds detected, across all methods. This is expected in urine samples, since metabolites and 
other physiological compounds are naturally present there. However, the focus οf this study is on 
exogenous compounds that are not naturally present in the body and give information for exposure 
assessment. Among them, therapeutics and prescription drugs were the most frequently detected 
ones, across all the analytical methods, followed by industrial chemicals. Personal care product 
ingredients and pesticide-related compounds were also commonly detected.  
Furthermore, an interesting observation during initial compound annotation for all the different 
individuals among the two time points, was that a greater number of features were detected with 
RP chromatography compared to HILIC (approximately 400 features with each RP method, 290 
features with HILIC in positive mode and 230 features with HILIC in negative mode). This might 
be attributed either to the fact that RP is more suited for the analysis of a wider range of compounds, 
from moderate polar to non-polar, or to differences in extraction efficiency between the two 
protocols.  
Further investigation is needed to better understand if variations in detected features are due to 
chromatographic methods or extraction protocols. The observation that some features were only 
detected with RP and others only with HILIC notes the importance of using different and ideally 
complementary analytical approaches to detect the broadest possible range of compounds present 
in the samples. 
8.4.4 Summary and Method Evaluation  
In summary, a non-targeted LC-HRMS workflow was employed to assess chemical exposure in 
human urine samples from different individuals, using both HILIC and RP chromatography in 
positive and negative ionization modes, and Compound Discoverer software for data processing. 
The analysis enabled the annotation of both endogenous and exogenous compounds, with a focus 
on the latter. Exogenous compounds included pesticides, pharmaceuticals, industrial chemicals, 
personal care product ingredients, additives and extractables, all annotated at Level 2a confidence, 
based on Schymanski’s classification. 
Pesticides and their metabolites were detected across all participants, and although a slightly higher 
number of detections occurred at the end of the plant-based intervention week, a clear exposure 
pattern linked to diet could not be established. Other exogenous compounds were also frequently 
==PAGE:91==
83 
 
detected across individuals, time points and methods, demonstrating the complexity of exposure 
assessments. As expected, endogenous metabolites were the most frequently detected class of 
compounds across all methods.  
These findings align with previous studies employing non-targeted analysis of urine for assessing 
human exposure. For example, Rubén Gil-Solsona et. al. [130], reported the detection of similar 
to this work compound classes, such as pharmaceuticals, plastic additives, personal care products, 
and pesticides, by using different analytical methods. Additionally, R. Nijssen et al. [131], aimed 
to gain insight into the relationship between dietary intake of pesticides and urinary excretion. This 
showed that different pesticides and pesticide metabolites were present in urine, however it was 
hard to obtain clear relationships between dietary intake and exposure. This might be due to 
multiple exposure routes, such as inhalation, dermal absorption, or previous dietary exposure, that 
can complicate the interpretation of compounds in urine related only to food consumption.  
Despite the informative results obtained, this study has some limitations, particularly when 
evaluated against best practices for non-targeted analysis (NTA) such as those summarized from 
Peter et al., 2021 [132] and Broadhurst et al. [124]. Several aspects of the analytical workflow 
were well aligned with the practices recommended. For example, pooled QC samples were injected 
to monitor the instrument’s performance. The respective PCA showed tight clustering of QCs, 
indicating system stability and reproducibility. Also, solvent blanks were included to evaluate 
possible contamination and background subtraction was applied during data processing. 
Additionally, the use of both RP and HILIC chromatography, with positive and negative ionization 
modes, enabled broad chemical coverage. Statistical and chemometric analysis were performed 
using PCA and volcano plots, and compound annotation was performed with confidence Level 2a, 
as proposed by Schymanski et al.  
However, some important aspects of best practices for NTA workflows were not fully included. 
One notable limitation of the present work was the absence of analytical standards. Neither 
isotopically labeled standards or spiking standards were used, preventing compound identification 
with Level 1 confidence, and limiting the ability to perform quantification. This limitation is 
relevant, since several of the compounds annotated - mainly pesticides and industrial chemicals- 
are known to pose health risks. As a result, while the study indicates the presence of potentially 
hazardous compounds, it does not assess their concentration, and a risk assessment study cannot 
==PAGE:92==
84 
 
be constructed. Future studies should aim to include reference standards, to improve identification 
confidence and enable quantitation.  
Other limitations of the study were that samples were not prepared in many replicates, due to time 
constraints. Only duplicate samples were available, and just one was injected twice. Furthermore, 
the injection of the samples was not randomized, increasing the risk that observed differences 
between samples might not be due to actual chemical variation, but due to instrumental drift. 
Finally, the study was also run in single batches and per method, so corrections for the different 
batches between runs could not be applied. These constraints should be addressed in future studies 
to enhance reproducibility and robustness, and have studies that completely follow best practices 
for NTA.  
Finally, another important limitation in most non-targeted workflows is related to the sample 
preparation [75] stage. As discussed in section 3.3, in non-targeted analysis of complex biological 
matrices like urine, achieving both broad chemical coverage and high sensitivity is hard. This is 
because extraction methods aim to retain as many compounds as possible, but at the same time 
they might extract other matrix components.  These matrix components might be interfering 
substances that can cause matrix effects or lead to false positives. The trade-off between the 
sufficient clean-up of the sample and the extraction of a wide range of compounds, without losing 
important features, is one of the challenges of non-targeted workflows.

9. Conclusions 
This Master’s thesis focused on various aspects of human exposure to pesticides and other 
chemical contaminants, through the analysis of human urine, using liquid chromatography coupled 
to mass spectrometry. The study employed both targeted and non-targeted analytical approaches 
to provide useful insights into both method development for the analysis of pesticide-related 
metabolites, as well as into broader chemical exposure. Each chapter focused on distinct but 
complementary aspects.  
In Chapter I, the focus was on the development and optimization of an analytical method to 
determine four dialkyl phosphate metabolites of organophosphate pesticides (DMTP, DMDTP, 
DEP, DETP) in human urine. Although the initial hypothesis (H1) was that a unified LC-MS 
method could be developed to analyze both DAPs and glyphosate/AMPA at the same time, results 
showed that the two groups of compounds had too distinct chromatographic behaviors under the 
tested HILIC/WAX conditions to be analyzed in a single method. As such, the hypothesis was not 
confirmed. Instead, for each group of analytes, separate chromatographic methods were found to 
be suitable, with Method B (1.2% formic acid and 10 mM ammonium formate in H2O, and 0.5% 
formic acid in ACN, as the mobile phases) and the Torus DEA column being selected for DAPs.  
The second hypothesis (H2), that optimization of sample preparation protocols will lead to 
acceptable recoveries of DAPs, was also not fully confirmed in the context of this work. While a 
plethora of extraction methods were evaluated, none of them fully met the SANTE 11312/2021 v2 
criteria for all analytes. Among the methods, an LLE protocol incorporating QuEChERS salts 
yielded the most promising results for three (DMTP, DMDTP, DETP) out of the four DAPs, 
achieving relatively high recoveries and acceptable repeatability. This could serve as a basis for 
further optimization. Due to time constraints, no other extraction methods were explored, and full 
method validation was not conducted. 
In Chapter II, a non-targeted LC-HRMS workflow was applied to urine samples collected during 
a dietary intervention study, to assess how a short-term shift towards a plant-rich diet could 
influence chemical exposure profiles. Hypothesis 3 was partially confirmed, meaning that while 
some pesticides and pesticide-related compounds were more abundant in the samples after dietary 
intervention, no consistent trend could be established, potentially due to the limited number of 
==PAGE:94==
86 
 
participants in the study or due to variability in dietary adherence and metabolism. Hypothesis 4 
was confirmed, since the combination of different extraction protocols and chromatographic 
methods led to the detection of a larger number of chemical features, with RP chromatography 
yielding the highest number of detections. 
Moreover, this non-targeted study enabled the annotation of a wide range of exogenous 
compounds, such as pesticide-related compounds, pharmaceuticals, industrial chemicals, and 
ingredients of personal care products. These findings demonstrate the applicability of non-targeted 
HRMS analyses for comprehensive exposure assessments. However, some limitations must be 
acknowledged, including practices that do not align with the guidelines for good practices in NTA 
workflows. For example, the absence of standards limited compound identification to Schymanski 
Level 2a, and quantification was not possible. Other limitations include the lack of replicates 
during sample preparation and the absence of randomization during injection, mainly due to time 
constraints. 
Despite the limitations, this thesis provides valuable insights into the field of human biomonitoring 
and exposure assessment. It highlights the analytical challenges in determining polar pesticide 
metabolites like DAPs in urine, and the broad applicability of non-targeted analysis in identifying 
chemical exposures, compared to what is typically monitored in targeted studies.  
Future work should focus on efforts to further optimize and validate the extraction method for 
DAP metabolites, ensuring compliance with the validation guidelines, and expanding the scope to 
DMP and DEDTP, which were excluded in this study, due to unavailability of the standards. 
Furthermore, for a better understanding of the effect of plant-based diets on human pesticide 
exposure, a larger number of individuals should be included during the sampling, dietary 
compliance across participants needs to be ensured, and exposure data with dietary and behavioral 
information needs to be included to trace sources of chemical exposure.

having R² values > 0.99, indicating excellent linearity.

Appendix 7: Short Description of the CD Workflow Employed for NTA 
 
Name: Food Research w Stats Unknown ID w Online and Local Database Searches_NTS_3ppm 
Description: Untargeted food research ID workflow with statistics: Detect and identify unknown 
compounds with differential analysis. 
Performs retention time alignment, unknown compound detection, and compound grouping across 
all samples. Predicts elemental compositions for all compounds, and hides chemical background 
(using Blank samples).  Identifies compounds using mzCloud (ddMS2 and/or DIA), ChemSpider 
(exact mass or formula), and local database searches against Mass Lists (exact mass with or 
without RT).  Performs spectral similarity search against mzCloud for compounds with ddMS2. 
Applies mzLogic to rank order structure candidates from ChemSpider and mass list matches. 
Applies spectral distance scoring to ChemSpider and mass list matches. Fills gaps by redetecting 
peaks by the Fill Gaps node. Performs differential analysis on detected compounds.
