Executive Summary
The objective of this work has been to study the feasibility of applying Machine Learn-
ing techniques for the estimation of added wave resistance. This has been achieved by
analysing the performance of different architectures of Neural Network algorithms on
three main systems of increasing complexity.
The main approach used for modelling the added wave resistance by neural network algo-
rithms is based on the extraction of latent patterns that determine the relation between
speed, geometry of the hull, heading angles, etc. and the added wave resistance. This
approach is defined as a supervised regression problem. Essentially, the neural network
model is trained on a dataset generated by different theories and for a set of input param-
eters chosen with the help of feature selection. The trained model is then able to compute
an estimation, prediction, of the target variable based on the relation between the input
parameters and the target variable extracted from the dataset.
The first two systems studied correspond to two datasets generated by semi-empirical
theories: the SHOPERA expression and the STAWave-2 transfer function. The chosen
neural network architectures have proved to be an excellent method for modelling these
systems with 2 and 5 input parameters, respectively. For the SHOPERA function dataset,
the mean added wave resistance has been modelled as a function of length between per-
pendicular and significant wave height, ¯RAW,pred = f(X) = f(Lpp, Hs). For the dataset
generated with the results from the STAWave-2 transfer function, the non-dimensional
added wave resistance is modelled as a function of length between perpendiculars, wave
frequency, mean draught, velocity and breadth, RAW,pred = f(X) = f(Lpp, ω, TM, VS, B),
respectively.
The third system analysed in this work is based on numerical calculations for the added
wave resistance based on Strip Theory, Salvesen et al. (1970), and Salvesen’s method,
Salvesen (1974). The model trained with the IShip database makes use of the added wave
resistance results used in Martinsen (2016) and provides a transfer function that maps
the non-dimensional added wave resistance as function of length-to-breadth, breadth-to-
draught and length-to-draught ratios, heading angle, Froude number and non-dimensional
wave frequency respectively, RAW,pred = f(X) = f(Lpp/B, B/TM, Lpp/TM, β, Fr, ˆω). The
model’s predictions are in good agreement with direct calculations and perform as accu-
rately as the previous Design Tool in general, Martinsen (2016).
In addition, this model has been extended and trained with results from a total of 6
different geometries and 20 scaling combinations, obtaining a transfer function of the
form RAW,pred = f(X) = f(Lpp/B, Lpp/TM, β, Fr, ˆω, CB, f) . The final model is able to
predict the added wave resistance for different conditions of 0 ≤Fr ≤0.25, 90 ≤β ≤180
[◦] and for ship geometries on the range 4 ≤Lpp/B ≤8, 8 ≤Lpp/TM ≤40, block
coefficient 0.52 ≤CB ≤0.84 and slenderness coefficient 3.36 ≤f ≤8.51. This extended
model shows good agreement with direct calculations and a relatively good match with
experimental results, limited by the accuracy of the numerical solver.

From a practical point of view, the neural network compiles all the information provided by
more computationally expensive calculations (numerical calculations from Strip theory)
to a model that is executed in less than 0.001 s and size less than 100 kB, based on
non-compute-intense calculation (matrix multiplications and activation functions).
In
addition, these estimations do not require the complete hull geometry but only the main
particulars of the ship and operational conditions. Furthermore, it does not require to
interpolate the result from an existing database, eliminating the need of accessing these
reference curves for each prediction.
The supervised regression problem of modelling added wave resistance is greatly influ-
enced by the database that the model is trained with. The predictions of the models can
only perform as accurately as the numerical calculations used for populating the database.
Throughout the study, the main tasks carried out have focused on the study of the ac-
curacy of the prediction of the models in comparison to direct calculation. In addition, a
comparison with experimental results is performed for three hull geometries and different
velocities and heading angles: KVLCC 2, RIOS and ULYSSES.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Overview
Ship design is greatly influenced by efforts to reduce the fuel consumption and conse-
quently CO2 emissions.
This approach is motivated by the EEDI, Energy Efficiency
Design Index developed by the International Maritime Organization, IMO, which rep-
resents a minimum energy efficiency level per capacity mile for different ship type and
size segments. The EEDI has been adopted by the Marine Environmental Protection
Committee (MEPC) in order to ensure energy efficiency of ships resulting in mandatory
measures presented in the resolution MEPC.203(62). As a consequence, this resolution
has motivated the ship industry to work in order to meet these efficiency requirements
which present some technical challenges:
For slow steaming ships, there are two main approaches to be in compliance with MEPC
resolution: to increase the speed of the ship by installing auxiliary wind or solar powered
systems which does not increase installed power or, to reduce the installed power and
design speed. Reducing power and speed represents a feasible option since the speed of
the ship is reduced at a smaller rate than the power needed to achieve it.
The latter option has been established as the main approach of the industry in terms
of easy implementation without increasing extensively the cost. However, this approach
also brings the necessity to take into consideration the safety of the ship. When reducing
the installed power on the ship, the performance on adverse weather conditions can be
jeopardized and could potentially cause some reduction in the manoeuvrability of the
ship.
In this situation of adverse weather condition and low speed, the main player affecting
the resistance experienced by the ship is the added wave resistance. For ships with high
design speed, a service allowance of 15-30% is added to the power curves for calm water
resistance. However, this approach is not feasible when considering EEDI requirements
for slow steaming ships.
This critical issue is considered of great importance by the shipping industry and regu-
lators. In this framework, a project such as Energy Efficient Safe Ship Operation (SH-
OPERA), funded by EU-FP7, has been developed to help the industry match these re-
quirements and facilitate the ship designing tasks for slow steaming ships.
Based on this work, the current needs of the industry and the increasing concern about
CO2 (materialized in EEDI index), other works from DTU have dived into the challenge
of quantifying the effect of the added wave resistance: Nielsen et al. (2019), Amini-Afshar
(2014).
The estimation of the contribution of the added wave resistance on ships generally requires
computationally expensive calculations and high-definition ship geometries. However, the

hull lines of the ships are not generally available in the research community and it exists a
necessity of a generalized model applicable for different ship types, heading angles and ship
velocities. In this framework, Martinsen (2016) and Nielsen (2015) present a Design Tool
that interpolates the added wave resistance curves based on the three main dimensions
of the ship as input: length, draught and breadth.
In addition, the applications of different Machine Learning techniques have increased
exponentially during the past decade. This development is motivated by the increas-
ing amount of data available from sensorization, IoT (Internet of Things) and the con-
tinuous improvement of algorithms related to pattern recognition, combined with more
cost-efficient computing power.
Let’s imagine a large amount of data generated by an unknown model for a set of known
inputs. Assuming the results of the unknown model are smooth and continuous with
respect to the inputs, by analysing the output, the inputs could be inferred. The more
data is analysed (set of inputs and output), the more accurate this inverse mapping can
be. Machine Learning techniques such as Neural Networks provide a powerful tool to
extract this latent behaviour, or inverse mapping, from these datasets.
For this current study, these algorithms can model the output (added wave resistance)
based on inputs (ship’s dimensions, heading angle and velocity) and provide an accurate
estimation eliminating the more time-consuming task of producing these results by stan-
dard methodologies such as Strip Theory, 3D potential methods or CFD (Computational
fluid Mechanics). Furthermore, Machine Learning techniques are a great tool for feature
selection by analysing the correlation of each input feature with the output, providing an
insight into the relative influence of each parameter on the modelled system.
Some examples of Neural Network algorithms used in maritime-related research are Mak
and Düz (2019), where the relative wave direction is estimated from ship motions by using
Long Short-Term Memory Recurrent Neural Networks (LSTM and RNN), and Cheng
et al. (2019), where sea state estimation is performed by using a model that combines
LSTM, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) and Fast Fourier Transform (FFT), so-
called SeaStateNet.
Inspired by these works, considering the increasing development in Machine Learning
algorithms and the trends towards data-driven approaches in industry, this Thesis is
proposed.

Calm-water resistance corresponds to the resistance experienced by the ship advancing
at a constant speed in calm water conditions. This component of the resistance can be
split into two types: viscous or frictional resistance and wave-making resistance. For a
practical approach, the components of the resistance for calm-water can be computed by
interpolating speed-power curves estimated from previously conducted model test ITTC-
1978 Methods, ITTC (2011).
Added resistance can be expressed as the ‘extra’ contributions apart from calm-water
resistance, Nielsen et al.
(2019).
These terms are divided into wind resistance, rud-
der correction resistance, shallow-water effect and added wave resistance mainly. Other
components of the resistance exist but they are not considered in this study.
Wind resistance depends on the direction and relative wind speed as well as the windage
area of the ship. In order to obtain an estimation of this effect on the total resistance ex-
perienced by the ship, two main approaches can be followed: Interpolation/extrapolation
with scaled-model tests and numerical simulations or CFD.
Shallow-water resistance is the extra contribution of the resistance experienced by a
ship advancing in shallow-water. This contribution has been widely studied and quantified
in different works such as Lackenby (1963).
In order to maintain a straight-line course due to the net force produced by wind and
waves to the ship, a correction in the rudder is needed. These corrections increase the
longitudinal resistance on the ship and is referred to as rudder correction resistance.

Added wave resistance is a measured resistance that quantifies the wave-related terms
of the resistance. This contribution is explained as a result of three phenomena: Wave
reflection, i.e.
diffraction of the incident waves by the ship, which translates into an
increased resistance due to the energy involved in this process. Ship motions, particularly
the vertical motions of heave and pitch. Interference of the incident waves and waves
generated by the ship when heaving and pitching referred to as ‘drift’.
In order to determine the added wave resistance, the resulting influence of the waves on the
ship motions has to be determined. The ship motions can be computed by using 3D panels
methods, CFD or Strip Theory. 3D panel methods are based on Green’s function and
potential theory to determine the velocity potential. CFD simulations are used to compute
the ship motions by solving the Navier Stokes equations applying the FVM (Finite Volume
Method).
In general, panel methods and CFD simulations are computationally more
expensive than Strip Theory, Pedersen (2000). Due to the availability of a strip theory
solver at DTU, IShip, and its version in Matlab, DTU Solver, Strip Theory has been
chosen to compute the ship motions and will be further explained in Chapter 3.

resistance: added wave resistance, Raw, and wind resistance, Rwi, are presented, where
typically the wind-related resistance is smaller than wave-related resistance.

3
A Review of Strip Theory
To compute the added wave resistance using the methods that will be presented in Chapter
4, it is necessary to determine the behaviour of the ship when interacting with external
forces exerted by the waves. In order to estimate the ship motions produced by these
interactions, the Five Degrees of Freedom of Linear Strip Theory is used. The concept
of strip theory was first introduced in Korvin-Kroukovsky and Jacobs (1957) and further
extended and studied in other works such as Jacobs (1958) and Salvesen et al. (1970).
In this Chapter, an overview of the Five Degrees of Freedom of Linear Strip Theory is
given and the general concepts of the calculation procedure are outlined, for a detailed
description see Salvesen et al. (1970).
The strip theory presented makes use of the following assumption:
• The forward speed of the ship is relatively low, Froude number Fr < 0.4.
• The hull is slender with only small gradual changes in the form geometry in the
longitudinal direction and has lateral symmetry.
• The frequency of encounter should not be too low or too high i.e. not too long or
too short waves.
• The viscous effects are disregarded for the fluid motions to be assumed irrotational,
allowing the use of potential flow theory. The viscous damping effects are small for
the vertical motion and thus justify this assumption.
• The steady perturbation potential and unsteady potential are assumed small with
respect to the undisturbed free-stream potential.
• The ship motion is considered linear.
• The geometric hull form above the free surface level is not considered.
In Strip Theory, the hull of the ship is divided into sections or strips by planes per-
pendiculars to the x-axis. The pressure forces and hydrodynamic forces on the hull are
computed using 2D potential flow theory. The sign convention and nomenclature for the
ship motions are presented in Figure 3.1.

4
Modelling of Added Wave
Resistance
Added wave resistance is defined as the mean contribution of the unsteady force exerted
by the encountered waves on the ship as well as the radiated energy due to the ship
motions. The estimation of the added wave resistance is a higher-order problem that
requires some advanced and robust methods to compute.
Two main distinctions can be applied when classifying the methods used for the estimation
of the added wave resistance, namely far-field and near-field methods. The basic difference
between the methods is whether one applied direct pressure integration over the hull (near-
field), or either momentum or energy conservation (far-field). For the far-field methods,
it is worth noting Maruo’s far-field method from Maruo (1960), strip method from Joosen
(1966) and Newman (1978), and radiated energy method from Gerritsma and Beukelman
(1972) and Salvesen (1978). For the near-field method, three approaches are worth noting:
asymptotic approach in short waves from Faltinsen (1980), Green function methods from
Grue and Biberg (1993) and Ye and Hsiung (1997) and Rankine panel methods from Kim
and Kim (2011).
For this study, two of the aforementioned methods are used: Salvesen’s radiated energy
for the long wavelengths and Faltinsen’s asymptotic method for the short wavelengths.
Both of these methods have been studied in depth and implemented in previous studies,
Nielsen (2015) and Martinsen (2016). The motions of the ship are computed the software
package IShip, and its version on Matlab, the DTU Solver, available from DTU. Both
solvers use Strip Theory for the task, Salvesen et al. (1970). These methods have proved
accurate and in good coherence with experimental results in Seo et al. (2013).

Machine Learning Overview
5.1
Introduction
Machine Learning is a subfield of computer science focused on the study of pattern recog-
nition by exploring algorithms that can learn from data and make predictions. These
algorithms operate by building a model from example inputs to make data-driven predic-
tions or decisions, instead of following predefined program instructions or rules, Bishop
(2006).
Machine learning tasks are classified into three main categories depending on the nature
of the problem to be modelled and signals or feedback available from the modelled system,
Russell and Norvig (2002):
• Supervised learning: The learning algorithms are presented with examples of
inputs and desired outputs. The goal of these techniques is to learn a general rule
that maps inputs to outputs.
• Unsupervised learning: Unlabelled data is given to the algorithms. The goal is
to infer the underlying structure of the data or hidden relational patterns.
• Reinforcement learning: Models are trained in a defined environment where the
goal is to achieve a certain outcome by providing a reward when the desired outcome
is partially achieved.
Alongside this classification based on the nature of the learning or training of the model,
another classification of machine learning tasks can be defined based on the type of output
of the model:
• Classification: The task in this type of problem is to assign a predefined class to
an unseen input. For example, classifying between a yacht, container ship or tanker
based on the main particulars of the ship. This is a supervised learning problem
because the model is trained with labelled data of ships, in which a set of main
particulars (inputs) corresponds to a class (output: yacht, container, tanker).
• Regression: A supervised machine learning problem in which, instead of classifying
by discrete outputs (classification), the output is continuous. For example, given
the main particulars of a ship, predict the added wave resistance for a range of wave
frequencies.
• Clustering: This task consists of grouping a set of inputs into undefined groups
or clusters. Unlike classification, the groups are not defined beforehand, which is
an example of unsupervised learning. For example, given a dataset of ship main
particulars, by applying a clustering technique, three main groups or clusters can be
obtained based on the similarity of the relation and value of their main particulars.
Machine Learning Applied to the Estimation of Added Resistance
21
==PAGE:32==
• Dimensionality Reduction: Another example of unsupervised learning is dimen-
sionality reduction. In this task, the inputs are simplified by mapping them onto a
lower-dimensional space. Given three inputs such as length, draught and breadth, a
dimensionality reduction algorithm could reduce these three input parameters into
two (applying a weighted operation on the original inputs). These techniques are
based on the influence that each input has on the output. For example, the output
could be predicted with the same accuracy by using ratios of these inputs, it could
be compared to unsupervised dimensional analysis.
Inside the Machine Learning framework, we can define another branch named Deep Learn-
ing. This subset of Machine Learning attempts to model high-level abstractions of data by
using complex structures, Schmidhuber (2015). The most well-known architecture used
in this framework is the feed-forward Neural Network or simply Neural Network (NN).

connected nodes or perceptrons. These perceptrons are arranged in layers, distributed in
three types: Input layer for the input parameters, hidden-layer for the calculations and
output layer which computes the final output or prediction.

Overfitting and Cross-validation
A common problem regarding the evaluation of the performance of the model is the so-
called overfitting. It is relatively likely to define a model whose architecture is of a higher
order of complexity than the problem requires to be modelled. As an example, in Figure
5.7, a case of overfitting similar to what it may occur in a neural network is presented. In

values of y follow a parabolic curve of order 3; On one hand, by modelling the system with
a polynomial regression of order 1, the behaviour of the system is not accurately captured,
the complexity of the model is low and is a case of underfitting. On the other hand, by
modelling the system by applying a polynomial regression of order 10, the accuracy of the
prediction is higher, at the point where the prediction matches the real value perfectly.
The latter is a case of overfitting, and this model will perform poorly when predicting a
new set of points.

In this Chapter, we will approach the problem of modelling added wave resistance by
applying a set of algorithms used for Machine Learning named Neural Networks.
A
Neural Network framework provides the necessary algorithms to recognize patterns in
a dataset and make a prediction based on the inputs of the problem. This technique
can be considered as multiple linear regression modelling of a function with several input
parameters and one target output to which the regression is applied. For this specific
study, the input parameters that are considered are the main characteristic of the hull
and the sea state.
The target output to be predicted in this case is the added wave
resistance, in the form of the transfer function RAW(ω) or directly the total mean added
wave resistance ¯RAW(ω).
The modelling of artificially generated data based on the SHOPERA function and the
STAwave-2 transfer function serves the purpose of introducing the methodology followed
in the study. Modelling an artificially generated dataset with a known function does not
add value to the prediction of the result but provides a valuable insight into considerations
associated with this data-driven approach. However, in the third section of this Chapter,
the modelling will be based on the IShip dataset.
As explained in Chapter 4, the IShip dataset has been generated using Strip Theory and
Salvesen’s and Faltinsen’s method for a total of 9 combinations of scaled ship hulls. In
the work carried out by Nielsen (2015) and Martinsen (2016), this dataset has been used
to create a model based on linear interpolation of the different results obtained by these
numerical simulations to provide an estimation of the added wave resistance based on the
main hull characteristic: the ratio of length between perpendiculars and breadth, Lpp/B,
the ratio breadth and draught, B/TM, the ratio of length between perpendiculars and
draught, Lpp/TM, Froude number, Fr, wave frequency, ω and heading angle, β. In this
third section, an automatic interpolation of this dataset is performed by a Neural Network
model without the need of the original (large) dataset, highlighting the advantages of this
approach and setting the scene for the next Chapters.
These advantages become clear when the logic between input and output is not defined by
a known function i.e. in Numerical Simulation of different ships, or when the relation be-
tween the results for different inputs are not accurately captured by a linear interpolation.
In addition, it simplifies the process of including extra parameters that can potentially
have an influence on the result of the target value.

Hyper-parameter Tuning
Another important part of deep-learning algorithms is hyper-parameter tuning.
This
technique provides an insight into how the performance is affected by modifying the
different parameters that control the training and architecture. In this case, an analysis
of the influence of the learning rate α (defined in Chapter 5) is performed. In Figure
6.10, it can be observed how for a medium learning rate, the performance of the model
generally increases and achieves faster convergence. This can be explained by the gradient
descent, where the finding of the global minimum of the loss function is influenced by the
size of the steps taken in each iteration. By using too big or too small steps, the profile
of the loss function cannot be captured appropriately and a local minimum can impede
further reduction of the loss function. To avoid this situation, not only the learning rate
can be set to a constant value but also to an adaptive learning rate.

6.3.1
Description of the Database
In this third example, the dataset to be modelled is the IShip dataset. This dataset has
been developed in Nielsen (2015) and Martinsen (2016) by combining the IShip simulations
for added wave resistance based on Strip Theory, Salvesen’s and Faltinsen’s methods and
WAMIT (1999) for zero forward-speed. The database consists of the non-dimensional
added wave resistance result obtained from the simulations performed in 6 ship geometries
for 9 different scales. These 6 geometries belong to two main categories: Bulk/tanker ships
(with high block coefficient CB ≈0.8) and container ships (low block coefficient CB ≈0.6).
In both works, the DTU method or DTU Design Tool is presented and validated. As
explained in Chapter 4, this method performs a series of linear interpolations for the
added resistance curve based on the value of the ratio of the main particulars of the ship.
From both studies the following conclusions are obtained: The coupling of Salvesen’s
method with asymptotic Faltinsen’s method for high frequencies produces nonphysical
results and inaccuracies in the estimation in this range of frequencies, as discussed in
the validation of the NN model which shares similar disadvantages. In addition, a more
refined database would potentially improve the result of the predictions. More specific
details of the methodology to improve the database will be given in Chapter 7.

Improved Database
In this Chapter, the DTU Solver for Strip Theory and Salvesen’s method will be used to
improve the database for added wave resistance. The process is based on four main ideas:
• Increasing the number of scaling ratios.
• Adding new parameters to the input parameters of the models.
• Include all results corresponding to the 6 base geometries used.
In the original
database using IShip the results were averaged in two classes, high and low block
coefficient resulting in two different databases.
• Compare the improvement by comparing both models trained with both databases.
7.1
Increasing the Number of Scaling Ratios
In this section, a new database is generated by using the DTU Solver. For the sake of
simplicity, only Salvesen’s method is applied to compute the added wave resistance and
Faltinsen’s method is not considered, nor are WAMIT solutions used for zero forward-
speed. The previous IShip database is computed with the results of a total of 9 combina-
tions of scaling ratios for 6 original geometries. These geometries belong to two categories
(low and high block coefficient, as explained in Chapter 6). For this section, the high
block coefficient database is used. The result for the three geometries of each class is

9
Discussion
In this study, Machine Learning techniques (Neural Networks and feature selection) have
been considered for modelling the added wave resistance. Overall, Neural Network algo-
rithms have proved to be a robust method to generalize the added wave resistance results
obtained by different theories and approaches.
For the models trained on the database computed by SHOPERA function, it has been
observed that good predictions are obtained by relatively simple architectures of neural
networks (5×5 or 10×10). These results are coherent with the complexity of the function
that is modelled, noting that the SHOPERA function has only two input parameters
X = [Lpp, Hs] in this case. The analysis of the accuracy and loss function on training
datasets suggests that generally, for a higher number of iterations and more complex neural
network architecture, the accuracy on the test dataset improves. However, as explained
in Chapter 5, the overfitting of a neural network, or any other regressor algorithm, plays
an important role. It has been established throughout the report that cross-validation,
test/training accuracy analysis and regular inspection of results is necessary to avoid the
overfitting of the model. This is used in order to obtain high confidence in the robustness
of the model’s predictions and its applicability for a specified range.
Similar results are obtained for the models trained with the database generated by the non-
linear STAWave-2 transfer function. In this case, the complexity of the system increases
to 5 input parameters X = [Lpp, ω, TM, VS, B]. This higher complexity problem is tackled
by using more complex neural network architectures, without the need of hard-coding a
non-linear interpolation scheme for 5 inputs variables. This, together with the iterative
process of reducing the mismatch between prediction and actual results from the database
(backpropagation and gradient descent), highlights the advantages of the simplicity of
this approach. The results obtained from the models show generally a good match in
comparison to direct results from the STAWave-2 transfer function. These results are also
computed for values of the input parameters not used in the training dataset, and in some
cases, with only 5 dataset points per variable in the training set (dataset generated for
TM ∈[5, 15] [m], VS ∈[1, 10] [m/s] and B ∈[5, 30] [m] of an equally spaced vector of size
M = 5). The good performance of the model for ‘untrained’ conditions suggests that the
neural network has successfully captured the latent behaviour of the system and how the
different variables influence the target: added wave resistance. In addition, it has been
observed that the fine-tuning of the model for parameters, such as the learning rate α,
affects significantly the accuracy and ability of the neural network to model the system.
When using neural networks to model a set of results from numerical simulations, the
validity of the predictions is analyzed from two perspectives: the performance of the
models with respect to direct calculations from these numerical solutions and, its perfor-
mance with respect to experimental observations. In Chapter 6 and 7, the validation and
analysis of the modelling are carried out regarding the former. By training the neural
network models with the low block coefficient database developed in Martinsen (2016),
Machine Learning Applied to the Estimation of Added Resistance
69
==PAGE:80==
promising results are obtained. In this case, the complexity of the problem increases up
to 6 input parameters X = [Lpp/B, B/TM, Lpp/TM, β, Fr, ˆω] (see section 6.3). By com-
paring the predictions of a neural network with 50 × 50 × 50 architecture, generally good
coherence is achieved. In the comparison with the results of direct calculations for 4 test
geometries KVLCC 2, Torm Lilly and Bulk Carrier (high block coefficient) and LPG/C
(low block coefficient), the predictions of the model are generally more accurate than
the Design Tool used in previous works, Martinsen (2016), Nielsen (2015). For the high
block coefficient ships, the neural network model is able to capture more consistently the
position and magnitude of the peak with respect to the direct calculations from IShip,
Salvesen’s and Faltinsen’s method in comparison to the Design Tool. It also provides a
smooth prediction along a range of values for the different input parameters, in contrast
with a manual interpolation such as the Design Tool. For the low block coefficient test
geometry, it fails to provide an accurate prediction as expected, since the model is trained
with the high block coefficient database. In addition, the region that corresponds to the
Faltinsen’s method for added wave resistance generally is not successfully predicted. This
is a consequence of the main variables involved in the calculation of added wave resistance
by near-field methods. Since the integration involves the wetted line of the waterplane
area and its normal vector, any model that does not include any parameter that defines
this geometry is not able to characterize this component of the added wave resistance.
Similar results are obtained by the Design Tool as expected.
In Chapter 7, an improved database is generated by computing the results for added wave
resistance with the DTU Solver (Strip Theory and Salvesen’s method). The influence of in-
creasing the number of scaling ratio combinations from 9 to 20 is analysed, achieving sim-
ilar performance. A closer match between predictions and direct calculations is achieved
for the model trained with the 7 input parameters X+ = [Lpp/B, Lpp/TM, β, Fr, ˆω, CB, f]
and the same 20 combinations without averaging results by block coefficient class (see
section 7.3). The extra two parameters that have been included for this database (CB, f)
is motivated by the correlation analysis that shows the dependency between output and
input. This model has shown an improvement in performance and the ability to general-
ize for a wider variety of hull shapes and ship types at the expense of adding two extra
parameters to the model.
Comparing the model’s prediction to experimental results allows us to have an insight
on, not only how the neural network models perform in a real example, but also how
physically reasonable are the numerical solvers used for generating the database. For the
KVLCC 2 case, and IShip database (IShip, Salvesen’s and Faltinsen’s method), the direct
calculations and the prediction of the model match fairly accurately the experimental
and CFD results for head waves.
On the same geometry, for the DTU Solver direct
calculations and the predictions of the model trained with the corresponding database
(Strip Theory and Salvesen’s method), the results obtained are significantly different
from the experimental and CFD results. For the RIOS test case, similar behaviour is
observed for the DTU Solver direct calculations. In that case, the prediction of the model
underestimates the added wave resistance in comparison to direct calculations as well,
which may be caused by the fact that the geometry is a scaled model different from the
geometries used to build the database. For ULYSSES test case, the DTU Solver results
match the experimental results for two different heading angles β = 150◦and β = 180◦
more accurately than the IShip-trained model. For both heading angles, the prediction of
Machine Learning Applied to the Estimation of Added Resistance

==PAGE:81==
the model with the extra two parameters X+ is closer to the direct calculation, highlighting
the advantage of generalizing the model.
The mean added wave resistance has been computed for the ULYSSES test case in a nat-
ural seaway. By integrating over the wave frequency the product of the transfer function
(in the form of direct calculations from the DTU Solver and prediction of the models) and
JONSWAP spectrum, good coherence between direct calculation and prediction results
are obtained. Due to the sensibility of the mean added wave resistance regarding the
position of the peak of the spectrum, this difference will be influenced by the disparity
of both solutions in that wave frequency range. Since the peak magnitude is generally
better predicted by the model X+ than the simpler model, closer results are obtained for
heading angles for which the peak of the added wave resistance is aligned to the peak
of the spectrum. Concerning the maneuverability and operational safety of a ship, the
maximum value of the mean added wave resistance plays a more important role. As a
consequence, the applicability of the predictive model is greatly influenced by the wave
spectrum characteristic, as it is discussed below.
By studying the mean added wave resistance for a range of zero up-crossing period Tz
and heading angle β for direct calculation and X+ model, good coherence between pre-
diction and direct calculation is obtained. In the ULYSSES test case, the neural network
model successfully captures the behaviour of the mean added wave resistance by showing
the same pattern as direct calculations. The mismatch on the predictions increases for
spectrum conditions where the peak of the spectrum lies outside of the peak of the added
wave resistance. This mismatch is considered acceptable since it does not exceed 10 % of
the maximum added wave resistance for that condition.

Conclusion and Future Work
10.1
Conclusion
The main objective of this work has been to study the feasibility of applying Machine
Learning techniques for the estimation of added wave resistance. This has been achieved
by analysing the performance of different architectures of neural network algorithms on
three main systems of increasing complexity.
The first two systems studied correspond to two datasets generated by semi-empirical
theories: the SHOPERA expression and the STAWave-2 transfer function. The chosen
neural network architectures have proved to be an excellent method for modelling this su-
pervised regression problem with 2 and 5 input parameters, ¯RAW,pred = f(X) = f(Lpp, Hs)
and RAW,pred = f(X) = f(Lpp, ω, TM, VS, B) respectively.
Motivated by the versatility of these types of regression models and the relatively simple
methodology to generalize the model by increasing the number of input parameters, neural
network algorithms have been used to model a third system based on numerical results.
The objective of this third part has been to train a model based on numerical calculations
for the added wave resistance based on Strip Theory and Salvesen’s method. The model
trained with the IShip database makes use of the added wave resistance results used
in Martinsen (2016) and provides a transfer function of the form RAW,pred = f(X) =
f(Lpp/B, B/TM, Lpp/TM, β, Fr, ˆω). The model’s predictions are in good agreement with
direct calculations and perform as accurately as the previous Design Tool in general.
In addition, this model has been extended and trained with results from a total of 6
different geometries and 20 scaling combinations, obtaining a transfer function of the form
RAW,pred = f(X) = f(Lpp/B, Lpp/TM, β, Fr, ˆω, CB, f) . The final model is able to predict
added wave resistance for different conditions of 0 ≤Fr ≤0.25, 90 ≤β ≤180 [◦] and for
ship geometries on the range 4 ≤Lpp/B ≤8, 8 ≤Lpp/TM ≤40, 0.52 ≤CB ≤0.84 and
3.36 ≤f ≤8.51. This extended model shows good agreement with direct calculations
and a relatively good match with experimental results, limited by the accuracy of the
numerical solver.
It is worth noting the effectiveness of these algorithms for modelling the latent patterns
that govern a system. They extract automatically, and without previous knowledge of
the influence of each input parameter on the target variable, the relation between speed,
heading angle, etc. and the added wave resistance. Once trained, the algorithms provide
the predictions based on non-compute-intense calculations: matrix multiplications and
activation functions.
From a practical point of view, the neural network compiles all the information provided by
more computationally expensive calculations (numerical calculations from Strip theory)
to a model that is executed in less than 0.001 s and size 100 kB. In addition, these
Machine Learning Applied to the Estimation of Added Resistance
73
==PAGE:84==
estimations do not require the complete hull geometry but only the main particulars of
the ship and operational conditions.
Special considerations have to be taken when using feedforward neural networks in terms
of overfitting. For that purpose, an analysis based on cross-validation and accuracy on
the test/training set has been performed for the models.
The supervised regression problem of modelling added wave resistance is greatly influ-
enced by the database that the model is trained with. The predictions of the models
can only perform as accurately as the numerical calculations used for populating the
database. Throughout the study, the main tasks carried out have focused on the study
of the accuracy of the prediction of the models in comparison to direct calculation. It is
worth noting that both solvers used for numerical calculations, DTU Solver and IShip,
give slightly different results (see Appendix E). The study of this discrepancy lies outside
of the scope of this work.
10.2
Recommendations for Future Work
The approach presented in this study for modelling added wave resistance as a super-
vised regression problem with neural network algorithms has demonstrated a reasonable
potential.
It has been shown that, given the right selection of input parameters, the models can
effectively extract the patterns that map the inputs with the target variable, providing a,
in computational terms, ‘light-weight’ tool to estimate the added wave resistance accu-
rately in relation to direct calculations. Based on these results and to further extend the
applicability of this approach and its study, the following guidelines and recommendations
are given for future work:
• Further study a method to make the models fully accessible. As first attempt to
distribute the model, an excel implementation has been considered. In Appendix
F, an example of a basic implementation of the model trained with IShip database
of 50 × 50 architecture is shown. The current solution computes the estimation
of added wave resistance by performing the matrix multiplication of weights and
biases for each layer. This basic implementation only allows the prediction for one
set of input parameters each time. It would be convenient to upgrade it to allow
a prediction for a range of wave frequencies and include the calculation of mean
added wave resistance by adding the wave energy spectrum.
• Increase the number of base geometries to generate the database with. The model
X+ has been trained for a total of 6 geometries and 20 combinations of scaling
ratios. By adding new geometries to the database the generalization of the model
could improve.
• Implement other methods to compute the added wave resistance such as Maruo’s
method. The numerical calculations from the DTU Solver are computed by using
Maruo’s method as well, which has not been included for the sake of brevity and
lower confidence in the numerical results for some geometries.
• Include loading conditions as input. Typically, cargo ships are operated at different
loading conditions from their design conditions. The modification on the trim or
Machine Learning Applied to the Estimation of Added Resistance
74
==PAGE:85==
draught of the ship would influence the added wave resistance results. By extending
the model to account for these extra conditions, the applicability and generalization
of the model would increase.
• This approach based on modelling a supervised regression problem with neural net-
works can be applied to others maritime-related problems. It would be convenient
for systems which behaviour is controlled by a high number of variables and can
be modelled by numerical calculations. These models, once trained with numerical
calculations, can potentially provide an accurate and quick estimation, especially
important in time-sensitive tasks such as sea state modelling or in situ ship fuel
consumption.
Future recommendations for a re-training of the model would include:
• Generating the DTU Solver database for a specific range of absolute wave frequency
instead of encountered wave frequency. The numerical calculations have been com-
puted for a specific range of encountered frequency and the models are trained as
a function of absolute frequency. This causes that the range of absolute wave fre-
quency for which added wave resistance results are computed will vary depending
on the heading angle and Froude number. Other suggested approach would be to
train the models for encountered wave frequency as well, for the sake of consistency.
