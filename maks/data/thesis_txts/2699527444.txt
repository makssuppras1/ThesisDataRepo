Abstract 
Oral drug delivery is the most preferred route of administration as it is the easiest and most 
comfortable administration route, which leads to high patient compliance. However, when a 
drug is taken orally, its bioavailability is affected by multiple factors, such as the first pass-
effect due to enzymatic degradation, large pH variations found throughout the gastrointestinal 
(GI) tract, and continuous mucus secretion in the GI tract. Furthermore, many oral drugs have 
poor permeability, stability, and solubility in the GI environment. As a result, the oral 
bioavailability of many drugs is low and in order to improve it, novel microscale active carriers, 
known as micromotors, have been explored. Micromotors are able to perform various tasks in 
biomedical areas purely by using their impressive motion abilities. They are often coated with 
a pH-sensitive polymer that protects the drug while the micromotors travel through the 
stomach. As the micromotors enter the intestines, the protective polymer coat starts to dissolve, 
resulting in an efficient drug release with enhanced tissue penetration.  
In this project, micromotors were loaded with furosemide and ketoprofen, separately, and 
coated with two different pH-sensitive polymers (Eudragit® L100 and Kollicoat® MAE 100-
55). The autonomous propulsion and lifetime of the micromotors were evaluated in a simulated 
intestinal medium. Furthermore, drug release in simulated stomach and intestinal media was 
evaluated in vitro. 
Propulsion studies indicated that the micromotors are capable of directional motion based 
on the bubble self-propulsion mechanism. It was shown that loading a drug onto the 
micromotor’s surface improves its velocity, and the choice of polymer (Eudragit® or 
Kollicoat®) does not affect the velocity. Lifetime studies demonstrated that the micromotors 
are able to generate bubbles for few minutes in an intestinal medium. However, when analysing 
the difference between the two drugs and the two polymers in regard to the lifetime, the results 
were inconclusive. Lastly, in vitro studies showed rapid drug release in the intestinal medium. 
As for the stomach medium, the Eudragit® coat served its purpose in protecting the drug, while 
the results for the Kollicoat®-coated micromotors were inconclusive.

1 Introduction 
Drugs can be administered by various routes of administration, such as orally, intravenously, 
subcutaneously, intramuscularly, nasally, ocularly, and rectally. Oral administration is the most 
frequently used route due to its non-invasive nature, convenience, and high patient 
compliance.1 
1.1 Conventional Oral Drug Delivery 
Oral drug delivery refers to the route where the drug is taken by mouth. Before the active 
pharmaceutical ingredient (API) can reach the systemic circulation, the drug needs to undergo 
absorption, which is represented by the first letter in the four-letter acronym, ADME. The 
acronym has been used to describe pharmacokinetics for the past 60 years. Absorption is the 
phase where the drug moves from the site of administration to the systemic circulation through 
either passive or active transport.  For orally administered drugs, the site of administration is 
the gastrointestinal (GI) tract.2 The presence and density of membrane transporters, lipid 
solubility, and the pH of the GI fluid all have an effect on the rate of absorption.3  
The letter D in ADME stands for distribution which refers to the phase where the drug 
moves around the body using the systemic circulation as a transportation system until it reaches 
its site of action.4 Various factors influence drug distribution, such as plasma protein binding, 
alterations in capillary permeability, cardiac output, local regional blood flow, affinity to tissue 
proteins, and the local pH.5  
While the drug is still inside the body, it can undergo chemical alterations in a process 
called metabolism, which is represented by the letter M. Metabolism mainly takes place in the 
liver, where high levels of metabolic enzymes can be found. These metabolic enzymes convert 
the drug into metabolites, resulting in the termination of the drug’s activity or the 
transformation of a prodrug into its active form. The main purpose of metabolism is to make 
the drug molecule easier to excrete by making it more polar.6  
Lastly, E stands for excretion, which refers to the elimination of the drug from the body, 
either in its unchanged form or as a metabolite. The major determining factors for drug 
elimination are the size, polarity, solubility, and plasma protein binding of the drug molecule. 
The kidneys mainly carry out the excretion process, but drug elimination can occur through 
other nonrenal excretion pathways, such as the liver, lungs, salivary glands, skin, mammary 
glands, and semen.7

1.1.1 Advantages 
Most patients prefer oral administration over intravenous injection, meaning that oral drug 
delivery results in higher patient compliance. Instead of having to go to the clinic to get a 
healthcare specialist to inject the drug intravenously, which can be rather painful, patients can 
simply take the drug orally in the privacy of their own homes. Furthermore, for most people, 
swallowing a tablet is as easy and natural as swallowing food.8 It is also safe, adverse effects 
are minimal and in most cases, it is suitable for long term use. Moreover, no special equipment, 
such as needles and syringes, is required for administration, nor are sterile precautions needed. 
Thus, the oral route is considered to be more economically friendly compared to the 
intravenous route.9,10 
1.1.2 Challenges 
Even though the oral route has great advantages, critical challenges are faced in oral drug 
delivery. These challenges are due to the harsh environment of the GI tract that drugs are 
exposed to after administration and during the absorption phase.2 All drugs have to overcome 
multiple barriers, which are primarily of chemical, enzymatic, or physical nature. The highly 
acidic environment of the stomach and the alkaline environment of the intestine are examples 
of chemical barriers. The enzymatic barriers refer to the digestive enzymes located along the 
entire length of the GI tract. Furthermore, the constant secretion of mucus that covers the 
surface of the GI tract is a major physical barrier that therapeutics have to overcome.11 
Moreover, many drugs have poor permeability, stability, and solubility in the GI environment. 
As a result, oral administration of therapeutics often results in low oral bioavailability, so 
achieving appropriate therapeutic levels can be challenging.12 
Bioavailability is an essential factor when it comes to drug administration. It is used to 
quantify drug absorption and refers to the fraction of the initial dose of an applied drug that 
successfully reaches the systemic circulation. For drugs administered intravenously, the 
bioavailability is 100%, whereas the bioavailability of orally administered drugs is usually 
much lower. If the drug has a relatively low bioavailability, a higher dose is required to achieve 
the minimum effective concentration threshold. Higher dosing not only increases the cost of 
therapy but can also cause toxic side effects on healthy tissues and cells in the body.13 Several 
factors cause such low bioavailability of oral drugs, such as the first-pass metabolism, the 
variable pH levels at different locations of the GI tract13, and continuous mucus secretion.12 
==PAGE:12==
 
3 
In order to reach the general systemic circulation, orally administered drugs must first travel 
through the long GI tract, adhere and penetrate through the mucus layer, transport across the 
epithelial barrier, infiltrate the hepatic portal vein (which enters the liver) before finally 
reaching the general systemic circulation.14 When a drug travels through the GI tract and the 
liver, it undergoes the first-pass metabolism, a process where the drug gets partially 
metabolised, resulting in a reduced drug concentration, which reduces oral bioavailability. 
Hepatic enzymes found in the liver and digestive enzymes found in the GI tract are some of 
the leading causes of the first-pass effect.15 
The different pH levels found throughout the GI tract are another factor that affects the 
absorption of drugs. The organs that make up the GI tract are the mouth, oesophagus, stomach, 
small intestine, and large intestine (Figure 1). The lowest pH value in the GI tract is found in 
the stomach, with a pH between 1.2 and 3.0.11 The small intestine, which consists of the 
duodenum, jejunum and ileum, has a pH level between 5.7 and 7.3.16 The pH in the large 
intestine, which is composed of the caecum, colon, rectum, and anal canal, is typically observed 
to be between 5.7 and 6.6.16 Many therapeutic drugs are sensitive to the drastic changes in pH 
along the GI tract, which can result in pH-induced hydrolysis, deamidation, or oxidation of the 
drug, which reduces the drug efficacy.11 In addition, the constant presence of acid and enzymes 
in the stomach leads to repeated degradation of the mucus layer. More mucus is secreted to 
compensate for the mucus degradation, resulting in a vicious cycle of continual mucus 
secretion.12

The continuous mucus secretion in the GI tract is one of the most significant barriers to 
drug penetration. Mucus is a complex hydrogel made up of glycoproteins (mucins), lipids, 
carbohydrates, salts, bacteria, antibodies, and cellular debris. Mucus is able to protect the 
exposed epithelial surfaces of the GI tract by effectively trapping and eliminating pathogens. 
Mucus thickness can vary depending on location in the GI tract, and it has been shown that 
mucus is thickest in the colon and the stomach, most likely to increase the protection against 
bacteria.12 Mucus layer decreases drug diffusion rate, especially for macromolecular drugs, 
such as drugs based on proteins and peptides. In fact, proteins with a molecular mass greater 
than 5 kDa can hardly penetrate the mucus layer.11 
Overall, the oral route undoubtedly presents several challenges to efficient and effective 
drug delivery that researchers have been trying to solve for the past decades. As a result, various 
novel oral drug delivery systems (DDS) have emerged, such as polymers/hydrogels, liposomes, 
nanoparticles, microcontainers and, more recently, nano/micromotors.17–19 
1.2 Micromotors 
Micromotors are microscale devices made to perform various functions, such as drug 
delivery20, environmental remediation21, bacterial separation22, and more. Micromotors have 
the ability to convert energy into mechanical motion and force that results in their self-
propulsion.22 Seventeen years ago, Paxton et al. reported the first work on self-propelled nano- 
and micromotors. They demonstrated rod-shaped nanomotors that were capable of moving 
autonomously in aqueous hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) solution by self-electrophoresis.23 Five 
years later, the same group presented silver chloride (AgCl) micromotors that moved in water 
under ultraviolet illumination by self-diffusiophoresis24, and this was just the beginning. Since 
then, numerous variations of micromotors have emerged in all kinds of shapes and sizes. One 
key feature of micromotors is their autonomous movement, which can be utilised to improve 
the performance of oral drug delivery.25 
Micromotors are generally divided into two categories based on their motion: external 
stimuli propelled and chemically self-propelled. External power sources that externally-
stimulated micromotors use for their motion are infrared light, ultraviolet (UV) irradiation, 
magnetic force, electrical field, and ultrasound (US). However, the chemically self-propelled 
micromotors have received much more attention since they do not require external energy but 
instead use other mechanisms for their movement, such as self-electrophoresis, self-
diffusiophoresis, bubble propulsion, or the Marangoni effect.26

1.2.1 Motion 
Two critical factors, Brownian motion and Reynolds number, directly affect and cause some 
challenges to the movement of micromotors. Brownian motion is the random motion of 
particles that are lighter than the environment they are situated in. This results in no preferential 
direction of particles’ flow, meaning that deviations from an intended path and inconsistency 
in velocity are likely to occur.27 The Reynolds number is the ratio of inertial forces to viscous 
forces and can be estimated from Equation (1). 
 
𝑅𝑒= 𝑣𝐿/  
 
 
 
 
(1) 
 
Here, 𝑅𝑒 is the Reynolds number,  is the density of the solution, 𝑣 is the mean velocity of the 
micromotor, 𝐿 is the characteristic linear dimension of the micromotor, and  is the fluid’s 
viscosity. 
The motion of micromotors in a liquid with high viscosity (low Re) is linear, while if the 
viscosity is low (large Re), the motion becomes circular.28 In a liquid with high viscosity, a 
pulling force stronger than the viscous force is required to propel the micromotor through the 
solution.29 Moreover, the trajectories of micromotors can change from a linear to a circular 
path at higher velocities.30 Overall, Brownian motion in combination with a low Reynolds 
number plays a significant role in micro/nano-scale systems. 
Self-electrophoresis 
Micromotors that move by self-electrophoresis mechanism (Figure 2A) are frequently 
bimetallic nanorods and Janus particles fabricated from two conductive materials. Two 
different electrochemical half-reactions appear at each end of the micromotor, where one end 
acts as a cathode and the other end acts as an anode. Between the two ends, an electric field is 
generated that causes electrons to flow inside the micromotor. This is subsequently followed 
by a migration of protons on the outside of the micromotor, which results in the propulsion of 
the micromotor in the opposite direction.31 
Self-diffusiophoresis 
The motion of micromotors created by self-diffusiophoresis (Figure 2B) relies on a 
concentration gradient of a solute that surrounds the micromotors that are situated in an 
aqueous solvent. The concentration gradient prompts the solvent to flow from low solute 
concentration to high solute concentration, resulting in fluid flow that triggers the micromotor 
to move in the opposite direction.32

Marangoni effect 
The Marangoni effect (Figure 2C) is a surface tension gradient phenomenon that causes motors 
to move. The system tends to reach the desirable lowest-free-energy state and thus causes the 
motors to move from a region of lower surface tension to a region of higher surface tension. 
However, the majority of motors that have been reported to move based on the Marangoni 
effect are in macroscale.26 Zhao et al. demonstrated the Marangoni effect on a millimetre-sized 
polymer capsule-motor that was capable of releasing N,N-dimethylformamide (DMF) 
asymmetrically onto the surface of an aqueous solution.33 As the motor released the DMF, the 
surface tension of the DMF/water mixture became lower than that of the water, causing a 
forward net force that pulled the motor through the water, away from the DMF/water mixture.

Bubble propulsion 
The bubble propulsion mechanism (Figure 2D) propels micromotors forward by means of a 
bubble generation from the micromotors’ bodies. A chemical reaction takes place between the 
micromotors and the aqueous solution that they are located in. As the bubbles grow and get 
ejected from the micromotors, a recoil effect occurs that results in the movement of the 
micromotors.34 However, in order for the micromotors to generate bubbles and move, 
micromotors need some kind of fuel. Various types of fuels have been used so far: iodine (I2), 
bromine (Br2), alkaline, and acid solutions34, sodium chloride (NaCl)21,35, simulated body or 
gastric fluid (pH ~1.3)36–38, and sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO3)39,40. Hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) 
has been the most commonly used fuel type as it provides a very high power output.34 
Surfactants, such as Triton X-10040–42, polyvinylpyrrolidone (PVP)36,37, or sodium dodecyl 
sulphate (SDS)43, are added to the fuel to promote the release of bubbles by lowering the 
interfacial tension. The interfacial tension remains relatively high without a surfactant, which 
is a problem for the generation and detachment of the bubbles from the micromotors.44 
Bubble-propelled catalytic or non-catalytic micromotors are some of the most commonly 
fabricated micromotors in the past decade. They have a high power output that results in a 
relatively high propulsion velocity. The three motion mechanisms mentioned before usually 
result in much slower velocity compared to bubble-propelled micromotors.30 The average 
velocities of bubble-propelled Janus micromotors are typically between 75-300 m/s21,28–
30,34,44, which is considered a superfast velocity relative to the micromotors’ size.34 
When a Mg-based micromotor is submerged into seawater or NaCl, a rapid spontaneous 
chemical reaction of Mg with water takes place, illustrated by Equation (2).45  
 
Mg(s) + 2H2O(aq) → H2(g) + Mg(OH)2(s)   
 
 
 (2) 
 
During the reaction, not only are hydrogen (H2) bubbles generated, but also a magnesium 
hydroxide [Mg(OH)2] passivation layer.37 This layer can interfere with the bubble generation 
process, but the combination of pitting corrosion and macrogalvanic corrosion ensures that the 
bubble generation can continue.46 The chloride ions (Cl-) are well known for their ability to 
promote pitting corrosion of magnesium in aqueous solutions. The Cl- ions penetrate the 
Mg(OH)2 passivation layer, where they subsequently balance the charge within the corrosion 
pits as the concentration of Mg+ cations builds up.21 The OH- ions are depleted within the pit 
as the corrosion continues, which prevents the passivation of the pit surfaces. When Mg+ and

Cl- ions are accumulated, the pit environment becomes weakly acidic, which further promotes 
the Mg dissolution in the pit. A gold (Au) layer is often incorporated into Mg-based Janus 
micromotors due to its vital role in macrogalvanic corrosion. When a Mg-Au micromotor is 
submerged into seawater, and both metals are in electrical contact, the Mg(OH)2 passivation 
layer corrodes preferentially to the Au layer.21 
1.2.2 Fabrication 
Micromotors can be fabricated in many different shapes and sizes using various fabrication 
techniques, such as physical vapour deposition36, template-assisted electrochemical 
deposition26, and strain engineering47 (Figure 3). Different fabrication techniques result in 
differently shaped micromotors.26 Physical vapour deposition, such as sputtering or electron 
beam evaporation, can be used to produce helical micromotors, nanorods, and Janus 
micromotors.26 Sputtering (Figure 3A) is most frequently used in the fabrication of Janus 
micromotors, where a glow discharge is formed between an anode and cathode using a suitable 
gas, usually argon (Ar). Gold is commonly used as the cathode target material, and the 
bombardment of the target with the argon gas ions will cause an erosion of the target that 
becomes visible as a blue coloured plasma. This plasma will form an even coating layer of the 
target material on the sample’s surface, which is placed on an anodic sample holder underneath 
the target.48,49 
Nanowire, helical, conical, and tubular micromotors can be prepared using the templated-
assisted electrochemical deposition technique (Figure 3B), where porous templates are utilised. 
The desired micromotors are synthesised within the pores of the templates.26 Strain engineering 
(Figure 3C) has been widely used in the fabrication of helical and tubular micromotors.26 Thin 
layers of materials are deposited onto a sacrificial layer, which then either spontaneously or 
manually scroll into helical/tubular micromotors.47 Two main techniques are used in strain 
engineering: the rolled-up technology and the self-scrolling technique.26 The rolled-up 
technology produces tubular micromotors, whereas the self-scrolling technique results in 
helical micromotors.

electrochemical couple.50 Furthermore, Mg has a high systemic toxic threshold (up to 1 mmol/L 
in serum), and it has a high allowable daily dosage (~400 mg).51 Moreover, it has been shown 
that supplementary Mg in drinking water can result in a better state of health and even help to

reduce the chances of getting cardiovascular disease.52 Mg is also a cofactor for hundreds of 
different enzyme reactions, and it has been suggested that Mg is necessary for blood pressure

The microspheres are often immobilised on a glass slide during the fabrication process and 
subsequently coated with one to four different coating materials, leaving a small opening at the 
contact point between the microsphere and the glass slide. This opening is essential for the fuel 
to make contact with the core material, and it ensures a steady reaction process and a gradual 
dissolution of the core. This helps in terms of prolonging the lifetime of the micromotors20. A 
wide range of coating materials have been used before: Platinum (Pt)36,37, gold (Au)38,42, 
titanium (Ti)21, nickel (Ni)21, zinc (Zn)58), titanium oxide (TiO2)20,35, and polycaprolactone 
(PCL)41, and various polymers, such as chitosan20, poly[lactic-co-glycolic] acid (PLGA)20,42, 
poly[N-isopropylacrylamide] (PNIPAM)36,39, and Eudragit®38. 
The size of micromotors can vary in microscale, but it mainly depends on the core material. 
Most Mg-based Janus micromotors found in the literature are approximately 20 m in 
diameter20,35,37,38,42, but micromotors up to 50 µm in diameter have been reported as well39–41. 
Chen et al. demonstrated that the lifetime of Mg-based micromotors is dependent on the size 
of the motor.46 The smaller the micromotor, the shorter the lifetime. Based on that, it is possible 
to control the lifetime by changing the diameter of the micromotor. Furthermore, the velocity 
of micromotors is also dependent on the size. Small micromotors usually exhibit a low velocity, 
even though the fuel concentration might be very high.34 All in all, size is an important 
parameter that must be taken into consideration when designing and fabricating micromotors. 
Gao et al. fabricated seawater-driven Mg-Ti-Ni-Au Janus micromotors for environmental 
remediation. The Ti layer acted as a conductor between the Mg core and the Ni-Au layers, the 
Ni layer enabled the micromotors to be guided magnetically, and the Au layer in combination 
with the Mg core facilitated macrogalvanic corrosion.21 Wang’s group fabricated Mg-based 
Janus micromotors composed of TiO2, PLGA, and chitosan layers.20 The TiO2 layer was added 
to act as a shell around the Mg microspheres to maintain their shape, and the PLGA layer was 
loaded with the drug. The chitosan layer protected the drug-loaded PLGA layer as well as

1.2.3 Application 
Self-propelled micromotors are primarily found in environmental and biomedical areas. 
Micromotors have shown great potential in environmental remediation, where they are capable 
of detecting and degrading different pollutants and chemical and biological warfare agents 
(CBWA) using an adsorption/catalytic action.35,58,59 It has also been shown that micromotors 
have excellent capabilities in various biomedical applications. They are able to pick up, 
transport, and release various cargoes in liquid medium. These attractive abilities make 
micromotors capable of performing complex tasks, such as targeted drug delivery20,36,60,61, 
microsurgeries62, cancer therapies63, biosensing64, bioimaging65, and separation of proteins and 
cells66. Herein, drug transport and drug release will be the main focus. 
Recent research in the oral drug delivery field focuses on specific challenges, such as 
targeted delivery, poor drug loading capacity, limited tissue penetration, and biochemical 
degradation of drugs.19,67 It has been proven that micromotors are able to overcome these 
challenges due to their great ability to provide a localised drug delivery68 and a higher drug 
loading capacity69, to enhance tissue penetration25, and to protect the cargo70 from degradation 
by enzymes and other chemical degrading agents. By enhancing the tissue penetration and 
localising the drug delivery, the incidence of toxicity towards the surrounding healthy tissues 
is minimised.25 Wang’s group demonstrated that it is possible to load micromotors with 
multicomponent cargoes that are autonomously released, followed by micromotors’ self-
destruction once their delivery mission has been completed.69 Utilising this combinational 
cargo delivery could be beneficial for individualised drug therapy for a given patient that 
requires simultaneous administration of more than one drug.  
Wang’s team has also shown that by introducing a pH-sensitive enteric polymer (Eudragit® 
L100-55) coating and tuning its thickness, it is possible to control where along the GI tract the 
micromotors penetrate the mucosal tissue.68 Uncoated micromotors showed significant 
retention in the stomach, while micromotors with thin enteric coating were able to reach the 
duodenum, micromotors with medium-thick enteric coating reached the jejunum, and 
micromotors with thick enteric coating were able to travel all the way to the ileum. This study 
==PAGE:21==
 
12 
proves the claim that it is possible to selectively control where and when tissue penetration and 
retention of the micromotors takes place. 
In 2015, Gao et al. reported the first in vivo study of synthetic micromotors using a live 
mouse model.25 The main focus of the study was the retention and cargo delivery of 
micromotors, as well as their toxicity profile. They prepared acid powered PEDOT/Zn 
micromotors that propelled based on the self-propulsion bubble mechanism. They showed that 
the self-propulsion led to significantly enhanced tissue penetration and retention in the stomach 
environment without causing any destructive effect on the gastric epithelial cells. Also, the 
ability of the micromotors to self-dissolute caused the cargo payloads to be released 
autonomously and efficiently. This active micromotor-based delivery approach offers 
impressive improvement of orally administered cargoes compared to the conventional passive 
diffusion.  
Nevertheless, even though a significant process has been made in the past years, most 
proof-of-concept studies are performed in vitro, and therefore, a lot of work has yet to be 
done.20,71 
1.3 Ketoprofen and Furosemide 
Ketoprofen is a propionic acid derivative and belongs to the group of nonsteroidal anti-
inflammatory drugs (NSAID).72 It has inflammatory, antipyretic, and analgesic effects and has 
been FDA approved for the treatment of rheumatoid arthritis and osteoarthritis.73 Ketoprofen 
is available in various forms, such as tablets, capsules, solutions, topical gel formulations, and 
rectal suppositories.74 Ketoprofen belongs to class II of the Biopharmaceutical Classification 
Systems (BCS), meaning that the drug has low solubility but high permeability.75 Poor 
solubility usually leads to challenges in absorption and therefore low bioavailability.74

meaning it has poor permeability and poor aqueous solubility; hence the oral bioavailability is 
relatively low.78 Also, it has been shown that the dosage form, presence of food, and underlying 
diseases can influence intestinal absorption, resulting in a highly variable bioavailability 
ranging from 20 to 60%.79 The use of an advanced drug delivery system such as micromotors 
or microcontainers could potentially improve the oral bioavailability of the poorly soluble

An optical microscope is frequently used to record and track the autonomous propulsion of

1.6 Coherent Anti-Stokes Raman Scattering Microscopy 
Coherent anti-Stokes Raman scattering (CARS) microscopy (Figure 7A) belongs to the family 
of coherent nonlinear Raman techniques and is mainly used to visualise living biological 
specimens, such as stem cell monitoring or lipid detection.88,89 The key requirements for

selectivity. Conventional Raman spectroscopy only meets the last two requirements, whereas 
CARS microscopy satisfies all three conditions. Furthermore, one of the main advantages of 
CARS microscopy is that it does not require labelling; hence, the approach is non-invasive and 
non-destructive.88 Moreover, since CARS has excellent sensitivity and chemical selectivity, it 
is possible to visualise the distribution of drugs on carrier particles90, such as micromotors.

1.8 µDISS Profiler 
µDISS Profiler (pION INC, USA) is an efficient instrument to study drug release. It is an in

probe is connected to a diode array spectrophotometer that immediately collects the entire

2 Research Aim 
The aim of this master project was to demonstrate proof-of-concept for the use of micromotors 
as a new approach for targeted drug delivery. Mg-based Janus micromotors were fabricated 
from biodegradable and biocompatible non-toxic materials. In-depth characterisation of the 
micromotors was done where the lifetime and motion of the micromotors was analysed, 
followed by an in vitro evaluation of the micromotors’ drug release performance.  
 
3 Hypothesis 
The micromotors can act as drug carriers that will release the drug in a simulated intestinal 
medium, whereas no release occurs in a simulated stomach medium due to polymer protection. 
Furthermore, the motors are capable of autonomous directional motion that will provide 
effective and fast drug transport and release.

4.1 Fabrication of Mg-Based Janus Micromotors 
Mg microparticles (catalogue no. FMW20, TangShan WeiHao Magnesium Powder Co.; 20  
5 m) (Figure 10) were used as the core to prepare the micromotors. 2 mg of the Mg particles 
were weighed using an analytical microbalance (Mettler Toledo, XPE26) and subsequently 
placed in a 1.5 mL Eppendorf tube containing 1 mL acetone (VWR, USA). Three wash steps 
were carried out to minimise the presence of impurities, and each step consisted of sonication 
and centrifugation. The Mg particles were sonicated in a 2210 DTH Branson ultrasonic cleaner 
(Branson Ultrasonics, USA) for 10 minutes. The solution was then centrifuged in a 5430 
Eppendorf microcentrifuge (Eppendorf, Germany) for 10 min at 9000 rpm. Lastly, the old 
acetone was carefully removed without disturbing the Mg pellet, and 1 mL of fresh acetone 
was added. 
 
Figure 10:The Mg microparticles used for the fabrication of the Mg-based Janus micromotors.

slightly lighter), 0.5 mL of the solution was dispersed onto glass slides (7.5x2.5 cm) and dried

sputtering time: 60 s, current: 40 mA, pressure: 0.1 mbar) using a 208HR Cressington high

4.3 Motion Study 
Before conducting motion studies, the micromotors were collected by light manual scratching 
onto a new glass slide using a scalpel. The glass slide was then placed under a Leica INM100 
optical microscope (Leica, Germany) coupled with different microscope objectives (2.5, 5, 
10, 20, and 50). Next, the fuel and the surfactant were dropped over the micromotors. 3 M 
NaCl was used as a fuel and 0.5 wt% Triton X-100 acted as a surfactant. The propulsion was 
then recorded for 20 individual micromotors for each type using the optical microscope. 
4.4 In vitro Drug Release Study 
The drug release was evaluated in a simulated intestinal medium using the µDISS Profiler 
(pION INC, USA). For comparison, a control experiment was performed in a simulated 
stomach medium. The experiments were carried out at 37 °C with a stirring rate of 100 rpm, 
and the path length of the in situ UV probes was 5 mm. Phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) (pH 
7.4) was used for the simulation of the intestinal environment. The PBS buffer was prepared 
by dissolving one PBS tablet (Sigma-Aldrich, USA) in 200 mL Milli-Q water (Merck 
Millipore, USA) using a magnetic stirrer for few minutes. A hydrochloric acid (HCl) buffer 
(pH 2.1) was used for the simulated stomach medium. It was prepared by slowly adding small 
amount of 32% HCl to 400 mL of DI water until pH 2.1 was reached. Before conducting the 
drug release experiments, all channels of the profiler were calibrated with their own standard 
curves. Once the standard curves had been obtained, the Si wafers containing the drug-loaded

bottom of freshly cleaned µDISS glass vessels. Finally, 10 mL of PBS was added to each

micromotors (p-value = 0.0178). Since only one test value out of the four shows a statistically 
significant difference, it cannot be assumed whether a polymer coating affects the lifetime. 
More experiments must therefore be performed to confirm if drugs or polymers affect the 
lifetime of micromotors.

5.4 Propulsion of Mg-Based Janus Micromotors 
Multiple motion studies were carried out to confirm that the Mg-based Janus micromotors are 
capable of autonomous propulsion in various media. The initial results were not promising as 
the micromotors were not able to move even though the bubble generation seemed to be 
sufficient. A combination of numerous factors was thought to be affecting the omission of 
movement, one factor being an excessive concentration of Mg particles on the glass slides. If 
too high of a concentration is used during the fabrication, it is likely that many particles will 
overlap each other. If that is the case, the Au sputtering does not result in a uniform coating, 
and some Mg particles might not even be covered if they are positioned underneath another 
Mg particle. Without a sufficient layer of Au, hydrogen bubbles are generated from multiple 
sides of the micromotors, resulting in no net movement.  
Another important factor is related to the short cleaning procedures that were carried out in 
the initial experiments. Insufficient cleaning of bare Mg particles might result in the appearance 
of impurities on the particles, which possibly can interfere with the adhesion between the Au 
layer and the Mg particle. Without proper attachment, liquid can get in between the two 
materials, causing bubble generation from different sides of the micromotors.  
Sputtering time is another critical parameter in the preparation procedure. If the sputtering 
time is too short, it can result in a non-uniform coating of Au. It was noticed during the 
experiment that when the sputtering time was 10, 20, or 30 s, the micromotors generated 
bubbles from multiple sides, causing the micromotors to stay in place and not move. 
The last potential reason for the lack of motion can be related to the strength of the fuels 
that were tested out. It was noticed that during the experiment, the majority of the micromotors 
sunk to the bottom and stuck to the glass slide. The contact between the micromotors and the 
glass slide resulted in a friction force45 that the micromotors had to overcome in order to detach 
from the glass slide and propel themselves forward. Since the micromotors were not moving 
at first, the strength of the fuels was probably not adequate. It resulted in a poor bubble force 
that was too weak to overcome the friction force between the glass slide and the micromotors. 
Thorough literature research was conducted to test various fabrication processes and 
experimental conditions. Around 80 different experimental setups were tested (Appendix B) 
before promising results were obtained. The fabrication process and the experimental 
conditions that resulted in the autonomous propulsion of the micromotors have been described 
in detail in the materials and methods section. The tracking and monitoring of the locomotion 
of all the different micromotor types are demonstrated in Figures 18-20. The figures illustrate

furosemide, and further studies must be performed in order to determine that.

5.5 In vitro Drug Release 
To quantify the drug release from the micromotors, in vitro experiments were conducted using 
the µDISS profiler™. Quantification of ketoprofen was not made due to difficulties in 
collecting the ketoprofen-loaded micromotors. When collecting those micromotors from the Si 
wafers, it was noticeable how sticky the micromotors were. This made it very difficult to 
remove them from the wafers, and therefore it was impossible to ensure that all micromotors 
had been collected. In contrast, it was much easier to collect the micromotors loaded with 
furosemide as no stickiness was observed. Therefore, it was decided to only quantify the 
furosemide-loaded micromotors. 
The obtained data from the release studies are presented in Figure 22. It is apparent from 
Figures 22A and 22B that furosemide was exponentially released from both Eudragit®- and 
Kollicoat®-coated micromotors in PBS (pH 7.4). The results show that both polymer coatings 
successfully dissolved as expected since Eudragit® L100 is dissolvable above pH 6.0101 and 
==PAGE:47==
 
38 
Kollicoat® MAE 100-55 is dissolvable above pH 5.5102. Both micromotor types had fully 
released the drug in less than 3 minutes, proving that the micromotors are capable of super-fast 
drug release. By increasing the thickness of the polymers, it is possible to delay the release68, 
which would be beneficial if the micromotors are needed to travel far in the GI tract before 
releasing the drug.

6 Conclusion 
The main goal of this master project was to develop Janus micromotors capable of autonomous 
directional motion. The micromotors were made from magnesium microparticles coated with 
gold, drug, and polymer. Numerous experiments were conducted in order to optimise the 
motion parameters. The work also explored how different drugs and polymers affect the 
velocity of micromotors and their lifetime. Lastly, the ability of the prepared micromotors to 
serve as a drug delivery system was assessed by quantifying the release of furosemide in vitro.  
At first, the micromotors showed no movement despite sufficient bubble generation. After 
numerous motion studies where different motion parameters were tested, it was finally 
demonstrated that all the different types of micromotors were capable of self-propulsion in 3 
M NaCl and 0.5 wt% Triton X-100. The average velocities ranged from 35 µm/s to 55 µm/s, 
and it was proved that the addition of a drug onto Mg-Au micromotors is beneficial to the 
velocity. However, due to a statistically insignificant difference, it was not possible to 
determine whether furosemide or ketoprofen is a better option. Furthermore, it was 
demonstrated that the choice between Eudragit® and Kollicoat® does not affect the velocity. 
However, whether the addition of a polymer to the surface of a micromotor is beneficial to the 
velocity could not be determined due to statistical insignificance. 
The lifetime studies revealed that the micromotors are capable of producing bubbles for 2-
4 minutes in 3 M NaCl and 0.5 wt% Triton X-100. However, it is not possible to conclude if 
the addition of drugs or polymers affects the lifetime due to statistical insignificance. Also, for 
the same reason, the difference between the two drugs and the two polymers in regard to the 
lifetime could not be determined.  
Results from the in vitro drug release studies showed full release of furosemide (100%) 
from Mg-Au-Furo-Eudr and Mg-Au-Furo-Kolli micromotors in PBS (pH 7.4) within 3 
minutes. In a simulated stomach medium (pH 2.1), results showed little release of furosemide 
(14%) from micromotors coated with Eudragit®. In contrast, a significant release of furosemide 
(85%) was unexpectedly observed from micromotors coated with Kollicoat®, probably due to 
an insufficient thickness of the polymer coat. 
In summary, the addition of a drug to Mg-Au Janus micromotors increases the velocity of 
the micromotors; the choice between Eudragit® and Kollicoat® will not compromise the 
velocity of the Mg-Au-Drug micromotor; the Eudragit®-coated Mg-based Janus micromotors 
play an excellent role as drug carriers that rapidly and efficiently release the drug at neutral pH.

7 Future Perspectives 
During this project, it was proved that Mg-based Janus micromotors have a great potential to 
join the selection of drug delivery systems. The prepared micromotors showed that they are 
capable of self-propulsion in 3 M NaCl and fast drug release in PBS. However, optimisation 
of few fabrication parameters should be studied in order to obtain even better results. The most 
important parameter for the propulsion of micromotors is the micromotors’ composition and 
the fabrication process. Therefore, it would be worth looking into other possible Au deposition 
methods in the future to see if they could improve the velocity of the micromotors. Also, 
looking into other metal alternatives would be interesting to explore. Furthermore, the drug 
loading technique that was used in this project should be reconsidered in order to reduce the 
amount of drug that goes to waste and ends up between the micromotors. As the results 
indicated, the excess drug potentially skewed the results of the drug release studies, and 
therefore by optimising the loading technique of the drug, such deviations could be reduced. 
Determining the drug loading capacity on a single micromotor is also something that should 
be done in the future. In this project, only the total amount of drug on a single glass slide or 
silicon wafer was roughly estimated. If micromotors are to be used in future medicines, the 
drug loading capacity must be established in order to know the number of micromotors required 
to reach the appropriate drug dosage. 
Regarding the drug release studies, the thickness of the Kollicoat® layer should have been 
adjusted to achieve better results for the drug release in the simulated stomach medium, but it 
was not done due to lack of time. Also, tuning the thickness of both polymer coatings would 
be interesting to explore in the future to see if the micromotors are capable of a sustained drug 
release, in contrast to the burst release that was seen during this project.  
Finally, a detailed cytotoxicity study needs to be performed in order to determine if the 
prepared Mg-based Janus micromotors are potentially toxic to humans. The viability of human 
cells should be evaluated at different micromotors’ concentrations, and both short-term and 
long-term toxicity should be studied.
