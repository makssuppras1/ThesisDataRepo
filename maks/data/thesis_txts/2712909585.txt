Abstract
In this paper, the performance of Sliding Mode Control (SMC) for class­D amplifiers is
investigated. The analysis is done using state­space representation models of the syn­
chronous buck, and on the basis that asymptotic convergence can be proved. The sim­
ulated results show promising results in terms tracking ability and disturbance rejection.
The experimental results were in themselves rather lackluster with a closed loop Signal­to­
Noise Ratio (SNR) at 69.7 dB. However, despite quite significant coupling on the feedback
paths, the closed loop system was able to improve both noise and disturbance. Further­
more, the SMC class­D amplifier was shown to have good disturbance rejection 46 dB,
confirming the theoretical results. Finally, frequency response measurements show that
the closed loop system maintains system linearity.

Introduction
Sound reproduction poses an interesting engineering problem, especially given that the
accuracy hereof has a readily quantifiable result through sonic perception. Hence it is no
surprise that the pursuit for audio fidelity is an ongoing one, and that the latest iteration of
audio amplifiers, the class­D amplifier, has been heavily scrutinized in recent times. The
concept of using a nonlinear amplifier such as the switch­mode based class­D amplifier
is somewhat counter­intuitive considering that the constituents of audio are entirely har­
monic and linear.
However, the benefits of switch­mode technology cannot be overseen, even in the au­
dio industry. Switch­mode nonlinear amplification offers vastly superior effiency by, in
the ideal scenario, seperating current and voltage as apposed to linear amplification
where current and voltage exists concurrently. Thus the challenge of creating a high
fidelity class­D amplifier is a classic example of the engineering stubbornness; take a
system which is seemingly incompatible or flawed and make it viable through technical
workarounds. The primary strategy to suppress distortion and disturbances in a switch­
mode amplifier is feedback systems, and while modern amplifiers achieve very good re­
sults in terms of THD+N, the design approach is somewhat convoluted and experimentally
based.
This project aims to approach the problem of control in a systematic fashion by using
modern control theory based on rigorous mathematical foundation. More specifically, the
aim is to investigate the viability of SMC applied to class­D amplifiers. The analysis will
include the theoritical background, necessary to understand both the class­D amplifier
and SMC, simulation, to validate theory, and finally an experiment.

Theoritical Background
The following chapter describes the theoretical background, establishing the
foundation for the project work. Firstly, constituents of the class­D amplifiers
are accounted for. Then linear models of both the amplifier and loudspeaker
are presented. The principle action of sliding is explained and essential math­
ematical proofs are derived. Next performance measurements of the class­D
amplifier are presented. Finally previous work with sliding mode design and
current acquisition will be discussed.
2.1
The Class­D Amplifier
The class­D amplifier has during the last decades become the most widely used audio
amplifier due to superior efficiency and, as a result, smaller packaging. As an example,
the ICEpower module 400A2 offers up to 92% effiency and down to 0.0008% THD+N[6],
owing to the fact that modern class­D amplifiers perform well both in terms of efficiency,
distortion, and noise. In comparison, the Class­A and Class­B amplifiers have a maximum
efficiency of 25% and 78.5%, respectively [20].
In simple terms, The Class­D amplifier can be separated into three stages, namely being
the modulator, the power stage, and the output filter. On figure 2.1 these basic stages
as well as a loudspeaker is depicted. The effect each stage has on the signal is shown
on the waveforms below. As seen on the figure, the modulator converts the sinusoidal
audio signal to a pulse­width modulation PWM signal. By using the PWM signal to control
the activation of an inverter stage, the PWM signal is effectively amplified before being
filtered through a low pass filter which produces a sinusoidal signal once again. Figure 2.1

2.1.1
Modulator
The function of the modulator is to produce a PWM signal from a sinusoidal signal. The
modulator usually consists of a comparator, which compares the audio signal to a higher
frequency signal. However, for SMC, this higher frequency signal is not needed, as a
oscillatory behaviour of the system itself is used to generate a PWM signal.
The principle action of the comparator is creating linear combination of the system states,
i.e. the output voltage Vo, the inductor current iL, and the speaker current ispk, is created.
This linear combination is known as the sliding surface, σ. σ is constructed to swing
around the audio input signal which acts as a reference. By subtracting the reference
from σ, a signal which swings around 0 is created.

property can be achieved through dual supply as seen with the half­bridge synchronous
buck, depicted on figure 2.6. The full­bridge, also known as the Bridge­Tied­Load (BTL),
configuration offers a single­supply configuration, depicted on figure 2.7.

Total Harmonic Distortion
Total Harmonic Distortion (THD) is an expression which establishes the ratio of a set of
higher harmonic frequencies to the fundamental frequency. Hence it is useful for estimat­

An audio amplifier is by no means ideal, and hence the audio signal will be subjected to
nonlinear behaviour from the amplification process. This nonlinear behaviour results in a
distorted audio signal. Essentially the shape of the sinusoidal audio signals is changed.
Depending on the type of distortion and severerity, distortion may or may not be heard by
human ear. In general there are three types of distortion:
• Zero­crossing Distortion. The sine wave is distorted at zero­crossing of the sine
wave. Class­B amplifiers are known for this type of distortion because of their turn
on threshold.
• Clipping. If the desired signal exceeds the rails of amplifier, the peak of the sine
wave will be clipped.
• Slew Distortion. Slew distortion is when the sine wave becomes somewhat skewed.
From a listeners perspective, these types of distortion appear different, as slew distortion
can be more difficult to detect than the other types.

2.6
Reviewing Current Acquisition Methods and Previous
Work
Previous work has been done on sliding mode for switch­mode converters, especially in
the field of power converters [18, 10, 4]. However, most published papers on the subject
are focused on the theoretical aspect of the control part and rely on simulations for their
results, often neglecting practical implementation.
An important topic of discussion for the practical implementation of sliding mode is the ac­
quisition of current. In [5], Høyerby designs a current estimator using sliding mode PID,
which has the benefit of having fewer components, as the currents are estimated rather
than measured. In the interest of cost optimisation, this current estimator is of course a
valid choice. However, the compensation network required must have some price in the
form of delay, and it’s not entirely clear from Høyerby’s paper what the performance draw­
backs are. As I understand it, his primary argument against conventional sliding mode is
that it does not include a hysteresis model and hence cannot be used to estimate prac­
tical implementations. However, to my understanding, while it is true that certain design
aspects of sliding mode are based on the ideal case, it does not necessary exclude the
Sliding Mode Control for Class­D Audio Amplifiers
23
==PAGE:36==
designer from combining hysteresis models with conventional sliding mode.
For this paper, a more conventional approach to SMC is chosen, as the currents and
voltages used for feedback are measured. Both output voltage and output current mea­
surements are rather straightforward. The ripple current, however, can be measured
either from the filter inductor or the filter capacitor. The drawback of measuring the filter
inductor, is that some complexity is added, as the signal is offset and the reference sig­
nal will have to be subtracted. On the other hand, placing a shunt resistor in series with
the filter capacitor can affect the system negatively as a zero is introduced or moved, as
described in section 2.1.3. While moving this zero can be accepted, if the zero is placed
at a significantly high frequency, for this implementation the inductor current feedback is
chosen.

6
Conclusion
This concludes the work on sliding mode control for class­D amplifiers.
The mathematical foundation for sliding mode control has been established, from which
the proof of asymptotic stability of the SMC class­D amplfier has been established. Fur­
thermore, theoretical optimizing schemes have been utilized, from which candidate sliding
surfaces have been created and analysed.
To gauge the performance said candidate sliding surfaces, state space models have been
created and used for simulations. These simulation indicate that sliding mode has good
performance in terms of both low distortion and high disturbance rejection.
Finally, a PCB has been designed and built to validate the theoretical and simulated find­
ings. The experimental results were in themselves rather lackluster. However, despite
quite significant coupling on the feedback paths, the closed loop system was able to im­
prove both noise and disturbance. Furthermore, the SMC class­D amplifier was shown
to have good disturbance rejection, confirming the theoretical results. Finally, frequency
response measurements show that the closed loop system maintains system linearity.
As a final summation, SMC has been shown to have certain desireable characeristics
such as good tracking ability and high disturbance rejection. While it cannot be validated
that it has the potential to outperform state­of­the­art commercial solutions, its viability can
certainly not be ruled out from these findings.

Future Work
7.1
Higher Order Sliding Surfaces
Higher order sliding surfaces, allows the design of a control law, where the relative scaling
of the states is dependent on operating point. There are several uses for this, as it allows
the designer lots of freedom to design a very specific behaviour for each operating point.
It seems therefore as a natural step in the analysis of SMC for class­D audio amplifiers.
7.2
Loudspeaker Integration
As indicated by the designed PCB and theoretical investigation of loudspeaker modeling,
it was initially an intention to include the current flowing through the voice coil and include
it in the feedback. If the feedback from the speaker was included instead of the output
voltage, it would allow for compensation of nonlinearites of the speaker. It is very possible
that SMC could be effective for this kind of speaker feedback inclusion.
