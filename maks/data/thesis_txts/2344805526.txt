ABSTRACT 
 
Nowadays the use of green energies is seen as future, sustainability, development and 
wellness. Denmark is among the leading countries in these technologies and is doing 
everything as possible to maintain this position. One of the biggest goals for the 
government is to be fossil fuel free by 2050. How to accomplish this objective? I am 
honestly not sure at all. The only thing is clear to me is the important role that 
technologies like PVs and EVs will play in this transformation.  
 
The use of PVs system and EVs have already started to increase and it is needed to have 
an idea of the impact that a wide spread use of them may have in the low voltages 
networks. This is the main object of this thesis, which is going to be treated to reach by 
the implementation of a power quality analysis in a Danish standard low voltage system. 
 
In the thesis are described several parameters as voltage supply, harmonics, voltage 
unbalance and flicker impressions. Through a Matlab/Simulink model and using 
different configurations, all these parameters are measured with different penetration 
levels of EVs and PVs, it is also taken into account the seasonal and load profile 
changes.

INTRODUCTION 
One of the main objects that researches and analysts are working on, related to the 
power system operation, is to achieve the greatest possible development of smart grids. 
Without any doubt, low voltage residential networks constitute a difficult and important 
part of this goal.  
 
The high spread of smart grids turns even more challenging if it taken into consideration 
the increasing number of demand side innovations in the low voltage network [1]. All 
these developments have resulted in a need for tools that can model and simulate the 
behavior of low voltage networks effectively.  
 
It is expected that these tools contain some demand related research activities, such as: 
 Integration of local generators as photovoltaics panels (PVs) and new loads as 
electric vehicles (EVs): the main porpoise is to find optimal models that can be 
able to simulate the behavior of the system in order to find and analyze some 
issues that can be faced during normal functioning. 
 Power quality: it is always really important and also necessary to analyze the 
quality parameters of the network. With the new connections of large loads and 
non-lineal devices it is even more essential.  
 
Through this paper and taking into consideration the previous aspects, a model of a 
residential network will be created in order to analyze different parameters regarding to 
the power quality that can be affected by the integration of PVs and EVs with high 
penetration in this network.  
 
The parameters that will be investigated are the voltage supply variation, harmonics, 
voltage unbalance and flicker. In all the cases it will be checked if the values obtained 
are within the acceptable range established by the European standard EN50160. With 
the results is expected to get an overall approach which helps to predict generic system 
behavior in the future. At the same time, the outcomes will show the network capacity 
to face an increasing in the penetration level of new technologies and still fulfill the 
standards requirements.

1.1 
Background 
Denmark is the country of EU with the highest share of renewables in its energy mix. It 
has been proved that, through the promotion of energy efficiency, renewable energy and 
active energy policy, sustainable economic growth is possible. For this reason Denmark 
will continue betting on green energies in the coming decades. One example is the 
energy strategy recently adopted by the Danish government; the object is that the 
country becomes in the first country independent of fossil fuels by 2050. 
 
The foregoing leaves no doubt of the present and future that the green technologies have 
in Denmark. Something that proves this is the quick growth of PV technology because 
of subsidies and also because the low impact on the urban landscape, green resources 
and ease of installation in private properties. It is also possible to perceive an increase in 
the purchases of EVs in the last years, although it is necessary to say that probably, the 
advantages of the EVs in the car market can be reduced in the short future according to 
new regulations. With this scenario, the study of the integration of these two 
technologies in the electric network has a vital importance. 
1.2 
Problem formulation 
Power distribution networks operate with sinusoidal voltage, any variation of the 
voltage level or deviation of this voltage waveform from the ideal sine wave is a really 
important matter from the power quality point of view.  
 
A high penetration of EVs can easily vary the normal functioning of a low voltage 
network because of the significant changes in the power demand that they can cause in 
the plugging or unplugging process. Meanwhile, residential PV generators inject into 
the system a distorted waveform current that enlarges the level of harmonics in the 
network.  
 
Taking into consideration the previous issues, the following points have been especially 
addressed in this thesis for each studied parameter: 
 Which is the impact in the system of a high penetration level of PVs and EVs? 
 Is there any variation of the impact during the year? Any seasonal changes? 
 Which penetration level is the limit to make feasible fulfilling with the standard 
requirements? 
1.3 
Methodology, scope and assumptions 
Beside the theoretical analysis, Matlab/Simulink is the main tool used to build the 
models and perform the simulations to analyze the impact of the high level penetration 
of EVs and PVs in the voltage supply variations, harmonics, voltage unbalance and 
flicker.  
==PAGE:16==
 
Introduction 
14 
In the implemented models are used close approaches from real data. This is the case for 
the PV production; the available data have some discontinuities and there is missing 
information in some months. The estimations are calculated by using frequency 
distribution tables, with the object of being able to provide results for each season of the 
year.  
It is also estimated the household consumption for autumn and summer, from the real 
data obtained in another study for spring and winter. All the estimations are assumed to 
be correct in the result analysis of each analyzed parameter. 
 
The PV production data is taken in cycle by cycle waveform, resulting in a huge 
quantity of inputs that make impossible to run simulations for long periods of time. It 
was decided to obtain the information for the first three cycles and assume the same 
behavior the rest of the time. As some of the parameters should be studied with time 
intervals according to the reference standard; the use of the frequency distribution table 
was required again.   
 
In all the calculations, except in flicker chapter, is assumed that the EVs charging 
process is not used to supply services to the network. The difference in this case is 
because disconnections and reconnections of the vehicles are likely to cause more 
flicker problems. The particular assumptions in this part are: 
 The starts and stops of the different vehicles’ charging are independent and the 
general summation law of flicker can thus be used, 𝑃𝑡𝑜𝑡= √∑𝑃𝑖

flicker will thus have the same value.

In this chapter will be explained all the steps that were needed to implement the models. 
In order to obtain valuables results a lot of aspects starting from the location of the

Data sources 
2.1.1 
PV generation 
Due to the assumption that in some houses there is a PV system producing energy, this 
section of the report is particularly relevant for the understanding of the model and also 
for the accuracy and usefulness of the results.  
 
All the values used for the simulations are taken from the measurements performed to a 
real PV system; this system is called “0.19. Byn 329 Solar II”, and is situated at DTU 
Lyngby Campus. The way to access the information is using the software “Elspec 
Investigator 4.3.0.2” and logging in with the proper user name and password provided 
by the supervisor of this thesis. After checking different parameters for one year period, 
it was selected the active power production in order to get an overall idea of the 
production behavior.

In Figure 2-3 can be seen an orange block, “From Workspace”, which reads the data 
values specified in a time series. This time series is created to link every value of the 
current with a specific instant from the interval we are using for the study, this interval 
goes from 0 to 0.06 seconds. The next step is to add the angle of the current signal, for 
this case it is needed to take into account three angles; the angle of the phase, the power 
factor angle and the angle at which the maximum power output is maintained. 
 
At the end of each phase of the subsystem, a current source is used. This is the way that 
Simulink uses in templates to model a dynamic load. In this case, instead of putting the 
current flowing to the earth, the current is flowing into the system. It allows injecting 
into the system a current with the same characteristics as the one in the real PV 
installation. 
 
It is also important to point that the simulations performed took into consideration two 
scenarios, one with 50% and the other with 75% of PVs penetration level. 
2.1.2 
EVs 
In this report for the simulation of EVs have been considered single phase charging with 
a charging current of 10A and 16A. Three phases charging would have been also nice to 
take into account but since they are better from the network perspective, it was decided 
to go for the worst possible case.  
 
The same procedure was chosen in order to simulate the penetration of EVs. Nowadays 
is almost completely unlikely that in a normal neighborhood more than a 30% of the 
neighbors have an EV; even though, it was decided to make the simulations with a high 
penetration level because it could be the case in the future and at the same time all the 
results and measurements will have a big margin from a security point of view.  
 
In both scenarios, with 50% and 75% level of penetration, the EVs are evenly 
distributed in the three phases throughout the network, with the intention of avoiding 
unbalances in the system.  
 
The connections in each house is shown in Figure 2-4; in the figure can be seen the PV 
subsystem, the internal consumption and also the single phase ground load that is used 
to model the behavior of the EVs.

Figure 2-6: Location of the network. 
2.2.2 
Network distinctions 
Even though the network is deemed reasonably representative for the low voltages grids 
in Denmark; it is essential to say that low voltages grids in the country are designed 
very differently depending on the policy and traditions of the various network operators 
as well as the characteristics of the area that is supplied [3]. Therefore, absolute 
conclusions cannot be made based in the results obtained, but it is pretended that they 
can provide value information about the tendencies that can be expected.  
 
In the network studied, the external grid has been modelled by using a three phase 
source with a short circuit level of 3MVA, at a base voltage of 10.5 kV with an X/R 
ratio of 7. The transformer has a rated power of 400kVA with a nominal ratio of 
10.5/0.42kV.

VOLTAGE MAGNITUD VARIATIONS 
In this part of the document are going to be shown different behaviours of the voltage 
which affect the power quality of the network.  
Taking into account some obtained results in the simulations, a deeper analysis will be 
presented regarding to some of the treated behaviours. 
3.1 
Short duration voltage variations 
3.1.1 
Sags or dips 
Sags or dips consist of a reduction between 0.1 and 0.9 p.u in the RMS value of the 
voltage. The duration for this event is clearly defined, but can be accepted a range from 
2 ms (about 1/8 of a cycle) to a couple of minutes. 
 
Voltage sags are normally associated with system failures, energization of large loads, 
starting of high power engines and the energization of power transformers. The harmful 
effects of voltage dips depend on its duration and its depth, being related to the 
disconnection of computer equipment, programmable logic controllers (PLC) and 
contactors. Also it has effects on the motors’ speed. In Figure 3-1 can be seen 
represented in a graph this phenomenon. 
 
Different possibilities exist to mitigate the effects of sags. The first it is to stabilize the 
voltage signal through network conditioners, which exist with different principles and 
technologies. 
 
==PAGE:29==
 
Voltage Magnitud Variations 
27 
 
Figure 3-1: Voltage sag caused by a single line-to-ground (SLG) fault. 
 
3.1.2 
Swells 
Swell is defined as an increase in the voltage root mean square (RMS) value between 
1.1 and 1.8 p.u; lasting from 0.5 cycles to one minute. 
 
As in the case of depressions, swells are associated with failures in the system; although 
they are not as common as depression. A typical case is the temporary rise in the 
voltage in the phases not failed during a line to ground fault. They can also be caused by 
the disconnection of heavy loads or energization of large banks of capacitors. In Figure 
3-2 shows a graphical image of this. [4] 
 
 
Figure 3-2: Instantaneous voltage swell caused by a SLG fault 
3.1.3 
Interruptions 
An interrupt occurs when the voltage decrease to less than 0.1 p.u. within a period of 
time which does not exceed one minute. 
 
Interruptions can be the result of system failures, damaged equipment or malfunctioning 
control systems. The interrupts are characterized by their duration as the magnitude of 
the voltage it is always less than 10% of its nominal value as can be checked in the 
following figure.

3.2 
Long duration voltage variations 
These are the variations with deviations of the voltage RMS value lasting for more than 
one minute. Long duration variations can be either overvoltages or undervoltages, 
depending on the cause of the variation. They are not the result of system faults but by 
load variations on the system and system switching operations. 
3.2.1 
Overvoltages 
Overvoltage is the increase in the voltage to a level higher than 110% of the nominal 
voltage value for more than one minute. They are usually caused by the disconnection 
of large loads or by variations in the reactive compensation on the system (e.g., 
switching on a capacitor bank). It is also probably to have this kind of problem when the 
system is too weak to maintain voltage regulation or when the control voltage is 
inadequate. Choosing a correct tap selection in transformers can help to avoid 
overvoltages.

Low voltages or undervoltages 
Low voltage means the reduction in the RMS value of the voltage to a level less than 
90% of the nominal value for a longer than a minute. It is caused by the reverse of the 
events that cause overvoltages; a load connection or a capacitor bank turned off. 
Undervoltages can also be present in overloaded circuits. 
3.2.3 
Sustained interruptions 
A sustained interruption is considered as the absence of tension manifested by a period 
exceeding one minute. This type of interruptions is often permanent and requires human 
intervention to reset the system.

Harmonic sources 
Harmonics are the result of non-linear loads, which to a sinusoidal signal they have a 
non-sinusoidal response. The main sources of harmonics are: 
 Arc furnaces and other arc discharge elements, such as fluorescent lamps. Arc 
furnaces are considered more as voltage harmonic generators that current, 
typically appearing all harmonics (2nd, 3rd, 4th, 5th, ...) but predominating the 
odd ones with approximately the following values with respect to the 
fundamental: 
o 20% of 3rd harmonic. 
o 10% of 5th harmonic. 
o 6% of the 7th harmonic. 
o 3% of the 9th harmonic. 
==PAGE:40==
 
Harmonics 
38 
 Magnetic cores in transformers and rotating machines that require third 
harmonic current to excite iron. 
 The inrush current of transformers produce second and fourth harmonic. 
 Adjustable speed controllers used in fans, pumps and controllers processes. 
 Solid state switches modulated control current, light intensity, heat, etc. 
 Controlled sources for electronic equipment. 
 Rectifiers based on diodes or thyristors for welding equipment, batteries 
chargers, etc. 
 Static reactive power compensators. 
 Stations in DC high voltage transmission. 
 AC to DC converters (inverters). 
4.4 
Harmonic effects 
4.4.1 
Cables 
The current distribution through the cross section of a cable is only uniform when it is 
direct current. When it is alternating current, the more the frequency increases, the 
bigger is the non-uniformity distribution of the current. 
 
The current density in circular conductors is largest near the surface when it is carrying 
alternating current. This is because the variation of the magnetic flux is bigger in the 
center, increasing the inductive reactance, resulting in a lower density of the current 
through the center of the conductor. This phenomenon is called skin effect. 
4.4.2 
Transformers 
Most transformers are designed to operate with alternating current at a fundamental 
frequency (50 or 60 Hz), implying that operating at nominal load conditions and a 
temperature not higher than the specified room temperature, the transformer must be 
able to dissipate heat produced by their losses without overheating or damaging its life. 
Losses in transformers consist of no-load or core losses and load losses, include copper 
losses, eddy current losses and additional losses in the tank, fasteners, or other iron 
parts. 
 
Individually, the effect of harmonics on these losses is explained below: 
 No-load or core losses: produced by the excitation voltage in the core. The 
voltage waveform in the primary is considered sinusoidal independently of the 
load current, so it is not considered any increase for non-sinusoidal current load. 
Although the magnetizing current is harmonic, these are very small compared 
with the load current, so its effects on total losses are minimal. · 
 Copper losses: if the load current contains harmonics, then these losses will also 
increase by the skin effect. 
==PAGE:41==
 
Harmonics 
39 
 Eddy current losses: these losses to fundamental frequency are proportional to 
the square of the load current and the square of the frequency, which is why it 
can be got an excessive increase of these in the windings leading correlatives of 
non-sinusoidal load (and therefore also in its temperature). 
 Additional losses: these losses increase the temperature in the structural parts of 
the transformer, and depending on the type of transformer will either contribute 
or not to the increase of winding temperature. It is considered to vary with the 
square of the current and frequency. 
 
4.4.3 
Capacitors 
When capacitors are installed a power system is possible to find resonance between 
them and the system. This effect produces voltages and currents of magnitude 
substantially higher than in the case of no resonance. 
  
Since the reactance of capacitor decreases with increasing frequency, this will act as a 
"sink" for high frequency harmonic currents. This effect increases the dielectric strength 
and heating within the capacitor. Heating is not a problem because the capacitors are 
designed with films and flakes that significantly reduce the losses. On the other hand the 
dielectric strength is important to consider because the voltages on the capacitors are 
additive to the peak voltage of the fundamental. 
 
Although the capacitor’s dielectric laminate design allows high voltages, they produce 
decrease the useful life and fatigue the dielectric when subjected to overvoltage for long 
periods of time. 
4.4.4 
Motors 
The greatest effect with voltage harmonics in the rotary synchronous machines and the 
induction motors is the increasing of the heat due to losses in the iron and copper at high 
frequencies. It can be said that harmonics affect both the efficiency of the machine and 
the developed torque. 
 
Harmonic currents in engines can increase audible noise emission compared with 
sinusoidal excitation. They can also produce distributions of the resulting flow in the air 
gap which can cause or increase the phenomenon called "cogging" (the refusal to start 
soft) or the one called "crawling" (high sliding) in induction motors. 
 
Current harmonics couple such as the fifth and seventh have the potential to produce 
mechanical oscillations in combination such as turbine-generator or engine load 
systems. These are produced when the torque fluctuates and is caused by an interaction 
between the currents and magnetic field harmonic fundamental frequency. For example, 
==PAGE:42==
 
Harmonics 
40 
the fifth and seventh can combine to produce harmonic torque in the generator rotor at 
the frequency of sixth harmonic. If there is a mechanical resonance frequency close to 
the electric one, high mechanical forces can be developed in some parts of the engine. 
This combination of the harmonic currents also affects the rotational flow of the 
machine, so it is required more current to produce the same work, resulting in a 
decrease of the efficiency. [5] 
4.4.5 
Other equipment 
Sensitive electronics equipment is susceptible to malfunction due to harmonics. In some 
cases these devices rely on the accurate determination of crossing zero voltage or other 
aspects of their own waveform, so distortion conditions can affect the proper operation. 
With respect to measuring equipment and instrumentation, they are affected by 
harmonic components, especially if they have resonance conditions that cause high 
harmonic voltages in circuits. In the case of meters it can be obtained positive errors or 
negative, depending on the type of meter and harmonics involved.

VOLTAGE UNBALANCE 
5.1 
Definition 
To beginning with, it is important to point that a symmetrical system generation is the 
one where the three voltages have the same magnitude and their phasors are 120° apart 
from each other. On the other hand, a symmetrical three-phase load is the one which 
generates three currents of magnitudes and equal phases with respect to the voltage. 
The voltage unbalance is the phenomenon that occurs in three-phase systems where 
voltages and/or angles between consecutive phases are not equal. The perfect balance of 
voltages is technically unachievable. The continuous change of load on the network 
causes a permanent change in the magnitude of unbalance. 
 
Just the connection of single phase residential loads can cause an unbalance state of 
charge in the three-phase system; hence voltage drops system will not be balanced, 
resulting in this way in uneven levels of voltages.  
 
Figure 5-1: Symmetric components 
The unbalanced systems can be analyzed following the fact that any set of N unbalanced 
phasors could be expressed as the sum of N symmetrical sets of balanced phasors, for 
values of N that are prime, resulting in only a single frequency component represented 
by the phasors. In a three-phase system, one of the set of phasors (positive sequence, 
ABC) has the same phase sequence as the original case; the second set has the reverse 
phase sequence (negative sequence, ACB) and in the third set the three phasors A, B 
and C are in phase with each other (zero sequence) as can be seen in Figure 5-1. 
Basically, a three unbalanced phases are converted into three independent sources. [7] 
==PAGE:48==
 
Voltage Unbalance 
46 
The positive sequence set corresponds to the power flow from the grid to the load, or 
what is the same, from the generator to downstream. The power supplied or power 
generated has only positive sequence representation; therefore, there is no generation of 
negative or zero sequence in symmetrical generation systems. 
 
The negative sequence set is an indication of the unbalanced existing in the three-phase 
system. It shows basically the lack of symmetry among the voltage phasors in the 
connection point. 
 
The presence of zero sequence components is linked to the connection with respect to 
ground. The homopolar currents are those that do not close the circuit by the active 
phases, but do it through the neutral or through the ground of the circuit. 
 
In Figure 5-2 can be seen a symmetric system with unbalanced load, generating 
currents and voltage drops. Each line current is decomposes into its three sequence 
components. The positive and negative components belong only to the active phases; 
however, the homopolar component closes the circuit through the neutral or ground. 
The neutral current is equivalent to three times the existing zero sequence in each of the 
phases. 
 
 
 
Figure 5-2: Currents

5.3 
Causes 
The main cause of this phenomenon is the single-phase loads on the three-phase system 
due to a non-distributed distribution, especially low voltage consumers with single-
phase connection. 
 
For equal dispersion of single-phase loads, the type of network configuration either in 
distribution or transmission, affects the spread of unbalance. The radial network 
configuration will show higher levels than a mesh one. 
 
The self and mutual impedances between unbalanced phases will present unbalances in 
the voltage drops even with symmetrical loads. Another cause is a three-phase capacitor 
bank with a phase out of service; this case will present an unbalance in the 
compensation of reactive current. Another source of unbalance is three-phase arc 
furnaces, which in normal operation, have variable load unbalances along the smelting 
process. [8]

5.5 
Unbalance effects  
In general, the effects are summarized in the emergence of inverse and zero sequence 
current components, resulting in: 
 Additional losses of power and energy. 
 Additional heating in machines, limiting the rated load capacity. 
 Reduction of distribution systems in power transport. 
 Propagation of unbalance to other connection nodes of the network.

FLICKER  
6.1 
Definition 
In the analysis of disturbances in electrical networks, the issues of greatest influence on 
the quality of electricity are the harmonics, overvoltages, interruptions and flicker. 
During the last several years, organizations and countries around the world have 
concerned more about flicker due to the consequences generated by this phenomenon, 
mainly on those exposed to it. 
Flicker is the variation of the intensity of the luminous flux, affecting human vision; this 
is generated by fluctuations (modulations) in the grid voltage. It is a problem of visual 
perception, since the phenomenon depends mainly on people who observe it. 
The discomfort caused in the vision causes premature eyestrain and irritability in the 
pupils due to the continuous expansion and contraction within the level of lighting, 
especially when a reader goes through the text of a book. [9] 
 
Statistically it was shown that the human eye is very sensitive to flicker, achieving 
maximum irritability at frequencies of 8.8 Hz with a modulation threshold of 0.25% of 
the voltage (light intensity). The most sensitivity range goes from 6 to 10 Hz, while for 
lower and higher frequencies the effect is progressively smaller. 
Frequencies considered as flicker depends on the rated voltage and frequency. For 
systems of 50Hz, flicker range goes from 0.05Hz to 35Hz at a rated voltage of 230V. 
For 60Hz systems the range stands from 0.05Hz to 42 Hz at 120V. Both flicker 
frequency ranges were recorded with incandescent illumination of 60W, and arise 
because of the difference in the time constants of the filaments for each voltage, causing 
different responses in frequency. 
6.2 
Causes 
Flicker can be motivated by disturbances introduced during the generation, transmission 
or distribution of energy; but these irregularities are generally caused by the use of large 
fluctuating loads (loads whose demand for active or reactive power fluctuates rapidly). 
The origin of flicker is in the behavior of lighting systems (filament and discharge 
lamps, mainly) to fluctuations in supply voltage. These variations in voltage occur 
mostly by sudden changes in the loads connected to the power grid, as occurs during 
normal operation of an arc furnace, motors with pulsating loads, arc welding machines, 
==PAGE:56==
 
Flicker 
54 
the connections of large induction motors, compressors, capacitor banks, welding 
machines, among others. The effect is even bigger if they are connected to deficient 
electrical networks with low short circuit power. 
 
Flicker can be classified according to their nature, into two categories: systematic or 
periodic and random or non-periodic. It is important to say that both of them can be 
present simultaneously.

not have a constant value but it is between certain values. To measure the severity of

6.5 
Physiologic effects because of luminous flux 
changes 
The most important effect of the variability of luminous flux is the physiological 
because it affects the ergonomics of the production environment, causing fatigue and 
decreasing workers concentration level. The response of human eye-brain combination 
has characteristics of bandpass filter between 0.5 Hz and 35 Hz, with a maximum 
sensitivity to the luminous flux at a frequency around 8-9 Hz. The physiological effects 
depend on the extent of luminous flux variations, the frequency spectrum and duration 
==PAGE:58==
 
Flicker 
56 
of the disturbance. The brain's response to light stimulus has an inertial characteristic 
with a time constant of about 300 ms, which means that the slow variations of the 
luminous flux are perceived while rapid variations are "slowed down". For example, 
two short luminous flux variations produced within a range of 300 ms are perceived as a 
single variation. [10]  
As the response to flicker varies from one person to another, for the quantification of 
flicker in the standards is used a standardized fully electronic which models the 
combination of the behavior of a tungsten light source with the human response to such 
behavior. Initially, the designs of measuring instruments were based on the simple 
observation of luminous flux, but then a model of human reaction (manifested in the 
form of discomfort or annoyance) to the fluctuation of luminous flux developed. The 
reference model is based on an incandescent tungsten filament lamp of 60W fed with 
230V.

voltage levels. The graph is defined by the voltage change percentage (ordinates or y-

lesser extent against a same variation of the rated voltage.

CONCLUSION 
 
7.1 
Results Analysis  
In order to analyze the results it is needed to take into account first, that the high 
penetration levels of EVs and PVs used are unlikely to happen nowadays in a normal 
Danish low voltage network. This means that the bad results obtained in the 
measurement of parameters like voltage unbalance and harmonics should be taken as an 
opportunity to find the right way to overcome them.  
 
Starting with the results of voltage supply, it can be said that despite of the presented 
distortions on the voltage waveforms and normal decreasing of the voltage value with 
increasing the distance of the feeder from the transformer; at any moment was reached 
neither the minimum nor the maximum limit established by the standards. Regarding to 
the flicker impression the results are within the allowed range but it is true that it would 
be much better to perform a deeper analysis of this parameter assuming a bigger 
interaction between the EVs' charging and the grid. 
 
About the harmonics analysis, should be pointed that the standard limits for the total 
harmonic distortion (THD) was not rebased, however individual limits of some third 
order harmonics did; meaning with this that the system is not ready to deal with such 
penetration levels.  
 
Lastly and not for that less important, it is the case of the voltage unbalance, without 
any doubt these were the poorest measurement according to the standard limits. The 
good part of these bad results is that can be established a direct link between poor 
performance and a higher PV production as well with positions further away from the 
transformer in the feeder. The analysis of this link can be by itself the start to find a 
solution to the problem.   
7.2 
Future work 
It could have been possible to investigate another parameters related to power quality 
but due to the limited time and lack of some necessary data, it was not possible.
