Summary 
The escalating global demand for sustainable energy and chemical production has intensified interest 
in gas fermentation as a renewable platform for generating value-added biochemicals. This study 
investigates the production of acetic acid from carbon monoxide (CO)-rich gas streams using 
thermophilic mixed microbial cultures in trickle bed reactors (TBRs). The aim was to enhance acetic 
acid productivity and selectivity under continuous operation at 60 °C, utilizing gas compositions of up 
to 90% CO. A series of kinetic experiments and microbial enrichment strategies were employed to 
understand the metabolic pathways involved in CO, CO₂, and H₂ conversion to acetate. Four key 
conversion routes were examined: (i) direct CO (60% and 100%) to acetate and water-gas shift (WGS) 
reaction followed by reductive acetogenesis using H₂ and CO₂, (ii) co-utilization of H₂ and CO₂, (iii) 
reduction of volatile fatty acids to corresponding alcohols and (iv) chain elongation. 
Experimental data from microbial batch kinetics revealed an acetic acid yield higher or close to ng the 
theoretical one, indicating an efficient substrate utilization towards acetic production and minimized 
by-product formation. The Trickle Bed Reactors (TBR), operated under thermophilic conditions, the 
acetic acid titer increased after the inoculation of Moorella thermoacetica, reaching a maximum titer 
of 2.1 g/L in the final stage of operation for both TBRs. This sustained acetate accumulation highlights 
the reactor's capacity to support prolonged microbial activity and consistent product formation. 
This work highlights thermophilic gas fermentation in trickle bed reactors (TBRs) as a promising and 
selective route for acetic acid production from industrial off-gases, with minimal by-product formation. 
Nonetheless, further research is essential to optimize system performance and ensure the scalability of 
this approach for industrial application.

1. Introduction and scope of the project 
The continuous growth of global energy demand, driven by the rising population and the expansion of 
industrial activities, necessitates the transition to alternative chemicals from renewable energy sources. 
In this context, syngas fermentation, which utilizes a gas mixture primarily composed of carbon 
monoxide (CO), hydrogen (H₂), and carbon dioxide (CO₂), has drawn increasing scientific interest 
over the past decade as a promising alternative for generating renewable energy (Gavala et al., 2021). 
Gas/syngas fermentation represents an efficient alternative for converting waste gases into valuable 
biofuels and chemicals. This approach is advantageous because of its high specificity in product 
generation, flexibility in substrate composition, and lower energy requirements compared to catalytic 
conversion techniques (Lanzillo et al., 2024). 
Despite these benefits, the key factor in utilizing syngas is the effective conversion of CO, as it serves 
as both a carbon and energy source for specialized microorganisms (Lü et al., 2018). Furthermore, CO, 
a toxic byproduct generated in substantial quantities by industries such as petrochemicals, power 
generation, and iron smelting, can be repurposed as a feedstock for the production of organic chemicals 
(Nam et al., 2016). One of the most promising applications of gas fermentation is the production of 
acetic acid, which not only serves as a valuable final product but also acts as an intermediate for further 
chemical synthesis (El-Gammal et al., 2017). This process offers the dual advantage of addressing 
waste management challenges while simultaneously providing economic benefits by reducing disposal 
costs and recovering carbon in the form of valuable products (El-Gammal et al., 2017). 
Gas fermentation relies on the Wood-Ljungdahl pathway, a metabolic route utilized by autotrophic 
microorganisms that utilize single-carbon (C1) compounds such as CO and CO₂ as carbon sources, 
while CO and H₂ serve as energy sources (Gunes, 2021). Unlike other microbial pathways, C1 
compounds are used simultaneously as carbon and energy sources, sustaining microbial growth 
(Gunes, 2021). The Wood-Ljungdahl pathway begins with a series of reduction reactions that convert 
CO and H₂ into acetyl-CoA, a key intermediate formed during acetogenesis (Gunes, 2021). Acetyl-
CoA can subsequently undergo further conversion during solventogenesis, leading to the production 
of biofuels and chemicals, such as ethanol, butanol, and butyrate, with acetate serving as a precursor. 
CO enters this pathway either directly through the carbonyl (Western) branch or as CO₂ via reversible 
water-gas shift reactions (Gunes, 2021). Notably, CO plays a crucial role in this process by providing 
both the carbon backbone and the electrons required for reduction reactions (eq.1).

In addition to acetic acid, other byproducts such as ethanol and butyrate can be produced. However, 
ethanol is often consumed alongside acetic acid to generate butyric acid (eq.2) (Quintela et al., 2024a). 
To optimize acetic acid production, it is essential to minimize its conversion into longer chain 
carboxylic acids such as butyric acid (Quintela et al., 2024a). Identifying key factors that influence 
acetic acid consumption and ethanol production is critical, as they indirectly promote butyric acid 
formation (Harahap and Ahring, 2023). By controlling these parameters, the metabolic pathway can 
be directed towards enhanced acetic acid synthesis. Beyond process parameters, the use of mixed 
microbial cultures presents significant advantages over pure cultures for syngas fermentation (El-
Gammal et al., 2017). Mixed microbial consortia exhibit greater adaptability to varying substrates, 
increased robustness, and reduced sterility requirements, making them more suitable for large-scale 
industrial applications compared to pure cultures (El-Gammal et al., 2017).

In addition to microbial community selection, optimizing bioreactor configurations plays a vital role 
in enhancing mass transfer efficiency for gaseous substrates (Asimakopoulos et al., 2018). Among the 
various configurations available, trickle bed reactors (TBRs) are particularly promising due to their 
high volumetric mass transfer coefficients (Asimakopoulos et al., 2018). TBRs are uniquely designed 
with packing material through which a recirculating liquid medium flows, promoting the formation of 
microbial biofilms essential for fermentation. These biofilms enable the efficient conversion of gas 
into liquid products by harnessing the metabolic capabilities of attached microorganisms (Gavala et 
al., 2021). While TBRs have been extensively studied for bio-methanation, their application in 
chemical production remains relatively unexplored, despite their significant potential for biobased 
chemical synthesis (Quintela et al., 2024b). 
The objective of this study is to enhance acetic acid productivity and specificity from CO using a mixed 
microbial consortium in trickle bed reactors. The investigation examines acetic acid production in two 
co-current TBRs supplied with 40% of CO and then changed to 90% CO under thermophilic conditions 
over 109 day period.

2. State-of-the-art 
This study explores the adaptability of thermophilic microbial communities to high CO gas 
concentrations for valuable chemical production in trickle bed reactors (TBRs). Based on available 
literature, limited research has focused on the adaptation of thermophilic microbes to CO in continuous 
systems, which could enhance chemical production and selectivity of the fermentation towards acetic 
acid. This work examines continuous gas fermentation in TBRs under 90% CO. 
2.1 Acetic Acid 
Acetic acid is an essential industrial chemical with applications in various sectors, including the 
chemical, food, and polymer industries (Li et al., 2015). Traditionally, acetic acid is produced from 
fossil fuel-based sources such as mineral oil and natural gas (Li et al., 2015). However, with the 
increasing depletion of these resources, alternative and more sustainable production methods must be 
explored (Li et al., 2015).  
Acetic acid plays a crucial role in multiple industries. It is primarily used in the production of vinyl 
acetate monomer (VAM), a key precursor for vinyl plastics, adhesives, textile finishes, and latex paints 
(Merli et al., 2021). Additionally, it serves as a feedstock for the synthesis of polymeric materials such 
as polyvinyl acetate, acetate esters, and cellulose acetate (Merli et al., 2021). It is also an essential 
solvent in the production of terephthalic acid, a raw material for polyethylene terephthalate (PET) 
manufacturing (Merli et al., 2021). In the food industry, acetic acid is widely utilized as vinegar, a food 
additive (E260), and a descaling agent in household applications (Merli et al., 2021). 
Currently, acetic acid is primarily produced through three petrochemical processes: n-butane oxidation, 
methanol carbonylation, and acetaldehyde oxidation (Merli et al., 2021). These processes rely heavily 
on non-renewable fossil fuel resources, which raise sustainability concerns (Cheryan et al., 1997). With 
the global push toward greener alternatives, it is necessary to develop renewable production routes that 
reduce environmental impact while ensuring a stable supply of acetic acid. 
Microbial fermentation of CO offers a sustainable and cost-effective approach for acetic acid 
production. Lignocellulosic biomass and C1 gases provide highly available and low-cost feedstocks 
for generating acetate-containing streams, which can be further converted by acetate-metabolizing 
microorganisms and/or chemical catalytic processes into a variety of bio-based products (Sim et al., 
2007). Compared to chemical processes, biological production has several advantages, including 
higher specificity, lower production costs, and minimal pollutant emissions (Sim et al., 2007). 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
One of the most promising biological routes for acetic acid production is gas fermentation, which 
utilizes CO, CO₂, and H₂ gases derived from gasification of biomass or industrial waste gases. This 
process is carried out by acetogenic bacteria, which convert gas components into acetic acid through 
the Wood-Ljungdahl pathway (Lim, 2018). Gas fermentation offers a highly efficient and sustainable 
approach to acetic acid production, making use of abundant and low-cost carbon sources while 
reducing greenhouse gas emissions. This approach aligns with the growing demand for bio-based 
chemicals and fuels, providing a renewable and environmentally friendly alternative to traditional 
fossil fuel-based production methods (Lim, 2018). 
Biological conversion, specifically gas fermentation, presents significant advantages over catalytic 
processes for fuel and chemical production. In terms of process efficiency, gas fermentation offers 
superior flexibility and robustness compared to metal-catalyst-based approaches like Fischer-Tropsch 
(FT) synthesis (Wilkins and Atiyeh, 2011). Biological catalysts are less sensitive to fluctuations in the 
gas composition, enabling them to tolerate varying gas ratios and contaminants such as sulfur and CO2, 
which can inhibit or deactivate traditional metal catalysts (Wilkins and Atiyeh, 2011). This attribute 
negates the need for expensive and complex gas-shifting processes, making gas fermentation more 
cost-effective. 
Moreover, gas fermentation systems typically demonstrate higher selectivity than FT catalysts, leading 
to greater production yields and fewer undesired by-products (Wilkins and Atiyeh, 2011). In contrast, 
FT catalysts exhibit a lower selectivity, often around 45%, resulting in a mix of by-products that require 
additional processing (Wilkins and Atiyeh, 2011). Biological catalysts, with their enhanced specificity, 
simplify downstream processing, reducing the need for costly separation or purification steps 
(Heiskanen et al., 2007). The operational conditions required for FT processes are another 
disadvantage when compared to gas fermentation. FT synthesis necessitates high temperatures and 
pressures (exceeding 7 MPa), which not only increases the capital and operational expenses but also 
impose technical challenges (Datta, 2011). On the other hand, gas fermentation operates under milder 
conditions typically around 30–40°C for mesophilic and 50–60°C for thermophilic and at atmospheric 
pressure or moderately elevated pressure up to 5 atm, while FT operates at higher temperatures around 
150-300°C. These mild conditions reduce the need for specialized high-pressure equipment and 
enhance safety (James, 2012). Moreover, gas fermentation utilizes self-regenerating microbial 
catalysts that require only a small fraction of the feed gas and growth media (Van Steen, 2008). Lastly, 
the use of biological catalysts in gas fermentation significantly reduces the reliance on expensive 
materials, such as metals needed in FT catalysts. This makes the overall process more economically 
viable (Van Steen, 2008). 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
Additionally, gas fermentation offers an opportunity to convert diverse and low-cost feedstocks—
ranging from industrial waste gases to biomass and municipal solid waste—into valuable fuels and 
chemicals, further enhancing its sustainability and economic potential (James, 2012).  
2.2 Gas fermentation 
The increasing demand for sustainable and carbon-neutral production of energy and commodities has 
driven the development of technologies that reduce carbon emissions. The production of first-
generation biofuels has raised concerns regarding competition between food and fuel, reinforcing the 
need for alternative biotechnological solutions (Kopke, 2014). One promising approach that has gained 
significant attention in recent years is gas fermentation, a process in which microorganisms 
anaerobically convert gaseous substrates into biofuels and biochemicals (Kopke, 2014). This 
technology presents a viable alternative to fossil-based processes while contributing to greenhouse gas 
mitigation and carbon recycling. 
Gas fermentation utilizes the metabolic capabilities of certain microorganisms to convert CO and CO₂ 
into valuable products. Fermentation organisms generate specific products in a single step, allowing 
the production of acetate, butanol, or 2,3-butanediol in controlled ratios (Kopke, 2014). Acetogenic 
bacteria, in particular, have demonstrated the greatest industrial potential due to their ability to employ 
the Wood-Ljungdahl pathway (WLP), a metabolic route that enables the fixation of C1 compounds for 
biofuel and biochemical production (Gunes, 2021). This process offers an efficient means of utilizing 
waste gases from industrial processes, such as steel manufacturing, oil refining, and the petrochemical 
industry (Gunes, 2021).  
The WLP begins with a series of oxidoreduction reactions that transform CO and H₂ into acetyl-CoA, 
which serves as a key metabolic intermediate in acetogenesis (Gunes, 2021). CO plays a central role 
in this process, providing both the carbon backbone and the electrons required for reduction reactions. 
Furthermore, CO fermentation can lead to the predominant production of acetic acid without requiring 
an additional energy source, enhancing carbon recovery efficiency (Battle-Vilanova, 2016 ; Sim, 
2008). 
Several factors influence the efficiency of microbial growth and metabolite production in gas 
fermentation. The composition and ratio of CO and CO₂ in the gas substrate directly affect fermentation 
performance, while the partial pressure of CO plays a crucial role in regulating cell growth and product 
yields (Sim, 2008).  
Additionally, pH control is essential for optimizing microbial activity and metabolic fluxes. Given 
these advantages, gas fermentation represents a highly promising and sustainable approach for acetic 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
acid production, offering a biotechnological platform that reduces reliance on fossil resources while 
supporting carbon-neutral industrial processes. 
2.3 Biochemical Pathways for Acetic Acid Production 
Gas fermentation relies on the Wood-Ljungdahl pathway, a metabolic process used by autotrophic 
microbes to assimilate single-carbon (C1) compounds such as CO and CO₂ as carbon sources, while 
utilizing CO and H₂ as energy sources, although certain microbes can use C1 compounds for both, 
facilitating their growth (Gunes, 2021). As illustrated in fig.1, this pathway begins with reactions that 
convert CO and H₂ into acetyl-CoA, a crucial intermediate formed during acetogenesis. Acetyl-CoA 
can subsequently be utilized in solventogenesis to produce biofuels and chemicals, including ethanol, 
butanol, and butyrate, with acetate serving as a precursor.

More specifically, CO enters the pathway either directly via the carbonyl (Western) branch or as CO₂ 
through reversible water-gas shift reactions, playing a dual role by supplying both the carbon backbone 
and the electrons necessary for reduction (Gunes, 2021). 
While acetic acid is a primary product of this pathway, byproducts such as ethanol and butyrate are 
also generated. Ethanol can be further consumed alongside acetic acid to form butyric acid (Quintela 
et al., 2024b). To maximize acetic acid yield, it is essential to minimize its diversion toward byproducts 
such as butyric acid (Quintela et al., 2024b). This optimization requires identifying factors that favor 
acetic acid consumption and ethanol production, as these conditions indirectly enhance butyric acid 
formation (Harahap and Ahring, 2023). By mitigating these influences, the metabolic flux can be 
directed toward increased acetic acid production. 
2.4 Microbial groups/cultures for thermophilic gas fermentation 
Pure and mixed microbial cultures play distinct roles in syngas fermentation for acetate production, 
each offering unique advantages. Pure cultures, particularly species within the Clostridium genus such 
as Clostridium ragsdalei, have been widely studied for their ability to efficiently convert syngas 
components into acetate (El-Gammal et al., 2017). However, several challenges must be addressed 
before large-scale commercialization, including gas-liquid mass transfer limitations, variability in 
syngas composition, microbial catalyst efficiency, and product recovery (El-Gammal et al., 2017). Pure 
cultures often require strictly sterile conditions to maintain optimal performance, making them less 
adaptable to industrial-scale operations (Singla et al., 2014). 
In contrast, anaerobic fermentation by mixed cultures has demonstrated high acetate yields and greater 
process stability, making it an attractive alternative for large-scale production. Mixed cultures require 
no sterilization, consist of diverse microorganisms that work synergistically, and can utilize a broader 
range of substrates, including those with toxic components, all while maintaining lower operational 
costs (Singla et al., 2014). They are also more tolerant to fluctuations in syngas composition, where 
pure cultures typically require a more consistent gas ratio to sustain productivity (Esquivel-Elizondo 
et al., 2017). Studies have reported acetate yields of up to 42 g/L using mixed culture fermentation, 
highlighting its efficiency in converting syngas into valuable biochemicals (Wang et al., 2017). 
Syngas fermentation for acetate production has been successfully tested with both mesophilic and 
thermophilic inoculate. Wang et al. 2017 demonstrated that an inoculum containing thermophilic 
bacteria, predominantly from the genera Thermoanaerobacterium and Thermohydrogenium, 
effectively produced acetate in both batch and continuous fermentation. Similar studies with 
mesophilic inoculate, dominated by Clostridium strains at temperatures between 35 and 37°C, have 
==PAGE:14==

shown comparable acetate production efficiency (Liu et al., 2018a; Luo et al., 2018; Rao et al., 2018; 
Zhang et al., 2013). These findings reinforce the potential of mixed culture fermentation as a robust 
and cost-effective approach for acetate production from syngas, offering a more adaptable and resilient 
alternative to pure cultures.

2.4.2.1 Enrichments 
Microbial enrichments are widely used in order to allow researchers to study microbial adaptation, 
metabolic interactions, and the biochemical pathways involved in substrate utilization under controlled 
conditions (Browne et al., 2016). In the context of CO metabolism, enrichment cultures help in the 
isolation and characterization of carboxydotrophic microorganisms capable of oxidizing CO into CO₂, 
a process relevant for industrial waste gas treatment, biofuel production, and microbial ecology (Drake 
et al., 2008). Carbon monoxide (CO) is a key component of syngas and industrial off-gases. It can 
serve as both a carbon and energy source for CO-oxidizing bacteria through pathways such as the 
water-gas shift reaction (CO + H₂O → CO₂ + H₂) and the Wood-Ljungdahl Pathway (WLP) (Ragsdale, 
2004).  
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
Studies have demonstrated that microbial communities enriched in CO-atmosphere can adapt to high 
CO concentrations and efficiently convert CO into CO₂ under anaerobic conditions (Anne M Henstra 
et al., 2007a). 
Microbial enrichments for CO oxidation have applications in syngas fermentation, biofuel production, 
and greenhouse gas mitigation. Studies have demonstrated that tailored enrichment strategies can 
enhance CO conversion efficiency and selectively cultivate desired microbial groups (Infantes, 2020).  
 
2.5 Reactor types for gas fermentation 
Both batch and continuous-flow bioreactors have been studied for syngas fermentation. In batch 
reactors, the gaseous substrate is introduced into a closed system where fermentation occurs, with the 
gas supply provided continuously. Liquid samples are collected at specific intervals during 
fermentation. (Klasson et al., 1990) investigated kinetic parameters through a series of batch 
experiments. Traditionally, syngas fermentation has been studied using continuous stirred-tank 
reactors (CSTRs). Other commonly explored bioreactors include bubble column reactors, monolithic 
biofilm reactors, trickling bed reactors, and microbubble dispersion stirred-tank reactors, all of which 
have been evaluated in both batch and continuous modes. This section provides a brief overview of 
the various reactor configurations used in syngas fermentation.

2.5.1 Continuous Stirred Tank Reactor (CSTR) 
The fundamental principle of CSTR operation involves continuous agitation to maintain uniform 
concentration and temperature throughout the reactor volume. One or more agitators, mounted on a 
shaft connected to a gearbox and motor (Fig.2 (B)), regulate rotational speed, which is a critical 
economic parameter influencing process sustainability (Klasson et al., 1990). In syngas fermentation, 
gaseous substrates and liquid culture media are continuously fed into the reactor to support microbial 
metabolism, while fermentation products are simultaneously withdrawn at the same flow rate. Baffled 
impellers ensure a high level of agitation, improving mass transfer between the gaseous substrate and 
microbes. Higher impeller speeds break gas bubbles into finer ones, increasing their accessibility to

The continuous stirred-tank reactor (CSTR) is widely utilized in biotechnological industries due to its 
straightforward operational principle and effective mixing capabilities. In syngas fermentation, 
however, its application presents several challenges. The CSTR requires high agitation speeds, 
prolonged mixing durations, increased bioreactor pressure, and optimized agitator and baffle designs 
to enhance the gas-liquid interfacial area, addressing mass transfer limitations (Klasson et al., 1990, 
1992; Riggs, 2008)  
To improve gas dispersion, impeller speed must be increased, which consequently raises agitation 
power input per unit volume for bubble breakup. This results in significant energy consumption and 
associated costs  (Klasson et al., 1990; Yasin et al., 2015). Moreover, excessive agitation can damage 
shear-sensitive bacteria, potentially destabilizing the fermentation process, making CSTRs less 
suitable for large-scale syngas fermentation (Klasson et al., 1992; Zhang et al., 2021). 
2.5.2 Bubble Column Reactor (BCR) 
Bubble column reactors (BCRs) are a viable alternative to continuous stirred-tank reactors (CSTRs) 
because of their lower energy consumption and superior gas-liquid mass transfer efficiency. The 
fundamental design of a BCR consists of a cylindrical vessel filled with liquid, where a gas sparger at 
the bottom generates bubbles that disperse convectively throughout the column (Fig.2 (D)), promoting 
mixing (Asimakopoulos et al., 2018). Efficient gas-liquid mass transfer can be achieved by reducing 
bubble diameter through microbubble dispersers (micro-spargers) and increasing the gas flow rate, 
which enhances the gas-liquid interfacial area (Kundiyana et al., 2011; Zhang et al., 2021). However, 
at higher gas flow rates, efficient mixing is achieved, but syngas conversion remains incomplete, 
necessitating gas recirculation to optimize fermentation efficiency (Shen et al., 2014). 
To address challenges such as cell washout due to high dilution rates, modifications to BCRs have 
been developed, including cell immobilization and configurations incorporating cell recycling with 
simultaneous gas feeding (Lee et al., 2020a; Zhang et al., 2021), both of which enhance cell density 
and acetate production. However, a key limitation of BCRs is the occurrence of excessive foaming, 
which often requires the addition of anti-foaming agents (Harahap and Ahring, 2023). 
The design of bubble column reactors must consider two critical factors: the diameter-to-length ratio 
and the type of gas sparger used to generate bubbles. Gas dispersion within the liquid phase occurs via 
convective flow, driven by density gradients within the column (Asimakopoulos et al., 2018). 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
2.5.3 Hollow Fiber Membrane Biofilm Reactor (HFMBR) 
A hollow fiber membrane placed inside a syngas fermenter facilitates the formation of a biofilm 
population on the outer microporous membrane surface, leading to the development of the hollow fiber 
membrane biofilm reactor (HFMBR) (Fig.2 (C)). In this reactor configuration, syngas flows through 
the interior of the hollow fiber module and diffuses across the microporous membrane into the liquid 
bulk region without forming bubbles (Wang et al., 2017). The biofilm naturally attaches to the outer 
membrane surface, where it grows by directly consuming the diffused syngas. The HFMBR is 
specifically designed to minimize energy consumption, shear stress, and reactor size requirements 
compared to conventional bioreactors (Shen et al., 2018). Orgill et al. (2013) highlighted that this 
reactor exhibits superior mass transfer characteristics compared to continuous stirred-tank reactors 
(CSTRs) and bubble column reactors (BCRs), which rely on high agitation speeds and elevated liquid 
flow rates, respectively. 
A major challenge associated with membrane-based processes is fouling or biofouling due to excessive 
biofilm growth, which reduces gas flux through the membrane. Consequently, bioreactor 
improvements focus on controlling biofilm thickness to mitigate biofouling. Additionally, the choice 
of membrane material is crucial to prevent liquid permeation and ensure resistance to high internal gas 
pressure. A viable solution is the use of a non-porous ultra-thin hydrophobic layer, which enhances 
membrane durability and performance (Munasinghe and Khanal, 2012). 
2.5.4 Trickle Bed Reactor (TBR) 
Trickle bed reactors (TBRs) are a type of packed bed reactor in which biofilm develops on the surface 
of packing material while receiving carbon and electron donors from syngas and essential nutrients 
from the liquid medium (Fig.2 (A)). The liquid phase trickles through the packed bed, ensuring 
continuous contact with both the gaseous and liquid substrates ((Zhang et al., 2021). 
Typically, TBRs are equipped with an inlet spreader to promote uniform distribution of gas and liquid, 
allowing them to operate in either co-current or counter-current flow modes (Gunes, 2021). 
Additionally, cyclic operation, where the fluid flow periodically alternates at the reactor inlet and 
outlet, has been explored; however, its complex hydrodynamics make process stability and control 
challenging in large-scale applications, making co-current and counter-current operations more 
favorable (Atta et al., 2014). 
TBRs are relatively simple to operate and maintain while offering high operational flexibility, 
including the ability to function under elevated pressure conditions, which enhances gas transfer into 
the liquid phase (Pagans et al., 2005). 
==PAGE:19==
19 
 
A crucial factor in their efficiency is the homogeneous distribution of the liquid film over the packed 
bed surface, where bacterial cells are highly concentrated. The formation of a thin liquid film around 
the packing material reduces mass transfer resistance, facilitating improved gas-liquid interactions 
(Mederos et al., 2009). Moreover, TBRs exhibit high fluid dynamic properties, closely resembling a 
plug flow regime, which further supports effective syngas fermentation (Pagans et al., 2005). 
Devarapalli et al. (2016) demonstrated that TBRs can be utilized for acetate and alcohol production. 
Increasing the syngas supply rate from 2.3 to 4.6 standard cubic centimeters per minute (sccm) favored 
biofilm growth and promoted acetate formation over ethanol production. Additionally, TBRs have been 
widely applied in pollutant degradation, including the removal of hydrogen sulfide, volatile organic 
compounds (VOCs), dichloromethane, and ammonia (Gunes, 2021). 
Overall, the key advantages of TBRs in syngas fermentation include high mass transfer efficiency at 
the gas-liquid interface, simple operation and maintenance, and the ability to function at elevated 
pressures, making them a promising bioreactor configuration for industrial applications. 
2.6 Effect of Reactor Operating Parameters on Acetic Acid Production 
2.6.1 pH 
Maintaining pH is crucial not only for cell growth but also for directing metabolic pathways in 
homoacetogenic bacteria to optimize the production of a desired compound. Failure to regulate pH can 
result in the formation of unwanted byproducts, ultimately reducing yield (Harahap and Ahring, 2023). 
Specifically, acetate fermentation requires a neutral pH to achieve significant concentration.  
Several studies conducted with different Clostridium species (C. autoethanogenum, C. ragsdalei, C. 
ljungdahlii and C. aceticum) demonstrated that a shift from neutral to acidic pH in gas fermentation 
leads to a transition from acidogenesis to solventogenesis, which results in reduced acetic acid yield 
and increased ethanol generation (Abubackar et al., 2011; Arslan et al., 2021; Infantes et al., 2020; 
Kundiyana et al., 2011). Mixed inoculum derived from anaerobic reactors processing municipal 
wastewater contain diverse microbial communities, including methanogens (Liu et al., 
2018b).Unregulated methanogenic activity enhances syngas-to-methane and acetate-to-methane 
conversion via hydrogenotrophic and acetotrophic methanogens, respectively, thereby increasing pH 
and methane production while decreasing acetate yield (Ragsdale and Pierce, 2008).Methanogens 
proliferate optimally at neutral pH, necessitating their removal prior to fermentation through heat 
treatment, extreme pH conditions, or the addition of 2-bromoethanesulfonate (BES) (Harahap and 
Ahring, 2023). Heat-shock treatment selectively eliminates non-spore-forming bacteria, such as 
hydrogenotrophic methanogens, while allowing spore-forming acetogens like Clostridium species to 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
persist (Kannaiah Goud et al., 2014). Methanogenic growth is optimal around pH 7.0, whereas 
acetogens achieve higher acetate yields at pH 5.5–6.0 with minimal methane production (Giduthuri 
and Ahring, 2023). Although pH levels between 8 and 10 can also suppress methanogens, excessively 
alkaline conditions reduce the conversion of volatile solids to acetate and pose a risk of equipment 
corrosion (Yang et al., 2016).Short-term BES application effectively inhibits methanogens by blocking 
the methyl Coenzyme-M (Me-CoM) formation pathway through competitive inhibition, while still 
permitting acetogenic growth (Chae et al., 2010; Park et al., 2019).  
Higher acetate production with minimal ethanol and methane accumulation was observed by (Liu et 
al., 2018b) at pH 9.0 in mixed cultures dominated by Clostridium and Acetobacterium. Additionally, 
Luo et al. (2018) reported maximal acetate yields with low methane, butyrate, and alcohol 
concentrations at pH 6.5–7.5 when BES was included during fermentation. Acetogenic activity peaked 
at pH 7.0, as indicated by greater cell numbers and species diversity (Xu et al., 2015). 
2.6.2 Temperature 
Temperature significantly influences fermentation by affecting both microbial productivity and gas-
to-liquid mass transfer. Mesophilic microorganisms grow optimally between 30°C and 40°C, while 
thermophilic species thrive at 55°C to 60°C. Although higher temperatures reduce gas solubility, the 
associated decrease in viscosity and the increase of diffusivity of the gas in the liquid can enhance 
mass transfer efficiency (Dutta et al., 2024; James, 2012). Temperature regulation is essential for 
optimizing acetate production, as variations can alter product concentrations (Harahap and Ahring, 
2023). 
For example S. Shen et al. (2020) observed that reducing fermentation temperature from 37°C to 25°C 
led to lower acetate concentrations due to organic acid reassimilation by C. carboxidivorans. Similarly, 
C. ragsdalei exhibited increased acetate production when the temperature rose from 32°C to 37°C, but 
levels declined when the temperature reached 42°C (Kundiyana et al. 2011). 
Fermentation at temperatures above 50°C is advantageous as it reduces energy demands for syngas 
cooling (Henstra, Dijkema, et al. 2007). However, maintaining an appropriate balance is crucial, as 
excessive heat can negatively impact microbial activity and acetate yield (Riggs, 2006). 
2.6.3 Medium 
Essential enzymes of the Wood-Ljungdahl Pathway (WLP), including Formate Dehydrogenase (FDH), 
Carbon Monoxide Dehydrogenase (CODH), Acetyl-CoA Synthase (ACS), Hydrogenase (H2ase), 
Phosphotransacetylase (PTA), and Acetate Kinase (ACK), are vital for carbon fixation in acetogens, 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
enabling the conversion of carbon dioxide (CO₂) and carbon monoxide (CO) into acetate. Metal 
cofactors play a crucial role in enhancing the activity of these enzymes, particularly FDH, 
monofunctional CODH, bifunctional CODH/ACS, H2ase, PTA, and ACK, thereby promoting the 
formation of acetyl-CoA, a key intermediate in acetate production (Han et al., 2020). 
FDH activity depends on iron (Fe), tungsten (W), selenium (Se), and molybdenum (Mo), and 
supplementing fermentation media with these metals improves its function by facilitating the reduction 
of CO₂ to formic acid (Harahap and Ahring, 2023). Likewise, CODH and ACS, essential for acetyl-
CoA accumulation, require Fe, nickel (Ni), and sulfur (S) at their active sites. Studies by Saxena et al. 
showed that increasing Ni concentrations up to 8.4 µM enhanced CODH activity in Clostridium 
ragsdalei, leading to higher acetate production, whereas nickel deprivation hindered cell growth, 
emphasizing its importance in CO fermentation to acetate (Drennan et al., 2004). The composition of 
the fermentation medium, particularly its metal content, significantly influences CO oxidation by 
CODH and acetate production via the WLP pathway (Harahap and Ahring, 2023). 
Hydrogenase, another key enzyme, requires Ni and Fe for efficient hydrogen oxidation, which 
generates reduced equivalents. In Moorella thermoacetica and Clostridium pasteurianum, the presence 
of Ni and Fe has been shown to enhance H2ase activity and boost acetate formation (Harahap and 
Ahring, 2023). Additionally, PTA and ACK rely on Mo for optimal acetate production, and Mo 
deficiency can lead to decreased gene expression and lower acetate yields (Han et al., 2020). (Han et 
al., 2020) further demonstrated that the removal of Ni, Fe, and Mo reduced acetate concentrations 
during syngas fermentation by Clostridium carboxidivorans P7, whereas the absence of Se and Co 
enhanced acetate production. Other metals, such as Co²⁺, Cu²⁺, Mn²⁺, Mo⁶⁺, and SeO₄²⁻, had no 
significant effect on acetate yields, highlighting the strain-specific requirements of acetogens for 
precise metal micronutrient levels. 
 
Beyond metal cofactors, nutrient optimization is critical for maximizing acetate production. Yeast 
extract has been shown to enhance cell growth and acetate yields during syngas fermentation; however, 
its high cost limits its feasibility for large-scale applications (Infantes, 2020). Alternative, cost-
effective nutrient sources, such as corn steep liquor (CSL), malt extract, and vegetable extract, offer 
promising substitutes. CSL, a byproduct of corn wet milling, is rich in amino acids, minerals, and 
carbohydrates, while malt extract provides additional carbohydrates derived from malted barley. 
Vegetable extract, with its high nitrogen and mineral content, also serves as a viable replacement for 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
yeast extract (Thi et al., 2020). Other potential substitutes include hydrolyzed cotton seed flour, 
hydrolyzed soy flour, and ethanol stillage (Harahap and Ahring, 2023). 
Vitamins also play a crucial role in cell growth and product accumulation during syngas fermentation. 
For instance, calcium pantothenate (vitamin B5) acts as an acetyl-CoA precursor for acetate synthesis, 
while vitamin B12 is a key component of the cobalamin-dependent methyltransferase (MeTr) enzyme 
involved in acetate production (Harahap and Ahring, 2023; Kundiyana et al., 2011). The addition of 
vitamin B12 has been shown to enhance acetate concentrations. (Harahap and Ahring, 2023; 
Kundiyana et al., 2011) further highlighted that nutrient limitation, in combination with a two-stage 
fermenter design, improves syngas productivity and conversion efficiency by modulating the redox 
cycles of the Wood-Ljungdahl pathway. Notably, calcium pantothenate, vitamin B12, and CoCl₂ play 
a significant role in optimizing fermentation conditions for C. ragsdalei. 
2.7 Knowledge gaps and research opportunities 
The production of acetic acid from carbon monoxide through gas fermentation presents a promising 
avenue for sustainable biochemical production. However, several knowledge gaps and challenges must 
be addressed to improve process efficiency and commercial viability. 
A significant challenge in this field is the impact of syngas impurities on fermentation efficiency. While 
numerous studies have demonstrated high acetate yields using simulated syngas, real-world syngas 
contain impurities that significantly inhibit microbial growth and metabolic activity. These 
contaminants, including nitrogenous and sulfurous compounds, necessitate extensive purification, 
increasing operational costs. Prior research has highlighted the impact of such impurities on microbial 
performance, emphasizing the need for further studies on microbial resistance and adaptation strategies 
(He and Zhang, 2011; Hu et al., 2013; Regis et al., 2023). 
 
Optimizing fermentation conditions is another crucial research direction. The adjustment of key 
fermentation parameters, including syngas composition, pH, and nutrient supplementation, can 
significantly impact acetate production efficiency. Studies have demonstrated that variations in pH and 
gas ratios influence product distribution between acetate and ethanol, suggesting that precise control 
of these factors could maximize acetate yields (Lee et al., 2020b; Xu et al., 2015). 
Advancements in bioreactor technology offer promising solutions to overcome mass transfer 
limitations. Innovative reactor designs, such as monolithic biofilm reactors and bulk-gas-to-atomized-
liquid reactors, have shown potential in enhancing gas-liquid mass transfer and improving acetate 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
production efficiency. However, these technologies require further validation at pilot and industrial 
scales to determine their practical applicability and cost-effectiveness (Sathish et al., 2019; Shen et al., 
2014). 
Furthermore, Adaptive Laboratory Evolution (ALE) emerges as a promising tool for enhancing the 
resilience and metabolic performance of microbial communities under the challenging conditions 
associated with 100% CO atmospheres. Recent studies have demonstrated the successful adaptation 
of mixed microbial consortia to CO-rich gas feeds in mesophilic TBRs. For example, Tunca et al., 
(2025) employed a gradual increase in CO concentration in the inlet gas stream to selectively enrich 
CO-utilizing microbial populations. This process resulted in consortia capable of converting CO 
predominantly into acetic acid with high selectivity and carbon conversion efficiency. These evolved 
microbial communities exhibited stable, long-term performance and maintained high levels of 
productivity, highlighting the potential of ALE to engineer robust and functionally optimized microbial 
ecosystems. Incorporating such evolutionary strategies into future work could play a pivotal role in 
advancing the scalability and industrial viability of CO-based acetic acid fermentation. 
Addressing these knowledge gaps and research opportunities will be essential for advancing the 
feasibility of acetic acid production through syngas fermentation, ultimately contributing to more 
sustainable and economically viable biochemical manufacturing processes. 
In summary, acetic acid production via microbial fermentation of CO, offers an alternative solution to 
the use of fossil fuels concluding any sustainability concerns. It is primarily produced through the 
Wood-Ljungdahl pathway (WLP) by acetogenic bacteria able to convert CO, CO2 and H2 into acetate. 
This process provides higher selectivity advantages over catalytic ones, as biological catalysts can 
tolerate impurities and require milder operational conditions. Moreover, optimizing parameters such 
as temperature, gas-liquid mass transfer, pH control, and microbial enrichment while selecting the 
suitable reactor configuration can significantly enhance acetate yields. 
Although syngas fermentation, specifically the bioconversion of carbon monoxide (CO) to acetic acid, 
has been the subject of extensive research, several critical gaps remain unaddressed. As discussed in 
the literature review and future perspectives, the application of mixed microbial cultures under 
thermophilic conditions, as well as strategies to enhance acetic acid productivity and selectivity in 
continuous operations within Trickle Bed Reactors (TBRs), remain underexplored areas of 
investigation.

Literature review’s key findings highlight areas of improvement that are further analyzed in this study, 
mainly focused on investigating temperature, as a way to increase the selectivity of the fermentation 
towards acetic acid, microbial enrichment and growth medium utilizing Trickle Bed Reactors (TBR).

Enrichment experiments and conditions 
All enrichment experiments were performed in 300 ml serum flasks with an active volume of 100 ml, 
consisting of 20 ml of inoculum and 80 ml of medium. The headspace was filled with 100% CO. First, 
three different flasks were flashed with N2 to create anaerobic conditions. Medium was introduced with 
the addition of the inoculum followed by the sealing with rubber stoppers and screw plugs and CO was 
added up to a total pressure of 1.8 atm. After inoculation, flasks were incubated in the dark at a 
thermophilic temperature of 60 ºC. A batch-enrichment technique was used in all enrichment series, 
consisting in the successive transfer of a fraction of the fermented broth (used as inoculum) into a new 
flask with fresh medium (20%). The enrichments were performed in triplicates with the best 
performing one, or a combination of them, being used as inoculum for the next transfer. 
It is important to note that, among the initial set of triplicates, one serum vial exhibited methane 
production, indicating the presence of methanogenic activity. To mitigate this and minimize residual 
methane, the contents of the vial were subjected to a boiling treatment under 100% N2. Following this 
thermal treatment, the culture was transferred into three new serum vials to establish a new set of

biological triplicates. Continued consumption of CO and gas production in these vials confirmed that 
the microbial community remained metabolically active post-treatment. 
3.2 Microbial Kinetics 
Growth Medium 
The growth medium used for the microbial kinetics experiment was the same medium used in the 
enrichment experiment which is given in Table 3. 
Inoculum source and methodology  
The inoculum for the five triplicates was prepared by combining a previously CO-enriched microbial 
consortium with a gas mixture consisting of 60% carbon monoxide (CO) and 40% nitrogen (N₂) at a 
total pressure of 1.8 atm. The exact same method, same gas mixture and the same pressure were used 
for two triplicates with heat pretreated previously CO-enriched microbial consortium that will be 
labeled as “heat pre-treated”. This fresh inoculum was prepared in a 300 mL flask, with a final liquid 
working volume of 100 mL and an inoculum volume of 20% (v/v), derived from a prior activity test. 
Serum vials were filled with 80 mL of sterile medium under continuous N₂ flushing to ensure anaerobic 
conditions. Following this, the vials were sealed with rubber stoppers and screw caps. The inoculum 
was then introduced anaerobically, and the headspace was pressurized to 1.8 atm with the CO/N₂ gas 
mixture using a precision pressure indicator (Model CPH6400, WIKA, Germany). All gases used had 
a purity greater than 99.9% and were supplied by Air Liquide (Denmark). The sealed serum vials were 
incubated in the dark at 60 °C with agitation at 100 rpm. Headspace pressure was monitored daily to 
ensure the inoculum remained in the active growth phase for subsequent kinetic experiments.

Reactor configuration 
The experimental setup includes two custom-built Trickle Bed Reactors (TBRs) based on a modified 
design by Asimakopoulos et al. (2018) tailored to meet the specific requirements of this study as can 
be seen in Figure 3. 
 
 
Figure 3: Setup of Trickle Bed Reactors. Including: (1) gas cylinder, (2) mass flow controller, (3) 
trickle bed, (4) liquid reservoir, (5) pH electrode, (6) liquid recirculation tubing, (7) liquid and gas 
outlet tube, (8) stirring magnet, (9) gas sampling port, (10) gas trap, (11) effluent bottle, (12) liquid 
media, (13) N2 gas bag, (14) liquid sampling port, (15) pH controller and peristaltic pumps for 
acid/base solutions, (16) base solution, (17) acid solution, (18) water bath and water jacket 
recirculation, (19) liquid inflow peristaltic pump, and (20) liquid recirculation peristaltic pump 
(Tunca et al., 2025).

Each reactor (3) contains a sealed, airtight column designed to regulate gas and liquid flows, ensuring 
efficient phase contact—an essential factor for effective gas fermentation. Gas flow into the TBR 
columns is precisely controlled using mass flow controllers (2) (Bronkhorst, Netherlands) to maintain 
constant gas flow. Each column has an empty bed volume of 370 mL, specifically designed to support 
a consistent gas flow rate. 
The columns are packed with BioFLO 9 material, a polypropylene/polyethylene blend that maximizes 
surface area for microbial growth and enhances gas-liquid interactions. The TBR system operates with 
a reservoir (4) volume of 400 mL, primarily housed in a reservoir with minimal retention on the 
packing material. A peristaltic pump (20) continuously recirculates the liquid from the reservoir to the 
top of the column, ensuring uniform distribution. Fresh medium is introduced three times daily via a 
timed pump (19), while excess liquid is discharged through an overflow system in the reservoir (7). 
Gas exiting the TBR column flows into the headspace of an anaerobically sealed reservoir before 
passing through a sampling port, a gas trap, and finally venting into a fume hood. The gas traps (10), 
which are empty and sealed flasks, prevent air backflow—particularly when gas consumption 
increases—helping to maintain anaerobic conditions. 
To regulate pH, a control system equipped with a pH electrode (5), a transmitter, and dosing pumps 
for acid (0.5 M HCl) and base (5 M KOH) (15) automatically adjusts the pH in the reservoir, ensuring 
an optimal environment for microbial activity. Instead of using a conventional flowmeter, nitrogen 
(N₂) concentration in the outlet gas is measured to determine flow rate, providing a more accurate 
quantification method. This setup enables precise control and monitoring of gas and liquid flow, pH, 
and anaerobic conditions, all of which are crucial for efficient gas fermentation. 
Operation of TBRs 
The reactors start operating as a batch system with recirculation, for 109 days in order to achieve the 
formation of biofilm on the packing material while avoiding accumulation of acetic acid. The gas 
mixture consisted of 90% CO and 10% N2 for the first 20 days where afterwards it was changed to a 
composition of 40% CO and 60% N2. TBRs operated at a pH of 7.5, a thermophilic temperature of 60 
ºC, and a gas flow rate of 3.8 ml/min (empty bed residence time, EBRT, = 97 min) while the HRT 
(hydraulic retention time) was 7 days.

3.4.1 Gas Sampling 
Gas outflow composition was analyzed using a gas chromatograph (8610C, SRI Instruments) with a 
thermal conductivity detector and two packed columns (Molsieve 13× and silica gel). The machine 
was heated to 65 °C, flashed with helium, and samples injected after a ramp to 140 °C.  
Before initiating the gas sampling process, it was essential to establish a calibration curve to ensure 
accurate quantification of the target components. Eight vials containing known concentrations of the 
analytes of interest (N₂, H₂, CO, CH₄, and CO₂) were prepared under controlled conditions. The results 
demonstrated the precision and accuracy of the calibration method employed.

4.1 Microbial Kinetics  
Kinetics experiments indicated no microbial growth in experiments 4 and 5 suggesting that neither 
chain elongation reactions nor the reduction of volatile fatty acids to their corresponding alcohols occur 
under thermophilic conditions. Consequently, no by-products are formed, eliminating the need for 
additional purification steps and thereby significantly reducing overall process costs. 
To determine the kinetic parameters, the average optical density (OD) of each triplicate was first 
calculated over various time intervals. These values were then converted to dry weight cell 
concentration (X, expressed as g C.D.W/L) using a linear equation derived from the calibration curve 
correlating OD to the dry weight concentration of the inoculum. Subsequently, the natural logarithm 
of X was plotted against time (h) to identify the exponential and death phases, from which the 
maximum specific growth rate (μmax) and microbial death rate (Kd) were determined (Appendix, 
Figures A1-A24).

4.2.3 Microbial community analysis 
At the end of the enrichment experiments, samples from bottles were taken for microbial community 
analysis. The result of the analysis is given in Figure 19. 60% CO enrichments showed an increase in 
relative abundance of Coprothermobacter proteolyticus, pointing to possible proteolytic metabolism 
and syntrophic interactions with more diverse bacterial groups. After the transfer of 60% CO adapted 
cultures to 100% CO, the microbial community was dominated by Acetobacterium wieringae, 
Moorella thermoacetica, and Sporomusa sphaeroides. Despite being traditionally mesophilic, the 
dominance of A. wieringae in thermophilic enrichments implies either significant thermotolerance or 
the emergence of an adapted variant. M. thermoacetica is also well-known thermophilic acetogen 
which can directly use CO to convert acetate (Jia et al., 2023). S. sphaeroides is another mesophilic 
acetogen found in the enrichment; however, it is highly heat-resistant which can resist until 80 °C 
(Möller et al., 1984). After the 2nd heat pretreatment, microbial consortia showed significant abundance 
of C. proteolyticus and Tepidanaerobacter acetatoxydans. T. acetatoxydans can proceed with the 
conversion of acetate to formate/ H2 and CO2 in a syntrophic consortium with a hydrogenotrophic 
methanogen, which can only proceed if H2 is continuously removed (Müller et al., 2015). In contrast 
to its typical role as an acetate-oxidizing bacterium, T. acetatoxydans was observed to engage in acetate 
production rather than its traditional acetate oxidation function (Fontana et al., 2018). This shift in 
metabolic activity could be attributed to the favorable conditions for the adaptation to CO/syngas 
environments, which may promote acetate formation rather than oxidation.

Conclusions 
This study demonstrated the potential of thermophilic trickle bed reactors (TBRs) for selective acetic 
acid production from CO-rich gas streams using mixed microbial consortia. The performance of TBRs 
throughout the 109 days was not satisfactory since the acetic acid production and the CO consumption 
were not stable for the biggest part of the experiment.  
The microbial kinetics experiment highlighted experimental yields close to the theoretical ones. The 
first experiment presented a yield of 0.34

of 0.5

𝑚𝑚𝑜𝑙 𝐶𝑂, indicates the active contribution of both the water-gas shift (WGS) reaction and 
subsequent reductive acetogenesis happening simultaneously. CO generated H₂ and CO₂, from the 
water gas-shift reaction, that afterwards consumed in order to fuel further acetic acid synthesis. The 
2nd experiment with 100% CO showed a similar acetate formation pathway but with lower efficiency. 
Under H₂ and CO₂, metabolism was dominated by homoacetogenic pathways, resulting in acetic acid 
as the primary product for the 3rd experiment. Heat pre-treatment reduced performance in both the 
second and third experiments, likely due to its impact on acetogens. In contrast, no microbial growth 
is exhibited in experiments 4 and 5 suggesting that neither chain elongation reactions nor the reduction 
of volatile fatty acids to their corresponding alcohols occur under thermophilic conditions. 
Consequently, no by-products are formed, eliminating the need for additional purification steps and 
thereby significantly reducing overall process costs. 
Additionally, enrichments at 60 °C successfully adapted to high CO concentrations, enabling stable 
acetate production with minimal methane or alcohol by-products. Chain elongation and alcohol 
synthesis were not observed under the tested thermophilic conditions, simplifying downstream 
processing. 
Overall, research has shown that Trickle Bed Reactors, mainly on mesophilic conditions, offer an 
attractive platform for industrial bioconversion of CO-rich gases into acetic acid, even though in this 
study they performed poorly. Future work should focus on increasing product titers beyond the current 
levels, exploring advanced biofilm management strategies. With further optimization, TBRs can serve 
as scalable, robust bioreactors for environmentally sustainable production of chemicals using industrial 
waste gases.
