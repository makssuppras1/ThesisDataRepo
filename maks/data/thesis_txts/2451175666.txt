Summary (English)
This project examines social phenomena using epistemic agents in a social net-
work. Many social phenomena, such as pluralistic ignorance, have already been
studied using Dynamic Epistemic Logic. We extend this research with a frame-
work based on modal languages. Using our framework we looked into pluralistic
ignorance, dog whistling, social norms and imposter syndrome.
We present a practical implementation of our framework and use it to further
examine our chosen social phenomena.
We examined how these phenomena
form and how they break.
We showed that pluralistic ignorance can be reached only in very speciﬁc sce-
narios. We also gave a concrete example of how dog whistles can be used in
opposition of social norms and how normative agents can see through these dog
whistles.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Heated political discussions over the past ﬁve years have shone a spotlight on the
political tactic of dog whistling. In this thesis, we will look into dog whistling
and other related social phenomena. We will examine the processes that form
these social phenomena and those that break them. We will do using a formal
logic framework and an implementation of such a network.
The social phenomena we will be focusing on are: pluralistic ignorance, dog
whistling, social norms, and to a lesser extent imposter syndrome. Pluralistic
ignorance describes a situation, where people collectively express an opinion
they do not hold because of a perceived consensus. Dog whistling is employing
coded language to convey one message to the public and another to a targeted
subgroup. We deﬁne social norms in terms of a belief an individual or group
considers to be unacceptable. Imposter syndrome describes a situation where
an individual feels like an imposter in a professional ﬁeld due to a perceived
inadequacy, often despite evidence to the contrary.
Dog whistles, social norms and imposter syndrome has never, to our knowledge,
been formalized into formal logic before. However pluralistic ignorance has been
discusses at length and formalized in previous works.
In Dynamic logics of networks: Information ﬂow and the spread of opinion
[Chr16], Zoé Christoﬀformalizes and examines pluralistic ignorance. She ex-

Chapter 2
Background and Framework
In this chapter we will propose a logical framework with which we will examine
and simulate a variety of social situations. We will ﬁrst introduce the back-
ground logical theories, Modal Logic and Dynamic Logic, since they serve as
building blocks towards the more complex logics.
We will introduce a selection of modal languages including Epistemic Logic,

After having introduced these theories, we will present our logical framework.
2.1
Modal Logic
Modal Logic is an extension of Propositional Logic; it provides a way to quantify
statements. There are a number of diﬀerent modal languages, each providing
one or more diﬀerent modal operators. In this section, we will look into two
simple operators as an introduction to modal languages.
Similarly to Propositional Logic, we will use an atomic propositional variable

p ∈Φ where Φ is the set of all propositional variables. We will also use an
atomic modal operator m ∈Mod where Mod is the set of all modal operators.
The language we will examine uses the modal operator 3. We will refer to this
language as L3.
The syntax of a language describes in which way symbols can be combined into
formulas. We will use Bachus-Naur form grammars to deﬁne the syntax of the
diﬀerent languages presented in this chapter.
The grammar deﬁning L3 can be seen below[BdRV01].
ϕ ::= p | ¬ϕ | (ϕ ∧ϕ) | 3ϕ
Modal languages often deﬁne the duals of an operator. The dual of 3 is □.
This duality can be formalized as □ϕ := ¬3¬ϕ [BdRV01].
In other modal
languages we will deﬁne the dual of an operator using angle brackets such that
⟨3⟩ϕ ⇔¬3¬ϕ ⇔□ϕ.
When deﬁning the semantics of L3, or a language based on it, we use Kripke
models. A typical Kripke model, M, is a triple M = (W, R, V ) [BdRV01]. Here,
W is the set of possible worlds. R is an accessibility relation connecting these
worlds, such that R ⊆W × W.
V is a valuation function which describes
whether a proposition is true in a given world. Formally V is deﬁned as a map
Φ →P(W).
Where the syntax of a language describes which sentence constructions are sen-
sible or legal, the semantics of a language describe when these constructions are
true. We understand a proposition to be true or false given a Kripke model, M
and a speciﬁc world w ∈W, such a pair is also referred to as a pointed Kripke
model.

Dynamic Logic
Modal Logic on its own is a static language - what is true remains true. Dy-
namic Logic introduces programs, which change the truth value of propositions
[HKT00].
Modal Logic uses only modalities and propositions, Dynamic Logic extends this
with programs. The atomic program, π, along with all other programs belongs
to the set of all programs, Π. We will refer to the language of Dynamic Logic
as LDY N.
Since we now have two diﬀerent types of symbols, our syntax is split into two
parts: the syntax of propositions and the syntax of programs. The syntax of
LDY N, as deﬁned in [HKT00], can be seen below.
ϕ ::= p | ¬ϕ | (ϕ ∧ϕ) | [α]ϕ
α ::= π | α; β | α ∪β | α∗| ψ?
The diﬀerent composite programs have the following meanings [HKT00]:
[α]ϕ "It is necessary that after executing α, ϕ is true."
(2.1)
α; β "Execute α then execute β."
(2.2)
α ∪β "Choose either α or β nondeterministically and execute it"
(2.3)
α∗"Execute α a nondeterministically chosen ﬁnite number of times (zero or more)."
(2.4)
ϕ? "Test ϕ; proceed if true, fail if false."
(2.5)

tics of them when applied to propositions.

2.3
Deontic Logic
Deontic Logic is the logic of norms. Its modalities express what ought to be,
what must not be, and what is allowed to be.
The language of Deontic Logic, which we will call LDEO introduces three diﬀer-
ent modal operators. These operators: O, F, and P, are used to describe what
ought to be, what is forbidden, and what is permissible.
As an example Deontic Logic allows one to formalize sentences such as "people
ought to be nice" or "it is forbidden to steal".
The syntax of LDEO is deﬁned by the grammar seen below [Han18].
ϕ ::= p | ¬ϕ | (ϕ ∧ϕ) | Oϕ | Fϕ | Pϕ
To describe the intuitive meaning of what should be that case and what should
not, we deﬁne an ideal world. Ideal here meaning that everything is at it should
be. In this sense, if it is the case in all ideal worlds that people are nice, then it
ought to be that way.
Formally, we deﬁne this with the Ideal Worlds Intersection [Han18].
Deﬁnition 1 (Ideal Worlds Intersection) There is a subset I of the set W
of possible worlds, such that: for all p, Op holds if and only if for all w ∈I,
w ∈V (p)

2.3.1
Subjective Ideal Worlds
Later on in this chapter, we will extend our pointed Kripke model with an
agent. Under this model, we will deﬁne ideal worlds to be subjective rather
than objective. In this way, what is an ideal world depends on the agent and
the world.
To show an example of what this means, we will look at the natural language
translation presented previously. Regular Deontic Logic allowed for the expres-
sion of sentences such as "people ought to be nice" and "it is forbidden to steal".
A subjective Deontic Logic would be able to express "Adam thinks people ought
to be nice" and "Eve considers it forbidden to steal".
We will formalize this with a family of relations i where {iw}w∈W , where iw :
A →W × W. These relations are not reﬂexive, they are not symmetric and
they are not transitive.
Using this relation we will then redeﬁne the semantics of LDEO to be subjective.
M, w, a |= Oϕ iﬀM, v, a |= ϕ for every v ∈W such that (w, v) ∈iw(a)
M, w, a |= Fϕ iﬀM, v, a |= ¬ϕ for every v ∈W such that (w, v) ∈iw(a)
M, w, a |= Pϕ iﬀM, v, a |= ϕ for some v ∈W such that (w, v) ∈iw(a)

2.4
Epistemic & Doxastic Logic
In this section, we will look into two modal logics known as Epistemic - and
Doxastic Logic.
In these languages we will extend the symbols with a set of agents known as A,
as well as two new operators. Both these new operators describe the properties
of the agents. Epistemic Logic uses K to describe knowledge and Doxastic Logic
uses B to describe beliefs.
The Kripke Model used here is slightly diﬀerent to the one from basic Modal
Logic. Where previously we had simply an accessibility relation for which other
worlds were considered possible, we here have two diﬀerent relations. We have
the epistemic accessibility relation RK

possible based on that agents knowledge. We also have the doxastic accessibility
relation RB

Epistemic Logic includes three further operators to deﬁne knowledge in groups.

everybody in the group knows, and everybody knows that everybody knows
etc. Distributed knowledge is something the group can ﬁgure out together by
pooling their knowledge.

2.5
Epistemic Friendship Logic
In this section, we will present Epistemic Friendship Logic based on [SLG13].
Epistemic friendship logic is a language intended for reasoning about agents in
a dynamic network connected by symmetric friendship relations.
We will refer to the language of Epistemic Friendship Logic as LEF L. It consists
of atoms of two types, propositional variables p ∈Φ and agent nominals n ∈
ANom.
Agent nominals function as indexical propositions used for identiﬁcation. As

The syntax of LEF L is deﬁned by the following grammar

a speciﬁc world. A relation fw in f is deﬁned as {fw}w∈W , where fw : A →
{a} × A, and a ∈A.
The accessibility relation diﬀers from the one typically used in Modal Logic.
Typically, R is used and deﬁned as R ⊆W × W. LEF L uses a family, k, of
equivalence relations, where each relation kw is deﬁned as {kw}w∈W , where kw :
A →W × W.
As such, the Kripke model for LEF L becomes M = ⟨W, A, k, f, V ⟩. The valua-
tion function, V , is altered to map propositional values, Φ, to subsets of W ×A.
In this fashion, all propositions are related to an agent and as such (w, a) ∈V (p)
denotes that the proposition p holds for agent a in state w.

2.7
Groups
The notion of a group is an intuitive concept when it comes to modelling a
social network.
It is already used in Epistemic - and Doxastic Logic when
deﬁning common knowledge and distributed knowledge. In this section, we will
look into a way of formalizing groups using a relation between agents.
2.7.1
Formalizing groups
To model groups as a relation between agents, we will start with a set of all
groups, G. Using this set, we will introduce a family of transitive and sym-
metric relations called s. s contains an element for each group, sg describing
==PAGE:26==
14

which agents share this group. We formally deﬁne sg as sg ⊆A×A. Intuitively,
(a, b) ∈sg can then be read as "Agent a and Agent b share group g". This
relation is symmetric and transitive, however it is not reﬂexive. If this relation
was reﬂexive, it would imply that all agents share each group with themselves.
However, it can be said to be quasi-reﬂexive. Quasi-reﬂexitivity can be formal-
ized as ⟨a, b⟩∈sg implies ⟨a, a⟩∈sg. We will consider G to always contain the
universal group of all agents, named ˆg.
To work with this relation, we introduce an operator Gg which can be read as
"everybody in the group g". We deﬁne the semantics of this new operator as
follows
M, w, a |= Ggϕ iﬀM, w, b |= ϕ for every b ∈A such that ⟨a, b⟩∈sg
Similarly, we deﬁne the dual of this operator ⟨Gg⟩ϕ := ¬Gg¬ϕ. Using this we
can deﬁne n being a part of the group g as ⟨Gg⟩n. We will abbreviate this to g.
An intuitive axiom of this group formalization can be deﬁned as follows.
Ggϕ →Gg′ϕ | g′ ⊆g
It states that if a proposition ϕ holds for a group g then it also holds for an
subgroups of g.
Intuitively, this holds since all members of subgroup g′ are
members of the group g.

2.9
Belief revision
One of the main dynamic properties of the framework is the beliefs of the agents.
Agents revise their beliefs based on the perceived beliefs of the other agents.
We say that agents that have some relations to each other inﬂuence each others’
beliefs either strongly or weakly. The relation between the two agents could be
one of many social relations, they could be colleagues, family, or friends. We
will in this paper focus on friendships.
2.9.1
Opaque agents
From Doxastic Logic we get the means to express the beliefs of an agent, we
will here be extending that notion. Opaque agents are agents, which have more
than one layer of beliefs, here limited to two layers. These two layers will be
a public layer and a private layer. In terms of beliefs this becomes: expressed
beliefs and inner beliefs. Opaque agents introduce a possibility for modelling a
large selection of diﬀerent social situations since it lends itself to things such as
peer pressure, societal norms, dishonesty, and taboo opinions. We refer to the
inner and expressed beliefs of an agent using their respective operators IB and
EB

3.1.3
Dynamics
Dynamic Logic and Dynamic Epistemic Friendship Logic are not directly trans-
lated into Prolog. Updating the model and changing the relations is still possible
however.
As all relations and operators eventually depend on the facts, changing the facts
allows for changing both the truth value of propositions and relations in a world.
As an example, in section 2.8 we deﬁne how to remove a friendship using General
Dynamic Dynamic Logic. Removing this friendship in LDEF L can be done using
the action [−Fn,m], which is deﬁned as:
[−Fn,m] = [F := cutF (n, m)][F := cutF (m, n)]
In Prolog this can be done in a simpler way. Prolog works under the closed world
assumption, this means that what cannot be proven is false. Knowing that,
we can remove a friendship by removing the facts thus making it unprovable.
Similarly to how we cut friendships in Dynamic Epistemic Friendship Logic, we
have to cut both from a to b and from b to a due to symmetry.

Chapter 4
Social phenomena
In this chapter, we will analyze four diﬀerent social phenomena using the frame-
work presented in Chapter 2. We will also show an example and test of each
phenomena using our implementation presented in chapter 3. We will examine
pluralistic ignorance, dog whistling, social norms, and imposter syndrome.
Pluralistic ignorance, dog whistling and social norms all describe ways in which
social agents can act in diﬀerent settings. Speciﬁcally, they deal with agents’ be-
liefs, how they aﬀect each other and what their consequences are. These agents
are all opaque, and thus have inner beliefs and expressed beliefs. Imposter syn-
drome, while not necessarily related to social networks, exists as an interesting
consequence of having beliefs that are not directly tied to knowledge or facts.
4.1
Pluralistic Ignorance
4.1.1
Introduction
Pluralistic ignorance describes a situation where everybody in a group believes
their beliefs diﬀer from those of other members of the group despite all of them
acting identically. The term originates from social and behavioral science in the
==PAGE:52==
40
Social phenomena
work of Allport and Katz [OGO86]. A common basic example of this phenomena
is in the setting of a college course. The professor has just ﬁnished the lecture
and asks the students whether anyone has any questions. No one raises their
hand, but not because no one has any questions. In the mind of an individual
student he weighs the fact that he does have questions against the fact that,
seemingly, no one else does. Pluralistic ignorance describes the case where all the
students are in above-described state. Many examples of this have been studied
including "hooking up"[MST15], student radicalism [Kor72], and student alcohol
use [SP98].
4.1.2
Formalization
Using her setting of a social network of opaque agents, [Chr16] deﬁnes pluralistic
ignorance as a state in which all agents express a belief contrary to their inner
belief. We will reduce this to a local phenomenon such that a group can be in a
state of pluralistic ignorance without all agents necessarily being involved. The
formalization presented in [Chr16] is:
PIϕ := A(IBϕ ∧EB¬ϕ)
We will alter this deﬁnition slightly into:
PIgϕ := Gg(IBϕ ∧EB¬ϕ)
We can still describe pluralistic ignorance as a global phenomena using the
universal group ˆg.
Modelling pluralistic ignorance in a group of agents allows for describing an
outwardly dishonest group.
4.1.3
Breaking pluralistic ignorance
Pluralistic ignorance is a case of all agents in the group acting insincerely, the
opposite of this is all agents acting sincerely. As such, we will say that pluralistic
ignorance is broken, if the agents begin acting sincerely. This sincerity can be
formalized as:
Gg(EBϕ ∧IBϕ)
Due to the nature of belief change within the network, pluralistic ignorance can
potentially be broken by a single agent expressing his true belief. This situation

UPIgϕ := @i(IBϕ ∧EBϕ) ∧Gg(¬i →(IBϕ ∧EB¬ϕ))

Having limited pluralistic ignorance from all agents to agents in a group allows

friendship relations can add relevancy to an agent that previously had none.
4.1.4

Pluralistic ignorance can be reached only from very speciﬁc situations. Speciﬁ-

expressing a belief contrary to their inner belief have at least one friend in

pluralistic ignorance are possible, we will call such networks NPIs.

Lemma 4.2 In any NPI where pluralistic ignorance does not hold, it must be

4.2
Dog Whistle
4.2.1
Introduction
A dog whistle is a publicly expressed, seemingly innocent statement that ex-
presses another hidden message to a speciﬁc subgroup. In his 2016 article for
Vox, Ian Olasov suggests partitioning dog whistles into three types, semantic,
contextual, and stereotype-dependent [Ola16].
A semantic dog whistle is most like coded language; a group of people agree
that saying a certain phrase has an additional meaning to them and then use
it publicly.
A rather modern example of this could be the use of seemingly
innocent emojis by the far right. The far right has taken to using the emoji
depicting milk, as a dog whistle for whiteness and/or white supremacy. Notice
that while there exists some vague connection between this emoji and its added
meaning, it is not a connection one would draw intuitively.
A contextual dog whistle conveys membership of a group by using phrases nor-
mally used in the context of that group. Bethany Albertson studied religious dog
whistles and determined that George Bush’s use of "wonder-working power" was
used as a dog whistle [Alb15]. The phrase had no signiﬁcant meaning to most
people but evangelical voters recognized it as coming from the hymn "There is
Power in the Blood.". In this way, George Bush managed to convey membership
of a group without those outside the group noticing it.
Stereotype-dependent dog whistles, as their name suggests, rely on stereotypes
amongst a group to convey a speciﬁc meaning to that group. In [Ola16], an ex-
ample is given of the expression "welfare queen". To the outgroup, this phrase
is economical, it conveys an opinion on welfare programs and their recipients.
To a racist ingroup however, a welfare recipient is stereotypically black, so talk-
ing negatively of "welfare queens" translates to talking negatively about black
people.
4.2.2
History
Historically, dog whistles have been used to spread racism and other extreme
political beliefs by appealing to those voters without turning away the general
electorate. Harvey LeRoy "Lee" Atwater was a strategist for the Republican
party under the Reagan administration. He gave an interview where he captured
the political and racial parts of dog whistling before the term was coined. In
==PAGE:57==
4.2 Dog Whistle
45
that interview, he said the following: "You start out in 1954 by saying, ’[N-
word], [N-word], [N-word].’ By 1968 you can’t say ’[N-word]’ – that hurts you.
Backﬁres. So you say stuﬀlike forced busing, states’ rights and all that stuﬀ.
You’re getting so abstract now [that] you’re talking about cutting taxes, and all
these things you’re talking about are totally economic things and a byproduct
of them is [that] blacks get hurt worse than whites."[HER05].
4.2.3
Formalization
We can now formalize the phenomenon of a dog whistle using pluralistic igno-
rance along with the notion of groups. The formalization of a dog whistle can
be expressed as below
DWϕ,ψ,g := Cg(PIgϕ ∧(EBψ →IBϕ))
Note that pluralistic ignorance here is a rather special case in that it is common
knowledge amongst the group that they are in this state. In most other examples
of pluralistic ignorance, it is unknown to the agents that they are in this state.
4.2.4
Purpose
A dog whistles’ natural environment is in politics, where it is used to appeal to
some voters without scaring away others. However, the general idea of a dog
whistle can also be applied to other environments or in other settings. One
of the other situations in which dog whistles can be applied is social games,
especially team games.
Kemps is a team based matching card game. Pairs of players compete to collect
four cards of the same rank and then signal that fact to the partner.
The
signaling is done with a non verbal secret previously agreed upon sign. Once
the partner has seen the signal he says "Kemps", if the partner who signaled
can then show four matching cards, they get a point. The game is played in
multiple rounds and opposing pairs can steal a point by calling "Kemps" ﬁrst.
A useful signal in this game is similar to a good semantic dog whistle - it must
appear innocuous to the other parties. Picking a signal such as clapping will be
recognized immediately and could likely get the point stolen. Picking a signal
which could potentially be a coincidence or natural such as a wink, scratching
ones head, or adjusting ones glasses will likely not be recognized as a signal.

Social Norms
4.3.1
Introduction
We will deﬁne a social norm to be a rule of a group, more speciﬁcally it is rules
regarding the opinions of a group. A social norm describes what beliefs a group
==PAGE:59==
4.3 Social Norms
47
considers to be unacceptable or taboo. An example of this might be that a
group might all agree that they consider a racist belief to be unacceptable.
4.3.2
Formalization
To formalize social norms we will combine operators from Deontic Logic with
those from Epistemic Friendship Logic. We will introduce what it means for
an agent to follow a social norm regarding a belief ϕ. An agent following a
social norm regarding belief ϕ means the agent considers the belief ϕ to be
unacceptable.
An agent who considers a belief to be unacceptable will intuitively not innerly
hold this belief nor will he express it. In our formalization, such an agent will also
disassociate from other agents who express or innerly hold this belief. However,
since inner beliefs are not public information, we will limit this to agents he
knows innerly believe in something he deems unacceptable.
The disassociation from agents based on their inner beliefs becomes relevant in
combination with the weak inﬂuence observation discussed in 3.2.4.
Formally we deﬁne adherence to a norm with the operator Nϕ as follows.
Nϕ := V (EBϕ ∧IBϕ ∧⟨F⟩(EBϕ∧↓n @aK(@nIBϕ)))

handles other agents who believe that ϕ. In one world, the friendship with these

4.4
Imposter Syndrome
4.4.1
Introduction
Imposter syndrome is the phenomena where people feel like they are invol-
untarily misleading their colleagues or friends as to their abilities and talent.
Imposter syndrome is a misnomer, it is not a disease or disorder, but rather a
widely spread social phenomenon. A study in 2018 subjected medical students
at Nishtar Medical College to a test based on the Clance Imposter Phenomenon
Scale 1. The results showed that 54.49% of the students showed severe signs of
imposter syndrome while a further 4.76% showed very severed signs.
The prevalence of imposter syndrome can be largely chalked up to how little it
is talked about. In many cases, groups experiencing imposter syndrome can be
said to be in a state of pluralistic ignorance about it - that is to say, they all
experience it, but no one acknowledges it.
4.4.2
Formalization
One of the interesting parts of imposter syndrome is that evidence showing
that a person is talented or skilled does not prevent imposter syndrome. Maya
Angelou is quoted as saying “Each time I write a book, every time I face that
yellow pad, the challenge is so great. I have written eleven books, but each time
I think, ‘Uh oh, they’re going to ﬁnd out now. I’ve run a game on everybody and
they’re going to ﬁnd me out.”. With this in mind, we will formalize imposter
syndrome.
ISϕ,ψ := K(ϕ ∧ϕ →ψ) ∧IB(ϕ ∧¬ψ)
To show how this expresses the illogical nature of the imposter syndrome, let
ϕ be the proposition "I have written eleven books" and ψ be "I am a talented
author". The above formalization then expresses "I know that having written
eleven books implies that I am a talented author, yet I innerly believe that I
have written eleven books and am not a talented author".
In a sense, this can be considered a situation where modus ponens does not
follow. This is because inner beliefs are not tied to knowledge or facts in any
way. Naturally, we would consider that if a person knows something, they would
believe it, but here that is not the case.

Discussion
In this thesis, we presented our work on developing a framework to examine so-
cial phenomena, how we implemented this framework with Prolog and Java and
ﬁnally we examined some of the social phenomena this framework can express.
We will here go into some of the key choices made along the way, and discuss
our ﬁndings.
5.1
Key choices
5.1.1
Simulation or Tests
In section 3.2, we showed that the Java code can be used both through tests
and by simulation with a repast model. As mentioned, the code base supports
both, although to a diﬀerent extend as we chose to focus primarily on tests.
We did this because we wanted to examine very speciﬁc situations where our
selected phenomena could occur. Notably, we showed that pluralistic ignorance
naturally occurs very rarely, showing this phenomena without the controlled
environment of a test would be unfeasible.

Extending the modelling part would allow for examining entirely diﬀerent ques-
tions to the ones we examined. As an example, by extending the simulation
framework with the ability to chain simulations, one could examine how the
initial distribution of expressed beliefs aﬀects the distribution at later points.
5.1.2
Closed world assumption
As noted previously, Prolog functions under the closed world assumption. This
leads to several important consequences, most notable is its impact on the rela-
tionship between knowledge, facts and beliefs.
It might appear natural that if something is known, or a fact, one would believe
it. However, introducing this as a rule in Prolog leads to unforeseen consequences
due to the closed world assumption. A rule specifying this connection could be
written as in listing 12.
1
evaluate(A, innerlyBelieves(P), W) :-
2
evaluate(A, knows(P), W);
3
evaluate(A, P, W).
Listing 12: Inner belief from knowledge or fact
However under this rule, queries of the form evaluate(A, innerlyBelieves(not(P)), W).
would succeed for any query where one of the inputs cannot be uniﬁed. As an
example, if we have a single agent, Jim, who innerly believes that ϕ in the world
rw. We can then successfully claim that he innerly believes ¬ψ where ψ is any
other proposition. This can be seen by the following execution
evaluate(A, innerlyBelieves(not(P)), W).
⇒
evaluate(A, not(P), W).
⇒
\ + evaluate(A, P, W).
In the above example \ + evaluate(A, P, W). will succeed where A is Jim, W is
rw and P is anything.

Dynamics
We chose to handle the dynamics of our framework in the Java networks rather
than in the Prolog implementation. Another approach would be to either imple-
ment Dynamic Logic and programs in our Prolog program or use a framework.
There are several diﬀerent frameworks for Modal Logic programming, these are
examined and compared to their contribution of a dynamic extension of Prolog
in [OAL12].
This approach was deemed to not be necessary since we have the Java program
to generate our models. In Dynamic Logic the basic idea is to use a program to
change the model. We believe that our method with the Java program altering
the model in the Prolog implementation comes quite close to this.
5.2
Key ﬁndings
5.2.1
Pluralistic ignorance
We showed in section 4.1 that pluralistic ignorance is very rarely reached through
belief revision. This then leaves the question of how does it occur, and how could
that be expressed. In the student example, where all students have questions
at the end of a lecture but no one raises their hand, one of our main assump-
tions does not hold. We assume that expressed beliefs are public information,
however in the student example the other students cannot diﬀerentiate between
a student expressing that he is undecided and a student expressing that he has
no questions. It might be a very natural position of a shy or unsure student to
be undecided about whether or not he wants to ask a question.
Another way to examine expresses beliefs could be through public announce-
ment. Using public announcement, the belief revision process could be sequen-
tial as opposed to parallel.
In our system, all agents revise their beliefs in
parallel, that is to say no agent revises before the others.
Using public an-
nouncement, it could go from agent to agent where each agent in turn revises
his belief and then publicly announces his resulting expressed belief.
The knowledge of expressed beliefs could also be tied to friendship. In this way,
we could say that the expressed beliefs of a are not known except by his friends.
This would result in very few agents being able to tell when other agents are
under weak inﬂuence, since they would need to share all their friends.
Not

about other agents inner beliefs.
5.2.2
Dog Whistles & Social norms
When examining the eﬀect that social norms have on dog whistling, we showed
an example of how an agent can come to realise an other agents innerly held
beliefs. Discerning this inner belief allowed the normative agents to disassociate
from the dog whistling agent, however the normative agent did not actually
discern the dog whistle.
A way to have agents actually discern dog whistles would be to introduce the
notion of related beliefs. Related beliefs can be examined in a qualitative and
a quantitative sense. In a qualitative sense to describe ϕ being related to ψ, it
must always be that case that ϕ ↔ψ. In a quantitative sense, we could say
there must be more agents for which the beliefs are related in a qualitative way
than there are agents for which they are not.
An as example consider the network of the discernBelief test which can be
seen in ﬁgure 5.1. In a qualitative sense, Pam cannot describe ¬ϕ and ψ as
being related since Michael disproves this case. However, under a quantitative
view, she would observe that it holds for Jim, Dwight, and Ryan but does not
hold for Michael. She could then express that it generally holds.
When examining social norms, we assumed as a default that a belief is per-
missible. This can be considered a facet of liberalism. John Stuart Mill, an
inﬂuential thinker on liberalism, argued that “the burden of proof is supposed
to be with those who are against liberty; who contend for any restriction or
prohibition. . . . The a priori assumption is in favour of freedom. . . ” [GCS18].
If one took a more authoritative view on social norms as it relates to publicly
expressed beliefs, permissible would likely not be the default, in fact the default
might be forbidden. We could then say that a belief is a priori not allowed
unless otherwise stated. In this way, we would formalise social norms to express
that a belief is considered permissible, rather than forbidden.
In the formalization of a social norm, we described that agents would not be
friends with people holding the unacceptable opinion.
However, this is not
necessarily the case in a real environment. An argument could be made for
disassociation leading to echo chambers - groups where no one disagrees with
one another because anyone who disagrees is shunned immediately.
We also chose from this formalization that the agents would choose to cut the

Imposter syndrome
Using our framework, imposter syndrome, as presented in section 4.4, is entirely
static. A couple of ways to break imposter syndrome are discussed in [Tho16],
one of these is having colleagues discuss honestly each others performance.
This seems like natural solution to imposter syndrome. A person who truly
believes that a coworker would tell them if they were unqualiﬁed would be less
likely to feel unqualiﬁed without evidence.
Such a solution could be implemented into our framework by the inclusion of
public announcement [van08b]. Public announcement would allow us to emulate
agents giving each other feedback. Based on the feedback of other agents, we
could create rules that allow agents to break or avoid imposter syndrome.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
6.1
Summary
By extending Dynamic Epistemic Friendship Logic into our framework, we man-
aged to create a framework with which we were able to express and examine
the social phenomena of pluralistic ignorance, dog whistling, social norms and
imposter syndrome. We showed how changes on a local scale, such as belief
revision or an agent gaining new knowledge, aﬀects the network as a whole by
changing the friendships. When examining and discussing these phenomena, we
showed how they occur and how they break.
We showed that an extensive implementation of a Modal Logic framework is
feasible and under the right circumstances, the translation from formal logic to
Prolog predicate is immediate and intuitive.
The Modal Logic framework proved to be of great use for an object oriented
social network implementation. Being connected to a logical framework allowed
this implementation to determine the validity of complex questions by simply
providing a model.
==PAGE:72==
60
Conclusion
6.2
Future work
As discussed previously, introducing public announcement to the framework
could serve several purposes such as making it possible to express how imposter
syndrome breaks through talking with peers. In this way, research could be done
into what types of networks allow for breaking imposter syndrome. Determining
what types of networks aﬀect imposter syndrome would be an interesting topic
in relation to real world imposter syndrome. An interesting way to look into
this would be to formalize the honest feedback suggested in [Tho16].
To implement public announcement, it would be added to the framework as a
program. It would then be worked into our Java social network implementation
along with the other dynamic parts. The honesty of an agent could be initially
assumed and then veriﬁed by their interactions along the way.
In section 2.9, we noted that while [Chr16] deﬁnes three diﬀerent types of agents,
we used only the ﬁrst. An intuitive extension is thus to examine the phenomena
examined here with a mixture of agents. As an example, it is likely that there
are more networks that can reach pluralistic ignorance than the ones we showed
here when there are multiple types of agents.
Multiple types of agents would also be an interesting factor when agents reason
about others inner beliefs through their observations of weak inﬂuence.
An
agent would have to ﬁrst recognize which types of agents the others are before
it could observe weak inﬂuence.
When examining the chosen phenomena, we used a formal logic based approach,
but these phenomena can also be examined through other means.
Network
science and graph theory could provide deeper insights to the occurrence and
dispersion of pluralistic ignorance in networks. Problems related to cliques and
closure could likely be shed some light on the complexity of these social network
problems, especially pluralistic ignorance.
6.3
Contributions
We contributed with a formal deﬁnition of how to represent sharing membership
of a group between two agents. With this relation, it is possible to model changes
to group structure using General Dynamic Dynamic Logic. This formalization
also provides a more formal deﬁnition of the groups used in common knowledge,
and distributed knowledge.
==PAGE:73==
6.3 Contributions
61
We also included a formalization of a subjective Deontic Logic, where ideal
worlds are dependant on an agent. This type of Deontic Logic better describes
many natural normative statements by putting them in the context of the agent.
When formalizing our concepts, we contributed with a formalization of pluralis-
tic ignorance in the scope of a group, along with formalizations of dog whistles,
social norms and imposter syndrome.
