Abstract
A high-order Boussinesq model has been implemented in one horizontal dimension in MATLAB.
The model has been extended to cover the surf zone, by including a breaking model. The break-
ing formulation includes an energy dissipation mechanism based on the eddy viscosity concept
as well as a breaking criteria. Three diﬀerent closure models, with varying complexity, have been
included in the model. The breaking criteria formulation are based on time derivatives of the sur-
face elevation. The Boussinesq model has been thoroughly veriﬁed against theoretical benchmark
solutions throughout the development. This included the veriﬁcation of linear dispersion, linear
shoaling, non-linear propagation, implementation check of imposed boundary conditions, imple-
mentation check of wave- generation and absorption zones and a moving coastline algorithm.
All three included model closures proved to initiate breaking accurately while also computing
accurate wave height decays inside the surf zone. The model contains a general ﬂaw, as it for
all three closures, computes mean water levels contradicting well established physical behaviour.
This remains unsolved.
Keywords
Numerical modelling, Boussinesq modelling, Wave breaking, Turbulence modelling

1
Introduction
A computational wave model, capable of accurately simulating wave-motion and -transformation
from deep water through the surf-zone is greatly desired by engineers. In the simplest 2D cases,
where a regular wave train shoals up to a very shallow shelf, such a model must be able to simulate
non-linear shoaling and breaking. Any exact solution to this would require a full solution to the
Navier-stokes equation which is still limited to very small domains. Thus approximate models
must be considered.
For the progression of waves until breaking, the Boussinesq type models are a obvious and
often used approach, as they oﬀer computationally cheap solutions. Boussinesq type equations
have been subject to much research, and they can today simulate most phenomena exhibited by
non-breaking waves with high accuracy in ﬁnite depth. The equations are derived under shallow
water considerations why they are depth limited in their abilities to represent dispersion- and
non-linear properties. Per design, the formulation does not include breaking induced energy
dissipation, making it a good model for shoaling waves from deep waters to the shallow coastal
region, but invalid inside the surf-zone.
The sought advance within coastal hydrodynamics, for this thesis, is to extend the high-
order Boussinesq model by Madsen et al. (2006) to cover the surf-zone by making it capable of
automatically break waves in shallow waters. Such an model extension will require two features:
A wave breaking criterion, and energy dissipation mechanism.
The energy dissipation due to wave breaking can be represented by e.g. an eddy viscosity
term in the dynamic surface condition of the Boussinesq formulation. The eddy viscosity concept
stems from turbulence modelling, where it also occurs in the ﬂow equations as a part of the
turbulent closure required. It is thus natural to seek a closure to the current issue within the
framework of turbulence modelling.
Here, a wide variety of methods of describing the eddy
viscosity exists. Most commonly, it is expressed in terms of the turbulent kinetic energy and an
additional turbulence property, requiring the addition of zero, one or two more partial diﬀerential
equations to the model. All three approached have been adopted in this work.
It has thus been sought to implement a Boussinesq model in one horizontal dimension, coupled
with a eddy viscosity breaking closure, capable of simulating accurate wave heights and water
levels.
For a thorough historical background in the advances within performance and capabilities of
Boussinesq models, it is referred to Fuhrman (2004) and Kirby (2016).

1.1
Thesis Structure
The structure of the thesis is outlined in the following. Section 2 presents the Boussinesq model of
Madsen et al. (2006). In section 3 the idea of wave-breaking within the framework of Boussinesq
modelling is introduced, where three diﬀerent closure models is presented.
In section 4, the
numerical methods applied in order to implement the models previously introduced are presented.

2.
The Boussinesq Model
2
The Boussinesq Model
The full description of non-linear water waves is complicated. It involves the Laplace equation
in the interior ﬂuid domain as well as non-linear boundary conditions at the seabed and on
the free surface, where the latter is part of the solution. For practical engineering purposes,
direct numerical methods for solving the full equations, are not always desirable as they are
computationally very time consuming. This has driven a continuous research eﬀort to improve
the accuracy of the so called Boussinesq-type equations, as they oﬀer a signiﬁcantly faster solu-
tion to the water wave problem, by making acceptable physical compromises. The idea of the
Boussinesq formulation is to reduce the problem to two dimensions, by eliminating the vertical
coordinate, while still retaining the eﬀects of non-hydrostatic pressure in contrast to the non-
linear shallow water equations. The required approximations regarding the expression of the
vertical and horizontal velocities leads to a depth limitation in the accuracy of the non-linear-
and the embedded dispersive properties. These shallow water limits have however been relaxed
signiﬁcantly over the years, why formulations able to accurately propagate and shoal non-linear
waves from practical deep water to very shallow water are available today. In the following, the
state of the art Boussinesq formulation by Madsen et al. (2006) is presented. The formulation is
presented in terms of one horizontal dimension and for slowly varying bathymetries.

3.
Breaking Model
3
Breaking Model
3.1
Introduction
The underlying irrotational ﬂow assumptions, which the Boussinesq model are derived under,
makes the model incapable of accounting for the transformation process taking place as the
wave breaks. A Boussinesq model is per deﬁnition incapable of describing the wave-overturning
occurring at breaking. Thus an additional physical ’breaking closure’ must be introduced, in
order for the model to be able to simulate eﬀects as waveheight decay and possibly breaking
induced set-up in the surf-zone.
Several attempts have been made to include diﬀerent types of breaking closures for Boussinesq-
type equations. The ﬁrst is an eddy viscosity formulation, which was originally developed by
Zelt (1991), and later extended by Nwogu (1996) and by Kennedy et al. (2000). The second
technique is the surface roller approach developed by Schäﬀer et al. (1993). A third concept is
based on turning of the dispersive terms in the vicinity of the breaking region while allowing
the dissipative nature of a shallow water bore to remove energy from a wave while conserving
momentum Zhang et al. (2014). In the current work, the eddy viscosity concept is investigated
and coupled with the aforementioned Boussinesq equations. No further attention has been paid
to the latter two techniques.
The concept of introducing an eddy viscosity term traditionally consists of two main features.
The ﬁrst feature is a trigger mechanism making it possible to localize the initiation and the
termination of a breaking event in space and time. The second feature is a mechanism introducing
the capability to dissipate wave energy during the breaking event.
Most commonly, the energy dissipation is governed by an eddy viscosity model, often utiliz-
ing the Boussinesq approximation to relate the ’breaking induced shear stress’ τij to the eddy
viscosity νT and velocity gradients. Eddy viscosity models exist in many diﬀerent complexities.
They are commonly classiﬁed in terms of the number of transport equations solved in addition to
the ﬂow equations i.e. the Boussinesq equations in the present work. A simple zero-equation, or
algebraic model, based on a mixing length coeﬃcient was successfully implemented by Kennedy
et al. (2000). This is a completely empirical closure based on dimensional arguments, requiring
iterative ﬁne-tuning of the mixing length coeﬃcient. More often a one-equation model is utilized
see e.g. Nwogu (1996) where a semi-empirical transport equation for the turbulent kinetic en-
ergy k is used. Such models are still incomplete, as they still require a turbulence length scale,
commonly related to a typical ﬂow dimension (i.e. ﬂow depth). It is desirable to avoid assuming
a length scale, which is possible with a two-equation model that accounts for both scales required
to build the eddy viscosity independently. This will automatically provide the turbulence length
scale or it’s equivalent and thereby makes a complete and more physically robust model. All two-
Page 6 of 60
==PAGE:20==
equation models use a transport equation for k, whereas the optimal second scale still appears
debatable with the speciﬁc dissipation rate ω often being the preferred choice. Whatever the
choice, the second transport equation is mainly empirical. No previous attempts of incorporating
such a model with Boussinesq type equations exist in the literature. It should be emphasized
that adding complexity to an empirical model does not guarantee higher accuracy. As both
zero- and one-equation closures have been investigated for diﬀerent Boussinesq-type equations,
the main focus of the present work, has been to investigate the behaviour of a two-equation
closure. However, in order to have a structured step-wise model-development, examples of all
three closures have been implemented and analyzed.
In the following, the energy dissipation term included in the dynamic free surface condition
is derived. The section proceeds to introduce the three diﬀerent closure models included, while
ending with a description of the breaker trigger Incorporated.

3.5
Breaker Trigger Mechanism
Generally, the point of breaking can be described by either phase averaged- or phase resolved
quantities. A phase averaged criteria includes wave characteristics that are representative for one
full phase of the wave e.g length, height and period. From Stokes theory, it can be shown that a
regular progressive wave will remain stable until the point where the horizontal particle velocity
at the wave crest exceeds the celerity or phase velocity of the wave. The wave will at this point
start to overturn and break. In deep water, the theoretical steepness limit is (H/L)max = 0.142.
Page 12 of 60
==PAGE:26==
This kind of phase averaged criteria thus describes the geometrical limits of a wave at breaking.
Another criteria can be formulated in terms of the water depth as Hb/hb = γ where γ = 0.78.
The ﬁrst criteria is typically relevant for deep water conditions while the second is for shallow
water, or coastal, conditions.
A phase resolving model utilizes information at certain locations of the wave. Initiation and
termination of breaking could thus be described by e.g. the vertical speed of the free surface
elevation and/or a critical local surface angle or the gradient or curvature of the surface velocities.
It is diﬃcult to implement a breaking criteria based on a phased averaged criteria, as the diﬀerent
waves present in the model at a given time need to be separated, and wave characteristics such
as length and height need to be determined. A phase resolving criteria are thus preferable as e.g.
the vertical change of the varying surface elevation, ηt, is already available from the Boussinesq
computations.
The adopted breaking criteria is that of Kennedy et al. (2000). The authors introduce the
scalar B, which has the function of a ’switch’ on various terms in the time stepping equations

Breaking Dissipation Closure
The code includes the option to choose between the three diﬀerent breaking induced dissipation
closures previously presented. The ﬁrst option, the zero equation model eq, (3.15), is easily

4.7
Analytic Functions For Wave-Generation
The model is implemented with the option to choose from either Stokes 1st, 2nd, 3rd order theory
or a stream function solutions to calculate the analytic solution inside the wave-generation zone.
The stream function implementation follows the theory described in Fenton (1988) and computes
the stream function theory solution for a wave of length L, height H, and either mean Stokes
drift or Eulerian velocity, for a water depth h using n modes and nsteps in non-linearity. For all
the simulations conducted during this work, zero Stokes drift (vertical mean transport velocity)
cs = 0 has been utilized to represent the ﬂow conditions in a wave-ﬂume, unless otherwise noted.
4.8
Numerical Dissipation - Savitzky-Golay Smoothing Filter
Numerical instabilities are very probable to occur for problems with varying bathymetries and
high non-linearity. Such instabilities will show as small disturbances with very high wave num-
bers, arising from the discretization.
These will cause the solution to become instable and
inevitable explode given suﬃcient time. Fuhrman (2004) showed that constant depth- and regu-
lar domains are linearly stable, why such problems are not of concern. It is however a necessity
to include a method of numerical dissipation to maintain numerical stability for aforementioned
problems. This is ensured by introducing a Savitzky-Golay (S-G) smoothing ﬁlter, see Savitzky
Page 20 of 60
==PAGE:34==
and Golay (1964). The build-in MATLAB function sgolayfilt is utilized, which allow for the
use of a arbitrarily high polynomial order as well as a user speciﬁed frame of smoothing at each
node. Fuhrman (2004) conducted a thorough analysis of the eﬀect of including higher orders in
the S-G ﬁlter. It was concluded that the best results were obtained when applying relatively
high order ﬁlters, which minimize the eﬀect on the physical aspects of a given problem. For most
applications, ﬁlters down to a order of six was proven suﬃcient, but it should be kept in mind
that the ﬁlter-choice is case speciﬁc. The implementation also oﬀers the option of specifying the
amount of time steps between the smoothing.
The ﬁlter routine is computationally very cheap relative to the overall simulation. Therefore,
including a larger frame, a higher order or more often smoothing is not necessarily a big expense.
It should however be noted, that for highly discretized non-linear problems, extra care should be
taken, as the ﬁlter eﬀect might have large nonphysical eﬀect on the higher harmonics, eventually
dissipating the wave. By balancing these considerations, a signiﬁcant increase in stability and
robustness is achieved at an very small expense in computational time and at similarly low loss
of accuracy.
4.9
Moving Coastline
In order to enhance the models ability to simulate breaking induced set-up and run-up, it must
include a moving boundary feature. For this purpose, the ’extrapolating boundary’ technique
is adopted, which was ﬁrst successfully utilized within a Boussinesq type approach by Patrick
J. Lynett and Joseph (2002) and later by Fuhrman and Madsen (2008). The procedure is done
after each stage evaluation of the various time-stepping variables. It starts by determining the
grid point location of the current wet-dry boundary. This location is taken as the point where
the total water depth d = η + h exceeds the threshold denoted as δb which is assumed to be
very shallow. The values at the last two nodes before this eﬀective wet-dry boundary will be
used as base of extrapolation into the dry zone, replacing the initially computed values. The
extrapolation is done for both η and ˜V . The purpose of the extrapolation into the dry zone, is
allowing the ﬁnite diﬀerence stencils to work on continuous smooth functions across the wet-dry
boundary. A couple of ad-hoc modiﬁcations were included in order to ensure the stability of the
procedure. It is possible for the model to generate a water-surface having a higher slope than the
sea bed at the wet-dry boundary. As extrapolating a water surface from such a condition could
result in the addition of a possibly inﬁnite amount of water, it should be dealt with. If this is the
case, the algorithm will move the wet-dry boundary step-wise into the wet region, until the slope
of the water surface is less than that of the sea bed. The variables will then be extrapolated
from this modiﬁed wet-dry boundary instead. As the largest stencils applied utilizes 7 nodes,
the extrapolation can safely be capped at a level occurring at least 7 nodes from the wet-dry
boundary. This capping will induce non-physical ﬂuctuations in the time-stepped variables after
Page 21 of 60
==PAGE:35==
solving for these, but such ﬂuctuations will be safely overwritten. Two options are thus available:
1) Deﬁning a given maximum threshold value of the variable which is certainly one stencil length
from the wet-dry boundary. 2) Specifying the number of nodes the extrapolation should extend
to (at least one stencil length), and then ﬂat-line the variable for the rest of the domain. The
model contains the option to choose either of the methods, but the second has been preferred in
this work, as no concerns should be taken in deﬁning a satisfactory threshold. If nothing else is
stated, the extrapolation extend is 20 nodes into the dry region.
The above mentioned procedure performed are for η and ˜V . As the method allows for the
extrapolating to both positive and negative values this method is not desirable for k and ω.
Instead, the PDE’s eq. (3.21) and (3.22) will include a ﬂag (denoted B2 in the code), which will
ensure that only the dissipation term is active in the dry region, i.e.:

5.8
Conclusion
The preceding section has served as documentation of the appropriate implementation of the
Boussinesq equations and accompanied numerical features providing a range of modelling options.
This includes both ﬁxed wall- and periodic boundary conditions, wave generation- and absorption
zones, and a moving coastline.
The Boussinesq model was proven to show expected linear dispersion- and shoaling charac-
teristics. Highly non-linear waves were shown to stay stable for at least 50 periods with minimal
error to the dispersion characteristics. The results showed no indication that the 50 periods
should be a simulation limit.
The application of the moving boundary algorithm hinted that far smaller time-steps should
be expected to be necessary when simulating breaking wave conditions, than without the moving
coastline algorithm.

Introduction
This section presents the investigation undertaken of the behaviour of the three implemented
breaking closure models. This involves the ability to accurately simulate the drastic decrease in
wave height from the initiation of breaking, and the breaking associated set-up and set-down in

The aim has been to accurately simulate the experiments of a spilling breaker onto a slopping

– One-Equation Closure
– Two-Equation Closure

The breaking closure is supposed not to violate the momentum conservation which is governed
by the dynamic free surface condition eq. (2.8), but that appears not to be the case for the
simulations conducted. If the momentum conservation was obeyed, the period averaged dynamic
surface condition at each node would be zero. This is clearly not the case, as such signiﬁcant
deviations are present inside the surf zone, after x ≈15 −16 m. The reason for this remains
unexplained.
6.5
Comparative Remarks and Discussion of Performance
Obviously, the general implementation of all three closure models has a ﬂaw as mean water levels
are wrongly computed. The following section considers the general performance of the models,
without any further notes on the violated momentum conservation.
The domain with a moving coastline generally takes slightly lower maximum surface elevations
right before breaking than the the simulations on a domain with a shallow shelf, which matches
the experiments by Ting and Kirby (1994) slightly better.
The simulations on the domain
with a moving coastline however shows surface elevation outside the surf-zone matching the
experimental results better.
The overall behaviour of the three models, on both investigated domains, are very encouraging
in regards to the objective of decaying wave heights inside the surf zone in a fashion that resembles
physical experiments. Only the two-equation model closure did not provide desirable results on
the domain with a moving coastline. It is however believed that this can be achieved with a
iterative tuning process of a few constants. Preferably, a set of parameters yielding the same
breaking results on both the two domains investigated here, but also e.g. a shallow submerged
bar, should be sought.
The eddy viscosity governs the energy dissipation and thus the wave height decay, which
is why the behaviour of this quantity is imperative, and is the best way to compare the three
methods. In the one-equation closure model the eddy viscosity is per deﬁnition only present in
the breaking wave fronts. As similar accurate wave height decays were achieved with this model
for both domains the eddy viscosity levels should also indicate the required and sought levels
in the two more complex methods, which allows for the existence of eddy viscosity in the entire
surf zone. This was successfully achieved without any tuning eﬀort for the one-equation closure
model in both domains. As discussed this was not the case for the two-equation closure, where
the modelled eddy viscosity levels either became signiﬁcantly to high resulting in erroneous rapid
waveheight decay or to low causing numerical instabilities.
This raises the question: Which closure model is the preferable choice? As the two-equation
closure model is a ’complete’ closure it should provide the most physical correct results. Whether
that is the case for the current implementation is hard to conclude. It does however compute
Page 54 of 60
==PAGE:68==
a constant level of eddy viscosity inside the surf-zone, so does the one-equation model, which is
also seen from RANS CFD computations of the surf-zone in Larsen and Fuhrman (2018). This is
a ability the zero-equation model is not capable of, which thus lacks a physical aspect here. The
main property for decaying the waves accurately is however the models ability to generate the
right level of eddy viscosity for each passing wave (also the ﬁrst in a series!). Each wave breaking
event should in principle be independent of the preceding event. Which is an embedded quality
of the zero-equation closure. This was however also achieved with the production rates utilized
in the preceding tests for the one- and two-equation models.
It is certainly desirable to include the physical accuracy associated with the added PDE’s, and
while they are not adding a signiﬁcant increase in computational cost, nor limited in any other
aspect to the zero-equation model, their use can be justiﬁed. Due the very good performance
and apparent robustness of the one-equation model compared to the two-equation model, the
latter requires further adaption before it should be utilized.
It should however ﬁnally be recalled, as Wilcox (2006) states: "Because algebraic models
are so easy to use, they should be replaced only where demonstrably superior alternatives are
available", why the use of the simple zero-equation model should not be underestimated.

7.
Conclusions
7
Conclusions
The high-order Boussinesq model by Madsen et al. (2006) has been implemented in one horizontal
dimension in MATLAB. The wave model has been coupled with a wave breaking formulation based
on the eddy viscosity concept. This involved the addition of a energy dissipation term to the
dynamic surface condition, which was derived from the Reynold averaged Navier-Stokes equation.
Three diﬀerent model closures has been implemented for the determination of the eddy viscosity,
arising from the utilization of the Boussinesq approximation. The closures are zero- one- and
two-equation closures, where adding equations adds completeness to the closure, resulting in
fewer ﬂow assumptions at the cost of added complexity in terms of additional PDE’s to be time
stepped.
The domain is discretized with 7-order ﬁnite diﬀerence approximations, and the time inte-
gration is done with a fourth-order four-stage Runge-Kutta scheme. The domain can both be
imposed with ﬁxed wall- or periodic boundary conditions. Relaxation zones serving as wave-
maker and sponge layers are available as well as a moving coastline feature. The latter requiring
ﬁner temporal discretization in order to stay stable. For wave generation, Stokes 1-3rd order
theory is available as well as stream-function theory. Generally, the high-order Boussinesq equa-
tions, including up to ﬁfth-order derivatives, requires the use of some kind of smoothing to stay
stable, where the common Savitzky-Golay ﬁlter has been utilized throughout the work.
The Boussinesq model has continuously been veriﬁed through the implementation phase.
Expected linear dispersion and shoaling properties were established, and stable accurate non-
linear progression was obtained up to a non-linearity of H/L = 0.11. The boundary conditions,
relaxations zones and moving coastline were all tested to work correctly as intended.
All three breaking model closures were tested against the experiments conducted by Ting
and Kirby (1994) for a spilling breaker. The tests were both conducted on a domain ending
with a shallow shelf with a absorption zone, and with the moving coastline. Generally, all three
closures were able to break the wave at the right place in space, and gave excellent wave height
decays inside the surf zone. Only the two-equation closure gave too rapid energy dissipation
when breaking on the domain with a moving coastline. This is however expected to be a matter
of tuning appropriate coeﬃcients to the production terms in the PDE’s, and not as a limitation
of the model. All three models computed quite accurate maximum surface elevations outside the
surf zone and at the point of breaking. The models however all proved a inability to compute
correct mean surface elevations inside the surf zone, as a stable set-down always emerged. An
investigation into the momentum balance showed that an period averaged excess momentum is
present inside the surf zone. The reason for this remains unexplained. Thus, except inability
to compute correct mean water levels, the zero- and one-equation models shows very good and
robust results. The two-equation model still requires tuning, but this is believed to be achievable.

8.
Future Works
8
Future Works
The ﬁrst priority of any future works with the current model would be to continue the search of
the presumed error in the system that ultimately leads to wrongly computed mean water levels.
Succeeding with this, the following would indeed be interesting additions to the code.
Test the model against other experimental benchmarks, e.g. the breaking wave also presented
in Ting and Kirby (1994).
It could be interesting to test in depth how the current model would compute the breaking if
completely neglecting the breaking coeﬃcient B, and thus making the PDE’s govern the entire
breaking process alone. This would most likely require signiﬁcant ﬁne-tuning of the turbulence
production, while decent results would still be far from certain.
Investigate the models capabilities to simulate a hydraulic jump.
Kazolea and Ricchiuto
(2018) discusses how this is not easily achieved with the current adopted breaking criteria, as a
hydraulic jump is a somewhat stationary event. Alternatively, a criteria based on the slope of
the wave front, as e.g. described in Schäﬀer et al. (1993). Kazolea and Ricchiuto (2018) reports
how their attempt with a one-equation model closure similar to that utilized in this work, was
not a unambiguous success. It could thus be of interest to investigate whether the two-equation
model closure possibly could provide improvements.
As was originally intended with the current work, a sediment transport module could be
included in the model.
This would require the calculation of the bed load and possibly the
suspended load. This could be done with the new practical engineering model for the friction
velocity presented in Larsen and Fuhrman (2019) , making it possible to predict the time variation
of the friction velocity based only on the free stream velocity signal. This seems to be a method
particularly well suited to the Boussinesq framework. Ultimately, including a additional PDE
governing the sediment transport, the sediment continuity equation, would make it possible to
include morphological updating.
