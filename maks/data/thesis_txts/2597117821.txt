Abstract
Multi-objective optimization is the logical extrapolation of standard operations research,
and represents a wide variety of problems.
In this thesis, a basis for understand bi-
objective optimization is presented through a review of the methodology for multi-ojective
optimization. In addition, two pre-existing algorithms are discussed and compared to
the Boxcutter method, which represents a new procedure for generating non-dominated
sets of points. Finally, the extension to tri-objective problems is introduced, and possible
means of adapting the Boxcutter method to three objectives are discussed.

Introduction
Multi-objective optimization is of great interest, primarily because of its large presence in
real world applications. As is often the case, practical versions of a problem have many
more complications to them than is initially present in the academic introduction. Take,
for example, the Traveling Salesman Problem. The TSP is a very well known IP problem,
and it is likely that every Operations Research student has been introduced to it. In its
standard form, the problem seeks to find the shortest route connecting a series of nodes.
Each node must be visited exactly once, and the route must form a single loop through
the nodes.
Now consider how this example would play out for an actual salesman. To begin with,
the traveler might decide they want the quickest route through the nodes, that is to say,
minimizing time spent. But in all likely hood, the traveler would also seek to save as much
money as possible. This means another objective is introduced, minimizing expenses.
The fastest route is rarely the cheapest, so now there are two opposing objectives that
must be balanced. Simply solving two separate single-objective models is insufficient,
unless one objective can be prioritized entirely over the other. This means new techniques
for solving for multiple objectives must be applied.
However, despite the ubiquitous nature of multi-objective problems, there exists no stan-
dard method of solving them. For single-objective problems, their exist various software
packages, such as Cplex [1], Gurobi [2], or GLPK [3], which can easily be given a problem
and solve for the optima. However, no such software yet exists for multi-objective work,
and new algorithms must be written from scratch to approach any given problem.
The goal of this thesis is two-fold. The first is to investigate a new method of effectively
searching bi-objective criterion space. This new method, dubbed the Boxcutter Method,
will be evaluated alongside two established methods, namely the a posteriori Box Method
[4] and the Balance Box Method [5]. Additionally, the application of the Boxcutter method
to three objectives will also be explored, but is left as an open-ended discussion. Second,
the hope for this thesis is that it may be used as a comprehensive introduction to multi-
objective optimization for future OR students, in order to further research of this type of
problem.
As a note to the reader, this thesis depends heavily on background knowledge from a few
key articles, namely [6], [4], [5], [7], and [8]. [6] is used to provide a basic introduction
to multi-objective optimization. It is assumed the reader has a background in Operations
Research, but has not had extensive experience with multi-objective optimization. [4] and
[5] are detailed to explain the workings of the a posteriori Box method and the Balance
Box method respectively. [7] is cited to provided the background to the conception of the
Boxcutter method. [8] is reviewed to provide understanding of how an algorithm might
be evaluated besides just the time to completion. The information from these articles is
covered here for the sake of completion, and sections with information stemming from
them are identified in their opening paragraphs.
Section 2 acts as an introduction to ordering and multi-objective optimization, and provides
the background information to the terminology utilized in the rest of the thesis. Sections 3,
4, and 5 provide details on the specific algorithms used for testing. Additionally, Section 6
discusses the qualities of a ‚ÄĚgood‚ÄĚ representation. In Sections 7 and 8, the results that this

Bi-Objective Optimization
When considering the bi-objective integer programming model, it is useful to think of it
as simply an extension of the single objective case. After all, the constraint matrix is
completely untouched by the addition of a second objective function. In this case, the
decision space of the model is structurally unchanged. This background is further detailed
in [6].

Like their single-objective counterparts, multi-objective problems can be both continuous

compares three algorithms which address this type of model.
Formalizing the Pareto front requires several definitions regarding the decision space (the

Of the Pareto Front, the lexicographic points are the most straightforward to find. Conse-
quently, all the algorithms examined in this thesis begin by calculating them. These points
are found by optimizing the objective functions in lexicographic order, that is to say the
order in which they are listed in the objective vector. There exist as many lexicographic
points as there are orderings possible given the number of objectives. In the case of the
bi-objective problem, this means there are two lexicographic points. The points are found
by first minimizing one objective as if by single-objective optimization. Then the second
objective is minimized, but with the added constraint that the first objective may not ex-
ceed its minimum value. These points form the extreme points on the Pareto front, with
all other non-dominated points located between them.

The objective then of multi-objective search algorithms is to as efficiently as possible find
the remaining non-dominated points. These algorithms fall into two main categories; de-
cision space search algorithms and criterion space search algorithms. Decision space
refers to the set of feasible x values, and algorithms of this sort often involve many forms
of branches and cuts. However, new types of branching must defined for multi-objective
problems, and as such developing new decision space algorithms requires a tremendous
effort. The problem is that decision space algorithms cannot take advantage of the legacy
of improvements found in single-objective solvers, such as CPLEX and Gurobi, which are
incredibly efficient at solving MIP problems. As such, they are often much more inefficient
without a serious investment.
Criterion space is the set of feasible f(x) values, and often involves bounds on various
objectives to search specific parts of the problem. These methods are therefore capable of
benefiting from increased performance of single-objective solvers. This thesis is primarily
concerned with criterion space search algorithms.

There are many qualities by which an algorithm may be judged, so only a select number
are discussed here. The most natural is total time until completion, when the algorithm
will return the Pareto front. However, even the most advanced algorithms may use unrea-

observe is how well the algorithm approximates the Pareto front after a small amount of

no longer see massive increases to their computational power, it becomes increasingly
useful to take advantage of the large number of cores available on modern machines.
This last quality is not formally measured here, but it may be useful to consider how well
a problem can be divided into multiple independent sub-problems. In theory, the brute
force method could assign each ňÜŌĶ to a separate core to increase the speed of the search.

Representations of Pareto Fronts
Due to the nature of multi-objective optimization, as the size of the models increases the
total run-time of the algorithms tend to approach unreasonable size. As such, it may be
more important for the algorithm to construct a useful representation of the Pareto front
quickly, rather than the entire non-dominated set. This means that an algorithm which
may take many iterations to finally search all the criterion space can be more valuable
than an algorithm which terminates relatively quickly, but which struggles to find points
early on. The question then is how to evaluate the resulting estimation. This thesis refers
to the metrics detailed in [8].
The most obvious metric is to compare the actual number of non-dominated points found.
However, this is insufficient in practice to determine the usefulness of the representation.
Consider the brute force method of searching the space discussed in Algorithm 1. Should
it be terminated part-way through its run-time, the resulting representation would in fact
poorly represent the true Pareto front. All the points discovered would be weighed to one
side, nearest the upper right lexicographical point (see Figure 6.1).
Figure 6.1: The representation from early termination of the brute force method.
This is clearly not a particularly useful estimation, as any non-dominated points favoring
the other objective are not located. The representation does not represent a large number
of points, and its members are clustered in one area. In the following section, several ways
of evaluating representations are discussed.
Ideally, a representation of the Pareto front should provide non-dominated points which
fairly represent the space between the lexicographical points, but which contains a min-
imal number of clusters of points. It is also advantageous to obtain these qualities with
a minimal total number of points, as the problem in question is the computational time
needed. This is to provide the decision maker, the one who is to make use of the opti-
mal solution, with the tools to make the best decision. Evaluations of these qualities are
discussed extensively in [8], and will be review here for completeness. These consist of
three criteria, namely coverage, uniformity, and cardinality.

Methodology
The main aim of this next section is to compare the new Boxcutter method to the es-
tablished methods of bi-objective optimization. The goal is to find a reliable method of
measuring the performance of the algorithm which will allow for sensible evaluation of the
various methods in relation to each other.
The primary metric by which the algorithms will be measured is the number of calls to the
optimization problem modeled in Model 2.4. When timing the methods it was determined
that more than 99% of the run time was spent computing this problem, so it is a reasonable
method of measuring performance. Therefore the progress of the various methods can
be graphed by taking the size of the current Pareto representation as a function over the
number of calls to Model 2.4. However, it should be made clear that the model used in
the Boxcutter method differs in constraints from that of the a posteriori and the Balance
Box method, so consideration must be given to the difference actual time between each
model.
When interpreting the results, there are multiple qualities of the algorithms of interest. The
most obvious might be which algorithm terminates first, providing the full Pareto front.
However, in real world applications, many problems are too large to be evaluated in their
entirety. Therefore it is also important to take note of how quickly each method approaches
close approximation of the Pareto front. These algorithms could be made to terminate
early, and still provide sufficient to fulfill the purpose of the full front. This can be done
because every point found by the algorithms is guaranteed to be non-dominated. Finally,
a measure of the run-time in seconds is measured, to give a sense of the real world
time needed to complete each method. Note, that the sizes across the different types
of problems are not comparable. This means although all tested methods may seem to
perform better on one problem type compared to the other, this may simply be due to a
difference between the number of variables in each problem. Direct comparisons should
only be drawn between the three methods on each problem type.
The performance of each algorithm may also vary based on the type of optimization prob-
lem they are applied to. Therefore it is prudent to compare the efficiency of the methods
on a variety of problems. Although it is not an exhaustive list, three types were chosen for
this thesis. The data for these bi-objective problems comes from the vOptLib library, [11].
For these experiments, the algorithms are applied to the Bi-Objective Set Covering Prob-
lem (SCP), the Set Packing Problem (SPP), and the Knapsack Problem (KSP). These
problems are easy to implement and test, as all coefficients and variables are composed
of integer values. While this gives the results of the comparison more legitimacy, it should
be noted that there are some short-comings as well. Most notably, all three problems cho-
sen for these tests utilize exclusively binary variables. Such overlap means that further
testing could be performed to explore the properties of the evaluated algorithms.
Finally, it is of interest to compare the quality of the Pareto front representations at a given
time. For each of the three problem types, the three algorithms will be evaluated part way
through their computation of the true front. Since the number of elements in the repre-
sentation is recorded for any given number of calls to the optimization of Model 2.4, the
representations can be reconstructed by saving the non-dominated points in order of their
discovery. The metrics detailed in [8] provide the primary evaluation of the representa-
tions‚Äô quality.

Transition to 3D
Having crafted an algorithm for the 2D bi-objective problem, the natural extension is the
step to tri-objective. However, the complexity of the problem does not simply scale up
by one, and the algorithm in question may require some adjustments. To illustrate this,
consider first the lexicographic points. For two dimensions, two points could be found
by optimizing the objectives in order, f1(x), f2(x) and f2(x), f1(x). However, for three
objectives there are six possible orderings of the objectives, resulting in six lexicographic
points. This is a factorial scaling, which quickly increases the size of the problem. Defining
the search space for many algorithms now falls to the ideal point and the nadir point. The
ideal point remains simple, but the nadir point becomes rapidly more complex. Many
heuristics exist to calculate it, but it remains a costly problem, as it scales as a factorial of
the number of objective functions.

Creating an algorithm to inefficiently calculate the tri-objective Pareto front is straightfor-

front. For example, a second weakness of the brute force method detailed above is that as
it solves for the Pareto front, it locates points neighboring the previous points it has found.
Thus, early termination of the brute force method would result in a skewed representation
which leaves large portions of the possible Pareto front un-searched.

Conclusion
In this thesis criteria space search methods for multi-objective optimization problems are
considered. Starting with an introduction into multi-objective optimization, two established
methods, the a posteriori Box method [4] and the Balance Box method [5] were detailed
to illustrate the search procedure. Then the new Boxcutter method, derived from part of
the hybrid method introduced in [7], is introduced as a new candidate for generating the
Pareto front.
To evaluate the performance of these algorithms, in addition to time until completion, var-
ious criteria for evaluating representations of the Pareto front were discussed [8]. These
include uniformity and coverage error. These metrics are important when a problem is
too large to solve to completion, when an estimation of the final answer is more feasible.
The three methods were tested against each other on three different integer programming
problems, the SCP, SPP, and KSP. In general, for the tests performed in this thesis it was
found that the Boxcutter performed better than the a posteriori method but worse than the
Balance Box method in terms of speed when finding the Pareto front. This was true of
both the SCP and SPP. For the KSP, it was found that the Boxcutter method performed
slower than even the a posteriori method. If speed is the primary concern for finding a
representation of the Pareto front, the Balance Box method is the best choice.
When this thesis started, a great emphasis was placed on the speed of the various algo-
rithms. However, in hindsight more focus should have been placed on the representations
generated by the methods in a given amount of time or iterations. For multi-objective prob-
lems, even fast algorithms may take a long time to solve large problems. If speed was
the main concern, the ŌĶ-Constraint method would be ideal. Instead, emphasis should be
placed on the representations given by the methods at various points in their search of
the criteria space. Given the data collected here, the a posteriori box method appears
to be the best choice for building a representation. For a given cardinality, it produced
the representation with the lowest coverage error and the highest uniformity. This is due
to the tendency of the Balance Box method and the Boxcutter method to find points in
clustered pairs.
Finally, the transition from bi-objective to tri-objective problems is addressed. Some theo-
ries are presented for how to adapt the original Boxcutter method to the problem. Ideally,
an algorithm that can generate a good representation could be found in future study, as by
the nature of the problem each objective causes the complexity to increase on a factorial
scale. If the current Boxcutter method were to re-purposed for tri-objective problems, it
would likely prove ineffective due to it‚Äôs nature of finding clusters of points. Some modifi-
cation could hopefully be found to increase uniformity in the representation.
In the future, better techniques could be found to apply the Boxcutter method to the tri-
objective case. More in depth analysis of the representations generated by these methods
could also be conducted. The Boxcutter may not have proven most effective in its current
form, but there might be a more efficient way to use it to generate a representation. It‚Äôs
