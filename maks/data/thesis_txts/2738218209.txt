Abstract
As electricity systems move toward higher integration of variable renewable energy sources to
meet national and global carbon reduction targets, the need for frequency-balancing services will
increase. Thus, new flexibility sources need to enter the ancillary service markets. This thesis
investigates the possible synergy effect of aggregating heterogeneous technologies in a portfolio
to bid in the Nordic synchronous grid’s Frequency Containment Reserve for Disturbances (FCR-
D) market under the Danish transmission system operator Energinet’s P90 requirement. This
novel requirement allows flexible demand and fluctuating production to enter the ancillary service
markets by allowing bids with a 90% delivery certainty and a 10% allowed shortfall.
Based on historical data, optimal bidding strategies were developed for a portfolio of electric
vehicles and a heat exchanger station connected to the Copenhagen district heating system, using
reformulations of a chance-constrained program. An Analytical reformulation was compared to
existing sample-based reformulations, which included an Exact mixed-integer linear program
reformulation, a relaxation of the Exact reformulation known as the ALSO-X algorithm, and,
lastly, a Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR) reformulation.
The research addresses two primary questions: Can aggregating heterogeneous flexibility sources
create a synergy effect for an aggregator? Can an analytical reformulation be used to find a
tractable solution to the chance-constrained program modeling the P90 requirement, and how
does this methodology compare to sample-based approximation programs?
Optimal bidding strategies were evaluated using a 3-fold cross-validation over one year. Results
showed a synergy effect of 13-18% in terms of earned revenues when bidding the flexibility of
the aggregated portfolio to the FCR-D market while still complying with the P90 requirement.
Although the Analytical reformulation is advantageous in computational time and transparency
compared to the other reformulated programs, the method is limited by the assumption that
the underlying flexibility distribution follows a well-known probability distribution.
This thesis contributes to the field by demonstrating a synergy effect in the aggregated portfolio
and deriving an analytical reformulation for the chance-constrained program.

Introduction
In today’s societies, turning on the light, charging our phones, or putting the kettle on for tea
are all trivial parts of our daily lives. We comfortably go about our day without thinking about
our constant electricity consumption. Electricity systems have, however, not always been as
secure and reliable as today, and behind the unnoticeable concept of power, a complex system
maintains stability. Various technologies, services, and actions are at any moment ready to be
activated in the event of a sudden power plant failure, transmission line breakdown, or rise in
electricity demand, such as when UEFA EURO football watchers collectively take a break during
half-time [1].
This network of actions and services, known as ancillary services, is largely responsible for main-
taining the security of electricity supply. These services range from reaction times of milliseconds
to a response within 15 minutes and are provided by electricity production or consumption units
altering their baseline power to match the disturbance.
As electricity systems move toward higher penetration of renewable energy sources, sustaining
energy security becomes an increasingly complex challenge [2, 3]. Renewable energy sources such
as wind and solar are inherently fluctuating, leading to imbalances between supply and demand,
which results in increased volatility and uncertainty. Climate stresses, increasing electrification
of transport and heating sectors, and the reductions of conventional synchronous generators also
contribute to instability.
Consequently, growing needs for ancillary services are projected in Denmark. This incentivizes
the need for market structures and regulatory frameworks that allow for new balancing service
providers [4]. These new actors include flexible consumption units, large-scale batteries, and
renewable energy sources like wind and solar.
Introducing fluctuating technologies in the markets is a challenging task for transmission system
operators (TSOs) as security of delivery is essential and ancillary services are the last response
before major grid instabilities. In Denmark, the transmission system operator, Energinet, has
introduced a framework, denoted as the P90 requirement, which allows fluctuating sources to
enter the market given they bid at a probability of delivery of 90%. Consequently, this require-
ment allows for a reserve shortfall in 10% of the time, which can greatly increase the number of
technologies and capacity offered to the ancillary service markets [5]. This thesis will investigate
how an actor can bid in the Frequency Containment Reserve for Disturbances (FCR-D) market
under the P90 requirement.
The P90 requirement is innovative and allows new units to enter the market. However, as 90%
certainty in a bid is still needed, the question of increasing the bid capacity at the 90% probability
arises.
This thesis will investigate the possible synergy effect of aggregating heterogeneous
technologies.
In this context, heterogeneous aggregation refers to the pooling of flexibility-
delivering units with distinct baseline profiles.
Additionally, a synergy effect here refers to
the possible increase in the capacity an aggregator can bid into a market when aggregating
heterogeneous technologies as compared to bidding these technologies separately. Homogeneous
aggregation of units of the same technology is still present.
This thesis investigates the possible synergy effect of an aggregator having a portfolio of units
with different electricity consumption patterns compared to having only one type of asset in its
portfolio. The study will be conducted through a case study using historical data from 1400

Optimal bidding strategies for the aggregator will be developed by using the historically available
flexibility capacity as input to reformulations of a chance-constrained program (CCP), hereby
allowing for a certain level of uncertainty in the capacity bids.
The first reformulation is a linear Analytical reformulation of the joint chance constraints of the
original problem. The latter three reformulations are sample-based (SB) inner approximations
of the original problem where the first method is an Exact reformulation using mixed linear
integer programming. The second sample-based reformulation is a linear relaxation to the Exact
program named the ALSO-X. This linear program uses an iterative algorithm to approximate
the Exact program. Lastly, a Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR) reformulation will serve as the
last sample-based program.
1.1
Research Questions, Hypotheses, and Contributions
This thesis poses the following research questions:
• Can aggregating heterogeneous flexibility sources create a synergy effect for an aggregator?
• Can an analytical reformulation be used to find a tractable solution to the chance-constrained
program modeling the P90 requirement, and how does this methodology compare to
sample-based approximation programs?
The first research question involves the synergy effect of aggregation of the technologies used
for the case study while the second question will look at how a possible analytical reformulation
compares to sample-based reformulations.
The research questions are posed based on the following hypothesis. Firstly, given that ancil-
lary services are delivered on an hourly resolution, the hourly minimum of available flexibility
capacity impacts the possible capacity bid. In the case of the P90 requirement, 10% of the
minutes of the hour can have a flexibility below the capacity bid. Consequently, if the 10th
percentile of flexibility capacity across the hour can be increased, an aggregator can increase its
bid quantity. It is hypothesized that, as the technologies in the case study are from different
stochastic processes, their aggregation will help to increase the hourly capacity bids, as hourly
variability is decreased. Furthermore, it is hypothesized that a synergy effect will emerge from
aggregation as the resulting flexibility capacity distribution will be tighter and distributed closer
to well-known distributions.
Related to the analytical reformulation it is hypothesized that a tractable model reformulation
can be found and that this formulation will be linear and result in a more computationally
eﬀicient program as compared to the sample-based approximations.
The contributions to research from this thesis are two-fold. Other studies have investigated
bidding aggregated portfolios of either EVs or heat pumps in different ancillary service markets
[6, 7], or mapping the current provision of ancillary services from heat pumps [8]. Furthermore,
the role of the aggregator in electricity systems has been explored [9]. However, the special
aggregation case of aggregating EVs and heating units has not been widely investigated in the
literature. Meesenburg et al. studied a case of electric vehicles and a community-scale heat
pump for providing ancillary services in Denmark [10] but not including the P90 requirement.
Furthermore, the study looked at bidding in the Frequency Containment Reserve for Normal
Operation (FCR-N) market, so the asymmetric nature of the FCR-D market has not been

2.1
Electricity Market Overview
2.1.1
Synchronous Grids
An introduction to electricity grids and frequency balancing can provide a motivation for the
need for ancillary services and their role in electricity markets. Electricity grids are composed of
an extensive network of interconnected cables, ranging from distribution grids at the consumer
level to high-voltage transmission grids that transport substantial quantities of energy across
regions and countries. The interconnection of countries on the transmission system level means
that electricity is transmitted at the same frequency in large areas using alternating current
(AC). A stable frequency is necessary to maintain grid stability; thus, the frequency is kept
within a narrow band of around 50 Hz in Europe [15]. The interconnection of different national
grids means that large geographical areas share the same frequency.
Electric grids sharing the same reference frequency are referred to as synchronous grids. Figure
2.1 depicts the different European synchronous areas. It can be seen that large parts of conti-
nental Europe and the western part of Denmark are part of one synchronous area, named the
Continental European synchronous area. In contrast, Sealand and the Nordic countries are part
of another synchronous area, the Nordic synchronous area. Additionally, the Baltic countries
share frequency, whereas Great Britain and Ireland have separate frequencies. Lastly, Iceland
is an isolated system. These synchronous grids are interconnected through direct current (DC)
cables, ensuring each grid maintains a distinct frequency [15].
Each country’s transmission system operators (TSOs) maintain the frequency at the nominal
value.
This is a challenging task, as the frequency is a result of a precise balance between
electricity consumption and production at all times. Furthermore, the grid’s stability is also
directly related to the inertia in the systems, which is mainly contributed to by synchronous
generators. The frequency will drop if there is a greater electricity consumption than production,
termed under-frequency, and rise if there is higher production than consumption, termed over-
frequency [16].
Historically, when electricity grids consisted of centralized thermal power plants, each syn-
chronous generator would measure the grid’s frequency and use droop control to adjust its
production to match any imbalances: increasing production if the frequency was below 50 Hz
and decreasing if the frequency was above 50 Hz. Thus, droop control involves distributing the

In addition to demand-supply mismatches, imbalances can arise from sudden faults and trips of
generators, transformers, transmission lines, or other critical grid components. If a significant
fault occurs, the frequency will drop considerably as large portions of the total production are in
deficit [18]. Such imbalances can be detrimental to grid stability, as the interconnected nature
of the grids means that faults in one country can affect the frequency and voltage in another.
If large enough imbalances occur and the grids lack suﬀicient capacity to cover any under- or
over-frequencies, this can lead to blackouts, cascading outages, or involuntary load shedding
[18].
Electricity grids are continuously experiencing an increase in renewable energy penetration. This
poses new diﬀiculties for TSOs, as wind and solar energy are variable and inherently stochastic.
Uncertainty in forecasts and sudden changes in production contribute to new sources of imbal-
ance [19]. Additionally, as the industrial, heating, and transport sectors become increasingly
electrified, the variability in electricity consumption patterns will also rise. Consequently, bal-
ancing needs are projected to increase where Energinet, the Danish TSO, e.g., projects a 3.000
- 5.000 MW/h need for all services across all of Denmark by 2030 [4].

move. This can be counter-intuitive when considering demands, as they operate opposite the

Another important aspect of grid stability is inertia.
Inertia refers to the rotational energy
generated by the turbines at thermal power plants. The rotational mass of the turbines creates
a resistance to imbalance and, therefore, delays the frequency deviation an imbalance can cause.
During periods with high renewable energy output and low inertia, the power system becomes
more vulnerable to sudden changes [4, 24].
2.1.2
Ancillary Services
To secure the necessary capacities needed for balancing, the TSOs of each country secure balanc-
ing through ancillary services, which cover several different markets to which balancing capacity
can be offered. Ancillary services are characterized by the speed at which the service must be
provided after a fault occurs. The services differ across the different synchronous areas. Den-
mark is subdivided into two regions following the natural division between the Continental and
Nordic synchronous areas. The western part of Denmark is named DK1, and the Eastern part is
DK2, as depicted in Figure 2.3. The different ancillary service abbreviations of services in DK1
and DK2 are further displayed. The meaning of each abbreviation is presented in Table 2.2.

Activation Times
The ancillary services in Denmark are further presented in Figure 2.4, which shows the activation
of each ancillary service in DK1 and DK2 depending on activation time and reserve quantity.
The types of reserves differ as follows: immediately after an imbalance, the containment reserve
is activated. The small energy change is enough to secure a momentary frequency stabilization.
In DK1, this is provided by FCR, while in DK2, it is managed by FRR, FCR-D, and FCR-N.
Subsequently, the restoration reserves activate larger reserve quantities, ensuring enough energy
quantities to restore the energy balance for shorter periods, which in Denmark is achieved by
activating aFRR. Finally, the replacement reserves provided by mFRR are deployed to replace
the energy quantity needed to balance the system fully. The figures show that in DK1, FCR is
fully activated within 30 seconds, aFRR is fully activated after 15 minutes, and mFRR, which is
activated later than aFRR, also has a 15-minute activation time [5]. In DK2, FFR has a reaction
time of 0.7, 1, or 1.3 seconds, depending on the activation level, with full activation at 49.5,
49.6, or 49.7 Hz. Additionally, FCR-D must be activated within 10 seconds, with 86% of the
capacity activated within 7.5 seconds and an energy delivery corresponding to 3.2 seconds of full
activation. FCR-N should be supplied within 3 minutes, with 96% activation within this time,
including the first 63% within 60 seconds. Lastly, in DK2, aFRR has an activation requirement
of 5 minutes and mFRR has one of 15 minutes.

Activation
Each ancillary service must be delivered as either a symmetric or asymmetric product, with
up- and down-regulation provided according to the specified symmetry or asymmetry.
The
activation of a reserved quantity, sold to one of the ancillary service markets, occurs within a
band around 50 Hz. The products with symmetric activation include FCR and aFRR in DK1,
and FFR and FCR-N in DK2.
FCR-D is procured as an asymmetric product, allowing for separate bids for up- or down-
regulation, denoted as FCR-D Up and FCR-D Down. Similarly, aFRR capacity is procured
separately for up- and down-regulation. In contrast, mFRR is only procured for up-regulation,
although Energinet has the possibility to purchase down-regulation if necessary [5].
As their name suggests, manual reserves are activated manually. A signal from Energinet acti-
vates automatic restoration reserves, while fast reserves are activated directly by the operators
of the individual units by measuring the frequency and activating accordingly. The activation
profile and frequency range of FCR-D and -N are shown in Figure 2.6.

Pricing and Financial Settlement
Bidders can be compensated for their reserved capacities by a pay-as-cleared (PaC) or pay-as-bid
(PaB) principle. In PaC markets, all accepted bids are paid at the same price as the marginal
price of the last unit. In PaB markets, the market actors are compensated at the individual
bid price they made if their bid entered the market. As of February 1st, 2024, only the aFRR
market in DK1 is paid at PaB. Before this, the FCR-D and FCR-N markets were also paid as
PaB [25, 28].
In all markets, providers are compensated for reserving their capacity, which accounts for the
opportunity cost of not bidding that capacity in the day-ahead or intra-day markets.
The
providers are also compensated for their energy deliveries if they are activated. However, FCR,
FFR, and FCR-D are not compensated for activation since these services are energy-poor and do
not require significant energy deliveries. Any measurable imbalances will, however, be reflected
by the unit owner having to pay or receive the imbalance price, as with other wholesale electricity
imbalances [25].
Energy activation of mFRR is compensated at a regulating power price, which is common in
all Nordic countries except in cases of congestion. This is an hourly price settled for both up-
and down-regulation. For FCR-N, the delivered energy is compensated at the balancing power
price, which is the same price used for mFRR compensation, for either up- or down-regulation.
Since FCR-N is frequently activated, the overall delivered energy is calculated by considering
both positive and negative energy deliveries. Lastly, aFRR is compensated at the spot market
price plus or minus 100 DKK/MWh, depending on whether up- or down-regulation is delivered
[25].

2.1.3
Requirements
As ancillary services are crucial to securing a stable grid in the event of significant failures, En-
erginet sets several specific requirements for each service. These generally include the activation
times mentioned in above sections and the minimum delivery time. The frequency-controlled
services, FCR, FFR FCR-D, and FCR-N, are not given by signals sent by Energinet, but instead,
the units should alter the demand or supply depending on the direct measurement of the grid’s
frequency [5].
The processing of the frequency signal and the automatic and manual signals sent from Energinet
are to be handled by the balance service provider (BSP). The requirements provide a set of tests
that a unit wanting to qualify to deliver a service must pass. The signal processing can be
done in different ways. Thus, Energinet allows the activation of the reserve to be within an
accuracy band around the precise activation that is wanted. This means that units are allowed
to overshoot and, to some extent, undershoot the amount of reserve activated when a signal
is induced. The tests also require a unit to show that a stable activation can be held across a
specific time frame. Lastly, the test should show that a unit can activate the reserves multiple
times across an hour in shorter and longer periods and at low and high activation. The specific
list of requirements for each service is long, and the reader is referred to the regulation paper
“Prequalification of units and aggregated portfolios” published by Energinet. However, the two
relevant requirements for the case study are presented below.
P90 Requirement
Energinet has set an additional requirement for units that consume or produce electricity with
uncontrollable fluctuating patterns to be allowed to provide ancillary services. While these units
can qualify to participate in the ancillary service market, they cannot bid their installed capacity
or the expected production or consumption value for the day of delivery.
Instead, a probabilistic forecast must be created and provided to Energinet. The forecast is used
to determine the 10th percentile of available flexibility, which corresponds to a 90% certainty in
the bid. This requirement will be referred to as the P90 requirement [5].
The requirement is set such that the unit will be available to provide the necessary activation
in the event of an imbalance most of the time, which, if the forecast is correct, is 90% of the
Preliminaries
13
==PAGE:27==
time. The requirement is progressive, allowing actors to have a reserve shortfall 10% of the
time. Considering the possible impacts of missing balance capacity, Energinet is thus accepting
that risk is tied to the stochastic units bidding under the P90 requirement. Energinet argues
that a 10% risk level is appropriate as reserves to balance a high certainty in delivery and new
capacity entering the market [5]. Furthermore, it is highlighted that even if the unit cannot
supply the total capacity, there will be few incidents where it cannot provide any activation.
Thus, even though reserve shortfalls are allowed 10% of the time, the activation shortfall will
not be 10%. Additionally, Energinet has established a 5% buffer, which means that aggregators
are first excluded from the market if there is less than an 85% probability that the sold capacity
will be available [6].
A central value of Energinet is to allow as much capacity as possible to enter the markets.
Therefore, they aim to enable all technologies to enter the market under technology neutrality
to ensure competition and even rights. However, even under fair market entry, renewable energy
sources will always be a fluctuating resource, and flexible consumption cannot be flexible if it is
not turned on or connected. It is essential to have certainty of availability and an estimate for
the realized consumption and production if the reserve is not activated, called the baseline.
Energinet differentiates between centrally controllable and non-centrally controllable units.
Only non-centrally controlled units must comply with the additional forecast and baseline re-
quirements.
The additional requirements for non-centrally controllable units are two-fold, involving an ex-
ante forecast and an ex-post baseline estimation.
The forecast should be made before gate
closure time and must, at a minimum, match the resolution of the market time unit which is
usually hourly. To prequalify, three months of forecast data must be provided to Energinet along
with the realized consumption and production. The unit can only be prequalified if the forecast
has an accuracy of 90% or above. If measurement data can be provided at the same resolution
as required for data logging in the prequalification tests, Energinet only requires one month of
forecast and measurement data. For FCR-D a second-level resolution is needed in this case. If
the methodology used for the forecast is changed, Energinet needs to be notified, and the new
forecast methodology must be provided [5].
Energinet calculates the ancillary service response as the difference between a baseline given by
the BSP and the measured real-time consumption or production. The BSP is responsible for
providing a good baseline, as this is the only measure of response accuracy. Inaccurate baselines
can lead to penalties even if the actual activation was correct, and thus, Energinet recommends
high-resolution baselines. For FCR-D, this could be 1 second to match the requirements for the
data logging system. The penalty can be exclusion from the market or repayment of payment.
LER Requirements
An additional requirement outside activation response, activation times, and P90 requirement
is the Limited Energy Reservoir (LER) requirement. This rule is primarily enforced to regulate
large battery energy systems from bidding capacities that require charging and discharging
strategies to deliver the entire reserve [29]. The LER requirement thus states that a unit must be
able to sustain a complete response for two consecutive hours without charging and discharging.
Furthermore, a unit is required to have an energy quantity stored at 20 minutes of full activation.
A last requirement states that the unit must have a capacity of 20% in the other direction of
regulation [5].
Energinet provides the following examples: to prequalify 1 MW of capacity from a LER unit
in FCR-D Up, a reserve of 0.2 MW in the FCR-D Down direction is needed. Additionally, 20

MWh. The LER requirements will not be included in the case study modeling but can readily
be included in future works.

2.1.5
Roles in Electricity Markets
Various actors and roles constitute electricity markets. The TSO usually operates the transmis-
sion grid of a country or a larger country region. The TSO is responsible for the transmission
grid’s stable operation, maintaining and expanding it as production and consumption capacity
develops, and setting transmission grid tariffs. The distribution system operator (DSO) operates
the distribution grid. It ensures grid stability and manages new connections of local production
and consumption.
A balancing responsible party (BRP) is an entity that manages either consumption or production
assets in the electricity markets, bidding the expected production and/or consumption in the
day-ahead and intra-day markets. A BRP is financially responsible for covering the costs of
imbalances between the electricity bought and sold and the actual realization of consumption
and production. To become a BRP, it is necessary to have financial liquidity so that it is possible
to cover possible costs. A balance service provider is an actor that delivers ancillary services to
the transmission system operator. A BRP or an independent aggregator can today govern the
role of providing ancillary services [31].
An aggregator is a role that groups the electricity from multiple consumers and/or producers.
An aggregator can, e.g., group the consumption from 1000 electric vehicles or 500 residential
heat pumps. This grouping of units is called the aggregator’s portfolio [5]. In Denmark, two
aggregator roles exist: an aggregator with balancing responsibilities (a BRP) and an indepen-
dent aggregator without balancing responsibilities (not a BRP) [32]. A representation of the
aggregator role can be seen in Figure 2.11.
The individual units in the independent aggregator’s portfolio will have an agreement with
a BRP, which is needed to buy and sell electricity [32, 33]. If the services the independent
aggregator delivers to Energinet are energy-poor, the BRP of the individual unit should not see
any imbalance in energy.

Figure 2.11: The role of the aggregator in the energy market [5].
An independent aggregator can today only deliver energy-poor services, whereas an aggregator
with balancing responsibilities can deliver all services. Energinet is, however, in the process of
changing the methodology around the roles of an aggregator with balancing responsibilities and
the independent aggregator so that independent aggregators can deliver all ancillary services
[32, 33]. Following workshops with market participants in 2021 and a public hearing from July
to August 2023, Energinet is changing 10 methodologies in several of their regulations to change
the independent aggregator’s role in delivering energy-heavy products as well [32]. By changing
the possibilities for an independent aggregator, Energinet argues that the markets will become
less discriminatory as both aggregator roles will have the same possibilities in the markets. In
the public hearing, the market actors had several concerns regarding the methodology change,
the biggest being how a BRP is ensured not to face additional costs related to any imbalances
caused by the independent aggregator delivering ancillary services with a capacity under the
responsibility of the BRP. [34]. The possibility of a rebound effect was furthermore raised as a
concern. This covers the phenomenon that if, e.g., an electricity-consuming unit is activated in an
ancillary service market, the demand will be shifted to another hour as the EV has the be charged
at some point and the house needs heating. In the new methodology, the independent aggregator
must provide financial compensation to the BRP of the units in its portfolio corresponding to
the energy imbalance the aggregator has caused.
Several special requirements exist for aggregated portfolios to comply with Energinet’s prequal-
ifications. For aggregated portfolios managed by an aggregator or independent aggregator, the
entire portfolio must be approved. This entails the tests using the aggregator’s control system
and aggregation tool. The portfolio is seen as one “unit” and tested as one, meaning that the
overall reaction of the entire portfolio is tested. In contrast, the individual unit can deviate from
the requirements of the tests. In this case, the units can only enter the markets when aggregated
and not be sold independently.
One can also test individual units of the portfolio separately, and if it follows the requirements,
it can enter the market on both the individual and aggregated levels. Specifications of the assets
in the portfolio must, at any time, be provided to Energinet, including capacity, geographical
location, etc. The portfolio can be extended with units with the same technical specifications
as units in the already prequalified portfolio following approval from Energinet. The maximum
extension without additional prequalification is 25% of the portfolio size or a maximum of 10
MW.
The same control system should be used during the portfolio’s actual operation. The aggregation
concepts and IT communication system must be described and sent to Energinet, which then
approves the methodology if it shows compliance with the requirements.

Description of Electric Vehicles
For an aggregator looking to group production or consumption in a portfolio for ancillary ser-
vice, bidding on electric vehicles (EVs) is valuable as they have fast charging and discharging
capabilities [36]. Additionally, an advantage for EVs providing ancillary services is that the EV
owner has already made the investment cost in the battery. An aggregator would have to invest
in the IT systems to control the EV but would not have to invest in installing any capacities.
An electric vehicle works by charging its battery, usually using an AC charger connected to
a residential house, but public and company electric vehicles exist. At residential houses, EV
charging can be timed such that the charge can start at a specific time, e.g., at night. When
charged, the EV will drive using an electric motor driven by the energy stored in the car’s
lithium-ion battery [37]. Figure 2.13 shows a graphic representation of an electric vehicle.
The battery can provide up-regulation to the grid by either charging the battery slower or
shutting off the charge entirely. In order to provide down-regulation, the EV should charge its
battery faster or start a charge. This can only happen if the EV is plugged in at the same time.

2.2.3
Experiences with Ancillary Services in District Heating Networks
Delivering ancillary services from units in the Greater Copenhagen district heating sector comes
with additional challenges as the units are not only part of the electricity market and network
but also have responsibilities in the district heating market and network. The main challenge of
units connected to district heating networks is that any deviation from the baseline production
will cause a disturbance in the district heating network. Helping the electricity grid can thus
cause problems in the heating network and, in a sense, shift the disturbance from one network
to another.
In the works of this thesis, it was thus thought relevant to investigate the experiences district
heating companies had with delivering ancillary service. A joint interview with CTR and Varme-
last was conducted during the work to discuss the risks heat network operators face in delivering
ancillary services. CTR has experience with delivering ancillary services on two heat boilers that
the company owns. The interview was conducted with an operations manager at CTR and an
economist at Varmelast.
CTR owns two heat boilers with a nominal installed capacity of 40 MW and 80 MW. The units
are qualified to deliver FCR-N as the fastest service. However, it has been estimated that the
heat boiler can be approved to deliver FCR-D services if desired. Although the boilers can
meet the qualifications for delivering containment reserves, it was in the interview underlined
that operational challenges exist. Specifically, CTR has experienced problems with delivering
FCR-N services, which are frequently activated.
Despite the mean fluctuations for up- and
down-regulation averaging close to zero over time, the constant ramping can cause cold plugs,
which are sudden temperature drops in the district heating network. These sudden temperature
drops will cause the district heating customers to possibly not receive district heating water
for a certain period. These cold plugs have happened and are currently the main reason for
the limited provision of ancillary services from CTR. Thus, although full FCR-D activations
are rare, the district heating operators underline the significant responsibility for maintaining a
secure supply for their customers. This is equally important as the possible additional revenue
stream from selling ancillary services.
It was further identified that response time is a barrier to operating in the FCR-D market. The
Preliminaries
21
==PAGE:35==
difference in response time, 60 seconds for FCR-N and 7.5 seconds for FCR-D, is big. There
is a limit to how fast the system can respond to activation, as the system’s inertia is directly
related to the response-ability. This functions like the inertia of thermal power plants, which
act as a force opposing frequency deviation after an imbalance. In the district heating network,
the inertia of the hot water body similarly acts as a force that must be counteracted before an
activation can occur.
Another risk of altering baseline production profiles of district heating units is the effect on
network pressure that this can have. Increasing production at a point in the system will lower
pressure, and decreasing production will increase the pressure. The pressure drop will not only
be local but can be seen in larger parts of the system, and as the Greater Copenhagen grid has
complex hydraulics, a direct linear effect cannot be anticipated.
The interview further highlighted that heat boilers can only be activated for fast reserves if they
run in standby mode. For a 40 MW unit, even a small stand-by power can be expensive in
terms of operational costs. This is especially a problem in the symmetric FCR-N market, where
the heating boiler must provide a symmetric bid and thus be running at the same capacity as
has been bid to the market to be able to provide the FCR-N down-regulation. Additionally, the
heating market in Copenhagen is cleared based on lowering operational costs, and the revenues
from ancillary services are not considered. Therefore, the heat boilers can only provide up-
regulation if they are part of the least cost operation of the heating system. However, in the
FCR-D market, the boiler could operate in the FCR-D Down market by running at a low standby
power.
In connection with this thesis, real-life tests were conducted by a specialist in heat exchangers at
HOFOR, to examine the effect that altering the baseline power consumption of a heat exchanger
station will have on the district heating system pressure. It was found that changing pump levels
by 20% had an acceptable effect on the system regarding operational limits when running at a
relatively low spring load capacity. Currently, the control systems at the heat exchanger stations
are preset to respond slowly to signals to avoid system damage. However, this activation time
can be reprogrammed using the existing equipment to a faster response time. However, this is a
simplification of the physics regarding an activation, as the ability of the pumps to increase or
decrease their power is linked with the rate at which the change happens. If a faster ramping
is programmed, the system will react differently as the pressure is also related to the pressure
gradient of the district heating water.
Varmelast does not impose fines if a unit does not deliver the precise quantity sold in the heat
markets as they look at a unit’s total hourly production and consumption. Thus, if energy-poor
ancillary services are delivered, this will only be observed if an energy imbalance can be seen.
However, the pressure drops and energy imbalance can be measured locally. If they are suspected
of posing risks to the district heating network, this could lead to reprimands from Varmelast.
Similar to the electricity market, Varmelast works with both day-ahead and intraday auctions.
However, there are fewer intraday markets than electricity markets, and the market is cleared
by a least-cost objective. The market timeline of Varmelast’s day-ahead and intra-day auctions
can be seen in Appendix A.1.4.

and reserve shortfall. Revenue is calculated as the earned profit in the FCR-D markets minus
an activation penalty. The reserve shortfall measures how the capacity bids comply with the
P90 requirement. Specifically, reserve shortfall is calculated as the minutes where the capacity
bid exceeds the available flexibility capacity in the test set.
3.2
Bidding Strategy Optimization Problem Development
To bid electricity consumption from variable sources in the FCR-D Up and Down markets, a
market participant needs to create and provide Energinet with a forecast that, together with
real-time measured data, shows a reserve shortfall of 10%. The underlying methodology of the
forecast is entirely up to the market participant and can be made in numerous ways. This thesis
will employ a mathematical programming approach to the forecasting problem using a chance-
constrained program (CCP) formulation of the available capacity to bid in the market. This
branch of models makes it possible to model the constraints of a mathematical programming
problem at a given certainty level, meaning that some of the model’s constraints are allowed to
be violated. This makes it possible to model the P90 requirement.
Firstly, an analytical reformulation of the chance-constrained program will be derived, which
is one of the main contributions of this thesis. Following the analytical reformulation of the
chance-constrained program, three sample-based (SB) reformulations will be presented. The
first reformulation is an Exact reformulation, which uses stochastic programming and binary
variables to approximate the CCP. Following this, a relaxation of the binary variables of the
Exact reformulation will be used together with an ALSO-X algorithm to provide a less com-
putationally expensive reformulation of the Exact program. The algorithm uses an iterative
approach to find a feasible solution approximating the Exact formulation. Lastly, a Conditional
Value at Risk (CVaR) sample-based reformulation of the original problem will be presented.
In the presented models, the LER requirements described in Section 2.1.3, are omitted from
the formulations. However, all models could be extended to include additional constraints to
further model the additional LER requirements although more changes would need to be made
for Analytical reformulation.

reserve shortfall, and looking at an hour with minute resolution data, 6 minutes are allowed to
have a reserve shortfall. On the other hand, the capacity must be available for the remaining
54 minutes of the hour. The constraint does not look at the magnitude of reserve shortfall.
Although the chance-constrained program can model the risk level prescribed by Energinet,
there is no closed-form solution to the problem and the problem cannot be solved directly in a
programming language. Therefore, the chance-constrained problem must be reformulated into
a tractable formulation using sampling or be reformulated analytically.
The problem is solved hourly as the Energinet requirements do not allow the asset owner to shift
the risk level between hours. If this was allowed and the program was solved for 24 hours instead,
the model could “save” the reserve shortfall to the hours of the day with the highest uncertainty
in the available flexibility capacity. By keeping the model at an hourly time resolution, the risk
level is kept constant throughout the day.

inherent structure of CVaR limits the expected value of the worst-case realizations within the
uncertainty set, resulting in more conservative bids. This will limit the capacity bid of each hour
as the main purpose of the reformulation is to minimize the expected value of the magnitudes
of reserve shortfall at the 1 −ϵ tail of the distribution, which is the Value at Risk (VaR) part of
the distribution corresponding to ϵ.
However, the model can be valuable if a market actor has a low risk level. The CVaR refor-
mulation can further work as a formulation to compare the Analytical reformulation with. The
computational time of the model is also an advantage, as the model is a convex LP.

Using the historical data of each electric vehicle charging, it is possible to calculate the available
flexibility capacity for up- and down-regulation for each electric vehicle. Preprocessed data was
provided by Emil V. Damm and Gustav A. Lunde with calculated flexibility capacities of each
electric vehicle across the time period in minute resolution. Thus, the methodology regarding the
calculations of the available flexibility capacity follows their methodology [6, 11]. The maximum
charging rate for each EV is calculated as the historical maximum power output of the charge
box the EV is connected to measured across the time period.
The up-regulation flexibility capacity is directly the measured power output of the charge box
connected to the vehicle, as this is the power you would be able to decrease or shut down in the
event of an FCR-D Up reserve activation. The calculations for down-regulation are, however,
more complex. A charging session is initiated by the electric vehicle owner when the EV is
plugged into its charge box. It is assumed that the electric vehicles are at maximum charged to
a state of charge (SOC) of 90%. In the time period when the EV is plugged in and not charging
at its maximum charging rate, there is thus an available flexibility capacity in the downward
direction. Additionally, even when the EV has finished charging, the additional capacity between
the assumed 90% of SOC and a 100% SOC is an available flexibility capacity for FCR-D Down.
The equations used in their calculations are shown in Appendix B.3.
The aggregated portfolio in this project also includes historical minute-resolution data of the
power consumption at a heat exchanger station in Copenhagen, owned by HOFOR. The same
historical period was used for electric vehicles. There is a substantial power consumption asso-
ciated with the pumps circulating water at the heat exchanger station. However, the historical
power output must be converted into available flexibility capacity for each minute.
It was
assumed that it would not be realistic to turn off the pumps completely or run them at the
maximum power consumption measured across the period. The 20% change, relative to the
consumption of power observed in the real-life experiment, was within the operational limits.
Therefore, it is assumed that as instability was not yet reached at 20%, a 30% possible ramp-up
and ramp-down of the pumps was assumed to also be feasible and used in the modeling of this
project.
The available flexibility was calculated as the historical electricity consumption multiplied by
30% in both directions. Additionally, the flexibility calculation takes into account the maximum
measured power consumption and the maximum measured ramp rate of the HES.
For example, if the historical consumption multiplied by 0.3 was higher than the maximum
historical differences observed between minutes when the consumption increased, denoted as the
maximum ramp rate, this value was used as the available flexibility for FCR-D Up. Similarly,
if the historical consumption multiplied 0.3 is larger than the maximum difference observed
between minutes when consumption went down, this ramp rate was used instead. The maximum
historical consumption value was used as an upper threshold for the electricity consumption of
the pumps located at the HES. This means that if the minute-level electricity consumption plus
30% exceeds the maximum historical consumption, the flexibility capacity will be set to the
difference between the maximum value minus and the consumption in that given minute. No
lower limit for down-regulation was assumed as even a low power consumption multiplied by 0.3
will not be 0. The equations used for these calculations can be seen in Appendix B.4.

3.3.3
Validation Process
Cross-Validation
Figure 3.11 presents a graphical illustration of the cross-validation technique used in the project.
A k-fold cross-validation using shuffled splits was used. The general concept of a k-fold cross-
validation (CV) involves splitting a data set into k parts and running the same model k times,
each time using one part of the data as an out-of-sample test set and the remaining k−1 parts as
the in-sample (IS) training data set. In this way, all data can be used both as IS and OOS data,
which is useful for data sets where one does not have enough data to split the data into separate
training and testing sets. Furthermore, cross-validation has the advantage that the framework
is tested multiple times on different datasets, making it possible to assess the volatility in results
across folds.
In cross-validation, all data is used once as testing data and k −1 times as training data in the
remaining folds. In this project specifically, a shuffled split was used to split the days of the
year into different folds. In a shuffled split, days are randomly selected from the sample when
creating the test set. The days picked for testing in one fold are 1/k days of the year, while the
training data is the remaining (k −1)/k days. The shuffled split was chosen because seasonality
effects were observed in the flexibility capacity data. In a traditional k-fold cross-validation,
the framework would use, e.g., the two first-thirds of a year’s data as input, and the latter
Methodology
41
==PAGE:55==
Figure 3.11: Shuffled split k-fold cross-validation [48].
third of the year would be used for testing. If seasonality exists, the framework will perform
badly in the OOS testing as it has not seen data from the given season tested. This problem is
mitigated using shuffled splits, as data from all seasons will be present in the training set. In
the modeling, a 3-fold cross-validation was used to ensure a large enough sample size for the
sample-based approaches as well as a high enough k to evaluate volatility in results across folds
and mitigate long computational times.
Key Performance Indicators
Two key performance indicators will be used in the validation process to assess the performance
of the different optimization problems. The first KPI is the revenue earned in FCR-D Up and
Down markets using the optimal hourly bids from the optimization models. The revenue is
calculated as the profit earned in the market minus a possible activation shortfall penalty.
Revenue is calculated as the difference between the profit and activating shortfall penalty for
every hour, summing the hourly revenues across all days and folds to obtain the one-year revenue.
The activation penalty cost was set to 5 times the FCR-D price in each hour to ensure a high
enough penalty to represent the likely cost that Energinet will face for missed activation. This
is similar to the penalty cost assumed in [6, 11].
The reserve shortfall in percentage is calculated as the minutes where the realized flexibility
capacity is below the capacity bid out of the total minutes. This was calculated for each fold
individually and for the entire scoped time. The detailed calculations for the revenue and reserve
shortfall can be found in Appendix B.9.

The in-sample reserve shortfalls indicate that the Analytical method has a reserve shortfall
greater than 10% IS for the HES, which is non-ideal and a limitation of the method. Such
significant differences in the IS reserve shortfalls of the Analytical method occur because the
function applied to the in-sample data is a poor fit and the 10th percentile of the cumulative
distribution function will therefore not accurately represent the underlying distribution.

bution with the fitted function can still result in significant errors in the tails of the distributions.
This can be represented by plotting Quantile-Quantile (Q-Q) plots of the expected vs. observed
percentiles of the fitted function and data input. The largest differences between the theoretical
and observed percentiles are seen at the tails, as seen in Appendix B.2. The method assumes
data is well-distributed and is subject to errors if this assumption is not met. However, the Ana-
lytical method shows reserve shortfalls under the 10% margin for the other portfolios, indicating

The above results showed that the novel Analytical reformulation of the chance-constrained pro-
gram performs better than the CVaR method in terms of revenue earned in the markets. How-
ever, the method was not robust regarding the found OOS reserve shortfalls. This can directly
be linked to how well the fits describe the underlying distribution, and several improvements
can be made. This includes tail fitting or tail-weighted fits, which focus on better fitting the
hourly observed values at only the tail of the distribution, which is the area of interest. Instead
of improving the fitting method, clustering techniques or conditionality can be used to find
distributions within the total data set that better resemble known distributions.
The primary advantage of the Analytical reformulation is its low computational time.
The
computational time of each optimization method is shown in Appendix C.2 for 3-fold, 5-fold,
and 7-fold CV. It can be seen that the Analytical method has the lowest computational times.
Additionally, the Analytical reformulation offers transparency, as it is straightforward to extract
and plot the fitted distribution. In contrast, it is diﬀicult to visualize the workings of the Exact
and ALSO-X reformulations.
The CVaR reformulation has a significant disadvantage in its low revenues but could be a viable
option for risk-averse aggregators. The method offers the advantage of lower variability in the
OOS reserve shortfall percentages. This means that although it does not fully exploit the allowed
shortfall within the P90 requirement, it ensures that there is no risk of overbidding. Additionally,
the CVaR method demonstrated the highest synergy effects, indicating that aggregation makes
it possible to significantly increase bid size while maintaining a low CVaR.
The Exact and ALSO-X methods were the best-performing methods in terms of revenues and
reserve shortfalls. In this case study, where the underlying uncertainty distribution did not fit
well with the fitted probability function, these two models should be preferred. However, as
the ALSO-X relaxation approximates the Exact reformulation regarding the obtained bids and
has significantly lower computational times, this method is preferred over the Exact method.
Nevertheless, the computational time of the ALSO-X method increases substantially with larger
problem sizes. Therefore, if a higher number of folds or a longer time period of data is obtained,
the problem size could be reduced through some form of scenario selection procedure.

Conclusions and Future Works
This thesis investigated the possible synergy effect in the FCR-D market of aggregating a port-
folio of electricity-consuming units consisting of electric vehicles (EVs) and a heat exchanger
station (HES) connected to the Copenhagen district heating network.
In a case study, the
aggregated portfolio’s calculated flexibility capacity was used to model the optimal bidding
strategy for the flexibility capacity to bid to the Frequency Containment Reserve for Distur-
bances (FCR-D) market, an ancillary service of the Nordic synchronous area, under the P90
requirement issued by the Danish transmission system operator, Energinet, for variable produc-
tion and consumption. The P90 requirement issues the asset operator to make bids with a 90%
delivery probability, thus allowing for a 10% reserve shortfall.
The optimal bidding strategies were found using four stochastic optimization programs, which
are all reformulations of an original chance-constrained program (CCP), allowing the capacity
bid to exceed the available flexibility capacity 10% of the time. The Analytical reformulation
is a linear program that uses the inverse cumulative function of the underlying probability dis-
tribution of flexibility to approximate the underlying distribution.
The Exact sample-based
(SB) reformulation is a mixed integer linear program reformulation of the chance-constrained
program that uses samples from the underlying flexibility capacity distribution to approximate
the distribution. The ALSO-X method applies an algorithm that approximates the Exact pro-
gram formulation by iteratively solving a linear relaxation of the Exact program. Lastly, the
Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR) reformulation, similar to the previous two reformulations,
uses samples from the underlying distribution to approximate the distribution. However, this
program minimizes the expected value of the 10% worst-case realizations of the reserve shortfall
distribution.
Using the four optimization programs, optimal bidding strategies were found and evaluated using
a 3-fold cross-validation (CV) and not applying conditionality in bids, meaning the capacity bids
remain the same across the days in each testing set. The revenue of the capacity bids across
the one year showed a 13-18% synergy effect of aggregating the flexibility of the EVs and the
HES. When bidding on the electric vehicles and HES alone, revenue of 3.7 mDKK and 0.23
mDKK, respectively, was found using the best-performing model. The Analytical reformulation
experienced higher reserve shortfalls than the allowed 10% under the P90 requirement if the
fitted inverse cumulative function did not represent the underlying distribution well, which
happens for the HES. The remaining optimization methods showed an average out-of-sample
reserve shortfall of 10%, or less in the case of the CVaR program, thus complying with the P90
requirement.
It was shown that, although the flexibility capacity of the heat exchanger station was modest,
a significant increase in revenue was observed in the aggregated portfolio. The synergy effect
was caused by the flexibility data of the EVs and HES being uncorrelated, having different daily
profiles, and creating more well-distributed probability functions.

5.1
Future Works
While the results showed that aggregating the two technologies creates a synergy effect, multiple
questions remain unanswered regarding the implementation of such an aggregated portfolio in
real life. This thesis took the perspective of the aggregator; however, the asset owner’s perspec-
tive should also be covered in future works. Firstly, the question of how the additional revenue
from aggregation should be divided between the different asset owners should be answered. The
question is not trivial, as each asset contributes equally to the synergy effect but not equally to
installed capacity and available flexibility capacity. Furthermore, the aggregator will also need
to be paid for its service. Thus, new business models must be developed to distribute revenues.
For electric vehicles, providing flexibility to the FCR-D market carries low risks, as owners will
not be significantly affected by slight deviations in charging time. However, providing flexibility
from assets connected to district heating networks is more challenging, as reserve activations
will impact the district heating networks. Therefore, further research is needed to assess the
cascading effects of providing flexibility in district heating networks.
This includes assessing how to mitigate pressure disturbances and the cold plug phenomenon in
district heating networks. Conducting more tests on the heat exchanger station to observe its
reaction to higher deviations from the baseline is crucial. The control system of the HES would
also need to be reprogrammed to achieve a faster response than the currently implemented
response time. Furthermore, the tests should include evaluations during both low and peak
consumption periods, preferably in different seasons.
The operators of the district heating network in Copenhagen highlighted that the water body’s
inertia is an obstacle in delivering FCR-D. The inertia acts as a force, making it hard for the
pumps to move the water faster even though the pumping speed increases. Conversely, it can
be argued that this inertia, similar to inertia in electricity systems, will work as a buffer for
providing up-regulation from the heat exchanger station. This should be investigated in future
works.
This assessment should be part of the project’s business case development. On the HES side,
the leading investment costs would be a frequency measurement system and changing the IT
systems so that the pumps respond fast enough to an activation. As HOFOR already provides
ancillary services on other assets, IT systems from these assets might be able to be reused for
the new project. The possible costs of component breakdown from activation should be assessed
together with the cost of not delivering heat to district heating customers.
Furthermore, a projection of FCR-D prices will need to be made. If the project’s payback time is
not short, which could be the case as the revenue is relatively modest for the HES, any changes in
mean FCR-D prices can affect the business case. As shown, FCR-D prices are volatile, and prices
skyrocketed as recently as April 2024. Thus, such projections are diﬀicult to make. However, as
FCR-D volumes are not projected to increase significantly while prequalified capacities increase,
prices could indeed decrease due to higher liquidity.
Another topic to include in future works and as part of a business case is the alternative of
selling flexibility in the intra-day market as the market has seen substantial growth in traded
volumes and should thus be a topic of interest [50]. Similarly, providing other flexibility services
such as voltage regulation, congestion management or power quality services could be of interest,
especially for the EVs. Other ancillary service markets could also be investigated.
The report showed that currently, heat boilers contribute to the flexibility markets in terms of
prequalified quantities. A topic for future work is to further investigate the role of this technology
in the Danish ancillary service markets, where they are dominant in multiple markets. There
Conclusions and Future Works

==PAGE:66==
might still be unused potential for providing flexibility from heat boilers, and the experiences
of district heating companies could be leveraged to introduce large-scale heat pumps to the
ancillary service markets as well.
A more minor topic for future work includes identifying patterns in reserve and activation
shortfalls.
Possible mitigation methods can be imposed if there are patterns in the FCR-D
activations or the observed reserve shortfalls of the case study.
Lastly, many possibilities exist to create a better methodology for producing probabilistic fore-
casts. Improving the applied chance-constrained programs is a first stepping stone. Furthermore,
introducing conditionality in bids could also increase bid size. Furthermore, the inclusion of lim-
ited energy reservoir (LER) requirements in the Analytical reformulation should be investigated.
Applying other fields of probabilistic forecasting, such as machine learning or time-series analysis
models, could be an interesting area of future research.
5.2
Perspectives
The integration of heating assets into the FCR-D services presents both opportunities and
challenges.
This section explores the broader implications of the case study results for the
coupling of electricity and heating markets.
Although it might be challenging to deliver FCR-D services on heat exchanger systems, one
could still argue that part of the results found in this case study can be extended in the sense
that the HES could be seen as a representative of other heating assets such as heat pumps at
residential houses. The heat exchanger station’s electricity consumption represents the heat
consumption of many district heating customers as the HES is connected to the transmission
grid. Thus, although barriers exist to implementing ancillary services on the exact heat ex-
changer station, the case study’s results can be used to argue that aggregating electric vehicles
and electricity-consuming heating units will show a synergy effect as electric vehicle charging
and heat consumption patterns are shifted from each other in their peaks.
Furthermore, using the heat exchanger station as a pilot project to provide ancillary services on
heating assets in HOFOR can lay the foundation for future projects. Gaining real experience
with delivering ancillary services on heating assets is crucial, especially with the company’s
ambition to build 550 MW heat boilers and 300 MW heat pumps [51].
The importance of providing ancillary services from all electrified sectors should be highlighted.
The district heating sector’s transition away from fossil- and biomass-based production is through
electrification, and with installed capacities in the 40-80 MW scale, it can be argued that the
district heating companies should see providing flexibility more as a duty, coming with the in-
creased electricity consumption, than a risk in not being able to provide heat. Additionally,
utility companies could become a central factor in flexibility markets as they are playing on the
“right” team as their main focus is customer utility, not sheer profit. This poses an even bigger
argument for utility companies to maintain the stability of the grid and have a holistic view of
the consumer’s interests.
A further argument for providing non-symmetric ancillary services from heating units is that
the market’s nature could have a carbon reduction potential compared to delivering symmetric
services. Instead of stalling wind turbines if they cause over-frequencies, electricity consumption
can be increased, thus allowing no production to be shut down. Similarly, for up-regulation,
demand-side flexibility provides the advantage of not increasing production from supply-side
generators that might have higher CO2 emissions than the production they will replace. This
argument is, however, mostly valid for energy-heavy products.
Conclusions and Future Works
53
==PAGE:67==
In conclusion, the findings from this thesis emphasize the potential benefits of aggregating various
electricity-consuming units and underscore the strategic importance of including heating assets in
the ancillary service markets. As the energy sector continues to evolve, harnessing the flexibility
of electrified sectors will be key to building a sustainable and resilient energy system.

A.1
District Heating Technologies
A.1.1
Heat Pumps
A heat pump is a possible asset in district heating networks with significant electricity consump-
tion. These units transfer heat from a heat source to a heat sink. The heat source can be air,
seawater, wastewater, drinking water, or waste heat from industrial processes. In this context,
the heat sink is the district heating water. Heat pumps operate cyclically, requiring electricity
for various components during different cycle stages [52].
Heat pumps utilize the thermodynamic properties of refrigerant fluids. In modern heat pumps,
the refrigerants commonly used are carbon dioxide (CO￿), ammonia (NH￿), or modern hyrdoflu-
oroolefins (HFOs) like R-1234yf, R-1233zd which have lower greenhouse gas emission potentials
[53, 54, 55, 52].
Refrigerants have a low boiling point, and a heat pump exploits the phase change of these fluids
to store and transfer energy. The heat pump operates using a four-step cycle, illustrated in
Figure A.1. Initially, the refrigerant passes through an evaporator, which absorbs heat from
the heat source, causing the refrigerant to transition from liquid to gas. Next, the refrigerant
moves through a compressor, which compresses the gas, raising its pressure and temperature.
The high-pressure, high-temperature gas then flows to a condenser, releasing heat to the heat
sink and condensing back into a liquid. Finally, the refrigerant goes through an expansion valve,
reducing its pressure and cooling it down, making it ready to absorb heat again. The majority
of the electricity consumed by the heat pump is used during the compression stage [52].
In terms of eﬀiciency, heat pumps are highly effective, with eﬀiciencies above 100% when mea-
sured by the coeﬀicient of performance (COP), which compares the heat output to the electrical
energy input. The COP for modern heat pumps typically ranges from 4 to 6 [53]. This means
that in terms of installed electric consumption capacity, heat pumps can operate with smaller
capacities, as 40 MW of heat corresponds to approximately 10 MW of electric consumption.
Regarding ancillary services, heat pumps have relatively low operating costs since they can
transfer more energy from the heat source than the electricity input needed. Therefore, they
should operate for a substantial number of hours throughout the year, providing up-regulation
during these hours. However, due to the complexity of their components, some existing heat
pumps may encounter issues with the ramp rates required for providing FCR-D [56].
The
investment costs of heat pumps are relatively high [39]. As of 2023, 499 MW of heat pumps
were installed in Denmark measures in heat capacity. This covers 159 installed units, mainly
air-to-heating types [39].

A.1.2
Heat Boilers
Heat boilers are large hot water ”kettles” typically built with capacities ranging from 40 to 80
MW in electric capacity (at 80 MW, two 40 MW boilers are built in series) [52].
These boilers heat water by passing a substantial electrical resistance through water with a
specific salt balance, creating heat in the electrolyte water.
This heat is transferred to the
district heating network via a heat exchanger. The district heating water is continuously pumped
through the heat exchanger in a separate stream to the heating boiler [52]. A representation
of a heat boiler is depicted in Figure A.2. The technology is relatively simple, comprising few
components. Heat boilers can ramp from 0% to 100% within 30 seconds, making them suitable
for both slow ancillary services due to their capacity and fast services due to rapid ramp rates
[52].
Heat boilers are primarily used for peak load as they directly use electricity with an eﬀiciency of
around 99%, leading to operational costs directly dependent on the electricity price [52]. As heat
demand is the highest in hours with high electricity prices this can limit the use of electric boilers
for base load. However, the 1700 MW installed heat capacity of electric boilers in Denmark in
2023 delivered 7000 TJ of energy [39].

Varmelast operates with a day-ahead and intra-day market similar to the electricity market.
The separate timelines for the gate closure times, heat demand publications, and markets clear-
ing publications are shown in Figure A.4. The day-ahead market starts with a heat demand
publication at 7:45 with the first GCT at 8:30, where the heat production entities give bids with
different combinations of production quantities and prices [58]. Based on the bids, Varmelast
sends a heat procurement for the total day to the CHP plants, which 9:30 sends their preferred
operating schedule back to Varmelast. At 10.30, Varmelast will publish the final heat production
schedule. On the day of operation, five intra-day bids and clearings exist to update the scheduled
plan to match the updated demand forecast, dispatch units according to the electricity price,
and balance imbalances from unit failures. The objective of the Varmelast market is to minimize
the overall operating costs for the system based on the operating costs given by the production
companies.
