Abstract 
This report investigates the possibility of using plants for phytoremediation or to facilitate 
degradation of pollutants in land deposited harbour sediment from Esbjerg harbour. The sediment is 
mainly polluted with tributyltin (TBT), and to a lesser degree, heavy metals and polycyclic aromatic 
hydrocarbons (PAHs), but it also contains high concentration of salt. Therefore, the report also 
evaluates the effects from the salt concentration, and how it develops. 
 
The sediment was characterised in terms of water content, pH, content of organic carbon, 
concentration of selected nutrients (N, Ca, Na, K and P) and the hydraulic conductivities. The 
sediments had high water contents (around 65 %), were slightly alkaline (pH: 7 – 8), and contained 
almost no organic carbon (< 4 %). The concentrations of nutrients were high and the hydraulic 
conductivities (10–7 – 10–8 m/s) corresponded well with the sediment mainly consisting of silt. 
 
Furthermore, the contamination in the sediment was determined by analysing for selected heavy 
metals (As, Cd, Cr, Cu, Ni, Pb and Zn), selected polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), 
tributyltin (TBT) and its degradation products dibutyltin (DBT) and monobutyltin (MBT). Based on 
the analyses it was found that the sediment mainly was polluted with TBT and Cu. The 
concentrations of the other heavy metals were very low and the concentrations of PAHs were 
almost not detectable. The plant effects on the concentration of PAHs were therefore not clear. 
 
Based on germination and growth tests it was found that the plants germinated well in sediment that 
had been on land for three years, while almost nothing germinated in the fresh sediment, except for 
the halophyte common glasswort (Salicornia europaea). Rapeseed and rutabaga (both Brassica 
napus) showed the best germination and survival, while tall fescue (Festuca arundinace var. 
Tower) was found to grow the best. 
 
Generally, the plant uptake of heavy metals was limited, but heavy metals with biological uses (e.g. 
Zn, Ni and Cu) were taken up better, and barley took up heavy metals the best, especially As, Pb, 
Cu and Zn. The plant uptake indicated that it was more related to the age of the plants rather than 
the concentration in the sediment. Also, the age of plants seemed to have an impact on the 
degradation of TBT into DBT and MBT, since tendencies indicated that: the elder the plant, the 
greater the degradation. This was correlated to the elder plants having more developed root systems 
that allowed the conditions in the sediment to be more aerobic (facilitating degradation) 
 
The salt content was concluded to influence the germination and growth results the most, much 
more than the pollutants. In the fresh sediment, the salt concentration in the pore water (16 g/L) and 
sediment (12 g/L) were found to be above the toxic level (1 g/L). The concentration in the already 
deposited sediment was lower (0.99 g/L and 2.62 g/L). The uptake of chloride was, in most cases, 
higher than Na, and greatest in the youngest plants (10 – 20 % removal), while the uptake was 
lower in elder plants (< 10 %). Barley had a better uptake of chloride (above average) than wild 
cabbage (Brassical oleracea ssp. oleracea) and kale (Brassical oleracea acephala var. sabellica). 
 
The development of the salt concentration was modelled in five layers according to two scenarios: 
1) sediment is dumped at once; 2) sediment is dumped over five years. It was found that the time it 
took to reach a salt concentration < 1 g/L was 3 years in the top layer and more than 9 years in the 
bottom layer if the sediment was dumped at once, while the times were 8 and 14 years, respectively, 
if the sediment was dumped over five years. It is therefore recommended that the confined disposal 
facility is divided into several sections, so the final height of sediment is reached at once.

1 Background 
1.1 Port of Esbjerg 
The harbour in Esbjerg was handed over to the Danish state in 1878 [1]. The construction of the 
port of Esbjerg was a result of the loss of the port of Altona1, which until 1864 was an important 
port in the Danish Kingdom. But due to the Second Schleswig War, where Denmark lost the two 
duchies, Schleswig and Holstein, Altona was lost. A new harbour towards west was therefore 
needed, and in 1868 it was decided by law to construct the new harbour at Esbjerg [2]  
 
The port of Esbjerg has included a variety of industries. Earlier the fishery industry in Esbjerg was 
quite large – in 1910 Esbjerg had the largest fishery port in Denmark. But now the fishery industry 
is very limited. However, other industries like the oil and gas production in the North Sea emerged 
up through the 1970s and Esbjerg became, and still is, a centre for the offshore industries. Around 
2000 the offshore wind industry expanded and led to the construction of the offshore wind turbine 
park at Horns Rev with the port of Esbjerg as the centre of discharging the offshore wind turbines 
[3]. 
 
Furthermore, ferries carrying both cargo and passengers between England and Denmark have also 
gone out to sea from Esbjerg. From 1875 until 2014 there was a ferry between Harwich, UK and 
Esbjerg, where passengers travelled to England. Now, only cargo is transported by ship between 
Esbjerg and Immingham, England [4]. The port also includes a little passenger ferry that sails from 
Esbjerg to the summerhouse island Fanø [5]. 
 
In 2015 the port of Esbjerg had 6432 ship arrivals (excluding the Fanø ferry arrivals), 114 calls of 
heavy lift vessels, 272 offshore wind turbines shipped and 4226 tonnes of goods handled [6]. The 
handled cargo mainly involved so called RoRo2 (40 %) and Bulk (26 %) [6]. This makes the port of 
Esbjerg the biggest Danish port in the North Sea. It consists of several harbour sections covering a 
total area of 450 ha and a quay length of 12 km [7]. 
1.2 Sediment and pollution in Esbjerg harbour 
The location of the port of Esbjerg towards the North Sea is not without problems. The top 
sediment of the North Sea consists of silt and clay, which can easily be re-suspended and 
transported [8]. This means that the sediment at the German Bight is transported north along the 
coast of Jutland by the Jutland current. Furthermore, the area of Esbjerg and south of Esbjerg is 
influenced by tidal streams3, which retard the sediment transport due to their perpendicular streams 
with respect to the Jutland current. These transport conditions allow the sediment to settle e.g. in the 
harbour area and the ship channels, thereby decreasing the water depth [9]. The water depth is 
already small, and removal of the sediments is needed in order to maintain the function of the 
harbour [8]. 
 
To complicate it even more, the sediment is most likely polluted, mainly from the North Sea4, 
especially with TBT, and to a lesser degree, with PAHs and heavy metals [8] [10]. Besides 
pollution caused by atmospheric deposition, sediment from the German Bight is affected primarily 
by the discharge of the river Elbe [8] [11] [12]. The polluted sediment is then transported from the

1.3 Thesis statement 
According to [13] the Danish Coastal Authority5 dredge 500,000 m3 harbour sediment each year, 
where 90 % of this is dumped in the North Sea. The remaining 10 % is too polluted with heavy 
metals, PAH’s and especially TBT to be dumped at sea. This sediment is therefore deposited on 
land. Ultimo 2014, an agreement between the Danish State, Esbjerg Municipality and the Port of 
Esbjerg was reached with NIRAS as consultant [16]. The goal of this agreement is to remove and 
clean up the harbour sediment. Part of this agreement means that sediment from the harbour Basin 6 
is going to be deposited on land due to a high TBT concentration. In contrast, 200,000 m3 of 
sediment from Basin 1, 2 and 5, the front harbour to Basin 1 and 2 and “bedding-area” is under 
evaluation to be dumped at a new dumping area in the North Sea [17]. 
 
This report will focus on the polluted sediment that is planned to be deposited on land and 
investigate the possibility of using plants for remediation and/or facilitating degradation of the 
pollution. Since, the sediment originates from the harbour, and the salinity of the North Sea is 
approximately 34-35 g/L [8], the report will also investigate the role of salt with respect to the 
plants. This has led to the following thesis statements: 
 
 Which plants, if any, can grow in the harbour sediment? 
 What effect do the plants have on the TBT and PAH concentration in the soil? 
 Do the plants take up salt and heavy metals from the soil? 
 How will the salt concentration in the soil change over time?

2 Theoretical introduction 
2.1 Heavy metals 
Heavy metals can be defined in different ways, but one of the most common definitions is that 
heavy metals have densities greater than 5 g/cm3 [18][19]. They are elements and will therefore 
naturally occur in the environment and are not only a result of human pollution. The natural 
concentrations of heavy metals in soil are relatively low and are defined as the background level. 
Based on an investigation during the 1990s6 the background level of heavy metals in Danish soil 
was mapped [18]. The concentrations of heavy metals can still be higher than the background level 
and yet not pose a risk towards human health. The soil criterion defines the level at which 
concentrations are evaluated to pose a risk towards human health [20]. Densities, the Danish 
background levels and the Danish soil quality criterions for selected heavy metals are presented in

An investigation of the background level in marine waters has not been evaluated in the same way 
as for terrestrial land, since the background levels in marine sediments strongly depend on the local 
geological and sedimentation conditions [18] [22]. So when sediments are removed e.g. by 
dredging, the concentrations in the sediment are compared with action levels in order to determine 
the fate of the dredged material (deposition on land or dumping at sea). There are two action levels: 
a lower and an upper action level. Concentrations below the lower action level are not considered to 
cause any effects, and the sediment can in this case readily be dumped at sea [22]. Concentrations 
above the lower limit indicate that the concentrations might cause effects. Sediments having 
concentrations between the two levels might be dumped at sea, but an assessment of the sediment, 
the dumping spot and alternative ways of disposal have to be performed. If the concentrations are 
above the upper action level, the sediment must be deposited on land [22]. Table 1 provides

2.1.1 Pollution in the North Sea 
The awareness of the pollution with nutrients and contaminants in the North Sea emerged up 
through the 1960s [23]. This consciousness resulted in the Oslo convention7 in 1972 and the Paris 
convention in 19748, which lead to the OSPAR convention9 in 1992 [9] [24]. Due to the OSPAR 
convention a monitoring program for the North Sea was established in order to evaluate the 
condition of the North Sea with respect to certain pollutants. The OSPAR commission identified 
Mercury (Hg), Cadmium (Cd) and Lead (Pb) as priority substances. This means that extra efforts 
have been put into monitoring and reducing the emissions of these heavy metals to the North Sea 
[23]. 
 
The pollution with heavy metals in the North Sea comes from three main pathways: atmospheric 
deposition, direct inputs (sewages and industrial effluents) and riverine discharges. The dominant 
pathway is the riverine discharges e.g. from the Elbe River and only to a minor degree from direct 
sources [23]. However, from 2005 and forward, the dominant input of Cu mainly comes from direct 
discharges, since Cu is used as an antifouling biocide on ships and marine equipment (e.g. cages) 
[23]. Even though the atmospheric deposition is not as great as the water pathways, the contribution 
is still important, since e.g. Hg and Cd from atmospheric deposition to the North Sea is 
approximately 20 % and 25 %, respectively, and for Pb it is around 35 % [12]. 
 
For all the pathways the inputs of Cd, Cu, Hg and Pb declined significantly during the period from 
1990 – 2006 with the largest decline rate during the 1990s [12] [23]. During this period, the riverine 
inputs decreased with 20 % for Cd and 75 % for Pb, while the reduction of Cu and Hg was 40 %. 
The emissions to the atmosphere of Pb also fell with 86%, and for Cd and Hg the emissions were 
reduced by 42 % and 30 %, respectively [12]. The reduction in riverine inputs was a result of better 
cleaning technologies used by the industries and the waste water treatment plants, which facilitated 
cleaner effluents. The atmospheric deposition reduction was mainly caused by phasing out of lead 
in petrol and, again, cleaner production methods during the 1990s [12] [18] [25]. 
 
In 2006, the riverine and direct discharges of Hg were down to around 8 tonnes and for Cd around 
20 tonnes, but still quite high for Pb and Cu – approximately 700 tonnes and 1000 tonnes, 
respectively [23]. In 2013, the concentrations in precipitation at the Esbjerg area were < 0.2 µg/L 
for Cd, Arsenic (As) and Chrom (Cr), whereas the concentration of Hg was below 7 ng/L. The 
concentrations of Pb and Copper (Cu) were a little bit higher (< 1.2 µg Pb/L and < 3 µg Cu/L) [25]. 
The dominant source of Cd, Pb and Hg to the atmospheric deposition now comes from combustions 
in power plants and other industrial processes [12]. 
2.1.2 Heavy metals in sediments 
The reduction in emission and input to the North Sea has also had a declining effect on the 
concentration in the sediment and biota found in the North Sea [10]. But the concentrations in 
sediments are still so high at the German Bight and along the southern part of the west coast of 
Jutland, that they pose a risk of chronical effects in marine species for Cd, Pb and Hg [10].

The concentration of heavy metals in the sediment will continue to be high for many years ahead, 
since sediments act as sinks where heavy metals can be bound (sorbed10) and slowly be released 
again [26]. However, the binding potential of the heavy metals strongly depends on the texture of 
the sediment. Sediment fractions, such as clay particles and organic matter, both have large negative 
surfaces where ion exchanges can occur and facilitate bonding of heavy metals [18]. Due to the 
negatively charged surface, especially positive divalent metals (e.g. Pb, Cd, Hg, Ni, Cu, Zn) bind 
strongly to the sediment, but are at the same time competing for the binding spots with other 
cations, such as calcium (Ca), sodium (Na), Potassium (K) [18][27]. Elements such as As and 
Cr(IV) will primarily appear as anions in the environment, and will therefore be less strongly bound 
to the sediment (fewer positive binding positions than negative ones) [27]. Furthermore, heavy 
metals can also be retained in the soil by forming complexes e.g. with sulphides, carbonates or with 
humic acids (hydroxyl or carboxyl groups), which are poorly soluble [18]. Precipitation only occurs 
at relatively high concentration in contrast to complexation and sorption, which occurs at all 
concentrations [18] [27]. Likewise, pH plays an important role, since decreasing pH values increase 
the mobility of most heavy metals due to the competition of the binding spots with H+ (except for 
As due to its negative charge). Increasing salinity will increase the organic matter’s tendency to 
flocculate and thereby release some of the bound heavy metals from the sediment. But an increase 
in salinity also means that the concentration of chloride (Cl–) rise and heavy metals such as Cd that 
have a high affinity for Cl– will therefore be dissolved from the sediment and form CdCl2 [18]. 
2.1.3 Heavy metals in plants and their toxicity 
Plants can take up heavy metals on leaves and stems deposited from the atmosphere or dissolved in 
the water phase in the ground through plant roots. Some of these heavy metals are essential for plant 
growth and called micronutrients e.g. Cu, Cr, Ni, and Zn [18] [28]. If the concentrations of these 
metals are below the essential level in the cell it will lead to deficiency symptoms, but at the same 
time, too high concentrations will have toxic effects11 on the plants [28]. Some heavy metals do not 
have a biological use and are toxic towards the plant e.g. Pb and Hg. However, the tolerance level 
depends on the plant species [28]. As can be seen from Table 2 the toxicity in the leaves of selected 
heavy metals differs, but Cr is the most toxic heavy metal followed by Cd, whereas Zn is far less 
toxic. It has been found that the concentrations of heavy metals in plants are greatest in: roots > 
leaves > shoot and stem > fruit and the concentration is also highest in older leaves than in younger 
ones [29].

The toxicity of heavy metals depends on the fraction that is bioavailable12 causing the toxicity of 
most heavy metals to be greater in sandy sediments than in clayed sediments. Also the pH plays an 
important role, since the toxicity of some heavy metals e.g. Pb increase at decreasing pH [18]. The 
salinity also plays a role, since the complexation with chloride increase the molecule size and 
thereby reduces the uptake of heavy metals across membranes. 
 
Heavy metals can be toxic towards plants, animals and humans, since many of them bond with 
thiols13, which are used by many enzymes in their structure. So when heavy metals have been 
bound to the thiols, they cause changes in the structure, which can inhibit or change the function of 
the enzyme leading to severe consequences on the organism’s ability to function [18]. Some metals 
in enzymes can also be exchanged with other metals e.g. heavy metals and thereby decrease the 
activity of an enzyme. 
 
In aquatic environments, heavy metals are mostly accumulated in the first step of the food chain, 
where it is not unusual to observe bioconcentration factors (BCF) above 20000 in mussels and 
oysters [18]. Even though higher animals are taking up heavy metals, the discretion of these in 
higher animals is relatively well-developed and do not pose a significant risk for biomagnification 
(except for Hg). [18]. But that does not mean that heavy metals are not dangerous. Most heavy 
metals are still toxic towards animals and humans at relatively low concentrations and causes 
different effects from allergic reactions (e.g. by Ni) to more severe effects such as skin deceases 
(e.g. “Black Foot Decease” caused by As) and blood deceases (e.g. lack of formation of 
haemoglobin and red blood cells caused by Pb) [31] [32] [33]. Many heavy metals are also 
considered to be carcinogenic (e.g. Pb) or teratogenic (e.g. Cd) and might cause reproductive

2.2 TBT 
Tributyltin (TBT) is a group of organotin compounds, which all have in common that they consist 
of a tetravalent tin atom (Sn(IV)) connected to three butyl-groups (–C4H9). The general formula of 
TBT is (n-C4H9)3 Sn-X, where X is an anion (e.g. O2– and Cl–) [39]. The structures of selected TBT 
compounds are displayed in Figure 1. The anion has a great impact on the physical, chemical and 
toxicological properties of the TBT compound [40]. Even though there are many different 
tributyltin compounds, data only exists for a few of them, and mainly on TBT oxide (TBTO). In 
seawater, TBT is mainly present as TBT hydroxide (TBTOH), TBT chloride (TBTCl) and TBT 
carbonate (TBTCO3), where the mutual distribution depends on the pH14 [41] [42]. 
 
The primarily use of TBT was as a biocide in antifouling marine paints, where tributyltin oxide was 
used as the active ingredient [42]. Other organotin compounds (mono- and di- substituted 
organotin) are widely used in other industrial processes e.g. as stabilisers in polyvinyl chloride 
(PVC) production. The production of tributyltin in 1996 was, compared to the total organotin 
production, relatively small (only 15 – 20 %) [43], and is not expected to have increased due to the 
ban of TBT use as antifouling biocide.

2.2.1 Physical and chemical properties of TBT 
Table 3 provides information on selected physical-chemical properties of TBTO and TBTCl, where

2.2.2 Degradation of TBT 
TBT can be degraded under different abiotic and biotic conditions by breakage of the bonds 
between the tin atom and the butyl-groups (debutylation), where TBT is degraded into dibutyltin 
(DBT) and further degraded to monobutyltin (MBT). DBT and MBT are more easily degraded than 
TBT [46]. Other degradation processes e.g. methylation16 can also occur both by abiotic and biotic 
processes, which concentration of tributylmethyltin in harbours verify [39]. 
 
In water, abiotic processes such as hydrolysis and photodegradation of TBT can occur. But 
hydrolysis only happens under extreme pH conditions and is therefore disregarded under normal 
conditions [39]. Photodegradation has been shown to occur in both field and laboratory studies, but 
is very dependent on environmental conditions such as turbidity and light [39] [46]. Instead, the 
main degradation in water and sediments is induced by microorganisms (mainly bacteria17), which 
are capable of degrading TBT under both aerobic and anaerobic conditions by debutylation. In 
seawater and in sediments, the biodegradation is regarded as the most important process [39]. 
Degradation in sediments occurs similar to the ones described for water, but the degradation rate is 
much slower [39]. Table 5 shows half-lives for TBT in the environment under different conditions.

2.2.4 TBT as antifouling biocide 
Marine growth, e.g. algae and molluscs on a ship’s hull, increases the friction resistance and thereby 
increases the energy used to propel the ship. Therefore, in order to ensure the highest efficiency and 
maximum performance of a ship, the hull needs to be free from marine growth. Back in time, hulls 
were coated with lime to keep them clean [51]. Later on, arsenic, mercurial compounds and the 
pesticide DDT were used [51]. These compounds acted as antifouling systems, since they all had a 
negative effect on the growth of biomaterial. 
 
During the 1960’s antifouling paints were developed that were both efficient and cost-effective 
using metallic compounds, especially the organotin compound tributyltin (TBT) was widely used. 
In the first antifouling paints (“free associated paints”) with TBT as the active substance, TBT was 
freely dispersed in the paint [18] [49]. Since the TBT was “free” in the paint, the leaching was 
uncontrolled and occurred quite fast, especially initially, and after only 18–24 months the hulls 
needed to be repainted [51]. In the late 1960’s, self-polishing paints were developed where TBT 
was bonded to a polymer base and only released when the seawater broke the bonds between the 
resin and TBT. Since TBT was only leaching from the surface layer, the leaching rate became 
constant and much slower than in the freely associated paint, and the ships first needed to be 
repainted after 60 months [51]. 
 
Back then, TBT was assumed to be much less dangerous than the other options on the market, and 
TBT coatings became very popular. In the 1970’s the majority of the world fleet and smaller ships 
(< 25 m in length) were coated by TBT [51]. But the great use of TBT began to show in the marine 
environment during the 1970s, where high concentrations of TBT in shellfish (cultivated oysters) 
caused the shellfish industry in France to almost collapse [39] [51]. This led to restrictions and 
regulations on the use of TBT – first by separate states and later on by international agreements. In 
1982, France restricted the use of antifouling paints and was followed by the UK in 1985 and in 
several other countries up through the 1980s. In the late 1980s and start of 1990s the issue of TBT 
was taken to an international level, when the International Maritime Organisation19 (IMO) 
recommended countries to limit the use of TBT [51] [52]. The recommendations tightened in the

2.2.5 TBT in the North Sea today 
TBT has since 2003 been regularly examined as part of the OSPAR monitoring program. Due to the 
phase out and ban of TBT, the concentrations have declined and the concentration in coastal waters 
and estuaries are measured to 1-10 ng/L, but concentrations in water from harbours are much higher 
20-460 ng/L [18] [39]. The concentrations in sediments are still very high, and the highest 
concentrations are found in harbours and coastal areas, but decrease with depth [11] [39]. 
 
Even though the concentrations have declined, the effects of TBT are still high. The effects are 
evaluated based on the amount of snails that suffer from imposex. From 1998 – 2007 the effects of 
TBT was obvious at most of the monitoring stations. The phase out and ban of TBT has reduced the 
effects from TBT so the amount of snails with imposex is not increasing anymore [11].The amount 
of snails with imposex was found to be over 70 % for Neptunea antiqua and above 10 % for 
Liomesus ovum in the North Sea in 2001 [54]. So the problem with TBT pollution is assumed to 
still persist, particularly in sediments and in harbours and in their vicinities.

2.3.2 Toxicity of PAHs 
Due to the lipophilic character of PAHs, they are easily absorbed and distributed to lipid tissues 
[61]. The acute toxicity of PAHs is limited and decreases as the molecular size increases, but long 
term exposure can cause effects on the immune- and reproductive system, and also teratogenic 
effects [56]. Some PAHs are even categorised as potentially carcinogenic26 and mutagenic, since 
the metabolism of these compounds results in e.g. epoxides, which can react with nucleic acids [61] 
[62]. The carcinogenicity of PAHs is found to increase with increasing molecular weight [55]. 
 
Due the effects from PAHs, the sum of the nine PAHs described above must be < 3 mg/kg DW and 
< 30 mg/kg DW in sediments to fulfil the lower and upper action levels set by the Danish 
authorities [22]. In Denmark, the soil criterion of benzo[a]pyrene is 0.3 mg/kg, while the soil 
criterion for the sum of four PAHs27 is 4 mg/kg. 
2.3.3 PAHs in the North Sea 
PAHs have, just as heavy metals and TBT, been included in the OSPAR monitoring program, 
which have concluded that the dominant pathway and source of PAHs in the North Sea is long 
distance transport in the atmosphere [11]. This means, that even though the emissions from the 
OSPAR countries have declined over the period from 1998 to 2007, and kept constant through the 
last decades (around 1000 tonnes per year), the emissions of PAHs from upcoming countries (e.g. in 
Asia) can lead to increase in PAH concentrations in the North Sea [11]. The monitoring program 
found that the concentration of PAHs in coastal shellfish (mussels) and in fish during the period 
1998 – 2007 declined, but the concentrations in biota declined faster than in sediment [11]. The 
concentrations in sediment, often in coastal areas and near harbours, are therefore still above a level, 
which can cause effects and 55 % of the monitoring sites measured unacceptable levels28 of PAHs 
in the sediment in 2007 [11].

technologies and does not cause adverse effects on the biological activities and the soil structure, in 
contrast to other technologies e.g. washing of sediments [64] [65] [66]. According to [64] and [65], 
phytoremediation can be divided into different subgroups depending on how the plants handle the 
pollutants:

2.4.1 Phytoremediation of heavy metals 
Plants take up heavy metals and might therefore be used for phytoremediation of these. It has been 
found in this study [63]32 that trees such as Salix spp. and Populus spp. can take up heavy metals 
and that the extraction efficiencies are best for Cd in Salix spp. – but the extraction is still very low 
(< 0.5 % over 10 years) [63]. The efficiency depends primarily on the growth rate, since an increase 
in growth rate induces an increase in heavy metal uptake. Based on this it is expected that slow 
growing species (e.g. trees) have lower extraction efficiencies than faster growing plants (e.g. 
crops). Some plants are good at extracting and accumulating heavy metals (hyperaccumulating 
plants) and these are normally found where mining spoils have occurred, since the plants have 
adapted to these environmental conditions over time. Thlaspi spp. (belonging to the Brassicaceae 
family33) can, for example, accumulate more than 3% Zn, 0.5% Pb and 0.1% Cd in their shoots 
[65]. The uptake can also be improved by adding compounds such as EDTA34 (acting as a chelat) to 
the sediment and thereby increasing desorption of heavy metals from the sediment into the water 
phase, from where the plants can take up the heavy metals. But desorption of heavy metals can also 
lead to a greater leaching rate [65]. 
2.4.2 Phytoremediation of TBT 
According to [67] and [68] TBT is not toxic towards higher plants (e.g. Salix spp. which survived 
concentrations of 10 mg/L TBTCl) and does not affect plant growth. Furthermore, the study showed 
that the degradation of TBT is greater in sediments where plants have grown (> 40 % faster) where 
the main explanation for this outcome was that oxygen was the limiting factor for the degradation 
[67]. Plants can, besides taking up the pollutants, remove water from the sediment through uptake 
and transpiration. The water is taken up by the roots, and the root depth is therefore important. For 
crops, the average root depth is almost 2 m with the lowest depths in silty soils [69]. According to 
[64], the most important pathway for water loss in sediments is evapotranspiration35. The removal 
of the water changes the conditions from anaerobic to aerobic and will thus facilitate and/or 
enhance degradation of the pollutants, just as was seen with TBT in [67]. This is further supported 
by [70], where the degradation of TBT in marine sediment was significantly higher when three 
halophytes (Halimione portulacoides, Spartina maritima, and Sarcocornia fruticosa) had grown in 
the sediment.

2.4.3 Phytoremediation of PAHs 
According to [71], several grasses can be used to degrade PAHs. Among these plants was tall 
fescue (Festuca arundinacea), which was capable of degrading 38 % of pyrene in 190 days [71]. 
Other plants such as maize (Zea mays) and rape seed (Brassica napus) can also enhance the 
degradation of PAHs by bacteria in the root zone significantly [71] [72]. The degradation is limited 
by the amount of bioavailable PAHs. High molecular PAHs36, for example, are so strongly bound to 
the soil, that these are not degraded to/at the same degree/rate as for low molecular PAHs37. The 
presence of high molecular PAHs in the sediment does not have any negative effects on the plant 
germination or growth, and in fact, the presence of benzo[a]pyrene and its degradation products 
were found to act as growth stimulants [73]. 
2.4.4 The salt problem 
Sediment from the North Sea will be influence by the high salinity of the North Sea, which lies 
between 34 to 35 PSU38 [8]. This high salinity is due to the concentration of ions in seawater, where 
the most dominant ions (by a factor of 10 to other major ions) in seawater are Sodium (Na) and 
Chloride (Cl–) [74]. Sediments with concentrations of soluble salts, sufficiently high to interfere 
with plant growth, are classified as saline. Another definition of saline soils is formulated by the US 
Salinity Laboratory, which states that saline soil has an electrical conductivity (EC) above 4 mS/cm 
[28]. The pH of saline soils will be more or less neutral with a small tendency towards alkaline. 
Measurement of the soils’ EC provides a fast and simple method for determination of the salt 
content in the soil [28]. 
 
When a plant species is exposed to a saline substrate, there are three major constraints: drought 
stress (lower water potential in the sediment than in the plant), ion toxicity (mainly caused by Cl– 
and Na+ 39) and imbalance in uptake and distribution of nutrients [28] [75]. It has been found that 
NaCl concentrations above 1 g/L caused inhibition of growth, inducing wilting and death of the test 
plants [76]. High concentrations of NaCl can also cause inhibition of enzymes reactions (e.g. by the 
PEP carboxylase40) and nutrients (e.g. Mn), but it can also lead to enhancement of other nutrients 
(e.g. P), which then in high concentrations are toxic [28] [77]. The concentration of salt in 
sediments, therefore, has a great effect on the growth potential of plants. In wetland rice, for 
example, the salinity is considered to be the dominant nutritional constraint on the growth [28]. A 
reduction in plant growth is also related to salinity, since high salinities will reduce the water uptake 
and shedding of leaves41 due to too high concentrations of salt in them [77]. 
 
Certain ions can also increase the salt tolerance. High concentrations of Calcium (Ca2+)42, for 
example, has increased the salt tolerance in Citrus species, where Ca2+ has supressed both the Na+ 
and Cl– transport to the leaves. But the impact of Ca2+ depends on the species – Brassica species, 
for example, are not affected by Ca2+ [28] [77]. Salinity is typically inversely related to the leaf size 
area, meaning that increasing salinities reduces the area from which transpiration occurs and limits 
the uptake of CO2 (thereby reducing photosynthesis).

2.4.5 Salt tolerance of plants 
According to [28], plants can be divided into four classes (Group I-IV) with respect to their growth 
ability at different salinity concentrations, going from halophytes in Group I to glycophytes in 
Group IV. Halophytes43 are plant species that actually benefits from salinity up to very high 
concentrations. This is in contrast to glycophytes44, which are severely inhibited by salinity even at 
very low concentrations. The tolerance levels differ both within a crop species but also during a 
plants ontogeny45; a plant can be highly sensitive as a seed, but be more tolerant at a later 
developing stage, or the opposite [28]. In barley, for instance, the salt sensitivity increases after 
germination, while the salt sensitivity for maize is related to tasselling and grain filling [28]. In 
saline substrate the shoot growth is more affected than the root growth, since the roots only take up 
and do not accumulate Cl– as much as shoots and leaves do. A plant can handle salinity by either 
salt inclusion (taking up the salt) or exclusion (reduced salt uptake). The major risk of exclusion is 
water drought, while the major risk related to inclusion is ion toxicity. Halophytes mainly make use 
of salt inclusion, where the plant accumulates NaCl (mainly in the leaves) and uses the ions to 
regulate the osmotic pressure over membranes (turgor) and are able to replace K+ with Na+ in 
different metabolic reactions (creating a big Na+/K+ gradient) [28]. Halophytes also have the ability 
to reduce the salt concentration in active photosynthetic tissue in different ways. The plant can 
either accumulate the salt in bladder hair46, secretion by salt glands, disposal of old leaves and 
relocation of the salt concentration to other organs [28] [77]. 
2.4.6 Halophytes and salt tolerant species 
Halophytes are often associated with succulent stems and leaves, or with bluish wax layers, and can 
be found in nature on sea coasts, salt marches and in salt deserts, and include species such as 
glassworts (Salicornia spp.) and Oraches (Atriplex spp.) [78]. Other species such as barley (H. 
vulgare) and sugar beets (B. vulgaris) are, according to [28], classified as a salt tolerant and 
halophilic species, meaning that it can grow normally, and to some degree, grow better at relatively 
low concentrations of salts, than at zero concentration. Salt marshes are low-lying areas located 
close to the sea and are thus often flooded by the sea during high tide. In Denmark, the southern 
part of Jutland’s west coast includes a lot of salt marshes and the Wadden Sea. The vegetation at 
these places consists therefore of plant species that tolerate both high water contents and salinities 
in the soil. One of the first species to grow on a salt marsh is Salicornia spp., which is followed up 
by grasses such as common saltmarsh-grass (Puccinellia maritima), cordgrass (Spartina spp.) and 
red fescue (Festuca rubra) [75]. Other species that can sustain with much water and moderate 
salinity are common reed (Phragmites australis) and broadleaf cattail (Typha latifolia), which both 
can be found in nature in areas such as bays and streams [79]. According to [80], oat (Avena sativa) 
is a crop, which do well in a cool climate and is more salt tolerant than other crops.

3.2.5 Hydraulic conductivity 
In order to determine the hydraulic conductivities of the sediments, a column experiment was set up 
using a glass column with an open top to facilitate water flow through the sediment due to 
gravitational pressure. For the sediment experiments the top of the column and the outflow glasses 
were covered by a permeable piece of paper to reduce the evaporation of water. 
 
The outflow from the glass column was controlled by a valve (open/shut) at the bottom of the 
column. Due to the valve, the outflow area was significantly smaller than the area at the top of the 
glass column. In order to obtain an outflow area equal to the top area a plastic ring was placed at the 
bottom of the column. To prevent the sediment from leaching from the column, a coarse grid 
covered by fleece was placed on top of the plastic ring. The experimental setup is illustrated in 
Figure 8. 
 
Before the experiments with the sediments, the initial experiments were performed to check if the 
fleece could have an impact on the setup. To do so, the glass column was filled with 5 cm and 10 
cm of water. Then, the valve was opened and the time it took the water to flow out was measured by 
noting the time when the water in the column was lowered by one cm. The results were then 
compared to the time it took for water to flow out of the column without using fleece. 
 
Water was not added during the experiment – meaning that water was only added at the start of 
experiment to achieve a specific height. However, since the experiment went on for several days 
(weeks); evaporation from the column and the outflow glasses occurred. This was monitored by 
comparing the volume of water remaining in the column at each measuring point with the weight of 
the water in the outflow glass. Also a glass with water also covered by a permeable paper was 
monitored regularly in order to determine the amount of water evaporating from a water surface. 
Furthermore, gauges measuring the relative humidity and temperature in the room were used to 
determine a theoretical value of the amount of water evaporating from the system. See Appendix 
1.1 for further information on the equations used to determine the theoretical evaporation from a 
water surface.

3.4 Contamination analyses 
3.4.1 Heavy metal concentrations in the sediments 
The concentrations of heavy metals in the sediments were measured before plant growth. 
3.4.1.1 Heavy metals in the sediments 
Preparation of the sediments 
The sediments were first prepared by heating the samples one day in an oven at 105 °C to remove 
water from the samples. Afterwards, the sediment was roughly crushed with a mortar and 
transferred to an agate grinding barrel placed in a vibration disc mill of the type Siebtechnik, in 
order to pulverise the sediment. The disc mill vibrated the sample with 710 min–1 for 15 s. The 
different instruments used for the sediment preparation are shown in Figure 12. 
 
Figure 12: Preparation of the sediment samples. 
Left: The sediment is crushed in a mortar. Middle: the agate grinding barrel: Right: the vibration disc mill 
Extraction of metals from the sediments 
After the sediment was dried and pulverised the heavy metals were extracted from the sediments 
according to the US EPA standard method 3051A. First, approximately 0.25 g of sediment was 
transferred to a special microwave container. Then, 9 mL HNO3 (65 %) and 3 mL HCl (37 %) was 
added in order to extract the heavy metals. The containers were then placed in special microwave 
heat mantles and placed in a microwave rack, where pressure and temperature was logged. The 
samples were then run in the Microwave, Multiwave 3000 Solv according to a program, where the 
heating power was increased to 1000W over 5 minutes and then kept there for 10 minutes. Then the 
power was increased to 1400 W over 5 minutes and there for further 5 minutes. After this, the 
samples were left to cool for 25 minutes. 
 
The samples were then transferred to 50 mL glass bottles. The microwave containers were rinsed 
with MilliQ water55 and the water transferred to the 50 mL glass bottles. The glass bottles were then 
filled with MilliQ water to increase the volume to 50 mL. Since, the entire sediment sample was not 
completely degraded, the sample was transferred to 45 mL plastic centrifuge tubes, which were 
centrifuged with 2500 rpm for 10 minutes in a machine of the type Hettich Zentrifugen Rotanda 46. 
This was done to get the remaining sediment to settle at the bottom of the tube, while the water 
phase was transferred to 25 mL plastic bottles, which were kept at room temperature until the 
analysis was performed.

3.5 Salt analyses 
3.5.1 NaCl concentrations in porewater and sediment 
The determination of the NaCl concentration (salt concentration) included several steps. First, the 
electrical conductivity (EC) of the porewater was measured using the conductivity meter WTW 
Cond 3310 (see Figure 6). As for the pH, the EC value was first accepted, when it was stable. The 
instrument was cleaned with distilled water after each measurement and regularly calibrated. 
 
To measure the EC in the drainage field sediments distilled water was added, until the drainage 
field sediments became a paste, of which EC could be measured. 
 
In order to convert the EC to a salt concentration, a calibration curve for NaCl was determined 
experimentally. This was done by measuring the EC at different known concentrations of NaCl 
dissolved in distilled water. As NaCl source, normal kitchen salt of the brand “Fine Salina Salt” 
was used. The concentrations of NaCl ranged from 0.12 g/L to 62.5 g/L, thereby covering the 
expected salt concentration range found in the sediments. The salt concentrations were then plotted 
versus the measured EC values. From the points a tendency line was created, and the equation of 
this line could be used for converting measured EC values to salt concentrations.

3.5.3 Salt model 
To simulate the leaching of salt in the sediment a model was used. A template setup of the model 
was provided by Stefan Trapp. The model made use of the Discrete Cascade Model BUCKETS 
method described in [82], where the sediment was divided into five equal sized layers with 
precipitation as only water input. The water losses in the model were evaporation and leaching from 
the bottom layer (Layer 5). Plants were not included in the model and the model is therefore 
simulating the salt development in bare sediment. Details on the input data for the model are 
presented in Appendix 1.6.

After the washed sediment from Basin 6b was analysed, the control standards gave deviations 19 % 
and 34 % based on the standards 501-402 and 502-413, respectively. 
 
The high deviations for the analyses for the washed Basin 6b sediment can be caused by the acid 
used in the preparation of the TOC samples interfering with the LECO CS 200 machine. The last 
result of the TOC measurement of the washed Basin 6b sediment gave a result that is considered to 
be false, since it is much higher than the other two TOC results and also higher than the TC content, 
which is impossible. The other results for TC and TOC are considered to be realistic (having the 
same content as the other Esbjerg samples), whereas the results for the washed sediment from Basin 
6b are connected with large uncertainties. 
 
The total carbon content is much higher in the control soil, than in the sediments (factor 7–8 
higher). This also applies to the content of total organic carbon. The low content of organic carbon 
also means that the content of organic matter is limited in the Esbjerg sediments. This means that 
there is less binding capacity of e.g. heavy metals and PAHs in the Esbjerg sediments than in the 
control soil. However, it is assumed that the content of clay is much higher in the Esbjerg harbour 
sediments than in the control soil, which, on the other hand, increases the binding of e.g. heavy 
metals and PAHs.

from a glass equal to the outflow glasses; 2) calculating a theoretical evaporation from the outflow 
glasses. The theoretical evaporation for the outflow glass was then fitted to the measured 
evaporation and based on this fit, an estimated evaporation from the columns was determined. The 
measured evaporation amounts observed during the experimental period were plotted versus time 
and a tendency line was created. The tendency line gave an almost perfect fit (R2 = 0.9986) and 
depicted the evaporation to 2.37 g/day. The theoretical evaporation for the outflow glass was 
estimated to: 20.8 g/day, which was too high (by a factor of around 10). To fit the theoretical value 
with the measured evaporation, the evaporation number (τ) was changed from 25 to 2.85, which 
changed the theoretical evaporation in the outflow glass to 2.37 g/day, and gave an estimated 
evaporation from the columns of 0.88 g/day. The measured and the calculated theoretical 
evaporations are provided in Appendix 2.5. 
 
The amount of water lost from the set-up was not added and is therefore a source of error. To limit 
this error, only measurements where the evaporation did not significantly affect the results were 
included in the final determination of the hydraulic conductivities. 
 
In the experimental setup, a fleece was used to prevent the sediment from leaching. To evaluate the 
possible effect from the fleece on the sediment experiments the outflow with and without the fleece 
were compared to see if there was a difference. It was observed that the time it took for the water to 
flow out of the column was approximately the same with and without the fleece. It can therefore be 
concluded that the fleece did not affect the results for the hydraulic conductivities found for the 
sediments. The data concerning the fleece test are provided in Appendix 2.5.

4.3 Contamination analyses 
4.3.1 Heavy metal concentrations in sediments 
The sediments were analysed for the following heavy metals: As, Cd, Cr, Cu, Ni, Pb and Zn in 
duplicates. The results are presented in Figure 25 – 27 and details on the measurements and the 
sample weights are found in Appendix 4.1. 
 
To accept the results, two criteria must be fulfilled: 1) the deviation between the control samples 
and the reference values must not exceed ± 15 %; 2) the results must be above the quantification 
limit. The results for all the sediment samples are above the quantification limits, and for the control 
samples, the deviations are less than ± 15 %. The results are therefore accepted. 
 
In general, the concentrations of the heavy metals are very limited in the control soil, which lies a 
factor 5 – 10 lower than in the Esbjerg sediments. The concentrations of the heavy metals are in 
general highest in the drainage field sediments (especially Drainage Field 2b) than in the basin 6b 
sediments. The differences between the lowest and highest measurements are in general very low. 
However, the differences are a little bit higher for Cu than for the other heavy metals. 
 
The concentration of Cd is the lowest and lies < 1 mg/kg for the Esbjerg sediments. However, the 
Cd concentration in the Drainage Field 2a sediment is much higher (2.91 mg/kg DW) than the other 
Cd results. The difference between the lowest and highest concentration is also very high, which 
indicates that one of the two measurements for Cd in Drainage Field 2a sediment is an outlier. The 
outlier is probably the highest measured concentration (5.36 mg/kg DW), since the lowest (0.45 
mg/kg DW) is in the same range as the other measured Cd concentrations.

4.3.2 Heavy metal concentrations in plants 
Concentrations of the heavy metals: As, Cd, Cr, Cu, Ni, Pb and Zn were analysed in duplicates in 
plant samples. From each sediment (Drainage Field 2, Drainage Field 2b, Basin 6b – washed and 
the control soil) a mixed plant sample was generated by taking equal amount of plant material from 
each plant species grown in the specific sediment. For the plant species barley, rapeseed and wild 
cabbage grown in Drainage Field 2a sediment, there was enough plant material to analyse the heavy 
metal in these species. The results are presented in Figure 28 – 30 and details on the measurements 
and the sample weights are found in Appendix 4.2. 
 
As for the heavy metal analyses in the sediments, the two criteria described in section 4.3.1 Heavy 
metal concentrations in sediments must also be fulfilled for the heavy metal concentrations in the 
plants. Again, the results for all the plant samples are above the quantification limits. The deviation 
of the control samples from the reference value were less than ± 15 % for most of the control 
samples. However, for two control samples (one for Cr and one for Cu) the deviations were –17 %, 
which is just outside the acceptance range. But other control samples gave deviations less than ± 15 
% for both Cr and Cu and the results are nevertheless accepted. 
 
Based on the results provided in Figure 28 – 30 it can be seen that the concentrations of As, Cd and 
Pb in the plants are below 1 mg/kg DW, and the Cr concentration just a little bit higher (< 2 mg/kg 
DW). The concentration of Cu and Ni is greater, < 10 and < 14 mg/kg DW, respectively.  Zn 
concentrations showed to be the highest concentration of heavy metals in the plants, where the 
concentrations range from around 50 mg/kg DW to 320 mg/kg DW. The differences between the 
lowest and highest measured values are highest for Ni, Cu and Cr relative to the content. 
 
The concentrations of Ni in the Rapeseed from Drainage Field 2a, the mixed samples from 
Drainage Field 2b, mix and the Basin 6b – washed were 8 mg/kg DW, 40 mg/kg DW and 16 mg/kg 
DW, respectively. These values are significantly higher than the other results found for Ni. On the 
figure, only the low measurement values for these samples are presented, since the high 
concentration measurements are considered to be outliers. The highest concentrations of Ni in 
Drainage Field 2a, Rapeseed, Drainage Field 2b, mix and in the Basin 6b – washed, mix were 14.37 
mg/kg DW, 74.62 mg/kg DW and 31.40 mg/kg DW, respectively. The lowest concentrations were 
1.55 mg/kg DW, 4.67 mg/kg DW and 1.45 mg/kg DW for Drainage Field 2a, Rapeseed, Drainage 
Field 2b, mix and in the Basin 6b, respectively, which lies in the range of the other Ni uptake 
concentrations and therefore seems realistic.

4.4.3 Salt model 
The salt model was set up using the Buckets model for two scenarios: 1) the sediment from the 
harbour is deposited once; 2) 0.6 m of sediment is deposited each year over a period of five years. 
The model does not include plants. 
 
To validate if the model could be used to simulate the development of the NaCl concentration in the 
deposited sediment over time, the model results are compared with the measured salt concentrations 
from Drainage Field 2a and 2b, which have been deposited on the ground three years ago. They 
were sampled at the depth of approximately 20 – 30 cm below the surface (Drainage Field 2a) and 
30 – 45 cm below the surface (Drainage Field 2b). Layer 1 in the model was therefore first set to a 
thickness of 0.6 m, since the concentration in a layer is the average over the layer thickness. The 
modelled concentration in Layer 1 after three years was then compared with the measured 
concentration from Drainage Field 2a (had been on land for three years, when the sediment was 
sampled). Afterwards, the thickness of the Layer 1 was increased to 0.9 m and this result compared 
to the measurement in Drainage Field 2b. Table 26 provides the simulation results from the model 
and the measured NaCl concentrations from Drainage Field 2a and 2b. It can be seen from the table 
that the simulated results are very close to the measured ones, and the models are therefore 
accepted. Furthermore, the concentrations are the same in model 1 and 2, which is because the 
concentration will develop identically in the top layer, since the initial concentration of salt is the

5 Discussion 
5.1 Germination and growth of plants 
The germination and survival in the drainage field sediments (2a and 2b) and in the control soil 
irrigated with distilled water was in general good. As can be seen from Table 32 between 17 and 20 
different plant species germinated in these sediments and several of these plant species had 
germination and survival rates of at least 90 %. There was no significant difference between the 
results from the control soil and the drainage field sediments, where a number of the plant species 
had germination and survival percentages of 100 % in the three sediments. The species that had 100 
% germination and survival percentages differed slightly, but especially rapeseed and rutabaga had 
a high germination and a survival in these sediments. Both rapeseed and rutabaga share the Latin 
name of Brassica napus (two variations of the same species) and belong to the mustard and cabbage 
family. Other species belonging to the mustard and cabbage family also did well in the germination 
test, i.e. wild cabbage, marrow-stem kale and kale – all three species are variants of Brassica 
oleracea. Other species such as barley and oats, both belonging to the grass family, also germinated 
and survived well in the sediments. Only one beet (chard, B. vulagris var. conditiva Alef) did well.

could indicate that the salt concentration was on the border of what the halophyte (common

The reason for the low germination in the Basin 6b sediment was suspected to be the salt 
concentration rather than the concentration of pollutants (heavy metals, TBT and PAHs). This 
suspicion was caused by the fact that the plants germinated and survived just as well in the 
contaminated sediments from the drainage field as in the control soil irrigated with water. To test if 
the salt concentration in the sediment was the reason for the low germination in the Basin 6b 
sediment, sediment from the Basin 6b was washed in order to bring down the salt concentration in 
the pore water to 1 g/L. Two new germination experiments were thus performed: 1) in the washed 
sediment; 2) in the control soil irrigated with 3.5 % saltwater. 
 
Germination and survival of the plant species in the washed sediment from Basin 6b gave similar 
results as the ones observed in the drainage field sediments and in the control soil irrigated with 
water. The result of the control soil irrigated with saltwater gave approximately the same results as 
for the natural Basin 6b sediment – only few species and seeds germinated. Again, the only species 
that germinated and survived fairly well was common glasswort (50 % germination and survival). 
 
It was also checked if the improvement in germination and survival in the washed Basin 6b 
sediment was not only caused by the removal of salt from the sediment, but also caused by a 
reduction in contaminants the sediment. This was checked by performing the same characterisation 
and pollution analyses on the washed sediment from Basin 6b as was done on the natural sediment 
from Basin 6b. Based on the characterisation analyses it was seen that the composition of the 
sediment in terms of nutrients, organic and inorganic carbon, pH and water content had not been 
significantly changed. Furthermore, the contaminant analyses did not reveal any significant change 
in heavy metal, TBT and PAH concentrations between the washed and natural sediments. Based on 
this, the concentration of pollutants (heavy metals, TBT and PAHs) found in the sediments did not 
seem to affect the germination and survival of the plant species. Instead, a high concentration of salt 
had a major impact on especially the germination of the seeds. 
 
The time it took for the first seed to germinate was a little bit lower in the sediments Drainage Field 
2b and Basin 6b – washed than in the Drainage Field 2a and the control soil. The difference is not 
significant, but could indicate that the presence of salt in low concentrations speed up the 
germination. The same indications cannot be observed for the time of maximum germination. It 
should also be considered that the times of the first and maximum germination are linked to the 
specific plant species and the environmental conditions (e.g. temperature, irrigation) more than the 
salt concentration. 
 
The germination test was succeeded by a growth test of the 12 plants with the highest germination 
and survival percentages. From the growth test it was seen that the plant heights were highest (and 
approximately the same) for the plants grown in Drainage Field 2a sediment and in the control soil. 
The plants in these two sediments were, on average, almost 10 cm higher than the plants grown in 
the other two sediments. The plants are also eldest in the control soil and in the Drainage Field 2a 
sediment (79 days), whereas the plant ages in Drainage Field 2b and Basin 6b – washed were much 
lower (51 and 31 days). The greater heights observed in Drainage Field 2a and the control soil can 
therefore be a result of a longer growth period and not so much that the sediments facilitate a higher 
growth. It is interesting to notice that the plant heights in the washed sediment from Basin 6b are 
approximately the same as in Drainage Field 2b, even though the plants are 20 days younger. This

indicates that salt content in the sediment plays an important role for the plant growth, since the salt

The water content in the plants was approximately the same, and quite high, in all four sediments – 
almost 90 % on average, but it differed from species to species. If the average water content in the 
plants is compared with the salt concentration in the sediments, it can be seen that the water content 
in the plants might be correlated to the salt concentration in the sediment. The water content in 
plants on average are lowest, when the salt concentration is highest (see Table 33), but the trend is 
not significant. The lower uptake of water with increasing salt concentration could indicate that the 
plants react on the salt concentration as excluders or is due to a lower water potential in the 
sediment. This means that one of the risks for the plants is drought. But based on the high water

water contents, the dry weights were highest in the plants from the Drainage Field 2b and lowest in

The wet weights (the amount of plant material harvested at the end of the experiment) are also

Based on the high nutrient contents in the sediments, the growth was not assumed to be inhibited by 
lack of nutrients, and the plants were therefore not fertilised during the test. Neither the 
contamination with heavy metals, TBT and PAHs is considered to have influenced the growth rate. 
The plant growth is considered to be mostly influenced by the salt concentration in the soil. 
 
The sediments consist of mainly silt with a clay fraction. These small particles, especially in 
Drainage Field 2b and Basin 6b, made the soil texture very compact, which made it a bit harder for 
the plant roots to infiltrate. So the plant roots grew mostly on the surface of the sediment, which 
could in real life make the root vulnerable e.g. with respect to low temperatures, insects and so on. 
Furthermore, when growing on the surface, the roots will not to the same degree create pathways in 
the soil, facilitating evaporation and degradation of contaminants. 
 
In all the sediments, barley, tall fescue, maize and oats were the highest, while the highest dry 
weights were observed for the grass-mix and the tall fescue. So tall fescue is considered to be the 
plant species that grows the best in the sediments based on the growth in height and production of 
dry weight plant material.

5.2 Effect of plants on the pollution  
5.2.1 Heavy metals 
Heavy metals in the sediments 
The heavy metal content was analysed in the two drainage field sediments and in the sediment from 
Basin 6b (natural and washed form). Most of the heavy metal concentrations in the sediment from 
Basin 6b (both in natural and washed form) are below the action levels stated by the Danish 
authorities. The concentration of Cr in the washed Basin 6b sediment exceeds the lower action level 
to a minor degree (0.52 mg/kg DW). The concentration in the natural Basin 6b is above the lower 
action limit, but the excess is so small that it is considered to be due to measurement uncertainty. 
Only the concentration of Cu exceeds the upper action limit by approximately 10 – 15 mg/kg DW. 
The sediment is therefore regarded as polluted with Cu coming from e.g. the use of Cu as active 
ingredient in a lot of antifouling paints. 
 
When deposited on land it is interesting to evaluate if the sediment is also considered to be polluted 
or not. The Cd in the sediment will lie in the average background concentration range, and the same 
can be said about the concentrations of Pb and Zn, though both Pb and Zn lie in the high end of the 
background concentration range, and well above the average. The situation is different for As, Cr, 
Cu and Ni, which all lie above the background concentration, but they are all below the soil quality 
criteria and the concentrations are therefore not considered to pose risks towards humans or the 
environment. It must be noted that if 50 % of the measured total Cr concentration consists of 
Cr(VI), the concentration is above the soil quality criterion, but it is considered unlikely that so high 
a fraction is Cr(VI). The heavy metal concentrations in the Basin 6b are therefore not considered to 
be a big issue, except for Cu. 
 
In the drainage field sediments, which were deposited on land three years before the sampling took 
place; the concentrations of heavy metals are higher than in the fresh harbour sediment from Basin 
6b. The concentrations are higher in the Drainage Field 2b sediment than in the Drainage Field 2a. 
This implies that some of the heavy metals in the top layer has been removed, either by leaching 
due to precipitation or has been taken up by the plants that were observed on the sampling location. 
The concentrations of As, Cd, Cr, Cu and Zn are still, for both drainage field sediments, above the 
lower action level. For Cu, the concentration in the Drainage Field 2b sediment is also above the 
upper action level by around 15 mg/kg DW (16 % exceeding). On the other hand, the 
concentrations of Ni and Pb is below the lower action level in the Drainage Field 2a sediment, but 
still exceeding the lower action level in the Drainage Field 2b sediment. 
 
The amounts of Cd and Zn in both drainage field sediments are within the background 
concentration range, and this applies to the Pb concentration in Drainage field 2a as well. The 
concentration of As is above the soil quality criterion in both sediments, whereas only the soil 
quality criteria for Ni and Pb are exceeded in the Drainage Field 2b sediment. Cr and Cu 
concentrations are all below the soil quality criteria in both sediments and this also applies for the 
Ni and Pb concentrations in Drainage Field 2a. Therefore, the sediment from Drainage Field 2a is 
only considered to be polluted with As, which occurs in concentrations that might pose risks 
towards the environment and humans. According to the Danish authorities, Drainage Field 2b 
sediment will still be classified as polluted with As, Ni, and Pb to a degree that is posing a risk 
towards humans and the environment. The sediment from Drainage Field 2b is also polluted with 
Cr and Cu, but the concentrations of these heavy metals are not considered to pose a risk towards 
humans or the environment.

Heavy metals in the plants 
In general, the uptake of heavy metals in the plants is relatively low, but the concentrations were 
higher in the Esbjerg sediments than in the control soil. The parameter that varies the most in the 
Esbjerg sediment is the salt concentration. Since there were no clear tendencies on which of the 
Esbjerg sediments that facilitate the best uptake of heavy metals, the salt concentration does not 
seem to have an impact on the uptake. The concentrations of As, Cd and Pb in the plants are very 
low, which indicated the uptake is poor. The bad uptake might be caused by the fact that these 
heavy metals do not have any biological functions. In contrast, Ni, Cu, Zn, and to some degree, Cr, 
is taken up in larger amounts. These heavy metals are classified as micronutrients and might 
therefore be the reason for the better uptake. 
 
The As, Pb, Cu and Zn concentrations were highest in barley compared with rapeseed and wild 
cabbage. On the other hand, the concentrations of Cd and Ni are highest in rapeseed, and only the 
Cr content is highest in wild cabbage. The uptake of all the heavy metals in barley was also higher 
than in the mixed plant sample, except for Cd. This could mean that barley is better than the other 
plants, included in the growth test, to take up heavy metals, especially As, Pb, Cu and Zn. If 
phytoremediation is chosen in order to bring down the heavy metal concentrations of especially Cu, 
the best results are gained by using barley rather than rapeseed and wild cabbage. 
 
To investigate if the concentration of the heavy metals in the soil had an impact on the uptake of the 
heavy metals in plants, BCF values were calculated. The BCF values were < 1 which indicated that 
the concentrations in the plants were lower than in the sediments (the sediments were the source of 
the pollution). However, the concentrations of Zn in barley grown in the Drainage Field 2a, and the 
mixed plant sample grown in the control soil, were higher in the plants than in the sediment. This 
indicates that the measured concentrations in the plants are either too high or the concentration in 
the sediment is too low, thereby giving an incorrect BCF value. The average BCF values were 
lowest for Cr, Pb and As (BCF < 0.025), indicating that these heavy metals are poorly taken up by 
the plants with respect to the concentration in the soil. The BCF values for Ni, Cu and Cd were 
higher (0.15 < BCF < 0.30), while the highest BCF value was for Zn (BCF = 0.491). Therefore, the 
plants can best take up Zn relative to the concentration in the sediments. 
 
All the BCF values for the control soil are higher than the BCF values found in the Esbjerg 
sediments. This means that the plant uptake of all the heavy metals in the control soil is better than 
in the Esbjerg sediments with respect to the sediment concentration. This might be due to the plants 
have a “saturation point”; a concentration in the soil above which the plants change action from 
being includers to being excluders. It could also be caused by the heavy metals being more strongly 
bound in the Esbjerg sediments (e.g. due to a higher clay content) causing the fraction of 
bioavailable heavy metals to be lower in these sediments than in the control soil. 
 
By comparing the BCF values for the different Esbjerg sediments, it can be seen that the BCF 
values are highest in the washed sediment from Basin 6b and lowest in the Drainage Field 2a. This 
might be due to the plants grown in Basin 6b were youngest and the eldest was from Drainage Field 
2a. The plants might have a higher growth rate in their early life than later on, and this higher 
growth rate might induce the uptake heavy metals to be greater. However, there are no clear 
tendencies, based on the BCF values, on whether barley, rapeseed or wild cabbage is taking heavy 
metals best up with respect to the sediment concentrations.

5.2.2 TBT 
The concentration of TBT in Drainage Field 2a is approximately 700 µg/kg DW in the natural61 
sediment. In Drainage Field 2b, the concentration of TBT is 630 µg/kg DW. But the concentrations 
of TBT in the samples from this sediment after plant growth are much higher – up to 1750 µg/kg 
DW. Therefore, the low concentration is considered to be incorrect, and the real concentration 
assumed to be approximately 1750 µg/kg DW. In the fresh sediment from Basin 6b, the 
concentration in the natural sediment is around 400 µg/kg DW. It can therefore be concluded that 
the concentration of TBT in the natural sediments from the drainage field is much higher than in the 
fresh harbour sediment (at least by a factor 2). This indicates that the concentration of TBT in the 
harbour is declining, which is a result of the TBT ban on ships. Thus, it is expected that the 
concentration will keep declining in the future. In the washed sediment from Basin 6b, the 
concentration of TBT is approximately the same as in the natural sediment. Therefore, the washing 
of the sediment did not cause TBT to be washed out. It is also an indicator of TBT being bound in 
the sediment (as predicted by the theory) rather than dissolved in the water phase. 
 
The concentration of TBT is considered to be highest in the deep sample from the Drainage Field 
2b, which is due to the oxygen concentration decreasing with depth and causing a slower 
degradation with depth. In the top layer of the drainage field, roots will have penetrated the top 
layer and removed water from the sediment and thereby improved the degradation conditions of 
TBT, in contrast to further down, where roots have not penetrated the sediment. 
 
In the mixed sample62 from Drainage Field 2a, the concentration of TBT is lower than in the natural 
sediment. This could indicate that plants have facilitated the TBT degradation. However, for the 
mixed samples from Drainage Field 2b and Basin 6b, the TBT concentrations are approximately the 
same between the natural (washed) and the mixed samples, indicating that the plants might not have 
any effect on the TBT degradation. The TBT concentration in the sediment, where barley has 
grown, is higher than the TBT concentration measured in the sediment, where wild cabbage had 
grown, for the drainage field sediments. However, in the Basin 6b sediment, the tendency cannot be 
supported. This could indicate that plants take up TBT, but this is not considered to be significant 
and it would not be expected according to the theory. 
 
To investigate if the plants have facilitated TBT degradation, the distribution of the fractions of 
TBT and its degradation products (DBT and MBT) were found. By comparing the distribution of 
TBT, DBT and MBT with the mixed, barley and wild cabbage sediment samples, it can be seen for 
the Drainage Field 2a sediment that the fraction of degradation products are greater after plants 
have grown in the sediment than before. Especially the fraction of MBT seems to increase. The 
same tendency can also be observed for Drainage Field 2b, but is much less clear. In Basin 6b, there 
is not observed any differences in the distribution of TBT, DBT and MBT before and after plant 
growth. This could imply that the plant ages play a major role (eldest plants in Drainage Field 2a, 
youngest in Basin 6b). The elder the plants, the higher the degradation of TBT into DBT and MBT. 
This could be because the elder the plants are, the more developed is the root system. The tendency, 
most clearly seen in Drainage Field 2a, could also be caused by the roots easier infiltrate the 
sediment in Drainage Field 2a, while the roots mainly grow on the sediment surface in Drainage 
Field 2b and Basin 6b.

5.2.3 PAHs 
In general, the concentrations of PAHs were very limited (just above the detection limits) in the 
sediments, both before and after plants had grown in them. The highest concentrations of PAHs 
were measured in the Drainage Field 2b and in Basin 6b. This might be due to the oxygen content in 
Drainage Field 2b sediment is lower than in Drainage Field 2a (thereby lower degradation). Also 
the concentration of PAHs in the harbour and in the North Sea has been higher than it is now (less 
pollution with PAHs). The concentrations are, in all of the samples, well below the lower action 
level, and compared to the soil quality criterion the concentrations are below this limit, too. The 
concentration with PAHs is therefore not considered to be a problem and is not expected to cause 
effects on humans and the environment. By comparing the concentrations of PAHs with the content 
of heavy metals and especially the TBT concentration, the concentration of PAHs is not considered 
to be important. The main pollution problem regards TBT. 
 
The concentrations of PAHs are approximately the same in the sediment before and after plant 
growth, which could indicate that plants do not have an effect on the PAH concentration. But, since 
the concentrations are so limited it is not possible to observe any tendencies whether the plants have 
an effect on the concentration of PAHs (enhanced the degradation) or not.  
5.3 The role of salt 
5.3.1 Salt concentrations in the pore water and sediment 
As indicated previously, salt has a great impact on the plants. Therefore, it is important to analyse 
the concentration of salt in the sediment, and in the pore water, in order to screen the sediment and 
decide if plants can be used to remediate or facilitate remediation of a contamination.  
 
It was found that the concentration of salt in the pore water was significantly higher (6-18 times 
higher) in the sediment from Basin 6b, compared to the sediments from the drainage field and the 
control soil. This is because the sediment comes directly from the harbour. The salt concentration in 
the pore water from Drainage Field 2a is almost the same as in the control soil (almost 1 g/L), while 
the concentration in the deeper sample from Drainage field 2b is somewhat higher (2.62 g/L). This 
indicates that some removal of salt especially in Drainage Field 2a has taken place, either by 
precipitation (leaching) or salt uptake by plants. The concentrations in the drainage field sediments 
also indicate that the salt concentration increases with depth. 
 
The concentration of salt in Basin 6b is way above the toxic salt level for plants (1 g/L). This also 
applies for the concentration in Drainage Field 2b, but to a lesser degree. In Drainage Field 2a, as 
well as in the control soil, the concentrations of salt are beneath 1 g/L and should therefore not have 
any impact on plant growth. 
 
Kd for salt in Basin 6b is 0.75, which means that some salt is stored in the sediment, but the biggest 
fraction is in the water phase. The situation is a bit different for the drainage field sediments, where 
the Kds are just above 1, meaning that the concentration is higher in the sediment than in the water 
phase. This is considered to be unlikely, since salt has a high solubility in water. The incorrect 
results might be due to errors in the experiment, where distilled water was added to the sediment so 
the electrical conductivity could be measured. The addition of water has most likely shifted the salt 
concentration between the sediment and the pore water. This would have the biggest influence of 
the initial Kd measuring point (largest water content compared with particle fraction), and since the 
this concentration was used for all the Kd measurement points, this might have led to incorrect 
results.

5.3.2 Plant uptake of chloride and sodium 
To investigate if plants could take up salt from sediment and in which amounts, the concentration of 
chloride in the plants’ leaves was measured. It was found that the concentration of Cl in the mixed 
plant leaf samples from the plants grown in the control soil and the drainage field sediments 
followed the salt concentration tendency. So the highest concentration of Cl was observed in the 
plants, where the concentration of salt was highest, and vice versa. For Basin 6b, the concentration 
of Cl in the plant leaves were much higher than in the other samples. This high concentration could 
be a result of errors in the analyses, e.g. impurities, since the samples were not filtrated before the 
analysis was conducted. But it could also be linked to the fact that the plants are much younger in 
the Basin 6b, than in the other sediments. The growth is greatest, when the plants are young and 
thereby the uptake of nutrients and other compounds might be higher at this stage of the plant’s life. 
This could explain the high concentration of Cl in the plant leaves. 
 
It could also be seen that the concentration of Cl in the leaves from barley was higher than in wild 
cabbage and, especially, kale. The Cl concentration in barley was also higher than the average (the 
mixed sample from Drainage Field 2a), whereas both wild cabbage and kale was taking up less Cl 
than the average. This indicates that barley is better to take up Cl than most of the plants grown in 
the Drainage Field 2a sediment. Since there is no data on the uptake from most of the other plants 
grown in Drainage Field 2a sediment or in the other sediments, the higher uptake of Cl by barley 
cannot be considered as significant. 
 
It was expected that the plants would take up Na easier than Cl, since 1) the Na ion is smaller than 
the Cl ion; 2) Na is used in plant kinetics. But instead, it was observed that the plant uptake of Cl 
was higher than for Na, except for in wild cabbage grown in Drainage Field 2a sediment and in the 
mixed plant sample from Basin 6b. This also meant that most of the plants removed a larger amount 
of Cl from the soil than Na. The higher uptake of Cl could be due to Cl coming from another source 
than NaCl, e.g. TBTCl or that Na is more strongly bound to the soil than Cl (cations have more 
binding spots than anions have). Furthermore, the concentration of Cl was measured in the plant 
leaves, whereas the concentration of Na was measured in plants in general. The concentration in the 
leaves is higher than in other parts of the plant, e.g. the stem. This could also have caused the 
concentration of Cl to be higher than the Na concentration. 
 
The removal of Na and Cl from the soil was relatively low from the drainage field sediments and 
the control soil (maximum 10 % removal). The removal was higher from Basin 6b and in barley 
from Drainage Field 2a (10 – 20 %). This could indicate that barley is good at removing salt from 
the sediment, but it also indicates that plant age, as previously described, might have an effect on 
the uptake on both Na and Cl. 
5.3.3 Development of salt over time 
Based on the Kd value for Basin 6b, it was seen that the concentration in both the pore water and in 
the sediment were high. This means that it will take much time to reduce the concentration of salt to 
a level, where it is not toxic towards plants. Therefore, the salt model becomes a useful instrument 
in order to predict when plants can be expected to grow in the sediment, and thereby when 
phytoremediation is a possibility. The salt model was used to simulate two situations: 1) the 
sediment is deposited on land at one time up to a height of 3 m; 2) sediment is deposited on land 
over 5 times so the total thickness becomes 3 m.

In the first salt model, the initial salt concentrations in the five layers are all the same. The 
concentration decreases in the top layer immediately, but the declines in concentrations in the other 
layers are retarded compared to the first layer. The deeper the layer is located, the more retarded the 
decrease will be. This means that a concentration below 1 g/L is first achieved in the first (top) layer 
and finally in the bottom layer. From Figure 41 and Table 27 it can be seen that it takes 3 years 
before the concentration is below 1 g/L in the top layer. In contrast, it takes 9.33 years before this 
concentration in the bottom layer is reached. It can also be seen from the figure that the 
concentration will decline mostly at the beginning, and then flatten out. The deeper the layer is 
located, the flatter the decrease becomes. 
 
The salt development in the top layer simulated by the second salt model is identical to the one in 
the first salt model. However, the initial concentrations in the other layers differ, thereby changing 
the salt concentration development. In the second, third and fourth layer, the initial concentrations 
are below the initial concentration in layer one, while the start concentration in the bottom layer is 
above the Layer 1 concentration. The concentration in the second layer will first increase, since it 
will receive concentrations from the top layer that is higher than itself, since it was the top layer the 
year before. But the concentration will start to decrease after 243 days. The concentrations in the 
third, fourth and fifth layer will decline from the beginning and will not be retarded in the same way 
as depicted by the first salt model. The second salt model indicates that the time before the 
concentration in the top layer is beneath 1 g/L is 3 years (equal to salt model 1), but the time it takes 
in the other layers are a little bit shorter (tLayer 5 = 9.09 years). 
 
Both models show that it takes 3 years after deposition of the final sediment, before the salt 
concentration in the top layer is below the desired 1 g/L. This means that sowed and/or planted 
seeds cannot be expected to germinate and survive before 3 years after the final sediment has been 
deposited. So it will take three years if the sediment is deposited all at once, but 8 years if the 
sediment is deposited over 5 years. The salt concentration of 1 g/L in the other layers is reached 
much later, according to both models. Concentrations below 1 g/L in the five layers are first met in 
the second salt model, but if five years are added to the times, the concentrations comes below 1 g/L  
much faster if the sediment that is deposited all at once. 
 
Therefore, it is recommended that the confined disposal facility is divided into smaller sections, 
where the sediment can be deposited all at once up to the final height e.g. 3 m. In this way it will 
take less time before plants can be used for either phytoremediation or facilitating the degradation 
of the contamination. The concentration of 1 g/L is reached earlier in the lower layers in the second 
salt model, but the difference is not so great that it will change the recommendation. 
 
The concentration of salt in the leachate from the bottom layer (outflow of the model) will be a little 
bit lower in salt model 1 than in the second salt model. The concentration will also stay 
approximately constant for some time before it decreases in salt model 1, while the concentration in 
salt model 2 will start the decrease from the beginning. 
 
Both models predict that the water contents in all the layers are equal to the field capacity (0.4 L/L) 
throughout the whole simulation period (10 years). This means that the anaerobic condition in the 
sediment is unchanged, when the water is lost only through either evaporation or by leaching from 
the bottom layer. If plants are introduced in the model, it is expected that the water content will 
decline, since plants take up water and transpire. Thereby, the plants will help to change the

Sensitivity analysis of the salt model parameters 
A sensitivity analysis of selected parameters in the salt model was performed to evaluate which 
parameters the model was most sensitive to. The parameters that the model is most sensitive 
towards should as far as possible also are the ones with the lowest uncertainty. If the parameter has 
a high uncertainty, and the model is very sensitive towards this parameter, this will induce that the 
model will be associated with a high uncertainty too. 
 
Based on the sensitivity analysis, it was found that on short term (after 1 year) the most sensitive 
parameter was the density of the soil. As the salt concentration has been reduced significantly in the 
soil (after 5 and 10 years), the density of the soil is still important, but the model becomes more 
sensitive towards the precipitation, if the precipitation decreases. It is therefore crucial to use a 
correct soil density and precipitation in the model. In the models used in this project, the soil 
density was found in literature based on the composition of the soil (the high silt content). The 
accuracy of the model would increase, if the density of the sediment from Esbjerg was 
experimentally determined instead. The precipitation used in this project is actual precipitation in 
the Esbjerg area for a five year period, and is therefore considered to be quite accurate and 
representative. 
5.4 Further work 
The tests performed in this project are all laboratory experiments. To improve the results, in situ 
tests at the Esbjerg harbour e.g. by planting plants on the site could be beneficial, since the plants 
then will grow under natural conditions rather than semi-optimised63 conditions in the laboratory. 
Bigger and longer tests would also allow more plant material to be produced. This would increase 
the number of analyses and/or replicates, which in that way could improve the validity and 
reliability of the test results by statistical analysis. It would also mean that more plant species could 
be investigated with respect to e.g. uptake of salt and heavy metals. By growing more plants over 
longer periods of time, it would also be possible to clarify if the uptake of heavy metals is greater in 
some plant stages than in others (e.g. higher in younger plants than in elder plants). 
 
The germination test of the halophyte, Salicornia europaea (common saltwort) showed to 
germinate in the sediment with highest salt concentration. It could therefore be beneficial to test if 
other halophytes give the same results, or even better germination and growth results. The growth 
of the common saltwort was quite low, so longer test periods, especially for the growth test, might 
provide enough data material to test the common saltwort and other halophytes for uptake of e.g. 
heavy metals and salt. 
 
There were some weak indications that TBT might be taken up by plants, and it could be worth 
investigating if this was true, since the theory predicts the opposite. If it is true that plants are taking 
up TBT, this would mean that phytoremediation of TBT would be an option, and not only use the 
plants to facilitate the degradation of TBT. Furthermore, the role of bacteria in co-operation with 
phytoremediation could also be investigated, since this might improve the degradation of the 
pollutants. It could also be investigated if bacteria would yield higher degradation rates of the 
pollutants than plants do.

6 Conclusion 
The germination and survival in the drainage field sediments were good and more or less equal to 
the results from the control soil. The species that showed the best germination and survival 
properties were rapeseed and rutabaga (both Brassica napus). Other species from the kale and 
mustard family (variants of Brassica oleracea) and the grass family (Hordeum and Avena sativa) 
also showed good germination and survival properties. The germination in the fresh sediment from 
Basin 6b was very limited and only the halophyte common glasswort (Salicornia europaea) 
managed to germinate and survive. A germination test in washed sediment from Basin 6b, where 
only the salt concentration was reduced was conducted. It showed germination and survival results 
very similar to the ones in the control soil and the drainage field sediments. Therefore, the poor 
germination in the sediment from Basin 6b is concluded to be due to the salt concentration and not 
the concentration of pollutants (TBT, heavy metals or PAHs). 
 
Based on the measured heights and plant weights, the growth experiment showed that the plant 
growth was best in the Drainage Field 2a sediment and in the control soil. The growth was lowest in 
the Drainage Field 2b sediment. The concentration of nutrients was not a limiting factor for growth. 
The major limiting factor for growth is concluded to be the salt content in the sediment; the lower 
the salt concentration, the better the growth. It was also found that the water content in the plants 
might be correlated to the salt concentration in the ground; the lower the salt concentration in the 
sediment, the higher the water content in the plant. The same can be said about the wet weights of 
the plants. Barley, Tall fescue (Festuca arundinace var. Tower), maize (Zea mays) and oats were 
the plants that were highest, while the grass-mix and the tall fescue gave highest dry weights. All in 
all, Tall fescue was found to grow the best in the sediments. 
 
Of heavy metals in the Basin 6b sediment, only Cu was found in a concentration that constitutes a 
pollution problem – the concentration is above the upper action level. The pollution is assumed to 
originate from the Cu application as biocide in antifouling paints. In the drainage field sediments, 
especially in Drainage Feld 2b, the concentrations of heavy metals were higher than in Basin 6b. 
The concentrations of As, Cd, Cr, Cu and Zn are all above the lower action level, and the Cu 
concentration is above the upper action level. Based on this, it is concluded that the concentrations 
of heavy metals is declining in the harbour. Furthermore, some of the heavy metals in the top layer 
in the drainage field have been removed, either by leaching to deeper sediments or by plant uptake. 
 
In general, the concentrations of heavy metals in plants were limited, but higher in the Esbjerg 
sediments than in the control soil. It was found that the salt concentration in the sediment has no 
impact on the uptake. The highest uptake in plants with respect to the concentration found in the 
sediment was for the Ni, Cu, Cd and especially Zn. The poor uptake was therefore considered to be 
related to heavy metals not having any biological uses. Barley was found to take up heavy metals 
best, especially As, Pb, Cu and Zn. Overall, the uptake of heavy metals by plants with respect to the 
concentration in the sediment were higher in the control soil, which indicated that the bioavailable 
fraction of heavy metals were higher in the control soil than in the Esbjerg sediments, due to a 
higher content of clay in the Esbjerg sediments (stronger binding potential). It was also observed 
that the younger the plants were, the higher the BCF values, which indicates that the plants take up 
more heavy metals when they are young than when they are older. 
 
The major contamination problem regards TBT which, in all samples, is significantly above the 
upper action level. But the concentration is declining in the harbour, where TBT is strongly bound 
to the sediment rather than dissolved in the water phase. By comparison of the Drainage Field

samples it was found that TBT is lower in the upper sediment sample, indicating that degradation 
has occurred. The plants might not take up the TBT, but the roots will take up water and increase 
the porosity of the sediment, thereby facilitating TBT degradation. Indications were also found that 
the older the plant, the greater degradation of TBT is observed. This might be because the elder the 
plants have more developed root systems. 
 
The concentration of PAHs was very limited (just above the detection limit in most cases) and far 
below the lower action level, and is therefore not considered to be a pollution problem. The 
concentrations in the sediments before and after plant growth were approximately the same and 
therefore, the plants cannot be said to have had any impact on the PAH concentration. Also, since 
the concentrations are so low, no significant trends can be concluded. 
 
Salt was found to have a major impact on the plant growth. The concentrations in the sediments 
were found for the Drainage field 2a and the control soil to be below the toxic level of 1 g/L. The 
low concentration in Drainage field 2a is considered to be due to leaching and to a smaller extent 
removal by plant uptake. The concentration of salt in the Drainage Field 2b was above the toxic 
limit (2.62 g/L), but especially the concentration in the pore water of Basin 6b the concentration 
was very high (16.21 g/L). Based on the found Kd value for Basin 6 (0.75) the concentration in the 
sediment fraction is also very high (12.23 g/kg DW). 
 
It was also found that plants take up chloride, and that barley had a higher uptake of chloride than 
the average, while wild cabbage and kale had an uptake lower than the average. By comparing the 
Na and Cl uptake it was found that the uptake of Cl was higher in most samples. This could be due 
to the Na being measured in the whole plant, while the Cl was measured in the leaves, where the 
concentration is assumed to be highest. The removal of chloride from the ground was also higher 
than the Na removal, and it is assumed that plant age plays a role in the salt uptake: the oldest 
removed < 10 % of the sediment concentration, while the younger plants removed 10 to 20 %. 
Therefore, it can be concluded that the younger the plant, the higher the uptake will be. 
 
The development of the salt concentration was modelled over a 10-year period according to two 
scenarios. The model predicted that it would take 3 years to get below 1 g NaCl / L in the top layer 
and just over 9 years in the bottom layer for both scenarios. The time it took to get below the 1 g/L 
was reached first in the second model, but since it took five years before the simulation was started 
in the second model, the actual durations in the second model are 8 years in the top layer and 14 
years in the bottom layer. It is therefore recommended that the confined disposal facility is divided 
into multiple sections with the final depth instead of spreading the sediment over a larger area over 
several years. The model did not include plants, and the water content was therefore constant in all 
five layers and equal to the field capacity. The inclusion of plants would decrease the water content 
due to uptake and transpiration. Including plants in the model is therefore suggested. The most 
sensitive parameters were found to be the density of the sediment at all times, and as the 
concentration of salt decreases, the precipitation also becomes a sensitive parameter.
