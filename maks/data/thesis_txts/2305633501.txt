Abstract 
Sprouts are vehicles for number of pathogens due to the favorable growth conditions for them 
during germination and none or only mild heat processing prior the consumption. Even though after 
1999 when the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) recommended 20,000-ppm hypochlorite as 
a decontamination method, which let to decreased the number of outbreaks, during the last E. coli 
outbreak related to spouts in 2011 in Germany, 50 people died and 908 got hospitalized for 
hemolytic uremic syndrome. Hypochlorite was proved insufficient and by many European countries 
including Denmark and Switzerland it got forbidden for its incompliance with organic production.  
One alternative decontamination techniques is cold plasma. This non-thermal method is suitable for 
treatment of organic materials including human tissues and food items sensitive to heat and 
chemicals. In case of seeds, one extra request exists - since to obtain sprouts from the seeds, 
germination takes place, thus an alternative decontamination method must be able to preserve the 
germination abilities.  
In this project a novel commercial type of non-thermal atmospheric pressure plasma was tested. 
Compared to previously used devices this type operates on a higher plasma power density (orders of 
10 W/cm3). Raw alfalfa seeds which are one of the most often linked to the sprout-related outbreaks 
served as a tested food substrate. Plasma treatment efficacy was tested on native seeds microflora 
as well as on artificially inoculated E. coli DSM 1576 and Listeria monocytogenes WSLC 1001. For 
better characterization of decontamination efficacy omitting the influence of substrate geometry, E. 
coli DSM 1576 was also tested on flat homogenous substrate. Optimization of decontamination level 
was done by varying treatment conditions such as treatment time, plasma power density, moisture 
of the substrate and number of treatments. 
Treatment of 5 min at full plasma power density (approx. 100 W/cm3) and with seeds moistened to 
25% reduced native microflora of seeds up to 2.78 log. E. coli artificially inoculated decreased up to 
4.72 log and L. monocytogenes more than 5.05 log. Two subsequent treatments of seeds with 25 % 
moisture for 1 min at full power containing native flora revealed 2.90 log reduction. Flat substrate 
inoculated with 109 CFU/ml of E. coli resulted in more than 7 log reduction. 
Essential condition for successful seeds treatment –preservation of germination abilities was also 
tested. All samples of all treatment conditions, including 1 min at full plasma power density with dry 
and seeds with 25 % moisture revealed no germination abilities, at given germination time (10 days). 
Only treatment of 1 min at reduced power (approx. 60 W/cm3) could preserve 15 % of germination 
abilities. Furthermore, scanning electron microscopy revealed serious damages on the seeds coat, 
incompatible with seeds survivability. Although plasma treatment achieved satisfactory microbial 
inactivation, germination abilities significantly declined. Thus, more tuning should be performed to 
balance sufficient microbial contamination and preservation of germination abilities.

Background  
The application of conventional thermal or chemical sterilization methods is often limited since many 
food products are sensitive to heat, moisture and a variety of chemicals. Plasma sterilization is a 
promising alternative to conventional sterilization methods. By applying an electrical field, a gas can 
be partly ionized. The resulting low-temperature plasma generates charged particles, reactive 
neutrals and UV photons, which can effectively destroy microorganisms. This research project 
focuses on the plasma inactivation of microorganisms in food and the characterization of factors 
causing detrimental effects on the microorganisms.  
Various Dielectric Barrier Discharges (DBD) which operate at ambient pressure are available in the 
Transport Processes and Reactions Laboratory at ETH Zurich. With these discharges flat substrates 
and granular materials in the mm range can be treated in a reactive plasma zone to decrease their 
microbial contamination. In a first experimental study the general feasibility to reduce Bacillus 
endospores on wheat grains could be shown while functional product properties were not affected.  
Furthermore, a volumetric DBD developed by the Transport Processes and Reactions Laboratory was 
applied to optimize the inactivation of natural and artificial microbial contamination on different 
seeds used for sprout production. Besides decreasing microbial contamination, plasma treatment can 
also influence germination and growth of seeds. In the current thesis, we will focus on the 
application of another type of DBD system. The so called Diffuse Coplanar Surface Barrier Discharge 
(DCSBD) system creates a thin sheet of plasma on a ceramic support. This novel plasma treatment 
system will be investigated for its suitability to treat granular materials and to characterize its effect 
on the surface microflora of seeds.

Goals  
The aim of the thesis is to investigate the feasibility of sprout seed treatment in the Diffuse Coplanar 
Surface Barrier Discharge (DCSBD) system and to characterize the effect of the treatment on the 
microorganisms harbored by the seeds. While the microbial load should be reduced, the germination 
and growth capability should stay unaffected or even be improved by the treatment. For this purpose 
the parameters defining the treatment (time, discharge power, gas composition and flow) will be 
varied in order to study and optimize their effect on the treatment efficiency. In order to gain a 
deeper insight into the underlying inactivation mechanisms, the inactivation of microorganisms on 
flat substrates will be investigated in order to better characterize the detrimental effects that the 
DBD treatment exerts on microbial cells.  
 
 
In detail, the following tasks and questions will be addressed:  
 
• Literature study on plasma treatment of microorganisms and plasma inactivation of microbes in 
food. In particular, the inactivation mechanisms should be reviewed in detail.  
• Optimization of the setup of the Diffuse Coplanar Surface Barrier Discharge (DCSBD) for 
inactivation of microorganisms on seeds for sprout production.  
• Plasma decontamination of model substrates and sprout seeds in the DCSBD and investigation of 
the influence of various process parameters (treatment time, gas composition, power input) and 
comparison with results from DBD experiments.  
• Quantification of inactivation rates for naturally present and artificially applied microorganisms by 
determination of the numbers for colony forming units (CFU) prior to and after plasma treatment.  
• Investigation of the influence of plasma treatment on the growth properties of seeds used for 
sprout production.  
• Investigation of the influence of plasma treatment on bacterial cells using microscopy techniques 
such as scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and atomic force microscopy (AFM).  
• Characterization of the inactivation mechanisms that cause detrimental effects on the bacterial cell 
during treatment in the DCSBD system experiments.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Sprout seeds 
Dry seeds naturally host high numbers of bacteria and germinating process provides ideal conditions 
for their growth to elevated levels on sprouted seeds. Some of the naturally occurring 
microorganisms on seeds are human pathogens or potential pathogens. They can be introduced to 
the dry seeds via dust and soil, contaminated agricultural water, inadequately treated manure 
fertilizers or insufficient workers hygiene (U.S. Food and Drug Administration, 1999). Warm and 
humid environment are optimal conditions not only for seeds to sprout but also for pathogens to 
proliferate. There are numerous known cases of outbreaks caused by eating raw sprouts, associated 
with Salmonella or enterohemorrhagic Escherichia coli (Niederberger, et al., 2013) (Smith, et al., 
2013). Other pathogens and opportunistic pathogens such as Bacillus cereus, Staphylococcus aureus, 
Listeria monocytogenes and Yersinia enterocolitica have also been identified on the sprouted alfalfa 
seeds (European Food Safety Authority, 2011). The number of normal microflora on dry seeds is 
usually 102 – 106 CFU/g, including coliform bacteria (104 CFU/g) and fecal coliforms (102 – 103 CFU/g) 
(Feng, 1997). During germination bacteria level on sprouts can reach 108 to 1011 CFU/g (Penas, 2008). 
Consumption of raw sprouts swamped with bacteria expose certain groups of population such as 
kids, elderly people or people with weaken immune system to risk of foodborne illness. Reducing the 
number of pathogens prior to germination is therefore crucial for the microbial safety of the sprouts.  
 
1.2 Recent disinfection methods 
In the United States the standard disinfection treatment for reduction of microbial contamination on 
seeds for more than a decade is 20,000 ppm calcium hypochlorite, recommended by the Food and 
drug administration (FDA) (Smith, et al., 2013). Chlorine treatment in not in compliance with 
production of organic sprouts and other fresh-cut products and its use is not authorized in countries 
such as Germany, Switzerland, The Netherlands, Belgium and Denmark (Betts & Everis, 2005), 
(Gomez, et al., 2009). Above that, the effectivity of this disinfection method has been found unstable 
(Montville & Schaffner, 2004). 
As an alternative to chemical treatment, hot water (90 °C) followed by rapid cooling treatment was 
successfully tested as disinfection method of mung beans in Japan (Bari, et al., 2008), (Bari, et al., 
2009). 
Another physical method, high pressure treatment, which is successfully adopted in juice processing, 
has also been tested. However, the drawback of this method seems to be a delay in germination 
(Neetoo & Chen, 2010). Biological treatment using competitive lactic acid bacterium, bacteriocins or 
bacteriophages to exclude pathogens from sprouts surface has been also studied. Although 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
satisfactory reduction rates were achieved in small scale, the method faces uncertainty about 
industrial application and potential negative effects on health of consumers (Ye, et al., 2010). Gamma 
irradiation treatment is another tested seeds decontamination method but its negative impacts on 
the yield, length and appearance of sprouts as well as possible nutrients loss prevent it from being 
widely accepted (Rajkowski, et al., 2003). Besides that, gamma irradiation has not been well accepted 
by the consumers neither the industry due to production of large amount of free radicals which 
require high security standards, (Moreau, et al., 2008).  
Since working with high doses of chemical disinfectant like chlorine can pose risk to workers and the 
environment and other alternative method seems to have drawbacks incompatible with the treated 
seeds substrate new emerging methods need to be assessed. 
 
1.3 Plasma as a promising method for bacterial decontamination of seeds 
A novel decontamination method requires rapidity, safety, cost-effectiveness and environmental 
friendliness without negative effect on seeds germination. Plasma is one of the promising techniques 
for bacterial decontamination combining physical and chemical synergistic effect. In particular, non-
thermal plasma treatment generated by electric discharge at temperatures close to ambient fulfills 
mentioned desired attributes better than conventional decontamination methods (Niemira, 2012)  
(Moreau, et al., 2008). 
 
1.4 State of the art 
1.4.1 Description of plasma 
Plasma is a partially or fully ionized gas composed of free electrons, positively and negatively charged 
ions, photons (UV and visible) and significant proportions of excited species, radicals and reactive 
species (Arjunan, et al., 2015) (Moreau, et al., 2008). In nature plasma occurs as thermal plasma in 
form of lightning. Thermal plasma is generated with high power (up to 50 MW) at high pressure. 
Thermal plasma is in its local thermal equilibrium - the temperature of heavy gas particles (ions and 
neutral gas molecules) is similar to the temperature of excited electrons. To the contrary, non-
thermal plasma has a gas temperature close to ambient (25 – 50 °C) and orders of magnitude higher 
temperature of excited electrons. Thus, non-thermal plasma has a non-equilibrium temperature 
character. In between non-thermal and thermal plasma lies microwave driven plasma, which has a 
gas temperature much higher than temperatures observed in non-thermal plasma, but the area of 
application is almost identical (Ehlbeck, et al., 2011). 
There are various setups for the generation of non-thermal plasma at atmospheric pressure 
available, including barrier discharges, corona discharges and micro hallow cathode discharges. In 
==PAGE:11==
11 
 
barrier discharges, the presence of one or more dielectric barriers between metallic electrodes is 
essential for discharge operation. Electrodes and dielectric barrier (DB) can have different 
arrangements (Figure 1). The arrangement of DB in respect to the tested substrate determines the 
vicinity of the sample and plasma. In coplanar and surface arrangement samples lie above the 
electrodes, while in planar setup sample lies in between two electrodes.  
 
Figure 1: Typical electrodes arrangements of cylindrical and planar barrier discharges 
(reproduced from (Wagner, et al., 2003)). (a) Coplanar setup with both electrodes embedded 
inside DB, (b) Surface discharge with one electrode embedded in BD, (c) Planar setup with DB at 
both electrodes, (d) Planar setup with one electrode covered in BD, (e) Planar setup with DB in 
the discharge gap. 
Discharge operation can be summarized as an electrical breakdown when high enough voltage is 
applied. Electrical breakdown is followed by development of plasma filaments also called 
microdischarges. These microdischarges last in range of nanoseconds and are uniformly distributed 
over the whole discharge surface. Important operational parameters of plasma discharge are 
discharge voltage, discharge current and electric power input. Every current pulse creates series of 
microdischarges. Development of microdischarges can be divided into three stages. In the first, pre-
breakdown stage (0.5 µs), is characterized by electrons accumulation close to the anode. After 
reaching a certain threshold, breakdown starts from anode surface. Second, propagation stage (1 to 
2ns), is characterized by propagation of ionization wave in the direction of the cathode and 
production of pairs of ions and electrons. The third, decay phase characterizes accumulation of 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
charge on the dielectric surface. It this phase, the light and the microdischarges appear. These three 
stages are repeated with next half-cycle of sinusoidal voltage. Short duration of microdischarges in 
very frequent intervals give in a macroscopic impression of homogenous stable plasma layer ignited 
on the dielectric discharge.  
1.4.2 Non-thermal plasma and its recent applications 
Since first development of plasma discharge more than 30 years ago (Muller, 1979), the spectrum of 
its application expanded to various fields. Non-thermal plasma, thanks to its relatively low 
temperature, is currently employed in biomedical applications including sterilization of human 
tissues and surgical instruments (Lloyd, et al., 2010), (Baxter, et al., 2006), dentistry (Chang & Chen, 
2014) or cancer cells apoptosis induction (Kim, et al., 2010). In the field of food technology non-
thermal plasma has become an effective tool for inactivation of microorganisms. Current studies 
demonstrated effectiveness of non-thermal atmospheric pressure plasma on reduction of microflora 
on seeds and granular substrates. The study of (Butscher, et al., 2016) demonstrated 3 logarithmic 
unit reduction of artificially contaminated endospores of Geobacillus stearothermophilus on wheat 
grains after 60 min Dielectric Barrier Discharge (DBD) treatment. This study also evaluated the 
influence of temperature, electric field and mechanical stress from vibration on viability of spores 
which are important factors to be considered working with susceptible material like bacteria. They 
could also demonstrate that inactivation of spores was due to direct effect of plasma-generated 
species. In another study conducted by (Knorr, et al., 2015) 30 min DBD treatment reduced 
Salmonella enterica by 4.1 logarithmic units and Bacillus subtilis spores by 4.2 logarithmic units, both 
artificially inoculated on black pepper. The detrimental effect of temperature was excluded and the 
quality of seeds stayed unchanged. In the study of (Zahoranová, et al., 2015) approx. 1 log reduction 
of naturally occurring bacteria on wheat seeds was reached after 120 s treatment and total growth 
inhibition of toxicogenic Aspergillus flavus was observed after 240 s Diffuse Coplanar Surface Barrier 
Discharge (DCSBD) treatment. Recent findings of (Kitazaki, et al., 2014), (Henselová, et al., 2012) 
(Stolarik, et al., 2015) revealed possibilities for enhancing growth of radish, maize and pea using 
plasma. 
1.5 Decontamination principle  
Although plasma decontamination has not been fully understood, some principles are already well 
known. The study of (Von Keudell, et al., 2010) suggests that erosion caused by ion and molecular 
bombarding is the main inactivation mechanism with help of UV photons. Plasma-generated species, 
including reactive oxygen species (ROS) (O2
-, OH-, HO2
-, CO3
-, H2O2, O3, O2 singlet) and reactive 
nitrogen species (RNS) (NO-, NO2
-,..) can in excessive doses cause oxidative DNA damage. 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
Subsequently they can promote oxidative stress and lead to cell senescence or even apoptosis (Circu 
& Aw, 2010). Mechanical effect of ROS and RNS on outer bacterial structures can lead to destruction 
or etching of cytoplasmic membrane, which allows plasma to propagate into the cell and cause DNA 
damage (Joshi, et al., 2011). Concerning human health and the environment plasma-generated 
species do not resign long term negative effect due to their short life span (Akishev, et al., 2008). In 
non-thermal atmospheric pressure plasma with air as working gas ROS and RNS are attributed 
dominant detrimental effect (Laroussi & Leipold, 2004).  
 
1.5.1 Parameters influencing bacterial inactivation 
Several research groups demonstrated plasma inactivation of Gram-negative (Lackmann, et al., 
2013), Gram-positive vegetative cells (Lu, et al., 2014) as well as bacterial endospores (Tseng, et al., 
2012),  (Butscher, et al., 2016) and viruses (Yasuda, et al., 2010) by cold plasma. Most of them 
attribute the cell death to the detrimental effect of DNA damage, while others to cell wall damages.  
The inactivation principle depends on type of microorganism and its membrane structure and 
chemical composition, resistance to reactive agents, DNA protection and number of bacterial layers 
(Arjunan, et al., 2015). According to (Butscher, et al., 2016) and (Lerouge, et al., 2001), type and 
geometry of substrate which microorganisms occupy can influence the efficacy of the treatment.  
Regarding plasma operating parameters, type of operation gas, its flow rate, plasma power input, 
frequency and voltage influence composition and amount of reactive species, heat generation, UV 
radiation and charged particles (Laroussi & Leipold, 2004). 
 
1.5.2 Diffuse Coplanar Surface Barrier Discharge device (DCSBD) 
DCSBD is a type of non-thermal atmospheric pressure air plasma which was initially developed for 
industrial need of fast, cheap, efficient in-line treatment for low added value products like metal 
foils, plastic films, textiles, wood or paper (Roth, 2001). In order to ensure short treatment time high 
plasma power density was required (at least in the order of 10 W/cm3). DCSBD can be operated with 
ambient air. Unlike others, it able to create homogenous layer of plasma with high power density 
without unwanted plasma filamentation (Černák, et al., 2009). This is in contrast to other non-
thermal atmospheric plasma devices such as Dielectric Barrier Discharge (DBD) or Atmospheric 
Pressure Glow Discharge Plasma (APGD) which in order to maintain relatively high power densities, 
need to use costly argon, helium or neon as working gases.  Otherwise increasing plasma power 
density in DBD or APGD would cause plasma filamentation and sparks (Rombolà, et al., 2006).  
DCSBD as any other non-thermal plasma has a non-equilibrium temperature character. The study of 
(Henselová, et al., 2012) performed with the same type of DCSBD device determined the vibration 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
temperature, which is related to level of excitation of the electrons, to be 2160 ± 222 K and 
rotational gas temperature which is related to actual temperature of gas, to be  300 ± 10 K. Excited 
electrons by colliding with neutral gas molecules create reactive ions and radicals with high 
temperature while the heavy particles have a temperature close to room temperature. This implies 
that non-thermal plasma is suitable for treatment of delicate organic materials (Černák, et al., 2009).  
This master project was conducted with the commercially available DCSBD device from Roplass – 
Robust Plasma Systems (Roplass, 2014a), igniting non-thermal plasma at atmospheric pressure using 
ambient air. This DCSBD device outperforms other plasma systems by homogeneity of its plasma 
increasing with increased power density without need for noble gas mixtures. High plasma power 
density enables very short treatment time (few s), with low energy consumption. It is robust, safe to 
use and capable to operate continuously (24 hours/ 7 days) even in humid and dusty industrial 
conditions while preserving long lifetime (Roplass, 2014b).  
Silver electrodes are arranged in many parallel strip-lines imbedded 0.5 mm deep in the ceramic 
plate made of 96% aluminum. Macroscopically homogenous plasma is ignited on the plate surface in 
a thin uniform layer. The alumina plate has a rectangular shape and dimensions of 93 x 230 mm with 
the plasma area of 80 x 200 mm covering most of the plate. The producer claims the plasma layer 
thickness to be 0.3 mm. Ceramic plate can be laid above or under the substrate what allows 
treatments of samples from different positions - sample can be placed inside the ignited plasma layer 
and therefore immediate vicinity with plasma is ensured (Figure 2) or sample can be put in a plastic 
housing with plasma applied from a desired distance. The discharge is powered by a high-voltage 
sinusoidal signal of 20 - 30 kV which can be manually adjusted, a frequency of 14 - 18 kHz and a 
plasma power which is governed by frequency and voltage of up to 100 W/cm3. Experimental setup 
and image of DCSBD can be found in Appendix A.

Discharge DCSBD and image of induced plasma with alfalfa seeds on ceramic surface 
(Reproduced from ( (Zahoranová, et al., 2015)). Device operates at atmospheric pressure with

1.6 Motivation 
Among sprouted seeds, alfalfa seeds have most frequently been implicated with outbreaks (Advisory 
Committee on the Microbiological Safety of Food, 2012). Despite the risk of foodborne illness, these 
sprouts have in the last decades become a popular food item for its perceived health benefits such as 
anticholesterolemic or anticarcinogenic properties (Donaldson, 2004). To ensure microbial safety of 
this product, efficient and user and customer friendly decontamination method has to be developed. 
For this purpose, cold plasma as a promising method was tested in this project.  
 
1.7 Aim 
In previous studies of the Laboratory of Food Microbiology and Transport Processes and Reaction 
Laboratory at ETH Zurich microbial decontamination and improved germination could be achieved on 
various grains with DBD plasma treatment. Alfalfa seeds revealed the highest log reduction from all 
studied seeds, which included onion, radish and cress seeds (Van Loon, 2015). This result could be 
due to the waxy, smoother surface with less valleys (Sikin, et al., 2013), which according to 
(Bermúdez-Aguirre, et al., 2013) provides less surface protection for the microorganisms than other 
tested seeds.  
This study continued using alfalfa seeds for investigation of the feasibility of a new commercially 
available DCSBD device. DCSBD plasma discharge enables treatment of granular or flat substrates of 
unlimited dimensions. The obtained results were to some extend compared to results of the 
previously used DBD device.  
This master thesis focused on foodborne pathogenic bacteria and native flora decontamination due 
to their connection to sprouts-associated outbreaks in the past. Due to general lower relative 
resistance of viruses and fungi it was assumed that these are inactivated along with inactivation of 
Gram-negative bacteria. In order to obtain the best microbial reduction, the device parameters such 
as discharge spatial arrangement, distance from the substrate, treatment time, plasma power as well 
as substrate parameters such as seeds quantity and seeds moisture were varied. The effect of 
treatment was tested on both, naturally occurring and artificially inoculated bacteria on the seeds 
surface. In order to study detrimental effects on more homogenous substrate, plasma treatment on 
flat filter surface was established. Finally, the correlation between the plasma effect on microbial 
inactivation and germination abilities was determined.

2 Materials and Methods 
In this chapter, methods of performed experiment are described. 
2.1 Bacterial strains, growth media and materials 
In this subchapter used strains of bacteria, growth media and necessary materials are described.  
2.1.1 Organisms 
Escherichia coli and Listeria monocytogenes were chosen to serve as model bacteria for studying the 
efficacy of plasma treatment on single foodborne pathogen. In purpose of observing plasma effect 
on bacteria with different membrane structures and chemical composition one representative from 
Gram-positive and one from Gram-negative bacterial group was chosen. Both microorganisms were 
provided by Gene bank ETH Zurich. 
2.1.1.1 Escherichia coli DSM 1576  
Escherichia coli is a Gram-negative rod-shaped, non-sporulating bacteria which either uses flagella for 
mobility or is immobile. It is facultative anaerobic mesophilic bacteria able to produce vitamins and 
suppress harmful bacteria in the human body. It colonizes lower guts and survives in natural 
environment which allows dissemination to new hosts.  Optimal growth temperature is 37 °C. E. coli 
is an opportunistic pathogen responsible for infections in enteric, pulmonary, urinary and nervous 
system. Six obligatory pathogenic E. coli virulent strains are recognized (enterotoxigenic, 
enteropathogenic, enteroinvasive, enterohemorrhagic, enteroaggregative and adherent-Invasive). 
Thermal inactivation temperatures are between 55 to 65 °C (UniProt, 2016), (Juneja, et al., 2009). 
2.1.1.2 Listeria monocytogenes WSLC 1001 
Listeria is a Gram-positive non-sporulating flagella-driven motile bacteria. It is a bacterium naturally 
present in the environment and animal and human feces. Growth temperatures are in range 
between 1.5 to 45 °C with at optimum at 30 to 37 °C. Temperatures above 50 °C have a lethal effect. 
Listeria can also slowly grow at refrigerating temperatures. It grows under both, aerobic and 
anaerobic conditions. Six species of Listeria have been identified (L. monocytogenes, L. ivanovii, L. 
innocua, L. grayi, L. seeligeri, L. welshimeri). L. monocytogenes is an opportunistic pathogen causing 
Listeriosis which is transmitted exclusively via consumed food. Listeriosis affects mainly pregnant 
women, immunocompromised patients, newborns and elderly. (Schmelcher, et al., 2010), (Food 
Standards Australia New Zeland, 2013).

2.2 Standard procedure for enumeration of microorganisms on seeds  
In order to quantify the surviving microorganisms of the seed surface after every plasma experiment 
seeds were subjected to the following procedure. Treated seeds were transferred from DCSBD device 
directly into stomacher bags with filter. Then, they were homogenized in stomacher for 3 minutes at 
room temperature to extract the bacteria from seeds surface. Buffer used for homogenization was 
Phosphate buffered Saline (1x PBS) in a ratio of 15:1 to the seeds. For example, 35 ml of PBS was 
used for homogenizing 2.5 g of seeds. Suspension was poured into Schott flasks. In order to prepare 
first decimal dilution, 100 ml of solution from Schott flask was added into an Eppendorf tube with 
900 ml of PBS. In order to prepare lower dilution, from this dilution after thorough mixing, 100 ml 
was taken and added to another Eppendorf tube with 900 ml of PBS. By repeating this process, the 
desired dilution series were made. 100 µL of desired dilutions or in case of using 150x15mm petri 
dishes, 1000 µL directly from the flask were inoculated on petri plates with suitable medium and 
spread with plate spreader to drying (Figure 5).  
 
Figure 5: Plating scheme of homogenized seeds suspensions (Reproduced from (Van Loon, 
2015)). 
Petri plates were subsequently incubated at conditions adequate to the studied microflora. Samples 
with E. coli were inoculated on Chromocult agar and incubated at 37 °C overnight. L. monocytogenes 
was plated on Oxford agar and incubated at 30 °C for 24 to 48 hours. In case of native flora, growth 
medium was PC agar and incubation conditions were 30 °C for 48 hours. Positive and negative 
controls specific for each experiment were prepared.  Plates containing 10 to 100 colony-forming 
units (CFU) were counted to determine microbial load on seeds. Each experiment was prepared in 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
two replicates and each replicate plated in duplicates. For calculation, first the plated duplicates with 
colonies between 10 and 100 were averaged, and CFU/g calculated based on dilutions on which 
colonies grew. Then the CFU/g of the independent replicates were averaged and Standard deviation 
was calculated. Microbial reduction expressed as decimal logarithmic was calculated as log of final 
CFU/g deducted from log of initial CFU/g given by the untreated sample. The colony forming units 
(CFU) were counted and compared with the control samples from the same day. It was due to factors 
such as unstable ambient air temperature and relative humidity, slight differences in processing time 
of Standard procedure, minor changes in incubation conditions and minor pipetting errors which 
might have an influence on results of efficacy of plasma treatment. 
2.2.1 Overnight culture  
In order to prepare fresh liquid overnight culture, E. coli DSM 1576 was inoculated and grown inside 
test tube with 9 ml Lysogeny broth (LB) at 37 °C overnight before every experiment. L. 
monocytogenes overnight liquid culture was prepared inside test tube with 3 ml Tryptic Soy Broth 
(TBS) + 1% glucose by cultivating at 30 °C overnight.

2.3 Pilot experiments 
In order to determine types of bacteria on seeds and evaluate influence of temperature, pilot 
experiments were conducted. 
2.3.1 Identification of naturally occurring microbial species on alfalfa seeds 
To support the literature-based characterization of naturally occurring microbial species on alfalfa 
seeds, microbial characterization of provided batch of seeds was performed.  
2.3.1.1 Detection of total mesophilic aerobic bacteria 
10 g of seeds were weighed and homogenized with buffer (PBS 1x) in stomacher for 3 min. 
Appropriate dilution series were prepared and 100 μl of dilutions were inoculated on non-selective 
PC-Agar. After incubation at 30 °C for 48 hours colonies were counted and CFU/g of seeds calculated.  
2.3.1.2 Detection of Listeria spp. and L. monocytogenes 
Detection of Listeria was done according to ISO method 11290-1:1996 (International Organization for 
Standardization, 1996). First, 100 ml from 225 ml selective enrichment broth are added to 25 g of 
seeds and homogenized in stomacher. Subsequently, the homogenate was added back into the 
bottle with the remaining selective enrichment broth and incubated for 48 hours at 30 °C. After 
incubation, approx. 10 μl of substrate from selective enrichment broth was streaked onto Oxford 
agar and ALOA agar using inoculation loop and incubated again for 48 hours at 30 °C. If typical 
colonies of Listeria spp. (Oxford agar) and colonies of L. monocytogenes (ALOA agar), they were 
inoculated on nonselective ½ BHI medium and cultivated at 30 °C overnight. Subsequently, sugar 
test, CAMP test and API Listeria tests could be performed to identify particular Listeria strains.  
2.3.1.3 Detection of Enterobacteriaceae, coliforms and E. coli 
10 g of alfalfa seeds were homogenized with 90 ml of dilution solution (VL) in stomacher and desired 
dilution series were prepared. Quantitative detection was done by streaking 100 μl of appropriate 
dilutions on VRBG agar which is a selective medium for bacterial group of Enterobacteriaceae. This 
was followed by aerobic incubation at 37 °C 24 to 48 hours. The next day, typical colonies were 
enumerated. For further simultaneous detection of coliforms and E. coli typical colonies were spread 
on Endo-agar and Chromocult agar and incubated aerobically at 37 °C for 24 to 48 hours. 
2.3.1.4 Detection of total number of spores and Bacillus cereus endospores 
1 g of alfalfa seeds was suspended in 9 ml of VL in test tubes and placed in preheated 80 °C water 
bath. When 80 °C was reached in reference tube 10 min holding time was started. Afterwards, closed 
tubes were cooled down under running water and 100 μl of appropriate dilutions were streaked on 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
non-selective PC agar and PEMB agar selective for Gram-positive bacteria. Typical colonies were 
enumerated and CFU/g calculated.  
2.3.1.5 Detection of Salmonella spp. 
25 g of alfalfa seeds was homogenized with 100 ml of non-selective enrichment peptone water, then 
added to the remaining of 225 ml buffer and incubated at 37 °C overnight. The next day 0.1 ml of this 
suspension was added to 10 ml of selective enrichment Rappaport-Vassiliadis Salmonella Enrichment 
Broth (RVS) and 1ml was put to another selective enrichment Muller Kauffmann tetrathionate Broth 
(MKTT). Both were incubated at 42 °C overnight. Afterwards, suspensions were streaked on selective 
Chromo-agar and XLD agar and incubated at 37 °C overnight. The next day, suspicious colonies were 
sub-cultured on non-selective PC agar and incubated applying the same conditions. In the end, 
colonies were tested with Salmonella bacteriophage (Schuppler, 2015). 
2.3.1.6 Detection of yeasts and molds 
10 g of alfalfa seeds was weighed and homogenized with 90 ml buffer for 3 min in stomacher. 
Appropriate dilutions were streaked on YGCB agar selective for yeasts and molds and incubated for 
72 hours at 30 °C aerobically. Number of yeasts per gram of sample was enumerated after 3 days, 
while molds after 4 to 5 days.  
 
2.3.2 Temperature measurements 
In order to determine the temperature on the ceramic plate during plasma treatment, fiber optic 
probe was placed on the surface of ceramic plate and temperature was measured at full plasma 
power density and reduced plasma power density, respectively.  
2.3.3 Influence of temperature 
To exclude the influence of temperature on inactivation of bacteria, dry heat experiments which 
approximate the conditions during plasma treatment were conducted. 5 g of dry seeds containing 
native microflora was placed inside the metallic cylinder through which flow of hot air was blown. 
The cylinder was placed in the water bath with temperature of 55, 65, 75, 85 and 95 °C, respectively 
and treatment time 10 min. The air was heated to the same temperature. After treatment, samples 
underwent Standard procedure as described in previous paragraph.

2.4 Optimization experiments 
After experiments of influence of temperature and identification of bacteria on the provided batch of 
seeds, optimization experiments could start. 
2.4.1 Effect of plasma treatment on artificially contaminated filters 
Filter 0.45 μm with grid served as a homogenous substrate for model bacteria (E. coli) on which the 
efficacy of plasma treatment was studied without the possible influence of heterogeneous seeds 
surface. Overnight culture of E. coli DSM 1576 was prepared and dilutions series 10-1 to 10-9 by 
mixing 900 μl of LB with 100 μl of E. coli solution in Eppendorf tubes were made. 200 μl of each 
dilution was streaked and thoroughly spread on the sterile filter in empty sterile petri plate and kept 
at room temperature for 30 min. Filters were plasma-treated for 1 to 10 min and in distance of 3 to 
10 mm from plasma discharge. Subsequently filters were placed on petri plate with Chromocult agar. 
Non-treated infested filters and non-treated non-infested filters served as the positive control and 
negative control, respectively. By inoculation of bacteria on filters and on petri plates with 
Chromocult agar, the capacity of filters to carry the desired load of bacteria as good as petri plates 
was tested. Samples were incubated at 37 °C for 24 hours.  
2.4.2 Effect of amounts of seeds per treatment 
Due to limited contact surface between seeds and plasma discharge on ceramic plate of the plasma 
discharge with dimensions of 80 x 200 mm, optimal amount of seeds per treatment which can be 
efficiently decontaminated had to be found. Samples of 2.5, 5, 7.5 and 10 g of alfalfa seeds 
containing only native flora were weighed into sterile conical tubes. Seeds were plasma-treated for 5 
min at full plasma power. Afterwards, they were transferred to stomacher bags and processed using 
Standard procedure. First CFU was counted on plates and then calculated per gram of seeds and only 
after log reduction was evaluated compared to untreated sample. Thus, the dependency on weight 
of seeds was excluded. 
2.4.3 Effect of mechanical stirring of seeds during plasma treatment 
The importance of active seeds stirring during treatment was evaluated. Sample of 5 g of seeds were 
plasma-treated from 5 to 15 min and stirred with sterile wooden toothpick constantly, poorly or not 
at all. “Poor” stirring was short time stirring performed every minute of treatment, while “constant” 
stirring was proceed constantly for the whole treatment time. After treatments, seeds were collected 
in stomacher bags and handled with Standard procedure.  
==PAGE:25==
25 
 
2.4.4 Effect of treatment time and plasma power 
In order to obtain values of most effective treatment time and power, experiments with variations in 
time and power were performed. Samples of 2.5 g and 5 g of seeds with naturally occurring 
microflora were weighed and plasma-treated for 1 to 15 min at full and reduced power, respectively. 
Each sample was stirred thoughtfully during treatment. Subsequently, samples were processed with 
Standard procedure. 
2.4.5 Effect of moistening of seeds prior the treatment 
In order to determine and confirm the hypothesis of previous experiments (Waskow, 2015) that 
increased moisture content of seeds might have a positive impact on bacterial inactivation, moisture 
experiments were performed. Samples of 2.5 g of seeds with naturally occurring microflora were 
weighed into conical tubes, moistened with MiliQ ultrapure water (125 – 750 µl) and kept for 3 hours 
in closed conical tubes on rotator to ensure thorough distribution of water on seeds. After, samples 
were plasma-treated with stirring for 5 min and full power and subsequently processed with 
Standard procedure. In this experiment, besides the untreated dry samples, treated dry samples 
served as a control.  
2.4.6 Moisture measurements 
Moisture content of wetted seeds was measured. Sample of 2.5 g of seeds were weighed and 
moistened with 125, 250, 375, 500, 625 and 750 µl of MiliQ ultrapure water, respectively. After 3 
hours on rotator they were placed inside heat-drying moisture analyzer. Wet weight was calculated 
by subtracting value of final weight from value of initial weight. Percentage of moisture was then 
obtained as a ratio of wet weight over initial weight, multiplied by 100.  
2.4.7 Effect of number of treatments 
Doubled treatment experiments were conducted to determine whether detrimental effect of plasma 
increases after application of plasma treatment multiple times on the same sample. Moreover, the 
hypothesis that although two mild subsequent treatments might have similar inactivation effect than 
one harsh treatment, germination abilities might be better preserved in case of applying milder 
treatment conditions was studied. 2.5 g of alfalfa seeds with naturally occurring microflora were 
moistened with 625 μl of MiliQ ultrapure water and kept for 3 hours on rotator. Directly after, seeds 
were plasma-treated applying mild treatment conditions of 0.5 min and 1 min and full power and 
reduced power, respectively. Subsequently, samples were collected in new conical tubes and stored 
at room temperature overnight. The next day, samples were again moistened with 625 μl of MiliQ 
ultrapure water, kept for 3 hours on rotator and treated applying the same treatment conditions of 
==PAGE:26==
26 
 
0.5 and 1 min with full and reduced plasma power. Then, samples were collected in stomacher bags 
and processed with Standard procedure.  
2.4.8 Effect of plasma treatment on artificially contaminated seeds 
In order to study plasma effect on model bacteria similar to those responsible for sprout-related 
outbreaks on the real substrate, 2.5 g of seeds with naturally occurring microflora were artificially 
contaminated with 250 μl overnight liquid cultures of E. coli and L. monocytogenes. At the same time 
seeds were moistened with 375 μl of MiliQ ultrapure water. Seeds were kept for 3 hours on rotator 
at room temperature. Right after, samples were plasma-treated for 5 min and full plasma power. 
Subsequently they underwent Standard procedure. Untreated infested moistened sample and 
untreated non-infested moistened seeds served as a positive and negative control, respectively. They 
underwent all procedures except for plasma treatment, so the influence of other factors could be 
excluded. 
2.4.9 Seeds germination experiment 
To study the influence of plasma treatment on germination abilities of seeds, germination 
experiments were performed. 2.5 g of seeds were weighed and plasma-treated for 0.5, 1, 3, 5, 10 
min with full and reduced power, respectively. 100 seeds of each 2.5 g sample were placed on a 
metallic grid with 100 spots. Paper towel moistened with Elix water using water atomizer was placed 
above the grid, another metallic plate was placed above the seeds in the spots and the system was 
flipped over. The paper towel then contained 100 seeds evenly distributed in a 10x10 square. The 
second moistened paper towel was placed above. Subsequently, the two papers with the seeds in 
between were wrapped into a roll and placed inside a transparent plastic container (Figure 6).

Samples were randomly distributed in the box, to avoid influence of the position in the box such as 
different access to water and light. The whole procedure took place inside laminar flow bench to

2.4.10 Effect of plasma on seeds topology 
To determine plasma treatment effect on seeds topology, samples were analyzed by Scanning

placed on alumina plate, fixed with conductive carbon and dried with vacuum. After that, seeds were 
sprinkled with platinum. SEM Leo 1530 with 2kV of acceleration voltage was used to carry out the 
images (Van Loon, 2015).

3.2.1 Effect of plasma treatment on artificially contaminated filters 
In order to study bacterial inactivation effect of plasma treatment on simplified substrate, membrane 
filters with pore size of 0.45 μm were used for experiments. These membrane filters are used in 
determination of purity of drinking water and they were provided sterile. Prior the experiment, filters 
were infested with liquid overnight culture of E. coli, kept for 30 min and then plasma-treated for 1, 
2, 3 min respectively, using full or reduced plasma power. Exclusively for this experiment, treated 
filters were placed in the distance (10, 5 and 3 mm) from the plasma discharge, instead of onto 
plasma discharge. All treated samples including samples treated with the mildest treatment 
conditions - for 1 min at reduced plasma power, at the distance of 10, 5 and 3 mm from discharge, 
respectively revealed total bacterial inactivation. Reduction value of more than 7.33 log was 
calculated theoretically, since all samples resulted in CFU/ml below detection limit. In the distance of 
10 mm from heated ceramic plate effect of heat can be excluded. Chemical stress due to cleaning of 
ceramic plate before and after each treatment with disinfectant can also be excluded because 
untreated infested control samples were also placed on the ceramic plate, without igniting the 
plasma. They revealed similar CFU/ml (3.8x109) as overnight culture (6.55x109). In case of filter 
placed 3 mm distance treated at reduced power revealed local burns. Images of experimental setup 
and filters can be found in Appendix D.

3.2.2 Effect of amounts of seeds per treatment 
Dimensions of ceramic plate and plasma zone ignited on its surface limit amounts of seeds that can 
be treated per treatment. If the amount of seeds which is placed on ceramic plate forms more than

covered by plasma. Moreover, alfalfa seeds placed in induced plasma tend to agglomerate due to 
higher electrostatic forces of plasma electrical field than inertia of gravitation of seeds as such. As a 
consequence, sample containing inadequate amount of seeds per treatment might be treated 
inefficiently. Thus, optimal amount of seeds was tested in relation to effective bacterial inactivation.

was also studied. 2.5 g of seeds containing native flora were moistened with different amount of 
sterile water (125, 250, 375, 500, 625 and 750 µl) kept for 3 hours and subsequently treated for 5

treatment.

3.2.8 Effect of plasma treatment on artificially contaminated seeds 
After significant 2.78 log reduction of moistened seeds (25 %) with naturally occurring microflora 
after 5 min full power one time treatment, the same experiment was conducted on artificially 
contaminated seeds. These seeds contained naturally occurring microflora with a level of approx. 104 
CFU/g on which artificial contamination was applied. For this purpose, 2.5 g of seeds were moistened 
with 375 μl of sterile water and inoculated with 250 μl of overnight culture of E. coli or L. 
monocytogenes. By applying both solutions the seeds contained desired 25 % moisture. After drying, 
samples were treated for 5 min at full plasma power, processed and plated on one selective and one 
non-selective medium.  
In case of L. monocytogenes, after 5 min treatment at full plasma power log reduction was calculated 
only theoretically (more than 5.05 log), since number of CFU/g was under detection limit on both 
selective (Oxford agar) and non-selective medium (PC agar). Overnight liquid culture of 
L.monocytogenes inoculated on seeds contained 108 CFU/ml. Untreated infested seeds which served 
as positive control revealed 107 CFU/g. 
In case of E. coli, after 5 min treatment at full plasma power 4.72 log reduction was achieved on 
selective Chromocult agar. Reduction of 4.86 log was observed on non-selective PC agar. Overnight 
liquid culture of E.coli with which seeds were infested was 109 CFU/ml. Untreated E. coli infested 
moistened seeds which served as positive control revealed 107 CFU/g on both selective and non-
selective media. Reduction of 4.72 log of E. coli and more than 5.05 log reduction of L. 
monocytogenes after 5 min full power treatment is significantly higher reduction compared to seeds 
containing native microflora treated under same conditions (2.78 log).

3.2.9 Influence of plasma on seeds topology 
To determine plasma treatment effect of DCSBD device on seeds topology, samples were analyzed by 
Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM). For this purpose, dry untreated sample and dry sample treated 
for 5 min at full plasma power density were prepared. At low magnification, images of treated 
sample revealed darker-looking coat stains, especially on the contact side with ceramic surface 
compared to homogenously appearing untreated sample. This might represent the rough surface 
caused by the exposure to elevated temperature and ion sputtering. At higher magnification 
differences in surface topology were even more significant. Treated sample revealed deep scratches 
which might be caused by ion bombardment during plasma treatment. Serious crevasses and coat 
perforation of treated sample was in notable contract with untreated sample with close and 
homogenous surface (Figure 17). Defective seed coat cannot serve its protective purpose, thus 
plasma can propagate inside the seed structures (Joshi, et al., 2011).

4 Discussion  
4.1 Discussion of limitation in the experimental setup 
The main challenge in experiments with microbial reduction was the obtained high standard 
deviation of present data points. This phenomenon may appear due to different initial load and 
composition of native flora in a gram of seeds, unsteady conditions of the environment such as 
ambient temperature and relative humidity, minor changes in incubation conditions as well as minor 
pipetting errors. Dependency on constant manual stirring of seeds during experiment might 
contribute to elevated standard deviation.  
Main challenge in germination experiments was to avoid contamination of seeds and other material 
with molds for which germination conditions were ideal for growth. It was also challenging to ensure 
homogenous but not direct exposure to light and to ensure steady ambient temperature. Plus, it was 
challenging to achieve high percentage of germinated control samples due to factors beyond control 
which influence germination, such as seeds growing hormones or other signaling molecules (Liu, et 
al., 2010)  
4.2 Discussion of effect of each parameter on microbial reduction 
Results obtained in this project indicate that process parameters such as treatment time, plasma 
power density, mechanical movement of substrate, number of treatments as well as substrate 
parameters such as amount of substrate, its homogeneity, and moisture have an impact on efficacy 
of plasma treatment. Optimal treatment conditions for seeds resulting in highest microbial 
inactivation were as follow:  
¾ seeds with artificially inoculated E. coli – 5 min, full plasma power density, 25 % moisture 
(4.72 log) 
¾ seeds with artificially inoculated L. monocytogenes – 5 min, full plasma power density, 25 % 
moisture (more than 5 log) 
¾ seeds with naturally occurring microorganisms – 5 min, full plasma power density, 25 % 
moisture (2.78 log) or 1 min, full plasma power, 25 % moisture, doubled treatment (2.90 log) 
4.2.1 Discussion of effect of treatment time and plasma power 
Not many studies of seeds decontamination have been made with the novel DCSBD type of plasma 
which compared to other types of dielectric barrier discharge generates macroscopically 
homogenous plasma at high power density without need of use of noble gases. The study of 
(Zahoranová, et al., 2015) is the closest in regards to treatment conditions as well as obtained results. 
Total (more than 2 log) inactivation of fungi was achieved after 2 min at full power and total (more 
than 1.6 log) reduction of bacteria after 5 min full plasma power, both present naturally on wheat 
==PAGE:44==
44 
 
grains treated with the same DCSBD device. In the study of (Deng, et al., 2007) performed with 
different DBD but also with high voltage (30 kV) and frequency (2000 Hz) E. coli artificially infested on 
almonds resulted in 5 log reductions after 30 s treatment. The study of (Mitra, et al., 2014) achieved 
1 and 2 log reduction of native microflora of Cicer arietinum with 2 and 5 min treatment at relatively 
high plasma power density of 10 mW/cm3 and voltage of 17 kHz. 
Treatment time and plasma power density seem to be determining factors in microbial reduction 
efficacy. As explained in the study of (Butscher, et al., 2016) of plasma inactivation of endospores, 
plasma power is determined by pulse frequency and pulse voltage. Plasma in reality ignites only for 
very short period of time in frequent intervals, what gives in macroscopic picture the impression of a 
constant homogenous plasma ignition. The interval of how often plasma ignites depends on the 
pulse frequency, while pulse voltage is responsible for the density of plasma-generated species per 
discharge cycle. Overall, efficacy of log reduction seems to depend on number of plasma 
microdischarges which is function of time and plasma power density thus, pulse voltage and 
frequency.  Higher plasma power density implies presence of more plasma-generated species what 
implies faster inactivation (Lerouge, et al., 2001). Increment of temperature with increasing pulse 
frequency and voltage might occur and should be considered in regards to bacteria inhibition. 
4.2.2 Discussion of influence of temperature 
Due to high plasma power density of this non-thermal atmospheric pressure plasma device which 
might raise the plasma gas temperature, thermal stress as a factor causing microbial reduction 
during plasma treatment was evaluated. Temperature measurements revealed temperature on 
ceramic plate surface to be 86 ± 1 °C (approx. 360 K) at highest plasma power density (input power 
approx. 400 W) and 80 ± 1 °C (approx. 353 K) at reduced plasma power density (input power approx. 
290 W). Several studies with seeds as a tested substrate e.g. researchers in the study of (Henselová, 
et al., 2012) estimated gas temperature on the same DCSBD device by Optical Emission Spectroscopy 
(OES) to be 300 ± 10 K (approx. 26 °C) measured at full power density (input energy 370 W), while in 
the study of (Zahoranová, et al., 2013) also by OES estimated temperature of the same device was 
determined to be 360 ± 20 K (approx. 86 °C) at full plasma power (input power 370 W). Study of 
(Černák, et al., 2009) reported 377 ± 18 K (approx. 103 °C) at 100 W/cm3 which with known plasma 
dimensions corresponds to full plasma power density (input power 400 W). Researchers in study of 
(Zahoranová, et al., 2015) measured gas temperature at input power 400 W which corresponds to 
highest plasma power density of DCSBD device identical to the one in this project and it was 
determined to be 370 ± 30 K (approx. 96 °C). In the same study, to refute the effect of elevated 
temperature it was argued that oil cooling system maintains temperature on ceramic plate below 
==PAGE:45==
45 
 
55°C. In addition to this argument, they claim, that seeds in mentioned studies were during plasma 
treatment in continuous motion, which might lower the actual seeds surface temperature.  
Since these papers did not give a clear picture about temperature created on the ceramic plate with 
ignited plasma, experiment of influence of temperature was performed in this project. Conducted 
tests resulted in maximum 0.46 log reduction in case of 95 °C hot air flowing around seeds in the 
metallic cylinder placed in hot water for 10 min. Air and water temperatures of 55, 65, 75 and 85 °C 
did not affect levels of microorganisms on seeds. However, in this experiment seeds were 
surrounded by hot air, not placed on hot ceramic plate, thus plasma treatment conditions were 
approximated, not mimicked and temperature on seeds during plasma treatment stayed unknown. 
Experiment with E. coli artificially inoculated on filters which were treated in distance of 10 mm from 
the discharge supports the negligible effect of elevated temperature in case the sample is placed in a 
distance from ceramic plate. It is assumed that at this distance, the temperature is close to ambient. 
Despite ambient treatment temperature bacteria were in this experiment efficiently inactivated. 
Treated samples resulted in CFU/ml that was under detection limit (more than 7 log). On the other 
side, filters placed only 3 mm from discharge got locally burned. Thereto, Scanning Electron 
Microscopy (SEM) analysis of treated seeds which were placed directly on ceramic plate revealed 
darker-looking stains on the contact side. One of the potential causes might be that seeds got locally 
burned or sputtered with ions. Parts of the seed which was not in direct contact with ceramic plate 
kept similar appearance as untreated sample. This would confirms the assumption that right above 
plasma discharge temperature drops close to ambient due to heat exchange with ambient air.  
Overall, it was concluded that even if temperature potentially plays a role in microbial inactivation, it 
is accompanied by other plasma-related effects, including plasma-generated reactive species or UV 
radiation and thus it is difficult to quantify the exact impact of temperature. Based on the results of 
filters placed 3 mm and 10 mm far from the discharge as well as results of SEM images the 
assumption that the impact of temperature might be discharge-distance dependent was suggested. 
However, it is an unconfirmed hypothesis. 
4.2.3 Discussion of types of microbial contamination 
Conducted experiments with seeds containing native flora revealed lower log reduction (2.90 and 
2.78 log) than reduction of artificially contaminated seeds (4.72 log). Determining factor may be the 
configuration of naturally present bacteria compared to artificially contaminated bacteria on the 
seeds. While native flora consist of various types of microorganisms and often creates biofilms 
(Izquier & Gomez-Lopez, 2011), which might be harder to inactivate, artificially inoculated bacteria is 
a single type of bacteria without a chance to create biofilms in short term from inoculation to 
treatment.  
==PAGE:46==
46 
 
Plotting obtained inactivation rates of seeds with native flora as a function of time illustrated 
biphasic inactivation characteristics, which is a model suggested by (Moisan, et al., 2001). They 
characterized this phenomenon as a fast decline in first minutes of treatment followed only by 
moderate reduction. The explanation to the first phase is an effect of UV radiation on directly 
accessible microbial DNA, while the second lower reduction is attributed to erosion. Erosion is a 
result of plasma-generated species which sputter seeds surface and accessible microorganisms (Von 
Keudell, et al., 2010). The study of (Fridman, 2008) suggests that erosion is elevated if more reactive 
species are available from the gas inlet and from available moisture of humid environment and 
substrate. 
Due to formation of biofilms and multiple layers of microorganisms, significant log reduction in first 
minute of treatment can be explained by exposition of first microbial layers to UV radiation, while 
layers which might be hidden in seeds morphological structures such as pits and gaps are inactivated 
after longer treatment time (Mitra, et al., 2014). As suggested by (Moisan, et al., 2002) in the study of 
biphasic inactivation curve, subsequent sputtering creates fissures and scratches and thus destroys 
these protective surface structures (Justan, et al., 2014). As a consequence, previously protected 
microorganisms might become exposed to plasma and inactivated.  
Experiments with artificially inoculated E. coli and L. monocytogenes on seeds resulted in highest 
inactivation rate from all experiments (4.72 log and more than 5.05 log). Although E. coli and Listeria 
spp. are in real substrates normally found in smaller amounts and surrounded by other 
microorganisms, eventually sheltered or incorporated in biofilms high reduction is a promising result. 
Study of (Lu, et al., 2014) tested E. coli and L. monocytogenes using atmospheric cold plasma with 
higher voltage of 70 kV. Study revealed greater sensitivity of Listeria treated for 5 s, but after longer 
treatment both types of bacteria were inactivated below detection limit. According to (Ermolaeva, et 
al., 2011), Gram-positive bacteria (Staphylococcus epidermidis, Staphylococcus aureus, Enterococcus 
faecium) were in general more resistant than Gram-negative bacteria (Pseudomonas aeruginosa and 
Escherichia coli) to plasma sterilization effects, but study also reveals that the susceptibility was in 
case of Gram-positive bacteria species-specific. To the contrary, the study of (Lu, et al., 2014),  argues 
that while in case of Gram-positive L. monocytogenes, reactive oxygen and nitrogen particles diffused 
the membrane with thick peptidoglycan layer and cause irreversible DNA changes, Gram-negative E. 
coli with outer membrane of lipopolysaccharides and proteins was more resistant to different 
environmental stresses including oxidative stress. 
4.2.4 Discussion of effect of moistening of seeds prior the treatment 
 Above mentioned erosion might be an explanation for higher, 2.78 log inactivation of seeds with 
native flora with elevated moisture compared to dry samples (1.58 log) achieved in this project. The 
==PAGE:47==
47 
 
environment with higher moisture can be an additional source of reactive oxygen species such as O2
-, 
O3, OH-, HO2
- and H2O2 which are created during plasma induction. These reactive species are 
discussed to be the most important inactivation agents in non-thermal plasma (Fridman, 2008). The 
trend that higher moisture content resulting in higher log reduction was observed. To the contrary of 
theory of erosion and our findings stands the study of (Bruggeman, et al., 2010) which suggests of 
increased moisture leads to quenching of plasma, thus lower inactivation rates are achieved. 
Moreover, spores harboring the surface may with increased moisture germinate and become 
vegetative bacteria which are more susceptible to plasma inactivation agents (Finn, et al., 2013). 
Darker coloring of suspension of moistened seeds compared to bright suspension of dry seeds could 
be explained by harsher conditions during plasma treatment due to more reactive species or else, 
simply by more dissolved bran. Another assumption for higher log reduction of moistened seeds was 
conceived in this thesis. It was suggested that the microstructures such as gaps and pits which serve 
as shelters for naturally occurring microorganisms might by moistening get flooded, thus 
microorganisms can float onto the surface and be inactivated. However, it is an unconfirmed 
hypothesis.  
4.2.5 Discussion of effect of number of treatments 
Two treatments with different conditions revealed similar log reduction of native flora – 5 min, full 
power resulted in 2.78 log and 2 times 1 min full power revealed 2.90 log. Due to biphasic 
inactivation curve, first minute of treatment in performed experiments revealed most significant 
reduction. Second treatment of 1 min again revealed steep decline. It was suggested that first short 
time treatment might inactivate first layers of microorganisms, disrupt biofilms and might erode 
some surface structures. Second short time treatment finishes inactivation of veil-less 
microorganisms floating from eroded structures. However, during second plasma treatment plasma 
might also penetrate inside the seed which might affect DNA and proteins of seeds. Thus, 
germination abilities can be changed. This hypothesis was confirmed when germination tests of 
double plasma treatment revealed that seeds lost the ability to germinate.  
4.2.6 Discussion of germination 
While obtained results indicated that DCSBD device can efficiently inactivate native flora as well as 
artificial contamination, effect of plasma on germination abilities revealed unsatisfactory results. In 
all cases germination was either suppressed or postponed by plasma treatment. Highest yield (15 %) 
was achieved with treatment of 1 min at reduced plasma power, with grown sprouts of approx. half-
length compared to untreated samples. However, germination in longer treatments or at full plasma 
power was not observed probably due to the same mechanisms as bacteria inactivation. As shown in 
project of (Waskow, 2015) once pulse frequency reaches certain threshold decrease in germination is 
==PAGE:48==
48 
 
observed. Effect of reactive oxygen and nitrogen species causing erosion and UV radiation damaging 
DNA and proteins might be too harsh for preservation of germination abilities. Study of (Waskow, 
2015) excluded influence of temperature on germination by heated gas treatment. Seeds were 
treated at 60–90 °C. Only temperature of 90 °C revealed decrease (25 %) of germination abilities, 
lower temperatures resulted in germination abilities of 70 – 80 % identical to control sample. 
As shown in several studies, treatment time and plasma power governed by frequency and voltage or 
as given in the study of (Butscher, et al., 2016) by number of plasma microdischarges have the 
biggest impact on germination abilities. The impact of temperature also should be considered. The 
study of (Henselová, et al., 2012) using the same DCSBD device from Roplass revealed enhanced 
length and weight of maize sprouts after 60 s treatment. Study of (Zahoranová, et al., 2015) revealed 
increased weight and faster germination after applying 20 – 50 s DCSBD treatment on wheat seeds. 
Longer treatment time led to inhibitive germination effect in both studies. In the study of (Mitra, et 
al., 2014) of chickpea (Cicer erietinum) achieved after 1 min significantly increased (89.2 %) 
germination compared to control (60.8 %). However, chickpea, maize and wheat are more spherical-
like shaped, have bigger size and more complex topology, compared to kidney-like shaped, smaller 
more homogenous alfalfa seeds. Thus, less contact with heated ceramic plate and less accessibility to 
seeds structures can be potential explanations for more successful results. Alfalfa seeds germination 
abilities seem to be negatively affected if exposed to temperatures above 55 °C (Kim, et al., 2006). 
On the other hand, study of (Kitazaki, et al., 2014) suggests that heat together with radiation and 
oxidative stress from radicals can initiate the secretion of growth hormones and accelerate 
germination. However, the optimal levels of these factors remain unknown. 
4.2.7 Discussion of substrate geometry and surface 
Filters or other flat substrates with artificially contaminated bacteria compared to seeds with native 
flora reveal higher reduction due to several factors. According to studies of (Hertwig, et al., 2015), 
(Schnabel, et al., 2012), (Butscher, et al., 2016) bacteria on flat substrates are exposed to plasma-
generated species and UV radiation to a greater extend, than bacteria sheltered in seeds surface 
microstructures such as pits and gaps. Moreover, flat geometry of filters compared to spherical 
geometry of seeds seems to be more approachable to plasma decontamination effects (Lerouge, et 
al., 2001). Almost no difference between bacterial concentration of overnight culture in the test tube 
and concentration revealed on infested untreated filters indicates that membrane filters are able to 
carry the entire load of inoculated bacteria and are suitable matrixes for transport and treatment of 
model bacteria for short period of time. 
==PAGE:49==
49 
 
4.2.8 Discussion of amount of seeds and stirring effect 
Samples of 2.5 g treated for 3 min on ceramic plate of dimensions of 80 x 200 mm revealed 
significantly higher reduction than sample of 5, 7.5 and 10 g. sample of 5 g after 15 min treatment 
revealed similar log reduction as 2.5 g sample after 10 min treatment. The differences in inactivation 
of bacteria on seeds surface in function of amount of seeds can be explained by layering of seeds and 
consequent inaccessibility of seeds to plasma-generated decontamination effects. This phenomenon 
can be eliminated by using smaller amounts of seeds per treatment which spreads on ceramic plate 
homogenously and thus forms only single layer of seeds. Seeds agglomeration during treatment 
which might be caused by strong electrostatic field can be significantly alleviated by mechanical 
stirring.  
4.2.9 Discussion of the efficacy of DCSBD and previously used DBD setup 
Previous studies conducted in the same laboratory of Food Microbiology at ETH Zurich with type of 
DBD device with different geometry operating at lower power density revealed different microbial 
inactivation results as well as germination results.  
Experiments of (Van Loon, 2015) achieved highest 2.04 log reduction of native flora of alfalfa seeds 
after 15 min 8kV and 10 kHz. Artificially applied E. coli DSM 1576 was reduced 2.27 log after 10 min 
treatment at voltage of 10 kV and frequency of 10 kHz. Germination abilities were enhanced after 3 
min treatment to 83 %. Untreated sample revealed 73 % germination. Reduced germination of 42 
and 48 % was observed after 5 and 10 min treatments, respectively.  
Experiments of (Waskow, 2015) revealed 1.66 log reduction of native microbiota on 17 % moistened 
alfalfa seeds after 10 min treatment and 2.51 log reduction of E. coli artificially contaminated on 
alfalfa seeds after 10 min treatment. Both experiments were performed at voltage of 8 kV and 
frequency of 10 kHz. Germination abilities decreased with increasing time, from 75.3 % to 66 % (4 
min), 33 % (8 min) and 27 % (10 min). SEM images of topology of DCSBD-treated sample revealed 
deeper scratches and fissures and surface significantly more performed than DBD-treated sample 
which can be a cause of the loss of germination abilities. DBD device performed only mild 
modification of seeds coating which could lead to increased permeability for nutrients and water and 
subsequently higher germination rates (Sera, et al., 2008). Overall, results obtained by performing 
experiments with DBD revealed lower log reductions but better kept the germination abilities.

5 Conclusion  
Sprouted seeds have in recent decade become a popular food item for its perceived health benefits 
(Studer, et al., 2013). From the microbiological viewpoint this fresh produce food item consumed raw 
can be a source of human pathogens and thus it can pose a health risk to the consumers. Sprout-
related outbreaks in the past caused many deaths and severe illnesses. Conventional 
decontamination methods have their limitations, from decontamination efficacy up to regions where 
the use is authorized. Novel decontamination method – non-thermal atmospheric pressure Diffuse 
Coplanar Surface Barrier Discharge (DCSBD) plasma as an alternative to conventional methods was 
successfully tested in this project. Pathogenic bacteria responsible for sprout-associated outbreaks in 
the past as well as naturally occurring microbiota on alfalfa seeds could be effectively inactivated. 
However, necessity of seeds germination posed a challenge for this plasma treatment method. 
 
The aim of the project was to investigate the feasibility of sprout seeds treatment in the DCSBD 
system. While the amount of microbiota should be lowered, the germination and growth abilities 
should stay unchanged or even enhanced by the treatment. For this purpose the process parameters 
such as treatment time, plasma power density and number of treatments as well as substrate 
parameters such as seeds moisture content and types of microorganisms on its surface were varied. 
In order to gain the insights into the underlying inactivation mechanisms, the inactivation of bacteria 
on flat substrates was investigated.  
Up to three log reduction could be achieved in the experiments of inactivation of microbiota 
naturally occurring on seeds. Up to five log reduction revealed the treatment of artificially inoculated 
Escherichia coli DSM 1576 and Listeria monocytogenes WSLC 1001 on seeds and more than 7 log 
reduction of artificially contaminated E. coli DSM 1576 on the flat membrane filters could be 
obtained.  
Determining process parameter in efficacy of plasma treatment was overall number of plasma 
microdischarges which is controlled by treatment time and plasma power density. Among other 
influential factors belong moisture and geometry of the substrate and number of treatments. Main 
factors responsible for microbial decontamination in non-thermal atmospheric pressure plasma 
operating with ambient air can be reactive oxygen and nitrogen species, UV radiation and ion 
sputtering. Unlike other DBD devices, in DCSBD influence of temperature could not be completely 
excluded due to higher plasma power density (pulse voltage and pulse frequency) generated in this 
device. Chemical stress due to cleaning agents applied before and after every sample was excluded 
as well as mechanical stress during seeds retention on a rotator. 
==PAGE:51==
51 
 
Plasma generated in DCSBD device had a negative effect on germination abilities of alfalfa seeds in all 
tested conditions. It might be caused by plasma-generated species sputtering the surface, UV 
radiation irreversibly damaging DNA and proteins of seeds and elevated temperature. Scanning 
Electron Microscopy images betrayed darker-looking stains on the contact side of their kidney-like 
shape of treated sample.  High magnitude images revealed complex system of crevasses and 
perforated seed coat.  
Studies from other researchers conducted with the same DCSBD device achieved enhanced seed 
germination of different sprouting seeds including pea, wheat and maize. Although these studies 
reported that DCSBD treatment is a suitable alternative to conventional seeds decontamination 
methods, high plasma power density and heated ceramic surface seems not to be tolerable for 
certain shapes and sizes of sprouting seeds such as alfalfa seeds.  
Advantages of DCDBD and other non-thermal plasma setups are short treatment time, low energy 
consumption, avoidance of use of toxic compounds or production of toxic by-products, simple 
handling and operation, authorization to use in Germany, Denmark, Belgium, The Netherlands and 
Switzerland (Fridman, 2008), (Scholtz, et al., 2015). It is safe to operate and scalable for unlimited 
dimensions of substrate. Based on other studies, plasma can enhance weight yield of sprouts and 
even shorten the sprouts growth time.  
While previously performed studies with DBD device offered moderate microbial reduction and 
preserved higher percentage of germination abilities, DCSBD setup resulted in higher log reduction 
but germination abilities declined. Thus, DCSBD might find great application as a decontamination 
treatment of food which does not require germination and also face issues with microbial 
decontamination such as fresh-cut salad or ready-to-eat products.

6 Outlook  
As revealed in conducted experiments, small changes in treatment conditions can have a significant 
influence on treatment efficacy. Thus, some of the experiments of treatment factors which revealed 
promising results should be carried out in bigger extend so the clear conclusion about their influence 
can be drawn.  
In order to obtain a list of microorganisms surviving plasma treatment, next to non-selective media 
the homogenized suspension should be during Standard procedure also inoculated on selective 
media.  
Due to presence of molds identified on seeds in pilot experiments and presence of mold 
contamination on seeds in germination test it would be useful to study reduction of molds on alfalfa 
seeds.  
Viability assay could be used to reveal whether bacteria were during plasma treatment inactivated or 
only harmed (Ehlbeck, et al., 2011).  
Based on obtained promising reduction of artificially contaminated seeds in this project, experiments 
should be repeated and inactivation curve should be obtained.  
Different types of sprouting seeds, in particular heavier, more spherical-like shaped of bigger size 
should be tested due to strong electrical field which is created in DCSBD. This way, necessity of 
stirring of sample due to agglomeration of small light seeds can be omitted. Else, installation of 
oscillator which would keep the sample in constant motion would ensure more uniform treatment 
(Zahoranová, et al., 2015). 
Effects of UV radiation alone should be tested in DCSBD. Although it was studied in low power 
density DBD, DCSBD operates by orders of magnitude higher power density which can lead to higher 
effect of UV irradiation towards bacteria inactivation (Laroussi & Leipold, 2004). 
Influence of different gas composition which was not tested in this project could be a way of 
improving microbial reduction and eventually enhance germination due to penetration of active 
oxygen molecules in inner structures of seeds, which might lead to faster nutrients decomposition 
and thus increased yield of germinated seeds (Zhou, et al., 2011). Germination abilities could be also 
increased if dry seeds are first plasma-treated and only after treatment moistened and germinated. 
According to (Randeniya & de Groot, 2015), (Volin, et al., 2000) air plasma can introduce hydrophilic 
thin layer which can cause increased water uptake abilities and consequently lead to faster 
germination. 
Atmosphere, including relative humidity of the environment in which plasma treatment is conducted 
should be controlled, since several studies (Bai, et al., 2010), (Bai, et al., 2011), (Gaunt, et al., 2006) 
suggest that ambient air with added humidity can as such enhance the plasma effectiveness. The 
==PAGE:53==
53 
 
study of (Hayashi, et al., 2011) of molecular changes of plasma-treated seeds showed the ability of 
plasma containing water to affect the composition of thiol compounds in living cell. Thiols are known 
for their participation in cell division and growth. Promising results from experiments with moistened 
seeds in this project should be developed further; especially shorter treatment times should be 
analyzed. 
Germination experiments should be continued beyond time dedicated for germination test in order 
to determine whether germination abilities were abolished or only postponed.  
Although the research conducted in this project proposed a few ways of improvement of microbial 
decontamination, to optimize alfalfa seeds decontamination without negatively affecting 
germination abilities more fine testing and tuning of plasma treatment should be conducted.  
Generally accepted methodology for investigation of microbial reduction should be developed for 
more convenient comparison of decontamination efficacy of different plasma setups.
