Abstract 
 
The goal of this master's thesis is to demonstrate the benefits of improving building envelope 
design approaches by improving the way houses/apartments are installed together. The 
improved design solutions described in this thesis result in a practically zero-cost solution, 
which is also convenient for prefab-houses manufactures to adopt. 
 
The aim is reaching a Nearly Zero Energy Building, by improving major parts in the house 
and how they installed together in easy way for prefab-houses manufactures to adopt, 
reducing the energy consumption and heat loss. 
 
This report investigates the following six elements; Wall, corner foundation, strip foundation, 
roof, venting hatch and window.  
After that analyzing thermal bridges in simulation software COMSOL for our solution, 
and finally evaluate the whole house using IDA-ICE.   
 
 
To meet the requirement for the wall: 5 different solutions were created.  
Solution 1: Solid Wood profile 45*300mm (300mm Rockwool) with total heat flux 0,1 W/m2K
 
 
including the thermal bridge effect 0,008 W/m2K  
Solution 2: STEICO SJ45 I profile (300mm Rockwool) with total heat flux 0,096 W/m2K 
 
 
including the thermal bridge effect 0,0048 W/m2K 
Solution 3: Masonite I profile (300mm Rockwool) with total heat flux 0,096 W/m2K 
 
 
including the thermal bridge effect 0,0047 W/m2K 
Solution 4: Normal I profile (300mm Rockwool) with total heat flux 0,097 W/m2K including
 
 
the thermal bridge effect 0,005 W/m2K 
Solution 5: Masonite I profile (410mm Rockwool) with total heat flux 0,075 W/m2K including 
 
 
The thermal bridge effect 0,0019 W/m2K 
The fifth solution was the best one to continue with. 
 
The heat loss coefficient for foundation under the external wall was calculated as DS418-
2011 Annex D, including the chosen wall and floor which includes (300mm air gap with I-
profile every 40cm, 500mm Rockwool and 100mm stones), the simulation showed a Linear 
heat loss coefficient ψ 0,13 W/mK and it is realistic and good results. 
 
We used strip foundation under the internal wall. The simulation showed a small thermal 
bridge effect (0,015 W/m). 
 
The roof- wall junction has 45mm massive wood construction (beams and columns) every 
1,2 meters, while the rest is insulation 
So, we make two simulations (two for rockwool only (with and without the outer corner) and 
the same for the massive wood), after that we calculated the weighted thermal bridge which 
is 0,021\ W/mK.

The venting hatch is made of two insulating elements made of 15 cm of gas filled foam with

1- Introduction 
Background 
Because of its large effect on many facets of the atmosphere and human activity in houses, 
global warming has gained considerable interest in recent years. Energy use for heating and 
cooling is one sector specifically having an influence on climate change. 
We have a legally binding target to reduce these emissions and make EU a climate-neutral 
by 2050 under the European Climate Law 2018. [1]  
  
“Globally, the situation is similar. Buildings absorb about 35% of the world's resources, 40% 
of the world's energy, 12% of the world's water supply, and create almost 40% of global 
greenhouse gas emissions.”. [2]   
 
“Today, out of 2,6 million homes, there are 1,1 million single- family homes in Denmark”. [4] 
[3] 
 
Several initiatives have been adopted to reduce the energy usage of houses. This involves 
steps carried out through constructive means to resolve the components of ventilation, 
heating, and cooling. The biggest efforts have been to minimize energy consumption by 
passive means in houses. This includes the materials used for building pieces, where the 
elements of the structure are the floors, walls, roof, and windows have been strengthened, 
for example.  
  
The problem is not completely solved, thermal bridges in framed windows and venting 
hatches, and the intersection between different elements for example: wall-roof and wall-
floor.  
For example, in buildings that are not sufficiently insulated, the thermal bridges showing a 
low comparative loss (typically under 20%) as total losses through roofs and walls are quite 
high (around >1 W/m2K).  
However, when they are insulated very well, the amount of losses attributed to thermal 
bridges is high (higher than 30%) as total losses are quite low (lower than 0.3 W/m2K). [5]  
  
This thesis focuses on discussing the viewpoint of making the proposed energy framework 
of 27 kWh/m2 official, by demonstrating ways of enhancing traditional one-story single 
Danish house It might be incorporated into designs that will have a positive effect on the 
energy framework. [6] 
  
Prefabricated houses will minimize up to 52% of waste production, according to industry 
analysts. Also, it can be extremely insulated. This strong insulation retains heating and 
cooling levels, helping reduce the air-conditioning loads in the warmer regions, and heating 
expenses in the colder parts of the world. [7]

The Aim 
The aim of the thesis is to develop prefab modular based single-family house with a very low 
energy use and a low cost. The specific aim is to investigate the possibilities to develop 
building envelope constructions with a very small effect of the thermal bridges on the 
transmission heat loss of the building. 
Furthermore, the aim is as follows:  
● To prove that nearly-zero energy homes can be constructed based on energy saving 
features and without any Products of renewable energy on the house. 
● To illustrate that prefabrication does not only save on construction costs, but also 
increases efficiency and results in lowered costs over the building's lifespan. 
● To develop prefab houses with frame-less windows with very low energy 
transmission  
● Suggesting and developing solutions for the issues of the thermal bridge in the 
assembly of plane construction elements.  
 
It also sheds light on how those ideas will impact the energy usage in the house, make it 
easy to implement for the construction companies, and the construction process to achieve 
near-zero energy building(nZEB).  
 
2- State of art 
2.1 Thermal bridges  
“Researches have revealed that the effects of cold bridges in well-insulated buildings can, 
depending on the junctions of the construction parts windows, walls, and foundations, 
increase annual energy consumption for heating between 14% and 50%.” (Saxhof et al., 
1993). 
 
A thermal bridge is an isolated building with a slightly lower insulation standard than the 
building itself. This ensures that more heat would flow through the cold bridge per surface 
than the rest of the house. [8] 
 
There are three types of thermal bridges [8]: 
1. A shift in orientation or variations in the surface of the envelope of the house. At 
edges and corners, additional heat losses arise. [8]

carried between the cold and the warm side, at penetrations for installations, 
constitute constructive cold bridges (e.g., pipes). [8] 
 
Figure 2- Building envelope penetrations 
3. A special type of thermal bridge that occurs in a certain regular pattern is a systemic 
cold bridge, so that the effect can be included in the construction's one-dimensional 
heat loss, e.g., Wall-ties, joists in a wall. [8]

3.2 Analyzing thermal bridges using COMSOL 
 
The study of the buildings' thermal values was carried out in a simulation program called 
COMSOL software.  This software using the Finite Element Method (FEM) to solve multi-
physical field fusion. The procedure for each investigation consists of designing the identical 
construction in Revit and then integrating it into COMSOL, which produces a mesh.  
  
The numerous meshes generated in the project include several “Partial Differential 
Equations (PDEs)” that explain heat transfer and provide an effective answer to the problem.  
The heat transfer equations in COMSOL are based on the Heat Flow Act of Fourier. The 
thermal flow relies on thermal conductivity and thermal diffusivity. [14]

 pg. 8 
Through COMSOL we will calculate the 2-D element total heat flux with an area X m2, and 
we subtract the 1-D element for the full insulated part (without frames) multiplied with the 
same area X m2, therefore we will have the extra heat loss.  
And the temperature difference between indoors and outdoors is 1K in order to simplify the 
simulation.  
“Normal Heat Flux used for 2D drawings in COMSOL, which can calculate the heat flux in 
the two directions, while total heat flux magnitude calculates for the three dimensions” 
[COMSOL help].   
3.3 Indoor climate and energy simulation by IDA-ICE 
 
It is a creative and accurate full-year, comprehensive and complex multi-zone simulation 
framework for the study of thermal indoor temperature as well as the energy usage of the 
whole house.  IDA ICE's physical models represent the new analysis and best available 
models, and the calculated results are well correlated with the measured evidence.  
  
IDA Indoor Environment and Energy (IDA-ICE) is a modern kind of modeling tool that brings 
performance building to a different stage. Precisely modeling the house, its equipment, and 
controls – maintaining the lowest possible energy usage and the highest possible comfort for 
the inhabitants. [15] 
In IDA ICE we use the area definition called ‘overall internal’ which means that:  
Overall internal: As internal, but includes also the cross-section area of the inner walls and 
slabs that are connected to an external surface. Half the cross-section area is attributed to 
each adjacent zone.[32]

9- Thermal Analysis for the venting hatches 
9.1 Description 
For designing a venting hatch considering how to escape without tools and the minimum of 
the thermal transmission see Figure 51 and Figure 52. 
 
The venting hatch is made of two insulating elements made of 15 cm of gas filled foam with 
a thermal conductivity of 0.022 W/mK. This results in a center U-value of 0.072 W/M2K. With 
some thermal bridge effects of the edge of the venting hatch I hope that we can have a total 
U-value of less than 0.1W/m2K similar to the insulated wall. 
The airtightness of the elements to the frame is made of magnetic seals like those used in 
refrigerators see Figure 52. 
 
The inner element is opened manually by pulling in a handle and left open during all summer 
period – or the period with need for ventilative cooling. 
The outer elements are opened and closed and positioned in a wanted percentage of 
opening by a chain motor that is installed in the top of the revealed frame.  This controls the 
ventilative cooling. 
Outside the outer element a security grill for protection against rain and burglars is placed. It 
has an opening of 75% equal to the effective opening of the hatch 
 
Security opening as a rescue opening is possible by the following steps: 
1.      manually pull open the inner element 
2.      turn the handle on the center of the outer element to release the coupling of the 
element to the chain motor and pull the element open 
3.      open the security grill using simple handles and push it to the outside.
