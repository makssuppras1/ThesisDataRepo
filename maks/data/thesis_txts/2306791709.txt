Abstract 
This thesis is based upon a conceptual 3D model, which stocks layers of information 
usable for a renovation. It considers primarily usable layers of information and not the 
construction of the model. The main topic covers; obtaining point clouds from different 
3D scanning technologies and how BIM models can be generated from their data.  
Tests of the generated BIM models shows it is possible to obtain an accuracy within 1cm. 
This thesis presents how point clouds can be obtained through photogrammetry for 
establishing a frame to draw BIM models upon, and furthermore how high resolution 3d 
thermographic models can be generated with an overlay from a high resolution RGB 
camera. 
Finally, a conceptual idea for digitalization of cracks on masonry walls is presented;  
The method and approach gives a renovation project an overview of cracks upon a 
building.

3 PROJECT DESCRIPTION 
This Thesis will mainly make investigations in the process from 3D scanning to generation/auto generation 
of a BIM1 model. The main goal of this thesis is to state how far towards a final BIM model it is possible to 
come with current software on the marked. Additionally, this thesis will investigate how layers of 
information could be added for a renovation purpose. 
3.1.1 
Why is this interesting? 
This is interesting because a BIM model is essential for the parties and will serve as basis for further layers 
of information. Furthermore, the scanning to BIM process is much desired in regards to the legacy building 
stock where BIM models are not available [1], [2]. 
3.1.2 
Questions that will be answered during this thesis are: 
• 
How does 3D scanning work? 
• 
What programs are currently available to generate BIM models from 3D scans and how good does 
they work? 
• 
What is the common use for 3D scanning’s? 
• 
Why is 3D scanning so important and when is it beneficial? 
• 
What layers of information could be implemented in a Registration model?

How will the thesis be made? 
The thesis will rely on examples from real life, done in collaboration with the Surveying Company LE34, as

4.2 THE GOOD RENOVATION, THE GOOD START, THE GOOD REGISTRATION 
When a building is deemed for renovation, the registration process is essential to be sure that all problems 
of the building are a part of the renovation. Several parties usually make registration of buildings for 
renovation, e.g. surveyor takes geometrical measurements, architects makes sketches and engineers 
records; cracks, tomographic pictures and moisture content. This means that each party conducts their own 
registration for their specific part of the project. Furthermore, [7] states that stakeholders obtains 
measurements, sketches of layout and classifications of building elements e.g. wall type, door type etc. 
during visits at the site, thereby stating that the registration of the building is an important part of a 
renovation. 
To achieve a good renovation, it is important to get a good project start. 
Additionally, no good start of a renovation project will come without a good registration. 
A way of collecting all surveying data could be a hosting BIM/3D model with layers of information for all 
parties. One person could make the digital registrations and the model could be used for all parties, far 
away from the site itself. Additionally, the survey should be fast and non-destructive. 
The survey could be made with a time interval to monitor the changes of the building, e.g. Crack 
development, Moisture absorption or if a post insulation have been beneficial.

4.3 DOES AUTO GENERATION OF BIM MODELS MATTER? 
[1] and [2] both acknowledge the importance of a Scanning to BIM software for capturing as-built 
information not only for renovation purposes, but also for archiving legacy buildings.  
Furthermore [1] states that the Government of the United Kingdom of 2011 have decided that by 2016, all 
building proposals are required to be “…fully collaborative BIM, with project and asset information, 
documentation and data in an electronic format.” This increases the demand for BIM models for old 
buildings, deemed for renovation.  
The praxis today, is that the majority of Revitization2 from Point clouds is outsourced to save costs; this is 
also the praxis at LE34.  
      70% of the time is used for reconstruction of the BIM model, while only 30% is used on the scan itself 
[7]. However, to get a deeper insight into this topic and correlate the time spent with the cost, a rough 
estimate of the cost through the workflow (scanning to BIM) have been studied.  
To elaborate how an auto generation could matter in this process, the prices LE34 uses are converted to 
factors and used in this study (Appendix 1). 
 (Please mind the real prices have been anonymized to a factor for competition purposes. 
The real prices have been seen and exist, please contact Søren Nørmølle at LE34 for further questions.)

5 3D LASER SCANNING 
5.1 HOW THE LASER WORKS. 
The 3D laser scanner (3DLS) is basically a distance-measuring and direction-logging tool. [10] 
The distance is calculated by sending a laser beam towards a target of interest, then the laser beam is 
reflected back to the 3DLS and the “time of flight” is accounted for. 
By correlation of the time, with the speed of light (299.792,458 m/s) [11] 
The distance can be calculated by [11]: 
𝑑𝑑= 𝑣𝑣∗𝑡𝑡 
 
Where 𝒅𝒅 is the distance, 𝒗𝒗 is the speed of light, and 𝒕𝒕 the time of flight. 
 
The time is established by atomic clocks, which typically is caesium clocks. 
The caesium clocks is accurate until two nanoseconds a day. [12] 
Furthermore, correlation of the beams direction with the distance, a point in a polar coordinate system can 
be established and afterwards converted to a Cartesian coordinate system. [13] 
All the points is made into a “point cloud” which then can be edited and used for drawing BIM. 
Today several kinds of 3D laser scanners is on the marked. For small objects (For scans performed closer 
than a meter to the object) typically the laser and the sensor is two separate modules and a static setup is 
made where the object is moved around. 
For 3D scanners for buildings and larger objects, the sensor and laser is in the same tool however, this kind 
of setup is typically not usable under one meters to the object. [10] 
Two kinds of 3DLS is on the marked, the common and cheap one is called “laser pulsed based” (Figure 11) 
and works like “time of flight” methodology. This method is time-consuming and usually only used for long 
distance scans (>120m). Another kind is called “Laser phased shift” (Figure 12) this kind has the possibility 
of measuring the phased shift between the outgoing and returning laser beam [13]. Light is particles sent in 
a phase, when it hits something the phase changes of the returning beam [14]. By measuring this change, it 
is possible to achieve a higher accuracy compared to the laser pulsed based. [10] 
However, the “Laser phased shift” has a limited range compared to the “laser pulsed based”. 
The scanners used in this thesis is: 
• 
ZEB-1 Geoslam – Pulse based (Appendix 3 for mail correspondence with geoslam) 
• 
Faro 3D Laser Scanner – Phase based [15]

5.2.1 
E57 
Because the plentiful range of file formats, some tend to use the open source based (E57) which works well 
across platforms (Hardware/Software). 
E57 is commonly used open source based file format for point clouds  
A committee of the industry (laser scanner hardware and software developers and consultants) developed 
the format. 
The committee decided for five requirements for the standard [17]: 
1. “Reliable interoperability – The data should be transferable from any vendor to any other vendor. 
2. Documented, unrestricted, and vendor neutral. 
3. Low barrier for adoption – The development cost for adopters should be kept to a minimum. 
4. Minimalist design – The design should be kept as simple as possible, while achieving the required 
goals. 
5. Open – The standard and its implementation should be made openly available and should be well 
Extensibility – The design should be extendable so that new capabilities can be added in the future 
without breaking the core functionality.”  
Many laser scanner manufacturers have chosen to integrate the E57 format into their newest versions of 
their software. This list includes InteliSum, Inc., Leica Geosystems, Optech, Inc., Quantapoint, Inc., Riegl 
Laser Measurement Systems GmbH, Trimble Navigation Ltd. and Zoller+Frӧhlich GmbH. [17]

5.3 3DLS SCANNING METHODS 
5.3.1 
ZEB-1 Geoslam. 
The ZEB-1 from GEOslam, is a fast way of achieving a point cloud of a building etc. 
The technology is called Simultaneous Localization and Mapping (SLAM) and works by surveying the 
surroundings and orientates itself in space. The ZEB-1 uses a MEMS Inertial Measurement Unit (IMU), the 
IMU logs the laser scanner (see Figure 14) 6 degrees of freedom, by doing so the 2D laser survey can be 
converted to a 3D laser survey, when the laser scanner is oscillating. [13] 
One of the main differences to the stationary scanner is that RGB data is not collected. 
So for merging the cloud, geometric shapes should be used, like spheres or boxes etc.  Since the shapes can 
be recognized/found in a point cloud editing tool. 
 
Figure 13 - ZEB-1 Geoslam in use 
 
Figure 14 - ZEB1 Geoslam build [18] 
The accuracy is +/- 0,1% [19] of the largest distance in the room. 
This means: “..if scanning a small room or office, 5m width, the accuracy could be as high as 5mm.  
However, if scanning a large warehouse or facility, 50m width, the accuracy could be lowered down to 
50mm (5cm).”(Appendix 3) The experience from LE34 is however that the error usually is within a couple of 
centimetres, regardless of the length of the room (Appendix 5).

5.3.1.1 Pre scanning 
• 
A stationary environment makes the best point clouds, meaning an environment where people and 
things move decreases the accuracy. 
• 
While scanning, it is important to make as many loops as possible and stop the scanning where you 
started the scan. This helps the scanner software make the point cloud and achieve data of the 
location several times. 
• 
The ZEB1 has problems with uniform geometry, like long corridors; in these cases objects should be 
placed to help the scanner software distinguish the scanned data, the objects could boxes or 
spheres. 
• 
Furthermore problems could display, if there is a lot of a windows and mirrors etc. (because of 
reflections and refractions3), In such a case the surfaces could be covered. 
• 
The maximum time a scan can be performed is 30minutes, meaning the scan is split afterwards and 
should be merged in a program afterwards. 
• 
Finally, it is good to plan a route beforehand to optimize the scanning and time usage.

5.3.1.2 During the scan 
The ZEB1 needs the operator to oscillate the scanner by moving the scanner head forward and back in a 
constant motion approx. one swing back or forward each second. 
The ZEB1 does not have a GUI on a screen to help the user. Furthermore, it does not have any options to 
change the settings of the scan. This however, can be changed by walking slow while surveying. [13] 
The ZEB1 has however a series of LED blinker codes, which each has a meaning (Figure 16). 
This is somewhat confusing if an unknown code shows while surveying. 
However the important once is: Green, which means data, is being logged as it should and Green/Orange 
which means the scanner motion is too fast.

5.3.3.1 Pre scanning 
Initially, it is important to establish what the subject is and what the usage for the point cloud should be  
There is a big difference in terms of obtaining accuracy for a BIM model to be used in relation to facilities 
management and accuracy of a scan of details/ornaments to make casting moulds. 
The accuracy and usage of the scan decides which kind scanning product/method to be used and how many 
scans/density of the point cloud are to be made. 
Next establishment of routes and placements of the scans should be made to avoid “scanning shadows”  
Scanning shadows is the area behind a scanned object.  Imagine a light pointed towards a object, behind 
the object a shadow will appear, this is similar to a scanning shadow. 
Make sure it is not raining; the scanner cannot be used while it is raining due to refraction of water 
droplets.

scanner will arise, within one meter radius of the scanner (Figure 26)  
3. Place references and if possible place them in different levels and lengths.

4. Make the Survey. 
5. Make sure your references is in view (preview the panorama)

5.5 GEOLOCATION OF POINT CLOUD BY GNSS 
For making sure the point clouds and BIM model is fitted within a global coordinate system, Global 
Navigation Satellite system (GNSS) references could be used. 
Systems under GNSS is as follows: GPS (American), GLONASS (Russian), Galileo (European) and several 
more. Each is independent from each other, but all relies on the same method.   
The satellites Is in orbit around the earth, varying between the systems from 19130 km (GLONASS) to 
23222km (Galileo) in height.  [24] 
The satellites logs its own position at a certain time, and sends it to the GNSS receiver. 
The receiver knows how fast the signal is travelling (approx. The speed of light) and the time is logged with 
atomic clocks which are precise as much as 2ns a day. 
However, this could still give error of as much as +/- 2.3meters, due to time is relative [25]. 
This is why a constant recalibration of these clocks insures a somewhat constant accuracy.  
 From this knowledge, a distance of the satellite can be established and because the location of the 
satellites is known, a sphere is established for each satellite. With the use of Trilateration,12 the intersection 
of these is where the receiver is placed, in theory as seen on Figure 42. [25] 
 
 
Figure 42- GNSS Positioning diagram [26] 
5.5.1 
Corrected Data and Real Time Kinematic 
Anomalies in the signal path between the satellite and the receiver, mainly due to space weather13, cause 
an error in the time of flight, which affects the positioning accuracy. By adding a base reference station or 
stations, this error can be reduced and accounted for. The base station is positioned at a known spot, 
where the exact placement is known, and the theoretical time of flight can be calculated. By comparing the 
theoretical calculated time and the obtained time of flight from the satellites, the error/deviation can be 
converted to correction data for the anomalies. 
  This correction data is then possible to download for the RTK14 [27] (See Figure 43) 
GPSnet.dk is a subsidiary to LE34, this company makes the correction data for RTK’s in Denmark the other 
company, which transmits correction data, is Leica SmartNet. 
The base stations for GPSnet.dk is seen on Figure 44. 
At the best case, the RTK can be within 2cm of error. [28]

5.6 OTHER WAYS OF GEOREFERENCING 
A total station (TST) could be used to established references from known points. 
The TST, uses a laser to measure distances, the TST furthermore shows the horizontal and vertical angle of 
the of the Laser beam. This knowledge can be used in several ways to calculate the location of an unknown 
point. Two ways of doing this, is as follows: 
5.6.1 
Traverses 
 
Figure 45 - Traverses, depiction of method 
The method behind traverses is to make a polygon consisting of known targets and unknown targets and to 
measure the angles and distance in every point between the previous target (back sight) and the next 
target (forward sight). 
The surveyor needs to start in a known target (B), making a back sight to another known target (A). 
Afterwards the surveyor are able to set up an unknown target at a desired place (1) and make a back sight 
to the known target (B) and a forward sight to another unknown target. 
This procedure is made for unknown target 2 and 3 as well. 
==PAGE:31==
 
Digital registration of buildings for renovation 
Thorbjørn Jørgensen  
 
Side 30 af 121 
 
The traverses final target should be in a known target (C), from this point the surveyor have the option of 
making a forward sight to another known target (D) or if possible directly to target B. 
If it is possible to make a survey from C to B, the survey from B to A and C to D could be neglected. 
In addition, a survey from C to B would used instead. [30] [31] 
From the measurements made during the survey, the unknown targets can be geolocated by formulas. 
The formulas is further described in [25]. 
5.6.2 
Resection  
The resection has the purpose to establish the location of an unknown target (P on Figure 46). 
3 known targets is as minimum required but 4 known targets should be used (M,A,N,B Figure 46),  in order 
to establish over determination of the unknown location. 
The total station is setup in the unknown target (P) and angles between the known targets is noted. 
The resection has the basis of the angles (V1 and V2 Figure 47) made under line segments. 
From the line segments, arise circular arcs, which at their intersection states the location of the unknown 
target. 
The precise location of the unknown target relies on the circular arcs do not fit on top of each other.  
If they fit on top of each other, the situation is called the “danger circle situation”. 
 
 
Figure 46 - Principle of resection [31] 
 
Figure 47 - Intersection of circular arcs [31] 
If there is made measurement of distance during a resection it is called “Free Stationing”. 
The free Stationing is solely dependent on two known targets for overdetermination, if two angles and two 
distances is measured in an unknown target.  
Because of the distances, the problem with the “danger circle situation” does not exist. [31] 
From the measurements made during the survey, the unknown targets can be geolocated by formulas. 
The formulas is further described in [25].

6.2 IMPORTANT WHEN MAKING PHOTOGRAMMETRY 
This section is mainly based upon experience within LE34. 
The following is Important to achieve good data for processing to point cloud: 
1. Overlaps between pictures 
For stitching pictures together and intersecting several rays, the software needs to see similar 
pixel geometry on different pictures. 60%-80% overlap between pictures is the norm. [36] 
2. Specific geometry for references 
It is hard for the program to distinguish surfaces if the pixel geometry is too similar e.g. white 
facades and hallways.  
3. High resolution and sharp pictures. 
Each pixel could be one point in a point cloud, less points means a coarser point cloud. 
4. Ground control points 
Several GCP’s helps to achieve a Global geolocated point cloud in the right scale and 
furthermore increases the accuracy of the point cloud. 
Alternatively, distances of objects could be used for stating the real world/image relationship. 
5. Moving objects  
Moving objects like cars, people or leaves etc. can make the merging of the pictures hard. 
6. Weather 
Photogrammetry cannot be performed during night or in hard sun. 
The hard sun makes deep shadows, which result in black areas without good pixel geometry.

of LE34) depending on the project size, the density of the point cloud and accuracy. 
 
• 
After the survey is performed, the logged reference point format and Flight log format is changed 
so the photogrammetry software (Pix4D) can read the files. 
 
• 
In the software Pix4D everything is stitched together via mosaic (pixel geometry) recognition [13] 
and the point cloud is processed. 
The output from Pix4D is a point cloud in a chosen format, an orthophoto or a DSM20. 
 
• 
Afterwards editing can then take place, e.g. removal of obstacles outside the area of interest.   
 
• 
The file is then processed converted into a point cloud file format that Autodesk programs can 
handle (.rcs) after this, the point cloud can be used in Revit for construction of a BIM Model. 
6.4 PROS AND CONS OF PHOTOGRAMMETRY 
The benefits of photogrammetry and especially aero triangulation, is that it fast and cheap to survey large 
areas, furthermore the point cloud obtained from photogrammetry, has as default RGB information. 
Basic models can be made easily without a big investment in equipment. 
However, the result is not that precise, LE34’s experience states that it is possible to obtain a mean error 
within 1cm and a max. Error below 5cm and is highly depended on good data e.g. Clear high-resolution 
pictures, several GCP points etc.  
Lastly, photogrammetry is usually not performed during night or hard sun.

7.1 WHAT TECHNOLOGY TO USE? 
In the following section, a short description of the different uses a point clouds is stated. 
1. Photogrammetry by drone  
a. Volume estimation 
Volume estimation is typically used in quarries, for estimation of the output and keeping 
track of the output in quarries. Thereby there is no need to guard the quarry, to keep track 
of truckloads leaving the property. The drone survey will state whether the calculated 
output is right or if unaccounted truckloads have been leaving the quarry. 
b. Exterior for Point cloud for BIM generation 
A common problem with obtaining point cloud for BIM generation is data of buildings roof 
or hard to reach places. Here the use of a drone is typically used to obtain such data. 
If the precision of point cloud is not too important, the drone could also survey the facade 
as well.  
c. Point cloud of large fields/areas 
At LE34, it is common praxis to make point clouds of large open fields, e.g. Golf courts, 
Train tracks and ruin cities. In this manner, it is possible to achieve point clouds fast and 
cheap which is fairly accurate. Alternative ways of achieving this data would be much more 
time consuming and might lack an overview.

8.1 OBTAINING BIM MODELS ON RENOVATION PROJECTS 
In the majority of larger newly built buildings, the drawings originate from BIM models.  
The praxis of using BIM models within a renovation project is not common, but when it is needed, it has 
four options: 
1. Either a BIM model is raised from old 2d drawings  
2. The building is surveyed by total station

of the point cloud or by auto generation of BIM models in a program.

8.3.6 
DURAARK Software 
The DURAARK software (Durable Architectural Knowledge) is a project, which is funded by the European 
Union and several European universities takes part in the Project. 
The lead beneficiary is however CITA from The Royal Danish Academy of Architecture (KADK). 
The purpose of the project is to construct a software which can recognize meaningful structures and shapes 
and automatically reconstruct a BIM model from an indoor point cloud.  
Furthermore, the software should on sight generate electric plugs and wiring routes for these, which is 
highly valued by stakeholders. [38] 
The software also tries to cope with lack of computing power by making a subsampling of the raw point 
cloud, which reflect the computer, the software is used on. 
Because the project is a publicly funded project, the file formats and software used has to be as open 
source as possible. Therefore, the only point cloud format supported is .e57 and the output is only .ifc (both 
is open source).  
Furthermore everything is written within a Linux environment, making it hard if not impossible to work 
with on a Windows machine, this is however known by the DURAARK team [7].  
The approach of the software is to generate infinity long surfaces (Figure 108) of the scan and by an energy 
minimization approach, the walls is found  (Figure 109) [2]. It works by having two costs: unary costs for the 
assignment of labels to faces and binary costs for pairs to adjacent faces. Unary costs guides where rooms 
or outside areas are located and binary costs guide the selection of sufficient edges for separating 
differently labelled faces. [39]

8.3.9.2 Scores for comparison 
To compare the different programs a partly objective scoreboard is made for each program. 
Please mind due to several possibilities of setting tolerances, the default settings is used for benchmarking. 
Making the score a result of “out of the box” capabilities of the software tested, this means a better result 
might be possible with other settings. 
The scoreboard consist of the following subjects (Appendix 14 for reasoning):  
8.3.9.2.1.1 Number of clicks 
The number of clicks is counted from the start of the program to the generated BIM model is 
final (without clean-up and editing of the BIM model). 
 
The real office room consist of 15 linear walls, without curtain walls. 
For the programs, which generates walls by clicking on the point cloud e.g. start and end of 
wall, the number of clicks for one walls is multiplied by 15 walls. 
 
Please note a lower number of clicks is beneficial. 
 Correctness of generated walls 
The correctness of the generated walls, states how many walls has to be edited after an 
generation, in percent. (15 correct walls generated out of 15 walls is 100%.) 
If walls is wrongly generated and must be deleted this counts as -1 wall. 
This only considerers the placement of the generated walls and not the thickness of the 
walls. This score will state how many “fake” walls is generated and how many is missing. 
This means walls which needs to be extended or edited is not a part of this score. 
 Mean deviation of generation  
The mean deviation states in percent how far the generated walls is from the real life 
measurements. (100% is no deviation.) 
If there is no option in the software to generate thicknesses of walls from the point cloud 
this will limit the maximum score to 50, since an edit of walls is due afterwards.

9.2 SCAN TO BIM OF BUILDING 118 
 
Figure 152 - Setting up the scanner at the 118 
 
 Figure 153 - Building 118 at DTU, Red arrow pointed at 118 
Building 118 at DTU was chosen for a 3D scan to BIM case study. This is due to a typical office building size 
and layout. Furthermore, it was possible to get an already built BIM model from CAS to compare the results 
with.  
Measurements made within BIM models, generated from the different scans, can be found in Appendix 16. 
9.2.1 
Purpose 
The purpose of this case study, was to have an actual on hand experience with the whole process “Scanning 
to BIM” moreover, to show what problems could arise during the process. 
Lastly what the deviation between of an already built BIM model and the obtained point cloud is. 
Please note this is not a test of how accurate the generated BIM model is, in regards to the real world. 
9.2.2 
Method 
Two scans was performed over a three-day period. One was of the northern façade, this was named scan 1 
and the other is of the southern facade which is named scan 2 (location is seen of Figure 153). 
The final (scan 3) was made around the whole building. 
The scans was performed without georeferenced targets, thereby the scans is made in a local coordinate 
system and mainly spheres of 139mm is used as targets, solely for merging of point clouds. 
Due to a lack of possibilities for placing targets in different levels, most of the targets is placed on the 
ground. 
Furthermore, targets, which had a high error, have been unmatched if possible, to risen the certainty of the

A thermographic camera works very similar to a normal camera. 
The main differences is the spectrum the camera captures. 
Where a normal camera only captures the visible spectrum (Figure 193). 
A thermographic camera captures the infrared heat radiated from objects, which is outside the visible

10.2 CRACKS 
Another common problem for old buildings is cracks. 
Several problems could cause cracks in the building, however the crack itself often gives clues to where the 
problem is located and what the cause is. 
Therefore, a good overview of the cracks could give vital information to diagnose the problem. 
Today, the typical way of surveying cracks is by manually by taking a stack of pictures and making notes to 
each one. E.g. Length, Width, location.  
This practice is time-consuming and it is hard to gain an overview especially in larger projects. 
Several studies within the field ( [44] [45] [46] [47] [48] [49]); registration for cracks by photos have been 
made, however most only covers concrete structures with a homogenous texture and most relies on edge 
detection (search for change in colours of pixels) as method and when a test is performed on arbitrary 
textures the cracks is much harder to find. Some of the studies mention bridges [44] and tunnels for/and 
subways [49] as a plausible user for such a product. One study covered cracks upon masonry structures; the 
conclusion was however that due to the pattern and roughness, noise was generated along with the cracks 
[50]. No commercial product was found. 
But also Laser scanners have been used for crack detection. [51] States when scanning cracks with 
distances less than 7,5 meters, it is possible to achieve errors within 1.1mm.  And with a distance of 5m and 
a scan angle of 0° it have been possible to minimize errors under 0.015mm. 
Furthermore the study showed that the orthogonal distance to the wall is of much higher importance 
compared to the angle of the scan towards the wall.

10.2.1 Visit at the National Road Directorate 
To get ideas from one way of digitalizing cracks, a visit was made to the Danish National Road Directorate. 
The National Road Directorate, has several ways of surveying the Danish roads, some of these involves 
scanning for cracks in the pavement by lasers. 
This visit had the purpose to understand how the equipment works and how the survey was performed. 
 
Figure 201 - Picture of ARAN (Vejdirektoratet) 
The ARAN stands for “automated road analyzer”. The vehicle is made to survey the state of the roads and 
in the end to make a conclusion for a client, if a renovation of the road is due.  (See Figure 201 for 
elaboration) 
The car logs the following: 
• 
Road in Pictures by HD Camera 
• 
Tilt of the car by 4 gyroscopes. 
• 
Roads texture and plainness by laser 
• 
Location of ARAN, by 2 RTK GPS receivers. 
•

To solve the problem of digitization of cracks on buildings, a program has started its development at the

10.2.2.1 Goal of program 
The goal was to construct a program, which could handle panorama orthophotos of walls with cracks. 
From these photos, information of the cracks could be computed. Length, Width as well as EXIF geolocation 
to assist the user locate the found cracks. The program could be used to give the user an overview of the 
cracks of the building, but also to make a periodical survey of the cracks to follow their development over 
time. The main difference between Wisecrax and the praxis at National Road Directorate is the use of RGB 
pictures instead of Infrared pictures. This should lower the investment in equipment, for a potential user.  
The following features will be a part of our program: 
• 
Serial definition of exclusion zone based on selected HSL properties 
• 
Recognition of individual cracks with spines (giving properties) 
• 
Grouping of cracks 
• 
Transparent step-by-step approach

10.2.2.3 Definition of bricks, joints and cracks 
To find the Bricks, Joints and Cracks, the colour information of the picture is used. 
There is several ways of describing colours; the way used in this program is HSL, which consists of Hue, 
Saturation and Lightness. 
HSL could be explained as a cylindrical shape (Figure 208), where HUE is the angle of the cylinder from the 
angle 0°-360°. Saturation is the diameter of the cylinder, and the Lightness the height of the cylinder. 
 
Figure 208 - HSL Methodology [53] 
Hue, could be considered as the “pure” colour, without tone nor tint. 
This states the colour tone of the item e.g. Bricks will be around 50°. 
Furthermore, please mind the joints and cracks is not possible to state with Hue alone. 
 
Figure 209 - HUE Depiction [54] 
Saturation, states the amount of pure colour with a number from 0 (no pure colour) to 1 (only pure colour). 
An example is the joints of the brick wall, where the number is close to zero. 
 
Figure 210 - Saturation, red as an example [55] 
Lightness, states whether the colour is dark or light, from a number 0 (dark) to 1 (light). As an example the 
cracks of the bricks wall, could be mentioned as close to zero.

10.2.2.4 How the length and width of a crack is established. 
To establish the length of the crack, the program splits the crack into segments (Figure 212).  
The program needs to decide whether to do this horizontally or vertically. 
If the bounding area of the crack has a distinct horizontal aspect ratio, the segments will be vertically slices 
arranged next to one another. If on the other hand the crack bounding area appears more vertical, the 
slices will be horizontal. 
The aspect ratio threshold for this process is 1:1.5 and 1.5:1. If the bounding area fits within both, it is 
deemed "almost square", and the secondary aspect is shown with a red colour. 
 
Within each segment there is computed a centre of gravity (COG), based upon the area (Figure 213). 
 
The centre of gravity for each of these segments add up to form a spine, which ideally approximates the 
shape of the crack. (Figure 214) 
 
Note the pink lines at Figure 215 perpendicular to the spines. 
These resemble 2d-raycast [56] lines going outwards starting at their centerpoints. The ray’s starts 
perpendicular to each centerpoint and follow the crude normal that can be constructed from the line 
between that centerpoint and the next. Once the rays hit a pixel which is not considered part of the crack 
body, the width at that centerpoint can be determined. 
 
Next, a search for nearby cracks is performed. This is done by computing the distance between 
centerpoints at the ends of each spine against one another. When the distance is below a set threshold, the 
two cracks are grouped.

10.2.2.7 Further development 
The program was constructed with the thought of: if it is possible to see the crack with human eyes, it 
should be possible to extract it by a program. 
However, a better filter have to be constructed, to sort the “fake” cracks from the real cracks. 
This could be done by telling the program that cracks found close to horizontal should be deleted.

brick. Afterwards a probable path between cracks and bricks could be generated. By coding, the program to

10.2.2.8 Early versions of the software 
An early version was based on RGB30 data translated into pixel lightness alone. It was discovered that other 
dark areas had properties that was falsely found to resemble cracks.  
At that point, it was decided to extend the criteria for picking out cracks in an image.  
Although HSL and RGB tend to contain an equal amount of data, it is worth mentioning that where we are 
interested in examining the saturation and lightness, it makes sense to translate the colours into this 
format. This process isolates the property of tint or colour into the concept of "Hue". 
Additionally from a human perspective, it is much easier to relate to HSL vs. RGB values. 
E.g. RGB values of absolute black is 0,0,0 and grey is from 1,1,1 to 254,254,254.

11 INSTRUMENTAL UNCERTAINTIES AND ACCUMULATED SOFTWARE ERRORS 
All the hardware used in this thesis, have errors within their measurements.  
While the GEOslammer states +/- 0,1%, it is likely to be more. 
The error of the FARO 3DLS, is stated to be within 2mm. But larger errors might occur, depending on the 
merging of point clouds and errors arising from refractions, reflections and the difference  in air 
specifications e.g. temperature.  
Furthermore, the “Scan 3” in the case studies, shows that errors occurs and should be looked for. 
The resulting error from photogrammetry is much depending on good high-resolution pictures and  
how the pictures is taken. Furthermore, with several references e.g. measurements taken on site or/and 
geolocated targets, the error decreases. However, it is hard to describe the error obtained by 
photogrammetry, but at LE34 it is typically hard to get below 5cm as max. error.  
 
The scanning to BIM software showed very different resulting error. However, by using the DURAARK 
software or the Edgewise software, it is possible to obtain BIM models within 12mm.  
Additionally, the generated BIM model is much dependent on accurate input in shape of point clouds. 
12 SUMMARIZED CONCLUSION 
12.1 3D LASER SCANNING 
3D laser scanning is in constant development and this thesis is only a snapshot taken at this the moment. 
Compared with the old praxis of using a total station the 3DLS gives the user several measurements. And 
gives the user a better and faster base for drawing up e.g. BIM models or deviations of a plane surface.  
However, sometimes the point cloud contains errors as “SCAN 3” in the case studies shows. Therefore, it is 
important to examine the point cloud for obvious errors afterwards.  
Additionally, it have been discovered that it is hard to find software support for GEOslam point clouds. 
Furthermore, the support of Retro marks and prism is still not possible for the software/hardware, this 
could increase the precision and make exact measurements for a specific point stated by the user in the 
field.  
12.2 SCAN TO BIM 
The DURAARK and Edgewise software, both generates good BIM models with few deviations. 
However, human interaction and pre-processing e.g. merging of scans and post processing e.g. Removal of 
fake walls, is still somewhat needed. 
This demands the user to have an insight of the building, to edit the right walls. 
At the moment the software performs best with point clouds, without furniture and trees etc. 
This means a better IA for cancelling points from furniture and better automatization would be beneficial. 
This cancelling process is however already a part of the DURAARK study. 
Additionally a software, which can merge, edit and generate BIM models as a whole, would ease the 
workflow and lower the files transferred between programs.  
Furthermore, much of the cost and time of a 3D scan to BIM process is put into the revitization of the point 
cloud. This states much could be saved, with the use of auto generation software. 
In addition, a DURAARK test case, shows that it is already beneficial to auto generate BIM model from point 
clouds, compared constructing BIM models from common method.

13 OUTLOOK 
13.1 3D LASER SCANNING 
The 3DLS, is likely to be more advanced in the future, a built in RTK could geolocate the scan stations and 
could make geolocated references less important. 
Errors might be produced due to reflections and refractions, a correction for this would be highly valued. 
Additionally, it is desired to investigate the initial point clouds in the field to look for errors e.g. reflections 
or missing targets, this might be possible in the future with the already established link by WiFI, USB etc. 
 FARO, have just released a Scan localizer (Figure 225), this should be seen as a competitor to the GeoSLAM. 
The Scan localizer is mounted underneath the FARO 3DLS and the tripod is mounted on wheels. 
The scan localizer has a built in IMU similar to the Geoslammer, this helps the scanner make the point cloud 
in real-time, when moving around. The accuracy is still not stated, but if the precision is close to a static 
scan, it could very much compete to the Geoslammer and expand the usability for the FARO 3DLS.  
 
Figure 225 - Commercial for the Scan Localizer [60]

much more precise and if searchable. Clusters of high heat transfer could be found fast. 
Additionally an auto extraction of prisms or retro marks could expand the use and make the use of total 
station pointless and additionally establish references on site for use over a period. 
New ways of scanning could while moving could be by drone, this could reduce the demand for human

13.3 PHOTOGRAMMETRY 
Through the last couple of years the method of obtaining point clouds by photogrammetry have risen. 
This is among other things, due to the birth of drones for the consumer market. 
The drone can independently survey fields alone by setting GPS waypoints. 
This method will most likely be much more developed during the next couple of years. 
 Not only will the advancement in drones make them capable of surveying more independent from a 
human pilot [64], but also the things, which the drone is capable of surveying, will expand e.g. Cracks, 
Thermographic models etc. In addition, with a steadily increasing accuracy of navigation for the drones e.g. 
GPS, Altimeter etc. the precision of the survey will increase.
