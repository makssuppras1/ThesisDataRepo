1 Abstract 
 
This thesis investigates the quantification of the building transformation potential 
used as a design parameter for renovation in the early design stage.  
 
The transformation potential is an indication of the convertibility, adaptability, and 
flexibility in a building. It presents the buildings ability to transform and be used for 
other purposes in future life-scenarios. It also presents to what degree the building 
components can be exchanged and dissembled when building systems should be 
upgraded. The transformation potential is relevant for the decision making in the 
design process and in the stage where the building shall either be renovated or 
demolished.  
 
The baseline study is a thorough reading of literature referring to building 
transformation. The important aspects of the study are presented in a preliminary 
literature review. The gathered knowledge on the area is used to create a framework 
for an inventory of transformation indicators presented in selected literature, such as 
spatial dimensions and layout of the structure in the building. The indicators are divided 
in categories, levels, and stages to cover the different dimensions of transformation. 
The result of an inventory is a literature matrix which presents a list of categorized 
indicators next to the number of supporting literature. The inventory creates a basis for 
the selection of relevant design indicators. The selected indicators together with 
selected measurement methods and rating indexes are used to create a transformation 
capacity tool. The influence of the indicators on a total transformation capacity score 
is incorporated as weightings based on the number of supporting literature presented 
in the inventory.  
The rating scale for the indicator scores is adapted from SAVE which is an existing 
assessment instrument used to map and register the value of preserving urban 
environments and buildings. 
The transformation capacity tool is tested and evaluated through a case study. The 
case study is an extensional building to the existing hotel and conference center named 
вҖңGreen Solution HouseвҖқ which is located in RГёnne on the island Bornholm. The Green 
Solution House and the extensional building is designed by the architectural firm 
3XN/GXN. The structurally system in the extensional building consists 100 % of CLT 
floors and decks. The quantification of the transformation capacity and visualization of 
the of the indicators performance showed how the hotel is well suited for a future 
transformation only limited by the fixed structural system.    
The limitations, advantages and continued investigations of the transformation 
capacity tool is discussed in the last part of the thesis.

4.1 Global challenges 
 
On a global scale, serious problems are scaling up.  The world population is growing 
rapidly and is by UN expected to be 9.7 billion in 2050 (UN, 2019a). The world is at the 
same time getting more urbanized meaning that half the population is living in urban 
areas today and that this ratio will raise to two thirds in 2050, if the current trend 
continues. This invokes a responsibility for the way the cities develop. (UN, 2018) 
 
Another challenge is the way we are exploiting the earthвҖҷs resources to an extend that 
will lead to limited availability of natural resources in the future and which clearly 
impacts the environment in a wrong way. UN Environment list the current issues and 
consequences of the development:

4.2 Challenges in the building industry

The global challenges do partly reflect in the rapidly growing building sector and the 
challenges connected to this growth. The building sector does by now generates large

4.2.2 Waste of resources in the building industry 
 
In Denmark, a demolition share has been allocated to the municipalities with the 
purpose of demolishing more buildings. The reason is that some building has been 
vacant for too long. The vacancy can be explained by the causes, that the buildings 
either/or lay in non-attractive areas, have a low market value, are in a bad condition, 
contains hazardous materials or have too much debt. B. Гҳsterby et al. 2019 argue that it 
is a problem that there is no clear definition of a building suited for demolition why the 
buildings are left to long for vacancy. This consequently accelerate the degradation of 
the buildingвҖҷs condition and lower the reuse potential of the building materials.  
(B. Гҳsterby et al., 2019)   
 
The renovation of a building versus the demolition often becomes a question of money, 
which also relates to the expected standards for a building in use. Buildings is by others 
expected to be energy optimized, robust in their structure, have a pleasant indoor 
climate and contain non-hazardous materials. Other reasons for not choosing to 
renovate the building are pointed out to be the arrangements in the building, which are 
not suitable to the user demand, or the non-attractive location of the building. (R. P. 
Geraedts & J. M. Van der Voordt, 2008) 
 
Morelli et al. present how an energy optimization through renovation of a multi-family 
building from 1850вҖ“1930 results in larger cost savings than in the case of a demolition. 
(Morelli et al., 2014) 3XN/GXN has also through practical project cases and research 
shown how money can be saved and the CO2 emission reduced by extending the use of 
building materials and the components rather than demolishing them. One example is 
the redesign of Quay Quarter Tower, a 49-story high-rise located in Sidney, which 
succeeded in the savings of 130.000.000 AUD and 7.5005 tons of CO2 by reusing 50% of 
the existing buildings structural system compared to completely tearing down the 
building and building up a new one. (Guldager Jensen et al., 2016)

4.3 Building transformation 
4.3.1 Initiatives taken to solve the problem 
 
Literature and case studies from the movement/approach вҖңOpen BuildingsвҖқ, started by 
the architect John Habraken in 1961, access how buildings can be designed for flexibility 
and be adaptive to the users changing needs and new building technology. This includes 
design rules and parameters set for flexibility but without methods for quantification 
and measurements. (Friedman & Krawitz, 1998; Habraken, 2002; Kendall, n.d.) 
 
Certification systems as BREEAM and DGNB are currently to show the degree of 
robustness and flexibility in new and old buildings through a performance assessment, 
based on graded criteria. The included criteria set the frame for a building which is 
adaptable and flexible in its design and can sustain necessary changes through a 
lifetime. For example, the criteria which include proper structural dimensions. The 
performance assessment is not dedicated to any particular stage in the buildingвҖҷs 
lifecycle and are hereby a list of criteria which can be used as a checklist during a design 
process or be used to assess and conclude on a complete building design. (BRE Global, 
2016; DGNB, 2020) 
 
Research projects from technical universities in the Netherlands also seek to find 
methods to determine and quantify the Adaptive Capacity of Buildings through 
assessment criteria. The broad line of research projects is based on a public-private 
initiative taken in 2012 in the Netherlands to promote sustainable buildings because of 
a pronounced problem with vacant buildings. Some projects present evaluation tools 
which include criteria, criteria weighting and performance indexes. (Durmisevic, 2006; 
R. Geraedts, 2016; R. P. Geraedts & J. M. Van der Voordt, 2008)

4.4 Objectives and approach  
 
Many initiatives are taken to estimate the performance and potential of transforming 
buildings. What seems to be missing is a connection between current scientific 
knowledge on the area and criteria used to quantify the transformation potential. What 
also seems to be missing is easy access to the assessment of the transformation 
capacity in the early design phase of a building, where the most important decisions are 
taken regarding the potential of the buildingвҖҷs future performance.  
 
The main objective of this thesis is therefor:  
To gather current scientific knowledge on the area of building transformation and 
transfer that knowledge into a tool which can be used to evaluate the design decisions 
in the early stage of a design process to advance buildings potential to transform in a 
later stage of their life cycle.  
 
The hypotheses are as followed:  
 
1. It is, through a literature study and an investigation of current knowledge on the 
area, possible to find the most relevant set of indicators suited for a quantification 
of the transformation capacity. 
 
2. It is possible to implement the indicators and their measure into a tool which make 
it easier to evaluate the transformation potential of a design.  
 
3. The performance output creates the basis for a comparison of decisions made in 
the design process which can lead the designer in the direction of a transformable 
design.  
 
4. An application of the tool on a case study will reveal potential adjustments related 
to the indicators and their measurements.  
 
The approach in the thesis will be:  
 
To first go through the literature and investigate the aspects of building transformation 
including the actors involved in building transformations, the processes related to building 
transformation and the design parameters are set for a transformable design.   
 
The next step, based on the knowledge from the literature review, will be to set the 
framework for an inventory of the indicators presented in the most progressive literature.   
 
Indicators for a transformation capacity tool shall be selected based on the inventory and 
suited measures of the quantification of the transformation potential found. 
 
Next the tool should be set up including the selected indicators and measures.  
 
The tool developed from the earlier step should as the last step be tested through a case 
study and adjusted to any finding.

5.1 Background 
5.1.1 The obstacles created in the past   
 
The industrialization of the building industry starting from 1945 and evolving through 
different decades until today is essential for the current challenges of the building 
industry. From before 1945 to now, the building industry has gone from being craft-
based productions and constructions, which where locally restricted, to being an 
effective mass production of building elements exported and imported from all over the 
world. The worker at the productions of building elements are to a greater extend 
unskilled, unlike before, due to the machine production, which makes basic product 
knowledge superfluous. The mass production accelerated after the 2nd world war where 
the was a socio-economic need to build fast, cheap, and thus effective . The result were 
buildings which express conformity, and which contain a range of synthetic and light 
components. (Beim, 2020)

Some decades after the 2nd world war the oil crisis causes an increased focus on the 
energy optimization of the building design which brought new synthetic insulation 
materials to the market. It also resulted in buildings with decreased window sizes and 
bad daylight conditions. Later, a system and modular thinking enters the market, where 
a more digitalized production is set to create fixed, all-in-one, mass-customized 
products with the purpose of creating cheap quality products which accommodate 
peoples need without being expensive. Currently the digitalization has raised to a new

evidence-based designs which are based on a variety of technologies and tools. (Beim,

5.2 Circularity 
5.2.1 Circular economy  
 
The aims of CIRCuIT is rooted in enabling a transition towards circular economy as an 
alternative to what is known as linear economy, which follows a take-make-dispose 
pattern. Circular economy builds on the idea that the industrial system design should 
be restorative as living systems are. This means that the systems should be optimized 
through a responsible and carefully planned material flow, so that the systems become 
part of a sustainable loop.  
 
 
 
One of the important aspects of circular economy is that the manufacturers and 
retailers are responsible for the use and the upgrade of their components or systems 
after the product is transferred to the buyer. The aim goal of circular economy is to 
separate economic growth with resource consumption and instead make it feasible to 
be sustainable. This should happen through a circular loop that includes the reuse of

5.3 Transformation 
 
The following section will introduce transformable buildings and try to define what 
transformation indicates. 
 
вҖңFirst, we shape our buildings, and then they shape us.вҖқ 
                                                                                                                                       Winston Churchill 
 
From this citation by Winston Churchill, Stewart Brand, author of the book How 
Buildings Learn, argues that Churchill is pointing in the right direction, but that he 
finishes the sentence to early. Stewart Brand argues that the sentence should be 
extended to: вҖңFirst we shape our buildings, then they shape us and then we shape them 
again вҖ“ ad infinitum.вҖқ  (Brand, 1994). Hereby the lifecycle of the building continues along 
with the different need of use.  
 
The danish architect SГёren Nielsen from the Architecture firm Vandkunsten also 
expresses it as: вҖңWe need to design and build houses, which can sustain for a long time. 
That by others means that they must be able to withstand to be built again and again and 
again. And this is related to having a ground structure that are flexible and construction 
technics, which are reversible (demountable). Which makes it possible to replace building 
parts without too many expenses and difficulties. The goal is better adaptability in the 
architecture.вҖқ  (Beim, 2020) 
 
Definition of transformation  
 
Building transformation is often described in context with flexibility and adaptability in 
the literature. A distinction between adaptability and flexibility is made by Steven GroГЎk 
who describes adaptability as вҖңcapable of different social usesвҖқ and flexibility as 
вҖңcapable of different physical arrangementвҖқ. The flexibility is hereby the physical 
rearrangement of the building and relates to the joining and division of rooms and units 
whereas adaptability relates to the variety of uses within the building without which can 
be created without any psychical changes.  (Brand, 1994) 
Flexibility and adaptability are qualities that can lead to a successful and easy 
transformation. Transformation is defined by The Cambridge Dictionary, as: the 
process of changing completely the character or appearance of something in order to 
improve it.  (Cambridge Dictionary, 2021)

The buildings will be referred to through the literature review.

designed and built at McGill University School of Architecture in 1996 and the concept

5.4 Building stages  
 
Decisions in the construction industry regarding transformations take place in several 
stages, over more than one period and with various stakeholders involved. It can be 
decisions at an Urban planning stage made by the government or decisions made by the 
user of a building unit on an interior design stage. Thus, it is important to get a view of 
the different transformation stages, which stakeholders cares about the 
transformation capacity and how the transformation in different stages affects one 
another. Another distinction which needs to be made is the distinction between the 
buildingвҖҷs arrangements and levels which have different properties and influences the 
transformation differently. The following chapter will try to introduce these different 
aspects.  
5.4.1 Building life cycle   
 
The buildings lifecycle consist of different stages which includes the production-, 
construction-, use- and end of life stage. The use phase leads in the end to either a 
renovation or a demolition and the renovation will lead to a new lifecycle of the building 
(Neel, 2020) The designer plays an important role in the design phase which precedes 
the construction stage. In this phase the designer has the possibility to create the 
foundation for future successfull renovation works  and plan for it by the use of analysis 
and documentation as seen in figure 11. (CTCN, 2021)

environment is to be divided in five levels where different parties are in control, either

The transformation of a building is closely related to the choice of a renovation and it

5.5.2 Component level 
 
The component level includes smaller parts of the building which are not the main 
carriers of the building or the systems.  
 
Elma Durmisevic describes two key indicators of the structural transformation 
connected to the components and their disassembly potential. The indicators are the 
independency and the exchangeability of the components. The independency refers to 
the functionality of the adjacent system or context which remains when the 
components are removed or exchanged. The exchangeability refers to the components 
ability to be exchanged with another component without ruining the context or be 
damaged itself. (Durmisevic, 2016) 
 
Other indicators which describes transformable components are the ease of access to 
the components, the avoidance of unnecessary treatments and finishes, simplicity in 
terms of working with the components and modularization and standardization of 
components. (Standard, 2020; Till & Schneider, 2007)

6.1.1 [A] A SECOND CHANCE FOR OLD BUILDINGS 
This study is the work behind the Transformation Potential Meter developed by Dr. Rob 
Geraedts and Dr. Theo van der Voordt from Delft University of Technology. The purpose 
of the assessment is to evaluate the potential of transforming vacant office buildings 
into residentials. The assessment is divided in steps according to the transformation

building as the second step and a risk assessment checklist as the last step. The 
feasibility scan of the building contains functional, technical, cultural, and legal aspects 
which are concretizes in gradual criteria. The point giving evaluation includes the 
location step and the building step. The score is given based on the count of вҖңyesвҖқ and 
the two steps are weighted differently and combined to a total score. The total score fit 
to a transformation class going from Not transformable to Excellent transformability. (R.

6.1.3 [C] DGNB - ECO2.1 / Flexibility and adaptability:  
DGNB (Deutsche Gesellschaft fГјr Nachhaltiges Bauen) is a certification system which

Denmark. It is adjusted to danish norms and reference values. The system includes six

The Economical quality covers the criteria group Economic Development which include

importance. The different typologies include: Office, Education, Residential, Hotel,

6.1.6 [F] FLEX 4.0 
Flex 4.0 is an instrument to assess the adaptive capacity of buildings. It is from 2016 
and it is a part of a research project on adaptive capacity of buildings which was started 
at Delft University of Technology in 2014 by Dr. Rob Geraedts. FLEX 4.0 is the newest 
==PAGE:37==
 
 
 
36 
 
version and it is based on an earlier version where an instrument with generally 
applicable indicators was combined with two other assessment instruments for 
respectively school buildings and office buildings including more specific indicators. 
FLEX 4.0 uses a rating system applied on all indicators where the user of the instrument 
gives a performance score between 1 = Bad and 4 = Best. The weighting of each 
indicator is a number between 1 and 4 and it is applied based on a method that compares 
indicators to each other. The user can change the rating if wanted. (R. Geraedts, 2016) 
6.1.7 [G] FLEXIBLE HOUSING 
Flexible Housing is a book written by Tatjane Schneider and Jeremy Till. A manual for 
flexible housing is created based on an examination of the history of flexible housing, 
case studies and a study on the making of flexible housing. The manual includes three 
section: the plan design, the construction, and the services. The plan design is divided 
into indicators applied to a building level, unit level and room level. The construction is 
divided into indicators on a building level and are supplied with construction principles 
which are important to consider. The service consists of a smaller number of 
indicators. The evaluation of the indicators is divided in cost, cost benefit and priority. 
(Till & Schneider, 2007) 
6.2 Selection of indicators  
The next step after the selection of the primary literature was to make an inventory of 
the indicators contained in the literature.  
6.2.1 Framework for inventory  
 
The systemization of the collection of indicators is based on the layer, levels and 
principles introduced in the literature review in 5.4.3.  
 
The matrix division includes:  
 
1. The two levels: The building level and the component level from section 5.5.1.6.   
Note: The system level is excluded to simplify the division, since the system level is 
an intermediate level between the building level and component level.   
 
2. The sharing layers and their associated building elements from section 5.5.1.6.   
Note: The layers Stuff and Site are excluded since the layers depends respectively 
on the user, the communal planner, or the landscape architect and not on the 
building designer which this thesis is applying to. A new layer added is the Building 
flow. The layer refers to the movement in the building including escalators, lifts, 
stairs, and corridors.  
 
3. The principles: space efficiency, accessibility, spatial adaptability, expandability, 
standardization, simplicity, convertibility, disassembly, versatility, exchangeability, 
==PAGE:38==
 
 
 
37 
 
durability, and independency. The principles were found in DS/ISO 20887:2020 
presented in section 6.1.4.  
 
A systemization was necessary to get around all indicators and to avoid the notation of 
the indicators more than one time during the collection.    
 
The next step was to go through the literature once again and note all the pieces of 
primary literature which supported the indicators inserted in the matrix. The pieces of 
literature were assigned by letters and then noted.   
 
 
 
The notation resulted in an inventory literature matrix that lists the indicators from 
selected literature next to the number of supporting literature.

6.3 Tool development methods  
6.3.1 Categorization of indicators  
 
The layers and levels are continued used to set up and arrange the tool and the 
indicators. The purpose is to ensure that all layers and aspects of the building are taken 
into consideration in the evaluation of the transformation potential and to create a 
distinction and awareness of different age rates of the layers.  
 
The categorization of the many indicators is a way to get more general information 
about the performance of the building.   
The overall categories: Dimensions and proportions, Layout and positioning, Access 
and disassembly and Capacity were implemented as criteria groups in the tool 
development. The overall categories are taken from the studies of Elsa Durmisevic and 
are explained in section 5.4.3. 
 
The indicators on the component level were categorized by the principles, accessibility, 
simplicity, standardization, disassembly, exchangeability, and independency from the 
inventory.

6.3.2 Numerical rating scale of indicators  
 
To quantify the evaluation of the buildingвҖҷs transformation capacity and the 
performance of the indicators, is it necessary to decide on a rating scale how to give 
the performance a value within a numerical scale.  
The measurements for the indicators, found in literature, should therefor all be 
converted to a numerical rating scale ranging from 1 to 9 where 1 is high performance 
and 9 low performance. The rating scale is taken from SAVE, explained in section 5.4.2.  
 
The reasons for adapting the grading scale from SAVE are that the entrepreneurs in the 
danish industry are already familiar with the scale and that the transformation capacity 
tool ideally could be accessed in combination with SAVE.

6.3.5 Weighting of the indicators  
 
The weighting of the indicators is a determination of how much different indicators 
should influence in an overall evaluation based on their importance. It is crucial for the 
output how inputs are weighted. Different weighting methods exist and can be used.  
 
A commonly used weighting method applied in the architecture, engineer, and 
construction industry is the Analytic Hierarchy Process (AHP). The method uses 
pairwise comparison to evaluate a set of criteria and afterward use them to find the 
optimal solution all from the perspective of the decision maker and an overall goal. The 
method is based on a hierarchy consisting of levels where the first level is the goal, and 
the second level are the criteria and objectives. More levels of sub criteria can be 
included, and the lowest level is normally a choice between alternatives. (Saaty, 1990) 
 
The Grading Method is a simpler version of the AHP method. A grading is assigned to a 
criterion with regards a main goal. The rating scale normally goes from 1 to 10, where 10 
represents the most important criterion on a level. All criteria on the same level are 
compared to the criterion on a higher level. The method is based on the criteria tree as 
seen in Figure 29. (Alanne, 2004) 
The gradings of the criteria on the same level are normalized, so they range between 0 
and 1 which means that the total sum of the criterium on a higher level is equal 1. The 
weighted score of the indicators and sub-indicators are all calculated by multiplying 
the score of the indicators with the normalized grading using following formula,

7.1 Literature matrix 
 
The literature matrix is the result of the literature review which led to an inventory of 
the transformation indicators presented in the selected primary literature. The 
literature matrix gives an overview of all the indicators and shows which indicators the 
sources agree on. The sources are assigned to letters which is shown in the bottom of 
the matrix in Table 13. The literature matrix is divided in the sharing layers along with the 
added layer Building Flow. All the indicators are assigned to the overall groups of 
indicators explained in section 6.4.3.  
The literature matrix shows that there is consensus about many of the indicators 
between the sources. The reason could be that the literature sources are sharing 
knowledge and that their findings are supported by each otherвҖҷs work.  
Some of the indicators which only are supported by one study could be described as 
minor details such as: lintels and framing for future openings created in the 
loadbearing/nonloadbearing partitioning walls, avoidance of electrical installations in 
nonloadbearing walls and position of doors. Nevertheless, are those indicators minor 
but important details which contributes to an easy transformation. The reason could be 
that those indicators are difficult to measure. The highest agreement of indicators 
between the sources seems to be connected to the layer structure and service. This 
indicates that these layers are most defining for the transformation potential, which 
makes sense since these layers are more fixed in their positions, dimensions, and 
constructions. The structure and service are defining for the space plan since it sets 
the limits for the spatial dimensions, layout, and volumes. The structure is a slow layer 
with the highest age rate, why it is important to design the structure, so the other layers 
have the possibility to be flexible.

8 Discussion 
 
This study consists of four parts which each have their pitfalls.  
There are several numbers of issues which needs to be addressed regarding the 
selection of relevant literature and the inventory of indicators. First, there is the 
selection of studies which is limited to seven, whereby it can be questioned if this is 
enough for a valid inventory as a basis for a tool. Next there is the selection of studies 
where it can be questioned if some of the studies are too alike. The literature study [A] 
from section 6.1.1 is developed by Professor Dr. Rob Geraedts and Professor Dr. Theo 
van der Voordt from Delft University of Technology. The same author, Dr. Rob Geraedts, 
is also behind the study and tool presented in study [F] from section 6.1.6.  The 
consequence of including studies with the same author is that the indicators 
indirectly/directly are similar and therefor make a wrong impression of the importance 
of the indicators in the inventory. Study [A] and [F] do have different focuses why this 
could make the problem less essential in this case. This brings another issue in 
consideration which is that the studies include different focuses. Study [G] from 
section 6.1.7 which is a book is as an example focusing on flexible housing and Study [C] 
from section 6.1.3 which is a certification system is dividing the indicators in different 
typologies, where some of the indicators are repeated as the same. To sharpen the 
inventory, different typologies could have been included in the matrix framework. Only 
the measurements of the indicators included in Study C are divided by typologies. The 
reason for not putting more work into the different typologies, is that the study needed 
be limited at one point.  
 
The selection of the indicators reflects the choice of studies, since the selection 
primarily is based on the rule that two studies should support the indicator to be 
included in the tool. The selection is there for depending on the right choice of studies 
which has already been discussed. It can still be considered if it is not worth to include 
the indicators which were only supported by one study and which was not included 
anyway. Some of the indicators were included despite the support from only on study, 
which was based on own assessment of the indicators justification and the fact that it 
was included in one of the studies which were official certification systems.   
 
In the development of the tool is the implementation and conversion of different 
measurement methods, gathered from different literature, one of the larger pitfalls.  
The analytic measurement method, which dedicated a score to different scenarios, is 
first of all different from the method that dedicates a score to different indexes. These 
methods are also different to the interpolation method, which dedicate the highest 
score on an upper limit and the lowest score on a lower limit or vice versa. The adaption 
and conversion of different methods was again a way to limit the study. The problem of 
adapting different methods is that it can cause a skewness of the measures and affect 
the validation of the result. The conversion of scores from one rating scale to another 
is another aspect which gives uncertain data. The rating scale which is used in study 
[F] from section 6.1.6 goes from 1 to 4, where the rating scale which is used is this thesis 
==PAGE:79==
 
 
 
78 
 
goes from 1 to 9. The difference between the scores gets bigger in this conversion, why 
this might cause that the rating in the adapted measure gets higher or lower than it was 
intended.  
 
It is overall difficult to exclude that the measures of the indicators are not overlapping 
since many of the indicators are closely connected. This could consequently cause 
misleading results in terms of the influence the indicators have on the final score. The 
skewness of the indicators influence would need to be tested through more use of the 
tool in case and pilot projects, since the practical results of the use would show the 
pitfalls. The test and use of the tool on pilot projects and cases are also a good idea in 
terms of discovering important circumstances and indicators which can be overlooked 
and neglected in a literature study.  
 
In the application of the tool on the case study, it become clear that the interpretation 
of the way to access the measurement of the indicators can cause differentiating 
analyzes and results. This can lead to the wrong assessment of the indicator 
performance of the concerned project. The ways the indicators should be accessed are 
not strictly one-sided for all the indicators, why the interpretations can differentiate 
from user to user.  
An example is the easiness of a vertical extension of the roof or basement, which is, at 
current state, more or less up to the users own assessment without any criteria given 
for such easiness.   
Clear definitions of the ways to access the indicator performances are, if more time had 
been given, an important factor for the result, which should have been made.  
A way to get around different interpretations, when the most sufficient measurements 
of the indicators are established, is to convert the excel tool to an analyzing BIM plugin 
where the design steps in the model are evaluated based on non-manipulative 
rules/measures.  
 
The final results showed that a high-performance rating might be too easy or difficult 
to achieve for some of the indicators. Two of the indicators which are particular in mind 
are the indicators on the component level: simplicity and standardization. The 
simplicity indicator achieved the highest score in the case study, which were based on 
the scores for the sub-indicators. The reason could be that two of the sub-indicators 
are measured from a yes or no question, which makes the measurement very simplified. 
The indicator which is a measure of part per components could be removed since it is 
very difficult to decided what parts per component indicates. Is it for instance all 
screws and sides in a frame or is it just layers or different material parts?   
The indicator standardization, defined by the modular measurements of the horizontal 
and vertical dimensions of the component, achieved a very low score in the case study.  
The total modular coordination in the end affects this score. It is a difficult task to fit all 
dimensions to modular dimensions, why it might be too difficult to get a high score.

9 Conclusion  
 
The outcome of a preliminary literature review led to the understanding that building 
transformation has many facets. New and old research on the area divides the 
transformation and the building in levels and categories. This is way to illustrate the 
different dimensions of a transformable design. The conceptualization of building 
transformation is still in progress and has not fully been defined, why the connection 
between current literature presented in this thesis is relevant.  
 
An inventory of transformable design indicators in progressive literature, research 
projects and certification schemes, on the area, exposes the most prominent 
indicators at the current state. The inventory creates a basis for the collection of 
relevant indicators and a possibility to weight the indicators in the quantification of the 
buildingвҖҷs transformation capacity. The result showed that the indicators which defines 
the structure and the service in the building especially are supported by the literature. 
 
A tool based on selected indicators and their measurements showed how the 
transformation capacity of a building design can be quantified and accessed. A 
visualization of how well the indicators perform together with a transformation 
capacity score for the building design gives the designer the possibility to weight ones 
decisions in terms of the buildings transformation potential.  
 
A case study, a hotel at Bornholm, showed how early decisions in a design process 
influences a potential transformation of a building and how difficult these design 
choices can be to change in a later stage of the design phase, such as the 
implementation and positioning of fixed CLT-walls in the floorplan. The case study had 
the final transformation capacity score on the building level of 3.06 which is categorized 
as medium transformation capacity. The final transformation capacity on the 
component level was 4.  The building have a good transformation potential, but one of 
the limiting indicators is the structural composition of CLT-walls and -floor decks.   
 
The tool can also be useful in the end-of-life stage when the choice between a 
demolition or a transformation should be made, since it gives an overview of the work 
that must be done to transform the building and it thereby indicates whether it is 
feasible or not. 
 
Both the inventory, the inclusion of indicators and measurements in the tool, and the 
way the tool is accessed could be improved by including more studies in the inventory, 
and by adjusting the tool to more case studies and further investigation.

10 Further recommendations  
 
One of the limitations of the tool is that the transformation capacity is not held up 
against another measure/quantification for example the cost and the environmental 
imprint of a transformable building design versus a non-transformable building design.  
The comparison between the transformation capacity and the cost and environmental 
imprint would bring strong arguments in consideration in terms of making the wright 
decisions.  
 
What would benefit the validation of the tool would be to apply it on more pilot projects 
including hotel, office, and residential buildings and adjust the tool to the findings.  
Especially a test study of the variating measures and indicators of the different 
typologies would make the analyzes more nuanced.  
 
In terms of converting the measures of the indicators into an analytic BIM plugin are 
some more suitable than others. The analytic measures are difficult to implement in a 
programming script, why these types of measures would need to be more specific and 
physically measurable in order to implement the measures in an analytic BIM tool.  
 
On a component level some of the indicators describe a set of qualifications which are 
attached to the component on an isolated level. These qualifications have the potential 
to be a part of a component dataset which are already implemented in a component 
data library such as the material passport.  
 
Further recommended investigations are:  
 
LCA 
The implementation of LCA to show the environmental impact between a 
transformation versus a demolition and the difference between a transformable design 
versus a non-transformable design to evaluate the impact of the design decisions.   
 
Cost estimation and LCC 
The implementation of a cost estimation of a transformation versus a demolition and 
the LCC (life cycle cost) of a transformable design versus a non-transformable design 
to show the impact of the design decisions. 
 
Adjustments 
Further adjustments and validation of the indicators and their measures should happen 
through pilot projects, case studies and testing.  
 
BIM-plugin  
The conversions of the Excel tool into an analyzing BIM-plugin which can evaluate 
design models from the early stage of a design process.

12.3 Appendix C вҖ“ Others  
 
Multifunctional areas:  
A multifunctional area is described as:  
вҖңGeneric rooms (e.g. rooms with the dimensions: 3 m X 3 m preferable 4 m x 4 m)вҖқ (Green 
Building Council Denmark, 2020)  
вҖңFunctional neutral rooms should ideally be 3.6 m wide and 4.0 m deep to accommodate 
different functional furniture layouts. A second preference is 3.2 m wide and 3.8 m deep. 
The dimensioning of such rooms will means, that the size of a traditional living room 
should slightly be reduced and that the traditional kitchen and bedrooms should be 
increased.вҖқ (Till & Schneider, 2007)
