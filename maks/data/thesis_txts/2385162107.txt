Abstract
In this thesis the use of spontaneous four-wave-mixing to generate photon-pairs in OAM
ﬁber modes for quantum communication is investigated. The ﬁrst part of the report deals
with an extension and generalization of existing theory describing the secure bit rates in
quantum key distribution in arbitrary dimension including imperfect state preparation.
This theory extension enables the calculations of secure bit rates to be performed with
real imperfect photon sources.
The second part of the report investigates photon-pair generation using spontaneous
four-wave-mixing. It is found that the single photon-pairs are entangled, and by measuring
the idler photon the state of the signal photon is prepared. For multiple four-wave-mixing
processes the signal photons created are in a coherent superposition, and can be used for
quantum key distribution. The probability that a signal photon is prepared in a given
state has been calculated, and the state is also calculated. Both depend on the generation
rate.
The third part of the report is concerned with calculating the secure bit rate for quan-
tum states determined by the transverse mode of a speciﬁc ﬁber - OAM modes. Consid-
ering the dispersion relation calculated with a ﬁnite diﬀerence numerical method, allowed
four-wave-mixing process are found that are able to create signal photons in orthogonal
modes with the same wavelength. A set of two processes is found, based on which the
secure bit rate of quantum key distribution can be calculated. A secure bit rate of around
16 kHz is found using realistic values for parameters of the system.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Communicating information privately is important for the safe use of the many commu-
nication channels of today. An example being shopping with a credit card online. If the
communication between the customer and the website is not secure, the credit card details
may be at the hands of eavesdroppers to the transaction. Thus keeping information secure
is identical to keeping money in a safe in the online world.
Companies in a competitive industry need to communicate privately inbetween part-
ners such as to keep potential production advantages secret from the competition. Indus-
trial espionage is a problem where large capital is at stake. Losing a competitive edge that
has been developed through large investments and industrial research can cause a loss of
proﬁt or in the end completely shut down a company [1].
The lives of people are in direct dependence of private communication in the military,
and great eﬀort has been made to avoid enemy eavesdropping in times of war. An example
being the german enigma machine used to encrypt and decrypt secure messages during
the second world war. This project investigates of a photonic system that has applications
in information security of communication.

1.2
Cryptography
Cryptography has been established to ensure that communicated information is suﬃciently
diﬃcult, ideally impossible, to obtain for a third party, by the use of cryptographic pro-
tocols. These protocols encrypt information in such a way that a message is encoded in a
ciphertext using an encryption key, for instance a string of bits. The ciphertext is sent to
a receiver, which decrypts the information again using a decryption key. The ciphertext
ideally has no information about the message without knowledge of the keys and as such

ing parties discerns the two types of secure communication that is typically distinguished:

In private key cryptosystems encoding keys are held by the sender and decoding keys
are held by the receiver, both without the knowledge of the keys by any eavesdropper - thus
the keys are private. Information can be encrypted in various ways e.g. by the Vernam
cipher in which the encoding and decoding key are the same. The sending party, typically
called Alice, has a message and a key of at least the same length. The message and key
could be strings of letters of the alphabet, in which case Alice adds the two modulo 26 to
obtain the ciphertext which is sent over a public channel. The channel can be eavesdropped
by a third party, Eve, however the ciphertext reveals no information about the original
message without the key. The receiving party, Bob, uses his key by subtracting it from the
ciphertext to obtain the original message. This system is completely secure as long as the
keys are randomly generated, kept private, and not reused [4]. An illustration of this cipher
is shown in Table 1.1. The biggest problem in private key cryptosystems is getting Alice
and Bob to decide on the key without Eve ﬁnding out. This requires either a completely
private communication channel or the physical exchange of keys, both of which are the
exact things that the system tries to do without! That is, if a private channel is already
available there is no reason to encrypt messages to send them over public channels, and if
the communicating parties already need to meet up, the private communication could just
be carried out immediately. Obviously a physically exchanged secret key could be saved
for later use, but the number of applications for this is very limited.
Public key cryptosystems on the other hand explicitly deal with the problems of private
key cryptosystems. Here the public key held by Bob is not a secret but completely available
to both Alice and Eve listening in on the communication. Alice encrypts her information
using the public key and sends it to Bob over a public channel much like with the private
key cryptosystem. The diﬀerence is that the decryption requires a completely diﬀerent
key than encryption, and is in general much more diﬃcult to perform were it not for the
fact that Bob has the decryption key himself, and is able to use it on Alices message. The
idea here is that the encryption process is much easier to perform than the decryption

used public key cryptosystems is called the RSA cryptosystem [5]. This cryptosystem is
based on a problem that is related to the prime number factorization of large numbers.
It is relatively easy to multiply two large primes on a classical computer, but going from
the large number to the prime factors has no known eﬃcient classical algorithm meaning
that it takes an amount of computational resources to solve which is exponential in the
number of bits of the number. Thus eavesdropping can be made suﬃciently hard by using
large enough numbers. See Appendix A for further details on RSA.
As stated, there is no known eﬃcient classical algorithm that can break the RSA, how-
ever it has not been proven that no eﬃcient classical algorithm exists. There is a chance
that an eﬃcient algorithm is found, and if that happens the secure information sent is
readily available to anyone, which makes it very dangerous to use a credit card online
etc. Furthermore, an eﬃcient algorithm does exists, albeit only realizable on a quantum
computer. This algorithm is known as Shor’s algorithm. Shor’s algorithm is discussed in
detail in Appendix B. Quantum mechanics however also provides a solution to the same
exact problem. It is possible to use the quantum mechanical collapse of wavefunction
and the no-cloning theorem to establish a completely private (quantum) communication
channel, where the privacy is guaranteed by the laws of physics and not the lack of com-
putational power of the eavesdropper[3, 6]. The private quantum channel, however, does
not work in such a way that a predecided bitstring can privately and deterministically be
transmitted, and as such does not work as a channel to carry an arbitrary message. It
is however possible to use the quantum channel between Alice and Bob and for them to
privately agree on a completely random bitstring, which is exactly what is needed for the
private key cryptosystem. Thus distributing keys privately using quantum mechanics is a
way to keep information secure in the future. It is commonly referred to as quantum key
distribution (QKD)[3, 6].

Chapter 2
Quantum Key Distribution
Current classical cryptosystems are unsafe against quantum algorithms and are not proven
secure against classical algorithms. Therefore new methods of communicating information
are needed. A quantum algorithm such as Shor’s algorithm is one in which quantum com-
puters have found a theoretical speedup in computation time, and other such algorithms
solving diﬀerent problems might be discovered in the near future. It is advantageous to
construct cryptosystems such that their safety does not rely on the diﬃculty of performing
some computation, but rather be information-theoretic secure - making no assumptions
about the computing power at hand of an eavesdropper, and still being safe if the eaves-
dropper possesses inﬁnite computational resources [2].
Information which is changed upon reading, cannot be cloned in an arbitrary state,
and only reveals the correct answer probabilistically unless measured correctly can be used
to communicate with complete security. These properties are exactly true for quantum
states, for which the no-cloning theorem applies, the superposition of states collapse under
measurement, and only reveals the correct measurement outcome with certainty if mea-
sured in the correct basis [7]. The use of quantum mechanics as an information carrier
is the ﬁeld of quantum communication. Quantum communication allows for studying the
quantum nonlocality of entanglement, do quantum state teleportation, perform networked
quantum computing, and QKD [8].
2.1
Quantum Mechanical Properties
Quantum key distribution is a way of using information on a quantum level to exchange
information between two parties, Alice and Bob, with complete security against eaves-
dropping due to the underlying quantum nature of the information shared. Using this,
Alice and Bob can in secrecy decide on a speciﬁc key string to use for performing private
key cryptography, which can be used to communicate information in between them clas-
sically. QKD requires that the states can be communicated between the two parties with
an upper bound on the error rate. The exact physical implementation of a quantum state,
whether it be the polarization of a photon or the electronic state in an atom for example,
does not matter in theory. However, it will become apparent that some physical systems
are more suited to perform communication with than others. To begin with however, the
basics of QKD are introduced.

With this in mind Alice now sends a string of qubits to Bob. The qubits are encoded
with a value in a speciﬁc basis. Alice, in order to ensure no-one knows the bit value or the
basis in which it is encoded in beforehand, should choose a bit value and a basis completely
randomly. Bob measures each of the qubits in one of the two bases at random with equal
probability, and keeps the measurement result to himself. The right basis is chosen with
a probability of 1 in the number of bases, in this case 1
2.
After the measurement Alice uses a classical channel, which is considered unsafe and
thus the information is available to Eve, to announce her choice of preparation basis
for each bit, and Bob tells her which of the measurements he performed were done in
the same basis as the qubit was encoded. When Bob measured in the correct basis, his
measurement result should with certainty give him the right bit when no noise is present,
as a consequence of the qubits within this basis being orthogonal. When Bob measured
in the wrong basis he got the wrong result of measurement with a 50% probability. This
means that when Bob and Alice compare bases they discard the instances in the string
where the wrong basis was chosen, and they are left with a sifted bitstring which they both
agree on. Thus this bitstring can be used as the private key in a private key cryptosystem.

Protecting against eavesdropping and noise
The ideal case of the BB84 protocol as just presented does neither represent the real
world, nor does it assume any eavesdropper being present, and as such has not really
proven to be safe against eavesdropping. Eavesdropping is Eve’s attempt to gain some
information about the bitstring Alice and Bob use as a key. Privacy ampliﬁcation can
be performed, which at the cost of bits reduces Eves information about the rest of the
bitstring. Eavesdropping introduces errors between the sifted bitstrings Alice and Bob
have.
Errors can be corrected such that Alice and Bob’s ﬁnal bitstrings have higher
agreement, but error correction comes at the cost of bits. Important measures for the
performance of the protocol are thus the information Eve has about the ﬁnal bitstring,
and the bitrate Alice and Bob can achieve after error correction and privacy ampliﬁcation
to within some upper bound on Eves information. First, however, an introduction to how

eavesdropping, error correction, and privacy ampliﬁcation can be performed.
If an eavesdropper attacks the system, it inevitably results in the measuring the qubits
sent by Alice. As the basis in which Alices bits are encoded is random and unknown to
anyone but her, Eve can simply attempt to perform the same action as Bob and measure in
a random basis for each qubit and resend a qubit encoded with the measurement result she
received. This is referred to as the intercept and resend strategy1. From the measurement
collapse of quantum mechanics, she changes the qubit if the measurement is performed in
the wrong basis. The string of qubits sent to Bob thus does not completely correspond to
the bitstring sent by Alice.
In real physical systems, however, Alice might produce multi qubit events. In multi
qubit events, Alice accidently sends two or more qubits with the same information encoded,
in place of just a single qubit. In these cases Eve can split the multi qubit stream and
measure just one of the states sent without destroying the bit value of the rest. This
is often referred to as a photon number splitting (PNS) attack [10]. This type of attack
is a way for Eve to achieve information without disturbing the signal, which makes the
protocol unsafe and thus multi qubit events should be kept to a minimum.
Alice and Bob, to detect the presence of an eavesdropper, compare some of their bits
and check if there are any discrepancies. These bits are to be discarded afterwards. Some
of the discrepancies are due to noise in the quantum channel, but should be attributed to
Eve nevertheless. This is due to the very unlikely situation in which Eve somehow replaces
the equipment of Alice and Bob with better equipment, possibly even totally noise free,
and she then hides her presence in the, by Alice and Bob, expected errors, thus giving Eve
information without being detected. Therefore all errors are attributed to Eves presence.
By performing checks Alice and Bob can detect the extent of the eavesdropping and thus
discontinue communicating in order to avoid having their secrets intercepted by Eve. This
is not quite satisfactory, as eavesdropping then disables the communication between two
parties. Alice and Bob can do better. If the error rate is determined to be less than some
level, the errors can be removed, and then privacy ampliﬁcation can be performed.
The errors are removed by performing parity checks over smaller sections of the bit-
strings agreed upon. This is done by e.g. counting the number of 1’s on each section of
the bitstring and noting whether it contains an odd or even number of 1’s. This is then
compared and if the parity is wrong the section of bits are discarded. This ensures that
the sections of the bitstring that remains have zero errors - or at least an even number,
with decreasing probability of higher numbers of errors. The parity checks give Eve some
information and it is then necessary to remove at least one bit from the section to counter
that. This is a way for Alice and Bob to reduce the errors.
Privacy ampliﬁcation is done by selecting a section of the sifted bits and use the parity
of each section as the recorded bit in the ﬁnal bitstring. This reduces the number of bits in
the ﬁnal bitstring by a factor of the number of sections, but at the same time this decreases
Eves knowledge of the ﬁnal bitstring. This is to be done such that the probability of Eve
determining the correct bit is suﬃciently close to 1
2, in which case the information she has
is none, and that Alice and Bob keep a satisfactory number of bits from which they can
make a key. Note that both the error correction and privacy ampliﬁcation are done on the
cost of the number of bits that are shared in the end. Thus having a low error rate and
multibit event rate, and at the same time keeping the bit rate high is necessary for Alice
and Bob to agree on a shared key of a desirable length within some timeframe [3, 6].

As discussed in the previous section the BB84 protocol is a way to perform quantum key
distribution, that takes advantage of quantum mechanics to ensure privacy. Sifting, error
correction, and privacy ampliﬁcation which are means of classical information processing
are performed at the expense of the classical bits left for Alice and Bob to use as a key.
In this section ways of quantifying and optimizing the protocol are discussed by deﬁning
a secure bitrate and a security parameter, and investigating how these depend on the
number of symbols, N, and mutually unbiased bases, M. It is important to note that
even though quantum key distribution takes advantage of the laws of quantum mechanics
the ﬁnal result is a classical bitstring, as opposed to a string of quantum bits on which a
quantum computer can perform computations. As such the measure of mutual information
in the ﬁnal bitstring is classical.

Collective and coherent attacks
In this discussion only the simple intercept and resend strategy has been considered. More
complex eavesdropping strategies on QKD are collective and coherent attacks. Collective
attacks describes strategies where Eve interacts with each of the signals seperately by
entangling a signal with a probe state, and after classical information exchange between
Alice and Bob the probes are measured collectively (i.e. Eve has acces to quantum memory
and gating) [20, 21]. Coherent attacks describes strategies where Eve is not limited at all,
and can perform interactions with multiple states and probes and store the information
until afterwards [22]. QKD has been proven secure against both of these types of attacks
[20, 21, 22, 23], although with diﬀerent error rates and secure key rates than is expected
from the intercept and resend strategy.
The choice of restricting Eve to the intercept and resend strategy in this project has
been made in order keep the treatment simple and intuitive. Intercept and resend allows
for straightforward calculations of correlations between security and key generation rates
dependent on the number of bases, symbols and rate of eavesdropping, using the theory
of the classical Shannon entropy. Thourough calculations of secure rates for the coherent
attacks with no assumptions and using the quantum mechanical von Neumann entropy
are beyond the scope of this report. The exact value of the bit rates is not of much sig-
niﬁcance compared to providing more general dependencies between diﬀerent parameters.
Furthermore the treatment has been done in the asymptotic limit of the bit strings. That
is, it is assumed that the number of bits within the string with a certain value is exactly
equal to the probability of that bit. This only works for bitstrings of inﬁnite lenght, and
as such ﬁnite key eﬀects have been ignored. Calculating the exact values of the secure
bit rates with regard to ﬁnite key eﬀects is very computationally diﬃcult and maybe even
impossible without further assumptions [22, 23].

Physical systems of implementation
Any discrete quantum system is in principle usable to realize the BB84 protocol. Not all
systems are in practice be equally suited for doing so, and there is a need to qualitatively
asses how well a system performs in the communication of information between Alice and
Bob. Primarily used in the ﬁeld of quantum computing Divincenzo’s 5+2 requirements
(5 for computing and 2 for communicating in between nodes) are generally accepted as
a measure for the performance of a quantum computer [24], the last two of which can
be used to determine if physical system performs well for QKD. The requirements are

Photons as information carrier
A well suited physical system to choose for QKD is the photon, which has many advan-
tageous properties also exploited in the use of light to carry out classical communication.
Photons travel at the speed of light allowing for fast communication, and have a num-
ber of degrees of freedom in which the computational basis can be chosen, such as the
two dimensional basis of polarization which is often mentioned in textbooks as a physi-
cal realization of the BB84 [3, 6]. Other bases include phase, frequency, and transverse
modes, which have the potential to be of higher dimension [12]. Quantum states form a
well-characterized basis with the possibility of creating any superposition. The states can
be initialized and controlled by beamsplitters, mirrors, q-plates, and spatial light modu-
laters [6, 25, 26, 27]. Optical ﬁbres can be used such that the states can be transmitted
[12]. Measuring can be performed by spatially separating the states using 50/50 beam-
splitters that ensure measurements are performed in either basis equally often, and using
single photon detectors at the diﬀerent spatial positions to detect the state of the photon
[12, 28].
There are some big technological challenges in realizing QKD that needs to be overcome
such as making single-photon sources and increasing the transmission eﬃciency.
On-
demand, indistinguishable single-photon sources do not exist, and sources which are non
deterministic, have multiphoton events, and create distinguishable photons reduce security
and throughput [29]. The lack of single photon sources have been circumvented by using
weak coherent laser pulses and decoy states with much less power to detect photon number
splitting attacks [30, 31]. The quantum state of the weak laser pulses is the coherent state,
which is a superposition of photon number states with poissonian statistics. The pulses are
attenuated such that the one photon probability is around 10% and the two or more photon
probability is small but nevertheless nonzero. This means that the largest probability is
for the zero photon state (around 90%) [12].
The use of weak laser pulses does not mean that improvements on current photon
sources are not beneﬁcial for QKD. A signiﬁcantly higher secure key generation rate is
achieved by using sources with a low multipair rate, called sub-Poissonian sources, as this
decreases the amount of information an eavesdropper has [32]. The secure bitrate decreases
more rapidly with increasing loss in the channel by using weak laser pulses compared to
single-photon sources [23]. This becomes problematic in long distance communication.
The bitrate decrease arises from both the error correction between Alice and Bob, and
from the increased privacy ampliﬁcation needed since the laser has to be operated at higher
power and thus has a higher rate of multipair events which gives Eve more information
[32].
Another problem in using weak laser pulses, apart from the multiphoton events, are
the zero photon events. The single-photon detectors experience dark counts and stray
photons which increase the error rate, and since the detectors on the receiving end must
be open for all the pulses even the empty ones, a high zero photon rate is detrimental.
Using heralded sources, in which photons are generated in pairs and one photon, the idler
photon, is used as an indicator of the presence of the other, the signal photon, solves this
problem as the detectors can be activated only when a photon is present [12].

Transmission of the quantum states over long are done in silica ﬁbers which have low
attenuation at telecom wavelengths (∼1.55µm), thus decreasing the error rate. Coupling
losses from lasers or nonlinear crystals into ﬁbers increase the error rate, and should be
avoided for the signal photon quantum state after detection of the idler. An implemen-
tation which generates the quantum states directly in the ﬁber avoids this type of loss.
Within the ﬁber, apart from attenuation, decoherence also occurs. Decoherence is when
a quantum state changes over time, and decreases the ﬁdelity when measured. For the
polarisation degree of freedom this means that an initially vertically polarised photon has
some probability of being measured as horizontally polarised after transmission.
The bitrate is currently low over reasonably short distances in the perspective of com-
munication. As an example hereof Liu et al. achieved 15 Hz over 200 km in 2010 [33].
Therefore it is interesting to investigate how the physical realization can be improved,
both with respect to the photon sources and transmission ﬁdelity [8, 12, 29].
The concept of QKD has now been introduced. The properties of quantum mechanics
has been used to justify the security of the BB84 protocol, and using concepts from classical
information theory the information rate and security in higher dimensional Hilbert spaces
has been calculated. A generalization of the results found in the litterature [11, 14, 34, 35]
dealing with the intercept and resend strategy has been done to include eﬀects of imperfect
sources and multibit events in the calculation of the secure bit rate. It has been shown
that a high dimensional alphabet, a low number of mutually unbiased bases, low multibit
events, and low eavesdropping rate leads to the best performance in terms of generating
secure keys. The advantage of using the photon as a quantum system has been discussed.

Heralded single-photon sources
In order to increase the secure bit rate of QKD the source needs to be improved to have
fewer multi- and zero photon events.
Although weak coherent pulses have been used
to perform QKD, the inherent low bit rate and short distances of this type of system
must be improved to become practical. In the last section it was concluded that single-
photon sources, higher-dimensional systems, and lower transmission losses increase the
performance of QKD.
A single photon is the lowest excitation of the electro-magnetic ﬁeld. The quantization
of the electric ﬁeld is carried out in Appendix D. A single photon in a mode is denoted
by the Fock state |1⟩which is an eigenstate to the quantized electromagnetic ﬁeld Hamil-
tonian. Typically a mode is assumed to have only one photon, and the quantum state is
denoted by another degree of freedom of the mode which is excited other than the photon
number. Some examples are polarization (|H⟩, |V ⟩), frequency (|ω1⟩, |ω2⟩) or transverse
ﬁeld distribution in a ﬁber (|m1⟩, |m2⟩). These single-photon states are quantum states
and can be used for QKD.
The single-photon states need to be created by a source. An ideal single-photon source
creates a single-photon state with certainty, with zero probability of multiphoton events
and subsequent photon emissions being indistinguishable from eachother. For the applica-
tion of QKD some requirements are more important than others. The indistinguishability
is less important if Alice is able to manipulate the photon into some desired state prior to
transmission. The singleness demand is relaxed by the possibility of privacy ampliﬁcation,
however this occurs at a cost of the bits transmitted and as such reduced the eﬀective bit
rate [29].
Deterministic sources use speciﬁc electronic transitions in atoms, molecules, or solid
state systems to generate a single photon. An external signal is applied and the system
experiences an electronic transition which creates a photon. The photons are generated
on demand, but often with a low extraction eﬃciency [29].
Another approach is to use probabilistic sources, where two photons are generated
probabilistically and the detection of one photon reveals the presence of the other. This
is called heralding and is realised through nonlinear processes in materials which create
photons in two modes with complete correlation in their number distribution. By heralding
the vacuum contribution can be eliminated, and if the generation probability is kept low the
single-photon pair term, |1⟩s |1⟩i, dominates. Multiplexing a large number of probabilistic

The heralding process is described by the heralding ratio: Ri = Psi/Pi. Here Pi is the
probability of detecting a photon in the idler mode, regardless of the mode of the other
photon. Psi is the probability of detecting both of them in the correct modes. This ratio
takes a maximal value of unity when the signal photon is guaranteed in the correct mode
when an idler is detected [37]. Therefore it is preferred to have losses on the idler over the
signal, such that the rate is highest.

Photon-pair generation through spontaneous four-wave
mixing
The process of four wave mixing involves four modes interacting in a material through its
third order susceptibility, χ(3). Two photons, commonly referred to as the pump photons,
change their frequency under the constraints of energy and momentum conservation. One
photon loses energy and one gains the same amount, thus transferring the two photons
into two diﬀerent modes, commonly referred to as the signal and idler modes.
Four
wave mixing is often carried out in centrosymmetric materials, in which the second order
nonlinear interactions do not occur (χ(2) = 0) [38].
This is because the second order
nonlinearities are often much stronger when present than the third order eﬀects rendering
the third order eﬀects of negligible magnitude. A commonly used centrosymmetric medium
is a ﬁber made from silica glass, where the long interaction length in the ﬁber allows for
the third order eﬀects to become signiﬁcant.

Multiple FWM processes
It has now been established how a single spontaneous-four-wave-mixing process can be used
to generate photon pairs, frequency ﬁltering of the idler photon has been included in the
theory, and the generation rate and quantum state of the photons have been determined.
This allows for using single photons for communication, however does not describe a system
usable for higher dimensional quantum key distribution. First and foremost, there is only
one state possible for the idler photon, and secondly no mutually unbiased bases have been
established to choose from. Using multiple four-wave-mixing processes however enables
this.

3.2
Heralding
The quantum state of the photons generated through four-wave-mixing inherently creates
entangled photons between the signal and idler transverse modes of the ﬁber. Once again
the indirect measurements can be performed on the idler thus giving information about
the state of the signal. This means that the speciﬁc state in which the signal photon is
projected unto is determined by the measurement, and is by the probabilistic nature of
measurements on the idler itself probabilistic. This means that it acts as a true random
number generator intrinsic in the scheme, and that any eavesdropper is unable to predict
the signal state a priori.
Alice is only left to choose the bases in which she encodes
the qubits, which using a 50/50 beamsplitter to couple the idler into either one of the
measurement apparatusses for each basis is also done completely randomly and with an
equal probability.

Orbital angular momentum states
Light possesses a number of degrees of freedom (DOF), such as frequency, momentum,
and polarisation, in which the quantum state of a photon can be encoded. Polarisation
has been used for theoretical and experimental realisations of the BB84 protocol [9, 33].
Polarisation forms a 2 dimensional Hilbert space with 3 mutually unbiased bases. For
higher dimensional QKD a higher dimensionality is required.
One DOF with a high
dimensionality is the orbital angular momentum of light, where the Hilbert space can be
determined by speciﬁc ﬁber modes used.
The angular momentum of light is divided into two parts, spin angular momentum
(SAM) and orbital angular momentum (OAM), like the angular momentum of an electron
in atomic physics. The spin angular momentum is associated with the polarisation states
and can take the two values of ±ℏfor right and left handed polarisation respectively [44].
The orbital angular momentum (OAM) of light was described by Allen et. al. in 1992
[45], and is related to the transverse distribution of a propagating light beam. In free
space Laguerre-Gaussian (LG) beams carry well deﬁned OAM.
The discovery of the OAM of light has lead to a large interest in diﬀerent applications,
such as imaging, communication and control of atoms [46, 47]. Entanglement between
OAM states has been measured [48], and a measurement of OAM quantum numbers of
single photons has been demonstrated [49], and a proof of principle QKD experiment
with OAM modes in free space has been performed [28], hinting towards that achieving
practically useful secure bit rates using OAM is possible.
As OAM is a conserved quantity, a ﬁber in which diﬀerent OAM modes are guided
and suﬃciently separated such that small pertubations such as bends does not couple
diﬀerent modes, allows for a new potentially very large DOF (inﬁnite in free space) for
communication [25]. A general class of ﬁber that preserves OAM in the presence of strong
perturbations is a ﬁber featuring an air core and a high index annulus region, imitating
the form of the Laguerre-Gaussian beam [25, 50, 51]. The preservation of OAM in the
presence of perturbations is advantageous for QKD for reducing noise and errors due to
crosstalk from the transmission. Therefore the properties of OAM ﬁber modes provide an
advantage for QKD.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
Throughout the report photon-pair generation using OAM ﬁber modes for quantum com-
munication has been investigated.
This system has applications in secure information
communication, and is provably secure which classical algorithms such as RSA is not.
The BB84 protocol, which a sender, Alice, and a receiver, Bob, use to communicate
securely, has been discussed in detail to introduce the concepts of error correction and
privacy ampliﬁcation. Higher dimensional alphabet QKD has been investigated in terms
of information theory with an eavesdropper, Eve, using the intercept and resend strategy.
It is possible to determine an expression for a secure bit rate using the measurement prob-
abilities between Alice and Bob and the knowledge about Eves presence, which manifests
itself in the errors between Alice and Bob. The theory has been extented to include eﬀects
of imperfect sources, which create states that deviate from the agreed upon bases and has
multibit events. It has been concluded that using two mutually unbiased bases, a perfect
source with no multibit events, and an alphabet a high dimension is best for QKD. The
advantage of using photons as a quantum system for communication has been discussed,
and the use of heralded photon sources over weak coherent laser pulses has been concluded.
The theory behind photon-pair generation using spontaneous four wave mixing for use
as a heralded single photon source has been introduced. The quantum state of the photon
pairs generated with this method has been calculated in terms of the generation rate and
joint temporal amplitude of the generated photons. By considering a relation between
which modes the photons are generated in using multiple four wave mixing processes,
a state for the photon pairs has been calculated. The state is a coherent superposition
between the modes, which shows that the Hilbert space of single photons can be increased
using multiple processes. The state and the related measurement probabilities of the signal
photon has been calculated when measuring on the idler photon in two orthogonal bases.
The orbital angular momentum states of light have been investigated. They use the
transverse distribution of photons span a potentially large Hilbert space, and are stable
against transmission crosstalk between modes due to conservation of angular momentum.
Using the index proﬁle of a speciﬁc air core ﬁber created by OFS-Fitel a ﬁnite diﬀerence
in frequency domain numerical method has been used to ﬁnd the allowed modes calculate
the dispersion diagramme for the ﬁber. This dispersion diagramme is investigated for
phase-matching to ﬁnd allowed four-wave-mixing processes, and two suitable processes
are found which create single photons in diﬀerent modes. This is not a high dimension
Page 58 of 59

of the Hilbert space, however it provides a proof of concept. The transverse mode of the
photons have been found in the two mutually unbiased bases, and the generation rate for
each photon has been calculated allowing for pump coupling errors. The secure bit rate is
calculated based on the errors in pumping and the errors introduced by an eavesdropper.
A maximal secure bit rate of around 16kHz is expected when no errors are present.
Quantum key distribution is a vast subject and some abstractions and idealizations
have been made to reach a secure bit rate within the scope of the report. By deeper
investigation of the constituent parts of a quantum key distribution setup, an increase
in the correspondence between the theoretical calculations and practically achievable re-
sults in experiments is expected. The following subjects are recommendations for further
investigation.
The Hilbert space of the generated photon is extended if better four-wave-mixing
processes are found. One choice of wavelength parameters was made, but by performing
a parameter sweep of the wavelengths it is very realistic to ﬁnd a better process due to
the high number of allowed processes. Only states with aligned spin and orbital angular
momentum were investigated, however the antialigned states are also possible to use, and
thus creating single photons with hyperentanglement between spin and orbital angular
momentum [60], and giving rise to many more allowed processes.
A large parameter
investigation of the allowed modes would be interesting to perform.
Even though error has been modelled in the photon source, only absolute errors have
been investigated. Error with a normal distribution from ﬂuctuations and noise have not
been considered. An extension of the theory and treatment to include this would account
for many real eﬀects.
Propagation through the ﬁber has been assumed to be free of
attenuation and other third order eﬀects than four-wave-mixing. Loss and crosstalk is an
important part of quantum key distribution with orbital angular momentum states [46].
Modelling the propagation of classical and quantum states through the ﬁber to ﬁnd out
how long the signals can be faithfully transmitted is also an interesting investigation.
Detection has been considered only in its most idealized abstraction. No attention
has been focused on quantifying the eﬀects of dark counts, detector eﬃciency, detection
wavelength, and stray photons. Furthermore the idealized detection at Bob and Eve for
the secure rates have been treated as error free. An investigation can be made to extend
the theory to account for more realistic detectors, which give diﬀerent values for the secure
bit rates.
An experimental realization of quantum key distribution using the theoretically de-
scribed modes at the found wavelengths is the ultimate goal of the theoretical work. For
now, although it might even be possible to perform a demonstration with the states con-
sidered, any experimental realization might very well have diﬃculty in achieving the 16.5
kHz generation rate reported in this work, however the conclusions on how certain param-
eters aﬀect the secure bitrate and which modes to use at which wavelengths are directly
applicable in an experimental context.

Appendix A
RSA cryptosystem
The currently most used public key distribution protocol is the RSA cryptosystem. In
order to describe the details involved in the steps of the system, a few concepts needs to
be introduced. The ﬁrst of which is the modulo operation, secondly the notion of a greatest
common divisor between two numbers, and ﬁnally Euler ϕ-function. The modulo operator
outputs the remainder of the division between two natural numbers, a and b ̸= 0. The
relation between the two natural numbers can conveniently be written: a = q·b+r, (q, r) ∈
N, r < b. With q representing the quotient of division of a by b, and r representing the
remainder of the division. Thus a mod b = r.
A greatest common divisor between two numbers, a and b, is the largest number that
divides the two with no remainder. It can be expressed as the largest number, n, that is
the solution to both a mod n = 0, and b mod n= 0. This is formally written gcd(a,b)=n.
If two numbers have 1 as their greatest common divisor, they are called relatively prime
or coprime.
The Euler ϕ-function takes the input N and counts the number of integers less than N
which are relatively prime to N. If the input to the function is a prime number the output
given is one less than the prime, since all smaller numbers are relatively prime to a prime
number; ϕ(p) = p −1. Also if the input is the product of two relatively prime numbers,
M and N, then the output is the product of the function on each of the numbers, that is:
ϕ(N · M) = ϕ(N)ϕ(M).
The RSA can be explained by the communicating parties, Alice and Bob, performing
the following steps.
1. Bob, the receiver, produces a random set of distinct primes p, q and computes the
product N = pq. This is used for generating the encryption and decryption key.
2. Bob choses an encryption integer, e. This integer together with N forms the public
key and satisﬁes the following relations: 1 < e < ϕ(N), where from the discussion
of the Euler ϕ-function we know the value of the upper bound ϕ(N) = ϕ(pq) =
ϕ(p)ϕ(q) = (p −1)(q −1). The second relation for e to satisfy is gcd(e, ϕ(N)) = 1.
3. Bob choses a decryption integer, d. This integer forms the private key and satisﬁes
the following relation: de = 1(mod ϕ(N)). This number is kept a secret, and e, N
are published.

4. Alice, who wishes to send a message, 0 ≤M < N to Bob, uses the public key (e, N)
to generate a ciphertext from her message by doing the following: C = Me(mod N).
This ciphertext is sent to Bob, and is in principle extremely diﬃcult for an eaves-
dropper, Eve, to decrypt without the decryption key, Bobs private key.
5. Bob now uses his private key, d, on the received ciphertext, C, to obtain the original
encoded message which Alice inteded to send: Cd = M(modN). The proof of this
result can be found elsewhere [3, 6] .
Given the public key, e, N, it is not easy for an eavesdropper to ﬁnd the decryption key
d, or decrypt the ciphertext in any other way using a classical computer. A relatively
straight forward method of decryption is possible for Eve to perform; factoring N of the
known public key. This method provides an obstacle in terms of computing complexity,
and is very diﬃcult to perform on a classical computer requiring computational eﬀort
that is growing exponentially1 with the size of the number N, and will take decades or
even centuries to compute with current computers if the number is chosen large enough.
Quantum algorithms exist that solve the decryption methods in polynomial time, and
thus chosing numbers larger and larger will not have as signiﬁcant an eﬀect on a quantum
computer.

Appendix B
Shor’s algorithm
Shor’s algorithm, or Shor’s factoring algorithm, is a quantum algorithm which eﬃciently
determines the prime number factorization of an integer. It is based on a number of steps
that are eﬃcient to compute classically and one step that is not.
The steps that are
performed eﬃciently using classical algorithms seek to reduce the problem to ﬁnding the
period of a function, which is the step that is not eﬃciently done on a classical computer.
The algorithm for ﬁnding prime factors of a number, N, is as follows.
1. Choose an arbitrary integer y < N.
2. Perform a check to see if y is relatively prime to N, that is: gcd(y, N) = 1. If this
is not the case, a non trivial factor has been found in the greatest common divisor
of the two.
3. If they are, as expected, relatively prime however, the following function is created:
f(a) = ya mod N.
The crucial part is here to determine the period, r, of this
function.
The period is deﬁned as f(a) = f(a + r), and it is noted that since
f(0) = y0 mod N = 1 that f(r) = yr mod N = 1 This period can be found quickly
by using a quantum algorithm.
4. If r is odd, go back to step 1.
5. Rewrite yr mod N = 1 as (yr −1)mod N = 0. By using that r is even (follows
from step 4) it can further be written as (yr/2 + 1)(yr/2 + 1) mod N = 0. If either
(yr/2 ± 1) is divisible by N by themselves, go back to step 1.
6. The greatest common divisors of (yr/2 ± 1) with N are nontrivial factors of N and
thus N is factored by gcd(yr/2 + 1, N) and gcd(yr/2 −1, N).
Before describing the quantum algorithm for ﬁnding the period a small example is pre-
sented. Suppose N = 15, a choise of y is made: y = 13. These two numbers are relatively
prime. The period of the function, f(a) = 13a mod 15, can easily be evaluated for this
small scale problem: f(0) = 1, f(1) = 13, f(2) = 4, f(3) = 7, f(4) = 1, and thus
the period is 4, r = 4. Determining yr/2 ± 1 leads to the numbers 132 + 1 = 170 and
132 −1 = 168. The factors are thus: gcd(170, 15) = 5 and gcd(168, 15) = 3 and the
number has been factored: 15 = 5 · 3.

Appendix C
Divincenzo’s requirements
The requirements of [24] are written primarily for quantum computing, however it will
also serve as a good overview of what qualities are expected from a QKD perspective.
The qubit is the information carrier, however higher dimensional quantum systems like
qutrits, ququarts, and so on can also be used in place of the term qubit. The requirements
are as follows:
1. A scalable physical system with well characterized qubits.
Having well characterized and scalable qubits means that the quantum system can
take any superposition and the state of one qubit does not prohibit any other from
taking any arbitrary state, regardless of the number of qubits used.
2. The ability to initialize the state of the qubits to a simple ﬁducial state.
This ensures that a qubit registre can be initialized to some bit value, such as 0, on
which the computation can be performed.
3. Long relevant decoherence times, much longer than the gate operation
time.
This requirement ensures that information is not lost faster than it is needed to be
used, thus keeping erroneus bit rates at a minimum.
4. A universal set of quantum gates.
This requirement ensures that a quantum computer can perform all the gates needed
in any quantum algorithm. Together with the initialization requirement it enables
preparing qubits in any state.
5. A qubit-speciﬁc measurement capability.
This ensures that measurements can be done on the qubit level, such that the indi-
vidual qubits can be investigated after computation.
+1 The ability to interconvert stationary and ﬂying qubits.
Stationary qubits are in the quantum computing ﬁeld the qubits that stay in place
and perform the computing, whereas ﬂying qubits travel and carry information (used
in QKD). This requirement allows the quantum computer to communicate informa-
tion in between nodes. This requirement connects the previous 5 with QKD. As
VI
==PAGE:72==
Chapter C - Divincenzo’s requirements
the ﬂying qubit should be able to convert to the stationary ones, for which the 5
ﬁrst requirements must hold, in essence these should also hold for the ﬂying qubits
not necessarily intrinsically, but at least in connection with the stationary qubits.
For example, if a quantum computer needs to be able to measure on the stationary
qubits, by virtue of ﬂying qubits interconnecting and thus changing the static ones,
this just as much corresponds to measuring the ﬂying ones.
This is reasonable from the discussion of the BB84 protocol in Section 2.2. The ﬁrst
requirement comes about from the ability to deﬁne the qubit basis by Alice and Bob.
The second and fourth requirement allows Alice to encode her bit string in the qubits,
by initializing to a string of zero’s and letting a gate perform the transformations on
the single qubits. The third requirement allows the faithful communication of the
bitstring, such that errors are not too prevalent. Finally the ﬁfth requirement allows
Bob to measure each qubit sent to him to determine the measurement outcome in
the basis he has chosen. As such all the previous requirements are to be considered
when discussing QKD.
+2 The ability faithfully to transmit ﬂying qubits between speciﬁed locations.
This requirement is similar to the third requirement from the perspective of QKD,
whereas for a quantum computer these diﬀer slightly as computational qubits might
be diﬀerent from the communicating ones. The discussion of the third requirement
