Introduction
The immune system consists of two parts, the innate and the adaptive immune response.
Innate immune responses are the first layer of protection against pathogens, as it reacts
quickly but unspecific. The adaptive immune response needs a longer time to get activated
but gives a more directed reaction against the pathogen. Major histocompatibility complex
(MHC) molecules play a key role in the adaptive immune response. Their function is to
present ligands to other actors of the immune system, which is required to facilitate a
response against pathogens [1].
There are two major types of MHC molecules, MHC class I and MHC class II, which differ
in their subunit composition. While MHC class I is expressed on the surface of all nucleated
cells, which are all cells but red blood cells (erythrocytes). MHC class II molecules are
only expressed on so-called professional antigen-presenting cells (APCs). These APCs are
mainly dendritic cells (DCs), macrophages, and B cells, which have their main function
in the immune response. Which type of ligands and from which origin is presented on
the MHC molecule depends on the class. For MHC class I ligands are internally collected
in the cell, mostly self-peptides from the regular cellular process or in case of a viral or
other intracellular infection pathogenic peptides. Also in the case of cancer MHC class I
could present cancerous peptides. MHC class II mainly presents ligands that are taken up
by the extracellular medium of the cell, e.g. by phagocytosis (uptake of a large particle
like a bacteria) or endocytosis (uptake of smaller particles like proteins).
Each MHC
class has its own mechanism of preparing the protein into a smaller peptide which is then
loaded on the MHC molecule, respectively proteosomes or proteases which are breaking
up larger proteins into smaller peptides for class I and class II. Ligands in the context of
MHC molecules are peptides that are fractions of larger proteins. Both MHC class I and
class II present their peptides as a peptide-MHC complex (pMHC) to T-cells and their
T-cell receptor (TCR). The MHC molecule is necessary for a TCR:peptide interaction
because only bound peptides get recognized by TCRs and a TCR is only primed on one
specific type of MHC molecule, the so-called MHC restriction. During their development
T-cells are tested for a positive reaction to self-MHC molecules and only develop further if
successfully establish an interaction. If no positive interaction happens, the T-cell would
undergo apoptosis and die. In the process of TCR generation, multiple steps are taken
to achieve a large variety of TCRs with different specificity for different peptides. MHC
class I also binds to the CD8 co-receptor while MHC class II binds to the CD4 co-receptor.
Thus both MHC classes target two distinct T-cell populations. In case of an engagement
of pMHC and the TCR, the T cell gets activated to further react. For humans the MHC
1
==PAGE:5==
system is called human leukocyte antigen (HLA) system. The loci HLA-A, B, and C are
equivalents to MHC class I and HLA-DP, DM, DO, DQ, and DR to MHC class II. The
HLA system shows a large variability in existing alleles for the mentioned loci. There are
currently at least 10,754 known HLA class II alleles known [2]. The polymorphic regions
are especially important to ensure the ability to bind an unknown peptide in at least a few
individuals in a population. The anchor residues which shape the binding of a peptide to
an MHC molecule are called a sequence motif. Determining this motif is an important step
for designing a peptide vaccine, as the peptide has to be bound and presented effectively
on MHC molecules to be working [1]. It has been shown that predicting binding motifs
plays a crucial role in the design process of e.g. neoantigen cancer vaccines [3].
In this project the method NNAlign_MA has been investigated that can align peptide
along MHC class II molecules to find the best match of binding. Despite the high pre-
dictive power, one drawback of NNAlign_MA is the long training time required.
The
goal of NNAlign_MA is the enable the usage of liquid chromatography-mass spectrometry
experimental data, after a pre-training with binding-affinity data. An illustration of how
NNAlign_MA works can be seen in Fig. 1.
NNAlign_MA is based on an artificial neural network (ANN) programmed in C. The
original implementation does not use any highly optimized math libraries. Intel MKL has
been introduced to the code and evaluated. Other optimization techniques, like the ICC
compiler, have also been investigated in this project.
Furthermore, different techniques, to boost the predictive performance have been imple-
mented and evaluated.

rendered illustration of a binding groove from an MHC class II molecule loaded with an

2.1
Dataset
The dataset used in this project was mostly obtained from NetMHCIIpan-4.2 by Nilsson,
Kaabinejadian et al. [6] and the Immune Epitope Database (IEDB) [7]. There are three
parts in the dataset: single-allele binding affinity, single-allele eluted ligand, and multi-
allele eluted ligand. In the dataset were only MHC class II samples, from human leukocyte
antigen (HLA) alleles and mouse (Mus musculus) alleles. Explicitly excluded were BoLA
(Bovine Leukocyte Antigen) alleles. The data can be characterized by binder/non-binder,
number of MHC molecules, and peptide length.
Single-allele data was obtained by binding affinity assays. Binding affinity assays measure
how strong proteins are binding together. In this project, it is the binding between peptides
and MHC molecules. The affinity is expressed as half maximal inhibitory concentration
(IC50) measured in nM. A higher nM means a weaker binding affinity between the peptides
and MHC molecules [8].
The binding affinity can be scaled to be between 0 and 1 with:
affscale = 1 −

Affinities above 0.5 are then considered binders (positives).
A newer method to obtain MHC binding data is the liquid chromatography-mass spec-
trometry (LC-MS/MS). The general workflow of this method is lysing the cell, purifying
peptide-MHC complexes with MHC-specific antibodies, detaching and separating peptides
from MHC molecules, separating peptides with chromatography, and infusing the peptides
into a mass spectrometer. Afterward, spectral annotation methods are applied to obtain
the amino acid sequence [9].
Instead of measuring a continuous value of binding affinity, LC-MS/MS results in binary
data points where binders are represented by 1 and non-binders by 0. Binders are the
peptides that could be found in the dataset. Non-binders are random peptides chosen
from the UniProt database [10], that are known to not bind. For each length of peptides,
the same amount of non-binder was chosen as there were binders in the dataset [4].
The reason to include not only binding affinity single-allele data points but also eluted
multi-allele data points is that BA assays neglect the internal processing of peptides before
the presentation of MHC molecules and BA assays can not be done in a high-throughput
4
==PAGE:8==
manner [11] [4]. Therefore data obtained with the LC-MS/MS method gives more biolog-
ically relevant data which can be helpful in addition to the binding affinity data.
Eluted single-allele data points can also be obtained with the previously described MS
method. But instead of having multiple MHC molecules expressed on the cell and leading
to convoluted data, cells are genetically engineered to only express one MHC molecule [4].
In total, the binding affinity dataset 516,440 contains data points. Peptides of length 13-21
were in the dataset, while most peptides had a length of 15 amino acids (>420,000). As
mentioned earlier for each peptide length the data is distributed in 1:1 for binders and
non-binders. Some alleles are highly represented, like DRB1_0101, while others are less
present, like HLA-DQA10102-DQB10604.

Artificial neural networks
Artificial neural networks (ANN) are a fundamental concept in computer science. ANN
are also build up of neurons like biological neural networks. One purpose of an ANN is
regression for a continuous target value or classification for discrete target values [12].
The training process of a neural network involves iteratively adjusting the weights of neu-
rons to minimize the error between the predicted values and the ground truth. Initially, the
weights are typically randomly initialized, and then the network goes through a series of
forward passes and backpropagation steps. During backpropagation, the gradients of the
weights are computed using techniques like gradient descent, and the weights are updated

After the forward-step, the error between the predicted value and the known truth can
be calculated. To correct the weights and therefore minimize the error a backpropagation
step is taken.
Backpropagation refers to the process of propagating the error backward through the net-
work, from the output layer to the input layer. It involves calculating the gradient of the
error with respect to each weight in the network. By using the chain rule of calculus, the
gradient is computed layer by layer, starting from the output layer and moving toward the
input layer.
There are multiple algorithms for backpropagation, one of them is gradient descent (GD).
The general idea is to update the weights by moving in the negative direction of the
gradient. This means that the weights are adjusted to decrease the error, aiming to find a
configuration that minimizes it. By iteratively updating the weights based on the gradient
information, the network gradually converges towards a better set of weights that yields

2.3
Compiler
A compiler is a computer program that translates human-readable programming code of a
specific programming language to a machine-executable form. There are various compilers
for different purposes, languages, and target machines. In this project, the focus is on the
programming language C, as the NNAlign_MA has been developed in C.
As the GNU Compiler Collection (GCC) compiler is a widely used compiler for C and
was previously used for NNAlign_MA, it served as a baseline for comparison for other
compilers. Version 11.3.0 has been used in this project [13].
The Intel C/C++ Compiler (ICC) was used as an alternative compiler, as ICC shows
very good performance on Intel machines, which were used in this project [14]. For this
project, the ICC version 2019.5.281 was used, as in newer versions compilation errors for
batched BLAS functions were occurring. By now ICC is deprecated and the Intel oneAPI
DPC++/C++ Compiler is maintained instead.
Both GCC and ICC can specifically optimize the code for a specific CPU. The CPU
architectures are shown in Appendix 6.1.
There are various flags for enabling the optimization techniques the compiler can apply.
Tab. 1 provides an overview of the used compiler flags. The -Ofast flags turn a list of other
techniques on, like -no-prec-div which could increase the speed at the expense of accuracy
[15].
Another technique is vectorization. Modern processors, like the ones used, can use Single
instruction, multiple data (SIMD) if turned on during the compilation.
From a set of
9
==PAGE:13==
SIMD-able instructions, a single instruction can be executed simultaneously on multiple
data points. The length of the vector that stores the data points is depending on the
hardware. The bigger the vector, the more data can be executed at the same time. The
data type is also important, as a 32-bit single-precision floating point number takes 4 bytes,
and a 64-bit double-precision floating point number takes 8 bytes. Therefore a vector of
length 256 bits for the first AVX version can store eight float numbers or four double
numbers. There are multiple versions of AVX, like AVX, AVX2, or AVX512, which differ
in the size of the vector they are using and the instruction set usable for SIMD. Another
older SIMD technique is SSE, which can also be used by Intel processors. No manual afford
was made to manually add SIMD pragmas to loops which would enforce SIMD but could
lead to errors if not done correctly. The ICC compiler can produce an optimization report
which recommends code sections for the usage of SIMD pragmas [16].
It has been verified that both, ICC and GCC, can detect AVX2, the best vector instruction
set available on the DTU HPC.
The most time-consuming function is forward_inp, as it does the complete forward-step
(90% of execution time in GCC). Both ICC and GCC claim to unroll and vectorize the loops
according to the optimization reports of the compilers. This verifies the correct selection
of compiler flags.
But only ICC is, according to the assembler code, able to vectorize
forward_inp because of a floating point reduction. To enable floating point reductions in
GCC -ffast-math would need to be set.
The flag -funroll-all-loops can be advantageous as the overhead of the loop could be limited.
The increment and stop condition of the loop can be calculated less frequently in unrolled
loops and the iterations can be hard-coded in the assembly code as repeating code blocks.

An important consideration for optimizing the execution speed is the cache structure of
CPUs. CPUs have a hierarchical structure of different cache levels, as illustrated in Fig. 9.
The further away the cache is from the computing unit, the core, the larger the memory
space and the slower the cache. Therefore the goal is to keep temporally relevant data close
to the core, as fetching data from the lower-level cache takes time. To find the relevant
data, the CPU requests the line from the closest cache (L1). If this results in a hit, the data
can be directly loaded into the core. If instead, the data is not in L1, the data is requested
from the next level (L2). This continues for all levels in the hierarchy. The overall goal
is therefore to keep the miss-rate as low as possible to avoid time-expensive loading from
lower-level cache [18].
It is known that for example, the ordering of nested loops influences the cache performance
[19]. For the introduction of new nested loops, this concept has been considered. In the
examples of Alg. 5 compared to Alg. 4, the inner two loops, which an executed more often,
iterate row-wise over the matrices and therefore match the row-major matrix storage in C
[20].

Another method for speed optimization is cache prefetching.
There is software-based
and hardware-based prefetching.
Software-based prefetching is disabled by default by
ICC while hardware-based prefetching is built in the CPU. The goal of prefetching is to
load data/instructions ahead of time from a slower lower-level cache into a faster higher-
level cache by predicting which cache line will be needed soon and loading it ahead of
12
==PAGE:16==
time. Successful predictions result in a higher hit and lower miss rates, which boost speed
performance [23].
A downside of code optimization is that the executable can be bigger, e.g. with the flag -
funroll-all-loops, more assembler code is added to the executable. This could even lead to a
reduction in execution speed, as the instruction from the executable has to be loaded in the
Instruction cache of the L1 cache, a phenomenon called code bloat [24]. The compilation
can also take a longer time, e.g. for ipo, as other code files get optimized as well. The
goal of this project was to optimize the execution time and other factors were therefore
not considered further.

multiple functionalities but in this project, only linear algebra and memory allocation have
been used. In the library linear algebra functions are implemented as an instance of the
Basic Linear Algebra Subprograms (BLAS) specification, first introduced by Lawson et al.
1979 [28].

While there are many implementations of BLAS like OpenBLAS, ATLAS, or cuBLAS (for
Nvidia GPUs), Intel MKL has been chosen as it promised the best performance in an Intel
CPU environment [29] [30]. The library is proprietary developed and specially optimized
for Intel processors but not limited to them.
The functions of BLAS, in general, can be divided into three levels: vector-vector (Level
1), matrix-vector (Level 2), and matrix-matrix (Level 3). As mentioned in the section
Artificial neural networks 2.2, the forward-step of ANNs can be described as a matrix-
matrix multiplication, a so-called general matrix multiply (GEMM) function in BLAS
[28].
BLAS calls can be executed on different data types. In this project, only the single precision
float has been used, as it was previously implemented. Even though a double precision
could eventually give a slightly higher predictive performance, it would significantly slow
down the execution time, as will be explained further in 2.3. As Intel MKL is developed
proprietary, it can not be investigated how the optimization works. But one has to assume
that techniques like ordering of loops, and loop blocking are used, as well as an efficient
algorithm for matrix-matrix multiplication [31] [32].

distribution of data takes a longer time than the benefiting of running the multiplication
in parallel [34].
Intel proposed the parameter Just-In-Time (JIT) code generation for small matrices in

2.6
NNAlign_MA
The computational methods NNAlign_MA by Alvarez et al. [4] is the foundation of this
project. NNAlign_MA has been developed for the investigation of the immunopeptidome,
the peptides presented by major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules on the sur-
face of antigen-presenting cells. NNAlign is an algorithm by Andreatta et al. which aligns
peptide sequences along a binder molecule, in this case MHC, to identify binding patterns.
NNAlign works only on single-allele (SA) data [36]. SA data points are only annotated to
one MHC. Other frameworks, like NetMHCpan [37] and NetMHCIIpan [38] use NNAlign.
The NNAlign_MA algorithm extends NNAlign for multi-allele (MA) data.
MA data
points are annotated to multiple MHCs.
The task of NNAlign_MA is to deconvolute
the peptide:MHC interaction from a MA peptides dataset to predict the binding motifs of
the MHC molecules [4]. An NNAlign_MA trained model serves as a pan-specific model
that has the potential to predict binding affinities on MHC molecules that it has not
been trained on. Like NNAlign, NNAlign_MA is facilitating this task by looping over
all possible binding cores for a 9mer, a peptide 9 amino acids long, and aligning the
peptide to the position that gives the highest score calculated by the network. Additionally,
NNAlign_MA can take insertions and deletions into consideration. Although this part
is not investigated for the project.
NNAlign_MA has been benchmarked on multiple
datasets, including MHC class I and class II data, and has been shown to perform very
well [4].
NNAlign_MA is an artificial neural network (ANN 2.2) with one hidden layer and the last
neuron of each layer acts as a bias, where the value is always set to 1. It has two output
neurons, one for binding affinity values and another for eluted ligand likelihood values. All
the other weights/neurons are shared to enable transfer knowledge of the two distinct but
related problems and improve the predictive performance [4].
Like NNAlign it starts with encoding the peptide sequence by BLOSUM encoding to
17
==PAGE:21==
incorporate amino acid similarity into the encoding, which results in a BLOSUM log-
odds vector. Additionally, the regions surrounding the peptide, known as peptide flanking
regions (PFR), are represented by a BLOSUM substitution frequency vector, which spans
a maximum length of three amino acids [4].
Before the training, all network weights are set randomly. The weights are updated by a
gradient descent back-propagation [8]. NNAlign_MA starts the training with a burn-in
period on only SA data points. In all following experiments, the burn-in duration has been
kept to 20 epochs as this was also originally used. [4]
After this so-called pre-training, MA data points get annotated to a single MHC molecule
by the trained model. This annotation is done by the previously trained weights and is
therefore dependent on the burn-in period. The best MA binder is found by iterating
over the multiple MHC molecules included in the selected cell line. Another iteration is
happening over the offset, which defines the 9mer that is the peptide binding core. The
combination with the highest score predicted by the neural network is assumed to be the
binding position on a MHC molecule [4]. This is illustrated in Fig. 11.

dosequence, peptide cores (red), peptide flanking regions (green), and the predicted score

ROC curves and AUC are useful because they provide a comprehensive evaluation of a
binary classifier’s performance that is not affected by the specific threshold chosen for clas-
sification. They also allow for easy comparison of different classifiers or different versions
of the same classifier. Furthermore, ROC curves and AUC are robust to class imbalance,
which is a common problem in binary classification when one class is much more prevalent
than the other [39].
2.7.4
Cross-validation
To avoid performance overestimation and model overfitting, where a model gives good
performance only on the training set, but can not generalize the knowledge on a different
dataset, partitioning of datasets has to be performed. Then a part of the training set is used
for the training and another part is used for the testing after the training. A structured
way to do this, without losing data to get trained on, is K-fold cross-validation (CV). In
this method, the data is split up into 5 equally sized parts. Each model gets trained on 4
of these parts and 1 part is used for the testing. This is done for 5 models and the assigned
test set changes for each model so that every part is used as both training and test set at
one point [40]. After testing each set individually on one of the five partitions, the test
sets are concatenated into one dataset, and performance metrics, like AUC, AUC 0.1, and
PPV, are determined on the complete set.
21
==PAGE:25==
Highly similar sequences should not be in the same data partition, as this would limit
the number of unique data points and thus the learning abilities. Pre-partitioned sets of
sequences with minimal overlap were made with a method introduced in NetMHCIIpan-4.0
[41].
To boost the performance and to enable reliable testing, the final predictions are made
by an ensemble of 100 models, as was also done for the original NNAlign_MA. The final
decision of the ensemble is determined as an average of all models decisions. The 100

3.2
Batch Gradient Descent
The gradient descent algorithm implemented in NNAlign_MA is called stochastic gradient
descent (SGD). For every data point a forward-step is done and directly afterwards the
weights are updated for a single-value loss. SGDs advantage is that it is able to jump
out of local minima to possibly better minima. But the convergence will be more instable
as one sample could lead it into one direction and a following sample in a completely
different direction. Furthermore, it can not easily be optimized time-wise. There are two
alternatives to this approach, the batch and mini-batch gradient descent. [46]
The batch gradient descent (BGD) does the forward-step for all data points in an epoch
and afterwards updates the weights once for an average of the gradient over all data points.
BGD is supposed do give the fastest convergence of gradient descent variations for convex
learning problems. [46]
For large datasets and common computing systems the entire dataset can not fit in the
memory. An important property of convex problems is that any local minimum is also a
global minimum. But many complex problems are also not convex learning problems but
have local minima that are not global minima [47]. As the decision whether a problem
30
==PAGE:34==
is convex or not for a problem with more than multivariate polynomial of degree four is
NP-hard, it can not be determined beforehand if the problem NNAlign_MA tries to solve
in convex, but only be interpreted afterwards [48].
A trade-off between SGD and BGD is the mini-batch gradient descent (MBGD). Instead
of taking a single sample or the complete dataset in one step, a batch of a specific size, the
batch size n, is constructed. For each batch in an epoch, the forward-step and then the
backpropagation with updating the weights is done. MBGD is supposed to allow faster
convergence then SGD with feasible requirements for the memory. It could potentially also
deal better with non-convex learning problems than BGD. [49]
Both SGD and MBGD have been tested in this project. BGD can not be tested with the
given training data size as there would not be enough memory to store the data. [50]
An aspect of batched calculations to be considered is that the backpropagation can also
be done in a batched manner.
The challenge is that the input batch can consist of two types, binding affinity or eluted
ligand likelihood. As the relevant output neuron is chosen for each data point based on
the datatype there has to be a differentiation in the backpropagation step. The problem
was solved by running the backpropagation step twice, for the binding affinity and eluted
ligand likelihood samples separately.
In Fig. 18, the mean loss for training and testing for 100 models in the ensemble is been
shown. It can be seen that the average loss for both, training and testing is lower for
batch gradient descent. Especially a lower test loss would indicate that the model is giving
better predictive performance and is not just overfitting due to a lower training loss. The
standard deviation for the training loss is equally wide for both methods. But for the test
loss, the standard deviation of MBGD is consistently narrower.

3.4
Reducing epochs
A parameter to be optimized in machine learning is the number of training epochs. A large
number of epochs could lead to overfitting, where the training loss is still decreasing while
the test loss is increasing.
As can be seen in Fig. 21, the training loss is going into slow decrease after an initial steep
decrease. The validation loss is not significantly moving in a direction after the decrease
after 50 epochs. These findings motivate to investigate the reduction of training epochs.

Reducing the number of training epochs would lead to a reduced execution time. It would
expected that the execution time scales linear for the number of epochs. The number of
training epochs for this experiment was reduced from 300 epochs to 100 epochs.
As expected the execution time got reduced by 40% compared to the baseline. The reason
that this is below the theoretical reduction of 66%, for 1/3 of the epochs, is that there is

the baseline with more training epochs. The difference is significantly worse (p-value ≤

3.5
MA annotation decay
As could be seen in Fig. 24, the times MA binders gets annotated a new allele per epochs
declines steep until around epoch 150, afterwards it is almost a plateau at a low level.
Therefore the idea came up to decay the frequency of MA annotation depending on the
epoch. It was decided to split the training into three parts. In the first 100 epochs the
MA samples were annotated an allele every epoch, like in the native implementation. In
the following 100 epochs, MA samples were annotated an allele every second epoch and
for the last 100 epochs, in every fourth epoch.
This approach speed up the execution time by 40%.
As can be seen in Fig.
23, the
performance was significantly worse but with only a marginal difference in mean values
(AUC: -0.0005, AUC 0.1: -0.001, PPV: -0.002). Further tests could find an optimal decay
strategy to get a speed gain without losing significantly predictive performance. Although
it would not be a big gain as already this significantly worse strategy gave 40% time saving,
which is a speed-up of 1.7.

3.7
Sample weighting
Sample weighting was investigated because it can be seen in the dataset that there is an
imbalance between the occurrences of the different alleles (classes), Fig. 8. As can be
seen in Fig. 27, there is a drop in all performance metrics at a sample size of 5096 (HLA-
DPA10103-DPB10101). This so-called class imbalance problem is due to the differences in
positive samples for some alleles in the dataset. It can be seen that there are alleles with an
39
==PAGE:43==
AUC above 0.6 but 0 PPV. This indicates an all-negative prediction, which is a common
phenomenon for the class imbalance problem [51]. A method to solve this problem is to
introduce a weight for samples that determines the importance during the training.
Another important factor is that also the performance metrics themselves get unstable.
Fewer testing data points lead to higher fluctuations in the performance, as there could
be a few random alleles that are guessed correctly. Sample weighting can not solve this
problem, as it does not change the testing but only the training.

The difficulty to determine the frequencies of classes in NNAlign_MA is that it can not
be done on the training dataset because of the convoluted MA binders. Therefore the
frequencies of classes are determined on the predicted test set. This could lead to overfitting
as it builds upon an already trained network.
The sample weights can then be multiplied onto the loss. It has been decided to use sample
weighting after the pre-training period. As the sample weights are positive numbers larger
than 0.0, a positive loss would be increased or decreased but still positive and a negative
loss would be increased or decreased but still be negative respectively. A higher absolute
loss then enforces a larger change onto the weights during the backpropagation.
The
weighting method has to be chosen reasonably. Too big differences between the native
sample weights would have the same effect as choosing a large learning rate η.
Common functions for the transformation functions are natural logarithm, logarithm with
base 2, logarithm with base 10, square root, or the identity function. As can be seen in Fig.
28, for the mentioned functions, log2 seems to be a reasonable choice, as it gives a steady
declining function over the sample size. For all functions and the normalization factors 1.0,
5.0, 10.0, 20.0, 30.0, 40.0, and 50.0 a grid search was done to test the performance in one
model with the same seed and parameters. It was proven that log2 with a normalization
factor of 40.0 gave the best results on the test set (data not shown).

4
Discussion
The main objective of this project was to reduce the computation time needed for training
as well as investigate techniques to improve the predicative performance of NNAlign_MA.
This project achieved both goals, to improve the speed as well as the predictive perfor-
mance. The reached speed-up of almost 15x is especially impressive as, like in the original
implementation only one thread is used. Combined with other accelerating methods, show-
ing also a good predictive performance like MA annotation decay, a speed-up of up to 24x
would be possible.
It could be seen that simply changing the compiler from GCC to ICC with reasonable
compiler flags gives an impressive acceleration of the execution time with a similar predic-
tive performance. As this change can be done easily in most software projects, it could be
interesting to investigate the speed performance in other projects as well. Especially as sav-
ing computational resources could also save energy and budget for running the HPC. The
usage of Intel MKL for the matrix-matrix multiplication only gave a speed-up when used
in combination with mini-batch gradient descent compared to the native implementation
compiled with ICC. The approach of reducing the number of training epochs reduced the
execution time but limited the predictive performance by doing so. The reduction of epochs
and decaying of the MA annotation requires a careful determination of their parameters
and strategies to find an optimal balance between speed and predictive performance.
The best approach to improve the predictive performance was sample weighting with the
log2 function. Other methods like mini-batch gradient descent or changing the compiler had
a positive impact on the computation speed and did not change the predictive performance.
Dynamic MA annotation did not have any influence on both predictive as well as speed
performance.
It could also be investigated whether parallelization in a multi-threaded version is possible
by manually introducing parallelism, as auto-parallelism has been shown to be not func-
tional. Although only using a single-core version has the advantage of saving resources,
most CPUs have multiple cores and HPC systems consist of multiple CPUs. Especially the
functions responsible for encoding peptides and MHC pseudosequences could potentially
be parallelized on the level of multiple samples being encoded simultaneously or even par-
allelize multiple encoding steps for a single sample. They have not been optimized beyond
the compiler level. Another way of parallelization is batched function calls, which are able
to handle multiple matrix-matrix multiplications at once and can distribute them over mul-
tiple threads, particularly useful for multiple small matrices as a single small matrix-matrix
multiplication can not benefit from parallelism.
46
==PAGE:50==
There are other oneAPI, non-BLAS standard, functions for small matrices which could
be investigated for their speed performance, like mkl_sgemm_compact. Also, the sparsity
of handled matrices could be investigated to eventually make use of specialized sparse
matrix-matrix multiplication functions, like mkl_sparse_s_mm. Those functions would re-
quire additional effort for implementation. oneAPI offers the library Deep Neural Network
Library (oneDNN) which could be used for completely rewriting the NNAlign_MA neural
network components.
Through the time savings achieved in the project, more time-consuming modifications,
which were not feasible before, to the algorithm and network could be investigated to
boost the performance even further. For example, another hidden layer could be intro-
duced as this could capture even more complex motifs. Also, the previously mentioned
insertion/deletion feature was not feasible for big datasets before, because the computa-
tional complexity is soaring. For the earlier described workflow in NNAlign_MA, this
would add another nested loop for the score calculation.
It can be concluded that optimizing a program time-wise needs thorough investigation even
though simple changes, like a different compiler, can improve execution speed a lot. Tuning
the predictive performance for a well-researched method like NNAlign_MA has been more
difficult. Only the sample weighting showed a significant and noticeable improvement.
