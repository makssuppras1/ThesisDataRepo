Abstract
Additive manufacturing (AM) of metals using Vat Photopolymerization (VPP) has
emerged as a frontier in the production of three-dimensional (3D) components with in-
tricate details and complex geometries on both macro- and micro-scales, especially for
applications in energy generation and storage. Despite the growing interest, achieving
high-resolution metal fabrication with tunable microstructures using state-of-the-art
powder-based AM technologies presents substantial challenges.
This thesis intro-
duces a novel methodology for fabricating sophisticated metallic structures through
the Mask Projection (MP) using metal salt precursors (specifically CuSO4) in a photo-
resin, followed by thermal and chemical reduction processes. The thermal decompo-
sition of the 3D-printed metal compound and subsequent chemical reduction offer
a promising route to synthesize metals components with tunable morphology and
complex geometries.Through experimental investigation, it explores optimal slurry
compositions, process parameters’ effects, and post-processing techniques for high
solid loading parts. The findings advance metal AM by demonstrating metal salts’
potential through VPP, offering a new approach to producing detailed metal parts
eﬀiciently and cost-effectively, thus broadening VPP applications in the industry.
Keywords: Additive Manufacturing (AM), Vat Photopolymerization (VPP), Digital
Light Processing (DLP), Metal Salt Precursors, CuSO4 (Copper Sulfate).

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Additive Manufacturing (AM) has revolutionized the production of three-dimensional
parts by adding thin layers of material in accordance with a digital model.
This
method enables the creation of intricate, customized components without traditional
tooling or molds, simplifying many conventional processing steps.
AM is particu-
larly advantageous in aerospace, medical, energy, and automotive sectors, allowing
for complex designs. Recent technological advancements in AM, including more af-
fordable lasers and enhanced computing capabilities, have solidified its status as a
cutting-edge manufacturing process pushing the resolution boundaries within the mi-
cro scale. This technology is increasingly being adopted in various industries, with
a focus on producing structurally robust and reliable parts through a comprehensive
understanding of materials, processes, and properties [9].
AM offers numerous advantages, including the freedom to design intricate structures,
the capability for mass customization, significant reduction in material waste, and the
ability to fabricate complex geometries. Additionally, it allows to perform rapid pro-
totyping, enhancing the eﬀiciency and speed of the manufacturing process [22]. .Vat
photopolymerization (VPP), one of the several AM processes, has drawn scientific
and industrial interest because it can create parts with a high degree of resolution
and an excellent surface finish. VPP’s utilization is restricted to polymer-based ma-
terials. Which limits its utility in sectors like aerospace, automotive, and biomedical
that demand metallic components. Metal slurry’s for use in VPP have been developed
to address this restriction. The objective of this project is to develop metal slurries
suitable for VPP, enabling the fabrication of metal parts. The aim is to determine
the optimal ratios of metal salts and other components for the development of metal
slurries by using metal salts route with different process parameters, optimizing pro-
cess parameters to produce metal green parts and sintered parts that have high solid
loading with good precision and resolution.
1.0.1
Vat Photopolymerization
Since the emergence of the initial Additive manufacturing system in the 1980s, a
diverse range of techniques has emerged. These include material extrusion, material
jetting, vat photopolymerization, binder jetting, powder bed fusion, sheet lamination,
and directed energy deposition.[35] Among these VPP, stands out as a widely recog-
nized fabrication technique in the realm of additive manufacturing. It has garnered
==PAGE:20==

extensive interest across various fields, including meta-materials, jewelry, footwear,
dentistry, and biomedical engineering [35]. This technology is characterized by its
multidisciplinary approach, where a photo-reactive resin is crosslinked upon exposure
to light at a specific wavelength. This seemingly straightforward technique, involv-
ing the sequential bonding of layers each representing a cross-section of a geometry,
culminates in the creation of physical three-dimensional objects. However, this sim-
plicity belies a complex process chain encompassing multiple steps and actions that
critically influence the quality and performance of the final product. Since its incep-
tion in 1984, VPP has evolved significantly, branching out into a diverse array of
machine configurations and material consolidation processes [27].
1.0.2
Working principle of Photopolymerization
VPP uses a liquid photopolymer resin to create three-dimensional objects. In VPP,
a vat or tank contains the liquid resin, and a light source, such as a laser or a digital
projector, which selectively exposes the resin to solidify it layer by layer, following
a digital design. This reaction generates insoluble, infusible, highly cross-linked net-
works. The process starts from a liquid resin, comprised of monomers, oligomers,
photoinitiators (PI), and additives (e.g. dyes, UV blockers, dispersants). Radical
photoinitiators are an essential component of a photopolymer resin used to start the
cross-linking reaction. Polymers are long chains formed by interconnected repeating
units of monomers and oligomers. Monomers are single units, whereas oligomers are
comprised of a few numbers of monomer repeating units. PI are molecules in charge
of generating reactive species (i.e. free radicals) when they are excited with radiation
(in this case, ultraviolet light). An eﬀicient PI exhibits fast curing rates, improving
photopolymer properties [27].

1.0.3
VPP Process:
Firstly, it is required to prepare a 2D digital model of the desired object that needs to
be printed using Computer-Aided Design (CAD) software. Then the designed model
should be sliced into thin cross-sectional layers.
Then, the vat should be set up.
The process begins with a vat filled with a liquid photopolymer resin. To fabricate
a part using VPP, a build platform is lowered into the vat, immersing it into the
liquid resin.
Then, a light source, typically a laser or a digital projector, is used
to selectively expose specific areas of the resin according to the desired part’s cross-
section. The light causes the exposed resin to undergo polymerization and solidify,
while the unexposed resin remains in a liquid state. Once the first layer is cured,
the build platform is incrementally raised or the vat is lowered to create space for
the subsequent layers. The process is repeated, exposing and curing one layer at a
time, until the entire part is completed. After the part is fully built, it is typically
rinsed or washed to remove any excess liquid resin and after which it undergoes
additional post-processing steps such as curing under UV light to ensure complete
polymerization [27].
1.1
Background and Motivation
A Growing Field in AM is VPP, and is primarily used for printing polymers. However,
its recent expansion to include ceramics has sparked interest in its potential for metal
printing. This project began with the question: Can VPP be adapted to manufacture
metals? A thorough literature review was initiated to explore existing studies and
applications in this area.
Challenges in Finding Literature on Metal VPP:
The quest for information
on metal printing using VPP revealed a significant gap in the available literature.
While companies like LITHOz have ventured into this realm, their focus remains
largely on ceramics. The limited public release of research in metal VPP, primarily
due to proprietary rights, poses a major barrier to progress in this innovative field.
Motivation:
The main motivation is to overcome barriers in metal VPP research,
There is a scarcity of public information and the secrecy surrounding current technolo-
gies in metal VPP which highlight the necessity for open research initiatives. This
project is driven by the goal of transcending these challenges and contributing mean-
ingful insights to the field of metal printing using VPP. The successful application
of VPP in producing detailed and intricate metallic structures could revolutionize
manufacturing processes across multiple industries such as biomedical devices and
micro-electromechanical systems, where the process’s high resolution and accuracy
are critical for functionality.
[28].

1 Introduction
Challenges in Metal-Based VPP:
The primary challenges in utilizing VPP for
metal fabrication lie in the integration of metal or metal precursor materials into the
photopolymer resin. This integration must maintain suitable properties and stability
for effective printing. Post-processing steps, such as debinding and sintering, also
require careful optimization to ensure the structural integrity and desired material
properties of the final product [33, 28].
Exploring Metal Salt Precursors:
Recent advancements in VPP have focused on
utilizing metal salts as precursors. This approach potentially addresses the challenges
associated with direct metal integration. The use of metal salts in conjunction with
suitable photopolymers facilitates the creation of green parts through VPP. These
parts are then converted into metallic components through thermal and chemical
treatments.
The development of this method holds promise for fabricating high-
resolution metal parts with complex geometries and microstructures, essential for
applications in microelectronics, biomedical devices, and aerospace components [18,
29, 31].
Contribution to the Field:
This research aims to bridge the knowledge gap re-
garding metal salt precursors in VPP for micro-component fabrication. Focusing on
CuSO4, the study intends to develop a comprehensive understanding of the process
parameters, material properties, and potential applications of the resulting metal
components. The findings could pave the way for more eﬀicient, cost-effective, and
versatile manufacturing methods for high-resolution metal parts, expanding the scope
and capabilities of VPP in industrial and technological applications.
1.2
Research Questions
1. Can metals be fabricated using VPP?
2. Is it feasible to develop metal slurries that remain stable without the need for
mechanical agitation and can be used for printing several days after preparation
without sedimentation?
3. What impact do variations in moisture content and other process parameters
have on the final quality of the manufactured part?

CHAPTER 2
State of Art
Many studies were conducted on AM during the 1970s and it was first patented in
1984 by Charles Hull. The manufacturing process can be classified broadly into 4
different categories. The first category is by subtractive manufacturing where the
process begins with the raw material and strip away slowly the excess material using
techniques like turning, milling, drilling, grinding, cutting, and boring.
The next
type of method is by forming - in which a mass of material undergoes a deformation
under pressure. The third kind of technique is by using casting, where the material
is heated up to its melting point and then poured into a specific mold to form the
object. Finally, the newly evolving technique which is the main focus of this paper
is AM. Though fundamentally AM encompasses a broader picture which involves the
entire workflow from design to manufacturing [4], for the context of this report AM
and 3D production are treated as synonymous. Compared to other techniques AM
production has many advantages like the ability to manufacture complex structures,
fast prototyping, lightweight and better strength to weight ratio parts.
2.1
Additive manufacturing process (AM)
In AM, the production begins with creating a 3D model using CAD software or by
scanning a physical object. This model is converted into Standard Tessellation Lan-
guage (STL) format, which accurately represents the object’s surface with a network
mesh of triangles. In the pre-processing stage, this mesh model is prepared by slicing
the STL file as in figure 2.1. During processing, the object is printed layer by layer,
supported by the platform, previous layers, or additional support structures. After
printing, postprocessing involves various refining procedures like washing, curing, pol-
ishing, debinding and sintering to enhance the object’s mechanical properties. The
eﬀiciency of these stages, particularly in VPP, significantly influences the final prod-
uct’s quality [24]. Traditionally, the object is sliced cross-sectionally to form a layer
and then laid on top of one another.

2.2
Metal Additive Manufacturing
Metal additive manufacturing (MAM) encompasses a specialized area within the
broader AM domain, focusing on metal printing. This field has expanded to include
a diverse array of technologies such as Powder Bed Fusion (PBF), Electron Beam
Melting (EBM), Selective Laser Melting (SLM), Binder Jetting, Directed Energy De-
position (DED), and Laminated Object Manufacturing (LOM), among others. These
processes enable the fabrication of parts from metals like Stainless Steel, Nickel, In-
conel, Chromium, Titanium, and Copper. The integration of MAM has prompted
an evolution in conventional manufacturing methods to enhance compatibility with
metal fabrication processes. The following are some of the predominant techniques
in the MAM sector, as documented in the literature [30].
Powder Bed Fusion (PBF) : This method utilizes a high-power laser (as in Direct
Metal Laser Sintering (DMLS)/SLM) or an electron beam (in EBM) to fuse metal
powder particles together, building the part layer-by-layer.
Manufacturers include: EOS, 3D Systems, Renishaw, SLM Solutions, Concept Laser,
Arcam.
Binder Jetting : In this technique, metal powder is joined using a binding agent in
successive layers to form a ”green” part. This part is then subjected to thermal post-
processing (sintering) to eliminate the binder, resulting in a solid metal part.
Manufacturers include: Desktop Metal, ExOne, Digital Metal, HP
Metal Material Extrusion : This process involves extruding a filament or rod that
contains metal powder mixed with polymer through a nozzle (similar to Fused De-

Additive manufacturing of Copper
Pure copper is a very essential metal which has good electrical, thermal properties
which are desirable in multiple fields such as:
• Electrical wiring (copper is a conductor of heat and electricity)
• Building material for roofing, plumbing, decorative elements and sculptures
• Constituent of various metal alloys, such as bronze (a copper-tin alloy), brass
(a copper-zinc alloy) and sterling silver (silver-copper alloy)
• Machinery and motors
• Marine applications: copper is biostatic, bacteria or other life forms will not
grow on it. It was used in shipping to prevent growth on hulls
• Sinks, faucets, doorknobs, handrails etc. Because copper is biostatic it is used
(in various alloys) in appliances that are handled by many people, to avoid
spreading bacteria
• Thermal management systems
• Aerospace industry
• Electrical vehicles
This widespread demand has fueled interest in the AM of copper. However, integrat-
ing copper into AM processes faces significant challenges due to several key issues [15,

Usage of Metal Salts over direct metal powder
In this research, the metal salt route is chosen instead of the metal powder route
for VAT photopolymerization of micro-metal components. The metal salt selected
is CuSO4, which will be used to produce pure copper through post-processing. The
metal salt route offers several advantages over the metal powder route in the context
of digital light processing (DLP) for additive manufacturing of metal parts[18]:
Uniform Dispersion: Metal salts can be uniformly dispersed in the photo-resin, leading
to better homogeneity in the final printed part compared to metal powders, which
may have issues with agglomeration and uneven distribution.
Reduced Sedimentation: Metal salts in the resin suspension can help mitigate issues
related to sedimentation and stability of the photo-resin during storage and printing

Gyroid pattern
Discovered in 1970 by NASA scientist Alan Schoen, a gyroid is
a structure found in nature with a distinctive geometry that lacks any straight lines,
known as a triply periodic minimal surface (TPMS)[17]. TPMS are surfaces that
exhibit zero mean curvature, distinguished by their ability to minimize local area,
meaning any small segment extracted from a TPMS will have the least possible area
when compared to any other segments with the same boundary conditions. In simpler
terms, a gyroid consists of a three-dimensional shape formed from the interlocking of
two-dimensional, sinuous lines, resulting in a durable and sturdy structure without
any straight lines. Additionally, it possesses chiral properties, making it impossible
for the gyroid to align with its mirror image.
3.2.1
Advantages of Gyroid infill
Gyroid infill offers several key advantages[3], including:
Nearly isotropic properties
Gyroid infill allows for the creation of parts that
exhibit uniform properties in all directions, akin to isotropic materials which maintain
consistent strength and characteristics under varying loads and stresses. Thanks to its
cubic symmetry, the gyroid infill closely mimics isotropic behavior, providing uniform
resistance across all axes. This contrasts with many 2D infill patterns that tend to
be stronger in one direction than others.
Good strength and infill density
Among AM infills, the gyroid pattern boasts
an exceptional ratio of density to strength. A notable study by MIT researchers in
2017 revealed that a gyroid-structured graphene, with just 5% the density of steel,
could surpass strength of steel tenfold[26]. This principle applies to AM techniques,
where the gyroid pattern enhances material strength significantly.
Less material consumption
The gyroid structure’s near-isotropic strength and
its superior strength-to-infill-density ratio. This not only leads to savings in material
costs but also results in lighter components, an important factor in many applications.
Faster printing speed
The unique, curved design of the gyroid infill pattern can
lead to faster printing times compared to other infill types. In a comparative analysis
by CNC Kitchen[3], a test cube with gyroid infill was printed in the same timeframe
as one with a simpler cubic infill (125%) and much more quickly than those with
honeycomb (310%) and 3D honeycomb (288%) patterns. Though this doesn’t apply

3.3
Slurries
The objective of the slurry development is to formulate and optimize slurries using
copper sulfate (CuSO4) metal salts as the primary metal precursor for effective use
in Vat Photopolymerization (VPP) processes. From the development of the slurry to
the final end product is discussed below and also visually represented in figure ??
Step 1:
Selection of material and preparation
Preparing basic slurries of
copper sulfate involves mixing CuSO4 with a photopolymer resin to create a stable
suspension. Two primary types of slurries are prepared one using hydrous copper
sulfate and another using anhydrous copper sulfate. Also, in these types of slurries
again slurries with varying concentrations of CuSO4 which are 35 vol% and 40 vol%
are prepared.
Step 2 : Ball Milling of Metal Salt
Materials and Equipment :
The ball milling (Pulverisette 6, classic line,
Fritsch, Germany) process employed a zirconium jar and yttrium-stabilized zirco-
nium oxide beads (10mm in size). These materials were chosen for their durability
and minimal risk of contamination, which is essential to maintain the purity of the
metal salt, copper sulfate (CuSO4).The copper sulfate used is from sigma Aldrich[8].
Initial Process and Challenges :
The initial milling was conducted dry at a
speed of 240 RPM for a duration of 12 hours. This approach faced several issues like
uneven grinding and agglomeration of the metal salt. The beads moved above the

Dry Sieving Challenges :
The dry sieving method posed health risks due to
the generation of airborne particles, leading to potential inhalation hazards and con-
tamination in the laboratory due to the flying of the dry powder particles.
Transition to Wet Sieving :
The methodology was thus adapted to wet siev-
ing, which proved to be safer and more effective. Wet sieving reduced the risk of
airborne contamination and facilitated easier handling of the powder. This method
also resulted in more uniform particle sizes, as evidenced by particle size analysis
and sedimentation tests in subsection 4.2.2, which showed that the wet-sieved slurry
maintained its stability and homogeneity, with metal particles not separating from
the resin even after 30 days. Particle size analysis using a particle analyzer confirmed
that over 90 percent of the particles were within the target range of less than or equal
to 20 microns, validating the effectiveness of the wet sieving process.
Step 4 : Dehydration Process
After sieving, the methodology incorporates a
dehydration step, specifically for experiments focusing on the development of anhy-
drous slurries. The objective of dehydration is to remove any residual moisture or
water particles from the metal salts. This is crucial for creating an anhydrous sample,
which is expected to exhibit different properties and behaviors in the final sintered
part compared to hydrous slurries.
Rationale for Anhydrous vs. Hydrous Slurries :
The investigation into
the differences between hydrous and anhydrous slurries is grounded in the principle
that the presence of water can significantly influence the physical and chemical char-
acteristics of the sintered parts. Water molecules in the slurry can affect the curing
process, mechanical properties, and dimensional accuracy of the final product. Thus,
assessing the performance of both hydrous and anhydrous slurries can provide valu-
able insights into optimizing slurry formulations for specific applications in VPP.
Method of Dehydration :
The dehydration step is carried out to ensure the
metal salts are completely anhydrous. This process is particularly relevant for salts
like copper sulfate pentahydrate (CuSO4.5H2O), which naturally contains water
molecules. The dehydration temperatures were meticulously determined based on
Thermogravimetric Analysis (TGA) of CuSO4.5H2O. TGA offers precise data on the
temperature ranges where water loss occurs, allowing for the determination of optimal
dehydration conditions that effectively remove water without degrading the chemical
integrity of the metal salts. To achieve this, we employed a carefully controlled heat-
ing method.The dehydration was performed in an oven named Nabertherm[23] at a
ramping rate of 5 °C/min, ranging from 30 °C to 300 °C. After the dehydration, the
metal salts are manually grinded with mortar and pestor to bring the blocks of metal
salts into powder form again. [14]

Step 5 : Preparation of photopolymer blend
Progressing to the creation of
a polymer blend, this step involved mixture of a UV blocker (LB1), a photoinitiator
(FT1), the chosen acrylic 3D resin, and a dispersant. In advancing the field of VPP,
the selection of an appropriate resin is crucial for the successful development of metal
slurries, as it fundamentally dictates the quality and consistency of the final printed
parts. The process commenced with the careful selection of a photopolymer resin,
chosen for its role as a binding agent for the metal salt particles.The resin’s viscosity is
essential for its processability and its impact on the final print quality. Simultaneously,
the resin’s reactivity is crucial for effective curing and bonding with the metal particles.
For this purpose, 3DResyn, a commercial resin from Resiner Technologies S.L,[2] by
its relative density of 1.083 g/cm³ was chosen. This particular resin was previously
validated for its effectiveness with ceramics prompting its experimental application
with metals. The resin was validated with ceramics by a senior Marina Artemeva in
her masters thesis on ceramics. [5]
The preparation phase involved an optimization process. Based on the initial test
results, necessary adjustments were made in slurry composition to balance flowability,
stability, and printability. The goal is to achieve a slurry that is not only stable and
maintains uniformity but also has the right flowability for the VPP process. These
components were carefully calibrated, with the final concentrations being 2% for the
photoinitiator and 1.5% for the UV blocker. These concentrations were determined
to be optimal, enhancing the curing process and improving the quality of the final
product.
Prior to the thesis, a specialized course [16]dedicated to the development of metal slur-
ries was completed. This course proved instrumental in refining the understanding of
various additive ratios, including those of the photoinitiator, UV blocker, and disper-
sants.The Photoinitiator and a UV blocker in different concentrations were added and
tested and particular concentrations were finalised. Also, dispersant, Disperbyk-111
[10](BYK Chemie, Germany), with a density of 1.16 g/cm³, was employed to decrease
the slurry’s viscosity and ensure a homogeneous suspension. This particular disper-
sant was chosen as it worked well with ceramics and was used in previous projects.
[5].The role of the dispersant extended beyond improving particle dispersion, it was
integral in preventing particle settling and modifying the slurry’s flow behavior and
viscosity. These enhancements contribute significantly to the stability of the slurry
and influence the quality of the final print by minimizing defects associated with un-
even particle distribution. The UV blocker’s inclusion was critical for controlling the
curing process, ensuring it occurred only in the desired regions. The photoinitiator,
FT1, played a pivotal role in initiating the polymerization reaction upon exposure to
light. The knowledge and methodologies derived from this course [16] were founda-
tional to the successful application and optimization of metal slurries in the current
research. The addition of the dispersant in this blend aimed to maintain an even
dispersion of the metal salts, an essential factor in achieving homogeneity.
Subsequently, metal salts were introduced, forming the final metal slurry. The utiliza-
tion of metal salts instead of metal powder was advantageous in terms of dispersion,
post-processing ease, and control over metal content in the printed parts.

3.5
Printing and cleaning
The post-fabrication cleaning process is very important in VPP. Post-production, the
green parts are typically enveloped in a layer of uncured photopolymer. This requires
a rigorous cleaning regimen to ensure the integrity and purity of the final product.
The green part is attached to the build plate. First, the green part should be separated
from the green part with the help of a metal spatula. Gentle force should be applied
to remove the green part without deformation. The initial cleaning stage involves
the application of a solvent. Isopropyl alcohol was utilized for its eﬀicacy in diluting
and dislodging residual photopolymer. It is crucial to note that liquid photopolymers
are generally hazardous and require strict adherence to safety protocols. Appropriate
personal protective equipment, including lab coats, nitrile gloves, and safety glasses,
must be worn during this process. Following solvent application, compressed air is
applied to further cleanse intricate geometries, ensuring that no polymer remnants
compromise the structure. Subsequently, the components undergo ultrasonic clean-
ing for 10 minutes. This sophisticated technique employs high-frequency vibrations
to agitate the cleaning solution, enhancing the removal of impurities through two
primary mechanisms: dissolution and cavitation. Dissolution is the process where
impurities are solubilized into the solvent, whereas cavitation involves the creation
of vapor-filled microscopic bubbles that, upon collapse, generate suﬀicient energy to
mechanically displace contaminants from the component’s surface.
The optimization of ultrasonic parameters, particularly frequency and temperature,
is critical.
These parameters directly influence the cleanliness of the component.
However, excessive cavitation can potentially damage the surface integrity of the
green part.
Therefore, a carefully calibrated cleaning protocol is vital, one that
delicately balances the ultrasonic frequency and temperature to prevent damage such
as breakage, or shrinkage.[27]. To avoid these unnecessary defects it is necessary to
set the temperature and frequency according to the sample. For all of the fabricated
samples in this project, the temperature set is 40°C and the frequency set is 37KHz.
After the post processing, the samples are then stored and then were used for optical
microscopy.

4.2.3
Effects of process parameters in slurry making
The process of sieving can influence the homogeneity and size distribution of particles
in a slurry. When sieving is done dry, it can lead to a controlled but not necessarily
uniform particle size distribution and may result in some powder wastage. On the
other hand, wet sieving tends to enhance particle distribution uniformity, which can
positively affect the quality and integrity of both the green and sintered parts.
Dry sieving often involves passing particles through sieves to separate them accord-
ing to size. However, this method can segregate particles unevenly and cause some
material loss due to the inability to capture finer particles. In contrast, wet sieving
involves adding liquid to the particles during the sieving process, which can help
achieve a more uniform particle size distribution.
This uniformity is crucial as it
impacts the flow and packing behavior of the particles, affecting the density and
structural integrity of the final product.
When a slurry with an uneven particle size distribution is used in VPP, it can lead to
parts with defects such as inconsistent layers, over-curing, and structural weaknesses.
Therefore, wet sieving can be considered a more suitable approach for preparing
slurries for VPP as it assists in creating a more homogeneous particle mix, leading to
fewer defects and better-quality parts post-sintering.

VPP, where UV light penetration is essential for initiating the polymerization reac-

Anhydrous Slurry Challenges :
On the contrary, anhydrous slurries presented
several challenges that complicate part fabrication and require the optimization of
manufacturing parameters as well as slurry development parameters. The absence of
water molecules due to dehydration and making it anhydrous increases the slurry’s
viscosity, making it more diﬀicult to achieve a uniform layer during the printing pro-
cess. This increased viscosity can lead to issues such as incomplete layer formation
and inaccuracies in the printed object’s dimensions and details. Additionally, the
opacity of anhydrous slurries poses a significant barrier to UV light penetration, po-
tentially leading to uneven or insuﬀicient curing of the resin. This factor is critical
in determining the success of the polymerization process. So, careful development
of slurry with even particle size and suitable viscosity for VPP is to be maintained.
Also, the agglomeration in the slurry causes penetration of the membrane and hence
makes it diﬀicult to fabricate The green part.
Furthermore, the adhesion properties of anhydrous slurries to commonly used build
plates, such as those made from Polymethyl methacrylate (PMMA), are notably infe-
rior compared to their hydrous slurries. So, an additional polymer base is fabricated
first to improve the adhesion. The lack of adhesion can result in printing defects,

requires modifications to the printing setup, such as adjusting the height of the build
plate and mainly slowing the feed rate and repositing delay of the buildplate to

to the build plate better.

4.3.3
Adhesion Improvement
During the initial stages of the experiments, adhesion issues were encountered, result-
ing in the failure of printed layers to adhere to the build plate. It became evident
that improvements in adhesion were necessary to ensure proper bonding between the
printed layers and the build plate [16].
The adhesion between the printed object and the build plate is a critical factor in
any printing process. Without a firm attachment, the printed object might detach
during the printing process, leading to printing failures, errors in the printed object,
or inconsistency in the print quality.
Here the metal layer does not adhere directly to the build plate. Instead, the metal
layer is printed on a membrane layer which necessitates the need for a strategy to
ensure secure adhesion to the build plate.
To mitigate this challenge of the metal layer’s adhesion to the build plate, a layer of
polymer as an intermediary was employed. The polymer layer serves to improve the
adhesion between the build plate and the metal layer, thus aiding in the successful
printing of the 3D structures.
The polymer layer, owing to its adhesive properties, helps to firmly secure the metal
layer to the build plate, ensuring the stability of the printed object throughout the
process. This strategic approach of using a polymer layer as an adhesive interface
was consistently employed across all trials to facilitate successful AM printing.
4.4
Geometry
4.4.1
Change of geometry
The initial selection of cylindrical gyroids for the project was based on their unique
structural properties, which are beneficial for various applications that require high
surface area and mechanical strength. However, during the sintering process, it be-
came evident that these cylindrical gyroids faced significant challenges in maintaining
their structural integrity. The inherent design of cylindrical gyroids includes sharp
edges and curves that are susceptible to external stresses during the sintering phase.
These stresses result from the thermal gradients and the differential shrinkage rates
experienced by the material, which are unavoidable aspects of the sintering process.
Such conditions led to deformation and, in some cases, the breaking of parts.
To mitigate these issues, a strategic decision was made to transition from cylindrical
to cubical gyroids. Cubical gyroids, with their more uniform geometry and reduced
==PAGE:64==
46
4 Results and Discussion
occurrence of sharp edges, inherently possess a better capability to withstand the
stresses imparted during sintering. This shape adjustment significantly minimizes the
stress concentration points throughout the structure, thereby reducing the likelihood
of deformation and ensuring that the part retains its intended shape post-sintering.
The cubical geometry facilitates a more uniform distribution of thermal and mechani-
cal stresses, which aligns with the thermal expansion properties of the materials used,
thus enhancing the structural integrity of the fabricated components.
4.4.2
Green part dimension variation
The copper sulfate hydrous green parts of all the samples are bigger in dimension
by little when compared to the anhydrous green parts which can be seen from 4.4.
The observed difference in size between hydrous and anhydrous green parts of copper
sulfate samples can be rationalized by examining the role of water molecules within
the crystal structure of these compounds. When copper sulfate incorporates water
to form its hydrous variant, the resulting crystal lattice expands to accommodate
the water molecules. This expansion is absent in the anhydrous form, which lacks
water in its structure, leading to more compact crystals and, consequently, smaller
dimensions in the green parts.

4.5.1
Use of Isopropanol over Ethanol
During the cleaning of the green part, in the beginning there was a confusion to
use isopropanol(IPA) or ethanol for cleaning. so a small experiment was conducted
by cleaning the cylindrical gyroids fabricated with same copper slurry are cleaned
and soaked one sample each in ethanol and IPA. Ethanol caused damage to the parts,
breaking them near the gyroid walls and leaving a white residue after drying. However,
the parts cleaned with IPA did not show these issues.
The reason behind this difference could be the properties of the two solvents. Ethanol
is a stronger solvent than IPA, which can lead to the resin components breaking
down or reacting, weakening the structure and changing its appearance. Its faster
evaporation rate could also cause stress and cracks in the parts, especially in delicate
areas.
On the other hand, IPA is milder and doesn’t react with or dissolve the resin as much,
keeping the printed parts intact during cleaning. This makes IPA a better choice for
cleaning these types of prints, as it avoids damage and maintains the quality of the
parts. [7]

5.1
Conclusion
Metal Additive Manufacturing (MAM) has emerged as a revolutionary tool for fabri-
cating metal parts, yet it faces challenges such as high entry barriers and limitations
related to feature size and surface quality. Vat Photopolymerization (VPP) methods,
particularly Mask Projection (MP), offer solutions to these issues but struggle with
processing metal-infused resins. This research introduces a novel metal AM method
using metal salt precursors, significantly enhancing VPP MP’s applicability across a
broader range of metals. This approach effectively addresses critical issues of com-
position, solid loading, curing and sedimentation, which have previously restricted
the utility of MAM through VPP MP. This thesis has particularly studied the use of
CuSO4 metal salt precursor instead of direct metal powder for fabricating metal parts
with complex geometries. The metal salt approach overcomes the challenges associ-
ated with directly printing metal oxides using MP due to the high attenuation effect
of oxide particles against UV light .The formulations of metal slurry and the optimiza-
tion of the manufacturing parameters, post-processing techniques were also discussed.
In comparing VPP with traditional metal AM techniques, this thesis demonstrated
VPP’s superior capability in producing detailed parts with complex internal struc-
tures, which are challenging to achieve with conventional methods. The analysis of
process parameters revealed critical insights into the effects of layer thickness, expo-
sure time, and post-curing on the mechanical properties and dimensional accuracy
of the printed parts. This thesis successfully demonstrates that it is indeed possible
to print metals using VPP. This is evidenced by the successful fabrication of green
parts using both hydrous and anhydrous copper sulfate slurries, which could then
be sintered into metallic copper structures. Also, metal slurries that do not require
agitation and resist sedimentation over time have been developed in this thesis as it
is important for the slurries to be homogenous to produce good parts. In previous
special course even after using a lot of dispersant, there was a need for lot of agita-
tion, as the slurry is getting sedimented very soon, the slurry needed constant stirring
in order to be homogenous. The use of wet milling and sieving processes proved to
significantly enhance the stability of slurries, making Slurry 3 process parameters the

5 Conclusion and Future Work
most suitable for VPP based on its reduced sedimentation and better long-term sus-
pension characteristics. Impact of moisture and process parameters were also studied.
The study found that moisture content and processing parameters significantly affect
the quality of the final parts. Hydrous slurries allow better light penetration, aiding
in more uniform curing, whereas anhydrous slurries, despite their increased viscosity,
can yield parts with better structural integrity and less oxidation. Hydrous slurries
displayed a higher cure depth across all energy doses compared to anhydrous slurries,
confirming the hypothesis that water facilitates deeper light penetration. However,
the cure depth profile for resin suspensions showed variability, which could have been
attributed to factors such as additives or errors caused due to manual measurements.
Quality of the manufactured green and sintered parts are analysed, noting the dif-
ferences in wall thickness, volumetric shrinkage, and oxidation between hydrous and
anhydrous samples. Particularly, sintered parts from anhydrous slurries showed less
oxidation and maintained better structural integrity, while hydrous slurries led to
higher shrinkage and potential for more pronounced oxidation.
5.2
Future Work
• Future work should include conducting tests like thermal conductivity, mechan-
ical testing and resistivity. Due to time constraint these tests weren’t conducted
but they would have given better insight and overall analysis of the applications
of the final parts.
• Further research could focus on optimizing the formulations of hydrous and
anhydrous slurries to improve stability, and enhance the uniformity of particle
size distribution.
• Additional studies could aim to achieve better control over the photopolymer-
ization process, including the careful measurement of energy doses to manage
cure depth more precisely and to ensure consistent quality in the printed parts.
It would also be beneficial to develop or refine measurement techniques, par-
ticularly for the resin’s cure depth, to reduce variability and potential errors
introduced by manual methods.
• The impact of additives on the photopolymerization process and the potential
effects of slurry aging on print quality are areas that could be explored even
further.
• More future work should include more precise control of process parameters
such as temperature and atmosphere in the open source VPP setup, especially
for controlling the viscosity of slurries and ensuring a homogenous particle size.
• The role of ball milling in creating agglomerates and its effect on the slurry
homogeneity needs to be studied further. Research could focus on determining
the optimal duration and speed of ball milling to prevent agglomeration.
==PAGE:79==
5.2 Future Work
61
• Further research may be directed towards understanding and mitigating oxida-
tion in sintered parts, particularly for the hydrous copper sulfate samples.
• Investigating the potential of hydrogels infused with metal precursors in VPP
could lead to advancements in the printing process and material properties.
• Manufacturing a larger number of samples in the same shape could allow for
more extensive testing and analysis, which was limited in the current study due
to the small number of samples.
