Summary
In this thesis we study in three dimensions the Calderón problem of de-
termining the electric conductivity distribution of a body, given exterior
electrostatic current and voltage measurements. Such reconstruction is of
interest in many fields notably medical imaging, where applications vary
from hemorrhagic stroke detection to breast tissue scanning. We consider
first the problem from the viewpoint of well-posedness showing with the
use of CGO solutions that while the conductivity distribution of a suffi-
ciently regular body is uniquely determined by exterior current and volt-
age measurements, it is not stable. We study direct non-linear reconstruc-
tion in three dimensions and prove that the frequency truncation of the
non-physical scattering transform provides an algorithm with regularizing
behavior thereby creating a link to the two dimensional regularized d-bar
reconstruction [Knu+09]. Reconstructions are smooth, but numerical tests
indicate they do not suffer severely from Gibbs effects. In addition, nu-
merical tests suggest an implementation of the regularized reconstruction,
given in [DK14], performs better than promised by theory. The thesis in-
troduces an investigation of reconstruction using a family of CGO solutions
with a Fourier series form. These considerations require additional treat-
ment. Finally details and theoretical justification for a forward problem
solver is considered, and to which numerical tests suggest instability for
small parameters and modest accuracy for larger parameters.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Imagine being able to tell the conductivity distribution of the interior of an
object only from measurements made on the exterior. In order to solve such
a problem, one may have to defy nature, and it comes with a price.
The problem of calculating the outcome of a certain model is often called
a forward problem. This type of problem arises in many fields of mathe-
matics. In particular, the field of partial differential equations enjoy the
company of many, who try to predict or simulate physical phenomena re-
duced to a model. An inverse problem is the reverse of a forward problem;
determine the parameters of a model, based on observations alone. The
Calderón problem1 is a famous inverse problem arising in a tomographic
technique known as Electrical Impedance Tomography (EIT), which has
a wide variety of applications including medical imaging [FB19; Che+02;
Kot96], industrial testing in [HL09; Dav+04] and subsurface geophysical
imaging [Zha+13; DL81] and is popular i.a. for its non-destructive prop-
erty. Central for EIT is the physical interaction between current and voltage
potentials in and on the surface of a body2 and the conductivity distribution
inherent to the body. The Calderón problem is then
Is it possible to determine the conductivity distribution of a body from
current and voltage measurements made at the surface and if so, how?
and was first posed by A. P. Calderon in 1980 [Cal80]. EIT is effective,
since inhomogeneous bodies often consist of segments diverse in terms of
conductivity, think: heart and lungs in a human body, or the conductive
effect of a crack in a piece of concrete. Besides being safer, smaller, more
portable and cheaper than existing tomographic systems [Vri07], a more di-
rect argument for EIT is that it may detect properties of an object simply
not physically possible by other systems.
We shall make the above problem of Calderón more precise: consider a
bounded domain Ωin Rn with a sufficiently regular boundary and with a

There is a number of interesting aspects surrounding the forward and
inverse problem of Calderón. An important aspect is that of uniqueness and
is concerned with whether the forward map F : γ 7→Λγ is injective. If Λγ
does not determine γ uniquely, there can be no inverse. A second aspect,
existence, is concerned with the characterization of the range of the forward
map F - indeed which data Λγ gives rise to a conductivity γ? Yet a third
is concerned with the stability of F; is the proximity, in some sense, of two
conductivity distributions determined by the proximity of corresponding
data? And if so, in which sense? These three aspects were identified by J.
Hadamard in 1902 [Had02] as crucial for many problems and serve us as a
general definition of well-posedness.

1.2
Structure of the thesis
In this thesis we look at the Calderón problem in the intersection of overde-
termination and immediate practical applications: three dimensions. The
thesis treats some of the aforementioned aspects and has a certain focus on
reconstruction. More precisely our main focus is: how do we perform direct
non-linear reconstruction for the Calderón problem in three dimensions in
practice? In practice portends in particular the notion of regularization.
The thesis can be summarized as follows:
• Chapter 2 and 3 introduce a number of reductive results concerning
the Dirichlet-to-Neumann map and the problem 1.1, which is reduced
to a Schrödinger equation. We introduce a family of solutions to the
Schrödinger equation known as complex geometrical solutions, which
is partly done for the necessity in reconstruction and to prove unique-
ness and a light edition of logarithmic stability of the Calderón prob-
lem. We introduce further a family of complex geometrical solutions
in a Fourier series basis introduced in [Häh96].
• Chapter 4 presents the theoretical justification for a well-known non-
linear reconstruction algorithm based on the non-physical scattering
transform. It presents a regularization analysis similar to the related
d-bar method in two dimensions [Knu+09], which relies on truncation
of scattering transform. Although the preparatory estimates in Sec-
tion 4.2.2 are well-known, at this time there is no similar regularization
analysis in three dimensions as presented in Section 4.2.3 known to the
author. We investigate also the possibility of using a different ”shape”
of truncation. In continuation of this, a number of properties of the
regularized reconstruction are presented, for example, smoothness.
• Chapter 5 motivates a reconstruction algorithm based on the family
of complex geometrical solutions in a Fourier series. The algorithm is
based on the non-linear reconstruction algorithm of Chapter 4. We
give this only a partial treatment, and summarize a number of further
steps necessary for this to be a viable reconstruction method.

CHAPTER 6
Numerical representations and results
In this chapter we give some details into considerations necessary to test the
reconstruction, Method 2, in practice. We start by considering the forward
problem of determining Λγ given a conductivity distribution γ ∈C2(Ω). We
use a method tailored to the unit ball, which entails introducing a class of
functions called spherical harmonics, which may be seen as a generalization
of eiθ on the circle to the sphere. Furthermore, we explore some details of the
numerical implementation of forward problem solver and the reconstruction
algorithm as implemented in [DHK12] and [DK14], respectively. Our goal is
to understand the parameter choices for numerical tests, which we consider
in Section 6.3.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusions
We wish to conclude on four themes of this thesis. Then we remark possible
further steps in direct and in more distant extension of the work.
Stability and regularization are central themes of this thesis. In order
to analyze the stability of the Calderón problem we have given well-known
fundamental results on CGO solutions due to [SU87; Nac88] and a hint of a
logarithmic stability estimate due to [Ale88] to conclude ill-posedness of the
Calderón problem. Indeed, the practicality of any reconstruction algorithm
ultimately requires ideas pertaining to the notion of regularization, since the
algorithm must be able to handle noise. From Theorem 4 we conclude that
Method 2, implemented in [DK14], yields reconstructions approximating
the true conductivity as the noise goes to zero, when using data sufficiently
accurate. Similarly, in the theoretical case in which we are gifted with a
noiseless Dirichlet-to-Neumann map, reconstructions of Method 2 converge
to the true conductivity as the regularization parameter goes to zero. The
crucial idea in this regularized reconstruction as in the two-dimensional case
[Knu+09] is the truncation of the non-physical scattering transform hereby
avoiding high-frequency data and severely amplified noise. In contrast to
the two-dimensional case [Knu+09], however, the proof is reliant on a gap of
the magnitude of the complex frequency (D < |ζ(ξ)| < ˜R(ε)) in which the
existence of unique CGO solutions is guaranteed and the noise level allows
a stable and unique solution to the boundary integral equation. Finally,
numerical results show the regularizing behavior of the reconstruction al-
gorithm works in practice and performs better than suggested by theory.
Moreover, the strength of the reconstruction algorithm is further empha-
sized by the fact that it works well for piecewise constant conductivities.
A natural theme for us to consider is then the properties of regularized
reconstructions. We find that they are smooth and possibly containing os-
cillating artefacts as a consequence of the low-pass filtering in the second
step of the algorithm. We conclude that a possible remedy, the smooth
cutoff functions, seems to induce a regularization strategy, expanding the
possible variations of Method 2. However, numerical tests of the imple-
mented algorithm using a Butterworth low-pass filter suggests that there
is no significant difference to conductivity reconstructions using the usual
truncation. This is likely due to the smoothing property of the last step in
the algorithm, as we see significant difference among reconstructed poten-

We have investigated the possibility of reconstructing conductivities

the results are unstable for small parameters and that we do not obtain very

Besides broadly applicable proposals such as making the computation more
efficient or considering methods for choosing the optimal truncation radius,
which is related to the general problem of choosing an optimal regulariza-
tion parameter, we wish to draw some attention to reconstruction using
hybrid data. The techniques we have seen in this thesis effectively reduce
the resolution of the reconstruction by avoiding high frequency data - we
pay a price for this stability. One proposed remedy is to obtain more data
by coupling the physical phenomenon of conductivity and current/voltage
potentials with a separate physical phenomenon, for example acoustic wave
propagation through an object. These extended methods need treatment
similar to the Calderón problem, such that practical reconstruction is feasi-
ble in the presence of noisy and uncertain models and data, see for example
[TPT20; JK20].
