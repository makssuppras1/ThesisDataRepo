Abstract
Single­photon emitters (SPEs) are crucial components for quantum photonic applications,
including secure communication, quantum computing, and nanoscale sensing. Hexago­
nal boron nitride (hBN) has emerged as a promising host material due to its wide bandgap,
high chemical and thermal stability, and capability to support quantum emission at room
temperature. However, the microscopic origin of this emission remains controversial.
While defects in the hBN lattice have often been considered responsible, recent stud­
ies suggest that organic molecules—such as polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs)—
adsorbed on hBN flakes may instead be the true emission centers.
This thesis investigates the origin and control of emission in hBN through a two­part study.
In the first part, mechanically exfoliated hBN flakes are subjected to various cleaning
protocols, including trichloroethylene (TCE) rinsing and oxygen plasma treatment. The
evolution of photoluminescence is monitored using confocal microscopy, hyperspectral
imaging, and second­order correlation measurements g(2)(τ). These analyses aim both
to assess whether cleaning suppresses background fluorescence or alters the emission
profile—thereby clarifying the origin of emission—and to establish a reproducible cleaning
protocol for preparing hBN substrates prior to molecular deposition.
In the second part, the focus shifts to the deliberate deposition of pentacene molecules
onto pre­cleaned hBN flakes, followed by spectroscopic characterization under cryogenic
conditions. The goal is to explore whether emission features similar to those observed
in uncleaned samples can be reproduced in a controlled manner using known molecular
systems. Observed emission stability and spectral characteristics support the hypothesis
that the signal often originates from surface­bound molecules rather than native defects.
Overall, this work contributes to the understanding and control of molecular quantum emit­
ters on hBN substrates and provides a step toward the development of scalable, tunable,
and stable single­photon sources based on 2D materials.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The way we understand light has changed dramatically over time. What was once thought
of simply as rays or waves is now understood, through quantum mechanics, as particles
of light—photons—each carrying a specific amount of energy. Although conventional
sources like the Sun emit incoherent light, advances in technology, especially lasers,
have introduced the possibility of coherent, monochromatic emission [1].
Now, research is focusing on an even more precise kind of light source: single­photon
emitters (SPEs), two­state systems capable of releasing photons one at a time with well­
defined energy. These single photons are fundamentally non­classical and can serve
as qubits, enabling functionalities in quantum communication, sensing, and computation
[1, 2]. Photon­based qubits are now considered a promising direction toward scalable
quantum information processing, offering advantages such as room­temperature opera­
tion and compatibility with existing photonic infrastructure [1, 3]. Companies like Google
and IBM have already demonstrated the potential of quantum computing with supercon­
ducting circuits, but challenges related to scalability and decoherence remain significant—
challenges that single­photon­based systems may help overcome.
Several solid­state systems have been explored as hosts for single­photon emitters, in­
cluding diamond color centers and quantum dots. Recently, two­dimensional (2D) mate­
rials have become promising hosts for SPEs, offering several advantages. Their atomic
thinness enhances light–matter interaction, allows precise positioning of defects, and fa­
cilitates easy integration into photonic and optoelectronic circuits [2]. Reduced dielectric
screening and quantum confinement in 2D systems improve radiative rates and isolate
emitter states from the surrounding environment, which helps reduce spectral diffusion
and decoherence [1].
Hexagonal boron nitride (hBN), a two­dimensional wide bandgap insulator, has become
one of the most studied hosts for SPEs. It exhibits bright and stable emission even at room
temperature, with single­photon characteristics confirmed through second­order correla­
tion measurements (g(2)(0) < 0.5), as shown in Figure 1.1b. It also has a high Debye–
Waller factor (approximately 0.82), indicating efficient emission into the zero­phonon line
[2, 1, 3].
Although the origin of emission in hBN (Figure 1.1a) has often been attributed to intrinsic
lattice defects [1, 3]—such as boron or nitrogen vacancies and antisite complexes—recent
work has challenged this interpretation. Neumann et al. (2023) [3] demonstrated that
many emission centers in hBN samples are not due to crystal defects but rather originate
from fluorescent organic molecules, specifically polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs),
trapped at the interface between hBN and the substrate. These molecules are introduced
unintentionally during sample preparation, particularly when solvent residues remain prior
to annealing. Their emission spectra are similar to those of known fluorescent dyes and
can even exhibit single­photon characteristics.
This finding significantly changes our understanding of hBN­based quantum emitters. In­
stead of being active emitters themselves, hBN flakes may act as passive, stabilizing hosts
for fluorescent molecules. This interpretation aligns with the observed heterogeneity in
emitter properties—including their broad spectral distribution (Figure 1.1c)—and suggests

1.2
Scope of Work
This thesis investigates the origin and control of emission centers in hexagonal boron
nitride (hBN), with the goal of understanding whether the observed photoluminescence
stems from defects in hBN or arises from surface­bound molecular contaminants. The
motivation comes from the increasing relevance of hBN in quantum photonics, where its
use as a host for single­photon emitters is promising, but the reproducibility and nature of
the emission remain unclear.
The scope of the project has two parts. First, a systematic investigation is carried out to
assess how various surface cleaning protocols—including trichloroethylene (TCE) rinsing
and oxygen plasma treatments—affect the optical emission of mechanically exfoliated
hBN flakes. Through confocal microscopy, hyperspectral photoluminescence mapping,
and stability measurements, the effectiveness of each cleaning cycle is evaluated, with
emphasis on photon count rates, spectral changes, and blinking behavior. These steps
aim to reduce background fluorescence and identify conditions under which emission is
minimized or altered.

Theoretical Framework
2.1
Light–Matter Interaction and Quantum Emitters
Single­photon emitters (SPEs) are quantum systems capable of emitting light one pho­
ton at a time. These emitters play a key role in the development of emerging quan­
tum technologies, including quantum communication, photonic quantum computing, and
nanoscale sensing [8]. Their ability to emit precisely one photon at a controlled time and
from a well­defined location distinguishes them from classical light sources and enables
a wide range of applications that require precise quantum state manipulation.
SPEs exhibit fundamentally quantum behavior, such as the phenomenon of photon an­
tibunching, a signature indicating that photons are emitted one at a time [9]. To under­
stand the underlying physical mechanisms driving single­photon emission, it is essential
to analyze how quantum systems interact with electromagnetic fields and how this in­
teraction drives both excitation and radiative decay processes. This requires describing
the coupling between the emitter’s discrete energy levels and the surrounding photonic
environment.
2.1.1
Classical and Quantum Descriptions of Light–Matter Interaction
Single­photon emission originates from the interaction between a quantum emitter and the
surrounding electromagnetic field. Understanding this interaction is essential because it
governs both the excitation of the emitter by an external light source and the subsequent
emission of a single photon. In quantum electrodynamics, when the emitter’s physical
size is much smaller than the wavelength of light, the coupling can be treated using the
electric dipole approximation [9]:
Hint = −d · E(r),
(2.1)
where d is the transition dipole moment of the emitter and E(r) is the local electric field at
its position.
This formalism describes how an external electromagnetic wave drives transitions be­
tween discrete energy levels. Absorption occurs when the field supplies energy matching
a transition, and emission follows when the excited state relaxes back to a lower level,
releasing a photon. The selection rules for allowed transitions arise naturally from the
symmetry of the dipole operator and the overlap of the initial and final wavefunctions [9].
This quantum description connects to the classical model of an atom as an oscillating
electric dipole. In the Lorentz oscillator model, bound electrons behave as harmonic os­
cillators with a natural resonant frequency ω0. An external field displaces the electron
from equilibrium, producing a time­varying dipole moment:
p(t) = −ex(t),
(2.2)
where x(t) is the electron displacement relative to the nucleus. These oscillations radiate
electromagnetic waves at ω0, linking the microscopic dipole motion to emitted light [10].
The oscillator strength is determined by the reduced electron mass and the restoring force,
following the Lorentz model of atomic dipoles.

In solid­state and molecular systems, these electronic transitions are often accompanied
by coupling to nuclear motion, giving rise to vibrational sublevels and structured emission

2.2
Vibronic Coupling and the Franck–Condon Principle
Optical transitions in quantum emitters are not purely electronic. Each electronic state
is associated with a manifold of vibrational energy levels due to nuclear motion or lattice
dynamics. Therefore, transitions between electronic states often involve simultaneous
changes in vibrational state, giving rise to vibronic transitions. These are responsible for
the structured nature of many emission spectra, which include a sharp zero­phonon line
(ZPL) and broader phonon sidebands (PSBs) [4, 10].
This structure arises from the quantum mechanical coupling between electronic and vi­
brational degrees of freedom, a consequence of the Born–Oppenheimer approximation
[10]. This approximation assumes that the fast electronic motion and the slower nuclear
motion can be decoupled, allowing one to treat the electronic wavefunction as paramet­
rically dependent on the fixed nuclear positions. As a result, each electronic state can
be represented as a potential energy surface with a ladder of quantized vibrational levels
(Figure 2.3a). The transition between these levels is thus not a single energy jump but
rather a set of possible vibronic pathways, each defined by a different pair of vibrational
quantum numbers.

remains effectively frozen due to the difference in timescales between electronic and nu­

electronic states depends on the overlap of their corresponding vibrational wavefunctions

2.2.2
Zero–Phonon Line, Phonon Sidebands, and the Debye–Waller
Factor
The emission spectrum of a quantum emitter is typically characterized by two distinct
components: a narrow zero­phonon line (ZPL) and a broader phonon sideband (PSB).
These features arise from the vibronic nature of the transitions and directly reflect the
interaction between the electronic and vibrational degrees of freedom [4, 10].
The ZPL corresponds to a purely electronic transition between the vibrational ground
states of the excited and ground electronic states (Figure 2.3c). This transition occurs
without the involvement of phonons and therefore retains the full electronic coherence
and energy specificity of the emitter. The PSB, in contrast, represents transitions from
the lowest vibrational level of the excited electronic state to vibrationally excited levels
of the ground state. These phonon­assisted transitions are a consequence of the struc­
tural relaxation occurring between electronic states with different nuclear configurations,
as dictated by the Franck–Condon principle [10].
The relative strength of the purely electronic ZPL compared to the full spectrum is quan­
tified by the Debye–Waller factor (DWF):
DWF =

which provides a measure of spectral purity. High DWF values indicate weak electron–
phonon coupling and are thus highly desirable in quantum photonics [4, 11].
2.3
Photon Statistics and Quantum Metrics
2.3.1
Second­Order Correlation Function
While the previous sections described how photons are emitted by quantum systems,
another important aspect is understanding the statistical properties of these emission
events. Unlike classical light sources, which emit photons in random bunches or con­
tinuously, single­photon emitters exhibit inherently quantum behavior that can be charac­
terized through correlation measurements.

In the ideal case, a perfect single­photon source exhibits:
g(2)(0) = 0,
(2.13)
meaning there is zero probability of detecting two photons at the same time. This behavior
arises from the quantized nature of the emission process: after emitting a photon, the
emitter must be re­excited before it can emit again, imposing a minimum time between
emission events [9].
The measurement of g(2)(τ) serves as the main criterion for validating quantum light
sources. Moreover, the shape and dynamics of g(2)(τ) provide further insight into the
internal level structure, excitation dynamics, and environmental interactions of the emit­
ter. For example, a dip at τ = 0 confirms the presence of antibunching, indicating that
photons are emitted one at a time. In contrast, if the function goes above 1 at longer
time delays, it may suggest that the emitter temporarily gets trapped in a metastable state
before returning to emit again, experiences spectral diffusion, or exhibits blinking due to
interactions with its environment [9].
2.3.2
Emission Quality: Brightness, Purity, and Indistinguishability
The performance of a single­photon emitter is characterized using three key metrics:
brightness, purity, and indistinguishability, collectively known as the BPI figures of merit
[8]. These parameters evaluate both the efficiency and quantum quality of emission and
together determine the suitability of a source for quantum technologies.
Brightness refers to the probability of emitting a photon per excitation cycle. It depends
on the excitation rate, the radiative quantum efficiency, and the collection efficiency of the
optical setup. Engineering approaches such as cavity coupling or photonic waveguides
can enhance brightness by increasing the radiative decay rate via the Purcell effect and
suppressing non­radiative channels.
Purity quantifies the likelihood that only a single photon is emitted per excitation event.
It is typically determined from the second­order correlation function at zero delay, with
P = 1 −g(2)(0).
A value approaching unity indicates a strong suppression of multi­
photon emission events. Experimentally, g(2)(τ) is measured using a Hanbury Brown–
Twiss (HBT) setup, which provides direct access to the photon statistics of the emitter
[9].
Indistinguishability describes how similar successive photons are in all relevant degrees
of freedom—spectral, temporal, and polarization. It is formally expressed as the squared
overlap of the single­photon wavefunctions:
I = |⟨ψ1|ψ2⟩|2,
(2.14)
where I = 1 corresponds to perfectly indistinguishable photons. High indistinguishability
requires the emitted photons to be Fourier­limited, which demands a long optical coher­
ence time T2 relative to the excited­state lifetime T1 [8]. In the ideal case of no pure
dephasing, T2 = 2T1, resulting in Fourier­limited linewidths [9].
Achieving simultaneously high brightness, purity, and indistinguishability is challenging
because these metrics are interdependent and strongly influenced by the emitter’s elec­
tromagnetic environment and host material. Optimizing BPI therefore involves both ma­
terial engineering—such as defect passivation and cryogenic stabilization—and photonic
design to tailor the local density of optical states and suppress dephasing channels.

Triplet States and Intersystem Crossing
This metastable level is often associated with a long­lived triplet “dark” state that interrupts
the fluorescence cycle and leads to bunching in g(2)(τ). In many systems, this state
is populated via intersystem crossing (ISC), a spin­forbidden but weakly allowed non­
radiative transition from the excited singlet state S1. Once in the triplet state T1, the emitter
can decay back to the ground state either non­radiatively or via a spin­forbidden radiative
process known as phosphorescence, typically on microsecond­to­second timescales [9,
4].
These triplet dynamics are illustrated with a Jablonski diagram (Figure 2.3c). The S0 ↔S1
singlet manifold supports fast, spin­allowed fluorescence on the nanosecond timescale,
while the T1 triplet state acts as a metastable “shelving” level. When population becomes
temporarily trapped in T1, the emission cycle is interrupted, producing characteristic pho­
ton bunching in g(2)(τ) and, in some cases, intermittent fluorescence known as blinking
[9, 4].
Prolonged occupation of the triplet state can also facilitate photochemical reactions with
the surrounding environment. In particular, interactions between T1 and reactive oxygen
species often lead to irreversible photobleaching, where the emitter permanently loses
its fluorescence [4, 12]. Both blinking and photobleaching originate from triplet­mediated
dynamics and represent key considerations when evaluating the long­term stability of
single­photon emitters.

2.5
Types of Quantum Emitters
Quantum emitters can be broadly categorized into three groups: (1) semiconductor quan­
tum dots (QDs), (2) optically active point defects in two­dimensional (2D) materials such
as hexagonal boron nitride (hBN), and (3) organic molecules, especially polycyclic aro­
matic hydrocarbons (PAHs) [9, 8]. Each group exhibits different advantages and physical
mechanisms for single­photon emission, and they differ in operating conditions, spectral
stability, and integrability with photonic architectures.
2.5.1
Emitters in Two­Dimensional Materials
Two­dimensional (2D) materials have emerged as promising hosts for single­photon emis­
sion due to their atomically thin nature, tunable electronic structure, and ease of integra­
tion into photonic platforms. Compared to traditional single­photon sources embedded in
three­dimensional crystals like diamond, silicon carbide, or zinc oxide—where emission
efficiency can be compromised due to light trapping in the bulk dielectric environment—2D
materials offer a significant advantage by reducing the dielectric thickness to the atomic
scale. This structural thinness not only enhances photon extraction efficiency but also fa­
cilitates direct integration with on­chip photonic circuits [13]. In addition, quantum confine­
ment and reduced dielectric screening enhance radiative rates and oscillator strengths,
while weak defect–phonon coupling improves emission coherence. Their planar geom­
etry allows atomic­precision control over defect formation, and their optical transparency
enables efficient photon collection. Furthermore, the open surface architecture supports
integration with optical cavities and enables manipulation techniques such as electric and
magnetic field tuning [1].
Single­photon emission has been demonstrated in several classes of 2D materials, in­
cluding transition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs) such as MoS2, WS2, and WSe2. In these
systems, localized excitons trapped in strain potentials or defect sites give rise to narrow
zero­phonon lines and high­purity emission. Recent work on WSe2 quantum emitters has
shown that combining hBN encapsulation with electrostatic biasing can suppress spectral
diffusion and reduce linewidths toward the transform­limited regime, demonstrating their
potential as low­noise, tunable single­photon sources for integrated quantum photonics
[14].
2.5.2
Hexagonal Boron Nitride as a Host Material
Among these materials, hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) stands out as a particularly favor­
able host for single­photon emitters (SPEs). It combines a wide bandgap (approximately
6 eV) (Figure 2.2b), high chemical and thermal stability, and the ability to host optically
active point defects that emit across a broad spectral range, from the ultraviolet to the
near­infrared. Importantly, these emitters remain stable and photoluminescent even at
room temperature, which represents a major advantage over other two­dimensional sys­
tems that often require cryogenic cooling.
Optical transitions in hBN are strongly excitonic in nature, characterized by tightly bound
excitons with binding energies around 130 meV. These transitions often involve phonon­
assisted recombination due to the indirect nature of the bandgap in multilayer structures.
As such, hBN exhibits pronounced vibronic features, including sharp zero­phonon lines
(ZPLs) and broad phonon sidebands (PSBs), arising from significant electron–phonon
coupling [15].
In terms of emission characteristics, hBN covers a broad spectral range from the ultraviolet
to the near­infrared (UV–NIR), with zero­phonon lines (ZPLs) typically observed between
550 nm and 762 nm (2.25–1.63 eV) [12], depending on the specific defect type and layer
configuration. These emitters show significant photostability, with fluorescence lifetimes

stability, and excellent photon purity positions hBN as a highly promising material for in­

2.5.3
Comparison of hBN with Other 2D Materials as Hosts for Quantum
Emitters
Single­photon emission has also been demonstrated in other 2D materials, such as tran­
sition metal dichalcogenides (TMDs), including MoS2, WS2, and WSe2. In these systems,
emission is typically enabled by exciton trapping at defects or in strain­induced potentials
[12, 1]. However, in contrast to hBN, quantum emission from TMDs has been primarily
observed under cryogenic conditions [12, 1], limiting their practical application in room­
temperature photonic devices.
Moreover, excitons in TMDs are generally of the Wannier–Mott type, which are not in­
herently localized [12, 1]. Instead, they must be confined through additional mechanisms
such as strain or moiré potentials to facilitate quantum emission. In contrast, hBN sup­
ports optically active point defects that are inherently localized and stable under ambient
conditions.
Another key advantage of hBN­based single­photon emitters (SPEs) is their high single­
photon purity, with second­order autocorrelation values reaching g(2)(0) = 0.01 (Fig­
ure 2.2a), indicating minimal multiphoton events [12]. This performance is superior to
many TMD­based emitters such as WSe2 and WS2, where g(2)(0) values are typically
higher (0.2 and 0.31, respectively) and often necessitate cryogenic conditions [12].
Additionally, hBN emitters exhibit narrow zero­phonon lines (ZPLs), with linewidths around
0.21 µeV—significantly narrower than in materials like MoS2 (0.5–6 meV) or GaSe (3.7–
5.2 meV) [12, 1]. The fluorescence lifetimes of hBN emitters range from 1.53 to 2.88 ns
[12], reflecting fast and efficient radiative recombination, while other 2D emitters like WSe2
and GaSe display a broader lifetime distribution (100 ps to 22 ns).
Moreover, hBN emitters remain optically active at high temperatures, with stable operation
demonstrated up to 800 K. Most TMD emitters, in contrast, require cryogenic cooling to
maintain single­photon emission. Their photon count rates are also lower—typically in the
kHz range—compared to the MHz­scale brightness of hBN emitters.
While some approaches have been proposed to mitigate these limitations—such as plas­
monic cavity coupling, electron­beam­induced defect creation, and Stark tuning via ex­
ternal fields—these remain largely experimental and lack the room­temperature stability
and brightness already demonstrated in hBN [1].
2.5.4
Intrinsic Defects in hBN as Candidates for Single­Photon Emitters
Defects in hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) are central to its role as a host for single­photon
emitters (SPEs). Quantum light emission originates from localized states introduced by
structural imperfections such as vacancies, antisite defects, and substitutional dopants
[15]. These point defects create discrete energy levels within the wide bandgap (∼6 eV),
enabling optical transitions that support non­classical photon emission [8].
Despite rapid progress, the microscopic origin of many emission centers remains unre­
solved. Multiple types of point defects can form—each with different emission characteristics—
resulting in a wide distribution of zero­phonon line (ZPL) energies from the ultraviolet
(∼4.1 eV) to the near­infrared (∼1.6 eV) [1]. Random formation leads to spectral and

2.6
Molecular Quantum Emitters
Organic dye molecules, particularly polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) such as
terrylene, pentacene, and dibenzoterrylene (DBT), are among the most popular single­
photon emitters. Their emission originates from well­defined electronic transitions be­
tween the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) and the lowest unoccupied molec­
ular orbital (LUMO) (Figure 2.3b) [9, 10]. Under cryogenic conditions and on clean crys­
talline substrates, these molecules exhibit minimal inhomogeneous broadening, high spec­
tral purity, and strong quantum yields. Their photophysical behavior—including antibunch­
ing at short timescales and bunching at intermediate delays—is consistent with their quan­
tum emitter nature and can be captured through second­order correlation measurements
[9, 10].
Typical ZPL emission wavelengths for PAHs range from 580 nm to 750 nm, depending on
the molecular species and host environment (Figure 2.3d) [17]. Terrylene and DBT emit
near 580–620 nm, pentacene typically at 600–650 nm, and perylene closer to 435 nm.
These ranges are important for applications requiring spectral alignment and are summa­
rized in Figure 2.3d, which includes the pentacene molecules studied in this work.
Recent theoretical developments by Gürlek and Wang [17] have emphasized that single
organic molecules—such as PAHs embedded in solid­state matrices—form hybrid quan­
tum systems with coupled electronic, vibronic, phononic, and spin substructures (Fig­
ure 2.3c).
In PAH­based quantum emitters, optical transitions occur between the electronic ground
state (S0) and the first excited singlet state (S1), arising from π →π∗HOMO–LUMO
transitions in the conjugated molecular backbone (Figure 2.3b) [4, 10]. Triplet states (T1)
can be accessed via intersystem crossing (ISC) and lead to delayed emission such as
phosphorescence, though in PAHs this process is weak, preserving high fluorescence
quantum yield.
As discussed in Section 2.2, the Franck–Condon principle governs the vibronic structure
of molecular emission through vibrational wavefunction overlap (Figure 2.3e). In PAHs,
weak electron–phonon coupling on clean hosts such as hBN yields high Debye–Waller
factors (DWF ∼0.8) and sharp ZPLs, making terrylene and pentacene excellent candi­
dates for coherent single­photon sources (Figure 2.3a) [4, 11].
The exceptionally low ISC rate in PAHs not only maintains high fluorescence quantum
yields but also suppresses blinking and photobleaching, making these molecules robust
single­photon emitters compared to many solid­state defect systems.

Experimental Methods and Set Up
This chapter outlines the complete methodology used for sample preparation, cleaning,
and molecular deposition, which forms the foundation for all measurements presented
in the following sections. It includes detailed procedures for substrate preparation, ex­
foliation of hBN flakes, cleaning protocols, and the steps leading up to low­temperature
spectroscopy. All samples follow a consistent workflow to ensure reproducibility across
trials.
3.1
Substrate Preparation and Pre­cleaning
The substrates used are silicon wafers with a 90 nm layer of thermally grown SiO2. Align­
ment markers are pre­patterned onto the wafers to facilitate optical characterization and
flake localization. Grid lines are created on the surface using a scratching tool, and the
wafers are then broken into approximately 1 cm × 1 cm squares.
Before proceeding with exfoliation, the substrates are tested under an optical microscope
to assess their surface morphology and identify any contamination or particles introduced
during the cleaving process.
To ensure cleanliness prior to flake deposition, a standardized pre­cleaning protocol is
followed. This consists of three main steps:
• Immersion in acetone for 5 minutes.
• Immersion in trichloroethylene (TCE) for 20 minutes.
• Oxygen plasma treatment for 2 minutes at 2 mbar pressure and a gas flow rate of
8–10 cm3/min.
This sequence is designed to remove organic contaminants and surface residues that
could interfere with flake adhesion and subsequent optical measurements.
3.2
Exfoliation of hBN
Hexagonal boron nitride (hBN) flakes are prepared using mechanical exfoliation with ad­
hesive tape. Two exfoliation methods are initially tested: standard exfoliation at room
temperature and heat­assisted exfoliation using a heater at 80 °C to improve flake trans­
fer efficiency by reducing contaminants on the flake. After comparative assessment, the
standard room­temperature method is selected for the remainder of the study due to better
reproducibility and flake quality.
The exfoliated flakes are transferred directly onto the pre­cleaned SiO2/Si substrates and
are immediately inspected under an optical microscope to locate suitable flakes for further
processing.
3.3
Cleaning of Exfoliated hBN
Following exfoliation, the hBN flakes undergo a multi­step cleaning protocol designed
to minimize background fluorescence and surface contamination. The main goal is to re­
duce the background emission and achieve lower count rates, thus preparing the samples
for subsequent molecular deposition. Each sample is first immersed in trichloroethylene

(TCE) for 20 minutes to dissolve adhesive residues and organic impurities. The TCE­
treated samples are then exposed to an oxygen plasma for 2 minutes under a pressure
of 2 mbar and a flow rate of 8–10 cm3/min. This plasma step facilitates the removal of

To evaluate and optimize this cleaning strategy, multiple cleaning cycles are implemented

Molecular Deposition Procedure and Cryogenic Setup
Pentacene is selected as the molecular emitter for deposition due to its stable optical

followed.
• Bottle 1: 1 mg of pentacene is dissolved in 1.5 mL of toluene.

• Bottle 3: 1 mL of fresh toluene is mixed with 20 µL of Bottle 2.

3.5.1
Cryogenic Optical Setup for Emitter Localization and Spectroscopy
The cryogenic optical setup is designed to characterize quantum emitters following molec­
ular deposition under low­temperature conditions (approximately 5 K). The setup enables
both spatial imaging and spectral analysis by switching between two detection paths (Fig­
ure 3.2).
Excitation is provided by a 532 nm continuous laser. The beam is directed through a
series of mirrors toward the optical axis of the microscope. Before reaching the sample, it
passes through a 400 mm focal length lens positioned in front of a 550 nm dichroic mirror.
The function of the 400 mm lens is twofold:
• When inserted, it creates a wide­field illumination spot at the sample plane, enabling
real­space imaging of a larger area.
• When removed, the beam is tightly focused into a small excitation spot at the sample
plane.
The beam is then reflected downward by the dichroic mirror and directed through a 60×
objective, which focuses it onto the sample. The sample, located inside a cryostat, emits
photoluminescence (PL), which is collected by the same objective lens.
As the PL propagates upward from the sample, it reaches the region just below the
dichroic mirror. At this point, a removable mirror determines the detection pathway:
• If the mirror is inserted, it intercepts the emitted PL before it reaches the dichroic mir­
ror and redirects it horizontally toward a camera. A 15 cm lens placed in front of the
camera forms a real­space image of the sample, allowing for the spatial localization
of quantum emitters.
• If the mirror is removed, the PL continues its path upward through the dichroic mir­
ror and is then guided through a set of mirrors and a 15 cm focusing lens, which
directs the beam into the entrance slit of a spectrometer for high­resolution spectral
analysis.

This chapter presents the key experimental results obtained during the two main phases
of the study: (1) optimization of the cleaning protocol for hexagonal boron nitride (hBN)
flakes, and (2) deposition and spectroscopic characterization of fluorescent organic molecules
on the cleaned hBN substrates.
The first objective is to develop a reproducible and effective cleaning procedure that re­
duces background photoluminescence (PL) and minimizes surface contamination on ex­

pentacene—onto the cleaned hBN flakes. Pentacene is chosen for its well­established
photophysical behavior under cryogenic conditions, making it a suitable candidate for
studying single­molecule emission on 2D substrates. A series of dilution and spin­coating
procedures is used to achieve isolated molecular coverage. Low­temperature spectroscopy
is performed to evaluate key optical properties such as zero­phonon line (ZPL) sharpness,
vibronic sidebands, emission stability, and Debye–Waller factor.
By addressing both the optimization of substrate cleanliness and the photophysical char­
acterization of deposited emitters, this chapter provides a comprehensive assessment
of the origin of emission and thus hBN’s role as a host platform for molecular quantum
emitters.

4.4
Photophysical Analysis in Room Temperature
To verify whether the bright emission centers observed after the final cleaning cycles could
correspond to single­photon emitters, second­order correlation measurements are per­
formed in room temperature. These measurements analyze how photons are emitted
over time by using the normalized intensity autocorrelation function g(2)(τ), where τ is the
time delay between two detected photons.
A characteristic of single­photon emission is antibunching behavior, characterized by a
dip at zero time delay, g(2)(0) < 0.5, which means that a true single emitter cannot emit
two photons at the same time. Figure 4.12 shows representative g(2)(τ) curves acquired
from two different emission spots, with fits based on the model described in the equation
2.25. The first emitter shows a minimum g(2)(0) = 0.351 ± 0.074, and the second emitter
achieves a deeper suppression of g(2)(0) = 0.271 ± 0.086. In both cases, these values
clearly are below the classical threshold of 0.5, confirming the presence of single­photon
emitters. The observed antibunching timescales are 1.98±0.87 ns for the first emitter and
1.78±0.72 ns for the second. Additionally, the correlation curves show photon bunching at
longer delay times, with characteristic timescales of 0.18–0.2 µs. This behavior suggests
that the emitter occasionally enters a metastable intermediate state before returning to
the bright state.

5.1
Discussion
5.1.1
Evaluation of the Cleaning Protocol
The cleaning procedure in this study combines repeated cycles of trichloroethylene (TCE)
rinsing with oxygen plasma exposure to reduce photoluminescence (PL) background and
remove surface contamination from exfoliated hBN flakes. A low background is important
prior to the deliberate deposition of molecules, as it allows for a more reliable investigation
into the origin of the observed emission. In particular, this enables a distinction between
emission from defects in hBN and that caused by molecular contamination—a discussion
also performed by Neumann et al. [16] and Smit et al. [6].
From our results, it is apparent that even though immersing the samples in a solvent
such as TCE is essential, alone it does not sufficiently remove fluorescent residues. Ef­
fective background reduction requires that solvent rinsing is followed by oxygen plasma
treatment. The plasma step likely removes tightly bound hydrocarbons or organic frag­
ments that remain after solvent exposure. However, the outcome of this process varies
across different flakes, likely depending on flake thickness, morphology, or unintentional
environmental contamination during exfoliation and transfer.
An important observation is that repeated plasma exposure does not always lead to im­
proved results. In some cases, additional cleaning cycles increase the number or intensity
of localized emission spots. This indicates that there exists an optimal combination of cy­
cle number and plasma duration. Excessive plasma exposure may activate new emission
centers rather than suppress background, likely due to new spots created by the oxygen
plasma becoming available for contamination to adhere to. This effect is consistent with
the findings of Schaumburg et al. [2], who show that oxygen plasma treatment induces
optically active defect states in hBN flakes.
Moreover, the time interval between successive cleaning steps may also influence the
effectiveness of the process. Prolonged delays between solvent rinsing and plasma treat­
ment could allow airborne or dissolved contaminants to reattach to the surface, counter­
acting the benefits of the previous cleaning step.
To evaluate the presence of isolated emitters following the cleaning procedure, stability
mapping and photon correlation measurements are also performed. The results show a
possible presence of single­photon emission both from a region on the flake and from
spots outside the flake, indicating that after TCE rinsing, the contamination, which is
spread across the substrate, can be considered as the origin of emission. This is partic­
ularly supported by the stability map, where spots located away from the hBN flake also
exhibit emission with spectral and temporal features indicative of localized quantum emit­
ters. Antibunching behavior is observed in the g(2)(τ) function, with g(2)(0) values well
below 0.5, confirming that the cleaned hBN flakes still host single­photon emission. Com­
bined, these results highlight two key observations. First, the emitters located outside the
flake suggest that contamination distributed on the substrate surface may be responsible
for the observed emission. Second, the fact that single­photon emission is still observed
on the flake even after multiple cleaning cycles indicates either that the cleaning is not
fully optimized or that some defect sites within hBN remain active.

After this first step of the study, it becomes clear that surface contamination introduced
during sample preparation plays a significant role in the observed emission. Although
hexagonal boron nitride likely contributes to the emission—either by acting as a host or
by stabilizing local emitters—it does not appear to be the only origin of emission. This
interpretation is consistent with observations by Neumann and Smit, who support that
emission might come from molecular residues. The observed reduction in emission fol­
lowing optimized cleaning cycles supports the idea that part of the fluorescence originates
from external sources. These findings suggest that while hBN offers a stable platform that
can enhance or support single­photon emission, additional factors—such as adsorbed
molecules or processing residues—must also be considered. This can also provide a
controllable and on­demand way of emission by choosing the right molecule to deposit
each time.
5.1.2
Discussion: Molecular Deposition and Cryogenic Characterization
The deposition of pentacene molecules onto cleaned hBN substrates aims to explore the
role of 2D hosts in stabilizing organic quantum emitters and to examine the photophysi­
cal characteristics of the molecules under cryogenic conditions. Although our approach
includes dilution in toluene and spin coating—standard methods to improve molecular
dispersion—we still observe evidence of clustering in the microscopy images, even in the
most diluted case (Bottle 3). Bright spots remain unevenly distributed and, in some re­
gions, appear aggregated or larger in size, indicating incomplete isolation of the deposited
molecules.
Comparable studies, such as the work by Smit et al., demonstrated that spin­coated poly­
cyclic aromatic hydrocarbons on hBN can give isolated emitters with narrow spectral sig­
natures when conditions are carefully optimized. In our method, however, spin coating
does not appear to significantly reduce clustering, suggesting that additional adjustments
are required to achieve better molecular isolation.
Nevertheless, spectroscopic data collected at 5 K reveal emission features consistent
with stable and well­defined molecular quantum emitters in selected regions. A spectrum
from a Bottle 3 sample exhibits a sharp zero­phonon line (ZPL) at 648.6 nm (1.91 eV)
with vibronic sidebands at 678 nm and 712 nm. The full width at half maximum (FWHM)
of 9.08 nm and a calculated Debye–Waller (DW) factor of approximately 0.7 suggest that
the emitter exhibits low phonon coupling and maintains strong optical coherence. These
values are in accordance with those reported by Singh et al.[18] for pentacene embedded
in high­purity organic crystals and also fall within the typical range of PAH molecules
studied in cryogenic single­molecule experiments, as discussed by Toninelli et al.[4].
Furthermore, the temporal stability of the emission over a 200­second measurement sup­
ports the presence of photostable emitters.
This behavior is characteristic of single­
molecule quantum emitters and aligns with observations by Neumann et al.[16], who
noted similar emission patterns from PAHs in PMMA films.
After establishing the spectral features of isolated emitters in Bottle 3 samples, we proceed
to a comparative analysis aimed at clarifying the origin of the emission. Initially, it is unclear
whether the emission arises from molecules deposited directly on the SiO2 substrate, from
aggregated molecular clusters, or from pentacene molecules onto hBN flakes. To resolve
this, we examine PL spectra from two distinct spatial regions: (1) areas likely containing
molecular clusters on SiO2 and (2) regions identified as exfoliated hBN flakes.
When comparing spectra from two distinct regions—one with a cluster­like morphology
and one on the hBN flake—no significant differences are observed. In both cases, the
Photophysical Properties of Quantum Emitters in Hexagonal Boron Nitride and Molecules

==PAGE:53==
ZPL appears at approximately 618–620 nm, a wavelength that is similar to emission from
toluene reported by Trinh et al. [19], suggesting that the solvent itself may dominate the
optical response.
The hBN flake region shows a slightly higher Debye–Waller (DW) factor compared to the
cluster, but the improvement is modest and insufficient to clearly attribute improved coher­
ence to the hBN flake. Interestingly, the ZPL intensity is higher in the cluster region, while
both spectra share an additional peak near 595 nm. The FWHM of the ZPL is essentially
the same in both cases, further highlighting the lack of a clear distinction between the two
environments. This suggests that under current conditions, hBN does not significantly
enhance spectral purity or coherence, and the observed differences may instead reflect
variations in flake thickness, surface roughness, or local molecule distribution.
Importantly, however, these results confirm that the deposited pentacene molecules are
optically active and emit in the expected wavelength range (e.g., around 640 nm), while
additional features such as the 620 nm peak may be attributed to residual toluene, con­
sistent with prior reports. This supports the idea that similar emission features frequently
observed in the literature may, in some cases, originate from unintentionally deposited or
residual molecular species.
In summary, while hBN may offer a stabilizing effect in certain conditions, its influence
on optical coherence is not straightforward and appears to depend on multiple factors
beyond just the substrate itself. Achieving clear, isolated single­molecule emission with
high spectral purity will likely require improved control over the deposition process, such
as better solvent handling or alternative strategies for molecular dispersion.
5.2
Future Work
This thesis provides a first step towards understanding the origin of emission in molecule–
hBN systems, aiming to develop a method for generating single­photon emission in a
deliberate and controllable way. The ability to choose specific molecules and engineer
the interface conditions is essential for tuning the emission properties and improving their
suitability for applications in quantum technologies.
A primary future direction is the further optimization of the cleaning protocol to prepare
substrates with minimal background fluorescence before deliberate molecular deposition.
Introducing thermal annealing steps (e.g., at 300–400 °C under inert gas flow) could help
remove resist residues and adsorbates remaining after exfoliation. Additionally, the tim­
ing between TCE rinsing and plasma exposure, as well as the number and duration of
cleaning cycles, should be systematically tuned. These adjustments are expected to re­
duce unintentional defect formation and result in cleaner substrates, which is essential for
isolating the true origin of emission.
To assess the contributions of the substrate and the host material, future experiments
could employ suspended hBN membranes. This approach would help determine whether
the emission arises from intrinsic hBN defects or from the hBN–SiO2 interface, as sug­
gested by Neumann et al. Clarifying this distinction is critical for controlling and engineer­
ing the emitter environment.
Another important avenue is improving the molecular deposition process to reliably achieve
single­molecule emission. Refining the spin­coating parameters to minimize clustering is
a key step, as well as exploring alternative deposition techniques.
Beyond hBN, investigating alternative 2D substrates such as graphene, MoS2, or WS2 will
help determine whether hBN provides unique advantages or if comparable performance
46
Photophysical Properties of Quantum Emitters in Hexagonal Boron Nitride and Molecules
==PAGE:54==
can be achieved on other van der Waals materials. Comparative studies may reveal
whether the atomically flat, inert surface of hBN is critical for enabling narrow zero­phonon
lines and high Debye–Waller factors or if similar behavior can be reproduced on other
platforms.
Finally, it will be important to combine experimental measurements with theoretical mod­
eling. By comparing the results with density functional theory (DFT) simulations of how
molecules interact with the substrate, we can better understand the mechanisms behind
the emission. Simulations that include realistic situations, such as single molecules ver­
sus clusters on hBN flakes, can guide the improvement of deposition methods and bring
us closer to creating controlled, on­demand quantum emitters with optimized optical prop­
erties.

A
Third Cleaning Trial – Sample 3
A third cleaning trial is conducted on a new hBN flake to further evaluate the reproducibil­
ity of the cleaning protocol. In this trial, only confocal PL scans are recorded at each step.
Figure A.1 shows the PL intensity maps of the flake before cleaning, after the first clean­
ing cycle (TCE + oxygen plasma), and after the second cleaning cycle (repeated TCE +
oxygen plasma).
Before cleaning, the flake exhibits a high background signal across its surface. After the
first cleaning cycle, the emission decreases as expected, similar to Samples 1 and 2.
However, after the second cleaning cycle, a significant increase in emission is observed
across the flake and the surrounding substrate. This behavior contrasts with the previous
two samples, where two successive cycles reduce the overall background fluorescence.
This unexpected increase in PL intensity suggests that some variation may have occurred
during the experiment. Possible explanations include non­uniform plasma exposure, par­
tial exposure of the flake during treatment, or unintentional re­adsorption of contaminants
between steps (likely due to non­optimal time intervals). Another possibility is that the
oxygen plasma begins to chemically modify the hBN surface after repeated exposure,
creating emission­active sites, as reported in plasma­induced defect studies. While the
exact cause is not determined, this trial highlights the sensitivity of the cleaning protocol
to experimental conditions.
