Paper I: Discard survival of trawled Norway lobster (Nephrops norvegicus) in Kattegat and 
Skagerrak. 
Abstract 
The demand of the commercially valuable Nephrops (Nephrops norvegicus)  is increasing and the 
fishery is considered as one of the most valuable in Kattegat and Skagerrak. It is for the largest part a 
mixed species fishery, which means that also gadoids, flatfish and other species of commercial value 
are caught. Trawl fisheries for Nephrops is regarded as one of the most discarding fisheries and a 
substantial part of the discards are constituted by undersized Nephrops that cannot be sold. The 
mortality of the discarded animals is often exceeding the survival of these and and it is generally 
considered as a waste of resources. The present thesis consists of a review and a paper describing the 
main problems of the fishery and investigating the discard survival of Nephrops after trawling.  
The review describes the importance of the Nephrops fishery and the associated discards are outlined 
and trawling effects and fate of discards are described. Finally, the Danish and European laws 
regarding the fishery and selective fishing gear are presented. Paper I investigates the discard 
survival of Nephrops after trawling in Kattegat and Skagerrak, including the amount of predation by 
birds, vitality assessment and observation studies. After fishing, the Nephrops were kept in on-land 
recirculating systems for 8 days without being fed. The mean survival was 20%. However, a big 
variation in the survival rates between test hauls was found (0 to 48%).

1. Introduction 
The present thesis aims to study the discard survival of Nephrops norvegicus in Skagerrak and 
Kattegat after trawling. An important element was to obtain a better understanding of the underlying 
factors influencing the chance of survival after being subject to fishing including different 
preconditions of the Nephrops.  
EU is implementing a new common fisheries policy, including a discard ban in all fisheries. This 
means that unwanted bycatch can no longer be discarded and must be landed or not caught in the 
first place. However, fish and crustacean species expected to have high survival rates after being 
discarded should be identified scientifically through experiments, in order to be excluded from the 
discard ban.  
The objectives for this discard survival assessment were an estimation of discard survival rate, 
including predation by birds, representative of the management unit. This was done by conducting a 
vitality assessment, assessing representative conditions, as well as captivity observations of 
individuals in different vitality states, to generate a damage index and an overall mean survival 
estimate. This approach was in accordance to the approach suggested by ICES WKMEDS REPORT 
2014. 
In the present review, the importance of the fishery and the associated discards are outlined, trawling 
effects and fate of discards are described. Finally, the Danish and European laws regarding the 
fishery and selective fishing gear are presented. 
2. Importance of the fishery 
The demand of the commercially valuable Nephrops is increasing, which has resulted in the increase 
of the catches in the North-east Atlantic throughout the last fifty years, although catches have been 
more or less stable since 1985 (Ungfors et al 2013). This fishery is commercially very important and 
is regarded as the most valuable fishery in Europe, where landings have exceeded more than 60,000 
tonnes per year (2010) across all countries. The vast majority have been caught in the north-eastern 
Atlantic, with UK landings representing the most significant part (>50%) (Ridgway et al 2006a; 
Ungfors et al 2013). Excluding the industrial fisheries, Nephrops constitute 11% of the total Danish 
fishery in terms of value and is the most valuable fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak. More than 3,000 
tonnes were landed in 2014 in Denmark alone, with a first sale value of more than 210 million DKK 
(based on landings 2014, extracted from NaturErhvervstyrelsens afregningsregister) (Bergmann et al 
2002; Krag et al 2008; Ungfors et al 2013).

3. Introduction of Nephrops norvegicus 
Nephrops norvegicus (Linnaeus 1758), often referred to as Nephrops, Norway Lobster, Langoustine, 
Prawn, Dublin Bay prawn or Scampi, is a red- orange member of the Nephropidae family, with a 
maximum total length of 25 cm (figure 1) (Bell et al 2013).  
 
Figure 1. Nephrops norvegicus caught in the Kattegat (ICES area IIIa). 
The maturity age is between 3 and 4 years, where females mature faster than males (Bell et al 2013). 
After maturity, male moults 1 to 2 times per year (in late winter/spring and in late summer/fall) and 
females only 1 time per year at the most (in late winter or after hatching of eggs) (Bell et al 2013).  
Nephrops are widely distributed in the north-east Atlantic, starting from Iceland and Norway in the 
north and down to Morocco and Greece in the south, but are absent in the Baltic Sea and the Black 
Sea (Bell et al 2013). They prefer the muddy seabed with more than 40% of the sediment consisting 
of silt and clay, which is generally located at depths of 20-800 m. Here, they burrow 20-30 cm deep 
holes in the mud, used as refuges and leaves them only for foraging or mating (Rice and Chapman 
1971; Bell et al 2013; Ungfors et al 2013). These burrows can be complex and have several entrances 
(Rice and Chapman 1971). Nephrops are opportunistic scavengers and leave their burrows for short 
feeding trips, foraging unselectively on polychaetes, crustaceans, molluscs and more rarely 
echinoderms (Bell et al 2013). 
4. Description of the fishery 
In the vast majority of the distribution area, Nephrops are fished with demersal trawls (mostly otter 
or beam trawl). However, there is a significant proportion being caught with creel in the western part 
of Scotland and Sweden (Catchpole et al 2006a; Ungfors et al 2013). The creel fishery is not used in 
Denmark, where the Nephrops are caught using trawls, ranging from small trawls designed to 
exclusively catch Nephrops, to large trawls designed for catching whitefish (Ulrich and Andersen 
==PAGE:7==
7 
 
2004; Ungfors et al 2013). The Nephrops fishery takes place all year round, although targeted mostly 
during summer, and is for the largest part a mixed species fishery, which means that the target 
species are constituted by Nephrops but also gadoids, flatfish and other species of commercial value 
(ICES 2004; Ulrich and Andersen 2004; Feekings et al 2012).  
The trawl with a usual speed on 2-3 knots quickly obtains Nephrops, with a swimming speed on 0.5 
to 1.5 knots, if they however get in the path of the trawl, as Nephrops are not herded by the fishing 
gear. Once captured in the net, the animals that escape are most likely to do so through the meshes in 
the lower sections of the trawl  (Newland and Chapman 1989; Frandsen et al 2010; Frandsen et al 
2011).  
As most of the fishery is a mixed species fishery, the catches are very diverse. Many factors are 
determining for the composition such as area, depth of fishing, season (influencing growth- and 
migration patterns), time of day and tide and of great importance the trawl efficiency and selectivity. 
Catch composition have shown to vary a lot even between close areas, within the same area and 
between years (Bergmann et al 2002; Frandsen et al 2010). Figure 2 shows the catch from Nephrops 
fishery in Kattegat. The catch was dominated by dab (Limanda limanda), cod (Gadus morhua) and 
non-commercial species of invertebrates (paper I). The Nephrops caught in Kattegat and Skagerrak 
are typically just around minimum landing size (MLS, carapace length 40 mm) (Paper I). 
==PAGE:8==
8 
 
 
Figure 2. Catch composition from Kattegat dominated by witch flounder, cod and invertebrates.  
The Nephrops stock size in Kattegat and Skagerrak is unknown, but underwater TV monitoring 
indicates an abundance of approximately 3,000 million individuals. Finally, catch per unit of effort 
suggests that the stock is exploited sustainably (ICES 2014a). 
5. Discards 
Discarding happens when the composition of the catch brought on deck is different from the one 
expected to be sold. Unwanted animals are thrown back into the sea as a response to various factors, 
such as the market situation and regulations (e.g. minimal size landing and quotas). Survival of 
discard varies, but is generally exceeded by the mortality and it is therefore generally considered a 
waste of resources (Bergmann et al 2002; Catchpole et al 2005; Harrington et al 2005; Broadhurst et 
al 2006; Sigurðardóttir et al 2015). Trawl fisheries for Nephrops is regarded as one of the most 
discarding fisheries, ranking as number eight among the 20 most discarding fisheries (when listed 
after type of fishery) and as number five when listed after gear type (Bergmann et al 2002). 
Bergmann (2002) found that in Nephrops fishery with otter trawl in the Clyde Sea, only 14 to 23% of 
the catch biomass consisted of Nephrops and 66 to 80 % of the total biomass was discarded. Similar 
==PAGE:9==
9 
 
observations were made in the Irish Sea where only 12 and 13 % of the catches respectively were 
made up by Nephrops (Evans et al 1994; Bergmann et al 2002). 
Discard composition 
The discard composition is determined by several factors such as area, season, depth of fishing and 
fishing gear (Feekings et al 2012). Generally, the composition is constituted by undersized Nephrops 
and undersized marketable fish. These fish consists mostly of whiting (Merlangius merlangus), 
haddock (Melanogrammus aeglefinus) and cod (Gadus morhua). A significant proportion of discards 
is often consisting of invertebrates such as whelks and starfish (Evans et al 1994; Bergmann et al 
2002; Catchpole and Revill 2008).  
6. Trawling effects on Nephrops 
Caught animals are exposed to several stressors when encountering the fishing gear. This can cause a 
decrease of quality in the product for the landed catch (e.g. wounds, flesh quality) and be fatal for the 
discarded and injured fish when returned into the sea (Botta et al 1987; ICES 2014b).  
The first physical contact between the Nephrops and the fishing gear is the interaction between the 
Nephrops and the ground gear. After this, Nephrops pass through in the direction of the codend, 
where typical catch behaviour is rolling across the bottom of the net until they arrive at the codend 
(Newland and Chapman 1989). In this part of the catch process, the physical properties of the gear 
(e.g. knot thickness, mesh shape, stiffness etc.) is likely to have a great influence on the damages 
incurred by the Nephrops. Not only the physical properties of the gear can affect the animals but also 
the catch size, deployment duration, towing speed and the process of getting the catch on board  
(Evans et al 1994; Wileman 1999). Environmental factors can also influence the vitality of 
Nephrops. The animals might be caught on large depths in almost complete darkness and they are 
brought to the surface. During this, important changes in terms of temperature, pressure, salinity and 
light intensity can occur. These factors will affect the survival/vitality of the Nephrops before the 
catch is even on board the fishing vessel (Harris and Ulmestrand 2004; Ridgway et al 2006c; ICES 
2014b). When the catch is on board, a completely new set of influences are brought upon the 
Nephrops and the rest of the catch such as air-exposure, handling and release of the discards to the 
water (Ridgway et al 2006c; ICES 2014b). 
Stressors like physical damages and air-exposure have shown to affect survival. First, physical 
damages can cause immediate death caused by crushing of the body, vital body parts or cause loss of 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
body fluids such as haemolymph (Evans et al 1994; Wileman 1999; ICES 2014b). Injuries incurred 
during fishing and physical contact from the fishing gear can lower the chances of survival; wounds 
can cause infections especially in stressed animals, which can directly cause mortality or can 
increase the risk of predation or lower chances of surviving competition. Finally, the chances of 
surviving also depend on which preconditions the animals are such as moulting state, size and sex 
(Wileman 1999; ICES 2014b).

happens within the first 24 hours (figure 8) (Ridgway et al 2006c; Paper I).

Not only the commercially fished stocks are affected by discarding, there are also environmental 
effects. Discards of marine organisms contributes to the food supply for scavengers such as seabirds 
and scavenging fish, whelks, crabs and starfish (Catchpole et al 2006a). The high levels of discards 
in the Nephrops fishery suggests that 37% of the energy demand for benthic scavengers are supplied 
from discards, which may give populations an advantage that would not naturally occur (Catchpole 
et al 2006b). The ecological effects from discarding could influence the natural energy flows 
(Catchpole et al 2006b). 
Many seabirds and especially gulls are gathered around the fishing boats, waiting for guts and 
discards to be thrown overboard. The present study found that 0 % of the discarded Nephrops were 
taken by seagull, whereas Evans et al 1994 found that 70% of Nephrops thrown overboard was taken 
by birds (Evans et al 1994; Paper I). 
8. Review of fisheries laws regarding Nephrops 
The laws and regulations described in this section is focusing on the Danish Nephrops fishery. The 
Danish law for fisheries were implemented to secure protection of living resources in both salt- and 
freshwater, to protect other living organisms and to secure a sustainable basis for fisheries and 
associated professions (Ministry of Food, Agriculture and Fisheries 2014). The European council has 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
set objectives for regulations in the Nephrops fishery and they serve the purpose of, as for many 
other types of fisheries, protection of juveniles and ensuring sustainable exploitation of the wild 
stocks (European Parliament and Council of the European Union 2013).  
To ensure the above-mentioned objectives, different management tools have been adopted. The 
Danish ministry for food, agriculture and fisheries can implement minimum landing sizes and total 
allowed catch (TAC) for specific species (Ministry of Food Agriculture and Fisheries 2014). The 
minister can also set requirements for the use, design, mesh-size, placement and number of allowed 
fishing gear (Ministry of Food Agriculture and Fisheries 2014). Below, the requirements for these 
are described.  
TAC and quota system 
Legislations are made in Europe to estimate the amount of discards, through collection and 
evaluation of biological data of all catches including discards (EEC 2000). Total allowable catch 
(TAC) and quotas are set depending on the results from the collection of data on biomass, length, age 
and species composition at a national level on the commercially most important fisheries. 
The catch has to consist of minimum 30% Nephrops and a maximum of 20% cod. Concerns about 
other species than the targeted fishery species, can also regulate the TACs. An example of this was 
the reduction on 10% of the Nephrops catches in the English fishery in 2001 caused by concerns 
about the declining population of the North Sea cod. The TACs were back to normal by 2004 (Evans 
et al 1994; Catchpole et al 2006a). 
Mesh size 
In Kattegat and Skagerrak the minimum inside mesh size (figure 3), when fishing for Nephrops as 
the target species, is 90 mm. However, a smaller mesh size (70-89mm) is allowed if no more than 
60% of the catch constitutes any mixture of cod, haddock, hake, plaice, witch, lemon sole, turbot, 
brill, flounder, whiting, dab, saithe and lobster (The European Parliament and The council of the 
European Union 2013).

Minimum landing size (MLS)  
Minimum landing size for Nephrops caught in ICES region III (figure 5) is 130mm in total body 
length or 40 mm carapace length (CL) (figure 4), and the minimum landing size for tales is 72 mm 
(European Council 1998). In the ICES regions I-IV (figure 5) (with the exception of region III and 
zone VIa and VIIa) the MLS is 85mm in total body length or 25mm CL and for tails 46mm. For the 
zones VIa and VIIa the MLS is 70mm in total body length or 20mm in CL and for tails 37mm 
(European Council 1998). This difference in MLS between the different areas are due to different 
size composition in the areas. Kattegat and Skagerrak have a higher number of large animals whereas 
the Farne deep have a higher proportion of Nephrops of a smaller size (Catchpole et al 2006d).

The area shown in the map in figure 6 is an important fishing ground for the Danish Nephrops 
fishery. In 2010 the Danish and the Swedish government made an agreement to protect the area to 
enhance the recovery of the cod stock there and only restricted fisheries are allowed; the area is

seasonally closed (Madsen and Valentinsson 2010). In the part of the area located in Kattegat, the 
area is closed in the period January 1st – March 31st. In the part of the area located in the Sound, the

Within this area, another region called “area 2” (Figure 6) is closed for all kind of fishery all year, 
with the exception of  trawl fishery with selective gear reducing the bycatch of cod to a minimum, in 
the period April 1st – December 31st (Ministry of Food Agriculture and Fisheries 2010). 
Another area called “area 3” (figure 6) is located in the south-eastern part of Kattegat. This area is 
permanently closed for all fisheries with no exceptions (Ministry of Food Agriculture and Fisheries 
2010).

Laws on discards 
Many laws and directives have been implemented to enhance the possibility for survival of discards. 
The catch is to be sorted immediately after the catch is removed from the gear (1999). For all 
species, for which quotas are set and can be legally landed, discarding is illegal (European 
commission 2012). In other parts of the EU, than Danish waters, some areas are closed for Nephrops 
fishery parts of the year for fishing with bottom trawl, creels or any other gear, which will encounter 
the seabed (European Council 1998). 
Discards ban / Landing obligation 
Since 1998, undersized marine organisms fished in Kattegat and Skagerrak can be kept on board and 
landed, within a limit of 10% of the total catch retained on board (European Council 1998), but in 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
2013 the European Parliament decided on a landing obligation system. All species caught, were a 
catch limit applies, have to be taken on board and retained on the fishing vessel. The catch has to be 
recorded, landed and if possible counted against the quotas (European Parliament and Council of the 
European Union 2013). All fish and crayfish, not included in the above mentioned regulation (No 
1380/2013), that are smaller than the minimum landing size is illegal to; keep on board the fishing 
vessel, reload, land, store, transport, sell or trade, and should immediately be discarded alive if 
possible (Ministry of Food Agriculture and Fisheries 2015). For the Nephrops fisheries in the North 
Sea, this is applicable from January 1st 2016 and for the other species caught, when targeting 
Nephrops, this is applicable from January 1st 2019 (European Parliament and Council of the 
European Union 2013). In Kattegat and Skagerrak, this is applicable from January 1st 2017 for the 
targeted species Nephrops, and from January 1st 2019 for other species with a catch limit in the 
Nephrops fishery (European Parliament and Council of the European Union 2013). The landing 
obligations do however not apply to species, where scientific evidence shows high discard survival 
from the gear and fishing practise (European Parliament and Council of the European Union 2013).  
9. Selective fishing gear 
Many things have already happened in improvement of decreasing the amount of unwanted bycatch, 
through regulations (described above) but also by improvement of selectivity of the gear (Catchpole 
and Revill 2008). Selectivity in trawls can be done by improving size and species selectivity of the 
trawls. Modifications made for improvement of size selectivity can target size selectivity, where the 
net can be seen as a filtration system, whereas modifications for species selectivity is based mostly 
on behavioural differences between the species. 
Codend modifications 
Smaller sized Nephrops tend to escape through the wing bases of the trawl where the larger sized 
Nephrops tends to escape through the codend. But in general, Nephrops escapes throughout the 
whole trawl, whereas fish tend to escape mostly from the codend (Briggs 1986; Briggs et al 1999). 
An increase from 90 to 120 mm mesh size could reduce the amount of by-catch retained including a 
50% reduction of  retained cod less than 40 cm, but would reduce the number of legally sized 
Nephrops (Madsen and Valentinsson 2010; Feekings et al 2012). Replacing the normally used 
diamond meshes with square- and hexagonal meshes would allow a higher escape of  by-catch 
species, such as whiting and haddock (Briggs 1986; Madsen and Valentinsson 2010). Improvement 
of selectivity in diamond mesh codends could be done by reducing the stretch of the meshes. For 
==PAGE:17==
17 
 
example by shortening the selvedge ropes or having fewer meshes in the circumference, which 
would hold the meshes more open during trawling (ICES 2004; Madsen and Valentinsson 2010).  
Sorting grids 
Sorting grids mechanically sort fish from Nephrops or shrimps, by allowing animals that fit through 
the grid panels to pass and be retained in the codend. However, it allows passage of larger fish, 
which are guided to an escape hole or window. Sorting grids can be an effective tool separating 
Nephrops from other species (e.g. cod) whose behaviour makes them stay close to the bottom during 
trawling and for improvement of size selectivity of Nephrops (Catchpole et al 2006d).  
 
Escape windows/Square mesh panels 
A section with different mesh size or type in the upper part of the net allows untargeted fish to 
escape while retaining Nephrops and targeted species. Escape windows have shown to improve 
selectivity in Nephrops fisheries, both in the North Sea, Irish Sea, Kattegat and Skagerrak (Briggs 
1992; Armstrong et al 1998; Madsen et al 1999; Revill et al 2007; Catchpole and Revill 2008; 
Madsen and Valentinsson 2010). However, placement in the right distance from the codend is 
important for the selectivity (Krag et al 2008). 
 
Cut away trawl 
This type of trawl has no upper panel above and before the ground gear, which allows fish to escape 
the trawl. As the catch behaviour of Nephrops makes them stay close to the bottom, the trawl will 
retain Nephrops as well as fish that also keep close to the seabed. The same principal is used in the 
traditional Nephrops trawls with low headline (ICES 2004; Catchpole and Revill 2007). 
10. Final remarks 
The present thesis aimed to study the discard survival of Nephrops norvegicus in Skagerrak and 
Kattegat after trawling and obtain better knowledge of the underlying factors influencing the chance 
of survival after being subject to fishing including different preconditions of the Nephrops. 
The mean survival rate for Nephrops were 20 %. However, there was a large variation between the 
different hauls, but with a general low survival (paper I). The present work indicates that discard 
survival is influenced by wide a range of factors. This study investigated different factors affecting 
the chance of survival and no specific factors were found as main contributors to the discard 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
mortality, which suggests that the chance of survival is complex and affected by combination of 
these factors.  
The best mitigating tool for reducing discard mortality would be to reduce the amount of unwanted 
bycatch and for the trawl fisheries for Nephrops, which is regarded as one of the most discarding 
fisheries (Bergmann et al 2002), a general reduction of unwanted bycatch is needed.

Paper I 
Discard survival of trawled Norway lobster (Nephrops norvegicus) in 
Kattegat and Skagerrak 
Anita Bruun Nielsen 
National Institute of Aquatic resources, Technical University of Denmark (DTU) 
Abstract 
Unwanted bycatch and the consequent discarding thereof is a big challenge in fisheries management. 
Often mortality of discarded animals exceeds the survival of these and it is therefore generally 
considered a waste of resources. The present study investigated the discard survival of Nephrops 
norvegicus after being subject to trawling and the underlying factors affecting the chance of survival, 
including predation by birds. The survival of the trawled Nephrops was assessed after 8 days of 
captive observation in tanks on land. Ten test hauls were conducted and showed a large variation in 
the survival between the hauls (0-48%). The mean discard survival between the hauls were 20 %, 
which was significantly lower than the survival of the control groups (73%) after 8 days of 
observation. The Nephrops were categorized according to damages, size, moult state, puncture of the 
concave bulging under the last pair of legs, movement after trawling and period exposed to air. None 
of the parameters showed a significant difference in the days of survival. 0% of the Nephrops 
discarded were taken by birds.  
 
Introduction 
The fishery of the burrowing crayfish Nephrops norvegicus, termed here as Nephrops, has over the 
last two decades evolved into the most valuable fishery in Europe, were landings have exceeded 
more than 60,000 tonnes per year (2010) across all countries (Ungfors et al 2013). The Nephrops 
fishery takes place all year round and is for the largest part a mixed species fishery (ICES 2004; 
Ulrich and Andersen 2004). Also in Skagerrak and Kattegat this fishery is considered the most 
valuable fishery including the bycatch of commercially valuable species (Krag et al 2008). More 
than 3,000 tonnes of Nephrops were landed in 2014 in Denmark, with a first sale value of more than 
210 
million 
DKK 
(based

==PAGE:22==

designed to exclusively catch Nephrops, to large trawls designed for catching whitefish (ICES 2004; 
Ulrich and Andersen 2004).  
Trawl fisheries for Nephrops are regarded as one of the most discarding fisheries, ranking as number 
eight among the 20 most discarding fisheries listed after fishery and as number five after gear type 
(Bergmann et al 2002b). Discarding happens when the composition of the catch brought on deck is 
different from the one expected to be sold. Unwanted animals are thrown back into the sea as a 
response to the market situation and regulations (e.g. minimal landing size). Survival of discard 
varies, but is generally exceeded by the mortality and it is therefore considered a waste of resources 
(Bergmann et al 2002b; Catchpole et al 2005; Harrington et al 2005; Broadhurst et al 2006). 
The discard composition is determined by several factors such as area, season, depth of fishing and 
fishing gear (Feekings et al 2012). Generally, the composition is constituted by undersized Nephrops, 
undersized marketable fish and a significant proportion of discards is often consisting of 
invertebrates such as whelks and starfish (Evans et al 1994; Bergmann et al 2002a; Catchpole and 
Revill 2008). Caught animals are exposed to several stresses when encountering the fishing gear, 
which can be fatal for the discarded and injured individuals when returned into the sea (Botta et al 
1987; ICES 2014b). Stressors like physical damages and air-exposure have shown to affect survival. 
First, physical damages can cause immediate death caused by crushing of the body, vital body parts 
or cause loss of body fluids such as blood (Evans et al 1994; Wileman 1999; ICES 2014b). Injuries 
incurred during fishing and physical contact from the fishing gear can lower the chances of survival; 
wounds can cause infections especially in stressed animals, which can directly cause mortality or can 
increase the risk of predation or lower chances of surviving competition with conspecifics. Finally, 
the chances of surviving also depend on which preconditions the animals are in such as moulting 
state, size and sex (Wileman 1999; ICES 2014b).  
The objectives for this discard survival assessment were an estimation of discard survival rate, 
including predation from birds, of the Nephrops fishery in Kattegat and Skagerrak. This was done by 
conducting a vitality assessment on board, assessing representative conditions, as well as a captive 
observation study of individuals in different preconditions and in different vitality states, to generate 
a damage index and an overall mean survival estimate.  
Methods 
Data collection 
In this study, a discard-survival assessment was conducted by combining two approaches: a vitality 
assessment and a captivity observation study.

Controls 
Control groups were collected in the same way as the test Nephrops. To reduce the impact from 
fishing and handling, the towing duration was shortened to 15-25 minutes (table 1) and Nephrops 
were re-immersed in less than 5 minutes. Two control groups (C3 and C4) were kept in the 
observation tanks during a period of at least 11 days. The Nephrops used for these control groups 
were survivors from previous observation studies. After a week without mortality, these individuals 
were used to investigate tank storage and transportation impacts respectively.  
Observation study 
288 Nephrops distributed in the boxes were put into 1,5 m-3 tank equipped with a biofilter, a protein 
skimmer and installed in a cooling container. The seawater temperature was 4.3-6.1 ºC and the 
salinity 33 ppt. Nephrops were not fed and mortality was checked daily. Dead individuals were 
removed and physical state was recorded (size, punctures, softness and damage category). 
Observation lasted between 8 and 41 days in total. 
 
==PAGE:25==

Damage/reflex index 
Development of a damage index and identification of the influencing factors were done by reviewing 
literature and studies, were vitality indices were used, and that were comparable to the present study 
(Harris and Ulmestrand 2004; Ridgway et al 2006a; Mehault et al 2011; ICES 2014b). Some of the 
factors used in this study have however, not been tested before. The index was used to place the 
Nephrops in one of three different damage categories; 0 (no visible damages), 1 (lightly damaged) or 
2 (heavily damaged) (table 2). In addition to damages, the Nephrops were also monitored for 
movement and punctures of the concave bulging under the last pair of walking legs (figure 2). 
Testing the impact of these punctures is to the knowledge of this study not tested before.  Movement 
were recorded on deck according to three categories: no movement (0), movement when provoked 
(1)  and movement (2). Carapace length (mm), sex, air exposure time (min) and softness of the shell 
were recorded. It was assumed that all damages recorded on deck and during the observation study 
were a consequence of trawling and handling on deck and that the Nephrops were unharmed before 
being subject to fishing.

Predation by birds 
An estimation of the number of seabirds following the vessel was made by counting birds on one 
side of the boat and time it by two. For estimation of the predation by seabirds, Nephrops were 
discarded through the discard slope and it was observed and quantified weather they were taken by 
seabirds or were sinking down.  
Data analysis 
Data was handled in Microsoft Excel (2013) and statistical analysis and figures were conducted 
using SigmaStat (13.0). Differences between groups were tested with paired t-test (two groups) or 
one-way ANOVA on ranks (three groups).  Mann Whitney U test or Kruskal-Wallis One-way 
Analysis of Variance on ranks were conducted when data was not normally distributed (Dytham 
2003).  
Analysis were conducted on the percentage Nephrops alive on the eighth trial day to have an equal 
observation period. Nephrops alive on day 8 of the observation study were regarded as survivors. 
Furthermore, a breakdown in the cooler system on June 20th resulted in an increase in water 
temperature measured to more than 20 ºC for boxes 2, 3, 4 and C1. The Nephrops that were subject 
to this temperature increase were not included in the statistical analyses.

Discussion 
Survival 
A very high variation in survival was found between the hauls, ranging between 0-48% for the test 
hauls and between 50-90% for the control hauls. There was a significant difference in the mean 
survival between the test and the control hauls, indicating that the catch and handling process did 
have and influence on the survival of the Nephrops.  
The mean survival on 20% found in this study was low, but in line with the survival rate found by 
Wileman 1999 and more recently by Ridgway et al 2006 (31% and 17-43%, respectively). Both 
experiments did observation studies, with Nephrops in tanks on land, similar to the method of this 
study. Edwards and Bennett 1980 observed a total survival of 45% of the caught Nephrops in the 
North Sea and 75 % in the Irish Sea, which is considerably higher than the survival rate found in the 
present study. However, their survival rate was estimated after only 24 hours of re-immersion 
whereas the survival rate from this study (20 %) was estimated after 8 days of re-immersion. The 
survival rate found after 24 hours in this study was 47%, which compares very well to the one found 
by Edwards and Bennet 1980, however, using this survival rate would be an overestimation, as more 
Nephrops die after 24 hours. Even though this observation was running for 8 days and the survival 
==PAGE:33==
33 
 
curve flattens out, running the experiment for a longer period might have reduced the survival rate 
even further.  
The temperature increase related to the breakdown of the cooling system in the container where the 
Nephrops were stored for observation obliged us to exclude the affected hauls from the statistical 
analyses. The survival of the excluded hauls varied from 32 to 36 %, which was above the mean 
survival for the test hauls. Including them would have increased the mean estimated survival rate. 
The methodology chosen for this study might have affected the survival rate. The survival estimates 
generated by captive observation might have been underestimated due to the effects of captivity. On 
the other hand, when excluding predation, survival estimates might have been overestimated (ICES 
2014b). One of the disadvantages of observation on land is transportation of the animals, which can 
cause stress and possibly higher mortality among the Nephrops. However, the control groups were 
significantly different from the test hauls, dead individuals were still found in the control group 
exposed to the transportation (boat and truck). 
The control groups for this study were not ideal, as the three control groups used for the analysis 
consisted of; one group (C2) fished the same way as the test hauls but with a shorter trawling 
duration, and two groups (C3 and C4) consisting of “leftover” Nephrops from previous ended 
observations. These were therefore survivors and possibly stronger individuals acclimatised to the 
conditions in the observation tanks. Ideally, one haul per fishing day with a short trawling duration 
(15 minutes) in addition to control groups for transport and observation tanks would have constituted 
a better control. However, weather conditions and low catches did not make this possible. Controls 
could also have been taken using creels as done by other studies (Ridgway et al 2006a; Mehault et al 
2011). However, creel fishery is not normally conducted in Denmark and there is therefore no 
restricted area for creel fishery in Danish waters. Considering this, the control groups in this study 
did follow the same patterns for survival as the test hauls, although it was with a significantly higher 
survival.  
Damage index  
In this study, mortality increased when damage category increased. However, the results were not 
statistic significant. It was expected to see a more explicit effect of damages on the survival. 
Ridgeway et al. 2006 found an increasing mortality with the extent of damages. All damages 
recorded in this study were assumed to be an effect of catch- and handling process, but some 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
damages (i.e. missing walking legs or claw) could have occurred before being subject to fishing (e.g. 
predation, etc). 
63 % of the Nephrops caught was recorded to have some kind of physical damage, which is in line 
with previous studies where more than 50% of Nephrops that had encountered the fishing gear had 
physical damages (Symonds and Simpson 1971; Edwards and Bennett 1980; Ridgway et al 2006a). 
This investigation was carried out on a small fishing vessel, but still had a relative high number of 
damaged Nephrops. We could expect a higher proportion of damaged individuals from bigger 
vessels, with more engine power, longer trawling time and bigger catches.  
Size 
A slightly higher percentage of the Nephrops above MLS survived, compared with the Nephrops 
under MLS, but no significant difference was found. The vast majority of Nephrops caught in this 
study had a carapace length close to the MLS. This means that most individuals were closer to 
individuals in the other size group than in their own size group, in terms of CL.  
Previous studies shows the same pattern result regarding size and damages. Ridgway et al. 2006 
found that size of the Nephrops influenced the susceptibility for damages. The larger individuals 
with a carapace length greater than 45mm seemed to be less susceptible to damage and thereby have 
a higher chance of survival. Mehault et al 2011 found that the larger the individuals, the lower their 
chance of being healthy, all though this was only investigated for individuals with a CL between 21 
and 32mm.  
Hardness 
The jelly individuals showed a lower survival than the soft and the hard individuals, but no 
significant difference was found between the three. In this study, the vast majority of the Nephrops 
had a hard shell (72%) which could be due to the season of fishing. Ridgeway et al 2006 collected 
data in May and September and found a very high amount of soft individuals (86.1 – 96.8 %) and a 
very low amount of soft individuals (0.8 – 1.9%), respectively. Data collection for this study was 
conducted in from May to end June, with only one trip in May and the rest in end June. Furthermore, 
the fishing for this study was conducted in Kattegat and Ridgeway et al 2006 conducted their data 
collection in the Clyde sea and differences could be affected by the area. The control groups had no 
“jelly” individuals and may not have been representative for this test.  
 
 
==PAGE:35==
35 
 
Punctures 
A higher percentage of unpunctured individuals than punctured individuals survived but there was no 
significant difference in both control and test groups. A puncture of the concave bulging under the 
last pair of walking legs could possibly reveal a puncture of the shell in another part of the body. 
Movement air exposure 
This study found no significant differences in survival between the individuals in the different time 
interval. However, the two groups that had been exposed to air for the longest period showed the 
lowest survival, which is correlated by the study carried by Ridgway in 2006c where they found that 
time of air-exposure had an effect on survival. The longer the Nephrops stayed out of the water the 
higher the mortality was.  
Ridgway et al 2006c recorded mortality in Nephrops exposed to air in up to 12 hours, divided into 
time intervals on several hours. In this study commercial practice were followed and the Nephrops 
were exposed to air for a maximum period of 1h24m, and they were divided in to intervals on 10 
minutes. 
Predation by seabirds 
In this study none of the discarded Nephrops were taken by seabirds (0% predation by birds). Evans 
et al 1994, found that 70 % of the discarded Nephrops were taken by birds. Differences in the 
percentage taken could be due to differences in methods. Evans et al 1994 were throwing Nephrops 
in groups of five overboard, whereas in the present investigation, Nephrops were discarded one at a 
time down the discard slope. 
Conclusion 
The mean survival rate for Nephrops were 20 %, however there was a large variation between the 
different hauls, but with a general low survival (paper I). The present work indicates that discard 
survival is influenced by a range of factors. This study investigated different factors affecting the 
chance of survival no specific factors were found as main contributors to the discard mortality, which 
suggests that the chance of survival is complex and affected by combination of these factors.
