Abstract
Security Assessment is among the most fundamental engineering problems of power system
operation.
An increasing complexity due to the renewable paradigm in power systems ren-
ders traditional approaches ineﬀective owing to their severe computational demands. In recent
years, deep learning has emerged as a promising alternative to the security assessment problem,
but deployment in real-life operation is limited by overconﬁdent predictions and little inter-
pretability. In this thesis, these two orthogornal challenges are adressed. The network models
of feed-forward Neural Networks, Bayesian Neural Networks, Monte Carlo Dropout models,
non-Bayesian Deep Ensembles are trained and assesed for their uncertainty estimation capabil-
ities. It is shown that Bayesian Neural Networks are less overconﬁdent and knows what they
know in contrast to previous power system security assessment networks. Further, it is shown
that by rejecting a fraction of the processed samples, high performance metrics can be achieved.
In addition, attribution maps that pinpoint what is relevant for a given prediction are exam-
ined as a tool for insight in the inner rationale of the models. The attribution map methods
of Gradients, Gradients Times Input, Layerwise Relevancy Propagation, Integrated Gradients,
Expected Gradients and DeepLIFT are used with diﬀerent baselines for understanding the net-
works and ensuring that they utilize reasonable input-output correlations. It is shown that the
methods generally produce explanations with high variability that are well aligned with human
domain expertise and intuition and that they provide valuable insights in both local and global
prediction strategies of the networks.

1.
Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
Machine learning systems have in recent years excelled in numerous ﬁelds including outper-
forming humans in computer vision tasks [1, 2], natural language processing [3], speech recog-
nition [4], materials science [5], drug discovery [6], genomics [7, 8, 9], medical diagnosis [10],
recommender-systems [11] and marketing and sales [12]. Widely considered one of machine
learning’s most signiﬁcant feats, the system of Alpha Go [13] beat the 18-time world-champion
Lee Seedol in the highly complex game of Go through unconventional strategies and creative
new moves in 2016. Its successor, AlphaGo Zero [14] achieved even higher performance and
was solely trained by playing against itself. In addition, recent advances have shown signiﬁcant
potential for the use of machine learning for operation and control of electric power systems,
including the ﬁeld of Power system security assessment [15, 16, 17]: The worldwide decarboniza-
tion of energy and transport systems leads to increased complexity and uncertainty in power
system operation, which can be adressed through the use of machine learning.
This plethora of succeses can largely be attributed to advances within deep learning, a sub-
stantial growth of accessible data and increasing computational power [18]. Deep learning is
composed of multiple processing layers, which learn representations of data with increasing lev-
els of abstraction. However, despite their predictive power, the deployment and development of
deep learning systems is hindered in safety-critical applications [19]. One of the main challenges,
deep learning methods face in these domains is the lack of transparency and explainability -
deep learning modalities are widely considered black boxes, which is disqualifying in sensitive
areas, where limiting the possibility of improper, non-robust and unsafe decisions and actions is
crucial [20]. Explainable AI (XAI) is concerned with ’whitening’ of the black box predictions.
This is done through exposing the decision-making process of the machine learning models to
humans in a comprehensible manner [20]. This allows for inferring novel patterns, that the non-
linear system has learned and for assesing the validity and thus, generalizability of the learned
prediction strategies. One line of interpretability is dedicated to assessing what speciﬁc input
features that led to the observed predictions in the output through so-called attribution maps.
An additional, separate goal for deep learning deployment in safety-critical areas is well-calibrated
uncertainty estimations to accompany their predictions [21]. Being able to trust the uncertainty
estimations of the model allows for utilizing alternative approaches when the deep learning sys-
tems are in doubt.
This thesis focuses on these two orthogonal goals, interpretability and
uncertainty estimation, using attribution map models along with Bayesian Neural Networks

(BNN’s) and it does so using power security assessment as guiding example.
1.2
Research Directions
Power system security assessment is concerned with evaluating the safety of possible future oper-
ating points for a power network and is used in online settings to foresee and avoid catastrophic
system failures accompanied by enormeous economical and social costs [22]. Power networks
are highly regulated systems, which must meet varying demands of electricity with a complex
production system [23]. Operating such complex networks safely, balancing the ﬂow from pro-
duction to demand without violating any power ﬂow limits (e.g. transmisison line overheating)
or destroying any equipment requires real-time system monitoring, fast contingency analysis
and corrective control [24]. Contingency analysis denotes real-time screening, simulating and
quantifying possible future problems such as unplanned failures of small parts of the power sys-
tem (e.g. a transmission line) or the loss/failure of individual equipment such as a generator or
transformer. A secure operating point needs to satisfy both time-independent static and time-
dependent dynamic security criterias. For this thesis, the focus is on Static Security Assessment,
i.e. the steady state following a contingency, and the so-called ”N-1” security criterion is used
as a binary class for a given operating point. This implies that the network security should be
guaranteed if one component fails. Conventional static contingency analysis on contemporary
power systems only takes tens of seconds to compute [16], however the increasing complexity in
the power systems are challenging the traditional approach. Power systems in the last decade
have undergone a substantial transformation with (a) liberalization of the electricity markets
[24], (b) decarbonization of electricity and transportation systems, where massive integration of
intrinsically stochastic renewable energy sources such as wind and solar increases the number
of possible system states and (c) increased stochastic loads due to e.g. electric vehicles. This
intermittent distributed stochastic generation and loading results in the power systems being
operated increasingly closer to their limits. It is speculated that these developments will render
classical simulations ineﬀective in the future due to their inherent computational burden [16, 17].
Arteaga et al. reported achieving more than 255x acceleration [15] and Du et al. have reported
achieving more than 100x acceleration for N-1 contingency screening using Deep Learning [16]
compared to traditional approaches.
While literature on machine learning in power system
security assessment has increased substantially in recent years, real-life adaption is challenged
by the risk aversion of the ﬁeld. In a recent survey of machine learning in power systems, it is
argued, that there is a need to convince the ﬁeld experts that the machine learning methods
are actually eﬃcient and reliable and it is argued that ”...interpretability of the machine-learnt
models is something that could not be neglected in future research”[17]. This thesis makes several
contributions towards this goal.

1.4
Structure of the thesis
The contributions are summarized as follows:
(1) Database building: Firstly, a database is built for a 9-bus-system [25] from the MAT-
POWER package [26] and a 118-bus-system inherent to the IEEE Power Grid Library [27].
Database building through simulations is often used in the academic litterature as historical
data is highly unavailable [17]. This might create substantial distribution shifts from historical
operating points, but also has the advantage of training near the security boundaries and thus
enriching the training set with observations that would rarely be encountered in real-life op-
eration [28]. The operating points are sampled with procedures, which ensure that both most
of the operating space is covered, but also that the database is class-balanced and resembles
conditions encountered in real-world operation. The security statusses of these observations are
then determined through power ﬂow (PF) solvers.
(2) Training deep uncertainty estimators: Secondly, simple feed-forward networks, MC-dropout
models [29], Bayesian Neural Networks [30] and non-Bayesian Deep Ensembles [31] are imple-
mented, hyperparameter-optimized through random search [32] and trained to binary classify
N-1 security. Furthermore, the calibration characteristics of these methods are analyzed along
with Platt temperature scaling improvements [33] of the methods. Misclassiﬁcation Detection
and Out-of-Distribution Detection capabilities of the models are assessed. It is found, that for
bigger systems, the ensemble methods outperform feed-forward Neural Networks.
(3) Attribution maps: Thirdly, the attribution map methods of Gradient [34], Gradient * Input
[35], ε-Layerwise Relevancy Propagation (ε-LRP) [36], Integrated Gradients (IG) [37], Expected
Gradients (EG) [38], DeepLIFT [39] are implemented and used to explain the individual predic-
tions of the neural networks and the overall, marginal eﬀects of the diﬀerent features. Further,
a novel explainability metric is introduced for the attribution maps and it is found that the
attribution maps align well with human domain expertise, but also that the maps suﬀer from
high variability.

the resulting datasets and covers implementation details, hyperparameter-tuning and training
of the networks. Section 4 uses these networks and analyzes classiﬁcation performance, uncer-

2.1
Power systems
This section is divided into a brief description of the equations governing operation of power
systems, a coverage of stability concepts and lastly machine learning within power systems with
special emphasis on database building in the ﬁeld.
2.1.1
Alternating Current Power ﬂow and Optimal Power Flow formulations
Most power systems today are operated using three-phase alternating current (AC) [41]. A
power system can roughly be divided into the generation system, the transmission network, the
load centers and their corresponding distribution systems [42]. The generation system supplies
high-voltage power through the transmission network to the load centers.
The distribution
system then lowers the voltage of the power and feeds it to nearby homes and industries. This
thesis is concerned with the high-voltage part of a power system and transmission from genera-
tion centers to load centers over transmission lines, using the so-called π-model of transmission
lines [41] under an assumption of steady-state operation. Power systems are predominantly in
steady state operation or in dynamical states that with suﬃcient accuracy can be regarded as
such. During most of operation, time-dependent variations of the system exist but are under
normal conditions so small, that an algebraic model of the system is justiﬁed. For some events,
dynamic phenomena can be extremely severe for the operation of the power system and this is
especially prevalent for some types of security assessment [43], but such considerations are out
of the scope of this thesis.
The power system is often represented as a graph, where each node, called a bus, represents the
connection of the transmission system with either a generator, a load center or represents the
reference bus (also called a slack bus), see ﬁgure 2.1. Each bus has 4 variables associated with
it: Active power P, reactive power Q, voltage magnitude |V | and voltage phase angle θ.
Active power P is the actual outcomes of the bus as it is intuitively understood. An active
power of 80W for a load bus means, that the bus actually absorbs 80 joules per second and an
active power of 80W for a generator bus means, that the bus actually generates 80 joules per
second. Reactive power Q doesn’t have an equally intuitive meaning, but is power that ﬂows
back and forth in the system, absorbed by inductive or capacitive component of the load, not

2.1.2
Stability and reliability
Power systems stability is deﬁned as the ability to regain a state of operating equilibrium after
being subjected to physical disturbance with most system variables bounded [44]. Power system
stability has been recognized as a critical issue since the 1920s as major blackouts have high-
lighted the need for operating power grids in a stable manner [44]. The stability of a system
depends on both the original operation point and the disturbance from this operation point and
is thus the property of the motion around an equilibrium point, or in some cases an equilibrium
cycle [44]. Several diﬀerent stability criteria exist, characterizing stability to diﬀerent types of
pertubations. At an equilibrium set, a power system may be stable for one physical disturbance
and unstable for another, and it is impractical to design power systems that are stable to all
types of possible disturbances. For this reason, a subset of the possible operation pertubations,
called contingencies, are selected and stability is calculated with relation to these. Following
a disturbance, if the power system is stable, it will reach a new equilibrium state with system
integrity preserved [44]. For real-time operation and planning, a constant evaluation of contin-
gencies is needed.
Stability criteria are in general classiﬁed as dynamical or static. Dynamic security characterizes
the ability to complete the transition to a postcontingent equilibrium state. Static security
characterizes the postcontingent state with relation to the compliance with permanent capabil-
ities of the system components. For this, the power ﬂow calculations as introduced in equations
(2.4)-(2.4) are used. The contingencies considered is the removal of buses and the criterion con-
sidered is N-1 stability. If the system still fulﬁlls the constraints equation (2.6)-(2.11) following
removal of any one bus, it is N-1 stable. In this thesis, focus is on using neural networks to
rapidly screen a large number of possible operating conditions with respect to the compliance
to the N-1 security criterion

2.1.4
Database building
Approaches and challenges
Large datasets of operating points as inputs and stability classiﬁcations or regressive state
quantities as targets are needed for training and validating security assessment machine learn-
ing models. Database building through simulations is often used in the power systems literature
as (1) historical data is highly unavailable [17] and (2) historical data is not suﬃcient to train
models, as information related to the security boundaries or insecure regions is highly under-
represented [45]. In this regard, some authors argue that the lack of unstable historical data
necessitates simulation [45]. Nevertheless, substantial work has tried to infer the underlying
data model from historical data [46, 47].
Konstantelos et al. argues, that historical data constitutes a natural starting point for ensuring
good generalization properties, as the training data is in vicinity of instances to be classiﬁed in
the future [46]. Further, Duchesne et al. argues that the representativity of the database has
big impact on the eﬀectiveness of the deep learning approach and that simulating the database
might create substantial distribution shifts from historical operating points [17].
Sun et al.
showed that historical power systens data among other properties exhibited (a) non-Gaussian
marginal probabilities and (b) non-linear, intricate covariance structures [47]. They proposed
an R-vine copula based model to represent and sample historical data. Konstantelos et al., in a
likewise fashion, adopted the C-vine pair-copula decomposition scheme to ﬁt and sample large
datasets [46]. In this thesis, overall load and solar energy distributions for a residential area
in Chicago in 2010 are used as historical data [48, 49] and as a starting point for an elaborate
sampling scheme, as seen in section 3.1. The data consist of hourly data for the extent of a
year, a total of 8,760 observations.
Determining the secure region of a power system is an NP-hard problem [45]. Simple brute-
force discretization of the input space and subsequent simulation on each operating point is
computationally intractable. For the NESTA 162 bus system, with discretization intervals of
1MW, a brute force discretization would require stability analysis of ≈1030 points within the
constraint feasibility hypercube [45]. In addition, most of the operating points in the constraint
9
==PAGE:16==
feasibility hypercube are N-1 unstable: The insecure space is several orders of magnitudes larger
than the secure space [28]. This can render retrieving an adequate number of secure points very
challenging [28, 45].
Thams et al.[45] characterizes two main challenges related to simulation database creation:
Creating databases, that are (a) information rich and (b) not too large. A high information
content is understood as the set being in the vicinity of the security boundary.
Venzke et
al.[28] extends the notion of a good database to accurately represent the decision boundary - the
sampled points should not only be in the vicinity, but should also cover most of the extent of the
decision boundaries. This thesis seeks to use database building principles, that addresses the
challenges described above. It uses Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) for small power systems
to cover the entire operating space, as the constraint-feasible hypercube is largely class-balanced
for low dimensionality. For the larger power systems, the latin hypercube samples are combined
with a Gaussian mixture model that is ﬁtted to secure, optimal operating points.

2.2.4
Optimization methods
The loss surface
Most modern optimization algorithms for minimizing the error functions of neural networks
build upon some variation of simple gradient descent. The nonlinearity of the mappings causes
the error functions L(w) to be highly non-convex. In addition substantial weight-space sym-
metries exist, meaning that multiple distinct choice of weights give rise to the exact same
input-output mapping [52]. Thirdly, for any rectiﬁed linear unit, if all incoming weights and
biases are scaled by α and all outgoing weights and biases are scaled by 1/α, the input-output
mapping of the unit remains the same. That is, every local minimum lies on a high-dimensional
hyperbola of equivalent local minima [56]. These intuitions led to forsaking of neural networks
by the machine learning community during the 1990’s as it was simply thought that learning the
suitable representations was infeasible as it was imagined that the optimizer would get trapped
in a local minimum [18]. However, theoretical and practical results point towards the high-
dimensional weight landscape having a large number of saddle points with very similar values
of the objective function [63, 64]. In practice, regardless of initial conditions of the solver, the
solutions are often of very similar quality [18]. It remains an active area of research whether
networks of practical interest have many encounterable local minima with high costs, but a
substantial body of work points towards that for suﬃciently large networks, most local minima
have low cost function values and that well-performing networks ﬁnd a low but not minimal
cost [56, 64, 63, 65].

larger batches give a more accurate estimate of the gradient while smaller batches give quicker
step evaluations. Orthogonal to these considerations, it has long been known that having very
small batches have a regularizing eﬀect due to the added estimation noise of exact gradient [66].
One signiﬁcant diﬀerence from general optimization is the stopping criteria. For general non-
convex optimization, the algorithm stops when a local minimizer has been found. Often in deep
learning, early stopping is used as a convergence criteria - when the validation error begins to
consistently rise, it is taken as a sign of overﬁtting and the algorithm is stopped. However, the
intuition of early stopping and overﬁtting in neural networks have very recently been challenged
by the double descent phenomena [62].
Extensions: Moments, adaptive learning rates and adam
Learning using stochastic gradient descent can be slow [56]. With noisy gradients or small but
consistent gradients, the method of momentum has been designed to accelerate training [67].
The idea is to use ’velocity’ as an update step, where the velocity is an exponentially decaying
average of the negative gradients. This captures general tendencies of the slope and accumulate
the gradient of the last few iterations, so that big steps can be taken even in high-dimensional
valleys [56]. Another extension is the use of adaptive learning rates, that changes along the
trainig instead of having one ﬁxed learning rate. This allows for taking small steps when it is
necessary and big steps when possible. The method of AdaGrad [68] adapts the learning rate
by scaling them inversely proportional to the square root of the sum of the historical squared
values of the gradient. RSMSProp modiﬁes AdaGrad by only accumulating the gradients in an
exponentially weighted moving average, so that instead of using the entire history of squared
gradients, only the last seen history of the learning trajectory structures are used [56]. The
method of Adam [69] combines the ideas of momentum and RMSProp with a few important
distinctions. Momentum is incorperated as an estimate of the ﬁrst-order moment of the gradient
and momentum is applied to the rescaled gradients. Secondly, Adam incorperates some forms

2.2.5
Regularization in deep learning
Weight decay as regularization technique was introduced in equation (2.27) and is one instance
of a broader class of parameter norm penalty regularization techniques [56]. Other popular
deep learning regularization techniques include dropout, data augmentation, early stopping,
noise injection and parameter sharing [56]. Weight decay, dropout and early stopping are the
regularization methods utilized in this thesis.
Dropout
Dropout was introduced to prevent co-adaptions, where feature detectors only are helpful in
context of several other speciﬁc feature detectors [71]. It does so by masking hidden units at
random at each forward and backward pass, forcing the neurons to learn robust representations
and forcing the network to rely population behavior [72]. Masking the hidden units can be
seen as injecting highly intelligent information destruction [56].
As an example, for object
detection if a neuron has learned a hidden unit, that is a grass detector and that unit is removed,
then the network has to learn to utilize the other neurons for making accurate predictions
- this rougly corresponds to an intelligent data augmentation, where grass is removed from
the image and the classiﬁer has to learn to be somewhat invariant under this transformation.
Dropout can also be motivated as an eﬃcient ensemble estimator. By removing nonoutput units
from an underlying base network, an ensemble consisting of all such subnetworks is trained
[56]. In practice, this is done by removing each neuron stochastically at each pass with pre-
deﬁned probability p(l) ∈[0, 0.5] at layer l. This acts as a very eﬃcient model averaging [71],
where instead of training several diﬀerent neural networks with each their hyperparameters and
architechtures, a collection of 2n thinned networks with extensive weight sharing is trained and
contained in one single network [73] with n neurons.
At test time, rather than arithmetically averaging the predictions of these exponentially many
thinned models (which is intractable in practice), the single neural network is used without
dropout and with scaled-down versions of the trained weights W(l)
scaled down = p(l)W(l). This is
called the weight scaling rule and the ensemble prediction using it is an approximation to the
geometric ensemble mean rather than the arithmetic one [73]. There is no theoretical argument
for the accuracy of this inference rule in deep nonlinear networks, but empirically it works well
[56, 74].
When introduced, dropout was found to be more eﬀective than other inexpensive
regularizers such as weight decay, ﬁlter norm constraints and sparse activity regularization [73].
Further, it has been concluded that dropout oﬀers additional improvements to generalization
error beyond those obtained by ensembles of independent models [74].

2.3
Uncertainty estimation
2.3.1
Motivation
As neural networks have become essential components in larger decision making pipelines [33],
the need for representing model uncertainty is of crucial importance [77]. This is especially
prevalent as standard deep learning models do not capture model uncertainty and tend to
make overconﬁdent predictions [33] that can be harmful or oﬀensive [78].
High-conﬁdence
predictions are often produced by softmax non-linearities, because the probabilities here are
20
==PAGE:27==
computed with the fast-growing exponential function [79]. Many aspects of learning crucially
depend on a probabilistic representation of uncertainty [77]. In May 2016, there was the ﬁrst
fatality from an assisted driving system, where the perception system mistook the white side
of a trailer for a bright sky, and if a high level of uncertainty could have been assigned to this
disatrous prediction, the system might have been able to make better decisions [21]. With model
uncertainty, uncertain inputs and special cases can be treated expliticly [29]. For instance, in
automated health care, control should ideally be passed to human experts when the conﬁdence of
a diagnosis classiﬁer is low [80]. Likewise, for the power system security assessment considered in
this thesis, if the conﬁdence of the security status of an operating point is low, more sophisticated
and computationally demanding computations should be conducted there. The harmful eﬀects
of overconﬁdent predictions and badly calibrated models in general was recently discussed [78].

Out-Of-Distribution and In-Distribution uncertainty
Out-Of-Distribution detection is concerned with letting the machine learning models detect
dataset shift. It has been observed, that when machine learning models are deployed in practice,
the distribution over observed data might shift and eventually be very diﬀerent from the original
training data distribution [84].
If a network is trained on one dataset and evaluated on a
completely diﬀerent dataset, then it should ideally output low conﬁdences - it should know
what it knows [31]. One severe case in power system security assessment would be, if the deep
learning system has been trained on a combination of simulated and historical data and a new
operating point very far from these distribution is being deemed secure with high conﬁdence,
while it in reality is insecure. This could lead to catastrophical operation. In this case, an ideal
23
==PAGE:30==
model would output high uncertainty, so that the point could be processed reliably with the
more slow PF solvers.
It is uncommon to see a uniform distribution outputted for out-of-distribution examples [79].
Random noise fed to a standard MNIST classiﬁer has been observed to give prediction conﬁdence
of ˆp = 91% [79]. Even though the conﬁdence viewed in isolation can be misleading, there are
often diﬀerences between in-distribution and out-of-distribution probabilities, but also between
predictions classiﬁed correctly and errorneously. Therefore, capturing the behavior of correct
in- or out samples are Sometimes suﬃcient to detect erroneous predictions or OOD samples
[79], but not necessarily to assess them in a well-calibrated manner.
The calibration properties of distribution shift in computer vision and text models were recently
assessed, and it was concluded that along with accuracy, the quality of uncertainty consistently
degrades with increasing dataset shift regardless of deep learning model [84].
In addition,
it was found that models that are well-calibrated on their training distribution are not well-
calibrated with covariate shift [84]. In this thesis, the correlation between predictive uncertainty
and dataset shift and the correlation between predictive uncertainty and misclassiﬁcation are
assessed in section 4.
2.3.3
Decomposition of uncertainty
There are many forms of uncertainty [77]. At the lowest level, uncertainty is introduced from
noise inherent in the observations such as measurement noise. At a middle level, there are un-
certainty about the model parameters. At the highest level, there is often uncertainty about the
general structure of the model. The probabilistic approach to machine learning uses probability
theory to represent all types of uncertainty [77].
The noise stemming from the noise in the observations is called aleatoric uncertainty. Uncer-
tainty stemming from ignorance about which model created our collected data is called epistemic
uncertainty [21]. Epistemic uncertainty disappears in the limit of inﬁnite data, whereas aleatoric
uncertainty does not. Epistemic uncertainty is required for detecting out-of-data observations,
which is exceedingly important in safety-critical applications. Bayesian and ensemble methods
generally estimate the epistemic uncertainty [85, 84], whereas probabilistic neural networks such
as mixture density networks capture aleatoric uncertainty.
The input-output-mapping considered in this thesis is completely deterministic without mea-
surement noise, so theoretically the aleatoric uncertainty should be negligible. In real-life ap-
plication, there exist measurement noise in the system, but the workﬂow imagined is, that a
state estimation system would be dedicated to this task. The problem at hand in this thesis is,
given a state, is it unsafe or not and in that case, the aleatoric uncertainty is zero.

Non-Bayesian Deep Ensembles
An alternative to a Bayesian treatment of neural networks was introduced by Lakshminarayanan
et al. [31] and consists of simply training an ensemble of normal neural networks and averaging
their predicitons. It has been noted, that the quality of BNNs exigently depends on (a) the
degree of approximation in the posterior distribution and (b) the appropriateness of the priors,
as priors of convenience (such as the diagonal Gaussian introduced in section 2.3.4) can lead to
unreasonable predictive uncertainties [31]. Further, BNNs have proved harder to implement and
slower to train. Lastly, recent work has shown that the Bayes posterior actually can perform
worse than point estimates obtained through stochastic gradient descent [94].
Non-Bayesian Deep Ensembles takes point of departure in ensemblic motivation, where it is
argued that while Bayesian Model Averaging performs soft model selection within the hypothesis
class, ensembles perform model combination, which can be expected to perform better when the
true model does not lie in the Bayesian hypothesis class [31]. Model combination works by
enriching the hypothesis space [95, 96]. The non-Bayesian Deep Ensemble slightly changes the
training pipeline in three steps
1. Using a proper scoring rule as training criterion
2. Training an ensemble of M networks independently of each other
3. (Optional) Use adversarial training to smooth the predictive distribution

2.4
Interpretability
This section is organized as follows: First, the need for interpretability is motivated and some
general distinctions are made. This is followed by the theory of attribution methods, what
constitutes explanations and their relation to baselines.
Two types of approaches to attri-
bution maps are covered: pertubation-based methods and gradient-based methods. For the
gradient-based methods, diﬀerent explanation axioms are covered and the methods of Inte-
grated Gradients, Expected Gradients, ϵ-LRP and DeepLIFT are covered. This is followed by
an overview of evaluation of attribution map methods.
2.4.1
Motivation
The focus on the interpretability and explainability of complex machine learning systems have
increased in recent years [99, 100], especially after the deep learning community was shocked
by the lack of robustness that adversarial attack research unveiled [101]. Comprehending the
reasoning of the machine learning systems can be motivated on diﬀerent levels. Firstly, by
understanding the complex, nonlinear models, new knowledge can be generated [19], as has
been seen within the game of Alpha Go [24], within previously unknown associations between
genes and diseases [102] or within chemical compounds and material properties [103]. In these
31
==PAGE:38==
cases, the explanation can prove even more valuable than the prediction itself [19]. Secondly,
explaining the decisions made can help unveil so-called ’Clever Hans’ predictors [20], where the
systems utilize spurious correlations between input and output in the training and testing set,
but where the correlations do not survive the distribution shift to real life applications. It has
been showed that competition-winning convolutional neural networks recognized boats by the
presence of water, trains by the presence of rails and horses by the presense of water marks [19],
which clearly does not extend to real life applications. Identifying and ﬁxing such ’Clever Hans’
predictors creates more robust ML applications. Lastly, important motivating criteria can be
(1) Safety, (2) Nondiscrimation against safe class such as race or gender, and (3) the right to
explanation [100]. The right to explanation was introduced by EU’s General Data Protection
Regulation (GDPR) and is concerned with automated individual decision making and proﬁling,
where an individual concerned with the calculation of matters such as credit scores and insur-
ance costs have a right to be given an explanation for the algorithms output. In this regard,
non-explainable machine learning models are de facto banned in some domains [104].
With the need motivated as above, it is important to note that a lot of applications do not have
an intrinsic need for interpretability. This is especially true when (1) There are no signiﬁcant
consequences for unacceptable results or (2) The problem is suﬃciently validated in real appli-
cations, so we trust the (imperfect) systems [100]. Both these points are strongly violated in
the domain of Power System Security Assessment [20]. For Power System Security Assessment,
interpretability would also help convince the ﬁeld that data-driven approaches are eﬃcient and
reliable [17]. In addition, explainability can help detect improper biases in the model or the
data and for this reason, it is thought that explanations are likely to become an important part
of the training and validation process of future AI models [19].
2.4.2
Distinctions and overall methods for interpretability
An important dimension to interpretability is the scope of the method. Global interpretability
yields general comprehension for how an overall system operates or for which general input
features are captured and how they are transformed [104]. Global interpretability could be con-
cerned with explaining the overall learned representations or the overall prediction strategies.
In contrast, local interpretability is concerned with explaining individual predictions on a case
by case basis. Global interpretability is very useful to capture general improper biases, but it is
very hard to achieve as the number of interacting parameters grows [104], so most research has
focused on local interpretability, especially attribution methods [105, 106, 104, 39, 38, 36, 37].
Attribution methods take a given predicted class probability or activation and decomposes the
output into relevancies, sometimes called attribution values or contributions, in the input space
(or, for some methods, in the hidden neuron spaces). Often these relevancies are signed to
account for their contribution direction to the output - did certain pixels in an image classiﬁ-
cation problem count towards or against a given class? Attribution maps are covered in detail
in section 2.4.3 and is the main interpretability methodology used in this project. Other in-
terpretability methods include concept testing/extraction [107, 108], distillation [109, 110] and
example inﬂuence/matching [111, 112, 113].

Facts and foils - Baselines and Shapley values
It has been emphasized that humans assign blame to a cause by comparing the outcomes of a
process including or not including such cause [104] - human attribution relies on counter-factual
conditions. Instead of asking why some event P happened, humans ask why P happened instead
of some (often implicit) event Q [114]. P, the target event, is referred to as the fact and the
contrast Q is referred to as the foil . Given the question ”Why did Gary smash his plate?”,
the implicit foil is ”instead of not smashing his plate”, which is an entire class of states. This
also pinpoints that explanations sought are often for surprising facts in contrast to expected

explaining surprising decisions in contrast to expected ones in a mathematical manner. This

often is used as contrast. Sundarajan et al. argues, that natural baselines exist in the input
space, where predictions are ’neutral’ [37]. Baselines are used in most attribution map methods
and can be motivated through the concept of Shapley values.

which is infeasible for more than 20-25 features [106] and for this reason, substantial research
has propsosed sampling-based methods to approximate the Shapley values [117, 118, 106].

for many attribution methods the canonical choice [104], which translates to the mean of the
training set, if the data has been zero-meaned prior to any modeling. The zero baseline is also
assumed for several attribution map methods, that do not allow the explainee to explicitly state
the baseline [105]. For image classiﬁcaiton, the zero baseline is often considered an uninformative

baseline, which looks like an ’average instance’. In addition, it is often assumed that using the

in classiﬁcation settings with many classes, however for binary classiﬁcation, uninformative
baselines are not well-deﬁned.
In such cases, it can be useful to select baselines with high
information content to highlight the most important feature attributions that resulted in an
outcome diﬀerence [114]. An example from credit scoring applications is choosing a candidate
with a ”perfect credit” as a baseline and then all deducted credit scores are explained relative
to this candidate such ”short credit history” or ”many defalt loans” [114].
For the binary

covered in section 3.1.1. A substantial assessment of the eﬀect of the baseline was recently
proposed [116].

2.4.7
Evaluation of interpretability
Unlike performance metrics such as accuracy, the interpretability criteria cannot often be com-
pletely quantiﬁed and there is little consensus on what interpretability even is and how to
evaluate it [100]. The axiomatic approach to attribution maps states intuitive, desirable prop-
erties, and deviation from these can be quaniﬁed to some extent. As an example, Montavon et
al. used deviation from explanation continuity as a metric of performance [123]. However, it is
unclear how the diﬀerent axioms should compare against each other and how good theoretical
properties translate into actual representations of the inner reasoning of the DNNs.
Non-axiomatic attribution method evaluation generally falls into four categories [124]: 1) mea-
suring sensitivity of explanations to model and input perturbations, 2) inferring correctness of
explanation from accuracy or conﬁdence drop upon feature removal (so-called ablation tests),
3) evaluating explanations in a controlled setting with some knowledge of feature importance,
and 4) evaluating explanations based on human visual assessment [124]. In this thesis, some
variations of evaluation category 2 and 3 are used. As the attribution maps produced are not
41
==PAGE:48==
easily visualized, category 4 is not applicable for power system security assesment.
Human evaluation
Evaluation of diﬀerent attribution map methods is often based upon human studies which
assess whether the rankings are meaningful to humans [125]. Diﬀerent attribution methods
can produce wildly diﬀerent explanations given the same input and the same model, so the
explanations that stay true to expert knowledge or plain human intuition about which features
are actually important are often used as a qualitative assessment. In this thesis, we carefully
pertube observations from classiﬁcation of one class to the other with one feature. In this way,
it is known that this feature resulted in the observed class and how the diﬀerent methodologies
then attribute this feature is used as a qualitative evaluation.
One should be careful using human intuition as a guideline for interpretability evaluation. The
recent schock that the deep learning community endured with crafted adversarial examples that
are hardly distingishible from originals [101] showed, that the deep neural networks are much
more sensitive to small pixel intensity variations than the human brain. It has therefore been
argued, that it is unreasonable to expect the image recognition tasks to be wildly diﬀerent, but
the explanations to be similar and not encompass this diﬀerence [104].

4.2.2
Misclassiﬁcation detection
Hopefully, the models are more uncertain about their misclassiﬁed predictions, such that cap-
turing prediction probability statistics about correct or in-sample examples is often suﬃcient
for detecting whether an example is in error or abnormal, even though the prediction proba-
bility viewed in isolation can be misleading [79]. By deﬁning an uncertainty threshold, such
error detection would allow for improving classiﬁcation metrics at the expense of not using the
neural network on a small subset of the data. In the case of power systems security assess-
ment, uncertain predictions could be sent to full PF calculators, sacriﬁcing a small amount of
computational eﬃciency for higher accuracy. By ordering the test set predictions according to
their uncertainties, removing the most uncertain predictions one at a time and observing how
the accuracy, true postive rate and false positive rate varies with the removal, one would be
able to set up an appropriate uncertainty threshold and send all observations that exceeds this
threshold to further processing. In this thesis, the ordering is tried for two metrics: Uncertainty
entropy, equation (2.44), and ensemble disagreement, equation (2.70). For the feed-forward NN,
the metric of ensemble disagreement is not deﬁned and only the entropy-measure is used. The
performance metrics’ variation with the threshold can be seen in ﬁgure 4.7 for the 9-bus system
and ﬁgure 4.8 for the 118-bus system.
For the well-performing networks of the 9-bus system, it

observations. The observations are ordered according to entropic uncertainty (solid lines) and
ensemble disagreement (dashed lines)
can be seen, that a threshold of the 2.5%-percentile of the uncertainty measurements eﬀectively
gives accuracies of 100%, true positive rates of 100% and false positive rates of 0%. That is,
the system can classify quickly and perfectly on 97.5% of operation space and it knows, when
slower but accurate PF solvers should be utilized. This is despite only having been trained for
1,000 epochs. For the 118-bus system, a similar phenomenon is observed: By sending a fraction
of the prediction to further processing, near-perfect prediction capabilities can be achieved. It
is hypothesized that this eﬀect will only increase with higher base accuracies, which in turn

will increase with further training of the models. The in-distribution misclassiﬁcation detection

4.3
Interpretability
In this section, the attribution maps covered in section 2.4 are used for the feed-forward neural
networks in the 9-bus and 118-bus systems. Only the inner workings of this single feed-forward
network are assessed. In addition to the diﬀerent methodologies, the eﬀect of the three base-
lines proposed in section 3.1.1 on the attribution maps is also examined. Firstly, it is assesed
how many steps should be taken for the methods of Integrated Gradients and Expected Gradi-
ents. Secondly, it is assessed whether the methods propose explanations that align with human
intuition when observations are being pertubed in simple ways. Then, diﬀerent examples of
the explanations of true positive, true negative, false positive and false negative predictions are
given for the 9-bus-system. Fourthly, the diﬀerent methods and diﬀerent baselines are evaluated
quantitavely through stochastic ablation and the AOPC-measure introduced in section 2.4.7.
Lastly, the local attributions are aggregated into global characteristics, which then reveal which
features that in general are important for the classiﬁcation of both classes.

5.1
Combining uncertainty models with interpretability
In this thesis, two orthogornal but complimentary goals were set up: Creating neural net-
works for power system security assessment that were both well-calibrated and interpretable.
It was found that ensemble-methods, including Bayesian Neural Networks, outperformed the
feed-forward neural networks in terms of calibration, but when setting up the interpretability
analysis, it was still the feed-forward neural network’s inner representations and prediction ca-
pabilities that was utilized and analyzed. The reason for this is that the theoretical framework
of the attribution maps is very infant. The interpretability ﬁeld has not focused on extending
attribution map methods to BNNs yet. Future work could be concerned with combining the
ensemble methods with the interpretability to produce deep neural networks that are better
suited for deployment in safety critical domains. The combination seems straightforward and is
loosely proposed here: Attribution maps are functionals of a given observation and the weights

5.2
Uncertainty Estimation
The results of this thesis motivates using Bayesian Neural Networks in safety-critical domains.
They were found to be harder to train, but it is appraised that the increased uncertainty
estimation, especially for worst-case performance, makes up for the sacriﬁce in training time.
Furthermore, the strong misclassiﬁcation capabilities of the method would have very big eﬀect
in power system security assessment, where some types of prediction errors are unacceptable.
Future work should focus on achieving state-of-the-art performances of the Bayeisan Neural
Networks in power system security as a proof-of-concept that high accuracies and well-calibrated
uncertainty estimations can be achieved simultanteously.
None of the methods considered were found to be good OOD detectors which poses a very signif-
icant challenge for their deployment. Distributional shift has been recognized as an important
problem for AI safety and it occurs often in real-life deployment [78]. The bad capabilities of
the models are observed despite several authors pointing to Bayesian Neural Networks and non-
Bayesian Deep Ensembles as state-of-the-art tools for estimating epistemic uncertainty [84, 85].
However, it has also recently been pointed out that state-of-the-art deep neural models assign
higher likelihoods to OOD inputs than in-distribution inputs [127, 128]. While the ﬁeld of OOD
detection using simple DNN architechture is maturing, real-life deployment could draw on a rich
research tradition within e.g. novelty detection [129] and potentially use auxiliary OOD detec-
tors as a quick preprocessing step before sending the screening data to the classiﬁers. One such
popular and intuitive strategy is to train a generative model on the training data and use that
to detect OOD inputs at test time [128]. A substantial amount of work has focused on this
idea and modiﬁcations of it [128, 130]. Another approach is to use temperature scaling and
adding small pertubations to the input to separate the softmax distributions between in- and
out-of-distribution [131]. Either way, there exist solutions that are easily implemented on top
of the well-performing networks considered.
82
==PAGE:89==
It is also completely plausible that the networks will exhibit good OOD detection in real-life
deployment. The OOD distribution considered in this thesis was quite na¨ıvely implemented
without any domain knowledge and might not correspond to any type of distributional shift
encountered in practice. Determining the kind of distributional shift that might occur at de-
ployment time requires deep expertise within the speciﬁc system, and it is possible that the
systems might be able to detect the kind of changes that can occur.
5.3
Interpretability
It was found that the attribution map methods generally (1) align quite well with domain
expertise and expert intuition and (2) possess important information about how the system
makes its predictions. However, besides the good results a great deal of variance among the
attribution methods and even among the same methods for diﬀerent baselines were observed.
This is despite the fact, that the explanations were for the same network and for the same
observation. That the attribution maps are uniﬁed in purpose, but are surprisingly divergent
has been observed before and has been posed as a major limitation to using attribution maps
[125, 132].
It is hard to determine how accurately the diﬀerent explanations represent the
logic of the model as there exists no attribution ground truth. This is not solely inherent to
power system security assessment. When looking at diﬀerent methods in image recognition,
the heatmaps vary wildly [104, 122, 133] and this represents that the resurgence of the ﬁeld is
very young. Determining the variance and (un)reliability of the interpretability methods is an
important stepping stone for determining where and how the methods should be used [132]. It
has been shown by removing important features and retraining the network, that the attribution
map methods considered in this thesis perform worse or on par with random assignment of
importance [125]. However, despite their limitations attribution maps are still powerful tools
to gain intuition about the model. In addition to ensuring that the model is not performing
Clever Hans predictions, it also simply serves as a way to understand the increasingly complex
deep learning models. Future guaranteeing of attribution reliability is crucial [78], especially in
tasks where visual inspection is not possible, as is the case for power systems, or the cost of
incorrect attributions is high [132].
One somewhat surprising ﬁnding in the interpretability analysis was the strong eﬀect of the
choice of the baseline. Diﬀerent baselines, that were meant to principally represent the same
overall fact led to wildly diﬀerent attribution maps. At the heart of each baseline is an as-
sumption about missingness in the model and the distribution of the data [116]. Further, it
has been argued, that if it is unclear to determine the impliciations of the baseline, then we are
limited to say anything about the reliability of the models [132]. It is still unclear how an ideal
baseline should be chosen: Using the class-conditional modes generally performed badly on the
evaluation metrics, but produced attribution maps that were highly aligned with the domain
expertise put forward. Using marginal medians was probably the most intuitive baseline and
performed quite well, however it didn’t align as well with domain expertise. Extreme classiﬁ-
cation points were intuitive for explaining unsafe points but ill-deﬁned for explaining safe points
83
==PAGE:90==
Lastly, as a ﬁnal precaution, attribution map methods have also been shown to be sensitive to
adversarial attacks [132, 134]. Ghorbani et al. generated perceptively indistinguishable images
with identical prediction labels but substantially diﬀerent interpretations for [134]. This fragility
is orthogornal to fragility of the prediction.
The circumstanced discussed above motivates developing more robust attribution methods as
an important future agenda of research.
5.4
Deep learning in Power System Security Assessment
It was stated in section 2.1 that one major challenge of deployment of machine learning sys-
tems in power system security assessment was convincing the ﬁeld experts that the models are
accurate and reliable. The work presented in this thesis are the ﬁrst steps along this path. It
was shown how the model logic can be examined. It was also showed how models that were
trained on small and medium-sized systems had logic that aligned with ﬁeld expert intuition.
In addition, it was showed that the proposed models demonstrated good calibration character-
istics and misclassiﬁcation detection, which could naturally be implemented into the security
assessment workﬂow to substantially speed up the calculations. Taking part of departure here,
the ﬁrst deployed models should be used in parallel and in combination with traditional ap-
proaches, that would take over when the systems are in doubt. One big challenge with sharing
and improving such system is the availability of the ﬁeld. For rapid advancing of using machine
learning systems with power systems, ﬁrst reliable databases should be made publicly available
[17]. This thesis work towards this goal by publishing all code and data used in the analysis. It
is believed that the proposed methods have great potential, not only for power system security
assessment, but also for paving the way for other risk-averse, traditional engineering ﬁelds.

==PAGE:91==
6.
Conclusion
Thus, several contributions towards well-calibrated and interpretable deployment of deep neural
networks within power system security assessment was made. It was found, that the proposed
sampling scheme, ﬁtting a truncated Gaussian Mixture Model to optimal power ﬂow points,
surmounted some of the challenges of power system database building as a high number of
safe, diverse samples were created without substantial computational eﬀort. Further, through
an elaborate hyperparameter-tuning and training, the network types of feed-forward neural
networks, Bayeisan Neural Networks, MC-Dropout networks and non-Bayesian deep ensembles
achieved accuracies of 99.56% on the 9-bus system and 96.94% on the 118-bus system with
considerable room for further training and improvement. The in-distribution calibration prop-
erties of the best-performing models were assessed. It was found, that the feed-forward neural
network had good calibration capabilities for the small 9-bus-system, but had quite severe worst-
case performance for the 118-bus system, achieving a non-scaled Maximum Calibration Error
value of 0.280. In contrast, Bayesian Neural Networks achieved non-scaled Expected Calibra-
tion Error and Maximum Calibration Error values of 0.0149 and 0.145, respectively. It was
found that simple Platt Temperature scaling was reasonably eﬀective for in-distribution cali-
bration improvement, but speculated that this eﬀect would not survive distributional shifts. As
a side-eﬀect of the calibration capabilities, the predictive uncertainty of mispredicted samples
were found to generally greater than of rightly predicted samples, especially for Monte Carlo
Dropout and Bayesian Neural Networks. A workﬂow was proposed, where a small fraction of
the samples would be sent for further processing at the beneﬁt of substantially improved accu-
racy, improved true positive rate and negligible false positive rate. Lastly, for the uncertainty
estimation, it was found that the networks were bad out-of-distribution detectors and several
possible solutions to this problem was discussed.
A major challenge of deployment of neural networks in power system security assessment is
their blackbox nature, so the attribution map methods of Gradients, Gradients * Input, Lay-
erwise Relevancy Propagation, Integrated Gradients, Expected Gradients and DeepLIFT was
tried for the class-conditional baselines of marginal medians, modes and output maximizers.
A quantitative ablation-type evaluation of the explanations revealed that Expected Gradients
was the most eﬀective in highlighting the few most important features followed by integrated
gradients, explaining from the marginal median. It was found that all the methods with the ex-
ception of Gradient and Gradient * Input produced attributions that were signiﬁcantly aligned
with human domain expertise for controlled pertubations. However, it was also observed that
the explanations of the methods varied considerably and that the choice of baseline had drastic
eﬀect on the kind of explanation produced. With lack of attribution ground truth, it is not clear
85
==PAGE:92==
which explanations that represent both the logic of the model and tries to answer the human
question of ”what makes this sample insecure?”. Thus, the variability of the explanations poses
a limitation on trusting their reliability. Despite this, it was shown how the maps could provide
valuable insight into both local and global relevancy of the diﬀerent features for all prediction
types. Several research directions of attribution map methods were discussed. It was proposed
to combine the ensemble methods with the attribution map methods to create networks that
are both interpretable and well-calibrated for future work and an infant analysis showed good
results for this approach.
It is concluded that the proposed methods are of great potential to improve power system
security assessment, but also for paving the way for using deep learning in other risk-averse,
traditional engineering ﬁelds that require reliable uncertainty estimations and interpretability.
