Summary (English)
The goal of this thesis is to construct a model for quantication of dietary habits.
Proper quantication of dietary habits is an important and used tool within cost
surveys, when one for example examines the dietary patterns leading to obesity.
Currently dierent models for quantication of dietary habits exist such as
weighing the food , photographed series of food portions etc.
Studies have
shown that generic shapes representing food portions work just as well as pho-
tographed image series, which is the main motivation for this thesis.
This project presents a model consisting of seven dierent generic shapes, which
a user can vary in two directions in order to obtain the size and shape most
corresponding to the portion eaten. Based on the indicated size and shape , the
weight of the portion eaten is then estimated.
The generic shapes are based on a K-means clustering performed on existing
photographed food portions image series which is currently used by DTU Food
for estimating portion sizes. For each generic shape, the user can vary size and
shape of the portion using a scroller, which is in the two main variance directions
based on principal component analysis.
The weight of the food is estimated through a basic linear regression performed
between the number of pixels, the food represents on the plate, and the weight
of the food.
The data , on which the results are based, was thoroughly preprocessed before
being used for the analysis. First only food displayed on plates was identied.
==PAGE:4==
ii
Next the food on the plates was identied and isolated. Finally the food was
rotated and translated in order to only have variation present in the data set,
which was related to the size and shape of the food. Based on this data set, the
model for quantication of dietary habits presented in this report was created.

Chapter 1
Introduction
When performing dietary studies one of the most important things to know, is
how much food the dierent participants consume. The most precise way of
documenting this is by having the participant weigh his/her food before every
meal. This however poses some challenges. Firstly, it is a great inconvenience
for the participant to weigh all of his/her food. Secondly, it can cause changed
dietary behavioural patterns due to an unnatural setting; and thirdly it requires
a lot of extra resources from the organisation performing the study to provide
a scale for all participants. Therefore, this method is not always practised or
preferred in real life [Gib05].
As an alternative the portion sizes can be measured using photographs of dif-
ferent portion sizes.
Within 24 hours of the meal, the participant indicates
which picture corresponds the most to what the participant has eaten. This
means that the calculation of nutrient and energy intake is dependent on the
participants ability to judge the amount of food consumed [NAD94] [TG05].
An example of an photographed image series for estimation of food intake can
be seen in Figure 1.1. Here four dierent sizes of pasta have been illustrated.
These images have been used in dietary studies performed by DTU Food [DIP].
The benets of photographed picture series are that they can cover a large
variety of food and can easily be distributed to many participants in large studies
[FC03] [RP00]. Studies have shown, that this is a valid method for determining

2.1
Was it a Slab, a Slice or a Sliver?
Written by McBride J.
Published 2001 in Agricultural Research.
Summary. The Food Model Booklet is an aid for food-survey respondents to
help them quantifying their food intake developed by Linda Cleveland and Linda
Ingwersen. The model is based on 3D look-a-like illustrations and research on
how people perceive quantities. It has been tested on focus groups and adapted
to their suggestions [McB01].
The two primary challenges which inspired the Food Model Booklet were that
food-survey respondents tend to underestimate their food intake or forget some
of it. These two problems were handled by creating generic shapes represented
on real plates, which the participants themselves could adjust. Furthermore,
the question part of the registration was automated such that snacks etc. were
not forgotten.
The main idea behind The Food Model Booklet is that instead of reporting the
food intake through interviews with an researcher and using measuring cups, the
participant is guided through the book by automated questions. The generic
shapes in the book are placed on real plates and can be adapted by the user, for
example through a movable arrow. An example of the dierent generic shapes
can be viewed in Figure 2.1.

Summary.
The results in this article are two-fold.
First of all the article
examines the accuracy of food portion-size estimates using aerial photographs,
angled photographs, images of mounds and household measures. The results
showed no statistically signicance, which is a very fortunate, because it is
cheaper to use images of mounds (generic shapes) and household measures than
to take photos of the food.
Secondly, the article examines the food-survey respondents preferences using
various presentations of digital images. For some types of foods there seem to
be a preference in the group for mounds or household measures. Indicating that
registration should include dierent kinds of presentations for dierent kinds
of objects. The participants also showed a clear preference for images showed
simultaneously and a preference for more images versus less.
Showing food
varying on a continuous scale with the extreme case shown in each end of the
scale might satisfy these preferences and further improve the accuracy.
Furthermore, the article raises the point that estimation of food portions is a
very dicult task. The estimations diers a lot from participant to participant,
and some participants make mistakes of 40% or more [SA10].
Hence the studies by Subar al et. underline the potential of generic shapes, but
also stresses the fact that it is very hard to estimate food portions exact.

2.4 Data Clustering: A Review
11
Summary. This article seeks to improve portion size estimation aids for Asian
food.
Since the composition and variety in products are very dierent from
Asian food opposed to Western food, an Asian specic portion size estimation
is needed.
Due to budgetary restrictions computer-assisted systems are not
feasible in Asia, hence there is a demand for cheaper methods. However, in order
to better understand the rising obesity and educate people in eating practices,
a more accurate tool for Asian food is also needed.
In this study 16 dierent food kinds were selected to represent all food groups.
Three dierent models were used to represent the foods, small images, large
images and drawn shapes / line diagrams. The results of the study indicates
that a combination of small images and line diagrams achieves the most accu-
rate solution, since the images performed dierent depending on the texture of
the food. Small photographs performed better for amorphous food, while line
diagrams performed better for non-amorphous food than photographs.
The overall conclusion was that a combination of portion size estimation aids
would lead to a more accurate and precise food recall estimation. The accuracy
of line diagrams motivates using generic shapes instead of photographs.
2.4
Data Clustering: A Review
Written by Jain A. K., Murty M. N. and Flynn P. J.
Published 1999 in Association for Computing Machinery Computing Survey
Summary.
This paper presents an overview of dierent methods used for
clustering. The pro's and con's of each of the presented methods are described,
and nally dierent applications for clustering is discussed. [JMF99]
Overall the paper denes two types of grouping schemes; hierarchical and par-
titional methods.
The rst produces a nested series of partitions, while the
second method produces just one partition. Due to this nested property, hi-
erarchical clustering is often quite expensive compared to partitional methods,
and is therefore not preferred for large data sets. Hence a hierarchical method
would not be suitable for this project, due to the size of our data set.
The simplest and most commonly used partitional algorithm is K-means. Its
popularity is due to the fact that it is easy to implement and the complexity
is O(n), where n is the number of clusters. Furthermore, it has proven to be
convergent and due to its simplicity it is often used for large data sets. The main
==PAGE:28==
12

draw backs of K-means is that it is very susceptible to the initial clustering and
may converge to a local minimum. A lot of variations of the method have tried
to address this particular issue.
Finally, the paper presents the many applications where clustering is applied,
such as object recognition, image segmentation and information ltering and

K-means is one of the simplest unsupervised machine learning methods for

assigning all of the points to the same cluster, which will result in a small
increase in the within-class dissimilarity.

increase happens.

Besides specifying the number of clusters an important part of the K-means

One issue is, that if a cluster center is initialized too far away from the data, it
will not move, since no points will be assigned to it, and it will remain a cluster
consisting of one single point or even zero points. Usually when talking about
the k-means algorithm, one is referring to Lloyd's algorithm, which is initialized
with centers randomly taken from the data points [Llo82], in this way a cluster
center existing of zero points can never happen.
In this project Matlab's built-in function kmeans.m has been used for the K-
means algorithm Here the initialization of the cluster centers are given by the
k-means++ algorithm [AV07]. Here the centers are rst chosen randomly. Then
the squared distances from the data points to the closest cluster center is cal-
culated and the data points are weighted according to these distances. This
approach is shown to have faster convergence than the method suggested by
Lloyd.

 Random Markov Fields
Random Markov Fields is a more intricate method, which can be used for
separating background and foreground. It can also be generalized to more
classes, however as it was the case for Otsu, this is not relevant for this
project.
Random Markov Field is based on the Bayes theorem, which says that the
posterior P(A|B) is equal to a prior, P(A), times the likelihood, P(B|A),
P(A|B) = P(A)P(B|A).
In Random Markov Field the prior is then, the assumption that pixels
resembles their neighbouring, while the likelihood is given as the likeli-
hood of a new observation to belong to the background or the foreground.
For each observation the posterior probability is then calculated for both
cases. The observation is then assigned to the case where the posterior
probability is the highest. After the assignment of all observations to either
foreground or background the likelihood functions are then updated based
on these new distributions. This process is continued until it converges
[Li09].

variety of applications: dimension reduction, feature extraction and data visual-

projecting the data onto a lower dimensional linear space, such that the variance
of the projected data is maximized [Bis06].
Since PCA basically identies the most variation, it is used in this project for
identifying the pixels in the images with the most variation. The hypothesis is
that the most of the variation between the images will be due to the changing
of the size of the food and shape. Hence this is what we are trying to explain.

After having performed PCA on the data set, the goal is to reduce the number

dierent measures of size.

problem and a regularization term. In the Lasso regression the regularization

6.3
Model for the variance in the images
In the previous section seven generic clusters were identied. In this section we
seek to explain the variance within these clusters.
This is done by rst applying Principal Component Analysis (PCA) within each
cluster. In order to evaluate which loadings are most important for describing
the size of the food, a Lasso regression between the scores of the PCA and three
dierent response variables in the data set is performed.
The error of this regression is then compared with the error achieved by using
only the rst two columns in the scores matrix. Surprisingly, the error for using
the two rst columns turns out to be smaller than the error for using the whole
scores matrix. This justies only using the two main loadings for visualizing the
change in the size of the data.
Principal component analysis
Application of PCA within the individual clusters is motivated by the fact, that
PCA seeks to describe data through principal components which maximizes
the variation. In the thoroughly preprocessed data, see Chapter 5, the only
variance left in the data is due to the food's size, shape and pixel intensity.
Hence the hypothesis is, that if the variance due to shape and pixel intensities
can be removed or diminished, then most of the principal components from the
application of PCA will explain the variation of the size of the food.
The variance due to the shape of the food is diminished due to the fact, that
PCA is performed within the identied clusters. These clusters were created in
order to maximize similarity of the shapes within them. The variance due to
the intensity of the pixels representing the food is removed by only performing
PCA on the masks of the images.
PCA is then applied to the masks of the images within the individual clusters.
By observing the rst three loadings in each cluster it is clear, that the hypoth-
esis is conrmed. The loadings do in fact describe the variation of the size, the
rst three loadings for Cluster 1 can be viewed in Figure 6.8.
Next the scree plot and the cumulative sum of the percent of variance explained
is plotted for all seven cluster in order to determine the optimal number of
relevant loadings and the corresponding percent of variance explained, see Figure

Lasso regression
In order to identify the components most important for describing the data, a
tenfold cross-validated Lasso regression is performed within each cluster between
the scores from the PCA and 3 dierent measures of food. Since Lasso is a
feature selection method, this will not only help identify the loadings relating
the most to the dierent measures, but it will also reduce the number of loadings
needed. Application of Matlab's built-in lasso.m to our data set identies the
optimal β′s for the Lasso problem stated below
∥Measure of size of food −S · β ∥2 +λ ∥β ∥1 .
The largest absolute values of β will dene which scores, and thereby also which
loadings, have the largest inuence on the size of the food. Recall from Section
4.3 that we work with three dierent variables of measure of the size for the
food:
 Weights - indicating the weight of the portion
 Measures - indicating the size of the portion relative to the relevant image
series, represented by the values {1, 2, 3, 4} corresponding to { mini, small,
medium and large } portions
 Pixels - indicating the number of pixels the food is represented by in the
respective image
The linear regression is performed for all three measures and the corresponding
β's are calculated within all 7 clusters. The number of components needed for
the linear regression, corresponding to the non-negative β-values, are stated in
Table 6.2. From this it is clear that weights and pixels generally need fewer
components than measures.

7.1
Quantication of dietary habits
The whole project is based on the assumption that one can accurately quantify
food intake based on 2D generic shapes. This seems like a very rough assump-
tion, since no information about height is provided and the shape of the original
food is somewhat smoothed in order to t to a generic shape. Hence one could

based on 2D generic shapes and that a generic shape should be able to explain

Estimation of portion size is a dicult task, and studies have shown that some
participants still misestimate by 40% or greater when asked to estimate their
food intake using photographs[SA10]. Our model should be assessed in com-
parison to these models and their accuracy , as well as to the weighing of the
food.
The model presented in this thesis makes it more likely for participants in dietary

dierent generic shapes, which can be useful, since the representation of one
type of food is rarely unique.
Therefore it is expected that the model will be more accurate compared to
image series.

7.2
The data set
The data set provided by DTU Food has been the foundation of this analysis.
However, since the images were not created for this purpose at all, there are
some limitations to the data set, which should be examined in order to assess
if new data is needed in order for the model to be able to properly quantify
dietary habits. The three limitations, which should be examined , are:
 Do es the data set successfully cover the needed food span for conducting
a dietary survey?
A lot of photographs were left out due to the representation of them , either
the image was angled, the food was not placed on a plate etc. Hence at
the beginning of the analysis it might have been fair to assume that the
data set covered the needed food span, since DTU Food has used it for
nationwide dietary studies. However, after the reduction of the data set
it should be examined if the food space is suciently covered for a new
dietary survey and whether new observations should be added.
 Do the images accurately describe food for this purpose?
One should keep in mind that these photographs were originally intended
to be used solely for dietary studies, not to be used to perform a clustering
to identify generic food shapes.
Hence when presenting the food, the
study planners have thought more about servings and how servings are
normally eaten, than on creating a uniform data set which could easily be
generalized in a model. E.g. a quarter of an apple is illustrated as an apple
wedge, while the whole apple is not cut into wedges (this is characteristic
for all fruit elements).
 Dierent representations of the same food are missing from the data set
The data set only contains one representation for each type of food. How-
ever, many types of food can be presented in dierent ways, for example
carrots, which can be chopped across, chopped lengthwise or displayed as
whole. Currently, the model would not be able to handle this. However,
if the data set consisted of multiple image series of the same kind of food,
where the only dierent between the image series was that they were dis-
played dierently from image series to image series, then the model would
be able to handle this as well.

Lasso regression
Performing a lasso regression between dierent measures for size and the scores
from the PCA, showed that pixels were the best indicator of size, which was not
surprising. Measures was only a relative indicator of size within the individual
image series, but not aligned throughout the data set, and weights were a blurred
variable since it also depends on density and height.
What was surprising though was that the regression between pixels and just the
two rst columns of the scores turned out to be more precise than the regression

wrongly correlated with the pixels. Even if this was the case, it was still showed
that using the rst two principal components for explaining the pixels was very
good.
Usability
Currently the user can scroll the generic shape in the two main directions.
However, for the scrolling to be successful the user should know what he/she

intuitively for all users to see and understand all the possibilities from two

 Active Appearance Models
The model that has been implemented here is very similar to the Active

The estimation of the weight of the food was built upon a linear regression
between the 2D area of the food and the weight of the food. In this project the

estimation actually turned out to be pretty good: 90% of the predictions had
an RSME equal to or below 9 grams.
The accuracy of the model might be biased due to the fact that it was built
on very few observations for each image series. Furthermore, the regression was
only performed on one representation of each food type. For the model to be
truly generic, it should be able to estimate the weight of e.g. carrots no matter
the representation of carrots; slices, sticks, grated etc. Currently, we have no
evidence for or against the model being able to do this, hence it should be
examined preferably on a large data set with more data points.
From the analysis of the estimation of the weights, there were some indications
that a linear regression might not be the best model for explaining the relation-
ship between the area and the weight. It is likely that a quadratic relationship
would be more appropriate, assuming that a linear growth in shape corresponds
to a quadratic growth in volume. Therefore dierent models beside the linear
regression should be applied to the data set.
It is quite likely that adding the third dimension to the model would make it
more accurate and able to cover dierent representations of food. However, in
many cases it would be preferable if one could nd a mathematical relationship
between the 2D area of the food and the weight of it, instead of building a 3D
model. This would be cheaper, easier presented on paper and involve less input
from the participants. However, 3D representations also have their advantages,
and possible 3D solutions will be presented as future work in Chapter 9.
Validation
The validation of the weight estimation involves obtaining more data points of
already existing food elements covered by the model. Based on the new data
points the weight of them should be estimated using the existing model. Based
on the predictions, the dierence from the true weight could then be calculated.
The additional data points that had been obtained could also be used for training
and for testing of other possible models.
It is important to note that this should be done for several types of food and
preferably also for dierent representations of the food, before the weight esti-
mation method can be veried or discarded.

This thesis presents a model for quantication of dietary habits. The model
is based on generic shapes, which a user can vary in the two main variation
directions to indicate his/her food intake.
Based on the indicated size and
shape of the food, the weight of the portion is then estimated.
The generic shapes were identied by applying K-means to a heavily prepro-
cessed data set consisting of images previously used for quantication of dietary
habits, resulting in 7 clusters. The genericness of these shapes was established,
rst by applying K-means to dierent sets and still reaching similar shapes, and
secondly by comparing the identied generic shapes with currently used generic
shapes.
The model for varying the size of the shape was established based on the appli-
cation of Principal Component Analysis within each cluster. A lasso regression
between the number of pixels that the food represents on the plate and the two
main principal components showed that there was a strong correlation between
the two. Meaning that the two main principal components were good at explain-
ing the size of the food. Together the two main principal components explained
between 50% and 78% of the variance present in the clusters, depending on the
cluster.
Finally, the estimation of the weight was based on a simple linear regression

ABSTRACT
In this study we introduce a model for quantiﬁcation of di-
etary habits. The model consists of 7 generic shapes found us-
ing K-means clustering representing over 100 different types
of food.
For each generic shape, the user can vary size and shape of the
portion using a scroller. The scroller is in the two main vari-
ance directions based on principal component analysis. From
the indicated shape and size of the food intake, the weight
of the food is estimated. The estimation of the weight of the
food is based on a simple linear regression.
The accuracy of the weight estimates are µRMSE±σRMSE =
3.99 ± 6.1 g.
Index Terms— Dietary management, food log, dietary
assessment, food reporting
1. INTRODUCTION
When performing dietary surveys one of the most important
things is to know how much food the different participants
consume. The most precise way of documenting this is if
the participant weighs his/her food every time. This however
includes some challenges. First of all it is a great inconve-
nience for the participant to weigh all his/her food. Second, it
can change the dietary behaviour of the participant due to an
unnatural setting, and third of all it requires more resources
from the organisation performing the study to provide a phys-
ical scale for all participants. Therefore, this method is not
always practised or preferred in real life [1].
As an alternative the portion sizes can be measured by
use of images of different portion sizes. After a meal the par-
ticipants then indicate, which picture corresponds the most to
what the participant has eaten. This means that the calculation
of nutrient and energy intake is dependent on the participants
ability to judge his/her amount of food consumption [2] [3].
An example of a picture series for estimation of food intake
can be seen in Figure 1, here four different sizes of pasta salad
have been illustrated [4].
The beneﬁts from photographed picture series is that they
can cover a relatively large variety of food and can easily be
distributed to many participant in large studies [5] [6]. Stud-
ies have shown, that it is a valid method for determining the
dietary patterns. The mistakes connected to the method can
Fig. 1: Example of a picture series displaying different sizes
of food portions. This picture series illustrates four different
pasta salad portions: mini, little, medium and large [4].
be quite high on individual level, since the participants most
likely have not eaten exactly what is portrayed on the pic-
tures. Furthermore, the pictures tend to be outdated within a
few years due to the food portrayed, the colors, the setting etc.
The study performed by Subar et al, [7], shows that
generic food picture series is just as precise as photographed
picture series and the study performed by Thoradeniya et al,
[8], further indicates that generic drawings provided a more
exact estimate.
The motivation for this project was inspired by the work
done by Linda Cleveland and Linda Ingwersen [9]. Together
they designed the Food Model Booklet, which is used in na-
tionwide food consumption surveys by the USDA (United
States Department of Agriculture). It is a tool helping partic-
ipants in dietary studies to better estimate portion sizes based
on generic shapes.
The goal of this study has been to identify generic shapes
for Danish food based on existing photographed images series
previously used in dietary studies. The goal was then to pro-
vide a tool which could change the size of the generic shapes
to correspond with the actual food eaten. Based on the indi-
cated shape the weight of the food would then be estimated.

2.2. Data Description and Preprocessing
This study was based on 151 image series consisting of 475
images, previously used to quantify food intake in dietary
studies. For all of the images the weight of the food presented
in the image was provided. The data was thoroughly prepro-
cessed before the analysis in order to achieve a data set, where
only the shape and size of the food were the differentiating
factors between the images. The steps of the preprocessing
can be viewed in Figure 3.
First the images were cropped to contain only the plate.
This was done by shooting points at it from above and below
in order to identify the edge of the plate. Next an ellipse was
ﬁtted to these points, to which the image was then cropped in
order to identify the plate.
Next a mask for the food was identiﬁed using Otsu’s
method [10]. Thirdly, the variation due to the position and
direction of the food, was removed by rotating and translating
the mask and the image of the food using the Principal Axis
Transformation [11].
This procedure was applied to all images in the data set.
For the analysis only the masks of the rotated and translated
food was used, to further eliminate the variation of the pixel
intensity.

2.3. Statistical Methods
For building the model for quantiﬁcation of dietary habits
three methods were used. First K-means was applied to iden-
tify generic shapes, next PCA was applied to the generic
shapes in order to explain the variation of these and ﬁnally
a linear regression was used to provide a weight estimate.
The following notation will be used
• X denotes the data matrix consisting of the relevant im-
ages. It is an N × d matrix consisting of N observa-
tions (images) denoted as xn, where n = 1, . . . , N,
each consisting of d dimensions, corresponding to the
number of pixels in the image.
• y denotes the response variable. It is an N dimensional
vector, consisting of the number of pixels that repre-
sents food on the plate.
2.3.1. K-means
In order to identify clusters of shapes, hence generic shapes
of foods, K-means was applied to the data set using the built-
in function kmeans.m provided by Matlab. The function
also have a standard initialization of cluster centers which
was used. The optimal number of clusters was decided by
observing the within-class dissimilarity w(k) as a function of
the numbers of clusters. The optimal k was the chosen at the
point just before a small increase appeared in w(k).
Initially K-means was applied to a reduced set of the data
set corresponding to around 75% of the masks of the prepro-
cessed data in order to identify generic shapes. Afterwards
K-means was applied to the whole data set in order to test the
genericness of the identiﬁed shapes. It turned out that much
of the shapes were the same for the two runs.
==PAGE:136==
Next K-means was applied to a subset of the data set in or-
der to respect the structure of the data set. It would not make
sense to have images from the same image series being as-
signed to different clusters. Therefore K-means was applied
to the subset containing only of the ﬁrst image in each im-
ages series, and after the identiﬁcation of the clusters, the rest
of the images in the image series was assigned to the cluster
containing the ﬁrst image its image series.
In order for this assignment to be a fair, it was tested that
the shape of the food did not change within an image se-
ries. This was done by gluing the images within each image
series together to one image, and then performing K-means
on these. The analysis showed that only a few observations
changed shape within an image series, but that this behaviour
was far from representative for the whole data set.

which can be seen in Figure 6. Here the user indicates the
shape and size of the food eaten, using the 7 generic shapes
and the variance scrollers produced by PCA, resulting in the
image in the far left corner. An regression based on the for-

of pixels they represent and their weight have been performed.
Plugging in the number of pixels of the indicated shape into
the regression results in an estimate for the weight of the new
portion. The mean and standard deviation of the RMSEs of
the 88 image series was given by
µRMSE ± σRMSE = 3.99 ± 6.1 g
Furthermore, it is worth noting that the 90%-quantile of
the RMSEs was equal to 9 g.

habits. The model is based on generic shapes, which a user
can vary in the two main variation direction to indicate his/her
food intake. Based on the indicated size and shape of the food,
the weight of the portion is then estimated.
The mean and standard deviation of the RMSE’s for all
the linear regression used to estimate the weight are
µRMSE ± σRMSE = 3.99 ± 6.1 g
For future work the model should be validated through
new images and full scale dietary studies.

6.1. Creating the optimal data set for the model
The data used for the creation of this model, was not orig-
inally intended for this purpose, which meant that a lot of
observations had to be left out. If one wanted to make a more
robust model using the approach presented in this article, a
new and larger data set respecting the following rules would
be required:
• All foods should be presented on the same plate
• The images should be taken from above at the same
height for all images
• No food should be presented in its original packaging
• Only one food item should be present on the plate, e.g.
breads with toppings should be presented separately, so
that the spread and the bread would be presented on
individual plates
• The representation of the food should not change within
an image series, e.g. an apple should not be represented
as wedges in some images and as a whole in others
• The length of an image series should preferably be
longer than 4, in order to create a more valid weight
estimation and never shorter than 4
