Abstract
Guidance on how to handle thermal climate stress is in strong demand around the world. Smartphone appli-
cations that implement the principles of thermal models already exist. However, the solutions lack usability,
interpretability in results and individualization. The work conducted as part of this thesis describes the devel-
opment and user testing of a smartphone application, ClimApp, that integrates human thermal models with
individual user characteristics.
The technical framework is centered around the Cordova application development framework allowing
builds to a wide range of software platforms. To store and retrieve data, the application communicates with
three external services (1) a user database, (2) weatherservicesand(3)anadviceengine. Thecommunication
between the services is handled through dedicated APIs. The user database stores settings, weather, and
feedback on user level. The weather services are Sensationmaps and Open Weather Map which handles
current and forecast weather. Lastly, the advice engine is based on logic from the HEAT-SHIELD project,
extended with ClimApp-speciﬁc functionality and advice.
By combining human thermal models and weather forecast data in a mobile application, it is possible
to provide protective strategies for an individual such that they can act timely according to their thermal
exposure. The decisions are made based on output from models for heat (WBGT ISO7243, PHS ISO7933),
comfort (PMV ISO7730) and cold (IREQ ISO11079). By calculating the recommended alert limit (RAL), a heat
risk indicator is provided to the user to indicate the severeness of the exposure accompanied by textual
advice on how to act in the current situation. The expected thermal strain may vary between individuals due
to differences in anthropometric characteristics such as height, weight, and sex but also as a consequence
of different levels of adaptation. Therefore, the population-averaged output from models will be insufﬁcient,
and feedback is used to adjust the advice to the individual. From user testing, it has been made clear that
the app interface requires further work to improve usability. Moreover, simple and operational advice is best
received by the users and making sure the application is translated into the necessary languages is essential
for its successful effectuation.

Resume (Danish)
Vejledning i, hvordan man håndterer termisk stress, er efterspurgt verden over. Smartphone applikationer,
der implementerer principper fra termofysiologiske modeller eksisterer allerede, men løsningerne mangler
brugervenlighed, fortolkbarhed og individualisering. Arbejdet udført som en del af denne afhandling beskriver
udvikling og brugertest af en smartphone applikation, ClimApp, der integrerer termofysiologiske modeller
med individuelle brugeregenskaber.
Udviklingen af ClimApp er bygget op omkring software frameworket Cordova som understøtter en bred
vifte af softwareplatforme. For at gemme og hente data kommunikerer applikationen med tre eksterne tjen-
ester (1) en brugerdatabase, (2) vejrservices og (3) en rådgivningsmotor. Kommunikationen mellem tjen-
esterne håndteres gennem dedikerede API’er. Brugerdatabasen gemmer indstillinger, vejr og feedback på
brugerniveau. De benyttede vejretjenester er Sensationmaps og Open Weather Map, der indsamler og ud-
stiller vejret lige nu samt vejrudsigter. Rådgivningsmotoren er baseret på logik fra HEAT-SHIELD-projektet og
udvidet med ClimApp-speciﬁk funktionalitet og rådgivning.
Ved at kombinere termofysiologiske modeller og vejrudsigtsdata i en mobilapplikation er det muligt at
give beskyttelsesstrategier til et individ, så de kan handle rettidigt i henhold til deres termiske eksponering.
Beslutningerne tages på baggrund af output fra modeller for varme (WBGT ISO7243, PHS ISO7933), komfort
(PMV ISO7730) og kulde (IREQ ISO11079). Ved at beregne den anbefalede alarmeringsgrænse (RAL) leveres
en varmerisikoindikator til brugeren for at indikere alvorligheden af eksponeringen som bliver ledsaget af tek-
stbaserede råd om, hvordan man skal handle i den aktuelle situation. Den forventede termiske belastning kan
variere mellem individer på grund af forskelle i antropometriske egenskaber, såsom højde, vægt og køn, men
også som en konsekvens af forskellige tilpasningsniveauer. Derfor, vil den gennemsnitlige resultat fra mod-
ellerne være utilstrækkeligt, og feedback bruges for at tilpasse rådgivningen til den enkelte. Fra brugertest er
det blevet gjort klart, at applikationens brugergrænseﬂade har brug for yderligere justeringer for at forbedre
appens anvendelighed. Derudover kan det konkluderes at enkel og operationel rådgivning bliver bedst mod-
taget af brugerne, og at sikre, at applikationen er oversat til de nødvendige sprog er afgørende for en vellykket
udbredelse af applikationen.

1.
The ClimApp Project
This section will give the reader an understanding of how this thesis relates to the ClimApp project and in
a broader sense describe what the ClimApp project is about. The aim is to familiarize the reader with the
overall goal of the project and clarify the tasks and contributions from the involved parties. This chapter will
also provide an understanding of why this project is relevant. For the sake of completeness the preliminary
overview of demands for the app capabilities as proposed by the ERA-net is included.
1.1
European Research Area for Climate Services
The ClimApp project falls under the European Research Area for Climate Services1 which has been designed
to boost the development of efﬁcient Climate Services in Europe. The ClimApp project ofﬁcially started in
2017 and is a collaboration between several European universities2 and supporting stakeholders3.
The overall aim of this project is to develop a mobile climate application, ClimApp, that integrates climate
forecast data with human thermal models. The models will combine individual user characteristics and
physiology to provide a user-friendly and interactive mobile tool to improve decision-making for adaptation
strategies when facing thermal climate challenges. Each of the project partners brings their expertise to
the project. During development, the focus has been on the usability of the app and how to translate expert
knowledge into easily understood coping mechanisms for people without the in-depth knowledge needed to
develop it. The work presented in this thesis is based on the knowledge and collaboration between all parties
and focus on the technical framework and development hereof.

Prior to writing this thesis, the author wrote two papers covering the preliminary work with the ClimApp
partners. The ﬁrst paper addressed the initial development of an Android application and its functionality,

1.3
Requirements
To give the reader an overview of the initial requirements of the app, the demands, as deﬁned in the ClimApp
scientiﬁc document [3], are shown below. The app should include:
1. Meteorology data; air temperature, wind speed, humidity and mean radiant temperature (cloud cover-
age if the radiant temperature is not available) These data will be transformed to thermal stress at the
individual level with possibility to input local micro-climate data.
2. User individual characteristics; age, gender, body weight and height (BMI), heat acclimatization.
3. Activity level (body metabolic heat production)
4. Clothing insulation (3-5 typical categories respectively in Heat-, Indoor-, and cold-mode)
From the list, the ﬁrst bullet indicates a need for weather data and a means of transforming this infor-

Introduction
When developing a smartphone application, there is more to it than creating a working solution. As Consolvo
et al put it in [4], mobile wellness systems need to be well designed, not only in terms of how they implement
speciﬁc behavior-change techniques but also in terms of how much burden they put on the user, how well
they integrate into the user’s daily life, and how they address the user’s privacy concerns. Pew found that
even though one ﬁfth (19%) of smartphone owners have downloaded at least one health application [5] —
continued active use of these applications is very low. A survey from 2011 by the Consumer Health Infor-
mation Corporation [6] found that 26% of downloaded health applications are used only once, and 74% are
abandoned by the 10th use. Because of this, as stated by [4], usability and design were found to be key con-
siderations related to continued use. Improving the design of mobile health applications is thus critical if the
potential of these technologies to help people reach their health goals is to be realized. At the same time, we
have to deal with the fact that 77% of users never use an app again 72 hours after installing it [7] and that after
a month this number is up to 90%. Thus, the development of the application needs more than just expert
knowledge, user-involvement and openness to maintain relevance. Therefore, this thesis is a combination
of principles from software engineering, human-computer interaction, user testing, and expert knowledge in
human thermal physiology.
Motivation
Currently, there exists no individualized smartphone application implementing the thermal models needed
to fully cover and advise people exposed to both extreme cold, comfortable and hot climatic situations.
Many existing applications relate to one of the domains, but none have yet succeeded in integrating them
all to provide full thermal coverage. The interpretation and subsequent actions to be taken based on the
results in existing solutions are partially up to the individual to analyze, which might lead to arbitrary or no
action. Furthermore, many of the existing applications have undecipherable or unsatisfactory user interfaces
prioritizing functionality over interpretability which might cater more to the academic than the average user
which limits the broad adaptation of the app. Collectively this results in poor user experience and the need
to consult more than one app to ﬁnd appropriate information is less than desirable. These are some of the

3.2
Thermophysiological models
This section introduces the different human-thermal models implemented in the app. The section is meant
to give the reader an understanding of what the different models calculate and the reason for including them
in ClimApp. How the models are used within the app will be explained and visualized in chapter 4.
3.2.1
Wet Bulb Globe Temperature, WBGT (ISO 7243)
WBGT is an environmental temperature arrived at by measuring dry-bulb (air) temperature, humidity, and
radiant energy/solar irradiation (usually direct sunlight being absorbed by clothing) measured with a globe
thermometer, used to calculate the thermal load on a person[14].
The WBGT (C ◦) is deﬁned as:
W BGT = 0.7Tw +0.2Tg +0.1Ta
(3.1)
Tw is the natural wet-bulb temperature, Tg is the globe temperature, which normally is measured but in
this case calculated from the solar irradiation and Ta is the dry-bulb temperature as proposed by [15]. They
provide the effects of relative humidity and the indication of the radiant heat from the sun, respectively[16].
Liljegren et al.[15] showed that, given accurate meteorological inputs (air temperature, wind speed, atmo-
spheric pressure, relative humidity, and solar irradiance), Tw and Tg can be satisfactorily obtained from a
model they developed based on fundamental principles of heat and mass transfer.
The WBGT is calculated using either of two equations based on whether there is any direct solar irradi-
ation. When the individual is exposed to direct solar irradiation the equation 3.1 is used, as shown above,
otherwise, the equation is modiﬁed to that below,
W BGT = 0.7Tw +0.3Tg
(3.2)
where the dry-bulb temperature is no longer taken into account and the globe temperature gets a slightly
higher impact on the result.
The implementation of the WBGT model in the app is based on the work by Liljegren et al.[15] and the C
code based on the same work provided on Github12 by Max Lieblich which, for this project, was translated
into Java by Jørn Toftum and subsequently made available by requests to a server by Kingma .

Predicted Heat Strain, PHS (ISO 7933)

The Predicted Heat Strain model was developed as an alternative to the
ISO 7933 (1989) Standard, Analytical Determination and Interpretation of
Thermal stress using Calculation of the Required Sweat Rate. The ISO stan-
dard had some limitations in predicting skin temperature, clothing’s inﬂu-
ence on convective and evaporative heat exchange, the combined effect
of clothing and movement, the link between core temperature and activity
and maximum exposure times[19]. To overcome some of these limita-
tions, a new model, the PHS was developed with a focus on validating
a strategy for assessment related to hot working conditions that can be
used by practitioners in the ﬁeld. Additionally, to take into consideration
the characteristics of different clothing items and their inﬂuence on the
prediction of heat exchanges with the environment and to deﬁne criteria
for maximum exposure time and inter-individual differences in sweat rate, evaporation efﬁciency, water loss
and increase in core temperature. Furthermore to improve the validity of prediction of mean skin tempera-
ture and review the algorithms used in the current ISO Standard. The output from the Predicted Heat Strain
model is the time, in minutes, until the body reaches a core temperature above 38◦C and until ﬂuid/sweat
loss reaches a certain amount of the body weight. The PHS model was found to discriminate more than the

WBGT index in deﬁning the severity of the potential heat stress. Figure 3.5 shows the range in which the PHS
model is valid. Outside these ranges, it is important to inform about the models’ validity or lack thereof.
3.2.3
Predicted Mean Vote, PMV (ISO 7730)
PMV predicts the mean value of the overall thermal sensation of a large group of people as a function of
activity (metabolic rate), clothing insulation and the environmental parameters air temperature, mean radiant
temperature, air velocity, and air humidity. This is used to predict the percentage of thermally dissatisﬁed
people (PDD index, derived from PMV index) among a large group of people. Thermal balance exists when the
internal heat production in the body is equal to the loss of heat to the environment. Thermal dissatisfaction
may be caused by too warm or cool overall thermal sensation but can also be the result of a thermally neutral
body exposed to unwanted heating or cooling of local body parts. The PMV model is used mostly indoors to
evaluate conditions in or near thermal comfort. The PMV model is not yet implemented within ClimApp.

IREQ (ISO 11079)
Cold environments comprise hazards to human beings due to the risk of excessive body heat losses. If
heat balance cannot be maintained, peripheral and progressively more central tissues cool down. Cooling

3.2.5
The Wind Chill Index
The wind chill temperature is a cold index describing the effective cooling in temperature when the wind is
blowing heat away from bare skin compared to the temperature measured with a thermometer. Siple and
Passel[21] measured the time it took to freeze water in a small plastic bottle suspended from a post on
the roof of the expedition in the arctic. From these observations, they derived the Wind Chill Index (WCI), a
three- or a four-digit number representing the rate of heat loss of the cylinder per unit surface area. Since its
publication, many ﬂaws have been pointed out, especially by Molnar [22].

Human-computer interaction (HCI) is a term that emerged in the 1980s as computers became available not
only in the lab as part of a researcher’s work, but also moved into people’s homes [28]. As the user group was
broadened, usability and interaction suddenly became important as these new, non-science educated users
did not possess the same knowledge and understanding of the computer as the engineers or scientists who
built and programmed the therm. HCI owes a lot to older disciplines such as human factors or ergonomics,
which is a ﬁeld of science and engineering concerned with human capabilities, limitations, and performance.

Furthermore, it is engaged with the efﬁcient, safe, comfortable and enjoyable design of systems for its users.
HCI focuses on human interaction with some sort of computing technology. It draws upon knowledge from
psychology (cognitive psychology and experimental psychology), sociology, anthropology, cognitive science,

3.5.1
Interaction elements
Interaction is when a human performs a task using a computing technology of any kind. The task that is
performed is the interaction. The task is goal-oriented meaning that the user tries to achieve some goal with
the interaction. This could be sending an email, checking their bank account, ﬁnding a route to a destination
from his current location, etc. Other times the interaction has a less goal-oriented manner, i.e when users
browse the internet, are on social media and the likes. However, no matter the task, it can be classiﬁed
as an interaction where human output is used as machine input. Figure 3.9 is a model showing a human
operator (to the left) confronting a machine (to the right). The human can monitor the computer state through
sensors and displays and can control the state through responders and controls. The interface (the dashed
line) is where the interaction takes place. This is also where behavioral events (interactions) are observed
and measured. With this model, the human can be simpliﬁed to three components: sensors, responders
and a brain. The sensors comprise of vision, hearing, taste, smell, and touch. The responders affect the
environment through motor control (movement) i.e through a ﬁnger to type, feet to walk, eyebrows to frown,
speech, etc. Lastly, where everything is put together, the brain which controls the intellectual activity: thinking,
reasoning, deciding, memory: store, retain and recall information. Language: communication. Of this, a
handful of notions are important when designing a mobile interface. It is desired to make the display easy
to understand such that the user can take actions based on the information provided without having to read
through large amounts of texts or have a lot of prior knowledge. The different forms of controls like changing
clothing, updating the weather and inputting personal information should be easy and intuitive such that
the time spent interacting with the device is minimal. Better performance is typically associated with faster
or more accurate behavior, and this leads to a fundamental property of human performance—the speed-
accuracy trade-off: go faster and errors increase; slow down and accuracy improves. Skilled behavior is a
property of human behavior whereby human performance improves through practice and repetition. Human
error can be examined from many perspectives. In HCI experiments testing new interfaces or interaction
techniques, errors are an important metric for performance. An error is a discrete event in a task, or trial,
where the outcome is incorrect, having deviated from the correct and desired outcome. The events are logged
and analyzed as a component of human performance, along with task completion time and other measurable
properties of the interaction. In testing the usability of ClimApp, looking at the errors performed by users will

3.5.2
The human factor
Human scientists, including those in HCI, confront variability. They look at how humans differ as female,
male, expert, novices, left-handed, right-handed, English speaking, Chinese-speaking, etc. The variability in
humans results in the work never being precise. A system might work well for a subset of people, but venture
to the edges along any dimension, and the system might work poorly, or not at all. This is why it is exceedingly
important to know the user. The more that is understood about the user, the better a system can be designed
to work as intended. The user is not anyone, the user is part of a speciﬁc, narrow, population that is interacting
with a particular system. For ClimApp the aim is for the user group to be broad. However, to achieve this, it
is important to start small. During the development of ClimApp, the target group has been outdoor workers,
and particularly for the user tests in this thesis, construction workers. As will be described in further detail in
chapter 5, the workers differ from one another in age, ﬁtness, smartphone capabilities, English level, etc. but
also compared to the developing team of ClimApp which creates a need to bridge the gap between workers
and academia.

Research
As stated by [28, p. 122, l.10-12], research is investigation or experimentation aimed at the discovery and
interpretation of facts and revision of accepted theories or laws in light of new facts. Research in HCI is
often called a user study as it focuses on experimentation with the user. The methodology is sometimes
ad hoc and other times more formally structured. A formal and standardized approach is preferred as it
brings consistency and makes the work reproducible. Reproducibility is crucial as this is how research can
be validated. Additionally, it opens up for comparisons to other studies. By gathering information from a
user study it is possible to formulate and test evidence. This way hypotheses may be formulated for the
sake of argument and investigation. In testing ClimApp, user studies will be one of the driving elements for
change. In HCI, theories are more relevant than laws as laws are binding and can, under given conditions,
be invariable which stands in contrast to the variability that is found naturally within human behavior. Thus,
because humans are so different, measuring their behavior with one standardized scale will never result in a
realistic conclusion.

Research methods
When conduction research in HCI, there are three common approaches: the observational, experimental and
the correlational method. All three are empirical (capable of being veriﬁed or disproved by observation or
experiment) as they are based on observations or experience, however, the objectives of the research are
different. How they differ will be described below.
Observational method This method studies behaviors by directly observing phenomena in a natural setting,
as opposed to crafting constrained behaviors in an artiﬁcial laboratory setting. This approach tends
to be qualitative rather than quantitative making the observational method achieve relevance while
sacriﬁcing precision [30, p. 76]. Techniques used in the observational methods include interviews, ﬁeld
investigations, contextual inquiries, case studies, ﬁeld studies, focus groups, think-aloud protocols,
storytelling, walkthroughs, cultural probes and the like. The observational method tries to explain
human behavior, why are we doing the things we do which is why the focus is on thoughts, feelings,
attitude, emotion, passion, sensation sentiment, opinion, etc. These qualities can be observed, but are
hard to measure.
Experimental method this method is also called the scientiﬁc method. Here, knowledge is acquired through
controlled experiments that take place in a laboratory or a more formal setting. The acquisition of
knowledge can mean either gathering new knowledge or studying existing knowledge for veriﬁcation,
refutation, correction integration or extension. Compared to the behavioral method the focus here is
not on relevance, but rather on precision as the factors of the real world is reduced. A controlled experi-
ment requires at least two variables: a manipulated variable and a response variable. The manipulated
25
==PAGE:44==
variable is typically a property of an interface or an interaction technique presented to the participant.
Exposing the participants to different conﬁgurations of the interface or interaction technique is the
means of manipulating that variable. A requirement for it to be a controlled experiment is that there
are at least two conﬁgurations as a comparison is key to the experimental method. Therefore exposing
a system’s ﬂaws without comparing it to something else is not experimental research. A manipulated
variable is also called an independent variable or a factor. A response variable is a property of human
behavior that can be observed, quantiﬁed and measured. The response variable is also called the de-
pendent variable. Often this variable is time. Given a task, how long time does it take to complete
the using each of the different conﬁgurations. It is important to note that even though the goal is to
measure human performance, a proper user study also takes comments, thoughts, and opinions from
the participants into account. The participants’ state of mind might not always be visible directly in
the numbers but it should not be ignored. From a properly conducted controlled experiment, the re-
lationship between dependent and independent variables is one of cause and effect. A change in the
interface causes the observed difference in the response variable. This is not true for research using
the observational or correlational method.
Correlational method in the correlational model relationships between variables is explored. The method
focuses on quantiﬁcation based on a predeﬁned set of variables. The data may be collected through a
variety of methods i.e., observation, interviews, surveys, questionnaires or measurement. Correlational
methods provide a balance between relevance and precision and they often accompany experimental
method if questionnaires are included in the experimental procedure. This way it is possible to look at
relationships by gender, age, level of experience, etc. It is, however, important to note that correlation
does not mean causation.
From the above methods, the observational method is highly relevant when testing an application. It is
important to understand why individuals do as they do and what led them to take the actions they did. The
experimental method is also a good option as this would enable methods such as A/B testing, where you
present the user with different buttons, different visualizations representing the same thing to ﬁnd out which
is easier to understand, etc. The correlational method could be used to look at differences within the user
group. This will be highly dependent on the number of test participants and the quality of the collected data.
The user testing of ClimApp will be described in chapter 5.

3.6.1
Design Thinking as a Complementary Approach
The human factor dilemma in section 3.5.2 is discussed in section 2 in [31]. Within the IT industry, more
than in any other industry, when dealing with technical products and services a technical perspective plays
a large role in both solving the problem and developing the solution. Not everyone possesses the necessary
skills (programming, architecture, etc.) to implement the solution and to get an understanding of the end
product to be able to communicate about it in technical terms. This moves the IT development into taking
place in a smaller expert world, where developers both in past and present times take on the designer’s role
despite not being trained within the ﬁeld professionally. Although the solutions created this way might be
technically ﬂawless, the solutions may shape up as incomprehensible or inappropriate from the user’s point of
view[31][p.7, l.25-26]. This happens because the design is not developed with the user in mind, but rather the
developer which might mean that what is important for the user may not be addressed. The consequences
of this might be inefﬁciency and loss of effectiveness for the user or, at its extreme, the user might choose
not to use the solution at all. An isolated technical perspective entailing isolated analytical thinking can thus
lead to an innovation trap: while spending much effort in the development of technically novel or reasonable
solutions, the clients do not see the solution’s distinctive value[31][p.8, l.3-6]. This is the reason why bringing
aspects of design thinking and human-computer interaction into the design and development phase is of
vital importance for the success of this project.
3.6.2
What is Design Thinking?
Various deﬁnitions of Design Thinking exist. In [31], Meinel and Leifer describe it as an iterative process of
ﬁve stages which are supported by the Interaction Design Foundation in [32] (see ﬁgure 3.11). At its core,

innovative products, systems, and services. Design thinking creates a vibrant interactive environment that
promotes learning through rapid conceptual prototyping[31][p. xiv, l. 6-7]. The Interaction Design Foundation
deﬁnes these stages as described below:
Empathise First you need to gain an empathetic understanding of the problem that should be solved. This
is done by consulting experts within the area and through observing, engaging an empathizing with
people to understand their experiences and motivations. It is all about gaining a deeper personal
understanding of the issue you are involved with. Empathizing allows the design thinkers to set aside
their assumptions about the problem area and instead gain insight into the real user and their needs.
As stated by the IBM Think Academy: Beginning with the end-user will help you focus on improvements
that provide the most value.[33][5:17-5:21].
Deﬁne During this stage, the information gathered during the Empathise stage is put together. The different

3.6.3
Principles supporting usability
Usability refers to the ease of access and use of a given product. The ofﬁcial ISO 9241-11 deﬁnition of usability
is the extent to which a product can be used by speciﬁed users to achieve speciﬁed goals with effectiveness,
efﬁciency, and satisfaction in a speciﬁed context of use. As described in [34], a usable interface should take
the following three categories into account: learnability, ﬂexibility and robustness. These principles will be
used to assess the usability of ClimApp in later chapters. The meaning of these terms is described below.
Learnability The ease with which an inexperienced user can use a system effectively. To successfully incor-
porate learnability the system needs to be predictable, meaning that the actions a user can take are
deterministic and gives the user an idea of which actions that can be performed. When changes are
being made by the user, it is important that the system change is visible to the user for example by
showing a notiﬁcation about the update or making sure the updated information is represented on the
29
==PAGE:48==
screen for the user to see. Additionally by incorporating known elements or actions, one can utilize the
user’s existing knowledge of how a system works, its familiarity, which makes learning how to use the
system easier. Furthermore, familiarity with a system makes it easier for the user to generalize about
its capabilities.
Flexibility The different ways a user can interact with the system. A system can be viewed as a dialogue
between a user and a system through an interface with the goal of achieving a task. Here, one often
distinguish between actions that are system and user-driven. System-driven events could happen
through a notiﬁcation sent to a smartphone device or by alarms at predeﬁned times of the day. User-
driven actions are initiated by the user and are often goal-oriented. A ﬂexible system allows for task-
migratability letting the system perform repetitive task of inputting existing information while also
making room for substitutivity, allowing the user to change existing values and settings e.g. changing
scale for unit of measurement because it is desired to personalize the input (height, weight gender)
increasing the customizability, the adaptation of a system to the individual.
Robustness The achievement and assessment of goals when a user interacts with a system. Robustness
supports actions leading to the successful completion of a goal by rectifying behavior and handling
errors made by the user. By exploring an interface, and letting the user observe the changes, the user
can get an idea of the inner workings of a system. If a goal is completed without having entered the
correct information, the user will be informed about the error (through a notiﬁcation or a guard) or the
system might use default values. To make it easy for the user, the information that is being inputted
needs to be stored (persisted) such that the user does not have to input the same information again.
Robustness also incorporates the ability to reach the desired goal after recognition of some error (re-
coverability) in a previous interaction, e.g. upon entering a value not expected by the system, the user
will be informed about the correct type of value instead of crashing. To make the user aware of when
a task is completed giving appropriate responses is important as it supports the user’s understanding
of the system.

Software Development
This section will introduce different methods used in the software development processes. Albeit some of
the development processes might be targeted at larger software development projects the concepts are still
applicable to smaller projects.
There exist various methods to organize, track and monitor software development projects. One of the older
methods is known as the waterfall model. This process is following a linear process passing through several
phases in a deﬁned order. The different phases are separated using milestones that describe goals and
deadlines and require extensive documentation of each phase. The phases are requirement and speciﬁcation
30
==PAGE:49==
analysis, program design, coding, testing, and implementation. The model is called the waterfall model as
it cascades from one phase to the next. This method is a plan-driven process, you must plan and schedule
all of the process activities before work can commence[35]. As pointed out by Royce in [36] if some of
the requirements fail to satisfy any external criteria in the testing phase, they will cause the need for major
redesigns. He states The required design changes are likely to be so disruptive that the software requirements
upon which the design is based and which provides the rationale for everything are violated. If errors or changes
are needed they will be extremely expensive as the testing only happens at the end of the development
process. Furthermore, the user’s perspective can only be included in the preliminary analysis in the beginning
and the test when the product is close to complete – as already discussed in the section above this will
become a problem for the end-user. This makes the waterfall method unsuited for this project which needs
a model open to changes throughout the process.

4.2.1
The knowledgebase
The knowledgebase has become the name of the object storing user information in ClimApp. The knowledge-
base is a Javascript object containing all information necessary for the app in terms of text, conﬁguration,
and individual user parameters. After publishing the ﬁrst version of ClimApp if the user updated the app, all
personal information would be overwritten by the information from the new version, as shown to the left in
ﬁgure 4.5. This meant that users on each update would have to input all their settings again, as their own
was overwritten by the default values. This is a tedious task that might lead users to either not updating their
app or stop using it.
To combat this the knowledgebase was given a version number which opened up to the possibility of
matching the version number of the knowledgebase currently on the device against that of the update. When-
ever the knowledgebase version was the same as that of the update, nothing new was added to the knowl-
edgebase, which meant that there would be no need to override the existing content. However, (inevitably)
in this scenario when the version number of the update exceeded that on the device, an override will still
happen. Therefore the knowledgebase was split into two segments; information that is overridable (okay to
delete) and information to persist (no delete on update).
Figure 4.5: Logic for updating the knowledgebase. Left: old, right: new
Now, information to persist is stored in one speciﬁc part of the knowledgebase, the user object and
everything else is treated as overridable. This means that whenever the update version number is greater
than the one installed on the device all information from the user object is copied into the new knowledgebase.
This way, new changes to the structure are included while the old values will be persisted using a recursive

The heat logic has been aligned with work done by the HEAT-SHIELD project8 to streamline the basis for how
heat stress is handled. As is indicated in ﬁgure 4.11 and previously mentioned, the advice is based on output

a part of the WBGT ISO7243). By calculating the recommended alert limit (RAL), a heat risk indicator is

current situation. The three ﬁrst grey bars correspond to the logic implemented by the HEAT-SHIELD project,
the subsequent three bars are additional advice and coping strategies as implemented in ClimApp. Each
colored rectangle vertically on the grey bars in the ﬁgure adds an extra level of advice to the output. The
advice is provided based on user-level either beginner (1) or advanced (2). For the advanced user, the advice

User testing
This section introduces the available user groups and which was selected. Additionally, this section will
describe how user testing was planned and executed and what the results of the testing were. Whether an
application like ClimApp is needed has already been established by ERA4CS which is why the focus in this
section is on whether ClimApp, at its current state, solves the right problem and what can be improved.
5.1
Selecting the user group
During several ClimApp partner meetings, it has been stated that the target group for ClimApp could be
anyone as the information in the app can be valuable not only for outdoor workers but also for individuals
such as hikers, family members worried about their loved ones, etc. However, designing for everyone is
designing for no one. As stated in the ﬁrst step in the design thinking process, it is important to be able to
set aside assumptions about the prospect user and focus on what the actual user needs. Therefore, part
of this work revolved around testing the app functionality with a prospect user group to get insight into the
users and to ensure that the app was developed with a concrete user group in mind. This helps in deﬁning
the problem, at least for one speciﬁc user group, which aids in making the app more relevant to the users
and creates a basis for validation and improvement. In the ClimApp scientiﬁc document, that outlines some
of the overall criteria the app has to meet, possible target user groups are deﬁned (see table below). To align
with the project goals, choosing users from within one of these ﬁelds would be beneﬁcial. Moreover, because
the user groups are all collaborators on the project, testing was thought to be easier to plan and execute.
As can be seen in ﬁgure 5.1, there are six different collaborators, children in kindergartens, elderly in
the UK and four groups of workers in Sweden, Denmark, and Italy and elderly in the UK. The collaborators
have different working conditions (hot/cold, indoor/outdoor) and vary from young to elderly. The needs of
the different user groups vary and does, for the outdoor workers, depend heavily on the season. From a
development perspective, it is not possible to successfully accommodate and develop for all user groups at
the same time. The children will not be operating a smartphone device themselves, as they cannot infer what
to do from the information given in the app (at least not without thinking of a different, simpler interface which
is not the scope of this project). The elderly might not know how or want to operate a smartphone device and

6.
Conclusion
By analyzing existing applications dealing with thermal stress it was made clear that currently none of the
available applications provide the necessary information on adaptation strategies, nor the level of complete-
ness in terms of handling both hot and cold environments as is needed.
The work in this thesis has resulted in a mobile application that can be downloaded for both Android and
iOS devices. The application integrates models for hot (WBGT, PHS, PMV, CAF, RAL) and cold (IREQ, windchill)
situations and provides feedback on thermal stress level, hydration, how to adjust clothing and duration limits
for exposure to different climatic situations. The application communicates with three external services (1)
a user database, (2) weather services and (3) an advice engine through dedicated APIs. The current state
of ClimApp allows users to test and submit feedback on its functionality and provides the basic information
to operate it successfully. While the development of the app is still ongoing, the overall frameworks and
methods for retrieval and processing of data are in place. Unlike competing solutions, ClimApp integrates
models for a larger spectrum of climatic situations, provides coping strategies, has a user-friendly interface
and allows for individualization which ultimately makes it applicable in a higher number of situations and
eliminates the need to consult with additional sources to assess the situation accurately.
From user testing, it was made clear that there is a need for this kind of app. Depending on the type
of work being performed, the device the information is displayed on might need adjustments. The current
wheels and buttons on the dashboard are hard to operate and need improvements for usability to be optimal.
Additionally, there is a need for simpliﬁcation of the phrasing of the advice for the users to better understand
and act on the information. Furthermore, language can be a barrier and therefore making the app available
in other languages is recommended.
By combining principles of human-computer interaction and design thinking in a user-centered approach
to application development, it has been shown that there exist differences in how the app interface and
related texts are perceived by the developing organization and the prospect user. This knowledge regarding
the differences are valuable and need to be handled for the app to support its target group. To be able to
accommodate each user group most optimally, and make sure that the app content ﬁts their needs, it is
recommended that the procedures presented in this thesis when testing the app further are followed.
In this thesis, the successful development of a mobile application capable of receiving, storing and pro-
77
==PAGE:96==
cessing user, GPS and weather data for thermal calculations was described. This in terms generates valu-
able, personalized feedback to the user on how to act to prevent heath-illnesses. With continuing work on
the models powering the app and how to explain the results to the user, we anticipate that this solution can
become the leading tool in thermal climate stress prevention by improving strategies for hydration, clothing
and work/rest schedule to ensure the health and safety of workers and the general population.

daily weather overviews for the day including minimum and maximum temperature, whether or not it will be
raining, etc. to better prepare the user for the weather as it is forecast to be throughout the day. This is
an important feature however it is estimated that it will take more time to implement as push-notiﬁcations
are triggered on a server based on events that happen within the app. Mapping out the structure and logic
for how this will be done will require a lot of time before an estimate can be made on the implementation
time. Currently, the PHS model is included in ClimApp. However, it does not take work/rest schedules for the
individual into account. To estimate the needed ﬂuid intake more accurately implementing logic to estimate
the ﬂuid intake by looking at an overall work/rest schedule for the user would further improve the application’s
79
==PAGE:98==
accuracy. With the data collected on the users from the feedback module, the possibility to extract and ﬁnd
user patterns arises. With the information that is gathered it would be possible to get an understanding
of how different user segments rates the model accuracy which might lead to adjustments in the models.
Currently, the clothing options within the app are narrow. The options are not made to ﬁt speciﬁc user groups
nor is it comprehensive for the types of clothing ensembles that can be expected for someone to wear. As
previously mentioned the clothing has been added as a minimum viable product to test the solution. With the
extended list of options, it would be preferable to divide the different types of clothing based on profession
or user types such that the available set of options is better suited to the individual. The extended clothing
options go hand in hand with the addition of different user proﬁles. By letting the user choose a ﬁeld of
work, the information in the app can be personalized such that only what is relevant within that profession is
shown. I.e such that an outdoor construction worker get clothing in the relevant style and work categories
rather than business or leisurewear. Implementing this feature requires an analysis of different professions
to ﬁnd the needed clothing ensembles and their clo values, which requires more research and testing.
