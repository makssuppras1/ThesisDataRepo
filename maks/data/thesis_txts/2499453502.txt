Aim of the thesis 
This master thesis is a part of a larger research project regarding the fungi of Monilinia 
fructigena (M. fructigena), which causes brown rot on e.g. apples.  
 
The aim of this project is to investigate correlation between metabolites from M. 
fructigena. and to determine if this can be used in general on other fungi.  
 
The following methods were used to determine correlation between metabolites: 
• 
MS (mass spectrometry) 
• 
MS/MS (mass spectrometer/mass spectrometer) 
• 
GNPS networking

Abstract 
The filamentous fungus Monilinia fructigena (M. fructigena) is important due to its 
effect on plants where it causes brown rot. From a chemistry point of view M. 
fructigena have some interesting metabolites. 
There are two main types of natural products the primary and secondary metabolites.  
The aim of the project was to determine the chemo diversity of a strain of M. fructigena 
and to determine if it include unknown as well as known compounds and if these are 
biosynthetically related 
 
The results in this report showed that only a few of the known metabolites from 
Monilinia was even within the sample. Of the 25 compounds that was known from 
literature 15 of them could be detected and only in small amounts. 
When so few compounds of the known compounds were found it would be impossible 
to network these and therefore an analysis of the main peaks in the base peak 
chromatogram was performed.  
Each of the main peaks and the main compound in each of these were analysed. The 
first analysis was done manually by comparing the fragmentation of the compounds 
after MS/MS. Two unknown compounds were found that had very similar MS/MS 
patterns. After an analysis within the GNPS network a search for these compounds was 
done and it was found that these shared a network. 
The GNPS network showed many large networks but due to time constrains an analysis 
of these was not done. 
 
From this project it can be concluded that there is still a lot of unknown chemistry 
created from M. fructigena. Furthermore, it can be concluded that it contains more 
pathways than was known from literature. Altogether the results confirmed that GNPS 
is a great tool for finding similar compounds from the fungus.

Dansk sammendrag (Danish summary) 
Den filamentøse skimmelsvamp Monilinia fructigena (M. fructigena) er af stor 
betydning pga. dens effekt på planter, hvor den forårsager brune rådpletter. Fra et 
kemisk perspektiv er M. fructigena interessant, da den kan producere flere interessante 
metabolitter også kaldet naturstoffer. 
Der er to hovedgrupper af metabolitter - primære og sekundære. Formålet med projektet 
er at undersøge hvilke typer af kemiske metabolitter, som produceres af en stamme af 
M. fructigena. Det vil sige specifikt at bestemme indholdet af henholdsvis kendte og 
ukendte metabolitter og om disse er biosyntetisk relateret.  
 
Resultaterne af projektet viste, at der kun var få kendte metabolitter fra Monilinia som 
var i svampe prøven. Af de 25 kendte metabolitter, som var kendt fra litteraturen var det 
kun 15 af disse, som kunne detekteres i prøven. Ydermere var alle disse kun tilstede i 
relativt små mængder. 
Da så få kendte stoffer blev fundet i prøven var det umuligt ved såkaldt GNPS analyser 
at påvise netværk imellem disse stoffer, hvorfor der i stedet blev fokuseret på analyse af 
hovedtoppene i base peak kromatogrammet (BPC). 
Her blev massespektrometrisk data analyseret for de ukendte hovedkomponenter i 
prøverne. Til start blev hovedtoppens største addukter manuelt analyseret og 
fragmenteringsmønstret af disse yderligere analyseret ved brug af MS/MS-analyse. 
Det viste sig, at to af disse ukendte komponenter havde meget ens MS/MS-mønstre. 
Bagefter blev det undersøgt ved brug af GNPS-netværks analyse og ved at søge på disse 
molekyler blev det fundet, at disse var i det samme netværk. 
Der var mange netværk af metabolitter i GNPS, men betinget af mangel på tid blev alle 
disse netværk ikke videre analyseret. 
 
Ud fra dette projekt kan det konkluderes, at der stadig er mange ukendte metabolitter i 
ekstrakter af M. fructigena. Endeligt kan desuden konkluderes, at GNPS er et godt 
redskab til at finde metabolitter, som tilsvare hinanden i svampen.

1. Introduction 
1.1 
Natural products 
 
Natural products have always played an important part of human life, in the old days 
natural products were only small molecules that could be found directly in nature. These 
natural products could be used to improve quality of life. Natural products do not only 
cover the products that have positive effect on humans but it also covers toxins 
produced by natural ways such as plants, fungi and bacteria.  
 
The original natural products were mostly oil extracts from plants such as cedar, cypress 
and liquorice. These compounds were dated to Mesopotamia 2600 BC, and was used to 
treat anything from coughs to parasitic infections [Cragg, 2001]. 
The use of natural products slowly evolved in time but were mostly simple extracts 
from plants until the discovery of penicillin from Penicillium chrysogenum was done by 
Alexander Fleming in 1929 [Tan, 2015]. When this was discovered another way to look 
at natural products was determined and the focus slowly shifted focus from plant-based 
compounds to fungal compounds. 
Since this time the interest in secondary metabolites have increased and now the main 
interest in natural products come from these secondary metabolites. 
 
1.2 Fungi 
  
Fungi are neither plants or part of the animal kingdom, they are instead something of 
their own and is known as the fifth kingdom in the tree of life. Fungi are the most 
dynamic dweller and can live in both soil, water and extreme environments, furthermore 
they are one of the most diverse microorganisms with an estimate of 5.3 million 
different species of fungi with only 100,000 fungi being well described [Chambergo, 
2016]. 
Fungi help the ecosystem by degrading dead plants, and dead animals to recycle carbon 
and other nutrients back into the soil.  
The fungi face a constant fight for survival from other fungi, bacteria and animals, to 
survive against these threats the response from the fungi is to produce compounds to 
fend off their attackers. 
Some of these compounds or secondary metabolites produced from the fungi are used in 
medicine and almost 50% of all approved drugs from 1981 to 2014 was discovered from 
natural products, natural product derivatives and synthetic drugs based on natural 
products [Newman, 2016].

1.3 Chromatography 
 
Chromatography can be divided into three categories; liquid-, gas and supercritical 
fluid. 
The supercritical fluid chromatography is the least used type of chromatography due to 
the difficulty of having a fluid at the super critical state, as well as most compounds 
could be analysed using either liquid chromatography or gas chromatography. 
To determine which type of chromatography to use when looking at samples it must be 
known if the compounds of interest are volatile or non-volatile. When working with 
volatile compounds gas chromatography is the type to use and if working with non-
volatile compounds it should be an advantage to use liquid chromatography [Harris, 
2016]. 
Since the compounds of interest in this project are non-volatile a liquid chromatography 
was used and therefore the project will only describe the liquid chromatography. 
 
1.3.1 Liquid chromatography 
 
Liquid chromatography (LC) was invented around the turn of the century. Since its 
inception different types of LC have been invented such as capillary LC, micro bore LC 
and High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC). As the name suggests the 
original type of LC was normal phase. Reverse phase was not invented before 1948 by 
Boldingh, since the invention of reverse phase LC this type has slowly taken over as the 
most commonly used type of LC [Harris, 2016]. 
LC is used in two main ways; either as an analytical method for determination of the 
individual compounds within a small sample or as a preparative method most 
commonly used in production to remove large amounts of unwanted substances in the 
downstream process to purify the final product. 
The analytical version is used for small amounts and this gives a better separation and 
higher resolution [Harris, 2016]. 
The following sections will give a short introduction to the normal phase and reverse 
phase chromatography.

1.3.1.1 Normal phase liquid chromatography 
 
Normal phase chromatography is the original form of LC. Normal phase 
chromatography consists of a polar stationary phase, and can be either run as a gradient 
where the mobile phase changes from a non-polar start to a polar end point slowly 
changing over time, or it can be run as an isocratic method where the mobile phase is 
constant. of the chromatography. 
 
1.3.1.2 Reverse phase liquid chromatography 
 
Reverse phase LC is the opposite of normal phase LC. The name reverse phase is based 
on the fact that normal phase was the first to be invented and the phases are reversed. 
Just as in normal phase the mobile phase can be either isocratic or a gradient for elution, 
and just as in normal phase the separation are better with the gradient elution than with 
isocratic and the separation is also faster when using the gradient, though sometimes if 
the gradient has to change very slow for maximum precision then an isocratic would be 
faster but it would come at the cost of separation [Canene-Adams, 2013], [Shire, 2015]. 
The mobile phase in reverse phase is always a mixture of water and an organic 
compound the most commonly used organic compounds are methanol and acetonitrile, 
see Table 2.

The stationary phase that is inside the column can be composed of many different non-
polar chains these chains are bound to a core or a bead, these are most commonly made 
from silica. 
The size of the particles used in the stationary phase affects the pressure lost in the 
column, which means that smaller bead size will increase the pressure and a larger bead 
will decrease the pressure. The reasons for using smaller beads is that they will increase 
the resolution/separation of the products due to the larger surface area. It has to be noted 
that due to the pressure lost in the column the particle size cannot go below a certain 
threshold since this would result in the column breaking [Shire, 2015], [Harris, 2016].

1.3.1.4 Retention in RP 
 
The retention in a RP LC chromatography column is still not totally understood even 
though it has been more than 50 years since its inception.  
The main model for explaining the retention within the column is the partitioning. 
Partitioning can be explained in three main steps: 
1.1 Creation of a cavity within the stationary phase 
1.2 Compounds from the mobile phase a transferred into the cavity 
1.3 The closure of the hole in the mobile phase due to the escape of the compound 
 
Figure 5 - Shows the creation of a cavity (left) and the compounds from  
the mobile phase entering the cavities (right) [Shalliker, 2005] 
 
The model, Figure 5, describes this as chemical affinity between the dissolved 
compounds in the mobile and the stationary phase. 
The chemical affinity between the stationary phase and the mobile phase could be 
dipole-dipole also known as Van der Waal. The dipole-dipole interaction would only be 
an option if the sample compounds can have temporary dipole moments. Interaction 
between the sample and the mobile phase could be hydrogen bonds. 
Another explanation for the retention in the column is the lattice model, this model does 
not only focus on partitioning as the main reason for retention. The lattice model adds 
the idea that cavities are created and then to be filled with compound, this stops other 
compounds from being bound within the cavities. This model also helps explain why 
the column can be overloaded. 
The retention in the column may not be explained solely by either of the factors and 
might very well be explained by a combination of both forces, and it is a mix of these 
factors that create the retention within the column. 
Since the stationary phase consists of a silica bound carbon chain which makes it 
behave more like a solid than a bulk liquid. When the surface density in the stationary 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
Page 21 
 
phase grows larger, this makes the partitioning less entropically favourable and with 
every molecule that is bound within the stationary phase due to partitioning the order is 
increased making further partitioning even less favourable. 
Due to this reason adsorption is more profound on higher density mobile phases till the 
point where the solute is excluded from the stationary phase. When using smaller 
carbon chains bound to the silica this reduces the freedom due to the small carbon 
chains having limited movement in comparison to larger carbon chains. When looking 
at smaller carbon chains as the stationary phase the binding done as partitioning is very 
unlikely to have a great influence on the retention within the column.

looking at the number of carbon atoms, this is true until the point where the samples 
carbon chains are approximately the same length as the silica bound chains in the 
stationary phase. At this point the retention factor changes from partitioning to a 
mixture of partitioning and adsorption and with even longer chains in the sample the 
retention changes to clean adsorption [Harris, 2016]. 
 
 
1.4 High Performance Liquid Chromatography 
 
High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) is an optimized version of LC. Both 
advantages and disadvantages are centred around the column in the system. 
The challenge with HPLC is that to increase the separation either the bead size has to be 
decreased or the column length has to be increased.  
The problem with making a longer column is that this would increase runtime this can 
only be acceptable if the compounds in completely stable and if an equipment can be 
used only for that one sample and would be allowed to run for days. Often the time 
needed to run the sample is simply not feasible due to the cost of having a long runtime 
on a single equipment. 
The bead size has a great effect on the separation of the sample within the column, 
when using larger beads, the separation will decrease but so will the pressure within the 
column. 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
Page 22 
 
When decreasing the bead size, the separation within the column is increased, this 
comes at the cost of needing a higher pressure and if the pressure is to high the column 
will burst. 
For an increased separation and having a runtime that makes the system able to analyse 
more than one sample every day, the column length can be reduced in combination with 
a smaller bead size, this would in turn increase the pressure lost in the column making it 
harder to run but it would be a lot faster than having the sample run on a long column to 
have the same separation. 
A change in temperature can also affect the systems efficiency when it comes to 
separation of the compounds within the column. This depends on the stationary phase 
and the mobile phase to determine if a higher or lower temperature will improve the 
performance of the HPLC. 
The main difference between normal LC and HPLC is that HPLC gives a better 
separation and has a faster runtime making it a lot more useful in modern separations 
[Shire, 2015], [Harris, 2016].

1.5.1 Diode Array Detector 
 
The Diode Array Detector (DAD) is a on a base level a light source that sends light 
through the substrate that comes out of the column, then the detector gathers the light 
and measures the light that goes through the liquid. Since the wavelengths that are send 
through the substrate are known the absorbed light is known and this results in a UV 
spectrum.  
A more detailed version of DAD is that the lamp is often a combination of either a 
tungsten lamp or a halogen lamp for the visible light and a deuterium lamp for the UV 
spectrum. The light covered by the DAD normally covers all of the visible light and into 
the UV light. The wavelength often covers from 200 to 800 nm, the ultraviolet light can 
go all the way to 1200 nm but is most commonly between 200 to 800 nm. The reason 
for not having anything below 200 nm is that this would just show single and double 
bounds light absorption. This is not very useful to determine the structure of a certain 
molecule since it does not give any essential knowledge to help determining the 
structure of a certain molecule [Rozing, 1996], [Fetzer, 2014]. 
 
Figure 6 - Shows the setup for a DAD with all the components [Fetzer, 2014] 
 
A detailed setup for DAD is shown in  Figure 6. This system uses a deuterium lamp as 
light source, in front of the light source is an Achromatic lens, this lens focused the 
light. 
The next part of the system is a shutter that holds a filter, this filter will be described 
later on. The shutter can contain more than one filter such as filters to control the light 
source and filters that can stop the light going through to the detector. 
The light will be sent through a sample vial that is a continuous flow this means that the 
detector can detect when a certain compound elutes from the column and when the same 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
Page 24 
 
compound is done eluting from the column. The light is then refocused and is led to a 
holographic grating that will diffraction the light and the light will be detected by a 
photo diode array [Rozing, 1996], [Fetzer, 2014]. 
 
1.5.1.1 Concave diffraction grating 
 
The concave diffraction grating or holographic grating is a grate that can split the light 
into the different wave lengths that has not been absorbed in the eluent.  
The reason that it is necessary to do the splitting of the light after the sample vial is to 
detect the intensity of each wave length. The number of grooves on a grate can vary 
from 65 to 5,000 grooves pr. mm this depends on the needs of the system and on the 
manufacture of the grate. 
Often the most important determinant for which grate is used is how well separated the 
light needs to be for the specific DAD [Fetzer, 2014]. 
 
1.5.1.2 Photodiode 
 
The photodiode is a light sensitive semi-conductor that can generate a current based on 
the amount of light that hits it.  
The detection is done by making small clusters of photodiodes, that will each cover a 
specific wavelength band from 0.6 to 2.0 nm depending on the precision needed from 
the DAD. 
The small change in nm between the different photodiodes helps giving a well-defined 
UV spectrum, each of the clusters of semi-conductor material consists of 3D crystal of 
either silicon or germanium. Each of these crystals are controlled for impurities of 
elements the periodic table.  
The main reason for using photodiodes is that there is a very low energy gap between 
the valence and the conduction band, which makes the photodiode optimal for detection 
of very small amounts of light. The absorption of light forces the electrons to leave the 
photodiode and due to the electron leaving a small current is created.  
The detection works when a small current is created when the light hits a specific 
photodiode. This means that high response from the sensor is equal to low absorption.

evaporate due to the powerful electric field and is turned into a spray of very small 
droplets. The outside of these droplets has either a positive or negative charge 
depending on which type of electrospray that is being used. Before the spray can enter 
the TOFMS the spray goes through an evaporation chamber.  
The evaporation chamber consists of a heated tube within this tube the droplets slowly 
evaporates making the droplets smaller and smaller. At the end of the tube all the

droplets have evaporated, the tube is then aimed into a small hole this results in that 
only the sample will be led on and the solvent molecules that are smaller will be 
removed. The larger molecular ions and ion clusters have a higher mass than the 
solvents and these will be moved into a drying gas that will carry off the last small 
molecules. 
At the end of the electrospray there are two momentum-based separators these will 
remove any unwanted solvents that are still within the sample [Patil, 2017], [Tang,

1.7 MS/MS 
 
Tandem mass spectrometry (MS/MS) is an important technique in the determination of 
structural elements of compounds as well as for determination of different metabolic 
pathways and which compounds are part of the different metabolic pathways. 
The MS/MS method can be used on samples directly taken from a fungus or bacteria 
sample as well as other samples that consists of a mixture of different compounds that 
should be analysed to determine if the compounds are related. 
The basic instrumentation for MS/MS is a combination of a mass analysers and a 
collision cell. The type of instruments being used for MS/MS can vary but the basic 
==PAGE:34==
 
 
Page 34 
 
concept is that first the m/z of the ions are measured in the first part of the mass 
spectrometer just after the column, then the m/z is measured again after a charge has 
been inflicted upon the already charged ion to cause fragmentation.  
It is very important that the ion that is inflicted upon, can change from its original form 
and fragment into smaller pieces. It is important that the ion is not completely broken. 
The original ion should not fragment completely so that it is not present in the ion 
chromatogram if this happens the molecule is too broken for analysis. As long as a 
small amount of the original ion is still present it is possible to analyse the fragments. 
This will mean that the energy added to the ion was not so high that all is fragmented 
into small parts, making it possible for parts to fragment more than once. 
There are several different ways for ion fragmentation, but the most widely applied 
method is collisional ion activation (CID).  
CID is based on the conversion of translational energy of the precursor ion into internal 
energy. This is then used to collision within a neutral gas such as helium, argon or 
nitrogen, this is all done within a collision cell. 
The collision energy is in the range of 10 to 100 eV. Both primary and secondary ions 
from the original product can be observed, as primary product ions can undergo further 
collision within the collision cell and therefore further fragment.  
For analysis of the data generated from a MS/MS analysis it is possible to look in the 
data from the first MS to find the precursor ion, and then look at the fragmentation of 
the selected ion in the data from the MS/MS data. This way of looking at the data makes 
it possible to look into fragmentation of single ions within the sample as well as look at 
every ion within a given sample [Niessen, 2017]. 
 
1.8 Global natural products social molecular networking (GNPS) 
 
To understand the concept in MS/MS is that repeated MS/MS acquisition of a molecule 
when using the same experimental conditions (same instrument, collision energy, 
abundance and charge state) the MS/MS spectra should be highly similar, this means 
that the fragmentation of the molecule is consistent. This makes it possible to enable the 
identification of MS/MS spectra to use libraries of reference compounds. 
By knowing this the concept can be extended. In other words, structurally related 
molecules also exhibit similar fragmentation, with some differences. Molecules that 
differ by addition of single atom or moiety, such as methylation, glycosylation or 
oxidation, does not change the molecular mass and bond energies of the molecule and 
because of this it will not drastically alter the gas phase chemistry of the fragmentation. 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
Page 35 
 
If the relative bond strengths between the two molecules do not drastically differ, the 
same bond will therefore have a similar fragmentation pattern within the collision of the 
mass spectrometer. Therefore, the MS/MS spectra from two very similar compounds 
may result in having corresponding product ions. The only difference between them 
should be the difference in the chemical moiety.  
Since the product ions should be corresponding to the molecules the fragmentation 
should be very similar and the only difference in the MS/MS product ions should be as 
follows: 
1. Appear at the exact same mass for both molecules 
2. Appear shifted by the mass of the chemical moiety

All of these possible alignments are explored and the best alignment according to a 
score is determined and is shown in the MS/MS spectra and is called the alignment 
score. 
The number of potential alignments is exponential (2^N, where N is the number of 
peaks). GNPS has implemented an algorithm that is efficient at approximating the 
alignment and can therefore save a lot of computational power and therefore money. 
The alignment that has been described was only the alignment of one MS/MS spectrum 
to another, but this cannot create an entire molecular network, this must be done for an 
entire dataset of MS/MS spectra. 
Since it is impossible to know which of the MS/MS spectra will be structurally similar 
to others all of the MS/MS spectra will have to be aligned withing a dataset. The GNPS 
calculated the score between the different spectra and this is the starting point of the 
molecular network. 
A filtration is done is done between the alignment of all the MS/MS spectra, this is in 
order to produce the molecular networks so these can be visualized and interpretable. 
The alignment score filtration is a very basic filter and is done by alignment score 
between the MS/MS spectra. Generally, the default is set to 0.7 cosine and minimum of 
six peak matches. If the number is reduced the false positive alignments will increase. 
A use of the molecular network is promiscuous MS/MS spectra that align too many 
MS/MS spectra due to a low complexity of fragmentation.  
==PAGE:39==
 
 
Page 39 
 
A counter measure for this is that only alignments between alignments between MS/MS 
spectra that mutually rank each other highly in the alignment score. 
Even with filters like the filters described in this section there is occasionally a false 
positive connection withing the network.  
False positive connections can cause hairball networks that make visualization and 
interpretation very difficult.  
A connected component size filter can help this by setting a maximum size of a single 
molecular network this is set to 100 by default. This process will help remove hairball 
networks by removing the lowest scoring alignments until the resulting network falls 
below the maximum set size. 
Limitation of a molecular network is that the molecular network is unable to align 
spectra from molecules that differ by more than two structural modifications. 
An observation of the limitation of the ameliorated in biological dataset because 
chemical modifications are enzymatically catalysed. In the event that two molecules 
differ in two or more distinct chemical modifications which is impossible to connect 
using a molecular network. An intermediate molecule which differs in only a single 
chemical modification to the respective molecule and this will link all three molecules 
into a single molecular family [GNPS Documentation].

• Three agar plug (6 mm diameter) of the fungal culture was transferred to a vial

• The vials were placed in an ultrasonic bath at full effect for 60 min.  
• Extracts were then transferred to new vials. 
• Evaporated to dryness under N2, and re-dissolved in 300 µL of methanol for 
further sonication over 15 min.  
• After centrifugation at 13,400 rpm for 3 min., the supernatants were transferred 
to new vials and subjected to ultra-high performance liquid chromatography-

The advantage of GNPS is that it is possible for networking of very complex substrates, 
such as metabolites from one or more fungi. In this report the simplest version of a 
substrate with only one fungus has been seen. This fungus produces so many 
compounds that only a small fraction of the data is being explored within this report.

The data that is being explored in the report by the use of GNPS would be almost 
impossible to analyse without the use of GNPS networking first of all the compounds 
being analysed are very small and would easily be overlooked for this reason. The use 
of GNPS networking can help to determine if compounds are within the same metabolic 
pathway and it can help determine compounds that otherwise would be lost due to them 
being in such small amounts within the original sample. 
 
The main advantages of GNPS is that it finds and relates every single compound that 
has been subjected to MS/MS. This is unlike a manual analysis of the MS/MS 
compounds, where the number of compounds that would have to be analysed is outside 
what is feasible. 
The GNPS network is very useful for analysis of known compounds since it is possible 
to determine where in the network the specific compounds are by the use of the 
monoisotopic molecular mass of a certain compound. It is also very effective at 
analysing for unknown compounds, and their relation to each other and known 
compounds, by looking at large networks and determine the different related molecular 
masses. This analysis can be conducted within the original chromatogram to determine 
if they are within the same peak as other compounds in the network and therefore might 
not be a different compound. 
A sample made up of only compounds thought to be related to each other can be 
analysed using HPLC-QTOF-MS in MS/MS mode to determine if they are related to 
each other. This could for instance be done to the known compounds from Monilinia, 
and by analysing these in this way the relation between the compounds can be 
determined. 
Another advantage of the GNPS networking is that it will show every compound and 
every network, this can also leads to its main disadvantage that since every network is 
shown it will be hard to interpret every single network since these consist of anything 
from 3 to 50+ compounds that has to be determined within the original compound.  
The advantage of manual analysis is that it is a fast way to determine if main peaks are 
related to each other, it can also be used if wanting to determine if known compounds 
show similar fragmentation and therefore are in the same metabolic pathway. 
The main disadvantages of manual analysis are that it is easy to overlook small 
compounds and for that reason it cannot be used to analyse every single compound in a 
chromatogram. It can also be difficult to find compounds that are not closely related.

Conclusion 
From the data seen in this thesis it can be concluded that the manual analysis of MS/MS 
data can be used to determine if compounds are related, this is only optimal for analysis 
of known compounds that are main components in the chromatogram since small 
amounts are difficult to analyse. Manual analysis can be used to make a visual 
comparison. 
Target analysis is a very fast and easy way to determine if the fungus being analysed is 
actually the is what was expected, but it gives very little new information about the 
fungus. 
To look at more complex samples the manual analysis is not recommended instead 
GNPS networking analysis can be used since it is fast and more precise than manual 
comparison of adduct and fragment patterns. The GNPS networking uses calculations to 
determine if the compounds are related.  
It is always recommended to use GNPS analysis even when looking at known 
compounds since it is less labour intensive than a normal manual analysis of MS/MS 
data. 
 
 
Future perspective 
For future work it would be interesting to look at more complex samples, or even 
extracts from two or more fungi in one. 
Pure standards from Monilinia with known structures could be analysed using the 
GNPS networking this could be done by mixing the known compounds and then 
analysing them using the exact same method used in this report. 
More fungi that are well described should be analysed to determine if the GNPS 
network can be used to determine if the compounds share a metabolic pathway. 
The formulas suggested would require confirmation from either running a sample that is 
the same as the suggested compound to look for the UV spectra and the MS as well as 
elution time to conclude if the suggested formula is the same. 
To determine if the structures are as described the two main compounds should be 
purified and then their structure can be determined by using NMR to prove the 
structures proposed here. This could help to determine if the suggested structures are as 
described.
