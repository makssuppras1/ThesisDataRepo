Abstract
This thesis addresses the high amount of uncertainty present when deciding on
an optimal strategy for capacity expansions in the biopharmaceutical indus-
try. Uncertainties pertaining to demand, yield, and clinical trial success are
considered and evaluated through three different solution frameworks: deter-
ministic Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP), Stochastic Programming
(SP) with Recourse, and a Markov Decision Process (MDP) trained with Deep
Reinforcement Learning (DRL). The results show, that the SP model quickly
becomes infeasible due to the high dimensionality of the problem. The Rein-
forcement Learning (RL) based model outperforms the MILP model, but the
quality of it’s solutions also deteriorates drastically when complexity gets too
high. Therefore, while the potential of this solution approach that allows for
stochasticity in highly combinatorial observation spaces is shown, an improve-
ment of the policy network’s architecture is necessary for the RL model to be
a viable option for industrial use.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Decisions related to capacity planning are crucial for a firm’s performance.
Excess capacity can increase operational expenditures and costs associated
with capital expenditures. On the other hand, insuﬀicient capacity may incur
goodwill costs besides missing out on opportunities for growth and profits
(Krajewski et al., 2019, p. 163).
Capacity planning decisions can be aggregated to different levels. This the-
sis addresses capacity decisions in the mid- to long-term planning horizon —
strategic decisions concerning construction of new facilities and tech transfers1
between existing facilities. These decisions are far from trivial, as the biophar-
maceutical industry hosts one of the most unpredictable planning environment
in any industry due to an inherently high dimensionality of uncertainty. Dur-
ing planning, manufacturers must account for uncertainty related to the suc-
cess of potential drug candidates during clinical trials and licensing approvals,
definition of market size and share for candidate drugs, and performance of the
manufacturing process in terms of varying product yields (Joseph, 2018). The
latter uncertainty of varying product yields’ gravity is especially pronounced
in the biopharmaceutical industry, as biopharmaceuticals are defined as drugs
produced in biological systems such as microbial or yeast fermentations, mam-
malian or insect cell cultures, or from natural sources (Jagschies, 2018). This
is in contrast to traditional pharmaceuticals which are produced via chemical
synthesis.
When carrying out a technology transfer, the yield achieved often varies be-
tween facilities. While the process for manufacturing a biopharmaceutical drug

has to be very well defined, tech transfers are almost always accompanied by
changes in batch size, equipment configuration, facility designs, and proce-
dures. This creates a need for manufacturers to proactively anticipate changes
in process performance and product quality (Rathore et al., 2018). Even if the
production setup is apparently completely identical, unanticipated differences
may be the root cause of performance discrepancies. In a tech transfer case
study, cell culture growth rates could not be reproduced. After considerable
investigation, the culprit was found to be the cell culture medium used. The
vendor had two manufacturing sites, and although the two sites had the same
release specifications, the medium from one site consistently outperformed the
medium from the other (Hodge, 2018). These differences in performance can
be significant — e.g. the CEO of AstraZeneca states that their best perform-
ing site produces three times more vaccine out of a batch when compared to
their lowest producing site (Pandey, 2021).
This industry trait affects its manufacturers. Companies such as Novo Nordisk
have come under scrutiny for not being able to meet demand, be it either due
to underestimating demand or failing to anticipate supply chain disruptions.
Novo Nordisk has since played catch-up with the demand through large in-
creases in their budget for capital expenditures targeted towards capacity ex-
pansions. However, companies are also fearful of overreacting to drug shortages
and thereby creating overcapacity which may need to be divested at a later
period (Ryder, 2023). This case is not unique to Novo Nordisk. Among the 30
largest biopharmaceutical companies by revenue, the FDA is currently report-
ing2 198 Active Product Ingredient (API) shortages resulting in 630 product
shortages (FDA, 2023), as seen in figure 1.1.
Strategic capacity planning is set to become increasingly important. Studies
show that development of new drugs becomes increasingly complex, in turn
increasing their development time and thereby costs. This paired with a de-
creasing probability of clinical success3 causes both capacity and demand to be
increasingly diﬀicult to predict (DiMasi et al., 2016). At the heart of this prob-
lem lies strategic capacity planning as this can make or break the competitive
advantage of a firm due to the high expenditures such decisions entail.

1.2
Problem Statement
This thesis concerns itself with strategic capacity planning under three types
of uncertainty: demand uncertainty, uncertainty of clinical trial results, and
capacity uncertainty.
Demand uncertainty represents how popular the medicine will be in future
time periods, modeled as a continuous stochastic distribution. This popularity
is related to the clinical trial result, which ultimately has a binary outcome:
success or failure. If the clinical trial results in a failure, the product will not
be available for the public and hence the demand will be zero.
The capacity uncertainty is related to the nature of manufacturing for biophar-
maceutical companies – as these companies deal with drugs which are based
on living cells, there is high uncertainty of output when implementing produc-
tion in new facilities, even if the technology is transferred from another facility
with well documented standard operating procedures.
These uncertainties lead to important decisions regarding where to produce
which products, whether tech transfers are needed to fulfill demand, and
whether new facilities need to be built to fulfill demand. This is illustrated in
figure 1.2.
==PAGE:13==
1.2 Problem Statement
4
Figure 1.2: Illustration of problem addressed in thesis.
In summary, the problem this thesis addresses is that of providing a model for
decision support that biopharmaceutical companies can utilize when deciding
whether tech transfers and construction of new facilities are necessary. Fur-
thermore, this model should not only disclose whether aforementioned tech
transfers and facility constructions are necessary but also when and where
these should occur, i.e.
where should various products be producible and
when should this production ability be available.
To exhaustively investigate the problem statement, a series of research goals
have been devised:
1: Develop an environment mimicking how the manufacturing operations of a
biopharmaceutical firm interacts with its market.
This environment should encompass all the uncertainties mentioned in the
problem statement. While the modeling of uncertainties presented in the the-
sis’ environment may not accurately represent those of every biopharmaceuti-
cal firm, these can easily be altered to better represent case specific settings,
as it is assumed that this information is internally known to the biopharma-
ceutical company in question. Furthermore, as this thesis is centered around
manufacturing operations, the ultimate output of the environment should be
the cost of a production setup opposed to the generated sales profit.
2: Explore various solution frameworks for optimal decision support.
As the environment will encompass a large amount of cumulative uncertainty,
exact solution methods may be intractable. Whether this is true will be inves-
tigated alongside with alternative solution methods for this type of problem.
More specifically, the investigated frameworks are as follows:
==PAGE:14==
1.2 Problem Statement
5
• Deterministic Mixed Integer Linear Programming
• Stochastic Programming with Recourse
• Markov Decision Processes solved with Deep Reinforcement Learning
3: Evaluate the performance of the solution frameworks.
The performance each solution can achieve in the developed environment
should be determined. This allows the frameworks to be compared with each
other and thereby outline their individual strengths and weaknesses.
4: Draw managerial insights.
Creating decision support models for various scenarios using the optimal solu-
tion framework can highlight preferred decision trends. Trends for scenarios
with various production setups and accuracy of uncertain variables will be
investigated.
5: Identify future research areas.
While this thesis attempts to lessen the gap between the academic and practical
use of decision support models by accounting for more case specific considera-
tions than previous research has, the presented model still contains simplifica-
tions and assumptions. These are outlined to present the shortcomings of the
current model which can be addressed in future research.

CHAPTER 2
Literature Review
The topic of strategic capacity planning in the pharmaceutical industry as been
the subject of research for multiple decades. Levis and Papageorgiou (2004)
proposed a two-stage multi-scenario MILP model to determine the optimal
product portfolio, manufacturing network, investment strategy, and produc-
tion plan in a pharmaceutical setting. Although this model had an ambitious
output, the only uncertainty it accounted for was that of clinical trial outcomes.
Marques et al. (2018) also explored the same problem, but did include more
uncertainty in their model. Although they also identified the clinical success
uncertainty as being the most detrimental uncertainty due to its binary nature,
they did also include demand uncertainty in their model. Interestingly, Mar-
ques et. al used a Multi-Objective Integer Programming (MOIP) model for
their solution approach. This allowed them to create a Pareto frontier, such
that the decision maker is informed of the trade-off between productivity and
cost.
A multi-objective solution approach had previously been utilized by Lakhdar
et al. (2007), who conversely investigated the trade-off between production
costs and customer service levels. They included in their model the possibility
to force production on multiple facilities in case of facility outages. They then
investigated how this production flexibility affected the two goals when expe-
riencing scenarios of low, expected, and high demand. However, as the model
was solved deterministically, it never accounted for any uncertainty when plan-
ning production. Interestingly, their study showed that including the flexibility
constraint did not have any major impact on operational performance in the
low and expected demand scenarios.
Although not specifically targeted towards the pharmaceutical industry, Sabet
et al. (2020) also devised an optimization model for strategic capacity man-

agement. Noteworthy regarding this model is that they allowed for relocation
of the product portfolio assuming a lead time for this process, similar to the
principle of tech transfers in the pharmaceutical industry. However, since the
paper wasn’t targeted towards pharmaceuticals, the presented model does not
account for clinical success uncertainty — only demand volume and sales price
uncertainty.
Lin et al. (2014) proposed a Stochastic Dynamic Programming (SDP) model
with an embedded Linear Programming (LP) model to generate a dynami-
cally updated capacity plan in each period. In their numerical study the SDP
outperforms both deterministic and stochastic programming approaches. How-
ever, the authors also address that their SDP model may be computationally
infeasible for realistic problems when considering the dimensionality problem.
The authors therefore propose the use of approximate dynamic programming
or reinforcement learning algorithms to address these issues in future research.
These types of algorithms have since been extensively studied. K.-J. Wang
et al. (2018) devised a two-stage decision-making process modeled as an MDP,
where the first stage aims to select the optimal portfolio of (uncertain) emerg-
ing manufacturing technology. The second stage action is then modeled by a
stochastic programming model to optimally allocate the resources facing un-
certain demand. However, this approach was still limited by computational
requirements.
Harsha et al. (2022) directly addresses the challenges of RL when it is used
for problems with large combinatorial action spaces and state dependent con-
straints, such as problems containing allocation decisions.
They propose a
deep-policy iteration method which combines an approximated value function
from a neural network with mathematical programming. They show that their
framework provides promising results compared to other widely used RL algo-
rithms in the context of an inventory management problem.
So far, a common theme in the reviewed literature is that the presented re-
search papers do not consider yield uncertainty. However, while remaining in
the context of capacity planning, this has been subject for some research in
recent years — although not in the context of the biopharmaceutical indus-
try. In the investigated research, this uncertainty is typically a product of
==PAGE:17==

unforeseeable weather conditions.
Both air traﬀic control and airport slot allocation is heavily affected by weather.
The capacity for incoming flights, either to an airspace sector or an airport, is
dependent on the traﬀic controllers ability to manage said flights. However,
the amount of flights a traﬀic controller can safely manage depends on exter-
nal variables which are highly uncertain, such as weather forecasts, as budget
allocations are made too far in advance to meaningfully estimate some of the
uncertainties. However, these industries also have some considerable discrepan-
cies when compared to the capacity planning decisions in the biopharmaceuti-
cal industry. Traﬀic controllers can heavily impact demand through measures
such as rerouting or delaying flights. Furthermore, the reviewed literature only
considered decisions related to operational expenditures (salaries and demand
management costs). Thus, the aspect of sequential decision-making was not of
high importance, as the papers deliberately ignored long-term improvements
in capacity through airport runway expansion or air traﬀic control facility
upgrades (D. Wang and Zhao, 2020; Starita et al., 2020).
Another industry which is heavily affected by weather conditions is the agri-
cultural industry. In their state of the art review of uncertainty in agricultural
supply chain management, Borodin et al. (2016) show that this field has re-
ceived lots of attention. This review also shows that the notion of yield uncer-
tainty is well-documented within this industry. However, research addressing
yield uncertainty is predominantly focused towards optimizing planting and
harvesting policies — not strategic capacity planning decisions. However, Gol-
mohammadi and Hassini (2019) do investigate how to optimize profits when
yield is uncertain and capacity is variable from period to period through pur-
chases and sales of farming land. The basis of their model is a news vendor
problem with pricing, where they introduce the concept of ”expected demand
fill rate elasticity” to account for both demand and supply uncertainty. How-
ever, their model only considers cases with a single type of crop and scenar-
ios where available farmland can be expanded continuously. Therefore, the
proposed model is not suitable for a biopharmaceutical setting with multiple
products and facilities.
From this literature review, a gap in research targeted directly towards strate-

tified. Although all the necessary topics are addressed separately, no single
study considers decisions regarding capacity expansion, product relocation and
production allocation in a problem setting with both multiple products and
facilities while assuming an existing portfolio and taking uncertainties regard-

therefore be to address all of these topics. It should however be mentioned that

on long-term strategic planning. The justification for this is, that a more de-

CHAPTER 3
Methodology
Various solution frameworks will be used to assess the optimal capacity expan-
sion strategy for a biopharmaceutical company. A commonly used approach
to capacity planning is through the use of a MILP model. This does however
have some shortcomings — a deterministic MILP model would fail to consider
the stochasticity of a realistic environment. However, a SP model may quickly
become intractable as the problem setting grows in size due to the curse of
dimensionality. Therefore, a third solution approach based on Markov Deci-
sion Processes is proposed. Finally, after implementing these three solution
approaches, they will be benchmarked against one another to determine their
strengths and shortcomings.

Since the stochastic variables in the model have many dimensions, some model-
ing assumptions must be made to reduce the amount of scenarios — otherwise
the problem would become intractable before it could be applied to any real-
istic scenario. The assumptions are therefore:

continue to do so for all periods in the planning horizon.

3.3
Markov Decision Process
The final solution framework consists of an MDP, which considers the interface
between an agent which learns and makes decisions, and an environment which
defines the current states and provides rewards based on the agents action
(Sutton and Barto, 2018). This interface is commonly illustrated as seen in
figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Interface between environment and agent in an MDP.
In this section we will discuss dynamics of the environment, the observable
states, the actions of the agent, the rewards, and finally the algorithm used to
train the agent.
3.3.1
Environment
The environment should mimic the dynamics of the interaction between supply
and demand in a biopharmaceutical company. To achieve this, the environ-
ment receives a sequence of actions and returns the cost of said actions. The
environment consists of a set of deterministic states which can be directly
==PAGE:30==
3.3 Markov Decision Process
21
influenced by the company, and a set of stochastic states which cannot be in-
fluenced by the company. The logic of the evaluation environment is illustrated
in figure 3.2.
Figure 3.2: Logic of evaluation environment.
The action variables relate to when the agent decides to initiate construction
of a new facility or initiate a tech transfer. Once the action is initiated, a
predetermined period of time will pass before the facility is available or the
product is producible at the given facility. Hence, a modeling assumption is
made here, as the possibility for delays and disruptions is ignored.
The MDP ends once the agent reaches the terminal state condition, which
is dependent on the period t – since T is described as the planning horizon,
the terminal state condition is reached once the agent continues beyond this
horizon. Hence, the model is a finite MDP.
The stochastic states were modeled as described in chapters 3.3.1.1, 3.3.1.2,
and 3.3.1.3. The environment has been implemented to Python as seen in
appendix C.

3.3.3
Actions
We wish to take actions that ensure that we can meet demand, but that also
minimize capacity expansion costs. Therefore, actions that need to be consid-

master production schedule also needs to be considered. However, the purpose
of this is not to choose the final production schedule, but rather to determine
whether demand can be met for the given period. Furthermore, since we ag-
gregate periods to be more suitable for long-term planning, the granularity of
a production schedule produced through this model would not provide much
benefit to a decision maker. Due to this, the action of production allocation is
deferred to the reward function. This is described in section 3.3.4.
We are left with two types of decisions — facility constructions and tech trans-
fers. If all these decisions were to be accounted for each period in one single
action, the action space A would grow incredibly large. Since all possible com-
binations of actions need to be considered, the action space would have a size
of |A|= 2|F|+|F|·|P|. Besides the action space quickly growing unwieldy large,
many of the actions will also be unfeasible.
Since this would considerably hinter the training performance of the MDP, the
action space is structured differently. Rather than including all the decisions
in a single action per period, multiple actions are allowed each period, where
each action then in turn only contains a single decision. A final type of de-
cision needs to be added to the action spaces — an indication that no more
investments need to be made in the period. The action space now has a size
of |A|= |F|+|F|·|P|+1. The action space then becomes:
A = {Uf, Vf,p, ∅}
(3.59)
==PAGE:36==
3.3 Markov Decision Process
27
Where Uf represents all the facilities f that can be constructed, Vf,p represents
all the possible tech transfers of product p to facility f, and ∅represents that
no more investments are to be made in the current period. Although the action
space has diminished considerably, we can still optimize the training process
by removing state-dependent illegal actions. Illegal actions include:
1. Facility construction of facilities which have already or are currently be-
ing constructed.
2. Tech transfers of products which are already producible at the facility.
3. Tech transfers of products to facilities which have not yet been con-
structed.
4. Tech transfers of products which have failed their clinical trials.
5. Tech transfers which would exceed a facility’s production capacity for a
given period due to the requirement of test batches during tech transfers.
6. Unnecessarily early tech transfers of a product. (Optional)
Here, violating one of the five first constraints would always lead to an infea-
sible or sub-optimal solution. However, the sixth constraint is slightly more
subjective, as it is up to the decision maker to describe ”unnecessarily early”.
For the implementation used in this thesis, unnecessarily early was described
as anything preceding the period a product was supposed to be released to the
market minus the time required for a tech transfer. However, for some specific
cases a larger time buffer for tech transfers may be required, as this constraint
otherwise may interact negatively with the fifth action constraint.
The implementation of action masking is done as proposed by Huang and On-
tañón (2020), who show that the policy gradients remain valid, that achieved
episode reward can be improved, and that the necessary training iterations
can be reduced when action masking.
The logic of the action masking is as follows: The policy function πθ of the
model uses a neural network to output unnormalized logits and then converts
them into an action distribution using a softmax function. For an unmasked
version of the policy function, this could be represented as follows:
==PAGE:37==
3.3 Markov Decision Process
28
πθ(·|st) = softmax(Lout)
(3.60)
Where πθ is the parameterized policy function and Lout are the output logits
from the final layer of the neural network. The softmax function then ensures
that the output logits are normalized such that they are in a range of 0 to 1
and all sum up to 1. When applying the action mask, the policy function then
transforms to the following:
πθ(·|st) = softmax(mask(Lout))
(3.61)
The mask function ensures that a large negative number is added to any output
node in Lout which represents an illegal action as described earlier in this
section. When passing a large negative number through the softmax function
the resulting probability of this action will become virtually 0, ensuring that
the agent will not pick this illegal action.

and hyperparameters into poorly-performing trials at predefined perturbation
intervals. It can then explore new hyperparameter configurations, either by
altering the current configuration slightly, or by randomly sampling new hyper-
parameter values. In all numerical experiments, the hyperparameters subject
to tuning were λ, γ, ε, c1 c2, and the learning rate.
3.4
Model Evaluation
To evaluate the performance of each solution framework, their performance are
assessed in a simulated biopharmaceutical environment. As the environment
used to train the MDP model was constructed to mimic a biopharmaceutical
setting, it can be repurposed for this task. The main Key Performance Index
(KPI) to determine the superior solution framework is the average total episode
cost, with an episode consisting of a set of individual periods.
The total
episode cost can be separated into backlog costs, tech transfer costs, and facility
construction costs. This separation may provide additional information to a
decision maker, as the optimal distribution of these costs may be subjective.
Finally, the spread of the total episode costs may also be a valuable KPI to
the decision maker, as their subjective risk willingness may affect their choice.

the limited population size.

4.3.3
Process Flexibility
An important concept when addressing uncertain product demand and manu-
facturing capacity is process flexibility, i.e. the ability to build different types
of products in the same facility. This is a topic addressed in the seminal pa-
per by Jordan and Graves (1995) where they share some insights regarding
preferable flexibility principles. Most notably is, that a similar service level
achieved through total flexibility (every facility can produce every product) can
==PAGE:56==
4.3 Discussion
47
be achieved through a limited amount of links between products and facilities.
The requirement is that the network representing manufacturing capabilities
should form a chain, such that each product can be reached by following the
edges in the network. It can be useful to think of this network as a bipartite
graph with |F|+|P| nodes – a node for each facility and product. The edges
between products and facilities then represent the manufacturing capabilities.
Besides chaining manufacturing capabilities, this chain should also be cyclic
such that each product has the same probability to benefit from the flexibil-
ity in the network. The network representing the manufacturing setup of the
medium case following the RL model’s policy can be seen in 4.14. The width
of the edges represent how often each connection between product and facil-
ity is established if the product successfully passes clinical trials. Table 4.5
provides information regarding each product’s demand profile, summarized as
small, medium, or large, and their respective probability to pass clinical trials.

From this brief analysis we can observe that the RL model ensures that a
lot of flexibility is present in the network. However, some of the edges also
indicate some sub-optimal tech transfer decisions occur: product 1 is always
made producible at every constructed facility. Furthermore, product 2, 3, and
4 each have roughly 63% probability of being tech transferred to every con-
structed facility. The Jordan and Graves paper indicate that the service-level
gains from this approach are minimal compared to more sparingly chaining
the manufacturing capabilities. However, the high backlog-costs inherent in
the biopharmaceutical industry may signify that these tech transfers aren’t
necessarily excessive as even a small increase in service-level can reduce costs
considerably. What remains peculiar is that product 1 gets transferred more
often than product 2 in spite of having identical demand, and also gets trans-
ferred more often than product 4 which both has higher demand and thereby
also higher uncertainty. This shows that, although a highly robust solution
is both necessary and identified by the RL model, some improvements to the
production network may still be possible.
4.3.4
Future Research
In section 4.3 it was identified, that further research towards optimizing the
policy network which governs the agent’s decisions is required for the RL model
to remain viable for larger use-cases. This policy network needs to both be able
to contain a large amount of features, but also better utilize time-dependent
information such as demand forecasts and clinical trials.
Throughout this
thesis the same configuration of hidden layers in the policy network has been
used for all experiments – 2 fully connected layers with 256 nodes each. A
simple attempt at improving the policy network could therefore be to scale
the width and depth of these hidden layers according to the size of the case.
Another suggestion to improve the policy network could be to implement self-
attention in an attempt to capture interactions between different features in
the observation space. Although self-attention is commonly used in sequential
neural networks (Vaswani et al., 2017) it has also been successfully used for
fixed-length representations in the context of PPO (Manchin et al., 2019).
==PAGE:58==
4.3 Discussion
49
Optimizing such a policy network could inadvertently also mitigate issues with
non-stationarity, assuming that a policy network which is more robust against
complexity can also provide decision support for a wider array of scenarios if
trained appropriately.
Once a more robust RL model is developed through aforementioned ameliora-
tion, the scope of its competencies can then be expanded. To further resemble
reality, more uncertainties in the form of supply chain disruptions, expansion
lead time variation, and product development lead time variation could be
included in the environment. Furthermore, the only current distinguishable
factors between facilities are construction cost and yield. This is rarely the case
in reality. Facility location can have high impact on logistics costs throughout
the entirety of a facility’s lifetime. Furthermore, strategic factors which may
be diﬀicult to convert to exact costs may be beneficial to include in the model.
Some of these strategic factors could suitably be pertaining to frameworks such
as PESTEL or Ghemawat’s CAGE framework (Ghemawat, 2007). Common
for these two frameworks are, that they assist in measuring the fit between a
company and a potential new market through cultural, political, environmen-
tal, geographical, administrative, and economic factors, among others.
Finally, investigating the RL model’s ability to consider the trade-off between
cost and service level could provide valuable insights for the decision maker
– reminiscent of the MOIP solution approaches by Marques et al. (2018) and
Lakhdar et al. (2007).

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
Throughout this thesis, three solution frameworks have been successfully de-
veloped. Each framework displayed various strengths and weaknesses:
The SP model did show potential to perform very well as it performance was
similar to the rolling horizon MILP model in spite of having very limited op-
tions for recourse. However, the scale and complexity of the capacity planning
problem rendered the SP model useless in all but the very smallest case, as
the amount of scenarios quickly grew too large to identify an optimal solution.
The deterministic MILP model had the advantage of always being able to
converge towards an optimal solution, although the optimal solution did not
consider any stochasticity. In spite of this inadequacy, the model did provide
usable solutions when recourse decisions was allowed, as shown in the rolling
horizon evaluation. This may be attributed to the necessary amount of fa-
cility constructions to meet demand having relatively low variance, and the
uncertainty during tech transfers decisions being low due to their shorter lead
time.
For all but the largest Novo Nordisk case, the RL model outperformed the
two other frameworks.
This is in spite of the model only being optimized
once during the first period of the simulation. This shows that the potential
advantage of the RL model is larger than indicated in the numerical results,
as reducing the magnitude of non-stationarity was shown to have a positive
impact on the solution quality. Besides less costs on average than the other
solution frameworks, the RL model also provided very robust solutions as it
prioritized having high process flexibility to account for uncertainty in the
environment. However, the quality of the RL model’s solutions deteriorated
as complexity increased. Therefore, an improvement of the policy network’s
architecture is necessary for the RL model to remain viable when providing
==PAGE:60==
5 Conclusion
51
decision support to cases with many products and facilities or highly time-
dependent uncertainty during a decision.
Finally, extensions to the biopharmaceutical environment, which was both
used to train the RL agent and evaluate each solution framework, were iden-
tified.
These extensions included modeling quality improvements, to more
realistically represent a biopharmaceutical environment, and decision support
improvements, to provide solutions better tailored for the individual decision
maker.
