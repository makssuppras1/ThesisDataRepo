Abstract
A recent novel design methodology has allowed assembly of ultrathin freestanding oxide mem-
branes into 3D artiﬁcial heterointerfaces. This methodology opens a wider range of degrees of
freedom for the stacking of monolayers, such as di↵erent materials or layer orientation, which
allows tuning of the material properties. The ability to change the layer orientation gives rise
to an inﬁnite number of possible conﬁgurations. This makes a comprehensive study impossible
with density functional theory. Therefore, we present a computational workﬂow to generate a
force ﬁeld to provide fundamental understanding of how to engineer the structure as well as the
ionic transport across and along the heterointerfaces.
We create a machine learning force ﬁeld (ML-FF) using a message-passing neural network
trained on density functional theory data at the PBE+U level. We present the design of an au-
tomatic end-to-end workﬂow to generate train data. The workﬂow starts by creating structures
from two ultrathin perovskite oxides with the Moir´e patterns arising from a twist angle. Then it
utilizes molecular dynamics and active learning to reduce the computational cost of train data
generation. The result is a highly accurate force ﬁeld.
The workﬂow is applied to study bilayer SrTiO3.
We show that the trained ML-FF is
highly accurate allowing for an extensive study of the bilayer stacking parameters. We ﬁnd that
twisting results in a change in the local coordination environment at the interface. This has been
shown to have an impact on the oxygen vacancy concentration and migration energy barriers
suggesting that twisting is an e↵ective tuning parameter for ionic conductivity. Thus by using
new methods, this work lays the foundation for future comprehensive studies of heterointerfaces
between ultrathin freestanding oxide membranes.

Introduction
The worlds rapidly growing energy demand combined with the requirement for sustainable
energy presents one of the major challenges of today. In order to meet this challenge, we need
new technological developments such as fuel cells, batteries or catalysis. Complex metal oxides
exhibit promising properties for these required developments. Therefore, studying new ways of
optimizing the use of such materials can be one of the keys to reach the goal of sustainability.
Recent advances in fabrication techniques have enabled the assembly of artiﬁcial heterostruc-
tures made of ultrathin freestanding oxide ﬁlms [1].
Transition-metal oxides (TMO) show
promising applications due to the strong electronic interactions especially from the 3d orbital
electrons. At heterointerfaces between TMOs, novel phenomena have been shown such as su-
perconductivity [2], thermoelectricity [3], and ferromagnetism [4].
It is well known that two-dimensional (2D) materials can o↵er unique properties when used
as building blocks for heterostructures through monolayers held together by van der Waals forces
[5]. The weak forces allow the layers to be fabricated with a relative twist angle ✓, which can
have a signiﬁcant e↵ect on the properties of the material [6]. For example bilayer graphene
shows superconductivity at a ”magical” angle of ✓= 1.1◦[7].
Similar to stacking of 2D materials, the new fabrication techniques of oxide heterostructures
allows for control of the stacking and twist angle introducing key degrees of freedom in the
process [1]. This allows for ”Moir´e engineering”, known as such for the Moir´e patterns that
arise when introducing a twist angle [8]. However, unlike 2D materials, TMOs do not bond by
van der Waals forces, changing the required understanding of the interface bonding.
Strontium titanate (SrTiO3, STO) is a prototypical example of a perovskite oxide. STO has
shown interesting properties such as high electron mobility via doping [9], 2D electron gas [10],
and ionic conductivity [11][12]. It has been shown that surface e↵ects are important in STO such
as allowing a tuning of ON/OFF of pyroelectric e↵ect in the material [13]. Ionic conductivity
of oxygen defects is important for applications such as solid oxide fuel cells, separation mem-
branes, or electrolyzers, and has been widely studied for bulk STO using rigid-ion potentials
[14][15][16][17]. Additionally, the material has also been studied using machine learning force
ﬁelds for structural phase transitions [18] and phonon transport [19].
This report presents a computational workﬂow to generate a force ﬁeld that can be used to
study the design parameters in ”Moir´e engineering”. The vast number of conﬁgurational options
when twisting and stacking requires a fast computational method, so density functional theory
is insuﬃcient. Therefore, we train a machine learning force ﬁeld using a message-passing neural
network on density functional theory data. This way, we can combine the accuracy of ab initio
calculations with the computational eﬃciency of a machine learning model. The workﬂow uses
a combination of molecular dynamics and active learning to speed up the learning process.
The aim of this study is twofold:
• Design a workﬂow to generate an accurate force ﬁeld, by training message-passing neural
network models on data generated by density functional theory simulations, combined
with molecular dynamics and an active learning scheme for eﬃcient data generation.
• Study the e↵ect of twisting, translation, and stacking on stability and ionic conductivity in
bilayer STO. This case study serves as a demonstration for how the workﬂow methodology
can be used to study ionic conductivity in a wide range of heterogeneous oxide interfaces.
Section 2 summarizes the theory behind the tools used in the workﬂow. Additionally, the
theory of Moir´e patterns in bilayers and how di↵usion can be studied computationally are
presented. Section 3 outlines how the speciﬁc steps of the workﬂow are performed. Section
4 presents the results of applying the workﬂow to STO. Both the accuracy of the produced
machine learning force ﬁelds are presented and insights regarding assembly of STO bilayer are
shown. Section 5 provides a discussion of the results presented in section 4.

In principle, solving the Schr¨odinger equation for the ground state of a system allows one to
ﬁnd various properties such as the enthalpy of formation. However, the many-body interactions
in the system means there are 3(P+N) coupled degrees of freedom, which cannot be decoupled
due to the Coulumb interaction. Therefore, the equation is not feasible to solve exactly, and
instead various approximations are employed in a trade-o↵to reduce the computational com-
plexity with as little accuracy loss as possible. The following chapter will outline the theory
behind various methods in pursuit of the best outcome in this trade-o↵.
First the ab initio framework of density functional theory (DFT) is presented. DFT provides
highly accurate results for a variety of problems at a reasonable computational cost for small
systems of atoms. Next, how to probe the dynamics of a system using molecular dynamics
(MD) is shown. While combining DFT and MD for ab initio molecular dynamics (AIMD) has a
reasonable computational cost for small systems and time span, when it is required to increase
either of these factors, the cost becomes excessive. Another approach is to use machine learning
(ML) as a function approximation for the DFT calculations. The ML method is much faster
than DFT, enabling calculations for more demanding systems.

2.1.3
Density functional theory
Another approach is to use the full electronic density as the fundamental variable of the many-
body problem. This was ﬁrst done by Thomas [22] and Fermi [23] by taking the kinetic, ex-
change, and correlation contributions from the homogeneous electron gas where good approxi-
mations are known [21]. This approach was later given mathematical support by two theorems
by Hohenberg and Kohn (HK).
HK theorem I states that the external potential is univocally determined by the electronic
density, besides a trivial additive constant [21]. As a corollary to this, if the density determines
the external potential vext(r) it also determines the ground state wave function Φ. In other
words, the whole system is completely described by the electron density in the ground state.

which has a minimum at the true ground state density and the value is the ground state energy

2.3
Machine learning force ﬁeld
When performing molecular dynamics, empirical FFs have the advantage of eﬃciency while
AIMD has the advantage of accuracy. A machine learning force ﬁeld (ML-FF) attempts to
combine these advantages. A ML model has the eﬃciency of empirical FFs while approaching
the accuracy of ab initio if trained suﬃciently. Additionally, no preconceived notion of bonding
patterns is required, allowing a ﬂexibility of the model if trained on suitable reference data [34].

2.3.2
Physical constraints
The deep FFNN model in equation 39 has no built in notion of physical or chemical theory and
can therefore violate natures laws. This will lead to both unphysical results and increases the
degrees of freedom for learning [34]. By using models that enforce physical constraints additional
information is inherent, which improves data eﬃciency and accuracy. This is important for ML-
FF as the DFT training data required is computationally expensive.
There are three constraints that should be fulﬁlled [34]. The ﬁrst constraint is energy con-
servation because these are closed systems so there are no outside forces. The kinetic energy
acquired by the atoms has to be equal to the loss in potential energy and vice-versa. The con-
straint is satisﬁed if the force is calculated by taking the gradient of the force ﬁeld as in equation
30. Conversely, care has to be taken if forces are predicted directly by the model as not every
vector ﬁeld is a valid gradient ﬁeld.
9
==PAGE:16==
The second constraint is that identical atoms should be indistinguishable. Switching the
coordinates of two atoms of the same type should not cause any physical di↵erence. However,
since all weights are generally di↵erent in a FFNN such a swap will cause a di↵erence in the
model output [33]. Additionally, the model is limited to a ﬁxed number of atoms, since the
weights are trained for a ﬁxed input size.
The ﬁnal constraint is rototranslational invariance, ie. E(R) = E(RR + T ) where R&T are
respectively rigid rotation and translation of the cartesian coordinates. These stems from the
conservation of linear and angular momentum. The result is that the energy only depends on
the relative position of the atoms. A way to incorporate this invariance in the model is to use
the pairwise distances and bond angles instead of position as the input [34].
The last two constraints have a signiﬁcance for how the atomic arrangement is numerically
represented, called the ﬁngerprint, such that it can be used as an input to the machine learning
model [36]. The ﬁngerprint has to fulﬁll the constraints, while uniquely representing the atomic
neighborhood. The radial distribution function is an example of such a ﬁngerprint.

2.5
Reduction of Ti
Analysis of the atomic charges in bulk STO has shown an ionic character for the Sr2+ bonding,
and a hybridization between the Ti4+ and O2−revealing a degree of covalency in their bonding
[43]. When introducing a neutral oxygen vacancy V⇥
O the two extra electrons will either localize
on Ti ions or delocalize. In the localized case, the electrons should either reside on two Ti ions
reducing them to Ti3+ or a single Ti reducing it to Ti2+. In the delocalized case, the extra
electrons are shared by a ﬁnite fraction of Ti ions. Finally, there is the combination where one
electron is localized and the other delocalized.
GGAs are unable to properly describe the strong correlation e↵ects of the Ti 3d states as
shown by an underestimation of the band gap by as much as 40-50% [43]. To study the e↵ect
of the functional Valentin et al. [44] therefore studied the reduction of Ti in TiO2 using PBE,
PBE+U, and the hybrid functional B3LYP. At the PBE level the results show full delocalization
of both electrons, which is inconsistent with experimental results. The B3LYP approach ﬁnds
three solutions: (a) localization at two di↵erent Ti ions (but not necessarily the closest under-
coordinated Ti ions), (b) localization at an undercoordinated Ti ion and delocalization of the
other electron, (c) delocalization of both electrons. The PBE+U results gave (a) and (b) type
solutions with electronic structure similar to the B3LYP approach. Thus, the results in general
show localization of one of the electrons on an undercoordinated Ti ion. The localization of
the second electron is less certain with both localized and delocalized solutions, however, the
di↵erence in energy between these two solutions is small.

Procedure
3.1
Nomenclature
The following nomenclature is used to refer to speciﬁc atomic positions:
• Conﬁguration: the initial bilayer that is created from a given set of rotation, translation,
and stacking of two layers.
• Structure: atomic positions of the relaxed conﬁguration.
• Image: the atomic positions for a given step in molecular dynamics.
• Pristine structure: a structure with no vacancies
• Vacancy structure: a structure with vacancies
The following abbreviations are used:
• ML-FF: machine learning force ﬁeld. The PaiNN model used for the force ﬁeld.
• AIMD: ab initio molecular dynamics. Molecular dynamics calculated using DFT.
• ML-MD: machine learning molecular dynamics. Molecular dynamics calculated using the
ML-FF.
Several force ﬁelds are trained on di↵erent data, and several test sets are made which will be
called:
• Xn-FF: force ﬁeld trained using a speciﬁc data set. X 2 (P, V ) refers to training on
only pristine data (P) or a combination of pristine and vacancy data (V). n refers to
the iteration in the workﬂow, ie 0 is trained on only initialisation data, 1 is trained on
initialisation+iteration 1 data etc.
• MD 2000K test: last 5% of MD images for conﬁgurations in the initialisation.
• Relaxation test: relaxation data for conﬁgurations not in the initialisation.
• MD 5000K test: MD images run at 5000K for conﬁgurations not in the initialisation. See
section 3.2.2 for more information.
3.2
Workﬂow
To study the properties of a given material, the theoretical components outlined in section 2
are combined in a computational iterative workﬂow designed to automatically create a data
set that, with enough iterations, will produce an accurate FF. The goal of the workﬂow is to
generate a diverse train set while minimising the computational time by performing as little
DFT calculations as possible. The challenge is twofold: ﬁrstly to generate realistic images that
are relevant for the training, and secondly to pick the most relevant of these images to label
with DFT. The ﬁrst part is solved by training a FF and using this to run ML-MD to generate
a pool of images. To solve the second part, the ML model has a built in uncertainty, so an
AL algorithm can select the subset of images from the pool that is most likely to provide new
information for the model.
Figure 4 shows an overview of the workﬂow, with the 4 repeated steps: ML-FF training,
MD, AL, and ﬁnally labeling. To start the iterations, an initialisation step creates a data set
for the ﬁrst ML-FF training. The workﬂow can brieﬂy be summarised as:
18
==PAGE:25==
1. Create an initial data set by generating a set of conﬁgurations, then relax the structure
with DFT, and perform AIMD for each conﬁguration using the relaxed structure as a
starting point.
2. Train a ML-FF until the model is converged.
3. Run ML-MD, until the uncertainty is too large, to create a pool of images.
4. Use AL to select the most uncertain images from the pool.
5. Calculate the energy and forces for the selected images using DFT. Update the data set
with the newly labeled images.
6. Repeat steps 2-5 until the ML-FF is suﬃciently accurate.
The workﬂow is based on an in-house implementation written by PhD student Xin Yang in
Python. In addition, I implemented the algorithm to create the Moir´e unit cell based on the
methodology outlined in section 2.4. The Vienna ab initio Simulation Package is used for DFT
calculations (see section 3.2.1 for details). The Atomic Simulation Environment [49] is used for
the molecular dynamics and nudged elastic band calculations.
The workﬂow is utilised twice to create two models, one with pristine conﬁgurations and
one with vacancy conﬁgurations. The pristine model is trained for two iterations, resulting in
a FF (P2-FF) suitable for pristine calculations. Then the initialisation is performed again for
vacancy conﬁgurations. The pristine data and vacancy initialisation data is combined as the
initial data set for the vacancy model. The workﬂow is run again for two iterations, resulting in
a FF (V2-FF) suitable for defect calculations. Note that relaxation data from the initialisation
was not included in the data set for the pristine model, but it was decided to include it for the
vacancy model.

To perform the DFT calculations, we use the Vienna ab initio Simulation Package (VASP)
code using the projector augmented wave (PAW) method [50][51]. PBE is used as the exchange-
correlation functional. The cuto↵energy is set to 500 eV and k-point density to 0.5 ˚A−1. We use
the Gaussian smearing method with σ = 0.03. To correct for vdW forces the DFT-D3 method
is used. The DFT+U method [25] is used with U = 5.8, J = 0 correction on the Ti 3d orbitals,
which has been found to be suﬃcient to describe the Ti 3d states [52][53].
Vacuum of 12˚A is introduced in the z-direction (as measured from the top atom position)
to avoid the e↵ect of periodic boundary condition. Even with this amount of vacuum, there can
still be dipole e↵ects from the non-existent repeated layer. To account for this, dipole correction
is turned on in the z-direction.
In the relaxation calculations, the electronic self-consistency cycle is stopped at an energy
di↵erence of 10−6 eV between iterations, and the structures are relaxed until the maximum force
was less than 0.03 eV/˚A. In the MD runs, the break for the SC cycle was set to 10−5 eV. The
MD uses Langevin dynamics with γ = 0.001 at a temperature of 2000K, and a timestep of 1 fs.
Spin polarisation is turned o↵for the non-vacancy structures and on for the vacancy struc-
tures.
Based on the results by Di Valentin et al.
[44] discussed in section 2.5, the initial
magnetic moment is set to 1 for the undercoordinated Ti ions. To reduce a degree of freedom,
a ferromagnetic solution is enforced by setting nupdown to 2.

3.2.3
Molecular dynamics
Once the ML-FF is converged, the FF is used to run MD. The FF-MD uses the relaxed DFT
structure as the starting point. We run an FF-MD simulation for each conﬁguration in the ini-
tialisation. This means 21 MD simulations are run in the pristine phase, and 25 MD simulations
in the vacancy phase. The goal with running this MD is to generate realistic images that the
ML-FF can learn from once labeled. Identical to the AIMD, Langevin dynamics is used with
γ = 0.001 at a temperature of 2000K with a timestep of 1fs.
The MD is run for 1000ps or until the uncertainty in the ensemble is too large. The un-
certainty is calculated as the standard deviation in the force prediction of the 6 models. If the
standard deviation goes above 0.2 eV/˚A, the MD run is immediately stopped. This hard stop
is designed to stop the MD before unphysical images are generated. If the standard deviation
goes above 0.1 eV/˚A (and below 0.2 eV/˚A) the image is noted as uncertain. If 1000 uncertain
images are collected the MD is stopped. The pool for the AL is the images noted as uncertain
and every 100 image of the MD.
24
==PAGE:31==
3.2.4
Active learning
Active learning is applied to the pool of images created in the ML-MD step. The distance is
calculated using the full gradient kernel as explained in section 2.3.5 with 500 random projec-
tions. Largest Cluster Maximum Distance is used as the selection method. 50 images are chosen
per conﬁguration. This means approximately 1050 images are added to the data set after each
iteration in the pristine phase (21 ⇥50), and 1250 (25 ⇥50) in the vacancy phase. Note this
number might be lower if some of the calculations do not converge.
3.2.5
Labeling
The labeling step uses the same DFT parameters as the relaxation in the initialisation except
for two parameters. Firstly, it is snapshot calculations and not relaxation, so the requirement of
maximum force less than 0.03 eV/˚A is removed. Secondly, the calculation is run for a maximum
of 60 (pristine) or 400 (vacancy) electronic self-consistency cycles. The calculation is stopped if
not converged within this limit, and the image is not added to the data set.
3.3
Descriptors
This section formally introduces some of the descriptors used to analyse the e↵ect of rotation,
translation, and stacking.
The interlayer distance is meant to represent how tightly packed the two layers of the bilayer
are. In the simple cases, this distance can be chosen as the atom distance from top to bottom
(A), bottom to bottom (B), or top to top (C) as shown in ﬁgure 10. However, when moving to
more complex structures, these representations become inadequate.
Figure 11 shows another seemingly simple case, where the layers are simply bent upwards.
While this speciﬁc case would not happen, it is meant to illustrate the issues that occur in the
much more complex reconﬁgurations. Method A and C completely fail to capture the packing,
which in this case is best represented by d. These issues occur because atoms far away from
each other in the xy-plane are compared, ie if the bending is less/more steep the result changes,
while d is constant. B performs well in this case, however, notice that the metric is still very
sensitive to the movement of the single Sr atom at the top right.
To solve these issues, we use the method illustrated in ﬁgure 11 to calculate the interlayer
distance. For a given atom in the top layer, all the z-coordinate distances to the atoms in the
bottom layer are collected. This is done only for atoms in a circle of diameter 2a in the xy-plane,
where a is the unit cell length in bulk. We do this for all atoms in the top layer. The interlayer
distance is calculated as the mean of all the collected distances. Using a circle in the xy-plane
introduce a locality to ﬁx the issue of A and C. Collecting all of the distances and averaging
solves the sensitivity issue of B.

A way to conﬁrm that the elongated bonds (> 2.1˚A) occur at the interface, and not at the
surface, is that there are no long bonds for the SrSr interface. There are no Ti-O bonds at the
SrSr interface, so the short bonds occur at the surface. In general, Ti3+ has a larger Ti-O bond
length, likely due to the larger radius of Ti3+ vs Ti4+, and the reduced repulsion that the O
experience. However, there is still a signiﬁcant overlap between the Ti3+-O and Ti4+-O bond
lengths.
The coordination in ﬁgure 15 show that when creating bilayers, the usual Ti-O coordination
of 6 in the bulk case is reduced to 5, as is expected since a bilayer is essentially two surfaces of
the bulk case. However, a little less than half of the Ti are able to recover the 6-fold coordination
at the interface. A greater proportion of the Ti in the TiTi interface are able to achieve the
6-fold coordination. In the vacancy case, some of the Ti3+ are unable to reconﬁgure suﬃciently
so ends in a 4-fold coordination.

Discussion
5.1
Machine learning force ﬁeld
The improvement in the learning curves (ﬁg 16) and accuracy tests (ﬁg 17) show that the
workﬂow is an e↵ective method to achieve a progressively more accurate ML-FF. By creating a
pool of likely MD images and selecting from these using an AL scheme, the workﬂow eﬃciently
selects diverse new train data (ﬁg 19&20).
In contrast, using just AIMD is ineﬃcient as it
generates highly correlated data. The trained FFs are highly accurate, with an error of only
0.14 meV/atom for P2-FF on the pristine MD 2000K test, and 1.21 meV/atom for V2-FF on
the vacancy MD 2000K test.
It is worth considering the need for a force ﬁeld. Creating the FF comes at a computational
cost, while providing no inherent insight in the material property of interest. The computational
resources could instead have been used for DFT calculations to study the material property
directly. However, Moir´e patterns are a case where the purely DFT approach is not feasible. To
exactly capture the Moir´e pattern, the size of the unit cell quickly increases, for example, the
largest unit cell to study the 16 rotations (ﬁgs 22-24) is 970 atoms. Additionally, running MD
to study the di↵usion requires a larger supercell to avoid the vacancy-vacancy interaction and
running it for the timescale required is too expensive. Accordingly, the properties of interest in
this study require an eﬃcient FF. To illustrate the scale of the eﬃciency improvement: one of
the DFT vacancy MD runs took 50 hours for 93 steps for a 159 atom system (using 40 cores on
an Intel(R) Xeon(R) Scalable Gold CPU 6148 @ 2.20GHz Skylake). In comparison, it took 2
minutes and 12 seconds to run 930 steps, averaged to 13.2 seconds for 93 steps, for a 1951 atom
system using the ML-FF (8 cores on a NVIDIA GeForce 3090).
Sch¨utt et al. [37] found a similar performance between training PaiNN using a force weight of
↵= 0.95 and ↵= 1, i.e. purely using the forces, for a range of test data sets, stating that using
energies for training is not always beneﬁcial. The hyperparameter test of ↵= 0.98 vs ↵= 0.90
in this study (ﬁg 18) shows that the choice of force weight can have a signiﬁcant impact on the
accuracy, however, there is no way to know the optimal value a priori.
The ML-FF accuracy test (ﬁg 17) shows that P2-FF performed well for all the pristine
tests. V2-FF also performed well for the MD 2000k and relaxation test, however there is room
for improvement in the MD 5000K test. Notably P2-FF performed better than V2-FF in this
test.
One of the main di↵erence between the pristine and vacancy data is that the model
has to account for the changes in oxidation state of the Ti. As the model only takes atomic
positions and atom type as input, there is no label to distinguish Ti4+ and Ti3+. The model
needs to learn the distinction and corresponding contribution from other factors such as bond
lengths or coordination. The comparison of Ti-O bond lengths (ﬁg 14) and Ti-O coordination
(ﬁg 15) between Ti4+ and Ti3+ show some distinction, with higher bond lengths for Ti3+ and
some cases of only 4-fold coordination. However, there is a signiﬁcant overlap between the two
distributions as well. It is seen from the pristine data that the interface e↵ects and twisting
result in a wider spread of Ti-O bond length as compared to the bulk length of 1.979˚A, with
values as high as 2.4˚A. Thus, the model has to incorporate two factors: bond extension due
to twisting and due to a change in coordination state, through the single feature that is the
bond length. Another indicator is the selection from the active learning. For the pristine data,
the AL selected images with higher formation energies, suggesting that more disordered images
were selected. In contrast, for the vacancy data images with similar formation energies to the
existing train data were picked, suggesting the model still needs to learn the relationship in the
train data, before moving to more disordered images.
Additionally, the electron localisation is not consistent for all the conﬁgurations, as it occa-
sionally localised on the next-nearest Ti. This is another feature not directly incorporated in
the machine learning model. Di Valentin et al. [44] note that this could either be an explanation
48
==PAGE:55==
for the good electrical conductivity in TiO2, or an error occurring due to the repulsion between
the two extra electrons because of an insuﬃcient supercell size.
A solution to these issues is to include the spin as a feature as done in the SpinGNN proposed
by Yu et al. [58]. The SpinGNN is designed to be implemented with other GNN architectures
to convert the FF into a magnetic FF. It is worth noting that this increases the degrees of
freedom in a system of N particles from 3xN, due to the 3 cartesian coordinates, to 6xN due
to an inclusion of the spin. The struggle of the model used in this study to learn the e↵ects of
the change in oxidation state can justify the increase in complexity. To further demonstrate the
need for added complexity, let us return to the better performance of P2-FF than V2-FF in the
vacancy MD 5000K test (ﬁg 17). The key di↵erence is that P2-FF is not encoding the e↵ects of
the oxidation state by only training on pristine data. A hypothesis is that P2-FF is better at
predicting the contributions everywhere else but near the vacancy, by not trying to encode the
change in oxidation state. The improved prediction everywhere else outweighs the error made
near the vacancy. We can see from the NEBs (ﬁgs 33-34) that there is an extreme uncertainty
when using P2-FF for the calculation.
This suggests that V2-FF is better at encoding the
vacancy e↵ect, even if it has a higher error in the vacancy MD 5000K test.
Another improvement to the workﬂow is the selection of conﬁgurations. Currently, we decide
on an initial set of conﬁgurations to create for the initial train data, which means it is up to
the user to select a diverse sample of conﬁgurations. It is the same conﬁgurations that are used
throughout the workﬂow, where at each iteration n images is selected from each conﬁguration.
However, there is no comparison between the conﬁgurations, so even if there is low uncertainty
for a given conﬁguration, n images is still labelled for this conﬁguration. This approach can
be suitable in the case where there is a ﬁnite number of possible initial conﬁgurations, as these
conﬁgurations are then explored evenly.
The aim of this study is to study an in principle
inﬁnite conﬁgurational space, so the ability to selectively explore uncertain conﬁgurations is
likely beneﬁcial. To achieve this, another approach is to collect the pool data created by the
FF-MD into a single pool from which the AL selects. Additionally, conﬁgurations not in the
initialisation should be included by relaxing the conﬁguration using the FF and creating pool
data using ML-MD. Thereby, a large number of conﬁgurations can be tested, removing the
importance of user selection and having to run DFT relaxations for all of then. Ideally, using
a single pool should result in a wide exploration of the conﬁgurational space, while the built
in representativeness aspect of the AL ensures that the conﬁgurations most often occurring are
included the most.
When using a ML method for a FF there is a trade-o↵in accuracy when creating an all-
purpose model, as seen when including relaxation data in the train set resulted in a more
accurate FF for relaxation calculations, but less accurate for high temperature MD (ﬁgs 20-21).
So tailoring the train data to a speciﬁc application has its merits. Currently the workﬂow uses
MD to create viable train data, at a high temperature to achieve as much diversity as possible,
with the intention that this provides a useful distribution of train data. It is not ensured that
high temperature MD gives train data suitable for NEB calculations, illustrated by the high
uncertainty at the saddle point for the NEB calculation (ﬁg 33). A beneﬁcial addition to the
workﬂow can be to include NEB calculations as a method to generate train data. It is also
worth noting that while the ensemble uncertainty gives an indication of the accuracy for the
found MEB, it does not give an exact indication of whether the found path is the correct MEB.
Ie, the model might provide exact energies for the found path, but have converged to a wrong
path. It is therefore valuable to create a test set of NEB calculations using DFT to ensure that
the FF is accurate enough to converge to the correct MEB.
The three improvements previously mentioned, (using a spinGNN, conﬁguration search, and
tailoring to NEB calculations) are speciﬁc to the defect chemistry investigated in this study.
For example. for non-magnetic study using a spinGNN is superﬂuous, only adding unecessary

5.2
Moir´e engineering
The stacking and twisting of bilayers has a signiﬁcant impact on the formation energy of the
structure (ﬁgs 22-24). The formation energy is well correlated with the Ti-O coordination for
the SrTi and TiTi interfaces and Sr-O coordination for the SrSr interface, showing that the
e↵ect of twisting is to break bonds at the interface. The three initial translations: 0, a/2 in x,
and a/2 in x and y are the three ways to make the most stable interface for respectively SrTi,
TiTi, and SrSr, as it places the Ti/Sr of the top layer on top of an oxygen. In the case of 0
translation for TiTi and SrSr, two monolayers are created rather than a bilayer as seen by the
large interlayer distance (ﬁg 13). Compared to most stable non-twisted bilayer of each interface,
the twisting results in a higher formation energy, ﬁtting with the fact that bonds are broken.
This means the bilayer is less stable, however as long as the bilayer is stable enough for the
given operation condition, the broken bonds can be beneﬁcial for ionic conductivity. Firstly, it
will likely lead to lower vacancy formation energy in certain oxygen sites, eg. in the cases where
an oxygen is only bonded to a single Ti instead of two. Secondly, this could result in dislocation
channels that are beneﬁcial for vacancy di↵usion.
Creating a vacancy caused an octahedral tilt, most pronounced near the vacancy, however
evident throughout the whole structure (ﬁg 27). It is well established that bulk STO undergoes
a phase transition at 105K from tetragonal at T < 105K to cubic [18][59][60]. The transition
is proposed to occur due to a soft phonon mode. When the temperature becomes low, the
vibration decreases, and a polarization occurs due to an o↵-center displacement of Ti atoms
[18]. A tilt is also observed at room-temperature by applying a strain by growing a ﬁlm of STO
on a substrate making the material ferroelectric [61]. Similar to the results observed in this
study, Choi et al. [62] used PBE+U to ﬁnd that an oxygen vacancy induced an octahedral tilt
in cubic STO. Zhang et al. [54] expanded on this ﬁnding, by studying the e↵ect of supercell
size on the oxygen vacancy migration energy barrier. They found that the distance between the
di↵using oxygen and its nearest oxygen was longer in a 3x3x3 and 5x5x5 supercell as compared
to in a 2x2x2 and 4x4x4, resulting in di↵erent calculated barriers. This e↵ect occurred because
the octahedral tilts di↵ered due to the supercell boundary conditions. Thus, there is precedent
from the bulk case that octahedral tilts occur, similar to what is seen for the bilayer case in this
study. The ﬁnding is interesting as the tilting can have a signiﬁcant impact on the properties
of the material. For example, Gao et al. [63] induced a large octahedral rotation through strain
to give an enhanced ionic conductivity in L0.9Sr0.1Ga0.95Mg0.05O3−δ. However, it is worthwhile
to conﬁrm the results using a more accurate functional such as HSE06 to ensure that the tilt is
not an error from the ML-FF or PBE+U calculations.
An oxygen vacancy present in one of the monolayers when forming the bilayer resulted in
a much more disordered bilayer as opposed to the oxygen vacancy appearing after the bilayer
is already formed (ﬁg 28). As seen by the octahedral tilting, the introduction of a vacancy
causes a distortion in the structure. A vacancy in the monolayer likely causes a distortion in
the layer, and using a distorted monolayer to create the bilayer results in a more disordered
bilayer. This has an e↵ect on the stability of the bilayer and also the interface formed, reducing
the chance that a dislocation path for di↵usion can be found. The result has implications for
the fabrication method, suggesting that for a more ordered bilayer, it is favorable to create the
bilayer at ambient pressure to avoid oxygen vacancy in the monolayer.

5.3
Di↵usion
As discussed in section 5.2, twisting breaks the symmetry of the perovskite structure, likely lead-
ing to a di↵erence in oxygen vacancy formation energy between di↵erent oxygen sites. Initially,
the plan was to identify the lowest energy sites to locate percolation paths for a vacancy in the
structure. Then NEB calculations could be used to ﬁnd the barrier that the vacancy would
have to overcome for di↵usion.
However, when the oxygen was removed, and the structure
was relaxed, the octahedral tilt occurred (ﬁg 27). Thus, the calculated energy measured both
formation energy of the vacancy and the energy change due to the tilt. The calculated value
varied signiﬁcantly with supercell size, where larger supercells led to much lower values, which
indicates that the calculated value was dominated by the change in stability due to octahedral
tilting. Therefore, the calculations were not suitable to determine the vacancy formation energy.
Instead, the barrier was determined using a MD approach (ﬁgs 29-31). For the bulk, the
migration energy barrier is determined to be ∆Hmig = 0.81eV. This is higher than the exper-
imentally found value of 0.6eV [64], study of the transition path using DFT 0.58eV [65], and
using an empirical pair potential giving 0.65eV [66]. To compare with a result using a similar
methodology, Schie et al. [17] found a value of 0.93eV in a MD study using a classical force
ﬁeld. This shows that while the ML-FF does not achieve a highly accurate result, it has value
compared to currently existing methods. Further note that the FF used here is not trained to
study the bulk, and is likely more accurate when used for bilayers.
For bilayer STO, no di↵usion is observed in the z-direction as expected.
The migration
energy barrier for a non-twisted bilayer is found to be ∆Hmig = 0.55eV, which is in accordance
with the ﬁnding by Li et al. [1] of ∆Hmig = 0.6eV. To compare with this result, a transition
at the interface was investigated (ﬁg 32), where the NEB calculation gave ∆Hmig = 0.77eV (ﬁg
33). This result is signiﬁcantly higher than as determined by the MD, indicating that the MD
found another di↵usion path. In the monolayer there are two inequivalent oxygen positions: in
the Sr sublayer (where the oxygen is 4-fold Sr and 1-fold Ti coordinated) and the Ti sublayer
(where the oxygen is 2-fold Sr and 2-fold Ti coordinated). Creating a vacancy at these two spots
was tested, and it was found that ETi,vac −ESr,vac = 1.64eV, ie the vacancy formation energy
in the Sr sublayer is lower and that is where the vacancy is likely to form. This can support the
di↵erence found for the MD and NEB, since the transition tested was in the Ti sublayer.
As shown by equation 60, di↵usion depends both on the mobility and concentration of oxygen
defects. Marrocchelli et al. [15] studied increased oxygen di↵usion along a dislocation in STO
bulk. They found that the increased di↵usion was driven entirely by an increase in vacancy
concentration ([V⇥
O]) rather than an increased mobility, as they calculated the barrier at the
dislocation to be 2.0eV as opposed to 0.9eV in dislocation-free bulk. They further note that
they found a large energy di↵erence in initial and ﬁnal state, because there are certain favorable
vacancy sites, due to a di↵erence in coordination environments. The main observation in the
present study is that twisting and stacking is a way to tune the coordination environment, so
the ﬁndings by Marrocchelli et al. [15] are likely applicable in this case as well. An application
where this is relevant is for the oxygen reduction reaction as the reaction depends on the factor
DV [V⇥
O] [15]. This means it is ideal to tune the bilayer such that there is both an increased
vacancy concentration at the surface and increase in mobility. However as seen, it can occur
that there is a trade-o↵between these two. Thus, there are several factors to study to gain an
in depth understanding of the di↵usion mechanism and ﬁnding the optimal conﬁguration for a
given application.
All in all, when studying the di↵usion there are three main arguments for the requirement
of a FF as opposed to using DFT. Firstly, the Moir´e patterns can only be captured using large
unit cells. Secondly, twisting breaks the symmetry in the structure, leading to a vast number
of inequivalent oxygen sites that need to be considered in the NEB approach. Finally, the MD
approach requires large supercells and to run for long timescales, which has been shown to be
51
==PAGE:58==
feasible within a reasonable time span using the ML-FF. Thus, the workﬂow presented in this
study is a valuable approach to studying ionic conductivity at heterogeneous oxide interfaces.
6
Conclusion
This report has presented a workﬂow that creates a highly accurate force ﬁeld (FF) to study
ionic conductivity in freestanding thin ﬁlm oxides. The workﬂow employs an iterative active
learning approach to eﬃciently collect train data for a message-passing neural network based
on the PaiNN architecture. To illustrate the application of the workﬂow, the methodology is
applied to study the e↵ect of twisting (rotation) and stacking on ionic conductivity in bilayer
SrTiO3. It has been shown that density functional theory is too computationally demanding for
this task due to the large unit cells required to capture the twist and the number of available
inequivalent di↵usion paths due to symmetry breaking when twisting.
Two FFs were created, one purely trained on pristine data (no vacancies), and another
including vacancy data. The accuracy of the models was tested on relaxation and high temper-
ature molecular dynamics (MD) data for both pristine and vacancy conﬁgurations. The pristine
model was highly accurate on the pristine high temperature MD test with a root mean squared
error of 0.66 meV/atom after two iterations. The defect structures proved harder to create a FF
for. The vacancy model had a root mean squared error of 6.08 meV/atom in the vacancy high
temperature MD test. The diﬃculty likely arises from the fact that both twisting and reduction
of Ti4+ to Ti3+ leads to elongation of the Ti-O bond. It is likely that the model could not
properly distinguish the two e↵ects.
Rotation of a layer in bilayer STO leads to a less stable structure, due to the breaking of Ti-O
and Sr-O bonds at the interface. While there is a loss of stability, the change in coordination
environment can be beneﬁcial for ionic conductivity due to a change in vacancy concentration
and migration energy barriers. Using nudged elastic band calculations the migration energy
barrier in TiO2 sublayer at the interface of non-rotated bilayer STO was determined to be
0.77eV. Fitting molecular dynamics results to an Arrhenius relation gave a migration energy
barrier of 0.55eV. The results showed that the introduction of a vacancy introduced an octahedral
tilt, which can have a signiﬁcant impact on the properties of the material. Additionally, a less
stable bilayer was created if the initial monolayer had a vacancy.
In conclusion, the results have shown that this workﬂow is an e↵ective way to study ionic
conductivity in oxide bilayers. With the tools in place, the next step is a comprehensive study
of the percolation path in twisted STO bilayer. Additionally, one can move to heterostructures
adding an important degree of freedom to the system. Three suggested improvements to the
workﬂow when using it for this application is to include spin in the representation for the
machine learning model, change the way the active learning selects from pool data to improve
the conﬁgurational search of twisting, and ﬁnally to include NEB in both the train and test
data to tailor the model for ionic conductivity.
