ABSTRACT 
The purpose of this thesis is to investigate the stochastic geometry analysis of Heterogenous 
networks, which have been part of the cellular network research for quite a long time. Using 
stochastic geometry, cellular network models resembling the real-life networks will be 
constructed.  
The tools that are present in stochastic geometry will be introduced that have been used for 
the modelling and analysis of wireless networks, in addition to the overview of some of the 
more general concepts regarding cellular networks and wireless channels.  
The baseline Poisson Point Process Heterogenous Network model that has been constructed 
using solely stochastic point processes to represent both the base stations and the users will 
be analysed using the tools available in stochastic geometry to demonstrate that analytical 
expressions for complex network models can be obtained.  
Following this, different point processes will be obtained from the Poisson Point Process to 
model more intricate Heterogenous Networks to show that the modelling procedure can be 
adapted to different situations and real-life requirements.  
The key performance metric that will be used throughout this thesis will be the coverage 
probability of the network model, an alternative to which can be the coverage CDF, and finally 
the data rate CDF for the different models.

1  - Introduction  
1.1 Introduction to 5G  
In recent years, the mobile network industry has experienced a significant growth in terms of 
the sheer number of devices that it must accommodate. This growth has caused a massive 
inflation in the data traffic that is taking place in the existing network. According to the 
projections made by Ericsson, this growth is set to maintain its pace and by the year 2023, the 
monthly global data traffic will have exceeded 100 exabytes with increasingly more reliance 
on 5G. In fact, by looking at Figure 1 [1], it can be intuitively deduced that there will be an 
exponential increase on the reliance on future generation technologies.  
 
Figure 1 Ericsson Mobility Report for 2017-2023[1] 
In addition to quantity of users, the expected standards for these users have risen 
considerably with the emergence of new applications such as video telephony in addition to 
the existing voice services. Adding to this the potential plans for the existing future generation 
mobile networks, which include things like Internet of Things (IoT) and sensor networks, the 
==PAGE:7==
6 
 
need for improvement to the current mobile network systems is apparent. This improvement 
emerges in the form of the famed fifth generation (5G) standards.  
The realization of 5G mobile networks is a gradual process that has been underway for many 
years, requiring the intricate planning and the successful integration of many technologies 
with each other to finally yield an optimum long-standing network that can accommodate the 
predicted growth. Some of the major technologies that stand out for candidates to be 
included in the 5G standard are: Heterogenous Networks (HetNets), Massive MIMO, 
millimetre-waves (mmW), Cognitive Radio Networks and finally device-to-device(D2D) 
communications that bypasses the cellular network infrastructure [2][3]. In this thesis, the 
larger area of focus will be the HetNets, which are more of an infrastructural issue compared 
to all the other technologies, since their cooperation with the mathematical modelling 
techniques of Stochastic Geometry form a powerful tool for the analysis of different physical 
deployments of heterogenous cellular networks in terms of coverage performance, which is 
an essential parameter for 5G networks. Even though they won’t receive attention here, the 
other specific technologies such as Cognitive Radio Networks (CRN), device-to-device (D2D) 
communication and MIMO are also technologies that are under development, with the 
potential of playing very important roles in the development of 5G, in addition to the 
emergence of novel radio access schemes such as Non-Orthogonal Multiple Access 
(NOMA)[4] .There are, of course, many more ways that each of these technologies can branch 
out within themselves, but overall they remain as the strongest building blocks for 5G. 
In addition to this growing mobile traffic, the infrastructure of the networks is also evolving. 
With the number of devices increasing exponentially, the networks required to accommodate 
them are becoming extremely versatile, with the locations of the base stations (BS) and users 
starting to be increasingly random. Furthermore, the current HetNets, with multiple tiers of 
cells such as macro cells, small cells, pico/femto cells [5] existing on top of each other in 
separate layers, makes this network configuration even more complicated. With this 
increasing complexity in the networks, it can be safe to say that in the future it will no longer 
be possible to model a mobile cell as a hexagonal grid[6]. Additionally, the increase in the 
number of users/devices will mean that the locations of these devices will be more random. 
As such, to accurately capture this real-life randomness factor, it becomes necessary to make 
use of more advanced tools rather than relying on fixed models. In recent years, stochastic 
geometry has been widely used in modelling these wireless systems with the interest of fully 
reflecting the randomness factor in real life[7]. 
Within stochastic geometry, there are several readily available models that are suited for 
various infrastructure conditions of the network. The most popular model being the Poisson 
Point Process (PPP) and the other models being a derivation of the latter, it becomes possible 
to emulate a wide variety of randomness in a mobile wireless system with the combination 
of some of these models that are suitable for certain parts of the heterogenous network. 
==PAGE:8==
7 
 
What makes these models so attractive to work with, is their so called analytical tractability 
in addition to their ability to capture the randomness of a real-life system. Using existing 
mathematical statements, it becomes possible to derive coverage probability and throughput 
expressions for a complex network, where the system model has been constructed using 
stochastic geometry, thereby reducing the behaviour of an entire network to a single 
expression. The system model constitutes both the locations of the base stations, and the 
locations of the users, since both incorporate a degree of randomness that needs to be 
accounted for in a modern network. 
In addition to the model of the physical infrastructure, it is also very important to accurately 
capture the physical channel. There are various ways to model these wireless channels. 
Depending on the situation, it becomes possible to relax the complexity of the channel model 
for analytical simplicity, where the accuracy of the results won’t be affected too much. 
The main goal of this thesis will be to model various HetNet configurations using the readily 
available tools in stochastic geometry. There can be different stationary point processes 
within stochastic geometry that can be used to model the macro cells and the small cells in a 
mobile HetNet in different. Necessary introductions to stochastic geometry and the physical 
channel models as well as the respective technologies will be separately provided for 
completeness. The question of optimality is of course always a subject of debate. Here, the 
key idea will be to work with the coverage probability to begin with.  
1.2 Brief Introduction to Relevant Technologies  
The rest of this chapter 1 aims to provide a degree of background knowledge on HetNets. At 
the end of the chapter, a more in-depth explanation will follow regarding the nature of the 
studies that will be conducted within each chapter.  
1.2.1 Heterogenous Networks (HetNets) 
HetNets, is a term used to refer to the use of different layers of cell configurations in a mobile 
network. While the 3G mobile network majorly consisted of only macro BS, modern networks 
have had to become more versatile to meet the growing demands of the market. As such, in 
a HetNet, it is common to find cells of different sizes such as micro/pico/femto cells in addition 
to the existing macro cell. The need for this k-tier cell layout can be associated with the 
growing traffic volume. The strategic deployment of these cells has become an efficient way 
of dealing with hot-spot areas as well as dense home networks. This accountability makes it 
so that HetNets form the infrastructural foundation of the 5G standard when the mobile 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
network model is concerned. An example of a 3-tier HetNet can be seen in Figure 2. 
 
Figure 2 Sample Heterogenous Network[8] 
The modelling of HetNets using stochastic geometry is a very popular research topic, 
considering that there are a wide variety of parameters to account for. Since the BS 
configurations in a HetNet are generally very versatile, it becomes necessary to use specific 
stochastic geometry models for specific scenarios. Even though the PPP is generally accepted 
as the conventional way to model these systems, more specific derivations of the PPP exist 
that can more accurately capture different parts of the HetNet. The main study concerning 
the HetNets will involve building an appropriate model, and later investigating the coverage 
probability of the network with respect to certain parameters such as the path-loss exponent 
of the wireless channel, the SINR requirement in the network for a connection, and finally the 
density of the stationary point process that was used to create the random locations of BS. In 
addition to coverage probability, the average throughput of the networks will also be studies 
so that the two most important aspects that constitute a mobile network -coverage and 
capacity- can both be present. A more detailed overview to these topics and parameters will 
follow in Chapter 2. 
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
The mathematical challenge in building an analytical stochastic geometry model for a HetNet 
lies with correctly deriving the interference model. As opposed to a single-tier layout only 
consisting of macro BS where only the interference of other macro BS needs to be accounted 
for, a HetNet’s interference model for a receiver node must account for interference from 
multiple layers of cells. For the sake of simplicity, if the correct models are used to build the 
system model of the network, then it might be possible to disregard certain layers at specific 
parts without losing too much accuracy in the result.  
The major part of working with HetNets will be to experiment with different combinations of 
stochastic geometry models and document which model is best suited for a given situation-
specific HetNet and/or for a given physical layout (referring to the modelling specific physical 
locations e.g. ultra-dense deployments, hotspot areas).  
1.3 Motivation and Objectives 
1.3.1 Objectives for HetNet Modelling 
As it was stated in the previous sections, there are many technologies which can come 
together to form the foundation for 5G. This thesis will carry out performance with a special 
focus on HetNets in order to establish an infrastructural foundation for 5G networks that can 
be further developed using the candidate technologies mentioned before, by testing out 
different physical deployments and comparing the effect of these deployments on coverage 
probability and throughput. 
While constructing the system model for the mobile network, a key factor will be to account 
for the randomness of today’s real-life networks. To do so, stochastic geometry models will 
be used. However, different models need to be used for different parts of the HetNet, and 
from time to time these models need to be experimented with to observe which combination 
will give the best performance. For instance, in[9], the authors have modelled the macro BS 
using a Matern-Point-Process (MPP), which is a thinned[6] version of the PPP that accounts 
for the existence of a minimum distance between each node in the network. In addition to 
this, femto cells are modelled differently depending on their use cases (hotspot areas or cell-
edges). The existence of these readily available stationary point processes is what makes 
stochastic geometry a very powerful tool for designing these system models for wireless 
networks. Experimenting with these will be a central point throughout this thesis.  
Finally, there can be several condition-specific parameters for each technology. For example, 
in[5][10], the authors have investigated the optimum small cell density deployment and have 
found a threshold after which small cell deployment in dense environments tends to have 
either a negative contribution to coverage performance or the performance saturates. For 
the stochastic geometry models used in these investigations, there are various parameters 
such as: macro cell and small cell radii, the exclusion region for the cells and sometimes the 
use of anchor cells[11]. Even though, the focus of this thesis is to work with different models

born of different point processes, it is still nice to be aware that there are endless parameters 
within a model that can be investigated. 
1.3.2 Objectives for Network Implications 
In the case of cellular networks, the two most important factors that need to be considered 
are coverage and capacity. Having models that focus on coverage, especially in the case of 
Heterogenous Network deployments where there will be several small cells inside larger 
macro cells, it will also be important to look at the models from a capacity point of view. There 
are already proposed methods and different ways of dealing with the connections of HetNets 
in a cellular network.  
Having said all this, the motivation of this thesis is to study the various combinations of these 
HetNets while experimenting with different system models to observe which combination will 
yield a certain coverage performance. Carrying out interference model calculations for the 
mathematical section of the thesis and accounting for the changes brought about by the 
integration of these stationary point processes in a simulated environment for the final hybrid 
models, which will then be analysed from a capacity perspective for a cross-referencing 
between two of the most important mobile network parameters (coverage and capacity) is 
the goal of this thesis.

2  - Stochastic Geometry and Wireless 
Channels 
 
The aim of this chapter is to introduce the concepts that have been well-established both in 
stochastic geometry and regarding the nature of wireless channels, to the create a foundation 
for the work that will be carried out in the subsequent chapters. Additionally, a brief overview 
of cellular networks will be provided to establish the relation between the stochastic models 
and the real-life networks. 
It has been mentioned before that the global cellular network has been around for some time 
now, and it has become one of the most important aspects of modern technology that binds 
the current day society. Without it, the average lifestyle would waver significantly in terms of 
convenience. As such, to take care of this technology and to make sure that it will meet the 
demands of the growing market, it is essential to develop new tools that will make it so that 
the development speed can match the generational demands.  
Until recently, the modelling and analysis of these cellular networks were rather 
cumbersome. The alternatives were: 
a) the oversimplified Wyner model[12] which was too idealized for any real-life 
implementation where the model itself is 1-D and the characterization of the BS 
elements is the same, meaning that each BS is treated as providing the same gain to a 
user. The model assumes inter-cell interference only from the two neighbouring cells 
deployed along a straight line. For the Wyner model to gain accuracy, the general 
approach would be to assume a large amount of average interference for the model 
in the uplink direction[13], which is a highly synthetic scenario only for test purposes. 
Even if the results were to be accurate for characterizing simpler single-tier networks, 
the physical deployment for HetNets would be impossible to emulate using only a 
single dimension in the physical model; 
b) a related model to the Wyner simplification was to combine the interference from 
several interfering nodes into a single random variable. The idea would be to calculate 
a single total interference value and from that point denoting the interference of any 
individual cell by a factor of this latter value. This would mean that the average SIR of 
all the cells were approximately the same[14]; 
c) studies where the all the interfering nodes were considered as opposed to the first 
case and they were considered independently as opposed to the second case, but 
their distance to the user was taken to be fixed instead of being based on a distance 
distribution, which obviously once again fails to capture the real-life scenario. 
==PAGE:14==
13 
 
The Wyner model has been used in the past for the modelling of 3G mobile networks, where 
the radio access scheme was CDMA (Code Division Multiple Access), the details of which are 
out of scope here. During the use of CDMA, the averaging techniques used in the Wyner 
model, especially the technique in point b) given above, were used to create a wireless 
network model where the SINR values for cells were created using averaging over the entire 
network. As the evolution towards LTE started and the orthogonal multiple access schemes 
started becoming more popular, the SINR over the cell became increasingly versatile. Despite 
these shortcomings, the Wyner model is still in effect, being used to model large multicell 
networks[15]. Nevertheless, the assumptions made during the construction of a Wyner 
modelled wireless network are way too ideal for a modern-day network[14]. 
With these models having fall-backs with regards to capturing the reality, it has become 
imperative to devise a methodology that can model the network in a way that will account 
for the randomness of the real-life scenario, where the heterogenous networks are starting 
to become increasingly versatile. Stochastic geometry can provide such a model, where the 
position-independent Wyner model falls short. Furthermore, such traditional models are 
incapable of reflecting the sheer number of variables in a cellular network, in particular the 
aggregate interference power at a node, governed by various stochastic processes such as 
the underlying distribution of the nodes in the network and properties of the wireless 
channel[6].  
This is where the tools from stochastic geometry have been used for the purpose of modelling 
these cellular networks while accounting for the randomness of the real-life deployment. It is 
possible to reflect this randomness by making use of various spatial distribution models, 
which will be introduced subsequently.  
In this section it will be shown that there are tools that can be combined to obtain what is 
called the coverage probability of the cellular network. This will involve the calculation of the 
distance distributions for the interfering nodes, followed by the total interference 
experienced by a user in the network, which will finally be used to calculate the coverage 
probability for a given user inside the network at a given time. The crucial thing to remember 
is that the deployments of the cellular BSs in the network are characterized by what is called 
a stationary point process, the most common of which used in these scenarios is the Poisson 
Point Process(PPP) that was mentioned in the previous chapter.  
In addition to the tools of stochastic geometry, introductory information will be provided 
regarding the modelling of wireless channels to provide a more complete background. This 
will include various path-loss models, the difference between small and large-scale fading, as 
well as some brief information on shadowing which isn’t so relevant in this piece of work but 
is nevertheless included for the sake of completeness.

2.1 Background on Cellular Networks 
This section will aim to establish a brief overview of a cellular network architecture by taking 
the LTE technology as a model. By first presenting the cellular network model in terms of the 
physical elements, it will be easier to associate with the mathematical models that will be 
used subsequently. 
Naturally a cellular network is very complex. Never minding the physical deployments, the 
underlying protocols and network properties are too detailed. This section will only provide 
enough information so that in Chapter 5, a comparison can be made between the existing 
cellular network structures and the network deployments implied by the Heterogeneous 
Network varieties experimented with in Chapter 4. 
The most basic cellular network as defined in LTE can be divided into the E-UTRAN and the 
EPC (Evolved Packet Core) as it can be seen from Figure 3[16].  
 
Figure 3 LTE Network Architecture [16] 
 
The E-UTRAN is comprised of: 
• UEs, which are the mobile users that are inside the network, 
• eNodeBs, which are the given names of the base stations in LTE which connect to the 
EPC using the backhaul connection. 
For a larger view of the E-UTRAN, where it is possible to see the deployments of the BSs 
themselves, the standard hexagonal grid model where the BS deployments are ideal is given 
in Figure 4. 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
 
Figure 4 Hexagonal Grid Model Tessellation 
Each red mark in Figure 4 represents a BS that is perfectly aligned with the rest of the BS to 
form a hexagonal tessellation using the Voronoi method. This model is highly idealized. 
Improving the BS placements shown in this model to adjust for real-life scenarios is where 
stochastic geometry will become a powerful tool. 
The first part of the thesis, namely chapter 3 and 4 will focus on the deployment of the E-
UTRAN. In a large-scale mobile network, the locations of the eNodeBs and the users will be 
modelled using stationary point processes (PP). A major focus will be on using different 
combinations of these PPs to create the E-UTRAN in a way that captures the increasing 
versatility of modern-day networks, where both the users and the eNodeBs are distributed 
randomly in HetNets. 
Moving towards the core, the EPC, is the server-based infrastructure of the network which 
can be described as the backbone of the network as far as the cellular structure is concerned, 
comprised of many different elements providing different services to connect the users to the 
IP Network. Very briefly, the elements of the EPC shown in Figure 3 are: 
• MME, the Mobility Management Entity, is responsible for keeping track of the UEs in 
network and performing necessary mobility and security management signalling. 
• S-GW, the Serving Gateway, carries the user traffic between the external IP Network 
and the cellular network terminals.  
• P-GW, the Packet Data Network, is the connection between the EPC and the IP 
Network. 
• HSS, the Home Subscriber Server, is a user database which stores the data of the 
subscribers, where the authenticity of each user is verified. 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
These different parts of the network are all connected to each other in different access 
mediums. The radio access part of the network, which is the connection between the UEs and 
the eNodeBs, is what is called the fronthaul of the network and it is the air interface, made 
up of wireless channels. Some of the mathematical representations of these wireless channels 
will be introduced later in this chapter.  
An important part of the network is the part that connects the eNodeBs with the EPC, referred 
to as the backhaul of the cellular network, requiring delicate optimization. This connection 
can either be wireless or wired depending on the deployment environment and the 
requirements for the connection.  
The next section will provide an overview to the backhaul technologies in a cellular network 
with emphasis on small cell and HetNet backhaul. 
2.1.1 Overview of Cellular Network Backhaul 
The backhaul of the network is the physical interface that allows for the BSs deployed in the 
E-UTRAN to connect with the backbone of the network. Optimizing the backhaul connection 
using specific technologies and adapting it to the specific physical BS deployment models is 
altogether a separate task.  
The most important thing to keep in mind while developing this connection between the BSs 
and the backbone is to make sure that no bottlenecks regarding capacity are caused by the 
backhaul.  
As the networks evolved into HetNets, the E-UTRAN which is shown in Figure 5[17] became 
increasingly populated with more types of cell deployments, hence the technologies that 
were developed started to complement this growth.  
Today, there are various ways to perform backhaul modelling in a HetNet, depending on the 
deployment of the small cells. Figure 6 [18] shows various ways of performing the backhaul 
connection between the BSs deployed in the fronthaul and the backbone.  
 
 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
 
Figure 5 HetNet BS Deployment  
There are five specific scenarios that are depicted in Figure 6. There are many more ways that 
the backhaul connection can be modified. The scenarios in Figure 6 provide an overview for 
the general possibilities. From right to left: 
• The first scenario shows the standard wireline backhaul, where the macro-BS directly 
connects to the backbone. This is a simple situation where there is no aggregation. In 
modern day networks, no single BS, regardless of whether it is a small cell BS or a 
macro cell BS, has no single dedicated fibre drawn directly towards the backbone. Such 
a deployment would be extremely costly to implement for each individual BS.

2.2 Background on Stochastic Geometry 
2.2.1 Point Processes 
Point processes form the basis of the network model. The point process can be described as 
a randomly distributed collection of points in a dedicated Euclidian space where the space is 
of dimension d. The mathematical representation for a Point process is given in equation 2.1. 
Ф = {𝑋𝑛, 𝑛∈𝑁}                                    (2.1) 
Each of the elements in the set Ф is a random variable which is generated out of the behaviour 
of the distribution that created the point process in the first place. The nature of this 
distribution can be selected by the user depending on the task that it will be used to 
accomplish.  
There are different types of point processes that have been deemed popular for the modelling 
and study of wireless networks. These are: the PPP(Poisson Point Process), the BPP(Binomial 
Point Process), PCP(Poisson Cluster Process), MPP (Matern Point Process). These will be 
explained later in more detail; however, the PPP forms the foundation for these models, and 
generally the PPP can in some way be transformed into most of these other point processes.

In the case of cellular network modelling, it has been established beforehand that the 
locations of the BS are not always predictable, in fact, they are becoming increasingly random 
with the evolution of the cellular network, which features the introduction of small-tier cells 
such as micro/pico/femto. The existence of these small-tier cells makes it so that the locations 
of the BSs are no longer possible to accurately model with the existing simplifications such as 
the Wyner model as it was explained at the beginning of this chapter. This is where stochastic 
geometry starts to gain popularity. Especially specific point processes are used to model the 
locations of these BSs in the heterogenous networks, as well as the locations of the users in 
the network.

The PPP is the stepping stone to obtaining other types of point processes. The mathematical 
features of the PPP render it ideal for this job, since it’s extremely versatile in terms of 
responding to various mathematical treatments. 
The displacement/mapping theorem is a term used to refer to the transformation of a point 
process to another one through the application of various of these treatments. Before 
understanding this theorem, it is important to briefly look into the properties of the PPP. 
PPP Mathematical Features: 
• Displacement: The displacement of a PPP will yield a different PPP. That, the 
displacement of an existing PPP by a random amount, say the addition of some 
random new points to an existing point in the PPP, will result in a new PPP with those 
new points.  
• Thinning: Thinning in stochastic geometry is the modification of classifying the existing 
elements of a PPP into separate point processes so that certain nodes in the PPP can 
be rejected or accepted depending on what is wanted at the time. An example to this 
might be that in Figure 9, certain nodes in the PPP were marked as being too close. 
This posed a problem because, in a real-life deployment, the BSs wouldn’t nearly be 
so close to each other, since such an implementation would be illogical in terms of 
performance. A thinning rule might be applied, stating that any nodes in the PPP which 
are within a certain distance from each other are multiplexed to create one final node. 
This way, a new point process can be obtained where the nodes that were close to 
each other are eliminated and the resulting point process is a thinned PPP. This 
treatment of making sure that the nodes aren’t close to each other has in fact become 
so popular that it has been assigned its own name, which is a Matern Point Process 
(MPP). In short, some of the points from the previous from the previous point process 
are deleted, forming what is called a thinned PPP. 
• Superposition:  Super-positioning can be described as the complementary operation 
to thinning. With thinning, the idea is to tone down the point process so that it will 
meet certain requirements, or rather it will have modified characteristics that are 
more desirable in certain situations. Super-positioning is the combination of random 
points from independent PPPs that will form a new PPP. Each independent PPP has its 
own rate, the resulting PPP from the superposition of the previous PPPs, is the sum of 
the rates of the old ones.  
Apart from the mathematical concepts concerning a PPP, there are also some pre-defined 
real-life statements about PPPs that have been accepted regarding their usage in the design 
of cellular networks: 
• The PPP is the baseline model for the abstraction of a network in a finite/infinite space 
where the users and the BS can coexist in finite/infinite numbers; 
==PAGE:25==
24 
 
• In cases where the number of elements in the PPP is known for a given region, the PPP 
turns into a BPP; 
• In real-life situations, where the location of BSs is geographically limited, it is a 
requirement to account for a minimum distance between each BS, in which case the 
PPP leans towards a hardcore process such as MPP. 
Having introduced some of the popular properties of the PPPs, it is now possible to talk about 
some of the more advanced tools that can be applied together with the PPPs for the modelling 
of wireless cellular networks.

process, usually through some of the means such as superpositioning and/or thinning,

In fact, throughout this work, a key focus will be to experiment with these derived point

2.2.1.6 
Slivnyak’s Theorem 
The final statistical tool that will be generally used is the Slivnyak-Mecke Theorem, which is 
more commonly referred to as the Slivnyak’s Theorem. The theorem states that for any PPP 
Ф, it is possible to add or subtract any element without altering the distribution properties of 
the PPP. The idea behind the theorem is that, because the distributions of the points in 
disjoint areas of the PPP are all independent, any specific property from the perspective of a 
single element must be there, irrelevant of the fact that whether there was a point there or 
not.  
This theorem allows for the injection of elements into the PPP. This is useful because custom 
placing a point into the PPP allows for the manipulation of the distances in the network model 
without making any changes to the properties of the nodes. Slivnyak’s theorem constitutes 
the beginning of the interference calculation, since it will almost always play a role in defining 
the user node, hence by extension the interfering BS.  
The single-tier cellular network calculations in chapter 3 will show how all these statistical 
tools are used to perform the interference calculations and from there the coverage 
probability.

distributions for modelling different parts of a heterogenous network.  
2.3 Background on Wireless Channels 
The modelling of the wireless channels in a network is a factor that is just as important as the 
spatial modelling of the network itself and will affect the outcome with equal importance. As 
such, it is necessary to obtain some preliminary information regarding this modelling. Even 
though only a single variation of the models presented in this section will be used for the 
practical applications in this report, the possible variations are included for completeness and 
to give the reader an idea of possible future studies. 
There are various channel factors that need to be accounted for while modelling these 
channels. Often, some of these can be ignored because the amount of accuracy that they will 
provide with the inclusion is sometimes not worth the mathematical complications that they 
will bring about. However, it is still worth mentioning the possibilities for the sake of 
completeness. Among these impairments are: the Doppler effect, multipath propagation, 
large-small scale fading, shadowing, and path loss.

2.3.1 Multipath Fading  
A wireless channel exists in a non-controlled environment, especially in the case of cellular 
networks, where the medium of access is the air interface. As such, this medium is bound to 
have what is referred to as scattering objects. The physical phenomenon of scattering is a 
topic within the physical sciences, hence rather than defining its origin, its implications will be 
analysed for wireless channels.  
These objects, which can be referred to as scatterers, will make it so that the received signal 
at a given node in the network, will be the superposition of several signals each with different 
time delays and phases. Each of these different signals is called a multipath component of the 
original signal and will result in what is called Inter-Symbol Interference (ISI)[25], causing 
severe degradation at the receiver end performance.  
Frequency Selective vs Flat Fading: 
It is possible to classify the multipath fading into frequency selective and frequency flat fading 
depending on the bandwidth of the signal and how it relates to the spread between different 
multipath components. 
To make the distinction between these two fading types, a term called coherence bandwidth 
has been denoted, which is a quantity determined by the doppler spread between the 
multipath components. The coherence bandwidth is a measure of the frequency separation, 
which determines the correlation of the fading between different multipath components of 
the signal. Equation 2.11 denotes coherence bandwidth.

3.1 Coverage Probability 
There are numerous different ways that this analysis can be carried out, depending on the 
wireless channel model. Different versions will be presented throughout the thesis as the 
subject develops. Before moving on with the actual procedure, both the pre-determined 
conditions and the methodology of the calculations need to be established. 
Firstly, since the tester constructs the cellular network model, it is known that the BS nodes 
are distributed according to a PPP. In addition to this, the user mobiles in the network are 
also modelled according to a point process. This is because the calculations of the coverage 
probability will be carried out by placing a mobile user in the origin 2-D PPP plane. Naturally 
with any generated point process, it is virtually impossible to guarantee that an element will 
appear in a given location. At this point one of the previously defined theorem’s, namely the 
Slivnyak’s Theorem defined in section 2.1.1.6 will be used to place an element at the origin of 
the working plane and because of Slivnyak’s Theorem, it will be possible to accomplish this 
without altering the distribution properties of the point process that generated the mobile 
users.   
Hence the user node that will be used for the coverage probability calculations has been 
established as the node placed at the origin of the 2-D plane.  
==PAGE:36==

Before moving on with the actual calculations, it is also important to define what coverage 
probability is. There are several different conceptual ways that this can be done, all of which 
will give the same result in the end but will require different mathematical processes. 
• The fraction of users that can achieve a given SINR at any given time; 
• The probability that a given user can achieve a given SINR at any given time; 
• The fraction of users in a given area of the network that can achieve a given SINR; 
• Mathematically, it can be defined as the complementary CDF of the SINR of a network, 
or in mathematical terms:𝑃[𝑆𝐼𝑁𝑅𝑢𝑠𝑒𝑟> 𝑆𝐼𝑁𝑅𝑇], which is the way it will be computed 
in this chapter. 
To carry out a coverage analysis, it is necessary to make some assumptions about the 
network. Perhaps the most important is the one concerning the selection of the serving BS 
node for a given user node. This is important because it will influence the interference 
calculations and the transmission distance. Usually, in such scenarios, the assumption is that 
the user connects to the nearest BS. This simplifies the interference calculation.  
Of course, the modern cellular networks are very versatile, and this may not always be the 
case. Nevertheless, this is not an unreasonable assumption, since a mobile user will generally 
choose to connect with the BS that offers the highest SNR. Assuming that this BS is the closest 
one is not an unfair assumption. There will be situations that the user will connect to another 
BS. An example might be that a moving user enters the domain of a small cell under a macro 
cell, where the network might postpone the transfer of the user to the small cell to wait if it 
will exit the small cell’s domain. This way, two premature handover procedures can be 
prevented, which would have caused extra control traffic in the network.  
Naturally, it is impossible to account for all the aspects of the network in a mathematical 
manner, and even extremely demanding to account for one single parameter, hence 
assumptions such as the user connecting to the nearest BS are a requirement to initiate the 
calculations.

3.1.2 Radio Access and Network Properties 
Before the characterization of the wireless channel, first an assumption is required regarding 
the radio access technology of the network. In LTE, the radio access used was called OFDMA 
(Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiple Access). Even though, stochastic geometry is used 
throughout this thesis to obtain the necessary results and that the focus is on understanding 
==PAGE:39==
38 
 
and using these stochastic geometry tools, the final objective is to determine an optimum BS 
deployment condition for a 5G cellular network. Since there is no readily available radio-
access technology that has been standardized for 5G, some assumptions in this aspect are 
required to proceed.  
The important assumption as far the stochastic analysis is concerned is related to the 
interference calculation. The assumption is that, since the radio access technology is expected 
to evolve from OFDMA, the mobile user will receive information only from its serving BS and 
interference from only the other BSs and not its own serving one. This assumption provides a 
fine line between the interfering BS and the serving BS and renders the SINR calculation 
feasible.  
There are of course many different situations where a mobile user might be connected to 
several BS at the same, an example being in the case of inter-cell interference coordination 
(ICIC) techniques that are being implemented in LTE. ICIC refers to the communication of the 
receiver with multiple BSs at once to achieve optimal throughput. Two of the most popular 
interference coordination schemes are Coordinated Multipoint (CoMP) ICIC and Joint 
Transmission (JT) ICIC. In the former case, in cell edge situations, the information that is 
required by the user will be readily available at multiple BS that promise SNR above a certain 
threshold, but the information will be transmitted by only one at a time. This will not change 
the SINR calculation, it will however mean that there will be a pool of active BS for a given 
receiver that can be used as the serving node at any given time. The members of this pool will 
have to be deducted for the interference calculation. The second option being JT ICIC, refers 
to the case where the BS coordinate among each to provide the receiver with different parts 
of the same information sequence. This is extremely difficult to implement due to strict 
synchronization requirements between the BSs that are connecting at the same time, and any 
mathematical model that will incorporate such a characteristic is almost sure to fall short of 
accurately capturing the dynamic of the underlying network. For implementing such a 
scenario, the SINR calculation given in equation 3.1 will have to be modified to account for 
having multiple serving nodes at the same time, while reducing the number of interfering 
nodes to a region outside the furthest node that is in connection. This might be possible to 
achieve by extending the assumption that the closest node is the one that the receiver 
connects, to say that the receiver connects to the “k” closest nodes at the same time. 
Realistically “k” can never be a very large number, but even having two or more nodes at the 
same time feeding information, will change the way SINR is calculated. 
The idea here is that, it will be difficult to account for existence of multiple serving nodes at 
the same, whether they are active at the same time or one at a time, the fact remains that it 
will render the SINR calculation very different from the standardized form in equation 3.1 by 
changing the numerator into a summation over the multiple serving nodes and the interfering 
nodes region to account for the newly determined serving nodes.  
==PAGE:40==
39 
 
Because of this, the assumption about the radio access technology, only allowing interference 
from the nodes apart from the serving node is made. This doesn’t mean that the OFDMA 
standard of LTE is in effect, but the new radio-access technology for 5G inherits this one 
property from its predecessor.

In this Figure 14, it is possible to see that with a higher number of BS deployment, the

certain value of PPP intensity, namely the BS intensity, a saturation can be observed in 
coverage probability. This saturation can be explained by thinking about the distances 
between the user mobile and its serving node and the interfering nodes. As the PPP intensity 
increases after a certain point, both the distance of the user to its serving node and to its 
interfering nodes decreases. Due to this decrease in distance to interfering nodes 
compensating for the decrease in distance to the serving node, it is possible to see that a 
higher intensity has no further effect, meaning that the gain from getting closer to the serving 
BS is cancelled out by the loss from similarly getting closer to the interferers. This means that 
deploying more BS after a certain point, not only doesn’t have a positive effect on the 
coverage probability, but also increase the cost of the network in vain. In fact, it wouldn’t be 
illogical to assume that after a certain while, the number of interferers will have increased so 
much beyond the logic frame of the mathematical mode, that the coverage probability 
becomes zero over the entire SINR threshold range. This of course requires very poor planning 
from an oblivious perspective.

3.2 Extension to Heterogenous Networks 
Having gone through a detailed depiction of how coverage probability is calculated. It is now 
time to implement this methodology for HetNets. For the last section of this chapter, the 
coverage probability will be calculated for a basic HetNet, where small cells are also deployed 
as PPP. Later, in Chapter 4, different stationary point processes will be used for different 
layers of the network and the users, to compare the coverage probability yield. This will allow 
for a better understanding of the difference between different physical deployments.  
For now, however, it is time to present the coverage probability for a k-tier HetNet. Since the 
previous chapter details the computation methodology, from here on out, the in-depth 
derivations will be omitted when unnecessary.  
To start with the coverage probability expression for a HetNet, it is first imperative to 
understand the differences between a multi-tier network and a single macro-BS network. In 
the expression that will be used here, the assumption will be that the user under analysis will

be connecting to a BS that provides the strongest signal. This is the same assumption that was

3.2.1 Modification of the Single-Tier Network to HetNet 
In a single-tier network, the characteristics of the BS were assumed to be the same. That is to 
say, the transmit power of individual BS are the same. In a multi-tier HetNet, there are 
multiple layers of BS deployed for specific purposes over the mobile network.  
• There can be a femto-cell deployment over a home network, in which case the 
transmit power of the femto-cell BS would significantly lower than that of the macro-
BS because it would be governing a considerably smaller area.  
• Another option might be a deployment along the cell-edges, to boost the capacity of 
the cell-edge users, since their connection to a macro-BS might not prove to be stable 
by the standards of the modern expectations.  
• Another possibility is the deployment of hot-spot small cells, where the idea is to 
determine especially busy places inside a macro-cell and boost the capacity of these 
hot-spot areas by deploying additional BS.  
Having listed all these possibilities, there can be other reasons that a network planner chooses 
to deploy a small cell. In each case however, the characteristics of the BS belonging to a tier, 
either the home-network femto-cell or the hot-spot pico-cell, will be different. This will mean 
that the transmit power of the BS belonging to different tiers of the HetNet will be different, 
as well as the deployment densities of the BS in these tiers. Separating the different tiers of 
the network in such a manner will assure that the mathematical model doesn’t stray further 
from the reality. Since reality is that small cell deployment is meant to optimize and 
compensate the shortcomings of a network in different way, it is logical to assume that the 
deployments compensating for different shortcomings will have different characteristics.  
With this idea in mind, looking back at the SINR expression in equation 3.1, it is now possible 
to modify this expression so that it will account for the different transmit power of the BSs in 
different tiers. Equation 3.7.1 gives the SINR expression for a HetNet, where 𝑝𝑖 is the transmit

3.2.2 Understanding the Coverage Regions 
In a single-tier network, the macro-BS, which are the only BSs, are deployed according to a 
PPP with a given density. In such a single-tier network given in Figure 9, the transmit power 
of the BSs were assumed to be the same, hence the tessellation corresponded to the classical 
Voronoi diagram, which was explained in chapter 2. In a HetNet, however, the cell boundaries 
are generated using a multiplicatively weighted Voronoi diagram, which takes into accounts 
the distance between the BS and multiplies this distance with a pre-determined weight factor 
to generate the coverage regions. In other terms, the coverage regions of the small cells 
directly correlated with a random weight for each BS in that small cell tier. The details of the 
weighted Voronoi tessellation are beyond scope and can be found in[28]. The methodology 
that will be used here to generate the coverage regions for the small cells is much simpler. 
Every BS will have an associated random weight, like the weighted Voronoi method, which 
will determine the size of the curvature coverage region around the BS. The associated weight 
will also be multiplied with a fixed tier parameter based on which tier of small cell BSs the 
coverage region is being generated for. This will serve to separate the different small cell tiers 
based on the assumption that the BSs from different tiers will have different transmit power.

4  - Simulations for HetNet Modelling 
with Thinned Point Processes 
 
In the previous chapter, a mathematical approach was used to obtain tractable expressions 
for the coverage probability of cellular networks. This was an analytical expression derived 
using the existing methodologies in stochastic geometry where the interference 
characterization played a key role.  
This chapter will extend the analytical study to a more practical level by performing iterative 
simulations on candidate network models where each tier of the HetNet is modelled using a 
different point processes for comparison.  In chapter 3, the PPP was used exclusively for 
modelling all the tiers, since in terms of obtaining a tractable closed form expression, it 
provides the smoothest characterization of the aggregate network interference. However, in 
the case of the simulations, more complicated point processes can be used to model the 
small-cell BSs of the network. After creating the models, the coverage probability, which is 
also the complementary CDF of the SINR, will be presented as a comparison factor between 
the different models.  
4.1 Thinned Point Processes 
It is possible to obtain countless permutations of point processes for different tiers of the 
network, however most of these wouldn’t make much sense in capturing the reality. In this 
case, it becomes necessary to make intuitive decisions in choosing which point processes to 
combine for emulating the different real-life situations that may occur. 
The modelling will be for a two-tier HetNet, consisting of a macro-BS tier and 1-tier of small 
cells. A total of six different models will be used to represent different cases from potential 
real-life deployments. These different models will vary in terms of the distribution of the small 
cells, as well as the distribution of the user mobiles in the network, while the macro-tier 
deployment for all the models will be the same. The six models and the real-life cases that 
they represent are explained later in this chapter. However, before that, it is necessary to 
explain the more complicated thinned point processes obtained from the original PPP and the 
way they are generated in addition to the background information that was provided on 
stochastic geometry and the basics of point processes in section 2.2 of this thesis. 
4.1.1 MPP (Matern Point Process) 
The MPP is the most fundamental modification that can be done to a PPP. In a 2-D PPP, the 
elements of the point process are distributed according to a Poisson random variable with no 
restrictions. This is a good starting point for analysis as it was shown in chapter 3, however as 
far as mimicking a realistic deployment it can fall short. The reason being is that, the elements 
==PAGE:62==
61 
 
of the PPP representing the BSs of the network can be randomly very close to each other, 
which would never be case in a cellular network, having two macro-BS very close. As such the 
PPP is modified into an MPP without losing any of the randomness factor that made the 
stochastic processes attractive in the first place. 
To obtain an MPP from a PPP: 
• PPP Ф is generated like before, 
• For every element of the point process Ф, a random weight 𝑤𝑖 is denoted where: 
0 < 𝑤𝑖< 1,
𝑖∈Ф, 
• A minimum proximity radius 𝑟𝑝 is determined; 
• For every element  𝑖, in the PPP Ф, from the elements in the region 𝐵(𝑥𝑖, 𝑟𝑝), the 
element with the maximum 𝑤𝑖, is kept while removing all the others.  
The MPP is used to create the macro-BSs in all the models and it remains the same throughout 
all the different configurations for the small cells. This way it is ensured that the macro-BSs 
are never too close to each other.  
4.1.2 PCP (Poisson Cluster Process) and MCP (Matern Cluster Process) 
A PCP is used when the elements are required to be placed densely in specific places rather 
than being uniformly distributed over the entire plane. To create a PCP: 
• PPP Ф1 is generated, which will be known as the father point process which will have 
the intensity 𝜆𝑓, 
• Next, for every element in the father process is denoted as a cluster centre, after 
which daughter points are generated around each of these centres. For an average 
number of 𝑁 daughter points for each cluster centre, the daughter point process will 
then have an intensity of 𝜆𝑑= 𝜆𝑓𝑁. This point process model is called a Poisson 
Cluster Process. 
• To obtain the MCP, from the PCP, the same algorithm that was applied for the PPP to 
MPP thinning is also applied to the daughter point process for each cluster centre, 
which will lead to an MCP. 
The MCP is one of the point processes which is used to model the small cells of the HetNet, 
while the PCP is one of the point processes used to model the users. 
4.1.3 PHP (Poisson Hole Process) 
PHP is another way of modelling the small cells of the HetNet. The idea with the PHP is to 
designate dead regions within the point processes based on centres so that no elements 
appear in certain zones. The way to generate a PHP is: 
• PPP Ф1 is generated like before to represent the macro-BS, 
==PAGE:63==
62 
 
• A second PPP Ф2 is generated for the BS of the small-cell tier, 
• A deletion radius 𝑟𝑑 is chosen, 
• For every element in  Ф2, the distance is calculated from the elements in Ф1, and if 
for any element this distance value is larger than the deletion radius, the element in 
 Ф2 is removed.  
This will result in a PHP small cell deployment where the small cell BS can never be too close 
to the macro-BS.  
Next, the models that will be used by combining these point processes will be explained. 
4.2 HetNet Models Obtained from Point Process Combinations 
 
There will be a total of six models, exploring the effect of the various point processes in small-
cell deployment and the user distribution. Each model is designed to represent a real-life 
situation that is likely to occur, both in terms of the distribution of the users and the potential 
small-cell deployments. For easement of terminology, the following reference style will be 
used: (X/Y/Z), where ‘X’ will represent the point process for the macro BS, ‘Y’ for the small cell 
BSs and ‘Z’ for the users. 
The models are created in a 20km by 20km grid. The generation of the BS elements are done 
by first determining the deployment densities of each of the separate tiers per km2 and then 
proceeding to generate the necessary number of BSs that would be present in the given 
model area with respect to the point process that is being used.

5.1 Capacity Calculations

that it can provide to its users as a measure of performance in addition to coverage. Achieving

5.1.1 Modification to Shannon’s Equation 
To obtain the data rate CDF curve for the systems, a modified version of Shannon’s Equation

The Shannon equation, generally related with the Shannon bound, is ordinarily an indication 
of the maximum obtainable spectral efficiency for a given system. For the formula in equation 
5.1, by introducing contingency parameters that take into account the efficiency of certain 
resources, a more real measure of spectral efficiency can be obtained. There are endless ways 
of modifiying the original Shannon’s Equation given in equation 5.2, so that it better accounts 
for the nature of the system at hand. For now, however, the expression in equation 5.1 will

Understanding the expression in equation 5.1 is relatively simple. There are three extra terms 
that have been introduced, the significance of which is explained below.  
• BW efficiency: In a mobile network, the bandwidth in a wireless channel can never be 
100% devoted to carrying user information bits. There are always control channel bits 
in place for synchronization and other control plane processes. The radio-access 
scheme for LTE, which is OFDM, has a redundancy measure in its implementation 
called the cyclic prefix which can take up additional BW. Finally, there are pilot 
sequences which are used for spatial assisting, where based on the factor of MIMO in 
place, will take up increasingly more BW. These being the main sources of BW usage 
in addition to the user information, there can be many others that lower the BW 
efficiency. Keeping all these mind, the BW efficiency for the spectral efficiency 
calculation is chosen to be 0.83, as given in [31] 
• SINR efficiency: Achieving full SNR efficiency is also not possible due to the error-
correcting schemes that are in place. An error-correcting code introduced redundancy 
bits into the system. The amount of these redundancy bits depends on the coding rate, 
determined by the pre-determined MCS (Modulation and Coding Scheme) indexes 
that have been prepared for LTE, which can be found at [32]. Adding to this the spatial 
scaling issues of MIMO, SNR efficiency sees a degradation. There can be many other 
specific reasons within the error-correction algorithms. LTE makes use of a Turbo 
Decoder[33], which is an iterative decoder that can be designed to adapt to the 
modulation scheme of the channel. The SNR efficiency is chosen to be 1.6 as given in 
[31] based of all the parameters that were explained. 
• 𝜼: This is a correction factor. The need for this correction results from the volatile 
relationship between the SINR and the SINR efficiency. Normally, for the SINR values, 
a separate fitting would have to be made to find the corresponding SIRN efficiency. To 
bypass this and use a fixed value of SINR efficiency, a correction factor is introduced. 
The value of this correction factor depends on the characteristics of the wireless 
channel and can deviate. For the calculations here, however, a fixed value is chosen 
based on the calculations in[31], which is 0.9.

a more futuristic value of the channel bandwidth.

The objective of this thesis has been to explore optimum HetNet deployments for future 5G 
networks. However, the modifications to the Shannon equation explained in the previous 
section have been for LTE, where the control channels and the radio access scheme are 
assumed to be the those in the LTE standard. Hence, at this point it makes sense to use the 
configuration for LTE-advanced. For this reason, the carrier-BW is chosen to 100MHz.

To obtain the bitrate CDF curve, the same methodology that was used in generating the 
coverage maps is once again used here, only with a slight extension. The coverage plane is 
divided into a 1000x1000 matrix, after which at each point the SINR is calculated. Using this 
SINR value, the SE at each point is found using equation 5.1, after which multiplying the SE 
with the carrier bandwidth will give the bitrate. 100 iterations are taken for a healthy average.

5.2 Network Discussions and Indications for 5G 
Now that all the relevant information regarding the stochastic models have been obtained; 
such as the coverage probability and SINR CDFs in addition to the SINR heatmaps in chapter 
4; the data rate CDFs with the corresponding heatmaps along with the data rate relation to 
the carrier bandwidth in chapter 5, it is now time to discuss how these pieces of information 
can help in the furthering of the 5G technology along with the analytical study in chapter 3. 
To begin with, the two most important aspects of a mobile network are coverage and 
capacity. Depending on the priorities of the mobile operator, the coverage probability curves 
and the data rate CDFs can be analysed for situation specific deployments. Each model has its 
own strengths and weaknesses.  
The (MPP/MPP/…) model gives a reasonably high coverage probability and supports high 
data rates in most regions, however, since the deployments of the small cells is far too 
frequent, the cost of deploying the physical network is far greater due to the higher number 
==PAGE:98==
97 
 
of BSs. Not only that, divining a backhaul connection to the backbone for such a crowded 
infrastructure becomes more complicated. The (MPP/MCP/…) model is situation specific. It 
gives the best performance for coverage probability in the case of clustered users centring 
around the cluster centres for the small cells, however apart from that, in a homogenous user 
distribution it proves to be weaker than the baseline model. In terms of bitrate, the model 
proves to be equal to the (MPP/PHP/…) model, which is a cost-efficient deployment scenario 
designed to enforce cell-edge performance while leaving the performance of the macro-cell 
interiors entirely to the macro-BSs. This deployment gives a slightly worst performance 
compared to the (MPP/MPP/…) model for a clustered user distribution, however proves to 
be almost as good in a homogenous user setup. Even though the bitrates offered are once 
again slightly worst, the small cell density is far smaller considering the difference in 
performance. This makes the backhaul planning much easier compared to the more crowded 
(MPP/MPP/…) model. As for the (MPP/MCP/…) model, the small cells are focused around 
specific locations that are projected as hotspots, in such a case the traditional density in the 
entire plane is of little consequence. Since the small cells are contained at specific locations, 
designing their connection to the backbone will be much easier in terms of physical 
complications.  
Even with all these results outlining the benefits of each model, in the end it is up to the 
mobile operator to make a choice. A trade-off between performance and cost is always the 
issue. Even though most of the models here show a very close performance it is safe to say 
that the (MPP/PHP/…) model is the most cost-effective configuration. It performs extremely 
high in the coverage probability curve, almost tying for number one in the homogenous PPP 
users case and showing that the bitrate percentiles are better than the clustered small cell 
deployment even with a significantly lower number of small cell BS. It would be reasonable 
to assume that this would be a good choice for suburban cellular network deployment. For 
urban areas, more dense models with more cost can be considered such as the (MPP/MPP/…) 
which gives the best results but with significantly higher amounts of infrastructure devotion. 
The final small cell model, (MPP/MCP/…), is highly situation specific as it can be seen from 
the coverage probability performance. It gives the best performance for its use case, which is 
accommodation clustered users, however falls short in uniform user distributions. The 
throughput of the model is weaker compared to all the other small cell models, hence it 
should only be considered for hot-spot deployments where a high consistent user density that 
carries majority of the traffic during the busy hours.  
These HetNet models form the foundation for the infrastructure of the future-generation 
mobile networks. Their analytical tractability and their stochastic properties make them easy 
to modify for specific situations. Even though these models are not so densely deployed, it is 
possible to directly play with their density and increase cell deployment to create 
infrastructures that can be more suited for supporting higher amounts of data traffic as it was 
==PAGE:99==
98 
 
projected by Figure 1. The work carried out here points out the strengths and weaknesses of 
some of the potential models that can be used for HetNets.  
Having said all this, all this research takes place within the bounds of the LTE technology. Yet, 
this thesis is based around optimizing the physical deployments of HetNets. The results are 
easily transferable to a 5G system because the mentality around the networks themselves 
will remain the same with new technologies being added that won’t have a major effect on 
the physical infrastructure. All these models were formulated using stochastic geometry, the 
main reason for which was that the stochastic point processes could capture the randomness 
of the BSs in a real-life network. However, that is not the entire reason. The cellular network 
models that are constructed using these point processes are subject to the tools that are 
readily available in stochastic geometry. Using these tools, it becomes possible to derive 
analytically tractable expressions for HetNet models where every element, including the users 
is derived using a stochastic point process. Even though in chapter 3, only the most basic 
channel models and point processes were used, the same methodology can be used for 
infinite different combinations of channel models, point processes representing the devices, 
mobile BS, etc… This brings into question; how can this be made beneficial to 5G planning? 
It is possible to take a stochastic geometry point of view for many other technologies that are 
considered candidates for 5G, such as D2D(device-to-device) networks, where the 
construction of the IoT network can be done stochastically, and countless variations of the 
baseline analytical expression can be obtained representing different situations. A very recent 
example for this is the usage of self-driving cars that are governed by a vehicular network[35], 
where each vehicle is represented by an element in a Cox process created by a 2-factor 
randomness including an independent 1-D PPP on a 2-D Poisson Line Process (PLP). 
Combinations like these allow for a broad range of experimentation regarding the relation 
between the physical deployments of the elements in a network and how the elements of a 
compound stochastic process can represent them.  
However, experimenting only with different stochastic processes isn’t the only manner of 
creating different models. There are many other candidate technologies within 5G that are 
based on higher efficiency in the spectrum, rather than optimum physical representation. 
Perhaps the most crucial one of these technologies is Massive MIMO. Massive MIMO is an 
entirely different topic of research. It is mentioned here because it is part of the spectrum 
optimization solution to 5G. This means that with regards to the network infrastructure it 
doesn’t impose an immediate change regarding the HetNet models, but rather focuses on 
transceiver design in individual BSs. It is possible to modify the analytical expressions for the 
stochastic geometry models of the cellular networks as it is done in[36][37] to create network 
models that theoretically employ Massive MIMO along with mmWs, which are often held as 
a complementary technology with MIMO.  Another example can be related to a Cognitive 
Radio system[38], where the idea is to optimize the existing spectrum rather than the physical 
==PAGE:100==
99 
 
deployment, meaning that the HetNet studies conducted here can be independently 
developed into more complex models without modifying the structures of the network. This 
implies that the results here can formulate the baseline performance for a network model, 
where the idea will be to improve the coverage and capacity performance using the candidate 
technologies for 5G. 
In addition to these various technologies and the modification of analytical models to account 
for them, the existing models can be further experimented by modifying the nature of the 
wireless channel that was simply defined as a Rayleigh fading channel for this thesis. Using 
the wireless channel models shown in chapter 2, it becomes possible to branch out even more 
with the potential possibility of the models that can be constructed. Combining the candidate 
technologies for 5G and devising the wireless channel environment allows for higher 
versatility in the modelling of these networks. 
Furthermore, 5G employs a brand-new radio access scheme known as NOMA (Non-
Orthogonal Multiple Access)[3], which is based on scheduling users on the same frequency 
and distinguishing based upon differences in power signatures at the receiver. Accounting for 
the usage of NOMA in a stochastic manner as it was done in[4] is another starting point for 
further building upon the HetNet models that were studied here. 
Of course, the question for the mathematical expressions is always the issue of analytical 
tractability. Having simple expressions for the behaviour of the entire network can make it so 
that any further research for that network model can be built atop the readily derived 
analytical expressions. This process progressively makes the innovation of new network 
models easier.  
The fact that the entire network can be constructed from stochastic point processes and made 
to resemble the real-life network, means that in the end, the behaviour of the model can be 
brought down to a single expression, which can be modified in any way the designer wants it. 
There are of course always questions of analytical tractability, but that’s part of the research 
that will create tractable expressions, where the network models are as close to reality as 
possible.

6  - Conclusion 
The 5G standard has many candidate technologies that have many possible combinations for 
obtaining the necessary performance requirements of the standard. In this thesis, perhaps 
the most baseline technology, that has already been in place in the LTE networks has been 
under focus: the HetNets. In today’s world, it is impossible to think of a mobile network that 
isn’t a HetNet, because it would simply be impossible to provide the promised performance 
with a single-tier network. This thesis takes the time-tested HetNets and uses stochastic 
geometry-based modelling techniques construct these intricate cellular networks.  
Three main points of study have been carried out here. First, it was shown that using the 
theorems and methodologies established in stochastic geometry, it is possible to derive a 
singular analytical expression that is tractable. Using this expression, the effects of different 
parameters relating to the network, such as the BS density in HetNets and the path-loss 
exponent were tested as well as the effect of the SIR threshold for different tiers.  
The second point, the baseline PPP model that was used for the analytical studies was 
extended to working with more complex point processes derived from the PPP to capture 
different real-life deployments for specific cases. Different small-cell configurations were 
tested in junction with different user distributions to see which combination gave the 
optimum coverage performance. Coverage heat maps were created for the models to better 
visualize the coverage regions for each of the models in terms of SINR. 
An extension to the coverage probability, the throughput of the network models that were 
developed were tested using a modified version of Shannon’s equation so that both the 
coverage and capacity information related to a network model would be available for the 
select models. Later on data rate heatmaps corresponding to each model were created for a 
better visualization of low bitrate regions.  
Finally, a discussion regarding each model took place, regarding the strengths and 
weaknesses of HetNet configuration depending on the situation.

were built using different stochastic point processes. It is possible to extend this study with 
even more point processes to optimize the HetNets. Additionally, as it was mentioned, the 
cell densities can be manipulated to increase capacity and coverage to lay a better foundation 
for 5G.

methodology in chapter 3, it can be possible to derive the analytically tractable expressions 
for different models. The derivations will be based on the difference in the distance 
distributions for each model and the definition of the wireless channel. These analytical 
studies go to show that the characterization of complex HetNets is possible with a single 
expression. These expressions are generally for describing the coverage probability of a 
specific network, however with further derivations it becomes possible to access more 
network parameters, such as modified ergodic bitrates and average throughput expressions. 
The derived expressions can then be combined with more intricate candidate technologies 
for 5G, such as MIMO, where the spatial scaling of the wireless channel as well as the inclusion 
of extra pilot sequences will have a potentially interesting effect on the analytical 
methodology. Additionally, the usage of mmW, where the spectrum will change, affecting the 
modelling of the wireless channel, where in higher frequencies different factors can come 
into play; D2D networks, which are an essential part of the IoT that will be a use case for 5G, 
can be further developed using these point processes where different models can be used to 
capture the random distribution of the devices across the 3-D environment in addition to the 
more popular models used in this thesis. Different solutions to Cognitive Radio and machine 
learning applications for dynamic modulation learning can be developed where the serving-
BS selection model for the chapter 3 study will be modified, leading to new ways defining the 
distances between the users and the BS.  
The mathematical modelling with the incorporation of different technologies and the 
modelling of the networks themselves based on different combinations of stochastic point 
processes yields many possible research extensions to the work that was carried out here.
