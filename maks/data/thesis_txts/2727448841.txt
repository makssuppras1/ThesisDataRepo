Abstract
This thesis addresses the problem of detecting sensor failures in Wind Turbines. The
collaborative company provided the available sensor package and defined sensor fail¬≠
ure modes. Two approaches for estimating the rotor speed were proposed, leveraging
existing wind turbine modeling procedures and the available sensor measurements.
The first approach is based on implementing the Extended Kalman Filter, which utilizes a
basic kinematic model and sensor measurements to determine the rotor speed and po¬≠
sition. The second approach utilizes the reduced drive train model and generator sensor
measurement to estimate the rotor speed. The estimated rotor speed is then used to
generate residuals for the proposed sensor packages.
The generated residuals are evaluated using the General Likelihood Ratio Test to detect
sensor failures. The proposed sensor failure modes were recreated, and the effectiveness
of the designed fault detection scheme was evaluated.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Wind energy generation has garnered significant attention due to the growing demand for
sustainable energy sources in the market. According to Borja¬≠Jaimes et al. 2022, wind
power is one of the most promising and rapidly expanding sustainable energy sources.
Recognized as an environmentally friendly and renewable energy option, wind energy
development has cultivated support from various global entities.
As the demand for wind energy rises, so does the complexity of wind turbine design.
Increased complexity in designs has led to a significant increase in operation and main¬≠
tenance costs for wind turbines (WTs). The cost becomes even more apparent when
dealing with offshore wind turbine systems in remote offshore locations.
To prevent system failures in these complex and large¬≠scale structures, the implemen¬≠
tation of fast fault detection and diagnosis becomes crucial. Rapid detection and diag¬≠
nosis of faults are essential procedures to minimize maintenance expenses and system
downtimes. Unscheduled maintenance resulting from system malfunctions can lead to
costly repair operations. Additionally, prolonged downtimes can result in significant power
losses in energy production.
Table 1.1 illustrates typical wind turbine faults, their severity, and failure rates [Borja¬≠
Jaimes et al. 2022]. The majority of failures with less severity are attributed to electric
components and control systems. In contrast to that, generator and gearbox malfunc¬≠
tions are less common but more significant in terms of severity.
As wind turbines are highly dynamic structures, having sufficient state information is cru¬≠
cial for achieving optimal control solutions. This state information is typically obtained
through selected sensors measuring variables such as rotor speed, wind speed, blade
pitch angle, nacelle yaw direction, and blade loading, combined with state estimation al¬≠
gorithms. The power generated by the wind turbine system depends on the wind speed
experienced by the rotor blades. However, accurate determination of rotor speed can be
challenging due to harsh environmental conditions and highly variable stochastic loads.
Ensuring precise rotor speed estimation is necessary to ensure optimal efficiency and
durability of the wind energy plant.
Moreover, since the size of wind turbines directly affects manufacturing and installation
costs, maximizing the precision of this process is highly imperative. One feasible ap¬≠
proach to achieve this is by ensuring the data integrity of the available sensor package.

1.2.3
Scope
During the lifetime of a wind turbine, sensors may experience deterioration or detachment
from the system. Partial detachment can cause gain errors, while drift errors may arise if
the state of detachment is not constant. In the case of complete detachment, the system
may experience a total loss of sensor measurements. Additionally, mounting errors during
turbine installation can introduce initial offsets or gain errors in sensor measurements.
Suboptimal calibration procedures can also result in residual gain errors.
Given the multitude of failure modes for a given subsystem within a wind turbine, this fault
detection scheme will be limited to detecting the following faults:
1. Axes on accelerometers rotated by 90, 180, or 270 degrees.
2. Incorrect gain settings of acceleration and gyro measurements during installation
(e.g., set to √ó2 or √ó0.5).
3. ‚ÄĚFrozen‚ÄĚ accelerometers and gyro measurements at fixed/constant values for sev¬≠
eral seconds.
4. Gain error in blade load sensors exceeding √ó1.2 or less than √ó0.8 times.
5. Sudden jumps in average values of blade load sensors within seconds.
6. Vibrations caused by the loose fastening of the cabinet where the sensor is placed.
Due to limited knowledge of the model parameters in the real referenced WTS, the tur¬≠
bine‚Äôs operating region was constructed through simulations using empirical data supplied
by Vestas Wind Systems A/S.

1.3
Literature Review
In this section, we aim to introduce the terminology related to fault detection and fault¬≠
tolerant control, which will be discussed further in this thesis. Furthermore, we will also
provide a brief overview of the relevant methods that are used in these fields.
Faults are typically regarded as deviations of a system from its expected or nominal be¬≠
havior. Significant changes in the dynamics of a system can result in a deterioration or
even a complete failure of the system.
Faults are classified as sensor, actuator, or plant faults [Borja¬≠Jaimes et al. 2022]. Plant
faults can alter the dynamic properties of the system, while unreliable sensor measure¬≠
ments can significantly compromise the controller‚Äôs effectiveness. Actuator faults can
either limit or stop the controller from influencing the control system.
The objective of the fault detection and diagnosis (FDD) strategy is to detect and identify
any occurring faults [Borja¬≠Jaimes et al. 2022]. The general FDD scheme usually con¬≠
sists of two units: fault detection part and fault diagnosis part. The fault detection unit is
responsible for determining the occurrence of a fault, while the diagnosis block precisely
identifies the affected component and describes the nature of the fault. These two units
work together to ensure efficient and reliable operation by continuously monitoring system
behavior, comparing it to expected patterns, and analyzing data to identify the root cause
of faults.
According to Borja¬≠Jaimes et al. 2022, existing FDD methods can be classified as follows:
1. model¬≠based techniques,
2. signal¬≠processing based¬≠techniques,
3. data¬≠driven technique.

Model¬≠based fault detection techniques in wind turbines use constructed system models
to identify faults. These techniques can be broadly categorized into three approaches:
residual signals, fault estimation techniques, and set¬≠membership approaches [Borja¬≠
Jaimes et al. 2022]. Residual¬≠based approaches involve comparing the measured out¬≠
puts with estimated state variables to generate residual signals. These residuals are then
evaluated against fixed or adaptive thresholds. Due to their simplicity, residual¬≠based ap¬≠
proaches are commonly used. However, decoupling the residual signals from noise and
model uncertainties is a challenging task. Parity equations, observers, and Kalman filters
are commonly used for fault estimation techniques, while set¬≠membership¬≠based FDD
approaches rely on generating a set of mathematical models that represent the system‚Äôs
behavior under normal operating conditions. Set¬≠membership FDD techniques focus on
identifying inconsistencies between measured inputs and outputs across all sets of mod¬≠
els. If the measured data falls outside the boundaries defined by the set of models, a fault
is detected.
Signal processing¬≠based techniques employ advanced mathematical and statistical op¬≠
erations to extract fault¬≠related information from the measured signals. These techniques
carefully analyze the signals collected from the system with the aim of identifying distinc¬≠
tive patterns or anomalies that indicate the presence of faults.
Data¬≠driven techniques offer an alternative approach by using acquired data from the
Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition (SCADA) system which is used for wind turbine
condition monitoring.
Fault¬≠Tolerant Control (FTC) methods are control systems that can handle component
malfunctions while maintaining performance and stability. The goal of FTC strategies is
to prevent faults from spreading and causing system failures, which can be costly and
pose safety risks.
FTC techniques can be classified as active or passive. A passive FTC system operates
with a predetermined nominal controller that is intended to withstand a specific range of
faults. Since the nominal control is still used in a presence of a fault potential decrease
in performance can occur. An active FTC system has a different controller that becomes
active when the fault is present. The job of an active FTC system is to reconfigure the
system in order to accommodate faults and maintain system stability. As real¬≠time fault
data is crucial for an active FTC, the controller must operate simultaneously with FDD
units.
Model¬≠based techniques for FDD
Observer designs are instrumental in reconstructing or estimating state variables, mainly
when these variables are not directly measurable, or their measuring poses a challenge.
By analyzing the system dynamics and utilizing mathematical models, observers can es¬≠
timate the unmeasured state variables based on available measurements. However, ob¬≠
servers can encounter specific challenges that need to be addressed. One significant
challenge is sensor measurement noise, which can introduce uncertainties and affect the
accuracy of the reconstructed variables. Additionally, accurate determination of the wind
speed poses another challenge that can impact the overall accuracy of the designed ob¬≠
servers.
In wind turbine fault detection, various adaptations of observers are utilized to enhance
the diagnostic capabilities. Some notable examples include Sliding Mode Observers,
Unknown Input Observers, and Linear Parameter Varying (LPV)¬≠based observers.
Kalman Filter (KF) as an optimal observer is an advanced estimation technique for fault

Extended Kalman Filters (EKFs) are non¬≠linear extensions of KFs that offer even better
estimation. However, their optimality relies on selected covariance matrices.
Signal¬≠based techniques for FDD
Signal¬≠based techniques in wind turbine FDD schemes rely on the signals acquired from
sensor measurements. These signals include electrical signals, vibrations, sounds, and
temperature [Borja¬≠Jaimes et al. 2022]. Based on the analysis approach, signal¬≠based
fault diagnosis techniques can be categorized into frequency¬≠domain, time¬≠domain, and
time¬≠frequency techniques [Borja¬≠Jaimes et al. 2022].
The single¬≠based approaches do not require detailed system modeling or redundant sen¬≠
sor measurements, which makes them robust and cost¬≠effective. However, signal¬≠based
techniques face challenges due to nonlinearities in the system, disturbances, and noise
in the measured signals. These factors can introduce difficulties in the accurate detection
and diagnosis of faults in wind turbines.
Data¬≠Driven techniques for FDD
The Data¬≠Driven approaches capitalize on the detailed data analysis that can effectively
identify subtle and significant deviations from the expected nominal behavior. Some of the
commonly employed data¬≠driven techniques in wind turbines are SCADA Data Analysis
and Condition Monitoring.
SCADA systems in wind turbines collect a vast amount of operational data, such as sensor
readings, power output, and wind speed. By analyzing this data using statistical methods,
anomaly detection algorithms, and machine learning techniques, the FDD scheme can
identify abnormal patterns and potential faults.
Condition monitoring techniques involve continuously monitoring various parameters, such
as vibration levels, temperature, oil quality, and component health, to detect deviations
from normal operating conditions. Machine learning algorithms can be trained to use this
historical data in order to create models that can identify early warning signs of potential
faults.

employed a Kalman filter to generate residuals, which were then evaluated using a gen¬≠

In the attempt to achieve reliable fault detection and accommodation schemes for the
wind turbine systems, Peter Fogh Odgaard, Stoustrup, and Kinnaert 2009 designed a

To address the challenge of isolating sensor faults from the converter system, a study of
Peter Fogh Odgaard and Stoustrup 2009 employed an unknown input observer. This has
proven to be quite a challenging task due to the inherent interaction between the estimated
sensor and actuator faults. In simpler terms, it can be challenging to differentiate between
a faulty sensor and a faulty actuator, as both can be the cause of an incorrect sensor
reading. By utilizing an unknown input observer, the study aimed to accurately detect
and diagnose faults in the converter subsystem while accounting for the uncertainties
associated with wind speed. The detection scheme for the converter faults is built upon
the estimated values of the actuator and sensor faults. Estimated faults are combined
in order to form detection signals which isolate and assign the fault to the sensor or the
actuator depending on a case.
The same technique was used again by Peter F. Odgaard and Stoustrup 2010, who im¬≠
plemented an unknown input observer to detect three different sensor fault scenarios in a
Peter Fogh Odgaard, Stoustrup, and Kinnaert 2009 benchmark model. The following sen¬≠
sor faults were considered in this paper: faults in the rotor and generator speed sensors
and a converter sensor fault. The proposed scheme detected the speed sensor faults
in question within the specified requirements given in the benchmark model, while the
converter fault was detected but not within the required time.
Another FDI scheme with the use of the Kalman Filter was explored by Chen et al. 2011.
This paper again specifically targeted the benchmark model introduced by Peter Fogh
Odgaard, Stoustrup, and Kinnaert 2009. Residuals were generated using the Kalman filter
approach, and these residuals were later evaluated using the generalized likelihood ratio
test and cumulative variance index. The designed fault detection scheme implemented
dual sensor redundancy to address the fault diagnosis challenge.
Kiasi et al. 2011 used the generalized likelihood ratio (GLR) test and the associated sta¬≠

Odgaard, Stoustrup, and Kinnaert 2009 benchmark problem. In the provided pepper,
several aspects related to the fault detection and diagnosis (FDI) modules of different
subsystems within a wind turbine were considered. The pitch system‚Äôs fault detection
and diagnosis module was divided into three parts. Each FDI module within the pitch
system was designed to estimate the states based on a linear Kalman filter with a corre¬≠

compliance with requirements, except for an abrupt change in pitch actuator parameters.
The FDI module of the converter system was designed using a simple linear Kalman fil¬≠

potential for formulating the new general FDD framework that can be applied to other wind

2.1
Wind Turbine Overview
Wind turbines are mechanical devices that transform the kinetic energy of the wind into
mechanical energy. They are classified into two types: vertical and horizontal turbines,
depending on the position of the rotor axis. The horizontal¬≠axis wind turbine (HAWT)
is the most commonly used in commercial wind power generation due to its efficiency,
reliability, and long¬≠standing presence in the industry. HAWTs have various sizes and
configurations, which are determined by various factors such as the number of blades,
rotor diameter, hub height, rated power, and control strategy.
On the other hand, vertical wind turbines (VAWTs) have a rotor axis that is positioned
vertically, with blades that rotate around a vertical axis. The gearbox and a generator in
a VAWT are typically placed near the bottom of the turbine structure.

The typical structure of a Horizontal Axis Wind Turbine consists of several interconnected
subsystems that work together to capture wind energy and generate electricity.
In literature, there are several principal subsystems, including:
‚ÄĘ Rotor System: The rotor system includes rotor blades, a hub central part of the rotor
where the blades are attached, and pitch servos housed in the hub. The rotor blades
play a vital role in converting aerodynamic forces into mechanical energy. The hub
element links the rotor to the transmission system, while the pitch actuators regulate
the pitch angle of the blades.
‚ÄĘ Nacelle: The nacelle is positioned at the top of the tower, and it houses critical
components such as the gearbox, low and high¬≠speed shafts, generator, control
systems, brake system, and other electrical components.
‚Äď Brake system: The brake system in a wind turbine serves an important safety
function which is designed to control and stop the rotation of the rotor and the
blades when necessary.

reasons during large wind speeds.
According to the ideal power curve, wind turbines can only generate power in the Partial
and Full Load Region. To accommodate these distinct control requirements employing
two separate sets of controllers is standard practice. Controllers are interconnected using
a bump¬≠less transfer mechanism to ensure smooth transitions between the regions. This
approach allows the wind turbine to adapt its control strategy based on the overall wind
conditions.
2.3
The Wind and its Measured Values
The wind phenomenon is the result of air masses moving across the Earth‚Äôs surface due
to variations in temperature caused by uneven solar radiation. These movements give
rise to powerful air flows known as geostrophic winds winds.
In the lower layers of the atmosphere, typically up to a height of 100 m, the movements
of the wind are influenced by frictional forces. Frictional forces, along with surface¬≠level
obstacles, can cause variations in wind speed and flow direction. Speed variations have
diverse amplitudes, which are manifested over a wide range of frequencies. Moreover,
larger bodies of water, such as seas and lakes, can generate air circulation that resembles
geostrophic winds. These wind patterns, influenced by local factors, are known as local
winds.
A wind turbine located at a specific area interacts with the wind, which is a combination of
geostrophic winds and local winds. The overall wind‚Äôs behavior is influenced by various
factors, including:
‚ÄĘ geographic location,
‚ÄĘ climate,
‚ÄĘ height level above the ground surface,
‚ÄĘ the surface terrain,
‚ÄĘ and the surrounding obstacles
Wind turbines are commonly designed to operate within the lower atmospheric layer,
known as the boundary layer. The boundary layer extends from the Earth‚Äôs surface up to
an altitude of around 200 to 500 meters.

3.5.1
Collective Pitch Control
The collective pitch controller is a fundamental control algorithm utilized in wind turbine
systems to regulate the pitch angles of the rotor blades. Its primary objective is to maintain
the rotational speed of the generator within a desired range, optimizing power output
while ensuring the structural integrity of the turbine. By continuously monitoring various
parameters such as wind speed, generator speed, and power demand, the collective pitch
controller adjusts the pitch angles of the blades accordingly.
Typically implemented as a PI (proportional¬≠integral) controller, the collective pitch con¬≠
troller operates by comparing the desired rotor speed with the actual rotor speed and
generating an error signal. A PI controller is commonly employed for the default pitch
control strategy due to its simplicity, stability, and effectiveness. The proportional term
provides an immediate response to the error, adjusting the pitch angles proportionally to
minimize the difference. The integral term eliminates steady¬≠state error by integrating the
error over time, refining the control response, and improving overall accuracy.
The collective pitch controller plays a crucial role in maintaining a constant rotational
speed of the generator, particularly in the full¬≠load region where optimal performance is
desired. By precisely controlling the pitch angles, the controller ensures that the generator
operates at its rated speed, maximizing power extraction from the wind.
3.5.2
Individual Pitch Control
Individual Pitch Control (IPC) is employed in wind turbine systems to optimize perfor¬≠
mance and efficiency by mitigating the impact of non¬≠uniform wind conditions. IPC allows
for independent adjustment of the pitch angles of each blade, redistributing aerodynamic
loads and maximizing power extraction across the rotor.
While collective pitch control adjusts the pitch angles of all blades collectively to regu¬≠
late generator speed and maintain steady power output, IPC provides blade¬≠level control
based on correcting pitch angle factors that regulate blade loading conditions and aerody¬≠
namic behavior. The blade load sensors measure the out¬≠of¬≠root bending moment, which
varies along the length of each blade as they rotate with the rotor. To assess structural
loads and turbine integrity, the measured bending moments are typically collected at the
root of each blade.
To facilitate analysis and control, the Multi¬≠blade Coordinate (MBC) transformation, also
known as the Coleman¬≠Parks or dq¬≠transformations, is applied to convert the measured
bending moments from the rotating frame aligned with rotor rotation into a fixed frame of

Fault Analysis
In any system, a fault refers to an event or occurrence that alters the system‚Äôs behav¬≠
ior, leading to deviations in its dynamical properties and affecting the performance of the
closed¬≠loop system.
According to Mogens Blanke et al. 2015, faults can be classified into three main cate¬≠
gories:
‚ÄĘ Plant faults: These faults involve changes in the dynamical properties of the plant.
When such changes cannot be tolerated, it becomes necessary to reconfigure the
overall control loop.
‚ÄĘ Sensor faults: Sensor faults occur when the readings from the sensors are signif¬≠
icantly affected, leading to substantial errors. The loss of accurate measurements
makes it challenging to observe the plant‚Äôs state fully.
‚ÄĘ Actuator faults: Actuator faults impact the controller‚Äôs ability to influence the plant,
resulting in a partially uncontrollable system.
When a system experiences faults, it cannot fulfill its intended purpose. Therefore, detect¬≠
ing faults promptly and preventing their propagation is crucial to avoid undesired system
behavior or even complete system failure. This responsibility falls on fault¬≠tolerant con¬≠
trol systems, which incorporate a general feedback/feed¬≠forward control structure and
possess a decision¬≠making layer that can react to faults and adjust the plant‚Äôs behavior
accordingly.
As described by Mogens Blanke et al. 2015, a fault¬≠tolerant control system consists of
two primary units:
‚ÄĘ Fault diagnosis: This unit focuses on detecting and identifying faults by comparing
measured input and output signals with the nominal behavior of the plant.
‚ÄĘ Control redesign: The controller is adapted to the faulty situation, ensuring that the
overall system continues to achieve its intended objectives.
This approach to fault tolerance is known as active fault¬≠tolerant control, distinguishing
it from robust/passive control strategies that rely on pre¬≠designed controllers capable of
withstanding predefined faulty conditions.
4.1
Fault Diagnosis
In fault¬≠tolerant control, fault diagnosis plays a critical role as the initial task in ensuring
the required operation of a system. This involves performing real¬≠time online diagnostics
under dynamic conditions, utilizing information from the derived dynamical model and the
plant‚Äôs real¬≠time input/output signals.
Fault diagnosis can be divided into several essential steps for detecting and identifying
faults within the system. These steps are crucial in determining the presence and nature
of faults. According to Mogens Blanke et al. 2015, the typical diagnostic steps include:
1. Fault Detection: The first step is to detect the occurrence of faults in the system.
This is achieved by comparing the measured behavior of the system with the ex¬≠
pected or nominal behavior. Various techniques can be employed to detect devia¬≠
tions or anomalies, indicating the presence of faults.
34

2. Fault Isolation: Once a fault is detected, the subsequent step isolates the spe¬≠
cific component or subsystem responsible for the fault. Fault isolation techniques
aim to narrow down the potential sources of faults in order to localize the fault and
distinguish it from other system disturbances.
3. Fault Identification and Estimation: After isolating the faulty component or sub¬≠
system, the next step is to identify the type and characteristics of the fault. This
involves determining the fault‚Äôs severity, occurrence, and specific properties. Esti¬≠
mation techniques may be used to quantify the fault and provide accurate informa¬≠
tion for subsequent fault¬≠tolerant actions.
These diagnostic steps are often iterative and may need to be repeated as new faults are
detected or as the system undergoes changes. In the context of wind turbine systems,
various fault detection and diagnosis (FDD) techniques were discussed in the Literature
Review section (1.3), with a particular emphasis on model¬≠based approaches.
In model¬≠based approaches, fault diagnosis relies on a derived mathematical model to
detect and identify faults. The mathematical model provides a set of constraints that
describe the system‚Äôs response to different inputs captures the system‚Äôs dynamics and

Model¬≠based constraints can take various forms depending on the nature of the system.

1. Differential Equations: Differential equations describe the relationship between
the system‚Äôs variables and their rates of change over time.
2. Algebraic Equations: Algebraic equations represent mathematical relationships
between variables in the system.
3. Boundary Conditions: Boundary conditions are constraints that define the behav¬≠
ior of the system at its boundaries or limits.
By incorporating these model¬≠based constraints into the fault diagnosis process, we can
effectively detect and identify faults by comparing the observed system behavior with
the expected behavior predicted by the mathematical model. Deviations between the
expected and actual values can indicate the presence of faults. Fault¬≠tolerant control can
use this information to take appropriate measures and lead the system to recovery.

Wind Turbine Sensor Failure Modes
Wind turbines can experience failures in their process, actuator, and sensor functions
at the system level. The most recent overview of WT failure modes conducted by San¬≠
telo et al. 2022 classified the pitch system, converter, generator, yaw system, gearbox,
control sensors, control communication, blades, and rotor hub as the most critical com¬≠
ponents. Table 2.1 outlines the occurrence and severity of typical faults. Among these,
sensor failures are relatively common but often less severe. However, effectively han¬≠
dling unreliable state information resulting from sensor faults is paramount. Neglecting or
mishandling such information can lead to incorrect control strategies, power losses, or, in
extreme cases, pose a significant risk to the structural safety of the turbine.
Sensors in wind turbines are responsible for monitoring and control, and as such, they
are subjected to various faults. Some common types of faults that occur in wind turbine
sensors include:
1. Stuck/Frozen Sensor: The sensor gets stuck or frozen in a particular position,
resulting in a constant or no output. This can happen due to environmental factors
such as ice formation or mechanical issues.
2. Bias/Offset Fault: The sensor consistently provides output with a constant bias or
offset from the actual value. This can occur due to calibration errors, aging of sensor
components, or electrical issues.
3. Drift Fault: The sensor output gradually deviates from the correct value over time,
even without external disturbances. Aging, temperature effects, or changes in sen¬≠
sor characteristics can cause this.
4. Noise/Interference: The sensor output contains random variations or disturbances
that are unrelated to the measured quantity. This can be due to electrical noise,
electromagnetic interference, or poor grounding.
5. Installation Fault: Issues in the installation or connection between the sensor and
the data acquisition system can cause intermittent or incorrect readings.
According to Qiao and Lu 2015, a variety of sensors, such as anemometers, accelerom¬≠
eters, encoders, particle counters, temperature sensors, AE sensors, oil level, voltage,
current and torque transducers, and humidity sensors, are installed in WTs for condi¬≠
tion monitoring purposes.
However, the sensor failure modes most often researched
and accommodated by model¬≠based approaches are pitch position measurements, rotor
speed measurements, and generator speed measurements. Multiple benchmark models
have mentioned an overview of these sensors‚Äô most common failure modes by Peter F.
Odgaard and Johnson 2013. The severity level of sensor faults in this benchmark model
is generally considered low due to the physical redundancy of the sensors. However,
physical redundancy is not always possible due to increased costs and area limitations.
The sensors package chosen for the scope of this project is responsible for monitoring the
change of aerodynamics impacting the rotor and preventing losses of efficiency in energy
capture. Rotor and rotor blades are observed by blade load sensors and IMU unit with a
focus on the accelerometer and gyroscope measurements.

Structural Analysis
To perform a comprehensive structural analysis of a system, it is necessary to abstract
and simplify the system‚Äôs dynamics into a set of constraints. During this process, the focus
is on the structural properties of the constraints, disregarding their specific characteristics.
The principles and procedures employed in structural analysis are based on the work of
Mogens Blanke et al. 2015.
A typical behavioral model of a system is defined as:
S = (C, Z)
Here, Z = {z1, z2, . . . , zN} represents the set of variables and parameters, and C =
{c1, c2, . . . , cM} represents the set of constraints.
A structural model of the system is represented by a bipartite graph, denoted as:
G = (C, Z, E)
This graph provides a visual depiction of the connections between the variables and con¬≠
straints. The set of edges E represents the relationships among the variables and con¬≠
straints within the graph.

an edge exists between a certain constraint and a variable, that connection in the matrix
is represented with a ‚Äô1‚Äô. Differential constraints and their causality are taken into account
when forming the incidence matrix. Causality in the incidence matrix is indicated by an
X.

Here, K is the subset of known variables, and X is the subset of unknown variables. The

unknown variable, enabling its determination. In other words, matching can be considered
as a subset of edges in the bipartite graph where no edge is repeated and no edge shares
a common node. Matching can be complete concerning C and Z. If matching is complete
concerning Z (|M| = |Z|), all of the unknown variables can be determined using the
constraints in the system.

5.1.2
Rotor Velocity Estimation: Extended Kalman Filter Approach
The following procedure is based on the research conducted by Berkemeyer and Lang
2021, which utilized Extended Kalman Filters in a sensor fusion approach to estimate
the rotor speed and position. Unlike the linear Kalman filter, the Extended Kalman Filter
incorporates the exact nonlinear state update and measurement functions to estimate
the states. Additionally, the Jacobian matrices are employed to approximate the linear
propagation of state covariance MathWorks 2023.
The extended Kalman filter loop consists of two crucial steps: the prediction step and the
update step.
In the prediction step, the filter uses the state model, which describes the system‚Äôs be¬≠
havior, along with the previous state estimate and the input from the previous sample.
By incorporating this information, the filter predicts the state of the system at the current
time step. Additionally, it calculates the uncertainty associated with this prediction, rep¬≠
resented by the a priori covariance matrix. Finally, the prediction step also generates the
expected system output based on the predicted state.
The following equations demonstrate the prediction step of the extended Kalman filter,
where the estimated state and its uncertainty are propagated forward in time based on
the system dynamics and input.

6.1
Windowed Gaussian GLRT
The Generalized Likelihood Ratio Test (GLRT) is a statistical method employed to detect
unknown changes in the statistical properties of a signal that can vary over time.
The GLRT relies on the fundamental principle that the statistical characteristics of the
observed data tend to shift when influenced by a fault. By comparing the likelihoods
of the observed data under two hypotheses‚ÄĒthe null hypothesis representing a fault¬≠
free system and the alternative hypothesis representing a faulty system‚ÄĒthe GLRT can
effectively identify significant deviations indicative of a fault.
Utilizing the framework for hypothesis testing provided by the Neyman¬≠Pearson theorem,
the GLRT actively employs the log¬≠likelihood ratio test to determine a outcome between
the two hypotheses. The explanation and development of the Windowed Gaussian Gen¬≠
eralized Likelihood Ratio Test (GLRT) heavily rely on the work Mogens Blanke et al. 2015.
Given a sample z(k) with probability density p(z(k) | őł) = p(zk | őł), the probability density

6.2
Design of the GLRT for Sensor Fault Diagnosis
This chapter focuses on determining the thresholds and window sizes required for the
GLR fault detection scheme. The design process for each sensor involves multiple steps,
with the main goal being to minimize false alarm detections and maximize the probability
of detection. It is worth mentioning that our residuals may not strictly meet the assump¬≠
tions of being Gaussian, white noise, and independently and identically distributed (IID).
However, the design principles presented in the previous chapter provide a general pro¬≠
cedure that can guide the design process.
To determine the threshold for each GLRT, we solve the integral equation in Equation 6.13
to achieve the desired probability of false alarm, denoted as PF . By setting PF = 10‚ąí5,
we have calculated the corresponding threshold value to be 10.
In order to determine the window size, M, certain information about the characteristics
of the residual is required. Initially, the mean value, mu0, of the non¬≠faulty residual is
calculated. Subsequently, a fault of significant magnitude is induced in the sensor mea¬≠
surement to determine the mean value of the residual in the faulty case ¬Ķ1. Additionally,
the variance of the non¬≠faulty residual ŌÉ is determined numerically from the histogram
plots. Once these values are acquired, it becomes possible to calculate the probability of
detection, based on the equation 6.17, by selecting a specific value for M. The window
size, M, is then iteratively adjusted until the desired probability of detection is achieved.
GLR parameters are further fine¬≠tuned through the simulation process to achieve optimal
fault detection performance. Simulation is done over 100 seconds.

need to be explored to address these specific issues..

Discussion and Conclusion
Based on the simulation results presented in the previous chapter, it can be concluded
that the proposed FDD scheme successfully achieves the following objectives in terms of
fault detection:
‚ÄĘ A feasible approach for rotor speed estimation was designed using the Extended
Kalman (EK) filter. Although this approach was not utilized for the actual estimation
in the current study, it provides a reliable method to estimate the rotor speed and
position based on accelerometer measurements. The EK filter incorporates the
system dynamics and measurement equations to iteratively estimate the rotor speed
and position while considering measurement noise and uncertainties.
‚ÄĘ Detection of incorrect gain settings in accelerometer and gyroscope measurements:
The GLRT design demonstrates reasonable performance in detecting gain errors in
both accelerometer and gyroscope measurements.
‚ÄĘ Detection of axis rotation in accelerometer sensor: The proposed GLRT design is
capable of detecting axis rotation in accelerometer measurements, indicating a suc¬≠
cessful identification of this fault type.
‚ÄĘ Detection of multiplicative faults with gain values: The GLRT design exhibits good
performance in detecting multiplicative faults characterized by gain errors. The de¬≠
tection accuracy improves as the gain error becomes more significant.
Due to the lack of periodicity in the rotor speed signal, false alarms are not observed in
the detection function of the gyroscope GLRT. A more accurate estimation of the rotor
speed also leads to a reduction in the sensitivity of the residual to false alarms. However,
upon examining the autocorrelation function, a high level of correlation among the residual
samples was discovered.
The presence of significant correlation among the residual samples hinders the capa¬≠
bilities of the GLRT, as its design assumes that the residual samples are independent,
identically distributed (IID), exhibit white noise characteristics, and follow a normal distri¬≠
bution. The violation of these assumptions reduces the effectiveness of the GLRT in fault
detection.
One possible approach to address the correlation issue is to whiten the residual using au¬≠
toregressive (AR) or autoregressive moving average (ARMA) models. By applying time
series analysis techniques, the trend of the residual can be modeled, allowing for pre¬≠
dictions of future values based on past values. An ARMA model utilizes the information
from the autocorrelation function in order to recreate the residual as an output, with white
noise as the input signal. The inverted ARMA model can be used as a filter to deliver
white noise to the residual, effectively reducing its correlation.
By whitening the residual using an appropriate ARMA model, the correlation can be mit¬≠
igated, improving the capabilities of the GLRT for detecting faults in the gyroscope mea¬≠
surements.
To improve the rate of false alarms in the detection function of the accelerometer GLRT, a
spectrogram analysis can be performed. This approach is based on the observation that
in the presence of a multiplicative fault, the detection function exhibits periodic behavior,
indicating a significant change in frequency compared to the nominal case.

To generate a spectrogram, the signal is divided into small overlapping segments, similar
to the moving window used in statistical change detection. For each segment, the Fast
Fourier Transform (FFT) is applied to obtain the frequency components. The resulting
spectrum is then plotted as a function of time, with the intensity of each frequency com¬≠
ponent represented by a color or grayscale value. The spectrogram provides valuable
information about the time¬≠varying frequency content of the signal, enabling the identifi¬≠
cation of changes, patterns, and spectral characteristics.

A major limiting factor when it comes to the overall performance of the fault detection
scheme is the error in the estimations which are later used for residual generation. The
accuracy of the blade load estimation relies on capturing the complex interaction between
the wind and the turbine blades. However, due to various factors such as wind variability,
turbulence, and aerodynamic effects, it is difficult to obtain precise and reliable measure¬≠
ments of the wind‚Äôs impact on the blade loads.

The lack of detailed information about the wind conditions and their influence on blade
loads introduces uncertainties and errors in the estimation process. These errors can
propagate into the residual generation stage and compromise the performance of the
fault detection scheme. As a result, detecting faults related to the blade loads may be
less accurate and reliable.
In addition to the challenges posed by the variability of wind loads, another significant
factor contributing to errors in the residual generation is the limited knowledge about sys¬≠
tem model parameters and dynamics. The accuracy of the residual, which is essential for
detecting faults, is affected by uncertainties and inaccuracies in the system model. The
lack of accurate knowledge about these model parameters can lead to discrepancies be¬≠
tween the actual system behavior and the predicted behavior based on the model. As a
result, the residuals generated based on the model predictions may not fully capture the
true system response, leading to errors in fault detection.

Future Work
A key aspect of fault detection is the accuracy of the system model. Future work can
focus on improving the estimation and parameterization of the model to reduce uncer¬≠
tainties and enhance its accuracy. This can involve data¬≠driven approaches, advanced
modeling techniques, and incorporating additional sensor information. By obtaining a
more accurate model, the effectiveness of the proposed FDD system can be significantly
improved.
An interesting direction for future research is to explore the integration of Inertial Mea¬≠
surement Unit (IMU) sensors in the nacelle to improve fault detection capabilities. IMU
sensors can provide additional information about tower movements and vibrations, which
can be correlated with the aerodynamic effects of the wind. By incorporating this sensor
data, it may be possible to estimate better the loads the rotor blades are experiencing.
Another route for future work is to develop an alternative approach for rotor speed es¬≠
timation by utilizing edge¬≠wise blade load sensors. A clear connection of the sensor to
the rotor position has been proven through this study. By leveraging the information from
these sensors, a more accurate and reliable rotor speed estimation can be achieved,
which can further enhance the fault detection system.
The estimation dynamics of blade load sensors can be upgraded to improve the overall
performance of the fault detection and diagnosis (FDD) system. This can involve refining
the modeling and estimation algorithms to capture the blade loads‚Äô dynamics better.
Another interesting avenue for future research is the development of a fault¬≠tolerant con¬≠
troller in the form of virtual sensors. Virtual sensors can be designed to estimate the
system states and fault conditions based on available sensor measurements and system
models. Integrating virtual sensors into the control system makes it possible to recover
the system from faulty sensor dynamics and maintain the desired performance even in
the presence of sensor faults.
