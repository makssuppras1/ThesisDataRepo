Summary
Bridge decks undergo different types of instabilities due to wind. Especially coupled flut-
ter is a threatening instability type among the considered instabilities. To increase the
flutter velocity, many research has been conducted and it was found that a streamlined
shape is one of the factors increasing the flutter velocity. In addition to that, it was
observed that changes in the fairings could improve the flutter velocity as well.
In this thesis, the deck section of 1915 Canakkale Bridge was chosen as a base design and
a scaled model of it with a 1/80 scale was constructed. The scaled model was subjected
to wind tunnel tests at Research and Education Wind Tunnel at DTU Civil Engineering,
Lyngby, Denmark. In the wind tunnel tests, changes in the flutter velocity depending
on the changes in the fairings were observed by utilizing a different fairing shape for
each test section.
When the results from the wind tunnel tests were analysed it was seen that:
• Flutter velocity decreases as the test section gets bluffer.
• Changes in the fairing angle with the horizontal affects the flutter velocity.
• Flutter velocity can be further increased by using streamlined fairings with curved
parts.

Introduction
Background
When designing a bridge deck, wind effect is one of the major concerns among many
other things. Since there have been many wind-related problems in the past, bridge
designers are cautious about both the static and dynamic effects of the wind.
With the necessity to cross longer distances over the years, long-span bridges with twin
box girders emerged.
Since these bridges are susceptible to a high amount of wind
load, prior to their construction they go through a careful design and modelling phase.
After the main design, scaled bridge models are subjected to the wind tunnel test to
observe the behaviour under a significant amount of wind. Although there are various
instabilities occurring on the bridges due to wind, the most profound wind effect that
could yield catastrophic results in the case of twin box girders is considered to be coupled
flutter.
Due to its cruciality coupled flutter is the subject of the many research conducted in
bridge engineering. As a result of the research conducted, it is known that the shape of
the deck cross section and especially fairings affect the onset velocity for coupled flutter.
As the deck shapes become smoother, an increase is observed in the flutter velocity. This
thesis work, along with the already discovered effects of the fairings, aims to investigate
the streamlined twin box girders with curved parts and the effects of these curved parts
on the flutter velocity and behaviour.
Thesis Objectives
In this master thesis work, twin box girders with different types of fairings are tested in
the wind tunnel. With these tests, the effects of the angle between horizontal and the
fairing, curvature of the fairing, and orientation of the fairing on the flutter velocity and
the behaviour of the deck section are aimed to be investigated.

Thesis Structure
The thesis is comprised of 3 parts in which the phases of thesis work are explained
in order. Literature review, experimental information, and experimental analysis and
results are explained in these parts respectively. The content of each part is explained
below:
Introduction: Background, thesis objectives and thesis structure.
Part I: Literature Review

ponents.
Chapter 2: Explanation of the bridge decks and detailing on the twin box
girders.
Chapter 3: A summary of the literature review explaining the static and

CHAPTER 1
Cable Supported Bridges
Cable-supported bridges are the bridge types to be constructed when large spans need
to be overcome. They span from 200m to more than 3000m and are extensively in use
with the increase of spans all over the world.
Cable supported bridges are differentiated from one another as cable-stayed bridge and
suspension bridge depending on their cable configuration:
Suspension bridges comprise a parabolic main cable and vertical hanger cables con-
necting the deck to the main cable. They commonly have a large main span and 2
side spans which in most cases symmetrical with side spans of equal size.(Gimsing &
Georgakis, 2011)
Figure 1.1: Suspension bridge systems with vertical hangers and cable support of three
spans (Gimsing & Georgakis, 2011)
Cable-stayed bridges contain straight cables connecting the deck to the pylons.
Cable-stayed bridges are classified according to stay cable location as fan system or harp
system. In the fan system, all stay cables radiate from the pylon top, whereas parallel
stay cables are used in the harp system. (Gimsing & Georgakis, 2011)
Figure 1.2: Main components of a cable-supported bridge (Gimsing & Georgakis, 2011)
==PAGE:20==
1 Cable Supported Bridges
5
Although cable-stayed bridges and suspension bridges differ from one another due to
different cable systems, the main structural components of the cable-supported bridges
can be summarized as below:
1. Deck is the structural element which carries the major part of the external load
on a cable-supported bridge. A bridge deck does not only carry the dead load, live
load and traﬀic load but it is also subjected to a large wind load due to its wind
area. (Gimsing & Georgakis, 2011)
The deck structure is not only responsible for carrying the loads but also partici-
pates in the transfer of the loads depending on the structural system. In principle,
the action of the deck can be divided into 3 main parts:
• Carrying the load locally between cable anchor points.
• Assisting the cable system in carrying the load globally.
• Distributing the concentrated forces.
2. Cable system carries the load and supports the deck.
In suspension bridges:
• Main cable is the main element carrying the load applied on a suspension
bridge. It is made of high-strength steel wires which are highly eﬀicient in car-
rying the loads and do not create buckling problems. The main cable supports
the deck by hangers and transfers the load to the pylons and anchorages.
• Hanger is the cable connecting the deck structure to the main cable. It
transfers the load applied on the deck to the main cable.
In cable stayed bridges:
• Anchor cable is the cable connecting the top of the pylon to the end support
and it plays an important role in the stability.
• Stay cables comprise straight cables connecting the deck to the pylons and
carrying the load transferred from the deck.
3. Pylons are structural elements that support the cables at the required heights
and transfer the vertical load from cables to the foundation.
4. Anchorage is the structure anchoring the main cable in suspension bridges and
anchor cable in cable-stayed bridges. By doing so, anchorage acts like the end sup-
port of a bridge restricting the movement in the horizontal direction. In addition,
it also transfers the load to the ground.

CHAPTER 2
Bridge Decks
The deck is the structural element which is subjected to the major part of the external
load. As explained in Chapter 1, the deck does not only carry the vertical loads but also
contributes to the stiffness of the overall system and withstands the major part of the
wind loading. Therefore, deck geometry and stiffness affect the overall bridge behaviour
greatly.
2.1
Historical Development
The first major cable-supported bridges were constructed in the early 1800s with the ad-
vancement of technology and production opportunities. Although detailed information
regarding the deck design is not brought to date, it is known that plate girders were
used in the first half of the century. With the advancement in the production of steel,
bridge decks started to change from girders to steel trusses.
Although many bridges were built with plate girders and trusses from the 19th century
to the 20th century, the importance of torsional stiffness and aerodynamic stability was
not fully understood until the Tacoma Narrows Bridge (TNB) collapse in 1940.
TNB had an extremely slender deck with a girder height of 2.45 meters. Instead of
a truss girder, the bridge was designed with a plate girder. Although it was designed
to withstand the designated traﬀic load and the static wind pressure, the deck was
too slender and lacked torsional stiffness. It must be also noted that the girder had a
bluff section as it can be seen in Figure 2.1 which is not favourable for its aerodynamic
stability.
The collapse of the TNB, showed the importance of the girder stiffness. Bridges designed
after the collapse used stiff truss girders to overcome the stability problems. In addition,
given that the collapse of the bridge was observed at a relatively low wind velocity
(19m/s) which is far from the design value, the importance of the aerodynamic design
was understood as well.
In addition to that, the collapse also showed the importance of the aerodynamic response
of the bridges and drew the attention of researchers in this area. Aerodynamic studies
became an important part of the design process for all bridges and also caused previously

2.2
Twin Box Girders
With the increase in span length, aerodynamic considerations regarding the bridge decks
become more pronounced and bridge engineers are forced to create solutions to overcome
the aerodynamic problems.
Coupled flutter is the main problem occurring with the increase in span length. While
flutter stability could be achieved with streamlined trapezoidal box girders for bridges
smaller than 1500m, this is not the case for longer spans. To achieve flutter stability for
spans longer than 1500m, the eﬀicient solution is considered as splitting the deck into
two box structures separated by a central air gap and interconnected by cross beams at
regular intervals. This structure is called the twin box girder.
==PAGE:24==

A typical twin box girder has a streamlined shape with a number of different geometrical
properties which can be adjusted during the design stage. While the main geometrical
properties are illustrated in Figure 2.3, they can be listed as below:
• Gap width,
• Deck width,
• Shape of the bottom corner facing the gap,
• Shape of the nose (fairing),
• Presence of the inspection walkways.
• Shape and position of the external wind barriers for traﬀic safety.
By tailoring these geometrical properties, it is possible to overcome the unwanted aero-
dynamic forces exerted on the girder. However, it must be noted that some of these
parameters directly influence the cost of the structure and therefore requires a compro-
mise between cost eﬀiciency and aerodynamic eﬀiciency. (Zasso et al., 2019)
Numerous studies are conducted about the twin box girders since their emergence. It is
proven that multi-box girders are advantageous in terms of aerodynamic stability:
• Twin box girders increase the lateral stiffness of the deck while having a relatively
low linear mass. (Argentini et al., 2022)
• Internal gap between the girders helps lowering the lift and moment derivatives
as well as the vortex shedding mechanism.
This results in the increase of the
critical flutter speed and it decreases the susceptibility of the bridge to wind-related
problems. (Zasso et al., 2019)
• Twin box girders are more cost eﬀicient compared to truss girders. (Larsen &
Larose, 2015)
Although they are mostly advantageous, twin box girders can also experience some
problems:

3.2
Buffeting
Buffeting is defined as forced structural vibrations which are caused by turbulent fluc-
tuations of the oncoming wind. (Vaz et al., 2018)
Although it does not have as catastrophic effects as flutter, buffeting could cause dis-
comfort to pedestrians, instability of vehicles or fatigue damage on the bridge deck.
(Montoya et al., 2020)
Buffeting concept was first developed in the aeronautical engineering on aircraft wings
and later on, it was applied to cable-supported bridges. While buffeting forces can be
formulated in different ways depending on the analysis approach, they are associated
with aerodynamic admittance function.

Vortex Shedding
Vortex shedding is an oscillating flow that takes place when the airflow is met by a bluff
body such as a bridge deck. The flow will be separated from the surface and the structure
will undergo vortices which are shedding on either side of the structure periodically and
forming a Von Kármán vortex street.
The vortex shedding process for trapezoidal sections is explained in detail by Larsen and
Poulin (2005):
==PAGE:33==
3.3 Vortex Shedding & Vortex Induced Vibrations
18
Figure 3.4: Sketch of flow/vortex pattern developing in the wake of a box girder cross-
section. (Larsen & Larose, 2015)
• The flow separates as it flows past the upwind knuckle lines (while partly reattach-
ing along the horizontal segments of the box section) and becomes fully separated
once it passes the downwind knuckle lines.
• The flow does not have the suﬀicient momentum to stay attached to the downwind
inclined panels.
Therefore, two free shear flows project downstream from the
knuckle lines.
• Shear flows forms vortex structures and these vortices entrain air from the external
flow and grow in size.
• The vortex is detached or shed from the section with the convective action of the
free stream and leaves room for the formation of a new vortex in the region below
the nose-tail line.
• A new vortex is formed in the same way as the previous one and shed from the
section leaving room for the formation of a new vortex in the region above the
nose-tail line.
• The process continues with the formation and shedding of the vortices in the region
above and below the nose-tail line. This cyclic process is referred to as von Kármán
vortex street.

form. These forces are centred at a frequency the same as the frequency of the vortex
shedding. This frequency is called vortex shedding frequency, fs and is explained by the

Galloping
Galloping, also known as bending flutter or translational galloping, is defined as a single-
degree-of-freedom (SDOF) aeroelastic oscillation which occurs in the transverse direction
to the wind.
Galloping is usually experienced by rectangular cross sections, D-shaped cross sections or
inclined circular cylinders. (Larsen & Larose, 2015) Non-streamlined box-girders with
relatively small width-to-depth ratio are particularly susceptible to galloping as well,

Coupled flutter (also known as classical flutter or two-degree-of-freedom flutter instability)
is a coupling between the first vertical and the first torsional modes of vibration due to
aerodynamic forces. (Brancaleoni et al., 2009)
Coupled flutter was first encountered for aircraft wings and flutter theory was firstly
proposed by Theodorsen (1934). This theory, which is based on flat plate airfoil, is the
foundation of the current flutter calculations.
Throughout the years with much research and development of the box girders, it is under-
stood that Theodorsen’s theory is not valid for the bridge girder sections, since they are
==PAGE:38==

aerodynamically different from aircraft wings. With this understanding, Theodorsen’s
formula is replaced by the aerodynamic flutter coeﬀicients which are known as flutter
derivatives.
Flutter derivatives are obtained through the wind tunnel test and they differ for each
deck section. Although wind tunnel tests are performed for major bridge projects, this
is not the case for all projects. To be able to obtain reasonably accurate results for the
critical flutter wind speed, designers need to make estimations/predictions for a possible
flutter wind speed.
For this purpose, a formula to calculate the critical flutter wind speed was proposed by

CHAPTER 4
Wind Tunnel Test
Wind tunnel test is an essential tool for bridge design in order to evaluate the aerostatic
and aerodynamic properties of the bridges. Since the collapse of the Tacoma Narrows
Bridge, the importance of the wind tunnel test is better understood and cable-supported
bridges are subjected to wind tunnel test prior to construction.
In wind tunnel studies, the goal is to reproduce the wind effects that the original bridge
will undergo on a reduced scale and observe the accuracy and inconveniences of the
design. It must be noted that wind tunnel tests do not yield the optimal design of the
bridge; however, they provide an insight into the accuracy of the design and help the
designer to achieve a better design in terms of aerodynamics point of view. (Vaz et al.,
2018)
4.1
Wind Tunnel Models
Mainly there are two types of wind tunnel tests that are conducted for cable-supported
bridges (Dowell et al., 2022) (Gimsing & Georgakis, 2011):
1. Full bridge model: As the name suggests, the full bridge model is a scaled
version of the entire bridge. It is considered the most realistic reproduction of the
interaction between the aerodynamic forces and all parts of the structure. In order
to fit into the existing wind tunnels, the usual model scales are about 1:300–1:500
and therefore, they require high accuracy of manufacturing.
Thus, full bridge
models are expensive and demanding.
2. Section model: Section models comprise a segment of the deck in a relatively
large model scale between 1:25 and 1:100. Due to the model size, the accuracy
required in manufacturing is less which makes these kinds of tests relatively inex-
pensive. Scaling effects are also reduced due to the large modelling size.
It should be noted that in the section model since the section stands alone indepen-
dent from the other parts of the bridge, interaction between different parts cannot be
taken into account. Therefore, the system damping due to interaction between global
and local oscillations in the cable system cannot be reproduced correctly in tests with

The size of the model is one of the first things decided in the planning of a wind tunnel
test. While deciding the size different aspects are considered such as the size of the wind
tunnel and the purpose of the test.
Scaling based on the size is defined as geometric scale. In all types of tests, the first
defined scaling condition is the geometric scale, which is given as:

In addition to the geometric scale, section models follow the similitude requirements
provided by different scaling laws.
1. Reynolds Scaling Law: In this scaling law, Reynolds number is accepted as the
dominating flow parameter which remains unchanged during scaling.
Remodel = Refull scale
(4.2)
2. Froude Scaling Law: In this scaling law, Froude number is accepted as the

Frmodel = Frfull scale

based on considerations regarding practical issues such as limitations of the wind
tunnel facility (size, airspeed, etc.) or the measurement equipment (maximum
forces, sample frequency, etc.). (Koss, 2017)
In all of the scaling laws, the ratio for the fundamental scales is chosen with respect
to the test condition. Then, dependent scale ratios are calculated according to these
fundamental scales according to the unit combination. (Koss, 2017)

CHAPTER 8
Results & Discussion
In this chapter, results and findings from the wind tunnel tests are presented. All tests
reported in the following sections are conducted at R&E-Wind Tunnel under the same
conditions. The results obtained from the tests are analysed via Matlab scripts and the
findings are discussed in the following sections.
Test Procedure
The experimental setup explained in Chapter 6 is utilized for each test section. Prior to
the start of each test, the eigenfrequency of the system is checked to obtain an accurate
result, and the average of these values is taken in the analysis of the particular system.
Wind tunnel test is started after the eigenfrequency analysis.
For each test section,
different measurements are taken at different wind speeds. Each measurement lasts for
30 seconds. Measurements start from very low wind speeds.
Wind speed is increased in each measurement. After the wind speed is increased, sta-
bilization of the wind speed is awaited.
Once the wind speed and the response are
considered constant, then the measurement is taken. This way, inaccuracy in the results
is tried to be minimized.
Increase in the wind speed is aimed to be very small to be able to observe the instability
point. Therefore, the wind speed of the wind tunnel is increased by around 0.5 to 1m/s
in each measurement. Measurements are continued until very high wind speeds which
coupled flutter could be observed.
Analysis Procedure
Analysis is conducted such that vertical and torsional motion would be considered sep-
arately. For each motion frequency, damping, variance and standard deviation of the
response, min, max. and mean value of the response are estimated with the help of a
Matlab script.

the wind speed graph is plotted. 3 wind speed types used during the analysis:
• Full scale wind speed is used for real-life correspondence of the wind speed.

scaled model.
• Reduced wind speed is the normalized wind speed according to the deck width

CHAPTER 9
Limitations & Future Work
9.1
Limitations
The work presented in this master thesis was conducted under some limitations which
future researchers should be aware of. These limitations could be listed as below:
• The wind tunnel had a dimension of 0.47m x 0.47m. Therefore, the section models
created for testing required to be able to fit in the wind tunnel. This restriction
required careful selection of the geometric scale for the section models.
• Foam and wooden parts of the model were cut by hand; therefore the accuracy of
these parts are not 100% in terms of dimensions. This might have slight effects on
the system.
• Due to angled parts, fairings could not be cut from foam and instead, they were
3d printed. This increased the total mass of the section.
• Different from the actual 1915 Canakkale Bridge deck section, the test section
model did not have wind barriers but only the deck itself. Therefore, the deck
width was smaller. Also, the inertia of the system was calculated to be lower than
the actual deck section. The comparison of the test results with real design could
not be performed.
• The foam material used to model the deck section started to wear off towards the
end of the tests. This required careful treatment of the section model and it might
have also affected the test accuracy of the last test section. Therefore, materials
and connection types should be also chosen very carefully.
9.2
Future Work
This master thesis work investigates both traditional and nontraditional fairing shapes
for twin box girders. Utilization of streamlined fairings and their behaviour under wind
are investigated in a short amount of time within the scope of the project. Results
presented in this work is favoring the streamlined sections with curved fairings; however,
due to the limited time frame of this thesis work the investigations were restricted to the
==PAGE:84==
9.2 Future Work
69
ones reported in this thesis. Therefore, some further work that could be investigated by
the researchers regarding streamlined fairing on twin box girders could include:
• Further investigation of streamlined sections with curved fairings and their im-
provement.
• Investigation and visualisation of the vortex formation for streamlined sections
with curved fairings.
• Investigation of the streamlined twin box girders with curved fairings for different
wind angle of attack.
• CFD analysis for streamlined sections with curved fairings and comparison of re-
sults with wind tunnel test.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
In this master thesis work, the fairing shapes of twin box girders and their effects on the
flutter behaviour are investigated via wind tunnel tests performed in R&E Wind Tunnel
at DTU. The main test section is based on the 1915 Canakkale Bridge deck section and
during the test period fairings were changed for each test.
In the first part of the wind tunnel tests, first three test sections are tested to investigate
the effect of bluffness and the fairing angle with the horizontal on the flutter speed.
• Results from the first part of the tests showed that bluff sections which are not
streamlined with any fairings undergo instability at a lower wind speed and both
heave and torsion response of the section is very sharp.
• When fairings are integrated into the test section as in the case of Test Section
1 and Test Section 3, the performance of the test section against the wind force
improves. The flutter wind speed increases and the response to the wind increases
rather mildly compared to the bluff sections.
• When Test Sections 1 and 3 are investigated, it is also observed that by changing
the angle of the streamlined fairing with the horizontal, the flutter velocity or the
response sharpness could be changed. Since the change in angle in the tests made
is small, the change in flutter velocity is small as well. Therefore, it can be said
that the change in the velocity would depend on the change amount in the angle
and it can be further improved by changing this angle.
In the second part of the tests, streamline test sections with curved fairings are tested.
These section types are not very traditional and their behaviour was unknown to the
author.
• Streamlined sections with curved fairings are tested to see if the flutter velocity
could be further increased with the curvature in the fairing. All of these fairings
had the same height and except for Test Section 7 all of them had the same width
as the original test section. From the tests it is observed that Test Sections 5
and 7 yielded a better performance than the original test section. Therefore, it
==PAGE:86==

could be said that the flutter velocity can be further increased with the help of the
curvature in the fairing.
• When the streamlined sections with curved fairings are compared to each other
it is seen that the Test Section 5 showed the best performance. It was followed
by the Test Section 7. Therefore, it can be said that to have an improved flutter
performance;
– Lower part of the fairing should have the same angle with the horizontal as
the entire deck.
– Curved part of the fairing should be in the upper part of the fairing and not
at the lower part.
• It is also observed that increasing the fairing length to make even more streamlined
fairing does not necessarily yield a better flutter performance. This can be seen
when Test Section 5 and 7 are investigated. Although Test Section 7 looks more
streamlined than Test Section 5 and is expected to have higher flutter velocity, it
starts showing instability at lower flutter velocity.
All in all, it can be concluded that there is room for improvement in the fairing design
of twin box girders. As the fairings become more streamlined and curves are introduced
in the design, the flutter velocity can be further increased. However, it must be noted
that further work is necessary to increase the knowledge about the streamlined sections
with curved fairings and their effects on the flutter velocity of the twin box girders.

APPENDIX A
Calculation of Wind
Velocity With Prandtl Tube
The wind velocity in the R&E Wind Tunnel is measured with a Prandtl (or Pitot) static
tube mounted on the upstream of the wind tunnel. General working mechanism of this
tube is illustrated in Figure A.1.
Figure A.1: Principle sketch of a Prandtl tube (Koss, 2017)
The velocity of a wind tunnel is determined by a Prandtl tube by using the fluid me-
chanics. The pressure in a fluid flow is a combination of contributions from:
• Atmospheric pressure, ρatm,
• Velocity related pressure, pv,
• Effect of internal pressure loss in the test section, pλ.
Background pressure inside the test section derives from summing up these mentioned
contributions. Since the background pressure remains constant for the constant test
conditions it is referred as static pressure pstat.
