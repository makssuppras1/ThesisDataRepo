Abstract
The semi-outdoor space consists of a transparent covering by which a unique environment that is nei-
ther inside or outside is created. By the use of measurements, simulations and a user survey this study
assessed the indoor climate at Hillerød Lilleskole with the main focus on the semi-outdoor space. In
this study, the indoor environment focuses primarily on the thermal environment and the air quality
relating to CO2 concentrations and relative humidity. The measured indoor climate was compared
with the experienced indoor climate by the user survey. The simulations provided knowledge about
the indoor climate in the semi-outdoor space throughout the entire year. In addition, five design varia-
tions of the semi-outdoor space that aimed to reduce overheating were assessed. The design variations
had two approaches; the fist was to reduce high temperatures through increased natural ventilation
by enlarging the opening area in the semi-outdoor space construction. The second approach was to
reduce overheating by external solar shading. The performance of the design variations were compared
concerning temperature, CO2 concentration, relative humidity and air movements.
In this project, it was found that the measured and simulated results for the semi-outdoor space were
consistent regarding temperature and air quality in the semi-outdoor space. The temperature in the
semi-outdoor space was dependent on the outdoor temperature where a linear regression model described
this correlation. The existing semi-outdoor space was simulated to provide thermal comfort 33% of the
time throughout the year when an 18-27°C interval was used.
All design variations enlarged this
time interval to 35-38% throughout the year. Additionally, all design variations extended the outdoor
temperature interval for which thermal comfort could be obtained in the semi-outdoor space by 1°C. The
relative humidity was found to be considerably high most of the time, but the CO2 concentration was
always low in the semi-outdoor space. Regardless of high humidity levels and fluctuating temperatures
were the occupants of the semi-outdoor space in general very pleased with the space. The results showed,
that the semi-outdoor space promotes social interaction between the pupils at school.
The placement of the ventilation openings influenced the temperature distribution and the air move-
ments in the semi-outdoor space. The results showed, that the vertical temperature distribution in the
semi-outdoor space should be changing minimal in the vertical direction as this resulted in lower air
velocities. For a semi-outdoor space where the top and bottom opening were the same size, an even
vertical temperature distribution could be obtained by placing the bottom ventilation openings at a
1.65m height above floor level. When the bottom opening was larger than the top openings, an even
vertical temperature distribution could be obtained by placing the bottom ventilation openings at floor
level.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The population of the world is growing and is expected to reach 9.8 billion people by 2050 [24]. Fur-
thermore, the rate of urbanisation is increasing as well, with the projection of two-thirds of the global
population living in cities by 2050 [24]. The current building stock is expected to increase 1% annually
due to new constructions [62]. With the threat of climate change in mind, this development must be
sustainable when considering future building style and urban planning measures. This entails a reduc-
tion of energy use in the construction sector and a shift to renewable energy sources [24].
To ensure the future of our planet, it has become a global agenda to minimise the consumption of
fossil fuels. In the Paris Agreement from 2015 196 countries agreed on reducing the fossil fuel consump-
tion and thereby lower the greenhouse gas emissions. Denmark has set itself a world leading target to
be a low-carbon society independent of fossil fuels by 2050 [36]. Together with the rest of EU, Denmark
has agreed on reducing the emission of greenhouse gases with 40% by 2030 compared to 1990 levels.
This action is a step towards a global change and a battle against the effects of global warming [22].
Today the building sector is responsible for 40% of the total energy consumption [50]. In line with
the desire for a sustainable transition, the building industry now has stricter regulations and building
codes [6] [29]. This has led to a sustainable development where the building industry is looking for new,
optimised and passive solutions. However, energy savings should not be at the expense of the indoor
climate in buildings. People in colder climates spend 90% of their time indoors because of shelter from
the weather and security [50]. However, green surroundings such as parks and nature increase human
health, creativity and quality of life [34] [51].
The present project explores an innovative architectural idea - the semi-outdoor space. A semi-
outdoor space is a space that is not outside and not inside. It is covered by a transparent material
allowing light to pass into the room while keeping wind and rain outside. The semi-outdoor space is
built according to the principle of a greenhouse and can have many different shapes, for instance, cover
one or more buildings, be the connection between two buildings or be an extension of a single building.
This is illustrated in figure 1.1 [16]. Semi-outdoor spaces can be used as part of residential, commercial
and industrial buildings.
Figure 1.1: Illustration of semi-outdoor spaces
One of the main advantages of the semi-outdoor space is that it can be occupied more often than
an outside space. It extends the possibility of residents to enjoy the outdoor environment with more

1.1
Objective
The objective of this study was to investigate the indoor climate at Hillerød Lilleskole. The main focus
was to assess the thermal environment, air quality and user satisfaction in the semi-outdoor space; sec-
ondly, the adjoining classrooms were assessed. Additionally, design variations of the semi-outdoor space
and the correlation of the thermal environment in the semi-outdoor space and outdoor environment
were investigated. This was achieved through measurements, simulations and questionnaires.
1.2
Scope
This study investigated the semi-outdoor space at Hillerød Lilleskole through measurements, simulations
in ”IDA Indoor Climate and Energy” (IDA ICE), the computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulation
program FloVENT and through questionnaires distributed among the users of the semi-outdoor space.
Due to the projects time-related scope, the measuring period was around two months. In order to
analyse the indoor climate of the semi-outdoor space for the entire year simulations were carried out.
The design variations investigated the degree of opening and external shading primarily related to air
temperature and air movement in the semi-outdoor space. Lastly, in order to link the physical indoor
climate with the experienced climate user questionnaires were distributed.

CHAPTER 2
Background
2.1
Semi-outdoor space
The idea behind the semi-outdoor space originated in the 1960s. Buckminster Fuller developed the
concept and had the idea of covering New York with a large geodetic glass dome. He realised early that
this construction could create a milder climate under the dome. Besides, Fuller predicted considerable
energy savings when transforming a city like New York into a semi-outdoor space. The dome structure
was supposed to regulate the temperature inside the dome and thus obviate a need for individual heating
and cooling systems [4]. His idea is viewed in figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Glass dome over Manhattan [4]
Fuller’s idea centred around a geodesic dome. A geodesic dome is a spherical shell structure composed
for triangles. This structure is distinguished by being very lightweight and at the same time strong.
Compared to any other structure it has the biggest volume to surface area ratio [26]. Although Fuller’s
dome over Manhattan was never built, it has spawned many thoughts about geodesic domes.
2.1.1
Semi-outdoor spaces in existing projects
Fuller’s geodesic dome and idea of creating a semi-outdoor space left its mark on development of private
homes. Close to the Arctic Circle in Sandhornøya, Norway, a family of six has built a geodesic dome
with an embedded private home. The dome, which is seen in figure 2.2, creates a semi-outdoor space
that allows the family to grow fruits and vegetables that would never be able to grow in that region [46].
The Danish company Drivadan A/S has specialised in building greenhouses and glass structures.
In Helsinge, a city in the northern part of Zealand, Drivadan A/S has built a home according to their
concept houseINhouse [18]. The home is seen in figure 2.3. The inhabitants enjoy the mediterranean

2.1.2
Effects of a green environment
It is proven that daily contact with nature will improve the physical and mental health state of a person
[34]. Staying in green areas do have a de-stressing effect on humans and will balance once inner clock
and thereby enhance the quality of sleep [34]. Even short contact or the possibility of looking out on a
green environment can help relieve the body’s stress level. Furthermore, contact with nature can make
people feel less tired and improve the concentration ability. It is shown that people feel a higher level
of quality of life if they have green areas in their local community [34].
A study among 17 Danish creative professionals investigated people’s creativity when surrounded by
nature. On a psychological level, there is no difference between the creativity of artists and the creativ-
ity of others when they create something new within a field. Through 17 semi-structured interviews, it
was found that nature and a green environment enhance creativity on multiple levels. The interviewees
expressed that they are more sensitive towards problems and get new ideas instead of replicating old
ideas when surrounded by nature. Furthermore, interviewees expressed that nature makes them feel
less stressed and more flexible. They can shift between different perspectives and view a problem from
different angles. Additionally, nature affects people’s mood in a good way. Peoples are more likely to
be creative in a good mood and not when they are depressed or sad. Naturally, not all types of nature
stimulate the same individuals equally [51].

The effects of a green environment are highly relevant in schools. A school should ensure the best
possible foundation for learning and understanding. It forms the foundation for the teachers to pass
knowledge onto the pupils and for everyone to express themselves creatively. The semi-outdoor space
can open up the possibility to enjoy nature and outdoor life on a daily basis. Even short contact like
4-7 min with nature can lessen sick days, reduce stress symptoms like headache and indigestion [34].
This can enhance the daily learning and the well-being of everyone.
2.1.3
Potential of energy saving
The semi-outdoor space promotes higher temperatures and lowers wind speeds compared to the outdoor
conditions. This can potentially reduce the heating loss from an embedded inner house or the adjacent
house. A case study in Montreal, Canada, on a fully gazed dome with an embedded inner house showed
that the inner house can save up to 60% of heating demand throughout the year compared to the same
house without the semi-outdoor space. This is due to the passive solar heating in summer and winter
[41].
The master thesis by Tranekjær [56] concentrated on how the shape and volume of the semi-outdoor
space influenced the indoor climate of the semi-outdoor space and the indoor climate and energy con-
sumption of the embedded inner house.
Two domes-, two rectangular-, and two triangular shaped
semi-outdoor spaces at respectively 750 m3 and 1500 m3 were investigated. The study showed that the
shape of the semi-outdoor space affected the results notable more than the volume of the semi-outdoor
space. Furthermore, an average annual energy reduction of 40% in the inner house that was encapsu-
lated in a semi-outdoor space was found. The savings were due to a reduction in the heating demand [56].
2.2
Thermal comfort
Thermal comfort is a state of mind where no thermal discomfort is experienced. The primary premise
for thermal comfort is that a person is satisfied with the thermal environment and does not experience
any discomfort due to high or low temperature, draught or the like. Individual differences both physi-
cally and mentally imply that a group of people exposed to the same thermal environment will not be
equally satisfied [2]. Experiments have shown that it is only possible to satisfy 95% of the people. In
reality a satisfaction rate of 90% is considered satisfactory [14] [47] .
The six standard indoor environment parameters can determine the thermal comfort level of a
person. The first four climate parameters are related to the occupied zone, and the last two are related
to the person [2] [47]:
• Air temperature
• Air velocity
• Humidity
• Mean mean radiant temperature

• Activity level
• Clothing insulation
The mathematical expression for calculating the Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) includes the six stan-
dard indoor environment parameters [47]. Based on the heat balance of the body, (PMV) is an index
that predicts the mean value of a large group of people in the same thermal environment. The PMV
index is a seven-point scale, that expresses an objective value (warm/ cold). The index does not indicate
the quality of the thermal environment [12][47].
The PMV index is derived for steady-state conditions and is shown in figure 2.6. Thermal comfort
can be evaluated by the Predicted Percentage of Dissatisfied (PPD). The PPD indicates the percentage
of people that with a given activity and clothing level will be dissatisfied while being exposed to the
same thermal environment. Figure 2.7 shows the correlation between PMV and PPD. It illustrates that
within the PMV range of -0,5 to +0,5 there will be less than 10% of dissatisfied. Even if the PMV value
is neutral, there will still be a minimum of 5% of dissatisfied [12][47].

2.3
General requirements for indoor climate in buildings
The expectations towards the indoor environment in buildings are mostly high and the user anticipates
thermal comfort. Through norms and the building code it has been tried to accommodate the users’
demands. DS/EN 15251 assesses the quality of the indoor environment by four categories where category
one is the best. The categories are meant as a recommended design input for dimensioning of buildings’
HVAC systems [12].
Category I High level of expectation and is recommended for spaces occupied by very sensitive and fragile
persons with special requirements like handicapped, sick, very young children and elderly persons
Category II Normal level of expectation and should be used for new buildings and renovations
Category III An acceptable, moderate level of expectation and may be used for existing buildings
Category IV Values outside the criteria for the above categories. This category should only be accepted
for a limited part of the year
According to DS 3033 the indoor environment can be assessed through nine parameters: venti-
lation rate, CO2 concentration, thermal conditions, radon, formaldehyde, particles, moisture/mould,
daylight/artificial lighting, and acoustic conditions [10]. However, not all nine indoor environment pa-
rameters are categorised in DS 15251. Radon, formaldehyde and particles are being addressed through
ventilation rates in the Danish building regulation [6].
The thermal indoor environment is mostly influenced by the air temperature, average airspeed, ra-
diation temperature of surfaces and secondly by the relative humidity. These parameters are required
by the building regulations and norms DS 15251 and DS 7730.

2.4
Thermal comfort in the semi-outdoor space
Throughout time there has been a significant focus on indoor climate which has lead to well-established
methods to evaluate the thermal indoor climate. Outdoor and semi-outdoor spaces have not had the
same focus which has resulted in no traditional methods of assessing thermal comfort or sensation in
==PAGE:19==
2.4 Thermal comfort in the semi-outdoor space
11
those spaces [28] [35]. A typical approach is to modify the evaluation methods used for the indoor
environment and adjust them to the outdoor environment. However, indoor thermal comfort models
can be challenging to use in outdoor and semi-outdoor spaces since they tend to overestimate discomfort
when applied to outdoor and semi-outdoor spaces [35]. The Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) has been used
to predict the occupants’ thermal comfort in semi-outdoor spaces. In order to use the PMV scale, the
comfort range was enlarged and made more moderate [50]. Such an approach can be inaccurate since
the thermal comfort in such an environment can be more complicated to predict. The reason for this
is that it depends on more variables than the PMV includes.
The thermal condition in the indoor built environment is assessed by classic steady-state methods,
which are based on a person’s heat balance. These models assume that the user is passive and does not
make any changes to improve the thermal conditions. Another approach is the adaptive principle where
the user actively contributes to adjust their thermal well-being. The adaptive model is based on three
inter-related aspects: Physiological, Behavioral (Physical) and Psychological [53]. These are described
below.
Physiological Adaptation does not have central importance in the case of semi-outdoor spaces. It
is more related to extreme environments as it includes body modifications in order to enhance
or lower the heat exchange between the human body and the surroundings. This is expressed by
sweating or shivering as the human body wants to maintain a constant core temperature [28] [49].
Behavioral (Physical) Adaptation describes the changes a person can make in order to adapt to
its surroundings or to adapt their surroundings to their needs. The two ways of adaption can
thereby be identified as a reactive and interactive adaption. Reactive adaption includes a persons
modification of clothing level, change of position or posture or consumption of warm or cold drinks.
If the adaption is interactive, the person will make changes to its environment in order to feel
comfortable. Examples of this could be turning on / off the air conditioning, opening a window
or turning on the radiator [28] [49].
Psychological Adaptation depends on multiple parameters and can, therefore, change from person
to person. Psychological adaptation includes the expectations a person may have towards the in-
door climate. If the person knows that the building is naturally ventilated they expect variations
in temperature and air movement, whereas in a mechanically ventilated building a constant envi-
ronment is expected. Moreover, naturalness, previous experience, time of exposure and perceived
control influence the psychological adaptation [28] [49].
These aspects can influence the perception of the thermal environment positively and negatively.
However, they should be considered when evaluating thermal comfort in semi-outdoor spaces [28]. Nev-
ertheless, some studies examine thermal comfort in outdoor and semi-outdoor spaces.
It has been
found that people who stay in outdoor and semi-outdoor spaces have a wider thermal tolerance than in
an indoor environment. This means that they accept higher changes in temperature better [35] [48] [49] .
Nikolopoulou et al. [48] examine people’s thermal sensation in outdoor spaces. The study conducts
case studies on open urban resting spaces in the city centre of Cambridge. It states that on an average

annual basis, 66% of people should be dissatisfied at these spaces. However, only 10% of the people were
dissatisfied when using these spaces. This low percentage of dissatisfied people is due to psychological
adaption and where people anticipate a warmer/colder climate than indoors. Furthermore, it is due
to physical adaption as seasonal variation of clothing and increased consumption of cold drinks with
increased temperatures. In addition to this, the article states that people like to feel warm and that

Olsen and Andersen [8] show in their diploma bachelor project that occupants of a semi-outdoor

Solar heat gain
Solar heat gain is of great importance to the semi-outdoor space as it is almost exclusively built in
glass. It is built according to the principle of a greenhouse and follows the greenhouse effect where the
sun’s short-wave rays can penetrate the greenhouse, while the long-wave radiation cannot penetrate
[43]. This will result in the semi-outdoor space being heated primary by the sun.
Solar radiation consists of direct and diffuse radiation. Diffuse radiation is solar radiation that has
been scattered into the atmosphere. Direct radiation has not been scattered and is emitted directly by
the sun. To what degree a vertical surface is affected by direct radiation depends on the position of the
sun and thereby the angle of incidence. On a clear day with no clouds, direct radiation accounts for up
to 90% of the total radiation [9]. Figure 2.10 shows the correlation between solar radiation wavelength
and incident flux [37].

2.6
Natural ventilation of the semi-outdoor space
Natural ventilation is important in buildings and rooms where no mechanical ventilation is used. This is
the case in many semi-outdoor spaces. For this reason, it is essential to have a good natural ventilation
design to reduce overheating hours and maintain sufficient air quality.
Natural ventilation occurs through pressure differences. These are created by wind pressure or by
the stack effect. Wind pressure and the stack effect can appear separately but will mostly be found
simultaneously [38].
The stack effect occurs when the air has different densities. The main reason for density differences
in the air is temperature and secondly humidity. Together this causes a pressure difference that drags
air in and out of the building. Warm air has a lower density than colder air and seeks therefore up-
wards. This makes the vertical distance between the intake and outtake of the air an important design
parameter as the thermal pressure grows with the distance [20] [38].
Wind pressure occurs when the construction is exposed to wind resulting in overpressure on the
wind exposed side. This leads to infiltration on the wind wards side and exfiltration on the leeward
sides of the building. The pressure on the facade is dependent on meteorological wind speed, wind
direction, the roughness of terrain, air density, building shape, and building height [21] [38].
In general, there are three types of facade openings. One-sided ventilation which has only one open-
ing towards outside, cross-ventilation that has two or more openings towards outside and the stack
effect that relies on thermal buoyancy. This is illustrated in figure 2.12. As no building is completely
airtight, infiltration and exfiltration occurs concurrently [38].

CHAPTER 3
Methodology
The current chapter covers the methodology applied in the present project. The project addressed
semi-outdoor spaces through a case study at Hillerød Lilleskole consisting of three different approaches.
The school was assessed through measurements, simulations and questionnaires. Figure 3.1 shows how
the elements that contributed to the project.
Figure 3.1: Methodology
The measured data provided the basis for a realistic image of the indoor climate at Hillerød Lilleskole.
The measurements were compared internally and with the measured weather data for the same period
to analyse the performance of the semi-outdoor space. The questionnaires provided an insight to how
the occupants used and experienced the semi-outdoor space. The questionnaires were supplemented
with an interview with the headmaster, Sanne Wiedermann, of Hillerød Lilleskole.
The simulation tool IDA ICE made it possible to simulate the indoor climate of the semi-outdoor
space throughout the year.
It was not possible to analyse the air movements in the semi-outdoor
space in IDA ICE. However, this was an important parameter when analysing natural ventilation.
In the Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) simulation tool FloVENT it was possible to analyse
air movement and temperature distribution in the semi-outdoor space. Moreover, it was possible to
simulate the design variations in IDA ICE and FloVENT to analyse the improvements achieved. In
this way, a holistic understanding of the semi-outdoor space and its possibilities was obtained.

Prior to the program being used to simulate air movements and temperature distribution in the semi-

accurate simulation of the geometry and natural ventilation which included the air temperature and
velocity. The validation was based on a comparison of measurements of air temperature conducted with
HOBO loggers and solar heat gain calculations. The validation ensured that all further simulations were

FloVENT was not able to derive solar heat gain through simulation of solar transmittance through
a window pane. For this reason, each surface was assigned a fixed temperature which converted radiant
heat to convective heat. The temperature was selected, so the heat flow was equal to the calculated
solar heat gain. Validation was performed based on the measured temperature.
FloVENT does not work with wind profiles and does not include the exterior surroundings in its cal-
culations. This excludes wind-driven natural ventilation and infiltration by wind pressure. The model
built in FloVENT was, besides the modelled openings in the facade, completely airtight and did there-
fore not allow any infiltration. For this reason, it was necessary to have an opening area in the top and
at the bottom of the simulation model as this ensured a heat flow. The opening area in the FloVENT
model in the bottom was 1.26m2 and in the top was 4.5m2. In the bottom, the openings were located
where the doors were and were thus symmetrical. At the top, the openings were located where the
semi-outdoor space had current ventilation openings. The resulting simulation did exclusively include
natural ventilation by thermal buoyancy. In order to keep the number of variables low, no thermal
loads from occupants, lighting or equipment were taken into account.

the interval of one month, where the first case was the 22nd of September and the second case was on

3.6.2
Solar shading
Solar screening has two main purposes. The first is to reduce overheating through preventing direct
solar radiation from transmitting through the glazing. The second is to minimize the risk of glare and re-
flections [27]. During summer, solar shading reduces overheating and during winter it improves thermal
insulation of the glazing. This could reduce thermal discomfort due to cold radiation and temperature
asymmetry [61]. The most efficient solar screening are external blinds as these hinder solar radiation
to transmit through the glazing. However, these are often more expensive than internal solar shading
which is easy to install and must not withstand wind and weather [27]. In case of the semi-outdoor
space both internal and external shading will hinder the view out to the exterior surroundings or to the
sky.
For design 4, the solar shading was applied on the pitched roof of the semi-outdoor space. The
external shading was only applied on the glass panels that were unable to open as it would conflict with
ventilation openings, see figure 3.44. The external shading was active from the 1st of May to 1st of
September in the same period as the ventilation openings shown in figure 3.29. During the colder months,
the solar heat gain was important to heat the semi-outdoor space, and therefore the solar shading was
inactive from September to April. The parameters for the integrated shading used in design 4 are listed.

direction. By shifting the same opening upwards and placing the lower edge of the opening at 1.65m

lower maximum velocity and a lower temperature at the outflow point were obtained by shifting the
opening to 1.65m above floor level.

high airflow out while having a low maximum air velocity. The comparison of design 1 and 3 showed
that bigger openings at floor height reduced high airspeeds at floor height and created a more uniform
temperature distribution in the room.
Design 5 had the same opening degree at the bottom but half the opening degree in the top as
design 3. This led to almost the same ability to reduce overheating since the temperature at the outflow
point differed with less than 0.5°C. Design 5 had lower air velocities at floor level and a more uniform
vertical temperature distribution that led to overall lower air velocities in the semi-outdoor space.
Design 4 was the only design that targeted overheating through external shading. Overall, design
4 performed well with the most uniform temperature distribution among the designs. The few door
openings caused the locally high air velocities. However, the air velocity was low in the remaining space.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
The purpose of this project was to assess the thermal environment, air quality and user satisfaction
at Hillerød Lilleskole through measurements, simulations and questionnaires. Additionally, different
natural ventilation strategies for the semi-outdoor space were assessed through five design variations
to see how the opening degree and solar shading effect the thermal conditions in the semi-outdoor space.
Overall, the measured and simulated results found in the thermal environment and air quality were
consistent with each other. The measured and simulated temperatures had a similar proportion of time
within the comfort interval for the measured period. The same applied to the results of respectively
CO2 concentration and relative humidity. Despite the fluctuating temperatures and the high relative
humidity level in the semi-outdoor space, the occupants were in general very satisfied with the semi-
outdoor space.
Regarding natural ventilation, a trend was found. The larger opening degree of the semi-outdoor
space the higher the reduction of overheating in the space. Alternatively, external shading could be
used to reduce overheating. Furthermore, the placement of the openings in the semi-outdoor space had
a significant impact on the vertical temperature distribution and air movement within the space.
5.1
Thermal environment
The measured results on the thermal conditions in the semi-outdoor space agreed overall with the sim-
ulated results. The measured semi-outdoor space had more time within the comfort interval due to
higher outdoor temperatures in the measured period than the simulated semi-outdoor space. For both
the measured and simulated dataset a linear correlation was found with equal slopes. However, the
intercept differed by 2.23°C. The difference might be due to the way IDA ICE handles solar radiation
compared to the real circumstances. Furthermore, the IDA ICE simulations did not take the surround-
ings into account. These influence the temperature development due to shadowing, e.g. from trees or
other buildings.
The average annual operative temperature varied 0.5°C across the designs and was found to be 5.4-
5.9°C higher than the average outdoor temperature. This temperature difference was almost twice as
high as the findings from the previous study by Papachristou et al. [50] where the difference was found
to be 3°C. The variation in temperature difference was presumably due to the schedule of the natural
ventilation system where the system in this project did not run on weekends. This caused extreme
temperatures in the semi-outdoor space that increased the average annual temperature. However, the
temperature difference of Papachristou et al. [50] was in agreement with the intercept of the linear
regressions found by the IDA ICE simulations of the design variations. At low outdoor temperatures,
the temperature difference between the outdoor temperature and the temperature in the semi-outdoor
space was between 2.4°C and 3.3°C
==PAGE:84==
76
5 Discussion
During the data processing, it had become clear that extremely high temperatures occurred when
the windows in the semi-outdoor space were closed on weekends. Due to the lack of natural ventila-
tion, temperatures above 50°C were simulated in the semi-outdoor space. If there had been natural
ventilation on weekends, the most extreme temperatures would have been erased during months with
overheating. This would have led to a greater proportion of time within the comfort interval for the
existing structure in figure 4.11 as for all design variations shown in appendix A.10. The results remain
valid and can still be compared across the designs, but the proportion of time within the comfort inter-
val would have increased if natural ventilation was active on weekends too.
The maximum temperature difference between the semi-outdoor space and the outdoor tempera-
tures was 25°C as presented in figure 4.24. However, the outflow at the high temperatures was low
which indicated that the ventilation openings mostly were closed. This caused high temperatures in
the semi-outdoor space and finally led to large temperature differences. When the ventilation openings
were open, the highest airflow was found at temperature differences of 8-12°C. The previous study by
Lin and Zmeureanu [40] found the maximum temperature difference of a dome-covered house on a single
day in February in Yellowknife, Canada was measured to be 12.4°C. Naturally, the semi-outdoor space
in the present project could reach higher temperatures since the IDA ICE simulation included all year
and not only a single day in February.
The largest average daytime temperature difference between ankle height and head height of a sitting
person was 3°C and was measured in September. The maximum temperature difference between the
measuring points was 8°C and was measured in September too. According to DS 7730, this temperature
difference can lead to thermal discomfort [14]. As the outdoor temperatures decreased the temperature
difference during the daytime between the two measuring points decreased likewise. This indicates that
the most considerable temperature difference between 0.1m and 1.1m could be found during summer.
This would require further measurements and investigations. However, the activities performed in the
semi-outdoor space and the time interval by which the space is used, indicate that discomfort due to
vertical temperature differences will be a minor concern.
None of the simulations showed thermal comfort in the months November to February since the
temperatures were below 18°C and thereby outside the comfort interval of 18-27°C. In total more than
half of the time the temperatures were below 18°C and none of the design variations were able to change
this circumstance. Nevertheless, the environment created in the semi-outdoor space will presumably be
more comfortable than the outdoor environment due to higher temperatures, no rain and no wind.
5.2
Air quality
Due to the large volume compared to the relatively low number of occupants was the CO2 concentration
measured in the semi-outdoor space close to the background concentration throughout the measurement
period. The simulations on the existing design and all design variations performed equally well and
gave no reason to investigate this parameter further. In this context, the air quality was always high in
==PAGE:85==
5.3 Natural ventilation in the semi-outdoor space
77
the semi-outdoor space. Based on the low CO2 levels, the semi-outdoor space can be used to get fresh
air and will be an excellent place to work and concentrate.
In line with the previous studies by Kjeldsen et al. [39] and Toftum et al. [60] it is seen that the
CO2 concentration measured in the new, mechanically ventilated classrooms was noticeably lower com-
pared to the old, naturally ventilated classrooms. The new classrooms were measured and simulated to
perform very well with respectively 87% and 100% of the time within category II or better. As expected
the measurements from the old classrooms showed that the CO2 concentration was higher than recom-
mended since the classrooms only were 61% of the time with category II or better. Throughout the
measured period, the CO2 concentration in the old classrooms increased from 24% of the time within
category III or worse in September to 45% of the time within category III or worse in December.
The CO2 concentration in the old classrooms was often above 1000ppm which currently is the rec-
ommended limit for CO2 concentration for indoor air. Poor classroom environmental conditions are
common in older constructions that are not renovated. They are associated with reduced well-being
and performance of school work [39] [60]. Furthermore, increased sickness absence can be another con-
sequence of poor indoor climate which may lead to decreased learning outcome [39] [60]. From this, a
reduction of the CO2 level may lead to an increased annual attendance [60].
The IDA ICE simulations showed that high relative humidity levels were a concern all year. During
winter the relative humidity level was 49%-71% of the time within category IV, corresponding to below
20% or above 70% relative humidity. During the warmest six months from April to September, the
relative humidity level was 7%-29% within category IV. A standard solution to this problem is to
increase the ventilation rate. During the summer months, the ventilation rate was already high in
the existing design. The design variations increased ventilation rates during the six summer months,
but only design 3 was able to reduce the proportion of time within category IV by 2% to the existing
design throughout the year. During the coldest six months, almost no ventilation occurred since the
temperature in the semi-outdoor space controlled the natural ventilation system. If ventilation was
increased during winter, it would be at the expense of thermal comfort since temperature would be
lower and the wind speed would increase.
5.3
Natural ventilation in the semi-outdoor space
The analysis conducted in IDA ICE showed in figure 4.21 that the larger the total opening degree of
the semi-outdoor space was the larger was the airflow out of the semi-outdoor space. Based on this
analysis, design 1 and design 2 showed similarities as their outflow was almost identical. The same was
presented in table 4.7 as it showed that design 1 and 2’s proportion of time above, below and within
the comfort interval of 18-27°C were identical too. The linear regressions revealed that design 1 and 2
achieved temperatures within the comfort interval at different outdoor temperature intervals.
Design 1 and 2 were examined in FloVENT, where the difference between them became apparent.
Design 1 was distinguished by having significantly higher air velocities which resulted from greater ver-
tical temperature differences. Furthermore, the maximum air velocity was higher, and the temperature

at the top outflow point was higher in design 1 too. Despite that IDA ICE simulations showed that
design 1 and design 2 performed equally regarding thermal comfort and natural ventilation, FloVENT
showed a clear difference between the designs.
Design 3 was the design with the largest opening degree, and design 4 was the only design operating
with external sun shading. Despite the two different approaches towards a reduction of overheating
was their ability to reduce overheating hours almost identical as seen from the time below, within and
above the comfort interval in table 4.7. Also, the temperature development in relation to the outdoor
temperature was almost identical as seen from the slope and the intercept found in the linear regression.
The analysis in FloVENT showed that the two designs resulted in different vertical temperature
developments and air movements. Design 4 had a more uniform vertical temperature development than

The biggest shortcoming of design 4 is that external solar shading prevents the view from semi-

The sky view will be blocked every time the external

heat gain derives from direct solar radiation and therefore it was important to screen for it to avoid
overheating. In practice, however, it will result in the solar shading being either rolled out much of the
time or that the external shading rolls in and out often during a day due to changing weather.
The simulation tool FloVENT has limitations on the geometry resulting in a simplified simulations
model. It was not possible to model the entire semi-outdoor space of Hillerød Lilleskole, so instead
a segment with simplifications on the geometry was modelled. The previous study by Tranekjær [56]
investigated indoor climate in semi-outdoor spaces with three different shapes and two different vol-
umes. It concludes that the shape of the semi-outdoor space has a bigger influence on temperature
development than the volume of the semi-outdoor space has [56]. The air movement is linked to the
temperature distribution and will as a result, also be controlled by the geometry of the semi-outdoor
space. The simplifications to the geometry will naturally influence the air movements in the design
variations; however, it is uncertain how they influence the air movements. All design variations were
assigned to the same limitations which made it possible to compare the designs between each other.
5.4
Adaptive behavior
The reason for the high tolerance and overall satisfaction with the semi-outdoor space may include psy-
chological and physiological factors. From previous studies by Goshayeshi et al. [28] and Nikolopoulou
and Steemers [49], it is known that these factors have a significant influence on how the climate is
experienced both outsides and in the semi-outdoor space.
Psychological parameters as expectation and experience influence the occupants’ perception of the
indoor environment in the semi-outdoor space. In the interview with headmaster Sanne Wiedermann,
==PAGE:87==
5.4 Adaptive behavior
79
it became clear that the school tells the pupils that the semi-outdoor space is not an indoor room and it
therefore follows the temperature development of the outdoor environment. However, the school would
like to use the semi-outdoor space all year. Thus, the school encourages the pupils to put on more
clothing so that they stay warm during colder periods in the semi-outdoor space. This physical change
will make the occupants more tolerant towards low temperatures [28] [49].
70% of the occupants used the semi-outdoor space at Hillerød Lilleskole for short stays lasting up
to 15 min. This can influence the answers to the question of thermal sensation during the week from
the 5th of November to the 9th of November. During short stays, it is not certain that the occupants
achieved heat balance in the semi-outdoor space and thus still be affected by the climate in which they
have previously stayed. One-eighth part of the respondent found the temperatures during that week
sufficient; however only 3 hours of the entire period was within the comfort interval of 18-27°C.
The questionnaire was based on the fact that none of the stays and activities that were carried out
in the semi-outdoor space were forced activities. The pupils were free to choose for what activities they
wanted to use the space. The psychological factor about the freedom of choice concerning the duration
of stay and type of activity has a positive impact on the assessment of general satisfaction with the
semi-outdoor space and thermal comfort in the week of November. A forced stay would possibly give
negative feedback on the overall satisfaction with the semi-outdoor space. In addition, the students’ ex-
pectation of the climate in the semi-outdoor space was not the same as the expectations of a traditional
indoor climate. Thus, the pupils will be more tolerant towards extreme and fluctuating temperatures
[28] [49].
The occupants’ perception towards draught and the ability to hear and speak in the semi-outdoor
space can also be different compared to traditional rooms.
If the occupants expect that there will
be more noise in the semi-outdoor space, they will be more tolerant towards high noise levels. The
same applies to draught. Also, the time of exposure is important in this regard. The main part of the
occupants stayed in the semi-outdoor space 15min or less which may be a too short time to experience
discomfort due to insufficient acoustics and draught [28] [49].
The environmental stimulation can have a positive effect on the occupants’ experience of the semi-
outdoor space too. Even short contact with nature stimulates the mental state of health and increases
the quality of life [51] [34]. The well-being of the occupants might influence the general satisfaction
with the semi-outdoor space.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This study aimed to assess the indoor climate of the semi-outdoor space at Hillerød Lilleskole and to
investigate the impact of different opening designs and solar shading design on the natural ventilation
and indoor climate in the semi-outdoor space. Furthermore, the aim was to compare the physical and
experienced indoor climate.
Overall, the measurements on the thermal conditions and the air quality in the semi-outdoor space
and the new classrooms were in agreement with the simulated results of the existing semi-outdoor space
structure. From the measurements and the simulations, it was concluded that with the semi-outdoor
space there was created an environment that was more comfortable than outdoors large parts of the year.
The temperature in the semi-outdoor space of the simulations followed the same temperature-time
distribution trend and spanned from -8.0-53.6°C. The average annual operative temperature in the semi-
outdoor spaces was 5.4-5.9°C higher than the average annual outdoor temperature. The temperature in
the semi-outdoor space was dependent on the outdoor temperature. For the measured data, the linear
regression with a slope of 1.3 and an intercept of 0.3 expressed this correlation. The simulations showed
a similar linear regression for the existing design and for the design variations where the slope of the
linear regression was 1.1-1.2. The intercept differed and was between 2.4 and 3.3.
All design variations were able to enlarge the proportion of time within the 18-27°C interval and
to enlarge the outdoor temperature interval to the existing design at which thermal comfort in the
semi-outdoor space could be achieved by 1°C. From the simulations, it was concluded that the existing
semi-outdoor space had 33% of the time within the comfort interval of 18-27°C whereas the outdoor
temperature had 16% of the time within the comfort interval. Through design 3, the design with the
largest opening degree, the comfort range was expanded by 5% as overheating was reduced. More than
half of the time the temperature in the semi-outdoor space was below the comfort interval.
The measurements showed a constant average vertical temperature difference of 3°C during the night
between the measuring point at floor height and the top of the semi-outdoor space. During daytime,
an average temperature difference of 3°C in September and an average temperature difference of 1°C
during November was found. The maximum measured temperature difference between 0.1m and 1.1m
above floor level was 8°C. This temperature difference might cause local discomfort.
All classrooms performed well and were measured and simulated to be within the 18-27°C interval
minimum 95% of the time. However, this temperature interval was more conservative than the recom-
mended temperature intervals in DS/EN 15251 [12].
The CO2 concentration in the semi-outdoor space varied only little between the simulations and

were at a minimum of 97% within category II or better according to DS/EN 15251 [12]. The measured
CO2 concentrations were even lower than the simulated concentrations for the semi-outdoor space. The
new classrooms were measured to be 87% of the time and simulated to be 100% of the time within
category II or better. The old classrooms were measured to be 61% of the time within category II or
better. From the investigation, it could be concluded that the semi-outdoor space could be used to
draw fresh air.
When the air quality was assessed according to the relative humidity, it was poor in all semi-outdoor
space designs due to high relative humidity. The measurements agreed and showed similar results. Only
design 3 was able to reduce the relative humidity by 2% compared to the existing design.
From the analysis on air distribution and air movement in the semi-outdoor space, it could be
concluded that the openings should be placed in a way so the semi-outdoor space achieved a uniform
temperature distribution as this resulted in low air velocities and ventilated the space evenly. This
could be obtained by having an opening area with a bottom/ top ratio of around 1 and placing the
lower openings around 1.65m above floor level or by having a bottom/ top ratio of around 1.5 and plac-
ing the lower openings at floor height. Otherwise, external shading could be used to prevent overheating.
The occupants of Hillerød Lilleskole were very pleased with the semi-outdoor space, and they enjoyed
the fresh air, daylight and nature that the semi-outdoor space provided them. Furthermore, the space
provided a basis for meeting other pupils from the other classes. These were the main reasons why
Hillerød Lilleskole originally built the semi-outdoor space. From the questionnaire, it could be concluded
that the students were tolerant of temperatures below the comfort range. It is expected that the same
will apply to temperatures above the comfort range as the occupants of semi-outdoor spaces accept
higher changes in temperature better. However, this would require a further investigation.
There was found no clear trend whether the occupants were troubled by draught or acoustics;
however, the investigations indicate that the semi-outdoor space can perform better. This would require
further investigations too.

A.3
Semi-struktureret interview with the headmaster Sanne
Wiedemann on the 12th of November 2018
Hvordan opstod ideen til Væksthuset?
Ideen opstod da vi skulle bruge nogle ekstra klasselokaler, men vi havde brug for noget andet end blot
tre klasselokaler. Vi havde brug for et sted, hvor eleverne fra 7. 8. og 9. klasse kunne have et ungdom-
sliv. Det skulle være et rum, hvor eleverne havde lyst til at være og som kunne mange forskellige ting.
Rummer skulle være i tråd med vores pædagogik, der skulle kunne modtages undervisning, men det
skulle også bruges som et vindue mod verden. Rummet bliver brugt til arrangementer og til en anden
==PAGE:107==
A.3 Semi-struktureret interview with the headmaster Sanne Wiedemann on the 12th of November 2018
99
måde at være sammen på. På Hillerød Lilleskole talte om at bygge ovenpå den eksisterende skole eller
om at bygge ud i verden. Vi endte med en helt anden løsning.
Hvad var forventningerne til Væksthuset og de nye klasselokaler inden det blev bygget?
Væksthuset er tænkt som et fællesskabende rum. Eleverne skal ikke bare være i deres klasselokaler hele
tiden, men det kan være svart at få teenager ud. De vælter ikke udenfor eller ud på boldbanen af sig
selv. Væksthuset giver dem en mulighed at være sammen på en ny måde, men med velkendte elementer
og møbler som fx trappen. I væksthuset får de lys, luft og natur på en anden måde og kan lægge mærke
til hvordan væksthuset ændrer sig i takt med årstiderne. Hermed får de en masse input, som de ikke
ville have fået hvis de havde været indendørs hele dagen.
Er forventningerne blevet indfriet?
Ja, eleverne lægger mærke til at væksthuset kan noget forskelligt. Rummet er i forandringer og vi
(skolen) har sagt til dem, at de skal tage en trøje på nu hvor det er blevet køligt. Eleverne har fået et
uderum, der er overdækket og det betyder at i skal tage et tæppe eller en trøje på. Eleverne får ikke
lov til at bruge terrasse varmeren. Rummet kan noget andet og får eleverne til at stille nogle spørgsmål
til vores måde at være sammen på, deres egen påklædning og akustik.
Hvem er Væksthuset til?
Vi havde ikke overvejede at bygge Væksthuset til de små klasser. De løber rundt og larmer og det
havde været helt forfærdeligt at have dem inde i glashuset. Akustikken i glashuset egner sig ikke til
de små klasser. Udskolingen, som bruger glashuset, skal vide hvad deres uderum kan også når det
kommer til akustik. Det er vigtigt at man er opmærksom på sin adfærd ude i glashuset. Akustikken i
klasselokalerne er rettet mod undervisning, så byggeriet rummer også den del.
Er der noget i er utilfredse med?
Nej, der er ikke noget der ikke fungerer som det ikke bør. Dog er det vigtigt at vi (Hillerød Lilleskole)
husker os selv på, hvorfor det var vi byggede væksthuset. Væksthuset er ikke et ekstra inderum, det
er et overdækket uderum. Når eleverne kommer og siger, at de synes det er koldt og at de vil have
tændt for terrassevarmerene, så må vi sige til dem at de skal tage en ekstra trøje på for de er først og
fremmest udenfor. Eleverne må gerne få noget frisk luft. Varmer vi væksthuset op, så bliver et jo blot
til et almindeligt drivhus og det er ikke meningen.
Lige nu er vi i gang med at lære at det hele ikke skal være så bekvemt. Hvis der er fodkoldt i
væksthuset på man tilpasse sig med et ekstra sæt sokker eller nogle hjemmesko.
Bliver væksthuset brugt til andet end skole?
Forleden dag blev det brugt til et arrangement om aftenen, hvor vi sad derude med stearinlys og
lyskæder. Det var rigtig hyggeligt og en stor succes. Til jul skal vi bruge det til julemarked, men vi
har mange planer. Vi vil gerne lave lydvandringer, samtalecafeer, musikarrangementer, debataftner og
generelt bruge det til at være sammen med andre mennesker. Vi vil invitere folk herud til andet end
bare undervisning. Til sommer ser vi mulighed for at kunne planlægge arrangementer uden at skulle
==PAGE:108==
100
A Appendices
tænke på om der kommer en byge eller to, for væksthuset har tag på. Hvis det er varmt kan dørene
bare åbnes og arrangementet føles lidt som at være udenfor.
Hvordan er alt i alt jeres tilfredshed omkring Væksthuset?
Generelt er tilfredsheden med rummet meget høj. Man kan pege på at akustikken i rummet ikke er
god til undervisning. Det kan være hårdt for lærerne at skulle undervise i rummet, men omvendt er
det heller ikke det rummet er lavet til. Det er vigtigt at vi (Hillerød Lilleskole) lærer at bruge rummet
på den rigtige måde og til de rigtige ting. Rummet giver os nogle fede muligheder og mere end vi havde
regnet med. Der opstår hele tiden ideer til hvordan vi også kan bruge rummet. Det nemmeste havde
været at bygge tre klasselokaler og et fællesrum, men det skulle det ikke være. Det var ikke det vi ville.
Vi var blevet enige om først at finde ud af hvad vi vil sammen og hvad vi vil med vores børn. Derefter
skulle vi finde ud af hvordan rammerne skulle se ud for det.
Hvordan kom i i kontakt med Drivadan A/s?
Vi blev ved med at tale om det at gøre noget andet og at inddrage uderummet. Vi ville ikke plastre
vores grund til med mursten eller byggeri. Det var vores arkitekt, Iben Nørgaard, der forslog at tage
op til glashuset i Helsinge for at besøge det. Først tænkte vi, at det ville være en dårlig ide at bygge
et nyt skolebyggeri som et glashus, men så kom vi op og besøgte dem. Det blev hurtigt klar for os, at
glashuset er et virkelig fedt rum og kan noget helt andet. Beboerne i glashuset i Helsinge får udvidet
deres hjem med et helt andet liv. Efter vores besøg gik vi hjem og var enige om at det var et glashus
der skulle bygges på Hillrød Lilleskole.
På trods af at Hillerød Lilleskole har en stor grund ville vi ikke have at den skulle bebygges så meget.
Glashuset giver mulighed for at invitere naturen ind og man kan kigge gennem glashuset, så grunden
fortsat virker åben. Ved at sætte palmer ind i glashuset får man fornemmelsen af et subtropisk miljø.
Alt i alt er meget tilfredse med vores Væksthus.
