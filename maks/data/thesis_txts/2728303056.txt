Abstract
With the rapid urbanization of cities worldwide, understanding and managing urban move­
ment have become critical for effective decision­making in urban planning, transportation
systems, and public policy. SimMobility, a transportation simulation platform, generates
large volumes of data capturing the complexities of urban mobility patterns. However, ex­
tracting meaningful insights from such complex data requires effective visualization tech­
niques and user­centric interfaces.
This thesis investigates the role of User Experience (UX) design in facilitating decision­
making processes through the visualization of urban movement data derived from Sim­
Mobility. The primary objective is to enhance the usability and effectiveness of a Minimum
Viable Product (MVP) that leverages UX concepts to provide decision support for urban
planners, policymakers, and transportation experts.
The research begins with an in­depth analysis of existing visualization techniques and
data representations commonly employed in the field. By studying the strengths and
limitations of these approaches, the thesis aims to identify opportunities for improving the
UX design of visualizations to address specific decision­making needs.
The next phase of the study focuses on understanding the requirements and expecta­
tions of target users. Through user­centered design methodologies such as interviews,
surveys, and usability testing, the research aims to uncover insights into their decision­
making processes, information needs, and preferences for visualization techniques.
Based on the findings, the thesis proposes a novel UX design framework tailored for
the MVP, encompassing data representation, interaction design, and visual aesthetics.
The framework aims to provide a user­friendly and intuitive interface that allows users
to explore, analyze, and make informed decisions based on urban movement patterns
derived from simulated data. Various visualization techniques, such as maps, graphs,
and animated visualizations, will be explored and integrated into the design framework.
The proposed UX design framework will be implemented as an MVP visualization tool,
which will be developed and tested iteratively. The iterative development process will
involve incorporating user feedback and refining the UX design concepts to ensure an
optimal user experience.
The evaluation of the MVP will be comprehensive, aiming to assess its usability, effec­
tiveness, and user satisfaction in aiding decision­making processes within urban planning
and transportation domains. To validate its effectiveness, extensive user testing and feed­
back will be gathered. Through iterative testing, the impact of the developed solution will
be measured, allowing for a comparison against existing visualization approaches and
industry standards. This process will ensure that the MVP meets the needs and expec­
tations of its intended users while providing valuable insights for further refinement and
enhancement.
The anticipated outcomes of this research include a deeper understanding of the potential
benefits and challenges associated with processing and visualizing urban movement data
from SimMobility using UX concepts and an MVP that demonstrates the feasibility and
value of user­centric design in urban mobility decision support.

1.1
Narrative Visualization
Static visualizations have historically played a crucial role in bolstering storytelling, of­
ten taking the shape of diagrams and charts that are seamlessly integrated into a larger
body of text. In this established format, the text serves as the primary means of con­
veying the narrative, while the accompanying image typically offers supporting evidence
or supplementary details (Segel and Heer 2010). However, there is an emerging class
of visualizations that seeks to go beyond this traditional approach by combining narra­
tives with interactive graphics and immersive experiences. This new approach, known
as narrative visualization, aims to harness the power of visual storytelling to engage and
communicate information in a more compelling and effective manner.
Narrative visualization enables the integration of narrative elements, such as animations,
buttons and storytelling with visual representations. It leverages techniques from both
data visualization and storytelling to create a coherent and engaging narrative experi­
ence for the audience (Segel and Heer 2010, p. 4). By intertwining data­driven visuals
with narrative structures, narrative visualization offers a unique way to explore complex in­
formation, reveal patterns and insights, and evoke emotional responses from the viewers
(Segel and Heer 2010, p. 5).
One key characteristic of narrative visualization is its interactivity. Unlike static visualiza­
tions, which present a fixed view of the data, narrative visualizations allow users to interact
with the content, explore different perspectives, and engage in a participatory experience
(Hullman, Diakopoulos, and Adar 2013). Interactivity enables users to delve deeper into
the story, uncover hidden details, and personalize their journey through the visualization,
fostering a sense of agency and immersion (Hullman, Diakopoulos, and Adar 2013, p. 4).
The potential applications of narrative visualization are vast. It can be used in fields such
as journalism, education, data journalism, digital storytelling, and information design. For
example, news organizations can use narrative visualizations to present complex stories
and data­driven insights in a more accessible and engaging format, enhancing the under­
standing and retention of information by the readers (Segel and Heer 2010, p. 4).
Researchers and practitioners have developed various approaches and tools for creating
narrative visualizations. Some focus on the design and aesthetics, aiming to create visu­
ally appealing and compelling narratives. Others emphasize the storytelling techniques,
drawing from narrative theory and literature to craft engaging narratives that resonate with
the audience (Kosara 2007). Additionally, technological advancements, such as virtual
reality and augmented reality, offer exciting opportunities for immersive narrative visu­
alizations for example, projects related to the construction industry where workers need

While narrative visualization holds great promise, it also poses challenges and consider­

1.2
Decision Making using Complex Networks
As in any field, the use of visualization tools provides unique challenges in decision mak­
ing. Complex networks provide a powerful framework for studying and understanding the
intricate relationships and dependencies among various entities in different domains, in­
cluding mobility in cities. Decision making, on the other hand, is a fundamental cognitive
process that involves selecting a course of action from a set of alternatives. The combi­
nation of complex networks and decision making offers a valuable approach for analyzing
and improving decision processes related to mobility and urban planning.
In the context of mobility in cities, complex networks can be employed to model and an­
alyze the intricate interactions between transportation systems, individuals, urban infras­
tructure, and other relevant factors. By representing these elements as nodes and their
relationships as edges, complex network models capture the complex patterns and dy­
namics of mobility systems, providing insights into decision­making processes that shape
urban mobility.
Complex networks can contribute to decision making in urban mobility through network
analysis techniques. By analyzing the network properties, such as centrality measures,
one can identify key nodes or entities that play crucial roles in decision processes. For ex­
ample, identifying influential transportation hubs, important road segments, or key stake­
holders can help in optimizing transportation planning, resource allocation, and infrastruc­
ture development (Crucitti, Latora, and Porta 2006).
Simulation and modeling techniques based on complex networks are also valuable for
decision making in urban mobility. By capturing the inter­dependencies and dynamics
of mobility systems, network­based models can simulate different scenarios and predict
their outcomes. For instance, agent­based models that incorporate individual mobility pat­
terns and interactions can simulate how different transportation policies or infrastructure
changes would impact traffic flow, congestion, or accessibility in a city. These simula­
tions provide decision makers with valuable insights to evaluate alternative strategies,
optimize resource allocation, and develop sustainable urban mobility plans (Pfaffenbich­
ler, Emberger, and Shepherd 2008).
Furthermore, complex network analysis can shed light on decision­making biases and
vulnerabilities in urban mobility. By studying the diffusion of information, influence, and
decision cascades within the mobility network, decision makers can identify potential bi­
ases, bottlenecks, or vulnerabilities in decision processes. This understanding can help

1.3
Motivation
In the specific domain of mobility data research, the challenges are multifaceted. Mobil­
ity data encompasses vast streams of information from various sources, such as GPS
devices, mobile applications, public transportation systems, and traffic sensors. Extract­
ing meaningful insights from this data requires sophisticated data processing techniques,
advanced analytics, and domain­specific expertise.
Recognizing the complexity and importance of mobility data research, the motivation ex­
tends to thoroughly understanding the user experience within this field. Through in­depth
user research, including interviews, surveys, and usability testing, it will gain invaluable in­
sights into the unique needs and pain points of mobility researchers and practitioners. This
empathetic approach will enable to develop a platform that aligns with their expectations,
maximizes efficiency, and empowers them to derive actionable insights more effectively.
Moreover, the development of a reliable Minimum Viable Product (MVP) is crucial to the
success and adoption of the proposed platform in the mobility data research field. The
MVP will serve as an initial version of the platform, offering essential functionalities while
maintaining a strong focus on stability and usability. By prioritizing the most critical fea­
tures and iterating based on user feedback, it can rapidly deploy a functional solution that
serves as a solid foundation for further enhancements.
The MVP will be meticulously crafted to accommodate the specific requirements of mobil­
ity data researchers. It will provide seamless data integration, scalable data processing
capabilities, and a suite of analytical tools tailored to address the unique challenges of
mobility data analysis. As the platform matures, additional features and functionalities
can be introduced, guided by ongoing user engagement and feedback.
In conclusion, this thesis seeks to improve the speed of analysis of mobility data research
by addressing the pressing need for rapid, standardized, and user­centric research meth­
ods. Through the development of a standardized platform with a reliable MVP, we aim to
empower mobility researchers and practitioners to extract actionable insights effectively
from vast and complex datasets.

2.1
Mobility Data Simulation and Policy Insights
To enhance decision­making visualizations and provide an enriched user experience, a
robust and central data source is crucial. In this context, SimMobility, a simulation platform
developed by the Future Urban Mobility Research Group at the Singapore­MIT Alliance
for Research and Technology (SMART), serves as the foundation for the project, acting
as a focal point for Future Mobility research evaluations.
SimMobility stands out with its comprehensive integration of mobility­sensitive behav­
ioral models and state­of­the­art scalable simulators. These capabilities enable accu­
rate predictions regarding the impact of mobility demands on various aspects, including
transportation networks, intelligent transportation services, and vehicular emissions. The
platform’s versatility extends to simulating the effects of diverse technology, policy, and
investment options within alternative future scenarios.
Notably, SimMobility’s remarkable strength lies in its ability to model millions of agents, en­
compassing pedestrians, drivers, mobile phones, traffic lights, GPS systems, cars, buses,
and trains. Moreover, the platform covers an extensive temporal scope, ranging from
second­by­second simulations to year­by­year projections, making it a valuable tool for
analyzing transportation dynamics on a national scale (SMART n.d.).
The SimMobility platform incorporates three sub­models, each catering to different time
scales:
1. Short­term (ST) simulator: This micro­level simulator operates with small time steps,
possibly fractions of a second. It includes essential agent decisions such as lane
changing, braking, accelerating, gap acceptance, and route choice. Notably, Sim­
Mobility’s short­term model surpasses conventional traffic micro­simulators by en­
compassing a communications simulator, pedestrians, and public transport ele­
ments (Adnan et al. 2016, p. 13).
2. Mid­term (MT) simulator: Transitioning to a mesoscopic level, the mid­term simula­
tor utilizes time steps ranging from seconds to minutes. It addresses agent decisions
related to route selection, mode of transport, activity patterns, and their scheduling.
The model is specifically designed for activity­based modeling, incorporating ex­
plicit pre­day and within­day behaviors, such as re­routing and re­scheduling, while
accommodating multiple transport modes (Adnan et al. 2016, p. 11).
3. Long­term (LT) simulator: Operating at a macro level, the long­term simulator fo­
cuses on much larger time steps, spanning days, months, or even years. It involves
agent decisions centered around critical life choices, including house and job loca­
tion selection, land development, and car ownership. As a land­use and transport

3.1
User­Centered Design and the Seven Fundamentals of
Design Principle
To ensure the successful development of the data visualization application for transport
analysis, a user­centered design approach was adopted. This approach places the needs,
preferences, and behaviors of the end­users at the core of the development process,
enabling to create a product that is intuitive, efficient, and enjoyable to use.
The seven fundamental design principles developed by Norman 2021 presents a valu­
able framework that serve as a useful guide for designers throughout the design process.
These principles encompass various aspects that influence user behavior and their in­
teractions with the product. By incorporating these principles into the design process,
designers can effectively communicate how the product works and what it offers to users.
1. Discoverability: The first priority was to make the application’s features and func­
tionalities easily discoverable to users. It was achieved this by employing a clean
and minimalistic interface that presented only the essential options, reducing clut­
ter and confusion. Additionally, it was implemented intuitive icons and clear labels
to guide users through the application effortlessly. A comprehensive onboarding
process was also included, allowing users to familiarize themselves with the appli­
cation’s capabilities quickly.
2. Feedback: Feedback is a crucial aspect of any interactive system. To enhance the
user experience, the application provides immediate and informative feedback for
user actions. Whether it is clicking on a button, selecting a specific data point, or
applying a filter, the application responds with visual cues and messages, reaffirming
the user’s interactions. This real­time feedback ensures users remain informed and
in control throughout their analysis process.
3. Constraints: To prevent users from making errors and to guide them towards the
correct usage of the application, has been integrated appropriate constraints. For in­
stance, when users attempt to input invalid data or misuse a feature, the application
gently guides them with contextual messages or temporarily disables inaccessible
options, promoting a sense of safety and preventing unintended consequences.
4. Consistency: Consistency in design fosters familiarity and reduces cognitive load
for users. By establishing a consistent visual and interaction design language through­
out the application. Elements such as buttons, menus, and data representations
14
Integration of UI and Data Visualisation of Individual Mobility Data from Complex Simulations for Policy
Insights
==PAGE:25==
maintain uniformity in appearance and behavior, which aids in creating a seamless
experience for users across different sections of the application.
5. Mapping: The principle of mapping is crucial for data visualization applications, as
it establishes a clear relationship between the data being displayed and the visual
representation. It was ensured that the application’s charts, graphs, and maps were
designed in a way that aligned with users’ mental models of the transportation data.
For example, geographical data was presented on a map with intuitive markers and
color­coding, facilitating a straightforward understanding of spatial patterns.
6. Affordance: Affordance refers to the perceived function or action potential of an
object. In the application, it was designed elements to exhibit clear affordances,
signaling their intended purpose. Buttons, sliders, and interactive elements were
designed with visual cues that invite users to engage with them actively. This ap­
proach reduces the learning curve and empowers users to interact with the applica­
tion confidently.
7. Simplicity: Simplicity was at the heart of the design philosophy. It is possible to
focus on prioritizing essential features and functionalities while eliminating unneces­
sary complexities. By keeping the application streamlined, it was possible to create
a user­friendly environment that enabled users to accomplish their transportation
analyses efficiently and without unnecessary distractions.

Challenges Faced and Lessons Learned
Crafting an MVP of this intricacy was fraught with challenges, both from user and devel­
oper standpoints.
On the front­end (section 4.4), users played a pivotal role in shaping functionalities. The
initial proof of concept (section 5.2.2) posed a formidable challenge, bridging the chasm

tization (section 4.3), mitigating cognitive overload risks stemming from an overly feature­

where it defines what kind of actions the user is entitled to (section 4.7).
In the backend, data processing emerged as a formidable challenge. Given the sub­
stantial volume of data in the mobility sector, establishing an interactive platform where
user­supplied data undergoes efficient processing within acceptable time frames posed
a significant hurdle. Numerous constraints demanded decision­making to steer product
direction. SimMobility’s data variety posed complexities – processing numerous files re­
peatedly for diverse users in limited timeframes proved daunting. Thus, project initiation
pivoted towards prioritizing a single file by the user perspective (subtrip metrics) to serve
as a foundational point for a more comprehensive product. Moreover, substantial efforts
were expended to ensure project reproducibility in future iterations. A complete overhaul
of code and structure was undertaken to maximize clarity for forthcoming developers. This
course of action, while resulting in a complete project restart, proved pivotal.

8.3
Points of Improvement
In the pursuit of refining the Minimum Viable Product (MVP) for visualizing mobility data,
several critical areas require immediate attention to elevate its overall effectiveness and
enhance user satisfaction.
The primary concern is the processing time. The MVP’s current performance faces chal­
lenges when dealing with extensive or intricate datasets, even with the improvement de­
veloped (section 6.1.1) there are still room for improvement. Addressing this issue in­
volves considering the integration of distributed computing frameworks, specifically ex­
ploring the potential of Apache Spark (Inc 2023). By distributing processing tasks across
multiple machines, data processing can be significantly accelerated, resulting in quicker
and more responsive outcomes.
Equally important is the visual aspect of the MVP, which necessitates comprehensive im­
provement. The current design lacks consistency and cohesiveness, potentially impeding
user engagement. A practical solution involves adopting established design systems like
Material Design (Material design 2023) or Ant Design (Group 2023). Such a strategic
shift can considerably enhance the visual appeal and user­friendliness of the interface.
Consistent use of fonts, color palettes, and layout structures can establish a strong visual
identity, ultimately enhancing the overall user experience.
Lastly, the final phase of MVP development requires a dedicated focus on user validation.
The process of iterative testing and user feedback integration holds utmost importance
in aligning the MVP’s functionalities with user expectations and needs. By incorporating
usability assessments, user interviews, and surveys, invaluable insights can be gained
to refine the user experience and ensure that the MVP effectively caters to user require­
ments.
In summary, the path toward an optimized MVP for visualizing mobility data involves ad­
dressing these core concerns. Prioritizing distributed computing for improved processing
efficiency, embracing established design frameworks for visual coherence, and rigorously
validating the MVP with user input can transform the product into a robust and user­centric
solution that resonates with users seeking insightful mobility data visualization tools.

• App Usability

or general analysis.
