Abstract
RF coils in form of a standing-wave microstrip transmission lines (MTL)
have found application in Magnetic Resonance Imagining (MRI). In high
static field, like 7Tesla considered in this thesis, these coils are supposed to
create rotating alternating magnetic field at Lamor frequency, which would
excite protons for the imagining. For higher quality of images, high unifor-
mity of such field is required.
Therefore, in the thesis, problem of field uniformity created by MRI
RF coils is investigated. Base for the work are two articles describing two
design concepts. The first one suggests stepping impedance in order to alter
current distribution along a MTL. The second one suggests splitting MTL
in series of sections and bridging them with capacitors, in order to create
flatter current pattern along a MTL.
As the first step, based on transmission line theory, algorithm (or for-
mula) is derived. That algorithm as an input takes desired current profile
along a MTL, and produces required characteristic impedances, bridging ele-
ments reactances and termination reactance. Based on that limit of current
shaping using stepped impedance is investigated.
In order to establish what would be the best current profile for par-
ticular application, convex optimization is applied. That should allow ob-
taining current profile in order to obtain field of particular properties, with
homogeneity maximized in certain subvolume. Using previously established
formula, complete design of a MTL was obtained.
Based on these two methods, number of prototypes were build and tested
in a workshop setting.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
For purpose of Magnetic Resonance Imagining RF coils, that are subject of
this thesis, are supposed to generate magnetic field of particular properties.
It is not only magnetic field strength that is important in imagining, field
uniformity is also a crucial property. The whole imagined volume should
have good uniformity in order to produce pictures with high contrast and
comparable between each other. Low uniformity leads to uncertainties in
an image, as intensity of a pixel can’t be contributed only to a material
property of represented subvolume. User has to account also for potential
inhomogeneity of the field.
Larger it is, larger the uncertainty [1].
Fur-
ther, larger homogeneity can cause also artifacts appearing in an image, as
discussed in [2].
Figure 1.1: MRI image with field inhomogeneity, map of inhomogeneity, cor-
rected image. Figure taken from [1].
Also, as discussed in [1], improving current distribution of a RF coil is
only one of the methods. However, paraphrasing authors, inhomogeneity
caused by RF coils is a systematic error. Therefore it can’t be accounted for
by averaging results or sophisticated filtering. Either it requires correction
data (which can be actually be obtained from numerical simulations) or by
improving the design itself. Of which two methods, the second one is the
most reliable.

1.2
Microstrip Transmission Lines in MRI
There are other designs for MRI RF coils, like Birdcage, planar coils or
open coaxials [6].
Authors of [6] name few reasons to choose MTL over
other designs for MRI RF coils:
1. MTL structure is simple (this authors intends to change) and very
easy to fabricate (this hopes to keep).

1.2 Microstrip Transmission Lines in MRI

2. Doesn’t require additional shielding (keeping ground planes sufficiently
large).
3. Occupies less space as compared to others.
4. Can be easily applied to volume coils of other shape than cylinder (eg.
dome shaped).
MTL based coils also have their own disadvantages. One that is in focus
in this thesis, is current distribution along it. For 7T MRI Larmor frequency
is about 298 MHz, thus wavelength in free space is about 1m. Therefore in
any standing wave coil longer than about 0.5m in current profile along the
coil there will be place where current magnitude is 0.
And overall field
uniformity will be rather poor. In practice, coils of length of about λ/4 are
used.
Obviously, over years this problem was addressed and worked. Few ap-
proaches are listed below, and serve as a base for further work.

1.2.2
Stepped impedances
Numerous articles appeared discussing possibilities of homogenization of
magnetic field using stepped impedance MTLs, namely [7], [8] or [9].
Proposed idea in aforementioned papers is to split MTL in odd number
of sections and vary impedance so, each odd section has low impedance while
each even section has high impedance.

1.3
Aim of the thesis
Gathering what was already said/written, the aim of this thesis is:
1. Investigate how much better current uniformity can be obtained using
stepped impedance approach.
2. Investigate how much field uniformity can be increased by altering
shape of a MTL.
3. Investigate better current profiles for MRI coil rather than uniform
(flat), that would result in higher uniformity of the field.
4. Establish method to design coils to match any arbitrary current profile
(or at lest quite a few).

Measuring uniformity
In the following thesis various designs and methods are presented and com-
pared with regards to uniformity of the field they are generating. As figures
with increasing complexity or complex compound sentences will appear, it
might be worthwhile to explain how to read them.
2.0.1
Homogeneity
Most commonly used ([10], [8]) measure of field uniformity is field’s homo-
geneity, which is defined as
min|B|
max|B|
, where B is a vector field of magnetic field, defined over arbitrary volume.
It can be also measure for |B+

As seen from the equation, closer the value to 1 (or 100%), better the
uniformity of the field. It is simple and good indicator. However taking
only maximum/minimum might be disadvantageous for designs where high
uniformity is obtained over limited FOV, but sudden drop in field magnitude
would render this indicator very poor.
2.0.2
Statistical index of dispersion
Statistical index of dispersion (IOD) ([11]), defined as
var(|B|)
mean(|B|)
(variance over mean value) will be used also along the previous measure to
resolve pointed out problem. Lower value indicates better uniformity within
examined volume.
This indicator represents better overall field homogeneity, rather than
extreme values. Higher tolerance might be named actually a disadvantage
of this indicator. In this thesis both statistic will be used to evaluate perfor-
mance of various designs.

CHAPTER 4
Microstrip analysis
Even though MTL structures are relatively simple to produce or describe,
they are actually bit difficult to properly analyze. In order to properly syn-
thesize numbers obtained from transmission line theory and optimization
procedures, it is necessary to analyze and understand how various parame-
ters influence their performance.
4.1
Height
In order to enhance strength of the magnetic field above a MTL, distance
between the strip and the ground plane is much larger as compared to a
regular MTL. That happens because in the ground plane currents flows in
opposite direction, therefore creating magnetic field that cancels field created
by currents flowing in the strip. Not much attention is given to choice of
a proper distance. In literature these values were usually between 5 mm to
20 mm. Thus in this work height of 10 mm is applied to all designs and
analysis.
4.2
Characteristic impedance
It is necessary to translate characteristic impedance into actual physical
dimensions of a MTL structure. In chapter 3 of [12] there are given formulas
developed by Wheeler for characteristic impedance MTL based on ϵR of
the substrate, width of the microstrip (W) and height between the ground
plane and the microstrip (H).
Assumption for this formulas is however
infinite ground plane.
In case of this type of RF coils however, ground
plane appears to be quite finite as compared to other dimensions. Therefore
CST analysis of microstrip lines with various sizes of ground plane was
investigated. Height is 10 mm.
Results are presented on figure 4.1. First of all, as expected, Wheeler’s
formula gives good approximation for larger ground planes, where for ground
plane of width of 50mm results are up to 10% off. Which still is actually a
quite good estimation.
Further, from figure 4.1 it is possible to conclude, that:

ious ground plane widths.
• For stepped-impedance MTL design, where main mechanism of homog-
enization is shape of MTL, it is most efficient to operate with small
impedances, where characteristic impedance doesn’t vary much with
width.
• For stepped-impedance MTL design, where main mechanism of ho-
mogenization is current shaping, it is the best to operate with higher
impedances, where small change of dimensions results in larger change
of characteristic impedance.

Effect of the ground plane size
MTL theory usually assumes perfectly conducting, an infinite in size ground
plane beneath the strip. Practice too.
However, as the gap between ground plane and microstrip is not much
smaller than width of the strip, the ground plane appears to be more finite
too.
Author found it difficult to find any good analysis on effects of size of
ground plane.
The closest to the problem appears to be [13], where au-
thors analyze region of validity of quasi-static solutions of Hertzian Electri-
cal Dipole over ground plane. They found that validity of solution critically
depends on thickness of substrate (distance between ground plane and mi-
crostrip).
Therefore few observations based on full wave simulations were made.
Unfortunately they are not supported by proper physical/mathematical anal-
ysis.
First of all, as observed on figure 4.1, reducing ground plane width re-

on figure 4.3 and 4.2 it doesn’t affect neither effective relative permittivity
constant nor propagation constant.

4.5
Varying impedance
As a way to find a proper series of characteristic impedances for multi section
MTL is established, it is necessary to sit on problem how to actually vary
characteristic impedance. Obviously, the most straightforward idea is to
vary width of microstrip. However it does have certain limitations:
• There is practical limit on how much impedance can be varied, there-
fore would be neat to have ability to extend that range further.
• In region nearest to the coil, the width of MTL has as much importance
as current within it, where further from the coil, it is current that has
the largest impact.
In particular, because of the second point, it would be desirable to
vary impedance without changing microstrip width (nor current distribu-
tion across microstrip cut-out).

Equivalence principle
By applying Surface Equivalence Principle it is possible to remove the whole
MTL structure and replace it with equivalent surface electric and magnetic
currents, which would create exactly the same fields as original structure
would (ch.

As such surface would be considered infinite,
validity of such solution would be limited to region above that area. Surface
enclosing the MTL structure can be arbitrary, thus it is possible to construct
solution that would be valid in any region in space, outside of the volume
enclosed by such surface.
If such surface would be chosen to be just above surface of the MTL
(figure 4.12), then the strongest influence of fields in that plane would be
from currents running in part of the MTL just beneath the chosen surface.
It is assumed then that in each x −y cut out fields on the chosen surface
are proportional to currents running through that x −y cutout in the MTL.
Then, using equations 6-107 to 6-111 from [4] it is possible to reconstruct
field created at any point in region of validity by any x −y cut-out. That
way a simple mapping, particularly useful for uniform-width lines (series
capacitors), can be constructed and applied for optimization.

Optimization
In previous chapter method for design of stepped transmission line was de-
veloped and design for maximally flat current profile was obtained. However,
as it will be seen, it might be desirable to obtain other, arbitrary, current
profiles.
For that purpose it might be suitable to use numerical optimization
techniques. There are two approaches possible:
• Direct - design of transmission line profile for most uniform field in
given volume in one step - using techniques like Genetic Algorithms,
Particle Swarm Search, Simulated Annealing...
• With intermediate steps - firstly, getting required current profile for
most uniform field in given volume. Secondly, design of transmission
line profile to obtain current profile as calculated earlier.
The main difference between these two is actually heuristics. Decision
on next step in algorithms like Genetic Algorithm or Simulated Annealing
both have random elements within them, thus every time algorithm starts
it might output different results.
They are very prone to local minima.
Periodic character of functions modelling transmission lines might embrace
that effect. On the other hand, if the problem is split in two subproblems,
both of them can be solved using deterministic algorithms.
It is desirable to maximally utilize deterministic approach and reduce any
amount of heuristics and stochastics. Comfort of having reproducible results,
invariant from Random Number Generator, simply mustn’t be undervalued
[20].
5.1
Fields to currents
Problem of finding right current distribution to obtain desired near or far
field radiation was thoroughly investigated in literature, as [21] or [22]. Var-
ious methods are investigated, both deterministic and stochastic.
In particular, [22] shows how to apply Quadratic Programming to find
current distribution that would increase homogeneity. Authors of the article
consider DC case, however it should be applicable also in a RF case. Authors

5.2.3
Complex optimization
Translation from DC to AC problem brings a problem of complex numbers.
In DC case, every member of A matrix was purely real, as well as x and B.
In this problem, of interest is optimization of magnitudes of fields, which
are complex quantities.
However, in region very close to a source, ratio of real components of
A to it’s imaginary components will be rather high.
In an optimization
carried out in here, the imaginary part was about 10−2 of the real part.
The only region, where the imaginary part is comparable (the same order
of magnitude) as the the real part, is near boundaries, where the values
are about 10−3 of values in other dominating regions. Therefore assuming
quite crude approximation, imaginary part is neglected and only real part
(practically equivalent to absolute value) of each element of A is applied.
5.2.4
What is optimized
From the equation 5.1 it is clear that what is minimized is actually sum of
squares of differences between obtained field and desired field at each given
position. Minimizing this value should increase field uniformity, however it is
not exactly the statistics used for measuring field uniformity (homogeneity
or IOD, ch. 2). It would be difficult, if even possible, to express convex

5.2.6
Tikhonov regularization
As said before, A ∈Rn×m is a matrix where A(i, j) represents magnetic
field at position j-th created by a small cut-out of MTL at position i-th.
Therefore, for some j, which represents points in space, would be much
smaller further from the i-th source as compared to other points, closer to
the source.
Therefore A would be a matrix with a mix of small and large values,
what is represented by high condition number of this matrix. When condi-
tion number is high, then a small change in x leads to a big change in B.
Therefore, optimization would be very unstable, and resulting x would tend
==PAGE:59==
5.2 Quadratic programming

to contain extreme (constrains) values. Any small error would be amplified.
That means the problem is ill-posed [25].
Good estimate of usability of matrix condition is looking at log10(cond(A)),
where cond(A) = ||A|| · ||A+|| and A+ is Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse.
That number is an estimate of how many last digits in given base are prone
to contain an error. As the tool used here is MATLAB, which has by default
16 digits of precision, it would be desirable to reduce that estimate of the A
matrix below that number to obtain reliable results.
Following [22], Tikhonov Regularization is a method in which by adding
additional terms to minimized function ill-posed problem might be turned
to well-posed one. Citing further, the optimization problem would be:
||Ax −B||2
2 + ||Tx||2
2
where T would be Tikhonov matrix. As stated in [22], to give preference to
solutions with lower Euclidean norm, the Tikhonov matrix might be chosen
as T = TI.
Therefore in order to solve initially ill-posed problem, it is necessary to
choose T properly. Too small number will fail to improve conditioning of
the problem. Too large will dominate the problem, so it wouldn’t be solved
with respect to A as much as to T.
Selection of T in this work is bit heuristic, as condition number of result-
ing coefficients matrix will be observed for increasing T. Moment, when T
will be sufficiently large to not loose more than 10 digits, it will be chosen.
As an example, results from 1D optimization is shown on figure 5.1.

2D/3D optimization
Finally, these RF MRI coils should end up arranged in some number around

As established earlier, assumptions allow to use discussed method only

possible.
5.6
Short summary
• It is possible to use convex optimization methods for optimization of
MRI coils, as long as area of interest is close to the MTL itself.
• Proper modelling in order to obtain good A is crucial. Model based
on Surface Equivalence Principle seems to be a good choice, however,
selection of the surface and the whole construction process must be
carried out more carefully. Perhaps a good idea is to construct B so it
accounts for currents near edges, and optimize only currents in some
limited length of a MTL.
• Achieved optimization process/algorithm serves as a good starting
point for further design optimization, rather than end-to-end opti-
mizer, for the moment being.

6.2
Currents against shapes
Despite basis for designs in this section is current shaping, it is actually
most likely due to physical widening of the center sections of the MTL that
gives higher uniformity of fields. Therefore a simple simulation was carried
out, where using 7 segment series capacitor design was applied. Values of
capacitors and termination were chosen so the current profile would match
current in the stepped impedance design. From comparison with such can
be concluded how much improvement is due to current shaping, and how
much to actually physical widening.
On figures 6.7 to 6.9 4 designs are compared:
• Reference uniform line.
• Simulated line as suggested by [8]. Statistics worse even than of an
uniform line are probably because of few reasons. One, almost 20 mm
thick PTFE (ϵr ≈2) is used to fill area between the ground and the
strip, therefore shortening effectively the wavelength. Secondly, the
narrowest patch is in the very center, thus instead of decreasing the
field, field is increased in there. But also it is possible that author of
this thesis made a mistake or misunderstood the paper.
• Stepped impedance line (3rd design).
• Series capacitor line with current shaped to match current profile as
in stepped impedance line (referred to as equivalent current line).
To authors own surprise, the equivalent current line actually represents
quite significant improvement over the uniform line. Above distance of about
60 mm −70 mm it has quite comparable statistics to the stepped impedance
line, and below that distance it actually has much better parameters.
Therefore stepped impedance line actually might be quite useful design,
and current shaping techniques proves to have an impact of overall perfor-
mance of a MRI RF coil.

CHAPTER 7
Experiment
To verify simulations and designs, a series of practical tests was performed.
These tests were performed in the EMC lab (the lab) in building 348 of
DTU. These were not proper tests using a MRI machine, as such is nowhere
to be found in the building. Instead, near field probing using an improvised
probe was performed.
7.1
Near field measurements
To estimate magnetic field magnitude, a VNA with a near field probe was
used. Therefore what was actually measured was |S2,1| between a MTL and
a probe.
As seen from equation 4.41 in [12], |S2,1| = V−
2 /V+
1 . Maxwell-Faraday
equation (table 4.1 in [4]) tells that voltage induced in a closed loop is
proportional to magnetic flux through area enclosed by that loop. Therefore
V−
2 is directly proportional to magnetic field magnitude passing through a
probe. As any tested MTL is matched to 50Ω, then V+
1 seen by the VNA will
be proportional to current in a MTL. And as magnetic field is proportional
to current, then ratios of |S2,1| measured along a MTL should give directly
ratios of magnetic field amplitude at these positions.
7.2
Test setup
To perform measurements a VNA was used, HP8753C VNA to be exact. A
matched MTL was connected to the Port 1, a near field probe was connected
to the Port 2, as on illustration 7.1.
As a near field probe a 2 turn hand-wounded air-core coil was used. Outer
diameter of the coil was 12 mm, wire thickness was 1 mm. Inductance of
the coil was roughly 101 nH. The coil was tuned with a capacitor in parallel
to self resonance at 298 MHz. Obtained |S1,1| (or actually |S2,2|, as the
probe was attached to Port 2 of the VNA) was as good as −20 dB, with
−18 dB ±0.2 dB for most of the measurements without need for rematching.
Between the probe matching circuit and the probe there is a ferrite, which
is supposed to choke currents induced on outer conductor of a transmission

7.3
Practical concerns
As much as theory worked out very well in the simulation, there are some
issues in physical realization of proposed designs.
7.3.1
Inductive parts
Some designs require termination to be inductive. High quality inductors
offer precision as high as 1%. The problem is trimming. In practice it might
be necessary to trim the value, to account for load or other effects didn’t
anticipated before. Trimmable inductors are bit more difficult to obtain.
However it can be resolved by increasing value of inductor and putting in
series a trimmable capacitor. And this is how it is resolved in designs of
series capacitor prototypes.
7.3.2
Sensitivity
Sensitivity of the coil is actually ridiculous. While trimming capacitors using
Vector Network Analyzer, one could observe even 10% variation in measured
S1,1 phase, just by moving head few centimeters above coil.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusions
MRI coils based on a MTL design appear to be very flexible devices, holding
still some possibilities to explore. This thesis had mostly theoretical focus
regarding design procedures and methods, and few conclusions can be drawn
out of this work:
• It is possible to, by only varying impedance, change current so propa-
gation constant ”appears” to be 1.4 smaller. However, that was quite
intuitive analysis, and proper analysis of equations derived might show
more interesting solutions.
• Field distribution can be varied by current profiles. Therefore with
current shaping various specialized MRI RF coils are possible for spe-
cific applications. Necessity for physical widening of the center of such
MTLs plays also significant role in increasing uniformity, however not
dominating. Improvement due to physical widening is visible mostly
further away form a MTL, where current shaping improves uniformity
consistently in the whole volume.
• Presented stepped impedance MTLs where quite violent, however,
hopefully, it should be possible to find a good balance between im-
proving current profile without need for widening sections of a MTL
to extreme.
• Apparently improvement in field homogeneity can be obtained using
stepped impedance approach. Not as much as with series capacitor
type of design, but the stepped impedance designs are much easier to
tune and produce.
• Much larger possibility of improvement in field uniformity holds series
capacitor type of design, as practically any arbitrary shape of current
profile can be obtained. Theoretically. In practice, it is very difficult to
trim termination properly to obtain desired current profile, and thus
field distribution.
• Appears to be possible to apply convex optimization methods in design
of MRI RF coils. It has obvious advantages over more popular and
widely used Genetic Algorithm or other heuristic/stochastic methods.
==PAGE:98==
90
8 Conclusions
• The most difficult part of the optimization proved to be construction
of the A matrix, which determines usability of the whole approach.
Among few tried models, most reliable appears to be model based on
Surface Equivalence Principle. Despite it is possible to simply apply
full-wave simulations for some optimization procedures like Genetic
Algorithm, the computational cost is incomparable.
• Imperfections of PCB production or lumped components can be to
quite large extent compensated by trimming a terminating capacitor.
• In practice trimming the termination is a tedious and frustrating piece
of work. In particular for coils with inductive termination. Not to
mention that they are ridiculously sensitive to changes.
8.1
Further work
In case this subject would be picked for further development, some problems
should be addressed in particular. These are listed below.
• Analyze properly transmission line theory in order to find other stepped
impedance designs that would result in a flatter current profile.
• Derive/define a method to determine proper width of microstrip for
better field uniformity - to improve the design physical width wise, not
current profile wise. Then there would be an elegant way to synthesize
both current shaping and physical widening in order to obtain design
for the highest homogeneity of the magnetic field.
• Investigate further subject of MTL modelling for better A construction,
and extend convex optimization method to full, n-channel arrange-
ment and optimize fields in 3D. That is necessary to obtain almost
end-to-end tool for designing specialized MRI coils.
• Develop proper near-field measurement setup for RF coils testing and
tuning in electronics laboratory conditions. For the moment being it
is consuming way too much time, doesn’t giving really much quality
in a change.
