Abstract 
 
The aim of the study was to evaluate the changes in quality parameters after date of 
minimum durability (MDD) in order to determine weather product remains safe for 
the consumption. Therefore, three different dairy products were subjected to 
microbiological (LAB, probiotics, enterococci, enterobacteria, pseudomonas, yeasts 
& moulds, L. monocytogenes), physical (pH, firmness, rheology) and sensory 
analyses after 2, 16 and 30 days of storage at 6 °C. The decrease in number of starter 
cultures as well as in probiotic bacteria occurred probably because of cessation of 
metabolic activity due to the long period of storage. However, product remained safe 
for the consumption since there were no contaminants or pathogens detected. The pH, 
firmness and apparent viscosity showed similar patterns of increase or decrease and 
did not changed markedly even after 30 days of storage after MDD. Sensory data 
revealed that there was no perceptible difference in smell, taste and texture identified 
between standards and samples after MDD. Only spontaneously occurred syneresis 
could be perceived (observed in package). According to this, dairy products (with low 
pH) remain safe for the consumption even 30 days of storage after MDD at 6 °C.

1 Introduction 
 
 
High amounts of food are wasted only because date of minimum durability (MDD) is 
reached. According to CODEX standard for the labelling of prepackaged foods 
(CODEX STAN 1-1985) “Date of Minimum Durability” (“best before”) means the 
date which signifies the end of the period under any stated storage and handling 
conditions during which the product will remain fully marketable and will retain any 
specific qualities for which express claims have been made. However, the food may 
still be perfectly satisfactory beyond this date. In addition, MDD is often confused 
with “use by” date, which is intended for foods that are highly perishable (such as 
meat or fish). Due to this reason, the German government planes a change in the food 
labelling and thus comprehensive database concerning the development of the food 
quality is necessary. This change would help to prevent waste of food, which just in 
Europe accounts annually for 90 to 100 million tonnes, and this figure is most likely 
to grow (Briefing European Parliamentary Research Service, 2015) . 
 
The shelf life of dairy products is governed firstly by microbiological safety and then 
product sensory quality including rheological properties. The spoilage of dairy 
products (yogurt and cheese) mainly occurs due to the development of moulds and 
yeasts which are considered the Specific Spoilage Organisms (SSOs) for this type of 
products (Mataragas et al., 2011). In addition, quality may be affected by the 
separation of liquid from the gel that appears on top of the product (syneresis), 
discoloration, formation off-odours, etc. (Suriyarachchi & Fleet, 1981). 
 
Taking this above into consideration, this study aimed to evaluate the changes of 
quality parameters after MDD in order to determine the possibility of consuming 
dairy products after MDD. Therefore, three different dairy products, Sahnequark 
(SQ), Activia Probiotic Yogurt (APY) and Bauer Cherry Yogurt-mild (BCYM), were 
stored until the double MDD at 6 °C. During this period of time the evaluation of 
microbiological, physical and sensory quality was performed 2, 16 and 30 days after 
MDD.

2 Theoretical Background 
 
2.1 Fermented milk products 
 
Milk fermentations generally involve the metabolism of lactose to lactic acid by lactic 
acid bacteria (LAB), resulting in reduction of pH (Surono & Hosono, 2011) and 
generation of flavour and texture by converting milk protein due to their proteolytic 
activities (Widyastuti & Febrisiantosa, 2014).  
Fermented milks are classified into three different types according to the starter 
culture and the process conditions providing suitable environment for used starter 
culture: 
1) Products obtained through lactic fermentation, where strains of mesophilic 
(which grow the best at 25-30 ºC) (Surono & Hosono, 2011) or thermophilic 
(grow at higher temperatures 37-45 ºC) (Surono & Hosono, 2011) LAB are 
used, 
2) Products of alcohol-lactic fermentation, including yeast and LAB, 
3) Products, where in addition to type 1) and 2) fermentation, there is mold 
growth (Surono & Hosono, 2011).  
Products examined during this study were obtained through the lactic fermentation. 
 
2.1.1 Lactic acid bacteria 
 
2.1.1.1 Lactobacillus spp. 
 
Lactobacillus species that “generally regarded as safe” (GRAS status) are used as 
starter culture (Lactobacillus delbrueckii subsp. bulgaricus) and as probiotic culture 
(Lactobacillus acidophilus). Subsequently, they may produce exopolysaccharides and 
bacteriocins and they contribute to the flavour of dairy products (De Angelis & 
Gobbetti, 2011). 
In terms of taxonomy, lactobacilli are Gram-positive, catalase-negative, non-spore-
forming, rod-shaped bacteria that produce lactic acid at the end of fermentation 
process.  Based on carbohydrate pathways, genus is spited into three groups:  
==PAGE:8==
 
3 
• Obligate homofermentative lactobacilli that convert hexose almost entirely 
into lactic acid, while pentose and gluconate are not fermented, 
• Facultative heterofermentative lactobacilli that may ferment hexoses entirely 
into lactic acid or to lactic acid, acetic acid, ethanol, and formic acid, and 
pentoses to lactic acid and acetic acid, 
• Obligate heterofermentative lactobacilli that ferment hexoses to lactic acid, 
CO2, acetic acid, and/or ethanol, and pentoses to lactic acid and acetic acid (De 
Angelis & Gobbetti, 2011).  
  
2.1.1.2 Lactococcus lactis 
 
Lactococcus lactis belongs to mesophilic group of LAB which principal role in dairy 
fermentation involves the metabolism of lactose to lactic acid and the conversion of 
milk proteins into flavor compounds. Regarding with taxonomy, they are Gram-
positive, catalase negative, facultatively anaerobic and non-spore forming. Lc. lactis 
cultures commonly used in dairy fermentations are classified as subspecies cremoris, 
lactis and lactis biovar diacetylactis (Coffey, 2017). 
 
2.1.1.3 Streptococcus thermophilus 
 
Streptococcus thermophilus is a member of thermophilic group of LAB that is usually 
used in a combination with one or several Lactobacillus species. S. thermophilus is a 
Gram-positive spherical to ovoid non-motile coccus which occurs in pair and chains. 
It´s major metabolite is acetaldehyde, responsible for the yogurt flavor. S. 
thermophilus is a commonly used as a mix starter culture together with Lb. bulgaricus 
in the production of fermented milk products including yogurt. Lactic acid and 
acetaldehyde production is lower when a single strain, either of L. bulgaricus or S. 
thermophiles, is used compared with that in a mixed culture (Trachoo, 2002). These 
two microorganisms interact synergistically. In a first stage, L. bulgaricus liberates 
essential amino acids from casein by proteolytic activity, thus stimulating the growth 
of S. thermophilus. In this stage, L. bulgaricus grows slowly because is 
microaerophilic. At the end of the first stage high lactic acid concentration is 
produced and therefore, the growth of S. thermophilus is slowed down. The second 
==PAGE:9==
 
4 
stage begins when S. thermophilus produces enough formic acid which stimulates the 
growth of L. bulgaricus (Trachoo, 2002). 
2.1.2 Probiotic bacteria 
2.1.2.1 Bifidobacterium spp. 
 
All members of the genus Bifidobacterium are gram-positive, non-motile, non-
filamentous, non-sporulating, anaerobic (few can tolerate oxygen) and saccharoclastic 
microorganisms with optimum growth between 37-41 °C (Bivati & Mattarelli, 2012). 
Generally they are catalase negative with the exception of few.  
Bifidobacterium grows slowly in the milk and therefore it usually incorporated 
together with yogurt cultures L. baulgaricus and S. thermophilus for production of 
fermented dairy products (Bivati & Mattarelli, 2012). 
 
2.2 Yogurt 
According to the Code of Federal Regulations of FDA (FDA, 1996c), yogurt is 
defined as the “food produced by culturing one or more of the optional dairy 
ingredients (cream, milk, partially skimmed milk and skim milk) with a characterizing 
bacteria culture that contains the thermophilic lactic acid producing bacteria, 
Streptococcus thermophilus and Lactobacillus delbrueki spp. bulgaricus”. However, 
some other strains such as Lactobacillus acidophilus and Bifidobacterium spp. tend to 
be added as adjuncts (Trachoo, 2002). The majority of German yogurts are offered 
with a designation "mild" which indicates that product is yogurt-like in texture, but 
the culture may be different from classical yogurt. The commercial process of yogurt-
mild making uses mixed cultures consisting of S. thermophilus and Lb. acidophilus to 
replace the yogurt starter culture (Robinson, 2002). 
 Nowadays, yogurt is retailed in one of two physical states, classified as set 
(continuous and undisrupted gel in retail containers) (Tamime & Robinson, 1999) and 
stirred (the acid gel formation in large fermentation tanks is disturb by stirring) 
yogurt type (Ozcan, 2013). Additionally, a flavour of stirred type yogurt is usually 
modified by the addition of fruit/flavours and sugar (sucrose) (Robinson, 2002; 
Trachoo, 2002). During this project the examination was performed on Bauer Cherry

Yogurt-mild (BCYM) and Activia Probiotic Yogurt (APY) with Cereals, which are 
stirred yogurts, and therefore the production of this type will be discussed more into

2.1.2 Manufacturing of yogurt 
 
The manufacturing process of yogurt comprises a lactic fermentation that destabilises 
protein system and consequently brings milk to gelification. The yogurt is made from 
a milk of the highest microbiological quality, containing no antibiotics, 
bacteriophages or chemical compounds that may interfere with LAB during the 
fermentation (Robinson, 2002). The manufacturing process of a stirred type yogurt is

After reception, the milk undergoes a standardization process in order to ensure a 
specific fat content in the final product. Likewise, fortification of milk solids not fat 
(MSNF) is carried out by addition of milk powder to liquid milk, thus increasing the 
total solids to 12-13% (Hutkins, 2007). MSNF can also be elevated through 
evaporation of water from liquid milk under vacuum or water removal by 
ultrafiltration. Stabilizers and other permitted ingredients tend to be added to milk at 
this stage as well. The next step in yogurt manufacture is homogenization of the milk, 
which breaks down the fat into small globules (a uniform size of around 1µm and 
ensures full incorporation of any dry constituent. Following this, the milk is passed 
through a plate heat exchanger and heated to between 90 °C and 95 °C for 5 to 10 min 
(Trachoo, 2002). The high temperature treatment improves structure formation, 
through the denaturation of major whey proteins, α-lactalbumin and β-lactoglobulin, 
and kills non-sporing pathogenic microorganisms. After the heat treatment has been 
completed, the milk is then cooled via a heat-exchanger to desired incubation 
temperature required in relation of used starter culture, usually between 40 and 42 °C 
(Hutkins, 2007). The next route the milk follows depends on the type of the yogurt 
being made. For the production of stirred type, the milk is pumped into insulated 
tanks where the culture is added. The milk is then incubated in a way that the entire 
fermentation process occurs in tanks. Once the optimum pH value is reached (around 
4.2-4.3) (Robinson, 2002), the milk proteins will have coagulated to form a firm gel, 
and the product is initially cooled to around 15 °C. Subsequently, the fruit and flavour 
ingredients are blended to cooled yogurt prior to packaging and then the mixture is 
filled into containers.  
 
2.2. Quark  
Quark is a natural, unripened, fresh cheese produced by acid coagulation of milk by 
proper starter cultures (Lc. lactis subsp. lactis, Lc. lactis subsp. cremoris and/or 
Leuconostoc spp. and citrate-positive strains of Lc. lactis) together with a small 
amount of rennet. The rennet is added in order to obtain a better separation of protein 
coagulum from the whey. This cheese is popular in central Europe, especially in 
Germany where is produced on a large scale in a variety of fat contents, ranging from 
==PAGE:12==
 
7 
fat-free type to a variant with as much as 40 % fat in the dry matter (Lucey, 2011) . 
The examination was performed on Milsani Sahnequark (SQ) with 40% fat content. 
2.2.1 Manufacturing of Quark 
 
Traditional manufacture of quark usually involves pasteurization (72 °C) of milk just 
before fermentation. However, since this heating and acidification conditions do no 
cause the denaturation of whey proteins, various methods have been developed in 
order to increase the whey protein content of quark and reduce losses in the whey 
(Lucey, 2011; Schulz-Collins & Senge, 2004 ).   
In Westfalia Thermoprocess the milk is subjected to a high-temperature-long-time 
pasteurization at 85-95 °C for 3-15 min to denature and co-precipitate the whey 
proteins onto the caseins and further heat treatment of acidified milk at 56-60 °C 
(thermization) for up to 3 min followed by cooling to 40 °C for centrifugal separation. 
The main difference in the production of UF Quark is the process used for the whey 
separation, which is ultrafiltration (Schulz-Collins & Senge, 2004). 
There are two more processes, Centriwhey and Westfalia Lactal process where Quark 
whey is heated at 95 °C and concentrated (TS content of denaturated whey product is 
different in these two methods). Subsequently, the denaturated protein is then added 
back to milk for the next batch of Quark (Schulz-Collins & Senge, 2004). 
After heat-treatment, milk is cooled down to 20-28 °C and coagulated with 
mesophilic starter culture (0.3-4 %) and small amount of rennet (5–15 ml standard 
rennet per 1000 l milk) within 16 h. The rennet is often added approximately at pH 
value around 6.3 in order to obtain a firmer coagulum.  Once desired pH value is 
reached (4.6), the coagulum is stirred. The mix is then heated at 60°C for 6 min and 
separated using either UF membrane (operating at 40-45°C) or Quark (centrifugal) 
separator (operating at 32-44°C). Eventually, the quark is optimally blended with 
cream and packed (Lucey, 2011; Schulz-Collins & Senge, 2004 ).  The manufacturing 
process of quark is described in Figure 2.

2.3.  Determination of the quality of dairy products 
The shelf life of a product represents the storage time at which overall quality, or the 
intensity of a specific sensory attribute reaches a failure criterion, thus making it no 
longer saleable (Lawless & Heymann, 2010). Important factors affecting the product 
quality are texture, color, aroma, taste and flavor (O’Sullivan & O’Sullivan, 2017). In 
order to evaluate these properties, two main methods are used, instrumental and 
sensory analysis (Berk, 2009).

Two analytical sensory tests were combined in this study for the sensory evaluation of 
products after MDD, discrimination test and descriptive analysis, and therefore will 
be discussed more into details. 
2.3.1.1 Discrimination/difference tests 
 
Discrimination tests are the simplest and most sensitive form of product testing. The 
most commonly used ones include paired comparison, duo-trio, triangle test and 
ranking test. In triangle test (used during this project) panelists are asked to choose 
which two of three products are alike, or which product is the most different from the 
other two. However, this test cannot be used to determine how large is the difference 
between samples. Therefore, it is common practice to undertake triangle test prior to 
further analysis of dairy products (Drake & Delahunty, 2011). 
 
2.3.1.2 Descriptive sensory analysis 
 
Descriptive sensory analysis involves techniques that seek to differentiate between 
products based on all of their sensory characteristics and to determine a quantitative 
description of all identified attributes. Furthermore, descriptive sensory analysis 
involves trained sensory panel. The sensory characteristics of dairy products that can 
be quantitative determined are appearance, aroma, flavor, texture, taste and after-taste. 
Different methods of descriptive analysis include flavor profile method, texture 
profile method, Quantitative Descriptive Analysis (QDA), quantitative flavor 
profiling and free-choice profiling. In order to implement each of these methods, three 
==PAGE:15==
 
10
stages are required. Firstly, it is necessary to select a panel of assessors to perform a 
sensory evaluation; secondly, terminology, which describes sensory characteristics of 
the product, need to defined and thirdly, these sensory characteristics need to be 
quantified. In this study QDA was used in order to quantitatively describe differences 
within the samples, using a set of well-defined attributes (Drake & Delahunty, 2011).  
 
2.3.2 Instrumental methods 
2.3.2.1 Rheological characterization  
 
Food rheology is the study of deformation and the flow of the food materials and their 
behaviour in the transient area between solids and fluid (Tabilo-Munizaga & Barbosa-
Cánovas, 2005). Rheology concerns the response of the material to the applied forces 
and deformation. The deformation of a material may be done longitudinal or lateral 
and is named as shear strain or stress, respectively. The shear stress, τ, is the applied 
force per unit of area and it is expressed in Pascals (Pa). Strain represents a relative 
deformation of the material due to the applied force (Rao, 2007). Stress-stain tests 
may be categorized as a small-strain testing, referring to a deformation of a sample 
when only small percentage of deformation is required to break the sample. On the 
other hand, large-strain testing leads to the permanent structural change of the sample 
(Tabilo-Munizaga & Barbosa-Cánovas, 2005). During this study flow behaviour of 
fluids was evaluated using rotational measurements and thus will be described more 
into details.  
One of the essential parameters characterising the flow behaviour of fluids and semi-
fluids is viscosity, η, which represents internal resistance to flow when shear stress is 
applied. The flow behaviour thus classifies them as Newtonian or non-Newtonian 
fluids. However, only few food systems show Newtonian behaviour (Rao, 2007; 
Chronakis, 2016). 
Non-Newtonian fluids may be time-independent, when they show dependence only 
on the shear rate and not on the duration of rate of shear, or time-dependent, when 
they show a dependence on both. When the material exhibits an increase of viscosity 
with increasing shear rate it is called shear-thickening or dilatant. In contrary, if the 
viscosity decreases with shear rate is called shear-thinning or pseudoplastic and both 
==PAGE:16==
 
11
are the time-independent properties (Rao, 2007; Tabilo-Munizaga & Barbosa-
Cánovas, 2005). 
 
2.3.2.2 Texture analysis 
 
Texture represents one of the most essential components of food quality that is 
closely related to the structural and mechanical properties. It can be evaluated, 
primarily on solid and semi-solid products, by mechanical methods in units such 
as force. In food texture analysis, standard tests such as stretching, compression 
and cutting are used to determine hardness, cohesiveness, elasticity, 
spreadability, stickiness, crispiness and other properties of food. During these 
measurements it is possible to control speeds and forces of application. 
(Chronakis, 2016). There are two types of tests that might be performed, 
destructive tests, like penetration and non-destructive tests, which include 
compression and stretching. Penetration test was used in this study in order to 
determine the hardness of SQ. The choice of the appropriate probe is 
fundamental. Cylindrical probes apply compressive stress by the surface and 
shear stressing by the edges and the outer surface area of probe (Exponent).

3.1. Materials 
Sahnequark (Milsani, Germany), Activia Probiotic Yogurt (Danone GmbH, Germany) 
and Bauer Cherry Yogurt-mild (Privatmolkerei Bauer GmbH & Co. KG, Germany) 
were purchased from a local supermarket and stored until MDD at 6 °C after which

3.2. Methods 
All the experiments were performed 2, 16 and 30 days after MDD. Before the 
analyses, except for the firmness, the samples were gently stirred in order to ensure 
homogeneity.  
3.2.1 Microbiological analysis

subsequent serial dilutions were prepared in a way that 1mL was diluted in 9 mL of 
Ringer´s and mixed uniformly with a vortex mixer (MS2 Mini Shaker, IKA GmbH & 
Co. KG, Staufen, Germany) (see Figure 3).

3.2.2 Determination of pH 
The pH of the samples was measured by electrode immersion using a digital pH meter 
(MP220, Mettler Toledo, Columbus, OH) after calibrating with pH 4.0 and 7.0 
standard buffers.  
3.3.2 Determination of firmness of Sahnequark  
The firmness of the SQ was determined with a TA.XT2i (Stabile Micro Systems, 
Godalming, UK) using a penetration of 0.5" diameter cylindrical probe (P/0.5R, 
Stabile Micro Systems, Godalming, UK) (see Figure 4). The test was performed 
directly in 250 g plastic container. Firstly, the probe moved down at pre-test speed of 
1mm/s until a surface trigger force of 0,1 g and then at a test speed of 0,5 mm/s into

3.3.4 Sensory testing 
 
The analyses were carried out in a partitioned sensory evaluation boots at Max-
Rubner Institute (Kiel, Germany) where the samples were assessed by a panel of 
institute stuff members. The panellists were familiar with sensory evaluation 
techniques that were performed on one product (Sahnequark/Activia Probiotic 
Yogurt/Bauer Cherry Yogurt-mild). The samples were presented in transparent cups 
with assigned 3-digit codes and the order was randomized for each panellists (see 
Apendix).  
Triangle test was conducted first (see Appendix). Three coded samples were 
presented to each panellist who had to determine which sample differ from other two. 
Discrimination was performed on samples before MDD and after MDD. 
Subsequently, two coded samples (before and after MDD) were presented and 
described using Descriptive Analysis technique, also referred to as profiling. The test 
was administrated using a modified 4-point scale, ranging from 0 (not detectable) to 3 
(strong detectable) regarding the following attributes: appearance, smell, taste and 
texture (see Appendix).  
Data were conducted in a way that Triangle-test results were evaluated first according 
to ISO 4120:2004 standard. If there was a perceptible difference between the samples, 
before and after MDD of product, then descriptive test was assessed in order to 
indicate among which attributes the difference was found.   
 
3.3.5 Statistical analysis 
Experimental data were analysed with one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) and 
Turkey multiple comparison test (P < 0.05) (if not otherwise stated) in order to 
identify significant differences. However, during the evaluation of sensory test results 
(profiling results) normality test failed, and therefore Kruskal-Wallis one-way 
ANOVA on ranks was performed.  The software Sigma Plot version 13.0 (Systat 
Software Inc., San Jose, CA) was used for statistical analysis.

4 Results and Discussion 
 
The results are presented in three main sections including: microbiology, 
physical properties and sensory analysis. In each section the results are followed 
by a discussion. 
4.1 Microbiology 
The microbiota present in tested products was enumerated by cultivation on media. 
ANOVA revealed that storage time after MDD had a significant effect on plate counts 
in all tested samples (P < 0.05). The changes in the viable counts of LAB, during the 
storage at 6 °C over the period of 4 weeks after MDD, are presented in Figure 6. As 
shown, the counts of starter cultures, Lc. lactis and S. thermophilus, remained high 
during the tested period in SQ and BCYM (> 8 CFU/g). Thus, the presence of 
probiotic bacteria did not suppress S. thermophilus viability. In addition, even after 29 
days after MDD, yogurt-mild was consistent with the guidelines that fermented milk 
should contain at least 107 CFU/g of starter cultures (to the MDD) (FAO/WHO, 
2011). However, in APY count of S. thermophilus decreased markedly after 30 days 
of storage, reaching the value of < 107 CFU/g. This might be due to the secretion of 
inhibition metabolites (e.g., bacteriocins) produced by Bifidobacterium spp. or due to 
the use of different strains (Mani-López et al., 2014).  
Regarding with probiotic cunts, L. acidophilus and Bifudobacterium spp. were 6,90 ± 
0,02 and 7,96 ± 0,05 log CFU/g, respectively at the beginning of storage. After 29 
days of storage, the counts in BCYM and APY decreased by 0.7 to 2.2 log, 
respectively. The viability of probiotic bacteria depends on culture mixture, strain 
type and storage conditions (Mani-López et al., 2014). Some authors reported an 
antagonistic effect of L. bulgaricus against probiotic bacteria due to the production of 
hydrogen peroxide that might partially damage the probiotic cells (Dave and Shah, 
1998). Taking this into consideration, the viability of L. acidophilus might remained 
higher then Bifidobacterium spp. during the tested period due to the starter culture 
used which consists only of S. thermophiles without any Lb. bulgaricus. However, 
decline in the probiotic population may happen because of the cessation of metabolic 
activity due to the long storage period (Mani-López et al., 2014).

Analysis of profiling results for SQ showed that just scores found for syneresis were 
significantly different (P < 0.05) among tested samples. Syneresis is an undesirable

water phase (Lucey, 2002). Syneresis might happen spontaneous or may occur when 
the gel is mechanically disrupted by agitation, cutting or being subjected to a 
centrifugal force (Mani-López et al., 2014). Since the appearance was evaluated right 
after opening the package of quark, in plastic container, it is to be concluded that 
syneresis in this case occurred spontaneously.

identify the differences between standard and samples after MDD during the whole 
period of storage.

5 Conclusion 
 
 
This study was designed in order to determine weather dairy products have 
satisfactory quality after MDD. Therefore, evaluation of microbiological, physical 
and sensory properties was performed 2, 16 and 30 days after MDD. After data 
analysis, microbiological and physical properties (except the rheology of SQ and 
APY) changed significantly during the storage period (P < 0.05). Decrease in viability 
of starter cultures as well as probiotic cultures was observed. However, products 
remained safe for the consumption since there were no contaminants (enterococci, 
enterobacteria, pseudomonas) and no pathogen (L. monocytogenes) detected in the 
samples. This is due to the reason that pH remained low 30 days after MDD and thus 
made products extremely safe. Additionally, there were no detected acid tolerant 
yeasts or moulds that tend to grow on the surface of the product.  
Regarding with the firmness of SQ, it decreased during the storage period after MDD 
which may be due to the temperature deviation during the measurements. Apparent 
viscosity slightly changed just in a case of BCYM. 
Sensory analysis showed that in most cases panellists could not identify perceptible 
differences between standard and samples with expired MDD. After profile analysis, 
syneresis was detected in SQ and APY, but the aroma was not noticeably altered even 
30 days after MDD. 
All in all, the quality parameters did not changed markedly and what is most 
important, products remained safe for the consumption with respect to the 
microbiological point of view.
