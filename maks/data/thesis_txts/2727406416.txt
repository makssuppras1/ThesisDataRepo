Abstract
Tryptophan hydroxylase 2 (TPH2) is the protein responsible for the rate-determing step of the biosyn-
thesis of serotonin in the central nervous system. The protein catalyzes the reaction that turns the
amino acid tryptophan into 5-hydroxytryptophan, a precursor to serotonin. Serotonin is an important
neurotransmitter found in both the central nervous system, as well as in the peripheral tissue. Abnor-
mal serotonin production has been linked to a wide range of both psychological and physical illnesses,
such as depression, ADHD, ulcerative colitis diseases, and irritable bowel syndrome. In spite of its
biological importance, the structure of full-length TPH2 has not yet been solved. This is due to the dif-
ficulty involved in isolating the full-length protein. The reason for this is so far unclear, though some
evidence suggests that the flexibility of the N-terminal residues is responsible for the instability of the
protein in solution. The result is that very little is known about the structure and function of the 47
N-terminal residues of TPH2. In this project, the structure of the 47 N-terminal residues of tryptophan
hydroxylase 2 was studied, using molecular dynamics simulations, bioinformatics tools, and circu-
lar dichroism spectroscopy. Molecular dynamics simulations were performed of the 47 N-terminal
residues, both as an isolated peptide, as well as forming part of the full-length protein. The full-length
protein simulations were performed both with monomer structures as well as with a tetramer. Struc-
ture prediction programs, AlphaFold2, I-TASSER, and trRosetta, were used to generate models of the
peptide structure, which were loaded into AMBER in order to perform simulations. The models all
suggested significant helix content, ranging from 30% to 70% of the peptide. Helices remained present
throughout simulations. No significant changes in structure was observed upon conjugation of the
peptide to the TPH2 protein. It was observed that the N-terminal residues in some simulations of the
full-length protein, would approach the active site to within 15-20 ˚A, while in other simulations they
would stay at distances between 40-70 ˚A away. This occurred in both monomer and tetramer simu-
lations. The tetramer structure contained the regulatory domains existing in two pairs, on either side
of a plane containing the four catalytic domains. In agreement with results from previous studies,
the simulations showed one pair existing with a constant distance between them, while the other pair
moved independently of each other. Circular dichroism spectroscopy was performed on a peptide con-
sisting of the 47 N-terminal residues. Analysis of the resulting spectra revealed high contents of beta
sheets, but no helices. The beta sheet content increased with increasing concentration. It is suggested
that these may be due to peptide-peptide interactions that would not be present in the simulations of
isolated peptides.

Introduction
Tryptophane hydroxylase (TPH) is the enzyme responsible for catalyzing the first and rate-limiting
step in the production of the neurotransmitter 5-hydroxytryptamine (5HT), also called serotonin. [1–3]
Serotonin is mainly found in the central nervous system, the gastrointestinal tract, and in platelets
found in the blood. Abnormal production of serotonin has been linked to a variety of mental illnesses
such as depression, ADHD, OCD, schizophrenia, as well as physical illnesses like irritable bowel
syndrome and ulcerative colitis diseases. [3, 4] TPH catalyzes the synthesis of 5-hydroxytryptophan
from tryptophan using Fe2+ as cofactor and O2 and tetrahydrobiopterin (BH4) as cosubstrates. 5-
hydroxytryptophan is then converted to serotonin by aromatic amino acid decarboxylase. The synthe-
sis of serotonin is shown in Figure 1
Figure 1: Biological synthesis of serotonin from tryptophan
Two isoforms of TPH exists, TPH1 which is found in the peripheral tissue (skin, gut, pineal gland) and
TPH2 which is found in the central nervous system. These are encoded by two different genes, the
gene for TPH1 located on chromosome 11, and the gene for TPH2 located on chromosome 12. [5] The
two isoforms share a sequence identity of 71%, with the catalytic domains having a particularly high
sequence identity of 81%. In spite of this high similarity, differences have been observed in the kinetic
properties of the proteins. For example, only TPH1 displays substrate tryptophan inhibition. [1] The
difficulty in purifying full length TPH stems partly from its instability. In particular, the regulatory
domain has been found to cause instability, and it was found that truncation in the N-terminal domain
improves purification yield, as shown by Tidemand et al. [4] Truncation of the 47 N-terminal residues
of TPH2 were shown to result in an 11-fold increase in purification yield. It was suggested that the
flexibility of these residues was responsible for the instability of the protein. This was based on an
alignment with PAH, which showed that residues 20-47 of TPH align with a region of PAH which is
known to be mobile. [6] The 47 N-terminal residues are not present in TPH1, as can be seen from the
alignment in Figure 2.
Figure 2: Domain alignment of TPH1 and TPH2 [7] RD: Regulatory Domain CD: Catalytic Domain TD:
Tetramerization Domain. Percentages indicate conservation
Tenner et al. observed a 4 fold increase in VMax upon deletion of the 44 N-terminal residues of TPH2,
suggesting an inhibitory effect. [5] However, upon fusion of the 44 N-terminal peptide to TPH1 no
change in enzyme activity was observed. It was suggested that the secondary structure of the N-
terminus and its linkage to the rest of the protein could be crucial for the inhibitory function. The role of
the 47 N-terminal residues is poorly understood, largely due to the difficulty in characterization caused
by its instability. The purpose of this study was to use computational techniques, including structure
prediction programs and molecular dynamics simulations, as well as circular dichroism spectroscopy,
to gain an insight into the structural characteristics of the 47 N-terminal residues of TPH2. Knowledge
of the structure of this region of the protein, is a vital part of determining its function and could possibly

4.1
Aromatic Amino Acid Hydroxylases
Tryptophane hydroxylase is part of the group of enzymes called Aromatic Amino Acid Hydroxy-
lases (AAAHs), along with Phenylalanine Hydroxylase (PAH) and Tyrosine Hydroxylase (TH). [8] The
AAAHs all catalyze monooxygenation reactions of aromatic amino acid substrates by use of a tetrahy-
dropterin as a cosubstrate. PAH is expressed mainly in the liver and the kidneys, and catalyzes the
hydroxylation of excess phenylalanine, an important reaction in the catabolism of this amino acid. [9]
Deficiency in PAH results in elevated levels of phenylalanine in the blood, which can lead to diseases
like phenylketonuria. [10] TH is found in the central nervous system and catalyzes a key step in the
biosynthesis of a group of neurotransmitters called the catecholamines, which includes dopamine and
adrenaline. [11] All three enzymes are composed of three domains: a catalytic domain of around 300
residues, a C-terminal domain of around 50 residues, and an N terminal regulatory domain of varying
lengths. The active site of the enzymes is virtually identical. Each contains a Fe(II) ion bonded to two
histidines and a glutamate. [8] The active site of of TPH2 is shown in Figure 3
Figure 3: The active site residues of TPH2 with bonds to the Fe(II) ion
All three enzymes form homotetramers, and the C-terminal domain has been established as the tetramer-
ization domain. [12] For PAH and TH, crystal structures are available of nearly the full length proteins
including regulatory domain, catalytic domain, and tetramerization domain. Structures are also avail-
able for the homotetramers. For TPH, only the catalytic and tetramerization domain has been solved
crystallographically [8]. The regulatory domain has been solved by NMR. A low resolution structure
of the homotetramer was recently obtained by Vedel et al. using cryo-EM. [13] In this structure the
catalytic domains exist in a plane, with the regulatory domains placed in pairs above and below the
plane. The authors suggested that the regulatory domains could exist in a monomer-dimer equilib-
rium, where they either form motionally restricted dimers, or move independently of each other. All
structures of PAH, TH, and TPH lack the first few N-terminal residues. [8] The N-termini of all three
enzymes appear to be highly flexible making high-resolution structures difficult to obtain. In PAH,
this flexible region contains the first 24 residues, while the flexible region of TH contains the first 70
residues. The N-termini of both TH and PAH are known to be involved in autoregulation of the en-
zyme. [14] [15] In 1999, a crystal structure solved by Kobe et al. of PAH showed that the N-terminus
extended across the active site of the catalytic domain. This structure lacked the first 19 residues. [16]
PAH has since been found to exist in a resting state, in which the N-terminus blocks access to the
catalytic site, and an active state, in which the N-terminus points away from the catalytic site. [13]
The transition from the resting state to the active state is dependent on the formation of a dimer of
two regulatory domains of the homotetramer. The regulatory domains move independently from one

4.2
Intrinsically Disordered Proteins
In the early days of protein research a protein was seen as a rigid crystal-like biomolecule. [18] The
knowledge that the structure of a protein was key to its biological function gave rise to the so-called
’structure-function’ model, where the substrate and the active site of an enzyme possess unique rigid
structures that fit each other like a lock and key. [19] Later the ’induced fit’ model emerged after stud-
ies had shown that binding of a substrate was accompanied by subtle changes in the active site of the
protein. However, studies of the past decade and a half have revealed that this description is only suit-
able for some proteins, with other proteins being highly flexible or intrinsically disordered. [20] The
degree of conformational heterogeneity can differ drastically between proteins. While some folded
proteins only undergo small conformational fluctuations around an average structure, other proteins
have structures that cannot be accurately described by a single average structure. [21] Intrinsically Dis-
ordered Proteins (IDPs) or Intrinsically Disordered Regions (IDRs) are proteins, or regions of proteins,
that do not fold into a single well-defined structure. Instead, the structure of these proteins are best de-
scribed as a weighted ensemble of different conformations that the protein converts between. IDPs and
IDRs are highly abundant in nature. An estimated 25-30% of eukaryotic proteins are mostly disordered,
and > 70% of signaling proteins possess long disordered regions. [20] For many of these proteins, high
conformational flexibility is key to their biological activity. Through comparative analysis of the se-
quence composition of disordered and ordered proteins, Williams et al. showed that certain residues
appear more frequently in disordered proteins, while other residues appear less frequently. [22] The
amino acids Ala, Arg, Gly, Gln, Glu, Lys, Pro, and Ser were determined as disorder-promoting residues,
while Asn, Cys, Ile, Leu, Phe Val, Trp, and Tyr were determined as order-promoting residues. The 47
N-terminal residues of TPH2, contains 26 of these disorder promoting residues, and only 13 order pro-
moting residues. High net charge and low mean hydrophobicity have also been identified as signs of
disorder, as high charge would cause electrostatic repulsion, and low hydrophobicity would remove
one of the main driving forces of compaction. [23] While some proteins are entirely disordered, it is
more common for parts of a protein to be disordered. Disordered regions in the middle of the protein
chain often function as linkers and spacers, providing separation and allowing movement of differ-
ent structured protein domains in multidomain proteins. However, disorder is even more common at
protein termini than for residues in the middle of the protein chain. [24] Disordered tails have been
observed to have a wide range of different functions. For example some disordered tails function as
entropic bristles, sweeping around large areas and excluding large particles without excluding smaller
ones. This has been observed for some neurofilament proteins. [25] Other disordered tails such as the
C-terminus of DNA glycosylase NEIL1 function as stabilizers, increasing the stability of complexes
upon binding to their specific partners. [26] Others function as inhibitors or competitors, facilitating
auto-regulation of protein activity, such as in PAH and TH. [24]
4.3
CALVADOS
Predicting the properties of IDPs based on the amino acid sequence is a field of active study. [27]
CALVADOS is a recently developed model used to predict the liquid-liquid phase behaviour of IDPs,
and to study their conformational ensembles. [28] The model involves a coarse-grained force field
used to run molecular dynamics simulations. A coarse-grained force field is a force field in which
groups of atoms, e.g. an amino acid, are modelled together as a single particle, thus reducing the
complexity of the system and reducing the number of interactions that need to be computed. [29]
This makes simulations with coarse-grained force fields faster, enabling simulations on larger time-
scales. The alternative to coarse-grained force fields are full-atomic force fields, in which interactions
are calculated between individual atoms. Molecular dynamics simulations and force fields will be

of probability plots of various structural parameters, and a potential energy map. Simulations with
CALVADOS can be run locally through the source code found on GitHub, or online through a Colab
notebook. [30] [28] The CALVADOS model was developed based on a large set of experimental data

4.4
Molecular Dynamics Simulations
The use of computer simulations in the study of biology, chemistry, and related fields has increased
as the capabilities and processing powers of computers continue to grow. [32] [33] Simulations can of-
ten be a convenient way to obtain information which would be difficult, time consuming, costly, and
sometimes even impossible to do using conventional laboratory methods, and the range of applications
seems virtually limitless. In addition, recent advances in machine learning and AI make computational
methods even more attractive in solving problems in a wide range of fields. [34] Within chemistry two
types of computer simulations are now particularly commonly used: Monte Carlo simulations, and
Molecular Dynamics (MD) simulations. Monte Carlo simulations use random numbers and probabil-
ity distributions, such as the Boltzmann distribution, to stochastically determine certain attributes e.g.
minimum-energy structures or thermodynamic properties. Conversely, MD simulations use knowl-
edge about the physical laws that govern movement of atoms and molecules to create trajectories that
can be used to elucidate the dynamic nature of chemical systems. This is useful in the study of proteins,
which are dynamic in nature and whose function often rely on multiple conformations. While MD sim-
ulations can be used to sample conformations, it can also be used to obtain pair-distribution functions,
thermodynamic properties, and much more. Since the first publications involving MD simulations the
technique has been steadily becoming more widely used, as can be seen in Figure 4.
Figure 4: Use of MD simulations in papers published between 1980-2021 [32]
In this project, MD simulations were used to sample the conformations of peptides and proteins, in
order to study their dynamic structure. In particular, it was used to study the transient structures of
the 47 N-terminal residues of TPH2, both as an isolated peptide as well as in the full length protein.
The following sections explain some of the main principles involved in running MD simulations, as
well as some of the tools used to analyze the resulting trajectories. An introduction is also given to
some of the computer programs used in this project.
4.4.1
Classical Mechanics
MD simulations are based on classical mechanics. Classical mechanics is the area of physics concerned
with describing the movement of macroscopic objects. While molecules are not exactly macroscopic,
their movements can to a certain extend be described by the laws of classical mechanics [35] [36]. In
classical mechanics, the movement of particles is described by the equations of motion. These equa-
tions can be formulated in three different ways, all of which describe the exact same movements. The
earliest formulation was given by Isaac Newton in 1687:

The equations of motion describe the evolution of the system in time. A system of N particles will be
defined by 3N spatial coordinates, and 3N momenta, giving a total of 6N coordinates and 6N equations
of motion. A space spanned by 6N dimensions would then be able to describe all possible states of the
system. This is called phase space, and the 6N dimensions could also be called the degrees of freedom
of the system. [35] [36] The evolution of the system in time can then be visualized as a point tracing a
line in phase space. If the system is one of constant energy, the trajectory will lie on a constant energy
surface, i.e. a surface made up of points in phase space all of which correspond to states with the
same energy. For some classical systems, it is possible to define an initial state of the system and then
integrate the equations of motion to give functions describing the trajectory in phase space. However,
chemical systems usually involve very large numbers of atoms and molecules, and it is not possible to
experimentally determine the positions and momenta of all atoms in such a system at any given time.

4.4.2
Statistical Mechanics and Ensembles
Statistical mechanics is the branch of physics that attempts to predict the macroscopic properties of
a system by considering the molecules and atoms that form it. [35] [36] An MD simulation describes
the evolution of the microscopic mechanical state of a system. It is statistical mechanics that enable
us to calculate macroscopic thermodynamic variables such as temperature and pressure during MD
simulations. The macroscopic state of a system is defined by a set of three thermodynamic variables,
e.g. the number of particles N, the volume of the system V, and the temperature T. However, for
any one thermodynamic state, there exists a multitude of corresponding microscopic quantum states.
The collection of microscopic states that correspond to the same macroscopic system is termed the
ensemble. A basic postulate of statistical mechanics is that the thermodynamic property of a system is
equal to the average of that property over all of the microscopic states:

4.4.5
Numerical Integration Algorithms
MD simulations use numerical integration algorithms to evolve the system in time. [35] Several such
algorithms exist. The general idea is to determine the current state of the system by calculating the
positions, velocities, and forces between particles, and then use an integration scheme to calculate
the positions and velocities in the future after some discrete time step, δt. This is then repeated for
some predetermined number of integration steps. The integration time step has to be chosen carefully.
If the time step is too small the simulation will not be able to sample any significant volume of the
phase space, without needing an unreasonable number of integration steps. However, if the time
step is too large the particles in the system will be propagated too far without consideration of the
change in the forces acting on them. This means that atoms and molecules can collide, and the forces
calculated in the next integration step will be very strong. A large time step will thus lead to an unstable
system. MD simulations are typically carried out with time steps of a few femtoseconds (10−15s).
Millions of integration steps are thus needed in order to simulate a few nanoseconds of a system.
Since the force calculation and integration of the system is the most computationally demanding part
of the simulation, doing this in a computationally efficient way becomes highly important. AMBER
supports a variety of integration algorithms, but the default is the velocity-Verlet algorithm. [39] The

4.4.6
Periodic Boundary Conditions
Compared to laboratory chemical systems, MD simulations are rather limited in terms of the number
of atoms involved. [35] The more atoms there are in a simulation, the more processing power is needed
per integration step. While the number of atoms and molecules in laboratory systems are on the scale
of Avogadros number (6.02 · 1023), MD simulations typically only simulate a few thousand atoms, for
example, a single protein with the solvent immediately around it. This poses a problem. Simulating
chemical systems this way would introduce so called finite size effects. In such a small system, a large
fraction of the simulated atoms would be surface atoms, and their movement would be directed by the
fact that they are not entirely surrounded by other atoms. However, in MD simulations, it is desirable
to be able to simulate the bulk conditions of atoms in solution. In order to do this, periodic boundary
conditions have to be introduced. The idea of periodic boundary conditions is to surround the simula-
tion box with replicas of itself. This would simulate bulk conditions, as the simulated atoms would feel
the forces from the replicas, but would not require simulating any more atoms. This is shown in Figure
6 (a). When the movement of an atom leads it out of the simulation box, an identical replica of the
simulated atom will simply enter the simulation box from the opposite side. Thus the total number of
atoms and replica atoms within the simulation box will remain constant. The replica atoms in periodic
boundary conditions are also called images. Periodic boundary conditions are often used along with
the minimum image convention, illustrated in Figure 6 (b).

4.4.7
The AMBER suite
Several programs exist that enable users to run MD simulations. The programs generally consist of
a set of force fields, solvent models, simulation protocols, an antechamper, an MD engine, and tools
for analyzing the simulation. [41] The program used in this project was the AMBER suite, a program
originally developed in 1975 at the University of California and today being developed through a col-
laborative effort by a long list of universities and companies. [46] The general energy expression for
the AMBER force fields was discussed in section 4.4.4. The specific force field used in the simula-
tions in this project was the ff19SB force field, a force field designed for protein simulations. AMBER
strongly suggests running the ff19SB force field with the OPC solvation model. A solvation model is
used to simulate the solvent surrounding the protein. This can be done explicitly, in which case the
solvent molecules are present in the simulation, or implicitly, in which case it can be represented as a
continuous medium or the effects can simply be calculated based on the solvent accessible area of the
protein. [41] The OPC model is an explicit solvation model used to simulate systems in water. It is a 4-
point, 3 charge water model, meaning the water molecules have four interaction points and three point
charges. The four interaction points are on the 2 H atoms, which are positively charged, a dummy atom
which is negatively charged, and the O atom which is the Van der Waals center. [39] The force field and
solvation model is loaded in the AMBER antechamper, LEaP. The antechamper is the program used
to generate the input files used in the MD simulation. AMBER supports several file formats, but to
run an MD simulation input files need to specify the parameters, the topology, and the coordinates.
The parameter file contains the parameters used in the force field. For the force field in equation (24),
the parameters would be kb, r0, kθ, θ0, VN, γ, Aij, and Bij. The topology file contains information about
which atoms are bonded to which. The parameter and topology information can also be stored in a
single file. The coordinate file contains the coordinates of the atoms at the start of the simulation. The
antechamber also allows the user to add ions, both for charge neutralization or to simulate a specific
chemical environment, as well as add and remove bonds, e.g. for the formation of sulfide bridges.
The input files are loaded into the MD engine to run the simulation. The primary engine in AMBER
is called Particle Mesh Ewald Molecular Dynamics (PMEMD). Minimization and heating are the first
steps required in an MD simulation. Minimization uses the AMBER force field and optimization algo-
rithms to relax the structure, ensuring that no steric clashes or inappropriate geometry exists within the
molecule. Heating ensures that the MD simulation starts at the desired temperature. Once minimiza-
tion and heating have been performed, the production run can start. The production is by far the most
time consuming part of the process. For the systems simulated in this project, the minimization and
heating usually took a few minutes, while the production took anywhere between 24 hours to more
than a week. In order to perform minimization, heating, and production in AMBER, it is necessary to
write input files that specify the conditions of the minimization, heating, and production. These input

CPPTRAJ predicts secondary structure based on the DSSP method, a method which predicts secondary
structures based on the positions of backbone amides and carbonyls. [51] Seven types of secondary
structure are recognized by CPPTRAJ: turns, bends, parallel beta sheets, anti-parallel beta sheets, al-
pha helices, 3-10 helices, and pi helices. Turns and bends are short segments of amino acids which
change the direction of the protein chain. Bends are defined as regions of high curvature. In the DSSP
method, the curvature is quantified as the angle between the direction of the backbone atoms. Turns
are defined as a single hydrogen bond between the carbonyl of residue i, and the amide of residue
i + n, with n equal to 3, 4, or 5. If two consecutive residues are assigned as turns with the same value of
n, the residues between the first and last residues involved will be assigned to a helix. For example, if
the carbonyls of residues i-1 and i are hydrogen bonded to the amides of residues i+3 and i+4, residues
i, i+1, i+2, and i+3 are assigned to a helix. In this case, n would be equal to 4. If n = 4, the helix is an
alpha helix, if n = 3 the helix is a 3-10 helix, and if n = 5 the helix is a pi helix. Beta sheets consist of two
or more beta strands placed next to each other lengthwise, and interacting through hydrogen bonding.
In the DSSP method, beta sheets are defined by the presence of two hydrogen bonds between sets of
three residues. Beta sheets are either parallel or anti-parallel, depending on the direction of the strands.
CPPTRAJ can be used to measure the distance between atoms, or groups of atoms in a trajectory. If
groups of atoms are used, the distance will either be measured from the geometric center or the center
of mass. Tracking the distance between certain atoms or domains during a simulation can be very
useful. It can be used, for example, in figuring out if certain atoms approach an active site. It is also
possible to calculate the amount of times during a simulation one group of atoms comes within a
set distance of another group of atoms. This can be useful when it is not known beforehand, which
residues might approach some region of a protein.
4.4.9
Structure Prediction Programs
Structure prediction programs are programs that take as input an amino acid sequence, and output
a secondary and/or tertiary structure often accompanied by a confidence score. These programs uti-
lize machine-learning and deep-learning methods, in various ways, to extract structural information
from amino acid sequence alone. Several such programs are available online, either as source code up-
loaded on platforms like Github, or as web servers on which a sequence can be uploaded directly. The
accuracy of the structures predicted by these programs vary. [52] The Critical Assessment of protein
Structure Prediction (CASP) experiments, organized by the Protein Structure Prediction Center, eval-
uates the current generation of these programs. [53] The experiments are carried out biennially using
recently solved protein structures. The CASP assessment serves as the gold standard for the assess-
ment of protein structure prediction programs. [54]

In 2020, Google DeepMind entered CASP14 with AlphaFold2, a deep-learning based structure pre-
diction program. The program outperformed all other methods in the competition, generating much
more accurate structures and was described as a paradigm-shift in the problem of protein structure pre-
diction. [55] The structures predicted by AlphaFold2 had a median backbone accuracy of 0.95 ˚A RMSD
(95% confidence interval 0.85-1.16 ˚A), as compared to the second best performing method (DeepAccNet
under the name ’BAKER’ [56]) which had a median backbone accuracy of 2.8 ˚A RMSD (95% confidence
interval 2.7-4.0 ˚A). [54] The all-atom accuracy of AlphaFold2 was 1.5 ˚A RMSD (95% confidence inter-
val 1.2-1.6 ˚A), while the second best method achieved 3.5 ˚A RMSD (95% confidence interval 3.1-4.2 ˚A).
The code has since been released on Github, and an article was released in Nature, describing the
method. [57] The program essentially consists of three parts. In the first part, the program creates an
Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA) and a pair representation. A multiple sequence allignment is
a collection of sequences similar to the input sequence. This is used to analyze correlations between
pairs of residues, the idea being that if a mutation of one residue is often accompanied by a mutation in
another residue, then this could imply that the residues are in contact with each other in the structure.
The pair representation is an initial representation of the structure, based on which residues are likely
to be in contact. This is based on a database search of proteins that may have a similar structure. In the
second part, the MSA and pair representation are analyzed in the EvoFormer module. The EvoFormer
module is a neural network which refines the pair representation based on information extracted from
the MSA. This process also involves a refinement of the MSA based on the pair representation. This is
done in several iterations, also called blocks. The refined MSA and pair representation are then used
as input for another neural network called the structure module. This produces a 3D structure model,
which is then passed back into the EvoFormer again. This continues for several iterations, until a final
model is produced. [54] Figure 8 shows the overall process.
Figure 8: Diagram of the AlphaFold2 pipeline [57]
The structures constructed by AlphaFold2 are accompanied by a confidence score called the predicted
Local Distance Difference Test (pLDDT). This metric is based on the Cα based local distance difference
test (lDDT-Cα). [58] The lDDT score measures the agreement between placement of atoms in a protein
model and the same atoms in an experimentally determined reference structure. It involves measuring
the distances between all atoms within a certain threshold distance of each other and not belonging to
the same residue. This creates one set of distances for the reference structure and one for the model.
The fraction of distances in the reference structure which are preserved in the model can then be cal-
culated. The lDDT score is the average of this fraction for a number of different threshold values. The
lDDT value can be calculated for all atoms in the structure or just for Cα atoms. The AlphaFold website
provides a suggestion of how to interpret the pLDDT scores of a generated structure. [59] The pLDDT
scores range from 0 to 100. A pLDDT score of >90 represents a highly accurately modelled residue,
a score between 90 and 70 represents a well-modelled residue, a score between 70 and 50 represents
a low confidence residue, and a score below 50 indicates a disordered residue. A low pLDDT score
being an indicator of disorder has been corroborated by Wilson et. al. [55] who found good agree-
ment between pLDDT scores assigned by AlphaFold2, and known disordered regions in proteins. A

key limitation for AlphaFold2, and many programs like it, is that the predictions are rooted in already
solved structures and similar sequences. [60] For AlphaFold2 to perform optimally an MSA of at least
30 sequences are required. Breugel et al. also showed that AlphaFold2 struggles to predict coiled-coil
structures, due to an under-representation of solved structures available in the PDB. [61] Another lim-
itation for AlphaFold2 is that the predicted structures are static. Conformational changes are key to
understanding protein function, and static models like the ones produced by AlphaFold2 provide little
to no information on these. [62] This is particularly relevant in the study of IDPs. IDPs are estimated
to encompass over 30% of sequences 30 amino acids or longer, based on sequences sampled from the
human proteome. [63] These cannot be accurately represented by a static model. The full version of
AlphaFold2 available on Github was not used in this project. Instead an online web server version of
the program was used. [64] ColabFold is a Jupyter Notebook available on Google Colab, which enables
users to run AlphaFold2 simulations online. The main difference between AlphaFold2 and ColabFold,
is that the homology search and MSA generation of AlphaFold2 is replaced by MMseqs2. MMseqs2
is a program for generating MSAs that runs faster than the one used in AlphaFold2. The structures
predicted by ColabFold are comparable in accuracy to those predicted by AlphaFold2. [65]
Another structure prediction program used in this project is I-TASSER [66] [67]. I-TASSER (Iterative
Threading ASSembly Refinement) was developed at the University of Michigan by Zhang et. al. in
2008, and has since become a widely used tool having predicted more than 700 000 structures submit-
ted from 160 countries. I-TASSER was ranked the best protein prediction server in CASP14 and the
fifth best method overall. Structure prediction with I-TASSER takes place in four steps, as shown in
Figure 9. The first step is the template threading, in which template proteins are found in the PDB
database. This is done by utilizing a number of different threading programs through a threading
server called LOMETS. The second step is the structural fragment assembly which takes the template
structures from the first step, and extracts structural fragments of aligned regions. Regions which are
not present in templates are built with ab initio modelling and the fragments are assembled to give a
large number of full length models. The assembly is performed by a Monte Carlo simulation. The pro-
gram then performs clustering on these models, and select a centroid from each cluster to continue the
modelling. Since the structure assembly is the most time consuming part of the process, the structures
are simplified by representing each amino acid as a Cα atom and a center of mass of the side chain.
The centroids are further refined by structure re-assembly, and a full atom model is generated by a
protein structure refinement algorithm called FG-MD [68]. Finally I-TASSER will attempt to predict
the function of the query protein using the algorithms COACH and COFACTOR, which compare the
structure against function template structures from the BioLiP database. [69]

The models generated by I-TASSER are accompanied by a confidence score, called the c-score. [66].
The c-scores range from -5 for the lowest level of confidence, to 2 for the highest. It is calculated based
on the quality of the threading alignment and the convergence of the Monte Carlo simulations. The
c-score estimates the global accuracy, while the local accuracy is reported as an l-score. The l-score
is an estimation of the distance deviation between residues in the model and in the native structure.
I-TASSER estimates this using a machine learning algorithm called ResQ. [70] The estimated distance
deviation has been found to have an average error of 2.21 ˚A.
The third structure prediction program used in this project was transform-restrained Rosetta. trRosetta
is based on a neural network similar to AlphaFold2 and the Rosetta prediction algorithm [71]. Like Al-
phaFold2, trRosetta starts by creating an MSA for the query sequence and uses this as the input for
a neural network. However, while AlphaFold2 extracts information on the contacts and inter-residue
distance, trRosetta attempts to extract information on the distances and contacts as well as the rela-
tive orientations of the residues. The relative orientations are defined by a set of angles, ϕ, θ, and ω.
The neural network will take the MSA of the query sequence as input and output a set of probabil-
ity distributions for the distance and orientation parameters, which are then used to create distance
and orientation dependent potentials. These potentials are then used for constrained minimization in
Rosetta. Rosetta is a structure prediction algorithm which allows incorporation of experimental re-
straints [72]. The initial models are made with Rosettas coarse-grained energy function, and the lowest
energy models are progressed to full-atom relaxation in Rosetta. The lowest energy model is then
chosen as the final model. The trRosetta pipeline is shown in Figure 10.

Proteus2 combines several de novo secondary structure prediction programs with a homology pre-
diction to predict the secondary structure of a sequence [73] [74]. Proteus2 first compares the query
sequence to a database of protein secondary structures using BLAST and creates an MSA. For each
residue in the query sequence, the program determines the sequence in the MSA with the highest sim-
ilarity and assigns its secondary structure to the query sequence residue. In this way, the secondary
structure will be determined based on homology. The program will then use several de novo sec-
ondary structure prediction programs to determine the secondary structure again, in what the authors
refer to as a ’jury of experts’ system. This involves using three neural network based programs (JNET,
PSIPRED, and TRANSSEC) to predict the secondary structure, and then using a fourth neural net-
work program (JOE) to combine these three predictions into one final predicted structure. Finally the
program takes the structure predicted by the homology modelling and the one predicted by de novo
prediction to combine these into the final structure. If no homologous sequences could be found, the
program defaults to the de novo prediction. If homology modelling is possible, the program will also
attempt to produce an image of the approximate tertiary structure. Proteus2 also generates a global
confidence score, as well as residue specific confidence scores. The residue specific confidence scores
are based on confidence scores from the three de novo prediction programs (JNET, PSIPRED, and
TRANSSEC), which are averaged and normalized to give integer values between 0 and 9. The global
confidence score is obtained by summing over the residue specific confidence scores, and dividing
by the number of residues. A model with a global confidence score of >80% is considered a reliable
model.

4.5.1
Circular Dichroism Spectroscopy
Circular Dichroism spectroscopy (CD) is a convenient method for determining the secondary structure
content of proteins and peptides. [75] It can also be used to study protein folding, and interactions.
The method relies on circularly polarized light, and the differences in absorption between different
secondary structure elements. Electromagnetic waves consist of magnetic and electric fields which
oscillate perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation. The directionality of the
oscillation defines the polarization of the light. Natural light is unpolarized, meaning the electric and
magnetic fields oscillate in many directions. In linearly polarized light the electric field oscillates only
in one plane. In circularly polarized light the electric field oscillates in a plane which rotates around the
axis of propagation. This rotation can occur in two directions, leading to either right-hand polarized
light or left-hand polarized light, as illustrated in Figure 12.
Figure 12: Schematic representation of right-hand circularly polarized light (RCPL) [76]
An asymmetric molecule will absorb left-hand and right-hand polarized light to different extents. [77]
The difference in absorption is given by.
∆A = AL −AR
(38)
Introducing Lambert-Beers law, the difference can be expressed in terms of the molar extinction coeffi-
cients.
∆A = (ϵL −ϵR) · C · l = ∆ϵ · C · l
(39)
Where C is the concentration of the sample, l is the path length, and ϵL and ϵR are the molar extinction
coefficients of left-hand and right-hand polarized light, respectively. ∆ϵ is called the molar circular
dichroism of the sample at a particular wavelength. Circular dichroism is also often reported in terms
of the molar degree of ellipticity. The molar circular dichroism is related to the molar degree of ellip-
ticity by equation (40).
[θ] = 3298.2 · ∆ϵ
(40)
The units of the molar degree of ellipticity is [deg · cm2 · dmol−1]. It is also sometimes reported in
terms of millidegrees (mdeg). A CD experiment will yield a plot of the circular dichroism, or degree of
ellipticity, against wavelength. Different secondary structures will result in different patterns as shown
in Figure 13.

The known patterns of the pure secondary structures make it possible to estimate the secondary struc-
ture content of a protein or peptide, based on a CD spectrum. In CD analysis, it is assumed that the
spectrum can be represented by a linear composition of the pure secondary structure, plus a noise term
which includes signals from aromatic chromophores. [75]
θλ = ∑εiSiλ + noise
(41)
In this equation, θλ is the circular dichroism of the protein at a specific wavelength, λ, εi is the fraction
of the protein with secondary structure i, and Siλ is the circular dichroism of secondary structure i at
wavelength λ. During analysis of a CD spectrum, the spectra of proteins or peptides with known struc-
tures are fitted to the unknown spectrum. One approach to this is to use ’pure’ secondary structures,
like the spectra shown in Figure 13, derived from short peptides with a single secondary structure. A
drawback with this method is that these spectra are not always good models for the secondary struc-
tures found in proteins. For example, the spectra of α-helices can be dependent on the length of the
helix. Another approach is to use a database of proteins with known structures and CD spectra as
the reference. The fitting can be done with various algorithms, such as linear regression and neural
networks. [78]

Methods and Experimentals
5.1
Simulations
AlphaFold2 structures were generated with the Google Colab version of AlphaFold2, ColabFold v.1.5.2.
[64] The peptide and protein sequences were uploaded, and any other settings were left to their de-
fault values. I-TASSER and trRosetta structures were generated on the Zhang lab [79] and Yang lab [80]
websites, respectively.
Structures were visualized in PyMOL version 2.0.6. Alterations to structures were also performed in
PyMOL, and are mentioned in the text.
LEaP was used to prepare structures for simulations. Structures were solvated using the OPC water
model, and the simulation box was generated so that a distance of 10 ˚A was ensured between the edge
of the box and the peptide or protein. Na+ or Cl−ions were added to balance the charge of the peptide
or protein, and additional ions were added to set the NaCl concentration to 150 mM. Since the size of
the simulation box changed from simulation to simulation, the number of ions required changed as
well. The number of ions required for a concentration of 150 mM was calculated based on the equa-
tions shown in Appendix F. The number of ions used in each simulation is also shown in Appendix F.
Simulations were performed in AMBER 19, using the ff19SB force field. Minimization was performed
using the steepest descent algorithm for the first 1000 steps, followed by the conjugate gradient algo-
rithm for the remaining steps. Systems were minimized with 2000, 2500, or 5000 minimization steps
depending on the simulation. The nonbonded cut-off distance was set to 8.0 ˚A, and electrostatic in-
teractions were summed using the Particle Mesh Ewald method. [35] Following minimization, the
systems were heated to 300 K. The system was heated to 300 K in 9000 steps with a time step of 0.002
ps, and was then kept at 300 K for an additional 1000 steps. The temperature was kept constant by
the Langevin thermostat [37], and the collision frequency was set to 2.0 ps−1. SHAKE was enabled
to constrain bonds involving hydrogen, and periodic boundary conditions were set for constant vol-
ume. After heating the production run was initiated. A time step of 0.002 ps was also used for the
production. The pressure was kept constant at 1.0 bar using the Berendsen barostat [40] and peri-
odic boundary conditions were set for constant pressure. For the minimization and heating, data was
printed to output files every 100 cycle, and for production this was done every 5000 cycle.
5.2
Circular Dichroism
Circular dichroism spectroscopy was performed using a Jasco J-1500 CD spectrophotometer. The sam-
ple consisted of peptide dissolved in a 20 mM Tris/HCl, 50 mM NaCl, pH 7.5 buffer. The buffer was
prepared by dissolving 2.38244 g Tris and 2.93063 g NaCl in 900 mL MilliQ water. The solution was
stirred until all salt was dissolved, the pH was adjusted with HCl, and the volume was adjusted to
1 L with MilliQ water. The buffer was then filtered with a vacuum filter, in order to remove air bub-
bles which can interfere with the polarized light. A stock solution of 1 mg/mL peptide in buffer was
prepared, and from this three samples were made with different concentrations. The concentrations
were measured using a NanoDrop microvolume spectrophotometer. The cuvette used for the CD spec-
troscopy measurements had a pathlength of 1 mm.

CALVADOS
The peptide sequence was loaded into an online Colab notebook capable of running CALVADOS sim-
ulations. Temperature and ionic strength was set to match the settings of the AMBER simulations
in the previous sections (300 K, 150 mM). The simulation time was set automatically to 70.7 ns. The
output of the CALVADOS simulations was a series of probability distributions of some structural pa-
rameters. Of these, two were parameters that CPPTRAJ is capable of computing, end-to-end distance,
Ree, and radius of gyration, Rg. Figure 30 shows a comparison of the probability distributions from the
coarse-grained CALVADOS simulation and the full-atomic AMBER simulations.

There are clear differences between the probability distributions of the two simulations. The CAL-

The average pLDDT of the generated AlphaFold2 structure is 82.33. This means that the overall struc-

PAH. The low confidence with which the N-terminal residues have been modelled, however, makes it
difficult to conclude anything based on the AlphaFold2 structure. Other regions of the proteins are also
modelled with low confidence, and expected to be disordered. These are residues 48-66, which are part
of the regulatory domain, and residues 142-153, which connect the regulatory domain to the catalytic
domain. Since NMR and crystal structures exist of the regulatory and catalytic domains, it was de-

catalytic domain of the AlphaFold2 model was replaced with the pdb structure 4V06 [83], and part of

Conclusion
Simulations were made of the 47 N-terminal residues of tryptophan hydroxylase 2 as an isolated pep-
tide. The simulations were started from 3 different structures, obtained from online protein structure
prediction software. All three programs predicted the peptide to have some helix secondary structure,
with AlphaFold2 and I-TASSER predicting the helix content to be just above 30% and trRosetta pre-
dicting the helix content to be 70%. Though the simulations showed some changes in the secondary
structure of the peptide over time, these helices appeared largely stable during simulations in the AM-
BER ff19SB forcefield. The average helix content of the simulations were between 19-33%, 29-37%, and
48-55% for the simulations started from the AlphaFold2, I-TASSER, and trRosetta model, respectively.
Though all three predicted models showed different radii of gyration, the mean radius of gyration were
between 10-15 ˚A for all simulations. The end-to-end distances of the predicted models were also quite
different, ranging from 30-50 ˚A. The mean end-to-end distance for all simulations were between 20-30
˚A. The secondary structure of the simulations were compared to results from the secondary structure
prediction program Proteus2. Proteus2 predicts two regions of helix structure, and one region with a
beta strand. The two regions of helix structure line up reasonably well with the results of the AMBER
simulations, though the extend of the helices differ, particularly for the helix closest to the N-terminus.
The region which Proteus2 predicts to be a beta strand lines up with a region which is predicted to be
a helix in the simulations started from the AlphaFold2 and trRosetta models. A simulation of the same
peptide was performed using CALVADOS. The results of the simulation was compared to the AMBER
simulations with respect to the probability distribution of the end-to-end distance and radius of gyra-
tion. The comparison showed that the probability distributions derived from the AMBER simulations
are considerably narrower and peak at lower values, than the ones produced by the CALVADOS sim-
ulation. The observed differences could result from the fact that AMBER uses a full-atomic model with
explicit solvation, while CALVADOS uses a coarse-grained model with implicit solvation. However,
additional experiments would be required to verify the results found in this study. Simulations were
made of full-length TPH2 monomers, using representative structures of the 47 N-terminal residues
from the three peptide simulations. The simulations showed no particular change in the secondary
structure of the N-terminal residues compared to the simulations of the isolated peptides. Likewise,
the radii of gyration and end-to-end distances were very similar to the ones observed in the peptide
simulations. The distances between the active site and the 47 N-terminal residues showed that in all
three simulations the N-terminus approached the active site at some point in the simulation. However,
none of the three simulations showed the N-terminus approaching as closely as what was predicted in

the AlphaFold2 model of full-length TPH2. Simulations were made of tetramers of TPH2. One simu-

the ∆47 simulations. Circular dichroism spectroscopy was performed on a peptide of the 47 N-terminal
residues. The spectra appeared to correspond with a largely unstructured random coil, though with
some indication that some amount of alpha helix or beta strand might be present. Predicted CD spectra
based on the representative structures of the N47P simulations, showed clear peaks at 210 and 220 nm,
characteristic of helices. These were not observed in the experimental spectra, indicating that these
structures were not present in the samples. Analysis using BeStSel fitting indicated high beta sheet
content, which increased with increasing concentration. It was suggested that these might be the result
of peptide-peptide interactions.
8
Future work
Future work could include continuation of some simulations. For example, some of the simulations
of the isolated peptide showed a decrease in helix content. It might be interesting to see if the helices
continued to unfold if the simulations were continued. In the simulation of the ∆47 tetramer, the fluctu-
ation in the distance between regulatory domain B and C appeared to be diminishing slightly, towards
the end of the simulation. It would also be interesting to see if this were to continue, or if the regulatory
domains would continue to move independently. The end-to-active site distance of the 47 N-terminal
residues of subunit B in the full-length tetramer simulation also seemed to be decreasing during the
simulation. It could also be investigated if this continued with further simulation. The simulations
of N47P were performed as isolated peptides. However, when performing CD spectroscopy peptides
in the sample can interact with one another, potentially influencing the structure. This could lead to
differences between the simulations of isolated peptides and experimental results. Simulations could
be performed of multiple peptides, in order to investigate if peptide-peptide interactions occur, and if
they affect the structure. Future work might also include further CD experiments. It was observed that
the beta sheet content increased with increasing concentration. Further CD experiments could be made
to investigate if decreasing peptide concentrations lead to decreasing beta sheet contents. Further CD
experiments could be performed with lower concentrations, using a cuvette with a longer pathlength,
in order to investigate if these beta sheets remain at lower concentrations of peptide.
