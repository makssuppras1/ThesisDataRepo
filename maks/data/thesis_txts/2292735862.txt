Abstract
Transmission lines have traditionally been constructed using steel truss towers with conductor
lines suspended in insulator strings. A new proposal for a pylon design has been made by
Bystrup Architects. In this new design the pylons will be made of ﬁbre glass which is a non-
conducting material. Since the material is non-conducting the conductor lines can be mounted
directly on the arms of the pylon. This results in very diﬀerent support conditions for the
conductor lines.
This thesis is mainly concerned with the dynamic behaviour of the conductor lines speciﬁcally
the aerodynamic instability known as galloping. A numerical model capable of simulating
this instability is developed. The formulation is based on a 3D non-linear bar element that is
expanded to include a torsional degree of freedom as well. The algorithm used is a momentum
based non-linear energy-dissipating algorithm.
As part the validation of the numerical model results are compared with results obtained in
similar studies. Part of the project involves a study of the eﬀect of the new connection be-
tween pylons and conductor lines as proposed by Bystrup Architects. This type of connection
is compared with the classic method of hanging the conductor lines in insulator strings. Fur-
thermore some of the key parameters concerning the galloping instability are studied namely
the damping ratio, sag to span ratio and the wind velocity.

1
Introduction
Transmission lines have traditionally been constructed using steel truss towers where the con-
ductor lines hang from the towers in insulator strings. This is of course necessary since steel
is a conducting material. The implication is that these insulator strings provide very ﬂexible
support conditions for the conductor lines which means that they are quite susceptible to
motion during dynamic loading such as wind. Recently a new power pylon design has been
proposed by Bystrup Architects. This design features a Y-shaped pylon as seen in Figure 1, a
more detailed geometry can be seen in Appendix A. In this new design the pylon is made of
ﬁbre glass which is a non-conducting material. Therefore the conductor lines can be mounted
directly on the arms of the pylon through a special mount. This will result in much stiﬀer
support conditions for the conductor lines. It is noted that the conductor lines hang from the
arms of the pylons in Figure 1. This is due to lack of updated renditions.
Figure 1: Pylon proposal by Bystrup Architects.
This master’s thesis deals with the dynamic behaviour of conductor lines during galloping and
studies the implications of the very diﬀerent support conditions of the conductor lines that
the classic insulator strings and the newly proposed direct connection provides.
Galloping is an aerodynamic instability that can occur when iced conductor lines are subject
to steady cross-wind of medium to strong magnitude. The galloping motion is characterized
by large amplitude and low frequency oscillations, as deﬁned by Den Hartog [1]. This type of
1
==PAGE:14==
instability is quite severe both in terms of phase ﬂash over and structural damage and it is
therefore of great interest to investigate this phenomenon in depth. The galloping instability
is a governing factor in determining the spacing between conductors. A reduction in galloping
amplitudes can thereby potentially lead to reduction of the pylon arms. Even though galloping
has been a research area since the 1930s there are still much room for further studies. This is
in part due to the complexity this phenomena both through the non-linear behaviour, coupling
between adjacent spans and wind and ice loading that are diﬃcult to predict. Furthermore
the occurrence of conductor line galloping is quite rare and therefore ﬁeld observations are
also sparse.
The approach in the current work is to set up a numerical model that is capable of simulating
the galloping instability of a conductor line with wind load and ice deposits and thereby
obtain amplitudes and frequencies of the vibrations with a given set of physical parameters
for the conductor line. The aim is to formulate a rather simple numerical model that is able
to simulate conductor line galloping with suﬃcient accuracy and reasonable computational
eﬀort. The numerical model is developed in Matlab by expansion of some existing code and
formulation of new code. The formulation is based on a 3D non-linear bar element that is
expanded with a linear torsional degree of freedom. A momentum based non-linear energy
dissipating algorithm is used to solve the non-linear equation of motion.
As part of the validation of the numerical model results are compared with results obtained in
similar studies. Part of the project involves a study of the eﬀect of the new connection between
pylons and conductor lines as proposed by Bystrup Architects where this type of connection
is compared with the classic method of hanging the conductor lines in insulator strings during
galloping. This is done by introducing static springs in numerical model. Furthermore some
of the key parameters concerning the galloping instability are studied namely the damping
ratio, sag to span ratio and the wind velocity.

2
Conductor Line Galloping
In this section the phenomenon known as conductor line galloping is described. After a brief
introduction the two main galloping mechanisms are outlined and discussed.
Galloping is a phenomenon that can occur on conductor lines located in cold climates where
ice forms on the conductor lines thereby creating a non-symmetric cross-section. When sub-
ject to moderate to strong cross-wind this aerodynamic instability can occur. It is deﬁned by
a low frequency, large vertical amplitude and a smaller horizontal amplitude vibration mode
often resulting in an elliptical shaped in-plane motion known as the galloping ellipse. For most
conductor lines galloping occurs as single loop or two-loop vibration modes depending on the
dynamic characteristics of the conductor line. When the galloping motion is initiated it can
go on for quite large time spans of up to 24 hours with amplitudes as large as 0.03 times the
span length, Den Hartog [1]. Galloping of conductor lines is therefore very critical as it can
lead to phase ﬂash-over of the conductors, fatigue damage of towers and connections and in
more severe cases to structural failure of towers and conductor lines and thereby total collapse
of the structure. The consequences of galloping are quite expensive both in terms of replacing
damaged parts and in terms of lost revenue. In Figure 2 two cases of severe structural failure
due to conductor line galloping can be seen. Since the consequences of conductor line galloping
are so severe it is of great interest to investigate why this phenomena occurs and potentially
avoid it.
Figure 2: Structural failure due to conductor line galloping.
Galloping has been observed since the early 1900s and in the 1930s Den Hartog formulated
an explanation of the observed phenomenon, his explanation is still valid but it does not
account for all types of galloping. Two types of galloping is proposed in the literature; the

2.1
Den Hartog Galloping
classic Den Hartog galloping mechanism which is purely driven by an aerodynamic instability
and then the torsional galloping instability which is driven mainly by torsional motion that
couples into vertical and lateral motion. The torsional instability is more critical for bundled
conductors and less so for single conductors. The reason for this is that the ﬁrst vertical
and torsional frequency for bundled conductors are usually very close to each other which is
not the case for single conductors. Galloping is in nature a non-linear dynamic problem and
therefore quite complex. It involves large vertical and horizontal motion but also torsional
motion. The complexity is increased by the fact that accretion of ice on the conductor lines
is hard to predict and often not uniform along the span length. Another factor that adds to
the complexity is the wind loading which can also vary greatly both in intensity, duration and
direction.
Galloping only occurs when a layer of ice is formed on the conductor line thereby creating an
asymmetric cross-section. An example of this accretion of ice can be seen in Figure 3.
Figure 3: Ice accretion on conductor line.
The formation of ice on the conductor lines can take many shapes and depends entirely on the
weather conditions. In most cases the iced cross-section will have the general shape of half an
ellipse on one side. This shape can then be angled in various ways due to the wind, or lack
of, during the formation of the ice.
2.1
Den Hartog Galloping
In the 1930s Den Hartog formulated an explanation of the galloping phenomenon that had
been observed on conductor lines located in cold climates during strong cross-wind. Galloping
only occurs when a layer of ice is formed on the conductor line thereby creating an asymmet-
ric cross-section. When this asymmetric cross-section is subject to wind acting perpendicular
to the conductor line the conductor line will act similar to the wing of an aeroplane and be
lifted up until the elastic forces in the conductor gets too large and pulls the cable down again
thereby creating large oscillations. In order to understand this explanation some basic aerody-
namics are needed. When wind acts on a circular cross-section the resulting force will have the
same direction as the wind which will result in pure lateral motion. When however the cross-
section is non-symmetrical the resulting force of the wind will have an angle relative to the
wind direction and thereby the force will have a vertical component. As illustrated in Figure 4.

2.2
Torsional Galloping
The other type of galloping mechanism that has been proposed is vertical galloping induced
by torsional motion. This form of galloping is more critical for bundled conductor lines. The
torsional galloping mechanism is not as well described in the literature as Den Hartog gallop-
ing. The torsional galloping mechanism was initially proposed by Nigol and Buchan [2]. They
were of the opinion that almost all galloping was due to this torsional instability and that the
Den Hartog instability is only the driving factor in a few cases [3]. Their article is mainly
based on experimental work, which was later criticized for being based on false assumptions.
None the less it is still viewed as an important contribution to the study of galloping.
It is tentatively suggested by McConnel and Chang [4] that torsional motion of a sagged
transmission line can cause vertical motion through axial-torsional coupling. This theory of
axial-torsional coupling is however not investigated thoroughly and is not as easily demon-
strated as the Den Hartog instability. This torsional should mechanism occur when the ratio
of the ﬁrst vertical frequency and the torsional frequency is close to 1. This is characteristic
for bundle conductors whereas for single conductors the ratio is usually between 0.1 and 0.3
[5], but this of course depends of the accretion of ice. Therefore galloping of single conductors
is generally purely an aerodynamic instability as predicted by the Den Hartog criterion.
The conclusion is that the Den Hartog instability is solely an aerodynamic instability that
is determined by the shape of the cross-section and the angle of attack of the wind while
the torsional instability occurs when the torsional frequency is close to the vertical frequency
which is the case for many types of bundled conductors.
It is noted that the torsional mechanism, meaning vertical or lateral galloping initiated by
torsional motion, is proposed by a few authors but not generally accepted nor proved convinc-
ingly. There is however agreement on the fact that torsional motion is an important aspect
of the galloping motion especially for bundled conductor lines and that all cases of galloping
can not be attributed as Den Hartog galloping.

3
Non-linear Bar Element Formulation
The aim of this thesis is in part to set up a numerical model that can accurately and eﬃ-
ciently simulate conductor line galloping. Galloping of conductor lines is a non-linear dynamic
problem and therefore the ﬁrst step is the choice of a non-linear element formulation. In the
present work a simple 3 dimensional bar element with a non-linear strain deﬁnition in the form
of the Green strain has been chosen. This element is chosen because it will, with suﬃcient
discretization behave similar to a cable as it only contains a single internal degree of freedom
corresponding to the axial force.
Galloping only occurs when the cross-section of the conductor line is non-symmetric due to
ice accretion. This non-symmetry of the cross-section will cause the cable to rotate around its
own axis and thereby apply a torsional moment. In order to include this eﬀect the standard
bar element is expanded with an additional degree of freedom that corresponds to linear tor-
sion. As mentioned this element is rather simple and therefore also quite cheap in terms of
computation time which makes it relatively eﬃcient.
In the work of Desai et al. [6] and Wang and Lou [7] a three-node isoparametric element was
chosen in the numerical formulation for simulation of conductor line galloping. This element
is more advanced than the element formulation used the present work as it contains a center
node as well as the two end nodes. The simple bar element will with suﬃcient discretization
produce similar results with similar computational eﬀort. In essence the choice is between
fewer more advanced elements or a simpler element formulation, but with more of them. As
stated the approach of this thesis is to use the simplest element formulation; the standard bar
element.
In this section the 3D non-linear bar element formulation used in the numerical model is de-
scribed. The non-linear strain deﬁnition is chosen and the internal forces are then determined
by use of the principle of virtual work. Then the tangent stiﬀness matrix and the mass matrix
are determined. The formulation follows the total Lagrangian formulation from Krenk [8].
Lastly the bar element is expanded by an additional degree of freedom in order to include
torsion. The notation used in the following is as follows; vectors are denoted with bold face
letters and matrices are denoted with capital bold face letters. The dimension is 3 since it is
a 3 dimensional formulation and 4 when expanded to include the torsional degree of freedom.
Consider a bar element with the initial end points A0 and B0 and end points A and B after
displacement as shown in Figure 5.

3.5
Assembly of Global System Matrices and Vectors
Until now all matrices and vectors have been on an element level. As the structure consists
of a number of elements these element based vectors and matrices must be assembled in a
global format. For bar elements all forces and displacements are described in terms of nodal
values. The global displacement vector simply contains the displacements of all nodes. The
resulting force is obtained by summation of the contribution from all elements attached to a
given node. This means that internal force vector contains the change in internal force from
node to node.
The global mass matrix and the tangent stiﬀness matrix are assembled in the same way.
Therefore only the tangent stiﬀness matrix is used to illustrate the procedure. The element
stiﬀness matrices contain contributions from two nodes and when assembled in the global
stiﬀness matrix the stiﬀness contribution of a common node between to element matrices are
added together. This means that a quarter of the two element matrices will overlap. An
example of this assembly procedure is illustrated in Krenk [8] Section 2.5.

3.6
Non-linear Energy-dissipating Algorithm
In this section the algorithm used to solve the non-linear equations of motion is presented.
As mentioned previously galloping of conductor lines is a non-linear dynamic problem and
therefore the algorithm used in the numerical model has to be based on the non-linear equation
of motion.
For linear problems the standard Newmark method conserves energy for a speciﬁc set of
parameters as described in greater detail in Krenk [8], this is however not the case for non-
linear systems. In order for the time integration to conserve energy and thereby provide a
stable solution, the algorithm has to be based on the change in system properties over a time
step, i.e a momentum based algorithm. Such an algorithm can be set up so it has the property
that it conserves energy and momentum or dissipates it in a controlled manner. The energy
dissipating algorithm has an additional built-in damping parameter in the form of = 1 + ↵.
This serves as an algorithmic damping parameter, meaning that high frequencies are damped
and numerical noise is reduced. Often it is lower frequencies that are of interest and this is
especially true for galloping. Note that for ↵= 0 the algorithm will conserve energy.

in a compact format, the full details and derivations can be found in Krenk [8]. Assuming

Numerical Model
The element formulation used in the numerical model and the expansion with linear torsion
has been the subject of Section 3. The algorithm used to solve the non-linear equations of
motion was described in Section 3.6. This section deals with the set up of the geometry of
the conductor line in terms of shape and number of nodes per span. Then the boundary
conditions are deﬁned by implementation of linear static springs to simulate both the classic
method with insulator strings and the recently proposed method with a direct connection.
Damping is introduced in the form of Rayleigh damping.
Furthermore the load vector is
deﬁned with both self-weight, ice and wind loading. Finally it is shown that the numerical
model is capable of simulating galloping instabilities of the Den Hartog type. As mentioned
previously the numerical model is written as Matlab code that has been developed by use
of existing code, expansion of existing code and writing of new code. The full Matlab code
is attached in Appendix K on a CD.
The conductor line is modelled using the non-linear 3D bar elements with a rotational degree
of freedom presented in Section 3. Each element has two nodes and each node has 4 degrees
of freedom, 3 corresponding to displacement in the x, y and z-direction, respectively and the
fourth corresponding to torsion. This simple 2-node bar element has been chosen as this is
very cheap in terms of calculation time and still able to model the behaviour of a conductor
line quite well. Due to the rather simple nature of the element it is very important to make
sure that a suﬃcient number of elements is used in order to obtain a satisfactory level of
accuracy in the results.
The initial shape of the cable is modelled by use of the function for a catenary with a given
span length Lx and initial sag d0. This equation is solved using the Matlab function fsolve,
details of the Matlab code can be seen in Appendix K. When the function for the catenary
shape has been obtained a given number of nodes is chosen and then the geometry of the
conductor line is modelled with straight lines between the nodes as seen in Figure 7. It is
noted that the nodes are equally spaced with respect to the horizontal length, this implies
that the elements are not of the exact same length. The diﬀerence is however very small since
the catenary is quite shallow with sag to span ratios ranging from 0.02 to 0.05.
This catenary shape is only used when setting up the initial geometry of the conductor line
and thereby determines the actual length of the conductor line L before any loads are applied.
This allows for adjustment of the sag, d, based on the chosen initial sag d0. When choosing
the number of nodes to use in the simulation a number of factors plays in. The main issue
is to determine an appropriate number of nodes in order to obtain suﬃcient accuracy in the

4.6
Simulation of Den Hartog Galloping
In the following the aerodynamic instability known as Den Hartog galloping, as deﬁned in
Section 2, is demonstrated. As stated in Section 2 the Den Hartog criterion states that the
cross-section is aerodynamically unstable when the negative slope of the lift curve is greater
than the drag curve for positive angles. For negative angles the cross-section is aerodynami-
cally unstable when the positive slope of the lift curve is larger than the drag curve.

and the diﬀerentiated lift coeﬃcient can be seen as function of the angle ✓0 in Figure 13. The
diﬀerentiated lift curve is simply obtained by diﬀerentiation of the Fourier series that are used
to obtain the curves, for further details see Appendix E. In Figure 13 it is clearly seen for
which angles the cross-section is dynamically unstable and where Den Hartog galloping can
occur with the given cross-section.

4.7
Simulation of the Torsional Galloping Instability
The other type of galloping instability that has been proposed is the torsional mechanism
described in Section 2. This instability is driven by torsional motion that couples into vertical
motion through the coupling parameter BT . Furthermore the ratio between the ﬁrst vertical
frequency and the ﬁrst torsional frequency should be close to 1, which is typically the case for
bundled conductors [4] for the instability to occur.
It was attempted to simulate this type of instability by running a simulation where the torsional
frequency is ’tuned’ to be very close to the ﬁrst vertical frequency. This ’tuning’ is done by
reducing the torsional stiﬀness GJ. Even with this tuning of the torsional stiﬀness and the
coupling parameter BT = 25 · 103 Nm it was not possible to simulate a galloping instability
driven purely by torsion with the given wind data and ice accretion. There are several possible
reasons as to why it was not possible to demonstrate a galloping instability driven by torsion;
(1) It is possible that the wind data used simply does not provide large enough moments
outside of the areas where Den Hartog galloping occurs.(2) It could be that the parameters
used in simulation does not facilitate the occurrence of torsional instability. (3) The last reason
could be that conductor line galloping initiated purely by torsion does not exist. Research in
the area is still lacking and it has not been deﬁnitively proven in the literature as also stated
in Section 2

5
Simulation Results
In this section the results of the numerical simulation of conductor line galloping is presented.
As also mentioned previously the new proposal by Bystrup Architects introduces a new type of
connection between the arm of the pylon and the conductor lines where the conductor lines are
mounted directly on the beam of the Y-pylon. This type of connection is only possible because
the pylons are made of ﬁbreglass which is a non-conducting material. This in turn provides
a much stiﬀer connection between the conductor line and the pylon when compared to the
classic method of hanging the conductors in insulator strings. Since the method of connecting
the conductors and the pylons directly is brand new it is of great interest to determine how this
will aﬀect the behaviour of the conductors during galloping. In order to make a comparison
between the two methods two simulations are run where the only diﬀerence between them is
the support conditions.
The parameters chosen corresponds to a 400 kV system with a Martin ACSR type conductor
with a span length of 300 m that is typically used in Denmark. Typically the conductors are run
in duplex meaning two conductors are placed side by side with a distance of approximately 0.4
m. This however is not included in the simulation as only a single conductor line is considered
in the numerical model.
Both simulations are run for a 3-span conductor line with identical parameters, wind speed
and initial angle of the ice. Furthermore they are set up to have the same sag at midspan and
thereby also the same horizontal tension component. It is noted that the implementation of
springs in the numerical model leads to some increased sag of the side spans. This increase
in sag is accounted for by lowering the initial sag, d0, for the side spans so that the sags for
all three spans are within 0.1 m of each other. The parameters for the conductor line used in
the simulation can be seen in Table 2, Case 2. The algorithmic damping parameter is set to
↵= 0.002, the time step is h = 0.1 and the tolerance for the residual was set to 10−3 which
means it is very low compared to the internal forces in the system. The maximum number
of iterations is set to 200, this is a very high number of iterations. In the ﬁrst time step
all of the self-weight is applied at once and with quite a large time step this means a lot of
change in the system from the ﬁrst step to the next. This ﬁrst step usually requires around
100 iterations after the ﬁrst step usually 1 to 4 iterations are required. The total number of
time steps is adjusted between simulations as it varies quite a bit long it will take to reach
a steady galloping state. Usually a steady state is reached in 300 to 600 s with a time step
h = 0.1 this corresponds to a total number of time steps between 3000 and 6000.

5.3
Concluding Remarks
These three examples illustrates that the galloping motion is dramatically diﬀerent when
parameters are changed. Some set ups will result in the classic galloping ellipse with a very
clear excitation of the ﬁrst vertical and lateral modes while for an other set of parameters the
response will be a mix of multiple modes and possibly coupling with adjacent spans resulting
in a much more irregular response that never truly ﬁnds a steady galloping state. It is seen
that the galloping motion tends to be in either the ﬁrst vertical or horizontal mode and that
the torsional motion is generally the most irregular. It is quite diﬃcult to predict how diﬀerent
parameters aﬀect the galloping motion however an attempt at this is done in the following
section.

Parameter Study
As mentioned previously there are many diﬀerent parameters that aﬀects both the occurrence
of conductor line galloping and also the amplitudes during galloping. In this section the eﬀect
that certain parameters have on the galloping instability are outlined. In a design process it is
useful to know the eﬀects of chancing certain parameters in order to make a design that is less
sensitive to galloping instabilities. Firstly the the eﬀect of the wind velocity is studied for three
types of support conditions. Then the eﬀect of the damping ratio is studied and lastly the
eﬀect of the conductor line sag and thereby horizontal tension component is investigated. It is
noted that all of these parameter studies are done for a 3-span conductor line with parameters
corresponding to Case 2. If not stated otherwise the boundary conditions corresponds to the
direct connection method seen in Figure 9.
6.1
Eﬀect of Wind Velocity
The relation between the wind speed and the amplitude during galloping is studied for a
conductor line with parameters corresponding to case 2 with initial angle ✓0 = −35° and sag
at midspan d = 11.6 m. A simulation is run for diﬀerent wind speeds and both the vertical
and lateral amplitude during galloping is found. This is done for the classic support conditions
with insulator strings abbreviated IS, for the newly prosed direct connection abbreviated DC
and with completely ﬁxed support conditions abbreviated Fixed. The results of this can be

6.2
Eﬀects of increased Damping Ratio
As mentioned the exact damping parameters for the conductor line are very diﬃcult to obtain
and therefore a rough estimate is used. The damping characteristics plays a large role in the
simulation of galloping, both in terms of the build up of energy in the system and in terms of
the amplitude at the ﬁnal galloping state. If there is suﬃcient damping in the system more
energy will be dissipated than what is put in during the build-up. In this case the damping
will have prevented the galloping instability of occurring. If however the dissipation of energy
is smaller than the energy put in during the build-up the instability will still occur but the
amplitudes will be reduced. For these reasons the damping ratio used plays a large role in
the simulation. In the following the eﬀects of both the occurrence of galloping instability and
the reduction of the amplitude during galloping is investigated. This is done by performing
a sweep through initial angle ✓0 from −180° to 180° a simulation is made for every 5°. The
result of this can be seen in Figure28.

diﬀerent sags: d = 11.6 m, d = 9 m and d = 6.7 m where a simulation is made per 5°. The

conductor lines can be spaced closer together if the sag is reduced and thereby the length of
the pylon arms could potentially be reduced.

7
Conclusions
It is concluded that the numerical model formulated in the current work using the 3D non-
linear bar element with a torsional degree of freedom is indeed capable of simulating conductor
line galloping in an eﬃcient manner.
This is due to both the use of the simple element
formulation and also due to the use of the momentum based energy-dissipating algorithm.
This algorithm is unconditionally staple and can therefore run with a relatively large time
step which reduces the computation time signiﬁcantly.
Furthermore it is concluded that the Den Hartog criterion is indeed determining for when
conductor line galloping will occur for single conductors. It is found that the numerical model
is not capable of simulating a galloping instability purely driven by torsional motion even
when the conductor line is tuned in such a way that the ﬁrst vertical and torsional frequencies
are very close and coupling between vertical and torsional motion is employed through a
coupling parameter. It was however found that the torsional motion of the conductor line still
plays a signiﬁcant role in the simulation of conductor line galloping, just not as an initiating
factor.
It is also concluded that the recently proposed pylon design by Bystrup Architects where
the conductor lines are connected directly with the pylon arms leads to a reduction of both
vertical and horizontal amplitudes during galloping for wind speeds below 14 m/s compared
with the traditional method of using insulator strings. The damping ratio was found to play a
signiﬁcant role in the simulation of conductor line galloping where an increase of the damping
ratio generally leads to lower amplitudes during galloping and in some cases even prevents
the galloping instability from occurring.
Furthermore it was found that reduction of the
sag of the conductor lines leads to a reduction of the lateral amplitudes and an increase of
the vertical amplitudes during galloping. This means that the spacing between conductors
could be reduced if the sag is reduced. Therefore the new pylon design proposed by Bystrup
Architects where the conductor lines are mounted directly on the arms of the pylon show
great promise in reducing the galloping amplitudes especially if additional damping can be
introduced through the pylons.
Finally it is concluded that even though conductor line galloping has been researched for
around 80 years there are still many aspects within the ﬁeld of galloping that still require
dedicated research in order to be fully explained. This is in part due to the complexity of the
dynamic behaviour and the diﬃculty of predicting ice accretion and wind loading. Furthermore
some key physical parameters such as the damping ratio are diﬃcult to obtain. When spans
are coupled the complexity is increased further leading to quite a complex phenomenon to be
analysed.
