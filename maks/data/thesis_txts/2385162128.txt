Abstract
In the quest for the optics design optimisation of Athena, ESA’s future X-ray observatory
mission, DTU Space is an active member contributing on the investigation, optimisation,
and development of the reﬂective coatings; a critical element for the success of the mission.
This study, developed within the scope of a master thesis, is dealing with the
investigation of the eﬀects of the lift-oﬀ, and cleaning procedures, on the reﬂective
coatings, during the photolithographic stage of their production.
Fourty silicon oxide substrates, were coated with a baseline coating design, and treated
with various lift-oﬀand cleaning processes. The eﬀects of the chemical treatments on
the sensitive coatings, have been evaluated with X-Ray Reﬂectometry and Atomic Force
Microscopy means.
The analysis of the acquired data, provided useful results for the behaviour of the
baseline design, which have the potential on improving the overall development process
of the optics for Athena.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
More than half a century ago, the discovery of the ﬁrst extrasolar X-ray emission from
the constellation of Scorpius (Sco X-1), established X-ray telescopes as an integral part
of modern day astrophysics and cosmology. Since then, a plethora of discoveries in the
X-ray spectrum, revealed many hidden aspects of the nature of the celestial bodies, that
were invisible even to our own imagination. From the most massive galaxy cluster in
the universe (2XMM J083026+524133), recently discovered by XMM-Newton[1], to the
ongoing exploration of dark energy by Chandra, there are still many mysteries to be
answered.
The Advanced Telescope for High-Energy Astrophysics - ATHENA, commissioned by
ESA, is the new entry to the arsenal of X-ray observatories. The Athena mission with its
unparalleled large eﬀective area, high angular resolution, and great sensitivity, will grant
great discoveries in places that are now secluded. A marvel of the modern day technology,
the Athena telescope, would not have been possible without major technological advances
of the recent years. One of these advances lays on the optics of the telescope, known as
Silicon Pore Optics (SPOs). The highly modular concept of their nature, and the great
eﬀective area to mass ratio that they oﬀer, classiﬁes the Athena in the Golden Quadrant
of the telescopes[2].
SPOs, are the product of more than a decade of on-going research and development,
by ESA and other major industries. DTU Space, in collaboration with ESA, is actively
participating in the research activity. The main activity of the X-ray team at DTU Space,
is the optimisation, and fabrication process of the reﬂective coatings, deposited on the
SPOs. These coatings are essential for the eﬀective reﬂection of the X-rays from the
mirror modules.
In this study, we are focusing on one of the many stages involved in the manufacturing
process of the SPOs. This stage concerns a procedure, taking place after the deposition
of the reﬂective coatings on the SPOs. The process involves a common, in the photolitho-
graphic industry, lift-oﬀand cleaning procedure, which aims at the removal and cleaning
of a pre-deposited photosensitive layer. This layer is necessary for the coating of the

SPOs with the reﬂective layers, as well as for the ﬁnal assembly of the mirror stack.
For the assessment, of the chemical eﬀect on the coatings, we produced test samples
that were coated with the same bilayer design used on the SPOs. No photoresist was
deposited on the samples, since we want to assess the eﬀect on the coatings, and not on
the resist. The coating process, was contacted at the magnetron facility at DTU Space,
while the chemical experiments at the cleanroom facility located at Danchip.
The analysis data were acquired with X-ray reﬂectometry and Atomic Force Mi-
croscopy means, with the available equipment being located at DTU Space, and Danchip
respectively. The data analysis involved a multiple ﬁttings on the X-ray data for the in-
terface parameters of the coated layers, while the data from the Atomic Force Microscope
were used for the extraction of the top layer roughnesses of the samples.
Since the lift-oﬀ, and cleaning process is essential for the production of the SPOs for
the Athena mission, it must be ensured that the ﬁnal product must meet the standards
set by the mission design. The reﬂectivity eﬃciency of the mirrors depends highly on their
speciﬁed design. Detouring, from their initial optimised design will cause a signiﬁcant
drop on their eﬃciency. Parameters like the thickness of each layer, and the individual
layer roughness should always stay within an accepted range.
Processes like the under study lift-oﬀand cleaning, involve solvents and other ag-
gressive chemicals, that are always prominent in damaging sensitive materials, like the
reﬂective coatings.
This study, aims on exploring the eﬀects of these chemical procedures, in order to aid
the on-going research around this matter. Concrete results, should be available before
the initiation of the time consuming mass production process of the 150.000 mirrors
required for the optical design of Athena.

Chapter 2
The Athena observatory mission
The ATHENA X-ray observatory is part of ESA’s Cosmic Vision Program, being the
second (L2) large class mission. The ﬁrst conception of the mission was initiated by
major space agencies shortly after the launch of the Chandra and XMM Newton X-ray
telescopes in 1999, and after nearly a decade of consultations and partnership withdraws,
the mission was ﬁnally adopted by ESA in 2010.
The launch of ATHENA into space is scheduled for 2028, aboard an Ariane-6 rocket,
with the ﬁnal deployment of the telescope being the stable Lagrange Point-2 orbit, of the
Sun-Earth system. The L-2 orbit, will oﬀer protection against the main X-ray emitter of
our stellar neighbourhood, the Sun, ensuring noise-free observations, as well as thermal
protection for the detector systems.
Figure 2.1: Conceptual design of the Athena observatory. Credit: ESA CDF study
The scientiﬁc goals set by the mission, portent many engineering challenges for the
design of the telescope. Operating from low- to mid- energy ranges of 0.3-12 keV, Athena
will utilize a new generation of X-ray optics capable of succeeding in that goal. The focal
length of the optics is ﬁxed at 12 meters, where the X-rays are focused on a set of two

2.1.
Detector Systems
2.1
Detector Systems
Athenas detection system setup is comprised out of two focal plane camera systems; the
Wide-Field Imager (WFI), and the X-ray Integral Field Unit (X-IFU). Both are designed
to make an optimal use of the collecting area provided by the optical design of the mirror
system. The detector systems are mounted on a mobile bench capable of moving the
desired detector into- and out of the optical axis, depending on the desired observation.
This enables the utilization of the advantages that each detector has to oﬀer, since WFI
oﬀers a wider ﬁeld of view, while X-IFU a higher spectral resolution.
2.1.1
Wide - Field Imager (WFI)
The WFI detector provides a 40 arc min Field Of View (FOV) with a high count rate of 1
Crab (or 2.4 x 10−9 ergs/s/cm2 at 2-10 keV). It is designed to operate within the energy
range of 0.1 to 15 keV, achieving an energy resolution of ≤150 eV at 6 keV (FWHM).
In the heart of the system lies a set of ﬁve depleted P-channel ﬁeld eﬀect transistor
(DEPFET) active pixel sensor (APS) matrices, with pixel sizes of 5 arc sec, to match
the angular resolution of the optical system. The actual physical size of the detector
is set to be approximately 14 cm, and due to practical reasons it cannot be manufac-
tured as single monolithic device. Hence, the actual detector is assembled out of ﬁve
DEPFET chips, organized in a form factor as seen on ﬁgure 2.2. Four large and identical
DEPFET detectors encircle a smaller one, with the latter intended for bright sources,
thus functioning with faster respond times. Each DEPFET is accompanied by two ASIC
systems; one readout and one switcher responsible for the fast and accurate register of
the counts. The whole unit is shielded against background and proton radiation with a
graded Z-shield and has a nominal operating temperature of less than -60oC[4].
Figure 2.2: Schematic of the detector layout, comprised out of ﬁve DEPFET chipsets.
Four large DEPFET sensors are located in the periphery of a smaller DEPFET sensor in
the center. [4]

2.2.
X-ray Optics
2.1.2
X-ray Integral Field Unit (X-IFU)
The X-IFU is the second detector of the ATHENA telescope. The function of the
detector is based on the principles of the quantum calorimeter. Incident X-ray photons,
are absorbed by a crystal that is kept in temperatures close to the absolute zero (≤0.1
K). The energy transferred by the photon to the crystal, causes a rise on the temperature
of the latter, which subsequently can be registered by the system. The higher the energy
of the photon, the higher the increase of temperature. The fact that the crystal is kept
at these low temperatures, allows great sensitivity and spectral resolution.
In particular, X-IFU utilizes a micro-calorimeter array of Transition Edge Sensors
(TES), that contain 3840 pixels. It operates at the energy range of 0.2 - 12 keV, with
an energy resolution of 2.5 eV (for E < 7 keV). The ﬁeld of view is 5 arc min, while the
count rate can reach 10mCrab on nominally bright point sources with time resolution
between the events of 10 µ[5].
2.2
X-ray Optics
Unlike visible light, X-rays can only be reﬂected under very shallow angles. The high
energy photons, penetrate or get absorbed by most materials. In order to reﬂect X-rays,
the incidence angle of the photons in respect to the reﬂective surface has to be extremely
low. For very smooth surfaces, and for incidence angles <1o, the reﬂection eﬃciency
can reach the monad. For higher incident angles, the eﬃciency remains high until a
critical angle δθ; above this angle the eﬃciency drops signiﬁcantly. The critical angle
is contingent with the energy of the incident photons, and the electron density of the
reﬂective material. This has to be considered when designing a reﬂective surface, since
speciﬁc materials and consequently electron densities, can reﬂect X-rays within a limited
range of energies. For a given energy, materials with high atomic number (Z), have lower
critical angles and vice versa. The mirrors of Athena in particular, exploit the properties
of more than one material to reﬂect low- and mid- range energies, as it is explained in
the following section.
Due to the grazing angles under which X-rays are reﬂected, the focusing geometry
of the optics has to be accommodated accordingly. In the early 1950s Hans Wolter, a
German physicist, introduced three types of imaging optical geometries, designed to
reﬂect X-rays. These setups, named after him are now known as Wolter type I, II, and
III.
Athena, as for most of the telescopes, utilizes the Wolter-I design.
In order to
produce an image while keeping a useful wide ﬁeld of view, two reﬂections have to
happen. Applying this principle, two mirrors are used, where the ﬁrst one is placed
lateral to a virtual hyperboloid while the second lateral to a paraboloid, as seen on ﬁgure
2.3. Incoming photons, are reﬂected from the internal surface of the hyperboloidal and
paraboloidal aligned reﬂectors, and are focused on the desired focal point. The advantage
of the Wolter-I design is the capability of nesting many mirrors together, allowing a
substantial increase of the eﬀective area whilst keeping the telescope aperture compact.

2.2.2
Reﬂective coatings
An important factor, if not the most, that determines the X-ray reﬂectivity of a surface, is
the right selection of materials that will be deposited on the mirror surface as a reﬂective
coating.
As mentioned earlier, X-rays are reﬂected on very shallow angles, and below a critical
angle they undergo a total external reﬂection. The critical angle depends on the electron
density of the surface material and on the energy of the incident photons:
θc = c√ρ/E
(2.1)
where c the speed of light, ρ the electron density, and E the photon energy. Materials
with high atomic number and thus high electron density, such as iridium, gold and
platinum can provide reﬂection under large critical angles. Past missions like Chandra
and XMM-Newton, have utilised these materials as the primary metal coating for their
optics. However, these materials have their own limitations since they are prompt to
energy cut-oﬀs for energies higher than 10 keV. For example, gold can reﬂect X-rays
at energies up to 6 keV, for an incidence angle of 0.75o, while for an angle of 0.5o up
to 9 keV. Above these energy levels, a signiﬁcant drop in total reﬂection is observed[7].
To overcome this issue, the properties of two materials can be combined, forming the
so-called multilayer coatings.
Multilayers are thin layers of two alternating materials coated on top of each other.
By alternating high and low density materials, a high electron density contrast can be
achieved between the two, creating in that way an artiﬁcial Bragg reﬂector.
For the optics of ATHENA, previous studies have shown that a multilayer design with
a linear graded thickness is the dominant choice for acquiring the desired eﬀective area in
the mid- energy range between 5-10 keV. The under consideration materials include the
combination of either W/Si, Pt/B4C, W/B4C or Ir/B4C [8]. Later optimizations on the
multilayer coating designs, have proven that Pt/B4C and Ir/B4C are the predominant
choice so far, oﬀering the highest eﬀective area with similar performances, especially at
the scientiﬁcally interesting energy region of 6 keV[6].
In the low energy range between 0.5-5 keV, the baseline design of a simple bi-layer of
Ir/B4C has been proven as the best performing coating, providing the highest reﬂectance
for that range [8][6].

3.1
Atomic Force Microscopy
3.1.1
Basic principles and equipment
The direct characterization of a surface and its features, is possible with the use of the
Atomic Force Microscope (AFM). The AFM is classiﬁed as a Near ﬁeld or Scanning
probe microscope, invented by G.Binnig and C.F.Quate in 1986. This innovative class of
microscopes allowed the detection of extremely small forces, caused by microparticles in
the range down to a single atom. It incorporates a number of non-destructive scanning
methods, requiring minimal or no sample preparation before each measurement.
The operating principle of the AFM is based on the divergence of a sensitive cantilever,
which scans the surface of the sample towards the X and Y axes. A sharp tip, mounted
on the edge of the cantilever, detects the topographic changes of the surface by registering
the minuscule interaction forces between the tip and the surface. As these forces grow in
intensity, the tip and consequently the cantilever diverges towards the Z axis, reﬂecting
the vertical changes of the surface. An optical system, that is based on a laser beam
12
==PAGE:20==
3.1.
Atomic Force Microscopy
that gets reﬂected by the cantilever, measures the angular deviation of the cantilever
as it moves, and translates it into a detectable signal. The reﬂected beam is detected
and converted into an electrical signal by a light detector, and digitised. Finally, the
cantilever is brought back to its original position by an automatic retraction system.
The equipment used in this study, oﬀered three modes of scanning; contact mode,
tapping mode, and peak force tapping.
In a contact mode measurement, the cantilever tip is in a repulsive contact with the
surface. By doing so, the contour of the surface is followed by the tip, reciprocating
the cantilever. The small deviations along the z-axis, in respect to the x- and y- axes,
provides the topographic characteristics of the sample. However, an inherent drawback of
the contact mode is the risk of causing surface deformations by the lateral forces applied
from the tip, potentially damaging the sample.
In the case of a sensitive sample, where contact mode is prohibited, tapping mode is
available. In tapping mode, the lateral forces are minimised, while the lateral resolution
is maintained in the same level as contact mode. The cantilever is oscillating close to its
resonant frequency, touching the surface for a limited amount of time. The amplitude of
the oscillation is within the range of tens of nanometers.
For a contactless analysis, peak force tapping is available. In this mode, the oscillation
frequency of the cantilever is much lower than its resonance frequency, and the behaviour
of the tip is dominated by the van der Waals attraction forces. As the tip advances close
to the surface, it is captivated by the long range force until it jumps into contact, then
instantly the short range repulsive forces take over. Once the tip moves to the unload
phase it reaches an adhesion minimum and is then released. Each peak force is monitored
by an algorithm and is used to trigger the retraction mechanism.
An improved version of the peak force tapping mode, ScanAsyst, was available by the
AFM Icon model, used for the surface measurements of this study. ScanAsyst, uses an
image correlation algorithm that controls all the individual scanning parameters, allowing
the acquisition of a high quality image, without complicated user adjustments of the
imaging parameters.[9]
For the sensitive nature of the coatings, the best option was to use the ScanAsyst
version of the peak force tapping. The automation of the process by the algorithm by
adjusting the scanning speed and the limits on the z-axis was crucial for protecting the
sample while acquiring the data with the least amount of noise.

3.2.2
XRR setup at DTU
Eight coated samples intended for the chemical experiments, and one reference coated
sample, were characterized using the XRR facility at DTU with a ﬁxed energy within a
range of incidence grazing angles. The angular resolution of the instrument is δ = 0.007o
while the beam footprint is 0.12x0.5mm2. The experimental setup can be seen on ﬁgure
3.3.
The x-ray source is a rotating copper-coated anode, that operates at 12kW. Accelerated
electrons hit the anode, and their deceleration give rise to a Bremmstrahlung spectrum
with a line emission originating from the coated copper. The excitation and decay
of the copper atoms, produce a characteristic Kα doublet at energies Kα1=8.047keV
and Kα2=8.028keV. The Kα1 line, is preferred as the main source, due to its higher
intensity. The two energy beams, are separated by a monochromator; an asymmetrically
cut Germanium crystal that utilizes a double bounce design, with miller indices (111)
(ﬁg.3.3). The two beams are spread out in angle space, and the desired energy can be
extracted by changing the incident angle on the crystal. The slits serve the purpose of
containing the beam into a narrow proﬁle, and minimise any unwanted reﬂections from
the monochromator. The attenuator is placed in front of the beam, in order to prevent
any saturation of the detector, in case of an unexpected increase in the intensity.
The sample is placed on a ceramic holder equipped with a pump. The pump can
hold the sample vertically, by creating a vacuum between the ceramic holder and any
ﬂat surface. The holder has a three axis freedom of movement, by utilizing sensitive step
motors, and can rotate the sample relatively to the beam. The reﬂection is detected by a
10cm wide methane gas detector, capable of rotating up to 90o in respect to the sample
providing stabilisation of the focus point.[13]

surfaces and certain PSD thresholds signifying that the surface meets a speciﬁed ﬂatness

A bilayer coating design was deposited on 50 witness samples, used later in this study.
The deposition was performed using the direct-current magnetron sputtering facility at

Coating chamber
The coating chamber, is a bell shaped structure that contains the vertical mounting
plates placed on a rotating ring facing inwards, while the magnetron cathodes are facing
outwards. On each rotation, the mounted samples move past the target and a thin layer
of sputtered atoms is deposited. The layer thickness is related to the rotational speed of
the outer ring, and to the power applied on the cathodes.

After acquiring the dynamic deposition rate, the next step is to secure the SiO2
substrates on the mounting plates. The substrates used for the fabrication of the baseline
coatings, were double side polished oxidised silicon wafers, with a Miller index of <100>,
and diced into a rectangular shape. The interface roughness, given by the manufacturer,
is <2.5Å. The exact dimensions of the substrates can be seen on ﬁgure 4.3.
Figure 4.3: SiO2 substrate dimensions. The substrate is 70 mm long and 10 mm wide.
The substrate thickness is close to 500µm.
The primary baseline design is a bi-layer of Ir/B4C. The ﬁrst layer comprised of
Iridium with a thickness of 10nm followed by an 8nm layer of B4C. Two plates of B4C
and Ir are mounted on the cathodes, and after a pause of 120 seconds necessary for the
the settling of the Argon gas ﬂow, the coating process begins. Power is supplied to the
iridium cathode until the desired deposited thickness of the layer is reached. Next the
power supply is turned down, and power is provided to the B4C cathode until again the
desired thickness is achieved.
It should noted here, that due to an error in the calibration process, the ﬁnal
thickness of the deposited B4C was 12nm, instead of 8nm. However, this does not
aﬀect the outcome of the chemical process, since the eﬀects should be independent
of the layer thickness. All further analysis was adjusted to the new thickness. The
deposited thickness of the Iridium layer, was 10nm as planed.

Chemical procedures
The following section serves the purpose of describing the procedure followed during
the lift-oﬀand cleaning process of the coated samples. A total number of forty samples
were subjected to eight chemical treatment combinations, involving Dimethylsulfoxide
(or DMSO), and Acetone for the lift-oﬀprocess, a highly diluted mixture of Standard

4.2.1
An introduction to the Lift-oﬀand Cleaning processes
The lift-oﬀand cleaning processes are the ﬁnal steps of the photolithography process,
which takes place after the wedge etching in the oxide layer of the plate, and prior to the
ﬁnal stacking of the SPOs. Initially a photo-sensitive resist is applied onto the side of
the plate with the etched wedges. The plate is then exposed to UV light under a stripe
patterned mask, hardening the resist only on the locations exposed by the mask. The
uncured resist is then removed and the reﬂective coatings are deposited on the whole
surface. The ﬁnal step is the lift-oﬀprocess which removes the hardened resist, leaving
behind alternating X-ray reﬂecting and bonding zones. Prior to stacking, the cleaning
process aims at removing the remaining resist particles that may be present on the plate.
Cleaning is an essential step for both the performance of the X-ray coatings as well as
for the stacking process of the SPOs. Remaining particles can cause scattering eﬀects on
the incident X-rays, and induce microscopical discontinuities in the superﬁcial bond of
the two SPOs. A summary of the photolithographic procedural stages can be seen on
ﬁgure 4.5.

5.2.1
Data handling
The acquired surface proﬁle of each point, was exported after each scan, and treated with
the available analysis software at AFM Icon setup. The software is an image processing
and analysis suite, named NanoScope Analysis.
Prior to the data analysis, it is critical to eliminate any unwanted features from the
scan lines. Using NanoScope Analysis, an individual least square polynomial ﬁt was
applied for each scanning line. This process is known as ﬂattening, which essentially is a
ﬁlter that modiﬁes the data by eliminating low frequency noises, and by correcting any
tilting in the orientation of the sample during the scanning procedure.
The result of this process is a ﬂattened image ready for analysis (Depth, Roughness,
Section, etc.), since it carries all the topographical information of the surface.
The ﬂattened data are exported into ASCII ﬁles, with each ﬁle corresponding to a
single point. The ﬁle contains all the scanning preferences used during the measurement
by the SCANASYST algorithm, along with the height distribution of the scanned area.
Before proceeding to the actual surface analysis, a ﬁnal step is required that will extract
the height distribution data. The ﬂattened data are imported in Gwyddion1 and exported
as a new ASCII ﬁle, now containing only the height distribution of the surface in the
form of a single 256x256 matrix. Any further analysis and visualisation is based on the
exported ﬁles containing the height distribution matrices of the scanned areas.
The acquired data have been visualised in 1-D and, 2-D format, and are available at

6.1
XRR analysis
6.1.1
XRR data ﬁtting
Eight samples were characterized using the X-ray reﬂectometry method, before and after
their chemical treatment, along with one reference sample for comparison.
The acquired data were ﬁtted utilizing the integrated Fresnel function of the IMD
software. The ﬁtting method was based on a Brute-force algorithm, developed within
the IDL environment, that calculates the reﬂectivity curve in respect to the incidence
angle, for each possible combination of thickness and roughness of each individual layer,
including the roughness of the substrate. The output of each combination was compared
with the reﬂection curve acquired from the XRR measurement using a chi-square test, and
each chi-square value was indexed along with the corresponding parameters. Finally, the
goodness of the ﬁt was assessed based on the minimum chi-square value. Data concerning
the reﬂectance at graze angles between 0 and 0.2o were excluded from the ﬁtting process,
due to the shadowing eﬀect of the beam footprint, as explained on Chapter 4.
The ﬁtting process was performed in three stages for each sample, with each subsequent
stage corresponding to a diﬀerent range of tested values for the thickness and roughness.
In particular, for the ﬁrst stage the thickness was tested within the range of 0-200 Åwith
a step of 10 Å, while roughness within the range of 0-20 Å, with a step of 2 Å. For
the second stage, the range was focused around the most probable value acquired from
the ﬁrst stage, with a smaller step. For the third stage, the roughness parameters were
constrained at 2.5 Å, while the thickness parameters were set free within a narrow range
around the most probable value acquired from the ﬁrst stage. This decision was taken in
order to focus only on the determination of the layers thickness, since in many cases the
constraining of the roughness parameters was not an achievable task. The value of 2.5
Å, reﬂects the expected roughness of the bilayer interfaces, at least for the cases of the
Iridium, and SiO2 substrate. The principle behind this assumption is that, given that
the speciﬁed by the manufacturer roughness of the SiO2 substrate is <2.5Å, every layer
coated on top of it should have a roughness with the same or less amplitude; where the
lower amplitude applies in the case where the roughness is diﬀused and is not transmitted

in the next layer.
The inability to accurately constrain the roughness parameters of the bi-layer, is due
to the limitations of the XRR measurement at 8.047 keV. The same limitations apply on
the determination of the B4C layer thickness as well, although not in the same extent.
The baseline design, is optimised for reﬂecting energies bellow 5 keV [8]. At 8 keV, the
B4C layer is virtually transparent, with the reﬂection mostly happening by the Ir layer.
This can be observed at the model ﬁgures showing the predicted B4C thickness in respect
to Chi-square values, presented at Appendix B. In these ﬁgures, the model suggests
multiple solutions for the thickness of the boron carbide. In most cases, the solution
with the lowest Chi-square observed, is the one where the boron carbide is completely
absent. To compensate that, during the second stage of the ﬁtting process, the range of
tested values was limited around the most probable thickness, which was the 12 nm that
have been deposited initially. For the Iridium layer, the 8 keV the model is managing to
constrain the thickness around a single value.
Beyond the scope of this study, the x-ray reﬂectance of the samples was also measured
within a range of energies, in contrast to the single energy measurement at 8 keV. The
measurements took place at the four crystal monochromator beamline of the National
Metrology Institute (PTB) at BESSY II, in Berlin. The energy range of the monochro-
matic radiation was from 3.6keV to 10keV, with high spectral resolution[17], at a ﬁxed
incidence angle of 0.6o.
The BESSY data concerning the sample that was treated with Cosine’s standard

being that reﬂectance was now ﬁtted in respect to the energy, instead of the incidence

6.1.
XRR analysis
The analysis of the XRR data, acquired before the chemical treatment, provided a
Boron Carbide layer thickness close to 12 nm, and a Iridium layer thickness close to 10
nm. All the small deviations observed are in the range of a few Angstroms, which is
within the expected 10% error of the magnetron sputtering process.
The acquired roughness of the SiO2 substrate is close to the value given by the
manufacturer, at 0.25nm.
The roughness values of B4C, and Iridium, are not well
constrained, and should not be considered as ﬁnal.
For the lift-oﬀprocess, DMSO seems to have no eﬀect on the B4C layer, with both
pre- and post chemical processing values of thickness being similar, and close to the
initially deposited 12nm. The same applies for the Acetone lift-oﬀprocess as well, with
the slight increase of the B4C thickness, being within expected error limits.
Moving on the standard process, involving the DMSO lift-oﬀfollowed by a Standard
Clean I RCA clean, we observe a signiﬁcant change on the B4C thickness. The acquired
ﬁtting parameter, indicates the complete removal of the top B4C layer. To assure the
validity of this result, we resort to the BESSY data. The ﬁtting of the energy data,
conﬁrmed the 8 keV results, showing the same destructive eﬀect on the B4C layer. By
comparing the BESSY data of the reference sample, and the DMSO/RCA sample, on
ﬁgure 6.1a, we observe a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the reﬂectance in the energies between
3.6keV and 7keV. The high reﬂectance on the reference sample above 4 keV is indicative
of the B4C layer by design, and the observed drop in reﬂectance on the DMSO/RCA
sample at this energy, may act as an additional indication for the absent of the B4C
layer.

XRR analysis
Both DMSO, and DMSO/RCA samples, were on the same batch during the DMSO
lift-oﬀ, with the latter being subjected to the RCA cleaning afterwards on a diﬀerent
batch. After the conﬁrmation acquired from the BESSY data, concerning the diﬀerence
in the B4C thickness, it seems that the destructive eﬀect is caused by the RCA. This
outcome, has inﬂuenced the ﬁtting process for the rest of the measurements that involved
the RCA, by focusing the ﬁtting range for the B4C thickness, around the smallest value
acquired from the ﬁrst ﬁtting stage, since it was expected to cause the same destructive
eﬀects.
Therefore, the ﬁtting process on the sample subjected to the Acetone lift-oﬀ, followed
by the RCA cleaning, gave a zero thickness on the B4C layer, as well. The same applies for
the other two processes involving the RCA cleaning, as well. Both chemical combinations
of DMSO lift-oﬀand RCA/Plasma cleaning, and Acetone lift-oﬀand RCA/Plasma
cleaning, estimate the thickness of the B4C layer to be zero. The B4C thickness of the
sample subjected to the RCA only, seems to be unaﬀected from the process giving a
value of 12.3 nm, although a zero value is highly possible as well, since it was not possible
to be constrained well from the ﬁtting process. The plasma cleaning process alone, seems
to have no eﬀect on the B4C layer, giving the same thickness as before.
Overall, on all samples, the Iridium thickness is unaﬀected from the chemical processes,
giving values close to the initial coated thickness of 10 nm, again within the 10% error
range.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
During this study, an investigation on the eﬀects of the lift-oﬀ, and cleaning procedures
on baseline coatings was performed. The data acquired through the characterisation,
were analysed and presented on this study, along with the conclusions.
Forty SiO2 substrates, were initially coated according to the baseline design of the
Athena mission. The bi-layer design is a 8nm thick layer of Boron Carbide, on top of
a 10nm thick Iridium layer. A miscalculation during the calibration process, on the
rotational speed of the external ring of the magnetron sputterer, lead to the deposition
of 12nm of Boron Carbide on top of 10nm of Iridium, although with no direct eﬀect on
the main framework of this study, whatsoever. All forty coated samples, were chemically
treated in a cleanroom facility, following standard photolithographic procedures. In
batches of ﬁve samples per process, eight combinations of lift-oﬀand cleaning procedures
were planned and executed. One of the processes, concerned the replication of a standard
lift-oﬀand cleaning procedure, followed in the past by a research partner of DTU Space.
The recipe of this process, involved a DMSO lift-oﬀfollowed by cleaning with the use
of a diluted in ratio of 50-1-1 mixture of the Standard Clean I (RCA clean). The other
seven processes, were a DMSO lift-oﬀ, a DMSO lift-oﬀfollowed by a RCA and plasma
cleaning, an Acetone lift-oﬀ, an Acetone lift-oﬀfollowed by a RCA cleaning, an Acetone
lift-oﬀfollowed by a RCA and plasma cleaning, a RCA cleaning alone, and a Plasma
cleaning alone.
Sixteen samples, eight for each process, were characterized before, and after their
treatment, through XRR and AFM means, and the acquired data were analysed.
The analysis of the XRR data at 8.047keV from the DTU Space facility, with
the combination of a single dataset acquired from BESSY measurements, indicate a
destructive eﬀect on the top B4C layer, for the standard process of DMSO lift-oﬀand
RCA cleaning. In fact, the acquired result from the combination of the two datasets,
indicate the complete absent of the top B4C layer after the process. For the remaining
seven samples, the characterisation was conducted only through 8.047keV XRR, and due
to the inherent limitations to constrain the thickness of the B4C layer at this energy, the
only conclusions are speculative. Nonetheless, the same reductive trend of the B4 layer,
is observed on all processes that involve the RCA cleaning. The XRR analysis of the
61
==PAGE:69==
7.1.
Future work
plasma cleaning, shows no eﬀect on the coatings. The Iridium layer, stays unaﬀected
throughout all processes, sustaining the original thickness. Same constraining limitations,
are observed on the estimation of the layer roughnesses, as well.
The second stage of analysis, concerns measurements conducted on the second set
of the eight samples, through AFM of the top layer. The acquired surface proﬁles were
utilised for the calculation of the RMS roughness of each sample. The results enhance
the speculations of the XRR analysis, since the same destructive eﬀect is observed on all
samples involving the RCA cleaning. The limited amount of measurements for before
the chemical process, does not allow a statistically signiﬁcant comparison between the
roughness values. However, in cases such as for the process of Acetone lift-oﬀfollowed by
an RCA and Plasma cleaning, and the RCA cleaning, the roughness increases dramatically
indicating this eﬀect. The 1-D and 2-D images of the surface proﬁles after the chemical
processes, show in all the cases that involve the RCA cleaning, the presence of structures
with signiﬁcant height. Where if we take under consideration the previous indications,
these structures are probably islets of B4C, remained on the Iridium layer. Again results,
from the DMSO and Acetone lift-oﬀ, show no signiﬁcant change on the roughness, and
no presence of peculiar structures on the surface. The plasma cleaning did not increase
the roughness, although from images acquired through the use of an optical microscope
show areas where the B4C coating was damaged.
This study has presented results through the XRR and AFM analysis, for eight
processes. However, for the extraction of more concrete conclusions, further study on the
eﬀects is deemed necessary.
7.1
Future work
The inability to constrain the B4C thickness from the 8 keV measurements, makes
necessary the characterisation of the bi-layers with energy beams in the low energy range.
The conceptual design of the baseline, is based on the high eﬀective area that it oﬀers at
energies below 5keV. A characterisation, with an energy beam at 1keV will shed more
light on the eﬀects of the RCA on the B4C layer. The high reﬂectivity of the B4C at the
low energy range will invigorate the overcoming of the B4C constraining issue.
A X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), will help to ascertain any possible con-
tamination of the coated layers with unwanted elements during the coating process, but
also it will investigate the level of oxidation of the B4C layer.
A transition electron microscopy (TEM) analysis, is also highly recommended in
order to acquire high resolution overview of the cross-section of the bi-layer, and draw
conclusions about the diﬀused roughness between the B4C, and Iridium layer. A scanning
electron microscopy analysis, could also be tried, although the resolution limitations of
this technique, is oﬀering low prospects of achievement.
A draft plan of the above mentioned processes, made during this study is available in
Appendix D. Each process is assigned to one of the reserved samples produced, and may
be used as a guide.
