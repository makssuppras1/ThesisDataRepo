Abstract
In recent years, the continuous development of silicon photonics has accelerated the de­
mand for compact and high­performance beam steering devices. Among various ap­
proaches, optical phased arrays (OPAs) have emerged as a promising solution for re­
alizing solid­state beam steering without mechanical motion. However, achieving uni­
form emission and high fiber–chip coupling efficiency remains a critical challenge, es­
pecially when transferring designs from simulation to fabrication. This thesis addresses
these challenges through the design, simulation, and layout preparation of a silicon nitride
(Si3N4) optical phased array intended for operation near 900 nm.
A complete simulation workflow based on Finite­Difference Time­Domain (FDTD) and
Eigenmode Expansion (EME) methods was developed to optimize key subcomponents,
including the grating coupler and multi­mode interference (MMI) power splitter. The 1×32
MMI design, derived from self­imaging theory, achieves power imbalance below 1 dB and
excess loss within 1.5 dB, demonstrating strong fabrication tolerance. The grating coupler,
designed using chirped and apodized structures, exhibits a simulated fiber–chip coupling
efficiency of approximately –5.2 dB, which aligns closely with reported values in literature.
A fabrication­ready GDS layout was generated following design­for­manufacturability con­
straints for single­pass electron­beam lithography.
The simulation and layout results confirm that the proposed design maintains stable op­
tical performance within realistic fabrication deviations, and the far­field beam uniformity
satisfies practical OPA requirements. This work thus establishes a validated workflow
from design to layout, bridging numerical optimization and fabrication feasibility. Future
extensions of this work include experimental characterization, integration of thermo­optic
phase shifters for active beam control, and expansion toward two­dimensional OPA ar­
chitectures for full spatial beam steering.
Key Words: Silicon Photonics, Optical Phased Array, Grating Coupler, Multi­Mode Inter­
ference, Finite­Difference Time­Domain, Design for Manufacturability, Beam Steering.

1.1
Background and Motivation
Optical phased arrays (OPAs) have emerged as one of the most promising technologies
for realizing solid­state beam steering and shaping without mechanical motion. By pre­
cisely controlling the relative phases and amplitudes of light emitted from an array of opti­
cal antennas, OPAs enable dynamic manipulation of the far­field radiation pattern through
constructive and destructive interference. This capability opens the door to numerous ap­
plications, including light detection and ranging (LiDAR), free­space optical communica­
tion, optical trapping, imaging, and holographic projection. In recent years, rapid progress
in silicon photonics and complementary metal–oxide–semiconductor (CMOS) compatible
fabrication techniques has allowed OPAs to be integrated on a chip, offering compactness,
stability, and scalability that mechanical scanning systems cannot match. Recent reviews
have summarized the rapid development of integrated OPAs for both beam steering and
beam shaping, highlighting the transition from uniform emitter arrays to more advanced
non­uniform and apodized architectures for improved beam uniformity and efficiency [1].
However, achieving high­performance beam steering remains challenging. A typical OPA
based on uniformly spaced and uniformly powered emitters produces a Gaussian­like far­
field distribution, where the optical intensity gradually decreases from the center toward
the edges. While such a Gaussian profile can be acceptable for many laboratory demon­
strations, it is less ideal for practical applications that require a uniform illumination area.
For instance, in LiDAR systems, a flat­top beam provides nearly constant intensity across
the scanning range, which helps maintain consistent detection sensitivity and reduces the
dynamic range required for signal processing. Similarly, in optical projection or imaging
applications, a uniform beam can ensure even illumination, leading to improved image
quality and reduced distortion.
To meet these demands, various beam­shaping techniques have been developed to mod­
ify the near­field distribution of OPAs so that the far­field pattern approaches a flat­top (top­
hat) profile. These approaches include the use of amplitude tapering inspired by antenna
array theory (e.g., Dolph–Chebyshev or Taylor weighting), non­uniform spacing between
emitters, and engineering of the grating emission strength along the array to achieve con­
trolled radiation intensity. The ability to tailor the emission profile in this manner directly
determines the uniformity, beam width, and sidelobe suppression ratio of the generated
beam, which are critical parameters for high­performance optical systems.
In this context, the design of a non­uniform grating emitter capable of generating a flat­top
beam becomes an important research direction. Such an emitter can provide a near­
constant far­field intensity within a target angle range while maintaining compactness
and compatibility with integrated photonic platforms. The development of this type of
OPA structure is particularly relevant for next­generation solid­state LiDAR and free­space
communication systems, where efficiency, uniformity, and scalability are equally impor­
tant.
Therefore, this thesis focuses on the design, simulation, and optimization of an integrated
OPA based on non­uniform grating emitters, aiming to produce a flat­top beam profile
with controlled intensity distribution.
The motivation behind this work is to contribute
to the ongoing effort of improving on­chip beam steering performance and to explore

1.2
Silicon Nitride as a Photonic Platform
Silicon nitride (Si3N4 or SiN) has become one of the most versatile and reliable material
platforms for integrated photonics, bridging the gap between high­index contrast silicon
and ultra­low­loss silica. Owing to its wide optical transparency window, low propaga­
tion loss, high power­handling capability, and excellent compatibility with complementary
metal–oxide–semiconductor (CMOS) fabrication processes, SiN enables scalable inte­
gration of passive and active photonic components across a broad spectral range—from
the visible to the mid­infrared region [2][3]. Its refractive index of approximately 2.0 at
1.55 μm provides sufficient confinement for compact waveguide design while maintaining
low sidewall scattering losses. Furthermore, the absence of two­photon absorption (TPA)
at near­infrared wavelengths allows SiN devices to operate under high optical intensities
without nonlinear degradation, a significant advantage over crystalline silicon.
Another crucial advantage of SiN lies in its ability to achieve ultra­low propagation loss,
which is essential for high­performance photonic circuits, resonators, and interferometric
systems. Traditional high­temperature SiN processes (up to 1200 °C) can introduce stress
and limit back­end­of­line (BEOL) compatibility. Recent advances have addressed these
limitations through optimized low­temperature and stress­controlled processes. In 2024,
Hou et al. demonstrated an anneal­free SiN fabrication process achieving a record propa­
gation loss of 0.08 dB/m, while maintaining full CMOS compatibility [2]. This development
marked a major milestone, enabling the integration of high­quality photonic devices on
wafers with electronic circuitry without requiring post­deposition thermal annealing. Such
improvements drastically enhance the manufacturability and yield of SiN­based systems
while reducing thermal stress and material cracking risks during processing.
In parallel, the scalability of SiN technology has been verified on large wafer platforms.
Pfeiffer et al. reported in 2024 the mass manufacturing of high­density, ultra­low­loss SiN
photonic circuits with wafer­level uniformity better than ±2% and typical losses below 0.5
dB/m, demonstrating that high­performance SiN devices can be produced efficiently using
standard 300 mm CMOS lines [4]. These results confirm that SiN is not only a labora­
tory research material but also a commercially viable and scalable platform for volume
production of integrated photonic systems.
The versatility of SiN extends across wavelength regimes, covering the visible, near­
infrared, and mid­infrared bands, which supports diverse applications such as biosensing,
quantum photonics, and optical phased arrays (OPAs). Luke et al. summarized in their
review that SiN combines a wide bandgap (~5 eV) with excellent thermal stability and low
linear absorption, enabling broadband transparency from approximately 400 nm to 3.6 µm
[3]. They also compared SiN with other common photonic integration materials—such as
Si and SiO2 —in terms of refractive index, transparency range, thermal properties, and
CMOS compatibility, showing that SiN uniquely balances low loss, strong confinement,
and process flexibility.

These collective advances highlight why SiN has emerged as a preferred platform for
beam­forming and optical phased array (OPA) applications. Compared to silicon, SiN
exhibits significantly lower thermal drift and propagation loss, which leads to improved
phase stability and reduced power consumption in large­scale OPA networks. Mean­

characteristics make SiN an ideal candidate for implementing non­uniform grating emit­

phase and amplitude control over long optical paths is required.
1.3
Objectives and Scope of This Thesis
The main objective of this thesis is to design and optimize an integrated optical phased
array (OPA) on a silicon nitride (Si3N4) platform capable of generating a flat­top beam
profile. The study focuses on developing design strategies that achieve uniform beam
shaping through non­uniform grating emitters while maintaining low optical loss, phase
stability, and compatibility with standard CMOS fabrication processes.
The specific objectives of the work are summarized as follows:
• To investigate existing optical phased array architectures and beam­forming tech­
niques, identifying design principles suitable for achieving a flat­top far­field distri­
bution.
• To develop and simulate a Si3N4­based OPA structure that employs non­uniform
grating emitters for controlled radiation strength and improved beam uniformity.
• To design supporting passive photonic components required for OPA operation,
such as coupling and power distribution structures.
• To validate the proposed designs through numerical analysis and prepare a fabrication­
ready mask layout compliant with standard cleanroom processes.
The scope of this thesis is limited to the conceptual design, optical simulation, and layout
preparation of the proposed Si3N4 OPA. Experimental fabrication and optical characteri­
zation fall outside the time frame of this work but are outlined as future extensions. The
outcomes aim to provide a practical design foundation and methodological framework for
subsequent implementation and testing.

1.4
Structure of the Report
The remainder of this thesis is organized as follows.
Chapter 2 provides a comprehensive literature review of optical phased array (OPA) tech­
nologies, including beam­forming principles, non­uniform grating emitter concepts, and
supporting passive photonic components such as edge couplers and multimode inter­
ferometers (MMIs). The chapter also highlights recent progress in silicon nitride (Si3N4)
integrated photonics and identifies the research gaps addressed in this work.
Chapter 3 presents the design methodology adopted for this thesis. It describes the over­
all design workflow, simulation tools, material parameters, and theoretical background
relevant to the development of Si3N4­based OPAs.
Chapter 4 details the design and simulation results of the proposed OPA and its asso­
ciated components. The performance of the non­uniform grating emitters, edge coupler,
and 1×32 MMI is analyzed and discussed in terms of beam uniformity and optical effi­
ciency.
Chapter 5 introduces the layout design and fabrication planning. It explains how the
individual components are integrated into a complete mask layout (GDSII) and discusses
key considerations for manufacturability under standard cleanroom processes.
Chapter 6 outlines the expected characterization and evaluation plan. Although fabrica­
tion and testing were not completed within the project period, this chapter describes the
proposed experimental setup and performance metrics for future validation.
Chapter 7 concludes the thesis by summarizing the main findings and contributions, dis­
cussing limitations, and suggesting directions for future research on Si3N4­based OPAs
and beam­forming systems.

2.1
Principles of Optical Phased Arrays
Optical phased arrays (OPAs) have emerged as one of the most promising approaches for
solid­state beam steering, offering a compact, high­speed, and fully electronic alternative
to mechanical scanning systems. The fundamental working principle of an OPA is based
on optical interference: an array of coherent emitters radiates light into free space, and
by precisely controlling the relative phase among these emitters, the far­field interference
pattern can be shaped to form a beam in a desired direction. As the phase distribution
across the array is varied, the direction of constructive interference changes accordingly,
enabling the beam to be steered without any mechanical motion. This principle was ex­
perimentally demonstrated at the integrated photonic level[5], who realized a large­scale
nanophotonic phased array on a silicon platform and demonstrated beam steering through
electronic phase control.
In a typical integrated OPA, light from a single source is divided into multiple on­chip
waveguides using power splitters or multimode interferometers. Each optical path con­
tains a phase shifter that provides fine control of the optical phase. When these phase­
modulated signals are emitted through grating couplers or edge emitters, the superpo­
sition of the coherent waves in free space produces a controllable far­field pattern. The
beam direction is determined by the phase difference between adjacent emitters and the
spacing between them. By incrementally tuning the phase difference, the main beam can
be swept across a continuous angular range. However, to prevent the appearance of
grating lobes—secondary peaks caused by spatial aliasing—the emitter spacing must re­
main smaller than half the operational wavelength in free space. This constraint defines
the maximum unambiguous steering range of the OPA and represents a fundamental
design limitation in both silicon and silicon nitride implementations.
In addition to phase modulation, the amplitude distribution across the emitters also plays
a significant role in beam quality. A uniform amplitude distribution yields a sinc­shaped
far­field profile with strong sidelobes, while a non­uniform distribution, such as Gaussian
or Hamming weighting, can suppress sidelobes and improve beam purity. The trade­off
between main­lobe width and sidelobe suppression is often managed through design op­
timization, depending on the application requirements—whether for LiDAR, optical com­

2.2
Beam Forming Techniques and Flat­Top Beam
Generation
Beam forming in optical phased arrays (OPAs) refers to the controlled synthesis of a de­
sired far­field radiation pattern by precisely adjusting the amplitude and phase of individual
emitters. Unlike the simple linear phase gradient discussed previously for beam steering,
advanced beam forming involves tailoring both the spatial phase and amplitude profiles
to achieve specific beam shapes, intensities, or coverage areas. The goal is to optimize
the energy distribution within the main lobe while minimizing sidelobes or distortions, en­
suring uniform illumination and stable system performance in various applications such
as LiDAR, optical projection, and free­space optical communication.
2.2.1
Beam Forming Mechanisms
The most fundamental beam forming approach is the linear phase progression, where
the phase of each emitter increases linearly along the array. This produces a single, nar­
row main lobe that can be steered across angles according to the interference condition
described in Section 2.1. However, more complex beam profiles can be generated by in­
troducing non­linear phase distributions, allowing for broader or multiple main lobes, focal
points, or even shaped intensity patterns in the far field. These non­linear distributions
are particularly useful in applications requiring specific illumination geometries, such as
wide­angle scanning or multi­target detection.
Phase control in integrated OPAs is typically achieved through thermo­optic or electro­
optic phase shifters integrated along each waveguide. Thermo­optic tuning, used in sil­
icon and silicon nitride platforms, provides high precision but relatively slow response
times due to thermal diffusion. Electro­optic phase shifters, while faster, require more
complex fabrication and higher drive voltages. Regardless of the tuning method, main­
taining precise phase uniformity across hundreds of emitters remains a key challenge, as
phase errors can lead to beam distortion and efficiency loss [5].
2.2.2
Amplitude and Phase Optimization
In practical beam forming, amplitude and phase profiles are often jointly optimized to
achieve the desired far­field shape. The optimization can be analytical or numerical, de­
pending on the target function. Analytical methods employ known apodization functions
such as Gaussian, Blackman, or Hamming windows to suppress sidelobes. Numerical
methods, on the other hand, utilize iterative optimization algorithms—such as Gerchberg–
Saxton phase retrieval, genetic algorithms, or gradient­based methods—to design cus­
tomized phase–amplitude masks that yield a specific far­field pattern. These approaches
are especially powerful for generating beams that deviate from conventional Gaussian or
sinc profiles.
In the context of OPAs, amplitude control can be implemented either by adjusting the
power distribution in the feeding network (e.g., through variable optical couplers or MMIs)
or by tuning the efficiency of individual emitters, such as their grating strength or emission
angle. Although this adds fabrication complexity, it enables fine control of the far­field
envelope and beam shape. Combined amplitude–phase beam forming thus provides a
practical pathway to realize advanced beam profiles beyond conventional steering.
2.2.3

A flat­top beam, also known as a uniform­intensity beam, features a plateau­like inten­
sity distribution across the main lobe. This beam shape is desirable in many applica­
tions where uniform illumination is required—such as optical lithography, 3D sensing, or
imaging—because it minimizes signal variation and ensures consistent detection or pro­

In summary, beam forming techniques in optical phased arrays encompass both the con­
trol of beam direction and the design of the beam’s spatial intensity profile. While linear
phase gradients are sufficient for narrow, steerable beams, more advanced approaches
using amplitude–phase co­optimization and non­linear phase profiles enable flat­top or
custom­shaped beams for application­specific performance. The ability to integrate these
functionalities within compact silicon or silicon nitride photonic platforms has accelerated
the development of programmable, high­speed optical beamforming systems, paving the
way for adaptive LiDAR, high­resolution projection, and intelligent optical communication.

Non­uniform and Chirped Grating Emitters
Non­uniform and chirped grating emitters are widely employed in optical phased arrays
(OPAs) to tailor the far­field radiation profile and improve beam uniformity. Unlike conven­
tional uniform gratings that emit with constant coupling strength, these advanced designs

circuitry.
2.3.1
Non­uniform (Apodized) Grating Emitters
In a uniform grating emitter, each grating period couples a fixed fraction of light out of the
waveguide, leading to an exponential power decay and a Gaussian­like far­field pattern.
This inherently limits beam uniformity and increases sidelobe levels. To address this,
apodized gratings employ spatially varying coupling coefficients κ(z) to redistribute the
emitted power along the grating length, thereby shaping the amplitude envelope of the
emitted field.
According to Zhao and Fan (2020)[6], optimizing κ(z) using apodization functions such as
Gaussian, cosine, or raised­cosine profiles allows a designer to suppress sidelobes while
maintaining high coupling efficiency. Their design framework further demonstrates that
appropriate apodization can achieve over 10 dB sidelobe reduction compared to uniform
gratings, while retaining high fabrication tolerance. Moreover, the gradual modulation of
refractive index perturbation along apodized gratings reduces back­reflection and broad­
ens the operational bandwidth.
In essence, apodized grating emitters provide intrinsic amplitude shaping and sidelobe
suppression, enabling smoother and more uniform far­field intensity distributions in OPA
systems.
2.3.2
Chirped Grating Emitters
While apodization controls the amplitude of emitted light, chirped gratings introduce ad­
ditional phase control by varying the grating period along the propagation direction. This
spatial chirp modifies the local Bragg condition, allowing different segments of the grating
to emit at slightly different effective wavelengths or angles. Such a mechanism encodes
a non­linear phase distribution across the emission aperture, facilitating advanced beam
shaping such as flat­top beams, broadened far­field coverage, or dispersion compensa­
tion.
Recent research by Ning [7] demonstrated an ultra­broadband step­chirped multimode
Bragg grating that effectively redistributes optical energy to achieve flat­top filtering and
uniform intensity across a broad spectral range. The step­chirped configuration—comprising
discrete grating sections with gradually increasing periods—enables efficient power equal­
ization and smoother spectral transitions compared to conventional linear chirp designs.
When applied to OPA emitters, such step­chirped structures can be used to generate
near­uniform far­field intensity profiles and mitigate beam distortion caused by fabrication
variations or wavelength­dependent emission.
Therefore, chirped grating emitters extend beam­shaping capability by introducing con­
trolled phase gradients through spatial frequency variation, offering a powerful passive
route to flat­top and broadband beam generation.

2.4
Edge Couplers and Mode­Matching Structures
Efficient light coupling between optical fibers and integrated waveguides is a fundamental
requirement in optical phased array (OPA) and photonic integrated circuit (PIC) systems.
Because OPAs depend on precise phase control across many channels, coupling inter­
faces must minimize loss and reflection to maintain phase coherence and overall sys­
tem efficiency. Among various coupling approaches, edge couplers and mode­matching
structures are widely used to overcome the large mode size and refractive index mismatch
between optical fibers and high­index integrated waveguides.
2.4.1
Principle of Edge Coupling
Edge coupling transfers light directly from an optical fiber into the cleaved facet of an on­
chip waveguide. In contrast to grating couplers, which rely on vertical coupling through
surface diffraction, edge couplers operate laterally and can achieve significantly lower
insertion losses when properly Roelkens [8] demonstrated that optimized edge­coupled
structures on silicon­on­insulator (SOI) platforms can outperform surface grating couplers
by several decibels, primarily due to the reduced scattering and polarization sensitivity in­
herent to in­plane coupling. However, the large mode field diameter (≈10 µm) of standard
single­mode fibers remains orders of magnitude larger than that of submicron silicon or
silicon nitride waveguides, introducing severe mode mismatch if no intermediate transition
is provided.
To alleviate this mismatch, most edge couplers employ a tapered geometry, in which the
waveguide gradually expands or contracts along the coupling direction. This geometric
transition adiabatically converts the confined on­chip mode into a larger, weakly confined
field that better overlaps with the fiber mode. The taper length and profile—linear, ex­
ponential, or polynomial—are chosen to balance compactness and adiabaticity. Expo­
nential tapers are particularly favored for their smooth mode transformation and reduced
back­reflection. Roelkens et al. further showed that adding an intermediate refractive
index layer, such as a silicon overlay, can improve coupling efficiency and bandwidth by
smoothing the effective index gradient at the fiber–chip interface. In essence, edge tapers
act as optical transformers, gradually reshaping the guided mode to achieve efficient and
low­reflection fiber­to­chip coupling.
2.4.2
Inverse Tapers and Spot­Size Converters
A more compact and efficient approach to mode matching was pioneered by Almeida
[9] through the concept of the nanotaper, also known as the inverse taper. In this struc­
ture, the silicon waveguide width narrows down to below 150 nm at the facet, forcing
the optical mode to expand into the surrounding low­index cladding. This produces a
quasi­Gaussian mode with a size comparable to that of a single­mode fiber, dramatically
improving mode overlap and reducing coupling loss. The taper width and length define
the adiabatic condition, where gradual mode evolution minimizes reflections and prevents

higher­order mode excitation. This principle laid the foundation for modern spot­size con­
verters (SSCs), which are now standard components in integrated photonic interfaces.
Building upon this, multi­layer and hybrid SSC designs combine inverse tapers with aux­
iliary cladding materials to further tailor the mode size and polarization response. By in­
troducing intermediate guiding layers—often made of SiON or polymer overlays—the re­
fractive index transition between the fiber and the high­index core can be made smoother,
enhancing coupling efficiency and reducing alignment sensitivity. These hybrid structures
preserve the broadband characteristics of the nanotaper while providing additional flexi­
bility in design. Consequently, the inverse taper and its derived SSC architectures have
become the most widely adopted solutions for high­efficiency, broadband coupling in mod­
ern photonic integration.
2.4.3
Performance and Practical Considerations
The performance of an edge coupler or spot­size converter (SSC) is determined by sev­
eral key metrics, including coupling efficiency, return loss, alignment tolerance, and po­
larization dependence.
• Coupling efficiency measures the fraction of input optical power successfully trans­
ferred into the on­chip mode. Optimized inverse tapers typically achieve coupling
losses below 1 dB when well aligned.
• Return loss quantifies the suppression of back­reflections, which should exceed
40 dB to ensure stable operation in phase­sensitive OPA systems.
• Alignment tolerance defines the acceptable positional deviation at the fiber–chip
interface. This tolerance can be relaxed by using hybrid tapers or larger mode con­
verters that provide broader effective coupling regions.
• Polarization dependence is another critical parameter, as polarization­induced
phase shifts can distort beamforming accuracy and degrade system uniformity.
State­of­the­art SSCs based on inverse tapers and hybrid polymer overlays have demon­
strated broadband operation from the visible to the near­infrared range, with sub­decibel
coupling loss and minimal polarization sensitivity. Altogether, these advancements have
made edge couplers and SSCs indispensable in ensuring reliable, low­loss optical inter­
faces for large­scale OPA and photonic systems.
2.5
Multi­Mode Interferometers (MMIs) for Optical Power
Distribution
In optical phased arrays (OPAs), precise and uniform optical power distribution across all
emitters is essential to ensure consistent phase modulation and stable beam formation.
Any imbalance in optical amplitude among array elements can lead to beam distortion,
reduced sidelobe suppression, and degraded steering performance. To achieve this uni­
form distribution, multi­mode interferometers (MMIs) are widely employed as compact,
fabrication­tolerant, and broadband power­splitting structures within photonic integrated
circuits (PICs).
2.5.1
Operating Principle of MMIs
An MMI operates based on the principle of self­imaging in a multimode waveguide. When
an optical field is launched into a multimode region from a single­mode input, the various
propagating modes interfere constructively and destructively along the propagation direc­
tion, reproducing one or more images of the input field at specific positions. By placing

2.5.2
Power Distribution in OPA Architectures
In OPA implementations, MMIs are typically cascaded in a tree­type configuration to
achieve 1×N power distribution, where N corresponds to the number of emitters in the
array. Each MMI stage splits the incoming light evenly into multiple branches until the
desired channel count is reached. For example, a 1×32 OPA feed network can be real­

hierarchical splitting structure is highly robust and compact, minimizing footprint while
maintaining near­equal optical intensity across all array elements.
MMIs also preserve high phase uniformity between outputs because the self­imaging pro­
cess produces identical optical paths for each branch, mitigating phase imbalance and
reducing beam degradation. Therefore, MMIs provide both amplitude and phase consis­
tency, which is critical for coherent beam interference in OPAs.
2.5.3
Design Considerations and Performance
Key performance metrics of MMIs include insertion loss, power imbalance, phase unifor­
mity, and bandwidth.
• Insertion loss: the total optical loss from input to output, typically below 0.5 dB in
well­optimized MMIs.
• Power imbalance: the difference between output channels, ideally within ±0.2 dB
to maintain beam symmetry.
• Phase uniformity: ensures consistent optical phase among outputs, essential for
interference­based beam steering.
• Bandwidth: reflects how stable the splitting ratio remains across the wavelength
range, which is crucial for broadband or chirped­grating OPAs.
The performance of MMIs can be fine­tuned through geometric optimization and refractive­
index engineering. Using silicon nitride (Si3N4) or low­contrast platforms can increase op­
erational bandwidth, while compact silicon MMIs offer smaller footprints but slightly higher
fabrication sensitivity. Simulation tools such as the beam propagation method (BPM) or
eigenmode expansion (EME) are typically used to optimize multimode region length and
width to balance uniformity and loss.

2.6
Summary and Identified Research Gaps
The literature reviewed in this chapter highlights continuous progress in optical phased
arrays (OPAs) and related photonic components toward achieving high­efficiency, com­
pact, and electronically steerable beamforming systems. Early studies established the
theoretical foundation of OPAs, demonstrating that coherent interference and phase con­
trol among multiple emitters enable dynamic and precise beam steering without me­
chanical movement. More recent works have advanced beamforming strategies through
amplitude–phase optimization and flat­top beam generation, aiming for uniform far­field
distributions and improved sidelobe suppression.
At the device level, non­uniform and chirped grating emitters have emerged as promising
solutions for passive beam shaping, enabling intrinsic amplitude and phase modulation
directly on­chip. However, their performance remains limited by narrow operational band­
widths, side­lobe instability, and fabrication sensitivity—particularly in high­index silicon
photonics. Likewise, while inverse­taper edge couplers and multimode interferometers
(MMIs) have improved coupling and power uniformity within OPA arrays, they still face
challenges in maintaining low­loss operation and consistent phase balance across a wide
spectral range.
These findings suggest that further progress in OPA performance depends not only on
optimizing individual components but on developing an integrated design framework that
unifies emission control, coupling efficiency, and signal distribution within a single photonic
platform. In particular, silicon nitride (Si3N4) offers unique potential for such integration:
its ultra­low propagation loss, broad transparency window, and excellent thermal stability
make it ideal for realizing broadband and uniform beam emission, yet its use in large­scale
OPA architectures remains underexplored.
Building upon these insights, the following chapter introduces a design methodology that
leverages the material advantages of Si3N4 and advanced grating engineering to achieve
a broadband, fabrication­tolerant OPA with improved emission uniformity. This work aims
to bridge the gap between theoretical beamforming optimization and practical, manufac­
turable on­chip photonic implementation.

3.4.3
Design Trade­offs and Practical Considerations
There exists an inherent trade­off between beam uniformity and radiation efficiency. Stronger
coupling enhances total outcoupled power but introduces phase distortion and reflection­
induced imbalance, while weaker coupling improves phase uniformity at the cost of effi­
ciency. During the MATLAB­based synthesis, monotonicity and smoothness constraints
were applied to κ(z) to balance these competing objectives. The continuous κ(z) pro­
file was realized as a piecewise constant design, compatible with lithographic resolution
limits, and a raised­cosine taper was implemented near both edges to mitigate ringing
artifacts in the far field.
For the fully etched SiN platform operating at 900 nm, the achievable coupling coefficient
typically ranges between 0.05 mm−1 and 0.3 mm−1, depending on the etch depth and grat­
ing period. These fabrication constraints were included during the synthesis process to
ensure manufacturability and avoid overcoupling near the grating entrance. Overall, the
proposed workflow—(i) empirical calibration from (Λ, DC) to κ, (ii) inverse synthesis of
κ∗(z) for flat­top amplitude shaping, and (iii) discrete realization with phase­compensated
chirp—provides a practical and fabrication­aware method for achieving flat­top beam for­
mation on the SiN platform. The final implementation of this nonuniform, step­chirped
grating structure is discussed in Section 3.5.

uniformity and phase coherence across the aperture.

5.3.1
Substrate and Pretreatment
The process starts with a thermally oxidized silicon wafer carrying a 300 nm low­stress
SiN film, which serves as the optical core layer. Chips are diced to 2 cm×2 cm pieces and
cleaned using acetone, IPA, and DI water. Prior to resist coating, an adhesion­promoting
surface treatment is performed using HMDS priming in Oven 2 (recipe 01). HMDS treat­
ment enhances the adhesion of the negative­tone resist ARN 7520.17 to the SiN surface.
For batches containing multiple small chips, a dummy 4–6 inch silicon carrier wafer is
used during spin coating.
5.3.2
Resist Coating
The ARN 7520.17 electron­beam resist is spin­coated on the chip using either LabSpin 02
or LabSpin 03 at 5000 for 60 s with an acceleration of 3000 s−1. The resist thickness is
approximately 380 nm after soft baking at 85 °C for 60 s. To minimize contamination, the
resist is transferred to a personal­use bottle instead of directly using the 1 L master bottle.
5.3.3
Conductive Layer Deposition
To suppress charging during electron­beam exposure, a thin aluminum (Al) conductive
layer is deposited using a thermal evaporator in the yellow room. The target thickness
is 20 nm (deposition rate: 1 Å s−1, recipe Al1_1A/s). Samples are covered with tin foil
during transfer to avoid exposure to ambient DUV light, as the resist is also sensitive in
that spectral range.
5.3.4
Electron­Beam Lithography
Patterning of the designed MMI and edge­coupler structures is performed using the JEOL­
JBX 9500­FS electron­beam writer with a single­pass exposure. The exposure dose is set
to 180 µC cm−2 at a beam current of 6 nA under condition file 6na_ap5. This configuration
provides a balance between resolution and throughput while maintaining good line­edge
definition for the SiN waveguides.
5.3.5
Development
After exposure, samples are developed in the E­beam Development Fume Hood 10 using
developer AR 300­46 for 130 s, followed by an additional 30 s Al­layer dissolution step
(total development time 160 s). After development, the samples are rinsed in DI water for
more than 60 s and post­baked at 85 °C for 60 s. The developed patterns are inspected
under an optical microscope to ensure feature integrity before etching.
5.3.6
Etching and Cladding Deposition (Planned)
In the final fabrication stage, the exposed resist pattern will be used as a hard mask for
reactive ion etching (RIE) of the SiN layer to a depth of 300 nm. After resist removal in
NMP and solvent cleaning, the device will be cladded with SiO2 using plasma­enhanced
chemical vapor deposition (PECVD) to form the upper cladding. This final step completes
the SiN photonic stack with symmetric optical confinement and environmental protection.

7
Conclusions and Future Work
7.1
Summary of Achievements
This thesis presented the design, simulation, and manufacturability analysis of a silicon
nitride optical phased array (OPA) with an emphasis on achieving efficient fiber–chip cou­
pling and uniform beam emission. The key contributions can be summarized as follows:
• A comprehensive simulation workflow based on FDTD and EME methods was es­
tablished to analyze and optimize the device performance, including grating emitters
and multi­mode interference (MMI) splitters.
• A 1×32 MMI design was successfully optimized using the self­imaging principle,
achieving power imbalance below 1 dB and excess loss within 1.5 dB, which are
within the practical fabrication tolerance range.
• The grating coupler was designed to provide high coupling efficiency and broad
operation bandwidth. Through chirped and apodized structural variations, the sim­
ulated fiber–chip coupling efficiency reached up to −5.2 dB, which agrees well with
literature values (Muñoz et al., 2017 [3]).
• A full GDS­compatible layout was prepared following standard fabrication constraints,
ensuring process compatibility with single­pass e­beam lithography on a 300 nm
Si3N4 layer.
Together, these results demonstrate a complete development cycle from theoretical de­
sign to fabrication­ready layout, providing a validated foundation for future experimental
realization and system­level integration.
7.2
Key Insights from Simulation and Layout
The simulation results indicate that the optical performance of the proposed structures
is highly sensitive to fabrication deviations such as etch depth and waveguide width. A
parametric sweep revealed that a ±10 nm variation in etch depth can lead to approximately
0.3 dB change in coupling efficiency. Similarly, MMI imbalance increases linearly with
waveguide width deviation, confirming the importance of precise process control.
The layout results also highlight that single­pass lithography can accommodate all critical
features while maintaining optical symmetry across the array. The expected phase error
among emitters was below 0.05π, ensuring a uniform far­field beam pattern with side­lobe
suppression better than −12 dB. These findings confirm that the simulated performance
lies within a realistic range considering typical fabrication errors reported in the literature.
7.3
Future Work
Although the simulation and layout results demonstrate the feasibility of the proposed de­
sign, several research directions remain open to further enhance its performance, scala­
bility, and practical integration.
First, experimental fabrication and characterization will be the immediate focus of future
work. The realized devices will be measured using a standard fiber–chip coupling setup
to validate coupling efficiency, beam uniformity, and polarization dependence. The mea­
sured data will be compared to the simulated results, providing quantitative feedback

for refining the simulation models and validating the robustness of the design assump­
tions. Special emphasis will be placed on process­induced deviations such as etch depth
nonuniformity, sidewall roughness, and overlay misalignment, which are expected to in­
troduce measurable optical phase errors and coupling variations.
Second, further optimization of the grating and MMI structures can be pursued using in­
verse design or adjoint­based optimization algorithms. These techniques, which have
recently shown superior performance in complex photonic topologies, can systematically
explore the design space beyond traditional parameter sweeps and yield configurations
with improved tolerance to fabrication noise. The introduction of chirped and apodized
gratings with variable etch depth profiles may enable coupling efficiencies beyond −3 dB
and broader spectral bandwidths exceeding 100 nm, as suggested by recent works on
apodized and step­chirped gratings.
Third, integrating active phase tuning mechanisms represents a promising direction to en­
hance the dynamic performance of the optical phased array (OPA). Incorporating thermo­
optic or electro­optic phase shifters at each emitter arm would enable beam steering
and phase correction under environmental fluctuations. The thermal crosstalk, response
speed, and power efficiency of such phase control units should be systematically investi­
gated to assess their viability for large­scale integration.
Fourth, extending the current one­dimensional OPA structure into a two­dimensional emit­
ter array will be a critical milestone for achieving full spatial beam steering. This transition
will require revisiting the MMI distribution network to maintain uniform optical power and
phase coherence across both dimensions. Additionally, exploring multi­level intercon­
nect architectures and low­loss vertical coupling schemes will be essential for reducing
footprint and maintaining array compactness.
Finally, from a materials and platform perspective, further reduction of propagation loss
and phase noise can be achieved by adopting ultra­low­loss stoichiometric Si3N4 or SiON
platforms, potentially integrated with backside Bragg reflectors for enhanced directionality.
Combining these with scalable fabrication techniques such as deep­UV lithography or
stepper­based patterning could bridge the gap between laboratory prototypes and wafer­
scale manufacturing.
Overall, future research will aim to establish a fully functional, experimentally validated
Si3N4­based optical phased array platform that can serve as a foundation for advanced
beam steering, LiDAR imaging, and on­chip optical communication systems. The work
presented in this thesis provides a solid groundwork for this development by integrating
numerical optimization, design­for­manufacturability considerations, and layout­ready im­
plementation.
