Thesis on Intrinsic Properties of Till
Abstract
At some time 30 % of the world’s landmass was covered by glaciers or ice sheets including
a signiﬁcant proportion of Denmark. This led to a high level of clay till in the topsoil
layers. The properties of clay tills are still not fully understood. Consequently, the till
parameters are often underestimated in the design of, for instance, foundations.
This thesis examines the intrinsic properties of Danish clay till experimentally. Intrinsic
properties are inherent to the soil and independent of the natural state. Thus, reconstit-
uted samples are applied in this study.
The samples applied in this thesis are from two locations, Hillerød and Kongens Lyngby.
Through classiﬁcation tests, the samples are found to be similar in particle size distribu-
tion. The soil samples can be described as composite soils diverse in their particle size, i.e.
the samples are multi-graded. The particle density is 2.662-2.670 g/cm3. Furthermore,
the samples have a low clay content (below 10 %) compared to foreign clay tills. This
also inﬂuences the Atterberg limits where the liquid limit is within the range of 17.1-35.1
% and the plastic limit 8.6-18.6 %. In general, the Atterberg limits are shown to decrease
with depth.
The compression curves for the soil samples are somewhat similar in shape and an intrinsic
compression curve is obtained. Thus, the intrinsic curve is normalised and an intrinsic
compression line describing the Danish clay till samples are obtained. The coeﬃcient of
determination (R2) of the intrinsic compression line equation, see below, is 0.9919 [-].

The intrinsic compression line (ICL) is compared to intact clay till samples. The intact
samples indicate a degree of anisotropy that is related to the glacial processes.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
During the Pleistocene Ice Age, glaciers covered large areas of the earth. It is estimated
that at some time 30 % of the world’s landmass was covered by glaciers or ice sheets
(Clarke, 2017). As a glacier advances, it deforms and erodes the bedrock and underlying
soil, and carries large amounts of sand, silt, gravel, and boulders. The unstratiﬁed deposits
laid down by melting glaciers are called till. The physical characteristics of till may vary
from glacier to glacier according to Das (2016).
Glacial soils have been discussed amongst geologists since the nineteenth century and this
continues today. According to Clarke (2017) “glacial soils are complex, composite spa-
tially variable soils that prove to be challenging not only in assessing their structure and
properties but also in ensuring that economical, safe designs are constructed”. The prop-
erties of glacial soils are still not fully understood. Consequently, the soil parameters of
glacial tills are often underestimated leading to diﬃculties in construction and overdesign
of foundations, cf. Clarke (2017).
In Figure 1.1, it is seen that a considerable proportion of Denmark has been covered
by glaciers that resulted in a high level of clay till in the topsoil layers (brown areas).
Thus, the importance of understanding and determining the properties of glacial soils (in
particular clay till) are crucial in Denmark.

1.2
Motivation
In 1990, Burland presented the concept of intrinsic properties which are inherent to the
soil and independent of the natural state, cf. Burland (1990). Thus, the properties of
reconstituted soils are termed intrinsic properties. The in situ properties together with
intrinsic properties can be used as a framework to produce design parameters (Clarke
et al., 1997).
The intrinsic properties of clays were ﬁrst presented by Burland (1990). Later, several
authors have described the intrinsic properties of foreign clays and clay tills. In particular,
the British civil engineer Barry Clarke has published research on UK glacial soils, see
Clarke et al. (1997), Clarke et al. (1998), Clarke (2017), and Clarke (2018). However, to
the best of this author’s knowledge, literature describing the intrinsic properties of Danish
clay till have not yet been published. Thus, it is of high relevance that this thesis focuses
on the intrinsic properties of Danish clay till.

1.3
Aim and Objective
This thesis is experimental in nature and investigates the intrinsic properties of glacial
clay tills employing one-dimensional compression tests. For this purpose, experimental
techniques used in geotechnical engineering for describing soil samples and testing the
behaviour in one-dimensional compression are conducted. In particular, the thesis aims
to identify appropriate soil parameters that can be used to predict the location of the
intrinsic compression line (ICL) for Danish clay tills. In detail, the main objectives of this
thesis include:
• To describe and classify soil samples.
• To perform one-dimensional compression tests to determine the location of the ICL.
• To interpret one-dimensional compression behaviour as a function of particle size
distribution and plasticity indexes.
• To compare the behaviour of intact tests to the ICL and evaluate the results based
on known geological processes.
1.4
Structure
The thesis is organised so that the topic of the intrinsic properties of clay till and the
aim of the project are introduced in Chapter 1 (introduction).
Chapter 2 (literature
review) presents the literature review and existing knowledge on clay till to discuss and
support the results of the experimental work performed as a part of this thesis. Chapter 3
(methodology and materials tests) describes the equipment and soil portions applied for
the investigation as well as the experiments performed to describe the samples and obtain
the location of the ICL. Chapter 4 (results and analysis) contains the results of the
experimental work along with a discussion of the results. Lastly, in Chapter 5 (conclusion),
the thesis ends with a conclusion where recommendations for future research are included.

Chapter 2. Literature Review

Chapter 2
Literature Review
The behaviour and intrinsic properties of clay till depend on several parameters. This lit-
erature review focuses on the parameters concerning the soil classiﬁcation and the existing
knowledge on intrinsic properties for foreign clay tills. The most relevant parameters con-
cerning the soil classiﬁcation are the soil structure and composition, particle density, and
plasticity limits. These parameters inﬂuence the one-dimensional compression behaviour
that form the basis of the intrinsic properties.
2.1
Soil Classiﬁcation
Clay tills are classiﬁed by their particle size distribution, consistency, and density, accord-
ing to Clarke (2017). Therefore, this section presents previous studies on these classiﬁca-
tion parameters.
2.1.1
Soil Structure and Composition
The structure and composition of a soil type rely on the geological history, homogeneity,
and anisotropy which are connected to the formation of the soil (Ovesen and Galsgaard,
2012).
The soil structure consists of the fabric (arrangement of particles and inter-particle bond-
ing. Fabric refers to the orientation and distribution of particles in the soil. Oftentimes,
the particle distribution is shown by a particle size distribution curve as shown in Fig-
ure 2.1.

Glacial soils are often described as composite soils and diamictons, which are a wide range
of non-sorted to poorly sorted soils or sediments (Clarke, 2017). In Figure 2.1, particle size
distributions of glacial soils from across the UK are shown. On the basis of the particle
size distribution curves, sorting and grading of the soils can be described. Thus, glacial
soils can be described as diverse in their particle size distribution, according to Figure 2.1
and the literature, see Clarke (2018), Larsen and Society (1995), and Clarke et al. (1997)
among others.
From Figure 2.1, it is also seen that even though all the soils are classiﬁed as glacial soils,
the particle sizes are diverse. According to Clarke (2017), the particle size distribution
can vary from single size to bimodal to multimodal and this can vary over short distances
or be extensive. Furthermore, the clay content is shown to vary between. However, the
tills described in Clarke (2018) all have a clay content higher than 10 %. The tills are
shown in a ternary diagram in Figure 2.2 (right). The ternary diagram is used to identify
the type of till, how it behaves, and the relationship between particle size distribution
and description.

The clay content in the soil aﬀects the soil behaviour (for instance, the Atterberg limits, see
Section 2.1.3) and the deformation of the substrate. An increase in clay content increases
the depth of deformation and a possible switch from a brittle to a ductile behaviour,
according to Clarke (2018). Thus, it is important to determine the clay content.
The orientation of the particles in the soil is aﬀected by the glacial processes. All glacial
tills are subject to gravitational forces during deposition, and many are also subject
to shear (Clarke, 2018). When the soils undergo shear, the vertical stress is no longer
the principal stress which means that there is a rotation of the principal stress axes
(Clarke, 2017). Thus, the possibility of shear results in potential anisotropy of the glacial
soils.
Hence, the degree and type of deformation and pore pressure and temperature
regimes during deposition cause the soils to be spatially variable. This entails that the
properties of glacial tills are variable both vertically and horizontally, making it diﬃcult
to obtain representative samples and selecting appropriate design parameters (Clarke,
2018). Therefore, it is possible to have tills with the same or similar composition but
distinctly diﬀerent properties.
The gravitational forces during deposition cause the compaction of the soil. However,
the compaction does not necessarily lead to overconsolidation of the tills (Clarke, 2018).
It is not possible to estimate the degree of overconsolidation and the coeﬃcient of earth
pressure at rest from the geological history of the sites. This is because of the high degree
of uncertainty regarding the stress conditions and pore water pressures imposed by past
glaciations (Clarke, 2017).

2.2.1
Intrinsic Compression Curves
The compressive behaviour of soils is shown in a semi-logarithmic diagram displaying
the relationship between the logarithm of applied vertical eﬀective stress and the void
ratio. The diagram shows the compression curves for the soils. As the vertical eﬀective
stress applied on the soil increases, the void ratio decreases, see Figure 2.4. Thus, the soil
becomes denser. The changes in the void ratio are not immediate but depend on the time
it takes for water to drain from the soil, cf. Mitchell and Soga (2005).
In Burland (1990), Clarke et al. (1998), and Habibbeygi et al. (2017), the intrinsic com-
pression curves for the investigated soil samples are found to be similar in shape (slightly
curved).
According to Clarke et al. (1998), the compression index is be diﬀerent for
diﬀerent clays. If the compression curves are to be of use in practice, they have to be
normalised with respect to the clay type. Thus, it is useful to normalize the intrinsic com-
pression curves with respect to the void ratio because it removes the eﬀects of plasticity
(decreasing plasticity results in decreasing void ratios).

Value of the Intrinsic Compression Line
There is uncertainty on the formation of primary deposits because they were formed below
an ice sheet, which could be tens or hundreds of metres thick. This created very dense
deposits that could not simply be replicated through standard consolidation procedures
in a laboratory environment (Clarke, 2017). Therefore, a framework for the interpretation
of standard tests needs to be developed, according to Clarke et al. (1998).
The study of the intrinsic properties of tills is part of the development of this framework.
For natural soils, the intrinsic compression line provides a useful means of assessing, for
instance, the degree of overconsolidation. Natural soils diﬀer from reconstituted soils in
a number of ways. These diﬀerences stem from the inﬂuence of structure (arrangement
of particles) and interparticle bonding (Burland, 1990).
The framework can be used in future determination of the soil parameters for clay till to
avoid underestimation leading to diﬃculties in construction and overdesign of foundations.

Equipment
In this thesis, the PARIO device and software were used to determine the particle size
distribution of the ﬁne-grain particles left in the pan. The PARIO device was measuring
the pressure over a period of 24 h. Subsequently, the particle size distribution was obtained
using the PARIO software. The software is based on Stoke’s law where the particle size
is obtained by the change in density because the particles are going to the bottom due to
the force of gravity. Details can be found in the user manual (METER, 2018).
There were several pitfalls when using the PARIO device.
First of all, the particles
had to be in homogeneous suspension when starting the measurements. Otherwise, the
software would return the wrong values. Therefore, the ﬁne grain particles were mixed
with distilled water and put on a shaking table for three hours before performing the test
and just before starting the test, the soil was shaken for 60 s before placing the cylinder
on the table. Thus, the soil was seen to be in suspension when the test was started.
Furthermore, the results from the PARIO software would only be correct if the input
parameters were inserted correctly. Hence, the correctness of the particle size distribution
from PARIO relied on the accuracy of the remaining classiﬁcation experiments. There
were many factors that could aﬀect each of the tests performed in the laboratory. However,
all tests were performed with high precision to avoid inaccuracies.
The recommended soil portion for performing the particle size distribution with the soft-
ware is 25 g, cf. METER (2018) Section 2.2.1.2. However, due to the limited amount of
soil available, the test was performed with a smaller portion than what was recommended.
This inﬂuenced the reliability of the result as the error on the mass fraction presumably
would increase from what was stated in the manual.

3.7
Incremental Loading Oedometer Testing
Equipment
The oedometer test was applied for this thesis in the determination of the consolidation
settlements of the soil portions. While carrying out the test, several parameters were
important in order to obtain a useful and satisfactorily result for the oedometer test.
In this thesis, the oedometer set-up was as shown in Figure 3.12a where the vertical
displacements were recorded as a function of time and data logged onto a computer. The
displacements were measured by linear variable diﬀerential transformers (LVDTs) with
a resolution of ± 1 µm. The vertical displacement referred to a reference position. The
reference was chosen as the initial height of the soil. The height was obtained by a known
reference height of a solid steel cylinder (hs = 23.065 mm). It was crucial to zero the
LVDT before it was used to measure the height of the solid steel cylinder and to keep
the transformer in the same position when dismantling the steel cylinder and the soil
was placed. Otherwise, the initial height of the soil would be unknown and it would
be impossible to obtain the consolidation settlements because of no known reference.
Therefore, the LVDTs were handled very carefully when setting up the oedometer tests.
Furthermore, it was essential to know the soil mass placed in the oedometer ring. Con-
sequently, the entire soil portion was poured into a bowl where the weight was recorded
including spoons applied when placing the soil in the oedometer setup. Subsequently, the
weight of the bowl, spoons, and leftover soil was recorded. The soil was placed relatively
fast in the oedometer ring and weighed to avoid loss.
The soil in the oedometer setup was aﬀected by increasing load and subsequently unload-
ing. When unloading the soil samples, the weight at the ﬁnal step of 250 g was kept on the
lever arm to avoid swelling of the samples. Thus, the ﬁnal reading was recorded and then
the water was removed from the cell while the soil was still aﬀected by the weight cor-
responding to a vertical eﬀective stress of 9.6 kPa. When the soil sample was dismantled
from the oedometer setup, the ﬁnal height was measured. The soil was not allowed to
swell signiﬁcantly during the dismantling process because the ﬁnal height was used for
calculating the initial void ratio. Therefore, the dismantling process was performed as
fast as possible to decrease the sources of error in the calculation of the void ratio.
Right after the dismantling of the samples, the weight of the saturated samples was
recorded. The surplus water was carefully removed with the ﬁlter paper to obtain the
correct water content at the ﬁnal stage. The water content was used to determine the
initial void ratio. Thus, if the samples were not weighted right after dismantling, the void
ratio would be incorrect which could aﬀect the compression curve.

Chapter 4
Results and Analysis
The tests are performed to classify and obtain intrinsic parameters for clay till. In Sec-
tion 4.1 and Section 4.2, the results obtained in the laboratory are presented. The results
are compared to results from intact clay till samples from Cityringen and literature val-
ues presented in Chapter 2, and the results are also evaluated based on known geological

4.1
Soil Classiﬁcation
Based on the results of the classiﬁcation tests presented in Section 4.1.1 to Section 4.1.3,
the soil types were identiﬁed. The soil samples were classiﬁed according to CEN (2018a),
which treats the identiﬁcation of composite soils because the soil samples are deemed to be
multi-graded based on the coeﬃcient of uniformity and coeﬃcient of curvature presented
in Figure 4.7 and Figure 4.8.
According to Figure 1 in CEN (2018a), the ﬁrst thing to consider is whether the soil is
laid down by natural processes. As described in Chapter 1, the soil samples are deposits
laid down by melting glaciers, and with it, the soil samples are classiﬁed as natural soils
and do not comprise signiﬁcant proportion of organic materials. Thus, the samples are
classiﬁed as mineral soils. Second, it should be considered if the particles larger than
63 mm weigh more than the rest of the soil and whether the soil stick together when
wet and remoulded. The remoulded samples provided by Aarsleﬀhave a maximum grain
size of approximately 25 mm and soil stick together even when it is wet. The electrical
forces between each of the clay particles lead to the cohesive eﬀect. Consequently, the soil
samples can be described as ﬁne composite soil.
As stated in Section 3.3, the soil type classiﬁcation is veriﬁed by applying the guide
to engineering geological soil description written by the Danish Geotechnical Society (in
Danish Dansk Geoteknisk Forening). According to Larsen and Society (1995), the samples

The mean particle size with depth for the two locations, Hillerød and Kongens Lyngby,

deeper soil layers. However, to the best of this author’s knowledge, this is not a general
trend described in any existing literature. Thus, further studies should be made before
any conclusions are drawn.
The variations in the particle size distributions are better portrayed in Figure 4.5 and

particle density result is within the required precision (i.e. 0.021 g/cm3 smaller than 0.031
g/cm3) and the average value is consistent with the literature values given in Table 2.2.

Atterberg Limits
Figure 4.15 and Figure 4.16 show the plastic limit and liquid limit with depth from
Hillerød and Kongens Lyngby, respectively. A liquid limit of 17.1-29.3 % and a plastic
limit of 8.6-13.2 % are measured for the Hillerød samples. By comparison, a liquid limit
of 26.2-35.1 % and a plastic limit of 12.7-18.6 % are measured for the Kongens Lyngby
samples. Thus, the samples are placed above the A-line on the plasticity chart shown in
Figure 2.5.
In Figure 4.17 and Figure 4.18, the plastic limit and liquid limit measured at the two
locations are compared with the measurements from the Cityringen project. In the ﬁgures,
there are scatter in the obtained values for the plasticity limit and liquid limit. However,
trend lines are drawn in the ﬁgures describing the overall trend with depth.
In Figure 4.17, it is seen that the magnitude of the plastic limit decreases with depth
before increasing again. In the SGI report, cf. Larsson (2001), the plastic limit for the
till samples is also decreasing with depth. However, the plastic limit is only measured for
the upper layers and with it, no increase or further decrease is observed. The contrary is
observed for the Dublin boulder clay where the plastic limit is seen to be uniform with
depth (Long and Menkiti, 2007). The samples investigated in Long and Menkiti (2007)
are almost identical in their particle size and moisture content whereas small diﬀerences
are observed in the particle size distribution in Figure 4.9 and the moisture content is
unknown.
In Figure 4.18, it is seen that the liquid limit measured for the Cityringen project is
almost constant close to the surface of the earth and subsequently decreasing with depth.
Whereas the measurements from the laboratory do not show the trend of a constant liquid
limit close to the surface. Thus, the liquid limit is shown to be decreasing with depth.
The liquid limit is decreasing from approximately 35.0 % to 18.0 %. A similar trend is
seen in the SGI report, cf. Larsson (2001), describing the trend for the Swedish clay till.

The intrinsic compression curve should illustrate the envelope that provides a boundary
beyond which a test on reconstituted soil cannot go. However, the intrinsic compression
curve chosen for each location in this thesis disregard a few of the compression curves that
deviates slightly from the general trend, see Figure 4.19 and Figure 4.20. For instance,

From Figure 4.19 and Figure 4.20, it is also seen that the void ratio decreases with
increasing vertical eﬀective stress. This is in agreement with known geological processes
and the description in Mitchell and Soga (2005) which says that changes in the structure
of the saturated soils at vertical eﬀective stress are accompanied by changes in void ratio.
At each location, the soil samples reach approximately the same void ratio at the ﬁnal
stress indicating the asymptotic value of the void ratio. The samples were tested with
varying initial void ratios.
At Hillerød, the ﬁnal void ratio is approximately 0.25 [-]
whereas the value is approximately 0.28 [-] at Kongens Lyngby.
The ﬁnal void ratio
depends on the shape of the grains and the degree of uniformity. Because the soils are
shown to be multi-graded, see Figure 4.8 and Figure 4.7, the soil samples are expected
to obtain a high degree of compaction (low void ratio). According to Terzaghi (1967),
the void ratio for multi-graded glacial till is 0.25 [-] in the natural state. The vertical
eﬀective stress applied in this study corresponds to a thickness of a glacier of hundreds
of meters. Thus, the samples are compacted and they are very dense at their ﬁnal stage.
Glacial soils at their natural state are also described as dense. However, it is not possible
to estimate the eﬀective stress conditions imposed by past glaciations according to Clarke
(2017). Even though, the vertical eﬀective stress of 4905 kPa applied on the samples are
to be somewhat similar to the natural state of the soil.
The void ratio on the compression line is related to the Atterberg limits. Thus, when the
liquid limit increases, the void ratio also increases, cf. Burland (1990). In Figure 4.19
and Figure 4.20, it is seen that the void ratios at 100 kPa and 1000 kPa are higher for the
Kongens Lyngby samples compared to the samples from Hillerød. In Section 4.1.3, it is
shown that the liquid limit is higher at Kongens Lyngby. Thus, the results are consistent
with the theory presented in Burland (1990).

Chapter 5
Conclusion
In this thesis, Danish clay tills from Hillerød and Kongens Lyngby are studied experi-
mentally. In particular, classiﬁcation and compression of selected till samples provided
by Aarsleﬀare investigated.
The thesis aims to identify appropriate soil parameters to be used to predict the location
of the intrinsic compression line for Danish clay tills. The Danish clay till samples are
classiﬁed according to CEN (2018a) and Larsen and Society (1995) where the particle
size distribution and Atterberg limits for each of the samples are applied. The samples
are shown to be similar in their particle size distributions, albeit, the particles are diverse
in their particle size. Thus, the soil samples are multi-graded. Furthermore, the samples
investigated in this thesis are shown to have a clay content on the low side compared to
clay till samples from the Cityringen project and the literature, see Table 2.1. Likewise,
the Atterberg limits are on the low side compared to the literature values, see Table 2.3.
However, the smaller Atterberg limits for the Danish clay till samples are explained by
the low clay content. In general, the Atterberg limits are shown to decrease with depth.
Compression curves for each of the Danish clay till samples are studied and an intrinsic
compression line is obtained for each location. From the compression curves, it is seen that
the void ratio decreases with increasing vertical eﬀective stress. Thus, the compaction of
the particles increases with the increasing vertical eﬀective stress. The ﬁnal void ratios
for the samples are approximately 0.25-0.28 [-]. This corresponds to the void ratio for the
glacial soils at their natural state of 0.25 [-] according to Terzaghi (1967).
The compression index is shown to be decreasing with depth. Correlations between the
intrinsic properties and plasticity indexes are investigated and compared to the literature.
From the relations, it is seen that the compression index is linearly increasing with the
void ratio corresponding to the liquid limit to eL = 1.0 [-] and after, the compression
index reach a steady-state value of approximately 0.24 [-]. When the compression index is
shown as a function of liquid limit no steady-state is obtained, however, the compression

The compression curves are somewhat similar in shape. Thus, the compression curves

5.1
Future Research
In this section, recommendations to improve the reliability and quality of the studies of
this thesis as well as recommendations to future research are presented.
The thesis is based on results from small samples. To get more reliable results larger
samples should be provided and till samples across Denmark should be investigated to
avoid the uncertainties of whether the results are due to local variations. Furthermore, the
intrinsic compression line is found for samples that are similar in particle size distribution
and the plasticity indexes are in the same order of magnitude (low plasticity). By studying
more samples, it can be seen if the ICL presented in this thesis are based on soil samples
that represent the general trends of Danish clay tills. In case variations occur, the intrinsic
compression line shown in Equation (4.3) should be reconsidered.
The intrinsic compression line is a valuable reference line for studying the compression
characteristics of natural sedimentary soils. In this thesis, it is shown that it is possible to
describe Danish clay tills by an intrinsic compression line. Thus, future research can be
related to the ICL, for instance studies on the relation between the sedimentation com-
pression line (SCL) and intrinsic compression line (ICL) can show the overconsolidation
ratio, anisotropy, etc.
Additionally, correlations between the Atterberg limits and intrinsic properties as well as
strength properties are investigated for glacial tills in the literature, see Burland (1990)
and Clarke (2018) among others. This can also be done on Danish clay tills to make the
determination of design parameters easier and to reduce the diﬃculties in construction
and overdesign of foundations. This will require a thoroughly prepared study of several
Danish clay till samples.
