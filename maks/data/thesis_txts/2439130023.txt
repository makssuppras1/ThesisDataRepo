Abstract
An unsteady model is prepared in the Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) software Star-CCM+,
where the wind turbine rotor is modeled through an actuator line model (ALM) which is imple-
mented in the CFD software in the current work. The ALM allows modeling the rotor through
body forces acting along lines. The body forces are the turbine loadings (lift and drag) acting along
the blades, while the lines represent these blades. The inﬂuence of the blade is modelled through
airfoil data, rather than through its geometry, allowing a simpler grid as well as a lower number
of necessary grid points. The ALM is formulated through the introduction in the CFD model of
time-dependent body forces, deﬁned as ﬁeld functions that depend on airfoil data. The body forces
are recognized by the code as components of the Navier-Stokes equations to be solved. The imple-
mentation of the ALM in star-CCM+ is validated by comparing the induction and the loadings to
those of a Blade Element Momentum (BEM) model. The implemented ALM is used to model the
rotor of Suzlon’s S97 wind turbine, under diﬀerent yaw oﬀset conditions. The available power in
the wake obtained with the ALM, is compared to the one obtained with an actuator disc model
(ADM), where the turbine loading is evenly distributed across a permeable disk of zero thickness.
Such comparison was done based on URANS calculations, while an extra LES case was computed
to get a rough idea of the implications of not resolving turbulence. The ADM and ALM comparison
through URANS showed quite similar results between both models, with increasing diﬀerence for
larger yaw miss-alignment angles. The generation of root and tip vorticity was achieved with the
ALM as opposed to with the ADM, therefore larger turbulence at these blade regions was found.
However, it was seen that the vortices dissipate at some distance from the rotor plane. In contrast,
instability of the vortices and turbulence generation could be captured with the LES case, which
leads to suggesting that further calculations are done combining the implemented ALM with a tur-
bulence resolution technique.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Wake steering for wake eﬀects reduction and increase of wind farm power production, is a commonly
discussed topic in the wind energy ﬁeld. The work performed in this thesis addresses CFD calcu-
lations in which wake steering is introduced through yaw oﬀset conditions. The rotor is modeled
using an actuator line model (ALM), which is implemented in the current thesis in the commercial
Navier-Stokes solver Star-CCM+. The results are compared to those using an actuator disc model
(ADM), and the diﬀerence in the power produced by a downstream turbine is computed.
1.1
Motivation
It is common in wind farms, that wind turbines operate within the wakes of each other, partially or
fully. This leads to reduction of the power produced by the turbines operating in the wake of the
upstream turbines, therefore lowering the overall power output of the wind farm. Since it is of higher
interest to maximize the power produced by the wind farm, than the power produced by a single
turbine, several techniques are considered in industry to do so. These techniques aim to reduce
wake eﬀects in the wind farm, by operating an upstream turbine under sub-optimal conditions [25].
This leads to the reduction of power produced by the upstream turbine, but also the reduction of
wake eﬀects. The goal is that the power reduction of the upstream turbine is over compensated by
the power increase of the downstream turbine.
Sub-optimal conditions of the upstream turbine may be non-optimal pitch angle, rotor speed, or yaw
miss-alignment. The latter is considered for the purpose of this thesis, which is done in collabora-
tion between Technical University of Denmark (DTU), Carl Von Ossietzky University of Oldenburg
(UniOl), and Suzlon Blade Science Center in Denmark. The thesis is motivated by investigations
of wake steering through yaw miss-alignment, for wind farm power increase.
CFD computations can be used to quantify the eﬀects of wake steering in a wind farm. The cost of
a CFD computation is largely dependent on factors such as domain size, mesh resolution, and the
complexity of the physics to be resolved. Regarding domain dimension, wind farm models must take
into account not only wake propagation in the stream-wise direction but also turbulent atmospheric
eﬀects, which results in large simulation domains in the order of a few kilometers. Regarding mesh
resolution, the desired degree of turbulence resolution will lead to a thinner or coarser mesh. Also,
1
==PAGE:18==
modelling the geometry of the turbine blades require small grids to solve the airfoil boundary layer.
Finally, regarding the complexity of the modeled physics, diﬀerent techniques exist to model a wind
turbine rotor. To reduce computational time when modelling a wind farm, it is common in industry
to use an actuator disc model (ADM), in which the turbine loadings are distributed along a per-
meable disc of zero thickness [5]. This approach neglects the geometry of the turbine blades, thus
allowing a simpler grid. However, wake dynamics caused by the interaction between the rotation of
the individual blades and the atmospheric conditions are not considered. The actuator line model
(ALM) follows in complexity the ADM. It considers the rotation of the individual blades, as well the
turbine geometry, but not through its full resolution but instead through a blade element method
based on airfoil data [6].
The current work is motivated by the desire to quantify the diﬀerence in the wake produced by
these actuator models, for a turbine operating under yaw oﬀset conditions. The aim is to do so
through a comparison of the power available in the obtained wake. The ADM is a preferred option
in wind farm modeling due to its low computational cost. However, since it neglects important wake
dynamics that aﬀect the power of a downstream turbine, the current work addresses the question
of whether these dynamics can be obtained with the second less expensive option, the ALM.
1.2
Project Scope
The scope of this thesis is divided in 4 main parts, which were performed individually. These are
listed below with a brief summary of the tasks covered in them.
1. Implementation of the ALM in Star-CCM+
This part of the thesis covers the creation of the wind turbine rotor model. On one hand, the
theory behind the ALM for wind turbines was reviewed. On the other hand, diﬀerent tools
available in the commercial CFD software Star-CCM+ were investigated. These tools were
combined with the ALM theory in order to implement the model in Star-CCM+. Sensitivity
analyses of parameters relevant to such implementation were performed. The loadings and
induction computed by the CFD model were validated against an external BEM code. Finally,
the implemented model was adapted to be usable for yaw miss-alignment cases.
2. Creation of CFD Model
This stage covers the creation of a CFD model in which not only the wind turbine rotor is
modeled through the implemented ALM, but also the environment in which the wind turbine
operates, i.e., the ABL. This stage covers the selection of the domain size, mesh, boundary
conditions, and modeling of the atmospheric wind shear and turbulence.
3. ALM vs. ADM for the S97 Wind Turbine
The created wind farm model was used to compute the wake behind a Suzlon wind turbine,
the S97. The results obtained with the implemented ALM were compared to those obtained
with a previously commercially implemented ADM in Star-CCM+. The available wind power

Background Theory
This section covers a general overview the background theory necessary to understand the work
performed in the thesis. The reader should understand about wind turbine aerodynamics, wind
turbine modeling using Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD), and the used CFD Navier-Stokes
software: Star-CCM+. Since this theory is extensive and most of it is already explained in detail
in recognized literature, only brief summaries are made of it, and citations are used repeatedly to
refer to the relevant documentation.
2.1
Basics of Wind Turbine Aerodynamics
A wind turbine uses the kinetic energy available in the wind to produce mechanical power in the
form of a rotating shaft, which will then be converted into electrical energy through a generator.
There are diﬀerent theories used to understand the process through which the aerodynamic power
available in the wind is transformed into mechanical power.
One-Dimensional Momentum Theory
The simplest of these theories is the one-dimensional (1-D) momentum theory for an ideal wind
turbine [1], which considers a friction-less disc that alters the wind speed slowing it down from the
free-stream velocity V0 to the velocity in the rotor plane u, and then to the velocity in the wind
turbine wake u1. This is sketched in Figure 2.1, where x represents the axial direction, point 1
corresponds to the upstream free-stream ﬂow, point 2 to the rotor plane, and point 3 to the wake
ﬂow. At point 2, the air velocity is reduced with respect to point 1 since its kinetic energy has been
extracted by the disc. At this point there is a sudden pressure drop ∆P across the rotor plane.
From here until point 3, the pressure recovers until atmospheric pressure P0. Due to conservation
of energy, as the pressure rises the velocity drops. In the ﬁgure, the light blue line represents the
expansion of the stream-tube. The expansion takes place due to mass conservation between points
2 and 3: ρuA = ρu1A1, where ρ is the air density, A is the rotor area and A1 is the cross-section
area of the stream-tube at the wake. Since the velocity drops from points 2 to 3, and the density is
constant for an incompressible ﬂow, the area must expand.

2.2
Basics of the ABL
The Atmospheric Boundary Layer (ABL) corresponds to the lowest part of the atmosphere, which
is directly aﬀected by the presence of the surface, including buildings, vegetation, elevation changes,
etc. Its depth may vary between a few hundred meters up until 2km. Above the ABL, the free
atmosphere is found, where the eﬀect of the ground is considered negligible. At this highest level
of the atmosphere the wind is almost geostrophic, which is the name given to the condition in
which the Coreolis force and the pressure gradients are in balance. Figure 2.3 shows a sketch of the
mentioned layers.
Figure 2.3: Sketch of atmospheric boundary layer and free atmosphere.
8
==PAGE:25==
In this study, the interest lies in the ABL, since this is the atmospheric layer in which most turbine
rotors are expected to be found. This is of course a very harsh generalization, considering on one
side that new technologies are focused on larger wind turbines that might extend out of the ABL,
and on the other hand, considering that the depth of the ABL is not constant, since it depends on
thermal eﬀects. Regarding the ﬁrst of these aspects, in this study it is dealt with small wind turbines
with hub heights below 100m, that extend until less than 150m at tip height. It is assumed that
they lie within the ABL. Regarding the second aspect, there are 3 main states deﬁning the condition
of the ABL in terms of thermal eﬀects: unstable, stable, and neutral stratiﬁcation conditions. An
unstable stratiﬁcation condition corresponds to a high convection degree between the ground and
the atmosphere, which occurs usually at day-time, when strong heating of the atmosphere from the
ground is converted into kinetic energy energy, enhancing strong vertical turbulent mixing. A stable
stratiﬁcation condition corresponds to a low degree of convection, usually seen during night-time,
when the colder atmosphere restricts turbulence generation. The width of the ABL is larger for
unstable than for stable stratiﬁcation conditions. Lastly, neutral stratiﬁcation conditions are said
to be encountered when thermal eﬀects are irrelevant compared to mechanical eﬀects, in terms of
turbulence generation [32].
The ABL, contrary to the free atmosphere, is said to be turbulent. In it, there are 2 main ways
in which turbulence is generated: thermally and mechanically. Thermally generated turbulence is
the one described in the previous paragraph, while mechanically generated turbulence is caused by
the vertical momentum exchange that occurs due to the presence of wind shear. The wind shear is
the vertical velocity gradient caused by the surface drag, which forces the velocity to be reduced to
zero at the ground. In the present thesis, only the latter form of turbulence generation is taken into
account, meaning thermal eﬀects are neglected, thus neutral stratiﬁcation conditions are considered.
In summary, the simulations in this work consider a neutrally stratiﬁed ABL, over uniformly rough
ﬂat terrain, and where horizontal homogeneity governs outside of the wake. Horizontal homogeneity
implies that the vertical proﬁle of the mean wind speed and the turbulence quantities are constant
in the stream-wise direction.
2.3
Wind Turbine Modeling using CFD
This section covers an brief overview of diﬀerent approaches used in the wind energy ﬁeld to model
a wind turbine. These approaches concern techniques used to model the turbine rotor, and to model
two of the phenomena within the atmospheric boundary layer that aﬀect wind turbine performance:
wind shear and turbulence.
2.3.1
Rotor Modeling
Several approaches may be used to model the rotor of a wind turbine in CFD. One of these ap-
proaches consists in doing full resolution calculations where the wind turbine blade geometry is
fully modelled, allowing to resolve the viscous airfoil boundary layer. The approach used to model
the turbine rotor may depend on whether the investigation is focused on the loadings, on the near
wake, or on the far wake. The near wake is considered to be the region behind the turbine rotor,
9
==PAGE:26==
in which the wake is highly inﬂuenced by the presence of the rotor. This is usually just a few
diameters downstream from the rotor plane. The far wake, in contrast, corresponds to the region
in which the wake is mostly characterized by small scale turbulence, while the presence of the rotor
is negligible. In studies where the main interest lies on the turbine loads, or in quantifying the near
wake, research has shown that full resolution of the rotor might be necessary to obtain accurate
results [11]. However, when the main interest lies in quantifying the far wake, other approaches
which simplify the rotor modeling technique can be used [7] [8] [9] [10] [11].
Specially for the simulation of wind farms where more than one wind turbine is considered, tech-
niques to reduce the computational cost are appreciated. Many studies have their main interest in
the development of the wake far from the rotor, and in the interaction of wakes of neighbouring
turbines, since these factors play an important role in the performance of the downstream turbines.
For these studies, the high computational cost that accompanies fully resolved calculations of the
wind turbine geometry is not justiﬁed. The necessity to reduce computational cost for such studies
has given rise to the use of the actuator disc model (ADM) and the actuator line model (ALM) in
wind energy.
The Actuator Disc Model
The actuator disc model introduced by Froude [4], who continued the work of [5], consists in repre-
senting a rotor through a permeable disc of zero thickness, and an area equivalent to the rotor area.
The blade loadings are distributed on the disc, allowing a simpliﬁed replication of the eﬀect caused
in the ﬂow by a real rotor. The basis of this method lies in the conservation of mass and balance
of momentum, thus this method allows accounting for the velocity reduction caused in the ﬂow by
the blade forces of a wind turbine rotor. Mikkelsen [2] has carried out an extensive set of studies in
which he applies both the ADM and ALM to wind turbines, for complex cases where rotor coning,
tower eﬀect, yawed inﬂow, wind shear, dynamic stall, or blade elastic deﬂection are considered. One
of the main shortenings of the ADM regards its inability to represent individual blade tip and root
vortices, which are essential for near wake characterization. Also, unsteady behaviour caused by
the interaction of the vortex system and the atmospheric turbulence can not captured. This leads
to the next actuator model.
The Actuator Line Model
The actuator disc model is followed in complexity by the actuator line model, introduced by Sorensen
and Shen [6]. In this method the rotor loadings are distributed along lines representing the blades.
Diﬀerently from the actuator disc model, this model takes into account the azimuth variation of the
applied loadings, which in the actuator disc approach are circumferentially averaged.
The method combines a 3-D Navier Stokes solver with the application of the distributed forces,
and it provided good agreement with the power curve of the 500kW Nordtank wind turbine in
the studies carried out by Sorensen and Shen in [6]. In that work, the actuator line model was
formulated in vorticity-velocity variables, while in [2] Mikkelsen combines the method with the 3-D
Navier-Stokes solver EllipSys3D, formulated in pressure-velocity variables. In his work, as recently
10
==PAGE:27==
mentioned, he extends the study performed by Sorensen and Shen, to more complex rotor conditions.
Also Troldborg [3], carried out an extensive study where he uses EllipSys3D in combination with the
actuator line technique to predict the wake ﬂow of wind turbine rotors under diverse ﬂow conditions.
In the present work, the actuator line model is implemented in the commercial 3-D Navier Stokes
solver Star-CCM+. Such formulation is addressed in Chapter 3.2.
2.3.2
ABL Modeling
It is well known that natural phenomena present in the atmosphere aﬀect the performance of wind
turbines, since the power produced by a turbine is directly related to its inﬂow wind. This is why
ﬁnding a way to model the atmospheric conditions aﬀecting wind turbine performance such as the
wind shear and the generation of turbulence structures is important. The power produced by a
wind turbine undergoing uniform and laminar inﬂow conditions will diﬀer from the power produced
by a wind turbine subject to sheared and turbulent inﬂow conditions [3].
Turbulence Modeling
In a wind farm, turbulent structures of diﬀerences sizes are available from diﬀerent sources. There
is turbulence generated from the vortex system shed by the turbine, from the local wind shear at
the ground, from thermal eﬀects due to atmospheric instability, and also from larger atmospheric
motions.
The mentioned sources of turbulence can be addressed in a CFD model in diﬀerent ways. One
common approach is to model all turbulent structures using a Reynolds Averaged Navier Stokes
(RANS) solver in combination with an eddy viscosity closure like k −ϵ or k −ω. This approach is
quite appealing because it has a low computational cost. However, for wind farms, it comes with
shortenings since it may dissipate interesting phenomena such as the vorticity sheet shed by the
root and tip of the blades, as well as the interaction of the vortex system with the atmospheric
turbulence. A more interesting approach is to use Large Eddy Simulation (LES), which addresses
the turbulence process and resolves large-scale turbulent motions, while only modeling the smallest
eddies [31]. It is common practice in wind farm CFD computations to prepare LES models in com-
bination with a turbulence box that replicates the atmospheric turbulent motions. A common way
of applying the turbulence, is to super-impose the velocity ﬂuctuations on the inlet mean velocity
[26]. Troldborg, however, has proposed doing so at a plane just upstream of the rotor, instead of
at the inlet [3]. In any case, this approach seems to replicate quite well the physics that take place
inside a wind farm. One way to pre-generate the atmospheric turbulence is using the Mann method
[27], [28], as done by Troldborg [3] and Mikkelsen [2] in previous investigations using the ALM.

3.2
ALM Implementation in Star-CCM+
As explained in Section 2.3.1, in the ALM the wind turbine loading is distributed along lines repre-
senting the blades. The loading is introduced in the ﬂow solver as a momentum source in the ﬂow,
which is solved as the body force term in the equation of momentum conservation, Eq. 2.21.
The ﬁrst challenge to overcome when implementing the ALM, is the application of the momentum
source at the relevant location, i.e. at the location of the turbine blades. To do so, the momentum
source was deﬁned as a ﬁeld function equal to zero in the absence of a turbine blade, and equal to
the desired loading at the locations coincident with a blade. Since the turbine blades are rotating,
the ﬁeld function was deﬁned on a rotating coordinate system, such that the location of the applied
momentum source is constant in time relative to this coordinate system.
3.2.1
Computation of Turbine Loading
In the present work, 2 independent approaches are considered for the computation of the turbine
loading. In the ﬁrst approach, the loading is computed through an external BEM code, while in the
second approach the loading is computed inside the CFD model, through an internal implementa-
tion of a blade element method.
It could be said that approach 1 represents a simpler version of approach 2 in terms of implementa-
tion. However, it is also quite limited since it depends on the loads produced by an external BEM
code. In this thesis, approach 1 was used to validate against the BEM code the induction of the
ALM, under the eﬀect of known loading. It was also used to make a sensitivity analysis of the
resulting wake ﬂow under diﬀerent conditions. Approach 2 was then validated against approach 1.

With approach 1, the turbine loading is externally computed through a BEM code. The BEM
code is run for diﬀerent tip-speed ratios λ, by ﬁxing the rotating speed and varying the inﬂow wind
speed. The resulting loadings are then tabulated as a function of the inﬂow velocity and radial
position, as shown in the skecth given in Figure 3.8, where tables go from the ﬁrst until the last

3.2.5
Sensitivity Analysis 3: Computational Time Reduction
As previously explained, the implementation of the ALM was achieved through the inclusion of a
momentum source acting in diﬀerent positions of the ﬂow ﬁeld. In the implemented model, the
momentum source was deﬁned through a ﬁeld function that ﬁnds the location of the actuator lines
and applies the turbine loading along them. Since the lines represent rotating blades, the location
of the actuator line points is variable seen from a ﬁxed coordinate system. In the ﬁnal implemented
model, the rotation of the lines was addressed through a rotating coordinate system centered in the
rotor center. This allowed probing the velocity components and applying the turbine loadings at
ﬁxed positions seen from the rotating coordinate system.
This section covers diﬀerent methods tested before reaching the ﬁnal ALM, with the aim of reducing
the computational cost of the model. These methods concern the probing of the velocities and the
computation of the loadings per blade segment. Finally, an overview of the time reduction achieved
through the progressive modiﬁcation of the methods is given. See Figure 3.20 for this. This sensi-
tivity analysis unlike the previous 2, was carried out for approach 2. The inﬂow conditions however,
are the same as for the other sensitivity analyses.
Field Functions vs. Expression Reports
In some situations, it is common practice in Star-CCM+ to make use of ﬁeld functions, to deﬁne
parameters that are not necessarily depending on the ﬁeld position. The reason behind this, is that
28
==PAGE:45==
the deﬁnition of parameters does not allow accessing information obtained through reports, such
as the velocity at a certain point. Deﬁning parameters as ﬁeld functions may or may not aﬀect
signiﬁcantly the computational time. This depends on the amount of parameters deﬁned this way,
on the frequency with which they are updated, and specially on the number of mesh cells, since they
are computed at each cell of the domain. In the implementation of the actuator model, the loadings
pN and pT at the diﬀerent segments of the blade are considered parameters since they are computed
locally based on the velocity probed at the segment of interest. In this particular case, 17 pN values
are considered per blade 1, leading to a total of 51 values. The same is true for pT , meaning than in
total 102 values deﬁne the loadings. Considering that the mesh has a number of cells in the order of
millions, there is a huge diﬀerence between computing 102 values vs. 102 times the number of cells,
which is the case when using a ﬁeld function to deﬁne each parameter. Therefore avoiding the use of
ﬁeld functions to compute the loadings is highly recommended. In a recent version of Star-CCM+,
the option to create expression reports was released. These allow deﬁning equations as expressions
that access other reports, and like parameters, they are independent of the ﬁeld position. Replacing
the use of ﬁeld functions by expression reports led to signiﬁcant time reduction.
Fixed vs. Rotating Coordinate System
The positions of the actuator line points are constantly changing due to the rotation of the turbine.
In the ﬁnal implementation of the ALM, a rotating coordinate system is used so that these positions
are time independent, avoiding the need to recalculate them at each simulation time. However, at
an earlier stage it was aimed to use a ﬁxed coordinate system. Using either a ﬁxed or a rotating
coordinate system aﬀects the method in which the deﬁnition of the actuator line points is made,
and also the way in which the velocities are probed. This is explained below.
• Methods to Probe Velocity
Due to the rotation of the turbine, the points at which the velocities are probed are also
time dependent. Probing in Star-CCM+ is commonly done through the use of derived parts
such as points, spheres, sections, among others, over which a desired quantity is extracted
through a report. In this case, the velocity was extracted as a mass ﬂow average over a sphere
surrounding each actuator line point, as sketched in Figure 3.19 [30].

Although the positions of the actuator line points are already deﬁned through parameters
(constant for the rotating coordinate system and time dependent for the ﬁxed one), the posi-

limitation for the ﬁxed coordinate system case since the time varying position of the derived

of such part at each simulation time. This option was discarded since it involved extensive
external extra calculations.
A diﬀerent option was considered for the ﬁxed coordinate system case, which is the collocated
ﬁeld function report feature in Star-CCM+. This feature considers 2 functions, which can be
called f1 and f2. The feature computes the value of f2 when f1 meets a certain condition,
e.g., the value of the velocity magnitude where the pressure is minimum. For the ALM imple-
mentation, this was used to ﬁnd the actuator line point position, by looking for the velocity
(f2) where the distance (f1) between the cell position and the actuator line point position was
minimum. In this sense, the expression report makes use of the parameters deﬁning the time
dependent coordinates of the actuator line points, to ﬁnd the velocity value. This operation
however also represented a high computational cost. The rotating coordinate system was a
much better option since a total of 51 spheres are created on it at the beginning of the simu-
lation, and no time dependency has to be further considered.
• Velocity Probed to Compute Tangential Loading
It is recalled that in order to compute the tangential loading pN, the tangential velocity VT
is required. For a ﬁxed coordinate system, the velocity components are probed in Cartesian0,
and then transformed to the tangential direction of each blade. This requires knowing for a
given time, to which quadrant each blade belongs to in order to do a proper transformation
and obtain the correct tangential velocity both in magnitude and direction. The conditioning
for diﬀerent quadrants is done per blade through an if-statement based on the angular position
of the blade φblade, thus based on the time t since φblade = Ωt. This operation however, showed
to take important computational time which seemed to be reduced when it was excluded.

Introduction of Yaw Oﬀset
The yaw oﬀset was introduced in the model using relative coordinate systems. As previously men-
tioned, a ﬁxed coordinate system, Cartesian0, is placed at the center of the rotor plane, with x
perpendicular to the inlet. The previously mentioned coordinate systems Cartesian1, Cartesian2
and Cartesian3, are centered at the same location, but with x direction subject to the desired yaw
oﬀset. The following sketch illustrates this for Cartesian0 and Cartesian2.

Figure 3.24: Sketch of Yaw Oﬀset using Cartesian0 and Cartesian2.
It is recalled that Cartesian2 is aligned in z with blade 2, thus the tangential direction for blade 2
coincides with y in Cartesian2. The same is true for Cartesian 1 with blade 1, and for Cartesian
3 with blade 3. For all 3 coordinate systems the x direction is the same, therefore when including
any yaw oﬀset, the 3 coordinate systems rotate about the z axis of Cartesian0. The yaw is positive

relative to the yawed coordinate system.
3.3
ABL Modeling for further Calculations
At industry, for wind farm simulations, it is common to model the turbine rotor with an ADM, in
combination with a k −ω eddy viscosity closure to model turbulence. Since one of the main goals
of the current thesis is to quantify the diﬀerence in the results when using the ALM as opposed to
the ADM in Star-CCM+, the turbulence modeling approach used in the latter was also applied to
the former.
It is also interesting to see the diﬀerence between using URANS and LES when modeling the rotor
with the ALM, since it is expected that the tip and root vortices develop diﬀerently when turbulence
is resolved instead of only modeled. For this reason, a single case was run using both techniques.
Some limitations were however encountered for the LES case, which are addressed in more detail in
the results section of that matter.

Chapter 4
ALM Calculations for S97 Wind Turbine
The ﬁnal version of the ALM (approach 2) was used to run simulations of the Suzlon S97 wind
turbine. Most data of this wind turbine is conﬁdential, therefore airfoil data is kept unmentioned.
The turbine has a rotor radius of 48.7m and hub height of 90m. The calculations were performed
for a sheared inﬂow, and the atmospheric turbulence was modeled using URANS and k −ω. The
deﬁnition of k and omega for the inlet and initial conditions was deﬁned according to the explana-
tion given in section 3.3, from where it is recalled that I = 12%. The velocity at hub height of the
sheared inﬂow corresponds to 8.011m/s. The turbine is set to rotate at 13.93rpm, corresponding
to λ = 8.87. This point was chosen as it is close to the design operating point of the turbine. As
explained in section 3.2.4, twice δtmax,C1 was used as time-step, which for the S97 at the given
rotating speed corresponds to ∆t = 2x0.03s = 0.06s.
Simulation Time
The simulations were run for a total physical time of 4min, which corresponds to a time when the
free-stream axial velocity at hub height has travelled 19D. The choice for the total simulation time
was based on the desire to have the wake transported downstream an axial distance equal to the
extension of the ﬁnest mesh region, thus 8D. The BEM results were used as a rough guideline in
this selection. From the induction computed in the BEM, it is not expected that the wake velocity
reaches a value below 5m/s. Therefore the time required for a ﬂow parcel with 5m/s velocity to
travel 8D was computed. An extra 20s were added to account for 10s used at the beginning of the
simulation to initialize the forces. Finally, the time taken for 4 extra rotor revolutions to be made was
also added, to compute time averaged data within this time frame and compare to the steady ADM.
Prepared Cases

creased from 0o to 30o in steps of 10o, and also a case for −20o was prepared. The current section
presents ﬁrst the results obtained with the ALM for the turbine under no yaw oﬀset. Then, the
results obtained introducing yaw oﬀset are shown.

The next observation to be done about Figure 4.15 regards the asymmetric nature of the wake
even for the case with no yaw oﬀset. This can be explained as a consequence of the wake rotation
combined with the wind shear, which causes low velocity air parcels from the ground to move up-

wards on one side of the turbine, while on the other side of the turbine high velocity air parcels are
transported toward the ground. This is also explained by Troldborg in [3]. The direction in which
this happens follows the direction of the wake rotation. In this case, the low velocity air parcels are
transported upwards on the left side, while the high velocity air parcels are transported downwards
on the right side. This causes the wake to have the lowest velocities on the former side, thus moving
its center to this side.

Chapter 5
ALM vs. ADM for S97 Wind Turbine
This section covers the comparison between the results obtained with the ALM and the ADM. The
comparison is done in terms of velocity and turbulence proﬁles, contour plots, wake center, and the
evaluation of available wind power in the wake, usable for power production of a second turbine.
It is recalled that no new implementation of an ADM was done in this thesis. The ADM calcula-
tions were performed using the commercial 1D virtual disc tool available in Star-CCM+. Technical
information about this implementation can be found in the software’s documentation [33]. It is
however relevant to point out that the used model applies a constant loading across the rotor area,
which is computed from a prescribed CT and P. The ADM calculations were performed for similar
inﬂow, turbulence and operating conditions than those of the ALM.
5.1
Results for 1 Wind Turbine
Figures 5.1 to 5.2 show the Vx/V0 proﬁle at diﬀerent positions downstream to the rotor plane, along
a line on a horizontal plane passing through the rotor center. Each ﬁgure corresponds to one of the
tested yaw oﬀset cases, and in them, the velocity proﬁles for both actuator models are given. The
ﬁrst disagreement between the models lies in the induction caused by the rotor. It can be seen for
all the yaw miss-alignment cases that the ALM produced a higher induction in the ﬂow than the
ADM. This is specially seen at the near wake. However, this diﬀerence becomes less noticeable as
it is moved further downstream in the wake.
The next observation to be made regards the shape of the velocity proﬁle in the near wake at 1R.
As expected, in the near wake the velocity proﬁles of the ALM show a double wake while the ADM
shows a single wake. This can be explained by a combination of factors. In the ADM the loadings
are distributed over a full disc that covers not only the swept area of the blades, but also the area
covered by the hub. As a result the disc produces a blockage over a full circular region, returning a
single Gaussian-like velocity proﬁle behind the rotor. In the ALM the loadings are distributed along
lines that start at the location of the blade’s root, thus at a radial position larger than 0m. The hub
is not modeled, which means that at the rotor center there is no blockage at all. The lack of any
blockage at the rotor center will evidently not result in a single Gaussian-like velocity proﬁle like
the one obtained through the ADM. However, the double proﬁle in the near wake is even present
51
==PAGE:68==
in wake measurements behind a real rotor. This is because in the near wake the velocity owes its
reduction to the lift generated along the blades. As the ﬂow is transported downstream, turbulence
causes the vortices shed by the tip of the blades to break, merging the double wake into a single
one. It would therefore be expected that including the hub in the ALM, still results in a double
Gaussian-like proﬁle at the near wake, which can not be obtained with the ADM unless a whole in
its center is considered.

5.2
Results for 2 Wind Turbines
For all the considered yaw miss-alignment cases, a second wind turbine was placed downstream to
the existing turbine, at a distance equal to 5D. There is no yaw miss-alignment for the second wind
turbine. The main goal of this section is to compute the power of a turbine placed in the wake of
the ﬁrst turbine, and to quantify the diﬀerence in doing so using an ALM and an ADM.
Since the results for the computation of a single wind turbine were available as part of the ﬁrst goals
of the project, these were used to initialize the computations including 2 turbines. This was done
with the aim of saving the computational time needed for the wake of the ﬁrst turbine to reach the
second one.
It is expected that the second turbine faces a lower V0 than the ﬁrst one, therefore Ωwas set lower
for the second turbine than for the ﬁrst one. This was done to avoid operating at an unrealistically
high λ. Since the inﬂow velocity for the second turbine is part of the simulation results, a speciﬁc
λ can’t be speciﬁed beforehand. This is assuming that the simulation has not been previously ran
only for the ﬁrst turbine, which is usually the case when modeling wind farms where all the wind
turbines are placed in the initial model. Ideally, there should be a controller that sets Ωfor the
second turbine as a function of the desired λ. This was however not covered in the scope of the
project. The rotating speed of the second wind turbine was set to Ω= 11.8rpm, which corresponds
to the lowest operating Ωof the S97 wind turbine.

downstream turbine, in the cases where the upstream turbine operates with a yaw oﬀset diﬀerent

however it can be hardly appreciated in this ﬁgure for the other cases.

Chapter 6
URANS vs. LES for ALM Calculations
LES calculations were performed for 0o yaw miss-alignment, under the same inﬂow and operating
conditions used in the URANS case. The calculation of l and I for the initial and boundary condi-
tions was done according to the explanation given in section 3.3, having I = 12% (as in the URANS
case). The main objective of the LES calculations was to gain a rough idea of phenomena that may
not be reﬂected in the previous results due to the turbulence modeling, thus to the dissipation of
the turbulent eddies.
It is important to mention that an extensive analysis of the LES results was not carried out in the
scope of this thesis. Mainly Vx and vorticity contours of the results obtained using both turbulence
modeling approaches are compared. The only modiﬁcation done from the URANS to the LES cases,
was the selection of this turbulence modeling approach. The domain size, mesh and boundary con-
ditions was kept unchanged, which limits the validity of the obtained results. For a proper LES
simulation a study should be carried out to carefully deﬁne the mesh resolution at the walls, in this
case at the ground. Also, the results showed signiﬁcant reﬂection from the boundaries, meaning
that the domain size is not large enough to resolve the large turbulence structures present in the
ﬂow. This is shown in Figure 6.2, where the left side shows the velocity ﬁeld at 3 perpendicular
planes aligned with the 3 axes of Cartesian0, and the right side shows the velocity ﬁeld at hub height.

Chapter 7
Conclusions and Future Work
In this research project, an Actuator Line Model (ALM) has been implemented in the commercial
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) software Star-CCM+. Two diﬀerent models have been for-
mulated which diﬀer in the approach used to obtain the wind turbine loadings; approach 1 imports
the loadings from an external BEM code, while approach 2 computes the loadings internally through
a blade element method. The induction produced by the CFD solver with the applied loadings from
approaches 1 and 2 has been validated against the external BEM model.
Approach 2 has been used to perform calculations on the S97 turbine from Suzlon, at an operating
condition close to design point. The simulations were done for a sheared and turbulent inﬂow. The
wind shear was modeled using the logarithmic law as inlet condition and rough terrain to avoid its
dissipation in the stream-wise direction. A turbulent proﬁle was speciﬁed at the inlet with turbu-
lence intensity I = 12% at hub height, and Unsteady Reynolds Averaged Navier-Stokes (URANS)
together with a k −ω Eddy viscosity closure was used to model turbulence. The simulations were
carried out for diﬀerent yaw oﬀset cases using the implemented ALM, and the results were compared
to those of a commercially implemented Actuator Disc Model (ADM) in Star-CCM+, in which a
constant thrust and power is assumed across the disc.
In all tested cases, the ALM produced a higher power and thrust than the ADM. For zero yaw
oﬀset, the thrust and power coeﬃcients, CT and CP respectively, in the ALM are 9% and 2% higher
than in the ADM. This diﬀerence is higher for increasing yaw oﬀset. The diﬀerence in CT and CP
led to a higher induction in the wake for the ALM. The velocity proﬁles of the models showed a
larger diﬀerence in the near wake, which dissipates as it is advanced further in the wake. Regarding
turbulence, the turbulent kinetic energy k is larger for the ALM than for the ADM, specially near
the root and tip of the blades. This is as expected due to the individual rotation of the blades
considered in the ALM, which produced tip and root vortex shedding. The diﬀerence in turbulence
is also larger in the near wake than in the far wake, which makes sense since at this region the wake
is highly inﬂuenced by the presence of the 2 diﬀerent rotors. The wake from the ALM showed a
faster recovery rate than that of the ADM, which can be explained by its higher level of turbulence,
enhancing more momentum mixing between the wake and the free-stream ﬂow than the ADM. The
faster recovery rate explains why the diﬀerence in the velocity proﬁles of both models is reduced in
the far wake. It also explains partly why the diﬀerence in k also drops in the far wake.
74
==PAGE:91==
The available wind power Pwake at the wake of both models was compared. Lower values were found
for the ALM due to the larger induction produced by it. The diﬀerence in recovery rate between
the models lead to variable power diﬀerences between them depending on the downstream position
from the rotor plane. The diﬀerences are higher in the near wake than in the far wake. This holds
for all tested yaw miss-alignment cases, however the diﬀerence between the models rises as the yaw
oﬀset is increased. In the case of 30o yaw miss-alignment, the diﬀerence goes from 40% to 10%,
from 4R to 16R respectively. In the case of 0o yaw miss-alignment, it goes from 25% to 2%.
For both actuator models, as expected, when introducing yaw miss-alignment the mechanical power
and thrust of the turbine were reduced, but the available power in the wake was increased. The
power produced by a turbine 5D downstream to the existing turbine was computed for both actu-
ator models. The thrust produced by the second turbine is larger in the ADM than in the ALM,
but mainly because it has a higher inﬂow velocity. The higher inﬂow can be attributed to the lower
thrust and power of the upstream turbine with respect to the ALM, but also to the lower turbulence
produced by the ADM. Based on the former reason, it is concluded that the power produced by
the downstream turbines are not entirely comparable, unless the upstream turbine in both models
extracts the same energy from the ﬂow. It is therefore suggested to re-run the ADM calculations
using CT and CP from the ALM, and not from the BEM, and quantify the diﬀerences seen for the
second turbine.
For zero yaw oﬀset a slight diﬀerence was seen between the horizontal wake center wcy of the models.
This diﬀerence is magniﬁed as the yaw oﬀset is increased. It is believed that such diﬀerence is inﬂu-
enced by the action of the rotation on the wind shear, which causes low velocity air parcels to move
upwards on one side of the turbine and high velocity ones to move downwards on the other side. This
motion is governed by constant loadings in the ADM, while in the ALM these loadings are time and
space dependent. The vertical wake center wcz showed no signiﬁcant diﬀerence between the models.
Rough LES calculations were performed for the non-yawed rotor and compared to the URANS
results. LES results showed a Kelvin-Helmholz instability at the tip vortices that is not captured
with URANS. This instability causes the smooth tip vorticity sheet to break into smaller vortex
structures. In URANS the vorticity sheet simply dissipates. It was also seen that the near wake
double velocity proﬁle merges into a single wake at diﬀerent downstream positions in both models,
happening closer to the rotor for URANS than for LES.
Other than the diﬀerent thrust and power produced by the ALM compared to the ADM, the down-
stream development of the wake in both cases is quite similar for both models when using URANS.
Due to time limitations deﬁned by the duration of the thesis, the performed LES calculations for
the ALM did not meet good practice standards in terms of domain size and mesh resolution. It
is recommended to use the implemented ALM in a properly prepared LES or DES model, and to
compare these results to those of the ALM with URANS, and to those of the ADM. One of the
most interesting phenomena to see from the ALM, is the development of the tip vortices in the far
wake and their eﬀect in a downstream turbine. Since it was seen that this is not captured through
URANS, the use of LES or DES is highly encouraged. The inclusion of a turbulence box was also
left out of the scope of the thesis, and should most deﬁnitely be considered in order to model the
diﬀerent length scales of the atmospheric turbulence. Finally, since the ADM does not compute
75
==PAGE:92==
thrust and power itself, for fair comparison of the actual model with the ALM, equal thrust and
power values should be imposed in it. Thus it is recommended, as previously said, to used the
output thrust and power obtained in the ALM as input for the ADM, and with such conditions
perform the LES or DES computations.
