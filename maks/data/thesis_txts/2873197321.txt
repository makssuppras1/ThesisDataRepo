Abstract
This thesis investigates the performance of natural ventilation strategies in residential build-
ings, with a particular focus on ventilation, thermal comfort, and the role of smart automa-
tion in improving indoor conditions and energy efficiency. The study combines theoretical
calculations, measurements, and dynamic simulations conducted in IDA ICE to assess a
range of natural ventilation scenarios, including single-sided ventilation, cross ventilation,
and stack effect ventilation.
A detailed case study of a detached single-family house in Copenhagen served as the ex-
perimental and simulation base. Ventilation performance was evaluated through tracer gas
decay measurements and comfort probes, while simulations were validated through theoreti-
cal airflow models and compared against measured data. The results highlight the influence
of opening configuration, wind direction, and seasonality on the ventilation performance.
Stack effect and cross ventilation consistently outperformed single-sided configurations, of-
fering ACH values up to twice as high, particularly during winter when buoyancy effects are
more pronounced. Flap-based ventilation resulted in the lowest ACH, which does not fulfill
the minimum requirement for adequate ventilation, suggesting the integration of a hybrid
solution to address the problem.
Further, smart automation strategies, such as adaptive heating and cooling controls and
dynamic window operation, were implemented in IDA ICE. These strategies demonstrated
potential reductions in overheating hours and energy consumption, particularly under future
climate projections for 2080. Comparative economic analyses of various heating systems are
conducted to evaluate which options may prove more cost-effective in the future. Surpris-
ingly, electric radiators could emerge as the more financially advantageous choice under
specific and realistic conditions, even when compared to more sophisticated and efficient
systems like heat pumps combined with underfloor heating.
Overall, the findings suggest that combining natural ventilation principles with smart au-
tomation enables residential buildings to maintain high thermal comfort while minimizing
energy demand, and that the shortening of the heating season in the future might make
economically viable heating solutions that nowadays are considered not really effective.

2.1
Introduction
In developed countries, people spend over 85-90% of their time indoors, predominantly at
home. This extensive indoor time necessitates effective ventilation to minimize health risks
from airborne pollutants. The correlation between ventilation rates and health has been
widely studied, suggesting that inadequate ventilation (< 0,4 (h−1) ACH ) may elevate
health risks [1].
What’s more, the building sector plays a crucial role in energy consumption and green-
house gas (GHG) emissions. According to the European Commission, buildings account for
around 40% of total energy use in Europe and contribute to approximately one-third of GHG
emissions [2]. Table 1 provides further statistics [3].

HVAC systems alone represent about 12% of final energy use globally, and up to 25% in
affluent or colder regions like the EU or Russia [3]. To address these sustainability concerns,
the European Union introduced Directive (EU/2024/1275) in May 2024, aiming to enhance
building efficiency and reduce emissions. This directive mandates that all new residential
and non-residential buildings be zero-emission by January 1, 2028, for public-owned build-
ings, and by January 1, 2030, for all others, with some exceptions [4].
Thus, ventilation systems play a pivotal role in achieving both energy efficiency and healthy
indoor air quality (IAQ). Natural ventilation, driven by climatic factors like temperature
differences and wind pressure, is a widely used approach.
The main objective of this study is to explore and evaluate natural ventilation strategies in
residential buildings to improve thermal comfort and reduce energy use. By focusing on al-
ternatives such as stack-effect ventilation, cross-ventilation, and the controlled use of façade
and roof windows, the aim is to assess the impact of air velocity and air change rates on
indoor conditions, as well as the influence of automation strategies, such as smart controls
of windows and shading devices, on thermal comfort and energy consumption.
The presentation of this work starts with a literature review, which aims to outline the
current state of research on natural ventilation strategies and thermal comfort in residential

2.2
Literature review
2.2.1
Performance of natural ventilation strategies in residential buildings
Several studies have assessed benefits of natural ventilation strategies both in terms of ther-
mal comfort and outdoor air exchange. Among the various strategies, single-sided ventilation
is often the most basic form and is typically used when openings are present on only one
façade. According to MIT OpenCourseWare [5], when single-sided openings are used, ACH
may rise to approximately 2–3 (h−1) ACH , particularly under weak wind and small thermal
gradients. In these cases, temperature reductions of 1–2 ◦C are possible, though the associ-
ated air velocities often fall below 0,15 m/s, limiting perceptible cooling and requiring longer
ventilation durations to maintain comfort. Supporting this, Nikas et al. (2014) [6] found
that in single-sided ventilation scenarios, where openings are present only on one façade, a
combination of wind and buoyancy forces can still induce significant airflow, typically result-
ing in 1–5 (h−1) ACH . In spaces resembling living rooms, average air change rates around
3–4 (h−1) ACH were observed under light wind conditions (≈2 m/s) and moderate tem-
perature differences between indoors and outdoors. These conditions were associated with
indoor temperature reductions of up to 2–3 ◦C, particularly during peak summer periods
when ventilation was enabled during early morning and late evening hours.
As a more effective approach, cross-ventilation, where openings are available on opposite
sides of a space, has been widely studied for its superior airflow potential.
A study on
cross-ventilated rooms [7] with realistic window openings showed that air velocity near the
occupant level often remained in the 0,2–0,4 m/s range, above the minimum perception
threshold (≈0,10 m/s) for air movement, thus contributing positively to thermal comfort
without causing draft discomfort. Similar findings were reported in a wind-tunnel and CFD-
validated study of cylindrical and cubic rooms [8], where cross-ventilation increased ACH by
a factor of 1,38, equating to roughly 1,5–4 (h−1) ACH , depending on geometry and wind

direction. Thermal analysis confirmed that such airflow was sufficient to maintain operative
temperatures within the adaptive comfort range defined by EN 15251 and ASHRAE 55,
particularly for Class II and III residential categories. More specifically, cross-ventilation
under light wind conditions (≈2 m/s) and moderate temperature differences (1–2 ◦C) can
achieve 2–4 (h−1) ACH in living-room geometries. Field studies [9] have documented oper-
ative temperature reductions of up to 3,1 ◦C, and indoor air velocities around 0,2 m/s when
sizable openings (≈75%) are used, ensuring that air movement remains within the comfort
range.
In recent years, attention has also turned to alternative implementations of cross-ventilation,
particularly through two flat roof windows. This configuration is especially relevant for build-
ings where façade openings may be limited or spatially constrained. A CFD and experimental
study [10] targeting roof windows showed that when wind speeds exceed ≈2 m/s, cross-
ventilation becomes the dominant airflow mechanism, producing ACH values comparable to
those seen in façade-based setups. Under such conditions, the calculated ventilative flow
rates align with measured upper bounds around ≈4 (h−1) ACH , indicating that this con-
figuration can provide effective natural ventilation and contribute meaningfully to thermal
comfort in living spaces.
Stack-effect ventilation, driven purely by buoyancy due to indoor–outdoor temperature dif-
ferences and building height, can produce moderate-to-high ACH values under favorable
conditions. A field investigation of a multi-chimney residential building in northern Europe
[11] recorded stack-driven ACH ranging from 0,11 to 2,44 (h−1) ACH , with rotary chimney
caps improving draft by approximately +0,40 (h−1) ACH , enabling peak flow rates close to
2,4 (h−1) ACH . These values are corroborated by passive stack systems in Nordic apart-
ment buildings, which consistently achieve 0,5–1,5 (h−1) ACH , even without enhancements,
demonstrating that when sufficient height and temperature differential (∆T) are present,
stack-effect can rival cross-ventilation in airflow and comfort potential.
However, as detailed in the AIVC Technical Note on residential passive ventilation [12], stack-
effect performance is frequently limited in typical single-family homes, where low ceiling
heights and limited stack height prevent the stack-effect from being fully expressed. In these
common residential configurations, cross-ventilation generally achieves significantly higher
ACH values and better airflow distribution.
Finally, beyond thermal comfort, natural ventilation has also been recognized for its role in
reducing energy use and environmental impact. A thermal simulation study on a passive
house in Vejle, Denmark, assessed the energy-saving potential of natural ventilation during
summer [13]. Designed primarily for heating efficiency, the house experienced overheating
issues, which were mitigated through natural ventilation, reducing mechanical ventilation
needs by 90,8% and saving 42 kWh of electricity over three months. Extrapolated to Den-
mark’s residential sector, this translates to an annual saving of 107 GWh, a reduction of
60,7 thousand tons of CO2 emissions, and €13 million in savings.

2.3
Smart ventilation and heating strategies for energy efficient homes
2.3
Smart ventilation and heating strategies for energy efficient
homes
Numerous studies have highlighted the potential of natural ventilation strategies to enhance
thermal comfort and reduce energy consumption in residential buildings, particularly in
Northern European climates such as Denmark. A PhD study of Psomas et al. [14] conducted
in Denmark and Sweden demonstrated that implementing ventilative cooling through auto-
mated window operation can significantly reduce the frequency and severity of overheating
events, with reductions of several degrees in peak indoor temperature during summer pe-
riods when compared to baseline mechanical ventilation systems. This research underlines
the importance of integrating dynamic control strategies, especially under future climate
conditions where overheating is expected to become more prevalent.
In parallel, the use of adaptive comfort models, such as those defined by ASHRAE 55 and
EN 15251, has been shown to be highly effective in reducing cooling energy demand. By
allowing indoor temperatures to fluctuate within acceptable comfort bands based on out-
door conditions, studies [15] report cooling energy savings ranging between 35 % and 43 %
compared to systems using fixed temperature setpoints . This approach not only reduces
energy use but also supports occupant satisfaction in naturally ventilated spaces.
On the heating side, recent Danish studies [16] have demonstrated the advantages of apply-
ing occupancy-based heating control. By adjusting heating setpoints according to real-time
presence data, rather than relying on static schedules, heating energy consumption was re-
duced by an average of 9 %, with certain household configurations achieving reductions of
up to 14 % . These results emphasize the value of smart heating strategies that align more
closely with actual occupant behavior.
Furthermore, research into demand-controlled natural ventilation systems [17], particularly
in the context of single-family homes in Denmark, has shown promising outcomes. By using
sensors that respond to indoor CO2 concentrations and humidity levels, these systems were
able to reduce ventilation rates by up to 37 % of the time without compromising indoor air
quality. As a result, fan energy use decreased by approximately 35 %, demonstrating that
responsive natural ventilation systems can maintain healthy indoor environments while re-
ducing unnecessary energy use. Taken together, these findings illustrate the strong potential
of combining natural ventilation, adaptive comfort models, and intelligent automation to
design low-energy, climate-resilient residential buildings. These strategies become especially
relevant when assessing building performance under projected 2080 weather conditions, pro-
viding robust design alternatives that address both energy efficiency and occupant well-being.

2.4
Research questions
2.4
Research questions
This study aims to investigate the performance and optimization of natural ventilation strate-
gies in residential buildings, with particular attention to thermal comfort, energy efficiency,
and the role of smart automation. The analysis is supported by full-scale measurements and
simulations conducted in IDA ICE, allowing for both technical and economic assessment of
ventilation and heating solutions.
Based on this framework, the following key research questions are addressed:
1. What are the most effective natural ventilation strategies for residential buildings in
terms of air change rate, and what are their main differences?
2. How do various automation strategies impact thermal comfort and energy consump-
tion?
3. How effective are different heating systems from a techno-economic perspective under
current and projected future conditions?

2.5
Theory behind natural ventilation principles
2.5
Theory behind natural ventilation principles
Natural ventilation refers to the movement of fresh air through indoor spaces without the use
of mechanical systems, driven instead by natural forces such as wind and thermal buoyancy.
It plays a critical role in passive building design by improving indoor air quality, enhancing
thermal comfort, and reducing energy consumption for HVAC systems. Unlike mechanical
ventilation, natural strategies depend heavily on architectural layout, climate conditions,
and user behavior [18] [19].
Several natural ventilation strategies exist, each suited to particular building types and
environmental contexts. In this chapter, we focus on three widely adopted techniques: single-
sided ventilation, cross ventilation, and the stack effect.
2.5.1
Single-sided ventilation
Single-sided ventilation occurs when openings are located on only one side of a room or
building. It relies on wind pressure fluctuations and indoor-outdoor temperature differences
to induce air exchange. This strategy is typically effective only up to a room depth of about
twice the height of the opening, limiting its reach in deeper floor plans.
Although relatively simple to implement, single-sided ventilation is highly sensitive to wind
conditions and typically provides lower air change rates than other strategies. Nevertheless,
it remains a viable solution in urban environments where cross ventilation may not be feasible
[20] [21].

such scenarios, incorporating both wind-driven and buoyancy-driven effects. As outlined in
Equation 1, this method provides reliable estimations but does not factor in the influence of

2.5.2
Cross Ventilation
Cross ventilation is one of the most effective natural ventilation strategies, achieved by
placing openings on opposite sides of a space to allow wind to flow straight through. The
effectiveness of this method is highly dependent on wind direction, building orientation, and
the positioning of internal partitions.
This method provides high air exchange rates and is suitable for shallow-plan buildings or
those designed with internal atria or courtyards. Cross ventilation not only enhances indoor
air quality but also supports cooling strategies in warm climates [24] [25].

on the mean airflow rate through a sequence of two openings. As expressed in Equation 3,
the formulation captures the combined impact of aerodynamic characteristics and spatial
geometry on the resulting airflow within the space.

2.5.3
Stack Effect Ventilation
The stack effect leverages temperature-induced buoyancy forces to drive airflow vertically
through a building. As warm indoor air rises and exits through high-level openings, cooler air
is drawn in through low-level inlets. This strategy becomes especially effective in buildings
with significant vertical height, such as multi-story atria or ventilation shafts.
Unlike wind-driven strategies, the stack effect can provide consistent airflow in calm weather.
However, it depends on a sufficient temperature difference between the interior and exterior,
which can vary with season and time of day [27] [28].

2.6
Thermal comfort assessment and categories
Thermal comfort in naturally ventilated residential buildings is a key aspect of sustainable
building design. Unlike mechanically conditioned buildings, naturally ventilated homes rely
on passive strategies The evaluation of thermal comfort in these buildings is based on an
adaptive comfort model, which considers the occupants’ ability to modify clothing, ventila-
tion, and other elements in response to external climate conditions. Additionally, some of
these systems may also be automated.
Thermal comfort in residential buildings is classified into four categories according to inter-
national standards such as ISO 7730 [30] and EN 16798-1 [31], as well as national regulations
like the Danish Building Code [32].
These categories define acceptable indoor conditions based on temperature, air velocity, and
occupant expectations:
• Category I: High-level comfort suitable for sensitive individuals (e.g., elderly, children,
or people with specific health needs).
• Category II: Standard comfort conditions for typical residential buildings with normal
occupant expectations.
• Category III: Moderate comfort deviations acceptable in buildings with lower comfort
expectations.
• Category IV: Significant deviations, acceptable only for temporary use or less demand-
ing environments.
2.6.1
Operative temperature
For residential buildings, the acceptable ranges of operative temperature in winter and sum-
mer are defined separately based on whether the spaces are mechanically conditioned or
naturally ventilated.
During summer and the transitional seasons (spring and autumn), adaptive criteria may be
used: this defines upper and lower temperature limits that vary according to the running
mean of the outdoor temperature. In contrast, for winter, the temperature limits should
align with those used for buildings equipped with mechanical cooling. Notably, the adaptive
method’s temperature range is only applicable when the running mean outdoor temperature
falls between 10 °C and 30 °C, meaning that during the winter period, even the naturally
ventilated buildings have to follow the same limits prescribed for the mechanically ventilated
buildings.
In the following, both of these criteria will be presented.

Measurements
For the purpose of this study, in situ data collection holds particular importance. Specifically,
the parameters to be collected at this stage are those that influence indoor thermal comfort
in the house. These include air temperature, air humidity, air speed, radiant asymmetry
and ventilation rate, which will be used to assess the effectiveness of different ventilation
systems.
3.2.1
Instruments and measurements
Testo 480 measurement
The Testo 480 is a device equipped with multiple sensors designed to record various environ-
mental properties. Specifically, it measures globe temperature, air temperature, air velocity,
CO2 concentration, and relative air humidity (RH). In particular, it relies on:
• a black globe temperature probe with a diameter of 15 cm, for measuring radiant heat,
with an accuracy of ± (0,3 °C + 0,1% of m.v.);
• an air quality tester to analyze the CO2 concentration with an accuracy of ± (75 ppm
CO2 + 3% of m.v.), the humidity with an accuracy of ± (1,8% RH + 0,7% of m.v.)
and temperature with an accuracy of ± 0,5 °C;
• a comfort probe to evaluate the airspeed turbulence, with an accuracy of ± (0,03 m/s
+ 4% of m.v.).
The device allows customization of the tracking duration and the interval between consec-
utive measurements. In this case study, the interval was set to 1 second, with a recording
time of 5 minutes.
This data tracking is particularly valuable for assessing indoor thermal comfort under spe-
cific conditions. One of the key parameters in evaluating thermal comfort is air velocity,
as it is crucial to ensure the absence of draft risks. Consequently, measurements for each
condition should be taken at different locations and heights within the task area (the space
typically occupied by people during daily activities) to confirm that no draft risks occur at
any level.
In this study, the sensor stand was positioned as shown in Figure 10a, and measurements
for each analyzed scenario were taken at heights of 0,1 m, 0,6 m, and 1,1 m. This location
was selected for data collection because it represents a realistic position where an occupant
might be seated, and it is likely to be directly influenced by the airflow. As such, evaluating
both local and global thermal discomfort based on measurements from this spot is expected
to yield results that closely reflect real-world conditions.

INNOVA 1303 measurement
The INNOVA 1303 is a gas analyzer, employed for tracer gas measurement, with the aim of
determining the ventilation rate for a specific ventilation configuration.
The underlying principle of this measurement is straightforward: it involves measuring the
time required for a specific gas to decrease its concentration within the testing environment
under predetermined experimental conditions, utilizing a concentration decay method.
The experiment assumes the following "ideal" conditions:
• complete air mixing;
• absence of internal pollutant production and sorption effects (i.e., the ability of indoor
materials to absorb and release airborne substances);
• no filtration, meaning there is no active system in place to remove airborne particles
or pollutants from the air circulating within the space.
Under these conditions, given the fully mixed air, the position of the sensor within the room
is irrelevant. Anyway, during the experiment, the sensor was placed according to Figure 11a.
To perform the experiment, the following pieces of equipment are required:
• a gas cylinder (in this case, R-134a), to serve as the reference gas whose concentration
will be monitored throughout the experiment;

During the measurements, although fans are intended to promote thorough air mixing, com-
plete homogeneity may not always be achieved. Some areas of the air might remain partially
unmixed, especially at the onset of data collection. As a result, recorded values may slightly
deviate from theoretical expectations. Nevertheless, the observed data typically aligns well
with predicted outcomes, with only minor discrepancies.
To assess how well the theoretical model fits the actual data, the coefficient of determination
(denoted as R2) was used. This statistical metric ranges from 0 to 1 and reflects how well
the model explains the variability of the observed data. An R2 value of 1 indicates a perfect
fit, where the model accounts for all variation in the observed data, while a value closer to
0 suggests a poor fit.
Once a sufficiently high R2 confirms that the decay model reliably represents the real con-
centration drop, the parameter n, the air change rate, can be confidently used as the ACH
value.

Automation startegies
In this project, several automation strategies were developed and implemented to dynam-
ically control heating, cooling, and natural ventilation systems in an occupant-responsive
manner. For heating, two control strategies were compared: a standard approach based on
a fixed temperature setpoint with no consideration of occupancy, and an advanced strategy
that incorporated occupancy status and room function to enhance energy efficiency while en-
suring thermal comfort. Similarly, two cooling control strategies were tested. The standard
method relied on a fixed setpoint and seasonal activation without accounting for occupancy
or adaptive comfort. In contrast, the smart cooling strategy applied an adaptive comfort
model, dynamically adjusting the cooling threshold based on recent outdoor conditions to
reduce reliance on mechanical systems while maintaining acceptable indoor comfort. A de-
tailed description of each strategy is provided in the following sections.

Standard heating control strategy
The initial approach consisted of a simple PI (Proportional-Integral) control loop, where the

did not take occupancy into account. The control logic was straightforward: the difference
between the room temperature and the setpoint was used by the PI controller to generate
the heating signal, which ensured thermal comfort but lacked any dynamic adjustment to
actual room usage.

One of the fundamental aspects of natural ventilation is its strong dependence on outdoor
environmental conditions. Since outdoor air serves as the primary source of ventilation for
indoor spaces, its characteristics, such as temperature, humidity, and air movement, directly
affect the indoor environment. This relationship becomes particularly critical when consid-
ering future climate scenarios. As global temperatures are expected to rise, the quality and
effectiveness of natural ventilation could be compromised. Warmer outdoor conditions may
lead to a reduced ability of natural ventilation to maintain comfortable indoor tempera-
tures, potentially resulting in increased thermal discomfort for occupants. What is currently
considered a tolerable or even beneficial ventilation strategy may, in the future, fall short of
ensuring adequate thermal comfort, especially during hotter periods. Therefore, understand-
ing and anticipating how outdoor climate evolution influences indoor comfort is essential for
designing resilient, naturally ventilated buildings.
In this context, Figure 26 and Figure 27 show outdoor temperatures under both the current
and future weather scenarios.
The reference year for the current weather scenario is 2021, whereas the future weather sce-
nario depicts the predicted weather conditions in 2080.
The simulations were based on two different weather files.
For the present-day scenario
(2025), the file used was DNK_KOBENHAVN-KASTRUP_061800(IW2), an IWEC2 dataset
representing a Typical Meteorological Year (TMY) for the Copenhagen area. This file, based
on historical weather data, provides statistically representative conditions and ensures com-
parability across scenarios by excluding extreme or anomalous years.
For the future scenario (2080), a synthetic weather file was generated using the Meteonorm
software and provided by VELUX. This file, also in .epw format, was created through down-
scaling of global climate models using stochastic weather generation. The process begins
with a future emissions scenario, defined by the IPCC, which is used to project large-scale
climate conditions. These projections are then statistically downscaled to produce hourly,
location-specific weather data representative of future conditions.
To simulate the impact of more extreme climatic conditions, the Representative Concen-
tration Pathway (RCP) 8.5 was selected as the underlying emissions scenario for the 2080
weather file. This high-emission pathway enabled the evaluation of how natural ventilation
and smart control strategies might perform under intensified environmental stress, highlight-
ing their potential effectiveness in a worst-case future climate context.

peratures, the outcomes are illustrated through graphs rather than precise numerical values.
This approach offers a clear overview of the underlying trends, prioritizing insight into the
general direction of the results rather than exact figures, which would be speculative and
potentially misleading. While this method still conveys the approximate magnitude of the
results, its primary value lies in highlighting the trends they follow.
First of all, a crucial input for the model is the heating demand under both the current
and future scenarios. Based on IDA-ICE simulations, the heating demand under present
conditions is 2.463 kWh/year, while in the future scenario it is expected to decrease to

depends on both the required output temperature (generally lower for floor heating systems
than for domestic hot water) and the outdoor temperature (a higher outdoor temperature
yields a higher COP for the same output). Given the complexity of accurately modeling all
these variables, an average COP of 2,5 is used throughout the analysis.
In contrast, electric radiators (and the electric boiler) are assumed to operate at near-perfect
efficiency, with a COP of 1, which represents the upper bound for this technology.

5
Discussion
In this project, three main concepts were addressed: the effectiveness of natural ventilation
strategies through ACH analysis, the use of automation strategies to evaluate energy-saving
potential, and an economic assessment to reflect the current and future trends in heating
systems. In this section, the key findings related to these three overarching themes are dis-
cussed in comparison with existing literature, followed by an interpretation of the results.
Natural ventilation strategies
Starting with the natural ventilation strategies, it can be observed that the measured ACH
values align well with ranges reported in the literature.
For the single-sided ventilation through the façade window, the measured ACH was 2,09
(h−1). According to MIT OpenCourseWare [5], single-sided strategies can reach 2–3 (h−1)
ACH . Similarly, Nikas et al. (2014) [6] report a broader range of 1–5 (h−1) ACH , depending
on environmental conditions.
For the stack effect configuration, the measured ACH was 2,19 (h−1). Literature data show
that stack-effect ventilation in residential buildings can vary between 0,11 and 2,44 (h−1)
ACH , as observed in a northern European case study [11].
The best-performing strategy was cross-ventilation between the kitchen and the façade win-
dow, which achieved a measured ACH of 3,14 (h−1). Studies on cross-ventilation report
typical ranges from 2 to 4 (h−1) ACH in living-room conditions under moderate wind and
temperature differences [7, 8, 9].
In summary, all measured ACH values fall within the ranges found in previous studies,
confirming the consistency between experimental data and existing research on natural ven-
tilation performance.
The simulation results, validated through comparison with the real measurements, enabled
a deeper understanding of the behavior of natural ventilation strategies under various con-
ditions.
Among the different approaches, the use of flaps, implemented in the real-case scenario as
a substitute for mechanical ventilation, showed the lowest ventilation rates. Without ac-
counting for infiltration, their standalone performance fails to meet the minimum threshold
of 0,38 (h−1) ACH , which is generally considered necessary to ensure adequate air renewal.
While flaps may contribute to background ventilation, they are not sufficient on their own.
Moreover, these openings are often closed under extreme weather conditions such as strong
winds or low outdoor temperatures, representing a critical limitation to their reliability for
continuous use.
89
==PAGE:98==
5 Discussion
An interesting outcome was observed in the single-sided ventilation scenario involving flat
roof windows. This configuration is less documented in the literature compared to the other
solutions, yet it achieved a measured ACH of 1,53 (h−1). While this demonstrates its capabil-
ity to enhance air exchange, the thermal comfort results indicated a potential for discomfort,
with a peak air velocity of 0,54 m/s recorded at neck level. This highlights the importance of
considering not only airflow volume but also distribution patterns and occupant positioning
when evaluating ventilation performance.
The stack effect strategy is theoretically one of the most effective natural ventilation mecha-
nisms due to buoyancy-driven airflow. According to the simulation results, it should achieve
the highest ACH among the tested configurations. However, the measured values did not
reflect this expectation. This discrepancy can be attributed to the relatively low height of
the residential building and the influence of external wind, which can disrupt or oppose the
expected buoyancy-driven flow.
This finding emphasizes a fundamental limitation of natural ventilation: its strong depen-
dence on external climatic conditions. Even if simulations indicate that natural ventilation
could replace mechanical systems under ideal circumstances, actual performance may vary
significantly. For example, elevated ACH during the winter season may lead to thermal
discomfort unless adequately controlled. These observations also reveal the limitations of
relying solely on simulation software to predict real-life ventilation behavior.
Additionally, the effectiveness of natural ventilation is strongly influenced by occupant be-
havior. In scenarios involving manual control, ventilation performance depends on when and
how users choose to open windows. Occupants may delay or avoid opening windows due to
noise, security, or weather conditions, which can reduce the potential for effective natural
ventilation. Even with automated systems, user preferences and overrides can alter expected
outcomes. This underscores the importance of considering real-life behavior when evaluating
or designing ventilation strategies.
Moreover, the results of this study are specific to the Danish climate, characterized by moder-
ate summers, cold winters, and variable wind conditions. While the strategies tested proved
effective in this context, their applicability in other climates, such as hot-humid or hot-arid
regions, may differ substantially.
Due to this, relying exclusively on natural ventilation strategies does not guarantee consis-
tent indoor environmental quality throughout the year. A more reliable approach may be
hybrid ventilation, which combines natural and mechanical systems. In such a setup, nat-
ural ventilation can be prioritized when outdoor conditions are favorable, while mechanical
systems can be activated as needed to maintain comfort and indoor air quality when passive
strategies are insufficient.

==PAGE:99==
5 Discussion
Smart control strategies
As was done with the ACH results, the main findings from the energy simulations are first
compared with the literature review.
For the fully integrated V4 configuration, which combines smart control of windows, shading,
heating, and cooling systems, the simulation results showed the strongest performance across
both current (2025) and future (2080) climate scenarios.
In 2025, V4 achieved a 12 % reduction in heating demand. This result aligns closely with
findings from Danish studies on occupancy-based heating control [16], which report heating
energy reductions of 9–14 % by adjusting setpoints based on real-time occupancy.
In the future climate scenario (2080), the energy-saving potential of V4 becomes even more
significant. The strategy results in a 26 % total energy reduction, with 33 % and 17 % reduc-
tions in cooling and heating demand, respectively. This outcome is supported by research on
ventilative cooling strategies under future weather conditions in Northern Europe [14], which
highlights the growing importance of automated window operation to mitigate overheating.
What stands out from this analysis is that, generally speaking, the heating season is expected
to shrink in favor of a longer cooling season. The energy-saving focus will shift: while until
now the priority has been to reduce heating demand through better insulation, in the future,
when higher outdoor temperatures are expected, highly insulated buildings may struggle to
dissipate excess heat. This could lead to an increased reliance on active cooling systems,
such as air conditioning, unless alternative strategies are implemented.
At the same time, longer cooling seasons will allow for more frequent window openings,
particularly during the mid-season and summer periods. This highlights the importance of
implementing automated window control, especially for flat roof windows, which can serve
as both a natural ventilation outlet and an effective means of addressing overheating risks.
It is also worth noting that the design of cooling systems will become increasingly relevant in
residential buildings. Ventilative cooling strategies, such as those based on adaptive comfort
models, will be essential to reduce cooling energy demand while still ensuring acceptable
thermal comfort conditions.
Techno-economic assessment
As previously discussed, the ongoing reduction in heating season duration is significantly
lowering the annual energy demand for space heating. In this context, the performance of
heat pumps is expected to improve due to generally warmer outdoor temperatures, poten-
tially leading to a reduction in their future primary energy consumption. In contrast, the
primary energy demand of electric radiators is unlikely to change, since their COP remains
constant.
Another crucial consideration is that, in both current and future scenarios, heating demand is
expected to decline year by year. However, in the earlier analysis, a constant heating demand
91
==PAGE:100==
5 Discussion
was assumed throughout the entire lifetime of both systems. If a decreasing demand had
been taken into account, electric radiators would appear even more economically favorable.
Although they require more primary energy than heat pumps for the same heating load,
a decreasing demand would lead to a greater absolute reduction in their energy use. This
means that, over time, their operational costs would decline more sharply, partially offset-
ting the improved performance and lower operational costs of heat pumps in future scenarios.
These dynamics are particularly relevant when assessing long-term economic performance.
As shown in Figure 52, radiators still emerge as more cost-effective in the future scenario,
and this conclusion holds even when accounting for the declining heating demand and im-
proving heat pump efficiency.
Moreover, these findings are transferable to other geographical contexts. For example, if the
same building were located in Italy, where heating demand is already lower and expected to
decrease further, radiators would become even more cost-effective. In such climates, their
higher operational cost becomes less impactful due to the overall lower heating needs. The
higher CAPEX for the heat pump is unlikely to be offset, as the OPEX wouldn’t be sufficient
to deliver a worthwhile return on investment.
When evaluating the carbon footprint of heating systems solely from the perspective of em-
bodied emissions, that is, emissions associated with material extraction, manufacturing, and
installation, the combination of electric radiators and an electric boiler generally performs
better than a heat pump coupled with a floor heating system. Electric radiators are materi-
ally simpler, requiring fewer components and minimal distribution infrastructure, while floor
heating involves significant amounts of embedded piping, insulation layers, and screed, and
heat pumps themselves contain complex electronics and refrigerants with potentially high
global warming potential (GWP) [57] [58]. Looking toward the future, although electric re-
sistive systems will likely continue to have a higher primary energy demand during operation
than heat pumps due to their lower efficiency, ongoing decarbonization of electricity grids is
expected to reduce this gap. This trend may render electric radiator systems increasingly
favorable, even in terms of overall carbon performance [59] [60].
Finally, it is worth noting that a discount rate of 0,03 was assumed, which is standard in
this type of analysis. If a lower discount rate were considered, the present value of future
operational costs would increase for both systems, making electric radiators slightly less
advantageous. Conversely, a higher discount rate would further favor the radiator system
economically.

6 Conclusion
6
Conclusion
This study set out to evaluate which natural ventilation strategies are most effective in terms
of air change rates in residential buildings, and to assess how different automation strategies
influence thermal comfort and energy consumption.
From both a practical and simulation-based perspective, stack effect ventilation emerged as
the most promising strategy due to its potential to generate airflow driven by indoor-outdoor
temperature differences and vertical pressure gradients. However, measurements revealed
that cross ventilation achieved the highest air change rate on the days when measurements
were conducted. This highlights the difficulty of designing resilient buildings based solely on
natural ventilation strategies, because outdoor conditions vary greatly throughout the year,
making it extremely challenging to identify a configuration that consistently delivers optimal
performance in every situation.
Another important finding concerns the use of flaps, implemented in the real-case scenario
as a substitute for mechanical ventilation. These demonstrated limited effectiveness, with
standalone performance falling below the minimum recommended threshold. This under-
scores their inadequacy in ensuring sufficient air exchange, particularly under less favorable
conditions.
Even though all tested strategies, except for the flap configuration, appear capable of meeting
the required ventilation rates throughout the year, it remains challenging to design a fully
passive ventilation system with consistent and reliable performance. As such, it is recom-
mended to complement passive strategies with hybrid systems, which combine mechanical
and natural ventilation to ensure comfort and indoor air quality year-round.
When it comes to smart control automation, in the current 2025 climate scenario, the con-
figuration with automated control of windows, shading, heating, and cooling achieved a 12 %
reduction in heating demand. Under the future 2080 scenario, energy savings became even
more substantial, with a total energy reduction of 26 %, including 33 % for cooling and 17 %
for heating, while still maintaining compliance with thermal comfort thresholds. These re-
sults highlight the potential of smart control automation in achieving meaningful energy
savings.
The analysis also revealed a broader trend: a shift in the seasonal energy balance.
As
outdoor temperatures rise, the heating season shortens while the cooling season extends. In
highly insulated buildings, this increases the risk of overheating and emphasizes the need for
effective cooling solutions.
In contrast, the shortening of the heating season leads to a decreased demand for heating,
resulting in an overall reduction in heating-related costs, regardless of the scenario. Given
this trend, it is likely that future investments in heating systems will favor solutions with
lower CAPEX and higher operational costs, rather than highly efficient but capital-intensive
alternatives. In this evolving context, technologies currently viewed as outdated, such as
electric radiators, may regain relevance by offering reliable heating performance at a lower
upfront cost.
This projection represents a broader trend based on certain economic conditions that have
93
==PAGE:102==

not materialized yet, but appear increasingly plausible. Furthermore, although the analysis
is based on a Danish house and Danish weather patterns, its insights could prove applicable
to other climates. As interest in cooling solutions continues to grow globally, the importance
of heating systems may gradually diminish or be redefined altogether. This could signal a
paradigm shift in how heating technologies are assessed and adopted.

INNOVA Multi Gas Monitoring Instruments 
Reliable, easy-to-use multi-point sampler and doser
INNOVA 1303 
• Full remote-control from a PC
• Automatic calculation of the 
amount of tracer-gas deliv-
ered, to the dosing location
• Discontinuous dosing facility
• Self-calibrating dosing system
• Self-test function
• Pneumatic system constructed 
of AISI-316 Stainless Steel and 
PTFE tubing to minimize gas 
adsorption
An increasing number of legislative 
measures are aimed at improving 
air quality in workplace environ-
ments. These improvements require 
gas monitoring equipment with the 
flexibility to provide sensitive and 
accurate monitoring in a variety of  
environments. 
The INNOVA 1303 Multi-point 
Sampler and Doser from LumaSense 
Technologies is designed to be 
remote-controlled from a PC over its 
IEEE 488/RS-232 serial interface and 
used in conjunction with an INNOVA 
1412 Photoacoustic Field Gas-Monitor 
to provide a flexible, sensitive and 
accurate monitoring system. The 1303 
greatly increases the area monitoring 
capabilities of the 1412 by drawing air 
samples through tubing from up to six 
sampling points, up to 50m away, and 
delivering them to the 1412.
Comprehensive air exchange analyses 
and ventilation efficiency checks are 
easily performed using the 1303’s 
dosing facilities. Tracer-gas is delivered 
through tubing to “label” the air in 
up to six locations. The amount of 
tracer-gas delivered is automatically 
calculated by the 1303. The labelled 
air is then sampled by the 1303 and 
delivered to the 1412 for analysis. Up 
to six temperature transducers can be 
connected to the 1303 and positioned 
at the desired sampling/dosing points. 
The 1303’s self-calibrating and self-
checking routines allow easy verifica-
tion of the unit’s operation and ensure 
reliable functioning.
Functions 
The 1303’s pneumatic system is shown 
in Figure 1. The sampler system is 
constructed of AISI-316 stainless 
steel and poly tetrafluoroethylene 
(PTFE) tubing to minimize adsorp-
tion of samples. The system has six 
inlet channels, each with a solenoid 
valve. Each inlet channel has a 
tube-mounting stub on the 1303’s 
frontplate. Six tubes of up to 50m 
connect each channel to the respective 
sampling point. The six inlet channels 
converge into one; a three-way valve 
then directs the gas sample to the 
1412 for analysis or through the pump 
to the waste-air outlet on the 1303’s 
back-plate. A pressure transducer 
checks the efficiency of the sampling 
pump and allows checks for blocked 
airways. An air filter is attached to the 
end of each sampling tube to keep the 
samples free of particles.
Application areas:
• Air sampling in six locations and de-
livery to a 1412 Photoacoustic Field 
Gas Monitor or a 1314 Photoacoustic 
Multi-Gas Monitor.
• Delivery of tracer-gas to six  
locations for air-exchange analyses 
with the 1412 Photoacoustic Field 
Gas Monitor
The Doser System
The doser system has six outlet 
channels, each with a solenoid valve. 
Each channel has a nozzle that reduces 
the internal diameter of the channel. 
This nozzles ensure that the rate of 
flow of tracer-gas to the dosing points 
is dependent only upon the tracer-gas 
supply pressure and temperature and 
is unaffected by the pressure in the 
dosing tubes or at the dosing point 
itself.
Three different nozzle sizes are used: 
one small (nozzle 1), four medium 
(nozzle 2-5) and one large (nozzle 6). 
Each size gives a different volume flow 
rate, allowing the amount of tracer-gas 
delivered to a particular dosing point 
to be matched to the requirement at 
that point by using the outlet channel 
which has the appropriate nozzle. Each 
of the six dosing outlet channels has a tube-
mounting stub on the front plate of the 
1303. Six tubes of up to 50m connect each 
stub to the respective dosing point. The 
tracer-gas and the carrier-air inlet channels 
mounted on the backplate of the 1303 
serve the dosing channels.
The tracer-gas inlet channel is pressur-
ized by the tracer-gas supply cylinder, 
which is connected by tubing to 
the inlet on the 1303’s backplate. A 
pressure and a temperature transducer 
give information on the tracer-gas 
supply; a fine filter ensures that the 
dosing channels are particle-free. The 
main valve on the tracer-gas inlet 
channel is used to enclose a volume 
of tracer-gas between itself and the 
dosing valves. This volume is used 
when calibrating the doser system. 
The carrier-air inlet pumps extra air to 
the dosing outlets to speed delivery 
of the tracer-gas to the dosing point. 
This inlet has a coarse air filter, a pump 
and a pressure transducer for checking 
the efficiency of the pump. Delivering 
a dose of tracer-gas to a dosing point 
50m distant takes one minute.The 
dosing system can deliver  an uninter-
rupted flow of tracer-gas over a period 
of time or a discontinuous dose, in 
which the amount of tracer-gas deliv-
ered is reduced by interrupting the 
flow at regular intervals during the 
dosing period.
The doser system is configured by 
specifying a dosing time-out: the 1303 
will stop any current dosing proce-
dure after the given time has elapsed 
without instructions from the control-
ling computer. This value is specified 
from the controlling computer.
Calibrating the Doser System 
The 1303 can calibrate each dosing-
nozzle automatically, so that the 
amount of tracer-gas delivered 
during a dosing procedure can be 
accurately determined by the 1303. 
A volume of tracer-gas is enclosed 
between the main valve and the 
dosing valves; this volume is then 
released through the nozzle to be 
calibrated. As the internal volume of 
the airways are known, the effec-
tive outflow area for each nozzle is 
calculated from the rate of pressure-
decrease measured in the dosing 
airway. The amount of tracer-gas 
delivered can then be calculated 
from the effective outflow area, 
the tracer-gas supply pressure and 
temperature, and the dosing time-
period.
Temperature Measurement
To complete the information about 
the air environment at the sampling/
dosing points, the 1303 is equipped 
with six temperature transducer inputs, 
suitable for use with the MM0034 Air 
Temperature Transducer, MM0035 
Surface Temperature Transducer and 
MM0060 Operative Temperature 
Transducer. Each transducer can be 
positioned up to 50m away from the 
1303.
Reliability
Reliability is ensured by automatic self-
tests of both hardware and software. A 
check of the pneumatic system can be 
performed on request by the control-
ling  computer. The 1303’s operating 
status can be read-out at any time; if an 
error exists, the type of error is given to 
help repair.

the doser system at the bottom. The use of non-reactive materials throughout minimizes gas adsorp-
tion in the internal air-channels.

Control of the 1303
The 1303 is fully remote-controlled from 
a PC using the IEEE/IEC interface. Control 
via the 1412 is also possible: the control-
ling computer communicates with the 
1412 over the RS-232 interface. The 
message is then transferred to the 1303 
via the IEEE/IEC interface. Commands 
and information requests are sent over 
the interface to the 1303 to control the 
sampler system; to set-up, calibrate, 
and control the dosing system; and to 
read-out data and command the perfor-
mance of self-tests.
System Use
The 1303 combined with the 1412 and 
a controlling computer offers wide-
ranging monitoring capabilities. The 
1303 makes it possible to perform 
multi-point air-exchange analyses and 
multi-point monitoring tasks in many 
different situations and environments, 
without changing the system compo-
nents.
An example air-exchange analysis 
system is shown in Fig.2. In such a 
system, the doser/sampler systems 
of the 1303 are used as follows. The 
doser system marks the supply-air of 
the room with a known amount of 
tracergas. The sampler system then 
takes a sample of the return-air from 
the room, and delivers the sample to 
the 1412 for analysis. 
While the 1412 performs one analysis, 
the 1303 takes the next sample for 
analysis from the room. As the amount 
of tracer-gas delivered to the room is 
known, and the remaining concentra-
tion of tracer-gas in the samples is 
determined by the 1412, the venti-
lation system performance can be 
calculated.
LumaSense Technologies’ INNOVA 7620 
Application Software – is available to 
give full coordination and control of all 
the dosing/sampling and monitoring 
functions of such systems. The 7620 can 
control one 1412 and up to two 1303 
units. 
A similar system set-up can be used 
for multi-point, multi-gas monitoring, 
using only the 1303’s sampler system. 
This system can be remotely controlled 
using Lumasoft Gas Multi Point 7850 
software. The sofware coordinates 
the function of the monitoring system 
comprising up to four 1303 and one 
1412 or 1314. Gas monitoring can then 
be performed in up to 24 different 
locations.

Inserting/Changing the Battery
Always replace all the non-rechargeable batteries at the
same time. Only use non-rechargeable batteries from
the same manufacturer and which have the same capacity.
When inserting the batteries, ensure that the polarity is
correct according to the illustration on the inside of the
battery compartment.
