Abstract
Technical University of Denmark
Department of Electrical Engineering
Master of Science in Engineering
by Jacob Petersen
In 2013, there were 25.7 million stroke survivors worldwide along with 10.3 million new incidents
- a number that is increasing every year. From the stroke survivors with a hemiplegic arm,
up to 55-75 % still had problems after three to six months, indicating a need for improved
rehabilitation techniques.
Motor imagery (MI) based Brain Computer Interface (BCI) systems have shown to promote
the reorganizational processes in stroke patients, whereby such systems look promising to com-
plement physical therapy for neurorehabilitation - especially when used to control functional
electrical stimulation (FES) of the muscles of the corresponding muscles. The aim of this project
is to develop an MI based BCI system that could potentially be used in neurorehabilitation of
hand motor function in stroke patients.
Two co-adaptive, three-class MI based BCI systems for real-time processing has been developed
and compared using publicly available data from the BCI Competition III Dataset V, whereafter
the winner was chosen for experiments with three MI hand motor tasks. The ﬁrst algorithm
utilizes the Filterbank Common Spatial Pattern (FBCSP) for feature extraction, and the other
algorithm utilizes Separable Common Spatio-Spectral Pattern (SCSSP) - both in combination
with a Multi-layer Perceptron (MLP) for classiﬁcation.
The system proved to be successful when using the competition data by showing an average
correct classiﬁcation rate (CCR) of 64.71 % for the SCSSP method. This proved superior to a
related study using the same feature extraction methods, but with other classiﬁcation methods.
However, the system did unfortunately show unimpressive results for the experimental data -
most likely caused by the diﬃcult same-limb MI tasks. An improved performance in the online
experiments is necessary in order to realize such a system.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This chapter includes the motivation for carrying out this project, the general hypothesis being
investigated, the aims and objectives, and ﬁnally the organization of the thesis.
1.1
Motivation
Stroke is the syndrome of a deﬁcit of blood perfusion and thereby oxygen to the brain, injuring
the brain tissue. There are two main types of stroke: ischemic (caused by clure of a vessel) and
hemorrhagic (caused by bleeding of a ruptured vessel).
According to [10] there were 25.7 million stroke survivors worldwide in 2013, as well as 10.3
million of people with new strokes and 6.5 million stroke deaths - making it, quote: ”a lead-
ing global health problem that requires urgent and sustained attention (...)”. Furthermore -
according to the same study - the absolute number of people who have a stroke every year is
increasing.
Among the stroke survivors, the speciﬁc manifestations are determined by the brain areas in-
volved - the primary impairment being upper limb hemiparesis, i.e. weakness or inability to
move upper limb in one side. [11] Studies have shown that up to 55 % to 75 % of stroke pa-
tients with a hemiplegic arm still had problems after three to six months, which emphasizes the
need for improved rehabilitation options. [12] Thus far, rehabilitation methods for stroke sur-
vivors include constrained-induced movement therapy, bilateral arm training and robot-assisted
training. However, the success-rate of these methods are limited, and - furthermore - require

Motor imagery (MI) is imagining or mentally rehearsing a given movement without (neces-
sarily) carrying out the actual movement. Several studies have concluded that MI can serve
as a clinically feasible, cost-eﬀective complement to physical therapy for post-stroke motor re-
habilitation, and that it may improve the outcome more than participation in therapy only.
[11, 14, 15]
Additionally, studies show that people can learn to modulate the amplitude of sensorimotor
rhythms (SMRs), i.e. brain activity related to movement, by practising MI of simple move-
ments. This knowledge can be used in conjunction with a brain computer interface (BCI) to
control external devices. Exploiting MI to develop MI based BCIs have in numerous studies
shown potential to serve as a tool for neurorehabilitation for post stroke patients to complement
standard therapy. [16–19] MI based BCI systems allow also the inclusion of patients with no or
minimal motor function to stimulate the recovery process of substitution i.e. the recruitment
of other brain areas to take over the functions of the damaged area. [14] More recent studies
have included a BCI controlled functional electrical stimulator (FES) that stimulates the nerves
innervating the aﬀected limb to induce movements - thereby further enhancing motor function
and promoting adaptive neuroplasticity, i.e. the brain’s ability to adapt function and structure
in response to environmental demands. [20–24]
In summary, numerous studies suggest that there is a potential for MI based BCI systems -
especially when including an FES - to complement physical therapy for neurorehabilitation of
the large, yet increasing number of stroke patients worldwide.
1.2
Hypothesis
The hypothesis that is being investigated in this project is that an MI based BCI system can
serve as a tool for neurorehabilitation of hand motor function.

2.1.3
The Neuron
The basic units of the nervous system are called nerve cells, or neurons, and the brain contains a
number on the order of 1011 neurons that comes in more than a thousand diﬀerent types - all of
which share the same basic architecture. Neurons have the ability to connect to each other and
form neural networks that can communicate with each other, which results in a corresponding
behaviour. [1]
The neuron has four deﬁned regions: the cell body, or the soma; the dendrites; the axon; and
the presynaptic terminals, which are all involved in generation of signals and communication
between neurons, and can be seen in ﬁgure 2.6. The soma is the metabolic center of the neuron
and contains the cell nucleus and endoplasmic reticulum, which are responsible for storage of
genes and cell protein synthesis, respectively. From the soma, the dendrites and axon branch oﬀ.
There are usually several dendrites, which are receivers of incoming signals, and the axon is the
signal transmitter. The electrical signals that are being conveyed are called action potentials,
which are so-called all-or-nothing nerve impulses with an amplitude of 100 mV and a duration of
about 1 ms. The initiation of the action potentials happens at the axon hillock, from which the
signal propagates down the axon, which happens at rates of 1-100 m per second. The axon is
insulated in fatty myelin sheaths, which increases the conduction speed of the action potentials.
Between the myelin sheaths, there are small uninsulated spots called nodes of Ranvier, at which
the action potentials are regenerated. At the end of the axon, there are ﬁne branches that can
connect to other neurons. Such a connection point is known as a synapse. The end of the axon
transmitting information is called the presynaptic terminal, whereas the receiving cell (most
often a dendrite) is called the postsynaptic terminal, and the gap between them (they are not
completely connected) is the synaptic cleft. [1]
There are two important principles for neurons, namely the principle of dynamic polarization
and the principle of connectional speciﬁcity. The former states that electrical signals within a
nerve only ﬂows in one direction - from the receiving end to the transmitting end (the axon).
The principle of connectional speciﬁcity states that each cell makes speciﬁc connections with
one another, thereby not forming random networks, but speciﬁc, discriminated networks. [1]

2.1.4
The Action Potential and Synaptic Transmission
As earlier described, there are more than a thousand diﬀerent types of neurons.
However,
the signaling is organized in the same way in all neurons.
At rest, all neurons maintain a
voltage diﬀerence of 70 mV across the membrane, which is called the resting membrane potential.
Since the net charge outside the membrane is arbitrarily deﬁned as zero, the resting membrane
potential is said to be at -70 mV. A membrane protein maintains an unequal distribution of
positively charged ions on either side of the cell by pumping Na+ out of the cell and K+ into
it. This pump is called the Na+-K+-pump. The membrane also contains ion channels that
is usually impermeable (impenetrable). However, when the neuron is stimulated (or irritated)
by input neurons, the membrane become more permeable to Na+, meaning that Na+-ions will
ﬂow into the cell, thereby changing the membrane potential to become less negative - this is
called a depolarisation. The more intense a stimulus, the more the membrane potential will
change. However, the more permeable the membrane will become towards Na+ into the cell,
the more permeable the membrane will also become towards K+ out of the cell. If the stimulus is
insuﬃcient to cause a greater permeability towards Na+ into the cell compared to K+ out of the
cell, an action potential will not occur. However, if the membrane potential reaches a threshold
of -55 mV, the permeability towards Na+ will increase signiﬁcantly, and the rapid inﬂux of Na+
ions will cause the membrane potential to reach +40 mV. This is shown to the left in ﬁgure 2.7.
At this point, the permeability towards K+ out of the cell is exceeding the Na+-permeability,
and an out-ﬂux of K+ ions occurs, causing a a negative shift called a repolarisation, followed by
a small undershoot called a hyperpolarisation, before it reaches the resting membrane potential
again. [1]
This electrical signal propagates along the axon until it reaches the synapse. The synapse
can be either electrical or chemical.
Electrical synapses convey information by opening so-
called gap junctions that conduct the ﬂow of ionic current from the presynaptic terminal to the
postsynaptic terminal. Electrical synapses provide instantanious signal transmission, whereas
chemical synapses have a signiﬁcant delay varying from 0.3 ms to more than 5 ms. At the
presynaptic terminal in chemical synapses, the action potential will cause Ca2+ channels to
open and Ca2+ ions enters the cell from the extracellular space - see ﬁgure 2.7. The inﬂux
of Ca2+ causes vesicles inside the presynaptic terminal to fuse with the membrane and release
transmitter substance into the synaptic cleft. The transmitter substance acts as a key to open
receptor channels at the postsynaptic terminal, which will change the permeability towards

2.1.5
Electroencephalogram (EEG)
Electroencephalography (EEG) is the recording of electrical activity - produced by the neurons,
as has been described above - in the brain. These recordings can either be invasive to record
activity of individual or several neurons in high spatial resolution, or non-invasive using surface
electrodes placed on the scalp in lower spatial resolution. As earlier stated, the electrodes of
interest in this project are surface electrodes. The surface EEG is an attenuated measure of
current ﬂow from the accumulated activity of many neurons that ultimately represents the col-
lective behaviour of cortical neurons close to the electrode(s). The electrical activity originates
from the neurons in the underlying brain tissue, which is undergoing ﬁltering and attenuation
through the layers of bone, skin and other tissue, thereby reducing the amplitude of the EEG
potentials to µV from mV in the neuron. The voltage measured is usually the diﬀerence between
the electrode at the site of interest called the active electrode, and another electrode distant from
the site called the reference electrode. [1]

2.2.1
Acute Stroke Management
The acute management of stroke starts with diagnosis to localize the concerned brain area,
determine the presence of ischemia or hemorrhage, the size of the vessel(s) involved and the
severity. The diagnosis is based on the patient’s history, as well as physical and neurologic
examination, including brain imaging tools. [3]
The purpose of the early treatment is to reverse the evolving ischemic brain injury, which can
salvage ischemic but viable brain tissue by the use of reperfusion techniques and protective
strategies, which can only be done within a limited window of time. Reperfusion techniques for
ischemic strokes includes breaking apart the blood clot and minimize further clotting, which
would worsen the situation if used in a hemorrhagic stroke. For hemorrhagic strokes, surgery
may be needed in order to prevent incarceration. [3]
2.2.2
Post Stroke Management and Rehabilitation
The purpose of the post stroke management is to prevent recurrent strokes and other medical
complications while promoting the best possible recovery through early and aggressive reha-
bilitation, which includes physicians, nurses, physical therapists, occupational therapists and
relatives. [3]

2.3.1
Principles
A BCI is basically a communication and control system that allows a person to control an
external device/environment by the power of thought. It includes a series of steps within a
closed loop system, and a general scheme of a BCI system is illustrated in ﬁgure 2.12. Each
step is discussed in the following subsections.

2.3.1.1
Signal Acquisition
The signal acquisition is measurement of brain activity while the subject is producing speciﬁc
brain patterns. The measurement of brain activity can be carried out using diﬀerent technolo-
gies, including magnetoencephalograhy (MEG), functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI),
positron emission tomography (PET), functional near infrared (fNIR) imaging and - the most
widely used - EEG, as has been described earlier in section 2.1.5. The other technologies are
not being explained further in this thesis, since it is outside the scope of the project. [2]

2.3. Brain Computer Interface
2.3.1.2
Preprocessing
The raw signal acquired from the subject’s brain needs to undergo a series of signal processing
steps in order to be able to translate the signal into commands. The ﬁrst step in this process
is preprocessing which includes simplifying the subsequent processing operations by increasing
the signal-to-noise (SNR) ratio without losing relevant information in the signal.
This can
be carried out by a number of ﬁltering techniques to reduce noise in the signals, including:
temporal ﬁltering, which is ﬁltering in the time domain; spectral ﬁltering, which is ﬁltering in
the frequency domain; and spatial ﬁltering is ﬁltering related to space, or electrode position
when using EEG. [2]
2.3.1.3
Feature Extraction
The next signal processing step is called feature extraction, which - as the name implies - to
extract the relevant features of the signal that can be used to translate the signal into commands.
A commonly used feature in MI BCIs are band power features, which is the variance of a speciﬁc
frequency band. Feature extraction is also useful for reducing the computational requirements
of the processor by dimensionality reduction. Dimensionality reduction can also be performed in
the spatial ﬁltering, since the information in the signal from certain electrodes may be irrelevant
or insigniﬁcant, and the electrode(s) can be left out of the subsequent processing.
2.3.1.4
Classiﬁcation
The last signal processing step is classiﬁcation. The features extracted from the brain signals
must be assigned with a class corresponding the mental state of the subject. This is carried out
by using machine learning techniques or heuristics.
2.3.1.5
Application Interface
The output from the classiﬁer is a class corresponding to the subject’s mental state. This has
to be sent to the application device/environment, as well as converted to a control signal that is
able to operate the application. This is carried out by an application interface, e.g. a bluetooth
interface between the BCI system and the control unit of an electrically controlled wheelchair.

2.3.1.6
Application
The application is the external device/environment that is being controlled by the BCI system.
This includes communication systems (e.g. a speller), transportation devices (e.g. a wheelchair),
prostheses, smart homes and entertainment platforms, among many other things.
2.3.1.7
Feedback
The last step in order to close the loop is feedback. This is an important step in allowing the
subject to learn how to control the system. Most often, the feedback is visual e.g. by showing
an output on a monitor, but it can also include auditory or tactile feedback.
2.3.2
BCI Categories
A BCI system can belong to a variety of diﬀerent categories, each of which are being described
in this section.
Exogenous or Endogenous
A BCI system can be either exogenous or endogenous. The BCI is said to be exogenous, if it
relies on an external stimuli in order to induce a certain brain pattern. Such brain signals are
called evoked potentials, and they can be either evoked visually (VEP), auditorily (AEP) or
somatosensorily (SEP). A widely used exogenous method is the steady-state evoked potential,
especially steady-state visual evoked potential (SSVEP). SSVEPs can be evoked by visually
stimulating the subject with a ﬂickering square on a monitor/an LED at a certain frequency. The
SSVEPs measured on the subject’s visual cortex will include oscillations at the same frequency
as the ﬂickering stimulus. Exogenous system have the beneﬁt of requiring little to no training
prior to usage. The system is called endogenous, if it does not require an external stimulus.
This includes MI BCI systems as are being utilized in this project. Endogenous system requires
training to be operated successfully. [26]
Dependent or Independent
The system can also be classiﬁed as either dependent or independent, and is distinguished by the
requirement of additional activities. An example of a dependent BCI is the SSVEP, which relies

Synchronous or Asynchronous
Another categorization is whether the system is synchronous or asynchronous. A synchronous,
or cue-based, system is based on external signals that tell the subject when to start controlling
the system, thereby limiting the voluntariness of the subject. The cue is often visual or auditory,
by showing a cue on a monitor or playing a sound that corresponds to the onset or oﬀset of
the operation. An asynchronous, or self-paced, system is continuously processing the subject’s
brain signals, and the subject can freely operate the system.
Online or Oﬄine
A BCI system is ideally operating in real-time, which is called an online system. The opposite
is an oﬄine system, in which data collection and signal processing are split. This is useful for
training an algorithm, as well as test an algorithm by simulating a real-time session. [26]

system, which is learning through machine learning algorithms; the user, who are learning how
to properly control the system through operant conditioning by taking advantage of feedback;
and both the BCI system and the user, which is called a co-adaptive system. [2]

2.3.3
The MI BCI Paradigm
There are several paradigms that can be chosen in BCI systems depending on the objective of
the system. The paradigm that is being used in this project is the MI BCI paradigm, which
utilizes the subject’s ability to modulate certain covert brain waves.
The most important brain waves in MI BCIs are the so-called sensorimotor rhythms (SMRs),
which are corresponding to imagination or preparation of the movement over the somatosen-
sory and motor areas. Alpha activity in the sensorimotor areas are called mu activity. The
most important frequency bands for MI are the mu and central beta waves in the EEG signals.
People can desynchronize, i.e. weaken or block, these waves by imagining movements. This
desynchronization corresponds to a decrease in oscillatory activity related to an internally or
externally induced event - before or during movement - and is known as an event-related desyn-
chronization (ERD), see ﬁgure 2.14. The opposite, which is an increase in oscillatory activity
is called an event-related synchronization (ERS), and can be observed after movement and dur-
ing relaxation. These ERD/ERS patterns can be observed in both the contra- and ipsilateral
hemisphere during MI, but the former are the most prominent. ERD can generally be seen in
the attended body-part areas during MI, whereas ERS is seen in the non-attended areas at the
same time. By providing the subject with feedback, he/she is able to learn how to modulate
the ERD/ERS patterns and thereby become better at controlling the external device. [2]
It is important to note that a roughly estimated 15-30 % of users are unable to achieve successful
control of SMR-based noninvasive BCI systems. This problem is called BCI illiteracy, and so
far little is known about the problem. [2]

3.1
MI BCI Algorithms
In 1999, Gerking-M¨uller et al [27] proposed the Common Spatial Pattern (CSP) feature extrac-
tion method. The CSP algorithm is today a well known spatial ﬁlter often used for MI BCIs.
Brieﬂy, it discriminate two populations/classes of EEG data by maximizing the variance of one
class while minimizing the variance of the other class, in order to reﬂect the cortical activity
of the user. The method was tested using bandpass ﬁlter with cutoﬀfrequencies of 8 and 30
Hz for preprocessing, as well as a linear Bayesian classiﬁer for classiﬁcation. The test included
classifying single-trial EEGs on three subjects during MI of left or right index ﬁnger or the right
foot, which yielded correct classiﬁcation rates/accuracies of 94, 90 and 84 %, respectively. Given
these promising results, the method has since been utilized/investigated in numerous studies in
order to ﬁne tune the algorithm. The authors discussed the main caveat of the method being
that it is highly impacted by artifacts, which will be ever-present in a practical application.

In 2007, Novi et al [28] extended the CSP method by introducing the Sub-band CSP (SBCSP).
They mention one of the challenges with the CSP method is the inter-subject variability with
respect to the chosen information frequency band. In order to ﬁnd subject-speciﬁc frequency
band, methods such as the Common Spatio-Spectral Pattern (CSSP) by Lemm et al [29] and
and Common Sparse Spatio-Spectral Pattern (CSSSP) by Dornhege et al [30] - both from 2005 -
performs an optimization on each subject, in which the solution of the ﬁlter coeﬃcients are highly
dependent on the initial points due to the inherent nature of optimization problems. Instead,
the SBCSP decomposes the signal into multiple frequency bands (a ﬁlter bank), followed by
using the CSP method and a linear discriminant analysis (LDA) on each band. A sub-band
score fusion is performed in order to ﬁnd the most discriminant band(s), which is /are then sent
to the classiﬁer (see ﬁgure 3.1. By evaluation on publicly available BCI competition III data,
the proposed method outperformed the CSSP and CSSSP, and showed similar results to the so
far best approach. The latter approach was performed by an exhaustive, manual search for the
most discriminating frequency band for each subject, which is a very time consuming process.
In 2008, the SBCSP was challenged by a similar method - the Filter Bank CSP (FBCSP),
proposed by Ang et al [6]. The main diﬀerence is that the SBCSP utilizes two classiﬁcation
steps - using LDA ﬁrst and another method after the score fusion - whereas the FBCSP only
has one classiﬁcation step by using a feature selection step of the spatial ﬁlters - see ﬁgure
3.2. In addition, any feature selection algorithm from the machine learning and computational
intelligence litterature can be employed for feature selection and classiﬁcation, which makes
the algorithm more generalized than the SBCSP. The method is compared to the CSP and
SBCSP using the BCI competition III datsset IVa, in which it yields superior classiﬁcation
accuracy. The classiﬁcation methods being recommended with the FBCSP algorithm are the
Nave Bayes Parzen Window (NBPW), Fisher Linear Discriminant (FLD) or Support Vector
Machines (SVM).

In the paper by Arvaneh et al [31] from 2011, they propose an alternative to the widely used
Common Spatial Pattern (CSP) method by inducing sparsity - calling it Spatially Sparsed
CSP (SSCSP). The problem with the conventional CSP method is that the performance is
often impacted by noise and artifacts - especially with small training data. The purpose of
the proposed SSCSP is to improve the classiﬁcation performance by removing irrelevant, noisy
and correlated information. The algorithm emphasizes channels with high variances between
classes and attenuates noisy channels with low and irrelevant variances. EEG data from 14
subjects of publicly available data sets - BCI Competitions III and IV) were used to test the
algorithm and compare it to using ordinary CSP in an oﬄine analysis. Along with the spatial
ﬁlter, the EEG signals were ﬁltered with a band pass ﬁlter using cutoﬀfrequencies of 8 and 35
Hz, and the classiﬁcation was carried out using a support vector machine (SVM). The proposed
method showed a signiﬁcantly improved performance in signals with very poor signal-to-noise
ratio (SNR) with an improved accuracy of 11 % on average, while the accuracy in signals with
good SNR remained roughly unchanged - which makes sense, since the purpose of the algorithm
is to attenuate noisy and limited data, and not improve already clean data.
In conclusion,
the proposed SSCSP algorithm proved to be eﬀective in improving the performance of the
BCI system for noisy and limited data. A study by Bamdadian et al [32] from 2012 mentions
another challenge with the CSP algorithm - namely that its performance is dependant on the
frequency range chosen beforehand. In order to come around this, a Filter Bank CSP (FBCSP)
has been proposed earlier, which can automatically select subject-speciﬁc frequency ranges for
band pass ﬁltering and thereby achieve higher performance than normal CSP. However, the
subject’s brain signals may change signiﬁcantly from the time of the calibration to the actual
test session (session-to-session nonstationarities), and even during the test session e.g. due to
fatigue. Therefore, this study proposes an adaptive system that not only updates its classiﬁer,

In 2014, Arvaneh et al [33] did another study, also claiming the challenge with the frequency

selecting the optimal regularization parameter. Additionally, the mutual information approach

In a study from 2015 by Song and Yoon [34], an adaptive CSP (ACSP) was proposed. The
challenge with the CSP algorithms with added regularization terms explained earlier, is that
most of them require some of the training data to have known class labels - the data that is
unlabelled will have an estimated class label to assign the data to a speciﬁc class in order to
update the covariance matrix of that class. Estimating the class labels erroneously would impact
the CSP learning and reduce the system’s classiﬁcation performance. The proposed algorithm
uses unlabelled EEG data and does not estimate class labels. Instead, it updates the spatial
ﬁlters for both classes simultaneously using adaptive weights. The ACSP method is compared
to the normal CSP method as well as the semi-supervised importance weighted extreme energy
ratio (SIWEER) - which is a theoretic equivalent to CSP but computationally more eﬃcient -
using four datasets comprised from a total of 26 subjects from BCI Competitions III and IV. All
EEG signals were band pass ﬁltered with cutoﬀfrequencies of 8 and 32 Hz, and SVM was used
for classiﬁcation. The results show that the proposed ACSP algorithm can be used in real-time
and outperforms both the classic CSP and the SIWEER algorithms in terms of: intra-subject
classiﬁcation and inter-subject classiﬁcation, using multi-subject and single-subject training
data. One major limitation to the algorithm is that its performance is highly impacted by EEG
artifacts. In future work, the authors would integrate advanced preprocessing and classiﬁcation
methods to improve the system, as well as include more EEG data for evaluation.
In 2016, Aghaei et al [7] proposed a separable common spatio-spectral patterns (SCSSP) fea-
ture extraction algorithm to address a number of problems when using a FBCSP, which is
discussed by Arvaneh et al in 2014 (see above). One of the problems is that is has a high
computational cost at the training phase, which can be problematic if using adaptive methods
that require frequent retraining or if using mobile/wearable devices with limited computational
power. Another problem is that each frequency band is processed independently and thereby
it does not consider correlations between diﬀerent EEG rhythms. The independent processing
of each frequency band is also making it impossible to compare and sort discriminatory power
across diﬀerent frequency bands. The proposed SCSSP algorithm jointly processes EEG signals
in both spatial and spectral domains and extracts the most discriminant spatio-spectral features
across all frequency bands. This method has signiﬁcantly less computational cost for training

A study from 2008 by Meng et al [35] proposed a BCI-FES training system for rehabilitation
of stroke patients for the ﬁrst time - to the knowledge of the author of this present thesis. The
authors of the study stated that MI and FES have individually been extensively applied in
rehabilitation of stroke patients. They further state that the passive FES that was so far used
lacked the important intention to recover the patients, while the pure MI showed performance
variability due to a lack of feedback indication. The goal of the study was to show the feasibility
of the integration of MI and FES in an MI-based BCI-FES training system for chronic stroke
patients and to evaluate the eﬀect of the therapeutic strategy. Two chronic stroke patients
attended to this pilot study including 20 sessions over two months, where each session included
four runs with 20 trials in each run. 18 electrodes were placed around C3 and C4, and data
was sampled at a rate of 250 Hz. Meanwhile, electromyography (EMG) was measured from
the forearm and upper arm to monitor motion artifacts. CSP was used before constructing
feature vectors using power in the mu band (8-15 Hz). Fisher discriminate analysis (FDA) was
used for classiﬁcation. The instructions for each trial was shown on a monitor in front of the
subjects as follows: three seconds of focus/preparation for the actual MI; six seconds of MI of
wrist and hand trying to trigger a certain success rate; either ﬁve seconds of FES if the success

In 2011, Tam et al [36] proposed a more user-friendly system by investigating the minimal

In a study from 2012, Arvaneh et al [37] investigated omitting the long calibration session

In 2016, Djamal et al [40] proposed an MI based BCI system using autoregressive (AR) model
and neural networks to classify hand grasping imagination.
Four subjects were included in
training and four other subjects were included for tests. EEG signals were acquired by wireless
Neurosky EEG headset. Each session included three trials of the grasping states: grasping,
loosing and relax - with 10 seconds of blank (no recording) in between MI states.
Feature
extraction were carried out using 20-order AR models, i.e. - in short - a model in which each
data point can be predicted/approximated based on previous samples. The AR model outputs
AR coeﬃcients that serves as inputs for the multi-layer perceptron (MLP), which is a certain
type of artiﬁcial neural network (ANN). In short, an MLP is a network of artiﬁcial neurons with
multiple layers: an input layer, a number of hidden intermediate layer and the output layer.
Each layer has a number of neurons with an assigned speciﬁc weight that will change during
training in order to yield the desired output value in the output layer using backpropagation,
i.e. changing the weights in the previous layer according to the error between desired and actual
values in the present layer. In this study, there are 100 neurons in the input layer, 20 neurons
in the ﬁrst hidden layer, 8 neurons in the second hidden layer and three neurons in the output
layer. The experiments served to compare the system with and without AR models, as well as
using conventional backpropagation compared to adaptive backpropagation, i.e. using optimal
learning rate and tolerance of mean square error (MSE). The results showed that using AR
increased classiﬁcation accuracy from 68% to 82% on average as compared to not using AR.
Using adaptive backpropagation increased classiﬁcation accuracy from 76% to 82% on average
compared to conventional backpropagation. This indicates that using AR models and MLP for
classiﬁcation of multi-class MI based BCI systems is a promising setup for future rehabilitation
systems.

This section includes the theoretical background for the SCSSP algorithm that was developed
by Aghaei et al in 2016 [7].
The SCSSP method is an extension of the conventional CSP method, and the pipeline for the
system model is illustrated in ﬁgure 4.4. An EEG epoch of Nt time samples is being acquired
from Nch channels placed on the scalp of the subject, while he/she is performing one of C tasks
Ωi. The signal is passed through Nf bandpass ﬁlters, which forms the acquired signal epoch into
a three-dimensional tensor consisting of Nt matrices of size Nf × Nch - each of which represents
a spatio-spectral EEG pattern at a certain point in time. The purpose of the SCSSP method is
to extract the most discriminant features from these matrix-variate patterns X∈ℜNf × Nch. [7]

5.1.1
Training
For the training stage, the optimal number of features extracted and number of neurons in the
hidden layers of the MLP were determined. This was carried out as in [7] by using threefold
cross-validation over the three sets of training data. In threefold cross-validation, two sets are
used for training and the other one set is used for evaluation. This is performed three times, so
each session acts as a test session exactly one time. By using cross-validation, the intersession
variability is also addressed.
In order to ﬁnd the optimal number of parameters, each cross-validation session is performed
with as many parameter combinations as practically possible. The ideal optimization method
would be to test every possible combination, but the computation time did not allow for this
within the time frame of this project.

Since there was only one training session (compared to three in the competition), 5x5-fold
randomized cross-validation was performed on the data. This is done by randomizing the one
second data epochs, after which 5-fold cross-validation is performed on the randomized data.
This is repeated ﬁve times resulting in a total of 5x5 = 25 validation runs for each combination
of features.
The number of features in the experiments diﬀer compared to that of the competition, since
only 16 channels are used. The number of features in total are in the interval from 8 to 96
incrementing with 22. The number of neurons in each hidden layer in the MLP are in the
interval from 1 to 101 incrementing with 10 for each test. This gives a total of 55 combinations,
which are calculated 25 times each. As in the competition cross-validation,, the average correct
classiﬁcation rate/accuracy over the 25 runs is calculated for each combination and compared to
each other using an interaction plot to ﬁnd the optimal settings for each algorithm. The system
is then trained using the algorithm with the optimal number of parameters on the training data.

Online Test Session

session, the timing is slightly diﬀerent in that the signal acquisition is prolonged to six seconds
instead of three. Furthermore, the subjects are provided with feedback: each 0.5 second, data

class label corresponds to the task being performed, the subject will achieve a green bar as
shown in ﬁgure 5.8a. Since each consecutive data epoch has a 50 % overlap, the ﬁrst prediction
will happen after one second, giving a total of 11 predictions for each task. If each prediction is
correctly classiﬁed, the green bars will ﬁll up the white space in the feedback diagram as shown
in ﬁgure 5.8b.

been completed. In each sub-session, the system is trained diﬀerently: in the ﬁrst sub-session,
the system is trained only using data from the oﬄine training session; in the second sub-session,

Chapter 7
Discussion
7.1
BCI Competition
BCI Competition III Dataset V was utilized for two objectives: 1) compare the algorithms used
in this project to the results of Aghaei et al [7], as well as the results of the other participants
in the competition, and 2) compare the FBCSP algorithm with the SCSSP algorithm to choose
the one with highest CCR for experiments.
Aghaei et al achieved an average CCR of 52.42 % and 59.77 % using the FBCSP algorithm
in combination with the NBPW and the linear minimum mean distance (LMMD) classiﬁers,
respectively. In comparison, an average CCR of 60.48 % was achieved in this project by using
FBCSP with the MLP classiﬁer (see table 6.2), thereby proving to be signiﬁcantly superior to
the NBPW approach, and similar to the LMMD approach.
Similarly for the SCSSP algorithm, Aghaei et al achieved an average CCR of 55.21 % using
the NBPW approach and 59.33 % when using the LMMD approach. An average CCR of 64.71
% was achieved using SCSSP with MLP in this project, thereby proving to be superior in both
cases by more than 5 % CCR (see table 6.2).
When comparing to the results of the other participants in the competition, the results of this
project would have ranked 6th. The winner of the competition achieved an average CCR of
68.65 %, thereby only beating the results of this project by 3.94 % CCR.
The results from the competition and this project suggest that subject 1 is able to control
his/her SMRs signiﬁcantly better than the other two - with subject 3 being the worst. The

7.2
Experiments
The results in the experiments are unfortunately not highly impressive - only achieving an
average CCR of 34.94 % across all subjects and runs (see table 6.4).
Only subject 3 achieved an average CCR of 40.91 % in run 3, and as ﬁgure 6.4 suggests, this
subject also showed progress through the trials. However, this is considered to be a special case,
since this pattern is not seen for any other subjects.
In order to run the system in real-time and output a prediction every 0.5 second, there was a
limit to the number of features included in the system of around 52 features. This caused the
system to have a sub-optimal number of features for seven of the 10 subjects. A solution to this
would be to include the optimal number of features for each subject. Another solution that was
thought to improve the results, would be to increase the epoch length to two seconds, instead
of one second. Therefore, a test was simulated using the EEG data from the experiments and
implementing an epoch length of two seconds and the optimal number of features (see table
6.5). The results from this approach were very mixed: some subject achieved worse results than
before, while others achieved better results, resulting in average CCRs being almost unchanged.
The progressive pattern that was seen from the experiments in subject 3, is now also seen in
subjects 2 and 7 - with subject 7 achieving 46.37 % CCR in run 3. However, in subjects 9
and 12, the pattern is diﬀerent with highest CCRs (above 40 %) in run 1. It is important to
note that simulating experiments means that the impact of feedback/operant conditioning is
removed, which is thought to have a signiﬁcant impact in a successful system. Considering that
the maximum CCR value is 46.37 % and the average CCR is around 35 %, the algorithm is not
considered successful for this particular experiment.
The low CCRs can be caused by various other factors, which are being discussed in the follow-
ing.

Chapter 7. Discussion
7.2. Experiments
The three hand movements: grasp, pinch and open hand, are all movements of the same
limb.
Several subjects expressed that it was very diﬃcult to not only imagine movements,
but distinguish between three movements of the same limb. Furthermore, several subjects also
expressed that it was diﬃcult to remember how to imagine movements in the online test session,
since the time between the sessions was around two months. The reason why the time between
the sessions was so long was because of the challenging development process of the real-time,
online system. Since the performance of the test subjects is crucial for achieving promising
results, the diﬃculty of the task is probably of high impact on the results.
Another system-related factor is the usage of an MLP classiﬁer, which is highly sensitive to
overtraining - i.e. it will not generalize well - if not used very carefully. Since the MI EEG
signals are often noisy and non-stationary, this could much likely be a signiﬁcant factor. Usage
of artifact reduction techniques, such as independent component analysis (ICA) could be utilized
to remove known artifacts, e.g. movement artifacts. Another classiﬁer, such as the NBPW or
LMMD classiﬁers could also be tested for comparison in a similar experiment.
Additionally, the system is trained three times in total on data, which is not necessarily con-
sidered ”good training data”. Given the nature of the experimental procedure (to investigate
progress over time), the ﬁrst few trials would probably be relatively worse than the subsequent
trials, since the subject had to get used to MI. Therefore, ”good training data” could be iden-
tiﬁed and used for training, instead of including all training data. The ”good training data”
could be either manually picked based on CCR achieved from the trials, or by selecting the
latter trials of each run.
Usually, prior to testing a BCI system, there is a long calibration session. Without changing
the electrode positions and carrying out the test session right after the calibration, not only is
the subject trained in MI, the system is calibrated to the subject with those exact electrode
positions and in that exact environment. In this project, it was investigated if the calibration
session could be performed on a diﬀerent day, since -ideally - a new user should be able to
operate the BCI system immediately. As indicated in the above discussion, the results did not
show any tendencies between the runs. One could argue, that for run 3 - in which the system
was retrained on the previous two runs from the same day - the calibration was performed on the
same day and in the same environment. However, the subject was provided with feedback for
these runs, which could impact the calibration, since the subject is undergoing learning through

operant conditioning. Thus, it cannot be rejected that the system would work by performing

Another consideration is that healthy subjects may ﬁnd it diﬃcult to imagine a movement

Finally, another improvement would be to make the system fully adaptive, instead of training

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to develop a co-adaptive, three-class MI based BCI system for real-
time processing that is capable of successfully classifying EEG signals corresponding to three
hand movements: palmar grasp, pinch grasp, and open hand to potentially aid stroke patients
in the neurorehabilitation of hand motor function.
Two three-class MI based BCI algorithms have been developed for comparison using the pub-
licly available BCI Competition III Dataset V. The ﬁrst system includes the FBCSP algorithm
for combined preprocessing and feature extraction of spatio-spectral features, whereas the other
system includes the SCSSP algorithm for the same tasks. For both algorithms, a two-layer
MLP was used for classiﬁcation. The results achieved were compared to those of Aghaei et al
[7], who also used the FBCSP and SCSSP methods, but with NBPW and LMMD classiﬁcation
methods.
The results achieved in this project using the competition data were generally superior to those
achieved by Aghaei et al - especially comparing the SCSSP algorithms for which more than a
5 % increased CCR was achieved. Additionally, when comparing this project’s results to those
of the other competition participants, this algorithm would have ranked 6th with an average
CCR of 64.71 % for the SCSSP method. The winner of the competition only achieved a 3.94 %
increased CCR compared to the algorithm in this project.
The SCSSP algorithm generally achieved better results than the FBCSP, for which reason the
former was utilized for experiments using 10 healthy subjects. Two experimental sessions were
carried out: an oﬄine training session, and an online test session. The former served for training
69 of 107

Chapter 8. Conclusion
both the subjects and the system, as well as determining the optimal number of parameters to
be included in the system. The online test session served to evaluate the system.
Prior to the online test session, the system was extended by introducing real-time feedback for
the user to utilize operant conditioning, and was retrained during the experiments. Thereby,
the system objectives of being a co-adaptive, three-class MI based BCI system for real-time
processing was fulﬁlled. However, the experimental results were generally unimpressive, showing
average CCRs around chance level (33 %). Hence, the objective of being capable of successfully
classify EEG-signals from the three speciﬁed hand movements, was unfortunately not met. This
may be caused by various factors, including inappropriate training data due to not including
a proper calibration session just prior to the online test session. The most signiﬁcant factor,
however, is probably that the movements included were both too diﬃcult for the subjects,
and too similar for the system to classify. The system was successful in classifying the three
MI tasks in the competition (left and right hand movement, as well as imagining words), and
several subjects included in the experiments in this study expressed that not only was it diﬃcult
to imagine movements, but especially to distinguish between three movements in the same limb.
Therefore, the chosen movements are considered to be the major factors leading to unimpressive
experimental results..

Chapter 9
Future Work
In order to develop the ideal MI BCI system for neurorehabilitation of hand motor function in
stroke patients, there is much work to be done in the future.
This project only evaluated one of many possible algorithm constellations, and focused on
testing the combination of SCSSP and MLP methods.
A way to improve the system is to
include artifact reduction techniques such as ICA or PCA methods. The system could also be
developed to be continuously adapting itself instead of manually adapting every X number of
trials.
This project also suggests to include easier tasks for the subjects in future online test sessions
in order to evaluated the system’s performance before taking it a step further.
Optimal signal processing algorithms to successfully predict the users’ intentions must be
developed, which requires more knowledge of the underlying cortical activities in the brain.
Thankfully, engineers, computer scientists and doctors around the world are striving to get
closer to a better solution for neurorehabilitation of stroke patients.
Such rehabilitation systems should ideally be quickly set up, mobile, aesthetically pleasing,
motivating and - not least - successful. Due to Moore’s law, processors are ever improving,
while small devices get smaller and smaller. High-performance dry-electrodes are also being
developed, which would ensure an easier and faster electrode placement.
Furthermore, virtual reality based systems are being developed e.g. to create a more motivating
and exciting rehabilitation environment, which could be included in a MI based neurorehabili-
taion system for stroke pateients. [43]
